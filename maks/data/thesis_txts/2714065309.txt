1 Abstract 
Aspergillus comprises a large genus of filamentous fungi and several of its members are of industrial 
importance for the production of proteins and other metabolites. Development of an eukaryotic cell 
factory for protein production can be an extensive and costly task and one of the hurdles to overcome 
is the accumulation of mis- and unfolded proteins in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) caused by protein 
overproduction. When ER-stress occurs a broad transcriptional response known as the Unfolded 
Protein Response (UPR) springs into action to increase the cell’s protein folding machinery and 
secretory capacity, while simultaneously reducing the burden caused by the incorrectly or un-
processed proteins. The UPR consists of several hundreds of genes and contains many interesting 
targets for up- and downregulation in the optimization of protein production, but still remains largely 
unexplored. In this study we take the first steps towards establishing a platform that can be used to 
investigate the UPR and its impact on protein production in two of the most well characterized and 
widely employed Aspergillus members, Aspergillus oryzae and Aspergillus niger.  
Towards this goal, we try to address two questions: How to reliably induce ER-stress in a manner 
characteristic for a production strain and how to measure the state of protein homeostasis and UPR 
signalling in Aspergilli. To answer the first, the CASCADE expression system is adopted, a method for 
consistent and stable chromosomal integration of a defined number of copies of a gene of interest, 
using strains containing a specific number of universal gene acceptor cassettes (GACs). For the second, 
a biosensor is constructed based on HacA, the regulatory master-switch of the Aspergillus UPR. 
12 chromosomal CASCADE integration sites (ISs) are selected for both A. oryzae and A. niger and a 
CASCADE GAC cloning vector was designed and constructed. Several plasmids for IS specific 
integration of the GAC were constructed, including Clustered Regularly Interspaced Palindromic 
Repeats (CRISPR)/CRISPR associated protein (Cas) plasmids targeting multiple ISs and vectors 
containing IS specific GAC repair fragments. However, no genomic integrations of the GACs were 
performed due to a lack of matching CRISPR/Cas and repair fragment vectors. Additionally, three 
different UPR biosensors and their expression vectors were designed and constructed, but Aspergillus 
transformations and subsequent proof of concept experiments were inconclusive.

2 Introduction 
2.1 Aspergilli as cell factories 
Filamentous fungi make up a large group of organisms distinguished from other fungi by their mode 
of growth, the formation of hyphae, thin branching filaments growing in an increasingly complex web-
like structure known as the mycelium. Due to their extensive use as cell factories in industrial 
biotechnology and role as pathogens to both humans and plants, filamentous fungi have a 
considerable impact on human prosperity worldwide with an estimated global market in the billions 
[1, 2], with the annual number of publications and patents painting a clear picture of Aspergillus as 
the most popular genus for biotechnological applications [3]. As of 2020 Aspergillus is recognized to 
include 446 named species [4], portraying high chemotypic diversity in the form of a large range of 
secreted compounds including bioactive peptides and proteins, organic acids, lectins, enzymes, 
hydrophobins, aegerolysins, polyketides and non-ribosomal peptides [5-7]. The focus of this study is 
on two of its most well known members, A. oryzae and A. niger. 
A. oryzae has historically been used for the fermentation of various foods and beverages in Asia and 
is the most popular mold for koji preparation. The earlier records of soy fermentation from China date 
back 3000 years and similar methods are thought to be introduced to Japan around 1300 years ago 
[8]. During fermentation the main role of the koji is to break down the large polymers in starch and 
protein rich raw materials through the secretion of amylases, proteases and peptidases and it is used 
for manufacturing products like shoyu, miso, vinegar, sake and shochu [9-11]. In addition to its 
traditional involvement in food production, A. oryzae was used in the production of one of the first 
patented industrial enzyme preparations, Takadiastase, by Dr. Jokichi Takamine in 1890 [8]. Nowadays 
the species is used to produce a variety of native and recombinant enzymes as well as other secondary 
metabolites. Some notable examples include native amylolytic enzymes, Flavourzyme peptidase 
preparation, Fusarium heterosporum lipase, Aspergillus aculeatus pectinesterase, kojic acid, Oryza 
sativa curcumin, Basidiomycetic erinacine, genome mined phaseolide A and human antibodies [12-
20]. 
The extraordinary capacity of A. niger to produce large amounts of citric acid when grown on sugar, 
even at low pH, was first described by James Currie in 1916 [21]. Up to that point citric acid was still 
obtained through extraction from citrus fruits, but during decades to follow the industry almost 
completely shifted to industrial fermentation as the method of choice while the process of citric acid 
fermentation was perfected [22]. Citric acid is commonly used in pharmaceutical, cosmetic and food 
industries and fermentation with A. niger is still the most popular means of production due to the high 
yields it can achieve [23]. In 2016 the global citric acid market was worth 2.50 billion united states 
dollars (USD) and is expected to grow to 6.28 USD by 2030 [24, 25]. In addition to citric acid, the species 
has successfully been employed in the industrial production of gluconic acid and extracellular proteins 
including amylases, lactoferrin, proteases, pectinase, chymosin, phytase, cellulase, hemicellulase, 
asparaginase, catalase and lipase [26-30]. 
In contrast to some of their cousins, both species lack any significant mycotoxin production and are 
classified Generally Recognized as Safe (GRAS) by the American Food and Drug Administration and 
World Health Organization [31-34]. The first fully sequenced genomes, A. oryzae RIB40 and A. niger 
CBS 513.88 were published in 2005 and 2007 respectively, with many more following after the 
emergence of next-generation sequencing [35, 36]. On the National Center for Biology Information1 
database there are currently 101 genome assemblies available for A. oryzae and 68 for A. niger.

However, as a side effect of the increased number of available genomes and their reduced publicatory 
impact there is a notable lack of annotation, only being available for 4 of the former and 31 of the 
latter assemblies. Only around 1.7% of genes for A. oryzae has been functionally verified and for both 
many of the genes fall in the category hypothetical proteins [26, 37]. The second great technological 
leap in Aspergillus research was the widespread adoption of CRISPR/Cas-based genome editing 
techniques, allowing a wide variety of genetic manipulations to be performed with relative ease [26, 
37-42]. These technological advancements have opened up the way to fully explore and exploit 
Aspergillus as eukaryotic cell factory, but the development of a production strain with sufficiently high 
yields comes with its own challenges. Cost reduction is a major driving force and competition is tough, 
with Escherichia coli and other prokaryotes as cheap and easy to engineer hosts for production that 
does not require post-translational modification and Saccharomyces cerevisiae as popular eukaryotic 
platform due to its fast growth, easy cultivation and vast knowledge base. Nevertheless, S. cerevisiae 
natively secretes only a small amount of proteins and as a result developed a low capacity secretory 
pathway, allowing competition from Aspergilli [43].  
After the initial step of selecting a GRAS host with available selection markers, promoters and 
preferably known integration sites (IS), there is a variety of strategies to further improve yield of a 
protein production strain [43]. A sufficiently high level of transcription of the gene of interest is the 
first requirement and can be achieved by using strong, constitutive promoters, introducing synthetic 
transcription factors and increasing gene copy numbers [43-48]. Once a certain level of transcription 
has been met, further increases do not necessarily lead to increased protein production as translation 
and secretion become a bottleneck. The process of secretion is still poorly understood, but starts with 
the translation of a protein by ribosomes, subsequent protein folding and post-translational 
modification in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER), further maturation in the Golgi apparatus and finally 
directed vesicle transport towards a final destination [43]. After transcription, mRNA needs to be 
effectively directed towards the ER, which can be achieved by fusing it with a strong signal peptide 
(SP). Potentially strong SPs can be selected by screening known secreted proteins or by using 
prediction software such as SingalP, but in vivo screening is generally needed as high secretion does 
not always translate well from one protein to another [43, 49, 50]. Alternatively, the protein of interest 
can be fused with a highly secreted native protein [51]. The next hurdle is overburdening of the 
secretory pathway. When unfolded or misfolded proteins accumulate in the ER, a stress response 
known as the Unfolded Protein Response (UPR) is triggered in an attempt to restore protein 
homeostasis by both increasing protein folding and degradation of unfolded proteins [43, 52, 53]. 
Overexpression of chaperones and induction of the UPR have been shown to lead to increases in 
protein production [43, 54-56]. Finally, to ensure product stability removal of natively secreted 
proteases and peptidases might be required, a considerable task given the high potential to produce 
protein-degrading of some of these species [43]. 
2.2 The Unfolded Protein Response 
Disruption of protein homeostasis of eukaryotes can occur under a variety of circumstances ranging 
from starvation, hypoxia, infection and chemical disruption of folding machinery or the folding 
environment, to overexpression of homologous or heterologous proteins and leads to the 
accumulation of mis- or unfolded proteins in the ER, canonically dubbed ER-stress. When these 
conditions arise, four different coping strategies can spring into action; translation attenuation in an 
attempt to suppress the incoming load of newly synthesized proteins, upregulation of folding 
machinery and expansion of the ER, initiation of ER-associated degradation of mis- and unfolded 
proteins and finally, as a last resort, programmed cell death [57]. The UPR is a large regulon consisting 
of hundreds of genes which dynamically adjust the ER folding capacity and burden on the ER through

negative feedback regulation (Fig. 1.A) and its control mechanism is highly conserved between species 
[58]. In yeast the main regulator of the UPR is Hac1p, a basic-leucine zipper (bZIP) transcription factor, 
which positively autoregulates [59]. Under normal circumstances the mRNA encoding Hac1p is 
translationally blocked by long range base-pairing between an unconventional intron (intron 2) and 
the 5’ untranslated region (UTR) of the mRNA (Fig. 1.B & C) [60]. Hac1p is post-translationally 
regulated by Ire1p, a transmembrane kinase/endonuclease lodged in the ER-membrane [61]. The 
Ire1p sensory domain is located in the ER-lumen and can bind the hydrophobic residues of unfolded 
proteins, leading to oligomerization and auto-transphosphorylation of the cytosolic endonuclease 
domain. In turn, activated Ire1p splices the Hac1 mRNA, allowing translation to take place. One of the 
results of Hac1p downstream signalling is the induction of chaperones, including BiP, a Hsp-70 type 
chaperone which can bind both unfolded proteins as well as the sensory domain of Ire1p, sequestering 
its ligand and preventing oligomerization (Fig. 1.D). Functional analysis of the yeast UPR revealed 381 
different open reading frames (ORFs) induced under ER-stress, of which only 208 have functional 
information available [62]. These include genes involved in glycosylation, protein folding, protein 
degradation, ER-Golgi transport, translocation, secretion, cell wall biogenesis, vacuolar targeting and 
lipid metabolism, underlining the scope of the UPR’s involvement in different aspects of the secretory

pathway. As opposed to the rather large 252 bp unconventional intron of S. cerevisiae Hac1, the intron 
of A. niger ortholog HacA is only 20 bp long and does not base-pair with the 5’ UTR, instead forming a 
stable stem-loop (Fig. 1.C) [63, 64]. Upon activation of A. niger Ire1p ortholog IreA the intron is spliced 
and a 230 bp truncation of the 5’ UTR of the mRNA is observed [63]. The exact mechanism behind the 
truncation is not well understood and while a sequence with high similarity to the unconventional 
intron is present in the 5’UTR, mutational analysis strongly suggests that IreA is not involved, as the 
mRNA behaves functionally identical under ER-stress, with or without the recognition site present. 
Instead, it is hypothesized that HacA serves as its own transcriptional activator downstream of the 
regular transcriptional start site, causing the 5’ UTR to only be 73 bp long. Furthermore, the full length 
mRNA is subject to translational attenuation caused by long-range base pairing between a region in 
the untruncated 5’UTR and exon 1, ensuring a certain threshold must be met before UPR signalling 
starts at full speed [63]. Two additional UPR activation pathways have been described in mammals, 
one mediated by the ER-associated transmembrane kinase PERK and the other by the ER-associated 
transmembrane transcription factor ATF6 [65]. While no additional activating pathways in fungi have 
been described, IraA/Ira1 independent activation of the UPR has been reported in both A. oryzae and 
S. cerevisiae [66, 67], indicating that alternative activation mechanisms are also present in these 
species, perhaps similar to those of other eukaryotes. ER-stress conditions can easily be induced with 
chemicals such as dithiothreitol (DTT) or tunicamycin [68-71], the former preventing formation of 
disulphide bonds and the latter preventing N-glycosylation, both resulting in the misfolding of most 
proteins. However, this artificial form of ER-stress does not necessarily reflect the dynamics that would 
occur in an overburdened production strain. 
2.3 Project aim 
It is clear that the UPR conceals many aspects of protein homeostasis that could prove invaluable as 
tools for the optimization of fungal cell factories, which brings us to the aim of this project: establishing 
a platform that can be used to investigate the UPR and its impact on protein production in filamentous 
fungi. More specifically, we try to address the following two questions: How can we reliably induce 
ER-stress that mimics the conditions found in a production host and how do we measure the state of 
protein homeostasis in Aspergilli? For answers we look towards a not so distant relative, S. cerevisiae, 
and adapt two systems developed in this species for use in filamentous fungi, the CASCADE expression 
platform and a UPR biosensor.

3 Experimental theory 
3.1 CASCADE 
In filamentous fungi, plasmid based expression is a relatively easy way to increase copy numbers and 
Agrobacterium tumefaciens and CRISPR-based methods have been described to perform multiple 
genomic insertions simultaneously [72-75], however, these techniques either lead to unstable copy 
numbers or are laborious and hard to achieve high copy numbers due to the requirement of 
performing multiple subsequent rounds of transformations. The CASCADE platform for S. cerevisiae 
allows consistent and stable marker-less integration of a specific number of copies of a gene of interest 
into the host genome by exploiting homology directed repair (HDR) DNA repair mechanisms [76]. First, 
a strain is engineered to have a certain number of gene acceptor cassette (GAC) sites integrated in the 
genome on sites with high transcriptional activity. The GAC consists of a 5’ HDR sequence (HDRS-A), a 
I-SceI target site, a counter selectable dominant selection marker (marker 1) and a 3’ HDR sequence 
(HDRS-B). I-SceI is a highly specific meganuclease originating from an intron in S. cerevisiae 
mitochondrial RNA [77]. A gene of interest can be inserted into all GACs through a five-step 
transformation and selection procedure (Fig. 2). First, the GAC strain is transformed with a donor 
template (DT) consisting of HDRS-A, a gene of interest (GOI) controlled by regulatory elements of 
choice, a different counter selectable dominant selection marker (marker 2) flanked by short repeat 
sequences and HDRS-B, using marker 2 for selection. Second, transformants that have lost marker 2

Fourth, transformants are grown on medium with selection for the I-SceI plasmid, allowing cleavage

3.2 CRISPR systems 
CRISPR/Cas systems are a form of adaptive immune systems found in prokaryotes, providing them 
with protection against viruses. The CRISPR array was first observed by accident in E. coli in 1987 by a 
group of Japanese researchers, but it took until the mid 2000s to unravel its function and in 2012 its 
potential as a powerful tool for genetic engineering was finally revealed with Cas9 [79-81]. Since then 
CRISPR/Cas-based techniques have revolutionized microbiology and its numerous applications are put 
to use in a large variety of pro- and eukaryotes including vertebrates, fungi and plants [82].  
Many different CRISPR-Cas classes exist, but the most widely used are the Type II systems (Fig. 3.A) 
[82]. These consist of a CRISPR array, a Cas endonuclease and small trans-activating RNAs (tracrRNA). 
The CRISPR array serves as ‘memory’ of the system and consists of a number of short palindromic 
repeats separated by unique sequences of specific length (henceforth spacer). The palindromic 
repeats of the transcribed CRISPR array (pre-crRNA) form small hairpins and the tracrRNA binds to the 
CRISPR RNA (crRNA) repeat sequences of the pre-crRNA. In turn the tracrRNA:pre-crRNA is stabilized 
by Cas, allowing cleavage by RNaseIII to release the processed tracrRNA:crRNA consisting of a single 
spacer and repeat in complex with a tracrRNA [83]. Finally, the Cas is directed by the tracrRNA:crRNA 
to find DNA sequences that are both homologous to the spacer sequence and have a specific motif, 
known as the protospacer adjacent motif (PAM), alongside it to subsequently bind and cleave the 
DNA.  
PAM sites and cleavage patterns are dependent on the CRISPR-system and the length of the spacer in 
conjunction with the PAM requirement make them highly specific. In CRISPR systems developed for 
genetic engineering usually a tracr-crRNA fusion called a single-guideRNA (sgRNA) is used. For 
multiplex engineering in Aspergillus with multiple sgRNAs a synthetic CRISPR-array-like design has 
been proposed (Fig. 3.B), consisting of sgRNAs interspaced with Aspergillus nidulans transfer RNAs 
(tRNA) under control of an A. nidulans U3 promoter and terminator [84]. Splicing by native RNase Z 
and RNase P releases the sgRNAs from the array.  
In this study, Mad7 is used, a type II CRISPR system developed by Inscrypta [85]. The PAM site of Mad7 
is 5’-YTTN-3’ and targets a 21 base pair (bp) sequence, cutting in a staggered fashion close to the 3’ 
end of the spacer, creating a 4-5 bp 5’ overhang. The nuclease has been released royalty-free for both 
academic and commercial use and has been demonstrated to work in prokaryotes, mice, human cell 
lines, plants and fungi [86-89]. With its different PAM site and unambiguous intellectual property 
rights, Mad7 provides an interesting alternative to Cas9 based methods.

3.3 USER cloning 
Uracil-Specific Excision Reagent (USERTM) cloning is a DNA engineering technique that can be use to 
seamlessly assemble one or multiple linear DNA modules (BioBlocks) and insert them into a cloning 
vector ligase free [90, 91]. The technique relies on the creation of single stranded tails on the 3’ end 
of each of the BioBlocks, which are complementary to the tail on the opposing strand of the next 
BioBlock. BioBlocks are amplified using primers that have a thymine-base approximately 8 bp from 
the 5’ end of the primer replaced by a uracil-base. Treatment with USER enzyme removes the uracil 
nucleotide from the DNA and subsequent diffusion of the short 5’ primer ends results in a single 
stranded 3’ overhang (Fig. 4.A). USER enzyme consists of Uracil DNA glycosidase and DNA glycosylase-
lyase Endonuclease VIII, the former being responsible for excision of the uracil base form the 
phosphodiester backbone and the latter cleaving the 5’ and 3’ ends of the resulting abasic site [92, 
93]. USER vectors contain specific cloning sites consisting of an endonuclease target sequence flanked 
by two nicking endonuclease target sequences on the opposing strands, resulting in two unique 8 bp

3.5 Design Aspergillus gene acceptor cassette 
The Aspergillus GAC is designed to be compatible with the quick two-step integration procedure 
described in section 3.1. As selection marker pyrG will be used since both strains are already pyrG-, 
thus no additional strain engineering is required and counterselection can be performed with 5-
fluoroorotic acid. As visual marker the fluorescent protein mRFP is used. The cassette consists of 
HDRS-A, the I-SceI recognition site, mRFP under control of A. nidulans PgpdA and TrpC, pyrG under 
control of A. nidulans Ptef and TpkiA and finally HDRS-B (Fig. 5). 
3.6 Selection CASCADE integration sites 
When assessing potential genomic ISs various characteristics of the selected locus should be 
considered. An ideal site has high transcription rates, is stable and does not interfere with the activity 
of any native genes in its vicinity. Finding sites that satisfy all criteria can be a difficult task and 
optimization of one aspect usually leads to concessions on another. 
Ubiquitous or highly expressed genes are expected to reside in euchromatic regions and placing an IS 
near them increases the chance that the transcriptional machinery of the cell can easily access the IS. 
Due to the homogeneous nature of the GAC’s, extra precaution should be taken to avoid potential 
loss of genetic material and GACs caused by homologous recombination between different GACs. By 
both having at least one essential gene between every IS and each IS and the telomere of the 
chromosomal arm it is located on, the risk of HDR crossover resulting in loss of genetic material is 
diminished, as this would lead to a non-viable phenotype. Finally, the noncoding regions surrounding 
a gene can contain various regulatory elements, sometimes at considerable distance from the gene. 
Placing an IS too close to neighbouring genes can cause regulatory elements to be displaced or lost, 
altering the expression of these genes. 
Potential ISs are identified with the Jbrowse genome browser plugin of FungiDB2, an integrated 
functional genomics database for fungi and oomycetes [95-97]. The parental strains used as reference 
are RIB40 for ORY2 and ATCC 1015 for NIG0158. Due to the available data for each strain the strategies 
for IS selection differ slightly. In both cases ISs are selected to have at least 1 kilobase (Kb) of noncoding 
DNA on either side of the IS to serve as homologous recombination sites (HRSs) for the GAC donor 
fragment, leaving a region containing at least two Mad7 PAM sequences between them. For A. oryzae 
each IS is placed on a different chromosomal arm and in the vicinity of a gene expected to be highly 
expressed, whenever possible. Instead of ensuring the presence of an essential gene, each IS is placed 
far from the telomere, assuming that each chromosomal arm contains some important genes and that 
loss of a large region of one of them would lead to a non-viable or at least heavily impaired phenotype. 
Additionally, the RNA expression profile of the IS is considered, preferring sites where non-coding RNA 
(ncRNA) is present but not abundant at the IS itself, implying that the locus is not heterochromatic 
while reducing the likelihood that the IS interferes with functional ncRNAs. In the genome browser 
the contigs of ATCC 1015 have not been assigned to chromosomes and RNA sequencing data has not 
been integrated. With the exception of NIG.IS2 and NIG.IS6 each IS has been placed on a different

3.7 Design Aspergillus UPR biosensors 
For S. cerevisiae a UPR biosensor (UPRBS) has been constructed by fusing two contrasting florescent 
luciferase genes with the hac1p 5’UTR as linker and attaching the unconventional intron to the 3’ end 
(Fig. 6.A) [98]. Under normal circumstances base pairing between the intron tail and the linker 
prevents translation, whereas under ER-stress conditions the intron is removed and both fluorescent 
proteins are produced. In this system the ratio between the two fluorescent genes is used as a 
measurement of UPR activity.  
Although inspired by this design, the Aspergillus biosensors cannot be constructed in the same way 
due to the fundamental differences between hac1 and hacA mRNA. Instead of the 5’UTR binding site, 
which is absent in hacA, the region of the unconventional intron itself is used as linker (Fig. 6.B). 
Because the intron constitutes only a small section of the mRNA stem-loop, it is not possible to create 
an IreA target site without introducing parts of hacA exon 2 and 3. In addition, mRNA secondary 
structure predictions with the RNAfold WebServer3 using RNAfold 2.4.18 from the ViennaRNA Package 
2.0 [99, 100] reveal that a larger and between A. oryzae and A. niger highly conserved section of exon 
2 and 3 is expected to participate in this stem loop structure (Fig. 6.C). Two UPRBSs are designed, one 
containing the smaller stem-loop region of A. oryzae hacA, based on the previously reported A. niger 
intron, and one containing the extended stem-loop predicted in this study. The UPRBSs consist of the 
coding sequence of mCitrine without stop codon, the A. oryzae hacA intron linker and the coding 
sequence of mRFP. Both are designed such that after splicing the 20 bp intron mRFP is in line with the 
ORF of the start codon. The sequence similarity between the A. oryzae and A. niger hacA intron is 
97.1% for the small and 96.2% for the large loop. Additionally the IreA splice sites themselves are 
entirely the same, thus if the UPRBS is functional for one species, it is likely that it will also be functional 
for the other. 
In addition, a hacA:mRFP fusion protein is created by replacing the stop codon of A. oryzae hacA with 
the coding sequence of mRFP, leaving the introns of hacA intact (Fig. 6.D). Although no experimental 
structures of HacA or Hac1 are available on the Protein Data Bank4 [101], the general structure of a 
bZIP has a N-terminal basic DNA binding region and a C-terminal leucine zipper domain [102], meaning 
that the mRFP sequence will be fused on the end of the leucine zipper domain. The DNA sequence of 
all designed biosensors can be found in Appendix II: UPR Biosensor Sequences.

3.8.1 ORY2::Δku70 
To knock out ku70 in ORY2, ORY2 protoplasts are co-transformed with a Mad7 plasmid containing two 
sgRNAs targeting the ku70 ORF and two overlapping HDR DTs (Fig. 7.). The DTs consist of the regions 
flanking ku70 linked with a 54 bp synthetic CRISPR-targeting site (CTS) containing multiple Mad7, Cas9

DTs are used to repair the double stranded break through HDR, resulting in complete removal of the 
coding sequence of ku70. As control of the transformation the ku70 locus is amplified through 
polymerase chain reaction (PCR) using extracted gDNA of transformants as template and a primer set 
that anneals outside of the region overlapped by the DTs.

3.9 Plasmid design and construction 
All used vectors as well as newly constructed cloning-vectors are designed to be USER cloning 
compatible, allowing them to easily be adopted in future projects. All plasmids contain an origin of 
replication (ORI) and ampR gene for propagation in E. coli. Additionally, plasmids used for fungal 
transformation bear an AMA-1 region and either hygB or pyrG as selectable marker to maintain the 
plasmid after transformation. AMA-1 is a palindromic sequence that was found in the A. nidulans 
genome and can be used for extrachromosomal replication of plasmids in fungi [103]. 
Linear DNA fragments for plasmid assembly are obtained through digestion of USER cloning vectors 
with PacI and Nt.BbvCI or PCR amplification with USER compatible primers. After linearization or PCR, 
fragments are separated through agarose gel electrophoresis and extracted from the gel. Fragments 
are assembled into plasmids through USER cloning and subsequently heat shock-transformed into E. 
coli DH5α. Transformants are screened with colony PCR using plasmid specific primers amplifying the 
USER cloning sites. Positive colonies are grown overnight before plasmid extraction. Correct assembly 
of the plasmid is verified through restriction enzyme (RE) analysis and regions containing PCR 
amplified genes or sgRNAs are Sanger sequenced to ensure no mutations occurred during cloning. 
Amplification of BioBlocks containing genes or sgRNAs is performed with PhusionU polymerase. 
Amplification of other fragments and colony PCR is performed with Pfu-X7 polymerase.  
An exhaustive list of primers and vectors as well as a brief description of their purpose can be found 
in Appendix III: Plasmids and Appendix IV: Primers respectively. Note that not all of the designed 
primers were used in this study or yielded an amplicon. 
3.9.1 CRISPR-Mad7 plasmids 
The CRISPR/MAD7 plasmids used to transform fungal strains contain AMA-1, hygB or pyrG as selection 
marker, Mad7 under control of an A. nidulans tef1 promoter and terminator and a synthetic CRISPR 
array under control of an A. fumigatus U3 promoter and terminator (Fig. 9.A). The CRISPR array 
consists of one or more sgRNA, each flanked by A. nidulans glycin tRNAs and each sgRNA consist of a 
guide RNA (gRNA) fused with a unique spacer sequence (Fig. 9.B). 
The plasmids are constructed by digestion of a pDIV298 (pyrG marker) or pAC2001 (hygB marker) USER 
vector and subsequent USER insertion of BioBlocks to construct the CRISPR-array. The first BioBlock 
contains the U3 promoter, a tRNA, gRNA and the 5’ side of the first spacer. The last BioBlock contains 
the 3’ side of the last spacer, a tRNA and the U3 terminator. Additional BioBlocks consisting of a 3’ 
spacer-end, tRNA, gRNA and 5’ spacer-end are added to increase the length of the CRISPR array, with 
each 5’ end matching the previous and 3’ end matching the next spacer. All linear spacer fragments 
are amplified using pDIV309, a pDIV298 derived plasmid containing a single sgRNA, as template. Note 
that the forward primers of the intermediate and final sgRNA BioBlocks bind to the template tRNA 
sequence and thus have two binding sites, one for each of the tRNAs in the CRISPR array of the 
template. In both cases the shorter of the two fragment corresponds to the correct BioBlock. 
RE analysis with NsiI is performed to verify correct assembly of the plasmid (Fig. 9.C). NsiI cleaves 
between the selectable marker and ORI and up- and downstream of the CRISPR array, resulting in 8.9 
Kb and 7.1 Kb fragments and a fragment with variable length of 300 bp + 127 bp per sgRNA.

4.3 Genomic DNA extraction 
For the extraction of Aspergillus gDNA, mycelium is scraped from a plate colony and mixed with 500 
μL breaking buffer (2% triton x-100, 1% sodium dodecyl sulfate, 100 mM NaCl, 10 mM TrisHCl, 1 mM 
ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid (EDTA)) and 200 μL glass beads (0.1-0.25 μm, Retch). The tissue is 
lysed in a FastPrep Cell Disrupter (FP120, Thermo) at speed 4 for 40 seconds before spinning the debris 
down with a table centrifuge and transferring 150 μL supernatant to a new tube. 15 μL 5M NaCl and 
400 μL cold 96% ethanol is added, vortexed and spun down for 3 minutes at 10.000xG. The 
supernatant is removed by decanting and the tubes are dried in a heating block at 50oC. The gDNA is 
resuspended in 200 μL MQ and used for PCR. 
4.4 Restriction enzyme digestion 
For RE analysis of plasmids a 10 μL reaction mix with a final concentration of 1x RE specific buffer, 0.5-
1 U/μL RE and 100-300 ng DNA is incubated for 1 hour at optimal temperature for RE activity. Specific 
temperatures and buffers used are based on the specifications provided by the manufacturer (NEB, 
New England BioLabs). 
For the linearization of PacI USER vectors a 30 μL reaction mix with a final concentration of 1x 
CutSmart buffer, 1 U PacI and 3 μg plasmid is incubated at 37oC overnight. Subsequently 58 μL MQ, 
10 μL CutSmart buffer and 2 μL Nt.BbvCI (10 U/μL) is added to the reaction mix and incubated at 37oC 
for another 3 hours. The linearized fragments are separated through agarose gel electrophoresis and 
extracted. 
4.5 Agarose gel electrophoresis 
Separation and visualization of DNA is performed through agarose gel electrophoresis using a RunOne 
Electrophoresis system (EP-2100, EmbiTec). DNA is loaded with 1x Loading Dye on an agarose gel (1% 
agarose, 1x Tris-Borate-EDTA, 1x SYBR safe) and ran for 20 minutes at 100V. Diagnostic gels are 
visualized with a Gel Doc XR+ Gel Documentation System (1708195, Bio-Rad). Extraction gels are 
==PAGE:31==
27 
 
visualized with a Safe imager 2.0 Blue Light Transilluminator (S37102, Invitrogen) and DNA fragments 
are extracted using a Zymoclean Gel DNA Recovery Kit (D4002, Zymo research). 
4.6 USER cloning 
To assemble linear DNA fragments into a plasmid a 10-20 μL reaction mix containing 100 ng backbone 
and an amount of insert BioBlocks corresponding to approximately 1:5 backbone to insert ratio based 
on molecular weight, 1x CutSmart buffer (NEB) and 1 U USER enzyme (NEB) is incubated for 35 minutes 
at 37oC and 25 minutes at 25oC. For USER cloning with very small BioBlocks such as spacer fragments, 
a 1:1 backbone to insert ratio based on absolute weight is used. The reaction mix is cooled on ice for 
2 minutes before heat shock transforming into E. coli. As negative control a reaction without BioBlock 
inserts is performed. After cloning, regions containing PCR amplified genes or sgRNAs are Sanger 
sequenced (Mix2Seq, Eurofins Genomics) to ensure no mutations have occurred during the 
procedure. 
4.7 E. coli heat shock transformation 
Competent DH5α cells are thawed on ice. 50 μL cells are gently mixed with at least 100 μg plasmid 
and incubated on ice for another 15 minutes. For transformation after USER cloning 10 μL of the 
reaction mix is used. The cells are heat shock transformed in a heating block for 60 seconds at 42oC 
and placed on ice for another 10 minutes. The transformed cells are transferred to plates with 
selective LB medium and grown for 16 hours before performing colony PCR on isolated colonies to 
verify transformation. Successfully transformed colonies are transferred to liquid LB and grown for 
another 16 hours before plasmid extraction with a Zyppy Plasmid Miniprep Kit (D4020, Zymo 
research). As positive control a transformation with a previously constructed plasmid bearing the 
selection marker is performed. As negative control a transformation without DNA is performed. 
4.8 Protoplastation 
Two day old Aspergillus culture grown in 100 mL liquid MM is filtered through Mira cloth (Millipore, 
Merck), washed twice with Aspergillus protoplastation buffer (APB, 1.1 M MgSO4, 10 mM Na-
phosphate buffer, pH 5.8) and resuspended in 20 mL APB containing 40 mg/mL Glucanex (Novozymes 
corp.). The mycelium is digested for 2 to 3 hours at 30oC, 150 RPM and filtered through Mira cloth. 
APB is added to the flowthrough up to a total volume of 40 mL before carefully overlaying the 
suspension with 0.5x Aspergillus transformation buffer (ATB, 1.2 M Sorbitol, 50 mM CaCl2.2H2O, 20 
mM Tris, 0.6 M KCl, pH 7.2) and centrifugating the tube for 12 minutes at 3000xG (acceleration 9, 
deceleration 4), forming a halo containing the protoplasts. The protoplasts are collected, transferred 
to a new tube and ATB is added up to a total volume of 40 mL before spinning the protoplasts down 
at 3000xG for 12 minutes (maximum acceleration and deceleration). Supernatant is discarded, the 
protoplasts are resuspended in 1 mL ATB and used for either fungal transformation or stored at -20oC. 
4.9 Fungal transformation 
Fungal strains are transformed by gently mixing 50 μL protoplasts with 0.5 μg circular DNA or 0.8 pmol 
linear DNA and adding 150 μL PCT (50% w/v polyethylene glycol-8000, 50 mM CaCl2, 20 mM Tris, 0.6 
M KCl, pH 7.5). The tube is mixed through inversion and incubated at room temperature for 10 minutes 
before adding 250 μL ATB, mixing and transferring the mixture to plates with selective TM. After 
transformants start to form conidia they are transferred to selective MM plates. Transformants with 
genomic modifications are purified through spore dissection. As positive control a transformation with 
a selection marker bearing AMA-1 plasmid is performed. As negative control a transformation without 
CRISPR repair template or selection marker bearing plasmid is performed.

4.10 ER-stress tests 
Plates containing 25 mL liquid MM are inoculated with 1.25 * 105 spores per mL and grown at 30oC. 
After 40 hours of growth 20 mM DTT is added to the plates and stirred gently. After 2, 4 and 6 hours 
of incubation the fluorescence of the culture is assessed qualitatively under a cyan light filter (SFA-
LFS-CY, NightSEA).

6 Discussion 
Twelve CASCADE ISs were selected for both A. oryzae and A. niger, based on the available data on 
FungiDB. In this selection process assumptions are made about the accessibility of DNA in regions 
containing highly expressed genes, oversimplifying the mechanisms underlying gDNA accessibility. 
Integrating detailed transcriptomics data under specific environmental conditions into the selection 
process might yield better results with regards to predicting potential expression levels in noncoding 
regions. Furthermore, Assay for Transposase-Accessible Chromatin followed by sequencing (ATAC-
seq) is a technique that can be used to detect open chromatin [107]. ATAC-seq has recently been used 
to identify and compare transcription binding sites and chromatin accessibility in Aspergillus species, 
including A. oryzae and A. niger [108]. While this type of data is usually analysed and presented on a 
genome wide scale, which is not very useful for the task at hand, further tailored bioinformatic analysis 
of the raw data might be a good way to approach IS design.  
The number of CASCADE related Mad7 and IS GAC plasmids constructed in this study, 1 and 9 
respectively, highlights the discrepancy in ease of assembly of these plasmids and the main reason 
why no additional IS GAC plasmids were constructed is the difficulty of assembling the corresponding 
Mad7 plasmids. The Mad7 plasmid that was successfully assembled contains 8 sgRNAs, though it 
should be noted that the CRISPR array was constructed in a pDIV298 vector and amplified as a single 
BioBlock before insertion into the pAC2001 backbone. Attempts at assembling pAC2001 based vectors 
with less sgRNAs have all failed. Both of the other Mad7 plasmids constructed were pDIV298 based 
and required only one USER cloning attempt each, indicating that the used pAC2001 vector might be 
compromised, which is supported by the sequencing results of the incorrectly assembled Mad7 
plasmids (section 5.2). Further analysis is required to verify if this contamination is present in the 
plasmid stock or only in the aliquots used in this study. Additionally, the U3 cassette without sgRNA 
present indicates that the CRISPR-array was looped out through recombination of the homologous 
tRNA-gRNA sequences, a phenomena that has been previously reported during experiments with 
these vectors. Instead of identical tRNAs, using unique tRNA spacers to separate each sgRNA in the 
CRISPR array could alleviate this problem. However, loop-out does not account for the incorrect 
orientation of the selection marker, or rather absence of Mad7 and its regulatory elements (Fig. 9.A 
for reference), suggesting that there are other rearrangements occurring which might not be 
prevented by utilizing alternative tRNAs.  
After the CASCADE sites have been established, they should be validated by inserting a gene of choice 
into the GACs. This requires co-transformation with a DT and an vector expressing I-SceI. The design 
of these is relatively straightforward. An expression vector that with a selection marker other than 
pyrG, carrying I-SceI codon optimized for Aspergilli under control of a constitutive or inducible 
promoter should be sufficient . As for the DT, an obvious choice would be to use a fluorescent marker 
contrasting with the GAC encoded RFP for easy visual screening. The DT fragment should consist of 
HRDS-A, the marker under control of Aspergillus regulatory elements and HRDS-B.  
Interestingly, an increase in gene copy number does not necessarily lead to an increase in protein 
production, with strains containing lower copy numbers sometimes exhibiting significantly higher 
production rates than their high-copy number counterparts [109]. When validated, the CASCADE 
strains can also be used to investigate the relation between gene copy number, transcription levels 
and protein production by integrating the gene of a protein with industrial importance in the GACs 
and monitoring product-specific transcription levels during cultivation. This can be done by 
quantification of specific mRNA with real-time PCR on extracted total RNA [110].

The Aspergillus biosensor transformations could not be verified through diagnostic PCR, but it is likely 
that at least the A. oryzae transformants contain their respective expression vectors, as no A. oryzae 
growth could be observed on the negative control plate. For A. niger on the other hand, it is much less 
likely that the selected colonies are transformants due to the A. niger contamination present on all of 
the transformation plates. Nevertheless, it was not possible to show proof of concept for any of the 
biosensors in either of the two strains. It is unclear if this is caused by the cultures not containing the 
expression vector, a lack of biosensor expression from the vector, insufficient induction of ER-stress, 
DTT concentrations completely inhibiting folding of the biosensors or the biosensors simply not 
working as intended. The constructed expression vector was not designed in this study and contains 
a 42 bp region between the Ttef promoter and USER cloning site, consisting of a start codon and 9 
additional residues, ending with a TGA stop codon in position 10. The intended purpose of this region 
is not clear and in all likelihood interferes with transcription. Before further experiments are 
conducted with the vector it should be reconstructed, either removing the entire ORF or at least the 
stop codon and the plasmid should be validated experimentally. The concentration of DTT used in the 
ER-stress experiments, 20 mM, corresponds with the concentration used in other Aspergills studies 
and should be sufficient for induction of the UPR [68, 111, 112]. However, in the ER-stress experiments 
performed by (Fang et al., 2015) to validate their A. cerevisiae biosensor much lower concentrations 
of 0.1 and 0.5 mM DTT are used. It is possible that at 20 mM DTT proper folding of the biosensors is 
completely inhibited. Additional tests with different DTT concentrations might be required to 
determine appropriate conditions for the ER-stress tests. Finally, even if proof of concept of the 
biosensors is possible with an expression vector, expression from a plasmid is not ideal for monitoring 
physiological responses due to variations in copy number and distribution, complicating quantification 
and interpretation of results. Genomic integration of the biosensor in a dedicated reporter-strain and 
subsequent validation for quantification might be a better approach for investigations into the UPR 
and its dynamics. Finally, the point mutation in the mCitrine domain of UPRBS1 (Appx. V, Fig. 27) 
changing Gly25 to Asp likely has an effect on the foldability and perhaps function of the protein due 
to the change from a non-charged residue without side-chain to a moderately sized negatively charged 
side chain. While it is possible that this mutation does not affect the function of UPRSB1 as a UPR 
reporter with respect to HacA splicing induced mRFP expression, it should be possible to acquire an 
unmutated version of UPRBS1 by sequencing additional pDIV_ExpV_UPRBS1 plasmids isolated from 
different transformant colonies or by performing an additional round of cloning using the UPRBS2 
mCitrine BioBlock. 
The authors of the previously described S. cerevisiae hac1 biosensor use the fluorescence of the 3’ 
protein relative to the fluoresce of the 5’ protein as a measurement of UPR activity. However, at low, 
uninduced ER-stress levels this does not distinguish between leaky transcription and baseline Ire1p 
activity. In the UPR biosensors described here, UPRBS1 and UPRBS2, an unspliced transcript will be 
unable to produce the 3’ protein because its coding sequence is not in frame with the start codon, 
thus the relative fluorescence will give a more accurate representation of UPR signalling. However, 
this does mean that if leaky transcription occurs the translated protein might induce the UPR due to 
its randomized and potentially unfoldable protein sequence. If possible, these biosensors should be 
redesigned with alternate codons, such that the protein sequence of the 3’ mRFP is unaltered, while 
the unspliced transcript ends in an early stop codon.  
As for the HacA:RFP fusion protein, assuming that the fusion with RFP does not completely 
compromise the general structure of the protein, the DNA binding ability should not be impaired since 
the RFP domain is located on the C-terminal, leucine zipper-end of HacA, but perhaps its ability to 
promote transcription is. If this is the case, HacA:RFP could affect the dynamics of the UPR by 
==PAGE:43==
39 
 
competing for DNA binding sites with native HacA. To prevent this, the basic domain of HacA:RFP 
could be altered such that it is incapable of binding to its target sequence. 
In addition, it should be taken into account that none these biosensors behave identically to native 
hacA. They do not contain the 5’UTR and thus they are not subject to translation attenuation. This 
means that the signal of the biosensors will arise faster than hacA transcriptional regulation. Instead, 
these sensors report IreA activity rather than HacA activity. To monitor downstream signalling of hacA 
a different biosensor should be designed, for example a fluorescent protein under control of 
regulatory elements induced by HacA, as has been demonstrated in yeast [113]. Alternatively, a HacA 
reporter fusion protein which contains the 5’UTR and is subject to attenuation, but is incapable of 
binding DNA could be used to monitor the amount of active HacA present. 
7 Conclusion 
Ku70 was successfully knocked out in A. oryzae strain ORY2, making it compatible with the CASCADE 
system. Twelve CASCADE ISs were selected for both A. oryzae and A. niger. An Aspergillus compatible 
GAC cloning vector, sgRNA plasmid targeting 4 A. niger ISs as well as several IS specific GAC repair 
templates were constructed. However, no GAC integrations were performed due to a lack of matching 
sgRNA-repair template pairs. Three Aspergillus UPR biosensor expression vectors were successfully 
constructed and an attempt to transform these into A. oryzae and A. niger was made. Successful 
transformation with these vectors could not be verified and subsequent proof of concept experiments 
for the biosensors were inconclusive. 
Matching sgRNA plasmid-repair templates should be constructed before the Aspergillus cascade sites 
can be established and validated. The biosensor expression vectors should be reconstructed, validated 
and transformed into both A. oyzae and A. niger to subsequently perform ER-stress tests as proof of 
concept for the sensors. 
Once functional Aspergillus CASCADE strains and a UPR biosensor have been established, they can be 
used to engineer dedicated UPR research strains by integrating the biosensor in the genome of the 
CASCADE strains. Multiple copies of homologous or heterologous genes can be inserted into these in 
a single round of transformation to monitor the effect of copy number of a gene of interest on ER-
stress and UPR signalling. In turn, when a strain with high levels of ER-stress has been generated, a 
variety of experiments such as up- and downregulation of genes in the UPR or introduction of 
additional heterologous genes can be performed to screen for targets that relieve ER-stress and 
increase protein production and secretion.
