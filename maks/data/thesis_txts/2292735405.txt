Abstract  
 
Background: Enteroaggregative Escherichia coli (EAEC) is associated with travelersвҖҷ diarrhea and 
is a common cause of diarrhea in children under the age of five (Estrada-Garcia and Navarro-Garcia 
2012). In order to survive, multiply and disseminate within the intestine of mammalian hosts, 
pathogens such as EAEC must execute a series of complex functions. Virulence determinants are 
either secreted to the bacterial cell surface or released into the external environment (Henderson and 
Nataro 2001). In this study, we investigated the characteristic of EAEC isolates collected from 
Danish children with travelerвҖҷs diarrhea in order to further clarify the pathogenesis of EAEC.  
Study design: 79 stool samples were selected from 66 children, who had traveled to different 
destinations with diarrhea and EAEC. A questionnaire was answered with regards to travelersвҖҷ 
destinations, symptoms reported from the children by their parents/guardians and type of diarrhea. 
A Multiplex polymerase chain reaction (PCR) was performed targeting the Serine Protease 
Autotransporter of Enterobacteriaceae (SPATEs), the astA gene, and the phylogenetic genes.      
Results: The most visited countries were Egypt, Turkey, Pakistan, and India (57.4 %), which are 
known to be among the high-risk areas of travelersвҖҷ diarrhea (Dr. Stuart Rose 2015). The age 
distribution of the children showed that most of the children were under the age of three. In our 
study 65 % of the EAEC strains were positive for one or more SPATEs or astA genes, where the 
Class II SPATEs were the most commonly detected genes. Furthermore, among the SPATEs and 
astA genes in this study, pic is the most common with 39.4 %, followed by astA with 33.3 %, 
whereas, the least detected genes were pet in 9.1 % and sigA in 6.1 % of EAEC strains. By 
phylogeny grouping, 38.7 % of the EAEC strains belonged to the phylogenetic group A, 27.4 % to 
B1, 12.9 % to B2, and 21 % to D.   
Conclusion: This study concludes that 65 % of the investigated EAEC strains tested positive for one 
or several SPATE genes or the astA gene with the Class II SPATEs as the most common genes. Pic 
was the most common SPATE gene among EAEC strains and was detected in 39.4 % of the strains. 
The second most commonly detected gene was astA (33.3 % of the strains) followed by the sepA 
gene (24.2 % of the strains). The least frequently detected SPATE genes in this study were sigA 
(6.1% of the strains) followed by pet (9.1 % of the strains). Out of the 79 strains, 38.7 % belonged 
to the phylogenetic group A, 27.4 % to group B1, 12.9 % to group B2, and 21 % to group D. Based 
on these results, most EAEC strains belonged to the phylogenetic group A. The study showed that

Aim  
 
Enteroaggregative Escherichia coli is a common bacterial cause of diarrhea among travelers 
worldwide, especially in children (Weintraub 2007). The pathogenesis of EAEC is complex and the 
strains are very heterogeneous (Estrada-Garcia and Navarro-Garcia 2012). The cytotoxic effects of 
EAEC involve the release of toxins, including the Serine Protease Autotransporters of the 
Enterobacteriaceae (SPATEs) (Hebbelstrup Jensen, Olsen et al. 2014). To fully understand the 
pathogenesis of EAEC, a characterization of the EAEC genes is needed.    
The aim of this study was to characterize EAEC isolates by multiplex PCR in EAEC strains 
collected from Danish children with diarrhea, who had travelled to various destinations. In this 
study we investigated: 
- 
SPATEs gene distribution among EAEC strains 
- 
Phylogenetic distribution among EAEC strains 
- 
Correlation between phylogenetic groups and the destination of the traveler among EAEC 
strains

Introduction  
Escherichia coli 
Escherichia coli (E. coli) is part of the normal flora of the human intestinal tract. In addition to 
these commensal variants, several pathogenic E. coli strains have been identified (Kohler and 
Dobrindt 2011). E. coli is a Gram-negative, rod-shaped and non-spore forming bacterium. The 
colonies are either singles or in pairs. E. coli is facultative anaerobe with an optimum growth 
temperature at 37 В°C and requires carbohydrate for the fermentation process. E. coli grows on 
simple culture media with smooth or rough edged circular colonies and without any pigment (Chart 
2012). In general, E. coli colonize the gastrointestinal tract in the human due to fecal-oral spread 
with food or contaminated water. The infection rate depends on personal hygiene and the 
environmental temperature (Sussman 1997). The mucous surface of the intestinal tract has a 
clearance mechanism via the brush border membrane, that removes particles including bacteria 
(Drumm, Roberton et al. 1988). E. coli has specific adhesion mechanisms, which helps to overcome 
the clearance mechanisms (Sussman 1997). Some E. coli strains have acquired virulence factors, 
which enables them to cause disease in healthy individuals. Many virulence factors of E. coli are 
known and even more information about the virulence factors are being uncovered (Sussman 1997). 
In any given infection with an E. coli strain, there will always be more than one virulence factor, 
which makes it difficult to fully understand the mechanisms behind the pathogenicity (Sussman 
1997). The E. coli strains causing intestinal diseases in humans have loosely been  categorized into 
six major pathotypes; enteropathogenic E. coli (EPEC), enterotoxigenic E. coli (ETEC), 
enteroinvasive E. coli (EIEC), enterohemorrhagic E. coli (EHEC), diffusely adhering E. coli 
(DAEC), and enteroaggregative E. coli (EAEC) (Estrada-Garcia and Navarro-Garcia 2012). The 
focus of this study will be on EAEC, which is closely described in the next section.      
Enteroaggregative E.coli (EAEC)  
Diarrheagenic E. coli is a major health problem and is responsible for high morbidity and mortality 
caused by diarrhea in children under the age of 5 years in developing countries (Estrada-Garcia and 
Navarro-Garcia 2012). However, EAEC is an important cause of diarrhea in all age groups in 
developing as well as in industrialized countries (Boisen, Ruiz-Perez et al. 2009). EAEC was first 
detected in 1987 in the stool of Chilean children with diarrhea, where Nataro et al. examined the 
adherence pattern of these bacteria with HEp-2 cell cultures and observed a characteristic вҖңstacked-
brickвҖқ pattern (Nataro, Kaper et al. 1987). This aggregative phenotypic characteristic of EAEC is

Pathogenesis and virulence factors of EAEC 
EAEC has a complex pathogenesis. According to numerous studies, the pathogenesis of EAEC can 
be described in three steps; 1) abundant adherence to the intestinal mucosa 2) biofilm formation 3) 
induction of mucosal inflammation and release of toxins (Hebbelstrup Jensen, Olsen et al. 2014) 
(Estrada-Garcia and Navarro-Garcia 2012) (figure 2). Bacterial fimbriae facilitate adhesion to the 
intestinal epithelium and the fimbriae are divided into four major groups; aggregative adherence 
fimbriae I-IV (AAF/I to AAF/IV) (Hebbelstrup Jensen, Olsen et al. 2014). In the intestines, 
fimbriae adhere by binding to several components of the extracellular matrix (e.g. laminin, collage 
IV and fibronectin) (Hebbelstrup Jensen, Olsen et al. 2014). EAEC increases secretion of mucus, 
which leads to the trapping of bacteria in a bacterium-mucus biofilm. Biofilm is situated mainly in 
colon and is an important pathogenicity trait of EAEC (Hebbelstrup Jensen, Olsen et al. 2014). This 
biofilm can prevent the transport of antibacterial factors such as antibiotics and it can evade the 
local immune system (Hebbelstrup Jensen, Olsen et al. 2014). These abilities are related to the 
bacteriaвҖҷs diarrheagenicity and can cause persistent diarrhea (lasting more than 14 days)

Serine Protease Autotransporters of Enterobacteriaceae вҖ“ SPATEs and EAST-1  
In order to survive, multiply and disseminate within the intestine of mammalian hosts, pathogens 
must execute a series of complex functions. Virulence determinants are either secreted to the 
bacterial cell surface or released into the external environment (Henderson and Nataro 2001). Due 
to Gram-negative bacteriaвҖҷs double membrane, these bacteria have developed systems to secrete 
proteins through their outer membrane. Autotransporter pathway (AT) is one of the protein 
secretion pathways, which include the Serine Protease ATs of the Enterobacteriaceae (SPATEs) 
(Henderson and Nataro 2001). SPATEs are proteases, as their name specifies, since they all contain 
a conserved serine-protease motif (GDSGS where S is the catalytic serine) (Dautin 2010). The 
SPATEs family is phylogenetically divided into two classes based on amino acid sequence of the 
passenger domain (Ruiz-Perez, Wahid et al. 2011). Class I SPATEs are cytopathic toxins that cause 
cell rounding in vitro and induce mucosal damage on intestinal explants (Ruiz-Perez, Wahid et al. 
2011). Although the action of class I SPATEs is not fully understood, it has been shown that these 
toxins enter eukaryotic cells and cleave cytoskeletal proteins such as fodrin in epithelial cells (HEp-
2 cells) (Canizalez-Roman and Navarro-Garcia 2003). Class II has a more diverse phenotype and is 
non-cytotoxic (Boisen, Ruiz-Perez et al. 2009). A study has shown that the role of Class II protein 
is to induce hypersecretion of mucus, cleave mucin and to increase the number of mucin-producing 
goblet cells (Navarro-Garcia, Gutierrez-Jimenez et al. 2010). Since a variety of leukocyte surface 
glycoproteins are structurally similar to those found on human mucin glycoproteins, Ruiz-Perez et

Signal peptide targets the protein from the cytoplasm and exports it through the inner membrane of 
the bacteria. A signal peptidase at the inner membrane cleaves the signal sequence, and the 
remaining part of the protein is released into the periplasm. Once the Гҹ-domain is located at the 
periplasm, it undergoes conformational changes into a Гҹ-barrel shape and is inserted in the outer 
membrane forming a pore. The passenger domain, which is the functional part of the protein and is 
responsible for the proteinвҖҷs virulence activity, is translocated through the pore to the bacterial cell 
surface, where it may undergo processing. The processed protein may be released from the cell or 
remain associated with the bacterial cell surface (Henderson and Nataro 2001), (Dautin 2010).

(Ruiz-Perez, Wahid et al. 2011). This study has focused on five types of SPATEs genes and one 
non-SPATE gene (astA). The investigated SPATEs are the following; pet (plasmid-encoded toxin,

Pet 
вҖңThe plasmid-encoded toxinвҖқ (or pet) is described as a 108-kDa protein with 302 base pairs 
encoded in a virulence plasmid (Henderson, Navarro-Garcia et al. 2004), (Boisen, Scheutz et al. 
2012). A study by Henderson et al. from 1999 examined the association of pet with the mucosal 
damage. They showed that the mucosal abnormalities were induced by wild-type EAEC strains and 
not the pet mutant with an inactivated gene. The mucosal abnormalities included dilation of the 
crypt openings, extrusion of colonic cells and loss of apical mucus from goblet cells (Henderson, 
Hicks et al. 1999). HEp-2 cells treated with pet exhibited some morphological changes, 
characterized by the release of their focal contacts followed by cytoplasm contraction, complete cell 
rounding and detachment (Dutta, Cappello et al. 2002). The same study showed spectrin to be a 
substrate for pet, due to the fact that pet cleaves this protein, which is a cytoskeleton component in 
cells (Dutta, Cappello et al. 2002).

displays cytopathic activity on several cell lines including HEp-2 cell lines (Guyer, Henderson et al. 
2000). These cells show morphological changes; cell elongation and detachment of cellular 
junctions from the monolayer (Guyer, Henderson et al. 2000). Guyer et al. have shown that the sat

SigA  
The sigA gene was identified in Shigella flexneri 2a and it is located on a pathogenicity island (PAI) 
termed shePAI (Al-Hasani, Henderson et al. 2000). The sigA gene is situated 3.6 bp downstream 
from another SPATE gene called pic on the shePAI. SigA gene encodes a 139.6-kDa protein, 
whereas the secreted form is only 103-kDa, indicating a proteolytical processing of the protein (Al-
Hasani, Henderson et al. 2000). Al-Hasani et al. have shown that high levels of sigA were 
detectable at 37 ВәC but not at 21 ВәC, indicating a temperature regulated expression of the sigA gene 
(Al-Hasani, Henderson et al. 2000). SigA seems to share a number of similar functions with its 
closest homolog pet, and Al-Hassani et al. showed its cytopathic activity towards HEp-2 cells with 
the release of cellular focal adhesion contacts, rounding of cells, and detachment (Al-Hasani, 
Henderson et al. 2000). 
Pic  
вҖңProtease involved in intestinal colonizationвҖқ (or pic) is 116-kDa protein secreted from EAEC and 
its alleles are found in S. flexneri 2a and UPEC (Harrington, Sheikh et al. 2009). Pic is encoded on 
the same pathogenicity island as sigA (Henderson, Navarro-Garcia et al. 2004). The expression of 
pic in EAEC is temperature dependent (Henderson, Czeczulin et al. 1999). Harrington et al. have 
investigated the role of picвҖҷs effect on colonization. A wild type EAEC and a pic mutant (lacking 
the pic catalytic serine) were used for the experiments. They used a streptomycin-treated mouse 
model and a standard growth media. After 6-8 hours, the growth of the mutant slowed considerably, 
on the streptomycin-treated mice, whereas the wild type strain had a rapid growth for at least 48 
hours. These findings support the hypothesis of pic promoting intestinal colonization. Furthermore, 
they showed that there was no difference in growth on standard media until addition of mucin, 
where the wild type demonstrated more robust growth. These findings have suggested picвҖҷs role in 
the use of mucin as substrate, when other preferred substrates (e.g. glucose) are limited. These data 
strongly support the other hypothesis, that the contribution of pic to the intestinal colonization of 
EAEC is nutritional (Harrington, Sheikh et al. 2009). Navarro-Garcia et al. did experiments where 
both wild-type EAEC strain and a pic mutant strain, were inoculated into the lumina of intestinal 
loops of rats. After 12 hours incubation, the pic mutant showed a decreased amount of secreted

SepA 
вҖңShigella extracellular proteinвҖқ (or sepA) is a 110-kDa protein localized on a virulence plasmid and 
a major extracellular protein of S. flexneri (Benjelloun-Touimi, Sansonetti et al. 1995), but it was 
also found broadly distributed in EAEC clinical isolates (Boisen, Ruiz-Perez et al. 2009). Indeed, a 
study from 2008 of 121 EAEC strains isolated of moderate to severe acute diarrhea among children 
0-59 months of age in Mali, suggested that sepA was most strongly associated with diarrhea in this 
group (Boisen, Scheutz et al. 2012). Benjelloun-Touimi et al. constructed a sepA mutant with 
attenuated virulence properties of the wild-type strain in a rabbit model of legated ileal loop. The 
mutant sepA was characterized by reduced fluid accumulation, reduced mucosal atrophy, and 
decreased tissue inflammation. These findings suggested that sepA might play a role in tissue 
invasion (Benjelloun-Touimi, Sansonetti et al. 1995).  
EAST-1 
вҖңEnteroaggregative E. coli heat-stable toxin 1вҖқ (or EAST-1) is a non-SPATE toxin with a molecular 
weight at 4.1-kDa (Yi-Wei Tang 2015 ). The open reading frame (ORF) of this toxin is called astA 
(EAEC heat-stable enterotoxin) and is localized on a 60-MDa plasmid (Menard and Dubreuil 2002). 
EAST-1 toxin has been proposed to be involved in the pathogenesis of diarrheagenic EAEC strains 
(Menard and Dubreuil 2002). However, some EAST-1 producing EAEC strains (astA+EAEC) do 
not develop diarrhea, which was confirmed in a study from 1995, where up to 38 % of adults and 
children without diarrhea had astA+EAEC (Savarino, McVeigh et al. 1996). This can suggest that 
some persons can be asymptomatic carriers and the astA+EAEC strains could provoke diarrhea 
under certain circumstances (Savarino, McVeigh et al. 1996). Savarino et al. have shown in a study 
from 1991 that EAST-1 is partial heat stable, when a fraction of culture filtrates from EAEC strains 
containing EAST-1 was heated at 65В°C for 15 minutes the fraction maintained 63 % of total activity

Phylogeny of EAEC 
Phylogenetic analysis of E. coli strains can be classified into four major groups; A, B1, B2 and D 
(Doumith, Day et al. 2012). The virulent extra-intestinal strains mainly belong to group B2, and to a 
lesser extend group D. Most commensal strains belong to group A and B1 (Doumith, Day et al. 
2012). Certain genes or DNA fragments can be used as specific phylogenetic group markers; chuA, 
yjaA, TspE4.C2, and gadA. The chuA gene is present in all groups B2 and D and absent in all 
groups B1 and A. In order to discriminate between groups B2 and D, yjaA gene is used. This gene is 
present in group B2. TSPE4.C2 is present in group B1 and absent in group A. gadA is used for 
internal amplification control to allow greater confidence when assigning isolates to phylogenetic 
group A based in the absence of any of the above three mentioned markers  (Doumith, Day et al. 
2012) (figure 5).

The EAEC isolates in this study were collected from stool samples from Danish children with

culturing of the stool samples the EAEC isolates were stored at -80 В°C and DNA had been extracted

Discussion 
Travel destination and age  
EAEC is a frequent cause of diarrhea that has received increasing attention during the past decade; 
among others as a cause of travelersвҖҷ diarrhea. This study focused on Danish children with diarrhea, 
who had travelled to various countries. The results from this research have shown that 57.4 % of all 
the patients in this research traveled to Egypt, Turkey, Pakistan, and India, which are the most 
visited countries. These results are consistent with вҖң2015 International Travel Health GuideвҖқ (Dr. 
Stuart Rose 2015) which places these countries as high-risk travel destinations for travelersвҖҷ 
diarrhea.   
The age distribution of the children shows that most of the children are under the age of three. The 
most common in this group were children at the age of one. This higher incidence of travelersвҖҷ 
diarrhea in these young children can have several explanations such as lack of hygienic awareness. 
Children place their fingers and other objects in their mouth, swallow water while swimming or 
bathing, and do not wash their hands very often. Other reasons can be lack of immunity to 
diarrheagenic bacteria.  
SPATEs and astA  
Out of the 79 EAEC strains examined in this study, 65 % of the strains tested positive for one or 
more toxigenic genes. Whereas, 35 % of the strains tested negative for any of the examined genes. 
13.6 % of the isolates were only positive for Class I SPATE genes, where 33.3 % only had Class II 
SPATE and 19.7 % of the 79 strains (Maarlev 2013) tested positive for both Class I and Class II 
SPATE genes. A study in 2009 of 55 EAEC strains showed that 94.5 % harbored SPATE genes 
including members of Class I and/or II, which is much higher than the results found in this paper 
(Boisen, Ruiz-Perez et al. 2009). The same study showed that 18.2 % of the strains had only a Class 
I SPATE, 9.1 % of the strains had only a Class II and 67.3 % were positive for both a Class I and 
Class II SPATE (Boisen, Ruiz-Perez et al. 2009). Interestingly, the most common SPATE among 
the EAEC strains in the current study was from Class II, while the study from 2009 had the opposite 
results. As it was mentioned earlier in the introduction section, Class I is cytotoxic to several 
epithelial cell lines and others have shown to cleave spectrin (cytoskeletal protein) in vitro, where 
Class II SPATE has a more diverse phenotype and is non- cytotoxic.  
==PAGE:29==
Characteristics of Enteroaggregative Escherichia coli isolated from Danish children suffering from travelersВҙ diarrhea  
 
 
29 
Liza Rasul, s093863, 7. august 2015 
Interestingly, pic (39.4 % of the strains) was the most common SPATE gene among the EAEC 
strains in our study. The exact mechanism of action of pic is unknown, but as described in the 
introduction, a research from 2009 has shown that it plays a role in the early stages of the 
pathogenesis by colonization to the mucosa (Harrington, Sheikh et al. 2009). One would expect pic 
to be found in all strains due to its role in the initiation of the diseased state, however in our study 
only 39.4 % harbored pic. Other studies on EAEC strains from patients with diarrhea reported a 
prevalence between 39 % to 63.6 % (Maarlev 2013 ), (Boisen, Ruiz-Perez et al. 2009). As pic is not 
present in all isolates, it indicates that other genes than pic might also participate in the colonization 
process.  
33.3 % of the investigated EAEC strains harbored the astA gene, which was the second most 
prevalently detected gene. It has been reported, that the prevalence of the astA gene in EAEC strains 
varies from 26 % to 86.6 % of the strains isolated from stools from patients suffering from diarrhea 
(Menard and Dubreuil 2002). The same study reported that astA was more frequently detected in 
EAEC strains collected from children than adults, but our findings cannot confirm or reject this 
statement due to the fact that no EAEC strains from adults were investigated in this study (Menard 
and Dubreuil 2002). However, a study from 2012, pointed out that the isolation rate of the astA 
gene in adults was higher than in children (Konno, Yatsuyanagi et al. 2012). The finding results in 
our study, which shows that not all isolates harbor astA, support Kenno et al. who reported that 
additional virulence factors other than EAST-1 is required to mediate diarrhea (Konno, Yatsuyanagi 
et al. 2012).     
SepA was found in 24.2 % of the strains as the third most prevalent toxigenic gene in this study. 
Boisen et al. found that 38.2 % of EAEC isolates from patients with diarrhea harbored the sepA 
gene (Boisen, Ruiz-Perez et al. 2009). In addition, this result was consistent with a study made by a 
student at SSI, who investigated isolates from Danish children with diarrhea, and showed sepA as 
the third highest (11 %) (Maarlev 2013 ). The exact mode of action of sepA has not yet been 
described, but our findings show, that sepA is not the sole mediator of diarrhea.  
The fundamental mode of action of sat is unknown but it is believed that sat is homologue to pet, 
and has cytotoxic activity on Hep-2 cell lines (Guyer, Henderson et al. 2000). In our study, 21.2 % 
of the tested isolates harbored sat. A study reported sat in much higher rate at 74.5 % (Boisen, 
Ruiz-Perez et al. 2009), while a student at SSI found sat in 38 % of tested EAEC isolates from 
children with travelersвҖҷ diarrhea (Maarlev 2013 ). A report from 2000 showed that 52 % of the

amino acids in the passenger domains of sat and pet are identical, meaning that these may be allelic 
toxins fulfilling the same roles in pathogenesis (Guyer, Henderson et al. 2000). Our results showed 
that generally the isolates harbored either pet or sat (except in one isolate) indicating that these 
toxins may allelically exclude each other.       
The least represented gene detected among the EAEC strains was the sigA gene (6.1 % of strains), 
which is a Class I SPATE and can have proteolytic activity, possible enterotoxic activity and 
cytopathic effects (Al-Hasani, Henderson et al. 2000). The same low prevalence of sigA was 
reported by Boisen et al. (7.3 % of strains) (Boisen, Ruiz-Perez et al. 2009) and the earlier study by 
a student at SSI (0 %) (Maarlev 2013 ). As mentioned in introduction section both pic and sigA are 
situated on shePAI, and one would believe that these two genes would be found with the same 
frequency. Surprisingly, the results show no such correlation, with 39.4 % of the strains containing 
pic and only 6.1 % the sigA gene. Out of these strains only one strain contained both genes at the 
same time. It is worth mentioning that shePAI, like several other PAIs, is unstable and 
spontaneously deleted at a frequency of 10-5 to 10-6 per cell per generation (Al-Hasani, Henderson 
et al. 2000).  
The Multiplex PCR results in this study generally pointed in the direction that no single virulence 
gene is likely to be the sole mediator of travelersвҖҷ diarrhea in children. It is important to remember 
that this study has only investigated the presence of the genes and not the expression of them. It is a 
possibility that the genes are present, but defected by mutations or simply not expressed.  
Distribution of the phylogenetic groups  
As mentioned in the introduction section E. coli is divided in four major phylogenetic groups; A, 
B1, B2 and D. The results from the phylogenetic grouping show that diarrhea-associated E. coli 
strains are distributed among all four groups. Out of the 79 pathogenic strains, 38.7 % belonged to 
phylogenetic group A, 27.4 % to group B1, 12.9 % to group B2, and 21 % to group D. Based on 
these results, group A was the most common phylogenetic group. The obtained results here were 
compared to a study performed by Christian Perez et al. who investigated the isolates from 
hospitalized children less than 5 years old with diarrhea in Costa Rica; (A=50 %, B1+B2=25 % and 
D=0 %) (Perez, Gomez-Duarte et al. 2010). This mentioned study showed that the majority of the 
isolates also belonged to group A. It is important to mention that the total isolate number in this 
study was only 8 and their focus was pathogenic E. coli strains in general, and not only EAEC

which our study investigated. To overcome this issue, another study, done by student at SSI, was 
used for comparison. This study was based on isolates from Danish children suffering from 
travelersвҖҷ diarrhea. The results from this research has shown to be as following; A=34.3 %, 
B1=33.3 %, B2=7.2 %, and D=23.7 % with a total isolate number of 97 (Kuhr 2013). There are 
similarities between our results, with group A as the most common, followed by B1, D and B2. As 
most commensal strains belong to groups A and B1, our results indicate that EAEC might be 
similar to commensal strains of E. coli, and therefore inhabit the intestines affecting the 
transmission rate between humans.  
Since some of the patients had traveled to high risk areas it would be interesting to see if there was 
any association between phylogenetic groups and the destinations. To do so, phylogenetic group 
results from high risk areas were compared with phylogenetic groups from low risk areas. In view 
of the fact that p-values in general for all groups were above 0.05, there was no significant 
association between phylogenetic groups and the travel destinations.   
Type of Diarrhea  
The results of diarrhea type showed that 71.2 % of patients had watery diarrhea, 53 % mucoid and a 
small portion had bloody diarrhea (4.5 %). As mentioned in the introduction section, EAEC can 
cause watery and mucoid diarrhea. A hospital-based case-control study from India done on 254 
children aged < 5 years with acute diarrhea, reported watery diarrhea as more frequent than mucoid 
diarrhea among EAEC positive children (watery=72 % Mucoid=28 %) (Dutta, Pal et al. 1999). Our 
results of watery diarrhea are in agreement with the reported results. Dutta et al. did not report any 
bloody diarrhea, while in our study three patients had bloody diarrhea (4.5 %). Compared to the 
mentioned report from 1999, the mucoid diarrhea was more common in our study. This can be due 
to the fact that our results on type of diarrhea were based on telephonic interview with the parents, 
and it might have been difficult for them to distinguish between watery and mucoid diarrhea. 
However, another study reported a prevalence of mucoid diarrhea with the range of 4.1 вҖ“ 55.2 % 
(Huang, Mohanty et al. 2006). The results in this paper is supported by the study of another student 
at SSI (watery=82 %, mucoid=68 %, and bloody=4 %) (Kuhr 2013). In our study, the gene 
distributions in watery and mucoid diarrhea were nearly identical, and no significant association between the 
diarrhea type and occurrence of the toxigenic genes were found, p-value > 0.05.

Conclusion and future perspective   
EAEC is an important cause of diarrhea in all age groups in developing as well as in industrialized 
countries. A large number of virulence factors and combinations have been associated with clinical 
illness, where their genes are under massive investigation. This paper focus on characterization of 
EAEC induced diarrhea in children, who travelled to several destinations. The most frequently 
visited countries were Egypt, Turkey, Pakistan, and India (57.4 %), and these countries are among 
the known high-risk area for travelersвҖҷ diarrhea. out of the 79 strains tested in this research, we 
conclude that 65 % are positive for one or more SPATE or astA genes, where the Class II SPATEs 
were the most common ones. Among the toxigenic genes, pic was the most commonly detected 
gene (39.4 %), followed by astA (33.3 %), whereas the least common genes were pet (9.1 %) and 
sigA (6.1 %). In addition to the gene groupings, phylogeny grouping was also performed. 38.7 % of 
the EAEC strains belonged to phylogenetic group A, 27.4 % to group B1, 12.9 % to group B2, and 
21 % to group D. The phylogenetic group results from high risk areas were compared with 
phylogenetic groups from low risk areas. Since P-values were above 0.05 in all groups, there was 
no significant association between phylogenetic groups and the travel destinations. When 
comparing diarrhea types between the patients, watery diarrhea showed to be the most common 
type affecting 71.2 % of patients. The gene distribution in watery and mucoid diarrhea was nearly 
identical, and no significant association between the diarrhea type and occurrence of the SPATEs or 
astA genes was found.   
We have managed to characterize EAEC diarrhea in children who had travelled to several countries, 
but more research is needed to clarify the findings. Some future investigations could be examination 
of more isolates, including a control group for support and comparison of the findings. Because this 
study is based solely on the presence of the genes, a future investigation could be examine the 
expression of these genes. A correlation between the actual toxins and pathogenicity of the bacteria 
could be investigated. Many studies, including our,  has shown the heterogenecity of pathogenic 
EAEC, therefore further investigations regarding several other virulence genes is needed to better 
understand the pathogenicity of this bacteria.
