Abstract
Currently, the everyday evolving acoustic metamaterials research area develops a wide variety of
structures with plenty of novel properties and applications. Part of the researchers’ focus is on
achieving a metamaterial that attenuates sound and at the same time provides airflow. The present
project aims to reproduce one of the latest developed acoustic metamaterials for sound attenua­
tion with sufficient ventilation. The analytical model: a transverse bilayer medium described with
Green’s functions, is developed in MATLAB with two different approaches (analytical and numer­
ical approximations), described in detail for easy implementation. The models for calculations and
simulations do not consider losses or vibroacoustic phenomena, simplifying the calculations. After
comparing and confirming results with the reference paper, the reference structure is simulated in
COMSOL. Next, a parameter study is performed, and used to implement a tailoring tool which
that calculates the parameter values and tailors them to the requirements for sound attenuation and
airflow for a specific application, in this case, a beam projector. The tailored structure is simulated
in COMSOL and compared with the reference model. Results show a highly tunable structure,
thanks to the models developed in MATLAB. A geometry with parameters easy to manipulate,
and with interesting results in the parameter study, as well as positive preliminary results when
stacking two structures with different resonance frequencies. Results show that their resonance
frequencies are maintained and the results are simply combined. The Fano­like interference phe­
nomenon is confirmed in the MATLAB models and all the simulated structures. Tailored structure
includes a 60% open area and a high sound attenuation for selected frequencies. However, geo­
metrical assumptions have yet to be improved for accuracy in the target resonance frequencies of
the structure. The final models and parameter studies form an acoustic metamaterial calculation
easy to tailor for a variety of applications.

Introduction and Literature Review
Metamaterials are the result of arranging common materials in specific structures so the final prod­
uct demonstrates unnatural properties that the materials themselves would not show in any other
arrangement. More formally, the definition of metamaterial was first introduced in 2001 by R.
Walser as ”macroscopic composites having a man­made, three­dimensional, periodic cellular ar­
chitecture designed to produce an optimized combination, not available in nature, of two or more
responses to specific excitation” [1].
The first experimental approach to a metamaterial was in 1999 by Pendry et al. based on their
previous research from 1996 [2]. This experiment included copper split­ring resonators which
demonstrated a negative permeability. The electromagnetic phenomenons that were being investi­
gated after this turning point inspired an idea of having similar behaviors in the optics and acoustics,
considering the negative permeability and permittivity as characteristics that could have an analogy
in these other fields.
Regarding optics, the research for photonic crystals that display these negative characteristics has
taken place since 1986 [3] and evolved since. Parallel to these, in acoustics, the phononic crys­
tals started to be investigated since 1993 [4], where a specific periodic structure where the waves
interact creates passbands and band gaps that can be tuned with simple geometrical parameters
of the lattice, and that indicate a change in the effective mass density and elastic modulus of the
waves. These two effective parameters do not depend on the intrinsic characteristics of the ma­
terial, but on the structure created with the material. So the behavior of the final structure will
indicate that mathematically, one of these terms, or both in certain cases, is negative. For a quick
understanding of how this parameters affect the behavior of the material, Kumar et al. [5] explains
that considering a plane wave and homogeneous medium, the wavenumber vector can be defined
as k = |n|ω
c where ω is the angular frequency, c the speed of sound in the medium, and |n| rep­
resents a refractive index, which in an analogy with the optic metamaterials, can be manipulated
to change the behavior of the waves when interacting with the metamaterial. This refractive index
can be defined by |n| =
√
ρ
E , with ρ is the effective mass density and E the bulk modulus. If
any of these last two values is negative, the refractive index and therefore the wavenumber vector
becomes imaginary, resulting in evanescent waves and not propagating. The propagation would
then occur when either both variables ρ and E are positive or negative. Later, efforts in displaying
these interesting properties included using different geometries, shapes, and materials in various
combinations, leading to a great amount of types of metamaterials, techniques and results as of
today.
In 2000, Liu et al. [6] developed the first acoustic metamaterial (AM) that would attenuate sound
waves. This was achieved by arranging spheres with a small size (mm or cm) coated with polymer,
which would resonate in a specific frequency range, thus breaking the mass law, showing that it is
possible to attenuate lower frequencies with a material much smaller than the targeted wavelength,
and opening the door for a lot of research in the new field.
Several reviews [5] [7] [8] [9] [10] [11] have extensively covered the progress in the last 20 years
in the AM field. This new sector has given way to various techniques, configurations, structures,
and ideas for a notably wide diversity of AM. On the other hand, it also has allowed that similar
structures take different names or are grouped under different categories, thus the classification of
all the current research is complex to follow.
One of the ways to classify the different metamaterials that currently exist is through their structure.

E.g., there are Helmholtz resonators, which make use of the Helmholtz resonator (a neck and an
enclosed volume) and take it to a mm or cm scale; or membrane­type AMs, which usually are
membranes that hold in the center a sphere of another material, forming a spring­mass system.
Fok et al. [7] divide the AMs into intrinsic and inertial, ”depending on wether the size of the
resonating elements is completely decoupled from the wavelength”. Considering the phase speed
of the materials that conform the metamaterial. E.g. low phase speed for intrinsic AMs such as
rubber, inclusions that conform a mass­spring­damper oscillator in each unit cell for inertial AMs.
AMs can also be distinguished by their working mechanisms, like Farzad et al. described in 2019
[12]. Considering if the AMs are passive or active, their negative parameters (negative mass den­
sity, negative bulk modulus, or both), and the working mechanism, e.g. resonant materials vs non
resonant (like the material presented from Liu [6] vs a space coiled material ”labyrinth AMs” or
”holey” AMs). Another way to classify them is through the application that is targeted. From steer­
ing waves, super­resolution imaging, focusing sound, attenuating sound, filtering, ventilation, and
more.
Recently, Zhang [11] developed a thorough classification of AM for waterborne or airborne sound
absorption, including membrane­type, cavity­based, gradient­index and AMs with solid scatterers.
In the same spirit, Kumar [5] focuses his research in AMs that aim to attenuate sound and simulta­
neously let airflow through the structure. Divided mostly through their applications, Kumar covers
acoustic facade systems and how their introduction in architecture since the early 20th century has
evolved up to plenum windows with sonic crystals additions that may offer a good sound insula­
tion for traffic noise and airflow for windows in buildings. Second in Kumar’s list are Helmholtz
resonators which have been around circa 1910, and now are the centerpiece of window panels to
let airflow and attenuate sound as much as 35 dB in a broad frequency range (400­5000 Hz), or
are inserted into small geometries to cover a more specific frequency range. The shapes and com­
binations are endless as well as the applications. An example of the use of resonators can be seen
in Kim et al. [13], where in­duct noise is reduced by replacing sections of a tube with MPPs, and
using backing resonators around the duct so as to not reduce the area in it (fig. 1.1). This configu­
ration results in low transmittance in a wide frequency range from low to mid frequencies, without
obstructing the airflow in said duct.
Figure 1.1: In­duct AM with MPP from [13]
Wang’s et al. [14] research presents an ultra­thin and durable open AM (fig. 1.2) where, while
focusing on durable materials and a steady structure that certain applications might prioritize, the
open area (OA) in the material is not as big as in other AMs. This material includes a rigid frame
covered with two thin plates and orifices in the plates that match the frame. The interference
between the wavefields at the orifice and plate regions are what causes a high transmission loss
(TL) at low frequencies (4̃30 Hz).

2
Methodology
This project aims to reproduce the results from the reference paper ”Ultra­open acoustic metama­
terial silencer based on Fano­like interference”, published by R. Ghaffarivardavagh et al. in 2019
[18]. The reproduction’s results can be used to tailor the AM for other specific applications such
as reducing noise from beam projectors in their first BPF, generally around 300 Hz.
The methodology sections will present the analytical model which Ghaffarivardavagh’s work is
based on, the assumptions for the model, and its implementation in MATLAB along with two
different approaches for the solution. Furthermore, the assumptions and setup when taking the
MATLAB results to a numerical simulation in COMSOL Multiphysics 5.6 are described. Finally,
a tailoring tool in MATLAB is presented, which calculates and suggests parameters considering
restrictions and geometrical limits of both the AM and the device to improve.
2.1
Analytical model
As mentioned on the introduction section, R. Ghaffarivardavagh’s [18] work aims to show an AM
that silences specific frequencies due to a Fano­like interference effect. Fano’s work in electromag­
netics [25] indicates an asymmetric scattering profile which is formed by the interference between
a wave in a discrete state interacting with a wave in the continuum state. The resulting profile in­
cludes characteristic peaks and dips, and such behavior is referred to as ”Fano­like” in this report.
With an acoustic energy reduction up to 94%, Ghaffarivardavagh’s AM is an acrylonitrile butadi­
ene styrene (ABS) plastic ring shaped structure as can be seen in fig. 1.5. The 0.14 m diameter
AM includes six equally distanced channels that circle around the structure with a helix angle of
8.2°(fig. 2.1), a reported thickness of the AM of 0.07 m, with an inner circle of 0.11 m diameter,
resulting in a 53% OA.
Figure 2.1: COMSOL structure of the helical AM. (Left) complete helical structure. (Right) cross
section of the helical structure, where parameters r1, r2, t, ϕ are indicated.

2.2.3
Critical issues
• Calculation of kn: This variable is represented by a vector and is finite, including all the
zero value positions in the J1(kn) Bessel function. After obtaining said vector, it should be
divided by r2 to get the proportional values to the specific system, thus kn is now the solution
to J1(knr2). It is important to take into consideration that the sums in Green’s functions start
at kn = 0, and the kn vector calculated generally does not include the first term being 0.
• If the calculation of said kn vector does not include zero as the first value: when adapting the
kn vector in the code, it is important to keep in mind that the analytical model integral’s re­
sults include an explicit division by kn. Therefore the first term of the kn vector is suggested
to be a number very close to zero, so the division is not undefined.

2.4
Tailoring for specific applications
Since the purpose of recreating R. Ghaffarivardavagh’s AM is to adapt it to different applications
and evaluating its performance, a simple MATLAB code that tailors the dimensions of the AM
depending on the dimensions of the target device was developed.
The inputs in the tailoring tool are the dimensions of the device, the desired ventilation percentage
(OA in the material), the thickness of the AM, and the center frequency of the noise that wants to
be silenced. Some restrictions are also included in the code so the tailoring tool’s output includes
a good and real approximation that can be implemented in COMSOL.
The tailoring works as follows:
• The shortest dimension of the device will be a limit for the outer radius r2 of the material.
• The OA or ventilation wanted defines the value for r1.
• The thickness of the material specifies the t parameter.
• The target frequency is used to calculate the helix angle as: ϕ = asin(2 ∗t ∗f/c0).
• Limit in minimum thickness considering the thickness of the walls of each channel is 1 mm
per wall.
• Limit in maximum OA, considering that the layers that divide the region 1 and region 2 are
3mm thick.
• Limit the r1 dimension, stating the minimum as 1.5 cm. Ghaffarivardavagh’s paper specifies
that the presented methodology does not impose any inherent limitation on the area fraction
of the central open region. Since the analytical model used does not consider losses, it is
important to set a limit. If r1 is too small, the size of region 1 (the hole of the AM) and its
interaction with the medium will not allow proper airflow due to losses.
The outputs of the tailoring tool are the target frequency f, r1, r2, t, ϕ, step, number of turns, thick­
ness of channel walls, thickness of dividing layer between r1 and r2, which are the inputs for the
MATLAB and COMSOL models. Several changes in the parameters will be done in the COMSOL
simulations for the tailored results, to verify the conclusions obtained from the parameter study.

Discussion
Results show there is a good agreement between the transverse bilayer model and the MATLAB
analytical and numerical implementations, as well as the COMSOL implementation. The biggest
difference and concern is the shift in the resonance frequency wanted, which is caused by the
numerical evaluations in MATLAB, and the geometry adjustments in the plastic walls and layers’
thickness that have to be considered.
The lack of technical specifications in the reference paper leads to results that are somewhat un­
certain. There is not a description of how these plastic walls are calculated in COMSOL, or if
the original values (r1 = 0.051m, r2 = 0.07m, t = 0.052m, and ϕ = 8.2°) are preserved by
increasing the total dimensions (due to considering plastic walls and layers). As mentioned in the
Methodology chapter, if there is no consideration for the plastic structure’s dimensions and the
original values are kept, the COMSOL simulation cannot be processed due to an overlap of the
channels because of the helix parametric curve.
However, with the parameter study is it easy to provide solutions to shift back the resonance fre­
quency to the desired one, by changing the helix angle or adjusting any other parameter as it may
convene. It is a matter of understanding the priorities in the device’s dimensions and the effect of
each parameter. The possibility of stacking the AM and broaden the frequency range to silence
shows another benefit of this geometry for an AM, emphasizing its qualities.
Although the models in MATLAB and COMSOL work and deliver a Fano­like asymmetric trans­
mittance profile, further investigation and adjustments in the models are needed so the target fre­
quency wanted matches the resonance frequency from the models.
Regarding specifically beam projectors and how the noise they produce can be effectively reduced
with the designed AM, further research is required regarding air flow to calculate the ideal OA
and maintain a good ventilation that does not affect the heating and performance of the device.
Knowing this, the limit in r1 can be stated and the parameter r2 (therefore, the OA) can be adjusted
so the transmittance reduction happens at the desired frequencies.
Generally speaking, the handling and tailoring of new designs by changing the parameters ends
up being direct and easy after going through the analytical model. The transverse bilayer model,
though relatively simple, is a good description of the physics in the ”real” AM. Adding a losses
calculation in the analytical model would result in a much more accurate calculation, which could
also show results closer to the ones of a real 3D printed model.
The tailoring tool in MATLAB facilitates the overview of dimension restrictions and desired mea­
sures. This tool can also be adjusted to include other outputs such as suggestions for different
combinations of parameters which all meet the same resonance frequency. This tool simplifies the
calculation for the parameters in different scenarios.
The finalized AM consists of a generally simple structure and, for this case, the dimensions can
easily be printed out in a 3D printer. Because of the simplicity of the materials and structure, the
AM could be taken into an industrial environment. Its ”scalability” or ”manufacturability” is not
hindered because of the dimensions or the inside structure. Nevertheless, taking this AM from a
laboratory to a factory would depend on other aspects like how many applications could be targeted
with just one set of parameter values, or the cost of producing a metacage with this AM built­in,
versus the cost of a common case for a beam projector, etc. Applications, parameter combinations
and materials available are endless, thus there is a lot of room to mix and match depending on each
need.

Future steps in the research would include
• Polishing the current numerical and analytical models to understand the source of error for
the shift in the resonance frequency between MATLAB and COMSOL and reduce the mis­
match.
• Choose a specific beam projector or device to test a printed version of the tailored AM on,

• Testing different possible scenarios for the AM like continuing with the research regarding

All future investigation would contribute to develop an AM that could lead to good solutions and
benefits in devices and structures that require air flow or even forced cooling. E.g. less noise pol­
lution in hospitals from the hospital machinery, which would help reduce stress levels and other
consequences in patients and doctors [31]; industrial machinery noise would benefit greatly from
this, reducing the stress levels and the damage in hearing of the workers, or even in an extreme sce­

can also be a great application, by adding the AM to windows, doors and therefore reducing the
use of ventilation systems, reducing the energy waste related to these applications. The simplicity
related to the materials that could be used in this AM is also related to saving energy and reducing
pollution when reducing the amount and type of materials used day to day applications. The air
flow, sound silencing and even letting light go through, all contribute to the potential applications
of the AM, and result in benefits that go from health to environmental friendly solutions.
The models in MATLAB and COMSOL as well as the parameter study were developed and ex­

spent in developing new AM trying to fit or fulfill certain conditions. The proposed tailoring tool
and model approximations to the structure seem to be malleable in many ways to produce a great
variety of solutions.

Conclusion
This project reports the reproduction of the acoustic metamaterial proposed by R. Ghaffarivar­
davagh with an analytical model in MATLAB and a COMSOL simulation to test the transmittance
of the structure. After confirming the correct operation of both models, a parameter study was
carried out, and a tailoring tool was developed in MATLAB to calculate the parameters needed for
a tailored version of the AM that fulfilled certain requirements.
A beam projector’s dimensions and its noise center frequency were used as a specific goal for the
tailored version of the AM.
The proposed transverse bilayer metamaterial analytical model represents accurately the effects
of the helical metamaterial created in COMSOL. The transverse bilayer metamaterial model uti­
lizes Green’s functions to describe the behavior of the waves through two regions with different
dimensions and refraction indexes. No losses are considered, all boundaries are rigid walls. These
assumptions are also used in the COMSOL simulations. The analogy between both models is ef­
fective: representing the length of the path that the wave has to travel with a certain refraction
index of one of the layers in the bilayer metamaterial model. This works well since the results in
both models are comparable.
Nevertheless, there are differences between the results that are caused by the lack of information
provided in the reference paper, the approximations in the numerical models, and the geometrical
adjustments in the COMSOL simulation. All these resulted in using values and assumptions to
complete the models, and these assumptions had a repercussion on the results. Generally the results
differ in the resonance frequency of the system, meaning that if the models (both MATLAB and
COMSOL) are executed with parameters for a specific target frequency, the results in transmittance
will have the same behavior but for a shifted frequency.
The parameter study’s findings were used to alter and rectify the results, so the target frequency and
resonance frequency could match, thus showing that the modification of the system’s results is easy
and effective. This is supported also by the result of just adding transmittance curves when stacking
AMs with different values in their geometry. Further research is required to refine the accuracy of
the models, investigate the performance in other applications, and investigate the performance as
a 3D printed material.
