Abstract
The employment of virtual sound environments for hearing tests has been proven effective in
acoustic research. Yet, to date, this approach has been rarely adopted for simulating hearôÙ
ing loss. This study constructs a new approach to hearing loss simulation that employs a
ring loudspeaker array, a 3D TuneôÙIn Toolkit Binaural Test Application, and virtual audioôÙvisual
environments. NormalôÙhearing young participants were invited to participate in a sound loôÙ
calization test, wherein they were asked to identify the target story among several competing
speech sources, with response times and errors being recorded. The test results indicated that
the introduction of virtual sound environments optimized the outcomes of participants in hearôÙ
ing loss simulations, manifested in shorter response times and higher accuracy rates. These
tests reveal the significance of visual cues in hearing loss simulations, and comparisons with
other studies confirm the validity of this simulation approach. Furthermore, the test results
also indicate that there is significant room for improvement in current hearing loss simulation
software.

Introduction
Differentiating specific conversations or topics within a complex and crowded environment is
a frequently encountered challenge in daily life, formally identified in audiology as the ãcocktail
party effectã[1]. While individuals with typical hearing abilities navigate such situations seamôÙ
lessly, those with auditory impairments often confront substantial difficulties, not even in a
complex scenario like a cocktail party. For instance, there is research involving both children
with hearing impairments and normalôÙhearing children[2]. The test regarding sound localizaôÙ
tion tasks indicated that children who had undergone sequential bilateral cochlear implants
exhibited errors that were approximately double those of normalôÙhearing children. To assist
individuals experiencing hearing loss in improving their auditory perception, researchers and
engineers have developed advanced hearing aids. These devices have sophisticated noiseôÙ
filtering capabilities, specifically designed to enhance speech intelligibility and minimize backôÙ
ground noise interference, thus facilitating more effective communication in noisy settings. In
addition, researchers have also developed numerous corresponding tools to enhance the inôÙ
vestigation of the mechanisms of audiology.
Among these, loudspeakerôÙbased Virtual Sound Environments (VSEs) are recognized as a
valuable tool. As the name implies, this environment provides participants with a virtual setting
to enhance realism, and a controllable and replicable setting for auditory research. Compared
to traditional testing methodologies, VSEs demonstrate a higher level of ecological validity[3],
which is the extent to which experimental conditions and outcomes align to some degree with
the context or environment of the real world. The reproduction of speech using microphone
array measurements in VSEs closely approximates the speech intelligibility observed in a refôÙ
erence room[4]. This similarity highlights the feasibility of VSEs as an alternative method for
conducting hearing tests.
Despite the extensive research on virtual auditory environments for individuals with normal or
impaired hearing, studies examining the impact of virtual audioôÙvisual environments (VSEs)
on hearing loss simulation are relatively scarce. Meanwhile, hearing loss simulation is a poôÙ
tentially powerful tool for both clinics and researchers. Therefore, this study integrates existing
research, designs auditory tests, and investigates the potential auditory implications of VSEs
on hearing loss simulation.
Research has been conducted on loudspeakerôÙbased virtual environments and summarized
various factors influencing participantsã performance in such environments. A primary factor
is the number of active loudspeakers. Research indicates that individuals with normal hearing
can accurately localize up to four speakers. However, when the number of sound sources
increases to six or more, there is a notable decline in performance[5]. This phenomenon is
consistent across another study from Ahrens and Lund[6].
Another crucial factor in auditory performance is the spatial arrangement of speakers, particôÙ
ularly the distance between them. Intuitively, smaller distances between speakers complicate
the task of comprehending spoken content and achieving accurate localization. Under some
conditions, research results support this surmise. For example, when different speakers overôÙ
lap in the signal spectrum, closely spaced speakers can create pronounced masking effects[7],
and speakers that are spaced farther mitigate these effects. Even without considering overôÙ
lapping spectrum, another research indicates that spatially separated sound sources are more
easily distinguishable than closely situated ones[8].
Furthermore, the position of the target speaker to the listener significantly impacts the accuôÙ
racy of both comprehension and localization. Studies involving hearingôÙimpaired individuals
using hearing aids in multiôÙtalker environments show that spatial localization is most efficient

2.1
Spatial Localization
Spatial localization, an essential aspect of auditory perception, pertains to the ability of the auôÙ
ditory system to locate the origin of sounds in threeôÙdimensional space. This complex process
relies on the brainãs interpretation of various auditory cues derived from the sound signals both
ears receive. The human auditory system deciphers these cues to construct a spatial map,
enabling individuals to navigate and interact effectively with their environment. Rayleigh proôÙ
posed a theory in 1896[11] that there are two critical key auditory cues for spatial localization
in the horizontal plane: Interaural Time Difference (ITD) and the Interaural Level Difference
(ILD). These mechanisms, each playing a distinct but complementary role, allow for precisely
determining sound source direction, contributing significantly to our spatial awareness and
auditory scene analysis. Additionally, the elevation cues in the human auditory system are
provided by the reflections from the pinna and outer ear[12]. However, in this study, particiôÙ
pants are not required to locate sound sources at varying heights, and therefore, this aspect
will not be discussed further.
Following this introduction, the detailed explanations of ITD and ILD illustrate their fundamental
roles in spatial sound localization.

2.2
AgeôÙrelated Hearing Loss
AgeôÙrelated hearing loss (ARHL), or presbycusis, is a common auditory impairment in the elôÙ
derly, primarily due to the natural aging process, but also influenced by environmental and
genetic factors.
The prevalence of AgeôÙrelated Hearing Loss (ARHL) is alarmingly high, significantly impacting
the quality of life and mental health of the elderly, as well as imposing a considerable burden
on healthcare systems. According to data from the National Center for Health Statistics in
the United States[17], in 2022, 31.1% of individuals aged 65 and over experienced hearing
impairments, and this figure increases to 40.3% in the population aged 75 and above. In conôÙ
trast, among individuals aged 18 to 34, the prevalence of hearing impairments was only 7.6%,
clearly indicating that aging is the most common cause of hearing loss.
The pathophysiology of ARHL involves the degeneration of inner ear hair cells and changes
in the auditory nerve and central auditory pathways, contributing to hearing decline.
ARHL manifests in distinct auditory symptoms:
ãÂ Audiometry Shift: For individuals with ARHL, audiometric testing reveals a significant
shift, with a higher decibel (dB) threshold needed for sound detection, particularly at
higher frequencies. This shift is a quantifiable indication of diminished auditory sensitivôÙ
ity. To represent different severity levels of ARHL, nine typical audiogram values have
been calculated by averaging audiometric data collected from 3753 subjects[18] and

3.7
Test Procedure
The testing location for this study was the AVIL Laboratory, Building 354, at the Technical
University of Denmark (DTU). The test had an average duration of approximately 2 hours and
comprised four stages: an information meeting, a corpus reading, a preliminary test, and the
formal testing session.
ãÂ Information Meeting
The first step with the participants was an informational meeting. In this brief session,
the subjects were informed of their rights, followed by an overview of the experimental
procedures and detailed information about the tests involved. After ensuring a compreôÙ
hensive understanding of the test, those willing to continue were requested to sign a
consent form.
ãÂ Corpus Reading
Before commencing the auditory tests, participants were required to familiarize themôÙ
selves with the speech materials. The test conductor provided the texts of ten stories in
a random sequence for the participants to read, allocating half a minute for each story,
with no continuation allowed after time elapsed. This step ensured that participants had
a basic impression of each story while maintaining the randomization of the entire testing
process.
ãÂ Preliminary Test
As mentioned, this test comprised three sets of hearing loss simulation and visual realôÙ
ity configurations. In the first set, participants wore the HTC Vive Pro VR system, with
the hearing loss simulation set to only audiometry deterioration. The second setãs conôÙ
figuration involved participants wearing the HTC Vive Pro VR system, with the hearing
loss simulation including audiometry loss, nonôÙlinear attenuation, temporal distortion,
and frequency smearing all worsening simultaneously. The configuration of the third set
was identical to the second regarding hearing loss simulation, except participants did not
wear the HTC Vive Pro VR system. Before the formal auditory tests, each participant
underwent a preliminary test with three trials in each setãs environment, totaling nine triôÙ
als, to familiarize themselves with the test procedures and properly use the HTC Vive
Pro VR system. The sequence of trials during the preliminary test was fixed.
ãÂ Formal Test
After the preôÙtest, participants can start the formal test. The formal test includes three
sets, with variables including the number of speakers (2, 3, or 4), speaker spacing (comôÙ
pact, moderate, or sparse), and proximity to the center (close or far). Thus, each set enôÙ
compasses 3*3*2=18 distinct conditions. Each condition consists of three trials, amountôÙ
ing to 54 trials per set and 162 trials to be completed by each participant during the formal
test. Each trialãs target location and interference arrangement is pseudoôÙrandom, conôÙ
trolled by MATLAB reading from a preôÙset table to manage the experimental process.
This table in the appendix displays the activated speaker across 54 trials. However, the
stories and storytellers, selected from a pool of ten stories and ten talkers, are randomly
generated. Before each set, the table is shuffled and randomly reordered to ensure the
randomness of the experiment. In addition, two measures were implemented to ensure
the randomness of the test. The first measure involved randomizing the starting point of
each story. The system randomly selects a point within each chosen audio file to comôÙ
mence reading, continuing to read until the end and then restarting from the beginning.
This approach was adopted because keyword locations vary across different stories. For
instance, in the ãgoplerã topic, the first word is ãgopler.ã Without randomizing the startôÙ
ing point of the audio playback, participants could easily comprehend the ãgoplerã topic.

5.1

5.1.1
The Impact of Virtual Reality in Terms of Response Time and Correct
Rate
The test output results encompass two aspects: response time and correct rate. Regarding
response time, an overview of the test results is conducted, disregarding variables such as the
number of talkers, spacing, and relative central distance and grouping them based on different
hearing loss simulation and Virtual Reality (VR) environment configurations. A comparison
between data sets 1 and 2 is required to validate this hypothesis. The overview data indicates
that if the participants do not wear a HeadôÙMounted Display (HMD), the average response
time will slightly increase by 1.3 seconds. According to the MannôÙWhitney U test results, there
is a significant difference (U = 158901.5, p = 0.0105) between the two comparative data sets
(Set 1 vs. Set 2).
However, when categorizing the response time results based on the number of talkers, their
spacing, or their distance relative to the center, it is observed that in most cases, the differences
in outcomes attributed to the VR environment are no longer significant (p > 0.05), which may
be due to the reduced sample size. Nonetheless, considering those figures illustrated in the
results section, regardless of how the response time data are categorized, the mean and
median of Set 1 are consistently higher than those of Set 2ëƒ it can be inferred that the VR
environment does exert some degree of influence on participantsã response times.
Regarding accuracy rate, not wearing an HMD leads to a 5.4% decrease in accuracy. The

5.2.2
The Inexactness of Hearing Loss Simulation
Firstly, the hearing loss simulation in this study occurs within the speaker. Therefore, if the
process of sound reception by the human ear is described in a simplified and idealized manner
(ignore the HeadôÙRelated Transfer Function, HRTF), it is as follows:
EsimuHL = (S ãHLsimu) ãR,
(5.1)

where EsimuHL represents the simulated signal received by the ear, S denotes the signal,
HLsimu represents the simulation of hearing loss on the signal, and R signifies the impact of
the room on the sound. However, for patients with actual hearing impairments, the process of
receiving auditory signals is as follows:
EHL = (S ãR) ãHL,
(5.2)
where EHL represents the signal received by hearingôÙimpaired people, HL donates the actual
hearing loss filter. It can be observed that the order of convolution, or signal processing, has
changed. In realôÙworld scenarios, the initial sound signal should first interact with the room and
then be processed by the human ear. However, in this study, the sound is edited first and then
subjected to the roomãs influence. Currently, there is no available approach or device to adôÙ
dress this issue. However, designing an alternative system that plays sound directly through
headphonesãwhere the headphones first simulate room acoustics and then perform hearing
loss simulation, or designing a device that receives sound within the ear canal for subsequent
hearing loss simulation could more accurately replicate realôÙworld conditions. This, however,
involves extensive and complex work.
Moreover, the hearing loss simulation itself is not flawless. Although it is currently the best
available hearing loss simulator, there are many instances where the data does not align well
when compared with Yanãs research[24], which recruited older individuals with ageôÙrelated
hearing loss and consistently provided a VR environment. For instance, in Yanãs study, when
the number of talkers was two, and the hearing aid settings were at the minimum level, the ocôÙ
currence rate of localization errors was approximately 20%. This indicates that even with the
introduction of hearing aids, the localization error rate remained significantly higher compared
to the approximately 5% localization error rate observed in this study. This indicates that the
hearing loss simulation is insufficient in simulating the deterioration of localization ability assoôÙ
ciated with presbycusis. A potential solution includes having multiple speakers simultaneously
play the same audio file, but quantifying this approach also necessitates extensive work.
5.2.3
Limitation of the potential places of loudspeakers
In AVIL, the loudspeakers are arranged in a relatively sparse configuration, with the azimuth
angle between adjacent loudspeakers being no less than 15 degrees on the horizontal plane.
This study employed 15 potential loudspeakers positioned at azimuth angles ranging from ôÙ
105ô¯ to +105ô¯, which caused some constraints on the spacing design. For instance, with two
talkers, the spacing of 45ô¯ to 75ô¯ corresponds to a moderate spacing level, whereas, with four
talkers, the spacing between the two farthest talkers ranges from 75ô¯ to 105ô¯, also classified
as a moderate spacing level. Thus, under the same spacing level classification, the more the
number of talkers, the smaller the average distance between talkers. Therefore, the average
azimuth between adjacent talkers differs when the number of talkers differs, although classified
under the same spacing level. A potential solution involves incorporating more speakers into
the AVIL, offering greater flexibility in experimental design.
5.2.4

This study considered variables across four dimensions: audioôÙvisual environment configuraôÙ

response time, correct rate, and the incidence of localization errors is not statistically signifiôÙ

Conclusion
In this study, an auditory test was constructed using a ring loudspeaker array, and some triôÙ
als utilized a headôÙmounted display (HMD) for visual rendering to create virtual audioôÙvisual
scenes. The study also employed the 3D TuneIn Toolkit Binaural Test Application v4.0 for
hearing loss simulation, exploring the impact of virtual audioôÙvisual environments in hearing
loss simulation and assessing various hearing loss settings within these environments.
The results indicated that introducing virtual visual environments generally improved particôÙ
ipantsã performance in simulated hearing loss tests, evidenced by reduced response times,
increased accuracy, and a lower localization error rate, but not a more stable performance
(smaller standard deviation of response times). However, this study did not reveal a definitive
factor of the enhancement of participantsã performance in VSEs ôÙ as statistical tests rarely
proved significant changes in performance under specific talker settings. The overall results
still partially illustrate the compensatory role of visual cues in hearing loss simulation. These
observations underline the necessity of visual cues in hearing loss simulations. Therefore,
for a more accurate hearing loss simulation, it is necessary to incorporate VSEs or compenôÙ
sate for visual cues during the processing of hearing loss simulation. However, the factors
influencing participantsã performance regarding loudspeakers in VSEs still require further inôÙ
vestigation.
Regarding localization error, this study illustrated that in VSEs, different severity levels of hearôÙ
ing loss simulation did not significantly affect the incidence of localization errors. This suggests
considerable shortcomings in current hearing loss simulation software in accurately simulating
the localization issues associated with ARHL patients.
It can also be concluded that in VSEs, individuals with healthy hearing exhibit a pattern durôÙ
ing hearing loss simulation tests. As the number of talkers increases, there is a significant
rise in the response time and deterioration in the correct rate. However, other factors related
to the talkers, such as talker spacing and relative distance to the center, do not significantly
impact the participantsã performance. This behavioral pattern aligns with the findings of Yanãs
research[24], indicating that although current hearing loss simulations in VSEs have some
limitations, they do replicate hearing impairment to a certain degree.
In summary, while hearing loss simulation in the audioôÙvisual environment constructed in this
study has limitations, it still adequately replicates the response patterns of ARHL patients.
More importantly, it demonstrates that visual cues should also be considered in hearing loss
simulations, meriting further investigation in the future.
