Abstract
The Maintenance of road infrastructure is essential for safety and economic sustainability.
Road inspection is based on traditional manual, labor-intensive methods and is prone to
human error. This thesis presents a fully automated road crack detection system based on
computer vision and deep learning. The system uses a line-scan camera for continuous
imagining at high speeds with a spatial resolution below 1mm. A calibration routine is
used to remove lens and perspective distortions that are inherent in cameras. A distance-
measuring instrument (DMI) synchronizes image acquisition with the camera’s movement.
An illumination module specifically made for line-scan cameras with high-power LEDs that
ensure uniform lighting (±5 DN variation) to support very short exposure times.
Recent advancements in deep convolutional neural networks (DCNN) have sparked interest
in utilizing DCNN models for crack detection. Four different architectures, U-Net, FPN,
HED, and U-Net with DenseNet backbone, were trained on publicly available datasets and
evaluated using geometry-based metrics. The U-Net model with a DenseNet201 backbone
with preprocessing achieved the highest performance (F1 score: 0.74 on smooth asphalt,
0.44 on noisy surfaces). System testing using the Pavement Surface Cracking Index
(PSCI) demonstrated effective crack quantification; the Pavement Surface Cracking Index
(PSCI) further demonstrates the system’s potential for evaluating pavement conditions.
Keywords: Road crack detection, line-scan camera, deep learning, pavement assessment,
computer vision.

Introduction
Road infrastructure is one of the most critical aspects of the modern society. Roads serve
as key conduits for the transport of products and people, driving economic development.
Therefore, maintaining roads is crucial for the sustainability of road infrastructure to ensure
safety and performance. Proper maintenance extends the lifespan of road infrastructure
but also reduces the overall cost associated with road repairs and reconstructions. Fur-
thermore, a well-maintained road improves traffic safety and reduces vehicle wear. Road
maintenance increases economic growth by ensuring smooth pavement, according to the
World Bank’s analysis on road infrastructure management [1].
Along the various forms of pavement distress, cracks in the road are significant due to their
ability to degrade the pavement faster. Road cracks are typically formed for two reasons:
Load-Induced Road Cracks, and Non-Load-Induced Road Cracks. Load-Induced
Road Cracks result from heavy loads on the pavement surface leading to cracking (e.g.,
traffic volume and vehicle weight). Non-Load-Induced Road Cracks are caused by
temperature, moisture, and geological conditions. For example, many road cracks and
potholes in Denmark occur during the winter due to temperature fluctuations and salt
usage.
One of the road maintenance challenges is road inspection to localize road cracks. Tradi-
tional road inspection methods often involve manual inspection by trained professionals.
These methods are simple but are time-consuming, labor-intensive, and prone to human
error. The errors include human fatigue and subjective judgment, which can comprise
the consistency of the localization of cracks. These limitations highlight the need for an
automated road inspection process.
One promising approach to automated road inspection involves integrating computer
vision technology into a vehicle to scan the roads for cracks automatically. Camera—or
laser-based systems are already being used for automated road cracking, significantly
increasing the inspection speed while circumventing human limitations such as fatigue
and subjectivity. When properly deployed, the computer vision-based system can achieve
high accuracy, reduce labor costs, and enhance road safety.
However, computer vision will face various environmental challenges, such as lightning,
distortion, blur, rain, and shadow interference. Additionally, it may struggle to detect cracks

on different pavement types and depend heavily on the quality of the image data. The
computer vision system must be carefully designed and tested, with a high-quality image
system that ensures no distortion and motion blur.
The thesis will be conducted in conjunction with Dynatest, a leading company that already
provides tools and technologies for road quality inspection. Dynatest currently uses a
laser-based system assisted by cameras for anomaly detection. The system is highly
accurate and allows inspection speeds up to 100 km/h. However, the current system has
downsides, including costly lasers and software licenses from Pavemetrics. Furthermore,
laser-based systems also carry the danger of not being eye-safe, which carries safety risks
when operating the system [2]. This thesis will take another approach and make a purely
camera-based system using line-scan camera technology and deep learning methods for
crack segmentation. The new system’s goal would be a more cost-effective automated
road inspection system with reduced eye safety requirements. The new system would
only serve as a cheaper alternative to the current laser-based as it will not be able to have
the same accuracy or three-dimensional measurements.
1.1
Objectives
The objects set to this thesis are both based upon general requirements and standards
set by Dynatest, but also by competitor’s similar products, for instance, Greenwood [3]:
1. Design a cost-effective imaging system for a vehicle that stays in sync with its
movement and avoids motion blur.
2. Have a maximum 1 mm spatial resolution/pixel resolution.
3. Create high brightness homogeneous illumination with less than +-5 DN variation
over the entire image.
4. Implement calibration methods to remove perspective distortion and lens distortion.
5. Develop a data-gathering system capable of storing high-quality images for analysis.
6. Develop a computer vision model capable of detecting road cracks in the images
created by the data-gathering system.
7. Ensure the solution is designed as future-proof for future Dynatest development.
1.2
Limitations
Since the project was done in collaboration with Dynatest, some design choices were
predetermined.
• A Line-scan camera must be used for the imaging system. It must be a Basler
line-scan camera.
• No budget for professional illumination. The illumination system must be designed
by ourselves.
• No mounting of the system on a car. This means that the thesis will focus on
developing a prototype that fulfills all the defined objectives, serving as proof of
concept.

Analysis
This chapter provides a broader context for the thesis, outlining the project’s overall
goals and key decisions that form the foundation for the subsequent chapters. It also
describes how to achieve the objections described in Chapter 1. It includes a state-of-the-
art comparison, outlining other approaches to road crack detection and the methodologies
upon which our thesis will be based.
2.1
State of the art
Since the 1970s, researchers have been developing various automated pavement crack
detectors to replace manual inspection methods. Researchers present many automated
crack detection systems, and three standard data-gathering methods are utilized: area-
scan, line-scan, and laser-based systems. Usually, they work with an image with a
sufficiently small spatial resolution that allows the system to detect cracks down to 1 mm
[2, 3, 4, 5, 6].
Area-scan cameras capture two-dimensional images of the pavement by illuminating a
wide area. In contrast, a line-scan camera captures one-dimensional image data where the
image is formed by integrating successive scans [2]. One of the biggest concerns of camera-
based solutions is illumination. It should be powerful enough to support low exposure
time at high speeds and maintain consistent imagining conditions with no shadows. For
instance, while working with a line-scan-based solution, an illumination device casts a
transverse beam that covers the full field of view of the line-scan camera [7]. Solutions like
halogen or fluorescent light [6], LED arrays [3], and laser-based illumination devices [2]
are used in road inspection systems to ensure proper lighting for image capture. Moreover,
one of the challenges in designing an imagining system with a camera is that the camera
is affected by different kinds of distortion. For line-scan cameras, Yao et al.[8] presented a
calibration solution for their road crack detection system to remove lens distortion.
An alternative solution to camera-based systems is laser-based, which can generate
three-dimensional images. For instance, the LCMS system by Pavemetrics can detect
road cracks and subsurface anomalies by measuring variants in the surface elevation
and texture [9]. Compared to camera-based, which can only generate two-dimensional
images, it allows for more accurate and versatile measurements. It is possible to get

three-dimensional measurements with cameras if used in stereo, but this thesis will not
explore this.
In deep learning and computer vision, modern approaches utilize digital image processing
algorithms or deep learning models to detect road cracks. Image processing is generally
either done with thresholding or edge detection [10, 11], whereas deep learning models
generally use deep convolutional neural networks (DCNNs) [12]. The models are trained to
classify as bounding boxes or semantic segmentation (pixel-level segmentation). Classic
semantic models include CNN [13], U-Net [14], FCN [15], CrackNet [16], SegNet [17],
Deeplab [18], PSPNet [19], Transformer [20]. Where the bounding box models are typically
based on the YOLO model. The biggest downside of deep learning models is the large data
requirement required to train a quality model. Public semantic datasets of road cracks are
rare as commercial partners create most datasets. But some public semantic datasets do
exist, for instance, the datasets Crack500[21], Crack Forest Tree (CFD)[22], GAPS384[23],
CrackTree200[24].
Furthermore, detecting road cracks can be categorized into offline and online approaches.
Offline detection involves processing and analyzing the collected road image after data
acquisition. This allows systems to use more advanced models that are not time-critical,
have less powerful hardware, and might increase the accuracy of crack detection. In
contrast, online detection involves real-time crack detection, where the cracks are found
while capturing the image. This approach has the advantage of identifying the cracks on
site and allowing for immediate repair if desired.
It is challenging to compare commercial solutions, as it is not common for manufacturers
to share information about the weaknesses of their systems. In general, all commercial
solutions report being able to identify cracks thinner than 3 mm. Furthermore, one of
Dynatest’s competitors has developed a similar solution that uses two line-scan cameras
and a deep learning model to detect road cracks [3]. They had impressive features,
such as custom illumination that was many times stronger than the sunlight, and had a
4-meter-wide image utilizing two line-scan cameras.
After the crack detection process, several ways exist to analyze the results. Serval
commercial solutions use advanced and often prohibited algorithms to calculate the severity
of the road damage. The algorithms quantify road quality based on cracks and potholes,
pavement surface conditions, and other road anomalies. A standard called ASTM E3303-21
[25] provides a standardized method for quantifying pavement surface cracking using digital
2D images or 3D data. The Pavement Surface Cracking Index (PSCI) is a dimensionless
number that quantifies the amount of surface cracking on pavements. It ranges from 0 to
100, where 0 indicates the worst possible condition, and 100 represents the best possible
condition.
2.2
Decisions
Several decisions were made in developing an automated road crack detection system to
ensure that the objectives and limitations were met, as stated in Chapter 1. Each decision
reflects the constraints of maintaining a future-proof design, high crack detection precision,
low cost, and a high-quality imaging system.
Hardware
The decision to use line-scan cameras was made with Dynatest as they desired a thorough
analysis of how they would work in a road inspection system. Dynatest chose line-scan
cameras because of their high sample capture rate and ability to capture continuous
images, which makes them great for road crack systems. We decided to work with a

camera with a 2048 × 1 pixels resolution and a very high sample rate of 172 kHz. The
camera was mounted at 0.91 m height and equipped with an 8 mm focal length lens,
allowing it to cover a range of 1.53 m. When the images were stitched together, the final
output formed a 2048 × 2048 image because this number is divisible by a 512 × 512 image,
which was the input of our detection model.
The illumination system would be a custom design, focusing only on providing illumination
for line-scan cameras rather than area-scan cameras. Thus, it only provides a transverse
beam of light. The system was decided to be designed with LEDs, which provide the most
efficient light and are widely available.
A DMI system was implemented to synchronize camera movement and ensure all images
were stitched together without distortion. The DMI was made using a programmable
encoder, where the resolution could be adjusted.
For data gathering, a computer with specifications that are good enough to handle the
workload of saving high-resolution images, training deep learning models, and running
deep learning models will be set up.
Prototype
A prototype was designed without the limitation of being mounted on a car. It was chosen
to build the prototype of a rolling workbench, which gave it mobility and decreased the
design complexity. However, the system has been designed to allow for mounting on a
vehicle in the future, with the prototype serving as proof of concept.
Calibration
To remove unnecessary distortion in the camera, the line-scan camera should be calibrated,
and artifacts and distortion should be removed to ensure high-quality images. A calibration
method involving a stationary line-scan camera and 1-D images was chosen to remove
distortion from the image.
Detection method
We employed a semantic segmentation model to identify fine cracks at pixel-level accuracy.
This choice supports our objective of detecting road cracks with minimal false negatives.
Furthermore, it scales well with additional training data, which is essential for future
expansions. Multiple semantic segmentation models will be explored and tested to identify
the best model for our solution. The training data will be from publicly available road
crack segmentation datasets. The images from our prototype will only be used to test the
model’s performance. Furthermore, by training the model on publicly available data, we
demonstrate the model’s ability to generalize and perform well on various pavement and
crack types captured by the prototype. The semantic model had an input of 512 × 512
pixels image size.
We will only focus on an offline detection system, as deep learning models can be slow
and computationally extensive. When the cracks were detected, the Pavement Surface
Cracking Index (PSCI) was chosen as a simple way to calculate the severity of road
damage.
Software
The open-source Python wrapper for Baslers Pylon SDK Pypylon was used to configure and
capture images from the camera. The data acquisition module also uses general libraries
like OpenCV and Numpy for saving and modifying images, threading for concurrent
process management, and os for storing the data.

3.1
Related work
One of the most critical aspects of crack detection is the vision system. Different data
collection technologies have been used, initially based on analog video capturing and
storage [26], where the system was semi-automatic with manual inspection after data
collection. After 20 years of development, more advanced technologies, such as laser-
and digital imaging systems, are now commonly used.
Laser-based systems have also been used for road distress detection. One example of
this is Pavemetrics Laser Crack Measurement System (LCMS) [9], which is also being
used by Dynatest. LCMS uses laser technology to create high-resolution 3D profiles of
the pavement surface. Laser-based systems like LCMS are known for their precision in
detecting micro-cracks and their ability to detect the depth of cracks.
The digital imaging system is typically based on area or line-scan cameras and usually
works with an image spatial resolution equal to or wider than 1 mm [2] [3]. Digital imaging
systems have been implemented in various solutions such as RoackCrack, [4], Fugro
Roadware’s Automatic Road Analyzer (ARAN) [5], PAVUE [6] and Greenwood’s Surface
Imaging System (SIS)[3]. Many of these systems used digital imaging systems, and most
utilized line-scan cameras for detailed surface inspections.
One of the challenges with digital imaging systems is illumination; as the vehicle moves
at high speed, the camera needs a proportional exposure time to avoid motion blur and,
therefore, needs a high-powered illumination device. Most solutions employ flashing LEDs,
Halogen, fluorescent lamps, or lasers [5][4]. Halogen or fluorescent lamps can only produce
white light and, therefore, can only remove shadows to a limited amount because sunlight
is not blocked. Furthermore, powerful halogen or fluorescent lamps require a substantial
amount of power. Some solutions to remove sunlight are near-infrared (NIR) wavelengths.
[2]. The advantage of using near-infrared light is that the sun can be filtered out using
a bandpass filter, allowing consistent light in varying lighting conditions. Most solutions

3.2
Principle of Area-scan Camera
An area-scan camera is similar to a standard digital camera. It is an imaging device
that captures a 2D image in a single exposure using a rectangular 2D grid sensor. The
fundamental principle of an area-scan camera is to capture a picture of the entire object
(or pavement, in this case), capturing both the vertical and horizontal pixels at once.
3.3
Principle of Line-scan Camera
A line-scan camera is designed to capture images of one vertical pixel at a time. This
differs from a typical area-scan camera, which simultaneously captures the whole frame.
The line-scan camera sensor is formed by a single line of photosensitive elements (pixels)
rather than a rectangular sensor used in area-scan cameras. Although the linear sensor
is only one pixel high, the sensor can be up to 16k wide comparing Basler’s product line
[27]—the pixel sizes on the sensor range from 3.5 x 3.5 µm to 14 x 14 µm. Since the
sensor is only one pixel high, the camera can achieve fast acquisition rates with high
resolution. The acquisition rate can reach 200 kHz, with an 8k resolution [27]. One of the
considerations when using a line-scan camera is that the sensor size is proportional to the
light required by the application. For example, a 5-µm pixel has a light-sensitive area of
25 µm², while a 14-µm pixel has an active surface area of 196 µm². This means that the
14-µm pixel has approximately eight times the surface area and, resulting in eight times
greater sensitivity to light [28].
The primary benefit of line-scan cameras is their high acquisition rate and high resolution
for large, detailed objects, making them ideal for imaging large or continuous objects.
The camera’s high acquisition rate enables it to take images of moving objects without
the motion blur commonly seen with area-scan cameras. It also benefits from a more
straightforward lighting setup, whereas uniform lighting for an area-scan camera can be
much more expensive.

3.4
Area-scan Camera or Line-scan Camera
The two main methods of pavement image acquisition are based on two-dimensional
area-scan cameras or one-dimensional line-scan cameras. Both methods have their
distinct advantages and limitations. The specific requirements for this thesis are speed,
illumination, and resolution.
Pavement surveys are often mounted on vehicles moving at high speed, where minimizing
motion blur can be challenging. Line-scan cameras have the advantage at high speeds as
they only capture one line with the vehicle’s motion, allowing much lower exposure time.
Area-scan cameras are more susceptible to motion blur in fast-moving environments. The
entire frame is exposed at once, and any movement during exposure can result in blurred
images.
When comparing the illumination requirements of a line-scan camera and an area-scan
camera, the general characteristic is that a line-scan camera requires higher irradiance.
But if you have two cameras, one a line-scan and the other an area-scan, with a resolution
of 1000 x 1000 pixels, their illumination requirements are different. Assume the area-scan
camera has an exposure time of 30 ms, and the line-scan camera should match that
exposure time. It should have a line rate of 30µs to generate the same frame each 30ms.
This means the illumination requirement is 1000 times more than the area-scan camera.
However, the area-scan camera’s illumination must cover an area that is 1000 times more,
making it very hard to create a large area of uniform light. The line-scan and area-scan
cameras need the same irradiance level in this case.
Now, suppose the object is moving past the camera. In machine vision, the best practice
is to limit motion blur to one pixel. There is no motion blur in the line-scan camera, as the
camera already moves with one pixel in the direction of travel. However, the area-scan
cameras need to minimize blur to one pixel, so the exposure time needs to be reduced to
30 µs. In this case, the irradiance of the area camera illuminations must be 1000 times
more than that of the line camera sensor [29].
This thesis chose to use a line-scan camera because it has clear advantages over an
area-scan camera.

3.5.4
Shading Correction
As part of the camera firmware, shading correction reduces image inconsistency due
to inhomogeneous light. One of the significant challenges in designing illumination for
camera-based systems is to create homogeneous illumination across the camera’s width.
Furthermore, if all LEDs are turned on an equal amount in a light bar, the middle of the
light bar will almost always be the brightest. This can be corrected by adjusting each LED
individually or correcting the image afterward, for example, using shading correction by
Basler [36].
Shading correction is based on two files stored on the camera containing appropriate
correction data. These correction values are applied to the original pixel values during
image acquisition. The two files are created to remove dark signal non-uniformities
(DSNU) and photo response non-uniformities (PRNU). DSNU refers to the slight difference
in brightness when the image is in total darkness, and PRNU happens when the camera
Road Crack Detection Using Camera
17
==PAGE:28==
pixels respond differently to the same amount of light. Furthermore, the PRNU correction
can also be used to adjust for inhomogeneous light.
Baslers Pylon SDK contains the functions for DSNU calibration [37], which creates the
correction files automatically. First, the camera must be placed in complete darkness, for
example, using the lens cover, and then DSNU calibration can be done by Pylon. DSNU
correction works by adding an offset to each pixel value. PRNU correction is done similarly
using white A4 papers along the illuminated surface. The exposure is adjusted so the light
has an intensity of around 95%, and then the PRNU calibration can now be done by Pylon.
PRNU correction works by adding a multiplier (gain) to each pixel value.

4
Camera Calibration
For our computer vision-based system, the image quality captured from our camera is of
great importance in determining the cracks in the pavement. A poor-quality image can
be blurred, distorted, or lack sufficient contrast, making it challenging to identify cracks
manually or use a neural network to learn patterns of cracks. Most parameters spring
from user errors like the camera not being adjusted to a suitable focal length, incorrect
exposure settings, or the camera being misaligned with the ground. Even imperfections
within the lens can cause lens distortion. With lens distortion, the relationship between 3D
world coordinates and image coordinates becomes nonlinear, leading to inaccuracies in
tasks such as crack measurements. However, line-scan cameras differ from area-scan
cameras, as the second dimension of the image in line-scan cameras is generated by
the sensor’s motion relative to the ground over time. Therefore, traditional methods for
camera calibration in area-scan cameras cannot be used for line-scan cameras.
We created three calibration routines for our camera, which calibrated for user errors and
camera imperfections.
1. Our camera was mounted on an adjustable camera rig, so our camera placement
had to be calibrated to ensure our camera was not skewed on any axis.
2. The most crucial routine was camera calibration, which was calibrating the nonlinear
relationship to undistort the camera images.
3. The second dimension of the image in line-scan cameras is motion relative to the
ground over time, which a DMI controls. The DMI also had to be calibrated; the
procedure is covered in chapter 5.
4.1
Related Work
Line-scan camera calibration research is not fully mature; standardized methods are not
fully integrated into tools. But in general, camera calibration requires three steps:
1. Find correspondences between pattern and world coordinates
2. Solve a linear solution
3. Perform nonlinear optimization

Line-scan camera calibration follows two main approaches:
Scan-based approaches are where the line-scan camera moves relative to a calibration
pattern with known features [38, 39]. Drareni et al. [38] presented one of the first novel
methods to calibrate a line-scan camera based on a linear push broom (same as a line-scan
camera) with a plane-based calibration method. All the mentioned research papers propose
a solution where the line-scan camera is moved with a constant velocity to generate an
image over a pattern. Furthermore, the position of the features from the pattern is found,
which forms correspondences to world coordinates. These methods provide promising
and robust results. The downside is the requirement of a complex setup; an example is
D. D. Li et al. [39] uses two cameras, a standard line-scan camera with a frame camera
mounted next to it.
Line-based calibration methods are based on line-scan camera models [40, 41]. This
calibration method differs from other methods as it only requires one line of data from the
line-scan camera, whereas an area-based solution requires multiple lines and movement.
Line-based calibration has the disadvantage of finding patterns to world coordinate cor-
respondences more difficult. The most common method is using a pattern with parallel
and diagonal lines to find the correspondences with cross-ratio invariance, proposed by
Horaud et al. [40]. Line-based calibration has the advantage of being simpler and requiring
minimal equipment.
Yao et al., [41] presented a line-based solution that construed a 2-D calibration framework
with the 1-D data. Yao et al. also worked on a pavement distress system and only used
the calibration to undistort the image. Their work had some assumptions about the camera
placement. However, in our system, precise camera alignment to the ground was not
feasible. To address this, we presented a calibration method for placement that was unique
to our solution. This method uses the same pattern with parallel and diagonal lines, but to
our knowledge, it has not been documented in any existing literature.

Non-linear Optimization
When the closed form is solved, the initial intrinsic and extrinsic parameters are found using

reprojection error in a maximum likelihood framework. To account for lens distortion, the
projection model is adjusted for radial distortion only, as tangential distortion is not present
in line-scan cameras (which capture 1-D image data). Following Zhang’s method, the
Levenberg–Marquardt algorithm is applied to iteratively refine the parameters by minimizing
the reprojection error.

Crack Detection Vehicle
In recent years, the development of automated crack detection systems has gained
attention, given their potential to improve infrastructure maintenance and road safety.
The most common systems consist of cameras and/or lasers mounted on a vehicle,
allowing continuous road scans without disturbing the infrastructure. The cars have
advanced imaging and processing capabilities to identify road anomalies such as potholes,
maintenance holes, and cracks [3].
This section describes the design of a prototype vehicle that encompasses the other
modules of the thesis. The design has multiple subsystems, including the vision system,
data processing, and mechanical structure. In addition to the vision system, a system
to track the length of the road is implemented. This system ensures that the images are
non-overlapping and captured at the calculated spatial resolution.

Vision systems based on camera technology must have a technology that can track the

additional costs for Dynatest and introduced challenges such as high-speed vibrations

GenICam
The machine vision industry has existed for many years and has only expanded with the

number of different SDKs and APIs increased proportionally, making it difficult to switch
brands as it could result in having to rewrite large portions of the software. To combat this
issue, GenICam released its first module in 2006, and it has since become the standard
for most large machine vision manufacturers. The GenICam standard makes a unified
API standard across brands, making it possible to change the brand of the camera with no
to minimal software changes[50].

Cameras using the GenICam standard include an XML-based camera description file

The transport layer (GenTL) transfers data between the camera and the application. It
establishes a standardized data stream method, ensuring efficient image data transfer
from the camera to the host system. GenTL manages the communication protocols and
works regardless of the camera’s communication interface (e.g., GigE, CoaXPress, USB,

8.1
Related work
Many methods exist for automatic crack detection today, with systems using cameras,
lasers, or a combination of the two to scan the roads. The detection algorithms for the
camera-based systems have generally been either thresholding [10, 11] or edge-detection
[60, 61]. The two methods work under the assumption that cracks consist of a narrow strip
of pixels that has a greater intensity of black than the surrounding pavement[2, 46]. This is
generally an excellent general approach. However, the cracks are often interrupted by
gravel, dirt, water, oil spills, or skid marks that disturb these algorithms, making the cracks
broader or narrower than in actuality, resulting in false positives and false negatives.
Deep convolutional neural networks (DCNNs) have gained traction in detecting road
anomalies [12]. The DCNNs can, by learning hierarchical representations from labeled
data, offer a more robust solution that is less sensitive to variations in lighting, texture, and
pavement conditions than the more traditional solution of solely relying on thresholding
and edge-detection algorithms. To achieve pixel-level segmentation for accurate measure-
ments of crack area models like U-Net or Fully Convolutional Networks (FCNs) have been
used to directly predict segmentation masks [57, 58]. Compared to relying purely on image
processing algorithms like thresholding and edge detection, the downside to this approach
is the requirement of large labeled datasets to allow for supervised learning. This can
be somewhat remedied by introducing synthetic data generation and augmentation into
existing data to overcome the scarcity in large datasets[54].
U-Net remains a popular choice as a baseline architecture for crack detection in pavement.
More advanced models like Feature Pyramid Networks (FPN) and Holistically-Nested

57
==PAGE:68==
Edge Detection (HED) explicitly fuse information from multiple feature scales [57, 59, 62].
This multi-scale approach shows promising results in crack detection in pavement, where
cracks can vary in width and continuity.
In the commercial arena of road inspection solutions, multiple sensing modalities are
typically combined. The synergy between robust CNN models, a system that can capture
high-resolution illuminated images, big datasets, and efficient deployment strategies makes

8.2
Principles of Deep Learning
Deep learning for crack segmentation builds on the fundamental principles of CNNs and the
concept of semantic image segmentation. This section will describe the more fundamental
theory behind this and the methodology behind how deep neural network models can
be trained using supervised learning. The subject will be described more generally to
understand better and establish the terminology and methods used.
8.2.1
Machine learning
Machine learning (ML) algorithm learns patterns or behaviors from data instead of relying
on hard-coded instructions. The machine learning techniques can be categorized as
supervised and unsupervised learning.
• Supervised learning: is where a relationship between an input x and a correspond-
ing label y is learned. The key element in the training is that the model has access to
the ground truth, which enables it to loop over pairs of (x,y) and update parameters
to improve the accuracy of its predictions. This is commonly used in tasks where
the desired outcome is known, such as classification, regression, and semantic
segmentation tasks.
• Unsupervised learning: trains without knowing the ground truth and can only
access the unlabeled data. The model trained using unsupervised learning aims
to discover hidden structures or patterns such as clustering, density estimation, or
outlier detection, all without direct guidance from labeled data.
A common objective in computer vision is semantic image segmentation, where every
pixel in the image will be assigned a class. This can be done as binary segmentation,
where the image will have one class and a background, or as multi-class segmentation,
where there can be multiple classes and a background. In segmentation tasks, supervised
learning is the most common approach. The input x would then be the image, and the
output y would then be the segmentation mask.

that stacks many layers of parameterized transformations. Each layer of the neural network
computes an output zl = fl(zl−1; θl) given the previous layers zl−1 and the models’ trainable
parameters θl. The chaining of these layers allows a neural network to approximate highly
non-linear mappings.

8.2.2
Image Segmentation
This thesis focuses on binary image segmentation to segment a targeted class from the
background. A grayscale image X ∈RH×W with height H and width W, can find a mapping
of the form:
f : X →Y
(8.5)
with Y ∈{0, 1}H×W as a binary mask that assigns each pixel to one of two classes, target or
background. X is labeled as either the foreground or the background. For crack detection,
the foreground would correspond to the cracks, and the background would correspond
to non-cracks (e.g., undamaged pavement and road lines). The goal would then be to
learn f using a dataset of labeled examples D = {(X(n), Y(n))}N
n=1. Each image X(n) would
be paired with the corresponding mask Y(n), which informs the model of the foreground
it must learn to detect. The goal is that once trained, f generalizes to unseen images,
providing accurate pixel-wise predictions of the target.
Encoder-Decoder Architectures
Encoder-decoder models have become the cornerstone for segmentation tasks in deep
learning. Studies as [57, 58] emphasize how this design balances local detail and global
context with their use of FPN and U-Net models. Models with this architecture work by
having the encoder gradually downsampling the input through convolution and pooling
operations, effectively capturing high-level representations of the data. The decoder uses
upsampling or interpolation to reconstruct a full-resolution output mask.
1. Encoder:
• Reduces spatial dimensions to aid in the capture of abstract semantic features.
• Can include a final bottleneck that holds condensed global information.
2. Decoder:
• Expands the compressed feature maps back to their original input size.
• Incorporation of skip connections from the encoder (e.g., U-Net), which pre-
serves fine-grained spatial cues necessary for precise boundary detection [12].
This architecture makes it possible to alternate between downsampling and upsampling,
allowing the network to learn how to identify small localized structures (like thin cracks),
even when embedded in a larger context [53].
Class Imbalance problem
A typical problem in segmentation tasks, especially binary image segmentation, is the
class imbalance problem. The problem is present when the dataset has a minority class,
60
Road Crack Detection Using Camera
==PAGE:71==
which will typically be presented as an underrepresentation of the target compared to the
background in tasks like this. Multi-class segmentation tasks can also over-represent one
class compared to the others. This can cause the model to show misleading results, as
even poor models can display high accuracy due to the skewed distributions of classes.
In a binary image segmentation task, this can be seen when the model accurately finds
the majority to be true negatives, which could be 99 % of the data, but is unable to find
the true positives, which only represent 1 % of the data [63]. The handling of the class
imbalance problem falls into two primary categories:
1. Data-Level Methods
• Oversampling minority regions or undersampling majority pixels to counter the
imbalance of the classes.
• Using data augmentation by using synthetic data to expand rare classes [12].
2. Algorithm-Level Methods
• Choosing loss functions that can handle imbalanced datasets such as Dice,
Tversky, BCE, or Focal loss, some of which can be tuned for this purpose.
• Using adaptive sampling to adjust how mini-batches are formed to include more
minority-class examples [55].
These methods can prevent the model from ignoring underrepresented features and
ensure that the model learns what is desired based on the task. By using the methods and
choosing the correct metrics to evaluate the model, the class imbalance will have much
less impact on the trained model.

8.3.1
U-Net
Figure 8.2: Simplified illustration of a UNet architecture
The original U-Net architecture was published in 2015 by Ronneberger et al. [64]. U-Net
is a type of CNN designed for image segmentation. Figure 8.2 has an encoder-decoder
architecture with skip connections that form a “U” shape, hence the name. Using skip-
connections is one of the key features of U-Net that maps connections between encoder

61
==PAGE:72==
and decoder steps. Skip connections help the network combine high-level (semantic)
information from the decoder with low-level (spatial) information from the encoder. This
gives a more precise segmentation, especially along object boundaries. After the decoder,
a final convolutional layer assigns each pixel to a class label. Furthermore, U-Net is
designed to work with small amounts of data. It was created and is still used in the medical
industry. In many ways, it is similar to crack detection. These include small datasets,
detecting rare small objects, and noisy images.
Our U-Net model consists of the encoder and decoder architecture described above.
The encoder applies five convolutional blocks (Conv →BatchNorm →ReLU) to extract
features, doubling the channels while downsampling with max pooling except the last layer.
The decoder uses transposed convolutions to upsample, concatenates the corresponding
encoder features via skip connections, and applies convolutional blocks to refine. Finally,
a 1x1 convolutional with sigmoid activation produces the segmentation mask. The U-
Net was primarily used as a baseline model because of its strong performance in image
segmentation tasks and popularity in all segmentation tasks.

8.3.5
Loss Functions
Loss functions are an essential part of the training of deep learning models. Loss functions
define a model’s deviation from its prediction to its ground truth, guiding parameter updates
during training and leading to the best model for the task. Multiple different loss functions
can be used for this purpose. The loss functions can be generalized into two distinct
categories, with cross-entropy variants being distribution-based and Dice or Tversky
variants being overlap-based. Overlap-based losses promote global agreement between
the prediction and the ground truth, penalizing the mismatches between the prediction and
ground-truth masks. Distribution-based losses measure errors per pixel.
This section will describe the most common loss functions for image segmentation and
their strengths and weaknesses.
Cross-Entropy (CE)
Cross-entropy is the most common distribution-based loss function.
It measures the dissimilarity between probability functions and can be used for multi-class
and binary tasks. The pixel-wise cross-entropy loss function for binary classification tasks
can be defined as:
ℓCE(pi, yi) = −[yilog(pi) + (1 −yi)log(1 −pi)]
(8.8)
Where pi is the predicted probability of a pixel belonging to the foreground and yi is the
ground truth of the pixel i.
ℓCE penalizes significant divergences between predicted probabilities and ground truth,
which can be inefficient in datasets with high-class imbalance. In these cases, the network
can minimize cross-entropy by predominantly predicting the background, thereby neglect-
ing the foreground class, resulting in a model with poor performance. The imbalance
problem can be addressed by introducing a weighting factor, which is used in balanced
cross-entropy (BCE).
Balanced Cross-Entropy (BCE)
In balanced cross-entropy, a weighing factor α ∈[0, 1]
is introduced to address the class imbalance and increase the importance of the minority
class. The function can be defined as:
ℓBCE(pi, yi) = −[αyilog(pi) + (1 −α)(1 −yi)log(1 −pi)]
(8.9)
This will give the class a higher weight, reducing the probability that the model will neglect
the minority class. The selected weight α should depend on the ratio of the classes and
be tuned based on this distribution.
Focal loss
The Focal loss function was introduced by [67] to address cross-entropy’s
issues with datasets suffering from high-class imbalance. Where cross-entropy with
high-class imbalance is overwhelmed with easily classified negatives, resulting in them
dominating the gradient, the Focal loss will downweight easy examples, resulting in a
focus on complex classifiable data. It can be defined as:
ℓFocal(pi, yi) = −α(1 −pi)γyilog(pi) −(1 −α)pγ
i (1 −yi)log(1 −pi)
(8.10)
where alpha =∈[0, 1] is the weighting factor for the foreground class, and γ ≥0 is the
focusing parameter that adjusts the rate of the downsampling of easy examples. When
γ = 0, the function will act as BCE, and the increase of γ will then, in turn, increase
the modulating factor, resulting in a smaller loss for the prediction of examples with high
probability.

Summary of loss functions
Each loss function offers distinct advantages and draw-

• Cross-Entropy (CE): Straightforward distribution-based function, well documented

as BCE.
• Focal Loss: An adoption of CE that focuses on harder examples with hyperparameter

• Dice Loss: Simple overlap-based loss function, good for the imbalanced dataset as
its focus is true positives promoting more focus on the foreground.
• Tversky: A flexible Dice loss adaptation allows for different penalties for false
positives vs false negatives adjusted by its two hyperparameters α and β.
In the case of crack detection, overlap-based approaches like Dice or Tversky can help cap-

8.5.4
Loss functions
To fully explore how a deep segmentation network behaves on various datasets, we have
trained the models using several loss functions, both distribution-based and overlap-based.
Comparing multiple approaches will give greater insight into which formulation provides
the best results, how much the choice of loss function affects the final models, and whether
the best choice will change based on the dataset.
Setup and Parameter choices:
• Balanced Cross-Entropy (BCE): To address the class imbalance, a weighting factor
α = 0.9 was chosen to weigh the foreground (cracks) higher. Due to the heavy class
imbalance, it was chosen only to do balanced cross-entropy and not cross-entropy,
as it generally performs poorly in datasets with high-class imbalance.
• Focal Loss: Following the guidance of [67], we set γ = 2 and α = 0.25 as they
had the best results with these hyperparameters. These settings will make the
training focus on the harder examples, emphasizing detecting cracks rather than the
background.
• Dice Loss: Dice loss was also chosen as it does well on datasets with high-class
imbalance. It has no hyperparameters that must be tuned.
• Tversky Loss: The ratio between the hyperparameters α and β will be a tradeoff
between precision and recall and correctness and completeness. After some testing,
it was chosen to go with two settings for the Tversky loss function, one configuration
with α = 0.7 and β = 0.3 and another with α = 0.3 and β = 0.7 to explore how
the parametrization affects crack detection and the influence on correctness and
completeness.
In this thesis, four different model architectures have been used: HED, FPN, U-Net, and U-
Net with DenseNet. To simplify the comparison between loss functions on various datasets,
the baseline U-Net model was chosen to be trained on GAPS384, CFD, and mixed datasets.
The models mentioned have been trained and tested without preprocessing applied. They
are then tested on our own labeled dataset. Route 1 was chosen for this purpose, as it
presents the least noisy pavement. The loss functions have been described in greater
detail in section 8.3.5.

9.5
Future works
The thesis objectives have been fulfilled. We have built a crack detection system using
line-scan camera technology, various calibration techniques, and deep learning methods,
making it scalable for future development at Dynatest. However, there are still areas
of the system that can be explored for greater accuracy, robustness, and real-world
implementation feasibility. Future work on the system can be categorized into three main
areas: camera, model, and mounting on the vehicle.
9.5.1
Camera
The choice of camera plays a crucial role in the quality of captured images and the system’s
feasibility.
Camera and Resolution Choice
It was decided early to go with a line-scan camera from Basler, as this technology allows
for high-speed image capturing with minimal motion blur. Initially, a 4k camera was used,
but this was later switched to a 2k camera. Both cameras fulfilled the spatial resolution
requirement. However, the exposure would need to be twice as high on the 4k camera
due to its smaller sensor size. In the future, it could make sense to switch back to the 4k
camera to allow for higher camera mounting while maintaining the spatial resolution of
below 1 mm if a more capable memory and illumination system is implemented.
Furthermore, synchronization and stitching of multiple cameras could be explored to create
a wider image covering the whole road. This approach would provide a notable advantage
for automated crack detection, allowing broader coverage in a single pass. Adding a stereo
pair of cameras could also be used for 3-D profiling; this helps estimate crack depth or
detect unevenness distresses that 2-D images might miss, giving it capabilities similar to
the LCMS system.
Calibration
The current camera calibration method corrects for lens and perspective distortion. The
process requires high precision due to the camera mount and the cardboard with the
calibration pattern. For future calibration, a more general setup could be made with a
Road Crack Detection Using Camera
89
==PAGE:100==
permanent mounting for the camera with a more precise pattern and precise adjustment
to working distance, allowing for easier and more accurate calibration. This would allow
for mass calibration if the system were to be mass-produced.
Future approaches could incorporate calibration patterns observed while the camera
system is in motion instead of only using a static calibration setup (1-D calibration). Area-
scan line-scan camera calibration methods have shown good results in the literature and
could have simplified the method, as a DMI is already calibrated to the system. This would
allow for even easier mass calibration, as the pattern would not have to be aligned with
the camera.
9.5.2
Model
The thesis presents a multitude of model architectures, loss functions, and datasets to
investigate what makes a great crack detection model. Due to the many factors involved in
training a deep learning segmentation model, the findings have been complex to interpret.
Our analysis has revealed clear patterns in model performance: Some approaches excel
broadly, others succeed only in specific scenarios, and a few demonstrate consistent short-
comings. This thorough analysis will help identify methods to enhance their effectiveness,
adaptability, and reliability in the future.
Expanding Labeled Datasets
In this thesis, two datasets have been captured by the prototype and manually labeled to
test the models’ performance on images captured by the system. Each dataset comprises
12 labeled images, making it suitable for testing but too small for training. Ideally, we would
train a model on various pavement types containing different types of cracks, making the
model more robust to varying kinds of pavement and increasing its precision by training
on data similar to its target data. The availability of public datasets that have labeled
multiple classes is also very scarce. By labeling the datasets in Dynatest, there would
be no permission issues, and they could be labeled with multiple classes, allowing for
the detection of different types of cracks, potholes, road lines, etc. This need is clearly
illustrated in image 9.5, which shows a maintenance hole cover that has been classified
as road cracks by our model due to it having trained on only non-cracked pavement
(background) and cracks in pavement (foreground). This would increase the road analysis
significantly and lead to higher precision and robustness, and the current precise LCMS
system could assist the work-heavy tasks of labeling.
Pavement images vary widely due to weather, lightning, and region-specific asphalt mixes.
One approach to address these differences is to use synthetic data generation. Synthetic
data is artificially created pavement images under various environmental conditions without
needing real images from various scenarios. This could, for example, be done in game
engines or procedural texture models. Another proven method is to synthetically generate
cracks in already existing datasets to increase the crack class, giving the model more
cases to learn about[54].
HED and FPN Tuning
While the U-Net variants had overall superior performance, the FPN and HED architectures
showed promise in detecting thin and discontinued cracks in noisy pavement (Route 2).
The poorer performance is likely due to insufficient utilization of the deep supervision
mechanism of these model architectures. The current models use fixed weights for the
side outputs. By adopting an attention-based mechanism for the side outputs, we could
enable the models to autonomously adjust the weights during training to adjust the learning
focus of the model to focus on regions with high crack probability.

Auto-encoder For Crack Connectivity
The best models presented in this thesis accurately detect the cracks, especially on
smooth asphalt, from dataset Route 1 as shown in image 9.2a. A common problem in
segmentation tasks for crack detection is the fragmentation of cracks, which results in the
model finding one continuous crack as multiple cracks due to a few pixels being classified
incorrectly, as seen on image 9.2a and 9.2b. This can be problematic when evaluating
the pavement conditions by PCSI, which is calculated by the length of the cracks and the
number of cracks on the pavement. An autoencoder solution could address this problem
by reconstructing fragmented cracks into coherent structures, significantly reducing the
problem of falsely fragmenting continuous cracks.
9.5.3
Vehicle Integration
This thesis outlines the effectiveness of line-scan camera technology with deep learning
methods for crack detection. A hand-driven prototype was created to emulate how the
system would work on a motor-driven vehicle. Most of the relevant considerations, such as
the encoder, camera, triggering circuit, and software, have been made to make it easy to
migrate the system from the prototype to a motor vehicle. Even with these considerations,
some challenges must be addressed to succeed.
Speed Adaptation
The prototype has been tested at a maximum of 6 km/h, where the final product ideally
should be able to operate at highway speeds at a minimum of 100 km/h (same as the
current LCMS system). The camera can capture images at this speed but with a slightly
lower exposure time of 27 µs compared to the 35 µs used on the darkest pavement images
on Route 1. This could be addressed by mounting the camera higher, reducing the spatial
resolution, and increasing the theoretical exposure time required to avoid motion blur.
The issue could also be addressed by increasing the system’s illumination, effectively
decreasing exposure time, and providing the same image quality.
Vibration and Wind Mitigation
Vibrations proved to be a great challenge on the prototype, solved by changing the wheels
to soft rubber to make lower-frequency vibrations that are not visible in the images. A motor
vehicle also has rubber wheels, which would result in the same low-frequency vibrations
from moving over the road. The motor vehicle brings other challenges, such as vibrations
from the motor. The wind and air resistance can also prove challenging when driving
at higher speeds, possibly pointing it to the non-illuminated pavement and ruining the
data. Most of these challenges can be addressed by mounting the camera inside a firmly
mounted box, shielding the camera from the elements, and reducing air resistance and
wind vibrations. The camera case would need to be mounted away from the motor to
prevent these vibrations from affecting the camera.
Data Management
The data throughput of the system is determined by the camera’s resolution and the
vehicle’s speed. With the vehicle traveling at 100 km/h, the data rate of the 2k line-scan
camera would be 4.55 GB/min or 273 GB/h. As discussed in section 6.2.5, this can be
reduced by utilizing image compression techniques, but even so, the system would still
need to store large amounts of data when data is collected over multiple hours. This
can be addressed by having large amounts of storage on the local server desktop in the
vehicle, making it necessary to clean the drives frequently after measurements. Another
way could be to make the segmentation run in real-time and save only the results, which
would greatly reduce the amount of stored data but require fast hardware to process the
images in real-time. Lastly, a combination of the solutions could be implemented where the
process in figure 9.1 is still followed. Still, after the offline processes have been completed,

Illumination Height and Camera Adjustments
The prototype’s LED light is mounted 12 cm above the ground, which is too close for a
motor vehicle. It would have to be raised to 30 cm above the ground, the same height as
the RSP system on the Dynatest vehicles. This would reduce the light output and require a
more powerful illumination source for the final system. The camera could also be mounted
at a higher position to capture a wider working area, but this would have increased the
spatial resolution.

Conclusion
This thesis presents the feasibility of a cost-effective, camera-based automated road crack
detection system using deep learning. By integrating the Basler line-scan camera with
our custom-made LED illumination array, high-quality images of dark pavement can be
captured with an exposure time down to 25 µs with a resolution of 0.75 mm/pixel. The
system has been built as a prototype and modified to remove high-frequency vibrations
and allow for effective real-world testing. Calibration routines ensure accurate geometry
with the correction of lens and perspective distortions, as well as DMI corrections. Live
stitching of images is accomplished through a PyPylon pipeline with camera triggers from
a DMI. An external light correction feature and adaptive exposure controls enhance the
consistency of environmental changes.
The high-quality data captured by the prototype has been tested on multiple deep learning
architectures and loss functions trained with different public datasets, revealing how various
factors affect crack segmentation performance. These experiments demonstrated how the
system’s performance hinges on a balance between dataset diversity, model architecture,
and loss function selection. Architectures combining spatial and semantic context, such as
U-Net with DenseNet, proved more effective at handling pronounced cracks on smoother
surfaces. This architecture also performed well in finding thin discontinued cracks on rough
surfaces when preprocessing was enabled.
While typical metrics (e.g., precision, recall, IoU) correlated well with visual outputs, they
only reflected the pixel overlap and not the subtle connectivity of cracks. This led to the
introduction of geometry-based metrics (e.g., correctness, completeness, and quality),
which serve as more topology-aware assessments.
The combination of precise image capturing with data-driven segmentation promises a
low-cost system with high-accuracy pavement analysis.

Figure F.1 shows a UML diagram of the data acquisition module. The top box shows the
LineScanCamera class, its variables, and functions. The class variables include camera
settings such as exposure and gamma, which allow for changing these camera settings
in the program. The module has many functions; most are required for the program to
work, while others offer different functionality. The capture_image functions have the
same core functionality: capturing and storing images. The core functionality comes from
the capture_image function, inspired by the official Pylon sample code [73] “USB_lines-
can_performance_demo_opencv.py”. The function captures images and stitches them in a
predefined size; the function terminates when the stitching is complete. The capture_image
dynamic function will keep capturing lines and stitching them until the user terminates
the script. The function also utilizes the setup_output_folder, which prompts the user to
define a folder to save the images and the save_images function. This led to the final
function capture_image_machine_learning, optimized by implementing threading to save
images and input the user interrupt. The final function will continuously take images like the
dynamic function but will save the images in user-defined sizes like the first capture_image
function. The function capture_image_exposure_correction adds the automatic exposure
control with the PID controller to the functionality from capture_image_machine_learning
with its structure and light correction.
The bottom box shows the class’s main function. This implementation was chosen because
it is flexible in future development. The class can be called from Dynatest’s other software
solutions with the chosen structure.
