of Environmental and Resource Engineering at the Technical University of Denmark under the supervision 
of Associate Professor Michael Zwicky Hauschild and Co-supervisor: Eldbjørg Blikra Vea. The MSc thesis 
was conducted in fulfillment of the requirements for acquiring an MSc degree in Environmental

Summary 
Agriculture is responsible for over 25% of global greenhouse gas emissions and 80% of global 
deforestation. Fertilizer use, agricultural N fixation, sewage discharge, and NOx emissions from fossil fuels 
contribute to overabundance of N causing hypoxia in coastal waters. Conventional agriculture dominates 
Danish agriculture, but organic agriculture is gaining popularity worldwide as a more sustainable 
alternative. More research on the absolute sustainability of agricultural systems is needed, both to identify 
a direction for developing sustainable agricultural practices, and to test the AESA method and possible 
limitations for such systems.  
The overall aim of this study was to address how the environmental performance of the agriculture sector 
can be improved and potentially become absolute sustainable (focusing on N emissions). This was done 
through an assessment and comparison of the environmental performance of various spring barley 
agricultural production systems. The project was carried out looking at three main objectives. Firstly, the 
study investigated how different agricultural production systems influence the N cycle and identified the 
most suitable N emission model for fair comparison. Secondly, a life cycle assessment (LCA) was 
conducted to compare four different spring barley production systems and address shortcomings in previous 
LCA studies regarding modelling N emissions. Finally, an absolute environmental sustainability 
assessment (AESA) was made for marine eutrophication impacts and assessed whether these four systems 
are absolutely sustainable. 
The study identified the SQCB-NO3 regression model as the most suitable model for N emissions and 
incorporated additional formulas and methods to calculate all N input and output. The research also found 
that an increased amount of organic carbon in the soil, deeper root length and better soil conditions etc. can 
reduce nitrate leaching. 
Based on the LCA results, it was found that the RE production system has the lowest impact score in 17 
out of 18 impact categories. The conventional production system has the lowest impact score in land use, 
while organic has the highest impact score in 12 categories, and CA has the highest impact score in 6 
categories. The highest normalized impact category was marine eutrophication, with the highest score in 
conventional and organic production systems. The LCA results indicate that the RE production system has 
the lowest environmental impact score in most impact categories, while organic and conventional systems 
have the highest impact scores in several categories. The process contribution analysis showed that the use 
of diesel in agricultural machinery and N emissions from fertilizer use are the most significant contributors 
to several impact categories.

In this study, a spatially resolved assessment of the AESA for marine eutrophication impacts showed that 
all four systems were sustainable in most potential locations. However, in 159, 94, 43, and 12 locations, the 
conservational, organic, CA, and RE production systems, respectively, were considered unsustainable (their 
values were above 1). When using the VA sharing principle, very few points were below 1 for all systems 
(36/601 for conventional, 39/601 for organic, 49/601 for CA and 55/601 for RE)  meaning that they are not 
sustainable for most locations. The sharing principles applied in this study may not be appropriate for food 
products, especially considering the VA principle. 
The study has identified several limitations in the data, including the need to improve the N model and 
consider the nutrient content of animal manure. Additionally, the diesel use process could impact the 
validity of the study's conclusions. The study's assumptions about carbon dioxide uptake by plants may not 
always be accurate, and the scenarios analyzed were limited to focus on one type of catch crop type and 
cannot be generalized for all production systems in Denmark. 
Based on the study's findings, we recommend that the agriculture sector prioritize reducing diesel use, 
limiting the use of fertilizers, stop using synthetic pesticides, increasing soil organic matter, using more 
catch crops and creating healthy soil conditions while also reducing crop waste. These recommendations 
can make agriculture more sustainable and reduce negative environmental impacts. While there are 
limitations to this study, it provides valuable insights into the environmental impacts of different 
agricultural production systems and highlights areas for further research and improvement. Which include 
examining more agricultural production systems, considering the nutrient content of animal manure, 
developing accurate N emission models, and incorporating land degradation, biodiversity, ecosystem 
services, etc., into the LCA. It is recommended that more sharing principles should be tested to improve 
the AESA part of the study.  It is also recommended to conduct as AESA including additional impact 
categories, in order to assess whether the production systems are sustainable considering e.g. climate change 
and ocean acidification impacts.  
This project contributes to the understanding of the environmental impact of agriculture production systems 
and provides a foundation for developing more sustainable agricultural practices.

Introduction 
1.1 The agricultural sector and its impact on the environment  
The agriculture sector is contributing to more than 25 % of all global greenhouse gas emissions 
(Springmann et al., 2016). Furthermore, 80% of global deforestation is caused by agriculture and 53% of 
threatened terrestrial species are negatively impacted by agriculture (Tanentzap et al., 2015). Also fertilizer 
use, agricultural N fixation, sewage discharge, and NOx emissions from fossil fuels, leads to an 
overabundance of N causing a decrease in oxygen levels which cause hypoxia which is an escalating 
environmental problem having an effect on the world’scoastal waters, leading to consequences for marine 
life (Beusen et al., 2016; Breitburg et al., 2018; Diaz & Rosenberg, 2008; Sobota et al., 2013; Vaquer-
Sunyer & Duarte, 2008; Zhang et al., 2013). 
Seeking more sustainable ways to do farming, alternative methods such as organic agriculture has gained 
more popularity around the globe. The aim by the European Commission’s Green Deal is to have 25 % of 
all agriculture land in EU under organic agriculture by 2030 (The European Commission, 2018). 
The Danish parliament decided in 2020 to reduce national CO2 emissions by 70 % in 2030 (The Danish I’s 
Climate Partnerships, 2020). Where Danish farming is assumed to be accountable for 25 % of the national 
emissions total (Nielsen Ole-Kenneth et al., 2020). At the same time, the Danish farming sector is aiming 
to become climate neutral by 2050 (Danish Agriculture and Food Council, 2019). Loss of biodiversity has 
furthermore created more awareness about farming’s impact on ecosystems and it is therefore farmers’ 
obligation to find way to take care of this (Potts et al., 2016).  
Danish agricultural activities, including the use of fertilizers, can also result in N emissions that contribute 
to elevated levels of N in the environment (de Notaris et al., 2018; Nitschelm et al., 2018). This excess N 
can have negative impacts on air and water quality, as well as on soil health. N emissions to soil, air and 
water from agriculture might have several unfavorable effects on different impact categories. Nitrous oxide 
(N2O) emissions, and Nitrogen oxides (NOx) form acid when interacting with water, oxygen, and other 
chemicals, contributing to acidification has a negative impact on climate change (Firschknecht & Jolliet, 
2016). Nitrate (𝑁𝑂3
− ) emission exposure to aquatic systems has a negative impact on marine eutrophication 
(Wolf et al., 2010). Pollution of groundwater due to 𝑁𝑂3
− leaching have a negative impact on the freshwater 
resource quality (Ward et al., 2018). Excess N and phosphorus in the water can cause algal blooms on the 
coast. Algal blooms can harm marine ecosystems, reduce the oxygen levels in the water, and lead to the 
formation of dead zones, where aquatic life cannot survive. In addition, some algal blooms can produce 
toxic compounds that are harmful to humans and wildlife (Diaz & Rosenberg, 2008; Vaquer-Sunyer & 
Duarte, 2008; Vea et al., 2022a; Zhang et al., 2013). 
This study will have a focus on the environmental impacts of spring barley, focusing on its nutrient 
emissions and impacts on marine eutrophication. Spring barley grain is one of the most widely grown crops 
in Denmark. Spring barley is a cereal grain that is widely cultivated around the world and is used for a 
variety of purposes, including the production of beer, whiskey, and other alcoholic beverages, as well as 
animal feed, flour, and other food products (Landburg og Fødevarer, 2022). In Denmark, spring barley is a 
popular crop due to its ability to thrive in cool, temperate climates and its ability to be grown as a rotation 
crop in coexistence with other grains or oilseeds. It is also a major export crop for Denmark, with the 
country exporting significant quantities of barley to other European countries, as well as to other parts of 
the world (YARA, 2023). 
The most common agricultural methods in Denmark are conventional and organic, but conventional 
agriculture dominates Danish agriculture, where organic agriculture makes up 12% of farmland (Hermann 
==PAGE:8==
8 
 
et al., 2019). Conventional agriculture has some clear and tangible benefits that can be seen within a very 
short time frame, such as higher yields, lower prices, and more efficient land use. However, it also has 
significant disadvantages, including soil degradation, negative impacts on biodiversity, and the use of 
fertilizers and pesticides that can have adverse effects on the environment and human health etc. While 
organic agriculture does not use pesticides, it typically requires more land area to produce the same amount 
of crops. Organic farming may also face challenges in terms of higher labor costs and potential lower yields 
compared to conventional farming, particularly in the short term. There are other approaches that should be 
investigated to see if we can make the agricultural sector more sustainable. In addition to conventional and 
organic cultivation of spring barley, two alternative approaches—conservation and regenerative 
agriculture—will be investigated in this research. Conservation agriculture focuses on minimal soil 
disturbance, permanent soil cover, and diverse crop rotations and can improve soil stability, water retention, 
and carbon sequestration (Hobbs et al., 2008a). Regenerative agriculture takes a holistic approach to soil 
and ecosystem health, with no synthetic inputs, and aims to regenerate and revitalize the ecosystem 
(LaCanne & Lundgren, 2018). However, the effectiveness of regenerative agriculture may vary 
depeendingon context. Both conservation and regenerative agriculture have potential benefits for soil 
health, crop yields, and reducing environmental impact (Hobbs et al., 2008a). 
1.2 Assessing the environmental performance and sustainability of agricultural systems 
To guide the agriculture sector in a more sustainable direction, Life Cycel Assessment (LCA) can be used, 
which is a widely accepted and used methodology for assessing impacts from agriculture (Nitschelm et al., 
2018; Notarnicola et al., 2017). However, researchers often point out that LCA lacks some very decisive 
factors when, for example, comparing organic and conventional agriculture (Klöpffer & Curran Ann, 2014; 
Meier et al., 2015; Montemayor et al., 2022; Pryshlakivsky & Searcy, 2021; van der Werf et al., 2020; 
Viana et al., 2022; van der Werf et al., 2020; Klöpffer & Curran Ann, 2014; BIFL, 2017; Meemken & 
Qaim, 2018; Debuschewitz & Sanders, 2022; Viana et al., 2022) . The LCA method often does not include 
factors such as biodiversity, soil quality and pesticide effects on non-target species, which are of great 
importance in relation to agriculture’s impact on the environment. Van der Werf et al., (2020) highlight that 
the current use of the method may lead to incorrect conclusions about ecological conditions and land use, 
respectively. When analyzing and comparing the environmental performance of organic agriculture and 
conventional agriculture LCA, organic agriculture is not always identified as the most sustainable way of 
farming. Indeed, in the review of Meier et al. (2015), 34 comparative LCA studies show a lower 
environmental impact for organic product per area, but a higher impact when evaluating emissions per 
product unit. However, the question is whether current LCA capture all relevant effects of different ways 
of farming and hence provides a fair basis of comparison.  The lack of LCA research on alternative farming 
techniques for food production, such as regenerative and conservational crop production, supports the 
relevance of this work. 
It is suggested in Meier et al. (2015) that more representative background data on organic farming should 
be used. When doing a comparative LCA on agricultural product systems, the specific characteristics from 
different agricultural systems are often not sufficiently differentiated for organic and conventional 
agriculture systems on the inventory level. An example is that N emissions calculations are usually based 
on the same assumptions for conventional and organic agriculture even though very different assumption 
should most likely be considered (Meier et al., 2015). Meier et al. (2015) also identified that the most critical 
point when analyzing the environmental performance of different agricultural production systems was 
related N-emissions, influencing eutrophication, acidification and global warming potential and 
biodiversity.  
==PAGE:9==
9 
 
Furthermore, to be able to assess whether a product is sustainable (or not), conventional LCA is not 
sufficient. For this, absolute environmental sustainability assessment (AESA) is needed, which is a method 
that can address whether production systems can be considered absolutely environmentally sustainable, by 
relating the environmental performance of the system to environmental boundaries, delimiting the safe 
operating space (SOS) of the system (Bjørn, Chandrakumar, et al., 2020; Ryberg et al., 2018b). For 
example, the nine boundaries that defines a safe operating space where humanity can survive, thrive, and 
develop for generations to come (Rockström et al., 2009; Steffen et al., 2015). Being able to identify 
methods of farming that can be considered absolute sustainable will enable decisions makers to put focus, 
money, and research on the right method (if existing) and not spending more effort on trying to optimize 
methods that will never become good enough in an environmental sustainability perspective. While AESA 
is still an emerging field, the assessment of agricultural systems is still scarce and limited to Bjørn, Sim, et 
al., (2020). 
More research on the absolute sustainability of agricultural systems is needed, both to identify a direction 
for developing sustainable agricultural practices, and to test the AESA method and possible limitations for 
such systems.  
1.3 Aim and objectives of this study 
The overall aim of this project is to address how the environmental performance of the agriculture sector 
could be improved and potentially become absolute sustainable considering N emissions, by providing a 
thorough assessment and comparison of the environmental performance and absolute sustainability of 
various spring barley agricultural production systems. Focusing on N-emissions, we intend to overcome 
some of the limitations in the current LCA highlighted in section 1.2.  
Specifically, three objectives are defined, in order to achieve the overall aim: 
1. Investigate how different agricultural production systems influences the N cycle and to identify 
whether life cycle inventory (LCI) models are capable of modelling potential N cycle differences, 
shortlisting the most suitable N emission model and suggest necessary improvements for more fair 
comparison. 
2. Make an LCA comparing four different spring barley production systems, where shortcomings in 
previous LCA studies regarding modelling N emissions identified in objective 1 are addressed. The 
four agricultural systems subject to the assessment are: 1. Conventional, 2. Organic, 3. 
Conservational (CA) and 4. Regenerative (RE). 
3. Make an AESA for marine eutrophication impacts and assess whether these four systems are 
absolute sustainable. A modified spatially resolved AESA method focusing on marine 
eutrophication will be used (Vea et al., 2022a) . This case study will look at whether this new 
method captures the differences between the use and leaching of N in the four different systems.

2.1 The N cycle  
  
Figure 2: Simplified version of the entire N cycle and associated emissions. Field emission refer to the two emission flows: 
ammonia and Nitrogen oxide. Soil emission refer to the three emission flows: dinitrogen monoxide, N and nitrate.*Catch crop 
and plant residues are not contributing to field emissions. If not removed from the soil, they become a part of the soil and act like 
fertilizer for the next year’s crops and therefore contributes to the soil emissions. 
Figure 1 illustrate the N cycle, presenting the N supply and the various N forms it can take. It also shows 
that runoff and erosion are excluded which is explained further in section 2.3. The figure also illustrates the 
field emissions, defined as those emissions occurring when inorganic and organic fertilizers remain on top 
of the soil and have not yet entered it. This consists of volatilization (NH3), nitrification (NOx). Soil 
emissions are defined as when the fertilizers have already entered the soil and consist of nitrate leaching 
(NO3-) and denitrification (N2O and N2). As shown in Figure 1, nitrous oxide (N2O) can be produced by 
denitrification in an anaerobic environment directly from nitrate (NO3-) (Andrade et al., 2021). 
Plants adapted to calcareous soils typically utilize nitrate, while plants adapted to more acidic soils best 
utilize ammonium meaning that crop in agriculture in Denmark mainly utilize nitrate (NO3-) which is behind 
the dashed arrow from ammonium (NH4+) to the crop (Pedersen & Christensen, 2008). 
The oxidation state of N is an important factor to consider in agriculture as it affects the availability and 
mobility of N in the soil. The oxidation state of N can vary from -3 to +5, depending on the type of N 
==PAGE:12==
12 
 
compound. For instance, ammonium has an oxidation state of -3, while nitrate has an oxidation state of +5. 
Nitrate is highly oxygenated, whereas N in its elemental form is strongly reduced and has a low oxidation 
state (Schachtman et al., 1998). 
Ammonium has a lower oxidation state than nitrate, making it less soluble in water and relatively stable. 
However, in calcareous soils, ammonium may be less available to plants as it tends to form insoluble 
compounds with calcium. Ammonium can be transformed into other N compounds, such as nitrate, through 
nitrification. This conversion process can benefit plants as nitrate is a more readily available form of N that 
can be taken up by roots. Nitrate has a higher oxidation state and is much more soluble in water, making it 
more mobile in the soil. This mobility can lead to nitrate leaching and contribute to environmental problems 
such as eutrophication of water bodies (Schachtman et al., 1998). 
2.2 Defining the agricultural production systems 
The four agricultural production systems subject to assessment are organic, conventional, conservational 
(CA) and regenerative (RE). They all consist of different agricultural principles. An overview of 
agricultural principles included in the four production systems addressed in this study can be seen in Table 
1, together with their potential benefits and drawbacks. A more detailed description of the principles can 
be found in Appendix F1. 
Table 1: Explanation of the agricultural principles that are used in the different agricultural production systems assessed in this 
study.  
Principles 
Description 
Reduced tillage 
Reduced tillage is a farming method that involves minimizing soil disturbance during crop growth, 
often by reducing the use of machines such as plows and stubble harvesters. Instead, a specialized no-
till sowing machine is used for planting (Villumsen & Hellebek, 2022). While weed removal still 
occurs, deep tilling is avoided, and harrowing is often used instead (LaCanne & Lundgren, 2018). 
Benefits of reduced tillage include improved soil health, higher crop yields, and reduced soil 
emissions. However, this approach may require more labor to manage weeds and other challenges 
(Haddaway et al., 2017; Kamal et al., 2019; Mungai et al., 2014; van Kessel et al., 2013). For more 
details see appendix F1. 
Animal manure  
Animal manure is a nutrient-rich waste product produced by farm animals like cows, chickens, and 
pigs, containing high levels of N, phosphorus, potassium, and other micro-nutrients. It is commonly 
used as an organic fertilizer, spread over fields or tilled into the soil in some agricultural production 
systems. Manure can also be transformed into compost, which breaks down the manure into an even 
more effective fertilizer (Admin, 2019). The benefits of using animal manure as an organic fertilizer 
include slow nutrient release, which can prevent over-fertilization, and improved soil structure, which 
promotes the growth of beneficial microorganisms. However, drawbacks may include slower plant 
growth compared to inorganic fertilizers and the need for more frequent application (Agriculture and 
Technology, 2017; Tuomisto et al., 2012; van der Werf et al., 2020). For more details see appendix 
F1. 
Catch crops  
 
Catch crops are grown in between or after main crops, and in this project, I investigate catch crops 
grown as a follow-up to the primary crop. A key benefit of catch crops is their ability to absorb excess 
N, thereby preventing leaching (de Notaris et al., 2018; Kirchmann & Bergström, 2001). Once 
harvested, the catch crops are left on the field and incorporated into the soil, providing organic 
fertilizer for the subsequent main crop the following year. This is particularly important given the 
amount of fertilizer and/or animal manure applied to the field. By serving as organic fertilizer, catch 
crops can impact NOx and NH3 emissions, leaching, and the amount of animal manure or inorganic 
fertilizer required for the field (de Notaris et al., 2018; O’Connell et al., 2014; Petersen et al., 2006; 
Zhao et al., 2020). Additionally, catch crops share similar benefits and drawbacks with animal manure. 
For more details see appendix F1. 
Inorganic fertilizer 
Inorganic fertilizers, which are synthetic materials produced in a laboratory, are used to add essential 
nutrients to the soil for plant growth. These fertilizers often contain a combination of the three primary 
macronutrients, Nitrogen (N), phosphorus (P), and potassium (K), along with other micronutrients that 
plants need. In conventional agriculture, inorganic fertilizers are widely used due to their ease of 
application, ability to provide a quick boost to plant growth, and capacity to address specific nutrient 
==PAGE:13==
13 
 
deficiencies. However, if not used carefully, they can also have negative environmental impacts, such 
as the pollution of water sources (Agriculture and Technology, 2017; GEUS, 2014). For more details 
see appendix F1. 
Pesticides  
Pesticides are used in Danish agriculture to control pests, diseases, and weeds, but their use is 
regulated to minimize risks to human health and the environment. Pesticides can help protect crops, 
but they can also harm non-target organisms and the environment. Integrated pest management (IPM) 
strategies are becoming more popular in Denmark to reduce pesticide use and minimize negative 
impacts. One positive aspect of pesticides is that they can improve crop yields and quality, but a 
negative aspect is that they can also harm beneficial organisms and contribute to environmental 
pollution if used improperly (Pretty, 2008).  
Weed harrow 
Weed harrowing is a non-chemical method used to control weeds by uprooting young weed seedlings. 
It is a sustainable alternative to pesticides because it doesn't introduce chemicals into the environment. 
However, weed harrowing requires frequent and timely operations to be effective, and can be time-
consuming and labor-intensive. One potential downside of weed harrowing is that it may not be as 
effective at controlling more mature weeds. On the positive side, weed harrowing can help prevent 
weed populations from building up and competing with crops. Additionally, it can improve soil health 
by reducing soil compaction and promoting aeration (Bajwa, 2014).  
The different principles (reduced tillage, growing of catch crops, use of fertilizer etc.) can be combined in 
various ways and to a higher and lower degree in the different production systems which is seen in Figure 
3. For example, a high amount of catch crop in the CA and RE production systems and a lower amount in 
organic and conventional production systems.

In the following paragraphs the concept and key characteristics of the four production systems will be

Conventional farming remains the dominant form of agriculture in Denmark, as well as in other parts of the 
world, as previously discussed in section 1.1. The core of this production system is the use of synthetic 
inputs, including inorganic fertilizers, pesticides, and genetically modified seeds. Animal manure is also 
commonly used as a form of fertilizer together with catch crops, which is used to protect the soil and provide 
organic fertilizer for the following year. The combined principles used is catch crops, animal manure and 
inorganic fertilizer which is also seen in Figure 3 (B. Hansen et al., 2000; Petersen et al., 2006; planta, 
2016). 
As mentioned in section 1.1, organic farming is the second most used agricultural method in Denmark, and 
it continues to grow each year with the creation or reorganization of farmland into organic systems. In this 
==PAGE:14==
14 
 
production system, animal manure is used as fertilizer, and pesticides or inorganic fertilizers are not added. 
Instead, harrowing is used for weed removal, and 30% catch crops are used to protect the soil, which then 
serves as organic fertilizer for the following year. Thus, catch crops and animal manure are the principles 
combined in the organic farming system which is seen in Figure 3 (Agriculture and Technology, 2017; 
Kirchmann & Bergström, 2001; Meemken & Qaim, 2018; Rodale Institute, 2022). 
In conservation agriculture (CA), agricultural principles used is minimal soil disturbance, permanent soil 
cover, and diverse crop rotations (Hobbs et al., 2008b). In 2018, almost 39,000 ha were cultivated according 
to the CA principles in Denmark. This corresponds to 1.6 percent of the total agricultural area and that is 
increasing these years (SEGES, 2021b). One of the reasons why this has been increasing in recent years is 
that more and more farmers have become aware of the properties of the soil in relation to the climate. They 
experience wet autumns, where it is difficult to establish crops, and they experience several periods of 
drought. With CA, the soil is stabilized so that it becomes both more passable and better able to retain water 
(SEGES, 2021b). The minimal soil disturbance, permanent soil cover and diverse crop rotation can also 
help to increase the amount of carbon sequestered in the soil, which can help to offset greenhouse gas 
emissions (Hobbs et al., 2008b).  In the CA production system in this study, animal manure is utilized as 
fertilizer, while pesticides are employed to remove weeds. In addition, 90% of the land is covered with 
catch crops to protect the soil, which is later used as organic fertilizer in the subsequent year. All the 
principles are combined in this production system also seen in Figure 3 (Christensen, 2022; SEGES, 2021b). 
The Regenerative agriculture (RE) production system is lacking a clear definition and is very differently 
understood around the world (Schreefel et al., 2020), but it is a more holistic approach than the other three 
systems which aims to regenerate and revitalize the health of the soil and ecosystem (Schreefel et al., 2020). 
It is based on the idea that by using farming practices that optimize the natural processes of healthy 
ecosystems, it is possible to build soil health, increase crop productivity long-term, and create more resilient 
and sustainable agriculture systems (LaCanne & Lundgren, 2018). Most of the principles of regenerative 
agriculture are like the CA production systems, but in the RE production system there is no synthetic input 
added (no inorganic fertilizer and no pesticides), which causes an even more complex and detailed use of 
these principles to create an agriculture that works in practice (Newton et al., 2020). Some promoters of 
regenerative agriculture argue that it has the potential to not only sustain but also strengthen the natural 
environment and contribute to a better natural environment and healthier ecosystem (Villumsen & 
Hellebek, 2022). However, it is important to note that the effectiveness of regenerative agriculture practices 
can vary depending on the specific context and farming operation (Newton et al., 2020). In the RE 
production system in this study, animal manure is utilized as fertilizer, while harrowing is done to remove 
weeds. In addition, 90% of the land is covered with catch crops to protect the soil, which is later used as 
organic fertilizer in the following year. All the principles except inorganic fertilizer are combined in this 
production system also seen in Figure 3 (SEGES, 2021b).  
Conservation agriculture focuses on conserving and enhancing natural resources, while regenerative 
agriculture aims to actively regenerate and revitalize the health of the soil and ecosystem. Both production 
systems can have many benefits, including improving soil health, increasing crop yields, and reducing the 
negative impacts of agriculture on the environment. However, regenerative agriculture takes a more holistic 
and progressive approach to build soil and ecosystem health on many levels, while conservation agriculture 
focuses on minimizing soil disturbance and conserving natural resources (LaCanne & Lundgren, 2018; 
(Hobbs et al., 2008).

2.3 Part 1A:  Effect of agricultural practices on N flows 
In the agricultural N cycle, organic N is derived from various sources such as seeds, catch crops, plant 
residues, animal manure, and inorganic fertilizers, or it can also arise from ammonium immobilization. N 
exists in mainly four different forms, namely atmospheric N, organic N, ammonium, and nitrate, as shown 
in Figure 1. Organic N cannot be taken up or utilized by plants but is the form that contributes to field 
emissions. Ammonium, on the other hand, is a useful form of N that can be taken up and used by plants, 
and is created through the mineralization of organic N, as well as deposition or fixation of atmospheric N. 
Nitrate is produced through the nitrification of ammonium and can also come directly from inorganic 
fertilizers. Plants can use nitrate, but it is also a form that can cause soil emissions, including leaching. 
Runoff and erosion is not considered in this study due to the Danish landscape conditions; no slope in the 
researched agricultural fields and relatively high granular structure and porosity (Munkholm, 2022; 
Rasmussen, 2022; Thierry, 2022). 
N deposition seen in Figure 2 section 2.1 refers to the atmospheric intake of reactive N into the biosphere 
as dry deposition, gases, and wet deposition as precipitation (APIS, 2016). Atmospheric N deposit on an 
average Danish agriculture land was set to 7-12 kg N per ha per year.  
N fixation in soil seen in Figure 2 section 2.1 is important for agriculture because even though air contains 
78% N, it is not the N that plants can consume right away (Hanrahan & Chan, 1997). N fixation is the 
process by which N is taken from its molecular form (N2) in the atmosphere and converted into ammonium 
==PAGE:16==
16 
 
(NH4+) that useful for plants which can take it up by the roots (Hanrahan & Chan, 1997; de Notaris et al., 
2018). This can happen in two ways. One is biological N fixation (BNF): N gas (N2) diffuses into the soil 
from the atmosphere, and different species of bacteria convert this N to ammonium ions (NH4+), which can 
then be used by plants. The other one is atmospheric N fixation: which is lightning that converts 
atmospheric N into ammonia and nitrate that enter soil with rainfall which is integrated in BNF when 
measuring that. Therefor when taking about BNF it normally includes atmospheric N fixation (Hanrahan 
& Chan, 1997).  𝑁𝑓𝑖𝑥𝑎𝑡𝑖𝑜𝑛 (BNF) is very hard to find data on, since the fixation can be challenging to 
quantify (Børgesen et al., 2019). Two articles were found with measured data ( 𝑁𝑓𝑖𝑥𝑎𝑡𝑖𝑜𝑛= 0.3-7. kg 
N/ha*year (Vitousek et al., 2013) and 𝑁𝑓𝑖𝑥𝑎𝑡𝑖𝑜𝑛 9-24 kg N/ha*year (Børgesen et al., 2019)). Several factors 
can influence fixation, for example fungi’s, which increase fixation. Reduced tillage can increase the 
content of fungi’s in soil (Mungai et al., 2014). Reduced or no-tillage was found to have a significant impact 
on the amount of N that was fixed from the atmosphere, fixating two and three times the amount of normal 
conventional tillage system, respectively (Faligowska et al., 2022).  
N-inputs are different in organic and conventional agriculture, where external N inputs on conventional 
production systems are usually higher than for organic production system (Meier et al., 2015). Organic N 
can be added to the soil from organic fertilizer, animal manure and biosolids, plant residues and biological 
fixation be legume plants. Organic N is being converted from the organic form to an inorganic form by 
mineralization, to be available for uptake by plants. Inorganic N mainly includes ammonium (NH4+), 
nitrates (NO3-), N gas (N2) and nitrites (NO2-) (Andrade et al., 2021). 
The different principles can influence volatilization (Andrade et al., 2021). According to The European 
Commission, (2018) organic fertilizers emits more ammonia (NH3) with an emission factor on 0.24 kg 
NH3/kg N applied and 0.12 kg NH3/kg N applied for inorganic fertilizers.  
According to many studies (Berntsen et al., 2004; B. Hansen et al., 2000; Børgesen et al., 2019 ; de Notaris 
et al., 2018) a difference in leaching from organic vs inorganic fertilizer is not identified. However, other 
studies argue that the N leaching is typically lower from organic farming than conventional agricultural 
systems due to the composition of the more extensive use of catch crops and the lower total input of N 
added to the organic agricultural system (B. Hansen et al., 2000). (Kirchmann & Bergström, (2001) also 
argues N leaching is influenced by whether catch crops are used of if the soil is bare. In conservational 
agriculture systems one of the important principles is precisely to keep the soil covered with plants all year 
round (Montcalm & Vestergaard, 2021). In Kirchmann & Bergström (2001) there is no correlation found 
between yields and leaching loads, not in organic neither in conventional agriculture. However, there was 
a linear relationship between leaching loads and N application in both systems (Kirchmann & Bergström, 
2001). This means that when the same amount of N is added to the two agricultural systems, there would 
be the same amount of N leaching for both systems. The systems N2O emissions are also significant 
determinant by the N input ((Petersen et al., 2006). 
Seges (Montcalm & Vestergaard, 2021) has carried out a study showing that there is a greater risk of 
leaching from plowed land than from agriculture using the reduced tillage principle. The results of 70 soil 
samples taken in the autumn of first-year wheat in 15 plowed fields, 19 harrowed fields and 35 direct-sown 
fields in 2019 and 2020 show a statistically reliable difference in N-min content in the soil with different 
tillage methods. This means that the mineralization of N in the soil is affected by the intensity of tillage. 
The study found that the N-min content in 0-100 cm soil depth was statistically higher in plowed soil 
compared to harrowed soil and uncultivated soil. The release of N from the soil’s pools therefore goes faster 
in a plowed soil (Montcalm & Vestergaard, 2021). 
==PAGE:17==
17 
 
A large part of the tillage in Danish fields takes place precisely in August and September before sowing 
winter seed. And these are also months with high temperatures that favor N mineralization. According to 
the study carried out from Seges 10 kg N more per ha on the plowed lands is lost in the form of leaching 
(Montcalm & Vestergaard, 2021). 
Despite the focus of this study being on N flows, we also investigated how the different agricultural 
practices can influence carbon flows.  
The agricultural sector significantly contributes to greenhouse gas emissions, particularly through tillage, 
which releases carbon bound in the soil and converts it to CO2 when exposed to oxygen. However, 
increasing the level of organic carbon (corg) in the soil can reduce nitrate leaching into groundwater. High 
levels of corg can increase soil structure stability, water retention capacity, and microorganisms, leading to 
reduced erosion, increased crop yields, and a smaller amount of N available for leaching (Cooper et al., 
2021). 
Reduced tillage is a principle that can reduce agriculture's climate footprint by up to 30 percent. By 
minimizing tillage equipment use, it reduces carbon lost from soil through oxidation and erosion and 
increases crop residue on soil surfaces. The use of catch crops, which keep soil covered with plants or 
residues, can also reduce erosion, and prevent carbon loss (Cooper et al., 2021). 
By adopting reduced tillage and catch crop principles, farmers can increase soil carbon storage, preserve 
soil structure, and reduce nitrate leaching, protecting groundwater. These practices can also have economic 
and environmental benefits. High levels of corg can lead to reduced nitrate leaching by increasing soil 
stability, water retention, and microorganisms. They can also facilitate anaerobic processes that convert 
nitrate into other N compounds, which are less mobile and less likely to enter groundwater (Hassan et al., 
2022). 
In summary, increasing organic carbon levels in soil can effectively reduce nitrate leaching and protect 
groundwater, while reduced tillage and catch crops can reduce agriculture's climate footprint and benefit 
soil health (Hassan et al., 2022). 
The effect the different agricultural practices have on N and carbon flows explained in this section was 
included in my models. How this was modelled, is explained in section 3.1.

3.1 Part 1B: N emission modelling 
In previous agriculture LCA studies, many different models have been used to estimate N emissions, 
unfortunately not always in a justified or suitable manner (Andrade et al., 2021). A literature screening was 
carried out to identify N models used in LCA and assess their suitability for comparing different agricultural 
production systems. Moreover, we defined criteria for selecting the most suitable model to be used in this 
study. See details on the literature search and model selection in Appendix A1 and A2. 
We did not identify any consensus model for modelling N emissions in the LCI phase of LCA (like e.g. 
pestLCI for pesticide applications), and none of the identified models can model the entire N cycle shown 
in Figure 2 in section 2.1. An ideal approach would be to use either a mechanistic model that can calculate 
all nitrogen (N) outputs based on a mass balance, or an empirical model that considers all N inputs and 
outputs simultaneously. Although this was not the case, 8 models was selected and evaluated for modeling 
N emissions in agricultural systems, using criteria outlined in appendix A2. The identified 8 models for 
modelling N emissions including comifer tailleur model (Comifer, 2015), PEF (The European Commission, 
2018), SALCA (Walter Richner et al., 2014), Daisy (University of Copenhagen, 2015), Animo 
(Groenendijk, 2006), FASSET ver 1.0 (Aarhus University, 2010), NLES5 (Børgesen et al., 2019)and 
SQCB-NO3 regression model (Wohlgemuth, 2009). 
 
The first step of the evaluation involved assessing the availability and language of the guidelines for each 
model. The Comifer Tailleur model (Comifer, 2015) and Animo (Groenendijk, 2006) was excluded due to 
lack of availability, and the CA model was excluded due to guidelines only being available in German. In 
step 2, we evaluated the models’ ability to consider all relevant N input, resulting in the exclusion of PEF 
(The European Commission, 2018). This was because PEF could not accommodate various N input 
parameters. In step 3, we evaluated other input parameters that could affect the model’s adaptability to 
different agricultural systems, resulting in the exclusion of NLES5 (Børgesen et al., 2019) as it was only 
able to incorporate irrigation as another input parameter. In step 4, we evaluated the remaining models for 
their user-friendliness and up-to-dateness. The Daisy (University of Copenhagen, 2015) model was found 
to be not user-friendly and FASSET ver 1.0 (Aarhus University, 2010) was found to be outdated. This 
resulted in the selection of the SQCB-NO3 regression model (Wohlgemuth, 2009) as the most suitable 
model for my research.  
SQCB-NO3 is an empirical model, that was developed to conduct sustainable quick check for leaching of 
nitrate from biofuel (Wohlgemuth, 2009), and use on leaching of nitrate in agriculture by  Bjørn, Sim, et 
al., (2020). The SQCB-NO3 regression model was then modified to facilitate better analysis of nitrate 
leaching in four production systems in this project. How this was done is explained in the following 
sections. We also incorporated additional formulas and methods to calculate the other field and soil 
emissions and to ensure the mass balance of the system where all N input and output should be included.

3.2.1 Goal definition 
The LCA performed as a part of this study intends to compare the four different agriculture production 
systems: 1. Conventional, 2. Organic, 3. CA and 4. RE (see section 2.2 for an overview), to see which

production systems to suggest optimization potential. 
This study is based in Denmark and all foreground data are from Danish localities therefor the projects 
conclusion are limited to these locations and cannot be used to make conclusion for similar agricultural 
production systems in other parts of the world.

3.2.2.2 
LCI Modelling framework 
The current LCA is considered situation A meaning microlevel decision support, which implies that system 
expansion is preferred when solving multifunctionality and average processes are used to model the 
background system of the study. As we will perform an AESA, allocation should be used. Meaning that in 
this project attributional LCA is performed using APOS (Allocation at the point of substitution), which 
mean that the product will carry some of the burden of the co-product in the different processes based on 
allocation. The allocation of the inputs and outputs of the systems between grain and wheat are based on 
the market value of the grain and straw. 
The animal manure from pigs used as fertilizer has a very low market value. Danish pig farmers would like 
to get rid of their manure, and it is therefore of great advantage that they can spread some of it on agricultural 
fields. Therefore, for animal manure, the cut-off version of the process of the Ecoinvent database was used, 
which is equal to animal manure having no market value. 
When possible, subdivision is always the best solution to multifunctionality. When grain and straw is 
harvested, they are separated and treated with different machines and actions which is happening in the life 
cycle stage 5 “postharvesting” seen in Figure 7. In this stage subdivision is taking place by dividing the 
different processes according to the product (grain) and co-product (straw) which is discussed further in 
section 3.2.3.3.

3.2.2.5 
Basis for Impact Assessment 
Based on the goal of this study, to find the most environmentally friendly agricultural production system 
and identify hotspots, we included all environmental impact categories to avoid trade-offs. The main 
advantage of utilizing a life cycle perspective is that it makes it possible to spot and prevent the burden-
shifting between processes or life cycle stages that occurs when efforts to reduce environmental impacts in 
one process or life cycle stage unintentionally result in (possibly larger) environmental impacts in other 
processes or life cycle stages (Hauschild et al., 2018). The method chosen to carry out the impact assessment 
is the ReCiPe 2016 excluding long-term emissions. Modelling impacts will be performed at midpoint level. 
The 18 impact categories included in the midpoint modelling method are: climate change (unit: kg CO2 
eq), fossil and nuclear energy use (MJ deprived), mineral resources use (kg deprived), photochemical 
oxidant formation (kg NMVOC eq), ozone layer depletion (kg CFC-11 eq), freshwater ecotoxicity (CTUe), 
human toxicity cancer (CTUh), human toxicity non-cancer (CTUh), freshwater acidification (kg SO2 eq), 
terrestrial acidification (kg SO2 eq), freshwater eutrophication (kg PO4 eq), marine eutrophication (kg N 
eq), particulate matter formation (kg PM2.5 eq), ionizing radiation (Bq C-14 eq), land transformation, 
biodiversity (m2 yr arable), land occupation, biodiversity (m2 yr arable) and water scarcity (m3 world eq) 
(Huijbregts et al., 2017).

& Lundgren, 2018; Nummer et al., 2018; Schreefel et al., 2020).

Across all four agricultural production systems, ammonia is the most significant airborne emission, 
accounting for 14-24% of all emissions. The primary source of ammonia pollution in the air is also farming, 
specifically the manure produced by livestock (Sommer et al., 2019). It is important to note that when it 
comes to ammonia emission, organic fertilizers have an emission factor (EF) that is twice as high as that of 
inorganic fertilizers. This means that the use of organic fertilizers contributes twice as much to ammonia 
emissions (see more in section 3.1.1.2). 
The nitrate loss through leaching is determined using the modified model described in section 3.1.1.3 which 
considers multiple parameters that may affect the leaching outcome to varying degrees (see appendix C). 
This is further explored in the sensitivity section 5, and it was essential to analyze these factors to identify 
the reasons for high and low leaching. The analysis revealed that the organic production system has a high 
leaching potential due to lower N uptake by crops, resulting from fewer crops being produced. Conversely, 
the conventional system has an even higher leaching potential due to the excessive use of inorganic 
fertilizers and large amounts of animal manure applied to the fields. In contrast, the CA production system 
demonstrates a lower leaching potential than the organic and conventional systems, primarily due to the 
high N uptake by catch crops, resulting from their extensive use in the production process. Finally, the RE 
production system has the lowest leaching potential due to the lowest N supply through fertilizer and high 
N uptake by catch crops due to the extensive use of catch crops on the field.

It is seen that when using the two different sharing principles, the results are very different. It is seen that 
for most locations (points in the figures), all four production systems in the baseline system are sustainable 
according to the sharing principle SQ (i.e. they are below 1 and does not occupy more than the SOS that 
was assigned to them). Is 159/601, 94/601, 43/601 and 12/601 locations the conservational, organic, CA 
and RE production systems can be considered unsustainable (their values are above 1). When using the VA 
principle, it is seen that very few points are below 1 for all systems (36/601 for conventional, 39/601 for 
organic, 49/601 for CA and 55/601 for RE), meaning that it is not sustainable for most locations. This can 
be explained by the relative high share to the grain product group (16% of all production groups) when 
looking at the emissions in SQ and only 0.21% when looking at VA of the production group compared to 
VA of all production in DK.  
It is out of the scope of this study to investigate the sharing principles in depth, but it is important to note 
that the choice of sharing principle is a critical part of AESA, as it is very subjective and has a high influence 
on the results (Ryberg et al., 2020) . The principles applied in this study are often used in AESA, but it can 
be discussed if they are appropriate to use for food products, especially considering the VA principle. Food 
products are necessary for our basic needs, but when applying to the VA, they are provided a share based 
on their economic value only. The economic value of food products is low, so they are assigned a small 
share, which does not consider their contribution to societal demands and needs. The GVA represents the 
value of the goods produced by the value chain, however, it excludes the degree of societal demands. The 
SQ principle favors product groups with historically higher emissions and gives them priority to continue 
with having the highest contribution to emissions. This can also be unfair to industries (or nations) with 
historically low emissions.

SOS of a specific riverbasin in a specific country. So it was not necessary to share a global SOS to Denmark, 
which is normally needed when doing an AESA.

RE and CA production systems, especially RE, have been found to be multifunctional, increasing

measured or assessed at a time.

kg/ha*year. This was done to explore its impact on the final LCA result, leading to a discussion about 
multifunctionality.

on several parameters. For example, how much of the N supply should come from animal manure, fixation, 
or catch crops that remain on the field? How much N is accumulated in the soil due to improved soil 
conditions, and how much is from increased N uptake resulting from longer and thicker root lengths? 
Moreover, how much N remains in the soil for the following year to reduce the need for external fertilizer 
supply? In this system, the N supply also depends on the amount of crop residues left on the field and how 
much is removed. Therefore, understanding the N flows in this system is crucial, and understanding the 
interconnectivity of the underlying factors is essential, which was also closely investigated in the study. 
Further investigation of this is particularly important for RE, which has been less studied in Danish 
agricultural land, especially since it is a new concept.

When including long-term emissions, toxicity impacts are often dominating, as the impact scores from 
metals are integrated over infinity. Besides considering the uncertainty in the modelling of the impacts, 
including long-term emissions or not presents a trade-off situation that should be considered. If including 
them, we represent all impacts, but they are in a way ‘diluted’ for the current generation when integrating 
over infinity, compared to when excluding long-term emissions, where the impacts may be large for current 
generations (when integrating over 100 years) (Hauschild et al., 2018). 
6 
Limitation of this study  
In this section limitations are explained. In potential future iterations of this study, one should focus on 
improving these limitations. 
N emission parameters: The study identified "N supply through fertilizer" and "N uptake by plants" as 
parameters that have a significant impact on the leaching result, which has a high impact on marine 
eutrophication. Although they are based on a lot of research, they are still uncertain because they are 
influenced by many factors and involved in a very complex system. 
Diesel use: Diesel burned in agriculture has a high impact on the conclusions of this study, and the process 
used for this is uncertain. The diesel use process closest to Denmark in ecoinvent was modified, but further 
investigation is necessary. 
Carbon dioxide uptake and release: The study assumes that the amount of carbon dioxide taken up by plants 
in conventional and organic systems is equal to the amount released. However, this assumption may not 
always be accurate, as there are many factors that can affect the carbon balance, such as soil type, climate, 
and crop management practices. Therefore, the results should be interpreted with caution. 
Small data set: It is important to note that the findings of this study are limited by the small data sets, the 
focus on only one crop type and one catch crop type, and the fact that it cannot be generalized for all 
conventional, organic, CA, and RE production systems in Denmark. This highlights the need to examine 
agricultural systems more locally and to recognize the significant variations that exist within different 
systems. 
Animal manure: The study focused on farming systems that use animal manure, as that was the only data 
available from Farmtal. However, conventional farming without animal manure as fertilizer is more normal 
==PAGE:64==
64 
 
in the region of Sealand. Investigating a scenario where inorganic fertilizers, catch crops or green manure 
are used instead of animal manure could have been interesting. 
Rye grass: The study focused on the most common catch crop (rye grass) in Denmark, it would have been 
interesting to investigate legume used for catch crop as well. 
Nutrient content: It is also important to consider the nutrient content of organic and conventional animal 
manure, as this may impact the overall environmental impact of the agricultural production system. 
However, this was not considered in this study or in other modelling studies of agricultural production 
systems (Montemayor et al., 2022). 
N model: There is currently a lack of an LCI model for agriculture that can accurately calculate N emissions 
while ensuring that mass balance principles are respected. This highlights the need for a model that can 
calculate all N outputs either based on a mechanistic model that can calculate all nitrogen (N) outputs based 
on a mass balance, or an empirical model that considers all N inputs and outputs simultaneously. Also, there 
are several factors that could impact the mass balance, such as the difference in nutrient content between 
organic and non-organic animal manure and accumulation, which is not widely applied in the modeling. 
Additionally, the SQCB-NO3 model needs to be adapted to the newest research about RE and CA 
production system influence on leaching. 
Accumulation: Some research states that there is a higher chance of accumulation in RE and CA, which is 
not included in the model, as soil accumulation accounts for 15-20% of total N input. In the modified 
SQCB-NO3 model an accumulation of only 1% in RE systems using scenario 2 is observed is relatively 
low compared to other systems, which may be due to the model setup. RE is designed based on fitting to 
the mass balance and not the other way around, so the result of RE is only reliable if the model is suitable 
for use on an RE production system. 
Functional unit: One limitation of the LCA method is the use of functional units, which only allow for the 
measurement of one function at a time. This can result in biased results, particularly when comparing 
production systems with different functions. It's important to note that LCA was originally designed for 
products and not ecosystem services. 
Ecoinvent processes: Another limitation is that Ecoinvent processes are not fully adjusted to organic or 
alternative farming systems. This raises questions about whether current LCAs capture all the relevant 
effects of different farming methods, making it difficult to provide a fair basis for comparison. 
Alternative farming techniques: the lack of LCA research on alternative farming techniques, such as 
regenerative and conservational crop production, made it hard to gain data and information and highlights 
the need for more work in this area. 
AESE principles: As discussed in section 3.3, the sharing principle is very crucial to the AESA. The AESA 
principles applied in this study may not be appropriate for food products, especially considering the VA 
principle.  
AESE and impact categories: it is a limitation that we only look into one impact category which is marine 
eutrophication.

7 
Recommendations  
7.1 Recommendation for agriculture sector 
Based on this study, and particularly the process contribution analysis in section 5.2.4, we provide the 
following recommendations to the agriculture sector, in order to improve their environmental footprint: 
Reduce the use of diesel: to reduce the use of diesel in agricultural machinery, it is recommended to promote 
reduced tillage practices, adoption of alternative energy sources, and use of more efficient machinery. 
Limit the use of inorganic fertilizers: to limit the use of inorganic fertilizers, it is recommended to promote 
the use of organic and natural fertilizers, such as compost and manure, in all production systems. 
Increase soil organic matter: to increase the adoption of practices that increase soil organic matter and lead 
to the storage of carbon in the soil, it is recommended to implement reduced tillage, diverse crop rotations, 
and catch crops. 
Encourage sustainable production systems: to encourage the adoption of more sustainable and 
environmentally friendly production systems, it is recommended to promote regenerative agriculture, which 
prioritizes soil health, biodiversity, and ecosystem services. 
Prioritize conservational and regenerative production systems: to prioritize the use of conservational and 
regenerative production systems, which have been shown to have a lower impact on all impact categories 
compared to conventional and organic systems. 
Increase use of catch crops: To increase the use of catch crops in all production systems, it is recommended 
to reduce nitrate leaching and provide a source of natural fertilizer for future crops. 
Consider alternative fertilizers: To consider the use of alternative fertilizers or nutrient management 
strategies that can reduce the amount of N available for leaching into waterways. 
Optimize fertilizer application: To optimize fertilizer application rates, timing, and methods to reduce the 
overall amount of N applied to crops while ensuring that crops still receive adequate nutrients for healthy 
growth. 
Implement alternative practices: To implement alternative practices such as integrated pest management 
and soil amendments to help reduce the need for excessive use of copper and nickel in agriculture. 
Improve soil condition: To improve soil condition and prevent leaching and other negative environmental 
impacts.’  
Reduce crop waste and consume a more plant-based diet: Alternatively, reducing crop waste or consuming 
a more plant-based diet could also be ways to address the increased land use requirements of these systems, 
which may also have additional benefits.  
8 
Recommendation for LCA practitioners and method developers 
Examine more agricultural systems: There is a need to examine agricultural systems more locally and 
recognize the significant variations that exist within different systems. Therefore, it is recommended to 
collect more data on different agricultural production systems and crops to obtain more accurate results, 
especially for CA and RE production systems. 
==PAGE:66==
66 
 
Test additional sensitivity scenarios: to test the robustness of the results, additional sensitivity scenarios 
could be included, for example analyze the influence of using inorganic fertilizers or catch crops or green 
manure are used instead of animal manure, or using legume cover crop systems instead of using rye grass 
as a catch crop. 
Consider the nutrient content of animal manure: Check if there is a difference in organic and conventional 
animal manure and why this is not included in any analysis and comparisons. 
Develop accurate N models: There is a lack of N models for agriculture that can accurately calculate N 
emissions while ensuring that mass balance principles are respected. Therefore, it is recommended to 
develop a model that can calculate all N outputs based on a mass balance or interconnection of different N 
output methods/models. 
Incorporate land degradation, biodiversity, ecosystem services, etc. into the LCA: Focusing on impacts per 
kilogram of product can lead to decisions being taken in favor of conventional agriculture. Therefore, it is 
recommended to develop methods to incorporate land degradation, biodiversity, ecosystem services, etc. 
into the LCA to have a more complete picture of the environmental impact of different production systems. 
AESA including additional impact categories: also recommended to conduct as AESA including additional 
impact categories, in order to assess whether the production systems are sustainable considering e.g. climate 
change and ocean acidification impacts.  
AESA sharing principles: To improve the AESA part of study, more sharing principles should be applied. 
Ideally, sharing principles developed for food products should be applied, or principles that consider the 
products contribution to societal demands and needs. 
9 
Conclusion  
This study investigated how different agricultural production systems influence the N cycle, and it was 
found that based on the different principles they apply, the N cycle can look very different in the different 
production systems. Focusing on organic, conventional, CA, and RE production systems, it was found that, 
for example, by increasing the soil organic matter by using the reduced-tillage principle in the regenerative 
and conservational production system, can decrease the leaching of nitrate. Moreover, it was found that in 
current LCAs, there is a lack of an LCI model for agriculture that can accurately calculate N emissions 
while ensuring that mass balance principles are respected. This highlights the need for a mechanistic model 
that can calculate all N outputs based on a mass balance or an empirical model where the different N inputs 
and N outputs were interconnected. 
The LCA assessment conducted in this study showed that the RE production system has the lowest impact 
score in 17 categories (all except land use), while conventional has the lowest impact score in land use. 
Organic has the highest impact score in 12 categories, and CA has the highest impact score in 6 categories. 
It is important to note that organic production has the highest impact score in most categories, mainly due 
to the lower yield. In the organic production system, the yield was 4000 kg/ha*year, while in the other three 
systems, the yield was 6600 kg/ha*year. By doing external normalization, global warming, ozone formation 
(terrestrial ecosystems), terrestrial acidification, marine eutrophication, and terrestrial ecotoxicity were seen 
to have the highest normalized impact scores. 
For global warming, the conventional production system has the highest impact mainly due to carbon 
dioxide emissions accounting for 90% in conventional, 91% in organic, 92% in CA, and 89% in RE 
production systems due to diesel in agricultural machinery, which is used less in CA and RE due to their 
==PAGE:67==
67 
 
emphasis on reduced tillage practices but also coming from inorganic and organic fertilizer. Dinitrogen 
monoxide is also contributing to this impact category, coming directly from soil emissions with 9% in 
conventional, 8% in organic, 7% in CA, and 10% in RE. 
For ozone formation (terrestrial ecosystems), the organic production system has the highest impact. It is 
Nitrogen oxides emissions accounting for 96-98% in all production systems, coming directly from 
nitrification of soil emission but also from diesel in agricultural machinery, which is used less in CA and 
RE due to their emphasis on reduced tillage practices. Dinitrogen monoxide coming from denitrification of 
soil emissions also has an impact on ozone formation with 9% in conventional, 8% in organic, 7% in CA, 
and 10% in RE. 
For terrestrial acidification, the conventional production system has the highest impact due to ammonia 
emitted by volatilization, which is part of the field emissions. It accounts for 78% in conventional, 78% in 
organic, 82% in CA, and 85% in RE of the overall contribution to terrestrial acidification. 
For marine eutrophication, the organic production system has the highest impact due to the substance 
nitrate, which is the substance that contributes 100% in all production systems. It mainly comes from 
leaching of nitrate from soil emissions but also from production of spring barley seed and rye grass seed 
production.  
For terrestrial ecotoxicity, the conventional product system has the highest impact, followed closely by the 
CA system. Copper and nickel are the main substances contributing to this impact category, accounting for 
72-73% of the impact in different categories. These substances can come from various sources in 
agriculture, such as organic fertilizer and diesel, but mainly from pesticides, which is also the reason why 
conventional, or CA production systems have the highest impact score. 
In this study, a spatially resolved assessment of the AESA for marine eutrophication impacts showed that 
all four systems were sustainable in most potential locations. However, in 159, 94, 43, and 12 locations, the 
conservational, organic, CA, and RE production systems, respectively, were considered unsustainable using 
the SQ sharing principle. When using the VA sharing principle, very few points were below 1 for all systems 
(36 for conventional, 39 for organic, 49 for CA and 55 for RE), meaning that they are not sustainable for 
most locations. 
To improve environmental performance, agriculture should reduce diesel use and limit inorganic fertilizers, 
while increasing soil organic matter and promoting sustainable and regenerative production systems. Catch 
crops should be used more, and fertilizer application rates should be optimized and also reducing crop 
waste. It is also recommended to conduct as AESA including additional impact categories, in order to assess 
whether the production systems are sustainable considering e.g. climate change and ocean acidification 
impacts. Furthermore, the sharing principles applied in this study may not be appropriate for food products, 
especially considering the VA principle. It is recommended that more sharing principles should be tested 
to improve the AESA part of the study. 
By implementing these recommendations, agriculture can become more sustainable, and negative 
environmental impacts can be reduced. Promoting sustainability is essential for a sustainable future, and 
agriculture has an important role to play in achieving it.

Appendix A - Literature search and N emission model selection  
A1 
Literature search on N emission modelling 
The search for relevant N models was conducted using the Scopus search engine with the query string 
shown in Figure 1, which was refined iteratively. The initial screening yielded 31 articles, which were 
examined to identify models of interest. Qualitative information about N models was extracted from each 
article and those that did not provide relevant information were excluded. Articles that provided 
quantitative information about specific models were selected for further analysis resulting in a total of 
eight models/methods being evaluated.

emission model for comparing different agricultural systems in LCA. The assessment process involved a 
set of criteria to ensure the most suitable model was chosen. The assessment consists of 5 steps where a 
model to be used in the LCA and AESA was selected in step 5 Figure 22.

Appendix F – Supplementary agricultural information  
F1 
Principles in agriculture  
Different principles (reduced tillage, growing of catch crops, use of fertilizer etc.) have been used in the 
different systems, which are composed in different ways, in some of the production systems some of the 
principles are not use used to a higher and lower degree. 
Principle: Reduced tillage 
Reduced tillage is a farming principle that involves reducing plowing or harrowing and other types of soil 
disturbance. This approach seeks to minimize soil erosion and compaction, while still maintaining soil 
fertility and productivity through the use of other management practices such as cover crops and crop 
rotations(Cooper et al., 2021). 
==PAGE:109==
109 
 
In recent years reduced tillage principle, where there is focus on soil health, has gained more ground in 
Denmark. reduced tillage principle used in agriculture is already very popular in other parts of the world, 
especially the United States. It is principle use in both conservational and regenerative agriculture, and it is 
a cultivation principle where seeds are placed in small holes, rather than entire areas being plowed up and 
weed harrowing is also not taking place (Kamal et al., 2019).  
Compared to conventional methods, where farmers use a lot of equipment to plough, harrow, sow and set 
the seed, the amount of soil disturbed in the No-Till principle is very small. Only one single machine is 
needed to drill the small seed holes, and it is only driven over the field once (Cooper et al., 2021). 
A study (Kamal et al., 2019) indicated that despite all the benefits, farmers in the UK and across Europe 
have been slow to adopt the No-Till method. Farmers has been asked and there was more reasons why they 
do not adopt to the reduced tillage method. Some were concerned about the financial costs of purchasing a 
machine for controlled seeding discouraged them and others was concerned that the replacement would 
lead to less yield compared to their proven methods. Also, farmers using the no-plough method, is risking 
to producing less at first if the seeds have a harder time germinating in the harder, less oxygenated, 
uncultivated soil, but evidence indicates that earthworms and roots can help restore a natural soil structure 
that reduces these problems over time (Kamal et al., 2019). 
Another study (S. MANGALASSERY et al., 2015) found no consistent differences in yield during the first 
ten years after a farm converted to the reduced tillage principle, which is another advantage for the reduced 
tillage principle.  
In the study Kochanek et al., (2022) lot of benefits of surface seeding have been found. To mention some: 
Surface seeding is generally easier and faster than planting wheat seeds at a specific depth and it eliminates 
the need to prepare the soil by tilling. Also surface seeding can help to reduce soil compaction, as the seeds 
do not have to be pressed into the soil. This can help to improve soil structure and promote better root 
growth. Surface seeding can reduce the labor cost of planting, as it eliminates the need to use specialized 
equipment to create seedbeds and plant the seeds at a specific depth. Surface seeding can also leave a 
protective cover of residue or mulch on the surface of the soil, reducing erosion, and helping to retain soil 
moisture (Kochanek et al., 2022). 
It's important to note that surface seeding use in reduced tillage principle is generally used in areas with 
high rainfall, adequate soil moisture and good soil quality. It may be less effective in dry or rocky soil or 
on marginal land, and for areas with low rainfall, the seed germination rate will be reduced, and the crop 
establishment will be less vigorous. Also, it's important to select a wheat variety that is adapted to your area 
conditions and to seed at the right rate, so the crop can compete with the weeds (Munkholm, 2020) 
In reduced tillage systems, organic matter can accumulate on the surface of the soil, it can provide a food 
source for microorganisms, which in turn can help to improve soil fertility, water holding capacity and 
structure. This leads to the formation of soil aggregates which improves soil structure and increases the 
carbon content of the soil. Additionally, in no-till systems, the soil is not being disturbed, which promotes 
the growth of beneficial microorganisms and mycorrhizal fungi, which can help to improve nutrient 
uptake by crops. This can help to reduce the need for synthetic fertilizers and pesticides, which can be 
costly and can have negative environmental impacts (Hassan et al., 2022) 
Crop rotation is important in all cropping systems, but it is even more important in a reduced tillage system 
(Andersen, 2017). The reduced tillage principle are used in many agricultural production systems. 
Principle: Fertilizer 
==PAGE:110==
110 
 
There is many ways to add fertilizer to a system. It can e.g. be though inorganic fertilizer or organic 
fertilizer where organic fertilizer could be catch crops and animal manure.  
Inorganic fertilizer are synthetic materials that are made in a laboratory and used to add essential plant 
nutrients to the soil. They often contain a combination of the three primary macronutrients: Nitrogen (N), 
phosphorus (P), and potassium (K), as well as other micronutrients that plants need to grow. Inorganic 
fertilizers are commonly used in conventional agriculture because they are easy to apply, provide a quick 
boost to plant growth, and can be formulated to provide plants with specific nutrient deficiencies. 
However, they can also have negative environmental impacts, such as pollution of water sources, if not 
used carefully (Agriculture and Technology, 2017). 
Organic fertilizers, on the other hand, are made from natural materials and are used to add essential plant 
nutrients to the soil. Examples of organic fertilizers include animal manure and catch crop. Organic 
fertilizers typically release nutrients more slowly than inorganic fertilizers, which can help to prevent 
over-fertilization. They also improve the soil structure and help to promote the growth of beneficial 
microorganisms. However, they may not provide as quick a boost to plant growth as inorganic fertilizers 
and may require more frequent application (Agriculture and Technology, 2017). 
Principle: Growing of catch crops (organic fertilizer) 
Catch crops are grown in between the main crops or after the main crops. In this project I investigate 
catch crops grown as a follow-up to the main crop. One of the main advantages of growing catch crops is 
that it takes up some of the leftover N, so it prevent leaching. The catch crops are not removed from the 
field after being harvested but incorporated into the soil, and therefore it also functions as organic 
fertilizer for the main crops which will be sown afterwards the year after. This plays an important role due 
to the amount of fertilizer and/or animal manure spread on the field. As the catch crops stays in the field 
and therefore function as organic fertilizer it has an impact on the NOX emission, NH3 emission, leaching 
and the amount of animal manure or inorganic fertilizer needed to be added in the field (De Notaris et al., 
2018).  
Catch crops are planted between regular crops to capture excess nutrients from the soil and prevent them 
from leaching away. These crops are often grown in rotation with other crops and are used to improve the 
fertility of the soil. After the catch crop is harvested, it is typically plowed under, which returns the 
nutrients it has captured back to the soil. Catch crops are small, fast-growing crops that protect the soil, 
reduce erosion, and add organic matter to the soil. They can also be used to suppress weeds, attract 
beneficial insects, and be use as fertilizer for the soil (De Notaris et al., 2018). 
Principle: Use of Animal manure (organic fertilizer)  
Animal manure is the waste produced by farm animals, such as cow, chicken, pigs, it is rich in N, 
Phosphorus, Potassium and many other micro-nutrients, it is widely used as organic fertilizer. It is spread 
over the field and tilling it into the soil. Manure also can be used to create compost which will break 
down the manure and make it an even more effective fertilizer (Admin, 2019).
