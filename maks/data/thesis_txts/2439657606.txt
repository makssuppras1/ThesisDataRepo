Abstract
Gobiidae is a wide spread and diverse ﬁsh family, however its distribution and abundance
are poorly understood due in part to their low commercial value. The main objective of this
study was to understand the distribution of coastal gobies from the Inner Danish Waters
and to model habitat suitability for the most abundant species. During the 2016 summer
a scientiﬁc cruise was conducted where gobies were collected with a beam trawl and en-
vironmental conditions such as temperature, salinity, oxygen saturation at the sea bottom
were measured and substrate types were obtained. Wave exposure was extracted from the
simpliﬁed water model (SWM). Overall, 20318 gobies were identiﬁed. Sand (Pomatoschistus
minutus) and common (P. microps) gobies represented 83% of the total number of gobies
with 16786 individuals. Followed by painted (P. pictus), round (Neogobius melanostomus),
black (Gobius niger) and two-spotted goby (Gobiusculus ﬂavescens) with 2143, 317, 959
and 113 individuals respectively. Length-weight relationship was analyzed (R2 ranged from
0.92 to 0.99) and distribution maps were drawn for all species. Generalized linear models
were used to explain the abundance of the sand and common goby complex in relation to
environmental drivers. The four best models were based on wave exposure; depth; salinity
and substrate type; salinity and temperature. The ﬁtted models were tested, and all of them
were invalidated. Although none of the models exhibited strong predictive ability, potential
habitat maps were built for the purpose of this thesis. These gobies prefer warmer less saline
waters, less exposed by waves and shallower depths.
Keywords: sand goby, common goby, length-weight relationship, glmmTMB.

1
Introduction
The family Gobiidae, popularly known as gobies, is a diverse ﬁsh family, with up to 1900
species [1]. In the Inner Danish Waters thirteen species of gobies can be found [2], these
are: Pomatoschistus minutus (sand goby), Pomatoschistus microps (common goby), Po-
matoschistus norvegicus (norwegian goby), Pomatoschistus pictus (painted goby), Gobius
niger (black goby), Neogobius melanostomus (round goby), Gobiusculus ﬂavescens (two-
spotted goby), Aphia minuta (transparent goby), Crystallogobius linearis (crystal goby), Le-
betus guilleti (Guillet’s goby), Lebetus scorpioides (diminutive goby), Lesueurigobius friesii
(Fries’s goby) and Thorogobius ephippiatus (leopard-spotted goby).
Round goby is an alien species from the Black, Caspian and adjacent Sea of Azov [3], it
arrived in the Baltic Sea in 1990 [4], with ﬁrst documented appearance in Danish waters
18 years later near Bornholm Island [5]. Studies have shown round goby does not exert a
high pressure on the native ﬁsh species [6], however food competition might occur with the
common roach (Rutilus rutilus), with small ﬂounders (Platichthys ﬂesus) and with vimba
bream (Vimba vimba) since there is spatial overlap and common food preference between
those species [6]. Nevertheless, the impact of round goby invasion in the ecosystem is un-
known [6].
Gobies are not important ﬁsheries resources, hence their distribution and abundance are
barely known [1]. However, they are important prey for commercial and non-commercial
species. Black and two-spotted gobies were frequently preyed by cod with sizes 150 to 450
mm in the Norwegian Skagerrak coast [7]. Together with cod, herring and whiting, gobies
are primary prey of harbour porpoise in the Danish waters [1]. Sand and black goby were
also present in the stomach content of harbour seals, where black goby represented up to
36% of weight consumed in the Danish waters [8].
In addition to being important as prey items, gobies perform valuable ecosystem functions.
Black and round gobies are “mesopredators”, medium-size ﬁsh prey of larger ﬁsh [9]. These
species eat “mesograzers”, small invertebrates herbivores such as amphipods and isopods
[10]. Black goby, together with the shore crabs and wrasses, can regulate the abundance of
other groups, such as these mesograzers, which results in cascading eﬀect on the vegetation
[11][12]. Abundance of mesograzers can impact the whole ecosystem. Studies have shown
eelgrass’ biomass been reduced 85% in northern Kattegat [13][14], which might be caused
by blooms of ﬁlamentous algae such as Cladophora spp, Ectocarpales and Ulva spp, due to
eutrophication and low grazing pressure [15] [16]. Cage experiments showed the mesopreda-
tors caused a greater than 95% reduction in mesograzers’ abundance [15][16]. This research
also showed that black goby feeding on the amphipod Gammarus locusta, which is the most
1
==PAGE:6==
important grazer of ephemeral algae in the seagrass, resulted in a ﬁvefold increase biomass
of the ephemeral algae [15]. Round goby also preys on the grazers of ﬁlamentous algae:
the isopods Idotea chelipes and gastropods, hence its predation could also aﬀect seagrass
coverage [6].
Overﬁshing also have an impact on sea grass biomass, due a top-down cascade eﬀects [17][18]
[16]. Because of removal of the top predators with ﬁsheries, mesopredators’ abundance (such
as gobies) might increase, which may reduce mesograzers’ abundance (small crustaceans and
gastropods that feed on algae), causing an algae increase [16]. The increase of mesopredators
due to removal of the top predators is known as “mesopredator release” [9].
Study area
The study area is restricted to the Inner Danish Waters (IDW). This area is a transition
zone between a saline and a brackish water body, the North Sea and the Baltic Sea, with
salinity varying from approximately 34 in the north and 8 psu in the southern entrance to
the Baltic Proper [19]. IDW can be divided into Kattegat, the Belts and Arkona Basin.
While the Kattegat is shallow with mean depth of 23 m [20], in the Belts are deep channels
up to 35 m [21] and Arkona basin is the deepest area with maximum depth 45 m [20].
The major threats to marine biodiversity in Denmark are human related and include: nu-
trient pollution from agricultural ﬁelds, commercial ﬁshing where trawling exert a major
impact and climate change [22]. Those are continuously impacting the marine habitat and
declining the habitat quality due to eutrophication and trawling causing loss of biodiversity
[22].
Biodiversity is essential for the ecosystem to be able to respond to changes [23], enhancing
the system’s resilience to environmental stressors such as climate change and marine pollu-
tion and protect from spread of diseases [23]. Seagrass habitats are important in the IDW,
as they oﬀer refuges and nursery for many organisms, including invertebrates and ﬁsh, and
prevent coastal erosion [24][25][26].
Motivation
Knowledge about gobies is lacking in the Inner Danish Waters. Even basic information such
as size are not available for some of the species. Overﬁshing, eutrophication, invasion of alien
species are some of the threats of this ecosystem and can heavily impact in the ecosystem.
Without knowing which species are present and their abundance is harder to understand its
impacts in the ecosystem.

Objectives
The overall goal was to describe the distribution of coastal gobies species (family Gobiidae)
from the Inner Danish Waters based in a scientiﬁc cruise conducted in July and August
2016. Thus, the speciﬁc goals were to identify gobies species present at depths above 4 m
in the Inner Danish Waters and to describe size, weight, length-weight relationship and spa-
tial distribution for all gobies species. Furthermore, this study investigates if environmental
parameters (depth, temperature, salinity, oxygen saturation, substrate types and wave ex-
posure) aﬀect the most common gobies species’ abundance in an attempt to describe habitat
suitability.

Material and Methods
2.1
Sampling
Collection of gobies together with abiotic information was conducted during the scientiﬁc
cruise Yngeltogt2016 throughout the Inner Danish Waters (IDW) from 18th July to 22nd
August 2016. In total, there were 146 sites sampled with a beam trawl and 36 sites sampled
with fyke nets. Because of time restrictions, only 98 trawl sites were randomly selected for
detailed processing in this study (Figure 1).
Figure 1: Map of the study area: the Inner Danish waters. The circles indicate
the sampling sites trawled by boat and the triangles by hand. The bathymetry
values are limited to a maximum of 120 m to allow suﬃcient contrast in the
relatively shallow study area.
4
==PAGE:9==
Trawls were carried out with a two meter wide beam trawl, with mesh size 4 mm and a
tickler chain attached ahead of the foot-rope, similar to that described in [27]. For depths
below approximately one metre, hauls were done by hand and at depths above one metre
were made by boat at a speed of approximately one knot for 3 minutes and 20 seconds.
Latitude and longitude were taken at the start and end of each trawl, thus trawled area
could be calculated. Depth at start and end of each trawl was recorded from either the
vessel’s echo-sounder or a knotted and weighed rope.
After trawling, abiotic parameters were investigated 0.5 m from the bottom, where salinity,
temperature and dissolved oxygen were obtained by the hand-held meter “WTW Multiline
Multi 3620 IDS” with oxygen probe “WTW OPTICAL FDO 925-P” and conductivity and
temperature with “WTW TETRACON 925-P”. Sediment was collected with a haps corer
with sampling area of 0.015 m2 and transferred to a plastic bag with site identiﬁcation and
stored on ice for the remainder of the day before being frozen at -18 ◦C until analysis. The
gobies found in each trawl were placed inside a zip lock plastic bag with site identiﬁcation
stored in the freezer and identiﬁed later in the laboratory.
2.2
Laboratory Description
The identiﬁcation of gobies was based on morphological features [28] [29]. Total length (TL)
was determined to the nearest lower half millimetre and total weight obtained at the nearest
milligram with a precision balance with 1 mg readability.
The determination between all specimens of sand and common gobies was untenable in the
timeframe available, thus they were grouped as the sand-common complex (SCC), where
for each site, the ﬁrst 20 SCC were identiﬁed by species and measured, then the next 100
individuals were measured and weighed and the remaining individuals were just counted and
their combined weight recorded. For painted goby, the ﬁrst 100 individuals were measured
and weighed, and the remaining individuals were counted and the combined weight was
recorded. All black, round and two-spotted gobies were measured individually.
2.3
Data Treatment
The trawled distance was calculated from the geographical start and end positions. Sites with
recorded trawled distance less than 40 m and greater than 151 m were excluded from further
analysis as they were considered erroneous. Trawled area was calculated as the product
of trawled length by 2 (beam trawl length). Mean depth was calculated as the average of
depth at the start and end positions. Length-weight relationship (LWR) was modelled for
all species identiﬁed with FSA package [30], using the equation:

(1)
Where W is the weight in grams, L is the total length in centimetres and a and b are LWR
parameters. The damaged gobies were not included in the calculus and outliers (visually
inspected) were removed.
Collected sediments were analyzed by trained technical staﬀaccording to the method out-
lined in the Danish Standard [31]. Substrate was recategorized after observing that only one
site was identiﬁed as “gravel and coarse sand” and two as “mud and sandy mud”. Hence, it
was recategorized as “coarse and till”, “mud to muddy sand” and “sand”.
Wave exposure was extracted from the simpliﬁed wave model (SWM) developed with soft-
ware WaveImpact 1.0 [32]. It is deﬁned as simpliﬁed because it uses the shoreline, instead
of bathymetry, to describe the coastal shape [33]. The SWM has proven useful in practi-
cal ecological applications [34] whilst being computationally less intensive (M. Isaeus, pers
comms 2018), with precalculated grids publicly available with horizontal resolution of 25 m
[35][36]. Wave exposure is based on wind strength averaged over 5 years [35], therefore it
does not vary seasonally or annually.
2.4
Generalized Linear Models
2.4.1
Model Building
Statistical models were built using R software version 3.4.4 [37]. Number of gobies were an-
alyzed by ﬁtting generalized linear models using the glmmTMB (Generalized Linear Mixed
Models using Template Model Builder) package version 0.2.2 [38].
Only the number of SCC is used in the statistical analysis because of time restriction. The
numbers of SCC per haul were used as the response, while the explanatory variables tested
were: salinity (psu), temperature (◦C), oxygen saturation (%), mean depth (m), substrate
(“coarse and till”, “mud to muddy sand” and “sand”) and wave exposure (m2/s). Oﬀset is
implemented to ﬁx sampling imbalance [39] and it is required to be speciﬁed in generalized
linear models [40]. In this study the response variable is count data and sampling eﬀort
varied from 125 to 300.5 m2, hence the variation in haul length was accounted as the oﬀset
term in the model in the logarithm form.
Before creation of models with the variables, correlations between environmental variables
were checked (Figure 9 Appendix). Temperature and oxygen saturation had moderate cor-
relation (R2 0.6) as expected. However, correlation was found also between salinity and
temperature (R2 0.7) and between salinity and oxygen saturation (R2 0.5). Combinations
with one to six explanatory variables were tested, resulting in 63 models. Interaction between
6

temperature and salinity was not included as these are independent parameters. Salinity in-
ﬂuences the oxygen solubility [41], however as temperature inﬂuences both solubility and
biological eﬀects that make it utilized, it was opted by only adding interaction between tem-
perature and oxygen saturation.
The family distributions used to model the number of SCC were Poisson and the negative
binomial, both log-linked to predictors (Table 1). The two parametrizations for the negative
binomial available on glmmTMB were tested: nbinom1 and nbinom2. The diﬀerence be-
tween them is how the dependence of variance aﬀects the mean. With nbinom1 the variance
increases linearly with the mean while nbinom2 variance increases quadratically [42].

2.4.2
Model Selection, Veriﬁcation and Validation
After models were created, their results were compared using the Akaike’s information cri-
terion (AIC) using function AICtab from the package bbmle [43]. As suggested by Richards
[44], only models that had ∆AIC within six of the smallest were retained. Nested models
were investigated further with the Likelihood Radio Test, to test whether adding an extra
variable signiﬁcantly improved the model or not [40].
The models selected were investigated further with the package DHARMa version 0.2.0 [45]
where residuals were standardized from zero to one, then tested for over- or under dispersion,
zero inﬂation and uniformity with available functions in the package.
Validation was carried out via sub-sampling cross-validation, where 95% of the sites were
used to predict the remaining 5% test dataset. This random subsampling of training and
7
==PAGE:12==
testing data from the whole data set was repeated 50 times in order to visualize the potential
range of predictive capabilities. For the random allocation of observations to training and
test datasets the package caret version 6.0-80 [46] was used. After predicting on the test
dataset, the root mean squared error (RMSE) was calculated as
RMSE =
rP(predicted −observed)2

(2)
Where n is the number of observations. The RMSE was compared to the response standard
deviation (SD) to indicate the model predictive capability using the rule of thumb where
RMSE should be smaller than half of SD of the measured data [51] to be considered low and
the model be considered appropriate.
Another validation was conducted using the whole dataset to predict for all sites using caret.
This method is known as resubstitution estimation [47], it is analyzed with caution in this
study since it can overestimate the model’s ability [48].
2.5
Prediction Maps
Monthly time series of sea bottom temperature and salinity were extracted from Kiel Baltic
Sea Ice-Ocean Model (BSIOM)[49]. The horizontal resolution of this hydrodynamic model is
2.5 km [49]. Selected models were used to predict for the whole range of study area using the
glmmTMB implementation of the function “predict” for July and August of 2016 and 2017
independently. These months were chosen as they were the months where sampling took
place and the same months one year later to look at changes in model prediction between
years. Only shallow areas up to 4 m are shown as sampling was restricted to this depth to
avoid extrapolation beyond the ranges of input data used for the model. Predicted models
using temperature and salinity showed up to 9 m because it is the shallowest depth available
on the BSIOM.

Discussion
Coastal gobies
Coastal gobies not identiﬁed in this study
Thirteen goby species are reported to inhabit the Inner Danish Waters [2]. Of these, seven
were not identiﬁed in this study: Crystal (Crystallogobius linearis), transparent (Aphia min-
uta), diminutive (Lebetus scorpioides), Guillet (Lebetus guilleti), Leopard-spotted (Thoro-
gobius ephippiatus), Fries’ (Lesueurigobius friesii) and Norwegian goby (Pomatoschistus
norvegicus). Methodology, habitat preference and size could be the reasons why these species
were not found in this study.
Crystal goby is a pelagic ﬁsh while transparent goby lives in the suprabenthic [53].
As
trawling was carried on the bottom, the gear may not have encountered them. Furthermore,
diminutive, Fries’, leopard-spotted and norwegian gobies are found at greater depths than
the present study surveyed (between 0.25 to 4.00 m). Diminutive goby was found in Scottish
seas at depths 5 to 17 m [54]. Fries’ goby is described as living at depths from 10 to 130
m and Norwegian goby from 30 to 280 m[55]. Leopard-spotted goby inhabits 6-40 m deep
[56]. Guillet’s goby is a small ﬁsh, with maximum standard length recorded of 2.40 cm [56].
There are few records of this specie in the UK and Scandinavia, its distribution is uncertain,
but is probably found between 2 to 30 m deep [56], however recent studies in the UK found
only at deeper areas at 8 and 10 m [57] and at 17 m deep [54]. Due to its small size, rareness
and distribution, it was most likely not found in the sites investigated and if caught, not
found during the selection on board.
Coastal gobies identiﬁed in this study
Sand, common, painted, black, round and two-spotted gobies were identiﬁed in the study
area. Besides the SCC, painted goby was the species with greatest density and had the
second highest number of occurrences.
The number of sand-common were almost eight
times greater than the second most numbered species. Hence, sand and common gobies are
important ﬁsh in depths up to 4 m in the IDW. Painted goby was distributed in all areas
sampled, including South of the island of Funen. The report from the Helsinki Commission
(HELCOM) aﬃrmed this species does not occur in the latter area [2]. This study then had
found painted goby where studies had not ﬁnd it there yet.
The invasive round goby outnumbered black goby in this study. This may be due to the
methodology and habitat preferences of these species. Areas with hard bottom could not be
trawled, meaning if the species inhabits this type of habitat it could not be caught with the
21
==PAGE:26==
gear employed in this study. If this it is the case, then it might be that black goby has a
more restricted habitat than round goby which was caught more frequently in this study’s
gear but also prevalently in static gears on hard bottom habitat (pers. Obs). Two-spotted
goby is a suprabenthic ﬁsh [53], which might explain why it was found in only 12 of 98 sites.
Hence, ﬁnding in few sites may not mean that it is rare, rather the methodology used was
not ideal to catch this species and resulted in haphazard sampling of it.
Gobies size
Without any other reports of size, no comparisons can be made for painted, black nor two-
spotted gobies in the IDW. This study’s reporting of length and mass for these species
contributes novel information.
In the Baltic Sea, females and males of common gobies measured 3.98 ± 0.03 cm and 3.95
± 0.03 cm respectively in the high saline sites of the Baltic Sea [58]. These values are quite
similar to the results in this study. Sand goby can reach up to 11 cm in the Baltic Sea [62],
in this study maximum length was 7.50 cm.
Black gobies can reach up to 18 cm and its life span is four years [28]. In the Adriatic,
surveys conducted during spring and summer for 1996 to 2008 at depths until 200 m found
black gobies from 7.00 to 14.50 cm with mean 10.99 ± 1.56 cm [63]. In this study black
goby ranged from 1.30 to 8.65 cm with mean length 4.01 ± 1.74 cm. Larger gobies were not
found in this study, it might be bigger black gobies inhabit deeper areas, that they prefer
hard bottom habitat or were able to escape from the net which was trawled at relatively low
speed.
A study from the IDW found round goby age 0 measured until 7.00 cm and up to 11.00 cm
were maximum age 2, reaching maximum size of 20.00 cm [5]. The oldest round goby found
in the IDW was 7 years old [5]. Based on the previous study [5], it is possible to anticipate
that the majority of round goby found were age 0 and few with age 1 and 2. None of the
round gobies had length greater than 8.65 cm, thus bigger round gobies are either found in
deeper areas, in areas with hard bottom or were able to avoid the net.

Sand-common gobies distribution
Models selected
Four independent models were selected which used depth, wave exposure, salinity and sub-
strate type or temperature and salinity to explain SCC abundance. Oxygen saturation did
not explain SCC abundance in this study, however it only ranged from 88.0 to 128.2%, which
are high values that may not cause oxygen to be physiologically limiting in the areas sampled.
Therefore, it can only be concluded that at high oxygen saturation, the oxygen saturation
does not inﬂuence SCC abundance.
Models with 3 variables were not selected as the best ﬁtted models and the reason might be
that one variable could explain satisfactory at diﬀerent ranges. In other words, in this case
means that observing a restricted temperature and salinity range it might be that depth is
crucial for the SCC numbers, however when inspecting with a wide temperature and salinity
range, temperature and salinity might have a greater inﬂuence in the SCC number. There-
fore, it is good to look all diﬀerent models obtained to understand SCC abundance. Possibly
with a bigger dataset a model with 3 variables could be selected as it might be too complex
the relation and it might require more sites for a better ﬁt.
Our measure of exposure is based on mean wind conditions of 2002 to 2007 [35]. If in any
one year there is an anomalous weather pattern, then the actual wave exposure at any one
23
==PAGE:28==
site would be diﬀerent than the ﬁve-year average used to predict.
According to the best ﬁtted models, SCC might prefer shallow and low wave exposed areas.
The predicted increase of SCC densities in 2017 compared to 2016 could be due slightly
warmer and less saline near the coast (Figure 12 and Figure 13 Appendix). A Swedish study
identiﬁed that both sand and common gobies could be found in the sheltered areas, while in
exposed areas only sand goby was abundant [64]. As those two species were merged together
this trend could not be identiﬁed in this study.
Model validation
Validation was carried out via sub-sampling cross-validation, where 93 sites were used to
predict the remaining 5. This random subsampling of training and testing data from the
whole data set was repeated 50 times to test the prediction ability in these sites and observe
the diﬀerence between the SCC number obtained in the ﬁeld and the predicted by the model.
All the four models selected would be invalidated using the cross-fold validation. It might
be that because of the small dataset, each time it removes ﬁve sites, one or more sites
were important ﬁtting the model, hence removing them made the ﬁtted models weaker and
reduced the predictive ability. In large datasets removing ﬁve sites might not reduce the
model ability, however in a small dataset it might.
Model limitations
Sand and common gobies were analyzed together, however it may make it hard to predict
as the occurrence of these species might be related to diﬀerent environment variables or at
diﬀerent intensity. The slightly diﬀerences in habitat preference of these species may mask
their habitat preferences when trying to model them together. According to Fonds and van
Buurt [65] sand goby inhabit a wider range of temperature and salinity than common goby.
Similarly, the use of all sizes in the abundance variable may mask a response in relation to
the environmental predictors. M¨uck and Heubel [58] found a positive linear relation between
common goby length and salinity when looked at this species along Swedish Kattegat to the
Gulf of Finland.
In this study, the trawled area ranged from 125 to 300.5 m2. Whilst we eﬀectively model
density per unit area by using the logarithm of the trawled area as an oﬀset, if one of the
species exhibits a patchy distribution, then by increasing trawled area we are increasing the
chances of encountering a patch of individuals. This could potentially bias the reported
densities at sites with a larger trawled area toward higher abundances. Also, if the trawled
distance varied due to diﬀerent trawled speeds it could also potentially bias the reported
24
==PAGE:29==
densities, as trawling faster could potentially catch more gobies and trawling slower could
potentially caught less gobies as it would be easier to escape, thus it would be diﬃcult to
compare them as the catchability changed with boat speed.
Besides the predictive variables studied, other factors could also have aﬀected SCC abun-
dance, for example time of the day and day in the year. It might be gobies would move
deeper at a speciﬁc time of the day, so for example in the afternoon would caught less ﬁsh,
but not because of temperature and salinity, but because of that speciﬁc time they move to
deeper areas. The day of the year could also be an issue since these gobies reproduce during
the summer, so maybe when looked an area it was before reproduction, and when sampled
in another region there were many more ﬁsh due to new recruits.
Study importance
Gobies are relevant preys for commercial and non-commercial species.
Black and round
gobies can also regulate the habitat by grazing the mesopredators, which can aﬀect the
vegetation and thus the whole ecosystem. A disruption due overﬁshing of top predators,
causing more gobies to survive (due to the “mesopredators release”) and introduction of
alien species, such as round goby, can also break the balance and aﬀect seagrass habitat,
therefore it is important study gobies.
Fisheries management shown to be unsuccessful frequently when it considered only the target
species [66]. Ecosystem-based ﬁsheries management (EBM) instead of evaluating only the
target species consider the whole ecosystem [66] and could beneﬁt from this study as length-
weight parameters are required not only for the target species. The general lack of empirical
studies of goby ecology, especially for the IDW, make this study’s ﬁndings an important
contribution to the knowledge of coastal ecology in Denmark.
Future research recommendations
Further use of this data
In this study, environmental parameters were used to understand only goby abundance and
not goby size or body condition (due to time constraints), however since total length and
weight were obtained in laboratory, it would be interesting to see if the spatial distribution is
related with size or body condition. Generalized linear models could be applied for Painted
goby as this species is present in enough number of sites. Besides Poisson and negative bino-
mial distributions, models could be built using another distribution, the Cornwal-Maxwell-
Poisson, which is a less known distribution which can handle overdispersed data [38].
Other approaches could be used do model species distributions, such as the maximum en-
25
==PAGE:30==
tropy (MaxEnt) models and Random Forest. MaxEnt only uses the presence data [67] in this
study from 98 sites, only 71 could be used for SCC, 38 for painted and less sites for the other
species, making it unfeasible to use it for other species that not SCC. A study comparing
Random Forest (RF) with MaxEnt showed RF was the one with the best performance [68].
Thus, using this model should be considered.
Further use of the gobies sampled
Firstly, sand and common gobies could be separated to investigate each species individually.
All trawl sites of the Yngeltogt2016 cruise could be analyzed to provide a larger sample size
for ﬁtting the model. With more information, a model that combines 3 parameters might
be possible or a model with a higher predictive ability.
The sites sampled with the stationary fyke nets could be analyzed in a future study. Although
it is not possible to have comparable densities with the trawl data, other time-based forms
of CPUE may be calculable. Length and weight of the specimens caught by fyke nets could
be incorporated in the length-weight relationship analysis.
Outside this work
Following the same study line, to better understand sand and common gobies distribution,
other studies could include a wider range of depth and salinity. In this study the survey was
conducted in July and August, other studies could be conducted in a narrow spatial area,
but with a greater temporal resolution to understand sand and common gobies population
ﬂuctuation in coastal habitats over time.
The strictly coastal nature of this study means information about some of the gobies species
that are found in the IDW were not sampled. In order to provide an overview of the ecology
of the gobies in IDW, deeper benthic, benthopelagic and pelagic surveys are required.

Conclusions
Overall, more than 20000 gobies were identiﬁed in the Danish waters in 98 sites. From this
study is possible to aﬃrm that sand and common gobies were the most abundant benthic
goby ﬁsh at depths up to four metres in the Inner Danish Waters in terms of numbers.
Painted goby was found distributed in all areas, including South of Funen, where their oc-
currence has not previously been documented.
Generalized linear models found relationship between sand and common gobies and depth,
wave exposure, temperature and salinity. High oxygen saturation did not inﬂuence SCC
abundance. Substrate type might aﬀect SCC densities, being mud to muddy sand a favourable
habitat, however it is necessary more studies to conﬁrm. SCC seems to prefer shallow areas,
less exposed to waves, less saline and warmer waters.
