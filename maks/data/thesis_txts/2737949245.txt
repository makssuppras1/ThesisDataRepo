Abstract
Saccharomyces cerevisiae is a valuable yeast cell factory used to produce a wide range of products.
For example, half of the insulin produced today is produced in S. cerevisiae, marking both
the importance and value of eﬃcient engineering. As a cell factory, it has many beneﬁts: its
16 chromosomes are sequenced, its use is ”generally regarded as safe” (GRAS) in industrial
processes, and the gene editing toolbox is vast and constantly expanding. CRISPR-Selective-
Ploidy-Ablation (CRI-SPA) is a high-throughput screening method using a CRI-SPA donor
strain (CDS) for transferring a gene of interest, or biosynthetic pathway, to a library of recipient
yeast strains. The process can be completed in under a week and allows the screening of
thousands of strains for improved production by generating huge amounts of information and
genetic ”hits” that can be reconstructed in the original production strain. However, the current
CRI-SPA system can be optimized by exchanging the nuclease from Cas9 to MAD7, thereby
removing any Intellectual Property (IP) issues related to its use. There is also a Material
Transfer Agreement (MTA) limiting the use of the CDS. In addition, the current system utilizes
a ura3 marker for counter-selection against the CRI-SPA donor strain’s genome, requiring the
recipient library to be Ura- which is a metabolic defect.
This study aims to construct a new CDS that overcomes the challenges of the current system.
The new CDS utilizes the IP-friendly nuclease MAD7 and contains a cassette in front of the
centromere consisting of a dual galactose-inducible promoter and the dominant thymidine
kinase (TK) marker gene on every chromosome. When galactose is present, the centromere is
destabilized, leading to chromosome loss. When the compound 5-ﬂuorodeoxyuridine (FUdR) is
also present, cells still harboring any CDS chromosomes are killed (counter-selection). Triple
gRNA plasmids were constructed to test the multiplexing capacities of MAD7 for integrating
the cassette. The success of CRI-SPA strongly relies on the ability to induce the loss of the
CDS chromosomes. 16 yeast strains, each containing the cassette on a single chromosome, were
therefore constructed and tagged with a yellow-ﬂuorescent protein (YFP) to follow the loss of
each chromosome following centromere destabilization. Finally, the constructed CDS was tested
in a CRI-SPA set-up by transferring the biosynthetic pathway for bikaverin production to a
selection of the yeast deletion library.
It is shown that it is possible to free three distinct gRNAs from the same plasmid using a
strategy comprising both ﬂanking tRNAs and direct repeats, thus illustrating a new method of
multiplexing using MAD7. A new CDS was constructed using the multiplexing strategy, and it
was possible to transfer the expression of bikaverin from the CDS to a library of recipient yeast
strains. The hits generated from the CRI-SPA screen of bikaverin resemble previous results using
the current CRI-SPA system. This study reports the ﬁrst use of a gRNA plasmid containing an
auxotrophic marker, rather than an antibiotic marker, for the transfer to the recipient library in
CRI-SPA. Thus, expanding the possibilities of working with the CRI-SPA system. It is shown
that the dual galactose-inducible promoter is not leaky and that a single copy of the TK marker
being expressed on any of the 16 chromosomes is enough to kill even a diploid cell. Overall, the
results regarding the new CRI-SPA system based on a dual galactose-promoter and a dominant
counter-selectable TK marker are promising

Resum´e
Saccharomyces cerevisiae er en værdifuld gærcellefabrik der bruges til at producere af en bred
vifte af produkter. For eksempel, halvdelen af den insulin der produceres i dag produceres i S.
cerevisiae, hvilket markerer b˚ade vigtigheden og værdien i e↵ektiv genteknologi. Som cellefabrik
har den mange fordele: dens 16 kromosomer er sekventerede, dens brug er ”generally regarded
as safe” (GRAS) i industrielle processer, og genredigerings-værktøjskassen er enorm og udvides
konstant. CRISPR-Selective-Ploidy-Ablation (CRI-SPA) er en high-throughput metode der
anvender en CRI-SPA donorstamme (CDS) til overførsel af et gen eller en hel biosyntesevej til
et bibliotek af modtager gærstammer. Processen kan færdiggøres p˚a under en uge og tillader
screening af tusindvis af stammer for forbedret produktion ved at generere enorme mængder
information og genetiske ”hits” der kan rekonstrueres i den oprindelige produktionsstamme. Det
nuværende CRI-SPA system kan dog optimeres ved at udskifte nukleasen fra Cas9 til MAD7
og dermed fjerne intellektuel ejendom (IP) problemer relateret til dets brug. Der er ogs˚a en
materialeoverførselsaftale (MTA) der begrænser brug af CDS-stammen. Ud over dette, anvender
det nuværende system en ura3-markør til modselektion af CRI-SPA donoren stammens genom
hvilket kræver at modtagerbiblioteket er Ura-, hvilket er en metabolisk defekt.
Denne undersøgelse har til form˚al at konstruere en ny CDS der overkommer de udfordringer
der er ved det nuværende system. Den nye CDS gør brug af den IP-venlige nuklease MAD7 og
indeholder en kassette foran centromeren som best˚ar af en dobbelt galaktose-inducerbar promoter
og det dominante thymidinkinase (TK) markør gen p˚a hvert kromosom. N˚ar galaktose er til stede,
bliver centromeren destabiliseret, hvilket leder til kromosom tab. N˚ar sto↵et 5-ﬂuordeoxyuridin
er ogs˚a til stede, bliver celler der stadig indeholder kassetten dræbt (modselektion). Tredobbelte
gRNA plasmider blev konstrueret for at teste multipleksing mulighederne for MAD7 ved
integration af kassetten. Success med CRI-SPA afhænger stærkt af evnen til at inducere tab af
CDS kromosomerne. Derfor blev 16 gærstammer der hver især indeholder kassetten p˚a et enkelt
kromosom konstrueret og mærket med et gul-ﬂuorescerende protein (YFP) for at følge tabet af
hvert kromosom efter centromere destabilisation. Til sidst blev den konstruerede CDS testet i
et CRI-SPA set-up ved at overføre biosynesevejen for bikaverin produktion til et udvalg af gær
deletions-biblioteket.
Det er vist at det er muligt at frigøre tre forskellige gRNA’er fra det samme plasmid ved hjælp
af en strategi omfattende af b˚ade ﬂankerende tRNA’er og direkte gentagelser, hvilket illustrerer
en ny metode til multipleksing ved brug af MAD7. En ny CDS blev konstrueret ved hjælp
af multiplekseringsstrategien, og det blev vist at det var muligt at overføre ekspressionen af
bikaverin fra CDS-stammen til et bibliotek af modtager gærstammer. De hits der genereres fra
CRI-SPA screeningen af bikaverin ligner tidligere resultater ved brug af det nuværende CRI-SPA
system. Denne undersøgelse rapporterer det første brug af et gRNA-plasmid indeholdende
en auxotrof markør, snarere end en antibiotika markør, til overførsel til modtagerbiblioteket
i CRI-SPA. Dette udvider mulighederne for at arbejde med CRI-SPA systemet. Det er vist,
at den dobbelte inducerbare-galaktose promotor ikke er utæt, og, at en enkelt kopi af TK-
markøren, udtrykt p˚a et hvilket som helst af de 16 kromosomer, nok til at dræbe selv en diploid
celle. Samlet set er resultaterne vedrørende det nye CRI-SPA system baseret p˚a en dobbelt
galaktose-promotor og en dominerende mod-selektions TK-markør lovende.

Introduction
Yeast is commonly used as a cell factory to produce valuable products [1]. Commonly known,
insulin is used to treat diabetes, and half of all insulin is produced in yeast, marking its
importance as a cell factory [2, 3]. There is a growing demand for more sustainable production
to which bio-based production of valuable products can greatly contribute, especially in the
bio-fuel and chemical industries [4]. To this end, eﬃcient gene editing tools and screening
methods are necessary. Thus, any advances that can speed up gene editing techniques or
screening for cell factory engineering are valuable.
CRISPR-Selective Ploidy Ablation (CRI-SPA) [5] is a new high-throughput method that builds
on the methods SPA [6], involving the high-throughput transfer of a plasmid to a recipient
library, and CRISPR, involving the targeted cut in the genome using a nuclease followed by
repair using a template with homology to the cut site [7]. A CRI-SPA donor strain (CDS)
carries out the nuclease-mediated transfer of a gene of interest (GOI) or a whole pathway to
the genome of the recipient strains. The current CRI-SPA system utilizes the nuclease Cas9
for the cut. However, there are some IP issues related to the use of Cas9 [8]. The CRI-SPA
system was built using a strain containing a pGAL1-URA3 cassette on every chromosome used
for destabilizing the centromeres to induce chromosome loss followed by counter-selection of
the ura3 gene. This strain was constructed for SPA [6], i.e., its use is limited to the terms of
its Material Transfer Agreement (MTA). The system is also dependent on the recipient library
being Ura- (i.e., they are not able to produce uracil on their own), which is a metabolic defect
[9] and limits the potential of the system. Thus, building a new version of the CRI-SPA system
that considers these facts would be advantageous.
An alternative to ura3 could be the thymidine kinase (TK) marker gene, as this is dominant in
yeast [10, 11] and could remove constraints on the recipient library. Currently, a limitation of
the CRI-SPA system is that recipient strains must be Ura-. As the deletion of ura3 changes the
cell metabolism [9], it may also a↵ect how a GOI or pathway is produced and may give false
positive or false negative hits. It has previously been shown that using the TK marker, even in
multiple copies, should be a viable strategy for counter-selection in yeast [12].

Aim of Project
This study aims to construct a new donor strain for CRI-SPA that overcomes the challenges
of the current system described above. The following features need to be implemented in the
new CDS: 1) a host free of MTA issues, 2) an endonuclease free of IP issues, e.g., MAD7,
and 3) a counter-selectable marker that removes the requirement of inherent markers in the
recipient libraries, thus expanding the possibilities for screening. To achieve this, a cassette
containing a dual galactose inducible promoter (pGAL1/pGAL10) and the dominant counter-
selectable marker thymidine kinase (TK) are to be integrated in front of the centromere in
all 16 chromosomes in the new CDS. The starting point of the experimental work performed
during this thesis was a previously constructed strain named sDIV510 [13]. This strain expresses
MAD7 and contains the pGAL1/GAL10-TK-tTEF1 cassette in front of the centromeres on Chr.
XV-XVI.
To accomplish the overall aim of this study, some sub-objectives have been deﬁned:
Part I: Develop new CDS
In this study, a new CDS will be constructed by a building-on-top strategy using sDIV510
as a starting point. The aim is to increase the cassette number until constructing a strain
containing the pGAL1/GAL10-TK-tTEF1 cassette on all 16 chromosomes, as illustrated in
Figure 2.1. When galactose is present, the pGAL1 promoter will start transcribing the cen-
tromeric DNA (CEN), thus destabilizing the centromere and inducing chromosome loss. When
5-ﬂuorodeoxyuridine (FUdR) is present, cells still containing the cassette are e↵ectively killed
because they express the marker TK turning FUdR into a toxic compound.
Part II: Quality control to ensure loss of all chromosomes
To test if all chromosomes can be lost, 16 single integration strains are constructed using MAD7-
expression yeast strains of opposite mating types as parental strains. The single integration
strains will have the chromosome containing the pGAL1/GAL10-TK-tTEF1 cassette tagged
with a mVenus-natMX cassette encoding a yellow ﬂuorescent protein (YFP) and resistance
against nourseothricin (NTC). These strains are mated to the parental strains of opposite mating
types, and the phenotype is followed when the centromere is disrupted. The aim is to validate
that all chromosomes can be lost.
Part III: Test developed donor strain using CRI-SPA on yeast deletion library
The constructed CDS is tested in CRI-SPA to validate its ability to transfer a biosynthetic
pathway to the deletion library. This involves mating the CDS to the library to form diploids,
activating the dual galactose-inducible promoter to induce selective chromosome loss of the
donor chromosomes, and counter-selection against any cells still containing the pGAL1/GAL10-
TK-tTEF1 cassette using the TK marker. The aim is to validate whether the new system works
in practice.

Gene Editing in Yeast: S. cerevisiae as a Valuable Cell
Factory
This section serves as the basis for understanding this study’s experimental strategy and related
thought processes. Relevant topics related to the gene editing of yeast will be explored to give
an introduction to past, present, and possibly future research relevant to this study.
3.1
Introduction to S. cerevisiae
Saccharomyces cerevisiae, also commonly known as Baker’s yeast, Brewer’s yeast, or Budding
yeast, is one of the most well-characterized yeast species due to its usage in baking, brewing,
and wine-making [14]. It was the ﬁrst organism to have its full genome sequenced [15]. Many of
the cellular processes in S. cerevisiae are conserved in higher eukaryotes, i.e., humans. Hence,
it has been used as a model organism to study biological processes [16], e.g., the cell cycle
[14]. S. cerevisiae is granted the ’generally regarded as safe’ (GRAS) classiﬁcation by the
FDA [14, 17], which means that it can be safely used in processes for food and pharmaceutical
industries. S. cerevisiae is a commonly used production host for a wide range of valuable
products, e.g., for the production of biofuels, biochemicals, and recombinant proteins [3, 16,
17]. The production of insulin to treat diabetes is probably the most well-known recombinant
protein produced in S. cerevisiae. Today, half of all insulin produced worldwide is produced
using S. cerevisiae as cell factory [2, 3]. In fact, yeast is used as a cell factory to produce 1/6
of all bio-pharmaceutical products approved for human use [1], marking both the importance
and value of eﬃcient engineering in yeast. In the area of biofuels, S. cerevisiae is commonly
used for the production of ethanol [16, 18], and is now being explored as a cell factory for
the production of other biofuels, e.g., n-butanol [18, 19]. Thus, there is great interest in the
continued research to better S. cerevisiae as a cell factory host and to improve methods for
eﬃcient genetic engineering and screening.
3.1.1
Mating types: MATa and MATa
In research, the S. cerevisiae strain, S288C, is the strain of which the full genome was originally
sequenced [15], and thus, is used as reference strain [20]. Today, many laboratory strains are
derivatives of this [21], and S288C has the beneﬁt of maintaining a stable mating type [20].
The yeast species S. cerevisiae can live in three di↵erent cell states: haploid of which it can
be of either mating type a (MATa) or a (MATa), or it can be a MATa/a diploid [22]. This is
determined by which gene is expressed in the MAT locus [22]. The MATa type is expressed from
a single gene, MATa1, while the MATa type is expressed by the two genes MATa1 and MATa2
[22]. In literature, Ya and Ya are sometimes used to describe the mating type-speciﬁc DNA,
where Ya encompasses the MATa1 gene, and Ya encompasses the MATa1 and MATa2 [22, 23].
Expression of either a or a at the MAT locus in a haploid cell is described as the cell’s mating
type. Mating to form a diploid cell occurs between two haploid cells, where one expresses MATa
and the other expresses MATa [22].
In a haploid cell, the gene expressed at the MAT locus can be changed by a process called
mating type switching by rearranging the DNA [22]. The cell contains additional silent copies of
both the a and a genes at positions close to the MAT locus, denoted hidden MAT left a (HMLa)
and hidden MAT right a (HMRa) [23, 24]. The HO-endonuclease is a site-speciﬁc endonuclease
that can cut and make a double-stranded DNA break (DSB) in the MAT locus [23]. The cell
contains additional silent copies of Ya and Ya at HMRa and HMLa, and these sequences can be

stable mating type (MATa or MATa, respectively) or exist as diploids, which can be sporulated
to form four daughter cells [27, 28]. Unlike the stable, laboratory yeast strains, industrial yeast
strains are more genetically diverse in that they are usually prototrophic, homothallic, and
can show abnormal ploidy (number of chromosomes), e.g., aneuploidy (the loss or gain of a
chromosome) or polyploidy (having more than two sets of each chromosome) [9, 24, 27, 28].
3.1.2
Chromosome segregation
For a cell to grow, it must duplicate its DNA to pass on a copy of each chromosome to its
daughter cell and ensure that all the genetic information is preserved. For S. cerevisiae, this
means that each of its 16 chromosomes must be duplicated and a copy of each passed onto its
daughter cell. The cell cycle describes the events leading up to cell division [29], i.e., when the
cell has duplicated all its contents and divides by budding of a smaller daughter cell (hence, the
name budding yeast) [30]. The cell cycle consists of di↵erent phases, or stages, with di↵erent
functions. An illustration of the cell cycle phases can be seen in Figure 3.2. First, the cell
increases in size in Gap 1 (G1), followed by the synthesis (S) phase, where DNA replication
occurs to duplicate each chromosome [31]. Each of the duplicated chromosomes consists of two
sister chromatids held together by cohesin, a complex of proteins forming a ring-like structure
that causes the sister chromatids to stick together [29]. In the Gap 2 (G2) phase, the cell
grows and synthesizes all the necessary proteins before proceeding to the mitotic (M) phase [31].
In the M phase (divided into subphases prophase, prometaphase, metaphase, anaphase, and
telophase), the sister chromatids are segregated equally between the mother cell and daughter
cell [31]. The cell cycle is completed with cytokinesis, during which the connection between

the cohesion keeping the sister chromatids together must be cleaved to allow segregation [37].

ensures that the chromosomes do not segregate before they have the correct orientation [29,

[35]. The orientation is measured by the tension at the kinetochore, as only a bi-orientation
will provide the correct tension [35]. When the tension is correct, cohesion is cleaved by the
enzyme separase. The cleavage of cohesion triggers sister chromatid separation [29]. The cell
may undergo chromosome segregation even if the tension at the kinetochores is wrong, i.e., if a
chromatid is either not connected or wrongly connected, resulting in aneuploidy [37].
Recently, researchers have experimented with the fusion of chromosomes to prevent sporulation,
thereby changing the number of chromosomes the cell contains (called karyotype engineering)
[38]. As all chromosomes contain information needed for the survival of the cell, the loss of a
chromosome is detrimental. This means that if two cells with di↵erent numbers of chromosomes
mate and form a diploid, sporulation cannot proceed, as the uneven number of chromosomes can
lead to chromosome loss during the segregation of chromosomes. Thus, by fusing chromosomes,
it is possible to change the number of chromosomes a cell has and prevent unwanted sporulation
from happening. For example, if a laboratory yeast strain were to escape the wild, it would be
unable to pass on its genetics.

3.1.3
Genetic engineering in yeast
Genetic engineering is based on targeted changes to an organism’s DNA [14]. S. cerevisiae is
a relatively easy-to-engineer organism due to the existence of high-eﬃciency transformation
protocols [39, 40], and the genetic toolbox comprising both known promoters, terminators,
reporter genes, and selection genes is vast [14]. Because the whole genome of S. cerevisiae have
been sequenced [15], there now exists online databases, e.g., Saccharomyces Genome Database
(https://www.yeastgenome.org/), with information regarding chromosomes, biological processes,
phenotypes, pathways, and more.
In genetic engineering, it is advantageous to quickly be able to screen and select cells that have
received the desired genetic changes. For example, the ade2 gene, which encodes an enzyme in
the adenine biosynthesis pathway, can be deleted. The cell will start to accumulate a pathway
intermediate that is a red pigment. Thus, cells with the deletion can be easily screened for
as they appear bright red compared to a WT cell [41]. In general, cells that have received
DNA can be selected using marker genes or screened for using reporter genes. A wide array of
selection markers [42, 43] and reporter genes [44] exist that can be utilized to identify cells with
the desired genetic changes.
Transformed cells can be screened using reporter genes that can be used to visualize if cells
express the transformed DNA and to what degree, e.g., if it encodes a pigment that gives a
distinct color change in transformed cells or a ﬂuorescent protein, e.g., the mVenus gene encodes
a yellow-ﬂuorescence protein (YFP) [44], and ﬂuorescence can be used to screen for cells that
have integrated this fragment. Selection using antibiotic markers is a type of dominant selection
as cells can only survive if they have received the antibiotic resistance gene [42]. Some common
antibiotic resistance genes are natMX, kanMX, or hph, which encodes for resistance towards
nouresothricin (NTC), geneticin (G418), and hygromycin B (hygB), respectively. [43, 45]. The
mentioned antibiotic resistance genes all work by encoding enzymes that bind and inactivate an
antibiotic compound. natMX encodes an NTC acetyltransferase that can acetylate the b-amino
group on the b-lysine amino acid in NTC [46]. Both hph and kanMX encode phosphotransferases
[47, 48] that phosphorylates hygB and G418, respectively, rendering the antibiotics inactivated.
In nature, wild-type (WT) yeasts are prototrophic, meaning that they can produce all the
amino acids they need to survive themselves. Many laboratory yeast strains have one or more
auxotrophies [9, 10], i.e., one or more genes in an amino acid pathway have been either deleted or
mutated, so they are non-functional. Thus, the strain becomes dependent on getting the amino
acid supplemented for survival. This serves two purposes: 1) this ensures that a laboratory
yeast strain cannot escape to the wild and survive [49], and 2) this makes it possible to select
for laboratory yeast strains that have received a DNA construct with a gene that restores an
auxotrophy [42]. Auxotrophic genes restore a cell metabolism pathway so that the cell no longer
requires a speciﬁc amino acid supplement to grow but can produce it itself. For example by
introducing a plasmid bearing a copy of a WT gene to complement the auxotrophy. Some
commonly used auxotrophic markers are the ura3, his3, and leu2 genes [42] that restore genes
in the uracil (ura), histidine (his), and leucine (leu) biosynthetic pathways, respectively.
The progress in genetic engineering techniques and the development of novel high-throughput
methods have accelerated cell factory engineering and will likely continue to do so in the future
[5, 50]. For example, USER fusion is an eﬃcient cloning technique for constructing plasmids
and cloning multiple pieces of DNA together to form a construct [50, 51]. Constructed plasmids
are often ”shuttle vectors”, i.e., they can shuttle between di↵erent organism [17, 52, 53]. Often,
plasmids are ampliﬁed in E. coli before being transformed into S. cerevisiae cells with high
eﬃciency [39, 40].

3.2
DNA repair pathways for double-stranded breaks
In this subsection, an introduction to DNA repair pathways for DSB using HR and NHEJ will be
given. DNA contains all the information needed to survive. Therefore, any damage to the DNA,
such as a DNA break, can be detrimental to a cell if it is not repaired correctly [56, 57]. If DNA
is damaged, the cell will arrest its growth until it is repaired. If the cell continues its progression
through the cell cycle with damaged DNA, e.g., a DSB, it may cause aneuploidy due to the
missegregation followed by degradation of the two DNA ends [58]. There are two main pathways
for the repair of double-stranded breaks (DSB) in DNA: Homologous recombination (HR) and
non-homologous end-joining (NHEJ). The preferred pathway depends on several factors, e.g.,
cell cycle, organism, and cell state (haploid/diploid) [57]. In the NHEJ pathway, the two ends
of the break are ligated together following the break [57]. NHEJ can lead to a perfect repair
or mutations by deleting or inserting bases when the break is repaired [8, 59, 60]. In HR, the
repair can only be ﬁxed if there is DNA with homology to the break present that can be used
as a template for the repair. If such a template is present, the repair is usually error-free [57,
59]. An illustration of the HR and NHEJ is shown in Figure 3.4. This is a simpliﬁed description
of NHEJ and HR, and both include di↵erent subtypes of repair.
Several factors inﬂuence the repair pathway favored. As described previously, the cell cycle
involves G1, S, G2, and M phases followed by cytokinesis. HR is the primary repair pathway
for DSB in S. cerevisiae and is favored at all stages of the cell cycle [59]. However, in the
G1 phase, NHEJ is usually utilized for repairs, as no repair template is present. HR is highly
eﬃcient in the S and G2 phases due to the presence of a sister chromatid that can be used as
a repair template via HR [57]. In diploids, NHEJ is downregulated, partly due to the protein
NHEJ regulator 1 (Nej1) that is expressed by a haploid-speciﬁc gene and, therefore, repressed
in diploids with both mating types (MATa/a) [61].
HR can lead to gene conversion, which is the non-reciprocal (i.e., one-way) transfer of genetic
information [55, 62]. Gene conversion can happen with or without crossing-over events [58, 62].
An illustration of di↵erent subtypes of HR is shown in Figure 3.5. Several HR mechanisms
can repair a DSB, but all start with the 5’-3’ resection of the ends at the break carried out by
5’-3’ exonucleases [58]. Synthesis-dependent strand annealing (SDSA), break-induced repair
(BIR), and double-strand break repair (DSBR) all result in gene conversion. For SDSA, there
are two models [62]: One proposes that both single-stranded DNA (ssDNA) ends of the break
invade and begin DNA synthesis at the homologous template before dissociating and annealing
to each other before ligation and gap-ﬁlling [62]. The other proposes that only one end of the
break invades a homologous template, starts DNA synthesis, and then anneals to the other
ssDNA end before the gaps are ﬁlled in [58, 62]. In DSBR, one of the ssDNA ends invades the
template and forms a displacement loop (D-loop), and starts DNA synthesis. Then the other 3’
end anneals to the template and DNA synthesis is also started here. Following DNA synthesis,
the 3’ ends are ligated to the 5’ ends, resulting in the formation of a double Holliday Junction
(dHJ) [62], that must be removed before the chromosomes can segregate. This can happen by

3.3
Metabolic engineering
As mentioned in Section 3.1, a wide range of products are produced using S. cerevisiae as
a cell factory. It is often needed to optimize the metabolic ﬂux to improve the production
of a given product in a host organism. This was previously limited to methods that allowed
the accumulation of random mutations [66, 67], e.g., using radiation or chemicals to induce
mutagenesis followed by extensive screening of strains that have gained beneﬁcial mutations
[66]. However, screening is extremely time-consuming, and it is uncertain if any strains will
have superior production capabilities from gaining random mutations. Furthermore, strains will
accumulate several mutations, making it diﬃcult to ﬁnd the exact mutation(s) responsible for
the desired phenotype [14]. In addition, random mutations o↵er no knowledge regarding the
function or regulation of biosynthetic pathways. With the evolution of genetic engineering, it
became possible to make directed mutations in metabolic pathways [67]. Metabolic engineering
is deﬁned as the directed engineering of metabolic pathways that improve the production of a
given product or introduce new metabolic pathways [66]. Thus, metabolic engineering is based
on genetic engineering [14].
The production of recombinant proteins by heterologous expression in S. cerevisiae is driven
by economics. For a product to reach the market, it must be proﬁtable, which is dependent
on the manufacturing price [67]. Thus, the improvement of strains to lower the production
cost is of great interest. This can be done by improving the product yield or changing the raw
material to a cheaper alternative, e.g., changing the carbon source from glucose to starch [67].
Introducing pathways in cell factories for the production of new products can also lower the
production price, if it is cheaper to produce the product in a cell factory, contra the current
production method. For example, many complex molecules are produced in plants and are hard
to synthesize chemically. If such products were produced in microbial cell factories, it would
greatly reduce manufacturing prices and provide a more sustainable alternative [68, 69].
A new addition to the metabolic engineering toolbox is the development of genome-scale
metabolic models (GEMs). These are mathematical models that can simulate and predict
changes in metabolic ﬂuxes. GEMs can be used to simulate the e↵ect of deleting a gene in
a metabolic pathway or the e↵ect changing the carbon source from glucose to galactose will
have on production [70]. Simulations provide information on how growth or production rates
could look and provide information that can be used to predict gene targets for cell factory
engineering. A GEM uses gene-protein-reaction relationships generated from genome annotation
data and published literature [70]. Thus, a GEM is only as good as the data available. This
means that the more data, the better these models become, which will further help facilitate
cell factory engineering [5, 70].
3.3.1
Reconstructing the bikaverin pathway in S. cerevisiae
Genetic engineering has allowed the expression of complex structures in yeast. The production
of the fungal polyketide bikaverin in S. cerevisiae [71] is an example of introducing a new
metabolic pathway to a heterologous organism [66]. Bikaverin was produced in S. cerevisiae by
reconstructing the pathway through intensive understanding and engineering of the bikaverin
pathway [71]. Bikaverin is therapeutically relevant as it is suggested to have both antifungal,
antibiotic, and anticancer properties [72]. To produce bikaverin in S. cerevisiae, the pathway genes
bik1, bik2, bik3, and bik6 were introduced on a plasmid vector [71]. The genes encode di↵erent
enzymes needed in the bikaverin pathway: a polyketide synthase (bik1), a monooxygenase
(bik2), a methyltransferase (bik3), and a permease (bik6). In addition, the genes ppt1 and npgA
were also introduced on the plasmid as they encode phosphopantetheinyl transferases (PPTases),
11
==PAGE:21==
that are needed for the activation of Bik1 to become functional [71]. It was found that the
expression of Bik1-3 and the two PPTases were essential and enough to produce bikaverin. As
bikaverin is a red pigment, colonies producing bikaverin are easily identiﬁed by their distinct red
color [71, 72]. Cases like the heterologous expression of bikaverin emphasize the possibilities for
producing complex structures in yeast. If such a complex structure as the polyketide bikaverin
can be produced, other therapeutically relevant and complex structures can surely be produced
in the easy-to-engineer S. cerevisiae.
3.4
Gene editing technologies
In yeast, several well-deﬁned gene editing technologies exist which can be used to facilitate
the construction of cell factories. In this subsection, relevant gene editing technologies and
developments on those technologies will be introduced.
3.4.1
Synthetic genetic analysis
A. H. Y. Tong et al. (2001) created a method termed ”Synthetic Genetic Analysis” (SGA) that
can be used to construct double mutants in an organized manner by crossing one yeast strain
(MATa) containing a mutation that one wishes to investigate to a vast library (⇡4700) of yeast
strains (MATa) all carrying di↵erent deletion mutations [73]. The method is a high-throughput
and relies on the use of dominant antibiotic markers. The mutation to be investigated is linked
to a natMX resistance gene, and the mutations in the deletion library strains are linked to a
kanMX resistance gene. The formed diploid cells can be selected on media containing both
antibiotics and made to sporulate by transfer to media with reduced carbon and nitrogen content.
Haploids with mating type MATa (i.e., the deletion strains) can be selected on media lacking
the amino acid histidine (his) due to the presence of a MATa-speciﬁc reporter (MFA1pr-HIS3)
that is only expressed in MATa cells but repressed in both MATa and MATa/a diploids. The
MATa-speciﬁc reporter restores auxotrophy for histidine when active, i.e., only MATa cells
will be able to grow when histidine is absent. The MATa haploid cells are then transferred to
media containing both NTC and G418 to select for MATa double mutants and scored based
on ﬁtness, i.e., how many spores survive and how they are growing compared to WT. This
information is used to identify redundant genes and study synthetic lethality [73]. Synthetic
lethality describes when two (or more) mutations together are lethal for the cell, but both of
the mutations by themselves are not [74]. Because SGA is a high-throughput method to create
double mutants, it is perfect to study and identify new synthetic lethal relationships between
genes. A synthetic lethal relationship between two genes could be if both genes are essential
in two di↵erent pathways that can substitute for each other, i.e., if one is deleted, the other
pathway compensates, and if both are deleted, the cell cannot compensate, and dies [73, 74].
3.4.2
Selective ploidy ablation
A. Hill and K. Bloom (1987) showed that introducing a conditional element upstream (UP)
of the centromere structure can disrupt centromere function if it activates transcription of
the centromeric DNA [75]. This is possible due to the small size of the centromere (⇡125
bp) [33, 34]. Using homologous recombination between the centromeric DNA of Chr. III
(CEN3), in a Ura- S. cerevisiae yeast strain, a fragment containing a ura3 auxotrophic marker,
and a galactose-inducible promoter (pGAL1) UP of the centromere, successfully replaced the
WT centromere with a conditional centromere responding to the presence of galactose [75].
Galactose promoters are commonly used inducible promoters, i.e., only active under certain
12
==PAGE:22==
conditions, in contrast to constitutive promoters (e.g., pTEF1), which are always active [76].
A galactose-inducible promoter is only active when galactose is present and if the preferred
carbon source, glucose, is absent [77], i.e., the condition for its expression is that galactose is
the sole carbon source. The strain containing the conditional centromere on Chr. III was mated
with a haploid strain of the opposite mating type to form a diploid with one WT CEN3 and
one conditional CEN3 [75]. In this set-up, the presence of galactose should lead to centromere
destabilization and subsequent LOH, as the diploid would only have the WT Chr. III left. Thus,
the associated Ura+ phenotype is lost. As expected, the induction of the pGAL1 in front of the
conditional CEN3, in fact, led to centromere destabilization and resulted in the loss of Chr. III
and simultaneous loss of the Ura+ phenotype in the diploid [75].
More than 20 years later, Reid et al. (2008) built on this information and expanded the concept
of a conditional centromere from one chromosome to all 16 chromosomes (I-XVI) [78]. By
introducing the pGAL1 in front of the centromere, as well as a ura3 marker gene, on all 16
chromosomes in individual yeast strains, 16 single integrations strains, each containing one
conditional centromere, were created. The single integration strains were mated with auxotrophic
strains containing the WT centromere. The formed diploids had induced chromosome loss due
to centromere destabilization of the conditional centromere when grown in media containing
galactose. Chromosome loss was conﬁrmed using counter-selection of the ura3 marker with
5-ﬂuoroorotic acid (5-FOA). Cells expressing the ura3 marker gene will die when grown in the
presence of 5-FOA due to the activity of orotidine-5’-monophosphate (OMP) decarboxylase,
encoded by ura3, that turns 5-FOA into the compound 5-ﬂuorouracil, which is toxic,[10, 79,
80], thus killing all cells still containing the chromosome with the ura3 marker gene and the
conditional centromere. The resulting strains were further validated for chromosome loss by
testing for auxotrophy by their loss of ability to grow when plated on media without the
auxotrophic marker found on the chromosome lost [78].
From one chromosome to all chromosomes in single integrations, Reid et al. (2011) further
expanded the use of a conditional centromere by creating a strain with a conditional centromere
on all 16 chromosomes in both mating types, thus combining the information from the 16 single
integrations in one [6]. The resulting strains contained a pGAL1 in front of the centromere
and the ura3 marker gene on every chromosome. The strains were used to develop a new
high-throughput method termed ”Selective ploidy ablation” (SPA), in which a strain containing
the conditional centromeres is used as a universal donor strain (UDS) [6]. SPA builds on the
method SGA [73] in that one yeast strain acts as a donor for a trait that can be passed onto a vast
library of recipient yeast strains using a high-throughput method, but instead of investigating
synthetic lethal double mutations, the library is used to screen for altered expression from a
transferred plasmid vector [6]. SPA also eliminates the waiting step of sporulation of diploids
that is used in SGA [73] for haploidization.
To test the functionality, the UDS strains were mated to 21 di↵erent strains containing di↵erent
auxotrophic marker genes covering the 16 chromosomes [6]. In the resulting diploids, the
auxotrophic recipient alleles were complemented by the UDS alleles. All 21 diploid strains were
put through the process of centromere destabilization and counter-selection with 5-FOA to
validate if the diploid strain could lose the donor chromosomes. The diploids were evaluated by
their ability to mate, as the ability to mate indicates loss of Chr. III locus that contains the genes
expressing the MATa and MATa mating types. Furthermore, the diploids were auxotrophic for
all the markers found on the ablated UDS chromosome.
LOH of every donor chromosome, in reality, leads to haploidization of the diploid strain to a
haploid strain, as only the recipient chromosomes are left following centromere destabilization
and counter-selection with 5-FOA. For SPA, the UDS is transformed with a plasmid which
13
==PAGE:23==
can then be transferred from the UDS to a large library of recipient strains upon mating by
automation. Following mating, the donor chromosomes are lost by two rounds of plating: First
on media containing galactose to disrupt the centromere and induce LOH, and then on media
containing 5-FOA to select for cells that lost all donor chromosomes and became haploid recipient
strains now containing the transferred plasmid [6]. Hence, it was successfully demonstrated
that the SPA procedure could be used for haploidization to the original recipient strain by
eliminating, or ablating, the chromosomes of the donor strain without the need for sporulation
(i.e., meiosis), as is required for SGA [73], which could otherwise lead to unwanted homologous
recombination events between the chromosomes of the donor strain and the recipient strain.
3.4.3
CRISPR-Cas9
CRISPR functions as the bacterial adaptive immune system [60], e.g., upon infection of the
bacteria with a phage, the phage will try to incorporate its DNA into the host bacterium.
As a defense mechanism against CRISPR acts by cleaving up the foreign DNA before it can
integrate into the bacterial genome. To do this, the CRISPR type II system integrates small
DNA sequences, called spacers, of foreign DNA into special sites in the bacterial genome, called
CRISPR loci [60, 81]. These sites are ﬁlled with sequences called clustered regularly interspaced
short palindromic repeats (i.e., CRISPR) and the spacers are incorporated in between the
CRISPR repeats [60]. At these sites, the DNA sequences can be transcribed and processed
into CRISPR RNAs (crRNAs) that are used to recognize foreign DNA, e.g., from an invading
phage. This is possible as the crRNA contains a variable part consisting of a small transcript
from the foreign DNA known as a protospacer sequence [60]. Depending on the speciﬁc system,
the crRNA either functions as a guide RNA (gRNA) alone or needs to pair with another RNA
molecule to be functional. For the latter, the crRNA forms a complex with a trans-activating
CRISPR RNA (tracRNA) [60, 82]. The protospacer sequence on the crRNA is complementary
to the target DNA and directs an endonuclease to cleave the DNA if a speciﬁc sequence, called
the protospacer adjacent motif (PAM) sequence, is also present at the site [60]. Hence, providing
an e↵ective defense against foreign DNA.
Class 2 CRISPR systems use a single multi-domain Cas protein which can be reprogrammed
by its crRNA to target any site wanted [82]. For this reason, class 2 CRISPR systems are
attractive to work with compared to class 1 systems which require complexes of hetero multimers,
i.e., several Cas proteins, to function [82]. Genome editing using CRISPR is therefore based
on class 2 systems utilizing a single Cas protein loaded with a crRNA [82]. The most used
nuclease is Cas9, an endonuclease from the CRISPR type II system in Streptococcus pyogenes
[60]. CRISPR-Cas9 technology is widely used for gene editing for a whole range of purposes. It
is used for targeted genetic engineering of cell factories to insert genes or whole pathways [71,
83, 84], and can be used therapeutically to treat human diseases [60], e.g., it may be used to
target a sick allele in an individual and replace it with a healthy allele.
The protospacer sequence determines the speciﬁcity of a gRNA as it is the part of the gRNA
that is homologous to the target site, i.e., by changing the protospacer sequence, the target site
can be changed to any site desired [54, 60]. The PAM sequence is an additional requirement
that needs to be present for the nuclease to be able to make a cut [60]. Hence, the CRISPR
system can be extorted by genetic engineering, as the sequence recognized can be easily altered
to recognize any target wanted if the PAM sequence is also present. A genetic engineer can
create a synthetic gRNA that functions without processing by replacing the tracrRNA-crRNA
complex with a single guide (sgRNA), eliminating the maturation steps for the guide [85]. The
sgRNA is formed by the fusion of the tracrRNA and crRNA molecules, hence creating a dual
RNA molecule. The gRNA is transcribed in a large mRNA, and upon mRNA processing, the
14
==PAGE:24==
gRNA is released and can lead its associated nuclease to the target site [82]. Thus, by expressing
a nuclease and its gRNA, a targeted break can be made in the cell. If the cell is also provided
with an RT with homology to the site where the breakage happened, the break can be repaired
eﬃciently [60, 82]. The RT can be constructed with any wanted deletion or insertion of DNA
[82], e.g., of a new gene or genes, and can easily be inserted at the target site.
As described in Section 3.1.3, fragments with homology can spontaneously integrate onto the
chromosome. However, higher eﬃciencies can be achieved if the transformation is coupled with
a DSB [54, 55, 60], e.g., from the expression of a nuclease and a gRNA to target the homologous
sequence on the chromosome and make a DSB. This will prompt the cells to use the transformed
fragment for the repair due to the homologous sequences [82]. It is, therefore, an extremely
powerful tool in the gene editing toolbox. If a nuclease, e.g., Cas9, causes the DSB, the nuclease
will make a DSB as long as it can recognize the target site. Over generations, this will eventually
either result in indels from ﬂawed NHEJ repair or cell death if the break is not repaired [8, 83].
If a site with homology is present, e.g., a RT, the cell can use HR to repair the break [60].
A TAPE (Technique to Assess Protospacer Eﬃciency) experiment can be carried out to determine
how eﬃcient a protospacer sequence is in targeting a speciﬁc site [84]. It involves targeting a site
using a gRNA to guide the nuclease but without providing the cell with a RT to ﬁx the break.
Without a RT, the cell will have continuous rounds of DNA break caused by gRNA guiding the
nuclease until it fails to repair the break and dies or mutates the target site or PAM site, so the
gRNA or nuclease no longer recognizes it. This experiment can determine how e↵ective the
gRNA is and, thus, if a new protospacer sequence should be designed and tested. Since the
preferred pathway is HR [59], not many cells will use the NHEJ pathway to repair the break.
NHEJ may also take generations to make a mutation [8, 83], as some will give a perfect repair,
until Cas9 will no longer recognize the target site and cut. If a RT is given, the cell can repair the
DSB with HR, and if the template contains speciﬁc mutations or whole genes/pathways, these
will be incorporated instead. Thus, most cells will die as they cannot repair the break when
there is no RT to use for DNA repair, although some may survive by repairing the break with
ﬂawed NHEJ. By comparing the number of surviving cells from di↵erent TAPE experiments
(i.e., testing di↵erent protospacer sequences) with the corresponding transformation plates (of
cells that were given a RT), it is possible to evaluate which protospacer works the best for a
given target site [84].
3.4.4
CRI-SPA: A new high-throughput method
Genetic engineering with CRISPR technology depends on the use of a nuclease, most often Cas9,
accompanied by a gRNA to a target sequence where the nuclease will make a DNA break when
it encounters its recognition sequence (a PAM) [82]. However, a limitation of CRISPR-Cas9
engineering is that it is not high-throughput. In SPA, a plasmid is transferred by mating of
compatible yeast strains and subsequent removal of the plasmid donor chromosomes by inducing
the conditional centromeres, following counter-selection with 5-FOA to select for cells that have
lost every donor chromosome [6]. SPA, on the other hand, is, however, limited to the transfer of
plasmids, which are not stable [17].
The information from both CRISPR-Cas9 technology and SPA was combined by Cachera
et al. (2022) to create a new and improved method termed ”CRI-SPA” [5]. CRI-SPA is a
high-throughput method using a CRI-SPA donor strain (CDS) to transfer a gene of interest
(GOI), or pathway, to a recipient library of Ura- yeast strains using Cas9-induced DSB and
subsequent repair using homologous sequences present on the CDS chromosome ﬂanking the GOI
or pathway. Hence, the CDS chromosome is used as an RT for the break. For the construction
15
==PAGE:25==
of the CDS, the UDS strains containing a pGAL1 in front of the centromere and the ura3
marker gene on every chromosome for SPA [6] were used as starting point. The UDS had
cas9 integrated on Chr. X, site 3 (X-3), as well as the auxotrophic gene leu2 to select for
transformants that have become prototrophic for leucine, i.e., have integrated cas9 [5]. The
CDS is further modiﬁed to contain the GOI at a speciﬁc site so that the Chr. of the CDS is used
as the RT for the break. The CDS strain will transfer a GOI by inducing a Cas9-mediated DSB
on the recipient chromosome following mating. The break is repaired using the GOI containing
homologous sequences located on the CDS genome. The CDS chromosomes are then lost by
inducing centromere destabilization in the presence of galactose. The cells still containing
any of the CDS chromosomes or that have performed homologous recombination events are
counter-selected against using the ura3 marker gene located only on the CDS chromosomes
using 5-FOA [79, 80]. Only cells still harboring any of the CDS chromosomes will express the
ura3 gene. Thus, the strain is haploidized back to the original recipient strain, now containing
the GOI. The strains can be assessed for improved production, and relevant phenotypes can be
coupled to the genetic background, e.g., if a deletion strain has an interesting phenotype, this
can be coupled to the speciﬁc deletion responsible for this phenotype. The speciﬁc deletion can
then be introduced into the production strain and evaluated for improved production.
CRI-SPA has several advantages as the new system gains the high-throughput screening from
SPA coupled with stable genome integration and versatile genetic engineering from utilizing the
CRISPR system. As a system, CRI-SPA is commercially attractive because it can be automated,
allowing simultaneous screening of thousands of strains and saving a vast amount of time.
When automated, the process can be completed in under a week and done in quadruplicates to
produce reliable results. In addition, CRI-SPA screens generate huge amounts of data that can
be used for systems biology [5], e.g., to improve GEMs, which will further accelerate not only
the understanding of biological processes but also cell factory engineering.
3.5
Beyond Cas9: Exploring the alternative nuclease MAD7
The current system for CRISPR genetic engineering is mostly based on the use of Cas9. However,
there are Intellectual Property (IP) issues related to the use of Cas9 as the owner of the IP
rights has not been determined [8]. Therefore, it is uncertain if it can be freely used, e.g., for
industrial research. Thus, the Cas9 system is not commercially attractive and some companies
may avoid working with it. Other nucleases exist, e.g., Cpf1 and MAD7. Cpf1 (also known
as Cas12a) is a Cas12a nuclease originating from the bacterium Acidaminococcus. MAD7 is
a codon-optimized Cas12a nuclease originating from Eubacterium rectale, structurally similar
to Cpf1, and commercially available at Inscripta Inc. [86]. Unlike Cas9, MAD7 was released
to be used freely for most research and commercial purposes royalty-free [87, 88]. This makes
MAD7 the only nuclease with a free commercial research license [82] and attractive to work
with. Thus, there is great interest in both the development and improvement of genome editing
tools utilizing MAD7 as a nuclease. An illustration comparing Cas9 and MAD7 is shown in
Figure 3.6. As mentioned in Section 3.4.3, the presence of a PAM sequence is a requirement
for a nuclease to cut, and it, therefore, limits the use of CRISPR systems if the PAM sequence
is absent. Nucleases di↵erentiate between targeting GC-rich or AT-rich DNA depending on
their PAM sequence. The widely used nuclease Cas9 recognizes the PAM sequence 5’-NGG-3’,
making it e↵ective for targeting GC-rich DNA sites but less e↵ective for AT-rich sites [82].
MAD7 recognizes the PAM sequence 5’-YTTN-3’, where Y indicates a C or T base and N
indicates either of the four (A, T, C, or G) [82], making it perfect for targeting AT-rich areas of
the genome. Unlike Cas9 which makes a blunt cut close to the PAM, MAD7 makes staggered
cuts at a distance to its PAM sequence leaving 5’-end overhangs [82]. Cas9 cleaves the DNA

For a gRNA to function and target the nuclease to a speciﬁc site, it must be e↵ectively released.
To free the gRNA, di↵erent approaches can be used. The gRNA sequence can be ﬂanked by
ribozymes on both sides which can self-cleave to free the gRNA located in between [89]. Another
way to free the gRNA is to use ﬂanking tRNA sequences which are processed by RNase P and Z
to release the gRNA [90]. Recently, researchers experimented with the use of ﬂanking ribozymes
and arrays of direct repeats (DR) of the crRNA to free several gRNAs from the same plasmid
to target the nuclease MAD7 to di↵erent sites simultaneously, i.e., exploring its multiplexing
capacities [82]. In this set-up, the DRs are cleaved using the ribonuclease activity of MAD7 itself
to free the gRNAs [82]. The ability to self-process by its ribonuclease activity is an advantage
of the class 2 type V-A CRISPR nucleases (e.g., MAD7) over class 2 type II nucleases (e.g.,
Cas9) [82].
3.5.1
The promise of CRI-SPA/MAD7 technology
The current CRI-SPA system has some limitations and can be optimized by exchanging the
nuclease from Cas9 to MAD7, removing any IP issues related to its use. It should also be
considered that there is a Material Transfer Agreement (MTA) related to the use of the current
CRI-SPA donor strain, as it was obtained and modiﬁed from the universal donor strain made
for SPA, constructed in [6]. In addition, the current CRI-SPA system utilizes a ura3 marker for
counter-selection against the CRI-SPA donor strain’s genome, requiring the recipient library
to be Ura-. Ura- is a metabolic defect [9] and changes the pathway ﬂux which may, in turn,
alter the ﬂux of other pathways. Therefore, some of the hits generated from CRI-SPA screens
may be false positives or false negatives that occur due to the altered metabolism from the ura3
defect. Hence, auxotrophies cause metabolic defects [9] and should be corrected to show the full
picture. This is possible by utilizing a dominant marker in the CRI-SPA system instead of an

5
Materials and Methods
In this study, a CDS was constructed with a cassette containing a dual galactose-inducible
promoter and a TK marker gene inserted in front of the centromere on every chromosome. This
section will describe the materials and methods used to construct, validate, and test this strain.
To show a complete story, data from a previous project [13] is also described in Appendix A.5.1.
5.1
Strains and plasmids
Linearized USER-compatible plasmids and PCR fragments were assembled using USER fusion
cloning and transformed into competent E. coli subsp. DH5a cells where they were ampliﬁed.
The plasmids are listed in Table 5.1. Assembled plasmids were puriﬁed from DH5a cells and
used to transform competent S. cerevisiae cells, together with repair fragments, to construct
the strains listed in Table 5.2. The S. cerevisiae strains BY4741 (MATa) and BY4742 (MATa)
are S288C-derivative laboratory strains, only di↵ering in their mating type and that BY4741 is
auxotrophic for methionine while BY4742 is auxotrophic for lysine [21]. BY4741 and BY4742
are the parental strains for the MAD7-expressing strains sDIV415 [12] and sDIV504 [13]
constructed in previous projects. sDIV504 was previously genetically engineered to contain the
pGAL1/GAL10-TK-tTEF1 cassette on Chr. XV-XVI (sDIV510) [13], and this strain was used
as the starting point for this study. sDIV510 was used as a parental strain to construct the CDS
by increasing the cassette number until it had been integrated on all 16 chromosomes. Both
sDIV415 and sDIV504 were used as parental strains to construct 16 single integration strains
used for quality control (QC). These strains all contain the pGAL1/GAL10-TK-tTEF1 cassette
as well as a mVenus-natMX cassette on a single of the 16 chromosomes.

5.2
Media and growth conditions
E. coli cells were grown in lysogeny broth (LB) (10 g/l tryptone, 5 g/l yeast extract, 10 g/l
NaCl, pH ⇡7-7.5) at 37°C, either in liquid media with shaking (150 rpm) or on agar plates
(20 g/l agar) O/N. Liquid media, as well as agar plates, were added ampicillin (amp) to a
concentration of 100 mg/l.
S. cerevisiae cells were grown in either yeast extract peptone (YP) (10 g/l yeast extract, 20 g/l
peptone) with glucose (YPD) (20g/l glucose), galactose (YP+gal) (20g/l galactose) or raﬃnose
(YP+raf) (23.57 g/l raﬃnose pentahydrate) as carbon source, or in synthetic complete (SC)
(7.25 g/l SC powder, pH ⇡5.8) using the appropriate amino acid drop-out with either glucose
(SC) (20 g/l glucose), galactose (SC+gal) (20 g/l galactose) or raﬃnose (SC+raf) (23.57 g/l
raﬃnose pentahydrate) as carbon source. To check for mitochondrial damage, strains were
grown on YP plates with glycerol as carbon source (YPG) (10 g/l yeast extract, 20 g/l peptone,
30 ml/l glycerol (100%). When appropriate, media was supplemented with antibiotics as follows:
NTC was added to a concentration of 100 mg/l, G418 was added to a concentration of 200
mg/l, and hygB was added to a concentration of 200 mg/l. For liquid media as well as double
selection on YPD+G418+NTC agar plates, the antibiotic concentrations were halved. For
counter-selection against the TK marker gene, FUdR was added to SC plates to a concentration
of 50 mg/l. Cells were grown at 30°C, either in liquid media with shaking (180 rpm) O/N or on
agar plates (20 g/l agar) for 2-4 days.
Distilled (DI) water was used for media preparation, and autoclaved Milli-Q (MQ) water was
used for all other purposes.
5.3
Generally applied methods
In this section, the most generally used methods will be described.
5.3.1
PCR ampliﬁcation
The primers used in this study are listed in Table A.1 in Appendix A.1. The fragments used
in this study were PCR ampliﬁed using either the Phusion U Hot Start DNA Polymerase
(Thermo Fisher Scientiﬁc) or Phusion Hot Start II High-Fidelity DNA Polymerase (Thermo
Fisher Scientiﬁc). For USER fusion cloning, the polymerase used was Phusion U, which is
compatible with uracil-containing templates. The PCR mixture and the PCR program are
listed in Tables A.2a and A.2b in Appendix A.2. PCR reactions were run on a Doppio Gradient
2x48 well thermal cycler. All PCR reactions, except for colony PCR, were added 1 µl DpnI
(NEB) and incubated at 37°C O/N to degrade the template DNA, followed by inactivation by
incubating the PCR tubes at 80°C for 20 min. before gel or column puriﬁcation.
5.3.2
Gel and column puriﬁcation
Gel and column puriﬁcations were performed using a NucleoSpin® Gel and PCR Clean-up
kit (Macherey-Nagel) as described by the NucleoSpin® manual with the adjustments that the
column drying step was adjusted to 2 min. For gel puriﬁcation, the ”DNA extraction from
agarose gels” protocol was used, and for column puriﬁcation, the ”PCR clean-up protocol” was
used.

5.3.3
Gel electrophoresis
Gel electrophoresis was used to conﬁrm the presence of DNA inserts and for gel puriﬁcation.
Gels containing 1% agarose added SYBR Safe DNA gel stain (Invitrogen) for DNA visualization
were used. Gels were run at 100 mV for 20 min in 1x TAE bu↵er. For PCR ampliﬁcation of
USER fragments, 5 µl PCR product added 1 µl of 6x DNA loading dye was used. For colony
PCR, 3-10 µl of PCR product was used. For gel puriﬁcation, between 25-100 µl of PCR product
or digested plasmid was added to the appropriate amount of 6x loading dye (made in-house)
and used. In general, 2-5 µl of DNA ladder mix was run to check that DNA bands showed the
expected size. The ladder mix used was either a 1 kb DNA ladder (NEB) or a 100 bp DNA
ladder (NEB), depending on the expected size of the DNA.

5.3.9
Transformation into S. cerevisiae using electroporation method
Frozen vials from Section 5.3.8 were thawed on ice for 20-30 min., while sterile electroporation
cuvettes and two 50 ml falcons tubes containing YPD+sorbitol (1.0 M) and sorbitol (1.0 M),

ice. The cells and DNA were transferred to an electroporation cuvette and kept on ice until
electroporation. The cells were electroporated using a Bio-Rad MicroPulser set to a predeﬁned
S. cerevisiae electroporation program (1.5 kV, 5.3 milliseconds). Cuvettes were then placed on
ice, added 1 ml of ice-cold YPD+sorbitol (antibiotic marker) and incubated for at least 5 min.

and incubated at 30°C for 3 hours to recover. The tubes were centrifuged at 8000 xg for 1 min,
the supernatant was removed, the pellet was re-suspended in 100 µl YPD+sorbitol, and 20 and
80 µl of each transformation were plated on selection plates.

5.3.10
Transformation into S. cerevisiae using LiAc/PEG/ssDNA method
The following method is modiﬁed and based on the protocol by R. Gietz and R. Schiestl [40].
One colony from an S. cerevisiae re-streak was inoculated in 5 ml YPD and incubated at 30°C
with shaking O/N. The next day, the 5 ml O/N culture was transferred to a 500 ml shake ﬂask
containing 50 ml YPD and incubated 30°C with shaking for 3-5 hours. Following incubation,
the contents of the shake ﬂask were transferred to a 50 ml Falcon tube and spun down at 3000
xg for 5 min. The supernatant was removed, and the pellet was re-suspended in 25 ml MQ,
then spun down at 3000 xg for 5 min. The supernatant was then removed, and the pellet was
re-suspended in MQ corresponding to a 100 µl added per transformation needed (maximum 1
ml) with a minimum of 500 µl MQ added. For each transformation, 100 µl were transferred to a
1.5 ml Eppendorf tube and spun down at 8000 xg for 30 sec. The supernatant was removed,
and DNA and MQ were added to each transformation tube. Plasmid DNA was added for a
concentration of 300-500 ng, and repair templates were added for a concentration of 400-1500
ng. Then 286 µl from a mastermix containing PEG, LiAc and ssDNA was added to each tube,
mixed by vortexing and/or pipetting and heat-shocked in a 42°C heating-block for 40 min.
The entire transformation mixture is listed in Table 5.7. Following heat-shocking, cells were
spun down at 8000 xg for 30 sec., and the supernatant was removed. For antibiotic markers,
a recovery step was included in which cells were re-suspended in 500 µl YPD and incubated
at 30°C for 2 h. When using auxotrophic markers, the 2 h recovery step is unnecessary. Cells
were spun down at 8000 xg for 30 sec. before the supernatant was removed. The pellet was
re-suspended in 100-200 µl of YPD (antibiotic marker) or MQ (auxotrophic marker) and plated
on selective plates using glass beads. A negative control containing only cells and no DNA and
a positive control containing the backbone plasmid were always included. TAPE experiments
were included when transforming with gRNA plasmids, i.e., where only gRNA and no repair
template were added.

Extraction of gDNA
When needed, genomic DNA (gDNA) was extracted from S. cerevisiae cells and used as template

were then transferred to a new Eppendorf tube and saved as gDNA.

5.6
Spot assay
Conﬁrmed diploid strains constructed in Section 5.5.5 were used in a QC spot assay to asses
chromosome loss. Strains were grown O/N in 3 ml SC+raf liq. and the optical density (OD) at
600 nanometers (nm) of the cultures was measured. The OD600 was normalized to ⇡1.0 by
diluting in MQ, a tenfold dilution row of ﬁve dilutions was made, and 3 µl of each dilution was
plated on YPD, SC+FUdR, SC+gal, and SC+gal+FUdR plates and incubated at 30°C. From
the SC+gal QC plate, cell mass was taken with a with 10 µl inoculation loop and streaked on
an SC+gal+FUdR plate and incubated at 30°C.
5.7
CRI-SPA protocol
A ROTOR HDA (Singer Instruments) pinning robot was used for pin replication with replica
pinning pads and rectangular petri dishes. For mating, a single CDS colony was inoculated
in 50 ml liquid SC-ura to maintain the gRNA plasmid, pDIV938, and grown O/N. The next
day, the cells were spun down at 3000 xg for 5 min. and the supernatant was removed. The
cells were re-suspended in 15 ml SC-ura and 120 µl were transferred to each well in a 96-well
plate that was pinned on top of a YPD plate before the recipient library was pinned on top and
incubated at 30°C O/N before being replica pinned onto selection plates. Cell mass was taken
from two colonies in the ﬁnal plate and used for colony PCR to check if donor Chr. II was still
present using primers PR DIV3767 and PR DIV3768.

6.2
Construction of a new CDS
The starting point for this project was a yeast strain, sDIV510 (CEN15-16), containing the
pGAL1/GAL10-TK-tTEF1 cassette integrated on Chr. XV-XVI constructed in a previous
project [13]. Two strategies were used to integrate the pGAL1/GAL10-TK-tTEF1 cassette
on the remaining chromosomes. For Chr. XIII-XIV, a strategy using transformations with
simultaneous expression of two gRNA plasmids with di↵erent antibiotic selection markers was
used to create strain sDIV511 (CEN13-16). A new strategy exploring multiplexing capacities
with MAD7 was used for Chr. I-XII. This strategy relies on a single plasmid encoding three
di↵erent gRNAs that each require liberation to be functional. Using this strategy, strains
sDIV512 (CEN10-16), sDIV513 (CEN7-16), sDIV514 (CEN4-16), and sDIV519 (CEN1-16)
were constructed. The CDS strain (sDIV519) was constructed by transforming GTS fragments
containing the pGAL1/GAL10-TK-tTEF1 cassette and gRNAs targeting speciﬁc sites in the
chromosomes near the centromeric DNA.
The GTS fragments containing the cassette were liberated by enzymatic digestion and used as
RTs for the breaks. An example of how GTS fragments were liberated showing gel electrophoresis
results compared with a virtual digestion with NotI/BspEI can be found in Figure A.20 in
Appendix A.7. The cassette was integrated so the distance between the pGAL1 and CEN was
68 bp +/- 17 bp. The exact distances can be found in Table A.8 in Appendix A.12.
Transformants were validated by re-streaking and colony PCR. If the integration resulted in
a color change, e.g., for insertion of bikaverin or ade2 deletion, visual inspection was used
initially to screen colonies.
To ensure that strains were healthy, a test for mitochondrial
damage was introduced by plating on YPG plates. Growth on YPG indicates that the cell’s

7
Discussion
In this section, the study’s results will be discussed in relation to what was achieved and
what could be improved. Advantages, limitations, and future perspectives regarding the new
CRI-SPA/MAD7 system will be explored.
7.1
The ﬁrst step: Construction of a new CDS
To construct a new CDS for CRI-SPA, a pGAL1/GAL10-TK-tTEF1 cassette was integrated on
every chromosome, as was done previously with the ura3-pGAL1 cassette to construct the UDS
for SPA [6, 78], that was later modiﬁed to become a CDS for CRI-SPA [5, 92]. As described in
Section 6.2, the cassette was integrated into a MATa background strain, and the resulting strain
was named sDIV519. It is important that the constructed CDS is healthy. Cells with a complete
loss of mitochondria can form very small colonies, known as petite cells, on plates containing a
fermentable carbon source [100]. The strain was therefore re-streaked on YPG (a picture is shown
in Figure A.36 in Appendix A.10.1). Glycerol is a non-fermentable carbon source that requires
oxidative phosphorylation to be used by S. cerevisiae as energy source [100]. As oxidative
phosphorylation is carried out in the mitochondria [100], growth on YPG can be used to assess
mitochondrial damage. The results on YPG showed that sDIV519 was able to grow and form
single colonies. Thus, it can perform oxidative phosphorylation and does not have mitochondrial
damage. For each transformation, the integration of the cassettes was ﬁrst validated using a
paring of primers outside the cassette with primers inside pGAL1 and tTEF1. The integration
was also checked using the two primers outside the cassette as an extra precaution. The results
in Section 6.2 show that sDIV519 contains the pGAL1/GAL10-TK-tTEF1 cassette on every
chromosome as the band has increased from ⇡1.5 kb (WT) to 3.5 kb (integration). Thus, a
new CDS for CRI-SPA was constructed, fulﬁlling the ﬁrst sub-objective of this study.
7.2
TAPE experiments for multiplexing constructs
To integrate the cassette on Chr. I-XII, a new approach was used where a single plasmid
expressing an auxotrophic marker was responsible for the expression and release of three distinct
gRNAs. This multiplexing strategy proved very eﬃcient as cells from the transformation plate
usually either contained all three integrations or zero. Thus, it was shown that a strategy
utilizing both ﬂanking tRNAs and small DR of the crRNA could e↵ectively be used to free
three gRNAs. It was also found that TAPE experiments may not be suitable for this strategy,
as both the transformation and TAPE plates would have the same amount of cells. This may
be due to the backbone construction that is based on the gRNAs looping out and could make
it easy for the remaining plasmid to function without the cassette. As there are DRs with
identical sequences, in addition to the two tRNAs, there is a potential for the sequence to
recombine and ”loop out” everything in between [24]. This could mean that the triple cassette
is looped out of the plasmid may cause a lot of cells to survive the selection without receiving
the pGAL1/GAL10-TK-tTEF1 cassettes, but only the plasmid. The colonies on the TAPE
plates do not show any of the expected bands, so they did not receive the GTS fragments by
mistake. Instead, it appears that they have received the plasmid with the selection marker but
are not targeted by the gRNAs. This can be explained if the triple gRNA cassette has, in fact,
been looped out because the gRNAs cannot guide MAD7 to the target site.
Another loop-put issue could be with the individual release of the gRNAs. Zhang et al. reported
that for multiplexing with Cas9 using an array of gRNA-tRNAs for targeting eight di↵erent
59
==PAGE:69==
genes, they obtained few or no colonies and concluded that this may have been due to a looping-
out issue [101]. Although not explicitly stated, this may be due to the repetitive sequences of
tRNAs ﬂanking the gRNAs, as described previously, or with the processing of the transcript
hindering the individual gRNAs from being looped out of the mRNA. If there is an issue with
one gRNA looping out of the transcript in this study, it might be that only two of the three
sites are targeted. If the cell then grows and loses the RT before the last gRNA is freed, then
the cell will die due to the DSB caused by the last gRNA. However, the eﬃciency of TAPE also
seems to be somewhat dependent on the multiplexing plasmid in question. As the only one,
plasmid pDIV929 targeting Chr. IV-VI (plates can be found in Figure A.23) showed a notable
di↵erence in survival between the transformation plate and the TAPE plate. Therefore, it may
also just be that the design needs to be optimized for TAPE to be meaningful in this strategy.
To summarize the most important ﬁnding, the new gRNA multiplexing strategy comprising
tRNAs and DRs proved to work very well.
7.3
Transformations targeting ade2 and XII-5
To further validate the new CDS, it was modiﬁed genetically to contain the hygB resistance gene
at the ade2 locus in sDIV519. In another transformation, the biosynthetic pathway for bikaverin
was integrated at the XII-5 locus in sDIV519. Common for both of these transformations was
that almost no cells survived.
For bikaverin (seen in Section 6.6), only two colonies were present on the two transformation
plates even though at least in the tens were expected. One of the colonies proved to have the
bikaverin insert and the other surprisingly had both WT and the bikaverin insert, indicating
that chromosome duplication had happened. A color di↵erence was also observed between the
two colonies. The low number of surviving cells could be explained by the di↵erences in cutting
by Cas9 and MAD7. Cas9 cuts close to the PAM (3 bp UP) whereas MAD7 cuts distal to PAM
(19 bp DW) resulting in the repair fragment also being cleaved after integration. Thus, a second
DSB event killed cells after integrating the fragment. This means that colonies containing the
band for bikaverin integration must repair the break using ﬂawed NHEJ, resulting in a mutation
so that MAD7 can no longer cleave the site. Furthermore, both the gRNA targeting XII-5 and
the bikaverin fragment contains the natMX gene encoding NTC resistance. Thus, when cells
integrated the fragment, there would no longer be a selection pressure to keep the plasmid. This
would lead to cells that lost the plasmid gaining an advantage, as it is a metabolic burden to
maintain a plasmid [17]. To overcome these challenges, the DW region targeted by MAD7 was
deleted simultaneously with the insertion of bikaverin using a gRNA with the auxotrophic ura3
marker gene. This strategy worked, and a strain containing the gRNA, the bikaverin pathway,
and the correct deletion of the DW region was used in CRI-SPA experiments.
The hygB integration at ade2 transformation (seen in Section 6.5) was successful in that it was
possible to ﬁnd cells that had gained the hygB resistance and were red due to ade2 disruption.
However, there were very few cells on the transformation plates. Previously, the transfer of
hygB into the ade2 locus has been very successful in getting a lot of transformants [12, 92]. This
indicated that something was wrong. The current theory is that the YPD+NTC plates used
by mistake may have added more NTC antibiotics than 100 mg/l. Therefore, cells die before
they have a chance to incorporate the fragment as they do not produce enough of the enzyme
nourseothricin N-acetyltransferase (encoded by the natMX gene), which inactivates NTC. This
theory is strengthened by the fact that the two positive controls from the transformation
integrating the pGAL1/GAL10-TK-tTEF1 cassette at Chr. XIII-XIV and hygB into ade2,
respectively, look very di↵erent. Pictures of the plates can be seen in Figure A.25 in Appendix
A.8.2, but are only described here. There are di↵erences in the cell edges, which can indicate
60
==PAGE:70==
if cells are healthy or if there is a large amount of cell death. There also appear to be the
same number of cells on both plates, even though one is the plating of a 10% transformation
mixture and the other 20%. Both support the theory that the NTC concentration is higher than
it should be. As the second bikaverin transformation was very successful, there is no reason
to believe that something should be wrong with the CDS strain, sDIV519. To sum up, two
separate transformations were accomplished by integrating a hygB resistance gene at the ade2
locus and the biosynthetic pathway for bikaverin at the XII-5 locus in sDIV519.
7.4
Functionality of the new CRI-SPA system
The new CDS was tested in a single strain CRISPR transfer of the hygB resistance gene. In this
experiment, sDIV519 cells containing the hygB integration at ade2 with and without the gRNA
plasmid were mated to BY4741. Diploids were isolated on SC-lys+NTC to select cells carrying
the gRNA plasmid or YPD+hygB to select cells without the gRNA plasmid. When the plasmid
is present, the recipient allele of ade2 should be targeted by MAD7, and the diploid cells should
become red and only show a band for hygB. When the plasmid is absent, the diploid cells should
show a white color as they contain one WT allele of ade2. This was exactly what was observed,
indicating that the CRISPR transfer was successful.
Knowing if all chromosomes could be lost from the new CDS was important. Therefore, 16
strains containing both the pGAL1/GAL10-TK-tTEF1 cassette and a mVenus-natMX cassette
on a single chromosome were constructed. The mVenus-natMX cassette was used to tag the
chromosomes and follow the phenotype of YFP expression following centromere destabilization.
As illustrated in Figure 7.1, the tagging cassette was placed on the longest chromosomal arm.
An area of 20 kb on the ends of the chromosomes was avoided due to the presence of telomeres
and the associated transcriptional silencing [102]. Distal to the telomere region, an area of
30 kb was used for the placement of the cassette. An area of 30 kb was necessary to be able
to ﬁnd a PAM sequence for MAD7 targeting to insert the cassette. When integrating the
mVenus-natMX cassette, it was again observed that only a few cells survived on YPD+NTC
plates, which could further indicate that the concentration of NTC in the media is higher than
expected. For CEN15-mVenus, only a single colony was present and appeared ﬂuorescent (shown
in Figure A.28 in Appendix A.8.4). Colony PCR validated 15 single integration strains for
integration of the mVenus-natMX cassette. Unfortunately, validating the CEN15-mVenus strain
by colony PCR was impossible, although the colony appears to be ﬂuorescent and has NTC
resistance. This could indicate that the fragment has integrated at another site or that the
appearance of ﬂuorescence is a visual artifact.
Depending on the mating type, the 16 single integration strains were mated to the parental
strains, BY4741 or BY4742. The resulting diploid strains were tested in a spot assay on SC
media containing either FUdR, galactose, or both galactose and FUdR. It became clear that the
diploid CEN15-mVenus cells were not actually ﬂuorescent. The cassette’s expression appeared
unstable as few cells seemed ﬂuorescent, but most cells did not. The integration sites used were
not previously tested, so this might be an unstable integration site. It could be that it is too
close to the telomeres and experiences transcription silencing. Another possibility is that the
cassette has not actually been integrated, but the appearance of ﬂuorescence is a visual artifact.
A previous project [12], constructed a CEN15 single integration strain using the same GTS
plasmid as in this study. Cubbin and Liu integrated the pGAL1/GAL10-TK-tTEF1 at Chr.
XV in sDIV415 and mated this single integration strain to a strain carrying a deletion of ade2
[12]. They observed that the diploid strains were white but after centromere destabilization and
counter-selection using FUdR, the diploids turned red due to loss of the healthy allele of ADE2
found on the Chr. XV containing the cassette [12]. It is therefore assumed that Chr. XV can

utilizing galactose need to be turned on and expressed before normal growth can be resumed.

SC+FUdR, as glucose is the preferred sugar, so growth is quickly resumed.
When assessing the functionality of the new CDS, it is important to keep the aim of CRI-SPA
as a screening method in mind. Thus, it is important to ask, ”What is the system’s function?”.
The strains generated in CRI-SPA are not used in further experiments but merely to generate
hits that can give an idea of what strains appear superior. For example, if a deletion strain
seems to increase the expression of a desired product, then it would be beneﬁcial to try and
delete this gene in the actual production strain and observe if a higher yield is obtained. This
means that even if the CDS donor chromosomes are not completely removed (ablated), it may
not be a problem in the CRI-SPA system. The system just needs to generate reliable data that
can be reconstructed.
Together, the results of this study indicate that all chromosomes can be lost. Thus, sub-objective
II of this study is fulﬁlled. The placement of the cassette in the new system is very di↵erent
from the current one, as illustrated in Figure 7.2. All cassettes are placed on the left of the
centromere, except for Chr. XIV, in the current system [78]. In this study, the cassette is placed
⇡half on the left side and half on the right side. The reason for this is that it was hard to ﬁnd
a PAM site for MAD7 that would result in a cut at a desired location close to the centromere.
In this study, single integration strains were each tagged on the chromosome containing the
pGAL1/GAL10-TK-tTEF1 cassette. To be truly representative, tagging each chromosome in
the new CDS instead may be beneﬁcial. It might also be valuable to the same in the current
CDS strain and compare the results of the two.

7.5
Perspectives on CRI-SPA screen of bikaverin production
The bikaverin-expressing sDIV519 strain containing an auxotrophic gRNA plasmid was used
in a CRI-SPA screen for ﬁnal validation of the CDS (Figure 6.19). Unfortunately, the plate
containing the recipient library had been in the fridge for over a year and was not pinned onto
a new plate before testing. All CRI-SPA plates for the process can be seen in Figure A.41 in
64
==PAGE:74==
Appendix A.11.2 but are only brieﬂy described here. On the mating plate, there is growth at
every position. However, from the ﬁrst pinning onto a diploid selection plate, several spots
appear empty. This indicates that the growth observed on the mating plate was from the
growth of the CDS strain. Likely because the recipient library strains have died from the long
incubation time in the fridge. Some heterogeneity is seen in the quadruplicates, which may be a
result of the di↵erent mutations in the cells. As the recipient library has been in the fridge for a
year, the cells may have accumulated di↵erent mutations, leading to the observed heterogeneity.
Some quadruplicates on the SC plate also appear to show heterogeneity on the edge of the plates.
However, this is due to edging e↵ects that cause the cells near the edge to have more nutrients
available. Thus, they can grow faster and have time to accumulate more of the pigment, giving
the impression that the cells are heterogeneous when, in fact, they are not. A picture of the
original CRI-SPA plate [5, 92] from ade2 disruption in the same part of the deletion library can
be seen in Figure A.42 in Appendix A.11.3 but are only described here. The original plate also
appears to show heterogeneity among the quadruplicates, and it should be noted that there are
also several spots that appear to be empty as if the cells had died. This indicates that there
may be some artifacts associated with the high-throughput of the CRI-SPA process. It may
also be that some of the deletions accelerate the accumulation of new mutations, leading to
heterogeneity or cell death.
It seems there are di↵erences in the expression of bikaverin that depend on the type of media.
This may be due to di↵erences in metabolic ﬂux between the two media types or growth-related
strains, as strains appear to grow faster on YPD than SC. For example, ROX1 looks redder on
SC, whereas on YPD, it does not look like a hit. On the contrary, it is harder to see deletion
strains YOL159C and NAB6 as hits on the SC plate, but it is evident on the YPD plate. It may
be a good idea to always include SC and YPD as media for screening colonies from CRI-SPA to
ensure that as many as possible hits are discovered. Alternatively, by using a media as closely
as possible resembling the industrial production of a given product would likely give the most
accurate hits. Overall, the hits generated from the CRI-SPA screen using bikaverin with the
new donor strain and a plasmid containing an auxotrophic marker agree with previous results.
The white-appearing deletion strains (SNT309, YOL162W, and STO1) have previously also
resulted in white colonies in a CRI-SPA screen - likewise, the deletion strains (YOL159C, NAB6,
ROX1) with increased redness have previously been in the top 50 hits for increased bikaverin
production (A.M. Vestergaard, personal communication, June 19, 2024). Together, these results
indicate that the new CDS performs comparably to the current CDS in CRI-SPA, thus fulﬁlling
sub-objective III.
Despite the use of an old recipient plate, the results that came out of the CRI-SPA screen
appear to mimic what has previously been seen using the current CDS. In addition, this study
was the ﬁrst test using gRNA plasmids with auxotrophic markers rather than dominant markers
in the CRI-SPA system. Thus, the CRI-SPA transfer functioned as a ”proof-of-principle” and
showed that auxotrophic markers can be successfully used for CRI-SPA experiments.
7.6
Advantages and limitations
The question of what new problems may be associated with this technology is unknown. It was
possible that the use of a dual galactose-inducible promoter would result in leaky expression,
which could induce centromere destabilization and lead to aneuploidy due to loss of chromosomes.
However, the results show that there is no leaky expression as the cells grew normally in the
presence of FUdR when galactose was absent.
An advantage of using the MAD7 nuclease is that it can target AT-rich DNA because of its PAM
65
==PAGE:75==
sequence. This means that it is possible to target new areas that cannot be targeted using Cas9.
The new system also makes use of the TK marker for counter-selection that is dominant in yeast
and fungi [10, 11, 94]. This means that more libraries are available for use in CRI-SPA as there
is no longer a requirement for the libraries to be Ura-. It can also result in healthier libraries if
they do not have the Ura- metabolic defect. The hits generated from CRI-SPA screens become
more reliable if the libraries have no metabolic defects and, thus, resemble WT and industrial
strains more. The dominant TK marker also has the advantage as it can be used in both yeast
and fungi, so the system can be expanded to other species for cell factory engineering. In that
way, the new CRI-SPA system functions as a ”proof-of-principle” for expanding to other cell
factories. If the system could be expanded, it would have an enormous impact on the tools
available for gene editing in non-conventional yeast and fungi.
A limitation of systems utilizing CRISPR technology is that the eﬃciency depends on the gRNA
protospacer, as illustrated by the TAPE experiments. On TAPE plates, there are often big
white colonies, which are cells that have received the gRNA plasmid but may have a mutation
in the protospacer sequence. They may also have repaired the DSB using NHEJ and have
managed to repair the break with each cell division so far. These colonies have a rough edge,
indicating that there is massive cell death when it grows from the portion of cells that have not
been able to repair the DSB. As mentioned, one of the multiplexing gRNA plasmids showed a
clear di↵erence between the transformation plate and the TAPE plate, so it could be that this
limitation can be overcome by optimizing the gRNA design to limit the number of colonies on
TAPE plates.
7.7
Future perspectives
The available USER backbones were plasmids pDIV025, pDIV026, pRS423, and pRS426.
pDIV025 and pDIV026 are used to provide antibiotic resistance against NTC and G418,
respectively. Backbones pRS423 and pRS426 were used for auxotrophic selection restoring the
histidine and uracil biosynthetic pathways, respectfully. For CRI-SPA, it has been customary
to use a dominant marker, e.g., an antibiotic, as cells must have the plasmid or die. With an
auxotrophic marker, it is possible that cells nearby can feed o↵the cells that have the plasmids
and survive even without the gRNA plasmid. Early on, it was decided to use the deletion library
for the CRI-SPA screen, as this was used in the original paper. Each strain contains a deletion
linked to the kanMX resistance gene. Therefore, the gRNA targeting XII-5 was constructed
with NTC selection. As a backup, it was also constructed with ura3 selection. For the ﬁrst
transformation of bikaverin, both the bikaverin fragment and the gRNA targeting XII-5 had
NTC selection. This means that cells lose the plasmid and that any cells still containing the
plasmid will have a disadvantage compared to cells that have lost the plasmid due to the extra
metabolic burden [17]. It was decided to test the CDS in CRI-SPA using the gRNA with ura3
selection instead of constructing a new gRNA with another antibiotic marker. It was found
that auxotrophic selection worked perfectly ﬁne in a CRI-SPA set-up, meaning that the toolbox
for CRI-SPA has been expanded. Therefore, it would be interesting to test other auxotrophic
markers in CRI-SPA.
Due to the dominant TK marker, it will no longer be a requirement for recipient libraries to be
Ura-. Thus, this auxotrophy can be ﬁxed. Likewise, it is possible to ﬁx the auxotrophies in the
CDS strain. Fixing an auxotrophy can be achieved by transforming the cells with a fragment
that restores the original WT gene, e.g., the ura3 gene. As mentioned, this may result in more
accurate CRI-SPA hits resembling industrial or WT strains, as the metabolic defect may result
in false positives or negatives. However, it should be noted that true hits should still be seen,
but it may not give the complete picture.
66
==PAGE:76==
To validate if the hits generated from a CRI-SPA screen are real hits, the deletions causing
interesting phenotypes should be reconstructed in the CDS (called reverse engineering). To
compare with the original CDS, the disruption of ade2 and transfer of hygromycin resistance
could be tested in a CRI-SPA set-up. This was done in [5, 92] using the deletion library plate
YKO 9 in a 384 set-up. As such a strain has been constructed in this study, it should be ready
for CRI-SPA testing. The comparison can tell if the two strains behave identically in CRI-SPA.
Another test would be the transfer of the biosynthetic pathway for betaxanthin, which was
also done in [5]. Betaxanthin produces yellow colonies, so the cells can be visually screened for
production.
It would be very beneﬁcial to have the CDS in both mating types. This would remove the
limitation of mating type on the recipient library, as some libraries may only have been
constructed in one mating type. It should be possible to construct a plasmid that can be
used to switch the mating type of the donor strain. Expressing the HO endonuclease from
a galactose-inducible promoter on a plasmid has previously been shown to induce mating
type switching in S. cerevisiae [103, 104]. It should be possible to construct such a plasmid
that can be used to switch the mating type of the new CDS. However, a galactose-inducible
promoter cannot be used in this set-up as the presence of galactose would also lead to centromere
destabilization as the pGAL1/GAL10-TK-tTEF1 cassettes would become activated. Therefore,
another inducible promoter should be used, e.g., the pMET25 or pCUP1 promoters that respond
to the absence of methionine (MET25) and the presence of copper (CUP1), respectively [105,
106]. pMET25 could be activated by growing the cells in liquid SC-met for di↵erent time points,
then plating an amount on SC or YPD plates and screening cells by colony PCR to ﬁnd cells
that have switched mating type. For use of the pCUP1, copper would have to be added to
the medium, making it more diﬃcult than simply using the already available SC-met drop-out
powder. Using a constitutive promoter could also be tested, although it seems that the papers
that have tested mating type switching using expression of the HO-endonuclease from a plasmid
have always done so using an inducible promoter [23, 26]. Building a gRNA plasmid targeting
the MAT locus could also be tried. However, it is possible that the gRNA will cut in both
the MAT gene and the silent locus (HMRa/HMLa). Xie et al. switched the mating type in
S. cerevisiae using CRISPR-Cas9 [26]. However, the strains used by Xie et al. lack the silent
regions HMRa and HMLa. Thus, Cas9 only cleaves once, and the desired MAT locus (a/a)
could instead be provided as an RT in these transformations [26]. For mating type switching,
the HO-endonuclease only cleaves in the MAT locus and not the silent regions. Therefore, it is
unknown if such a strategy would work in our strain that contains the silent regions, thus MAD7
would cleave both the MAT locus and the silent regions. Alternatively, if these approaches do
not work, it is possible to build on top of the sDIV415 strain [12] in the same way as sDIV519
was constructed in this study. Although more time-consuming, it would work, as demonstrated
in this study. If it were managed to switch the mating type of sDIV519 from MATa to MATa or
build-on-top in sDIV415, then a new CDS in both mating types would be available for CRI-SPA
experiments. This would ensure no limitations on the recipient library with regard to mating
type.
In the original CRI-SPA paper, they integrated the GOI to be transferred in front of the cassette
and integrated an additional ura3 counter-selectable marker ﬂanking the GOI [5]. This was
done to counter-select against cells that had repaired the DSB by BIR or crossing-over events
instead of SDSA, as they would have received the counter-selection marker also [92]. To be
comparable, the bikaverin transfer would have to be accompanied by an additional ﬂanking TK
marker. This might also explain some of the heterogeneity seen in the CRI-SPA screen if some
cells repaired the break using BIR or crossing-over events, which causes LOH.
It would also be interesting to study how well each chromosome is lost, e.g., using the CEN
67
==PAGE:77==
single integrations strains tagged with the mVenus-natMX cassette constructed in this study.
The diploid cells could be grown on an SC+gal plate to induce chromosome loss and then
plated on SC+gal+FUdR plates to see how many cells survive. When this is done in a spot
assay, the OD600 is measured and adjusted to ⇡1.0. Therefore, it is known how many cells are
plated, and should be possible to calculate the number of surviving cells from the dilutions.
This could show which chromosomes to optimize. If there are problems with the chromosomes,
e.g., if there is a problem with Chr. II, then the CDS can be rebuild from an intermediate
strain, e.g., strain sDIV514, which contains the pGAL1/GAL10-TK-tTEF1 cassette at Chr.
IV-XVI. As mentioned in Section 3.1.2, an alternative could be to fuse the chromosomes [38].
The chromosomes are fused by targeting the telomeres on the two chromosomes to be fused
using CRISPR technology and providing an RT to fuse the telomeres. At the same time, the
centromere is targeted by another gRNA-nuclease complex and deleted using another RT [38].
Thus, should any problems occur in the future, then there are strategies available to solve these
problems.
To sum up, the CRI-SPA screen was successful and produced results similar to what has
previously been observed when using the current CDS strain. The main objective of this study
was to construct a new CDS that could be used in CRI-SPA and overcome the challenges of
the current system. To this end, three sub-objectives were set: I) developing the new CDS, II)
quality control to ensure loss of all chromosomes, and III) testing the developed donor strain
using CRI-SPA on a yeast deletion library. All three objectives were met, and together, they
fulﬁlled the main objective. The newly constructed CDS strain opens the doors to targeting
AT-rich sites in the genome. Furthermore, the use of a dominant TK marker opens the doors to
restoring auxotrophies in the libraries to resemble WT and industrial strains, thus providing
more reliable hits. It might also be possible to expand the CRI-SPA system to non-conventional
yeast strains and fungi. Overall, the development of the CRI-SPA system provides a solution to
high-throughput transfer and screening of thousands of strains simultaneously. The proposed
new version of CRI-SPA promises to overcome the challenges of the current CRI-SPA system
and help expand the gene editing toolbox of S. cerevisiae. Systems like CRI-SPA can help
accelerate cell factory engineering and be a valuable tool for generating genetic hits for cell
factory engineering and simultaneously huge amounts of data that can help progress systems
biology approaches.

8
Conclusion
The main objective of this study was to construct a new donor strain for CRISPR-Selective-
Ploidy-Ablation (CRI-SPA). The CRI-SPA donor strain (CDS) utilizes the IP-friendly MAD7
nuclease and contains a pGAL1/GAL10-TK-tTEF1 cassette in front of the centromere on
every chromosome (= 16). This was achieved by a multiplex gRNA strategy guiding MAD7
to target the integration sites. A strategy comprising both ﬂanking tRNAs and direct repeats
to free the gRNAs was used to integrate the cassette. When active, the cassette leads to
centromere destabilization that induces chromosome loss. Furthermore, the cassette allows for
counter-selection in the presence of the compound FUdR.
16 strains were constructed for validation, each containing the pGAL1/GAL10-TK-tTEF1
cassette on a single chromosome. These strains were genetically tagged with a mVenus-natMX
cassette on the chromosome containing the pGAL1/GAL10-TK-tTEF1 cassette. The strains
were mated to form diploids that were used to assess single chromosome stability in a spot assay
on SC+FUdR, SC+gal, and SC+gal+FUdR plates. It was shown that a single copy of the TK
marker being actively expressed at any of the 16 chromosomes is enough to inhibit cell growth,
even in a diploid cell, when FUdR is present. When the TK marker was not actively expressed
(i.e., in the absence of galactose), all strains grew normally, indicating that the dual promoter
was not leaky. Thus, it was demonstrated to be an e↵ective counter-selectable marker to be
used in a CRI-SPA set-up. The results of this study indicate that all chromosomes can be lost
during the CRI-SPA process.
The CDS strain was tested by CRISPR/MAD7-mediated transfer of a hygB resistance gene
simultaneously with the deletion of the ade2 gene in a single strain experiment. This resulted
in a red phenotype due to the accumulation of an intermediate in the adenine biosynthesis
pathway when the MAD7 gRNA was present and a white phenotype in the absence of a gRNA
to target MAD7. This functioned as a proof-of-principle and showed that the constructed CDS
was able to correctly target the recipient allele of ade2 and transfer hygB resistance.
The biosynthetic pathway for expression of the polyketide bikaverin was transferred to the XII-5
locus on the CDS chromosome using a gRNA with the auxotrophic marker gene ura3. The
pathway was then transferred to a part of the deletion library in a CRI-SPA screen for validation
of the CDS. It was established that gRNAs with auxotrophic markers are usable in a CRI-SPA
set-up.
To conclude, the new version of the CRI-SPA system removes the limitations of the current
system and can be used to generate CRI-SPA hits and accelerate cell factory engineering.
Furthermore, this study report the ﬁrst use of a gRNA plasmid with an auxotrophic marker for
CRI-SPA. Overall, the results of this study are promising regarding the new CDS strain and its
use in CRI-SPA in the future.

Construction of GTS plasmids
To construct gene targeting sequences (GTS), sequences with homology upstream and down-
stream of the centromeric DNA were PCR ampliﬁed to contain USER-compatible ends. The
PCR reactions run to construct the GTS fragments with USER-compatible ends are listed in
Table A.3. Plasmid pDIV820 (See Figure A.17b) contains the CRI-SPA cassette containing
a dual galactose-inducible promoter transcribing the TK marker. pDIV820 was linearized by
digestion with restriction enzymes PacI and Nt.BbvCI. The PCR fragments containing homology
upstream (UP) and downstream (DW) were cloned into the linearized backbone accordingly, e.g.,
GTS plasmid 1 (pDIV868) was constructed by USER-cloning of PCR fragments CEN 01 UP
and CEN 01 DW into linearized pDIV820. Transformed cells were validated by colony PCR
using primers PR DIV0103, PR DIV0104, and PR DIV3379 and the plasmids were puriﬁed and
sent for sequencing. Validated GTS plasmids were saved for later use.
Construction of single gRNA plasmids
To construct single gRNA-expressing plasmids, plasmid pDIV822 (See Figure A.2) encoding a
single gRNA targeting the centromeric DNA at Chr. XV was used as template for PCR reactions
to replace the gRNA sequence. This was done in two PCR reactions; one amplifying a universal
promoter part and the second amplifying the gRNA sequence to replace the gRNA sequence
already present on the template DNA (as described in Table A.4). The PCR fragments were
USER-cloned into USER-ready pDIV025 and pDIV026, e.g., gRNA UP and gRNA CEN 01 A
were cloned into pDIV025 to construct a gRNA targeting CEN1 at Chr. I (pDIV883). The
gRNAs were constructed in two versions (A/B) with di↵erent protospacer sequences expressing
di↵erent antibiotic markers, i.e, version A plasmids confer resistance towards NTC (pDIV025
backbone) and version B plasmids towards G418 (pDIV026 backbone). Transformed cells were
veriﬁed by colony PCR using primers PR DIV1254 and PR DIV1255. Plasmids were puriﬁed
and sent for sequencing and validated gRNA plasmids were saved for later use. The PCR
reactions run to construct the gRNA plasmids are listed in Table A.4.
TAPE experiments
To test the protospacer sequences, the plasmids expressing single gRNAs targeting the centromere
on all chromosomes were transformed into the yeast strain sDIV504 expressing MAD7 without
any repair template (i.e., TAPE). Note that all gRNAs have two versions (A and B) with
di↵erent antibiotic markers, except for the gRNA targeting Chr. XV [12].

pDIV821 and pDIV882, respectively) targeting Chr. XV and XVI. Transformed cells were

XV-XVI by
