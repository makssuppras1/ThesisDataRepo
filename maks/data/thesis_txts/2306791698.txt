Abstract 
 
The Aviation is one of the fastest growing industries in the world. This development is raising the concern 
about environmental issues such as waste management, energy consumption and air pollution. The aim of this 
study is to understand and describe the waste management system applied in Thomas Cook Airlines, which is 
one of the leading companies in the air transportation sector. Ergo, samples from the combustibles fraction of 
a flight have been collected; a waste characterization and a moisture content analysis have been performed. 
Moreover, a Life Cycle Assessment was completed to describe the current situation and propose adjustments 
to the system. 10 Scenarios have been modeled in EASETECH, simulating the baseline scenario and other 
possible solutions. Waste characterization results’ have shown that food waste and plastic are the main 
contributors to the total waste composition, covering together 75% of the wet weight analyzed. The former 
was composed mainly of bread leftovers, while the latter acknowledged a high quantity of plastic bottles made 
from PET. Aluminum cans accounted 3% of the combustibles and 6% in the total waste. The moisture content 
analysis revealed significant discrepancies between default and measured values, with an average difference 
of 15% from the moisture content found in EASETECH database and those calculated from the samples. Food 
waste reported the highest difference, decreasing by 36% the water content from default to measured values. 
The outcomes of the Life Cycle Assessment highlighted satisfactory results for the current scenario but 
disclosed potential for improvements by introducing further sorting and recycling of Plastic bottles and food 
waste or by increasing the sorting efficiencies of the system. Those scenarios where only landfill or incineration 
was applied, revealed lower performances than the others. The contribution analysis, communicate that 
aluminum recycling and the waste-to-energy process are the two main contributors to the total impact of the 
system. Among the parameters tested in the sensitivity analysis, the model have shown highest reactions when 
incrementing aluminum sorting efficiency and electricity recovery from incineration, confirming those as key 
parameters of the system.

1 INTRODUCTION 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
During the last decades, issues as the resources depletion and the climate change have raised the 
attention to new greener solutions in order to avoid the massive use of virgin materials and to 
decrease air emissions. A sector which can strongly contribute to this improvement is the Solid 
Waste Management (Laurent et al. 2014). "Waste is a left-over, a redundant product or material 
of no or marginal value for the owner and which the owner wants to discard" (Christensen 2011). 
In the past waste has always been seen as something to get rid of with no potential value. For that 
reason, almost all the waste was sent to landfill. Nowadays, a big change in the approach is taking 
place and the waste is becoming a new resource (Fudala-Ksiazek et al. 2016). Exploit its potential 
is one of the main challenges of our time. Measures to improve the waste handling systems are 
implemented in almost all sectors, but not always in the most efficient way. The waste produced 
is different from industry to industry, so different solutions must be considered.  
However, a common approach valid for all the circumstances have been developed in order to 
help the decision-makers to identify the right path to follow. This is called waste hierarchy and 
aims at priorities disposal methods based on their efficiencies (Pitt & Smith 2003). According to 
the hierarchy, the order of application should be as follow: waste prevention, reuse, recycling, 
recovery in terms of material and energy and finally landfilling (Christensen 2011). The message 
is first to minimize the waste production when it is possible, otherwise to reuse and recycle 
(Schmidt et al. 2007). Landfilling must be avoided at most or reduce to few materials, i.e. 
hazardous waste. This because landfill 
==PAGE:14==
2                                                                                                            Chapter 1 - Introduction                                   
 
 
 
 
 
can be a threat for the environment, has high costs and a long after-care period (Weng et al. 2015). 
On the other hand, reuse and recycling have many benefits as the savings in the extraction and 
the manufacturing of virgin materials and the connected energy used. Between reuse/recycling 
and landfilling is placed the incineration process, which is commonly applied in many countries 
because it allows the recovery of electricity and heat from the waste, but at the same time shows 
some issues such as air emissions. It is interesting to notice how the first recommended 
suggestion, waste prevention, is not even a disposal method. However, this is theoretically the 
most efficient way to handle the waste and the best weapon to fight it (Pitt & Smith 2003). The 
first goal of the waste hierarchy is to promote those waste disposals, which have the better 
efficiencies and lower impact to the environment. These guidelines are mainly useful for decision 
makers, which are for instance municipalities or companies because they have to establish the 
sorting system and the waste management in general (Schmidt et al. 2007). 
When designing a waste management system (WMS), many factors have to be considered, as the 
waste composition, the budget or the culture of the interested population. For these reasons, it is 
important to analyze each sector in order to highlight the main hotspots that need to be improved. 
This project will focus on one specific sector, which has a huge importance in the global economy: 
the aviation industry (Lufthansa 1997; British Airways Limited 1995). Today's tourism and 
international trade would not be even thinkable without the growth of the airlines that is happening 
in the last years (Mehta 2015). Air transport is one of the fastest growing industries also from a 
financial point of view, as confirmed in Figure 1.1.

an average annual growth of 6.5% in the flight demand and 0.5% in number of passengers (Leahy

2015; Current Market Outlook 2007). Unfortunately, the aviation sector is known as one of the 
major polluters (Pitt & Smith 2003). Not only the waste production but also energy consumption, 
noise, air pollution and hydrological damages are some of the controversies connected to the 
aviation (Pitt & Brown 2001). This leads to the need of a sustainable transportation, which means 
sustainable from a social, economic and environmental point of view.  
Following the waste hierarchy, the first step when planning and implementing a waste 
management system is to avoid waste production and, when this is not possible, to reuse or recycle 
the materials. This suggestion can be applied especially in the inflight waste production (Schmidt 
et al. 2007). Here, some of the main issues are the high possibilities in waste avoidance from 
magazines, flyers or similar and the source separation during the flight in order to facilitate the 
disposal treatments. Recycling, especially, has many benefits not only for the environment, but it 
is also convenient for business and from a social point of view. It is not clear how big the 
percentage of recyclables is in the inflight waste, since the waste composition change radically 
within different countries, period of the years and from the policies applied from the airline 
companies. Some studies have tried to estimate it, leading to different results. Li et al. (2003) 
found that between 45-58% of the inflight waste is potentially recyclable. Similar results are 
reported from Sustainable Aviation (2010); they state that over half of the waste can be recycled. 
Prashant Mehta (2015), sustain that the share is even higher, and around 60% of it can be easily 
recycled. Finally, the Airport Cooperative research program (2014) reported that the quantity is 
instead between 25%-35%. Comparing the results of these 4 studies, a range from 25 to 60% is 
recorded. Hence, the results are characterized by high uncertainties which lead to differences in 
the results. However, what all these studies have in common is that even the lowest shows a high 
recycling potential, highlighting the importance in the optimization of the recycling scheme for 
the inflight waste.  
It is important to understand the benefits that an efficient recycling plan leads to achieve. Many 
environmental advantages are hidden in the recycling, for instance, the avoidance of using virgin 
materials and natural resources, or the energy savings in the material production. Moreover, 
recycling decreases the amount of waste produced, and can, therefore, be connected with the 
prevention, which is the first priority of the waste hierarchy. Recycling is also a way for the 
airlines companies to show their willingness to improve their sustainability and can help 
advertising themselves with the public (Jenks et al. 2014). As mentioned before, recycling is not 
only a good option from an environmental point of view, but it is also valuable for the economy 
and the society. It is estimated that in 2010 the airlines companies spent more than $20 million to 
dispose the waste produced in their aircrafts, while the market value of the recyclables contained 
in those waste was around $18 to $26 million. Recycling produces also jobs, and it is, therefore, 
a benefit for the society. Table 1.1 shows that the recycling industry supports up to 35 times the 
jobs per ton of waste compared to landfilling and incineration (Jenks et al. 2014).

Recycling has many advantages for the environment, the society and also the economy. However, 
in the aviation industry, there are many issues to handle a good recycling scheme which can only 
work if all the stakeholders collaborate. Nowadays, some of the main obstacles to face are:  
 
Many companies still do not want to face the problem, and continue to produce huge 
amount of inflight waste without applying a consistent waste management plan; 
 
Many companies are costly driven rather than aiming at the environmental performances; 
 
Some airports are not modern and do not have some required equipment to guarantee a 
good waste management of both the waste generated in the airport and the one from the 
aircrafts; 
 
Many companies are simply not aware of the advantages they can achieve with better 
environmental performances, i.e. they are not aware of the possible revenues by selling 
the waste, or they do not know which are the best practices to apply;  
 
Each country has different management system and this is often an obstacle for the airline 
companies which have to apply a different scheme depending on the country they land 
in; 
 
There is not a uniform opinion yet regarding the best system to apply. 
1.1 
INFLIGHT WASTE 
Among all the waste produced in the airport, the inflight waste is the most interesting to 
investigate. This because from a waste generation point of view, airports are similar to cities, 
therefore the waste produced can be compared to the municipal solid waste, even though some 
differences in the composition appears (Mehta 2015). For that reason, similarities in the Waste 
Management Plan can be found between municipalities and airports. If for this type of waste 
management, many studies have been conducted and a wide range of possible solutions are 
already available, the inflight waste is different in regards to the production and the composition. 
Therefore it is more interesting for further investigations. Firstly, inflight waste compose 20% of 
the total airport waste and it is, therefore, a relevant share (Mehta 2015). Inflight waste is defined 
as the waste produced from the passengers during the duration of a flight and it is generally 
divided into 2 categories: 
 
Cabin Waste: is composed of everything it is collected during the cleaning of the aircraft 
after landing (Li et al. 2002); 
 
Galley Waste: is the trash collected from the bins and the compactors (Li et al. 2002) 
Currently one of the main issues while analyzing inflight waste is the lack of data on its 
composition. Its characteristics and its volume can change a lot from one flight to another, 
depending on many factors as the duration of the flight, the capacity and the configuration of the 
aircraft, the types of meal served, the catering, delays in flight etc. (Mehta 2015). Likewise

1.2.1 Sustainability and ISO14001 standards 
Receiving such a big number of costumers, Thomas Cook Airlines aims to provide a sustainable 
service. For that reason, they incorporate a sustainable and social commitment as one of their 
fundamental principles (Thomas Cook Airlines 2015). The company is indeed ISO14001 
certified. This certification aims mainly to enhance the environmental performances of a company 
and help to achieve some environmental targets (Keniston-Longrie 2015). Moreover, label 
implies some duties to respect in order to be certified. The company has to provide competencies 
and awareness in regards to the environmental topics, they need to implement an internal and an 
external communication of their procedures, meaning that they need to keep both the employees 
and the audience informed about their standards and their environmental performances. Finally, 
they have to provide reliable documentation about the company's sustainability achievements 
(Keniston-Longrie 2015). One of the advantages of being ISO certified is that this shows the 
public the willingness of the company to pursue a sustainable business.  
1.2.2 Environmental focus 
It is estimated, that around 92% of the total environmental impacts from a charter flight are 
generated from carbon emissions, cabin waste and in-flight service products. Thomas Cook 
performs very well in regards to the carbon emission, indeed in the last environmental report, they 
presented an average emission per passenger of only 71.5 gCO2 per passenger kilometer, where 
the average of the 5 largest European airlines companies is about 93 g CO2 gCO2 per passenger 
kilometer (Thomas Cook Airlines 2015). The inflight waste management is also a very interesting 
topic in the recent years since the growing awareness of the audience is growing regarding the 
high possibilities for recycling and energy savings that waste can provide. This year Thomas Cook 
Scandinavia reports 1158 tons of cabin waste generated, which correspond to 0.489 kg/guests. 
This is a good improvement, considering the previous year average of 0.51 kg/guest (Thomas 
Cook Airlines 2015). 
1.3 
GOAL OF THE PROJECT 
This project is mainly focused on the inflight waste management in Thomas Cook Airlines at 
Copenhagen Airports and it is divided into 2 phases.  
The first is about a full understanding of the waste management system in Thomas Cook. Many 
stakeholders are involved in the process, as the cleaning and the catering companies, therefore it 
is currently not clear what the role of each partner is and which WM scheme is applied. In order 
to investigate that, the facilities in the airports have been visited and their supervisors are 
interviewed in order to clarify the process. Furthermore, samples of the waste produced during a 
flight are collected and analyzed. A waste characterization and a moisture content analysis are 
performed in order to get information about the waste generation of the process.  
The second phase of the project regards a Life Cycle Assessment. LCA is a decision support tool 
which aims to analyses the entire product's life cycle, from the extraction of the resources to the 
disposal of the waste. It quantifies all the emissions, the consume of resources and the 
==PAGE:20==
8                                                                                                            Chapter 1 - Introduction                                   
 
 
 
 
 
environmental impact caused by the product directly or indirectly (European Commission 2010). 
The goal is first to describe the current situation and then to apply some possible adjustments to 
improve its environmental performances. Variation in the waste composition, the segregation and 
the disposal are tested. In order to accomplish that, an LCA is performed using the LCA software 
EASETECH. 
This chapter has tried to give the reader a general picture of the current inflight waste management 
system and a brief presentation of the company which will be the interest of the study. There is 
currently not much literature about this topic since its importance is growing in the recent years. 
Finally, a presentation of the structure of the study was presented.

2.1 
OVERVIEW 
The first challenge of the present study is to investigate how the inflight waste management is 
handled in Thomas Cook's flight. This is guided mainly by 2 companies: Sodexo1 and LSG Sky 
Chefs2. The former is the cleaning company, which takes care of cleaning the flight after landing,

the 9 aircrafts inspected this year, 5 of them were A330, while the other 4 where A321. The

results of sampling campaign can be seen in Appendix 2. The results reported from the catering 
were collected from the company Gate Gourmet, which was the previous partner of Thomas Cook 
until the February 2016. However, no relevant changes in the amount and the composition of the 
waste are expected, therefore the volumes are considered trustful for the project. According to 
Thomas Cook, the biggest share of the volume belongs to the fraction combustibles, which

In order to investigate the whole procedures which are performed by Thomas Cook Airlines and

A Life Cycle Assessment is composed by 5 phases, being them Goal and Scope Definition, Life 
Cycle Inventory (LCI), Life Cycle impact assessment (LCIA) and Interpretation. LCA is an 
iterative process, therefore all these phases have to be repeated to improve the quality of the 
results. Initially, the Goal and Scope Definition are performed: during this stage, the system and 
the objective of the study are clearly defined. This step is crucial for a further correct use and 
interpretation of the results (European Commission 2010).

the current inflight waste management system of the company Thomas Cook Airlines. The 
analysis will be focused on the procedures applied in Copenhagen Airport. Possible outputs of 
the study are an increase in the awareness concerning weakness or strength of the current system 
and suggestions for improvements.

2.2.1.2 Method, Assumptions and Impact Limitations 
Regarding the methodology, it was decided to use EASETECH, a specific LCA software 
developed by the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). Unlike other general LCA software, 
EASETECH is developed specifically for waste scenarios, from the waste generation to the 
emission to the environment (Clavreul et al. 2014). Furthermore, the ILCD Handbook was used 
as a reference along all the process and its requirements have been fulfilled (European 
Commission 2010).  
The LCIA method used is the one recommended from the European Commission: “ILCD 
recommended – 2013 NR”. 
The method considers the following Impact Categories (IC): Climate Change (GWP), Terrestrial 
Acidification (TA), Terrestrial Eutrophication (TE), Photochemical Oxidant Formation (POFP), 
Stratospheric Ozone Depletion (ODP), Human Toxicity (carcinogenic) (HTc), Human Toxicity 
(non-carcinogenic) (HTnc), Iosining Radiation (IR), Freshwater Eutrophication (FE), Marine 
Eutrophication (ME), Eco-toxicity (Ecotox), Resource Depletion of abiotic resources (fossil) 
(Rdfos), Resource Depletion of abiotic resources (reserve base) (RD), and Particulate Matter 
(PM). 
During the LCIA, the analysis focuses on 6 of those impact categories, which are considered the 
most relevant for the present case. The choice of the impact categories was conducted based on 
the specific contribution that each of them could give to the analysis and the interpretation.  
The chosen IC are Climate Change, Human Toxicity (non-carcinogenic), Freshwater and Marine 
Eutrophication, Resource Depletion (fossil) and Eco-toxicity. 
Climate Change (GWP) is the threat related to the greenhouse effect on the atmosphere. 
Anthropogenic activities are contributing enhancing its development provoking the phenomenon 
called global warming, which causes the rising of the temperature across the planet in the long 
period.  Melting of glaciers, rise of sea level, extreme storms, droughts, impact on agriculture and 
land availability, loss in biodiversity are some of the other drawbacks. The main greenhouse gases 
produced from anthropogenic activities are CO2, CH4 and N20. The characterization factor of 
climate change is called Global Warming Potential as it is measured in kg CO2-eq/kg gas (Heidi 
K. Stranddorf; Leif Hoffmann; Anders Schmidt; 2005; Hoffmann & Schmidt 2003).  
GWP is chosen in this project because it is considered the most important impact category 
representing the most debated environmental topic nowadays. Moreover, the high utilization of 
Waste-to-Energy (WtE) technologies in the model justifies the choice because of the GHG 
emissions related to the incineration plant.  
Human Toxicity (non-carcinogenic) (HTnc) aims at assessing the toxicological effect on human 
health of the chemicals emitted into the environment. Human toxicity is expressed in number of 
cases/kgemitted , and considers the impact on the human health caused by each unit of toxic 
substance emitted into the environment. The number of compounds to consider is limitless; 
therefore the model is very complicated and provides an estimation of their impact to the human 
health (Heidi K. Stranddorf; Leif Hoffmann; Anders Schmidt; 2005; Hoffmann & Schmidt 2003). 
Eco-toxicity (Ecotox) determines the impact of all toxic substances to the environment. As for 
Human Toxicity, it is very complicated to model a wide range of substances within a single 
==PAGE:27==
Chapter 2 - Materials and Methods                                                                                         15 
 
 
 
 
category. The score is combined in one Equivalent Factor (EF) describing the repercussion on air, 
water and soil from acute and chronic effects of the substances. The main contributors on Eco-
toxicity are organic compounds, metals and pesticides (Heidi K. Stranddorf; Leif Hoffmann; 
Anders Schmidt; 2005; Hoffmann & Schmidt 2003). 
Human Toxicity and Eco-toxicity are inserted in the analysis in order to assess how the system 
performs regarding toxic impact categories. Moreover, high Eco-toxicity is expected in one of the 
scenarios implemented in the model, where Landfill is the only disposal treatment used.  
Freshwater and Marine Eutrophication (FE, ME) describe a phenomenon that takes place as a 
consequence of nutrient enrichments in a water basin. Especially during the summer, the sunlight 
combined with the high presence of nutrients enhances the algae growth, which creates a layer on 
the water surface. This layer inhibits the sunlight to reach the bottom of the basin, creating a 
gradient in the temperature from the surface to the bottom. This temperature discrepancy does not 
allow the natural circulation of the oxygen in the lower part of the basin creating anaerobic 
conditions in the long period. The final stage of the eutrophication is the death of the flora and 
the fauna populating the bottom layer and the formation of toxic substances as H2S due to 
anaerobic conditions. Eutrophication can be detected from the high turbidity of the water caused 
by the high concentration of algae and it is caused by the elevated quantity of nutrients as P and 
N in the water (Heidi K. Stranddorf; Leif Hoffmann; Anders Schmidt; 2005; Hoffmann & 
Schmidt 2003). 
Marine and Freshwater Eutrophication are chosen especially in relation to the utilization of the 
Anaerobic Digestion in the model. One of the outputs of the Anaerobic Digestion is the digestate, 
which is a substance used to substitute other fertilizers. Savings in the utilization of N and P are 
then expected and with them also in the eutrophication potential.  
Resource Depletion, fossil (RDfos) assesses the effect of the material utilization on the future 
availability. The principle of sustainability is to fulfill our needs without compromising the 
possibilities for future generations (UNECE 2016). Resource depletion (fossil) weight the impact 
provoked from the consumption of fossil fuels (Heidi K. Stranddorf; Leif Hoffmann; Anders 
Schmidt; 2005; Hoffmann & Schmidt 2003). 
It is considered due to the presence of Aluminum in the system, the recycling of which allow 
considerable savings of energy and as a consequence less consumption of fossil fuels. Indeed, 
aluminum is expensive to produce from an energetic perspective.  
2.2.1.3 Decision Context 
Following the ILCD guidelines, there are currently 4 possible decision context situations, being 
them A, B, C1 and C2. A and B are called respectively "Micro-level decision support" and 
"Macro-level decision support" and they are used when it is assumed that some operative 
decisions can be driven from the results of the LCA. C1 and C2 are called "Accounting". C1 if 
the system interact with other systems, C2 if it does not (European Commission 2010).  
This project belongs to situation A because the company might decide to undertake some small 
changes in its system due to the results of this study. Moreover, this study will use average data 
regarding energy production and consumption. It is, therefore, an Attributional LCA.

2.2.2.2 Impact Assessment Criteria 
The choice of the LCIA method is a very important step, and it must be consistent with the goal 
of the study. It is important to adopt a method, which covers all the main impacts related to the 
system in order not to leave some important information out of the analysis (European 
Commission 2010).  
The method, which is going to be followed in the present study is the ILCD recommended, and it 
covers 14 impact categories. Table 2.1 presents all the impact categories with their normalization 
factors. Normalization factors aim to convert all the impact categories in the same unit, which is 
called Person Equivalent (PE) and is obtained dividing the characterized results for the 
normalization factor. The strength of the normalization is that allow understanding to which 
impact categories the system has the highest impact. This is useful information that cannot be 
driven through the characterized results since they provide impact scores with different units and 
therefore comparison across impact categories is not allowed.  
However, within the normalized results, one must be careful during the analysis because even if 
the results are comparable the impacts should not be summed, since some of the impact categories 
are considered more important than others. Therefore even if in the system one impact category 
scores more than another, the latter might represents a topic with higher importance in a global 
scale. For example, Climate Change is generally considered the most important impact categories, 
so even if it might have a lower normalized score then others it should still be considered 
individually (European Commission 2010).  
If the LCA practitioner wants to be able to sum across different impact categories in order to 
provide a compact result represented by only one number, a weighting must be performed. It 
consists on assigning distinctive quantitative importance to each impact category (European 
Commission 2010). This allows the comparison of the results across impact categories but is very 
subjective, because it is up to the author of the study to evaluate the importance of each IC. Hence, 
in this study, only characterized and normalized results will be analyzed, due to the high 
uncertainty related to the weighting method.

2.2.2.5 Data quality assessment 
The Life Cycle Assessment is a type of study, which requires the collection and the utilization of 
a considerable amount of data. Hence, it is important to assess the quality of these to evaluate the 
validity of the study. In the present, data specificity and the quality of the data source are 
investigated.  
In order to assess the data specificity, the evaluation is conducted by the division of the data into 
three categories, being them product specific, site specific and general. The first cluster applies 
to all those data that are precisely found in relation to this project. The second category refers to 
those which are not representing exactly the process interested but that are still reliable because 
they are taken from similar studies. The latter category is instead for all those data, which are 
general, so they are generally estimated. It is intuitive to understand that the quality and the 
reliability of the data decrease from the first to the third category.

example of bad practice. Indirectly, this should highlight the goodness of Thomas Cook Airlines 
current performances.  
This is not an attempt to say that Thomas Cook`s performances are already good enough and they 
should not be improved, however it is always a good idea to set high and low scores in order to 
have a range of comparison where to place each scenario.  
Therefore, this scenario was modelled applying no source separation and sending all the waste to 
landfill. 
This scenario could not be performed in Denmark, where there are severe restrictions for 
landfilling, however, it can be realistic for other European countries which still make a wider use 
of landfill as a disposal treatment. Processes from EASETECH Database are used to model 
landfill scenario (Olesen & Damgaard 2014a). 
2.2.2.6.7 Scenario 1b (S1b): Old moisture content 
The aim of this scenario is to reproduce a situation identical to the one in the Baseline scenario, 
with the exception of the moisture content applied to the material fractions.  
Scenario 1b will measure the difference made from the moisture content analysis. This is 
interesting to analyze how accurate the average data provided in the database are for this process. 
As for Scenario 1a, this scenario is interesting in order to assess how important can be the 
utilization of real data instead of average process within the LCA. 
2.2.2.6.8 Scenario S1c, S3a and S4a: 100% Segregation Efficiency 
The last three scenarios are built to simulate what could happen to the performances of S1, S3 
and S4 if the segregation efficiencies would reach 100%.  The goal is to understand how a well-
performed segregation can modify the performances of an entire system.  
It was chosen to perform this test only on these three scenarios for different reasons: S1a and S1b 
are similar to S1 and therefore only S1 was considered in order to avoid redundant results. S2 and 
S5 were instead left out because their efficiencies are already set to 100%, having them only one 
disposal treatment (Waste-to-Energy and landfill). 
S1c simulates S1, S3a simulates S3 and S4a scenario 4.

The goal of the Life Cycle Inventory is to quantify the elementary flows that the system exchanged 
with the environment (Laurent 2015). This is one of the most important phases of the entire LCA, 
because it includes data collection and modeling of the system in EASETECH (European 
Commission 2010).  
3.1 PLANNING AND DATA COLLECTION 
High-quality data are a basic requirement for any kind of study, in order to provide reliable results. 
This step of the Life Cycle Assessment aims to build a clear idea of all the data needed for the 
model, and it is useful to identify the quality required. The data collection is an iterative phase, 
because it is constantly updated along the process (European Commission 2010).  
The model is divided into the 5 stages of the waste management: waste generation, source 
separation, collection, transportation and disposal. Sub-processes are then detected and the data 
needed for the project are presented in Table 3.1. Some processes required very specific data 
because they highly influence the results, e.g. the waste composition. For others, site specific or 
general data can be satisfactory, e.g. collection and transportation. The quality of the data is 
influenced by many factors, such the time frame of the project, the data availability, number of 
data required or financial budget.

Due to the limited timeframe of the project, it was not possible to collect specific data for all the 
processes, therefore some assumptions have to be made in order to fill the lack of data.  
Aiming at presenting real data regarding the waste characterization, the company provided some 
samples which have been analyzed. However, the samples collected were limited to 15.7kg of 
combustibles waste (wet weight), which are not enough to propose a truly reliable data. It was not 
==PAGE:43==

possible to collect and analyze a wider amount of waste mainly for the limited time frame of the 
project and the difficultness while collecting the samples.  
Since the samples were not representing all the waste composition of a flight, it was not possible 
to derive precise sorting efficiency from the waste characterization. The company did not provide 
any documentation regarding sorting performances. Therefore, assumptions had to be made in

3.3 INFLIGHT WASTE COMPOSITION 
One of the most important stages of the project, has been the collection and the analysis of the 
samples provided by the company. The following section will describe the methodology and the 
procedures used to calculate the values insert in the model. 
3.3.1 Waste characterization 
Accurate data regarding waste characterization are a crucial starting point to plan a waste 
management system (Edjabou et al. 2015). Thomas Cook Airlines provided very limited data on 
the waste composition, regarding only the share between aluminum, combustibles, magazines and 
glass. No information regarding the waste characterization of the combustibles was accessible 
(Appendix 2 – Thomas Cook Data). Hence, samples have been collected and analyzed for the 
project.  
According to Edjabou et al. (2015), the waste characterization is a process, which is composed 
by three phases; the sampling, followed by the sorting and finally the data analysis. A scientific 
and consistent approach to each of these three steps is strongly influencing the quality of the 
results.  
Firstly, the samples collected should be representative from a geographical and temporal 
perspective. Moreover, the more various the composition of the waste, the bigger samples is 
needed to include all the possible fractions in the more realistic quantity. In this project, due to 
limited timeframe, only a small sample was collected. The writer knows that the results might 
contain some uncertainties due to the not full geographical and temporal representativeness of the 
data. However, it was decided to proceed anyway with the analysis due to the academicals aim of 
the project. A full waste characterization analysis was considered a very useful experience to 
perform, both for the project itself and academically. 
The second step is the sorting activity. One of the biggest challenge acknowledged during the first 
part of the project, was to find comparable studies in order to build a solid idea of the inflight 
waste composition. However, due to inconsistencies regarding the nomenclature used in different 
==PAGE:44==
32                                                                             Chapter 3 - Life Cycle Inventory Analysis                                
 
 
 
 
 
studies, only a partial comparison was possible, as explained in Chapter 1. Unfortunately, there 
is not an international approved sorting methodology to follow yet, therefore this paper will follow 
the one proposed from Edjabou et al., (2015) in Municipal solid waste composition: sampling 
methodology, statistical analysis and case study evaluation. 
The last phase of the waste characterization, is the data analysis. Unfortunately, due to the scarcity 
and low representativeness of the samples obtained, no certain conclusion can be driven from the 
samples. Nevertheless, the data were analyzed and considered as trustful for this project.  
If Thomas Cook Airlines is interested in carry on further study to improve its knowledge about 
the composition of the waste they produce, the first step suggested hereby is to perform a full and 
reliable sampling campaign. To do so, representative samples collected in order to provide reliable 
data.  
The following section will describe the methodology followed to perform firstly the waste 
characterization and then to calculate the moisture content of the samples. 
3.3.1.1 Sampling methodology 
The first step of the sampling campaign has been collecting the waste. The samples have been 
provided as part of the waste collected from Sodexo in one flight, and was composed only by 
bags belonging to the combustibles fraction. No samples from the aluminum or magazines 
fractions were available for the analysis. Since the combustibles bags collected from Sodexo are 
identical of those handled by LSG Sky Chefs, the analysis was conducted only in that sample. 
The results were then applied to the entire combustibles fraction in the model. This assumption is 
considered valid since there are no differences between the two fractions received by the 
companies. Regarding the aluminum and the magazines, data provided from TMA were used to 
estimate the share in the total composition (Thomas Cook Airlines 2016). Furthermore, the 
aluminum moisture content was then calculated from the fraction found in the combustible bags, 
while for magazines data from EASETECH have been applied.

3.3.1.1.4 Moisture Content 
Subsequently, it was decided to analyze the moisture content of the samples. Understand the dry 
weight is very important for many reasons, e.g. that the water quantity influence the recycling 
possibility for a material (Magrinho & Semiao 2008). 
In order to perform the analysis, the procedures from the European Standard were applied 
(CEN/TC 335 2004). Each fraction was analyzed separately, and only fractions from Level 1 
characterization were investigated. 
Firstly, all the fractions have been shredded separately, in order to make them in small pieces. 
The importance of shredding the materials in mainly for two reasons; to assure a good mixing 
within a fraction, and to make the material easier to handle it (Illustrations in Appendix 3). 
The principle behind the procedure is to dry the samples in the oven at a constant temperature of 
105 °C (± 2 °C) and leave the samples there until the mass is constant. It is important that the 
temperature is kept at 105 °C and not higher, in order to avoid the evaporation of other volatile 
substances (CEN/TC 335 2004).  
Accordingly to the International Standards, the containers have been prepared and weighed before 
and after being filled up with samples. The containers had different dimensions, and at least 2 of 
them were filled with each fraction. Due to the small dimension of the oven, a maximum of 2 
fractions could be measured at the same time.

3.3.1.3 Collection and Transportation  
Collection and Transportation are generally stages which are not highly influencing the results of 
the LCA, unless big distances have to be travelled to collect and transport the materials.  
Collection and Transportation are expressed as the fuel consumption of the truck used to collect 
the waste (wet weight) from the Aircraft and move it to the collection facility and from there to 
the disposal facilities. Since no data regarding the collection and the transportation are available 
in this case, it was decided to use the same process for the collection and the transportation of the 
waste.  
All the trucks are assumed to have a weight between 28t and 32t and to comply with EURO5 
technology standard. The emissions are modelled for Danish situation but can be applied for every 
vehicle utilizing EURO5 standards (Olesen 2013; Winther & Slentø 2010). 
Since in the present study the distances in the transportation are low, they were estimated to 5 km 
for the collection inside the airport, while 100 km for the transportation to the external disposal 
facilities. Only the transport to the Waste-to-Energy facility was measured and the result was 7.5 
km3. Regarding the transportation of fly ash, iron scrap, aluminum scrap and bottom ash from the 
incineration plant to further disposal treatments, distances of respectively 500, 200, 200 and 100 
km were utilized (Bisinella 2016).

3.3.1.5 Disposal Technologies 
Many Processes have been used across the scenarios to model the disposal technologies, most of 
them were taken from already existing processes in EASETECH database, while for Waste-to-
Energy a specific process was built. The following section describes the inventory used to build 
the model.  
3.3.1.5.1 Aluminum Recycling  
Aluminum recycling was modelled using a process considering aluminum scraps as input, which 
were melted and reprocessed in the facility to new aluminum sheets. The overall recovery ratio is 
of 94% of the initial material quantity and only 6% of the collected scrap is loss in the process 
(Swedish Recycling Industries’ Association 2008).  
The recycling substitute the production of virgin aluminum from raw materials (bauxite, energy 
carriers, water) (International Aluminium Institute 2007). 
The original electricity consumption is substituted with the Danish electricity grid.  
3.3.1.5.2 Paper Recycling 
The process utilized to model the recycling of the magazines collected in the aircraft considered 
as mixed newspapers and magazines as input, giving newspaper and pulp as output. The ratio in 
the substitution of the production of virgin newspaper is 86% (Schmidt & Strömberg 2006; Lood 
2010).  
==PAGE:58==
46                                                                             Chapter 3 - Life Cycle Inventory Analysis                                
 
 
 
 
 
The magazine recycling substitutes the production of newspapers from wood and auxiliary 
materials (Frees 2005). 
The original electricity consumption is substituted with the Danish electricity grid.  
3.3.1.5.3 Plastic Recycling 
The process utilized to model the recycling of plastic considers PP, PE, PS or PET as inputs and 
plastic granulates as output. It consists on grinding the material for recycling. The 75% of the 
material is considered recyclable, while the remaining material is sorted and sent to incineration. 
After the subtraction of the non-recyclable materials, an efficiency of 97% is considered for the 
process (SWEREC AB 2007; SWEREC 2006). The process substitutes the production of HDPE. 
As for the other processes, the electricity consumption is substituted with the Danish electricity 
grid.  
3.3.1.5.4 Anaerobic Digestion 
The anaerobic digestion was modelled through a process which considers organic waste as input, 
such as garden waste, waste from restaurants or from food stores and provides as output biogas 
and digestate.  
The biogas is then utilized for combined heat and power production and energy substitution.  
The digestate is modeled to substitute other organic fertilizers in land application with air, soil, 
and surface and groundwater emission due to Use of Land. The rate of the fertilizer substitution 
is set to 40% for N, while 100% for K and P, based on the Danish farming practice (Anon n.d.; 
Børgesen et al. 2001; Bruun & Sander 2006). Emissions of heavy metals to agricultural soil were 
estimated based on the heavy metal content in N, P and K (Audsley et al. 1997; Hansen, T. L., 
Bhander, G. S., Christensen, T. H., Bruun, S., & Jensen 2006) 
3.3.1.5.5 Thermal process (Waste-to-Energy) 
Waste-to-Energy process was modelled according to a personal communication (Bisinella 2016). 
The process considers municipal solid waste as an input and fly ash, bottom ash, waste water, iron 
scrap and aluminum scrap as outputs. Moreover, recovery of heat and electricity is performed.  
Electricity recovery efficiency is set to 28%, while 78% is the heat recovery. Avoided heat 
production is considered from natural gas. Electricity recovery is modelled according to the ratio 
assessed for the Danish electricity production, as well as the electricity consumption. (Danish 
Electricity Grid).  
Regarding the outputs of the process, they are considered transported to further treatments. Fly 
ash are utilized for the neutralization process of acid waste from titanium industry (NOAH n.d.; 
Astrup 2008). Aluminum scraps are sent to recycling facilities and the same process described in 
section 3.1.3.6.1 is utilized. Metal scraps are instead shredded and reprocessed to steel sheets in 
different dimensions and profiles for EU market (Ovako n.d.). Bottom ash are landfilled having 
residues and leachate as output. The leachate is collected, some is distributed to Waste Water 
Treatments Plants and some remain untreated. A part of the leachate is considered as uncontrolled. 
==PAGE:59==
Chapter 3 - Life Cycle Inventory Analysis                                                                             47 
 
 
 
 
All the electricity consumption in the processes is modelled according to the Danish electricity 
grid. 
3.3.1.5.6 Landfill 
In order to model the landfill, a process from the database was used. It considers the material and 
the energy used for building and running a landfill with a capacity of 3.5 million t. of waste 
(Brogaard & et al 2013). The output of the landfill is the methane production (US EPA 2011).  
The gas collection is modelled as an average performing landfill and it stops after 55 years: the 
gas collection pass from 35% within the first 5 years, to 75% after 40 years. Gas which is not 
collected is leaking and it is either emitted to the environment or utilized for heat and power 
generation. Collected gas is oxidized and emitted to the environment.(Olesen & Damgaard 2014b) 
The second output from the landfill is Leachate, modelled with a precipitation from 500 to 1200 
mm/year. Part of the leachate is collected and sent to waste water treatment plant, while the rest 
is emitted to the ocean (Olesen & Damgaard 2014b).  
In Appendix 3, a table will all the processes used in the model is presented.

system in S1 (93%). Obviously, reaching 100% segregation efficiency is utopian in the real world,

segregation is always one of the most important phases of the waste management system, and if 
it is conducted properly, it strongly contributes to increase the efficiency of the whole

the substitution of organic fertilizers with the digestate produced during the Anaerobic Digestion.  
Finally, the main contributions to Resource Depletion are from the WtE and the aluminum 
recycling, but also recycling of plastic performs high savings when applied, due to avoidance in 
the utilization of oil in the plastic production. S5 is as always the exception and performs very 
low savings compared to the other scenarios. Those, derives from the utilization of Landfill gas 
to produce energy which substitutes energy production from coal.  
4.3 SUMMARY 
Characterized results showed savings for all the scenarios regarding the considered impact 
categories. Only Scenario 6 performs poorly. Resource Depletion and Global Warming Potential 
presented the highest scores.  
A general trend can be addressed from the contribution analysis: Waste-to-Energy and aluminum 
recycling are the key stages because they are the main contributors in the total impact. Anaerobic 
digestion performs a net contribution for some impact categories such as Eco-toxicity, Human 
Toxicity and for Marine Eutrophication, while net savings for Freshwater Eutrophication. Plastic 
recycling is used only in 4 scenarios, and it highly contributes to the savings for GWP and RDfos.  
Generally, most of the scenarios perform net savings in all the impact categories, with some 
exceptions due to drawbacks of the technologies used (i.e. the toxicity of the digestate produced 
from the anaerobic digestion).

5 DISCUSSION 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
This chapter discusses the main outputs obtained during the waste characterization of the samples 
and presents the results of the sensitivity analysis performed on the model.  
5.1 IMPORTANCE OF THE WASTE CHARACTERIZATION  
The first element which is going to be discussed is the importance of the waste characterization. 
Data quality is probably the most influencing parameter in all the LCA, and it is therefore crucial 
to provide the highest possible quality in the input data.  
The analysis of the waste composition revealed some unexpected results, which contrast with the 
expectations deliberate from Thomas Cook Airlines at the beginning of the project: 
 
A considerable amount of food waste was detected in the combustible fraction (46%), 
almost completely composed by bread leftovers. The original opinion of the Thomas 
Cook Airlines` referent was that the leftovers of the meals were collected in the carts and 
handled from the Sky Chefs (the catering company). In reality, almost no food waste is 
collected separately (only some leftovers from the meals, but very few quantity), and all 
the waste ends up in the combustibles fraction. This can make a big difference when 
planning the waste management system, since food waste is characterized by a high 
moisture content and is therefore not convenient to incinerate it, if alternatives are 
possible; 
 
The glass fraction detected is negligible. Thus, there is no possibility for recycling; 
==PAGE:70==
58                                                                                                               Chapter 5 - Discussion                                 
 
 
 
 
 
 
The plastic fraction is composed mainly by plastic bottles (74%) and food containers 
(22%). As proposed in S3, there is room for recycling plastic bottles, if properly sorted 
in order to avoid contaminations. Food containers, cannot be reused right now, due to the 
high food contamination. A possible improvement of the system could be the introduction 
of bioplastic containers, which could be sorted together with the food and disposed 
through biological treatments. This idea was proposed to the referent in the company, 
who was willing to undertake such a path. However, the strict regulation regarding the 
utilization of the food leftovers can be an issue which has to be further investigated.  
Performing the moisture content analysis, the results have been compared with the values found 
in EASETECH database. The difference between default and measured values is on average of 
15% and the biggest differences have been detected from the food waste fraction (36%) and from 
the miscellaneous combustibles (23%).  
In the scenario analysis the influence of the waste characterization and the moisture content 
analysis in the final results were investigated, by comparing S1 with S1a and S1b. 
This step is considered very important, because it did not just provide with more representative 
data that improved the quality of the results, but it also helped to have a deeper understanding of 
the waste composition by seeing and directly analyzing it.  
5.2 SENSITIVITY ANALYSIS  
Sensitivity analysis identifies how the outputs of the model vary incrementing the value of a 
parameter in the inputs (Bisinella et al. 2016). The sensitivity analysis performed in this project 
is composed by two phases: a perturbation analysis, which aims to determine the effect of a 
change in the input parameter on the results, and a scenario analysis, in which some processes are 
tested individually in each scenario to assess changes in the results (Clavreul et al. 2012).  
5.2.1 Perturbation analysis 
The perturbation analysis has been implemented increasing some of the parameters “one-at-a-
time” by 10% of their original values. For each scenario, the most influential parameters have 
been tested, and the model has been run one more time for each of the parameters. The ratio 
behind the choice of the parameters was based on the contribution analysis results performed in 
Chapter 4. It was decided to analyze those parameters, which are part of the life cycle stages with 
higher contributions to the final impacts.  
Moreover, not all the scenarios have been tested, since some of them are very similar to each 
other. It was then decided to test S1 representing also S1a, S1b and S1c. Scenario 3 representing 
also S3a, S4 also for S4a and finally Scenario 2.  
Scenario 6 was not included in the perturbation analysis because it was considered not interesting 
to analyze. Indeed, it just represents a trend of bad waste management performances but it is very 
far from the reality in TCA.

The model is very sensitive to Electricity recovery from Waste-to-Energy because a big  share of 
the total waste is sent to incineration, therefore as showed in the contribution analysis (Chapter 
4), the incineration process is one of the key parameters of the system. The model is sensitive to 
this parameter for all the Impact Categories, but mainly for Global Warming Potential and 
Resource Depletion (fossil). Indeed, for those 2 impact categories, this is the most sensible 
parameter in all the scenarios tested. These results were expected due to the fact that savings 
derived from the electricity recovery are related to the avoiding production of energy from fossil 
fuels, and it is therefore connected with GWP and Resource Depletion (fossil). 
Another trend can be noticed: since decreasing the waste flow sent to incineration (the highest 
share of waste sent to WtE is in S2, then S1, S3 and S4), also the sensitivity to that parameter 
decreases across the impact categories. Scenario 4, which is characterized by the lowest quantity 
of waste burnt, has low or negligible NSR for 4 out of 6 impact categories for the electricity 
recovery. On the other hand, in scenario 2, where 100% of the flow is incinerated, the model is 
high sensitive to electricity recovery in all the IC tested.  
Also the aluminum sorting efficiency, is a very sensible parameter for the model, mainly in 
Scenario 1 and 3 and this is probably related to the presence of Zinc and Copper. However, the 
model is not very sensible to the aluminum sorting efficiency in Scenario 4. This probably due to 
the way how the NSR is calculated. Since the value presented are all normalized, if one parameter 
has a very high Sensitivity Ratio, the others will results negligible compared to that. But the reader 
must be careful analyzing the results, because some of them might be negligible compared to the 
reference but not if considered individually. However, the advantage of the NSR calculation is 
that helps to highlight and focus to the most sensitive parameters of the model, helping to 
prioritize the intervention.  
Finally, Scenario 5 is high sensitive to the variation in the Food waste segregation, especially for 
HTnc, Eco-toxicity, ME and FE. This is explained by the fact that increasing the efficiency more 
food waste is sent to Anaerobic Digestion. As discussed in Chapter 4, high uncertainties are 
related to the impact from anaerobic digestion to Toxic impact categories, related to the leaching 
of heavy metals as Zinc and Copper from the digestate produced. On the other hand, ME and FE 
are expected to be sensitive to this parameter because it increases the quantity of organic fertilizer 
substitution.  
All the other parameters resulted in low or negligible Normalized Sensitivity Ratio from the 
perturbation analysis performed, meaning that the model is not sensible to their increments.

6 CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
The environmental impact of 1 ton of inflight waste coming from Thomas Cook Airlines aircrafts 
landed in Copenhagen Airport were investigated through the analysis of the waste samples and 
the performance of a Life Cycle Assessment. Conclusions can be driven from the results 
documented in Chapter 4 and Chapter 5. 
Firstly, the high discrepancies in the results between scenarios where default or specific data 
regarding the waste composition were used, teach the importance of building a solid LCI when 
performing a Life Cycle Assessment. Indeed, these discrepancies were already predicted during 
the waste characterization and the moisture content analysis, from the diversity detected between 
default and measured data.  
Moreover, investigating the results of the current WMS (Scenario 1), good performances were 
assessed. For all the Impact Categories considered, S1 scores environmental savings, and 
comparing its performances with a bad practice scenario (S6), very big differences can be 
observed.  
However, Scenario 3 and 4 demonstrated the potential of the system, which performances can be 
improved by further segregating the waste at the source into 1 or 2 more fractions. This study 
analyzed the effect of Plastic and Food Waste recovery, respectively through plastic recycling 
and anaerobic digestion, highlighting benefits in the overall environmental impacts. Moreover, 
Scenarios 3a and 4a, showed that large gains can be achieved also increasing the sorting 
efficiency. In those scenarios, the efficiencies modelled were 100%, which is not possible in the 
real life, but those results are important to highlight the possible potential improvements. 
==PAGE:82==
70                                                                     Chapter 6 - Conclusions and Recommendation                                
 
 
 
 
 
Another output of the study comes from the comparison between the Baseline Scenario and 
Scenario 2, where all the waste was sent to incineration. Scenario 1 performed better in all the 
impact categories, determining that it is worthy to segregate even only aluminum and paper than 
utilizing only Waste-to-Energy treatments.  
The contribution analysis highlight that the aluminum and the Waste-to-Energy are the most 
influencing processes in the system, being them dominant scores in the contribution for the Impact 
Categories chosen in this study. Furthermore, the sensitivity analysis confirms this by proving 
that the model is high sensitive to increments in the electricity recovery ratio in the thermal 
treatment and to the aluminum sorting efficiency. 
Thanks to the results and the discussion performed during the study, some recommendations for 
Thomas Cook Airlines can be driven: 
1. Further investigations and analysis on the waste generation are needed in order to improve 
the dataset of this study and to be able to provide more solid conclusions; 
2. Study why the sorting efficiencies are currently so poor and train the crew to raise the 
attention and improve the quality of the sorting during and after the flight; 
3. Consider the possibility to modify the current system by further sorting other fractions as 
plastic bottles and food waste, which can give benefits for the environment and enhance 
the performances of the waste management system; 
4. Investigate the laws concerning food waste produced during flights to understand if 
further possible solutions as the food waste segregation and the substitution of plastic 
packaging with bio-plastic can be implemented.
