Abstract
The subject of this thesis is an analysis of rail freight services in Denmark.
As the general mindset of politicians in these years are focused on climate changes
and sustainable solutions the ball is in the railways court, and it is important to
keep this momentum. Understanding the development of the rail freight transport is
essential in order to learn from the failure of the past. Until the 1950’s the railway
was the primary transportation mode when it came to land based transportation.
But with a rapidly increased eﬃciency of truck operations the railway lost its position
as a transportation market leader over a short period of years. The railway has
not been able to handle this transition, as the each country had its own agenda
for railway operations. The European Union was therefore forced to adopt a plan
of action within railway operations. This resulted in several directives that should
open the market competition, generate good conditions for rail freight services and
improve the standardization of all infrastructure elements in all member states. The
United Kingdom was encouraged by this, and due to a historical bad development
within the railway industry took the initiative to take the full step and privatized
the entire railway system. The big impact did however not appear, and recently the
infrastructure manager became once again a governmental body.
In Denmark the domestic rail freight services have decreased since the 1950’s as well.
Despite several attempts to right the decreasing trends, the fact is that domestic
freight transport has a very little impact in Denmark. The constructions of both
the Great Belt Fixed Link and the ¨Oresund Fixed Link have increased the rail
freight transport in Denmark. However, the growth was primarily within only one
traﬃc type, namely the transit traﬃc between Sweden and Germany. Within some
years another mega project will be completed and once again the primary winner
will be the transit freight traﬃc. This makes it diﬃcult to ﬁnd the political incen-
tives that should encourage freight operators to put up domestic rail freight transport.

The current limitations in the system are not on the actual rail haul itself, but
rather on the terminal designs and operations, and high costs for truck operations
in connection with intermodal solution. Poor designs of the large terminals require
time-consuming operations, such as shunting of rail wagon and changing the direction
of the locomotives in the process. But also that, two out of three terminals are
operated by DB Schenker, the principal rail freight operator in Denmark, may have
the impact that several potential customers from the road hauling industries may
see this as a contribution to DB Schenker’s road division. The pre-truck hauling and
post-truck hauling are in general quite ineﬃcient compared to regular road hauling.
This is based on a high percentage of non-loaded driving with and such operations
are not within the general cabotage regulations.
In order to eliminate the limitations it is shown that the impact of minimizing road
hauling in connection to intermodal solutions is very substantial. In general this
does not generate many new customers to the rail freight market. The suggestion
is therefore to create a governmental strategic rail freight department, clustering
customers at strategic locations within the rail network, but also have a focusi on
upgrading the existing cargo terminal in order to make it attractive for international
freight operators will ﬁnd it attractive to schedule regular stops within Denmark.
In that way more international relations will be created and develop new demands
within general rail freight services in Denmark.

Chapter 1
Introduction
In the last decades the transport sector has experienced a growth why roads become
more and more congested, the NOx and CO2 emissions increase with the result of
poor air quality, human health problems and poor welfare. Therefore the focus on
sustainable transportation modes has been sharpened. The combination between
large amount of goods or passenger makes a train to such a mode.
Throughout the last century the Danish railway has shrunk, and so has its rail
freight services. With the competition from trucks, the Danish railway system has for
many years been shaped to serve passengers. But, with the opening of the ¨Oresund
Fixed Link in 2000, and the Great Belt Fixed Link in 1996, the country has observed
an increase in rail freight traﬃc. However, the traﬃc is primarily transit between
Central Europe and the rest of Scandinavia. Many European countries has a similar
story and it is only few countries where the rail freight service are more dominant
than passenger services, see Figure 1.1. From the map it is very clear that Denmark
is very much focused on the passenger transport in spite of the rail freight. The
shape and small size does make it diﬃcult to put up a feasible railway business. In
the recent years more rail freight services have actually been established despite this,
and seem that they found a solution that competitive with the corresponding truck
solution.
The general infrastructure in Europe is ﬁlled with bottlenecks that create a poor
service level. This is very related to the fact that European countries have had their
own agenda for developing the railway, which have resulted in a mix of diﬀerent
signaling systems, various electriﬁcation types, each country with their own set of
rules and some places various track gauge.

1.1
Source criticism
The report is based on a review of scientiﬁc papers published in diﬀerent trans-
port journals, conference proceedings from Traﬁkdage i Aalborg, conference slides
from The Danish Railway Association’s Annual Railway Conference and technical
reports from consultants and authorities. The scientiﬁc papers are mainly used
in the investigations of the development in Europe and the impacts of the railway
reforms. Furthermore, are they used for identifying the international experiences
with generating rail freight services. The proceedings from Traﬁkdage i Aalborg have
been available on the conference homepage. The conference is an annual conference
in Aalborg, with attendance from both consultants, authorities and academics. The
papers are mainly written in Danish. The conference slides from The Danish Railway
Association’s Annual Railway Conference are primarily used to get an overview of
some issues. The conference is typical attended by technical experts like consultants,
and in minor grade academics. At this conference some of the speakers do have their
own agenda, and not present a topic from an objective point of view. The technical
reports are typically analysis of the intermodal terminals and infrastructure elements,
most of those are written in Danish. Besides that two interviews of employees from
Rail Net Denmark have been carried out in order to get some speciﬁc information.
In the thesis are used many statistical data which accessed from Eurostat, Statistics
Denmark, Oﬃce of Rail and Road, the World Bank and historical books. The
data has in general been very limited and very unclear how it has been derived.
Investigating e.g. the track length in the several countries showed a general deviation
between the information provide. This indicates an inconsistent data collection, and
it is very unclear when to use what.

2.1
Political visions
2.1.1
Modern Transport Policy in Denmark
Through the last decades the diﬀerent governments have had a generally positive
policy on promoting rail transport. Although the general scope has been to improve
the passenger services, many of the initiatives that have been implemented also have
inﬂuenced the freight services. Here could be mentioned the Great Belt Fixed Link
and ¨Oresund Fixed Link as two major projects. Although investments in the two
connections have been seen as large and important improvements, the politicians had
for a long period neglected money to maintain the railway network. This induced
some catastrophic years through 2005 and 2006, where teared tracks and poor infras-
tructure caused poor reliability and punctuality measures. This forced the politicians
to grant extra DKK 4.5bn in 2006 and have caused large amounts of track works
since 2007 (Ritzau, 2014).
In 2009 the national parliament of Denmark passed one of the most ambitious
transport agreements for a long time. Through the agreement ”En grøn transport-
politik”’ (Danish Ministry of Transport, 2009) the politicians invested DKK 9.1bn to
improve the transport system. The agreement considered several big investments in

2.1.2
EU Policy - An Age of Railway Reforms and Uniﬁcation
The European Union has since the beginning of the 1990’s aimed for a more eﬃcient
and sustainable transport sector, where rail transport is one of the key modes to
obtain these goals.
Increase of market share compared to road, reduced travel
times and reduction of the regional and local emissions are main objectives for this
transformation. The process was initiated in 1991 by European Council’s acceptance
of directive 91/440/EC concerning liberalization of the railway market (European
Union, 1991). Earlier, most countries had the infrastructure and operations integrated
in one, typically state owned, company by which the level of competition was low,
both within freight and passenger traﬃc. Based on the directive, infrastructure
and operations were vertically separated into two independent companies deﬁned
as ”‘Infrastructure Manager”’ (IM) and ”Railway Undertaking”’(RU) or ”‘railway
companies”’. Performing the separation has been diﬀerent throughout Europe, where
the pioneer countries were Sweden, Germany and Great Britain. That generated
three frameworks that has been the baseline for other European countries. The
Swedish and British railway systems are “fully separated”, which means that the

2.2.1
Femern Belt Fixed Link
The Femern Belt connection is a very widely discussed project, which connects the
German peninsula Fehmarn with the Danish island Lolland with an approximately 20
km long tunnel connection, as displayed in Figure 2.3. The tunnel will be able to serve
both road and rail transportation, in diﬀerent tubes, where the rail alignment and
tunnel conﬁgurations are designed for 200 km/h. In addition to the tunnel, lines in
both Denmark and Germany have to be upgraded for double track, ERTMS- signaling
system, electriﬁcation and 200 km/h as well. The Danish land side upgrades cover
the alignment from Rødby to Ringsted in central Zealand. With an increased speed
of 200 km/h instead of the currently 120-160 km/h the diﬀerence between the speed
of freight trains and passenger trains increases substantial. Heterogeneous traﬃc,
and by this diﬀerent speeds, is capacity consuming, and slow train types have great
impact on the faster. Therefore new sidings are installed at three locations; Glumsø,
Orehoved and Holeby. Orehoved is the landside connection at Storestrømsbroen,
which is a combined road and rail bridge, with two lanes and one track, respectively.
The bridge is the only single track section at the Ringsted-Femern line. In 2011
the Rail Net Denmark registered cracks in the bridge causing close down of traﬃc.
Further calculations concluded that the bridge did not fulﬁl current standards and
regulations for absorbing brake force from freight trains. Following, the Danish
Road Directorate, Rail Net Denmark and Sund & Bælt 2 investigated scenarios and
have got ﬁnance for a bridge, including two tracks, two lanes, bike paths and sidewalks.
The new connection will reduce the distance with approx. 160 km, and by this
the travel time will be reduced considerably and more freight trains will choose the
route via Femern instead of the existing route via Padborg. The Danish Transport
Authorities expect train path for two freight trains per hour through Denmark, one
via Padborg and one via Femern, while the traﬃc in 2027 will be composed of two

The Danish System
The freight market has been through signiﬁcant changes since the ﬁrst modern railway
lines in the 19th century. The following chapter will introduce the development in the
Danish rail freight services in section 3.2, while section 3.3 outlines the organizational
developments, focusing on impact of the European Union directives. Further, section
3.4 outlines some of the important infrastructure elements in Denmark.
3.1
Historical Overview
For many years after the opening of the ﬁrst railway line in Denmark, Copenhagen -
Roskilde in 18471 freight was an important part of the rail service. The following
50-80 years the railway network was extended substantially and entailed easy access
to even very small towns. In this golden age of the Danish railway the competitors
were horse wagons and from 1896 an early edition of lorries. Therefore, the railway
could easily compete on speed and volume. Quickly, the lorries were improved and
became a great threat to the railways. The danish rail freight market share has been
descending since lorries became more eﬃcient during World War II. The lorries won
big market share with its fast and ﬂexible routes and providing the possibility to
move freight in smaller amounts. This was, and still is, a contrast to the railway
which is ﬁxed to the rails which were placed in the late half of the eighteen century,
and can only handle loading processes at a station. Further, the rail freight typically
has its advantage on long distances and a feasible freight service typically requires
hauling of large volumes of freight, which is quite the opposite of the Danish network.

the weight of the commodities was descending in the period from 1950 to 1970. This

operating the terminal is required to fulﬁll “Modtagepligt”, which demands to operate
and treat all railway companies on fair and equal conditions. In this thesis ports are

3.4.2
Ports
The connection between Denmark and other continents goes through either port or
the airport. This section will point out the state of the Danish ports that entail rail
freight access, and focus on two new intermodal terminals. The ports in Denmark are
very divided when it comes to the type of goods handled. Fredericia processes tank
vessels, Aarhus is the largest container port, while bulk vessels arrive at a large number
of ports in Denmark, although Aarhus, Aalborg and Aabenraa have the majority of
the transport (Danish Ministry of Transport, 2005). Furthermore, many power plants
are located next to the sea and have own ports to handle coal or wood pellet deliveries.
One of the big beneﬁts of having a rail freight terminal linked to the port is the fusion
of vessels with large amount of freight from other continents and easy access to trains
that will distribute containers or trailers in the hinterlands. By this the railway
becomes part of a larger transport chain, where the rail share of the costs will aﬀect
the overall price less. Instead of having a typical intermodal terminal that relies on
pre-hauls and post-hauls to nearby companies, the terminal will be served by both
trucks and vessels. The Transport Agreement of 2009 placed money into developing
railway access to ports in Denmark. In Denmark 14 ports have their own rail tracks
(Rail Net Denmark, 2015c), and in the recent years more ports have started focusing
on interaction with rail traﬃc. In fact the majority of the domestic operations in
Denmark have a port as either origin or destination. In 2007-2010 tracks at the port
of Aarhus were renewed (Clausen, 2015b) and transport from the APM Terminal
was improved. Lately operations from the port of Aalborg (Østhavnen) have been
started and a sand-train from the port of Thyborøn will start operating to companies
in Central Jutland. Every Wednesday and Friday operations are scheduled between
the port of Køge and Høje Taastrup Intermodal Terminal. At the port of Esbjerg
(Western Jutland) and the port of Hirtshals (Northern Jutland), mainly have a high
share of trailer transport, hereof mainly RO-RO, and railway terminal would be very
attractive. By this both terminals have designed and constructed new intermodal
facilities.

3.5
Summary of the chapter
The chapter introduced to the development of the freight market in Denmark since
the the 1950’s. It was shown that the railroad despite many attempts to keep market
failed and that the road fast became the principal transport mode. This was mainly
caused by the high ﬂexibility of the trucks in combination with shutdown of several
infeasible railway line. Of the diﬀerent commodity groups the goods hauled today
is manufacturing products, where in 1950 it was bulk commodities. The general
decrease is also a story about the general trend of customer, that required just-in-time
deliveries. Despite the general decrease some companies have still been able to put
up a feasible solution. A good example of this is Carlsberg’s beer-train.
The chapter also investigated the three current terminals, the new terminal in
Esbjerg as well as the upcoming terminal in Hirthals. For the ﬁrst three the general
design and accessibility are major limitation to have cost-eﬀective and time-eﬃcient
solution. In general the strip tracks are to short which require shunt movements
that easily could have been leave out, if the terminals were design properly. The
new terminal in Esbjerg is another of not learning from the previous cases. It is a
very poor design with generally low ﬂexiblity and also are required to have long time
consuming shunting movements.

Reforms, deregulation and liberalization have been synonyms for the railway in the

countries with diﬀerent views on how to operate and regulate the rail.

Investigating the freight market, the liberalization, decentralization and open access

as six companies1. The largest one was the bulk division, “Trainload Freight”,

ﬁve of seven companies coming out of British Rail. The last company Freightliner
was responsible for container traﬃc between ports and hinterland terminals, and was
sold to management buyout (Drew, 2006).

The evaluation criteria for cost-beneﬁt analyzes are normally the Net present
value (NPV), internal rate of return (IRR) and Beneﬁt/Cost rate (B/C-rate). The
NPV is the beneﬁts and costs over time aggregated into one number index value,
as expressed in equation (5.13). The equation indicates if the aggregated beneﬁts
are higher than the aggregated costs the operation is proﬁtable, NPV > 1. In the
simulation model no actual beneﬁts have been computed, meaning that NPV <
0. The denominator in the NPV formula is the so called discount factor, where r

Concept
But, not many companies in Denmark have the same large ﬂow of goods and especially

Chapter 7
Recommendations
In chapter 6 three diﬀerent initiatives were analyzed. It was found that all three
initiatives have some potential in order to generate more freight traﬃc in Denmark.
Some of the initiatives are quite costly and requires cooperation from several other
sectors than the railway sector itself. It is not just one initiative that will generate
more freight, but the interaction between several that will have the largest impact.
The recommendation can therefore be summarized in the following four headliners:
 Set up of a strategic rail freight department
 Generate a smooth interface between modes - updating loading facilities
 Generate an international demand - before focusing on domestic
 Reduce costs of road hauling, when combined with rail transports
Strategic rail freight department
A solution that was not analyzed in the previous chapter was to set up a department
in the Danish Ministry of Transportation, or in the Danish Transport Authorities,
responsible for developing a healthy rail freight system, both in terms of infrastructure
and market competition. A strategic rail freight department should be based on
experts in rail freight service and by this cut oﬀmost of the general poor decisions
that have been made. The main goal for such a department is to create, and carry
out, a Master Plan that provides directions and guidelines for Rail Net Denmark,
freight operators, local authorities and national policy.
One of the action for such a department could be to streamline the rail freight market.
An issue, pointed out in chapter 3, is the market situation, where the principal freight
operator are running fare most of the services in Denmark, and who also operates
two out of three intermodal terminals. As the terminals’ infrastructure are owned by

Rail Net Denmark it is suggested that the terminal operations should be outsourced
to a company, which only focus is to perform terminal operations. This will create a
neutral player at key points which will encourage other rail freight companies to use
the terminal, as they no longer contributes to a competing freight company.
Interface between modes
Re-opening of loading facilities and in general make a more eﬃcient terminal operation,
changes have to be implemented at the terminals. A general concern of all currently
terminals is poor access from the main line to the terminal. Here it is recommended
that each terminal has minimum one strip track with a length of minimum 835 m.
This will reduce access time to the terminal, and derive a more eﬃcient terminal
operation as there will be no shunt movement. Especially the operations at Høje
Taastrup would beneﬁt of a more directly access to the terminal. The terminal
operates mainly trains in relation with areas in Jutland, so it is very ineﬃcient to
have the current access way. For operations at loading facilities the lifting equipment
should follow the train instead of being stagnated to the facility. Hereof the expensive
equipment will be more utilized, and the investments are repaid faster.
Generating a demand
The more eﬃcient operations at the terminals is also a way to encourage transit traﬃc
operators to schedule more stops at the Danish terminals, and by this generating
more goods. The author believes that it is necessary to focus on generating an
international relation, before the domestic rail freight services in Denmark will
attractive. For this it is important to focus on companies with large freight ﬂows
out of Denmark, such as Danfoss, Grundfoss and Rockwool. While freight s import
to Denmark should come companies as Ikea, car manufactures and manufactures
of construction materials. But to encourage Danish companies to import or export
via rail Danish infrastructure must be able reach the companies, in order to keep
encourage potential customers. The Danish rail freight service must in the ﬁrst years
ﬁnd its potion in the freight market as a feeder services to the big larger transport
chains covering several countries.
Locating terminals at ports and close to highway will be an easy way to access to
larger freight ﬂows. But also, the opening and re-opening of loading facilities should
be focused on the larger cities, or areas with close connection to companies. At those
locations companies are located closer to the facility and by this the amount of road
haul is minimized. Clustering of companies, as suggested, is one way to generate
more freight volumes in Denmark. The idea should be contribute to generate more

From chapter 5 and chapter 6 it was found that the pre-hauling and post-hauling had

highly recommended that cabotage transportation should be allowed in connection

Issues not covered
The analysis of the Danish rail freight system did not cover issues such as rolling

Sidings
A siding is a track that is used for passing and overtaking trains, typical installed on
single track lines, but also common on double track lines. For operations in Denmark
a siding is a limitation to the train length at many lines. As mentioned in beginning
of thesis is the maximum train length 835 m, but according to Rail Net Denmark
(2015d) it is rarely that a train can operate through with full length. Even at the
transit corridor there is a limitation to max. train length of 750 m for trains making
a stop in Nyborg. Although since the the maximum train length is 740 m in Germany
and 730 m in Sweden the transit traﬃc will see this as a problem.
At line in Western Jutland several places it is only allowed to have a maximum train
length of 245 m. This is a major limitation, especially if DB Schenker as planned
starts operating sand-trains from Thyborøn to Central Jutland.

Rolling stock concerns the trains and the rail wagons and how those a able to move
around on the Danish network. As most of the Danish lines, except of the transit
corridor, are not electriﬁed the freight operators are required to own diesel locomotives
in order to access other destinations than those on the transit corridor. According

Chapter 9
Conclusion
Throughout the last 20-30 years the railway has been under large organizational
changes. One of the primary reasons is the high competition generated by trucks
since the Second World War. The ﬂexibility of the truck is the primary competitive
advantage across Europe. The European Union has since the beginning of the 1990’s
been focused on achieving higher market shares for the railway. This has been con-
ducted through liberalization of the railway market and creation of interoperability.
The focus in this thesis was to evaluate Danish rail freight services.
The Dan-
ish freight train market has, as the rest of Europe, been a victim for a steadily
increasing market share, in favor of truck traﬃcking. Despite the fact, that there has
been several attempts of adjustments from the rail industry, without any signiﬁcant
impact. There is no doubt that the shutdowns of railway lines, and general freight
facilities along main lines, nationwide in recent years has made it diﬃcult for freight
operators to reach potential customers without including trucks in the transport
chain. The analysis shows that in the Danish system by spring 2015 are a major
lack or poor interchange possibilities between road and rail traﬃc. The existing
intermodal terminals in Denmark (Padborg, Taulov and Høje Taastrup) all have
inadequate strip track lengths, meaning that trains must shunting operations in order
to locate the rail wagon groups next to the lifting equipment. This makes terminal
operations very time consuming and therefore unattractive for transit cargo traﬃc
making a potential stop at the terminal. Two new terminals are designed in Esbjerg
and Hirtshals. The new terminal in Esbjerg is designed with the same mistakes as
the existing intermodal facilities, including very poor access ways into the terminal.
It was found that this solution does not give enough incitement for freight operators
to use the facility. Contrary the design of Hirtshals terminal, where the principals
of simple design and easy access have made the construction costs rather cheap.

==PAGE:132==
9.
Conclusion
The Hirtshals terminal should therefore be seen as a baseline for potentially new
terminals.
The analysis of the system also concluded that current rail freight services are mainly
feasible due to the fact that the truck hauling is very limited, if any at all, and that
terminal operation in at least one end is conducted by the customer itself.
The variations in the rail freight of system in the Netherlands and The United
Kingdom are quite diﬀerent compared to Danish rail system. It was found that
Netherlands primarily operates freight traﬃc from the port in Rotterdam to hin-
terland terminals. Here especially the fairly new Betuweroute from Rotterdam to
Germany was found as an important link to have transport moved to the port by
rail instead of by road. The idea of a pure rail freight line was found very tempting,
but a solution like are only feasible when it is connected with a major port.
The United Kingdom was investigated based on the diﬀerent approach to the rail
reform in Europe. Here the whole railway was privatized in 1996 which had some
minor positive impact in the ﬁrst years, but seen over the whole period the major
impacts did not appear as expected. And with Network Rail becoming a governmen-
tal body, the whole organization reminds of the Danish. Therefore it was concluded
that full privatization has not been a success in UK.
In order to create the most eﬃcient initiatives to impact rail freight services, a
pre analysis were conducted based on a newly developed simulation tool. The simu-
lations showed that with standard conditions, an intermodal system is competitive
with a corresponding road system at rail distances above 631 km, if the train is
hauled by an electric locomotive. If the train was hauled by diesel locomotives it
was competitive with roads above 781 km. It was concluded that such distances
is not available in Denmark, why initiatives needs to be applied locally in order
to minimize this distance. The same analysis was conducted by comparing actual
terminal to terminal operations. It was concluded that by removing the road haulage,
the breakeven distance could be reached at a very short distance of only 30 km and
39 km, for electric and diesel hauled locomotives, respectively. An analyze of how
costs were aﬀected by the diﬀerent elements in the cost-beneﬁt analyze, showed that
pre-haul and post-haul operations were signiﬁcantly inﬂuential elements in the costs
in an intermodal solution.
From the simulation model and conducted analyze of the Danish system, three
initiatives where put up, and included in a recommendation. The recommendation
118
==PAGE:133==
argued that four actions were required in order to have an eﬃcient rail freight system
in Denmark:
 Set up of a strategic rail freight department
 Generate a smooth interface between modes - updating loading facilities
 Generate an international demand - before focusing on domestic
 Reduce costs of road hauling, when combined with rail transports
All recommended actions are interrelated and therefore would the implementation
of one would lead up to implementation of the next. But, there is no doubt that
implementation of cabotage driving in pre-hauls and post-hauls would be the most
eﬃcient solution by itself. The analysis has shown that a freight connection between
Aalborg and Høje Taastrup, with electric locomotives will have the same costs as an
equivalent road solution if cost per km is decreased from DKK 32 to 20 DKK. Such
a decrease should be possible if cabotage operations could be implemented.
It can, by the analysis performed be concluded that with the general size and
shape of Denmarks, will be diﬃcult to generate enough rail domestic freight traﬃc
to put up an adequately eﬃcient rail freight solution. Major limitations to the
system are poor terminal designs, lack of loading facilities and a market generally
controlled by one mastodont. In order to generate more rail freight traﬃc, companies
must be clustered to a strategic railway point, and by this reduce the level of road
hauling, but the terminals are also required to be upgraded in order to be adequately
time-eﬀective and cost-eﬀective operations in order to be attractive.
