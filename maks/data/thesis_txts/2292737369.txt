Abstract
This thesis is an approach to broaden the spectrum of possible graphene
devices.
Ab initio calculations are made on graphene sheets connecting orthogonally
in T-junctions. First, periodic structures following the typical zigzag and
armchair directions are examined calculating the relaxed geometries, the
transport properties, and the binding energies when the graphene edges are
passivated with various species. It is found that T-junctions should be stable
and capable of transmitting electrons in all three directions depending on the
speciﬁc conﬁguration in the interface.
As a further exploration of the possible connection between two graphene
sheets, the energies and bond types of graphene nanoribbons connecting
perpendicular to a base graphene sheet have been calculated for a range of
starting conﬁgurations. It is found that the armchair directions are preferable
for the connections.

In 2010 the Nobel Prize in Physics was given for “groundbreaking experiments regarding
the two-dimensional material graphene”[1] to Andre K. Geim and Konstantin S. Novoselov,
who in 2004 isolated graphene for the ﬁrst time. Since its discovery, the amount of
literature about graphene have been growing rapidly, as illustrated in Figure 1.1, showing
the number of articles published each year as found by the article search engine DTU
FindIt. But what is it that is so fascinating about this material, that it has received such
a great interest from the scientiﬁc community?

Graphene is a one atom thick honeycomb-lattice material made entirely of carbon atoms,
see Figure 1.2(a). It was the ﬁrst two-dimensional material to be isolated[2] although
other types, such as MoS2 and hexagonal boron nitride (hBN), have been found later[3, 4].
Its properties are quite remarkable; in 2008 Lee et al. [5] measured the tensile strength
by pushing holes in suspended graphene with an atomic force microscope. The result was
a Young’s modulus of 1.0 × 109 Pa, 500 times more than the approximately 200 × 106 Pa
of steel[6] and the ascertainment that graphene was the strongest material ever to be
measured.
Besides its mechanical qualities, graphene is also characteristic in its distinctive electronic

The band structure of graphene is quite remarkable: the Dirac cones lead to special relativistic
behavior of the electrons, and the charge mobility is very large. Because of the sp2 hybridization
of the atomic orbitals, the band structure is furthermore described surprisingly well by a nearest-
neighbor tight-binding calculation as the one shown here.
Graphene is especially studied for its possible future as the basis for nano-electronics.
A great eﬀort has gone into altering the electric properties of this semi-metal for that
purpose, from changing the chemical composition[9, 10, 11] and stacking layers[12, 13] to
creating nano ribbons[14] and bending them[15].
In the spirit of creating nano-devices, Wang et al. [16] succeeded in creating a one-
dimensional contact between a metal electrode and the edge of a graphene sheet, by
encapsulating the graphene in a stack of hBN. The entire stack was etched resulting in

such a junction is shown in Figure 1.4(b).

to calculate the bonding properties between the graphene layers as well as the electrical
transmission through the three terminal system.

2.1
Computational methods
The main computational technique used in this project is the broadly used Density Func-
tional Theory (DFT) method. In the following, it is sought to explain the fundamentals
of DFT and afterwards the modiﬁcations to include Green’s functions for transmission
calculations. The software used is the SIESTA package and the TranSIESTA extension
for the transport.
2.1.1
Density Functional Theory
The following section draws on many sources, but in particular the DFT introduction in
Kohanoﬀ[17].
In one sense, quantum mechanics is easy. We know the governing Schrödinger equation,
all we have to do is solve it. This, however, turns out to be an almost impossible task as
soon as the systems of interest consists of more than a few atoms. Since the positions
of all electrons are correlated, we cannot just solve a system of N functions of three
variables, but have to solve the much more complex many-electron equation. However,
due to the work of Hohenberg, Kohn and Sham[18, 19], there is a way to simplify the
problem considerably.
But ﬁrst some initial assumptions to make the problem easier.

2.1.1.4
Various concepts in DFT
Following is a brief introduction to a collection of diﬀerent parameters and concepts
relevant in DFT calculations.
Basis sets: It is always necessary to have some kind of basis in which you represent the
Hamiltonian. There are three main ways to handle this and they are as follows:
Plane waves: This method is essentially a Fourier representation of the electron
density. The basis set is truncated at some energy level, in that way ignoring
density changes of very high frequencies. One of the advantages is the non-
ambiguous way to enhance the basis - simply choose a higher cutoﬀenergy.
Besides that, the basis functions are naturally orthogonal and the construction
implies some kind of periodicity, i.e. it is a natural choice for crystal structure
calculations. This approach is utilized in for instance the VASP and Dacapo
codes.
Grid representation: Another method is to directly represent the electron den-
sity on a real space grid. This is the method originally implemented in the
GPAW code and it has the advantages, that, like the PW method, convergence
is easily controlled by a single parameter. In this case the density of real space
grid points. Besides that, the grid representation eﬃciently parallelizes.
LCAO: The Linear Combination of Atomic Orbitals is in some ways the most
natural way to represent the electrons. The idea is to use functions located on
==PAGE:13==
CHAPTER 2. THEORY
9
the single atoms and spreading spatially from there. Instantly we see how this
method in its construction includes a decent start-guess on the ﬁnal electron
density.
Two methods are generally used. Gaussian functions that have the advantage,
that the overlap is very easily calculated, and are implemented in for exam-
ple GAUSSIAN; and atomic-like orbitals that looks like the normal atomic
shells. The latter method is the technique implemented in SIESTA and used
throughout this thesis. LCAO is capable of giving good results with a very
small basis set.
As we will see in the following section on transport calculations, a localized
basis set is necessary to use the Green’s functions approach for ﬁnding the
transmission through systems.
Basis size: As described above, the grid and PW basis sets simply extend their precision
with a single parameter. The atomic-like basis in SIESTA is however a bit more
complicated. The minimal basis set is called Single Zeta (sz) and consists of one
radial function per orbital within the valence electrons. I.e. the basis functions are
written as
Φnlm(⃗r) = ϕnl(r)Ylm(⃗r) ,
(2.16)
where ϕnl(r) is a numerical radial function, and Ylm(⃗r) is a spherical harmonic
function. Now it is possible to expand the basis in two ways. One can include
more radial functions per angular momentum leading to Double and Triple Zeta
or one can include functions for angular momenta that are not part of the ﬁlled
atomic orbitals. The latter expansion is called polarized functions. A DZP (Double
Zeta Polarized) basis set will include two radial functions for each valence state
and an additional polarized function for a larger energy shell. For carbon, that
has the electronic conﬁguration 2s2 2p2, this will result in two s-like orbitals, six
p-like orbitals (two times px, py, pz), and ﬁve d-like orbitals for the next angular
momentum. In total 13 functions per atom. In general, this basis size is believed
to be large enough to give accurate results[21].
Pseudopotentials and pseudowavefunctions: As already implied, we are mainly
interested in the behavior of the valance electrons as the core electrons are bound
tightly to the nucleus and thus contributes minimally to the interesting properties.
It is, however, of great importance, that the core electrons are treated in some way
and not entirely forgotten. This is done through pseudopotentials and pseudowave-
functions. The functions are smoothed across the region close to the nucleus in a
way such that the overall properties are conserved. The pseudopotentials used in
SIESTA is of the so called norm-conserving kind[22].
k-points: Many quantities in DFT are found by integration over the Brillouin zone. In
practice, this is done by summing over a set of points in k-space. As k-space is

Exchange-correlation functional: As already mentioned, the xc-functional is of great

as that particular point.

2.1.2.3
List of methods and techniques
The following is a small list with notes on various methods used.
TranSIESTA: For the relaxation of atomic structures the DFT code SIESTA was used
employing the methods described in the DFT section. After the minima were found,
semi-inﬁnite electrodes were added to the structures using the TranSIESTA package.
The software uses Green’s functions techniques to calculate the eﬀect of the leads
via self-energies using the Rubio-Sancho method, and ﬁnd the Hamiltonian. The
SIESTA calculations are periodic in all directions, while the TranSIESTA ones are
periodic in the directions perpendicular to the transmission direction(s). In practice,
the electrodes are calculated in separate TranSIESTA calculations where the correct
bulk Hamiltonian and the self-energies are found. These lead-Hamiltonians are
then substituted into the SIESTA Hamiltonian and the entire system is updated
while ﬁxing the part including the leads.
TBtrans: After the Hamiltonian have been found with TranSIESTA, TBtrans can be
used to calculate the actual transmission spectrum. As input, it needs to know
the size of the device region, which should be chosen as small as possible, while
ensuring that there is no connection across the region. This is essentially utilizing
the block matrix structure introduced earlier. Besides the transmission, TBtrans
can return the DOS and the LDOS on speciﬁc atoms and orbitals and any other
information that can be derived from the Green’s function.
k-points (again): As in DFT, it is important to include a sampling of the k-space
when calculating the transmission. As the structures in this step are only periodic
transversely to the transport directions, this is the only direction where k-points are
needed. The transmission is more sensitive to the k-point sampling than the energy
and therefore, there are always more k-points in the transmission calculations.
Buﬀer atoms: When the leads are introduced in TranSIESTA, it is important to have
the correct coupling between the existing structure and the leads. The leads are
placed by substituting some of the existing atoms, and since the leads are inﬁnite,
the coupling should be like the coupling to something bulk-like beforehand. This is
ensured by placing atoms on the outside of what will become the electrode in the
SIESTA calculations. A sketch of the setup can be seen later on Figure 3.17. For a
two terminal setup, there is rarely a need for buﬀer atoms, as the structures often
are periodic and thus have a bulk-like behaviour from SIESTA. The problem arises
with the third terminal in our systems as this structure does not have a periodicity.
Hence the need for the buﬀer atoms.
Sancho Rubio method: In a semi-inﬁnite chain the self-energy at the end describes
the eﬀect of the chain on the end atom. The method to calculate this by Sancho,
Sancho and Rubio[29] works by iteratively removing every other atom in the chain
and correcting the couplings and energies on the remaining ones. It is clear that
==PAGE:22==
CHAPTER 2. THEORY
18
the coupling between these next-to-neighbor sites will eventually go to zero and the
self-energy is found. It need not be interaction between individual atoms; the chain
can consist of cells with many atoms interacting only with the neighboring cells.

3.2.1.3
Passivation of edges
If this device is to be fabricated, it will most likely involve some kind of etch as in
Wang et al. [16]. In the paper, Wang et al. suggests that the graphene edges might be
passivated with H, F or O from the etch; they further treat the edges with an O2 plasma
to change chemical composition as done in Bittencourt et al. [31] and Felten et al. [32].
With this maneuver, the goal is to get only oxygen on the edges and therefore they only
examine this case in their DFT calculations.

relax all the structures. In practice the structures relaxed with the szp basis set were
copied, the bottom atoms of the arm were changed, and the arm was shortened to only
two unit cells. The new structure was relaxed further, also with the szp basis. In the
cases where no connection was made, the entire arm was moved 0.4 Å closer to the sheet,
and the calculation was run again.

Ghosts and overbinding
A problem with localized basis sets is that the description
of a system is dependent on the position of the atoms in a way that enlarges the binding
energies. The eﬀect is called basis set superposition error. Imagine a lonely atom described
by a localized basis set. If we add another set of basis functions next to it, but without
adding an actual atom, we would expect that the calculated energy gets lower, simply by
the fact that more parameters are included in the calculation. This is somewhat similar
to choosing a larger basis set. Because of this, comparing energies for the connected and
unconnected structures is not just a matter of taking the diﬀerence. What one usually
==PAGE:33==
CHAPTER 3. COMPUTATIONS AND RESULTS
29
does is to add for instance the base graphene layer as ghost atoms, i.e. just adding the
basis functions and then comparing the energies for those calculations. We have not used
ghost atoms in these calculations, but since the eﬀect overbinds, it would most likely
result in even less likelihood that the oxygen and nitrogen passivated structure would
bind.
It would be interesting for further studies to look at the eﬀect of single passivating atoms
between a longer streak of pure carbon atoms.
3.2.2
Transport calculations
Using the equilibrium Green’s functions method described in the theory section (2.1.2) the
transmission through these three-terminal setups have been calculated. In the base, the
electrodes have been inserted in the part that was ﬁxed during relaxation, and in the arm
the approximately 14 outermost atoms were removed as buﬀer, and the electrodes was
substituted into the next atoms. This buﬀer setup ensures a more bulk-like Hamiltonian
in the electrode region. In all the calculations, 100 k-points were used in the transverse
direction.
In case of the passivated structures where the arm was very short, the carbon atoms in
the arm were removed and nine unit cells (36 atoms) were inserted at the same angle
instead. The outermost 12 atoms were treated as buﬀer (and hydrogen passivated at
the end), the following 12 atoms were ﬁxed, and the remaining 12 carbon atoms that
connects to the passivation species were allowed to relax. See Figure 3.17 for a visual
overview.
Continuing from the geometry section, all transmissions are calculated with the szp basis
set, unless otherwise noted.
As done with the relaxation, the transmission results with diﬀerent basis sets have also
been compared. As in Figure 3.12 the polarized single zeta basis sets generally gives
results identical to the full dzp basis while the smallest basis lacks some of the features.
Especially the electron-hole asymmetry in 3.12(a) is missing from the sz calculation. The
transmissions through the other structures with other basis sets are shown in Appendix A.
In Figure 3.13(a) are shown all three directions in the simple zigzag setup. The structural
symmetry is directly reﬂected in the fact, that left↔mid and mid↔right transmission are
identical. Surprisingly, it turns out that there is a larger probability for electrons moving
from the base sheet up into the intersecting sheet, than to continue in the left↔right

The same is true for Fig. 3.13(d), but for the mid-right direction.

3.3
Ribbons on sheets
To study the T connection between two sheets of graphene for diﬀerent directions than
the two cardinal ones, we have looked at graphene nano ribbons connecting to an inﬁnite
periodic graphene sheet in various ways. Two ribbons were chosen, a zigzag and an
armchair. They are shown as the short ones in Figure 3.19. These ribbons were placed
with the bottom right carbon atom one bond-length above a ten-by-ﬁve unit cell graphene
sheet. The starting placements were chosen as the three high-symmetry sites, on-top,
bridge, and hexagon, as illustrated in Figure 3.20. For each starting site, the ribbons
were rotated in steps of ﬁve degrees within the angles also shown in the ﬁgure. All of
these in total 39 setups per ribbon were relaxed with single zeta basis set. The smaller
basis set was chosen since it is computationally cheaper and the results from the cardinal
directions indicated that the geometries were pretty accurate when compared to the
larger basis sets. Pictures of all the relaxed ribbon structures can be seen in Appendix B.
3.3.1
Energies, directions, and bonds
The energies for all the relaxed structure are shown as a function of starting site and
angle in Fig. 3.22(a) and (c). The polar plots shown alongside display the mean energy
of the three ribbons started at each angle. For angles above 30◦where ribbons have not
been calculated for all sites, the results are transformed using the symmetries in the
setup such that there are always three values per angle.
Below the blue energy curve, a density map of the atoms in the relaxed structures is
shown. I.e. the bottom two rows of atoms in each ribbon (including rotated and mirrored
ribbons to account for the symmetries) were translated to have their outermost atom
in (0,0). The coordinates were all projected down to the plane and a Gaussian Kernel
Density plot was made of the points. The bandwidth (variance) of the Gaussian functions
was chosen to give a visually pleasing result. All in all, the density map is darker where
there is a larger probability of ﬁnding an atom, and thus, it indicates the direction of the
relaxed ribbons.
For the zigzag ribbon, the result is very much as one would expect. The energy is lowest
at 0 and 60 degrees corresponding to the zigzag (shifted) cardinal direction structures.
Looking at the density map, it is a bit spread out, but it is clear that there are fewer
relaxed structures around 30◦and 90◦. The results from the armchair ribbon (Fig. 3.22(d))

thorough examination reveals, that the high energy contributions to the mean comes

4
Conclusion and Outlook
The concept of graphene T-junctions have been examined in multiple ways. Using Density
Functional Theory it is found that connections should be possible when the graphene
edges consists of pure carbon. However, if the edges are passivated with H, N, O or F
that can come from etching, it is not energetically favourable to create a bond between
the base graphene sheet and the orthogonal sheet. Scanning a range of starting positions
and angles, the preferred directions for ribbons bonding to a sheet are found. For the
relatively narrow ribbons used, it is seen that both the zigzag and the armchair types
have the lowest energies along the armchair direction in the base layer.
Using Green’s functions methods, the transmissions through these three-terminal systems
were calculated and it was discovered that transport between all three electrodes is
possible, depending on the type of connection in the junction.
Many things are still left to look into. It could be interesting to look at the eﬀect of
wider ribbons in diﬀerent directions, and inﬁnite sheets at the angles where periodic
boundary conditions are possible to see if the results converge towards each other. Also
passivation on only a subset of the atoms in the junction and the eﬀect of edges not
following the simple zigzag or armchair directions. Another important thing to look into,
is the inclusion of hexagonal boron nitride layers in the calculations, to ﬁnd what changes
that might bring to the bonding capabilities.
The eﬀect of a potential on the diﬀerent leads using non-equilibrium Green’s functions
methods could answer the intriguing question of whether this structure could be gated
to function as a transistor.
Besides these computational outlooks, an experimental realization as proposed would be
a very interesting way forward.
