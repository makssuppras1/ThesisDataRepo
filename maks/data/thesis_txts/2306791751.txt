Abstract
Nanoparticles are becoming a prominent ﬁeld of research and development in the modern
world. However due to their size on the order of magnitude of 100 nm or less, it is difﬁcult
and costly to evaluate their quality, composition and physical properties using classical
techniques. Micro- and nanoresonators offer a compact and relatively cheap alternative to
these methods. Strings and cantilevers have been the state of the art in the past, but those
are limited in terms of number of possible modes to measure on. The devices described in
this report consist of perforated silicon nitride membranes that were designed, fabricated in
a clean room environment and measured upon after theoretical calculations and ﬁnite ele-
ment method simulations using COMSOL of its expected performances. The project aims
to achieve a proof of concept that the device can be used reliably to conduct photother-
mal infrared spectroscopy of nanoparticulated samples with magnetomotive actuation and
readout. The theoretical and simulated values are in good agreement with each other, and
even though the fabricated devices exhibit lower eigenfrequencies than expected, it is in-
deed capable of performing photothermal IR spectroscopy on indomethacin particles, as
the generated spectrum is in agreement with Fourier transform infrared data.

Chapter1
Introduction
As more and more technologies are able to scale down due to advancements in man-
ufacturing, the use of the word ”nanotechnology” becomes increasingly prominent in ev-
eryday life. Whether it is regarding electronics, biomedicine; whether it is regarded as a
tremendous leap forward or a potential health hazard, it seems as though everything from
cars to phones to toothpaste now include ”nanotechnology”.
In particular, we are now able to produce nanoparticles: manufactured particles on
the nanometer scale that are of interest either because of their high surface-to-mass ra-
tio (in catalysis, for example) or simply because of their size or material properties. In
biomedical applications, for example, one can imagine using a decided type of nanopar-
ticles as tags for cells or molecules. One could also imagine adding particles of a harder
material to a polymer in order to increase reinforcement.
As in any manufacturing process though, there needs to be a way to investigate
the ﬁnished product’s properties. There is also a need for ultrasensitive detectors that can
monitor the aerial concentration of potentially hazardous particles. Hence, particle sensors
are a ﬁeld of great interest in today’s micro-and nanotechnological research. Traditional
methods used to study particles on such small scales include XPS (X-ray photoelectron
spectroscopy), FTIR (Fourier-transform infrared spectroscopy), ISS (ion scattering spec-
troscopy), and many more. However these methods, although extremely accurate, are
often slow and expensive to use, partly due to the fact that some of them require UHV
(Ultra-high vacuum) to function properly.
Micro- and nanoresonators offer an exciting alternative to these methods. In essence,
a micro-resonator is a vibrating device such as a cantilever, string or membrane with fea-
tures in the micrometer range. Due to the small size of these devices, any particle sampled
on it will have a great impact on the resonator’s behaviour either due to the particle’s mass
or other physical properties, depending on the device’s working principle. Over the last 20
years, interest has risen in this ﬁeld, as shown in ﬁg. 1.1 and different types of devices are

1.1
Particle detection
Particle detection using micro- or nanoresonators is being done with different meth-
ods for actuation and readout. The general state of the art though in terms of accuracy is to
use a vibrometer to measure the resonator’s detuning, whether it is actuated using a piezo-
electric device or other methods, such as magnetomotive actuation. These techniques are
well suited for applications such as mass spectrometry but the vibrometer is an expensive
and cumbersome machine. This is why this device’s concept aims to use another method
for particle analysis, namely photothermal IR spectroscopy.
1.2
Motivation and objectives
In the Nanoprobes group at DTU Nanotech, several group members have already
worked on particle detection using micro- and nanoresonators. Photothermal IR spec-
troscopy has been done using strings (Yamada et al., 2013) but has never been done on
membranes. I was interested in this project because of its highly experimental nature and
because it is would be mostly hands-on. I have previously had experience using the clean
room facility at DTU, amongst other times for my Bachelor thesis, where I developed a

Scientiﬁc approach
In order to accomplish this task in a structured way, I will perform some theoretical

spectroscopy on the particle-sampled devices.

Chapter2
Theory
This chapter will introduce the basic theory applied to the device developed in this
project. Membranes dynamics, infrared spectroscopy and magnetomotive actuation are
explained, as those are the three fundamental working principles utilized by a membrane
resonator performing photothermal spectroscopy of nanoparticulated samples.
2.1
Membrane dynamics
The understanding of the membranes dynamics is a crucial point prior to the de-
vices’ fabrication and use in order to obtain an opinion on the required dimensions and
performance of the ﬁnal device. One of the ﬁrst parameters to deﬁne is the eigenfrequen-
cies of a bare square membrane. When particles will land on the membrane’s surface, a
shift in the eigenfrequency at which the membrane is driven will be observed, due to the
extra mass on the resonator. The shift is linked to the type and quantity of the particle
species, it is therefore essential to estimate the most accurate value before applying the
particles on the surface. To allow an air ﬂow through the membrane during the deposition
of the particle, the design of the vibrating membrane features perforations across the sur-
face. However, for the purpose of the analytical approach the membrane has been assumed
isotropic and continuous. Indeed, previous academic work (Pedersen et al., 1995) as well
as internal communication has led to the conclusion that it is unlikely that the ﬁnished per-
forated devices will have a behaviour very different from non-perforated, ﬂat membranes.
As such, in order to calculate their eigenfrequencies, an isotropic continuous membrane is
assumed. This enables us to use a simpliﬁed expression for the fundamental frequencies,
derived from the differential equation of Kirchhoff-Love plate theory, and leading to the
following solution of the resonance frequency in [rad/s] (Lavrinenko, 2014).

2.2
Magnetomotive actuation and readout
The membranes will be actuated using a method called magnetomotive actuation
(Venstra et al., 2009). The basic working principle for this is that the membrane is put
inside a magnetic ﬁeld, and a current is passed through the chip perpendicularly to the
magnetic ﬁeld. The ﬁeld produced by the current interacts with the static ﬁeld to produce
a magnetomotive force which is based on the Lorentz force equation (Ekinci, 2005). Thus,
my membranes need to feature one conductive path to allow AC current to pass through.
The signal is also read from the chip by utilizing an electromotive force. A second
path on the membrane moves in the static magnetic ﬁeld, as it is on the membrane and
the membrane is undergoing actuation. By measuring on this secondary path’s current
induced by the vibration, we can read the amplitude of the membrane’s displacement.

2.3 Photothermal IR spectroscopy
2.3
Photothermal IR spectroscopy
The method used for characterising is called photothermal infrared spectroscopy.
In essence, an infrared source is directed at the devices and its heating power makes the
membrane expand, leading to a reduction in tensile stress, which is reﬂected as a change
in the resonance frequency of the device. When molecular species are sampled on the
membrane surface, they are excited into vibration modes by absorption of the infrared
photons, causing photothermal heating. The sample’s energy is then transferred to the
resonator through vibrational energy relaxation, which again induces heat in the membrane
and causes detuning (Yamada (2016), Kim et al. (2013)).

Chapter3
COMSOL simulations
In order to verify my theoretical values, which are obtained using an approximated
formula for square membrane dynamics, I have use the ﬁnite element modeling (FEM)
simulation program COMSOL 5.2 to create models of both membrane dimensions (1000
x1000 µm and 500x500 µm). After simulating the natural eigenfrequencies of the mem-
branes, I added a heating element to simulate the frequency detuning due to infrared heat-
ing that the actual devices will experience.

use 2D geometry in a 3D environment, so the thickness of the membrane does not need

simulate displacement out of plane (Z-axis). The membrane simply consists of squares
with dimensions 1000x1000 µm or 500x500 µm. The selected material is Silicon Nitride

Membrane heating
To simulate the expected shift in eigenfrequency caused by an incident infrared ray,
I modelled the incident ray as a small circle of radius 5 µm, which is the expected QCL
spot size, in the center of the square membrane. I placed it there because ideally, I will
try to center the incident laser beam as much as possible. I then used the heat transfer in
thin shells module from COMSOL, as it applies the same principle of 2D modelling in a
3D environment. Besides the initial conditions, I added a temperature of 293.15 K at the
edges of the membrane and a heat source in the aforementioned circle.
In order to get COMSOL to simulate the effect of the heat source on the membrane’s
eigenfrequencies, I had to add two stationary study steps before the eigenfrequency step.
The ﬁrst study would only take into account the heat transfer in thin shells physics. The
second stationary step and the eigenfrequency step only take into account the membrane
physics. I then entered different values in the power of the heat source in the expected
range the QCL would exert, and simulated the eigenfrequencies. The QCL manufacturer
indicates an average power between 0.5 and 15 mW, though with a maximum peak power
of 150 mW.
Although there was a decrease in eigenfrequency, the changes were much smaller
than expected in the average power range (approximately 20 Hz less per mW). However,
at the expected peak power of 150 mW, the shift was of about 2 kHz, which is much
more substantial. Considering the linear behaviour observed in the average power range
simulations, I plotted the decrease in eigenfrequency for the ﬁrst mode as a function of
heating power in ﬁgure 3.5.

Design
In this section I will showcase my device designs, explaining my reasoning behind
the different designs and how they are expected to perform. A sheet covering the naming
convention can be found at the end of the process ﬂow document in appendix.
The ﬁrst series of devices, simply named A series, consisted in simple square mem-
branes where the perforations covered the entire membrane. There are two membrane
sizes, 1000x1000 µm and 500x500 µm. Each size then has two perforation possibilities,
1 or 2 µm perforations. The perforations are evenly spaced, meaning there is 1 µm of
material around each 1 µm perforation and 2 µm of material around each 2 µm perfora-
tion. These devices do not feature gold paths for actuation or readout, they are intended to
be used as a benchmark to measure the eigenfrequencies of the different dimensions and
study the effects of the perforation.
As explained in the theory section, the working principle behind the actual devices
(i.e. the ones with metal) is magnetomotive actuation, where a current passes through the
device as it is submitted to a transverse magnetic ﬁeld, which induces a magnetomotive
force, thereby actuating the membrane. As such, some metal paths have to cross the mem-
brane to allow a current to ﬂow. I had to ﬁnd a compromise in terms of size and amount of
metal present on the membrane: if there is for example only one slim metal track crossing
the membrane, it could be enough to actuate it but difﬁcult to readout. However if there
was too much metal on the membrane it would lose its ﬂexibility and could warp with
Joule heating. So, I ﬁgured the best way to accomplish magnetomotive and accurate read-
outs was to have multiple tracks in different conﬁgurations.
The ﬁrst conﬁguration is the B series. The membrane looks the same as for the A
series, but it features three horizontal metal paths that can be used for actuation or readout.
For devices with 1 µm perforations, the paths are 5 µm wide, and for 2 µm perforations,
the paths are 10 µm wide. This will also apply to the other devices with metal paths. This
design should be the most robust and straightforward as I expect the actuation path to be-
have like a string, which would then transmit its movement to the rest of the membrane.

Fabrication
In this section I show the clean room fabrication process steps I did in order to
fabricate my resonators. The facility in which I did the fabrication is the Danchip class
10-100 cleanroom on the DTU Lyngby campus. The process ﬂow document used during
the fabrication itself can be found in appendix. The wafers that were used are 350 µm
4-inch silicon wafer, polished on both sides as I would have to align on both sides of the
wafer for photolithography.
5.1
LPCVD Silicon Nitride deposition
The perforated membranes are made from silicon-rich, low-stress silicon nitride,
which I made by ﬁrstly depositing a 100 nm layer using a Low Pressure Chemical Vapour
Deposition (LPCVD) furnace. I chose this method because it produces a much more uni-
form and high-quality nitride than other deposition such as Plasma-Enhanced Chemical
Vapour Deposition (PECVD). There is another recipe called stoichiometric nitride depo-
sition, but the nitride ﬁlms from that recipe have a much higher native tensile stress ( 1100
MPa compared to 230 for low-stress). Therefore, to lower the risk of crack on those es-
pecially large membranes I decided to grow a low-stress nitride. The membranes are also
to be freed from the underlying silicon substrate using a wet KOH etch that routinely lasts
over 5 hours, and even though this chemical etches high-quality nitride at an extremely
slow rate, it is still aggressive and could have damaged membranes with higher stress. A
higher stress would have increased the sensitivity of the devices, but on the grounds cited
above I chose to put yield before performance.
To deposit a layer of silicon nitride, the wafers are put in a quartz boat and driven
in the quartz tube, as shown in ﬁgure 5.1. The deposition is performed in four steps: gas
transport to the surface of the wafers - adsorption of the gases on the surface - reaction
of surface reactants (which does not involve the substrate atoms) - and ﬁnally desorp-
tion of the byproducts (here, HCl and H2). The standard recipe for the low-stress nitride
called ”LOWSTR” is selected, and the furnace is then pumped down to a pressure of 120
15
==PAGE:25==
Chapter 5. Fabrication
Figure 5.1: A schematic drawing of a LPCVD furnace (Curley et al., ret. 2016)
mTorr. The deposition happens at a temperature of 830-845◦C, at a rate of approximately
5 nm/min. The chemical reaction that takes place at the surface is:
3 SiH2Cl2 + 4 NH3 ! Si3N4 + 6 HCl + 6 H2
In effect, the wafers are covered on both sides with a thin ﬁlm of silicon nitride, the
width of which is determined by the deposition time. In the case of this project, I required
a 100 nm thickness, which means a deposition time of 20 minutes. After deposition,
the wafers cool down and are taken out to a proﬁlemeter to measure the thickness of the
deposited nitride. The measured thickness turned out to be slightly more than expected,
at 110 nm, but this 10% increase is of little importance to the behaviour of the device,
considering how large the other dimensions are compartatively.
5.2
Photolithography 1 and ICP Silicon Nitride etch
Now that the wafers are covered in a silicon nitride layer that I can work with, the
perforated membranes themselves are to be etched out in the nitride. In order to do so,
I used a very standard photolithography method to imprint the mask details in a layer of
photoresist, which would itself serve as a mask for the nitride Inductively Coupled Plasma
(ICP) dry etch.
Photolithography is a very common and robust process used in micro- and nanofab-
rication. The basic working principle consists of three steps, shown in ﬁgure 5.2. First, a
layer of light-sensitive polymer commonly called photoresist or resist for short, is spun on
the wafer in liquid form and is often baked so as to solidify. Then, the resist is exposed
to UV light through a lithography mask, which is commonly made from electron-beam
etched chrome on silica. Due to its chemical nature, the resist will then become soluble
where it is exposed to the UV light (positive resist) or insoluble (negative resist). The
exposed wafer is then developed in a special chemical called a developer, which is often
speciﬁc to the photoresist. For a positive resist, after development, the areas exposed to
the UV light will be free from resist and in my devices’ case, the silicon nitride will be
exposed. Then, one can use a dry etching method to etch the exposed areas.

A radio frequency (RF) electromagnetic ﬁeld is applied to the bottom plate, where
the wafer is lying, which initiates plasma by ionizing the gas in the chamber. The gases

that is supplied for the creation of the plasma is produced by electromagnetic induction

more ions to etch with, resulting in lower processing times.

5.6
KOH etch and PECVD Silicon Nitride strip in BHF
KOH etching of silicon is, like the photolithography I described above, a very stan-
dard and frequent microfabrication process, especially when fabricating resonators, as de-
vices need to be released from the underlying silicon substrate. It is an isotropic process,
which means KOH etches silicon at an angle of 54.7◦, as shown in ﬁgure 5.9.
Figure 5.9: A schematic drawing of the isotropic KOH etch of silicon (Inductiveload, 2010)
Again, due to the presence of metal on my wafers, I was not allowed to use the KOH
baths with pre-made KOh solution in them. Instead, I had to make my own solution by
mixing 28% KOH pellets in water in a large beaker in a fumehood. The mixture had then
to be brought to 80◦C, as that is the temperature at which the etch rate is most often known
to be around 1 µmm/min. However, in order to be sure I would not over- or under-etch,
I took the wafers out regularly to measure the etch proﬁle, which resulted in the wafers
staying in the KOH beaker for 6 hours. After those 6 hours, I did notice a bit of gold
damage in some areas of the wafers, which could be due to the uneven PECVD nitride
deposition. Nevertheless most of the devices seemed functional.
Finally, it was time to get rid of the PECVD nitride and dice the chips from the
wafers. In order to do so, the nitride is dissolved in a 8% BHF solution. This took around
10 minutes and was also done in a fumehood bath. Once the PECVD nitride was gone
(which one can see with the naked eye, once the color of the wafers revert to the color of
the LPCVD nitride lying underneath), the chips were diced and taken out of the cleanroom
to be prepared for experiments. This was done by using a diamond-tipped pen to outline
the chips and pressing on the wafer until it makes a clean break following the line. This is
known as mechanical cleaving and is possible thanks to the crystalline nature of silicon.

actuate the chip and read the signal out using a vibrometer. The chip is simply stuck alone
(without any PCB) on the piezo using a carbon pad. The vibrometer that was used is a
Polytec MSA-500 and works by emitting a laser downwards towards the actuated device
and measuring the time interval until the reﬂection returns. This way, the vibrational
frequency of the device can be measured.
6.2.3
Lock-in ampliﬁer
In order to consistently actuate at a chosen eigenfrequency, a lock-in ampliﬁer is
used. In essence, a lock-in ampliﬁer is a homodyne detector (device which detects frequency-
modulated radiation) followed by a low-pass ﬁlter, which passes signals with a frequency
lower than a certain cutoff frequency and attenuates signals with frequencies higher than
the cutoff frequency. The ampliﬁer I used is a Zurich Instruments HF2LI, shown in ﬁgure
6.7.
Figure 6.7: The middle equipment was used, a Zurich Instruments HF2LI lock-in ampliﬁer

By sweeping over the expected eigenfrequency, I see a resonance peak. I can then
lock onto that peak using a phase loop lock (PLL), which will make the ampliﬁer send a
signal that actuates the membrane at the selected frequency. I can monitor the phase error
and frequency shift to evaluate if the lock-in is good.

Chapter8
Discussion
The results shown above are a clear sign that the devices I designed and fabricated
work as intended. Unfortunately some designs did not function as expected, most notably
the ones featuring intersecting metal paths. When trying to measure on a C series chip,
it was not possible to subtract the signal used for actuating from the readout signal, but it
may be possible with additional electronics. Moreover, due to a lack of time, some designs
were not tested at all. The F series will most likely exhibit the same issue as the C series,
as it features similar crossing paths. There was unfortunately not enough time to test the
D series, but it would certainly be interesting to ﬁnd out how the corner actuators behave
as opposed to centered ones. As for the G series, it was not the focus of this project to
try and limit stress and strain, as was the idea behind the design. Although the E series
design did give some meaningful data, the B series is clearly superior due to its robustness
and ease of use. The straight parallel paths prevent cross-talk and can provide a more
uniform magnetomotive force. In addition to this, since the particles are scattered all over
the membrane and are not centered on smaller area, aligning the laser so that it effectively
irradiates as many particles as possible is much easier.
Although the devices’ eigenfrequencies in practice had values in the same order of
magnitude as calculated in theory and simulations, there is still a relatively large gap which
can be due to a number of things:
• The theoretical model was very simple as opposed to the actual device. Attempting
to model the perforations as was done by Pedersen et al. (1995) can surely result in
closer values.
• The COMSOL model was also simpliﬁed. Perhaps simulating a small area of the
membrane with perforations and homogenizing that area’s properties to the rest of
the membrane (Stavroulakis et al., 2013) can give a membrane model that replicates
the behaviour of perforated membranes.
• The material properties that were used can of course differ from theory to practice.
The native stress of the nitride that I assumed to be 300 MPa, for example, was only

Chapter 8. Discussion
last measured in 2014 and it may have been different at the time of the fabrication.
This can apply to any of the fabrication steps.
In the design section, I mentioned the devices would feature two different sizes of
perforations. In table 7.1 there is a consistently higher eigenfrequency for the C1 chip,
which features 1 µm, compared to the C2 chip, which features 2 µm perforations. This
would indicate that the size of the perforation does have an inﬂuence on the membranes’
eigenfrequencies. Larger perforations seem more likely to reduce the rigidity of the mem-
brane.
In line with this observation, the proximity effects observed in ﬁgure 5.11 indicate
the perforation structures were deformed. This shows there is room for improvements in
terms of the perforations’ quality. A way to improve that would be to use deep-UV lithog-
raphy, which has a higher resolution in terms of minimum detail size. The drawbacks from
this method are that specializede masks need to be ordered, and at Danchip I would not be
allowed to handle the machine myself. Of course, it is also overall much more expensive
to use, and since I wanted to focus on a fast process, allowing for more batches to be made
if necessary, I ruled out this technique.
The fabrication had a relatively high yield (approximately 60%), but there are some
areas of the process where damage was still sustained and should be improved. For starters,
despite the PECVD nitride deposition, some minor damage, especially to the gold paths,
was sustained. Augmenting the quality of the protective nitride would certainly have a
positive impact on this, though designing my own PECVD recipe would’ve been a long
and arduous process, about which I have unfortunately little knowledge. There are some
specialised holders for protecting one side of a wafer at a time when etching in KOH which
also could have improved the frontside protection. Unfortunately these holders can only
contain one wafer, so for the sake of throughput I did not use them in this fabrication round.
Another way to approach it would be to do the gold lift-off process once the membranes
are released, but this implies using a manual resist spinner that does not hold the wafer
down with vacuum. Indeed, the strong vacuum could potentially break the membranes.
The manual spinning method however is also time-consuming, so for the same reasons as
the frontside protection, it was not tested.
Another step where heavier damage was sustained is when manually cleaving the
chips. Drawing a line across the wafer using a diamond-tipped pen is quite inaccurate,
and as a result of that a fair amount of chips or even wafer sections did not break along
the intended line. This can also easily be improved. First of all, it would be beneﬁcial
if the manual cleaving method was kept, to allow the KOH to etch from the front side at
the lines surrounding the chips, effectively creating a ”pre-cut” wafer. This way, simply
applying pressure to one side of the wafer would break it cleanly across the line. I had also
considered using a saw to cut along the lines, but this implies depositing a thick layer of
resist on the frontside, which serves as protection from particles created by the saw. This
is not ideal when fragile structures such as 100 nm thick membranes are on the frontside
of the wafer.

This study was explorative - not only were the devices’ designs brand new, they
needed to be fabricated and the experimental setup and procedure was not yet in place.
As such, a lot of time was spent on trial and error, for example the C series was initially
the main focus of my investigations. Since I was however unable to get a clearer signal
from the device through signal manipulation, I had to turn to another device design, which
turned out to work much more efﬁciently. Likewise, it took some time to properly set up
the numerous experiments, especially the ones using IR.
Using the different experimental setups available and comparing the data thereof, I
am able to conclude the magnetomotive actuation provides a solid alternative to the use of
piezoelectric devices, and that the use of a vibrometer for monitoring the eigenfrequencies
of the devices can be replaced by a lock-in ampliﬁer. The strong reactions of the devices
when exposed to IR light indicates they are able to conduct photothermal spectroscopy of
nanoparticulated samples with high sensitivity. However, the IR spectrum that was com-
pared to the FTIR spectrum was obtained when measuring upon a device that was saturated
with particles. The next logical step in the development of this device is to try and optimize
sensitivity, so that a clear signal can still be obtained with fewer particles on the membrane.

Conclusion
Over the course of this Master project, I have set out to make a proof of concept
of a microresonator that I designed, fabricated and experimented upon. The membrane
dynamics aspect of the device have been explained and the eigenfrequencies calculated
in theory, simulated using FEM and measured with a piezo and vibrometer method. To
prove that the electromotive actuation and readout method is a valid replacement for this
technology, I have designed and used different experimental setups. The device has been
shown capable of being used to conduct photothermal IR spectroscopy, both with and
without sampled indomethacin particles, and it is reasonable to assume it can be used to
characterize other nanoparticulates. I have gathered the theoretical, simulated and experi-
mental data I had acquired and discussed possible reasons for any discrepancies and have
suggested improvements to the procedures I have followed.
Looking back at the project’s objectives, I have succesfully designed, fabricated and
measured upon a functional perforated membrane microresonator with the ability to per-
form photothermal infrared spectroscopy, which allows for a detailed analysis of nanopar-
ticulate samples. These ﬁndings imply that these membranes are a suitable alternative to
state-of-the-art methods, and provide a cheaper solution which also uses less cumbersome
external equipment.
As aforementioned in the discussion, there is room for improvements on the ﬁrst
generation of devices, both in terms of yield and quality. The potential beneﬁts over the
current generation warrant further research using these designs for nanoparticle analysis.
