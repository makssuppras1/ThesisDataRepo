Abstract 
 
iii 
 
Abstract 
This thesis addresses two matters related to the application of phase change materials (PCMs) in 
buildings – the influence of the ventilation strategy on the performance of PCM enhanced ceiling 
panels, and the resiliency behaviour of such panels in a future climate scenario containing heat 
waves. An experimental analysis of a mixing ventilation (MV) and displacement ventilation (DV) 
strategy was used to assess the indoor thermal performance when combined with the PCM panels. 
The analysis of the operative temperature of three scenarios with identical conditions showed that 
the upper limit of 26 °C in Category II according to EN 16798-1 could not be maintained when 
no ventilation system was active. However, with the application of the ventilation system, 
maximum operative temperatures of 25.9 °C and 25.3 °C were found for the scenarios with MV 
and DV, respectively. Thus, thermal comfort was increased when a ventilation strategy was active 
and DV performed better than MV. Besides, the effect of self-regulation is shown for the PCM 
panels because they were capable of adapting their effective cooling power when higher room 
temperatures occurred.  
Evaluating the resiliency of a validated PCM model to overheating induced by a changing climate, 
this study shows that with the utilisation of thermal mass, a higher level of resistivity and a lower 
level of sensitivity can be achieved compared to a conventional system. An all-air system was used 
as reference but this model showed less ability to withstand the thermal outdoor stress. The thermal 
comfort found for the PCM model was with 49 % of the time within Category II better than the 
all-air model, which had only 33 % in that category. This argues for the potential of PCMs to 
increase the resiliency of buildings while having additional benefits, such as overall increased 
thermal comfort and energy savings.

Introduction

1 Introduction 
Our planet is facing immense global challenges induced by climate change (CC), causing many 
problems worldwide. CC refers to a long-term shift in weather conditions, addressing temperature 
and weather patterns. Global climate is affected by natural and human-induced activities, but since 
the beginning of the industrialisation in the 19th century, human action has been the main driver of 
climatic change [1]. The consequences of CC are tremendous and it causes life on Earth to suffer 
from its impact. Examples of the numerous impacts are global warming, intense drought, 
desertification (land degradation), wildfires, heat waves, water scarcity, storms, melting glaciers, 
rising sea levels, floods, disruption in food production and security as well as change in the 
ecosystem and loss in biodiversity [1234]. Both frequency and severity of these events will increase 
in the future but policy makers around the world set goals to reduce greenhouse gas (GHG) 
emission and even to reverse the process by removing carbon emission from the atmosphere. In 
fact, CO2 and other GHG emissions which are released to the atmosphere, primarily due to burning 
of fossil fuels, are the origin of human-induced climate change [1]. With the Paris Agreement the 
European Union (EU) has settled a legally binding framework for its members to take actions to 
limit global warming to 2 °C above the pre-industrial level. Moreover, the EU has decided to reduce 
its emissions by at least 55 % by the year of 2030 and to create a climate neutral economy with net-
zero GHG emissions by 2050 [5].   
With this context, a closer look should be put on residential and commercial buildings. Most of 
them were built before thermal standards were introduced, making them energy-inefficient and 
responsible for the majority of energy used in the built environment. Indeed, buildings in the EU 
consume 40 % of the energy and release around 36 % of all its GHG emission [6]. National and 
international building regulations address this problem by increasing the thermal envelope of 
buildings with additional insulation and by making them more airtight. This reduces the energy 
used for heating, but, on the flip side, makes them more vulnerable to overheating by extreme 
weather. The rising frequency and intensity of heat waves which are often accompanied by power 
outages have significant consequences on the indoor environment in buildings and their cooling 
systems [7]. Extreme heat can cause several health issues such as heat stroke and exhaustion. 
Especially elderly, very young, and vulnerable people are at high risk to suffer the fatal 
consequences of heat waves [8]. Therefore, it is recommended by experts in the field of building 
performance assessment and indoor climate to make future buildings more resilient against 
disruptive events, specifically against heat waves and power failures. Current buildings and their 
cooling strategies may not be prepared for future challenges induced by the changing climate. Low-
energy cooling solutions that work well today could potentially fail during these disruptive events 
in the next decades [7].  
A possible solution to increase the resilient cooling of buildings and to mitigate the impact of 
disruptive events on the operation of buildings and their indoor climate is by increasing their 
thermal mass with phase change materials (PCM). It is shown that the utilisation of PCM can 
reduce overheating in buildings and shift the cooling demand to outside-peak periods [9]. Also, the 
required capacity of active cooling units such as ventilation system, chiller, fans, etc. can be reduced. 
With their carbon reduction potential, PCMs can actually help solving the root-problem of climate 
change (GHG emissions) and mitigate its impact on the indoor thermal environment. PCM 
application in buildings offers a large potential because they can be implemented in many different 
construction materials, such as concrete, glass fibre, wallboard and insulation materials [10]. They 
are relevant for the refurbishment of existing buildings to reduce energy consumption and heat 
stress during extreme weather conditions [11]. For this purpose, the resiliency of PCM enhanced

Background and Fundamentals

2 Background and Fundamentals 
2.1 Phase Change Materials 
A Phase change material is a latent heat storage substance that absorbs or releases heat during the 
transition from one state of aggregation to another. Their main benefit is the storage of energy in 
form of latent heat which leads to a greater heat storage capacity than conventional building 
materials [12]. The volumetric heat capacity per unit is 5 to 14 times larger than sensible heat storage 
materials [13]. There are three main groups in which PCM can be classified into [14], which are 
shown in Figure 1.  
 
Figure 1: PCM classification [14]. 
The groups are organic, inorganic and eutectic PCM. They can be found for any required melting 
range regardless of the type of application. Organic PCM can generally be characterised as 
chemically stable, non-corrosive and non-toxic, as described by Baetens et al. [12]. They usually 
undergo no supercooling and the thermal conductivity of paraffin compounds is low. Inorganic 
PCMs have generally higher latent heat and thermal conductivity. They are cheap and non-
flammable. However, their drawbacks are corrosivity, supercooling and phase decomposition. 
Eutectics are compositions of two or more components in such a proportion that lowest melting 
temperatures are reached. They have a sharp melting point and a higher volumetric storage density 
than organic PCMs. 
There is a variety of industries where PCMs have been used in, such as medicine, textile, foams, 
slurry, solar energy storage and building materials [15]. The isothermal behaviour of the phase 
change and the high energy storage potential makes it so valuable for most of these applications. 
2.1.1 PCM working principle 
The working principle of PCM is described using an example of a solid-liquid cycle for a PCM 
micro-capsule. Solid-liquid is the most common application form of PCM in buildings [16]. A 
macro-encapsulation strategy was used in the experiments conducted for this investigation.  
==PAGE:18==
Background and Fundamentals 
 
4 
 
 
Figure 2: PCM working principle solid-liquid [17]. 
Figure 2 shows a single PCM capsule which undergoes the aforementioned process. If the PCM is 
in solid state and the surrounding temperature rises, the PCM starts melting and absorbs heat from 
its surroundings. The resulting cooling potential can be used to remove heat from buildings or in 
other applications where cooling is required. During the phase change itself, the temperature of 
the material remains constant (see Figure 3) until it fully reaches liquid state. From that point on, 
the capsule increases its temperature and sensible heat is stored. 
However, if the surrounding temperature falls again, the whole process is reversed. First, the PCM 
temperature drops to the melting point. Then the material solidifies and latent heat is released. This 
energy in form of heat can be released to the environment or, for instance, be used as heat source 
for heat pumps (or any other applications where heat is required). The PCM temperature during 
solidification remains constant (isothermal process) until the material fully reaches solid state. 
 
Figure 3: PCM correlation between thermal energy and temperature [18].

2.1.2 Applications in buildings 
Research concerning phase change material applications in buildings began already in the 1970s  
with a focus on space heating and cooling as well as thermal energy storage [14]. A large amount 
of articles and papers have been published over the past decades concerning this topic because of 
its energy saving potential while also increasing thermal comfort inside buildings. Current research 
focuses mainly on the implementation of PCM in gypsum wallboards, concrete structures and 
insulation materials. Wallboards enhanced with PCM is the widest used solution at this time 
because they are cheaper and easier to install in testing facilities compared to the other options. 
However, there are also other thermal storage applications in buildings that were investigated such 
as hot water heat storage, pipe insulation, cool thermal energy storage or double facades with 
PCM [12].  
Buildings with lightweight construction often suffer from overheating and from a high demand for 
cooling capacity during occupied hours because of their low thermal mass [19]. Therefore, the 
PCM enhanced components are especially relevant in these type of buildings. The large heat storage 
potential of the encapsulated material makes their behaviour similar to thermally active building 
systems (TABS). With that effect comes also the advantage of shifting the peak cooling loads and 
reducing total energy consumption [20] which is described in Figure 4. Peak load is reduced by the 
passive cooling capacity that PCMs naturally provide. Thus, active cooling devices (chiller, air 
handling units, fans, etc.) can be designed smaller which reduces initial cost for HVAC [21]. Due 
to the shift in energy demand from on-peak to off-peak periods a large energy saving potential can 
be achieved. It allows to use natural cooling and renewable energies more easily which helps saving 
carbon emissions and reduces electricity consumption [21]. 
 
Figure 4: PCM load shift and energy reduction [20]. 
The use of night-radiative cooling with photovoltaic/thermal panels (PV/T) was studied by 
Bourdakis et al. [10,11] and Bogatu et al. [16]. Hereby, PCM ceiling panels were installed in a room 
and an office scenario was simulated. The PCM provided passive cooling during the day and the 
absorbed heat was released during the night via radiative cooling through PV/T. It was shown that 
==PAGE:20==

an acceptable indoor environment was reached during the days at a very low energy usage 
compared to conventional cooling systems.  
Moreover, PCMs can also be coupled with other technical building services such as heat pumps 
and heat pipes [24]. When being incorporated to a heat pump, it can enhance the thermal efficiency 
and increase thermal comfort conditions. Researchers are currently also investigating the 
implementation of PCM in ventilation systems to reduce energy consumption and increase thermal 
comfort.  
2.2 Resilient cooling of buildings 
The “Annex 80 - Resilient Cooling of Buildings” addresses the need of considering resiliency as an 
important design criteria already in the early design stages of a building. It is part of the Technology 
Collaboration Programme (TCP) Energy in Buildings and Communities (EBC) of the International 
Energy Agency (IEA) and has been formed in July 2019. There are many definitions of resiliency 
depending on the respective situation but recent literature by Zhang et al. states that “resiliency in 
the context of the building environment refers to the ability of buildings and their systems to 
continue functioning as intended in the face of natural hazards imposed by climate change” [7]. 
Four resiliency characteristics are proposed by the aforementioned literature to assess the resiliency 
of cooling strategies. They can be classified to:  
 
Absorptive capacity: Ability to absorb the impacts of disruptive events (e.g. thermal mass) 
 
Adaptive capacity: Ability to adapt the system to new situations (e.g. solar shading, self-
regulation) 
 
Restorative capacity: Ability to recover from undesired conditions (e.g. night cooling) 
 
Recovery speed: Time required for the recovery process to reach a pre-defined thermal 
condition (e.g. active/passive discharge through ventilation system, water flow rate in 
TABS or similar systems)  
Furthermore, there are three categories in which the resiliency characteristics can be assessed – 
high, moderate and low. They are used to evaluate cooling strategies and their systems according 
to their resiliency capacities individually for each type of disruptive event [7].  
This work primarily focuses on the capability of buildings to withstand and recover from heat 
waves and power outages. For the scenario of heat waves, PCM enhanced building materials are 
considered to have a high absorptive capacity because of their high thermal mass [7]. In the 
experimental part of this study it was shown that they also have a moderate/high adaptive capacity 
because they can maintain or even increase their cooling-capacity when the room temperature rises. 
The restorative capacity is highly dependent on whether or not the system is combined with an 
active/passive discharge technology. Therefore it varies from one system to another and has a 
range from low to high. The recovery speed of MEPs is considered to be moderate because it takes 
several hours to discharge the components [7]. 
 
 
==PAGE:21==
Background and Fundamentals 
 
7 
 
However, vulnerability is another characteristic of resiliency that is considered by many researchers, 
while others separate it from the definition of resiliency [9]. Vulnerability gives indication of how 
sensitive a building system is to different types of shocks. Another important aspect of defining 
resilient cooling is that a building can experience failure. Building failure occurs when it is no longer 
capable of providing the intended functionalities or indoor climate. The definition of resilient 
cooling characteristics and risk factors by Attia et al. is shown in Figure 5. 
 
Figure 5: Definition of resiliency characteristics and risk factors by Attia et al. [9]. 
The aforementioned literature defines the four resiliency characteristics vulnerability, resistance, 
robustness and recoverability. They are to be separated from the resilient cooling characteristics. 
Moreover, time frame is another aspect to highlight. This includes both duration of a possible 
disruption but also the question of how far in the future the disruption will occur. Climate change 
is the major driver for more frequent and severe heat waves. As this is a global problem, which 
cannot be solved easily within a foreseeable time period, experts agreed that it should be defined 
as a long-term disruptive event [25]. On the other hand, power outages are more likely to be 
assigned to short-term disruption. 
Increasing the thermal mass of a building using PCM could overcome or mitigate the impact of 
power outages and heat waves. It has been shown that this cooling strategy can effectively reduce 
peak indoor temperatures and overheating hours, thus, improving indoor thermal comfort if used 
appropriately [25]. However, a study by Arce et al. [26] showed that the cooling effect of PCM 
might not be effective on very hot days when the outdoor temperature is above the melting range 
of the PCM and no active discharge was applied. Free cooling was found to be effective in this 
study but the high outdoor temperatures did not allow the PCM to complete its full phase-change. 
Therefore, it is crucial to select a PCM with an appropriate melting range according to the 
respective case of application so the PCM can return to its initial state.

3 Methodology 
3.1 Experimental work 
In order to investigate the influence of different air distribution principles on the thermal 
performance of PCM enhanced ceiling panels, several experiments were conducted in a climatic 
chamber. All experiments had a duration of 4 days per run. The chamber was a close representation 
of an office room and the operation under different conditions was studied. Looking more closely 
at mixing ventilation (MV) and displacement ventilation (DV), experiments with different 
ventilation rates and supply air temperatures were conducted. Because of the different working 
principle of the two air distribution methods, it was expected to see a difference in the performance 
of the MEPs. The assumption was that in case of MV the MEPs would have a better thermal 
performance than with DV because cool air was supplied horizontally along the ceiling. The forced 
convection would help to increase the heat transfer coefficient while also providing cooling to the 
ceiling. This would lead to a faster discharge during the night and a slower increase in temperature 
of the panel during operation. The MEPs were equipped with pipes embedded in the PCM, which 
allowed circulation of chilled water for active discharge of the material. Experiments were 
conducted with and without the ventilation system and active discharge. 
Temperature and heat flux sensors were used to monitor the condition of the panels. In addition, 
temperature sensors were attached on the surfaces of walls (inside and out) and floor but also the 
temperature of the ambient, slab and plenum was measured. Moreover, two stands were installed 
inside the chamber to measure air temperature at different heights (0.1 m, 0.6 m, 1.1 m and 1.7 m) 
as well as the operative temperature. The heights were selected based on ISO 7730 corresponding 
to the heights of ankle, abdomen and neck of a seated person as well as the height of the neck of 
a standing person [27]. All sensors were calibrated before the start of the experiments to ensure 
accuracy and legitimate data collection. For the experiments with MV, anemometers were installed 
with a distance of 5 cm to the ceiling in order to measure air speed and air jet temperature at 
different distances from the supply air diffuser. The diffusers were selected based on the air 
distribution inside the room and the ventilation rate was calculated based on Category II according 
to EN 16798-2. A smoke test was conducted to visualize that no short circuit occurred, which is a 
requirement for an efficient ventilation of spaces. Another purpose of the experiments was to use 
the measurements for the validation of a simulation model. In specific, the measurements of the 
laboratory temperature was used as boundary conditions for the simulation model to validate the 
PCM component. With the validated model further investigations were carried out on the resiliency 
of the MEPs. 
3.1.1 Climate chamber description 
All experiments were carried out in the climate chamber 6 located at the International Centre for 
Indoor Climate and Energy (ICIEE), Technical University of Denmark (DTU). An extensive 
description of the chamber can be found in Bogatu et al. [16,29]. The chamber was designed to 
represent a two-person office room and is equipped with ceiling panels containing PCM, a piping

used to simulate solar heat gains since the chamber does not have any exterior windows or walls. 
It can be adjusted in steps of 25 W to deliver heat into the room and is controlled by a schedule. 
Its maximum heating power was 235 W (≡ 10.4 W/m² floor area).

Mixing ventilation: 
The principle of mixing ventilation is that fresh air is supplied outside the occupied zone (usually 
from the ceiling or the wall) at a high velocity which leads to recirculation of air inside the 
room [32]. Mixing of supply and room air occurs and an exhaust diffuser assures to remove 
pollutants and other emissions from the room. The air velocity inside the occupied zone should be 
below the required level not to cause local thermal discomfort.

Displacement ventilation: 
In case of displacement ventilation, fresh air is supplied at floor level inside the occupied zone with 
a low velocity. The supplied air is usually cooler than the room air and, therefore, a pool of cold 
and fresh air is created over the entire floor, which displaces the warm and contaminated air. With 
this ventilation principle, the thermal forces of the heat gains inside the room are responsible for 
the distribution of air [32]. When the cool air reaches the heat gains, it starts rising and creates a 
thermal plume around the heat source. The exhaust diffuser is usually placed on the ceiling so that 
warm and contaminated air can be extracted outside the occupied zone.

3.1.3 Sensor calibration 
To ensure a high degree of accuracy in the obtained data, the temperature sensors were calibrated 
prior to the experiments. The ventilation system of the climate chamber was used to create the 
required conditions in the chamber between 16 °C and 32 °C. Nonetheless, it was likely that the 
supply air temperature undergoes slight fluctuations and air movement could influence the 
measurements. The sensors were therefore put into an insulated box to mitigate those fluctuations.  
3.1.3.1 Temperature sensor 
Before deploying the PT1000 sensors on their respective positions at the ceiling, walls, floor, 
plenum, slab and stands, they were placed inside an insulated box for calibration. This assured that 
all sensors measured a stable temperature. Besides the PT1000 sensors, a reference sensor was also 
placed inside the calibration box. A reference sensor is a device that measures temperature highly 
accurate and has a low uncertainty. Calibration certificates based on national and international 
standards confirm the accuracy of the reference sensor. This allowed to compare the measurement 
results of the PT1000 sensors with the more accurate reference sensor and to correct the measured 
difference. During calibration, it was ensured that both sensor types were immersed in the same 
depth as guidelines recommend [33].  
==PAGE:30==
Methodology 
 
16 
 
Figure 14 shows the calibration box with all temperature sensors included. A gap of around 3 cm 
from the top cover to the sides of the box was allowed to have minimal air exchange between 
calibration box and the rest of the climate chamber. Besides, three table fans were placed inside 
the chamber. This assured perfect mixing of room air and preventing thermal clouds and 
stratification. The accuracy of the PT 1000 sensors was ± 0.2 °C. 
 
Figure 14: PT 1000 sensor calibration. 
Unless required, temperature sensor don’t have to be calibrated for the entire range of the sensor 
but rather for the specific range where the sensor is planned to be used in [33]. Therefore, five 
temperature conditions ranging from 16 °C to 32 °C in steps of 4 °C where chosen as calibration 
temperatures because temperatures outside this range are unlikely to occur in office buildings. With 
this amount of calibration points it can be shown if the sensor behaviour is linear or not. For a 
correct calibration it is vital that the temperature to be measured becomes stable at the desired 
calibration point. In this case, it was aimed to reach a stable temperature for at least 30 minutes 
while a fluctuation of ±0.05 °C was tolerated. The measured difference from the PT1000 sensors 
to the reference sensors could not be adjusted directly. Instead, coefficients were used to correct 
the sensor’s reading into the temperature measured by the reference sensor.  
After the calibration, the PT1000 sensors were mounted again at their intended locations. During 
this action, thermal paste was used to increase the thermal conductivity between the panel surface 
and the PT1000. To reduce the influence of the ambient, a small piece insulation was put around 
the sensors. On top of that, a reflective tape was used, which reduced the radiation received by the 
surrounding surfaces and ensured a better contact to the ceiling.

3.2.2 Dynamic simulation of a resilient cooling strategy 
Resilience is not something that can be measured directly but rather is evaluated based on measures 
or performance indicators. The analysis in this work orients on the “Dynamic simulation guideline 
for the performance testing of resilient cooling strategies” [41] developed by IEA EBC Annex 80. 
==PAGE:35==
Methodology 
 
21 
 
It is a straight forward report that determines the procedure of conducting an investigation of a 
resilient cooling strategy.  
Future weather files were created by the Annex 80 for several locations around the world with 
varying climates and locations, ranging from cold and humid climate (e.g. Stockholm, Sweden) to 
extremely hot and humid (e.g. Singapore). Both typical meteorological years and years containing 
extreme weather with heat waves and power outages are available. Moreover, three time periods 
were defined – contemporary (2001 – 2020), medium-term future (2041 – 2060) and long-term 
future (2081 – 2100) [41]. Copenhagen, Denmark was chosen for this study, which represents a 
cold and humid climate, and the resiliency was assessed for a year in the long-term future with the 
most severe heat wave. The resilient cooling strategy to be evaluated was the utilisation of thermal 
mass through PCM enhanced ceiling panels. In order to make conclusion whether or not a building 
becomes more resilient when a certain resilient cooling strategy is applied, it has to be compared 
to a reference system. The reference system has the same building parameters and conditions but 
only with another cooling strategy instead. In this investigation, an all-air system was chosen for 
comparison because it is a commonly applied cooling method in the built environment. The 
ventilation rate of the ventilation system in the reference building varied between 165 m³/h (air 
exchange rate 2.7 h-1) and 114 m³/h (air exchange rate 1.9 h-1) which was calculated according to 
EN 16798-2 based on Category 1 and 2, respectively. Cooling setpoint was 26 °C which 
corresponded to the higher volume flow and the supply air temperature was 18 °C (for both 
buildings). The simulation time frame was the cooling season from the 1st of April to the 30th of 
September 2090 in both simulations. The construction materials used for this simulation is oriented 
on an investigation on activated slab heating and cooling systems by Olesen & Dossi [42].

4.2 Conclusion 
In summary, several experiments were conducted in chamber 6 to study the impact of increased 
heat gains and different air distribution principles on the thermal performance of the MEPs. It was 
shown that increased internal and solar loads do not only result in higher indoor operative 
temperatures but also lead towards an increase in cooling capacity. This self-regulatory effect is 
often observed by TABS which makes them behave similar [47]. The MEP were able to absorb 
more heat in total and it also requires more time to discharge them. 
It was expected that that an increased supply air temperature and a reduced volume flow result in 
a higher indoor operative temperature. Both could be proven for the experiments with DV but 
only the first statement held true for MV because higher laboratory temperatures during MV-1 
reduced the transmission heat losses from the chamber to the hall compared to MV-4. Moreover, 
it was shown that more heat was removed by the ventilation system in the DV scenarios than in 
the MV scenarios when no water flow was active. The explanation lays in the working principle of 
the two ventilation strategies because the return air temperature is higher when DV is applied. 
However, this could not be shown in the experiments where active discharge of the MEP was 
applied. The reason for this could be the higher laboratory temperature in MV-1 or the lower higher 
supply temperature in DV-1 but further investigations are necessary to make this conclusion. 
Nevertheless, the ventilation system supports the cooling of the room but reduces the effective 
cooling capacity of the MEP, regardless of the applied air distribution principle. The additional 
cooling provided led also to shorter discharge periods of the MEPs. 
Generally, a better indoor comfort was found for the experiments with DV than with mixing or 
without ventilation. This suggests that in buildings with high expectations on the indoor thermal 
environment the combination of MEP+DV should be preferred over the MEP+MV. However, 
further investigations are required.  
Finally, the discharge effect of a horizontal air supply along the ceiling on the MEP was shown. 
Surface temperatures were lower up to 3 K in the case of MV compared to DV for the investigated 
panels. However, it has to be mentioned that this effect was only present for panels which were 
close to the supply air diffuser. It was expected that this influence would be present for the entire 
throw length of the diffuser but it was only proven for a distance of 0.84 m. The measurements of 
panel 38 and 35 showed that the effect was negligible for a distance of 1.68 m and further distances 
despite a calculated throw length of 2.6 m. The air jet temperature was too high to provide cooling 
to the panels at a distance further than 0.84 m. However, it has to be considered that the air 
distribution might have been influenced by the rough surface of the ceiling and by the thermal 
plume of the internal heat gains. Thus, further investigation are required to investigate this effect 
more in depth. Besides, it was shown that the air jet velocity and, therefore, the throw length 
increases as the surface temperature of the ceiling rises. Thus, further studies are required to 
quantify that effect so it can be considered for the design of future MV systems when combined 
with MEP.

5.1 Validation of simulation model 
Experimental data for the validation was gathered during the period from the 1st of April 2022 until 
the 5th of April 2022. Out of these 5 days, only the monitoring data from the 4th day were used for 
the validation itself to ensure steady periodic conditions. The first three days served as preliminary 
lead time to give the chamber enough response time to adjust to the new setup, whereas the last 
day was used as control data to show that steady state was reached. No significant difference 
between results from the 4th to the 5th day was registered and therefore it was assumed to be steady. 
As the physical representation of the office room (climate chamber 6) does not have any external 
walls or windows but is located inside a hall (Climate Square) instead, measurements of the air 
temperature in the hall were taken during the entirety of the project. From these measurements a 
boundary file was created which served as input data for the software simulation during validation. 
With that, both the experimental setup and the simulation model had the same boundary conditions 
during the specific time where the physical experiment was conducted with specifically defined 
heat gains, water flow rate and operating schedules.  
Nevertheless, there is a slight deviation from the software model to the physical setup. While the 
actual chamber has 48 PCM tiles with a size of 0.6 m x 0.6 m, the TRNSYS model calculates with 
one uniform PCM ceiling of 17 m² which gets charged and discharged uniformly. Also, the software 
model does not take the location of the heat gains into account and assumes therefore a uniform 
distribution of loads. In reality, however, the amount of absorbed heat in PCM panels is affected 
by the location of the heat sources, as it was shown in previous studies by Bogatu et al. [28] and 
Weinläder et al. [48]. Similar correlations were also found in this investigation. Besides, the distance 
of the panel to the cold water manifold is another aspect to consider. The closest panel to the 
manifold received cooler water the following ones because the water itself got heat up while cooling 
the PCM. 
To make up for these differences, one approach was to divide the measurement data used for 
validation into two phases, passive and active operation. Hereby, the first phase indicates that there 
is no active discharge of the PCM panels and heat loads are present. Panel 25 was used for the first 
phase because it best describes the heat absorption of the PCM based on its location in the room 
because it received both radiant heat from the heating net and convective heat due to the thermal 
plume of the occupants and equipment underneath. On the contrary, especially the panels located 
in the corners received less heat from these sources.  
The second phase expresses the time period where active discharge is possible. Panel 39 was used 
during that stage as it is close to the middle of its respective water circuit and therefore had an 
averaged water flow temperature compared to the start and the end of its circuit. The results from 
the cooling power validation based on panel 25 and 39 are shown in Figure 39.

One possible reason for the mismatch between the results could be that some fractions of the heat 
flux sensors lost direct contact with the ceiling over time and therefore could not measure the total 
heat flux present. Another explanation could be found in the fact that the thermal conductivity of 
the PCM in the model was increased from 0.2 W/m.K to 1.2 W/m.K which leans on previous 
research by Koschenz et al. [49]. This study shows that the increase in thermal conductivity can be 
done in case there are metal fins between the PCM panels, however, this was not proven for the 
ceiling installed in chamber 6 and needs further investigation. Besides, another reason could be that 
the enthalpy model, which is used in the Type 399 component, does not represent the real 
behaviour of PCM materials when shifting between the solidifying and melting curve [37]. Lastly, 
it has to be mentioned that in total there were 12 heat flux sensor installed while there are 48 PCM 
panels in the suspended ceiling. Even though the attached sensors were spread out over the PCM 
ceiling, they only give an indication of how the entire ceiling performs. This means that with regards 
to the whole ceiling, values can still differ to a certain extent.  
A more reliable way on how to validate the cooling power was considered to be based on the 
energy extracted by the water circuit. With this approach all MEPs are included in the validation 
phase and this leads to a more concise analysis. The graphs of the heat extracted through the piping 
system are presented in Figure 41. 
==PAGE:59==
Resiliency Analysis of PCM enhanced ceiling panels

Figure 41: Cooling power validation water side. 
It can be observed that active discharge of the ceiling began at 6 p.m. for both measured and 
simulated values according to the defined schedule and temperature setpoint. In the measurements, 
however, a maximum cooling power of 90.8 W/m² was reached while it was only 67.7 W/m² in 
the simulation. On the other hand, the measurement graph is declining faster compared to the 
simulation and they intersect at around 9 p.m. At the end of the discharge period, the values from 
the measurement were lower than the simulation values. The reason behind the deviations may lie 
in the use of different heat transfer coefficients between the steel pipe and its surrounding material 
(PCM in the real panel and steel in the simulation).  
Solidification is an exothermic process which means it releases heat during the phase change from 
liquid to solid state. When water circulation starts, the PCM capsules directly around the pipe 
freezes and results in a higher heat exchange then the same material without phase change. After 
the solidification of the close by zone, the heat transfer is defined by the conductivities and surfaces 
of the two adjacent layers. The TRNSYS model cannot account for this effect and calculates with 
a constant coefficient. Assuming that the simulation calculates with a lower heat transfer coefficient 
than the real chamber, the return temperature does not increase in temperature as much and the 
temperature difference between supply and return is lower. With a lower temperature difference 
the extracted heat is also lower at the same volume flow than the measurement. But this also means 
that the cooling power after 9 p.m. (intersection between the graphs) is higher since it could not 
remove as much heat in the first place as the experimental run.  
The RMSE calculated based on this method was 79.6 W which is fairly similar to the result based 
on the mean PCM ceiling. This values is still higher than the allowed 60 W given by VDI 6020. 
However, the total heat removed during the day of validation was 5211 Wh for the measurement 
and 5053 Wh for the simulation which results in a difference of only 3 %. Since this deviation is 
rather small the model can be considered as being accurate. This allows to use the model for further 
investigation without resulting in critical errors.

5.2 Comparison of PCM model and all-air system 
The performance evaluation of the developed models are described in this section. The resilient 
cooling strategy to be assessed was the utilisation of thermal mass with PCM enhanced ceiling 
panels. For the purpose of evaluating this strategy, a reference all-air system was created for the 
same building. Both systems are described in section 3.2.2. The simulated time frame was from the 
01st of April until the 30th of September 2090 with a future weather file created by the Annex 80 
for the purpose of the assessment of resilient cooling strategies in a changing climate [41]. 
Copenhagen, Denmark was chosen as the location to be investigated and the weather file contained 
the most severe heat waves in the long-term future. All simulations were carried out with the 
transient simulation software TRNSYS and a simulation time step of 30 min was used.

temperatures.

be increased when setting the control temperature for discharging the panels to 22.5 °C instead of

might also increases the time with high temperatures because the PCM could not absorb as much 
heat before exceeding the upper melting range. An optimization study could conclude on the 
optimal cooling setpoint. Another possibility is to choose another PCM with a different (ideally 
more narrow) melting range.  
For the all-air system, a higher ventilation rate could be used to reduce extreme high room 
temperatures. A lower supply temperature might not be applicable due to risk of condensation and 
mould growth.

5.2.3 Key performance indicators  
As described in section 3.2.2, key performance indicators were used for the assessment of 
overheating as suggested by the Annex 80 [41]. The first indicator presented is the Indoor 
Overheating Degree (IOD) which is a measure to quantify the risk for overheating [44]. It was 
calculated to a value of 0.32 °C for the PCM and 1.47 °C for the reference model. This means that 
the building with the all-air strategy is at higher risk to suffer from overheating. The operative 
indoor temperature is more often and more severe above the upper comfort limit (26 °C in this 
study) and, therefore, a lower comfort level can be concluded.  
The Ambient Warmness Degree (AWD), which indicates the severity of high outdoor temperatures 
above a certain threshold [43], was calculated to 3.16 °C in both scenarios. This value is not affected 
by the chosen cooling strategy or the building construction because in only takes the outdoor air 
temperature and the fixed base temperature (18 °C in this study) into account.  
Lastly, the Overheating Escalation Factor (αIOD) was calculated for both models. It is an indicator 
used for the sensitivity of a building to overheating and its resistivity to a warmer climate induced 
by climate change [41]. In the PCM model, the αIOD was calculated to 0.10 whereas in the reference 
system a value of 0.47 was found. Comparing these two values, it can be concluded that the all-air 
system is much more sensitive to a warmer climate and cannot suppress the outdoor thermal stress 
as good as the building with MEP. This makes the PCM model more resilient to the threats caused 
by climate change. An overview of the KPIs is given in Table 17.

5.3 Conclusion 
To conclude, the resiliency behaviour of MEPs was investigated using the dynamic simulation 
software TRNSYS. First, a model was created with the same geometrical and thermal properties as 
chamber 6 for the purpose of validating the PCM component (Type 399). With that model, the 
thermal performance was investigated and compared to the measurements from the experimental 
results. Identical profiles were used for the internal and solar heat gains in the simulation and the 
experiment. Measurements of the ambient temperature during the experiment were used as an 
input for the simulation to assure the same boundary conditions. Different approaches for 
validation were used, but the one considered most accurate showed that there was only a difference 
of 3 % between the energy removed from the water circuit in the model and experiment. Moreover, 
the RMSE in operative temperature was only 0.2 °C, therefore being far below the VDI 6020 
tolerance of ±1.5 °C.  
Thus, the validated Type 399 component was used for further investigations, especially for the 
analysis of a resilient cooling strategy. Two additional models were created, one containing the 
validated PCM component and the other one as an all-air system. Both strategies were applied in a 
middle office of a multi-storey building and a future weather file of Copenhagen, Denmark was 
used containing most severe heat waves in the long-term future. A better performance in relation 
to thermal comfort was found for the PCM model. Peak indoor operative temperatures were 
reduced and generally a higher share of time within Category 2 was achieved compared to the 
reference system. The PMV-PPD analysis also showed that thermal discomfort was higher in the 
all-air system, especially due to high indoor temperatures. In contrast, the majority of the time with 
thermal discomfort in the PCM model was found in the morning hours due to low temperatures 
after the panels were discharged.  
Finally, the findings were supported by the overheating assessment based on key performance 
indicators (KPI), which expressed a higher risk of overheating for the all-air system. In addition, a 
lower level of sensitivity and a higher level of resistivity was found for the PCM model, which 
makes it a more resilient cooling strategy than the one used in the reference system.

Discussion 
 
55 
 
6 Discussion 
The aim of this study was to investigate the resiliency behaviour PCM enhanced radiant ceiling 
panels. An additional goal was to examine the impact of two commonly used air distribution 
principles on the thermal performance of the MEP and to conclude on the compatibility of 
coupling the two systems with each other.  
The experimental part showed that MEPs are able to adapt their effective cooling power based on 
the internal and solar loads. Because of the high thermal capacity of the PCM, a self-regulatory 
effect is created, which can often be observed in TABS [47]. Generally, a better indoor thermal 
comfort was registered when a ventilation system was applied compared to the operation without. 
Fluctuating ambient and supply air temperatures made it difficult to quantify the effect of changing 
the ventilation parameters (qsup and Tsup) but the analysis of the results showed that a higher supply 
air temperature caused higher operative temperatures in the room. This was expected because the 
heat removed by the ventilation system was reduced. The same effect was also expected when 
decreasing the volume flow but this could only be proven for the scenario with DV. Previous 
investigations have shown that a higher laboratory temperature affects the transmission of heat 
from the chamber to the hall [45]. In this study, discrepancies in Tlab of up to 1.7 K were measured 
and that might influenced the overall performance of the system. Heat can be stored in the thermal 
mass of the construction materials of the chamber which did not allow to cool down as much 
during the night. Slightly better indoor thermal comfort was found for DV than for MV but the 
reason could not be clearly identified and needs therefore further investigations. It can be argued 
that the working principle of DV allows to remove more heat because the exhaust temperature is 
higher compared to MV. This held true for all scenarios without water circulation but not for the 
scenarios with active discharge. The reason for that could be the fluctuations in Tsup (discrepancies 
up to 1.6 K) and Tlab (discrepancies up to 3.1 K). Lastly, the experimental work showed that 
horizontal air supply along the ceiling helps to discharge the MEP, however, this effect is only 
limited to panels that are close to the supply air diffuser. This influence was shown for a distance 
of 0.84 m but was negligible for a distance of 1.68 m. Thus, expectation that this effect would be 
present over the entire throw length was not met. However, a rough ceiling surface and distraction 
due to the thermal plume of the internal heat might have affected the air flow pattern. In order to 
quantify the effect of discharge due to forced convection, further investigations are required.  
The simulation part compared a middle office room in a multi-storey building under operation 
with two different cooling strategies – thermal mass utilisation with PCM and an all-air system. A 
weather file in the long-term future containing heat waves was used to study the resiliency of the 
system with MEPs. The initial aim was to keep the thermal comfort level within Category II for 
90 % according to EN16798-1 but it was not reached for either of the developed models. However, 
the analysis of the thermal comfort levels showed that the PCM model performed better than the 
all-air system and was able to maintain the comfort level within Category II for 49 %, while it was 
only 33 % in the reference model. In contrast, an investigation by Quesada et al. [51] found that 
the all-air system performed slightly better than the PCM model. The reason for that can be found 
in the use of different ventilation rates. While in the all-air system by the aforementioned author a 
ventilation rate of up to 8.5 h-1 was used, this study had a maximum air exchange of 2.7 h-1 
corresponding to the minimum air flow rate in Category I. The difference in cooling power 
provided by the ventilation was therefore less compared to the one in the literature.  
However, the resiliency analysis of the PCM enhanced ceiling panels based on KPIs showed that 
the office with MEPs had less risk of overheating. Due to the increased thermal mass, a lower level 
of sensitivity and higher level resistivity was reached compared to the all-air system. The procedure 
of the resiliency evaluation leaned on the dynamic simulation guideline by the Annex 80 [41]. No

similar investigation was found where a quantitative or qualitative comparison of a PCM and an 
all-air system was carried out in a future climate scenario based on the proposed evaluation 
parameters. Thus, this study can help for the decision making of future research activities and for 
the design of resilient cooling strategies.  
Though, the developed models also have some limitations. First of all, only the location of 
Copenhagen, Denmark was studied and similar performance cannot be guaranteed in other climate 
zones. Besides, the Type 399 component used for the simulation of the PCM can fail in simulating 
the phase-change correctly when large time steps are used in the simulation, as described by 
Delcroix et al. [52]. The problem can be minimised using a time step of less than one minute but 
since the simulations were carried out with a time step of 30 min, this might have influenced the 
results. Finally, the investigated systems were not optimized. Adding solar shading would reduce 
solar heat gains and the implementation of window opening control based on indoor air 
temperature could possibly provide natural cooling. Both would lead to less overheating and a 
higher thermal comfort. It was unexpected that the time outside Category III in the PCM model 
was mostly due to temperatures below 22 °C, which occurred at the beginning of occupancy. An 
optimized setpoint for the discharge of the MEPs as well as a narrower but higher melting range 
of the PCM could help increasing the thermal comfort even further.  
Despite the limitations, the analysis showed that PCMs have an enormous potential to make 
buildings more resilient in a changing climate. It is important that researcher see this potential and 
further develop and optimize the application of PCMs in buildings to be prepared for future 
extreme events.

Conclusion 
 
57 
 
7 Conclusion 
The conducted experimental study shows that indoor thermal comfort increases when a ventilation 
system in combination with MEPs is employed. Additional cooling capacity is provided by the 
ventilation system, which supports the MEPs in cooling the room. It is important to consider the 
cooling power provided due to ventilation because it can help reducing the amount of required 
PCM and shortening the periods required to discharge the material. Due to its high heat capacity 
in the melting range, the PCM has a self-regulation effect, so the cooling power adapts to the 
thermal situation in the room. The comparison of MV and DV shows a better performance for 
DV in terms of thermal comfort. This suggests that DV should be the preferred air distribution 
principle in combination with a PCM ceiling, but further investigations are required to support 
these findings as there was no similar comparison found in literature.  
Moreover, this study shows that the implementation of PCMs in radiant ceiling panels can reduce 
the risk of overheating in buildings and increase the indoor thermal comfort. Previous 
investigations have shown that PCMs can also reduce the energy consumption and therefore the 
carbon emissions. Combining these two advantages, PCMs address the root-cause of climate 
change (GHG emissions) and help to mitigate its negative impact on the thermal comfort in 
buildings. The problems caused by CC has to be taken serious in the building industry and the 
concept of resilient cooling strategies should gain more importance in the design of future 
buildings. PCMs can also be easily retrofitted in refurbishment of buildings and a similar behaviour 
to TABS can be achieved. The comparison between the two developed models shows that the 
IOD for the model with MEP was 0.32 °C, while the reference system had a value of 1.47 °C. The 
Overheating Escalation Factor was more than four times higher for the all-air system than for the 
PCM model, which indicates a lower resistivity to the outdoor thermal stress. In warmer outdoor 
temperatures caused by global warming, overheating will occur more frequently and severe in the 
investigated all-air system than in the model containing MEPs. With 49 % compared to 33 % of 
the time within Category II, a better indoor thermal comfort was found for the PCM model than 
for the reference system. The initial comfort aim of maintaining 90 % of the time within Category II 
was therefore not reached, but recommendations are proposed to optimise the models in order to 
reduce the hours of discomfort.  
All in all, it can be expected that the application of PCMs in buildings will increase in the future 
due to their numerous benefits. However, it is now appealed to the manufacturer in the building 
industry to acquire this knowledge and to develop building components so they can be accessible 
for future building projects.
