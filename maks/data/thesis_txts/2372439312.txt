Abstract
From topology optimisation to structural system
Mads Bundgaard Bloch-Sørensen, Technical University of Denmark, June 2017
This project focuses on the transition of the suggested material accumulation from topology
optimisation to a concrete strutural system. Possible engineering solutions will be compared
to traditional designs on the background of material savings.
The structure investigated is an Arctic shelter located on Greenland with a half circular
barrel shape with a radius of 40 m. Only a two dimensional cross section of the structure
is analysed, and is exposed to wind loading as the dominant load case only. Two diﬀerent
types of wind loading is considered; the ﬁrst is dictated by the Eurocode and second is
identiﬁed by means of wind tunnel pressure measurement data on a small scale model of
the structure. This data is then scaled to the same wind speed and risk of the previous
load in order to compare these. A topology optimisation algorithm is explained and an
existing implementation of the algorithm is utilised and slightly modiﬁed for the purpose
of this paper. The algorithm is supplied with a combination of diﬀerent wind load types,
support conditions and relative mass ratios, and the resulting designs are discussed and
three designs are selected for further analysis.
Finite beam element theory is explained, and the three selected designs are manually in-
terpreted as an actual structural system using ﬁnite beam elements. For comparison is
two existing structures with similar shape also modelled using the same elements. The
structures are analysed and discussed, the topology optimised designs shows slight improve-
ment regarding overall deﬂection of the system, but no direct improvement is total mass used.
The topology optimisation is therefore a very relevant tool in the search for optimal structural
design. This paper proofs the concept, however the employed optimisation goal does not
necessarily provide a lighter design but has a tendency to develop a stiﬀer design.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The usual process of designing a structural system from an engineering standpoint starts out
with a roughly deﬁned topology, an outline of where the structural elements are going to be
located and how they are going to carry the loads they are exposed to. This can be due to
the design of the architect, previously solved problems with similar boundary conditions or
simply engineering intuition. What is left is the dimensioning of these element to ensure the
integrity of the system. This approach does not necessarily result in an optimal structural
design.
An optimal design from an engineering aspect is a design that uses a relative small amount
of material yet still results in a very stiﬀstructure. One method of approaching the optimal
design is using topology optimisation. Topology optimisation is a mathematical method
that can be used to optimise material layout within a given domain subject to loads and
boundary conditions.
1.1
Objective
The objective of this paper is to employ topology optimisation to develop a light weight
roof structure to be deployed in Arctic areas. The ambition of the structure is to provide
shelter against the harsh Arctic environment by establishing an artiﬁcial biosphere within
the structure. The main conditions that causes the Arctic area to appear very hostile is
the high wind speeds, vast amounts of snow and the freezing temperatures and especially
in combination. The artiﬁcial biosphere will only actively mitigate the wind and snow eﬀects.
The structure should be large enough to contain smaller structures within to accommo-
date other facilities needed by the inhabitants. The exact dimensions of the structure
is not the important factor, however a circular outer shape with a radius of 40 m and
an arbitrary length is used. The limitations of the internal structure is restricted to 2D
frames with a center distance of 20 m. The domain of the frame is chosen as the full half circle
The aim to decrease the weight of the structure is due to the diﬃculty of reaching these
remote areas and the costs involved in transporting goods. Furthermore, the used materials
must be able to resist the environment in a manner that makes them practically mainte-
nance free. Lastly the materials should not cause unnecessary contamination of the area
surrounding the structure.
The external load is focused on wind loading. Two diﬀerent wind loads are examined: load
dictated by the Eurocodes and load identiﬁed using wind tunnel tests.

1.2
Thesis structure
This thesis is overall divided into ﬁve parts. The ﬁrst and major part declares the mathe-
matical theory behind topology optimisation and how it is used. The next part, described in
chapter three and four, is dedicated to clarify the structural requirements, including loading
and material selection, of the structure to be designed and how these requirements are ap-
plied to the topology optimisation process through discretisation of the continuous boundary
conditions and applied loads. The third part investigates the topology optimisation and
how diﬀerent initial conditions aﬀect the designs and optimal initial conditions and designs
are found. The fourth part analyses the topology optimised designs and translates these
into actual structural systems consisting of beam elements. This part also undertake the
analysis and translation of two existing structures to the a ﬁnite element beam system for
comparison. The last part quickly discusses ﬁnite beam element theory and then evaluates
the performance of these systems and compare the results to evaluate how well the topology
optimisation accommodate the design criteria compared to the existing structural solutions.
1.3
Limitations
Linearity
The calculations performed in this paper does not take any form of non linear behaviour
into consideration. Local element buckling is therefore not taken into account and is not
part of the dimensioning.
2D calculations
All structural analysis is performed in two dimensional space only. This means that only
forces perpendicular to the half circular surface of the structure are considered. This also
means that global buckling of the structure is omitted in the analysis.
Materials and imperfections
All materials are assumed isotropic linear elastic and without imperfections.
Connections
No structural connections is considered in this project. No additional weight associated with
these are included in the analysis, nor are the load transferring capabilities between element
assessed. This is applicable for both the analysis of existing and newly developed designs.
Roof
A type of rooﬁng for the structure is suggested but not further included in the calculations
as this is beyond the two dimensional analysis performed. However the roof is assumed
completely rigid and weightless and transfers the applied load without delay onto the
underlying supporting structure.

2.1
Introduction
Topology optimisation is one of the newer techniques added to the civil engineering toolbox.
Topology optimisation is diﬀerent than sizing optimisation in the way that topology optimi-
sation has no knowledge about where or how many elements or holes are involved in the
design but must determine these features based on a limited amount of material. The goal
of topology optimisation is to determine how material is optimally distributed within the
given domain, subject to loads and boundary conditions in such a way that the performance
of the system is maximised. However performance of a system is no absolute and depending
on the ﬁnal goal diﬀerent performance measurements can develop diﬀerent results.
In the following the topology problem is deﬁned and solved based on a existing script written
by Ole Sigmund and modiﬁed to solve the speciﬁc problem investigated (Sigmund, 2001).
2.2
Problem formulation
The topology optimisation problem itself can be formally described in a mathematical
manner, but in order to do so the problem must be fully understood. A given domain
is subject to applied loads and possible boundary conditions, that the optimisation can
make use of to carry the loads. The structure to be constructed is limited in size e.g. a
fraction of the domain size, meaning that only a part of the total domain is allowed to
be used. The optimisation is fundamentally how to distribute this fraction, as the shape
and supports of the structure are unknown. This is regarded as the Material Distribution
Problem (Bendsøe and Sigmund, 2003). Additional constrains regarding location and size
of predetermined holes and areas requiring solid inﬁll can be explicit presented, even thou
this can be considered part of the domain restriction.
2.2.1

in a maximum global stiﬀness that is the main goal of the optimization. If dynamics is
temporarily ignored maximum global stiﬀness is generally reﬂects optimum design result
within the ﬁeld.

2.4
Optimal criteria method
The optimal criteria method is one method used to solve the previous mentioned minimum
compliance problem. The approach is an iterative update method that based on an initial
design with equally distributed material. The displacements for the design under load
updates is determined and and the design is updated using a scheme to achieve a less
compliant solution. The process continues until a satisfactory small change in compliance
between two designs is obtained.

2.5
Finite element method
Despite the topology optimisation theory mostly describes every point within a continuous
domain, this is impractical if not practically impossible to accomplish computationally. The
domain is therefore divided into a ﬁnite number of elements that interact with each other,
thereby the name. This approach makes the calculations accomplishable, and the theory
within ﬁnite element as well deﬁned. In the following the bilinear element is discussed and
utilised to solve the topology optimisation problem.

CHAPTER 3
Structural Design
3.1
Introduction
This chapter outlines the overall design criteria of the structure. This includes the loads,
that the structure is exposed to, and the material used in the structural system. The two
wind loads applied are based on the Eurocodes and wind tunnel tests.
3.2
Design Criteria
The structure is only analysed in a two dimensional plane. The domain is a half circle on a
horizontal ground with a radius of 40 m, hereafter also called a single frame. The inﬂuence
length of each frame is 20 m, meaning that each support 10 m of roof on each side of the
frame. The material used in the structure must be environmentally friendly and practically
maintenance free. The loading is dimensioned according to a location in the Arctic area of
Greenland, and the main load condition is wind load.
3.3
Loading
The structure is loaded with two diﬀerent kinds of load, one is the Eurocode which is a
general set of rules regarding structural design that speciﬁes how the design should be
conducted within the European Union. The other is loading derived from a model of the
structure exposed to wind in a wind tunnel. This approach is also subject to the Eurocode.
This produces a diﬀerent load scenario and often results in a load with smaller magnitude
as the load is speciﬁc for the structure in question.
3.3.1
Eurocode load
As the structure examined is designed to be used in Arctic areas, the starting load case for
the topology optimisation is based on the Eurocodes. The Eurocodes in question are:

structures (Dansk Standard, 2007b).
• DS/EN 1991-1-4/AC:2005 - Provides corrections to DS/EN 1991-1-4:2007 (Dansk
Standard, 2010a).
• DS/EN 1991-1-4 DK NA:2015 - Deﬁnes the conditions for implementing DS/EN
1991-1-4 in Denmark for construction work in obedience with the Danish Building

Load cases and consequence class
Consequence classes are based on the risk of personal injury and social consequences in case
of structural failure. The span of the structure is very large which makes failure a very
dangerous situation for people within. Additionally the structure is intended for the Arctic
regions where the distance to the nearest civilisation can be very large and rescue time can
be measured in days depending on the weather conditions. These factors results in a high
consequence class, CC3, yielding the consequence class factor KFI = 1.1. The Eurocode
states that a number of diﬀerent load cases, i.e. combinations of applicable loads, is to be
assessed to ensure that the structure is safe and sound when several load types are present.

3.4.1
Aluminium
Aluminium has one third of the stiﬀness of steel, but is almost only one third of the weight of
steel in return. Aluminium is not prone to corrosion as aluminium oxide spontaneously forms
in a thin layer when aluminium is exposed to air. Unlike steel, the oxide is impermeable
and adheres to the parent material. Aluminium therefore has a long service life and is
appropriate to use in harsh environments e.g. Arctic areas. The speciﬁc type of aluminium
used is 6061-T6 which is commonly used in bicycle frames and is thereby widely available
and has a yield stress of 240 MPa (Dansk Standard, 2007a, (Table 3.2a)). The properties of

4.5
Applying self weight
Each element contribute to the weight of the whole structure and the self weight is therefore
included in the deﬂection calculations. The mass is applied to the elements in the same
manner as the wind load, as a force on the element nodes. The mass of a single element is
included by distributing the mass evenly as forces on the four corner nodes of the element
in a downwards vertical direction as shown in Figure 4.5. The self weight is only included in
the FE model and not within the topology optimisation algorithm, as this causes problems
for the sensitivity analysis (Bruyneel and Duysinx, 2005). The ﬁrst part of right hand side
of (2.17) does not disappear and as a result BK is no longer strictly positive. This is a
problem as (2.12) uses the square root of BK. The self weight is therefore not included
in the optimisation process itself but in the FE loads only. As a result of not including
self weight in the optimisation process, the design shows substructures only pressent due
to the mass of the substructure itself, an example of this is shown in Appendix B.3. To
compensate for the appearance of these substructures a threshold density of the elements,
in order to include their self weight, is introduced. A empiric value of 0.025 was chosen,
meaning that the mass of elements with a density of less than 2.5 % was not included in the
FE calculations. This eﬀectively removed these substructures without major interference
of the remaining structure. It is observed that the method used to include self weight can
cause the optimiser to alternate between two designs, however it slowly converges towards a
stable solution. The Matlab script used to add self weight is shown in Appendix A.7.

5.2
Volume fraction
The volume fraction is overall a very important parameter for topology optimisation, as it is
constant throughout the optimisation process. The ﬁnal design is therefore dependent on the
volume fraction. To assess the inﬂuence of the volume fraction, a number of optimisations
are performed where all parameters but the volume fraction, is held constant. The volume
fraction in these test ranges from 5 % to 95 % and the result is shown in Figure 5.1.
It is clear that a too high volume fraction renders the structural design almost completely
indistinguishable. As the ﬁnal structure is intended to be constructed from beams, a volume
fraction in excess of 40 % is unuseable as the structure no longer compares to the capabilities
of a beam but rather adopt a perforated plate-like structure. In the low end of the volume
fraction, ranging from 5 to 20 % a change in basic structure is observed, as the second
diagonal element from the bottom seem to lower the attachment point to the two main
columns as the volume fraction increases, where a further increase in volume fraction only
increment the number of sub elements and increase their thickness. It is therefore concluded
that a volume fraction of 20 % allows for suﬃcient material to fully develop the basic
structure of the design but still results in a solution that is interpretable as a beam structure
and is therefore used in the following optimisations.

7.2.6
Loading
There is two types of loading for the FE model, self weight caused by the beams themselves
and external load imposed by the wind. The self weight of each element is strictly found
using the cross section area of the element and the density of the aluminium which yield the
mass per length unit. The element loading is initially deﬁned as a distributed load matrix
dL with two columns, one for load in the x direction and one for load in the y direction
according to Figure 7.1, so the self weight can simply be subtracted from the distributed
load in the y direction. The external loading on the other hand exist as two diﬀerent types,
Eurocode and wind tunnel load. As the Eurocode load has only three diﬀerent load sizes,
the FE models are created in such a manner that no elements intersect two load zones as
shown in Figure 3.2. The speciﬁc loading according to the Eurocode can simply be included
similarly to the self weight. The wind tunnel loading ﬂuctuates enough that a value from a
single point cannot be used as the load across that entire element. Therefore the load is
integrated and evenly distributed across every element similar to Figure 4.4. This approach
loses the rotational moment the wind load exerts on individual elements, however it is

natural frequencies cannot be determined as better or worse than existing structures, based

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
This paper investigates topology optimised structural systems applied in Arctic areas as
a method of creating a habitable biosphere. As a case, is a light weight half circular two
dimensional design domain with a radius of 40 m assessed with no internal void requirements.
The external load on the structure is based on a location on Greenland. The found designs
has been compared to interpretations of existing structural solutions exposed to wind load
dictated by the Eurocodes and found using wind tunnel tests.
The total magnitude of the load dictated by the Eurocode is considerably larger than the
load identiﬁed using wind tunnel tests. The Eurocode load is conservative compared to
the resulting wind tunnel load. The topology optimised designs also shows clear diﬀerences
depending on the load type applied. When the topology optimisation algorithm was exposed
to the Eurocode load a deeper truss system develops due to the larger sudden load changes
from positive to negative pressure at the top front of the structure. Generally a larger
amount of elements is created when compared to the designs developed exposed to the load
determined by the wind tunnel tests. The total weight of the structures is highly dependent
on the load, as a 35 % decrease in mass is observed when the applied load is changed from
Eurocode load to wind tunnel load. It is therefore optimal to apply wind tunnel load over
Eurocode load.
The performance of the developed structures are measured by amount of material used and
lowest deﬂection magnitude under load. It appears that the analysed structures roughly
follows a tradeoﬀcurve between these two performance measurements. Designs requiring a
small amount of material shows large deformations while designs requiring larger amount of
material shows less deformation. Among the analysed structures, the structure with the least
absolute deﬂection is one of the topology optimised designs. The structure with the least
required material is an existing structure. However for the latter the resulting deﬂections
are of a magnitude that is possible to inﬂuence the load distribution and non linear eﬀects
should be taken into account. This designs is therefore omitted in ﬁnal assessment of the
designs.
As the mass of the structure is so dependent on the load type, the results are discussed per
load type. For the structures dimensioned exposed to the Eurocode load, the three best
designs includes only one of the existing analysed structures. The three designs creates a
straight line in the deﬂection-mass plane, and is close to each other. It is therefore diﬃcult to
strictly declare the best structure although the topology optimised designs are at either side
of the existing design and therefore arguably is preferable depending on what performance
measure that is desired the most. For the designs dimensioned under the wind tunnel load
the best designs is a topology optimised design and this is regarded as the overall best.
==PAGE:82==
66
8 Conclusion
Lastly natural frequencies of both the topology optimised designs and the existing structures
was identiﬁed, however this shows no strict advantage or disadvantage connected to either.
The topology optimisation is therefore a very relevant tool in the search for optimal structural
design. This paper proofs the concept, however the employed optimisation goal does not
necessarily provide a lighter design but has a tendency to develop a stiﬀer design.

CHAPTER 9
Further work
The limitations and assumptions made within this paper gives space for further studies
in many diﬀerent areas. The limitations mentioned in the Introduction makes for obvious
ways to continue development of this study, but some other ways to further directions for
analysis is discussed in the following.
The analysis of snow load and how snow can build up around a circular structure and attach
to the expected ETFE surface is highly relevant, as this can not only drastically increase
the imposed load on the structure but also change how the wind load distribution aﬀects
the structure. A natural follow up to this analysis is a CFD analysis of the structure and
the snow inﬂuence of the wind ﬂow.
Within the dynamic domain, a time series analysis performed on the ﬁnite beam element
structures to assess their stiﬀness and deﬂection are relevant. The required wind loading
data from the wind tunnel tests exists already, making this analysis possible with limited
requirements.
The topology optimisation goal used in this paper is not necessarily the optimal when
designing structures of very low self weight. This goal could be investigated or modiﬁed
to obtain diﬀerent structures which are superior regarding self weight. An increase in grid
ﬁneness in the topology optimisation might also alter the solutions obtained, however this is
a computational expensive solution. Lastly the self weight inclusion within the topology
optimisation algorithm can be investigated.
The interpretation and translation from topology optimisation mass distribution to a ﬁnite
beam element system has been done on a engineering intuition level and could be formally
described and approached from a more mathematical aspect. The connection selection can
also be subject to an optimisation process.

The structure has eﬀectively no imposed load, which eliminated the need to access two of
the load cases. The self weight of the structure is below 10 of the total absolute vertical
wind load and the structure is therefore assumed suﬃciently strong to carry the self weight
of the members. Snow load is a very important load, especially in Arctic regions. Howevery
