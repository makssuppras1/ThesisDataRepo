Abstract
Manual quality control (QC) of hydrological sensor data is resource-intensive and increasingly
difficult to sustain at national scale. Inconsistent labeling, frequent anomalies, and large vol-
umes of time series data challenge traditional workflows. This thesis investigates the use of
recurrent neural networks—specifically long short-term memory (LSTM) architectures—to
support automated anomaly detection and imputation in Danish stream water level observa-
tions.
To compensate for the absence of labeled anomaly data, a synthetic anomaly framework was
developed, enabling controlled injection of anomalies found in the original data series. This
allowed for structured model training and evaluation. Two model types were examined: a
sequence-to-sequence LSTM (LSTM 1) that uses auxiliary inputs from nearby hydrometric and
meteorological stations, and an autoregressive LSTM (LSTM 2) trained solely on target-station
and weather data. LSTM 2 was further augmented with memory protection and anomaly-aware
loss weighting to reduce overfitting to corrupted inputs.
LSTM 2 consistently outperformed LSTM 1 in reconstruction accuracy, achieving a root mean
squared error (RMSE) of 52 mm and a Nash–Sutcliffe efficiency (NSE) of 0.95, compared to
LSTM 1’s RMSE of 141 mm and NSE of 0.65. However, LSTM 2 exhibited a tendency to
overfit to the injected synthetic anomalies, limiting its effectiveness for anomaly detection and
imputation.
To help mitigate this overfitting, true anomaly labels were included during training—not to im-
prove reconstruction accuracy directly, but to guide the model away from learning anomalous
patterns as normal. Despite this, LSTM 2’s reliance on labeled data reduces its suitability for
fully autonomous deployment in real-world settings, where such labels are typically unavail-
able. While approaches such as iterative self-labeling or internal anomaly flagging could help
overcome this limitation, they were not explored in this study.
Anomaly detection was evaluated using a residual-based method with a static z-score threshold,
which yielded limited sensitivity, particularly for low-amplitude or gradual anomalies. LSTM
1 generated more true detections, likely due to its noisier outputs, but lacked the reconstruction
quality needed for imputation. These outcomes likely reflect the constraints of the detection
methodology rather than the full potential of the models.
The synthetic anomaly framework proved useful for repeatable training and evaluation but is
constrained by its simplified assumptions. More realistic, ambiguous, and compound anomaly
types should be incorporated in future work to better reflect operational conditions.
In conclusion, this study demonstrates the potential of recurrent neural network models to
support automated QC of hydrological data, particularly for imputation tasks. However, fully
integrated detection and correction remains an open challenge. Future research should focus
on unsupervised detection strategies, more realistic anomaly modeling, and broader validation
across diverse hydrological settings.

1. Introduction
Environmental monitoring is essential for understanding and managing the dynamics of natural
systems. Globally, the deployment of environmental sensors has expanded significantly, with
approximately 26.6 million devices in operation as of 2024, a number projected to grow to 89.5
million by 2034 (Future Market Insights 2024). These sensors provide critical data that support
applications such as flood forecasting, water resource management, and climate monitoring.
In Denmark, the danish Agency for Green Transition and Aquatic Environment (Styrelsen
for Grøn Arealomlægning og Vandmiljø, SGAV) operates an extensive network of 424 hy-
drometric stations to monitor the nation’s water systems. This infrastructure is vital for both
immediate operational decisions and long-term strategic planning in water governance.
The Challenge of Data Quality and the Need for Automation
Despite their importance, environmental sensors are inherently susceptible to anomalies caused
by various factors such as sensor drift, biological interference, extreme weather events, equip-
ment malfunctions, and human error. These anomalies can significantly compromise the in-
tegrity of the data, leading to misleading insights and errors in downstream applications such
as hydrological forecasting and resource planning (Miller et al. 2025).
To ensure the reliability of sensor data, quality control (QC) is essential. Traditionally, QC has
been performed manually, involving visual inspection and correction of suspect time series.
However, this process is labor-intensive, time-consuming, and prone to subjectivity and relies
on expert domain knowledge. A comprehensive study involving 674 streamflow time series
and 43 evaluators revealed a low inter-evaluator agreement rate of just 12%, demonstrating
the variability and bias inherent in manual QC. Notably, the study also found that more anoma-
lies were reported during low-flow periods, suggesting seasonal biases or evaluator fatigue
(Strohmenger et al. 2023).
At SGAV, multiple full-time employees are currently dedicated to performing manual QC
across the national hydrometric network for up to 3 months. Given the growing volume and
complexity of environmental data, this approach is increasingly unsustainable. The repetitive
nature of the task, coupled with the limited scalability of human-centric workflows, highlights
the urgent need for automated QC systems (Strohmenger et al. 2023; Olawade et al. 2024).
Automation offers a path toward scalable, consistent, and efficient data validation. By lever-
aging machine learning and anomaly detection algorithms, automated systems can detect and
potentially correct anomalous data patterns, reducing the dependency on manual intervention
and improving the robustness of environmental monitoring programs (Strohmenger et al. 2023;
Jones et al. 2022).
Existing Approaches to Automated QC and Anomaly Detection
To address the limitations of manual quality control (QC) in environmental monitoring, a va-
riety of automated approaches have been developed. These methods fall broadly into three
categories: rule-based systems, statistical models, and machine learning (ML) techniques.
Rule-based systems operate on predefined logic, such as flagging values outside fixed thresh-
olds or detecting sudden jumps. These systems are relatively easy to implement and under-
stand but often fail to generalize to diverse and dynamic conditions, particularly in complex
==PAGE:15==
2
hydrological environments (Xaqt Team 2022). Importantly, it should also be noted that these
rules would require individual tuning for each sensor, which exacerbates the amount of work
required to set up these rule-based systems.
Statistical models, including approaches like ARIMA, attempt to describe the expected be-
havior of environmental variables based on historical patterns. These models are valued for
their simplicity and ability to quantify prediction uncertainty, making them useful for filter-
ing. However, they typically assume stationary behavior and linear relationships, assumptions
that often do not hold in small, flashy catchments like those found in Denmark, where abrupt
and nonlinear changes in water levels are common (Kaur et al. 2023). Moreover, their practi-
cal application usually requires clean input data, which can be a limiting factor in large-scale,
real-world deployments where automated preprocessing is not yet reliable.
Machine learning models offer a flexible, data-driven approach that can learn intricate, non-
linear patterns in time series data without requiring explicit modeling of physical processes,
which typically requires long calculations times (Albared et al. 2024). This makes them well-
suited for identifying anomalous behavior in complex systems such as environmental sensor
networks. Their capacity to model seasonality, delay effects, and other subtleties of temporal
dynamics provides a promising alternative to manual QC (Binetti et al. 2024).
However, many ML-based methods rely on assumptions that are not easily met in real-world
monitoring scenarios. These include the availability of large, clean, and well-labeled datasets,
as well as relatively stable conditions for training (Zhang et al. 2019; Zhao et al. 2020). In
practice, environmental data, especially from small and variable streams, tends to be noisy,
incomplete, and frequently influenced by exogenous disturbances such as sensor faults, veg-
etation growth, or manual maintenance. These challenges complicate training precise ML
models for automated QC.
Moreover, few existing approaches are designed to operate effectively when trained directly
on unclean or anomaly-polluted data, which is often the only available resource. While recent
work has explored methods that combine detection and imputation in a unified framework, their
robustness under real-world constraints, such as those faced by Danish hydrological monitor-
ing programs, remains an open question (Zhao et al. 2020). While obtaining clean training
data is possible, doing so typically requires extensive manual labor and financial resources,
constraints that are not always feasible for large-scale or continuous monitoring efforts.
Danish streams, in particular, pose unique challenges. They are typically small and highly
sensitive to localized hydrological and anthropogenic influences (Friberg and Murphy 2006).
Rapid water level changes are often driven by localized rainfall, aquatic vegetation (grøde)
that alters hydraulic resistance seasonally, and human interventions such as dam operations
or manual stream cleaning. These factors contribute to non-stationary, noisy time series with
abrupt, non-periodic fluctuations conditions under which traditional QC methods and many
existing ML models tend to underperform. A further complication arises from the fact that
genuine fluctuations in the data, resulting from natural or operational variability, can closely
resemble anomalous patterns. This increases the risk of false positives in automated anomaly
detection systems, as distinguishing between legitimate hydrological behavior and sensor or
recording errors becomes inherently difficult. Furthermore, labeled datasets required for su-
pervised approaches are scarce or non-existent in this context (Albared et al. 2024).

2. Related Work
Effective quality control (QC) of hydrological data requires two key components: a forecasting
model to represent expected system behavior, and a quality control method to identify and
manage deviations from it. This section reviews literature on both components, with a focus
on methods applied in catchment hydrology and their relevance to Danish stream monitoring.
Forecasting Approaches in Catchment Hydrology
Forecasting streamflow or water levels is a prerequisite for automated QC. Many recent stud-
ies have applied deep learning methods, particularly LSTMs, to model hydrological behavior
in both large and small river systems. While progress has been made, especially in larger
catchments, smaller streams present unique forecasting challenges.
Forecasting in Large Catchments
Several studies demonstrate strong performance of LSTM-
based models for streamflow forecasting in large river systems. For instance, Luo et al. 2025
introduced a classification-enhanced LSTM that achieved near-perfect NSE scores (0.98–0.99)
by integrating ensemble learning and upstream/downstream information. Similarly, Nevo et
al. 2022 deployed an operational ML-based flood forecasting system across South Asia, com-
bining LSTM-based predictions with classification and hydrodynamic models. These systems
perform well in stable, data-rich environments and benefit from consistent patterns and scale
effects present in large rivers. However, they generally rely on clean, labeled data and are not
designed for anomaly detection or imputation tasks.
Forecasting in Small Catchments
In contrast, small streams often exhibit rapid fluctuations
and irregular hydrological responses, making forecasting more difficult. Studies like Hunt et al.
2022 and Hu et al. 2020 highlight that while LSTM models can capture nonlinear dynamics in
small basins, they require extensive preprocessing and clean datasets. Albared et al. 2024 ap-
plied DL with uncertainty quantification for small rivers, but did not address data quality issues
or provide architectural transparency. These limitations make current forecasting approaches
less reliable in noisy or incomplete datasets typical of small stream monitoring networks.
Forecasting methods are mature and operationally deployed in large rivers with clean data.
However, for small, flashier systems like those in Denmark, model performance remains highly
sensitive to data quality, and robust forecasting under noisy conditions is still an open problem.
Anomaly Detection and Quality Control Methods
The second component in automated QC involves identifying and correcting anomalies in time
series data. Several machine learning-based strategies have emerged, aiming to detect errors
using statistical methods or model-based residuals.
Learning-Based QC Models
Miau and Hung 2020 proposed a Conv-GRU hybrid model
for detecting water level anomalies by learning spatial-temporal features and flagging residu-
als. While promising in scenarios without labeled anomaly data, the model still requires clean
training data and cannot fully distinguish natural fluctuations from anomalies without prior
filtering. Similarly, Kulanuwat et al. 2021 combined sliding window statistics with imputa-
tion methods, linear, spline, and bidirectional LSTM, to detect and repair anomalies. Their
==PAGE:17==
4
approach performed well in datasets with strong seasonality or identifiable patterns but was
less robust in irregular time series.
While several anomaly detection techniques exist, most assume the availability of either clean
training data or clear statistical separability between anomalies and signal. For Danish streams—
where true fluctuations can resemble anomalous behavior, existing methods fall short. A
more flexible, hybrid approach, potentially combining model-based forecasts with uncertainty-
aware detection strategies, is likely required.

Research Gaps and Questions

2.1. Research Gaps and Questions
The existing literature reveals several limitations that constrain the applicability of current
anomaly detection and forecasting methods in real-world hydrological monitoring—particularly
within the Danish context.
First, many machine learning models depend on clean, labeled datasets for training. This as-
sumption is rarely met in practice, which means a lot of resources are used in the aim of cleaning
datasets. Second, most approaches treat anomaly detection and forecasting as isolated tasks,
overlooking the operational benefit of models that can jointly identify and correct anomalous
values. Third, much of the research is tailored to large, stable river systems, with limited rel-
evance for the highly dynamic and irregular behavior of small Danish streams. Finally, few
studies address the operational constraints of national monitoring systems, such as scalability,
automation, and robustness to real-time data corruption.
Addressing these gaps, this thesis investigates the feasibility of a machine learning-based
framework for automated quality control of Danish stream water level data. The goal is to de-
velop methods that are both practically deployable and robust to the unique challenges posed
by noisy, unlabeled time series. In response to these challenges the thesis aims at answering
the following questions:
1. Does a recurrent machine learning architecture that distinguishes between observations and
model predictions as input outperform a standard LSTM architecture for anomaly detection?
2. Is it possible to develop a machine learning model that can simultaneously detect and impute
anomalies in water level time series without requiring clean training data?
3. How can a synthetic anomaly framework be designed to enable robust evaluation and train-
ing of anomaly detection models in the absence of labeled hydrological data?
4. Does incorporating input from nearby hydrometric stations improve predictive accuracy
compared to using single-station data?

3. Background
This section outlines the hydrological data correction workflow at SGAV (Styrelsen for Grøn
Arealomlægning og Vandmiljø), emphasizing the motivation for automation and identifying
critical sources of error in the current manual pipeline.
3.1. Motivation for Automation
The current manual correction process at SGAV is highly resource-intensive, requiring ap-
proximately 15 employees for up to three months each year to correct the past 1 years data. As
the agency faces increasing difficulties in recruiting staff for this labor-intensive and repetitive
task, automation of the process offers a promising solution. By applying machine learning tech-
niques, it is possible to reduce manual workload, improve the consistency of corrections, and
enhance the overall accuracy of the hydrological data. Automation would not only streamline
operations but also ensure more reliable data for critical applications such as flood forecasting
and water resource management.
The following sections provide a detailed description of SGAV’s data workflow, including data
collection, error identification, and correction procedures.
3.2. Workflow at SGAV
SGAV maintains and operates 424 hydrometric stations throughout Denmark. These stations
primarily record water levels using pressure sensors. To ensure data accuracy, these sensor
measurements are validated against manually recorded staff gauge readings and an annually
updated datum reference. The overall workflow is illustrated in Figure 1.
Figure 1: Overview of data correction pipeline at SGAV
Data Collection
Three types of relevant data are collected by SGAV: 1) Automatically recorded water levels
via pressure sensors, 2) Manually observed staff gauge readings, and 3) Datum measurements
establishing the reference elevation.
Technicians visit each station 18 times per year, monthly during summer and biweekly in winter.
During these visits, they read the staff gauge and compare it with sensor readings. If the
discrepancy exceeds 20 mm, the data logger is adjusted. If this occurs more than once a year,
further inspection of sensor installation is performed.
Datum measurements are performed annually using either differential GPS (with a theoreti-
cal accuracy of 20mm) or a relative measurement method based on a fixed local benchmark.
However, based on operational experience, SGAV has observed that the field accuracy of GPS

measurements tends to be closer to 40–50mm. As a result, SGAV is increasingly favoring the
benchmark-based method. Each year, the height difference between the fixed point and the
staff gauge is checked. If no change is detected, no further action is taken. If a discrepancy
is observed, new reference points for both the staff gauge and the fixed point are established
using GPS.
Each sensor records measurements every 15 minutes and transmits data every 1–4 hours via a
4G LTE modem (Ringgaard 2023).

Error Identification
Following data collection, manual inspection is conducted to identify anomalies in the sensor
data. The primary method involves comparing sensor values with staff gauge readings; dis-
crepancies exceeding 20 mm are flagged. The staff gauge is treated as the ground truth, and
sensor data are adjusted accordingly.
Multiple types of errors can affect data integrity. These are categorized below, following the
classification in Niels Bering Ovesen 2011.
1. Sensor Data Errors
Sensor errors may result from technical malfunctions (e.g., power failures, logger issues, or
restricted float movement), environmental conditions (e.g., ice formation, sedimentation, or
debris accumulation), or improper sensor placement. Although technicians assess sensor posi-
tioning during site visits, subjective judgment and environmental variability can still introduce
inaccuracies.
2. Staff Gauge Reading Errors
Despite being the reference measurement, staff gauge readings are susceptible to human er-
ror. These may include unit error, decimal misplacement, typographical errors, or inconsistent
reading techniques among technicians. Additionally, difficult access at some stations can com-
promise measurement precision. The accuracy of the measurement is influenced by the water
==PAGE:22==
9
surface conditions. Under calm water conditions, the precision can be as high as 2mm, whereas
in wavy or turbulent conditions, the uncertainty increases to approximately 2cm.
3. Datum Errors
Datum values, which serve as the reference elevation for water levels, are typically updated
once a year. Errors in datum is typically arising from the GPS inaccuracies.
Data Correction
The correction process adjusts sensor data based on flagged discrepancies. Typically, linear
interpolation is used to align sensor values with the most recent staff gauge reading. If a dis-
crepancy persists across several readings, the full interval is corrected. Physically implausible
values (e.g., negative water levels) are flagged, and, in some cases, erroneous data points are
removed rather than corrected.
If a shift in the datum is identified, both the sensor and staff gauge data are adjusted accordingly
using linear interpolation. The corrected datasets are preserved as a new time series reflecting
these adjustments.
This process is not immune to uncertainty. Linear interpolation may oversimplify complex
hydrological dynamics, and the involvement of multiple staff members can introduce subjec-
tivity in correction decisions. E.g. correction is supposed to be done only once a year, but some
employees chose to do this correction when a new staff gauge measurement is taken. This can
lead to overfitting to the staff gauge measurements. Additionally, missing data points are not
imputed, resulting in persistent gaps. As with many environmental time series, the inherent
complexity of water level data makes it difficult to detect all errors comprehensively.
The result of this process is a time series of corrected sensor data. Figure 3 illustrates the
difference between raw and corrected data alongside the manual staff gauge measurements as
well as threshold at which data is removed.
Database and Downstream Applications
Both raw and corrected datasets are stored in a public database accessible to external stake-
holders. The long-term vision is to consolidate all hydrometric data in Denmark within this
centralized repository. Organizations such as the Danish Meteorological Institute (DMI) uti-
lize the data for applications like flood forecasting (Ringgaard 2023).
Internally, SGAV employs the corrected water level measurements to develop rating curves for
discharge estimation. The broad range of downstream applications underscores the importance
of a robust quality assurance process that minimizes errors and ensures the highest possible data
quality.

4.6. Data Preprocessing
Before training the model, errors in vst raw which could be identified with simple statistical
methods were removed. This was done to avoid having to train a machine learning model
to find anomalies which easily can be found by a simpler statistical approach as well as avoid
confusing the model with data easily removed. In this way the model only has to find anomalies
which are more difficult for the domain expert to detect. The error types removed were flatlines,
spikes and periods with a certain amount of frost. Also point in vst raw below 0 was removed.
Statistics regarding removed data can be seen in Table 3. Further the data was aligned to have
a common timestamp as well as missing values were handled differently for the time series.
Lastly the data was splitted into training, validation, and test years as well as the data was
scaled.
This will be described in details below, in the order which they were performed.
Flatlines
are defined to be segments with identical values for 30 or more consecutive mea-
suring points.
Spikes
were detected by calculating the 25th and 75th percentiles of each station’s time series.
Dynamic thresholds were then set for each station, using 1.5 times the 25th percentile as the
lower boundary and 4 times the 75th percentile as the upper boundary. This approach allowed
the definition of spikes to adapt to the unique baseline of each station. The constants 1.5 and 4
were chosen through trial and error to best capture anomalous values. Additionally, a minimum
limit of 0 mm was imposed on the lower boundary, since water levels cannot logically fall
below zero.
Frost Periods
were identified based on cumulative sub-zero temperature readings. Specifi-
cally, periods in which the sum of temperatures remained below a predefined threshold were
considered frost events. A buffer period of 24 hours following each identified frost period was
also removed from the dataset to account for potential lagging effects. The threshold was ini-
tially set to -35 ◦C, based on the assumption that severe frost conditions may lead to anomalous
sensor behavior or unreliable measurements.
Alignment
of all the datasets to a common 15-minutes timestep was done. Both precipita-
tion and temperature data, which are recorded hourly, required resampling to align timestamps
across the datasets. Precipitation gaps were filled with -1 to prevent the model from learning
from missing values, as precipitation presence is crucial. Temperature data was resampled
using forward and backward filling, as its gradual changes make interpolation a reasonable
approximation. To create an aligned dataset with all features, vst raw was resampled by round-
ing each measurement to the nearest 15-minute mark. This ensures a uniform dataset with
timestamps at 8:00, 8:15, 8:30, 8:45, and so on. Further, the aligned dataset was cut to start
and end, where data is available for both precipitation, temperature, and vst raw. The start
date corresponds to the first valid precipitation measurement for station ’46, January 4, 2010
and end date is the last valid vst raw measurement, January 7, 2025. The fully preprocessed
dataset was then serialized and stored for downstream analysis.

Splitting and scaling
of the data was conducted chronologically by year to reflect the real-
world operational use case, in which data must be processed one year at a time. The dataset
was partitioned as follows: the training set included data from 2010 to 2021, the validation
set spanned 2022 to 2023, and the test set comprised the period from 2024 to early 2025. An
overview of this temporal split, along with the preprocessed stream water level data for station
’46, is shown in Figure 9.
Each subset serves a distinct purpose within the modeling workflow. The training set is used
to calculate the loss and update the model’s weights via backpropagation. The validation set is
used during training to monitor the model’s performance on unseen data, detect signs of over-
fitting, and support hyperparameter tuning and early stopping. Importantly, the test set is held
out entirely from the training process and is used only once at the end of model development
to evaluate final performance on truly unseen data, thereby simulating real-world deployment
conditions.
Following the data split, feature scaling was performed to ensure that all input variables had
comparable ranges. Specifically, standardization was applied, transforming each feature to
have a mean of zero and a standard deviation of one. Separate scalers were fitted on the
training set alone, and these fitted scalers were then applied to the validation and test sets to
prevent data leakage, which could otherwise bias the evaluation results.

5. Methodology
The objective of this thesis is to detect anomalies in stream water level time series obtained
from sensor measurements and provide corrected values for the anomalous periods. To achieve
this, a multi-step methodology has been developed, comprising the following components:
1. Synthetic Anomaly Framework: A synthetic anomaly framework was designed for
water level data. This enables testing of QC performance at different quality levels as
well as supporting training of models using data which include known anomalies.
2. Development of LSTM 1: A sequence to sequence LSTM model developed to predict
stream water levels with the aim of using the output to detect anomalies in the original
stream water level series.
3. Development of LSTM 2: An autoregressive LSTM forecaster model build using an
LSTM cell. The model switches between using original observed stream water level and
its own predictions as input.
4. Performance Assessment: Model performance is assessed using standard classification
metrics, vinge data, and synthetic anomaly framework.
5.1. Synthetic Anomaly Framework
In the absence of a clean, labeled dataset, the development of a synthetic anomaly framework
was necessary to facilitate the reliable evaluation and training of models for anomaly detection
and data-correction. The original dataset contains no explicit ground truth labels indicating
whether a given data point is erroneous. Moreover, in environmental time series, anomaly
classification often requires domain expertise and remains inherently subjective. Certain phe-
nomena, such as sudden drops in water level following aquatic vegetation removal, can closely
resemble errors despite being valid measurements. This ambiguity makes it difficult to assess
model performance based solely on observational data.
To address this challenge and avoid relying on subjective interpretations, synthetic anomalies
were systematically injected into the dataset. This approach facilitates controlled experiments
under known error conditions, enabling robust model evaluation.
The synthetic anomaly framework provides three key benefits:
1. Robustness evaluation: It enables systematic testing of model resilience under varying
levels and types of feature corruption.
2. Prediction quality assessment: It supports the evaluation of a model’s ability to recon-
struct the original water level signal without reproducing the injected anomalies.
3. Supervised training support: It allows for the generation of labeled anomaly flags
to facilitate supervised learning and evaluation through metrics such as the confusion

matrix, which is important for SGAV who are also interested in false positive rates.
Anomaly Types: Selection and Justification
To ensure realistic and relevant error simulation, an extensive visual inspection of the three
available water level time series was performed. This inspection identified seven recurring
anomaly types: missing data, offsets, baseline shifts, drifts, spikes, flatlines, and noise. Of
these, all but distinct noise patterns were clearly observed in the target series (station ’46).
Representative examples of the identified anomalies (excluding noise and flatlines) are illus-
trated in Figure 10.
Figure 10: Examples of anomalies identified in historical water level data from Station ’46. The graph show
missing data, offset, baseline shift, drift, and spike from the target station with both a zoomed vision as well as
the original placement of the anomaly. All examples shown are found through visual inspection.
From the identified error types, four were selected for synthetic injection based on frequency
of occurrence, visual clarity, and relevance for model evaluation.
1. Spikes: These anomalies were frequently observed in the datasets. While extreme
spikes are removed during preprocessing (defined as falling outside the range [1.5·Q1–
4·Q3]), moderate spikes remain. The injected anomalies replicate the characteristics of
these residual spikes, ensuring the framework reflects realistic data conditions.
2. Offsets: Present in all datasets, offsets are visually distinct and persist over time, adding
temporal complexity. Their inclusion is essential for testing a model’s ability to detect
longer-duration anomalies.
3. Drifts: The most commonly observed and edited anomaly by SGAV. Their gradual
onset and persistence make them especially challenging to detect without using vinge
observations, making them essential for evaluating temporal sensitivity.
4. Noise: Although not distinctly observed in the target station, noise is included to simu-
late distinct patterns commonly found in sensor data. Additionally, the framework must
also support feature stations where noise was observed.

5.3. LSTM 1, Sequence to Sequence Prediction Model
This section presents the development of an LSTM model using meteorological data to predict
stream water levels. The primary objective is to predict stream water level time series accu-
rately enough so that it is suitable for anomaly detection by afterwards computing z-scores and
applying threshold-based classification based on residuals. Additionally, the predictions are
intended to reconstruct missing or anomalous data points.
The model is implemented in PyTorch and follows a structured workflow encompassing archi-
tecture design, training configuration, and a series of experiments. A simplified architecture
was initially adopted to establish a baseline and enable systematic evaluation of additional
model components in later stages.
Figure 12 illustrates the overall structure of the many-to-many LSTM model, comprising input
features (yellow), LSTM layers (green), and output predictions (grey).
Figure 12: Simple architecture of a many to many LSTM model utilized in LSTM 1. Yellow indicate input,
green LSTM layer, and grey output to each timestep.
Model Architecture
The core of the model is a single-layer LSTM network with a hidden size of 128, followed by
a fully connected (FC) layer that maps the LSTM output to a single prediction per time step.
This architecture enables the model to capture temporal dependencies in the input data and
produce a continuous sequence of predictions aligned with the input sequence.
The input comprises two meteorological time series, precipitation and temperature, from the
target station. These were structured into a three-dimensional tensor with shape (1, 70080,
2), where 1 denotes the batch size, 70080 represents the full length of the validation dataset
(i.e., the total number of 15-min timesteps), and 2 corresponds to the number of input features.
Using the entire validation sequence in a single batch preserves temporal continuity, allowing
the model to learn long-range dependencies across the complete validation period without re-
initializing model states.
The LSTM layer processes the input tensor and outputs a tensor of shape (1, 70080, 128),

representing the hidden state at each time step. This is passed through the FC layer to produce
the final output tensor of shape (1, 70080, 1). The result is then flattened into a continuous
time series aligned with the original sequence which is used for anomaly detection using MAD
based z-score (see section 5.5). This described architecture is visualized in Figure 13 and will
be referred to as the baseline architecture.

Model Architecture 2
An extended version of the baseline architecture includes a ReLU activation function and an ad-
ditional fully connected (dense) layer. This is intended to introduce non-linearity and improve
the model’s capacity to learn hierarchical feature representations.
ReLU (Rectified Linear Unit) is a commonly used activation function defined as f(x) =
max(0, x). It enables the model to capture complex, non-linear relationships in the data by
allowing only positive activations to pass through. Compared to traditional activation func-
tions like sigmoid or tanh, ReLU helps mitigate the vanishing gradient problem and generally
leads to faster convergence during training (Singh 2024).

in equation 1, where n is the number of data points, yi is the true observation, and ˆyi is the

5.4. LSTM 2, Autoregressive Forecaster Model
This section presents LSTM 2, a custom autoregressive model designed for stream water level
forecasting and anomaly detection. In contrast to LSTM 1, which uses a pre-packaged LSTM
architecture, LSTM 2 is built around a single LSTM cell to provide greater control over se-
quence modeling and autoregressive behavior. The primary goal of LSTM 2 is to leverage the
advantages of autoregression for accurate forecasting, while avoiding the undesired replication
of anomalies present in the target data. The overall structure is illustrated in Figure 15.
Figure 15: A simple architectural overview of the autoregressive LSTM deployed in LSTM 2. The architecture
includes a fed back of the models own predictions every other week (t = [673, 2x673]).
The model is implemented in PyTorch and is structured as a many-to-many forecasting model
with iterative feedback. It accepts sequences of input features and generates predictions at each
timestep, feeding its own previous outputs back into future inputs during training.
Model Architecture
The core architecture of LSTM 2 consists of a single LSTM cell with a hidden size of 24. This
is followed by a fully connected (FC) layer that maps the LSTM’s hidden state to a single
output value at each timestep. The model processes inputs sequentially: at each step, it takes
==PAGE:48==
5.4
LSTM 2, Autoregressive Forecaster Model
35
the current input along with the hidden and cell states from the previous timestep. This enables
temporal memory retention and sequential modeling of the water level dynamics.
A central characteristic of the model is its autoregressive design. Specifically, the model in-
cludes the target variable, vst raw, as one of the input features. While this enhances short-term
forecasting accuracy, it increases the risk of overfitting, especially to anomalous target values.
To counter this, an alternating bi-weekly input mechanism is used during training:
• Week 1: The model receives the original feature values, including the vst raw target.
• Week 2: The model uses its own previous predictions in place of the target feature.
• This cycle continues throughout training.
This weekly alternation continues throughout training but is not applied during validation. That
is, as it is expected that the best reconstruction results will be obtained through original values
only. The goal is to simulate a cleaner training distribution by limiting the frequency with
which anomalies in vst raw are exposed to the model.
The alternating pattern serves two purposes:
1. Mitigating overfitting to the raw vst feature, which contain anomalies or measurement
noise.
2. Reducing prediction drift that commonly occurs in models relying solely on autoregres-
sive inputs.
Due to the bi-weekly alternation, the batch size is constrained to cover at least two full weeks of
data (2·672 = 1344 time steps), enabling the model to observe both original and autoregressive
inputs during each sequence. The initial batch size was set to 10·672 + warm up for stability.

Memory Protection Mechanism
To address degradation of the internal memory during extended anomaly periods (such as drifts
and offsets), a memory protection mechanism was implemented. This mechanism aims to
preserve a ”clean” cell state that serves as a reference for stabilizing model behavior during
corrupted intervals.
The mechanism works as follows
• During normal periods, the current cell state is stored as a clean reference
• During flagged anomaly periods, predictions are generated using a blended cell state,
computed as:
70% preserved clean memory
30% current state (potentially corrupted)
• An exponential smoothing filter (α = 0.1) is applied to avoid abrupt shifts in the cell
state.
Binary anomaly flags control whether memory protection is active. This strategy allows the
model to retain useful temporal dependencies while minimizing contamination from erroneous
sequences. The mechanism was tested using the synthetic anomaly labels and would require
integration with a real-time anomaly detection system for deployment.

Experiment 2: Modifying alternating pattern
Building on insights from Experiment 1, Ex-

overfitting to anomalous inputs. The baseline approach alternated weekly (672 timesteps) be-

or amplifying exposure bias in the presence of anomalies.
Experiment 3: Extending Alternation to Validation
While the alternation pattern was orig-
inally applied only during training, Experiment 3 tests whether applying the same strategy
during validation could improve generalization and reduce the replication of anomalies.
The exclusion of alternation in the validation phase was based on the assumption that clean
data simulation during training would suffice for learning general patterns, and that original
values should be used during validation to avoid compounding prediction errors. However,
previous experiments demonstrated that despite high accuracy, the model still reproduced input
anomalies, suggesting that the underlying training approach may not sufficiently discourage
overfitting to anomalies.
Therefore, this experiment applies the same alternating input strategy in the validation phase
to align training and validation dynamics. The aim of this experiment is therefore to determine
whether extending the alternation pattern to the validation phase can mitigate overfitting to
erroneous target data while maintaining high predictive accuracy.

This exper-

However, early results indicated that the inclusion of anomaly flags as input alone was insuf-

(1) a dynamically weighted MAE loss that reduces learning from corrupted targets, and (2) a
pattern penalty that discourages following abrupt or irregular changes typical of anomalies.

rectly imitating the target series during anomalous intervals. While this supervised approach
assumes access to perfect labels, an unrealistic condition in practice, it offers valuable insight

indicators are available. In real-world settings, the feasibility of this approach depends on the
availability of reliable anomaly detection methods, rather than perfect synthetic flags.

5.5. Anomaly Detection
Anomaly detection was applied to the output of the best-performing configurations of both
LSTM models, with the goal of identifying sequences in the original time series that should
be replaced with model-generated predictions. This dual-purpose highlights the critical impor-
tance of both accurate forecasting and reliable anomaly identification within the SGAV use
case.
The detection strategy employed a modified z-score based on the Mean Absolute Deviation
(MAD), computed over a sliding window of 16 time steps (equivalent to approximately 4 hours)
initially. This method was selected for its easy implementation as well as robustness to outliers

and its ability to adapt dynamically to local fluctuations in the residual time series. The rela-
tively short window was inspired by findings from Kulanuwat et al. 2021, which recommend
smaller windows for volatile or noisy datasets, such as hydrometric records.
A threshold of 5 was selected through visual inspection to strike a practical balance between
sensitivity (detecting actual anomalies) and specificity (minimizing false alarms). Different
combinations of window size and threshold were tested to evaluate their influence on detection
performance, with results summarized in Table 29. However, no extensive grid search or
statistical optimization of the threshold was performed, which is acknowledged as a potential
area for further improvement.
To improve interpretability, the framework introduced three confidence levels based on the
magnitude of the z-score:
Low confidence: z-score ∈[threshold, 1.5·threshold]
Medium confidence: z-score ∈[1.5·threshold, 2·threshold]
High confidence: z-score > 2·threshold
These confidence levels were used solely for visualization and interpretation purposes. It is im-

To enable model evaluation under controlled conditions, synthetic anomalies were injected into
the validation set. This allowed for a standardized ground truth to assess detection performance,
while ensuring that model training occurred exclusively on raw data. However, this setup was
only applicable to the unsupervised model, LSTM 1.
In contrast, the best-performing version of LSTM 2 employed a supervised learning strategy,
which required access to anomaly labels during training. Consequently, the model could not
be trained solely on clean data, leading to a divergence in the experimental conditions. This
methodological distinction should be carefully considered when comparing performance be-
tween the two model types.

distributed data, although a correction factor k=1.4826 is applied to make it comparable to the

5.6. Model Performance Assessment
To evaluate the performance of the proposed models, a combination of quantitative metrics and
qualitative analysis is employed. This multifaceted approach is necessary because different
models may perform better on different aspects of the prediction task, and no single metric can
capture all relevant dimensions of model quality.
During training, validation loss is monitored to assess generalization performance and detect
overfitting. For post-training evaluation, three primary metrics are used: Root Mean Squared
Error (RMSE), Mean Absolute Error (MAE), and the Nash–Sutcliffe Efficiency coefficient
(NSE). These metrics were selected because they each highlight different characteristics of
prediction error and are commonly used in hydrological modeling literature.
• RMSE quantifies the average magnitude of prediction error by computing the square
root of the mean of squared differences between predicted and observed values. It is
sensitive to large errors due to the squaring term, making it useful when large deviations
are particularly undesirable. RMSE is measured in the same units as the target variable.
• MAE measures the average absolute difference between predicted and observed values.
Unlike RMSE, it weights all errors equally and is therefore more robust to outliers. MAE
is also expressed in the same units as the target and provides an intuitive measure of
average prediction error.
• NSE (Nash–Sutcliffe Efficiency) is a normalized statistic that compares the predictive
power of a model to that of a simple mean-based predictor. NSE values range from −∞
to 1, where 1 indicates a perfect match between predictions and observations, and values
less than 0 indicate that the mean of the observed data is a better predictor than the model.
NSE is included to be able to compare between studies (Luo et al. 2025).
In addition to these numerical indicators, visual inspection plays a critical role in evaluating
model effectiveness, especially given the context of anomaly detection. The primary objective
is not merely to minimize error, but to ensure that the model does not replicate anomalies
present in the raw sensor data. Therefore, visual comparisons are conducted between model
predictions, the raw sensor data (vst raw), and the manually observed staff gauge readings
(vinge), which are treated as the ground truth. This process helps identify whether a model
is falsely learning to reproduce data errors, and whether it can smooth or correct apparent
anomalies.
Furthermore, model performance is evaluated within the anomaly detection framework (see
Section 5.1). This includes the injection of synthetic anomalies for three distinct evaluation
purposes:
1. Robustness testing, to determine the model’s resilience to anomalies.
2. Assessment of prediction quality in the presence of known anomalies.
3. Support for supervised training, enabling the calculation of classification metrics such
as precision, recall, and F1 score.
Classification metrics such as the ones mentioned above are calculated from the confusion
matrix. The confusion matrix provides detailed insight into anomaly detection performance by
reporting true positives (TP), true negatives (TN), false positives (FP), and false negatives (FN).
This is especially relevant in the SGAV context, where false positives, i.e., mistakenly flagging
==PAGE:57==
44
correct data as erroneous, can result in unnecessary data corrections and the potential loss of
valid information. Minimizing such errors is therefore a key criterion in model evaluation.
Precision (Equation 11) measures the proportion of correctly identified anomalies out of all
instances the model labeled as anomalies. High precision indicates a low rate of false positives,
which is particularly important in this use case to avoid undue modifications to valid data.

considered simultaneously (Kashyap 2024).
By integrating both quantitative and qualitative assessment methods, this framework aims to
identify models that not only perform well statistically but are also practically applicable to
SGAV’s operational needs.

Experiment 5: Memory Protection Mechanism
The memory protection mechanism de-
scribed in Section 5.4.1 was implemented and tested to address the limitations observed in Ex-
periment 4, particularly the model’s tendency to have its memory corrupted during extended
anomalous periods.
The memory protection mechanism demonstrated improvements over Experiment 4, as evi-
denced in comparative visualizations across the full validation year (Figure 26b) and detailed
subplot analyses during specific anomalous periods (Figure 27). However, the effectiveness of
this approach varies significantly depending on the nature of the synthetic error type, revealing
important insights about the mechanism’s underlying assumptions and limitations. For offset
anomalies, the memory protection mechanism shows clear improvement (figures 27a and 27b).
The model predictions (green) in Experiment 5 maintain much better alignment with the orig-
inal data (blue) compared to Experiment 4, where predictions followed the corrupted offset
data more closely. This improvement stems from offset errors being essentially ”correct data
at wrong levels” - they preserve temporal relationships and patterns while only shifting the ab-
solute baseline. The memory blending strategy (70% historical good memory + 30% current
information) successfully maintains more of the valid temporal dynamics while reducing ad-
herence to the incorrect baseline shift. Noise anomalies demonstrated notable improvements
with some remaining challenges. Comparing figures 27c and 27d, the memory protection
mechanism produces more stable predictions that better resist the erratic fluctuations present
in the corrupted data. The multi-segment structure of synthetic noise errors, which combine
offset-like baseline shifts with high-frequency variations, creates a challenging scenario where
the memory protection must simultaneously handle multiple ”new normals” within a single
anomaly period. While the mechanism effectively reduces sensitivity to high-frequency noise
==PAGE:77==
6.2
LSTM 2
64
components, some influence from corrupted data patterns remained. However, drift anomalies
revealed a fundamental limitation of the approach. Figures 27e and 27f show that while Exper-
iment 5 predictions are more conservative, they fail to track the gradual changes appropriately,
creating larger gaps between predictions and the true underlying trend. Unlike offset errors,
drift anomalies involve gradual changes that may represent legitimate system evolution rather
than pure corruption. The memory protection mechanism’s assumption that preserving histori-
cal ”good” states is always beneficial conflicts with scenarios requiring progressive adaptation.
The 70% retention of old memory prevents the necessary gradual adjustment to changing con-
ditions, resulting in predictions that systematically lag behind actual system evolution. This
reveals a critical design trade-off: the very mechanism that protects against corruption also
inhibits legitimate adaptation to gradual environmental changes.
The residual effects observed across all anomaly types could potentially be minimized by ad-
justing the memory decay parameter, though this introduces a complex optimization challenge.
A lower decay parameter (e.g., 10% new information) might better handle offset and noise er-
rors but would exacerbate drift tracking problems, while a higher parameter would improve
drift adaptation at the cost of corruption vulnerability during offset anomalies.
While the memory protection approach showed marked enhancement over the baseline anomaly-
aware loss, several fundamental limitations constrain its effectiveness. Beyond parameter sen-
sitivity, the approach’s reliance on ground truth anomaly labels from the synthetic framework
represents a critical practical limitation. In real deployment scenarios, robust unsupervised
anomaly detection methods such as MAD z-score analysis, isolation forests, or other statisti-
cal outlier detection techniques would be essential prerequisites, introducing additional com-
plexity and potential error propagation that must be carefully evaluated in system design. The
inclusion of weighted loss function (Experiment 4) and memory protection (Experiment 5)
caused a slight decrease in conventional performance metrics as seen in table 28. However,
this reduction should not be interpreted negatively, as it likely reflects the model’s improved
resistance to following anomalous patterns rather than a degradation in predictive capability.
The varying effectiveness across different anomaly types suggests that future work might ben-
efit from adaptive memory protection strategies that adjust their behavior based on detected
anomaly characteristics.

6.3. Overall Results From the Two Models
The results in Table 28 show that the autoregressive LSTM (LSTM 2) consistently outperforms
the standard LSTM (LSTM 1) across all predictive metrics, achieving lower RMSE and higher
NSE scores. This performance advantage reflects LSTM 2’s autoregressive structure, which
allows it to leverage past predictions for more accurate forecasting.
However, this predictive strength also introduces a fundamental limitation for anomaly detec-
tion. LSTM 2’s reliance on previous outputs can cause it to replicate anomalies present in the
input, reducing its capacity to distinguish between normal and anomalous behavior. To address
this, supervised strategies incorporating true anomaly flags were introduced, i.e. weighted loss
functions and memory protection mechanisms. These enhancements improved robustness, but
they also introduced a dependency on accurate anomaly labels during training.
This reliance on ground-truth anomaly flags undermines the feasibility of using LSTM 2 as a
true 2-in-1 model. If anomalies must be known in advance to enable supervised training, post
hoc anomaly detection becomes redundant. While LSTM 2 shows strong potential for jointly
detecting and correcting anomalies, realizing this potential would require an internal anomaly
detection mechanism that operates iteratively during training, an approach not explored in this
study.
In contrast, LSTM 1, while less accurate overall, does not rely on supervised anomaly labels,
making it more aligned with unsupervised detection tasks, albeit at the cost of lower recon-
struction performance. The next section explores the implications of these differences in the
context of applied anomaly detection.

6.4. Anomaly Detection
This section evaluates the anomaly detection capabilities of LSTM 1 and LSTM 2 when paired
with a residual-based method using a modified z-score (MAD). Detection was performed on
model residuals, with synthetic anomalies serving as ground truth anomalies. The results are
summarized in Table 29, and illustrated in Figures 28 and 29.
Among the tested parameter combinations, a threshold of 5 and a window size of 150 timesteps
(about 40 hours) yielded the highest F1 scores and was therefore used for final evaluation.
Anomalies were identified based on local deviations from the median residual, with detected
points color-coded by confidence level, and synthetic anomalies shown as blue-shaded regions
in the figures.
Overall, detection performance was modest for both models, and results should be interpreted
with caution. LSTM 1 achieved higher F1 scores and detected more synthetic anomalies than
LSTM 2. This is likely a consequence of its noisier predictions, which produce larger residuals
that are more likely to trigger anomaly flags. In contrast, LSTM 2’s smoother and more accu-
rate reconstructions, while beneficial for forecasting, often produce smaller residuals, limiting
their effectiveness for threshold-based detection—particularly for subtler patterns such as drift
or offset.
Figures 28 and 29 help illustrate these tendencies. Both LSTM 1 and LSTM 3 appears to flag
sharp, isolated anomalies but struggles with more complex, low-amplitude deviations. LSTM
2, while avoiding some injected anomalies in its predictions, often fails to generate a strong
enough signal for reliable detection. In some cases, the model even reproduces corrupted
patterns, further complicating residual-based identification.
This highlights both architectural and methodological limitations. The static detection thresh-
old, which was not thoroughly explored, is unlikely to generalize well to real-world data with
varying dynamics.
Alternative strategies such as adaptive thresholding, validation-based tuning, or probabilistic
approaches could potentially improve sensitivity and reduce false positives. Methods such as
grid search Kulanuwat et al. 2021, context-aware statistical techniques Zhao et al. 2020, or
anomaly-informed training loops Zhang et al. 2019 represent promising directions.
It is also important to note that evaluation was limited to synthetic anomalies. Real anomalies in
the raw dataset, some of which may closely resemble natural variability, remain unlabeled and
were excluded from scoring. This likely underestimates both the challenges and the practical
performance of the models in operational settings.
In conclusion, neither LSTM 1 nor LSTM 2, when paired with this basic residual-based ap-
proach, meets the requirements for robust and reliable 2-in-1 anomaly detection and imputa-
tion. LSTM 1 is unsuited for correction tasks due to weak reconstructions, while LSTM 2
depends on externally provided anomaly labels during training. Future efforts should prior-
itize integrated or semi-supervised detection mechanisms that adapt to real-world conditions
and reduce reliance on static thresholds.

7. Discussion
7.1. Key Insights
This thesis explored whether an LSTM-based model could simultaneously detect and correct
anomalies in Danish stream water level data, a “2-in-1” approach designed to reduce SGAV’s
reliance on manual quality control. To overcome the absence of labeled real-world anoma-
lies, a synthetic anomaly injection framework was developed to enable controlled training and
evaluation of model behavior under corrupted conditions.
Two model types were investigated: LSTM 1, a sequence-to-sequence architecture using addi-
tional hydrometric stations, and LSTM 2, an autoregressive model relying mostly on its own
past outputs. Across experiments, LSTM 2 demonstrated stronger reconstruction accuracy and
greater robustness to noisy auxiliary inputs, but both models revealed key design trade-offs
limiting their suitability for true 2-in-1 deployment.
Synthetic Anomaly Framework
The synthetic anomaly framework enabled systematic evaluation and training of anomaly de-
tection models in the absence of real labeled data. It provided controlled, reproducible setups
for assessing model robustness, learning behavior, and detection performance. However, the
framework is inherently constrained by the assumptions embedded in the anomaly design: the
types, durations, and magnitude ranges of injected anomalies reflect a limited understanding
of real-world sensor failures and hydrological anomalies.
Moreover, excluding ambiguous phenomena such as baseline shifts, where it is unclear whether
a deviation represents sensor error or true hydrological change, limits the realism of the train-
ing distribution. As such, while synthetic anomalies are essential for development, future

70
frameworks should better reflect real-world uncertainty, ambiguity, and compound behavior.
Model Design Trade-Offs
LSTM 1 provided contextual information by incorporating nearby stations but performed poorly
in reconstruction, even though it did manage to adjust to the signal and mimic trends from the
target data. Its architecture proved fragile and ultimately unsuitable for imputation. How-
ever, its residuals, due to being more volatile, resulted in higher detection scores under the
MAD-based method. That said, this likely reflects the limitations of the detection approach,
particularly the use of static thresholds, rather than a meaningful advantage in detection capa-
bility.
LSTM 2, by contrast, achieved consistently better reconstruction and forecasting performance.
But this came at the cost of reduced anomaly detection sensitivity: the model often replicated
anomalies, particularly when trained without safeguards such as the weighted loss function or
memory protection. When trained with true anomaly flags, LSTM 2 learned to avoid corrupted
data but became reliant on accurate labels during training. This creates a contradiction for 2-
in-1 usage: if true anomaly flags are already known, downstream anomaly detection becomes
potentially redundant. The model’s potential as a 2-in-1 solution thus depends on integrating
internal or iterative anomaly flagging, an approach not explored in this study.
In summary, LSTM 1 is not suited for a dual-purpose role due to weak reconstruction, and
LSTM 2, while more capable of accurate imputation, is constrained by its dependence on
true anomaly labels. A true 2-in-1 model would require internal, iterative anomaly detection
mechanisms to function in an unsupervised or semi-supervised setting.
7.2. Methodological Limitations
While this study demonstrates potential for anomaly detection in hydrological time series, sev-
eral methodological limitations must be acknowledged. These limitations affect both the ro-
bustness of the conclusions and the generalizability of the approach.
The dataset used in this project was limited to only three hydrometric stations within the same
geographical region. This narrow scope restricts the generalizability of the results to other
catchments or hydrological regimes. Additionally, precipitation data, an important input fea-
ture, was only available from 2010 onward, reducing the length of the training history and
potentially limiting the model’s ability to learn long-term patterns.
Feature preprocessing was minimal. The precipitation and temperature inputs were not cleaned
prior to use, and the water level features (vst raw ’45 and ’47) underwent only basic prepro-
cessing. As a result, the models, particularly LSTM 1, may have been exposed to noise and
inconsistencies in the input data, leading to reduced predictive performance. Furthermore, the
target time series contained significant gaps, especially in the early years, which could impair
the model’s ability to learn meaningful temporal patterns.
When evaluating the effect of anomalies in LSTM 1’s feature stations, a fixed random seed was
not applied. As a result, the placement and magnitude of injected anomalies varied randomly
across different error multiplier settings. This introduces inconsistency in the experimental
conditions, potentially leading to unequal levels of disturbance across configurations. Conse-
quently, the observed performance degradation may reflect the randomness of injection rather
than the systematic effect of corrupted features, limiting the reliability of conclusions drawn

from this experiment.
Due to the absence of clean, labeled anomaly data, a synthetic anomaly framework was im-
plemented. However, the design of this framework relied on visual inspection by non-domain
experts, introducing subjectivity and potential bias. The selection of which anomaly types
to include was based on perceived visibility rather than systematic criteria, which may limit
the representativeness of the synthetic anomalies. Moreover, since evaluation metrics were
largely based on synthetic data, the results may not fully reflect the models’ effectiveness in
identifying real-world anomalies. No classification of anomaly types was performed, which
could otherwise provide insights into model sensitivity to different anomaly characteristics or
prevalence across stations.
The LSTM 2 model exhibited a tendency to overfit to autoregressive inputs, often reproduc-
ing anomalies present in the input data rather than learning the underlying hydrological and
meteorological patterns. To address this, the model was trained using true anomaly flags de-
rived from synthetic data. However, this reliance on labeled anomaly information presents
a challenge for real-world deployment, where such labels are rarely available. The memory
protection mechanism, designed to shield the model from learning during anomalous periods,
also showed limitations—particularly during prolonged anomaly sequences. In these cases,
the stored ”clean” memory became outdated, degrading the model’s ability to recover once the
anomaly ended. This was especially evident in drift anomaly scenarios.
Several critical parameters, such as the memory decay rate and anomaly detection threshold,
were fixed throughout the experiments. While these static values offered a controlled setup
for testing, they may not be optimal across all contexts. An adaptive approach to thresholding
or memory management could potentially enhance robustness and generalizability, especially
under variable data conditions.
7.3. Outlook
Contribution and Further Work
This work demonstrates several methodological contributions beyond basic feasibility: (1) a
synthetic anomaly framework enabling controlled evaluation without labeled real-world data,
(2) alternating training serving as an alternative to the standard autoregressive approach, po-
tentially leading to less overfitting to own input, and (3) anomaly-aware loss functions that
enable learning from partially corrupted training data.
While LSTM 1 proved impractical due to its reliance on clean auxiliary inputs and generally
poor NSE scores, LSTM 2 showed more promising results and provides a proof-of-concept
for anomaly-resistant forecasting. Although not yet deployment-ready, LSTM 2 highlights
key trade-offs in learning from noisy data and managing uncertainty in prediction. Its rela-
tive success in reconstructing realistic water level series indicates that with further refinement,
especially in the anomaly detection phase itself, a system like this could help reduce manual
labor in SGAV’s QC process.
The models were only evaluated on a small number of stations, but the methodology is gener-
alizable. With improvements to anomaly realism and better handling of auxiliary input data,
this approach could be scaled to SGAV’s national monitoring network. This would support
more consistent correction workflows and enhance the quality of long-term data archives.

Future Work
1. Use of Interpolation for Small Gaps: Incorporating linear or spline interpolation to
fill short gaps in the target series could improve model stability and reduce unnecessary
anomaly flags during training.
2. Improve Auxiliary Data Quality: Auxiliary inputs such as temperature and precipita-
tion should be cleaned to handle potential sensor noise or missing values. Their quality
can directly affect model predictions and anomaly detection reliability.
3. Move Beyond Perfect Anomaly Labels: Future versions could integrate unsupervised
methods such as median absolute deviation (MAD) z-scores or isolation forests to detect
anomalies without requiring exact ground truth. These methods better reflect real-world
uncertainty, though they require additional complexity, such as tuning thresholds and
managing false positives.
4. Confidence-Based Training: Implement probabilistic anomaly detection that provides
confidence scores rather than binary flags, enabling gradient-based transitions between
normal and protected processing modes rather than abrupt switches for LSTM 2.
5. Add Penalized Detection with BCE Loss: A binary detection head trained with binary
cross-entropy (BCE) loss could be added to explicitly penalize undetected anomalies.
This approach introduces challenges, including the need for labeled anomaly flags, class
imbalance, threshold tuning, and careful balancing of loss functions. Nevertheless, it
could improve interpretability and detection performance.
6. Anomaly Threshold tuning: An adaptive threshold strategy could be implemented for
the final anomaly detection to allow for dynamically changing when anomalies are found
In the broader context, this thesis supports the idea that automated anomaly detection in hydro-
metric data is not only feasible but scalable, provided that models can be made more robust
to imperfect data and uncertain labels. With continued refinement, systems of this kind could
reduce manual workload and increase consistency in the national hydrological monitoring net-
work.

8. Conclusion
This study investigated the potential for automating quality control in Danish stream water
level time series using machine learning. It focused on evaluating model architectures, com-
pensating for the lack of labeled anomaly data, and exploring whether a single model could
support both anomaly detection and imputation. Key components were assessed in terms of
robustness, operational feasibility, and methodological constraints. The research addressed
the following core questions:
1. Does a recurrent machine learning architecture that distinguishes between obser-
vations and model predictions as input outperform a standard LSTM architecture
for anomaly detection?
The autoregressive architecture (LSTM 2), which alternates between observed and pre-
dicted values during training, demonstrated more stable reconstruction and superior per-
formance across forecasting metrics compared to the standard LSTM (LSTM 1). Its de-
sign, including memory protection and anomaly-aware loss weighting, helped mitigate
overfitting to corrupted data. However, when evaluated with a simple residual-based
method for anomaly detection, LSTM 1 produced higher detection scores, likely a result
of its noisier predictions creating more threshold, crossing residuals. This highlights
that improved reconstruction does not directly translate to better anomaly detection un-
der static, threshold-based methodologies. The findings suggest that architecture alone
does not determine detection performance; the choice of detection strategy is equally
critical. It should also be noted that the overall detection method was relatively weak,
and this likely contributed to the underperformance of LSTM 2 in anomaly detection.
2. Is it possible to develop a machine learning model that can simultaneously detect
and impute anomalies in water level time series without requiring clean training
data?
The study shows that partial integration of detection and imputation is achievable. LSTM
2, when trained with synthetic anomaly labels, can learn to ignore corrupted data and re-
construct clean output. However, its dependence on external flags prevents it from oper-
ating independently as a 2-in-1 model. In the absence of labeled training data, the model
risks learning from faulty inputs. This dependency underlines a key limitation: while
the architecture supports imputation, the lack of integrated detection means the model
cannot function autonomously. Realizing a fully unified detection-correction system
would most likely require prior, internal or iterative anomaly identification mechanisms,
which remain a promising avenue for future work.
3. How can a synthetic anomaly framework be designed to enable robust evaluation
and training of anomaly detection models in the absence of labeled hydrological
data?
The synthetic anomaly framework developed in this study allowed controlled injection
of common anomaly types, spikes, drifts, offsets, and noise based on patterns found
in real Danish water level data. This enabled structured model evaluation and super-
vised training despite the lack of labeled real-world anomalies. However, limitations
were noted: the anomaly types were defined heuristically, ambiguous behaviors were
excluded, and random seeds were not fixed in all experiments, introducing variability.
While not fully reflective of real-world uncertainty, the framework nonetheless provided
a valuable foundation for evaluation and supervised training support.
==PAGE:87==
74
4. Does incorporating input from nearby hydrometric stations improve predictive ac-
curacy compared to using single-station data?
Including spatial context via nearby stations (as in LSTM 1) showed benefits under clean
input conditions by leveraging correlated hydrological signals. However, when anoma-
lies were introduced into feature stations, the model’s performance degraded, demon-
strating its sensitivity to input quality. This fragility makes multi-station setups less re-
liable in real-world applications where upstream sensors may themselves be corrupted.
The single-station autoregressive model (LSTM 2) avoided this issue, offering more ro-
bustness at the cost of losing contextual sensitivity. These findings suggest that while
multi-station input can improve prediction, it must be used cautiously and in conjunction
with strong input validation mechanisms.
In summary, this study shows that recurrent machine learning models—particularly autore-
gressive designs like LSTM 2, offer promising tools for automated quality control of hydro-
logical data. The synthetic anomaly framework supported structured evaluation in the absence
of labeled data, and the architectural modifications explored helped improve model robust-
ness. Still, neither model fully satisfies the requirements of a unified detection and imputation
system, largely due to the limitations of external anomaly flagging and the simplicity of the
detection method used.
Future progress will require integrating more adaptive detection mechanisms directly into
model training, improving the realism of synthetic anomalies, and ensuring robustness to noisy
auxiliary inputs. These developments will be essential for scaling machine learning-based QC
to operational settings and enabling consistent, efficient management of national hydrological
datasets.
