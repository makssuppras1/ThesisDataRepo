Abstract
The market for indoor asset tracking systems has grown significantly in recent years as
there are clear economic benefits in deploying such systems in retail, logistics, health-
care, and other industries. Most of the currently used systems use Angle of Arrival or
Time Difference of Arrival localisation methods which imply high hardware costs and can
not be implemented on fully battery-powered networks. On the other hand, localisation
solutions based on the Received Signal Strength Indicator method generally suffer from
low localisation performance.
This thesis explored possibilities of improving Received Signal Strength Indicator locali-
sation by using a mesh network with a high number of devices for asset tracking. Unlike
traditional localisation methods, mesh-network-based algorithms can utilise all node-to-
node ranging data in the network, not just the anchor-to-asset connections. An anchor is
a node with a fixed known location.
Several mesh-network-based algorithms were developed by modification and extension
of the state-of-the-art RSSI-based localisation algorithms. They were tested in a low-
anchor-density environment where they needed to rely on mesh network data, as many
nodes did not have a direct connection to an anchor. The best performing algorithm vari-
ant involving Two-Anchor Localisation, Localisation Loos Function, Boundary Conditions,
and Gradient Descend methods localised assets within 2.92 m of accuracy in lab condi-
tions. This represents only a 6.1 % decrease in performance compared to state-of-the-art
algorithms in a high-anchor-density environment, which achieved an accuracy of 2.75 m
in our experiment with the same hardware.
The newly developed algorithms were additionally tested in a real-life scenario of an office
building in which the accuracy degraded to 12.42 m and Gradient Descend optimisation
needed to be removed from the algorithm due to its noise sensitivity. Lastly, ways to
improve the developed algorithm’s robustness in future work were suggested.

Introduction
1.1
Importance of indoor localisation
The proliferation of position and location tracking systems has proven to be greatly ben-
eficial for both private industries and governmental organisations around the world. The
Global Positioning System (GPS), which was originally developed for the needs of the
U.S. military, is estimated to have generated 1.4 trillion USD in economic benefits since
1984, when it was made available for the public, in the U.S. alone [1]. However, a major
limitation of GPS and other satellite-based positioning systems is that they are not avail-
able inside buildings. Expanding the position location services to indoor environments
presents huge economic potential. Examples of indoor asset tracking applications are
the localisation of goods in logistic centres or retail, localisation of equipment in hospitals
and other facilities, localisation of IoT devices, tracking of personnel for security and ac-
cess control, indoor navigation, etc. Therefore, there has been a growing market demand
for Indoor Positioning Systems (IPS) in recent years.
1.2
Research problem introduction
Most of the currently commercially available IPSs are based on the Angle of Arrival or
Time Difference of Arrival method which requires precise installation and hardware capa-
ble of precise measurements. This leads to high costs of installation and hardware. An
alternative to this approach is a system based on the Received Signal Strength Indica-
tor (RSSI). RSSI-based IPSs are more cost efficient but their performance characteristics
(e.g., accuracy, precision, robustness) are inferior.
One of the proposed ways these shortcomings can potentially be overcome is by using a
mesh network with a high number of devices. In a traditional IPS, the location of assets

1.3
Project objectives
The goal of this thesis is to explore the introduced problem and to that end following
objectives are defined:
1. Research existing commercial and state-of-the-art indoor asset tracking solutions
as well as technologies and algorithms they are based on.
2. Define a use case for mesh-network-based indoor asset tracking and its minimum
requirements.
3. Evaluate performance and find optimal parameters of traditional (non-mesh-network-
based) localisation algorithms in lab conditions with high anchor density.
4. Extend and modify the algorithms into mesh-network-based algorithms applicable
also in low anchor density conditions.
5. Evaluate the performance of designed mesh-network-based algorithms in lab con-
ditions with low anchor density.
6. Validate mesh-network-based algorithms in the use case scenario based on the
requirements.
1.4
Report structure
The report is divided into eight chapters with this introduction being chapter 1. Chapter 2
provides a theoretical overview of state-of-the-art localisation methods, relevant wireless
protocols, commercial solutions, algorithms, and performance measures. In Chapter 3,
a practical use case and its requirements are presented. Chapter 4 concerns itself with
the positioning system design including hardware, software, and installation. In Chapter
5, the localisation performance of traditional algorithms is evaluated and their optimal
parameters are identified. Chapter 6 introduces mesh-network-based algorithms and their
performance is evaluated. In Chapter 7, the proposed new algorithms’ robustness is
verified in the use case scenario. Lastly, Chapter 8 outlines possible future work and
recommendations for adapting the algorithms into a product.

2.1
Position location problem and terminology
The position location problem is concerned with estimating a position of an object in a 2
or 3-dimensional coordinate system defined by known reference points. The system that
solves the problem is referred to as Positioning System (PS) or in the case of a real-time
solution Real-time Location System (RTLS). PS deployed inside buildings is then called
Indoor Positioning System (IPS). In this thesis, the following terminology is used for the
components of ISP:
• Asset nodes (assets) – tracked devices or tags with location unknown before using
the system;
• Anchor nodes (anchors) – devices or tags with known fixed locations which are input
to the system;
• Gateway node (gateway) – a device that ensures the connection of the network to
a computer or cloud running the localisation application.
sectionLocalisation performance Different performance characteristics are used to as-
sess the quality of the IPS and the localisation algorithm. Depending on the use case
different performance might be required. Therefore, it is important to consider the mini-
mal requirements and define design goals for the system. This section presents the most
important performance characteristics.
2.1.1
Accuracy
Accuracy defines how close is the estimated location to the actual location of the asset.
Its measure is therefore a distance; in 2 dimensions usually Euclidean distance is used. If
multiple estimates are calculated for each asset, the accuracy is defined as the distance
from the actual position to the mean position of the estimates. Accuracy is affected by the
Systematic Error in the system.
If the accuracy of the localisation algorithm which localises multiple assets is being eval-
uated, a way of aggregating the errors is needed. Usually, Root Means Square Error
(RMSE) is used, i.e. square root of the mean of the squares of the error values.
2.1.2

other, i.e. how reproducible is the estimate. Its measure is standard deviation σ in 1-

2.1.3
Update period
An important performance characteristic is the period it takes the system to produce the
next location estimate. It depends on the time it takes to collect enough measurements to
produce an estimate Tdata and the computation time of the algorithm Tcomp. Tdata is deter-
mined by the number of measurements required, the period between the measurements,
and the time it takes to transfer the information to the computer running the localisation
algorithm. Tcomp is generally hard to determine because it depends on the exact compu-
tational resources of the on-site or cloud computer that performs the calculations. If Tcomp
is lower than Tdata it can be neglected because the algorithm can execute in parallel to
data collection, generating no additional time penalty.
2.1.4
Scalability
Scalability is the ability of the system to handle a growing number of devices and signal
transmissions [3]. It can be quantified e.g. by the maximum number of nodes in the
network.
2.1.5
Energy consumption
Energy consumption is an important consideration for IPS especially because asset nodes
are usually battery powered. Energy needs, therefore, determine the lifetime of the sys-
tem before maintenance or replacement of the nodes is needed. Energy consumption is
determined by the signal strength and frequency of communication and is quantified in

2.1.6
Robustness
Robustness is the ability of the system to operate under stress or with invalid input. Invalid
inputs can be caused by objects blocking the signal path, signal interference, the Doppler
effect, or other factors [3]. Outlier filtering can be used to limit the number of invalid inputs.
2.2
IoT technology stack
The indoor asset tracking systems usually relay on devices interconnected in a Internet
of Things (IoT) network. The IoT technology is conceptually divided into seven layers in
the OSI model [4]:
1. Application Layer – handles user interaction with the system;
2. Presentation Layer – handles translation of the data for the Application layer;
3. Session Layer – handles connection between computers;
4. Transport Layer – handles delivering and error checking of data packets;
5. Network Layer – handles routing of data packets to logical addresses;
6. Data-Link Layer – handles node-to-node data transfer of data frames;
7. Physical Layer – handles electrical or optical transmission of unstructured data.
The Physical and Data-Link Layers are the most interesting from the point of view of an
asset tracking system design, because they are directly effected by the nodes’ physical
locations.
2.3
State of the art: Localisation methods
The techniques used for positioning wireless assets can be generally divided in four cat-
egories depending which information they rely on: Angle of Arrival (AoA), Time of Arrival
(ToA), Time Difference of Arrival (TDoA), and Received Signal Strength Indicator (RSSI).
2.3.1
Angle of Arrival
Angle of Arrival (AoA) method is based on finding the angle of incidence at which the
Radio Frequency (RF) signal reached the receiver. Unlike all other presented methods, it
is a direction finding method, meaning the node is positioned based on signal directions
and not signal travel distance (ranging) [5]. A disadvantage of the AoA method is that
is requires an array of antennas which makes the hardware complex. AoA transceivers
include one or several active elements making them power hungry and impractical for
battery powered applications [6]. On the other hand, an advantage of AoA systems is
that they do not require precise time synchronisation of the measurements.
2.3.2
Time of Arrival
Time of Arrival (ToA), also refereed to as Time of Flight (ToF), is a method based on finding
the time signal spent travelling from transmitter to receiver. From there, the distance
between the nodes, refereed to as range, can be estimated and positioning algorithm
(e.g., trilatration) used to localise the node. The disadvantage of this approach is that it
requires precise clocks synchronised between all nodes, adding complexity and cost to
the hardware. It is especially difficult to measure travel time of RF waves which travel at
the speed of light, therefore the systems are often use sound waves instead. Regardless
of the medium, the ToA systems additionally only work well in clear line-of-sight scenarios
and are susceptible to errors caused by sound or electromagnetic wave reflections [6].

2.3.3
Time Difference of Arrival
Time Difference of Arrival (TDoA) is a method which is trying to address some of the
problems with ToA. The method is also based on ranging. The difference of time of arrival
at two separate receivers is calculated. The time difference together with knowledge
of ToA at third node can be used to localise the transmitter. This method still requires
synchronised clocks but only at the receivers, therefore the hardware cost of a TDoA
system can be lower compared to AoA or ToA. However, the disadvantage of the system
is that is requires a lot of centralised computation [3],[6].
2.3.4
Received Signal Strength Indicator
Received Signal Strength Indicator (RSSI) is a value indicating the signal strength at the
receiver. RSSI is a relative measure and its unit is not standardised but higher values
mean stronger signal [7]. If a reference point is set, RSSI can for example be expressed
in decibel-milliwatt (dBm), which is a logarithmic unit with reference to 1 milliwatt (mW).
0 dBm corresponds to 1 mW, 10 dBm to 10 mW, 20 dBm to 100 mW and so on. RSSI-
based methods then work by translating the signal strength information into a an estimate
of distance between transmitter and receiver. This is possible because the signal power
decays with distance [3].
2.4
State of the art: Localisation technologies
The asset tracking systems and IoT networks more generally use a variety of wireless
protocols which correspond the definition of the Data-Link and Physical Layers in the OSI
model. The remainder of this section presents the most common wireless technologies
used for asset tracking and compares them.
2.4.1
Ultra-wide band
Ultra-wideband (UWB) is a communication technology, which was originally used in the
military domain but more recently became commercially available. It uses RF signals in a
wider band than most other wireless protocols. This provides some useful characteristics
such as large bandwidth, robustness against fading and multi-path interference but it also
makes the system implementation more complex. Its physical layer is standardised under
IEEE 802.15.4a [8], [9].
Asset tracking using UWB
UWB was one of the most popular technologies for the development of IPSs in recent
years. This is due to the possibility to achieve high localisation accuracy, typically be-
low 30 cm, while retaining the high scalability of the system. UWB-based systems can
support thousands of tags. Although, usage of UWB has some disadvantages including
limitations of transmitting power imposed by regulators to prevent interference with other
RF technologies and the relatively high cost of receivers. UWB-based IPSs usually use
TDoA or ToA localisation methods [9].
2.4.2
Bluetooth Low Energy
Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE), standardised in 2010 as part of the fourth version of the
standard IEEE 802.15.1, is commonly used for communication between two devices on
a short distance, typically under 100 m. Compared to the original Bluetooth standard, it is
focused on energy efficiency and was designed primarily for battery-powered IoT devices.
The most common way of BLE networking is using Generic Access Profile (GAP) which
defines two device roles: Central or Peripheral. Central devices are usually high-power
devices e.g., smart phones, computers. Peripheral devices are usually small battery-
powered devices e.g., keyboards, headphones, and smart home equipment. Both Central
can connect to multiple Peripherals and Peripherals to multiple Centrals, however, the
latter is uncommon [10].

Asset tracking using BLE
BLE technology is used in IPSs including commercially available systems. The most
common localisation methods used with BLE are RSSI-based. However, recently systems
were proposed that use the AoA method to achieve higher localisation accuracy. Some
systems based on AoA can localised assets within 25 cm [11].
2.4.3
LoRa Wide Area Network
LoRa Wide Area Network (LoRaWAN) is a network protocol designed for networks with
low-power end devices. It is standardised by International Telecommunication Union as
ITU-T Y.44800, and it is maintained by the non-profit organisation LoRa Alliance. LoRa
Alliance is responsible for the certification of compatible end devices. LoRaWAN is used
mainly to operate large-scale IoT networks in agriculture, industry, and logistics.
The typical topology of a LoRaWAN network is star-of-stars, where battery-powered end
devices are connected to local gateways which are connected to a central server. The
gateways and end-devices use single-hop RF communication and gateways are con-
nected to the server using a standard IP connection. The protocol allows for bi-directional
communication but most of the traffic is expected to be in the central server direction.
RF communication between end-nodes and gateways is distributed across different fre-
quency channels and the LoRaWAN protocol specifies rules for channel selection and
transmission timing to prevent interference [12].
Asset tracking using LoRaWAN
Based on a white paper published by the LoRa Alliance [13], localisation of LoRaWAN end
devices is possible natively using TDoA and RSSI methods. TDoA-based localisation is
possible if the device is within the range of at least three gateways and indoors it can under
certain conditions archive up to 10 m accuracy. However, LoRaWAN TDoA localisation is
not suitable for tracking mobile assets.
2.4.4
WiFi
WiFi is a wireless implementation of the Ethernet protocol. It is based on IEEE 802.11 and
was defined by an industry consortium [14]. WiFi was created for Local Area Networking
(LAN) and connecting devices, such as laptops and phones, to the Internet.
WiFi network typically has a star topology in which end devices communicate with an
Access Point (AP) connected to the Internet. A group of devices connected to a single
AP is called a Basic Service Set (BSS) and can support up to 255, however, in practice
the number is usually lower. WiFi protocol utilises complex modulation to provide a very
high data transmission rate and operates in a 2.4 or 5 GHz band [14].
Asset tracking using WiFi
WiFi technology is not very suitable for indoor asset tracking despite being already present
in many buddings. RSSI methods can be used to estimate the end-device distance from
AP but multiple AP connections would be needed for more precise localisation which
is not typical. Furthermore, WiFi is optimised for high data transfer which also means
high power consumption [15]. Therefore, WiFi is mainly used as an additional source of
positioning data alongside other technologies e.g., GPS.
2.4.5
ZigBee
ZigBee protocol builds on top of the IEEE 802.15.4 physical layer and is maintained by
the Connectivity Standards Alliance (CSA) formed by hundreds of companies and organ-
isations. It was for designed low-power or low-bandwidth applications e.g., smart home
devices [16].
ZigBee creates self-organised multi-hop mesh networks. This means that messages can
travel by multiple node-to-node transmissions and the protocol automatically handles rout-
Indoor Asset Tracking
7
==PAGE:16==
ing without Application Layer involvement. There are two types of ZigBee devices based
on capabilities: Full-function Devices (FFD), and Reduced-function Devices (RFD). FFDs
can communicate with all devices in the network and RFDs can only communicate with
RFDs. On top of that, ZigBee devices are classified into three network roles: coordinators,
routers, and end devices. Coordinators (FFDs) are responsible for managing the network,
relaying messages, and authenticating new nodes. Routers (FFDs) relay messages. End
devices (RFDs) can only communicate with coordinators or routers. ZigBee networks use
2.4 GHz, 915 MHz, and 868 Mhz frequency bands and typically can transmit up to 250
Kbits/s [16].
Asset tracking using ZigBee
ZigBee is not a popular technology for asset-tracking applications. No commercially avail-
able ISP based on ZigBee was found during the research. However, this problem was
explored academically. [15] presents a ZigBee-based system able to localise a person
within 2 × 2 m area with 95% accuracy using a combination of RSSI methods.
2.4.6
NeoMesh Protocol
NoeMesh is a proprietary protocol created by the company NeoCortec. It is optimised for
low-power battery-operated applications and small message payload sizes. Compared to
most other protocols, it has almost no limit on network size or network depth i.e., number
of hops [17].
Network properties
NeoMesh Protocol-based networks are self-organising and time-synchronised. It creates
a mesh network in which all nodes in the network are of the same tape, eliminating the
need for a complex setup with routers, coordinators, and end device nodes. NeoMesh
nodes form the network automatically after being powered up. Each node periodically
transmits a beacon signal on a dedicated beacon channel and detects the beacon signals
of other nodes. Upon detecting each other the nodes synchronise their beacons creating
a time-synchronised network. The beacon signals contain information about scheduled
data transmission times. At these times, the nodes transmit payload data and synchro-
nisation messages. The payload data messages can be used by high-level software to
transmit data through the network (e.g., sensor readings, commands for IoT devices, and
RSSI values). The mean benefit of time synchronisation is high energy efficiency allowed
by the fact that the nodes are in sleep mode during normal operation and only wake up
at times scheduled for transmissions.
As is typical in a mesh network, each node has a limited number of neighbour nodes to
which it communicates directly and messages to further parts of the network are routed via
a series of neighbour-to-neighbour transmissions, i.e. hops. The number of neighbours
is 4 to 6 in the case of NeoMesh Protocol and was chosen to balance energy efficiency
and network stability. The neighbour connections are mutual, meaning both connected
nodes consider each other a neighbour.
The throughput of the network depends on message destinations and network configu-
ration. In general, a destination node can receive one payload message per neighbour
per Scheduled Data Period (i.e., Scheduled Data Rate), which is configurable. A lower
period means higher throughput but also higher energy consumption.
Frequency spectrum
To prevent interference from other RF signals, the protocol uses Frequency Hopping
Spread Spectrum (FHSS). 15 logical channels, each operating on a different frequency,
are used for scheduled data transmissions and the 16th channel is reserved for beacon
signals. Protocol versions exist for 2.4 GHz, 915 MHz, and 868 MHz frequency bands
depending on the hardware used [17].

Use case
3.1
Smart building installation
Equipping buildings with a large number of IoT devices is a growing trend in the indus-
try. IoT sensors provide information on temperature, humidity, CO2 concentration and
other variables, which can be used to optimise heating, ventilation etc. In a large smart
office building, factory, or storage facility, this can represent thousands of IoT sensors. To
reduce the material and installation cost, sensors can be connected to a wireless mesh
network which transmits the sensor data to a gateway. The gateway passes the data to
the application managing the smart building, which runs on the local server or in the cloud.
Company NeoCortec provides the solution described above. The sensors equipped with
NeoCortec hardware modules, or running their firmware on custom hardware, create a
self-organising mesh network and route messages to the gateway using the NeoMesh
Protocol. The protocol also allows the detection of RSSI between neighbouring nodes in
the network.
However, the installation complexity of such a system is still high because the location of
each sensor must be correctly recorded during the installation. If the installation involves
hundreds or thousands of IoT sensors and is done by non-experts, the potential for hu-
man error is very high. Furthermore, such error is hard to detect without time-consuming
manual check. An incorrect operation of the heating, ventilation, or another system can
then lead to significant finical loss or even permanent damage.
Considering the RSSI information is already available via the NeoMesh Protocol, an as-
set tracking system could be developed, which would estimate the locations of the IoT
devices after the installation without the need for any additional hardware. Only locations
of a limited number of nodes (anchors) would have to be recorded manually and the ap-
plication would then localise the rest. This would greatly reduce the installation time, cost,
and risk of error.

WTSP.2400 antenna
The WTSP.2400 patch antenna was selected for its small size, suitability for a tag device,
and circular polarisation, which ensures a maximum 3 dB performance drop when the
orientation changes [24]. This is important for an asset tracking application, where the
orientation to the transceiver dynamically changes.

Preprocessing
The performance of the algorithms can theoretically be improved by improving the quality
of the input data. The distance estimates based on RSSI contain valid samples as well

In the preprocessing step, a maximum (cutoff) distance value is set and all higher val-
ues are removed as outliers. To avoid setting this value manually for each environment
a heuristic method was proposed: The base cutoff distance is the distance between the

filtering. The aim is to determine the optimal value of the cutoff multiplier M for the lab ex-
periment. The assumption is that the value should generalise for another setup in similar
conditions.

Finding optimal parameters
The reason the Maximum Likelihood algorithm performs better on 16 channels is its sen-
sitivity to outliers. The multi-path interference will happen in many different ways on 16
wavelengths and the errors will ”cancel each outer out” more effectively upon averaging
the estimates. While on a single wavelength, the multi-path interference will bias the result
in one direction. This does not apply to the Min-Max algorithm which is very robust and
therefore multi-path interference has a minimal impact. In the case Min-Max algorithm
a single-channel configuration leads conversely to more consistent measurements and
better accuracy.
To determine the optimal number of estimates, accuracy and measurement collection
time, which represents the maximum update period, must be balanced. In our case, a
reasonable compromise was to stop collecting measurements when the change is RMSE
after including one more sample becomes permanently lower than 10 cm. This corre-
sponds to 35 estimates in the case of the Maximum Likelihood algorithm and 6 estimates
in the case of Min-Max.
Next, the optimal cutoff multiplier M for preprocessing was determined. Figure 5.7 shows
the impact of M on RMSE for both algorithms. The values shown are calculated under the
previously determined optimal frequency spectrum and the number of estimates for each
algorithm. The optimal values of M for Maximum Likelihood and Min-Max algorithms are
1.2 and 0.9 respectively.

6.2
Naive iterative localisation
Normally only nodes with three or more anchors (which are not in a straight line) can be
localised with the Maximum Likelihood algorithm because otherwise, the set of equations
will not have a single solution1. For this reason, only a few assets can be localised using
the unmodified algorithm in a low-anchor-density scenario. A naive solution to this prob-
lem is to localise assets iteratively; first localise the assets neighbouring enough anchors,
and then ”promote” them to the role of anchors for the next iteration. This process is re-
peated until all assets are localised or no further assets can be localised because of a
lack of connections to anchors.
It was observed that in a low-anchor-density scenario, 6 RSSI values per estimate are
not sufficient for localisation because they are not guaranteed to contain data from all
neighbouring anchors after preprocessing. Therefore, the number of values per estimate
was increased to 12 to ensure data from all neighbours are in the sample.
6.2.1
Results and analysis
In a network in which each node has only 4-6 neighbours and there is a low starting
density of anchors, the algorithm is unlikely to reach and localise all assets. Figure 6.2
shows the network after running the Naive Iterative Maximum Likelihood algorithm using
the lab experiment data. Only 5 out of 9 assets could be localised.
The Naive Iterative Maximum Likelihood algorithm does not provide a solution which lo-
calises all assets in most cases, therefore this approach is not suitable for localisation in
low-anchor-density scenarios. The algorithm needs to be modified to be able to reliably
localise all assets.
6.3
Two-anchor localisation
The first proposed method to improve the Maximum Likelihood algorithm is to extend
it to allow localisation based on only two anchors. Proposed two-anchor localisation is
based on the same principle as Geometric Multilaterisation, however, only two circles are
drawn based on the estimated distance to the two anchors. The method produces two
solutions, one on each side of the anchor connector line, or no solution in case the circles
do not intersect. In our statistical implementation, an intersection point is determined for
each pair of RSSI values measured by the two anchors, and then an average location is
calculated on each side separately, producing two location estimates.
To produce a final location estimate a method must be developed to evaluate which of two
solutions is more likely to be correct. Two evaluation methods are suggested and tested
in this work – localisation loss function, and boundary conditions.
6.3.1
Localisation Loss Function
A Loss Function is a way of measuring the quality of the algorithm output. It measures
the distance between the algorithm’s current output and expected output. It is used for
algorithm optimisation [25].
The expected outputs of a localisation algorithm are the actual locations of the assets in
2-dimensions. However, if we want to use the loss function as a part of the localisation
method, these are unknown to us. The workaround is to focus on distances between the
assets instead. Actual distances are also unknown, nevertheless, their approximation can
be made based on the RSSI data. An approximation of an actual distance is the mean

6.3.2
Boundary conditions
Another method for eliminating incorrect localisation solutions is defining a spatial bound-
ary in 2 dimensions which must contain all position estimates. The estimates outside the
boundary are simply discarded and only estimates inside are considered while computing
the mean estimated locations of assets.
To avoid unnecessary complexity the boundary was implemented as a rectangle defined
by two points – one at the minimum (x, y) coordinates, and the other at the maximum
coordinates. This is sufficient for the purposes of this work because both lab and validation
experiments were conducted in buildings with rectangular footprints.
The disadvantage of the boundary method is that it requires additional information about
the location of the building walls constituting the boundary. However, considering that the
anchor coordinates needs to be recorded anyway, recording the boundary limits will not
make the setup process significantly more complicated. On the other hand, the advantage
of the boundary method is based on the actual physical limitations of the system, which
makes it more reliable.
6.4
Iterative localisation with two-anchor method
When the Two-anchor Iterative Localisation algorithm is applied, a set of 2n localisation
solutions is created, where n is the number of nodes with two anchors and therefore
two estimated locations. If the boundary conditions are considered, the out-of-bounds
solutions are removed from the set. Then, the loss function is used to determine a solution
or multiple solutions with the lowest loss. The selected solution or solutions are used as
a starting point for the next iteration. If there are multiple solutions at the beginning of an
iteration, variants are calculated for each and all of them are included in the set evaluated
by the loss function.
The Two-Anchor Iterative Localisation algorithm was tested in two modes: with loss func-
tion only, and with loss function and boundary conditions. Because two-anchor localisa-
tion is more likely to produce larger errors and is less needed in later iterations, where
more nodes are already localised, a version of the algorithm in which the two-anchor lo-
calisation is only used in the first iteration was tested for each mode. The setup used for
evaluating the methods is described 6.1.

θ = [x1, y1, ..., xn, yn], where n is the number of nodes and (xi, yi) are coordinates of i-th
node. Vector θ also represents a set of parameters of the loss function which GD aims
to optimise. Initial node placement θ0 needs to be chosen from which the optimisation
starts. Furthermore, the following hyperparameters which affect the behaviour of the GD
must be set:
• Learning Rate (γ) – defines size of the iteration step. If the step is too small, the
convergence towards the minimum will be too slow. Conversely, too large γ can
mean that the algorithm will not converge.
• Tolerance – sets a limit on change in loss between subsequent iterations. If the
change is lower than the limit, the iteration stops. A lower limit will generally lead to
a better convergence but at the cost of more iterations and thus longer computation

6.6
Iterative localisation with two-anchor method and
gradient descend
This section explores possibilities of integrating Gradient Descend into the Iterative Two-
Anchor Localisation. The goal was to improve the accuracy by further optimising the
solutions using GD. Firstly, optimal hyperparameters of the GD algorithm for the conditions
of the experiment were identified. Secondly, the iterative localisation was extended with
GD optimisation step at each iteration and the results were evaluated.
6.6.1
Hyperparameter selection
Initially, a hyperparameter search was conducted to identify the best hyperparameter con-
figuration for the experiment. Ideally, the search would be done at every instance when
GD is applied, however, that is not feasible due to computation cost. Therefore, the search
is done only once on a typical solution example and the assumption is that the hyperpa-
rameters will generalise reasonably well for other optimisations under similar conditions.
Variant 2 in the first iteration from the previous experiment (see Figure 6.3a) was selected
as an example solution for the search. It is an early solution with relatively low loss, which
is a type of solution on which a good optimisation performance will have the biggest im-
pact. The search was done for learning rates in the interval [0.1, 1.5] with step 0.1, and
tolerances in interval [10−5, 10−1] with logarithmic step 1 i.e., one order of magnitude
between each value.
Figure 6.4 shows the results of the hyperparameter search for L1 and L2-norm loss func-
tions in terms of RSME of the localisation solution after the last iteration. The number
of iterations, which correlates with computation time, for each hyperparameter combina-
tion is shown in Figure 6.5. The hyperparameter combination of L2-norm loss function,
learning rate 1.1 and tolerance 10−3 was selected as a compromise between speed and
accuracy.
6.6.2
Extending algorithm with gradient descend optimisation
As described in 6.4, when using the Two-anchor Iterative Localisation, a set of solution
variants is created at the start of each iteration and then solution or multiple solutions
with the lowest loss are selected. In the case of the algorithm extended with GD, an
optimisation step is added after the selection. GD optimisation is applied to the selected
solution and the output of the optimisation is then the starting point for the next iteration. If
multiple solutions are selected, optimisation is applied to all of them and the optimisation
output with the lowest loss is then selected i.e., the solution that was optimised most
successfully (based on the loss). This process is repeated at each iteration and the output
of the last iteration is optimised as well.
Applying the optimisation to each solution variant, not only the one(s) with the lowest loss,
was also experimented with but this approach did not provide any accuracy benefits and
required significantly more computation time.

7
Validation
In this chapter, the proposed modifications and extensions of the localisation algorithms
were validated on an experiment simulating the use case defined in Chapter 3. The perfor-
mance was measured and assessed from the point of view of the use case requirements.
The goal was to assess how well the algorithms designed under lab conditions generalise
and perform in the real-life use case scenario.
7.1
Experimental setup
The experiment was conducted on one of the floors of a building of the Department of
Electrical and Photonics Engineering at DTU Campus Lyngby. The experiment area di-
mensions were 57.6 × 14.7 m, amounting to approximately 847 m2. It was comprised of
the central corridor, two staircase entrances, and total of 25 rooms, used as offices and
meeting rooms, on both sides of the corridor. The rooms were fully furnished. The data
used for validation were collected in the early afternoon, starting from 14:22, on a work-
day when the space was actively used by university staff. This was done to simulate the
use case scenario in which it would be impractical to restrict access to the building. The
nodes were attached to walls and immovable objects to make sure they will not change
location during the experiment.
A total of 37 trackers and a single gateway node were used in the experiment. Not all the
rooms were used because some were not accessible, however, the nodes were roughly
evenly spread around the experiment area with 1-4 nodes in each accessible room de-
pending on the room size. Three different scenarios in terms of anchor placement were
considered:
1. Scenario A – 4 anchors in the extremities of the building;
2. Scenario B – 10 anchors, roughly one in every second accessible room;
3. Scenario C – 16 anchors, roughly one in each accessible room.
The same data and trackers were used for evaluating each scenario. The anchor designa-
tion was set by the localisation application prior to executing the localisation algorithm i.e.,
only the locations of anchors were used by the algorithm. The experiment area floorplan
and node types are shown in Figure 7.1.
7.2
Iterative localisation with two-anchor localisation
Firstly, the iterative localisation without Gradient Descend optimisation was tested. Al-
gorithm with boundary conditions and two-anchor localisation only on the first iteration
(number 4 in Table 6.1) was used as it provided the best pre-optimisation performance in
the lab experiment. Considering the neighbour connections are expected to be less sta-
ble and the number of outlier measurements higher, a theoretically more robust version
of the algorithm with two-anchor localisation (number 3) was tested as well. Optimal pa-
rameters determined in Chapter 5 (see Table 5.2) was used for the underlying Maximum
Likelihood algorithm.

In the case of all three scenarios (A, B, C), the GD optimisation did not converge already

Conclusion
The main goal of the thesis was to explore the possibilities of improving existing state-
of-the-art RSSI-based algorithms for indoor asset tracking by including all of the ranging
data available in a mesh network and to assess the performance characteristics of the
modifications. The project used mesh networking technology developed by the company
NeoCortec to lay the groundwork for the possible development of asset-tracking features
for the technology.
Firstly, a literature and market study was conducted in which state-of-the-art localisa-
tion methods, algorithms, technologies, and commercial solutions were documented and
compared. Two state-of-the-art algorithms were for selected experimental evaluation us-
ing NeoCortec mesh-networking technology: Maximum Likelihood algorithm and Min-Max
algorithm.
Secondly, the performance of the aforementioned algorithms was tested in lab conditions
with a high density of anchor nodes. It was discovered that the Min-Max algorithm is more
robust towards outliers, however, when input preprocessing is applied Maximum Likeli-
hood algorithm will outperform it. The Maximum Likelihood and Min-Max algorithms could
localise assets with an accuracy of 2.75 and 3.64 m respectively when preprocessing was
applied.
Next, ways of improving the Maximum Likelihood to perform well in low-anchor-density
scenarios were explored. The Naive Iterative version of the Maximum Likelihood was not
able to localise most of the assets. Therefore, several modifications were tested using
Two-anchor Localisation, Localisation Loss Function, Boundary Conditions, and Gradi-
ent Descend optimisation. The best-performing algorithm variant without GD optimisa-
tion was able to localise assets with 3.18 m accuracy in the low-anchor-density scenario.
This result was further improved by applying GD optimisation to 2.92 m accuracy, which
represents a 6.1% accuracy decrease compared to localisation in a high-anchor-density
environment.
Lastly, the algorithms developed in lab conditions were tested on a real-life use case sce-
nario in an office building with furniture, equipment, moving people and other sources of
noise. The GD optimisation was found to be unsuitable for the high-interference scenario
and the performance of the best algorithm without optimisation degraded to 12.42 m.
Overall, the lab tests showed promising results for the newly developed algorithms, how-
ever, further work must be done to improve robustness in real-life high-interference sce-
narios.
8.1
Future work
8.1.1
Algorithm improvements
The following improvements are suggested for the algorithms developed and tested in
this work:
• More sophisticated Boundary Conditions algorithm – Potential improvements in ac-
curacy could be gained by implementing a more advanced Boundary Conditions
algorithm similar to an algorithm proposed in section 4.3 of [3] which would incor-
porate boundary location directly into location estimate calculation. The current im-
plementation only excludes out-of-bounds location estimates after the calculation.
Indoor Asset Tracking

==PAGE:52==
• De-noised Localisation Loos Function – Current becomes too noisy for Gradient De-
scend optimisation to be effective outside of lab conditions. Presumably, an alter-
native definition of the Loss Function or preprocessing technique could be devised
which would make the Loss Function less noisy.
8.1.2
Application improvements
Several improvements are suggested which would make it easier to adapt software de-
veloped during the project into a product.
• User-friendly GUI – Currently functionality of the Python tool can be accessed through
the API of the Python packages or Python console. Localisation visualisation is
available but must be requested via API or console. This makes it difficult to use for
non-programmers. A simple Graphical User Interface should be developed for users
to configure the localisation algorithm and visualise the localisation result overlaid
on the building floor plan.
• C/C++ rewrite – Python language was suitable for efficient development of the lo-
calisation and vitalisation tool in the context of the project due to the abundance of
packages providing statistical and graphing tools. However, Python is not suitable
for a production version of the tool due to its low performance and external depen-
dencies. It is recommended to rewrite the tool in a compiled language such as C or
C++ before distributing it.
