Abstract 
 
This project aims to build up a start-up that makes use of electric trucks for delivering 
furniture and goods in Copenhagen. The purpose of this project will be initially to analyse 
the Vehicle Routing Problem (VRP) and develop five different scenarios to understand the 
impact the various constraints have on the optimal solutions. First, a compact mathematical 
formulation will be provided for each scenario in order to give the reader an overview of the 
problem. Secondly, the optimal solutions will be computed through the use of Rapidis 
Logistics Planner for ArcGIS, a software from Rapidis company that lets users solve 
complex transportation optimization problems. Then, the second part of the project will 
focus on the affordability to implement an electric truck fleet. To do this, one of the optimal 
solutions computed by the software will be exploited to make a comparison between the 
usage of conventional diesel and electric trucks, with a view to costs and sustainability. 
While the economic costs analysis will mainly take into account the potential savings from 
the usage of electricity instead of fossil fuel, the sustainable analysis will concern the CO2 
saving emissions. It will be essential for the start-up to meet the requirements on the 
sustainable level as today it represents the intrinsic value every company should aim to. The 
combinations of VRP optimization and costs-sustainable analysis will provide fundamental 
insights on the convenience of implementing the start-up. For the lack of time and resources, 
the project will only focus on the optimization of the transportation, rather than looking at 
the business model and risk analysis.

1.1 Literature review 
 
Electric vehicle routing problem (EVRP) encloses several areas, and therefore this literature 
review will be focus only on those areas which best suited to the topic. Also, it must be taken 
into account the sustainable side of the project implementation. Having a battery electric 
vehicle (BEV) fleet involves a greener perspective and consequently a potential reduction of 
CO2 emissions. During the past ten years, the number of published papers dealing with EVRP 
has been exponentially growing because of the awareness in the potential of electric vehicle 
usage. Obviously, this chapter cannot cover all the details of the EVRP. The purpose of this 
chapter is rather to give a general idea of what has been done so far on these topics. Moreover, 
it is assumed the reader has already an adequate foundation in relation to the overmentioned 
topics. The study of the literature ended on 28th February 2021 and papers published after this 
date will not be considered.  
The market share of electric cars is insignificant in most countries, with the possible exceptions 
of Norway and the Netherlands (Yang, 2014). Electric vans and trucks are even more rare 
because of several real or perceived factors related to cost, driving range, payload, reliability, 
and availability. Electric trucks operate on batteries that have a limited life and are 
characterized by high costs as well as specific degradation and charging patterns, which must 
be taken into account in fleet size and mix decisions. In addition, because the number of electric 
recharging stations is still small compared with that of conventional fuel stations, the limited 
range of BEVs becomes a critical constraint in purchasing and operational decisions (Samuel 
Pelletier et al., 2016). Also, it should be considered that while BEVs require lower maintenance 
costs and incur lower operating costs, they have high purchasing costs. 
However, when coupled with interesting incentives, BEVs can sometimes become an attractive 
option for goods distribution. As Taefi et al. (2015) point out, “many initiatives and 
involvement in EV experiments are driven by companies’ awareness and anticipation of 
regulations for less environmentally-friendly vehicles becoming more restrictive in the future.” 
Deployment of electric freight vehicles should not be automatically associated with 
profitability, but rather with exploration and preparation in anticipation of expected future 
developments. Therefore, the cost competitiveness of BEVs depends significantly on future

technological developments related to these vehicles, coupled with governmental investment 
in adequate infrastructure and incentives (Samuel Pelletier et al., 2016). 
Given the current state of technology, BEVs are more appropriate for city distribution than for 
long-haul applications because of the battery limitations. Typical daily distances traveled by 
urban delivery trucks are often lower than the range of commercial BEVs (Feng and Figliozzi, 
2013), which has been shown to be suitable for the urban routing patterns of some logistic 
service providers, even if the reduced payload of the vehicles remained a concern expressed by 
some drivers (Ehrler and Hebes, 2012). BEVs are more suitable for last-mile deliveries in urban 
areas involving the transportation of relatively light goods, frequent stop-and-go movements, 
limited route lengths as well as low travel speeds (Nesterova et al., 2013), (E-Mobility NSR, 
2013). Their reduced pollution and noise are also more relevant in urban areas and thus more 
likely to be rewarded by incentives.  
 
Several new routing problems associated with the utilization of electric vehicles have been 
studied in the last years to simplify their integration into distribution operations. The common 
goal in these problems is to use a fleet of electric vehicles to deliver goods to a set of customers 
at minimal cost, as shown by (Conrad and Figliozzi, 2011), (Felipe et al., 2014), (Goeke and 
Schneider, 2015), (Montoya et al. , 2017), (Hiermann et al., 2019). Almost all these models 
consider the possibility for the vehicle to stop at charging stations along the routes. In reality, 
when BEVs are used for goods distribution, it is common not to use the public infrastructure 
and to fully charge them overnight at the depot (Nesterova et al., 2013), (Naberezhnykh et al., 
2012), (Taefi et al., 2015). Fast charging infrastructures remain relatively scarce in most 
countries except Japan (IEA and EVI, 2013). Therefore, for most countries it seems logical that 
vehicles should mostly recharge at the depot. Furthermore, fast charging may reduce battery 
lifespan. The cost of recharging should also be accounted for when addressing the issue of 
charging policies, since electricity is often cheaper during night and off-peak hours (Samuel 
Pelletier et al., 2016). 
Besides the cost’s advantages deriving from the usage of the electric vehicles, it is fundamental 
to consider greenhouse gas emissions (GHG) consequences. Nowadays, it is crucial to address 
and achieve as many Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) as possible since being 
environmentally responsible is a necessary step for all companies, to save the parts of nature 
that humanity has left. Hongbo et al. (2017) have studied GHG using electric vehicles and have

1.3 Motivation and goal 
 
The process of moving goods and furniture from one apartment to another is a daily 
occurrence, especially for those cities which are highly populated or are host to a large 
number of students, such as Copenhagen. When a tenant is moving out of the apartment, in 
most cases he has to free the apartment of all furniture and goods he brought with him when 
moved in. Therefore, the tenant has to choose one out of these three options: throw the 
furniture away, or bring them with him, or sell/give it for free to another person.  
The main problem occurs with the transportation of furniture, which involves the three 
above-mentioned possibilities. Neither the seller nor the buyer wants to take responsibility 
for the transportation since it involves high costs. Also, there is no possibility to store the 
furniture, which must directly go from one apartment to another one. Furthermore, everyday 
people sell or give for free furniture and personal objects for many other reasons. Several 
websites provide a page in which the buyer and seller can agree on the price of the furniture 
but not on the transferring modes of goods from one place to another. The model will be also 
be applied to this case. 
The purpose of this project is to create a model that makes use of an electric fleet of trucks 
with drivers to move furniture and other goods from apartments to a depot, and vice-versa. 
The case will be discussed as a VRP which aims to minimize the transportation costs in terms 
of distances and the number of vehicles employed. It will be decided a strategical location 
for the depot which will work as storage for goods as well as a charging station for the electric 
fleet. ArcGIS software will be used to get an optimized solution that fits with the area and 
the number of trucks considered.  
 
The main outcome of the project will point to demonstrate the convenience of investing in 
an electric fleet of trucks from a cost perspective. Achieving a favourable result could be the 
starting point to implement the start-up in the future. Besides, the use of an electric fleet will 
low Co2 emissions. It is crucial to address and achieve as many SDGs as possible since being

2 Modelling the Vehicle Routing Problem 
 
2.1 Overview of the VRP 
 
The last decades have seen an increasing utilization of optimization packages, based on 
operations research and mathematical programming techniques, for the effective 
management of the provision of goods and services in distribution systems (Moghdani et al., 
2021). A large number of real-world applications, both in North America and in Europe, 
have widely shown that the use of computerized procedures for the distribution process 
planning produces substantial savings (generally from 5% to 20%) in the global 
transportation costs.  
 
It is easy to see that the impact of these savings on the global economic system is significant. 
And today, more than ever, it is not just a matter of costs. Optimization of the costs involves 
the minimization of the distances, and therefore, of the emissions due to fuel consumption. 
As a consequence, minimize the distances travelled plays a vital role in the sustainable 
position of the company. 
 
2.2 Structure of the VRP 
 
This project faces the problem of the distribution of furniture and goods between one depot 
and final customers. Therefore, it is identified as Vehicle Routing Problem (VRP). The 
distribution of goods concerns the service, in a given time period, of a set of customers by a 
set of vehicles, which are located in one depot, are operated by a set of drivers, and perform 
their movements by using an appropriate road network. In particular, the solution of a VRP 
calls for the determination of a set of routes, each performed by a single vehicle that start 
and ends at the depot, such that all the requirements of the customers are fulfilled, all the 
operational constraints are satisfied, and the global transportation cost is minimized (Toth 
and Vigo, 2002). 
 
The road network, used for the transportation of goods, is generally described through a 
graph, whose arcs represent the road section and whose vertices correspond to the road 
junctions and to the depot and customer locations. Each arc is associated with a cost, which 
==PAGE:16==

generally represents its length, and a travel time, which possibly dependent on the vehicle 
type or on the period during which the arc is traversed (Toth and Vigo, 2002). 
Both customers and vehicles have several distinctive characteristics.  
Concerning the customers: 
 Vertex on the road graph in which the customer is located. 
 Amount of goods which must be delivered or collected at the customer (demand) 
 Period of the day during which the customer can be served. 
 Time required to deliver or collect the goods at the customer location (service 
time). 
  
Concerning the vehicles: 
 Home depot of the vehicle. 
 Capacity of the vehicle expressed as the maximum weight or volume the vehicle 
can load. 
 Subset of arcs of the road graph which can be traversed by the vehicle. 
 Cost associated with the utilization of the vehicle (Start cost). 
 
The routes must satisfy several operational constraints which depends on the nature of the 
transported goods, on the quality of the service level, and on the characteristics of the 
customers and the vehicles. The most common operational constraints are the following: 
along each route, the current load of the associated vehicle cannot exceed the vehicle 
capacity; the customer served in a route can require only the delivery or the collection of 
goods; and the customers can be served only within their time windows and the working 
period of the drivers associated with the vehicles visiting them. (Toth and Vigo, 2002). 
 
Evaluation of the global cost of the routes, and the check of the operational constraints 
imposed on them, requires knowledge of the travel cost and travel time between the depot 
and the customers and between each pair of customers. For each node i and j on the graph, 
an arc (i,j) is defined whose cost cij is given by the cost of the shortest path starting from 
node i and arriving in j. The travel time tij, associated with each arc (i,j) is computed as the 
sum of the travel times of the arcs belonging to the shortest path from i to j (Toth and Vigo, 
2002).

2.3 Development of different scenarios 
 
In this project, five different scenarios will be presented, analyzed, and applied to a real-case 
routing problem. The purpose is to show how the optimal solution varies every time new 
constraints are added, approaching the real case solutions. The scenarios can be subdivided 
in three categories as follows. 
 
Scenario 1 is the more general and less constrained one, a simple VRP. In this case, neither 
the time windows nor the capacity will be considered. What is expected before the 
calculations, is an optimal solution which is more convenient than the remaining scenarios. 
On the other hand, scenario 1 is just an unlikely real case since time to visit the customer and 
capacity of the trucks must be included unless the VRP involves a very few customers. 
However, the purpose of scenario 1 is just to show an easy solution and use it as a 
comparation to the following scenarios. 
 
Scenario 2 and 3 include the time window constraint. Time windows place a time constraint 
on tasks (pickups or deliveries). This constraint forces a task to be performed inside the time 
window given. As a consequence, it can be expected an optimal solution worse than scenario 
1 but at the same time more real. The difference between scenario 2 and scenario 3 will be 
the time windows assigned. The higher the time of the time window, the more flexible the 
routing will be. The difference between the two optimal solution will be part of the analysis. 
 
To conclude, Scenario 4 and 5 take into account both the time window and the capacity 
constraints. In other words, these two scenarios reveal the more constrained and real routing 
case. Each truck will have a limited loading capacity, and this is expected to bring the usage 
of more trucks compared to the other scenarios. However, a starting cost of the resource will 
try to limit the utilization of several trucks. The trade-off between trucks utilization and costs 
has a high impact on the overall costs and therefore must be considered. 
 
The comparison between scenarios will give important insights on how the time flexibility 
and the capacity affect the optimal solution. One of these scenarios will be deeply analyzed 
and implemented for the cost and sustainable analysis, which concerns the second part of the 
thesis.

The following sections will present the mathematical model formulations of the different 
scenarios. A clarification that should be made here is that the word “problem” in acronyms

The distinction is important as it is the formulation of a VRP that is important from a 
methodological standpoint (Psaraftis et al., 2015), but then the resolution of the specific real-
world problem can be made in a different way. That is why it is crucial to provide the reader 
with an overview of the mathematical problem formulation, even though ArcGIS software 
works through the use of heuristics that will be explained in Heuristics methods from Rapidis 
(Chapter 3.4).  
For each scenario, the problem formulation will be first discussed, followed by the 
corresponding mathematical model and the explanation of the constraints. Scenarios 2 and 3 as 
well as scenarios 4 and 5 have the identical formulation since the difference in the constraints 
is just a value – time windows duration and capacity. As the main difference between the 
scenarios is due to the addition of some constraints, the formulation of the problem will be 
similar and partially sharable between the scenarios.

2.4 Scenario 1 – VRP without time window and capacity constraint (VRP) 
 
2.4.1 Problem formulation 
 
The transportation requests consists of the distribution of goods from a single depot, denoted 
as point 0, to a given set of n other points, typically referred to as customers, N = {1,2,...,n}. 
The fleet of trucks K = {1,2,...,|K|} is assumed to be homogeneous, meaning that |K| trucks 
are available at the depot and are operating at identical costs.  A truck that services a customer 
subset S ⊆ N starts at the depot, moves once to each of the customers in S, and finally returns 
to the depot. A truck moving from i to j runs into the travel cost cij. 
 
As already mentioned in the previous chapter, this scenario involves the lowest possible 
number of constraints. It is an ideal case, since time windows and capacity constraints are 
usually taken into account by the routing companies. Regarding the objective function, it 
aims to minimize the total costs and it is therefore the same as the other scenarios. Figure 1 
shows a simplified example of the problem.

3.4 Heuristics methods from Rapidis  
 
After introducing all the different models, this chapter will explain the heuristic methods 
used by Rapidis logistics planner optimization solver. VRP is a NP-hard problem (Toth and 
Vigo, 2002) and therefore finding an optimal solution is most likely to be impossible or 
impractical. The purpose of heuristic method is to approach to problem solving that uses a 
practical method or various shortcuts in order to produce solutions that may not be optimal 
but are sufficient given a limited timeframe or deadline.  
 
Rapidis’ heuristic method is divided into two main steps: The construct initial plan and the 
improve plan. The former, starts with the inclusion of the lowest cost task into the best 
position, followed by the iteration. The latter is the Improve plan which takes advantage of 
the best improvement hill climber move selection strategy. It consists of searching 
neighborhoods through moves and select best improvement move until no more 
improvements are available.  
Below the list of the main moves is presented. 
 Swap pairs of tasks (classic move) 
 Move task to another resource into best position (classic move) 
 Swap small sub-sequences between 2 resources (classic move) 
 Remove task, reinsert into best position (classic) 
 Remove group of tasks – reinsert into best positions (destroy/repair move – large 
scale neighbourhood). 
 
When doing a move, the delta cost is computed – the cost after minus the cost before. If the 
delta cost is negative that means the total cost has decreased – in that situation, this move is 
selected and performed. Else, it is discarded, and it should be tried something different.  
Therefore, every move performed drives to changes in the plan of the trucks. 
In the end, the implementation of a heuristic is based on the design of data structures to 
represent a fleet of trucks, each of them having a sequence of tasks to perform. These data 
are manipulated, for instance, by switching the tasks between two trucks. The crucial point 
is to efficiently evaluate each attempted move.

Is it valid? For instance – Are there any time-windows broken? Can the new task-sequences 
fit into the truck working time? Can the load fit into the truck?  
If valid, what are the new costs of the trucks and their changed task-sequence – did transport 
time increase or fall? Is there the need to activate an extra truck?

5 Economic costs analysis 
 
5.1 Volvo FL Electric specifications 
 
The Volvo FL Electric contains six 600V traction batteries. Just like a regular battery, a truck 
battery produces electricity through a series of electrochemical reactions between the anode, 
cathode and electrolyte. In the Volvo FL the battery is made from low-weight Lithium, Nickel, 
Manganese, Cobalt, and Oxide. Each traction battery pack has an estimated lifetime of 8 years. 
As electric energy is supplied, it creates rotating magnetic fields, causing the rotor to spin. This 
provides the mechanical energy needed to turn the truck’s gears and wheels (Volvo Truck 
Global, 2021).  
 
In electric trucks, regenerative braking uses the electric motor as a generator to convert much 
of the kinetic energy that is normally lost when braking. That energy goes back to the vehicle’s 
traction battery, where it is stored. The overall operating range of the truck is up to 260 km. 
Since the truck runs on electricity, there are both fewer moving parts and fewer vibrations. 
There is a 10 dB difference between the drive-by noise of a diesel Volvo FL and an electric 
version. That’s perceived as half as loud (Volvo Truck Global, 2021). 
 
The Volvo FL Electric will be available in the next years and can already be ordered. Pre-sales 
for the full European line-up of Volvo Trucks electric models will begin in 2021 with 
production to follow in 2022. While the energy costs of BEV delivery trucks can be nearly four 
times lower than diesel trucks, the downside is that their purchase costs can be three times 
higher (Feng and Figliozzi, 2013). Even though Volvo FL Electric’s price has not yet been 
revealed, it can be assume that each truck will cost 1’346’400 [DKK]. The assumption was 
made based on the average price of similar EVs trucks ( EVcompare.io, 2021). Figure 7

6.1 CO2 emissions comparison  
Several papers and studies try to assess the exact footprint emissions computation of 
vehicles. Also, some software, such as EOLICA (Eiolca, 2021) allows allocating a footprint 
emission to each good shipped. This can be done by knowing all the precise specifications 
of the vehicles as well as the goods they move during the routing. It is the case, for instance, 
of a truck shipping trees from a forest to the wood manufacturer company. The company 
needs to know the precise amount of material they buy. However, since the start-up of this 
project does not deal with a specific product, but, conversely, on any goods or furniture that 
can be bought or sold to anyone, the computation will be done on a more macroscopic level.  
Besides, it could be studied how the load affects fuel consumption. In this case, as the object 
of this project is not an industrial heavy transportation problem, it can be ignored. This is 
also justified by the fact that the average number of tasks each truck needs to perform in 
CVRPTW1 scenario is 14. Considering the weight each task could have, shipping objects 
such as wardrobes and beds, the total weight will be hovering between one and two tons. 
Those values are far away from the heavyweights the trucks can load and therefore, it is 
assumed that the load does not affect fuel consumption. 
Thus, the computation of the CO2 emissions is done differently, starting from the diesel’s 
physical characteristics. Given the weight of 1 liter of diesel is 835 grammes, and that it 
consists of 86,2% of carbon, it means that there are 720 grams of carbon per liter of diesel. 
To produce CO2, meaning to combust this carbon, 1920 grams of oxygen are needed. The 
sum is then 720 + 1920 = 2640 grams of CO2/liter diesel. From table 8, the average fuel 
consumption for the diesel truck considered is 2,74 [l/km]. Then, the CO2 emission 
consumption is shown by formula 28 below.

7 Conclusions 
 
7.1 Conclusion 
This project has made it possible to achieve relevant results in the transportation sector. The 
purpose was first to apply different VRP models to develop different scenarios, run them with 
ArcGIS software, and check the impact the time and capacity constraints have. It turned out 
that firstly, it exists an inversed correlation between the number of trucks utilized and the task 
each of them performs. Secondly, the percentage of task time overcomes the transportation 
time since the routing was made in a limited urban area to avoids the autonomy battery problem 
and enable the trucks to route all day. Thirdly, the time window constraint dominates the 
capacity one, and that CVRPTW1 is the scenario with more travelled distances and therefore 
the impact on the travelling cost is the highest. 
Starting from the last consideration and from the fact that CVRPTW1 can be assumed as the 
most real case scenario - and consequently applicable to this start-up project - it was chosen for 
the final cost computation and sustainable analysis. The same analysis could have been applied 
to all the other scenarios. The analysis was crucial to justify the choice of using electric trucks 
instead of fuel ones. In particular, the starting step was to take advantage of the optimized 
distances from the ArcGIS computation of CVRPTW1scenario. The analysis then required data 
about energy and fuel consumption as well as truck specifications. As a result, the potential 
savings allow to payback the electric truck fleet in a short-midterm period as if for the same 
period fuel trucks would be used. 
Finally, the sustainable analysis computes the CO2 savings related to the usage of electric 
trucks. Taken into account all the possible drawbacks, such as the limited battery life and the 
huge number of emissions released during their production, the model reveals high potential 
environmental benefits. Also, the analysis confirmed that the choice of investing in the electric 
truck fleet covers three important SDGs. This is what all the companies, no matter if start-ups 
or multinational companies, should aim to target as the environment needs to be preserved for 
the future.

7.2.2 Usage of other types of vehicles than electric trucks 
The project took into consideration the usage of electric trucks to offer a delivery service as 
they seem to be the most likely to take the scene over the next years. Plus, the sustainable 
analysis in Chapter 6 showed how the impact from CO2 emissions can be reduced by leaving 
the conventional diesel trucks. Moreover, the model was built to exploit the routing in short 
distances, overcoming the autonomy and charging problems the electric vehicles are still 
struggling with. Nevertheless, three more types of vehicles are worthy of the world’s 
attention for the next years. 
Autonomous vehicles. They deserve an own paragraph as it might actually be applied to each 
type of vehicle. According to (Hu et al. , 2020) it is a vehicle that is capable of sensing its 
environment and moving safely with little or no human input. It combines a variety of sensors 
to perceive their surroundings, such as radar, lidar, sonar, GPS, odometry, and inertial 
measurement units. Advanced control systems interpret sensory information to identify 
appropriate navigation paths, as well as obstacles and relevant signage (Lim et al., 2019). The 
main challenges are two. Firstly, Artificial Intelligence is still not able to function properly in 
chaotic inner-city environments. Secondly, a car's computer could potentially be compromised, 
as could a communication system between cars. Despite the big challenges, the usage of 
autonomous vehicles would dramatically reduce the costs, especially for numerous fleets. 
 
Hydrogen vehicles. They offer the prospect of plentiful supplies of clean transportation energy. 
Unlike electric vehicles, the problem related to the batteries’ disposal could be exceeded. The 
power plants of such vehicles convert the chemical energy of hydrogen to mechanical energy 
either by burning hydrogen in an internal combustion engine, or, more commonly, by reacting 
hydrogen with oxygen in a fuel cell to power electric motors (Voelker, 2017). Widespread use 
of hydrogen for fuelling transportation is a key element of a proposed hydrogen economy. 
However, fuel cells are relatively expensive to produce, as their designs require rare substances, 
such as platinum. Currently, the bulk of the car, bus, and light-truck market look set to adopt 
battery-electric technology, which is a cheaper solution than fuel cells. And beyond the 
technology improvement, it must be economically convenient as well.

Drones. In technological terms, it is essentially an unmanned aircraft, a drone is a flying robot 
that can be remotely controlled or fly autonomously through software-controlled flight plans 
in their embedded systems, working in conjunction with onboard sensors and GPS (IoT agenda, 
2021).  To achieve flight, drones consist of a power source, such as battery or fuel, rotors, 
propellers, and a frame. The frame of a drone is typically made of lightweight, composite 
materials, to reduce weight and increase manoeuvrability during flight. They serve a variety of 
purposes, such as recreational, photography, commercial and military, their two basic functions 
are flight and navigation. Furthermore, they could become the vehicles of the future. Amazon 
has already made a plan to make deliveries through the usage of drones. They are light 
deliveries, but it is not excluded that the power will give the opportunity to carry bigger goods. 
Currently, Ehang Chinese company (2021) is developing a drone that can carry people. 
Even if now, someone could see the aforementioned vehicles as a remote future, technology is 
evolving faster than ever. New tools and discoveries are driven by accessibility to technologies 
as well as business convenience and, during the last years, sustainable perspective. It is enough 
to think that the first electric vehicle was built by Porsche a century ago (2021). It will be up 
to the ongoing generation to exploit the technologies which better fit the future needs, in a cost-
efficient and sustainable perspective.
