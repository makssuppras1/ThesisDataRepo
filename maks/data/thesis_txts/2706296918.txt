Abstract
Aim
The main aim of this study is to formulate a constitutive relation of a porcine
bladder. This will be used to create a numerical model in order to reduce the
amount of physical experiments of bladders and additionally do other research
that is challenging to perform experimentally. The desire for the numerical model
is to be useful in the future research for substitution of dysfunctional human
bladders.
Methods
The methods used are numerical simulations which contained cycle test, rup­
ture tests, ball burst test and hydrodistention test, which have been developed
and solved in Comsol Multiphysics 5.6. The results have been processed in Mat­
lab and fine polished in Inkscape. Convergence studies was used to confirm the
accuracy of the simulation while the experimental results from prior studies was
used to compare the numerical results.
Results
The study showed that the behavior of the bladder is a combination of linear
elastic and viscoelastic material model of J. M. Burgers. Furthermore, the simu­
lation results revealed that the most reasonable strain speed should be low when
performing the experimental tests. The strain distance should not be elongated
more than 50% as the hysteresis showed that the results were then the same if
elongated further. The strain speed had no impact on the ultimate failure point.
The results demonstrated that the ball burst test is not directly comparable to
hydrodistention test, therefor it is recommended that the hydrodistention test
to be performed experimentally instead of the ball burst test. Overall, the re­
sults suggest that numerical simulations of experimental investigations are very
achievable.
Keyword: Comsol, Pig bladders, Simulation, Rupture test, Cycle test,
Ball Burst test, Hydrodistention test, Material models, Viscoelastic, Matlab,
Numerical model.

CHAPTER1
Introduction
The purpose of this thesis is to develop a numerical simulation (finite element
model) of the biomechanics of the urinary bladder, based on a pig bladder.
1.1
Motivation
There are two primary reasons to investigate the bladder’s biomechanics.
Firstly, very few research have been conducted on this bladder in general. Addi­
tionally, there are several disagreements on which test parameters to use, such
as speed, strain distance for the cycle test, bladder state (fresh or frozen), and
so on, which results in research reaching conflicting results. Today, it is evi­
dent that mechanical qualities also play a significant role in achieving biocom­
patibility for an artificial material[1]. All tissues are composite materials from a
mechanical standpoint. Among the main constituents of biological tissues, col­
lagen and elastin fibers have the most significant mechanical qualities, affecting
the overall mechanical behavior of the tissues in which they are found[2]. By be­
ing able to define the bladder’s constitutive relation, we may obtain insight into
the local bladder wall mechanics.
Secondly, the motivation of this project is to contribute to existing research to­
wards the development of an artificial bladder and to clarify areas of dispute. The
ultimate goal is to be able to achieve a complete understanding and description
for the characteristics of the bladder.
1.2
Research Question
The following questions are addressed in this project:
• Is it possible to formulate a constitutive relation that captures the biome­
chanical response of the pig bladder?

1.3
Scope
This project has a variety of interesting concepts that may be fascinating to pur­
sue further. It would seem natural to utilize microscopes to further record the
description of the bladder’s characteristics, given earlier experimental results in­
dicate that the bladder’s skin is longer when removed from the muscle. Due to
time limits and COMSOL limitations, the scope of this study is restricted to eval­
uating the bladder in its whole without regard for fiber orientation.
Chapter 2: Background
The biological characteristics of the urinary bladder are described, as well as the
theory necessary for this thesis (constitutive equations of the fluid and solid me­
chanics, finite element method).
Chapter 3: Preliminary collection of experimental data
The procedure for preparing the bladder sample and performing the different
mechanical tests is described in detail.
Chapter 4: Simulation
Finite Element model implementation and structure construction in COMSOL
Multiphysics 5.6.
Chapter 5: Results
The results of the COMSOL model simulations are presented in this chapter.
Chapter 6: Discussion
The discussion is centered on the acquired results, with model limits, simplifica­
tions, and assumptions provided and analyzed, as well as comparisons to previ­
ous literature.
Chapter 7: Conclusion
The major results are summarized and compared to the thesis’s expected out­
come. The conclusion discusses how the thesis’s objective was met. The conclu­
sion contains reflections on how the thesis’s goal was fulfilled.
Chapter 8: Outlook
Finally, a presentation of future potential research that may be performed as a
continuation of the current thesis is provided.

2.1.1
Anatomy
The UB is a musculomembranous hollow structure in the pelvic cavity that lies
above and behind the pubic bone.
The UB is a hollow organ that collects and disposes of urine at low pressure. UB
is able to keep the pressure nearly constant due to the stress­relaxation char­
acteristic of the bladder wall[4]. The bladder empties quickly and completely
during normal voiding by first lowering intra­urethral pressure (urethral relax­
ation) and then contracting the detrusor muscle constantly.
The bladder’s primary goals are to hold large quantities of urine at low pressures,
control urination, and maintain a proper chemical gradient between urine and
blood.
2.1.2
Physiology
Urine is transported from the kidneys to the bladder through the ureters. They
enter through the dorsa­caudal side of the bladder. The urethra, the bladder
outlet, begins at the caudal end. The detrusor muscle forms the majority of the
bladder wall. The trigone, the triangle­shaped region between the ureter outlets
and the urethral opening, seems to have a distinct structure. The bladder neck or
base is the portion of the bladder that creates the entry to the urethra. This por­
tion of the detrusor is proven to be histologically separate from the detrusor[5].
The lower urinary tract’s role is to preserve continence. There are two phases in
which the bladder and urethra serve opposite roles. During the collection phase,
the bladder serves as a temporary storage for urine that is continually generated
by the kidneys. During the micturition phase, the gathered urine is excreted
from the body. The bladder and urethra wall musculature, as well as the urethral
sphincter, provide this double role. Both the detrusor and internal sphincter are
made of smooth muscle, but the external urethral sphincter is made of striated
muscle[6].
The walls of the UB are composed of four layers, as seen in Figure 2.2. The mu­
cosa layer is composed of an epithelium referred to as the urothelium. It is sup­
ported by a loose connective tissue layer known as the lamina propria. Except
in the trigone area, the mucosa of the empty bladder is folded. The urothelium
is up to six cells thick in this state[6].

2.2
State of the art
Although biomechanics of the UB is a relatively new field of study, the medical
concerns linked with it are as ancient as humanity. Exploration of multifunc­
tional integrative processes at all structural levels is essential to separate com­
plicated mechanisms and, as a result, generate effective treatments for bladder
diseases.
Ritz (1909) developed the discrete variational method for the linear elastic Kirch­
hoff plate, and Galerkin (1915) published the article on FEM for linear elastic con­
tinua, however both Ritz and Galerkin relied on test and trial functions through­
out the whole domain as support. Courant (1943) invented the use of triangular
and rectangular ”finite elements” to solve the 2D St.­Venant torsion problem of
a prismatic bar (Poisson equ. ), and Clough and his colleagues (1956) published
the first modern 2D finite element model for arrowed aircraft wings[8].
1975 was found to be one of the earliest bladder simulations[9].

2 Background
There are just a few models of the UB. The bladder mechanics are significantly
simplified in all of these models. They are all built on the belief of a uniform
distribution of factors such as stress and strain inside the bladder. Addition­
ally, their data and scientific procedures are deficient in terms of how they are
done[10].
The study of the UB’s biomechanics, particularly the physiological and patho­
physiological, seeks to get a better knowledge of the UB remodelling and disease
processes.
Debilitating incontinence, which affects both men and women, is characterized
by an overactive bladder, urine incontinence, bladder blockage, and cancer[11].
In the majority of industrialized countries, radical cystectomy is the standard
treatment for localized muscle­invasive and metastatic bladder cancer (MIBC)
[12].
Continent and incontinent urinary diversions are the two types of urinary di­
versions. One of the most common urinary diversions is an abdominal urine
diverticulum such as an ileal or colon incontinent conduit (urostomy). [13]
As a consequence, biomaterials for bladder repair and replacement are being de­
veloped.
Bladder biomechanics investigations will help researchers to better understand
normal bladder function and how illnesses and therapies impact it, in addition to
enhancing bladder tissue engineering. Because of a number of variables such as
test speed, load history, sample preparation, and environmental circumstances,
data from UB studies have proven difficult to compare across publications. In
addition, when it comes to quantitatively defining UB’s mechanical characteris­
tics for FEM studies, there has been a lack of research. FEM studies may be used
to confirm tissue characteristics by establishing a constitutive relation that can
be used to explain the stress­strain relationship of UB.
This properties of analysis is fundamental because it may both validate and en­
hance the knowledge of UB while eliminating the requirement for physical inves­
tigation and more sophisticated types of analysis than are presently accessible.
A lack of detailed data on bladder biomechanics is one of the most important
barriers in the field of bladder tissue engineering[14].

Viscoelastic material[16]
Viscoelasticity is a feature of materials in materials science and continuum me­
chanics that refers to materials that exhibit both viscous and elastic properties
when deformed, which signifies that the material exhibits characteristics of both
a liquid and a solid. There are two fundamental properties of viscoelastic mate­
rials:
1. Time­dependent behavior
2. Permanent deformation
When a stress is applied to viscous materials, such as water, they resist shear
flow and strain linearly with time. However, when elastic materials is stretched,
it contract and rapidly return to its initial condition when the force is removed.
The majority of biological materials behave viscoelastically, with three different
material behaviors depending on the loading history.
1. Strain­rate dependency: A strain­rate dependence occurs when a material
is exposed to a load and its strain depends on the velocity. The higher the
speed, the larger the stress value become.
2. Stress­relaxation: When a constant force is applied to a material, the force
gradually decreases over time.

Newtonian:
The fluid is assumed to be isotropic and homogeneous Newtonian, and the flow

equations. Stress is directly proportional to the velocity gradient in a Newtonian
fluid. The equation can be described as follows:

Incompressible:
In fluid dynamics, an incompressible flow is one in which the density of any
fluid parcel, i.e. any infinitesimal volume of fluid moving in the flow, stays con­
stant. This form of flow is also known as isochoric flow, which comes from the
Greek word ”same space/area.” It is vital to distinguish between a ”incompress­
ible flow” and a ”incompressible fluid”: the former is a property of the flow, while
the latter is a property of the material. Although all fluids are compressible by
definition, several are considered incompressible since the density fluctuation is
small in most situations.
Mass may also be considered in the situation of an incompressible fluid; hence,
based on the law of conservation of mass, it is like saying that the divergence of
the flow velocity v is zero.

2.4
The finite element method
Partial differential equations can be used to represent spatial and time­dependent
problems in fluid and solid domains. The FEM is a computer­aided technique
for solving system equations using approximate numerical solutions. FEM can
be used to solve differential equations numerically and approximate the true so­
lution. In vivo systems are often made of systems with complex and irregular
shape. As a result, using FEM to simulate human in vivo structures and sys­
tems is advantageous. The structures in a system are meshed by separating the
surfaces into small elements with various structure characteristics such as tetra­
hedral, pyramidal, or hexahedral shaped elements. The mesh element size influ­
ences the accuracy of a simulated solution. COMSOL Multiphysics can be used
as a FEM modeling tool, with the goal of properly simulating the behavior of a
realistic in vivo system or experimental setup[21].

2.4.1
Arbitrary Lagrangian-Eulerian formulation
(ALE)[22]
Solid mechanics equations are often expressed in terms of material coordinates,
which are fixed to the material in its reference configuration and follow the ma­
terial as it deforms. This is referred to as the Lagrangian formulation. On the
other hand, it is more convenient to explain the behavior of fluids using the so­
called Eulerian formulation, which uses a spatial coordinate system. Indeed, in
this case, rather than observing the state of individual material particles, it is
often quantities of interest, such as pressure, at fixed points in space that are
explored. The water filling the bladder (WFTB) simulations developed in this
thesis are FSI models, which means they must account for both fluid and solid
mechanics, necessitating the use of a different coordinate system to express con­
stitutive relations that can accommodate moving boundaries between the fluid
and solid domains. By using the FE method, another system, called the mesh
coordinates, can be defined in which the domain boundaries are fixed. Thus,
mesh coordinates enable the possibility of moving boundaries without requir­
ing the mesh to follow the deformation of the material. The ALE formulation
refers to the reformulation of physics equations in the mesh coordinate system
(in this case, solid and fluid mechanics equations). Thus, by adding a pointwise
mesh velocity #»v mesh, the Navier­Stokes Equation (2.13) can be rewritten as:

4.1
Overview
This chapter will describe the process of creating Comsol models. Four different
simulations are performed, but three distinct geometries were used. The four
different simulations include an ultimate failure test, a cycle test, a ball burst test,
and a simulation of WFTB. Additionally, the boundary and initial conditions will
be specified, as well as the solver’s configuration.
4.2
Material properties
Four simulation models have been created as part of this thesis. The ultimate
failure and cycle tests are performed entirely using bladder material. The blad­
der material is a combination of linear elastic and viscoelastic material(Burgers),
with parameters shown in Table 4.1. The ρ and ν are extracted from Chunhui Li
et al., while Young’s modulus was mainly sourced from Chunhui Li et al., but it
was later modified to correspond to experimental results[27].
To determine the Burgers model’s parameters, various parameters were tried
to assess their effect on the stress­strain relations. It was discovered that shear
modulus plays a critical role in determining the magnitude of the variation in
maximum stress at various speeds. i.e., the smaller the difference in stress val­
ues required at the various speeds, the lower the shear modulus should be. How­
ever, there is a limit to the minimum shear modulus that may be used to achieve
convergence. The relaxation parameters had a substantially smaller effect on
the stress value, but had a large effect on how the stress and strain relationship
progressed as the bladder was retracted.

4.3.5
Selection of Time-Domain[31]
There are two types of issues for which a stationary solution is not appropriate:
1. When the inertial forces are no longer meaningless, Newton’s first law
must be applied to the full problem.
2. When the material model has time dependencies, such as viscoelasticity.
The most common method is to conduct a Time Dependent Study. There are no
restrictions on the time dependence of the loads in this kind of analysis, and you
may include any type of nonlinearity.
Because it is apparent from the experimental tests that the pig bladder has cer­
tain viscoelastic characteristics, all Comsol simulations were done using a Time
Dependent Study.
The reason it was considered important to specify the time domain in which the
study was done is that, in theory, you may easily perform a stationary study while
working with a viscoelastic material without setting off Comsol’s warning bells.
This may be accomplished by using the sweep parameter function, which sim­
ply changes the value of a parameter, such as displacement. However, the only
method to determine that stationary study does not make sense when working
with viscoelastic materials is to have a particular expectation for the outcome or
to inquire about certain viscoelastic variables that turn out to be empty.
4.3.6
Selection of solver[31]
The characteristics of the two types of dynamic problems discussed above are
very different. The displacements of inertial forces have second­order time deriva­
tives in the entire structural dynamics problem, while creep and viscoelasticity
only have first­order derivatives.
These equations have very different physical and numerical characteristics.
In COMSOL Multiphysics, there are two general solvers for time­dependent prob­
lems.
==PAGE:52==
30
4 Numerical Comsol setup
• For structural dynamics problems, the Generalized alpha method is recom­
mended. If Structural Transient Behavior is set to Include inertial terms
in the physics interface settings, this is the default solution.
• For first­order problems, the BDF method is recommended. If Structural
Transient Behavior is set to Quasi Static in the physics interface settings,
this is the default solution.
Since viscoelasticity is time­dependent it only has first­order derivatives, be­
cause the first­order derivatives of motion / displacement describes velocity.
As a result, the BDF method is used as a solver.
For the cycle test, the ball burst test, and the WFTB, a fully­coupled solver ap­
proach using the direct solver was selected because to its potential for more ro­
bustness than the usual segregated approach. This approach uses an automated
highly nonlinear (Newton) method, which enables the solver to automatically
choose a damping factor and can make the solver to be more careful with each
method iteration[32]. The initial damping factor was adjusted to 1E­4, the min­
imum damping factor to 1E­8, and for some models, the maximum number of
iterations was increased (up to 50).
Segregated solvers are used for the ultimate failure test. This is because the
damage module requires a significant amount of computational resources to
solve. Additionally, the segregated solver can accelerate the convergence of the
solver[33]. The default settings for the segregated solver have been unchanged,
but the damage module (crack phase field variable) has its own segregated sec­
tion.

CHAPTER6
Discussion
If the geometry is simple, linear (first order) discretization can be used for many
FEM simulations. Although we used a mapped mesh with a rectangular shape
function in our situation, it can be observed in Figure 4.7 that when linear dis­
cretization was applied, it was generally the poorest for achieving convergence.
This is referred to as the ”volumetric lock” or ”poisson’s lock” problem[35]. This
is true for materials with a Poisson’s ratio close to 0.5 (like rubber). Because the
mesh is unable to execute simulations accurately, there will only be stress but
no strains. There are many options for dealing with this. One way to do this
is to increase the number of elements, which in our case should be at least 200
h_elements. Alternatively, you can increase the discretization order. As a result,
cubic Lagrange was the most optimal discretization in our situation.
When doing a simulation, it is usually ideal to begin with a basic geometry and
material to ensure that the setup and result are correct. Initially, the cycle and
ultimate failure models were analyzed using simply a basic linear material with
defined Youngs modulus, Poisson’s ratio, and density. Shortly after, viscoelas­
ticity was added. Following that, the setup complexity was raised by using a
non­linear material setup together with viscoelasticity.
However, it was immediately realized that using Comsol’s linear elastic material
module produced the exact same result. The only difference was that Comsol
took 6 minutes and 17 seconds to complete the linear module. However, it took
16 minutes 37 seconds for the Non­linear module. As a result, it is assumed that
the material exhibited nonlinear viscoelastic behavior, as the results presented
in Chapter 5 clearly prove. When subjected to minor strains, viscoelastic mate­
rials act linearly; yet, when subjected to large strains, they behave nonlinearly,
which means that although the module is referred to as a linear viscoelastic ma­
terial, it cannot be termed linear[36, 37, 38]. The purpose for the repeated us­
age of the Linear elastic module was to simulate ultimate failure using the Crack
module, which is accessible only during the linear elastic module.

usage of the Burgers model inside their Hyperelastic module, far more accurate
results and fiber orientation might have been studied.

Normally, the hysteresis would be expected to go towards zero when the strain
speed is reduced. One possible explanation for the rise when the strain speed is
below around 5 % is because the same number of increment points is not used.
Figure 5.5 shows a cycle test with 5 rounds. The study was forced to stop when
the stress value reached zero [Pa] during contraction. This was done because
otherwise, Comsol would show negative stress values, indicating that the ma­
terial was subjected for compressing. Besides, the method that is used is also
the method that Comsol recommends[40]. Getting negative stress values would
only make sense if the material was subjected to plastic deformation, which it is
not in our case. Figure 6.2 show an example of a viscoplastic stress strain dia­
gram. When a viscoplastic material is exposed to a particular amount of strain,
the material deforms. As a result, the load condition is similar to that of a metal,
where an elastic region is first seen followed by a less steep arc, which indicates
deformation. Thus, plastic deformation is no where to be seen in the achieved
numerical results. One of the characteristic of a plastic deformation is that the
material cannot be returned to its initial length after being subjected to deforma­
tion. It would be necessary to compress the material in order for it to return to
its initial length, which demands a force that compresses the material, resulting
in negative stress values.
Figure 6.2: Stress­strain example of a viscoplastic material[41].
Thus, as seen in Figure 5.5.B, the Comsol simulation only briefly reaches the
stress value of zero [Pa] for each cycle, in contrast to the experimental results,
which show several zero values for each cycle due to the machine retracts the
bladder to its initial length.

As previously stated, the bladder wall is believed to be viscoelastic, but since no
literature on viscoelastic tubes could be found and the fact that the bladder also
exhibits elastic properties, it is assumed that the same explanation applies to
our case. As seen in Figure 5.11, the arrows are directed in the opposite direc­

As seen in Figure 5.11, two different inlet velocities are used with a maximum
difference in stress values of just 0.4 MPa. It is important to highlight that the
velocities used represent the water’s inlet velocity, which is not the velocity expe­
rienced by the bladder wall. When the velocity of the bladder wall is observed at
a point near the bottom of the bladder, it is observed that at a 10 [mm/min] inlet

whereas at a 2400 [mm/min] inlet velocity, the bladder wall is exposed to a max­

on the bladder walls strain speed. As a consequence, the difference in results
does not reflect the same difference that is noticed for the other results, such as
the cycle test and rupture test. As a result, if the same difference is to be seen,
other inlet speeds must be used.

is obvious that the Ball burst test is not directly comparable to the WFTB test
because it does not capture the wave effect that the bladder wall is subjected to.

stress distribution on the bladder, which suggests that it may be advisable to re­
consider exploring the possibilities of being able to set up experiments in which
one can fill the bladder with fluid and extract all of the results from.

CHAPTER7
Conclusion
There have been performed comprehensive study on the mechanical properties
of the UB by implementing four different simulations setup in parallel. The sim­
ulations were carried out under loading conditions that were quite near to the
bladder’s physiologic setup. For the first time, WFTB was used to analyze the
whole bladder wall in its natural state. Cycle test revealed that the combina­
tion of linear elastic and viscoelastic material model of J. M. Burgers accurately
represents the bladder’s mechanical characteristics. Additionally, the cycle test
results indicate that very low strain speeds should be used and that the results
are very sensitive at low strain speeds, implying that the strain speed should be
chosen carefully. If large strain speeds are requested, then it makes no sense
to use a strain speed more than 50%, since the hysteresis values for the cycles
tests are the same when the strain speed is greater than 50%. The only time a
strain speed more than 50% makes sense to use, is when determining the blad­
der’s ultimate strain failure point, since rupture testing proved that the speed
was independent. Additionally, cycle testing demonstrated that the strain dis­
tance should not be extended beyond 50%, since the hysteresis indicated that
the results would be the same if extended further. Both the cycle test hysteresis
and the ball burst simulations were found to be similar to prior experimental
tests. Comsol simulations revealed that the ball burst test is not directly compa­
rable to the WFTB test. Which gives rise to that new possible test trials should
be tried. Nonetheless, the WFTB test revealed some fascinating results about
the bladder’s behavior when it’s filled with water, such as the bladder wall being
exposed to wave motions. In general, since there is a lack of literature on UB,
both in terms of simulations and experimental investigations, it has been shown
that numerical simulations of experimental studies are quite achievable.

CHAPTER8
Outlook
This thesis opens up a lot of new research fields. It might be interesting to look at
how the fiber orientation of the bladder affects the way it works. In general, there
has been great disagreement concerning how the fibers are oriented and whether
the bladder should only be tested longitudinally or transversely. In addition, it
could be worthwhile to look at the effect of kidney stones on the ureters, as well
as the overall effect on the bladder. Nevertheless, it might simply be a simple ex­
amination for calcified ureter tubes. This thesis opens the door to investigating
the influence of urine quality on the bladder, i.e. doing chemical simulations to
determine whether it impacts the mechanical characteristics of the bladder.

APPENDIXB
Project Plan
B.1
Introduction
The aim of this project plan is to establish the thesis’s goal and reasoning. This
master’s thesis will be completed in the autumn semester of 2021 at DTU’s De­
partment of Health Technology.
The project will last from 2nd of August to 2nd of January and will be worth 30
ECTS points. In the MSc.project, Marie Sand Traberg and Fatemeh Ajalloueian
will be the supervisors.
B.1.1
Hypothesis
• Is it possible to formulate a constitutive relation that captures the biome­
chanical response of the pig bladder?
• Is it possible to determine if there are any correlations between the nu­
merical parameters calculated and the biomaterial available?
• Is it possible to apply the Constitutive relation to other organs with dif­
ferent parameters?
• Is it possible to investigate the layered structure of bladders with focus
on the biomaterial properties?
B.2
Work task
The project plan contains a time plan. The project plan includes essential work
tasks that must be completed as the project progresses. The work­task includes:
Project planing, Develop and implement a constitutive relation, Comparison of
data and material characteristics, Analyse result, writing halfway report, Verifi­
cation, Validation and writing the final report
==PAGE:88==
66
B Project Plan
B.2.1
Develop and implement a constitutive
relation
This part is regarded as the thesis’s main goal. The primary goal is to see what
we can come up with in terms of constructing a mathematical model that de­
scribes the material characteristics of the pig bladder. This will be accomplished
by the development of mathematical models that examine finite strain, hypere­
lastic, viscoelastic, creep, and other model determinations. The material model
will then be used in a COMSOL FEM study, with multiple FEM analyses per­
formed in respect to fiber content to investigate the effect of different fiber ori­
entations and ­sizes on the bladder. Furthermore, a FEM model consisting of
several layers, each of which represents a layer of the bladder, will be created,
and the properties of each layer will be investigated.
B.2.2
Comparison of data and material
characteristics
A lot of experimental data was acquired in the prior study. The prior data will
be utilized as a reference in this project to compare the constitutive relation that
will be developed. In addition the prior data will be used to compare different
urogenital organs and biomaterials. If it turns out that more experimental tests
are required, they will be conducted as part of this project. Additionally, various
FEM (finite element model) tools / software will be examined, with a focus on
COMSOL and FEniCS in terms of numerical performance and model flexibility,
to see if there are better alternatives.
B.2.3
Analyse result
The results will be analyzed and compared to existing data as well as comparable
studies.
B.2.4
Writing Half-way report
There will be a half­report written. This enables me to be faced with difficulties
long before the thesis is due. The deadline for submission is October 20th, 2021.

B.2.5
Verification and validation
Verification ensures the quality of the constitutive relation, whereas validation
ensures that the constitutive relation achieves its intended goal of accurately de­
scribing the characteristics of the pig bladder. The correctness of the constitutive
relation will be assessed using data from previous projects, and the precision will
be evaluated using finite element model simulation.
B.2.6
Report Writing
