Abstract
In precast reinforced concrete structures, the capacity of the connections between the
precast elements is essential for the overall behavior of the structure. The vertical con-
nections between RC wall panels have to possess an adequate strength to transfer shear
when horizontal loads have to be sustained. Since the introduction of the construction
with precast elements in the 1960’s, a number of tests have been performed on the be-
havior and capacity of various vertical joint designs exposed to shear.
This study will investigate the behavior and capacity of keyed shear connections when
subjected to both shear and normal force in combination. The investigation is relevant
as the connections often have to transfer both load actions. The project includes a litera-
ture study, where previous ﬁndings of similar tests will be emphasized, and the execution
of a novel experimental program conducted in a custom build test setup that allows for
loading in two principal directions without restrictions for relative displacements during
failure. The experimental program consists of sixteen test specimens that are tested to
failure.
For prediction of the capacity of the keyed shear connections, ﬁrst order rigid-plastic
upper bound solutions will be developed for a number of possible failure mechanisms.
Furthermore, a detailed analysis of the load-displacement behavior will be carried out
by use of Digital Image Correlation (DIC), where the focus remains on the inﬂuence of
the level of conﬁnement. The results of the push-oﬀtests will be analysed and compared
to the predicted results, as well as to the previously performed tests.

Resumé
I præfabrikerede armerede betonkonstruktioner er kapaciteten af samlingerne mellem
de præfabrikerede elementer afgørende for konstruktionens overordnede opførsel. De lo-
drette samlinger mellem armerede beton vægelementer skal have en tilstrækkelig styrke
til at overføre forskydning, når de vandrette belastninger skal optages. Siden det blev
introduceret at bygge med præfabrikerede elementer i 1960’erne, er der udført en række
tests, der undersøger opførslen og kapaciteten af forskellige lodrette samlingsdesigns ud-
sat for forskydning.
Dette studie vil undersøge opførselen og kapaciteten af fortandede forskydningssam-
linger, når de udsættes for en kombination af både forskydning og normalkraft. Un-
dersøgelsen er relevant, da samlingerne ofte skal overføre begge typer laster. Projektet
omfatter et litteraturstudie, hvor tidligere resultater af lignende tests vil blive fremhævet,
og afviklingen af et helt nyt eksperimentelt program udført i en specielfremstillet testop-
sætning, hvor det er muligt at påføre belastning lodret og vandret uden begrænsninger for
relative ﬂytninger ved brud. Det eksperimentelle program består af seksten forsøgsem-
ner der testes til brud.
Første ordens stift-plastiske øvreværdi modeller udvikles for en række mulige brud-
mekanismer til at estimere kapaciteten af de fortandede forskydningssamlinger. Desuden
vil der blive udført en detaljeret analyse af last-ﬂytnings forholdet ved hjælp af Digital
Image Correlation (DIC) (Dansk: digital billede korrelation), hvor fokusset forbliver på
indﬂydelsen af normaltryks niveauet. Resultaterne fra push-oﬀtestene vil blive analy-
seret og sammenlignet med de forventede resultater såvel som de tidligere udførte tests.

1 Introduction
1
Introduction
The precast method was introduced to the building industry in the 1960’s and has since
then been used for all sorts of structures around the world, e.g. buildings and bridges.
The precast method is prevalent and the prefabricated concrete components have a
number of beneﬁts that stand out from the other forms of structural concrete elements.
Apart from being cast in a controlled environment in a factory, which improves quality
and precision, the precast method is also considered as an economic and environmen-
tally friendly method that reduce the risk of time loss. Furthermore, the erection of the
precast elements on site is fast compared to in-situ constructions. The precast method
is very eﬃcient, however, the overall performance of precast concrete structures is highly
inﬂuenced by the connections between the prefabricated concrete elements. The vertical
connections between RC wall panels have to possess an adequate strength to transfer
shear when horizontal loads have to be sustained.
A number of researchers have previously investigated diﬀerent vertical joint designs when
subjected to shear. The tests of the connections between prefabricated RC elements have
shown that the geometry of the joints has a big inﬂuence on the behavior and capacity of
the connections. Among other things, various failure modes have been observed which
are especially caused by the design of the connections. However, research on vertical
connections subjected to both shear and normal force in combination is lacking in the
literature.
The purpose of this study is to investigate the behavior and capacity of keyed shear
connections subjected to conﬁnement and loaded in shear. The research is very much
relevant as the connections often have to transfer both load actions, e.g. dead load and
wind load. The main focus will be on the level of conﬁnement, where the inﬂuence of dif-
ferent levels of conﬁnement on the behavior and capacity of the keyed shear connections
will be investigated. Furthermore, the inﬂuence of a varied level of conﬁnement during
test will be investigated as well. The investigation of a varied level of conﬁnement during
test is extremely interesting as connections between RC wall elements might experience
an unexpected change in load distribution that changes the magnitude of the loads the
connections are subjected to. The sudden change in load distribution might come from
the collapse of a wall panel. For prediction of the capacity of the keyed shear connec-
tions, the theory of plasticity is used. First order rigid-plastic upper bound solutions
will be developed to determine the capacity of the keyed shear connections.
The experimental campaign consists of sixteen identical tests specimens, which con-
sist of two prefabricated L-shaped elements grouted together with mortar.
The test
specimens are either only subjected to shear, to both shear and normal force in combi-
nation or to both shear and normal force in combination where the magnitude of normal
force is varied during the test. The test specimens are tested to failure and the results
from the push-oﬀtests will be analysed and compared to the predicted results.

1 Introduction
1.1
Problem Description
Prefabricated RC wall elements are widely used in the construction industry and are of-
ten cast with a keyed shear joint. Since the introduction of the construction with precast
elements, a number of tests have been performed on the behavior and capacity of various
vertical joint designs subjected to shear. However, the keyed shear connections between
RC wall elements are often subjected to both shear and normal force in combination.
In this thesis, an experimental campaign will be conducted to investigate the inﬂuence of
diﬀerent levels of conﬁnement on the behavior and capacity of keyed shear connections
when the connections are subjected to both shear and normal force in combination. As
part of the research, the last part of the experimental campaign will include an inves-
tigation on the behavior and capacity when the level of conﬁnement is varied during
tests. The results from the push-oﬀtests will be analysed and compared to developed
ﬁrst order rigid-plastic upper bound solutions, as well as to the previously performed
tests.

2 Methodology
2
Methodology
The baseline of this study will be the Upper Bound Model based on the Theory of Plas-
ticity. The theory of plasticity is based on a number of assumptions which are then used
to develop upper bound models based on work equations for a geometrically possible fail-
ure mechanism. The upper bound models are then used to determine the load-carrying
capacity of keyed shear connections.
The experimental campaign consists of sixteen identical test specimens, that consists
of two prefabricated L-shaped elements grouted together with mortar.
Three of the
test specimens are used as reference tests, where no normal force is applied to the test
specimens and are thereby only subjected to shear. The rest of the test specimens are
subjected to both shear and normal force in combination. Displacement transducers
(LVDT’s) are attached to the test specimens and are used to measure the displace-
ments of the connections. The data from the displacement transducers will be used to
investigate and analyse the behavior of the connections at diﬀerent levels of conﬁnement.
During the push-oﬀtests of the test specimens, the development of cracks will be recorded
with a high pixel camera and processed in a software called ARAMIS. It is a software
package for three-dimensional image photogrammetry. The ARAMIS software is an op-
tical 3D deformation measuring system allowing movements of pixels in a series of stereo
photographs to be translated into displacement vectors. This is useful during the push-
oﬀtests as fast rates of deformations will be possible to track. The data from ARAMIS
will be used for the analysis of the test specimens and compared to the theoretical failure
mechanisms.
The sixteen concrete test specimens will be tested to failure and analysed in order to
identify any relations between the level of conﬁnement and the load-carrying capacity
and failure mechanisms.

3 Theory of Plasticity
3
Theory of Plasticity
In this section, the essential parts of the theory used within this project are brieﬂy
described. More detailed descriptions of the theory can be found in the literature by
Nielsen and Hoang (2011) [12].
The theory of plasticity can be tracked all the way back to at least the time of Galileo,
but the general formulation of the theory of plasticity was given by Gvozdev in 1936 [12].
The work of Gvozdev was not known in the Western world until the 1950s. However, a
similar theory had meanwhile been developed by the Prager school at Brown University
[4].
The theory deals with materials that are able to deform plastically under a suﬃciently
high constant load. These materials are referred to as perfectly rigid-plastic materials. A
perfectly rigid-plastic material is deﬁned as a material that is experiencing zero strains
at stresses below the yield limit and arbitrary large plastic strains for stresses at the
yield limit. The yield limit is denoted fY . The deﬁnition of a rigid-plastic material is
illustrated in a stress-strain curve in Figure 3.1.
Figure 3.1: Uniaxial stress-strain relationship for a rigid-plastic material.
A perfectly rigid-plastic material does not exist in reality, however the assumption of
such a material behavior is applicable when the plastic strains are much larger than the
elastic strains. The theory is often used for determination of the load-carrying capacity
made with the ideal materials, which is referred to as a limit analysis.
The theory of plasticity includes three important concepts:
• The yield condition
• The normality condition
• The extremum principles

3 Theory of Plasticity
The yield condition deﬁnes the limit at which the material becomes plastic, the normal-
ity condition describes the relationship between stresses and strains once the material
has become plastic and the extremum principles deﬁne the load-carrying capacity of a
structure by use of theorems. These matters deﬁne the theory of plasticity and are by
their simplicity a powerful tool for structural analysis.
3.1
Yield Condition
The yield condition can be described by a set of generalized stresses, Q1, ... , Qn, which in
combination will cause yielding. The generalized stresses, Qi, will have the corresponding
generalized strains denoted as qi. The yield surface is assumed to be convex and has a
closed surface around the point of no stresses, i.e. Qi = 0. The yield condition, which
determines the yield point for arbitrary stress ﬁelds, is deﬁned as,
f(Q1, Q2, ..., Qn) = 0
(3.1)
The generalized stresses may also be selected as sectional forces (e.g. N, V, M) with
corresponding strain components.
According to the yield condition of the perfectly plastic material behavior, combinations
of stresses satisfying Equation (3.1), f = 0, will cause yielding of the material where
arbitrary large plastic deformations are possible without any change in the stresses. This
is illustrated in a Q1,Q2-coordinate system in Figure 3.2.
Figure 3.2: Yield surface.
Combinations of stresses fulﬁlling f < 0 will be equal to a stress ﬁeld that can be
sustained by the material with no deformation, i.e. no strains. A combination of stresses
resulting in f > 0 is not possible.

3.3
Extremum Principles
The load-carrying capacity of a structure is essential when determining whether a struc-
ture has the suﬃcient capacity to withstand a given load or not.
For determination of the load-carrying capacity of a rigid-plastic structure, the theo-
rems from the theory of plasticity formulated by Gvozdev in 1936 and independently
by Drucker et al. in 1952 can be very useful. The theorems presuppose that the yield
condition is convex and that the normality condition is fulﬁlled. The theory of plasticity
consists of three theorems – the lower bound theorem, the upper bound theorem and the
uniqueness theorem and are introduced respectively,
“The load determined from a statically admissible and safe stress
ﬁeld will be smaller or equal to the actual collapse load of the struc-
ture”
– the lower bound theorem.
A statically admissible and safe stress ﬁeld corresponds to a distribution of stresses
satisfying the equilibrium conditions and the statical boundary conditions, while the
stress distribution being within or equal to the yield surface, i.e. f ≤0.
“The load determined from using the work equation on a geomet-
rically possible collapse mechanism will be greater or equal to the
actual collapse load of the structure”
– the upper bound theorem.
A collapse mechanism is a displacement ﬁeld that is geometrically possible according to
the boundary conditions. This theorem states that even the smallest increase in load
cannot be carried by the structure.
“If the lower bound and the upper bound procedure lead to identical
results, then this load is the exact collapse load of the structure”
– the uniqueness theorem.
Using the extremum principles, the load-carrying capacity of a structure can be estimated
either from analysis of statically admissible stress ﬁelds (lower bound solution) or by use
of the work equation, where the rate of work performed by the external loads equals the
rate of internal dissipation (upper bound solution). It is only the upper bound theorem
that will be used within this project.

3.4
Failure Criterion for Concrete
Normally, two failure mechanisms are compared when referring to the movement of a
body in connection with failure – a sliding failure or a separation failure.
A sliding
failure is seen where a fracture results in a movement in the surface of fracture, such as
when a part of the body slides parallelly to the other part. A separation failure is seen
where the fracture occurs perpendicularly to the surface of fracture, i.e. the body in a
fracture is separated into two bodies without being in contact with each other.
The failure of a body is often characterized by either a brittle- or ductile fracture. A
brittle fracture is described as a sudden fracture of the body whereas a ductile fracture is
associated with large deformations. The larger the deformations associated with a failure
the more ductile the material is. It is often desired that a construction has as ductile a
failure as possible, as it will give a warning of failure that will enable an evacuation of a
building. These matters are the reasons concrete is reinforced with steel, since steel is a
material close to satisfy the assumption of a perfect rigid-plastic. Structural concrete is a
quasi-brittle material more than a plastic material, however, when introducing the eﬀec-
tiveness factor to the concrete strengths, structural concrete can be treated according to
the theory of plasticity. The eﬀectiveness factor will be further described in Section 3.4.1.
The failure criterion for a large group of materials appear to satisfy the Coulombs fric-
tional hypothesis, including concrete [12]. The hypothesis states that failure occurs as
sliding in planes, which means that the shear stress,|τ|, in the section exceeds the sliding
resistance. This is called a sliding failure. The stresses satisfy the following equation,
|τ| = c −µσ
(3.5)
where c is the cohesion, µ is the coeﬃcient of friction and σ is the normal stress. The
coeﬃcient of friction can also be described by the internal angle of friction, ϕ, as µ = tan
ϕ. A material complying with the failure condition seen in Equation (3.5) is referred to
as a Coulomb material.
An assumption of a limitation on the tensile stress is then included in the hypothe-
sis, which is related to a separation failure.
The separation failure occurs when the
largest tensile stress is equal to the separation resistance, fA, i.e.
σ = fA
(3.6)

Equation (3.5) and (3.6) is called a modiﬁed Coulomb material. The modiﬁed Coulomb

the concrete. By introducing a factor called the eﬀectiveness factor, ν, it is possible to

Assumptions
This section will contain a description of a literature study of earlier performed push-oﬀ
tests of keyed shear connections through the ages. Based on the former similar tests and
the design of the test specimens in this thesis, a number of assumptions have been made
prior to the tests of the keyed shear connections.
The behavior of keyed shear connections have been investigated by a large number of
researchers throughout the years, in which the connections were subjected to both shear
and a combination of both shear and normal force. A SBI-report has been made by
the Danish Building Research Institute [3] (in Danish: Statens Byggeforskningsinstitut),
where the research of keyed shear connections up until 1976 has been collected. The
tests of the keyed shear connections collected in the SBI-report show that the geometry
of the keys has a big inﬂuence on the capacity of the connection and the observed failure
modes. Especially two failure modes were observed, either shearing oﬀthe shear keys or
crushing and shearing oﬀthe corners of shear keys. The two observed failure modes are
illustrated in Figure 4.1.
Figure 4.1: The two observed failure modes.
The geometry of the shear keys and the design of the test specimens have varied in
many of the test campaigns, in order to ﬁnd out how the design inﬂuences the capac-
ity. It turns out that an increasing key height reduces the capacity of the connection,
whereas the capacity increases if the key area is increased. Aside from the inﬂuence of
the geometry of the keys on the capacity, the geometry also has an impact on the failure
mode. A high ratio between the height and depth of the keys result in shearing oﬀthe
corners of the shear keys, whereas a small ratio causes shearing oﬀthe shear keys. In
addition, in the majority of the tests, cracks developed at the interface between the grout
and the element concrete for relatively small loads. These cracks are expected to take
place as a consequence of shrinkage. It is also found that the capacity is bigger when
the transverse reinforcement overlap compared to when the reinforcement is distributed
along the height of the joint.

6.1.1
Failure Mechanisms

for plane strain is met, and the assumption of α = ϕ is used. The angle γ becomes the
only optimization parameter.

6.1.5
Internal Angle of Friction
As previously described, the failure mechanisms are treated as a plane strain problem.
This means that the choice of the angle of friction, ϕ, is important for the estimation of
the capacity of the keyed shear connections. The angle of friction depends on a number
of factors, e.g. aggregate content and size, and is earlier investigated by a number of
researchers. For normal strength concrete, an angle of friction equal to 37◦(k = 4) is
commonly adopted, whereas for mortar it was found that an angle of friction equal to
30◦(k = 3) can be used with reasonable accuracy, cf. Sørensen [13]. This means that
ϕ = 30◦will be adopted in this thesis since the connections are grouted with mortar.

8.3
Sources of Error
The following sections describe the possible sources of errors that have been associated
with construction and testing of the test specimens.
8.3.1
Construction of Test Specimens
The U-bars were not bent so they were completely circular as it is seen in Figure 7.2.
This might aﬀect the stresses inside the connections and aﬀect the results.
During the grouting of the keyed shear connections, a vibration table was used to dis-
tribute the mortar around the reinforcement. The vibrations might have slightly moved
the L-shaped elements and changed the width of the connections. However, clamps are
attached to the pallets and test specimens to avoid this scenario.
8.3.2
Test of Test Specimens
During the positioning of the vertical actuator and the steel plate that provides the
uniform conﬁnement, small eccentricities might occur. The eccentricity of the steel plate
can have resulted in a small torsion which possibly inﬂuences the test results. To avoid
any eccentricities, the positioning of the actuator and steel plate was checked before the
start of each test.

9.1.2
Change in Failure Mechanism
It is previously described how the failure mode should change from one to another as
the conﬁnement increased. This is shown in Figure 9.8. It is noticed how the failure
mode should change from a corner cut oﬀto a complete key cut oﬀas the level of
conﬁnement is increased, since this failure mode has the lowest capacity. At what level
of conﬁnement the failure mode should change is dependent on whether or not a diagonal
crack is present. However, all test specimens experienced a corner cut oﬀof one key and
one diagonal crack, which is not in accordance with the theory.
Figure 9.8: The upper bound models based on Mechanism A, B, C and D.
Page 76 of 197
Department of Technology and Innovation
==PAGE:90==
9 Experimental Analysis
There may be several reasons why there is no change in failure mode. As previously
described, the geometry of the shear keys has a big inﬂuence on the failure mode. If
the ratio between the height and depth of the keys was smaller, the failure mode may
have changed to a complete key cut oﬀ. However, it still does not correspond to the
theoretical upper bound models. Another reason for the missing change in failure mode
may be explained by the phenomenon aggregate interlocking. Aggregate interlocking is
a form of mechanism that is able to transfer loads across a narrow irregular crack from
one side to the other by contact between the aggregates. The capacity might be bigger
than ﬁrst expected due to the aggregate interlock eﬀect. This is due to a large inter-
lock area because of the geometry of the keys and small aggregate size, which makes
the shear keys to strong to achieve a complete cut oﬀ. If the key geometry was smaller,
the capacity of the keys might be reduced and a change in failure mode could be achieved.
It can also be discussed that a change in failure mode is not achieved because the test
specimens are subjected to both shear and normal force in combination. The normal
force might make it harder for a complete key cut oﬀto happen because the connections
are pressed together. It can therefore be argued that when a keyed shear connection is
subjected to a combination of both forces, a corner cut oﬀmight be the only geometri-
cally possible failure mechanism.
It should be mentioned that the ARAMIS measurements, seen on the third and fourth
page of every test specimen visualization in the test report found in Appendix A, do not
indicate any form of strains/cracks that should ’trigger’ a complete key cut oﬀ.
Nevertheless, even though a change in failure mode is not happening, it is more conve-
nient with a corner cut oﬀof the shear keys as this failure mode, just after a small level
of conﬁnement, has a higher capacity compared to the complete key cut oﬀas seen in
Figure 6.5. In addition, the corner cut oﬀfailure mode is more ductile since one can
imagine that shearing of a key corner does not happen as suddenly as a complete key
cut oﬀ.

Overall discussion
It can be concluded from the comparisons between the load-displacement curves seen in
Figure 9.13, 9.14 and 9.15 that when the level of conﬁnement is increased during the
tests, the curves ”jump” up and ﬁts with good accuracy the curves when the magnitude
of normal force is the same and the level of deformation of the reinforcement is identical.
This seems to show that when the level of conﬁnement is increased, the cracks are
pressed together and the connection gains the capacity that corresponds to the given
normal force. This means that if there should be a redistribution of the loads applied
to a keyed shear connection where the conﬁnement is increased, the capacity of the
connection is increased. However, the observations seen in Figure 9.16 seem to indicate
that when the level of conﬁnement is signiﬁcantly decreased, the cracks start opening a
lot faster and the capacity drops relatively fast. The capacity is below the expected level
when compared to the specimens with the corresponding magnitude of normal force and
it seems like the capacity of the keyed shear connection ”run out of steam”.

10 Conclusion
10
Conclusion
This thesis presented a study of keyed shear connections between prefabricated concrete
structures. The experimental study of this project has been set out to investigate the
inﬂuence of the level of conﬁnement on the behavior and capacity of keyed shear connec-
tions subjected to conﬁnement and loaded in shear. A number of sixteen test specimens
have been constructed and tested experimentally in order to investigate how the level
of conﬁnement inﬂuences the shear capacity. First order rigid-plastic upper bound solu-
tions have been developed to predict the shear capacity of the keyed shear connections.
Two main failure modes have been investigated when developing the upper bound solu-
tions for the keyed shear connections. The two failure modes relate to local failure of
the shear keys, either complete key cut oﬀor local key corner cut oﬀ. Due to previously
observed diagonal cracks that span between opposite placed shear keys in combination
with one key failure mode, four upper bound solutions have been established.
The observations and analysis of the test specimens have shown an overall remarkable
good agreement between the test results and theoretical calculations. The push-oﬀtests
showed that all test specimens experienced a corner cut oﬀof one key with one diagonal
crack that spans over the other pair of keys. This agreed with the observations from the
ARAMIS measurements. When comparing the test results to the corresponding failure
mechanism calculations, a mean value of 96% and a standard deviation of 4% was cal-
culated. An investigation of the eﬀectiveness factor, ν, and internal angle of friction, ϕ,
showed that the eﬀectiveness factor that is adopted in this thesis satisfy the developed
upper bound models. The optimal internal angle was found to range between 31◦to
33◦, which is a little bit diﬀerent from the adopted 30◦. However, this is due to the use
of pre-mix mortar. The internal angle of friction was then changed in the theoretical
calculations. A new comparison between the new theoretical calculations and the test
results showed that the ideal relation (calculated/tested = 1) ranged from 98% to 101%.
The level of conﬁnement applied to the keyed shear connections has been the main
topic of this thesis. The level of conﬁnement was varied during the test campaign and
it was found that when the level of conﬁnement increased, the shear capacity increased
as well. This was expected, however, there are strong indications that the normal force,
reduced by a friction coeﬃcient, just can be added to the shear capacity. In addition,
an investigation of a sudden change in level of conﬁnement showed that the shear ca-
pacity would then be similar to the shear capacity of a keyed shear connection with the
corresponding level of conﬁnement.
Finally, the failure mode was expected to change when the level of conﬁnement was
increased, however, this was not the case in this test campaign. There may be several
reasons why there is no change in failure mode. The reasons could include the geometry
of the keys and connection, an unexpected aggregate interlocking capacity or the fact
that when a keyed shear connection is subjected to conﬁnement and loaded in shear,
the complete key corner cut oﬀis not possible. Nevertheless, a corner cut oﬀis more
convenient as it has a higher shear capacity after a small level of conﬁnement is applied

to the connection and is more ductile.
Based on the experimental tests, the following tendencies related to the behavior and
capacity of the keyed shear connections may be pointed out:
• The upper bound model with one corner cut oﬀof one key and one diagonal crack
is accurate when predicting the shear capacity of keyed shear connections subjected
to conﬁnement and loaded in shear.
• The adopted eﬀectiveness factor satisfy the theoretical models.
• The optimal internal angle of friction range from 31◦to 33◦when using pre-mix
mortar.
• The level of conﬁnement has a great impact on the shear capacity of the keyed
shear connections. However, not the behavior.
• The normal force can be added to the shear capacity when reduced by a friction
coeﬃcient.
• A sudden change in level of conﬁnement results in an increase or decrease in shear
capacity if the level of conﬁnement is either increased or decreased respectively.
• The failure mode does not change when the keyed shear connections are subjected
to conﬁnement and loaded in shear.
It can be concluded that the push-oﬀtest results obtained within this project generally
show an overall satisfying agreement with the developed theoretical models when using
the eﬀectiveness factor adopted in this thesis. The results from the push-oﬀtests are
consistent and reliable. The optimal internal angle of friction of the mortar range from
31◦to 33◦which agree with previous tests. It can furthermore be concluded that when
the level of conﬁnement applied to the keyed shear connections is increased, the shear
capacity increases as well. There are strong indications that the normal force can be
added to the shear capacity when it is reduced by a friction coeﬃcient. Finally, if the level
of conﬁnement suddenly increases due to a redistribution of loads, the shear capacity
will increase and with good accuracy have the same shear capacity as a corresponding
connection subjected to the same level of conﬁnement.

11 Recommendations for Future Research
11
Recommendations for Future Research
The main focus of this experimental study has been to investigate the inﬂuence of the
level of conﬁnement on the behavior and capacity of keyed shear connections subjected
to conﬁnement and loaded in shear. The observations and analysis of the test results
have shown some tendencies that with good reason could be investigated further. The
following recommendations for further research and variations to the test campaign:
• Variations on the design/geometry of the keyed shear connections when subjected
to shear and normal force in combination, to see if the failure mode change as
the theoretical solutions suggest or verify that when a keyed shear connection is
subjected to both load actions, only a corner cut oﬀis possible.
• Investigation of the optimal internal angle of friction when using a pre-mix mortar
or a self-mixed mortar, to state the most optimal angle for each kind of mortar.
• Investigation on the friction coeﬃcient that reduces the normal force, too see if the
tendency that the reduced normal force just can be added to the shear capacity
and to determine the most optimal friction coeﬃcient value.
• Investigation on the inﬂuence of the level of tension, instead of conﬁnement, on
the behavior and capacity of the keyed shear connections subjected to tension and
loaded in shear, to see if the upper bound model maintain its accuracy at predicting
the shear capacity of the keyed shear connections and show the same tendencies
found in this thesis.
