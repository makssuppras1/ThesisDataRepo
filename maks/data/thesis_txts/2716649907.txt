Abstract
This thesis will look at the possibility of designing, building, and flying a flight computer, on a
rocket with a solid rocket motor. And it’s being done in a collaboration with the Danish Student
Association for Rocketry (DanSTAR) at DTU in Denmark. DanSTAR already has a bigger flight
computer for controlling their bigger rocket (Fornax) so this is for looking into designing a smaller
and more simple flight computer, for their simpler and smaller upcoming test rocket called Firefly.
This project is only about designing and building the hardware for the flight computer Their software
team from DanSTAR are making the software for it. We were also supposed to launch it on a
rocket at the end of this project. The main goal of this computer is for tracking the flight of the
rocket, and send the Telemetry down to Earth. Future versions should also be able to deploy the
parachute when the rocket hits Apogee. The computer is a small PCB measuring 10x3 cm, with
a microcontroller that’s the main processor that has a build in radio, an initial measurement unit
(IMU), an air pressure sensor, some flash memory, and a GNSS receiver. The microcontroller I’m
using is the STM32WL55CCU7, with a radio that broadcast on 868 MHz. The IMU is a Bosch
BNO055, and the GNSS module is a u-blox NEO-M8, an air pressure sensor is MS5611 from TE
and a flash chip called W25N01GV from Winbond. A part of this project was also to look into a
ground station. Still, the solution I ended up going with was to use a normal Raspberry Pi, with
an Adafruit RFM95W radio receiver that can receive signals at 868 MHz. But because of an over-
ambitious time plan internally at DenSTAR they canceled the launch of the small rocket to focus
on their bigger rocket because that is a part of a rocket competition (EuRoC) in Portugal so they
needed to be ready for a certain date. Also due to a lack of volunteers for the software team they
ended up needing to focus all their work on their bigger rocket. In the end, I end up writing a
small bit of software for testing purposes, and I had a friend help me with writing some software
for more elaborate tests. In the end, we tested the computer and we can communicate with the
microcontroller the power supply is working and we can turn on and off the cameras connected to
the computer, and they all worked

Resume
Dette speciale vil se på muligheden for at designe, bygge og flyve en flycomputer på en raket med
en solid raketmotor. Det sker i samarbejde med Danske Studenterforening for Raketry (DanSTAR)
på DTU i Danmark. DanSTAR har allerede en større flight computer til at styre deres større raket
(Fornax), så dette projekt er til at se på at designe en mindre og mere simpel flycomputer, til deres
enklere og mindre, kommende testraket kaldet Firefly. Dette projekt handler kun om at designe
og bygge hardwaren til flycomputeren. Softwareteam i DanSTAR laver softwaren til den. Vi skulle
også affyre den på en raket i slutningen af dette projekt. Hovedmålet med denne computer er
at spore rakettens flyvning og sende telemetrien ned til Jorden. Fremtidige versioner skulle også
være i stand til at sætte faldskærmen ud, når raketten rammer Apogee. Computeren er et lille PCB,
der måler 10x3 cm, med en mikrocontroller, der er hovedprocessoren, der har en indbygget radio,
en Inertimåleenhed (IMU), en lufttrykssensor, noget flashhukommelse og en GNSS-modtager.
Den mikrocontroller, jeg bruger, er STM32WL55CCU7, med en radio, der sender på 868 MHz.
IMU’en er en Bosch BNO055, og GNSS-modulet er en u-blox NEO-M8, en lufttrykssensor er
MS5611 fra TE og en flash-chip kaldet W25N01GV fra Winbond. En del af dette projekt var også
at se ind i en jordstation. Alligevel var løsningen, jeg endte med at gå med, at bruge en normal
Raspberry Pi, med en Adafruit RFM95W radiomodtager, der kan modtage signaler ved 868 MHz.
Men på grund af en overambitiøs tidsplan internt hos DenSTAR aflyste de opsendelsen af den lille
raket for at fokusere på deres større raket, fordi den er en del af en raketkonkurrence (EuRoC)
i Portugal, så de skulle være klar til en bestemt dato. Også på grund af mangel på frivillige til
softwareteamet endte de med at skulle fokusere alt deres arbejde på deres større raket. Til sidst
ender jeg med at skrive en lille smule software til testformål, og jeg fik en ven til at hjælpe mig
med at skrive noget software til mere omfattende tests. Til sidst testede vi computeren, og vi kan
kommunikere med mikrocontrolleren, strømforsyningen virker, og vi kan tænde og slukke for de
kameraer, der er tilsluttet computeren, og de virkede alle sammen.

2.1
System architecture
Now that we have our requirements, we can start to design the board itself. The first thing to do is
to make a block diagram and to get the overall system architecture in place. The main thing is of
course the microprocessor itself; it is the component that controls all the other things in the board
and dose the data processing from the raw sensor data. For what I found out in my research most
people use three sensors to determine the things we want to monitor, those are a IMU, a GNSS
receiver and air pressure sensor, and these need to be connected directly to the microprocessor.
the next things we need is what we are going to do with the data when we are done collecting
and processing them, we need some kind of memory to store them on the computer itself, and we
need a radio to transmit them down to the ground so we can keep a live view of what happening
with the rocket. We also need some kind of switch in order to turn the power on and off for the
cameras. And then that last thing we need is a power source to power everything.

System design
Now that the components have been selected, then I need to start designing our system. The first
thing I do, is to make the schematics. For the schematics I need to investigate how all the sensors
and the MCU is going to communicate with each other. One of the most normal ways to connect
sensors to the MCU is by using the I2C protocol. This is the protocol developed by Phillips in the
1980’s it is easy to use because you can daisy chain all of your components together only by using
two wires. One of the limitations with this protocol is that it is a slower communication bus. Another
option is to use the SPI bus which is a bit faster but it requires 3 wires that can be dasy chained and
one dedicated line from the MCU to each of the sensors. However, one significant advantages of
the SPI bus is it is much faster. We can also combine the two protocols and use I2C on some of
the sensors and SPI on some of the others. The GNSS receiver can also communicate by UART,
but you can only use it between two specific chips like a like a microprocessor and a sensor or two
different microprocessors or the microprocessor and a computer. One of the things we need to
consider is how often we want to get data from the sensors. Because the data processing of the
IMU data it is happening on the built in microprocessor of the IMU. We can look in the data sheet
and see that it can output data with a rate of 100Hz. The barometer has a response time of 1
microsecond which is equal to a sample rate of 1000 Hz, but it makes it easier to process the data
after the flight if we just keeping the IMU and the barometer on the same sample rate. Therefore,
we will use sample rate of 100Hz for both of them. The GNSS has sample rate of 10Hz in its in
its most accurate setting where users three of the four GNSS constellations, which is more than
enough because of the precise data we are getting from the IMU. Because our data rate not above
100Hz, the I2C should be more than enough. Another advantage is that it is easy implement too,
and it is the communication protocol I have most experienced in designing. Because we are only
using one bus for all the sensors it would make it a bit easier designing the software, because
everything is communicating over the same protocol. The flash module only communicates via
SPI, so we will write to the Flash. It is also an advantages that the memory has its own dedicated
bus, because we then can read and write to it as we please and we don’t have to wait for any
other devices on the lines, and because SPI has such a big bandwidth we can write to it in the
same pace as we are getting the data, and processing it. The next thing we are investigating

3.9
Layout
Now when I am finished with all the schematics, I can start doing the layout of the board. When
making a layout for the PCB, it is here where I decide the physical size of the board, place our
components and the design the traces between them. One of the first things we have to decide is
how many layers we’re going to have in our PCB, DanSTAR normally used 4 layer for their boards
for their flight computer. I looked into if I needed a two layer or four layer PCB, but I decided
on a four layer PCB also because that is what being used in all the other boards so we keep it
consistent. With a four layer PCB there is 2 signal layer one on the top and one on the bottom
and in the middle we can have a power layer and a ground layer, and because we have both a
ground and a power layer that makes rooting much easier. The next thing we do is define the
outlines of the board which we decided in our mechanical design 3.10. then I start putting down
by the major components should be the only thing we have requirement about where it needs to
be is the IMU because that should preferably be in the center of the board so if we mount the
board in the center of the rocket the IMU should be in the center of the rocket also and it makes it
easier for it to measure rotations. we also need some distance between the MCU and the GNSS
module Because that is why our two radios are and we need some distance between them so that
you don’t interfere each other. The next thing we place on the board is the connectors as they
need to be on the side of the board, as they need to be accessible to plot cables into them. we
have four different kind of connectors on this board too SMA connectors for the antennas one for
broadcasting and one for the GNSS module. then we have a power connector, two connections to
power the cameras and finally a connector for for connecting to the two external flight computers
which we can put together into one connection. it’s to minimize any damage due to plugging in the
wrong cables I choose to use different plugs on all of the connections. For the power connection I
used Molex Micro-Fit, for two reasons one of them is that DanSTAR it’s already using Molex Micro-
Fit for their power connector and it’s a common use to use molex connectors for power cables in
computers and other electronics. Another advantage as is locks in place with a small plastic lock
on the connection that way it won’t fall out during the heavy vibration of the rocket launch, against
something that’s already been tested as DanSTAR used to them before on their bigger rocket. It
is also rated 10A of power so that it’s more than enough for our needs. For the power connection
for the cameras I choose a connector from JST from the XA line, I have worked with this before
and it works very well. It also has a small plastic lock on the connector to keep the plug in doing
the rocket launches, and it’s rated for 3A of power so it should be more than enough for our needs.
for the connector to the two external flight computers I used I used the same JST XA connector as
I used for the camera power but I chose a 3 pin connector instead of a 2 PIN that way you cannot
pluck it into the wrong one and and ruin the other flight computers.

MCU even though it should be possible through that protocol. But I sold it on some wires to make a
makeshift you are connection to the MCU, and through that we were able to get messages printed
out on our computer. It be a good idea to put a UART on the computer on a future version, also for
extracting the data on the flash chip. As an SWD connection it’s primarily used for programming
and debugging a device not for expecting data from flash memory. A small circuit I designed with
the two MOSFET also who did work as expected, it was able to turn the power on and off for
the two cameras they were connected through the battery via the PCB. One thing that can be
approved for the next version is the antenna circuit going from the MCU to the SMA connector for

Conclusion
The goal of this thesis was to design and build a flight computer for tracking A rocket doing launch
and decent and transmit telemetry to a ground station. And I ended up building a computer that
should be able to do all of our goals. I tested as much as I could, so I was able to program the
