Preface
This thesis is prepared at the Technical University of Denmark within the Department of
Civil Engineering under the supervision of Associate Professor Varvara Zania and with an
external supervisor, Carsten Lyse. The work was carried out in the period January - June
2018 and the thesis marks the completion of the Master of Science in Civil Engineering
and corresponds to 30 ECTS points.
The investigation realized in the thesis is performed in cooperation with Rambøll, where
a case study of a composite breakwater located Morrocco is considered. The target of
the project is to investigate the interaction between the caisson, water and soil during an
earthquake and how hydrodynamic loads are inﬂuencing the response of the entire system.
Kongens Lyngby, October 12, 2018

Abstract
The aim of this thesis is to investigate the behaviour of a composite breakwater and un-
derneath soil layers during an earthquake, as well as the inﬂuence of the hydrodynamic
loads on the behaviour of the entire system. The composite breakwater is composed of a
concrete caisson and rubble mound which serves as a foundation for the caisson.
The ﬁrst Chapter presents the motivation of performing this investigation, also the main
objectives of the project and general presentation of the adopted methodology used in this
study.
The next Chapter introduces into the problem by reviewing the design codes and method-
ologies used for oﬀshore structures design. The introduction into the origin of the problem
is realized through an investigation of the past occurred disasters and their causality. State
of the Art section oﬀers a brief description of other performed studies in the same ﬁeld as
this thesis.
A Ground Response Analysis consisting of a linear, equivalent linear and non-linear ap-
proaches is presented in Chapter 3. Through this kind of analysis, the actual seismic forces
acting on the structure are derived, as the soil conditions have a signiﬁcant inﬂuence on
transmitting seismic waves.
The hydrodynamic aspect of the problem is studied in Chapter 4. A numerical analysis is
performed on concrete wall where several types of acceleration are applied.
The considered case study is investigated in the Chapter 5. Three models are established,
the ﬁrst one represents a ground response analysis of the site only, in the second analysis the
structure is added in the model. While in the last one, a full Soil-Structure-Water Interac-
tion is investigated. The last chapter, presents an investigation related to the uncertainities
of the soil parameters.

INTRODUCTION
1.1
Motivation
The breakwaters have a signiﬁcant role in protecting the coastal communities against dif-
ferent natural hazards. Therefore, a proper design of this structures may save thousands
of lives and prevent disastrous damages on the shore such as Fukushima disaster. Accord-
ing to Figure 1.1 there are numerous areas of seismic activity very close to coastal areas
which caused signiﬁcant damage to the port structures and nearby communities. As the
breakwaters are on the ﬁrst line of the coastal infrastructure protection system, a suitable
design may reduce the level of damage and save lives.
The last few decades several earthquakes had induced signiﬁcant damages to composite
breakwaters followed by severe damages on the coastline. The main cause of the break-
water’s failure is related to the foundation failure, occurring due to liquefaction and poor
consolidation of the soils. However, the seismically induced failure process of breakwater
foundation has not been well understood [1]. The tilting, overturning, sliding and settle-
ment of oﬀshore structures (e.g. quay walls, breakwaters) has been recorded during the
past earthquakes in Los Angeles (USA) in 1994, Kobe (Japan) in 1995, Kocaeli (Turkey)
in 1999, Athens (Greece) in 1999, Sumatra (Indonesia) in 2004 and Tohoku (Japan) in
2011.[10]
A devastating earthquake hit Japan in 1995, also known as Great Hanshin earthquake or
Kobe earthquake. The Mw = 6.9 which lasted for 20 seconds caused signiﬁcant damage
to port structures, 120 out of 150 breakwaters and quay-wall were signiﬁcantly damaged.
A team of researchers headed by H. Seikiguchi investigated the damages caused by earth-

quakes using an acoustical surveying technique. The damages were signiﬁcantly lower than
at Tohoku Earthquake, 2011, due to absence of tsunami. As a result of seismic hazard, the
breakwaters settled (1.4m-2.6m) almost uniformly along their longitudinal axis [11] and
left the harbour unprotected.
The 1999 Kocaeli earthquake with Mw = 7.6 killed around 17.000 people and the nearby
city of Izmit was severely damaged. Several breakwaters in the Izmit bay suﬀered little
damage, also other coastal structures were damaged during earthquake. The earthquake
caused a tsunami in the Sea of Marmara that was about 2.5m high and as in a consequence,
the death of 155 people.
The breakwaters failure does not occur only during severe earthquakes.
The Patras

several moderate earthquakes happened prior to the major settlement of the structure.
The 1984 seismic activity occurred in the vicinity, the rubble mound breakwater suﬀered
considerable damages due to ampliﬁcation of seismic forces by the thick soft clay layer
on which mound was founded. Therefore, those minor damages induced by seismic forces
along with hydrodynamic forces caused the decrease of structural safety factors below the
acceptable level.[12]
The most powerful earthquake ever recorded in Japan struck the coastline of Japan on
March 11, 2011, afterwards named Tohoku Earthquake. The Mw = 9.0 earthquake gen-
erated strong ground motions which signiﬁcantly aﬀected the island of Honshu, also it
induced a series of tsunamis that destroyed the coastal infrastructure and nearby com-
munities including the Fukushima Daiichi nuclear power plant.[3]The highest recorded
tsunami reached up to 40.5 meters. The failure of the world’s deepest breakwater which
was supposed to protect the coastline from signiﬁcant waves, was caused by tsunami, but
after investigating the damages, the main cause was associated with rubble mound failure.
The Rubble mound represent the foundation for the caisson in this case, as this type of
breakwater is considered composite. The currently used design codes and methodologies
have contributed to the acceptable seismic performance of the port structures, especially
when the earthquake motions are in the prescribed design level. Even using design codes
and methodologies, seismic caused failures of breakwaters continued to occur around the
world. A big part of this methodologies are mostly using force-balance approach, where
the port structures are designed to resist a prescribed level of seismic force which is deﬁned
as a fraction of gravity.[21]
The earthquake induced failures of breakwaters have been attributed by several researchers
to either a geotechnical and/or a hydrodynamic instability [12]. The seismic soil response
may result to ampliﬁcation of the ground motion, while excessive shear deformation during
the earthquake may occur. Cyclic strength degradation may lead to a bearing capacity
failure as well. Moreover, tsunamis and sea waves are generated during or after earthquakes
inducing hydrodynamic pressures at the breakwaters. It is quite common that the two

1.3.1
Ground Response Analysis
The seismic waves are deﬁned as the energy which travels through diﬀerent soil layers.
Due to presence of the softer soil layers above the bedrock, the soil motion at the top of
the soil layer is slightly diﬀerent than the one at the bedrock level. This phenomenon can
be investigated using a non-linear Ground Response analysis. Most probably the seismic
forces will be ampliﬁed by the softer soil layers, consequently this will inﬂuence the response
of the caisson. The non-linear analysis along with the chosen soil model will adjust the soil
parameters for each time increment during the earthquake. This will model a very close to
reality soil behaviour and will lead to accurate results.
1.3.2
Hydrodynamic loads
L. Pelecanos et al.[5] developed a numerical method based on a analytic method (Chopra
AK. (1967) [26]) which computes the hydrodynamic pressures on dams. The problem is
solved by using a FE software. For a more realistic approach diﬀerent boundary conditions
of the reservoir are deﬁned. The reservoir water is modeled as a linear material having
proprieties very close to reality. The load was applied as prescribed values of acceleration
in the horizontal direction along the bottom and left vertical boundaries.
1.3.3
Soil-Structure-Water Interaction
This part will represent an investigation of the Caisson-Soil-Reservoir behaviour during
synthetic generated earthquake based on previous records in the South part of Mediter-
ranean Sea. The mutual inﬂuence of each element of the model is very important as it can
signiﬁcantly change the response of the entire system. Hence, a hydrodynamic analysis
along with a ground response analysis will be performed in the same simulation and on the
same model.

2.1
Design Procedures
The devastating consequences of earthquakes were known for many years, but only in the
1960s the ﬁrst design guidelines for port and harbour structures were adopted.
In 1997, the International Navigation Association (PIANC; formerly the Permanent In-
ternational Association for Navigation Congresses), tried to focus international attention
on the devastating eﬀects of earthquakes on port facilities. A working group was formed
named PIANC/MarCom/WG34, which published a book, entitled ‘Seismic Design Guide-
lines for Port Structures’[21]. This book is used worldwide as a guideline for seismic design
of port structures.

2.1.1
Performance based design
The seismic design codes are based on the principle that a structure will resist a certain
design seismic force, but there is no information related to the performance of the structure
at the moment when the force-balance limit is exceeded. Using a conventional design and
assuming that this limit will not be exceeded even for very large seismic forces, will lead
to an exaggerated high construction cost.
In the performance-based design, two appropriate levels of earthquake motions are usually
used.
• Level 1 (L1)
• Level 2 (L2)
The Level 1 (L1) represents the earthquake motions which most likely will occur during
the lifetime of a port structure. The lifetime of a port structure is 50 years. The L1 is
deﬁned as an earthquake with probability of exceedance of 50% during the lifetime of a
structure and a return period of 75 years.
Level 2 (L2) is associated with strong earthquake motions which are not occurring so
frequently. The probability of exceedance for L2 is 10% during the lifetime of a structure,
while the return period is 475 years.

2.2
Damage to breakwaters: Case history
Any kind of failure of a structure is a catastrophic event with disastrous consequences. Each
failure of a structure always leaded to an improvement of the design codes or development
of new design methods. Therefore, a review of several breakwater damages occurred in the
past, will highlight the major problems of breakwaters subjected to earthquakes.
The failure of Patras detached rubble mound breakwater was studied by C.D. Memos
and J.N. Protonotarios[12] (1993). The investigation was carried out by establishing an
analytical model, where the problem was split in hydrodynamic and geotechnical approach.
The hydrodynamic forces were calculated by considering the motion of the virtual mass of
water in the vicinity of the structure. While the ground response analysis was performed
in SHAKE code.
They come to the conclusion that the failure of the breakwater was
caused by several moderate earthquakes.
The failure mechanism was characterized by
major settlement of the structure, while hydrodynamic loads were not the main cause of
the failure. However, this played only a secondary role in the failure mechanism. As it can
be observed in Figure 2.7 the input motion at bedrock level was signiﬁcantly ampliﬁed by
a thick layer of soft clay.

The failure or damage of composite breakwaters subjected to earthquakes may be caused by
geotechnical failure or by the hydrodynamic action. Also, the failure may occur under hy-
drodynamic loads where the foundation already suﬀered a certain degree of damage.
Jianhong Ye [18] in his research investigated the behaviour of the seabed and the composite
breakwater under strong earthquake motions. The investigation was carried out by using
a numerical method and a FE software.As a starting point of the investigation the con-
solidation state of the seabed should be determined, because of the action of hydrostatic
pressures and self-weight of the structure. The consolidation status should be considered
as initial condition for the seismic analysis. The seismic response of composite breakwater
is much more intensive in the horizontal direction than in the vertical direction, also a
signiﬁcant sway of the caisson was noticed, the horizontal displacement at the top of the
caisson was around 46cm. The ampliﬁcation of the seismic load is more pronounced in the
caisson than in the rubble-mound, due to lower slenderness ratio in the rubble-mound. The
seabed response is also ampliﬁed in both directions, while the ampliﬁcation in the seabed
varies with depth, higher ampliﬁcation at the top and it decreases with depth. The hori-
zontal seismic wave is ampliﬁed for all frequencies, while the vertical wave is ampliﬁed only
at low frequencies (0.8-3.3 Hz). The liquefaction was mostly observed at the surface of the
seabed, the maximum liquefaction depth is 2.6m (1D criterion) and 5.2m (3D criterion).
Another observation is related to Young’s modulus E and its inﬂuence on seismic response
of composite breakwater. A soft seabed absorbs the seismic wave energy, but in the same
time the stiﬀness of the foundation must not be aﬀected in order to have suﬃcient bearing
capacity.
R.Ozaki et al.(2004) [2] studied in more detail the stability of the caisson type breakwater.
The developed method of veriﬁcation uses a SDOF system, in order to verify the caisson
stability to sliding and overturning.

presented in Figure 2.10. They stated that the failure mode of the breakwater is assumed

observed form the results that the higher ampliﬁcation is associated with the response of
the foundation soil rather with the structure. High stiﬀness of the structure is the cause of

X. Yang et al. (2014) performed a research [9] related to dynamic response of a rubble-

Displacement according to [9]
For a proper veriﬁcation of the breakwater’s seismic resistant performance, the dynamic
displacement and residual deformations are the most important. Residual deformations
were described in [9] as crest lowering and lateral spreading, where the maximum vertical
deformation was reached at the crest, while spreading occurred at the feet of the slopes.
The ﬂuid-structure interaction have an important inﬂuence on the residual displacements
of the structure. There was observed that horizontal residual displacements is 9.11% larger
than vertical residual displacement for the Friuli earthquake and it is increasing to 15.5%
for Kobe earthquake. These residual displacements are not increasing with the magnitude
of the earthquake, also no evidence was found of the direct relation between these two.
This phenomenon is a result of the frequency content of the ground motions and natural
frequencies of the breakwater.
Hydrodynamic pressures according to [9]
As it was mentioned before the hydrodynamic loads represent a small part of the inertia
forces of the structure. However, they should be taken into account since they tend to
modify the fundamental period of oscillation by increasing the virtual mass [22].
The
hydrodynamic forces were greater than those calculated with Westergaard’s solution due
to the eﬀect of the vertical component of the seismic wave.
It was also observed that
the ﬁrst peak value of hydrodynamic load coincides to a frequency very close to natural

both horizontal and vertical seismic wave.
One of the ﬁrst investigations on hydrodynamic pressures on dams during earthquakes was
carried out by Westergaard in his research [4]. The study was performed in connection
with the design of the Hoover Dam and its seismic behaviour investigation. A straight dam
with a vertical upstream face was considered, subjected to a horizontal harmonic seismic
loading in a direction perpendicular to the dam. The water body is considered to move
back and forth along with the dam, only a certain part of the water is moving with the
dam while the rest of the reservoir is inactive. This dynamic problem was transformed into
an equivalent static problem, where an inertia force was introduced in a direction, opposite
to the direction of acceleration of the dam. The greatest inertia forces due to moving of
the mass of the dam were occurring at t=0,T,2T.. where T is the period of horizontal
vibrations of the foundations. The inﬂuence of the hydrodynamic action of the water was
considered to be neither excessively large nor negligible.
Further, Chopra AK. (1967) [26] investigated the same problem as Westergaard, but the

2.4.1
Types of earthquakes
The Earth’s outermost layer is called Lithosphere and it consists of several large rock slabs
which are called plates. Each of the tectonic plates extends to a depth around 80 km and
forms the Earth’s crust.
The movement of these plates provides an explanation of the earthquake’s origin. The
collision between adjacent lithospheric plates, are main cause of the severe earthquakes
around the globe.
Due to movement of the tectonic plates towards each other, at the
subduction zone a signiﬁcant amount of energy is released which cause the movement of
the crust. The earthquakes might be caused also by the sliding of the plates along the
fault.
The diﬀerent types of movements of the tectonic plates cause diﬀerent types of
seismic waves and intensity.

2.4.2
Seismic waves
During an earthquake, seismic waves radiate from the earthquake source below the ground
surface as opposite sides of a slipping fault rebound in opposite directions thus decreasing
the strain energy in the rocks. Consequently, the seismic source is spread out through
a volume of rock. The wave propagation phenomena caused by an earthquake, which is
not too extreme can be deﬁned using linear elastic theory. There are three main types
of elastic waves make up the shaking and cause damage in an earthquake. These waves
are very similar in many important ways to those observed in air, water and other elastic
solids, but only two of these waves propagate within a body of solid rock and soil.
P-waves
The fastest body wave which can travel through a solid is called P-wave or Primary waves
and it also can be detected ﬁrst. The P-wave can be detected several seconds before the
S-wave arrives. Hence, this method of detecting earthquakes prior its occurrence is widely
used in Japan to alert the population few seconds before the earthquake.

Rayleigh waves
The Rayleigh waves are a type of surface acoustic wave which are travelling along the
surface of solids. Rayleigh waves include both longitudinal and transverse motions that
decrease exponentially in amplitude as distance from the surface increases. They are gener-
ated by the interaction of P- and S- waves at the ground surface and travel with a velocity
that is lower than the P, S, and Love wave velocities. The particle motion of surface waves
is larger than that of body waves, so the surface waves tend to cause more damage. In the
case of Rayleigh waves, the motion is of a rolling nature, similar to an ocean surface wave

GROUND RESPONSE ANALYSIS
Ground Response Analysis(GRSA) is a practical method of estimating the seismic response
of soil. Therefore, it is widely used to predict the ampliﬁcation or attenuation of seismic
signal. The behaviour of the soil during a seismic event is non-linear, consequently a non-
linear GRSA provides most accurate results. However, there are other more conservative
methods which estimates the seismic behaviour of the soil, such as One-Dimensional(1D)
Linear Ground Response analysis and Equivalent Linear Analysis.
3.1
Linear analysis
Since the soil has a non-linear behaviour during earthquakes, the ground response analysis
for linear elastic soil can be considered a crude approximation of the dynamic behaviour
of the soil. Therefore, to have a better understanding of the calculation methods and how
results are inﬂuenced by the type of analysis, a linear analysis is performed. This section
is mainly focusing on the Linear ground response analysis, where the results are validated
using PLAXIS, DeepSoil and MatLab.

elastic soil.

The Hardening Soil Small stiﬀness (HSS) model is not primary developed for this kind
of analyses, but it showed good results in other researches as well as several validations
performed by Brinkgreve et al. [19]. Hence, this soil model will be used for Non-linear
ground response analysis.
In dynamic conditions, the soil is subjected to cyclic shear loading. The hysteretic loop
generated during cyclic shear loading and it has two sequences: the loading and unload-
ing paths, this happens because of the irreversible behaviour of the soil. The Hardening
Soil Small stiﬀness (HSS) model was developed by Benz [27]. The HSS assumes elastic
behaviour during unloading and reloading. But the strain range in which the soil can be
considered truly elastic is very small. Once the strain increases the soil stiﬀness decreases
non-linearly. The HSS model which is used in MIDAS is based on the Hardening Soil (HS)
model and uses the same parameters as HS except the two additional parameters.

4.1
General information
Earthquake behaviour of the dams which are retaining a considerable amount of water was
investigated by several researchers, using analytic as well as numeric methods. The Caisson
type breakwaters are not concrete dams, but the dynamic action of the reservoir on the
vertical dam uses the same principle as dams. The diﬀerence is mainly that breakwaters
have water on both sides and water reservoir is much larger than in a dam case. However,
the height of the breakwaters is lower than the height of a dam, as well as reservoir depth is
lower taking into account that these are structures located close to the shoreline.The weight
and stiﬀness of the caissons are much lower, because the voids are ﬁlled with granular
material while dams are fully made of concrete. Geometrical and Stiﬀness parameters of
the structure will aﬀect the eigenvalues, which consequently might have an inﬂuence on
hydrodynamic loads. The magnitude of the hydrodynamic loads depends on load frequency,
proprieties of the stiﬀdam and foundation, considered length of the reservoir and the
applied boundary conditions (BCs).

5.2
Geometrical parameters of the breakwater
A real type of a composite breakwater has a shape which diﬀers from the one presented
in Figure 5.3. The reason behind these dissimilarities is related to particularities of FE
modelling and its further analysis.
In order to reduce the computational time of the
analyses and minimize possible numerical errors, the shapes of the adopted elements and
soil layers must be simpliﬁed. Therefore, equivalent properties are assigned to each material
where the geometric shapes are simpliﬁed. Adjusting material parameters such as unit
weight, makes the stress/strain state to remain unchanged from the real case. Complex
geometric shapes might lead to an inaccurate mesh set, which during the analysis might
suﬀer excessive deformations, consequently leading to issues related to non-convergence of
the solution followed by analysis failure. An example of several possible shapes that might
generate numerical errors during calculation are thin soil layers or structural elements,
curved shapes or formed very sharp angles. If the soil layer is too thin for the selected
mesh size a poor mesh results, curved shapes lead to generation of mesh elements with
very sharp angles which are unstable during the analysis.

Conclusion
In this section the SSI analysis was performed by adding the breakwater, rubble mound and
vibro-compacted sand to the model. The hydrodynamic loads are disregarded by deﬁning
the sea water as water table, in order to have only the hydrostatic pressures and to ensure
that the total vertical stress in the soil is diﬀerent than the eﬀective one.
The performed eigenvalue analysis, provided the ﬁrst mode-shapes and eigenvalues with
highest MPF. The ﬁrst and the second mode-shapes (Figure 5.41 and 5.42) illustrate a
higher displacement of the top layer (soft mud) and loose sand, while in the grey marl
the displacements are very small. Thus, the eigenvalues of the entire system are mainly
dominated by the eigenfrequencies of the top layer. Grey marl is very stiﬀand thick layer,
consequently its eigenfrequencies are higher and is not sensible to low frequencies.
The relation between shear strains and damping is analysed by comparing the shear strain
diagram (Figure 5.45) along the depth with the maximum acceleration proﬁle (Figure 5.46).
It can be concluded that the shear strains are inﬂuencing signiﬁcantly the damping of the
signal. The induced high shear strains in the loose sand layer and vibro-compacted sand,
are decreasing the intensity of the accelerations in the above layers. Also, the maximum
acceleration proﬁle in the far ﬁeld is compared with the one below the caisson. Higher
accelerations are developed under the caisson, due to absence of the soft mud layer and
existence of an additional vertical load from the rubble and caisson. The soft mud layer is
very soft and high damping occurs there.
The inﬂuence of the maximum input acceleration on the frequency domain is studied by
comparing the Fourier amplitude spectrums of the N1 SYN1 and N3 SYN1 earthquakes,
which have maximum acceleration of 1.37 m/s2 and 2.5 m/s2. It can be noticed (Figure
5.69 and 5.70) that the earthquake which has a higher maximum acceleration, tend to have
the maximum amplitude at a lower frequency. This is a result of how the plastic strains are
inﬂuencing the eigenfrequency of the soil. A stronger seismic input motion induces more

5.7.6
Conclusion
The (SSWI) analysis was performed with the purpose to investigate the inﬂuence of the
hydrodynamic loads on the stability of the caisson and its post-earthquake operational
performance.
The key results are compared with the (SSI) analysis in order to evalu-
ate the relevance of designing the breakwaters by taking into account the hydrodynamic
pressures.
The investigation of the shear strains, highlighted some critical areas where the shear strains
have maximum intensity. According to Figure 5.76, the highest shear strains are observed
at the surface of the vibro-compacted sand, as well as in the soft mud at the boundary
with vibro-compacted sand, also below the corners of the caisson. The shear strains in
the rubble mound are caused by the rocking of the caisson, while the shear strains in the
soft mud are occurring due to big diﬀerence in shear wave velocity of two layers. Such
a variation of shear wave velocity between two adjacent layers, induces a concentration
of energy at the boundaries as the wave velocity drops suddenly.A closer look into the
permanent deviatoric strains, showed the same critical locations as for shear strains, but
the permanent/plastic strains are lower than the shear strains.
The resulted horizontal displacement time-history is compared with those from the (SSI)
analysis.
In terms of maximum horizontal displacement the (SSWI) gives a higher re-
sponse, but the diﬀerence is insigniﬁcant. While the permanent displacements have the
largest diﬀerence less than 5 mm for the N1 SYN2 earthquake. Figure 5.84 illustrates the
distribution of the permanent horizontal displacements, which are mainly concentrated at
the top corners of the vibro-compacted sand.
Besides the horizontal displacements, the permanent vertical displacements/settlements
are compared with the those resulted from the (SSI) analysis. The results are presented
in Table 5.8 and the (SSI) analysis shows a slightly larger values of vertical displacement
than the (SSWI) analysis. In the (SSI) case the settlement is more uniform than in the
(SSWI) analysis, this lead to the idea that rotation of the caisson occurs due to inﬂuence
of the hydrodynamic loads.
Consequently, an evaluation of the rotation is performed and the results are presented
in Table 5.9.
The time-histories of the rotation, show no signiﬁcant diﬀerences in the
permanent rotation angle, while the maximum values are higher in the (SSWI) case. A
higher acceleration is increasing rotation angle, however the input maximum acceleration is
just 0.25g which cannot be considered a severe earthquake. In a case of a severe earthquake
most-likely the permanent inclination will increase signiﬁcantly and the serviceability of
the breakwater might be aﬀected.
The risk of slope failure is evaluated according to Geological Survey’s of California Guide-
line (2008) [37] and the conclusion is that there is unlikely to have slope failure.

6.4
Conclusion
The soil uncertainties are investigated for 3 layers independently. The uncertainties related
to soft mud and loose sand are investigated by re-running again the (SSI) analysis, while the
uncertainties related to vibro-compacted sand are evaluated by establishing 2 combinations
of soil parameters and by changing them in the (SSWI) analysis.
The change in mud properties does not aﬀect the response below the caisson as this layer
is dragged in that area. However, in the far ﬁeld there are some changes in response. It is
==PAGE:165==
Chapter 6. Soil deﬁnition uncertainty
148
noticed that the PI induces changes in acceleration ﬁeld, by increasing the PI, a decrease
in acceleration ﬁeld occurs and vice versa.
If the shear stiﬀness is increased, then the
accelerations are slightly decreasing. The soft mud layer seems to be very sensitive, to
change in the stiﬀness parameters.
The examination of the loose sand layer, has revealed no signiﬁcant changes in the response
in the far ﬁeld and under the caisson. Mainly the accelerations are changed within the
layer, but this does not aﬀect the response at the top of rubble mound. Being a thin layer
compared with others, it cannot induce signiﬁcant changes in the response at the surface
or at least the adopted parameters must diﬀer a lot from the original.
The inclination time-histories showed a slight increase in maximum inclination angle once
the soil stiﬀness parameters are lowered. Maximum tilting angle of the caisson is directly
proportional with the peak acceleration of the input motion. While the relation between
stiﬀness of the soil and permanent inclination angle was not found. Besides the comparison
between the combinations in the same type of analysis (SSWI), several time-histories were
compared with similar one from the (SSI) analysis. The main observation is related to
the maximum tilting angle which is always slightly higher in the (SSWI) analysis. The
permanent deformations have very low values and no diﬀerences were noticed, except from
the N3 SYN1 earthquake, where the permanent inclination was higher in the (SSI) analysis
(Figure 6.35).

CHAPTER 7
CONCLUSIONS AND DISCUSSIONS
7.1
Conclusion
The main purpose of the thesis was to evaluate the Soil-Structure-Water Interaction of
a caisson type breakwater, which is located in an area with moderate seismic activity.
Besides that, the uncertainties of the soil parameters were also investigated. Two diﬀerent
types of analysis were considered: the (SSI) and (SSWI) analyses. The diﬀerence between
these two is related with the way how the sea water is deﬁned. By deﬁning the water as a
water table (SSI), the hydrodynamic loads are going to be neglected, but if the sea water is
deﬁned as a linear elastic material, then the hydrodynamic pressures are activated.
Several locations in the soil layers have been found to be critical. The evaluation of this
areas was performed based on the location and intensity of the deviatoric strains. The top
of the rubble mound, exactly below the corners of the caisson is one of the areas where high
plastic deformations occur. The tilting of the caisson, induces these plastic deformations
in the rubble mound. An evaluation of the risk of slope failure was performed, based on
the Geological Survey’s of California Guideline (2008) [37] and the conclusion is that there
is unlikely to have slope failure. The maximum registered permanent displacement in the
rubble mound is only 33.8 mm, they should be at least 15 cm in order to have a slope
failure. Another, location where the plastic deformation occur is the boundary between
soft mud and vibro-compacted sand. Due to a low bearing capacity of the soft mud, it fails
due to horizontal movements of the area under the caisson.
The maximum horizontal displacement of the caisson is larger in the (SSWI) analysis, due
to inﬂuence of the hydrodynamic loads. While the diﬀerence in permanent displacements
149
==PAGE:167==
Chapter 7. Conclusions and discussions
150
between (SSI) and (SSWI) analyses are very small and can be considered the same. The
maximum horizontal displacement is 81 mm, while the permanent one is 29mm. Besides the
horizontal displacements, the permanent vertical displacements/settlements are compared
with the those resulted from the (SSI) analysis. The results are presented in Table 5.8 and
the (SSI) analysis shows a slightly larger values of vertical displacement than the (SSWI)
analysis. In the (SSI) case the settlement is more uniform than in the (SSWI) analysis, this
lead to the idea that rotation of the caisson occurs due to inﬂuence of the hydrodynamic
loads.
An evaluation of the inclination/rotation of the caisson was performed. The maximum
values are higher in the (SSWI) case, while the permanent one show no big diﬀerences
between (SSI) and (SSWI) analyses. The permanent inclination angle tend to increase
once the peak ground acceleration also increases. In a case of a severe earthquake most-
likely the permanent inclination will increase signiﬁcantly and the serviceability of the
breakwater might be aﬀected.
Regarding the intensity of the hydrodynamic loads, it can be stated that there are overesti-
mated by the Westergaard solution in the range of 15.5%-25%. For N1 level of earthquake
analytical solution overestimates the pressure at the top and underestimates it at the bot-
tom. While for the N3 level of earthquake, the hydrodynamic pressures are overestimated
for the entire height of the caisson. Another diﬀerence from the analytical results is the
point of application of the total force, for each of the cases it is located lower. Such a diﬀer-
ence is inﬂuencing the overturning moment making it slightly lower than in the analytical
case.
The stability of the structure is not highly sensitive to changes in stiﬀness of the vibro-
compacted sand. A softer vibro-compacted sand, tend to induce higher permanent rota-
tions and for some of the earthquakes to increase the total hydrodynamic force.
The analysed caisson type breakwater, can be considered enough stable to fulﬁl the opera-
tional criteria after the earthquake. For moderate earthquakes the hydrodynamic pressures
are overestimated by the analytical solution, however the (SSI) analysis shows results sim-
ilar to (SSWI) analysis in terms of stability parameters, such as horizontal displacements,
vertical displacement and the resulted rotations.
