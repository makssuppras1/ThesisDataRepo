Abstract
HVAC systems are the major contributors to energy consumption in oﬃce buildings in
the USA. They are responsible to provide adequate air quality and thermal conditions
in indoor environments. The prevalent concept to account for thermal comfort in the
HVAC design and control is the Predicted Mean Vote (PMV) model. However, this
approach is not focused on predicting thermal comfort of individuals and is based on
parameters diﬃcult to estimate in real applications.
Personal Comfort Models (PCM) is the state-of-the-art approach to appraise thermal
comfort at a personal level. It takes advantage of concepts such as machine learning
and Internet of Things to combine feedback from occupants and measured data from
a building. This information is used to incorporate personal comfort requirements into
the design and control of HVAC systems.
This thesis project presents two diﬀerent evaluations of PCM based on ﬁeld assessments.
The ﬁrst evaluation considered a speciﬁc PCM approach to control HVAC systems based
on thermal preference with regards to room air temperature. Personal thermal pref-
erences were modeled by a fuzzy logic categorization that allowed to combine inter-
individual predilections, obtaining a demand-driven set point tested in an oﬃce HVAC
system. The results showed that only 28.6% of the occupants improved their thermal
comfort and the energy consumption increased 14.5%.
The second assessment evaluated the performance of PCM and PMV regarding the pre-
diction of personal thermal preferences. Thermal preference votes were appraised as a
function of relative humidity and room air temperature measurements from a ﬁeld eval-
uation. This data was used to train Artiﬁcial Neural Network (ANN), Na¨ıve-Bayes (NB)
and Fuzzy Logic (FSM) methods, comparing them with a PMV-based algorithm. The
results showed that all the methods had a better predictability performance of personal
thermal preferences than guessing them randomly. The diﬀerence between the perfor-
mance of PCM and PMV methods was not substantial.
Unlike expected, the PMV
method showed a considerable capability of predicting individuals’ thermal preferences,
with a probability of 70% of guessing them correctly.

Chapter 1
Introduction
This opening chapter presents an introductory contextualization of thermal comfort
evaluation in oﬃce buildings. Additionally, it is evidenced the problematic appraised in
this thesis project. The frameworks that will be employed as well as the aims of the
project are also presented in the following pages.
1.1
Background and project description
According to Sorrell (2015), a substantial improvement of energy eﬃciency and a re-
duction of the energy demand are needed in order to tackle climate change. The global
contribution from buildings to the total primary energy consumption corresponds from
20 to 40 % in developed countries and 30% of the CO2 emissions, even exceeding the
industrial and transportation sectors.
Among all the building typologies, oﬃces are the most energy intensive of all, accounting
for 50 % of the consumption of non-domestic buildings (P´erez-Lombard et al. (2008)).
The predominant end use consumption comes from HVAC systems, corresponding to 50
% of the total energy consumption in the USA (P´erez-Lombard et al. (2008)). A large
proportion of this energy is used to provide thermal comfort in indoor spaces. Ther-
mal comfort is deﬁned as the ”Condition of mind that expresses satisfaction with the
thermal environment” (ASHRAE 55 (2013)). Studies show that providing an adequate
indoor environment will increase productivity and reduce the number of sick leave days
(Wargocki et al. (2000), Abdou and Lorsch (1994)).
The prevalent approach to account for thermal comfort in the HVAC design worldwide is
based on the Predicted Mean Vote (PMV) model, developed by Fanger (1970) almost 50
years ago. The concept of this model is to predict the overall thermal sensation of occu-
pants (an expression of how people perceive their thermal environment) based on indoor
environment and human related variables. Grounded on the PMV model, the Standards
ASHRAE 55, EN15251 and ISO7730 were formulated, establishing the conditions that
will provide thermal comfort to the majority of the occupants. Notwithstanding, the

PMV method requires data that is diﬃcult to estimate accurately such as: metabolic
activity rate, clothing level and air velocity. Moreover, the method does not allow ad-
ditional input variables than the predeﬁned ones and also shows a poor predictability
performance when applied to individuals, as established by Auﬀenberg et al. (2015).
Van Hoof (2008) suggests that thermal comfort at a personal level can only be achieved
when occupants have an eﬀective control over their own thermal environment.
A new approach to model thermal comfort at an individual level has grown attention in
the precedent years, so called Personal Comfort Models (PCM). Their key characteris-
tics are: they take individuals as units of analysis, focused on using easy-to-obtain and
cost-eﬀective data, employ feedback from occupants and have the ﬂexibility to adapt to
new data and diﬀerent algorithms to process it. Kim et al. (2018b) mention that PCM
techniques not only can be applied to characterize the conditions that will fulﬁll the
individual thermal comfort requirements of an occupant, but also can be used to design
and control space conditioning systems in a building.
The framework applied in this thesis project proposes a performance evaluation of PCM
methods in two diﬀerent manners. In the ﬁrst approach, a speciﬁc PCM approach to
control HVAC systems is tested in a real open-plan oﬃce building. The outcome of
this method, originally developed by Ghahramani et al. (2014), was analyzed in terms
of thermal comfort and energy consumption. For that, feedback from occupants was
obtained from a digital thermal preference survey tested in a continuous basis, while
measurements of thermal environment parameters were registered.
The second approach carried out in this project evaluates the predictability eﬀectiveness
of three diﬀerent PCM methods and a PMV-based algorithm, with regards to thermal
preference.
The information from a ﬁeld study, corresponding to thermal preference
surveys votes and thermal environment measurements, was also employed for this eval-
uation.
This thesis is structured as follows. (1) The conventional thermal comfort models are
described and a review of the state of the art of PCM is described. (2) A brief description
is made about the concept of machine learning, performance evaluation techniques and
the learning methods employed. (3) A description of the technical speciﬁcations of the
oﬃces is made, as well as the considerations contrived for a dynamic simulation. (4) The
results from the user-driven HVAC control framework are shown. (5) The classiﬁcation
performance approach is described and the results are analyzed. (6) A general discussion
is performed. (7) The ﬁnal conclusions and future work are assembled.

Chapter 2
Thermal Comfort Models
This chapter describes the design and characteristics of models that attempt to evaluate
thermal comfort.
A description of the methods that compose the current basis for
thermal comfort estimation in indoor environments is made in the ﬁrst part. The second
part is dedicated to the deﬁnition of data-driven methodologies that attempt to portray
thermal comfort at a personal level, so called Personal Comfort Models (PCM), and the
latest studies in that direction.
2.1
Conventional methods
There are two methods that construct the basis for thermal comfort management in the
current building standardization: PMV and Adaptive Thermal Comfort. The funda-
mentals of these conventional methods will be deﬁned and explained in this section.
2.1.1
PMV
It is not an easy task to set the indoor environment parameters that would satisfy the
majority of users inside a room. One of the most extensively used methods is the Pre-
dicted Mean Vote (PMV), which is able to predict the mean value of the overall thermal
sensation of a large group of people. In other words, it predicts how people will perceive
their thermal environment. The model considers that thermal comfort, deﬁned as the
”state of mind that represents satisfaction with the thermal environment”, is a function
of two personal parameters: clothing insulation and metabolic activity; and four indoor
environment parameters: air temperature, mean radiant temperature, air humidity and
air velocity (Fanger (1970)).
The PMV model, was developed based on a body heat balance and measured data ob-
tained from series of experiments in controlled climate chambers in the late 1960s. It
assumes steady state and considers the whole body is a single element. The calculation
of the PMV index is shown in Eq.2.1.1.

PPD = 100 −95 · e−(0.03353·PMV 4+0.2179·PMV 2)

The PMV index allows to determine the overall thermal sensation of a group of people
under diverse conditions. As a result of that, the fundamentals of the model have deﬁned
the prevalent criteria to account for thermal comfort in the design and control of space
conditioning systems for decades. The basis of the PMV model was also incorporated
in the standards ISO 7730 (2002), ASHRAE 55 (2013) and EN15251 (2007).
Notwithstanding the foregoing, the calculation of the PMV index requires expensive
instrumentation to measure thermal environment parameters, especially air velocity.
It also requires clothing and metabolic rate as inputs, which are diﬃcult to estimate
accurately. It performs better near neutrality, when having low or moderate metabolic
rates, Toftum (2004). Finally, as described by Auﬀenberg et al. (2015), it is limited to
predict thermal sensation for groups of people but not at a personalized level.
2.1.2
Adaptive thermal comfort
The adaptive thermal comfort model is based on the idea that a person is an active par-
ticipant when it comes to reaching his thermal comfort. Here, the following statement
applies: ”If a change occurs as to produce discomfort, people react in ways which tend
to restore their comfort” (De Dear et al. (1998)).

An analysis of human subjective response to indoor climate was made in 160 buildings,
creating the RP-884 database. It was studied how such response was associated with
contextual, architectural and outdoor climatic factors.
The adaptive model is a linear regression model which associates the indoor accept-
able temperatures with outdoor climate parameters. To create the model of thermal
adaptation, three interrelated types of adaptation were taken into account: behavioral,
physiological and psychological. It was found that in buildings with HVAC, the preva-
lent adaptation method was the behavioral one. In this case the predictions of the PMV
method were more accurate, mainly due to adjustments in clothing level and indoor air
speed.

2.2
Personal Comfort Models
Personal Comfort Models (PCM) are state-of-the-art methods focused on predicting oc-
cupants’ thermal comfort at an individual level, commonly based on data-driven learning
techniques. As described by Kim et al. (2018b), the main characteristics of PCM are:
1. Centralized on modelling individuals instead of groups of people.
2. Focused on using cost-eﬀective data, easy to obtain.
3. Use occupants feedback and measured data to create models.
4. Allow a ﬂexible analysis of the data.
5. Capable of getting adapted to new data.
Applying PCM methods allows to comprehend the particular needs of the occupants
with respect to their thermal comfort. These methods have the potential to improve
the control and design of space conditioning systems, taking advantage of the diﬀerent
sources of data that can be obtained from a building. Internet of things (IoT) and Build-
ing Management Systems (BMS) are some of the state-of-the-art technologies applied in
buildings that can beneﬁt from PCM implementation.
2.2.1
BMS and IoT incorporation.
BMS are computer based systems that control and monitor the operation of building
services in a centralized and integrated manner. Their aim is to decrease energy con-
sumption, reduce commissioning expenses and fulﬁll occupants comfort requirements
(Clark and Mehta (1997), Shaikh et al. (2014)). However, there are issues with BMS
systems’ operation mainly related to compatibility, i.e., how a system adapts to the par-
ticular needs of a building (Lowry (2002)). The network of sensors and actuators with a
centralized processor in buildings with BMS, is a suitable scenario to integrate personal
comfort models. The data processing, analytics, optimization and actuation commands
that take place in a BMS computer can be provided with occupants’ response to improve
the adaptability of the system to users’ requirements (Kim et al. (2018b)).
IoT is the interconnection of physical objects, establishing a network that allows them to
connect and exchange data with each other. It has been recently applied in the building
sector, where its main focus is on automation, employing wireless sensors to control
diﬀerent systems in a building. Combining PCM and IoT can enhance HVAC control
operation.
An aggregation of indoor environment sensors and HVAC measurements
with occupants feedback, has the potential to reduce energy consumption and improve
thermal comfort in modern buildings (Chuah (2014)).

2.2.2
Current drawbacks
The prevailing issues of PCM are related to a lack of standardization. They mainly
focus on the predictive capabilities rather than developing a systematic tool for general
applications. Moreover, some of the approaches used in PCM methods are not well-
founded on current thermal comfort research.
2.2.3
How to develop a PCM?
As explained by Kim et al. (2018b), the design of a PCM requires the following procedure:
1. Data collection (Which data should be used)
2. Data preparation (Prepare data for modeling)
3. Model selection (Select the learning algorithm for a given data set and application)
4. Model evaluation (Validate predictive performance of the model and readiness for
its use)
5. Continuous learning (Update the model based on new data)
First, the process starts with a data collection phase, where the sets of data that will be
used are deﬁned. The data then has to be arranged in a format ready to be modeled.
An algorithm should be selected according to the application aims and feeding data.
Unlike traditional data analysis methods based on statistical modeling, a wide variety
of machine learning techniques can be used in PCMs (a topic that will be discussed in
Chapter 3). Later, a model evaluation should be made in order to appraise how good
the model is in predicting individual’s thermal comfort, identifying opportunities for
improvement and providing a basis for comparison among diﬀerent models. Finally, the
model has to adapt to changes in the data, particularly in occupant’s feedback, in order
to not loose prediction accuracy over time.
2.2.4
State-of-the-art
In the last decade there has been an increasing number of studies regarding personal
comfort models, even before the name of the approach was conceived as such. Notably,
many of the diverse attempts to model thermal comfort at a personal level did not
originate from traditional thermal comfort research, but instead from independent mul-
tidisciplinary endeavours between experts of diﬀerent ﬁelds such as: computer science,
mathematical modeling and thermal comfort, among others.
One of the ﬁrst approaches to integrate thermal comfort into HVAC control was by us-
ing the PMV index as the metric unit to integrate users’ comfort. Examples of that are
the eﬀorts of Kolokotsa et al. (2006), Bermejo et al. (2012) and Calvino et al. (2004).
All of them used an alternative multi-valued logic called fuzzy logic, to characterize the
diﬀerent thermal comfort categories given by the PMV. However, that approach relied
on appraising continuously parameters such as air velocity and personal-related param-
eters, which in real applications turned out to be diﬃcult to implement.

==PAGE:24==

Later, the focus of thermal comfort inclusion into control systems had a change of di-
rection. The new idea was to model thermal comfort by using parameters easy and
cheap to determine, taking advantage of the state-of-the-art computer-based modelling
techniques. Diﬀerent learning techniques have since been tried, depending on the data
availability and the focus of the method. For instance, a machine learning procedure
named Bayesian Networks, was the tool used by Ghahramani et al. (2015) to model
thermal comfort preference. Their approach achieved a 70 % accuracy when predicting
votes from occupants in a ﬁeld study.
Another study that used the same technique
was carried out by Auﬀenberg et al. (2015). In such evaluation, the temperatures in
the comfort zone were predicted by using information from the RP-884 data base, the
same one used to develop the adaptive thermal comfort model. This approach showed
an improved predictability accuracy of PCM compared to conventional methods such as
PMV and Adaptive.
The study made by Liu et al. (2007) tried a diﬀerent procedure based on modeling
thermal sensation by employing Artiﬁcial Neural Networks (ANN). For that, continuous
measurements of indoor environment parameters (air velocity, operative temperature
and relative humidity) were used together with personal related parameters obtained
through questionnaires: clothing level, metabolic activity, thermal sensation, among
others.
Despite the above, there has not been many cases where PCM are applied to control
HVAC systems in ﬁeld studies. Fuzzy logic algorithms were employed by Jazizadeh et
al. (2014) and Ghahramani et al. (2014) to model thermal preferences of occupants in
real oﬃces. That information was then used together with measurements of air temper-
ature and air ﬂow from a ventilation system. A comfort-driven set point was calculated
and implemented in a HVAC control system on a continuous basis. The results showed a
reduction of energy consumption as well as improvements of thermal comfort. Neverthe-
less, the performance of that methodology relies substantially on occupants’ continuous
feedback through personal comfort surveys. As described by Jazizadeh et al. (2013),
keeping the consistency of occupants’ participation in thermal comfort surveys is a chal-
lenging task, where diﬀerent types of surveys were developed to overcome that issue.
As a way to avoid relying on human data acquisition through surveys, there are experi-
ments focused on ﬁnding correlations between occupants behaviour and thermal comfort.
The assessment lead by Kim et al. (2018a), applied a Personal Comfort System (PCS)
that allowed occupants to regulate the temperature in their local working area. The
occupants’ behaviour when regulating their local thermal environment, together with
survey information and thermal environment measurements, were used as input data to
feed six diﬀerent machine learning algorithms to predict thermal preference. The results
showed that PCM had a mean prediction accuracy of 73 %, much higher than conven-
tional thermal comfort methods, that were only 53 % accurate.

One of the latest approaches to model thermal comfort are a number of studies that try
to use bio-metrical data from occupants as input data to feed learning techniques. For
instance, Choi and Yeom (2017) used skin temperature measurements and physiological
data to predict thermal sensation, processing the data with diﬀerent machine learning
techniques. Their results showed a 93 % accuracy when predicting thermal sensation.
The characterization of thermal comfort at a personal level and its inclusion into HVAC
control systems is still a developing ﬁeld. There are opportunities of improvement in
the standardization and coordinated eﬀorts to progress further in the implementation of
PCM in real applications, assuring their reliability and performance.

3.1
Why machine learning?
Since its beginning in the early 1950s, machine learning was conceived as the ability of a
machine to mimic humans’ capacity to learn from previous events. The concept is based
on developing a logical procedure or algorithm that improves at a speciﬁc task based on
previous experience. Machine learning is an important sub-area of artiﬁcial intelligence,
which is a broader ﬁeld that aims to develop intelligent, thinking machines.
Nowadays, the concept of machine learning is becoming more and more popular not only
in research and industrial matters, but also in people’ daily lives. It is applied in multiple
ﬁelds, from ﬁnancial trading and online search engines to personalization of marketing
strategies, smart cars and many other applications.
The concepts of data and information are commonplace when referring to machine learn-
ing terminology, and they will be used extensively along this report. The term data sim-
ply alludes to facts collected in a meaningful manner. When data have been processed,
interpreted, organized or structured in a way that is useful, then it is called information.
For instance, in a building, measurements of indoor air temperature along a period of
time can be referred to as data and the average air temperature during occupancy hours
could be mentioned as information.
There are two main types of problems that machine learning methods try to solve:
regression and classiﬁcation. The ﬁrst corresponds to a problem where a set of data
is given and the objective is to predict a mapping function with continuous outputs
(uninterrupted data within a certain range). In the second, an algorithm has to predict

a discrete output (disconnected or discontinuous data) based on evidence associated
with a phenomenon. A group of discrete data is also called class or set. The concept of
classiﬁcation will be further discussed in this chapter, since it is the base for the thermal
preference study carried out in this project. Later, three machine learning techniques
are explained: Fuzzy Systems Modeling (FSM), Artiﬁcial Neural Networks (ANN) and

3.2
Classiﬁcation
In statistics, the problem of classiﬁcation corresponds to identifying to which category
or class (subgroup of data) a new data point belongs. An algorithm that aims to classify
data (so called classiﬁer), needs to be provided with data whose association to diﬀerent
categories or degree of membership is already known. Here the data provided is called
learning data or training data-set (TRD).
For instance, assume that an algorithm wants to classify types of ﬂowers. Then, charac-
teristics such as: size, color, number of petals and diameter; are provided together with
the corresponding types of ﬂowers, which is information used for training the algorithm.
Afterwards, it creates a correlation based on the training data, where diﬀerent math-
ematical methods can be implemented for such purpose. Then, when the algorithm is
supplied with new data about an unclassiﬁed ﬂower (so called testing data (TSD)), it is
able to determine which type is, based on the information previously learned.
A question that now arises is to know how good a classiﬁer performs. When a data
point has been correctly allocated in a category ”A”, it is called true positive. Likewise,
the data that was correctly not allocated in that category, is called true negative. On
the other hand, the data that was incorrectly classiﬁed as ”A” is called false positive.
Finally, false negatives are data that was supposed to be ”A” but was classiﬁed in an-
other category (Fawcett (2006)). Taking into account the preceding, the true positive
rate (TP-rate), also named hit rate or recall, is deﬁned as:
TP rate = True positives
Total positives
(3.2.1)
The false positive rate (FP-rate), also called false alarm rate, is deﬁned as:
FP rate = False positives
Total negatives
(3.2.2)
The TP −rate states the proportion of positives correctly classiﬁed, whereas the FP −
rate gives the probability of wrongly allocating a category as negative. In order to see
the relation between both parameters and characterize graphically the performance of a
classiﬁer, the ROC curve was developed.

3.2.1
ROC curve
The receiver operating characteristics (ROC) is a graphical tool that allows to visualize
and compare classiﬁers based on their performance. It consists of a two-dimensional
graph, in which the FP −rate is placed on the x-axis and the TP −rate on the y-axis.
The main concept behind the curve is to represent graphically the trade oﬀbetween
beneﬁts (true positives) and costs (false positives). As shown in Fig.3.2.1, the larger
the ratio between true positives and false positives, the better the performance of the
curve. The dashed diagonal line in the middle of the graph represents the strategy of
random guessing. That implies that, when guessing a class, a classiﬁer that produces 90
% of correct guesses, also generates 90% of wrong guesses. The aim of a classiﬁer is to
produce points in the ROC curve that are in the upper left triangle of the graph, above
the random guessing line (Fawcett (2006)).

random and bad classiﬁers, respectively. Source: Fawcett (2006).
3.2.1.1

formance of a classiﬁer, it may be wanted to compare classiﬁers only with a single-

which, as the name suggests, is calculated as the area below the ROC curve. The AUC

determines the probability a class will be correctly classify as such. Thereupon, it can
only take values between 0 and 1, where 1 is the maximum and 0 the minimum a clas-

3.3
Fuzzy Systems Modeling
Fuzzy Systems Modeling (FSM) are machine learning techniques based on fuzzy logic,
a multi-valued logic grounded on the statement that the truth of any aﬃrmation is a
matter of degree, introduced by Zadeh (1996). It is used to describe vague concepts and
to handle the idea of partial truth. Unlike classical logic where a variable can either take
the values 0 or 1, in fuzzy logic an answer can also be any real value in between that
range .
In FSM methods the input data is classiﬁed as fuzzy sets. A fuzzy set is a set with a well
deﬁned boundary, which contains elements with only a partial degree of membership. In
other words, a fuzzy set is a class that contains values that are also shared among other
classes. It is used to represent a vague concept described by a linguistic variable, such
as: tall, fast, warm, low and other similar things.
For instance, a gymnastics teacher is categorizing his students based on their heights.
Instinctively, the tallest student of the class will be classiﬁed as ”tall”, whereas the one
with the lowest height will be assorted as ”short”. A student with a few centimeters less
than the tallest will be categorized as ”average”, even though he can also be considered
as tall. In this case, the height of the third student belongs to two fuzzy sets: ”average”
and ”tall”. However, the teacher decided to give him a higher degree of membership in
the category ”average”, which is the reason why he was classiﬁed as such.
How much an input value belongs to a fuzzy set, is described by a membership function.
It corresponds to a curve that describes the relationship between inputs and degrees of
membership. This function can only take values between 0 and 1, although its shape
can be whatever shape is more convenient for the analysis.

==PAGE:30==

Even though probability and fuzzy logic theories may seem similar, as both consider
that true variables can take values between 0 and 1, they have diﬀerent approaches. The
ﬁrst deﬁnes the truth as a mathematical model of ignorance (lack of knowledge), while
the second is a mathematical model of vagueness (lack of clarity).
In order to construct a fuzzy logic based model, three main steps are needed: Fuzzyﬁca-
tion, Rule application and Defuzzyﬁcation. The main concepts behind each process will
be explained in the following sections (Chaturvedi (2017)).
3.3.1
Fuzziﬁcation
As seen in the height classiﬁcation example shown before, the utility of fuzzy sets depends
on humans critical ability to construct appropriate membership functions depending on
the context.
The process of categorizing the data into fuzzy sets is called Fuzziﬁcation. For each
input variable, two or more membership functions are deﬁned, which convert the data
into fuzzy sets. A qualitative category (linguistic value) is then established for every
fuzzy set (high, low, medium). The shape of a membership function can be diverse,
but at least three constants are needed in order to deﬁne its boundaries. Table 3.3.1
displays two examples of membership functions µ(x) used in this project, where a, b, c
and d represent the well deﬁned boundaries of each function.

3.3.2
Fuzzy rules
Even though humans are not rational, they usually make decisions based on rules. Many
of those rules are if-then statements, for example: ”If its raining outside, I should bring
Page 29
==PAGE:31==

a raincoat”, ”If I am hungry, I should eat”. Similarly, the reasoning of fuzzy logic is
also based on if-then rules. A single fuzzy if-then rule has the form described in Eq.3.3.1.
If x is A, then y is B
(3.3.1)
Where A and B represent linguistic values and x and y are the input and output ele-
ments of a certain process or phenomenon. The ﬁrst part of the rule (x is A) is called
Antecedent and the second (y is B) is the Consequent or conclusion (Wang (2003)).
Accordingly, the working process of FSM is classifying outputs based on the category
that the inputs are allocated. For instance, if the CO2 level in a room corresponds to
the category ”very high” a ventilation system based on FSM can increase the fan speed
to the ”high ﬂow” category. Nevertheless, the exact speed that will be provided to the
fan is calculated by a defuzziﬁcation process.
3.3.3
Defuzziﬁcation
Up to this point, the inputs and outputs of the FSM process are established as degrees
of membership within the antecedent and consequent parts respectively.
Converting
the consequent fuzzy sets into quantitative results that can be used for control systems,
corresponds to a process called ”defuzziﬁcation”. There are multiple techniques to trans-
form the fuzziﬁed information into numerical outputs, depending on the types of actions
wanted for the control strategy. The most common used ones calculate the numerical
outcome of a fuzzy set as the average of the elements inside or the maximum value they
reach (Chaturvedi (2017)).

Artiﬁcial Neural Networks (ANN hereafter) are techniques to simulate non-linear prob-

Machine learning techniques that perform based on classic logic reasoning (statements
that are either true or false) are being less employed nowadays. When applying such
methods to real problems, they are either reduced to a small set of things or are not

Bayesian methods are the machine learning applications that use the basic principles of
probability, based on the Bayes’ theorem shown in Eq.3.5.1. This theorem calculates
the probability of an event given knowledge of previous events related to it. (Herlau,
Schmidt, and Mørup (2016))

There are two main machine learning mechanisms based on the theorem previously de-
scribed: Bayesian Networks and Na¨ıve-Bayes classiﬁcation. The ﬁrst one is a method
that considers the relationship between the features that inﬂuence a phenomenon, whereas
the second assumes conditional independence among the variables, i.e, all variables that
aﬀect an event are independent from each other. For instance, y represents the event
that your mother calls you, x1 is the situation that you lost your job and x2 the case that
you have economical issues. Then you hear the phone ringing, so p(y|x1, x2) is the prob-
ability that your mother is calling you either because you lost your job or because you
have economical problems. In the case of Bayesian networks, the relationship between
x1 and x2 should be known in order to apply the Bayes theorem shown in Eq.3.5.1. On
the other hand, Na¨ıve-Bayes classiﬁcation assumes that x1 and x2 are independent from
each other. In the example, there is no connection between the case that you lose your
job and that you have economical problems, but your mother may be calling you for
either of the two reasons. The Na¨ıve-Bayes theorem is expressed as follows:

gives the probability that a random variable belongs to a certain interval. Some of the
most common PDF in Na¨ıve-Bayes algorithms are the Normal, Kernel and Multivariate
Multinomial Distributions.

This chapter describes the method to characterize the personal thermal preferences of

4.1
Field investigation
The aim of the project was to analyze the performance of PCM methods in real appli-
cations. As mentioned in Sec.2.2, PCM are empirical methods focused on the character-
ization of individual’s thermal comfort. They are based on occupants’ feedback as well
as indoor environment measurements, which allows them to adapt to entirely diﬀerent
testing conditions and to have a ﬂexible post processing from the data obtained.
Open plan oﬃces were the scenario selected for the experiment as they represent a chal-
lenge for thermal environment control. They contemplate an agglomeration of many
occupants sharing the same thermal environment, where ﬁnding the right operating
conditions for the HVAC services is a demanding task.
The assessment was made in three open plan oﬃces located in diﬀerent buildings, with
people under diverse working conditions. The ﬁrst two oﬃces, Oﬃces A-1 and A-2 hence-
forth, are university oﬃces, where students work daily on their academic activities. The
third oﬃce, Oﬃce B henceforth, is placed in a company oﬃce building, where workers
performed daily professional activities.
In each oﬃce, occupants were asked to periodically ﬁll a survey where they should state
their thermal preference regarding their thermal environment. They were asked to give
their feedback about their preference to be warmer, colder or no change. At the same
time the survey was being executed, measurements of air temperature and relative hu-
midity were recorded continuously. Additionally, spot measurements of indoor thermal
environment parameters were sporadically taken.

The response from occupants was analyzed with regards to variations of the indoor air

IV. Thermal discomfort proﬁles
A thermal discomfort proﬁle (TD-proﬁle henceforth), corresponds to a graphical rep-
resentation of the personal preferred air temperature and how the discomfort level will
increase for a diﬀerent room temperature, a concept deﬁned by Ghahramani et al. (2014).
It resembles a defuzziﬁcation process (presented in Sec.3.3.3), which generates a function
as the outcome of the FSM procedure.
The further a Ta value is from the preferred temperature (Given by the temperature with
the maximum membership degree in the NC fuzzy set), the higher the thermal discom-
fort is. Correspondingly, the temperatures that belong into the limit categories of the
TPI scale (MC and MW), are the ones with the highest thermal discomfort. Thermal
discomfort is assumed to vary between 0 and 3, as an analogy of the thermal preference
degrees of intensity, described in ISO 10551 (2006). Each point of the TD-proﬁle is then
calculated as a multiplication of the numbers 3, 2 , 1 and 0 with the degrees of member-
ship of the categories from the furthest to the closest to the preferred temperature. As
Page 45
==PAGE:47==
Chapter 4. Methodology
represented in Fig.4.2.4 A and B, the fuzzy logic characterization from Occupant A is
converted into a TD-proﬁle as a multiplication between the degrees of intensity (shown
above the fuzzy sets) and the degrees of membership. For example, if a Ta of 21 oC on
the proﬁle from Occupant A is taken into account, the corresponding thermal discom-
fort level will be calculated as the multiplication: TD(Ta = 21) = 0.7 · 2 + 0.3 · 3 = 2.3,
represented by the red dashed lines in the graph. Likewise, considering a Ta of 25.2oC,
the discomfort level is calculated as: TD(Ta = 25.2) = 1·0 = 0. This corresponds to the
preferred Ta since it yields the minimum TD. All the points that constitute a TD-proﬁle
are calculated in the same manner.

VI. Set point calculation
The estimation of a demand-driven set point for the HVAC system is a result of the
optimization of the combined TD-proﬁles. The set point corresponds to the air temper-
ature that minimizes the thermal discomfort of all the occupants combined, represented
as the red dashed vertical line in Fig.4.2.5. However, in order not to obtain extreme
temperature values as set points, a lower limit of 20 oC (LL line) and an upper limit of
26 oC (UL line) were assumed. Those values correspond to the lower and upper tem-
perature limits recommended in EN15251 (2007) for energy calculations in oﬃces with
indoor climate category II, given clothing levels between 1 clo and 0.5 clo.

Chapter 10
Classiﬁcation performance
In this chapter is described the evaluation of PCM methods and the PMV model with
regards to their classiﬁcation performance when predicting occupants’ thermal prefer-
ences. In the ﬁrst part is deﬁned the methodology applied to construct and evaluate the
methods. A sensitivity analysis is then performed to deﬁne the optimal conﬁguration of
each method. In the last part, the outcome of the classiﬁcation of real data is examined.
10.1
General description
Sec.2.2 described that the aim of Personal Comfort Models (PCM) is to characterize
the thermal comfort of occupants at an individual level. This approach can be used for
climate control strategies, where the information proceeds from a combination between
direct feedback from occupants with measurements of environmental parameters. As
described by Kim et al. (2018b), the ﬂexibility of PCM allows them to use diﬀerent
algorithms to process the data and compare them with each other. The method that
will be used for control strategies will be the one that shows the best performance in
terms of predictability of individuals’ thermal comfort.
Based on the thermal preference data obtained from each participant, it is aimed to
evaluate the performance of diﬀerent algorithms with respect to how well they perform
predicting thermal preference. As described in Sec.7.1, measurements of indoor air tem-
perature Ta and relative humidity RH were taken for each occupant’s thermal preference
index TPI. Until this point, only the Ta measurements were used to create a thermal
preference proﬁle for each occupant, according to the UD-HVAC framework.
In this chapter, the purpose is to assess the predictability of the thermal preference
votes (TPI) based only on measurements of Ta and RH. Even though, there are more
parameters that inﬂuence thermal comfort, such as: air velocity, radiant temperature,
clothing level and metabolic activity; the approach in this assessment seeks to employ
parameters that are easy and cheap to measure in a continuous manner.
This will
allow to evaluate the potential use of classiﬁcation methods to control HVAC systems

in open-plan oﬃces.
10.2
Considerations for the performance evaluation
The assessment consists of testing diﬀerent methods based on their ability to predict
three diﬀerent thermal preference classes or categories: ”Warmer”, ”No change” and
”Colder”. That is to say that each method will have to predict whether an occupant
wants higher, lower or no variation of the air temperature in his/her thermal environ-
ment.
Four diﬀerent methods were taken into account, three PCM-based algorithms: Artiﬁcial
Neural Networks (ANN), Na¨ıve Bayes (NB), Fuzzy Systems Method (FSM) and one al-
gorithm grounded on the Predicted Mean Vote (PMV) method. The ﬁrst two methods
were provided with measurements of Ta and RH as inputs and the thermal preference
categories as outputs. The three classes consider TPI values from 0 to 7 to be ”Colder”,
8 to 10 for ”No change” and 11 to 18 for ”Warmer”.
The algorithms were provided with part of the total amount of data from an occupant
to create a connection between inputs and outputs, deﬁned as training data (TRD).
Then, their performance is tested with inputs and outputs that were not used before for
training, denominated testing data (TSD).
The FSM method was only provided with the thermal preference categories as outputs
and the corresponding Ta measurements as inputs. The reason only one parameter was
used, is because the methodology to create the fuzzy sets was the same one applied for
the UD-HVAC approach in Chapter 8. Including RH measurements as inputs would
complicate the set up of the FSM model and would not allow to analyze the performance
of the same methodology used previously in this project. However, unlike in Chapter 8,
only three fuzzy sets were used for the FSM model in this section, representing the same
three classes that the other 3 classiﬁers needed to predict (Colder, Warmer or No change).
Finally, a PMV-based classiﬁcation method was created based on the assumption that
a PMV value below -0.5 is associated to a preference towards ”Warmer” temperatures,
above 0.5 means a predilection to the category ”Colder” and values between -0.5 and
0.5 indicate a preference to ”No change”.
Only three occupants’ data were used as base data to compare the classiﬁcation perfor-
mance. It was denoted in Sec.8.1.1 that the thermal preference votes from the occupants
were not evenly distributed along the TPI scale. Some of the occupants showed less
predilection to one or two categories, which complicates the task of evaluating the perfor-
mance of a classiﬁer when the data is not homogeneously distributed among the classes.
The last is a basic premise to evaluate the performance of classiﬁers, mentioned by Ferri
et al. (2009).
Taking that into consideration, the three occupants with more evenly

distributed votes along the TPI scale are considered for the analysis in this chapter:
occupants 1, 7 and 8 from Oﬃce A-1.
The method applied to evaluate the classiﬁcation performance corresponded to the ROC
analysis explained in Sec.3.2. The classiﬁers were built as discrete classiﬁcation methods
i.e., they give a class label as output and not the probability of belonging to a class.
For instance, given a measurement of Ta and RH, the classiﬁer may give the following
answer: ”those conditions will make occupant X prefer a warmer room air temperature”.
The previous statement can be right or wrong, for the reason that every output was
categorized either as a false positive (FP), false negative (FN), true positive (TP) or
true negative (TN) (explained in Sec.3.2). Based on the numbers of correct and incorrect
answers, the ROC curve was created for each category by using only three points. The
ﬁrst point was the false positive rate (FPR) and true positive rate (TPR) in the x and y
axis respectively; and the other two were simply the thresholds 0 and 1 in the plot. The
Area Under the Curve (AUC) was then determined as the average between the areas
under the curves of each class predictions of a classiﬁer. That allows to have a simple
scalar number instead of a whole plot, to characterize the performance of a classiﬁer.
10.3
Sensitivity analysis and method assembly
For the classiﬁcation algorithms considered there are multiple possible conﬁgurations,
described more in detail in Chapter 3. It was considered in this evaluation that the
comparison between classiﬁers was between the best performing adjustments of a clas-
siﬁer in terms of the AUC value produced. Accordingly, in order to ﬁnd the optimal
performance of a classiﬁer, a sensitivity analysis was executed in such a way that the
parameters chosen to construct an algorithm were those that yielded the highest AUC
value. In Fig.E.3.1, located in the Appendix E, are illustrated the sensitivity analysis
results of the four classiﬁers tested. In all methods, except from PMV, the ratio between
the training and total data was varied to ﬁnd an optimal balance. That is to say that
from all the data available from each occupant, it was found the optimal proportion
between TRD and TSD.
In the case of the PMV-based method, the calculation of the PMV index was made by
the implementation in Matlab of the algorithm deﬁned by ASHRAE 55 (2013). Three
parameters that are normally assumed were varied in this algorithm to ﬁnd its best per-
forming conﬁguration. The metabolic activity rate of the occupants was varied between
1-2.1 [met], corresponding to the range of typical physical activities performed in oﬃces,
according to ASHRAE 55 (2013). Also, the clothing level was varied between 0.5-1.3
[clo], which accounts for typical garments for winter and summer, represented in Table
A.0.2 placed in the Appendix A. Finally, the air velocity was varied in the range 0.1-1,
representing the maximum mean for summer and winter in landscaped oﬃces, described
in ISO 7730 (2002). The ﬁnal conﬁguration of the PMV-based method used in the clas-
siﬁcation performance analysis is presented in Table E.4.2 in the Appendix E.

I. Acquisition of the data
The Ta and RH measurements and the corresponding thermal preference categories are
read by the algorithm.
II. Data standardization
The input data is standardized to eliminate the inﬂuence of diﬀerent orders of magnitude
of the data in the learning process of an algorithm (Herlau, Schmidt, and Mørup (2016)).
This process is described in Sec.E.1 in the Appendix E.
III. Data ordering
The data of each occupant is ordered randomly only once to minimize the inﬂuence of
the data arrangement. The same random order for each occupant’s data set is kept
Page 83
==PAGE:85==

during the whole sensitivity analysis.
IV. Division between TRD and TSD
The total amount of data is divided between training data (TRD) and testing data
(TSD), for which diﬀerent ratios were tested to ﬁnd the optimum. For instance, if the
ratio between training/total data is 0.4, the ﬁrst 40% of the data from step (III) is used
for training and the rest for testing.
V. Training phase
Here, the algorithm establishes a connection between Ta and RH with the thermal
preference categories. In the case of NB, through iteration, the method calculates the
standard deviation and mean of the TRD that best ﬁts a Probability Density Function
(PDF). Three diﬀerent PDF were tested in order to adjust the input and output data:
Normal, Kernel and Multivariate Multinomial Distribution (mvmn).
In the case of ANN, the algorithm calculates the weights w that best describe the
connections between inputs and outputs, through an iterative process. To convert the
data from one layer to the following one, a transfer function is needed.
Since three
types of layers are considered, two transfer functions can be used at the same time
in the algorithm. Three diﬀerent transfer functions were tested: Log-Sigmoid (logsig),
Hyperbolic-Tangent Sigmoid (tansig) and a Linear transfer function (purelin), (a better
representation of those functions can be found in Sec.3.4). Additionally, the number of
hidden layers was also varied to ﬁnd the highest performing value.
VI. Testing phase
The outputs of the algorithms are obtained and compared with the real outputs. The
AUC is then calculated based on the number of times a thermal preference category is
correctly guessed. Since ANN tends to produce highly speciﬁc results for one set of data
(Herlau, Schmidt, and Mørup (2016)), over-ﬁtting and the number of iterations were
veriﬁed for this algorithm, as shown in Fig.E.2.1 in the Appendix E.

Chapter 11
Discussion
This thesis project aims to analyze the outcome of the UD-HVAC framework in terms
of energy consumption and thermal comfort. The project also seeks to compare PCM
and PMV methods with regards to personal thermal preference predictability. Finally,
it is aimed to give recommendations to implement PCM methods in real applications.
Taking into consideration the goals of this project, a discussion of the results is made in
this chapter. First, the characterization of occupants’ thermal preferences is examined,
appraising inﬂuential factors observed in the ﬁeld study. The implementation of the UD-
HVAC approach is then analyzed, considering the outcomes in terms of thermal comfort
and energy consumption. Later, the classiﬁcation performance of the PCM and PMV
methods tested is discussed. How PCM methods can be implemented in oﬃce buildings,
is the following topic assessed in this chapter. Finally, the limitations of the framework
applied in this thesis project are presented, including opportunities of improvement for
future assessments.
11.1
TPI variation during the evaluation
The results presented in Sec.7.1 evidenced that a larger number of votes per person
was achieved in Oﬃce A compared to Oﬃce B as a result of the methodology applied
to change the room air temperature in each case. Changing the temperatures actively
(opening windows and turning on/oﬀelectric heaters) as performed in Oﬃce A, gener-
ates more participation in the survey. Although, people will keep their participation level
as long as there are incentives to do it. A higher participation is a response to an active
agent (person executing the evaluation) who is making them lose their current comfort
level, even when the actual room temperature does not change considerably. Varying
the temperature passively (without participants being aware of it) as executed in Oﬃce
B, will induce a lower number of votes per person and the proportion of occupants par-
ticipating will be less. However, the participation level will be more constant along the
surveyed period, as illustrated in Fig.7.1.2. Erickson and Cerpa (2012) suggested that,
by increasing their participation, occupants may expect to give their preferences more
88
==PAGE:90==
Chapter 11. Discussion
importance and thus improve their comfort level. This is something that has to be taken
into consideration when performing a participatory sensing application.
Taking a look into the variability of the thermal preference votes with regards to the
variability of Ta and RH presented in Fig.7.2.1, it is noted that a larger variation of these
two thermal environment parameters produces more scattered TPI values. Nevertheless,
the results suggest that there were factors that inﬂuenced the variation of TPI that were
not related to Ta nor RH. Those elements could be thermal environment related or
associated with the methodology applied in the assessment. Air velocity is a parameter
that was not measured and it was likely to have an important eﬀect on the results.
Occupants placed next to windows, ventilation diﬀusors or even corridors, could have
been intermittently aﬀected by the air draft around them. Another factor is related
to the manner the temperatures were varied in Oﬃce A-1, as mentioned earlier in this
report. Opening windows and controlling actively space conditioning devices inside the
oﬃce had a psychological and behavioural eﬀect on the participants. The temperature
drift presented in Table 7.3.1 was also a factor that inﬂuenced the TPI variability. Some
participants’ thermal preferences were aﬀected by the rate the air temperature was varied
more than the temperature level itself, based on the ﬁndings from Kolarik et al. (2009).
As a consequence of all the factors previously mentioned, the votes of participants did
not reﬂect entirely the variations of air temperature or relative humidity. This has an
important eﬀect when modeling personal thermal preferences, which will be analyzed in
the following section.
11.2
Implementation of UD-HVAC control
11.2.1
Characterization of personal thermal preference
The results in Sec.8.1 showed that is possible to model personal thermal preferences
by using the fuzzy logic approach described in Sec.4.2. The study from Ghahramani
et al. (2014) presented well formed fuzzy sets as well as their corresponding TD-proﬁles,
similar to the example case illustrated in Fig.4.2.4. Occupants in that study were brought
under a temperature variation between 19 to 31oC during the evaluation. On the con-
trary, the results presented in Sec.8.1 exhibited that some of the fuzzy sets were not well
deﬁned and there were incomplete TD-proﬁles. In the ﬁeld assessment performed in this
project was only possible to vary the temperature up to 26oC and just brieﬂy down to 20
in the case of Oﬃce B, observed in Fig.8.1.1 and Fig.8.1.2. This had an inﬂuence on how
well the personal thermal preferences were modeled by using the fuzzy logic framework
presented in Sec.4.2.
Fanger (1975) determined the standard deviation of the preferred temperature of a group
of seated persons for diﬀerent clothing levels and air velocities. He found that the temper-
ature standard deviation was the lowest when no clothing and the highest air velocity
tested (5 [m/s]) were taken into account. The standard deviation for people without

The standard deviation of the preferred temperatures found in the TD-proﬁles of Oﬃce

low result for Oﬃce B suggests that the preferred temperatures for occupants from this

To have a better representation of occupants’ thermal preferences is required that the
TPI values are as distributed as possible along the thermal preference scale (from 0 to
18) and this variation should be fundamentally caused by a large variability of the air
temperature, an issue discussed in the previous section. If the preceding conditions are
not fulﬁlled, two consequences may arise. One possibility is not being able to create the
TD-proﬁle for an occupant since their thermal preference categories or fuzzy sets cannot
be deﬁned. This was the case for Occupants 9 and 11 from Oﬃce B (Fig.8.1.2D and
F respectively). In their thermal preference proﬁles, an increase of Ta does not mean a
decrease of TPI neither the opposite. This precludes the formation of more than one
fuzzy set, thus, the creation of a TD-proﬁle. The other possibility implies that, due to
a lack of variability of the TPI values, an extrapolation will be required to deﬁne the
uttermost fuzzy sets. The preceding is observed in basically all the proﬁles from Oﬃce B
shown in Fig.8.1.2. This does not necessary represent in reality how an occupant would
prefer his/her thermal environment.
The results from the correlation analysis illustrated in Fig.8.1.3 showed the dependency
between TPI and Ta. The closer a correlation coeﬃcient is to either the value 1 or -1,
the stronger is the relationship between the variables that form a thermal preference
proﬁle. On average, the correlation for occupants from Oﬃce A-1 is higher than from
Oﬃces A-2 and B. Nevertheless, in Oﬃce B the correlation reaches higher values (up to
0.66) compared to the other oﬃces. In Oﬃce A-1 there is a larger amount of outliers or
values appreciably outside the trend of the majority (see Fig.8.1.1 A, B, F and G). Those
values are caused by the same reasons discussed at the end of the previous section: an
inﬂuence of not measured thermal environment parameters and changing the room air
temperature actively.
In spite of the fact that an occupant may had an active participation in the thermal
preference survey, his/her preferences were not included in the demand-driven set point
calculation if his/her thermal preference proﬁle was narrow (lack of TPI variability) or
without a clear TPI-Ta correlation.
It is expected that some participants will not have a major predilection to diﬀerent tem-
peratures, even when the air temperature was considerably modiﬁed. An example of the
last was the preference proﬁle of Occupant 7, illustrated in Fig.8.1.2-C, in which there
was no correlation between TPI and Ta, even though it was an active participant along

11.2.2
Thermal comfort evaluation
From the set point calculation illustrated in Fig.8.2.1, it was estimated that when the
UD-HVAC set point was implemented in Oﬃce B, non of the participants considered in
the optimization would vote outside the TPI range of 6 to 12. Nevertheless, as observed
in Fig.8.2.5, only 3 out of 7 participants showed a thermal preference within that range
in the third week. In fact, only 28.6% of the occupants that participated actively in the
survey improved their thermal comfort with the UD-HVAC set point.
There were three main sources of errors that inﬂuenced the outcome of the UD-HVAC
framework in the ﬁeld assessment. First, there were Hobo-loggers placed far from where
the occupants were sitting, considering that their measurements were used to create the
corresponding TD-proﬁles, illustrated in Fig.4.1.3. This caused a systematic error, as
each TPI from those occupants was associated with a wrong Ta. Second, the actual
temperature supplied in the room diﬀered from the comfort-driven set point calculated
in the optimization. As shown in Fig.8.2.2 the set point applied in the BMS system
did not match with the comfort-driven set point calculated of 24.5oC, probably because
the BMS system was not able to incorporate it. Third, the temperature measurements
used to create the TD-proﬁles, obtained from the Hobo-loggers (see Fig.8.2.4), diﬀered
from the temperature read by the BMS system (see Fig.8.2.3). This implied that the
optimized comfort-driven set point was not in accordance with what the BMS system
was measuring. In practice, some occupants were exposed to the highest temperatures

The variability of a person’s preferred temperature from day to day was studied by

constantly the information provided to control HVAC systems based on thermal prefer-

is more appropriate or not. Assessing thermal comfort through a participatory sensing

strategy at a personal level, without requiring information about the clothing level of
occupants or which activity are they performing. Nevertheless, it is not possible to see
the inﬂuence of indoor air velocity on the thermal preference votes.

11.3
Classiﬁcation performance
The three thermal preference proﬁles most distributed along the TPI scale were se-
lected for the classiﬁcation performance analysis. In spite of that, the data from those
occupants was not homogeneously distributed among the classes,i.e., their data was not
equally dispersed into the three categories considered for this evaluation: Warmer, No
change and Colder. Homogeneous distribution is a basic assumption to apply a multi-
class AUC to evaluate the classiﬁcation performance, described by Ferri et al. (2009)
in Sec.3.2.1. Since only 3 out of 15 proﬁles were selected for the evaluation, in reality,
it is unlikely to obtain homogeneously distributed data from all occupants in an oﬃce.
It is required that their TPI votes are scattered by putting them under diﬀerent room
temperatures during equal periods of time, when obtaining the training data. Hence, it
is very challenging to characterize accurately the classiﬁcation performance of a learning
algorithm that aims to predict occupants’ thermal preferences.
In Sec.10.4 the classiﬁcation performance results showed that the performance of all
the four methods tested is better than random guessing (AUC=0.5). Therefore, all the
classiﬁers tested will probably predict more positive instances than negative ones, which
corresponds to forecasting more accurately thermal preference votes than simply guess-
ing them.
The best performing method corresponds to the Na¨ıve Bayes algorithm (NB), being able
to predict 73% of the thermal preference votes correctly, given only measurements of RH
and Ta (shown in Fig.10.4.1). Additionally, the NB method also exhibits less diﬀerence
in performance when predicting diﬀerent classes, in spite of the diﬀerence in the amount
of data between them (shown in Fig.10.4.2).
The PMV-based method reaches a similar performance than the NB method, which
showed a correct predictability of 70% of the thermal preference votes (shown in Fig.10.4.1).
This remarkable result is opposed to the ﬁndings obtained by Kim et al. (2018a), where
the mean AUC of a PMV based method was only 52%. There are three main diﬀerences
between their considerations and the procedure applied in this thesis project.
They
did not optimize the air velocity, clothing level and metabolic rate to obtain a better
predictability. Their occupants voted directly either of the three categories instead of
selecting a TPI value. Finally, in this assessment only 3 occupants’ data sets were ex-

amined, contrarily, the study from Kim et al. (2018a) analyzed 34 occupants. For a more
appropriate comparison it would be pertinent to contrast both approaches for a similar
number of occupants and an equivalent amount of information from them.
Considering the aforementioned, there is not a substantial diﬀerence between the classi-
ﬁcation performance between the PCM methods tested and the PMV-based algorithm,
under the considerations taken in this evaluation. Nevertheless, it would be of interest
to make a comparison between both for a longer period of time, taking into account
seasonal variations of thermal preferences. More data could be used to train the PCM
methods, eventually improving their predicting performance.
The results displayed in Fig.10.4.3 revealed that the NB method requires less informa-
tion than the other PCM. The reason of this is related to the mechanism behind the
learning process of each method. An ANN model creates a solution by adjusting the
weights w, which are factors that activate each neuron where each transformation takes
place. That is made to diminish the error between the predictions and the actual data
(mentioned in Sec.3.4). The more training data is supplied to the model, the better
adjusted the weights will be, improving the predicting performance. Similarly, the NB
model adjusts a Probability Density Function PDF, calculating the mean and standard
deviation of the training data, which are then used to predict the testing data. Thus, a
PDF becomes better adjusted when more training data is provided. However, since NB
adjusts a function and not individual parameters, it needs less information than ANN.
This machine learning method needs to adjust the weights of each neuron to create a
network that represents accurately the phenomenon. Consequently, large amounts of
data are needed to improve the performance of an ANN method.
Unlike the other two learning techniques, the FSM method implemented does not rely
on an iterative process in order to diminish the error between predictions and the real
data. The training data is used to create the fuzzy sets, by calculating the maximum
memberships (center of a fuzzy set) and their boundaries. Then, the testing data is pre-
dicted according to the fuzzy sets previously created. Providing more training data will
not reduce the predicting error and will not change considerably the shape of the fuzzy
sets calculated with less data. Consequently, the predicting performance will diminish
with a larger amount of training data, as illustrated in Fig.10.4.3.
When implementing a participatory sensing approach, the amount of data provided to
the learning methods is proportional to the participation of the occupants. Using a NB
algorithm allows to have less information to learn occupants’ preferences, without di-
minishing considerably its predicting performance. Similarly, less information is required
when implementing a FSM. Having the need for less information from the occupants will
improve their participation for longer periods of time, allowing a PCM-based HVAC con-
trol system to maintain its performance.

11.4
Implementation of PCM in oﬃce buildings
According to Kim et al. (2018b), a PCM needs to receive direct feedback from occupants,
prioritize easy-to-obtain and cost-eﬀective data and have the capacity to adapt to new
data. Based on the ﬁndings of this thesis project, it is also recommended to implement
a method that has a high predictability performance and uses as less training data as
possible. The NB method tested is able have a high AUC, with a reduced amount of data
and only two parameters used as inputs (room air temperature and relative humidity).
This allows to have a reliable method which is capable to adapt to new data, accounting
for variations of the thermal preferences and changes of the occupants in the oﬃce.
The more integrated are the diﬀerent zone temperatures into the control of the indoor
environment, the better performance a PCM method will have. This is specially focused
on landscaped oﬃces areas, where there is a considerable diﬀerence between indoor cli-
mates within one single room. Buildings with networking technologies such as Internet
of Things (IoT) or Building Management Systems (BMS) will be very suitable to in-
corporate PCM-based control. It is not possible to assure an adequate performance of
PCM when zone level control is not present and is unfeasible to implement in buildings
that lack a computer-based centralized control.
To deal with the discrepancy between the air temperatures measured by the BMS and
the measurements used to create the TD-proﬁles, two solutions are proposed. One op-
tion is to implement sensors based on Internet of Things (IoT). This sensors can have
a wireless connection to the BMS system and additional monitoring devices. The same
sensors that provide data to the BMS system can generate the data used either for the
UD-HVAC approach or other PCM-based control frameworks. That would require to
have at least a sensor placed in each zone, where it can capture a representative tem-
perature of the area, being as close as possible to occupants’ workplaces. The second
option is to characterize the statistical correlation between the temperature set point
and the room air temperature read by the continuous measuring devices. Accordingly,

the TD-proﬁles can be constructed in the way that thermal discomfort is deﬁned as a
function of set point temperatures instead of air temperatures. The aggregation and
later optimization of this new TD-proﬁles will provide an indoor air temperature that is

When implementing a participatory sensing approach to collect data for a PCM-based
control systems, a simple survey interface is recommended. The survey applied in this
thesis project showed to be eﬀective and reliable with regards to obtaining occupants’
feedback. However, the voting process can be improved by providing an easier access to
the survey, e.g., through a smart-phone application or a computer program, which could
also be complemented with periodic reminders to improve participation.
11.5
Limitations and opportunities for improvement
• Focus of the optimization
The UD-HVAC approach implemented was only focused on thermal comfort. The
set point obtained was not restricted by energy consumption, which was the reason
why it increased substantially in the dynamic simulation model. Including vari-
ables related to energy consumption, such as: air ﬂows in the ventilation system
or water ﬂows through radiators, would allow determining a balance between a
thermal comfort improvement and a reduction of energy consumption.
• Evaluation period
The limited period assigned for this project did not allow to analyze the outcome
of the UD-HVAC approach regarding seasonal variations of thermal preferences.
Also, it could have been evaluated the period of time a thermal preference proﬁle
was representative for an occupant. More occupants and more votes per occupant
could have been considered in the optimization, as more votes were obtained.
A ﬁeld assessment for a longer period would have allowed a better estimation of the
classiﬁcation performance of the PCM-based methods, having more data provided
to the machine learning techniques. That could have improved the performance
of methods such as ANN, that rely on large amounts of information for a better
performance.
• Inﬂuence on TPI variability
In this thesis project was considered that variations of thermal preference votes
were mainly owed to variations of the air temperature, when enough information
was provided from a participant. Taking that into account, a question arises for
future assessments: What is the amount of votes per occupant that will minimize
the error of assuming that TPI variations are mainly grounded on variations of
Ta?.

• Number of thermal preference categories
Taking into account the reduced amount of occupants’ votes, the number of ther-
mal preference categories should have been reduced from 7 to a lower number of
5 or even 3 classes. Each fuzzy set associated to a category could have more data
points per set. Even though some precision would be lost, as a wider range of Ta
would be inside each category, the fuzzy sets and consequent TD-proﬁle of each
occupant could be better deﬁned.
• Occupancy assumptions
Occupants’ behaviour inﬂuenced the applicability of the UD-HVAC approach. It
is not suitable to implement this method when the occupancy is very low. This
will lead to a lack of votes to characterize the preferences of people in a reasonable
amount of time. It was also assumed that occupants voted in their workplaces,
since it was not possible to verify occupancy. Furthermore, oﬃces where occupants
are able to modify the set points directly by themselves, where they can open
windows and have personal heating/cooling devices not considered in the HVAC
control system, are not applicable for the UD-HVAC framework presented in this
thesis. Nevertheless, the preceding restrictions are not relevant for PCM based on
participatory sensing in general. The information about occupants’ behaviour can
be used as additional training data to improve the predictability performance of
machine learning algorithms.
• Diﬀerences in measurements
In the UD-HVAC approach, calculating the demand-driven set point based on
temperatures measured by Hobo-loggers in the case of this assessment, did not
account for the diﬀerence between the set point applied in the control system and
the actual air temperature in the oﬃce. IoT sensors or a correlation between set
points and air temperature can be used, as mentioned in Sec.11.4.
• Costs of miss-classiﬁcation
Miss-classiﬁcation costs (i.e., not classifying correctly a thermal preference cate-
gory) were not taken into account in the evaluation of the classiﬁcation perfor-
mance. Classifying a ”Warmer” category as ”No change” does not have the same
implications as categorizing it as ”Colder”. This should be taken into account
when implementing PCM in real applications.

Conclusions
• When implementing the demand-driven set point in a real BMS system, the out-
come evidenced a low performance with only 28.6% of the occupants improving
their thermal comfort.

category and requires less information than the other techniques to have a better

Chapter 12. Conclusions
• The participatory sensing procedure deﬁned as part of the UD-HVAC approach,
gives a better representation of the outcome of a HVAC control strategy compared
to the PMV model.
• The outcome of the UD-HVAC approach can be improved in terms of thermal
comfort by creating a connection between the temperature used to characterize
the personal thermal preferences and the temperature read by the BMS system.
That can be achieved either by using IoT sensors in both cases or implementing a
correlation between both measurements into the methodology of the approach.
12.1
Future work
• Implement a parameter associated to auxiliary HVAC energy consumption (i.g.,
ventilation air ﬂow or water ﬂow through radiators) into the optimization process
of the UD-HVAC framework, analyzing the outcome in terms of thermal comfort
and energy consumption.
• Make an evaluation of the PCM and PMV algorithms analyzed in this project in
a real BMS system, considering a period of time long enough to account for the
seasonal variations of thermal preferences.
• Evaluate the UD-HVAC framework in a ﬁeld assessment, where IoT sensors provide
data to the BMS system and data to create the TD-proﬁles. The considerations
proposed in this thesis project to characterize personal thermal preferences, should
be taken into account.
• Analyze the period of time that the data used to create a thermal preference proﬁle
(or other PCM based on thermal preference) is representative for an occupant.
• Consider occupancy as an additional input variable for diﬀerent types of PCM.

E.2
Over-ﬁtting detection
In Fig.E.2.1 is shown how over-ﬁtting can be detected in a simulation. When the Mean
Squared Error (MSE) of the testing data increases more than for the training data, the
solution found is excessively speciﬁc for the data of one occupant. In other words, when
the same algorithm is applied to the data of other occupants, the number of correct
thermal preference predictions will be lower. Conﬁgurations of the ANN algorithm that
presented over-ﬁtting, were discarded.
