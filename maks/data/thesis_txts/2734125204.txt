Abstract
This master thesis investigates the electrical and chemical properties of calcareous layer formed
on offshore wind turbines subjected to Impressed Current Cathodic Protection (ICCP). Through
research, the aim is to simulate polarization curves of the calcareous layer, enhancing the design
standards set by DS/EN ISO 24656 and DNV GL RP B401. Building on prior research at DTU, this
study examines the impact of environmental factors and ICCP settings, particularly focusing on the
rate at which the potential is decreased over time.
The methodology involves applying either a constant current density or a predetermined electrode
potential to steel specimens submerged in artificial seawater. The results include monitoring of
potential and current density, SEM/EDS­analysis images, and calculations of IR­drop and depolar­
ization curves. The IR drop and depolarization analysis helps determine whether the calcareous
layer has developed resistance properties over time.
Key findings reveal significant differences in potential stability and chemical composition between
calcareous layers formed in water with flow and water without flow. The study highlights variations
in these properties under different environmental conditions, particularly in relation to the rate at
which the potential is decreased over time. However, several uncertainties are associated with the
test setup, and improved methods for monitoring the formation of calcareous layers are suggested.
These findings underscore the necessity for further research to refine ICCP models for offshore
wind turbines, taking into account the complex interactions between environmental factors and
ICCP effectiveness.
To obtain a better understanding of the formation of calcareous layers, future research is depen­
dent on stable, predictable, and realistic parameters. To sufficiently control these parameters, it is
suggested to conduct in­situ investigations.

1
Introduction
The offshore wind farms in the North Sea are expected to expand the capacity from renewable
energy from 30GW to 300GW within 2050 in compliance with the Ostend Declaration [2]. To com­
ply with the agreement, offshore wind turbines (OWT) are now being installed at deeper waters,
surpassing depths of 40 meters [3]. In line with the increased demand of OWT, further research
is important to ensure optimal design of the structures. A special focus lies on minimizing environ­
mental impact, hereby expanding the structures lifetime and adapting installation and operation to
more extreme conditions, e.g. deeper waters.
This master thesis focuses on an OWT with a monopole (MP) foundation as seen in Figure 1.1, as
it is used for more than 80 % of all OWT [4]. The MP consists of a steel pipe driven into the seabed,
with a diameter typically between 6 and 9 meters. At the top of the MP a transition piece (TP)
is placed, whereby cathodic protection (CP) in the form of Impressed Current Cathodic Protection
(ICCP) is installed to prevent corrosion of the OWT. At depths up to approximately 40m the structure
is sufficiently protected through anodes installed at the TP, however, at deeper grounds additional
anode cages are implemented with a price tag of approximately 500 000 DKK per unit [3].

However, to reach maximum depths for ICCP protection, the MP is typically designed for the most

2
Problem Statement
2.1
Background and motivation
In line with the expansion within the offshore wind industry, DS/EN ISO 24656 and DNV GL RP
B401 has implemented strict requirements in the design of Impressed Current Cathodic Protection
(ICCP) [5], [7]. The approach is considered conservative, as it takes the effect of time variation
of sea currents, hydrodynamic effects, and the formation of calcareous layers on the steel surface
and implements a safety factor of 1.5 in the design. The possible effect of electrical resistance of
the calcareous layer is thereby not accounted for.
Through research and field tests, results have however shown that a calcareous layer on the steel
surface might contribute with a potential drop on the steel surface due to it’s resistant properties
[9], [10]. The field tests indicates that the current density can be increased without exceeding a
potential of ­1100mV at the steel surface at the location of the TP, consequently increasing the
range of ICCP. This furthermore makes it possible to install OWT at greater depths without the use
of additional anode cages [11].
2.2
Project content
This project is a continuation of previous projects [10], [12], [13], [14], [15], which were performed
using a flume system on DTU, almost identical with the one being used in this thesis. This mas­
ter thesis aims to investigate the electrical and chemical properties of the calcareous layer being
formed on a steel specimen subjected to ICCP, both with and without seawater flow. Furthermore,
it is investigated whether the scan rate for lowering the potential influences the formation of the cal­
careous layer, and whether this changes the morphology or the electrical and chemical properties
of the calcareous layer.
The content of this project is to:
• Investigate the electrical and chemical properties of calcareous layers in seawater both with
and without flow.
• Investigate the effect of lowering the potential by use of different scan rates (rate at which
potential is decreased over time).
• Contribute to establish a better model for the design of ICCP within the electrode range be­
tween ­1000mV and ­1100mV (Ag/AgCl/seawater). By including the electrical resistance of
the calcareous layers in design simulations, the range of ICCP can be increased.
• Determine the change in resistance of a calcareous layer by measuring IR­drop for a steel
specimen subjected to current density ­400mA/m2 for 22 days with and without seawater flow.
• Analyze the calcareous layer to determine chemical composition, morphology and micro
structure to predict the electrical resistance of the layer.

Theory
This section consist of general theory related to corrosion processes, followed by more in depth
theory essential to understand ICCP of steel in seawater environment.
3.1
Corrosion mechanisms
Corrosion is in acknowledged literature broadly defined as ”an attack on a metallic material by
reaction with its environment” [6]. Corrosion is furthermore divided into three groups, namely wet
corrosion, dry corrosion and corrosion in other fluids. As this thesis considers an OWT located in
seawater, the theory is limited to wet corrosion.
Wet corrosion appears in aqueous liquids with the presence of oxygen, where the seawater acts as
the liquid electrolyte in an electrochemical process as, shown in Figure 3.1 [6]. The figure illustrates
the coherence between the anodic and cathodic reaction in seawater, and how the reactions are
generally balanced within the circuit as summarized in Equation 3.1 to Equation 3.3. Through the
anodic reaction, a metal M undergoes oxidation, whereby M2+ ions are released into the electrolyte.
Through the general cathodic reaction O2 is reduced by obtaining free electrons conducted through
the metal from the anode reaction. Lastly, the electrical circuit, M(OH)2 is formed in the electrolyte
by free metal ions M2+ and OH−ions released through the cathodic reaction.

Metal subjected to aqueous environments, such as seawater, are prone to corrosion through elec­

point where the corrosion rate is reduced, i.e. the immune area in the Pourbaix diagram as further
described in section 3.3.

3.4
Impressed Current Cathodic Protection
Impressed Current Cathodic Protection (ICCP) systems are a vital method for protecting large struc­
tures from corrosion [5]. These systems utilize an external power supply, with the ability to adjust
the current density applied in relation to the driving voltage as illustrated in Figure 3.3 [5]. The ICCP
method is especially suitable for structures where high driving voltage is required. ICCP minimizes
the number of anodes needed, making it a practical choice for extensive installations. However,
the reliance on a stable power supply can lead to higher costs for installation, maintenance, and
electricity. Despite these costs, ICCP remains a preferred solution for safeguarding large­scale
metallic infrastructures.

possible oxidation reactions on the anode [16].
The requirement for CP, provided by ISO 24656, indicates a minimum and maximum potential for
carbon manganese steels in aerated seawater [5]. The accepted potential where the structure is
considered fully protected ranges from ­800mV and ­1100mV with reference to Ag/AgCl/seawater.

The cathodic reaction occurring when applying ICCP in seawater differ, depending on environmen­

3.5
Current density
In the context of ICCP systems, the current density [mA/m2] is applied at the bottom part of the TP,
at the location of anodes in Figure 3.5. The current density applied must ensure a potential within
the protective range on the entire MP, conducted by upholding the necessary driving potential [5].
The current density is continuously monitored and altered depending on the obtained potential on
the steel surface, influenced by environmental factors described in section 3.8 and the formation of
calcareous layer on the steel surface.
The current density is typically set to a fixed value for ICCP, generally between ­185mA/m2 and
­625mA/m2 for seawater up to 20◦C [5]. ISO 24656:2022 however states these values are not
applicable the first couple of days after installing CP, as the current density needed to polarize steel
from it’s natural potential down to the protected range (­900mV to ­1100mV) is significantly higher.
The standard states, ”Initially, the current delivered will be at least 50% greater than calculated due
to the higher driving voltage” [5].

3.6
Calcareous deposits
Calcareous deposits, which can form on the steel surface due to ICCP, acts as a protective layer
with the ability to protect the steel against corrosion [5]. The formation process is greatly influenced
by the electrical potential, with primary formation occurring between ­900mV to ­1100mV [6], [22].
The formation of calcareous deposits can be expected due to the following chemical reactions,
where the steel surface functions as the cathode.
The reduction of oxygen generates hydroxide ions near the polarized surface which increases the
pH and results in a higher concentration of carbonate ions [5], [23]. The formation and chemical
composition of calcareous layers varies in line with an increasing pH value. [6], [24]. Previous
studies indicate that very high pH values favours formation of magnesium hydroxide (Mg(OH)2),
while less high pH values favours formation of calcium carbonate (CaCO3) [25], [26].
The chemical reaction of formation of calcareous deposit is shown in Equation 3.8, where the pre­
cipitation of CaCO3 occurs.
Ca2+ + CO2−
3 + −→CaCO3(s)
(3.8)
Additionally, Mg(OH)2 precipitate, which similar to CaCO3 forms depending on the potential on the
steel surface. The chemical reaction is given as:
Mg2+ + 2OH−−→Mg(OH)2(s)
(3.9)
The presence of the calcareous layer diminishes the oxygen reduction in Equation 3.1 by acting
as a physical barrier, preventing cathodic reactions to occur on the steel surface [6]. Previous
research indicate that these calcareous layers decrease the current density required to protect
the structure. The current density required to obtain sufficient ICCP is heavily dependent on the
properties of the calcareous layer, where the main components are Mg(OH)2 and CaCO3 [8], [22],
[27]. The composition of the layer has been reported to depend on environmental factors such as
pH, temperature, current density and water flow rate [16], [28].
As mentioned in section 3.3, H2 is formed if the steel specimen is in zone f [29]. The H2 gas has
been reported to penetrate through the calcareous layer which consequently cracks and scales off.
3.7
Polarization for ICCP
Potential is measured in cathodic or anodic over­voltage which is defined as the difference between
real and equilibrium potential [6]. When corrosion occurs the real potential on the surface lies
between the equilibrium potential of the anodic and cathodic reaction, as shown in Figure 3.6. In
the case of ICCP, the electrode potential is forced into the immune region, making the steel the
cathode in the electrochemical cell.

Figure 3.6: The effect of overvoltage in anodic or cathodic direction [6].
Activation polarization is simply the activation energy required for a chemical reaction to occur on
a surface. The rate is set by the mass or charge transfer across the interface, as for instance the
formation of H2 at the cathode [18].
Concentration polarization is determined by the mass transport within the solution [6]. Two possible
scenarios can arise: either there is an insufficient supply of reactants at the electrode surface, or
there is an accumulation of reactants [6].
Additionally there is resistance polarization where oxide films or calcareous layers on specific steel
samples exhibit an ohmic resistance that influences the potential on surfaces [6], [30]. The resis­
tance will cause an ohmic drop, which is measured as the variation in potential just before and right
after the current interruption. All materials have an inherent resistance, which increases in line with
a longer conductive path [30]. Consequently, an increased resistance results in a decrease in po­
tential for a constant applied current, resulting in the decrease in potential as shown in Figure 3.5.
The ohmic drop, also known as IR­drop, can be expressed by

where R [Ω] is the resistance and I [A] is the electric current. The magnitude of the drop can describe
the properties of the calcareous layer formed on the steel specimen.
The initial stage of depolarization is referred to as the IR­drop [31]. This IR­drop is a measure of
the resistance of the calcareous layer and is computed using potential difference between the last
measurement during current exposure and the first two measurements after current cessation [31].
Depolarization is as such used to characterize the calcareous layers effect on ICCP, as the depo­
larization will depend on the thickness and porosity of the calcareous layer. A dense layer could
prevent O2 from diffusing through the layer, reducing the availability for cathodic reduction accord­
ing to Equation 3.2 and even H+ ions availability could be reduced causing less formation of H2 (g)
according to Equation 3.6 [6], [22].

3.8.1
Oxygen
Corrosion is a redox reaction whereby the corrosion rate is determined by the available amount
of dissolved oxygen in the solution [16]. The cathode reaction utilizes the oxygen, resulting in the
formation of hydroxide. Availability of dissolved oxygen depends on the geographic location, water
depth and temperature.
Figure 3.7: Oxygen level in North Sea [32].
As seen in Figure 3.7, the oxygen levels reach their peak during the winter months and their lowest
point during the summer months. The oxygen ranges between 6.4mg/L­ 10.7mg/L [32].
3.8.2
Temperature
The corrosion rate is highly influenced by the temperature in seawater, as chemical reactions occur
more rapidly at higher temperatures which decreases the demand for electric current to obtain CP
[6]. At elevated temperatures the amount of dissolved oxygen is decreased, however the diffu­
sion constant for oxygen is increased, which may result in a slight increase of the current density
required for cathodic protection. At lower temperatures, the solubility of CaCO3 increases, which
reduces the protection from the calcareous layer and increases the current demand [8], [16], [25].

3.8.3
Salinity
Salinity, as defined by ISO 24656, refers to the ”quantity of inorganic salts dissolved in seawater”
and is measured in units of g/kg or ppt [5]. Within the realm of CP, an elevation in salinity can
diminish the amount of dissolved oxygen present. Consequently, this results in both a reduction in
anode resistance and an increase in the resistivity of the seawater, potentially intensifying the cor­
rosion rate [16]. Moreover, increased salinity generally leads to higher conductivity in the seawater,
further influencing electrochemical processes relevant to CP. It’s essential to note that salinity can
vary with sea depth, leading to fluctuations in resistivity and conductivity. Such variations are critical
considerations in the design of an OWT.
The average salinity in the north sea in 2020 was measured to be 3.42% [16].
3.8.4
pH
The pH value significantly influences the corrosion rate by affecting the equilibrium potential of re­
actions within an aqueous solution, as outlined in [6]. It is noteworthy that the pH value is subject
to dynamic alterations due to factors such as current density, the presence of surface films (includ­
ing coatings and calcareous layers), and the applied current density. In this regard, it has been
observed that increasing the applied current density can lead to a subsequent increase in pH [7],
[34].
It is standard practice in the design of CP systems to assume a pH level within the range of 7.4 to 8.4.
Nevertheless, it is essential to acknowledge potential local variations, as depicted in Figure 3.9b
[16].

3.8.5
Flow velocity
Tides, wind and oceanic currents are factors causing periodic fluctuations in the sea level, result­
ing in varying water flow [5]. Increased flow velocity raises oxygen levels on the surface metal,
accelerating the corrosion rate. To ensure a stable ICCP, the current density is increased, delay­
ing polarization of the steel. Furthermore, an increased flow velocity may impact oxide layers or
calcareous deposits formed on the steel surface [6].

State of the art
In the following sections research relevant for this master thesis is presented and discussed, high­
lighting the key findings to be used in further research concerning the electrical and chemical prop­
erties of calcareous layers formed on a steel surface subjected to ICCP. The aim is to elaborate
on the mechanisms and factors influencing the formation of calcareous layers, to investigate the
possibility of optimizing ICCP on an OWT within the requirements given by DS/EN ISO 24656:2022.
4.1
Structure, composition and morphology
The paper, [37], investigates the basic structure, composition and morphology of calcareous layer
formed on cylindrical steel specimens polarized in seawater at ­900mVSCE = ­910mVAg/Agcl/seawater.
The results showed a small increase in the applied current density shorty after start, followed by a
significant decrease where the potential eventually stabilized.
The key outcomes and conclusions from the paper are listed in the following:
• ”Within the first 60 min Mg(OH)2 is formed with a thickness 10−8m which significantly reduces
oxygen flux to the cathode. The steady­state cathodic current density associated with the film
increased in proportion to flow rate”.
• Subsequently CaCO3 forms an increasing layer at a slower pace.
• ”Morphology of CaCO3 was more coarse the higher the current density, actually the flow rate,
due to a steeper (OH−) gradient and a reduced driving force for nucleation”.
• ”First, the pH increase near the metal surface occured in association with oxygen reduction;
second, CaCO3 formed on the Mg ­ rich film and not the steel”.
• ”The fact that current density was relatively constant prior to 2,000 min suggests that the sepa­
rate unit CaCO3 particles had little or no effect on net oxygen availability and that impingement
was required for current density reduction”.

4.4
Connecting the dots
The presented research indicates the complexity regarding the problem statement. The electrical
and chemical properties of calcareous layers are dependent on several varying factors, e.g. tem­
perature, pH, salinity, water flow and applied current density. Across all papers there is an common
understanding that the effects of a calcareous layer on the steel surface improves the ICCP on an
OWT, however further research is needed to exact a methodology that is applicable during the
design process.
This thesis intends to build upon subjects previously explored in the mentioned projects. To in­
clude a new aspect in the research the effect of varying scan rates and it’s effect on the properties
of the calcareous layer is implemented, thereby adding a new layer of understanding to existing
knowledge.

Divergences from Reality: Limitations and
Assumptions of the Experiment
Creating an experimental setup within the given time­frame and the available resources provided
for this master thesis entails certain unavoidable limitations and assumptions when comparing to
expected real life scenarios. This section briefly presents the most significant factors that differ
from the reality, possibly affecting the validity of the outcomes. These factors should be addressed
in subsequent research on the subject to ensure findings more compatible with the complexities of
real­world scenarios.
6.1
Artificial­ vs natural seawater
Artificial seawater contains inorganic salts with a concentration above 0.005mg/L [40], meaning
organic matter, suspended matter and marine life is not included. The artificial seawater has a
temperature of approximately 16◦C throughout the experiments, compared to an average temper­
ature in the north sea of 10.52◦C [33].
ASTM specifically notes that ”where corrosion is involved, the results obtained from laboratory tests
may not approximate those secured under natural testing conditions... especially where effects of
velocity, salt atmospheres or organic constituents are involved” [40]. The environment in the glass
container have several differences compared to a realistic case, as no flow is added whereby local
development of pH and formation of chlorine gas (Cl2) might occur. The small confined glass
container might lead to an increase of corrosive effects, making the experiment less comparable
with a real setup [5], [17]. The flume system is more comparable with a real case as flow is added,
however the effect of a closed system is not sufficiently documented.
6.2
pH Variability and stabilization challenges in experimental
systems
The pH varies throughout the experiments due to the use of relatively small volumes of artificial
seawater. Ideally, it should have been well­buffered to minimize such variations. In real­world
scenarios, the pH is generally more stable due to greater access to seawater due to water flow.
The pH was initially measured shortly after preparation, with readings exceeding 8. However, a
significant decrease in pH was observed when retesting the same water before commencing the
experiments. The cause of this decline is not definitively known, but it is presumed that the salts may
not have fully dissolved during the initial pH measurement of the artificial seawater. Furthermore,
the ASTM’s recipe suggests an increase in pH to more closely match realistic values, implying
an expectation of initially lower pH than reality (7.4­8.4) should be assumed [34], [40]. ASTM
furthermore notes ”Prepare the solution and adjust the pH immediately prior to use”, which was not
performed in this master thesis.
6.3
Time perspective in formation of calcareous layer
An OWT is usually designed with a lifetime of approximately 25 years [5]. A constant constant cur­
rent density is applied, leading to a decrease in potential on the steel surface. This decrease occurs
over a period of several years, substantially longer than the time periods applied for experiments
performed in conjunction with this master thesis [3]. To accelerate the formation of calcareous lay­
ers a higher current density is applied compared to what is generally used [5], for which the exact
effect is unknown.
Cathodic Protection of Offshore Wind Structures

==PAGE:49==
6.4
Coating of steel structure
The surface of the test specimens are not coated in the experiments, as they would be on a MP
[5], [7]. However, due to high risk of blistering and damage during installation, OWT are designed
as uncoated structures. A reduction factor n is implemented if the structure is coated to account for
these defects and the progressing degradation of the protective properties of the coating.
6.5
Effect of hydrodynamics
The effect of hydrodynamics is not included in this experimental setup due to lack of sufficient
experimental setup. The previous project performed on the setup, [15], indicated errors related to
the flow applied in the flume system.

8
Discussion
This section is presented in accordance with the results presented in chapter 7. Potentials are with
reference to Ag/AgCl/seawater electrode.
8.1
Preliminary experiment ­ Glass container without water flow
The preliminary experiment consists of 10 test runs, whereby results from test 1, 2, 7, 9 and 10 are
disregarded. The results from test 3, 4, 5, 6 and 8 have some uncertainties related to the correct use
of equipment, varying pH in artificial seawater, incorrect sequence control and wrong experimental
setup as presented in Table 7.2. These uncertainties impacts the results with an unknown effect,
making interpretation of the results challenging.
8.1.1
pH
The pH measured during the preliminary experiments vary between tests, whereby some are below
the average pH in seawater (7.4­8.4) and some are above [16]. As presented in the Pourbaix
diagram, Figure 3.2, an elevated pH increases the risk of corrosion at lower potentials [16], [18].
The corrosion results in an increase of OH−, which is utilized to form Mg(OH)2 and CaCO3, as
explained in section 3.6. The elevated pH thereby benefits the formation of a calcareous layer.
8.1.2
Current densities by different scan rates
ISO 24656:2022 states a design current density generally between ­185mA/m2 and ­625mA/m2
applied in areas near the anodes [5]. To accelerate the polarization, the current density is higher
for a short period after installing the ICCP. Subjected to a constant current density, the potential
decreases over a longer period (possibly several years).
The preliminary experiment is performed with the goal to investigate the impact of lowering the
potential in the range of ­1000mV to ­1100mV, by use of different scan rates. The results indicate
that if the potential is lowered too rapidly, the current density will exceed realistic proportions. Test 3
showed that instantly lowering the potential from ­700mV to ­1100mV required a maximum current
density of ­14 767mA/m2, similar to what obtained by Jesper Lund [14]. Test 4 required a maximum
current density of ­6675mA/m2 when the potential was lowered to ­1100mV over a period of 48h.
Test 5 and 6 had a maximum applied current density of ­7852mA/m2 and ­7250.2mA/m2 when the
potential was lowered to ­1100mV during the initial 60h. ISO 24656: 2022 states that higher current
density is common at the initial phase of ICCP, however levels of current densities as high as those
obtained in test 3­6 are not considered common practise [14], [26], [37], [44].
Results from test 3­7 indicate that if the goal is to limit the applied current density, it is crucial to
avoid reducing the potential either too rapidly or too gradually. However, findings from these exper­
iments indicate that the current density needed to achieve the desired potential reduction within the
desired timeframe is significantly higher than what is typically applied in practical OWT operations
[5]. It’s worth noting that this simulation is a simplified experiment that does not account for several
real­world factors as mentioned in chapter 6. Nonetheless, the disparity in current density is so
substantial that it renders the results unrealistic for practical application.

OCP measurements
Test 8 is aimed to investigate the electrical properties of the calcareous layer by monetoring the IR
­ drop every 24h. To accelerate the formation of the calcareous layer the current density supplied is
set to ­1000mA/m2, with OCP measurements every 24h. The results show the potential decreases

the sequence setup is used further in the main experiments as it provides interesting information

8.2
Main experiment ­ Glass container without water flow
The main experiment in the glass container is an extension of the preliminary experiment, however
with less uncertainties as described in section 5.3. In the following sections test 11 is presented,
and compared with test 5 and 6 as they are conducted using the same scan rate. Following, a
comparison of test 12, 13 and 14 is performed focusing on IR ­drop and SEM/EDS­analysis post
exposure.
8.2.1
Test 11
Test 11 is performed to explore if lowering the potential to ­1100mV before applying a constant
current of ­500mA/m2 would initiate the formation of a calcareous layer early on, resulting in a
more negative potential compared to a test where constant current density is applied from the
start. The results show that the potential stabilizes for applied constant current of ­500mA/m2,
whereby the current density is increased to ­1000mA/m2 and subsequently ­1500mA/m2. As the
potential stabilized for each current density applied, the effect of lowering the potential to ­1100mV
through potentiostatic sequence and keeping it constant at ­1100mV for 6 hours was investigated
(after 350h). This is chosen based on results from the preliminary experiment test 6, where the
calcareous layer was kept intact for several hours before scaling off for a potential kept constant
at ­1100mV. The constant current density is set to ­1500mA/m2 both prior and post the reduction,
which as seen on the graph resulted in minimal changes to the potential. It is however noted that
within this period there are significant signs of the calcareous layer scaling off, which might have
impacted the potential. Nonetheless, the results show that the potential stabilizes, most likely due to
the fact that there is no water flow, supporting Rikke Kofoeds conclusion that water flow is beneficial
when aiming to form a calcareous layer to decrease the potential [10].
8.2.2
Comparison test 5, 6 and 11
Test 5, 6 and 11 are all performed by use of the same scan rate for the first 60h as seen in Figure 8.1.
When comparing the tests it is however seen that the current density needed for this scan rate
differs between the three test. To further investigate the reason for the variations, water samples
are compared showing a difference in pH. As seen in Figure 8.1, test 5 and 6 both have a pH of
8.06 and progress similarly as seen on the graph. Test 11 on the other hand has a pH measured
to be 8.58, and is subjected to a higher current density.
Looking closer at the results just after test start, Figure 8.1 shows a starting potential after OCP at
­690mV for test 5, ­680mV for test 6 and ­629mV for test 11. The starting potential of the cyclic
sweep is set set to ­700mV, whereby the current applied to test 11 is significantly higher, as the
difference from the natural potential is greater. To remove uncertainties arising from varying initial
potentials, the main experiments are conducted with an initial 24h OCP. This extended period allows
the potential to stabilize, ensuring a consistent starting point for all experiments.

8.2.3.4
SEM/EDS ­ analysis
The SEM/EDS­analysis of test 11 to 14 show some variations in chemical composition. By com­
paring the images with the graphs showing the potential and current for each test, a hypothesis is
formed regarding the development and composition of the calcareous layer. It is noted that only one
image from each test is discussed in depth, however the SEM/EDS­analysis showed some varia­
tions between the different test samples originating from the same steel specimen due to uneven
calcareous layer. These pictures are gathered in Appendix B.2.1.
The main findings for the hypothesis are:
• The density of (Mg(OH)2) varies in relation to the current density applied.
• A higher current density might form a denser (Mg(OH)2) layer.
• H2 formation at the steel surface creates cracks in the calcareous layer, this appears at lower
potentials and results in calcareous layer scaling off.
• H2 escapes easier through a thin porous (Mg(OH)2) layer than for a denser layer of (Mg(OH)2).

Hypothesis/ further discussion points
Through test 11­14 it is seen that calcium carbonate forms at the steel surface when the current
is high. These results are similar to what Rasmus Schjelde obtained when applying a constant
potential of ­1100mV, whereby the applied current density was also high [15]. Furthermore, the
tests seemed to stabilize throughout the experiment, whereby supporting the fact that water flow
has a beneficial impact on the calcareous layer [10].

Main Experiment ­ Flume system with water flow
8.3.1
Experimental setup and errors
The quality of the outcomes from the flume experiment was inconsistent due to adverse conditions.
While the experimental setup was created to simulate real­world conditions adequately, a combina­
tion of inexperience with the equipment and unexpected errors resulted in certain conditions, e.g.
flow rate and pH, did not meet the intended parameters.
Results presented in subsubsection 7.3.1.1 indicated that connecting three steel plates to one
power supply caused irregularities in the current density applied. The results show that if one
steel specimen is poorly connected, the error affects all steel specimens in the flume. To avoid this
irregularity and obtain more reliable results the steel specimens should have been connected to
one power supplier each. As the steel specimens affected each other, analyzing each individual
test is difficult as the current density applied deviated from the expected ­400mA/m2.
The initial thought was to have constant flow in flume 2 and no water flow in flume 1. The exper­
imental system is however quite complex, where technical issues with the 3­way valve resulted
in similar conditions in both flumes. Both flumes were exposed to flow, starting on day 8 of the
experiment and lasting until day 20 as air in the tubes prevented flow the initial 8 days.

Suggestions for further investigation
9.1
Limitations in experiment duration
The duration of the experiments are limited due to the time­frame of 5 months for this project.
Several experiments in the flumes should be conducted and the duration of these experiments
should be increased in order to be able to reach stable conditions for the calcareous layer. When
aiming to compare several tests, tests should be performed with similar length.
9.2
Effect of varying scan rates
The results from tests without water flow suggested that lowering the potential to ­1100mV prior to
exposure with constant current would have a positive effect on the development of the potential and
the calcareous layer. For future tests this lowering of potential could be interesting to investigate
for the flume setup as well.
9.3
Complexities and uncertainties of the flume system setup
The experiment conducted in the flume system experienced unforeseen circumstances and errors
which could have been avoided with better knowledge of the system. In future research these
uncertainties should be avoided, where it is recommended to use either one power supplier or one
potentiostat channel for each steel specimen. Additionally multiple checks of connections should
be conducted prior to start, no tape should be added on the steel and the same artificial seawater
should be used if comparisons are performed on several tests. The system should be run someone
with knowledge on the setup, as errors easily occur when controlling the three way valve and water
pump. The pH in the artificial seawater should also be buffered prior to use, as the metal surface
per sea water volume is large compared to real world scenario.
9.4
Challenges in replicating real World conditions for calcareous
layer formation
The development of calcareous layers is significantly affected by environmental conditions and
shows variation even with minor differences between test settings. To sufficiently investigate the
formation of calcareous layers tests should be performed on real OWT, as it is extremely difficult to
obtain a stable realistic environment in the DTU lab. The effect of seawater flow and current have
a substantial impact on the formation of the calcareous layer, unobtainable in the flume setup [10].
9.5
Consistency in SEM/EDS­analysis imaging
Imaging of chemical composition by SEM/EDS should be provided using consistent colours for
each element. It is more difficult to compare the amount of e.g. Ca if one test displays it as purple
and another test displays it as yellow.

9.6
pH measurements
Future experiments should include more realistic environments where pH adjustments are less ex­
tensive. Introducing a buffer to stabilize pH levels in a well­controlled environment is recommended
to avoid the uncertainty of varying pH. This improvements is key to achieving more accurate and
representative results, particularly regarding the interaction of pH with steel in marine conditions.
In line with ASTM, the pH should be adjust immediately prior to use.

Conclusion
In this master thesis, the electrical and chemical properties of calcareous layers are investigated
in water with and without flow. The final aim is to establish an enhanced model for the design of
ICCP systems within the electrode potential range of ­1000mV to ­1100mV (Ag/AgCl/seawater).
During this project, the effects of varying scan rates were explored by evaluating the IR drop and
analyzing the chemical composition of the formed calcareous deposits by SEM/EDS ­ analysis.
It is important to note that several errors occurred throughout the experiment, making it difficult
to exactly correlate distinct parameters as effects of potential and scan rate to the formation of
calcareous layers. Nonetheless, the following conclusions can be drawn from the study:
• The potential remained stable in experiments conducted without water flow, while it decreased
in the flume system where water flow was applied. However, it remains uncertain whether the
potential could reach the desired ­1100mV, as the experiment was halted at ­1030mV due to
time constraints. These results suggest that experiments conducted in water without flow may
not fully align with real­world conditions, where constant tidal flow continuously influences the
formation of calcareous layers.
• Investigation of varying scan rates showed that the scan rate applied in test 13 resulted in
lower potential than the scan rate applied in test 12 and 14. It is important to note that the pH
levels during these experiments were measured between 5.12 and 5.8, which is lower than
the typical seawater pH range of 7.4 to 8.4.
• The unexpected development of the IR­drop in the flume experiment precludes drawing defini­
tive conclusions from those results. However, observations from tests 12 and 13 suggest that
reducing the potential to ­1100mV before applying a constant current leads to an increase in
the resistance of the calcareous layer. Further research is needed to implement this in a
model for the design of ICCP.
• The SEM/EDS­analysis revealed significant variations in the chemical composition of the cal­
careous layers. In tests conducted without water flow, Mg enriched deposits predominated,
often forming two distinct layers. In contrast, the tests performed in the flume setup obtained
a layer dominated by Ca, encapsulated by a thin layer enriched with Mg towards the steel
surface and a thick layer towards the artificial seawater.
After several years of research on the formation of calcareous layers on steel specimens subjected
to ICCP, it can be concluded that the complexity of these experiments significantly affects the results
obtained in previous M.Sc. theses at DTU. There is a common understanding across all studies
that the presence of a calcareous layer on the steel surface enhances the effectiveness of ICCP for
OWT in the sense that lower currents are needed for CP. However, factors such as temperature,
pH, salinity, water flow, oxygen concentration and exact control of the experimental setup play a
crucial role in achieving reliable results.
The findings of this master thesis highlights the importance of controlling the parameters associated
with the experimental setup. In light of these findings it is proposed to consider the possibility of
conducting in situ investigations of OTW, minimizing the gap between theoretical models provided
by DS/EN ISO 24656 and real­world variability.

Glass container with still water
This procedure is used for preliminary experiment and main experiment without flow, both in glass
container.
1. Wash glass container and fill with artificial seawater 15 cm from bottom, take water sample.
2. Sand blast steel plate, handle with plastic gloves to avoid contamination.
3. Cover one side of steel specimen with tape, connect to orange wire using titanium bolt and
place under water. (changed to without tape, both sides exposed)
4. Connect anode net to blue wire using titanium bolt, place under water.
5. Connect electrode to red wire, place tip under water.
6. Check potentiostat using dummy box, resistance and current.
7. Check potential before test start by use of voltmeter.
8. Connect all wires to potentiostat.
9. Select input values on software, chose file name and run all sequences.
10. Sequence logs every 60sec for galvaniostatic and potentiostatic, every second for OCP.
11. Put camera in front of glass container, manage setting to ensure a clear image and save
image every 10 min.
12. After last sequence is ended, a water sample is collected from the container.
13. Remove steel specimen, rinse with distilled water and dry with cold air. Store in air tight box.

A.2.2
Flume system
1. Drain the flumes, vacuum both flumes and water pipes running through the cooling system.
2. Wash flumes and fill with artificial seawater, approximately 100L.
3. Sand blast steel plates, handle with plastic gloves to avoid contamination.
4. Connect steel specimens to orange wires using titanium bolt and place in flumes, 6 speci­
mens.
5. Connect anode net on each side of steel specimens, blue wire using titanium bolt.
6. Place 1 reference electrode in each flume.
7. Collect water sample, check oxygen and pH.
8. Attach lid on flume system and tighten bolts, turn valva to correct setting.
9. Turn on cooling system, flow system and pump to circulate water in cooling system.
10. Connect steel specimens, anode nets and electrodes to circuit panel.
11. Connect power supplies to circuit panel, turn on current density.
12. Turn on data logger.
13. Put cameras in front of each ”window” on the flume, manage settings to ensure a clear image.
14.
15. After 8 days, remove 1st steel specimen from the flume. Collect water sample, check pH and
oxygen.

A.2.3
Post processing of steel specimens
1. Cut corner piece and center piece from each steel specimen, approximately (1x1)cm.
2. Steel specimens are embedded in epoxy, 9 minutes pressure (20kN) and temperature rise
(180◦C).
3. Steel specimens are ground from grain 220 to 400, followed by polishing of the cross section
by use of 3 and 1 µm crystals.
4. SEM analysis and ESD analysis by use of Zeiss Sigma 300 and EDS ultimax 65 from Oxford
Instruments, software Astec.
