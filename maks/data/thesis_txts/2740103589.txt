Abstract
In the present study, scanning electron microscopy (SEM) is used to identify the relevant
fractographic features on the fracture surface of specimens having experienced static and
fatigue crack growth. The fracture features associated with the different opening modes, the
typical fracture behaviours in static and fatigue crack growth, and the fracture behaviours
close to the crack tip and remote from the crack tip are the key considerations in this study.
The results and experimental methods of the study are valuable in providing insights into
the dominant fracture mode, delamination damage initiation and propagation site in glass
fiber-reinforced epoxy composites. The approach used in the thesis can serve as guidelines
as to how to approach and undertake a failure analysis.
The typical fracture features generated under static loading are scarps, riverlines and
cusps.
Striations and matrix rollers are the common features of the fracture surface of
fatigue crack growth. All the fatigue fracture features generated under static and cyclic are
compared to understand the influence of the fracture mode on fracture features.

1
Introduction
1.1
Delamination failure in fibre-reinforced composites
Fiber-reinforced epoxy laminated composites are widely used in the aerospace and au-
tomotive industries due to their potential benefits such as high stiffness, high strength-to-
weight ratio, and high fatigue resistance properties. Due to their orthotropic properties,
composites exhibit greater strength in the fibre direction but have reduced strength when
subjected to loads in other directions [9]. Traditional fibre-reinforced composites do not
provide enough reinforcement in the thickness direction, making them susceptible to delami-
nation failure. Delamination failure occurs when the layers or plies of the composite material
separate or peel away from each other and can be triggered by a number of factors such as
impact damage, notches, manufacturing defects, and stress concentrations associated with
structural discontinuities [10, 11]. Delamination in composite structures originates from one
single loading mode or a combination loading modes [5]. Delamination failure was recorded
to initiate from a ply-splitting failure site during impact testing [10]. Under compressive
loading, the presence of delamination in fibre-reinforced epoxy composites can lead to a lack
of support on the load bearing layers, leading to the initiation of microbuckling failure [12].
1.2
Background information into fractography of composites
Fractography is the examination of the fracture surfaces of damaged materials. It is a
powerful and reliable technique in the investigation of fracture surfaces in fibre-reinforced
composites, which reveals internal damages such as fiber-matrix debonding, fiber breakage,
or delamination that might not be visible externally. The results gained from fractographic
studies are of significant importance. Fractographic analysis can provide a means to deduce
fracture morphologies of the fracture surfaces of interest and insight into the physical pro-
cesses by which composites are damaged and fail [5, 12]. The results of which can lead to
improved design and manufacturing processes. Additionally, fractography is useful for exam-
ining undamaged material to assess material quality [13]. In summary, fractography provides
a detailed understanding of fracture features and failure mechanisms in fiber-reinforced com-
posites, which is essential for early detection and prevention of material catastrophic failures.
The Scanning Electron Microscope (SEM) is one of the most common fractographic tech-
niques used to provide a detailed analysis of the fracture surfaces of composites structures,
offering insights into crack propagation direction and fracture morphologies [5, 8, 6]. When
examined under SEM, the fracture features loaded under static [14] and cyclic loads [15] can
be microscopically distinguished. Fractography is a reliable method to identify the fracture
mechanisms and modes, which assist in investigating the plausible root causes of a failure
case of wind turbine blades. Regarding post-mortem analysis, Miao and her partners con-
ducted a crack characterization of the static and fatigue crack behaviours of a wrinkled glass
fibre laminate, cut out from a failed blade after full-scale fatigue testing [16], with X-ray CT
and SEM being used in the study to obtain fracture details of the wrinkle defects. Details
such as fractographic morphologies, dominant fracture modes and fracture path in fatigue
and static failure surfaces can be obtained in the study.

1.3
Contribution of the thesis
The goal of the present study is to apply the most advanced fractography techniques,
particularly SEM, to examine the fracture surfaces of static and fatigue specimens taken
from previous mechanical testing phases. By examining these surfaces, information such as
specific fractographic features can be obtained. It is of crucial importance to distinguish
those features as they can assist in the identification of crack initiation and growth direc-
tion, failure mechanisms, and dominant fracture modes. The method of correlating those
internal fracture features and the local opening history of the points in the fracture surface
applied to the specimens is valuable in deducing the plausible root cause of failures. The
work described herein in the thesis contains valuable information from the preparation of
specimens, potential recommendations while using SEM and discussion of the results ob-
tained. The goal of the thesis is to contribute to the existing knowledge of fractographic
features in fibre-reinforce epoxy composites and assist composite engineers or wind turbine
technicians who are interested in the field of composite fractography. The potential outcome
of fractography is that one can examine a failed piece of material taken from a wind turbine
blade to explore the fracture modes and conclude the plausible root cause of failures.
1.4
Structure of the thesis
The report is structured as follows. Section 2 introduces a number of literature sur-
veys related to the topic of Linear Elastic Fracture Mechanics on inelastic materials and
composites fractography. Problem statement is introduced at the end of Section 2. In Sec-
tion 3, experimental method is introduced with specimen types and their coordinates setup,
samples preparation for a SEM analysis, equipment used in the study and experimental
techniques in SEM. In Section 4, the SEM examination results with of static and fatigue
crack growth images are compiled. Section 5 discusses the results obtained during SEM ob-
servation experiment and the differences of fracture features from each specimen, followed by
recommendations for future work. In Section 6, conclusions are drawn with major findings
in the study.

2.1
Linear Elastic Fracture Mechanics
The general approach of using Linear Elastic Fracture Mechanics (LEFM) is to assume
the solid body to be homogeneous, isotropic, and linear elastic material. In LEFM, there is a
universal stress field ahead of the crack tip where the magnitude of the singular stress fields
can be measured by the stress intensity factor K. The fracture process zone (FPZ) of inelastic
deformation is considered to be small in comparison with the size of the crack and any other
characteristic length. The FPZ is embedded in a K-dominant zone, where the stresses follow
a universal stress field, allowing external external loads and geometry to communiate with
this small FPZ through the K-dominant zone [2]. The inelastic fracture process of LEFM
is suitable for the concept of small-scale yielding in metals or small-scale fracture process
zone in composites [17, 2, 18]. Under small-scale yielding, the crack is expected to grow
when the critical value of stress intensity factor K approaches the material’s resistance to
fracture [17, 2]. The basis of applying LEFM on an elastic body is shown in Figure ??. R
represents the boundary of inelastic region, and D is the boundary of the K-dominant region.
We consider the case where R is small compared to D and any other geometrical dimension.

Large-scale bridging occurs when the fracture process zone at the crack tip is long enough

It is common to partition the crack opening displacements into three different loading

Fracture toughness is the resistance of a material to crack propagation. Fracture tough-
ness is typically controlled through failure processes with varying mode mixites such as Mode
I (opening), Mode II (in-plane shearing) and Mode III (our-of-plane shearing) [9]. To accu-
rately characterize the fracture toughness of a material, it is crucial to develop test models
that closely resemble loading conditions. The benefits of these tests are twofold. Firstly,
they enable the development of an analytical model for calculating the energy release rate
and mode mixity. Secondly, the use of these tests allows the fracture features of static and
fatigue failures to be generated under controlled conditions.
The double cantilever beam (DCB) is relevant for the determination of the potential
energy release rates when testing under pure Mode I [22] whereas the end notched flexure
(ENF) test specimen and end loaded split (ELS) are widely used for pure Mode II testing
[23]. In reality, delamination failures in fibre-reinforced epoxy composites are rarely driven
under a single mode, making it important to develop test methods for the measurements of
fracture resistance under mixed-mode loading. Several testing methods have been employed,
taking into account the effects of combined stresses such as the uneven bending moments
(DCB-UBM) test specimen [3], the mixed-mode bending (MMB) [24, 25] and mixed mode
flexure (MMF) [26]. The mixed-mode tests can be utilized to measure fracture toughness
components across various mixed-mode ratios ranging from pure Mode I to pure Mode II
while using the same specimen geometry [22, 3, 24]. The mixed-mode ratios can easily be
varied by changing the load position on the loading lever.
With regarding to testing of Mode III, the split cantilever bending (SCB) test [27] and the
out-of-plane loaded double cantilever beam (ODCB) test [28] are relevant.

2.2
Fractographic analysis
2.2.1
Fractographic techniques
Optical microscopy and scanning electron microscopy (SEM) are two commonly used
methods to study the fracture surfaces of fibre-reinforced composites [5]. An optical mi-
Page 7 of 237
==PAGE:21==
croscope uses refraction as light bends as it travels through objective lenses to the image
of the specimen being tested. An optical microscope can achieve a theoretical resolution
of the order of 0.01 nm [5], although, in practice, aberrations limit this value. The major
advantages of SEM over optical microscopy include higher depth of field and magnifications,
simple preparation and ease of operation [13], allowing crack propagation and fracture mor-
phologies on the fracture surfaces to be observed [5]. SEM has a depth of field that is 100
times greater than that of an optical micoscope [4]. SEM utilizes high-energy electrons with
shorter wavelengths to achieve much higher magnification images [5]. In SEM, an electron
beam scans across the fracture surface of interest to form an image. The electron beam
travels through a vertical path under a relatively high vacuum condition.
The electron
beam generates electrons by thermionic emission from a tungsten filament that burns at
high temperatures. As these electrons interact with the atoms in the sample, they produce
signals that contain information about the surface tomography of the sample. The various
types of signals include secondary electrons (SE), back-scattered electrons (BSE), charac-
teristic X-rays and light (cathodoluminescence) (CL), absorbed current (specimen current)
and transmitted electrons [4].
It can be either used the Secondary Electrons (SE) or Backscattered Electrons (BSE)
in a SEM machine. SE are a result of inelastic collision of electrons coming into contact
with the surface of the sample and have a relatively low energy (1 to 50eV) [4]. SE is an
optimal choice, providing detailed surface morphology and fine topographical details. BSE
is a result of elastic collision and scattering between incident electrons and specimen nuclei
or electrons [4].
BSE is mainly used for material composition analysis and topographic
imaging [4]. Before SEM examination, the examined fracture surface needs to be covered
with a thin carbon layer or gold layer, in order to make it electrically conductive.
The
value of accelerating voltage to be used during SEM experiment has both advantages and
drawbacks. High values of accelerating voltage can help bring the sharpness or quality into
the captured images. However, there exists some charging problems that might occur in
the fracture regions, making the features of the fracture surface become obscured. In an
event of charging, it is recommended to reapply another conductive layer on the sample or
reduce the accelerating voltage. The scanning rates in SEM control the quality of the image.
Higher scanning speeds are recommended when roaming around the failure sites for fracture
features, while lower scanning rates can help obtain high resolution images [14]. The two
micrographs of a 1-mm steel ball are shown in Figure 5 for visualizing the two most common
types of images that are generated under SE and BSE in SEM.

2.2.2
Static fracture features
The macroscopic appearance of Mode I fracture are typically rough in texture with nu-
merous broken fibres, flat, dark, and reflective [6, 8]. Mode I static failure surfaces can
exhibit extensive fibre bridging [5]. In the process of fibre bridging, the fibres of adjacent
plies can help bridge the delamination plane and isolate the crack [29]. The typical features
of Mode I (peel) fracture are textured microflow, riverlines and scarps [5]. Riverlines, or river
markings, are cracks moving at different planes that eventually converge to form a single
crack, the direction in which multiple riverlines are converged can indicate the direction of
local crack propagation from the Mode I fracture surfaces [8, 6, 12]. Riverlines are form due
to local variations in the matrix propeties. Scarps, or cleavage steps, are multiple fractures
initiate along the crack front, begin to propagate on several slightly different planes, and
then subsequently converge onto one plane [6, 5]. Textured microflow can be identifiable in
SEM by the granular appearance of the matrix surface, which can provide the information
on local crack growth directions within the resin [12, 8]. All the Mode I static features are
shown in Figure 6.
Figure 6: Features of Mode I static fracture from [5] (magnification of x600). The black
arrow indicates the direction of crack growth.

The Mode II fracture surfaces were found to be dull and non-reflective [8, 6]. The presence
of cusps is one of the most important fractographic features observed in composites, allowing
Mode II failures to be distinguished from Mode I failures [8]. Initially, cusps are formed by
a number of micro-tensile failures within the matrix of composites due to shear loading
[6]. As the shear stresses increase, the number of tensile fractures eventually increase and
elongate, eventually converge to form cusps as shown at the 45° angle as shown in Figure
7. As supported by a study in characterizing the macroscale fracture process in a brittle
adhesive layer, it shows the Mode II crack growth originates from an array of curved tensile
microcracks of Mode I [30].

The number of cusps was found to be numerous and frequent in numbers when Mode

Mixed-mode fracture surfaces are generally a combination of the morphologies observed
under pure Mode I and Mode II fracture surfaces [5]. As the Mode II component increases,
the crack planes reorientate at an angle to the laminate plane, eventually coalescing and
forming cusps, from which information such as mode mixity, shearing direction and crack
growth direction can be deduced [12]. As the Mode II loading stress reduces, the shape of
the cusps becomes shallower [8].

2.3
Problem statement
In the present study, scanning electron microscopy (SEM) is applied with the goal of
obtaining the most relevant fractographic features of static and fatigue crack growth in spec-
imens selected from the previous mechanical testing phase. These test specimens include
the double cantilever beam (DCB) subjected to static and cyclic loading, and an element
test specimen with multiple cracks. The fracture features associated with the different load-
ing modes, the typical fracture behaviours in static and fatigue loading conditions, and the
fracture behaviours close to the crack tip and remote from the crack tip are the key considera-
tions in this study. The fractographic analysis is conducted on the test specimens made from
glass fiber-reinforced epoxy composites, however the findings in the report and experimental
methods may be applicable to other composite materials. It is not only the main interest
in capturing those fracture feature images but also in ensuring a reliable method to achieve
and interpret the results. As part of the fractographic analysis study, the typical fracture
features found on the fracture surfaces can be compared to distinguish the differences under
static and fatigue crack growth. The goals of this study include:
• Develop a methodology to find the matching features on both fracture sur-
faces at high magnifications.
• Apply the existing knowledge in the field of fractography to identify:
1. Whether the crack growth is static or cyclic.
2. The direction of crack propagation.
Page 15 of 237
==PAGE:29==
3. The mode mixity.
4. Whether the crack growth rate is stable or unstable under cyclic loading.
• Correlate microscale failure mechanisms observed on the fracture surface
with the corresponding mode mixity and loading conditions.

4.4
Summary of results of static DCB specimens
The presence of riverlines and scarps is a crucial evidence of Mode I static features.
Matrix features become an important part in identifying matching features of Mode I static
fracture surface. The direction in which the riverlines converge can provide information of
the local crack growth direction. Under Mode II static, it is possible to deduce if a fracture
surface is fibre-rich or imprint-rich region at low magnifications. On the fracture surface
of the Mixed-mode I-II (41° mode mixity), it was difficult to conclude this. Cusp features
generated on the fibre-dominated fracture surface of Mixed-mode I-II (68° mode mixity) as
shown in Figure 158a and and Mode II as shown in Figure 146a and Figure 145a have a good
agreement with cusps on the fibre-dominated region from reference as shown in Figure 8.
Cusps on the fibre-dominated region tend to point away from the failure site (ie. the opposite
direction of the crack growth direction), while cusps on the imprint-dominated region point
in the direction of crack growth. The physical appearance of the cusps are controlled by the
fracture mode. As the 0° tilt angle, cusps found on Mixed-mode I-II (41° mode mixity) and
Mixed-mode I-II (68° mode mixity) fracture surfaces appear at a much shallower angle in
comparison with cusps found on those of Mode II static. Cusps generated under Mode III
static are different from those cusps generated by in-plane shearing. The comparison of all
the cusps generated under static can be referred to Figure 83. Figure 84 and Figure 85.

5
Discussion
5.1
Static specimens
5.1.1
Mode I static sample
Of all the images taken in SEM, the fracture features are not visible when viewing in
SEM from the maximum FoV to 1000 µm. The fracture surface of lower beam exhibit fibre-
dominated region, while the upper beam fracture surface shows mainly imprints with only
a few fibres. From the FoV of 500 µm to FoV of 60 µm, riverlines and scarps become visible
on the fracture surface as shown in Figure 138, Figure 139, Figure 140.
The surface of
exposed fibres was quite clean, with little matrix debris remaining on the fracture surface.
A number of broken fibres were observe from the fracture surface because fibre bridging
becomes prevalent under Mode I static opening. Riverlines tend to form adjacent to the
scarps and perpendicular to the scarp direction. In the last images of 60 µm, scarps seem to
align perfectly to the crack propagation direction.
5.1.2
Mode II static sample (ψ = 89.5°)
As, the fracture features are not visible from the maximum FoV to 1000 µm. Checking for
matching features was done concurrently during zooming in process. The Mode II fracture
surface is mainly involved with the characteristics of matrix failure.
Certain amount of
matrix was lost due to sliding abrasion between the fracture surfaces during shear loading.
Generally, specific features of Mode II static crack growth are not detectable from maximum
FoV to 1000 µm. However, when gradually lowering the magnification to the FoV of 500
µm, 200 µm and 60 µm, the cusps were seen. Cusps were seen from the lower beam fracture
surface to tilt to the left, signifying the shear loading direction to drive the crack growth
damage. On the upper beam, which was significantly fibre imprints, the cusps are pointing
towards right (the crack growth direction). The lower beam shows the opposite movements
of the cusps (going to the initiation site of failure).
Fibre spacing is closely linked to the size of the cusps and the number of the cusps. Small
interfibre spacing leads to larger amount of cusps and the size of cusps become smaller, while
high fibre spacing results in fewer number of cusps and the size of cusps become bigger.
Tilting the cusps at an angle is useful in verifying the movement direction of the cusps
in Mode II static loading, which is going to the left on the lower beam and going to the
right on the upper beam. The opposite movement of both matching fracture surface can be
concluded. Evidence of pulling force from static opening of specimens is observed, the tails
of cusps are pointing in the vertical direction.
A discussion of how cusps are formed is briefed here, this is phase one of cusps formation

5.1.3
Mixed mode I-II static specimen (ψ = 41°)
When viewing the fracture surface at high magnifications, it was hard to conclude which
surface is fibre-dominate or imprint-dominated. When zooming closer at the FoV of 500 µm,
200 µm and 60 µm, the lower (matching) surface shows cusps at a much shallower angle as
compared to the cusps in Mode II. On the lower beam, the cusps were seen to the left. The
other side of the fracture surface at the same location exhibited no cusps but only scars. The
initiation of failure site can be signified by the movement of the cusps on the upper beam.
Cusps on the upper beam tend to move in the crack propagation direction. At the FoV of
200 µm, the fibre mingling on the fracture surface is dislocated a little from the imprint on
the other side.
At the 0°, 55° and 70°, cusps appearances exhibit a much shallower angle on the fracture
surface. Cusps tend to occur in the matrix between adjacent fibres which can be explained
how cusps are formed. Cusps on both upper beam and lower beam fracture surfaces are seen
to move in opposite directions to each other. Same as Mode II static loading, cusps on the
upper fracture surface move in the crack growth direction. When taking another 10 locations
that contain cusps on the lower beam, the pointing angle of the cusps can be approximated,
which was found to be at 39 ± 0.5 °.

5.1.4
Mixed mode I-II static specimen (ψ = 68°)
When viewing the fracture surface of Mixed mode I-II (68°mixity) at higher magnifica-
tions, the lower fracture surface is seen to be dominated by fibres and the upper fracture
surface is mainly contained with matrix. As the Mode II loading component increases, the
size and the number of cusps increases. Significant matrix losses were seen on the fracture
surface at this mixed mode loading. From FoV of 500 µm to 60 µm on the surface of up-
per beam fracture, the movements of the cusps agree reasonably well in a certain direction.
Cusps are moving to the left on the lower beam fracture surface, meaning the shear loading
direction goes to the right. On the upper beam fracture surface, cusps are moving to the
right or in the direction of crack propagation.
When tilted the sample at 0°, 55°and 70°, cusps appearance of 68°mode mixity has a
higher depth in comparison with cusps found on the fracture surface of 41°mode mixity.
The opposite movement direction of the cusps matches quite well on both fracture surfaces.
The size of the blank area in the matrix varies significantly in the matrix, which could be
explained by the local variations in the matrix properties. When approximating the angle
of cusps on the lower beam, the value was found at approximately 49.54°.
5.1.5
Mode III static specimen
In the Mode III static fracture far from the end position at the crack tip, significant
amount of matrix was lost due to the two fracture surfaces coming into contact with each
other, making the task of checking identical features a difficult one. Cusps generated by
Mode III static load are seen to move sideways, meaning cusps are oriented out of the
original plane. The fractures mainly occured in the matrix with very little matrix debris left
on the fibre surfaces. On the fibre-dominated fracture surface, the lower beam surface, cusps
move approximately vertically to the left. The opposite movement of the cusps on the other
side is observed. Of all the images taken from the FoV of 500 µm to Fov of 60 µm, the sizes
of the cusps also vary significantly.
On the fracture surfaces near the crack tip, cusp have characteristics of pure mode loading
followed by Mode I opening during sample preparation. Taking the cusps in the middle of
the sample pure Mode II, one face shows cusps while the other only exhibit scars. Cusps
were seen to move diagonally down on the upper fracture surface. Cusps close to the inner
edge and outer edge of the sample have characteristic of Mode I and Mode II opening. The
opening force to complete separation could have appeared to influence the shape of the cusps
on these two fracture surfaces.

Fatigue specimens
5.2.1
Mode I fatigue specimen
Cusps were observed on the Mode I fatigue fracture surface, but they are different in
appearance in comparison with Mode II and Mode III static cusps. The author could not
quite explain how these cusps are developed on the fracture surface. Same as the previous
cusps, cusps on the upper beam are seen to move in the direction of crack propagation. When
Page 113 of 237

viewed the cusps at 55°tilt angle on the sample, cusps are slightly curve and regularly space.
The size of these cusps become roughly similar in the same fibre imprint. The opposite
movement direction of the cusps on both sides can be confirmed when viewing the matching
fracture surfaces.
Features such as debonding fibre lengths in the imprint were observed at multiple location
on the upper fracture surface. There are variations of fibre debonding lengths in the same
fibre imprint, between different imprints or event at different examined locations. The highest
obtainable debonding length is at the location 2, which is average of 96.8 mm.
5.2.2
Mixed mode I-II fatigue specimen (ψ = 20°)
When viewed at low magnifications, the fracture surface was likely to be a imprint-
dominated fracture surface. Of all the fracture images on the upper beam, no matrix rollers
were seen to form. However, several shallow cusps are moving to the right or the direction in
which the crack propagates. Cusps found on the fracture surface of 20° mode mixity fatigue
crack growth are different from those generated by static load. Cusps generated under fatigue
crack growth have similar sizes in the same matrix region and are quite regularly spaced.
No fibre debonding marks were visible in the imprints of fracture surface.
5.2.3
Mixed mode I-II fatigue specimen (ψ = 50°)
When viewed at low magnifications, the fracture surface exhibited mainly fibres. No
formation of matrix rollers was seen in all fracture images of Mixed mode I-II fatigue with
50° mode mixity. Cusps on the fatigue fracture surface appear at a shallow angle and point to
the crack growth direction. Cusps generated under 50° mode mixity fatigue crack growth are
quite similar in size and regularly spaced in the matrix between fibres. No fibre debonding
marks were visible in the imprints of fracture surface.
5.2.4
Mixed mode I-II fatigue specimen (ψ = 75°)
When taking the cusps on the fracture surfaces for a comparison, it is important consider
the features of the same fibre spacing as well as fracture surfaces where the cusps were taken
have the same characteristics (fibre-rich or imprint rich region).
Features such as matrix rollers are found on the fracture surface of 75° mode mixity
fatigue crack growth. The number of rollers are numerous on the failure site. At the FoV of
200 µm and FoV of 60 µm, the many deteriorated cusps are not completely detached from
the fracture surface, and the sizes are much smaller compared to matrix rollers. Smaller
interfibre spacing leads to smaller cusps and more number of cusps.
5.2.5
Mode II fatigue specimen
At low magnifications, the fracture surface of Mode II fatigue crack growth was seen to be
dominated by fibres. It was quite difficult to distinguish the appearance of fracture surface
generated under Mode II fatigue crack growth and 70° mode mixity loading. The Mode II
fracture surface was generated by many matrix rollers. A very few number of deteriorated
Page 114 of 237
==PAGE:128==
cusps remain on the surface. There are signs of cusps in the matrix between fibres but they
are quite obscured by the matrix debris on the surface.
5.2.6
Summary
Features generated close to the slip foil (ie. remotely from the crack tip position) are
generated under pure mode loading with very little influence of opening due to sample
preparation. The typical fracture features of Mode I static loading are riverlines and scarps,
which can correlate to the global fracture damage growth direction. Cusps are seen to form
in Mixed mode I-II, Mode II and Mode III static loading, which is an important result
of shear loading of two fracture surfaces moving relative to each other. The crack growth
direction and shear loading direction can be drawn from the movement of the cusps. Cusps
found on the upper beam (imprint-rich region) tend to move in the crack growth direction.
The fracture surface of shear loading tend to be obscured with matrix debris which can
destroy the evidence of matching features. When checking for matching fracture features,
the opposite movement of the cusps on both fracture surfaces can be confirmed. One fracture
surfaces shows cusps, the other will show only scars. The cusps generated under pure Mode
II loading experience a little pulling force from the appearance of the tails of the cusps. The
size and number of the cusps can vary widely at different fibre spacing. At the Mode II
component increases in mixed mode loading, the angle of the cusps increases. To properly
identify the angle of the cusps, it is recommended to tilt the tested sample at an angle of
55° to 70°. Cusps found on Mode III fracture surfaces are different than those found on
Mode II or Mixed mode I-II fracture surface. Cusps in Mode III mode sideways out of the
original fracture plane. When taking the cusps at the crack end location, their appearance
was observed to be impacted by separating force due to opening. Cusps in the middle region
of the specimens have characteristics of Mode III followed by Mode I while cusps in the outer
and inner edge regions of the specimens have an appearance of Mode II followed by Mode I
opening.
Mode I fatigue fracture has characteristics such as cusps and fibre debonding in the
imprints. However, the physical appearance of these cusps were quite different from static
cusps. Cyclically generated cusps are regularly spaced, and there is not many variations in
terms of the size of the cusps. For the Mixed mode fatigue sample of 20° and 50° mode
mixity, no matrix rollers were generated but multiple shallow cusps were observed on the
fracture surfaces. At higher to lower magnifications, there is no significant differences. As
the Mode II fatigue component increases, features like matrix rollers become a dominant
part of the fracture surface.

6
Conclusions
Fractures in composites behave differently under the case of small-scale fracture process
zone versus large-scale fracture process zone. In small-scale fracture process zone, the crack
grows independently of its surrounding microstructure, while in large-scale fracture process
zone cracks might involve complex interactions like fibre bridging. Before entering the steady-
state growth, the crack has to propagate to a certain distance before the propagation of the
crack does not change as the crack extends. The direction of fiber debonding marks is useful
for identifying the rate of crack growth during fiber bridging. However, this method is not
reliably for investigating progressing direction of the crack as fibre debonding marks moving
in different direction on the matching fracture surfaces. SEM observations on static fracture
surfaces often exhibit cusps, which are evidence of shear loading. However, cusps were also
found on the fatigue fracture surface.
