Abstract
In this thesis, I cover a relatively new method that enables the training of large language models
with a substantially reduced memory footprint by eagerly applying the parameter updates during the
backward pass. This method is not limited to large language models, and could potentially work for
most models in deep learning, although one should be careful in the case of using back-propagation
through time with recurrent neural networks. I then proceed to investigate the claims that there exists
an internal truth representation in large language models exhibiting a linear structure, and potentially
also containing more information about truth than the model expresses during generation. Here I
find some indications supporting both claims, although more work is needed to definitively conclude
whether this is always the case. I also investigate whether it is possible to obtain better detection with
a non-linear probe compared to a linear one, and do not observe any big advantage to the non-linear
probe. Lastly, I investigate whether the method of Inference-Time Intervention (ITI) presented in (1)
will work across different model architectures, and how well the method generalizes out of distribution.
Here I only observe improvements from ITI on the models that have not been instruction-tuned. I
also find that while ITI may improve the model when evaluated on in-distribution data, it seems to
come with a similar performance deterioration on out-of-distribution data. The code for this project
is available on GitHub: https://github.com/MikkelGodsk/Masters-thesis.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Since its launch in November 2022, ChatGPT has become a household name. Being widely used by
people across different domains, it poses a significant challenge in educational contexts. The easy access
to autogenerated texts is a major concern since it makes honest evaluation of students challenging. For
this reason, it is often seen as the enemy in academic contexts. In spite of these challenges, chatbots
also come with a huge potential to aid students in their learning, by more closely approximating a 1:1
student-teacher ratio by providing a personal tutor. This has been attempted in Harvard University’s
CS50 Introduction to Computer Science with good results and positive feedback from the students (7).
A common framework for building chatbots that are aligned with human values is the 3 H’s: We
want chatbots to be helpful, honest, and harmless, which are meant to be understood in their literal
sense (8; 9).
Although CS50 had success with implementing a chatbot based on ChatGPT, these
models often tend to suffer in the honesty aspect and are very prone to so-called hallucinations. In
(10), they break down the term into three types: 1) input-conflicting hallucinations, in which the
model answers a different question than it was asked, 2) context-conflicting hallucinations, in which
the model is inconsistent with what it just said, and 3) fact-conflicting hallucinations, in which the
model generates text that is conflicting with the established truth.
Various methods for improving the honesty aspect have been proposed. One is to simply edit the
activations of the model during inference (1; 11; 12) since the models seem to be aware of their own
falsehoods to some extent (13; 14; 15; 16; 17).
A second approach could be to attempt fine-tuning the model for better alignment with its internal-
ized (parametric) knowledge (10; 18; 19). Such fine-tuning may also be feasible with limited resources
using recent advances in parameter-efficient fine-tuning (e.g. (20; 21)), which can significantly reduce
computational costs.
Another concern to consider is the lack of democratization of the technology since the models
are often close-sourced (18) or only have their parameters available but are not fully open-sourced
(22; 23; 24; 25; 26; 27). Training the models also typically requires an inaccessible number of GPUs,
with total GPU time during training typically reported to be in the millions of total GPU hours
(23; 24; 28).
In this thesis, I will make the following contributions:
1. I will present and analyze a relatively new approach that can be used to significantly reduce
the computational resources required for model training. This approach is not limited to large

2. I will investigate the model’s internal representation of truth, how it may not be fully expressed
during the model’s generation, its geometry, and test how well the approach from (1), using

2.1
Language Models
In this section, I will introduce a theoretical framework for describing language models while trying to
avoid over-formalization. It will closely follow the formalism from (29; 30). All language models are
assumed to be uni-directional (also known as causal), i.e. they predict the next token based on the
previous tokens, never the other way around.
2.1.1
Pre-requisites for language modeling
To define a language model, I introduce the following concepts: an alphabet, a string, a language, and
a language model.
We define an alphabet Σ as a finite non-empty set of symbols. We define a string y over the
alphabet Σ as a sequence y “ y1y2y3... of symbols contained in Σ. The language over the alphabet is
defined as the Kleene-closure Σ˚ which contains all finite strings over Σ. Notice that Σ˚ is an infinite
set, but only contains strings of finite length. We further denote the empty string by ε.
We often augment the alphabet with an EOS token (End-Of-Sequence token) signaling the gener-
ation procedure to stop. We denote the alphabet augmented with the EOS token as ¯Σ “ Σ Y tEOSu,
but only consider the language as Σ˚ (29; 30).
I will further add a BOS token to ¯Σ, i.e.

A similar formalization of languages is also extensively used in e.g. automata theory, a widely
studied topic in computer science (see e.g. (31)), as well as multiple classical NLP methods (see e.g.
(29)).

Decoding strategy
The formalization of a language model provided in eq. 2.1 merely gives a probability distribution over
strings. This alone is insufficient for finding the most probable string. As it turns out, finding the
globally optimal string requires brute force due to the non-Markovian structure. Instead, we attempt
to find a locally optimal solution by using a decoding strategy. Decoding strategies come in two flavors:
stochastic and deterministic. These include sampling (stochastic), greedy search (deterministic), and
beam search (deterministic). In greedy search, we greedily pick the most probable token yt P ¯Σ in
each factor in the product in eq. 2.1, while terminating the generation procedure upon selecting the
EOS token. In beam search, we do not restrict ourselves to consider just one sequence but instead,
generate in a tree-like fashion while only keeping the top-k most probable sequences at any layer. In
essence, it is a pruned breath-first search (29).

2.3. COMPUTATIONAL TRICKS FOR LLMS
2.2.3
Computational complexity of self-attention
As noted in (41), the computational complexity of each self-attention layer is Opn2 ¨ dq for a sequence
of length n and with hidden dimension d. Efforts have been made to improve on this quadratic time
complexity, including linear attention (52), or Mamba (53) where the latter abandons the transformer
paradigm in favor of a state-space model.
2.3
Computational tricks for LLMs
Training modern neural networks is often very computationally intensive and often suffers from a
memory bottleneck. During backpropagation, the standard approach requires storing 1) the model
parameters, 2) the gradients, 3) the model activations, 4) the optimizer states, 5) other potential
intermediary values from the gradient computation. If we denote the total number of model parameters
as Nmodel, then the gradients also require memory to store Nmodel values. Further, if using the Adam
optimizer, the optimizer state requires memory to store 2Nmodel values. Typically these are all stored
on the GPUs VRAM, but sometimes memory off-loading to the CPUs RAM is used where some of the
tensors are stored in CPU RAM instead (54; 55; 56). Offloading comes with a drawback in terms of
speed since data transfer is typically a slow process (57; 58).
Luckily, efforts have successfully been made to circumvent these issues of huge memory require-
ments, which has further democratized the training of larger models.

2.5
Internal Representations
We refer to the activity within the network as the activations or internal representations, which live in
the activation space Rn for some n individual to each layer. In the activations, specific patterns may
represent specific semantically meaningful attributes. I will refer to these as the concept representations
which refer to a specific characteristic in the activation patterns whereby the concept is represented.
The literature seems to use the term internal representation a bit vaguely to define one of the two (see
e.g. (1; 63; 64; 65; 13; 14; 15)).
It is commonly conjectured that the concept representations have a linear structure14. This hy-
pothesis is referred to as the linear representation hypothesis (see e.g. (67; 6)). Furthermore, a second
hypothesis, called the superposition hypothesis, claims that the representations may not be orthogonal,
thereby enabling more concepts to be represented in a layer, than there are dimensions in the layer (6).
The orthogonality could otherwise have allowed for single-neuron representations of concepts. This is
also congruent with the idea of population codes in neuroscience (see e.g. (68; 69)), i.e. that biological
neurons do not code for concepts on their own, but concepts are encoded in the activity patterns of
neural populations instead. In (63), they also consider the network activations in a specific layer as a
linear combination of concept vectors representing the datapoint, since they also find that the concepts

Probing
In (65), they introduce the concept of a linear probe, which fits a logistic regression to the layer
activations of the neural network model against some target. Considering the linear representation
hypothesis, a linear probe could be a particularly good choice for investigating the internal represen-
tations. In (63), they use images exhibiting the specific concepts versus ones that do not (e.g. to fit
a probe to the concept of stripes, they could use images of zebras, tigers, zebra finches, Stars and
Stripes, etc., against images of people, firetrucks, dogs, etc.) to fit their linear probe. Again, they see
evidence that concepts may, at least in part, be linearly represented.
However, we may also use a non-linear probe which can e.g. be an SVM with RBF kernel (66), or
a multi-layer perceptron (12).
2.5.2
Truth direction
A debated topic (see e.g. (1; 13; 14; 15; 16)) is whether large language models possess a concept
representation of truth.
In (14), they find that different GPT-models tend to answer much more
consistently when asked to list the authors of scientific references that truly exist, as compared to
references that do not. Interestingly, this contrast in consistency could indicate that the model may
contain some information about whether or not it truly knows what it is talking about.
In (15), they propose a method called SAPLMA, which trains a shallow neural network as a probe
for truthfulness on the internal representations of the LLM. Here they report a 70%-90% detection
rate of the truth of a statement with a SAPLMA probe fitted on a middle layer of LLaMa-2-7b. In
(1; 11; 12), they take it one step further and report that it is possible to elicit more truthful answers
on TruthfulQA. Here they use a method they dub Inference-Time Intervention, which consists of
adding a vector (the truth vector; see section 3.4.4) to the activations of specific attention heads in the
LLM during inference. This points towards a causality between the truth direction and the truth of
generation. However, it is unclear how well the method generalizes to other datasets.

2.7. PARAMETRIC KNOWLEDGE
In (76), they introduce a method of extracting part of a knowledge graph from an LLM, without
querying from a reference (ground truth) graph. Here they do take the precision into account but also
acknowledge that recall may be poorly defined in their scenario due to the varying coverage of entities
in Wikidata.
It is often colloquially said that LLMs possess an internal knowledge graph/base (see e.g. (16),
(76)) in its parametric knowledge.
2.7
Parametric knowledge
In (39), the authors propose the so-called Superficial Alignment Hypothesis, which asserts that most
of an LLMs parametric knowledge is acquired during the pre-training phase, and that the alignment
phase mostly teaches the model to respond properly. The authors test this by instruction tuning a
model on a small dataset of 1000 curated responses with diverse prompts. Here they manage to obtain
a LLaMa model whose response is in 43% of cases on par with or even preferred over GPT-4.
In (10; 16), it is argued that the alignment phase of training may incentivize the model to exceed
its knowledge boundary by trying to guess the answer of a question it does not have the pertinent
knowledge to answer. The knowledge boundary refers to the limits of the model’s knowledge, and
it is crucial for the model to know to be metacognitive. It is further argued that even if we include
responses expressing abstention, we still run the risk of misaligning the model’s metacognition in the
cases when it does possess the pertinent knowledge to answer truthfully.
The conjecture is further investigated in a very recent paper (77), where they introduce a method
for assessing what knowledge the PaLM-2M base model (78) likely possesses and to what extent,
based on the generation likelihood. They use a knowledge graph paradigm, based on Wikidata (73), to
determine factuality. Here they find that fine-tuning the LLM on knowledge it does not seem to possess,
seems to result in a degradation of closed-book QA test performance over the base (pre-trained) model,
while fine-tuning on known knowledge does not have such a detrimental effect. Furthermore, they find
that the facts unknown to the base model are eventually learned but slowly so, while decreasing the
closed-book QA test performance. Also, they find that fine-tuning on known facts leads to slightly
higher closed-book QA test performance indicating better utilization of the pre-existing knowledge.
Although they only tested a single model, it casts some nuance on the previous discussion, and suggests
that while the superficial alignment hypothesis may have some merit, it should also be taken with a
grain of salt.
In (79), they find that LLMs mostly learn to memorize facts frequently mentioned in the dataset,
while scaling up the model does not help with learning infrequently mentioned facts.

In popular contemporary deep learning frameworks (PyTorch, Tensorflow e.g.), it is common practice
to separate the gradient computation (the backward pass) from the optimization step. In PyTorch,
this looks like this:

This approach requires storing the gradients of the model in memory, while the gradients take up
as much space as the trainable parameters themselves, as described in section 2.3. For training big
models, this can serve as a bottleneck.
I will now sketch out the narrative of the following section since it includes examples, counterex-
amples, and counterexamples to the counterexamples. If confusion hits, the reader may refer back
to this outline. The structure is as follows: 1) I will first define a general model that allows for an
improved method for performing back propagation, which I shall refer to MEBP (Memory-Efficient
Back Propagation; 2) I will then show that parameter sharing between layers in the network can be
a problem, e.g. if we train an RNN with an improper implementation of MEBP; 3) I will then show
that there may exist exceptions to this exception, by using stochastic gradient descent without weight
decay; 4) I will then proceed to show the proper way of using MEBP with RNNs, which does allow
for memory savings; 5) finally, I will discuss some implementation details and more.

Although the model was derived based on the assumption of no parameter sharing between layers,
we may still be able to get memory savings in some (potentially many) occasions.
To determine
whether this would be the case for a model, you would need to examine the computation graph and
potentially optimize the order of computations similarly to the RNN examples. If, for instance, only a
part of the network has parameter sharing, but the remaining parameters are not shared, then memory
savings may still be easily obtainable, especially for the non-shared parameters as their updates would
typically only depend on the activations and error signals. Without giving a proof, I conjecture that
this will sometimes be the case, and in those cases it may be beneficial to only store the activations
and error signals for the layers with shared parameters, whenever they collectively are smaller than
their gradient in terms of memory consumption.
We may also be able to use some regularization techniques efficiently, if we can collapse their terms
to scalar values within the layers, and then add the terms to the pass-through stream. In principle, this
would postpone computing the gradient component of the regularization term for a given parameter
until we are also computing its other gradient components in the corresponding layer.
The above analysis/discussion is original work, and I conceived the idea independently with in-
spiration from the concept of feedback alignment (somewhat unrelated to LLMs; refer to e.g. (83)).
However, the idea behind the algorithm has recently been somewhat explored in (5; 84) (with a further
extension in (85)), but it does not seem to be widely used yet.

To briefly summarize this section: 1) If we update our parameters eagerly as we compute the
gradients, we can save memory during training while not performing (many) more operations than
we otherwise would have; 2) We have to be very careful with networks that could be formalized as
having weight-sharing between layers, since that can, depending on the model, result in the algorithm
providing no memory savings; 3) As they point out in (5), gradient normalization across all model
parameters is not particularly elegant.

3.3
Generation of unfactual statements
One way of viewing unfactual statements is simply as being non-existing edges in a reference knowledge
graph. Here we need to make the assumption that the chosen knowledge graph has complete knowledge
of our task, which is unlikely to be true. To create a dataset with both existing and non-existing edges,
we should be careful to not make the non-existing ones too easy to guess without possessing relevant
knowledge. For instance, if we pick a subject in the category of cars (e.g. Toyota Prius) and ask
whether it attended a specific university (e.g. Harvard), then it is easy to guess that the answer is
”No” since cars do not attend universities. This motivates a restriction on the subject, relations, and
objects, so as to keep nonsensical statements out of the dataset as they would be too easy to guess.
Another example of a statement that would make sense, but may be implausible-sounding, could
be to ask ”Did Donald Trump attend the Technical University of Denmark?”, which may also sound
highly unlikely but not impossible. Therefore, many statements like this one would likely also be too
easy to guess, motivating a further restriction on the fabricated statements.
Taking these considerations into account, I propose the procedure of algorithm 4 to create plausible-
sounding statements. In example 3.3.1, I demonstrate using the procedure to generate a dataset about
which universities different politicians attended.

The software implementation of MEBP uses hooks in PyTorch as in (84). To get it to work with
HuggingFace required a careful read-through of the source code for HuggingFace’s trl.SFTTrainer,
and a hacky mock-optimizer that is compatible with it while implementing the memory-efficient back-
propagation procedure. I also implemented a small optimization by setting param.grad = None for
the given parameter, which I found to be slightly faster than the usual .zero grad call in an early
test.9
To my knowledge, this is the first implementation of the MEBP/LOMO method compatible with
HuggingFace.10
The implementation was unit tested as follows: 1) Two identical networks are instantiated (one is
a copy of the other), 2) I verify that their parameters are indeed copies and not just references to the
same tensor-objects in memory, 3) I train both networks, one with the usual method, and one with the
memory-efficient back-propagation procedure, with the same hyperparameters on randomly generated
data (of course, ensuring that the models get exactly the same batches at the same time steps), and
4) I finally test whether the parameters of the networks have exactly the same parameters (floating
point equality). After training on 100 batches of 5 examples on a CPU (since a GPU can introduce
numerical discrepancies between runs), the test passes by asserting that the two networks are exactly
the same.
I furthermore test the implementation on the OPT-125m model while trained with the HuggingFace
trainer for a few batches. Here I instantiate two models the same, train one with the usual approach,
and train one with MEBP. Setting max grad norm=None disables gradient norm clipping, giving exactly
the same parameters for the two models after training.

4.2
Truth representation
In the following, I will be investigating the existence of a direction of truth (refer to section 2.5.2).
Here I will first attempt to reproduce the results of (13), claiming that truth is linearly represented in
a Large Language Model. Here it is claimed that we can predict whether simple statements are true
or false based on a linear probe fitted to activations in the intermediate layers of the model. Inspired
by (15), I will further investigate whether you can get a better prediction of truth using a linear probe,
than by simply asking the model.
Although (1; 13) indicate that the truth representation exhibits a linear structure, it is not im-
mediately clear why that would necessary be the case (as demonstrated in (66) introducing Concept
Activation Regions, this should not be taken for granted). For this reason, I will investigate whether
I can obtain better generalization performance using a non-linear probe than a linear one.
Lastly, I will investigate whether it is indeed possible to elicit more truthful behavior by editing
the model activations as done in (1) across different models. However, here I will be altering the
method slightly. I will furthermore test the method’s ability to generalize to both in-distribution and
out-of-distribution datasets, which are constructed to evaluate the models ability to answer correctly
on different tasks.
The exact methodology has developed alongside my understanding and the code and datasets used
to conduct the experiments. For this reason, there will be some variability between how exactly the
experiments are conducted. This variability is mostly concerning the datasets, models and text-formats
used.

4.2.2
Non-linearity and generalization of the truth representation
Since it is not obvious why the concept representations of truth would necessarily be linearly structured,
although it is already hinted at by e.g. (1; 13), I will now investigate whether I can fit a non-linear
probe to obtain a higher detection accuracy than the linear one. Here I compare a linear SVM to
an SVM with RBF kernel. Note that the comparison between the RBF kernel and the linear kernel
is also done in (66), in which they demonstrate that it may be beneficial to think about concept
representations as regions rather than vectors.
In this experiment, I will make the transition to instead use the representations outputted by the
attention heads, before projection. Here I refer to headi of equation 2.34. The main reason for this
transition is to start more closely following the approaches of (1; 17), in which they linearly intervene
on selected attention heads.
The experiment follows the blueprint:
1. I choose the first 4000 instances of each dataset and discard the rest due to lack of computational
resources. If a dataset is smaller, it keeps its size. Here the data gets formatted as in table B.2.
2. I give the model a statement from TruthfulQA and compute its activations for all attention-heads
throughout the model.
3. I then use Bayesian optimization5 to find the best head throughout the entire model for predicting
whether or not the statement is true. This is performed for a linear SVM probe and a non-linear
RBF kernel SVM probe separately. The γ-hyperparameter of the non-linear SVM is also tuned
in its run. In the procedure, I optimize for the validation accuracy.
4. After 1000 trials with Bayesian optimization, I select the best-scoring hyperparameters and then
retrain a probe with these on the combined training and validation partitions.
5. I compute the 95% Jeffrey intervals of the accuracy on the test partition.
6. Finally, I compute the 95% Jeffrey intervals of the out-of-distribution detection accuracies for
the other datasets.
7. I then repeat the experiment to estimate the probe in-distribution accuracies across all datasets
individually. Here I repeat the above procedure for all datasets, but skip step 5. This is done
to get a sense of whether the probes trained on TruthfulQA will generalize well to the other
datasets.
8. I then use the SVM plots from section 3.4.2 to visualize the (greedily picked) directions6 which
have highest discriminability between the true and false statements. Here I use the respective
heads picked in step 7.

In this experiment, I will be using a different text formatting than in the previous two. Here I opted

using a non-linear probe to detect truth. Since this is the case, I will now follow the approach of (1)
and use a linear intervention along the mass-mean direction attempting to reproduce their results.
Here I use the following workflow for each model:
1. I first partition TruthfulQA (in statement-form) into 3 partitions of roughly 1
3 each using Scikit-
Learn’s StratifiedGroupKFold class.
This is done while grouping the statements by their
associated question, and then ensuring that each group is entirely contained within one of the
three partitions (i.e. no statements belonging to question i are found in two different groups). I
do this to be on the safe side with respect to avoiding information leakage. I also stratify over
the statement labels (True/False) and the category (e.g. Law, Economics, etc.) to which the
question belongs. This stratification procedure is performed to ensure that the distributions of
the partitions are as similar as possible.
2. I then rank each attention head (considered across all layers) based on how well we can detect
the truth of a statement (i.e. the TruthfulQA entries in statement-form) in its activations with
the mass-mean probe using the following procedure:
(a) Using 5-fold CV, I estimate the average accuracy of each probe for a head over partition 1.
I rank the heads based on accuracy and save the ranks in sorted order.
(b) I then retrain the probes on the full partition 1.
3. I then use the second partition to select the hyperparameters α (the number of standard devi-
ations to shift the activations), and K (the number of best heads to intervene on based on the
rankings, i.e. we select the top-K) by grid-search. Here I optimize for the MCCLA (defined in
section 3.4.1) on partition 2 of TruthfulQA in question-form. The truth-directions in which I
shift the activations are those found by the mass-mean probes in the previous step. Here I had
to limit the grid to pα, Kq P t1, 2, 4, 8, 16u ˆ t2%, 4%, 6%, 8%, 10%u, where the percentages are of
the total number of heads in the model in question. This limitation was done, because this step
is very time-consuming, and I saw good performance with these settings for multiple models.
4. I then use the third partition to compute a 95% CI for the TruthfulQA MCCLA of the intervened
and non-intervened models, which are the ones I report.
5. I also compute a 95% CI of the MCCLA on the selected out-of-distribution datasets for the
intervened and non-intervened models.

large ones to fit into memory. When evaluating on TruthfulQA with MCCLA, the model is normally

Discussion
5.1
Memory-efficient training
In the experiments of section 4.1, I found that memory-efficient back-propagation yields substantial
memory savings on larger models by eagerly applying the gradients in the backward pass, while it
does not seem to increase the training time much for bigger models. However, when scaling up the
input, the relative memory savings diminish. This happens because a larger fraction of the memory is
now used to store the inputs and activations, whereas the gradients have a constant memory footprint
equal to the model parameters.
The method may often apply to networks with less-trivial architectures than the standard feed-
forward network if we carefully build the computation graph in the forward pass to have a high temporal
locality for parameter usage. However, this method comes with the weakness that we cannot readily
normalize over all gradients, as pointed out in (5). This could pose an issue, since gradient clipping
by the total gradient norm may offer better convergence during training.
Although the method has a bigger memory footprint than QLoRA, it may still be useful for the
pre-training task. Since QLoRA only works for models that have already been pre-trained, it will not
be a proper choice if we want to train a language model from scratch on relevant domain knowledge.
Recall here that learning new knowledge in the fine-tuning phase is possible, but seems to potentially
promote hallucinatory behavior (77).
5.2
Truth representation
For the experiments on the truth representation, I generate the politicians dataset from Wikidata. Here
I use the assumption, as noted in section 3.3, that the knowledge graph has complete knowledge of
the task at hand. This adds some uncertainty to the task with an obvious potential for false negatives
due to missing relations. Hence, the dataset can only serve as a proxy for the ground truth and would
be difficult to evaluate for fitness since the ground truth is not readily available. However, as seen in
the results, the dataset does seem reasonable as compared to the other datasets used.
In the first two experiments with the truth representation (sections 4.2.1, 4.2.2), I manage to
reproduce the results from (13), indicating that the concept of truth exhibits a linear structure on
simple datasets. I found for some simple datasets (cities and politicians), it seems that we may be
==PAGE:62==

able to better detect the truth of a statement with a probe, than we can by asking the model the
corresponding straightforward question. This supports the narrative that the model ”knows when it
is lying”, as colloquially put in (15). However, on the common claims and counterfactuals datasets,
this is contested by having no statistically significant difference in performance between the probe
and model. It is difficult to guess whether this is simply due to the linear probe being too simple,
whether the selected layer (layer 15 as in (13)) does not adequately encode the truth representation on
those datasets, or potentially due to the datasets containing ambiguous or controversial statements,
or statements that are simply too hard for the model to understand (as claimed in (13)). Another
possible explanation is that maybe the model does not simply ”know” more than it is generating
on these datasets. However, the success of applying inference-time intervention (in section 4.2.3) to
pre-trained models (i.e. the versions that were not finetuned), could indicate that the claim is true, at
least for these.
Furthermore, I found no significant difference between the probe performances between the chat
and pre-trained LLaMa-2-7b models on any of the evaluated datasets, suggesting that the linear truth
representations of (13) have been developed during the pre-training phase, and not much further
developed during fine-tuning. This would seem to follow the Superficial Alignment Hypothesis from
(39). However, it does seem that the Yes/No CL accuracy improves on common claims with fine-tuning,
but also deteriorates on the cities dataset. As for now, since the experiment was only conducted for
LLaMa-2-7b, we should take the above conclusions as more of an indication, and in the future, it would
be appropriate to rerun the experiment with more models and datasets.
In the experiment comparing the linear probe to the non-linear probe (section 4.2.2), I did not see
any clear, unanimous evidence that using a non-linear (SVM with RBF kernel) probe would be much
better for detecting truth than a linear one. This fits with the narrative of e.g. (1; 13), as well as with
the linear representation hypothesis (explained in section 2.5).
In the experiment testing the generalization of Inference-Time Intervention from (1) to other
datasets (section 4.2.3), I found that while the method does seem to yield an improvement for some
models when trained on TruthfulQA, it does not seem to generalize well out of distribution, and fur-
ther seems to harm the performance on most of the selected OOD datasets. This is also consistent
with the observation from the previous experiment (refer to table 4.5), where I found that the linear
SVM probe did not seem to generalize across the other datasets when trained on TruthfulQA. It is
also noteworthy that ITI seems to generally work much better for the pre-trained models, than their
respective fine-tuned counterparts with non-overlapping confidence intervals.
An aspect of ITI that is not immediately clear, is why shifting the head activations by multiple
standard deviations out of the distribution (of activations) will tend to lead to more truthful behavior.
In (1), they found an optimal value of α “ 20 for LLaMa-2-7b. In table 4.12, I also see that relatively
high values for α are chosen (typically 8 or 16) for the models that positively respond to the intervention,
and α “ 1 for those that respond negatively. Interestingly, α “ 1 is chosen for all of the instruct models,
except for LLaMa-3-8b. Considering this, it is also currently unclear whether instruct models generally
respond worse to ITI, or whether they require hyperparameters in a different range than the pre-trained
models. However, increasing the overall search space will require substantial computational resources,
since this is a very time-consuming part of the procedure. Meanwhile, the failing instruct models do
have a detectable truth representation for TruthfulQA seemingly on par with the pre-trained models.
In (12), they use a non-linear (MLP) probe to rank the heads based on detectability of truth.

mean vectors are then chosen as an average over these. Curiously enough, the authors do report an
improvement in performance on their selected OOD datasets when using their (allegedly improved)
NL-ITI method (Non-Linear Inference-Time Intervention). However, they also list a deterioration in
performance on two out of three OOD datasets when using the ITI implementation from (1), which
they do not comment on. In (12), they do not quantify the uncertainties of their estimates, so it is
uncertain whether the difference is statistically significant. The reported TruthfulQA MC2 performance
of LLaMa-2-7b-chat in (12) is a bit on the higher end of what I found, but coincides with my computed
95% confidence interval in table 4.11. A possible reason is that I use 4-bit quantization, which could
somewhat impact the model performance.
A weakness of the experimental setup is the usage of MC Continuation Likelihood Accuracy (MC-
CLA), since it is not completely clear whether this is a good metric for evaluation. Although it is
commonly used to evaluate models on TruthfulQA, it has not, to my knowledge, been evaluated for
how well its score correlates with human evaluators on answers produced through decoding. However,
the alternative for TruthfulQA would be to train a GPT-Judge model for this sole purpose, which
would likely be time-consuming and expensive.
As a last remark, I want to point out how using MCCLA on the neg-cities dataset poses an
interesting problem: To score high in this setup, the model must be good at assigning high likelihoods
to all countries that the city in question is not located in. For instance, asking ”Which country is the
city Copenhagen not in?” with the two possible choices ”Denmark” and ”China” would clearly require
the continuation likelihood of ”China” to be higher than that of ”Denmark”.
However, since the
correct answer here was randomly picked, the model would be required to also rank all other countries
(USA, Sweden, Italy, Mexico, etc.) higher than ”Denmark” as a continuation to obtain a good score on
average. So essentially, this setup tests how well the model assigns the lowest continuation likelihood
to the incorrect response (the correct city).
5.3
Future work
In the first experiment on the truth representation (comparing probe performance to model perfor-
mance), it would make sense to rerun it on more models and datasets. In my experiment, half of the
datasets I used were found to be difficult to probe in (13), and hence it would be interesting to use
either much bigger models (e.g. Claude), or more datasets of a simpler format (e.g. produced using
the method of section 3.3). It would also be interesting to see if we could train e.g. an MLP probe on
the more challenging datasets to obtain better performance than the model continuation likelihood.
For the knowledge probing in general, we could see it as a classification task. I only report on the
knowledge probe accuracies, although reporting on precision/recall would also be interesting.
In the experiment with Inference-Time Intervention, I intervened on all tokens rather than the last
token. It would be interesting to rerun the experiment while intervening only on the last token, or to
use NL-ITI from (12) across the selection of dataset and models.
Also, it would be interesting to investigate the reason for the lack of generalizability of ITI, since
(13) show that the linear probes do seem to generalize well across datasets. However, here one must
keep in mind that (13) use much simpler datasets than TruthfulQA.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
In this thesis, I have covered a new method that allows for training deep learning models using
less memory, by eagerly applying the gradients during the backward pass.
I found that this can
substantially decrease the memory footprint during training, while not adding much computational
overhead to larger models. I have also investigated the internal representation of truth in large language
models. Here I found some evidence supporting the claim that large language models may generate
factually incorrect information, while its internal states are ”aware” that the generated information is
false, on simple datasets. However, the results are mixed when considering more challenging datasets,
since I did not see clear evidence for the datasets used in (13), yet I saw that ITI improved some
models on TruthfulQA, which is arguably not a simple dataset. Therefore, more investigation would
be appropriate. I also did not see any evidence suggesting that we can substantially improve truth
detection using non-linear probes compared to linear ones. I managed to reproduce the results from
(1) across a variety of models. Here it seems that the method does not equally affect all models. On 4
out of 5 instruct models, I did not see any improvement from ITI, potentially due to poorly adjusted
hyperparameters for the method.
However, it is unclear if the instruct models would necessarily
require different parameters than their pre-trained counterparts, where I mostly saw evidence of an
improvement. Across the board, ITI does not seem to generalize well to out-of-distribution datasets,
which is a major downside to using the method in practice.
