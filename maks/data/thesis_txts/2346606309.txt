Abstract 
 
Staphylococcus aureus is one of the pathogens which cause most nosocomial infections globally. 
Since it has a comprehensive ability to survive antibiotic treatment and host immune response it is a 
huge problem for the society. S. aureus possesses several virulence factors, and the biofilm 
formation, which especially is related to chronical infections, results in organisms that have a high 
tolerance to antibiotics and the host immune response. When knee prosthesis surgeries are 
performed 1-2% of the patients end up with an osteomyelitis infection despite use of highly sterile 
methods, and the only successful treatment is an expensive replacement of the prosthesis, since the 
tolerance for antibiotics is too high. In this project, research of the chronic bone infection 
osteomyelitis, caused by S. aureus is examined by an in vivo rat experiment. During a surgery, a 
GFP-tagged S. aureus MN8, CRJS1 was injected into the medullary channel of the left rat knee. 
Afterwards, a rat-sized knee prosthesis was inserted and the infection was allowed to develop for 
two weeks. The prostheses and samples from rat tissue and bone were examined by confocal laser 
scanning microscopy and by isolating bacteria.  
The results of the rat prosthesis research showed that it was not possible to identify the GFP-tagged 
S. aureus strain by confocal laser scanning microscopy, since there was too much auto-fluorescence 
from eukaryotic blood and tissue residues from the rat.  Furthermore, several of the isolations of 
bacteria from the samples showed that the bacteria were not fluorescent; however, they were all S. 
aureus. Since much of the work was experimental trials, the conclusion was that the methods had to 
be improved before the results could be trustworthy. The suggestions for improvement included to 
improve the fluorescent tagged strain, to use other methods for confocal microscopy, and to store 
the samples differently. Furthermore it was suggested to focus more on isolating bacteria and 
research the genotypic and phenotypic changes of the strain.  
Additionally, agar based screening and colony morphology of 58 selected mutants from the 
Nebraska JE2 Mutant library was performed in order to find mutations correlated with specific 
phenotypes, so that knowledge could be used to select clinical S. aureus strains which could be 
relevant to use in the rat experiment. The result of the JE2 mutant library research showed that nine 
mutants differ from the JE2 wildtype. From this result it was not possible to find a correlation 
between the screened phenotypes and biofilm formation. Furthermore six out of the nine mutations 
had in other studies showed similar results.

Resumé 
Staphylococcus aureus er en af de patogene bakterier, der er skyld i flest hospitalserhvervede 
infektioner. S. aureus besidder mange virulensfaktorer, hvoraf biofilmdannelse særligt er relateret 
til kroniske infektioner, da biofilm resulterer i større tolerance over for antibiotika og værtens 
immunforsvar. I 1-2 % af alle knæproteseoperationer udvikler patienten en osteomyelitisinfektion, 
som er en kronisk infektion i knoglen. Dette sker på trods af højtudviklede sterile forhold under 
operationen. Den eneste succesfulde behandling mod en proteserelateret osteomyelitisinfektion er 
meget dyr, da det er nødvendigt at fjerne protesen og indsætte en ny, fordi behandling med 
antibiotika ikke vil virke, når der er biofilm involveret.  
I dette projekt blev osteomyelitis, forårsaget af S. aureus, undersøgt via et in vivo rotteforsøg. 
Rotterne fik injiceret en GFP-mærket S. aureus, CRJS1, direkte ind i knoglen i det venstre knæ ved 
en operation. Derefter blev en knæprotese i rottestørrelse indsat, og infektionen kunne derefter 
udvikle sig i to uger. Proteserne, samt knogle og væv fra rotterne, blev derefter undersøgt ved 
konfokalmikroskopi og ved at isolere bakterierne. Resultaterne fra rotteforsøget viste, at det ikke 
var muligt at identificere de GFP-mærkede S. aureus med konfokalmikroskopet, da blod- og 
vævsrester fra rotten var autofluorescerende og dermed ikke kunne skelnes fra bakterierne. 
Derudover blev der ved isolering af bakterierne opdaget, at ingen af de isolerede bakterier var 
fluorescerende. Alle isolaterne var dog identificeret som værende S. aureus. Da de fleste af 
eksperimenterne bestod af afprøvninger af ukendte metoder, var den vigtigste konklusion, at der var 
flere trin i rottemodellen og den efterfølgende udforskning, skulle forbedres i procedurerne. Forslag 
til forbedringer inkluderede forbedring af den GFP-mærkede stamme, benyttelse af andre metoder 
til konfokalmikroskopi, og anden opbevaring af prøverne. Derudover blev det foreslået at fokusere 
mere på at isolere bakterier fra rotterne og udforske de genotypiske og fænotypiske ændringer af 
stammen.  
Derudover blev der lavet agarpladebaseret screening og kolonimorfologi på 58 udvalgte mutanter 
fra Nebraska JE2 mutantbiblioteket for at finde mutanter, der havde specifik sammenhæng med 
bestemte fænotyper, så den viden kunne bruges til at udvælge relevante kliniske stammer, der kunne 
bruges i rottemodellen. Resultaterne af undersøgelsen med JE2 mutant biblioteket viste at ni 
mutanter afveg fra JE2 vildtypen. Ud fra dette resultat var det dog ikke muligt at finde en 
sammenhæng mellem biofilmdannelse og de undersøgte fænotyper. Derudover havde syv af ud af 
de ni mutationer tidligere i andre studier haft lignende resultater.

1. Introduction  
 
The gram positive bacterium Staphylococcus aureus (S. aureus) is a predominant pathogen that 
causes a wide range of severe human infectious diseases, including skin infections, food poisoning, 
toxic shock syndrome, pneumonia, brain abscesses and endocarditis [Bose et al., 2013]. Many of 
the diseases occur frequently since S. aureus has its normal habitat in the nasal tract, and are 
therefore also easily dispersing. By damage the bacteria can gain access into the body and start an 
infection. One of the medical challenges is to control and decrease the rate of the nosocomial S. 
aureus chronical bone infection, osteomyelitis, in relation to prosthesis surgeries. Despite highly 
sterile methods for inserting prostheses, osteomyelitis caused by S. aureus during implant surgeries 
is the reason for complications in 1-2% of the cases [Lew & Waldvagel, 2004]. Osteomyelitis 
causes physical trouble and pain for the patient, and there is a risk of the infection to spread and 
become a lethal blood infection [Montanaro et al., 2011].  
Moreover S. aureus has numerous virulence factors which are strongly regulated by genetic factors, 
however, biofilm formation is one of the most important virulence factors of pathogenesis of 
implant-associated chronic infections [Søe et al., 2013], since biofilm formation gives S. aureus 
several advantages in protecting the S. aureus cells from antibiotics and host immune response 
[Archer et al. 2011]. To date, there is no other successful treatment for chronic biofilm infections in 
prostheses than surgery and replacement of the prosthetic joint [Archer et al., 2011]. However, this 
treatment is very expensive, since it requires multiple expertises in infectious diseases, vascular 
surgery, orthopedic surgery and/or plastic surgery [Lew & Waldvagel, 2004]. 
In order to develop therapeutic strategies for prevention and treatment of infections caused by S. 
aureus, it is important to understand the pathogenesis of the bacteria and have a deep understanding 
of the biological an physical feature the biofilm possesses [Bjarnsholt et al., 2013, Lister & 
Horswill, 2014, and Luong & Lee, 2007]. Several studies have been done, and much knowledge 
about the virulence system and mechanisms in S. aureus has already been achieved in laboratory 
research. However, knowledge of clinical strains of S. aureus and their behavior in vivo, and 
especially in the human host, is still highly relevant to obtain. Furthermore, the existing studies 
mainly cover the mechanisms of S. aureus in acute infections, and research of chronic infections is 
still missing [Bjarnsholt et al., 2013].

To investigate S. aureus in vivo, Søe et al., 2013 has developed a rat prosthesis model, which is 
suggested to simulate human osteomyelitis infections more than other animal prosthesis models, 
since a specially designed rat-sized knee prosthesis is included. The rats are injected with a S. 
aureus MN8 strain, and a rat-sized knee prosthesis is inserted by surgery. In order to obtain 
additional information from this method, in a previous project by the author of this report, an 
insertion of a fluorescent protein expressing into the S. aureus MN8 strain’s chromosome by 
bacteriophage mediated transduction. This will make it possible to visually identify the injected 
bacteria, to detect the genotypic and phenotypic changes related to the infection lifestyle. 
Furthermore, it would be possible to observe the biofilm formation in a confocal laser scanning 
microscope, and thereby get information about the location and structure of the biofilm.  
The aim of this project is to investigate clinical Staphylococcus strains and their ability to form 
biofilm in vivo during implant infections in rats. This includes in vivo research of a GFP-tagged 
clinical S. aureus MN8 during a rat knee prosthesis surgery. Furthermore, screening of a selected 
number of up- and down-regulated mutants from the S. aureus JE2 mutant library will be used to 
find a correlation between mutations and their infective abilities in order to detect other clinical 
strains, which could be relevant to use in the rat model. 
1.1 Rat model 
An experimental in vivo research of osteomyelitis has been developed by MD. Niels Søe [Søe et al., 
2013]. Several research sessions have shown that the following procedure gave the best simulation 
of a human osteomyelitis infection. A dose of 103 CFU/rat is injected in the medullary channel of 
the bone in the rats; half of the dose in femur and half in tibia. Doses of 102-105 have also been 
tested, however, the conclusion was that the rats would clear the infection too fast if the dose was 
102 and the infection would be too aggressive and expel the prosthesis after the inoculation for 
doses >104 . The specially designed rat sized knee prosthesis is afterwards inserted, the metal part in 
femur and the plastic part in tibia. The following 14 days the infection will have opportunity to 
develop in the rat. At day 7 and day 14, X-rays will be performed. At day 14 the prostheses are 
taken out and several tissue samples are taken for isolation of bacteria and determining the 
microbiological evolution. Additionally, bone and soft-tissue samples are collected to perform 
histology of the infected tissue [Søe et.al, 2013], in figure 1 a set of the rat sized prosthesis is 
shown, the metal part fits in femur and the plastic part in tibia.

1.2 Osteomyelitis  
Since osteomyelitis infections is the focus of the rat surgery in this project, some important details 
of osteomyelitis is explained in this section.  
As a result of the many virulence factors and biofilm formation factors S. aureus possesses, chronic 
nosocomial infections is a huge problem for the society. The severe chronic bone infection, 
osteomyelitis, is a major issue in relation to prosthetic joint infection. The main organism to cause 
osteomyelitis is S. aureus, although osteomyelitis can also be caused by several other bacteria than 
S. aureus, and it can also appear in other situations than prosthesis surgeries. When the bone is 
infected, the infection includes several parts, such as the soft tissue, periosteum, cortex and bone 
marrow. Osteomyelitis is only in 20% of the cases treatable with antibiotics [Bengtson S, 1993]. As 
previously mentioned the only successful treatment is to remove the infected area, including 
replacement of the prosthesis.  This treatment requires a multidisciplinary tactic and is therefore 
also very expensive. Experts in plastic surgery, orthopedic surgery, vascular surgery and infection 
diseases have to be involved, for it to be a safe treatment [Lew & Waldvagel, 2004]. The cost for a 
replacement of the prosthesis when the knee is infected is US$ 50,000-62,100. In contrast a 
prosthesis surgery of a non-infected knee is US$ 8,600-15,000 [Bengtson S, 1993; Mariani et al., 
1998]

1.3 Staphylococcus aureus 
The opportunistic pathogen S. aureus is a gram positive bacterium, which causes numerous chronic 
and acute infections in both humans and a number of animal species [Kluytmans et al., 1997; Sia 
and Berbari, 2006]. S. aureus can cause three types of infections; firstly, toxinosis, which includes 
scalded skin syndrome, food poisoning and toxic shock syndrome, secondly superficial lesions such 
as wound infections, and thirdly, systemic and life-threatening diseases, including endocarditis, 
pneumonia, meningitis, brain abscesses and osteomyelitis [Bien et al., 2011; Shaw et al., 2004; 
Weese, 2010]. Additionally, S. aureus can colonize humans without causing disease. Up to 20% of 
all humans carry the S. aureus in their commensal flora, primarily in the nasal tract [Langley et al., 
2003; Ariyanti et al., 2011; Kluytmans et al., 1997]. Additionally, a study has suggested that 
humans who are colonized by S. aureus have a higher risk of developing a disease caused by S. 
aureus, than humans that have never been colonized, however they also have an important role for 
S. aureus spreading among individuals [Archer et al., 2011; Bien et al., 2011]. S. aureus is the 
common staphylococcus causing infections [Luong and Lee, 2007], which can be the cause of the 
immense variety of virulence factors, including production of antigens, enzymes, toxins and the 
ability to form biofilm [Otto et al., 2014]. Biofilm forming pathogens are highly tolerant to 
antibiotics and cause chronic infections, in contrast to planktonic growing cells, which cause acute 
infections. Acute infections can often be treated with antibiotics [Bjarnsholt et al., 2013] whereas 
the chronic biofilm containing infection can only successfully be treated by surgically removing the 
infected area [Lew & Waldvagel, 2004]. 
One of the most infective strains of S. aureus is the Methicillin resistant Staphylococcus aureus 
(MRSA), which is found in up to 64% of the screened isolates from colonized individuals in US 
[Pang et al. 2010]. 
Also a new strain of Vancomycin resistant Staphylococcus aureus (VRSA) has been discovered. 
This strain is resistant to all known antibiotics [Langley et al. 2003]. Although, it is still a rare 
strain, it is giving problems of treating VRSA infections due to the antibiotic resistance.  
S. aureus possesses numerous virulence factors which is one of the reasons that it is so successful. 
The relevant virulence factors for the rat research and the mutant library research will be explained 
in the following section.

1.3.1 Virulence of Staphylococcus aureus 
The virulence of S. aureus is controlled by several complex regulatory systems, which result in the 
production of a variety of virulence factors, including toxins, enzymes, surface-bound proteins and 
biofilm [Otto et al., 2014; Fay et al., 2013; Bose et al., 2013]. Not all of the systems are fully 
understood; moreover the knowledge of the regulatory system is very important in order to develop 
prevention of and treatment for S. aureus infections.  
Biofilm formation is one of the most important virulence factors in relation to chronic infections 
such as osteomyelitis, since biofilm protects the cells from the host immune response and treatment 
with antibiotics [Søe et al., 2013].  
All the virulence factors are a result of complex gene regulation, strongly influenced by 
environmental changes, such as oxygen, osmolarity and pH, which are partially changed by the 
infection itself and characterize the different stages of infection. This includes expression of 
adherence factors in the early stage, so the bacteria can attach to the host surface, and production of 
toxins in the late stage of infection. The gene expression is directly regulated by regulatory loci, 
including staphylococcal accessory regulator (sarA) and accessory gene regulator (agr) [Bien et al. 
2011].  
1.3.2 Staphylococcus aureus biofilm formation 
Biofilm is a matrix of extracellular polymeric substance that attaches bacterial cells to each other, a 
substratum or a surface, creating a microbial derived sessile community. Layers of biofilm with 
diverse access to nutrients and oxygen results in a high variety of phenotypic properties. 
Furthermore, the extracellular matrix is able to up concentrate the nutrients by sequestering them 
from the surrounding environment or act like a diffusion barrier to antibiotics and slow down the 
consummation of these, which in some cases results in deactivation of the antimicrobial factors 
before reaching the inner layer of the cells [Archer et al. 2011]. In figure 3, the process of S. aureus 
biofilm formation is illustrated.  When the skin is damaged the S. aureus can gain access into the 
body and adherence factors allow it to attach to the host matrix. When the cells are attached the 
early biofilm formation begins, and increases when the amount of cells increases by cell division. 
The auto inducing peptide (AIP) which is known to be the quorum sensing compound is secreted 
and is taken up by other cells in the biofilm. AIP is inducing the upregulation of SarA expression 
leading to synthesis of numerous virulence factors. AIP reaches the threshold level which is leading

Biofilms have the ability to make an environment where the cells are protected from host defense 
and antibiotics, though, channels help nutrients enter the biofilm to reach the cells in the inner 
layers. The structure, form and size of the biofilm depend on the surrounding environment [Archer 
et al., 2011]. Additionally, S. aureus is often seen as aggregates where the cells are only attached to 
each other and not to a surface [Haaber et al. 2012].

S. aureus biofilms are highly tolerant to antibiotics. One of the reasons for this is that the cells in a 
biofilm are capable of evading stress caused by the host organism or antibiotics. The antimicrobial 
factors result in a low metabolic activity and downregulate the division of the cells of up to 1% of 
the population. These slow growing cells, called persister cells, are highly tolerant to the introduced 
environmental stress and can result in multi-drug tolerance. Persister cells are not mutated cells and 
can later go out of this downregulated state and reestablish the infection, however mutations also 
occurs in biofilms[Archer et al. 2011].  
The compound of B-1,6-linked N-acetylglucosamine called the Polysaccharide intercellular antigen 
(PIA) is important for biofilm formation in S. aureus since the biofilm matrix contains large 
amounts of PIA. PIA is synthesized by Ica proteins which are encoded by icaABDC locus 
[Oliverira & Cunha, 2008].  The synthesis of PIA is upregulated under anaerobic conditions by 
SrrAB. [Wu, Wang & Tao, 2011; Archer et al., 2011] 
1.3.3 Toxins 
A large variety of toxins are produced by S. aureus, including membrane-damaging toxins. The 
membrane-damaging toxins, hemolysins, which affect the erythrocytes, are also a very important 
virulence factor related to S. aureus infections and especially food poisoning [Ariyanti et al., 2011, 
Otto et al. 2014]. Hemolysin exists in different variations; alpha hemolysin, beta hemolysin, delta 
hemolysin and gamma hemolysin. Alpha hemolysin does not only have a hemolytic effect, but also 
a dermonecrotic and a neurotoxic effect [Otto et al., 2014; Ariyanti et al., 2011]. Beta hemolysin 
contains sphingomyelinase that breaks down sphingomyelin in the cell membrane and surrounding 
the nerve cell axons in humans and animals.  However, these are more active against erythrocytes of 
sheep and bovines [Ariyanti et al., 2011]. The hemolysins are receptor-mediated, except for delta-
hemolysin, a member of the phenol-soluble modulins (PSMs), which attaches directly at the 
membrane and form short living pores [Otto et al., 2014]. 
Also toxins attacking the leukocytes are produced by S. aureus. These are called leukotoxins [Otto 
et al., 2014]; however, this will not be specified in this report. 
1.3.4 Extracellular proteases 
Other important virulence factors are the four extracellular proteases produced by S. aureus: 
staphopain (Scp), cystein protease (SspB), staphylococcal serine protease (V8 protease, SspA) and 
metalloprotease aureolysin, (Aur). These four proteases are able to degrade numerous important

heavy chain [Otto et al., 2014; Karlsson et al., 2002]. Aur cleaves several important host proteins 
and additionally it inactivates PSMs, which have a major influence on the development of 
osteomyelitis [Otto et al., 2014]. Furthermore, it has been suggested that the proteases change S. 
aureus from an adhesive to an invasive phenotype by degrading cell surface proteins, including 
fibronectin binding protein and protein A [Karlsson et al., 2002]. 
1.3.5 Staphyloxathin, S. aureus pigmentation 
The golden pigment staphyloxanthin which is characteristic for many S. aureus strains is an 
important virulence factor, since it protects the bacteria from oxidative stress caused by immune 
cells. The five genes, crtO, crtP, crtQ, crtM and crtN, in the crt operon encode the biosynthetic 
enzymes for staphyloxanthin. However it has also been shown that other genes have an effect on the 
biosynthetic pathway for staphyloxanthin, including argA, argB, argC and sarA  [Fay et al., 2013; 
Clauditz, 2006].  
1.3.6 Accessory gene regulator  
Agr system is the regulator of many of the virulence factors in S. aureus biofilm formation as 
previously explained but also toxins such as hemolysins and the previously mentioned proteases 
and other host damaging enzymes [Junecko et al., 2012; Pang et al., 2010]. The promoters of the 
agr locus, P2 and P3, control the RNAII and RNAIII transcripts. RNAII codes for AgrB, AgrD, 
AgrC and AgrA proteins, while RNAIII is the main effector for the agr system and is responsible 
for the upregulation of secreted virulence factors and downregulation of cell surface proteins [Pang 
et al., 2010].  
1.3.7 Motility of S. aureus 
S. aureus is well known as a non-motile bacteria, however is has recently been reported by Pollitt et 
al., (2015), that S. aureus is in fact able to move by an active motility forming `comet tails´ under 
certain conditions. This form of motility is most in comparison with gliding motility, which is 
defined as a continuous smooth movement of bacteria in groups or unaided. The force driving the 
bacteria glide, is different depending on the organism involved, however it can be extrusion of 
slime, membrane protrusion, cell twisting, attachment mechanisms of a surface, including the type 
IV pili mechanism. Politt et al., (2015) reported that there was none of the known gliding motility 
mechanisms observed, and that it is not yet known how the motility mechanism is working,

2.2.1 Restriction modifications 
Unfortunately, chromosomal manipulation of S. aureus is complicated, since the majority of S. 
aureus strains possess strong restriction barrier, which prevents foreign DNA to make genetic 
changes when penetrate. Four restriction-modifications (RM) are identified, however S. aureus only 
possesses three of them, type I, type II and type IV [Monk & Foster 2012]. 
TypeI encodes a Host Specificity of DNA (hsd) Specificity (S) protein, as well as a modification 
(M) protein and a restriction (R) endonuclease. These constitute the complex HsdM2HsdS, which is 
responsible for methylation of DNA by recognizing a specific DNA sequence. The HsdR can 
protect the methylated DNA from being cleaved [Jones et al., 2015 and Monk & Foster 2012]. 
Type II is a rare type of RM in S. aureus. It encodes DNA-methylases and restriction endonuclease 
enzymes (REases). These are directly recognizing a specific DNA sequence and digest it [Jones et 
al., 2015].  
Type IV is the most simple of the restriction barriers, and furthermore it reprecents the major part of 
the RM in S. aureus. This type is a single protein, which are able to sense the methylation status and 
inhibits the foreign DNA. [Jones et al., 2015 and Monk & Foster 2012].

2.2.3 Bacteriophage mediated transduction 
As earlier mentioned RN4220 can obtain DNA from E.coli. This can be done by electroporation, 
which is a very simple procedure, where the principle is to add electricity to the cells, for them to 
form pores and take up the DNA. The RN4220, with the inserted DNA, can now be used as a donor 
for the bacteriophage mediated transduction. The steps in a transduction is seen in figure 7. A 
proper bacteriophage, used as a single copy integration vector, is attaching to the donor cell. The 
enzymes of the bacteriophage breaks down the bacterial DNA and the bacteriophage is replicated 
containing pieces of the bacterial DNA. The bacteriophages lyses the donor cell, and can thereby 
transfer the donor DNA to new cells. The bacteriophages are attached to the recipient strain and 
injects the DNA, which are integrated by homologous recombination into the chromosome of the 
recipient strain.

containing the donor DNA, thereafter the donor cell is lysed and the phages are carrying the donor DNA to the recipient strain, and 
injects it. The DNA is inserted into the chromosome by homologues recombination.

3.8 Manipulation of S. aureus 
The method used for manipulation of S. aureus is bacteriophage mediated transduction. Before the 
transduction could be performed, the phage lysates was prepared.  
3.8.1 Preparing phage lysate 
Phage stocks of the two bacteriophages φ11 and φ80α were diluted in 0.9%NaCL and 10 mM CaCl2 
to the concentration 10-1 – 10-4  and 100µL of diluted phages was added to 100µL ON cultures of 
the donor strains supplemented with 15µL of 100mM CaCL2. The mix was incubated at room 
temperature for five minutes in order to allow adsorption of phages to bacteria.  
Next, 45ºC  heated molten TSB top ager with 0.5 mM CaCl2 was added to the phage-bacteria 
solution and poured onto TSA+CaCl2 plates and incubated at 37ºC ON.  
The plates where almost all cells were lysed was selected and phage was collected with and isolated 
from the top agar by centrifugation at 5000 G followed by sterile filtering the supernatants with 
0.45µm filters.   
To determine the concentration of phage for dilution the above steps except for the collecting step 
were repeated with the recipient strains, RN4220 and the collected phage from last step. The 
efficiency of plating (EOP) of the phages on recipient strains was compared to the EOP of RN4420 
and thereby the effective multiplicity of infection (MOI) of 0.1 could be calculated. 
3.8.2 Transduction 
ON cultures of the recipient were added to the tittered phages to achieve a MOI of 0.1. TSB and 0.5 
mM CaCl2 was added up to a final volume of 2.0mL , and the infection of the recipient strain was 
allowed by incubating the solution for 20 min. at 37ºC with 200 rpm 
Ice-cold sodium citrate was added to the solutions to avoid the cells being lysed by the phages. 
Subsequently, the solutions were centrifuged and the pellet was re-suspended in sodium citrate. The 
collected cells were plated as non-diluted and 10-fold diluted on TSA plates with 2mM sodium 
citrate and tet 2ug/ml. 
The plates were incubated 1-2 days at 37ºC. Next the Fluorescence was verified by fluorescent 
microscopy.

4. Results 
 
The purpose of this study was to investigate clinical Staphylococcus aureus strains in vivo, during 
implant infections in rats. Furthermore to explore the potential of other clinical strains to use in the 
rat model, screening of selected mutants from the S. aureus NTML.  
In this section the details of the research and the results will be precented.  
4.1 Evaluation of the GFP-tagged S. aureus, CRJS1, for in vivo rat model.  
Prior to the rat surgery the strain used, CRJS1, had to verified as an appropriate strain for the in vivo 
research for several reasons. Firstly, to be able to compare the result to the research of the wildtype 
strain S. aureus MN8. Secondly, the reason why the genes were inserted into the chromosome, was 
that it had earlier been shown that the genes were lost in vivo, if the genes were on a high copy 
plasmid instead [Jacobsen, 2015]. For that reason it was important to verify that the strain with 
chromosomal integration of the genes was stable as it was theoretical presumed. Thirdly, when 
making experiments with animals it is important to relate to the ethics of the subject. It is unethical 
to expose the animals to disease and mortality if the preparatory work is not done well to secure that 
the results can be used.  
Thus, the strain was tested in relation to stability of the inserted genes, fluorescence and tet 
resistance. Furthermore, growth rate was compared to the wildtype S. aureus MN8 (IMG168). 
Additionally, it was verified if the gfp gene was inserted into the expected place of the chromosome 
by sequencing the attachment sides of the chromosome. Also, the plasmid which was used as donor 
for the gfp gene was sequenced in order to suggest potential improvement of the strain.

5. Discussion 
 
In this section, the results obtained from this study will be discussed. 
The aim of this project was to investigate clinical Staphylococcus aureus strains and their ability to 
form biofilm in vivo during implant infections in rats. This included in vivo research of a GFP-
tagged clinical S. aureus MN8 during a rat knee prosthesis surgery. Furthermore, screening of a 
selected number of up- and down-regulated mutants from the S. aureus JE2 mutant library were 
used to find a correlation of mutations in clinical strains in order to use other relevant clinical 
strains for the rat model.  
The research contained two parts; the rat prosthesis in vivo research and the mutant library research 
of selected mutants from the NTML.  
5.1 In vivo rat prosthesis model 
From an earlier study [Shadegg, 2015] the GFP-tagged S. aureus MN8, CRJS1, was created. This 
strain was in the present study used for injection into the medullary channel of the left knee of the 
rat, before inserting the prostheses. Before the rat surgery CRJS1 was tested for stability of the 
inserted genes; the fluorescent signal and tet resistance. The results showed that the strain was 
stable and had the same growth rate as the wildtype S. aureus MN8 (IMG168) See figure 13. 
Therefore the strain could be used in the in vivo research.  
Unfortunately, it was discovered after the surgery, by laboratory assistant Ulla Johansen, that the 
control S. aureus MN8 (RH), which according to the information available, should be the exact 
same strain as IMG168, grew differently than IMG168 see figure 19. Furthermore, there were 
clearly differences in the color. S. aureus MN8 (RH) was white and S. aureus MN8 (IMG168) 
pigmented, See figure 18. Therefore it was concluded that S. aureus MN8 (RH) and (IMG168) was 
different strains. This was important information, since S. aureus MN8 (RH) no longer was a 
reliable control strain for the research of CRJS1.  
5.1.1 Rat sample – isolation of bacteria 
After the rat surgery, the infection was allowed to develop for 14 days. Seven and 14 days after the 
surgery, X-rays were taken of the knee and after the 14 days the prosthesis was removed and a 
small piece of tissue and bone from the rat knee was taken and stored at -20ºC in 50% glycerol.

Subsequently, the samples of the prosthesis and tissue were used for analyses by confocal laser 
scanning microscopy and isolation of bacteria from the rat.  
Bacteria were successfully isolated from both tissue and prostheses, see table 6. However, the 
CFU/ml was low for most of the samples, but that could be either because of the fact that the 
infection was week, as it is also seen at the X-rays and in the journals, see table 5 or many of the 
cells could have died as a consequence of changing the environmental conditions, from the rats 
anaerobic environment where the cells lived in biofilm communities, to the aerobic condition where 
they had to survive living as planktonic cells.  
Since the control sample (prosthesis from rat 190), which had been included in a microtiter 
experiment where CRJS1 was allowed to grow biofilm on the prosthesis, had one of the lowest 
CFU/ml, it might also indicate that not all the bacteria were isolated from the samples.  
Moreover, more bacteria were isolated from samples and prosthesis from the control rats 183 and 
184, than the samples infected with CRJS1. All the isolated bacteria were checked for GFP signal 
on the fluorescence microscope, and none of the samples from rats injected with CRJS1 had a 
fluorescent signal. Though, it was tested by MALDI-TOF biotyping which bacteria the isolations 
contained. All the tested isolations were S. aureus, see Appendix S.  
It was assumed that the GFP-tag would be stable in vivo as well as it was shown in vitro when the 
genes were inserted into the chromosome, however there could be a risk that the assumption was 
not true. On the contrary it would be more probable if the solution of bacteria or the rat wound was 
contaminated before the injection. Since it was shown earlier that the S. aureus MN8 (IMG168), 
which was used as wildtype for construction of CRJS1, was poor growing, it would also be 
probable that a contamination of another S. aureus could outmatch CRJS1 [Hibbing et. Al., 2010] 
Generally, from the X-rays, records and numbers of isolated bacteria in the earlier rat studies by 
Niels Søe, it was shown that both the control strain and CRJS1 only result in moderate infections 
compared to the earlier rat experiments where the infections developed to be more severe see 
Appendix V. It is not proved why the infection was less severe than in earlier rat experiments, 
however, some suggestions could be that there was a dilution error or, if there in fact was 
contaminations before the injection, it would be possible that the bacteria had used energy to oppose 
the other strain, which could affect the virulence against the host.

5.1.2 Rat sample - Confocal laser scanning microscopy 
The prostheses were observed by confocal laser scanning microscopy, however, it was not possible 
to distinguish between GFP-tagged S. aureus and eukaryotic blood and tissue residues on the 
implants because of autofluorescence in the green spectrum see figure 17. In a study, Leavesley, 
(2010), they show that it is possible to solve this problem by perform a hyper spectral assay, with 
spectral microscopy. By this method it is possible to detect GFP-tagged cells, even though the 
autofluorescence from the tissue has a stronger signal. Furthermore Peptide Nucleic Acid 
Fluorescence in Situ Hybridization (PNA FISH) and DAPI is used to solve the problem [Kragh et. 
Al., 2014]. In this project, DAPI was used on prosthesis from rat 186, however it did not work, 
which might have been because there was no fluorescent bacteria, which also the fluorescence 
microscopy of the isolated bacteria from the tissue sample from rat 186 confirmed.  
Additionally, it was planned to observe the tissue samples in confocal laser scanning microscope, 
however, apparently the tissue samples were ruined by the choice of storage conditions. According 
to Thomas Bjarnsholt the tissue samples could have been used if they had been added to formalin 
instead of 50% glycerol and if it was snap-freezed and stores at - 80ºC rather than slow cooling to - 
20ºC. 
5.1.3 Improvement of the GFP-tagged strain 
The sequencing of the plasmid sPK104 containing the fluorescent gene, went wrong, and therefore 
it is not possible to go into details about how to improve it, since it cannot be confirmed if the 
plasmid are compounded as expected.  
Though, some suggestions of what could be relevant to improve can be discussed. First of all the 
fluorescent tag could be changed so the problem with the autofluorescence would be diminished. In 
the previous project where CRJS1 was created, two other strains were subsequently created. Both 
strains had m-cherry inserted at the chromosome by phage mediated transduction. Unfortunately, 
the fluorescent signal was not strong enough to be seen at single cell level by fluorescence 
microscope, and therefore these strains were not used in the in vivo research. If it is possible to 
create m-cherry tagged S. aureus MN8 with a strong fluorescent signal, the strain could be used in

5.1.4 Choice of animals for the prosthesis model 
Finally the rats is a suitable animal to use in this project for now, but later, when the optimal 
method has been developed, it might be an idea to use other animals, which have an immune 
response more similar to the human immune response. Generally larger animals are more like 
humans, but also much more expensive to use [Byrom et. Al, 2010]. Rats immune response is very 
efficient, and are more likely to clear the infection, which obstruct the infection to be observed for 
more than few weeks [Søe et. Al, 2013].  
5.2 Mutant library 
The other part of the project was to screen 58 selected mutants from the NTML (USA300), for 
hemolysin production, protease production by plating assays. Additionally colony morphology and 
motility assay were performed. This was done to examine a correlation between mutations and 
phenotypes in order to be able to select other clinical strains which could be relevant to explore in 
the rat model.  
The motility assay was only performed on the USA300 JE2 wildtype, since there was no signs of 
motility on the plates, whereas the study [Pollitt et. Al, 2015] did manage to make a USA300 isolate 
motile. Since I only had limited time, it was only tried once, and it is not known what went wrong, 
but since S. aureus is generally known as non-motile, it may generally be difficult to create the right 
conditions for it to be motile. Therefore it would be possible if a small environmental factor, could 
have had an influence on the motility. However, since they both were a USA300 it was expected 
that the JE2 Wildtype would be able to be motile.

5.2.1 Protease, hemolysin and pigmentation 
58 mutants were tested for Protease and hemolysin production by plating assays on blood agar 
plates and casein (skimmed milk) plates.  Additionally colony morphology was investigated, 
However, changes in phenotype were only seen when investigating pigmentation and size. Since it 
was not clear if the changes in size was due to that the colonies was located close together or 
because of the mutations, it was chosen, not to have the size changes in focus in this report.  
Nine mutants showed changed phenotypes compared to the wildtype JE2, see table 8. Five of the 
nine mutants had shown an up-regulation in biofilm formation and four a down-regulation in 
biofilm formation in Signe Trøst Nielsens earlier studies. Based on that, it would not be significant 
to make assumptions of, if there is a correlation between biofilm formation and the other 
phenotypes.  
 Another study [Fay et. Al, 2013] had also tested the mutants in the mutant library for hemolysin 
production, protease production and pigmentation. The results of that study are seen in the 
supplemented material S1 in Fay et. Al, (2013). Not all of the strains are described in that results, 
and some of the mutants did also give other results than the results obtained in this study. This 
could be because they use other medium for the research or other growth conditions. They are using 
rabbit blood agar for both hemolysin and pigmentation assay, and in this study there was used horse 
blood agar for hemolysin and LB agar for pigmentation. The mutants that showed to have the same 
phenotypes as it was studied in the present study was SAUSA300_0963 (qoxA), SAUSA300_1989 
(agrB), SAUSA300_0752 (clpP) and SAUSA300_2060 (atpA).  
However these results are not directly comparable, since in Fay et. Al, (2013) the results were based 
on the magnitude of the changes, and in the present study the results are based on the visible 
changes from the Wildtype. 
5.2.2 Phenotypic changes in the mutants 
Nine mutants showed phenotypic changes in one or several of the phenotypes hemolysin 
production, protease production or pigmentation. In this section the mutations and the phenotypic 
changes will be discussed in relation to other studies.  
SAUSA300_0963, with a mutation in the gene qoxA and qoxC (Quinol oxidase, subunit II) showed 
downregulation in protease production and upregulation in pigmentation. It has previously been 
shown that mutations in qoxB leads to increased pigmentation [Lan et. Al, 2010]. The same study

also showed that many of the genes that affected the pigmentation was also affecting other 
virulence factors such as Protease production and hemolysin production, since the mutations was 
related to the Accessory gene regulator (agr) system, which is leading to expression of several of 
the virulence factors. Supplementary information of the agr system is found in Appendix W.  
SAUSA300_1989 with a mutation in argB, (Accessory gene regulator protein B), was 
downregulated in both protease and hemolysin production. Arg is necessary for AIPs, which is S. 
aureus quorum sensing compound, to be synthesized. This compound is a part of the regulatory 
system of almost all the virulence factors including proteases and hemolysis, therefore it was 
expected that a mutation in agrB would downregulate both protease and hemolysin production 
[Junecko et al. 2012, Pang et al., 2010]. 
SAUSA300_1641 was mutated in (gltA Citrate synthase II), was more pigmented than the wildtype. 
In [Ding et al, 2014] it is shown that the staphyloxanthin pathway shares acetyl CoA with the citrate 
pathway. According to that fact it make sense that a mutation in Citrate synthase II will result in 
more acetyl CoA to staphyloxanthin synthese if acetyl CoA is the constraining factor in this 
pathway.  
SAUSA300_0752 clpP (ATP-dependent Clp protease proteolytic subunit) mutant was 
downregulated in both protease production and hemolysin production. According to Frees, 
Sørensen & Ingmer, (2005) Protease expression is directly regulated by clpP. And according to 
Hsieh, et al., (2008) hemolysin production is downregulated by Rot, during the exponential phase. 
In the end of the exponential phase RNAIII binds to rot by means of clpP and clpX and Rot is 
degraded. Subsequently the expression of hemolysin and proteases can begin.  
SAUSA300_2060 atpA (ATP synthase F1, alpha subunit) was downregulated in hemolysin 
production. Since the clpP, was ATP-dependent and it was a regulator for both hemolysins and 
proteases, it could have correlation to that, however no specific information was avaivable of the 
exact effect of atpA. However, ATP is necessary for many processes in a cell, so it could also be an 
effect of several synthesis in S. aureus, for example argC needs ATP to sense AIP which, as 
previously explained, is an important proteases and hemolysins should be produced [Bronner, 
Monteil & Prevost, 2004]

5.3 Further studies 
Since S. aureus is still a challenge, especially in chronic infection as osteomyelitis related to 
prosthesis surgeries, it will still make sense to accomplish the same study as it was requested in this 
study. Although it is necessary to make changes in the used strain and the procedure for the surgery 
and the handling of the samples, as it was previously discussed, the rat model has definitely 
potential, however the following improvement of the experimental process will be recommended.  
- 
Be completely sure the control strain is the same as the wildtype strain used for the 
fluorescent protein expressing S. aureus used for the in vivo research.  
- 
Make a test to know if the inoculated bacteria are correctly diluted right before injection. 
This can be done by measuring OD600 or plating and calculating CFU/ml. The advantage 
measure OD600 is that you get the answer immediately after measuring; however the 
advantage by plating is that you will be able to discover if there is contaminations in the 
culture.   
- 
The storage of the prostheses and the tissue samples should be by adding the samples to 
formalin and snap-freezing them at - 80ºC 
- 
Improving the Fluorescent tagged strain, by using another fluorescent color, another 
promoter which is down-regulated by the essential compounds for the virulence factors.  
- 
Use other methods to solve the problems of autofluorescence of blood and tissue from the 
rats, such as DAPI or FISH staining.   
- 
Focus more on isolation of the fluorescent tagged bacteria and make research of genotypic 
and phenotypic changes this strain could have obtained by the in-vivo life-style.   
- 
Consider using other animals to have a more human-like immune response affecting the 
infection and to be able to observe the infection for longer time.

6. Conclusion 
 
The purpose of this study was to investigate clinical GFP-tagged Staphylococcus aureus ability to 
form biofilm in vivo, during implant infections in rats. Additionally, screening of selected mutants 
from the S. aureus JE2 mutant library was performed to find a correlation between the mutations 
and phenotypic features that could be interesting to investigate in the in vivo rat model.   
The rats were infected with a GFP-tagged S. aureus MN8, CRJS1 and the prostheses and tissue 
samples were examined. During confocal microscopy it was concluded that it was not possible to 
distinguish the GFP-tagged S. aureus from the eukaryotic blood and tissue. Another conclusion was 
that the rat model has to be improved, since a putative contamination occurred and the fluorescent 
tagged S. aureus did not survived in the rats. Furthermore the storage method of the samples should 
be changed.  
Moreover selected mutants from the mutant library were tested for hemolysin production, protease 
production and pigmentation. Based on the results, it could not be concluded if there was a 
correlation between biofilm formation and the tested phenotypes. However, six out of the nine 
mutants with phenotypic changes, had in other studies showed similar phenotypic changes.

W. Accessory gene regulator  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
                
 
Agr regulatory system  The Accessory gene regulator (agr) system is the regulator of many of the 
virulence factors in S. aureus, biofilm formation as previously explained but also toxins such as 
hemolysins and the previously mentioned proteases and other host damaging enzymes [Junecko et al., 
2012, Pang et al., 2010]. The promoters of the agr locus, P2 and P3 control the RNAII and RNAIII 
transcripts. RNAII codes for AgrB, AgrD, AgrC and AgrA proteins, while RNAIII is the main 
effector for the agr system and is responsible for the upregulation of secreted virulence factors and 
downregulation of cell surface proteins [Pang et al., 2010].  
AgrB is a transmembrane protein, which processes the AgrD, precursor for AIP before secretion. The 
amount of AIPs increasing and eventually the threshold level of AIP is reached and AIPs binds to 
AgrC histidine kinase. The AgrC is activated and the regulator Agr is phosphylated. The result in 
transcription of P2 and P3. The transcription of P2 and P3 result in expression of both the agr genes 
and RNAIII which is regulator of expression of several virulence factors [Pang et al., 2010].
