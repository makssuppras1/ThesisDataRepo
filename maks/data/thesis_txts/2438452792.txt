Analysis of Biological Samples with Surface Enhanced Raman Spectroscopy (SERS) 
I 
Abstract 
This thesis test, explains and produces self-organized hybrid substrate for Surface Enhanced 
Raman Spectroscopy. Optimization steps of the synthesis of a Fe3O4|ZnO@Ag composite is in-
vestigated statistically and illustrated graphically. Using p-aminothiophenol as a probe molecule 
the enhancing properties are tested for the Fe3O4|ZnO@Ag composite. The particles are collected 
on a nail by use of a magnet. Surface homogeneity on the nail is optimized, while assuring high 
enhancing effects. Successful attachment of the p-aminothiophenol is achieved and 4,4’-dimer-
captoazobenzene was formed. Reproducibility of the optimized substrate was 15% for spot-to-
spot and 15% for batch-to-batch. To fully understand the mechanisms an in-depth literature study 
was performed on Surface Enhanced Raman Spectroscopy placing focus on its applications on 
biological samples.

4. Introduction 
Surface Enhanced Raman Spectroscopy (SERS) has been studied wildly since the tech-
nology was invented in 1977 and main concepts of SERS have been understood for ap-
prox. 20 years. Some claim that SERS is at the level of development that Nuclear Magnetic 
Resonance (NMR) was in the early 70s. [1] [2] 
The possibilities of the technic are endless due to the ability to detect very small quantities 
in a relatively short time and application such as measuring illicit drugs, adulterants to food-
stuffs, biomolecules etc. have been investigated. The technic is new and varies from clas-
sical Raman in many ways and is not completely understood yet. Many subjects within 
SERS are discussed and researchers are struggling with reaching consensus. Since the 
technology is relatively new searchable databases are limited as only a small number of 
compounds have been studied. The technology is furthermore sensitive to impurities or 
matrix component and often the samples need preparation to give good results. SERS is 
furthermore successful as a fingerprinting technic but can also successfully study and re-
port rapid and sensitive quantification technics. [2] Reproducibility is difficult due to the lack 
of studies, knowledge within the field, as well as the fact that variabilities of enhancing 
systems are endless. 
This field of study has reproduced earlier studies and has also combined and modified 
others’ work to get magnetic nanoparticles for SERS applications. The technic is tested 
and optimized under simple circumstances and thereby gives an impression of technics 
available and how they can be used when not working under ultra-clean conditions. Liter-
ature covering SERS for biological samples will shortly be presented and discussed, while 
the experimental part will focus on the optimization of the signal enhancement of these 
particles. SERS is a broad field, but here attention is paid on quantitative method develop-
ment from earlier studies and compared to the experimental results. Shortly perspectivation 
is done towards and other applications.

5. Raman Spectroscopy 
This section will briefly explain the relevant theories of Raman Spectroscopy, which are 
essential to have insight into the discussions with regards to the results obtained from this 
study. Theories are then applied to the instrument used to get a full picture of collected 
data, parameters that affect data and how to comment on a Raman spectrum.  
The basic concept of Raman Spectroscopy involves incident photons from electromagnetic 
radiation (light) hitting the sample of interest. The sample scatters the photons of that elec-
tromagnetic radiation. The process of Raman can be divided into two steps; induced dipole 
and re-emission. These cannot be decoupled since they happen instantaneously. Scat-
tered light is a result of absorption and then emission of the incident light by the sample. 
Raman Spectroscopy deals with inelastic scattering, meaning that energy is lost between 
the incident photon and the emitted photon. This inelastic scattered light is called a Raman 
shift, since the wavelength of the light is shifted. Raman shifts are larger for Raman modes 
with higher frequencies and is measured in wavenumbers corresponding to the involved 
vibrational mode causing the wavelength shift. In general, Raman Spectroscopy examines 
the frequency of scattered radiation, which reflects the analyte’s bond’s vibration’s fre-
quency. The lighter the atom the higher the vibration frequency. When radiating a typical 
sample 90% of the radiation is absorbed. Absorption happens, when excited molecules 
either gain or give energy to the photon colliding with the molecule. For Raman Spectros-
copy to work the molecules under investigation must be anisotropically polarizable resulting 
in both parallel and perpendicular distortion. When having less tightly bond electrons the 
atoms are more easily polarized and then more polarity results in less polarizability. The 
vibrations of the molecule must lead to change in polarizability, which is the primary selec-
tion rule of Raman Spectroscopy. The constant changes of the molecules such as the ro-
tations and vibrations can be predicted from the scattered light. To excite the electrons from 
the ground state a certain amount of energy is required, and different samples therefor 
requires different wavelengths of the light. When the molecules gain energy from the pho-
ton the radiation is called anti-Stokes and when giving up the energy to the photon, the 
radiation is called Stokes. Rayleigh is when there is no change in frequency of the scattered 
radiation.

The theory of Raman Spectroscopy is aware of the elastic scattered light Rayleigh scatter-
ing, but instruments do not deal with this, besides filtering it off. Rayleigh scattering occurs 
due to electric polarizability of the bonds in the material since the light distort the electron 
cloud by being an external electric field. The induced dipole moment is achieved when 
polarizing the bonds, which switches on and off at the same frequency as the incident 
radiation when dealing with Rayleigh scattering. When dealing with Raman Spectroscopy 
virtual energy levels are used to explain the lifetime of a falling electron when it is not going 
to one of the excited states. The virtual states explain how the Rayleigh scattering occurs, 
where the lifetime is very short. The intensity is typically a few percent lower than the inci-
dent light.  
The fundamental transition within an analyte is most often strong in a Raman spectrum 
(when allowed by the selection rules. See appendix 13.2 for a short note on molecular 
spectroscopy), while the overtones are much weaker. The fundamental transition is from 
the lowest vibrational energy level to the first excited state. Overtones are transitions to 
higher excited states. Combination bands are formed by Fermi resonance, which means 
that the vibrations have similar frequencies and thereby couples and interact. [3] [4]  
When analyzing a Raman spectrum peak position, integrated intensity and full-width at half 
maximum (FWHM) is used, to give information about the analyte. Polarizability derivative 
tensors and interaction of that tensor with the electromagnetic field of light controls the 
normal mode of the analyte. Normal modes refer to the vibrations of the sample from the 
classic 3N-6 rule (this study will not deal with linear molecules), which is describing the 
degrees of freedom of the vibrations. The shape of a band is decided by the dynamics of 
the analyte at a surface (or in condensed phases). FWHM for a Gaussian curve is given by 
the width of the peak at the half maximum of the height and is used as a universal param-
eter to describe resolution of a spectrum.

Characterizing all parameters contributing to band shape requires much work with mathe-
matic models, but the most obvious contributor is the lifetime of the excited state and the 
molecular motion. Lifetime of the excitation state can be found from the width of the bands. 
Line broadening reveals information about molecular motion and intermolecular forces con-
trolling the motion and molecular reorientation and vibrational relaxation. To give an indi-
cation of the band resolution, FWHM is often used. In figure 1, this is indicated by a red 
line. When band position if shifted the mechanism is called blue or red shifted bands. Blue 
shifts refer to a shift increasing the wavenumber, since the frequency of the band is in-
creased, while red shift refers to a decrease in wavenumbers.  [2] [5] [6] 
Performance of Raman instruments are affected by environmental properties such as tem-
perature, dust and vibration. Since vibrations are very sensitive to temperature, it should 
be kept constant. Results are further affected by the medium of the sample. When light is 
curved or bent it is called refraction, which is due to the difference in the speed of the light. 
Refraction index describes how much the light bent in each material. Rough surfaced sam-
ples result in diffuse light scattering and thereby less intense signal. [2] [7] 
Background noise is the primary issue with Raman Spectroscopy analysis. This issue is 
reduced nowadays as the newer instrument can count signals of high precision and reso-
lution. Reduction of noise from fluorescence, stray and room light, solvent etc. is of high 
importance and should preferable be neglected as much as possible. [2] [5] [4]

6. Surface Enhanced Raman Spectroscopy 
Surface Enhanced Raman Spectroscopy is an ultra-sensitive technology combining sam-
ples with wisely chosen nanoparticles and Raman Spectroscopy, resulting in localized sur-
face plasmon resonance (LSPR), which enhances signals, that could not be observed with 
regular Raman Spectroscopy. Enhancement can be summed into two main mechanisms; 
excitation (local field) and re-emission (radiation). The noble metals in the nanostructure 
makes the resonance when an electromagnetic field is presented. Furthermore, charge 
transfer mechanisms enhance signals. The biggest part of the SERS technology is the 
nanoparticles and could have been dubbed “Nanostructure Enhanced Raman Spectros-
copy”. [2] [6] [15] 
This section will explain the theories and experiences with firstly the theories behind the 
enhancements, secondly the metallic nanostructures, known as substrates, and then lastly 
the needed properties of the analytes.  
6.1 
Surface plasmons: Electromagnetic Enhancement 
The oscillations of the electrons are called surface plasmons, which can be excited by light. 
Surface plasmon resonances are defined by collective electronic interactions between

metal atoms and electrons. This can be understood as electromagnetic effects. Metals 
have different electrical properties and therefore differing optical properties. Free conduc-
tion electrons move around in a background of positive ions, called free electron plasma. 
Plasmons are representing the elementary excitations of the charge density oscillations in 
a plasma. Plasmons are also called quasi particles since they are always lossy and highly 
interacting. One can say that plasmons are to the plasma charge density, what photons 
are to the electromagnetic field, where photons are representing the elementary excitation 
of the electromagnetic field oscillations. Plasmons are therefore also classified as charge 
density oscillations. Decay is always seen in these oscillations due to various loss mecha-
nisms, such as collisions. Plasmons are the main reason for enhancement and electro-
magnetic effect in SERS, but is more seen as plasmon polaritons, rather than pure plas-
mons. Polaritons are defined as the interaction between light and the free electrons plasma, 
shown as electromagnetic waves. Surface plasmon polaritons are then the energy shared 
between the plasmon and the photon, being a mixed mode, meaning that plasmons cannot 
exist without an associated electromagnetic wave. When dipolar transitions couples to the 
electromagnetic field in the vacuum state an intrinsic quantum effect occurs called modified 
spontaneous emission. The electromagnetic field around the surface is exhibiting quantum

6.3 
SERS substrates 
Any metallic structure that can enhance Raman allowed bands is defined as a SERS sub-
strate. These substrates most often use silver and gold, due to their simplicity in the syn-
thesis and their very efficient reflection of light in the visible region. The substrates in gen-
eral consist of colloids having colloidal properties. The nucleation of the substrate particles 
is triggered by reducing agents, temperature etc. The growth is quenched by the amount 
of reducing agent, temperature and other chemical variables. In some cases, stabilizing 
==PAGE:24==
 
 
Analysis of Biological Samples with Surface Enhanced Raman Spectroscopy (SERS) 
16 
agents are used to prevent the substrate from aggregation. Most studies report poly-dis-
persity of sizes and shapes. This will result in what is called “homogeneously broadening”. 
To produce/synthesize nanostructures, the use of reducing agents are often necessary, 
since the metals are needed to produce the enhanced signals. The amount of reducing 
agents depend on the quantity of metal salts. [2]  
SERS substrates are basically colloids, which are metastable systems. Coulomb (electro-
static) repulsions and van der Waals attractions are balanced to compose the colloids struc-
ture. Metallic colloids are usually charged negatively, which stabilizes the system. The Der-
jagun-Landau-Verwey-Overbeek (DLVO) theory is the most widely used theory to describe 
these systems to predict the outcome and properties. This theory does not encounter hy-
drodynamics. Simplifications are done, to be able to use DLVO theories and results from 
this is only indicative due to often observed poly-dispersity within the substrates. Attractive 
forces, which is always dominating, is responsible for irreversible aggregation. The DLVO 
theory can be used semi-quantitatively by estimating the number of analytes per colloid, 
the amount of surface charges and ionic strength of the solution, when the analyte is not 
added. [2] [3] 
The largest enhancements are expected when the resonance wavelength and the wave-
length of the incident laser and/or Stokes-shifted SERS photon are very close. Gap-plas-
mons have a larger local field enhancement. The enhancements in general occur between 
two particles, due to gap-plasmon resonance (coupled-LPS resonance). Aggregation is 
therefore needed for the substrates to be SERS active. Often ions are added to colloidal 
systems to introduce aggregation and salt will decrease the double layer (κ-1). By adding 
salt, the system becomes less “clean”, but it is easier to carry out the synthesis. The opti-
mum salt concentration can be determined by trial and error. Aggregation, irreversibly 
bounding, is seen when the system overcome the positive potential hump of the positive 
DLVO potential, and is decreased by ionic interactions. Aggregation is first seen as dimer 
clusters, then trimers and so on. Coagulation is seen as precipitate, resulting from contin-
uing aggregation. The energy barrier/hump is given by Boltzmans constant and system 
temperature and increases with colloid cluster size. Still unstable aggregation of the col-
loids is undesirable since it gives irreproducible and time-dependent results when synthe-
sizing substrates. Under the right conditions, partially aggregated colloids have long term 
stability. Aggregation increase the peak area by broadening the band.  Stabilization can be 
increased by adding steric interaction by use of polymers or long chained ions, since the 
colloids cannot get to close to each other. [2] [3] [26]

aggregates do not affect the extinction significantly.

Different kinds of substrate can be formed. A common version is 2D planer metallic struc-
tures, which are condensed having fractal-like clusters structures. Many studies find these 
substrates ideal due to their large electromagnetic enhancements. This characteristically 
high electromagnetic enhancements also result in photobleaching or -destruction, causing 
instability and loss of Raman scatter. These are often inhomogeneous and non-uniform at 
the surface, which can be seen from scanning electron microscopes (SEM) in a scale of 
nanometers. It is a well-known problem that achievement of uniform surfaces of planar 
structures is difficult. In general, planar substrates compromise with the highest enhance-
ments to achieve higher uniformity, which is desirable when it comes to the development 
of quantification methods. This follows the SERS uncertainty principle, which states that 
the more reproducible the enhancement is, the lower the enhancement factor of the signal. 
[2] [6] [27]

6.4 
Analytes and probe molecules 
Adsorption of the Raman active substrate is an important part of SERS and occurs in be-
tween the small aggregates as a analyte-substrate coupling, which causes the observed 
enhanced signal. [18] 
Analytes must be attached to, or in proximity of the metallic surface of the substrate. Ad-
sorption properties of the probe molecule has an impact on the SERS performance. E.g. 
negatively charged analytes will be unlikely to adsorb on the metallic colloid surface, since 
the negatively charged surface will repulse these analytes. Neutral species can attach ef-
fectively, if containing appropriate functional groups such as thiols. Neutral species might 
have a slower adsorption process than attractive positively charged ones. On the other 
hand, positively-charged analytes might reduce total negative charge of the substrate caus-
ing changes of the enhancements. Higher affinity of the analytes displaces surface species 
and affect the surface chemistry. To avoid analyte effects on the substrate surface suffi-
ciently low concentrations should be chosen. Contribution to the intensities from the analyte 
solution itself (classical Raman Spectroscopy) is in most cases negligible, due to the very 
low concentration (10-5 M).  
Analytes are adsorbed by either chemisorption or physisorption. Meaning either by a chem-
ical bond or by electrostatic interactions. Depending on the concentration, mono- or multi-
layers of analytes are adsorbed. When chemical bonds are formed, structure of the analyte 
will most likely change, affecting the vibrational and electronic structure. Other species may 
affect the adsorption, by changing the pH, being competitors or influencing the surface 
charge. Adsorption properties can e.g. be affected by adding halide ions. [2] [28] 
Size and shape has a major impact on results. Different conditions make the analyte bind 
to the metallic nanostructured surface differently and gives differing signal intensities of the 
bands. The larger the cross-section of a molecule the stronger the interaction between the 
target particle and the projectile (according to quantum mechanics). Collision is also more 
likely achieved with larger cross-sections resulting in higher intensities. [22] [27]

The laser in a Raman Spectrometer often illuminates on more than one particle. To get a 
SERS method with high reproducibility and stability, the particles must show no disordered 
stacking and still give high intensity measurements. [27] A study by Ruoping et al. showed 
that dispersion technics can be evaluated to uniformly distribute the analytes molecules on 
the substrate surface. They found that using a half-half dilution method gave a more uni-
form analyte distribution relatively to a highly concentrated dilution. The half-half dilution is 
done with a lower concentration into a substrate solution and left for 1 hour, while the higher 
concentration was added to a diluted substrate solution. Layered absorption was also 
tested, whereby the substrate was prepared, dried and then let to reaction with the analyte. 
This method was not as good as the half-half dilution. [29] 
6.5 
Factors and related topics of SERS 
Classical Raman bands are varied due to many parameters. Band position will vary due to 
chemisorption changing the coordination and polarizability of the analyte. Extra peaks ap-
pear due to photochemistry, interactions with the atmosphere and field gradients activating 
silent modes of the analyte. Degradation of intensity appears due to photodegradation, -
bleaching and -desorption. Relative intensities will be different due to molecule absorption 
resonance effects, the orientation of the analyte, molecular migration, rotation, diffusion 
and translation. The exact position of the molecule on the substrate surface strongly affects 
the local field enhancements. Width varies because of interactions and motion of the ana-
lyte. Experimental parameter, such as sampling time triggering photochemistry, can have 
an impact on the measured bandwidth. The sample can undergo photoinduced change 
during a scan, resulting in a spectrum showing Raman shifts from two different chemical 
systems. How well the probe molecule gets adsorbed on the metallic surface will affect the 
intensity of the signal. [2] [6] 
The enhancement factor (EF) is most often used in papers regarding SERS. Quoted values 
have a high degree of variance, depending on how the EF are calculated. The electromag-
netic enhancement is considered to contribute the most to the signal enhancement. But 
chemical enhancement is also observed within SERS but is with a lower contribution to the 
enhancement. EF is defined relatively to regular Raman properties of the same molecule 
under the same conditions. To give an impression of the substrates average property, av-
erage EF is used. An often-used estimate is defined by the substrate given by

enhancement, because SERS effect depend on distance between surface and analyte. But

A factor being more relevant when adsorption is not selective, is the total SERS substrate

Optical forces might also influence the system. It has been shown that clustering can be 
produced by radiation pressure in the substrate colloids. This can also be turned around 
and be used to get a better understanding of the hotspot formation. At large power densities 
the molecules can get trapped by the thermal activation and are dependent on position of 
the analytes of the substrate. Laser can also affect the diffusion on the substrate surface. 
To get a full picture of the system of interest, the exact geometry of the substrate should 
be clear, as well as the optical properties. The orientation and position of the analyte should 
be known. The optical setup, meaning excitation, wavelength, polarization, angle of inci-
dent, power density and beam profile should be known and noted. [2]  
Separation of the contribution to SERS by charge transfer and the electromagnetic 
field/surface plasmons is very difficult to measure and define. Spectra will show both mech-
anisms and will in many cases cause variations from measurement to measurement. [22]

7.1 
Side of origin of procedure 
This project takes its stand from the work of Alula et al., which concerned the optimization 
of the quantitative analysis of creatinine in urine samples. By developing Fe3O4|ZnO@Ag 
nanoparticles the intensities of Raman Spectroscopy where enhanced selectively for the 
analysis of creatinine in urine sample by simply adjusting the pH to avoid interfering com-
pounds.  
Fe3O4 particles (also called magnetite) are paramagnetic and can be attracted with a mag-
net. They have a large surface-to-volume ratio and aggregate easily to minimize their sur-
face energies. These particles easily get oxidized by air, and dispersibility and magnetism 
decrease. Fe3O4 particles are desirable within biological samples due to their magnetic 
properties, chemical stability and biocompatibility. The particles are handy since the ana-
lytes can potentially be separated from a complex matrix by the simply use of a magnet 
and avoidance of filtration and centrifugation is achieved. The paramagnetic material fur-
ther enables the opportunity to manipulate the couplings between the particles. Under the 
synthesis of these particles, no oxygen can be present since it will rapidly oxidize to Fe2O3. 
More polydispersity is observed at higher temperatures under the synthesis, but also ag-
gregation and size are reduced. By increasing stirring rate, the size of the particles de-

8.4.1 Laser power density  
The samples are sensitive to the laser power density and clearly shows fluorescence as 
pictured in figure 27. Pictures show different samples after exposed to 100 mW laser power. 
Fluorescence is a well-known interfering problem when using Raman Spectroscopy. The 
analyte simply absorbs the light and emit it, which affects the baseline and masks the bands 
of the probe molecule. [5] [7] 
The laser has a wavelength of 532 nm using a power of 100 mW. The Neutral-density (ND) 
filter can be used to filter off the power. Using the filter with the optical density of 1%, gives 
less than 1 mW, no damage of the sample is observed from Real Time Display (RTD), with 
1 sec. interval.  
Figure 27 Pictures from LabSpec after exposing the sample to full laser power using a 
green laser of 100 mW 532 nm.  
Complications caused by laser power is called photo-bleaching or photo-desorption of the 
analytes. The selection of laser power density and probe molecule should be done carefully 
to avoid these complications. Figure 28 gives the spectra when measuring the same spot 
and starting the measurements from lowest to highest power, by decreasing the ND filter-
ing. ND filter at 0.1% gives the highest signal in this case. These spectra are of the final

applies heat, which leads to red shift, meaning that after 4 measurements of 10 s. each,

curves and these values gets constant from 50 μm and up as well as the intensity, implying 
that 50 μm and larger spot areas will result in the same resolution and intensity.

measurements resulted in less sample error relative to the use of a Raman microscope 
when sampling less than 100 points. [14]

DMAB is formed by oxidizing pATP to a radical cation by photooxidation. Photooxidation 
occurs by raising the Fermi level of the metal. Surface enhanced photochemical reactions 
on metal nanostructures are suggested to be caused by two mechanisms: optical electric 
field increasement and enhanced absorption. Having neutral to acidic conditions such as 
in methanol the pATP radical reacts with the resonance structure forming tail-to-tail and 
head-to-tail reactions. Head to head is uncommon due to electrostatic repulsion. The silver 
acts as a catalyst by transforming the light energy to chemical energy. A high photochem-
ical cross-section is present for larger silver nanoparticles, since the metal states are filled 
to a continuum, which supports both charge transfer and surface plasmon processes. Here 
it gets interesting though, as the DMAB bands are still in the spectrum without the presence 
of silver. 
By using 1064 nm laser it has been shown that the DMAB formation is avoided, while 514 
nm laser causes the photochemical reactions and higher laser power induces the formation 
and vice versa. [26]

Raman Spectroscopy (SERRS), where the sample is irradiated with exciting radiation 
whose energy correspond to that of the electronic transition in the adsorbate. The bands 
within the chromophore are then selectively enhanced further, compared to bands with no 
resonance. [6]

8.8.3 Reproducibility from optimization 
Within this study spot-to-spot variation is defined as the variation between spots on the 
same sample. Batch-to-batch variation is defined from several samples made from the 
mixed portion of Fe3O4|ZnO particles. Batch-to-batch for the Fe3O4|ZnO particles is thereby 
left out from this study, to avoid too many varying parameters. From the optimization ex-
periments the percentages of variation from spot-to-spot was from 9-36%. These are simply 
found by taking the average standard deviation of the best optimization condition for each 
tested parameter and dividing it with the average intensity. The variation from batch-to-
batch was found from the standard deviation from batch-to-batch divided by the average 
intensity. These values varied from 4-33%. The experiment testing the optimal reaction 
time for pATP has the high variations of 36% and 33% respectively. This might be due to 
the lower concentration as stated in section 8.7. The last optimization step where 24 hours 
of reduction reaction time was applied shows a spot-to-spot variation of 15% and batch-to-
batch is 15%. It is stated that good spot-to-spot and batch-to-batch reproducibility within 
SERS is achieved below 20%. [3] Since this batch from step II procedure 3 consist of a mix 
of particles, to get an average optimization with the available equipment, using the same 
portion of Fe3O4|ZnO particles, is predicted to show higher spot-to-spot reproducibility.

this statement does not take its hold. This is given in table 3. These batches do not go

9. Applications and biological samples 
Many SERS methods have been developed towards biological samples. SERS seem to 
have a promising future for detection of biological samples. SERS substrates show a low 
degree of toxicity making its application for biological systems very attractive. E.g. zinc 
oxide has shown unique results when it comes to analysis of blood samples for diseases 
and is in general promising due to the lower number of hotspots. Enhanced vibrations can 
be obtained for complicated biological structures observed with minimal interference when 
the wavelength of the laser is close to its absorption. [5] [21] [40] 
This section will shortly compare three examples including biological samples with the pro-
duced substrate to give some perspective. Furthermore, two remarkable SERS technics is 
shortly mentioned.  
9.1 
Comparable studies to the produced substrates 
The focus on the produced substrate was to make it usable for quantitative methods. SERS 
substrates made from silver do well with protein due to the COO- and -SH groups. Sand-
wich SERS systems have been developed from silver ions to detect specific sequences for 
identifying single nucletides of Hepatitis A and B, HIV, Ebola and Variola viruses. Kaminska 
et al. used fuchsin to bind antibodies and substrate since assurance of attachment can be 
challenging as sufficiently strong signals requires work with measuring biological samples 
by SERS. By forming a sandwich structure antigen and antibody is immobilized resulting 
==PAGE:64==
 
 
Analysis of Biological Samples with Surface Enhanced Raman Spectroscopy (SERS) 
56 
in high reproducibility, stability and enhancement. Quantification of Hepatitis B virus antigen 
in human blood reached a detection limit of 0.01 IU/mL and a deviation of less than 10%. 
[3] [53] A similar sandwich structure could have been tested, by switching step III and IV 
and simply add silver as the final step, since it has been shown, that pATP as well binds to 
magnetite-zinc oxide complex.  
Goodall et al. combined electrochemistry and silver nanoparticles for the detection of early 
preeclampsia by quantifying uric acid in urine. It was reported that uric acid is competing 
with sulphate and chloride when it comes to binding to silver nanoparticles. When having 
competing ions, these with take some of the available active sites, and make decrease the 
signal from the uric acid, as well disturb the signals. Goodall et al. used NaF as supportive 
electrolyte since fluoride exhibits very weak specific adsorption on silver. This group sug-
gest removal of the interfering compounds, when using SERS without electrochemical 
technics for analysis of uric acid in urine. Detection of urine was found to be easy below 
0.4 mM and was assumed to offer a successful tool for this detection. [15] The produced 
substrate from this study might as well show promising results when it comes to detection 
of low concentration a pATP solution of less then 1 mM, clearly showing the DMAB finger-
print SERS spectra. Alula et al. found that concentrations of creatine above 1 μM would 
show the same results, since the actives sites are occupied at higher concentrations. This 
states how the sensitivity of SERS gives limitations when it comes to being applied for a 
broad interval of concentration. 
Westly et al. produced silver nanoparticles from the Leopold and Lendl method. It was 
found that bands of uric acid changed in cases of saturation. This resulted in lower signal 
for higher concentrations of uric acid (100 μM) giving a nonlinear relationship. They sug-
gested that the concentration affected the orientation of the molecules on the nanostruc-
tured surface giving a variation of the intensity and broadening of certain peaks. They sug-
gested 5-100 μM as the optimum concentrations for their SERS analysis. The particles 
from this study reported reproducibility at 1.5%. [54] The substrates from the performed 
study showed a reproducibility being ten times higher at 15% and will clearly not give a 
better alternative to a quantification method. 
9.2 
Single molecule SERS 
Single molecule SERS (SM-SERS) have been enjoying much attention within the field of 
the technology and are faced with the same general problems. Small concentrations of 
analytes are usually selected such 0.1 molecule/colloid. The argument in this method is, 
that statistically there can only be one or less molecule per colloid using such low concen-
trations. But dealing with such low concentrations require a high degree of awareness to-
wards avoiding contamination and dilution errors. Since SM-SERS is proposed to only be 
observed at certain colloids, some studies point out, that this could be due to larger-than-
==PAGE:65==
 
 
Analysis of Biological Samples with Surface Enhanced Raman Spectroscopy (SERS) 
57 
average effective surface areas, which are then adsorbing more analytes. These colloids 
might e.g. be a dimer of the colloid, giving the larger surface area. SERS fluctuations can 
be caused by blinking and spectral shape and has been characterized as a sign of SM-
SERS. The problem with these studies is the uncertainty of the concentration estimates 
and the unreliable statistical assumptions. The contribution from these kind of signals 
needs to be avoided within the development of a quantification method but seems impos-
sible from this study. Tip enhanced Raman Spectroscopy (TERS) has shown great results 
when it comes to SM-SERS, but only for specialized lab with fitted equipment. TERS shows 
high reproducible results but is difficult to make applicable. The SM-SERS investigation 
has been opening doors towards detection of drugs for forensic uses e.g. anti-doping tests, 
spiking in drinks, identification of fingerprints etc. [2] [3] 
9.3 
Lithography SERS 
Lithographic technics have further been developed resulting in high control over the geom-
etry and thereby the homogeneity. Lithography technics provide a high level of both homo-
geneity and reproducibility and might have shown the best SERS result to date. Still this 
procedure faces complications such as fabrication of interacting particles and control of the 
gap between particles. Reproducibility for this technic has reached around 10% of the re-
sidual standard deviation for the peak height. The same holds true when comparing the 
substrates. These results are desirable for analytical purposes, but the methods suffer from 
high cost, specialized equipment and long preparation time. [2] [3]

10. Discussion 
From this study of a probe-substrate complex, several issues and possibilities have been 
pointed out  and confirmed from literature. This section will state these pros and cons to 
finally reach a conclusion on the analysis of biological samples with SERS.  
The first concern within this study is the lack of testing the quality control parameters to 
assure that the instrument was following these. Using autocalibration and having a poly-
morph intern standard gives no impression on how the instrument was running within this 
study. The spectra of calibrations were saved but disappeared from the folder for an un-
known reason. The instrument is newly installed, so variations should be minor, but this is 
not known for sure due to the lack of (useful) data.  
This project showed great and promising prospective results towards using self-organizing 
substrates for the development of qualification and quantification methods. Magnetic col-
lection has already been proposed to give satisfactory results for biological samples since

great properties giving a big variety of shapes and sizes with highly reproducible results. 
Coating the particles with silver assures a high level of enhancement. The particles are 
easy to produce and can potentially be made with limited equipment available.   
The biggest challenge met was to reach a high degree of reproducibility. The experiments 
were made under a low degree of quality control to get an impression of the use outside 
specialized laboratories. The reproducibility showing 15% on both spot-to-spot and batch-
to-batch fit within the proposed needs being below 20% and thus confirms acceptable 
SERS activity. But e.g. EFs are known to differ between research studies due to varying 
assumptions and inadequate descriptions. By leaving out information researchers can eas-
ily claim high reproducibility. In this study one could claim that batch-to-batch reproducibility 
was reached at 3% from table 3, even though it is based on two replicates with one batch 
of Fe3O4|ZnO particles. Towards reaching a quantitative method, the reproducibility would 
in most cases require at 10% to be applicable, which this study can only reach by coinci-

11. Conclusion 
A self-organized hybrid SERS substrate was produced from optimization steps consisting 
of a Fe3O4|ZnO@Ag composite. The substrate showed satisfying magnetic properties, 
which could easily be collected on a nail and analyzed. The probe molecule pATP was 
used to evaluate enhancing properties of the substrate. The pATP easily attached to the 
substrate and formed DMAB. Strong enhancement was achieved of the reacted pATP to 
DMAB. Reproducibility of the optimized substrate was 15% for spot-to-spot and 15% for 
batch-to-batch.

13.2 Molecular spectroscopy 
This note is made from [4].  
Raman spectrometer has a monochromatic incident laser beam, which is passed through 
the sample and radiation scattered is monitored from the front face. All kinds of samples 
can be analyzed. Spectroscopy results from electronic transitions, where molecules un-
dergo changes by rotation and vibration. Emission is the transition from a higher (E1) to a 
lower (E2) energy state. The molecule has excess energy and emits a photon to reach a 
lower energy state. Raman examines frequency (𝑣) of scattered radiation by molecules to 
get information about changes in the molecular state. Stokes radiation is associated with 
lower 𝑣, where the incident photons collide with molecules and give up energy. Anti-Stokes 
radiation is associated with higher 𝑣, where incident photons collect energy from excited 
molecules, Rayleigh radiation is when the scattered light has no change of 𝑣. The photon 
energy can be described by
