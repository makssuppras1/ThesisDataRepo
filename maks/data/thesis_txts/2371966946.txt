Abstract
Based on the theory of cosmoclimatology the eect of a nearby supernova on Earth's cloud
cover is investigated. Clouds exert a strong cooling eect by increasing Earth's albedo,
and therefore a change in the radiative properties of clouds can aect global climate. Re-
cent 60Fe results have strongly suggested that a supernova happened at a distance of 50
pc, ∼2.2 million years ago. Such an event would change the cosmic ray energies incident
at Earth. The energy spectrum would depend on their propagation which is aected by
the ambient galactic magnetic eld. In this work a weak turbulent magnetic eld is as-
sumed and the calculated spectra show that the cosmic ray intensity would increase by
up to three orders of magnitude the rst 300 years following the supernova. The eect
such an event would have on the ionization of the atmosphere is studied, and the results
show that the ion concentration would increase by up to a factor four down to ground
level. Also, the increased ionization in the lowest 15 km of the atmosphere will persist
up to 10,000 years after the supernova. The changes in the ionization of the atmosphere
would aect aerosol and in turn cloud processes.
The eect of the atmospheric ionization on aerosol processes was studied through exper-
iments in an 8 m3 reaction chamber in Skylab at DTU Space. The nucleation rate of
aerosols at 1.4 nm as a function of ionization and sulfuric acid concentration was mea-
sured. These are the rst direct measurements of aerosol nucleation rates at radiation
levels corresponding to a nearby SN. We nd that the nucleation would increase by 300%
the rst few hundred years after the supernova.
The growth of aerosols from 1.4 nm to cloud condensation nuclei sizes (∼50-100 nm) was
studied with a full numerical box model, modied with results from the experiments.
According to standard aerosol dynamics the increased nucleation would not lead to a
signicant increase in cloud condensation nuclei which could aect cloud processes. How-
ever, evidence for an ion mechanism that helps the aerosol grow exists, and when this is
included in the modelling, the eects from the supernova increases the concentration of
cloud droplets up to 50%. This translates to a change in radiative forcing of -4 W/m2
and equilibrium surface temperature decrease in the range -1.2 to -5.6 K.

Resumè
Dette projekt er baseret på teorien om kosmoklimatologi og omhandler en enkelt super-
novas eekt på Jordens skydække. Skyer har en stærk afkølende eekt ved at påvirke
Jordens albedo, og defor kan en forøgelse af antallet af skyer have stor indydelse på
Jordens klima. Det er blevet påvist at der skete en supernova i en afstand på 50 pc
fra solsystemet for ∼2.2 millioner år siden. En begivenhed som denne ville ændre den
kosmiske stråling ved Jorden, og det resulterende spektrum vil afhænge af partiklernes
udbredelse igennem det interstellare rum og Mælkevejens magnetfelt.
I dette projekt
antages det, at det magnetfeltet var svagt og turbulent, og de udregnede spektra viser,
at intensiteten af den kosmiske stråling ville stige med op til tre størrelsesordener i de
første 300 år efter supernova eksplosionen. Det undersøges hvilken eekt dette ville have
på ioniseringen af Jordens atmosfære og resultatet er, at ion koncentrationen ville stige
op til en faktor re i forhold til den nuværende baggrunds ionkoncentration.
Fra 10
km højde og helt ned til jordens overade vil den øgede ionisering vedblive i 10.000 år
efter supernovaen. Ændringerne i atmosfærens ionisering ville påvirke aerosol- og dermed
skyprocesser.
Eekten af den forøgede ionkoncentration blev studeret med eksperimenter i et 8 m3
reaktionskammer i Skylab på DTU Space. Nukleationsraten af aerosoler ved 1.4 nm som
funktion af ion- og svovlsyrekoncentrationen blev målt. Disse målinger er de første di-
rekte målinger at nukleation af sekundære aerosoler ved strålingsniveauer der tilsvarer en
supernova i solsystemets nærhed. Resultatet viser, at nukleationen stiger med 300% de
første få hundrede år efter supernovaen.
Aerosolernes vækst fra 1.4 nm til skykondensationskerner (∼50-100 nm) undersøges ved
brug af en komplet numerisk boks model, som er modiceret med resultaterne fra eskper-
imentet. Ifølge standard aerosolteori vil den øgede nuklaetion ikke føre til signikant ere
skykondensationskerner, som kan påvirke skyprocesser. Dog ndes der beviser for, at der
eksisterer en mekanisme, hvormed ioner hjælper aerosolernes vækst. Når dette inkluderes
i modellen, er eekterne fra supernovaen, at koncentrationen af skydråber stiger med 50
%. Dette svarer til en ædring i strålingspåvirkningen på -4 W/m2 og et temperaturfald i
intervallet -1.2 til -5.6 K.

Preface
This master thesis describes the impact of a single supernova on Earth's cloud cover. The
work was carried out at DTU space at the Technical University of Denmark in the Divi-
sion of Astrophysics and Atmospheric Physics. The Division studies physical processes in
planets, the Solar System, stars, galaxies, and the universe as a whole. Also, the Earth's
atmosphere, where Earth meets space is studied. This thesis is a contribution to the work
of the Cosmoclimate group that investigates eects of comic rays on clouds and climate.
The project corresponds to 30 ECTS and was carried out between the 2nd of January 2017
and the 2nd of June 2017. The project was supervised by Professor Henrik Svensmark
and Senior Researcher Martin Bødker Engho. The project consists of both theoretical
and experimental work and the use of models. The work was executed in the following way:
During the rst month of the project a literature study was performed to gain knowledge
about the state of the art within the eld. In addition the experimental setup was pre-
pared. This included acquiring an additional radioactive source from DTU NUTECH.
Measurements started on February 7th and the last measurement was taken on March
21st. February and March were primarily used for measurements and data processing and
analysis as data were acquired. The weeks following the measurement series were used
for data analysis.
During the rst weeks of April, the cosmic ray spectra and the resulting atmospheric
ionization were calculated. And following this, a numerical model was used to study the
growth of aerosols. The eect on the climate and the uncertainties in this estimation were
worked out during the nal month of the thesis. The report and nal presentation was
also prepared during the nal month.
The project was presented in front of scientists and engineers at a "Science club" event,
held in the Division of Astrophysics and Atmospheric Physics, on May 18th.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Supernovae are estimated to occur in the Milky Way at a rate of ∼1-3 per century (e.g.
(Fry et al., 2015)). At this rate it is inevitable that, over the course of geological time,
some such events will have occurred within a distance of about 100 pc from Earth. Super-
novae at these distances will deposit ejecta on the Earth and Moon and change the cosmic
ray spectrum. Investigation of these ejecta have conrmed that multiple supernovae hap-
pened at distances less than 100 pc from the solar system ∼2-3 Ma (Myr ago) (Fimiani
et al., 2016; Knie et al., 2004). Also, the local observed cosmic ray spectrum shows sig-
natures of a supernova ∼2 Ma (Kachelriess et al., 2015; Savchenko et al., 2015). There
has long been speculation that abrupt changes in the Plio-Pleistocene African climate
occurring ∼2.8 Ma, might be related to supernovae (e.g. (Knie et al., 2004)) through
a mechanism related to cosmic rays, but no denite link has been established yet. The
climate changes resulted in a cooler and drier African climate that possibly caused signi-
cant developments in hominid evolution (DeMenocal, 1995). Thus, it is of special interest
to investigate this link in order to understand the mechanisms that caused the cooling ∼
2.8 Ma. In addition, the same mechanism can inuence present day and future climate
due to periodic variations in the cosmic ray spectrum and the possibility of a nearby su-
pernova explosion in the future (e.g. Betelgeuse at a distance of ∼200 pc). Such eects,
if validated, should be included in global climate models.
It is well documented in a large number of studies that changes in solar activity corre-
late with changes in climate (Christiansen et al., 2007). Initially speculations about this
link were directed towards changes in the solar irradiation. But satellite measurements
of the solar constant have concluded that the variations are too small to explain the ob-
served temperature changes (Lean et al., 1995). A correlation between a global average
of low cloud cover and the cosmic ray ux incident on the atmosphere during a solar
cycle, see Figure 1.1, has been observed (Marsh and Svensmark, 2000). Since clouds can
aect Earth's climate through the radiation budget, this correlation was the rst direct
observational suggestion of a link between cosmic rays and climate. If such a link exists
long term variations in cosmic rays should reect variations in Earth's temperature. An
example of this, seen in Figure 1.1, is the correlation between cold icy climates found from
the geological record of the past 550 million years and the Solar system's encounters with
spiral arms of the Milky Way galaxy, where explosive blue stars and cosmic rays are more
concentrated (Shaviv and Veizer, 2003).

This thesis is based on the theory of cosmoclimatology, rst introduced by Svensmark
(1998). According to cosmoclimatology the ions produced in the atmosphere by cosmic
rays assist aerosol formation. Some of the aerosols grow into cloud condensation nuclei
(CCN) and aect cloud microphysics thereby increasing the number, lifetime, and albedo
of clouds. Clouds have a net cooling eect on Earth's radiation budget of about -27.7
W/m2 (Hartmann, 1993) and are therefore an important negative climate forcing. Con-
sequently, the processes that connect cosmic rays and clouds could possibly be causing
climate change in the past and now.
Around 50% of all CCN originate from aerosols that are formed by nucleation of atmo-
spheric molecules (e.g.
(Merikanto et al., 2009; Yu and Luo, 2009)).
The nucleation
process is therefore important for understanding the microphysical properties of clouds
and the mechanism that can link cosmic rays and climate. Few measurements exist that
quantify parameters aecting and assisting nucleation and most models rely on assump-
tions. Recent laboratory measurements by Dunne et al. (2016) determined the dependence
on trace gas and ion concentrations. These and other measurements, e.g. (Svensmark
et al., 2007) have proven that ionization caused by cosmic rays help the nucleation pro-
cess. In this project we expand on these results by measuring nucleation at ionization
levels corresponding to those following a nearby supernova.
The aerosols have to grow from the freshly nucleated particles with diameters of a few nm
to CCN sizes which are of the order of 50-100 nm. They grow primarily by condensation
of gasses and standard atmospheric models predict that the increased number of particles
will have used up more of the atmospheric gasses that they grow from and therefore be in
a more competitive environment than otherwise. The aerosols therefore grow slower and
more of them are lost to scavenging before they reach CCN sizes. Thus, the prevailing
theory predicts that although more particles are formed by cosmic rays, the increase in
CCN will be insignicant, because the probability of any particle surviving is much lower.
2
==PAGE:16==
However, results from Svensmark et al. (2013) and the correlations between cosmic rays
and clouds (e.g. (Marsh and Svensmark, 2000; Shaviv and Veizer, 2003)) indicate that
the cosmic rays help the growth process as well as the nucleation process and thereby
also increase the number of CCN. In this thesis the subsequent growth process, from the
freshly nucleated aerosols to CCN sizes, is studied with the use of a full numerical box
model. The modelling is performed using standard aerosol dynamics and by including a
mechanism where cosmic rays help the growth process.
Changes in radiative forcing are connected to changes in global equilibrium surface tem-
perature through a linear relationship known as the climate sensitivity. Thus, it is possible,
although it includes many uncertainties, to quantify the eects that the changes in clouds
would have on the climate.
Just as the elements that human beings are made of all have their origin in supernovae
the climate changes that shaped the hominid evolution might also be caused by these
events. The thought of an even stronger link between human destiny and the stars is
fascinating. The link between cosmic rays and climate deserves attention and this thesis
is a an attempt to quantify the eects of a single supernova that exploded 2.2 Ma at a
distance of 50 pc from the solar system.
1.2
Aim of the Thesis
The objective of the thesis is to study and investigate the impact of a single supernova
on Earth's cloud cover. This will be done through both theory of cosmic rays, models
of their interaction with the atmosphere, through experiments performed in Skylab, and
models of aerosol growth. By using the full chain of results from changes in the cosmic
ray spectrum to changes in CCN we estimate what eect a nearby supernova such as the
one 2.2 Ma would have on Earth's cloud cover and thereby the climate. The following
specic objectives are achieved:
• Calculate the cosmic ray spectrum resulting from a supernova at 50 pc distance
from the solar system. The spectrum can be used to calculate the ionization of the
atmosphere.
• Calculate the ionization, caused by the nearby supernova, in order to know what
ionization levels are relevant for the further work.
• Test and quantify, through experiments, the role of condensing gas and ions in the
nucleation of aerosols at ionization levels corresponding those caused by a nearby
supernova. The results can be used to scale the nucleation rate for given atmospheric
conditions and ionization level.
• Estimate the eect that the supernova would have on the number of CCN, using a
full numerical model.
• Estimate the eect that the supernova would have on the climate through changes
in the radiative properties of clouds, using results from the model.

2.1
Theory and Method
2.1.1
Supernovae and Galactic Cosmic Rays
There are two main types of supernovae (SNe). Type I happens in binary star systems
where one of the stars, a white dwarf, steals matter from its companion star. Eventually,
the white dwarf accumulates too much matter and explodes, resulting in a SN. Type II
SNe occur when a massive star (> 8 M⊙) ends its life. Type II SNe are the most inter-
esting with respect to eects on the Solar System, since the relative frequency of type I
and type II SNe in the solar neighbourhood is NtI/NtII = 0.15 (Tsujimoto et al., 1995).
Therefore, the focus of this thesis is on type II SNe. These happen because as the star runs
out of nuclear fuel, the stellar core can no longer withstand its own gravitational force.
The core collapses, resulting in the explosion of a SN during which the star's luminosity
increases by as much as 20 magnitudes and most of its mass is blown away at a very
high velocity. The pressure and temperature in the shock wave caused by a SN is high
enough for nuclear fusion reactions that produce heavier elements to occur. Therefore,
core collapse SNe are the predominant sources of elements heavier than helium in galaxies.
Type II SNe are further split into categories, depending on the size of the progenitor
star. The most common ones are Core Collapse SNe (CCSN) and Electron Capture SNe
(ECSN). The latter occur when smaller mass stars (8-10 M⊙) collapse. These stars form
cores consisting of oxygen, neon and magnesium (O, Ne, Mg) (instead of an iron core
like more massive stars) which are supported by the quantum mechanical eect of de-
generacy. When the core reaches a mass of ∼1.37 times the mass of the Sun, electrons
become captured by magnesium, which causes loss of hydrostatic support and the core
collapses. Because the electron capture cores are smaller than the cores that produce core
6
==PAGE:20==
collapse SNe, the ejection is less energetic and creates fewer heavy elements. The energy
output from an ECSN is ∼1050 erg compared to ∼1051 erg released by a CCSN (Wanajo
et al., 2009). The enormous amount of energy is initially carried by the expanding ejecta
and is then transferred to the shocked interstellar gas and to kinetic energy of relativistic
particles, which form what is known as a supernova remnant (SNR). Part of the atoms
from the interstellar gas and the relativistic particles are accelerated in the shock front of
the SNR and become Galactic Cosmic Rays (GCR) that propagate into the interstellar
medium (ISM) at relativistic velocities (Ackermann et al., 2013).
GCR consist mainly of nuclei of which 90 % are protons and 8 % heavier nuclei, the last
2% are primarily electrons (Büsching, 2004). Their energies range from 1 GeV up to ∼1011
GeV, but only energies below 106 GeV have SNRs as their sources. The highest energy
cosmic rays (CR) are believed to have extra galactic origin, produced by more powerful
galaxies than the Milky Way (Kachelriess, 2008).
The rate of SNe in the Milky Way is 1-3 per century (Fry et al., 2015). In vicinity of the
Solar System, here dened as being within the Local Bubble of approximately 100 pc in
diameter (Feige et al., 2016), this translates to 1-3 SNe in 10 My. The nearest type II SN
candidate that is expected to explode within the next one million years is the brightest
star in the Orion constellation, Betelgeuse, at a distance of ∼200 pc.
2.1.2
Suggetions of Nearby Supernovae
The CR ejected from a SN explosion can travel great distances, depending on the galactic
magnetic eld and the density of the ISM. The particles may even overcome the solar
wind pressure and reach the inner part of the heliosphere and some may deposit on Earth
and the Moon. These debris can then be used as indicators for nearby SN events.
The radioactive iron isotope iron-60 (60Fe) with a half life of 2.6 Myr (Wallner et al., 2015)
is an important timer for astrophysical applications in the million-year time range. While
most of the iron in the universe is 56Fe which is a stable nucleus made up of 26 protons and
30 neutrons, 60Fe has 34 neutrons making the isotope unstable and therefore radioactive.
60Fe was produced in the solar system nearly 4.6 billion years ago during the formation of
the solar system and is therefore instinct by now. Only very small concentrations arriving
as CR are expected on Earth and the Moon. Type II SNe are considered the dominant
source of 60Fe in the Milky Way (Knie et al., 2004). In case of a suciently close SN, the
ejected material will leave a 60Fe signal far above the natural level in the solar system.
The long half-life of 60Fe makes it possible to search for such a signal during the past
several millions of years.
60Fe is thus a unique indicator and timer for nearby SNe. A
60Fe signal was observed in deep-sea crust by Knie et al. (2004) and further investigated
by Feige et al. (2016), Fry et al. (2015) and Fry et al. (2016). These studies found that
multiple SNe occured within a distance less than 100 pc the last 10 Myr. Most probably
during two distinct periods 1.7-3.2 million years ago (Ma) and around 6.5-8.7 Ma. Sim-
ilar signals are also found in samples from the Moon (Fimiani et al., 2016). Additional
evidence for a SN occurring ∼2 Ma is found in the current GCR spectrum. These results
7
==PAGE:21==
are presented in (Kachelriess et al., 2015) and (Savchenko et al., 2015).
Most recent sources place the latest event at a distance of ∼50 pc ∼2.2 Ma, probably
origination from the Tucana-Horologium (Tuc-Hor) stellar association, which was at a
distance of ∼60 pc, 2.2 Ma (Fry et al., 2016). The Scorpius-Centaurus association (Sco-
cen) was earlier considered the most probable origin, but this association was further away
at the time (∼130 pc) and is therefore less likely.
The assessment, by (Fry et al., 2015), of possible sources for the 60Fe deposition, came
to the conclusion that an electron capture supernova (ECSN) would be the most likely
candidate for several reasons. First, these events are most common because they arise
from lower mass stars (8-10 M⊙). Also, the mass is within the range of the members in
both the Sco-Cen and Tuc-Hor stellar associations. Other arguments, such as the lack of
certain isotopes that would be produced in higher mass SNe, are presented and discussed
in Fry et al. (2015). This single event; 2.2 Ma at 50 pc distance from the solar system, is
the focus for the rest of this work.
2.1.3
Terrestrial Eects from a Nearby Supernova
SNe that occur within a distance of ∼10 pc from Earth are considered to be within the
"kill distance". The kill distance is the range at which a SN can create extinction-level
disruptions to the biosphere (Fry et al., 2015). Obviously, the SN event at 50 pc distance
does not fall within this category, however, it will still aect Earth, primarily through
the eects caused by CR, as the analysis by Melott et al. (2017) concludes. The impact
may vary dramatically depending on the assumed magnetic eld between the source and
Earth, as will be discussed in the following. Generally the tropospheric ionization down
to ground levels will increase and irradiation by secondary particles on the ground and
in the upper ocean will intensify and increase the overall radiation load on terrestrial
organisms by approximately an order of magnitude (Melott et al., 2017).
More studies comment on the fact that the more recent SN activity (1.7-3.2 Ma) occurred
at the same time as Earth's temperature started to decrease during the Plio-Pleistocene
transition (Eg. (Feige et al., 2016; Knie et al., 2004; Melott et al., 2017)). Figure 2.1 shows
a record of global temperatures for the past 500 Myr and it is seen that there is evidence
of generally cooling climate starting ∼3 Ma. The cooling resulted in a transition from the
warm Pliocene to the cold Pleistocene period beginning about 2.6 Ma (Melott et al., 2017).
The causes of this trend and transition are poorly understood (e.g. (Filippelli and Flores,
2009; Lisiecki and Raymo, 2007)) and with the previously mentioned correlations between
CR and climate in mind, a link is emerging that deserves attention. Although a single SN
can not explain the cooling trend, the increased SN activity might have contributed. In
addition, there were abrupt changes in the African climate during this period (DeMenocal,
1995) where the African climate became cooler and drier. These abrupt coolings are not
understood either and could possibly be linked to a single SN (Knie et al., 2004). This
thesis tries to quantify the eects that the most recent SN would have on the ionization
of the troposphere and thereby on aerosol and cloud formation in order to estimate the
possible eect on global climate.

2.3
Discussion and Caveats
The resulting spectra are based on several assumptions that are adequate for the purpose
of this project. However, a few of them deserve a comment, and could be avoided in order
to obtain more accurate spectra.
We assume an instantaneous release of CR from the SNR. In fact, the acceleration of
CR from a remnant does not happen instantaneously, but is delayed until the remnant
expands enough to release the CR. Then, it grows until it reaches a maximum, and then
decreases. This could be included in the model to better estimate the timing of the in-
14
==PAGE:28==
creased CR ux. In order to do that a time-dependent source term, such as Eq. 5.53 in
(Büsching, 2004), should be included in the diusion approximation.
The prior formation of the local bubble is essential for the propagation as diusion and,
therefore, for the resulting spectrum. This scenario is favored by the physics of the Lo-
cal Bubble (Melott et al., 2017), however, if this is not the case, a largely coherent eld
aligned along the line of sight to the SN would put an upper bound on the TeV-PeV
CR ux increases at ∼104 (Melott et al., 2017). Yet, in case of a transverse eld the
additional ux would be below current levels.
The eect of GCR modulation by solar activity and the geomagnetic eld is not included
since it is small compared to the SN-induced variations and only aects a small range of
the CR energies induced by the SN (10-14 GeV). However, it might inuence the result-
ing ionization (especially at higher altitudes where lower energy CR have great ionization
eects) and cause variations with latitude, that would be worth including in future studies.
We believe that the approximations that are made are adequate for this purpose, and
the resulting spectra, seen in Figure 2.5 are used to study the atmospheric eects of CR
accelerated by the SNR at 50 pc distance.

3.1
Theory and Method
The GCR that penetrate the heliosphere and the geomagnetic eld move through Earth's
atmosphere and interact with air molecules in it, initiating a cascade of secondary par-
ticles. The secondary particles include protons, neutrons, electrons, muons, and pions
and some of these particles reach Earth's surface and even penetrate it. On their way,
the secondary particles ionize the molecules and atoms of the atmosphere leaving behind
a trail of ions. The atmospheric ionization caused by GCR, called cosmic ray induced
ionization (CRII), is dicult to measure directly and research in this eld is largely based
upon models. The models that are most suited to study CRII for the lower atmosphere
consider the nucleonic-electromagnetic-muon cascade, initiated by CR, to its full extent
using a Monte Carlo numerical simulation. Dierent Monte Carlo based models have
been compared and agreement within about 10% with each other and with direct mea-
surements in the troposphere has been found. Thus, these models provide a useful and
reliable tool to study CRII in the lower atmosphere (Usoskin et al., 2008). In this thesis,
a model is not used directly but tables created by Atri et al. (2010) are convolved with
the spectra calculated in Chapter 2. The tables are based on the CORSIKA (COsmic
Ray SImulations for KAscade) simulation tool and extend the CRAC model developed by
(Usoskin et al., 2006) to higher energies. The CRAC model can simulate the atmospheric
ionization from the ground up to the top of the atmosphere and covers the ionization
from primary CR nuclei (predominantly of protons and He-particles) with energies from
several tens of MeV up to 1 TeV. The model by Atri et al. (2010) contains energies in the
range of 10 GeV to 1 PeV. The extension to higher energies is only necessary because a SN
enhances the ux of CR with energies above 1 TeV. Normally, lower energy CR dominate
the atmospheric ionization.

Aerosol Particle Formation
In this chapter the theory of aerosol formation is reviewed, including the eects of ions. A
parametrization of nucleation is presented, which is used to quantify the relative inuence
of parameters in the nucleation process. The second part of the chapter presents the
experimental part of the project. First, the setup and experiments are presented. Second,
the data and results are analysed. Third, the experiments and results are discussed.
4.1
Theory
Liquid or solid particles suspended in the atmosphere are dened as aerosol particles.
Aerosol particles aect Earth's radiation budget directly by absorbing and reecting solar
radiation. They also have an indirect eect by inuencing cloud formation, if they grow
large enough and become active cloud condensation nuclei (CCN) on which water vapor
can condense and form droplets. There are two main groups of aerosol particles: primary
particles have direct sources such as pollen, sea salt particles or soot. These have a size of
typically 1 µm and are not aected by cosmic rays. Secondary particles are produced in the
atmosphere by condensation of gasses. Recent results by Dunne et al. (2016) suggest that
about 43% of cloud-forming aerosol particles in the atmosphere are secondary particles
that originate from nucleation. This agrees well with other studies (Merikanto et al., 2009;
Yu and Luo, 2009).
Sulfuric acid (H2SO4) is the primary ingredient in the production of secondary aerosols
because of its low vapor pressure and ability to bond with water, which is ubiquitous in
the atmosphere (Curtius, 2006). H2SO4 is primarily produced in the atmosphere from
sulfur dioxide (SO2) via oxidation by the OH radical, produced photochemically with
ultra violet light coming from the sun. If the H2SO4 collides with other molecules, they
start forming small clusters of molecules that can grow into new stable aerosols. This
process is termed binary (for H2O and H2SO4) homogeneous nucleation and this is how
secondary aerosol particles are formed.
Nucleation often involves other substances as
well, the dominant ones being ammonia (NH3) and organic molecules. These processes
are termed ternary and organic nucleation respectively. Ions help the nucleation process
by stabilizing the molecular clusters, this process is termed ion induced nucleation. The
next sections review the theory of the nucleation processes.

4.1.1
Nucleation Theory
The nucleation of aerosols is essentially the process of transition from single gas-phase
molecules to a stable liquid or solid bulk-phase. The most crucial part of the process
is whether the transition happens or not. This depends on minimization of Gibbs free
energy, G, as the system will strive to reach thermodynamic equilibrium. For particles of
small radii it is more energy ecient to evaporate molecules than to condense. Therefore,
small clusters of molecules will most likely fall apart into separate gas molecules. How-
ever, if the particles grow large enough to overcome the nucleation barrier (explained in
the following and illustrated in Figure 4.1), they become stable aerosol particles and can
grow large enough to become CCN that can form cloud droplets.

4.1.3
Conditions in the Atmosphere
The discussion so far has involved only one substance, but as mentioned in the beginning
of this chapter, in the atmosphere most often two, three or more substances are involved
in the nucleation process. The most important and relevant binary nucleation process
in the atmosphere is that of sulfuric acid and water (H2SO4/H2O) and ternary processes
most often involve ammonia (NH3). The nucleation theory for binary and ternary systems
will not be elaborated on in this report, as the concept of the energy barrier from Figure
4.1 is similar.
The typical concentration range of gas-phase H2SO4 in the atmosphere is 106 −107 cm−3.
The concentrations vary with location, time of the day and outlet of SO2 which can be
both anthropogenic and natural. Ions are ubiquitous in the lower atmosphere and are
mainly produced by GCR, forming 1-40 ion pairs cm−3 s−1 depending on factors such
as altitude, latitude and the solar cycle. Above land the ionization is higher than above
ocean due to natural radioactivity from soils, and the maximum ionization is at altitudes
of ∼12 km.

4.2.3
Design of Experiments
The experiments were conducted by permitting ultraviolet (UV) light to enter the cham-
ber for 20 minutes at a time to generate H2SO4. The H2SO4 concentration depends on
the intensity of the UV light, thus, by varying the intensity between measurements, the
H2SO4 concentration was varied. Once sucient H2SO4 was present, nucleation started
and continued until sucient H2SO4 had decayed to losses to the chamber walls. The
nucleation rate was measured at the respective cuto diameters with the PSM and CPC.
The procedure usually lasted around 6-14 hours for a single run under xed gas condi-
tions, depending on the sulfuric acid concentration, because the system had to return to
its initial conditions before a new measurement was started. In between measurements
the ionization conditions were varied by changing the amount of lead shielding in front of
the sources. At least one hour before each measurement the lead bricks or lead curtains
were put in the right position to allow the ionization level to stabilize before the nucleation
starts.
The experiments were designed to obtain large variation and span in the parameters (ion-
ization and H2SO4 concentration) in a time ecient way. The concentration of nucleated
aerosols made in one measurement had to decrease to zero before the next measurement
started. This process took several hours and depended on the number of aerosols. That
puts an upper limit to the H2SO4 concentrations which were measured, because too high
concentrations yielded a particle count which took long time to decrease. The lower limit
of the H2SO4 concentrations was chosen based on the detection limit of the instrument.
The particle count had to be above the noise level of the instrument. The CPC with
cuto diameter of 4 nm was the limiting instrument since only very few aerosols managed
to grow suciently large before being lost to chamber walls at low H2SO4 concentrations.
Every fth measurement is performed as a reference experiment with a standard ioniza-
tion and UV intensity, to avoid unnoticed drift in parameters or instruments. A list of
settings and the number of measurements at each setting is presented in Table 4.3. In
between the measurements the PSM has to be drained (once a week) and water added
to the humidier (two times per week). For safety reasons, measurements using high
radiation levels were planned to be conducted while no one was working in the lab.

4.2.4
Losses and Uncertainties
The freshly nucleated particles are subject to losses to the chamber walls and to the
sides of the tubes leading them to the counters. In this study it is not feasible to fully
quantify all the losses involved. However, the systematic loss of aerosol particles in the
sampling system was estimated using the Particle Loss Calculater (PLC) developed by
von der Weiden et al. (2009). The loss depends on the dimensions of the sampling tubes,
their inclination, angle of curvature and the ow rate. The particle size also aects the
losses since more large particles than small ones are lost in bends and curves of the tubing
system. This is because the large particles have higher inertia and therefore a higher
tendency to keep moving straight ahead in stead of following the ow through the bend.
This type of loss is called inertial deposition. Since we measured small particles (< 4nm)
this eect was not considered important. Gravitational losses aect particles of size about
0.5µm and cause the particles to deposit on the lower most surface of the tube. This ef-
fect was not relevant either because of the size of the particles we are measuring. The
most important loss mechanism in the tubes is diusion on to the walls of the tubes.
Brownian motion of small particles (<100 nm) creates a net transport from high to low
concentrations. Because the particles deposit on the walls, the areas near the walls be-
come low concentration areas and diusion generates a constant net transport towards
the walls. Diusion was the only signicant loss mechanism in this case. In order to
minimize losses and simplify the calculations the sampling tubes were made as short as
possible and as similar as possible for the sampling for both PSM and CPC. The setup of
the PSM and CPC's is shown in Figure 4.3 including the dimensions of the sampling tubes.
The sizes of the freshly nucleated particles that were measured lie outside of the validity
range of the formulas used in the PLC and the result is taken as an approximation.
However, according to von der Weiden et al. (2009) is still useful.
The loss function
is shown in Figure 4.7 and it is seen that the losses of the 1.4 nm particles are ∼50%
whereas they are only ∼15% for the 4 nm particles detected by the CPC. The losses lead
to underestimation of the nucleation rate. Since the detection eciency of the counters
is not a step function at the cut-o diameter, particles with diameters below the cut-o

4.4
Discussion and Caveats
The increase in nucleation rate with H2SO4 concentration is slightly steeper than predicted
by the results from Dunne et al. (2016). This could be a result of an underestimation of
the H2SO4 concentration at the largest values. It might result from the measurements or
calibration of H2SO4 concentration or possibly from the interpolation from section 4.3.3.
The directly measured concentrations of H2SO4 are compared to the interpolated values
in Figure 4.17 to investigate any systematic eects from the interpolation. The gure
shows that there is no systematic underestimation of the interpolated values (diamonds)
relative to the measured values (dots).
The range of H2SO4 concentrations is narrow, with all measurements having concentra-
tions within one order of magnitude. This might cause the steep slope of the H2SO4
dependence. The review in Sipila et al. (2010, Table 2) have shown that lab experiments
have obtained a large range of values (from 2-8) and the results from this study (∼5) lies
well within this range.
There remain some missing pieces in order to fully account for the variables in atmospheric
nucleation processes. The most important is the temperature dependence. Results from
Dunne et al. (2016) report that the ion concentration dependence falls o with decreasing
temperature. It would be benecial for the study to have measurements at low tempera-
tures, relevant for the whole troposphere, especially at 3-5 km height where low clouds are

ment of NH3 and organic substances. In spite of the missing pieces we show a consistency
with the results from Dunne et al. (2016). The resulting parametrization of the nucle-

background GCR to nearby SN levels, was used in the following to scale nucleation rates

Modelling Aerosols Growth
This chapter presents the theory and issues related to growth of aerosols. It introduces
the model and model parameters that were used to calculate the eect of the increase
in aerosols on the number of CCN. A range of parameters were tested and the results
are presented for three specic cases. The model itself and the results obtained with the
model are discussed. The calculated increase in CCN is used in continuation (see chapter
6) to estimate the impact that the SN would have on Earth's climate.
5.1
Theory and Method
Nucleation leads to a molecular cluster of a size which is just stable in the given atmo-
spheric conditions and which acts as an aerosol particle. This particle can grow suciently
to become a CCN and thereby be able to inuence the climate. In most atmospheric con-
ditions a critical cluster has a size of about 1.5 nm while a CCN has a diameter of at least
>50-100 nm (Mironova et al., 2015). Thus, substantial growth and mutual coagulation
of new aerosol particles is required before an active CCN is formed.
5.1.1
Aerosol Growth
The aerosols grow by coagulation and condensation of sulfuric acid, water and other con-
densable species, like organics that are much more abundant in the atmosphere than
sulfuric acid. Figure 5.1 illustrates the growth process of an aerosol particle. The growth
depends on the concentrations of the condensing substances and the relative humidity,
and these concentrations need to be suciently large in order for the aerosols to grow
into CCN. During their growth there is the possibility that the small aerosols coagulate
onto large pre-existing aerosols, and thereby are lost. According to standard aerosols dy-
namics, the likelihood that a particle will grow suciently is low and decreasing with an
increased number of small aerosols. This is primarily because the many freshly nucleated
particles will have used up more of the growth material (e.g. H2SO4) and therefore be
in an environment with low concentrations of the material to grow from. This will cause
them to grow slowly, which leaves more time for loss to larger particles and other loss
mechanisms. Consequently, numerical simulations based on standard dynamics predict
that even if more aerosols are produced by enhanced ionization, the response in CCN is
low (Pierce and Adams, 2009; Snow-Kropla et al., 2011).

Experimental results by Svensmark et al. (2013) have shown that the relative increase
in aerosols produced by ions stays constant from critical cluster sizes to diameters larger
than 50 nm. The results were in line with the theory and numerical simulations if the
H2SO4 concentration is held constant. The contradiction between the standard aerosol
theory and experimental results suggests that there is still an unknown mechanism related
to ion induced nucleation and growth. It is well established that the presence of a charge
on an aerosol can accelerate the growth through electrostatic interactions. However, this
eect is negligible for clusters with diameters above 10 nm (Yu and Turco, 2001), and
does not explain the results seen in Svensmark et al. (2013). Some eect that results in
eectively no loss of H2SO4 despite increased nucleation needs to be investigated. There
has been a few attempts to explain the mechanism (e.g. (Bork et al., 2013)), but so far,
no accepted theory exist.
Whether an aerosol is activated and becomes a cloud droplet, does not only depend on
its size but also on the surrounding environment, especially the supersaturation. The
activation diameter at 0.2% supersaturation of water, representative of stratiform clouds,
is approximately 75-120 nm (Pierce and Adams, 2009). Thus, in the following, a CCN is
dened as a particle with a diameter above 75 nm. The growth rate of a new particle in
the atmosphere is of the order of a 1-20 nm per hour, but can be as low as 0.1 nm per
hour, depending on the environment (Kulmala et al., 2004). This means that the growth
time from small aerosols into CCN can be up to more than a week. The slowest GR are
seen above the ocean and since most of Earth is covered by ocean such conditions are
modelled in the following.

5.2
The Model
A full numerical model describing the interactions between aerosols, ions and condensible
gas is used to study the growth of aerosols particles under the atmospheric conditions
following a SN. The model describes the growth by coagulation, by condensation of gas
molecules onto the surface of the particles, and by the nucleation of new particles. It also
considers losses to walls in experimental situations and to larger aerosol particles in the
atmosphere. The parametrization presented in Chapter 4 is used to scale the nucleation
parameter for a given ionization.
The model consist of general aerosol dynamic equations including eects from ions via
electrostatic forces. They describe the formation and interaction of both positive and
negative ions, the interaction between the condensible gas (H2SO4) and the aerosols, and
the evolution of positive, negative and neutral aerosols of size denoted by index k. The
model calculates the aerosol distribution as a function of particle volume divided into
a number of discrete nodes (k). The nodes have logarithmic intervals ranging from 0.7
to 300 nm. As a part of this project the parameter describing the nucleation rate of
new particles is modied according to the results from section 4.3.6 and is given by the
parametrization:

Choice of parameters
When modelling the eects from the SN, rst, the atmosphere with ambient GCR ion-

Discussion and Caveats
The two ways of modelling loss during aerosol growth have shown to yield quite dierent
results. Loss to ambient aerosols such as sea salt particles, depends on the size of the
ambient particles because of the size-dependent coagulation coecient. In the atmosphere
the ambient particles will have a wide range of sizes (0.01-100 µm (Seinfeld and Pandis,
2006)) and the optimal model would represent this with a size distribution. This is not
possible in the model applied here, and case A shows the consequences of this, when
choosing an ambient aerosol size of 2 µm. The result is that the loss of large secondary
aerosols is underestimated because the coagulation coecient decreases with size faster
than the average of all represented primary aerosol sizes would. This is clear from Figure
5.4a, where the concentration of particles above ∼40 nm falls o very slowly. The param-
eters (sea salt diameter and concentration) were varied to test a wide range of ambient
particles sizes, but no size seemed to represent the average loss and yield results similar
to those obtained by other models.

The nal concentration of CCN is very dependent on the loss terms, which represent
the size distribution of ambient aerosols. Therefore, since this is undetermined a large

The concentrations of condensible gas is the most important parameter when estimating

The eectiveness of an aerosol particle to act as a CCN is not only a function of the

dominated by primary particles, thus having large losses, while concentrations in remote

In the simulation, there is only sea-salt ambient aerosol and H2SO4 is the only condens-

Impact on Earth's Climate
This chapter aims at estimating the impact of the increase in CCN, calculated in Chapter
5, on clouds and thereby on the global climate. There are many uncertainties related
to the microphysics of clouds, and the implications of these, on the estimated values of
temperature change, are discussed.
6.1
Theory
6.1.1
Radiative Forcing from Aerosols
When averaged globally, clouds have a net cooling eect on Earth's radiation budget
of about -27.7 W/m2 (Hartmann, 1993).
Generally, low clouds have a cooling eect
by reecting sunlight and high thin clouds have a warming eect by acting as a green-

forcing (ERBE observations (Hartmann, 1993)). In comparison, according to the IPPC,
the forcing from anthropogenic CO2 in the atmosphere is 1.68 W/m2 (Myhre et al., 2013).

The Cloud Lifetime Eect
The second indirect eect, the cloud lifetime eect, rst suggested by Albrecht (1989),
is caused by the fact that the activation of a larger number of CCN sets an upper limit
of the droplet size. Simply put, smaller droplets are lighter and therefore do not fall as
precipitation but stay in the atmosphere longer. Precipitation is a major sink for clouds
and when attenuated, cloud lifetime is enhanced and the overall fraction of cloudiness is
increased. This eect is estimated in Lohmann and Feichter (2005) to be roughly as large
as the albedo eect. However, estimates of the cloud lifetime eect are very uncertain and
depend on the model, thus the climatological signicance of this aspect of the indirect
eect needs a great deal more investigation (Lohmann and Feichter, 2005) (Penner, 2001).

the two indirect eects are of the same order of magnitude. Therefore:

6.3
Discussion and Caveats
As is evident from above, the impact on climate, from the increased number of CCN
is dicult to quantify. The estimates presented in Table 6.2 rely on assumptions and
approximations that may not always hold in the real atmosphere.
Some assumptions rely on present climatological conditions, thus, may not be valid 2.2
Ma. First, the equilibrium climate sensitivity, λ, includes feedback mechanisms when
coupling equilibrium temperature with forcing. The feedback mechanisms depend on the
state of Earth at the time of the forcing, because they include mechanisms such as melting
of ice, atmospheric composition, ect. The value of λ, as it is now, may therefore not be
a good estimate for the climate sensitivity 2.2 Ma. Generally, the atmospheric compo-
sition at the time, especially the concentration of H2SO4, is an important uncertainty.
As concluded in Chapter 4, the concentration of condensing gas is the most important
parameter for nucleation rates and thereby concentration of aerosols. One can speculate
that the concentrations were lower 2.2 Ma since the largest contributor to the SO2 in
today's atmosphere has anthropogenic origin (Penner, 2001), but volcanoes and biogenic
sources also contribute, and these might have been more eective back then. As this is
unknown, we can only assume that the concentration was similar to what is observed
today.
A main uncertainty in the overall radiative forcing change is that (at least when including
the ion eect on growth of the aerosols) we no longer deal with small perturbations in
the CCN concentration, but rather increases of up to 50%. In this case many of the
63
==PAGE:77==
assumptions break down, e.g. the assumption that the concentration of cloud droplets
correspond linearly to the concentration of CCN. At these CCN concentrations water can
become the limiting factor, and a smaller fraction of the CCN become activated. Conse-
quently, the implicit assumption that the forcings are additive may not be true. One can
imagine, that the indirect eect saturates at high CCN concentrations, either due to the
lack of water in the atmosphere or due to establishment of negative feedback loops.
Feedback loops could also be linked to some eects that are related to long wavelength
forcing. In particular, liquid-water clouds with more CCN are lighter and can reach higher
altitudes. As a consequence, they will be colder and emit less radiation into space, im-
posing a positive forcing. This eect is not included in the estimation.
The assumed linear relationship between ∆F and ∆Ts does not necessarily hold for radia-
tive forcing associated with aerosols because the nature of the response to a forcing can
depend on the vertical distribution of the forcing (Seinfeld and Pandis, 2006). Aerosols
can inuence the energy balance, by scattering and reecting solar radiation at dierent
altitudes. As a consequence, feedbacks that involve the atmospheric lapse rate can be
established, and these are complex and nonlinear. Thus, quantication of the eects of
the generated feedbacks, and aerosols in general, on climate is still limited and the level
of scientic understanding lacks detailed knowledge (Penner, 2001).
A large portion of the uncertainty in the CCN budget stems from largely unconstrained
estimates of how newly nucleated particles grow to sizes where they act as CCN. The
likelihood that small aerosol particle will grow to CCN size depends on the competition
between the rates of growth and loss processes and, as evidence suggest, the ambient
ionization. The growth and loss rates are size and location dependent. Because dierent
sources of aerosols (nucleation and primary emission) occur in dierent parts of the tro-
posphere, some parts, where conditions are favorable, may be more eective than others
at generating CCN. For example, areas with high concentrations of condensable gases
and low concentrations of primary CCN would maximize the growth of aerosols. Such
conditions are expected above the ocean, during daytime, where up to 70% of CCN are
formed by nucleation (Yu and Luo, 2009). In addition, the geographical location of the
clouds also determines the size of the forcing. This is largest above ocean as clouds here
have a larger relative eect on Earth's albedo than clouds above continents, because of
the low albedo of the ocean. These eects combined add to the credibility of the conclu-
sion of this report since we simulate conditions similar to those in the marine troposphere.
Since there are several variables whose uncertainties are of the order of >100 %, a quan-
titative assessment of the uncertainty of the ∆Ts estimates is not made. The estimates
can however be compared to results from other studies: The climate impacts discussed in
Marsh and Svensmark (2000) consider the sum of the indirect eects from the increased
ionization caused by the perturbations in GCR ux over a solar cycle and due to the
generally enhancing solar magnetic eld. In table III in Marsh and Svensmark (2000) the
estimates of radiative heating are summarized and an 11.2 % change in GCR lead to a
net change in forcing of ∼1.4 W/m 2. Since the eects from the SN are much larger
than those discussed in Marsh and Svensmark (2000), a direct scaling is inappropriate,
64
==PAGE:78==
but their results do contribute to the conclusion that if the ion mechanism does exist the
estimates from case C in table 6.1 are of the right order of magnitude.
As seen from case C in Table 6.1, according to standard aerosol dynamics the increase
in negative forcing from low clouds, resulting from the enhanced nucleation following the
SN, is -0.3 W/m2. Consequently the temperature would decrease by no more than 0.4 K,
although it depends on the climate sensitivity at the time. Compared to the cooling trend
seen in Figure 2.1 this would have little, if any, eect. The main reason for this is that the
CCN numbers are scarcely aected by changes in ionization in this type of model. If the
ion growth mechanism is included, the impact becomes much larger and although opposite
in sign, the forcing of -4 W/m2 corresponds to more than twice the estimated forcing from
CO2 in today's atmosphere (Collins et al., 2013). The resulting temperature change, with
all the uncertainties in mind, is comparable to the changes during the transitions from
Pliocene to Pleistocene period, seen in Figure 2.1. Therefore, if the ions help the growth
process of aerosols as is seen experimentally (Svensmark et al., 2013) and from correlations
between CR intensity and clouds (e.g. (Marsh and Svensmark, 2000; Shaviv and Veizer,
2003; Svensmark et al., 2016)), the single SN could impact the cloud cover and thereby
the global surface temperatures by up to a more than 5 K cooling for several hundred
years. The impact would decrease with time, but even 5,000 years after the event, the
cloud cover and thereby the climate would still be aected. A negative radiative forcing of
that order (-4 W/m2 ), that persists for hundreds of years would have large climatological
consequences and could cause abrupt climate changes.

Conclusion
This thesis investigates the eect of a single SN on Earth's cloud cover through the use
of theory, experiments, and models. Evidence suggests that a SN at 50 pc distance from
the Solar System occurred ∼2.2 Ma. This event is the focus of this thesis and its ef-
fects on the CR spectrum, atmospheric ionization, nucleation and growth of aerosols, and
thereby clouds and climate is quantied. The SN coincides with a period known as the
Pliocene-Pleistocene boundary, where the Earth experienced abrupt climate changes and
a general global cooling. The results from this project support the speculations that the
SN contributed to the climate changes through a mechanism involving CR.
The CR spectra resulting from a SN at 50 pc distance were calculated by assuming that
the CRs propagated through a weak and turbulent magnetic eld, such as the one ex-
pected inside the Local Bubble. The spectra, seen in Figure 2.5, show, that at the peak of
the CR increase the total intensity would be up to three orders of magnitude larger than
the present day background spectrum. The increase would fall o with time, and be back
to background levels after 30,000 years. The CRs penetrate the atmosphere and create
ion pairs on their way. The atmospheric ionization caused by the SN was calculated and
the results are showed in Figure 3.2. These showed that the ion concentration of the
atmosphere would increase by up to a factor four in the rst few hundred years following
the event and the largest increase is in the lower troposphere.
Ions help the nucleation of secondary aerosols. The process of nucleation was studied ex-
perimentally to quantify the role of the high ionization levels. Nucleation was measured
from background ionization levels to levels that slightly exceed the maximum ionization
expected from a SN and at gas concentrations slightly above atmospheric levels. The
results, in Figure 4.16, show that the nucleation of particles of 1.4 nm in diameter could
be increased up to almost an order of magnitude by increasing the ionization from lowest
to highest level.
The experimental results were used to scale the eects of various ionization levels. The
increased atmospheric ionization following the SN would lead to higher nucleation rates
for up to 10,000 years. The maximum increase would occur in the rst 300 years after the
SN, where nucleation rates in the troposphere would be approximately tree times higher
than usual. Thus, three times as many particles that could have the potential to grow to
CCN sizes (∼50-100 nm) and aect cloud processes, would be formed. The higher num-
ber of small particles would result in a more competitive environment where the particles
would grow slower because the amount of condensible gas, from which they grow, per
66
==PAGE:80==
particle would be lower. Consequently, more of them would be lost to scavenging before
they become CCN. In addition, the increased number of aerosols would lead to higher
coagulation losses. Therefore, according to standard aerosol theory, the large increase in
aerosol concentration would only result in a small increase in CCN.
The growth of aerosols was studied using a full numerical box model that was modied
with results from the experiments. The model results, presented in Table 5.1, show that,
as expected from the prevailing aerosol theory, the maximum increase in CCN following
the SN would be of the order of ∼2.5%. Generally, an increase in CCN enhances the
reectivity of clouds primarily by making them more white and prolonging their lifetime,
thereby increasing the total amount of clouds. The subtle enhancement in CCN con-
centration could under the given assumptions cause a negative radiative forcing of -0.3
W/m2. According to the present day climate sensitivity this is insignicant relative to
the general cooling 2-3 Ma. The calculated radiative forcings and temperature changes
are presented in Table 6.1 and Table 6.2.
However, if there exists a mechanism involving ions that help the aerosols grow, as has
been suggested by experimental results, the consequences of the SN would be much larger.
The mechanism was included in the modelling by simulation of a constant concentration
of condensible gas. According to the box model, in this case, the concentration of sec-
ondary CCN would double, leading to a radiative forcing of -4 W/m2 and equilibrium
surface temperature decrease in the range -1.2 to -5.6 K. The eects from the SN would
decrease with time, and return to pre-SN levels after ∼10,000 years as the ionization levels
returned to normal. Such cooling could have contributed to the general climate change
that is seen 2-3 Ma and could have caused a period of abrupt cooling such as the one seen
in Africa around the same time. Thus, the ion growth mechanism is very important for
determining the climatological eects of the SN.
In summary, by using the full chain of results from changes in the CR spectrum to
changes in CCN the eect a of a nearby SN on Earth's climate was estimated.
The
results contribute to the speculations that the most energetic explosions in our galactic
neighbourhood, the SNe, in addition to enriching the solar system with heavier elements,
also helped shape the evolution of life on Earth by aecting past climate.
7.1
Outlook
Many aspects of this thesis could be improved or expanded on in further work. Many
suggestions for improvements were mentioned in the discussion following each chapter,
but some of the main points and some additional ones will be presented in the following.
A thorough review of the climate and state of the atmosphere at the time of the SN would
benet the analysis. A review of the state of the art knowledge about the transition from
the Plioscene to the Pleistocene would provide a better overview of missing pieces and
thereby how the SN theory ts in. Also, a review of the uncertainty related to sediment
samples would allow for a better analysis of the timing of the cooling and the SN, and
67
==PAGE:81==
how these two match. As mentioned in the discussion of the CR spectrum, by including
a time dependent source term the temporal evolution of the increase in the CR intensity
would become more accurate.
Regarding the experimental part of the project, several extensions could benet the re-
sults: 1) The CI-APi-TOF mass spectrometer needs calibration.
If this is done, the
absolute uncertainty on the sulfuric acid concentrations would be reduced dramatically.
2) Adding the extra dimensions of temperature, NH3, and organics to the measurements
would result in a more complete parametrization and an even better ability to compare
the results with Dunne et al. (2016).
It would be very interesting to measure the concentration of aerosols all the way up to
CCN sizes under high ionization levels. This requires high stability of ionization and
ambient gasses over the growth time which lasts from a few days to more than a week.
This level of stability is hard to achieve but has been achieved before by Svensmark et al.
(2013). The results from such an experiment would add to the evidence for the ion growth
mechanism and serve as comparison for model results, which would in turn improve the
model.
The numerical model could be modied to include: 1) A variable H2SO4 concentration
that follows the cycle of photochemical production. 2) A dynamic nucleation rate that is
updated based on the actual concentration of H2SO4 and ions and the parametrization
developed in this thesis. 3) A size distribution of ambient aerosol particles for the loss
calculations. These three modications would improve the model and make it resemble
the true atmosphere even more. Alternatively, the growth could be studied with global
aerosol models, such as the GEOS-Chem model.
However, the most important step to fully quantify the eect that a SN would have on
global climate is to understand the role of ions in aerosol growth.
