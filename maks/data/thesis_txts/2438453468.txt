1. INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
Innovation plays a signiﬁcant role in the evolution of technology and various industries, paving the
foundation for progress in several areas. At the national level, innovation drives economic growth and
is central to knowledge-based economies. One of the key determinants for innovation at a national level
is the innovation infrastructure, which encompasses the science and technology policies of the country,
as well as the country’s ability to support research in diﬀerent R&D labs and knowledge institutes
such as universities (Furman and Porter, 2001).
While innovation infrastructure is important at a national level, this increased focus on innovation
translates to the nation’s ﬁrms, since a nation’s competitive abilities depend on the innovative capacity
of its ﬁrms (Porter and Stern, 1998). When referring to the innovative capacity, we are focusing on the
ﬁrms’ potential to create commercially viable innovations (Furman and Porter, 2001). The innovative
output depends on the ﬁrms’ abilities and resources to explore potential ideas, and the innovative
capacity relies on many determinants such as the culture of the ﬁrm, internal processes, and the
external environment (Neely and Hii, 2014). The process of innovating requires not only competencies
to generate and create ideas but also competencies that support in the commercialization of the ideas
like marketing, business management and so on (Fagerberg, 2006). The commercial sphere surrounding
ﬁrms make them the most likely places where innovation can take place (Rogers, 2010).
This leads to the discussion on what can be done to improve the innovative capacity of a ﬁrm.
As mentioned earlier, one of the key determinants identiﬁed under innovation infrastructure was uni-
versities. Universities are interesting as they serve as a "bridge between technology and companies"
(Porter and Stern, 1998). Universities have been known to drive innovation by providing ﬁrms with
suﬃciently skilled human resources and by supporting ﬁrms with infrastructure and R&D support
(Cohen et al., 2002; Mansﬁeld, 1991; Pavitt, 1991; Salter and Martin, 2001). The emphasis of gov-
ernments on utilizing public research for gains in the innovative capacity of a nation has resulted in
encouraging university-industry collaborations (Barnes et al., 2002). Universities are required to be
actively involved in applied research which can have commercial applications while also maintaining a
balance in pursuing basic research (Feller and Geiger, 1993).
While there is plenty of research that explores the nature of this university-industry relationship
and its potential beneﬁts, our research aims to contribute to the ﬁeld by providing quantitative insights
of these beneﬁts of collaboration for the innovative capacity of ﬁrms. A lot of literature agrees that
patent data is a viable option for assessing innovation (Acs et al., 2002; Trajtenberg, 1990; Burhan
et al., 2017; Ikeuchi, 2017). Patent applications and grants can show trends about innovation activity
(Crosby, 2000).
Based on this area, the following research question has been outlined:
• Do successful collaborations with universities yield a positive impact on a ﬁrm’s innovative ca-
pacity?
We answer this question, by breaking the question down into the following sub-questions:
• What is the trend in a company (before and after the collaboration) in terms of:
– Patent ﬁlings?
– Patent diversity?
By acquiring a list of companies that collaborated with the Technical University of Denmark (DTU)
over research and inventions, we were able to use their patenting activity to study the impacts of
collaboration through a statistical approach. The pattern in a company’s way of ﬁling for a patent,

by providing quantitative insights of the beneﬁts of collaboration. The results are meant to facilitate
discussion on how university-industry collaborations can be improved.
Overview of the thesis
The thesis will begin by exploring relevant literature on university-industry collaborations and the
potential beneﬁts for a ﬁrm in collaborating with a university in the literature review. It also explores
innovation indicators and the selection of patents as an indicator in this study. The choices made in
the thesis and strategic approach to the analysis are then discussed in the methodology part of the
thesis. The data sampling chapter displays the data that was collected along with the processes and
methods that were used in collecting it. The next chapter combines the results and the analysis to give
a detailed report of the various analyses conducted and the ﬁndings from each of them. We then start
the discussion where we discuss the key ﬁndings from the study and possibilities of future research.
Finally, we end with a conclusion that summarizes the entire article.

2. LITERATURE REVIEW
2
Literature Review
In this section, relevant work in the ﬁeld of literature will be discussed to provide a theoretical foun-
dation for our research. Since we are interested in studying the impact of university-industry collabo-
rations on innovative capacity, the section explores the topic of the role of universities as a driver for
innovative capacity of ﬁrms. The section also covers the beneﬁts or motivations for both organizations
as well as elements that make university-collaboration successful. This section also covers literature
about measuring innovation in order to choose an appropriate indicator.
Companies throughout the world all have diﬀerent goals that they want to achieve.
For some
companies, diversifying their product portfolio is an important objective, while other companies may
be more focused on improving their products and services.
Irrespective of their goal, innovation
is a powerful tool that can help either improving eﬃciency in the industries or solve many of the
challenges that companies may be facing, such as gaining a competitive edge over other companies in
their ﬁelds (Cavusgil et al., 2003). Hence, innovation is increasingly being made an integral part of
many companies’ processes (Dodgson et al., 2008). Companies can co-operate with a wide variety of
stakeholders either inside or outside their industry. Some of the common partners can be other ﬁrms
(even competitors), suppliers, customers, public research centres, and universities. Collaboration is
seen as an important approach to improve innovative capacity by stimulating transfer of knowledge
and experience across sectors (Inzelt, 2004; Perkmann et al., 2011).
On the other hand, the interest of governments to spur economic development by nations has re-
sulted in the emergence of new roles for universities in society in recent decades such as technology
transfer roles (Lee, 1996). The pressure from governments in general, combined with the need for
companies for external sources for innovation has led to increased collaborative eﬀorts. From an inno-
vation point of view, the interaction between companies and universities is beneﬁcial to both of them.
University-industry collaborations are furthermore of particular interest due to the complementary
roles of each organization. Universities oﬀer primary resources like technology expertise and data in
exchange for company resources like funding, equipment, market access, etc. (Perkmann et al., 2013).
Working with companies oﬀer many beneﬁts to universities. Members of the industry will typically
support the research through funding or new equipment. This type of support greatly helps universities
improving their own research and the quality of their work. Furthermore, the collaboration between
companies and universities is a crucial linkage for knowledge transfer, as mentioned before. One of the
most important types of knowledge transfer is the practical knowledge that student-graduates at the
universities receive by getting exposed to the industry either through industry-related projects or by
being hired by a company in the industry. This relationship, in turn, beneﬁts the industry since the
graduates will be better equipped with knowledge about the industry, once they graduate. University
faculty oﬀering consultancy by taking on various research problems in the industry is yet another type
of knowledge transfer that beneﬁts both the universities and companies.
From a company’s perspective, the beneﬁts are signiﬁcant as these beneﬁts inﬂuence its innovative
capacity. Companies can rely on the public science from universities to solve various problems when
it comes to commercializing their products or inventions (Baba et al., 2009). A special characteris-
tic about collaborating with universities is their diversity in research groups and research potential
(Segarra-Blasco and Arauzo-Carod, 2008). For industries, it is a valuable way of exploring new tech-
nology and its commercial applications.
According to (Baba et al., 2009) the commercialization of sciences is a one-way knowledge transfer,
where the companies draw upon knowledge from universities for their own commercial gain. With that
being said, companies themselves do not have an inﬁnite capacity to receive information. Research
has shown that the absorptive capacity of a company is related to its own ﬁrm’s employees; and even

the collaboration between universities and industries does not necessarily turn into a success if the
company does not have the adequate absorptive capacity (Baba et al., 2009).
This is also why a
collaboration with universities can be a great asset to companies. Continuous collaboration between
the universities and companies can result in mutual trust between the researchers, which improves
their co-operation even further (Gulati and Gargiulo, 1999). With new ways of thinking and tackling
various problems, universities are able to solve problems in a diﬀerent light which can improve the
work of the researchers in the industry.
Studies that also mention that universities and companies need to share similar competencies and
capabilities in order for a collaboration to become successful (Petruzzelli, 2011). Antonio Petruzzelli
however also points out that too similar competencies and capabilities in the same ﬁelds may be
detrimental for innovation, since innovation is usually the result of complementary knowledge from
other ﬁelds or areas that the company may not have focused on previously. With a wide span of codiﬁed
knowledge, universities are able to complement the companies’ own research with new thinking, which
can result in the commercialization of new inventions.
Technology transfer is also particularly interesting, since it ties closely in with knowledge transfer.
Some of the key activities in technology transfer lie within giving guidance to companies developing
new products or services, helping entrepreneurial start-ups, or providing patent and licensing services
(Santoro, 2000). This technology transfer also helps in commercializing new technologies and bringing
new ideas and improvements out into the industry. It is especially important for companies in the
industries, since a technology transfer helps them solve technology-related problems and the collabo-
rations even help them create new technologies that can help them stay competitive or even gain a
competitive edge (Santoro, 2000). Studies have shown that knowledge spillovers from universities also
have a signiﬁcant impact on the product innovation of companies (Maietta, 2015), which is why there
is a lot to be gained for a company by collaborating with a university.
The literature shows that ﬁrms that diversify into technological areas, and that share a common
knowledge-base with their current portfolio, tend to beneﬁt from this diversiﬁcation (Leten et al., 2007).
Added knowledge from other related ﬁelds can help the company gain additional knowledge for its
established technologies. The more related the new technology ﬁelds are to the pre-existing technology
ﬁelds, the more reduced the costs are for coordinating their new portfolio among all their technology
ﬁelds (Leten et al., 2007). Additionally, collaborating with universities can help the company diversify
into new technological areas (Santoro and Saparito, 2003).There should also be a clear diﬀerentiation
between larger and smaller companies.
Larger companies are more likely to either be present in
many diﬀerent technology ﬁelds, or they have the economic means to venture into new ﬁelds. Smaller
companies, however, are more restricted due to the costs of investing in new types of projects outside
of one’s portfolio (Leten et al., 2007; Lin and Chen, 2005).
With knowledge and technology transfer between universities and companies being of particular
interest to this study and related to the innovative capacity of companies, it’s important to also
determine ways to assess the innovative capacity. As mentioned before, the innovative capacity is the
company’s potential to create commercially viable innovations in the form of inventions (Furman and
Porter, 2001; Neely and Hii, 2014).
While innovation may be diﬃcult to measure due to its diverse aspects (Ikeuchi, 2017), research has
shed light on diﬀerent types of indicators of innovation. J. Hagedoorn and M. Cloodt suggest several
diﬀerent indicators that can be used to measure innovative performance in companies (Hagedoorn and
Cloodt, 2003). Among the many diﬀerent indicators that have been studied such as patent counts,
R&D inputs, annual reports, surveys, etc., it was concluded that there weren’t any major diﬀerences
between them (Hagedoorn and Cloodt, 2003). While there is some controversy regarding using patent
counts as a measure of innovation (Burhan et al., 2017; Spiro, 2017), literature overall indicates that

3. METHODOLOGY
3
Methodology
This section describes the methodological process that has been used to answer the research questions.
It explains the steps that are involved while discussing the strategic choices and thinking behind each
step in the process.
Figure 1 serves as a visual representation and guide of this process.
Figure 1: The 3 phases of the methodology.
The study was scoped to DTU, which is classiﬁed as a "technical university". In Denmark and
many countries, this is a type of university that has an increased focus on engineering, technology and
applied sciences. Therefore, companies that collaborate with DTU are mostly technologically oriented
companies that focus on technical innovations. It allows us to use innovative capacity indicators like

patent data eﬀectively, since patent data is one of the better suited innovation measurement indicator
for technology innovations (Acs et al., 2002). Scoping this study to DTU ensures a possibility of a
decent sample size of companies compared to other universities. The reasons behind the time span are
explained in detail in the ﬁrst phase of this section.
From a holistic perspective of the thesis, the methodology can be broadly classiﬁed into 3 phases

3.1
Phase 1: Identifying collaborating companies
University-industry collaborations take several forms which Bonarccorsi, A. and Piccaluga, A. try
to capture in a broad framework (Bonaccorsi and Piccaluga, 1994).
The framework ranges from
low organizational involvement which includes consultancy or guest lectures, to high organizational
involvement such as university—industry co-operative research centers. Each type of these collabora-
tions have diﬀerent outcomes with the most common being research outcomes through inventions and
research articles. Therefore, a collaboration can be in the form of an invention but it can also be in
the form of a collaborative journal or book. In this thesis, we look at the joint involvement of the two
organizations through the research outcomes of these patents and publications. There can be many
other types of collaborations such as in the R&D phases of a company, however information like this
is conﬁdential and thus diﬃcult to retrieve, especially in a quantitative approach where the data of
many companies needs to be retrieved. Patents and publications are by far one of the most accessible
and reliable indicators that can be retrieved easily in large quantities.
The collaborating companies that are being focused on are the companies that have diﬀerent
types of collaborations with DTU. For this purpose, a patent is an excellent indicator of this type
of collaboration, since it is well documented and can be used in a quantiﬁable manner. It’s freely
available to the public through various databases online and it also shows the collaborating applicants
in meta data.
However, not all inventions are patentable, and if the focus is only on one university (DTU), it
might be diﬃcult to ﬁnd a sizable data sample. Therefore, we found it necessary to take diﬀerent
types of collaborations into consideration. While patents mostly focus on inventions, other types of
collaborations can result in publications such as books, journals, etc. By combining the data from
these co-publications with the data from the patent databases, a sizable data sample can be generated
for the analyses at the later stages.
Co-patenting is deﬁned as a patent by two or more applicants and all of them are noted in the
applicant ﬁeld of a patent. It implies a collaborative eﬀort in the development of the invention and
hence can be justiﬁed as an indicator for collaboration. Co-publishing is similar to the concept of co-
patenting but with respect to collaborative eﬀorts while publishing research articles, journals, books,
etc.
As mentioned, there are several ways to identify these kind of collaborations and the collaborating
companies. By far the most eﬃcient approach was to use online databases. Many extensive databases

exist on both patents and publications, and the 5 databases ’Espacenet’, ’EP full-text search’ (EPAB),
’Global Patent Index’ (GPI), ’Scopus’, and ’Web of Science’ (WoS), were all free to use and allowed
us to retrieve data in large quantities, making them a rich and valuable data source for this study.
Furthermore, these databases also contained the relevant meta-data.
We chose these databases to retrieve as many company names as possible, since they cover both local
national patent oﬃces, European Patent Oﬃce (EPO), and World Intellectual Property Organization
(WIPO) combined. Using several databases also ensured that additional patents with collaborating
companies were retrieved, which the other databases may not have been able to retrieve. This helped
us make the process eﬀective. It’s important to note that not every collaboration results in a patent.
Some collaborations do not result in inventions that fulﬁll the patenting criteria of various patent
oﬃces or some companies may decide not to ﬁle for a patent for a number of reasons. Therefore, in
order to extract even more types of collaborations with diﬀerent companies, publications were included
in the data sample as well.
To get a sample of collaborating companies, we search for companies that collaborated speciﬁcally
with DTU and in the time span of 2005 to 2016. The years were selected due to changes in patent
ownership regulations that were implemented in 2001 by the Danish government. Prior to 2001, patent
rights could be owned by individual researchers (Retsinformation, 1999), thus, it is diﬃcult to identify
collaboration links between organizations and universities since patent data would not display DTU’s
involvement in the patent. Not having this concrete link implies that we cannot analyse the proper
impacts of collaboration on the innovative capacity of companies eﬀectively, and thus not use patents
to identify collaborating companies. We exclude the years up to 2004 because of a combination of
the lag in implementation of the law plus the 18 month application period that a patent could take
until it was published. Both factors make data collected prior to 2005 unreliable for the purpose of
the study. Since the goal was to get an overview of all collaborating companies by using both patents
and publications as an indicator of collaboration, an identical time span was used for the publications
as well.
3.2
Phase 2: Finding necessary data
At this phase, we need to ﬁnd an indicator that can adequately measure the innovative capacity of the
companies that we have collected for the entire time span.
Patents were chosen as our indicator for innovative capacity, based on several reasons. One reason
is because the patent data is freely available in various online databases, and the patent data has a
standardized structure that allows us to more accurately compare between the diﬀerent patents. The
availability of patent data also allows us to accumulate a large amount of data to use for our samples.
Finally, using patents ensures novelty of the inventions that are ﬁled, since a requirement by the
various patent oﬃces is that the inventions have a degree of novelty.
Since this study will be focusing on the collaboration between DTU, which is a technical university,
most of the companies that it collaborates with will be technical companies. These companies are
expected to invent and have technical innovation outputs.
The Organization for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) HAN and Citations database
is used for this purpose since they oﬀer a rich amount of patent data from WIPO, EPO and United
States Patent and Trademark Oﬃce (USPTO) for a large number of companies. Additionally, OECD
HAN database provides a unique identity called Harmonised Applicant Names (HAN) Identiﬁer for
each company based on some level of name normalization. It makes it more eﬃcient for us to attribute
the patent data to a speciﬁc company without losing patent data due to name variations of companies.
Only the patent number (also called publication number) and the year of publishing of the patent is
of interest since we want to have the number of patents on a yearly basis for each company.

Additionally, the years of collaborations as well as the earliest collaboration point is required for
each of the companies. The collaboration years for the companies that collaborated over patents are
retrieved from patent databases previously used to get the sample. The application years are used to
represent a single collaboration over an invention. To avoid getting duplicate collaboration years for
a single invention we ensured that patents are grouped according to their priority numbers. By using
the application year, we are able to get as close to the real collaboration point as possible.
For the collaborations done over publications, we use the publication year since it is the only date
available. Comparing the collaboration year done over patents and publication, we can identify the
earliest collaboration point depending on which year is earliest.
One of the research questions posed for this thesis is to identify the diversity of the patents, and to
analyse, if the companies would start patenting in other technology ﬁelds compared to the years before
their ﬁrst collaboration with DTU. In order to look into the diversity of companies patenting, the
International Patent Classiﬁcation (IPC) categorization was included in the data by categorizing as
many patents as possible under the IPC Concordance Table developed by World Intellectual Property
Organization (WIPO) (WIPO, 2009).
3.3
Phase 3: The analysis
Assumptions
In order to conduct the analysis, a number of assumptions are made. Most of the assumptions have
been covered in the respective sections, but the sub-section below provides an overview of some of the
important assumptions for clarity purposes.
Given that we used data only in the time span from 2005 to 2016, it means that a company in our
sample could have had a collaboration prior to the year 2005. We however assume that early years in
the time span of the study display negligible impact on the patenting activity of those collaborations
priors to years being studied. Along those lines, we also consider that informal collaborations that have
no documented output like publications or patents have negligible impact on the patenting activity of
a company.
The companies are grouped by their subsidiaries while analyzing their patenting activity. Subsidiary
companies usually have strong links at a company and departmental level. There is knowledge ﬂow
internally and resources are shared. By grouping the companies, we can eliminate the internal factors
that inﬂuence innovative capacity and get a better understanding of the impacts of collaboration on
the company as a whole.
We perform the analysis on a yearly based time-frame. This means that patenting activity and
collaborations are noted on a year-level, so months and days are not taken into consideration during
the analysis. For displaying the companies’ patenting activity, the publication year of a patent is used.
Using publication year implies that only published patents are used for the analysis. Since a published
patent has to pass the test of novelty at the patent oﬃce, we can consider the patent data to be more
representative of an innovation.
On the other hand, collaborations done over patents (or inventions) are represented by the appli-
cation year of that patent. The application year is chosen since it is the earliest possible date that
comes close to representing the real collaboration point, which would normally be months before the
ﬁrst patent application. Additionally, a single collaboration year represents a single invention and not
just a single patent. Therefore patents are grouped by their priority number before the application
year is retrieved.
For the beginning of the Analysis Section 5, we use a sample of non-collaborating companies as a
control group. The companies that are not present in the sample of collaborating companies but are
present in OECD list of companies make up the sample. This of-course meant that non-collaborating
companies were more speciﬁcally companies that did not collaborate with DTU and/or not in the

time span. We assume that since DTU has such a large number of collaborating companies, the non-
collaborating company sample gives a fair representation of non-collaborators. Additionally, we also
go a step further to retrieve non-collaborating companies based in Denmark to get an even better
representation of non-collaborators. The reasoning behind it is that if a company does not collaborate
with a DTU which is present in its local area, the likelihood of collaborating it with other universities
are low. And thus, the sample will give a fair representation of non-collaborators.
Methodology behind the T-tests
To start oﬀthe analysis, a series of T-tests are conducted to derive initial inferences from the data.
T-tests are a great starting point as they show the diﬀerences between the means of two samples.
The means are compared through a t-value that takes into consideration variance within the group.
The Welch two sample T-test and the Paired T-test are the two T-tests that are used. The Welch
two sample T-test is an appropriate T-tests because it is an adapted method of the Student’s T-
test for when the two samples have unequal variances and unequal sample sizes which is often the
case with our samples (Welch, 1947). By default, the null-hypothesis for the T-tests assumes that
there is no signiﬁcant diﬀerence between the means of the two groups being compared. Therefore, a
standard statistical signiﬁcance value of 5% or lower will reject the null-hypothesis, and is thus used
as a threshold for the tests.
The T-test analysis is started by comparing the sample of collaborating companies against a control
group of non-collaborating companies. A Welch Two Sample independent T-test was selected since the
samples being compared are of unequal sample sizes. Another comparison is done against a control
group of non-collaborating list of companies based in Denmark. This control group is considered to
be more representative of non-collaborators since if a company is located in Denmark and does not
collaborate with DTU, the likelihood of it not collaborating with any other university will be low.
In an attempt to analyse the impacts of collaboration more clearly, we create an adjusted sample
that has multinational companies removed. Multinational companies have a number of factors that
contribute to their high patenting behaviour. These companies are also often collaborating with several
other universities and institutes. Hence, including them may skew the data in the sample when studying
DTU’s impact on the companies’ innovative capacity. Therefore, this test is redone after removing
outliers in the adjusted sample.
To decide the outlier companies in the sample, two approaches
are used. In the ﬁrst approach, we limit the sample to the ﬁrst 3 quartiles of the total number of
patents, assuming that collaborators within the 75% of the patents serve as a good representation
of collaborators. In the second approach, the company names are arranged according to their total
number of patents. The list is then manually scrutinized to check for the presence of multinational
companies to help decide the limit of the cutoﬀ. The approach is subjective since there is no clear
indicator of multinational companies from the data we collected and consequently, no clear boundary
line where multinational companies end in the list. Moreover, the total number of patents is also
dependent on the type of industry and hence even international companies with a lower total number
of patents can remain in the adjusted sample. Nonetheless, the approach serves as validation for the
previous approach of using the ﬁrst three quartiles. The adjusted sample is used to validate the results
of the analyses methods in the future.
The next several T-tests are chosen to examine the population of the collaborating companies to
get a better idea of the impact of collaboration. We begin by examining whether companies that
collaborated on or before 2010 perform better than companies that collaborated later than 2010. The
logic behind this test is that, by comparing companies that collaborate early on in the time span with
companies that collaborated later in the time span, it is possible to get an indication of whether the
timing of collaboration plays a role in the overall patenting activity of a company. The year 2010 was
selected as the cut-oﬀpoint between early and late collaborating companies since it falls in the middle

==PAGE:17==

of the time span and allows us to get a reasonable sample size to represent both groups. The Welch
Two Sample independent T-test was used for this purpose to compare the unequal sample sizes.
Another aspect that is interesting to examine, is the inﬂuence of frequency of collaborations with
the eﬀect of ﬁrm patenting activity. This is carried out by comparing the companies that collaborated
many times versus companies that collaborated a few times. To diﬀerentiate between the companies
that collaborated many times versus the companies that collaborated few times, we use two approaches.
For the ﬁrst approach, the median of the number of collaborations is used as the limit to separate the
sample, while for the second method, the mean is used. The number of collaborations here is the
total of the number of both co-patents and co-publications. By using two diﬀerent cut-oﬀvalues to
diﬀerentiate between low and high collaborating companies, a more signiﬁcant insight can be achieved
in order to understand whether the number of collaboration play a role in patenting activity. The results
pave the way for the linear regression models, to examine for a relationship between the number of
collaborations and patenting activity.
It is then followed by some Paired T-tests to compare the patenting activity before collaboration
with the patenting activity after collaboration. In order to do the comparison eﬀectively, the years
before and after have to be the same. Given that diﬀerent companies collaborate for the ﬁrst time in
diﬀerent years, we calculate the total number of patents for ﬁxed spans before and after collaboration,
in order to compare them. A 5-year span and 3-year span before and after is chosen for this analysis.
If needed, a 4-year span comparison will be conducted. The year of collaboration is excluded from
the analysis in the 5-year span comparison while the 2 years immediately after collaboration are left
out in the 3-year comparison.
The year of collaboration is excluded since it is not clear whether
the patents in the collaboration year fall before or after collaboration.
Besides the fact that we
are using years and not the exact dates from data, it is also extremely diﬃcult to identify a clear
collaboration point. Collaboration is indicated by the application date of a patent or publication date
(for a publication) which could be months if not years after the actual collaboration began.
The
two years after collaboration are excluded in the 3-year span comparison because we expect to see the
eﬀects of collaboration more signiﬁcantly after a lag. Ideally, it is best to exclude the years immediately
before and after collaboration but since our total span is 11 years, it is diﬃcult to do so without losing
a signiﬁcant amount of data.
The test described above only tells half the story. It examines the sum of patents before and after
collaboration, but does not account for the inconsistency/ﬂuctuations in patent ﬁlings on a yearly
basis. Therefore, we also do a Paired T-test to compare the average patents ﬁled per year in the years
before collaboration against the average patent per year in the years after the ﬁrst collaboration.
We then shift the level of analysis to check for diﬀerences between companies that collaborated over
patents and companies that collaborated over publications. The ﬁrst T-test in this section compares
companies that collaborated only over patents versus those that collaborated only over publications.
The intention of this test is to check if the method of collaboration impacts overall patenting activity.
A 5-year span before and after Paired T-test is considered for the sub-samples of companies only co-
patented and the companies that only co-published. By comparing the years after collaboration with
the years before for companies that only co-patented or co-published, it is possible to examine which
medium of collaboration has a signiﬁcant impact on patenting.
Similarly, the Welch Two Sample independent T-test is used to compare the sample of companies
that co-published (and also possibly co-patented) against those companies that did not. The T-test is
then repeated, but this time to compare all companies that co-patented against those companies that
did not. The purpose of these T-test are the same as the above, which is to check if the method of
collaboration is a factor while analyzing impact of collaboration. The main diﬀerence in these T-tests
are that they account for the companies that collaborated over both patents and publications. Thus,
enabling us to account for the potential eﬀects of co-patenting and co-publishing on our sample data.

Finally, as a parallel analysis of the eﬀects of collaboration on the technology ﬁelds, we performed
a couple of Paired T-tests. The ﬁrst T-test examines the number of technology ﬁelds of the patents
ﬁled in a 5-year span before collaboration and compares them with the technology ﬁelds in the 5-years
after collaboration. Note that the technology ﬁelds is counted on a yearly basis and is the unique
number of technology ﬁelds for the patents in a particular year. The technology ﬁelds of the patents in
the year of collaboration are taken into account in the before or after situation as it is not clear if the
patent lies before or after. This T-test is performed to study the diﬀerence in the number of unique
technology ﬁelds the company had patented.
An extension of this analysis is to then examine the spread of the technology ﬁelds after collabo-
ration. In order to get an overview of the expansion into new technology ﬁelds over the time span,
we carry out a second Paired T-test that compares the number of unique technology ﬁelds in the
5-year span before collaboration to compare with the number of unique technology ﬁelds present over
the total 10-year (5 years before plus the 5 years after) time span. The data during this test will be
arranged so as to only display a count when a company patents in a new technology ﬁeld. The test
examines the expansion of the company’s patenting activity into new technology ﬁelds.

Co-Publications as a source of collaborators
This is the most crucial and ﬁrst step in our thesis, since the number of companies extracted determines
the accuracy of the analysis. The publications are recorded through databases like Scopus and WoS,
which are among the most widespread databases in diﬀerent scientiﬁc ﬁelds (Guz, 2009). The con-
tributors to a publication are co-authoring research publications and are described in the publication
aﬃliation. We deﬁne such publications as co-publications and are for this study viewed as indicators
of collaboration.
For our study, we identiﬁed all publications aﬃliated with DTU. In Scopus, the organizations are
entered under the aﬃliations sections. Web of Science has a diﬀerent structure with the Author and
Reprint address columns holding organization information.
We gathered all collaborating entities and ﬁltered out university and institution names. The names
were then cleaned for misspellings and ambiguity, to retrieve the companies that collaborated. An
additional challenge that is special to the publication databases is the presence of address data along
with the company names. This posed a challenge, since the data does not necessarily start with the
company name. We overcame the challenge with a two stage method: The ﬁrst stage involved split-
ting the column that contained collaborating companies into its diﬀerent entry names and extracting
company names that had the typical company post-ﬁx like AS, APS, CO etc. (for a full list, see
Section 8.2). In the second stage we compared the remaining names and matched the names with
a OECD database named 201709_HAN_NAMES which contains company names. Thus addresses
could be removed. Matching with the OECD database also helped at the later stages when the com-
pany names are matched with the HAN identiﬁers, which are used to extract the patents related to
the speciﬁc companies.
Scopus Searching for DTU in Scopus gave a total of 49762 results on Scopus, and 1961 unique
company names, making it the largest data extraction for this project. Because of its comprehen-
sive nature, we found it to be a very useful source, especially when it came to ﬁnding collaborative
companies. The organization-enhanced feature, which grouped name variations and misspelling of or-
ganizations such as DTU together, oﬀered a more reliable search. A detailed description of the search
process on Scopus can be found in the Appendix under Section 8.1.5. The main meta-data used from
Scopus was the Aﬃliations, Publication year and the their own Electronic Identiﬁer (EID), which is
basically a unique ID given to every publication.
WoS Like with the Scopus database mentioned before, the Web of Science database has cleaned
up name variations, oﬀering reliable searching for DTU related entries. This database was used largely
because of its comprehensive publication data set. The procedure for searching on the Web of Science
database can be found in the Appendix Section 8.1.6. The data extraction resulted in 32026 results
and 1669 unique company names.

4.2
Meta-data
The publication number is the number given to a patent by the patent oﬃce when it has been
published, and is one of the primary ways of identifying a unique patent. The publication numbers of
a single invention will vary depending on which patent oﬃce it is applied in. For example, a patent
ﬁled at the European Patent Oﬃce (EPO) will begin with ’EP’, standing for European Patent.
This two-letter code is followed by a serial number between 1 and 12 digits (Espacenet, 2018a).
The day the patent gets published, is also considered the publication date.
The applicant is the organization(s) that apply for the patent.
This data is critical to our
research, as it helps to identify companies that co-patented with DTU, which in turn is an indicator of
collaboration. The day a patent is applied, it gets an application number and application date. The
application date is also critical to the study as it reveals the earliest successful collaboration point in
time. The year in which a co-patent is applied is taken as the collaboration year in the later stages of
the analysis in the thesis.
The ﬁrst time an application is ﬁled for an invention, a right of priority is created. The Priority
number is a cross-reference application number which is given while claiming the priority rights. It
is given when claiming a novelty/obviousness of the invention relative to other prior art (Espacenet,
2018b) within the 12 months between stage 2 and 3 (see Figure 3). This number is essential as it allows
for grouping of patents according to families and thereby, ensuring that a single patent is considered
per invention even if it was ﬁled at diﬀerent patent oﬃces.
Introduction to the patent databases
In order to retrieve the names of the companies that co-patented with DTU, the 3 patent databases
were used. The databases are brieﬂy introduced in the coming section followed by how they were used.
Espacenet
The ﬁrst patent database that was used was Espacenet. Espacenet is a patent database, oﬀering
data for over 100 million patents worldwide, including local national patents, European EPO patents
and also worldwide WO patents (Oﬃce, 2018c). It is a reliable database to use due to the way the
database is structured and how search queries work. The DTU website has a direct link(DTU, 2018)
which includes a recommended search query in order to retrieve DTU’s patents, which we modiﬁed
slightly to also limit our search query to the relevant time span.
A total of 1194 results were retrieved from Espacenet, of which 61 were unique company names.
EP full-text search (EPAB)
The EPAB database shares the same database with Espacenet, however, it only covers European
patents (EP). While Espacenet provides free access to over 100 million patents worldwide, EPAB
oﬀers more options for search queries and data to be extracted (Oﬃce, 2018a). Due to the structure
of EPAB’s database and search query compared to Espacenet, certain patents may not appear in one
database whereas it might be retrieved in the other.
Therefore combining the databases together
ensures a more comprehensive data collection.
A total of 634 results were retrieved from EPAB, of which 37 were unique company names.
Global Patent Index (GPI)
The GPI database is identical to the EPAB database previously mentioned, although one major
diﬀerence is that it not only covers European patents, but also patents worldwide. (Oﬃce, 2018d)
A total of 2448 results were retrieved from GPI, of which 78 were unique company names.

4.3
Patents from the companies
In this phase, data was collected on the patents ﬁled for each company. We carried out this task
by using the OECD HAN database (201709_HAN_NAMES) and OECD citations database (Cita-
tions_201709). What makes the OECD databases unique is that the OECD HAN database provides
a special ID called HAN identiﬁer for every company name after harmonizing the names. Harmonized
names mean that the names are treated for misspellings and other inconsistencies and that variations
of the name of a company can be identiﬁed with a single HAN identiﬁer. The OECD HAN database
provides a connection between a HAN identiﬁer and all the patents ﬁled in the European Oﬃce, World
Patent Oﬃce and US Patent Oﬃce for the respective identiﬁers. This makes it a very useful database
for the study. A limitation of this database is, however, the unavailability of priority numbers which
could help grouping the patents across oﬃces to get a single patent per invention. By choosing to
account for the inﬂuence of collaboration on patenting behaviour in the diﬀerent patent oﬃces, we
overcome this shortcoming.
The OECD Citations database on the other hand provides the publication years for the patent
numbers. Using a string matching algorithm, we ﬁrst used the list of company names that we collected
to ﬁnd HAN company names which provided the respective HAN identiﬁers. These HAN identiﬁers
could then be used to get the patent numbers and the years of publication for the companies. The
process also acted as a ﬁlter since not all the company names retrieved from the databases are technical
companies that are actively patenting in the last 12 years.
The ﬁnal part of the data collection required us to get the collaboration years for the companies that
were retrieved from the source databases. This was a challenge because collaborations done through
patents across the diﬀerent databases and within the databases could have multiple collaborations for
a single patent family. We solved this by doing an overlap check across the three patent databases.
The ﬁndings displayed that apart from a few patents (1 in EPAB and 4 in Espacenet), all patent

4.5
Quality and limitations of the data
The reliability and robustness of the data collected has largely been based on the quality and structures
of the databases. EPAB and GPI operate in an SQL based environment. Hence, the data is well
structured in order to carry out queries.
All the company names had to be ﬁltered in some way from the meta-data, since each patent
or publication may include inventor names, addresses, and other types of noise. Through algorithm
based on text-ﬁltering, we were able to ﬁlter out the noise for the vast majority of entries so that
only company names would remain. We veriﬁed our outcomes by manual examination of the resulting
name lists.
Because entering company names and other relevant information regarding the patents and publi-
cations have largely been made manually by the applicants themselves, many of the results included
either misspellings or naming variations and/or abbreviations of the company names, making it even
more challenging to identify and successfully group the diﬀerent results together based on each com-
pany. This has been the case for all the patent databases (EPAB, GPI, and Espacenet).

As such, the data is not perfect, however, after thorough inspection we feel conﬁdent that only very
few patents/publications contain inconsistencies and errors.
The OECD database contains some inconsistencies of its own. We largely depended on OECD’s
own grouping algorithm, which consists of cleaning the company names from punctuations, accents,
abbreviations, and uses dictionary on a country-level basis. Their algorithm also consists of harmon-
ising their data with the data of company names from the ORBIS©database. The results from this
algorithm is what their HAN identiﬁers reference. However, we were still able to ﬁnd many incon-
sistencies in OECD’s HAN identiﬁers, where in many cases, one company would have several HAN
identiﬁers associated to it.
This is why we found it necessary to create our own algorithm, which, based on the HAN identi-
ﬁer grouping, takes it a step further and removes these inconsistencies. By grouping together HAN
identiﬁers that referred to the same company, we were then able to give each unique company an ID,
which is the ﬁnal grouping that we’ve used in order to analyse the patenting trend for the diﬀerent
companies.

T-tests
The following list describes the T-tests that were conducted based on the methodology section. The

Step 1 consists of T-tests 1 and 2 to compare collaborators with non-collaborators.
T-test 1
examines the eﬀect of collaboration by comparing the collected sample of 852 collaborating companies
against 10,000 random non-collaborating companies. T-test 1 (adjusted) examines for the same eﬀect
of collaboration but this time for adjusted samples where outliers are removed. A patent sum cut-oﬀ
level of 76 patents was set for the collaborating companies using the ﬁrst 3 quartiles method and a cut-
oﬀpatent sum level of 50 patents was used for the non-collaborating companies. The adjusted sample
of collaborating companies thus, was reduced to 640 companies and the non-collaborating companies
became a total of 9836. The logic behind removing outliers is explained in the methodology section
phases 3 under T-tests. T-test 2 is similar to the previous two tests but compares the collaborating
companies of 852 of the sample with 3799 non-collaborating companies that are only based in Denmark.
Step 2 consists of T-tests 3, 4 and 5 that explore only the sample of collaborating companies to
analyse the impact of collaboration in greater detail. T-test 3 analyses the impact of collaboration by
splitting the full sample into two sub-samples. The ﬁrst sub-sample has 521 companies that collabo-

2010. T-test 3 (adjusted) splits the adjusted sample of 640 companies into a sub-sample 376 companies
that collaborated on or before 2010 and compares it with the second sub-sample of 264 companies that
collaborated after 2010. The adjusted sample is used since it is assumed to represent the sample better
while analyzing the collaboration impact.
T-test 4 compares a low collaborating sub-sample of 560 companies that collaborated less than or
equal to 2 times against a high collaborating sub-sample of 292 companies that collaborated greater

Stepwise regression
By performing a forward stepwise regression, the Akaike information criterion (AIC) for each of the
variables is used to output the optimum model for the dependent variable of the "number of patents
after collaboration". This method has been used in order to validate the ﬁnal model that has been
arrived via the linear regression method. As the input variable for the ﬁrst stepwise regression, the
variables of the number of collaborations was chosen along with the variables of number years after
collaboration and the number of patents before collaboration. Note that the code selects variables by
the AIC and does not identify the variables as control variables. Additionally, this code uses the full
sample to calculate AIC for the variables.
The output of the code displayed that the dependent variable of the number of patents after col-
laboration is function of all the three input variables. From the stepwise output (shown in Section 8.3,
the number of patents before collaboration is the ﬁrst variable to get selected for the model due to the
lowest AIC. It is then followed by the number of years after collaboration variable. Lastly, the number
of collaborations variable get included in the model to complete the stepwise regression approach.
The process is repeated for the adjusted sample by starting out by inputting all the variables
of number years after collaboration, the number of patents before collaboration and the number of
collaborations. The output displayed a relationship of the dependent variable based on the 3 inputted
variables. The result matches our ﬁndings from the linear regression model. Hence we can conﬁrm that
the ﬁnal function to predict the number of patents is dependent on mainly the number of collaborations
while being controlled for the years after collaboration and number of patents prior to collaboration.

6. DISCUSSION
6
Discussion
From the analysis, some of the most interesting results and ﬁndings help us answer our research
question, whether collaboration had a positive impact on the innovative capacity of companies.
The initial ﬁndings conﬁrmed our expectations that collaborating companies performed better than
non-collaborating companies in terms of patenting activity. The assumption draws upon literature that
points out to the potential direct beneﬁts like technology transfer, research resources and human re-
sources as well as indirect beneﬁts of organizational learning and innovative culture by collaborating
with universities. The result backs the argument that collaborating with universities has a positive
impact on the overall innovative capacity and consequently provides reassurance of patents as a rea-
sonable proxy for measuring the innovative capacity. Failure to ﬁnd a signiﬁcant diﬀerence between
collaborators and non-collaborators at this point would question the validity of continuing to further
analyse the impact of the collaboration within the sample.
The analysis also provided a few interesting secondary ﬁndings due to the surprisingly huge mag-
nitude of the diﬀerence between the two groups. Because there are many high patenting multinational
companies in the collaborating group while there are not many in the non-collaborating group, it
suggests that DTU has a strong position in the innovation system because it inﬂuences a lot of these
high-patenting companies. It also suggests that the majority of multinational companies desire to
collaborate with DTU and indicates the importance that multinational companies place on collabo-
rating and utilizing research resources at DTU. It goes to suggest that companies ﬁnd beneﬁts from
collaborating although whether theses collaborative beneﬁts have an impact on innovative capacity
remains debatable.
Depending on the timing of the ﬁrst collaboration for a given company, we also observed that
companies that collaborated prior to midpoint of 2010 in the time span perform signiﬁcantly better in
their overall patenting activity than companies that had their ﬁrst collaboration later than 2010. It
means that collaborating early on can give a company its competitive edge concerning inventing and
patenting more. This result builds upon the previous one which suggests that while collaborating is
vital to boosting innovative capacity, being an early collaborator gives a company an advantage over
other companies.
We also wanted to understand whether the frequency of collaborations made a diﬀerence to the
patenting activity of a company. A higher amount of overall patenting for companies that collaborated
more would hint that the number of collaborations play a role in improving the innovative capacity. On
comparing companies that collaborated higher than 2 times, which is the median number of collabora-
tions, with companies that collaborated less than or equal to 2, it was found that higher collaborating
companies patented signiﬁcantly more. The outcome was the same for the adjusted sample even when
we increased the threshold to the mean of 6 collaborations to diﬀerentiate between a low and high
collaborating companies. The ﬁndings indicates that there is a relationship between the number of
collaborations and the patenting activity and the results led the way for further analysis through a
regression model.
The ﬁndings when comparing the patenting activity in the years after collaboration with the patent-
ing activity in the years before, indicated that there may be signs of collaboration positively impacting
the innovative capacity of ﬁrms, but the evidence is not concrete. The applied T-tests to compare the
5 years after collaboration with 5 years before, indicated a higher number of patents in the years after
collaboration for both the full sample and the adjusted sample. However, when we shift to a 3-year
span comparison, there is no signiﬁcant diﬀerence identiﬁed by the T-test between the spans before and
after collaboration even though a qualitative insight into the sample shows that 51% of the companies
patented more in the span after collaboration while 31% patented lesser in the span after collaboration

when compared to the 3 years before. One of the factors considered to inﬂuence the outcome was the
presence of low patenting companies. Over 50% of the companies from the sample had not patented
over 10 times in the time span. When removing those low collaborating companies to analyse the
companies with an intermediate amount of patenting activity, we still did not get a signiﬁcant result
on a 3-year span comparison. The result questions the validity of the 5-year span analyses. A closer
look at the trend and number of patents ﬁled per year suggests that the contradictory results could be
due to ﬂuctuation in the patenting activity on a yearly basis. There appears to be an upward trend
in patenting activity when seen holistically and can be the cause of getting positive results for the
5-year span comparison. Alternatively, it could imply that collaboration does have long-run beneﬁts
which can be only seen later in the years. This could be the case because collaboration inﬂuences the
company’s culture to be more conducive to innovation. One of the core aspects of that culture is a
high ﬂow of knowledge internally within the company. It facilitates organizational learning which in
turn helps in developing an absorptive capacity to assimilate new information in order to commercially
apply it through inventing (Cohen and Klepper, 1996). Cultural change can be a slow process and
therefore its inﬂuences on the increase in inventions and patenting activity are probably only visible
later. The results suggest that spans greater than 4 years are more appropriate to capture the impacts
of collaboration on the innovative capacity since a 4-year span comparison did not result in a signiﬁcant
diﬀerence between the spans before and after either.
Another ﬁnding that caught our attention was that there was no signiﬁcant diﬀerence in the overall
patenting activity between companies based on their method of collaboration. Companies that col-
laborated over patents and companies that collaborated over publications, both performed similarly
to each other when compared by their overall patenting activity. Furthermore, neither of the groups
showed a signiﬁcant diﬀerence in patenting behaviour in the 3 or 5-year span after collaboration than
when compared to the respective 3 or 5-year span before when analysed separately. One would think
that collaborating over patents would result in a higher impact on the overall patenting activity of
that company since the process of collaborating over an invention can directly inﬂuence the companies
internal processes for inventing and innovating, and put them in a better position to patent more in
the future. While the result of collaborating over patents could be due to the small sample size of
companies that co-patented, a comparison of the processes and mechanisms behind collaborating over
patents and publications could be the best way forward in understanding this outcome.
The understanding that the number of collaborations has a positive eﬀect on the number of patents
after collaboration is also conﬁrmed with the logistic regression results. More speciﬁcally, a signiﬁcant
positive relationship is identiﬁed between the number of patents a company ﬁles after collaboration
based on the number of collaborations through publications.
The same relationship could not be
analysed based on the number of collaborations over patents as the sample is too small for a regression
analysis. It can be a possibility for future research by expanding the scope to more universities.
Finally, the ﬁndings from the technology ﬁelds show that in general, companies diversify into
patenting in new technology ﬁelds as the years progress. This relates to the increase in patenting
activity over the years and indicates that companies venture out into new technologies as they patent
more. It suggests an increase in innovative capacity since companies show inventive capabilities in
technologies that they may have no prior experience. Whether that is due to collaboration remains
questionable since the results do not show that companies are patenting in more unique technology
ﬁelds after collaboration when compared to the years before collaboration.

7. CONCLUSION
7
Conclusion
The analysis conducted has led to several interesting ﬁndings regarding the impacts of university-
industry collaboration on the innovative capacity of ﬁrms.
Firstly, by analysing the behaviour in
patenting ﬁlings before and after the ﬁrst collaboration, we see that companies tend to patent more in
the years after collaboration compared to the years before. While the indications are not strong on a
before-after comparison, the general trend in patent ﬁlings over the 11 years in the study indicates a
rising trend. Moreover, the number of patent ﬁlings display a signiﬁcant relationship with the number
of collaborations that a company has with the university.
With these results we answer the ﬁrst
sub-question.
Additionally, by analyzing the patenting diversity for the second sub-question, the results displayed
diversiﬁcation into new technology ﬁelds as we analysed the years before collaboration with the years
after. It is hard to attribute the result to collaboration with complete certainty since there were no
signiﬁcant correlations found between the number of technology ﬁelds after collaboration and number
of collaborations. The results are still interesting considering that an increase in the number of patents
doesn’t necessarily imply an increase in number of technology ﬁelds for those patent classes.
Based on our results, we can suﬃcient reasons to believe that university-industry collaborations
positively inﬂuence the companies’ innovative capacity.
By using DTU as the collaborating university and patent data as a proxy for innovation, we
ensure that the impact on technological innovations could be analysed and measured to a reasonable
extent since DTU is a technical university which considers collaboration with external partners to be
important (DTU, 2016).
The data sample covers a vast amount of companies that collaborated over patents and publica-
tions, which gives us a fair insight into the impacts of collaboration between companies and universities.
The choice of collaboration indicators (co-patenting and co-publishing) was unique to this thesis and
suﬃcient since they represent the majority of the outcomes from collaborative eﬀorts between univer-
sities and companies. Additionally, the patent and publication data was freely available for the study
in a number of diﬀerent databases. For the collaboration over patents, we used the patent application
dates to get as close to the real collaboration point as possible.
Patents work well for this particular study as an innovation indicator, since it is feasible to ﬁnd
patent data for several hundred companies compared to other innovation indicators such as R&D, and
especially given the time available for the thesis. Patent data was collected from three of the biggest
patent databases resulting in a rich amount of data for the analysis. Furthermore, by using granted
patents, we ensure that patents resemble innovation more convincingly due to the novelty criteria that
a patent needs to meet while being published by a patent oﬃce.
The analysis was veriﬁed by the adjusted sample that had multinational high-patenting companies
removed and thus resembles a clearer impact of collaboration.There seemed to be indications of im-
proved patenting activity after collaborations with DTU. While not a conclusive statement, we believe
that a pattern could potentially exist.
The diversiﬁcation of the patents which were analysed by their IPC codes (technology ﬁelds) also
showed signs that the companies may diversify into new technology ﬁelds. However this too does not
show clear evidence.
In the 3 and 5-year trend study, we carried out several tests to check for the inﬂuence of collaboration
on low patenting, medium patenting, and high patenting companies. Thus, covering speciﬁc segments
of patenting behaviour to understand whether there are any eﬀects from collaboration. We believe

7. CONCLUSION
that even though greater resources could have shed more light on the matter, our study for a 5-year
span conﬁrms some of the current scholarly notions about the impact university collaboration has on
companies. A 4-year and 3-year span did not however show any clear indications.
We believe that these ﬁndings show that there are indications that collaboration has an impact
on the innovative capacity of companies, in terms of patenting activity and diversiﬁcation of the
patents. While we cannot say for certain based on our results, we believe this is a basis for further
investigation. Future studies would beneﬁt from a greater data sample, which could result in clearer
trends, or a more clear indication that an impact does not exist. Extending the time span and looking
at more universities outside of Denmark could also help shed some light on the patenting behaviour
among companies after a collaboration. For future research, it would also be interesting to analyse the
diﬀerence in impacts on innovative capacity based on whether the company collaborated over patents
or over publications. By providing these quantitative ﬁndings into the impacts of DTU, we hope to
further stimulate collaboration activities and encourage technology transfer.

Processing the data
It was quickly noticed that many companies that had been identiﬁed in the Espacenet database,
were absent in the EPAB database. This was to be expected to some degree, since Espacenet searches
in a worldwide collection of more than 100 countries, whereas the EPAB database only searches through
patent applications granted by the EPO (EP patents).
However, looking through individual patents, it was found out that even EP patents with companies
and DTU mentioned, were not properly found in the EPAB database. As an example, the following
patent was looked into:
EP2330294 (A1)
This patent application was retrieved during the query in EPAB with the A1 document kind code,
because the applicant is listed as "Danmarks Tekniske Universitet". Yet when this patent application
is retrieved in Espacenet, both the university and the company it has been working with is listed, as
can be seen in Fig. 35.

It can furthermore be seen that two document kind codes have been published, the A1 kind code

If another query search is conducted for just this patent publication number in the EPAB database
as such:
PUN = "EP2330294"
2 documents are retrieved. The A1 document with DTU mentioned as an applicant, and the B1
document as well with Bladena as the applicant, with no mention of DTU. Presumably, DTU has
given or sold the full rights to Bladena by the time the patent was granted at B1.
Because of this, the B1 document was obviously not retrieved in the original query since it only
looked up the variations of DTU as applicant and only found the A1 document. Whereas Espacenet
merges the A1 and B1 documents together, was therefore able to retrieve both applicant informations
under the same result.
This explains why there were some companies like Bladena who were never picked up during the
initial query. Therefore, the challenge is to retrieve all document kind codes for the same publication
numbers, in order to ensure that all information is retrieved.

conducted to achieve this:

Excel ﬁle.
2. By using Excel formulas, the text in each cell can be trimmed using the MID function and "PUN
= " and " or " is added as raw text in each cells automatically (see Fig. 36).
3. With the help of a VBA script, every cell in the column is merged into one big cell, which can
then be copied and pasted in to the query window on EPO’s website with no formatting issues.
