1. Abstract 
Fusarium avenacem is the most common Fusarium sp. found in infected crops in northern Europe. 
Fusarium infection is estimated to cost billions of dollars in lost yield every year. A large contributor 
to this loss is post-harvest infection of crops, causing them to be contaminated with several toxins 
and other unwanted chemicals. 
A toxin produced by F. avenaceum is 2-amino-14,16-dimethyloctadecan-3-ol (2-AOD-3-ol). 2-AOD-3-
ol has been shown to inhibit the NADH-fumirate reductase system, which is part of the anaerobic 
metabolism in eukaryotes. Structural similarities between 2-AOD-3-ol and the class of compounds, 
fumonisins has led to the suggestion that 2-AOD-3-ol may be an evolutionary precursor of 
fumonisin. 
The gene cluster responsible for production of 2-AOD-3-ol in F. avenaceum has been identified. A 
putative function for most of the genes in the cluster has been proposed based on homology 
alignment. Based on the putative function of the genes, biosynthetic pathway has been suggested. 
In the current study it was attempted to delete the three core genes of the cluster, a polyketide 
synthase, an aminotransferase, and an oxidoreductase. Deletion of the polyketide synthase and 
oxidoreductase was successfully achieved through use of Agrobacterium tumefaciens mediated 
transformation, and verified by PCR using three 3 different primer sets. 
The two deletion strains were chemically analysed using UHPLC-MS, and the final intermediate, 2-
AOD-3-one, was identified, strengthening the proposed biosynthetic pathway of 2-AOD-3-ol. 
Attempts at deletion four additional genes: the two flanking the cluster, as well as PKS12 and aurP, 
were unsuccessful.

3. Introduction 
Filamentous fungi are ubiquitous in nature and vital to the ecosystem as decomposers of dead 
organic material (Meinhardt & Esser, 2001). While many filamentous fungi are saprophytic, a large 
number exhibit pathogenic activity towards plants or animals (Meinhardt & Esser, 2001). Plant 
pathogenic fungi are responsible for loss of large amounts of crops each year, either through 
infection of the plants in the field or through post-harvest infection of the crops (Golinski et al., 
2002). The health risks associated with consumption of infected crops are significant, so the infected 
crops have to be discarded with a sizable economic loss for the food industry (Uhlig, Jestoi, & 
Parikka, 2007). 
Crops subjected to post-harvest infection often contain mycotoxins (Golinski et al., 2002). 
Mycotoxins is a term referring to the group of secondary metabolites that are toxic to humans. 
Secondary metabolites are not essential to the growth or proliferation of the producer, but serve 
other functions such as protection from UV light, signalling or chemical warfare. 
In the temperate climates of northern Europe and Canada, Fusarium sp. are among the most 
common crop spoiling fungi, with F. avenaceum being present in more than 90% of spoiled crops in 
Norway (Uhlig et al., 2007). 
A major contributor to the toxicity of grain infected by Fusarium sp. is the fumonisin group of 
mycotoxins (Gazzotti et al., 2011). Fumonisins is characterised by having a sphingolipid-like 
backbone as the core of the compound, modifications of the backbone vary within the class (Musser 
& Plattner, 1997). The core structure is shared with 2-amino-14,16-dimethyloctadecan-3-ol (2-AOD-
3-ol) leading to the hypothesis that 2-AOD-3-ol is an evolutionary precursor to the fumonisin class 
(Uhlig, Petersen, Flåøyen, & Wilkins, 2005). This hypothesis was supported by investigations 
revealing that 2-AOD-3-ol has similar, although less effective, cytotoxic effects (Uhlig et al., 2005). 
This sparked interest in the biosynthesis of 2-AOD-3-ol, and a type I iterative polyketide synthase 
cluster was linked to the production of the compound. The cluster contains seven genes, a putative 
function has been proposed for five of the genes based on homology alignment. Three of the five 
genes are suspected of being directly involved with the biosynthesis of 2-AOD-3-ol. The putative 
function of these three genes formed the basis of a hypothetical biosynthetic pathway of 2-AOD-3-
ol. However, this proposed pathway has not been verified in the lab. 
This study aims at creating seperate deletion strains for each of the three core genes in the 2-AOD-3-
ol cluster in F. avenaceum and analysing them chemically to try to verify the proposed biosynthetic 
pathway.

4. Background 
4.1. Fusarium avenaceum 
Fusarium avenaceum is a generalist plant pathogen, capable of infecting a range of plants including 
several crops, and is the most commonly isolated Fusarium species for Norwegian grain (Kosiak, 
Torp, Skjerve, & Thrane, 2003; Lysøe et al., 2014). Cereals infected with F. avenaceum is known to 
cause crown rot and head blight. Crops infected while in storage are contaminated with mycotoxins 
making them unfit for consumption and thus provides a significant economic burden (Uhlig et al., 
2007). In spite of this few studies have attempted to elucidate the genetic basis for the wide host 
range or the biosynthesis of secondary metabolites (L. Q. Sørensen et al., 2014). 
Like most other filamentous fungi, F. avenaceum is known to produce a range of secondary 
metabolites including moniliformin, aurofusarin, fusarin C, antibiotic Y and 2-AOD-3-ol (J. L. Sørensen 
& Sondergaard, 2014). A genomic study has been carried out on three samples of F. avenaceum 
isolated from barley in Finland, spring and winter wheat in Canada. The study showed that F. 
avenacem have a great potential for secondary metabolite production. The fungal genome encodes 
with 75 to 80 genes for enzymes belonging to the polyketide, non-ribosomal peptide, terpene, 
alkaloid and indole-diterpene synthases classes. 25-27 of the genes belong to the iterative type I 
polyketide synthase (PKS) class (Lysøe et al., 2014). The three isolates shared a core set of 24 type I 
PKS genes, which is remarkably high compared to six other Fusarium species which contain between 
13 and 18 type I PKS genes (Lysøe et al., 2014). 
4.2. Polyketides 
Polyketides (PK) is a group of compounds produced by most microorganisms and present an 
immensely diverse range of biological activities, including many of commercial value such as 
antibiotics or pharmaceuticals (Jenke-Kodama & Dittmann, 2009; Liou & Khosla, 2003). Common for 
all of them is that they are synthesised by gradual elongation of a poly-β-ketone backbone with 
potential reduction of the ketone group of each link happening before the next one is added (Liou & 
Khosla, 2003). Several catalytic domains are required to carry out this function and the arrangement 
of these domains are the basis for the subdivision of polyketide synthases into the types I, II and III 
(Jenke-Kodama & Dittmann, 2009; Liou & Khosla, 2003). 
The first step of PK biosynthesis is loading of the starter unit by the acetyl transferase (AT) domain 
which is subsequently transferred to the aceyl carrier protein (ACP) domain. An extension unit is 
then recognised by the AT domain and then loaded onto the ACP domain, after which the 
ketosynthase (KS) domain catalyzes a claisen condensation between the newly loaded extension and 
the growing polyketide (Cheng & Du, 2015). At the end of the cycle the newly extended polyketide is 
moved from the ACP domain, where it is attached through the recently added extender unit, to the 
KS domain, after which a new cycle can begin. While a module only requires these three domains to 
function, three additional reducing domains are commonly observed: the ketoreductase (KR) 
domain reduces the keto group to an alcohol group, the dehydrogenase (DH) domain reduces the 
alcohol group to a double bond, and the enoylreductase (ER) domain fully reduces the double bond 
(Cheng & Du, 2015). When the backbone is finished it is released by a thioesterase (TE) domain and 
subjected to modification any additional tailoring enzymes found in the gene cluster.

4.2.1. Type I 
Type I polyketide synthases are found in bacteria and fungi and are characterised by having all the 
domains required to elongate and reduce the backbone molecule located on a single large mega 
peptide stemming from a single gene (Jenke-Kodama & Dittmann, 2009). This group is further 
subdivided into the modular and iterative type I PKSs. 
4.2.1.1. 
Modular 
The modular type I PKS can be considered as a series of modules which each extend the polyketide 
with a single unit as depicted in Figure 1. Each module contains its own version of the KS, AT, and 
ACP domain with the exception of the initial loader module which only contains an AT and ACP 
domain (Cheng & Du, 2015). In addition to the three required domains, each module may contain its 
own set of reducing domains which reduces the functional group of the unit attached by the 
previous module. The growing polyketide is sequentially elongated and potentially reduced by each 
module until a module with a TE domain is encountered, at which point the enzyme-linking 
thiolester is hydrolysed to an acid group and the polyketide released. This makes it possible to 
predict the length of the final ketide chain as well as the degree of reduction of each keto group. The 
modular type I PKS is the only non-iterative type of PKSs and is only found in bacteria (Jenke-Kodama 
& Dittmann, 2009). 
 
Figure 1 - Modular type I PKS. All domains are located on a single megapeptide, subdivided into various modules. Loader 
module contains an AT and ACP domain which loads the first acetyl unit. The AT and ACP domain in module 1 facilitates the 
loading of a malonyl unit, which extends the original acetyl unit by 2 carbon atoms. In this case the presence of KR, DH, and 
ER domains facilitate the full reduction of the keto group on the original acetyl unit. The process repeats in module two, but 
the lack of an ER domain means the ketogroup on the first extender unit is not fully reduced but leaves a double bond. In 
the end the TE domain releases the polyketide with an acid group at the last added extender unit. 
4.2.1.2. 
Iterative 
Unlike the modular type I PKS, the iterative type I PKSs typically only have a single version of each 
catalytic domain, which functions repeatedly on the product, as illustrated in Figure 2, until it is

4.2.2. Type II 
The defining characteristic of type II PKSs is that they possess all the same catalytic domains of the 
type I PKS, but that each domain is located on its own monofunctional protein, stemming from a 
distinct gene (Liou & Khosla, 2003). The individual proteins form a large enzyme complexe that carry 
out their function much like the iterative type I PKSs (Liou & Khosla, 2003). Like the modular type I 
PKS, the type II PKS has only been found in bacteria (Jenke-Kodama & Dittmann, 2009). 
4.2.3. Type III 
Type III PKSs consists of a homodimer of monofunctional peptides containing only a KS domain. To 
compensate for the lack of any ACP domain they bind Coenzyme A (CoA) as it delivers the extender 
units (Shen, 2003). The KS domain then attaches the growing polyketide to the extender unit as it is 
still bound to CoA. Once the elongation is complete CoA then transfers the chain to the KS domain, 
CoA is released and the cycle may repeat (Shen, 2003). 
4.3. Fumonisins 
Fumonisins are a group of mycotoxins known to be produced by Fusarium species and are a big 
contributor to the toxicity of infected gain (Gazzotti et al., 2011; Müller, Dekant, & Mally, 2012). 
Fumonisins are characterised by a 19-20 carbon chain derived from a polyketide with a nitrogen side 
group at carbon 1 or 2 depending of the length of the chain, and an alcohol group at the 2nd or 3rd 
carbon, depending on the length of the chain (Musser & Plattner, 1997). The subdivision of 
fumonisins into the four subgroups: A, B, C and P is based partly on the length of the chain and partly

4.3.1. Structual similarities between fumonisin B1 and 2-AOD-3-ol 
Breaking the structure of fumonisin B1 down, it can be considered to consist of three sections: The 
carbon backbone, and the two tricarballylic ester groups. A striking similarity between the backbone 
and 2-AOD-3-ol is seen in Figure 4. Both are long, fully reduced polyketides with an amino group on 
the second carbon and an alcohol group on the third. Both have two methylations, although only the 
methylation at carbon 16 is shared across the two, and the other is positions at carbon 12 and 14 in 
fumonisin B1 and 2-AOD-3-ol respectively. Going beyond that the differences start to emerge, as the 
backbone of fumonisin is two carbon longer and has two additional alcohol groups at carbon 5 and 
10. The most striking difference is the two tricarballylic acid esters on carbons 14 and 15.

4.4. Antibiotic Y 
Antibiotic Y, named for its yellow fluorescence, is a bioactive compound produced by F. avenaceum 
that has been shown to be toxic towards B. subtilis and E. carotovora while showing no toxicity 
towards chicken embryos or mouse cells (Goliński, Wnuk, Chełkowski, Visconti, & Schollenberger, 
1986). Despite the apparent pharmaceutical potential, the biosynthetic pathway of antibiotic Y is still 
not known 31 years after its discovery. An isotopic labelling study has shown that the antibiotic Y 
molecule is derived from the intermediate YWA1, which is known from biosynthesis of aurofusarin, 
suggesting a link between the two metabolites (Klitgaard et al., 2015). The aurofusarin cluster in F. 
avenaceum contains two genes, aurE and aurP, which has no homologues in the aurofusarin cluster 
in F. gramenarium. (Skovbjerg, 2016). Based on the predicted function of the two genes, combined

hypothetical biosynthetic pathway was proposed in which nor-rubrofusarin, an intermediate in the

4.4.1. Effects of deletion of PKS12, aurE, and aurP 
Skovbjerg attempted to create deletion strains of three genes involved in the proposed biosynthetic 
pathway: PKS12, aurE, and aurP. PKS12 is known to be facilitating the production of YWA1 
(Rugbjerg, Naesby, Mortensen, & Frandsen, 2013), and the deletion of the gene would be expected 
to make the fungus unable to synthesise antibiotic Y as well as aurofusarin. No significant build-up of 
precursors or intermediates is expected from the deletion, as PKS12 facilitates the first step in the 
biosynthesis of these compounds, and the precursors are acetyl-CoA and malonyl-CoA, which the 
fungus can be expected to use in other parts of the metabolism. 
Deletion of aurP would be expected to stop the production of antibiotic Y, but not of aurofusarin, as 
the branching of the pathways occur right before the reaction facilitated by aurP. Build-up of nor-
rubrofusarin would then be expected to increase the flux through the aurofusarin pathway and 
cause increased production of aurofusarin. 
Deletion of aurE, would stop antibiotic Y production further down the proposed pathway and would 
as such cause a build-up of the unnamed hypothetical intermediate produced by aurP, which the 
fungus is presumed to not need for any other pathway. Build-up of the intermediate might cause 
inhibition or reduced expression of aurP and would again lead to increased aurofusarin production, 
although not to the same degree that deletion of the aurP gene would. 
4.5. ATMT (Agrobacterium Tumefaciens Mediated Transformation) 
Agrobacterium is a genus of common soil-dwelling bacteria living mainly as saprophytes, although 
some have shown to have pathogenic properties and cause disease in plants (Frandsen, 2011). The 
method of infection involves transfer of tumor-inducing DNA from the bacteria to the host plant and 
has been particularly well described for A. tumefaciens (Citovsky et al., 2007). A tumor inducing 
plasmid (Ti plasmid) contains the genes encoding the transfer mechanism as well as the transferred 
DNA (T-DNA). The T-DNA is flanked by 24 bp directional imperfect repeat sequences known as the 
left and right border (de Groot, Bundock, Hooykaas, & Beijersbergen, 1998). A single stranded copy 
of the T-DNA is synthesised from the right to the left border and prepared for transfer. Once it has 
entered the infected plant cell it is transported to the nucleus where it is integrated in the host 
genome, which ensures stable replication (Citovsky et al., 2007). The construction of Ti plasmids with 
easily modifiable T-DNA regions allowed for the exploitation of the virulence system of A. 
tumefaciens for genetic modification of plants through co-cultivation of the plant and the 
transformed A. tumefaciens. This transformation method was improved further with the addition of 
acetosyringone, which artificially induces the virulence system (Frandsen, 2011). 
In 1998 the technique was found to not only be useful for the genetic modification of plants, but also 
for several filamentous fungi (de Groot et al., 1998). This provided an alternative to the protoplast 
transformation techniques the protoplasts which is time consuming to prepare, suffers a relatively 
low transformation efficiency and often resulted in multiple integrations of the intended gene. (de 
Groot et al., 1998; Frandsen, 2011). 
Lacking an efficient transformation protocol for F. avenaceum, Sørensen et al. developed a USER-
Brick vector construction system for the construction of Ti plasmids (L. Q. Sørensen et al., 2014). 
Standardised parts of the vector is amplified by PCR and assembled in E. coli through USER cloning to 
form the finished Ti plasmid, which is then purified and transformed into A. tumefaciens (L. Q. 
Sørensen et al., 2014). The system was designed to allow for eight standard modifications, including

gene replacement. Gene replacement can be an efficient deletion strategy if the chosen gene is 
replaced by a marker gene, such as hygromycin B resistance which is illustrated in Figure 6 (L. Q.

By standardising the complimentary USER overhangs on the primers used to amplify the fragments

5. Objectives and Hypothesis 
The aim of this thesis is the characterize the genetic basis for 2-AOD-3-ol biosynthesis in F. 
avenaceum. This will be done by deleting genes predicted to be involved in the production of 2-AOD-
3-ol by Agrobacterium tumefaciens mediated transformation (ATMT). Deletion mutants will be 
cultivated on a range of media and subsequently chemically analysed to investigate the effects of 
both the media and deletions in relation to 2-AOD-3-ol production. Additionally, this project will aim 
to create and verify deletion strains of three genes proposed to be involved in biosynthesis of 
Antibiotic Y. 
2-AOD-3-ol is a compound produced by F. avenaceum. The gene cluster linked to the synthesis of 2-
AOD-3-ol in F. avenaceum, Figure 7, consists of seven genes five of which has been identified by 
homology alignment. The remaining two genes in the cluster are flanking the cluster on either side 
and no characterised homologue has been found for either of these. Of the five identified genes, 
three are core genes directly involved in the production of 2-AOD-3-ol while the remaining two are 
proposed to be a transcription factor and a transporter. The three core genes encode a type I 
iterative PKS (FA04704), an aminotransferase (FA04705) and an oxidoreductase (FA04708). This 
project aims to create knockout strains of these three genes and characterise them chemically by 
use of HPLC-MS.

5.1. Proposed biosynthetic pathway of 2-AOD-3-ol 
The proposed biosynthetic pathway for 2-AOD-3-ol starts as the PKS synthesises a fully reduced 
polyketide from one acetyl-CoA and eight malonyl-CoA with two methylations positiontioned carbon 
12 and 14, totalling 18 carbon atoms. The aminotransferase is then proposed to facilitate 
condensation of alanine at the acid end of the polyketide. Finally, the oxidoreductase is proposed to 
reduce the remaining keto group to an alcohol group forming the final product. The proposed 
pathway is shown in Figure 8. This project will aim to verify the proposed pathway by deleting each 
gene involved in the pathway and chemically analyse the deletion strains to detect build up of any 
intermediates.

6.5. Transformation of A. tumefaciens 
Competent A. tumefaciens LBA4404 cells and electroporation cuvettes were placed on ice prior to 
transformation, one cuvette was prepared for each plasmid. 
1µL of plasmid solution and 50µL of competent cell solution were mixed in the cuvette, air bubbles 
removed by gentle tapping the cuvette on the table after which the exterior of the cuvette was 
wiped with a paper towel to remove any water remaining from the cooling. Electrotransformation of 
A. tumefaciens was performed with an Biorad Micropulser (Biorad) using setting ECI corresponding 
to a voltage of 2.5 kV, capacitance of 25 µF and a resistance of 200Ω. Immediately after the 
electroshock 450µL of SOC medium was applied to the cuvette. The mix was transferred to a sterile 
Eppendorf tube and incubated for 90 minutes at 28°C and 350 RPM. 
The contents of the Eppendorf tube was plated on two plates of LB+kanamycin using 1/10 of the 
volume on one plate and 9/10 of the volume on the other. The plates were left to incubate for three 
days at 28°C. Select colonies of each strain was transferred to culture tubes containing 10 mL of LB 
media supplemented with 50µg/mL of kanamycin and incubated at 28°C at 150 RPM for 2 days. 
Three culture tubes of liquid IMAS supplemented with 50µg/mL of kanamycin was inoculated with 
100, 200 and 300µL of each of the strains and incubated at 28°C at 250 RPM for 2 days to reach an 
OD600 of 0.5-0.7 prior to co-cultivation with F. avenaceum. 
6.6. F. avenaceum spore harvest 
F. avenaceum 05001 from the strain collection of Rasmus John Normand Frandsen was grown on 
CYA plates for 4 days after which a sterile solution of 0.01% tween80 and 0.9% sodium chloride in 
H2O was poured over the plate and spores were disloged mechanically with a sterile drigalski 
spatula. The spore solution was filtered through sterile miracloth and stored at 5°C for up to 5 hours 
until co-cultivation with A. tumefaciens was initiated. 
6.7. A. tumefaciens mediated transformation of F. avenaceum 
Spore concentration was measured by cell counting and diluted to a concentration of 2*106 
spores/mL in liquid IMAS medium. The diluted spore solution was mixed 1:1 (v/v) with the A. 
tumefaciens culture and pipetted up and down to mix. 200µL of the mix was plated onto plates of 
IMAS medium overlaid with sterile AGF220 and incubated at 28°C for 3 days. Three controls were 
made and incubated in the same manner, one negative control containing only a sterile filter, one 
positive control of spore viability containing only 100µL of the diluted, unmixed spore solution, and 
one positive control of A. tumefaciens viability containing only 100µL of the unmixed A. tumefaciens 
culture. 
After 3 days of incubation the filters were moved to DFM plates supplemented with 150 µg/mL 
hygromycin B and 300 µg/mL cefoxitin (DFM S1) and left to incubate for another 3 days.  
Following the incubation on DFM S1 plates, the filters were moved to DFM S2 plates, which were 
supplemented only with 150 µg/mL hygromycin B and not with cefoxitin and incubated for 3 further 
days. 
Using a sterile toothpick each colony visible on the filters after the selection round 2 was transferred 
to a separate DFM plate supplemented with 100 µg/mL hygromycin B.

Spectrometry (UHPLC-HRMS) performed on an Agilent 1290 Infinity UHPLC system (Agilent

was used. MS detection was performed in both positive and negative ionization on an Agilent 6545 
QTOF MS equipped with Agilent Dual Jet Stream electrospray ion source with a drying gas 
temperature of 250°C, gas flow of 8 L/min, sheath gas temperature of 300°C and flow of 12 L/min. 
Capillary voltage was set to 4000V and nozzle voltage to 500V. Mass spectra were recorded at 10, 20 
and 40 eV as centroid data for m/z 85-1700 in MS mode with an acquisition rate of 10 spectra/s. 
Lock mass solution in 70:30 methanol:water was infused in the second sprayer using an extra LC

Chesire, UK) as lock masses. The [M+H]+ ions (m/z) 186.2216 and 922.0098 respectively) of both

8. Discussion

8. Discussion 
8.1. Effect of deletions 
Deletion of the polyketide synthase in the 2-AOD-3-ol cluster, FA04704, did not cause the 2-AOD-3-
ol mass to disappear in DFM or CYA media samples, as it did in the rice media. The deletion of the 
oxidoreductase, FA04708, however did caused the 2-AOD-3-ol mass to disappear in the DFM and 
CYA samples, but not in the rice media. A possible explanation for the continued presence of the 
[M+H]+ and [M+H-H2O]+ adducts in the DFM and CYA samples of the ΔFA04704 strain could be a 
degree of unspecificity in the enzymes encoded by FA04705 and FA04708. If they were to catalyse 
their respective condensation and reduction of a differently methylated fatty acid, the results on the 
mass spectrum would be indistinguishable from 2-AOD-3-ol. The expression of the genes related to 
production of this other polyketide being regulated differently from the 2-AOD-3-ol cluster could 
account for the occurrence of the mass not being consistent across the DFM, CYA and rice media 
samples. 
As MS cannot measure the structure of a compound but only a mass from which a composition can 
be deduced, the mass could be an unrelated compound with an identical chemical composition. That 
this other compound has the same retention time would indicate that the polarity of the compound 
should be almost identical to 2-AOD-3-ols, meaning that the structures of the compounds would be 
almost identical. 
The explanation that FA04705 and FA04708 enacts their activity on a different polyketide of a similar 
mass does not account for the 312.3268 m/z correlating to 2-AOD-3-one, the proposed precursor of 
2-AOD-3-ol, being absent in the samples of the ΔFA04704 strain. If unspecificity was the case, a 
build-up of the final intermediate similar to that of the wildtype would be expected, unless the 
approximate 90% reduction means that the mass signal from the intermediate becomes too weak to 
distinguish from background. 
Deletion of the oxidoreductase, FA04708, is seen to cause a significant build-up of the mass 
correlating to the final intermediate of the 2-AOD-3-ol pathway, 2-AOD-3-one. The inability to 
reduce the ketogroup and synthesise 2-AOD-3-ol would be expected to cause a build-up of the 
intermediate acting as substrate in the reaction. This is consistent with the hypothesised 
biosynthetic pathway. 
8.2. Validation of constructed strains 
During validation of transformants the PCR reactions designed for test of the ΔFA04708 transformats 
proved very easy to work with whereas both flanks in the ΔFA04704 strains, and particularly the 
T4/RF-2 fragment, proved very difficult to amplify. Multiple strategies were attempted focusing 
mainly on the possibility of defect reagents and optimizing the annealing temperature, as a range of 
prediction tools calculated the Tm of the FA04704-T4 and RF-2 primers more than 5°C higher than 
the other primers designed for the test of ΔFA04704 transformants. Switching to the CxL buffer 
designed for the pfuX7 polymerase greatly improved results but also resulted in several unexpected 
bands in many of the reactions, suggesting unspecific primer binding. Addressing the unspecific 
binding by raising annealing temperature further does not seem like a possible solution, as the 
annealing temperature was getting close to the elongation temperature as it was. Another path 
could be to reduce the primer concentration to make the energetically suboptimal bindings less 
likely to occur, which would reduce the likelihood of unwanted sections being amplified. Combining 
==PAGE:45==
8. Discussion 
44 
 
the change to CxL buffer and reduced primer concentration is likely to be an improvement to the 
applied protocol for validating constructed strains by PCR. 
8.3. Difficulties with construction of a ΔFA04705 strain 
The repeated failure to construct a ΔFA04705 strain can have multiple explanations. One possible 
explanation is hinted at by the sequenced portions of the plasmid, which confirmed that the HRS 
blocks was assembled correctly with the HygR block, but also suggested three mutations in less than 
50 bases within the HygR block that was covered by the sequencings. With a coverage of only a 
single sequencing reaction the confidence in the validity of these mutations is not overwhelming, 
and as they are all positioned very early in the promotor for the HygR gene the effect may be 
negligible. They do however hint at the possibility of undiscovered and potentially fatal mutations 
within the resistance gene itself. Standard protocols for the treatment of the plasmids does not at 
any point rely on the function of the marker block, but makes use of the kanamycin resistance on 
the backbone fragments. Since no selective pressure against mutations in the marker block is 
applied, a loss-of-function mutation could have gone unnoticed until hygromycin selection in the co-
cultivation protocol repeatedly fails to leave any live transformants. Another possible explanation is 
12,14-dimethylhexadecanoic-acid has an undesired bioactivity and that the cell has no way of 
getting rid of it, eventually leading to cell death. This explanation would rely on the assumption that 
the RTA1 like protein that FA04707 encodes, is in fact a transporter and acts as resistance for 2-AOD-
3-ol by moving it out of the cell. As RTA1 is shown to be a transporter of 7-aminocolesterol (Soustre, 
Letourneux, & Karst, 1996), it seems probable that RTA1 and the RTA1 like protein encoded by 
FA04707 relies in part on recognition of an exposed amino group on the compound to facilitate 
transportation. If this is the case, then the deletion of the aminotransferase in the pathway will 
mean that the transporter can no longer recognise and thus no longer transport the intermediate 
produced, causing it to build up in the cell with potentially fatal results. This hypothesis could be 
supported as the intermediate shares a lot of structural similarity to hexadecenoic acid, which has 
been shown to be toxic to bacteria in concentrations as low as 30 ppm (Johannes & Litaay, 2016). 
Given the current accessibility of sequencing, examining the HygR block of the pRF-HU2::ΔFA04705 
plasmid in search of loss of function mutations in the gene would be reasonable next step in the 
troubleshooting of construction this strain. Should a loss of function mutation be found within the 
gene, a new stock of plasmids would have to be prepared using a newly amplified stock of the 
marker brick. During the amplification of this fragment, it would be advisable to avoid using MgCl2 in 
the PCR reaction mix if possible. 
8.4. Difficulties with construction of ΔFA04703, ΔFA04709, ΔPKS12, and ΔaurP 
strains 
Although only one attempt was made at creating ΔFA04703, ΔFA04709, ΔPKS12, and ΔaurP strains in 
this study, each construct has been attempted in other studies using the same plasmid 
stock(Rasmus, Assoc, Frandsen, & Biology, 2014; Skovbjerg, 2016). It is possible although highly 
unlikely that each of the genes are vital in one process or another, however the absence of 
transformants with even incorrectly inserted marker genes makes it seem more likely that a 
mutation in the HygR marker is to blame. Such a mutation could have been introduced either at 
during the PCR reaction where the use of MgCl2 has been known to increase the rate of mutation in 
amplicons, or as a random mutation during replication in E. coli, which in the amplification step is 
not subjected to any selection against mutations in the HygR gene. The HygR cassette used for the 
==PAGE:46==
8. Discussion 
45 
 
constructions of the of the ΔPKS12 and ΔaurP plasmids is from the same stock as the one used for 
the ΔaurE plasmid which has been successfully used to transform F. avenaceum previously 
(Skovbjerg, 2016). This would suggest that any mutation of the gene will have occurred after the 
initial amplification by PCR. 
As with the ΔFA04705 strain, a reasonable next step in the troubleshooting of the construction of 
these deletion mutants would be to have the resistance marker in the plasmid sequenced to verify 
whether the trouble is caused by a non-functional marker gene. An alternative strategy could be to 
clone the vectors into E. coli under kanamycin selection, and then inoculating a colony living in 
kanamycin supplemented media onto hygromycin supplemented media. Since both resistance genes 
are located on the same plasmid no cloned colony should be resistant to one but sensitive to the 
other. If the transformed E. coli thrives in the presence of both selective agents a defect hygromycin 
resistance gene has been eliminated as an explanation for the failure to construct transformants. 
8.5. Phenotypic observasions 
Observing that the only visible difference between the strains is a variation in the intensity of the 
coloration, would suggest that the genomic change causes has an impact on pigment production in 
the fungus. This is supported by the complete lack of coloration across all three strains in the three 
media in which the investigated cluster appears to be silent. While the lack of conjugated double 
bonds makes it unlikely that 2-AOD-3-ol itself contributes to the coloration, it may serve as an 
inducer or substrate for pigment production facilitated by gene clusters located elsewhere in the 
genome. The absence of red pigment in LB, YPD and YPG and the absence of 2-AOD-3-ol in the same 
media could suggest that production of several secondary metabolites of F. avenaceum are not 
induced by conditions on these three media. 
8.6. Identified metabolites 
This study has identified 2-AOD-3-one as an intermediate in the biosynthetic pathway of 2-AOD-3-ol. 
While the lack of a ΔFA04705 strain leaves a gap in the proposed pathway, the loss of 2-AOD-3-ol 
and significant build-up of 2-AOD-3-one observed when comparing the F. avenaceum wildtype to the 
ΔFA04708 strain serves as strong evidence, that the reduction proposed as the last step in the 
hypothetic pathway is indeed the reaction facilitated by FA04708. Given that 2-AOD-3-one has been 
identified as an intermediate it seems likely that 12,14-dimethylhexadecanoic-acid is another 
intermediate as there are a limited number of ways an aminotransferase could synthesise 2-AOD-3-
ol. However, in this study 12,14-dimethylhexadecanoic-acid has not been identified, as 
measurements of the calculated adduct masses was not found to disappear or even be reduced with 
the deletion of FA04704. A possible explanation for this could be that the aminotransferase encoded 
by FA04705 converts the 12,14-dimethylhexadecanoic-acid into 2-AOD-3-one too soon after 
synthesis for the build-up of 12,14-dimethylhexadecanoic-acid to be registered above background 
signals. This seems plausible if the compound is even nearly as toxic as unmethylated hexadecanoic-
acid has been shown to be (Johannes & Litaay, 2016).

9. Conclusion

9. Conclusion 
This study shows that the deletion of the oxidoreductase gene, FA04708, causes a significant 
reduction in the production of 2-AOD-3-ol, as well as a large build-up of the intermediate 2-AOD-3-
one. From the data can be concluded that the oxidoreductase encoded by FA04708 does in fact 
facilitate conversion of 2-AOD-3-one into 2-AOD-3-ol as was proposed in the hypothesised 
biosynthetic pathway. 
A complete absence of coloration of F. avenaceum grown on YPD, YPG, and LB media, as well as the 
absence of any masses correlating to 2-AOD-3-ol and its common adducts was observed. Based on 
those observations this study can conclude that several secondary metabolites are not produced 
when F. avenaceum is grown on YPD, YPG, or LB media. 
It has been shown in this study, that F. avenacem produces far greater amounts of 2-AOD-3-ol in 
DFM than in CYA media. Although the data gathered in this study suggests that even more 2-AOD-3-
ol is produced when F. avenaceum is grown in rice instead of DFM, the different extraction protocols 
means that the validity of that finding remains uncertain. 
The data obtained in this study shows that the [M+Na]+ adduct is not a commonly observed adducts 
of 2-AOD-3-ol. Identification of 2-AOD-3-ol on a mass spectrum can instead be done through 
identification of the [M+H]+ adduct and the [M+H-H2O]+ adduct. 
Plasmids previously created for the deletion of the genes PKS12, aurP, FA04703, and FA04705 has 
been shown to have correctly assembled homologous recombination blocks and is concluded to be 
able to facilitate homologous recombination. The functionality of the resistance gene in the marker 
block of the plasmids has not been determined however. Sequencing the resistance gene would be 
an obvious next step to determine the cause of the transformation problems observed for these 
plasmids. 
A F. avenaceum ΔFA04704 strain has successfully been constructed and verified. The deletion 
mutant has been cultivated on six different media and the production of 2-AOD-3-ol and 
hypothesised intermediates were analysed by UHPLC-MS. From the data can be concluded that the 
polyketide synthase encoded by FA04704 play a significant role in the biosynthesis of 2-AOD-3-ol. 
Two F. avenaceum ΔFA04708 strains has successfully been constructed and verified. One of the two 
mutants has been cultivated on six different media and the production of 2-AOD-3-ol and 
hypothesised intermediates has been analysed by UHPLC-MS. From the data can be concluded that 
the oxidoreductase encoded by FA04708 plays a significant role in the biosynthesis of 2-AOD-3-ol.
