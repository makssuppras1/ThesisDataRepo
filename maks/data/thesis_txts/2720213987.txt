Abstract
The expression of speciﬁc microRNAs (miRNAs) involved in gastrointestinal cancer (GIC)
and chronic inﬂammatory enteropathy (CIE) was investigated in tissue samples from dogs.
Background
Studies show that alterations in miRNA expression are the cause and /or consequence of nu-
merous diseases. These miRNA expression/abundance patterns in tissue and in circulation
are being explored by many researchers for their regulatory role as well as their potential as
novel biomarkers in disease.
Methods
Total RNA was extracted from 20 healthy, 13 GIC, and 24 CIE canine intestinal biopsies
and converted to cDNA. Levels of 96 relevant miRs (based in our previous studies and in
literature) were investigated using quantitative real-time PCR (qPCR) in a high- throughput
platform (Biomark).
Results
18 microRNAs were found to be diﬀerentially expressed between GIC and healthy and cancer
and CIE groups. Some of them are known to be related to cancer and inﬂammation from
human studies. None of them correspond to the miRNAs found deregulated in serum or feces
of the same dogs. Further analysis considering the tumor site in the GIC group needs to be
done. Moreover, the genes targeted by the deregulated miRNAs could be also investigated
in the same tissue samples.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Dogs are second only to humans in receiving medical surveillance and preventative health care
[Ostrander et al., 2019]. A recent survey by Gallup (2022) found that 30 million Americans
had a dog experiencing cancer in the last ten years. In addition, the survey investigated the
impact of having an ill pet, showing that the canines’ illness had signiﬁcantly impacted the
owners’ quality of life. Providing a diagnosis can be challenging as diagnostic procedures for
dogs are often costly, potentially invasive, and time-consuming. An example of a complicated
process for providing a diagnosis of a gastrointestinal disorder is shown in ﬁgure 1.1
Figure 1.1: shows an entire consultation process at the hospital for companion animals
at Copenhagen University in case of a non-obvious condition with non-speciﬁc symp-
toms of a gastrointestinal disorder. (Lyngby, 2022 personal communication)
1
==PAGE:12==
Gastrointestinal disorders manifest in various unspeciﬁc clinical signs, such as vomiting,
diarrhea, weight loss, and anorexia; these non-speciﬁc clinical signs can make a ﬁnal diag-
nostic challenging because it is diﬃcult to distinguish between gastrointestinal cancer (GIC)
and chronic inﬂammatory enteropathy (CIE) [Lyngby et al., 2022]. In addition, the time-
consuming diagnostic process can delay the diagnosis, consequently causing tumor progres-
sion and potentially worsening prognosis [Araújo et al., 2022];[Dandrieux and Mansﬁeld, 2019].
In recent years veterinary oncology has shifted, with more demand for genetic screenings
and better diagnostics [Ostrander et al., 2019]. A potential solution for improving future
diagnostics of gastrointestinal disorders is the implementation of microRNAs (miRNAs) as
biomarkers in clinical practice. miRNAs are small, non-coding molecules important for gene
regulation and intercellular signaling. They are found in tissues and circulating in body ﬂuids
and other extracellular environments. Studies in various medical ﬁelds have identiﬁed miR-
NAs as promising biomarkers for diagnosing numerous medical conditions based on miRNA
proﬁles [Condrat et al., 2020]. Diﬀerent conditions can therefore be recognized by having
distinctive miRNA expression proﬁles [Cui et al., 2019]. Lyngby et al. (2022) have already
taken a promising ﬁrst step in establishing a non-invasive diagnostic biomarker panel in feces
and serum to distinguish between GIC and CIE in dogs.
1.1
Objectives and hypotheses
This project is a further expansion of the study of Lyngby et al. (2022) aiming to investigate
the miRNA expression proﬁles in tissue biopsies from the same dogs (GIC, CIE, and healthy
control groups) using quantitative real-time PCR (qPCR) with the main objective of inves-
tigating their relative expression to understand the molecular mechanisms involved in each
disease.
Hypotheses
• H1 There are speciﬁc miRNAs expressed in gastrointestinal tissue from dogs that are
healthy, have GIC, or CIE.
• H2 Some miRNAs in feces and serum correlate with tissue miRNA expression proﬁles
from dogs that are healthy, have CIE, or GIC.

CHAPTER 2
Background
2.1
MicroRNAs (miRNAs)
Deﬁnition
MicroRNAs (miRNAs) are small, non-coding molecules important for gene regulation and in-
tercellular signaling. They are found in tissues and circulating in body ﬂuids and other extra-
cellular environments. In recent years, interest in miRNAs has increased due to their involve-
ment in gene regulation pathways and association with many diseases [Tüfekci et al., 2014];
[Paul et al., 2018]. Current research increasingly identiﬁes extracellular miRNAs as potential
biomarkers for a variety of diseases [Wang et al., 2016];[Huang, 2017].
The conservation of miRNA genes in the genome across diﬀerent species points to the im-
portance of miRNAs’ role in biological processes. MiRNA genes are located in the introns,
exons, and intergenic regions of protein genes [Smolarz et al., 2022].
3
==PAGE:14==
Figure 2.1: miRNA biosynthesis. Figure modiﬁed from [O’Brien et al., 2018].
MiRNA Biosynthesis
The biogenesis starts with RNA Polymerase II transcription of the pri-miRNA from the
DNA sequence (nr.1 in ﬁgure 2.1). The double-stranded RNA molecule folds into a pri-
miRNA hairpin structure.
The maturation of miRNA happens throughout several steps
and can be classiﬁed into canonical and non-canonical pathways, with canonical being the
dominant pathway. The pri-miRNA ﬁrst needs to be cleaved. In the canonical pathway,
DiGeorge Syndrome Critical Region 8 (DGCR8) recognizes speciﬁc motifs within the pri-
miRNA, and a ribonuclease III enzyme, Drosha, cleaves the pri-miRNA duplex at the base
of the hairpin structure. The processing by the enzyme complex results in forming a 2 nt
3’ overhang on pre-miRNA [Denli et al., 2004];[Alarcón et al., 2015]. In the non-canonical
pathways, miRNAs bypass Drosha/DGCR8, and pre-miRNAs are generated by splicing
4
==PAGE:15==
(nr.2 in ﬁgure 2.1). In the canonical pathway, the pre-miRNA hairpin structure is exported
throughout the cell nucleus into the cytoplasm by Exportin5/RanGTP (nr.3 in ﬁgure 2.1)
[Alarcón et al., 2015][O’Brien et al., 2018]. Subsequently, a complex of TRBP RNA-binding
protein and RNase III endonuclease Dicer process the pre-miRNA and cleaves oﬀthe ter-
minal hairpin resulting in a mature 17–25-nucleotide miRNA/miRNA duplex. One of the
strands of the miRNA duplex is the guide strand, and the other is a passenger strand. Both
strands may be functional, depending on the 5’ end composition [O’Brien et al., 2018]. The
miRNA duplex combines with the complex to form an active RNA induced silencing complex
(RISC). The guide strand is incorporated into the binding domain formed by Ago 2 protein
and GW182, with the help of the HSC70–HSP90 chaperone machinery, while the passenger
strand is degraded (nr.4 in ﬁgure 2.1) [Condrat et al., 2020].
MiRNA function
The target mRNA is recognized by the microRNA carried in the RISC based on the comple-
mentarity of the sequence. The sequence can be a perfect match or partly complementary
[Duan et al., 2014]. Most bindings are partly complementary, where the miRNA binds to a
speciﬁc site known as the "seed" region. The seed region of mRNA is deﬁned as the ﬁrst
2–8 nucleotides [Filipowicz et al., 2008]. Because the miRNA sequence can be partly com-
plementary one miRNA molecule can target many mRNAs, and one mRNA can be inhibited
by diﬀerent miRNAs [Smolarz et al., 2022][Condrat et al., 2020]. miRNAs are shown to bind
to diﬀerent regions of their target mRNA including 3’UTR (untranslated region), 5’ UTR,
coding sequence, and within promoter regions [Pillai et al., 2004][Lytle et al., 2007].
The
diﬀerent binding sites have diﬀerent eﬀects on gene regulating, binding to 3’UTR, 5’UTR,
and coding regions induces transcriptional repression or degradation while binding to the
promoter region induces transcription [Lytle et al., 2007][Liu et al., 2005].
In addition to targeting mRNA, miRNAs can trigger intercellular signaling pathways by
acting as ligands and directly binding with the cell surface as Toll-like receptors (TLR)
[Chen et al., 2013][O’Brien et al., 2018][He et al., 2013].
It’s estimated that more than 50% of mRNA of protein-coding genes are the target of miR-
NAs [Krol et al., 2010]. miRNAs regulate the expression of the targeted mRNA via. cis- and
5
==PAGE:16==
trans-regulation as shown in ﬁgure 2.2. In Cis-regulation the miRNA either 1) targets the
mRNA directly, inducing transcriptional repression or degradation 2) by altering or inter-
acting with the promoter of the mRNA coding gene at post-transcriptional levels. In Trans-
regulation, the miRNA targets mRNAs that encode transcription factors or regulatory mech-
anisms that alter the transcription of their target gene [Liu et al., 2009][Nikitina et al., 2012].
Figure 2.2: Mechanisms of miRNA-mediated gene regulation [Liu et al., 2009]
All of the above variables contribute to the formation of complex networks of regulation
mediated by the miRNAs [Smolarz et al., 2022].

Circulating miRNAs
The main role of miRNA in mammals is gene regulation [O’Brien et al., 2018]. miRNAs
can elicit their function inside the tissue where they are synthesized or can be released into
circulation. Release into circulation can be by passive transport such as leakage secondary
to apoptosis, or from the environment of inﬂammation, or by active transport like exo-
somes/microvesicles, lipoproteins, and RNA-protein complex [Arroyo et al., 2011][Cheng, 2015].
The two diﬀerent mechanisms of release are known as vesicle-associated and non–vesicle-
associated [Cui et al., 2019] (ﬁgure 2.3). The circulating miRNA can bind to speciﬁc proteins,
which can be released directly or in vesicle-associated form [Wang et al., 2016].
Figure 2.3: Non–vesicle-associated and vesicle-associated miRNA release. Figure modi-
ﬁed from [Li et al., 2013].
The non–vesicle-associated form is mediated by release in 1) a protein complex, RISC (ex-
plained above); 2) in lipoprotein carriers high-density lipoprotein (HDL) and low-density
lipoproteins (LDL). HDL and LDL are lipid particles composed of a phospholipid bilayer shell
and hydrophilic core that can act as vesicles and carry miRNA [Michell and Vickers, 2016],
and 3) in the nuclear RNA-binding protein, nucleophosmin 1 (NPM1), involved in the ex-
port and protection of external miRNAs [Maggi et al., 2008]. The vesicle-associated miRNA
is transported in vesicles such as exosomes, microvesicles, and are released into circula-
tion by apoptotic bodies [Gallo et al., 2012][Arroyo et al., 2011].
It’s estimated that 10%
7
==PAGE:18==
of the circulating miRNAs are secreted in vesicle-associated form, while the 90% are se-
creted in non–vesicle-associated. Both transports prevent the digestion by RNases present
in the ﬂuids and make miRNAs a very stable molecule in circulation compared to proteins
[Cortez et al., 2011]. Studies suggest that some miRNAs are shuttled between diﬀerent sub-
cellular compartments where they regulate various processes, including transcription, trans-
lation, alternative splicing, and DNA repair. Others are secreted into extracellular ﬂuids,
acting as signaling molecules in the intercellular processes of recipient cells in distant tis-
sues [O’Brien et al., 2018]. miRNAs are proven to exist in all body ﬂuids including plasma,
serum, urine, saliva, seminal ﬂuid, cerebrospinal ﬂuid, breast milk, and other extracellular
environments [AlečkoviÊ and Kang, 2015][Cortez et al., 2011].
The abundance of circulat-
ing miRNAs has been proved to diﬀer in disease, especially due to the many examples of
interactions with the immune cells [Schickel et al., 2008].

2.1.1
MiRNAs as Biomarkers
A biological marker (biomarker) is a measurable indicator of a biological condition (i.e.health
or disease). Due to recent decades’ progressive optimization of techniques to detect miRNAs,
these small molecules’ role has become more evident [Galvão-Lima et al., 2021]. As previ-
ously described miRNAs are discharged from the cells, and are found circulating in bodily
ﬂuids.
The protective vesicles and protein complexes make extracellular miRNAs highly
stable.
miRNA has been shown to resist degradation at room temperature for up to 4
days, withstand multiple freeze-thaw cycles, boiling, and high and low pH [Chen et al., 2008]
[Mitchell et al., 2008]. Many studies about disease, including cancer and inﬂammation have
shown alterations in local and global miRNA expression. In some cases deregulated miRNAs
are the cause of disease, in others consequence of the disease [Wang, 2018][Zhang et al., 2018]
[Oliveira et al., 2019]. These deregulated expression patterns of miRNAs might potentially
be used as novel diagnostic biomarkers.

2.2
Chronic inﬂammatory enteropathy (CIE)
Chronic inﬂammatory enteropathy (CIE) is an umbrella term used to describe miscella-
neous gastrointestinal inﬂammatory diseases [Wennogle et al., 2017]. For the disease to be
chronic the dogs need to have clinical signs lasting at least three weeks. The clinical signs
include: diarrhea and/or vomiting, weight loss, changes in appetite, nausea, and abdomi-
nal pain [Dandrieux, 2016][Wennogle et al., 2017][Holmberg et al., 2022]. Diﬀerent types of
CIEs manifest in one or more diﬀerent parts of the gastrointestinal tract (GI). Depend-
ing on the severity, CIE may develop to involve both the small and the large intestine, or
even manifest in the stomach [Cerquetella et al., 2010]. CIE must be evaluated by histo-
logical examination of the mucosal inﬂammation and the cells in the intestinal wall. The
histopathologic ﬁndings are given a score based on the severity of the condition (based on
the WSAVA scoring system). The evaluation of the condition goes from "mild" to "severe"
[Wennogle et al., 2017]. And the last criterion for giving a diagnosis of CIE is that other
causes of chronic enteropathy (kidney disease, liver disease, endocrine diseases etc.) have
been ruled out [Jergens and Heilmann, 2022].

The lumen of the small intestine is inhabited by commensal bacteria, and the epithelial cells
are separated from the lumen by a mucus layer. Activation of the immune system leads to
a response of inﬂammatory cells, which cascades a reaction of innate and adaptive immune
cells [Baumgart and Carding, 2007].

The presence of the immune cells mediates the formation of diﬀerent compounds which cause
damage to the gastrointestinal tissue (see ﬁgure 2.6) [Stiegeler et al., 2021][Podolsky, 2002].
Some studies have suggested that changes in intestinal microbial diversity and distortions of
tight junctions between the epithelial cells disturb the epithelial and mucus layer barriers,
and drive the progression of CIE [Yeshi et al., 2020].
Once the protective layer is inter-
rupted , it leads to a cascade of immune cells contributing to an increased inﬂammatory
condition in the host [De Mattos et al., 2015][Cao et al., 2017]. Further interruption of the
mucosal barrier may occur, which allows the process to become chronic [Greger et al., 2006].
The epithelial cells have pathogen-sensitive innate immune pattern recognition receptors in-

innate immune cells. The innate immune system contains many immune cells, including
macrophages, dendritic cells, neutrophils, and natural killer cells. During CIE, the number
of macrophages increases, which induces proinﬂammatory signaling, leading to the activa-

of diﬀerent damaging compounds, which macrophages use to eliminate speciﬁc pathogens,

2.3
Gastrointestinal cancer (GIC)
Cancer is a condition deﬁned by cell growth out of control. Cancer is among the leading
causes of death in both humans and dogs [Hahn et al., 1994]. Tumors in dogs develop twice
as frequently as in humans and progress at a more rapid development pace [Hahn et al., 1994].
Canine gastrointestinal cancer (GIC) has similar histopathological features to GIC in humans
and has been suggested as a valuable model for human GIC [Hugen et al., 2017]. GIC is es-
timated to account for 8% of cancers in dogs [Lyngby et al., 2022]. It manifests in various
clinical signs, such as vomiting, diarrhea, weight loss, and anorexia [Lyngby et al., 2022].
The signs can develop gradually over weeks or months making diagnosing more diﬃcult.
As in humans, tumors in dogs can be benign (non-cancerous) or malignant (cancerous)
[Miettinen et al., 2002].
Benign tumors consist of an abnormal growth of cells that stay
at their primary site, where they slowly grow and sometimes turn into malignant tumors.
Malignant tumors consist of abnormal cell growth, which can be locally invasive and invade
other sites via the bloodstream or the lymphatic system [Patel, 2020]. The most common
type of GIC in dogs is Gastric carcinoma (GC), a malignant tumor type. GC is a hered-
itary condition that occurs mostly in breed-speciﬁc cases. Breed predispositions has been
reported most convincingly in the Belgian shepherd, Tervueren and Groenendael, but is
also observed in Staﬀordshire bull terrier, Rough Collie, Bouvier des Flandres, Standard
poodle, Norwegian elkhound, Hovawart and Chow-Chow [Hugen et al., 2017]. GC develops
from the epithelial cells that line the surface of the intestine and can extend throughout
the stomach [Lowe, 2001]. As the tumor progresses, the mucosal lining of the stomach is
aﬀected, and the wall is thickened with abundant ﬁbrosis tissue to protect the cancer cells
[Petras, 2010]. Tumor cells release signals that attract many immune-inﬂammatory cells.
These cells play an important role in the development of the disease. Indeed, the microen-
vironment of cancer is associated with a high density of tumor-associated macrophages and
T-cells (CD4+, CD8+ ,and FoxP3+), some natural killer cells (NK cells), and a few B-cells
[Wen et al., 2022][Ma et al., 2022a]. It has been proposed that the immune cells induce vari-
ous proinﬂammatory signaling molecules promoting tumor-related inﬂammation and inducing
immune-cell cascade [Wen et al., 2022]. As described in chapter 2.3 CIE, some miRNAs have
14
==PAGE:25==
been shown to play an essential role in the immune and inﬂammatiory response by either
enhancing or inhibiting signaling pathways [Stiegeler et al., 2021]. Nevertheless, the com-
plex dynamic of the cancer microenvironment is not fully understood and further research is
warranted [Ma et al., 2022a].

GIC is a complex condition that is inﬂuenced by a combination of diﬀerent environmental
and genetic factors [Hugen et al., 2017][Machlowska et al., 2020]. While the most widely ac-
cepted theory is that tumors are initiated by mutations in cell DNA, it’s also been found
that many tumors are initiated because of alterations in the expression of suppressor genes
[Croce, 2009]. Diﬀerent studies have shown examples of dysregulation of miRNA expression
patterns in cancer tissues [Cui et al., 2019]. There can be diﬀerent causes for the deregula-
tion of miRNA gene expression. Some of which include deletions of coding regions, ampliﬁ-
cations, alterations in the promoter, single-nucleotide polymorphisms in the genome, changes
in transcription factors, problems with the biosynthesis pathway, transcriptional errors etc.
[Zhang et al., 2007][Croce, 2009]. The origin of some cancers has been directly linked with
alterations of miRNA-controlled functions caused by the change in the expression pattern
[Nikitina et al., 2012][Croce, 2009]. These alternated patterns can lead to changes in the tis-
sue microenvironment by disturbing the balance between apoptotic mechanisms and cellular
proliferation [Galvão-Lima et al., 2021].

3.2
RNA isolation
3.2.1
Handling of RNA
When working with RNA samples, precautions must be taken. RNA is highly susceptible to
degradation due to both the initial chemical instability of the molecule and the omnipresence
of ribonucleases (RNases). RNases are enzymes that destroy RNA by digesting it. They are
stable, don’t need any co-factors to remain active, and are diﬃcult to inactivate. Even a brief
amount of time with RNase contamination is enough to destroy RNA [Roche, 2015]. There
are numerous sources of contamination with RNases; hands, laboratory equipment, air, lab-
oratory plastic ware, etc. Latex gloves are worn whenever handling samples or equipment to
prevent contamination from the hands. Equipment is kept clean, and working sheets in the
fume hood are changed regularly. Tubes are kept closed whenever possible to avoid contam-
ination from the air. Only RNAse-free certiﬁed microcentrifuge tubes are used. Laboratory
porcelain and steel tools are only used once and should be cleaned thoroughly and baked at
180 ◦C overnight.
3.2.2
RNA isolation procedure

Extraction of the total RNA, including small RNAs from biopsy samples , was carried out
according to the protocol "Puriﬁcation of Total RNA, Including Small RNAs, from Animal
Tissues" from the miRNeasy® Mini Handbook (Appendix E). Only samples stabilized in
RNAlater prior to freezing were selected for further extraction (expanded on in the opti-

handled per puriﬁcation.
Figure 3.4: Example of a sample
The biopsies were stored at -80◦C in RNase-free cryotubes as seen in ﬁgure 3.4 example.
During the extraction from the tube, liquid nitrogen was added regularly to keep the tissue
frozen. Tissue was not allowed to thaw to prevent degradation. The size of the samples
was necessary to take into account due to the column’s limited loading capacity. Therefore,
biopsies (ex. surgical biopsies) exceeding the recommended maximum weight of 30 mg were
made into smaller pieces using a mortar and pestle. Frozen pieces were submerged in 700µl
QIAzol Lysis Reagent (QIAzol). The blades inside the M-tubes rotate utilizing a combi-
nation of turbulence and mechanical shearing. As a result, cell membranes get destroyed,
and the viscosity of forming liquid is reduced so the RNA can be liberated from the cells.
Complete homogenization and tissue disruption are required to maximize RNA yield. After
the homogenization, the samples were incubated at room temperature to promote further
dissociation of the RNA.
Subsequently, chloroform was added to QIAzol to induce phase separation. The addition of
chloroform facilitates the organic extraction of RNA. Phase separation is promoted by cen-
trifugation at 4◦C. During centrifugation, the homogenate gets separated into three phases;
an aqueous phase, white interphase, and a pink organic phase. All the cell debris and proteins
are in the organic phase, DNA is in the interphase, and RNA is in the aqueous phase. The
21
==PAGE:32==
upper phase containing the RNA was transferred to a new RNAse-free Eppendorf tube. The
volume of the liquid was measured to calculate the amount of 100% ethanol for addition (1.5
volumes of ethanol need to be added). After the addition of ethanol and mixing, the sample
was transferred into the provided RNeasy Mini spin column. Ethanol facilitates binding of
all RNA from 18 nucleotides and longer to the column’s membrane. After centrifugation,
RNA remains bound to the membrane. Next, the column must be washed several times
with RWT and RPE washing buﬀers to remove all the contaminants from the membrane.
RWT is the stringent washing buﬀer that removes phenol and impurities. RPE is a mild
washing buﬀer that eliminates traces of salts [QIAGEN, 2020]. Furthermore, a DNase digest
step was performed between the washing steps to remove all the remaining DNA from the
sample. After the membrane was washed and free from contaminants, RNA was eluted in
RNAse-free water.

3.3
Determination of RNA quantity and quality
Prior to making (complementary DNA) cDNA, all samples were assessed for quality and quan-
tity. As described in the previous chapter, RNA is highly susceptible to degradation, and it’s
therefore vital to test the extracted RNA. According to the Minimum Information for Publi-
cation of Quantitative Real-Time PCR Experiments (MIQE) guidelines [Bustin et al., 2009],
a starting material of insuﬃcient quality interferes with the outcome of the qPCR gene ex-
pression data. RNA samples of inadequate quality should be hence removed from further
processing.
3.3.1
Quantity control
The concentration of RNA in the samples was determined by UV spectrophotometry. 1µl
of each RNA sample was measured using the NanoDrop (Thermo Scientiﬁc, Hvidovre, Den-
mark). The NanoDrop software calculates the concentration by measuring absorbance at
260 nm and applying Beer’s Law. Each absorbance unit is equivalent to containing 40µg of

single-stranded RNA/ml [Mannion et al., 2016]. To compare the gene expression in diﬀerent
samples, MIQE guidelines advise using approximately the same amount of RNA starting
material. The determined concentration was hence utilized to make adequate dilutions for

The measured concentration of all samples are found in Appendix B.

31
==PAGE:42==
Figure 3.15: Thermal protocol for qPCR
Figure 3.15 shows a graph of the qPCR steps. The program starts with the thermal mix
step, then raises the temperature to 95 ◦C to promote heat activation of the TaqPolymerase,
followed by 30 PCR cycles and melting curve analysis. In the ﬁrst step of PCR cycle the
temperature is raised to 95◦C for 5 min to denature the double-stranded DNA molecules. In
the second, it’s lowered to 60◦C for 20 min to promote the annealing of the speciﬁc primers
and elongation of the strands by Taq polymerase. At the end of each PCR cycle the amount of
ﬂuorescence is measured. When EvaGreen® binds to double-stranded DNA, the ﬂuorescence
increases by >1000-fold. The chip contains a passive reference dye Rox that the camera
inside the BioMark platform uses to regulate autoexposure, normalizing the signal when
taking pictures of the wells [Weissensteiner et al., 2003]. The results are ampliﬁcation curves
with initiation, exponential, and plateau phases. As more DNA is ampliﬁed, the ﬂuorescence
intensiﬁes, setting a threshold level. When a sample crosses the threshold, the speciﬁc cycle
of ampliﬁcation (Cq) value is determined [Adams, 2020]. Due to the non-speciﬁcity of the
EvaGreen®, the dye also binds to primer dimers or nonspeciﬁc ampliﬁcation products. The
control for unwanted products, the thermal cycler, is set to do a melting curve at the end of
the run (expanded further on in the next chapter) [Strachan and Read, 2004].

4.1
Evaluation of primer performances
The performance of each primer in the qPCR reaction was evaluated based on the primers’
melting curve and standard curves.
1.Melting curve evaluation
After the ﬁnal cycle of the qPCR ampliﬁcation, the thermal cycler is set to do a melting
curve. The melting curve analysis consist in 3 steps, denaturing at 95◦C , decrease tempera-
ture to 55◦C and increase temperature to 95◦C again while recoding the drop of ﬂuorescens.
As seen in ﬁgure 4.1, the melting curve should only have one peak equivalent to one amplicon
in the sample. If the melting curve has two peaks, it is interpreted as two diﬀerent amplicons,
which might be a result of primer dimer or unspeciﬁcity of the primers, which could have
bound to another cDNA [Downey, 2014].

4.2
Manual curation
Accurate statistical analysis requires high-quality data. Therefore, all the data was inspected
by manual curation using Fluidigm Real-Time PCR Analysis software (v.4.7.1, Fluidigm).
The aim of manual curation was to do a quality check and remove the outliers from the
data. The process of manual data curation is shown in the ﬂowchart ﬁgure 4.3 and will be
commented in the chronological order used.
Figure 4.3: Manual data curation ﬂowchart
1. Check of the negative controls.
The negative controls included nuclease-free water as a no template control (NTC) and sam-
ples without Poly-A polymerase as a no ampliﬁcation control (noPAP/NAC). Theoretically,
nothing should be ampliﬁed in the negative controls. However, if a consistent signal is de-
tected, it could indicate contamination or primer dimers. If there is a signiﬁcant amount of
ampliﬁed contamination in the negative control samples, whole chip results should be dis-
carded. In the samples with low signal, the ampliﬁcation curves of the negative controls are
compared to the samples to check whether they overlap. As a rule of thumb, if the Cq values
between samples and noPAP/NTC are less 3-5 cycles apart, the sample should be removed.
36
==PAGE:47==
2. Ampliﬁcation curve inspection.
Ampliﬁcation curves for each primer pair are inspected. Ampliﬁcation curves with an overall
high Cq value indicate a low/no presence of the target miRNA. If all the Cq values for a
speciﬁc assay are very high or not-ampliﬁed in a high percentage of the samples, the assay
is excluded from the analysis.
3. Melting curve inspection
The overall melting curves are evaluated based on their symmetry. The peaks should be on
top of each other without extra small peaks. If there is more than one peak, it’s interpreted
as unspeciﬁc primer binding (primer dimer) or as primers are bound to another cDNA. If
there is more than one clear peak present in the melting curve for a speciﬁc assay, the assay
has failed and is excluded from further analysis. Most of the samples have a nice distinct
peak in the melting curve, but few samples don’t. The outliers in an assay are then detected
by having extra/diﬀerent peaks or by lack of ampliﬁcation and they are excluded.
4. Primer quality check
As described in previous chapter, the primers were reviewed based on the standard curve
plots. The standard curve is used to calculate eﬃciency and coeﬃcient of correlation, R2.
Assays with values outside the accepted range are removed from the analysis. PCR eﬃciency
should be between 80-110% and the Rsq should be >0.98. If some assay does not show these
values, the assay is removed from further analysis.
5. Replicate comparison
The replicates (3.4 cDNA synthesis ﬁgure 3.10) are used to control the technical variation
introduced during the cDNA synthesis due to contaminants carried over from the RNA
isolation (i.e phenol, ethanol). The diﬀerence in Cq values of the replicates is calculated by
subtraction. The diﬀerence should not be more than 1,5 cycles apart. If the latter is the
case, the replicates’ melting curves must be reviewed to check for abnormalities in the plot
in order to ﬁnd the replicate that did not amplify properly. The replicate performing badly
is removed from further analysis.

4.3
Data pre-processing
After the manual curation, the qPCR experiment’s raw data must be processed by removing
technical variation before doing statistical analysis. GenEx Pro software (v. 7.1.1.118, Mul-
tiD Analyses AB) was used to pre-process the data. Steps of the pre-processing are seen in
ﬂowchart ﬁgure 4.4.
Figure 4.4: Flowchart of steps of the pre-processing
1.Interplate calibration
qPCR experiments containing a larger number of samples (>96) that cannot ﬁt in a sin-
gle chip, and they are run in diﬀerent chips which introduces an inter-chip variation. In-
deed , due to a baseline being created separately for each run, a bias is systematically
introduced. Therefore, it is necessary to correct the diﬀerences between the 2 runs/chips
[MultiD Analyses AB, 2001]. Interplate calibration of the Cq values from both chips was
done. The calibration was performed automatically by the Genex software, using the most
stable sample (2.B6) present on both chips.
2.Eﬃciency correction
The calculated eﬃciency value for each miRNA assay was applied to correct the measured
Cq values. The software uses eﬃciency to calculate each assay’s uncertainty and correct the
concentration in the samples, so the estimation of the concentration is within a 95% conﬁ-
dence interval [MultiD Analyses AB, 2001].
38
==PAGE:49==
3.Normalization
Normalization of the qPCR data needs to be done to minimize the technical noise, such as the
variation in the integrity of RNA, eﬃciency of reverse transcription reaction, and amount
of cDNA sample added in the qPCR reaction.
Therefore, the ﬁnal quality of the whole
dataset depends on the normalization method [Udvardi et al., 2008] [Huggett et al., 2005].
Two methods can be applied:
1.Use the global mean to normalize the samples. This method can only be applied to large
datasets.
2.Use of reference genes for normalization. All the assays are evaluated based on the stability
of the expression level using geNorm and NormFinder. geNorm and NormFinder are gold
standard methods incorporated into the Genex program for analyzing and ranking expres-
sion stability [MultiD Analyses AB, 2001]. A panel of 3 reference genes exhibiting the least
variable expression levels across all the samples and not-being co-regulated was chosen for
the dataset’s normalization; resulting references are shown in the table 5.5.
4.Average of technical repeats
The Cq values of the sample replicates are averaged and merged into one value.
5.Relative quantities
A reference group for data is selected. The group set as the reference samples was the healthy
control group of dogs and it was set to 1. Subsequently, all the other samples are scaled to
the control group.
6.Conversion to Log2
The data is converted from Cq values to Log2 scale, a more suitable data format for statistical
analysis on PCR data. Statistical analysis were performed using the GenEx Pro software.

Discussion
6.1
RNA extraction
RNA extraction of some samples failed due to a chemical reaction caused by RNAlater buﬀer.
Therefore, the extraction process of the pieces frozen in RNAlater was optimized by adding
an extra step to melt the buﬀer before homogenization. The added extra step did not aﬀect
the RNA yield or quality measured subsequently in the samples.
6.2
Quantity and Quality control
Reliable data relies on the quantity and quality of samples. The control step ensured that
almost all samples were quantiﬁed successfully.
6.3
cDNA synthesis
Due to the extra control step in the Mx platform and FlexSix chip, it was possible to check
for errors in the cDNA synthesis before loading the samples on the Fluidigm 96.96 chip. A

==PAGE:61==
systematic error was found due to the control, and the aﬀected cDNA samples were done
again. A cross-check showed that all the remade sample replicates generally had good repro-
ducibility on the 96.96 chip run. Prior to running the samples on the 96.96, a FlexSix chip
was used to verify the MX results and for the standard curve’s initial control.
6.4
Manual data curation
Bad assays: qPCR data curation cleaned the data from failed or poor-performing assays.
In total 20 assays (out of the initial 96 assays) were removed. Most of the failed assays did
so in both chips, indicating consistently bad-results. The few that variated also had poorer
performance on the second chip.
Bad replicates: The list of removed replicates shows that some replicates only had a few
data points removed, while others performed badly in all assays. As seen in ﬁgure 5.2 (bad
replicates mapped red), most of the marked positions are along the rim, indicating more
vulnerable positions. It could be the case that some of the samples were contaminated due
to inadequate sealing on the PCR plate while preamplifying or while removing sealing. The
sealing of the plates should be reevaluated.
6.5
qPCR data pre-processing
In the pre-processing we did interplate calibration in order to remove technical variation be-
tween the 2 chips run. Subsequently qPCR data was corrected for each assay PCR eﬃciency.
In fact, 4 assays were removed due to bad PCR eﬃciency (falling outside the range of 80-110
%). Furthermore, the qPCR data was normalized. As described previously, the accuracy and
reliability of the gene expression analysis depend on data normalization. Results from the 3
diﬀerent normalization panels showed signiﬁcant diﬀerences in the observed expression pat-
terns. Selecting reference genes is among the current technical challenges of using miRNAs
as biomarkers. This lack of a standard normalization process unavoidably introduces bias to
the data. After reviewing the three options of normalization panels, a decision was made to
use panel 3. The top genes selected by geNorm, belong to the same family of let-7 regulated

==PAGE:62==
by the same promoter. This co-regulation can potentially introduce bias to the data based
on normalization with only let-7. Therefore, the use of genes that are not was assessed to be
a better option.
6.6
Statistical analysis
The statistical analysis showed a diﬀerence in the expression of 18 miRNAs. All the signiﬁ-
cant expression diﬀerences were observed in the comparisons of healthy vs GIC and CIE vs
GIC. None of the genes were signiﬁcantly diﬀerentially expressed between healthy and CIE.
The statistical analysis (5.7) showed a diﬀerence in the expression of 18 miRNA between
GIC, CIE, and healthy control groups, respectively. 9 miRNAs showed signiﬁcant expression
diﬀerence between healthy and GIC and between CIE and GIC; 8 miRNAs showed signiﬁ-
cant expression diﬀerences only between healthy and GIC and 1 miRNA showed signiﬁcant
expression diﬀerence only between CIE and GIC (see Tukey-Krammer post-test table 5.7).
None of the genes were signiﬁcantly diﬀerentially expressed between healthy and CIE.

macrophages [Sheedy, 2015]. Many studies have conﬁrmed miR-21 expression is enhanced in
solid tumors, including breast, ovarian, lung, prostate, pancreatic, and thyroid, as well as in
colorectal cancer [Markou et al., 2016].
miR-126
miR-126 is detected upregulated in GIC cancer tissue compared to control and CIE. Other
studies have detected miR-126 involved in diﬀerent cancers, including genital tracts, breast,
thyroid, lung, gastrointestinal and other cancers [Ebrahimi et al., 2014]. Interestingly, miR-
126 has been connected to having both suppressor and oncogenic roles [Selven et al., 2021]. In
colorectal cancer tissue, overexpression of miR-126 has been observed to reduce cell prolifer-
ation and increased apoptosis, conﬁrming the cancer suppressor role [Ebrahimi et al., 2015].
Also, miR-126 has been shown as an oncogene by support the progression of cancer by pro-
moting the formation of blood vessels into cancer cells [Ebrahimi et al., 2014].

6.7
Tissue vs. serum and feces
The miRNAs were found to be most deregulated between GIC, CIE, and control in serum
and feces samples from the same animals according to Lyngby et al. (2022) were miR-451,
miR-223, miR-27a, miR-20b, miR-148a-3p, and miR-652. None of the mentioned miRs were
found to be statistically signiﬁcantly regulated in tissue.
Even though other studies have found signiﬁcant correlations between fecal miRNA and
miRNA from the gastrointestinal tract [Cui and Cui, 2020], in this study, the circulating
miRNAs do not reﬂect the expression pattern in tissue.
The relationship between miRNA in tissue with miRNA in body ﬂuids largely remains unclear
due lack of research [Cui and Cui, 2020]. Therefore, further research into the tissue of origin
of the excreted microRNA into feces and circulation (serum) is warranted.
6.8
miRNAs as biomarkers
The ﬁeld of miRNA research is still in the early stages of development. However, the potential
use of miRNAs as biomarkers is actively explored in numerous conditions [Condrat et al., 2020].
In recent years, research has been conducted to uncover many of the miRNAs functions. Al-
though many miRNAs have been connected to diﬀerent malignancies, consistent results are
missing [Galvão-Lima et al., 2021]. As a biomarker, the miRNA should be reliable in the
accuracy of disease prediction.
There are many examples of current ﬁndings lacking re-
55
==PAGE:66==
producibility. A study with diﬀerent teams proﬁling the same tissue showed a signiﬁcant
lack of reproducibility in ﬁndings. Even though the samples were analyzed using the same
proﬁling method, there were unable to reach a consistent detection of the expression levels
[Condrat et al., 2020]. The ﬁeld of miRNAs lacks accuracy in detection due to initially low
abundance of circulating miRNAs (i.e. blood , urine). qPCR is a sensitive method that re-
quires pre-ampliﬁcation. However, the pre-ampliﬁcation step increases the risk of alteration
in the actual concentration [Chen et al., 2009].
Currently, no standardized protocols have been developed for detecting miRNA and solving
all the technical challenges of the process [Galvão-Lima et al., 2021].
An additional obstacle is the methods used to analyze the data reliably.
There are no
validated panels of reference genes that can be used for qPCR data normalization in diﬀerent
tissues and body ﬂuids, this lack of reference genes is the primary source of the introduction
of bias into the data [Veryaskina et al., 2022].
The lack of universal methods and panels of miRNA speciﬁc for each disease makes it chal-
lenging to use miRNAs for diagnostic purposes; the ﬁeld of miRNA requires much more
investigation. Many challenges lay ahead in applying miRNAs as biomarkers in clinical prac-
tice.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusions and future objectives
H1 18 speciﬁc miRNAs were expressed diﬀerently in tissue samples of GIC compared to CIE
and the healthy group.
H2 None of the miRNAs found deregulated in feces, and serum in a previous study by Lyngby
et al. (2022) were found to be signiﬁcantly deregulated in tissue from the same dogs.
The important role of miRNAs in many malignancies has become more evident in recent
years.
As described in previous chapters, miRNA has many properties that make it a
promising non-invasive biomarker. However, there are still many challenges that need to
be addressed. Some of the technical challenges that need urgent solving are a lack of re-
producibility, of standardized protocols, and of reference genes to use for qPCR data nor-
malization. Another problem is the inconsistency and fragmentation of the study results.
There is no clear relationship between speciﬁc miRNAs and consistent deregulation patterns
in diseases. The network of suggested regulation pathways makes it diﬃcult to navigate.
Further research in miRNA networks is therefore warranted.

Agilent RNA 6000 Nano Kit

Always wear gloves when handling RNA and use RNase-free tips, tubes and water.
1. Thaw samples at 4◦C.
2. Ladder, Nano marker and nano-gel dye mix must equilibrate for 30 min to room tem-
perature before use. Protect dye and dye mixtures from light. Remove light covers
only when pipetting. The dye decomposes when exposed to light and this reduces the
signal intensity.

chips.
B Vortex solution well. Spin tube at 13000 g for 10 min at room temperature.

5. Loading the Gel-Dye Mix
A Place a RNA 6000 Nano chip on the chip priming station.
B Pipette 9.0 µl of gel-dye mix in the well marked.
C Make sure that the plunger is positioned at 1 ml and then close the chip priming
station.
D Press plunger until it is held by the clip.
E Wait for 30 seconds then release clip.
F Wait for 5 seconds slowly pull back plunger to 1ml position.
G Open chip priming station and pipette 9.0 µl of gel-dye mix in the wells marked.
6. Loading the Agilent RNA 6000 Nano Marker
A Pipette 5 µl of RNA 6000 Nano marker in all 12 sample wells marked.
7. Loading the Ladder and Samples
A Pipette 1 µl of prepared ladder in well marked.
B To minimize secondary structure, heat denature (70◦C, 2 minutes) the samples
before loading. Place on ice.
C Pipette 1 µl of sample (range 25-500 ng) in each of the 12 sample wells. Pipette
1 µl of RNA 6000 Nano Marker in each unused sample well.
D Vortex the chip for 1 min at 2400 rpm.
E Run the chip in the Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer within 5 min after loading the
samples.
F Load the chip and press start, select data ﬁle and add sample name.
G After end run, clean the electrodes as described above.

Prepared in advance
Preparing the Gel (use within 4 weeks)
1. Pipette 550 µl of RNA 6000 Nano gel matrix into a spin ﬁlter.
2. Centrifuge at 1500 g ± 20 % for 10 minutes at room temperature.
3. Aliquot 65 µl ﬁltered gel into 0.5 ml RNase-free microfuge tubes. Store at 4◦C.
Preparing the RNA ladder (after arrival of the kit)
1. After reagent kit arrival pipette the Ladder in RNase-free vial.
2. Heat denature it for 2 min at 70◦C.
3. Immediately cool down the vial on ice.
4. Prepare aliquots in RNase-free vials with the required amount for a typical daily use.
5. Store aliquots at -80◦C. After initial heat denaturing, the frozen aliquots needn’t be
heat denatured again.
