Abstract
This thesis investigates whether proximity to renewable energy technologies, specifically
onshore wind turbines and large-scale photovoltaic (PV) installations, is associated
with public acceptance of consumer CO2 taxation in Denmark. Using a nationally
representative survey of 4,024 respondents conducted in 2022 and high-resolution spatial
data based on driving distance buffers, the study integrates individual level coordinates,
socio-demographic variables, and detailed measures of renewable energy exposure to
explore spatial, social, and contextual mechanisms shaping attitudes toward fiscal climate
instruments.
Ordinary least squares and ordered probit models are estimated to assess the relation-
ship between renewable energy exposure and CO2 tax acceptance. The analysis reveals
that acceptance is positively associated with higher education and urban residence, while
households using straw as their primary heating source show significantly lower support.
Spatial exposure to renewables displays a non-linear relationship: higher concentrations
of nearby wind turbines and greater municipal penetration of rooftop PV are linked to
lower support for CO2 taxation, suggesting possible substitution effects between visible
renewable infrastructure and fiscal policy instruments.
Additional robustness tests indicate that the results remain stable across alterna-
tive spatial specifications and randomization orders of the survey questions, with no
statistically significant differences between sequences. However, significant heterogeneity
becomes evident across age groups, with older respondents showing stronger negative
responses to nearby wind turbines, whereas subgroup effects for large-scale PV remain
insignificant. These findings imply that attitudes toward CO2 taxation cannot be ex-
plained solely by economic reasoning or proximity based arguments such as the NIMBY
effect, but rather reflect broader contextual, perceptual, and socio-demographic dynamics.
The estimates should be interpreted as associations rather than causal effects. The
study concludes that understanding public acceptance of climate policy requires an
integrated perspective that considers how renewable energy deployment interacts with
fiscal instruments within local contexts.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
A variety of tools have been employed over the years to reduce CO2 emissions, in line with
the global commitment to achieve climate neutrality by 2050. Market-based approaches
targeting negative externalities have always been considered, and CO2 taxes are still seen
as one of the most cost-effective policy instruments for addressing climate change. In
fact, in Denmark, CO2 taxes are already applied to many consumer products, with prices
linked directly to their associated emissions (Danmarks Folketing, 2024; Skatteministeriet,
2021; Turna, 2025). However, they are not always preferred by the public and can impact
social inequality (Ladenburg, Kânoğlu-Özkan, and Soytas, 2024). Meanwhile, the rapid
growth of wind and solar power in recent years has established them as the largest sources
of carbon-free renewable energy. But they remain controversial, as local acceptance
is often conditioned by the ‘not in my backyard’ (NIMBY) effect. Yet the concept of
NIMBY has been widely criticized as overly simplistic, as it reduces opposition to a mere
rejection of project location while overlooking the diverse socio-cultural and contextual
factors shaping acceptance (Devine-Wright, 2009; Wolsink, 2000).
This raises important questions: What exactly drives attitudes towards climate
policies? Once wind and solar reach a power capacity that surpasses conventional sources,
will CO2 taxes still be necessary? Several papers have explored these issues, showing
potential interactive effects between the acceptance of low-carbon technology and climate
policy instruments (Ladenburg, Kânoğlu-Özkan, and Soytas, 2024). While no direct
correlation has been found between wind turbine exposure and support for CO2 consumer
tax, significant synergies arise when exposure is analysed along with socio-demographic
factors such as gender, age, and income. In the case of PV panels, acceptance is not
solely influenced by the technology itself, but heavily driven by a variety of contextual
factors, as shown in the master thesis by Espina Carreño (2024). Research suggests
that acceptance of renewable energy technologies is highly context-specific and strongly
shaped by the location of these facilities (Leiren et al., 2020; Rodríguez-Segura et al.,
2023). These findings reveal certain gaps in the literature that this study aims to address.
Previous research has used data from national surveys to analyse the general accep-

tance of low-carbon technologies and different climate policies (Ladenburg, Kim, et al.,
2024). However, people’s support may be conditional on such technologies being located
near their homes. Additionally, the data used in these studies are outdated. Another
limitation is that some studies assume that all respondents within a postal area are
equally affected by wind turbine exposure, as in the master thesis by Shankar (2023).
This assumption may lead to misleading conclusions, as households near the boundary of
a postal zone might have similar wind turbine exposure to those in adjacent areas, but
their exposure is not considered within their own zone.
This study aims to expand the research on proximity-acceptance interactions regarding
wind turbine and PV exposure and CO2 consumer tax acceptance. It will leverage updated
data from a survey about attitudes towards energy technologies for nearly 4000 Danish
citizens, along with relevant spatial and socio-demographic data from each respondent.
The spatial data includes detailed information on the number of wind turbines and PV
installations within varying distances from the exact location of the respondents, as
well as other surrounding features that might influence their preferences regarding CO2
taxes, based on where they live. The objective is to test whether the proximity to wind
turbines and PV panels and acceptance of CO2 consumer taxes are related, and how
these relationships are conditioned by socio-demographic characteristics. Additionally,
this research aims to move beyond the oversimplified “NIMBY” perspective and explore
it in depth, by linking high-resolution exposure data on wind turbines and solar PV
installations with support for a national CO2 consumer tax, rather than to project siting
alone. The analysis employs individual-level coordinates and road-distance measures,
compares multiple spatial scales and functional forms, and examines socio-demographic
heterogeneity and survey-order effects to capture context-dependent mechanisms.
In summary, the aim of this study is to analyse whether living near renewable energy
systems (PV/WP), correlates with higher or lower acceptance of CO2 consumer taxes,
and how this relationship is influenced by socio-demographic factors. To achieve this,
spatial data analysis will be conducted, and statistical modeling techniques, including
linear and ordinal regression models, will be employed to examine the relationships
between acceptance levels, proximity to wind turbines and PV installations, and various
socio-demographic factors. When combined with policy considerations, this study will
offer a more comprehensive and context-specific perspective, helping policymakers assess
and optimize different technology-policy combinations.
In light of these objectives, this thesis addresses the following research questions:
==PAGE:13==

3
1. To what extent is proximity to wind turbines and solar PV installations associated
with public acceptance of CO2 consumer taxes in Denmark?
2. How do socio-demographic characteristics influence this relationship between re-
newable energy exposure and CO2 tax acceptance?
3. To what extent can the observed patterns be understood beyond the oversimplified
“NIMBY” framework?
The present study starts from the hypothesis that residential exposure to renewable
energy installations does not systematically affect citizens’ acceptance of the CO2 tax.
In other words, average support for the tax is expected to remain stable across different
levels of exposure. The logic of this null hypothesis is that acceptance should remain
essentially flat across exposure groups, without systematic variation. Consistent with the
absence of strong prior expectations about the direction of any deviation, the hypothesis
is tested using a two-sided approach. At the same time, previous research suggests that
heterogeneous effects may arise once socio-demographic interactions are considered, for
instance with respect to age, gender, or household income (Ladenburg, Kânoğlu-Özkan,
and Soytas, 2024).

CHAPTER 2
Literature Review
Carbon taxes have been found to be among the most cost-effective tools for reducing CO2
emissions (Li and Zhao, 2017). However, they often face strong public resistance (Klok
et al., 2006). This opposition is driven by a lack of understanding, distrust in government
motives, and perceptions of unfairness (Levi, 2021). In addition, low-carbon subsidies
are often preferred, especially when the cost of switching to less carbon-intensive goods
is high (Steg, Dreijerink, and Abrahamse, 2006). This trend highlights that the choice
of climate policy instrument is highly dependent not only on their actual effectiveness,
but also on how effective and equitable they are perceived to be in comparison with
alternative measures. Public acceptance is therefore heavily influenced by how the costs
and benefits of such policies are distributed. Individuals tend to favor climate measures
they perceive as fair, transparent in revenue use, compensating those most affected,
and clearly explaining how the policy works and what its benefits are (Maestre-Andrés,
Drews, and Jeroen van den Bergh, 2019). In particular, acceptance tends to increase
significantly when tax revenues are allocated to environmental initiatives or returned to
citizens through rebate schemes (Carattini, Baranzini, et al., 2017).
At the same time, understanding public responses to renewable energy infrastructure
offers valuable insight into how individuals evaluate climate action, not only at the
policy level but also in terms of local implementation. While these technologies are
generally perceived positively and may be seen as substitutes or complements to carbon
taxes in reducing emissions, their deployment often faces local resistance, particularly in
the case of wind turbines. This resistance is largely driven by concerns over visibility,
noise, and perceived environmental impacts (Ladenburg, Kim, et al., 2024; Ladenburg,
Termansen, et al., 2013). More specifically, factors such as the number, density, size
of installations, and the frequency of visual encounters influence local acceptance. In
many cases, acceptance declines when individuals are exposed to a high density of small
turbines in their surroundings (Ladenburg and Dahlgaard, 2012), whereas fewer but
larger turbines may be more acceptable, as shown in the master thesis by Shankar (2023).
Regular exposure, such as daily encounters, can reinforce familiarity but does not always
==PAGE:16==

lead to increased acceptance. For instance, exposure to onshore wind turbines may
reduce acceptance of wind power, while increasing relative support for other technologies
such as solar or biomass (Ladenburg, 2014). Nevertheless, some individuals may support
the development of green projects if they perceive that these will bring benefits to the
community. This phenomenon is sometimes referred to as ‘yes in my backyard’ (YIMBY)
effect (G. Brown and Glanz, 2018). These findings suggest that the NIMBY hypothesis
does not fully explain opposition patterns. Indeed, opposition to renewable energy
projects cannot be reduced to mere selfishness or narrow place-protective behaviour,
but often reflects broader concerns about fairness, trust in institutions, and attachment
to place (Devine-Wright, 2009; Wolsink, 2000). While some residents oppose nearby
installations, others with prior exposure or contextual familiarity may show greater
tolerance or even support (Betakova, Vojar, and Sklenicka, 2015; Ladenburg, 2015a).
The observed patterns reveal the complex dynamics involving spatial context, technology
type, and local experience in shaping public acceptance.
Another key limitation involves the way spatial exposure is measured. Some studies
assume that all individuals within the same postal area are equally exposed to nearby
wind turbines (Ladenburg, Knapp, and Petrovic, 2024). However, this assumption can
be misleading. People living at the edge of one postal zone may be just as close to a
turbine as those in a neighboring zone, yet their exposure is not accounted for. This kind
of simplified approach can distort results, especially when visual proximity or density-
based measures would offer a more accurate reflection of how people actually experience
renewable infrastructure, as discussed both in prior research Ladenburg, Termansen, et al.
(2013) and in the master thesis by Shankar (2023).
Despite numerous previous studies on public acceptance of climate change mitiga-
tion technologies, most academic research examines attitudes toward renewable energy
technologies and carbon pricing mechanisms separately, often overlooking potential in-
teractions between the two. As a result, existing knowledge of how spatial exposure to
renewable infrastructure may influence support for fiscal climate policies, such as CO2
taxes, remains limited (Carattini, Kallbekken, and Orlov, 2019; Ladenburg, Kim, et al.,
2024). In addition, a significant share of empirical work relies on outdated or geograph-
ically aggregated data (Ladenburg and Dahlgaard, 2012; Ladenburg, Kânoğlu-Özkan,
and Soytas, 2024; Ladenburg, Knapp, and Petrovic, 2024). Given the evolving nature of
public opinion in response to technological and political developments, there is a clear
need for updated, spatially detailed, and nationally representative datasets that enable
==PAGE:17==

robust analysis of how public acceptance varies across social and geographic contexts
(Maestre-Andrés, Drews, and Jeroen van den Bergh, 2019).
Recent research has begun to explore the interaction between technology acceptance
and different climate instruments, with a recent PhD thesis by Zuch (2025) examining how
familiarity with local energy infrastructure influences support for broader climate policies.
Ladenburg, Kânoğlu-Özkan, and Soytas (2024) examines how exposure to renewable
technologies, such as wind turbines, may influence how individuals perceive and support
carbon taxes, providing a critical precedent for this thesis. Although exposure alone
does not consistently lead to increased support for carbon taxes, it is associated with
higher support among women, older individuals, and people from low-income households.
These results indicate that the impact of technology exposure on policy acceptance is
not uniform, but shaped by individual-level characteristics. Comparative studies also
show that citizens often evaluate taxes and renewables not independently, but relative to
each other (Carattini, Kallbekken, and Orlov, 2019; Levi, 2021). Moreover, individual
experiences and perceptions of local infrastructure may condition this relationship, yet its
interaction with solar PV technologies remains largely unexplored, particularly regarding
their interaction with climate policy acceptance and how this compares to public responses
to wind power (Ladenburg, Kim, et al., 2024).
These insights support the idea that technology and policy should not be evaluated
in isolation. Instead, integrated analyses that account for spatial, demographic, and
perceptual variables are essential to understand the complexity of public support for
climate action. Such an approach can contribute to the design of effective policy mixes
that combine carbon taxes with investment in renewable energy and green subsidies,
improving both environmental outcomes and public acceptance (J. van den Bergh et al.,
2021; Carattini, Carvalho, and Fankhauser, 2017). These mixed policy strategies may
help overcome the limitations of carbon pricing alone. While pricing mechanisms provide
a market signal that reflects the cost of emissions, they often lack the political support
needed for large-scale implementation unless complemented by tangible and context-
specific alternatives, such as investments in renewable infrastructure (Rosenbloom et al.,
2020).
Taken together, the discussed findings reveal several important gaps in the literature
that this thesis aims to address. First, most studies examine the acceptance of renewable
energy technologies and climate fiscal instruments separately, despite growing evidence of
potential interaction effects. Second, PV technologies remain underexplored in the context

of their relationship with climate policy support. Third, a significant share of the literature
relies on outdated public opinion data and low-resolution spatial information, such as
postal areas, which may fail to capture relevant local variation and lead to misleading
conclusions. Finally, interaction effects between technology exposure and carbon tax
acceptance are rarely investigated using spatially detailed and socio-demographic data.
While a significant number of studies have examined public acceptance of CO2 taxes,
relatively few incorporate exposure-related variables to assess their influence on attitudes
toward fiscal climate instruments. To better contextualize this thesis within the existing
literature, a comparative table of selected research on CO2 tax acceptance is provided
in Table 1. This table illustrates how sensitive tax acceptance is to different types

Against this limited background, the present study focuses explicitly on proximity
to renewable energy technologies, asking whether and how the local energy landscape
influences CO2 tax acceptance. Among the few studies that do consider exposure, the
only one addressing proximity to renewable energy infrastructure relies on subjective,
self-reported measures, such as whether respondents believe they live near wind turbines
(Ladenburg, Kânoğlu-Özkan, and Soytas, 2024).
While Ladenburg, Kânoğlu-Özkan, and Soytas (2024) provides a first and important
step by documenting heterogeneous links between wind turbine exposure and CO2 tax
support, the present study pushes this line of research further by integrating updated
high-resolution objective spatial data with individual-level survey responses from nearly
4,000 Danish citizens. Notably, it does not limit the analysis to wind turbines but also
incorporates proximity to solar PV installations, thereby extending the scope of previous
work. The survey data are also about a decade more recent than those used in earlier
work. Together, these features provide a more robust and precise assessment of how
renewable energy deployment influences support for CO2 taxation. This question is
particularly important because if renewed evidence shows that CO2 tax acceptance varies
systematically with the surrounding renewable energy landscape, then support for such
fiscal instruments cannot be understood solely in economic terms but must also be seen
as influenced by the energy landscape in which people live. The analysis explores how the
number and share of wind turbines and solar PV installations within varying distances
from respondents’ homes relate to attitudes toward CO2 consumer taxes, and how these
relationships vary across socio-demographic groups. It further accounts for ordering
effects in the survey design, and it also controls for household ownership. In doing so,
the study offers a more detailed understanding of how people experience and respond to
climate mitigation strategies, and how this experience shapes their support for carbon
taxation. Finally, it aims to move beyond simplified NIMBY assumptions by examining
the interaction between spatial context, individual characteristics, and public attitudes
toward policy instruments.

CHAPTER 3
Data
The survey used in this study gathered responses from 4,126 individuals and was designed
to analyse people’s perceptions of energy-related issues, including the acceptance of
CO2 taxes. It explored different aspects of Danish households’ energy behavior, such as
electricity consumption, energy flexibility, and attitudes toward new energy technologies.
The specific survey questions actually used in this study are presented in Appendix A.
Some of the main limitations and strengths are highlighted in this section.
Respondents evaluated their acceptance of a broad range of energy technologies and
policy instruments, covering nuclear power, district heating systems that utilize surplus
heat from energy and industrial production, the expansion of wind turbines (on land, at
sea, and in coastal areas), the deployment of solar panels (on residential and industrial
roofs as well as in open fields), energy-saving technologies, and several variants of carbon
capture and storage (CCS) solutions located either offshore, nearshore, or onshore in rural
or urban areas. The questionnaire also included two fiscal instruments: one targeting
citizens and one targeting corporations through CO2 taxation. Only the item concerning
the citizens’ CO2 tax is analyzed in this thesis, while a detailed analysis of technology
acceptance across all items is presented in Ladenburg, Kim, et al. (2024).
Respondents were randomly assigned to one of two versions of the questionnaire in
which the sequence of items on CO2 taxation and energy technologies was reversed. In
one version, the question on CO2 taxes was placed before the block of technology-related
items (tax–to–nuclear order), while in the other it appeared at the end of the block
(nuclear–to–tax order). The nuclear–to–tax sequence is provided in Appendix C.

contains responses from 513 postal codes, i.e., 87.1% of all postal areas covered in the
dataset. The survey thus does not cover respondents from every postal code area in

Additionally, the dataset does not include several contextual and socio-political
variables that could have provided a more comprehensive picture of the factors influencing
CO2 tax acceptance. It lacks information on respondents’ awareness of, or attitudes
toward, specific local renewable energy projects, which could influence broader attitudes
toward climate policy, particularly if certain projects are perceived as controversial or
unfairly distributed. The absence of this information limits the ability to fully assess how
direct or indirect exposure to renewable technologies affects tax acceptance, as individuals
often exhibit varying inclinations based on project-specific attributes (Graziano and
Gillingham, 2015; Heras-Saizarbitoria, Cilleruelo, and Zamanillo, 2011).

influence acceptance of a CO2 tax (Ladenburg, Kânoğlu-Özkan, and Soytas, 2024). In
addition, socio-political indicators such as political orientation were not recorded, yet our
examination of the relationship between advanced education and CO2 tax acceptance
suggests that it could be an important explanatory factor. Several contextual factors
relevant to rural residents are also missing, including views on the role of government, the
availability of alternative transportation, preferences for market-based versus regulatory
solutions, and economic ties to extractive industries. Furthermore, measures of perceived
fairness in CO2 taxation, together with trust in institutions and attachment to place, also
limit the ability to capture opposition patterns accurately, challenging the oversimplified
assumptions of the NIMBY hypothesis. The absence of these variables may limit the
ability to account for important determinants of attitudes toward both renewable energy
technologies and climate policy instruments, particularly in rural settings, an issue further
addressed in the Discussion.

attitudes such as political orientation, institutional trust, or perceptions of climate change

words, omitted-variable bias would arise only if individuals who hold different climate or

CHAPTER 4
Methodology
This section outlines the analytical approach used to examine the relationship between
renewable energy exposure and CO2 tax acceptance. It begins with an overview of
the research design, data structure, and variables, followed by the model specification
and estimation strategy used to test the hypotheses and identify statistically significant
patterns.
4.1
Research Design, Data, and Variables
This study employs a quantitative, cross-sectional design in which each observation
corresponds to an individual respondent in a nationally representative survey conducted
in Denmark. The chosen approach is well suited to addressing the research questions, as
it combines objective spatial measures of renewable energy infrastructure with subjective
perceptions, attitudes, and socio-demographic characteristics that can only be captured
through direct survey responses.
Spatial and survey data were provided linked in CSV format, with individual re-
spondent coordinates matched to official infrastructure data from Statistics Denmark.
The dataset included pre-computed spatial exposure measures based on road-distance
calculations from respondents’ locations to wind turbines, PV installations, and forested
areas. All subsequent data preparation and analysis were conducted in Python.
The dependent variable, Tax_Citizen, measures the extent to which respondents
support “using taxes and fees to reduce the citizens’ CO2 emissions.” As an ordinal
categorical variable, it was coded on a five-point ordinal scale ranging from 1 = To a very
small extent to 5 = To a very large extent. This ordinal mapping allows the variable to
be treated consistently in regression models that account for ordered categories.
The variable Q_Order captures the randomization of the questionnaire sequence. It
reflects whether the CO2 tax question was presented before or after the set of technology-
related items, and is later analysed in Survey Design Effects.
==PAGE:34==
24
4 Methodology
Key independent variables capture spatial exposure to renewable energy infrastructure,
all measured as driving-distance buffers rather than straight-line distances.
Together, these spatial measures capture complementary dimensions of how respon-
dents may experience renewable energy infrastructure in their surroundings: (i) detailed,
distance-based indicators derived from respondents’ exact household coordinates (e.g.,
number of onshore wind turbines and utility-scale PV within a specified driving-distance
buffer); and (ii) aggregated contextual indicators available only at broader adminis-
trative scales (e.g., the municipal share of households with rooftop PV). In addition,
an environmental control quantifies nearby forest cover to account for differences in
landscape characteristics. This distinction makes clear which indicators reflect precise
individual-level exposure and which represent broader contextual conditions.
For wind turbines, the variable N_Wind_6km measures the number of turbines within 6
km from each respondent’s home. In the final specification, its squared term (N_Wind_6km2)
is employed. For solar energy, two measures are considered: PV_Share_5km, which cap-
tures the local surface coverage of large-scale PV installations within 5 km, and PV_Share,
the share of households with rooftop PV installations in the municipality. For PV_Share,
the log-transformed version (Log_PV_Share) is used, while no transformation is applied
to PV_Share_5km. An additional contextual control, Forest_3km, measures forested area
within 3 km.
Although the municipal share of rooftop PV (PV_Share) provides useful contextual
information on local renewable penetration, it is based on aggregated administrative
data. As such, it shares the same limitation as previous postal-zone measures discussed
in Data: respondents living near municipal borders may experience exposure patterns
that differ from the municipal average. This variable should therefore be interpreted as a
contextual indicator rather than a precise spatial measure of proximity.
Control variables capture a range of socio-demographic, household, and contextual
characteristics, including housing type, ownership status, primary heating source, car
and PV ownership, history of power outages, age, marital status, education level, income,
gender, urban versus rural residence, the sequence in which questions appeared in the
survey, and information treatment variables related to carbon capture technologies. Bi-
nary categories (e.g., gender, rural vs. urban, rental vs. ownership, renewable vs. fossil
heating) were coded as dummy variables, nominal categorical variables (e.g., education
levels, heating type, and home type) were one-hot encoded, income was normalized to
the [0,1] range, forest variables were standardized, and age was centered by subtracting

the minimum observed value. Boolean variables such as Power_Outage were converted
to integers (1/0). To avoid multicollinearity and reduce redundancy, several variables
were merged into broader groups or excluded from the analysis. Multicollinearity arises
when two or more predictors are highly correlated, such that one can be linearly pre-
dicted from the others with a high degree of accuracy. For example, including all car
ownership or CCS information treatment categories simultaneously would create perfect
collinearity, since one category can be exactly inferred from the combination of the others.
To avoid this, some categories such as Car_Both and Info_CCS_Baseline were omit-
ted. Similarly, several other housing dummies were removed (House_Own, House_Coop,
House_Apt, House_Room, House_Other) to prevent redundancy and multicollinearity
in the model specification.
In addition, overall counts of rooftop PV installations
(N_PV) were dropped because they are already captured by the ratio PV_Share, and
Home_Size showed strong correlation with dwelling type and tenure.
In regression
models, such redundancies increase standard errors, reduce the reliability of coefficient
estimates, and complicate interpretation (Gujarati and Porter, 2009). To simplify the
specification and facilitate interpretability, the following groupings were implemented:
House_Villa and House_Cluster were combined into a single category (House_Villa);
Edu_Academic_Training and Edu_Bachelor were collapsed into Edu_Bachelor; and
Edu_Master and Edu_PhD were grouped as Edu_MasterPhD.
After all data cleaning and processing steps, the final analytical sample consisted of
4,024 respondents with no missing data.

model, which provides straightforward interpretation of the coefficients and comparability
across alternative specifications.

The main focus of the analysis lies on the spatial exposure variables in Zi, particularly

shaping support for a CO2 tax. It is important to note that these models capture
associations rather than causal effects. The relationship between renewable energy
exposure, especially wind turbines, and CO2 tax support can in principle operate in both
directions, either because local renewable exposure directly shapes citizens’ attitudes
toward CO2 taxation, or because individuals with pre-existing policy preferences self-
select into areas with more (or fewer) renewable installations. The results should therefore
be understood as correlations rather than causal claims.
4.3
Estimation Strategy
In line with the stated null hypothesis, the empirical models evaluate whether exposure
to renewable energy installations is associated with systematic variation in CO2 tax
acceptance. Given the lack of strong prior expectations regarding the sign of the effect,
the estimations rely on two-sided significance tests.
==PAGE:37==
4.3 Estimation Strategy
27
The analysis followed a hierarchical modeling strategy in which alternative buffer
sizes and combinations of spatial exposure variables were iteratively tested. For each
specification, the explained variance (R2) was recorded in order to assess which measures
of exposure contributed most strongly to model fit. Buffer distances of 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, and
10 km were compared, and specifications including both continuous shares and dummy
indicators were examined to determine whether indicator variables added explanatory
power beyond percentage measures.
The combination that yielded the most consistent improvement in model fit was:
S∗= {PV_Share_5km, N_Wind_6km, Forest_3km}
In cases where both dummy and continuous specifications existed for the same type
of exposure (e.g., solar, wind, or forest), the choice between them was guided by their
relative explanatory contribution and interpretability. For solar, the continuous share
within 5 km was preferred, as it captured more variation in local penetration than a
simple presence indicator. For wind, the count of turbines within 6 km was selected,
not only because it provided a stronger fit than the dummy, but also because it allowed
modeling of non-linear threshold effects consistent with the literature (Betakova, Vojar,
and Sklenicka, 2015; Ladenburg and Dahlgaard, 2012). For forest exposure, neither the
absolute area (Forest_3km) nor the relative share (Forest_Share_3km) showed statistical
significance. The absolute measure was retained, as it offers a clearer interpretation
of local forest cover and the proportional specification did not provide further insight.
This yielded the optimal set adopted as the baseline spatial model, ensuring that the
specification avoided redundancy and remained interpretable.
Results from alternative buffer distances revealed that the explanatory contribution
of wind exposure was relatively stable: across most buffer sizes, wind-related variables
remained statistically significant, in some cases at p < 0.01. By contrast, neither PV
exposure nor forest cover yielded robust statistical significance across any specification.
Detailed results of these robustness checks are presented in Appendix F. Based on these
comparisons, wind exposure was the only variable to show consistently significant effects
across alternative buffer distances.

captured the relationship between local exposure and CO2 tax acceptance. These compar-

CHAPTER 5
Empirical Analysis
This section presents the main empirical findings obtained from the regression models
described in Methodology. It begins with the results of the baseline estimations, fol-
lowed by analyses of question order effects and heterogeneity across socio-demographic
groups, highlighting the variables that show the most robust and statistically significant
associations.
5.1
Main Results
For ease of interpretation, the main analysis relies on linear regression, which allows direct
interpretation of the estimated parameters in terms of the level of CO2 tax acceptance.
As a robustness check, an ordered probit model was also estimated to explicitly account
for the ordinal structure of the dependent variable.
The results of the baseline OLS specification are presented in Table 3, while Figure 4
illustrates the estimated coefficients with their 95% confidence intervals. The adjusted R2
of the model is 0.0346, indicating that although the overall explanatory power is limited,
as expected in individual-level survey data, the included variables capture systematic
variation in support for a consumer CO2 tax. The results of the ordered probit (see
Table 12 and Figure 9 in Appendix I) are consistent with the linear regression estimates
and do not alter the overall conclusions, confirming that the results are not sensitive to
the treatment of the dependent variable as continuous.
When looking at socio-demographic factors, the estimates show that higher levels of
education are clearly linked to greater acceptance of the CO2 tax. Respondents holding
a Master’s degree or PhD are significantly more supportive, while those with vocational
training show no evidence of a difference relative to the reference group of respondents
with primary or secondary education. Within this lower-education group, individuals
coded as Edu_DK (those assumed to have primary or secondary education but who did
not explicitly report it) show notably lower support, suggesting that non-response in this
category is systematically associated with weaker acceptance of the tax.
==PAGE:42==
32

Gender differences are relatively small but point towards lower support among
men compared to women, although the effect does not reach statistical significance
at conventional thresholds (p > 0.05). Household income has no systematic positive
association with acceptance. For respondents who did not report their income, the
continuous Household_Income variable was set to the sample mean of (600,000–699,999
kr.), and a separate dummy (Income_DK) was included to capture this group. The
coefficient on Income_DK is negative and significant, indicating that income non-response
is systematically associated with lower tax acceptance. Living in urban areas is positively
associated with acceptance, while most household characteristics, such as housing type,
heating system, or car ownership, as well as contextual factors like experienced power
outages, do not show clear effects. One notable exception is households that rely on straw
as their main heating source, which are significantly less supportive. The information
treatments related to carbon capture and storage (CCS) similarly show no systematic
influence on CO2 tax acceptance.
The analysis of spatial exposure variables shows systematic relationships between
local renewable energy landscapes and CO2 tax acceptance. At the municipal level, the
log-transformed share of rooftop PV installations is negatively and strongly associated
with acceptance, suggesting that greater solar penetration correlates with reduced support
for consumer carbon taxes. The logarithmic specification implies that the first increases
in PV adoption within a municipality are associated with the strongest reductions in tax
support, while additional increases have progressively smaller marginal effects.
This result is consistent with the idea that visible alternatives to taxation, such as
subsidies for renewable technologies, may reduce willingness to back fiscal measures. By
contrast, the share of large-scale PV installations within 5 km does not reach significance,
and forest cover within 3 km also shows no effect, indicating that these contextual factors
play a limited role. The most notable result concerns wind exposure: the quadratic
specification within a 6 km radius reveals a significant negative effect, pointing to a non-
linear relationship. While exposure to a small number of turbines does not significantly
affect attitudes, higher concentrations are associated with much lower acceptance of the
CO2 tax. This pattern suggests a threshold effect rather than a linear distance decay,
where the presence of multiple nearby turbines triggers stronger opposition to taxation.
Overall, the evidence demonstrates that renewable energy exposure, particularly to wind
power, is systematically related to attitudes towards carbon pricing.
Compared to the findings of Ladenburg, Kânoğlu-Özkan, and Soytas (2024), the

results of this analysis highlight an important difference. While their study found no
average effect of wind turbine exposure on tax acceptance unless interactions with socio-
demographic factors such as gender, age, and income were taken into account, the present
results identify direct and statistically significant correlations even without introducing
such interaction terms. In particular, the negative association with wind exposure and the
substitution effect of solar PV suggest that technology–policy interactions are observable
in this dataset without the need to include interaction variables. Nevertheless, the overall

understood as associations rather than causal effects.
Taken together, the analysis shows that CO2 tax acceptance is shaped both by
socio-demographic characteristics and by the local energy landscape. Higher educa-

household groups such as straw-heating households are less supportive. With respect to
spatial variables, greater municipal-level penetration of rooftop PV systems and high
local concentrations of wind turbines are associated with lower acceptance. These findings
therefore provide evidence of exposure–acceptance relationships operating through differ-

level, with no statistically significant influence of large-scale PV within 5 km.

5.2
Question Order Effects
In the previous survey by Ladenburg, Kânoğlu-Özkan, and Soytas (2024), the question
on CO2 taxation appeared in a fixed position, and all respondents received the same
item sequence (see Appendix B). In contrast, the present survey design introduces
randomization to examine whether the order of questions influenced reported support
for CO2 taxation. Respondents were randomly assigned to one of two versions of the
survey that varied the sequence of the energy-technology questionnaire: “To what
extent do you think Denmark should use the following technologies to reduce CO2
emissions?” (see Appendix C). In total, 2,024 respondents received the sequence from tax-
to-nuclear (bottom–up), while 2,000 respondents saw the reverse order from nuclear-to-tax
(top–down). The baseline OLS model was re-estimated separately for each version of the
questionnaire, using identical specifications but restricting the sample to respondents
exposed to either the tax-to-nuclear or the nuclear-to-tax sequence. The estimated
coefficients with 95% confidence intervals are illustrated in Figure 5, and the complete
regression results for both models are provided in Appendix J.
A large survey-methods literature shows that order and visual context are rarely
neutral and can systematically shape answers even when content is identical. Controlled
experiments in web surveys and classic split-ballot studies demonstrate that even modest
==PAGE:47==
5.2 Question Order Effects
37
shifts in placement or sequence can alter responses (Bishop and Smith, 2001; Couper, F. G.
Conrad, and Tourangeau, 2007; Tourangeau, Couper, and F. Conrad, 2004). In stated-
preference designs, repeated blocks often reveal substantial order and learning effects:
later blocks tend to show lower error variance and produce shifts in parameter estimates
and willingness-to-pay measures compared to earlier blocks (Carlsson, Mørkbak, and
Olsen, 2012). Here, “repeated blocks” refer to multiple sets of similar stated-preference
tasks shown to the same respondent. In this study, the tax-to-nuclear vs. nuclear-to-
tax split serves as an analogue: although the tasks are not identical, the preceding
technology block provides context that can shape subsequent responses on CO2 taxation.
Additionally, people tend to favor options that appear first in a list, and changing the
order can alter the variability and correlations of answers even when underlying attitudes
remain stable (Krosnick and Alwin, 1987). This dynamic is also relevant in our survey:
when respondents first evaluate more controversial or distant options such as nuclear
power or underground CO2 storage, this may spill over into how they subsequently assess
familiar technologies like rooftop solar panels or policy instruments such as CO2 taxes.
Overall, estimating models separately by question order is a standard diagnostic for
assessing order sensitivity in CO2 tax acceptance.
Beyond survey mechanics, contextual spillovers show that answering questions in one
domain (e.g., environmental attitudes) can influence later judgments in other domains,
framing subsequent policy evaluations (Nilsson, Bergquist, and Schultz, 2017). In the
previous survey by Ladenburg, Kânoğlu-Özkan, and Soytas (2024), the technology block
appeared at the beginning of the survey, whereas in the present version it was placed near
the end, following several attitudinal and contextual items (see Appendices A and B).
Such framing shifts illustrate distal order effects, where prior survey content may influence
how subsequent valuation questions are understood and answered (Cai, Cameron, and
Gerdes, 2011).
The results reveal clear differences between the two groups. In the tax–to–nuclear
sequence, higher education (Master’s/PhD) is positively and significantly associated
with tax acceptance, while respondents coded as Edu_DK and Income_DK are significantly
less supportive. The quadratic wind exposure variable (N_Wind_6km2) shows a negative
and significant effect, indicating that the impact of nearby wind turbines is only robust
when the tax question is asked first. In addition, the log-transformed municipal share of
rooftop PV installations has a marginally significant negative association, whereas the
local 5 km share of large-scale PV remains non-significant.
==PAGE:48==
38
5 Empirical Analysis
In contrast, when the nuclear question comes first, wind exposure is no longer
significant, and the strongest predictors of acceptance shift toward socio-demographic
factors. Respondents living in urban areas or reporting married or single status (relative to
other categories) are significantly more supportive, while education and income variables
become less robust. Among the spatial variables, the municipal share of rooftop PV
remains weakly negative but does not reach statistical significance, and the 5 km share
of large-scale PV again shows no effect.
Taken together, these results suggest a modest but systematic order effect: the
statistical significance of renewable energy exposure variables, especially wind, depends
on whether CO2 taxation appears first or last in the survey, consistent with prior evidence
that sequencing can shift measured associations even under random assignment.
Nevertheless, to further examine whether the order in which the survey questions
were presented truly influenced the estimated relationships, independent two-sample
t-tests were conducted comparing the coefficients from the two randomized versions of the
questionnaire (tax-to-nuclear and nuclear-to-tax). The results for the spatial variables
are summarized in Table 4, while the complete list of coefficient comparisons is provided
in Table 15 in Appendix J.
The tests evaluate whether the parameter estimates obtained under each order differ
significantly. For the spatial exposure variables, the results indicate that none of the
coefficients differ significantly at conventional 5% levels across the two orders. The
wind exposure variable (N_Wind_6km2) shows the largest discrepancy, with a negative
coefficient that is statistically significant only when the CO2-tax question appears first,
yet the difference across orders is borderline significant at the 10% level (p = 0.063). This
pattern, therefore, appears to indicate that respondents who received the tax-to-nuclear
version were slightly more responsive to nearby wind turbines. Likewise, the estimates for
solar PV exposure (Log_PV_Share and PV_Share_5km) and forest cover (Forest_3km)
show negligible differences (p > 0.40 in all cases). These results suggest that, although the
apparent statistical significance of certain parameters may vary depending on question
order, the underlying coefficients remain broadly consistent between the two versions.
In line with the spatial variables, only a few socio-demographic parameters show
marginal differences across orders (e.g., Single, Married and Heating_DH), and even
these effects remain small in magnitude.

CHAPTER 6
Discussion
This section discusses the main findings in relation to the existing literature and the
research questions outlined in the Introduction, beginning with the variables of highest
statistical significance.
6.1
Socio-Demographic Factors
Individuals with advanced education tend to have greater environmental awareness, better
understanding of climate policies, and higher willingness to support climate mitigation
measures like carbon taxes (Alberini, Ščasný, and Bigano, 2018; Becchetti and Conzo,
2022; Tatham and Peters, 2023; Zhang, Ferreira, and Karali, 2024).
However, the effect of education on climate policy support is not straightforward. As
Welsch (2022) argues, while education generally improves climate knowledge, it can also
intensify ideological and moral polarization, making it an imperfect tool to counteract
climate skepticism. Similarly, Ballew et al. (2020) finds that education does not reduce
ideological differences but rather amplifies them, particularly among conservatives, and
even more so among conservative white men. A. R. Brown and Hamilton (2021) further
demonstrates that support for carbon taxes does not increase uniformly with education
but varies significantly depending on political orientation. For instance, while education
enhances support among Democrats, it may reduce it among Tea Party conservatives.
Although this study does not include a variable capturing respondents’ political orien-
tation, other mechanisms may help explain the observed relationship between education
and support for CO2 taxation. One possible explanation is that individuals with higher
education levels are more likely to recognize carbon taxation as an economically efficient
tool for addressing climate change. Economists and international organizations strongly
recommend carbon taxation as the most efficient way to reduce CO2 emissions, internal-
izing the externality by taxing consumption based on its carbon intensity (Ghazouani
et al., 2020). In this logic, the price of a good reflects its associated carbon emissions,
allowing market mechanisms to drive more sustainable choices. Individuals with higher
==PAGE:54==
44
6 Discussion
education may be more familiar with these concepts and therefore more likely to support
such instruments, regardless of their personal beliefs about climate change. In contrast,
those with lower levels of education may be less exposed to this economic reasoning and
thus less predisposed to support it.
Place of residence also plays an important role in shaping attitudes toward CO2
taxation. Urban residents tend to support CO2 taxation more than rural residents.
Ewald, T. Sterner, and E. Sterner (2022), in a study conducted in Sweden, finds that
rural populations express significantly less support for carbon taxes compared to urban
counterparts. This opposition is primarily attributed to perceived unfairness and a lack
of viable transportation alternatives. Similarly, Mildenberger et al. (2016) documents a
persistent pattern in Canada: urban populations are more likely to acknowledge climate
change and support mitigation policies, while rural areas, particularly those economically
dependent on extractive industries, tend to be more skeptical.
Ideological orientations may also contribute to this disparity. In several contexts,
including parts of the United States and Europe, rural residents often exhibit greater
skepticism toward government intervention and show a preference for market-based
solutions over regulatory or tax-based approaches (Tatham and Peters, 2023; Zhang,
Ferreira, and Karali, 2024). Although such explanatory variables cannot be directly
tested in this study due to data limitations, the observed urban–rural gap in attitudes
toward CO2 taxation may reflect not only differences in infrastructure and access to
information, but also broader political and economic worldviews.
6.2
Energy Use Patterns
Households using biomass fuels such as straw, pellets, or firewood may perceive the CO2
tax as a threat to their heating practices, particularly if they believe that future policies
will tax all emissions, whether fossil-based or biogenic. Although straw is typically
considered CO2-neutral, some may fear that future regulations could cease to recognize
it as such, resulting in taxation nonetheless. This interpretation also fits with the result
for the Heating_OilGas variable (people using gas heating), which also shows a negative
association with CO2 tax acceptance, although without statistical significance.
A possible explanation for this pattern is linked to energy dependence. Umit and
Schaffer (2020) finds that support for carbon taxes is lower among individuals with
high energy dependence, understood as a low perceived ability to reduce their energy
==PAGE:55==
6.3 Renewable Energy Exposure
45
consumption. This profile may include households that rely heavily on heating fuels,
whether straw, pellets, or firewood, as they might feel particularly vulnerable to cost
increases resulting from taxation.
Alternatively, biomass use, like wind turbines, may be viewed as a CO2 mitigation
strategy in itself, thereby reducing the perceived need for a CO2 tax. In this sense, the
use of straw may reflect a belief in competing instruments for climate action rather than
opposition to climate policy per se. This interpretation is consistent with findings by
Ugarte Lucas, Lund, and Gamborg (2023), who show that public support for woody
biomass in Denmark is largely driven by belief in its CO2 mitigation potential. Thus, the
use of straw, like woody biomass, may be perceived as an alternative climate strategy,
which could diminish public support for additional instruments such as CO2 taxation.
Moreover, Baron, Bellassen, and Deheza (2013) highlight that the EU climate policy
framework has historically prioritized energy substitution through biomass as a central
mitigation tool. This suggests that, as with wind power, the adoption of biomass does not
necessarily indicate resistance to climate policy, but rather a preference for incentive-based
mechanisms over coercive measures such as carbon taxes.
In addition to household energy use, local energy reliability could also play a role
in shaping tax acceptance. Although the variable capturing experienced power outages
showed no significant effect in this study, it may become more relevant in future contexts.
For instance, following the widespread blackout in Spain in April 2025 (ENTSO-E, 2025),
public perceptions of energy reliability could gain greater relevance, calling for further
research on this factor as an additional contextual driver of climate policy attitudes.
6.3
Renewable Energy Exposure
A higher share of photovoltaic installations in the municipality is associated with lower
acceptance of the CO2 tax. This negative effect aligns with the pattern found for wind
turbines, suggesting that a greater local presence of renewable infrastructure does not
necessarily translate into stronger support for carbon taxation as a policy tool. This
stands in contrast to Ladenburg, Kânoğlu-Özkan, and Soytas (2024), which reports
no significant average effect of wind turbine exposure on CO2 tax acceptance in the
full sample, with significant relationships detected only through interactions with socio-
demographic factors. The present analysis identifies significant associations already
at the aggregate level and further extends the scope by incorporating both wind and
==PAGE:56==
46
6 Discussion
PV exposure, while controlling for a broader set of household and socio-demographic
characteristics, including housing type, heating system, car ownership, and detailed
education levels. By contrast, Ladenburg, Kânoğlu-Özkan, and Soytas (2024) focuses
exclusively on wind and uses a more limited set of control variables. This broader scope
and finer spatial detail make the findings more robust and increase their external validity.
Initially, this could indicate that investment in renewable technologies leads to a
perception that additional fiscal measures are less necessary. However, the relationship
between renewable energy exposure and CO2 tax acceptance is not straightforward, and
the available data do not allow causal conclusions to be drawn.
Two competing and potentially complementary pathways can be considered.
First, exposure may directly shape attitudes: individuals living near turbines or
in areas with high PV penetration might perceive these technologies as sufficient or
preferable climate mitigation instruments, thereby reducing their support for carbon
taxation. This interpretation aligns with the idea that both PV and wind turbines are
typically seen as substitute technologies that contribute to decarbonization, reducing
the perceived need for additional regulatory or fiscal climate measures. Gerlach et al.
(2011) and Villena-Ruiz, Honrubia-Escribano, and Gómez-Lázaro (2023) conclude that
photovoltaic and wind power are complementary technologies with minimal temporal
competition, which is consistent with the finding that both forms of exposure follow the
same pattern.
Second, the relationship may also work in the opposite direction: individuals who
are less accepting of CO2 taxation might be more likely to live in areas with a stronger
renewable presence. For instance, rural residents, who tend to be more skeptical of
taxation, more conservative in political orientation, and less supportive of government
intervention (Tatham and Peters, 2023; Zhang, Ferreira, and Karali, 2024), are also more
likely to live in regions where wind and PV infrastructure is concentrated (Ladenburg,
Hevia Koch, et al., 2020). On the other hand, urban residents are generally more aware
of environmental issues and more supportive of climate change mitigation policies such as
carbon taxation (Ewald, T. Sterner, and E. Sterner, 2022; Howe et al., 2015; Mildenberger
et al., 2016). From this perspective, the observed association may reflect underlying
socio-demographic and ideological patterns rather than a direct effect of exposure.
It is worth noting that both mechanisms may operate simultaneously. Some residents
may adjust their attitudes in response to local infrastructure, while others may have
selected their place of residence according to preferences or constraints that correlate
==PAGE:57==
6.4 Survey Design Effects
47
with attitudes toward climate policy. Given the cross-sectional design, it is not pos-
sible to clearly separate these dynamics. Additional approaches would be required to
determine whether renewable exposure causally influences tax acceptance or whether
the observed association is instead driven by spatial sorting of individuals with different
policy preferences. For instance, longitudinal survey designs could track how attitudes
evolve before and after the deployment of new infrastructure, while quasi-experimental
strategies exploiting natural variation in infrastructure placement could provide stronger
causal insights (Bingaman, Firestone, and Bidwell, 2023; Deschênes and Meng, 2018).
The implications of each interpretation differ substantially. If exposure reduces
acceptance of CO2 taxation, then renewable deployment could indirectly undermine
support for fiscal climate instruments, pointing to a potential policy trade-off.
If,
on the contrary, the association reflects self-selection, then the location of renewable
infrastructure aligns with broader socio-political divides, suggesting that community
identities and political culture shape both energy landscapes and climate policy attitudes.
Acknowledging these possibilities is essential for interpreting the results: while the
associations observed here are robust, the causal direction remains uncertain.
6.4
Survey Design Effects
The survey ordering variable suggests a modest but significant ordering effect: respondents
shown more technical or infrastructure-based policies (e.g., nuclear power, district heating,
wind turbines) earlier in the survey were slightly less supportive of CO2 taxation policies,
which appeared later. The position of CO2 taxes at the end of the list may have led to
lower prioritization. This suggests that the sequencing of policies in a survey can shape
perceived importance or support, beyond individual policy preferences.
Nevertheless, results from independent two-sample t-tests demonstrated that, despite
variations in the statistical significance of some coefficients, particularly the spatial
variables such as N_Wind_6km2, the differences between the two model versions were
not statistically significant, although respondents in the tax-to-nuclear version appeared
slightly more responsive to nearby wind turbines.
This finding implies that while question order can influence the apparent strength
or significance of certain predictors, the underlying relationships between explanatory
variables and CO2 tax acceptance remain stable across both versions. That is, sequencing
shaped perception but did not alter underlying attitudes. Overall, the results confirm
==PAGE:58==
48
6 Discussion
that survey randomization did not lead to systematic shifts in parameter estimates.
It should also be noted that ordering effects may partly capture learning or fatigue
mechanisms rather than framing alone. In repeated survey or choice tasks, respondents
often become more consistent as they gain familiarity (learning), or conversely less
attentive as they progress (fatigue). Empirical studies show that both processes can
occur simultaneously and are not easily distinguished, meaning that apparent order
effects may, in part, reflect these position-related dynamics rather than genuine attitude
change (Campbell et al., 2015; Carlsson, Mørkbak, and Olsen, 2012). Given the present
design, where the CO2 tax question appeared either at the beginning or the end of
the technology block, the estimated ordering effect could therefore be confounded with
fatigue or learning effects, a limitation that should be kept in mind when interpreting
the results.
Furthermore, distal order effects may also have played a role in this survey. While
the questions within the technology block were randomized, the position of the block
itself within the questionnaire was fixed. The placement of a block in a survey can
likewise influence how respondents interpret subsequent items. When the CO2 tax
question appears early, as in the survey by Ladenburg, Kânoğlu-Özkan, and Soytas
(2024) participants may respond with relatively unformed attitudes and greater attention,
whereas placing it toward the end, as in the present survey following several attitudinal
and contextual items, can anchor responses in preceding topics or encourage consistency
with earlier views (Cai, Cameron, and Gerdes, 2011).
6.5
Robustness and Heterogeneity of Effects
The analysis also reveals heterogeneity across age and income groups. The older age
group shows the strongest and clearest effect, which may be related to perceptions of
risks associated with proximity to renewable energy infrastructure. In this case, older
respondents appear to become more negative toward CO2 taxation as their exposure to
wind turbines increases. This finding differs notably from that of Ladenburg, Kânoğlu-
Özkan, and Soytas (2024), who identify the opposite pattern: younger individuals become
less accepting of CO2 taxation when exposed to wind turbines, whereas older individuals
tend to become more supportive.
One possible explanation for this divergence is the temporal difference between the
two surveys. The earlier survey (2011–2012; N = 2,386; ages 20–67) covered a younger
==PAGE:59==
6.5 Robustness and Heterogeneity of Effects
49
age range, whereas the present study relies on a larger sample collected roughly a decade
later (2022; N = 4,024; ages 20–92), which may reflect broader societal changes in
attitudes toward climate policy and renewable energy infrastructure, following more
recent policy debates. It is therefore plausible to argue that generational dynamics have
shifted, reversing the earlier pattern. A second explanation relates to measurement.
In the current study, wind exposure is defined objectively as the number of turbines
located within road-distance buffers of each household, as well as large-scale PV shares.
By contrast, in the earlier study, exposure was self-reported, based on respondents’
recollection of how many turbines they could see from their households. As discussed in
Data, self-reported measures introduce potential biases, since they depend on individual
awareness and attention, while the spatial measure employed here is independent of
perception. This methodological difference may contribute to the contrasting findings and
highlights the importance of carefully modeling renewable energy exposure in attitudinal
research.
Income heterogeneity is also observed, although the effects are weaker and less statis-
tically significant. Low- and high-income respondents tend to display lower acceptance
of CO2 taxation, but this effect is not as pronounced as the one associated with age. For
younger individuals, as well as for respondents with medium incomes, the relationship
between wind exposure and tax acceptance is minimal and not statistically significant.
In none of the age or income groups does the large-scale solar PV share show a
statistically significant effect on tax acceptance (p > 0.15). This suggests that the
relative presence of photovoltaic installations in the vicinity does not indicate a clear
or systematic association with attitudes toward CO2 taxation across socio-demographic
groups.
The absence of significant effects for PV contrasts with the results obtained for
wind turbines. While the literature on age-related differences in attitudes toward PV
remains scarce, existing studies on wind power have highlighted generational dynamics,
with younger individuals often being more receptive to renewable energy deployment
(Hamilton, Hartter, and Bell, 2019; Ladenburg, 2008). In the case of PV, however,
such associations are not evident, leaving the interpretation of the present results at the
frontier of current research.
One tentative explanation may relate to the nature of the exposure itself. Wind
turbine exposure encompasses elements such as size variation, high visibility in the
landscape, and potential noise impacts, all of which makes them more noticeable to
==PAGE:60==
50
6 Discussion
nearby residents. By contrast, large-scale PV installations are typically less visible and
less intrusive at the household level, which may reduce their relevance in shaping attitudes
toward CO2 taxation. This distinction between more visible infrastructures such as wind
turbines and less noticeable ones such as large-scale PV could partly explain why wind
turbines appear to be associated with tax acceptance while the latter do not.
Future research could investigate whether the interaction of turbine size and density,
which are not captured in the present dataset, helps explain these differences. For instance,
younger respondents may be more frequently exposed to larger or more numerous turbines
than older respondents, potentially explaining age-related patterns in the relationship
between wind exposure and support for CO2 taxation.
6.6
Beyond NIMBY
A proximity-based interpretation of local energy politics, often simplified as NIMBY,
struggles to explain when and why nearby infrastructure translates into support or
opposition. Prior research emphasises that proximity is not a causal mechanism in itself
but a proxy for a bundle of social and institutional mechanisms, including perceived
fairness, place attachment, trust, and specific project attributes (Devine-Wright, 2009;
Graziano and Gillingham, 2015; Wolsink, 2000). The evidence in this study aligns
with that broader understanding. Three empirical patterns are particularly difficult to
reconcile with a simple assumption that closer distance automatically leads to greater
opposition: (i) responses to wind turbine exposure follow a non-linear pattern, with
apparent threshold effects rather than a steady decline in acceptance; (ii) the sensitivity of
tax attitudes to question order indicates that contextual framing matters, suggesting that
acceptance is shaped by contextual and framing effects rather than by spatial distance
alone; and (iii) significant variation across age and income groups points to distributive
and ideological patterns rather than uniform proximity effects.
However, two limitations constrain how far the present analysis can move beyond the
NIMBY framework. First, the available exposure measures do not capture the size or
capacity of wind turbines and PV installations, the number of PV installations within
road distance, nor the frequency of daily visual encounters. The absence of such attributes
makes it harder to move beyond the oversimplified NIMBY perspective highlighted in
the literature.
Second, the cross-sectional design of the dataset limits any causal inference about

6.7 Policy Implications
51
the direction of the relationship between local energy landscapes and attitudes toward
CO2 taxation. While proximity and acceptance appear to be correlated, the data cannot
determine whether renewable exposure influences tax attitudes, or whether citizens
with particular policy preferences tend to live in areas with more (or fewer) renewable
installations.
The observed combination of non-linear exposure effects, context sensitivity, and
socio-demographic heterogeneity clearly indicates that a proximity-only explanation is
insufficient. NIMBY, in this sense, functions as a noisy shorthand for a more complex set
of social, spatial, and institutional mechanisms. Treating local support or opposition as
mere “NIMBYism” therefore risks overlooking the mechanisms through which acceptance
is actually formed.
6.7
Policy Implications
While the empirical design of this thesis does not allow for establishing causality, it
is nonetheless relevant to reflect on the potential policy implications if the observed
associations between renewable energy exposure and CO2 tax acceptance were indeed
causal. Two possible interpretations can be drawn, each pointing to different climate
policy challenges.
First, if proximity to wind turbines and higher municipal penetration of photovoltaic
systems directly reduce support for CO2 taxation, this points to a potential policy trade-
off. Renewable energy deployment may unintentionally undermine public willingness to
accept fiscal climate instruments, as people may perceive local renewable infrastructure
as an alternative means of achieving decarbonization. In such a scenario, large-scale
renewable deployment and consumer carbon taxes could become politically difficult to
pursue at the same time. Policymakers would therefore need to consider complementary
strategies to maintain support, reinforcing the idea that taxes and renewables are not
substitutes but rather complementary tools.
The other possibility is that causality runs in the opposite direction: individuals
with lower acceptance of CO2 taxation may be more likely to reside in areas with high
renewable penetration, which are often rural regions. In this case, the implications
point to spatial and socio-political self-selection. The observed relationship between
exposure and tax acceptance would thus reflect broader socio-political and spatial divides
rather than a direct effect of local infrastructure. Policy challenges would then center on
==PAGE:62==
52
6 Discussion
addressing perceptions that climate policies disproportionately affect rural households
and on ensuring that the benefits of renewable deployment are equitably distributed
across regions.
In practice, both mechanisms may operate simultaneously. Whether the link is driven
by local exposure shaping attitudes, or by people with certain preferences clustering in
specific areas, the implication is the same: technology choices and fiscal instruments
should not be treated in isolation. Future policy mixes will need to carefully integrate
fiscal instruments with visible investments in renewable infrastructure, ensuring that
one does not undermine the legitimacy of the other. Acknowledging these dynamics can
help policymakers anticipate resistance, design more robust climate policies, and sustain
broad-based public support for the transition toward climate neutrality.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusions
This thesis examined whether spatial proximity to renewable energy technologies is
associated with public acceptance of a consumer CO2 tax in Denmark. By combining a
nationally representative survey of 4,126 respondents with detailed spatial information
on nearby wind turbines and photovoltaic (PV) installations, the study explored how
local exposure to renewable infrastructure interacts with individual and contextual
factors shaping climate policy preferences. Using ordinary least squares and ordered
probit models, the analysis assessed both direct associations and variations across socio-
demographic and spatial conditions.
The results indicate that acceptance of a CO2 tax is positively associated with higher
education and urban residence, while households using straw as their primary heating
source show lower support. Spatial exposure to renewables reveals a more complex
pattern: proximity to onshore wind turbines and higher municipal shares of rooftop PV
are both linked to reduced support for CO2 taxation. These relationships remain robust
across alternative model specifications and question order treatments. Respondents
who received the tax-to-nuclear questionnaire version appear slightly more responsive
to nearby wind turbines, although overall coefficients remain consistent across versions.
Together, the findings suggest that exposure to visible renewable technologies may act as
a contextual factor shaping attitudes toward fiscal climate instruments.
While the study does not permit causal inference, the results align with mechanisms
identified in the literature on environmental attitudes and policy acceptance. In particular,
the negative association between renewable proximity and CO2 tax support is consistent
with visibility and substitution dynamics: individuals who directly experience local
renewable development may perceive it as a substitute for national-level fiscal measures
or view additional taxation as less necessary. At the same time, these effects could reflect
fairness and distributional concerns, as those more exposed to renewable installations
may also perceive unequal local burdens from the energy transition. Distinguishing
between these mechanisms represents an important step for future research.
Several limitations should be acknowledged. The dataset lacks variables capturing
==PAGE:64==
54
7 Conclusions
climate change perceptions, political orientation, and institutional trust, which could
influence both exposure patterns and attitudes. However, as discussed, omitted-variable
bias would arise only if such attitudes correlate systematically with spatial exposure, for
which no evidence currently exists. The study also relies on one contextual indicator,
the municipal share of rooftop PV, which is aggregated at the administrative level and
therefore less spatially precise than the distance-based measures. Despite these limita-
tions, the analysis improves substantially on previous studies by using individual-level
coordinates and driving-distance calculations, allowing for a more detailed understanding
of spatial exposure.
Overall, this thesis contributes to the literature by integrating detailed spatial data
with public opinion analysis on climate taxation. It demonstrates that local renewable en-
ergy exposure can be associated with the social acceptability of fiscal climate instruments,
highlighting the need to consider spatial and contextual heterogeneity when designing
equitable climate policies. Future work could extend these findings by combining experi-
mental or longitudinal data with more comprehensive attitudinal measures to further
examine perceptual and distributive mechanisms underlying public responses to the green
transition.

CO2 capture and storage (CCS) is a technology where CO2 is captured at power plants
or industries. The CO2 is transported via pipes/by ship to the storage site, and pumped
deep into the subsoil, where nature itself ensures that the CO2 stays there.
The technology has been used abroad for almost 40 years and in Denmark the possibilities
