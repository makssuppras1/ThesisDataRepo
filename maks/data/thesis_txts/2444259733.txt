Abstract
Urinary tract infections (UTI) are among the most common bacterial infectious diseases in the world. In
the diagnosis of UTI a reliable urine sample is the key challenge for both normal voiding and catheterized
patients. To improve the urine sampling method for catheterized patients, the aim of this project was
to develop a prototype of a new urine sampling method with the ability to collect an uncontaminated
urine sample directly from the bladder for diagnostic purposes.
Two types of catheter-integrated urine micro-samplers were designed with the aim of testing two
di↵erent sampling mechanisms. The two sampling mechanisms were developed based on capillary pres-
sure in microchannels and urine ﬂow in the catheter respectively. In the computer aided design program
Autodesk Fusion 360, 3D models of the micro-sampler were designed and converted to 2D slices to
promote manufacturing of the prototypes by stereolithograpic 3D printing. The two prototypes were
iteratively redesigned and improved as a result of premilinary testing of integration with a catheter,
insertion through a urethra model, sampling of liquid, and extraction of the collected liquid. The two
ﬁnal designs were further tested for proof of concept of functional design requirements and bacterial
contamination from the urethra during insertion of the catheter into the bladder. Tests were conducted
at Department of Health Technology DTU and Coloplast A/S.
The micro-samplers were successfully manufactured by 3D printing and integrated with the Coloplast
SpeediCath Flex catheter. The micro-samplers were inserted through a urethra model una↵ected and
demonstrated sampling directly from a bladder model. A maximum sample volume of 80µL was collected
with the micro-sampler, which was considered to be suﬃcient to diagnose UTI with an in vitro research
hIL-8 point-of-care test. Contamination tests indicated a mean contamination level of minimum 15%
compared to a limit for bacterial contamination of ≥104CFU/mL.
The catheter-integrated micro-sampler prototypes were considered successful and concluded to have
a potential for future research.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Urinary tract infections (UTI) are among the most common bacterial infectious diseases in the world and
accounts for 8.6 million visits to primary care physicians a year in the US alone.[1] UTI is an inﬂammatory
response of the urethral epithelium to bacterial invasion from outside the urethra. Intermittent catheters
are intended for self-catheterization or to be inserted by a healthcare professional depending on the ability
of the patient, with the aim of promoting drainage of the bladder 4-6 times daily. The introduction of
a catheter into the bladder through urethra has the side e↵ect that externally induced microorganisms
and pathogens can interfere with the mucosa of the urethra and bladder.[2] The natural voiding of urine
helps cleaning the urethral mucosa by washing out the pathogens with the urine ﬂow. This cleaning
mechanism is impaired when using a catheter, and the pathogens are therefore not naturally washed
away each time urinating.
Catheter-associated UTI (CAUTI) is the most common health care-associated infection worldwide
and the most frequent complication to indwelling and intermittent catheterization, and can lead to
complications such as pyelonephyritis and septic arthritis[3][4]. Catheterized patients have no or reduced
bladder control, and do therefore need a medical device for drainage of the bladder and collection of a
urine sample to diagnose UTI.
Eﬃcient, fast, and reliable diagnosis of UTI is of big interest for patients and health care professionals
worldwide. The key challenge is to lower the rate of false-positives due to contamination of the urine
test sample from the urethra, which is crucial for preventing over-treatment with antibiotics.
Motivated by the need of a more convenient and reliable diagnosis of CAUTI, the aim of this project
was to develop a prototype of a new urine sampling method for catheterized patients with the ability
to collect an uncontaminated urine sample directly from the bladder. More speciﬁcally the aim was to
explore the potential of designing a catheter-integrated urine micro-sampler with the ability to collect
a suﬃcient volume of urine to diagnose UTI, and to use 3D printing to manufacture a prototype for
proof of concept testing. The word micro in urine micro-sampler will cover a sampler designed with ﬁne
spatial details and utilize forces being dominant at the micro-scale.
The project aim was further developed into the following hypotheses:
1. A urine sampler can be integrated with an existing intermittent catheter without entailing addi-
tional user discomfort or higher risk of infection upon usage.
2. Urine can be collected directly from the bladder by a micro-sampler based on capillary action.

3. Urine can be collected directly from the bladder by a micro-sampler by utilizing a sampling method
driven by urine ﬂow and integration of a venturi channel.
4. A suﬃcient volume of urine to diagnose UTI can be collected with the urine micro-sampler.

This project will test the hypotheses by developing a urine micro-sampler prototype to be integrated
with a Coloplast SpeediCath Flex catheter for men.
Urinary catheters are medical devices which promote drainage of the bladder for patients who are
unable to promote natural urine drainage. To allow the urine to drain, the catheter is inserted through
the urethra into the bladder.
Bladder drainage problems primarily include urinary incontinence or
urinary retention, which both can be neurogenic, age-related, or due to other physical diseases.[5] The
most common side-e↵ects and complications experienced by intermittent catheter users are urinary tract
infections, minor bleeding, and pain.[6] These complications are minor compared to permanent indwelling
catheterization, and the intermittent catheters are thus the preferred treatment when regular drainage
of the bladder is possible.
UTI is usually diagnosed clinically by analyzing the symptoms of the patients and conﬁrm with
an biochemical and bateriological analysis of an urine culture in the laboratory. Intermittent catheter
patients are often spinal cord injured patients, who have a limited sensation and ability for detecting
incipient symptoms for UTI.[5] A biochemical and bacteriological analysis of the urine is thus very
important for early diagnosis of CAUTI. Currently, clinical tests focus on targeting speciﬁc markers,
such as total colony forming units (CFU) count or biological markers, and the speciﬁc contamination
rate of the test sample is therefore dependent on this test method. When analyzing a urine sample for
total CFU count two common uncertainties can arise: bacteria can either be present in the urothelium
without bacteria in the urine (bacteriuria) or bacteriuria can result from contamination of the urine
sample.[7, p. 237] These two situations lead to false-negatives and false-positives respectively. False-
negative diagnosis results in undetected and untreated UTI, which can induce further complications and
reduced quality of life. False-positive diagnosis leads to unnecessary antibiotic treatment, which in the
worst case leads to antibiotic resistance. These two situations are of high priority to reduce. To obtain
the most reliable outcome of the urine test, the urine collecting method must entail the lowest possible
contamination rate, which will be addressed in this project.
The most common urine collecting method used is the mid-stream-clean-catch, in which the ﬁrst
voided urine is not collected and used in the test sample. An advantage of using this method is the
non-invasiveness, which for already catheterized patients is irrelevant. Due to catheter handling the
mid-stream-clean-catch collecting method is inconvenient for catheterized patients, and cannot be ob-
tained completely without bacterial contamination since the introduction of the catheter gives rise to
bacteria access. For catheter patients a more accurate and easy method for collecting urine samples for
analysis is of interest, which is investigated in this project by designing and manufacturing two prototypes.

Several manufacturing methods for developing a prototype of the designed urine sampler can be uti-
lized. 3D printing is a widely used and well-known process to manufacture prototypes by designing 3D
models in Computer Aided Design (CAD) programs and print typically via layer-by-layer addition of
printable materials. Many di↵erent 3D printing techniques can be used depending on the material and
spatial resolution requirement. The concept of 3D printing is to split 3D CAD data into thin layers,
which is afterward transferred into the 3D printer. Choosing stereolithography for 3D printing in this
project gives possibilities of a high spatial resolution obtained by solidifying thin layers of a pre-polymer
solution in a controlled manner by photopolymerization, and to use materials prior tested to be biolog-
ically compatible. Biological compatibility was considered important to avoid toxicity and irritants in
the handling of the device and during biological tests. The stereolithographic printing process also gives
a high degree of reproducibility, ensuring the same output of each print. Worth noticing is that material
choice is limited by the requirements of being compatible with the stereolithographic 3D printing method.
This thesis will ﬁrst guide the reader through the essential knowledge of the urinary tract physiol-
ogy, pathogenesis of UTI, and current diagnostic and urine sampling methods to detech UTI. As the
micro-sampler prototypes were developed to be integrated with an existing Coloplast catheter, current
catheters, catheter materials, and prototype manufacturing methods will be presented. All to give nec-
essary background knowledge to understand the thoughts behind the design and development of the
catheter-integrated urine micro-samplers. Subsequently, the design requirements of the micro-sampler
will be introduced, and the following two potential solutions to the clinical issue will be presented:
1.
A catheter-integrated urine micro-sampler developed with the capabilities of collecting urine di-
rectly from the bladder by taking advantage of the capillary forces dominating at the micro-scale.
This method requires a dry environment for the micro-sampler until reaching the bladder.
2. A catheter-integrated urine micro-sampler developed with the capabilities of collecting urine di-
rectly from the bladder by taking advantage of the urine ﬂow through the catheter lumen by using
it as driving force for urine sampling into the micro-sampler chamber.
It is chosen to test two solutions in the project to compare and to gain knowledge and experience with
the concept of integrating a urine sampler with an intermittent catheter. Both solutions are presented
with mathematical explanations for the sampling theories, a review of the design history, speciﬁcations
of the proposed designs, and proof of concept experimental tests of the integration with an existing
catheter, ﬂuid sampling and extraction, and insertion of the micro-sampler through a urethra model. Pig
urethra tests and bacterial contamination tests were performed in collaboration with Coloplast which
will be presented.
Based on the current catheter manufacturing methods, industrial manufacturing
possibilities of the designed micro-sampler will be proposed and discussed.
Based on the biological
tests, the advantages and disadvantages of the two proposed micro-samplers will be discussed, including
material choice and contamination rate. Lastly, future work and the potential of the catheter-integrated
urine micro-sampler sampling method will be discussed.

Background
This chapter will provide the necessary background knowledge on urinary tract physiology and patho-
genesis of UTI. Current diagnostic methods will be introduced to get an overview of what is essential
for a collected urine sample in respect to sample volume requirements, clinical markers, and detection
levels. Urine sampling methods will be presented to get an overview of existing methods, which the
micro-sampler developed in this project will be an potential alternative to. As the micro-sampler was
developed to be integrated with an existing Coloplast catheter, urinary catheters and existing catheter
materials will be presented. Lastly potential 3D manufacturing methods for printing the prototypes will
be discussed.
2.1
Physiology of the Bladder and Urethra
The urinary bladder acts as a storage reservoir for continuous ﬂow of urine from the kidneys through
the ureters to the bladder. The bladder can distend due to folds in the lumen, transitional stretching
epithelium, and smooth muscle cells of the wall. When the wall is stretched due to urine storage the
outgoing pressure increases and receptors are activated, which both activates the smooth muscle cells
of the bladder, increases the conscious urge to urinate, inhibit the micturition reﬂex to avoid automatic
urination, and constricts the external urethral sphincter until voiding is appropriate. Normal voiding
initiates due to a further increase in signals from the bladder, which activates the micturition reﬂex,
relax the external urinary sphincter, and voluntary contract the abdominal muscles.[8] If signals from
the bladder to the spinal cord is disrupted due to spinal cord injuries this will cause incontinence. From
the bladder, urine is voided through the urethra. The male urethra is approximately 20 cm long and
divided into three parts: the prostatic urethra, the membranous part, and the spongy part. Transitional
epithelium lines the urinary bladder and the prostatic urethra, followed by stratiﬁed columnar epithelium
which lines the membranous and spongy part. The transitional epithelium, also called the urothelium,
can react on stretches and has the ability to accommodate urine volume changes. The female urethra
is 3-4 cm long, much shorter than the male urethra.[8] This makes the male catheterization much more
complicated, and the user of focus in this project is therefore men.

2.2. URINARY TRACT INFECTIONS
CHAPTER 2
2.2
Urinary Tract Infections
Urinary tract infection (UTI) is deﬁned as an inﬂammatory response of the urothelium to bacterial
invasion. The inﬂammatory response of the uroepithelium is usually associated with bacteriuria, the
presence of bacteria in the urine, and pyuria, the presence of white blood cells in the urine. Pyuria
indicates infection or inﬂammation of the uroepithelium due to bacteria or indwelling foreign bodies
such as catheters.[7, p. 237]
UTI often initiates when either bacteria virulence increases or the defense mechanism of the uri-
nary tract is reduced.[7, p. 239] The pathogenesis of UTI can be explained by interactions between
uropathogens and the host, where bacteria most often enters the urinary tract from the bowel reser-
voir and adheres to the uroepithelial mucosa which has a main role in the ascending of UTI. To sur-
vive in the bladder environment uropatogens have to form bioﬁlm to be protected from immunologi-
cal responses.[9]Uropathogenic Escherichia coli (E. coli), which is the most common bacteria causing
community-acquired UTI, expresses adhesions factors that promote attachment to the uroepithelium.[7,
p. 242] Microorganisms of the normal ﬂora of the periurethral and urethra area act as a barrier for
uropaathogenic colonization and prevent uropathogens to adhere to the uroepithelium. Changes in this
microenvironment can cause an increase in the receptivity of the uroepithelium for uropathogens.
UTI can be classiﬁed as either uncomplicated or complicated UTI, which refers to the functionality
and structure of the urinary tract, were complications increases the risk of acquiring bacteria and decrease
the chances of responding on treatment. Complicated urinary tract infections can either be associated
with anatomic abnormalities, pregnancy, indwelling urinary catheters, or urinary tract instruments.
Early diagnosis and initiation of treatment of complicated UTI is essential for decreasing the risk of
further complications.[7, p. 238]
Catheter-associated UTI (CAUTI) is the most common of the complicated UTIs and health care-
associated infection worldwide[4]. The incidence of CAUTI is associated with the duration of catheter-
ization and is therefore largely increased for indwelling compared to intermittent catheterization. The
risk for developing bacteriuria is 10% per day and 1 −3% per catheterization for indwelling and inter-
mittent catheterization respectively. Additional risk factors for developing CAUTI are female-gender,
the absence of systemic anti-microbial agents, and deﬁcient catheter-care.[7, p. 299]
The pathogenesis of CAUTI often initiates by the introduction of bacteria from the catheter insertions
and is either caused by mechanical inoculation of urethral bacteria or nonsterile catheterization technique.
These two factors are dominant for both indwelling and intermittent CAUTI. Uropathogenic growth in
the catheter-system either occur from growth within the urine in the bladder or growth on the catheter
surface (extraluminal), were bioﬁlm can cause catheter encrustation.[7, p. 299] Extraluminally acquired
uropathogens account for 2/3 of catheter-associated bacteriuria, where the most frequently isolated
bacteria species for short-term catheterization is E. Coli.[4] It is worth noting that bacteria do not grow
very well in urine. This is important knowledge with respect to the bacteria count and symptoms, since
a relatively low bacteria count, CFU/mL, can induce UTI symptoms such as frequent bladder emptying
which combined with low growth rate will keep down the bacteria count.

2.3
Diagnosis
A very common type of UTI is cystitis, inﬂammation of the bladder, which is clinically manifested
by symptoms such as dysuria, frequent urination, urgency, and to some extend suprapubic pain. A
more severe progression of UTI is pyelonephritis, which is associated with fever, chills, and ﬂank pain[7,
p. 250] Since both symptomatic and asymptomatic UTI can occur, the diagnosis of UTI and CAUTI
must be a combination of clinical symptoms and urine culture. The 2009 International Clinical Practice
Guidelines for Diagnosis, Prevention, and Treatment of Catheter-Associated Urinary Tract Infection in
Adults deﬁnes CAUTI as the presence of symptoms and a CFU count of ≥103 CFU/mL.[4] Collecting
completely uncontaminated urine samples for a urine culture is very diﬃcult, which is why diagnosis
criteria and urine sampling methods will be discussed in this section.
Urine samples are collected and analyzed in a urine culture to estimate the number of uropathogens
or with a point of care (POC) testing tool described below. The urinary tract and urine are in healthy
conditions free of bacteria and inﬂammation, and an analysis of urine cultures can thus conﬁrm the pres-
ence of a UTI. As mentioned in the introduction, both false-negatives and false-positives are frequently
observed in UTI diagnosis, since the amount of bacteria is either too low to be detected or above the
threshold due to bacterial contamination of the urine sample.[7, p. 250]
Laboratory culture urine test is the gold standard microbiological test method for identifying the
infecting microorganisms and antimicrobial susceptibility, but due to the delay in test result during
laboratory test and incubation, the method entails an uncertainty of the diagnosis for 18-48 hours, which
can either result in missing treatment or over-treatment. The colony count criterion for UTI diagnosis
is often set to 104 −105 CFU/mL for laboratory cultures, where the higher limit will increase the false-
negatives and the lower limit of 104 CFU/mL is thought to increase the sensitivity. The lower threshold
has a risk of obtaining false-positive results which will entail over-treatment of healthy patients. The
threshold is further dependent on the sampling techniques as described below, and must be decreased
when diagnosing catheterized patients or patients where the urine sample is obtained by suprapubic
aspiration since the reliability of the sample is higher.[7, p. 300]
The standardized microscopic examination of urine is performed by centrifuging 5-10 mL urine,
where the sediment is to be analyzed for assessing pyuria by counting white blood cells. The microscopic
examination is also used for bacteria identiﬁcation in non-centrifuged urine which is either unstrained or
gram-strained.[7, p. 2939][10] The threshold for a microscopic ﬁnding of bacteriuria is 105 CFU/mL, for
which 90% of all infections are found to be bacteriuria. Lower CFU infections, such as 102-104 CFU/mL,
are harder to detect microscopically due to the low amount of urine that can be observed in a microscope,
which will entail false-negatives.[7, p. 250] False-positives can occur due to contamination of the urine
sample by bacteria from the healthy vaginal bacteria environment, such as lactobacilli, which during
gram staining will be detected in the microscope as gram-negatives although they are gram-positives.
Even though the detection limit is high, the microscopic urinalysis is good for rapid identiﬁcations of
bacteria and white blood cells.
POC diagnostic testing tools are of high relevance for rapid diagnosis of UTI. They will make it
possible to obtain faster diagnostic information and avoid unnecessary urine cultures with a less expensive
and portable medical device. The challenge for POC diagnosis is to obtain suﬃcient biological information
to identify the pathogenic presence, and some POC application therefore make use of preconditioning

initial test for UTI, detects nitrite, leukocyte esterase, proteins, and white blood cells as a marker for

2.4
Sampling Methods
The following di↵erent urine sampling methods are the ones mainly discussed in the literature.[18, 19, 20]
1. Suprapubic aspiration (SPA)
2. Catheter specimen urine (CSU)
3. Mid-stream-clean-catch technique (MSCC)
4. Bag specimen urine (BSU)
A study by S. Tosif et al.[20] from 2012 examined the contamination rates of the four di↵erent urine
sampling techniques in children aged less than 2 years (mean age of 7 months). The samples consisted
of 599 included urine samples with 34% from CCU, 16% from CSU, 14% from SPA, 2% from BSU, and
34% from unknown collecting methods. A contaminated urine sample was deﬁned by having two or more
organisms grown (mixed growth) and having a colony count above ≥104 CFU/mL for SPA and CSU, and
≥108 CFU/mL for CCU, BSU, and unspeciﬁed samples. The study found the highest contamination rate
in BSU (45%) followed by CCU (26%) and unknown collecting methods (20%). The sampling techniques
with the lowest contamination rates were SPA with 1% and CSU with 12 %. The study emphasized
that the choice of urine sampling technique is very important for avoiding contaminations and to obtain
more accurate results when diagnosing children.[20] SPA was preferable and suitable for urine sampling in
small children in hospital settings with a trained clinician present, and where completely uncontaminated
urine samples are essential for the diagnosis of UTI. The contamination threshold for CSU deﬁned by S.
Tosif et al. will be used later in this project.

SPA or suprapubic puncture is the most invasive urine collection technique since the urine is collected
from a sterile needle and a syringe which is inserted through the abdominal wall into the distended bladder
usually by guidance from an ultrasound scanner. This technique is, however, the most sterile one and
permits microbiological analyses.[21]
CSU or urethral catheterization sampling is deﬁned as a urine sample collected by inserting a sterile
catheter through the urethra into the bladder and can both be obtained from an intermittent and a
permanent indwelling catheter.[21] The study by S. Tosif et al.[20] found the technique the second most
uncontaminated urine sampling method, and showed a signiﬁcant improvement compared to the third
most uncontaminated known technique, CCU, which induced 26% contamination. CSU is, however, a
more invasive sampling technique than CCU, MSCC, or BSU, and even though a diagnostic accuracy
review study by Holm and Aabenhus [18] stated that voided samples would cause 5-10% more false-
positives compared to SPA and CSU, they did not ﬁnd that enough to outweigh the discomfort and
complications associated with catheter usage. For catheterized patients where disadvantages as discom-
fort and invasiveness are unavoidable, the complication associated with contamination during insertion of
the catheter is the key issue. The micro-sampler developed in this project is therefore particular relevant
as an alternative for the CFU.
MSCC is a voided urine sampling technique in which the labia or penis gland is cleaned before the
urine is collected from the middle portion of the voided urine.[18, 21] This is done to avoid the ﬁrst voided
urine, which is often contaminated by the bacteria from the urethra and the urinary tract. The MSCC
technique is easy, noninvasive to the patient, do not require additional equipment, can be performed in
any clinical setting, and is the recommended urine collection method by National Institute for Health
and Clinical Excellence (UK)[22] and by the Handbook for Danish doctors[23]. An additional advantage
of MSCC is that the risk of introducing external bacteria into the bladder is avoided, but as the urine
sample does pass the urethra prior to collection, the risk of introducing contamination in the sample
from the distal part of the urethra may be introduced.[24] The MSCC sampling technique is limited to
the patients who do not have any impairments associated with bladder voiding, and proper collection is
presumed to be diﬃcult for elderly patients. In a study by Karacan et al.[19] they found that MSCC
was the most preferred collection method, with 92.1 % of the cases, for children older than 24 months,
but showed contamination in 14.3 % of the 532 children included in the test.
BSU collection method is the least invasive and easiest way to collect a urine sample. In the mentioned
study by Karacan et al.[19] they found that BSU was by far the most preferred collection method for
children younger than 24 months. Of the 517 BSU urine samples collected 43.9 % was found to be
contaminated, which made this method the least reliable sampling method. Among the contaminated
samples 44.9% were repeated. Only 29.8% were positive in the repeated test, which makes 70.2% false-
positive if contamination was not considered.

2.6
Manufacturing
The additive manufacturing method, 3D printing, provides production of complicated 3D structures
from 3D CAD models. The principle behind 3D printing methods is to print several layers on the top of
each other (layer-by-layer) with properly attachment between each layer to create a continuously three
dimensional structure. Prior to the printing processes, 3D CAD models must be intersected in layers by
a slicing software to convert the 3D model into numerous 2D pictures which represents the contour of
each layer in the 3D model. The distance between each intersection represent the layer thickness of each
layer fabricated during the 3D print. Several 3D printing techniques can be used depending on material
choice and spatial resolution requirements. Investigated for this project was stereolithography (SLA),
selective laser sintering (SLS), and extrusion-based 3D printing.
The SLA manufacturing method cures a liquid photosensitive prepolymer by light exposure. SLA
permits high 3D resolution of 20−50µm and reproducibility of complex structures, but have a restricted
material span.[30] SLA printing provides a high manufacturing speed of complex structures, since the
speed is the same for each layer independent on structure complexity.[31]
SLS 3D printing sinters a ﬁne powder in a controlled pattern layer-by-layer, meaning that after the
sintering of one layer the building platform is lowered the distance corresponding the intersection in the
3D model and a new layer of powder will cover the surface.[32] SLS 3D printing permits thermoplastic
polymers and eliminates the need for supporting structures, but is not suitable for printing small cavities
at the microscale, since the excess powder must be removed after printing.[33]
Extrusion-based 3D printing is widely used for production of sca↵olds and multiple material struc-
tures. The process utilizes pneumatic pressure to dispense the polymer continuously from a movable
printing head.
An increased structure complexity will result in an increased manufacturing time.
Extrusion-based 3D printing permits a wide choice of materials, is inexpensive, but provides poorer
spatial resolution (≥100µm) for printed structures compared to SLA printing, since the resolution is
dependent on the extrusion head and viscosity of the material.[30]
In this project SLA printing was chosen to manufacture the micro-sampler prototypes to obtain high
resolution and reproducibility of the designed structures.
In a controlled manner the stereolithographic 3D printer cures a liquid photosensitive prepolymer
solution into a cross-linked polymer as a result of light exposure. This process is called photopolymer-
ization. The prepolymer is a liquid photocurable solution, which consist of a monomer, a photoinitiator,
10
==PAGE:18==
2.6. MANUFACTURING
CHAPTER 2
and a photoabsorber. The photoinitiator initiates the polymerization process by forming free radicals
when exposed to light and thereby initiate the polymerization of the monomer and formation of a cross-
linked network. The photoabsorber controls the penetration depth of the exposed light and thereby the
thickness of polymerized monomer. The light intensity must comply with the gelling threshold of the
monomer and the slicing thickness of the 3D print.
The prepolymer solution is added to a container with a transparent bottom where the light can be
transmitted through. The printed structure is cured in the gap between the bottom of the container and
a platform. A entire layer of prepolymer solution is exposed by light at the same time and the platform
is elevated after the exposure, to allow a new prepolymer layer to cover the gab. [31] This method is
called a top-down fabrication method. The method requires that the ﬁrst printed layer is chemically
attached to the platform and the subsequent layers can be attached layer-by-layer, meaning that each
solidiﬁed layer must be connected. This is important to take of notice in the 3D designs, and can be
solved by adding support structures to all free hanging structures. The xy-resolution of the 3D print
is determined by the pixel size and optics of the light exposure unit. The z resolution of the structure
is determined by the step height of the SLA print, slicing thickness of the 3D design, and penetration
depth of the exposed light determined by photoabsorber concentration and light intensity.
The material choice for stereolithography consists of both the monomer, the photoinitiator, and the
photoabsorber.
All components should be miscible and biocompatible to avoid toxicity during han-
dling and biological tests. The photoabsorber should absorb light in the same spectrum as for which
the photoinitiator is activated, which should be compatible with the light source used in the printer.
The monomer should have crosslinking functionalities e.g. from acrylate groups.[31] The monomer se-
lected for printing prototypes in this project should facilitate mechanical stability of microscale struc-
tures, allow high resolution, and match the mechanical properties of catheter materials described above.
Poly(ethylene glycol)diacrylate (PEGDA) was used as printing material, since it is biocompatible, non-
toxic, and have non-fouling properties.[34, p. 171,414] PEGDA 700 (Mw = 700g/mol) has previously
been used by Zhang and Larsen [35] as a monomer for SLA printing to print di↵usive mechanical stable
micro-channels and tested to have a shear modulus of 0.4 MPa.
The micro-sampler printing method was adopted from Zhang and Larsen [35] and printed in a pre-
polymer solution consisting of 90% PEGDA 700 (monomer), 6 mg/mL Quinoline Yellow (QY) (photoab-
sorber), and 5 mg/mL Lithium phenyl-2,4,6-trimethylbenzoylphosphinate (LAP) (10% vol/vol) (pho-
toinitiator) in a aqueous-solution. LAP and QY, purchased from Sigma-Aldrich, are water soluble and
have low toxicity.[36]
The printing process is further described in section 5.1, and the choice of increasing the concentration
from 20% to 90% is described in Section 5.2.

Capillary action
Capillary pumps can be illustrated from a horizontally placed microchannel, as illustrated to the right in

spontaneous ﬁlling mechanism of capillary pumps does thus only work if the channels to be ﬁlled are

The micro-sampler design type 1 was based on the idea of integrating a urine micro-sampler onto the

described in Section 3.2.1 page 13. An additional criterion for design type 1 was that the functionality

functional design requirements 1, 2, 3, 4, and 5: The urine micro-sampler should enter and go through

promote ﬁlling, collect a suﬃcient volume of urine from the bladder, and have the ability to release the
collected sample outside the body. Three iterations of the design history will be presented: The overall
aim of the ﬁrst iteration was to 3D print a mechanical stable micro-sampler with the right material and
dimensions, and the aim of iteration two and three was to reﬁne the design to be integrated with the tip
of the catheter.

This chapter will present the stereolithographic 3D printing process and the polymerization mechanism
used to manufacture the micro-sampler.
The rationale behind the choice of increasing the PEGDA
concentration from 20% to 90%, as introduced in Section 2.6 page 11, will be explained from relevant

5.1
Stereolithographic 3D Printing Process
All designs are made in the 3D design tool Autodesk Fusion 360, converted to an .stl-ﬁle, sliced in
the slicer software Slic3er with a thickness of 40 µm, and loaded into a MATLAB (Mathworks) script
controlling the stereolithographic printer. The stereolithographic 3D printer is home-built by prof. Niels
B. Larsen, DTU Health Tech, and is constructed as a top-down fabrication method as described in
Section 2.6 page 10. The light source used in the 3D printer was an LED with wavelength of 365nm.
The printed structure is chemically binded to a square cover glass attached to the printing platform
before printing.
The platform is attached to a movable stage, which lower the platform into a vat
covered with non-adhesive transparent foil at the bottom and ﬁlled with pre-polymer aqueous solution
(90% (vol/vol) PEGDA(Mw = 700g/mol) and 10% (vol/vol) 6 mg/mL QY and 5 mg/mL LAP). The
pre-polymer solution was exposed with light from beneath for 3 seconds before the stage was elevated
40µm to the new position while the prepolymer solution was covering the surface between the vat and
bottom of the printing platform. The ﬁrst 3 layers were exposed 4 times each for 3 seconds with 10µm
vertical displacement between each position and the 4th layer was exposed 2 times for 3 seconds with
20µm vertical displacement between each exposure. This was to increase the bonding strength to the
cover glass. After printing, the structure was stored in DI water.

The degree of crosslinking is deﬁned as the density of crosslinked points in the polymer network, which

parallel to the stress. When the stress is removed the strain will instantaneously drop to zero.[27, p.195]

The ability of a material to absorb energy and deform without fracturing is deﬁned as the toughness of
the material. The toughness is an important mechanical property for a material, which should withstand
fracture when a defect is present. Toughness can be experimental measured by a compression test with a
low strain rate and is calculated by the area under the curve of a tensile stress-strain graph to the point
of fracture. High tensile strength of a material is thus a balance of high strength and ductility.[53, p.169]

maintain the good biocompatible properties of PEGDA, the SLA printing abilities, and with the aim
of improving the mechanical properties of the material used for the printed structure, the monomer

Biological Tests
This chapter will present the biological tests performed at Coloplast A/S in their pre-clinical R&D de-
partment. Following tests were performed: 1) Pig urethra and glass bladder test, 2) Zone of inhibition
of glue, catheter, and sampler adhered to the catheter, 3) Extraction test for glue, 4) Sampling test with

In all biological tests one speciﬁc bacteria was used, which was chosen to have the ability to isolate that
speciﬁc bacteria from others present in the test setup. This was important since it was not possible to
keep a sterile test environment with both pig urethra and a non-sterile sampler included. To isolate
the bacteria, a Luria Broth (LB) agar plate with 25 mg/mL chloramphenicol antibiotic was used, which
allowed detection of only bacteria resistant to chloramphenicol. The Escherichia coli UTI 89 bacteria used
in the test has a chloramphenicol resistence gene with GFP plasmid Taxonomy 364106. Uropathogenic
E.coli can be associated with acute or recurrent urinary tract infection. In separate agar plates, the PBS
media and saline used in the test were tested for not containing the UTI 89 bacteria. The pig urethra
used in the tests was delivered to Coloplast from an abattoir the same morning as used for testing. The
pig urethra were prepared by cutting o↵all surplus fat and tissue. The bladder was detached right above
the prostate gland to maintain the external sphincter for proper enclosure around the glass bladder model
in the tests.

Chapter 8
Industrial Scale Production
In this chapter, the perspectives of an industrial scale production are discussed based on the current
production methods of urinary catheters.
The catheter manufacturing process is comprehensive and includes obtaining the technical features of
the catheter such as the tube, tip, eyelets, and connector, but also coating, sterilization, and packaging
are important for the manufacturing process before shipment to the consumer. In this section coating,
sterilization, and packaging are not discussed. Current SpeediCath Flex catheters are manufactured from
extrusion of long hollow tubes. The extrusion method is based on the manufacturing of long tubing by
trusting polymer in a molten state through a shaped die with a constant rate and exits into a cooling
tank to control polymer crystallization.[55] Extrusion is a fast and e↵ective fabrication method for large
batch production and has low tooling cost, but for catheter manufacturing it requires post extrusion
production steps to obtain all required features next to the catheter body including e.g. tip and drainage
eyelets.
Injection molding is another way of fabricating catheters allowing for more complicated shapes. A
liquid polymer is injected into a shaped mold where it is solidiﬁed. Suitable materials for both injection
molding and extrusion are thermoplastics since they can be shaped from heat treatment. Figures from
a Coloplast US Patent US9084867B2 [56] presenting a method of injection molding a catheter assembly
is seen in Figure 8.1. The mold, which overall consists of two parts: a long cylindrical cavity forming
external catheter surface (3 and 4 in Figure 8.1a) and an inner solid long cylindrical pin deﬁning the
inner surface of the catheter (7 and 9 in Figure 8.1a), are withdrawn from each other post polymer
injection, and the catheter assembly can be removed. The eyelets are also provided during the injection
molding from component 10 and 11 in Figure 8.1a having the shape of the eyelets.[56] In Figure 8.1a the
component 3 and 4 are two separate components which might cause wear and entailing protrusions in
the outer surface of the catheter and are critical disadvantages of injection molding. Figure 8.1b shows
a mold where component 41 is one single piece designed to avoid tears and protrusions, and component
43 a cylindrical pin forming the inner surface of the catheter. The one-piece mold setup has two overall
requirements to enable the catheter to be removed from the mold: 1) No radial protrusions and 2) a
lower friction force between the catheter and component 41 than the catheter and component 43 to
avoid that component 43 is loosening from the catheter when pulling. To mold the full catheter in one
injection, the one-piece setup must be combined with a multi-piece mold to mold catheter connector or

Chapter 9
Discussion
Today the standard of care sampling method entailing least contamination was found to be the suprapubic
aspiration (SPA) technique, which is invasive, infrequently used in adults, requires clinical expertise, and
is not suitable for diagnosis outside the hospital.[20, 24] Providing a catheter-integrated urine-sampler
with the aim of collecting urine directly from the bladder lumen was considered of high relevance and
a breakthrough within the urine collection methods. In this project the two urine micro-samplers were
therefore designed with the approach of integrating the sampler with the proximal end of a catheter
with the aim of investigating the potential of collecting the urine internally instead of outside the body.
For adults not using a catheter, this sampling method will rise a trade-o↵between contamination and
invasiveness, but for catheter patients invasiveness is unavoidable and contamination is a key issue.
The two designed micro-samplers demonstrated good integration with the catheter by adhesion with glue.
This integration method is not suitable for clinical use or manufacturing processes but demonstrated a
potential of a micro-sampler being attached to a catheter. The micro-sampler should be integrated with
the catheter in the manufacturing process or perhaps act as a add-on device which could be integrated
when needed.
From the proof of concept experiments it was demonstrated that the sampler should
preferably be integrated above the ﬂex tip constriction, and it can therefore be discussed if the micro-
sampler should be implemented instead of the tip to retain the ﬂexibility and low insertion resistance.
The two sampling techniques investigated in this project both demonstrated great potential and
success in the proof of concept tests performed from a clinical perspective. Even though the capillary
e↵ect sampling technique in design type 1 successfully collected spontaneously sampling liquid, the
method required a dry sampling environment and atmospheric pressure by the ventilation port inside
the bladder.
These requirements conﬂicted with the current boundary conditions of a ready-to-use
catheter environment and the urinary tract physiology. A larger potential was seen for the ﬂow based
sampling technique utilized by a venturi channel in design type 2, which was independent on catheter
environment, and collected sampling liquid simultaneously with bladder voiding through the catheter.
This sampling technique required implementation of a venturi channel in the catheter lumen, but as
described in the perspectives of industrial-scale production thin tubes are easily fabricated by extrusion
processes and the extra venturi channel would therefore presumably not entail production problems
compared to the structural complexity of the micro-sampler.

As the only current commercialized POC diagnosis method for detecting UTI in humans is the urine
dip stick, a satisfactory volume of collected urine was not directly correlated to a current commercialized
product for human use. The RapidBac and Antagen hIL-8 LFA POC tests requires 150µL and 80 µL
urine for detecting UTI respectively.[15, 17] Considering improvement of test methods to require less
volume, a minimum of 60 µL was considered as a suﬃcient sampling volume, which the design type 2
exceeded. In the biological tests for the design type 2 micro-sampler, a collected volume of 80 −90 µL
was achieved, which however conﬂicted with the theoretical volume of 67.9µL and must have contained
excess ﬂuid from the catheter. To comply with the volume requirements of the RapidBac test method, the
volume should be expanded, and even though a larger volume was possible to achieve by increasing the
size of the micro-sampler, it was decided not to pursue this further since proof of sampling concept was of
priority. As discussed for the design type 1 micro-sampler the manufacturing and urethra insertion of a
larger micro-sampler was possible, and the potential of obtaining a micro-sampler volume of 80 −100 µL
was considered possible.
Bacterial contamination tests were performed two or three times as preliminary pre-clinical tests
why only tendencies can be extracted from these tests. Taking sampling representativity into account
the tests indicated a mean contamination level during retraction of the catheter through the urethra of
15% for both urine micro-sampler designs and 49% during insertion for design type 2. These results
indicate an approved contamination rate during retraction compared to functional requirements of 
30%. Considering the contamination rate compared to the RapidBac POC test method with a detection
level of 103 CFU/mL, the mean contamination level during retraction for the micro-samplers were 151%
and 147% for design type 1 and 2 respectively which indicated a contamination rate too large.
From a design perspective, the contamination of design type 1 was a↵ected by the outer implementa-
tion of the extraction port, which caused a source of contamination from the extraction technique of the
collected sample. Unlike the ﬂow based sampling technique of design type 2 where the urine is ﬂowing
through the sampling chamber as long as the urine is voiding through the catheter, the sample collected
in design type 1 is not replaced after being collected inside the bladder. A risk of contaminating the
collected sample during retraction through the urethra is therefore present for design type 2 if the bladder
and urethra is not completely voided before retraction of the catheter. This is therefore a disadvantage of
the sampling technique in design type 2. To utilize the advantages of design type 1, the design, however,
requires total isolation from urine and moisture until reaching the bladder and atmospheric pressure
inside the bladder to allow the air being replaced with urine. The isolation could potentially be solved
by an outer shielding coating on the bars in front of the sampling inlets. However, the disadvantages of
using a coating and the reason why it was not further investigated in this project, was that the residuals
of the coating properly would be collected inside the micro-sampler as a result of the spontaneous ﬁlling.
Bacterial contamination is most relevant in clinical analytical test methods testing for total CFU
counts or speciﬁc CFU count for which the relevant target bacteria can be found in the urinary tract.
The Antagen hIL-8 test method would not be a↵ected by bacterial contamination from the urinary tract
since the analytical target is a biological marker released from inﬂammatory cells. Relevant for the An-
tagen hIL-8 test method would be a urine sample with good bladder representativity. In the biological
sampling test results, the micro-samplers type 1 and 2 indicated a ≥82% and ≥74% representative bacte-
ria count in the collected sample compared to the expected count. These results indicate that the sample
collected by the micro-sampler did not mimic exactly what was expected. At the same time it is worth

With the potential of developing an integrated urine sampling method, which automatically collects a
urine-sample directly from the bladder during catheterization and thus makes sampling of urine easier
for the patient, user perspectives are worth investigating and must be evaluated through usability studies.
The 90% PEGDA (Mw 700) material composition used for the micro-sampler prototype o↵ered suﬃcient
mechanical properties to withstand the proof of concept and biological tests performed in this study.
During the last biological test, 2-3 samples were, however, destroyed due to too rough handling compared
to the material toughness, which conﬁrmed that the material needs to be changed in order to obtain a
more durable prototype which can be used for more comprehensive testing.
To maintain the advantages of using stereolithographic 3D printing, the commercialized FormLabs
3D printer with the Clear resin was used for testing if the printer could provide a suﬃcient resolution
to print the design 1. The Clear resin was the one utilizing the highest resolution and was supposed
to support a print resolution of 100, 50, and 25 microns.[59] The test failed as the printed sampler was
completely solid presumably due to insuﬃcient printing resolution. A material with a higher tensile
elongation at failure and toughness is needed to comply with the urethra anatomy without breaking. A
possible solution could be either the Flexible or Durable FormLab resins with 75% and 49% elongation
at failure respectively.[60, 61] Both resins have a higher tensile strength than the 90% PEGDA (Mw 700)
material, but slightly lower than the polyurethane used for SpeediCath catheters.[28]. To obtain 3D
printing with these two resins, a high resolution printing process would then need to be established.
Injection molding of the micro-sampler could also be a possibility, as discussed in the Chapter 8, which
would allow for usage of the polyurethane SpeediCath catheter material.[28]

Chapter 10
Conclusion and Outlook
The aim of this project was to develop a prototype of a new urine sampling method for catheterized pa-
tients with the ability to collect an uncontaminated urine sample directly from the bladder for diagnostic
purposes.
Two di↵erent prototypes of catheter-integrated urine micro-samplers was designed and with success
integrated to the proximal end of the Coloplast SpeediCath Flex intermittent catheter. The prototypes
was manufactured by 3D stereolithographic printing and was tested to have the suﬃcient mechanical
stability to withstand integration with the catheter as well as insertion and retraction through a urethra
model.
By iteratively optimizing the design, a micro-sampler with capillary e↵ect sampling was developed.
The design was tested to demonstrate successful liquid uptake from a bladder model with the boundary
requirements of a dry sampling environment constricting against the SpeediCath ﬂex catheter conditions.
A micro-sampler with venturi e↵ect sampling was also developed, utilizing the current conditions of the
SpeediCath ﬂex catheter. By integrating the micro-sampler instead of the catheter ﬂex tip and with a
channel integrated in the catheter lumen, the micro-sampler was capable of collecting a urine sample
directly from the bladder concurrently with the normal catheter usage. A sample volume of 80µL was
collected from the bladder model by the latter micro-sampler prototype, which was considered suﬃcient
to diagnose UTI with the preliminary in vitro research hIL-8 POC test. The few contamination tests of
the two micro-samplers showed contamination levels of 15% and 49% compared to the limit for bacterial
contamination, and with a success criteria of 30% this was partially considered as a successful result.
At the end all aims of the project was achieved. The catheter-integrated micro-sampler prototypes
and new sampling techniques were considered as successful and as a small breakthrough within urine
sampling methods.
A direction for future work could be to improve the prototype material, conduct comprehensive con-
tamination studies, and develop manufacturing prototypes for large scale production to verify the future
potential of a catheter-integrated micro-sampler. To improve the design of the micro-sampler, biological
experiments showing where the contamination is accumulating would be needed.
