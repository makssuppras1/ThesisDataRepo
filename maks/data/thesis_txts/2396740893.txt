Abstract
Purpose: Develop a method to obtain reliable automated patellar instability knee measurements.
Measurements include: sulcus angle, lateral trochlear inclination, tibial tuberosity-trochlear groove
distance, patellar bisect oﬀset, lateral patellar tilt, Insall-Salvati index, modiﬁed Insall-Salvati index
and Caton-Deschamps index.
Materials and Methods: Materials include 3T MRI sagittal and axial supine scans sequences
from 50 patients. Scans from 40 patients were augmented with anatomical landmarks relevant for
the measurements to build an atlas, and 10 patients were kept as test data. An atlas-based segmen-
tation was performed using the Python medical image registration library SimpleElastix to obtain
relevant landmarks for the test patients, that were then used to compute the measurements.
Results: Results were judged acceptable if within the deﬁned ’typical error’ range for manual mea-
surements performed by experts. Number of acceptable results out of 10 test patients: sulcus angle
(2), lateral trochlear inclination (0), tibial tuberosity-trochlear groove distance (2), patellar bisect
oﬀset (3), lateral patellar tilt (2), Insall-Salvati index (4), modiﬁed Insall-Salvati index (3), Caton-
Deschamps index (4).
Conclusion: At this point, the method does not perform well enough to be used in a medical con-
text. However, the obtention of acceptable measurements shows that the method has potential to
perform well, if improved and used on a larger set of more appropriate data.
Keywords: patellar instability, automated measurements, MRI, atlas-based segmentation, SimpleE-
lastix, sulcus angle, lateral trochlear inclination, tibial tuberosity-trochlear groove, patellar bisect oﬀ-
set, lateral patellar tilt, Insall-Salvati index, modiﬁed Insall-Salvati index, Caton-Deschamps index.

Introduction
1.1
Project context
Patellar instability is a common disabling condition and one of the main causes of anterior knee pain
in patients. It refers to a range of injuries that occur when the kneecap is pulled out of its resting
place, partially or completely. Complete dislocations are often recurrent and can lead to progressive
cartilage damage and severe arthritis [7]. Most patients suﬀering from patellar instability are young
and active individuals, with women in the second decade of their life at highest risk [7, 14, 25].
Diﬀerent surgical options exist to treat patellar instability, depending on the origin of the problem,
but all require precise measurements of various anatomical factors beforehand. However, acquiring
accurate and reliable measurements in a consistent way is far from easy.
In Danish hospitals, measurements are currently obtained manually from static sequences of the knee,
where the patient is placed in a supine position (lying down) in the scanner. The sequences consist
of series of slices taken along three orthogonal imaging planes (axial, sagittal, coronal).
While those sequences provide a rather complete depiction of the knee joint, navigating through the
slices in search of relevant anatomical landmarks can prove diﬃcult and depends strongly on the
reader’s experience. It leads to bias in the measurements and oﬀers little repeatability, which is far
from ideal as the nature of the surgery depends on those measurements.
1.2
Aim of the Project
Our aim is to alleviate such problems by trying to automate the way the measurements are made,
in order to reduce bias and allow repeatability.
We would like to obtain reliable automated measurements that describe the position of the patella
(knee cap), relative to the trochlea (bone groove in which the knee cap rests).
In order to do that, we intend to ﬁrst build an atlas, a set of scans annotated manually by an expert
reader with the anatomical landmarks needed for the measurements. This atlas will then be used
to perform an atlas-based segmentation, which consists in matching as well as possible the new scan
with the scans from the atlas (registration), then transferring their anatomical landmarks to the
new scan (segmentation). Once anatomical landmarks are obtained for the new scan, the diﬀerent
measurements can be computed.

Medical Background
This chapter aims at providing an overview of the targeted condition, i.e. patellar instability, its
treatment and the diﬀerent measurements we will be working with during this project.
2.1
Patellar Instability
Under normal circumstances, the kneecap (patella) is located in a groove (trochlea) at the bottom of
the femur, as shown on Figure 2.1. The patella slides up (extension) and down (ﬂexion) the trochlear
groove when the knee moves. Patellar instability occurs when the patella comes out of the trochlea
or does not rest properly in it, causing a subluxation or dislocation.
Figure 2.1: Bone Structure of the Knee [9]
Patellar instability can have various origins. It can be the result of an accident (trauma to the knee),
but can also be linked to several anatomical factors:
Trochlear dysplasia
A shallow or abnormally shaped trochlea (Figure 2.2) can be diﬃcult for the
patella to slide in, causing the patella to go oﬀ-track. It has been identiﬁed as the most common
factor when it comes to chronic patellar instability (occuring in 85% of the cases [16]).
Patella alta
For some patients, the patella naturally rest in an abnormally high position compared
to the femur. The patella then sits in a part of the groove that is very shallow and any strong
contraction of the quadriceps is likely to pull the patella out of the trochlear groove.
Stretched soft tissues
The patella is held in place in the trochlea by tendons and ligaments
(Figure 2.3). If some of those tissues are damaged, too stretched or too ﬂexible, they are no longer
able to maintain the patella properly in place. The patella will then dislocate more easily.
Muscles weakness
The muscles around the knee also maintain the patella in place.
It often
happens that the lateral (outer) quadriceps is stronger and tighter than the medial (inner) quadriceps,
causing the patella to be pulled out of the trochlea.

2.2. TREATMENT
2.2
Treatment
Patellar instability originating from muscle weakness or from an accident can be treated with the
help of a physical therapist, provided that none of the tendons or ligaments were badly damaged [12]
[25]. On the other hand, patellar instability due to an abnormal trochlea or damaged tissues often
require surgery since, even after the patella is put back into place, there is a very high risk that it
will dislocate again (40-60% [14]). Most common surgical procedures include:
• Trochleoplasty
When the problem originates from a severely abnormally shaped trochlea, several bone reshaping
procedures exist to allow the patella to slide properly in the trochlea. It is however a diﬃcult,
demanding surgical procedure that requires high levels of precision [16, 17, 25].
• Medial Patellofemoral Ligament (MPFL) Reconstruction
Most lateral patellar instability problems originate from an injured or over-strecthed MPFL (Figure
2.3), that can be reconstructed or replaced in order in stabilize the patella laterally. It produces
very good results with very low levels of redislocation [6, 25].
• Tibial Tuberosity Realignment
Should the patellar instability be due to any type of misalignment of the patella compared to the
trochlear groove, the most common solution is to change the insertion point of the patellar tendon
on the tibia (tibial tuberosity, see Figure 2.3) to realign the patella. In case of a patella alta, the
tibial tuberosity is advanced distally (towards the feet), in order to lower the patella. In case of
a lateral misalignment, the tibial tuberosity is moved laterally (towards the outside) or medially
(towards the inside) to allow the patella to rest properly in the trochlear groove [11, 16].
2.3
Radiological Measurements
All surgical procedures mentioned previously require a high level of precision. A large range of mea-
surements exist to assess the origin of the problem, its severity and what kind of surgery it calls for.
Unfortunately, radiologists and doctors tend to rely on a set of measurements and criteria speciﬁc
to their practice. Even when the imaging techniques used are the same, the conditions in which
the measurements are done vary greatly. There is a real lack of a gold standard when it comes to
patellar instability measurements [15, 27]. We selected the ones most commonly used in hospitals in
Denmark. It is those we hope to be able to measure reliably and consistently at the end of this project.
When a patient comes in with recurrent dislocation issues, the doctor systematically checks whether:
• The trochlear groove is too shallow or too ﬂat (trochlear dysplasia).
It can be assessed using the sulcus angle (Figure 2.4) and the lateral trochlear inclination angle
(Figure 2.5). The trochlear groove is considered too shallow if the sulcus angle exceeds 144° or if the
lateral trochlea inclination angle is below 11°. A trochleoplasty might then be necessary [16, 15, 25].
• The patella is misaligned with the trochlear groove (patellar misalignment)
* The Tibial Tuberosity-Trochlear Groove distance (Figure 2.6) gives a good idea of the lateral force
exerted by the patellar tendon on the patella and should not be larger than 20mm. Any larger

Theory
This chapter introduces the key concepts of medical image analysis and methods that will be used
to perform the automated measurements related to patellar instability.
3.1
Image Registration
When working with medical images, it is important to be able to compare diﬀerent images, be it
to monitor changes in a single patient or to compare diﬀerent patients. It requires however that all
images are in the same coordinate system.
Image registration is the process of transforming diﬀerent images into a common reference coordinate
system, which is often an image to which one wants to align the other images. In other words, image
registration consists in ﬁnding a geometrical transformation T that aligns the points p of an input
image with corresponding points q in a reference image, as illustrated in Figure 3.1.
Figure 3.1: Image Registration [10]
Registration methods are based on the assumption that, at the optimal registration, the two images
will show the highest degree of similarity. It is an optimization problem that aims to maximize the
similarity/minimize the diﬀerences between the two images, by adjusting the geometrical transfor-
mation parameters at each step using a given optimization method.
3.1.1
Geometrical Transformations
Geometrical transformations can be divided into diﬀerent categories:
• Rigid transformations
Rigid transformations include all transformations that do not change the distance and angles
between points nor the shape of elements presents in the image: translation, rotation, reﬂection or
a combination of those. It is often used for mono-modal intra-patient registration, when the patient
is scanned at diﬀerent times with the same scanner, to account for diﬀerences in position [21, 23].
• Similarity transformations
Similarity transformations add isotropic scaling (identical in all directions) to rigid transformations.
It is often used for multi-modal intra-patient registration, when a patient is scanned with diﬀerent
types of scanners, as it compensates for both changes in position but also in spatial resolution.

CHAPTER 3. THEORY
3.1. IMAGE REGISTRATION
• Aﬃne transformations
Aﬃne transformations add shearing to similarity transformations.
It is often used to correct
geometric distortions/deformations in case of faulty image acquisition.
• Elastic transformations
While all three previous types of transformations were global and applied uniformely to the entire
image, elastic transformations are more ﬂexible and allow for local deformations. Elastic transfor-
mations help to account for anatomical variability between patients.
Elastic transformations, and more speciﬁcally B-splines based transformations, are well suited for
this project, as the available data originate from a large number of patients with very diﬀerent knee
anatomies.
B-splines functions are deﬁned piecewise by polynomials passing through a series of control points,
or knots, to create smooth curves. Those functions only have a local support, which means that
they only aﬀect the range between the ﬁrst and last knots, and are equal to zero elsewhere. That
last property makes B-splines very useful for local adjustements of the registration, as changing the
coeﬃcients for one B-spline basis function will only aﬀect a very local area of the image [1, 23].
3.1.2
Similarity Measures
The goodness of the registration, i.e. how well the input image is aligned to the reference image,
is given by a similarity measure. The similarity measure is taken at each step of the registration
and guides the transformation process until the similarity measure reaches its maximum. Similarity
measures can be based on diﬀerent criteria, the most popular being:
• Sum-of-Squared-Diﬀerences (SSD)
This similarity measure is based on the assumption that corresponding points in both images should
have the same intensity. It computes the pixel-wise mean squared intensity diﬀerence between the
input and the reference image [10]. It should be as close to zero as possible for the registration to
be valid. It implies that this similarity measure should only be used for images with very similar
intensity distributions, like MR images from the same scanner.
• Correlation Coeﬃcient (CC)
Should the images intensity distributions be less similar but still related by a linear transfor-
mation, the correlation coeﬃcient similarity measure can be used.
It computes the pixel-wise
cross-correlation normalized by the squared root of the autocorrelation of the images [10]. It should
be as close to 1 as possible for the registration to be valid.
• Mutual Information (MI)
When the images intensity distributions are very diﬀerent, a more general similarity measure has
to be used. The mutual information provides information on how much the intensity distribution
in one image depends on the intensity distribution in the other image. It is a measure of the
dependency of the probability density distributions (PDF) of the intensities of the input and the
reference image [10]. It is extremely useful for multi-modal registration.
Theoretically, an SSD similarity measure should have been suﬃcient, since the scans used in this
project were all acquired on the same scanner with the same procedure. It turned out however that
the distributions were not as similar as ﬁrst assumed and a MI similarity measure was chosen instead.

3.1.3
Optimization and Regularization
At each step of the registration, new parameters are computed for the geometrical transformation,
in order to guide the registered image towards a better alignment, until the registration is optimal.
Those transformation parameters are obtained using an optimization method that can be coupled
with a regularization term to make sure only reasonable transformations are applied.
A large range of optimization methods exist: Gradient Descent, Adaptative Stochastic Gradient De-
scent, Conjugate Gradient, Quasi-Newton LBFGS,... Not all methods will be described, as most are
rather complex. An emphasis will be put on Gradient Descents methods, and more particularly the
Adaptative Stochastic Gradient Descend (ASGD) that will be used for this project, as it requires
very few parameters and tends to be more robust than most other methods [10, 22].
The Gradient Descent is an optimization algorithm designed to ﬁnd the minimum of a function. At
each iteration, it takes a step (of ﬁxed size) in the direction of the negative of the gradient (down the
slope) of the function to minimize (diﬀerence between the two images), until it reaches a minimum
(or a given convergence criterion) where the gradient is equal to zero. A very simple illustration (in
2D with only one minimum) of the method is presented in Figure 3.2.
The Adaptative Stochastic Gradient Descent is a stochastic approximation of a Gradient Descent
with an adaptative step size rather than a ﬁxed one.

3.2. IMAGE SEGMENTATION
3.2
Image Segmentation
When a medical image is fed to a computer for analyzing, all the computer sees is a matrix of values
that represent pixels intensities. It does not see a knee made of bones and cartilage and needs to be
told not only which features are of interest in the input image, but also where they are located.
Image segmentation is the process of extracting or detecting features of interest (objects, boundaries,
points) from an input image. It is done by assigning, manually or with the help of an algorithm, a
given label to all the pixels that belong to a speciﬁc feature of interest.
Diﬀerent types of segmentation exist, depending on the feature of interest (point, region, bound-
ary,...), on the type of scans used and the criteria on which the segmentation is based (intensity,
color, prior information,...). There are so many diﬀerent segmentation methods that only the one we
used will be described here: the atlas-based segmentation.
3.2.1
Atlas-Based Segmentation
Atlas-based segmentation methods have the ability to segment images for which the features of in-
terests are hard to identify and vary signiﬁcantly in terms of shape and size across individuals.
In our case, the features of interest are the diﬀerent anatomical landmarks that will be used to
compute the automated measurements presented in section 2.3. Since those landmarks are already
diﬃcult to identify manually and require the help of an experienced reader, an atlas-based segmen-
tation was chosen.
An atlas is deﬁned as a set of reference images Ri augmented with their corresponding segmentations
Si. The term segmentation can be confusing at times but, in this report, it will always refer to a
label image in which the location (relative to the image considered) of each anatomical landmark
needed for the measurements is represented by a dot (group of pixels) to which an identical integer
value is assigned (label number). The background is assigned the value 0.
The atlas-based segmentation consists of two steps that are illustrated in Figure 3.3:
• Registration
Each atlas image Ri is registered to the input image to be analyzed, by ﬁnding the transformation
Ti that aligns best the image Ri to the input image.
• Label propagation
Each segmentation Si is warped using the corresponding transformation Ti found above while
registering Ri to the input image. The result is a set of transformed potential segmentations S′
i,
which are then combined to obtain a single segmentation label image for the input image.
To sum up, atlas-based segmentation methods have the advantage of being performant where most
other methods fail when it comes to images with no well-deﬁned relation between features and pixel
intensities, but also account for the variability in shape and size in patients [20]. On the other hand,

In order to obtain automated measurements for any new knee scan using an atlas-based segmentation,

The results is a set of potential segmentation label images for the input image.
As explained before, the segmentations consist of label images in which the pixels are assigned an
integer value depending on whether:
• The pixel’s location corresponds to an anatomical landmark in the atlas image, in which case the
pixel is assigned the landmark’s label number as value (see Figures 4.3 and 4.3).
• The pixel’s location does not correspond to any anatomical landmark in the atlas image, in which
case the pixel belongs to the background and is assigned the value 0.
The label propagation step generally involves some degree of interpolation, as some pixels of the newly
created segmentation label image might not have been assigned a value. The default parameters of
TransformixImageFilter replaces the missing values by a linear combination of the surrounding

values, resulting in pixels being assigned a ﬂoat value and not belonging to any label. In order to
avoid that, the ResampleInterpolator parameter was set to "FinalNearestNeighborInterpolator".
The missing values are then no longer replaced by linear combinations of surrounding pixels, but
rather by the nearest neighboring pixel value, which is an integer value.
An additional complication appeared during the label propagation process.
Scan sequences may
diﬀer largely from one patient to another and do not always start or end at equivalent anatomical
locations. Some might display more tibia or femur than others (for axial sequences) or start more
laterally (for sagittal sequences) for example. Should the atlas and input images be very diﬀerent,
labels might be warped to a slice that does not exist in the input image, causing the segmentation
label image obtained after the label propagation to miss some labels. In those cases, the segmentation
is dismissed from the set of potential segmentations.
4.3.3
Potential segmentation label images combination
The diﬀerent potential segmentations have to be combined into one ﬁnal segmentation in order to
obtain the anatomical landmarks for the input image.
At this point, the potential segmentations take the form of label images, where each landmark label
consists of a group of pixels. It is highly impractical to compute measurements. Landmarks labels
should ideally be represented by a single point instead of a group of pixels. The single-point labels
could then be averaged across all potential segmentations, in order to generate single-point landmark
labels for the input image, that could then be used to compute the radiological measurements.
In a ﬁrst time, LabelShapeStatisticsImageFilter was used to convert each label image into a label
map, allowing to extract the labels coordinates and attributes. The coordinates of the centroid of each
label (single point) were then computed for each of the label maps obtained using GetCentroid().
The label centroids were ﬁnally averaged across all potential segmentations to obtain a single-point
landmark label map for the input image.
The single-point landmarks labels for the input image are given as a list of physical coordinates
(x,y,z).
Those physical coordinates were additionally converted to indexes (relative to the input
image) for later display in ITK Snap using TransformPhysicalPointToContinuousIndex().

Discussion
This chapter discusses and elaborates on the results obtained in the previous chapter and comments
known issues to work on.
6.1
Discussion of Results
The atlas-based segmentation results, i.e. the ﬁnal sets of anatomical landmarks obtained for each
of the ten patients, turned out to be rather unsuccessful. Only six complete sets of landmarks, two
axial (one left knee and one right knee) and four sagittal (three left knees and one right knee) were
all located where they should be.
From the analyzed data, the method appears to perform better on left knees than right knees, as well
as sagittal over axial sequences. An explanation for that could be the variation in size of the diﬀerent
atlases. The left knee atlas contains twice as many scans as the right knee atlas, and more axial than
sagittal sequences had to be removed from the atlas due to dimensions issues. Larger atlases generally
produce better results, as it is more likely to contain knees that are similar to the one being analyzed.
Another reason that might explain why sagittal sequences produced better results than axial ones,
is directly linked to the sagittal sequences and anatomical landmarks themselves. First, the sagittal
measurements only require 5 anatomical landmarks, while the axial measurements require 8 land-
marks. The probability of wrongly locating a landmark increases with the number of landmarks to
identify. In addition to that, all sagittal landmarks can be found on slices located around the middle
of the scan sequence (lateral center of the knee). This is not the case for axial landmarks, such as the
tibial-tuberosity landmark which is located among the most inferior slices of the scan sequence. It
makes the sagittal landmarks much less sensitive to where the scan sequence starts or ends. Finally,
the spacing between slices for the sagittal sequences (3,3 mm) is smaller than for the axial sequences
(3,6 mm), which increases the probability of the two images to be registered to have more in common,
and thus of obtaining a better registration/atlas-based segmentation.
Even though some of the sets of anatomical landmarks obtained during the atlas-based segmenta-
tion were not always located where they should have been, some still produced better measurements
than expected, as they were sometimes simply translated a little further while conserving angle and
distances.
Axial measurements did not do very well, with an average of 2 successful measurements out of 10
patients. The patellar bisect oﬀset did a little better than average with 3 acceptable measurements,
while the lateral trochlear inclination angle did not include any single acceptable measurement. The
lateral trochlear inclination angle however is known to be a diﬃcult measurement, as it is highly
dependent on the slice from the scan sequence chosen.
Sagittal measurements produced better results than the axial measurements here too, with 3 (for

To sum up, more than half of the results obtained were not deemed acceptable, indicating that the

the known issues presented in the next section.
6.2
Known Issues and Proposed Solutions
Diﬀerent issues with the current materials and methods were identiﬁed as key problems that need to
be solved in order to obtain more accurate results.
6.2.1
Anatomical Landmarks Label Images Combination
During the atlas-based segmentation process, each atlas image is registered to the input image and
its segmentation is transformed accordingly to obtain a potential segmentation for the input image,
as explained in the previous chapters. All potential segmentations then need to be combined into
one ﬁnal segmentation for the input image.
At this point, all potential segmentations are simply averaged and have the same weight in the ﬁnal
segmentation. A good segmentation (issued from an atlas image that very closely matched the input
image during the registration) will thereby have the same weight as a poor segmentation (issued from
an atlas image for which the registration only stopped because the maximum number of iterations
was reached, not because the similarity measure reached its maximum), resulting in a rather poor
ﬁnal segmentation, and thus poor measurements.
In order to obtain better ﬁnal segmentations and measurements, the registration similarity measure
results should be taken into account, so that poor potential segmentations issued from poor regis-
trations are not taken into account during the potential segmentations combination process. It was
not implemented during this project due to lack of time, since it proved to be a challenging task.
The SimpleElastix package does not, at this point, provide an easy access to the registration’s ﬁnal
similarity measure value, so the value has to be extracted from hundreds of output lines in the console
using string parsing methods.
Integrating the registration’s ﬁnal similarity measure into the potential segmentations combination
process could signiﬁcantly improve the results.
6.2.2
Atlas Size
An obvious yet important issue is the size of the atlas. At this point, the sagittal atlas only contains
19 left knees and 10 right knees, while the axial atlas only includes 14 left knees and 7 right knees
due to the removal of sequences with dimensions issues. This hardly accounts for the large variation
38
==PAGE:40==

6.2. KNOWN ISSUES AND PROPOSED SOLUTIONS
in knees within a population, which can make it diﬃcult to ﬁnd knees in the atlas that are remotely
similar to the new knee being analyzed. A much larger atlas would drastically increase the chances
of ﬁnding similar knees in terms of shape and size.
Of course, as mentioned before, building an atlas is not a light task and requires time, as it necessitates
enough patients and an expert with time on his hand to perform the initial manual segmentation.
6.2.3
Scanning Procedure
The scan sequences currently included in the data set are the ones standardly used for patellar in-
stability diagnostics by doctors at the hospital. Those sequences often do not start or end at similar
anatomical locations, contain relatively few slices (30 for sagittal and 38 for axial sequences) and are
separated by rather large distances (3.3 mm for sagittal and 3.6 mm for axial sequences) relative to
the whole knee. While those sequences seem to fulﬁll their purpose when measurements are done
manually, they become rather problematic when trying to obtain automated measurements.
To begin with, some anatomical landmarks are in fact located in regions contained between two
slices. While this lack of information is not an issue when performing measurements manually, as
it is compensated by the doctor’s knowledge of the knee anatomy, it can lead to imprecise measure-
ments when using automated methods, since the computer does not have the same knowledge at hand.
On top of that, the limited amount of available slices raises additional issues. It is diﬃcult for the
registration to perform well when the scan sequences that are to be matched consist of slices located
at very diﬀerent anatomical locations. On top of that, as explained in the materials and methods
chapter, labels sometimes disappear when being warped from a slice that exists in the atlas image
sequence, to a slice that does not exist in the input image sequence during the label propagation.
This resulted in a rather large number of scan sequences being discarded during the atlas-based
segmentation.
Attempting to adjust the scanning procedure to always obtain the same kind of sequences would
be a challenging and time-consuming task, as patients vary largely in size. On the other hand, 3D
MRI scanning procedures recently became available on some of the scanners at the Frederiksberg-
Bispebjerg radiology department for supine knee examinations.
The 3D sequences consist of 512 x 512 x 203 slices (respectively in the axial, coronal and sagittal
planes) separated by 0.41 mm. Using those sequences would:
• Produce better registrations and reduce the amount of disappearing labels. The images sequences
to register are likely to have more in common, now that they both consist of a much larger number
of slices, with very little spacing between them.
• Generate more precise measurements. It is unlikely for any anatomical landmarks to be located in
regions not covered by the available slices, as the spacing between slices is extremely small in all
directions. The information left-out would no longer be signiﬁcant.

6.2.4
Manual Segmentation Tools
ITK Snap is a very practical segmentation tool, from being relatively easy and intuitive to use, to
being compatible with SimpleElastix. It turned out, however, that using ITK Snap segmentations
to perform an atlas-based segmentation required using large brush size (4mm) manual dots to rep-
resent the anatomical landmarks, risking otherwise having labels disappear when being scaled down
or compressed during the label propagation step.
This requirement made the manual segmentation (creation of anatomical landmarks dots on the scan
sequences) much less precise than originally, when the doctors picked their own anatomical land-
marks and computed their own measurements using standard DICOM medical image viewers such
as Horos [3] or OsiriX [8].
It would be an interesting idea to ﬁnd a way to make the manual segmentation more precise or
alternatively using diﬀerent software. The possibility of using point sets instead of segmentation
images was investigated, but label images turned out to be much easier to program, as point sets
have to be written manually to the disk ﬁrst.

Conclusion
As stressed before, patellar instability measurements are extremely important when it comes to
determining whether a patient needs surgery or not, and which type of surgery is needed. Those
measurements depend unfortunately heavily on the reader, which leads to bias in measurements.
We had thus set ourselves for goal to investigate whether or not it was possible to obtain reliable
atlas-based automated patellar instability measurements, in order to reduce bias.
We started by acquiring suitable scans for 50 patients, 10 of which were kept as test patients while
the other 40 patients were used to build an atlas. The scans to be used for the atlas were augmented
by a doctor with anatomical landmarks relevant to the measurements targeted. The atlas built was
used to perform an atlas-based segmentation that generated a set of anatomical landmarks for each
of the 10 test patients. The anatomical landmarks were then used to perform the diﬀerent patellar
instability measurements.
The measurements generated were then compared to measurements obtained manually by experi-
mented readers. The results were deemed acceptable if the diﬀerence between the results obtained
and the doctors results did not exceed the standard inter-observer error (standard deviation between
measurements for diﬀerent experimented readers) for the measurement. Some measurements per-
formed better than others, with 4 acceptable results out of 10 (Insall-Salvati and Caton-Deschamps
indexes), while others did not include any acceptable results (lateral trochlear inclination), but none
of the measurements generated the consistent and reliable results we were hoping for.
Key problems with the current materials and methods were identiﬁed. In order to improve the mea-
surements accuracy, some changes should be implemented. Only segmentations issued from atlas
images successfully registered to the input image should be taken into account during the segmenta-
tion process, in order to obtain more accurate anatomical landmarks. The atlas should include more
patients and consist of 3D sequences instead, to account for the variability between patient knees.
The obtention of acceptable results for some patients shows that the method works, to some ex-
tent. We believe that using atlas-based segmentation methods to achieve reliable patellar instability
measurements is possible provided that the improvements mentioned above are implemented.
