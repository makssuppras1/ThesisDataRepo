Abstract
The Danish society is striving towards an energy sector independent of fossil fuels and instead relying on sustainable
energy sources. This transition can only be achieved if the domestic and industrial sectors maximize their energy
eﬃciency. The energy intensive casting industry beneﬁts substantially from being highly energy eﬃcient because
the cost of energy represents a big share of the over all costs. In this work the Danish foundry Frese Metal- &
Staalstoeberi A/S is the subject of an energy mapping and optimization aiming to decrease their overall energy usage.
The complete energy map of the production revealed that the largest power consuming process is the ventilation, here
35 % of the total power is consumed. A power reduction of 491’800 kWh/year is available if the current ventilation
system is replaced, by an eﬃcient ventilator unit with frequency regulation. The pinch analysis showed that the total
space heating demand can be covered, by the combined heat recovery of the exhaust air in the ventilation, and the
excess heat found in the furnace cooling system. A heat recovery unit with an eﬃciency of 85 % will be able to reduce
the heating demand by 748’700 kWh/year. Investment of 4’003’815 DKK into improved equipment must be made in
order to achieve the aforementioned heat and power reduction. The feasibility of the suggested ventilation design
results in a positive Net Present Value of 1’444’841 DKK, an Internal Rate of Return of 11.8 % and a Payback Time
of 8.4 years. A sensitivity analysis of the investment factor, revealed that the project is economically feasible even
through a ± 30% variation of the investment factor. Frese requires an IRR above 10% and a PBT below 5 years,
before an investment can be made. The project meets Frese’s requirements when the investments are reduced by
25%. The second largest power consuming process is the melting in the inductance furnaces. It is observed that the
operation of the furnaces varies substantially from melt to melt. If a more optimal melting procedure is adapted, up
to 880 kWh power can be reduced per melted ton metal. An energy performance indicator can be implemented to
help monitoring and visualizing the importance of eﬃcient melting procedures to the operators of the furnaces.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Denmark has been one of the leading countries within clean energy policy for 25 years [7]. The incident that started
Denmark’s path to a cleaner energy consumption was the ﬁrst oil crisis in 1973, where the war in the Middle east
brought the price of oil to new heights in Western Europe. With high oil prices the Danish policy makers started a
path toward a more self suﬃcient and sustainable energy system [10]. As a part of this journey the electricity and
heat production was being converted to coal and natural gas in stead of oil, for self suﬃciency. In 1985 the energy
eﬃciency was increased in the industrial and domestic sector and in the energy production sector [10]. By 2011 the
energy production surpassed the consumption, and a large part of this energy was produced by sustainable sources,
mainly wind power and biomass. The goal for Denmark is to be independent on fossil fuels by 2050 and increasing
the energy eﬃciency of the industrial sector is an important part of this goal.
In order to increase the energy eﬃciency, projects that look into the end-users of the energy and minimize the energy
use while still performing the same service for the end-users, must be made. Many companies are looking into energy
optimization due to the large economical beneﬁts, which are often available in energy savings. From a political
perspective, energy optimization has also become a more popular topic as the eﬀects of global warming are becoming
increasingly serious. The energy optimization can be used as a tool to inﬂuence the industries, to reduce their carbon
footprint, or by passing legislation which gives economical support to projects that aim to reduce energy consumption.
These support plans, combined with the reduced expenses for energy, makes it attractive for many companies to
optimize their energy consumption.
1.1
The potential for energy savings in the industry
With the ever growing demand for large amounts of energy for industrial and domestic uses, new and more eﬃcient
ways of producing power are being developed. Along side this development an important fact has become clear, that
not only shall the power be produced in a more sustainable way but also the use of the energy must be optimized. As
a part of the energy strategy for 2050 the Danish government have a sub goal to reduce the gross energy consumption
by 2020 with 7% compared to 2010 [7]. This is a step towards the long-therm goal which is for the energy supply
sector to be independent from fossil fuels by 2050 [10]. In order to achieve this goal several energy eﬃciency initiatives
has been set in motion, such as the 2012 Energy Eﬃciency Directive which is established to help the EU reach its
20 % energy eﬃciency target by 2020 [7]. In the 2012 Energy Eﬃciency Directive, large enterprizes obligated to

Chapter 2
Energy Mapping
As society strives towards a greener and more sustainable future, more and more legislation and requirements are
imposed on industries to improve their energy eﬃciency and reduce emissions. It is required that the industrial sector
reduces their gross energy consumption, by 6% compared to 2006 values[10], as a part of the transition to a more
sustainable energy sector. The ﬁrst step to improve the energy consumption is to understand how much, and where
the energy is used. How much energy there is used is not diﬃcult to ﬁnd, every month the energy supplier will inform,
how much energy was used, and how much that costs. But knowing where in the production, how much each process
uses and to what degree that energy is used, that is very diﬀerent. The energy consumption for each process must
be deﬁned in as detailed a way as possible, as the ﬁrst part of a optimizations process. This process is called the
energy mapping. In this chapter the energy mapping process is described along with the methods used to complete
the mapping.
2.1
Methodology of Energy Mapping
In this thesis the approach to the energy study of Frese is based on the methodology used in the ISO 50001:2011
Energy Management system[1]. The ISO 50001:2011 provides guidelines for companies that will improve their energy
performance. This includes increasing energy eﬃciency, reducing energy consumption and reducing energy losses. The
goals for implementing this standard are both to reduce green house gasses, which beneﬁts the environment and to
reduce energy cost for the industry which are consistent with the goals for this thesis. The concept for eﬃcient energy
planning is presented in Fig. 2.1 where a concept diagram shows the energy planing process. The process has three
main phases, listed below:
• Planing inputs
• Energy review
• Planing outputs
The purpose of a having a plan for management of energy use is to insure that the company continuously improves their
energy consumption. By systemizing supervision of the energy consumption, it is more likely that good opportunities
are discovered and that a high energy eﬃciency standard can be maintained. A key step to getting an energy plan
started is to establish and evaluate the energy consumption and energy eﬃciency of the foundry. To establish this an

energy review must be undertaken. The purpose of an energy review, is to identify the energy consumption for all
energy vectors and establish in which areas opportunities for energy optimization are present. Another purpose of

The main planning input for the energy review is, past and present energy consumption. As a part of the energy

To understand why the energy consumption was so heavily relying on electrical power(EP), an inspection of the
production was conducted as a part of the start up of the project mid January 2017. During the introduction of the
foundry it became clear that the main electricity consumer is the inductance furnaces that is used to melt the alloys.

Primary energy ﬂow: EP, NG, LPG, Oil and DH: These energy ﬂows are often seen as inputs into equipment/process
where the energy is used as a signiﬁcant part of the process.
Secondary energy ﬂow: Energy, often in the form of heat left over after a process when the primary energy have
been converted. This energy can be as warm water in a cooling system of an induction coil, where the primary energy
is electricity that melts an alloy. In this case the heated water is a secondary energy ﬂow, this could also be hot excess
gas from a combustion process.
In order to map the secondary energy ﬂows in the production, temperatures and the ﬂows of process streams are the
key properties needed to quantify the potential in these ﬂows. The energy that is available in these secondary ﬂows
are not utilized enough in older production facilities since the awareness and economical willingness to utilize waste
heat have been neglected. The easiest property to determine through experiments/data logging was the temperature
of various streams. A temperature logger (ELMA 718A) was available at Frese that was used to measure and log the
temperature of exhaust gas from the three sand mold dryers. This was used to log the temperature of the exhaust air
of the ventilation system and for the logging of the temperature of the metal in the furnace. The thermocouple used
was unable to log the temperature of the metal in the furnace at high temperatures. It was used until the signal was
lost form the thermocouple which was destroyed in the process. Another thermometer capable of withstanding the
high temperatures of the molten metals, was then used to measure the remaining temperatures before the pouring.
Information on the air volume ﬂows in the ventilation was provided by Jens Jacobsen from Frese who has measured
these ﬂows with wind speed meters inside the ventilation shafts with a Testo 435-4 multifunction instrument.

2.2.2
Inductance furnace
At Frese there are a total of 6 induction furnaces where 3 have a capacity of 1000 kg and 3 have a capacity of 500 kg.
The inductance furnace consists of a large hollow cobber coil, with ceramic lining to protect the coils and to retain
the molten metal until casting. The working principle of the furnace can be explained by the following. The copper
coil surrounding the crucible is connected to a high current AC-source, creating a magnetic ﬁeld forms around the coil.
This magnetic ﬁeld induces eddy currents in the metal in the furnace, the materials electrical resistance to the eddy
currents will generate heat. Another considering factor to the heating is hysteresis which occurs when the magnetic
ﬁeld changes as a result of the frequency. The magnitude of the induced current is dependent on the strength of
the magnetic ﬁeld and the rate of the change in the magnetic ﬁeld. Therefore a high frequency, high current power
source is used to induce the magnetic ﬁeld. The electrical resistance in the metal combined with the high current,
results in heat generation that eventually melts the metal. In Fig. 2.5(a) one of the coils used can be seen while under
maintenance, and a more detailed schematic of an inductance furnace can be seen in Fig. 2.5(b).

Sand cooler
Once the sand is separated from the castings it is brought through a ﬁlter that sort the smallest particles from the
remaining sand. The small sand particles are removed from the production loop and discarded, the larger sand particles
==PAGE:45==
2.2. RESULTS OF THE ENERGY MAPPING
33
are reused in the production until they are broken into small sizes, after which these are also discarded. The sand
reaches high temperatures during production due to the heat transfer from the molten metal and before the sand can
be used in the production, this has to be cooled to bellow 25 ℃. The sand is ﬁrst sent to a out-door silo where the
sand is cooled from free cooling. When production requires sand or the outdoor temperature is not low enough to
support free cooling the sand cooler is used. In Fig. 2.21 a schematic of the sand cooler can be seen. The sand is
cooled by a compressor driven refrigeration cycle with a buﬀer tank. The warm sand enters the cooling chamber
which has a bundle of tubes spaced out inside. Cooling water is ﬂowing through the inside of the tubes, absorbing the
heat from the sand. When the sand reaches the desired temperature a valve opens in the bottom of the chamber and
the sand is transported to a holding silo, before it is reused.

2.3
Discussion
The historical data shows an increase in overall energy consumption except for a small reduction last year. The energy
trend can be linked to the production rates up till 2014, in 2015 and 2016 the production rate has decreased, but the
energy consumption has not followed this trend. In 2014-2016 energy consumption have been more or less constant,
while the production of goods was reduced by 12 % in 2015 and 38% in 2016 compared with the production in 2014.
The source of this increased energy consumption has not been identiﬁed, because the energy logging that has been
kept at Frese which would reveal the distribution of the electrical energy, was stopped in October 2014. The speciﬁc
end-users which have caused this trend has not been identiﬁed, making it diﬃcult to correct this trend, without the
information of the individual power consumers. The logging of the power use must be reinstated, in order to identify
which end-users that have performed ineﬃciently.
From the distribution of the electrical power consumption for previous years reveal that the largest consumers are the
inductance furnaces, the ventilators namely the ITK ventilator and a group of equipment in Hillo hall. The mapping
of the inductance furnace on a historical level shows that the average thermal eﬃciency of the furnaces is decreasing
from 41% in 2011 33% in 2014. This reduction can be a combination of equipment ware and operation of the furnace.
In the mapping of the furnaces operation patterns revealed that the operation procedure for the furnace varies a lot
from batch to batch. Variances in the duration of 8 the sample melting were between 62 and 256 minutes, with just
as varying power inputs to the furnaces. When a melting of 1 ton of ABZ can be completed in 62 - 95 minutes at
thermal eﬃciencies of 72-50 %. At times the melting took 225 - 256 minuets which leads to eﬃciencies of 12 - 14 %.
A change or information in the procedure, can reduce the power consumption of the furnaces. It is possible that in
periods where the productivity is lower, and that this reduce pase of production, results in this more passive procedure.
Where the process is dragged out in longer melts instead of short eﬃcient melts. The radiation and convection losses
from the furnace are not as large a part of the problem initially expected. Is is hard to reduce these losses to below 1
% [13], which mean that the potential power reduction is not as large as initially assumed.
The mapping of the furnaces also revealed a potential for a reduction in space heating by using the generated heat in
the cooling system for space heating. There is also a possibility to produce DH in the summer months where the
heating demand internally is low enough that the excess heat from a technical perspective, can be supplied to the DH
network when the furnaces is in operation. The technical and economically feasibility will be discussed in Chapter 3.
The largest group of electrical end-users is the ventilation systems. It is here that the largest share of the power is
consumed. It is also here that the largest potential for reducing the energy consumption, both the power and heat
consumption. The result of the mapping reveals that the combined power consumption of the ventilation systems is

Challenges of collecting data
In a company as Frese, the main focus is to deliver high quality goods and to keep delivery orders within the promised
dates. The focus on energy consumption and logging of data is not relevant for the daily production. Some of the
challenges of this project was to understand the production site, the processes and the ﬂows through the production.
The site has been around since the 1960’s, much has changed and many new equipment and systems have been
installed. With these new systems, temperature gauges and other devises are installed which allows for gathering of
data. However the data required for an intensive energy study is more comprehensive than what is installed in many
of the systems. Especially the mass and/or volume ﬂows has proven to be a challenge to collect. Which is a very
important variable in the energy calculation. The ﬂows in the cooling system used in the inductance furnaces, was
measured by using an advanced ﬂow meter which could measure the ﬂows of liquids inside piping. For the air ﬂows
in the ventilation system, measurements of the air speed have been done by the staﬀat Frese and these data was
supplied along with the dimensions. Since these air ﬂows are measured in a turbulent air ﬂow, the measurements will
have deviations and will not be the same over the entire canal cross section. This will however be assumed to be a
suitable approximation.

2.3.2
Experiences with energy mapping and analysis
At the beginning of this project, the author’s only previous experience with energy analysis, was through assignments
and project from the courses at DTU. These projects where mainly theoretical, or if based in real systems, the relevant
data was often provided. In this project the ﬁrst challenge was to plan the which steps and in what order the actions in
the project should be. In order to collect the needed data, to map and analyze the energy use, an initial understanding
of the system and the way the diﬀerent parts of the production worked together was needed. This understanding
was continuously expanded through the project, during visits to the site and through interview with operators. An
important experience learned through the project, was how important it is to understand a system, and its boundaries.
The understanding of the system should be the ﬁrst part of the project. Information on parts of the systems that are
hard to asses due to lack of documentation, can be collected from the designers/operators of the system. They are
familiar with the system, and will have information that may be important for the understanding of the system and/or
the way it is operated.
Here is an example of why it is important to understand the system before measurements and data logging is begun.
During the ﬁrst visit on site after the introduction, logging of temperatures in cooling water system, and the power
consumption of the induction furnaces was begun. In the two following visits the logging of the data was continued.
It was at the end of the third visit, that the it became clear that there are two cooling systems, one for the large and
one for the small furnaces. The data that was collected during that period, was a mixture of temperatures from both
systems and could not be used. There was no clear indication on diagrams or labeling, that showed which system the
thermometers were connected to. When the thermometers were initially discovered, did the placement match fairly
well with a PID diagram that was provided from the archives at Frese, see Section 6.1. This PID has not been updated
for many years, but is still the best diagram of the cooling system available. The correct thermometers was located,
after following the pipes around the production, until the system was deﬁned and the new data logging could begin.
When working with insubstantial data, to try to ﬁll the holes in the data, with suitable assumptions and estimates.

Conclusion
Frese have been consuming between 3’466 - 4’567 MWh of energy per year over the last 6 years, the bulk of the
consumed energy is electrical power which is used in the ventilators, induction furnaces and in various cleaning and
production processes. The energy mapping uses energy consumption data from 2013, due to a lack of suﬃcient data
in newer years. The data on electrical consumption is well deﬁned in 2013 along with the consumption of DH, fuel oil
and LPG. The historical trend reveals that the increased productivity from 2011 to 2015 also increased the energy
consumption, but as the productivity was reduced in 2014 and 2015 the energy consumption stayed the same. A
possible explanation for this can be described by the large variation in the operation procedure of the furnaces. When
operated at optimal conditions, was the furnace capable of archiving thermal eﬃciencies in the range 58 - 73 %
while at times the thermal eﬃciency is only 12 - 14 %. Periods with low productivity can lead to a more relaxed or
unfocused production method, which would reduce the eﬃciency of the process and increases the power consumption.
The thermal part of the energy consumption, has stable with the variations following the trend of the productivity. The
switch from NG ﬁred boilers to being connected to the DH network have not increased or reduced the consumption
substantially. There is however identiﬁed potentials to reduce the consumption of DH by implementing heat recovery
on the ventilation systems and by utilizing excess heat from the furnace cooling system. There is also identiﬁed a
potential to reduce power consumption in the ventilation sector, by replacing current equipment, such as the ITK
ventilator with new modern ventilators.

opposite demands for heating or cooling. When multiple process streams are used in a plant, heating and/or cooling

∆Tmin
It is important to have data on the process streams before a pinch analysis can be made, it is also important to deﬁne
a ∆Tmin which is the minimum temperature diﬀerence between two streams. According to the laws of thermodynamic
then heat can only move unaided from a warm to a colder reservoir which requires a positive temperature diﬀerence
(∆Tmin) between the hot and cold stream. It is not feasible to assume a ∆Tmin of 0°C since that would require
an inﬁnitely large heat transfer area. The smaller the ∆Tmin is the more expensive the heat exchangers gets, it is
therefore a balance between the economics of the investment cost and the operation costs of the external energy
sources, an example is shown in Fig. 3.4. In practicality a ∆Tmin of 5 °C is more realistic and accepted value for low
temperature processe[4], and is therefore used in this thesis. Essentially ∆Tmin determines the eﬃciency, actual size
and the price of the heat exchangers.

Composite Curves
The energy recovery can be illustrated graphically with composite curves, which are T-h (temperature - enthalpy
curves). In a composite curve diagram the two sides hot and cold are treated individually. The hot side starts with
the lowest temperature and sets the enthalpy value to 0, since enthalpy is a relative value any other value can be
assumed, but 0 makes sense. Then the CP is combined within each temperature interval, and the change in enthalpy
in each interval is calculated. The hot composite curve (HCC) can be drawn from the changes is enthalpy within each
temperature interval starting at the lowest temperature and at 0 enthalpy. For the cold composite curve (CCC) it
starts by adding the minimum required external cooling to the enthalpy value then the curved is plotted same as
the HCC for an illustration see Fig. 3.5. The section of the graph where the CCC surpasses the HCC represents the
required external heating, and in the opposite end represents the required external cooling, the section in between the
cooling and heating is the potential heat recovery. With increasing ∆Tmin the CCC will be shifted sideways towards
the right which will increase both the required cooling and heating. The pinch point is found where the two composite
curves are closest to each other, the temperature between the two curves is the deﬁned by ∆Tmin.
Instead of using 14’448 kW on cooling and 13’360 kW on heating the external energy consumption can be reduced to
680 kW for cooling and 2’000 kW for heating by utilizing the available energy streams.

3.2.2
Induction furnace
The methodology of the onion diagram shown in Fig. 3.1 states that the optimization of a process must ﬁst be focused
on the core of the energy service. For the induction furnace the service which the power is used for, is to heat and
melt the charge. First the performance of the current system is analysed based on the data gathered and described in
the Section 2.2.2.
Energy service
In the melting process the energy source used is electrical power used in the coil to induce the current in the charge.
The goal of the energy use is to heat the charge until it melts whereafter it is super heated to a unspeciﬁed temperature
before it is poured. For ABZ the temperature before pouring can vary between 1280-1320 °C, the speciﬁc temperature
is determined by the recipe used for the product that is being casted. To challenge the energy service with regards to
lowering the temperature of the melt, is to insure the forms are not completely ﬁlled up. It possible to lower the
pouring temperature if better control of the temperature can be managed. New power controller units which can
provide this control, are available on the market.
The energy used is connected to the mass of the charge. If the size of the charge is larger than what is required then
a lot of energy is waisted by melting metal, only to let it solidify and remelt it again later. Propper control of the
charge size and the required metal is important and can save a lot of energy. In the introduction, the yield factor was
introduced. For 80 % of the production the yield is only between 40-60 %. To illustrate the diﬀerence between the to
extremes of this interval, the diﬀerence means that if the yield is 40 % then 1’152’000 kg of ABZ need to be melted
compared with 780’000 kg if the yield is 60 %, that is 384’000 kg. There is a huge potential for reducing energy
costs by increasing the yield factor. It requires 85’500 kWh to melt 384’000 kg corresponding to 3 % of the total
power consumption in 2013. However it is important that there is poured enough metal into the forms so the ﬁnished
product is of suﬃcient quality. If enough metal is not poured into the forms the product can be ﬂawed and is rejected
and the casting process is required once again. There is a ﬁne balance between the technical process of casting and
eﬃcient energy use, the recommendation is to focus on increasing the yield factor when designing forms, and products
since the author is not experienced in the requirements for casting it is better left to the personal at Frese.
Process and Equipment
Looking at the energy mapping of the melting process in the inductance furnace, four factors that inﬂuence energy
consumption stands out. The ﬁrst factor is the very varying melting time, which is the time it takes to complete a
melt and pouring of 1000 kg of ABZ. The duration ranged from 62 minutes to 256 minutes and the result of this wide
range in the duration, is also visible in the eﬃciencies of the individual melts. The eﬃciencies varies from a low 12 %
to an impressive 73 %, compared with the expected eﬃciency of such furnaces, which ranges from 50 % to 70 % [22].
This showing that the equipment is capable of operating at quite eﬃciently. The goal will be to always operate at an
eﬃciency above 50 %, as it is shown that the equipment can do so in three of the cases. To achieve this, key factors
that causes the high eﬃciency must be identiﬁed. The duration of the melt is known to be a key factor, as the longer
==PAGE:70==

the molten metal is kept at high temperatures, where the heat losses are largest, the more energy is required. When
investigating the impact of the durations, it is clear from Table 2.2 that the three samples with the highest eﬃciency
are those with the lowest melt durations. However it is not only the duration that inﬂuence the eﬃciency, the power
that is used during the melt is obliviously a large factor. From the shape of the power curves in Fig. 2.6, it can be
seen that the power use, in the three best samples, start close to or only shortly at a power consumption is below
200 kW. After this the power is increased to 350 - 500 kW for the remaining time of the melt and then the metal is
poured. The combination of a short and well controlled power use in the furnace, is the key to high eﬃciencies.
Operation
With the optimal operating procedure identiﬁed, then it is interesting to see when the non-optimal procedure are
being used and more important why this less eﬃcient procedure is use. From the power curves shown in Fig. 2.6
three types of melting can be found.
The ﬁrst is called the Hurry and wait therm, which was coined by L.V.Whiting of NRCan’s CANMET,[14]. This is
an expression of the practice where the charge is melted as fast as possible at high power loads, and is then kept
at the high temperatures for a substantial period before it is poured. Of the samples logged at Frese, two can be
identiﬁed as following the hurry and wait process, which are the samples taken on May 16th. These two samples have
the highest average power loads of 414 and 480 kw and a duration of 210 and 225 min respectively. The combination
of very high power loads and long holding times, leads to the bulk of the power being transformed to heat in the coil.
Furthermore, the heat is removed by the cooling system while the hot charge is radiating heat out to the surroundings.
The second type is a Delayed melting, where the furnace is kept at a low power load 150 kW for long periods. At
this power load, the majority of the power is also lost to the cooling of the coil. After the period with low power
load the power curve looks like the optimal melting, but because of the delay a lot of power is put into the furnace
which does not contribute to the melting. Of the samples taken at Frese the one at April 12th and May 10th, both
show this characteristic. However the power curve on the 12th of April can almost be described as a preheating
of the charge. Preheating of the charge is a well known practise in the industry, but it is mainly for safety reasons
to insure that no water is left in the charge, which can lead to an explosion when the water is instantly vaporized.
There is also a beneﬁt to preheating the charge such as shorter melt times in the furnace, and if an excess heat
source available, utilization of this heat will reduce the energy demand in the furnace.[14]. However the beneﬁts of
preheating the charge are often only relevant in from a cost perspective if the gas prices are lower than the electrical
power prices.From 2017, this will not be the case for Frese due to the reduction in the PSO tax. It must also be noted
that preheating is conducted in a separate process, often by using a furnace oﬀgas (if available) or by burning gas,
not in the inductance furnace it self. Adding a preheating process, where the charge must be moved to the furnace
afterwards, also adds a lot of complications which are hard to justify economically or process wise.
The last is the Optimal melting which, as mentioned, is a ramping up of the power load in short time steps and
operating at max high power load but avoid unnecessary holding, and superheating of the charge.
If the delayed melting process, that was used on the 12th of April and the 10th of May, instead used the optimal
melting process the following power reduction can be achieved. On the 10th of May the power load is 50-60 kW
during the ﬁrst 183 minutes of the sample. After the 183 minutes the power is turned up and the melting is begun,
according to the optimal procedure. In these three hours, 159 kWh is used, which essentially only produces warm
water in the cooling coil. If the power load alternative was turned on at the 183 minute mark, a power reduction of
26 % can be achieved, increasing the thermal eﬃciency of the melt from 36 % to 49 %. For the sample taken on the

Good Housekeeping
Another key factor in eﬃcient melting, is to reduce heat the losses that occur when 1000 kg of metal is heated up
to some +1300 °C. The main source of heat loss form the furnace is the large amount of thermal energy that is
generated in the coil and is removed by the cooling water. In the current setup all the thermal energy in the water is
moved to the outdoor air via the air coolers. When none of the heat is used in other process or for space heating, it
can only be classiﬁed as a loss, which reduces the eﬃciency by a substantial amount. The large furnaces produces on
average 60.5 MWh of thermal energy, this is based on the data plotted in Fig. 2.11. The range of the thermal energy
production form the minimum and maximum potential is found to be 46.8 MWh and 104 MWh respectively. If the
thermal energy was instead utilizing in the foundry as space heating. It could reduce the need for external heating in
the form of DH, resulting in a signiﬁcant saving in energy cost could be obtained. The varying heating demand over
the year results in periods where all of the heat cannot be used internally, it will therefore still be necessary to use the
air coolers to insure that the furnaces can operate. The cooling water is not the only loss factor. As determined in
Section 2.2.2, the radiation and convection losses are combined responsible for 4.4 % of the total power input. In the
installation at Frese today, there are no covers on the furnaces and it is well known that in order to operate under the
best praxis in the casting industry the furnace cover on for as much of the time as possible. The fact that there are
no covers makes this very hard to obtain, therefore installation of covers for the furnaces are recommended. With a

Optimization of furnace heat generation
When a process such as the induction furnace generates thermal energy, there are two ways to manage the heat
generated in the coil. The ﬁrst is to remove the heat from the coil so the furnace can operate. Another approach is
to use the heat it in the production. From the pinch analysis the best candidate is to use the heat as space heating in
the production, and thereby reduce the DH consumption. In Fig. 3.13, the total DH consumption is plotted along with
the three levels of expected excess heat production. The high setting of expected excess heat, seem to be unrealistic
to achieve over longer periods. This level of excess heat might occur occasionally but it is not beneﬁcial to have this
much excess heat. If the high setting is ignored, then the two other option can be used, for space heating in the
ceiling fan-coils, radiators or other low temperature heater units. The annual energy reduction in DH is found to be
381 or 476 MWh for the low or mean settings respectively. In the annual heat reduction calculation the low demand
in the summer months is taken into consideration. It can be expected that this would be reduced even further if a
more optimal operation of the furnaces is implemented, but despite a possible reduction in the excess heat production,
there is still an energy reduction available in the range of 31-39 % of the yearly DH use.

temperature from the return lines of the DH network is unlikely during the summer, where the losses in transmission
are the lowest.

Ventilation
It is well known in the metal industry, that a large part of the yearly energy consumption is used in the motors driving
the ventilation systems, which insures a good working environment. Numbers from The Danish Energy Association
[23] show, that 26 % of the power used in the industry, is used for ventilation. An analysis of ventilation in the
general metal industry, reveals that the eﬃciency of the ventilation systems are 55 %. The most dominating losses are
due to incorrect loads (19 %), bad regulation (12 %) and losses in the motors (12 %). The ventilation systems at
Frese consumed 33.9 % combined, of the total power consumption in 2013. The largest single unit at Frese is the
ITK, it alone consumes 22 % of the total power per year. The general energy savings potential within the ventilation
technology across industry sectors is found to be 22.2 % [23]. During the energy mapping process, the potential for
improvements in the ventilation systems was identiﬁed. In this section, that potential is explored along with the eﬀorts
required to actives that potential. Along with suggestions on measures that will improve the general eﬃciency, and
what the economical beneﬁts are to such implementations.
Energy service
The high requirement to the ventilation are set by the authorities to insure proper working condition for the employees,
it is therefore essential that there is a functional ventilation and ﬁlter system. To provide the required ventilation 9
ventilators uses 13 canals to remove old contaminated air and deliver replacement air in order to avoid draft. As
mentioned in the mapping section the total air ﬂow that is vented out from the buildings is 144’500m3/h and 133’000
m3/h is being supplied.
Looking at the overall ventilation system, then it becomes clear that it is a system that is patched together with
equipment from many diﬀerent generations and with diﬀerent philosophies behind the design. Some units like the ITK
is designed to cover three processes in the production, where as units no. 5 to 13 are small units that are installed in
the area where an increased demand was discovered or stricter regulation was implemented. There are pros and cons
for both types of philosophies the smaller decentral units can be shut oﬀwhen there is no demand from that area, but
the smaller units often have a worse eﬃciency compared to larger units and the eﬃciency increases dramatically when
the load share decreases. When there are more units then maintenance is required on each unit which will increase
the operational costs, heat recovery are also not so relevant for smaller units since the investment cost of the unit is
increased substantially when heat exchangers are included in the installation.
The larger central units does not makes maintenance easier since there are fewer units and the larger size of the motor
will often have higher eﬃciencies. However, if the larger system has to cover diﬀerent loads, pressures or changing ﬂow
rates from diﬀerent areas of the production some form of control is required. In older systems as the ITK a control
method of using dampers to control the ﬂow rate and pressure, this form of control does however increases the losses
in the system. A more eﬃcient method uses a frequency conveter to regulate the rotational speed and there by the

3.3
Energy Indicators
Indicators can be used to quantify the variation in energy consumption from year to year and to compare two years
where the production has varied. They can be used to measure the improvements which are being implemented
and to monitor key factors in the production. Indicators are a good tool to use and requires very little work if the
procedures are in place to calculate them. Well selected indicators can reveal a lot of information on the eﬃciency of
production and where to direct the attention in order to ﬁnd new opportunities to improve. There are three types of
indicators that are commonly used. They are; Economical, Energy and Environmental. For an energy optimization
project, these are often connected since they all depend on the energy consumption. This energy project aims to
decrease the energy consumption, it is therefore natural to use the energy consumption as one of the key factors for
the indicator. To calculate performance indicators a measure of the performance of the production is needed. Some
performance indicators often used in foundries are the EP consumption of the furnace per kg of metal melted, tonnes
melted per employ per month, or tonnes shipped goods per total plant employee [14]. As a performance indicator, the
amount of shipped goods is chosen to be the main factor to measure the performance of the foundry. This is also the
preferred factor at Frese today. It is a good choice as it can be connected to the revenues, thereby linking it to the
economics, which it is the actual best indicator of the performance. The shipped goods can also be linked to the
energy consumption, as the required energy consumption increases with the production rate.
It is important to have a balanced amount of indicators and to use those that have been decided. The indicators can
be a great tool to involve and inform employees of their part in the project to a more energy eﬃcient foundry[14].
The indicator have to be designed to the right use, and it is important not to overuse indicators as this can create
an information overload [26]. With speciﬁc indicators designed to help explaining which eﬀects good housekeeping
can achieve in energy savings, and they can be used to communicated the result of the current eﬀorts of the energy
optimization. In the end, a culture can be created where the operators strive to improve the energy eﬃciency in their
daily operations.

Discussion
The pinch analysis revealed that there is a potential to eliminate the need for external heating for space heating.
There will be a demand for heating for domestic use. To reduce the domestic consumption, addition controls must
be implemented on the cooling system, to increase the supply temperature. There where some energy streams that
was excluded from the pinch analysis due the dynamics of the energy ﬂow. The pinch analysis covered the combined
utilization of both heat recovery of the exhaust air and the cooling water. These where also analysed separately out
side of the pinch analysis. If the cooling water alone is utilized for space heating, then it could reduce the yearly
consumption of DH by 31 - 39 %.
The heating demand drops to almost zero, in the summer period. During this period, the possibly to produce and sell
heat to the DH network was investigated. The analysis shows that the required temperature of 20 - 25 °C on the
return from the DH network makes it unfeasible to utilize the excess heat in the DH network.
In the energy mapping a potential for reducing the power consumption of the inductance furnace was identiﬁed, here
in the energy analysis this potential was more thoroughly investigated. The optimal melting procedure was identiﬁed
as a short melting where the power is gradually, over as short a period as possible, increased until the charge is melted
and has reached the desired temperature. When the temperature is obtained, the charge must be poured and the
furnace shut oﬀor reﬁlled. Two other procedures named "Hurry and wait" and "Delayed melting" was observed during
separate visits. These procedures where much less eﬃcient and uses more energy and more time to melt the charge.
The power curves of the "Hurry and wait" and "Delayed melting" was adapted to ﬁt a more optimal operation patten,
which was used to derive a speciﬁc power reduction per ton. A full picture of which procedures are followed, and
how large a share of the total melting follow which procedure, could not be determined, based on the 8 samples. To
uncover the full potential of an improved operation procedure, more data is required.
The control of the circulations pumps used in the furnace cooling system, was investigated and a reduction of 10’500
kWh was identiﬁed. However if it is compared with the other reduction initiatives then this will seem unimportant. It
must be stated that this improvement will also increase the temperature of the water out of the furnace to 80 °C,
which can increase the utility of the excess heat. The water can be used internally to provide hot water for domestic
purposes, that would not have been possible without control of the temperature.
A thorough analysis of the ventilation system was undertaken, and the results show that only one of the current
ventilator units have a SFP that is better than SFPref, this unit is the newest installation in Hillo hall, all the remaining
have a worse SFP. The ITK unit has the highest SFP=9.2 kW/m3/s which could be reduced by 85%, if modern
equipment was used instead.

what components that must be included.

Operation
A new system shall be designed to operate at a more optimal setting and that is able to regulate when the demand
for ventilation is reduced. This can be achieved by having frequency controllers on the motors that drive the fans, a
system design where units are placed in parallel so if the ﬂow rate decreases enough one can be turned oﬀand the
other can handle the remaining ﬂow. This separates itself from a system with multiple single units by the fact that
here the air is still connected to the same stream allowing for heat recovery and ﬁltering.
Equipment
With a new system with highly eﬃcient motors shall be installed with a lower SFP, these motors have a higher
eﬃciency not only in the operation point, but also when the load share is reduced, which will be important if frequency
controllers are installed. Such a system must also have heat recovery installed, here a double pass counter ﬂow heat
exchanger is the best ﬁt, since it provides the highest eﬃciencies and have the least moving parts, compared with
the liquid battery system. A rotary heat exchanger was also investigated due to their high eﬃciencies, and their
good latent properties which improved the air quality inside the foundry. These are however not suited for use in a
production facility, since the canales in the rotary heat exchanger will have air and particles from the exhaust side,
and carry that into the supply stream. This contamination makes the rotary heat exchanger a pour choice and the
contamination can be a breach on regulation in the requirements to the quality of the supply air, it has therefore
been rejected. After the heat recovery and be for the air is supplied it should be heated up to the desired supply
temperature if 21 °C with a support heat exchanger heated by DH. When the DH is used in the ventilation unit, a
lower temperature can be achieved and the employees will not experience draft if the supplied air is preheated. The
radiators that are used to day can be used during the night or weekends, or in periods where the ventilation system in
not in operation, to keep the building warm so that the indoor climate is preserved when the ventilator is turned oﬀ.

Conclusion
The aim of the energy analysis is to investigate and understand the processes that was identiﬁed in the energy
mapping. The chosen processes is the inductance furnace and the cooling system that is needed for the operation
of the furnace along with the ventilation systems. Results from the analysis show that there is a power reduction
potential of up to 491’800 kWh/ year from combining and upgrading the ventilation system to a modern system
with high eﬃciency motors and controls. By implementing heat recovery in that new system can a reduction of up
to 748’700 mWh/year in heat energy. Smaller reductions can also be achieved by implementing frequency control
on the currently uses ITK ventilator, this will reduce the power consumption by 196’77 kWh/ year and the heat by
119’666 kWh/year. By implementing control on the circulation pumps used in the furnace cooling system can a power
reduction of 10’500 kWh be achieved, along with a higher outlet temperature from the furnace, which can be increase
the range of application the cooling water can have internally. Covers for the furnaces during melting, and the eﬀect
that they will have on the power consumption is determined to be a reduction of 22’000 kWh/year. Analysis of the
operation of the furnace revealed that there is a large energy reduction potential to be achieved by changing the
operating procedures of the furnaces use. Reductions of up to 880 kWh/t of electrical power per ton of melted metal
can be gained if some of the most ineﬃcient procedures are changed. Improvements of the operating procedures will
also lead to reduced time used to melt the metals, which can mean that extra products can be produced per day. The
available data on the procedures are limited, and to specify the real potential more data is needed.
There are several initiative that will reduce the energy consumption at Frese, initiative with the highest energy
reduction is the design option 2 for a new ventilation system. The replacement of the ITK ventilator combined with
the regulation of the operations conditions results in the largest power reduction of 17 % per year. In the new design
is the heat recovered in a two pass cross ﬂow heat exchanger, which reduces the need for heating by 73% per year.

4.1
Economic evaluation method
In the this chapter the method used to describe the economical evaluation of the projects in presented along with the
assumptions used in the method and models. When evaluating an investment in a project it is crucial that it will
create more value for the company, compared with other projects or by being in a bank. For some projects this is the
criteria that is evaluated and is a substantial part of the decision of the investors. A project that can illustrate this, is
the change from NG based heating to DH heating. The investment was required to exchange the heat and source had
a projected pay-back time (PBT) of 1.7 years according to the investment foundation. These types of investments
are very attractive for private companies because of the high rate of return on their investment. Private companies
often has a set of requirements to the internal rate of return (IRR) for the project to be over a set limit determined
by the company policy. The practise often depends on the size of the company and how often it is involved with
larger investments. For Frese, the policy is that the IRR must be 10 % or above and the PBT shall be under 5
years. Projects with lower economical indicators can be invested in if they are essential for production. An example
is, if old machinery breaks down, it can be repaired or new equipment can be bought to replace the old. Here the

In order to utilize the excess heat, investments must be made to connect the cooling system, with the hot water
system that connects the heater units in the foundry. It is important that a control system is also installed to insure
that the return temperature of the cooling water is lowered enough to cool the coil, and not to cold so that water
can condense in the power cabinet. In the warm summer months the demand for heating is almost zero, and in this
period, the air cooler will cool the system instead of the heater units. It will be investigated whether or not this
surplus of heat can proﬁtably be used to produce DH and sell it to the distribution company. In order to produce
DH, the supply side of the network needs to maintain a temperature of 60 - 95 °C, and the period where Frese can
produce DH is in the summer months, where a 60 °would be a suﬃcient supply temperature. The high temperatures
of 95 °C are required in the cold periods, due to losses in transmission, which are highest in the winter. If production
of DH should be implemented, another control system for the pumps must be installed, to secure that a more stable
temperature can be delivered out of the coils. The technical limitations to a water temperatures of 80 °C in the
cooling system also further complicates the production of DH, which reduces the period of DH production. It was
determined that having to consistently deliver the required temperatures of 60-80 °C, would make the case unfeasible
due to the variation in the heat generation.
The methodology of the onion in Fig. 3.1 diagram also states that the optimization of a process must ﬁst be focused
on the core of the energy service. For the production of thermal energy in the inductance coils, the focus must
therefore be on reducing the production of thermal energy. This will increase the eﬃciency of the furnace it self, by
optimizing the energy service, then the process, the equipment, the control, the O&M and last but not least the
housekeeping. After the system is fully optimized, then the waste energy is used for other internal processes, space
heating and then in the very end production of DH. The most eﬃcient process for batch melting, is the inductance
furnace which is already used, since it is a ﬂexible and fast to start up when needed. Results from the previous
investigation of the furnace and the equipment itself shows that it can perform as expected when operated properly.
However if an upgrade of the furnaces was preformed, newer and more eﬃcient options are available, since the present
furnaces are from the 1980’s. It is unlikely that such an upgrade would be preformed when the existing equipment are
capable of performing at the top level of the expected range for that type of equipment.

4.6
Conclusion
The aim of the economic analysis is to evaluate the feasibility of the energy reducing initiatives. Some initiatives were
evaluated as a maximum allowable investment based on a assumed life time. For the reduction of heat loss from the
furnace must the yearly investment not exceed 14’000 DKK. Utilization of the excess heat from the cooling system can
support an investment of 665’000 to 836’000 DKK and still be within the required range of Frese’s investment policy.
Economic evaluation of the savings potential connected to the improvements to the operation of the furnace, shows
a savings potential of 105 - 580 DKK/ton. It is unknown how large a share of the melts are melted during which
procedure. To determine the full savings potential more data is required.
The two new design options for ventilation systems are both economical feasible with a positive NPV. None of the
proposed solutions live up to Frese’s strict requirements to investments of an IRR of 10% or higher and a PBT of
under 5 years. Design option 2 come the closest with a IRR of 11.8% and a PBT of 8.4 years. Design option 2 still

the project. Without the support is then the project becomes infeasible. The current energy agreement from which

Chapter 5
Conclusion
The danish energy sector must by 2050 become independent from fossil fuels. To accomplish this goal the danish
industry must be as energy eﬃcient as possible. The metal and steel foundry named Frese Metal- & Staalstoeberi
A/S is located in Slagelse, Denmark. Casting is an energy intensive process and Frese is looking to optimize its energy
consumption. In this project a complete energy mapping of Frese is made and used to identify energy intensive sectors
in the production. These are then analysed an optimized using i.a. a onion diagram to optimize the energy service at
its core and pinch analysis to maximize heat recovery from excess heat.
Before an energy optimization can be performed, the energy ﬂows in the production was mapped by using the ISO
50001:2011 Energy Management system[1], and the large energy consuming processed identiﬁed. From this the
complete mapping of the energy ﬂows shows that the main power consumption are being used in the ventilation
systems and not in the inductance furnaces, where Frese are earning its revenues. It is seen from the mapping of
the inductance furnaces that the procedures regarding the melting process are varying or not being followed. Three
types of melting procedures are being observed during the mapping process, two of which are very ineﬃcient and
should be stopped. These procedures used large amounts of power over very long periods. It is recommenced that
new procedures are implemented so that the current ineﬃcient use is ended, which will result in reduction of power
but also in the duration of the melts. To support the process of improving the operation of the furnaces, investments
can be made in control software and new power controls to support the new procedures. By improving the procedures,
reductions of 160 - 880 kWh per ton melted metal, which can reﬂect into a reduction of 105-580 DKK/t are expected.
Further improvements to the inductance can be made to reduce the heat losses during operation of the furnace. The
heat losses can be reduced by covering the top of the crucible with a cover made from a refractory material. If the
covers ﬁts closely enough then such an implementation will reduce the heat losses by 3.4 % of the total power input
into the furnace, 22’000 kWh per year. Investment cost for the covers must not surpass 14’000 DKK per year since
this is the break even point for the investments. The covers will also reduce the time it takes to melt the charge. The
circulation pumps used in the furnace cooling system, can me regulated to reduce the power consumption by 10’500
kWh per year. This regulation also increases the ﬂexibility of the excess heat, by increasing the temperature outlet of
the water. The excess heat generated in the inductance furnace has the potential to cover the entire demand for
space heating, which is 727’000 kWh per year. If this excess heat is used for space heating, then a tax must be paid
equal to 0.183 DKK/kWh of process heat that is used for space heating, resulting in a reduced cost for heating of
113’000 DKK per year. The mapping of the ventilation, shows that the largest part of the power is consumed in the
ITK ventilator, which have the worst SFP of all the ventilator units. The ITK uses 85 % more power than a modern
unit with the same requirements. There is a huge potential to reduce the heat demand by using heat recovery on the
93
==PAGE:106==

CHAPTER 5. CONCLUSION
supply and exhaust ﬂows. Explicitly there is potential for recovering 727’000 kWh of heat in the ventilation, which is
not subjected the energy tax. The heat reduction will reduce the cost of DH by 300’978 DKK per year.
Based on the available energy reduction potentials in the ventilation sector, two new design options were suggested.
A simple feasibility study was made for each of the solutions, which showed that both suggestions where economical
feasible. Only design option 2 lived up to the requirement of an IRR above 10 % but neither of the suggestions met
the limit of a PBT under 5 years. Both design options were analysed in a sensitivity analysis where the investment cost
factor was varied ±30 %. Design option 2 is despite an increase of +30 % still feasible and is still recommenced as a
good investment, with an iRR of 11.8 %, NPV of 1’445’000 DKK and a PBT of 8.4 years, at the normal investment
factor.
