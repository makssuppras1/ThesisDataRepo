Abstract
This thesis introduces methods for early detection of voltage instability on DC mi-
crogrids. Through the wide deployment of PVs, batteries, electric cars, and power-
electronic-connected resources, DC microgrids present advantages with respect to
their AC counterparts in terms of efficiency, simplicity to control, and potentially
costs. For remote off-grid locations, it has been shown that the DC microgrid is a
more cost-effective solution than the AC. The methods introduced in this thesis can
also be easily extended to any DC grids, including High Voltage DC grids.
Early detection of voltage instability in DC microgrids is particularly interesting
as it helps avoid unnecessary load shedding, allow for better use of the power reserves
in case of emergency, and reduce costs related to power interruptions and availability
of power reserves.
This thesis introduces two different approaches for the early detection of voltage
instability.
The first is using a centralized monitoring system requiring real-time
communication links with sources. The developed method achieves high theoretical
accuracy and fast computation times. In practice, however, the time delay from the
communication required, the risk of missing communication links, and the non-perfect
measurement devices can limit the applicability of this technique.
In order to overcome these, a second approach is implemented for fully decentral-
ized voltage instability detection. As the detection is performed locally at the different
generators, this second approach is grid size independent. To predict voltage insta-
bilities based on early measurements, machine learning classification functions are
employed and compared in order to determine the most appropriate method. Based
on this comparison, the most promising method is further adapted in order to op-
timally fit the DC microgrid voltage instability problem and improve the detection
accuracy. Further enhancements to the method are proposed to eliminate the number
of misclassifications. The introduced approach achieves high detection accuracy, with
only local measurements, and at a very fast computation time.
The thesis concludes with the discussion of the positive impacts this early voltage
instability detection can have on the DC microgrid operation.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Context
Microgrids are envisioned to be introduced at large scale in the future mainly in
order to simplify the integration of distributed generation and storage systems. In
particular, direct current (DC) instead of alternative current (AC) present attrac-
tive features when it comes to microgrids. However, the DC microgrid technology
is not perfectly mature yet and requires improvement notably for the control and
data exchanged within the grid. Due to the high penetration of unpredictable renew-
able energy sources in such grids, the use of early voltage instability detections is
particularly important in order to react rapidly to disturbances, operate safely and
economically.
Consequently, two different early voltage instability detection techniques are im-
plemented to predict voltage levels after a disturbance. The first technique developed
is a voltage analytical prediction, that is model-based, introduced due to its the-
oretical accuracy. The second is a voltage data-driven prediction that relies on a
pre-established data-base.
Its main advantage is that it does not require central
computation, thus real-time communication links are no longer required for that pur-
pose. As a consistent data-base creation can computationally become an issue with
microgrids, a computation reduction technique is proposed in order to cope with
large data-base and/or large grids. For the data-driven prediction, Matlab machine
learning classification methods are compared in order to identify the most suitable in
instability detection based on early measurements.
An adapted version of the most suitable classification method is then implemented
in order to improve the overall efficiency in instability detection. Finally, the different
implications of the early voltage instability detections conclude the thesis.
1.2
Thesis structure
This thesis is structured as follow. First of all, Chapter 2 is dedicated to a short
literature review introducing the different concepts studied. Chapter 3 details the
different motivation of studying this subject.
Afterwards the methodology of the
thesis is divided in three distinct chapters. Firstly, Chapter 4 introduces the different
control methods implemented in order to ensure microgrids stable operation points.

CHAPTER 2
Literature review
A short literature review introduces and describes briefly the different concepts tack-
led throughout this thesis. It is divided in two parts, one dedicated to the microgrid
concept; and the other to the control theory employed.
2.1
Microgrid
There exists lots of definitions in the literature about microgrids such as in [1] or [2]
among others. The microgrids can generally be defined as a network of low voltage
able to manage supply and demand. They are generally composed with generators,
lines, loads and storage systems in order to handle the consumption and the produc-
tion of unpredictable renewable energy sources. Two different operating modes can
be utilized : grid connected mode (if there exists a point of common coupling with
the utility grid) or islanded mode (acting autonomously).
Microgrids are projected to be one of the key in the energy transition from a
conventional power system to a smart grid [2] due to numerous benefits for the con-
ventional utility grid, users and environment.
With constantly reduced prices for
distributed generation [3], more and more distributed generators are expected to be
connected at the distribution network. One of the main targets in the study of cou-
pling microgrids with conventional power system is to achieve an easier integration of
this distributed generation [2] and mitigate renewable fluctuations by using the flex-
ibility of battery storage systems [4]. Furthermore, by using distributed generators,
losses through the grid are reduced since the energy is produced and consumed locally.
In addition, a higher penetration of non-polluting distributed generators would reduce
the CO2 emissions caused by conventional fossil-fueled power plants [5]. Moreover,
the microgrids can also provide ancillary services to the main grid such as mentioned
in [6] and [7]. They present an improved power reliability and quality as they can
still operate in case of utility grid blackout. Due to the huge economical implications
related to blackouts as listed in [8], this reliability improvement is non-negligible.
The listed advantages are non-exhaustive and summarize the main benefits noted
in the literature. All these benefits have raised the interest of researchers all over the
world for a couple of years and numerable studies have been conducted in this field
in order to render it a viable technology.

2.1.1
AC or DC microgrid
Nowadays, most of the microgrid projects are operating with AC [9] instead of DC
since the AC technology is commonly used in distribution grids for its ease to change
voltage levels and a more mature protection technology [10].
However, the DC
presents serious advantages compared to AC for microgrids applications such as listed
here after.
• The emergence of DC loads such as electrical vehicles (EVs), battery energy stor-
age systems (BESS), fuel cells,... etc and DC generators, mostly photovoltaic
panels (PVs), is rendering the choice of a DC network more and more inter-
esting [11]. By using DC, lots of the network appliances could get rid of their
previously used less efficient AC/DC rectifiers and instead use a DC/DC con-
verter reducing the losses [10] and [12]. In fact, except some rotating machines
(washing machines, fans...), most the electrical devices present in households
could run on DC and consequently increase their overall efficiency.
• The control of DC network is easier since it only depends on the voltage while
in AC the frequency and voltage has to be handled simultaneously [11]. Fur-
thermore, there is no need for synchronization with the main grid as the grid is
electrically decoupled with converters. These converters also act as a firewall,
not allowing disturbances from the AC utility grid to spread to the DC network
and vice-versa.
• In remote areas with weak or nonexistent utility grid, the efficiency of DC over
AC is obvious thanks to its higher efficiency and an easier control [13] and [14].
As a reminder, it is interesting to mention that more than 1 billion people are
still living without a safe and reliable access to electricity.
Although the advantages are evident, the technology is not perfectly mature and
requires improvements in protections schemes [10], costs (investment cost announced
to 2.2$/W in [15] which starts to be viable) and control.

2.1.2.1
First level
This first level of control is distributed meaning that it operates solely excluding real-
time communication links with neighbors and/or a central controller. It is presented in
the literature as a droop controller correcting the current generation based on voltage
changes at the generator. From an AC conventional power system point of view, it
can be compared to the primary frequency control. Practically, the droop controller
has the ability to stabilize the voltage of the system after a change in consumption
or production but cannot restore it to its initial value. Therefore a secondary control
is required in order to restore the grid voltage to 1 [pu] after a disturbance.

2.1.2.2
Second level
The steady-state voltage drift is removed by using the second level of control. This
second level of control ensures that the system is operating at the required operation
set points established by the Microgrid Central Controller (MGCC). While the first
level of control does not require communication links, the second is using it in order
to ensure the energy dispatching in the microgrid.
2.1.2.3
Third level
The last level of control is managing the relation with the utility grid by communi-
cating with the distribution system operator (DSO) and market operator (MO). It is
controlling the power flow through the microgrid and the ancillary services provided
to the utility grid.
This report is focusing on the first and second level of control by limiting the
communication required at the second level of control.
2.2
Control methods
Nowadays, there exists two different control methods which are model-based con-
trol (MBC) and data-driven control (DDC). Introduced at the beginning of the 19th
century, the model-based control is the oldest control technique and has been stud-
ied thoroughly up to now. On the contrary, the data-driven control appeared more
recently in order to overcome several drawbacks of the MBC.
2.2.1
Model-based control
Model-based control is the most popular theory used in order to control a system.
The main idea being to use a model describing the dynamics of a true system in
order to implement a controller [18] and [19]. The efficiency of the controller relies
mainly in the accuracy of the model with respect to the reality.
As example, a
commonly used model-based control design for simple systems is based on the state-
space representation of linear systems [20].
There exists two main methods to determine the system model : first principles or
identification. The first principles method determines the dynamic equations ruling
the true system and implements them in order to model the system. The identification
method uses approximation of the system by applying probability techniques such as
variance of error from the identified model to the true system.
However, the models are not perfectly modeling a whole system since some un-
modeled dynamics or noise can persist leading to more or less significant inaccuracies
between the model and the true system. A citation of Georges E.P. Box expresses
this fact appropriately [21]: ”Essentially, all models are wrong but some are useful”.
==PAGE:21==
2.2 Control methods
7
Despite these uncertainties, techniques such as robust control method have been de-
veloped in order to consider them in the model leading to a sufficient accuracy for
different applications. Moreover, techniques have been developed in order to quan-
tify the robustness of MBC with for instance Bode and Nyquist diagrams in linear
systems [20].
Nowadays most of the systems are controlled using MBC techniques with a rel-
atively good efficiency, though its limits appear in large systems. As a matter of
fact, the control of larger systems where MBC becomes more challenging due to the
difficulty of modeling the true system. The inherent complexity of large systems leads
the research to a new control technique that is not model dependent.
2.2.2
Data-driven control
The data-driven control is different from the model-based by not attempting to de-
scribe and model the system but considering it as a black-box. Based on measure-
ments from the true system, control actions can be taken thanks to a data-base relying
on previous events and/or pre-established scenarios.
The data-driven control has been introduced in several articles with different def-
initions such as in [18] or in [22] which is formulated such as :
”Data-driven control is the control theory and method, in which the controller is
designed merely using online or offline I/O data of the controlled system or using
knowledge from the data processing without explicitly or implicitly using information
from the mathematical model of the controlled process, and whose stability, conver-
gence, and robustness can be guaranteed by rigorous analysis under certain reasonable
assumptions.”
The strength of data-driven control is to get rid of any complicated system models
implementation. This control theory is therefore very suitable for large system appli-
cations in which the dynamics cannot be expressed easily. In such systems, the control
efficiency and robustness relies mainly on the data-base management techniques used
in order to control the true system.

CHAPTER 3
Motivation
3.1
Motivation for an early detection of voltage
instability
With microgrids, several reasons are leading to an early detection of voltage instabil-
ity.
1. Grid services : As it is the case in AC power systems economics [23], the
control actions (primary and secondary) provided by the generators are services
ensuring the grid’s normal operations. It means that the generator owners need
to be remunerated accordingly since the action of services results in changing
their production levels to a non-economical set.
Therefore, an optimal use
of those services is required in order to operate economically which can be
achieved by predicting the voltage levels and consequently the associated need
for primary and secondary control. For the microgrids studied in this thesis,
the droop controller is constantly enabled to follow the consumption while the
secondary control use has to be optimized.
2. Grid stability : Deviations from the base voltage are concerning for the system
stability when they are large i.e. over or under the stability limit chosen between
0.95 and 1.05 [pu].
Buses remaining at extreme voltages could damage the
several electrical appliances connected. Knowing this, an early prediction could
estimate the voltage expected at the new equilibrium point after a disturbance
and decide whether forward actions have to be taken preventively. An early
detection would therefore allow early actions that limit the voltages encountered
beyond the stability limits.
3. Load shedding : In case of large disturbances and the incapacity for the gen-
erators to provide the required power in order to circumvent the disturbance
an anticipative and limited load shedding can be effectuated. This protective
actions is taken in order to avoid a total blackout of the system.
4. Control actions : Detecting a voltage instability early could limit the resources
required in order to restore the system electrical levels.

As explained, the prediction of voltage is an issue that has to be coped with and in
order to achieve this prediction, two different approaches are studied : an analytical
which is model-based and a data-driven.
3.2
Motivation for a model-based and data-driven
prediction
Though the model-based control appears to be the easiest and most efficient theoret-
ically, in practice some unmodeled dynamics, due to the assumptions, noise,... etc,
can render this method less efficient than expected. Moreover, the numerous commu-
nications links are an issue in microgrids. Limiting the amount of data exchanged
between the different stakeholders of a microgrid is necessary.
By using a distributed data-driven prediction instead of the model-based, real-time
communication links would no longer be needed with a central controller in order to
predict the system stability. These characteristics make the data-driven prediction
applicable on larger grids without significant changes in computation time since the
predictions are calculated locally.

Modeling of power flow
First of all, the Laplacian admittance matrix is formed in order to compute the power
flow inside the network using Kirchhoff’s laws. The grid is composed of three element
types : sources, loads and lines. Nodes are numbered and sorted in order to have
firstly the generators and then the loads. Moreover, the generator convention is used
for the sources and loads. Therefore, the current flowing from the sources is positive
at any time and the load current is, on the contrary, negative.
In addition, two
assumptions are made on both systems.
• The lines have a resistive behavior only which is a reasonable assumption for
small distribution lines [25].
• The generators are fully controllable and no upper bound is imposed on the
power production level. The focus of this thesis is mostly on the early voltage
prediction algorithms and less on the different implications of power production
limits or uncertainty.

CHAPTER 6
Data-driven voltage
instability detection
The data-driven voltage instability detection presents three main benefits compared
to the analytical voltage detection.
1. It eliminates the need for real-time communication links between generators
and the MGCC used in order to compute the voltage levels reaches. It is worth
mentioning though that the generators are not fully decoupled from the MGCC
and the remaining communication links are utilized for other purposes such as
production scheduling or power dispatching for instance.
2. Computations are distributed among the different generators’ internal comput-
ers rendering the prediction grid size independent and can therefore be applied
on larger grids without significant changes in the computation time of the volt-
age prediction.
3. As the generators are acting solely, the loss of prediction at one bus could be
counterbalanced by other generators.
It can be mentioned that the data-driven approach is not detecting instability
exactly like with the analytical approach.
In fact, while the analytical approach
gives a numerical value for the several bus voltages, the data-driven approach is
instead indicating with a binary variable whether a simulation is predicted to be
unstable or stable based on early measurements. This binary variable is then used to
state whether control actions are required or not. The data-driven voltage instability
detection focuses on constant power loads since it is the most challenging with the
meshed microgrid.
The next paragraphs are dedicated to the creation of the data-base and then the
determination of most appropriate prediction method in order to improve it after-
wards. The test is carried out on the meshed microgrid Figure 4.1.

6.1
Definitions
The definition of the different concepts utilized in the following sections are intro-
duced.
1. Scenario : Simulation where a disturbance occurs leading to an overvoltage or
undervoltage situation.
2. Undervoltage scenario : Scenario experiencing voltage values below 1 [pu]
due to the sudden outage of one generator while the system was at a stable
voltage.
3. Overvoltage scenario : Scenario experiencing voltage values above 1 [pu] due
to a load tripped from the grid when the system was at an equilibrium point.
4. Predictors : Voltage derivative and the current injected at one bus as soon as a
disturbance is noticed for one scenario. These variables are early measurements
saved and utilized afterwards in order to detect possible voltage instabilities of
one scenario.
5. Class : Binary variable indicating whether a scenario is stable (all voltages
within 0.95 and 1.05 [pu] all the time) or unstable (at least one voltage bus
over 1.05 [pu] for overvoltage scenarios and under 0.95 [pu] for undervoltage
scenarios). This class is associated to the predictors indicating whether, with
those predictors, a scenario is stable or not.
6. Observation : Voltage derivative and current injected at one bus as soon as a
disturbance is noticed for a new scenario tested.
7. Data-base : Matrix of predictors and their associated class utilized to detect
instability with new observations.
8. Voltage at misclassification : Most extreme voltage encountered after a
disturbance among the different buses of the microgrid.

6.2.2
Number of scenarios
The number of scenarios is chosen at 500 since it benefits a number predictors suffi-
cient in order to predict relatively accurately new observations. The different classifi-
cation methods are thus comparable. Less scenarios would lead to uncertainties due
to the fewer number of predictors present in the data-base. On the contrary, more
scenario does not change the prediction of the different methods.
6.3
Data management
The number of scenarios and/or the size of the system can become computationally
challenging to handle.
Therefore in order to generate a consistent data-base in a
reduced amount of time a specific data-base creation method is proposed.
As a matter of fact, the only variables needed in order to create the data-base
are the predictors and the class. With the classical method, (4.4) is solved and the
predictors are extracted after the disturbance occurrence. Then in order to obtain the
class, the dynamics equation (4.4) can be solved on a time interval guaranteeing the
system to have reached the new equilibrium point. By comparing the most extreme
voltage attained with the stability limit, the class is found. The solution of such a
problem generates more data and computation than what is actually needed in order
to derive the data-base.
The proposed method calculates the predictors such as with the classical ODE.
However, in order to estimate the class the constant power algorithm presented in
section 5.1.3 is utilized to calculate the stable voltages at the new equilibrium point.
==PAGE:41==

The most extreme value is then compared to the voltage limit in order to obtain the
class. This proposed method has the benefit to avoid unnecessary calculations of the
transitory period from the disturbance to the new equilibrium point.

generate the data-base. For instance, a data base of 100 undervoltage scenarios takes

6.4.5
Comparison test results
The classification methods are compared in order to determine the most appropriate
in predicting instability. The decision criteria are decided to be based on the accuracy
in prediction and the time to estimate the class, since the system has to react quickly
to disturbances. In practice, it can be seen that the probability density function of
the voltage at misclassification is distributed along a gaussian curve. The accuracy
criterion is thus based on three variables : the percentage of misclassification, mean
value of the voltage at misclassification and standard deviation from this mean value.
Those variables are chosen since they are sufficient to characterize the voltage at
misclassification probability density function.
In order to have a representative panel of voltage at misclassification, the estima-
tion of the different classification methods are tested on 1000 load scenarios presenting
random loads such as presented in 6.2. The number of scenarios is chosen relatively

6.6
Probability distribution
In order to determine the probability distributions P(xi|Ck), a data-base has to be
created with a large number of predictors related to each class in order to have a
representative function of the actual distribution.
The loads are chosen such as the data-base was created in section 6.2 for over-
voltage and undervoltage scenarios. Equal number of stable and unstable scenarios
is required in order to have as much predictors for one class as for the other.
A data-base of 5000 undervoltage and overvoltage scenarios is created in order
to have a good approximation of the probability distribution. Once the probability
distribution built based on the predictors collected, a linear interpolation allows a
continuous probability calculation. Moreover with random loads, a smaller data-base
could lead to uncertainties in the probabilistic curves due to a lack of information for
different predictors. As those probability distributions are used as deciding functions
in equation (6.7), they should approximate the true curve as accurately as possible.
A larger data-base of 15,000 scenarios has also been tested but without significant
improvements.

7.4
Economical aspects of the voltage prediction
The benefits of the voltage prediction lie in the energy saved by optimizing the sec-
ondary control use. In a microgrid not implemented with a voltage prediction, the
integral control is constantly enabled in order to avoid large voltage deviations from
the base voltage. The action of such controller requires energy while the system may
not become unstable after a disturbance. Therefore, the energy utilized by the in-
tegral control could have been saved if the microgrid had been implemented with a
voltage prediction.
One can argue that, even though the voltage is stable at 0.96 [pu] for instance,
it cannot remain at that value.
It is assumed that every 5 minutes, the MGCC
commands the different generators to dispatch their power production in order to
meet the demand and restore the system voltage to 1 [pu] in an economicall optimal
way. It acts as a tertiary control in conventional power systems [23] and therefore
handle the possible remaining steady-state voltage deviation from the base voltage
at less cost.

Implications of the misclassified scenarios
As presented up to now there is no distinction between the different misclassified

techniques implemented. Some energy is then lost in the non-required action of
the secondary control.

detecting the instability, their integral control action will limit the voltage level
such as presented in 7.2.1. However when none generator is predicting accu-

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
This thesis introduces methods for early detection of voltage instability on DC micro-
grids.
To deal with disturbances, primary and secondary control have been implemented
on the presented dc microgrids. Analytical model-based techniques have been devel-
oped to predict the voltage level at the post-fault equilibrium. This thesis demon-
strated that such methods can achieve 99.99 % accuracy and very fast computation
times. However, practical constraints inherent to the normal operations for measure-
ments and communications can lead to measurements errors, missing communication
links, and noise. Such uncertainties limit the applicability of such techniques in real-
life.
Moreover, the analytical prediction computation time is grid-size dependent
meaning that larger grids would require more time to detect instability and therefore
rendering this method less attractive.
Later in the thesis, a data-driven approaches have been introduced. The advantage
of a data-driven approach is that it is based on local measurements, eliminating
the need for additional communication.
Four different machine learning methods,
chosen from Matlab toolboxes, were used for the voltage instability detection, in order
to determine the method that led to the best classification results at a minimum
time. Out of these methods, the Naive Bayes approach appeared to be the most
efficient. A proposed Naive Bayes predictive technique has then been developed in
order to improve the overall efficiency by using the true probability distribution of
the predictors. The probability distribution was created based on a data-base of 5000
scenarios. Fast computation times for the database creation were achieved thanks to
an analytical formulation introduced in this thesis, which offered up to 18.16 times
faster computations.
To eliminate the classification error, the proposed Naive Bayes method has been
extended with a second measurement. The results show that there is only 0.1% prob-
ability that no generator will detect an unstable overvoltage scenario, while there is
0.0% probability that no generator will detect an unstable undervoltage scenario. In
case of the non-detected overvoltage, the maximum value reached at the post-fault
equilibrium is 1.0513 [pu], violating the stability boundary by only 0.0013 [pu]. Al-
though the need for the second prediction increases the computation time by at least
200ms, the gain in prediction accuracy justifies the time delay. Thanks to the different
improvements of the Naive Bayes method, it has been demonstrated that fully decen-
tralized early detection of instability can be implemented in DC microgrids instead
