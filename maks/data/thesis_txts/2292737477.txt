A B S T R A C T
This is a theoretical tight binding study of the effects sublattice asymmetric doping in
nanostructured graphene. We investigate how nanostructure affects the properties ear-
lier shown for sublattice asymmetrically doped graphene sheets. In particular we look at
graphene nanoribbons with different edge geometry, junctions between sublattice prefer-
ence domains and circular disorder regions embedded in undoped graphene nanoribbons.
We discover that the stability of band gap predicted for asymmetrically doped graphene
relies heavily on the edge structure as it only appears for ribbons with armchair edges and
not for zigzag ribbons. This is explained through the use of effective crystal models as well
as the position dependence of the response of disorder in graphene nanoribbons. Zigzag
ribbons show big peaks in the density of states for low energies otherwise predicted to have
a band gap, allowing a weak scattered current to leak through near edges, and it poses a
potential problem for experiments looking for the predicted band gap.
At domain boundaries where the preferred sublattice is swapped, we predict the formation
of channel along the boundary with high conductance where the surrounding graphene
has a predicted band gap for the same energies. This possibly allows for the construction
of a highly protected 1-d channel. Lastly we also show a lensing effect from the circu-
lar conﬁned disorder which also may have future application for controlling the path of
electrons.

1.1
motivation
In 1965 Gordon E. Moore made a prediction that the number of transistors in dense inte-
grated circuits would double approximately every year [3]. So far the trends holds mostly
true but some obvious physical limits are getting near, where the transistor size cannot be
reduced any further.[4]. In order to keep making faster electronics a new material is needed.
Graphene is a 2 dimensional material which consists of a hexagonal carbon lattice, which
can be split into two triagular sublattices. Since the conﬁrmation of its existence [5] it has
been heralded as a possible new candidate for faster electronic devices. This is especially
because of high electron mobility as well as great thermal and mechanical properties [6].
However pristine graphene, as a semi-metal, has no bandgap. This lack of a bandgap gives
logic transistors made from graphene a low on-off ratio. The search for a way to circumvent
this has given birth to the ﬁeld of bandgap engineering where the goal is to open a bandgap
without losing too much mobility, (usually there is a trade off between the two [6]). Many
methods have been tried so far including, e. g. nanoconstriction using ribbons [7], stacking
of monolayers[8, 9] and mounting on a substrate [10, 11, 12]. Doping the graphene by sub-
stituting some of the carbon atoms with similar sized atoms, such as nitrogen or boron,
has also been investigated, and it has been found that a periodic placemnet of the dopants
leads to the opening of a bandgap[13] but that randomly distributed dopants does not lead
to such a badgap[14]. Recently it was discovered that in certain situations, [15, 16, 1] dop-
ing of graphene is not completely uniform. In fact it can break one of the most important
symmetries of graphene, the sublattice symmetry. This breaks with otherwise reasonable
assumption that such doping would be completely random especially since the two sub-
lattices are completely equivalent. In spite of this, the sublattice segregation in chemically
vapor deposited graphene has been seen to be over 90% with sublattice domains stretching
more than 100nm, see [1] and ﬁgure 1. As a possible explanation of this, it has been shown
that Friedel oscillations[17] in graphene have strong sublattice asymmetry[18], lowering the
total energy of sublattice distributions. The discovery of this effect has given rise to theoret-
ical study of asymmetrically doped graphene. One such study[2] show that this asymmetry
gives rise to the opening of the elusive bandgap, see ﬁgure 2. However, these results are
made for ideal graphene, which is an inﬁnite streching 2-d material. Real graphene sam-
ples for CVD grown graphene[19] typically have grain boundaries where graphene grown
from different nucleation points and with different orientations meet[20]. Also as stated
before the sublattice segregation domain sizes are of the order of at most 100nm. Small

1.2
goal of the thesis
The goal of this thesis is to investigate the interplay between the nanostructuring of graphene
and sublattice asymmetric disorder and look at the experimental consequences that may
arise. Also we want to look for device possibilities arising from nanostructuring. To ac-
complish this we look at the transmission through nanoribbons with sublattice asymmet-
ric doping using a tight binding model, and look at discrepancies compared to the ideal
graphene case. In order to explain the mechanisms we also look at the density of states
both locally and averaged over the device and we introduce some simple effective models.

2.1
tight binding
In general the tight binding or LCAO1 method is an approximation to solid state structures
in linear combinations of the atomic orbitals. This means that the state spectrum becomes
discrete and that enables us to make a matrix representation much more easily in this
basis of these atomic orbitals. In general, each orbital then has an on-site energy associated
with it and every pair of orbitals has matrix element to describe the overlap between the
orbitals. This in turn is closely related to the probability of an electron "hopping" from one
orbital to the other. Because of this the matrix element is often called the hopping integral
or hopping element. In our case we consider only a single atomic orbital per atom, and
for graphene this would typically be the π-orbital. Furthermore we only consider nearest
neighbor interaction, meaning that all hopping elements between atoms which are not
immediate neighbors will be zero. An illustration of this is shown in ﬁgure 3, with black
lines representing the connections different from zero. If we use the numbering in the

2.2
green’s functions
When calculating the electronic properties it is often very effective to use Green’s functions,
and so an introduction into the subject is in order. A very thorough introduction on the
subject can be found in many advanced solid state text books e. g. [23, 24], but for this
introduction the greatest inspiration has been a set of course notes made for course 33442

differential equations. The equation we want to solve is the Shrödinger equation (SE):

∂tψ(t)

2.4
effective crystal models
We want to examine the mechanisms behind the behavior caused by the asymmetric dop-
ing. In order to do this, we compare to two effective crystal models. There is two parameters
these models depend on. The site speciﬁc average concentration of the dopant, ci, and the
potential increase on the site caused by the dopant, ∆.
2.4.1
Virtual crystal approximation
The virtual crystal model, or virtual crystal approximation, is the simplest effective model
for impurities we can imagine. It only includes the average change in potential. I. e. an
extra onsite energy of ci∆is added to any already existing onsite energy. In its purest form
it allows for a system that has impurities to be modeled as perfect periodic crystal with
slightly different onsite energies. Our version will be sublattice dependent meaning that
the concentration used will depend both on the asymmetry and concentration. This model
is very simplistic and does not take any scattering caused by the impurities into account.
Because of this we also include a slightly more inclusive approximation, namely the CPA.
2.4.2
CPA - Coherent Potential Approximation
The CPA was ﬁrst published in 1967 by Paul Soven [30] to model substitutional disorderd
alloys. Similar to the virtual crystal approximation it is an effective medium model that
gives a uniform medium. It is a self-consistent method to calculate an effective Green’s
function that assumes that the local scattering by every site is averaging to zero, which

As the nature of graphene will play a large role in this thesis, an overview of its properties
is in order. A general review of the electronic properties can be found in [32].
3.1
crystal structure
Graphene is a 2-d hexagonal or "honeycomb" crystal made of carbon atoms. A carbon
atom has 4 valence electrons distributed in 1 s and 3 p orbitals. When bonding to form
graphene, the s orbital hybridizes with two of the p orbitals to form what is known as sp-2
hybridized carbon. This form of carbon has the three hybridized bonds lying in the plane
where they form strong covalent bonds between the carbon atoms called σ-bonds, using
three of the four valance electrons per atom. The last valence electron is in the last p orbital,
the pz orbital, perpendicular to the plane. The pz orbital has a node in the graphene plane
and thus does not mix with the other three orbitals. Furthermore the three electrons in
the hybridized orbitals are more strongly bound and thus have a lower energy than the
electron in the pz orbital. The electrons in the pz orbitals are called the π-electrons and
occupy the states near the Fermi energy, and are thus responsible for most of the transport
in graphene.

Length and width
In ﬁgure 12 a zigzag ribbon is depicted together with indications of L and W. Whilst the
directions of W and L stay the same for armchair and zigzag the differences lies in the
different layouts of the unit chains. In general L will be the number of unit chains and each
individual chain will consist of 2W atoms. Figure 13 shows the two different chain types.
In order to get the full armchair description the chains needs to go from one edge to the
other and back again, whilst the zigzag periodicity is captured using a chain that only goes
from one edge to the other. Note that with this naming convention there is going to be a
total of 2 · L · W atoms in the device region. In the armchair it is going to be 2L atoms long
and W atoms wide, and in the zigzag L atoms long and 2W atoms wide. This discrepancy

4.4.2
Extra parameters
In order to properly describe the different heterostructures, we introduce two new param-
eters, boundary width and radius. The radius is only relevant for systems with circular
disorder where the radius is just the radius of the circle that contains the disorder. The
boundary is introduced every time two domains with different asymmetry meet, and con-
tains a linear interpolation of the asymmetry over the width of the boundary. It is important
to know that the radius is chosen in units of the lattice constant, whereas the boundaries
are indicated as number of atoms in the case for the splits and in units of the lattice con-
stant for the circular region disorder. This was done because of the way the boundary was
deﬁned in the different cases. For the split cases the boundary was deﬁned using the site-
indices of the atoms, whereas for the circular disorder we used the coordinates. Deﬁning
them this way also allows for easy comparison between the boundary and the width and
length in case of the split systems and between the boundary and the radius in case of the
circular disorder.

still run enough different disorder conﬁgurations to get representative averaged conduc-
tances and DOS for the particular system. However since the scaling of the runtime with
length of the device along the transport direction is different from the scaling of the width
across, with them being respectively L and W3, it is beneﬁcial for the systems to be long
and narrow, i.e. a ribbon type system.
5.1
choosing the system size
The ﬁrst thing we needed to do was chose a system size suitable to see the effect of the
asymmetricity. However to make sure that exactly this effect can be differentiated from
effects of the intrinsic nanostructure of the ribbon, such as interference between left and
right lead and the band gap intrinsic to armchair ribbons, and to ensure that the length
and width were representative of as many systems as possible, we initially investigated the
system with a ﬁxed concentration and asymmetry and scaled the length and width. The
length was investigated by looking at the transmission for a number of different energies,
E = −0.25, −0.15, −0.05, 0.05, 0.15, 0.25, and varying the length from L = 50 to L = 1000.
The width was the most limiting factor and was basically chosen as a trade off between
speed and size, i. e. as large as possible where we could run the results we wanted within
a reasonable time frame, and also at the same time we wanted to ensure metalicity of the
armchair ribbons. In the end we settled on two sets of length and width. The ﬁrst had
W = 50 and L = 1000 as the width and the length respectively and was mainly used
for the concentration sweeps of the standard ribbons since it was sufﬁciently wide to see
mostly gap effects and still efﬁcient enough to run many energy points and many different
conﬁgurations of disorder. For the situations where we did not need many energy points
or many different conﬁgurations of disorder, we used L = 500 as the length of the system
and a width of WZ = 100 and WA = 101 for zigzag and armchair respectively. Doubling
the width and halving the length enabled us to keep almost (the armchair width is not
exactly doubled) the same number of atoms in both sets.

5.3.5
Single site scattering.
The position dependence of disorder in graphene nanoribbons is a topic which has been
studied in the literature both using tight binding [40] and from ﬁrst principles[41, 42, 43].
In particular the zigzag ribbons have a strong position dependence in the scattering seen
in the conductance and the density of states. To see this effect we checked the conductance
and density of states of a zigzag nanoribbon with W = 50 and L = 1 with disorder placed
in one of the 6 sites nearest the edge, see ﬁgure 28. We see that only the even sites, which
are the sites the share sublattice with the edge we are looking at, give a response in the
conductance and the DOS for the energy range we look at. For the 3 positions that gives
a response we see that a dip in the transmission corresponds to an increase in the average
density of states at the same energy. We also see that the response in the LDOS is not
evenly distributed, but localized near the dopant and with a larger response on the same
sublattice, i. e. every other atom. From this we can infer that for the zigzag ribbon, the
behavior including the lack of a band gap is down to the interaction between two effects.
The effect of the average sublattice asymmetric potential quenches the DOS as we see for
the VCA, where as local disorder near the edge gives rise to low energy localized states,
that lets a highly scattered current leak through the device. For completion the equivalent
graphs are shown for the armchair ribbon in ﬁgure 29. There we see no extra dips in the
conduction or density of state in the energy range we are looking at.
5.3.6
Experimental consequences.
Since the results by Lherbier et al. indicated that graphene with asymmetric nitrogen dop-
ing should have a band gap[2], and experiments have shown that such asymmetric doping
actually occur [15, 16, 1] , one would expect to be able to detect this band gap or the trans-
port gap experimentally as well. However, the graphene in question is grown using chemi-
cal vapor deopsition (CVD) [19, 6] and such methods usually have multiple nucleation sites
resulting in grain boundaries[20, 6], where graphene ﬂakes with different lattice orientations
meet. Such grain boundaries can act similarly to edges for nanoribbons, and we should
expect similar features depending on the geometry of the grain boundary, including the
ability of zigzag edges to prevent the formation of a band gap. Thus experimental efforts to
ﬁnd such a gap may be hampered by the presence of grain boundaries. Alternatively dop-
ing of non-CVD grown graphene e. g. with hydrogen adatoms [44], or single crystalline
graphene [45] could get around this problem.

7
C O N C L U S I O N
We have investigated graphene nanostructures sublattice asymmetrically doped with ran-
domly placed generic weak scatterers using a nearest neighbor tight binding model. It was
found that armchair nanoribbons have a transport- and band gap form gradually with in-
creasing sublattice asymmetry, similar to the results shown for graphene by Lherbier et al.
[2]. At the same time the scaling of the band gap with concentration was found to be similar
to the Egap ∝C0.75 found for graphene sheets. We tested two effective models, the VCA
and CPA, and found that the density of states was well explained by the CPA, which only
includes the simple scattering events and the average change in potential, which means
that the appearance of the density of states can likely be explained as an interaction be-
tween these two effects.
For zigzag nanoribbons we found that both the density of states and the transmission
behaves quite differently from that of the armchair nanoribbons and graphene sheets. The
zigzag nanoribbon develop no band gap with sublattice asymmetrical doping, but expe-
rience very quenched conductance. The investigation with the VCA and CPA, found that
CPA still recreated the same behavior as the tight binding results in the zigzag ribbons,
indicating that it is the same simple effects that caused the band gap for the armchair that
now interacts with the zigzag geometry to give this different behavior. We showed that the
different zigzag DOS and transmission signatures could be explained from the position
dependence of single disorders, which gave rise to an increase density of states near one of
the zigzag edges, which also allows for a weak highly scattered current to leak through the
ribbon. This difference between armchair and zigzag edges can have large consequences
for the stability of the band gap experimentally, as CVD grown graphene often has grain
boundaries where zigzag edges commonly occur.
We also looked at junctions between sublattice domains, where the preferred sublattice
changes from one to the other, and found the formation of a highly conducting channel
along the boundary that can serve as experimental indicator that can be observed using
STM, or even used as narrow channel surrounded by an insulator in the energy range
where the band gap occurs in the bulk graphene. It was shown that the stability of the
channel depends both on the edges and on the geometry of the interface between the do-
mains. Especially it was shown that for zigzag ribbons parallel to the domain boundaries
the alignment between dopants and edge, which could be broken into two parts with either
dopants on both edges or dopants on neither, had a huge impact on stability of the channel.
67
==PAGE:82==
68
conclusion
Finally we looked at the effect of a circular region with sublattice asymmetrical doping
within clean graphene. It was shown that such regions scatter the current around them
and could thus be used for focusing the electronic beam. The focusing effect of the zigzag
ribbons was smaller than that of the armchair since the leakage through the region was
larger, consistent with the different conduction properties we found in the ﬁrst part of the
thesis.
==PAGE:83==
8
O U T L O O K
In spite of the edge instability of the band gap feature that has been shown, it seems there
is still a lot of potential for device application. The use of the heterostructures as different
kinds of electronic wave guides has already been discussed. This also includes the lens-
ing effect we saw. Also this study focused on the gap energy range when looking at the
local properties, but interesting local behavior could be found be examining the conduct-
ing energy ranges. Junctions in the asymmetric doping domains can be thought of as mass
junctions and they may have more properties similar to those of p-n junctions. Furthermore
since the sublattice or pseudospin symmetry is broken there might also be future applica-
tions within pseudospintronics as different device regions can have different pseudospin
polarizations associated with them.
It may also be worth considering different doping types. Here we only considered weak
scatterers, but other disorder types such as magnetic impurities or hydrogen adatoms may
have additional effects that arise from sublattice asymmetry. In particular the hydrogen
adatoms are interesting, both because they have already been observed to be breaking the
sublattice symmetry [44] and because hydrogen adatoms are less ﬁxed than substitutional
nitrogen, and might allow for in situ alterations to the local impurity distribution.
