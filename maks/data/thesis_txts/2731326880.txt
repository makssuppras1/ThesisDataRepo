Abstract
Proteases make up one of the most important groups of industrial enzymes, as they have
applications in a broad variety of industrial fields, such as textile, detergent, food, among others.
However, they are difficult products, due to their proteolytic activity.
Thus, there is great
interest in exploring high protease production yields in microbial cell factories. Filamentous
fungi are advantageous cell factories for proteins, as they are natural high producers of a large
variety of extracellular enzymes. In this project, protease-deficient strains of the model fungus
Aspergillus nidulans was employed as host strains for the heterologous production of the serine
protease, ProtA, from Aspergillus niger. The aim was to successfully introduce and produce
the heterologous protease, in order to explore whether a protease-deficient background would
aid in achieving efficient production levels. This was executed by two different gene editing
approaches: CRISPR-Cas9-mediated gene targeting in NHEJ-deficient strains was used for
target integration of the protA gene into the genome. Additionally, transformation in an NHEJ-
proficient strain facilitated random, multi-copy integration. The production of extracellular
protease in transformants was assessed by protease assays. Therefore, an applicable skim milk
assay was developed, with the intent to ease detection of proteolytic activity and make it possible
to screen for high protease-producing strains. Unfortunately, confirmation of ProtA production
on the skim milk assays was not obtained for either of the transformant strains. Therefore,
further investigation of the protease production in the obtained transformants is necessary, in
order to determine whether or not the protease-deficient A. nidulans strains are suitable host
strains for heterologous ProtA production.

1
Introduction
Proteases are enzymes that cleave the peptide bonds of amino acid chains by hydrolysis. It is
a vast and diverse group of enzymes, that are not only essential for the maintenance of life,
but are also used in a variety of industries, including food and pharmaceuticals [26]. In fact,
they make up a very large part of industrially important enzymes, as approximately 60% of all
commercial enzymes are proteases [4]. However, proteases prove to be difficult products, due
to their proteolytic activity, and thus there are challenges associated with production. This
project will explore whether a protease-deficient background will be helpful in achieving an
efficient production of a heterologous protease, namely ProtA from Aspergillus niger, using the
filamentous fungi Aspergillus nidulans as a host organism.
1.1
Filamentous Fungi as production hosts for heterologous proteins
Filamentous fungi have been used as sources of metabolites and enzymes for centuries. In the
last few decades, molecular genetic tools and efficient gene targeting techniques have made
it possible to improve the protein production, and filamentous fungi are consequently widely
exploited as cell factories for both homologous and heterologous enzymes [1] [35].
Filamentous fungi have several advantages over other microbial cell factories in terms of protein
production. First of all, they are natural secretors of a large variety of proteins, mainly enzymes,
and often in abundant quantities. The high production of extracellular enzymes makes for an
advantageous down stream process, which makes them ideal for industrial protein production [4].
Furthermore, they facilitate post-translational modifications, such as the formation of disulfinde
bonds, glycosylation, methylation, and phosphyrylation, which aid in efficient protein folding
and may affect the stability and catalytic activity of some enzymes [1] [46]. These abilities
makes them advantageous host organisms for complex eukaryotic proteins. Secreted proteins
are synthesized with a signal peptide, that guide the protein to the endoplasmic reticulum from
where they are transported through the secretory pathway. The secretory pathway is also where
post-translational modifications occur. Finally, filamentous fungi are able to grow on relatively
cheap media, resulting in inexpensive cultivation [22].
One of the main fungal hosts used for the production of enzymes are Aspergillus spp. Many
Aspergillus-derived enzymes have been applied in industrial areas, such as food and feed, biofuels
and biochemicals [22]. Aspergillus spp. are effective decomposers of organic substrates due
7
==PAGE:9==
to their high production and secretion of extracellular enzymes [33].
The most frequently
used industrial species are Aspergillus niger and Aspergillus oryzae, which are widely used in
fermentation for production of a variety of enzymes in the food industry, among other things [23].
A. niger is considered an industrial workhorse, and much progress has been made for protein
production using A. niger.
Even though filamentous fungi, including Aspergillus spp., are considered attractive host sys-
tems for heterologous production, the yield of heterologous protein are often low. For example,
some studies have reported production levels of heterologous enzyme that are 400 times lower
than homologous enzyme in A. niger [22]. This can, among other things, be due to low stabil-
ity of heterologous mRNA, misfolding of heterologous protein, or due to degradation by native
extracellular proteases [18]. In terms of extracellular proteolytic activity, heterologous proteins
are more often degraded than native proteins [46]. Improved fungal strains for the production
of secreted proteins can be obtained by rational strain design, and the development of protease-
deficient host strains have proved successful in yielding higher heterologous protein production,
in some cases [35] [46].
1.1.1
Aspergillus nidulans
Within the Aspergillus genus, Aspergillus nidulans has played a significant role as a model
organism for genetics and cell biology studies. A. nidulans has a well-characterized sexual cycle,
in addition to an asexual cycle, a trait which makes this species stand out from many other
Apsergilli. It has been studied extensively, leading to a significantly advanced understanding
of eukaryotic cellular physiology, metabolic regulation, cell cycle control, DNA repair, among
many other things [9] [2].
It has a haploid genome, simple nutritional requirements, rapid growth rates, and is usually
harmless to humans.
A combination of these traits, along with well-developed genetic and
molecular techniques, contribute to its role as a well-established model organism [34].
The
fungus has also been used as a versatile cell factory and is a prolific source of many natural
products. However, it has not been as widely focused on for protein production, as the industrial
Apsergilli counterparts.
The sequence of the A. nidulans genome was made available in 2005, which gave rise to the
development of improved gene-targeting techniques shortly thereafter. This was, among other
things, assisted by the identification of the nkuA gene, a gene involved in DNA repair by non-
homologous end joining (NHEJ), which will be further elaborated on in section 1.4 [34] [28] [2].

1.1.2
A. nidulans growth and life cycle
The vegetative growth of A. nidulans consists of filamentous, multi-nucleated cells called hy-
phae, which grow through apical extensions and branch sub-apically, accompanied by repeated
mitoses [34].

The A. nidulans life cycle comprise both an asexual and a sexual cycle, as seen in figure 1.
Thus, the fungus can proliferate through production of asexual mitotic spores (conidia) and
sexual meiotic spores (ascospores), which can both germinate, produce hyphae, and create a
new mature colony [45].

==PAGE:11==
In asexual reproduction (presented by orange arrows in figure 1), hyphae form asexual repro-
ductive structures, called conidiophores, that produce conidia. Conidia are uni-nucleate spores
that germinate under nutritive conditions. In the germination process, the number of nuclei
increases, and the cell is separated into compartments and starts branching, resulting in the
formation of hyphae, thus completing the asexual cycle. Hyphae or hyphal branches can also
collide and fuse together, through a process called anastomosis.
This can be the beginning of the sexual cycle. In sexual reproduction (presented by pink arrows
in figure 1), hyphae coil together to form sexual fruiting bodies, called cleistothecia. Hyphae
fuse to create ascogenous hyphae, which are bi-nucleate. The nuclei undergo karyogamy, which
is followed by meiosis and two mitotic divisions to produce eight ascospores, contained within
asci in the cleistothecium. Ascospores germinate under nutritive conditions to produce hyphae,
thus completing the sexual life cycle [41] [34].
Anastomosis can also result in a heterokaryotic fungi. Heterokaryons are produced when ge-
netically different strains fuse together and make hyphae containing variable mixtures of nuclei
with different genotypes. There are no mechanism for maintaining consistent ratios of the two
different types of nuclei, and therefore heterokaryons are intrinsically unstable.
In the lab,
heterokaryons are also produced during transformation [34].
1.2
Microbial proteases
Proteases are a class of enzymes that catalyze the cleavage of peptide bonds between amino
acids by hydrolysis. It is a highly complex group of enzymes, which are present in all living
organisms, and they carry out a large variety of physiological and metabolic functions trough
structural and functional diversity [26] [42] [15]. They are also referred to as peptidases or
proteolytic enzymes [5].
Many proteases are produced as zymogens, which is an inactive form of the enzyme. Some
zymogens are activated by external factors, where a change in the environment induce a con-
formational change, or they may be activated by binding of a specific substrate to produce
the active conformation. Zymogen activation may also involve cleavage of a pro-peptide. A
pro-peptide, or activation segment, is a sequence ranging in size from 2 to 100 amino acid
residues, which is fused with the protease and prevents proteolytic activity until it is cleaved.
The most common pro-peptides are N-terminal sequences that sterically block the active site
10
==PAGE:12==
of the enzyme [21]. Apart from the role in hindering proteolytic activity, pro-peptides are also
often important for the folding, stability and sorting of the precursor protease [17] [5].
Filamentous fungi are known to produce and secrete a number of enzymes, including proteases,
as means of interacting with their environment. Extracellular proteases are involved mainly in
the hydrolysis of large peptides into smaller units that are able to be absorbed by the cell [15].
Moreover, filamentous fungi produce intracellular proteases, which are involved in different
critical functions. Fungal non-secreted proteases play a key role in protein turnover, maintaining
a balance between protein synthesis and protein degradation, and also take part in recycling
of other peptides. They are also involved in other intracellular processes, including protein
maturation, signal peptide processing, apoptosis and reproduction, to name a few [27] [21].
Different mechanisms regulating the production and secretion of proteases in filamentous fungi
have been descried.
The presence of substrate can induce protease production, while high
levels of amino acids, NH +
4 , or easily metabolizable carbon sources may repress production [4].
Correspondingly, production of extracellular proteases may increase when insufficient levels of
carbon, nitrogen or sulfur are present. Extracellular enzymes may also be secreted continuously
at low levels regardless of the substrates available [10] [15].
1.2.1
Classification of proteases
Proteases differ in properties, such as substrate specificity, nature of their active sites, catalytic
mechanism, optimum pH, optimum temperature, and stability profile. Thus, they are classified
according to various sets criteria [47].
When the cleavage site of the peptide chain is considered, proteases are divided into two ma-
jor groups, endopeptidases and exopeptidases. Endopeptidases act in the inner region of the
polypeptide chain, distant from the termini, resulting in the liberation of shorter peptide chains.
Exopeptidases, on the other hand, cleave the peptide bonds near the termini of the polypeptide
chains, resulting in the release of free amino acids (See figure 2). They can liberate either a
single amino acid residue, a dipeptide or a tripeptide. Dependent on whether they act at the
N- or C terminus, exopeptidases can be classified as either aminopeptidases or carboxypepti-
dases [4] [24] [36].
Based on the composition of their active site, proteases are further classified into four major
subgroups: serine proteases, aspartic proteases, cysteine proteases, and metalloproteases [36].
Serine proteases are characterized by having a serine group in their active site. They contain a
11
==PAGE:13==
Figure 2: Exopeptidases and endopeptidases. Proteases are divided into two major subgroups dependent on
the position of the cleavage site in the polypeptide chain. Endopeptidases act in the internal part of the peptide
and release peptide fragments. Exopeptidases act near the temini and release free amino acids. Reference: [43]
catalytic triad, which is formed by a serine, a histidine and an aspartic residue. Serine proteases
are numerously found across viruses, bacteria, and eukaryotes, suggesting that they are vital
for the organisms. They have a broad substrate specificity, and have applications in a number
of various industries [4] [12]. Aspartic proteases are endopeptidases that depend on aspartic
acid residues for their catalytic activity, and are commonly known as acidic proteases [36]. The
cysteine proteases are a rare group of proteases, and none have been found in filamentous fungi,
so far. The activity of cysteine proteases depend on a catalytic dyad consisting of cysteine and
histidine [4]. Metalloproteases are the most diverse group of proteases, and include enzymes
from a variety of origins . They need a metal ion for their activity [36].
Furthermore, proteases can also be classified by their catalytic pH optima: acidic (pH between
2.0 to 6.0), neutral (pH 6.0-8.0) and alkaline (pH 8.0-13.0) [4].
1.2.2
Industrial relevance of microbial proteases
Proteases are one of the largest group of industrial enzyme, and account for approximately
60% of the total enzyme sale in the world [40]. Due to their wide specificity and multifaceted
application, they are used extensively in a variety of industrial fields [4] [42]. Two thirds of the
proteases produced commercially have microbial origin [15].
In the food industry, proteases have a large range of applications, as they are used in processes
such as brewing, cereal mashing, cheese manufactering, and in the production of protein hy-
12
==PAGE:14==
drolysates [20]. Protein hydrolysates with high nutritional value can be used for blood pressure
regulation, for infant food formulas, and for therapeutic dietary products. The hydrolytic qual-
ity of proteases is also exploited for hydrolysis of gelatin, casein and whey, as well as meat
protein recovery and meat tenderizing, and the alkaline and neutral proteases of fungal origin
play an important role in the processing of soy sauce and other soy products [4].
One of the most predominant applications of industrial enzymes is their use as detergent addi-
tives, and proteases in laundry detergent account for approximately 25% of the total worldwide
sales of enzymes. This is an example of an industrial field, in which enzymes have provided
a more environmentally friendly alternative to very chemical-intensive processes. Another in-
stance of this is the leather industry, which have introduced alkaline proteases into the leather
treatment process, achieving a more sustainable production and manufacturing [42]. Hence,
proteases can be viewed as an alternative to many chemicals involved in various biochemical
processes [37] [5].
In addition, proteases are also used in the pharmaceutical industry, where they play an impor-
tant role in various pharmaceutical drug formations [37] [20], as well as having a range of other
applications in other fields, such as biofilm removal, processing of keratin wastes, pest control,
degumming of silk and peptide synthesis [42] [40] [37] [21].
In proteomics, proteins are enzymatically digested into smaller peptides, which are better suited
for sequencing with tandem mass spectrometry (MS/MS). The digestion is typically performed
using a sequence-specific protease, that cleaves proteins after or before specific amino acids.
Trypsin is the most used protease in proteomics, typically yielding relatively short peptides,
due to its specific cleavage after the basic amino acids arginine and lysine [39]. However, not all
tryptic peptides are easily analyzed by MS. For example, in proteins that are either very rich
or very sparse in arginine and lysine residues, the peptides can become either too short or too
long to be identified by standard MS, and therefore alternative proteases are of interest [39].
1.2.3
Aspergillus niger protease ProtA
ProtA is an extracellular protease produced by Aspergillus niger [7]. The enzyme belongs to the
serine peptidase family S28, and has a catalytic triad consisting of Ser-His-Asp at its active site.
It has an acidic pH optimum showing highest activity around pH 4.0. It is a proline-specific
endoprotease, which catalyzes the hydrolysis of peptide bonds between proline and any other
given amino acid, both in peptides and in large proteins [26] [16] [48].
13
==PAGE:15==
In the coding sequence for the ProtA gene, a putative signal peptide sequence can be identi-
fied, corresponding to the extracellular location. Using the SignalP-6.0 online service [6] for
prediction of signal peptides predicts a signal peptide cleavage site in the sequence. A study
of a protease with high sequence homology to protA, conducted by Edens et al. (2005) [7],
observed that the amino terminus of the mature protein was located approximately 20 amino
acids downstream of the putative cleavage site of the signal sequence. This suggests that the
enzyme is synthesized as a precursor containing a pro-peptide.
ProtA might have industrial potential, since peptide bonds involving proline are notoriously
difficult to cleave [7]. The enzyme might be of interest in the field of proteomics, where it
can allow efficient digestion of proline-rich proteins, or can be used in cases that require acidic
conditions, such as disulfide bond mapping [39].
1.3
Introduction to experimental methods
1.3.1
USER cloning
USER (Uracil-Specific Excision Reagent) Cloning is a PCR-based cloning technique that al-
lows seamless assembly of two or more DNA fragments, using PCR-generated complementary
overhangs, in a one-step reaction. The generation of the overhangs is based on the excision of
an uracil nucleotide. The uracil nucleotides are introduced in the set of primers, from which
the DNA fragments are amplified, thus creating fragments that are extended to include a single
uracil base in the 3’ end region. When treated with USER enzyme mixture, the uracil is excised
and the fragment lying upstream from the cleavage site is broken off, thus creating a 3’ single
strand overhang of optimally ∼8-12 nt [32] [38].
In this project, USER cloning was used to construct a plasmid containing the DNA fragment,
which would be used for transformation and gene targeting (Integration fragment). PCR prod-
ucts with complementary overhangs were generated for the gene of interest, a promoter and
a terminator, which were all assembled into a plasmid backbone containing a cassette for as-
sembly. The plasmid backbone was enzymatically digested by PacI and Nt.BbvCI to generate
suitable overhangs. Thus, the integration plasmid was constructed with a single USER fusion
reaction. The USER cloning strategy can be seen in figure 3.

1.3.3
Homologous Recombination and Gene targeting
Homologous recombination (HR) is a DNA repair mechanism that relies on a homologous se-
quence as the template for repair. Gene targeting involving integration of transforming DNA
sequences into the genome by HR is an extremely useful technique [28]. It is traditionally per-
formed using linear DNA as substrate, as linear DNA mimics that of a double stranded break
occurrence, and repair proteins are thus recruited to facilitate integration [31]. In eukaryotes,
there are two pathways that efficiently repair double stranded breaks in DNA: Homologous Re-
combination (HR) and Non-Homologous End Joining (NHEJ). While HR requires a template for
repair of the strands, NHEJ involves direct ligation of the broken ends [44]. In gene targeting,
the transforming DNA is provided as a repair template by including long homologous sequences
corresponding to the target locus in the genome. However, linear DNA not only recruits HR
repair proteins, but may also recruit NHEJ repair proteins, resulting in random integration in
the genome. In Aspergilli, as with most filamentous fungi, NHEJ is the dominant repair mech-
anism, and for wild-type strains gene targeting efficiencies are therefore low, as the majority
of transforming sequences integrate heterologously. In addition, integration into multiple sites
often occurs [28].
The length of homologous DNA sequences required to obtain useful frequencies of HR-directed
integration vary depending on the fungal strains and the genetic engineering strategy. In this
project, sequences of 1 kb were used, referring homology to the chromosomal sequences upstream
and downstream of the desired integration site in the genome.
When choosing a target integration site for heterologous protein production, it is important to
select a locus, where consistent high transcription is expected. Consequently, it is necessary
to chose a site in the chromosome, where there is an open chromatin structure. Therefore,
integration sites are often selected based on close vicinity to continuously transcribed essential
genes.
1.3.4
CRISPR-Cas9
Clustered regularly interspaced short palindromic repeats (CRISPR) and CRISPR associated
protein 9 (Cas9) is a bacterial immune mechanism that has been engineered into a powerful gene
editing tool, which has revolutionized biotechnology. The genetic engineering system relies on
only two components, the Cas9 nuclease and a single chimeric guide RNA (sgRNA), to introduce
DNA double stranded breaks (DSBs) at specific genomic locations, which can be exploited for
very precise and efficient gene targeting [31].
16
==PAGE:18==
The CRISPR-Cas9 technology is based on the recognition of 20 bp target sequence in the
genome. The sgRNA contains a 20 bp recognition sequence, called the protospacer, which binds
to the complementary strand of the sequence in the genome. Cas9 binds to and is guided by the
sgRNA, which enables the nuclease to accurately recognize these target sequences and introduce
a precise DSB within them. Cas9 can be easily engineered to target different DNA sequences
by constructing and expressing genes encoding novel sgRNAs harboring protospacers designed
to be compatible with desired target sites within the genome. The only limitation is that a
Protospacer Adjacent Motif (PAM) sequence needs to be present at the target site, downstream
from the binding site of the protospacer. This is a short sequence of three nucleotides, and
the motif is dependent on the specific nuclease. For Cas9, the PAM sequence is the commonly
occurring NGG motif [31].
Figure 4: CRISPR-Cas9 system. The CRISPR-Cas system introduces a specific DSB at a desired site in the
genome. sgRNA guide the Cas9 nuclease by binding of the protospacer to the target site. Error-free repair by
NHEJ will result in continuous generation of DSBs until a mutation is introduced. The HR repair pathway can
be exploited for gene targeting by providing donor DNA that serves as a repair template. This way, a sequence
of interest can be integrated into the specific site in the genome.
The CRISPR-Cas9 system has been implemented in Aspergilli [30]. As previously described,
the two repair mechanisms for DSBs in Aspergilli are NHEJ and HR. If a DSB is not repaired,
the fungus will die. The NHEJ pathway repairs breaks by recruitment of the proteins Ku70
17
==PAGE:19==
and Ku80 to protect the DNA ends, as well as the Lig4 protein complex to ligate them back
together [44]. With a Cas9 nuclease present to induce a DSB, this repair event will happen
continuously until a mutation arise in the target sequence, preventing Cas9 to recognize and
cleave the DNA (See figure 4). Deletion of the nkuA gene, encoding a Ku70 subunit, obstructs
the NHEJ pathway, making the HR pathway the only viable repair mechanism in case of a
DSB, which can be exploited for gene targeting. Thus, the CRISPR-Cas9 system can introduce
mutations into a specific site in NHEJ-proficient strains, while it causes cell death in NHEJ-
deficient strains. However, by providing DNA that serves as repair template for HR, efficient
gene targeting can be achieved in NHEJ-deficient strains [30].
The CRISPR-Cas9 system is implemented by transforming the fungus with a CRISPR-vector
containing a gene encoding Cas9 and a gene encoding the desired sgRNA.
1.3.5
Skim milk assay
A way to identify proteolytic activity is by using a skim milk assay or casein assay. Casein, a
large macromolecule, is one of the major proteins found in milk [50]. The protein is relatively rich
in hydrophobic amino acids, especially proline [7]. In a skim milk assay, milk agar plates are used
to demonstrate the degradation of casein by extracellular proteases produced by microorganisms
present on the plate. The media contains skim milk powder, which acts as the casein source,
and which gives the media opacity and a whitish color. Following inoculation and growth of a
microbial culture on the plates, organisms secreting proteases will exhibit a zone of proteolysis,
which is presented by a transparent zone around the growth.
1.4
Project aims and Experimental strategy
The aim of this project is to successfully introduce and produce the heterologous protease
ProtA in different strains of A. nidulans, in order to explore the potential of protease-deficient
A. nidulans strains as efficient hosts for heterologous protease production. Furthermore, in
order to ease the assessment of protease-producing strains, an additional objective consists in
the development of an applicable protease assay.
The experimental approach consisted of the following steps: First of all, to correctly assemble
the construct for transformation. Secondly, to obtain successful transformants. Next, to develop
a sufficient protease assay for screening of proteolytic activity secreted by fungal strains, and
finally to verify and asses the production of heterologous product.
18
==PAGE:20==
With the interest of exploring ProtA production, different A. nidulans host strains with exciting
strain backgrounds for good production were used. Two different approaches for integration
and production of heterologous protA were implemented. For targeted gene integration, NHEJ-
deficient strains NID2531 and NID2788 were used. Additionally, the NHEJ-proficient strain
NID2908 was used, in order to explore the possibility of obtaining a high-producing strain by
means of random integration of multiple copies of the gene into the genome.
A schematic
overview can be seen figure 6.
The following is a short assessment of the host strain backgrounds. The NID2531 strain was
used as a reference strain in the gene targeting experiments. Prior to this study, this strain had
been engineered for gene targeting with the following genetic changes: Firstly, a disruption of
veA1, a gene involved in sexual reproduction of A. nidulans [13]. This mutation is favorable,
as it ensures vegetative growth of the fungus. This way, interference and crossing of genetic
material by means of sexual reproduction is avoided. Secondly, knockout of nkuA, the gene
involved in non-homologous end joining. This ensures efficient gene targeting, as the fungus
is forced to repair Cas9-generated DSB by integration of the donor DNA through HR. Lastly,
disruption of argB2, an arginine-synthesizing gene. This mutation is used as an auxotrophic
selection marker, to determine the presence of co-transformed plasmids. The A. nidulans master
strain, NID2788, is a protease-deficient strain, which has the same background as NID2531, with
the additional knockouts of 15 native extracellular proteases. In the NHEJ-proficient strain
NID2908, nkuA is intact, while also containing the protease-deficient background. Additionally,
NID2908 harbors a deletion of the pyrG gene as an auxotrophic marker. PyrG encodes an
enzyme of the pyrimidine biosynthesis pathway, which is important for uridine synthesis. A
protease-deficient background can prove beneficial for heterologous protein production, due to
both a lessened burden on the secretory pathway, as well as a reduced degradation of the
produced protein of interest [3]. An overview of strains can be seen in table 1.

The construct for integration was comprised of the constitutive pTEF promoter, the ProtA-

harbored the argB gene as the selective marker.
After transformation and recovering of successful homokaryotic transformants, the production of
the protease, as well as its proteolytic activity, should be assessed. The proteolytic activity was
initially assessed on skim milk assays, however it proved troublesome to achieve good analyses.
It was therefore a key objective to develop a successful protocol for an applicable skim milk
assay for use with protease-producing filamentous fungi, such as A. nidulans.

4
Discussion
4.1
Verification of strains and constructs
4.1.1
Tissue-PCR complications
Transformants that had undergone targeted integration events were verified with tissue-PCR.
The primers that was used bound to sites directly upstream and downstream of the integration
site, returning a small band size of ∼500 bp in case of no integration, and a band size of ∼3600
bp in case of correct integration of the transforming DNA. This set-up worked adequately for
verifying NID2788 transformants, resulting in distinct insert-bands, as well as a distinct band for
the negative control. With regards to NID2531 transformants, however, it proved practically
impossible to obtain insert bands, only obtaining the negative control band, as well as wild
type bands for some colonies. This seemed inexplicable, having followed identical protocols for
transformation and verification as for the NID2788 transformants, as well as having repeated
experiments multiple times.
The absence of both wild type and insert band could be blamed on an technical error in the
reaction, and these results could not be used to draw any conclusions about the transformants.
However, due to the lack of any positive results, the absence of a wild type band in some
colonies, while it was present in others, was interpreted as an indication of interference of the
genomic DNA around the integration site, and thus that an integration event had taken place,
even though the correct insert was not able to be verified. Some speculations that might explain
the absence of positive results could be that the transformed DNA had been integrated at a
different site, or that multiple copies were integrated into the integration site, thereby disrupting
the tissue-PCR results. This could have been caused by homologous recombination involving
sequences in the donor DNA that was also present in other parts of the genome, or as a result of
successive rounds of homologous integration at the integration site. Tandemly repeated copies
of the transforming DNA, as a result of homologous integration, has been observed in other
studies of A. nidulans [8]. If this was the case, it could yield fragments that are too large to
return a PCR result with the primers that were used.
One way to investigate this absence of positive results could have been to perform a tissue-
PCR using an alternative set of primers. For example, primers binding only within the ProtA
sequence might reveal whether the transformed DNA is present in the genome at all. In general,
alternative primer pairs could have been used to bring more information about the nature
41
==PAGE:43==
of the integration events into light. For example, it could have been useful to use a primer
binding outside one of the homologous down- and upstream regions to verify whether or not
the integration fragment had been inserted in the correct site. In order to avoid fragments
too large, this would also require a primer that binds within the insert. The protA gene is a
relatively large sequence, and when choosing potential primers the size of the returned bands
must be taken into account.
4.1.2
Plasmid verification
Correct assembly of the integration plasmid was verified by restriction analysis. However, with
complications arising regarding both confirmation of correct gene integration, as well as proof
of proteolytic activity, it would have been beneficial to perform additional verification. Verifi-
cation of the plasmid by sequencing could have been an option to eliminate any doubt about
the accuracy of the integration sequence. This would serve as confirmation that the up- and
downstream regions were appropriate for homologous integration, as well as confirmation that
the expression sequence of promoter, the protA gene, and terminator was assembled correctly
with no disruptions that could hamper expression in the fungus. A quicker way to further
investigate the accuracy of the plasmid structure could also have been performed with PCR,
using primers binding to the promoter, terminator, or within the protA gene, for example.
4.2
Development of skim milk assay
The development of a skim milk assay that produces clear, demonstrable results was a prominent
part of the project, and an important tool for detection and screening of proteolytic activity in
host strains and transformants. Skim milk media was made with minimal media (MM), which
was used for normal growth of fungi, as the foundation. The MM media was supplied with skim
milk powder, as well as other relevant supplements. Different combinations and concentrations
of media contents were explored, in order to achieve a useful assay.
4.2.1
Influence of nutrient starvation and substrate availability
The first round of skim milk media (SMM1), was made with 1% skim milk powder. In addition
to regular MM media with supplemented skim milk powder (SMM1.1), skim milk media was
made without a nitrogen source (SMM1.2) and without a glucose source, in order to test whether
nutrient starvation might help promote protease production. Fungal colonies were grown on the
plates to compare any potential differences in appearance of proteolytic activity. However, no
42
==PAGE:44==
clearing zones were observed on SMM1.1 or on SMM1.2 (nitrate-depleted plates), while there,
unsurprisingly, were no growth on the glucose-depleted plates. Since no proteolytic activity
could be detected on either of the assays, it is not possible to derive any actual effect of nitrate-
depletion on protease production from these results.
This resulted in a new approach for skim milk media preparation, in which the glucose concen-
tration was decreased substantially (but not fully), while the level of skim milk powder present
in the media was explored. An applicable assay resulting in detectable zones of hydrolysis was
only achieved when both increasing the concentration of protein powder, while decreasing the
concentration of glucose (SMM2.3). Therefore, it is not possible to attest whether the appear-
ance of proteolytic activity was solely affected by the glucose starvation. Nonetheless, increasing
the concentration of skim milk powder in the assay did appear to have a positive influence on
the appearance of proteolytic activity. This influence could be ascribed to the increased opacity,
making potential clearing zones more visible, and thus making for easier detection. Addition-
ally, it might also be possible that the influence can be ascribed to an increased substrate
concentration in the media, and that a higher availability of substrate have a positive influence
on protease production.
4.2.2
Procedure for skim milk media preparation
In terms of the procedure for making the media, problems arose regarding the addition of the
skim milk powder. Firstly, it proved troublesome to fully dissolve the skim milk powder and
for it to entirely integrate into the media, when added before autoclavation of the media. Sec-
ondly, problems arose with the media contents separating after autoclavation. These problems
were addressed by exploring alternative approaches for making the media. At one point, it was
hypothesized that the prolonged exposure to high temperatures in the autoclave could have
resulted in denaturation and coagulation of the skim milk proteins, thereby negating the ap-
plicability of the assay. Under the assumption that autoclavation might negatively impact the
skim milk substrate, the protein powder was dissolved separately in 100°C MilliQ water before
being added to the autoclaved media. This was especially complicated when dissolving larger
concentrations of protein powder. In turn, this method raised problems with contamination, as
the dispersion was far to viscous to be filter-sterilized, and contaminants were not eliminated in
the autoclave. To circumvent this issue, another approach was tested, in which the skim milk
powder was dissolved and autoclaved separately, before being added to the autoclaved media.
This resulted in homogeneous media with fully dissolved contents and higher opacity.
43
==PAGE:45==
4.2.3
Minimal media as basis for production assay
While nutrient-starvation of glucose and nitrogen might increase protease production, it has
been shown that growth on defined minimal media restrict protein production in general, on
account of production yields usually being limited on minimal media. For example, a study of
the microorganisms E. coli and S. cerevisiae showed that levels of amino acids, essential for
protein synthesis and cell growth, are significantly higher in complex media than in minimal
media cultures [19]. Therefore, it would have been advantageous to test protease production
on skim milk plates made with complex media as the basis. As complex media might promote
protein production, and thus protease production, this could have resulted in an assay with
more apparent results. The use of minimal media as the basis for the assay may also explain
the postponed appearance of proteolytic activity witnessed on the plates. The protease assay
plates were monitored for at least one week before clearing zones were detectable for A. nidu-
lans colonies, though this was not the case for A. niger and the observed contaminants. The
hypothesis is that using a complex media basis for the assay might have assisted in achieving
quicker results.
4.3
Production of heterologous ProtA
The production of heterologous ProtA was assayed on skim milk plates. While no proteolytic
activity could be detected for any of the NID2788-pA strains, NID2531-pA and NID2531 strains
produced visible clearing zones. This indicates that ProtA is non-detectable on the assay, and
that the clearing zones around the NID2531-pA colonies were caused by native proteases. These
results could have been caused by various potential scenarios, which include the possibility that
either the fungus was not producing a lot of protease, that the produced proteases were not
active, or that the proteases did not interact with the particular substrate in the assay. Each
of these scenarios will be discussed in the following section.
One scenario is, that the heterologous protease is not produced, or that it is not produced in
high enough concentrations to be detected. As it was seen with the skim milk assay, proteolytic
activity took a while to appear, and it could be hypothesized that the production and secretion
of protein on the assay is relatively low. From the tissue-PCR results of NID2788-pA1 and
NID2788-pA2, it is assumed that the protA gene, as well as the promoter and terminator, is
present in the genome, and should be expressed. However, even though transcription is assumed
to be high at IS1, the actual level of transcription is unknown. It is also known that the gene
44
==PAGE:46==
has a signal peptide sequence, so the protease should be secreted, and therefore should be able
to be detected, if produced.
Another scenario could be, that the ProtA protease is produced, but has low catalytic activity,
making it non-detectable on the assay. Different factors can affect the activity of the protease,
such as pH level, the cleavage of a pro-peptide, or misfolding. Further investigation into the
possible reason for absence of detectable activity should be conducted. If the activity of the
protease, or the lack thereof, can be understood and controlled, it could be taken advantage
of in the production process, since one of the major challenges in protease production is the
auto-degradation of product. This could be controlled, for example, by pH regulation of the
media.
Another reason for ProtA apparently not being detected on the assay, could be that the substrate
in the assay simply is not suitable for this protease. However, there have been examples of other
studies using ProtA or similar proteases for degradation of casein substrates. For example, a
study [7] found that an extracellular S28 proline-specific protease derived from A. niger could
be used for de-bittering of casein hydrolysates for food purposes by degradation. With casein
being the major protein source in milk, as well as being relatively rich in proline, it would be
expected that a skim milk assay would be an evident and suitable choice for ProtA proteolysis
detection. This might suggest that other factors are at play.
There is also a small possibility that the difference in proteolytic is caused by different integration
events, as indicated on the comparative skim milk assay (Figure 15), in which the clearing zone
around NID2531-pA is visibly bigger than those around the control NID2531. This could just
be a coincidence, and further evidence would be needed to draw such conclusion. A way to
investigate ProtA production further could be by SDS-PAGE.
A proteolytic assay, such as the skim milk assay, depend on enzyme activity, and does not give
direct indications of enzyme production. A way to determine if the protein was produced at
all, is by SDS-PAGE. Analyzing the supernatant from transformant cultures by SDS-PAGE
would demonstrate if the protease was produced and secreted, and in what amounts. In this
project, it was intended to use SDS analysis in order to verify the production and secretion
of the heterologous ProtA. However, it was not managed in the available time, and so there
are questions regarding the ProtA production that remain unanswered. An SDS-PAGE could
also be used to determine the stability of the produced product over time. As one of the main
challenges of protease production is the degradation of product, it would be beneficial to know
45
==PAGE:47==
how stable the secreted product is over time, and in particular, to be able to compare the
stability of product between the two production strains, NID2531 and NID2788. Alternatively,
analysis by proteomics would give much more definitive information about the state of ProtA
production in all of the used host strains.
4.4
NID2908 transformantion and screening
With intent of obtaining a high-producing protease strain, by means of random integration of
the protA-gene into the genome, the integration fragment was transformed into NID2908. The
donor DNA was co-transformed with the same CRISPR-plasmid used in the gene targeting
experiments (pCIS1). When the experiment was planned, this was initially not intended, as a
DNA DSB is not necessary for random integration. The linearized integration fragment would,
in itself, mimic broken DNA and promote the NHEJ pathway, resulting in random integra-
tion into the genome. However, a co-transformation plasmid, containing an AMA1-sequence
and an arginine selective marker, was needed in order to ensure efficient transformation. Due
to a lack of an alternative plasmid, the CRISPR-vector was used for this purpose.
It was
hypothesized that the DSB would be repaired, resulting in disruption of the integration site,
either by mutations or integration by homology. Yet, provided with enough donor DNA, the
linearized fragment would still promote random genomic integration. The desired outcome was
that multiple integration events would take place, resulting in potential high-producing strains.
Therefore, high concentration of donor DNA was supplied in the transformation. According to
studies, depending on the receptiveness of the protoplast, multiple copies of the transforming
DNA will be assimilated and integrated, even at low concentrations [8]. Therefore, there was a
high chance of multiple integration events occurring, depending on the quality of the protoplast.
In order to screen for ProtA-overproducing NID2908 strains, transformants were assayed on
skim milk plates. It was the hope that possible integration of multiple copies of the protA gene
might have resulted in a level of protease production high enough to be detectable on the skim
milk assay. However, this was not the case, as screening did not result in detection of any
proteolytic activity, and thus detection of ProtA production was proved to still be a challenge.
4.5
Future perspectives
As discussed in the sections above, there are several further experimental measures that could be
taken, in order to fully investigate the nature of ProtA production in the various A. nidulans host
46
==PAGE:48==
strains used in this project. Transformants acquired from the different experimental approaches
should be analyzed, either by SDS-PAGE or proteomics, in order to shed light on the state of
protease production in these strains. Additionally, there is a need for better screening of ProtA-
producing transformants, such as the ones obtained from the NID2908 transformations. This
calls for further optimization of the developed skim milk assay, for example by substituting the
media basis for a media better suited for high protein production.
Nonetheless, the fact that prominent extracellular proteolytic activity could be detected around
NID2531 colonies, while nothing was able to be observed for NID2788 and NID2908 strains, act
as confirmation that the knock-out of proteases in these strains has been highly effective. This is
a testament to the potential of NID2788 and NID2908 as really promising hosts for heterologous
protein production. Therefore, further cell factory optimization and protein production in these
strains would be worth exploring.

==PAGE:49==
5
Conclusion
The purpose of this study was to explore the use protease-deficient host strains for protease
production in A. nidulans.
The main objective was to obtain production and secretion of
heterologous ProtA in various host strains, by using two different approaches for gene integration
into the host genome, one for targeted integration, and one for multi-copy, random integration.
While integration of the sequence for ProtA expression was verified in some strains, namely
NID2788-pA1 and NID2788-pA2, it was not managed to obtain proof of production in either of
the produced transformants, as ProtA was not detected on the skim milk assays. This calls for
further investigation into the nature of integration and production of ProtA in these strains.
Furthermore it meant that no conclusions could be drawn about the effect of the protease-
deficient strain background on heterologous ProtA production.
Another key objective was to develop an applicable protease assay, in order to ease the detection
of proteolytic activity. A new protocol for preparation of a skim milk assay was established.
This assay was successful in producing clear and demonstrable results, however, clearing zones
were often slow to appear.
Even though an applicable assay was developed, there are still
potential for further optimizations, which could make it a better screening tool for proteolytic
activity and high protease-producing strains.
