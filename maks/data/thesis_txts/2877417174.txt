Abstract
Quantum networks require electro-optic phase shifters that can modulate photons with minimal
voltage and minimal device length to reduce decoherence and enable scalable architectures.
These devices rely on the linear electro-optic (Pockels) effect, whose response is effectively
instantaneous. Achieving a low VπL is therefore essential, since it directly links the driving
voltage and interaction length to the induced phase shift. BaTiO3 (BTO), when grown with its c-
axis oriented, offers Pockels coefficients up to an order of magnitude larger than the commonly
used LiNbO3, making it an attractive candidate for compact, low-voltage phase shifters.
Multiple groups have attempted to grow high-quality, thick BTO films. Molecular Beam Epitaxy
has demonstrated very high Pockels coefficients, but the achievable film thickness and scalab-
ility remain limited. RF-magnetron sputtering, in contrast, can deposit thicker layers over larger
areas and is therefore more promising for scalable device fabrication, although reported electro-
optic performance still lags MBE. This motivated an exploratory study of sputtering parameters
to understand how pressure, oxygen flow, substrate choice, and annealing influence crystallinity
and stoichiometry of BTO films.
In this thesis, BaTiO3 was sputtered on (100) STO/Si wafers and on (100) STO single crys-
tals. X-ray diffraction was used to evaluate crystallinity and preferential orientation, and X-ray
photoelectron spectroscopy was used to assess the surface composition. The results show
that sputtering at 20 mTorr, combined with high-temperature growth over several hours, pro-
duces BTO films up to roughly 500nm with the highest observed degree of c-axis preference
on STO/Si, although the films remain polycrystalline due to the textured STO. In contrast, de-
position on (100) STO single-crystal substrates led to epitaxial BTO growth, confirming that the
underlying STO quality strongly determines film orientation.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Interconnected quantum computers and the promise of higher security in data transmission
are among the main reasons why quantum networks are so highly sought after and actively re-
searched — both by large companies and academic institutions. However, to enable a quantum
network, we need components and engineered systems which operate reliably. In the quantum
realm that means, higher modulation rates while lowering interference to reduce decoherence
(<1 VπL). Quantum networks running in parallel with the ”classical” internet will enable us secure
communication, e.g. quantum key distribution but also secure access to quantum computers
and clock synchronization [62].
Quantum key distribution offers information-theoretic confidentiality, since any eavesdropper
would disturb a quantum state and thus detectable (no-cloning theorem) [67]. These applica-
tions demand photonic components capable of ultrafast and precise control over single photons
while maintaining phase coherence. Therefore, the material properties of the electro-optic mod-
ulators are central to advancing the field of quantum communication.
Currently, the predominant material platform for high-speed optical modulation is Lithium
Niobate/ LiNbO3 (LNO). LNO is widely used because it combines transparency in the optical
communication window with a strong linear electro-optic (Pockels) effect and can be integrated
into silicon photonic platforms [55]. The Pockels effect allows modulation of the refractive index
proportional to the applied electric field, enabling direct conversion between electrical and
optical signals. However, while LNO modulators have been optimized for classical telecom-
munications, they face intrinsic limitations for quantum applications. Specifically, conventional
LNO devices require comparatively high driving voltages and large device footprints to achieve
sufficient modulation depth, limiting both their energy efficiency and scalability in cryogenic
or low-noise environments used for quantum computing as well as integration into CMOS
processes [57].
But for quantum networks, higher modulation rates while decreasing and the applied voltage,
which could indirectly interfere with the signals, is necessary. Here comes Barium Titanate/
BaTiO3 (BTO) into play. BTO thin films have a 30 times higher Pockels coefficient (in epitaxially
grown c-axis orientated Barium Titanate/ BaTiO3 (BTO) r42 = 923 pm
V [3]) than LNO (r33 = 31 pm
V
[55]) and can be deposited on silicon, enabling monolithic integration with photonic circuits.

1.2
Research Problem
Depositing BTO on silicon has some constraints since it has a lattice mismatch with silicon. To
circumvent that, Strontium Titanate/ SrTiO3 (STO) will be used as a buffer layer since it has a
very small lattice mismatch and can be grown on silicon while BTO can be epitaxially grown
to the deposited STO layer [3]. The issue is that currently BTO with high Pockels coefficients
have been deposited with Pulsed Laser Deposition (PLD) or Molecular Beam Epitaxy (MBE)
[2, 3, 38], but either low thicknesses or the Pockels coefficients with its VπL are low, coupled
with the slow deposition process. For that reason RF-magnetron sputtering comes into play to
counteract the scalability issue.
But so far, no results have been achieved to come close to one micrometer thickness paired
with a Pockels coefficient near a MBE deposited thin film [43]. Many parameters from target
bias, chamber pressure, gas mixture, substrate to target distance, substrate temperature during
sputtering and post-deposition annealing have to be tested to optimize the sputtering process.
1.3
Objectives
The objectives for this work were to:
• Summarize and evaluate peer-reviewed articles on RF (radio frequency) magnetron sput-
tering of metal oxide thin-films, identifying trends and gaps relevant to the thesis
• Operate a plasma-induced RF magnetron sputtering system for various deposition para-
meters to achieving epitaxial growth of Barium titanate thin films
• Analyze deposited thin films using XRD, XPS, and SEM, and interpret the data to assess
crystal structure, composition, and surface morphology
• Replicate an established experimental procedure for measuring the Pockels coefficient of
Barium titanate, based on published protocols
• Document and present experimental results and analysis in a thesis suitable for publica-
tion

Polarization and Modulation of Light
Light is an electromagnetic wave. As quantum field theory teaches us, any electromagnetic
wave is a propagation of quantized excitations (photons) [37] in the electromagnetic field, but
this doesn’t help us for this scope of thesis. We treat light as a propagating wave of electric and
magnetic fields [17].
Polarization of light
Polarization describes how the electric field vector behaves as light travels through space.
There are three types of polarization, linear, circular and elliptical polarization (see fig. 2.1).
In linear polarization, the electric field vector alternates in a fixed axis, e.g. goes ”up and down”
the x axis.
Circular polarized light, here the electric field vector rotates in a circle around the plane of
propagation.
In elliptical polarization, the electric field follows an ellipse, both rotating and changing mag-
nitude periodically. [7]
Polarization can be used to encode light with information (more on that later in this chapter).

Modulation of light
The modulation of light is a process where an electrical signal changes/ controls a property of
light, be it the amplitude, frequency, phase or polarization.
A way to modulate light is to e.g. letting two electromagnetic waves propagate the same path,
but in one path the wave encounters a medium with a different refractive index than the propagat-
ing medium, thus the propagation speed changes in the part the wave propagates through the
medium and in the end when both photons converge, a phase shift is seen. That is basically
the description of the working principle of the Mach-Zehnder interferometer.
There are different ways and effects which modulate light, for this thesis, we focus on the linear
electro-optical effect called the Pockels effect.

Sputtering is a so called physical vapor deposition process. Here a plasma will be induced by
introducing a gas and a cathode, in which a bias will be applied. This results in electrons being
attracted and repelled, in their path, they bump into the gas molecules and knock out other
valence electrons and consequently ionizing the molecules [10].
On a target, a DC bias can be applied, but for dielectrics like STO and BTO, a RF bias (Typically
13.56 MHz) has to be applied. The reason for that is, that with DC bias on a non-conducting
target, charge will built up on the target surface, that leads to arcing, resulting into process
instabilities.
Magnetic fields will be applied parallel to the target surface, that will have the effect that
electrons are getting ”trapped” and are following a drifting path (E × B drift) in a circling form
along the magnetic field lines and thus ionizing more atoms along their way.
So now onto how the deposition actually happens, due to the negative self-bias of the target,
the positive attracted ions knock out the atoms off the target, a so called ion bombardment. The
knocked off particles find their way onto the substrate and form via nucleation and growth a thin
film.

Experimental Approach
This project is, somewhat, based on Saikrishnan’s thesis [31] itself, the first steps were to try to

Chapter 5
Discussion
5.1
Crystalline Phases and Texture Development
The contour graphs don’t show gradients, but looking at the FHWM and peak intensity contour
graphs (in the python output file on Github), they do show gradients, which is expected. The
reason why the ratio or integrated area (100)/ (110) was chosen to evaluate the c-axis growth, is
that it has more predictive power, since using the integrated areas than solely the FWHM or the
peak intensity, ratio shows the preference of c-axis orientation more reliably. Since deposition
7 gave the highest integrated area ratio of peaks (100) and (110), the potential for the highest
crystallinty lies with those parameters, that is why those parameters were chosen, but random
variances can’t be excluded here. The plane (100) is the c-axis oriented plane, this orientation
is the preferred one for maximum pockels effect exploitation. the (110) plane is an indicator of
a polycrystalline/ textured BTO thin film and using the integrated area ratio is the better way to
indicate which crystal orientation is preferred.
The grazing incidence XRD on deposition EX1 does reveal that its amorphous, which is ex-
plained by having no annealing and sputtering temperature at room temperature, so the energy
for recrystallization is lacking.
But since some crystallinity and crystal orientation is observed in the BTO layer, the likely cause
is that during the heating ramp up to 700°C, which takes roughly 30 minutes, plus the 4 hours
sputtering of BTO at 700°C, will provide some energy for recrystallization.
When comparing the different r-coefficients, the average would be 0.093, but that wouldn’t be
accurate to infer that there is a slight correlation between position to target distance and the
integrated area ratio, since a lot of peaks were too weak to be detected, so the data are for
deposition 2, 3, 6, and 7 are incomplete in that sense. Also it seems that the values do vary a
lot. Due to all those constraints, inferring a correlation here, is not advised.
For the deposition on the STO, the XRD measurements do show that predominately the c-axis
oriented growth is present. In deposition 9 the (110) peak does increase with distance to the
target whereas it is not apparent in deposition 10 and 11, but to conclude that the extra oxygen
during the sputter process will suppress the (110) growth has to be investigated further before
making final conclusions about it, due to small sample size. For that the data sample size is to
small to make hard inferences out of it. But it can be said that if the STO layer is polycrystalline
or partly amorphous, the BTO is likely to be textured as well, since in the wafer sputtering that
was the case and the XRD confirm the polycrystallinity, which is for the STO crystals not the
case. Here we can conclude that the crystallinity and crystal orientation of STO also strongly
influence the crystallinity and crystal orientation of the subsequent BTO layer.
38
Deposition and characterization of BaTiO3 thin films on Si for quantum networking
==PAGE:51==
Looking at the distance graphs, the STO’s integrated area seems to be rising with the distance
to the target, which shouldn’t be the case, since the crystals should be all the same, but it can
be taken as a margin of measurement/ instrument error. But since the change in integrated are
of STO is much less than that of BTO and the r-coefficients are similar, we can conclude that a
closer position to the target leads to higher crystallinity of BTO. But careful here, the samples
size is rather low, so the conclusion shouldn’t be set as a low, furthermore we can’t extrapolate
the result to say that the closer the sample is the target, the higher the crystallinity is, infinitely.
Also the fit is quadratic whereas the Pearson coefficient show a linear correlation.
Looking at the area values, it seems that extra oxygen during the sputter process has no affect
on the crystallinity, but the unusual high value on 9-1 seems like an outlier. The extra oxygen
seems to help to get a more uniform crystalline layer, since without any oxygen, the integrated
are drop more sharply with target distance. The reason that position 1 has in each deposition
the highest value is, that in that position the sputtered particles have the least gas-phase scatter
collision.
The hitting angle does decreases with distance which also decreases deposition
flux [61], additionally radiative heating from the plasma would be higher in closer position that
farther ones, which do favor epitaxy.
Comparing it with Posada et. al. [43], they did not specify their target to substrate distance,
which might differ with the setup used in this project, they also sputter at 700°C but with a gas
mixture of 70% : 30% Argon:Oxygen ratio. They also annealed at 750°C in flowing oxygen for
30min to 1 hour in a tube furnace, the annealing was done in this project in the process chamber
of the Lesker system under vacuum.
5.2
Stoichiometry
The XPS measurements suggest that on the wafer depositions, titanium is lost. The reason
for that may be resputtering mechanism, since it RF-Power was at 200W and titanium has a
lower mass than barium, that may a reason why titanium is decreased in comparison to barium.
Surface effects due to high carbon contamination likely has distorted those values. The barium
and oxygen ratio, is also skewed, in that sense that oxygen is sometimes more, sometimes
less, although some loss is to be expected since both depositions were done in pure argon
atmosphere and the annealing under vacuum. So the caveat is that in these measurements,
the surface was not etched, therefore the measured carbon contamination up to 30% is likely
contributing to a distorted measurement. Using the XPS values of the wafer samples to infer
anything is not advised.
Similarly for the STO crystal, since the surface was not etched here either. Disregarding 10-3,
due to high carbon contamination, it does seem that titanium and oxygen are getting lost during
the sputter and annealing process, the reason for titanium deficiency is mentioned before. But
the differences the amount of oxygen during the sputter process doesn’t seem to make any
difference in the stoichiometry. A reason for the oxygen loss, might be the 2 hour long annealing
process done under vacuum. The reason why even the 5% oxygen atmosphere during the
sputtering process didn’t make a difference, may be that the threshold is too low, Posadas et al.

Due to the lack of etching and low sample size, inference from these values should be taken

Chapter 6
Conclusion
6.1
Summary of Findings
The purpose of this project was to optimize sputtering parameters of BTO for a high Pockels
coefficient, using XRD measured (100) peak as a proxy, to be used in the industry for mass
production with the application in quantum networking.
XRD revealed that the deposited STO with no heating during sputtering results to an amorphous
film, while heating during the BTO deposition provides partial recrystallization. BTO grown on
this layer will mostly be (110) dominant, so rather polycrystalline, with area ratio (100)/ (110)
varying from 0.10 to 0.26 depending on the position.
Adding 1-5% of oxygen during sputtering did not counteract the oxygen loss, which XRD re-
vealed. An insufficient oxygen atmosphere during sputtering process and annealing under va-
cuum might be the cause for that.
XPS showed deficiency in titanium, but could be confounded since only the surface was meas-
ured and that without etching (surface was carbon contaminated).
The XRR and SEM measurement suggest a rough surface, while ellipsometry shows that STO
is deposited from 12nm to 14nm thickness and BTO 200nm to 500nm, which results in a growth
rate of 1-1.67nm/min and 0.83 to 2.08nm/min respectively.
The main findings are that the buffer layer of STO has to be of high crystallinity and preferred
crystal orientation (that would be the (100) plane) so BTO can be grown epitaxially in a c-axis
orientation. Being closer to the target, in the scope of off-axis sputtering and the current setup
in the Lesker system, seems to enable a higher c-axis oriented growth of BTO.
6.2
Limitations of the Project
The annealing temperature is lower than the sputtering temperature, which goes against literat-
ure consensus, additionally anneal under vacuum further decreases the oxygen.
The amount of crystallinity and grain size could not be measured due to the time constraint
and inexperience. In the XPS results, the surface should have been etched to eliminate carbon
contaminants, which likely undermined all measurements, to further validate the composition
an Energy X-Ray Dispersion (EDS/ EDX) measurement should have been done.
The statistical power is insufficient, since sample size is too low to make significant correlation
form the data.
The growth rate for each layer and the thicknesses of the deposition have been inferred from a
single deposition (EX1 and EX2) since thickness measurements like ellipsometry, reflectometry
and XRR are difficult to do with multiple layers, but since the parameters are the same, the
transfer of the thickness and growth rate should be adequate, but still imprecise and should
only give a very rough estimation.

Ultimately the VπL value is important, which does depend on the crystallinity and crystal orient-
ation of BTO, but other factors like electric leakage and optical containment do play a role as
well, so inferences on the Pockels coefficient and VπL can’t be made with only the results of
this thesis as an argument. The measurement of the VπL couldn’t be done, since it was out of
scope regarding the time for this project.
6.3
Recommendations
For measuring crystallite size and crystallinity, a rocking curve analysis is advised as well as an
atomic force microscopy measurement for gauging the roughness,
For continuing to get a high Pockels coefficient and more importantly a good VπL value, rocking
curve XRD should be done to measure grain size and crystallinity and the sputter temperature
should be increased for the deposition of STO, my suggestion is to sputter both materials at
700°C, with more than 5% oxygen (turbo speed likely has to be set under 50%) and anneal
at 750° in an pure oxygen atmosphere and then measure its crystallinity, grain size, crystal
structure and composition. Since the material is rather thick, heat resistant tape can be put on
the wafer to cover some areas, to later use profilometry to measure the thickness. The annealing
should be done at a oxygen rich atmosphere like Posadas et. al. did, they used a tube furnace
and annealed with pure oxygen flow. EDX for further validation of thin film composition is also
recommended.
Since this project will be continued at the plasma lab and the MBE from the lab in Risø is getting
transferred there, using it to create a thin STO layer and using RF-magnetron sputtering to grow
BTO is the recommended way to grow STO and BTO epitaxially.
