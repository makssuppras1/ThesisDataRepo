Abstract
A quickly changing climate is increasing demand for energy sources that have a lower
impact on the environment than sources processing fossil fuels. The leading asset in the
ﬁeld of renewable energy production is wind turbines. With an increasing amount of wind
turbines installed, more regions became dependent on this source. Therefore it is of the
highest importance to ensure that the environmental changes will not affect the lifetime
of the structure. New wind farm projects by USA, China and Vietnam, are considered in
the seismic prone areas, which adds new issues to the design process.
This master project describes the dynamic response of the 10MW DTU reference
wind turbine when exposed to different types of environment. Time domain analysis was
accomplished using aero-servo-elastic-code HAWC2 developed by the Wind Energy De-
partment of DTU. Operating and parked state of a wind turbine was considered together
with different types of coupled natural loading. Various wind velocities were applied
along with wind-generated sea states. For seismic part of the analysis inﬂuence of far-
ﬁeld, near-ﬁeld motions on the response of the wind turbine were studies as well as the
effect of site conditions, which was represented by seismic records from loose and dense
soil proﬁles. Earthquake records were extracted from the PEER NGA West database and
then paired according to standard parameters such as soil type and moment magnitude of
the event.
At ﬁrst validation of the HAWC2 seismic module was carried out using the OpenSees
platform. Validation used a simpliﬁed model of the wind turbine, due to differences
between both software. Six seismic motions were used, with three accounting for low
structural modes of the structure and three accounting for higher modes. After successful
validation time domain analysis was performed, results show a higher response in case of
near-ﬁeld motions as well as for excitation recorded in loose soil proﬁles. The inﬂuence
of wind ﬂow can be noticed at lower wind velocities; however, the response is governed
by seismic motions. For higher wind speeds is it clear, the wind dominates the response.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
With the ever-growing world population, there is increasing demand for electric power.
Considering rapid climate changes and precautions taken by the United Nations in Sus-
tainable Development Goals[5], two options for clean energy are favored, solar and wind
energy. The removal of energy sources based on processing fossil fuels requires the instal-
lation of new renewable sources. Figure (1.1) illustrates the prediction of energy produc-
tion from various energy sources. As can be seen the usage of renewable energy sources
almost triples since 1990.
Figure 1.1: Prediction of energy sources usage[2]
A signiﬁcant part of recently produced green energy comes from wind farms. As
shown in Figure (1.2), leading positions in wind energy hold China, United States, and
Germany. It can be noticed that China generated 2/3 of total wind power produced in
2018. Out of almost 550 GW of total wind power produced in 2017 19GW were produced
by offshore wind farms, as shown in Figure (1.3). As presented offshore wind power
production experiences a signiﬁcant increase, with prediction conﬁrming this trend.

1.1
Aim and Overview
Design of wind turbine is a complex process, which requires much knowledge, about
mechanics, electronic and aeroelasticity. To prevent severe damage and economic loses,
the design process requires the utmost attention. When comparing onshore and offshore
wind turbines, offshore wind turbines are more delicate to analyses. Considering bigger
rotors with capacity from 6 to 10 MW, environmental loads are also more unpredictable.
Coupled loading form wind, waves, and seismic motions can prove critical for such an
important structure. This complicated issue provides engineers with many challenges.
Therefore it is essential to investigate and model such situations for a better understanding
of the structural behavior of wind turbines.

CHAPTER 2
Literature review
This chapter represents a brief overview of the research papers and studies that have been
published recently and are related to the offshore wind turbines and their exposure to
concurrent loading, mainly focused on seismic excitation as well as on aerodynamic and
hydrodynamic loading. Literature shows the common opinion among engineers that the
seismic loading is not as relevant for the wind turbines as aerodynamic or hydrodynamic
loading[7]. However, to provide the safe design and to ensure that the wind turbine will
endure in extreme conditions also the seismic excitation has to be taken into considera-
tion. The wind turbine is very slander structure thus it is susceptible to lateral forces and
deformations caused by horizontal loading.
To be able to analyze complex problems such as wind turbine under different loading,
the different approaches are required. They are mainly divided into analytical, numerical,
and experimental studies that use the probabilistic and deterministic methods. Due to the
variety of cases available, for this study, only papers that are related to wind turbines with
various foundation conﬁguration are used.

expanding to the seismic prone areas, China, Taiwan, Japan, which means that there is a
high probability that wind turbine will be exposed to the multi-hazard environment. Thus

of structure is rather costly is of utmost importance to investigate its response due to the

Katsanos Evangelos, Thöns Sebastian, and Georgakis Christos T.[7] presented the
overview of 155 scientiﬁc publications, considering wind turbines and seismic loading,
of which majority, was produced before the year 2000. This report described that the
deterministic methods were majorly, around 80%, used to determine the response of the
wind turbine, while only 20% applied the more advanced probabilistic approach. The
publication represents the comparative evaluation with emphasis on exposure of wind
turbine to seismic loading. Assessment considering concurrent loading should be carried
out for wind turbines located in a seismic prone environment. Another suggestion was to
use full FEM models to capture the complex dynamic response of the wind turbine. The
authors observed that much wind-turbine dedicated software is missing the precise treat-
ment of the seismic loading. Effect of different operational stages, as well as the result of
soil-structure interaction (SSI), was considered in various publications. Additionally, it
was proposed to investigate advanced modeling techniques and with that introduction of
some speciﬁc issues such as scouring or earthquake-induced liquefaction.
Several research papers, based on numerical simulations focused on the investigation
of fatigue of the tower structure and critical joints[8] or effects of corrosion on the load-
carrying capacity[9]. Occasionally the locations of new wind farm projects are related
to seismic prone areas, which causes that engineering problems related to seismic load-
ing are occurring more often. Moreover, more conventional deterministic approaches or
utilized probabilistic methods are required.
Modal response spectrum analysis (MRSA) along with a time-history representation
of seismic motions, used for more complex response history analysis (RHA), is used to
determine the impact of seismic excitations on the response of the structure.
Number of research papers[10], [11] that followed methods prescribed in the National
Design Codes or Standards [12], [13]. These studies described a greater effect of wind
ﬂow comparing to seismic loading. One of the reasons for such results might be that
seismic codes did not directly address the wind turbine structure. Therefore the choice
of some parameters that are inﬂuencing the response became problematic. On the other
hand, contemporary codes describe the inverted pendulum structure, which can be used
as the closest approximation to wind turbines.

upper-bound of behavior factor,q, as well as response modiﬁcation factor,R. Regarding
the importance factor, in the past, the wind turbines were usually categorized with impor-

rapidly, there is a suggestion to assign importance class IV. for the wind turbines. An-

The behavior of 400 kW and 2MW wind turbines designed according to the Japanese
design code was studied by T. Ishihara and M. W. Sarwar [15]. A full non-linear FEM
model, using lumped mass, was developed including the tower-rotor coupling. To deﬁne
the contribution of higher modes to the response of structures, the time domain analysis
was carried out and then compared with the semi-theoretical codiﬁed method. Later on,
the safety factor was introduced to the proposed semi-theoretical formulation for the de-

size wind turbine, the effect of higher modes is neglectable while for bigger size wind
turbines, it should be considered. Considering different types of foundation structure Na-

investigation of the seismic response of a horizontal axis wind turbine on two bottom-

both resting on pile foundations[16].
It was found out that when investigating ﬁxed foundation cases, the moment at the
bottom part of the foundation as well as at the root of the blades increased signiﬁcantly
when the seismic loading was introduced. In the case of a ﬂexible foundation, the results
showed that moment at the base of monopile did not raise a lot however the moment at the
root of the blades increased signiﬁcantly compared to the ﬁxed foundation. It is caused
due to increased acceleration at the top part of the wind turbine. The authors suggested
that a detailed analysis of the whole wind turbine should be part of a seismic assessment
since the seismic actions were governing the response. The simpliﬁed models, where
lumped mass is used, did not provide sufﬁcient data regarding blade roots. Followed

of structural dynamic which advise that higher stiffness represents higher acceleration

different soil proﬁles.

with a short period, which is signiﬁcantly more than short periods of loose soil proﬁles.

As mentioned before the experimental studies represent another way of investigation
of wind turbine’s behavior. The ﬁrst real-time seismic analysis on the full-scale wind
turbine was accomplished in 2004, where 70 ft. tall wind turbine was exposed to one of
the historical ground motions Landers with magnitude 7.6. The test provided the ﬁrst step
towards a better understanding of an impact that seismic events have on the wind turbines.
Prowell I, Uang C, Elgamal A, Luco J, Guo L [25], undertook the shake table test
on a full-scale 65kW wind turbine. For the testing Network for Earthquake Engineering
Simulation Large High-Performance Outdoor Shake Table at the University of California,
San Diego was used. The test was executed using two different conﬁgurations, in the ﬁrst
scenario the base was shaken parallel to the axis of rotation of the rotor and second when
earthquake motion was applied in the direction perpendicular to the axis. The authors
focused on the degradation of grout at the tower base, and in the second part on the loss
of bolt torque at the connections between tower segments. From the results was concluded
that the rocking of the base inﬂuenced the reduction of global stiffness and therefore the
contributions to reduction had to be split into structural softening and loss of stiffness in
the base connection. Another ﬁnding showed that deterioration of the joint between tower
and foundation structure was found as one of the possible failure mechanisms. The report
also conﬁrmed the conclusion made by Ishihara and Sawar[15] and later by Prowell [25]
that response of the smaller wind turbines is governed by ﬁrst mode response. It was also
conﬁrmed that higher modes play a signiﬁcant role in the seismic design of large-size
wind turbines.
After Japan’s ﬁrst wind farm survived the Great East Japan Earthquake and with
that associated tsunami, it became one of the signiﬁcant interest for Takashi Matsunobu,

Kamisu offshore wind farm Phase 1, which consists of 2MW wind turbines, was struck
by an earthquake that generated the tsunami waves as well. After the wind farm resumed
its operation, there were no signiﬁcant damages seen on the structures. Based on the
measured data, the authors conducted the time history analysis, using a model with 30
nodes with concentrated lumped mass. For analysis, BLADED code and DYNAMIC Pro
code were utilized according to the Japanese Building Code. Results from time history

In recent years it can be observed that wind farms are placed close to faults of earth-
quake zones, which brings another type of seismic motions into the design process.
"Pulse-like or Near-ﬁeld" seismic events and the method of how to identify several re-

epicenter distance; therefore, it is essential to investigate how signiﬁcant threat they rep-
resent for wind turbine structure compared to the far-ﬁeld seismic motions.
As explained in [27], the ﬁrst period of a wind turbine can be close to the pulse period
of moderate to large earthquakes. This information led to the expectation of high seismic
demands. Near-ﬁeld motions used in the time-history analysis were previously identiﬁed
in the study formed by Rupakhety et al. (2010)[28]. Results showed that the effect of the
near-ﬁeld motions is signiﬁcant, and in some cases, it can even exceed the design wind
effects. It was found out the using the Eurocode 8[13] deﬁned response spectrum, based
on far-ﬁeld ground motions under-estimated the response of the structure when analyzing
the effect of near-ﬁeld motions. It was concluded that it is necessary to take into account
the near-fault forward-directivity inﬂuence for the wind farms placed in areas close to
active earthquake faults.
Similarly, the collapse of the on-shore wind turbine structure as a result of combined
effects from wind loads and strong near-ﬁeld seismic excitation was investigated [29].
Later on, analysis results and responses due to far-ﬁeld motions were compared. The full
non-linear model was created and validated by the static pushover test of a small cylindri-
cal tower. Near-ﬁeld motions were selected using wavelet analysis, which was described
by Baker J[30]; another criterion was moment magnitude, which had to be higher than
5.5. Non-linear time history analysis showed that combined effects of once-in-50-years
wind loading and strong near-ﬁeld earthquakes cause smaller displacement on the top
then the yield displacement. It was pointed out that the wind turbine structure has some
anti-seismic capability. The analysis also depicted that when comparing response due to
far-ﬁeld seismic motion with the response due to near-ﬁeld motions, the displacement
due to near-ﬁeld motions was higher by approximately 33%. Presented seismic collapse
vulnerability curve illustrated the fact that wind turbine has outstanding seismic collapse
resistance. The PGA of the motions when 50% probability of collapse occurred reaches
20 and 27 m/s2 for far-ﬁeld and near-ﬁeld motions respectively.

domain. Although this code is meant to be used for the analysis of offshore wind turbines
in case of seismic loading, it has not been validated.

3.2
Horizontal Axis Wind turbine simulation Code 2nd
generation (HAWC2)
HAWC2 is aero-elastic servo code that had been developed by the Aeroelastic Design
Research Program at DTU Wind Energy, DTU Risø Campus in Denmark. The primary
purpose of the code is to calculate the response of the wind turbine using a time-domain
analysis based on the Newmark method[35]. It is built up from several parts, the structural
part where the user assemble the full structure. The next two parts deﬁne the orientation
and constraints of the individual elements; the aerodynamic part and controller part fol-
lows. The aerodynamic part introduces the aerodynamic forces to the structure while the
controller part regulates rotor elements. Additionally, the hydrodynamic and seismic parts
can be added to the code if necessary. Detailed description and theory behind particular
parts of the code are described further in this chapter.
Structural part of HAWC2 code
The structural module of the code uses ﬂexible multi-body formulation. Multi-body sim-
ulation describes the interaction of different objects using constraints at connecting in-
terfaces, by modifying stiffness, the body can be either ﬂexible or rigid. This method
used in the HAWC2 code is known as “ﬂoating frame of reference,” which was described
by Ahmed A. Shabana[36]. Bodies are coupled together by algebraic constraints. These
constraints deﬁne a ﬁxed relative position among bodies or can be modiﬁed by setting
free more degrees-of-freedom(DOF) to simulate bearing. Each body in HAWC2 code is
modeled as an elastic Timoshenko beam-column element with 6 DOF and deﬁned mass,
stiffness, and inertia distribution. Using a multi-body framework means that wind turbine

Controller part of HAWC2 code
As the name implies, the controller part of the code affects the operation of the wind tur-
bine, using various predeﬁned constants. This piece of code can regulate the pitch of the
blades, can be applied for multiple wind speeds as well as to switch between operational
modes. Two operational modes are considered when describing wind turbines, partial
and full load operation. These modes are based on classical proportional-integral control
theory. The main feedback sensor of the controller is generator speed, to switch smoothly
between two operational modes the output in the form of generator power is taken into
account. The controller manages the collective blade pitch angle and electromagnetic
generator torque to control the wind turbine. In case of low or excessive wind loading the
controller can simulate idle/parked mode or emergency shut-down respectively.

Environmental loads within HAWC2 framework
Proper implementation of environmental loads plays a signiﬁcant role in simulations of a
multi-hazard environment. The reliable outcome from wind turbine response requires a
solid theoretical background of natural load mechanisms. The following section describes
the theory, which is then applied in the HAWC2 code.

Waves and Hydrodynamic load
Sea state and hydrodynamic loading have contributes considerably to the response of the
offshore wind turbines. HAWC2 allows simulating different types of waves as well as
current forces. The Morison equation is used to outline hydrodynamic load interaction.
The HAWC2 hydrodynamic part is based on linear wave theory. This theory assumes that
wave elevation can be considered as a small disruption on the mean water depth. The
water surface is then simpliﬁed as a sinus function, and wave kinematic is derived based
on the wave potential. However, this approach is only valid for small waves in large wa-
ter depth. The limitation of the linear wave theory is in consideration of the mean water
level. This assumption causes issues when the wave surface is below the mean water
level through or when the wave surface is above at crest. To deal with this problem cor-
rection approach known as Wheeler stretching is introduced. By this method, the actual
elevation of the wave surface is taken into account. The regular stream function wave
model used in HAWC2 is formed on the method described by Chaplin from Southampton
University. This procedure is dealing with a similar wave type as the linear wave theory,
with a difference in symmetry around water level and wave steepens. A more realistic
and sophisticated approach to deﬁne the sea state is an irregular wave model. In HAWC2,
this model stands on the theory presented by Szinozouka. Irregular time series can be
approximated by a superposition of many sinusoidal functions with magnitudes based on
the frequency input spectra and random phases. The intensity of irregular waves, as well

Validation of the HAWC2 seismic
module with OpenSees
As mentioned in Chapter (2), in order to obtain precise results from the time-domain
analysis, it is essential to provide validation of the used code. The validation process is
recommended for a determination of whether the implementation of the structural prop-
erties, as well as external loads, are handled as expected. In this chapter, validation of the
HAWC2 seismic module with OpenSees platform is presented.
4.1
OpenSees
Open System for Earthquake Engineering Simulation (OpenSees), developed by Paciﬁc
Earthquake Engineering Research Center, is an open source software framework that
enables to develop and simulate the performance of structural and geotechnical sys-
tems subjected to earthquakes. This framework is object-orientated for FEM analysis.
OpenSees same as HAWC2 contains several modules that are used in FEM analysis.
Model builder block is used to create structure; structural elements can be deﬁned us-
ing element level(force-deformation model), section level(moment-curvature model), and
ﬁber level(stress-strain model of material). Domain block describes the state of the model
in time and stores the object created by the Model Builder. The recorder part monitors the
user-deﬁned parameters. Analysis block moves the model from state of time ti to ti+dt.
4.2
Limitations and Differences
To be able to compare and validate HAWC2 code, models with the same dynamic prop-
erties are required in both software. The different modeling methods indicate that it is
challenging to fulﬁll this requirement. While the HAWC2 code is used only for analy-

CHAPTER 5
Time-Domain Analysis
A wind turbine can be deﬁned as an engineering structure that uses a rotor to capture
the wind and transforms it into electric power. With the increasing worldwide trend of
using wind turbines for generating electric power, the importance of their safety design
increases as well. The location of the wind turbine is responsible for the exposure of
structure to the various loading. The controller can partly control the response of the
wind turbine; this device was described in Section (3.2). It can be concluded that the
interaction between the wind turbine and the wind is dependent on the state of the wind
turbine.
Moreover, in this part, the different states of the wind turbines are described as well
as their inﬂuence on the total response. Later on the description, the natural loads chosen
for the time domain analysis is presented, and at last, the selection of the seismic event is
described.
5.1
Modes of the wind turbine
When investigating the aeroelastic characteristics of the wind turbine structure, the con-
nection between the ﬂexibility of the structure and the loading experienced are accounted
for. Differences in deformation of the structure are determined by the variations of the
aerodynamic loading, which are conditioned by the orientation of the tower and the
blades. This type of dependency is referred to as a coupled problem. One of the cou-
pled problems when considering the wind turbines is the pitch control. Because the wind
turbine is meant to harvest wind energy as efﬁcient as possible, the pitch of the blades has
to be ﬂexible. Controller module enables to regulate the pitch angle of the blades as well
as the electromagnetic torque of the generator according to the wind speed, in order to
maintain constant power output and to prevent any damage during extreme environmental
conditions. By changing the controller parameters, the various states of the wind turbine
43
==PAGE:62==
44
CHAPTER 5. TIME-DOMAIN ANALYSIS
can be obtained.
Based on the controller parameters three basic states can be identiﬁed
• Parked state
• Operating state
• Emergency Shut-down
Parked state
Park or idling state as the name entails means that in this state, the generated power
output is minimal. This state is obtained by changing the pitch angle of the blades to the
maximum pitch angle set in the controller module, in this case, 82 deg. Blade with this
pitch angle is parallel to the wind ﬂow; thus, the area exposed to the wind is signiﬁcantly
smaller; hence, the forces experienced by wind turbines due to wind are less. However,
the park state is activated when the measured wind speed is not sufﬁcient or when the
wind speed is very high. The 10MW DTU RWT is set to enter the park state if the wind
speed exceeds the cut-out wind speed, 28 m/s. When sensors on the hub measure wind of
velocity 25 m/s, the controller will send the signal to pitch the blades until it reaches the
82 deg and pitch velocity lower than 3 m/s.
Operating state
During the operating state, the wind turbine generates the maximum amount of power.
As it can be seen in Figure (5.1), the operating stage contains several phases based on the
mean wind speed at the hub. These wind speeds govern the stages in the operating state,
• Cut-in wind speed 4 m/s
• Rated wind speed 11.4 m/s
• Cut-out wind speed 25 m/s
==PAGE:63==
5.1. MODES OF THE WIND TURBINE
45
Figure 5.1: Wind speed regions and pitch regulation
When the wind turbine initializes the operating stage, at ﬁrst controller has to go through
all the deﬁned parameters. If the parameters are veriﬁed, the controller sends the signal to
change the yaw angle of the shaft and hub. The blades of the wind turbine are now fully
exposed to the wind ﬂow when wind velocity at the hub reaches the cut-in speed, which
is the velocity that is necessary to overcome the friction between the hub and the blades,
the rotor starts to rotate. From Figure(5.1) two main stages can be identiﬁed, partial
load operation and full load operation. Partial load operation begins with the ﬁrst region
where the rotor speed is kept constant at the minimum value,Ωmin, while the generator
torque is tuned. Coming to the second region, the pitch angle is constant, and the rotor
speed increases up to nominal value, Ωnom, in order to boost power capture. When the
wind turbine enters the third region, the rotor speed is constant, and the generator torque
is adjusted. As the wind speed increases, the wind turbine gets into full load operation
stage. When the wind speed increases the generator torque increases as well to balance
the aerodynamic torque, which means that more power is generated, the fourth region
starts when wind speed reaches the rated wind speed. Now the generated power comes to
a nominal value, Pnom. To maintain constant rotor speed and the constant power output,
the pitch angle of the blades is increasing. The full load operation stage lasts until wind
speed hits the cut-out wind speed. When wind speed exceeds the cut-out wind speed, the
wind turbine starts to change the pitch angle until it reaches the park state.
Emergency shut-down
Emergency shut-down is a speciﬁc state of the wind turbine. Structure enters the emer-
gency stage when the acceleration at the hub reaches its threshold in a very short time,
==PAGE:64==
46
CHAPTER 5. TIME-DOMAIN ANALYSIS
can be experienced during an earthquake event. A threshold is determined by the devices
that are placed inside the nacelle, in order to prevent damage to the apparatus.
In this study, only parked and operating stages are investigated. The parked state has
a conﬁguration similar to the idling state, so the pitch angle is ﬁxed set to 82 deg, and
the shaft is blocked for any rotation. By setting the pitch angle to 82 deg, the area of the
blades exposed to the wind is minimal.

Seismic Load
Even though the wind and waves have a signiﬁcant effect on the response of the wind
turbine, the main objective of this study is to evaluate the inﬂuence of different types of
seismic motions. Analysis conducted within this study investigated the impact of
• Far-ﬁeld motions

• Near-ﬁeld motions
• Motions recorded in the dense soil proﬁles
• Motions recorded in the loose soil proﬁles
Far-ﬁeld motions are currently used in the seismic assessment when predicting the
behavior of the wind turbines. However, due to an enormous expansion of the wind
energy, more and more wind turbines are being placed in the areas close to the seismic
faults, e.x. Taiwan, Japan, China. These near-fault motions are characterized by high
intensity over a long period, which causes signiﬁcant accelerations at the top to the wind
turbine. Such accelerations can be critical for sensitive equipment within the nacelle. It
is important to mention that seismic motions were applied only in a direction parallel to
wind and wave propagation.
In order to choose a representative set of the seismic motions, speciﬁc criteria have to
be determined. Using the PEER NGA West 2 database and applying the following criteria
30 far-ﬁeld motions were selected.
Criteria for far-ﬁeld motions
• Peak ground acceleration (PGA) higher than 0.1 g
• Maximum four records per seismic event
• Epicentral distance of the recording station more than 25 km
• Acceleration proﬁles to account for lower and higher structural modes of the 10
MW DTU RWT
As mentioned, pulse-like motions are often much more devastating than motions
recorded far from the source. In recent years several studies concluded that the domi-
nant period, the shape of the pulses and amplitude are the critical characteristics of the
near-ﬁeld records. These features are mainly determined by the source properties, side
effects, and the location of the recording station. Another important aspect of the pulse-
like motions is the rupture during the earthquake event. When propagation velocity of
the rupture towards the site is close to the shear wave velocity, energy from the seismic
motion is represented by a large pulse at the beginning of record[39]. Considering the ori-
entation of the pulse to the fault plane, the pulse perpendicular to the fault plane contains
a higher energy pulse than the pulse parallel to the fault plane.
For the analysis, the method for determination of the near-ﬁeld or pulse-like motions
mentioned in the study by Chang et all.[31] was used. In the beginning, the records with
a dominant pulse are extracted form the PEER NGA West 2 database, then the pulse start
period and the pulse end period are found, by Peak Point Method. By subtracting the end

from the rest of the record and maximum energy of half cycle of the truncated series is

CHAPTER 6
Results and discussion
The time-domain analysis was conducted using the HAWC2 code. The wind turbine
structure was subjected to a combined effect of aerodynamic, hydrodynamic, and seismic
load. The main intention of the analysis was to investigate the impact of different types
of earthquake motion. In total, eight sets of simulations were run, containing operating
and parked stage of the wind turbine as well as far-ﬁeld, near-ﬁeld, soft-soil and dense
soil seismic events. The total duration of the simulation was 600s, wherein an earthquake
was implemented after the 50s to account for the inﬂuence of the transient state. The time
step of the analysis was set to 0.01s, which based on Figure (6.1) provides relatively good
accuracy and acceptable computational time.

6.1

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
The response of the 10 MW DTU reference wind turbine under concurrent loads was
studied using time domain analysis. For analysis areo-elastic-servo code HAWC2 was
used. The inﬂuence of aerodynamic, hydrodynamic, and seismic loads was presented
in the form of acceleration, displacement, and moment envelopes. These envelopes de-
scribed the impact of different wind speed and types of seismic motions on the behavior
of the wind turbine. The following section outlines important remarks found throughout
the project.
1. Inﬂuence of different wind speed
a) Operating Conditions
i. Maximum acceleration is obtained at 2/3 of the total height of the wind
turbine for all studied cases
ii. Highest acceleration is observed when pulse-like motions and motions
recorded in soft soil proﬁles are introduced in an earthquake alone case,
due to higher acceleration for lower and higher structural periods of the
wind turbine. This is generally followed by coupled loading where these
motions are present.
iii. Acceleration caused by combined load case with 24 m/s is closest to the
maximum acceleration
iv. Signiﬁcantly higher impact of the seismic motion is noticed compared to
the inﬂuence of the wind ﬂow, for the wind speeds studied in operating
conditions
v. Response signiﬁcantly inﬂuenced by wind forces is present by the dis-
placement envelopes

7.1
Further work
Wind turbine offers multiple engineering challenges. Considering the dynamic part of the
analysis, this study is mainly focused on the impact of different types of seismic motion.
However, several other areas of interest could have been found during the study, which
had to be left out due to the time frame of the project.
Regarding types of seismic motion, the impact of vertical seismic excitation can be
found interesting, due to structural demand on the joint between tower and shaft. One
of the limitations of this study was the use of a linear-elastic model. This can be sub-
stituted by using the non-linear model followed by the investigation of changes in the
==PAGE:124==
106
CHAPTER 7. CONCLUSION
stiffness properties of the material. Another limitation was the use of a ﬁxed base founda-
tion model; the inﬂuence of soil-structure interaction (SSI) could have been included in
time-domain analysis. Another phenomenon related to SSI and can jeopardize the wind
turbine is liquefaction, which occurs when excessive pore water pressure develops due
to intense seismic motion leading to the reduction of effective shear stresses of saturated
cohesionless soil.
The response of the wind turbine was reduced to the response of the main tower struc-
ture along with monopile. On the other hand, the investigation of the blade, the behavior
could reveal some critical information regarding blade roots. Blade root moment is con-
sidered crucial for wind engineers. Fragility analysis can provide valuable knowledge
regarding structural demand exceeding structural capacity.
To make an analysis more robust broader spectrum of wind velocities and seismic mo-
tions is required. The study is limited to regular wave case, although the irregular waves
are a more realistic interpretation of sea state. Related to wind and waves is the topic of
hydrodynamic and aerodynamic damping. However, the exact inﬂuence on the response
of the wind turbine is uncertain. Additionally, extreme environmental conditions, such as
the presence of hurricanes or tsunami, can be subject for further investigation.
