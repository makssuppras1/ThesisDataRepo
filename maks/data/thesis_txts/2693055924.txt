ABSTRACT 
Obesity is an continues increase health problem and it is of great interest to understand the under-
lying mechanism involved in the metabolic syndrome and obesity-associated comorbidities. It is 
hypothesized that the metabolic syndrome and the comorbidities are caused by a dysfunction in 
adipocyte functionality due to the obese state. This dysfunction in the adipose tissue has been as-
sociated with insulin resistance, type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, chronic lipotoxicity, in-
flammation, etc. Adipocytes stores energy as triglycerides and releases it as free fatty acids by lipol-
ysis for energy use. Furthermore, adipocytes release different adipokines, some with pro-inflam-
matory effects. These adipokines along with the increased amount of free fatty acids in the circula-
tion are a part of the unbalanced response to obesity. The deviant metabolism seen in obese adipose 
tissue contributes to systemic inflammation and the comorbidities. When investigating the progres-
sion and biological mechanisms related to the disease process it is very important to choose the 
right model. This project uses a porcine model to investigating gene expression of adipocyte be-
cause of the many anatomical and physiological similarities between pigs and humans. The basic 
gene expression of different pig breeds; Production pigs, Göttingen pigs and Ossabaw pigs was com-
pared and it is suggested that there is a much larger differences in Ossabaw pigs compared to the 
other pig breeds. Furthermore, in this project gene expression analysis was performed on adipose 
tissue from obese and lean Ossabaw pigs. The genes analysed was related to lipolysis and some 
inflammatory markers. It is suggested that there is a higher gene expression of lipolysis genes in 
visceral adipose tissue compared to subcutaneous adipose tissue. Additionally, it is hypothesized 
that there is a lower expression of lipolysis genes in fat tissue from obese Ossabaw pigs compared 
to lean Ossabaw pigs. Activation of inflammation-related genes in adipose tissue from the obese 
animals is suggested to be more highly expressed. The gene expression from the different breeds 
showed a higher activity of several genes in the Ossabaw pig and indicates an enhanced preparation 
to handle intracellular fat and macrophages. The majority of the genes relevant for the lipolytic 
activity showed a higher gene expression in the visceral adipose tissue. Additionally, obesity seems 
to affect the regulation of gene expression of the lipolysis enzymes, two of the lipolysis regulating 
receptors, leptin and Interferong. Only three genes showed higher expression in the subcutaneous 
adipose tissue. This indicates a higher lipolytic activity and higher amount of inflammation in the 
visceral adipose tissue and that the obese state affects the basic gene expression in both the visceral 
and subcutaneous adipose tissue. Another observation was that the obese pigs had an increase in 
the expression of leptin in both adipose tissues, just as observed in human obesity. The high expres-
sion of inflammatory markers in the visceral adipose tissue could be related to the increased 
amount of leptin during obesity.

Chapter 1 
SCOPE & INTRODUCTION  
1.1 Motivation  
Obesity is a continuous increasing global health problem [1], it has been estimated that by the year 
2030, 45% of the United States population and 48% in the United Kingdom will be defined as obese 
[2]. Obese individuals have a major risk for developing a wide range of diseases and unhealthy con-
ditions such as type 2 diabetes (T2D), insulin resistance, cardiovascular diseases, gastrointestinal 
disorders, respiratory problems, muscular disorders, physical issues, and cancer [3]–[5]. All these 
risks associated with obesity are called comorbidities and are defined as the metabolic syndrome 
(MetS) [3], [4]. Obesity is seen in both adults, children, and the elderly and affects both men and 
women. The amount of people being obese is increasing in both high- and low-income countries. 
The increasing number of individuals suffering from obesity is a worldwide concern, furthermore, 
the increase in obesity rates is both a health and economic burden for every country [5]. It is nec-
essary to understand the underlying mechanism involved in the MetS for further therapy and treat-
ment [6] and to help obese individuals with losing weight and help them maintain the weight loss 
[7]. The biology and complexity behind MetS are still not well known and it is essential to under-
stand the pathophysiological state of these conditions to help this global problem [1]. The type of 
animal model chosen for the research is very important when investigating even basic aspects of 
the metabolism and development of obesity. The traceability of the results relies on the type of 
model used in the research and experiments [8]. When looking at the pathogenesis of metabolic 
disorders, one will see there is a lack of big animal models. The type of animal model used for the 
investigation of the MetS and the disease development is very important because it needs to mimic 
human biology and its complexity. Especially mice have been used, but they do not represent hu-
man biology as well as for instance farm animals [9]. Most commonly used models are the rodent 
models, which are very useful, as they are inexpensive. But they differ in biology and their metabolic 
mechanisms when compared to humans; therefore, a better and more accurate animal model is 
needed [10]. 
1.2 Big Animal Model 
It is necessary to choose the right type of model that is biologically accurate when studying pro-
gression and biological mechanisms related to the disease process. Porcine models are an excellent 
model for investigating human biology because of their genetic, anatomical, physiological, and 
==PAGE:11==
 
11 
importantly metabolic similarities to humans [10]. There is a gap between rodents and humans 
when looking at anatomy and physiology, and a porcine model would be a much better choice for 
the research. Pigs are much more similar to humans and give more precious insight into the patho-
physiology of the MetS and obesity [10], [11]. Pigs’ organs are anatomically very similar to humans, 
and the same applies to their sensory innervation and blood supply, which also means that pigs are 
susceptible to infections that are very close or identical to species that infect humans [12], [13]. 
There are mostly advantages in using big animal models, with only a few challenges (figure 1) [10].  
 
Figure 1: An overview of the advantages and disadvantages with a big animal model [10]. 
The use of multiple animal models for research can contribute to the translational line, however, 
the models that mimic human disease, such as porcine models, are very much lacking in the re-
search today. Pigs as models for human disease have the potential to integrate and translate into 
human medicine, thereby connecting the gap between simpler animal models (e.g., rodents) and 
humans. There is a requirement for safer testing in larger animals before studies enter into human 
clinical testing. There has been a lot of success of therapy in mice, but despite that, pig models seem 
to be more correct to predict failure and success in humans. Pigs, especially Ossabaw miniature 
pigs, are very important for research because they mimic humans across a broader spectrum from 
healthy to disease states. The complications connected to the MetS both long- and short-term need 
a more complex model for investigating the underlying mechanisms. Ossabaw pigs summarize 
some of the pathogenesis of MetS and T2D due to their genotype and tendency to develop obesity. 
The Ossabaw pig has a very high amount of fat stores compared to other mammals, and they mimic 
the development from relatively insulin sensitive to insulin resistant and T2D [14], [15].

1.3 Objectives & Outline  
For this project the main goal is to investigate and characterise adipose functionality in a big animal 
model of obesity with focus on characterization of the basic expression of genes important for adi-
pocyte function in adipose tissue of the Ossabaw pig. This is a study of adipocytes from a total of 10 
obese and 12 lean Ossabaw miniature pigs, along with 14 lean production pigs and 4 lean Göttingen 
pigs. The adipose tissue from the pigs is characterised with respect to gene expression. The porcine 
model is the chosen model for investigating the adipocyte functionality because of their similarities 
to humans [10]. The overall aim is to investigate adipocyte functionality in relation to obesity and 
the MetS. This project focus on gene expression of important factors for adipocytes functionality in 
relation to lipolysis along with some inflammatory markers in obese and lean pigs. It is hypothe-
sized that there is a higher expression of lipolysis genes in visceral adipose tissue compared to sub-
cutaneous adipose tissue, and a lower expression of lipolysis genes in fat from fat pigs versus lean 
pigs. Furthermore, that there is more activation of inflammation-related genes in adipose tissue 
from obese pigs compared to lean pigs. Additionally, Ossabaw pig is expected to be the best porcine 
model when studying healthy to disease states. The function of the adipose tissue is essential to 
know for the understanding of the complexity behind the disease development during obesity [1], 
[6]. It has been suggested that a dysfunction in the positive or negative energy balance is linked to 
the development of the MetS [7], [16] and it is hypothesized that the metabolism of the adipocytes 
is related to the obese state and the aberrant metabolism of the adipose tissue affects the systemic 
inflammation supporting the development of obesity-associated co-morbidities [17], [18]. 
 
This project includes analysis of gene expression by high throughput real-time quantitative poly-
merase chain reaction (qPCR). The qPCR analysis is used to determine the gene expression of se-
lected genes important for the lipolysis and inflammatory markers from both visceral and subcuta-
neous adipose tissue and is comparted between obese and lean Ossabaw pigs, and between the 
different breeds of pigs. The gene expression analysis includes 33 genes but with the main focus on 
20 genes of interest, 15 genes involved in the adipocyte functionality and 5 inflammatory markers.

BACKGROUND  
2.1 Obesity & Metabolic Syndrome  
Obesity and its associated risk factors, the MetS, is a continuous increasing health problem world-
wide. In the last decades there has been a higher focus on the biology behind the MetS, but the 
understanding of the precise mechanisms involved is still unclear. The pathophysiological state of 
obesity is very complex, it seems to arise primarily from the imbalance in the energy intake and 
energy expenditure but is also suggested to be affected by the genetics and lifestyle of the individual 
[1]. Obese individuals will have a rise in body fat giving them a body mass index (BMI) higher than 
“normal” [2]. The World Health Organization (WHO) has defined overweight and obesity as abnor-
mal fat accumulation that results in risks to the health. Individuals with BMI ³ 25 kg/m2 are most 
often considered overweight and obese individuals are considered to have a BMI ³ 30 kg/m2 [3]. 
There seems to be a link between the biological processes in the development of obesity and the 
MetS in relation to the fat cells; the adipocyte functionality and adipocyte tissue expandability. For 
a long time, fat tissue was seen only as a storage organ, but now it is known as a very active organ 
containing adipocytes that produce and secrete hormones and cytokines, which is important for the 
metabolism [19]. Adipocytes releases several different cytokines also called adipokines, some with 
pro-inflammatory effects being part of an unbalanced response in obesity [20]. The clarification of 
the underlying mechanism causing the co-morbidities of the MetS is lacking and is limited by the 
available animal model of obesity. A study by Pawar et al., 2015 has shown how high-carbohydrate 
and -fat diet resulted in obesity, hypertension, high level of triglycerides and insulin resistance in 
domestic pigs, which is some of the most common features of the MetS in humans [9].  
 
Obesity is characterized as an abnormal and extreme amount of fat involving an accumulating num-
ber of adipocytes [3]. The adipocytes work as storage for energy, the size passively reflecting the 
positive or negative energy balance; the difference between energy intake and energy expenditure. 
Some claim that obese people have themself to blame for their weight because of the positive energy 
balance where they overeat and are less physically active than others, thereby increasing their body 
mass, but there are several studies that state that this is false. Studies with overfeeding show that 
the increase in body fat mass, because of excessive energy intake, exceeds the amount of the posi-
tive energy balance during obesity development. They also show that the body composition is 
==PAGE:14==
 
14 
restored afterward when the overfeeding is stopped. Most obese individuals trying to lose weight 
and maintaining a negative energy balance end up regaining the body weight. It is a huge challenge 
to identify the biological mechanisms that drive and alters the persistent body composition [7]. 
Obese individuals frequently have a dysfunctional metabolism and dysfunction in adipocyte func-
tionality [4]. A dysfunctionality in the adipose tissue has been speculated to be a huge factor in the 
decrease in insulin sensitivity in obese subjects [21], and the visceral adipose tissue may be linked 
to cardiovascular risk factors through stimulation of the MetS [22]. The metabolic abnormalities 
and co-morbidities related to the MetS (figure 2) among others include ectopic fat deposition, im-
paired glucose tolerance, dyslipidemia, hypertension, T2D, cardiovascular diseases, gastrointesti-
nal disorders, respiratory problems, and muscular disorders [3], [7].  
 
Figure 2: An overview of some of the metabolic abnormalities and co-morbidities associated with the metabolic syn-
drome [3], [7].   
When being obese the fat storage capacity will be exhausted with the consequence of chronic lipo-
toxicity and inflammation [7]. This is seen by elevated levels of non-esterified fatty acids (NEFA) in 
the circulation and pro-inflammatory factors such as interleukin (IL) 1b, IL-6, tumor necrosis factor 
a (TNFa), and infiltrating immune cells leading to low-chronic inflammation. This low-chronic in-
flammation associated with obesity is suggested to be a major contributing factor for the develop-
ment of insulin resistance [3]. Increased NEFAs in the circulation increase the production of hepatic 
glucose production by inhibiting suppression of gluconeogenesis mediated by insulin [22]. Obesity 
leads to an increase in NEFAs to the blood circulation, these NEFAs are a result of lipolysis where 
triglycerides that are stored in lipid-droplets in adipocyte cells, are broken down and released as 
NEFAs and glycerol [23], [24]. A study by Arner & Rydén, 2015 investigated the relationship be-
tween circulating NEFAs, glycerol, and insulin resistance in obese and non-obese individuals. The 
study showed the fasting level of NEFAs and glycerol was much higher in obese individual com-
pared to non-obese. They also found that there was a linear relationship between BMI, fat mass, 
and the levels of NEFAs and glycerol in the circulation. They conclude that the increased amount of 
==PAGE:15==
 
15 
lipids released from adipocytes is an important factor in the elevated levels of NEFAs and glycerol 
in the circulation in obese individuals [23].  
2.1.1 Obesity in Big Animal Model  
When working with big animal models the use of pigs is an obvious choice. Minipigs such as Ossa-
baw, Yucatan, or Göttingen are the most used lines because they can be brought up to adulthood at 
a reasonable cost [10], [14]. Obesity in pigs is mostly induced by high-energy high-fat/high-carbo-
hydrate diets. This promotes elevated levels of cholesterol, LDL and HDL cholesterol, and dyslipi-
daemia and impaired glucose tolerance, which is all observed in obese mini pigs, but they do not 
develop a diabetic phenotype, this requires gene manipulation. This can be done by manipulation 
of the b-cell function by genetic modification. Furthermore, studies have shown how pigs might 
function as donors of b-cell replacement therapies of insulin-dependent diabetes [10]. Most of what 
is known about obesity and MetS is based on studies from rodents and humans. The step from the 
research of rodents to humans is not optimal because rodents such as mice differ both in anatomical 
and physiological aspects compared to humans [10], [14], [25]. Research of adipose function and 
adipokines have been performed on mice and on humans and the results are very different. An ex-
ample is the protein adipsin that is produced by adipocytes. It was shown that the release of adipsin 
was decreased in obese mice, but when performed in humans an increase in the release of adipsin 
was seen. Another example is TNFa that in obese mice is released into the circulation while in hu-
mans it is a local factor within the adipose tissue [25]. Pigs are similar to humans in multiple ways 
including lipoprotein profiles, adipose tissue composition and gastrointestinal tract anatomy and 
physiology. These similarities in adipose tissue composition are very important when investigating 
obesity and the MetS. When Ossabaw mini-pigs are fed a high-energy diet containing a high amount 
of fat and carbohydrate they develop the natural feature of the MetS. Just like humans, they develop 
visceral obesity, glucose intolerance, hypertension, dyslipidemia, etc. Ossabaw pigs are that mam-
mal who has the highest total of body lipids and develops MetS more rapidly than other pig models 
[26] and both morphological and biological remodelling of adipose tissue during obesity has been 
observed. Furthermore, an increase in the size of adipose cells and in M1 macrophage markers was 
seen in Ossabaw pigs, and there was an elevation in systemic levels and increased expression from 
adipocytes of inflammatory cytokines such as TNFa [9]. The development of obesity and metabolic 
syndrome involves many factors, therefore Ossabaw pig is very suited as an animal model for stud-
ying the effects of obesity and the MetS [26].

2.2 Subcutaneous & Visceral Adipose tissue 
Fat accumulation can happen at two different areas in the body, in the subcutaneous adipose tissue 
and visceral adipose tissue. The visceral fat is present around the gastric viscera in the mesentery 
and around the abdominal organs, and it is very different from the fat in the subcutaneous areas. 
The visceral adipocytes have more metabolic activity and are more sensitive to lipolysis than adi-
pocytes in the subcutaneous adipose tissue, which is much more active in the absorption of triglyc-
erides and free fatty acids from the circulation [27]. The visceral adipocyte tissue is hypothesized 
to be an important component in MetS and the associated disease development [22], and it is sug-
gested to be connected to alterations of cardiac-associated structures [22], [28]. The endocrine 
function along with lipolytic activity and insulin response differ between the visceral adipose tissue 
and the subcutaneous adipose tissue; visceral fat secrets a higher amount of lipids, adipokines, and 
pro-inflammatory factors than the subcutaneous adipose tissue  [27], [28]. There are several stud-
ies that suggest an association between accumulation of the visceral adipose tissue and the co-mor-
bidities of the MetS shown by the removal of visceral fat compartments that have beneficial meta-
bolic effect in animals and in humans. In addition, that removal of subcutaneous adipose tissue did 
not have an impact on the risk factors of obesity. It is hypothesized that the function, not the fat 
mass, of visceral adipose tissue, is connected to cardiovascular risk factors [22] and the increase in 
visceral fat is linked to an increase in insulin resistance and a higher risk of developing T2D [19]. 
The action of insulin has been shown to increase or be unchanged in the subcutaneous adipocytes 
while the visceral adipocytes have a higher glucose uptake stimulated by insulin. A study by Fawcett 
et al., 2010 has shown that some genes, such as fatty acid-binding protein, are more highly ex-
pressed in visceral adipose tissue compared to subcutaneous adipose tissue, while other genes are 
more expressed in the subcutaneous adipose tissue than the visceral adipose tissue such as leptin 
[19]. Leptin secreted from adipocytes have been linked to insulin stimulation and glucose uptake. 
Lipolysis has been seen to affect leptin expression as well as exposure to free fatty acids, each of 
which indicates a state of energy mobilization [29]. The visceral adipose tissue contains many in-
flammatory cell types [19] and has been shown to express more genes encoding pro-inflammatory 
cytokines than the subcutaneous adipose tissue [20]. This is a potential factor for the development 
of obesity-related risks because of increased inflammatory response. It has been observed that NE-
FAs released to the circulation is exposed to the liver and it can lead to an increase in the inflamma-
tory response of the liver [19].  
2.3 Adipocyte functionality  
Adipocytes function as the body’s fat storage depot and are the one type of cell that almost unlim-
ited can increase in number. They also work as endocrine cells and secret signalling proteins 
==PAGE:17==
 
17 
affecting functions on the muscles, liver, etc [30]. There are mainly three different types of adipo-
cytes illustrated in figure 3. They are characterized as white adipocytes, brown adipocytes, and 
beige adipocytes. The white adipocytes differ morphologically from the other two adipocyte types 
by storing fat from food intake for energy use. The white adipocyte tissue is the main site for the 
storage of the fatty acids in lipid-droplets. The two other types of adipocyte tissue are mostly used 
for thermogenesis. They have fewer and smaller lipid-droplets and a higher number of mitochon-
dria. Furthermore, they contain an uncoupling protein-1 (UCP-1), which allows the use of the en-
ergy from the fatty acids to generate heat [1], [24].  
 
 
Figure 3: The white adipose cell functions as an endocrine organ; it contains a low number of mitochondria and a 
lipid-droplet for storage of triglycerides. The beige adipose cell mainly functions as regulator of thermogenesis; it 
contains a higher number of mitochondria and several lipid-droplets. The brown adipose cell functions as regulator 
of thermogenesis; it contains a very high number of mitochondria and many lipid-droplets [31]. 
The primary function of adipocytes is to control energy homeostasis [32]. Adipocytes together with 
several adipokines are known to be metabolic active and to secrete different hormones that help 
regulate appetite, satiety, and energy metabolism [1], [24]. These adipokines affect the metabolism 
in other tissues as well as effecting neuroendocrine pathways [24]. Fat as triglycerides is accumu-
lated and stored in adipose tissue and serves as a very important biological process where the tri-
glycerides are reserved as energy for future use. The dynamic of adipocyte functionality is deter-
mined by the balance of uptake and synthesis of fatty acids along with the release of the fatty acids. 
The number of adipocytes, the development of new adipocytes, and the apoptosis of current adipo-
cytes are all factors affecting the functionality of the adipocyte tissue. It is very difficult to under-
stand the balance of fat dynamics. Single elements of the processes regarding the adipocyte function 
and development are well-known knowledge, but the regulation and interplay with the rest of the 
body are not well understood [7]. The fat is stored in concentrated form as triglycerides in large 
lipid-droplets in the adipocyte cell. When the body experiences energy need, the triglycerides are 
hydrolysed by different lipases, which results in the release of NEFAs to the circulation where it is 
transported to tissue to be oxidized in mitochondria and converted into energy [20], [24]. The fat 
==PAGE:18==
 
18 
mass of the body is defined by the balance of storage and removal of triglycerides in the adipocytes 
and the size of adipocytes differs between individuals. A study by Arner et al., 2011 determines that 
it is most unlikely that the rate of triglyceride removal itself has an impact on the adipocyte size. 
Their data suggested that the exchange of lipids between the adipocytes is not related to the size of 
the cells. They suggest that it is a combination of high storage and low lipid removal rate that gives 
a triglyceride accumulation in the adipocyte tissue resulting in excessive body fat mass. Further-
more, the turnover of the triglycerides may also be a factor involved in for instance the overall in-
sulin effects and that lipolysis could determine the lipids turnover in adipocytes [33].  
 
Adipocytes secrete adipokines that both, directly and indirectly, regulates the metabolism. Exam-
ples of these types of adipokines are monocyte chemoattractant protein-1/chemokine ligand-2 
(MCP-1/CCL2) and TNFa, which are involved in the inflammatory response in adipose tissue. This 
adipokine-mediated inflammatory response modulates the metabolism, lipolysis, and lipogenesis 
in the adipocytes [24]. A study by Pawer et al., 2015 showed that in a domestic pig model there was 
an elevated expression of MCP-1 in subcutaneous adipose tissue and an increase in inflammatory 
macrophages [9]. Adipocytes sense energy storage as a cell-autonomous way where hormones such 
as leptin are secreted, thereby initiating a feedback loop where food intake is reduced and the sym-
pathetic nervous system is activated. Furthermore, adrenaline and noradrenaline are released, 
which increases lipolysis and the thermogenic process. This shows how adipocytes control both 
storage and the metabolization of energy by a closed-loop brain-adipocyte connection. There are 
several other factors secreted by adipocytes that have an important effect on systemic energy me-
tabolism, this includes adiponectin, fibroblast growth factor 21, and inflammatory proteins. When 
there is a continuous positive energy balance, the closed-loop brain-adipocyte connection is pushed 
to over its limitation, thereby ending in hypersecretion of insulin, leptin, catecholamines, and sev-
eral other hormones [32].   
2.3.1 Lipolysis   
Lipolysis is the process where fatty acids stored in adipocytes are released as NEFAs [34]. The fatty 
acid is stored as triglycerides and broken down into NEFAs and glycerol by different lipases and 
then released into the circulation [23], [24]. The hydrolysis process of triglycerides is regulated by 
different lipases such as hormone sensitive lipase (HSL). The NEFAs are mainly transported to the 
liver, muscle, and heart. The control of the lipid flux in the body can result in abnormal regulation 
when obese, leading to acute and chronic elevation of NEFAs in the plasma [35]. Lipolysis is con-
trolled by hormonal regulation. One of the main factors in this regulation is catecholamines. They 
play an important role because of their acute lipolytic effect. In humans, adipocytes are stimulated 
==PAGE:19==
 
19 
by catecholamines through b1-, b2, and b3-adrenoceptors, and they are inhibited through the a2-
adrenoceptor [34], [36]. The b1-3-adrenoceptors control the intracellular 3',5'-cyclic adenosine 
monophosphate (cAMP) concentration and thereby activates cAMP dependent protein kinase, pro-
tein kinase A (PKA), which phosphorylates perilipin and HSL leading to degradation of triglyceride 
from lipid-droplets. Perilipin also promotes a/b-hydrolase domain containing protein 5 (ABHD5) 
activity, which activates adipose triglyceride lipase (ATGL) necessary for facilitating the hydrolysis 
[35], [37]. A simple illustration is shown in figure 4.  
 
Figure 4: Catecholamines binds theb1-3-adrenoceptor, which leads to a change in the concentration of cAMP and 
the activation of PKA. PKA phosphorylates Perilipin and HSL leading to breakdown of triglycerides. Perilipin pro-
motes activation of ABHD5 that binds and activates ATGL resulting in the release of NEFAs and glycerol to the circu-
lation [35]. 
Catecholamines such as adrenaline and noradrenaline along with insulin act as regulators of lipol-
ysis, but natriuretic peptides (NPs) are also shown to have a strong lipolytic and lipid-mobilizing 
effect. In mammalians, three main NPs have been identified atrial natriuretic peptides (ANP), B-
type natriuretic peptides (BNP), and C- type natriuretic peptides (CNP). Their effect is more wide-
spread, but they are mainly involved in the regulation of endothelial permeability, blood pressure, 
and intravascular volume. The NPs bind the NP receptor, guanylyl cyclase-linked receptors (NPRs), 
and therethrough mediate their function, which is illustrated in figure 5. NPR-A mainly binds ANP 
and BNP, where NPR-B binds the CNPs. The NPR-C binds all the NPs [35], [38]. The lipolysis 
==PAGE:20==
 
20 
stimulated by NPs has been shown to promote an increase in intracellular guanosine 30,50-cyclic 
monophosphate (cGMP) through the NPR-A and NPR-B. When activated the pathway relies on in-
tracellular cGMP kinetics. The downstream effector of cGMP, the cGMP-dependent kinase 1 (cGK-
1) also known as protein kinase G (PKG) promotes perilipin and HSL phosphorylation and thereby 
induces lipolysis. NPR-C differs in its function by playing a role in the removal of the NPs from the 
circulation and regulate the concentration of NPs in local tissue [35], [38].  
 
Figure 5: NPs binds an NPR-A or NPR-B leading to an increase in cGMP that activates PKG. PKG promotes phosphor-
ylation of Perilipin and HSL resulting in breakdown of triglycerides. Perilipin promotes activation of ABHD5 that 
binds and activates ATGL leading to the release of NEFAs and glycerol to the circulation [35]. 
NEFAs and glycerol are released to the circulation by the relocation of adipocyte lipid-binding pro-
tein (ALBP) to the HSL, which then promotes the efflux of NEFA by the monoacylglycerol lipase 
(MGL), whereas glycerol is being channelled out of the adipocyte by aquaporin-7 (AQP-7), which is 
a water glycerol transporter that is located in the plasma membrane (see figure 7) [35].  
 
Fatty Acid-Binding Protein (FABP) has been suggested to actively facilitate the transport of fatty 
acids to different organelles in the cell. FABP4 is mainly expressed in adipose tissue and macro-
phages and is crucial for the intracellular transport of fatty acids. It has been suggested that FABP4 
activates HSL and thereby regulates lipolysis [39], [40]. It has been shown in a mouse model that 
FABP4-deficient adipocytes resulted in reduced efficiency of the lipolysis [40]. Furthermore, a high 
==PAGE:21==
 
21 
rate of catecholamine-induced lipolysis was found to be related to dysglycemia, insulin resistance, 
and increased blood pressure in visceral adipocyte tissue, this relation was not seen in subcutane-
ous adipocyte tissue. It has been suggested that catecholamine-induced visceral adipose lipolysis 
has a pathogenic role in the cardiovascular risk factor development in relation to obesity [22]. Oth-
ers have also shown that the effect of catecholamines on the lipolytic action is reduced in the sub-
cutaneous adipose tissue of obese individuals. Catecholamine resistance has been linked to an in-
crease in a2-adrenoceptor activity and a decrease in β2-adrenoreceptor expression and action, and 
a low expression of HSL, thereby decreasing the maximum effect of catecholamines [36]. Both cat-
echolamines and NPs are important for the control of lipid mobilization and the release of NEFAs 
from adipose tissue and these pathways are important for the understanding of the disease devel-
opment related to obesity. There is a limitation in the research related to the effects of NPs because 
ANP-induced lipolysis does not work in rodents and a more suitable animal model is needed [35]. 
A porcine model will be an obvious choice for further research of the NP induced lipolytic pathway 
[10], [14] and will help to understand the exact mechanism behind the lipolysis and to understand 
the NP induced lipolytic activity in the development of the comorbidities of the MetS [41].  
2.3.2 Lipogenesis  
Lipogenesis is the process of fatty acid synthesis as a response to the diet intake, and it takes place 
both in the liver and the adipocytes [42]. It is considered a contributor to the maintenances of tri-
glyceride homeostasis [16]. Lipogenesis is a metabolic pathway where carbohydrates are synthe-
sized into fatty acids, which subsequently are stored in lipid-droplets in the adipocytes [23], [24], 
[43]. Diet containing carbohydrates are stimulators of lipogenesis both in the liver and adipocytes 
while fasting only leads to reduced lipogenesis in the adipocyte tissue. A high plasma glucose level 
stimulates lipogenesis by glucose being converted into acetyl-CoA and thereby promoting fatty acid 
synthesis. Importantly glucose also promotes an increase in lipogenesis by stimulating the release 
of insulin and at the same time inhibit the release of glucagon from the pancreas. The increase of 
glucose uptake in adipocytes happens by the recruitment of glucose transporters to the plasma 
membrane along with activation of lipogenic and glycolytic enzymes. These effects are carried out 
by insulin binding to the insulin receptors on the cell surface leading to activation of tyrosine kinase 
activity [42]. Insulin affects the expression of lipogenic genes. Insulin controls this through sterol 
regulatory element binding protin-1 (SREBP-1) in adipocytes. SREBP-1 is activated by insulin both 
transcriptionally and post-translationally, and insulin mediates its export from the endoplasmic re-
ticulum to the nucleus so that it can bind to sterol regulatory elements to promote the target genes 
[16].  
 
==PAGE:22==
 
22 
During the lipogenesis glucose is taken up by the cell through a glucose transporter where glucose 
enters the glycolysis where pyruvate is generated. Pyruvate is transported into the mitochondria 
where it enters the tricarboxylic acid cycle (TCA) where citrate is produced. Citrate is released from 
the mitochondria to the cytosol where it is converted into acetyl-CoA by the enzyme ATP-citrate 
lyase (ACLY). Furthermore, the fatty acid synthesis starts by converting the acetyl-CoA into malo-
nyl-CoA by the Acetyl-CoA carboxylase (ACACA) and then the biosynthetic enzyme fatty acid syn-
thase (FASN) synthesis the malonyl-CoA into palmitate followed by a series of reactions giving fatty 
acids [16]. This is illustrated in figure 6. Lipogenesis is a very complex metabolic pathway that is 
highly regulated and dysregulation in these mechanisms can lead to major health risks [43].   
 
Figure 6: Glucose is transported in to the adipocyte by a glucose transporter. Glucose is converted into pyruvate by 
glycolysis. Pyruvate is transported into mitochondria and enters the TCA cycle giving citrate that is released to the 
cytosol. ACLY converts citrate into acetyl-CoA that enters the fatty acid synthesis. Acetyl-CoA is converted into malo-
nyl-CoA by ACACA. Then FASN converts malonyl-CoA into palmitate and after several more reactions it provides 
fatty acids [16]. 
2.4 Insulin Response in Adipocytes 
Insulin resistance induced by obesity is a major factor in the risk development of T2D and cardio-
vascular disease [44]. By the year 2030, the global number of cases of T2D is meant to reach 350 
million [24]. Insulin resistance has been defined as the failure to maintain a normal physiological 
response to insulin [32]. Insulin receptor substrate (IRS) mediates the function upon binding of 
==PAGE:23==
 
23 
insulin to the insulin receptor. The IRS protein is identified in various mammalian species. IRS-1 is 
dominant in insulin signalling in skeletal muscle and adipocytes. IRS-2 seems to have a compensat-
ing activity for impaired insulin action on IRS-1. The function of IRS-3 has yet to be investigated in 
humans and it seems that humans lack a functional IRS3 gene. The role of IRS-3 in a mouse muscle 
model suggests that that IRS-3 mediates insulin action when IRS-1 is lacking in functionality [45]. 
The insulin receptor is a part of the tyrosine kinase receptor family. When the insulin receptor is 
stimulated by insulin, IRS-1 activates phosphoinositol-3-kinase (PI3-K) signalling that increases 
Phosphatidylinositol-(3,4,5)-trisphosphate (PiP3) action, leading to activation of serine/threonine 
protein kinase (AKT) also called protein kinase B (PKB), which then activates phosphodiesterase 
3B (PDE-3B). PDE-3B converts the cAMP into adenosine 5’-phosphate (5’-AMP), which then coun-
teracts the lipolytic effect of catecholamines but it is not that of natriuretic peptides [41], [46]. Fig-
ure 7 shows the pathway of IRS-1 upon stimulation of insulin to the insulin receptor and its effect 
on the catecholamine stimulated pathways.  
 
Figure 7: Control of adipocyte lipolysis. The pathway of catecholamines through b1-3 adrenoceptor, for atrial natri-
uretic peptides thorough the NP receptor (NPR A) and for insulin through insulin receptors. Intracellular cAMP is 
regulated by catecholamines through b-adrenoceptor-mediated adenylyl cyclase (AC) activation, and through insu-

regulation of catecholamine stimulated lipolysis. Atrial and B-type natriuretic peptides (ANP and BNP) stimulate

induces alteration of lipid-droplet that facilitates the action of HSL on triglyceride hydrolysis. Perilipin phosphoryla-

translocation of HSL from the cytosol to the surface of the lipid-droplet. PKA and PKG (cGK-I) phosphorylates sev-
eral other substrates, which also influence the secretion of various molecules from adipocytes. Upon binding of insu-

binding protein), Gs (stimulatory GTP-binding protein)) [35].

It is known that insulin stimulates glucose uptake and glucose synthesis; it is an important factor of 
lipogenesis in adipocytes. In obese subjects, the function of insulin is reduced meaning that insulin 
has a lower ability to stimulate glucose transport and lipogenesis [36]. The glucose concentration 
in the circulation is dependent on the effect of insulin [47]. Insulin increases the uptake of glucose 
in adipocytes by intracellular localization of glucose transporter type 4 (GLUT4) from the cytosol 
of the adipocyte to the plasma membrane. GLUT4 is the main glucose transporter in the insulin-
regulated transport of glucose. It is around 10% of the whole-body insulin-stimulated glucose up-
take that takes place in the adipose tissue. It is shown that the glucose uptake is significantly differ-
ent in the subcutaneous adipose tissue compared to the visceral adipose tissue [48]. There are sev-
eral hypotheses of how obesity related insulin resistance is involved in the development of co-mor-
bidities of the MetS [24]. In several studies with Ossabaw pigs, it has been shown that the develop-
ment of insulin resistance when being obese is linked to the development of the co-morbidities of 
the MetS [49]. It is hypothesized that the level of NEFAs mediates insulin resistance. This is sup-
ported by being demonstrated in both animals and humans where elevated levels of circulating 
NEFAs caused peripheral insulin resistance. The effect and deposition of circulating NEFAs seem to 
be contributing to the insulin resistance with the deposition being into non-adipose cells such as 
muscle. This supports the hypothesis that NEFAs released to the circulation and the increased up-
take of triglycerides into non-adipocyte cells leads to insulin resistance rather than excess body fat 
by itself [24]. Another hypothesis is that the increase in lipolysis may also modulate the adipocyte 
secretion of adipokines that may favour inflammation in the adipose tissue. The increase of circu-
lating NEFAs leads to impermanent insulin resistance thereby accumulating fatty acids in the liver 
and skeletal muscle tissue. Furthermore, this lipid accumulation and insulin resistance are consid-
ered to be a state of lipotoxicity [48]. It is suggested that inflammation is directly linked to the de-
velopment of insulin resistance, as it has been seen that obese individuals with insulin resistance 
express a high degree of inflammation in adipose tissue, where non-obese individuals remain insu-
lin sensitive and do not express adipose tissue inflammation [50], [51]. The main factors suggested 
to promote insulin resistance are the cytokines TNFa and IL-1b [32]. 
2.5 Inflammation  
Obesity has been associated with inflammation and has been seen in correlation with insulin re-
sistance and T2D; it has been suggested that co-morbidities of MetS are linked to low-grade chronic 
inflammation. The state of chronic inflammation most often is present in three stages illustrated in 
figure 8. It is triggered by stress and factors related to cell stress followed by an acute adaptive 
inflammatory response and a last a long-term maladaptive phase that results in complications. In 
obesity, this response could be triggered by homeostatic stress from a positive energy balance in 
==PAGE:25==
 
25 
adipocytes that responds by releasing chemokines. The complications of this type of response can 
result in reduced metabolic flexibility, insulin and catecholamine resistance, etc. [32].  
 
Figure 8: In obesity the chronic inflammation is triggered by homeostatic stress because of the positive energy bal-
ance, which gives rise to a physiological adaptive response. After some time, the adaptive response develops into a 
maladaptive response that has pathological effects. Over time the system will try to re-establish homeostasis. This is 
with new set point for factors such as weight, blood-sugar level, insulin and other hormones leading to the patholog-
ical effects of long-term obesity [32]. 
Macrophages found in the adipocyte tissue have an impact on energy metabolism, such as the pro-
inflammatory M1 macrophages that have shown to promote steatosis and adipogenesis [1]. Re-
search have shown that IFNg is related to the development of insulin resistance and is associated to 
M1-cytokine expression from macrophages. It is known that IFNg is a key regulator of the macro-
phage function [52]. It is furthermore seen that regulatory Cd8+ T-effector cells produce IFNg, and 
it is also suggested to have an important role in adipose tissue inflammation induced by obesity 
[32]. Both macrophages and adipocytes secrets MCP-1, TNFa and IL-1b, which affects the adipocyte 
function by increasing lipolysis and decrease the triglyceride synthesis. This leads to the known 
state of increased NEFAs in the circulation [24]. Several studies have shown that an oversaturated 
level of NEFAs to the circulation has a direct impact on pro-inflammatory macrophages through the 
Toll-like receptors (TLR), a part of the pattern recognition receptor (PRR) family, resulting in a 
worsened adipose tissue inflammation [48]. NEFA induces inflammation upon binding to the TLR4 
and TLR2, which then promotes nuclear factor-kB (NF-kB) signalling that leads to secretion of 
chemokines such as MCP-1 by the adipocytes. It is shown that both TLR4 and TLR2 are more highly 
expressed in individuals with obesity [32]. With obesity, there will be an accumulation of dead ad-
ipocytes. These dead and dying adipocytes sends different signals that contribute to inflammation 
in the adipose tissue by recruiting macrophages from the blood to the adipose tissue leading to an 
increased number of macrophages in the adipose tissue. Other PRR receptors, the NOD-like recep-
tors (NLR), also sends signals induced by obesity. The stressed and dying adipose cells activate the 
NLRs that mobilize leukocytes to the tissue and limit the damage. NLRs in macrophage activates the 
NALP3 inflammasome, known as cryopyrin, which promotes the production of IL-1b and IL-18 by 
caspase-1. It is shown that obesity also induces the production of caspase-1 and IL-1b in adipose 
tissue. Studies with mice have shown that Nlrp-3- and Casp-1-deficient mice are resistant to obesity-
induced inflammation. In obese individuals, the inflammation in the adipose tissue is caused by a 
higher proportion of pro-inflammatory macrophages rather than an increase in inflammation in 
==PAGE:26==
 
26 
each macrophage [32]. Further, there is a crosstalk between the metabolism pathway and inflam-
mation. This can be seen with the lipid sensitive nuclear receptor PPARg, which is activated by mac-
rophages from adipose tissue, which is suggested to limit inflammation, but also continue to keep 
adipose tissue in a state of chronic low-grade inflammation [32], [53], [54]. The inflammatory re-
sponse to obesity can have both beneficial but mostly harmful effects depending on many different 
factors. There is a need to determine the more precise and crucial molecular events connected to 
metabolism and the inflammatory state [32]. 
 
The protein Serum Amyloid A (SAA) has shown to be more expressed in adipocytes from obese 
humans and a potential factor in the obesity related inflammation [55]. It is an acute-phase protein 
that is induced my obesity and inflammation [56]. It has been suggested that SAA may be directly 
linked to the cholesterol metabolism and seems to have lipolytic activity [55]. With increased SAA 
production from adipocytes limits the triacylglycerol accumulation leading to increased release of 
NEFAs into the circulation, which may be a contributing factor to insulin resistance during the obese 
state. Furthermore, an interaction between SAA and HDL seams to facilitate degradation of HDL-
cholesterol leading to dyslipidemia [55], [57]. Studies have also shown that SAA may regulate lipol-
ysis through activation of PKA and thereby regulate the expression of perilipin [56]. Another acute-
phase protein is Orosomucoid (ORM), which has anti-inflammatory and an immunomodulatory ef-
fect [58], [59]. It is hypothesized that ORM plays a role in maintaining metabolic homeostasis in 
low-grade inflammation induced by obesity. Research with Ossabaw pigs showed a higher concen-
tration of ORM during obesity, which is a similar to what is seen in humans [59].  
 
It has been observed that obese pigs have elevated levels of circulating TNFa, which is related to 
the adipose tissue inflammatory response [60]. Furthermore, the elevated level of TNFa has also 
been linked to insulin resistance [9]. It has been shown that the transformation of adipose tissue 
macrophages from anti-inflammatory (M2 macrophages) phenotype to inflammatory M1 macro-
phages secretes TNFa, which causes inflammation in the tissue [60]. Research using porcine models 
has shown that macrophages secrete the cytokines TNFa and IL-6 and that the amount of these 
cytokines was increased in obese subcutaneous adipose tissue [9]. An increase in TNFa and IL-6 
expression are hallmarks of the chronic inflammatory state along with the increased concentration 
of IL-6 in the circulation [61], which have been observed in different porcine models [61], [62]. IL-
6 is more highly expressed under basal conditions than TNFa and additionally, IL-6 is circulating at 
significant concentrations, whereas TNFa is at quite low concentration. These observations are par-
allel to what is seen in humans. The regulation of IL-6 production and the signalling in adipocytes 
from pigs is of importance in the development and characterization of a more accurate and effective 
==PAGE:27==
 
27 
model of obesity and the MetS. It is seen in both humans and Ossabaw pigs that IL-6 is elevated 
when being obese. Furthermore, several adipokines such as adiponectin and leptin seem to be 
linked to inflammation and obesity in pigs in the same manner as in humans [61].

3.2.1 Primer design  
First primer sets were designed by the use of Ensembl and primer3 (v.0.4.0). cDNA sequences cov-
ering the Ossabaw pig genome for each gene of interest were identified in Ensembl. The appropriate 
primer was selected in primer3 with respect to the PCR product size with a range between 80-120 
nucleotides, the primer melting temperature (TM,) was set to minimum 59 °C and maximum 61 °C 
and Breslauer et al., 1986 [68] was used for thermodynamic parameters; chosen by Primer3. Fur-
thermore, it was preferred to design primers spanning over introns to avoid amplification of gDNA. 
Two sets of primer pairs were designed for each gene of interest in order to find the most optimal 
primers for the qPCR. The detailed protocol is shown in appendix B.  
3.2.2 Purification of RNA 
The RNeasy Lipid Tissue Mini Kit (Qiagen) is used for optimal cell lysis and purification of tissue 
containing a lot of fat, such as brain and adipose tissue. The protocol is shown in appendix C. The 
RNeasy kit is used to isolate total RNA by integrating phenol/guanidine-based sample lysis and sil-
icamembrane purification. The QIAzol Lysis Reagent is used for promoting cell lysis of the tissue 
and thereby inhibit RNases and homogenizing the tissue samples. When adding chloroform and 
centrifuge the homogenate, it will be separated into three different phases. RNA being in the upper 
phase while DNA partitions into the interphase and protein partitions into the lower phase. After 
collecting the upper phase, ethanol is added to provide the best binding conditions. The samples 
are added to a Mini spin column where the total RNA binds the membrane and the contaminants 
are washed away leaving the purified RNA molecules. The washing step is done at least three times 
by centrifugation in order to get the most optimal purification. DNase I treatment is done for further 
DNA removal because qPCR analysis works with a low-abundant target and this RNA application is 
sensitive to very small amounts of DNA. The RNA is again washed at least three times. Then RNase 
free water is added to the samples and centrifuged to collect the flow-through that contains the 
RNA. Efficient disruption and homogenization of the sample are necessary for total RNA purifica-
tion. A complete disruption of the plasma membrane of the cells is required for the release of all 
RNA in the samples and homogenization is required for reducing the viscosity of the lysates pro-
duced by the disruption. If the purification isn’t performed optimal it will lead to less pure RNA that 
it will impact the result of the gene expression analysis [66].  
3.2.3 NanoDrop & Bioanalyzer 
The RNA purity and quantity were measured spectrophotometrically by NanoDrop (NanoDropTM 
One/OneC Microvolume UV-Vis Spectrophotometer). The purity of the sample is assessed spectro-
photometrically by measuring the absorbance via NanoDrop, thereby measuring A260/280 and 
==PAGE:31==
 
31 
A260/230 ratio by measuring nucleic acids at absorption up to 260nm [18], [69], [70]. Good quality 
RNA is estimated to have an A260/280 higher than 1.8 [18], [71]. The A260/280 gives an insight into the 
type of proteins present along with an indication of the purity. The A260/230 is an indication of con-
tamination of different types of phenols, salt, carbohydrates, etc., and should also be above the value 
of 1.8 [71]–[73]. RNA concentration affects the absorbance ratios meaning that if the RNA concen-
tration is very low, the contaminant, no matter which, will impact the ratio.  
 
The quality of the RNA was further tested on an Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer with Agilent RNA 6000 
Nano kit. The Agilent 2100 Bioanalyzer uses microfluidic electrophoresis to separate the RNA in 
the samples. The RNA quality is based on 18S and 28S ribosomal RNA bands and smaller products. 
RNA integrity number (RIN) is a way to estimate the RNA quality. The RIN classifies the RNA quality 
from 1 to 10, where 1 is a degraded RNA product and 10 is the most intact RNA product. RIN higher 
than 5 is considered to be valid for further qPCR analysis [70]. The sample moves through micro-
channels and is injected into separation channel where they are electrophoretically separated. The 
components will then be detected by fluorescence and shown as bands and electropherograms 
(peaks). Several peaks mean degraded RNA, ideally only two peaks (18S and 28S ribosomal RNA) 
should be visible giving the best RIN number [74] 
3.2.4 cDNA synthesis & Pre-amplification  
The cDNA synthesis was performed according to the protocol in appendix D with the use of Quan-
tiTect Revers Transcription kit (Qiagen), thereby converting the extracted RNA into cDNA by revers 
transcription. To ensure for technical variation, duplicates for each RNA sample were made. The 
amount of necessary volume of RNA solution was calculated based on the result from the spectro-
photometrically analysis on the NanoDrop. 500 ng RNA pr. synthesis is the most optimal and only 
samples with more than 100 ng RNA pr. synthesis were accepted, otherwise it can lead to either too 
low or too high expression and therefore cannot be detected during the qPCR [18], [75]. 
 
TaqMan PreAmp Master mix (Applied Biosystems) and Exonuclease I (New England Biolabs) was 
used for the pre-amplification. The pre-amplification was performed in order to increase the 
amount of initial cDNA several-fold, thereby amplifying the target genes to be measured. Pre-am-
plification of the cDNA, results in a quantifiable amount for the qPCR analysis [76]. A primer mix 
containing all primers used for the qPCR was made and used in the process. After pre-amplifying 
the samples, they were all exonuclease treated in order to secure that all excess of primers was 
removed. It is very important when doing pre-amplification that the right number of cycles are 
used. If there are too few cycles then the RNA cannot be detected, and if the cycles are too many 
==PAGE:32==
 
32 
then there will be too much expression of the genes to be analysed. The detailed protocol for the 
pre-amplification is shown in appendix E.

The Fluidigm Biomark gives a magnitude of high throughput real-time qPCR. When being compared 
to more conventional platforms it gives a very high amount of data by using the nanofluidic chips 
that contains fluidic networks so they automatically combine the samples with primer-assays. This 
results in reaction densities way higher than what is possible with methods such as Rotor-Gene Q, 
and also reduces the number of liquid-handling steps and the volume per reaction [72], [77], [78]. 
The Rotor-Gene Q enables high-precision real-time PCR, and is mostly suited for in vitro diagnostic 
applications. It is an easy method for advanced users as well as beginners, the software is easy to 
use and a relatively fast way to do PCR, but it can be challenging if testing a high number of samples 
and a high number of primer-assays [78], [79]. When doing PCR with the Rotor-Gene Q it is only 
possible to test one assay for each sample, but this is a fast way to do PCR compared to the Fluidigm 
Biomark [72], [77]–[79]. 
==PAGE:33==
 
33 
 
Figure 11: Illustration of the Rotor-Gene Q 72 rotor-disc [78]. 
Both methods measure the fluorescence in the samples in order to determine the amount of prod-
uct. The fluorescence is detectable by using dsDNA (double stranded DNA) binding dye such as 
EvaGreen® or SYBRTM Green, which binds dsDNA. The binding dyes are able to bind to the synthe-
sized product, thereby increasing the fluorescence [77]–[79]. By the qPCR the increase in fluores-
cent signal is measured, which is proportional to the amount of product produced during each PCR 
cycle. An individual reaction is determined by the PCR cycle at which the fluorescence reaches over 
a defined threshold of background fluorescence. This parameter is called the threshold cycle (Ct) or 
crossing point (Cp). The more target there is in the starting material, the lower the Ct value will be. 
It is the relation between the fluorescence and the amount of amplified product that gives an accu-
rate quantification of the target molecules. The binding dye binds to synthesized dsDNA during each 
PCR cycle, thereby the fluorescence will increase by each cycle. For maximal amplification efficiency 
the amount of PCR product needs to be doubled each time [77], [80]. A PCR run gives a quantifica-
tion cycle (Cq) value for each sample for each primer-assay. The Cq defines the number of PCR cy-
cles where the fluorescence reaches the defined threshold. Meaning that the number of PCR cycles 
is relative to the mRNA concentration. Thereby a high concentration of mRNA will result in a low 
Cq value [80], [81]. When performing qPCR, the annealing of the primers to their sequence is an 
important and difficult step, because it has to happen at the right temperature before the primers 
can anneal to their target and at the same time prevent nonspecific annealing and primer-dimer 
formation. By analysing the melting curve, the specificity of the primer annealing can be deter-
mined. The PCR efficiency determines the rate for the polymerase to convert the reagents to PCR 
products (dNTPs, oligonucleotides, and template cDNA). The efficiency is important to determine 
because it indicates if there are problems with the qPCR. A low efficient reaction can indicate con-
tamination, inhibitors, wrong temperature, poorly designed primers, amplicons with secondary 
structures, etc. A very high efficiency can be a result of primer dimers or nonspecific amplicons [18]. 
The product of the reactions was analyzed by melt curve analysis (MCA) when using the dsDNA 
binding dye. When doing qPCR with the qPCR MCA protocol different phases are involved. The 
phases are illustrated in figure 11 [77]. 
 
==PAGE:34==
 
34 
 
Figure 12: PCR Thermal protocol. The x-axis is the time in seconds and the y-axis is the temperature in Celsius. The 
first phase is the thermal mix phase that establishes the right reaction setup. The UNG (uracil-DNA glycosylase) and 
hot start phase activate the DNA polymerase and prevents UNG enzymes carryover contamination, thereby minimiz-
ing the changes of contamination. The PCR cycle phase includes a denaturation phase where the dsDNA turns into 
single-stranded DNA (ssDNA) and then changes the amount of fluorescence, which makes up the melting curve. The 
DNA polymerase can withstand up to 95°C, which is the temperature used doing the denaturation phase. During the 
annealing phase complementary sequences hybridize at the calculated Tm of the primers is used. The last step is the 
melting curve analysis [72], [77].  
Standard curves were included in each chip run. A standard curve is made from a dilution series 
from a pool including all the samples from the same tissue. This is used for describing the dynamic 
range by a dilution series. The standard curve is used to determine the efficiency of the qPCR for 
each gene, which is used for correction of the amplification efficiency [72], [80], [81]. Furthermore, 
reference genes are included in the gene analysis, which is used for correction of possible technical 
variation [80], [82]. Typically, three or four reference genes are included [83] and should have non 
or a minimal change in expression between all the samples regardless of the condition or possible 
treatment [80]–[82]. Several controls are also included when doing qPCR. -RT (non-revers tran-
scription) controls are used to check for contamination of gDNA. It will be visible if any gDNA is 
present by resulting in a melting temperature corresponding to the product or higher. A non-tem-
plate control (NTC) is included for detection of contamination [80]. The setup for the Rotor-Gene Q 
is shown in appendix H and the setup for each chip is shown in appendix I.

3.2.6 Data analysis  
At first the so-called pre-data analysis was performed with the Fluidigm Real-Time PCR Analysis 
software [84], and the Rotor-Gene Q Series Software [78], [79]. All the samples were evaluated by 
their melting and amplification curves, while the -RT samples were compared to the same sample 
that had the reverse transcriptase enzyme included. The NTC was evaluated to ensure that there 
was no contamination [80]. Furthermore, the pre-data analysis was continued in the software 
GenEx (MultiD) v7. The basic aspect of these calculations is that each PCR cycle doubles the amount 
of DNA, meaning that the Cq, which is the number of cycles needed to cross the threshold is an 
inverse exponential function of the amount of cDNA in the sample. The Cq values were adjusted to 
the efficiency; E=10(1/slope)-1 (slope of the standard curve). The efficiency for the qPCR has to be 
between 80% and 120% and the value of R2 should be above 0.95 in order for the standard curves 
to be accepted and for it to be analysed in the GenEx software. The samples were then normalized 
to the reference genes that has been previous selected by the programs GenEx and NormFinder and 
calculated as DCq = Cq(sample gene) – Cq(ref genes). Then these ratio values were transformed into 
linear values by calculating the 2-exponential value, 2exp(-DCq). All the replicates were averaged 
and the Cq values were converted to the relative expression units; the lowest expressed sample was 
set to 1 and the other adjusted to that sample. This is done in order to get a relative expression level 
in order to more easily compare values [85], [86]. 
 
Data analysis was continued in the Prism v9 software where statistical analysis was performed. The 
data was transformed into log2 scale before the statistical analysis. In order to do the statistical 
analysis all data was normality tested and an un-paired parametric t-test was applied. The un-
paired parametric t-test was performed in order to test for significant difference for each gene be-
tween the different pigs and tissue. Statistically significance was accepted for p-values <0.05 and a 
fold change >2 [84].

4.1.2 Summary of Results  
When looking at the lean pigs, one group of genes was significantly more highly expressed in sub-

These genes include ADRA3, ABHD5, and LEPR. On the other hand, some of the other genes were

pigs compared to the other breeds. ADRB1 was in the subcutaneous adipose tissue of production 
pigs tending to be more highly expressed than in both subcutaneous adipose tissue and visceral

was significantly higher expressed in Ossabaw subcutaneous adipose tissue than in production 
pigs, and also tended to be higher in visceral adipose tissue. IFNg was significantly higher expressed 
in both tissues from the Ossabaw than in the Production pigs.

decreased in obese pigs: ABDH5, NPR1 (SAT), LIPE (SAT), PLIN1 (tendency to be decreased in both 
tissue), IRS1 (SAT), IFNg  (SAT); and the following were increased: LEP (increased in both tissues, 
more highly expressed in VAT), PNPLA2 (VAT, tendency for SAT), ADRB3 (tendency to increase, 
both tissues).

DISCUSSION  
5.1 Big Animal Model  
In this project lean and obese Ossabaw pigs and lean Production pigs were studied in relation to 
obesity and the MetS. The gene expression between lean Production pigs and lean Ossabaw pigs 
was also a part of this analysis. A few differences between the Production pigs and the Ossabaw 
pigs were observed. The Production pigs had lower expression of PLIN1, LEPR and LEP in the sub-
cutaneous adipose tissue compared to the Ossabaw pig. This might be speculated to either play a 
direct role in, or be a consequence of the obese-prone phenotype of the Ossabaw pig. PLIN1 being 
important in the lipolysis regulation [35] and the findings on the hormone leptin and its receptor 
LEPR are of particular interest as subcutaneous adipose tissue expression of all three genes are 
increased in the Ossabaw pigs compared to production pigs. This may seem counterintuitive as LEP 
reduces appetite; however, this can be seen as an example of obtained leptin resistance [2], [17], 
[35], [87]. This could potentially contribute to the obese Ossabaw phenotype. The other observation 
in this work is that obesity is indeed accompanied by an increase in LEP expression in both tissues 
in the Ossabaw pig, just as observed in human obesity, while no change in adipocyte LEPR expres-
sion is seen [32]. In the visceral adipose tissue FABP4 was more expressed in the Ossabaw pig than 
the Production pig. In the case of FABP4, the higher baseline (i.e., lean state) expression in Ossabaw 
pigs may suggest a heightened preparation to handle intracellular fat in visceral adipocytes and 
macrophages, and may contribute to the increased obesity comorbidity risks in Ossabaw pigs, as 
FABP4 is known to promote cardiometabolic disease [39], [40], [88]. LIPE showed to be signifi-
cantly increased in the visceral adipose tissue, indicating a higher activity in this tissue compared 
to the subcutaneous adipose tissue only in the Ossabaw pig. LIPE coding for HSL, which is associated 
with FABP4 in relation to regulation of the lipolysis, is both upregulated in the visceral adipose 
tissue indicating a connection [39], [40]. Furthermore IFNg  were lower expressed in both types of 
tissue from the Production pig compared to the Ossabaw pig. IFNg being important for the obesity-
induced adipose tissue inflammation in humans, which the Production pig did not seem to mimic 
where the Ossabaw pig showing a higher expression in both the subcutaneous and visceral adipose 
tissue. Also, a small change in the IFNg expression in the obese Ossabaw pig was found, showing 
that obesity impacts the IFNg expression in the Ossabaw pig, which supports IFNg expression being 
impacted by obesity, even though the exact regulation is unclear. [32]. Furthermore, ADRB1 
==PAGE:51==
 
51 
showed a significant difference in the expression between the visceral and subcutaneous tissue in 
the production pig, being more highly expressed in the subcutaneous adipose tissue. No difference 
between the two tissues was observed in the Ossabaw pig [17], [89].   
5.2 Lipolysis related genes 
The genes ADRB1 and ADRB3 codes for the receptors regulating the function of catecholamine pro-
moting release of NEFA to the circulation and has been associated to altered regulation of catechol-
amine induced lipolysis in adipocyte due to obesity [89]. No difference in the gene expression was 
observed in the obese Ossabaw pigs compared to the lean pigs, indicating no change in expression 
during the obese state of the Ossabaw pig. [35], [89]. The ADRB2 gene has shown to be highly fre-
quent in relation to obesity and has been suggested to be the main regulator of catecholamine in-
duced lipolysis in both the visceral and subcutaneous adipose tissue [5], [89]. This could explain 
why no change in regulation of ADRB1 and ADRB3 was found. The number adrenoceptors have been 
shown to be higher in the subcutaneous adipose tissue than the visceral adipose tissue [20], which 
could be why there is a higher expression of ADRB3 in this tissue. Furthermore, the b3-adrenocep-
tor is more sensitive to catecholamine regulation in the visceral adipose tissue than the subcutane-
ous adipose tissue [27]. The b3-adrenoceptor sensitivity is suggested to be affected by the obese 
state and lead to decreased lipolysis activity and thereby increasing fat storage [5], [90]. However, 
the expression of ADRA2 showed no difference in gene expression, it has otherwise previously been 
suggested to be affect by obesity with its anti-lipolytic activity [89], [91]. An increase in the activity 
of b3-adrenoceptors is associated with decreased activity of a2-adrenoceptors and induces the lip-
olytic response in visceral adipocytes. The subcutaneous adipocytes are larger compared to visceral 
adipocytes due to the more increased lipogenesis activity in the subcutaneous adipose tissue. This 
higher level of lipoprotein uptake has been shown to result in differences in adipocyte size and it is 
hypothesized to have lower lipolytic activity than the visceral adipose tissue [5], [27]. 
 
Lipolysis induced by NPs is decreased in the obese state due to lower ratio of NPR-A and NPR-B 
receptors in several animal models and humans. NPR1 expression is increased in fasting conditions 
and an increase in energy supplies and insulin inhibits the gene expression [41], this could be why 
there is a downregulation of NPR1 in the subcutaneous adipose tissue from the obese Ossabaw pigs. 
Furthermore, a low NPR-A/NPR-C ratio has been strongly associated with obesity by a lower ex-
pression of NPR-A and a higher amount of NPR-C [41], [92], this could also be why there is a de-
crease in NPR1 expression. The NPRs have not been linked to a specific type of adipose tissue [89], 
which for the NPR1 and NPR2 seems to be expressed equally in both the visceral and subcutaneous 
adipose tissue. NPR3 was significantly higher expressed in the visceral adipose tissue compared to 
==PAGE:52==
 
52 
the subcutaneous adipose tissue, which could be because of the high lipolytic activity in visceral 
adipocytes [27]. It has been proposed that increased expression of NPR-C in both adipose tissue is 
associated with increased fat mass, thus leading to lower actions of ANP on adipocytes and thereby 
a decreased level of plasma ANP. It is suggested that a decreased level of plasma ANP as a conse-
quence of obesity is linked to the development of hypertension [35]. An increase in NPR3 expres-
sion in the obese Ossabaw pigs is not seen here.  
 
The subcutaneous adipose tissue is more sensitive to the effect of insulin than the visceral adipose 
tissue, but different spliced variations of insulin receptors have been shown to be differently ex-
pressed, some more in the visceral adipose tissue than in the subcutaneous adipose tissue. Insulin 
has a lower ability to regulate lipolysis in visceral adipose tissue and IRS1 has been seen to have 
lower protein expression in this tissue [93]. Both IRS1 and IRS2 was found to have higher gene ex-
pression in the visceral adipose tissue from both the lean and obese Ossabaw pigs compared to the 
subcutaneous adipose tissue. This could be in relation to insulin being a regulator of gene expres-
sion, and with subcutaneous adipose tissue being more sensitive to antilipolytic effects could be 
explaining the lower gene expression of IRS1 and IRS2 [45], [93]. Furthermore, it has been shown 
in Ossabaw pigs that a feed high-fat and high-fructose diet can induce insulin resistance and the 
diet can induce abnormalities of the insulin signalling by diminishing the IRS-1 and the response to 
PI3-kinase, which could explain the downregulation of IRS-1 in the obese Ossabaw pig [9]. IRS2 is 
like IRS1 more expressed in the visceral adipose tissue than the subcutaneous adipose tissue. IRS2 
is known to have a compensating function when IRS1 is lacking which could be why there is not 
difference in the IRS2 gene expression between the lean and obese Ossabaw pigs, because this stage 
has not yet occurred [45].  
 
As expected, the gene expression of the enzymes important for the lipolysis was seen to be differ-
ently expressed in the obese Ossabaw pigs compared to the lean Ossabaw pigs. PLIN1 codes for the 
protein Perilipin that functions as a regulator for ATGL and HSL trough ABHD5 meaning that a 
downregulation of this gene could lead to lower activation of the ATGL and HSL. The PLIN1 and 
ABHD5 is both downregulated in the subcutaneous and visceral adipose tissue from the obese Os-
sabaw pigs, thereby apparently downregulating the lipolytic activity in both tissue during obesity. 
This could also be why there is a downregulation in gene expression of LIPE in the subcutaneous 
adipose tissue [5]. PNPLA2 coding for ATGL is as the only enzyme upregulated in the subcutaneous 
adipose tissue. Previously it has been demonstrated in both porcine and human obesity studies that 
there was a reduced ATGL protein expression even though an increase in PNPLA2 mRNA expression 
was observed [94], [95]. This could be why there is an upregulation of this gene, and due to the 
==PAGE:53==
 
53 
visceral adipose tissue being more lipolytic active this is also why it only is seen in this tissue [27], 
[94]. It is also suggested that Leptin is a regulator of the PNPLA2 gene and leptin upregulates the 
gene expression during obesity. LEP is also upregulated during the obese state, which could be a 
factor in the upregulation of PNPLA2 seen in the obese Ossabaw pigs [95]. Furthermore, LIPE and 
PNPLA2 has been seen to be more highly expressed in the visceral adipose tissue probably due to 
the higher lipolytic activity in this issue [94]. Only ABHD5 was seen to be more highly expressed in 
the subcutaneous adipose tissue, which otherwise is suggested not to be differently expressed in 
the two types adipose tissue [94].  
 
In general, the genes relevant for the lipolytic activity showed a higher gene expression in the vis-
ceral adipose tissue and obesity affected the regulation of these genes [27]. Even though it is diffi-
cult to say anything about the activity on protein level it seems that obesity affects the general gene 
expression of the genes coding for the enzymes and a few of the receptors important for the lipoly-
sis. Only ADRB3 and ABHD5 showed to be more expressed in the subcutaneous adipose tissue ra-
ther than in the visceral adipose tissue, which was not as anticipated. 
5.3 Leptin 
The visceral adipose tissue is known to contain a higher number of inflammatory cytokines and 
immune cells, and they are more metabolic active and has a higher capacity of generating NEFAs 
compared to subcutaneous adipose tissue, where the subcutaneous adipose tissue is greater at ab-
sorbing glucose and free fatty acids [27]. The levels of Leptin in the body are linked to the fat mass, 
being increased with energy intake and decreased during periods with for instance fasting [87]. 
This supports the results showing that LEP is upregulated in both the visceral and the subcutaneous 
tissue in expression during obesity. Leptin affects several pathways by activation, which leads to 
metabolic changes such as increased glucose uptake and secretion of proinflammatory cytokines 
[20], [61], [87]. This type of regulation is seen in both humans and pigs [61]. The higher amount of 
inflammatory cell types and high expression of pro-inflammatory cytokines could be impacted by 
the increased amount of leptin in the visceral tissue seen during obesity. The increased amount of 
leptin in the subcutaneous tissue could also impact the increased uptake of glucose in the tissue 
during the obese state [27], [29]. Fawcett et al., 2010 [19] says Leptin is being more expressed in 
the subcutaneous adipose tissue, this is not seen here. The LEP gene has a high expression in the 
visceral adipose tissue compared to the subcutaneous adipose tissue from Ossabaw pigs, which is 
also shown previously [96], but is upregulated in both adipose tissues as a response to the obese 
state of the Ossabaw pigs. The results showed a significant higher expression of LEPR in the subcu-
taneous adipose tissue compared to the visceral adipose tissue. The difference between LEPR 
==PAGE:54==
 
54 
between the two tissues could be linked to this high uptake of free fatty acids and glucose in the 
subcutaneous adipose tissue [27]. The expression of the Leptin receptor doesn’t seem to be affected 
by the obese state. It is previously seen that there is an increased amount of Leptin during obesity, 
but no increase in leptin receptors, which has been suggested to be a part of the development of 
Leptin resistance due to obesity. This agrees with the result in this project showing only an increase 
in LEP during the obese state [32]. 
5.4 Inflammatory markers 
FABP4 is suggested to facilitate the transport of free fatty acids to different organelles in the cell in 
relation to lipid oxidation in the mitochondria. FABP4 is important for development of insulin re-
sistance and atherosclerosis in relation to inflammation and obesity [40]. FABP4 is also important 
in regulation of HSL and contributing to upholding a balance between the lipogenesis and lipolysis 
[39]. FABP4 is expressed at a very high level in the visceral adipose tissue indicate that FABP4 plays 
an important role in the regulation of lipogenesis and lipolysis in this tissue. The gene coding for 
HSL is also shown to be more expressed in the visceral adipose tissue, which could be linked to high 
expression of FABP4 [39], [40], [89]. Furthermore, FABP4 might promote pro-inflammatory signal-
ling in immune cell such as macrophage, which is why it is increased at such a high amount in the 
visceral adipose tissue. Additionally, the high levels of FABP4 have been linked to induced levels of 
insulin, also a factor associated with obesity [32], [97]. ORM is a protein regulating metabolic ho-
meostasis and is furthermore linked to inflammation. It has previously been shown that ORM is 
induced in the adipose tissue from obese mice playing a role in metabolic homeostasis by regulating 
inflammation [98]. ORM concentration is significantly increased in obese humans, and earlier stud-
ies have shown elevated serum levels of ORM in obese Ossabaw pigs [99]. In this study no difference 
in gene expression of ORM was seen between the lean and obese pigs, but ORM was highly expressed 
in the visceral adipose tissue compared to the subcutaneous adipose tissue. A study with ORM iso-
lated from adipose tissue and liver concluded that ORM mRNA abundance in adipose tissue is ex-
tremely small compared to the liver, therefore, adipose tissue makes minimal contribution to cir-
culatory ORM and the effect of ORM from the adipose tissue is very limited [58], but is rather related 
to inflammation [58], [98]. IFNg was significantly decreased in subcutaneous adipose tissue from 
obese Ossabaw pigs. In mice study, IFNg have shown to stimulate expression of TNF-a, but decrease 
stimulation of IL-10 and that IFNg regulates the metabolism along with inflammation dependent on 
the body wight [52]. The decrease in IFNg could be a response to the obese state of the Ossabaw pig, 
but was expected to be increased.

cells, which is in agreement with the result from this study, showing that the inflammatory markers 
investigated are higher expressed in the visceral adipose tissue. It was expected that there would 
be a higher gene expression of the inflammatory genes in the obese Ossabaw pigs. The reason for 
not seeing any difference in the gene expression of the inflammatory genes between the lean and 
obese pigs could be in relation to the age of Ossabaw pigs. The obese Ossabaw pigs were only be-

in the gene expression of the inflammatory markers [14], [26].

CONCLUSIONS  
This study was conducted in a porcine model with Production pigs, Göttingen pigs, and Ossabaw 
pigs. The gene expression from the different breeds showed higher activity in several genes of the 
Ossabaw pig. The Ossabaw pig seems to be the better model than the other breeds due to the indi-
cation that they have enhanced preparation to handle intracellular fat mainly in visceral adipocytes 
and macrophages and have increased response that may contribute to the increased obesity comor-
bidity risks. The results in this obesity study showed a change in basic gene expression of several 
genes important for lipolytic activity in adipocytes from obese Ossabaw pigs. The majority of lipol-
ysis related genes were either more highly expressed in visceral adipose tissue or the same com-
pared with subcutaneous adipose tissue. Only two lipolysis related genes had higher expression in 
the subcutaneous adipose tissue; ADRB3 and ABHD5. Considering changes induced by obesity the 
genes coding for enzymes, ABHD5, LIPE and PLIN1 were decreased in the obese pigs, contrary to 
PNPLA2 which was upregulated. The two genes coding for receptors NPR1 and IRS1 was also down-
regulated in obese Ossabaw pigs. Furthermore, the expression of the Leptin receptor didn’t seem 
to be affected by the obese state, but the results showed leptin to be upregulated in both the visceral 
and the subcutaneous tissue during obesity. Several inflammatory markers were analysed and 
showed ORM and FABP4 to be much higher expressed in the visceral adipose tissue than in the 
subcutaneous adipose tissue. IFNg was equally expressed in the adipose tissues but downregulated 
in the subcutaneous adipose tissue from the obese Ossabaw pigs. Overall, this study indicates that 
the visceral adipose tissue has higher lipolytic activity and more inflammatory activity compared 
to subcutaneous adipose tissue and obesity has an impact on the basic gene expression of lipolysis 
related genes along with the hormone Leptin.

FURTHER PERSPECTIVES  
For further studies, it will be interesting to examine gene expression for even more of the lipolysis 
related genes and inflammatory markers as only some were selected for further analysis in this 
project [5]. Furthermore, it would be exciting to investigate the same gene expression in older Os-
sabaw pigs that have been given fed high fat, high fructose, medium cholesterol diet for a longer 
time to see if there is further change in the regulation of lipolysis related genes and if an increase in 
inflammation is found [14]. The changes seen in the basic gene expression would be interesting to 
examine at higher protein levels. The mRNA expression cannot be mirrored as the amount of pro-
tein activity; therefore, it would make sense to study the protein expression [100]. Since changes 
happens in the expression of the enzyme-coding genes, it would be very interesting to examine 
these at an enzyme active level. Leptin showed to be one of the genes most affected by obesity. It 
would be exciting to look at adipocytes in different breeds of pigs including Ossabaw pigs at a pro-
tein level of leptin to see if the change observed at the mRNA level is also to be seen at the protein 
level. It could be further interesting to study leptin resistance in lean and obese Ossabaw pigs and 
since Leptin is also suggested to be altered in the brain and circulation this should also be looked 
further into [101], [102].

APPENDIX B: Protocol for qPCR primer design 
Guidelines for qPCR primer design for gene expression analysis (mRNA) 
 
1. Open two tabs in browser: 1) Ensembl Genome Browser - http://www.ensembl.org/, 2) Pri-
mer 3 - https://bioinfo.ut.ee/primer3-0.4.0/ (Primer 3 URL sometimes changes, google 
“Primer 3” if this one is outdated)  
2. In Ensembl: ”Search:” drop-down menu, choose relevant species – “for”, type gene of inter-
est. Press Go. Select gene from results list (typically top result) 
 
 
 
 
3. Check table, this will show how many splice variants exists – hopefully only one, otherwise 
you have to identify regions that are identical across splice variants if you want primers to 
amplify all splice variants 
 
There are three splice variants in this example, but they differ only in the 5’ end, so it should 
be possible to identify primers that will amplify all splice variants 
 
 
4. Click on relevant link under Transcript ID in the table – in this example it could be any of 
them. 
5. Left-hand menu (close to the bottom), click “Export data”. Output format should be FASTA. 
Select only cDNA to be exported. No need to change other settings. Click “Next” 
==PAGE:69==
 
69 
6. Export format: choose “Text”. New tab will open, the top sequence will be the cDNA se-
quence (identifier includes “cdna:protein_coding”). Copy/paste this sequence to Primer 3 
 
 
7. Go back to Ensembl tab, left-hand menu, click on “Exons” 
8. Rules of thumb for designing forward primer: 
a. keep 3’ end GC rich, approx. 3 out of 6 bases at the end should be G or C. 
b. design primers that amplify sequences close to the 3’ end of cDNA – most reverse 
transcription methods employ oligo(dT) primers (that anneal to the poly(A) tail of 
mRNA) to prime the synthesis of cDNA, so the 3’ end of mRNA is generally more 
likely to be successfully reverse transcribed. If the reverse transcription also em-
ploys random primers (which could possibly anneal anywhere to the mRNA), one 
can be less strict regarding staying close to the 3’ end. 
c. firstly try to design a forward primer over the first intron, i.e.  half of the primer on 
either side of the intron. If not possible, design a primer on the 5’ side of an intron 
and have Primer 3 pick a reverse primer on the 3’ side, so that the product includes 
an intron. In this case, remember to check where the reverse primer is located; some-
times Primer 3 will pick a reverse primer that is partially located in two exons (like 
the over-intron design – this is good, and the primer name should thus indicate “oi” 
rather than “i”, see naming instructions below). If this also fails, move on to the next 
intron and try and design a forward primer over this intron. 
d. when deciding whether to try and design an over-intron primer or an including-in-
tron primer type, generally chose the type that allows you to stay the closest to the 
3’ end. 
e. when designing two primer pairs for the same gene, make sure that the products do 
not overlap – or at least make sure to make a note of it if it cannot be avoided. 
9. Forward primer design example, over-intron: pick approx. half of the primer sequence (3’ 
half) on the 3’ side of an exon: 
 
 
Then pick the 5’ half of the primer from the 5’ side of the exon:

70 
 
 
Combine sequences as input in Primer 3 as forward (left) primer: 
 
 
Leave field for reverse (right) primer empty and let Primer 3 define this. Make sure that 
thermodynamics settings are set to Breslauer 
 
 
Change a few of the setting before continuing, product size range should be 80-120, Primer 
Tm should be Min = 59.0, Opt = 60.0, Max = 61.0 – this is primarily important when designing 
assays for the BioMark (Fluidigm) platform, as all assays should then have same Tm and 
similar amplicon sizes ensures that different targets will be amplified with similar efficiency. 
If using a different (more conventional) qPCR platform, then these rules are less important. 
 
 
 
10. Click ”Pick Primers”. Adjust primer length and GC content until Tm is acceptable and no 
secondary structures are predicted. Primer 3 will suggest a primer pair and show you

11. Assign a name to the primer pair that includes organism, gene designation, primer design,

ssc_LIPE_oi_83_R, respectively.  
12. Over-intron primers are preferred as they can only amplify cDNA and not genomic DNA.

APPENDIX C: Protocol for RNA Purification  
Protocol: Purification of miRNA and mRNA from tissue (RNA later) using GentleMACS 
 
Kit: 
 
miRNeasy Mini Kit (Qiagen, # 217004) 
 
 
RNase-Free DNase set from Zymo Research 
 
Homogenizer:  
GentleMACS + M tubes 
 
1. Place the centrifuge at 4°C. 
 
2. Remove DNase from freezer. 
 
3. Wash the working area with 0.5 % NaOH solution and find homogenizer, scalpel, weighing 
trays, tweezers, tips, and pipettes. 
 
4. Dilute RPE/RWT buffer with 96-100 % EtOH at first time use. 
 
5. Transfer 1 ml of QIAzol Lysis Reagent to the desired number of M tubes. 
 
6. Dissect approx. 30 mg tissue with scalpel (a small piece of about 3x3 mm), cut into smaller 
pieces and transfer to M tube with QIAzol Lysis Reagent. It is important that the tissue is 
placed on the edge of the orange blade to ensure sufficient homogenization. Scalpel and 
tweezers are washed with 0.5 % NaOH between each sample. 
 
7. The tissue is homogenized on program RNA_02.01 (fresh tissue). Leave tubes with 
homogenate at room for 5 minutes (or until the other samples are finished). 
 
8. Add 200 μl of chloroform to each M tube, close the lid of the tube and shake vigorously for 
15 seconds. The tube is left at room temperature for 2-3 min. 
 
9. Transfer homogenate mix to a 1.5 ml tube (found in kit) and centrifuge at 12000 g/14000 
rpm for 15 min at 4°C. Switch off the cooling function afterwards. 
 
10. The homogenate is now divided into 3 phases. From the top phase is taken approx. 300 μl 
(2x150 µl) for a 2 ml tube (found in kit). 450 μl (1:1.5) of 100 % ethanol is added in the 
tube. The homogenate is mixed with the pipette and the pipette tip is left in the tube to be 
reused in step 11-12. 
 
11. Pipette up to 600 μl of sample into a RNeasy Mini Spin Column in a 1.5ml collection tube 
(found in kit). Close the lid and centrifuge at 8000 g/10000 rpm for 15 sec at room temp. 
Discard the flow-through. 
==PAGE:74==
 
74 
 
12. Repeat step 11 with the rest of the sample (max 600 μl).  
 
13. Pipette 350 μl RWT buffer (check that buffer solute is not precipitated) to the column and 
centrifuge at 8000 g/10000 rpm for 15 sec at room temp. Discard the flow-through. 
 
14. Mix DNase I incubation mix: per sample, mix 10 µl DNase I stock solution with 70 µl DNA 
Digestion Buffer. E.g. for 12 samples, mix 120 µl DNase I stock solution with 840 µl DNA 
Digestion Buffer. Mix gently with pipette. 
  
15. Pipette 78 μl DNase I incubation mix directly onto the RNeasy silica gel membrane and 
place the column at room temperature for 15 min (minimum). 
 
16. Pipette 350 μl RWT buffer to the column and centrifuge at 8000 g/10000 rpm for 15 sec at 
room temp. Discard the flow-through. 
 
17. Add 500 μl of RPE buffer to the column and centrifuge at 8000 g/10000 rpm for 15 sec at 
room temp. Discard the flow-through. 
 
18. Add 500 μl of RPE buffer again to the column and centrifuge at 8000 g/10000 rpm for 2 
min at room temp. Discard the flow-through. 
 
19. Transfer column to a new collection tube (regular tube with lid cut off). Centrifuge at 8000 
g/10000 rpm for 1 min to dry the column. 
 
20. To elute, transfer the column to a new 1.5 ml tube, then add 50 μl of RNase free water to 
the RNeasy silica gel membrane. Centrifuge at 8000 g/10000 rpm for 1 min at room temp. 
 
21. Flow-through (contains the RNA!) is collected and pipetted again on the membrane, after 
which the column is centrifuged at 8000 g/ 10000 rpm for 2 min at room temp (same 1.5 
ml tube). 
 
22. Placed at -80 °C for storage.
