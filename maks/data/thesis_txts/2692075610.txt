Abstract
The purpose of this study is to test the opportunity of detecting fawns in ﬁelds before harvest
with the use of modern computer vision methods. This was done by equipping a drone with
a Raspberry Pi performing object recognition with an RGB camera and object detection on
a thermal camera.
Neural Network training images of a fawn dummy was collected with a drone, and several
recognition models were trained to determine which were the most suitable for doing the
object recognition on the Raspberry Pi.
With the knowledge from tests performed on the Raspberry Pi with real drone videos of
the fawn dummy, it was possible to perform several tests with a drone equipped with a
Raspberry Pi, thermal and RGB camera, ﬁrst in an indoor test arena and then a second
test outdoor in a high grass ﬁeld, with the cameras mounted on a gimbal to reduce vibrations.
It was found from the indoor test, that it was possible to detect and recognise the heated
fawn dummy with both cameras, but that the vibrations from the drone was interfering
with the cameras. Furthermore it was found from the outdoor test that the environment
with high sun and 30 degrees, were overexposing the camera used and a recognition was not
possible, but it was however possible to detect the heat signature from the fawn dummy
with the thermal camera. A reference test with an outdoor drone camera was carried out
and showed a successful recognition.
It was concluded that the proof of concept was successful, and that it is possible to ﬁnd
fawns in ﬁelds before harvest with the use of object recognition on a RGB camera and
object detection with a thermal camera.
If a successful product was to be developed, a higher resolution thermal camera and a better
outdoor RGB camera should be used, combined with a more powerful micro computer for
faster and more detailed recognition.

Introduction
Every year approximately 20.000 fawns are killed in the hay production in the danish
ﬁelds[15], these fawns suﬀers a fatal fate when the farmers are cutting the tall grass that the
fawns are hiding in, some are lucky and are killed instantly but some gets mutilated by the
grass cutter and suﬀer a long and painful death.
Also the tractor operators get aﬀected by the traumatic experience of seeing a mutilated
fawn staggering away, where some quit due to this traumatic experience. Furthermore the
farmer suﬀers a ﬁnancial loss when hay has to be discarded due to contamination that, if
not detected, can give botulinum toxin to the animals consuming the hay, usually with a
deadly outcome. [17]
Many diﬀerent attempts to save the fawns have been tried in the past, and so far the most
used method is to manually search the entire ﬁeld with dogs to locate the fawns, a very time
consuming operation that usually takes several days, and still has a big margin of error. The
goal of this study is to use some of the newest technologies and methods to autonomously
search the ﬁelds for fawns before harvesting the hay. The recent robustness and pricing of
drones make it an attractive and fast way for farmers to inspect their ﬁelds, and the applica-
tion of drones has exploded in the recent years. This study will investigate the opportunities
of using computervision on a thermal camera and a RGB(Red,Green,Blue) camera for double
veriﬁcation, using a neural network to recognise fawns in a ﬁeld while ﬂying autonomously.
The objective is to launch an autonomous drone that is able to navigate the entire ﬁeld,
while looking for fawns, and if a fawn is found returning its GPS coordinates and an image
of the fawn for the operator to verify. Real drone ﬂights will be carried out to gather training
data and to do the ﬁnal testing of a dual camera setup carrying out object recognition and
detection mounted on a drone. Furthermore a discussion about the results and the processing
of data will be carried out, and a reﬂective discussing about further improvements will be
covered.

Roe deer biology
The Roe deer is the most common deer in Denmark, and lives in all of Europe. The deer
is a relatively small deer and weighs only 20-25kg, the deer has a fast reproduction and this
is due to three major properties they are polygamous, get 1 to 3 fawns per litter and are
already sexually mature by the age of one.
Figure 1: Roe deer with fawn, copyright by Marc M. Andersen
The roe deer normally gives birth to two fawns in May-June and hides these fawns in high
vegetation diﬀerent places in order to hide their fawns from predators, and ensure that if one
fawn is taken by a predator the other fawn will survive. In the ﬁrst 2 weeks of the fawns life
it lies in the high vegetation and hides as seen in ﬁgure 2, its ability to almost not emit any
scent, makes it hard for the predators to ﬁnd them. During these 2 weeks the roe deer visits
its fawns a couple of times a day to give milk. After the 2 weeks, the fawns are capable of
keeping up with the roe deer and follow it around until the age of 1 year. [22]

Computervision
This section will cover some of the theory need for making this study possible.
Computervision is a method that makes it possible to use the computational power of a
computer to process an image for the purpose of making the computer "see" like a human.
This method is a high-level task that makes it possible to extract features from the real
world eg. shapes, patterns and symbols known as object recognition and object detection,
this has a variety of applications and can combined with actuators be considered as AI.
In this project several computervision methods will be used to perform object recognition
and object detection, and will be considered machine learning.
6.1
Image representation
Before the processing of image data can begin, a short explanation on how an image is
digitally represented is needed. An RGB image is stored as a 2 dimensional array of 0-255
values with the camera resolution as the x- and y-sizes deﬁning the 2 dimensional array.
Each fundamental color, Red, Green and Blue, has its own channel with its 2 dimensional
array, where the intensity of the respective color is represented with a 0-255 value, with 0
being black and 255 being full color representation. An illustration of the RGB channels can
be seen from ﬁgure 4.

6.2
Neural Network
A NN(Neural Network) is a series of algorithms that uses interconnected layers of connections
with weighted decisions to draw conclusions from the input data.
To perform the object recognition, a NN is trained using an already existing NN training
model, that with a new data set is capable of re-training a NN to recognise one or more new
desired objects, this could be a recognition of bird species but also just one object like a
fawn. [20]
In this study the SSD MobileNet V2 FPNLite 320x320 training model is used, the selection
of this model will be further described in section 7. By dissecting the name of the training
model, the following information is found:
• The SSD deﬁnes the architecture of the detection method.
• The MobileNet V2 tells the structure of the Feature Extractor.
• The FPNLite is a modiﬁcation or an add-on to the Feature Extractor and interconnects
the Extracted features.
• The 320x320 means that the input images is re-sized to 320x320 pixels before being
fed into the Feature Extractor.

The Single Shot multibox Detector or SSD for short, has an architecture consisting of 3
stages: the Feature Extractor, the Detection Heaps and the NMS(Non Max Suppression).

The Feature Extraction section is a set of convectional layers that extract Feature Maps
from the image, a key feature in the SSD Architecture is that instead of just one feature
map, it extracts multiple feature maps during the convolutions, resulting in diﬀerent feature

computers a bigger and more complicated Feature Extractor like VGG16 or RCNN/Faster-

low computational power, the less demanding MobileNetV2 Feature Extractor is used.

6.2.2
MobileNetV2
The MobileNetV2 is a backbone single DNN architecture optimised for mobile devices and re-
source constrained computers and functions as the feature extractor in the SSD architecture.
The key features in reducing the computational cost of the DNN are the use of Depthwise
Separable Convolutions, Linear Bottlenecks and Inverted residuals. MobileNetV2 is based
on the Depthwise Separable Convolutions as seen from ﬁgure 10.
Page 10 of 92
==PAGE:23==
Masters - Thesis
6.2
Neural Network
Figure 10: MobileNet NN structure
Depthwise Separable Convolutions are replacing the full convolutional operations by splitting
the convolution in to two layers. The ﬁrst a depthwise convolution that performs lightweight
ﬁltering by applying only one convolutional ﬁlter per input channel. The second is a point-
wise convolution that does a ”1x1” convolution that builds new features by computing linear
combinations of the input channels, this way the resource needed for the convolution is re-
duced to roughly one eighth/ninth the cost of a standard full convolution, with only a small
reduction in precision.
The Linear Bottlenecks are a reduction in nodes compared to the previous layer, that reduces
the dimensionality and complexity of the layer to a low-dimensional subspace. When expand-
ing the number of nodes into a high-dimensional subspace layer again, the complexity and
computational demand is increased. The MobileNetV2 works on the principle that even in
low-dimensional subspaces the necessary information is kept and that the high-dimensional
subspaces works as an increment in details, therefore Inverted residual connections are im-
plemented between the "bottlenecks" in the MobileNetV2 structure. Where the layers are
reduced to a "bottleneck" a "shortcut" is made to pass forward information deeper into the
NN. This divergin convergin eﬀect of nodes on the layers with residual connections between
the "bottlenecks" makes it possible to train NN’s deeper, faster and better. An illustration
of a residual connection between bottlenecks can be seen from ﬁgure 11.

6.2.3
FPNLite
A Feature Pyramid Network or FPN is an independent add-on to the feature extractor as
seen in ﬁgure 12. The FPNLite is a lite version of a FPN that helps increasing the results
of the backbone model by parsing down low level feature information to the next layer of
feature information, increasing the level of information of the next layer.
Low semantic
feature levels can be explained as the shape of the fawn and high semantic levels like the
patterns on the back of the fawn. This parsing down of low level feature informations is
resulting in a broad range of high and low level feature informations in the lowest steps
of the FPN. The FPN therefore increases the semantic information of larger objects in the
image resulting in a higher conﬁdence score of the recognition. [6]

6.3
Drone
A drone is an UAV(Unmanned Areal Vehicle), but is commonly referred to as an unmanned
quad-copters or hexa-copters like the the one shown in ﬁgure 25, no matter the size or
numbers of motors the basic principles are the same. The basic components in a drone are
the same:
• Flight controller
• ESC
• Transmitter
• Receiver
• Propellers
• Drone Motor
• Battery
• Power Port Module
Flight controller is the unit that controls the signals to the ESC by the inputs from the
operator or the GPS, gyro and compass.
ESC, Electronic speed controller determines how much throttle each rotor needs to
perform a given task. The motors are conﬁgured is such manner that half of the motors
are rotating in a clockwise direction and the other half in a counter-clockwise direction, this
increases stability and makes the inertia of the rotating motors to balance out. A normal
conﬁguration of the motors are to position them so that the spinning blades are rotating in
towards the drone to further increase stability.
The inertia can be used to control the yaw of the drone by slightly increasing the speed of
the motors rotation in one direction and slightly decreasing the speed of the motors rotation
the opposite direction, creating an unbalance in rotational inertia making the drone rotate.
Furthermore will a increase or decrease on all the motors simultaneously, control the ﬂight

Transmitter/receiver is the unit that manages the ﬂow of data to and from the drone.
This could be control signals but also a video feed or information about the drone location or
condition. A normal frequency to use is 2.4GHz an is widely used for navigating drones near
the operator, while a 5GHz connection is used for drones further away from the operator,
some drones have a hybrid 2.4GHz/5GHz transmitter to automatically use the band with
the best connection.
Some drones also have a GPS attached to verify their global position in the air, and keep
them steady in the air. This function is great for compensating for wind and for controlling
the drone even if the connection is lost to the drone, and can also be used for guiding the
drone autonomously. A very useful function is the RTH (Return To Home) that guides the
drone back to its take-oﬀposition. An other useful add-on to a drone is a camera or thermal
camera, that makes it possible for the operator to live stream what the drone is seeing (FPV)
and capturing images and videos.

GPS
When explaining the GPS(Global Positioning System) systems it should be considered that
there is not one GPS system but several. The most common GPS signals are the Rus-
sian GLONASS, Chinese BEIDOU COMPASS, American GPS, European Galileo position-
system and the Japanese QZSS. The concept for all of these systems are the same, but with
a variance in satellite height and number. The basic concept in GPS are a triangulation
between 3 or more satellites, preferably more than 10, where the distance from the satellites
to the receiver can be used to triangulate the position of the receiver in comparison to the
location of the satellites, and from the satellites position approximate the position of the
receivers position on the earth in all 3 dimensions. An illustration of the triangulation of
the satellites can be seen from ﬁgure 13.
Figure 13: GPS triangulation
When the position is obtained it can be displayed in diﬀerent formats like the NMEA or
GCS format that use diﬀerent ways to express the location of the receiver.

Selection of training model
This section covers the API selection and which modes was further used.
A well dimensioned training model is crucial when training a NN for object recognition. In
particular, the computational power of the computer training the model and the computer
performing the detection, is important to take into considerations when choosing the training
model, but just as important a balance between detection speed and detection precision. For
facial recognition a high detection precision is preferable, while a counting of cars parsing in
an area will prefer a fast detection speed. If the computer doing the detection has a good
dedicated GPU it will be able to do the recognition of a computational heavy NN faster.
For this project a Raspberry Pi with low computational power is used, due to its small size
and low weight.
All models were CPU trained on a personal laptop with 16GB of ram, using an Anaconda
virtual environment. Three learning platforms were tested in this study to determine the
best platform for training the NN.
7.1
Cascade trainer GUI
The Cascade trainer GUI tested, is a Haar cascade algorithm normally used to detect or
recognise faces by searching the image for simple patterns like corners, t-joints or lines. The
training is preformed by a CNN tweaked by triplet training, where the model gets 3 images,
two true images and a false image to further adjust the CNN to get better true-positives and
less false-positives.[24]
The Haar cascade method was tested to detect the fawn dummy, but the result was very
poor, with close to none true-positives and a lot of false-positives.

TensorFlow
TensorFlow is an open source end-to-end machine learning platform owned by Google, it is
an python driven API capable of using a variety of diﬀered NN training models for detec-

7.3
TensorFlow Lite
TensorFlow Lite also know as TFLite is a set of tools that makes it possible to convert Ten-
sorFlow models into smaller and lighter models with the use of FlatBuﬀers [5]. FlatBuﬀers
are a library that uses serialization to convert a trained TensorFlow model into a .tflite
format. The .tflite format is not human readable because the new ﬁle is compressed for
a smaller code size. The smaller code size makes it possible to be processed without being
unpacked, resulting in faster object recognition and loading time.
The TFLite tools works best if the model used is optimized for mobile devices, furthermore
does it only work with TensorFlow 2 models.
Due to its faster object recognition and smaller code size, a TFLite conversion was used for
the ﬁnal recognition.

Discussion
The purpose of this study was to develop a combination of hardware and software to be
mounted on a drone, making it able to ﬁnd fawns in ﬁelds autonomously before harvest.
The results from the tests showed that it is possible to ﬁnd and detect fawns in ﬁelds from
their heat signature using a thermal camera, furthermore did the tests show that it is pos-
sible to recognise a fawn in a ﬁeld with a NN from a drone. It was however discovered from
test that the thermal camera used on the Fawn Detection unit was not suitable for outdoor
use, and that a better camera should be used in the future.
The task of integrating autonomous navigation on the drone, did show to be a bigger task
than estimated, and the task was negated for the purpose of a better performing detection
and recognition of the fawn.
The collection of GPS coordinates of the found fawns was partially implemented in the form
that a well working GPS unit was successfully used in the project, but that a oﬀset correc-
tion of the GPS was proven a bigger task than ﬁrst thought, and a more simple method of
calculated a oﬀset of the GPS coordinates was implemented.
The Raspberry Pi has been a good platform for the proof of concept, but it has some con-
strains with its limited processing power. This could be boosted with the Google Coral USB
Accelerator that is a small external Edge TPU coprocessor, that has been proven to increase
object recognition with a factor of 10[2], ideally increasing the fawn recognition to 20FPS.
In this study the "SSD MobileNet V2 FPNLite 320x320" was used for the fawn recognition,
but other recognition models like the YOLO(You Only Look Once) or tiny YOLO could be
used. Each recognition model has its advantages and could all be used for the fawn recogni-
tion with a descent result, but the "SSD MobileNet V2 FPNLite 320x320" was chosen due
to its fast recognition and fairly good certainty.
The fawn dummy used was a good stand-in for real fawns, with the fawn dummy is was
easy to gather training data and perform tests, it made it possible to perform early testing
of recognition models before real fawns was to be found. Even though the dummy had a
Page 46 of 92
==PAGE:59==
Masters - Thesis
realistic size, pattern, color, shape and thermal signature, it would be preferred to test the
Fawn Detection unit on real fawns, and to gather training data from real fawns to train a
more accurate NN for future use.
As the thermal camera was able to detect the heat signature from the fawn in high grass, it
worked as intended, but the camera has an build in automatic temperature compensation,
that gave a lot of false-positives when the high heat signature from the fawn was not in
the image. It was therefore a necessity to use the thermal detection as a double veriﬁcation
when a fawn was recognised with the RGB camera, and not to use it all the time, or to
use the heat signature to detect the fawn ﬁrst and then do a double veriﬁcation with the
RGB camera. The tests with both cameras were performed at 10m, but the reference test
showed that the NN, with a better RGB camera, was able to ﬁnd the fawn from 30m. From
knowledge sharing with "droneinspect.dk" it is veriﬁed that a fawns heat signature can be
detected from a height of 80m with a "DJI Mavic Enterprise Advanced" that has a 640x512
thermal camera resolution. Furthermore does this drone have a 48MP RGB camera capable
of zooming its camera with a resolution high enough to potentially perform fawn recognition
from 80m or higher.
For future work on this project a new data set with drone images of real fawns is needed,
but also a upgrade of hardware is needed. If the Fawn Detection unit should function as an
external unit for a drone, a camera similar to the DJI mini 2 or better should be attached
to a gimbal, furthermore a thermal camera without automatic temperature compensation is
required to adjust the temperate to a spectrum where only the fawns would stand out. If
better cameras would be used, a more powerful micro computer or custom computer capable
of performing fawn recognition at a higher resolution and speed would be a must. And of
course a lighter and smaller GPU unit would reduce the overall weight and size of the Fawn
Detection unit.
An other way to go with this project is to perform a cloud based image analysis with the use
of 5G, which has the capabilities of transmitting large amounts of data with a low latency.
This way a server could perform the object recognition and detection instead. An outsource
Page 47 of 92
==PAGE:60==
Masters - Thesis
of data processing would extend the ﬂight time of the drone excessively, because no further
weight from the add-on unit should be carried, but also the power of the processing is
saved. This cloud based concept would preferable be implemented on a drone like the DJI
Mavic, that already has a very good RGB and thermal camera mounted on a gimbal and
the capabilities of adding an upgraded GPS unit to further enhance the localisation of the
fawns found.
With the implementation of a cloud based project like this, on a drone like the DJI Mavic,
combined with an autonomous navigation software like the UgCS, autonomous ﬁndings of
fawns in ﬁelds could be performed in heights of 80-100m at a great speed, making it possible
to search large ﬁeld in a short amount of time.

Conclusion
The purpose of this study was to develop a combination of hardware and software to be
mounted on a drone, making it able to ﬁnd fawns in ﬁelds autonomously before harvest.
To be able to ﬁnd fawns in a ﬁelds, computervision was applied and a NN was trained
to perform the fawn recognition. Throughout the project several NN were trained, and a
comparison of the diﬀerent NN were completed to select the most suitable NN for this project.
For further veriﬁcation of the fawn a second camera was added to detect the heat signature
from the fawn, this was a redundant method in the ﬁnding of fawns with computervision.
Further more a marking of the fawns found in the ﬁelds was implemented in the form of a
GPS.
A prototype consisting of a Raspberry Pi, RGB camera, thermal camera, GPS and a cooling
fan installed in a slim 3d printed case were developed. The Fawn detection unit was tested
in several environments both in a contained environment inside ASTA, but also in diﬀerent
more realistic outdoor scenarios, a cut grass ﬁeld test and a high grass ﬁeld test.
The results of the indoor test showed that it was possible to ﬁnd the fawn with both the
RGB camera and the thermal camera, but that the vibrations from the drone interfered with
the cameras and made it impossible to perform tracking on the fawn recognition.
The results from the outdoor test was quite similar and revealed that the camera was not
able to perform recognition in an outdoor environment, this could be due to the fact that
the camera is designed primarily for indoor use, and was not able to cope with the high
sun. The thermal camera was not able to ﬁnd or detect the heat signature of the fawn in
the reference test on the cut grass due to disturbance from the heated soil. It was however
able to to ﬁnd and detect the heat signature of the fawn in the test of a fawn in high grass,
which is the intended environment. An extra reference test of the NN’s capabilities were
carried out with a camera designed for outdoor drone use. This test proved the hypothsis
that the NN was able to detect the fawn if a more suitable camera was used, the test proved
that it was possible to recognise the fawn from a height of 30m, three times further that the
benchmark test of 10m.

It can be concluded that it is indeed possible to ﬁnd fawns in ﬁelds before harvest, with a
drone and computervision.
