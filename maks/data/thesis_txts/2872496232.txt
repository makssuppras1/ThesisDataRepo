Abstract
Single-photon emitters (SPEs) based on transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDs) are of
growing interest for quantum photonic applications due to their two-dimensional nature,
strong excitonic response, and compatibility with on-chip integration. However, the per-
formance of these emitters is often limited by low brightness and inefficient light extraction.
To address these challenges, this thesis focuses on the design, simulation, and charac-
terization of nanophotonic structures aimed at enhancing the spontaneous emission rate
and collection efficiency of TMD-based emitters.
Tungsten diselenide (WSe2) monolayers, in hexagonal boron nitrin (hBN) photonic struc-
tures, are used as the emitter platform. The optical properties of these heterostructures
are characterized through reflectivity and photoluminescence measurements.
Atomic
force microscopy (AFM) and scanning electron microscopy (SEM) are used to evalu-
ate flake thickness and etching quality. These experimental inputs inform a simulation
framework developed using Lumerical FDTD and MODE solvers.
Circular Bragg gratings (bullseye cavities) are designed to improve optical performance,
with their parameters optimized through a combination of universal scaling and Bayesian
optimization techniques. The resulting cavity designs are fabricated and evaluated ex-
perimentally. Reflectivity spectra and photoluminescence measurements are analyzed to
assess the influence of cavity geometry on light–matter interaction.
This combined theoretical and experimental study contributes to the development of more
efficient single-photon sources by integrating TMD emitters with nanophotonic cavities
tailored enhanced quantum emission.

Introduction
1.1
Area of Interest
Quantum technologies have progressed rapidly since their inception in the early 20th
century. The driver of recent advancements is the so-called second quantum revolution,
which encompasses applications such as quantum computing, quantum communication,
and quantum sensing. Among these, quantum computing has drawn particular attention
due to its potential to solve certain classes of problems exponentially faster than classical
computers.
Unlike classical systems that rely on binary bits (0 or 1), quantum computers operate with
quantum bits, or qubits, which can exist in superpositions of states. This is enabled by
the principles of quantum mechanics, particularly superposition, which allows qubits to
represent and process a continuum of information simultaneously.
Another fundamental property is quantum entanglement, where the quantum state of
one qubit is intrinsically linked to that of another, regardless of spatial separation. En-
tanglement is a critical resource for quantum teleportation, error correction, and many
quantum algorithms.
Together, superposition and entanglement empower quantum algorithms such as Shor’s
algorithm, which can factor large integers exponentially faster than the best-known clas-
sical approaches. Since the difficulty of factorization underpins widely used encryption
methods like RSA, quantum computing poses both transformative potential and serious
cryptographic challenges.
There are several physical implementations of qubits, including trapped ions, supercon-
ducting circuits, and semiconductor quantum dots.
This project focuses on photonic
qubits, where information is encoded in the quantum states of photons, such as their
polarization, time-bin, orbital angular momentum, or spatial mode [1].
Photons possess several properties that make them highly suitable for quantum informa-
tion processing:
• They interact weakly with the environment, which makes them inherently robust
against decoherence.
• Photonic systems can operate at room temperature, avoiding the need for cryogenic
cooling.
• Photons travel at the speed of light and are thus ideal for quantum communication
and networking.
Multiple photonic platforms are being explored, each with specific trade-offs in scalabil-
ity, integration, and photon generation. A critical distinction lies in the nature of the light
sources: non-deterministic sources emit photons probabilistically, such as in sponta-
neous parametric down-conversion, where emission events are governed by chance. In
contrast, deterministic sources can emit a single photon on demand for each excitation
cycle, which is crucial for scalable quantum circuits.
This project investigates deterministic photonic qubits based on two-dimensional (2D)
materials, specifically transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDs) like WSe2, and hexagonal
boron nitride (hBN). These atomically thin materials are uniquely suited for integration into

exciton binding energies, and compatibility with van der Waals heterostructure.
Recent studies have demonstrated that localized excitons in monolayer TMDs can emit
single photons under optical excitation, with deterministic control achieved via strain or de-

However, their quantum efficiency and brightness remain limited, primarily due to inef-

nanophotonic cavities have been employed to enhance light–matter interactions through
the Purcell effect [5], which accelerates spontaneous emission by modifying the local pho-
tonic density of states. Certain cavity geometries, such as circular Bragg grating (bulls-
eye) structures, offer the additional advantage of broadband operation and directional
emission, improving both the emission rate and collection efficiency [6]. When coupled
with strain-engineered TMD monolayers, these structures provide a scalable path toward
efficient, on-chip quantum light sources.

Extensive research has focused on engineering optical cavities to enhance the Purcell
factor [7, 8, 9], which quantifies how much a cavity environment can accelerate the spon-
taneous emission of an emitter. As described in Equation (5.5) of [10], the luminescence
efficiency η is governed by the competition between radiative and non-radiative recombi-
nation processes:

Here, Γrad and Γnrad represent the radiative and non-radiative recombination rates, re-
spectively. Enhancing Γrad through the Purcell effect (i.e., reducing the radiative lifetime)
leads to improved luminescence efficiency—provided non-radiative channels remain sup-
pressed.
Transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDs), such as WSe2, have emerged as promising
candidates for deterministic single-photon sources due to their atomic thickness, strong
excitonic binding, and compatibility with van der Waals integration. However, their quan-
tum emission performance is often limited by low spontaneous emission rates and in-
efficient photon extraction, which are particularly critical for scalable quantum photonic
circuits.
One approach to address these limitations is the integration of emitters into photonic
nanostructures, such as circular Bragg grating (bullseye) cavities. These cavities enhance
emission by modifying the photonic density of states and improving collection efficiency
through directional emission. Their effectiveness, however, is highly sensitive to spa-
tial alignment, with lateral displacements of just tens of nanometers leading to significant
reductions in the Purcell factor [7].
Hexagonal boron nitride (hBN) is used in this study as cavity material due to its flat-
ness and favorable dielectric properties, including low optical loss and a relatively high

1.3
State of the Art
With the growing interest in scalable quantum photonic platforms, recent research has
shifted toward integrating quantum emitters with photonic architectures that enhance per-
formance metrics such as emission rate, brightness, and collection efficiency. Among
the various cavity designs explored, circular Bragg grating bullseye (CBGB) cavities have
proven particularly effective in coupling surface-level quantum emitters with photonic modes
in compact, broadband structures.
CBGB cavities are specifically designed to provide directional emission and enhanced
extraction efficiency by satisfying higher-order Bragg conditions. Their geometry enables
coupling to transverse electric slab modes with vertically extended field profiles, which
is critical for maximizing interaction with emitters positioned at or near the top surface of
the cavity. Additionally, the use of reflective bottom layers (such as DBRs or gold mirrors)
further boosts collection efficiency by redirecting otherwise lost emission into the collection
optics.
In a recent implementation, a hybrid CBGB platform was demonstrated using a WSe2
monolayer transferred onto an Al0.31Ga0.69As bullseye cavity, which featured a central
SiO2 nanopillar to induce strain and localize the excitonic emitter [7].
This structure
achieved more than a five times enhancement in photoluminescence intensity compared
to planar references. The cavity also demonstrated a second-order correlation function
of g(2)(0) = 0.25, confirming single-photon emission. Importantly, the authors reported
that the Purcell enhancement was highly position-dependent, with coupling dropping by
nearly 50% when the emitter was laterally displaced by just 70 nm from the center of the
cavity.
These findings show the importance of precise emitter placement and cavity design for
optimizing light–matter interaction in 2D-material-based quantum photonic systems.

Background
This chapter presents the theoretical basis for the study of single-photon emitters (SPEs),
focusing on their role in quantum photonics. It introduces the core principles of single-
photon emission and the metrics used to evaluate emitter performance. Special emphasis
is placed on hexagonal boron nitride (hBN) and transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDs)
as promising two-dimensional materials for quantum photonic applications.
2.1
Single-Photon Emitters
Single-photon emitters (SPEs) are one of the building blocks in quantum technologies,
particularly in quantum photonics. Deterministic SPEs are defined as systems capable
of producing one and only one photon per excitation cycle—on demand and with high
fidelity [13]. These emitters enable key operations in quantum communication, quantum
key distribution, and photonic quantum computing [1].
The fundamental physical model behind most SPEs is the two-level quantum system.
The goal is to engineer a system—such as an atom, quantum dot, or defect site—into
having only two accessible energy states between which a single optical transition can
occur [14]. In this project, the two-level systems are realized using two-dimensional (2D)
materials, which will be discussed in more detail in Section 2.2. These materials are par-
ticularly attractive due to their strong excitonic effects, atomic thinness, and compatibility
with nanophotonic integration [15]
Figure 2.1 shows a simplified energy diagram of a two-level emitter. An electron initially
occupies the ground state |0⟩. When energy is supplied to the system (e.g., through
optical or electrical excitation), the electron transitions to the excited state |1⟩. It can then
relax back to the ground state by spontaneously emitting a photon with energy ∆E =
Eexc −Eground. This energy is consistent for each emission cycle, which ensures that the
emitted photons are spectrally identical [10].
Figure 2.1: Schematic of a two-level system where the photon energy is given by ∆E =
Eexc −Eground.
This two-level emission process lies at the heart of most SPE platforms, from isolated
atoms to solid-state systems such as quantum dots, color centers, and strain-localized
excitons in TMD monolayers. One of the major challenges is the ability to engineer and
tune ∆E to match the desired application—e.g., spectral overlap with a cavity mode or

achieved using strain or local dielectric environment [16].

2.1.2
Indistinguishability
Indistinguishability refers to the ability of a single-photon emitter (SPE) to generate pho-
tons that are identical in all relevant quantum properties, such as frequency, polarization
and spatial mode. This is crucial for many quantum information protocols, where multi-
photon interference is required to implement entangling gates [1, 13].

Mandel (HOM) interferometer. A schematic of the experimental setup is shown in Fig-

2.2
2D Materials
Two-dimensional (2D) materials have emerged as new platforms for quantum optics due
to their atomically thin geometry, strong light–matter interaction, and tunable electronic
and optical properties [19]. These characteristics allow them to support tightly confined
excitons, sharp emission features, and flexible integration into nanophotonic structures.
The ability to engineer their properties via external stimuli—such as electric fields, strain,
or dielectric environment—adds another layer of tunability that is highly attractive for quan-
tum device applications [15, 20].
In contrast to traditional bulk materials, 2D materials can be stacked into van der Waals
heterostructures without the need for lattice matching, enabling seamless integration of
dissimilar materials with tailored functionality [21]. This usnique stacking capability al-
lows the design of hybrid quantum photonic platforms that combine different optical and
electronic functionalities in a single, atomically sharp interface.

Among the large family of 2D materials, two classes have received particular attention
for quantum light emission: hexagonal boron nitride (hBN) and transition metal dichalco-
genides (TMDs). The following sections focus on these two material families in greater
detail.
2.2.1
Hexagonal Boron Nitride
Hexagonal boron nitride (hBN) has emerged as a versatile material for quantum nanopho-
tonics, particularly as a dielectric platform for photonic structures. Its wide bandgap en-
sures optical transparency across the visible spectrum,and its mechanical stability make
it suitable for etching, high-resolution lithography, and cryogenic operation. As a van der
Waals material, hBN can be exfoliated into thin flakes and seamlessly integrated with
other two-dimensional crystals, enabling layered photonic architectures.
These properties make hBN a compelling choice for fabricating nanophotonic structures.
Its relatively high refractive index and low optical loss allow for tight mode confinement
and high-quality optical resonances. These characteristics are especially relevant for
integrated devices where cavity enhancement plays a central role.
Several studies have demonstrated the feasibility of using exfoliated hBN to construct
photonic crystal cavities, nanobeam resonators, and circular Bragg grating structures,
confirming its suitability as a low-loss photonic medium [22, 23, 24, 25]. Its transparency,
mechanical robustness, and compatibility with 2D heterostructures make it a strong can-
didate for scalable quantum photonic platforms.
Although hBN can host optically active defects, this study focuses on its role as a photonic
substrate and cavity material, with WSe2 used as the emitter layer due to its emission
characteristics as stated in Section 1.2.
2.2.2
Transition metal dichalcogenides
Transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDs) are another major class of 2D materials capable
of single-photon emission. In monolayer form, TMDs exhibit direct bandgaps and strong
excitonic effects due to reduced dielectric screening. SPEs in these systems are generally
induced by local strain, edges, or defects that confine excitons into potential wells.
The resulting emission is typically redshifted from the free exciton resonance and displays
narrow linewidths, sometimes below 10 meV, with polarization control and high photosta-
bility [19]. These features make TMDs suitable for integration into photonic waveguides,
cavities, and on-chip nanostructures.
However, challenges persist in achieving deterministic and reproducible emitter formation.
Emission wavelength, brightness, and stability vary significantly due to heterogeneity in
the strain field or substrate interactions. Improvements in deterministic strain engineering
and lithographically defined traps have enabled better control, but uniform emitter arrays
remain an open goal.
TMDs offer a promising combination of bright emission, tunability, and integration poten-
tial, making them one of the most compelling candidates for scalable on-chip quantum
photonic applications.

2.3
Cavity-Enhanced Emission: Bullseye Structures
Efficient generation and extraction of single photons from quantum emitters can be signif-
icantly enhanced through integration with optical cavities. Among the various strategies,
bullseye (circular Bragg grating) cavities and nanopillar-based strain engineering offer a
compelling platform due to their ability to simultaneously increase the Purcell factor and
photon collection efficiency.
A figure of merit for single-photon sources is the Purcell factor, which quantifies the en-
hancement of the spontaneous emission rate. This is typically achieved when a quantum
emitter is positioned inside an optical cavity with a high quality factor (Q) and a small mode
volume (V ).

Optimization Procedure
To optimize the geometry of the cavity, two distinct methods were employed: the Universal
Design Method and Bayesian Optimization. The Universal Design Method ensures proper
wavelength scaling and material dispersion compensation. Bayesian Optimization then

3.1.4.2
Bayesian Optimization
To fine-tune the parameters for simultaneously maximizing both the Purcell factor and the
collection efficiency, a different optimization approach was adopted—Bayesian optimiza-
tion. This method is known for being sample-efficient and particularly effective for optimiz-
ing complex, black-box functions. In this case, the objective landscape is six-dimensional
and not explicitly known.
Bayesian optimization works by building a probabilistic model of the objective function,
typically using a Gaussian Process. It then uses an acquisition function, such as ex-
pected improvement, to decide where to sample next. This strategy allows for intelligent
exploration and exploitation of the parameter space, requiring far fewer evaluations than
random search methods. This is especially valuable for computationally expensive tasks
like numerical simulations.
The main drawback of this approach is that it doesn’t inherently support parallel evalua-
tion, limiting the use of available simulation resources for faster optimization.

Fabrication Procedures
3.2.1
Exfoliation
To obtain flakes of varying thicknesses, we employed the mechanical exfoliation tech-
nique. This process involves peeling layers from a bulk crystal, in this case, hexagonal
boron nitride (hBN), using adhesive tapes. By repeatedly pressing and lifting the tape,
successive ”generations” of flakes are produced, gradually thinning the material.
This method is especially important for isolating monolayers of transition metal dichalco-
genides (TMDs), where exfoliation must continue until atomically thin layers are achieved.
The exfoliated flakes are ultimately transferred onto a polymer host, typically polydimethyl-
siloxane (PDMS), which offers two advantages.
First, for thicker hBN flakes, PDMS facilitates straightforward inspection under an optical
microscope and enables application of the transfer matrix method (TMM) (see Appendix A
and Section 3.3.1). Second, for atomically thin TMD layers, PDMS allows rapid identifi-
cation via room-temperature photoluminescence measurements using our microscope
setup.

3.3.3
AFM Principle
Atomic Force Microscopy (AFM) is a scanning probe technique that enables nanoscale
surface characterization by physically probing a sample with a sharp tip attached to a
flexible cantilever. The technique was first introduced by Binnig, Quate, and Gerber in
1986, who demonstrated that “the force between the tip and the sample can be kept
constant, and the surface topography can be imaged with atomic resolution” [30]. The
tip–sample interaction induces a deflection of the cantilever, which is measured optically
via a laser beam reflected into a photodiode detector.
AFM can operate in various modes depending on how the tip interacts with the surface. In
contact mode, the tip continuously touches the surface, allowing for high-resolution imag-
ing but at the risk of damaging soft materials like 2D monolayers. In non-contact mode,
the cantilever oscillates near the surface without making physical contact, minimizing in-
teraction forces but often with reduced resolution or feedback stability.
In this work, tapping mode (also called intermittent-contact mode) was used to character-
ize WSe2 monolayers and hBN encapsulation layers. Tapping mode offers a balance be-
tween resolution and non-destructiveness by oscillating the cantilever near its resonance
frequency and briefly contacting the surface at the bottom of each oscillation cycle. This
minimizes lateral forces and sample deformation while maintaining reliable topographic
contrast. It is particularly well-suited for fragile, atomically thin layers like hBN and TMDs,
which can be easily displaced or scratched under continuous tip contact.
In tapping mode, changes in oscillation amplitude or phase are used to generate topogra-
phy and phase-contrast images, allowing for detailed visualization of features such as step
heights, wrinkles, or nanopillar-induced deformation without damaging the monolayer.

3.3.4
SEM Principle
Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) is a high-resolution imaging technique that utilizes
a focused beam of electrons to probe the surface of a sample. When the primary electron
beam interacts with the specimen, it generates various signals, including secondary elec-
trons, which are used to construct detailed images of surface morphology. The method
was pioneered by Knoll and Ruska in 1932 [32].
Unlike optical microscopy, SEM offers superior depth of field and spatial resolution down
to a few nanometers, making it an essential tool for characterizing nanostructures and
thin films. In this work, SEM was employed to confirm the structural integrity of fabricated
nanocavities. The technique provided feedback during device fabrication by revealing
features such as the etch angle of the hBN flakes.
The contrast in SEM images arises from variations in how different areas of the sam-
ple emit secondary and backscattered electrons, which depend on material composition,
surface curvature, and topographic height. Because SEM is based on electron–matter
interactions rather than light reflection or absorption, it is particularly well-suited for imag-
ing non-transparent nanostructured materials like metal layers, dielectric stacks, and 2D
materials on various substrates.

5
Conclusion and Outlook
In this work, a photonic cavity design was simulated, fabricated, and characterized with
the goal of enhancing single-photon emission from localized quantum emitters. A trench-
based cavity geometry was developed and optimized using FDTD simulations to achieve
spectral resonance in the 730–740 nm range, where the target emitters are expected to
emit. This process involved the development of custom scripts to automate the design
pipeline while accounting for fabrication constraints.
Although photoluminescence measurements of the final device did not reveal enhanced
single-photon emission, likely due to emitter quenching or misalignment during fabrication,
important progress was made in validating the cavity platform. A high-Q resonance mode
was consistently observed around 880 nm in both reflectivity and PL spectra. While this
mode was not the initial design target, its presence indicates strong optical confinement
and enhanced collection efficiency, likely associated with the cavity’s fundamental TE
mode. Notably, the mode’s spectral position remained stable across different geometry
variants, showing both its robustness and the need for finer control to shift it into the
desired spectral range.
Looking ahead, this high-Q mode represents a promising foundation for future optimiza-
tion. By refining the cavity geometry to shift the mode toward the emitter wavelengths,
improved spectral overlap can be achieved. Furthermore, integrating emitter-stabilizing
strategies—such as electrical contacts or local strain engineering—may enhance coupling
and preserve emitter brightness during and after fabrication.
In conclusion, while enhancement of single-photon emission was not observed in this
iteration, the work demonstrates a viable and tunable cavity platform. Its photonic perfor-
mance aligns well with simulations and provides a solid basis for future integration with
quantum emitters in deterministic quantum photonic architectures.

Transfer Matrix Method
The Transfer-Matrix Method (TMM) is an analytical technique used to calculate the optical
response of multilayer structures composed of dielectric or metallic films. It provides a
systematic way to compute the amplitude reflectance and transmittance from a stack of
layers with prescribed thicknesses and refractive indices. The method is based on wave
optics and the application of boundary conditions at each interface.
The procedure involves the following steps:
• The multilayer structure is conceptually divided into individual elements, each rep-
resenting either a single homogeneous layer or a boundary between two layers.
• For each layer or interface, an individual transfer matrix Mi is defined. This matrix
describes the relationship between the forward and backward propagating wave
amplitudes on either side of the layer.
• The individual matrices Mi are derived from the Fresnel coefficients for reflection
and transmission, using relations that convert between scattering matrices (S) and
transfer matrices (M).
• The total transfer matrix for the stack, M = MNMN−1 · · · M1, is obtained by multiply-
ing the matrices in sequence, starting from the first interface to the last.
• Finally, the overall scattering matrix is retrieved by converting the total transfer matrix
back to the S matrix, from which the amplitude reflectance and transmittance are
directly extracted.
Mathematically, for a single layer of thickness d and refractive index n, the transfer matrix
is given by:

Code Used In Simulations
The simulations and optimizations presented in this thesis were supported by a modular
Python framework developed specifically for Lumerical FDTD and MODE Solutions. This
framework automates the setup, execution, and post-processing of nanophotonic cavity
designs—particularly bullseye structures—by integrating the Lumerical API with optimiza-
tion routines such as universal parameter scaling and Bayesian optimization.
The code includes tools to:
• Generate simulation geometries and import refractive index data.
• Automate local and remote job submissions to HPC clusters via SSH.
• Perform parameter sweeps and optimization using scikit-optimize.
• Extract and visualize simulation results, including Purcell factors and collection effi-
ciencies.
The full codebase is available on GitHub at: https://github.com/henricolini/Circular-Bragg-
Grating-Optimization.git
