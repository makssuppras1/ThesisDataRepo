Abstract
The thesis explores the application of Multiphoton Lightsheet Fluorescence Microscopy
(MP-LSFM) for detecting diseases in tissue, with a particular focus on colonic structures.
Colonic diseases, ranging from acute infections to colorectal cancer (CRC), impose sig-
nificant health burdens globally. Traditional diagnostic methods often involve invasive
procedures and lengthy processing times, which limit their efficacy. MP-LSFM combines
the advantages of two-photon excitation and light-sheet microscopy. It offers deep tis-
sue penetration, superior optical sectioning, and reduced photodamage and phototoxicity,
making it a promising alternative for non-invasive tissue imaging.
The study aims to determine whether MP-LSFM can distinguish between healthy and dis-
eased colon tissues by visualizing distinct morphological characteristics. It hypothesizes
that MP-LSFM can differentiate between normal and diseased colon morphology, identify
specific biomarkers indicative of disease presence, and offer superior advantages over
other imaging modalities such as Multiphoton Microscopy (MPM), Optical Coherence To-
mography (OCT), and traditional histology.
Through detailed imaging protocols and comparisons of healthy and infected colon sam-
ples, this research demonstrates the potential of MP-LSFM in identifying morphologi-
cal abnormalities associated with infections and cancer. The results confirm that MP-
LSFM has been successfully utilized for the first time, to differentiate between healthy
and Staphylococcus epidermidis-infected colon tissues by using the crypt shape as a
biomarker. The effectiveness of MP-LSFM was compared with MPM and histology, demon-
strating that MP-LSFM offers comparable structural details of colon tissue to these es-
tablished imaging methods. MP-LSFM is providing high-resolution, three-dimensional
images of colonic tissues, facilitating early disease detection, and advancing colorectal
cancer screening and research. Comparisons with MPM, OCT, and histology underscore
the enhanced capabilities and advantages of MP-LSFM in terms of resolution, depth pen-
etration, and non-invasiveness.

Introduction
1.1
Background and motivation
Colonic diseases can range from acute infections to cancer and impose a significant
health burden on individuals of all genders across the globe [1]. Therefore, understanding
colonic pathology is crucial for accurately diagnosing and treating colon diseases, guid-
ing interventions, and developing targeted therapies to improve patient outcomes. It also
helps in identifying early disease signs for timely intervention. Colon infection may result
from infection with bacteria, viruses, or parasites. These cause an inflammatory form of
diarrhoea, which comprises the majority of cases that manifest as acute diarrhoea, such
as fever, tenesmus, abdominal pain, red, and mucoid loose bowel movements [2]. The
infection could develop into chronic inflammation. The prolonged inflammation stage dam-
ages the GI tract. Many studies [3], [4], [5] show intestinal microbes significantly impact
the development of colorectal cancer (CRC). It remains unclear whether microorganisms
or their products can cause CRC in humans directly or induce inflammation that leads to
tumorigenesis [6]. The study of the connection between chronic inflammation and cancer
is still being investigated widely.
CRC is one of the most common cancers globally. It ranks as the third most frequent
type of cancer. According to the World Health Organization (WHO), CRC caused around
930,000 mortality and nearly 2 million new cases in 2020. It is expected to be 1.6 million
deaths (an increase of 73%) and an increase of more than 3 million new cases in 2024 [7].
In Denmark, over 5,000 individuals are diagnosed with colorectal cancer, and annually,
1.800 people pass away from colon cancer [8]. However, colorectal cancer tends to be
found at an advanced stage, and there are few therapeutic options available. Therefore,
cancer screening is important for detecting cancer and preventing its development. In
the clinic, the diagnosis of cancer requires a biopsy, and patients need to undergo an
invasive procedure to take pathological tissue and examine it with a histological process.
This traditional procedure has risks of complications during the acquisition of samples
from patients, and the chemical process would take considerable time. To overcome
this limitation, various imaging tests are being used to identify cancer, such as Magnetic
Resonance Imaging (MRI), Computed Tomography (CT scan), and ultrasounds, which
provide imaging with wider field of view but lack cellular resolution [9]. Advanced tech-
niques like Positron Emission Tomography (PET) or Single Photon Emission Computed
Tomography (SPECT) imaging require specific labels, which can be complicated, costly,
and time-consuming [10]. As a result, this study adopted new technology, using Multi-
photon Lightsheet Fluorescence Microscopy (MP-LSFM ) to overcome these limitations.

1.2
Thesis aim and hypothesis
The study aims to determine if the MP-LSFM could be a potential technique/system to
visualize tissue morphology, focusing on colon structure. The main goal is to distinguish
between normal and diseased colon morphology. Differentiating morphological character-
istics between healthy and diseased colon tissue can indicate different types of colon dis-
eases or stages of cancer. This improvement could help in the early detection of colorectal
diseases, which could be developed into the application for colorectal cancer screening
or acknowledge further research and drug development, which will be discussed further
in section 7.5.
In the subsequent paragraph, additional hypotheses will be proposed with more precise
expectations regarding the studies.
• MP-LSFM can differentiate between healthy and diseased colon tissues based on
distinct morphological characteristics.
• MP-LSFM can identify specific biomarkers within colon tissues that are indicative of
disease presence, such as CRC and Staphylococcus epidermidis infections, among
others.
• MP-LSFM offers superior advantages over other imaging modalities such as opti-
cal coherence tomography (OCT), multiphoton microscopy (MPM), and traditional
histology in the detection of diseases in tissue samples.

2.1
Anatomy and structures of the colon
The colon is a part of the digestive system, which includes the organ known as the gas-
trointestinal (GI) tract. It is located in the abdomen underneath the stomach and is the
longest segment in the GI tract, connecting the small intestine to the anus. The colon is
found after the cecum, which is a pouch connected to the junction of the small and large
intestines. The colon has several segments, starting with the ascending colon, transverse
colon, and descending colon, which are upward from the cecum along the right side of the
abdomen, run across the abdomen from right to left and travel down the left side of the
abdomen, respectively. The end part of the colon is the sigmoid colon, which is S-shaped
and connects to the rectum. The last part of the colon is connected to the anal canal [11]
[12], as illustrated in Figure 2.1.
Figure 2.1: Anatomical human colon. Figure sourced from reference [11]
The colon’s functions are to digest food partially through the actions of its bacterial flora
and to absorb water, electrolytes, vitamins, salts, and minerals from the undigested food
[13]. It is also responsible for the formation and storage remained solid waste, known as
stool, before it moves forward to the rectum and passes through the anus to exit the body
[12]. The structure of the colon is a circular tube that has space in the middle called a
lumen, which is the path of digested solid food to move further to the anus [14].
The colon consists of 4 layers. Starting from the outer to the inner:
• Serosa is the outermost layer part of the visceral peritoneum. This layer is very
smooth, which prevents friction between the colon and surrounding organs in the
abdominal cavity [15] [14].

• Muscularis propria is the layer next to the serosa toward the inner part. It is di-
vided into two sub-layers: the outer longitudinal layer and the inner circular layer.
They are responsible for facilitating the transportation of food particles throughout
the gastrointestinal system (wave-like muscle contractions), controlled by smooth
muscles [15] [14].
• Submucosa is the third layer from the outer; it comprises the nerve submucous
plexus, blood vessels, and elastic fibres composed of collagen that maintain the
shape of the intestine while allowing for increased capacity for stretching [15] [14].
• Mucosa is the innermost and closest layer to the lumen, which is the central cavity
of the colon where the contents (e.g., digested food, faeces) pass through. Thus,
this layer directly interacts with gut contents. The epithelium layer primarily com-
prises columnar epithelial cells (crypts of Lieberkuhn) and goblet cells responsible
for producing and secreting mucus to lubricate the colon hollow and facilitate fae-
ces to move smoothly. These crypts extend through the lamina propria, the layer
of connective tissue containing blood vessels and immune cells that defend against
pathogens and the common microbes in the colon. The muscularis mucosa is an
outer layer of the mucosa that has a function similar to that of the muscularis propria,
which is controlled movement, but in the local mucosa area [15] [14], as demon-
strated in Figure 2.2.
This study focused on visualizing this structure, mainly on the crypt structures, be-
cause it is the first symptom of the abnormality of the epithelial tissue in colorectal
cancer.

2.2
Hallmarks of Cancer
Based on a prior study by Douglas et al. [17], ”Hallmarks of Cancer” is the term used
to describe the capabilities of cancer development and progression, as demonstrated in
Figure 2.3.
1. The ability to sustain proliferative signalling. Cancer cells can be signalled to continu-
ously divide and grow due to excessively stimulated growth factors.
2. The ability to evade growth suppressors is the process by which cancer cells develop
without slowing down the growth process that normally occurs in normal cells.
3. The ability to resist cell death: Cancer cells can avoid and inhibit mechanisms of apop-
tosis, which normally helps eliminate damaged or unwanted cells.
4. The ability to replicate continuously, which cells can divide unlimitedly.
5. The ability to induce angiogenesis in tumour cells can form new blood vessels to supply
the nutrients and oxygen they need to grow.
6. The ability to invade nearby tissues and spread to distant sites in the body (metastasis).
Figure 2.3: Hallmarks of Cancer. Figure sourced from reference [17]
2.3
Biomarker for cancer
The natural biomarkers for this study are nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (NADH) and
flavin adenine dinucleotide (FAD) because they are native endogenous fluorophores that
are autofluorescent. This means they naturally emit fluorescence without the need for
external dyes or labels when exposed to specific wavelengths of light [18] [19]. They
offer several advantages over external fluorophores, such as biocompatibility, reduced

3.1
Fluorescence contrast mechanisms
Fluorescence is a physical phenomenon in which a substance absorbs light or other elec-
tromagnetic radiation at one wavelength (often ultraviolet light) and then emits light at
a longer wavelength, usually visible light [32]. Fluorescence contrast mechanisms in mi-
croscopy are methods that utilize the properties of fluorescent dyes, proteins, or molecules
to visualize specific structures within a sample [32]. Fluorophores are chemicals that can
re-emit light upon excitation, absorb energy at a specific wavelength and then emit it at a
different wavelength when exposed to the right conditions [19] [33]. These mechanisms
exploit fundamental fluorescence processes to enhance microscopic images’ contrast and
clarity. The fundamental principles of fluorescence are illustrated in Figure 3.1 and de-
scribed below.
Absorption (excitation): Fluorescent molecules (fluorophores) absorb photons of light at
a specific wavelength. This energy absorption excites the electrons in the fluorophore,
raising them to a higher energy state (excited state) [32].
Excited state: The fluorophore remains in this high-energy state for a short period of time,
typically on the order of nanoseconds. Some energy may be lost during this time through
non-radiative processes, such as vibrational relaxation [32].
Emission: The excited electrons return to their ground state by emitting photons of light.
The emitted light has a longer wavelength (lower energy) than the absorbed light due
to the energy lost during the excited state. This emitted light is detected and forms the
fluorescent signal used in imaging [32].

3.2
Fluorescence microscopy
Fluorescence microscopy is a specialized type of optical microscopy that uses the phe-
nomenon of fluorescence to study the properties of organic and inorganic substances.
It is particularly powerful for biological applications, as it allows for the specific labelling
and imaging of cellular structures and molecules. It employs a specific light source, such
as a laser or a high-intensity lamp, that emits particular wavelengths of light to excite the
fluorophores in the sample. When these fluorophores are exposed to the appropriate
wavelengths, they absorb the energy and then emit it at a different wavelength, enabling
the visualization of various cellular components and structures [32]. In this study, external
fluorophores were not used. Instead, natural fluorophores in the samples, such as NADH
and FAD, were utilized. These natural fluorophores have been used in the detection of
fluorescence in previous studies [19] [25], as explained in Section 2.3.
Fluorescence microscopy can be categorized into several techniques based on the method
of excitation, including single (one) photon fluorescence microscopy (SPEM), two-photon
fluorescence microscopy (TPEM), and multiphoton microscopy (MPM), which involves
two or more photons for excitation [32]. Two-photon fluorescence microscopy is the pri-
mary method used in this study.
Figure 3.2 demonstrates the theoretical background of single-photon (a) and two-photon
(b) excitation in fluorescence microscopy. SPEM involves a single high-energy photon
(blue arrow) absorbed by a fluorophore, which then transitions from the ground state (S0)
to an excited state (S1). The fluorophore emits a photon (green arrow) as it returns to the
ground state, resulting in fluorescence emission [35] [32].
On the other hand, TPEM relies on two lower-energy photons (red arrows) simultaneously
absorbed by a fluorophore, transitioning it from the ground state (S0) to an excited state
(S1). Similar to one-photon excitation, the fluorophore emits a photon (green arrow) as it
returns to the ground state, resulting in fluorescence emission [32] [35]. These photons
have roughly half the energy needed to excite the fluorophore. Hence, TPEM typically
uses longer wavelengths than SPEM, reducing photobleaching and phototoxicity outside
the focal volume, enhancing cell viability during imaging. It offers greater tissue penetra-
tion depth due to near-infrared light, which scatters less in biological tissues. Additionally,
it provides high-resolution and deep-tissue imaging in live specimens [32] [35].

Light-sheet fluorescence microscopy (LSFM)
Light-sheet fluorescence microscopy is also known as selective plane illumination mi-
croscopy (SPIM) [35]. LSFM illuminates the sample with a thin sheet of light perpendicu-
lar to the detection lens. This method selectively illuminates and images thin slices of the
sample, allowing the lightsheet to go through the sample.

Figure 3.4 (a) illustrates the fundamental concepts of LSFM. The excitation laser beam is
shaped into a thin lightsheet using a cylindrical lens. This lightsheet illuminates a specific
plane within the sample. The fluorescence emitted from the illuminated plane is collected
by the detection objective and is oriented perpendicular to the lightsheet. The fluores-
cence light is then captured by a sensitive camera (CCD or sCMOS), allowing for high-

resolution imaging. By scanning the lightsheet through the sample, a series of optical
sections can be obtained to reconstruct a three-dimensional image.
Lightsheet fluorescence microscopy (LSFM) is highly beneficial for imaging due to its high
speed and high temporal resolution. One of its key advantages is reduced photobleaching
and phototoxicity, as only the plane of interest is illuminated, making it ideal for long-
term live imaging of large specimens. This is particularly advantageous for developing
applications for in vivo or clinical studies. LSFM also boasts fast acquisition speeds,
allowing parallel imaging of entire planes. Additionally, it offers excellent optical sectioning
ability without the need for a pinhole. However, there are challenges in sample mounting
and positioning, as precise alignment of the objectives is required [35].
The Gaussian beam is a type of light beam commonly used in lightsheet microscopy.
It is the most common and simplest type of laser beam. Therefore, it is the main laser
beam used in this study. The Gaussian beam has a bell-shaped intensity profile, with
the intensity of the light being highest at the centre and falling off exponentially towards
the edges. This results in a bell-shaped curve when plotted. Figure 3.4 (b) shows how a
Gaussian beam diverges as it propagates, spreading out and losing power density over
distance. This profile provides uniform illumination in the central region of the beam, which
is beneficial for illuminating small areas of a sample. Since it focuses light on a small spot,
it achieves high resolution, making it ideal for detailed imaging of fine structures within a
sample.

4.1.5.2
Two-Photon Microscopy (MPM)
MPM uses near-infrared light to induce multiphoton excitation in the fluorophores. This
process requires high photon density, typically achieved with pulsed lasers [47]. Two or
more photons are absorbed simultaneously by a fluorophore at an excitation wavelength
double (for two-photon) the wavelength used in conventional fluorescence microscopy.
The MPM uses two bandpass filters designed to allow only specific ranges of wavelengths
to pass through.
1. 500-550 nm bandpass filter: This filter allows light with wavelengths between 500 and
550 nm to pass through. The light in this range is typically associated with green fluores-
cence, called the ”green channel”. This filter will capture that emission using fluorescent
dyes or proteins emitting light in the green spectrum. This channel is useful for visualizing
structures or molecules in the sample that are tagged with green-emitting fluorophores,
such as GFP (green fluorescent protein) or other similar dyes.
2. 570-640 nm bandpass filter: This filter permits light with wavelengths between 570
nm and 640 nm to pass through. The light in this range corresponds to red fluorescence,
which will be called the ”red channel.” This filter is used to detect emissions from red flu-
orescent dyes or proteins. This channel is used to visualize structures or molecules in
the sample that are tagged with red-emitting fluorophores, such as RFP (red fluorescent
protein) or similar dyes.
MPM offers several significant advantages, making it an invaluable biological research
tool. One of its primary benefits is the ability to perform live cell and tissue imaging,
making it suitable for long-term studies with reduced phototoxicity and enabling in vivo
applications. Additionally, MPM reduces photodamage as the excitation is confined to
the focal plane, minimizing damage to surrounding tissue. Furthermore, MPM provides
high-resolution images with fine details, enhancing the ability to observe intricate cellu-
lar structures and processes. These attributes make MPM particularly advantageous for
studying dynamic processes in live tissues while preserving their integrity and achieving
detailed visualization [47] [48].

5.2
Preparation of biological samples
This section details the preparation of the colon samples, encompassing everything from
acquisition to their preservation.
5.2.1
Dissection techniques
The initial step of colon acquisition is dissection, an essential process in which biological
organs or samples are dissected to examine their structure and/or functions. This study
includes the dissection process of harvesting the colon from the piglets. The subject is
placed in a surgical tray with the ventral (belly) side up.
The steps of dissection are demonstrated in Figure 5.2.
Incision 1 is done using a shape scalpel, cut down from the middle of the thorax to the end
of the pelvic region to open the abdominal cavity, making two lateral sides. Alternatively,
this step can be modified by cutting approximately 2 cm with a scalpel and continuing
cutting along the midsagittal line to the abdomen with scissors. This method helps to
avoid cutting too deeply, damaging the internal organs, and cutting the umbilical vein and
arteries [51].
Incision 2 involves cutting above the umbilical cord. This incision is critical because it
allows the dissection to progress without damaging the umbilical vessels, which could
22
Multiphoton Lightsheet Fluorescence Microscopy for Disease Detection in Tissue
==PAGE:34==
lead to unnecessary bleeding and obscure the surgical field. The umbilical cord area in
pigs, as in humans, is a delicate region where the umbilical vein and arteries are located.
These structures are vital for fetal circulation and are located near other crucial abdominal
organs. The dissection avoids these structures by making an incision above the umbilical
cord, thereby minimizing the risk of accidental damage.
Incision 3 involves cutting into the middle of the hind legs. This incision is designed to
open the lower abdominal and pelvic areas further. It provides an enhanced view and
more room to manipulate within the pelvic cavity, where parts of the colon are located.

5.2.2
Sample handling and preservation
After the dissection to obtain the colon, the identification of the colon involves locating
the cecum, which is the connecting part between the small and large intestines. Figure
5.1 demonstrates each section of the pig and human colons. The colon was harvested
approximately from the ascending part to the descending part — 30 cm for 10 pieces.
The colon samples contained faeces that needed to be cleaned; thus, the next step is a
cleaning process. The colon was temporarily kept and cleaned with phosphate-buffered
saline (PBS) because it has a salt concentration similar to that of the human body. This
helps maintain the osmotic balance, preventing cells from swelling or shrinking, which
could cause damage or lysis. It aids in stabilising the pH of the solution, ensuring that it
remains at a pH level of around 7.4, which is close to the physiological pH. This prevents
significant pH shifts that could potentially alter cellular functions or enzyme activities within
Multiphoton Lightsheet Fluorescence Microscopy for Disease Detection in Tissue
23
==PAGE:35==
the samples, and for the preservation of cellular functions. By mimicking physiological
conditions, PBS helps preserve cellular functions and structures during the storage period
[38]. The colon was longitudinally cut from its tubular shape into a single sheet to make it
easier to clean the remaining stool. The cleaning involved 6-7 washes with PBS solutions
until no faeces remained.
To determine and compare the fluorescence emissions of the fresh and fixed samples,
the samples were divided into two preservation methods.
5.2.2.1
Preservation for fresh samples
The fresh samples were taken from the first part of the colon (the ascending colon), the
middle part (the transverse colon), and the distal part (the descending colon).
Fresh samples were stored in Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle’s Medium (DMEM) and refrig-
erated at 4°C. The solution contains 4.5 g/L glucose and is without L-glutamine from
BioWhittaker. It is a widely used cell culture medium in biological and medical research as
it contains a high glucose content, a customizable nature, and a nutrient-rich composition.
It supports the short-term storage of fresh samples by providing a stable, nutrient-rich
environment that maintains cellular viability and metabolic activity. However, it is most
effective for short-term storage (hours to a few days) and is not intended for long-term
preservation [52].
5.2.2.2
Preservation for fixed samples
The rest of the samples were fixed with 4% formaldehyde for 24 hours, dehydrated with
70% ethanol (EtOH) for 2 hours, rinsed with PBS to clean out of ethanol, and stored in
PBS at approximately a refrigerator temperature (4 °C).
Fixation with 4% formaldehyde has several benefits, as formaldehyde cross-links proteins
and other biomolecules, stabilizing the tissue structure. This preservation is crucial for
maintaining the sample’s architecture, ensuring that the microscopic anatomy remains
as close to its natural state as possible during analysis. It also inactivates enzymes that
can cause autolysis (self-digestion) and putrefaction (decomposition by bacteria). This
prevents tissue degradation, essential for obtaining accurate and reliable imaging results
[53] [54].
Additionally, formaldehyde fixation ensures that the natural fluorescence emitted by the
tissue remains intact. This is particularly important for techniques like MP-LSFM in this
study, where natural fluorescence is used to image the sample. Lastly, fixed samples
are safer to handle as the fixation process neutralizes potential pathogens [54]. This
is particularly important when working with infectious or potentially hazardous biological
materials, which is a part of this study.

5.3
Histological techniques
Histology is the study of the structure of cells and tissues at the microscopic level. Proce-
dures in histology are designed to ensure precise and efficient preparation and analysis
of tissue samples [55].
The histology workflow overview is illustrated in Figure 5.3.
Figure 5.3: Histology workflow overview. Figure adapted from [56].
1. Dissection samples is the initial procedure for dissecting a piglet to obtain a colon
sample. This section is described in detail in the previous Section 5.2.
2. Placed into cassettes is the step to place samples in the cassettes. Colon samples
were collected from the ascending, middle, and descending sections (one piece from
each) of both healthy and diseased colons. These samples were immediately stored
in cassettes in 4% formaldehyde at the dissection site. The cassette is used to protect
the tissue samples during processing, preventing them from being lost or damaged. It
provides a convenient way to handle multiple samples and identify them.
3. Tissue processing is a crucial series of steps aimed at preparing biological tissue
samples. The cassettes are placed into a tissue processor, where the tissue undergoes
treatments to remove water and replace it with a solidifying medium, typically paraffin.
This process involves several stages to preserve the tissue’s structure, enhance its visi-
bility for detailed study, and prepare the sample for paraffin embedding [57] [58]. There
are two main steps in the tissue processing section:
3.1 Dehydration: The process of dehydrating fixed tissues involves passing them through
Multiphoton Lightsheet Fluorescence Microscopy for Disease Detection in Tissue
25
==PAGE:37==
a graded series of ethanol concentrations, ranging from 50% to 100%, for 30-60 minutes
each. This step ensures the removal of all water from the sample.
3.2 Clearing: The colon sample is then cleared in a substance such as xylene or
toluene for approximately 15 minutes. This step makes it possible to infiltrate the tissue
with paraffin, ensuring complete saturation.
4. Tissue embedding is the procedure for placing colon samples embedded in paraffin
to create a paraffin block. This step involves placing the tissue in a mould and filling it
with liquid paraffin, which is then allowed to solidify. The embedded paraffin is used in
structural support for cutting thin sections, making it easy to handle a very thin section and
more precise to cut. It also allows long-term preservation storage at room temperature
[59].
5. Paraffin block of tissue is a solid paraffin block containing the tissue. This block
provides support for the tissue, making it easier to cut thin sections ready for sectioning.
6. Tissue sectioning is the step to place paraffin-embedded colon tissue in a microtome,
a machine that slices the block into very thin sections, typically only a few micrometres
thick. These thin sections are necessary for microscopic examination.
7. Stretching of sections is the process of stretching thin tissue sections in a water bath
to remove wrinkles and ensure they are flat.
8. Tissue section capturing is a step that involves capturing the stretched tissue sections
from the water bath onto microscope slides. After the tissue sections are captured onto
microscope slides, they undergo hematoxylin and eosin staining. This involves:
8.1 Hematoxylin staining: Hematoxylin is applied to sample sections. It stains cell nu-
clei, giving them the colour blue or dark purple [55]. Hematoxylin is a basic dye that binds
to acidic components within the cell, particularly the DNA and RNA, which are abundant in
the nucleus [55] [57]. It can be used to observe differences in the number and appearance
of nuclei between cancerous and normal cells.
8.2 Rinsing and bluing: The slides are rinsed in water to remove excess stain, and
sometimes, a bluing agent is used to sharpen the blue colour of the nuclei [55].
8.3 Eosin staining: Applied dye eosin to sample sections. Eosin is a common dye in
stain cytoplasm, extracellular matrix, red blood cells, collagen, etc. It is giving pink or red
colour. This enables the observation of alterations in morphology, cell distribution, and
tissue sample structure [60].
8.4 Dehydration: After staining, the slides are dehydrated again through ethanol to
prepare for clearing. This step usually starts directly with 95% ethanol followed by 100%
ethanol. Each step might last only a few minutes to remove all water from the tissue.
8.5 Clearing: After dehydration, the ethanol is replaced with a clearer, such as xylene
or toluene, for approximately 10 minutes, which is miscible with both ethanol and the em-

bedding medium used in the next step. Clearing makes the tissue transparent, facilitating
the transmission of light through the sample during microscopic examination and making
the sample sections clear and ready for mounting.
8.6 Mounting: The tissue sections are then covered with a mounting medium and a
coverslip to preserve the stained slide for microscopy.
9. Microscopic analysis is the final step to prepare slides that are examined under a
microscope, which is critical for diagnosing diseases. It aims to study the detailed archi-
tecture of tissues and conduct research on cellular mechanisms. This is important for
accurate diagnosis, research, tissue morphology, and pathology understanding. There-
fore, this step will be compared to the results from MP-LSFM.

Colon imaging results
The MP-LSFM images were acquired without additional labels or stains. The fluores-
cence was naturally emitted from native fluorophores present on the mucosal surface
of the colon. As explained in Chapter 2, the colon’s mucosal layer is altered due to an
infection. This layer is in direct contact with the external environment and is particularly
susceptible to changes during infection. Therefore, the results in this chapter will describe
the structure of the mucosal layer in the colon.
The term ”fresh sample” in this study refers to colon samples stored in a cell viability
solution for no longer than 24 hours from dissection. If samples are stored for longer than
24 hours, this is specified in the text. The freshness of samples is crucial, as the structure
of the colon may degrade over time.
The term ”fixed sample” refers to colon samples that have undergone the fixation method
detailed in Section 5.2.2. These samples were fixed with formaldehyde and stored in
PBS. Formaldehyde fixation preserves the natural fluorescence of the samples, allowing
them to be used at any time.
All groups of the MP-LSFM images of fresh samples in the Section 6.1 (healthy and in-
fected groups) and fixed samples in Section 6.2 (healthy and infected groups) used the
short pass filter at 700 nm, which allows fluorescence emission from 400 - 690 nm to pass
through as illustrated in the Appendix A.10.
The Section 6.3 (fixed healthy and infected) uses various filters to examine the fluores-
cence emitted from the colon structure. Therefore, the filters are applied differently.
The colon image results from MP-LSFM are shown in Section 6.1, 6.2 and 6.3 were
zoomed in to provide the best structure imaging by MP-LSFM. The magenta-coloured
(purple) in the figures is not intended to indicate any specific reasons related to fluores-
cence spectra; more importantly, it is intentionally selected to provide a better visualisation
of the cellular structure of the colon mucosa layer.

Comparison of fresh and fixed preservation techniques
Comparison of healthy colon samples imaged of MP-LSFM in Figures 6.1 and 6.2 display
streaky fluorescent feature lines, likely due to the shadowing effect. This phenomenon
occurs because certain structure parts obstruct the lightsheet as it passes through the
sample, preventing it from illuminating deeper regions of the sample. Consequently, these
blocked areas and some cellular structures remain unlabeled, while the surrounding struc-
ture is made to fluoresce, providing a negative contrast shadow image [66], leading to the
appearance of dark lines in the images. These dark lines run alongside the magenta-
coloured fluorescence, highlighting the areas where the lightsheet could penetrate and
excite the fluorescent molecules. To address this issue, dual illumination LSFM involves
introducing two light sheets from opposite sides of the sample. By illuminating the sample
from two directions, the likelihood of any one region being completely shadowed is signif-
icantly reduced. Each light sheet can illuminate regions that the other might miss due to
shadowing [67].

7.1.4.1
Potential fluorophores that emit fluorescence in the MP-LSFM system.
This section discusses the potential fluorophores responsible for emitting fluorescence
during experiments in the MP-LSFM system. The short pass filter at 700 nm was em-
ployed by both the fresh and fixed groups of healthy samples, which enables fluorescence
below 700 nm to pass through (see Appendix A.10).
It is important to understand the origin of the fluorescence signal. Therefore, identifying
the specific substrates or cellular structures responsible for the emitted fluorescence is
crucial for accurately interpreting the imaging results. This can help distinguish between
different types of cells and substances within the tissue, such as autofluorescent cellular
components. Understanding the fluorescence source can enable more precise assess-
ments of the tissue’s condition and the presence of specific cell types, such as goblet
cells, epithelial cells and others.
Due to the excitation wavelengths in two-photon excitation being almost twice as long as
those in single-photon excitation, the process of exciting the fluorophore is different, but
the emission properties are similar. Therefore, if a fluorophore is normally excited by 400
nm light in single-photon mode, it can also be excited by two 800 nm photons in two-
photon mode. The fluorescence emission wavelength of a fluorophore does not change
between single-photon and two-photon excitation. A fluorophore excited by 400 nm light
in single-photon mode and by 800 nm light in two-photon mode will emit fluorescence at
the same wavelength in both cases. The excitation mechanism differs, but the emission
properties remain the same [32] [70].
The fluorescence intensity depends on several factors, including the efficiency of two-
photon absorption, the intensity of the excitation light, and the fluorophore’s properties.
Generally, the fluorescence intensity for two-photon excitation is proportional to the square
of the excitation light intensity due to the need for simultaneous absorption of two pho-
tons [32]. For example, a fluorophore excited by 400 nm light might emit fluorescence at
500 nm. In two-photon excitation of the same fluorophore, when excited by two 800 nm
photons, it will still emit fluorescence at 500 nm [70]. Thus, the fluorescence intensity ob-
served in two-photon excitation does not simply translate directly from the single-photon
excitation intensity [71].
The fluorescence intensity observed in two-photon excitation is not directly proportional to
the intensity observed in single-photon excitation. Current understanding suggests that
the fluorescence intensity in two-photon excitation is approximately proportional to the
square of the excitation intensity [32] [70]. However, further investigation into the mecha-
nisms of two-photon excitation is needed. When determining the fluorescence emission
from tissue, the best approach to identify which fluorophores appear in the colon tissue
during two-photon excitation is to compare with single-photon excitation data, as there
are no existing studies providing fluorescence emission spectra of tissue fluorophores in
two-photon excitation.

7.4
Limitation of study
There are several limitations in this study that should be addressed to improve the ro-
bustness and accuracy of future research. The main limitations relate to the laser for
Multiphoton Lightsheet Fluorescence Microscopy for Disease Detection in Tissue
61
==PAGE:73==
MP-LSFM and the samples.
The laser was under maintenance several times throughout the study, affecting the ex-
periments’ consistency. The laser had issues tuning to the desired wavelength specified
by the authors and had a lower average power output than required. These factors may
have influenced the experimental results and their reproducibility. Additionally, the avail-
able laser at 1030 nm is not the ideal wavelength for exciting the fluorophores effectively.
Most common fluorophores in biological imaging require excitation in the 700-1000 nm
range for efficient two-photon excitation. Longer wavelengths (beyond 1030 nm) often
reduce fluorescence yield because many common fluorophores are not efficiently excited
by these wavelengths [74].
There were limitations concerning the availability and variety of colon samples. Specifi-
cally, there was a lack of colon cancer and colitis samples. As a result, the closest model
to human disease that could be used was a piglet colon sample infected with S. epider-
midis. While this model is useful, it may not fully represent the complexities of human
colon diseases. Therefore, this limitation should be considered when interpreting the
study’s findings. Future research should aim to incorporate a broader range of disease-
specific colon samples to enhance the validity and applicability of the results.

Conclusion
This research has established Multiphoton Lightsheet Fluorescence Microscopy (MP-
LSFM) as a highly effective tool for colon disease detection in tissue, particularly within
the colon. The technique’s ability to provide high-resolution, three-dimensional images
has proven advantageous for differentiating between healthy and diseased tissues based
on distinct morphological characteristics. Additionally, MP-LSFM’s capability to identify
endogenous biomarkers such as FAD offers a non-invasive method for detecting disease-
related metabolic changes. The results of this study clearly demonstrate the capabilities of
MP-LSFM in effectively distinguishing between healthy and diseased colon tissues. The
results highlight the potential advantages of MP-LSFM compared to conventional imaging
methods such as optical coherence tomography (OCT) and traditional histology.
Nevertheless, the study acknowledges certain limitations that require further investiga-
tion. These include the need for more extensive validation across diverse tissue types
and disease models and improvements in sample preparation protocols and parameters.
Additionally, integrating MP-LSFM with advanced image analysis techniques, such as
machine learning, could significantly enhance diagnostic accuracy and efficiency.
Future research should address these limitations by refining imaging protocols, expand-
ing the range of pathological samples studied, and exploring the integration of MP-LSFM
with other diagnostic methods. This would not only improve the robustness of disease de-
tection but also facilitate its application in clinical settings, ultimately contributing to earlier
diagnosis and better patient outcomes in diseases like colorectal cancer and chronic in-
flammatory conditions.

A.4
Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)
Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT) is a non-invasive imaging technique that uses light
waves to take cross-section pictures of biological tissues. It works similarly to ultrasound
imaging, except it uses light instead of sound. It has a broad-spectrum, low-coherence
light source, typically near-infrared, is used to capture detailed cross-sectional images of
biological tissues. The light is split into two paths: one directed towards the tissue, where
it reflects and scatters from various structures, and the other towards a reference mirror.
When the light reflects and scatters back from the tissue and the reference mirror, the
recombined light creates an interference pattern due to the different phase shifts caused
by varying travel distances.
This interference pattern is detected by a photodetector or spectrometer and contains
information about the depth and intensity of reflections within the tissue. Signals are
processed using Fourier transformation to convert the time-domain signals into depth-
resolved images. By scanning the light beam laterally across the tissue, OCT constructs
high-resolution two-dimensional cross-sectional images, which can be combined to form
three-dimensional representations.
OCT offers several significant advantages that make it a valuable imaging modality. Firstly,
it allows for rapid acquisition, as the technique is straightforward and can capture images
quickly. Secondly, OCT enables real-time imaging due to its fast image acquisition, facil-
itating real-time visualization of tissues. Additionally, OCT provides a large field of view,
enhancing its utility by allowing the capture of wide areas, which can reveal peripheral
lesions or conditions. This capability improves diagnostic accuracy, increases efficiency,
and simplifies clinical workflows, making OCT an essential tool in both research and clin-
ical settings.
OCT, despite its benefits, has several disadvantages. Firstly, when configured for a large
field of view (FOV), OCT offers lower resolution, which may not capture the fine details
of small structures as clearly as higher-resolution imaging methods such as multipho-
ton light sheet fluorescence microscopy (MP-LSFM) and multiphoton microscopy (MPM).
This can be a limitation when detailed tissue architecture is necessary for precise diag-
nostics. Secondly, OCT has limited depth penetration, typically effective up to a few mil-
limetres, making it less suitable for imaging deeper tissues. Additionally, OCT has limited
contrast, which is less effective in differentiating between tissue types than fluorescence
techniques.

A.6
Imaging acquisition techniques of MP-LSFM and MPM
This section provides a detailed description of sample imaging acquisition techniques of
MP-LSFM and MPM.
Before using the laser beam of the MP-LSFM, visible light is used to visualize the structure
of the samples and propose adjusting the focus. This step reduces the time needed to
find the focus of the samples, minimizes the risk of photodamage and photobleaching, and
decreases the time spent using lasers. Using visible light is also easier because there is
no need to wear protective goggles, which are required when using the laser. This makes
it easier to adjust the focus without the additional complication of eye protection. Adding
this step to the initial focus on the sample makes the process more straightforward and
less challenging, ensuring a more efficient and safer workflow. Figure A.8 illustrates the
sample that captions while using the visible light to adjust the system’s focus.

A.7
Shortpass filter
A shortpass filter is an optical filter that allows wavelengths shorter than a certain cut-
off wavelength to pass through while blocking longer wavelengths. In fluorescence mi-
croscopy, a short pass filter might be placed in the optical path to ensure that only the
excitation light (which is of a shorter wavelength than the emitted fluorescence) reaches
the sample [80][81].
Figure A.10 represents a transmission graph for a filter identified as FESH0700. The
graph shows the percentage of light transmission through the filter across different wave-
lengths. Blue Shaded Region (400 - 690 nm) is the shaded region that indicates the
wavelength range where the filter allows light to pass through. According to the graph,
the filter transmits light almost fully (close to 100%) between 400 nm and 690 nm. The
transmission sharply increases around 400 nm and sharply decreases around 690 nm,
indicating that the filter effectively blocks light outside this range.
Figure A.10: ”The blue shaded region in the graph represents the transmission region of
the filter, 400 - 690 nm.” [80]

A.8
Light intensity of fixed healthy and infected colon
samples using various filters
This section presents the fluorescence intensity in fixed healthy and infected colon sam-
ples. Various optical filters were employed to assess the differential fluorescence signals,
providing insights into the structural and biochemical differences between healthy and
infected tissues.
Intensity offers critical insights into the presence, quantity, and spatial distribution of spe-
cific molecules or structures within a sample. Whether high or low, variations in fluo-
rescence intensity can reveal significant information about the biological and chemical
properties of the sample, including the concentration of fluorophores. High fluorescence
intensity indicates regions of elevated metabolic or biological activity where particular pro-
teins or compounds are abundant. Additionally, increased fluorescence intensity can in-
dicate certain biomarkers or pathological changes, such as the overexpression of specific
proteins in cancerous tissues.
Gray Value (or grayscale intensity value) in figure A.11 and A.12 in the y-axis represent
the brightness of a pixel in a grayscale image. The gray value is a numerical represen-
tation of the intensity of light that a pixel represents, with lower values corresponding to
darker pixels and higher values to brighter pixels. The gray value in ImageJ is crucial for
analyzing the brightness and intensity distribution in an image, which can be used to infer
various properties and concentrations within the sample being studied.
The x-axis in figure A.11 and A.12 present the distance (µm) used in measuring the gray
value in figure 6.6 and 6.7.
