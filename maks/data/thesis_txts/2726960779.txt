Abstract 
 
This thesis presents an investigation into the reusability of fired clay bricks, focusing particularly on 
their durability under salt attack, a common degradation mechanism. With the increasing 
importance of sustainable building practices, the reuse of construction materials has become a 
pivotal topic in the field. However, the lack of standard, in-situ methods for determining brick 
reusability necessitates further research. To address this gap, this study employs a number of tests, 
including ionic measurements, visual inspection after accelerated salt tests, and ultrasonic 
measurements. 
Visual results from the accelerated salt tests revealed potential damage types that could impact the 
bricks' integrity, such as efflorescence, granular disintegration, and blistering. Ionic measurements 
provided insights into changes in brick composition before and after weathering, while ultrasonic 
measurements indicated internal changes of the material. Despite some variability, noteworthy 
relative changes were observed across different types of bricks. 
The findings underline the potential of these testing methods in determining the reusability of 
bricks, with the possibility for further refinement. This work contributes to the promotion of 
sustainable building practices, advocating for the reuse of fired clay bricks. Moreover, it emphasizes 
the necessity for standard, in-situ criteria for brick reusability. It is recommended that future 
research continues to refine these testing techniques and further explore the thresholds observed to 
enhance understanding of brick durability under salt attack.

1. Introduction 
 
1.1 Background  
 
Fired clay bricks are a popular choice in the global construction industry due to their durability, 
versatility, and affordability. As the building industry is one of the largest consumers of raw materials 
and energy, the reuse of fired clay bricks in new constructions offers significant potential for waste 
reduction and resource conservation. However, assessing the reusability of these bricks is complex, 
necessitating evaluations of durability and structural integrity while preserving bricks for potential 
reuse. Their exposure to environmental factors, such as salt, can cause significant deterioration, 
impacting their strength and lifespan. 
The purpose of this study is to assess the durability of fired clay bricks in the presence of salt. To 
achieve this goal, three test methods will be used to evaluate the deterioration process and the 
extent of damage caused by salt exposure. Before the tests, samples of different categories will 
undergo the RILEM Accelerated salt test (Lubelli et al., 2023) followed by comparisons with 
reference set of bricks. More specifically, we aim to explore the relationship between the visual 
inspection, which appears to be the dominant method for assessing fired clay bricks for reuse in the 
industry, and objective, qualitative methods. 
The results of this study aspire to inform the construction sector about brick durability, assisting in the 
selection of construction materials for reuse in environments prone to high salt exposure. 
Investigating the durability of fired clay bricks in the presence of salt is important for multiple reasons: 
1. Material Selection: The choice of building materials is a crucial decision for any construction 
project. By understanding the durability of fired clay bricks in the presence of salt, builders 
and architects can make informed decisions about the suitability of this material for their 
projects, helping to ensure that the building will last over the years. 
2. Environmental Considerations: Exposure to salt can cause significant damage to many types 
of building materials, including fired clay bricks. Understanding the impact of salt exposure 
can help to minimize the environmental impact of construction and reduce the need for 
repairs and renovations over time. 
3. Cost-effectiveness: Repairing or replacing damaged building materials is expensive. By 
understanding the durability of fired clay bricks, builders and owners can make informed 
decisions about material selection, helping to reduce the long-term costs of maintenance and 
repair. 
4. Reuse of material: Reusing building materials is an essential sustainable and cost-effective 
practice. However, a structured and informed approach is necessary to maximize the benefit 
of reuse.

1.2 Fired clay bricks and their properties 
 
Fired clay bricks have been widely used in construction for centuries due to their durability, strength, 
and thermal properties. They are typically made from a mixture of clay, water, and other additives, 
which are shaped and then fired at high temperatures to achieve the desired physical and 
mechanical (Cultrone et al., 2000). 
 
 
1.2.1 Porosity and its influence of fired-clay bricks properties 
 
Porosity is a fundamental property of fired clay bricks that affects their mechanical strength, thermal 
properties, and durability (Larbi, 2004). The porosity of fired clay bricks is determined by the clay 
composition, firing temperature, and firing time (Stefanidou et al., 2015). Higher porosity generally 
leads to lower mechanical strength and increased susceptibility to moisture and salt damage 
(Delgado et al., 2016). However, it can also contribute to better thermal insulation properties 
(Anjum et al., 2022). Different types of porosity, such as open and closed porosity, can have varying 
effects on the performance of the bricks. Open porosity refers to the pores that are connected and 
allow fluid flow, whereas closed porosity consists of isolated pores that do not contribute to fluid 
transport.  
 
1.2.2 Water absorption 
 
Water absorption, a property directly related to porosity, plays a critical role in the durability and 
resistance of bricks to moisture-related damage(Yue et al., 2022). High porosity generally leads to 
increased water absorption, making the bricks more susceptible to frost damage, salt crystallization, 
and other forms of weathering  (Delgado et al., 2016). Conversely, bricks with low water absorption 
and lower porosity are generally more resistant to these issues. The pore size distribution and 
connectivity also influence water absorption, with smaller, well-distributed pores typically resulting 
in lower water absorption rates. In some cases, the use of additives, surface treatments, or specific 
firing techniques can help reduce water absorption and improve the performance of bricks with high 
porosity. 
It is worth noting that fired-clay bricks are mostly used for its visual and aesthetic properties, and 
thermal and physical properties are not a significant consideration in Scandinavia. Hence, 
mechanical strength and thermal conductivity are not in focus in this study.

1.2 Factors influencing fired clay brick properties 
 
Factors such as clay composition, firing temperature, firing time, and additives significantly impact 
the above properties, and understanding those would help better characterise the bricks and 
generate further understanding on assessing their reusability.  
 
1.2.1 Clay composition 
 
The composition of the clay used for making fired clay bricks significantly impacts their properties. 
The type and proportion of clay minerals, as well as the presence of non-clay components such as 
quartz, feldspar, and carbonates, can affect the brick's porosity, strength, and thermal properties 
(Stefanidou et al., 2015). Furthermore, the presence of impurities, such as organic matter and 
soluble salts, can also influence the brick's behaviour during firing and its final properties. 
 
1.2.2 Firing temperature and time 
 
The firing temperature and time play crucial roles in determining the properties of fired clay bricks 
(Cultrone et al., 2000). Higher firing temperatures generally lead to increased vitrification and 
densification of the clay particles, resulting in higher strength and lower porosity. However, 
excessive firing temperatures can cause deformation or bloating of the bricks due to the release of 
gases from the clay.

1.3 Industry practice in re-using fired clay bricks 
 
In the construction industry, it's common practice to reuse bricks during renovations without 
formally assessing their condition. However, this could potentially lead to compromised structural 
integrity (e.g., through water intrusion from the compromised façade), aesthetic degradation (e.g. 
efflorescence), and thus, increased maintenance costs. Therefore, conducting rigorous evaluations, 
including physical testing, visual inspections, and chemical analyses, becomes essential to ensure the 
safety and longevity of structures.

In some cases, the use of these bricks may also have a negative impact on the overall aesthetic 
appeal of the building. To ensure that the building is safe and secure, it is important to assess the 
condition of all building materials, including bricks, and make informed decisions about their reuse. 
This may involve carrying out tests to determine the strength and durability of the bricks, as well as 
visual inspections to identify any signs of damage or deterioration. 
There are, however, several methods that can be used to determine the durability of fired clay bricks 
with implications to reusability, including: 
Physical testing: Physical tests, such as compressive strength tests, can be performed on fired clay 
bricks to determine their resistance to wear and tear over time. These tests can also be performed 
on samples that have been subjected to salt exposure, providing valuable insights into the impact of 
this environmental factor on the bricks' durability. 
Visual inspection: A visual inspection of fired clay bricks can provide valuable information about their 
condition and the extent of any damage caused by salt exposure. This can include observations of 
cracks, chipping, and discoloration, as well as an assessment of the overall appearance of the bricks. 
Chemical analysis: Chemical analysis can be performed on fired clay bricks to determine the 
presence of salts and other contaminants. This can provide valuable information about the extent of 
salt exposure and its impact on the bricks' durability. 
A combination of these methods can provide a comprehensive understanding of the durability of 
fired clay bricks and their resistance to salt exposure over time. This information can be used to 
make decisions about the selection and use of fired clay bricks in construction projects, helping to 
ensure their long-term performance.

2.1.1 Raw Materials and Manufacturing Process 
 
The durability and reusability of bricks are strongly influenced by the raw materials and 
manufacturing process. The quality and properties of the clay, such as its mineralogical composition 
and plasticity, play a significant role in determining the final properties of the fired bricks (Cultrone 
et al., 2000). Firing temperature and duration also affect the brick's microstructure, porosity, and 
mechanical properties, which in turn influence its durability. 
 
2.1.2 Environmental Factors 
 
Environmental factors such as exposure to moisture, freeze-thaw cycles, and salt crystallization 
significantly impact brick durability (Balksten & Strandberg-de Bruijn, 2021). Moisture ingress can 
lead to the deterioration of bricks through physical, chemical, and biological processes (Delgado et 
al., 2016). Freeze-thaw cycles can cause cracking and disintegration of bricks due to the expansion of 
water upon freezing. Salt crystallization, a common issue in coastal environments and historic 
buildings, results from the migration of soluble salts through porous bricks, causing efflorescence 
and subflorescence, which can lead to the disruption of the brick matrix and reduced durability ((Pel 
et al., 2004); (Yue et al., 2022)). In Scandinavia – a primarily coastal environment, salt crystallization 
is a major environmental factor affecting brick durability. 
 
 
2.1.3 Surface Treatments and Consolidants 
 
Surface treatments and consolidants can improve brick durability by reducing water ingress, 
protecting against salt crystallization, and enhancing mechanical properties. Hydrophobic 
treatments, such as silanes and siloxanes, can reduce water absorption and mitigate damage caused 
by moisture and freeze-thaw cycles (Graziani et al., 2016).

2.1.4 Impact of Additives and Impurities in Clay 
 
Additives and impurities in clay can affect the durability and reusability of bricks. The presence of 
organic matter, carbonates, and sulphates in the raw clay can lead to undesirable reactions during 
the firing process, affecting the brick's microstructure, mechanical properties, and durability 
(Cultrone et al., 2000). The choice of additives and the control of impurities in clay are critical to 
achieving the desired properties and performance of bricks in various applications. 
 
2.2 Test methods relevant to reusability 
 
The evaluation of fired clay bricks' properties for reusability should ideally involve the use of Non-
Destructive Test (NDT) methods to avoid damaging the bricks during the assessment.  
In the field of materials testing and evaluation, NDT methods play a crucial role in assessing the 
condition, integrity, and durability of materials without causing any damage or alteration to their 
original state. NDT techniques are particularly valuable for the inspection and preservation of 
cultural heritage sites, historic buildings, and other delicate structures, as they allow for in-depth 
analysis without compromising the integrity of the materials.

a brief description of each method's purpose and application. 
 By understanding the strengths and limitations of these methods, professionals can select the most 
appropriate technique for their specific needs and ensure the preservation of invaluable cultural and 
historical assets.

Visual Inspection and Ultrasonic measurements are selected from the list of Non-destructive test 
(NDT) methods, as they present unique advantages when assessing brick reusability. 
Visual inspection can provide insight into the external condition of bricks. This method is non-
destructive and relatively straightforward, making it a practical initial step in the assessment 
process. Visual inspection can identify significant issues, such as cracking, which could impact the 
reusability of bricks in construction or restoration projects. However, one big limitation is its lagging 
nature as a method; as reviewed earlier, damage can occur internally first. No visible damage does 
not exclude any invisible damage. 
On the other hand, ultrasonic methods offer the ability to examine bricks' internal structure without 
causing damage. These methods can identify subsurface flaws, such as internal cracks or 
detachments, which may not be evident through visual inspection. Ultrasonic techniques can also 
measure the density and elastic properties of the bricks, providing further insight into their 
structural integrity and durability. This data can be particularly useful in assessing whether bricks are 
structurally sound enough for reuse. 
Combining both visual inspection and ultrasonic methods allows for a comprehensive examination of 
bricks. While visual inspection provides valuable information on the bricks' surface condition, 
ultrasonic methods examine the material deeper, revealing any hidden issues within the brick 
structure. Together, these techniques provide a robust toolset for assessing brick reusability in a way 
that preserves the integrity of the material. Additionally, it is important to mention that both 
methods are inexpensive and can both be applied on site, while none of them requires technical 
skills or expertise.  
To better assess their efficacy and interpret the results from their measurements, Ion 
Chromatography (IC) Measurements (destructive) are used in combination. Salt attack will be 
simulated, with measurements taken before and after.

3.1.1 Open Porosity 
 
Open porosity is the proportion of void space in a material that can be accessed from the surface. It 
directly influences the permeability of the material and its susceptibility to weathering processes. 
Open porosity greatly influences the material's susceptibility to damage from weathering processes, 
including salt crystallization. 
In the context of fired-clay bricks, open porosity determines how easily water and other fluids, along 
with the dissolved salts, can penetrate the material. During the salt weathering process, salts 
dissolved in water infiltrate the bricks through the network of interconnected pores. When the 
water evaporates, the salts crystallize within these pores, causing pressure build-up and potential 
damage to the material. 
 
3.1.2 Closed Porosity 
 
Closed porosity is defined as the isolated or non-interconnected void spaces within a material. These 
pores do not contribute to the material's permeability but can affect its other physical properties 
such as density and specific heat capacity. 
In terms of weathering processes, closed porosity plays a less direct role compared to open porosity. 
The isolated pores can host crystallizing salts, but as they are not connected to the overall pore 
network, the pressure build-up is confined to the local environment of the pore. Nonetheless, if the 
closed porosity is significant, the accumulation of these local stress points could contribute to overall 
damage to the material.

To estimate the total porosity more accurately (Φ_total), methods such as mercury intrusion

• 
Mercury intrusion porosimetry works by forcing mercury, a non-wetting liquid, into a porous 
structure under controlled pressures. Since mercury doesn't wet most materials, it will only 
enter the pores when enough pressure is applied to overcome the capillary resistance. By 
progressively increasing the applied pressure and measuring the volume of mercury

again, at the same pressure. The difference in the volumes gives us the true volume of the 
sample, which can be used to calculate the total porosity (Semel & Lados, 2006).

3.2 Salt Attack 
 
Salt attack is a multifaceted degradation mechanism significantly affecting the durability of various 
building structures, particularly those employing fired clay brick masonry (Abu Bakar et al., n.d.).; 
Balksten & Strandberg-de Bruijn, 2021). Various salts introduced into the structure can contribute to 
material deterioration through processes such as efflorescence, subflorescence, and crystallization 
(Delgado et al., 2016). 
Salt crystallization pressure is the force exerted by salt crystals as they form and grow within the 
pore structure of a porous material, such as fired-clay bricks. This pressure can cause stress on the 
material's structure, leading to mechanical damage, such as cracking, spalling, and disintegration. 
Salt crystallization pressure is influenced by factors such as the type of salt, the degree of saturation, 
the pore size distribution, and the environmental conditions, including temperature and relative 
humidity. 
==PAGE:24==
19 
 
3.2.1 Salt Types 
 
Salt weathering, also known as salt attack, can manifest in two primary ways (Balksten & Strandberg-
de Bruijn, 2023). Firstly, chlorides, from sources such as sea salts or food storage, can lead to the 
deposition of a white layer of salts that expand outwards from the surface, a process referred to as 
efflorescence. Secondly, salts inherent to the masonry can proliferate within the porous media, 
instigating material loss from within, a process termed subflorescence. The particular salts present, 
and their ensuing interactions can have a significant impact on the extent of masonry damage. 
 
Different salts have different properties that can affect their crystallization pressure and impact on 
porous materials. Common salts involved in salt weathering include sodium chloride (NaCl), sodium 
sulfate (Na2SO4), magnesium sulfate (MgSO4), and calcium sulfate (CaSO4) (Delgado et al., 2016). The 
specific characteristics of each salt, such as solubility, hydration state, and crystal morphology, can 
influence the crystallization pressure and the resulting damage to the material. 
 
3.2.2 Efflorescence and Subflorescence 
 
Efflorescence involves chlorides, commonly from sea salts or food storage, depositing a white layer 
of salts that grows outwards from the surface. Conversely, subflorescence arises when salts inherent 
to the masonry proliferate within the porous media, leading to material loss from within. The 
particular salts present, and their ensuing interactions can significantly impact the degree and cause 
distinct types of damage. As summarized by (Balksten & Strandberg-de Bruijn, 2021): 
 
• 
Sodium Sulphate Attack: Sodium sulphate can occur in bricks that are slightly under-burnt, 
stemming from sulphate reactions with combustion gases to produce sulphuric acid. Sodium 
sulphate can crystallize within the masonry surface and switch between anhydrous sodium 
sulphate (Na2SO4) and decahydrate (Na2SO4·10H2O), resulting in notable damage due to 
hydration pressure and crystallization pressure. 
 
• 
Gypsum (Calcium Sulphate) Attack: Calcium sulphate (gypsum) can form on lime-based 
surfaces in the presence of airborne sulphur. It often crystallizes inside lime mortar through 
the sulphation of calcium carbonate, leading to significant material loss, especially on 
surfaces not exposed to rainfall. 
 
• 
Chloride Attack: Sodium chloride, or halite, often deposits on the masonry surface, 
generating salt crystals and long strands. Although not typically causing severe damage, 
sodium chloride can generate surface dissolution during hydration or dehydration processes.

• 
Temperature: This includes factors such as the range of temperature fluctuations and the

• 
Chemical exposure: This includes exposure to chemicals in the air or in the ground, such as

==PAGE:34==

Accumulation stage process 
In total, 18 specimens (see figure 10) undergo exposure to salt solutions, prepared from Na2SO4 salts 
and demineralized water, with concentrations of 5% and 10% by weight. This exposure occurs under 
room conditions (22±2°C, 45±15% RH) with the weight of the salt solution being equivalent to the 
capillary moisture content of the material. To reduce evaporation during the absorption process, the 
solution reservoir can be shielded. When the salt solution has been fully absorbed, the bottom 
surface of each specimen is encased with a paraffin film and a layer of tape, and their mass is 
subsequently recorded. 
The specimens are then transitioned into a drying process at 40±2°C / 15±5% RH in a box with 
restricted ventilation. To maintain the optimal conditions, the box is covered with a layer of 
Japanese paper which enables air exchange but limits air flow. The specimens are placed in a way 
that maintains a 2 cm distance from the box walls. A sensor is used to monitor the temperature and 
humidity levels inside the box, and the weight of each specimen is regularly recorded. This stage, 
referred to as the accumulation stage, is finished when 80% or more of the introduced water has 
evaporated.  
At the end, efflorescence and damage is photographically documented and reported according to 
the damage atlas (MDCS, n.d.) 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
    
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Propagation stage process - sodium sulfate 
The propagation stage typically includes four 3-week cycles. In the current study, due to time 
limitations only three 3-week cycles were performed, with each cycle involving specimen cooling, 
rewetting, and drying. In each cycle, specimens are cooled at room conditions, and their mass is 
recorded before and after rewetting with 80% of the initial water amount. The specimens are then 
dried at room conditions for up to 24 hours, followed by 476 hours of drying at 40±2°C/15±5% RH. 
After each cycle, the specimens' condition is documented and assessed. 
After completing the three cycles, the specimens' state is documented, their mass is recorded, and 
the sealing is removed. The paraffin film is also removed, the specimen's final mass is recorded, and 
the surface is brushed to visually assess material loss. 
After the end of the last cycle photographically collected data is gathered and presented in the 
relevant document (Appendix B).

4.2.4 IC measurements on bricks 
 
Ionic concentration (IC) measurements offer valuable insights into the chemical composition of 
bricks, revealing the presence of anions and cations that may originate from the manufacturing 
process, raw material impurities, or environmental exposure.  
IC measurements also shed light on the presence of soluble salts on bricks, which can cause brick 
deterioration, particularly in environments with fluctuating moisture levels (Espinosa-Marzal & 
Scherer, 2010) . By dissolving samples in a testing solution, their concentrations can be measured. 
Information about the types and concentrations of salts can be used to assess the environmental 
conditions bricks have been exposed to and their potential for further deterioration (Steiger & 
Asmussen, 2008). 
IC measurements revealed the concentration and type of salts present on the brick surface, allowing 
an estimation of potential salt-induced deterioration (Steiger & Asmussen, 2008). High 
concentrations of salts indicate higher risk of deterioration.

5.1 Visual Inspection after Accelerated salt weathering 
 
After 3 cycles, with a duration of 3 weeks each, a detailed table was compiled including pictures of 
all specimens after each stage or cycle, as well as description of damage at the end of the 3 cycles 
according to the damage atlas (MDCS, n.d.), followed by damage extend degree (Appendix B). 
Table 4 provides an overview of the common damages that have been identified at the total of brick 
specimens that participated in the test. 
 
 
Table 4 Summary of Visual Inspection results related to Damage Atlas 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:43==

The testing results for the brick samples show a range of effects. All 18 bricks tested have shown 
signs of efflorescence, indicating that water-soluble salts have migrated to the surface of the bricks. 
Granular disintegration, a process where the surface of the bricks breaks down into small, granular 
particles, was observed in 11 of the samples. Subflorescence, the formation of salt deposits below 
the surface of the bricks, was noted in 8 of the bricks. Furthermore, 7 of the bricks displayed signs of 
blistering, where the surface of the bricks bulges outwards due to underlying pressure or decay. 
Chromatic alteration, a change in the color or visual appearance of the bricks, was identified in 10 of 
the brick samples. Lastly, 7 of the bricks showed signs of layering, a process where the bricks start to 
separate into distinct layers. These results indicate a range of potential issues related to salt attack 
and weathering effects on the tested brick samples. 
 
Upon the conclusion of the visual inspection, it has been noted that the bricks under the MX1 
category demonstrated the highest extent of damage, with a simultaneous occurrence of several 
types of damage. The MX1 category represents bricks that are ideally suited for a passive 
environment, i.e., environments with minimal exposure to aggressive substances or extreme 
weather conditions, therefore the results were actually expected.  
 
Finally, the red bricks that belong in the category MX3.2 and were used in our tests, had pre-existing 
damage to different degrees. As anticipated, the weathering tests not only increased these initial 
damages but also revealed new types of degradation. These results were expected considering the 
bricks had already undergone natural weathering while they were part of masonry structures. The 
tests have highlighted the tendency of these previously used bricks to sustain further damage or 
develop new forms of deterioration when exposed to additional weathering conditions. 
 
Overall, it would be expected that further damage would have been revealed if more cycles of salt 
weathering would have been performed to the specimens. This was not possible though due to time 
limitations.

Measuring the particles (cations) in the samples pre- and post-salt-weathering can provide a strong 
insight on the changes in composition of the bricks. 
As discussed previously, Ca (Calcium), Mg (Magnesium), K (Potassium), and Na (Sodium), in the form 
of various salts, can potentially cause damage to brickwork through salt crystallization, especially 
under fluctuating moisture conditions, while S (Sulphur) can be related to a sulfate attack. 
 
 
Table 7 IC analysis of results 
 
 
 
Reviewing the results of the measurements, an immediate observation is that there is variability on 
the measurements between the three samples used on each phase of the experiment. The Relative 
Standard Deviation (RSD) is a statistical measure of the dispersion in a data set and is used here to 
help interpret the results more accurately. 
RSD is lower on the samples taken before the accelerated salt test, but higher afterwards, which is in 
line with the challenging nature of precisely modelling salt weathering. Similarly, there is high 
variance in the pre-weathering samples for MX3.2 bricks, as they have probably been exposed to salt 
attack to some extend already.  
Despite the high RSD on the weathered samples and with that understanding in perspective, the 
relative change on average is also a valuable observation; the relative change between the phases of

6. Discussion  
 
The study sought to investigate the effects of salt weathering on different types of brick samples, 
compare the visual result with scientific measurements from different type of tests, and finally 
confirm or deny the reliability of the visual assessment of the material as the only method of 
assessment before potential reuse.  
 
Visual inspection of the salt weathered bricks revealed efflorescence, granular disintegration, 
subflorescence, blistering, chromatic alteration, and layering. The extent of these damage types 
varied among different brick categories, with the MX1 category (ideally suited for passive 
environments) demonstrating the highest extent of damage. Significant to the purpose of the study, 
it is documented that bricks previously used in masonry structures were prone to further 
deterioration upon exposure to additional weathering conditions, supporting the importance of 
considering both pre-existing and potential future damage in the selection and use of building 
materials. 
 
The ultrasonic measurement results indicated varied responses to salt weathering among the brick 
categories. However, higher variance in this category suggests the need for more in-depth study to 
confirm this trend and understand its implications. Intriguingly, the test found that a higher 
concentration of weathering solution is not necessarily introducing any significant change in 
transition time, as seen in the MX1 and MX3.2 – MX4 categories based solely on the ultrasonic 
measurement. 
 
The IC measurements offered insights into changes in the composition of the bricks after 
weathering. Despite notable variability in the measurements post-weathering, significant relative 
changes were observed. For instance, the MX3.2 - MX4 category bricks had the lowest initial 
measurements for Ca, K, Na but showed an increase in K post-weathering. Interestingly, the MX3.2 
used bricks showed a higher concentration of these metals at the outset, likely due to prior 
environmental exposure. A rise in conductivity following weathering was also noted, correlating with 
the addition of an electrolyte (sodium sulfate) during the weathering process. 
 
In general, the results from this study support the damaging effects of salt weathering on bricks, 
demonstrating various physical and compositional changes that may significantly impact brick 
integrity. It is suggested that further studies need to be conducted, potentially with more 
weathering cycles, to investigate these effects in greater depth, in order to support the development 
more comprehensive mitigation strategies. Additionally, the study highlighted the impact of the 
brick's initial state (new vs. used) on the weathering outcomes, suggesting the need for careful 
consideration of brick histories in planning and decision-making.

Future research may focus on establishing a threshold for elements concentration leading to visible 
damage conditions, although such efforts would require extensive exploration and validation. From 
the data collected in the study, it seems feasible with further exploration to establish thresholds at 
least in Na and S for Severe (2) and Limited (1) damage as seen below.  
 
An application for such understanding could identify bricks close to the threshold, so reused bricks 
can be sampled and determine if reusing them would adversely affect a construction. If those 
thresholds could be established, it would be valuable to examine the relationship with ultrasonic 
measurements as done in this study. However, as is also reported in the literature (Menendez, 
2016), it has been challenging to identify patterns in the ultrasonic measurements’ dataset.

7. Conclusion 
 
In conclusion, this study has achieved its primary objective of investigating the durability of fired clay 
bricks under salt attack and assessing their reusability. The results of the visual inspection agree with 
the documented in literature potential of the salt weathering process to significantly affect brick 
integrity. The impact is documented in accordance the Damage Atlas contributing to the body of 
knowledge documented damages, specifically. This information is crucial in understanding the 
extent of degradation these bricks might endure in a real-world scenario, affecting their potential for 
reuse. 
Ionic measurements provided important insights into the changes in the bricks' composition pre- 
and post-salt weathering, while ultrasonic measurements provided limited insight. Capturing the 
compositional shifts in bricks before and after salt weathering showed promising potential in 
estimating the reusability of bricks, with the condition that future research should be carried out for 
further refinement and standardization-categorization.  
In terms of the brick categories, the traditionally manufactured bricks (MX3.2 - MX4) appear to 
demonstrate a higher resistance to salt weathering compared to the others, suggesting their 
potential for reuse might be higher. However, more detailed studies are necessary to confirm this. 
The results from this study contribute to the field of building practices by promoting the reuse of 
building materials, especially fired clay bricks. The findings also underline the importance of 
standardized, in-situ criteria for determining brick reusability.

8. Limitations and Further studies 
 
This study, as is common with experimental research, faced certain limitations that should be 
considered when interpreting its results and proposing future investigations. 
One significant constraint was the time available for the experiment, which only allowed for three 
cycles of accelerated salt testing instead of the initially planned four. The reduced number of cycles 
may have limited the depth of insight into the brick durability and reusability under extended salt 
attack. Future studies should aim to include more testing cycles to more thoroughly evaluate the 
bricks' responses to prolonged salt weathering. 
Another limitation occurred from the lack of data concerning the used brick samples. Without 
complete background information about the conditions these bricks had been exposed to prior to 
the study, a fully detailed assessment of their responses to salt weathering was challenging. This 
limited the ability to attribute observed changes with certainty to the salt weathering process or pre-
existing damage, therefore affecting the certainty of our conclusions about their reusability. Further 
research should attempt to collect more detailed data for used brick samples, or at the very least, 
acknowledge this as a potential source of variability. 
The methodology of this study primarily focused on non-destructive testing (NDT) methods. While 
these are valuable for preserving the sample integrity, they sometimes offer a less complete 
understanding of material properties and degradation mechanisms compared to destructive tests. 
Incorporation of additional testing techniques could provide corroborating evidence and validate the 
findings from the NDT methods. 
In light of these limitations, future research should aim for a more comprehensive approach, 
blending destructive and non-destructive tests and seeking opportunities to incorporate advanced 
testing techniques like MIP, where feasible. A study on Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) of bricks could be 
a very useful tool, opening the way to better determine or estimate the lifespan of the material and 
the environmental impact of its use. An LCA study requires a holistic approach to data collection, 
including detailed background information on used brick samples, something that would also 
enhance our understanding of brick weathering. 
 
By addressing these limitations, future studies can substantially contribute to the sustainable 
construction field, offering robust assessments of brick reusability and promoting responsible 
resource usage.
