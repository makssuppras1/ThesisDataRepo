Abstract
This thesis presents the development and implementation of a medium­scale automated
buffer mixing system designed to comply with Current Good Manufacturing Practices
(cGMP). The project addresses the inefficiencies and inaccuracies of traditional manual
buffer preparation methods in pharmaceutical laboratories.
Central to the system is a user­friendly graphical user interface (GUI) facilitating precise
dosing of stock solutions. The system’s development journey involved designing, devel­
opment, configurations and integrating a GUI into the systems functionalities.
This innovation significantly reduced buffer preparation time by 90% compared to manual
methods, offering a step­change in laboratory efficiency. The detailed design, develop­
ment, programming approach, and the analysis of the results are extensively further dis­
cussed, underlining the system’s potential to revolutionize buffer preparation in medium­
scale laboratory settings.
Additionally, the thesis proposes future developments aimed at further improving the sys­
tem performance and expanding its capabilities.

Introduction
The pharmaceutical manufacturing industry is essential to global healthcare, as it pro­
duces critical medications and therapies.
These products are key to maintaining the
health and overall wellness of people around the world. One fundamental process within
this sector is buffer preparation, the creation of carefully measured mixtures to main­
tain specific pH levels in pharmaceutical formulations. The present scenario for buffer
preparation ranges from, very small­scale automated systems, and manual operations
for medium and laboratory settings to large­scale automated systems. In the laboratory,
manual buffer preparation is a tedious and time­consuming process, subject to human
error, contamination risks, and issues in accuracy and reproducibility, which can directly
affect product quality and patient safety. [3], [4].
Before delving further into the thesis, it’s crucial to understand the context and need for
these technologies in the pharmaceutical industry. The evolution of buffer preparation
methods, from manual to automated systems, has been driven by the demand for higher
precision, efficiency, and compliance with regulatory standards.[5] This shift is especially
relevant in the context of medium­scale laboratories where the balance between cost,
efficiency, and compliance is critical.
The majority of the automated buffer preparation systems for large­scale manufacturing
depend on laboratory results that are still manual. Additionally, these systems are often
too large and expensive to be feasible for medium­sized laboratory settings. On the other
hand, small­scale automated systems fail to meet the demands of medium laboratory op­
erations due to their limited capacity. The significance of solving this problem extends to a
broad range of stakeholders, including pharmaceutical companies, regulatory authorities,
healthcare professionals, and patients. Automation would allow pharmaceutical compa­
nies to reduce costs, increase productivity, and improve product quality.[6][5] For regu­
lators, enhanced compliance with current Good manufacturing practices (CGMP) would
ensure improved patient safety.
1.1
What are Buffers?
A buffer is a solution designed to maintain stability, particularly in pH levels during analyti­
cal applications such as those performed in water quality analysis. A buffer solution plays
a key role by counteracting fluctuations in pH level that could arise from the addition of
samples that are acidic, or base. Therefore analysts gain a critical tool for controlling and
stabilizing pH levels by using buffers. This control is not just a matter of convenience but
is often a necessity for the accuracy and reliability of the analytical procedures. Maintain­
ing control over variables in an experiment or analysis is crucial for ensuring the accuracy
and repeatability of the results. During an analysis, it’s essential to consider a range of
variables, including both chemical and physical factors. For instance, introducing a small
amount of a sample into a solvent can result in a notable shift in pH levels. This shift may
then lead to other alterations, including changes in solubility and the precipitation of the
sample. Consequently, this highlights the critical role of buffers in many applications.

Manual buffer preparation process
The manual process of buffer preparation involves a series of precise steps performed by
the user to ensure the correct composition of the buffer solution:
1. User weighs the chemical on the scale and transfers it to a glass bottle.
2. Adds water to the same glass bottle, this mix creates one stock solution. Note: The
first two steps are iterated until all stock solutions used for that specific buffer are
created.
3. These stock solutions are then combined with water in specific proportions to create
the final buffer solution.
The second and third steps can be visualized in figure 1.1, where the buffer composition
is explained. The conventional approach to preparing buffers is a complex process with
Figure 1.1: Buffer composition
several aspects that could be optimized for better efficiency.
• Ergonomics: The manual method can be ergonomically inefficient, potentially caus­
ing discomfort due to fixed positions or repetitive movements.
• Time Efficiency: It is a slow process that requires careful weighing, mixing, and
documentation.
• Monotony: The repetitive nature of manual tasks may lead to a monotonous work­
flow, affecting user focus and productivity.
• Precision and Record­Keeping: Accuracy of the buffer preparation and the thor­
oughness of the documentation are critical, manual processes are susceptible to
human error.
• Space Utilization: Manual preparation can require substantial bench space to ac­
commodate all necessary materials and measuring devices.
1.2
Current Good Manufacturing Practices cGMP
In the pharmaceutical industry, Good Manufacturing Practices (GMP) are essential guide­
lines that ensure product quality and safety. The term ’Current Good Manufacturing Prac­
tices’ (cGMP) is derived from GMP, with the ’C’ emphasizing the need to stay current
with the latest standards and technologies. cGMP represents an evolving, up­to­date
version of GMP, ensuring that manufacturing processes adapt to new advancements and
changes in the industry.

cGMP and its relation to GxP
Gxp is an acronym where ’G’ stands for ’good’, ’x’ is a
variable, and ’P’ stands for ’practices’ [7] which are a set of guidelines that ensures that
products are manufactured and distributed safely and effectively.[[8] ]
GxP therefore refers to a set of rules that guide the entire value chain such as. GMP­ Good
manufacturing practice, GLP ­ Good Laboratory Practice, GDocP ­ Good Dodumentation
Practice, and GAMP ­ Good Automated Manufacturing Practice
The core principles of GxP are:
1. Traceability: The ability to trace what happened
2. Accountability: The capacity to determine who did what and when
3. Data Integrity: How trustworthy is the generated data from the system?
cGMP, is a subset of GxP [7],[8], [9] which refers to the regulations connected to the
current manufacturing aspect of drugs, medical devices and Active Pharmaceutical in­
gredients (API).
cGMP, as part of the broader GxP guidelines, focuses on the current standards in drug,
medical device, and Active Pharmaceutical Ingredient (API) manufacturing. It extends be­
yond GMP by emphasizing the necessity of up­to­date practices in the design, construc­
tion, and maintenance of facilities and equipment to prevent contamination and ensure
product quality. This includes adhering to well­documented procedures and conducting
thorough validation processes. The transition from GMP to cGMP underscores a commit­
ment to continuous improvement and adaptation to evolving industry standards.
1.3
Problem Formulation
As part of its commitment to global sustainability objectives, Novo Nordisk (NN) consis­
tently aims to improve its operational practices. One of the areas that have come to their
attention is the increase in reported cases of pain and injuries related to manual and repet­
itive lifting within the laboratories. This contrasts with a general decreasing trend in the
same type of injuries in production and other areas, attributed to the increased adoption
of collaborative robots and other automated solutions.
Future buffer preparation must address the need for improvements in the current manual
process and align with increased process efficiencies and throughput resulting from auto­
mated solutions to avoid becoming a productivity bottleneck. It needs to address speed,
safety, accuracy and quality aligning with the company policies that outline patient safety
and product quality.
The purpose of this thesis is to identify, develop, and evaluate a solution for Novo Nordisk
that handles the current buffer preparation challenges and showcases the advantages of
an automated buffer mixing process.
This solution will serve as the baseline to verify the feasibility of such a system in meeting
Novo Nordisk’s requirements. The current steps of buffer preparation must be identified
and analyzed, and Novo Nordisk’s requirements within the highly regulated industry need
to be assessed for compliance with the GMP rules.
1.4
Problem Statement
How can an automated buffer mixing system be developed to significantly reduce over­
all buffer preparation time while enhancing accuracy, consistency, and compliance with
Current Good Manufacturing Practices (CGMP)?

1.5
Project Goals
The process of buffer preparation requires a high level of precision, as the buffers must
maintain specific acidity or alkalinity levels within a precise concentration range. The
main goal of this project is to develop an automated buffer mixing system for medium­
scale laboratories. To fulfill the primary goal, this is divided into five achievable targets,
outlined as follows:
1. Design an automated buffer mixing system for medium­scale laboratories.
2. Develop a minimum viable product of the automated buffer mixing system.
3. Design a user­friendly interface for the system.
4. Validate the compliance of the System with CGMP and user satisfaction.
5. Document the build and steps required to reproduce the system.
1.6
Scope
The scope of the thesis is to design and assess the performance of an automated buffer
mixing system for mid­sized laboratories in the pharmaceutical sector, aiming to boost
precision, consistency, and speed in buffer preparation, in line with cGMP. The research
addresses key issues in manual buffer creation, exploring solutions to reduce these met­
rics. This involves evaluating system requirements such as dosing capacity and error
margins, and considering limitations related to programming language and operational
constraints, all while ensuring patient safety and product quality in drug development.
Figure 1.1 illustrates the boundaries of the study’s focus. Specifically, it highlights that
preparation of stock solutions using chemicals and water, falls outside the scope of this
thesis. This delineation helps to clarify the extent of the research and the specific areas
it will cover.
1.6.1
System requirements
The requirements for the project will be guided by the project goals and were generated
during the initial meetings with the client. The word ”client” refers to the system benefi­
ciary, Novo Nordisk laboratory located in Hagedornsvej 1, 2820 Gentofte. These specified
requirements also serve as the criteria for the minimum viable product of the project.
The system should:
• Prepare a 2000g buffer in about 3 minutes.
• Prepare various volumes of buffers, ranging from 500g ­ 2000g.
• Dose from 15 different stock solutions.
• Have a maximum dosing error for stock solution or water of less than 3%.
• Flush the pumps to clean the tubing before dosing, eliminating any residual salt

• Have thorough documentation, mainly focused on code description.
• Keep minimum dependencies of libraries in the software development, for ease of

Peristaltic pumps work on peristalsis, sequentially compressing and releasing a flexible
tube to move fluids. This creates a wave­like motion for propulsion, with the fluid entirely
enclosed in the tube, ensuring purity and minimizing contamination. The pump consists
of a flexible tube, known as the peristaltic hose or tube, enclosed in a circular casing.
Inside the casing, a rotor with a series of rollers compresses the flexible tube. As the
rotor turns, the part of the tube under compression closes (or occludes), thus forcing the
fluid through the tube. The criteria for selection emphasize contamination prevention,
adaptability, precision, and maintenance ease. The rationale behind choosing peristaltic
pumps is detailed in table 4.2.

4.5
Software Development
To facilitate seamless communication and control of the Buffer mixing system, a closed
feedback loop was implemented, as illustrated in the figure below [4.22] The feedback loop
allows for the exact amount of solution to be dispensed by making real­time adjustments
based on the scale’s readings. The system operates In the following way
Figure 4.22: Feedback control loop for the system
1. The user inputs the desired mass of the stock solution into the laptop.
2. The laptop commands the Labjack to activate the pump.
3. The pump dispenses the solution, which is then weighed by the scale.
4. The scale sends the measured mass back to the laptop.
5. The laptop calculates any difference between the desired mass (target) and the
actual mass (output), which is considered the error.
6. The laptop adjusts the command to the Labjack accordingly, to reduce the error by
dispensing more solutions if necessary.
7. The process repeats until the error is minimized and the target mass is reached.
8. Once the target is reached, the laptop instructs the Labjack to turn off the pump,
stopping the dispensing process.

Watchdog Timer (WDT)
The Watchdog Timer (WDT) plays a crucial role in microcontroller­based systems, func­
tioning as a fail­safe mechanism. It monitors the system’s operation, ensuring that the soft­
ware executes as intended. The watchdog is adept at detecting and responding to system
malfunctions, safeguarding systems against potential operational errors or anomalies.
A core functionality of the watchdog is to require regular ’check­ins’ from the microcon­
troller. If the microcontroller fails to reset the watchdog timer within a predefined interval,
it indicates that the system may have malfunctioned and triggers a system reset. This
mechanism is vital for maintaining system integrity and preventing potential failures due
to software errors or unexpected operational issues.
Different operational modes of the watchdog cater to varying safety requirements: Time­
out Mode:

• Pulse duration ­ refers to the duration of time the current is being supplied to the
pump. While the value difference may seem small, it has a significant impact as it
reduces the dosed quantity for each pulsation from 0.25 to 0.22 grams on average.
The process of determining this value involved identifying the shortest pulsation
duration at which all 114FD pumps completed half a rotation. This critical point was
established to be at 0.035 seconds. However, to account for potential variations and
hardware differences between pumps, a conservative value of 0.045 seconds was
selected to provide a margin of safety and reliability.
• Slow mode threshold ­ translates to how many grams before target the pump switches
from continuous to pulsed pumping. This parameter reduced the time significantly
as seen in table. With each pulsation dispensing 0.22g of stock solution or water,
the number of pulsations required decreased from 23 to 14. This decreases the
pulsation total time for each pump from 37 seconds (required for 5 grams) to 22
seconds (needed for 3 grams). Consequently, a 2­liter buffer containing 3 stock so­
lutions and water can be prepared more efficiently, reducing preparation time by 60
seconds.
• Scale Stabilization pause ­ considers the impact of forces exerted by the dispensed
liquid. A longer period allows the scale to stabilize before reading resulting in in­
creased measurement accuracy. In the previous configuration, the system initiated
the next pulsation before the scale value had reached stability.
• Stop threshold ­ represents the limit at which the system terminates dosing, con­
cluding the buffer preparation. Lowering this limit enables the system to approach
the target value more closely, enhancing accuracy. For example, if one pump´s
target is 50 grams and the current value is 49.95 grams, the system will terminate
operation. Conversely, if the actual value is 49.85 grams, the system will execute
one more pulsation.
• The water pump swap threshold defines the transition point at which the system

The feedback from the primary users was very positive with the majority rating the overall
usability as ”Very easy” or ”Somewhat easy.” This indicates that the interface adheres to
the development goals of ease of use and intuitiveness.
The learning curve for operating the GUI was found to be minimal, suggesting that users
with varying technological proficiency can easily adapt to the system. This seamless
transition is crucial as it facilitates ease of the adoption of the new system.
Users expressed a lot of satisfaction with the ”make buffer” workflow, which closely aligned
with their standard operating procedures. Suggestions for improvement included the ad­
dition of visual indicators for dosing progression, which could further enhance user inter­
action.
The final GUI not only fulfils its functional requirements but also caters to the users’ prefer­
ences and operational needs. It effectively bridges the gap between complex automated
processes and user­friendly interactions, allowing users to focus on their primary tasks
without being overwhelmed by the technology. This balanced approach to complexity
and usability is a testament to the project’s success, confirming that the automated sys­
tem supports rather than hinders the laboratory staff’s workflow.

tory environments. Key factors contributing to this scalability include:
• Modular Physical Design: The system’s design fits within a standard laboratory cab­
inet, as seen in its implementation at Novo Nordisk. This modular approach means
it can be easily integrated into different lab setups without requiring significant space
modifications.
• Robust Hardware: The choice of industrial­grade components makes the system
durable and reliable, even in continuous operation. This robustness is crucial for
scalability as it ensures consistent performance across different operational inten­
sities and environments.

6.0.6
Development Challenges:
Despite the advantages brought in by automating the buffer­making process, the system
posed new challenges that were identified throughout the course of the project. Some of
the identified challenges are:
1. High initial Cost: To build a robust system, there was needed to use reliable industrial
high­grade components which presented a high initial investment. The purchase of
the components was limited to those available from the company’s approved vendor
list, which limited the variety of selections. Additionally, ongoing maintenance costs,
such as the replacement of tubing and pumps, contribute to an increase in total
expenditure
2. Training and Competency Development: Implementing the new system necessi­
tates specialized training for laboratory technicians and users. This training covers
new competencies in database management, and other technical skills needed to
maintain the system, marking a shift from the traditional manual processes of buffer
preparation. This effectively makes ongoing training necessary to keep up with the
latest updates and improvements. Therefore the users need to acquire new skills to
work with the system which is different from what they were used to in the manual
buffer­making process.
3. Regulatory Compliance: Ensuring the system meets stringent regulatory standards
is paramount for validation. Therefore, for the system t, too be accepted as GMP
compliant there was a need to ensure that the processes were validated and met the
standards set by the authorities. Compared to the manual buffer­making process
that was already validated and had been used for a very long period.
4. Technical Issues: Reliance on an automated system introduces the risk of malfunc­
tions and downtime, which can adversely affect buffer preparation schedules and
overall product quality.
5. Cybersecurity: As a computerised system, the buffer station when connected to the
company’s IT systems, poses additional cybersecurity risks, requiring robust secu­
rity measures to protect sensitive data and intellectual property. The system adds
another layer of potential cyber threat entry points into Novo Nordisk´s IT system
increasing the company’s vulnerability.

7
Conclusions
The development of the automated buffer mixing system in this project marks a significant
step in laboratory automation, particularly in laboratory buffer preparation. As demon­
strated through the results and feedback from the project stakeholders, automating the
buffer­making process has brought forth several notable advantages, which include:
1. Increased accuracy and reduced errors: The system’s design significantly mini­
mizes human error, thereby increasing accuracy in buffer preparations.
2. Productivity: The system streamlines the buffer preparation processes, reducing
the time and labor required to make buffers compared to manual methods. With
the improved efficiency, the overall buffer preparation capacity is increased and also
frees up personnel for other tasks. For example, in the department where the system
was implemented, one only needs to spend three minutes in preparing a buffer
instead of 30 minutes, as before.
3. Traceability and data integrity: With the elimination of manual recording of data, the
automation of data recording in the system, allows for real­time documentation of
all processes and parameters such as weights, volumes, and time. This capability
simplifies cGMP compliance, by offering precise traceability and supporting data
integrity.
4. Reduced Contamination Risk: The system design limits human contact with buffer
components lowering contamination risks, essential for maintaining sterility in asep­
tic processes.
5. Consistency and Quality: Automating the process ensures uniform buffer composi­
tions, thereby maintaining the quality and consistency of final products.
The above attributes significantly contribute to the pharmaceutical and bioprocessing in­
dustries, providing a solution that addresses previously unmet needs in buffer system
design and development.
The journey of this project has been enlightening, with the design, development, and test­
ing phases providing valuable insights into practical, compliance­oriented pharmaceutical
processes. This project not only synchronizes with current trends in buffer preparation
automation but also paves the way for further innovation in laboratory automation. It
promises to increase efficiency, accuracy, and compliance in future applications, aligning
with the broader trends observed in the field. As a testament to the effectiveness and
innovation of the buffer mixing system developed in this thesis, it is noteworthy to mention
its real­world impact and acceptance. By the time of submitting this thesis, the system
has already been implemented in three different laboratories. This not only demonstrates
the system’s practicality and relevance in the industry but also underscores its viability
and scalability. The adoption of the buffer mixing system in these laboratories serves as
a significant validation of the research and development efforts undertaken in this thesis,
and it paves the way for further advancements and wider usage in the field.

Additional (GUI) Features

Currently, the admin panel does not allow users to edit chemical data and buffer recipes.

1. Weight threshold activation for Dosing: A new feature that automatically detects
the weight of the bottle placed on the scale, would be a great additional functionality.
The program would not start dosing unless the weight of the bottle placed on the
scale falls within a predefined range. This feature will prevent dosing if no bottle is
placed on the scale, thereby avoiding accidental spills and ensuring that the process
starts only under the correct conditions or placement of an appropriate bottle on the
scale. The software can also be extended to automatically set a maximum limit for
the amount of buffer that can be dispensed. This will enhance the system’s safety
and reliability by ensuring that dosing occurs only when all parameters are correctly
aligned.
2. Option to copy or change some parts of a buffer recipe The current system
allows users to create, delete, and modify entire buffer recipes. However, there
may be instances where users need to copy and modify specific components within
an existing buffer recipe without having to recreate the entire recipe. To address this,
the system should be equipped with a feature that enables users to copy and modify
individual components within a buffer recipe. This would provide greater flexibility
and efficiency in recipe management
3. Use of a tablet with Touchscreen Interface instead of a laptop: In the new itera­
tion, instead of running the program from a laptop, the system will be operated via a
tablet, replacing the current laptop­based control. Tablets have touch­screen inter­
faces, which can make systems easier to operate compared to traditional laptops.
The shift to tablets will improve user experience by providing a more interactive and
engaging interface. Furthermore, the tablet’s touchscreen is built for straightfor­
ward, efficient cleaning, crucial for GMP compliance. Tablets have also an intuitive
design that simplifies navigation, making it more user­friendly for tasks needing reg­
ular adjustments or selections.
Web based system Currently, the buffer systems software runs on a local application

advantages, such as direct control over the system and offline operation, it also limits
accessibility and flexibility. Transitioning to a web­based interface will help
• Improve Accessibility: A web­based interface would eliminate the need for instal­
lation on individual computers or tablets, allowing users to access the system from
any device with an internet connection.
• Centralized Management and Control: A web­based interface would provide cen­
tralized management and control over the buffer System, enabling administrators to
monitor system usage, track performance data, and manage user access permis­
sions.
• Future scalability and flexibility: A web­based interface would pave the way for future
scalability and flexibility, allowing more buffer systems to run on the same application
and the integration of additional features and functionalities without the need for
software updates on individual computers.
