Abstract
The extremely reactive zero valent iron nanoparticles (NZVI) offer the possibility to solve long-
lasting problems that have been difﬁcult to deal with by classical technologies of water treatment.
Laboratory studies have demonstrated great success in puriﬁcation of uranium and arsenic, in
addition to the reduction of heavy metals, nitrates, phosphates and pesticides in contaminated water.
Although NZVI have been widely used in groundwater treatment research, limited information is
available on its application in waste water and drinking water treatment.
In this study, the feasibility of using NZVI for water treatment was evaluated by means of
experiments and mathematical modelling. To account for the contamination caused by the NZVI it-
self, immobilization of NZVI has been proposed. Alginate beads produced by entrapping the NZVI
in a gel matrix (entrapment beads) and encapsulating NZVI in the core of a gel bead (encapsulation
beads) were studied to determine the optimal NZVI immobilization technique for water treatment
applications. Entrapment beads exhibited the best performance. The puriﬁcation of phosphate as a
contaminant model reached 99 % in 60 minutes using NZVI immobilized in entrapment beads.
An alternative NZVI immobilization technique for water treatment was proposed. The tech-
nique involved entrapment of NZVI in microscale alginate ﬁbres that can be waved into an active
membrane. By incorporating NZVI in alginate ﬁbres, it is expected that high water treatment
properties can be obtained. The ﬁbre elasticity was investigated in order to evaluate if the ﬁbres can
withstand membrane-fabrication and water pressure. It was found that the strength of the alginate
ﬁbres declined with increasing NZVI concentration.
A simple simulation of a water treatment unit was constructed. The model indicated that a tank
containing 27 kg NZVI immobilized in beads can treat 500 litres of water with an efﬁciency of 93
% after 10 hours of operation. The efﬁciency declined to 89 % after 50 hours of continuous ﬂow
due to the loss of NZVI reactivity. According to established standards this removal is classiﬁed as
good. The results of this study can represent a step towards real water treatment units using NZVI.

Resume
De ekstremt reaktive nul valente jern nanopartikler (NZVI) giver mulighed for at løse langvarige
problemer, der er vanskelige at håndtere ved klassiske teknologier for vandbehandling. Laborato-
rieundersøgelser har vist stor succes i oprensning af uran, arsen, tungmetaller, nitrater, fosfater og
pesticider i vand. Selvom NZVI har været ﬂittigt anvendt i forskning for grundvands behandling, er
kun begrænset information tilgængelig for anvendelsen i spildevand- og drikkevandsrensning.
I dette studium blev muligheden for at bruge NZVI til vandrensning vurderet ved hjælp af
eksperimenter og matematisk modellering. For at tage højde for forurening forårsaget af selve
NZVI, er immobilisering af NZVI foreslået. Alginat kugler fremstillet ved indslutning af NZVI i en
gel matrix og ved indkapsling af NZVI i kernerne af alginat kugler blev undersøgt for at bestemme
den optimale teknik for NZVI immobilisering til anvendelse i vandrensnings anliggende. Indslut-
tede NZVI kugler udviste den bedste præstation. Ved brug af fosfat som model for forurenende
stoffer, nåede oprensningen 99 % på 10 timer.
En alternativ immobiliserings teknik af NZVI til vandbehandling blev foreslået. Teknikken
involverede indslutning af NZVI i mikroskala alginat-ﬁbre, der kan væves til en aktiv membran.
Ved at inkorporere NZVI i alginat-ﬁbre, forventes det, at der kan opnås høje oprensnings egen-
skaber. Fibrenes elasticitet blev undersøgt med henblik på at vurdere, om ﬁbrene kan modstå
membran-fabrikation og vandtryk. Det blev fundet, at styrken af alginat ﬁbre faldt med stigende
NZVI koncentration.
En simpel simulering af en vandbehandlings enhed blev konstrueret. Modellen indikerede,
at en vandrensnings tank med 27 kg NZVI immobiliseret i kugler kan behandle 500 liter vand
med en effektivitet på 93 % efter 10 timers operation. Effektiviteten faldt til 89 % efter 50 timers
kontinuerlig drift gundet nanopartiklernes tab a reaktivitet. Ifølge etablerede standarder kan denne
oprensning klassiﬁceres som god. Resultaterne af dette arbejde kan udgøre et skridt i retning mod
reele vandsrensnings enheder baseret på NZVI teknologi.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Demands for advanced water treatment techniques have intensiﬁed during the recent years due
to the need of providing safe potable water. Various technologies have been employed to remove
contaminants from waste waters before reuse or release to the environment, including chemical
precipitation, ﬁltration, ion-exchange and sedimentation [1] [2] [3] [4] [5]. Traditional methods
for contaminant removal are complex and costly [6]. Furthermore, classical technologies have yet
to solve long-lasting problems with high content of radioactive material or persistent contamination.
Zero Valent Iron nanoparticles (NZVI) represent a new environmental remediation technol-
ogy that has a great potential of being a solution to some of the most challenging contamination
problems [7]. The unique properties of NZVI come from their reduction capabilities, their high
speciﬁc surface area that allows extraordinary reactivity and their small size that permits intimate
contact with contaminants [8] [9]. Recently, NZVI have become a strategic material with broad
beneﬁts within in situ treatment of contaminants in groundwater and soil. Several aspects make
NZVI advantageous over existing remediation techniques, such as their ability to treat radioactive
material, TNT and pesticides. In addition, NZVI are believed to provide improved extent and rate
of contaminant remediation [9].
The mobility and migration potential of NZVI in groundwater and soil rise concern regarding
their potential harm. A perspective study in 2012 highlighted that scientists are putting pressure
on nanotechnology considering it the ultimate solution for most of the environmental problems.
Thereby, the scientists focus only on the results without considering any potential problems caused
by nanotechnology. This perspective study also highlighted that the application of NZVI in various
groundwater sites today is conducted without knowing the fate and toxicological assessment for
both environment and human health [10]. Thus, to prevent the nanoparticles from uncontrolled
dispersion in the environment, nanoparticle immobilization approaches have been investigated in
earlier studies [11].

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
The use of immobilized NZVI could potentially eliminate the risk of contamination caused
by the free nanoparticles. Today, two different immobilization methods are reported, namely the
entrapment and the encapsulation of NZVI in gel beads, respectively. The entrapment method im-
mobilizes the nanoparticles in a massive gel-bead, while the encapsulation method immobilizes the
nanoparticles inside a hallow core of a bead. Although several NZVI immobilization studies have
been conducted, no research has reported which immobilization technique is most efﬁcient for water
treatment. Another question not usually addressed in the context of treatment with immobilized
NZVI, is the required amount of necessary beads to achieve a practical effect within reasonable time.
This project suggests the use of immobilized NZVI for waste and drinking water treatment.
While it has been observed in experimental studies that the treatment of a single type of contaminant
is near completeness, the situation might get more complex when more than one contaminant type
is present in the water. Therefore, the performance of encapsulated and entrapped NZVI when
using one or two contaminants is interesting to investigate. If immobilized NZVI in beads is to be
used for water treatment, it will be important to determine which bead-immobilization method is
most sufﬁcient. Furthermore, the efﬁciency of upscaled use of beads for water applications need to
be addressed by means of mathematical modelling, before deciding if NZVI-beads are suitable for
water treatment. Moreover, new more innovative approaches for NZVI immobilization need to be
developed to lift the NZVI technology to the real-life level within water applications. This study
suggests to immobilize NZVI in micro-alginate ﬁbres which can be weaved into active membranes
for water puriﬁcation purposes.
1.1
Project Objectives
The overall goal of this project is to investigate the possibility of applying immobilized nanoparticles
to water treatment. This goal is divided in the following objectives:
1. Investigate the performance of the two main NZVI immobilization techniques, namely the
encapsulation and entrapment in gel and determine the most relevant method for real-life
applications.
2. Find and evaluate the treatment efﬁciency when upscaling the application of immobilized
NZVI in beads by a simple simulation model.
3. Present a new innovative NZVI immobilization technique as an alternative to the bead
immobilization method.

2.1
Remediation with zero valent iron nanoparticles
In recent years NZVI have received a considerable amount of attention due to their ability to react
strongly with different substances. This is due to the high surface to volume ratio, which makes the
difference between the bulk- and the nano-material. Because of their highly reactive properties,
NZVI are employed in different applications to remediate persistent compounds in the environment
and transform them into a non-toxic state [12] (Figure 2.1).

In addition to the surface-to-volume ratio, the structure of the NZVI is responsible for the high
reactivity. An NZVI consists of an active-core of primarily zero-valent iron and a shell comprised
of mainly iron oxyhydroxides due to oxidation of the iron as shown in Figure 2.1. This structure
gives the NZVI the ability to act as an electron donor, which allows reaction and remediation with
the substances in the water [12].

The pH effect on the reactivity of the remediation rate can be described by the point of zero
charge and the ligand exchange of the nanoparticles as illustrated in Figure 2.2. The pH level can
effectively change the NZVI surface charge which allows the contaminants to be sorbed by NZVI.
The point of zero charge for NZVI is around pH 7.7. By reducing the pH level, the NZVI will be
positively charged and this will allow the sorption of anions. Studies have shown that the uptake
rate of a contaminant under different pH ranges reveals a maximum uptake rate at pH of 3.5 [15].
Another explanation for the pH effect on the NZVI reactivity was provided by Liou, et. al. in
2005, stating that by reducing the pH level below 7 the water will have acidic properties. The acid
will help melting the oxidized shell around the single particle; this process will bring the core of
the particle into contact with the chemicals in the water. The increase of pH during the chemical
reaction will increase the reaction time and lower the removal efﬁciency. To keep the removal
efﬁciency at a stable level with a relatively steady rate, the pH level should always be kept at the
same low pH level of around 3 [16].

2.1.2
Characteristics of zero valent iron nanoparticles
The NZVI used in this study (Nanofer 25 slurry, NANO IRON, Czech Republic) are a highly
reactive aqueous dispersion of the nanoparticles stabilized by inorganic material. The average
particle size is 50 nm. The nanoparticles have a tendency for agglomeration and sedimentation.
Agglomeration describes the attraction and adherence between the NZVI that eventually form
larger assemblages. Both agglomeration and sedimentation reduce the NZVI effective surface area.
The speciﬁc method for NZVI production is not stated by the manufacturer.
To preserve the reactivity of the NZVI during storage, the NZVI were kept in stabilizing
slurry with a high pH level around 11-12. Since these nanoparticles are highly reactive, it was
recommended that the slurry was kept at a temperature between 2-4 oC. A study conducted by the
manufacturer showed that the NZVI will only lose 9 % of their reactivity after 35 days if stored at a
low temperature. If stored at room temperature the loss of the reactivity will be around 17.5 % [8].
2.1.3
Reactivity of zero-valent iron nanoparticles
The NZVI are classiﬁed as very reactive due to their structure and size. One of the chemical groups
that NZVI can react with is acids. During the reaction process with acids, hydrogen gas is produced.
By the end of this reaction the NZVI will be neutralized and will have no reactivity left. Thus, the
hydrogen gas was used as a measure of the reactivity of the particles. In the experiments hydrogen
was measured in a system consisting of glass containers.

In this study, the toxic immobilization materials were excluded. Since Carrageenan requires
high temperatures it was excluded as well, leaving alginate and polyvinyl alcohol gel as options.
Since alginate is the most common material to immobilize NZVI, alginate has been chosen for this
study. Alginate material is a natural compound that is produced from brown seaweeds. Moreover,
it is a salt that when added to water produces a viscous solution. The alginate solution has a
unique property of transforming into a gel when calcium ions (Ca2+) are present [23]. Alginate is
known to be non-toxic, biodegradable and commonly used to encapsulate medicine, which are all
important properties when considering an environment friendly material. Today, alginate beads are
used in different industries such as the food industry and the medical industry to deliver different
enzymes and drugs. In addition, alginate beads have been utilized to introduce microorganisms
into the environment for bioaugmentation purposes.
There are two different immobilization methods in beads; either a solid structure that is used
to entrap the targeted substance (entrapment) or a halo structure that contain a double shell and a
suspension of the targeted substance inside the shell (encapsulation), see Figure 2.3.

These two different types of immobilization are mainly the outcome of either an external

Lambert-Beer law
The analysis of the quantity of contaminants in water samples was conducted by spectrophotome-
try. Spectrophotometry is the quantiﬁcation of the reﬂection or transmission with respect to the
wavelength. The Lambert-Beer law describes this relation by the attenuation of electromagnetic
radiation in the absorbent, for instance a solution of a dye in a liquid. The law is expressed by the
exponential function in equation 2.6:
I = I0exp(−αL)
(2.6)
where I0 is the initial intensity, I is the intensity after travelling the distance L and α is the
absorbance coefﬁcient that depends on the characteristic wavelength for a substance. Within small
ranges, the linear relationship between absorbance and concentration of an absorbing specie is
described in equation 2.7:
A = αLc
(2.7)
where A is the measured absorbance and c is the analyte concentration.
2.4
Scanning Electron Microscopy
Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) was used to characterize the immobilization items fabricated
in this study. In a SEM, a beam of electrons from an electron gun is focused on the sample by
electromagnetic lenses. When the electron beam interacts with the atoms of the sample three
scenarios can arise: Firstly, the electrons can either pass through the sample without colliding with
any atoms. Secondly, the electrons can inelastically collide with electrons in the sample, hereby
out-knocking so-called secondary electrons. Finally, the electrons can elastically collide with the
nucleus of the sample atoms, creating the so-called backscattered electrons. Different detectors are
used to collect the departing electrons to create an image of the sample. In this work, the secondary
electron detector was used to characterize the samples [25].

2.5
Energy-Dispersive X-ray analysis
When an electron beam is hitting the sample surface in a SEM with inelastic interaction (see section
2.4), the secondary electrons that have left the atoms leave holes in the inner shells. These holes
in the inner shells make the atoms unstable. In order to stabilize the atoms, electrons from outer
shells drop into the inner shell, thereby emitting energy in the form of X-rays. The energy and
wavelength of the emitted X-rays are characteristic for the atom and the shell they are released
from. Thus, the elemental composition of a sample can be determined by Energy-dispersive X-ray
analysis (EDX). Since this work focuses on NZVI, X-rays emitted from iron would have peaks
at 704, 6400 and 7057 eV, depending on the parenting shell. Furthermore, EDX can quantify the
elements it is detecting. Usually the quantitative analysis is standardless, meaning that the elements
are calculated based on the area under the electron beam using an algorithm that compensate for
any artefacts [25].

entrapped or encapsulated in alginate, has been reported for remediation of various contaminants.
Several of the laboratory experiments achieved 100 % removal, however, there is a remarkable
difference in the NZVI concentration and time used to achieve full remediation. Kanel et al.
reported 100 % arsenic III removal when using 1 g/L for 12 hours [34], while phosphate was
remediated by Almeelbi et al. in 30 minutes using an NZVI concentration of only 0.4 g/L [15].
This difference in NZVI concentration and time needed to achieve complete treatment indicates that
these factors are dependent on the speciﬁc contaminants. Nonetheless, the review in Table 2.2 also
shows that the removal efﬁciency for the same contaminant using the same NZVI concentration
can vary from study to another. This could be explained by the study of Westerhoff. Westerhoff
compared the uptake for NZVI obtained from different suppliers. His experiments were conducted
using a pH level of 2 and using the same nitrate concentration for all the experiments. The results
showed signiﬁcant variations in the removal efﬁciency. Nanoparticles obtained from Masterbuilder
Inc. showed 20 % removal efﬁciency after 5 hours, while NZVI obtained from Aldrich showed 62
% removal efﬁciency after 1 minute [35]. The effect of the pH level was studied in earlier studies
using several batches with only the pH level as the variable. It was found that by decreasing the pH
level, the required time for reacting with nitrate will be reduced [36].

NZVI have received great attention due to their unique reactive characteristics. Because of their
nanoscale size, NZVI are highly mobile and tend to agglomerate due to magnetic and van der
Waals forces. The agglomeration forms larger particles that eliminate the high speciﬁc surface area
of NZVI which gives them their unique advantage over macro-scale ZVI. Inactivation of NZVI
due to interaction with oxygen in water or air is also an issue that shorten their reactivity time.
Furthermore, the perceived risks related to the release of NZVI to the environment is a general
concern. To overcome these problems, NZVI have been entrapped or encapsulated in porous gels.
However, no thorough studies have evaluated which immobilization technique is most beneﬁcial
for site applications or for water treatment. In this chapter, the performance of immobilized NZVI
was demonstrated using NO−
3 and PO3−
4
as model contaminants.
3.1
Concept of NZVI based water treatment system
A potential water treatment unit based on NZVI technology could be comprised of a water tank
with continuous mechanical stirring, inlet of the contaminated water and an outlet of the treated
water. The tank contains the NZVI immobilized in beads. The tank and the outlet are separated
with a ﬁlter that can prevent the beads from exiting the tank and contaminating the treated water.
The basic components of the proposed NZVI water treatment system are sketched in Figure 3.1.
The system might be used for waste water or drinking water treatment applications.

3.4.1
Immobilization of nanoparticles in alginate beads
One of the reasons behind immobilization of NZVI is to prevent the free spreading of the nanoparti-
cles in the environment. The isolation of NZVI from the surroundings by beads enables harvesting
them due to their larger diameter compared to single NZVI. There are two main methods to
immobilize NZVI in alginate beads [50] [40], namely encapsulation and entrapment. These two
methods have been investigated in this chapter. Figure 3.3 shows a typical setup for the production
of beads, where a power supply was set to 5 V (1) and was used to provide power to a peristaltic
pump (2). The pump withdrew the mixture from a reservoir (3) and pumped it to the other end
of a 1 mm tubing (4) and an attached nozzle of 0.5 mm in diameter (5). The mixture was then
pumped drop-wise into the solution (6), hereby creating the beads. In the following, the speciﬁc
immobilization procedures will be presented.

3.4. METHODS
23
Entrapment of NZVI in alginate beads
Entrapment of NZVI in the alginate structure is possible by adding the NZVI suspension directly
into the alginate solution. The standard entrapment of NZVI was achieved by the following steps at
room temperature [40]:
1) 30 ml of deionized water was ﬂushed with nitrogen gas for 30 minutes to decrease the
oxygen concentration as much as possible.
2) 1.0 g alginate was added into the water under continuous magnetic stirring, resulting in a
ﬁnal concentration of 33.3 g/L. After 30 minutes the mixing was stopped and the solution was left
at room temperature for one hour to allow the air bubbles to escape.
3) 5 g NZVI was added to the batch. 1 mL of the NZVI slurry equals 1 g NZVI as stated by the
manufacturer. The NZVI suspension was added to the alginate solution and was stirred thoroughly
with a glass rod.
4) CaCl2 solution was prepared by adding 23.2 g of CaCl2 into 500 ml of deionized water.
5) The alginate-NZVI mixture was added drop-wise into the CaCl2 solution by a peristaltic
pump and using a ﬂow rate of 1 mL/minute from a height of 12 cm. This height has been reported
in literature to be the optimal for obtaining a spherical shape of the beads [40]. During the pumping
process the alginate solution was continuously mixed with a glass rod to ensure a homogeneous
mixture. The beads were formed instantly when the alginate drops became in contact with the
CaCl2 solution. The diameter of the beads was controlled by the nozzle of the system outlet, where
the size of the outlet is proportional with the size of the bead diameter. The beads were allowed to
harden in the CaCl2 solution for 24 hours at 4 o C before using them in the experiments.
Two types of entrapped NZVI in beads were prepared under two temperature conditions. The
standard entrapment was prepared by CaCl2 at room temperature and the heated entrapped beads
were prepared by heating the CaCl2 to 60o C. The heating of the CaCl2 was reported to provide
different properties of the beads due to the different cross-linking between CaCl2 and alginate [51].
Encapsulation of NZVI in alginate beads
NZVI can be encapsulated in alginate beads where the shell of these beads consists of alginate and
the core contains a suspension of NZVI in CaCl2 solution. By this technique the hardening of the
beads takes place internally, see section 2.2. The standard encapsulated NZVI in alginate beads
were generated by the following steps [50]:

1) CaCl2 solution was prepared by adding 0.25 g of CaCl2 and 4 g of Maltodextrin into 6 ml of
deionized water that had been ﬂushed with nitrogen gas for 30 minutes. Maltodextrin was used
to increase the viscosity of the mixture to achieve a bead shape as close to a sphere as possible.
Maltodextrin is produced from starch and can be easily digested. It is normally used in food
industry, especially for soda and candy production [52].
2) 5 mL NZVI suspension (5 g NZVI) was added to this solution and stirred for 5 minutes with
a glass rod to ensure complete mixing under continuous stirring.
3) The alginate mixture was prepared by dissolving 10 g alginate into 1 L of deionized water.
4) The CaCl2 and NZVI mixture prepared in step 2 was then dropped into the alginate by a
peristaltic pump from a height of 6 cm. The height was reported in literature to result the closest
shape to a sphere [50]. During the pumping process the CaCl2 was continuously stirred with a
glass rod to ensure a homogeneous mixture. The alginate was continuously stirred with magnetic
stirring at 2000 rpm. This ensured that the beads would not accumulate under the outlet nozzle and
cause a change in the free fall height of the drop. In addition, by continuous stirring, deformation
of the bead shape would be avoided when hitting another bead sitting in the beaker.
5) The beads were harvested from the alginate solution and rinsed with deionized water several
times. Then the beads were added to CaCl2 solution with a concentration of 46.4 g/L and left to
harden for 24 hours at 4 oC.
Thicker alginate bead walls than presented above were prepared in order to uncover if a thicker
shell provides a more robust bead. This was done by increasing the CaCl2 concentration 10 times
the concentration of the CaCl2 in the standard encapsulation described above.
For all immobilization techniques, the beads were rinsed several times after the hardening
process before using them in the experiments.

3.4.3
The reactivity loss test
The reactivity of entrapped NZVI in beads was investigated after the beads were used in experiments.
The beads were prepared by having them in batches with one contaminant in the water (KNO3).
Samples were taken from this batch every hour to measure the contaminant concentration and the
pH level. A time span of 10 hours was selected, since most of the experiments were run at least for
10 hours. After 10 hours the beads were harvested from the bottle, washed with water and then
reused in a second batch with new contaminated water. The same procedure was followed before
harvesting the beads and reusing them in a third batch. Thus, the difference between the slopes of
the uptake rate curves would represent the loss of reactivity over time.

It was expected that the contaminant removal efﬁciency for immobilized NZVI was reduced
compared to free NZVI, since the contact of the immobilized nanoparticles with the contaminants
in the water was not immediate. It can be seen that the entrapped NZVI had approximately the
same removal efﬁciency as the free NZVI, while the encapsulated NZVI showed higher removal
efﬁciency than the other two experiments. The reason for this difference between the encapsulated
and the entrapped NZVI might be due to the diffusion length. For the encapsulated NZVI beads, the
contaminants need to diffuse through the shell of the beads before reaching a concentrated amount
of NZVI in the core, hence the diffusion length equals the shell thickness of the bead. For the
entrapped NZVI the contaminants do not need to diffuse through a speciﬁc length before meeting
a nanoparticle, however, it needs to diffuse all the way through the bead to obtain the highest
probability of meeting the highest amount of NZVI. Therefore, the maximum effective diffusion
length when using entrapped NZVI in beads corresponds to the bead diameter when assuming a
straight diffusion path. The differences in diffusion lengths means that the diffusion time is smaller
for encapsulation beads than the entrapment beads. A rough estimation of the diffusion time using
equation 2.28, where the diffusion coefﬁcient of NO−
3 is 605 µ m2/s shows Tencapsulated ∼1 min
and Tentrapped ∼55 min. This indicates that the treatment process using encapsulated NZVI beads
was faster than the treatment with entrapped NZVI beads.
An additional factor that was causing this difference in the removal efﬁciency was the osmotic
gradient across the encapsulated beads. The concentrated salt inside the beads was much higher
than in the water which accelerated the osmosis process. This factor was not relevant for the
entrapment beads.

be considered higher than the hydrogen test since it depended on automatized spectrophotometry,
while the hydrogen test depended on reading the measuring cylinder.
3.6
Discussion
The different evaluation points explained in section 3.2 were used for the determination of the best
method for reality applications:
The robustness of the beads is one of the important aspects needed to be tested. It was observed
through the different experiments that the encapsulation beads can be damaged by the magnetic
stirring while such an effect was not seen on the entrapment beads. The robustness of the beads
is a necessary element when a system with mechanical stirring is applied or when the beads are
harvested for second use. Due to this reason, the thick shell encapsulation bead was introduced to
see if it will be able to replace the standard encapsulation bead and keep the same performance.
However, when comparing the thick shell beads to the entrapped beads, it can be observed from the
experiments that the entrapped beads have a higher robustness than the other proposed types.
The removal efﬁciency was the second criteria taken under consideration when assessing the
different immobilization techniques. The experimental results revealed that the encapsulation beads
had higher removal efﬁciency compared to the entrapment beads. However, the difference between
the removal efﬁciencies was insigniﬁcant (around 2 % higher for the encapsulation beads than
the entrapment beads). Such a difference cannot be considered as a border line for pointing to a
preferable technique.
The release of NZVI was one of the important criteria, since the main idea was to prevent
NZVI from being free in the environment. It was observed that NZVI were released to the water
from the encapsulation beads due to the rupture of these beads while stirring them in the reactor.
==PAGE:73==
3.6. DISCUSSION
49
Although no release was visible from the entrapment beads, no actual measurements were made to
test whether NZVI were being released.
The preservation of the NZVI reactivity was the last criterion. From the reactivity tests, it
was found that the immobilized NZVI reached the same removal efﬁciency as the free NZVI, how-
ever, in a longer time span. In the same time, the reactivity time of these two application techniques
had a signiﬁcant difference: the immobilized NZVI showed high reactivity compared to the free
NZVI, which could be due to the isolation of the NZVI from the surroundings and prevention from
reacting with the oxygen in water. This is also conﬁrmed by previous articles demonstrating that
the oxygen in water will have a signiﬁcant impact on the removal efﬁciency and the NZVI reactivity.
Another evaluation point that could have been tested intensively, but was not included in the
evaluation, was the bead digestion by the bacteria existing in e.g. waste water. Such a behaviour
can lead to release of NZVI into the environment. Before considering the application of the
immobilized NZVI into reality applications, this aspect needs to be fully examined. The digestion
by bacteria would mainly depend on the immobilization material, why a material like Polyvinyl
alcohol gel could be more beneﬁcial.
From the above ﬁndings and observations, recommendation can be given for the entrapment
beads to be the optimal immobilization technique to be used in further studies and possibly for
reality applications.
The obtained results from this study were compared to results obtained from earlier studies,
presented in Table 2.2. One of the studies stated that the uptake rate for the NZVI can be different
from one supplier to another. This will mean that even if the same conditions had been used for the
experiments conducted in this study the uptake rate would have been different.
A study conducted on NO−

removal efﬁciency in 2-4 hours. The results obtained from the current study showed an uptake of
11 % after 2 hours. Some of the previous studies clearly showed the effect of pH on the results,
where a change in the pH level by a magnitude of 1 can lead to different uptake rates which also
could be one of the reasons why the results are different. However, due to the different providers of
NZVI, the results are incomparable.
Results obtained on the PO3−

removal, however, are appearing to be within the same range
as the results from earlier studies. The results showed that the used NZVI in the current study
was able to remove 94 % of the applied PO3−

3.7
Summary
In this chapter, NZVI were immobilized in encapsulation beads and entrapment beads. The per-
formance of the immobilized NZVI was characterized and compared to the performance of free
NZVI. Experiments conducted for only one contaminant (NO−
3 ) showed that the best removal
efﬁciency was obtained by the standard encapsulated NZVI in beads, closely followed up by the
entrapped NZVI in beads. Since the difference between the efﬁciencies was insigniﬁcant and due
to the low robustness of the encapsulated NZVI beads, the entrapment bead was evaluated as the
most sufﬁcient immobilization technique for NZVI.
The performance of the entrapped NZVI when two contaminants were added to the water
(NO−

in only 1 hour. This observation led to

CHAPTER 4
Upscaling simulations
Engineering problems can be answered by mathematical equations that can be solved either analyt-
ically or numerically. However, analytical solutions are not always applicable; in fact they can only
be applied in certain cases [28].
A majority of these mathematical models are forced to be solved numerically in order to ﬁnd
solutions for complicated sets of equations [28]. The numerical solution depends on transforming
continuous equations into a series of problems. Such a solution is normally solved using computer
software like MATLAB and COMSOL Multiphysics.
COMSOL is applied in different research areas since it has the ability to solve a wide range
of physical and chemical partial differential equations such as the Navier-Stokes equation and the
Poisson’s equation. The numerical method used in this software is FEM as described in section 2.7.
In the following, the efﬁciency of NO−
3 treatment using entrapped NZVI in beads was investi-
gated by a simple COMSOL model.
4.1
Model development
The immobilized NZVI showed high contaminant uptake efﬁciency (up to 99 %) in water in the
laboratory experiments cf. Chapter 3. However, the results from the laboratory experiments did not
provide information regarding the real-life applicability of the immobilized NZVI. The obtained
efﬁciency was from batch experiments with ﬁxed amount of water, while the proposed treatment
system involves continuous ﬂow. By a continuous ﬂow model, the stirring of the beads inside
the reactor can be avoided. It is learned from Chapter 3 that the stirring might harm the beads
causing release of nanoparticles, which can contaminate the water. Today, the continuous ﬂow
treatment is the primary method of treating waste water. Furthermore, the cost per each gallon
treated by continuous ﬂow is far less than batch treatment. Finally, continuous ﬂow water treatment
51

systems can be automated, and can be an effective solution for high-volume applications [59] [60].
The continuous ﬂow setup was simulated by a three dimensional FEM embedded in COMSOL
Multiphysics 5.0 modeling tool box.
4.1.1
Model design
To study the removal efﬁciency of the immobilized NZVI, a simple model was simulated. The
reactor for the water treatment was considered to be a cylinder with a diameter of 1 m and height
of 1 m. The inlet height was 20 cm and had the same diameter as the cylinder. The inlet was placed
on top of the reactor and the center of the inlet was placed on the center of the reactor. The reactor
outlet was the bottom of the reaction cylinder. The ﬂow rate was set at the inlet to 50 litre/hour and
100 litre/hour.
The reason for choosing the inlet diameter to have the same size of the reactor was to minimize
the dead area in the reactor where no water ﬂows. Since the design of an optimal treatment system
was not one of the goals of this study, the applied model was only used to give a preliminary indica-
tion about the amount of water that can be treated and the corresponding amount of immobilized
NZVI required to the treatment.
The ﬂow through the inlet was a free ﬂow since there were no beads present at the inlet. How-
ever, the cylinder was ﬁlled with beads, thus the ﬂow would be governed by the porous medium
equation.
The model was constructed by combining two physics from COMSOL, one for the ﬂow and
the other for the water treatment with beads. The ﬂow model was solved with a steady state solver.
The results from this model were saved and used for the reaction model which was solved with a
time dependent solver [61].

Free and porous media ﬂow simulation
This model used Navier-Stokes equation to describe the ﬂow through the free part of the model

the free ﬂow normal velocity was applied while for the porous medium the continuity of pressure
was considered [63].

For the reaction model a physics titled "transport of diluted species" was used. In this model
the evolved chemical species transported by diffusion and convection were studied. The model

The tank was assumed to be empty at the beginning of the simulation and was afterwards

4.3
Discussion
4.3.1
Limitations of assumptions
The permeability of the beads: This assumption will affect the ﬂow rate since the ﬂow rate has
a direct proportionality with the permeability. This will eventually affect the hydraulic retention
time and the amount of NO−
3 removed by the NZVI. For example, increasing the permeability of
the porous medium will increase the ﬂow through the system leading to lowering the hydraulic
retention time and reducing the removal efﬁciency.
Reactivity approximation: The uptake rate was assumed in equation 4.3 and contains a com-
bined positive and negative error. This approximation can have a great inﬂuence on the amount
of NO−
3 removed by the NZVI and will mainly depend on the actual removal efﬁciency during
the continuous ﬂow mode. If the slope of the slope of the uptake rate was increased, the amount
of treated water would similarly increase and the detention time could be lowered. Thus, higher
water throughput would have been achievable. Changing the model input to information obtained
from continuous ﬂow experiments instead of batch mode experiments would have enhanced the
reliability of the model output.
Model design: The shape of the reactor could also have an inﬂuence on the removal efﬁciency.
Increasing the height of the system would increase the hydraulic retention time which would lead
to higher removal efﬁciency. However, increasing the height would also increase the amount of
immobilized NZVI used in the system. Furthermore, the size of the inlet and the outlet and their
position in the system can have an effect on the results. Introducing inner partition walls will create
a ﬂow labyrinth, increasing the ﬂow path and the hydraulic retention time and leading to an increase
in the removal efﬁciency.
A thorough investigation of the sensitivity of these assumptions is of great importance before
reality applications of a similar water treatment setup.
4.3.2
The model performance
The evaluation of the model performance was based on the evaluation points of section 4.1.5:
Realistic amount of NZVI: An earlier study treated waste water in a similar reactor as pre-
sented in this chapter using 75 kg free NZVI in 1600 L tank in a pilot scale experiment [13]. The
results of the simulation is comparable to the pilot study which indicates applicability of the NZVI
amount suggested in the model.
==PAGE:84==
60
CHAPTER 4. UPSCALING SIMULATIONS
Amount of treated water and contaminant removal efﬁciency: This model was conducted
using NO−
3 as a contaminant example. If the reaction equation that was fed into the simulation
system was for PO3−
4 , the amount of the treated water could be increased since the NZVI showed
a possibility to reach 99 % removal efﬁciency after only 60 minutes. Furthermore, the reactivity
depends on the NZVI type as it was shown in section 2.9. Thus, if the NZVI was obtained from
another supplier with a different reactivity, the amount of treated water would also vary. If this
method was decided to be used for a speciﬁc NZVI type, laboratory experiments have to be con-
ducted to ﬁnd the speciﬁc reaction rate before implementing in the model.
Finally, to evaluate the performance of the model output, the obtained efﬁciency was compared
to the minimal values considered in Directive 91/271/EEC from an urban waste water treatment
plant with conventional treatment of a typical domestic waste water in terms of mass removal.
The overall assessment of the performance can be considered unsatisfactory if the removal of
NO−
3 is between 0-70 %. The removal is considered acceptable if it is between 70-85 %, while it
is considered good between 85-100 % [65]. Thus, the performance obtained by the model after
50 hours is good, since the minimum obtained efﬁciency was 89 %. However, the amount of
treated water in the model is humble compared to actual waste water treatment plants, which
makes it unclear whether the simulated setup is beneﬁcial in reality. Conclusively, when taken
the uncertainties introduced by the assumptions and the very simpliﬁed setup into account, the
simulation has shown good removal performance, which might be an indication for the possibility
of using immobilized NZVI for actual water treatment.
4.4
Summary
In this chapter a simple conceptual model for water treatment using immobilized NZVI in en-
trapment beads was presented. The aim was to conduct a preliminary assessment of the upscaled
experimental data to determine whether immobilized NZVI would be appropriate for reality use.
The model exhibited a contaminant removal efﬁciency of 93 % after 10 hours of operation and 89
% after 50 hours, revealing a indicative good performance. However, the amount of treated water
after 50 hours was only 2500 litres, which suggests low throughput compared to actual treatment
sites. Due to the rough assumptions and simple model setup the results of this model can only be
considered indicative and not conclusive regarding the applicability of entrapped NZVI in alginate
beads for water treatment.

Active ﬁbres for water treatment
with membranes
Naturally, spiders produce in a sophisticated procedure micro-scale ﬁbres by generating a liquid
that hardens when exposed to air [44]. This process has inspired scientists working with polymers
to reuse this technique in producing micro ﬁbres that can be used in different areas. One of the
known ﬁbre types made by this production technique is the alginate ﬁbres.
Alginate ﬁbres are known to have high moisture adsorption along with ion exchange capabilities
[66]. Due to these properties these ﬁbres are used in fabrication of biomedical sensors [44] [45], to
generate artiﬁcial tissues [45] [67] [68] and to encapsulate ﬂavors, enzymes, proteins, drugs and
active components [69] [70] [71] [72].
In this chapter a novel method for treating the contaminated water is presented. The main
idea is to fabricate a membrane that can be used for removal of contaminants in water while
the water is diffusing through the membrane. The membrane itself is comprised of small ﬁbres
containing entrapped NZVI. The membrane can be fabricated manually by designing a special
weaving machine that can handle the ﬁbres gently and also provide a close spacing between the
ﬁbres which cannot be provided by normal weaving machines. The pore size will have impact on
the diffusion rate of the water through the membrane and the amount of contaminants that can be
removed. The membrane pore size can be controlled by the diameter of the ﬁbres and also by the
spacing between the ﬁbres in the weaving machine.
It is essential to understand the effect of immobilizing the NZVI in the ﬁbre structure before
proceeding with the membrane fabrication and testing. In this chapter, ﬁbres that contain different
concentrations of NZVI were fabricated along with NZVI free ﬁbres. The mechanical properties
of these ﬁbres were tested by an AFM. This would give an understanding of the effect of the

5.1
Concept of the active ﬁbre membrane
The concept of the active membrane is to introduce a water puriﬁcation technique that uses a
permeable membrane that does not only ﬁlter but also treat contaminants. The membrane will
reduce the energy used in other similar techniques, i. e., reverse osmosis. The active membrane will
probably require less energy to push the contaminated water through to achieve puriﬁcation than in
reverse osmosis. Reverse osmosis depends on supplying excessive pressure on a semi-permeable
membrane, which will force the water to diffuse through the membrane while the other molecules
(contaminants) will stay on the other side of the membrane.
The presented active ﬁbre membrane is a possible alternative to the reverse osmosis setup
since the treatment will depend on the chemical reaction between the NZVI and the contaminants
ﬂowing through the membrane pores and ﬁbre pores. An illustrative sketch of this idea can be
seen in Figure 5.2. The sketch illustrates how the contaminated water is pushed into a perforated
tube. Active membranes are wrapped around the tube with a separating mesh between them. The
membrane will hold the large impurities on the inner side of the membrane while treating the
dissolved contaminants. Only the treated water is allowed to pass through the membrane.
This active membrane system can have several applications. For instance, it can be used in
drinking water puriﬁcation from groundwater.

5.2.1
Fibre fabrication
Initially, the alginate solution and the CaCl2 were prepared by following the same procedure
presented in section 3.4.1. Then different NZVI concentrations were added to the alginate solution.
The applied concentrations were 0.3 g/L NZVI and 3.3 g/L NZVI in alginate solution.
The ﬁbre fabrication was conducted by a setup consisting of an injection part and a reaction
part. The injection part involved a syringe pump (KR Analytical Ltd) connected to the reaction part
by a tube and a nozzle. The diameter of the nozzle decided the ﬁbre diameter. The two chambers in
this system were ﬁlled with alginate and CaCl2 solution. A sketch of the ﬁbre fabrication setup is
shown in Figure 5.3.
When the alginate mixture was withdrawn into the syringe from the preparation beaker, nor-
mally few small air bubbles would start to appear in the mixture even if the mixture had 30
minutes to release the air into the atmosphere when it was in the beaker. After taking the mixture
by the syringe, it was necessary to ensure that all the air bubbles were pushed out. This could
be done by waiting for 5 to 10 minutes and then push the syringe piston upwards to release the
air. The air bubbles can cause discontinuities in the produced ﬁbre, deformation and loss of strength.

dryness itself changed their mechanical properties, comparing the different dry ﬁbres to each other
might give a relative indication about their elasticity.

5.4
Discussion
The SEM characterization revealed non-uniformities and cavity-like structures on the surface of
the ﬁbres containing NZVI, while the NZVI free ﬁbres showed a smooth surface. Associating this
to the viscoelastic measurements it is observed that the NZVI free ﬁbre or ﬁbre with low NZVI
concentration showed the best elastic properties compared to the NZVI ﬁbres. Thus, it can be
postulated that the NZVI weakens the ﬁbre structure and lowers the elasticity. Furthermore, this
means that the handling of the ﬁbres when fabricating the membrane can be rather challenging.
The treatment efﬁciency using a NZVI concentration of 0.3 g/L needs to be tested in order to ﬁnd
out if it is sufﬁcient for waste water treatment. In addition, the maximum NZVI concentration that
can be used in the ﬁbres without compromising their elasticity needs to be investigated. However,
if this concentration is not sufﬁcient for contaminant puriﬁcation, this challenge can be overcome
by designing other ﬁbre structures. Fibres similar to microtubes where the NZVI content ﬂows in
the middle of the ﬁbre could preserve the elasticity of the structure. Since the fabrication of this
type of ﬁbres require complicated setups it is out of the scope of this study.
5.5
Summary
To assess different immobilization techniques than the entrapment and encapsulation beads often
used in literature, this chapter presented the pre-stages of a ﬁbre-based membrane capable of
degrading contaminants in water. The main concept of the active ﬁbre membrane is to provide
simultaneous ﬁltration and treatment of the water.
In this chapter, active alginate ﬁbres containing NZVI were fabricated and characterized. The
elastic modulus of ﬁbres free of NZVI and ﬁbres containing small (0.3 g/L) and large (3.3 g/L)
concentrations of NZVI was investigated to describe the stiffness of the ﬁbres. It was found that
increasing the NZVI concentration lowers the stiffness of the ﬁbres and makes them more fragile.
SEM characterization of the ﬁbres similarly revealed that deformations and clusters were formed
on the ﬁbres when NZVI were added.
A weaving machine for membrane fabrication was fabricated and successfully tested on dried
ﬁbres.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusions
Treatment of water using zero-valent iron nanoparticles (NZVI) has been a popular topic that in
recent years has experienced a boost in activity. Pollutants, which were hardly removed by standard
technologies, could be effectively removed by NZVI. A main cause behind the high reactivity of
NZVI is their small size. However, whilst their small size gives NZVI their unique characteristics
that are beneﬁcial for decontamination, it is simultaneously a cause of concern with regards to
the risk posed by the NZVI residual in the puriﬁed water. Furthermore, a potential challenge
associated with the application of NZVI is the preservation of the reactivity of the nanoparticles.
Therefore, earlier studies have developed NZVI immobilization techniques to overcome the prob-
lem of nanoparticle release and to preserve the NZVI reactivity.
Despite the boost in NZVI studies, limited knowledge regarding the optimal NZVI immo-
bilization technique is available. In addition, the possibility of applying NZVI for waste water
treatment or drinking water applications has hardly been reported. Thus, the goal of this study was
to experimentally determine the optimal immobilization technique for NZVI. Furthermore, it was
an aim to use the results from the experimental part of this study in a simple simulation model
to achieve an indication about the applicability of immobilized NZVI for real water treatment.
Ultimately, it was a goal to suggest a new innovative immobilization technique for NZVI to be used
for water puriﬁcation.
In the experimental part, NZVI were encapsulated in the core of an alginate bead (encapsulation
beads) and entrapped in a matrix of alginate (entrapment beads). The performance of these two
types of immobilization techniques was compared to the performance of not immobilized (free)
NZVI. Using nitrate as a contaminant model, the uptake was best for the encapsulation beads
with 94 % within 17 hours. However, due to the instability of the encapsulation beads and their
tendency to tore, the entrapment beads were best evaluated, although their contaminant removal
efﬁciency was 92 % within 17 hours. Additionally, the removal efﬁciency was investigated in
the presence of nitrate and phosphate in multi-contaminant experiments. It was found that the

The second experimental part of the study aimed at developing a new NZVI immobilization ap-
proach for water treatment. This was met by incorporating NZVI in alginate ﬁbres. The long-term
intention was to fabricate a membrane using the alginate ﬁbres as threads, hereby creating an active
membrane for water to be pushed through and puriﬁed. Although the membrane was not fabricated,
the examination of the elasticity of the ﬁbres showed that the higher the NZVI concentration the
more fragile the ﬁbres became.
A simple simulation model was introduced to upscale the water treatment with entrapment
beads. The model initially supports the potential application of NZVI for water treatment purposes,
as the obtained efﬁciency of 89 % is within the good performance range deﬁned by the Waste
Water Treatment Directive. However, due to the prematureness of the model, it does not irrevocably
conﬁrm the suitability of this technique for real applications.
6.1
Future directions
Several additional experiments or changes in the experiments presented in this work can be con-
ducted to achieve an enhanced evaluation of the investigated immobilization techniques. The
following sections will elaborate on where it is believed the focus should be.
In the experiments, no release of NZVI was visible from the entrapment beads. However, this
observation needs to be conﬁrmed by means of actual analysis of the water in the batches. In
addition, the pH of the experiments was only initially reduced. Since it has been observed that
the increase of pH has a negative impact on the contaminant removal efﬁciency, the continuous
adjustment of pH to a lower level throughout the experiments could reveal different efﬁciencies
than obtained in this study.
The performance of the beads needs to be investigated under a continuous ﬂow mode to ob-
tain correct uptake rates that could enhance the equations derived in this study. Continuous ﬂow
investigations of the immobilized NZVI performance can constitute a strong tool that can provide
valuable information to upscaling models.
The active ﬁbre needs further development before fabricating the active membrane. The robust-

APPENDIX E
Deployment of NZVI Waste
E.1
General information
Zero-valent iron nanoparticles (NZVI) waste needs to be treated after each experiment before it can
be disposed into a waste ben marked with (X waste + Fe oxide). To treat NZVI waste, a proper
amount of potassium hydrogen sulfate needs to be added into an open bottle and left for a proper
time, both can be calculated or estimated based on the next two sections. The reaction time depends
on the amount of NZVI that is needed to be neutralized and how reactive the NZVI particles are.
This reaction will not develop any heat. The main outcome from the reaction is hydrogen gas which
can evolve rapidly. The reaction bottle needs to be shaken every 5 minutes in order to allow the
acid to reach the agglomerated particles.
E.2
Experimental work
Experiments showed that 1 g of NZVI will need between 330-360 seconds for the acid to neutralize
the NZVI. In addition, the company provider (NANOIRON) for the nano tester kits stated in the
kits’ manual that 5 g of NZVI will require 45 minutes for the reaction to end. Based on these
numbers the hydrogen evolution rate needs to be calculated before wasting NZVI into the waste
barrel. The neutralization process needs to be conducted in a fume hood.
The experiments were monitored for 2 hours in order to ensure that the reaction actually
ended in the ﬁrst 360 seconds. No change in evolving hydrogen volume was noticed after 360
seconds. The amount of hydrogen evolved during the laboratory experiment was 97 ml while in
the calculations (as it is seen below) it was expected to have around 400 ml of hydrogen evolving.
Again, the amount of hydrogen that is evolving from the NZVI will depend on NZVI age and
if it has been used in experiments or subjected to air. All these aspects can affect the NZVI
reactivity and change the amount of hydrogen evolving from the experiment. The NZVI used in the
