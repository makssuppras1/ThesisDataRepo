Abstract
This thesis explores the tensile behaviour and deformation characteristics of 3D­printed
synthetic plant roots intended for geotechnical applications. Synthetic root specimens
were fabricated using resin blends with varying stiffness, and their mechanical response
was examined through tensile testing and direct shear experiments. Optical fibre sensors
were attached to measure surface strain along the root during loading.
The tensile tests indicated that both geometry and resin composition affect the elastic
modulus and failure stress of the material. In the shear box tests, the more flexible roots
tended to show a more distributed strain response and continued accumulating strain after
local slip events, suggesting increased interaction with the surrounding soil. The stiffer
roots, by contrast, exhibited earlier strain drops and more limited deformation, which may
indicate a lower degree of soil mobilisation. Strain patterns were also influenced by the
orientation of the root in the setup and the placement of the fibre, pointing to the presence
of three­dimensional boundary effects.
These findings indicate that material deformability may contribute to improved soil inter­
action, potentially enabling greater mobilisation of surrounding soil.

1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Roots play a vital role in soil stabilization and erosion prevention by reinforcing the soil
and altering its mechanical properties. However, there is still limited understanding of
the full range of effects roots have on soil strength and stability. Studying roots in their
natural environment is challenging due to their inherent variability, fragility, and sensitivity
to environmental conditions.
Additive manufacturing, commonly referred to as 3D printing, offers a promising approach
to synthetically recreate roots. This enables controlled laboratory experiments with syn­
thetic roots designed to mimic the mechanical properties of natural roots. Such synthetic
models allow for systematic investigations of how various factors—such as root geometry,
surface texture, and stiffness—influence root­soil interactions. Using synthetic roots also
facilitates repeatable experiments, overcoming the variability and limitations inherent in
studies relying on natural roots.
While some studies have explored the interaction between 3D­printed root models and
soil, the number of such investigations remains limited. Existing research has primarily
focused on assessing how the presence of root analogues affects soil behaviour [1, 2, 3].
1.2
Problem Statement and Objectives
Natural roots display significant variability in mechanical properties due to differences
in species, age, and environmental conditions, making it challenging to isolate specific
factors that govern their interaction with soil. This variability complicates the systematic
study of root reinforcement mechanisms in geotechnical contexts.
The primary objective of this thesis is to develop and characterize synthetic root analogues
with mechanical properties that approximate those of natural roots. To achieve this, tensile
tests were performed on selected 3D printing materials—polylactic acid (PLA) and two UV­
curable resins (PrimaCreator Value UV Tough and UV Flex)—to evaluate their suitability
for replicating root behaviour.
Using 3D scanning and stereolithography (SLA) printing, root geometries were recreated
and tested under controlled conditions. The synthetic roots were subjected to direct shear
testing in sand, with strain monitored using optical fibre sensors attached to the root sur­
faces. These experiments aimed to capture local strain distributions within the roots dur­
ing shearing, providing insight into how synthetic roots deform and interact with soil under
load.
The workflow included analysing tensile data from natural root tests, selecting appropri­
ate materials for additive manufacturing, and performing tensile tests on printed material
samples. Finally, three direct shear tests were conducted using synthetic root structures
printed with two different materials, to investigate how the root material influences soil–
root interaction under shear. In these tests, an optical fibre sensor was also embedded
along a branch of the root to measure strain during soil deformation.
By focusing on the mechanical characterization of printing materials and the strain re­
sponse of synthetic root structures under shear, this work establishes a foundation for
using additive manufacturing in controlled root­soil interaction studies.

1.3
Scope and Limitations
This thesis focuses on the early stages of development and testing of synthetic root mod­
els, with an emphasis on material characterization and surface friction analysis. The ten­
sile tests on natural roots used for comparison were conducted by Otim et al., using sam­
ples from a single plant species (Salix sp.) at selected ages (30, 60, 90, 150, and 180
days) [4]. These tests serve as a reference for mechanical properties but do not represent
the full variability of mature root systems. Only raw data from these experiments is used
in this thesis; the tests themselves were not carried out by the author.
The synthetic roots do not aim to replicate the full complexity of natural root systems but
serve as standardized models for controlled root­soil interaction experiments. Further­
more, no comprehensive finite element modelling of the root structures within the direct
shear is undertaken. Instead, the focus is placed on simpler strain analyses to establish
baseline data for future research.

2
Theory
The use of vegetation is an effective method to reduce erosion and improve slope stability.
Plant roots increase shear resistance and cohesion by binding the soil together. Slopes
covered with vegetation are often more stable than bare slopes, as root systems reinforce
the soil and help prevent it from being washed away. In addition to these mechanical ef­
fects, vegetation also influences hydrological conditions by increasing infiltration, reducing
surface runoff, and lowering pore water pressure through transpiration [5, 6]. Together,
these mechanical and hydrological effects contribute to the stabilising influence of vege­
tation. As a result, vegetation is commonly used as a natural means of stabilising slopes
and controlling erosion [7].
2.1
Root­Soil Interaction
Plant roots influence the mechanical behaviour of soil, though the nature of this influence
depends on numerous factors. These include root stiffness, geometry, branching patterns,
and surface texture, among others [8]. Roots contribute to the soil’s shear strength and
resistance to erosion. In addition, they absorb water and act as natural reinforcements
within the soil matrix.
The mechanical contributions of roots to soil stability occur primarily in two ways: through
friction between the roots and soil, and through mechanical interlocking with soil particles.
Some studies also suggest that the radial expansion of growing roots may contribute to
increased shear strength by compacting adjacent soil and enhancing contact with soil
particles. Laboratory experiments have confirmed these effects under a variety of stress
conditions [8].
In addition to their mechanical role, roots also influence the soil chemically. They release
organic compounds known as root exudates, which serve multiple functions—many of
which are still not fully understood. One key function is their ability to bind soil particles
together, promoting the formation of larger aggregates. This aggregation improves soil
cohesion and contributes to overall soil strength [9].
Root­soil interaction is also affected by root surface characteristics. Studies have shown
that a rougher root surface increases friction during root pullout, which enhances soil
stability [10]. In some plants, fine structures such as root hairs help seedlings anchor
themselves in the ground. This principle may also be beneficial when designing synthetic
root systems [11].
The structure and geometry of plant roots play a decisive role in determining how they
reinforce soil and resist external loads [12]. Root systems exhibit large variability in both
form and spatial arrangement, which directly influences their mechanical interaction with
the surrounding soil.
Thicker, woody roots act as primary structural elements that anchor the plant and resist
soil movement, whereas finer roots increase the overall surface area in contact with the
soil, enhancing friction and interlocking [12]. The tapering of roots — where root diameter
decreases along the length — is particularly important for effective stress transfer, as it
allows roots to deform gradually while mobilising tensile resistance [2].
Different root types contribute to soil reinforcement in distinct ways. For example, tap­
roots grow vertically downward, penetrating deep soil layers and anchoring the plant [13].

Lateral roots spread horizontally, increasing coverage and binding soil in the upper lay­
ers [14]. Some species develop buttress roots, which extend diagonally or laterally from
the base. These structures increase the plant’s resistance to overturning and provide ad­
ditional stability, particularly in shallow or unstable soils [15]. The spatial orientation of
roots further affects soil mechanical behaviour. Laboratory studies have shown that roots
aligned perpendicular to the direction of shear displacement can act as anchors, resist­
ing soil movement more effectively than roots aligned parallel to the shear plane [16]. In
particular, roots that cross the shear zone vertically have been shown to mobilise tensile
resistance and enhance soil stability, as demonstrated in four­dimensional imaging ex­
periments [17]. Similarly, denser or more branched root networks provide greater contact
with soil particles, enhancing cohesion and reducing susceptibility to erosion [18].
Root systems also commonly grow in complex, three­dimensional networks that bind soil
aggregates together and improve soil structure. Aggregated soils resist erosion and defor­
mation more effectively due to the stabilising influence of interconnected roots [19]. Plant
root growth exhibits considerable plasticity in response to environmental conditions such
as soil moisture, nutrient availability, mechanical impedance, and temperature. Bengough
et al. [12] note that root elongation is commonly limited by a combination of water stress
and mechanical impedance, especially when penetration resistance exceeds 0.8–2 MPa
or matric potential drops below –0.5 MPa. Mechanical impedance may also trigger adap­
tive responses such as increased root diameter and branching, which enhance root–soil
contact and improve anchorage. These structural adjustments are particularly important
in weak or compacted soils [20].
Roots also exhibit directional growth known as tropism, allowing them to respond to lo­
cal environmental gradients. Hydrotropism—the growth toward zones with higher water
potential—is particularly relevant under unsaturated conditions. Dietrich [21] describes
how hydrotropism directs root growth toward moisture­rich areas, thereby increasing root
presence in parts of the soil profile where water availability is highest.
When soil undergoes shear deformation, roots spanning the shear zone experience bend­
ing and buckling, often adopting an S­shaped configuration, as shown in experimental
studies using four­dimensional X­ray computed tomography (4D XCT) and digital volume
correlation (DVC) [17]. These deformations are influenced by structural and spatial char­
acteristics of the roots, including diameter, stiffness, and orientation relative to the shear
zone [17].
Recent modelling efforts treat rooted soil as a composite material, where roots and soil
are considered as distinct, interacting constituents [22]. In such models, root geometry —
including length, diameter distribution, and branching pattern — is a key factor governing
the stress–strain response of the soil–root system, both under serviceability conditions
and at failure. Understanding these geometric aspects is therefore essential for predicting
how root systems contribute to soil reinforcement in natural and engineered environments.
Roots do not interact with the soil in isolation but form symbiotic relationships with mi­
croorganisms, most notably mycorrhizal fungi. These fungi colonize the root surface and
extend into the surrounding soil through fine hyphae, significantly increasing the functional
root area. In this mutualistic relationship, the fungi enhance the plant’s access to water
and nutrients—particularly phosphorus and nitrogen—in exchange for root exudates in
the form of carbohydrates [8].
In addition to nutrient exchange, mycorrhizal fungi contribute to soil structure by promoting
the formation of stable aggregates through physical entanglement of particles and the se­

2.2
Soil Properties Relevant to Soil Interaction
The mechanical interaction between roots and soil also depends on the properties of the
soil itself. Soil is a complex material composed of minerals, organic matter, water, and air.
Its behaviour is influenced by the composition and relative proportions of these compo­
nents. Roots, in turn, can alter the surrounding soil both physically and chemically. Stud­
ies have shown that the soil at the root–soil interface, often referred to as the rhizosphere,
behaves differently from the bulk soil [24]. This is due to several factors, including root
exudation, microbial activity, water uptake, and mechanical disturbance from root growth.
These processes can lead to localised changes in porosity, water content, and particle
rearrangement, which in turn influence soil strength and deformation behaviour near the
root surface.
Soil texture refers to the relative proportions of sand, silt, and clay particles. Sandy soils
are typically coarser, with larger pores and lower cohesion, whereas clay­rich soils are
more cohesive but less permeable. The texture of a soil affects both the ability of roots
to penetrate it and their capacity to provide anchorage. It also influences how the soil
deforms in response to root growth and pressure [1, 25].
Soil structure, which describes how soil particles are arranged into aggregates, plays
an important role in water movement, aeration, and root development [26, 27]. Well­
structured soils with stable aggregates can enhance root proliferation and improve the
mechanical interaction between roots and soil.
The frictional and cohesive properties of soil are strongly influenced by its water content.
Soils with high moisture levels typically exhibit reduced frictional resistance and increased
plasticity, both of which can affect the pullout resistance of roots. In unsaturated soils,
matric suction may also play a role in enhancing root anchorage by increasing effective
stress and apparent cohesion [28].
Soil compaction significantly influences root growth. Highly compacted soils restrict root
penetration, which can limit anchorage and root development. While compaction may
increase the physical contact between roots and surrounding soil, it generally impedes
root elongation and reduces root system effectiveness. Bulk density is commonly used
as an indicator of soil compaction, as it reflects how tightly soil particles are packed [29,
30].
2.3
Root Properties
To better understand how roots influence soil behaviour, synthetic models can be useful
by providing a known and controllable structure for testing. These models must mimic the
geometry, mechanical properties, and interactions of real root systems. This is a com­
plex task, as real roots are sensitive to environmental conditions and exhibit significant
variability.
The goal of creating synthetic roots is not to replicate every biological function, but rather to
focus on the most relevant aspects for geotechnical performance. This includes structural
characteristics that influence root–soil interaction, such as root diameter, surface texture,
and branching patterns. Emphasising these functional components allows synthetic roots
to be tailored for geotechnical applications [31]. Factors like root orientation and insertion
angle also influence mechanical behaviour in soil [32].

The mechanical properties of roots vary depending on several factors, including species,
age, and environmental conditions. As a result, replicating their exact strength and stiff­
ness is challenging. Previous studies have investigated the material behaviour of roots
through laboratory testing. For example, Zhang et al. conducted tests on two types of
mangrove roots: prop roots from Rhizophora stylosa and pneumatophores from Son­
neratia alba [33]. These two root types serve different biological functions—prop roots
provide mechanical support above ground, while pneumatophores primarily facilitate gas
exchange in saturated soils—which helps explain the differences in their mechanical per­
formance.
The Young’s modulus was determined through linear regression within the elastic re­
gion of the stress–strain curve. The prop roots, with an average diameter of approx­
imately 21 mm, exhibited a tensile Young’s modulus of 17.2 ± 3.1 GPa, whereas the
pneumatophores, with a diameter of around 10 mm, showed a much lower modulus of
0.81±0.21 GPa. These results indicate that the structural role of the prop roots is reflected
in their significantly greater stiffness.
In terms of bending strength, the prop roots had an average value of 83±11.9 MPa, while
the pneumatophores exhibited a lower strength of 29.9 ± 4.4 MPa. This again aligns with
their function—prop roots must withstand mechanical loading and environmental forces,
whereas pneumatophores are not load­bearing and have lower structural requirements.

Plant roots play a crucial role in enhancing soil mechanical stability by increasing shear
strength, improving soil structure, and mitigating erosion processes [5, 18, 19]. This soil­
root interaction is often quantified using root reinforcement models, which predict how
roots contribute to slope stability and soil strength [22, 6, 35].
One of the earliest empirical approaches was proposed by Wu et al. [36], introducing
a method to estimate the additional cohesion provided by roots in soil, which remains
fundamental in stability assessments.
More advanced models incorporate micromechanical principles and probabilistic failure
theories to describe how roots fail and transfer loads to the soil. Schwarz et al. [6] applied
a Weibull survival function to account for progressive root failure under tensile loading,
providing improved insight into root bundle behaviour. Similarly, fibre­bundle models with
Monte Carlo simulations have been used to incorporate variability in root strength and
spatial distribution [35].
Experimental studies, including in­situ pullout tests [37, 28, 38], reveal the complex, non­
linear interactions between roots and soil, influenced by factors such as root orientation
[16, 32], soil moisture, and root system architecture [14, 13].
Recent modelling frameworks integrate detailed root architecture with continuum­based
soil mechanics. Meijer et al. [22] proposed a numerical approach that captures stress
transfer from roots to soil, supported by high­resolution imaging techniques such as 4D
X­ray computed tomography [17].
The mechanical contribution of roots depends on parameters such as root stiffness, di­
ameter, and branching patterns [31, 12]. Numerical analyses further show that species­
specific root morphology strongly affects anchorage capacity and resistance to external
loads like wind or earthquakes [1, 34, 3].
Despite significant advances, modelling root­soil interaction remains challenging due to
system heterogeneity [24, 7]. The use of synthetic root analogues and additive manufac­
turing presents new opportunities to experimentally validate and refine root reinforcement
models [2, 39].
In laboratory testing, repeatability and precise control are essential. Natural roots vary
considerably in shape, material properties, and age, and they can only be used once. In
contrast, synthetic roots can be produced with consistent properties, making it easier to
isolate the effects of specific parameters on soil behaviour. Liang et al. highlight how 3D­
printed roots are useful for controlled testing due to the inherent variability of natural roots
[2]. These synthetic models not only allow for variation in root geometry and stiffness, but
also enable systematic testing of different soil conditions, such as compaction or moisture
content.
The main purpose of using synthetic roots is to support research into how roots rein­
force soil. Testing various configurations and orientations provides insight into how roots
contribute to increased shear strength and overall soil stability. This facilitates controlled
experimentation and improves our understanding of root–soil interaction mechanisms.

Optical Fibre Sensing in Root–Soil Interaction Studies
The interaction between plant roots and soil plays a critical role in enhancing soil strength
and stability, yet capturing the complex deformation patterns within root­reinforced soils
remains a challenge. Distributed fibre optic sensing (DFOS) offers a unique approach for
addressing this, by providing continuous, high­resolution strain measurements along the

entire length of an optical fibre embedded within the soil or attached to root structures
[40].
In geotechnical research, DFOS has been applied to monitor deformation in underground
excavations, soil slopes, and laboratory soil models, offering insights into strain locali­
sation, progressive failure mechanisms, and soil–structure interaction under both static
and dynamic conditions [41, 42]. The technology enables strain field mapping with high
spatial resolution, making it particularly suitable for laboratory­scale experiments where
small­scale deformations need to be resolved [43].
Most DFOS systems for strain measurement operate based on Rayleigh backscatter­
ing, where microscopic fluctuations in the fibre’s refractive index scatter a portion of the
transmitted light back towards the source [44]. By analysing the characteristics of this
backscattered signal, it is possible to derive continuous strain profiles along the entire
length of the fibre. Modern optical backscatter reflectometers (OBR) can achieve spatial
sampling intervals below one centimetre and spatial resolutions down to approximately
1 cm under controlled conditions [45]. However, in geotechnical applications, factors
such as soil heterogeneity, installation technique, and environmental noise typically limit
the effective resolution. Nevertheless, the ability to obtain distributed, high­density strain
measurements makes DFOS a promising tool for monitoring complex soil­structure and
soil­root interaction systems.
Although the use of DFOS in root–soil interaction studies is still relatively new, its abil­
ity to non­invasively measure deformations along irregular geometries, such as roots or
bio­inspired models, provides valuable data on how roots influence soil stability. This tech­
nology offers the potential to quantify biological reinforcement effects, investigate failure
mechanisms in root­reinforced soils, and improve understanding of root­soil mechanical
behaviour.

3
Method
This chapter describes the methods used to analyse the mechanical behaviour of both
natural and synthetic root materials. The main focus has been on comparing the material
properties of natural roots to those of 3D­printed materials intended for synthetic root
structures.
3.1
Natural Root Data
To provide a benchmark for evaluating the mechanical properties of the synthetic roots,
tensile tests of natural roots were analysed. The data originate from a previous experiment
conducted by Otim et al., in which roots of Salix species were grown under controlled
conditions and tested at various ages: 30, 60, 90, 150, and 180 days [4].
The roots were grown in pots filled with fine sand (grain size 0.3–0.5 mm) using a pluviation
technique to ensure consistent packing. Only the primary roots were used for testing.
These were trimmed to approximately 100 mm in length and prepared by applying hot
glue at both ends, enabling a secure grip during testing and resulting in an effective test
length of 50 mm. The root diameters were typically around 1 mm, and the variation along
the root length was accounted for by averaging diameter measurements taken at multiple
points using a digital microscope. The tensile tests were conducted at a constant rate of
10 mm/min until failure [4].
For this study, raw tensile test data from a selection of these natural roots were obtained
and re­analysed. The analysis focused on tensile strength, strain at failure and elastic
modulus. This provides a basis for comparing natural and synthetic root performance.
To reduce noise in the data, a one­dimensional Gaussian filter was applied to the deriva­
tive of the stress. A sigma value of 10 was chosen to smooth out local fluctuations without
removing overall trends, as the data points were closely spaced. This filtering improved
the reliability of calculated parameters such as tensile strength, strain at failure, and elastic
modulus.
3.2
3D Printing
Additive manufacturing, commonly known as 3D printing, is a layer­by­layer manufactur­
ing technique in which the geometry is defined by a digital model. This contrasts with
traditional subtractive methods, where material is removed from a solid block. Among
the most widely used 3D printing technologies are Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM) and
Stereolithography (SLA), which were both considered in this study.
3.2.1
Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM)
FDM is a commonly used 3D printing method that extrudes thermoplastic materials, such
as polylactic acid (PLA), through a heated nozzle. The material is deposited in thin strands
to build up each layer of the model. This method is accessible, cost­effective, and offers
relatively fast print times, making it suitable for producing moderately complex geometries
[46].
However, FDM has several limitations. The resulting prints often exhibit a visible layer
pattern and a rough surface finish, which may affect both aesthetics and mechanical per­
formance. Mechanical properties such as tensile strength are influenced by factors in­
cluding layer height, infill density, and print orientation. In particular, the orientation of the
print can significantly affect tensile performance [47].

3.2.2
Stereolithography (SLA)
SLA is a printing technique that uses ultraviolet (UV) light to cure liquid resin layer by
layer. This method enables finer details and smoother surfaces compared to FDM, and is
therefore particularly suited for replicating complex natural geometries such as root struc­
tures. In addition, specially formulated resins have been shown to mimic some biological
root functions, such as water absorption [49].
As with FDM, the mechanical properties of SLA prints depend on both the material and
the post­processing procedure. Typical post­processing includes cleaning in solvent (e.g.
isopropanol) and additional UV curing to ensure complete polymerisation [50]. Some
resins are specifically formulated to enhance flexibility or impact resistance, which can
improve the biomimetic performance of synthetic roots.
3.2.3
Material Selection and Comparison
The similarity between synthetic and natural roots depends heavily on both material choice
and printing parameters. While both FDM and SLA offer a variety of materials, SLA gen­
erally provides higher detail resolution and broader material customisation. FDM, on the
other hand, is typically faster, cheaper, and more sustainable [39]. Both technologies face
challenges when printing long and thin geometries such as root structures.

3.3
Material Testing
To evaluate the mechanical properties of the 3D­printed materials, a series of tensile tests
were conducted. The purpose was to compare the results with tensile properties of natural
roots and to select suitable materials for use in synthetic root structures.
3.3.1
Material and Geometry Selection
Natural roots are characterised by relatively low tensile strength and high variability in both
strength and strain at failure. In contrast, 3D­printed materials typically exhibit higher
strength and more consistent mechanical properties. Achieving comparable properties
to natural roots using commercially available materials is therefore challenging, unless
custom materials are developed.
To approximate the mechanical behaviour of natural roots, two commercially available
resins from PrimaCreator were selected for stereolithography (SLA) printing: UV Value
Tough and UV Value Flex. These resins were chosen for their contrasting mechanical
behaviours and their compatibility for mixing in controlled proportions, allowing for tuning
of material properties to better match those of biological roots.
SLA is a vat photopolymerization method in which ultraviolet (UV) light is used to se­
lectively cure liquid resin into solid parts. SLA­printed materials are known for their high
geometric precision and smooth surface finish. However, they typically exhibit more brittle
failure behaviour compared to thermoplastics, and their mechanical properties are sensi­
tive to formulation, post­curing, and print parameters [50].
The Tough resin is formulated to provide improved impact resistance and load­bearing
capacity. According to manufacturer data, it has a tensile modulus ranging from 1.86 to
2.65 GPa and an elongation at break between 11–21%, along with a Shore D hardness
of 76–80 and notched impact strength of 60–80 J/m [51]. In contrast, the Flex resin offers
greater ductility, with a tensile modulus between 1.78 and 2.39 GPa and an elongation
at break of up to 130%, along with lower hardness (65–75 Shore D) and impact strength
(41–48 J/m) [52].

However, this geometry often failed near the clamping area due to stress concentrations,
making the results unreliable. To improve test validity, a new specimen geometry was

3.3.2
Printing Challenges and Test Limitations
Several challenges were encountered during the printing and testing process. The PLA
specimens printed using FDM often showed failure near the mounting points, and due
to the anisotropic nature of FDM prints, material inconsistencies occurred, especially in
thinner regions. The printer struggled to deposit material uniformly in the narrow sections
of the test specimens, resulting in small voids and weak points. These inconsistencies
significantly affected the reliability of the test data.
Additionally, removing support material from the thin PLA specimens without damaging
them was difficult. As a result, this geometry proved unsuitable for accurate tensile testing
using FDM.
To evaluate the feasibility of using PLA for printing thin root­like structures, a test print was
examined under a microscope. As shown in fig. 3.2, the print exhibited clear layer lines
and poor resolution in fine geometries. The layer­by­layer nature of FDM printing results
in anisotropic and highly heterogeneous material properties, which make mechanical per­
formance less predictable—especially in slender structures where small imperfections are
critical. Due to these limitations in geometric accuracy and material homogeneity, PLA
and FDM were deemed unsuitable for producing realistic root analogues, and further test­
ing was conducted exclusively with SLA­printed specimens.

To better understand how roots influence the shear strength of soil, it is important to study

different scanned roots. One root is positioned in the center, while the other was copied 12
times and arranged symmetrically in a circle around it. The goal was not to replicate a fully
realistic root system, but rather to have a well­defined structure to observe how changes
in material properties affect the test results. The root structure tested in the shear box is
shown in fig. 3.5.

in the materials used for printing and in the placement of the optical fibre sensor. In two
of the tests, the fibre was attached along a branch oriented facing forward in the shear
direction, while in the third test the fibre was placed on a side branch, perpendicular to the
shear direction. All roots were positioned vertically in the sand with identical placement
around the shear box.

Interpretation and Summary
The results show that both tensile strength and stiffness tend to increase with root age,
while strain at failure appears largely unaffected by either age or diameter. No strong
or consistent relationships were found between mechanical properties and root diameter
across the dataset, even when controlling for age.
For a root with a diameter of approximately 1 mm and an age of 180 days—the oldest

roots in the test series—the measured Young’s modulus was around 80 MPa, and the
maximum tensile strength was approximately 6 MPa. These values are based on Salix
species grown under controlled conditions. In the literature, however, considerably higher
values have been reported for roots of other species, ages, and growing environments.
For example, Zhu et al. [3] reported a Young’s modulus of around 3240 MPa and a tensile

Altogether, these findings underline the complexity of root material behaviour and the
importance of species, age, and test methodology. The variability observed here and
in the literature should be taken into account when modelling root–soil interactions or
designing synthetic analogues.

4.2.5
Test Shape and Material
The small test shapes used in this study, with diameters between 0.8 and 1.2 mm, were
chosen to approximate the size of the printed roots used in later experiments. This allowed
for a realistic indication of how printed roots of similar dimensions would behave mechan­
ically. However, printing at such small scales reduced geometric precision. In particular,
the middle section of the specimens tended to become more elliptical than circular, which
resulted in a larger cross­sectional area than intended. On average, the smallest shapes
had about 40% larger cross­sectional area than designed, while the 1 mm and 1.2 mm
shapes had areas approximately 33% and 35% larger, respectively.
The cross­sectional area was calculated assuming an elliptical shape, based on the av­
erage of three diameter measurements along each axis. While this method provided a
practical approximation, the deviation from a perfect ellipse introduced some uncertainty.
Because the area depends on the square of the diameter, small errors in measurement
had a disproportionate impact on the calculated stress and modulus values. Although not
ideal, this approach yielded a reasonable estimate of the mechanical properties of printed
parts at this scale.
To obtain more reliable material data, standardised tensile tests were also performed on
the 50/50 Tough/Flex mix. These tests revealed clear differences compared to the smaller
printed specimens, highlighting the influence of specimen geometry on mechanical per­
formance. The standardised specimens showed higher stiffness, strength, and strain at
failure, likely due to their more consistent dimensions and reduced sensitivity to geometric
imperfections.
Material selection was limited by the availability of resins suitable for SLA printing. Pure
Flex resin was excluded due to its very high elongation, while pure Tough resin was con­
sidered too stiff and brittle for the application.
The 50/50 mix offered a compromise
between these extremes and yielded mechanical properties within the range of natu­
ral roots tested in this study, though still below values reported in the literature (e.g.,
σmax = 57.89 MPa and E = 3240 MPa in [3]). In particular, the strain at failure of the small
50/50 specimens was comparable to the values measured for natural roots in this study,
while the standardised specimens generally showed higher values.
Interestingly, the 100% Tough resin showed significantly higher stiffness than the natu­
ral roots analysed in the previous section. Its Young’s modulus and strength were both
markedly greater than the values observed in those tests, suggesting that the material is
considerably stiffer than typical root tissue under similar conditions. However, literature
sources report a wide range of values for natural roots, and some root systems may ex­
hibit comparable or even higher stiffness depending on species, age, and environmental
conditions [3].
It is also important to note that the 3D­printed specimens are made from a homogeneous
polymer material, while real roots are complex biological structures composed of longitu­
dinal fibres bound together. This anisotropy means that a natural root may exhibit different
stiffness in tension and bending, as fibres can carry axial load effectively but offer less re­
sistance to transverse deformation. In contrast, the printed material is expected to show
similar mechanical behaviour regardless of the loading direction. Since only tensile tests
were performed in this study, no conclusion can be drawn regarding bending behaviour.
Nonetheless, this difference in structure should be considered when interpreting the me­

In all tests, the cross­sectional area used for stress calculations excluded the optic fibre,

The results presented in this section demonstrate how the mechanical behaviour of syn­

the proportion of Flex resin systematically decreased both strength and stiffness while
increasing ductility, enabling control over the material response depending on applica­

highlighting the importance of using consistent and well­defined shapes when comparing

Although the mechanical properties of the synthetic materials did not fully match those
reported for natural roots in the literature, several combinations—particularly the 50/50

4.3
Direct Shear Tests
This section presents the results from the direct shear tests conducted on synthetic roots
instrumented with optical fibre sensors. The primary aim was to investigate how the pres­
ence of synthetic roots influences the mechanical response of sand under shear, and
how the roots themselves deform during loading. Two materials were used for the roots:
PrimaCreator Tough and a 50/50 blend of Tough and Flex.
Strain measurements along the roots were captured using optical fibres glued to the root
surface. These fibres measure strain along its length, relative to a baseline measurement.
These measurements are susceptible to noise—particularly in zones of friction and pres­
sure from sand particles. For the first test (50/50 Tough/Flex), measurements were saved
from each fibre reading during the test. In subsequent tests, an improvement learned from
the first test by continuously monitoring was implemented, only readings with acceptable
noise levels were manually saved, resulting in approximately 60 valid measurements per
test. The saving process is time­consuming due to the fibre optic software.

These observations underscore the role of local 3D interactions between root geometry
and soil, and they highlight that even small differences in orientation can influence strain
transfer and deformation response.

bio­inspired soil reinforcement, where materials that accommodate movement may offer

Conclusion
This thesis explored the mechanical behaviour of synthetic roots made from different resin
blends, with a particular focus on how material stiffness influences deformation and inter­
action with surrounding soil under shear loading. Tensile tests and direct shear experi­
ments revealed that the material composition of the roots affect their ability to engage with
the soil.
The results demonstrate that softer, more flexible materials exhibit more distributed strain
development and allow for greater deformation without failure. These roots mobilised a
larger volume of surrounding soil, which was reflected in higher peak shear stresses and
continued strain accumulation even after local slip events. In contrast, stiffer roots resisted
deformation more locally and showed reduced engagement with the soil after early strain
drops.
The findings highlight that material stiffness is not the sole determinant of root performance
in reinforcing soil. Rather, deformability and the capacity to adapt to loading appear to play
a central role in promoting soil stabilisation. Additionally, the orientation of the root within
the soil, and the three­dimensional interaction between root geometry and the surrounding
sand, were shown to influence strain transfer and deformation response. These spatial
effects, while subtle, underscore the need for careful consideration of both material and
placement in future designs.
As this research represents an early­stage investigation, several limitations should be
noted. The number of tests per configuration was limited, and the use of a single fibre
placement per test restricts the ability to generalise findings. Furthermore, the testing
setup simplifies the complexity of natural soils and root architectures.
Future work should include a broader set of experiments with increased replication, more
detailed 3D fibre placement strategies, and potentially tests in more realistic soil envi­
ronments. Investigating how branching patterns and fibre­reinforced geometries affect
mobilisation may also offer valuable insights for bio­inspired root design. In the context
of geotechnical applications, understanding the balance between stiffness, deformability,
and soil mobilisation will be key in designing effective synthetic root systems for erosion
control and slope stabilisation.
