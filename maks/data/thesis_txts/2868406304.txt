Abstract
The global crisis of antimicrobial resistance (AMR) has created a
need for innovative therapeutic strategies that go beyond traditional
antibiotics. This thesis investigates protein-based therapeutics targeting
Enterotoxigenic Escherichia coli, a major cause of gastrointestinal
infections.
Specifically,
the
work
focuses
on
neutralizing
the
adhesion mechanism mediated by the CfaE adhesin, a virulence factor
involved in bacterial colonization.
Two approaches were explored:
single-domain antibodies (sdAbs) derived from natural immune systems
and computationally designed minibinders.
The sdAbs demonstrated
robust binding affinities. Minibinders, designed de novo using advanced
computational tools such as RFdiffusion, ProteinMPNN, and AlphaFold2,
were developed to offer structural simplicity and enhanced stability.
However, due to challenges in experimental validation, the binding
affinity of these minibinders could not be conclusively determined,
preventing a direct comparison to sdAbs. Additionally, despite extensive
troubleshooting, the consensus CfaE construct could not be successfully
expressed and purified.
The case of gastrointestinal infections is
particularly relevant when developing alternative pharmaceutical solutions
to antibiotics, as the commensal microbiome is greatly impacted by
antibiotic use.
Even when antibiotics are administered for infections
outside the gastrointestinal tract, oral antibiotic intake disrupts the
gut microbiota, leading to adverse health complications.
By targeting
bacterial adhesion rather than bacterial survival, neutralizing therapeutics
such as sdAbs and minibinders may offer a microbiome-preserving
alternative that reduces selective pressure for resistance while preventing
infection.
Despite the technical challenges encountered, this work
contributes to the foundation of non-antibiotic therapeutic strategies against
bacterial pathogens. By advancing the design and application of targeted
protein-based therapies, this research contributes to the development of
sustainable solutions for AMR and reinforces the importance of preserving
gut microbiome health in the fight against bacterial infections.

Introduction
For nearly a century, bacterial infections have been treated using antibiotics,
a practice that began with the accidental discovery of penicillin by
Alexander Fleming [1]. Penicillin, a compound produced by mold, became
the first widely used antibiotic, revolutionizing the treatment of bacterial
diseases. Since then, numerous antibiotics with varying mechanisms of
action have been developed.
These drugs work by targeting essential
bacterial processes, such as cell wall synthesis or protein production,
to either kill the bacteria or inhibit their growth [2].
The impact was
immediate, with antibiotics drastically reducing mortality rates from
bacterial infections. Although antibiotics has saved man lives, there are
complications asssociated with their wide-spread use, and therefore a need
for alternative solutions. This thesis investigates not only the optimization
of sdAbs targeting Enterotoxigenic Escherichia coli (ETEC) adhesion
but also explores how computationally designed minibinders, with their
smaller size and simpler structure, may complement these sdAbs. Together,
these technologies could offer a synergistic toolkit for addressing both
existing and emerging challenges in AMR
1.1
The AMR crisis
Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) refers to the ability of microorganisms,
to resist the effects of drugs that once killed or inhibited them.
The
phenomenon of AMR is not new but has accelerated dramatically in
recent decades due to the widespread and often inappropriate use of
antibiotics, in medicine and agriculture, has placed selective pressure
on bacteria, encouraging the development of resistance.
In the 1940s,
penicillin resistance in Staphylococcus aureus emerged, marking one of
the first indications that resistance could undermine the effectiveness of
these drugs [3]. Since more antibiotics were introduced, but resistance has
subsequently followed, leading to the rise of multi-drug resistant strains and

the ongoing AMR crisis we face today. The impact of AMR is alarming,
with WHO identifying it as one of the top 10 global public health threats[4].
In 2019, bacterial AMR was associated with 4.95 million deaths worldwide,
with over 1 million deaths directly attributed to drug-resistant infections
[5]. Similarly, in 2021, AMR was linked to 4.71 million deaths, including
1.14 million directly attributable to resistant bacterial infections. Without
decisive action, projections estimate that AMR could result in 39 million
deaths over the next 25 years, equating to more than three deaths every

in older populations, where the burden has increased significantly over the
past three decades. The global Disability-Adjusted Life Years (DALYs)
attributable to AMR in 2021 were approximately 46.5 million, highlighting
the profound impact of resistant infections on both mortality and long-term
health outcomes. Forecasts suggest that by 2050, deaths attributable to
AMR could rise to 1.91 million annually, with associated deaths reaching
8.22 million, if no significant interventions are made [5, 6, 7].
This
increase is driven by the growing number of resistant infections that cannot
be treated with existing antibiotics. Common pathogens such as E. coli,
Streptococcus pneumoniae, and Mycobacterium tuberculosis are evolving
resistance to first-line antibiotics, complicating treatment regimens and
resulting in longer hospital stays and higher healthcare costs [4].
The
World Bank report on AMR outlines the potential economic devastation
if AMR remains unchecked.
It predicts a global GDP decline of up
to 3.8% by 2050 in a high-impact scenario, equivalent to an annual
shortfall of $3.4 trillion by 2030 [8].
Healthcare costs are expected

see a 25% increase by 2050[8]. Livestock production in these regions
may also decline by up to 11%, compounding food security challenges.
AMR could force 24 million additional people into extreme poverty by
2030, predominantly in low-income nations, further exacerbating global

In addition, many

and complications.

therapeutic applications, including serving as an alternative to antibiotics

Antibiotic’s effects on gastrointestinal health
The widespread use of antibiotics,
particularly for GI infections
caused by Escherichia coli, has significant limitations and unintended
consequences[12, 13, 14]. GI infections are a major global health issue,
with E. coli being one of the leading pathogens responsible for bacterial
diarrheal diseases[15].
According to the World Health Organization
(WHO), diarrheal diseases are the second leading cause of death among
children under five, accounting for approximately 9% of all child deaths[16,
17]. This translates to over 500,000 deaths annually, with the highest rates
observed in low-income regions such as sub-Saharan Africa and South
Asia, where access to clean water, proper sanitation, and healthcare is
limited [17]. In these areas, E. coli infections are prevalent, exacerbating
the burden on already strained healthcare systems.
E. coli is not only
a significant cause of childhood diarrhea but also a leading pathogen
in travelers’ diarrhea, affecting millions of people visiting developing
countries every year[14]. Travelers’ diarrhea is the most common illness
affecting international travelers, with an estimated 20-50% of travelers
to high-risk regions (such as parts of Africa, Latin America, and South
Asia) experiencing this condition. This translates to approximately 10-20
million cases annually worldwide. While the majority of cases are mild
and resolve within a few days, some can be severe, causing dehydration
and necessitating medical intervention[18]. The most common pathogens
include ETEC, Shigella spp., Salmonella spp., and various viruses and

1.3
Novel approaches and trends
Pharmaceutical development has undergone a significant paradigm shift,
transitioning from a primary focus on small molecule drugs that act as
agonists or antagonists of specific receptors to protein-based therapeutics
that emphasize the interplay between biological molecules [32]. Small
molecules, while effective in many contexts, often face challenges
such as off-target effects, rapid clearance, and toxicity.
In contrast,
proteins and peptides offer unique advantages, including high specificity,
biocompatibility, and the ability to form complex interactions. Advances
in recombinant DNA technology and molecular design have enabled the
creation of protein-based materials with tailored properties for therapeutic
delivery. These innovations have opened the door to designing therapies
that not only block or activate pathways but also modulate multi-protein
networks, creating opportunities for interventions in diseases previously
considered untreatable [32].
Market trends reflect this transition, with
peptide- and protein-based drugs emerging as a rapidly growing segment.
While still representing a smaller share of the overall pharmaceutical
market, the approval rate of peptide drugs is approximately double that
of small molecules[33].
This shift reflects a broader understanding of
biological complexity and a move toward therapies that integrate new
strategies and opportunities in treating diseases.
Protein-based drugs,
despite their therapeutic potential, are notoriously difficult to administer
orally due to the body’s natural mechanisms for digesting proteins. This
limitation has confined the use of protein-based therapeutics to intravenous
delivery, which presents challenges in accessibility, patient compliance,
and cost.
Recent efforts have sought to overcome these barriers.
For

In recent years, treatment strategies have increasingly focused on
neutralization as a mode of action, particularly in the development of
biotherapeutics. Neutralization as a therapeutic strategy revolves around
the ability to specifically bind and inactivate harmful molecules, such
as toxins, enzymes, or virulence factors, without directly targeting the
pathogen itself.
This approach contrasts with traditional antimicrobial
treatments, which often aim to kill pathogens or inhibit their growth,
potentially disrupting host systems and beneficial microbes. Neutralizing
agents, including antibodies and designed proteins, work by directly
interfering with the activity of a target molecule—blocking its interaction
with host cells, disrupting its active site.
The goal is to eliminate the
pathogenic effects at their source, thereby reducing disease severity [37].
Recent studies have demonstrated the potential of rationally designed
proteins for neutralizing highly potent toxins, such as those found in
snake venom, reflecting the versatility of this approach [38]. A notable
advantage of neutralizing strategies is their high specificity, which allows
for therapeutic precision while minimizing adverse effects. However, this
specificity, while beneficial for reducing toxicity, can also limit the range of
their applicability. As a result, designing and selecting neutralizing protein
structures requires a delicate balance between achieving selectivity and
maintaining versatility. For example, broadly neutralizing antibodies are
designed to bind specific epitopes while also accommodating structural
diversity across related toxins or antigens, enabling them to remain
effective against a range of variants [37]. This specific approach leverages

advances in technologies like phage display as a form of high-throughput
screening, allowing researchers to identify and optimize molecules that
neutralize the pathogenic elements of interest.
In addition to their role and potential in neutralizing toxins and pathogens,
antibodies have become integral to modern medicine, with applications
spanning diverse therapeutic areas. Their ability to bind specific targets
with high affinity has been harnessed in cancer immunotherapy, where
antibodies such as immune checkpoint inhibitors block proteins like PD-1
or CTLA-4, enhancing the immune system’s capacity to recognize and
attack tumors [39, 40].
Similarly, monoclonal antibodies are used to
neutralize inflammatory cytokines in autoimmune diseases or to block
viral proteins in infectious diseases[41].
These developments illustrate
the versatility of antibodies as neutralizing agents and their potential to
address a broad range of clinical challenges. Despite their widespread
success, the use of antibodies in therapy is not without challenges. One
significant limitation is their structural complexity, which contributes
to difficulties in production and purification [42].
Additionally, the
large size of antibodies can hinder their ability to penetrate tissues
effectively, limiting their efficacy in certain therapeutic contexts, such as
targeting antigens within dense tumor microenvironments [42]. Stability
is another concern, as full-length antibodies are prone to degradation
under harsh conditions, complicating storage and transport, particularly
in resource-limited settings[40].
Combined, these factors contribute to
the high cost of antibody-based therapies, which can limit accessibility
and widespread adoption. Addressing these challenges requires innovative
approaches that simplify design and production while maintaining or
enhancing therapeutic efficacy.

Single-domain antibodies
One
promising
class
of
molecules
addressing
the
limitations
of
conventional antibody therapies is single-domain antibodies (sdAbs).
These compact, single-chain proteins—derived from the heavy-chain-only
8
Designing protein structures for neutralizing CfaE in E.coli for treating GI infections
==PAGE:16==
antibodies naturally found in camelids—exhibit high specificity and
binding affinity comparable to full-sized monoclonal antibodies, while
offering several distinct advantages.
With a molecular weight of
approximately 15 kDa, sdAbs are significantly smaller than traditional
antibodies, enabling enhanced tissue penetration and access to antigenic
sites that are often inaccessible to larger antibody molecules [43, 44].
This structural simplicity facilitates their expression in microbial systems,
reducing manufacturing costs and streamlining purification—a typical
bottleneck in the production of conventional antibodies.
Another
defining feature of sdAbs is their exceptional stability and resistance
to proteolytic degradation [36].
Unlike full-sized antibodies, which
often require refrigeration and careful handling to maintain functionality,
sdAbs retain their activity across a broader range of temperatures and
environmental conditions. This robustness is particularly advantageous in
resource-limited settings, where cold-chain storage is impractical, and in
oral formulations, where resistance to digestive enzymes is essential[36,
45].
As their name implies, single-domain antibodies consist solely of the
variable domain of an antibody, lacking the constant Fc region present
in conventional antibodies.
The absence of the Fc region eliminates
effector functions such as antibody-dependent cellular cytotoxicity,
antibody-dependent cellular phagocytosis, and complement activation,
which are typically mediated through interactions with Fc receptors
on immune cells.
Instead, the neutralizing capacity of sdAbs relies
exclusively on their ability to bind with high specificity to target antigens,
thereby blocking critical interactions or enzymatic activities. By directly
occupying functional epitopes, sdAbs can inhibit pathogen entry, toxin
activity, or receptor-ligand interactions essential for disease progression
[46]. Although sdAbs do not directly activate immune responses, their
therapeutic potential can be enhanced through the design of multivalent
constructs. Bivalent or multivalent sdAbs can simultaneously bind multiple

9
==PAGE:17==
targets, facilitating target aggregation and increasing the likelihood of
immune cell recognition and clearance [47].
This mechanism mimics
natural immune processes, where clustered antigens are more effectively
identified and eliminated by the immune system.
By combining their
structural simplicity, exceptional stability, and adaptable mechanisms of
action, sdAbs represent a versatile platform for developing next-generation
therapeutics across a broad spectrum of diseases.

a single construct.
1.5
Consensus designs
Consensus design is a rational approach to creating synthetic proteins that
encapsulate the most conserved and functionally critical elements across
a family of homologous proteins. Multiple sequence alignment has been
performed on the CfaE adhesins from different ETEC strains to identify
conserved regions critical for structural stability and receptor binding.
Then a synthetic ”consensus” sequence is generated, incorporating these
conserved residues while excluding rare or strain-specific variations. This
sequence aims to preserve the overall structure and function of CfaE,

conserved structural and functional features, consensus design reduces
the need to develop separate neutralizing proteins for each CfaE isoform.
This streamlines the development process and increases the likelihood
of creating a single therapeutic capable of broad-spectrum neutralization.
The method of obtaining the design itself of a consensus protein, relies
heavily on computational tools trained for these purposes. Advancements
in this computational protein-biology field as a whole, has also created new
opportunities for novel approaches to protein designs [49]. For this project,
neutralizing proteins designed through these computational methods are of
great interest and are further investigated for their potential in application
compared to sdAbs.
1.6
Minibinder designs
Apart from sdAbs, of which a few promising candidates have already
been identified for CfaE, another type of small neutralizing protein gaining
traction in therapeutic research is what are known as binder designs.
The term “binder” reflects both their function—binding specifically to a
target—and the innovative computational methods used in their creation.
Binder designs are proteins engineered to interact with target molecules,
and their development relies heavily on advancements in computational
biology. These tools have evolved rapidly in recent years, exemplified
by the Nobel Prize in Chemistry awarded last year to David Baker, who
advanced AI-driven methods for designing de novo proteins[50]. Among
binder designs, minibinders represent a particularly exciting category.
These are smaller versions of binders, typically ranging from 30–65 amino
acids in size, as opposed to the larger 100–150 amino acids found in
conventional binders[51, 52, 53, 54, 55]. While there is no strict definition
or size cut-off for minibinders, their reduced size gives them unique
advantages in stability, design simplicity, and therapeutic application.
Binder designs are synthetic proteins created using computational tools
capable of predicting their structure, stability, and interactions with other

13
==PAGE:21==
molecules. Unlike naturally occurring antibodies or sdAbs, binders are
designed de novo, meaning they are not limited by the strategies or
constraints of natural evolutionary processes.
The design of sdAbs is
an outcome of the immune system’s evolutionarily developed ability
to generate highly specific binding molecules.
This process relies on
the diversity and adaptability of the variable (V) domain, achieved
through mechanisms like somatic recombination and hypermutation[56].
During B cell development, V (variable), D (diversity), and J (joining)
gene segments are randomly recombined, generating a vast array of unique
sequences[57]. Diversity also arises from nucleotide insertions or deletions
at the junctions of these segments. Upon antigen exposure, antibodies
undergo further refinement through somatic hypermutation and affinity
maturation, ensuring that only those with the highest binding affinities
are selected and expanded[56]. These evolutionary strategies allow the
immune system to create and ’design’ sdAbs capable of recognizing an
immense variety of antigens, reflecting the remarkable precision and
adaptability of natural processes.
The philosophy behind de novo design is to create proteins optimized
solely for their intended function - binding a specific target - without being
restricted to structures found in nature. This freedom enables researchers
to explore entirely new configurations that nature may never produce. A
more detailed description of the computational tools used in this project
can be found in Appendix A. Why is there a need to create something
nature cannot?
Natural methods like phage display already allow for
the discovery of sdAbs capable of binding almost any target of interest.
However, just as evolution produces organisms optimized for survival
within specific constraints, it rarely generates structures optimized for
non-natural purposes.
Computational tools can break free from these
constraints, designing structures that perform functions beyond what
nature could evolve.

Minibinders, offer several distinct advantages that stem from their
smaller size and simpler structure.
Their compact design inherently
improves stability, as smaller proteins are less prone to misfolding or
aggregation. This makes minibinders particularly well-suited for use in
harsh environments, such as the gastrointestinal tract, where they can
maintain activity despite exposure to extreme conditions. The simplicity
of minibinders also facilitates their design and production.
Smaller
structures allow for more accurate computational predictions of folding
and interactions, simplifying and streamlining the development process.
Their reduced complexity also makes them easier to express and purify
in biological systems, minimizing the risks of complications during
production. Based on these attributes, using minibinders could potentially
reduce the cost and time associated with their development.
Another
notable benefit of minibinders is their ability to access targets that may
be inaccessible to larger molecules.
Their small size enables them to
penetrate dense tissues and traverse biological barriers, expanding their
applicability to therapeutic contexts where larger proteins might struggle
to reach their targets. Although this attribute is not particularly relevant
for this project in particular - it is one to keep in mind for other applications.
Other than the practical and economic advantages afforded by their
smaller size and simplicity, minibinders offer another key benefit:
a
greater proportion of their structure is dedicated to the binding site
compared to larger binder designs or even sdAbs. This increased binding
site-to-structure ratio allows minibinders to maximize their functional
interactions with target epitopes, while minimizing the risks of other parts
of its’ structure to unintentionally act as a binding site for unwanted targets.
SdAbs, although highly effective, are products of an evolutionary strategy
refined over millions of years as part of the immune system. This strategy
includes built-in safety mechanisms to minimize off-target effects and
cross-reactivity[58, 59], although it has not been established wether they

are completely unable to bind small molecules[60].
For instance, the
structural regions of sdAbs that are not directly involved in antigen binding
are evolutionarily designed and compacted in order to avoid interactions
with unintended targets[59]. These safety measures help prevent off-target
binding, reducing the risk of toxicity or immune system misdirection. In
contrast, binder designs, created through computational de novo methods,
lack these evolutionary safeguards.
As their structures are inherently
random, regions of the protein distal from the designed binding site presents
a concern for potential off-target binding when evaluating the toxicity and
safety of binder designs for therapeutic use[52].
The reduced size of
minibinders inherently mitigates this risk. With fewer distal regions outside
the binding site, the likelihood of unintended interactions decreases. This
structural simplicity, combined with a minimized risk of cross-reactivity,
Is the rationale behind, why we have focused on specifically minibinders
in comparison to sdAbs for this project, rather than binders of similar size
to that of sdAbs.
1.6.1
Complementarity between minibinder and sdAb designs
SdAbs and minibinders represent two distinct yet similar strategies in the
pursuit of next-generation protein-based therapeutics.
The interplay between these two modalities is particularly intriguing
in therapeutic development.
SdAbs bring evolutionary robustness and
broad-spectrum applicability, while minibinders contribute structural
diversity and the ability to target highly specific or unconventional epitopes.
For instance, recent advancements in RFdiffusion have demonstrated the
ability to generate protein designs with atomic-level accuracy, as seen in
the successful creation of binders for challenging epitopes [61].
Such
computational tools can also refine existing sdAb frameworks, merging the
strengths of both approaches.
By leveraging the complementary strengths of sdAbs and minibinders,
it is possible to create a versatile toolkit for therapeutic innovation.
These technologies can be combined to address a wider range of targets,

2.2.1
de novo minibinder design
Minibinders were designed using a computational pipeline involving
RFdiffusion, ProteinMPNN, and AlphaFold2.
The process began with
RFdiffusion to construct a 3D protein backbone tailored to a given target
and specific binding hotspots.
The generated structure was then input
into ProteinMPNN to predict amino acid sequences corresponding to
the designed backbone.
Finally, these sequences were assessed using
AlphaFold2 to predict the three-dimensional structure in relation to
the target protein.
AlphaFold2 outputs confidence scores, including
the predicted Local Distance Difference Test (pLDDT) for structural
accuracy and the Interaction-protein Predicted Alignment Error (IpAE)
for binding likelihood. High-confidence designs were manually assessed,
with acceptable candidates requiring a pLDDT score of ≥95 and an
IpAE score of ≤10. ChimeraX was used for visualization and to guide
iterative improvements based on manual inspection of the models. For
readers interested in a more detailed background description of these
computational tools, please refer to A.2

Discussion
4.1
Challenges in obtaining and purifying the Consensus protein
Although one of the wildtype variants upon which this consensus sequence
is based could be successfully expressed in the BL21 (DE3) expression
system and purified using gravity flow HisTrap IMAC, the same approach
did not work for the consensus sequence.
Despite trying different
induction methods, optimizing temperatures, refining the elution steps, and
fractionating the eluates with NGC while still using a HisTrap column, we
were unable to achieve successful expression and purification. To address
this, we switched to the T7 Shuffle expression vector, which includes
folding assistants and is more suited for proteins requiring disulfide bond
formation and isomerization - processes that are important for both the
wildtype and consensus versions of CfaE, which contain two disulfide
bonds each. However, despite this modification, SDS-PAGE analysis still
showed a prominent band around 15 kDa in the elution fractions, along with
other impurities of higher molecular weight than the expected 22.6 kDa
for the consensus protein. MALDI-TOF analysis confirmed these results,
suggesting that the protein might be prematurely truncated. This led us
to reconsider the expression strategy. We hypothesized that expressing
the protein in the cytoplasm rather than the periplasm could help mitigate
the risk of protease-mediated truncation, as the periplasm is often a site of
proteolytic degradation. However, this concern does not align with the fact
that the wildtype protein is naturally expressed in the periplasm without
such issues. And the consensus sequence does not contain any unique
protease recognition sites compared to the wildtype, making it unclear
why the consensus construct would be more susceptible to degradation.
The issue was further highlighted by a pattern of frameshift mutations
(insertion of T) observed during sequencing of the extracted DNA from
transformants in either BL21 or T7 Shuffle strains.
These mutations
Designing protein structures for neutralizing CfaE in E.coli for treating GI infections
57
==PAGE:65==
occurred at the beginning of the region encoding the TEV recognition site,
just after the His-tag. The recurrence of this mutation in multiple isolated
transformants strongly suggests that it could be a factor contributing to the
unsuccessful expression and purification of the CfaE consensus protein.
The recurring T insertion mutation observed in the region encoding the
TEV protease recognition site (ENLYFQG) immediately following the
His-tag suggests an inherent instability linked to the specific nucleotide
sequence, rather than the combination of a His-tag and TEV site itself.
While His-tags and TEV sites are widely used in biotechnology without
issue, the arrangement of codons in this particular construct may make
the sequence more prone to errors during DNA replication. The repetitive
nature of the His-tag sequence (HHHHHH), combined with the specific
codons encoding the TEV site, might create regions in the DNA that are
more likely to slip or misalign during replication.
This highlights the
importance of considering codon composition and sequence context when
designing constructs to ensure stability during propagation and expression.
However, this may not be the sole explanation, as we also encountered
similar issues with transformants that did not exhibit the mutation, and
in these cases, the full genomic sequencing of the transformants appeared
normal. This recurring challenge of expressing the CfaE consensus protein
could be partially attributed to its bacterial origin. Proteins resembling
those naturally produced by E. coli often face additional complications
during recombinant expression due to proteolytic susceptibility [63], codon
usage preferences [64, 65], and folding requirements. CfaE is a surface
protein native to E. coli and is typically expressed in the periplasm, where
proteases are more active [66]. Even when altering the expression system
and strategy, these inherent characteristics may still pose obstacles. While
these challenges are not unique to CfaE, they reflect the complexity of
expressing bacterial-like proteins in bacterial systems and the importance

4.2
Designing minibinders
The four minibinders selected for in vitro validation were chosen from
thousands of candidates based on their predicted structural confidence
and the strength of their predicted interactions with the predefined target.
Initial rounds of RFdiffusion denoising were guided using conditional
information, specifically an interface hotspot, followed by iterative rounds
of partial diffusion targeting the same hotspot.
This process resulted
in constructs adopting alpha-helical structures, which were modeled to
interact with the CDRs of the target. In contrast, the construct designed
without conditional information during the initial diffusion rounds adopted
a beta-sheet-based structure and was predicted to interact with the target in
a side-on manner. This observation is particularly intriguing, as AlphaFold
predictions of the sdAb H7 binding to the CfaE wildtype - using only
the sequences as input - also revealed beta-sheet-based conformation and
similar side-on interaction, resembling a clamp on the target’s side rather
than directly on its binding region (see Figure 4.1).

4.3
Evaluating minibinders
Minibinders were successfully expressed and purified.
Additional
characteristics, such as mass and secondary structure, were validated using
MALDI-TOF and CD spectroscopy, respectively, for some of the samples.
The reason only one of the minibinders underwent CD analysis is that
the amounts of the other minibinders were too low to reach the 200 µL
solution with a concentration of 0.3 mg/mL required for the CD method.
SDS-PAGE gel analysis of the minibinders, which were further validated
by western blot, showed bands corresponding to a molecular weight of
approximately 40 kDa (See Fig. 3.12). Although the theoretical molecular
weight of these minibinders is around 20 kDa, this same trend was observed
with the benchmark sdAb C12 (theoretical molecular weight: 15,019.49
Da), which also exhibited a clear band around 40 kDa. This may suggest
multimerization or other complications during the gel run. All samples
were denatured by boiling at 95°C for five minutes with DTT, but given
that minibinders are known for their exceptional stability, this may not
have been sufficient for full denaturation. However, this does not explain
why C12 also displayed a band at 40 kDa (See Fig. 3.13). The molecular
weights of some minibinders were confirmed by MALDI-TOF analysis,
with results consistent with the expected mass. The most interesting aspect
of these minibinders is the binding affinity data obtained from the DELFIA
assay, which will be discussed further.
The negative controls in this round of DELFIA experiments yielded
higher-than-expected TRF signal values, with an average of 796 compared
to the typical 50 observed in previous runs. This elevated background
signal resulted in an increased limit of detection, set at 796. While this
adjustment allowed for consistent analysis, it also introduced challenges in
interpreting weak signals, particularly for the minibinder samples (WT1,
WT2, C1, C2). Signals close to the LOD may reflect background noise
rather than genuine binding interactions, complicating the assessment of
minibinder performance (see Fig. 3.17). Potential sources for the elevated
negative control signals include non-specific binding of assay components,

Statistical analysis of the DELFIA experiment results conducted for

minibinders was comparable to that of the benchmark bivalent sdAbs (C4
and C12). Based on these results alone, the analysis suggests that the
minibinders’ binding affinity is nearly equal to that of the benchmarks,
but they were significantly outperformed by the bivalent sdAbs H7 and
F1. However, when examining data from a previous experiment using the
same benchmarks and analyzing it with an F-test, we found a significant
difference in the performance of the benchmarks between two rounds of
the same method. This discrepancy could indicate inconsistencies with
either the method or the benchmarks themselves. Given this uncertainty,
it would not be appropriate to draw conclusions about the minibinders’
binding affinity in comparison to the benchmarks, as the reliability of
either the method, the benchmarks, or both is questionable.
These observations highlight the challenges in assessing the performance of
de novo-designed minibinders, particularly when considering limitations in
experimental reproducibility. While the binding affinities observed for the

results must be interpreted cautiously in light of the elevated background
signals and inconsistencies across experimental runs. To provide additional

5
Conclusion
This thesis explores innovative strategies for addressing the pressing
global challenge of AMR by targeting the adhesion mechanisms
of ETEC. Specifically,
the study focused on the development of
neutralizing protein-based therapeutics - sdAbs and computationally
designed minibinders - intended to inhibit the function of the adhesin CfaE.
By leveraging both evolutionary insights and state-of-the-art computational
methods, this work sought to (1) obtain and evaluate a consensus CfaE
construct for binder screening, (2) design and characterize de novo
minibinders targeting CfaE, and (3) compare their binding performance
to that of sdAbs previously identified through phage display.
While
significant progress was made in computational design and expression of
minibinders, the experimental work was unable to conclusively validate
their binding affinity.
As a result, a direct, quantitative comparison
between sdAbs and minibinders could not be achieved. Without validated
affinity data, it remains uncertain whether minibinders can provide
functional advantages over sdAbs in neutralizing CfaE-mediated adhesion.
Future work will be necessary to optimize the purification and functional
characterization of the minibinders to fully assess their therapeutic
potential.
Furthermore, despite extensive troubleshooting efforts, the
consensus CfaE construct could not be successfully expressed and purified,
and the underlying reasons for this remain unclear.
This highlights
the inherent challenges of designing and producing synthetic protein
constructs, particularly when modifications are introduced to improve
stability or mimic conserved structural elements.
Understanding the
bottlenecks in expression and solubility will be relevant for future studies
aiming to utilize consensus-based designs for binder screening. Despite
these setbacks, this work successfully demonstrated the feasibility of de
novo protein design using RFdiffusion, ProteinMPNN, and AlphaFold2,

==PAGE:72==
laying the groundwork for future advancements in computationally guided
therapeutic development.
The findings support the broader strategy of
targeting virulence factors rather than bacterial survival, which could
reduce the selective pressure driving antibiotic resistance. Ultimately, this
work highlights the potential of integrating computational and experimental
approaches to develop next-generation protein-based therapeutics. While
additional validation is required to assess the functional efficacy of the
designed minibinders, this research contributes to the foundation for
alternative, non-antibiotic strategies against bacterial pathogens.
By
refining and expanding upon these methods, future studies may help
develop scalable, effective treatments that mitigate bacterial adhesion and
contribute to the global effort to combat AMR.

6
Future perspective
The observation that minibinders designed using RFdiffusion - without
predefined conformational constraints - resembled the predicted binding
region of the sdAb H7, which has demonstrated exceptional binding
affinity, presents an interesting avenue for further investigation.
The
sdAb, H7, was initially thought to bind directly with the binding
region, though computational structural studies from this project suggests
possible side-on interaction.
However, given that RFdiffusion, which
iteratively denoises structural features based on naturally occurring
proteins, identified a similar binding interface independently, further
studies are needed to clarify whether these interactions are indeed
structurally and functionally equivalent. Investigating this recurring trend
could improve our understanding of how RFdiffusion-guided designs
align with experimentally validated binding interfaces. To systematically
evaluate the minibinders and sdAbs, more advanced and reliable functional
assays should be conducted. A logical starting point would be BLI to
directly compare the binding affinities of minibinders to those of sdAbs
H7, F1, and established benchmark sdAbs, as this method was also
employed by Watson et al [67]. However, binding affinity alone does
not necessarily correlate with functional neutralization.
To determine
their neutralization capacities, a flow chamber assay with mammalian
intestinal cells simulating gastrointestinal conditions in the presence of
ETEC would provide valuable insights. This experimental setup would
allow for direct assessment of how effectively minibinders and sdAbs
prevent bacterial adhesion. Additionally, complementary assays such as
competitive ELISA or bacterial co-culture adhesion assays could be used
to further validate neutralization efficacy. By correlating binding affinity
with functional outcomes, these studies would advance our understanding
of protein-protein interactions in the context of pathogen neutralization.

If wet-lab validation confirms that both minibinders and sdAbs interact
with the target in a side-on manner, this would provide strong evidence
supporting a hybrid approach that combines computationally designed
binders with phage display-derived sdAbs.
While the computational
method has not produced conclusive results, demonstrating potential for
identifying high-affinity binders, the complementarity to the method of
identifying sdAbs, remain to be fully explored.
Future work should
investigate whether computationally designed minibinders and naturally
selected sdAbs target distinct but functionally relevant epitopes and
whether their combination enhances neutralization more effectively than
either approach alone. Regarding the AMR crisis and the development of
alternative treatments for bacterial infections, the long-term implications
of a neutralization-based approach remain an important area for evaluation.
While targeting virulence factors instead of bacterial viability may reduce
selective pressure for antibiotic resistance, it does not eliminate the
possibility of bacterial adaptation.
Pathogens could evolve alternative
adhesion mechanisms or structural modifications to evade neutralizing
proteins, necessitating continuous efforts in binder optimization and
genomic surveillance. The inhibition of bacterial adhesion to mammalian
cells remains a highly promising therapeutic approach, and its broader
applicability to other bacterial targets should be further explored.

The context of this appendix is a more detailed description of the
computational tools for readers interested.
A.1
RFdiffusion for protein binder design
RFdiffusion is a computational tool that utilizes a denoising diffusion
probabilistic model (DDPM) to design protein binders with high specificity
and affinity. This method builds on advances in machine learning and
protein structural biology, allowing for the generation of novel binder
conformations by progressively reversing a process of structural ”noising.”
The process begins with the target protein sequence and generates potential
binder structures by introducing random perturbations to structural frames.
RFdiffusion then learns to denoise these frames, ultimately predicting
native-like protein structures that are optimized for binding[68].
The model operates by providing only the sequence of the target protein,
without requiring predefined structural constraints. Thousands of potential
binder-target complexes are generated through diffusion, starting from
an initial random noise configuration and gradually transitioning to
structured states. This approach enables the exploration of a wide range
of conformations, particularly useful for intrinsically disordered regions
(IDRs) or proteins with flexible structures[68, 67]. To refine the initial
designs, RFdiffusion applies partial diffusion. Unlike the full diffusion
process, partial diffusion introduces a controlled level of randomness to
both the binder and target protein. This preserves critical structural features
while optimizing interactions between the two molecules. By balancing the
introduction of variability with the maintenance of essential conformational
elements, partial diffusion increases the likelihood of producing binders
with high shape complementarity and binding affinity[68, 61].

Once the model generates a library of candidate designs, these are filtered
and validated based on computational metrics.
Key evaluation criteria
include predicted alignment error (pAE) and predicted local distance
difference test (pLDDT) scores, which assess interface quality. Additional
structural metrics, such as binding energy, hydrogen bond count, RMSD,
and surface contact area, further narrow down the candidate pool.
A.2
ProteinMPNN for Protein Sequence Design
ProteinMPNN is a deep learning-based tool developed to address
the protein sequence design problem, where the aim is to identify
amino acid sequences that fold into a given backbone structure.
Unlike traditional energy-based methods such as Rosetta, ProteinMPNN
employs a message-passing neural network (MPNN) to predict sequences
based on structural inputs, offering higher accuracy and computational
efficiency[69].
The design process begins with the input of a protein
backbone, where features such as interatomic distances, backbone dihedral
angles, and relative orientations of backbone atoms are encoded as nodes
and edges within a graph representation. These structural features inform
the neural network about the geometric and chemical context of each
residue position. ProteinMPNN processes this information through a series
of message-passing operations, refining the graph representation to predict
the most likely amino acid for each position[69]. A key innovation of
ProteinMPNN is its use of autoregressive decoding, which predicts amino
acids iteratively while considering the sequence context. This approach
is order-agnostic, allowing flexibility in the design process. For example,
fixed residues—such as those critical for binding—can be preserved during
design, while other positions are optimized[69]. ProteinMPNN’s ability to
account for the local structural environment is particularly advantageous
for designing diverse sequences. By training the model on a wide range of
high-resolution protein structures, it achieves a balance between capturing
general sequence trends and fine-tuning specific features for stability and
functionality. The model’s robustness is further enhanced by incorporating

Resumé - Danish
Den globale krise med antimikrobiel resistens (AMR) har skabt et behov for
innovative terapeutiske strategier, der går ud over traditionelle antibiotika. Denne
afhandling undersøger proteinbaserede terapeutika målrettet mod Enterotoxigen
Escherichia coli, der forårsager gastrointestinal infektion.
Arbejdet fokuserer
specifikt på at neutralisere adhæsionsmekanismen medieret af CfaE-adhesinet
- en virulensfaktor, involveret I bakteriel kolonisering.
To tilgange blev
udforsket: enkelt-domæne antistoffer (sdAbs) afledt fra naturlige immunsystemer
og computer-designede minibindere.
SdAbs viste robuste bindingsaffiniteter,
som er optimeret af evolutionsbaserede mekanismer.
Minibindere, der blev
designet de novo ved hjælp af avancerede computerværktøjer som RFdiffusion,
ProteinMPNN og AlphaFold2, blev udviklet for at facilitere strukturel enkelhed
og forbedret stabilitet. På baggrund af udfordringer med eksperimentel validering
kunne bindingsaffiniteten af disse minibindere ikke bestemmes endeligt, hvilket
forhindrede en direkte sammenligning med sdAbs.
Desuden, på trods af
omfattende troubleshooting, kunne den konsensusbaserede CfaE-konstruktion
ikke succesfuldt udtrykkes og oprenses.
Spørgsmålet om gastrointestinale
infektioner er særligt relevant, når alternative farmaceutiske løsninger til antibiotika
udvikles, da det kommensale mikrobiom påvirkes kraftigt af antibiotikabrug.
Selv når antibiotika administreres for infektioner uden for mave-tarmkanalen,
forstyrrer oral antibiotikabehandling tarmmikrobiotaen,
hvilket kan føre til
sundhedskomplikationer.
Ved at målrette behandling mod bakteriel adhesion
snarere end bakteriel overlevelse, kan neutraliserende terapeutika som sdAbs og
minibindere tilbyde et mikrobiom-skånende alternativ, der reducerer det selektive
pres for resistens, samtidig med at infektion forhindres. På trods af de tekniske
udfordringer, der blev mødt, bidrager dette arbejde til grundlaget for alternative
terapeutiske strategier mod bakterielle patogener.
Ved at fremme design og
anvendelse af målrettede proteinbaserede terapier bidrager denne forskning til
udviklingen af bæredygtige løsninger mod AMR og understøtter vigtigheden af at
bevare tarmmikrobiomets sundhed i bekæmpelse af bakterielle infektioner.
