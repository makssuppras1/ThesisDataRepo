Abstract
This thesis concerns the robustification of path-replanning methods in Collision Avoid-
ance (CA) scenarios at sea. This entails the proposal of a model, that should capture
uncertain elements.
A model describing Own Ship (OS) maneuvering is proposed, where there is an
emphasis on low parameterization. The model parameters are derived, using a model
from Fossen’s MSS-toolbox, and a bounded uncertainty to relevant parameters are stated.
The Target Ship (TS) are assumed to keep a nearly constant velocity. This velocity is
perturbed with white Gaussian noise, to model contingent maneuvers. Using the state
covariance of TS, the 99.7% confidence ellipse is assumed to contain the TS state with
probability 1.
A relatively new method, Funnel Libraries for Feedback Motion Planning, is applied.
Through the method, finite-time invariant sets, known as funnels, along a finite-time
trajectory are computed. Eleven trajectories form the library, to which the finite-time
invariant sets are computed. It is secured, that sequencing the funnels will also result
in an invariant set. Using the confidence ellipses of Target Ship and the funnels, it is
possible to find maneuvers whereto no risk of collision can be associated. All maneuvers
where the two sets intersect all have a risk of collision and are discarded for that reason.
The funnels are used as motion primitives in an A*-planner to compute the evasive
maneuver. Two general and two scenario specific metrics of evaluation constitute the
cost-to-go functions. Firstly, a safety metric is computed based on the popular concept of
Closet Point of Approach (CPA), Distance to CPA (DCPA) and Time to CPA (TCPA),
that will penalize OS maneuvers towards TS. Secondly, a metric that penalizes the
funnels based on the nominal trajectories length and rudder actuation. The first scenario
specific metric accounts for OS being the give-way vessel in a crossing scenario, making
it steer behind the TS. The second scenario specific metric is used in a head-on scenario,
penalizing maneuvers where OS finds TS on its starboard side.
Lastly, a heuristic
function that steers OS towards a goal, defined as being between the next Waypoint
(WP) and the point where TS intersects the path of OS.
Two simulations demonstrate a proof of concept of the proposed robust path re-
planner.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Collision of vessels or grounding in maritime industry can have severe consequences for
the environment and will impact the ship owners as well as companies depending on the
commerce offered by the vessels [1]. It has been reported that a significant number of
collision and grounding cases are due to human errors [2][1]. To prevent collision at sea,
the International Maritime Organization (IMO) has since 1972 agreed upon regulations,
known as Convention on the International Regulations for Preventing Collisions at Sea
(COLREGs), that serve as a guideline for the vessel operators to choose an appropriate
evasive maneuver, based on the encounter scenario.
The statistics describing human error in collision and grounding, has been an argu-
ment for research in the direction of making autonomous vessels at sea [2]. The parts
that relate to the Guidance, Navigation & Control (GNC) have been made autonomous
to a large degree [3]. However, there are other components in an autonomous system
such as vessels at sea that require additional research. Among those areas are the CA at
sea, which is one of the key components for reaching the goal of a temporary unattended
bridge or even a fully autonomous vessel.
The subject of CA is common in vehicle systems, be it aerial drones of marine surface
vessels. Added to the already high complexity of the CA problem at sea are various
constraints imposed by the necessity for compliance of any solutions to the COLREGs.
In the literature the methods seek to incorporate the COLREGs in the path re-planner,
carrying out the CA.
The literature presents various solutions for CAs method that are COLREGs com-
pliant, while some even propose optimal solutions that are derived based on some per-
formance metrics [2]. There is however no notion of how an uncertain maneuverability
of OS or an unknown ocean current will influence the CA. Many methods implicitly
assume that the path for an evasive maneuver can be tracked perfectly. However, if the
ship under some circumstance has a lower maneuverability or some unaccounted ocean
current acts on the ship, it may not be possible to track the path with the precision
assumed OS would be able to.
Larger deviations from the path may then result in
an unacceptable path for CA since there is no notion of how severe this deviation can
become. This deviation might lead to violations of some safety distance or worse, in a
collision.
The following section highlights some of the methods in the literature for CA at sea
with focus on how uncertainty is treated in the motion prediction of the vessels in a CA
method.
==PAGE:22==
2
1 Introduction
1.1
Related work
Many of the studied methods are able to integrate the performance metric of reaching
a goal or set of goals through the shortest path, such as through the Fast Marching
Method [4]. The authors of [4] introduce a metric that penalizes each node based on
their distance to a non-admissible node.
The authors use this as a way to describe
uncertainty related to a TS motion prediction.
The RRT-Star (RRT*) method is used in [5] to plan the evasive maneuver. The
metrics account for the shortest path to the goal, a safe distance to TS or static obstacle
as well as a minimum curvature of the planned path to account for the turning radius of
OS. There are however no reflection on how uncertainty of the model used to evaluate the
minimum turning radius capabilities will affect the RRT* algorithm. Also, the uncertain
motion prediction of the TS is not accounted for in the method.
A method such as Dynamic Window employed in [6] and [7] optimizes over the surge
velocity and yaw-rate, to find the optimal velocity pair, that will prevent the collision of
OS with a TS. An obstacle or TS is taken into account by finding a set of non-admissible
velocity pairs. A safety distance to each obstacle and TS that enlarges their occupancy
of the configuration space is added.
Another method that uses velocity pairs to perform evasive maneuvers is the Velocity
Obstacle method used [8]. This method uses the relative velocities of OS and TS to find
the set of velocities that will lead to a potential collision, given the relative position
of the vessels.
This set is referred to as the Velocity Obstacle.
This leads to a set
of ”allowed” velocities for which the authors introduce a penalizing cost in a discrete
grid, that minimizes the reciprocal of a collision time and the deviation from the original
path through the along-track and cross-track measures. In order to handle the uncertain
motion prediction of TS, the Velocity Obstacle is enlarged. In the paper it is treated
as a soft constraint, allowing the method to decrease the volume of the set and find
admissible velocities.
A Model Predictive Control strategy for CA is reported in [9][10]. An eloquent en-
semble of cost functions are used, based on which the Model Predictive Control finds the
optimal evasive maneuver. These metrics take the COLREGs into consideration by eval-
uating the relative position and heading of the ships and discretely finding the suitable
rule for penalizing the associated maneuvers. Although discussions on the uncertainty
involve the unknown intentions of TS and the effect of noise, the authors of [11] assume
accurate knowledge of their OS states and assume that the desired trajectory can be
achieved, thereby having no notion of the ships situation along the trajectory.
The survey in [2] presents an extensive overview of CA at sea and compares their
disadvantages and advantages. The methods mentioned here are a few of those that
discuss metrics of evaluation and uncertainty related to TS motion prediction. However,
the methods above and those discussed in [2] do not consider uncertainty related to the
motion predictions of OS, as also concluded in [2]. Also, the methods that try to account
for uncertainty by enlarging the occupancy area of for example a TS in the configuration
space do not state the specific reason of this exact enlargement or just use some estimate.

There is therefore no connection from this enlargement to the models used to generate
the motion plans.
1.1.1
Robust motion planning
An effort was made in [12] to include unknown disturbances and modeling errors into
the motion planning. There, the authors designed a Maneuver Automaton [13] where,
through the assumption that the uncertainty enters into the modeling framework by
a Gaussian process, they can relate a variance to the motion primitives. The motion
primitives are subsequently used in an A-Star (A*)-planner. The robustness stems from
the fact that the planner takes into account the state variance and computes a probability
of collision with obstacles. In the method there is no clear indication of how the Gaussian
process models the uncertainty in the systems dynamics and the process noise is also a
design parameter. In addition, this limits the analysis to linear systems.
The same framework of using an A* planner for planning evasive maneuvers is used
in [14]. Herein, the authors relate probabilities of collision to the maneuvers of OS in the
presence of obstacles and TSs. The planner takes into account the contingent maneuvers
of TS by overlaying this into the probability of collision calculation. For that purpose
they develop an intention model of TS based on simulations. The probability of collision
is solely based on the uncertain motion prediction of TS. Hence there is not mention of
how uncertainty related to OS affects the algorithm. The goal of the algorithm is to find
the path that has the minimum risk of collision.
A minimum risk is a good index for evaluating the feasibility of the path when it
comes to checking for collision. However, there is still a risk of collision associated to
the maneuver and the methods discussed so far still fail to consider uncertain knowledge
of OS. How can one find a certificate, that a maneuver is associated with 0% risk of
collision, given an uncertain knowledge of OS and a tracking model of TS? The question
boils down to having a clear understanding of the whereabouts of OS and TS in the
future given uncertain knowledge of the models and unknown disturbances acting on
the systems.
To answer the first part of the question above, namely how can one generate plans,
that take into account the uncertain knowledge about OS dynamics, the method pro-
posed in [15][16] can be used. The method provides tools for reasoning about parametric
uncertainty in the model and unknown disturbances acting on that system. The method
assumes that the parametric uncertainty and unknown disturbances are bounded and
the bounds are known. Through this method, one will be able to generate a finite-time
invariant set about a trajectory, known as a funnel in the literature [17][16], which can
be used in a motion planner to address the topic of robustness in an algebraic way. It
is this method, known as Funnel Libraries for Robust Feedback Motion Planning that is
used in this thesis.
==PAGE:24==
4
1 Introduction
1.2
Scope of thesis
The focus of this thesis is to investigate methods for enhancing the robustness of CA at
sea algorithms against model and environment uncertainty. To this end, the following
goals are pursued:
• Perform a literature study of specific topics relevant to the thesis work.
• A modeling framework of both OS and TS is proposed, wherein it is possible to
consider uncertain knowledge about the dynamics.
• A method for robustly planning an evasive maneuver is proposed, integrating the
above proposed modeling framework. This method has to be used together with
metrics of evaluation in a planner to find the optimal evasive maneuver.
• Finally, an evaluation of the method with respect to the robustness hereof is pre-
sented.
The research was conducted in the context of ShippingLabs CA framework. A coarse
overview of that framework is shown in Fig. 1.1. The Situation Awareness (SA) module
takes in the available sensor information and is capable to anticipate all the TS intentions.
Based on this, it informs the Officer on Watch (OOW) of which TS or obstacle should
be the first object to take action against. Based on the scenario the SA also suggests a
course of action, for example which suitable COLREGs rule should be followed.

Reference frames
A vehicle at sea can experience motion in six directions. The six directions are illustrated
in Fig. 2.1. If it can move freely in those directions as in the figure, it is said that the
system experiences motion in six Degree of Freedom (DOF).
For the maneuvering of surface vessels at open sea the DOF corresponding to surge,
sway and yaw are the most important since they relate to the speed and heading of the
vessel. If in calm sea, the pitch and heave DOF do not affect the heading and forward
speed. In the maneuvering equations such as (2.1) therefore the surge, sway and yaw
motion are often only considered. Sometimes a four DOF model of surge, sway, yaw and
roll is established if the goal is to account for the strong coupling between yaw and roll,
and if there are actuators to mitigate the roll motion [3].

2.5
Guidance, navigation & control
The Guidance system deals with the generation of a path that will guide the ship towards
a given goal point. There exists multiple ways of generating the path, in the shipping
industry WP generation is common. However, one could also generate trajectories with
discrete values that needs to be tracked. Again, there exists different methods to generate
those trajectories [3].
The Navigation system deals with the problem of utilizing the information from
the sensor system on board. This information is fed to the control system to provide
information for the feedback control. It can also be used as input to the Guidance system
to account for weather for example [3].
The control system uses the information from the Navigation system to follow the
path generated by the Guidance system. When WP are used as the guidance for the
ship, often a Line Of Sight (LOS) guidance is used to produce a desired heading. This
desired heading is used as a reference for a PID controller, controlling the rudder of the
ship. In the case of the ship tracking a trajectory, more advanced control systems will
become useful [3].

A comprehensive study on target tracking models is discussed in [23]. The challenge of

3.1
Feedback motion planning
Generally the topic of motion planning can be separated into three categories. Firstly,
the generation of motion plans is done without considering the system at all and a
simple path in the configuration space is found. Secondly, the dynamics are considered
as constraints in the generation of the motion plans. Lastly, a feedback law, for tracking
a trajectory for example, is considered in the motion planner, that allows the planner
to take into account, that the system may find itself in an unexpected state during
execution. This is known as feedback motion planning [13].
The motivation for using feedback motion planning stems from the fact that fu-
ture states which the system may evolve to, are uncertain and can be unpredictable.
In control theory, methods for handling such uncertainty have been proposed through
stochastic control and robust control [13]. It is therefore worthwhile to use to use meth-
ods from the control theory to reason about robustness of a motion plan. To this end,
in conjunction with the method used in this thesis, Lyapunov theory and invariant sets
will be introduced.
3.2
Lyapunov Theory & Invariant sets
The concepts developed by Lyapunov can be used, when analyzing the stability of equi-
librium point in a nonlinear system. The following theorem shows Lyapunovs direct
method.

tion ˙x = f(x). The stability of an equilibrium point is said to be assymptotically stable
if there exists a Lyapunov function V (x) where
• V (x) ≻0
• ˙V (x) = ∂V (x)

The above theorem provides an algebraic way of assessing the stability of system
at an equilibrium point. The following definition will play an important role in the
generation of the funnels.
Definition 3.2.1 (Invariant set [24]). A set G is an invariant set for a system such as
˙x = f(x) when a system trajectory starting in G will remain in G for all time.
The funnel library method builds on the concepts above by using Lyapunov functions
to compute the Region of Attraction (RoA) around a trajectory, that is, an invariant
set along the trajectory [25]. Since the trajectory is of finite length, this is also known
as finite-time invariance of the trajectory. Sub-level sets of the Lyapunov functions can
be used to express invariant sets as in Def. 3.2.1, where the sub-level sets will be given
as V (x) = ρ, for some 0 ≤ρ [21]. The Lyapunov functions therefore provide good
means to determining invariant sets. However, finding a Lyapunov function that fulfills
the local version of Theorem 3.2.1 for any system can be quite cumbersome, which has
been a limiting factor in the usage of finite-time invariance methods in feedback motion
planning [17]. If a Lyapunov function for a multivariate system has been determined,
it still remains a challenge to find the largest ρ, corresponding to the largest invariant
set. If it is possible to increase ρ it is said that one verifies a larger finite-time invariant
set, thereby increasing the volume of said set. This is of interest when used in feedback
motion planning, since it is crucial to know the whereabouts of the system along the
trajectory.
It has been shown, that if the Lyapunov functions are limited to be a polynomial func-
tion of x, one can leverage the advances made in Sum-of-Squares (SOS)-programming.

3.4.4
Sequential Composition of Funnels
The intention with the funnel library method is to compute funnels for N trajectories.
In order to use them in a planner, the funnels are used as motion primitives and need
to be sequenced together. To this end it is needed to form a notion of how they can be
robustly sequenced together due to sets of initial and final conditions and ensure that
the resulting sequenced set is also a finite-time invariant set.
In [16] three definitions of sequential composition of funnels are introduced. Only
two of them are shown here since they are relevant for the use of the funnels in planning
the whole evasive maneuver. The definitions are based on those from planning with
trajectory library and the notions of the same name from [25].
Definition 3.4.2 (Sequential composition [16]). An ordered pair (F1, F2) of funnels
F1 : [0, T1] →P(Rn) and F2 : [0, T2] →P(Rn) is sequentially composable if F1(T1) ⊂
F2(0).
The above definition means that the set at the end of the first Funnel F1(T1) should
be contained by the set at the start of the second Funnel F2(0). Speaking in terms of a
funnel, the inlet of the second funnel F2 should contain the outlet of the first funnel F1.

Using the Funnels and NCV in the Evaluation
Framework
Given the funnel, representing a set of the configuration space which OS can occupy, and
the confidence ellipse of the TS, representing the obstacle region in the configuration
space, one can compute the worst case distance between the two vessels by considering
the smallest distance between the two resulting ellipses. The smallest distance is found
through a KD-tree search [13] using Matlabs function knnsearch.
The maneuvers, wherein the two sets of a segment prediction intersect, means that
there is a risk of collision and it is discarded.
Having this notion of the worst-case distance means that f1, f5,1 and f5,2 can be
evaluated on this worst case distance. The metrics can therefore be evaluated based
on the uncertainty considered by the semi-algebraic set shown in (5.4) as well as the
uncertain motion prediction of TS through the NCV-model.
5.3
Optimal Planner
Given the funnel library there should be established a way of sequencing the funnels to
form a path for an evasive maneuver, that should minimize a cost, expressed through
the metrics of evaluation introduced in the previous section. In this section the planners
investigated for that purpose will be introduced.

5.3 Optimal Planner
45
5.3.1
Random papidly exploring tree star
Initially it was the intention to apply the funnel library in an RRT* algorithm, due to
its completeness and that is proven to be optimal [37]. Extending the generic Rapidly-
exploring Random Tree (RRT) algorithm to the use of the Funnels is fairly straightfor-
ward. However, when working with the optimal planner extension of the RRT becomes
more nuanced. The RRT* achieves its optimal path in two steps [37].
Firstly, when expanding the tree after steering a random sampled node to the closest
node in the tree, the node inside a ball around the steered node with the lowest cost
is selected instead the nearest node, making that the parent node. Being constrained
to a trajectory library or funnel library in this case, it may be possible to connect
to this nearest node while maintaining sequential composition. For the RRT* where
differential constraints are considered, one will find a series of appropriate inputs to be
able to connect the newly steered node to the nearest node with the lowest cost. This
series of input is very unlikely to coincide with the inputs used to generate the trajectory
library.
Secondly, the tree needs to be rewired.
This step searches for nodes in the tree
around the newly connected node in an appropriate radius of the node. If a connection
between the newly added node and to a node in said radius will result in a lower cost
of such a node, then this node will change its parent node to the newly added node.
This means that the edge between such a node to whatever parent node it had before, is
removed and an edge between the newly added node to such a node is added. Again, the
same reason for making rewiring using trajectory or funnel libraries almost impossible
as discussed in the first step also applies here. There is a little chance of having the
appropriate input match that of the inputs used to generate the trajectory library.
5.3.2
Graph-search planner
For the reasons discussed above and based on suggestions from [16], graph-search plan-
ners were chosen for planning with funnels, taking into account the previous metrics of
evaluation.
Given a start vertex q = qs = 1, it is the goal of the graph-search planner to find a
path in the graph that leads the system to the goal G, represented as a vertex qG. The
simplest graph-search planner is Djikstra’s algorithm. Given a graph, one can compute
the distances between the vertices and denote this as the edge cost. Starting from qs
traversing the graph via for example two edges to reach a third vertex, the cost will be
the sum of the two edges. This will be denoted as the cost-to-come function G(qs, qi),
where qi denotes the resulting vertix, when the algorithm explores a certain part of the
configuration space, based on the trajectory library.
To guide the search in the direction of qG one can employ a heuristic function that
for example takes into account the distance between the vertices and the goal. This
function will be denoted as the cost-to-go function C(qi, qG).
Ones the cost-to-come from qs to an arbitrary node qi as well as the the cost-to-go
from qi to qG is computed, the sum of the cost-to-come and cost-to-go, H(qs, qi, qG) =

5.4
Summary
This chapter introduced how the function ρ(t) is initialized and how the semi-algebraic
set W is defined as an ellipsoid. The numerical issues due to dimensional parameters
was also commented, being the reason why the funnel generation has to be done in the
prime system. It was shown, how the funnels can be visualized and used in a planner.
Next, the six metrics of evaluation used in this thesis is introduced. A safety metric
based on TCPA and DCPA is used to steer OS away from a TS. A metric penalizes
the trajectories based on the length traversed and how much the rudder is actuated.
A metric is used to guide the ship towards the goal and a metric penalizes deviations
from the original heading. There are two scenario specific metrics; the first accounts
for a crossing scenario, steering the OS behind TS, and the second accounts for a head-
on scenario, where each maneuver where OS finds TS on its starboard side is heavily
penalized. Finally, an A*-planner uses these metrics of evaluation as costs to find a
sub-optimal path to the goal region.

Discussion
This section offers a discussion on the method of using the funnel libraries in conjunction
with an A* planner.
6.3.1
Trajectory library
The trajectory library designed in Section 4.3 was designed based on how the ship should
advance for each segment of the evasive maneuver. To enable the ship to alter its course,
integer steps in the rudder angle was carried out to generate trajectories that allowed
the OS to make turns. Therefore, when analyzing the trajectory library, there is no
notion if the final condition of such a trajectory is reached in an optimal way. Also, the
trajectories final condition are not in a well-defined grid.
In [16] the Direct Collocation method to generate an optimal trajectory is used.
Through the means of a nonlinear optimal program solver, the method takes in the
system as differential constraints, the initial conditions and the final conditions. The
solver finds an optimal trajectory from the initial to the final conditions based on a
cost function which often is formulated based on a weighted quadratic expression of the
inputs. This means, that the results is a locally optimized trajectory, which assess the
amount of control expenditure the system may expend to reach a final condition. The
cost computed by the Direct Collocation can then be used as a cost of the trajectory.

6.3.2
Funnel Library
The most challenging part of using the method discussed in this thesis for CA at sea,
is the generation of the funnel library. The challenges lie undoubtedly in the numerical
instability of the SOS-program.
The challenges which were faced in the course of this thesis related to the SOS-
program, were first that of scaling the problem correctly. For the problem at hand using
the Prime System helped alleviate the numerical errors due to scaling. However, the
scaling issues might still cause problems. This was still a limiting factor when defining
the semi-algebraic set for the uncertain parameters and disturbances. A small bound
==PAGE:77==
6.3 Discussion
57
might introduce numerical issues again, which was noticed specifically when defining
the bound for Cd. The nominal value of the parameter is small and a 10% increase will
result in a very small bound.
Secondly, defining the semi-algebraic set for the uncertain parameters also posed
itself as a limitation. Consider again (5.3) and (5.4), the obvious difference lies in the
number of constraints and decision variables it adds to the resulting SDP. The limitation
of using W2 over W1 is, that W2 does not give a certificate of finite-time invariance when
all unknown parameters are evaluated at their respective bounds whereas this holds for
W1. However, when using W1 the resulting solution status of the SDP-program, was
reported to be UNKOWN by Mosek, meaning that the problem has numerical issues, and
Mosek may not be able to solve the problem correctly. This prohibited the genration of
the funnels. There were no numerical issues when using W2.
When the addition of the constraints and decision variables through W hinder the
use of the method, the authors of [16] suggest that removing redundant decision variables
are key in getting a working SOS-program. Therefore, if having a system such as the one
in this thesis, prone to numerical issues, the assumption of using Spotless and the Drake
toolbox [22] as a black-box does not hold. It would be necessary to be able to view
and manipulate the resulting constraints and decision variables in the SDP to locate
the decision variables and parts that give rise to numerical issues to make the problem
solvable again.
There is continuous work in the development of the drake-toolbox [22] by Russ
Tedrake and his team. Their focus is to a better extend eliminate the numerical issues
due to redundant variables as well as scaling the problem internally, when going from an
SOS-program formulation to a SDP formulation [16][21]. Since the drake-toolbox does
not support Matlab any longer, but continue their work in C++, it would be advisable
to migrate to either C++ or using their Python-bindings in Python.
6.3.2.1
Extension
As discussed in Chapter 3, the funnels are generated as backwards reachable sets. This
amounts to maximizing the volume of F. Another approach would be to compute the
forward reachable sets.
Contrary to the backwards reachable sets, the set of initial
conditions are fixed, and the funnel is now minimized, to find the set of final conditions
which the system might evolve to when tracking a specific trajectory [16].
6.3.3
Planning with funnels
Having verified the funnel library for sequential composition, carrying out the com-

The development of evaluation metrics is an ongoing process. Another approach

6.3.3.1
Collision detection for set-based operations
As mentioned in Section 5.2.2 the planner has to use the sets ℓ, T and G to compute the
metrics of evaluation. Operations such as rotating an ellipse, and checking whether a
point lies inside a set will consume computation time, and will prolong the time it takes
for the planner to find a path. It is not needed to for example axis align the goal region
but was done to simplify and ease the implementation of checking when OS is inside G
or whether ℓintersects T . Therefore, the computation time can be greatly reduced if
more elaborate methods for computations with sets are applied.
On the other hand, there exists available toolboxes in Matlab for that purpose, such
as MATLAB Robotics System Toolbox. Reformulating the sets in terms of object used
by that toolbox would have been an approach. However, due to limited time, this could
not have been done, owing to the fact, that an interface for the objects from the drake-
toolbox to the objects from the Robotics System Toolbox should be created. If the
development is continued in Python, following the suggestion from [16] for the purpose
of checking if the F intersects with T , the Bullet Collision Detection & Physics Library1
can be used.
6.3.3.2
Planner
While the additional operations due to the introduction of funnels through set operation
plays a role in the long computation time, also the resolution of the grid and hence the
length of the trajectories play a role. This becomes especially important in graph-search
planners such as the A*-planner [14]. Increasing the length of the trajectories, could
reduce computation time.
If using the locally optimized trajectory generator discussed in Section 6.3.1, one can
also ensure, that the final conditions fit inside a well-defined grid, allowing to limit the
expansion of vertices. Such a trajectory generation where the final conditions are in a
well-defined grid was done in [39]. Designing the trajectory library in such a fashion
allows one to use the probabilistic complete and optimal planner RRT*, enhancing the
path found by the planner.

6.3.3.3
Safety area
It has been shown that the ship operators prefer a safety distance between the OS and
TS [40].
The authors have used data to empirically estimate that the ship domain,
interpreted as a safety area, can be approximated by an ellipse corresponding to a major
axis of 8 ship length and a minor axis of 3.2 ship length. The safety area is a measure
of the minimum distance the operator wants to have to other ships for comfortable
navigation.
This safety area can be superimposed onto F, resulting in ℓbeing inflated by the
safety ellipse. The same can also be used on T , if needed. Doing so the ship operators
sense of comfort while navigating is also accounted for in the A* planner using the funnel
library.
Finally, the results shown in Sections 6.1 and 6.2 are generated considering the ship
as a point mass. Therefore, to account for the geometry of the ship, the funnel needs
to be inflated by for example an ellipse. However, if the funnel is already inflated as
described above, the geometry is also considered.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
This thesis presented the introduction of robust feedback motion planning for Collision
Avoidance (CA) at sea. The scope of the thesis along with the contributions have been
stated in the introduction.
The studied literature showed, that the methods used for CA at sea do not consider
the effects the Own Ships (OS) model uncertain have on the planning of an evasive
maneuver. This becomes critical as unforeseen effects such as decreased maneuverability
or unaccounted ocean currents may result in an unacceptable deviation from the planned
evasive maneuver. Instead only Target Ship (TS) motion uncertainty has been addressed
in the existing literature, specifically by being described as a random process.
State-of-the-art modelling that includes first-principle concepts was pursued in this
study to describe the dynamics of both OS and TS. The focus has been to keep pa-
rameterization low, enabling the construction of a nominal model to which parametric
uncertainty and unknown bounded disturbances can be associated. The uncertain model
was able to account for the uncertainty’s effect on the maneuverability of the OS. The
TS was modeled as a random process, which enables the derivation of a set, T , based
on the 99.7% confidence ellipse, that is assumed to hold the possible states of TS by
certainty 1.
In order to take into account the uncertainty considered in the model for OS, a
method called Funnel Libraries for Robust Feedback Motion Planning has been used.
Computing robust finite-time invariant sets about nominal trajectories, denoted as fun-
nels, through the use of Lyapunov theory and SOS-programming, facilitates robust plan-
ning of an evasive maneuver for the OS, taking into account the uncertainty described
in the model.
A trajectory library consisting of 11 trajectories was designed to yield motion prim-
itives, that included turns and straight motion, enabling the ship to plan an evasive
maneuver. It was further secured that the sequentially composing of the funnels yielded
a robust finite-time invariant set.
The funnels together with the individual confidence ellipse of the TS were used to
define various metrics of evaluation, for the maneuvers found through an A* planner.
Also, if there is an overlap of the two sets in the configuration space through some
maneuver, there is a risk of collision, which means that this maneuver will be discarded.
Thereby, only maneuvers without a risk of collision will be explored by the planner.
Four general metrics and two scenario specific metrics have been defined.
A safety
metric that has TCPA and DCPA as arguments, penalizes the maneuvers that steer OS
towards TS. A second metric penalizes the selection of funnels from the funnel library
based on the length traversed by the ship and on the magnitude of the rudder angle.
==PAGE:82==
62
7 Conclusion
The third and fourth metric are the heuristics that guide the OS towards the goal G,
which is a subspace of the configuration space, stretching from the point of collision to
the next WP. The fifth metric contains two scenario specific metrics. The first accounts
for a crossing scenario, where it favors maneuvers that steer OS behind TS. The second
accounts for a head-on scenario, where OS may not find itself on the port side of TS.
Therefore, any maneuvers that moves OS on the port side is heavily penalized.
Two encounter scenarios demonstrate a proof of concept using the funnel libraries in
a A* planner, where the metrics of evaluation are used as costs to guide the search. The
results show that planning with funnels allows the notion of where OS and TS are with
respect to each with certainty one, meaning that the method enables to robustly plan
evasive maneuvers for CA at sea.
7.1
Future work
Considering the discussion on the method and results in Chapter 6, there can be listed
the following basis for future work.
Firstly, the generation of a locally optimized trajectory library through for example
the Trajectory Collocation method. This enables the generation of a trajectory library
that has final conditions in a well-defined grid and paves the way to use planning algo-
rithms such as the RRT*.
Secondly, an investigation on how the semi-algebraic set describing the uncertainty
W1 can be used over W2 to be truly able to generate a funnel serving as a certificate
of robust funnels for the bounds shown in Table 4.4. Since it is the numerics of the
problem that is the main limiting issue, a migration to C++ or Python is advised in
order to make use of the continuous support by Tedrake and his teams Drake-toolbox.
Lastly, to keep the computation time related to finding the path for an evasive
maneuver low, a thorough implementation of set-based operations needs to be carried
out.
Another option is to make use of already available software, mentioned in the
discussion.
