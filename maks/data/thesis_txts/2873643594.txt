Abstract
In this thesis, we develop a general probabilistic framework for inverse problems in infinite-
dimensional vector spaces. The formulation is based on measure-theoretic probability
and addresses several limitations of the traditional Bayesian framework. We provide a
general existence and uniqueness theorem for the posterior under minimal assumptions,
thereby resolving two aspects of ill-posedness.
Specializing to the case of Gaussian priors and likelihoods on separable Hilbert spaces,
we derive an explicit expression for the posterior measure using Gaussian disintegration
theory. A key contribution is the formal unification of Gaussian measures on reproducing
kernel Hilbert spaces with kernel-based methods, enabling exact posterior computation in
infinite-dimensional settings. This establishes a deeper theoretical connection between
Gaussian processes and Gaussian measures.
The framework is applied to a problem in system identification, where it is shown that the
second-order stable spline kernel defines the covariance operator of a Gaussian measure
on an exponentially weighted Sobolev space. This insight provides a rigorous justification
for using a Gaussian measure with covariance operator represented by the stable spline
kernel as a prior distribution, and significantly strengthens existing results on the regularity
and integrability of the associated sample paths. Numerical experiments demonstrate that
the posterior mean accurately reconstructs the true solution and that the credible intervals
provide meaningful uncertainty quantification.

Introduction
The subject of inverse problems is an important field within applied mathematics and
computational sciences. Inverse problems are concerned with the inference of unknown
physical quantities from indirect measurements and arise in a wide range of scientific and
engineering disciplines, including system identification and medical imaging.
Let X and Y be topological spaces. The mathematical structure underlying an inverse
problem is to determine an unknown quantity x ∈X from the observed data y ∈Y , where
the two are related by a forward operator G: X →Y such that
y = G(x).
(1.1)
A fundamental difficulty in solving the inverse problem is that it is typically ill-posed in the
sense of Hadamard [17]: the solution may not exist, may not be unique, or may not depend
continuously on the observed data. Therefore, some form of regularization is required
to mitigate the ill-posedness and stabilize the inversion. Some common regularization
strategies include variational approaches and probabilistic methods.
In this thesis, we develop a novel probabilistic framework for inverse problems in infinite-
dimensional vector spaces, generalizing the Bayesian formulation presented in [35, 36, 9].
While this framework builds on well-established results from measure-theoretic probability
theory, a general framework for probabilistic inverse problems has, to the best of our
knowledge, not been pursued in the literature. For a comprehensive review of probabilistic
regularization strategies for inverse problems, we refer to Lassas et al. [23].
The level of abstraction involved in this formulation requires considerable mathematical
maturity, and it is natural to ask whether such a general approach is justified. As we
will demonstrate, the answer is affirmative. By allowing the solution to be a probability
measure, we can establish a general existence and uniqueness theorem under minimal
assumptions. Moreover, we are not limited to probability measures which are absolutely
continuous with respect to a ω-finite measure as for the application of Bayes’ theorem.
To explore these ideas more concretely, we focus on the class of probabilistic inverse
problems with joint Gaussian distributions. As we will see for the special case of separable
Hilbert spaces, the solution is again a Gaussian measure, and explicit expressions for its
mean and covariance operator can be derived.
A central question for practical applications of probabilistic inverse problems with joint
Gaussian distributions concerns computational aspects and how Gaussian measures in
infinite-dimensional spaces can be represented programmatically. We provide a solution
to this problem in the special case of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces by representing
the covariance operator by a kernel function. This will allow for exact inference in infinite-
dimensional vector spaces on a finite computational budget. Moreover, it provides a new
perspective on kernel-based methods, which in many respects is more natural than the
traditional connection to Gaussian processes.
A guiding principle underpinning the development in this thesis is to avoid discretization
until the last possible moment, if at all necessary. This principle is enormously empowering
throughout numerical analysis and allows us to confront the infinite-dimensional nature of
many inverse problems.

To develop the theoretical and computational framework for inverse problems outlined
above, we proceed as follows.
In Chapter 2, we introduce some preliminary theory on probability measures on vector
spaces. The theory is vast, so we focus on the most important concepts and results on
ω-algebras, measurability, and characteristic functionals.
In Chapter 3, we develop a general probabilistic framework for inverse problems and
provide a general existence theorem under minimal assumptions. Moreover, we show
that the Bayesian approach is consistent with this framework.
Chapters 4, 5 and 6 are devoted to Gaussian measures on locally convex spaces. We
provide a rigorous interpretation of Gaussian processes as random functions and explore
Gaussian disintegration to compute the solution to a probabilistic inverse problem. This
discussion will motivate kernel-based methods for representing Gaussian covariances.
Finally, in Chapter 7, we apply the framework to problems in system identification. We
show that the stable spline kernel introduced by Pillonetto et al. [31] is the covariance
kernel of a Gaussian measure defined on an exponentially weighted Sobolev space. The
chapter concludes with some examples that demonstrate how the theoretical insights from
earlier chapters lead to practical and computationally tractable inference methods.

In this chapter, we present preliminary theory for probability distributions on vector spaces
as a highly condensed version of [6, Chapter 7], with additional insights from [40]. We
begin in a purely measure-theoretic setting, without reference to a topology, to simplify
notation and exposition. We then consider locally convex spaces, which provide the ap-
propriate level of generality for a rigorous interpretation of Gaussian processes as random
functions.
Our first goal is to equip a real vector space X with a suitable ω-algebra that makes the
vector space operations measurable. This motivates the following definition:
Definition 2.1. A measurable vector space over R is a pair (X, A), where X is a real
vector space and A is a ω-algebra on X such that:
1. The addition map + : X × X →X is A ⊗A-measurable.
2. The scalar multiplication map · : R × X →X is B(R) ⊗A-measurable.
Measurable vector spaces are defined in analogy with topological vector spaces, replacing
continuity with measurability. The concept is mentioned briefly in [6, p. 52] and developed
in more detail in [37]. We define random vectors in analogy with random variables.
Definition 2.2. Let (!, F) be a measurable space, and let (X, AX) be a measurable
vector space. A random vector is a mapping ε : ! →X that is (F, AX)-measurable.
In classical measure theory, a ω-algebra A of measurable sets is typically fixed and taken
as the domain of a measure µ. However, in infinite-dimensional vector spaces, the choice
of domain is a delicate matter, and simply extending the measure µ to a larger ω-algebra
is not always resolved by completing A with respect to µ. For details, see [6, Chapter 7].
To take advantage of finite-dimensional reductions, it will often be convenient to work with
a ω-algebra that is generated by a collection of real-valued functions Γ ⊆RX on X. It is
particularly useful when X lacks a natural topology, or when the Borel ω-algebra B(X) on
X induced by the topology is too fine for practical purposes.

3
Probabilistic Framework for Inverse
Problems
In this chapter, we develop a probabilistic framework for inverse problems formulated in
the language of measure theory. The motivation for adopting a probabilistic framework
arises naturally from the observation that physical measurements are often contaminated
by observational noise, which introduces aleatoric or random uncertainty into the model.
In addition, uncertainty may arise from incomplete knowledge about the underlying system
or model parameters—this is known as epistemic uncertainty.
To develop the probabilistic framework, let (X, AX) and (Y, AY ) be measurable vector
spaces, and let the mapping G : X →Y be (AX, AY )-measurable. A natural model for
inverse problems in the presence of additive noise is given by
y = G(x) + η,
(3.1)
where η is a Y -valued random vector, referred to as the observational noise. We assume
that η is distributed according to a probability measure ν on (Y, AY ). Because (Y, AY ) is
a measurable vector space, the mapping y = G(x)+ η defines a Y -valued random vector
for each fixed x ∈X.
The additive noise model suggests that for every fixed x ∈X, the observed data y is a
random variable distributed according to a probability measure µx on (Y, AY ) depending
on x. We refer to µx as the likelihood of the data y given the unknown x. The likelihood
serves a similar purpose as the data misfit term in a variational approach.
To perform statistical inference on the unknown x from the data y, we charge the unknown
x with a prior probability measure γ on (X, AX). The prior γ encodes our prior knowledge
or assumptions about the unknown x before observing the data y and serves a similar
purpose as the regularization term in a variational approach.
The problem of inferring the unknown x from the observed data y now becomes a problem
of determining the “conditional” distribution of x given the data y. We use quotation marks
because the interpretation of conditional distributions in probability theory is subject to
philosophical discussion; see, for example, Hájek [18] and Meehan et al. [28].
Let (!, A, µ) be a probability space. Kolmogorov’s axiomatization of probability theory
includes a definition of conditional probability via the familiar ratio formula:

3.1
Transition Probabilities
In this section, we introduce the measure-theoretic machinery necessary to formulate
a probabilistic framework for inverse problems. We define transition probabilities and
extend the concept of product measures to the case where one of the measures is given
by a transition probability. Although transition probabilities are not (regular) conditional
probability measures, they are closely related and arguably better suited for describing
the probability distribution of y given a fixed x ∈X.
Our goal is to construct a probability space (!, A, µ) which is sufficiently rich to support
two independent random vectors ε : ! →X and η : ! →Y , distributed according to γ
and ν, respectively. While such a probability space is not unique, the construction in this
section serves both as the foundation for our definition of probabilistic inverse problems
and as the basis for extending Bayes’ theorem to infinite-dimensional vector spaces.
A comprehensive treatment of transition probabilities can be found in the two-volume
monograph by Bogachev [6, Chapter 10.7].

3.3

We conclude this chapter by showing that the probabilistic inverse problem introduced in
the previous section generalizes the classical Bayesian inverse problem. The extension
of Bayes’ theorem to infinite-dimensional vector spaces has been studied by Stuart [35],

analysis and computation.

a ω-finite measure in infinite-dimensional spaces. Although this may not be a significant

5
Gaussian Processes as Gaussian
Measures
In order to use Gaussian measures as prior and likelihood in practice for probabilistic
inverse problems, we must develop methods for constructing such measures on Banach
or Hilbert spaces that can be discretized. Previously, we saw that Gaussian measures on
separable Hilbert spaces can be constructed via the Karhunen–Loève expansion, using
the eigensystem of a self-adjoint, non-negative, trace-class operator. While this approach
lends itself to discretization through truncation of the series, its applicability is limited to
settings where an explicit orthonormal basis is available.
In this chapter, we present an alternative approach to discretizing Gaussian measures
using kernel-based methods and explore their close connection to the Karhunen-Loève
expansion. Although kernel-based methods and Gaussian process priors are widely used
in Bayesian inverse problems, a rigorous theoretical foundation for linear inverse problems
has only recently been established in [30, 27]. Our goal is to provide a rigorous treatment
of kernel-based methods and to provide a new perspective as restrictions of Gaussian
measures to suitable subspaces inspired by [7, Example 2.3.11].
We start by reviewing the basic theory of stochastic processes, focusing on their standard
interpretation as (RT , E(RT ))-valued random functions. Here, RT denotes the space of
real-valued functions on a non-empty index set T, equipped with the usual operations of
pointwise addition and scalar multiplication. When endowed with the topology of pointwise
convergence, RT becomes a locally convex space; see Appendix A for details. While this
perspective might be sufficient for statistics, it is inadequate for inverse problems, because
RT is generally too large for most interesting operators to be well-defined.
To address this limitation, we derive sufficient conditions under which a Gaussian measure
on (RT , E(RT )) can be restricted to a suitable Banach space where the forward operator
is well-defined. We conclude by examining the Hilbert space setting and provide our own
proof of the sufficiency direction of Driscoll’s Theorem, which reveals a deep connection
between kernel-based constructions and the Karhunen–Loéve expansion.

5.2
Restriction of Gaussian Measures to Trace-σ-algebras
In this section, we investigate conditions under which a Gaussian measure on (RT , E(RT ))
can be restricted to a Banach subspace X ⊆RT . We begin by developing intuition for
the types of conditions that a restriction theorem for probability measures should impose.
Building on this foundation, we establish sufficient conditions under which a probability
measure on (RT , E(RT )) admits a restriction to a Banach subspace (X, E(X)). We show
that under these conditions, the restriction of a Gaussian measure remains Gaussian.
In general, a Banach subspace X ⊆RT is not measurable; that is, X /∈E(RT ). Therefore,
any useful restriction theorem should accommodate the possibility of restricting to a non-
measurable subset. This motivates the following definition:

and only if !0 ∈A. Hence if !0 ∈A and µ(!0) = 1, then by restricting the domain of a
probability measure µ on (!, A) to (!0, A) we obtain again a probability measure.

assign a measure to !0, but we still need to ensure that !0 has ”full measure” in a suitable
sense. A natural generalization is to use the concept of outer measure, defined by

5.3
Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces
In this section, we derive several useful results for simplifying the theory of restrictions
of Gaussian measures to trace-ω-algebras in the Hilbert space setting. The presented
results can be found in the foundational work of Aronszajn [3], with additional insights
from Berlinet et al. [5].
Definition 5.8. A Hilbert space H ⊆RT , equipped with an inner product (·, ·), is called a
reproducing kernel Hilbert space with reproducing kernel K : T × T →R if:
1. K(·, t) ∈H for all t ∈T;
2. (x, K(·, t)) = x(t) for all x ∈H and t ∈T.
The last condition is called the reproducing property in the view that x(t) is reproduced
by the inner product of u with K(·, t). In particular, a reproducing kernel reproduces itself:
(K(·, t), K(·, τ)) = K(τ, t) = K(t, τ),
∀τ, t ∈T.

clarifies the terminology introduced in the previous section. We prefer the latter, because
the reproducing property is evident from the definition. The following theorem shows that
the two definitions are equivalent.

The intuition behind the necessity part is that if ι is not a Hilbert–Schmidt operator, then
necessarily γ∗(HK) < 1. Since HK is a linear subspace, it follows from the zero-one
law for Gaussian measures that γ∗(HK) ∈{0, 1}, and therefore γ∗(HK) = 0. For further
details on zero-one laws for Gaussian measures, we refer to [7, Chapter 2.5].
The kernel representation of covariance operators, together with Driscoll’s Theorem as
developed in this section, raises important questions about the role of Gaussian processes
as prior distributions in statistics—particularly in the context of inverse problems. This is
because, for a sufficiently regular positive semi-definite function R: T × T →R, one
can construct a reproducing kernel Hilbert space HK large enough that the canonical
embedding ι: HR →HK is Hilbert–Schmidt. This guarantees the existence of a Gaussian
measure on HK with covariance operator having kernel R.
As we will see in Chapter 7, this condition can be verified for specific kernels using Sobolev
embedding theorems. A more general approach, introduced by Steinwart et al. [34], is
based on Mercer’s theorem and spectral powers of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces.
These ideas have been further developed by Kanagawa et al. [19, 20] to analyze Gaussian
processes and kernel-based methods more generally.
Perhaps the appeal of Gaussian processes lies in the flexibility afforded by the choice of
covariance function or kernel. However, this freedom is short-lived, as one pays the price
of working in the locally convex space RT .

Application to System Identification
In this chapter, we apply the probabilistic framework developed in this thesis, together
with kernel-based methods, to a classical problem in system identification. The aim is to
recover the impulse response x of a linear time-invariant system from noisy observations
of its output y, given a known input u. The system is modelled by the Volterra equation of
the first kind:

The application of kernel-based methods to system identification can be traced back to
Pillonetto et al. [31], who introduced a new kernel function designed to encode desirable
properties of the impulse response. A Gaussian process equipped with this kernel was
used as a prior in a Bayesian formulation of the identification problem. Since the forward
operator involves an integral operator, one must ensure that the integral is well-defined.
This amounts to verifying that sample paths of the Gaussian process belong to an Lp
space. To this end, the authors conducted a spectral analysis of the kernel and showed
that the sample paths almost surely belong to Lp for all p ≥1 (see [31, Proposition 10]).
However, as we have already seen, Gaussian processes are not always the most natural
way to model the prior, particularly in the context of inverse problems. In this chapter, we
offer an alternative perspective by showing that the same covariance kernel gives rise to
a Gaussian measure on an exponentially weighted Sobolev space. This construction not
only restricts the prior to a subspace of Lp where the convolution operator is well-defined,
but also provides a rigorous foundation for stronger regularity properties of the sample
paths, including weak differentiability and exponentially weighted integrability.

8
Conclusion
In this thesis, we have developed a novel probabilistic framework for inverse problems and
established a general existence and uniqueness theorem under mild assumptions. We
derived an explicit solution for the case where both the prior and likelihood are Gaussian
measures on separable Hilbert spaces. This approach is grounded in measure-theoretic
probability and avoids discretization until the last possible moment, thereby preserving
the infinite-dimensional structure of the problem and ensuring mathematical rigor.
A key contribution of this work is the formal unification of a probabilistic framework and
kernel-based methods. By representing the covariance operator of a Gaussian measure
by a covariance kernel, we obtain a computationally tractable method of exact posterior
computations in infinite-dimensional spaces using only finite-dimensional linear algebra.
This discussion reveals a deep connection between Gaussian processes and Gaussian
measures on reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces.
To demonstrate the utility of the probabilistic framework in practical settings, we applied
it to a classical problem in system identification. We showed that the second-order sta-
ble spline kernel is the covariance kernel of a Gaussian measure on an exponentially
weighted Sobolev space. This result not only provides a rigorous mathematical founda-
tion for using the kernel as a Gaussian prior in system identification, but also significantly
strengthens existing insights into the regularity and integrability of the associated sample
paths. These theoretical insights were further supported by numerical experiments. In
both cases, the exact impulse response was within the 99% pointwise credible interval,
and the posterior mean provided a close approximation to the true solution.
While this thesis establishes a rigorous and tractable framework for infinite-dimensional
inverse problems, several important directions remain for future work.
In Chapter 3, we provided existence and uniqueness results for solutions to probabilistic
inverse problems. However, we did not address the stability of the posterior with respect
to the choice of prior, likelihood, or perturbations of the observed data. Understanding
how posterior inferences respond to such modeling choices and perturbations is crucial
for ensuring the robustness and reliability of the framework in practice. Closely related to
this is the question of posterior convergence as the amount of data increases.
A central assumption underlying the computational aspects of this thesis is the use of
Gaussian measures and linear forward operators. While convenient for computation, we
acknowledge that many interesting problems may involve non-Gaussian measures and
non-linear operators. Developing methods to handle these problems, through analytical
approximations or sampling techniques, is a significant challenge for future work.
Finally, hyperparameter estimation of the prior and likelihood covariance kernels remains
a significant computational challenge. While line search suffices for optimization over
one parameter, real-world applications often require optimization over several parameters
under tight computational constraints. Addressing this effectively is essential for applying
the framework to more complex and practical scenarios.
