Abstract
Green hydrogen plays a vital role in modern energy systems, serving as an important bridge
between energy and transportation, and the chemical industry, where proton exchange mem-
brane (PEM) electrolyzers are particularly advantageous due to their high efficiency and fast
response times compared to Alkaline electrolyzers.
These characteristics make PEM electrolyzers are more suited for integration into power sys-
tems dominated by renewable energy sources.
Considering the low inertia and fluctuating
characteristics of renewable energy sources, the impact of large-scale electrolyzer integration on
the renewables-domaineted power systems (RPS) needs be thoroughly analyzed.
This study aims to analyze the integration of large-scale PEM electrolyzers into renewable
energy-dominated power systems (RPS) and their impact on system stability.
Firstly, a dynamic model of a 1 MW PEM electrolyzer is established, detailing its working
principles and interactions with the power grid.
Subsequently, a two-stage AC/DC converter and its control strategy are developed to ensure
the stable operation of the electrolyzer, resulting in a comprehensive MW-level electrolyzer unit
model.
Finally, by analyzing the dynamic frequency response of power systems, a frequency response
model for RPS is established, and a dynamic frequency regulation control for electrolyzers is
developed.
The study results indicate that high-power electrolyzers significantly impact the stability of
RPS, and the proposed control strategy effectively enhances system stability. This research
provides theoretical support and practical guidance for the application of PEM electrolyzers in
RPS.

1
Introduction
1.1
Background
As the global demand for clean energy and sustainable development continues to grow, elec-
tricity consumption has become a central aspect of modern society. Since the Industrial Revo-
lution, electricity usage has dramatically increased, driven by ongoing population growth and
industrial development. Fig.1.1 shows the global energy consumption. This surge in electricity
consumption has become a crucial indicator of socio-economic stability and growth [1]. As this
background, more renewable energy is needed.
Figure 1.1: Global energy consumption.
Power-to-X refers to the process of transforming renewable electricity, such as that generated
from wind, into a different form of energy (denoted as ’X’). This ’X’ typically manifests as an
energy carrier, with renewable hydrogen being the most common product.[2]. Hydrogen is an
energy storage medium for efficiently storing and transporting intermittent renewable energy
sources (e.g., solar and wind) and a zero-emission energy source, which is crucial for reducing
greenhouse gas emissions.
Over the past decade, many hydrogen energy demonstration projects have emerged worldwide to
explore sustainable energy solutions. Hydrogen is a non-toxic gas, allowing for enhanced safety
during storage and transportation[3]. Since hydrogen can be generated from renewable energy
systems (RESs), it is considered clean hydrogen, helping to reduce greenhouse gas emissions[4].
Among the methods of hydrogen production, water electrolysis is a key technology.
This
process, which breaks down water into hydrogen and oxygen through electricity, is highly
efficient and environmentally friendly when powered by renewable energy sources. As depicted
in Figure 1.2, water electrolysis can be coupled with various renewable energy sources such as
wind, solar, and hydro energy, highlighting its versatility in clean energy applications. The
electrolyser serves as a pivotal component in this setup, converting renewable electricity into
1
==PAGE:11==
clean energy stored in hydrogen, which can then be utilized across different domains, including
transportation, energy storage, and industrial processes.
Currently, there are three main types of electrolysers used in water electrolysis: alkaline elec-
trolysers, proton exchange membrane (PEM) electrolysers, and solid oxide (SO) electrolysers,
each with unique characteristics suitable for different applications in the Power-to-X landscape.
Figure 1.2: Applications based on water electrolysis process supplied by renewable energy
sources [5].
Alkaline electrolysers are the most traditional and mature technology and have been used for
decades. However, they operate at lower temperatures, have slower reaction rates, and are
relatively inefficient. Solid oxide electrolysers operate at higher temperatures and are more
efficient, but they are more expensive and durability is a challenge.
PEM electrolysers show a number of significant advantages over both types of electrolysers,
these are mainly reflected in Ref. [6].
1. High working current density, which contributes to the direct separation of reactant hy-
drogen and oxygen, avoiding cross-gassing and ensuring the safety and high purity of the
product gas.
2. High electrolysis efficiency, with a compact electrode spacing that reduces working voltage
and energy consumption, resulting in a more compact electrolytic cell structure.
3. Non-polluting reaction, with the use of pure water as the electrolyte, which eliminates
corrosion to the tank body and avoids the production of alkali mist, ensuring higher gas
purity.
4. Light weight, as water serves both as a reactant and a cooling medium, negating the need
for an additional cooling system and thus reducing the overall volume and weight of the
device.
2
==PAGE:12==
5. Compact structure, facilitated by the use of a solid polymer electrolyte membrane that
can withstand large pressure differences and has unidirectional conduction effects on
hydrogen ions.
1.2
Research objective
The paper aims to analyze PEM electrolyzer interactions within a renewable energy-dominated
power system (RPS), exploring methods to enhance the system’s frequency stability, focusing
on the following key research tasks:
• Analyzing the characteristics of the PEM electrolyzer and developing a model that presents
its dynamic response features.
• Designing a closed-loop controller for the PEM electrolyzer and optimizing the controller
parameters based on the PEM’s characteristics.
• Investigating the impact of the PEM electrolyzer on the stability of RPS and examining
dynamic frequency regulation control to improve frequency stability.
1.3
Thesis structural
This thesis is structured into four main chapters. Chapter 1 introduces the background and
objectives. Chapter 2 focuses on the modeling of PEM electrolysers, presenting both dynamic
and static models, and developing a 1MW dynamic PEM stack model. Chapter 3 addresses the
modeling and control of electrolyser converters, including AC and DC converters, culminating in
a 1MW PEM electrolyser unit model. Chapter 4 analyzes the stability of renewable-dominated
power systems, introducing SFR and DFR models and proposing a hybrid SFR model for
enhanced stability.

Modeling of PEM Electrolyser
With the advancement of electrolysers, modeling has become crucial. This technology can be
applied to rectifier design, DC/DC converter development, off-grid hydrogen production system
optimization, and grid frequency regulation. This chapter introduces the current status of the
research and subsequently derives both static and dynamic models of electrolysers.

State of the art

into hydrogen ions and oxygen gas. The hydrogen ions move through the membrane to the

PEM electrolyser modeling approaches can be categorized into static and dynamic simula-
tions[8]. Static models assume that all state variables remain constant over time, offering a
general evaluation of cell and stack performance.
One notable static model is the semi-empirical model for alkline electrolyser first proposed by
Ulleberg in 1998, which integrates thermodynamics with empirical electrochemical expressions
[9]. In 2003, with further discoveries highlighting the significant impact of temperature on
5
==PAGE:15==
electrolyser performance, Ulleberg modified the original semi-empirical model to incorporate
temperature and electrode area parameters [10], thereby enhancing its accuracy and applica-
bility. This method is also applicable to PEM electrolysers, as noted in Ref. [11]. Despite its
accuracy, Ulleberg’s model does not consider gas pressure, which also influences performance.
Therefore, some authors in Ref [12] modified the model and considered the influence of gas
pressure, making it more comprehensive and reliable.
Compared to the static model, dynamic models account for the temporal variations of state
variables, capturing the transient behavior of the electrolyser during renewable hydrogen pro-
duction.PEM electrolysers can be connected to renewable power sources, which are subject to
uncertainties and generation fluctuations, resulting in transient operating conditions. There-
fore, it is necessary to use a dynamic model to simulate transient behavior and analyze the
changes under these uncertain operating conditions.
Ref [13] utilized Simulink to create a dynamic model of a PEM electrolyser, focusing on mass
and energy conservation at both the anode and cathode. This model, rooted in simulations,
was validated through experiments that examined the transient dynamic behavior of the PEM
electrolyser. Based on this work, the authors in Ref. [14] proposed an RC parallel structure,
illustrated in Figure 2.4. In this model, R1 and R2 represent the Faraday resistance of the
electrode surfaces, and C1 and C2 represent the double-layer capacitance formed anode and
cathode electrode surfaces, respectively.
In summary, the evolution of PEM electrolyser modeling from static to dynamic models has
significantly enhanced the understanding and optimization of these systems. The following
sections will introduce the derivation of both static and dynamic models.
2.2
Static model
The static model for a PEM electrolyser describes the relationship between the cell voltage and
the various over-potentials that occur during operation. The cell voltage of a PEM electrolyser
is the sum of several potential components, including the reversible voltage, the activation
overvoltage, the ohmic overvoltage, and the concentration overvoltage. These overpotentials
arise from the kinetics of the electronic charge transfer, mass transfer, and ohmic losses in the
electrolyte. The relationship can be expressed as [15]:
Ucell = Urev + Uact + Uohm + Ucon
(2.4)
where Urev [V] is the reversible voltage. Uact [V], Uohm [V], and Ucon [V] are the activation,
ohmic. It has been reported that Ucon is much lower than Uohm and Vact, for this reason, many
authors take Ucon = 0 in their studies [14, 16].

Modelling and control of Electrolyser Converter
The power converter serves as the interface between the power grid and the electrolyser stacks,
converting the three-phase AC input to a DC output for the stacks. Given the wide voltage
and current range required by the stack, this paper employs a two-stage AC-DC converter to
supply the necessary DC input for the electrolyser. The first-stage AC-DC converter provides
a stable DC link voltage, while the second-stage converter regulates the voltage for the stack,
as illustrated in Figure 3.1. This section will elaborate on how this nonlinearity necessitates
specific design requirements for the electrolyser converter. The inherent nonlinearity of the
electrolyser’s polarization curve and the hysteresis characteristics of electrolysers voltage would
require an appropriate converter control design to ensure electrolyser’s reliable and efficient
operation.
Input system technical parameters include input power (Prated) of 1 MW, line frequency (fline)
of 50 Hz, line-to-line voltage (Vrated) of 400 V, Rc of 0.001 Ω, DC link voltage (Vdc) of 1200 V,
and switching frequency (fsw) of 10 kHz.

3.2.2
Controller design
The controller comprises three stages of power-voltage-current, as shown in Figure 3.10. The
PI controllers are implemented in the voltage and current control loop. The error between
the reference voltage and the actual value is processed by a PI controller, which outputs the
reference value for the current loop.
The output range of the PI controller is constrained
to ensure the current reference value remains within limits. The error between the current
reference value and the actual current value is then processed by another PI controller to
obtain the duty ratio of the switch. The output range of the current PI controller is restricted
to 0 to 1, ensuring that the switch does not enter a short-circuit state. The thesis employs the
ratio of the power reference to the current reference as the voltage reference value, leveraging
the relationship where power equals the product of voltage and current. This method is chosen
for two key reasons. First, the high bandwidth of the current loop allows the actual current to
closely match its reference value. Second, using the current reference value ensures it remains
non-zero during startup, thanks to the control loop’s limiter, thus avoiding any issues with the
divider’s operation. Additionally, a limiter is implemented in the power control loop to prevent
the voltage reference from becoming excessively large, protecting both the converter and the
electrolyzer from potential damage.
To respond to fluctuations in the frequency of the grid and adjust the power consumption of
the electrolyser, the ratio of the power reference value to the current reference value is used as
a reference for the voltage control loop. The current reference value, rather than the actual
current value, is used as it is constrained to a minimum of 100. This prevents issues with the
divider not functioning properly during startup when the actual current is zero.

4.1
Frequency Response of Renewables-Dominated Power Systems
In the event of a frequency disturbance in a power system, all governor-controlled generators
work together to restore the system frequency to its nominal value. For modeling purposes,
all generators in a power system can be represented as a single equivalent generator, with an
inertia constant equal to the sum of the inertia constants of all individual generators. Addition-
ally, the overall frequency response of the power system also depends on the combined droop
characteristics of all the generators’ speed governors [30]. An equivalent droop (RG) can be
calculated to represent the aggregate effect of all individual droops in the system.
To analyze the system frequency when there is a mismatch between instantaneous generation
and demand, a typical generation unit, as shown in Fig.4.1, includes a speed governor, turbine,
rotor inertia, and load. In the event of a frequency disturbance in a power system, all governor-
controlled generators collectively act to restore the system frequency to its nominal value.

ensure stable operation. This chapter culminates in the development of a comprehensive MW-
level electrolyzer unit model that includes both the converter and the electrolyzer stack.
Chapter 4 begins by analyzing the dynamic frequency response of power systems and estab-
lishing a frequency response model for RPS. Using the FCR-N frequency provision in DK2 as
a reference, a dynamic frequency regulation control for electrolyzers is developed and applied
to the proposed MW-level electrolyzer unit model. The impact of high-power electrolysers on
the stability of RPS is then analyzed within this frequency response model. Simulation results
have demonstrated that this strategy can improve system stability.

Future work
The proposed DFR strategy from PEM electrolysers could be further examined in realistic and
complicated power systems, to prove its effectiveness more realistically. Some well-validated
IEEE benchmark systems could be considered such as the IEEE 9-BUS system or larger systems
One avenue for future research is to conduct a parametric sensitivity analysis to identify key
parameters affecting the performance and stability of the electrolyser grid integration. This
includes parameters such as the Short Circuit Ratio (SCR) of the power grid, dynamic perfor-
mance parameters of the electrolyser, and converter parameters. By quantitatively analyzing
their impact, we can understand their influence mechanisms and subsequently optimize these
parameters from both a control and parameter optimization perspective to enhance system
stability.
Another direction is the control design for large-scale PEM plants. This involves considering
the differential operation of multiple units within the plant and optimizing their grid integration
performance and grid service capabilities from a coordinated control perspective. By focusing
on the synergistic control of these units, it is possible to improve their collective performance
and contribution to grid stability and service.
Future research can also conduct detailed cost-benefit analyses and lifecycle assessments to
evaluate the sustainability and economic viability of integrating PEM electrolysers into the
power grid.
