Abstract
Integrating Renewable Energy Sources (RES) into modern energy systems requires flexible
energy systems capable of adapting to the unpredictability of renewable energy produc-
tion. One strategy for addressing this challenge is through Coupled Multi-Energy Systems
(CMES), developed to co-optimize the use of various energy sources and carriers, poten-
tially improving the entire system’s flexibility, eﬀiciency, and security. However, effective
decision making in a CMES with the inherent uncertainty of RES production requires
appropriate tools for decision-makers to make informed decisions without complete infor-
mation.
This thesis investigates the benefit of applying stochastic programming to model and ad-
dress wind power production uncertainty in a CMES with gas, power, heat, and hydrogen
as energy carriers. A policy is created to allocate energy production in the CMES while
minimizing the expected system cost over a simulation horizon of 24 hours.
The pol-
icy models a sequential decision making process, which determines the optimal dispatch
decisions for the CMES for the entire simulation horizon. This is done by recursively
solving the Multistage Stochastic Economic Dispatch (MSED) problem for a shorter look-
ahead horizon and implementing the “here-and-now” decisions as effective decisions. The
stochasticity of wind power production is modeled in the policy by incorporating 1455
normalized wind power production scenarios reduced using K-medoids clustering for each
stage in the look-ahead horizon.
The policy is tested using a case study system and 45 simulated wind power realizations. A
[1, 4, 2, 2, 2]-scenario tree with a look-ahead horizon of four hours is selected as the optimal
base case configuration for the case study system. The policy’s performance is evaluated
by comparing the total system cost of implementing the effective decisions obtained from
the policy with those obtained from the Expected Value (EV) and Optimal-in-Hindsight
(OiH) benchmark programs. The evaluation shows that applying the stochastic policy on
the case study system resulted in a total system cost of 45,376,133 DKK. The solution
of the benchmark programs demonstrates limited potential for improvement by using the
stochastic policy due to the slight difference of 439,159 DKK between the costs of the EV
and OiH solutions. Nevertheless, using the stochastic policy on the case study system
leads to a 0.11 percent reduction in system cost compared to the EV program, which
amounts to an average daily saving of 43,879 DKK. The relatively small cost reduction
and room for improvement for the stochastic policy can primarily be explained by the high
flexibility of the case study CMES, which enables it to adapt eﬀiciently to less-than-ideal
decisions across any solution type.
This thesis demonstrates that adjusting the algorithm used for planning operations of
the CMES from a deterministic model to a stochastic one can reduce total system costs.
With further refinement of the model, the cost reduction is expected to increase for the
stochastic solution.

1.1
Context and Contribution
In recent years, there has been an increasing global focus on climate change. This has led
to a worldwide green transition that promotes sustainable development and advocates for
greater use of Renewable Energy Sources (RES). However, many of these energy sources are
non-dispatchable in the sense that their energy generation cannot be scheduled to match
the energy demand. This aspect of the green energy transition introduces uncertainty into
energy systems, which brings significant technical challenges [1]. Therefore, making RES
the primary sources of energy generation requires systems that can adjust to varying levels
of renewable energy as well as planning tools to assist in decision making when energy
production is uncertain.
One strategy for addressing these challenges is to utilize the synergies between different en-
ergy systems through Coupled Multi-Energy Systems (CMES) [2]. CMESs are developed
to coordinate and optimize the use of various energy sources and carriers, potentially
improving the flexibility, eﬀiciency, and security of the entire system.
However, inte-
grating different energy carriers creates planning complexities for the overall system [3].
Eﬀicient planning and decision making within complex energy systems require appropri-
ate tools. Mathematical programming provides a structured approach that helps optimize
large-scale problems while ensuring that the complex constraints of these systems are met.
However, the unpredictable nature of renewable energy production introduces additional
challenges for decision-makers, who must make decisions without complete information.
Stochastic programming expands upon mathematical programming and provides a tai-
lored framework to model and address uncertainty. This equips decision-makers to make
more informed decisions under uncertainty, ultimately enhancing the eﬀiciency and cost-
effectiveness of managing energy systems [4].
Existing literature has already investigated the advantages of co-optimizing a multi-energy
system instead of optimizing each energy system separately. This project, however, fo-
cuses on two key aspects. Firstly, it creates a linear model for a complex coupled energy
system with various energy carriers. This model, along with a representation of the unpre-
dictability of wind power production, is used to address the stochastic Economic Dispatch
(ED) problem for the system. Secondly, the project implements a multistage planning
process, which involves making a series of decisions over a longer period to enhance the
system’s performance.
Specifically, the objective of this master’s thesis is to create a policy for minimizing the
expected system cost of a CMES with gas, power, heat, and hydrogen as energy carriers
over a simulation horizon, taking into account the impact of uncertain wind production.
Along with a modular codebase for the policy, it provides a foundation for other researchers
to build upon for examining decision making under uncertainty in CMESs.

1.2
Thesis Objectives
The process of making decisions under uncertainty, particularly in optimizing dispatch
decisions for a CMES over a defined time horizon, can be summarized in three primary
steps as illustrated in Figure 1.1. These steps outline the research goals and methods for
this thesis, and are detailed in the following.
1.
Modeling the System and Identifying Decisions: The initial phase focuses
on understanding the dynamics of the CMES and identifying decisions.
The primary
focus is on dispatch decisions, and the goal is to optimize these decisions to achieve the
most cost-effective operation of the CMES. Additionally, this step involves identifying and
characterizing the uncertainty present within the system.
This project focuses on the
uncertainty related to wind power production. Hence, a forecast model is necessary to
accurately predict wind power’s behavior over the planning period.
2.
Formulating a Solvable Model: The next step involves converting the system
model into a form that can be solved analytically. For this project, this will include:
i.
Formulating the optimization problem for minimizing system cost over a time horizon
as a Multistage Economic Dispatch (MED) problem.
ii.
Extending the optimization problem to a Multistage Stochastic Economic Dispatch
(MSED) problem, which considers uncertain wind power production.
iii. Reducing the complexity of the MSED problem to make it computationally tractable.
3. Solving the Problem: The final step involves solving the MSED problem by convert-
ing the mathematical program into source code and solving it using an algebraic solver.
This is implemented using the Python programming language. In addition to implement-
ing the model in code, this step also includes developing a case study system for a CMES,
which can serve as a basis for testing the model.
This requires conducting literature
research to create a dataset.

Stochastic Programming
Stochastic Programming (SP) is rooted in mathematical programming and is a powerful
decision making tool for solving large-scale constrained optimization problems under un-
certainty. In SP, the optimal “here-and-now” decisions are determined by considering a
range of possible future scenarios of uncertainty realizations and strategically allocating
resources to ensure both adaptability and effectiveness of the system [6]. Consequently,
the quality of the optimal decisions from the SP is tightly linked to how well the possible
future outcomes of uncertainty are represented in the program.
Uncertain future outcomes are often described by uncertain data, which needs to be trans-
formed into a form that can be implemented in an SP. This uncertain data is represented
by stochastic processes, which can be continuous in time and have a continuous state
space. However, solving a large-scale optimization problem involving continuous stochas-
tic processes is technically impossible. Therefore, converting the continuous stochastic
process into a discrete approximation is usually required before finding a solution to the
SP. To achieve this, a continuous stochastic process, ξ, can be approximated by a discrete
process, λ. For this discrete process, λ(ω), represents a realization for a specific scenario
in a finite set, ω = {1, 2, . . . , N}, where ω is an index for the scenarios, and N is the
total number of scenarios considered. Each scenario is associated with a probability of
occurrence, π(ω), representing the likelihood of that scenario occurring, and all possi-
ble outcomes are accounted for by &N
ω=1 π(ω) = 1. This discrete representation of the
stochastic process allows for its integration into the optimization framework. Thus, the
deterministic equivalent program can be used to model the SP.
Multistage Stochastic Programming
A Multistage Stochastic Program (MSP) provides a framework for a sequential decision-
making process. Each stage in the program represents a point in time where decisions can
be made, and as more information is revealed over time, the program is updated to reflect
the gained knowledge. An MSP incorporates this sequential decision-making process by
allowing initial here-and-now decisions to be modified with “recourse” decisions as new
information becomes available in subsequent stages [7].

The information structure of the decision making process can be illustrated using a sce-
nario tree as shown in Figure 2.1. The tree consists of nodes and branches, where each
branch represents an uncertainty realization with an associated probability, and each node
represents a decision point. The root node marks the first stage decisions made at the

An essential aspect of SP is that decisions within the program at any given stage must
be based only on the information available until that stage without presuming knowledge
of future events. This aspect is called the principle of “non-anticipativity”. Constraining
an SP by the principle of non-anticipativity is imperative for obtaining valid solutions.
The non-anticipativity constraints for each stage of the program can be derived from the
scenario tree by creating subsets of scenarios that share a common path until that stage,
i.e., share the same history.
2.2
Uncertainty Modeling via Scenarios
Addressing uncertainty via scenarios in an SP framework involves two main steps, as this
chapter intends to demonstrate. The first step, presented in Subsection 2.2.1, aims to ac-
curately model the stochastic behavior of the uncertain variable. This involves generating
a comprehensive set of scenarios, each representing a likely realization of the uncertain
variable during the planning period. This step usually requires generating numerous sce-
narios to fully describe the realization space, which leads to computational challenges in
solving the SP. Consequently, the second step, presented in Subsection 2.3.1, aims to re-
duce the large set of scenarios to a manageable set of representative ones to maintain the
original scenarios’ underlying characteristics.
2.2.1
Scenarios
This thesis addresses the uncertainty associated with wind power, an inherently volatile
parameter due to the stochastic nature of wind patterns and the process of converting
wind energy into power [8]. Given the increasing expansion of wind power capacity and the
complexities in describing the nature of wind patterns, techniques for accurately predicting
wind power have been and continue to be a significant area of research as described in [9],
[10], and [11].

2.3
Solution Approach
When dealing with n stochastic variables, each with m possible outcomes, the number of
branches per stage is b = mn. As this is extended to T stages, the total number of scenarios
increases exponentially to b(T−1) = mn·(T−1). The exponential increase in the number of
scenarios at each stage quickly makes an SP computationally intractable. Consequently,
the main challenge in MSP is reducing computational complexity without significantly
compromising the model’s accuracy. This thesis aims to solve the Multistage Stochastic
Economic Dispatch (MSED) program using a clustering method to reduce the number of
scenarios and a “Rolling Horizon” method to reduce the number of stages. The details of
these methods are discussed in this section.
2.3.1
K-medoids Clustering Technique
In Subsection 2.2.1, the 1500 scenarios used in this project are described. The scenar-
ios were divided into two groups: 1455 were allocated for in-sample data use, while the
remaining 45 were reserved for out-of-sample testing. The plots of the scenarios for the
out-of-sample testing are provided in Appendix A.2. The main challenge is reducing the
1455 in-sample scenarios to a computationally tractable number while still preserving
information about the entire sample. This thesis addresses this challenge by using the
K-medoids clustering technique [14].
Clustering techniques are a category of unsupervised learning algorithms designed to par-
tition data points into groups, called “clusters,” such that data points assigned to the same
cluster share more similarities than to data points assigned to any other cluster, as mea-
sured by their pairwise dissimilarities [15]. Two frequently applied clustering techniques
are K-means and K-medoids clustering.
K-medoids clustering is a method that represents a cluster with the data point, known
as the “medoid”, which is the most centrally located data point within the cluster. It is
similar to the K-means clustering technique, which uses the average of all points in the
cluster, the “centroid,” to represent the cluster.
Despite K-medoids having higher computational cost than K-means, this project uses K-
medoids as the clustering technique. The decision to opt for K-medoids over K-means
clustering is based on the difference in cluster representation [16]. K-medoids clustering
assigns an actual data point to represent each cluster, thereby preserving the genuine
variations and patterns within the wind power scenarios. Furthermore, K-medoids clus-
ters demonstrate greater robustness towards noise and outliers, whereas K-means clusters
may take values significantly divergent from the data points within the cluster [16]. How-
ever, both clustering techniques will be empirically tested, and their performance will be
compared in Subsection 5.1.2.

The structure of a scenario tree is defined by two parameters: the number of stages,
ti, and the number of child nodes, Kti, that each node branches into at stage t [17].
The methodology employed in this study to construct the scenario tree based on the two
parameters is outlined below. It uses the widely adopted Partitioning Around Medoids
(PAM) as a K-Medoids clustering algorithm [14].
Step 1: Observation of Wind Power
The realization of wind power at stage t1 is observed, defining the root node of the scenario
tree. Sample scenarios for the progression of the wind power observed in t1 are selected
based on this realization and a predefined filtration value representing the maximum devi-
ation observed in the normalized wind power values between two consecutive time steps.
All scenarios at this stage are considered equiprobable.
Step 2: Scenario Reduction using K-medoids
At stage t + 1, the scenarios are reduced to Kt medoids employing the K-medoids clus-
tering technique, utilizing Euclidean distance for measuring pairwise dissimilarities. The
occurrence probability for each medoid is computed as the aggregated probabilities of
the scenarios within its cluster. This reduction process and probability assignment are
repeated at subsequent stages (t + 2, . . . , T), following the same methodology.
Step 3: Derivation of Final Scenarios
By stage T, the final scenarios are derived by identifying distinct routes from the root
to each leaf node within the scenario tree.
The probabilities associated with these fi-
nal scenarios reflect the accumulated probability of reaching each leaf node, providing a
comprehensive outlook on potential outcomes.

2.3.2
Rolling Horizon Technique
Solving multistage stochastic optimization problems is often challenging due to the in-
creased complexity of solving problems over long time horizons with several stages. The
“Rolling Horizon” method, outlined in this section, effectively addresses this issue. It does
so by iteratively solving the problem over shorter time horizons and then combining the
optimal decisions from each iteration to determine the optimal solution for the entire time
horizon [18]. This methodology is especially relevant when problems are solved in real-
time, as it incorporates the most current and, thereby, most accurate information into the
decision making process [19].
In the context of this study, the Rolling Horizon method is applied to use the most
recent and accurate near-term wind power production forecasts. It also serves as a time-
based decomposition technique to reduce problem-solving complexity, which decreases
computational time [20]. Figure 2.4 shows a schematic overview of the method.
Figure 2.4: Overview of Rolling Horizon method. The optimization problem is solved for the
entire simulation horizon, in this case 24 hours, by iteratively solving the problem for a smaller
look-ahead horizon, in this case 2 hours. The optimal decisions for t found by solving the prob-
lem for the look-ahead horizon t to t + H are implemented as effective decisions. Effective deci-
sions are fixed from the preceding stages and carried over to the following stages.
At stage st, information on wind power realization for that stage and the scenarios con-
structed based on the wind power realization is used to solve the optimization problem
for a suﬀiciently small look-ahead horizon. The look-ahead horizon is defined from t to
t+H, where H is the number of hours the algorithm looks ahead. This yields the optimal
decisions for stage st, implemented as effective decisions, at. The process moves to the
next stage, st+1, now informed by the effective decisions from the previous stage and the
wind power realization at the current stage st+1. This new horizon’s optimization problem
is solved, extending from t + 1 to t + 1 + H. Through this iterative approach, the algo-
rithm rolls forward and systematically identifies the optimal decisions across the entire

simulation horizon. Towards the end of the process, when t + H exceeds the simulation
horizon T, the look-ahead horizon is determined as T −t. This implies that at stage T,
the optimization problem is solved as a deterministic problem without scenarios for future
wind power realization.
In the Rolling Horizon algorithm, the planner determines the duration of the look-ahead
horizon H, which poses a trade-off. For a fixed number of variables, increasing the duration
of the look-ahead horizon decreases the number of scenarios to be included in the policy
to maintain the optimization problem’s computational tractability. This trade-off balance
will be empirically investigated in Section 5.1.3 to determine a suitable look-ahead horizon
to enhance decision making outcomes.
2.4
Solution Evaluation Methods
When solving an SP, evaluating the solution and providing justification for choosing to
solve an SP over a deterministic one is essential.
This section presents two benchmark models: the Expected Value (EV) and the Optimal-
in-Hindsight (OiH). Following this, it outlines the methodology for using these benchmark
programs to compute two quality metrics: the Expected Value of Perfect Information
(EVPI) and the Value of the Stochastic Solution (VSS). These metrics are used for eval-
uating the performance of the stochastic model investigated in this thesis.
2.4.1
Benchmark Models
Expected-Value Benchmark
A commonly applied method for handling problems with uncertainty is using a “best guess”
estimate for the realization of the uncertain parameters. This method is represented by the
deterministic EV program, which uses expected values to depict uncertain parameters and
thus constitutes a program that is conceptually and computationally easier to solve than
the stochastic program [5]. The Expectation of Expected Value (EEV) solution obtained
from the EV program is used as a benchmark, with the underlying premise that if the EV
program performs as well or better than the stochastic program, the additional effort in
solving the stochastic model is not justified.
Optimal-in-Hindsight Benchmark
Another approach relies on solving the program while assuming perfect foresight of un-
certainty realizations.
The OiH solution, also called the Wait-and-See (WS) solution,
is obtained by replacing the uncertain parameter with its realization, thereby retrospec-
tively providing the most optimal decisions. This approach is not possible in practice,
as decisions must be made before the realization of uncertainty, but it can be used as a
benchmark, which represents the best possible solution if all future outcomes are known
in advance.
The two benchmark models are essential for evaluating the quality of solutions obtained
from an SP. In the case of a minimization problem, the OiH solution represents the best
possible performance that can be achieved by the SP and thereby serves as a lower bound.
On the other hand, the EEV solution represents the performance the SP must exceed to
be considered effective and serves as an upper bound in this regard. Therefore, these two
benchmark solutions can define the range for the Recourse Solution (RS) as in:
OiH ≤RS ≤EEV.

2.4.2
Quality Metrics
Based on the solution obtained from all three programs, EV, OiH, and RS, the performance
of the SP can be expressed through metrics. The metrics used in this project include the
Expected Value of Perfect Information (EVPI) and the Value of the Stochastic Solution
(VSS), as described below.
Expected Value of Perfect Information
The EVPI is defined as the maximal amount a decision maker is willing to spend to acquire
perfect information about future events, for instance, by improving the accuracy of the
forecasting model. For minimization, the EVPI can be computed as the difference between
the RS and the OiH solution:
EV PI = RS −OiH.
(2.8)
A low EVPI metric for a minimization problem indicates that the stochastic solution is
close to the optimal solution, indicating good performance.
Value of the Stochastic Solution
On the other hand, the VSS is defined as a metric for assessing the advantage of employing
an SP approach compared to a deterministic one.
For minimization, the VSS can be
computed as the difference between the EEV and the RS:
V V S = EEV −RS.
(2.9)
A positive VSS metric for a minimization problem indicates that the RS outperforms the
EV. The higher the VSS, the greater the benefits of using a stochastic model.

Modeling of the Coupled
Multi-Energy System
This chapter introduces the Multistage Stochastic Economic Dispatch (MSED) problem
formulated for the Coupled Multi-Energy System (CMES). It begins with an overview of
the investigated CMES, followed by a presentation of the constraints used to characterize
the system, which leads to the formulation of the MSED problem.
3.1
System Description
A CMES is an integrated system that optimizes and coordinates the generation, storage,
conversion, and consumption of various energy carriers to promote effective planning and
operation of the system. The driving force of a CMES is the belief that managing multiple
energy sources in a coordinated manner has two main benefits: 1) improved energy eﬀi-
ciency and 2) increased energy supply security [21]. This approach is especially beneficial
in systems with a high share of renewable energy, where the flexibility of CMESs plays a
crucial role in adapting to the unpredictability of renewable energy production.
This project investigates a CMES with four distinct energy carriers: power, gas, hydrogen,
and heat. This section provides an overview of the system’s components, outlining their
interconnections and functionalities.
Generators
The system includes the following power producers: gas-fired generators, conventional
coal-fired generators, wind farms, and Combined Heat and Power (CHP) plants. Although
all are classified as power producers, the CHP plants have the additional capability of
producing centralized heat distributed via a district heating network.
Coupling Components
In addition to the power producers, the system has three coupling components that man-
age energy conversion among the different energy carriers. These components include an
electrolyzer that uses electricity to produce hydrogen via electrolysis, a fuel cell that gen-
erates power from hydrogen, and a methanizer that synthesizes methane from hydrogen.
Storage Units and Demands
The system also features storage facilities and demand components for all four energy
carriers. The dashed box in Figure 3.1 represents building units, which serve as additional
heat demands. These demands can be met by either the district heating network, a private
gas boiler, or an electric heating device.

3.3
Decision Policy
This thesis proposes a policy for minimizing the expected system cost of a CMES over a
time horizon, T, as described in equation 2.1. The policy is the proposed methodology to
map the information available at time t to the effective decision made at stage st. The
policy is summarized in Figure 3.2.
Figure 3.2: Schematic of proposed policy. The stochastic look-ahead policy is characterized by
the number of scenarios and the length of the look-ahead horizon.
Starting at time t1, information on the wind power realization is obtained.
Based on
the realization value, scenarios outlined in Subsection 2.2.1 are reduced through the K-
medoids clustering method detailed in Subsection 2.3.1.
The MSED program for the
CMES, as described in Section 3.2, is then solved to optimality, and the here-and-now
decisions are implemented as effective decisions. This procedure is iteratively conducted for
t ∈{t1, . . . , T} using the Rolling Horizon method, explained in Subsection 2.3.2, ultimately
identifying the decisions that result in the minimum system cost over the entire simulation
horizon.

4
System Data
This chapter presents a case study system for the coupled multi-energy system (CMES)
presented in Chapter 3. A case study system is necessary to test the proposed decision
policy numerically. For this project, the case study system has been designed to meet the
power, heat, hydrogen, and gas demands in the approximate dimensions of DK11. The
dataset was constructed by researching standard sizes for system components. The size
and number of unit components were adjusted to ensure the system could adequately cover
the power, heat, hydrogen, and gas demands. This chapter details the case study system’s
technical specifications and load profiles. While most technical parameter values are based
on existing literature, some are customized to meet specific modeling requirements.
4.1
System Demand
To ensure consistency with the wind scenarios described in Subsection 2.2.1, system de-
mand data for DK1 from 16 March 2023 at 6 pm to 17 March 2023 at 5 pm was used
unless stated otherwise. All four system demand profiles can be seen in Figure 4.1.
Figure 4.1: Demand profiles for power, gas, heat, and hydrogen.
1The Danish electricity system is divided into two bidding zones: DK1 and DK2. DK1 represents
West Denmark, including both Funen and Jutland [13].

==PAGE:34==
The peak load hours for power are 1-2 and 14-19, which correspond to the evening hours
from 6-7 pm and morning hours from 7 am-12 pm. The peak heat load hours are 4-6 and
13-19, which correspond to the evening hours from 9-11 pm and the morning hours from
6 am-12 pm. The peak hydrogen load hours are hour 2, corresponding to 7 pm, and hours
19, 20, and 22, corresponding to the hours around noon. The gas load fluctuates, and
there is no peak load period.
Power Demand
The power demand profiles were created using the gross power consumption data of DK1
obtained from Energinet [22]. The gross power consumption was adjusted to exclude the
power-to-heat consumption, as this is already modeled within the system through CHP
units and electric heating devices in the buildings and is, therefore, not considered part of
the system demand.
Gas Demand
The gas demand profiles were estimated based on the monthly natural gas consumption in
TJ for March 2023 in the DK1 area from Energinet [23]. 62% of the total gas consumption
was estimated to be used for heat and electricity generation [24]. This percentage was
deducted from the gas consumption as gas-to-heat and gas-to-electricity consumption are
modeled within the system through CHP units and gas boilers and, therefore, are not
part of the system demand. The monthly average was scaled to hourly values and set to
fluctuate within a range of 10% to model variations. Finally, the energy content of natural
gas was expressed in MWh using a conversion factor of 277.77 TJ/MWh.
Heat Demand
The heat demand profiles were derived from data on private and industry heat consump-
tion for the area of DK1 obtained from Energinet [25]. The data was adjusted to exclude
heating for apartment houses, summer houses, and detached houses, as this is modeled
within the system via the building units and, therefore, not as a part of the system demand.
Hydrogen Demand
The estimation of hydrogen demand was derived from Denmark’s total hydrogen pro-
duction, which amounted to 18,000 metric tons in 2021 [26]. This estimation assumes
that hydrogen production directly reflects consumption for the same period. The yearly
production data was broken down into hourly production rates and converted to MWh,
assuming the energy equivalent of 1 metric ton of hydrogen is 33 MWh [27]. Further-
more, hydrogen consumption was adjusted for an assumed increase of 20% in hydrogen
production from 2021 to 2023.

System Units
The system includes seven gas-fired generators, five coal-fired conventional generators,

The power generators of the system are four wind farms, seven gas-fired generators, five
conventional generators, and four CHP units.

tional coal-fired generator capacities typically range from 200 to 1000MW, and CHP unit

Electrolyzer
The electrolysis technology is still in its nascent development stage, making it diﬀicult
to determine a standardized capacity range. Currently, only a few electrolyzers are in
operation. However, according to the Danish Energy Agency, six projects will establish
an electrolysis capacity of more than 280 MW in Jutland in the coming years [35]. Ad-
ditionally, the Danish company Topsøe reports that they will construct the world’s first
industrial-scale electrolyzer facility with a capacity of 500 MW [36].
The modeling choice for this case study resulted in a final capacity of 160 MW. The
hourly ramp rate is set at 100 MW, corresponding to 62% of the electrolyzer’s maximum
capacity. This models the operational restriction on electrolysis use. Recent electrolyzer
designs have reported eﬀiciencies as high as 80%, which is assumed in this case study [37].

In the literature, it is found that a methanizer’s eﬀiciency can range from 75 to 83%,
[38], [39]. The specifications for the methanizer were based on a comparative case study,
which included both methanizers and fuel cells [40] as well as modeling decisions aimed
at promoting the use of fuel cell technology and the hydrogen storage unit. As a result, a
methanizer capacity of 20 MW and an operational eﬀiciency of 75% is used.

Experimental Results
To implement the proposed policy framework detailed in Section 3.3, a Python program
was developed using the Pyomo optimization library. It was a priority to create a modular
and well-organized codebase to facilitate easy usage and future add-on development. The
source code is available as an attachment to this thesis. All simulations were conducted
using the algebraic solver Gurobi 11.0.0 on an Intel® Xeon® Processor E5-2660 v3 running
at 2.60 GHz with 8 GB of RAM.
This chapter aims to achieve three goals: first, to conduct a numerical evaluation of key
modeling decisions; second, to exemplify the use of the stochastic look-ahead policy; and
third, to characterize the solution derived from the policy.
The analyses included in this chapter are based on performing 45 separate experiments for
each type of program: stochastic (RS), Expected Value (EV), and Optimal-in-Hindsight
(OiH). The choice of 45 experiments was considered suﬀicient after observing that solu-

policy on a specific wind power realization. To illustrate the general trends of wind power

5.1.3
Scenario Tree Construction
This analysis aims to identify the most effective scenario tree for the stochastic look-ahead
policy. The process involves two main steps: first, identifying the maximum number of
scenarios that can practically be included in the Stochastic Program (SP), and second,
constructing the scenario tree that leads to the best performance of the SP based on the
number of scenarios found in the first step.
Number of Scenarios
In this project, the main limitation was the restricted number of sample scenarios, while
the number of variables used in the Multistage Stochastic Economic Dispatch (MSED) pro-
gram was not the issue. The limitation occurred when the stage-wise K-medoids clustering
technique was applied to the restricted set of 1455 sample scenarios, as the clustering al-
gorithm encountered situations where it had to split a single cluster into more sub-clusters
than the number of scenarios it contained. It was found that a maximum of 32 reduced
scenarios could be included for a 5-hour look-ahead horizon in all 45 experiments, whereas
extending to a 6-hour look-ahead horizon was not feasible.
The trend illustrated in Figure 5.4 demonstrates that incorporating additional scenarios
into the program leads to decreased system cost and increased computational time. This
motivates using the maximum possible number of scenarios in the program, and conse-
quently, 32 scenarios were employed in the stochastic look-ahead policy. Although this
increased the computational time required, it was deemed acceptable since the computa-
tional time remained within reasonable limits. It is important to note that the differences
in system cost between the scenario tree configurations are less than 1 %, which will be
elaborated on in Section 5.2.

computational time for obtaining effective decisions for a single time step within the policy is
referenced on the secondary y-axis.

Scenario Tree
Constructing a scenario tree involves determining the length of the look-ahead horizon and
the branching scheme used for each stage in the look-ahead horizon. There is a trade-off
between these two parameters because as the look-ahead horizon increases, the number
of branches per stage should decrease for a fixed number of variables to ensure that the
optimization problem remains computationally feasible. This balance entails that a too-
short look-ahead horizon may yield solutions with limited foresight, failing to provide a
comprehensive view. On the other hand, an excessively long look-ahead horizon is forced
to include fewer branches per stage, which can lead to a loss of statistical information
and loss of non-anticipativity. This analysis aimed at improving the policy’s performance
by investigating the optimal combination of look-ahead horizon and branching schemes.
Even though the number of scenarios was limited before the number of variables, the
project still sought to maintain a fair balance between these two parameters. To achieve
this, each combination of look-ahead horizon and branching scheme resulted in a unique
scenario tree comprising 32 scenarios. Figure 5.5 shows the scenario trees and the system
cost obtained using the stochastic look-ahead policy with these specific trees.
Figure 5.5: System cost and median running time for different scenario tree configurations. The
median computational time is reported for obtaining effective decisions for a single time step
within the policy.
Based on the results of the analysis shown in Figure 5.5, it can be observed that the
policy yields the best results when the [1, 4, 2, 2, 2]-scenario tree is used. It is important to
note that the differences in system cost between the various scenario tree configurations
are small, each differing by less than 1%. However, despite the minor differences, the
[1, 4, 2, 2, 2]-scenario tree has been selected for the policy.

Discussion
In this chapter, the main findings of the thesis are discussed and placed within a broader
context.
Additionally, the study’s methodological limitations are outlined, along with
recommendations for future research.
6.1
Main Findings
The proposed stochastic look-ahead policy, designed to minimize operational costs of a
Coupled Multi-Energy System (CMES) over a given time horizon, was tested and evaluated
using the specifically developed case study system.
The policy’s performance was evaluated by comparing the total system cost of implement-
ing effective decisions obtained from the policy with those obtained from the Expected
Value (EV) and Optimal-in-Hindsight (OiH) programs. It was found that applying the
stochastic policy on the case study system had limited potential for reducing cost. This
limitation was due to the already small difference between the costs of the EV and OiH
solutions. To increase the cost difference between the two benchmark programs, technical
parameters such as capacities, eﬀiciencies, and ramp rates were adjusted for the case study
system, resulting in slight improvements. This demonstrated that the inherent flexibility
of the case study CMES enables it to adapt eﬀiciently to less-than-optimal decisions across
any solution type. However, the results showed consistently better performance using the
stochastic policy over the EV program. Specifically, in the base case system, choosing
the stochastic policy led to a 0.11 percent reduction in system cost compared to the EV
program, which amounts to an average daily saving of 43,879 DKK.
The system’s behavior was examined by comparing the dispatch decisions generated by
the stochastic policy against the system’s demands. The investigation revealed a flexible
system capable of co-optimizing the production of hydrogen, heat, gas, and power under
uncertain wind power production while meeting the system demand. Furthermore, the
extensive testing demonstrated the logical operation of the system components, and the
system was found to be stable under a wide range of wind power production levels.
6.2
Limitations of Methods & Future Work
6.2.1
Mathematical Model of CMES
This thesis investigated the Economic Dispatch (ED) problem for the CMES. A limitation
of the ED model is that it does not consider network constraints for all energy carriers and
thereby assumes infinite transmission line capacities and disregards transmission losses.
This exclusion is regarded as the main limitation of the model, as it leads to an overly
optimistic estimation of the system’s ability to handle unexpected changes in power pro-
duction. This may explain the observed minimal differences in system costs between the
solution approaches. A logical progression would be to refine the ED model to incorporate
the network of each energy carrier. In practice, this would be done by formulating the DC
Optimal Power Flow (DC-OPF) for the CMES for not only power but all energy carriers.
47
==PAGE:57==
More detailed modeling of the components’ dynamics could further improve the model’s
accuracy. For instance, it could be of interest to consider start-up and minimum run-
ning times for generators, as well as minimum production capacities. These operational
constraints could be incorporated into the ED model by adopting the Unit Commitment-
Economic Dispatch (UC-ED) problem formulation. However, integrating the DC-OPF
model would notably expand the number of variables in the program, while incorporating
the UC-ED model would introduce binary variables. As a result, these enhanced models
would come at the cost of increased complexity when finding optimal solutions. Solv-
ing these enhanced models would require simplifying the problem in other ways by using
relaxation or decomposition techniques.
6.2.2
Uncertainty Modeling
This thesis employed scenarios generated in advance by a probabilistic forecasting model.
While the limited number did not result in unstable solutions during the in-sample test,
it constrained the number of scenarios that could be included in the program, as well as
options for constructing scenario trees. It would be worthwhile to investigate alternative
methods for creating scenarios, for instance, through time series models. More scenarios
will enable the policy to be employed with longer look-ahead horizons and broaden the
possibilities for customizing scenario trees, thereby potentially improving the performance
of the stochastic policy.
Furthermore, the thesis considers normalized wind power production as the only uncertain
parameter in the system, even though other system parameters, such as fuel costs and
energy demands, are also related to uncertainty.
Future studies could investigate the
impact of incorporating multiple uncertain parameters into the model for a more accurate
system representation. However, adding more uncertain parameters to the model would
increase the complexity of the problem and, thereby, the computational cost required to
solve it.
6.2.3
Case Study System
The work in this thesis places a strong emphasis on ensuring that both the mathematical
model and Python codebase are easily extendable. To achieve this, a modular structure
was adopted, which allows for a straightforward addition of new energy resources and
components to the model. For instance, including other highly utilized technologies into
the model, like solar power installations, biogas plants, and electric vehicles, would be
interesting to model the real-world energy systems of today more closely.
The case study demonstrated that the system’s reliance on CHP units was a limiting
factor in the system’s ability to fully utilize the available wind power. Extending the heat
generation to include excess heat from the electrolyzer could be the next step to increase
the system’s flexibility.
Another way to increase flexibility in the system could be to
enhance demand-side flexibility through smart buildings. In this project, smart buildings
were implemented on a modest scale by indoor temperature control; however, this could be
extended to include, e.g., private smart charging of electric vehicles and peak load control.

Conclusion
The operation of modern energy systems encounters significant challenges due to the
unpredictability and variability of renewable energy sources. Therefore, there is a need for
increased flexibility to enhance renewable energy utilization. A key strategy for enhancing
flexibility while improving system eﬀiciency is through the coupling of multiple energy
sectors. This thesis develops a multistage stochastic look-ahead policy for a Coupled Multi-
Energy System (CMES) that addresses wind power uncertainty. It leverages synergies
across different energy sectors to ensure optimal economic operation with minimal wind
power curtailment.
The policy development followed the three steps of a decision making process:
1.
Modeling the System: A comprehensive mathematical model was formu-
lated for an energy system incorporating heat, power, gas, and hydrogen as energy
carriers. The model simulated a flexible energy system capable of accommodating
uncertain wind power production. The system included gas supply points, gas-fired
generators, conventional generators, Combined Heat and Power (CHP) units, wind
farms, electrolyzers, fuel cells, methanizers, storage units, and buildings.
2. Formulating a Solvable Model: The Multistage Stochastic Economic Dis-
patch (MSED) problem was formulated for the CMES and constituted the mathe-
matical framework for optimizing dispatch decisions for cost-effective system opera-
tion. To model the stochasticity of wind power production, 1,500 scenarios created
from a probabilistic forecasting model were used to define the range of possible wind
power outcomes for the planning period. The K-medoids clustering technique was
used to reduce the number of scenarios to a smaller set of representative scenarios.
This simplification made the problem computationally tractable without losing the
statistical information of the wind power’s unpredictability. The “Rolling Horizon”
method was adopted to simulate the sequential decision making process inherent
in energy dispatch. The strategic combination of the number of reduced scenarios
included in the program and the length of the look-ahead horizon of the Rolling
Horizon method collectively defined the stochastic policy.
3. Solving the Problem: A case study system of the CMES was developed using
literature sources and customized values to meet specific modeling requirements.
The system was designed to generate energy on a scale that could meet DK1’s
approximate power, heat, hydrogen, and gas demands. A Python-based modular
codebase for the stochastic look-ahead policy was developed using the Pyomo library;
the case study and the codebase allowed for numerical testing of the policy.
49
==PAGE:59==
The policy was numerically evaluated and tested using the case study system for a 24-
hour planning horizon. The experimental investigations were performed by simulating 45
realizations of the available wind power production throughout the planning horizon. From
the experimental results of the case study system, three main conclusions were drawn:
• An analysis to determine the optimal combination of the look-ahead horizon and
branching scheme, under a constraint of 32 scenarios, identified that a [1,4,2,2,2]-
scenario tree yielded the best performance.
This structure effectively balances a
comprehensive view of the future while maintaining suﬀicient statistical information
on uncertain wind power scenarios.
• The implementation of the stochastic policy using 45 simulated wind power ex-
periments resulted in an average system cost of 45,376,133 DKK for the base case
system. To numerically evaluate the policy’s performance, the system cost of the
policy solution was compared to the system costs of the Expected Value (EV) and
Optimal-in-Hindsight (OiH) benchmark solutions. It was found that implementing
the stochastic policy had limited potential in reducing system costs because of the
high flexibility of the case study CMES. However, the results showed that imple-
menting the stochastic policy led to a 0.11% reduction in system cost compared
to the EV program for the base case system, resulting in an average daily saving
of 43,879 DKK. This evaluation demonstrated that the total system costs can be
reduced by changing the operational planning algorithm for the CMES from a de-
terministic approach to a stochastic one. The cost savings are expected to increase
further as the stochastic model is refined to include network constraints for modeling
a more realistic but less flexible system.
• The solution of the stochastic look-ahead policy demonstrated the CMES’s adapt-
ability to adjust to varying levels of wind power by exploiting co-optimizing across
the heat, power, hydrogen, and gas sectors. It was discovered that the CHP units’
requirement to fulfill the system’s heat demand alone constrained the amount of
wind energy that could be incorporated into the system.
The present thesis aimed at laying a foundation for other master’s or Ph.D. students to
build upon. The next steps in the further analysis could involve expanding the model
to consider the network constraints of the energy carriers and transmission losses. Addi-
tionally, the model could incorporate solar power installations, biogas plants, and electric
vehicles into the system or investigate the potential of enhancing demand-side flexibility.
