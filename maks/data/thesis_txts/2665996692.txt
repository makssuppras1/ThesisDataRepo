Abstract  
 
This master thesis investigates how different control strategies on a building heating 
system level, affect its energy consumption and peak power need delivered by the district 
heating networks. The investigation is mainly divided into two different cases. The first 
case is regarding space heating controls and is based on the DTU building 118. It combines 
a mixture of simulation in the dynamic simulation program IDA ICE and measured data from 
a building management system to evaluate control systems of internal heating systems 
and district heating substations. The second case is regarding domestic hot water tank 
control and is based on data from an apartment suite in Nordhavn.  
The main results of the space heating case at DTU, include how it is possible to save 
energy by having different types of temperature setbacks of the building. The setbacks 
come with a price of high peak loads in the system. Simulations with on-site measurements 
of different setback strategies show how the negative effects could be mitigated by 
having a slow, gradual reheat of the building in the morning, instead of a quick high-
intensity period. The most important results include, it was possible to develop a weather 
compensation curve with minimised supply temperatures, with the capability of heating 
the building to acceptable temperatures. This was based on measured heating 
consumption and outdoor temperatures and shows how a building can be operated at 
comfort temperatures at reduced supply and return temperatures.  
The main result of the domestic hot water case in Nordhavn displays how it is possible to 
limit the heating capacity while always maintaining comfortable temperatures. This is done 
by having a safety mechanism, that allows for bypassing the limitation if the domestic hot 
water supply temperature is below a certain threshold. The data for both normal operation 
(a week) and the year peak (New Year’ Eve) shows it is possible to use the control strategy 
year-round without dropping to critical temperatures. It is possible to use the strategy to 
reduce the heating power used outside peak hours, but also lower the peak loads to a 
minimum. Furthermore, the data reviewed show that it might be possible to set the 
capacity limit lower, to achieve lower return temperatures at night. 
The two different cases have high synergetic potential in terms of limiting peak load and 
lowering supply and return temperatures. It is technically feasible to implement both the 
lowered weather compensation curve and the domestic hot water capacity limitation in 
most buildings, but no exact analysis of this has been performed.  
All the methods investigated, will be to the benefit of the building owners in terms of 
lowered heating use & lower distribution heat losses. It will also benefit the district heating 
suppliers in terms of lowered supply and return temperatures. The lowered temperatures 
support the efficient use of renewable sources of energy like heat pumps in the energy 
system of the near future.

1 Introduction 
 
This part of the report will describe the motivation and aim of the report. As well as some 
background information.  
The motivation of this report is to help drive the green transition. According to 
“Regeringens Klimapartnerskaber”, the intelligent control – or smart control - of 
buildings alone has the potential to save 1.2 million tons of CO2 pr. year [1]. Many 
buildings do have intelligent control, but a lot of systems are outdated and might not fit the 
new heating distribution systems. Intelligent control in this context is meant to utilise the 
technologies and knowledge that exist today, to improve the efficiency of both new and old 
buildings. 
One of the main aspects of this report will be looking at energy savings in office buildings 
and other similar buildings that are not in use all time. A key problem from a systems point 
of view is during Monday morning when people return to work, many buildings need to be 
reheated from an energy-saving setback.  
The reheating after setbacks, combined with high dwelling domestic hot water use in the 
mornings, creates a peak load in the district heating networks. It entails that the supply 
temperature of the district heating network must be increased to provide all the buildings 
with the required heat. The high supply temperature leads to high-cost heat losses in the 
distribution networks and increased wear and tear of the pipes. The peak power also leads 
to unnecessary greenhouse gas emissions, since a lot of peak load supply units are based 
on carbon fuel. This is described more in chapter 2.

 Aim 
This section will present the general aim of the project: to investigate how different control 
strategies for space heating and domestic hot water affects the energy consumption and 
thermal comfort of buildings. The specific aim is to simulate, test and evaluate some new 
proposals for the improvement of the operation of heating systems in existing buildings - 
to save energy, reduce peak load and still provide comfortable indoor temperatures.  
1.1.1 Space heating 
The main part of this report focuses on different control strategies for an old office building 
with a 1-string radiator system. Part of the report focuses on a simulation model built in 
the dynamic simulation program IDA ICE, while the other part of the report focuses on 
testing the strategies in the real building.  
The control strategies that are possible to easily implement in the existing building 
management system are evaluated for a testing period from November 2020 to January 
2021. The different tested control options include: 
• Testing continuous heating in the heating season with a supply temperature based 
on a weather compensation curve corresponding to the standard operation of the 
building before any tests are conducted.  
• Testing a setback of the supply temperature (10K and 20K) at night. This is to see 
the advantages and drawbacks of this well-established energy-saving control.  
• Testing continuous heating in the heating season with a minimised supply 
temperature based on a new weather compensation curve. This is developed from 
measured heating consumption for different outdoor temperatures and the heating 
capacity of all the radiators in the building. This decrease in supply temperature 
could decrease the return temperature and the heat losses from the local district 
heating system.   
All the items on the above list were also tested to see if the current heating equipment was 
sufficient, to maintain comfortable temperatures using different heating controls.  
The simulation part focuses first on continuous heating operation for the month of April 
2020, because it has measured space heating, domestic hot water and electricity 
consumption that can be used to evaluate the model against. After the evaluation of the 
model, different control systems are evaluated on a one-year basis through simulation, 
using a Design Reference Year file.

In addition to the tested solutions, the following variations are evaluated through 
simulation: 
• Simulations of a setback at night that treat weekends as nights. This should further 
decrease the energy use but will increase the peak load Monday morning.  
• Simulations of a setback of the building, done by turning the heating system off 
during nights, which is a well-known strategy to save energy.  
• Simulations of a setback of the building, done by turning the heating system off at 
nights and weekends. This is a variation of the above.  
• Simulations of a step-based increase of the supply temperature after a setback. This 
is to reheat the building without the high peak loads and the high supply and return 
temperatures, which is the result of conventional reheating of the building in a short 
time.

1.1.2 Domestic hot water 
A substantial part of the report is based on setting a limitation on the heating capacity of 
the domestic hot water heating system. This is done to reduce peak loads by changing the 
control functions such that the heat exchanger is only allowed to use higher amounts of 
power if there is a risk of delivering water under comfortable temperatures. This strategy 
has been tested by HOFOR and DTU Civil Engineering on an apartment suite in Nordhavn, 
as a part of the “Energylab Nordhavn” project. “Energylab Nordhavn” is a portfolio of 
projects, all related to developing smart solutions for the future sustainable energy system. 
Details of the projects can be found on the website Energylab Nordhavn. The focus of this 
thesis is to evaluate the data generated by an ECL controller at the apartment suite and 
show how it functions on both a short- and long-term basis. 
==PAGE:13==

1.1.3 Combination of the improved controls 
The two aspects, space heating and domestic hot water, are in this project separated in 
terms of experimental tests. However, they could be solutions that can be implemented 
together in most buildings for even better synergy. Combining the control systems could 
lead to even lower supply temperatures, return temperatures and peak loads in the 
morning due to both shifting and shaving.  
The domestic hot water solution is possible in buildings with low circulation heat losses as 
described in the original project report. The system with a safety mechanism is currently 
not available in the market, but that is only a matter of time.  The gradual reheat is possible 
in some building management systems. However, lowering the supply temperature based 
on consumption data and radiator heating capacities is possible in all buildings where 
energy meters are installed.

 District heating loads 
This section will describe when and how peak loads in the district heating system occur. 
The district heating system in Denmark has undergone huge technological advancements 
in the course of its history. From being steam-driven and coal-fired in the first generation., 
to now being waterborne with low temperatures, squeezing as much power out of the water 
as possible, enabling increasing amounts of renewable energy integration. More on this 
will be presented in the next chapter.  
However, the system is not perfect. One of the problems that the district heating 
companies face is peak loads. The heating load differs naturally throughout the year, with 
the seasons (and their changing) outdoor temperatures. Peak load for the domestic hot 
water systems comes because most people take a shower in the morning. It can also occur 
from events like New Year’s Eve, because of the synchronous cooking of lavish meals in 
almost every household.   
Many buildings, that are not used all day, have a temperature setback outside the use 
hours and then they need to be reheated in the morning. This reheating combined with 
high domestic hot water use in the morning gives high peak loads on the weekdays. This is 
illustrated by the data from Varmelast in Figure 5: 
Where the heating load can be seen rising until the clear peak at 8:00 on weekdays. The 
heat load is then lower throughout the day, before rising again in the evening when the 
outdoor temperature drops. 
(Varmelast is a collaboration, controlling the heating distribution and production in the 
Capital Region. Varmelast is representing the three biggest district heating companies in 
the Capital Region: CTR, VEKS and HOFOR.)

Getting back to the Danish ambition to reduce carbon emissions substantially, the heat 
loads in the morning leads to unnecessary high supply temperatures in district heating 
networks. That means higher than needed grid losses, more strain than necessary in the 
network and increased use of fossil fuel through peak boilers - which is exactly what is not 
needed if the carbon reductions should be achieved. Furthermore, there is an obvious 
economic benefit in saving energy.  
As mentioned, when peak load in the district heating system needs to be delivered, an 
amount of this is delivered through gas or oil boilers that emit more CO2 pr. energy unit,  
The system, although a lot of improvement over last years, still has a huge range of carbon 
emissions pr. unit of heat, as can be seen in Figure 6 below from Varmelast: 
In situation B, a morning peak load can be seen, and most of that extra load after situation 
A is delivered through natural gas boilers. Leading to an increase in emissions of 5 kg/Gj.  
As seen in situation C, the average CO2 emission can go as high as 43 kg/Gj, because a lot 
of oil is being used to provide the heat load. And in situation D, the emission can be as low 
as 8 kg/Gj. The range of emissions from the Varmelast distribution can therefore within a 
short time vary with up to ≈ 540 %. 
The peak loads can be helped with both some new and well-developed methods, some of 
which are described in the next section. Besides the next chapter, this report will be 
focused on some specific methods that can directly help either HOFOR or DTU save energy 
and therefore help achieve the goals set for Denmark and the rest of the European Union.  
The main investigations are, again, concerning: 
• Space heating control strategies showed through simulations and measured data 
from a practical test on building 118 at DTU Lyngby Campus 
• Capacity limitations on power for heating of domestic hot water tanks showed 
through data from a project at EnergyLab Nordhavn.

3 Literature review 
 
This part of the report presents literature and findings, that are centred around the main 
problem stated in the previous section. For now, the peak load in the system, especially 
Monday morning, is managed by the district heating operation by increasing the supply 
temperatures. The higher temperatures are achieved while using polluting peak load 
boilers.  
If the goals set for Denmark must be achieved, the district heating system must likely be 
converted, to adapt to the newest forms of renewable energy sources that can be 
transformed into district heating. 
Finding ways to reduce the peak loads in the networks, meaning lower supply and return 
temperatures, makes it is possible to implement low-temperature district heating.  
This is also known as 4th generation district heating (4GDH). 
 4th Generation district heating 
This section is an introduction to why and how 4th generation district heating is needed in 
the future energy system. That energy system is needed, to achieve the carbon reduction 
and neutrality goals proposed by the Danish government. A cornerstone is the 
implementation of better district heating networks, that allow for more flexible energy 
sources to be utilised. The development of different generation district heating systems is 
well described by Lund et al. in [2].  
As described in [3] by Lund et al., there are some core challenges district heating networks 
needs to address, to be able to integrate them as a sustainable part of the future energy 
system.  
The most important being the ability to provide low-temperature district heating. This to 
achieve:  
• Ability to integrate recycled heat from low-temperature sources like industrial 
processes or wastewater, like the plan is to do at Roskilde Forsyning (FORS) [4] 
• Ability to integrate renewable energy sources, like solar power coupled with heat 
pumps, which can provide low-temperature heating at high coefficients of 
performance (COP). 
• Limit grid losses, to be able to provide district heating to low heat-dense areas. 
In short, to be able to utilise low supply temperatures, the system must be geared for it.

3.1.1 Economic viability 
This section will introduce the economic feasibility of implementing 4th generation district 
heating. It has been shown in the work done by Lund in [2] that the economic benefits of 
implementing 4th generation district heating exceed the cost by a good margin. However, 
the gap is differentiated for different energy sources. In the work by Averfalk and Werner 
[5] big benefits can be seen, if the supply temperatures are lowered, for the energy sources: 
• Geothermal heat 
• Heat pumps   
• Industrial waste 
The reason for this is that for these systems, more low-quality energy can be utilised using 
lower temperatures in the district heating networks. In the current energy systems, these 
heat sources are under-utilised and need to be implemented more fluently in the energy 
systems to secure the green transition. However, the work shows that the benefits of 
lowering the supply temperatures are small for Combined Heat and Power (CHP) plants. 
Since they are already efficient in the temperature range used today.  
Work done by Østergaard & Svendsen in [6], appeals that improving the Danish district 
heating system, to lower the supply and return temperatures, is economically feasible from 
an energy system perspective. By making it possible to reduce DH return temperature. 
Furthermore, their work shows that it can also be economically feasible for individual DH 
customers. this implies that it is economically feasible to renovate the heating systems of 
older buildings. However, this needs capital investments that are not always available. Big 
system changes generally tend to have longer payback periods and might not be 
prioritised.  
Alternative low-cost strategies are therefore in high value, not only to the consumer but 
also to the district heating supplier because simple guidelines can lead to better energy 
efficiency without big capital expenses.  
3.1.2 Routes to 4th generation district heating 
This section will present some ways to get to 4th generation district heating. There exist 
many different approaches to help achieve the predefined goals of 4th generation district 
heating, and a few have been chosen to be presented here. These approaches are either 
new, promising, or relevant to the project – or any combination of these superlatives. The 
focus is on how reducing the peak loads can be achieved.  However, active flexibility is not 
included in this report to maintain focus. Energy efficiency in general contributes to lower 
demand from the user. Typical energy efficiency measures are passive reductions through 
a renovation of for example windows. Or achieved by choosing 5 cm more insulation when 
constructing new buildings. The energy efficiency of especially new buildings has been the 
focus in Denmark since the first Danish building code was introduced in 1961. In the years 
since more spotlight has been given to the renovation of buildings.

3.1.2.1 Demand-side management 
Demand-side management, in this context, means to control, at what times heating in a 
building is needed. Typically, in the form of a control system, demand-side management is 
a general trend in energy services and might be the strongest focus of electricity carriers.  
Most carriers now allow customers to pay the price of the energy at the time of their 
consumption. Typically, the cost would be lower during the night because most people are 
asleep and the load in the system is low. Allowing users to buy cheap energy, at times that 
are beneficial for the supplying companies, since it shifts the energy use away from the 
peak load hours, depending on the adaptation level. Therefore, dynamic energy prices are 
considered a form of demand-side management. 
This form of demand-side management could also be implemented in the heating sector. 
Since the heating production is also planned and auctioneered to fulfil the demands at the 
lowest possible cost for the district heating companies. It might become even more 
relevant if, the electricity-generated heating begins to become more dominant in the 
production of heat, like in the scenario investigated by Soysal in [7].  
Allowing users that invest in technology to get heating bill reductions in this form, would 
be known as peak shifting. Meaning the strategy will shift some of the peaks to other parts 
of the day through economic incentives. 
3.1.2.2 Temperature setbacks 
Temperature setbacks can be beneficial in buildings where the usage follows a pattern, like 
an office building. It is beneficial when the buildings are empty for a period, and therefore 
the building does not need to maintain a comfortable temperature, but merely a non-
condensation temperature [8]. Setbacks can be made in many forms, it can be during the 
night, the weekend or the day. It can be on an individual room or a whole building level. In 
newer buildings, with individual room sensors, it can be beneficial to do it at room level. 
This is for example done in the new Villa Copenhagen hotel, where unoccupied rooms have 
a lower temperature setpoint and are then reheated when the rooms are booked. 
In older buildings, like the case building 118 described later, it is typically only possible to 
control a setback on a building level. Unless a major renovation of the heating system has 
taken place.  
The setbacks can be useful in terms of saving energy, and thereby reducing the average 
load in the system, but as previously described, setbacks can lead to huge peak loads 
coming Monday morning, if not controlled correctly. Therefore, a part of this report is 
investigating how a slow reheating of the buildings can be achieved.  Guelpa show in [9] 
that peak load can be shaven by their method of “rescheduling heating systems” by 5 – 35 
% depending on their chosen limitations.

3.1.2.3 Domestic hot water 
Another form of demand-side management would be to limit the domestic hot water load. 
This is possible, because this load normally follows a stable consumption pattern, peaking 
in the mornings when people take hot showers and in the afternoon when people cook 
dinner. Of course, the situation is different with the, at the time of writing, problematic 
COVID-19 disease. People use domestic hot water more asymmetrical, meaning lower 
peak loads, which is in favour of the district heating networks.  
A way to lower the heating loads in the district heating system could be to utilise electric 
heaters locally as shown in the work by Ruysschaert in his master thesis [10]. In the case 
described, some domestic hot water use, and its associated circulation loss, will be shifted 
from the district heating system to the electricity system. This makes sense in a future 
scenario with a lot of renewable electricity.  
Another option is to restrict the heating capacity of the hot water tank, in periods when the 
water supply temperature is not critically low. This control strategy has been described in 
[11]. The work is showing that it is not needed to re-heat the domestic hot water tank as 
soon as the energy has been used. This option is a key concern of this report and will be 
explored further in chapter 5. 
3.1.3 The novelty of this thesis 
This section will describe the focus of the thesis. As described, the report will have a focus 
on achieving energy efficiency of buildings and district heating networks through different 
means. This is mainly done through two different cases: 
The first case is concerning space heating systems. The focus is here on showing what a 
night and weekend setback means for the energy use and peak loads of a typical old office 
building. Furthermore, since the supply temperature is based on the outdoor temperature, 
the section aims to answer how low the supply temperatures to the building can be, at a 
given outdoor temperature, to help the district heating network.   
The second case involves an investigation of a domestic hot water capacity limitation 
implemented at an apartment suite in Nordhavn. Looking at how a safety mechanism was 
implemented and how the system was operating. Furthermore, it includes a description of 
why the capacity limitation can be lowered further, to ensure as low as possible return 
temperatures.

4  Measurements and simulations of DTU Building 118  
 
This part of the report will describe the test of different space heating control strategies, 
that was performed in the DTU building titled 118. It will start with a general description, 
followed by the simulation performed in the dynamic simulation program IDA ICE. It will 
end with the scale test of setbacks and a new weather compensation curve.  
This first part will be an iteration of the different options of control presented in the 
introduction. To act as a guide through the original project idea and the actual project 
outcome. 
The original plan with building 118 was to record the temperatures in the building using 
continuous heating controlled by the already implemented weather compensation curve. 
This was done to get a reference of indoor temperatures using the data loggers described 
later and to get an idea of the heating consumption, using the internal building 
management system (BMS). This part was both performed in simulation and a test.  
This was to be followed by a test of a night and weekend setback, where the supply 
temperature was lowered outside work hours (work hours being from 6:00-18:00), to see if 
a noticeable temperature drop appeared, and lower energy consumption could be 
identified. As described earlier, there exist many forms of setbacks. In this project, three 
different kinds of methods were evaluated: 
1) The night setbacks were done by lowering the supply temperature outside work 
hours. This was tested in the building and simulated in IDA ICE. 
 
2) The night and weekend setbacks were done, like the previous variation, but with 
the key difference that the weekends are treated as one long night. Meaning a 
setback will be continuous throughout the weekend instead of being on and off 
every day. This was not able to be done in using the building management system in 
building 118. Instead, simulations were used to give an indication of the results 
using this option. This might be relevant in other buildings, where it is possible to 
implement both a night and weekend setback. 
The above setbacks were tested in two variations, one where the supply temperature was 
lowered by 10K and another lowered by 20K. 
3) The heating systems were switched off entirely to achieve a setback of 
temperatures in the building. This option was evaluated through simulation in IDA 
ICE only.

4.2.7.6 New weather compensation curve  
An integral part of 4GDH is utilising low supply and return temperatures to better utilise 
renewable sources of energy.  However, in old buildings with 1-string radiator systems, for 
example, it can be hard to achieve a high cooling of the water. Especially with a convector 
system as in building 118. They are simply not made for high cooling.  
A way to get lower return temperatures is then to lower the supply temperature, as they are 
closely related. However, if the supply temperature is lowered too much, not enough heat 
will be supplied to the building. The lowest possible supply temperature can be calculated, 
and how it was done for building 118, will be described in this section. 
A short introduction to the procedure: 
• Start with calculating the full heating capacity of the building. Done by summing the 
capacity of each radiator as well as other heat sources, such as uninsulated pipes.   
• Then analyse the heating consumption data for different outdoor temperatures. 
Based on this a heating coefficient can be calculated. 
• Hereafter, the cooling of water through the heating system for different 
temperatures must be estimated. This can be a fixed value or be dependent on the 
supply temperature. 
• Finally, as a result of the above inputs, a minimized supply temperature is 
calculated. It ensures that the heating system outputs the heating needed for 
different outdoor temperatures.  
The first step is to estimate the total heating capacity of the building. Using the same 
progress as described in section 4.1.1.3 the heating capacity pr. radiator using the 
reference temperature set 70/55/20 ºC was calculated. That means taking the heating 
capacity pr. m convector and adjust it for the windowsill placement. Finally adjusting for 
the difference in surface area between a square plate and a circular plate.  
For building 118, it gives an estimated 1027 W/module.  
Furthermore, the return pipe has a different heat loss using this temperature set. This was 
estimated to be 45.7 W/m2, using the calculation tool “ROCKTEC”. Each module has an 
estimated 110 W extra heating capacity.

comfortable temperatures during the work hours.

4.4.4.1 Too low temperatures 
This section gives some possible explanation why the first new weather compensation 
curve resulted in too low temperatures.  
An explanation of the lower temperature could be that the weather compensation curve 
was based on heating consumption from the winter season 2019-2020. That heating 
season can be assumed to have a considerably higher occupancy than the 2020 COVID-19 
affected building use. This matters because higher internal gains mean lower heating 
consumption to get the same indoor temperatures.  
Another explanation was described in section 4.3.3, showing how when the supply 
temperature is dropped, the heating capacity in the gables is not high enough to keep the

 Partial conclusion 
This section will conclude on different control options investigated in building 118 at DTU. 
The main takeaways include the following items: 
Firstly, it was shown that a gradual reheat after a setback does have the potential to limit 
peak loads. The implementation in IDA ICE worked to an extent, but it seems not possible 
to easily implement the control into the existing building management system. 
Furthermore, the results did not show any promise in reducing the return temperatures, 
which is also why it might have less impact than the second big finding:  
It was shown, through the full-scale test that it was possible to heat the building to almost 
22 º using a new weather compensation curve. A curve, in which the supply temperature 
was lowered by 10-12 ºK compared to the original. Because it is a 1-string system it means 
the return temperature was also effectively reduced by 10-12 ºK. This was even in a period 
with limited internal gains, because of the COVID-19 situation keeping people at home. 
Using the lowered weather compensation curve was possible in the building where the 
current heating system was not even made for heating the entire building.  
It is however recommended that the gable offices and 2nd floor hallway get additional extra 
heating capacity, to compensate for the higher heat loss compared to the rest of the rooms. 
One could argue that this is something, that could have been done when they stopped 
using the ventilation as the heating system 50 years ago.  
The method of lowering the supply temperature should be possible in all similar DTU 
buildings. Thereby potentially saving up to 15 % of the distribution losses in the current 
system. Even more if the domestic hot water system was modernised.

5 Domestic hot water capacity limitation in Nordhavn  
 
This chapter will describe the domestic hot water system implemented in an apartment 
suite in Nordhavn, as part of the “Energylab Nordhavn” project. The system has the 
potential to lower peak loads because the system will delay the reheating of the tank as 
much as possible. Unless the tank temperature drops below comfort temperatures. The 
following section will describe how the control functions in practice, and how peak loads 
are handled both on a normal weekday, but also when the yearly peak happens on New 
Year’s Eve.  
 Description of the Nordhavn Project 
This section will introduce the project in Nordhavn. The original project was performed in 
2018 and it was focusing on limiting the heating capacity to a minimum. Thereby reducing 
peak loads on the DH system and achieving lower return temperatures because of better 
district heating utilisation. Their original conclusion was that it was possible, but some 
problems under peak loads were found. The biggest problem occurred on New Year’s Eve 
2018, where the tank temperature dropped to as low as 32.5 º C before a manual overwrite 
of the limitation was performed.  
Therefore, the original project group figured it would be beneficial to improve the control 
system further by adding a safety mechanism so that when the tank temperature gets 
critically low the capacity limitation is overwritten automatically.  
The extra control mechanism was developed and implemented in the domestic hot water 
system, and this report aims to show that the capacity limitation, with a safety mechanism, 
works.A detailed description of the project, the original system and the safety mechanism 
can be found in [11] 
Some basic information about the project is that the hot water tank holds 2.5 m3 and the 
nominal heating power of the heat exchanger is 143 kW. Furthermore, the domestic hot 
water system was designed to supply 72 flats and was built in 2017.  
 
Figure 56 Showing the power supplied (grey) and the tank temperature (black) on New Year’s Eve 2018 
==PAGE:78==
69 
 Control system  
This section will introduce the control system implemented in Nordhavn. The control 
system implemented in the domestic hot water tank was done by using a Danfoss ECL 
controller. More info about this product can be found in [23]. This control is an existing 
solution that could be implemented virtually anywhere. However, the safety mechanism is 
not available on the market yet.  
The integrated controller tries to maintain a tank temperature of 55 ºC, but under the 
restraint that it may not use more than 25 kW out of its 143 kW capacity. (Unless the tank 
temperature drops too far below the setpoint, and the safety mechanism allows for higher 
heating use.   
That means, most of the time, the tank temperature is not a controlling factor, but rather 
the power supplied to the tank. This is done by having the ECL getting feedback from the 
energy meter ≈ every 12 s. Based on the flow measured, it calculates the power supplied to 
the tank. If it is too high the tank temperature setpoint is lowered until the power supplied 
is below the limitation. If the tank temperature drops below 47 ºC at the safety mechanism 
takes over and the power is unrestricted.  
It should be mentioned that the tank heat exchanger spiral does not reach the bottom of 
the tank as seen in Figure 57. The figure is not the exact geometry of the installed tank, but 
the principle is the same. The spiral not reaching the bottom means that the water in the 
bottom of the tank only will be heated when the draw-off is low and the circulation 
gradually heats it. This will mostly happen at night.  
When cold water enters the tank after a draw-off, it drops to the bottom, and first after 
some time, it will influence the return temperature. It means there is a slight delay in the 
observed tank temperatures and the actual temperatures.

kW. The average heat delivered was 39 kW, significantly higher than the capacity 
limitation of 25 kW.  
To reiterate, the original system had a strict capacity limitation of 25 kW – at the risk of 
getting too cold water out of the showers during peak loads. The results above show how 
the safety mechanism makes sure this does not happen.

 Partial conclusion 
This section will conclude on the use of a domestic hot water heating capacity control in 
Nordhavn.  
The capacity limitation control shows potential in limiting the peak power delivered to the 
user, while still maintaining the required temperatures for comfort. Both on a weekly use, 
but also during the peak load situation of New Year’s Eve.  
The control also allows for a slower reheating of the tank after the peak periods are over, 
compared to a typical operation. 
However, the tank reheats quickly during the night period with no draw-off.  
This can be seen on high return temperatures late at night, as shown in Figure 64 below,

The system could be improved further, by limiting the heating capacity even more.

6  Discussion 
 
This part of the report will comment on the limitations of the methods and the 
implications of the findings.  
 Implications 
This section will look at some of the implications of the findings in the results.  
6.1.1 Lowering supply and return temperatures 
Lowering the return temperatures was the plausible result in both the Nordhavn project 
and the Building 118 test. In the Nordhavn project, it can be achieved by reducing the 
threshold for the capacity limitation. In the 1-string heating system at DTU, it was shown 
possible by lowering the supply temperature.  
This effectively lowers the return temperature due to its close correlation to the supply 
temperature. Reducing the supply and return temperatures allows for a wider range of 
possible renewable energy sources in the district heating distribution system. Having a 
positive effect on the district energy system overall. 
Lowering the supply and return temperature at DTU was quantified directly in the section 
on distribution heat losses for the local DTU transmission network, but it is hard to 
quantify for the entire district heating system. How much the supply temperature can be 
lowered in the individual buildings is dependent on the building itself, but the promising 
results with a heating system such as building 118’s, show great potential for other 
applications of the method.  
6.1.2 Lowering peak load 
Lowering the peak load was shown as the result of both the Nordhavn project and the 
Building 118 test, without the loss of comfort for the individual users. Even though the 
lowering of peak load is not quantified in terms of economic or climate impact in this 
report, there are great implications. Lowering the peak load on a building level can lead to 
benefits both for the climate and the district heating suppliers. As discussed previously, the 
peak loads need peak boilers, and peak boilers are not as environmentally friendly as the 
baseload providers such as CHP plants or waste incineration plants. Furthermore, they 
demand big capital investments and are expensive to operate. 
If it is possible to lower the peak load on a building level, as shown in the report, it is 
possible to avoid the CO2 emissions that are a by-product of the oil or gas-boilers used to 
deliver the peak load. If the oil and gas boilers are not needed, the district heating 
suppliers can save substantial amounts on capacity investments.

 Limitations 
This section will briefly state some limitations in the method, suggesting that further 
studies could be beneficial.  
6.2.1 Experimental results  
The results show that it is possible to heat the building to comfort temperatures with 
different methods. Especially if taking measures in critical rooms with high heat losses, like 
the gables. However, the testing was not performed at maximum stress for more than a 
brief period. A cold winter, with long periods of negative temperatures, could mean 
thermal discomfort.  
It can be discussed if the design criteria temperature is happening with such a low 
frequency, that this is not a general concern.

7 Conclusion 
 
The part of the report will reiterate the findings in the report and highlight the most 
important discoveries. It will be divided into the conclusions for the space heating control 
investigation for building 118 and for the domestic hot water control investigation at 
Nordhavn.  
The main finding of the space heating project at DTU Building 118 is, that it is 
recommended to apply the method of lowering supply temperatures based on heating 
consumption, wherever possible.  
Furthermore, the tests and simulations gave the following results and inspired the 
following conclusions: 
Using the original weather compensation curve, combined with a temperature setback of 
the supply temperature at night, showed in simulation energy savings compared to 
continuous heating. However, it means a higher peak load on the district heating system 
and a risk of too cold buildings in the morning.  
The test results show that with a low setback (10K), the indoor temperature in building 
118 is barely lowered daily, but with a high setback (20K) the building cannot reheat itself 
before work hours.  
Using a 4-step gradual increase of supply temperature after a night/weekend setback, with 
a longer reheating period, simulations showed that this will still save energy compared to 
the reference case but will reduce the peak load significantly. However, the return 
temperatures were not lowered.   
Using a newly designed weather compensation curve, and operating the building based on 
this, showed in both simulations and tests, that the supply temperature can be lowered 
substantially without losing thermal comfort. This was possible in a building where the 
current heating system is not even made for heating the entire building. However, the 
results suggest that extra heating capacity in the gable offices is necessary to install and 
that heating capacity in the hallway will allow for even lower supply temperatures.  
The method of lowering the supply temperature should be possible in all similar DTU 
buildings, potentially saving up to 15 % of the energy in the current system and even more 
if the domestic hot water system is modernised.  
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:92==
83 
The main finding of the domestic hot water project in Nordhavn is, that it is 
recommended to apply the capacity limitation, wherever possible.  
Furthermore, the tests and simulations gave the following results and inspired the 
following conclusions: 
The safety mechanism, that was implemented after the initial project period, works. When 
the tank temperature drops too low, the capacity limitation is turned off and the power 
goes as high as necessary to keep thermal comfort in the domestic hot water. For all other 
times, the power use will stay below the set capacity limitation.  
The combination of reducing the power use continuously, while having a safety mechanism 
in place to avoid too low supply temperatures, shows great promise in terms of reducing 
peak loads and return temperatures.  
The return temperatures during the nights are still high, suggesting that the heating 
capacity could have an even lower limit. According to the findings, a lower limit should not 
increase the risk of not being able to provide comfortable temperatures. 
Both investigated projects show great promise in terms of benefits for the building owners 
and the district heating suppliers: 
The main benefit of the building owners is the lowered heat use, as well as a lower 
distribution heat loss in their local distribution. Furthermore, reducing the return 
temperature of the district heating has an economic incentive to the building owners 
provided by the district heating suppliers, since the district heating suppliers benefit from 
this as well.  
One main benefit of an improved system is if the peak loads can be reduced. This means 
lower supply temperatures, which in turn means less expensive peak boilers and savings 
on distribution heat losses. It also means the possibility to utilise more renewable heat 
sources in the production of district heating.  
The final remark will be a reemphasising of the point, that these two systems can and 
should be applicated together wherever possible: 
Using the lowest possible supply temperature for all outdoor temperatures is suitable in 
most buildings controlled by a building management system. It might need some small 
adjustments after the first implementation, but after these have been made, the supply and 
return temperatures can be effectively lowered. 
Using the domestic hot water capacity limitation is suitable to implement in buildings with 
low circulation heat losses. The biggest hurdle is, that it is not yet available on the market.  
A great deal of buildings has the opportunity and the prerequisites to utilise both systems 
causing a synergetic effect. It would help transform the good system of today into the 
superior system that we crucially need tomorrow.

8 Future work 
 
This part of the report is based on the work done during the master thesis and describes 
suggestions for future work areas, that could supplement the findings in the thesis.  
 Possible areas to investigate 
8.1.1 Domestic hot water capacity limitation in offices 
It could be investigated how the capacity limitation of domestic hot water described in 
chapter 5, is beneficial in office buildings like 118. The peak loads are predictable for 
domestic hot water in dwellings, but for offices, the load is more evenly distributed 
throughout the day.  
8.1.2 Domestic hot water capacity limitation in Nordhavn  
It could be investigated how low the domestic hot water capacity limit in the dwelling can 
be lowered, to avoid high return temperatures during the night. Followed by 
experimentally show that it functions better, without loss of comfortable temperatures 
during peak hours.  
8.1.3 Domestic hot water circulation heat loss in offices 
It could be investigated how much the circulation heat loss is in proportion to the actual 
hot water use in building 118. To evaluate if the advantages of the circulation loop, justify 
the high heat loss. Instead, an option to be investigated could be to have electric heat 
exchangers installed at the kitchenette’s and toilet’s draw-off points. This would also allow 
for the supply and return temperatures to be lowered even more in the distribution system. 
This since the district heating does not need to be warm enough to deliver domestic hot 
water at 55 ºC. 
8.1.4 Using a rolling average weather compensation control 
It could be investigated how avoiding unnecessary high supply temperatures could be 
achieved by improving the heating system control. Instead of being controlled by a weather 
compensation curve that follows the outdoor temperature 1-1, the control could be based 
on a rolling average of the outdoor temperature. That would allow for the use of the 
thermal mass of the building. Using the control, peak temperatures could be evened out 
and an already sufficiently warm building is not overheated because of a single cold night.   
8.1.5 Control north and south façades differently  
In the thesis and the associated work, the two heating circuits for the North and the South 
façade, was controlled the same way, and the supply and return temperatures are almost 
identical. However, they can be controlled differently. It could be a future investigation to 
investigate differentiated control in building 118. For example, the lower higher heat loss 
from the northern façade could mean, that a further reduction of supply temperatures is 
possible.
