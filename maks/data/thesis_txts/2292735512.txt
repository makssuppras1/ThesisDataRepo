Summary (English)
This thesis is a study of single- and multi period models for stock market trad-
ing. The thesis has two main objectives. The rst is a study of models relating
risk and return. The second is the modelling of expected return of each asset.
This part investigates whether the return of a single asset is dependent on the
industry, the general economy or other descriptive or non-descriptive variables.
The modelling of the risk-return relationship is based on a literature study of
existing objectives and constraints. The models are implemented and tested on
two portfolios. The test results are discussed based on a number of parameters:
The CPU time, the return, the risk, and the Sharpe-Ratio.
The tests showed that Markowitz, the mean-variance and the Sharpe-Ratio
model yielded approximately the same result regarding the optimal Sharpe-
Ratio. The result of the Value-at-Risk model diered greatly from the other,
with a much larger variance and return. The larger CPU time of the SOCP is
made up by the fact that it only needs to run once for the Sharpe-Ratio model.
The study of the return function is two fold. First, a statistical study of the
behaviour of the historical return is performed. Here we use PCA to look for
industrial dependencies. Secondly the model of the expected return is discussed.
We test three dierent models on the index tracking model in the multi period
framework.
The rst model, used the historical mean as an estimate of the
expected return. The second model includes the overall market behaviour in a
factor model, and third, we compute the factor model based on factor analysis.
We noted that even though the factor analysis model did not contain a physical

return.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The nancial world, and in particular the stock market, is becoming a more and
more complex thing. As the technology advances, the time, between an event
and the eect is detectable on the stock market, is decreasing.
In the 1950's Markowitz proposed a mathematical model for portfolio selection
[CT06]. The goal of the model was to help investors create a portfolio, which
achieves the highest possible expected return for a given level of risk [HGT11].
Since then the number of models for portfolio selection has increased drastically.
Today, convex models exist for single period markets, such as Markowitz port-
folio selection model, and for multi period markets.
As with all other models, the models for portfolio selection is based on a number
of assumptions regarding market behaviour. In particular, the models discussed
in this thesis all assume that the market is complete and that trading performed
by the investor does not inuence the stock market.
The complete market assumption enable us to model the risk and return of each
asset. The techniques to do so may be dierent based on the model, but they
all rely on historical data. For highly volatile markets this may be an issue.
However in this case, the market can no longer be said to be complete. This
means that the models presented in this thesis can not be trusted in times of a
nancial crises etc.

1.1
Financial Concepts
This section gives a short introduction to the most widely used concepts and
terms in nance used in this thesis.
The portfolio selection problem chooses the assets, which stock prices will either
yield the best prot or yield a certain prot at a minimum risk. An asset is a
product that has a market value, i.e. an item with an exchange value. A stock is
a type of security in a company or asset which gives you part ownership [Pli97].
A portfolio consist of a number of stocks in dierent assets [HGT11]. A market is
any, place physical or on-line, where goods of any type are traded. The markets
may have a one way price or a two way price. The one way price is, what we
know from the stores. Here there is only a buying price. The two way price,
is the prices at which the trader will buy or sell his asset. These prices are
called the bid and ask prices. Examples of the use of two way prices are: stock
market trading, and currency trading [Pli97]. The bid price is the price which
the buyer is willing to pay for a certain stock, and the ask price is the price the
seller wants for the stock. In an open market where everyone can trade with
everyone, one normally sets both a bid and ask price. If a bid price is high, a
trader will normally want to sell his assets to a buyer, just like he wants to buy
the asset if the asking price is low [HGT11].
The dierence between the bid and ask price is called the spread. If the spread
is small compared to the market spread, it normally indicates that the trader
is willing to do a lot of business, where a wide spread usually indicates an
unwillingness to trade.
When a trader is setting up his portfolio he may buy a certain amount of stock
in an asset. This is called 'taking the long position' or 'going long'. In most
markets the trader may also sell assets which he does not own. This is done by
borrowing the stocks from someone who owns them. In order to close such a
position the trader will have to buy the stocks and return them to the owner
[HGT11]. This is called 'going short'.

1.1.4
Complete Market
In most literature the market is assumed to be complete, i.e. it must full the
following six assumptions [Mer14]:
1. All assets have limited liability.
2. There are no transaction costs, taxes, or problems with indivisibility of
assets (i.e. it is possible to buy fractions of a stock).
3. There are a sucient number of investors with comparable wealth levels
so that each investor believes that he can buy and sell as much of an asset
as he wants at the market price without aecting it.
4. The capital market is always in equilibrium (i.e. there is no trading at
non-equilibrium prices, in other words, the spread is zero).
5. There exists an exchange market for borrowing and lending at the same
rate of interest
6. Short-sales of all assets, with full use of the proceeds, is allowed.

1.3
Project Outline
This thesis is a study of single- and multi period models for stock market trad-
ing. The thesis has two main objectives. The rst is a study of models relating
the risk and return. Specically, we focus on Quadratic Programming (QP) and
Second Order Cone Programming (SOCP). The second objective is the mod-
elling of the expected return of each asset. This part investigates whether the
return of a single asset is dependent on the industry, the general markets econ-
omy or other descriptive or non descriptive variables.
More precisely, Chapter 2 describes the properties of portfolio selection. We give
a short introduction to the dierent models found in the literature. Markowitz
selection problem, the mean-variance problem, Sharpe Ratio problem, and value-
at-risk problem is introduced and additional constraints are suggested.
Chapter 3 provides a case study of two dierent datasets. The datasets consists
of historical data collected from Google nance. The time series of the stock
prices are investigated as well as trends within industries. Secondly we try se-
lecting an optimal portfolio based on the models described in Chapter 2.
Chapter 4 introduces the concepts of multi period programming, and a discus-
sion of the dierent models described in the literature. The Model Predictive
Control (MPC) strategy and dynamical programming are described in detail.
The chapter focuses on two objective functions: The index tracking model, and
the mean-variance model, from the single period, which is expanded to a multi
period framework.
The stochastic behaviour of the return function is discussed in detail. We intro-
duce the concepts of factor models to model the expected return. In particular,
we explain how the market behaviour is included in the prediction of the ex-
pected return, and how we might model the expected return based on factor
analysis.
Chapter 5 provides a case study of the index tracking model described in Chap-
ter 4 based on the data from Chapter 3. Specically the model is tested for
three dierent models of the expected return: The expected return based solely
on the historical mean, a factor model including the market behaviour, and a
factor model based on factor analysis.

2.1
Risk Measurements

2.8
Discussion
The models in this chapter are some of the most commonly found portfolio
models in the literature.
Markowitz portfolio problem and the mean-variance problem are both quadratic
programmes with linear constraints. The advantage of these type of models is
that they are easy to solve. However, the performance of these models depend
on the accuracy of the expected return and covariance matrix. In Section 2.3
we noted that even small changes in the minimum return could create drastic
changes to the portfolio selection [Cal08]. Herzog, [Her05] and Cornuejols et al
[CT06] both argue that some kind of sensitivity analysis or robustness check is
needed to ensure a reliable solution for these type of models.
The Sharpe-Ratio model is also based on the expected return and variance, but
has the advantage that we do not need to perform the optimization multiple
times to nd the optimal SR as is the case for Markowitz and the mean-variance
model. However, the model transforms into a SOCP which numerically are much
harder to deal with [Cal08].
The Value-at-Risk model is also a SOCP. The advantage of this model is that
we use the VaR instead of the variance, which does seem to model the risk
better c.f. Section 2.1 and
[Cal08]. Most often this model is only optimized
once, since normally we want our model to be accurate to a certain degree, e.g.
95 %. Naturally some uncertainty will always be involved, since the mean and
variance of the distributions we model our random variables after, are still only
estimates. The fact that we do not need to perform the optimization multiple
times makes this model more popular for the multi period models, c.f. [Her05],
[Ale10], [Cal08] and [Inf06].
It is clear that there are advantages and disadvantages with all the models. In
the next chapter we test the performance of the models on two datasets.

disadvantages of all the models. Markowitz portfolio model is great if we want
to imitate the behaviour of a stock by a number of other stocks and thereby
reducing the overall risk.
The reason this model is suitable for this type of
problem is that it takes a minimum return as input, in this case, the expected
return of the stock we want to model. The model is not optimal for general op-
timization since we have to perform the model multiple times for a wide range
of minimum returns.
The mean-variance model also needs to be computed multiple times with vary-
ing values of λ. However, this model has the advantage that it shows us how
much the solution depends on the importance of risk and return.
The Sharpe-Ratio model only needs to be solved once. Granted the SOCP is
more complex to solve, and does therefore take a longer time to solve. How-
ever, since we only need to solve the problem once, this model seems the better
choice, if we have no prerequisites regarding the minimum expected return or
our adversity to risk.

Closed loop optimization
Where the open loop optimization model determines the sequence of portfolio

at every time stamp. I.e. at each new time step, we add the new data to the
model and performs the multi period optimization once more [Cal14].

the rst time stamp is revealed. This data is included in the data set, and the
oldest data point may be taken out. A new estimation of the expected return
and variance is then performed and the optimization for the entire time frame
is then performed once more.

Conclusion and Perspective
6.1
Conclusion
In this thesis we have described and discussed the models of single period stock
market trading. The models have been tested on two portfolios consisting of 30
assets each. We showed that based on the current data, we saw no indication
that an industrially related correlation existed for the returns and stock prices
of each asset. We discussed the advantages and disadvantages of the SOCP
compared to the QP. The SR model only needed to be computed once and pro-
vided approximately the same result as Markowitz portfolio selection model.
This made up for the longer CPU time compared to the QP models. The QP
models provided us with the entire ecient frontier, thereby allowing the trader
to make an informed choice regarding the risk-return trade o. The VaR model
needed to be performed a multiple of times. The SOC framework of the problem
increased the running time of this model dramatically. The model did however
provide a completely dierent result compared to the other three models, c.f.
Figure 3.17.
The thesis also described and discussed the models of multi period stock mar-
ket trading. We discussed the advantages of a closed loop framework compared
to consecutive single period models and open loop frameworks. The updating
scheme of the portfolio was developed and the eect of cost functions in partic-
ular transaction costs were discussed. The MPC problem of index tracking was
==PAGE:110==
96
Conclusion and Perspective
described as well as the dynamical multi period version of the mean-variance
model.
We discussed the importance of a good model for the return function, and tested
the models on the index tracking program. The return function discussed were:
Models of dierent time frames based on historical data, a single factor model
with a benchmark modelling the market behaviour, and a factor model based
on factor analysis. We found that the factor analysis model provided the most
consistent results of the two factor models, thereby limiting the necessity of
multiple scenario testing in order to obtain a robust solution. Furthermore, the
factor analysis model also provided the lowest tracking error, thereby indicating
the best estimate of the return.
6.2
Perspective and future work
From Chapter 5 we noted that the risk-return models are only as good as the
the predicted expected return. Using a factor model for the expected return
seemed to yield a fairly good estimate. The factor analysis applied in our mod-
els were constant for each time frame. However we know that as time changes
so does the behaviour of the general stock market. Implementing a dynamic
factor analysis to our expected return as described in Ludvigson [A14], might
therefore be a better approach than the static model presented in this thesis.
Secondly, an implementation of the return as a factor model based on multiple
market factors, might show better results than the single market factor. The
model would provide better insight to which factors plays an important role in
determining the value of the stock price. If we were able to identify the major
components, we might be able to make our models more robust to great changes
in the stock market.
Furthermore the implementation of the SOCP in a multi period framework,
might provide some interesting results for the VaR model, since this provided
some unique results in the single period framework.

Self Evaluation
Working with this project has been quite an experience. The outline of the
project was quite clear to start out with. However as the project progressed, it
became clear that some aspects were more important than what I rst assumed.
In April I nished the rst draft of the single period model, and started working
on the multi period model as planned. However at the end of May I revisited
the single period model and rewrote most of the text. This iteration process,
took a lot longer than anticipated.
Furthermore the original outline of the multi period factor changed quite a bit
towards the end. The reason for this was in two parts. 1) it bothered me that
the factor models were never fully explained in the articles I read. 2) The index
tracking model gave me the possibility of testing the return model. The model
showed that we could not get a better result without nding another may of
modelling the return.
