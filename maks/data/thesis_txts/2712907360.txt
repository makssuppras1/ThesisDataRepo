Cathodic protection
Abstract
The offshore wind industry expands in line with the increased focus on sustainable energy.
The growing market requires optimization of all parameters relevant to cathodic protection,
of which one of them is the calcareous layer forming near the anodes. The implementation
of the effects of the calcareous layer in the dimensioning of cathodic protection will improve
the protective range of the anodes, which could lead to large savings.
This project aims to characterize the electrical and chemical properties of the calcareous
layer near anodes used for cathodic protection. The influence of electrode potential and
water flow is examined. The findings will add to a better understanding of the effects of the
calcareous formation during offshore cathodic protection.
The experimental setup aims to simulate impressed current cathodic protection of an offshore
monopile structure in North Sea conditions.
Six cold rolled ’fine plate’ steel specimens
of 100x100x2 mm3 in synthetic seawater are cathodically polarized to -1000, -1050 and -
1100 mVAg/AgCl/seawater respectively. Three specimens are exposed to tidal water flow and
three specimens to constant water flow. The calcareous layer formed on the steel surface
is characterized by ongoing measurements of the electrical properties and the surrounding
electrolyte. Subsequently, the calcareous layer is examined with optical microscopy, SEM-
EDS, TGA/DSC and cross-sectional analysis.
The results show that the optimal cathodic conditions for calcareous precipitation are -1050
mVAg/AgCl/Seawater for tidal water flow and -1100 mVAg/AgCl/Seawater for constant water flow.
Tidal water flow is more favorable for developing a calcareous layer, with a denser structure
compared to constant water flow. The maximum ohmic resistance is found at 1573 mΩ· m2
with tidal water flow at -1050 mVAg/AgCl/Seawater. The chemical composition is primarily
CaCO3 with small compounds of Mg(OH)2 along the steel surface.
The experiments indicate significant improvements by including the effects of the calcareous
layer in dimensioning of offshore wind structures.

Introduction
1
Introduction
The Esbjerg Declaration was signed the 18th of May 2022 and promised a tenfold growth
of the offshore wind capacity in the North Sea to 150 GW in 2050 [2]. The offshore wind
industry is a growing market worldwide. The year-over-year growth from 2019 to 2020 was
53 % corresponding to a total wind power global capacity of 793 GW [3]. The development
of the industry goes hand in hand with the increasing focus on sustainable energy, which
requires innovation and optimization in order to withstand the growing market and demands.
Corrosion protection is important for dimensioning offshore wind structures. The harsh en-
vironment with wind, waves, and high salinity challenges the corrosion protection system,
which should ensure stability and minimal maintenance throughout the service life. Corro-
sion protection can be done with cathodic protection with anodes, where the dimensioning
is based on standards and regulations. The traditional method is the use of galvanic anodes
(GACP). This method is trustworthy, but comes with some disadvantages; limited protec-
tion capacity, maintenance requirements, and pollution of nonferrous metals in the water.
Another corrosion protection method is the use of impressed current (ICCP). The method
costs less than GACP, has unlimited protection capacity, and releases fewer elements to the
ocean environment. The ICCP method introduces new ranges of corrosion protection and
thereby new fields of investigation.
Offshore wind structures are constructed in numerous variations, but the three main types
of structures are monopile, tripod, and jacket structure as shown in figure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: Three types of offshore wind structures; Monopile, tripod, and jacket structure
[4].

Introduction
Roughly categorized are the monopiles applicable for water depths of 0-25 m (up to 50 m
with XXL-monopiles [5]) and tripod and jacket structures of 20-50 m [6]. The sub-structures
are installed with a transition piece on top, where the wind turbine is installed. The choice
between the different substructure types is a balancing of costs related to water depth, soil
conditions, water flow and more.
Figure 1.2: Installation of transition piece on monopile [7].
The monopile, which will be the focus of this thesis, is installed with such great forces that
any pre-installation of corrosion protection is impossible due to large mechanical impacts.
It is therefore a big advantage to limit the installation of the protective anodes to the top
of the monopile; The transition piece. This places great demands on the size and range of
the corrosion protection system, as the usable locations for future wind farms are reaching
deeper waters further from shore. Alternatively, an anode cage could be installed at the sea
bed, but the costs are very high at approximately 500.000 DKK per structure [8].
Dimensioning of corrosion protection systems is based on computer simulations with various
input values. Important parameters to consider are the required protective capacity, amount
of anodes, size, and position in order to cathodically protect the monopile structure. One
phenomenon which is not fully understood is the effect of the calcareous layer forming near
the anodes as a result of the cathodic protection. The electric resistance of the calcareous
layer could be advantageous in order to improve the protective range of the anodes, which
could lead to large savings.

Problem Statement
2
Problem Statement
2.1
Background and motivation
The norms and standards that dictate the conditions for corrosion protection of offshore wind
structures demand a minimal electrode potential of metal surfaces at -800 mVAg/AgCl/seawater
and a limit of -1100 mVAg/AgCl/seawater [9]. These values are fixed and are chosen to ensure
cathodic protection of the monopile structure. The limit of -1100 mVAg/AgCl/seawater is to
minimize hydrogen embrittlement of the steel, which could lead to a weakening of the struc-
ture.
Dimensioning of impressed current cathodic protection applies simplified assumptions. One
is that the generated calcareous layer on the steel surface has a fixed effect on the current
consumption. Theory and previous research [10, 11] indicate that the calcareous layer could
contribute beneficially to the corrosion protection system, thereby extending the protective
range.
2.2
Project Content
This master thesis aims to characterize the electrical and chemical properties of the cal-
careous layer near anodes with impressed electrical current. Effects of cathodic protection
are tested with experimental work and strive to simulate North Sea environmental condi-
tions. The experimental setup is a successor of previous theses [10, 11] specially developed
to simulate North Sea offshore conditions. Potentiostats maintain the electrode potential at
a constant level and allows for monitoring of the electrical properties of the calcareous layer
throughout the experimental period. Six test specimens of 100x100x2 mm3 sandblasted steel
plates are tested simultaneously; 3 at constant water flow and 3 at tidal water flow.
The main focus is to obtain a deeper understanding of the electrochemical current require-
ments at the border of overprotection at -1100 mVAg/AgCl/seawater. The calcareous layer’s
electrical and chemical properties and composition will be examined with the use of optical
microscopy, thermogravimetric analysis, scanning electron microscopy, and cross-sectional
analysis.

3.2

Corrosion and corrosion rate depends on numerous parameters in North Sea environments.
Algae growth and other biological influences are left out due to the use of synthetic seawater

3.2.1
Oxygen
Oxygen is required for the corrosion process since it is a main component in the cathodic
process, where oxygen is consumed at the cathodic surface as hydroxide is formed. The
amount of oxygen can thereby become a limiting factor for the corrosion rate.
The oxygen concentration in the North Sea varies throughout the year between 10.7 mg/L
in the winter and 6.5 mg/L in August and September [14].

3.3.1
Galvanic Anode Cathodic Protection
Galvanic Anode Cathodic Protection uses the advantages of the electrochemical reactivity
series by sacrificing a less noble material relative to the structure relevant for protection.
This method is widely used in offshore structures, ship hulls, and harbors with the typical
materials used as zinc and aluminium alloys. The processes of the GACP are shown in figure

Anode reaction GACP:
Zn −→Zn2+ + 2 e−
(4)
The main advantage of GACP is the reliability of the system as the system does not rely
on any external energy.
As long as the relation between the surface area of anode and
cathode is maintained is the structure protected. At the same time, there is no danger of
overprotection with sacrificial anodes since the lowest free corrosion potential of zinc and
aluminium is -1050 mVAg/AgCl/seawater. The downside of GACP is the need for replacement
and the environmental concerns about the fact that materials are continuously being released

3.4
Polarization
Polarization is the change of a material’s natural electrode potential as a function of the
related current required. A schematic drawing of the cathodic polarization curve for steel in
water is shown in figure 3.10.
Figure 3.10: Schematic of the steady-state cathodic polarization curve for steel in seawater.
The free corrosion potential is ϕcorr, whereas the most positive potential at which protection
is achieved is ϕprot, and ϕoverprot is the level of overprotection. [20].
The cathodic polarization process starts at the free corrosion potential whereby the current
density rises until it reached ϕprot in which the cathodic protection is achieved. The cathodic
process is powered by the reduction of oxygen on the steel surface leading to calcareous
precipitations. At low potentials is hydrogen evolution thermodynamically possible leading
to an increase in the current density.

Depolarization is the natural process of a material returning to its natural electrode potential.
GACP and ICCP systems are designed to force an electrode potential different from the

Calcareous deposits
With cathodic protection may a calcareous layer form on the cathodic steel surface due to
the local alkaline environment with the production of OH−. The calcareous layer can thereby
reduce the current density necessary to uphold the electrode potential.
The composition of the calcareous deposits varies with water flow, pH, temperature, current
density and more. The main components are CaCO3 and Mg(OH)2 according to equation
11 and 12 [19].

The resistance of the calcareous layer may depend on the composition and morphology of
the deposits. As the border of over-protection is reached around -1100 mVAg/AgCl/seawater
is cleavage of water increased as an additional cathode reaction as shown in equation 6c.
This reaction is more willing to happen through the chalk layer since dissolved oxygen is not
required. Assuming a calcareous layer with the ability to diffuse water into the steel surface
will the calcareous layer potentially grow almost unlimited.
The current density affects the ratio of calcium and magnesium, where magnesium hydrox-
ide typically will form at high current densities [19]. The mechanisms and composition of
calcareous layers are examined further in section 4.1.

5
Experiment
The experimental setup used for the project is the third generation of a testing principle
developed in January 2020 [10]. The test setup is developed specifically to simulate North
Sea ocean conditions related to water flow, temperature, and water composition. The test
setup was improved and refined in 2021 [11], where performance difficulties and reliability
of the system were improved. Both studies [10, 11] obtained promising knowledge about
calcareous deposits forming on the steel surface with the influence of tidal water flow.
5.1
Principle
The setup aims to simulate impressed current cathodic protection of an offshore monopile
structure in the North Sea conditions:
• Water temperatures of 10 °C.
• tidal water flow up to 0.8 m/s.
The monopile is simulated with 100x100x2 mm3 steel plates which are cathodically protected
in synthetic seawater with potentiostats. It is expected to see a calcareous layer forming on
the steel plates during the experiments, whereby the resistance of the deposits will increase.
The focus is to characterize the electrical and chemical properties of the calcareous layer
and the influence of electrode potential. The electrical properties are examined with instant-
off and depolarization, after which the chemical properties are subsequently examined with
optical microscopy, SEM-EDS, TGA/DSC, cross-sectional analysis.
The experimental procedure aims at:
• Simulate cathodic protection of a monopile structure in the North Sea.
• Characterize electrical properties of the calcareous layer near the anodes.
• Examine the chemical composition and morphology of the calcareous layer and the
influence of electrode potential and water flow.
• Relate calcareous electrical and chemical properties to the polarization curve of steel
in seawater and dimensioning of offshore wind structures.

Simplifications
6
Simplifications
Water flow
North Sea is cyclic with an average maximum water flow of 0.8 m/s. The test facility is
capable of similar water flow, but a maximum water flow of 0.6 m/s is chosen, due to the
limitation of the cooling system. According to earlier studies [11], water flow at 0.8 m/s lead
to overheating water temperatures above 20 °C. The water flow at 0.6 m/s is thereby chosen
to maintain water temperatures at reasonable levels; On average 15.0 °C.
Water temperature
The average North Sea water temperature is 10 °C. The test setup is capable of cooling, see
section 5.2.8, but the heat generation of the water pump limits the water temperatures at
15 °C with 0.6 m/s flow rate.
The temperature was held stable at 15 ±0.5 °C through the main period of the experiment.
It should be noted, however, that the starting temperature was 20 °C, but the temperature
stabilized at 15 °C after 16 hours.
On day 14 a power shut down led to a momentary
temperature increase up to 20 °C, as the cooling water pumps were not working.
The
temperature was brought down within 12 hours.
Test sample
The test sample should preferably be a scaled-down monopile structure, but due to simpli-
fications, the monopile structure is simulated with 10x10 cm steel plates. This is done to
ensure suitable water flow around the test sample. Size effects may influence the precipita-
tion of the calcareous layer due to variations in the water flow. However, size effects will not
be regarded, due to the complexity of the mechanisms.
Closed water system
The test setup uses a closed water system of 100 L per exercise. The amount of water should
constitute an adequate quantity of minerals for the processes. Water quality is monitored
to detect any variations in temperature, pH, oxygen level, salinity, bicarbonate-, chlorine-,
magnesium- or calcium content.

8
Discussion
The discussion follows the chronological structure in results section 7. All electrode potentials
mentioned in the following section are against Ag/AgCl/seawater unless otherwise stated.
8.1
Preliminary experiments
8.1.1
Verification of potentiostats and data measurements
From tests with one, two and three specimens at varying distances, the potentiostatic per-
formance of chrono amperometry is verified. The percentage deviation was estimated to be
4.1 % or less for all parameters, which is acceptable.
However, it should be noted that the preliminary tests were performed with stagnant water
whereas the water flow in the main experiment is up to 0.6 m/s. The water flow induced
short-term irregularities in the polarization process.
The signal of the datalogger when
scanning seemed to affect the potentiostatic current which led to misleading datalogger
measurements of the current density. The deviations were solved by extracting data directly
from VoltaLab controlling the potentiostats.
Another deviation appeared for the current density in the tidal flume, which can be seen
in figure 7.5 to 7.7.
The error consists of periodic deviations in the current density to
a significantly lower level. The exact solution for the deviation was never found despite
numerous attempts of adjustments including the exchange of the potentiostats. External
measurements with SCE thus showed that the electrode potential of each steel specimen
was maintained despite the lower current. The fault might be found in the assembly of the
electric circuit, but it was prioritized to continue the experiments without disassembling the
electric circuit panel.
8.1.2
Calcareous deposits at different electrode potentials
From figure 7.1 clear variations are seen in the formation of the calcareous layer. A fine-grain
calcareous layer made of small-sized spheroidicals is formed at both -900 and -1000 mV, with
a denser structure at -1000 mV. A clear change in the structures is seen at -1100 mV where
the calcareous layer forms in flakes with holes to the bare steel. Flakes of the calcareous layer
were also found on the flume bed. This indicates a very aggressive cathode reaction that
forms hydrogen bubbles at the steel surface. Hydrogen bubbles burst through the calcareous
layer, leaving holes in the calcareous layer for the bare steel. This indicates that -1100 mV
is not beneficial for the formation of a thick calcareous layer; At least not initially. Also, the
current density reached above 7000 mA/m2, which is many times higher than the conditions
of reality around 200 mA/m2.
It should be noted, however, that the initial experiments were performed without water flow.
The main experiments are performed with strong water flow, which will significantly affect
the calcareous formation and current density. Water flow will slow down the precipitation of
calcareous deposits as the hydroxide formed at the steel surface is transported way; The local
Page 55 of 64

alkaline environment cannot be maintained. The water flow will increase the mass transport
of reactants to the cathodic steel surface leading to significantly higher current densities.
The initial investigations of electrode potentials of -900, -1000 and -1100 mV contribute to a
better understanding of calcareous development at different electrode potentials. Intending
to build and document the largest possible calcareous layer is -1000 mV chosen as the starting
point for the main experiment.
8.1.3
pH experiment near anode and cathode
The multi-colored pH environment between anode and cathode is shown in figure 7.3. From
the figure is it clear that pH develops locally at the steel plate and the titanium anode. The
steel plate shows color indications of pH 10 or more, which corresponds to the theory of
the cathodic process in equation 6c. Hydroxide causes local alkaline environment, which is
indicated by the purple color. Clear color indications of pH 2 or less are seen at the titanium
anode. Here, anode oxidation reactions take place with the production of H+, which causes
local acidic environment.
In figure 7.3c is a thin dark area seen around the steel plate. However, the dark color is not
visible from the titanium screw, which indicates that titanium is not included in the cathode
reaction due to strong passivation. This substantiates that titanium is an optimal material
for making a metallic connection to the steel plate without affecting the processes.
8.2
Main experiment
It was desired to achieve stabilized conditions for the calcareous layer to determine the con-
ditions applicable for stabilized conditions in real life. It must be made clear that stabilized
conditions were not achieved after 62 days of cathodic protection. The results relevant for
discussion in this thesis are therefore meant as an indicator that contributes to relevant
knowledge for future studies.
8.2.1
Current density
The current density (figure 7.5 to 7.7) varies throughout the experimental period between
1691 and 22 mA/m2 for tidal water flow and 2398 and 108 mA/m2 for constant water flow.
The initial phase demands high current densities due to the bare sandblasted steel with
direct access for oxygen diffusion. As a calcareous layer builds up is the current requirement
lowered.
It is seen from figure 7.6 that the tidal current density fluctuates drastically in the first
5 days of exposure. During periods of high water flow is the oxygen diffusion very high
and the hydroxide produced on the steel surface flushed away immediately allowing great
current consumption. In periods of quiet water flow is the oxygen diffusion lowered and the
hydroxide produced on the steel surface more willing to remain. Local alkaline environment
around the steel surface leads to increased precipitation of calcareous deposits.

of the calcareous layer discussed section 8.2.3.

8.2.3
Resistance of calcareous layer
The resistance of the calcareous layer is shown in figure 7.10. Throughout the experimental
period is the ohmic resistance of the calcareous layer significantly larger for tidal water flow
compared to constant water flow. For each potential shift, the resistance of the calcareous
layer decreases initially. The greatest decrease is seen for tidal water flow shifting from -1050
to -1100 mV electrode potential, where the ohmic resistance decreases by 76 %. At the
shift from -1000 to -1050 mV is the decrease 7 %, whereas the decreases are 26 % and 21
% respectively for constant water flow. This could indicate that the potential shifts lead to
initial damage of the calcareous layer with increased hydrogen embrittlement forming on the
steel surface.
The strongest growth in the resistance of the calcareous layer is seen for tidal water flow
at -1050 mV, where also the greatest resistance of the calcareous layer is measured at 1573
mΩ· m2. With a minimum current density of 100 mA/m2 for real-life offshore structures
will the maximum resistance lead to an IR-drop above 150 mV according to equation 13.
Potential IR −drop :
100 mA/m2 · 1573 mΩ· m2 = 157.3 mV
(13)
If the resistance of the calcareous layer was included one-to-one in models of dimensioning of
offshore monopiles it could lead to large expansions of the protective range of ICCP systems.
The results indicate great potential for stabilized conditions where the resistance could grow
even larger.
8.2.4
Water quality
In figure 7.11 are selected water measurements displayed. It is seen that the pH, oxygen and
conductivity of the water are relatively stable. However, the free chlorine and bicarbonate
content shifted as the experiment progressed. Chlorine affects the polarization conditions
and bicarbonate content affects the level of calcareous precipitation. According to Hoover’s
alignment chart in appendix A.3 the change of bicarbonate content is affecting the saturation
pH of calcareous deposits. For pure synthetic water is the saturation pH 6.9, and for the
worst-case measured is it pH 9.1. This will affect the precipitation of calcareous deposits,
whereby it led to a change of the water on day 11 and 32.
The main components of calcareous deposits are assumed to be magnesium and calcium.
ICP water measurements show that the content of magnesium and calcium remain stable at
high levels throughout the experimental period. It was not possible to detect any decrease
in calcium or magnesium content in the water due to calcareous deposition.

Suggestions for future work
9
Suggestions for future work
Size effects
Due to flow differences throughout the specimens (10x10 cm), significant differences have
been observed in the type of calcareous layer formed (structure, thickness). The general
characterization of the calcareous layer in this project is based on characteristic uniform
areas. It will be interesting to investigate the morphology and resistance throughout the
steel specimen i.e. whether the design of the specimen can be optimized so that size effects
are minimized.
Water flow and temperature
The North Sea conditions averages temperature of 10 °C and water flow up to 0.8 m/s.
Due to limited cooling capacity, was water flow velocity of 0.6 m/s chosen to withstand a
reasonable water temperature of 15 °C. An improvement of these parameters will require a
rebuild of the test facilities which was not possible during this project.
Potentiostats
Two potentiostats were used to polarize the steel specimens. Optimally, one potentiostat
should be used for one steel specimen allowing simultaneous studies of various electrode
potentials.
During the experiments of this thesis, the signal of the datalogger momentarily affected
the potentiostatic current which led to misleading datalogger measurements of the current
density.
The reason for this was not found but should be investigated further in future
studies.
Time limit
The construction of a thick calcareous layer takes time. After 39-62 days, no stabilization
has yet been seen in the development of the calcareous layer. The electrical resistance of
the calcareous layer could grow even larger for stabilized conditions which encourage future
long-term experiments.
Calcareous layer development at other electrode potentials
To include the resistance of the calcareous layer in the dimensioning of offshore structures,
is it necessary to map the entire potential spectrum from -800 to -1100 mV. In this project,
the focus has been to investigate the development of the calcareous layer near the anodes.
It will be interesting to investigate the properties and development of the calcareous layer
at other electrode potentials, corresponding to areas further from the top of the monopile.

Conclusion
Six 100x100x2 mm3 steel specimens are cathodically polarized at electrode potentials of
-1000, -1050 and -1100 mVAg/AgCl/Seawater in tidal and constant water flow of 0.6 m/s.
The effects and properties of the calcareous layer forming on the steel surface are examined.
The results show the following:
• The optimal conditions for developing a calcareous layer are -1050 mVAg/AgCl/Seawater
for tidal water flow and -1100 mVAg/AgCl/Seawater for constant water flow.
• Tidal water flow is more favorable for calcareous precipitation compared to constant
water flow. The calcareous layer is denser for tidal water flow and has greater thickness
and weight.
• A maximum ohmic resistance of the calcareous layer is found at 1573 mΩ· m2 with
tidal water flow at -1050 mVAg/AgCl/Seawater.
• The chemical composition of the calcareous layer is primarily CaCO3 with small com-
pounds of Mg(OH)2 along the steel surface.
• The experiments contribute to the development of a polarization curve for potentials
between -1000 and -1100 mVAg/AgCl/Seawater. However, it must be noted that stabilized
conditions were not fully achieved.
The results contribute to a better understanding of the mechanisms applicable for impressed
current cathodic protection of offshore wind structures. The experiments indicate significant
improvements by including the effects of the calcareous layer in dimensioning of offshore
wind structures.

samme potentiale og strømstyrke. Ved disse test-forsøg er afvigelserne klargjort. Afvigelserne
er acceptable for det videre forsøg.
Ved udførsel af tilsvarende forsøg med varierende kabellængder i kobber blev forskellen i
strømstyrken målt til 0.2 mA. Trods den lille indflydelse af kabellængden, holdes kabel-
længder så vidt muligt ens for alle parrallel-forsøg. Særligt for titan-ledninger hvor mod-
standen er større end for kobber. Titan-ledninger bruges i overgangen fra tørre til våde
samleled; F.eks. ved fastmontering på plade og titannet. Titan-ledninger benyttes her for
at mindske galvanisk påvirkning af systemet, da titan er særlig korrosionsbestandigt og pas-
siveret.
Ud fra tabel C.2 ses en afvigelse fra outputkanalerne på potentiostaterne op til 1.4 mV
svarende til en afvigelse på 0.12 %.

Ved -1100 mV ses en klar ændring i kalklaget struktur som består mere af flager, hvor der også
ses flager ved siden af prøveemnerne. Dette tyder på en meget angressiov katodereaktion,
som danner brint ved ståloverfladen. Brintbobler springer igennem kalklaget og efterlader

Et hovedfokus for dette speciale er opbygge og dokumentere det størst mulige kalklag. Med
udgangspunkt i mikroskopbillederne C.9a til C.9f tyder det altså på at -1100 mV ikke er
gavnlig for dannelse af et tykt kalklag; I hvert fald ikke indledningsvist. Det skal dog be-
mærkes at de indledende forsøg er udført uden vandstrømning. De rigtige forsøg udføres med
kraftig vandstrømning, som vil påvirke kalkdannelsen betydeligt. Vandstrømning vil bremse
dannelsen af kalklag, da det dannede hydroxid ved ståloverfladen bliver skylles væk, hvorved
den høje lokale pH ved ståloverfladen ikke kan opretholdes i samme grad. Det forventes
derfor at se væsentlig højere strømtætheder med vandstrømning.
Fra TGA på figur C.10 ses det at det dannede kalklag primært består af magnesiumhydroxid
og en lille del calciumkarbonat. Dette stemmer fint overens med pourbaix diagrammerne A.6
og A.7, hvor det ses at magnesium udfældes ved pH over 9.0 og calcium ved pH over 12.0 ved
-1100 mV. Forholdet mellem magnesium- og calcium-indholdet i kalklaget er strømnings- og
potentialeafhængigt, så dette forhold vil blive undersøgt nærmere i de kommende forsøg.

Ved tre forsøg ved hhv. -900, -1000 og -1100 mV Ag/AgCl havvand er det vist at forsøgsop-

dannede et mere tæt kalklag med flagestruktur og revner. Ud fra TGA er det påvist at det
dannede kalklag ved -1100 mV primært består af magnesiumhydroxid med en lille andel af
calciumkarbonat.
