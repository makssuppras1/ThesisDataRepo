Abstract
In the current time of antimicrobial resistance crisis, it is important to evaluate novel tools that
can be potentially repurposed as better antimicrobials. In particular, it is relevant to improve the
selective targeting of the pathogens of interest, in order to not disrupt the microbiome. CRISPR-Cas
systems pose a suitable alternative, as they can be programmed to target and kill bacteria selectively.
However, their eﬃciency against P. aeruginosa has not been fully elucidated.
Furthermore, an
optimal antimicrobial would protect from recurrent infections. In this work, I propose the use of
a self-transmissible broad host range plasmid (CRISPRkill) as a vehicle to deliver the CRISPR-Cas
constructs.
As a model pathogen, I focused on P. aeruginosa, including isolates obtained from
cystic ﬁbrosis patients, as currently, treatment for these infections fails in the clinic. Additionally,
as potential delivery method, nanoparticles were assessed for their capacity to transport a plasmid
to bacteria. For the plasmid vehicle, I showed that the strain PA14 does not accept the broad-host-
range plasmids pMATINGα-GFP and R64, indicating a challenge when targeting this bacteria and
its clinical derivatives. On the other hand, strain PAO1 and DK01-S3 isolates did accept and transfer
said plasmid. For the cargo of pHERD30T plasmid, both endogenous and exogenous CRISPR-Cas
systems were tested to determine both the most eﬃcient eﬀector complex and the best genomic target
for antimicrobial use. Targeting by the P. aeruginosa endogenous I-F CRISPR-Cas system resulted in
the killing of PA14 and DK02-27, while leaving DK02-28 and DK01-S3 intact, making the endogenous
approach suitable only for some populations. Out of the four tested systems for exogenous targeting,
II-A and I-C systems achieved the highest killing eﬃciencies for PAO1 and DK01-S3. There were
no signiﬁcant diﬀerences in killing when targeting three diﬀerent loci - 16S rRNA, 23S rRNA and
phzM, supporting the robustness of this approach. Out of the tested nanoparticles for transfer of
plasmids to bacteria, only polyethylenimine (PEI25K) was able to transfer the plasmid; however,
this result could not be replicated. Altogether, the results of this work point out both the beneﬁts
and challenges behind the CRISPRkill antimicrobial approach, reinforcing the need for further work
to optimize its suitability for a wider range of pathogens.

2
Adaptation of P. aeruginosa in CF patient lungs.
At the initial stage of
colonization P. aeruginosa has to overcome a harsh environment caused by a strong
immune response, including ROS. There is also variable availability of nutrients and
oxygen, as well as the presence of high concentrations of antibiotics. Even though
there is some level of cell death, some bacteria can persist due to pathoadaptive
mutations and intrinsic resistance to antibiotics.
The adaptive mutations lead to
a change of phenotype where P. aeruginosa is acquiring antibiotic resistance and is
becoming more persistent. However, this results in lower virulence and leads to bioﬁlm
formation and loss of ﬂagella and virulence factors. The bacterial persistence leads to
inﬂammation and tissue damage. Figure adapted from Rossi et al. (2020) [24].

1
Introduction
Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) describes the capacity of microorganisms to overcome antimi-
crobial substances, mainly by the selection of mutations and horizontal gene transfer (HGT). The
central selective pressure is the over- and misuse of antibiotics, a common problem in low-income
and high-income settings. Currently, AMR is one of the most signiﬁcant public health threats. A
recent report estimated that 4.95 million deaths were associated with infections caused by antibiotic-
resistant organisms in 2019 [1]. Furthermore, there is a dramatic increase in human deaths predicted
in the upcoming decades due to therapeutic failures. By 2050, deaths caused by AMR will increase
up to 10 million per year worldwide [2]. This number is higher than predictions for other diseases
such as cancer and diabetes (Fig. 1). This can be explained as, over the years, it has been observed
that bacteria develop resistance to each conventional chemical antibiotic that has come to the market
[3]. Together, the continuous emergence of bacterial resistance and the low discovery rate of novel
antibiotics, depict the urgent need to develop novel antimicrobial strategies that would represent a
sustainable alternative to antibiotics.
Figure 1: AMR death predictions by 2050. Predictions of deaths caused by AMR, cancer,
cholera, diabetes, diarrhoeal disease, measles, road traﬃc accidents, and tetanus per year by 2050.
Figure adapted from [2].
AMR in bacteria is a multifaced and complex issue as highlighted by One Health initiatives [4].
To limit the spread and acquisition of AMR, animal and environmental health must be considered.
Lately, it has been demonstrated that the AMR genes spread among humans, animals, and the envi-
ronment, which comprise a resistome. The prevalence of AMR genes and organisms in soil is caused
by the introduction of human and animal waste products. As a result of this, the freshwater source is
one of the main routes for the spread of AMR [1]. Another reservoir that is ideal for AMR spread is
in hospitals due to the high amounts of antibiotics consumed and susceptible patient population [5].
Often, bacteria is transferred through contact between patients and healthcare workers. Every time
1
==PAGE:16==
a patient is transferred from one ward to another, there is a risk of the spread of antibiotic-resistant
bacteria [6].
Cystic ﬁbrosis (CF) is a genetic disorder caused by a mutation in the cystic ﬁbrosis trans-
membrane regulator gene, which encodes for an essential protein involved in ion transport across
epithelial tissues [7]. As a result of this mutation, patients with CF have altered bicarbonate and
chlorine homeostasis, leading to the production of thicker mucus. This severely aﬀects the lungs and
other mucus-producing organs. As this mucus is high on nutrients and hard to clear from the lung,
CF patients are more susceptible to chronic bacterial infections, with 80% to 95% of cases leading to
death due to respiratory failure [8]. Pseudomonas aeruginosa is the main pathogen causing chronic
lung infections in CF, which is commonly found in hospitals [9]. Additionally, other pathogens that
persist in lower airways are Staphylococcus aureus and Haemophilus inﬂuenzae [8]. These infections
are often untreatable, as bacteria quickly adapt to their host, evade the immune system, and develop
AMR. Thus, a treatment targeting pathogens eﬀectively would signiﬁcantly improve the life quality
and expectancy of CF patients.
1.1
Persistence and adaptability of P. aeruginosa in CF patients
P. aeruginosa is a Gram-negative, facultative anaerobic bacteria. Its occurrence is common
in natural settings such as soil and water. However, it also thrives in other environments such as
animal/human feces [10]. Overall, P. aeruginosa remarkable ability to adapt to various niches can
be explained by the plasticity of its genome, which ranges between 5.1 and 7 Mb in size [11].
Consistently, P. aeruginosa is also an opportunistic pathogen, with the ability to infect a
broad range of hosts such as humans, animals [12], and plants [13]. It is often present in immuno-
compromised patients, such as those suﬀering from CF, burn wounds, or diabetic foot ulcers [14,
15]. In a patient that is not immunocompromised, the presence of P. aeruginosa is often associated
with keratitis and otitis media infections [16, 17]. In CF patients, P. aeruginosa is usually acquired
in early childhood and persists for years despite the treatment with antibiotics [18]. Moreover, P.
aeruginosa is one of the six pathogens that are part of ESKAPE along with E. faecium, S. aureus,
K. pneumoniae, A. baumannii and Enterobacter spp. [19]. These pathogens are responsible for most
nosocomial infections and are highly prioritized in developing new antimicrobials. The high AMR
level of these pathogens allows them to escape the bactericidal action of antimicrobial agents [19,
20]. Speciﬁcally, P. aeruginosa infections are diﬃcult to treat due to the high levels of intrinsic and
acquired antibiotic resistance. Some of the common antibiotic classes that P. aeruginosa is resistant
to are aminoglycosides, cephalosporins, ﬂuoroquinolones, and carbapenems [21, 22]. Moreover, the
occurrence of multidrug- and pandrug- resistant strains is growing worldwide [23].
Both virulence and intrinsic antibiotic resistance are essential for the survival of P. aeruginosa
at the initial stage of CF lung colonization. During infection, there is exposure to strong inﬂammatory
responses such as reactive oxygen species (ROS) that are released due to treatment with antibiotics
[25]. In addition, due to environmental stress exposure, the expression of diﬀerent sets of genes and
pathoadaptive mutations that allow adaptation to a more persistent but less virulent phenotype is
induced. This adaptation can lead to bioﬁlm formation or loss of ﬂagella and virulence factors [26,
27]. The overview of the adaptation process from a naïve strain to an adapted strain can be seen in
Figure 2. Naïve strains are classiﬁed for their phenotypic proﬁle as bacteria have a normal growth
rate, low antibiotic resistance, and low bioﬁlm-linked traits, while an adapted strain has slow growth,
increased antibiotic resistance, and increased adherence [28].

Overall, the rapid adaptation of P. aeruginosa to the lung has one main outcome: treatment
failure. However, this scenario also replicates for many other bacteria and their host environment.
Therefore, it is pressing to ﬁnd new strategies by which these resistant bacteria could be reached and
killed, regardless of their adaptation stage. Furthermore, it is also essential to work towards more
targeted approaches that do not aﬀect the commensal microbial communities of the host, as it has
been shown that they play relevant roles in their physiology [29]. Additionally, an optimal system
would allow for quick and simple customization, to make treatment sustainable in time, responding
in a timely manner to the evolution of the pathogens.
In order to fulﬁll these requirements, I propose the use of self-replicating and self-transferable
plasmids coding for speciﬁc nucleases as a viable alternative to chemical antibiotics. The speciﬁc
nucleases explored in this work correspond to CRISPR (clustered regularly interspaced short palin-
dromic repeats)-Cas(CRISPR-associated protein) systems. These are ribonucleoprotein complexes
that can be programmed to target strains of interest within a community through sequence-speciﬁc
binding.
Plasmids would act as trojan horses, introducing themselves into bacteria, expressing
the killing machinery that would wipe out a speciﬁc population. Furthermore, due to their self-
transmissibility, the system would generate long-lasting immunity by acting through the commensal
microbiota. As a starting point for this project, optimal carrier plasmids and CRISPR-Cas systems
had to be selected, for which the state of the art is described below.

1.2
Broad-host-range self-replicating plasmids
Bacteria usually carry extrachromosomal DNA molecules in addition to a chromosome. These
molecules are called plasmids and can have either linear or circular form [30].
Plasmids can be
distinguished by various characteristics such as size, modes of replication and transmission, host
range, and type of genes that it carries.
Smaller size plasmids are usually present in high copy
numbers, while larger plasmids are maintained at the low copy number [31, 32]. The presence of the
origin of replication (oriV) site is the minimum requirement for independent replication of a plasmid.
In nature, both plasmids that can be stably maintained only in closely related hosts and plasmids
that can replicate in multiple unrelated hosts can be found [33]. The latter receives the name of
broad-host-range (BHR) plasmids.
The host range of a plasmid depends on the interaction between its replicon machinery and
the enzymatic machinery of the host [34]. As plasmids compete for the host machinery, the previous
presence of a plasmid in a host can limit the acquisition of further plasmids, deeming some incom-
patible. Plasmids grouped by incompatibility are present in high abundance in nature, especially in
stressed environments such as antibiotics or heavy metals. Each incompatibility (Inc) group consists
of related plasmids that share genetic elements involved in plasmid replication and partitioning [35].
Plasmids in the same Inc group cannot be stably maintained in the same host. There are 14 Inc
groups in P. aeruginosa , with many having a BHR [36, 37]. One group is called IncP, with plasmids
classiﬁed into two classes: IncPα and IncPβ [38]. Some examples for IncPα class are plasmids R18,
R64, RK2, RP1, and RP4, while some examples of IncPβ plasmids are pB10 and pKJK10 [39]. They
can remarkably transfer between and replicate in almost all species of the Alpha-, Beta- and Gamma
proteobacteria.
Considering the development of a new antimicrobial based on plasmid transfer, it would be
advantageous to have a replication initiation independent of host initiation factors for the antimi-
crobial to have a minimum amount of constrains. In nature, this is achieved by having the genes
needed for replication encoded on the plasmid, resulting in BHR [33].
Furthermore, the presence of multiple origins of replication might be an advantage, as a single
plasmid can have an origin that is functional in one host and another origin that is functional in
a diﬀerent host. For example, pJD4 (IncW) from Neisseria gonorrhoeae contains three origins of
replication, where ori1 requires DNA Polymerase I (Pol I) to function, but the other two origins –
ori2 and ori3 do not require Pol I [40].
The structure of the origin is also important since diﬀerent bacterial species have diﬀerent
requirements to initiate replication. A good example of this criteria is the BHR RK2 plasmid, which
is a part of the IncP family. Its origin is comprised of a TrfA (trans-acting replication function)-
encoding gene and the replication origin (oriV), with repetitive sequences (iterons) for the binding of
both TrfA and the DNA polymerase [41]. This structure allows not only for replication in Escherichia
coli, but also in Pseudomonas putida due to the presence of three additional iterons [42]. Notably,
the region with 4 DnaA boxes is necessary for RK2 replication in E. coli but is not essential for the
replication in P. aeruginosa [43].
Considering the move of our antimicrobial plasmid from host to host, it is also crucial that
defense mechanisms against horizontal gene transfer cannot recognize it. Therefore, few restriction
sites can be an advantage, in order to avoid possible restriction machineries. Such observations were
made in IncP plasmids, which are BHR and contain few sites for restriction enzymes compared to
narrow host plasmids. For example, RK2 plasmid contains only ﬁve restriction sites [44].

Beyond replication, how a plasmid transfers between bacteria can also aﬀect its range. The
plasmid-based antimicrobial is expected to transfer through conjugation, a process depending on
cell-to-cell contact through dedicated machinery, the pilus. In particular, conjugation also impacts
the BHR of the plasmid. A self-transmissible plasmid requires an origin of transfer (oriT), MOB
genes (otherwise known as Dtr genes for DNA transfer replication), and genes for mating-pair ap-
paratus (pilus) formation [45]. However, a large proportion of plasmids are not self-transmissible.
These plasmids can be mobilized only by the presence of a helper plasmid that would provide a
mating apparatus [46]. For this work, we explored the use of two BHR self-transmissible plasmids:
pMATING and R64.
1.2.1
pMATINGα-GFP plasmid
pMATINGα-GFP was kindly provided by Dr. Victor de Lorenzo from the Centro Nacional
de Biotecnología (CNB) in Madrid, Spain, and its main components are outlined in Figure 3. This
plasmid is a synthetic derivative of IncPα group plasmids. It shares its Tra2 (mating pair formation
genes) core, Tra1 (DNA transfer and replication genes) core, and RK2 origin with IncPα group
plasmids. Additionally, the plasmid carries an inducible promoter to express inserted genes (XylS-
Pm), a msfgfp gene coding for the green ﬂuorescence protein (GFP), a toxin-antitoxin (TA) system
(hok-sok), and a kanamycin resistance cassette.
This plasmid has been particularly designed to have a BHR: it has an RK2 origin and carries
genes that allow transfer and replication.
Its ability to disperse in a population was previously
conﬁrmed in P. putida (unpublished data, De Lorenzo laboratory), yet not tested in P. aeruginosa.
To do so, ﬁrst it needs to be optimized, mainly due to the presence of a kanamycin resistance
cassette, as it generates two challenges: i) P. aeruginosa is intrinsically resistant to kanamycin, ii)
such plasmid cannot be used in a clinical setting due to the presence of the resistance gene. Therefore,
the resistance cassette would have to be removed before use in a clinical setting, meaning that the
plasmid maintenance would rely on the hok-sok TA system for pMATINGα-GFP plasmid.

1.3
Adaptive immunity of CRISPR-Cas systems
CRISPR-Cas loci are DNA segments found in bacterial and archaeal genomes and plasmids.
They encode adaptive immune systems against foreign DNA, such as plasmids and phages [52]. The
adaptability of the CRISPR-Cas immunity lies in the ability to incorporate foreign DNA as spacers
in CRISPR arrays [53], which consist of conserved direct repeats separated by unique sequences
(spacers) [54]. Usually, cas genes are located adjacent to CRISPR loci [55].
CRISPR-Cas mediated adaptive immunity occurs in three stages (Fig. 5). First, spacer ac-
quisition takes place. In this stage, a short DNA fragment (protospacer) is recognized in the foreign
DNA and incorporated into a CRISPR array right after the leader sequence, with the subsequent
addition of a repeat. The process is usually mediated by Cas1 and Cas2 enzymes and depends on an
adjacent protospacer motif (PAM), a short and conserved sequence of 2-5bp that ﬂanks the foreign
sequence [57]. The PAM acts as a self vs. non-self safeguard mechanism, preventing autoimmunity
by cleavage of the CRISPR array. Once the protospacer is integrated into the CRISPR array, it is
named a spacer. As spacers integrate in a directional manner, CRISPR arrays represent the infection
history of the organism.
Secondly, CRISPR RNA (crRNA) biogenesis occurs. Here, the CRISPR array is transcribed
into a precursor-crRNA that is subsequently processed into short mature crRNA molecules by cleav-
Figure 5: The three stages of CRISPR-Cas adaptive immunity.
During acquisition the
protospacer region is recognized and incised from the foreign DNA. This leads to the CRISPR
array’s transcription and processing, leading to the expression of crRNAs and cas genes. During
interference, the viral DNA is cleaved by Cas nuclease. Figure adapted from Hampton et al. (2020)
[56].
7
==PAGE:22==
age with ribonucleases [58]. Each mature crRNA contains a unique single spacer with a portion of
the adjacent repeats in the array.
Target interference is the last stage. In this stage, a ribonucleoprotein complex is formed with
Cas proteins and an individual crRNA. This complex scans the DNA, searching for a PAM and
a matching region using base complementarity to the crRNA. Once the protospacer is recognized,
target destruction takes place by a Cas nuclease.
In nature, archaea and bacteria encode a large diversity of CRISPR-Cas systems, and in recent
years, extensive eﬀorts have been made to classify them [59]. Principally, they are grouped according
to the components of their interference machinery.
CRISPR-Cas systems are mainly grouped into two classes: in class 1 there are systems with
multiprotein eﬀector complexes, and in class 2, there are systems with single eﬀector complexes.
Even though class 2 systems have only a single multidomain crRNA-binding protein, it is functionally
analogous to the entire eﬀector complex of class 1. Another diﬀerence is that class 1 systems are
found in bacteria and archaea while class 2 systems are mostly limited to bacteria [59]. Class 1 can
be further grouped into three types (I, III, IV) and 33 subtypes. Class 2 also includes three types
(II, V, IV) with 17 subtypes in total [59]. Each CRISPR-Cas type can be distinguished by signature
genes and the typical organization of their loci.

in Figure 6, there are overall Cas1-Cas13 proteins. Across all the subtypes identiﬁed until now, Cas1
and Cas2 proteins are the only ones conserved in the majority of CRISPR-Cas systems [60].
1.4
Type I, II and V CRISPR-Cas systems
1.4.1
Type I CRISPR-Cas systems
Every type I system has a cas3 gene, which encodes for a large protein that possesses single-
stranded DNA (ssDNA)-stimulated ATPase activity, coupled to unwinding of DNA/DNA and RNA/DNA
duplexes [61]. Typically, the helicase domain is fused to an HD family domain which possesses en-
donuclease activity, used for cleavage of the targeted DNA. A remarkable characteristic of Cas3
nucleases is that it leads to processive degradation of DNA due to its ssDNA exonuclease and 3’-5’
helicase activity [61, 62]. Notably, cas4 is not present in I-E and I-F systems. In I-F1 and I-Fv
subtypes, cas3 gene is fused to cas2 gene. Up to date, type I systems are divided into nine subtypes
I-A to I-F3, which can be distinguished by the features of their operon organization (Fig. 7) [63].
Figure 7: Classiﬁcation of type I CRISPR-Cas systems. Genes involved in the eﬀector complex
formation are highlighted in orange. Figure adapted from Makarova et al. (2020) [59].
Type I-C CRISPR-Cas employs only three kinds of Cas proteins (Cas5, Cas8 and Cas7) to form
the crRNA-guided surveillance complex called Cascade (CRISPR-associated complex for antiviral
defense) that further recruits Cas3 after target binding [63]. This makes it a compact system to
work with at the DNA level. I-F2 is a variant of I-F that shares a high degree of similarity with
adaptation modules (Cas1 and Cas2) and Cas6f, while the rest of the eﬀector module is impressively
diﬀerent, therefore, is commonly called I-F variant (I-Fv). The I-Fv complex lacks the small and
large subunits, also formed by only three kinds of Cas proteins (Cas5, Cas7 and Cas6) [64]. Another
remarkable diﬀerence between I-F and I-Fv is that there is signiﬁcant structural variation in the

binding regions of anti-CRISPR proteins. Until now, anti-CRISPR proteins against I-Fv have not
been found [65].
1.4.2
Type II CRISPR-Cas systems
Compared to type I CRISPR-Cas, only one multidomain protein is needed for all the functions
of eﬀector complexes, target DNA cleavage, and maturation of crRNA. This protein is encoded by a
signature gene of type II CRISPR-Cas: Cas9 [66]. The Cas9 protein has a bi-lobed architecture in
which the target DNA and single guide RNA (sgRNA) are placed in the interface between the two
lobes. sgRNA is a synthetic construct consisting of two parts: crRNA, which contains DNA-binding
spacer sequence, and tracrRNA, which functions as a binding scaﬀold for the Cas nuclease [66]. Two
nuclease domains of the Cas9 protein are involved in DNA degradation: RuvC-like nuclease (RNase
H fold) and a HNH (MrcA-like) nuclease domain that is positioned in the middle of the protein [66].
Each of the nucleases is responsible for the cleavage of one strand of the target DNA. There are
four subtypes of type II-A systems (Fig. 8). Every locus of the type II subtype additionally to cas9
gene has cas1 and cas2 genes. Besides these, most type II systems also have one or tracrRNA loci,
involved in crRNA processing [67]. Type II-A CRISPR-Cas with Cas9 from Streptococcus pyogenes
is the most studied eﬀector. Earlier investigations have shown successful induction of bacterial cell
death in antibiotic-resistant and clinically relevant bacteria using this protein [68, 69, 70].
Figure 8: Classiﬁcation of type II CRISPR-Cas systems. Figure adapted from Makarova et
al. (2020) [59].
1.4.3
Type V CRISPR-Cas systems
The diﬀerence between type V and type II systems lies in the domain architecture of their
eﬀector proteins. The Cas9 contains two nuclease domains, whereas the type V eﬀector (Cas12)
contains a RuvC-like domain that cleaves both strands leading to double-strand breaks (DSBs) [71].
There is a high diversity of type V systems. In total 17 subtypes of type V have been characterized
until now (Fig. 11). While some variants have smaller RucV-like-domain proteins like V-U, others
have dsDNA cleavage activity and ssDNA cleavage activity [72, 73].
10
==PAGE:25==
Figure 9: Classiﬁcation of type V CRISPR-Cas systems. Figure adapted from Makarova et
al. (2020) [59].
Cas12a nucleases of the V-A type are becoming more widely used as it is reported that their
speciﬁcity is higher than other nucleases, and the editing results in fewer oﬀ-target eﬀects [74].
Another advantage of Cas12a is that the crRNA does not require a tracrRNA as SpCas9, and
eﬀector proteins are smaller [75].
1.5
CRISPR-Cas as a potential novel antimicrobial
CRISPR-Cas systems introduce DNA breaks through the binding of the eﬀector complexes to
the target. Usually, DSBs are repaired either through RecA-mediated homologous recombination
(HR) using a matching copy of DNA or through error-prone non-homologous end joining (NHEJ)
mechanisms [76]. However, in most prokaryotes, the NHEJ machinery is absent. Therefore, if the
continuous DNA damage caused by CRISPR-Cas self-cleavage is not repaired through HR, it will
lead to cell death [77]. This approach is the basis for the development of CRISPR-Cas antimicrobials.
Even though the main purpose of CRISPR-Cas in prokaryotes is to target invasive nucleic
acids, they can occasionally target the DNA from their host. For example, this can happen when
acquired CRISPR spacers from phages or plasmid DNA show homology to chromosomal sequences
[79]. It has also been shown that the usual outcome of self-targeting is cell death. For example, when
the native type CRISPR-Cas system of E. coli was directed against integrated prophage, it led to
the death of 98% of the population [80].
11

Figure 10: The antimicrobial mechanism of CRISPR-Cas The Cas9 induced DSB can be

et al. (2021) [78].
There are two main strategies to use CRISPR-Cas for targeting bacteria - endogenous and
exogenous.
Endogenous strategy means the delivery of self-targeting CRISPR arrays, hijacking
the Cas genes already encoded in the genome of the host. In contrast, in the exogenous strategy,
the CRISPR targeting array is co-delivered with the corresponding eﬀector Cas machinery. The
advantage of the endogenous approach is that the construct to be delivered is simpler and smaller
in size. However, editing eﬃciency depends entirely on endogenous Cas genes that might not always

timicrobials. If the bacteria has an endogenous CRISPR-Cas system, the self-targeting CRISPR

the other hand, an exogenous strategy can be used where both the CRISPR array and cas eﬀector

be present or expressed in the recipient strains [81]. On the other hand, exogenous systems could
have varying performance in diﬀerent hosts, depending on their expression eﬃciency [78].
1.5.1
Resistance to CRISPR-Cas systems
There are several ways in which targeting by CRISPR-Cas can be unsuccessful. For example,
mutating the target or the CRISPR-Cas system can allow the survival of bacteria. Some studies
have shown how deletions in the target can leave bacteria intact [82, 83]. Nevertheless, if virulence or
resistance genes are targeted, the outcome can still be positive since it will result in the sensitization
of the population.
Due to the intense selective pressure applied by CRISPR-Cas immunity, many mobile genetic
elements (MGEs) have evolved to produce a response against CRISPR-Cas, known as anti-CRISPR
(Acr) proteins. One of the earliest discoveries of acr genes was found in phages that inhibit the type
I-F CRISPR-Cas of P. aeruginosa. Following this, many other Acr proteins have been discovered.
Until now, 54 Acr protein families have been discovered to inhibit nine distinct CRISPR-Cas types
[84].
Therefore, it is important to consider the context of the target pathogen to maximize the
chances of killing by CRISPR-Cas antimicrobial.
1.5.2
Delivery of CRISPR-Cas DNA vectors
Delivery is one of the main challenges for implementing CRISPR-Cas antimicrobials. Usually,
CRISPR-Cas antimicrobials are delivered in the form of a DNA cassette. Therefore, direct delivery
of DNA into cells by natural transformation would be the most straightforward option. However,
there are a limited fraction of naturally competent bacteria; even in such case, typically, only a small
fraction of the population would take up the DNA cassette [85]. For this reason, most of the CRISPR-
Cas delivery attempts have been demonstrated via phage transduction or plasmid conjugation [86].
One well-studied approach is CRISPR-Cas delivery using phage particles as vectors [69]. Even
though there has been some success with the use of phages, there are also some limitations. One
of the critical limitations is that phages have a limited host range. Several factors aﬀect the host
range of phages, such as the absence of surface receptors, entry exclusions systems, restriction-
modiﬁcation systems, and CRISPR-Cas systems that can degrade phage DNA [70]. Furthermore,
even if populations are sensitive, resistance to phage infection can arise quickly, mainly through the
mutation of receptors [87].
Another way how CRISPR-Cas can be delivered is via conjugative plasmids from an innocu-
ous/inactivated donor strain. For instance, a modiﬁed RK2 IncP plasmid has been repurposed for
the delivery of TevSpCas9 dual nuclease and gRNA, with a gentamicin resistance gene. Two types
of plasmids were compared in this study: cis-acting, which means that the conjugation machinery
facilitates the transfer from E. coli to S. enterica was encoded on the same plasmid as the CRISPR-
Cas system; and trans-acting plasmid, meaning there was no conjugation machinery encoded on the
plasmid. The cis-acting plasmids resulted in higher transfer eﬃciency than trans-acting ones. Diﬀer-
ent sgRNAs were designed, and killing eﬃciency of 100% was achieved for some of the targets [88].
Several other studies have developed IncP-based BHR conjugative plasmids carrying CRISPR-Cas9
for gene editing [89, 90] , mostly focusing in gut microbiota.
Another study developed a targeted-antibacterial-plasmid (TAP) that combines conjugation
and CRISPR-Cas to achieve the strain-speciﬁc antibacterial activity. TAP uses conjugation tra genes
13
==PAGE:28==
encoded on an F plasmid that can be transferred to E. coli strains and closely related Gram-negative
Enterobacteriaceae. For the selection of transformants, diﬀerent antibiotic-resistance cassettes were
used. TAP constructs induced eﬃcient Cas9-speciﬁc killing of E. coli in multi-species environment
[91].
One of the challenges for mentioned plasmids is that antibiotic selection is present, which is
not applicable for use in the clinical setting. There have been attempts in antimicrobial CRISPR-
Cas delivery without antibiotics. For example, RK2-based self-transmissible plasmid (pHELP) was
co-delivered with an R1162-based mobilizable plasmid (pCRISPR) encoding CRISPR-Cas cassettes.
It was shown that these two plasmids were co-transferred to the majority of recipient cells in the
absence of antibiotic selection [86].
Compared to phage delivery, BHR conjugative plasmids can transfer DNA to various species
of bacteria. One of the limitations of conjugative plasmid-mediated CRISPR-Cas delivery is low
DNA transfer eﬃciency. Like phages, antimicrobial CRISPR-Cas plasmid conjugation can face entry
exclusion systems and restriction-modiﬁcation systems of CRISPR-Cas systems that would damage
the plasmid.
Finally, one of the novel strategies for CRISPR-Cas delivery is nanoparticle-conjugated plas-
mids or complexes. The applications of nanotechnology have been explored in great detail. However,
so far, only in mammalian cells. CRISPR-Cas9 has been formulated for the delivery in many dif-
ferent nanomaterials such as lipid-based nanoparticles, polyethylenimine (PEI) based particles, or
gold-based particles, among others, for diﬀerent therapeutic applications [92, 93, 94]. Yet the ability
to package plasmids and deliver them to bacteria remains elusive.

Objectives

essential elements form this system: a plasmid, a CRISPR-Cas system, and an initial delivery vehicle.

system.

3.3
Transformation of bacteria by conjugation
The donor and recipient strains were grown overnight in LB-Broth supplemented with the
appropriate antibiotic at 37◦C with shaking (225 rpm).
The following day, the donor and the
recipient strain were mixed at the chosen ratio, and pre-warmed LB-Broth in 37◦C was added up to
a total volume of 1000 µL.
The mix was centrifuged for 2 min at 7,000 x g. The supernatant was discarded, and the pellet
was washed twice with 1 mL pre-warmed (37◦C) LB-Broth by centrifugation for 2 min at 7,000
x g. After the last wash, 30 µL of pre-warmed LB-Broth in 37◦C was added, and the pellet was
resuspended. The cell suspension was transferred onto a 13-mm cellulose acetate ﬁlter membrane
placed on non-selective LB agar plates and was incubated at 37◦C overnight.
After the incubation, the ﬁlter with the conjugation mix was picked up by using ﬂame-sterilized
forceps and placed into a 1.5 mL Eppendorf tube containing 200 µL 0.9% NaCl. The cells were dis-
lodged from the ﬁlter by vortexing for 30-60 seconds.
100 µL of the suspension was plated on
selective media, and the other half of the mix was plated on non-selective media. The antibiotic-
resistant transformants were selected either on VBMM selective media containing the appropriate
concentration of antibiotics in the case of E. coli-Pseudomonas transfer and in the case of Pseu-
domonas-Pseudomonas transfer the transformants were selected on LB media with appropriate an-
tibiotic concentration. VBMM contains citrate as a carbon source which can be used to select against
E. coli since it does not express citrate transporter during aerobic growth [102].

Transformation of bacteria by electroporation

putida) with shaking (225 rpm).
A 3 mL culture was used for each electroporation.
First, the
bacterial culture was centrifuged at 16,000 x g for 2 min. The supernatant was discarded, and then

Transformation of bacteria by heat-shock
5 µL of Gibson Assembly/Golden Gate mix was added to 100 µL of rubidium chloride chem-
ically competent E. coli DH5α cells.

CRISPR-Cas plasmid construction
To test the killing eﬃciency of CRISPR-Cas systems, plasmids were constructed using pHERD30T
as a backbone. The main components of the plasmid, such as the origin of replication, antibiotic
resistance, and promoters, are functional in all tested microorganisms. The arabinose inducible pro-
moter araBAD was used to drive the expression of cas genes for pHERD30T-based constructs, while
the rhamnose inducible promoter rhaBAD was used for pCas3cRh plasmid. The cas genes were
introduced into the pHERD30T backbone using Gibson Assembly. For Gibson Assembly, the cas
genes and the CRISPR array had to be inserted into pHERD30T backbone. Genes were ampliﬁed by
PCR as operons, and products were puriﬁed. The pHERD30T backbone was digested with enzymes
that leave overhangs compatible with the overhangs of the insert (XbaI and NcoI). Digestion mixture
containing water, 1 µg of plasmid, restriction enzymes and Thermo FD 10x buﬀer was incubated for
1h at 37◦C. For pHERD30T-I-Fv, two fragments were assembled to the backbone: I-Fv cas genes,
and a fragment with terminator and restriction sites (SpeI and XhoI) for insertion of a minimal
CRISPR array structure (Repeat-Spacer-Repeat) carrying Golden Gate compatible restriction sites
for spacer exchange.

targeting 16S rRNA, 23S rRNA and phzM were cloned by oligo annealing and Golden Gate assembly
using BsaI restriction (except for V-A system, where BbsI restriction sites where used) and T4 DNA
ligase (Thermo Fisher Scientiﬁc), as previously described by Larson et al. (2013) [106]. Figure 13
shows the detailed construction of each plasmid.

4.4
CRISPR-Cas systems and target gene choice for targeting P. aeruginosa
by exogenous strategy
As not all pathogens carry endogenous CRISPR-Cas systems, or they might not be expressed
under the infection conditions, the delivery of full CRISPR-Cas systems, including the eﬀector pro-
teins, was explored. Four representatives of diﬀerent types of CRISPR-Cas systems were chosen.
As the large deletions generated by the Cas3 nuclease challenge DNA repair, two representatives of
27
==PAGE:42==
type I systems were chosen: I-C and I-Fv. The I-C system tested was from P. aeruginosa and I-Fv
system was from Shewanella putrefaciens. The Cas9 endonuclease is well characterized and is an
established method in genome editing. Moreover, it has been shown that P. aeruginosa genome can
be edited by the use of CRISPR-Cas9 [109]. For this reason, the II-A system from S. pyogenes was
included in the killing assay. Finally, the Cas12a enzyme, a signature protein of V-A systems, has
also been applied successfully for editing the P. aeruginosa genome, in particular the eﬀector from
Francisella novicida. Therefore, the V-A system was also included in the killing assays [110]. It is
important to note that other systems might be successful for this purpose. However, due to time
constraints, only four systems were chosen for this work.
Beyond the choice of CRISPR-Cas systems, determining whether the target selection aﬀects
the killing eﬃciency and speciﬁcity is of high importance. As shown before, targeting diﬀerent genes
can result in diﬀerent killing eﬃciencies [88]. The targets chosen for killing eﬃciency testing are
phzM, 16S rRNA, and 23S rRNA. In P. aeruginosa, phzM is involved in the production of pyocyanin
[111]. Pyocyanin, a virulence factor, is often present in high concentrations in the sputum of CF
patients, indicating that this compound might cause tissue damage in chronic lung infections [112].
Since it is a secondary metabolite, phzM expression it is not essential for growth [113]. Ribosomal
RNA (rRNA) is a non-coding RNA which is the predominant component of ribosomes and is involved
in the synthesis of proteins. Due to the high conservation and speciﬁcity level, 16S rRNA is often
used to identify strains and determine phylogenetic relationships. It is also less susceptible to HGT
[114, 115]. 23S rRNA, another ribosomal RNA, is also used in phylogenetic studies. However, not
to such extent as 16S rRNA [116]. P. aeruginosa strains carries four copies of 16S rRNA and 23S
rRNA [117]. Since 16S rRNA and 23S rRNA are used in phylogenetic studies, targeting these genes
might be an advantage in multi-species environments. In addition, their targeting would generate
multiple cleavage points in the genome.

5
Discussion
5.1
Challenges in the development of broad host range plasmid with CRISPR-
Cas eﬀector speciﬁcally targeting PA14
There are several challenges in developing the CRISPRkill system that would speciﬁcally target
PA14.
First, it was determined that there is no transfer of pMATINGα-GFP or R64 to PA14.
Therefore, it can be assumed that PA14 exhibits some restrictive activity on the plasmid. There
could be several mechanisms for this, such as incompatibility between pMATINGα-GFP and plasmids
present in PA14, restriction-modiﬁcation systems, chemical defense or TA systems, or another defense
system. According to the National Center for Biotechnology Information, PA14 does not have any
plasmids, so an incompatibility between two plasmids can be excluded. It was also checked that
the RK2 origin is compatible with PA14 by electroporation of pSEVA621 plasmid with an RK2
origin. Transformants were obtained, indicating that the RK2 origin can be recognized by the PA14
machinery. Additionally, it was observed during killing eﬃciency assays that fewer transformants
were obtained for pHERD30T transfer to PA14 compared to the other two strains - DK01-S3 and
PAO1. This indicates that, in general, there are challenges in introducing even simple constructs
into this strain.
No studies address the question of low plasmid eﬃciency to PA14. However, many studies
focus on the transfer of the PAPI-1 pathogenicity island. One of the studies observed that transfer
eﬃciency between PA14 that already has PAPI-1 island and PA14 that does not have one is much
lower than for the same type of transfer for PAO1. Based on this, it was implied that this could
be due to an unknown mechanism that would exclude the acquisition of additional copies of the
island [122]. It might be that the exact mechanism is responsible for no or low transfer eﬃciencies
observed in this project, assuming there are certain similarities between the transfer machineries of
these MGEs.
More speciﬁcally, it could be that the endogenous I-F CRISPR-Cas system of PA14 targets
these plasmids. PAO1 does not have a CRISPR-Cas system, and it was also shown that DK01-S3
might have an inactive CRISPR-Cas since it was not aﬀected by endogenous targeting. Therefore, the
two strains that do not exhibit CRISPR-Cas activity could accept the pMATINGα-GFP plasmid
while there was no transfer to PA14. It has been shown previously that P. aeruginosa genomes
with active CRISPR-Cas are smaller than those without CRISPR, indicating that the CRISPR-Cas
system is preventing the integration of viruses and mobile elements [123].
Another challenge could be the vector size - self-transmissible BHR plasmids are usually large,
and adding a whole CRISPR-Cas system increases size even more.
For PA14, transfer of small
plasmids is done routinely, therefore the large size of pMATINGα-GFP and R64 could explain the
low eﬃciencies. Therefore, endogenous targeting can be considered since it also leads to high killing
eﬃciency and signiﬁcantly reduces the size of the vector since only a CRISPR array is needed.
However, in this project, it was determined that such an approach is not eﬀective in every P.
aeruginosa clinical isolate. Speciﬁcally, the introduction of CRISPR array with spacer targeting
phzM was ineﬀective in DK01-S3 and DK02-28, while signiﬁcant killing was observed for PA14 and
DK02-27. The lack of killing for DK01-S3 and DK02-28 could be due to a lack of expression of cas
genes due to adaptation in CF patient lung. A study has been done where CRISPR arrays were
reconstructed for diﬀerent P. aeruginosa clinical isolates from CF patients. In this study, it was
determined that the CRISPR arrays did not change over time in one patient, but they vary among
42
==PAGE:57==
diﬀerent patients. This indicates that there is no evidence of active evolution of CRISPR within CF
patients supporting the argument that the CRISPR-Cas system could be inactive in DK01-S3 [124].
This project showed that DK01-S3 could not be targeted via an endogenous CRISPR-Cas
system while targeting the same gene in an exogenous manner by II-A and I-C systems resulted in
high killing eﬃciencies. Therefore, an exogenous system needs to be exploited to kill bacteria, making
the problem of the vector size even more challenging. Subsequently, assessing which components of
these large plasmids are absolutely essential for transfer would be necessary. This would allow for
the construction of a synthetic minimal BHR self-replicating vector. Furthermore, the strategy of
providing the transfer machinery in trans could also be further tested, as it has been previously
shown to work in E. coli [91].
5.2
Killing eﬃciency of CRISPR-Cas constructs and resistance development
While testing diﬀerent CRISPR-Cas systems, it was determined that II-A and I-C systems
provided the highest killing eﬃciency based on both growth assay and killing eﬃciency estimation
based on CFU count. However, for I-Fv, there were inconsistencies between the two types of assays.
In the growth assay, bacteria survived, while based on the CFU counts, killing eﬃciency for the I-Fv
system was close to the II-A and I-C systems. This could be due to low or abnormal complex forma-
tion. It might be that the I-Fv complex assembles slower than for the other CRISPR-Cas systems,
leading to the early appearance of escaper cells in liquid media, which take over the population.
Additionally, there might be other reasons for the diﬀerence in killing eﬃciencies for diﬀerent
systems.
One reason could be the level of expression of the eﬀector module.
For example, for
SpCas9, it was previously shown that the expression level of SpCas9 directly correlates with the
killing eﬃciency of E. coli K-12 MG1655 [78]. Although the expression was controlled by the same
promoter (araBAD) for II-A, V-A, I-Fv systems, and rhaBAD for the I-C system, which have been
proven to provide high transcript yields when induced, the problem could arise during translation
of the proteins. These CRISPR-Cas systems are not native and were not codon optimized for P.
aeruginosa. Therefore the possibility of low translation eﬃciency or, further down, misfolding of the
eﬀectors should be taken into consideration. There were signiﬁcantly lower killing eﬃciencies for the
V-A system than for the other systems. This case could be explained by a suboptimal expression of
the Cas12 protein in P. aeruginosa. To conﬁrm this, Western Blot or proteomic analyses should be
done.
I-Fv and I-C are class 1 systems, while II-A and V-A are class 2 systems. Class 1 systems
have multiprotein eﬀector complexes, while class 2 have single eﬀector complexes. Thus, there might
be diﬀerences between killing timing for the two classes as it takes longer to process and assemble
the CASCADE complex rather than a single eﬀector complex. Notably, both I-C and II-A systems
achieved similar killing eﬃciencies representing both classes and two type nuclease activities.
CRISPR array was optimized for the I-C system to avoid spacer excision by disrupting homol-
ogy between the two repeats [62], while there was no such optimization done for the I-Fv system.
For one of the I-Fv escapers, there was a repeat and spacer deletion in the plasmid targeting phzM
indicating recombination between the two repeats, while for I-C system escaper plasmids, there were
no mutations found. There were also other single-point changes in plasmids extracted from I-Fv
escapers. However, frameshift occurred only for one of the colonies in cas5, while other mutations
were silent. The phenomena of changes in CRISPR array as resistance mechanism has already been
observed before [125, 126]. Therefore, it could be that the protection from spacer excision for I-C
43
==PAGE:58==
had a role in better killing performance than the I-Fv system and other systems.
Another aspect is the speciﬁcity of Cas endonucleases. SpCas9 is known for tolerating muta-
tions in the target sequences as opposed to Cas12a, which is more speciﬁc [127, 128]. The ﬁrst few
nucleotides following the PAM sequence mismatches have been reported to be more damaging for
binding and cleavage by Cas12a than SpCas9 [127, 129]. Subsequently, it can serve as an advantage
for SpCas9 in terms of introducing more oﬀ-target DSBs than Cas12a resulting in higher killing by
the II-A system than the V-A system. In this project, no mutations were found on sequenced target
genes. However, it cannot be excluded that there is an occurrence of mutations either in the PAM
or protospacer region as it was not evaluated for all escaper colonies. For V-A escapers, mutations
were found on the plasmid. Speciﬁcally, most of the escapers lacked cas12a which also explains
the low killing eﬃciencies obtained. This could point towards a higher toxicity of the expression of
the protein. A similar pattern was observed for II-A escapers where cas9 was only partly aligned.
Additionally, a frameshift was detected on the aligned region of one of the escaper colonies for cas9.
Reduction of viable cells when non-targeting constructs were introduced was observed. This
could indicate that the expression of cas enzymes is toxic to the cells. Oﬀ-target cleavage events
could cause toxicity. For example, cas9 has been shown to exhibit toxicity in some microorganisms
[109]. The oﬀ-targeting eﬀects would not be problematic if it was desired to only target one speciﬁc
species. However, suppose CRISPRkill is intended to be introduced into many diﬀerent species as
initially planned in this project, it could cause clinical challenges as it could aﬀect the survival of
commensal strains. For toxicity, further optimization of the coding sequence and the expression
levels should be performed. Furthermore, there have been attempts made to improve CRISPR-Cas9
editing speciﬁcity. For example, Cas9-FokI fusion protein or Cas9 nickase can improve speciﬁcity as
they require simultaneous recognition by two sgRNAs [130, 131].
5.3
CRISPR-Cas as preferred target for mutations
No mutations were found in the 16S rRNA and phzM targets for PAO1 and DK01-S3. Further-
more, plasmid DNA was extracted from PAO1 escapers, and a number of mutations were found in the
2 kb of the sequenced plasmids (Table 6). Several mutations were found in cas genes and CRISPR-
Cas arrays for all of the CRISPR-Cas systems except for I-C. Although no mutations were found
in the I-C system plasmid sequenced regions, the presence of mutations cannot be excluded since it
was only a fraction of the plasmid that was sequenced. These results indicate that CRISPR-Cas is
the preferred site for mutation.
The reason why the targets are left unchanged while the targeting plasmids are mutated could
be that pHERD30T is a high copy number plasmid, imposing pressure on chromosome stability.
Since the plasmid is rapidly replicating, there is a higher chance of introducing mutations to it,
which would allow the bacteria to survive. All of the targeted genes are located on chromosomes
in PAO1. Since the chromosome replication rate is lower than for a high copy plasmid - there is a
lower chance of occurrence of mutations on the target gene. A similar escape pattern was previously
observed for CRISPR-Cas chromosomal targeting.
In this study, the mechanism of how E. coli
confers resistance to SpCas9 mediated killing by insertion and deletion mutations in cas9 gene as
well as its regulatory elements [78].
44
==PAGE:59==
5.4
Evaluation of chosen CRISPR-Cas targets for use in P. aeruginosa
16S rRNA and 23S rRNA initially were chosen as targets due to the presence of several copies
of each in the genome. This allows by the design of one spacer to introduce several damage sites in
the genome. Moreover, these two genes are critical for the survival of the bacteria. On the other
hand, phzM is not essential for survival and is present in one copy in the genome. In this project,
it was shown that the killing eﬃciency does not diﬀer for these targets. Furthermore, it was also
previously demonstrated that CRISPR-Cas9 eﬃciency does not necessarily depend on the amount of
the cutting sites [78]. Therefore, this shows that target selection for a CRISPRkill system could be
focused on speciﬁcity, targeting non-essential accessory genes of strains in order to achieve depletion
of only a small subset of the microbiome.
5.5
PEI25K as potential delivery vehicle for CRISPR-Cas constructs
Only PEI25K showed potential in delivering the pHERD30T-GFP plasmid to PAO1, out of
all the tested nanoparticle formulations. Unfortunately, this result was only observed once, with
no success in replicating it.
Overall, there were challenges in designing experiments to test the
delivery of the plasmid to bacteria. For example, it was unknown how many particles there were in
a particular concentration. Therefore, choosing the ratios between the PAO1 and the particles was
diﬃcult. Also, the eﬃciency of plasmid encapsulation in each particle type was unknown. In order
to be able to design experiments better, more information about the nanoparticles would need to be
obtained.

==PAGE:60==
6
Conclusion
Overall, CRISPR-based antimicrobials have several challenges, starting from the delivery of
DNA vectors to intrinsic resistance of the cells due to the repair of DNA damage. This project was
separated into three modules: development of BHR self-replicating plasmids, determining eﬃcient
CRISPR-Cas killing systems, and delivery of plasmids by nanoparticles. As for the ﬁrst part, there
was no success in ﬁnding BHR self-replicating plasmid out of the tested plasmids, pMATINGα-GFP
and R64, that would act as a vector for delivery of CRISPR-Cas to diﬀerent P. aeruginosa strains.
Particularly, for the project, the challenge was that no transfer to PA14 was observed, which means
that it could not be targeted by the use of these plasmids. For the second part, an eﬃcient way
of killing PAO1 and DK01-S3 strains by CRISPR-Cas was found. Since no BHR self-replicating
vector was found, pHERD30T plasmid variants with CRISPR-Cas systems (II-A, V-A, I-Fv, and I-
C) were tested. The highest killing eﬃciency was observed for II-A and I-C systems. There were no
signiﬁcant diﬀerences in killing between the chosen targets (16S rRNA, 23S rRNA and phzM). The
results for these two systems correlated both for growth assay and killing eﬃciency estimation based
on CFU counts. For the I-Fv system, growth was observed in the growth assay while killing eﬃciency
estimated by CFU counts was similar to II-A and I-C systems. For the last part, it was determined
that the PEI25K material might have the potential to deliver plasmids to PAO1. However, this
result was only observed once, with no success in replicating it.

7
Future Perspectives
7.1
Project direction
Taking into consideration the objectives of this project, there are mainly two ways to move
forward - either focus on the targeting of PAO1/DK01-S3 strains or proceed with PA14 as a target.
If the ﬁrst option is chosen, it could be possible to establish proof of concept by using pMATINGα
plasmid as the CRISPRkill vector since it is transferable to PAO1 and DK01-S3.
Otherwise, it would be necessary to ﬁnd a BHR self-replicating plasmid that could transfer to
PA14. As a starting point, diﬀerent BHR self-replicating plasmids could be screened for transfer in
PA14, and the reason for the lack of transfer occurrence could be determined.
One of the plasmids that could be tested is pSN1216-29. This is a BHR plasmid with an
Inc group not identiﬁed yet.
It is known to transfer to P. putida, E. coli, P. resinovorans and
Alphaproteobacteria. Compared to the R64 plasmid that was tested in this project, this plasmid is
four times smaller in size. However, it also carries genes encoding for a type IV secretion system,
which could potentially facilitate transfer between P. aeruginosa strains. Another advantage of this
plasmid is that it does not carry antibiotic resistance genes, which would be beneﬁcial in the clinical
setting [132].

7.2
Replacement of resistance gene on CRISPRkill by toxin-antitoxin systems
Another problem is that maintenance of Inc plasmids, including the plasmids tested in the
project, usually relies on antibiotic resistance genes, which is not applicable for clinically relevant
use. Therefore, another approach to plasmid maintenance in the cell must be found when moving
on with the project. One potential approach is using the toxin-antitoxin (TA) system for plasmid
maintenance. The principle is that plasmid-free cells are either eliminated or their viability is reduced
by TA, leading to interspecies plasmid dissemination. For example, E. coli that produces extended-
47
==PAGE:62==
spectrum beta-lactamases (ESBL)-enzymes have been shown to have several TA on ESBL-plasmids
, which have a role in plasmid maintenance. TA systems such as those found on ESBL-plasmids -
hok/sok, srnB/C, vagC/D, pemI/K - could be explored for the potential replacement of the resistance
genes on CRISPR-Cas constructs [133].
7.3
Speciﬁc targeting of P. aeruginosa on a strain level
Another challenge met during the project was that with the available tools, it was impossible
to design spacers that selectively target and distinguish P. aeruginosa on the strain level. For PAO1
and PA14, the two strains’ chosen genetic targets are almost identical, except for a few SPMs in
the 16S rRNA and 23S rRNA genes.
For the design of spacers that would allow diﬀerentiation
between similar strains, a speciﬁc algorithm such as CSTB could be used (https://cstb.ibcp.fr).
This algorithm allows the design of CRISPR-Cas9 spacers of size 18-23 bp, and allows the exclusion
of the organisms in the analysis that must have no motif in common with the targeted organisms
[91]. Furthermore, targeting regions not conserved between strains, such as accessory genes, could
also be an option. As a downside, this would require more resources and time since the sequence of
the genome of the target strain would be required.
7.4
Improvement of killing eﬃciency assay
Even though high killing eﬃciencies were obtained by targeting diﬀerent P. aeruginosa strains,
it is essential to account that the electroporation eﬃciency of the plasmid was not taken into con-
sideration. Considering electroporation eﬃciency killing eﬃciency of the original population would
be lower. In order to estimate the actual killing eﬃciency, the transformation eﬃciencies ﬁrst need
to be estimated for each plasmid construct.
Another consideration for the assay is that the CRISPR-Cas construct would not be delivered
via electroporation in the clinical setting. Therefore, a higher relevance experiment for the project’s
scope could be the use of triparental conjugation as delivery for CRISPR-Cas constructs. There
would be two donor strains - E. coli DH5α with pHERD30T-CRISPR-Cas construct and the same
strain with the helper plasmid RK600.
Another improvement can be made by designing a more biologically relevant and conclusive
experiment to show the speciﬁc targeting by the II-A system.
The experiment setup could be
similar as described in Section 4.8. However, other ﬂuorophores would have to be used since the
experiment failed due to the read out of both mKOk and GFP signals in DK01-S3-mKOk. Another
approach to estimate the speciﬁcity of each of the CRISPR-Cas systems is by introducing diﬀerent
SPMs at the diﬀerent positions. Then, killing eﬃciencies can be compared between the targeting
spacers and the mutated spacers. Various numbers and positions could be mutated in order to assess
oﬀ-targeting sensitivity.
Such an experiment could also point out whether the killing caused by
pHERD30T CRISPR-Cas constructs with non-targeting spacers was caused by oﬀ-targeting events
or the toxicity of the expression of Cas enzymes.
48
==PAGE:63==
7.5
Better understanding of escaping strategies
In this project, it was impossible to sequence the whole escaper plasmids due to problems with
plasmid DNA extraction. The obtained concentrations were too low to send plasmids for sequencing
by Nanopore. To better understand escaping strategies of P. aeruginosa from targeting by diﬀerent
CRISPR-Cas systems, a plasmid DNA extraction protocol from P. aeruginosa needs to be optimized
in order to obtain appropriate concentrations.
7.6
Potential delivery vehicles for CRISPR-Cas constructs
In this project, there was no clear indication that the pHERD30T-GFP plasmid could be
delivered to PAO1 with the assistance of received nanoparticles except for a single replicate success
with PEI25K. Therefore, alternative delivery vehicles such as membrane vesicles (MVs) or probiotics
could be considered.
MVs are released by various prokaryotes and have a function in the delivery of various cell-
cell interaction factors [134]. MVs-mediated gene transfer has been observed across a broad range
of bacterial species, including Gram-negative bacteria [135, 136]. The advantage of MV is that it
protects DNA from degrative enzymes and does not require a donor as opposed to the conjugation
process [137].
Several attempts have been made to pack the plasmids into bacterial MVs [138].
However, the mechanism of DNA capture into MVs is not fully elucidated yet. Therefore, it has not
been applied much in plasmid delivery.
There is already a proof of concept on how probiotics can be used for the delivery of CRISPR-
Cas9. Conjugative plasmid TP114 with CRISPR-Cas9 has been delivered via conjugative probiotic
strain, which resulted in Citrobacter rodentium infection clearance in mice while leaving the rest of
the microbiome intact [139]. Such a result indicates a high potential for using probiotics as delivery
vehicles for CRISPR-Cas constructs.
