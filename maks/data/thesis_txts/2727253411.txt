Abstract
Denmark’s government has the ambitious plan to build two energy islands to accommo­
date renewable energy production in the North Sea and in the Baltic Sea, and is also
aiming to increase its electrolysis capacity to 4­6 MW by 2030.
Integrating Power­to­X with offshore wind power is considered to be a potential option to
achieve high amount of green hydrogen production and decarbonize other high emitted
sectors. However, achieving gigawatt­scale water electrolysis plant encounters various
obstacles concerning technological viability and market implementation. In this context,
the flexibility to load adjustments plays a key role for the integration with a fluctuating
renewable energy source.
This project deals with the integration of a 1 GW electrolyzer plant with 1 GW offshore
wind power. The analysis of the current work evaluated a total of 8 scenarions with differ­
ent combinations of stack configuration, electrolysis technology and control strategy. In
addition, 26 scenarios more are conducted to analyze the sensitivity of different param­
eters. The different scenarios have been modeled in Python and using Energinet’s wind
data as input. This project uses the closest estimations for a giga­watt scale electrolyser
plant commissioned in 2030, from manufacturer’s data, other research papers and some
calculations based on price forecasts.
The project concludes that AEL technology is still considered a better option for the giga­
scale project in both technical and economical perspective. Its reduced CAPEX, longer
stack lifetime and lower consumption rates (kWh/kg) are crucial factors that supports this
technology. And, despite ramp rate is better for PEMEL, the fluctuations of the wind power
barely exceeds the ramp rate limits of AEL. This decision can be reversed if in the future
years, PEMEL technology achieves better consumption rates than AEL.
Keywords: Power­to­X, energy island, control strategy, offshore wind, electrolyzer, stack,
ramp rate, consumption rate, Bornholm, green hydrogen, AEL, PEMEL.

Introduction
Over the past century, concern around the climate change and CO2 emissions has been
increasing due to several changes observed in the planet, such as the increase temper­
ature, frequency of natural disasters, rise in sea levels, and many other effects in earth
ecosystem.
Recent global environmental commitments, such as the Paris Agreement (COP 21), have
led to increase the deployment of renewable energy sources in the electricity grid. The
agreement has been ratified by 191 countries, where its primary goal is to limit global
warming to bellow 2 degrees Celsius above pre­industrial levels and to pursue efforts to
limit it to 1.5 degrees Celsius by the end of this century [1]. Moreover, as governments
aim to decrease carbon emissions and lower the dependence on fossil fuels, the demand
for green hydrogen has been surged rapidly and is expected to rise substantially in the
coming years.
The increasing share in the past years of variable energy in the energy mix, such as
wind and solar, requires additional facilities to integrate better these technologies into
the grid and avoid curtailment of energy when the production exceeds the demand. The
need for flexibility in the electric power system, has made Power­to­X, and specially, green
hydrogen as an attractive solution. Hydrogen is a flexible energy carrier with vast potential
application in many sectors. This versatile resource has numerous applications, serving
as a means for long­term storage and balancing services for variable renewable sources.
It can also be utilized as a clean fuel for zero­emission transportation, blended into the
existing natural gas grid for distribution and storage purposes. Moreover, it finds utility
in reduction processes within the industrial sector and acts as a catalyst for converting
renewable energy into different energy carriers, including synthetic methane, synthetic
liquid fuels, ammonia, and methanol.[2]
Historically, hydrogen production relied on fossil fuels, which resulted in the emission
of substantial amounts of CO2. However, over the last few decades, there have been
considerable developments in electrolysis and renewable energy generation, which have
enabled the production of green hydrogen at a viable cost. With the help of incentives and
policies, green hydrogen is undergoing significant investigation around the world, with the
objective of producing hydrogen without carbon emissions that, with a small incentive,
can compete with traditional hydrogen production methods. [3]
In July 2020, the European Union adopted an strategy to achieve ambitious goals for the
development and deployment of hydrogen infrastructure in the following years. Almost
all member states have included plans for clean hydrogen in their National Energy and
Climate Plan, including Denmark. In the first phase (2020­2024) for the EU road map to
develop renewable hydrogen using mainly wind and solar energy, the government plans
to install at least 6 GW of electrolyzers in the EU and a reach a production of up to 1 mil­
lion tonnes to decarbonize existing hydrogen production from other sources. The second
phase, from 2025 to 2030, the EU has set the objective to install at least 40 GW with a
production of 10 million tonnes. A coordinated effort is needed to achieve the 2030 objec­
tive, when the total electrolyzer capacity installation currently is below 1GW per year.[4]
[5]
Moreover, wind energy generation in Europe in 2022 reached new records in terms of
absolute production (489 TWh), and for share of consumption met by onshore (14.1%)

The creation of energy islands as integrated systems combining offshore wind farms with
electrolyzers for hydrogen production, align perfectly with the objectives of offshore wind
power installation and hydrogen production objectives. The energy islands promote syn­
ergy between these two sectors by utilizing the abundant renewable energy generated by
the offshore wind farms to produce green hydrogen. This integration supports the EU’s
goals of increasing the installed capacity of wind power and expanding the production of
green hydrogen. The coordinated efforts to develop energy islands will play a crucial role
in achieving the ambitious goals set by the European Commission’s REPowerEU Action
Plan and the strategy for hydrogen infrastructure development.
1.1
Background
Green hydrogen from water electrolysis could contribute to the defossilization, being con­
sidered key technology for future energy systems with hydrogen being used directly in
various applications such as mobility and industrial sectors, as well as serve as feed­
stock for the production of hydrocarbon fuels and chemicals like ammonia or methanol.
Another high potential use for this resource would be acting as a flexible loads and there­
fore, they can provide frequency restoration ancillary service, while producing hydrogen
for the different end­users. Also from the demand side, the built­in storage capacity can
be leveraged as a buffer to dynamically adjust hydrogen production in real­time to align
with the needs of the power system. However, despite these benefits, its integration in
power systems remains little studied and continues to suffer unfavorable economics. [7]
In June 2020, during the Climate Agreemeent for Energy Industry, a majority in the Danish
Parliament approved the Danish Climate Agreement, where they agreed to establish two
energy islands. One will be an artificial island the North Sea with an initial capacity of
3 GW and the other is Bornholm, which will serve as en energy island in the Baltic Sea
with a capacity of 2 GW. After some investigations conducted by COWI, the island in the
Baltic Sea were later expanded to accommodate one additional GW capacity if necessary
in addition to the already planned 2GW, for a total capacity of 3 GW. The windfarms will
be placed 15­20 km from the shore, and the substation will be located onshore, between
Arnager and Boderne. In addition, they commited to prepare a strategy for Power­to­X.
[8] [9]
In 2021, the Danish government published ”The Government’s strategy for POWER­TO­
X”, aiming to build 4­6 GW of electrolysis capacity by 2030. This emerging trend is spread­
ing globally, where green hydrogen is envisioned as a key solution for the realization of
green societies in the future. [11] [12]

Research questions
There are not many papers researching about the synergies between offshore wind power
and electrolyzers, specially in giga­scale. This master’s thesis tries to answer the following
central questions:

1.3
Methodology
Through this project, a thorough research has been done to obtain the technical param­
eters on the state­of­art technology of the electrolyzers. Some of the values used are
real values obtained from Original Equipment Manufacturer (OEM) catalogues, and some
other are obtained from estimations from other research studies. Information from the
Danish Energy Agency (DEA), Energinet and other sources have been consulted in or­
der to understand the current situation and future perspectives on the development of the
Bornholm Energy island. By using this information, the sizes and the system configura­
tion could be obtained for the model. Furthermore, the Offshore wind energy data was
collected from real data shared in Energinet’s database and finally, the development of
the Hybrid system model was created in Python. In the following chapter, more details on
the system created is provided.

Project Description
2.1
Objective
There is a focus on supplying renewable electricity directly to electrolyzers. However,
detailed analysis of such operating strategies via modeling and simulation of the dynamic
behavior of alkaline and proton exchange membrane electrolysis is lacking.
The objective of this Master’s thesis is to design and analyze different configurations of a
portfolio of 1GW electrolyzers for the Bornholm energy island, with the goal of understand­
ing the synergies between offshore wind power and hydrogen production. The study will
explore the use of Alkaline and PEM electrolyzers of varying sizes and quantities to com­
pare the performance of the different combinations of these technologies for producing
hydrogen from offshore wind power.
To achieve this objective, the project will compare the performance of different electrolyzer
configurations by testing larger or smaller capacities of electrolyzers while maintaining
a total amount of 1 GW. Each scenario will be tested with two different control strate­
gies. Through detailed modeling, factors such as ramping speed limitations and specific
plant characteristics, including hydrogen plant capacity, load range, and efficiency, will be
taken into account to assess the system’s behavior. The results will include information
such as the amount of hydrogen produced, operational hours, and standby time for each
electrolyzer in the different scenarios. Furthermore, data on electricity consumption from
both the grid and the wind farm, energy curtailment, production rates (kg/h), consump­
tion rates (kWh/kg), and certain economic values will also be presented. Moreover, this
study include a thorough sensitivity analysis to compare which parameters affect more to
the system’s performance. These results aims to identify the most efficient and effective
electrolyzer portfolio.
Overall, the thesis will contribute to our understanding of the potential for using elec­
trolyzers to produce hydrogen from offshore wind power and will provide insights into the
optimal configuration of electrolyzers for the Bornholm energy island.
2.2
Bornholm energy island
The state of this project is still immature. The agreement to establish the energy island
was approved in June 2020 and expected to be full commissioned in 2030. Until today,
some technical dialogues have occurred between the Danish Energy Agency (DEA), En­
erginet and potential bidders with the purpose of assessing the potential bidders whether
the Bornholm Energy Island is still considered attractive to establish within the current
framework set for the project. The publication of contract notice and procurement specifi­
cations is planned to happen in Q4 2023, the award of concession will occur in Q4 2024,
the Point of Connection (POC) ready in 2029 and full commissioning in 2030.[14]
The project consist in three Offshore Wind Farms located in three different areas in the
south­west of Bornholm, about 15 kilometers from shore. The areas of the plants are
presented in Table 2.1. Within this area, a total of 3.8 GW (including overplanting) can
be accommodated with turbines of maximum height of 330. These divisions have been
made considering environmental protection zones, sailing routes, etc. Finally, a total of
up to 7 offshore transformer substations can be constructed. [8] [9] [15]

2.3.1
Electrolyzer location
Determining the location of electrolyzers, whether offshore or onshore, is an important
factor in hydrogen production. This decision can affect enormously to the total cost of
the plant, not only for the capital costs, but also for the operations, maintenance and
transportation needs.
Placing the electrolyzer onshore is currently less expensive than offshore, due to sub sea
electricity transmission and the need of a pipeline infrastructure for hydrogen transporta­
tion. However this is expected to shift past 2030, when the costs for this projects falls as
production scales up. [17]
The optimal location for integrating a Power­to­X facility, whether onshore or offshore, is
not definitively determined, as there are advantages and disadvantages associated with
each option. Further studies are needed to fully evaluate the best approach. However,
for the purpose of this thesis, the hydrogen production plant is considered to be onshore,
mirroring the original plans for the Bornholm energy island. Here are some reasons why
Power­to­X onshore can offer more advantages compared to offshore
1. Installation cost for equipment: Generally, installing equipment offshore is more ex­
pensive than onshore and it is more costly to maintain. Offshore installations require
specialized equipment, vessels and personnel, which can increase the overall cost
of the project.
2. Land occupation: Big installations of electrolyzers might require a big area that can
result unpractical if installed offshore. In addition, placing the plant onshore will offer
more scalability and flexibility for future expansions.
3. Waste heat and oxygen recovery: By installing the facility onshore, the byproducts,
such as heat and oxygen, can be more easily recovered, increasing the energy
efficiency of the system.
4. Accessibility: An onshore plant is easier access for maintenance, control and moni­
toring. Sending personnel to offshore locations can be more logistically challenging,
time consuming and subject to weather conditions.
5. Backup power: Electrolyzers can easily adjust the power to suit the conditions from

the intermittent power source of a wind farm. However, some shutdown periods
can happen, and low amount of energy is required to maintain system in standby
for quick startup. Keeping the hydrogen production plant onshore and closer to the
grid, will enhance the power system stability by having a reliable backup power.
A previous study [18] compared the different Levelizez Cost Of Hydrogen (LCOH) for
three different electrolyzer location: onshore, offshore and in­turbine, being the latter the
less favorable and the other two have similar results.

2.3.3
Electrolysis technology
An electrolyzer is a device that receives DC electricity and pure water and splits the
molecules of water into hydrogen and oxygen atoms through a electrochemical reaction.
The resulting molecules are stored separately with a high percentage of purity. Although
the technologies operate slightly different, all have an anode and cathode that are sepa­
rated by an electrolyte. Currently, there are two main technologies that are commercially
available for the production of hydrogen:
• Alkaline Electrolyzer (AEL): Work by using a liquid alkaline electrolyte solution of
potassium hydroxide (KOH) or sodium hydroxide (NaOH) to facilitate the transfer
of hydroxide ions (OH­) through the electrolyte from the cathode to the anode with
hydrogen being generated on the cathode side. The electrodes are separated by
a diaphragm, which is non­conductive to separate the product gases and transport
the hydroxide ions from one electrode to the other. The diaphragm is typically a thin
porous foil with a thickness between 0.050 to 0.5 mm. Alkaline electrolyzers have
been commercially available for many years and are used to split water into hydro­
gen and oxygen using electric energy [21]. A disadvantage is that lower current
densities are reached, making the cells larger compared to the PEMEL [22].
• Proton Exchange Membrane Electrolyzer (PEMEL): This technology uses a solid
polymer electrolyte (SPE) to conduct protons from the anode to the cathode while
insulating the electrodes electrically. When current is applied to the cell , water splits
into hydrogen and oxygen. The polymer electrolyte allows the PEM electrolyzer to
operate with a very thin membrane ( 100­200 μm) while still allowing high pressures,
resulting in low ohmic losses, primarily caused by the conduction of protons across
the membrane (0.1 S/cm) and a compressed hydrogen output [23]. This technology
allows higher pressure on the gas products, potentially saving cost for subsequent
gas pressurization [22].
Another high potential technology which is under intense research and development is
Solid Oxide Electrolyzer (SOE), with the promises of high efficiency and flexibility.
Many of today’s largest commercial electrolyzer units have power limits of 5 MW. In the
coming years, it is expected to grow to 10 MW. This is not insignificant given that less
than a decade ago “big” was measured in kilowatt [3]. However, in order to effectively
achieve integration with giga scale offshore wind farms, it is crucial to scale up the size
of the electrolyzers further. To address this requirement, a modular approach has been
introduced, where several smaller stacks are grouped together to create large ones. This
way, varying capacities can be created, allowing for flexibility in the design. Additionally,
the aggregation of various stacks can help with having more compact solutions, with lower
weight and reduced footprint, potentially saving costs due to land property. This modularity
can also bring the advantage of enhanced operation under harsh offshore conditions, and
also the maintenance can be done on individual stacks without the need to interrupt the
operation of the whole plant, improving the system’s reliability.
Both technologies have different technical parameters that can affect the performance of
the hydrogen production in terms of absorbing renewable energy sources (RES). In the

2.3.4
Energy consumption and efficiency curve
Energy consumption plays a significant role in determining the hydrogen production. The
power input that is used in the hydrogen plant is solely determined by the power produced
by the wind and the ability of the electrolyzer to harness this power. Then, the amount
of hydrogen production is only dependant on the consumption rate. Therefore, having
a good efficiency curve influences the revenues obtained from the hydrogen production.
Due to these factors, it becomes crucial to carefully examine the definition, calculation,
and interpretation of efficiency in electrolysis technology.
The energy consumption rate at nominal load (kWh/kg H2) for this work is obtained from
the proposal of the European Commission, based on a 100 MW electrolyzer [24]. An

expected. These values also coincide with the values of energy consumption for elec­

compared to other papers that have considered smaller sizes of electrolyzers [12], be­

the overall efficiency due to more optimal and efficient use of the electrical infrastructure.

it depends on the partial load, i.e., the ratio between the power consumption at a specific
moment and the nominal power of the electrolyzer. Based on the efficiency curves from

2.3.5
Ramp rate
The ramp speed, also known as the ramp rate, refers to how quickly electrolyzers can
adjust their production levels. The rate at which they can respond depends on factors
like proper installation of power electronics and gas flow storage. The ramp rate plays
a crucial role in determining the capacity of electrolyzers to accommodate fluctuations in
input power from renewable sources like wind or solar.
Many papers agree on ramping speeds between 0.2­20% PN /second for AEL , and be­
tween 10­100% PN /s [7] [25] [28]. However, for this study real values from the man­
ufacturer NEL are considered. According to the information in [29], the ramp speed for
AEL is 10% PN /min and 100% PN /min for PEMEL. Although these values are for small
scale electrolyzers, same values can be applied for the big MW­scale electrolyzers of this

model. In [30], assumes that the response time of the electrolyzer does not increase sig­
nificantly for capacities in the range of MW compared to kW. Furthermore, this assumption
is supported by the fact that a MW­scale electrolyzer consists on many smaller unit, and
therefore, the same ramp rate of these smaller units can be considered.
The ramp rates of electrolyzers are influenced by the pressure and temperature condi­
tions, with higher system pressure favoring improved ramp rates. Consequently, PEM
electrolyzers demonstrate better ramp rates compared to alkaline electrolyzers, as the
latter typically operate under atmospheric conditions. On the cell level, PEM electrolyzers
have lower thermal capacity, further contributing to their enhanced ramp rates. Moreover,
the use of a solid electrolyte in PEM stacks allows for the elimination of the liquid elec­
trolyte circuit. As a result, PEM electrolyzers exhibit a smaller volumetric energy density,
influencing positively its flexibility against fluctuations. [22]
2.3.6
Stack lifetime
The lifetime of AEL and PEM electrolyzers is a crucial aspect to consider in hydrogen
production projects. Currently, AEL electrolyzers typically operate for nearly 80,000 hours,
while PEM electrolyzers have a lifespan of around 40,000 hours. However, advancements
in technology aim to extend their operational lifetimes by 2030, and remain longer for the
next decade.
Extending the lifetime of electrolyzers is essential to reduce the frequency of replace­
ments during the project’s operation. It has significant economic implications, as fewer
replacements translate to reduced costs associated with installing and commissioning
new units. Moreover, a longer operational lifetime ensures a more stable and reliable
hydrogen production process, supporting continuous and uninterrupted supply. Ongoing
research and development efforts aim to enhance the durability and performance of elec­
trolyzers, enabling longer and more efficient usage. For the purpose of this work, it has
been considered 90,000 hours of stack lifetime for AEL and 50,000 hours for PEM. [25]
2.3.7
States
Proper operational modeling of electrolyzers may require three states: on, off, and standby.
However, in this paper only two states are considered: on and standby. This choice is
based on the understanding that in the context of an offshore wind farm, there are only a
few instances of near­zero production power. Thus, it can be more cost­effective to turn
the electrolyzers into standby mode rather than turning them off completely. In addition,
when the electrolyzer is turned off, it takes more time to reach its full load compared to
when it is in standby mode. This process is known as cold start, and repetitive cold starts
can negatively impact stack lifetime. [12] [28]
1. On state: The electrolyzer produces hydrogen within its load range. The hydrogen
production rate depends on the input power and the efficiency. Although electrolyz­
ers can work beyond their nominal load (up to 200%) [25], in this study a maximum
of 100% maximum work load is considered. Operating at higher capacities can
accelerate degradation of the lifetime of the stacks.
2. Standby state: When the available power for one of the stacks falls below the min­
imum load threshold, the electrolyzer does not ramp up and no hydrogen is pro­
duced. However, the electrolyzer still consumes power to maintain the system’s
temperature and pressure for a rapid startup. To sustain this standby state, the
electrolyzer requires a minimal power consumption, typically around 1­5% of the
electrolyzer’s nominal capacity for multi­MW electrolyzers and between 10­20 % in
the case of kW­scale, because of the Balance of Plant (BOP) [31] [12]. For the pur­
DTU Wind and Energy Systems
15
==PAGE:28==
poses of this paper, a value of 2 % of the nominal power is considered for standby
power consumption.
In this study, the minimum power thresholds for transitioning electrolyzers from normal
operating mode to standby mode are set at 15 % for AEL [32] and 10 % for PEMEL [7]
[33].
It is not recommended to operate alkaline electrolyzers at lower loads due to concerns
about impurities in the gas products. Operating alkaline electrolyzers at low loads can
potentially lead to gas crossover between the cathode and anode, and form a flammable
gas mixture when the 2% of the volumen in H2 is O2. Two main factors contribute to these
issues: crossover through the diaphragm and crossover caused by circulated electrolyte
mixing.
Therefore, under low wind conditions the frequency of start­stops of the electrolyzers is
increased to ensure the system’s safety. A lower minimum load power allows for a wider
operational range of electrolyzers. As a result, the electrolyzers can capture a larger
amount of energy and curtail less power when operating under low load conditions [26].
Another reason for switching electrolyzers to standby mode is when the input power fluc­
tuation from the offshore wind farm exceeds the ramp­up or ramp­down speed of AEL
and PEMEL systems. In such cases, electrolyzers are tripped to standby mode to pre­
vent degradation and stress on the components, which can affect overall system stability
and control [34] [35].

Control strategies
Designing a good control system for managing a portfolio of electrolyzers is crucial for
optimizing their operation. In this master’s thesis, the focus is on studying two different
operation modes to absorb and utilize the available wind power as effectively as possible.
By studying different operation modes and analyzing their performance, this research
aims to compare two control strategies that can harness the maximum amount of wind­
generated power.
2.4.1

an offshore wind farm is based on a sequential mode operation. This means that the
electrolyzers function one at a time in a specific order. Initially, the first electrolyzer begins
its operation and receives input power from the wind farm. If there is enough power to run
the next electrolyzer, it continues ramping up until it reaches its maximum load capacity.

Economic Analysis
This section of the master thesis digs into the economic analysis of a combined offshore
wind power and hydrogen production system. While numerous studies have examined
the economic aspects of offshore wind power and hydrogen production individually, the
integration of these technologies on a giga­scale poses a unique challenge that necessi­
tates further exploration.
A significant hurdle encountered in this analysis lies in the scarcity of comprehensive liter­
ature pertaining to the capital costs of giga­scale electrolysis plants. That is because the
absence of operational facilities of such magnitudes. Consequently, reliance on reliable
sources is used to estimate the capital costs associated with this integrated system. In
this study, the figures provided by the Hydrohub Innovative Program, together with the
Levelized Cost Of Hydrogen (LCOH) annual report by Lazard, constitute the foundation
for estimating the economic figures.
This analysis will compare the economic viability and magnitude of costs of this giga­
scale project. The outcomes contribute to an enhanced comprehension of the economic
potential and feasibility of this integrated system.

4.3
Key findings
In this section, a thorough analysis is derived from the results. The objective is to gain
deeper understanding on the performance of each scenarios and observe patterns from
the simulations. The most relevant insights findings are described in each section.
4.3.1
Technology of the electrolyzer
Observing the results, it is visible that the performance of the alkaline electrolyzers out­
performs PEMEL in terms of hydrogen production across all the scenarios. Although
the energy consumed by PEM electrolyzers to produce hydrogen is higher, AEL technol­
ogy achieves higher quantity of hydrogen production due to its lower consumption rate.
For instance, in the scenario featuring 5 stacks, each with a capacity 200 MW each, the
AEL consumption rate is 52.23 kWh/kg, whereas the PEMEL consumption rate is 55.39
kWh/kg (as shown in Figure 4.1e and Figure 4.1f). These values are however slightly
higher than subsection 2.3.4, because the calculation also considers the energy used to
maintain the stacks in standby mode, which increases the amount of energy consumed
without producing any hydrogen.
Figure 4.2 illustrates the operational and standby status of the electrolyzers. A small
difference can be spotted between the two technologies, particularly in the 1 x 1000 MW
scenario, where the number and length of the red stripes are fewer and shorter for PEM
technology. This indicates that PEM electrolyzers switch to standby mode less frequently
and for shorter periods. This happens because given the same nominal load for both
AEL and PEMEL, the minimum load at which PEMEL can operate is lower (10 %PN for
PEMEL and 15 %PN for AEL) and therefore, the operation range is broader than AEL.
Consequently, PEM electrolyzers require fewer transitions to standby less times.
4.3.2
Size of stack and number of electrolyzers
Keeping the total installed capacity of one gigawatt, there are some differences when
varying the size and quantity of the stacks. In both technologies, the smaller the size of
the stack, the less hydrogen produced and the higher the consumption rate. Overall, the
performance of the system is worse when the sizes are reduced for both sequential and
load sharing control methods.
The reason behind this phenomenon lies in the fact that each stack has a minimum load
requirement for initiating electrolysis. Between 0% and the minimum threshold (repre­
sented as a percentage of the nominal load), the stacks do not produce any hydrogen.
Since, in most cases, the electrolyzers operate in sequential order for both control meth­
ods (as will be explained in subsection 4.3.3), increasing the number of stacks results in
more interleaved gaps in production, leading to a lower overall production of the system.
The other reason is that for higher amount of electrolyzers, more energy is required to
maintain the standby mode whenever the electrolyzers are not utilized. That is why the
overall consumption rate is worse for increasing number of stacks.
However, there is a special case observed in the scenario 7 using alkaline technology
Figure 4.1e, where the trend changes. This occurs because, in this particular portfolio
with 50 electrolyzers, in order to keep the remaining electrolyzers in standby mode when
the minimum load is not reached, the energy is drawn from the first stack. This phenom­
ena occurs only for alkaline due to narrower operational range, making it more likely for
the stacks to trip into standby mode and consequently not producing hydrogen when the
minimum load requirement is not met.

4.3.3
Control strategy
For each scenario, two different control methods have been simulated. In the first one, the
sequential control activates the electrolyzers when the preceding one reaches its maxi­
mum load, keeping it in standby mode if this condition is not met. The second control
method, activates all the electrolyzers simultaneously, aggregating the ramp up of all the
individual electrolyzers and capturing the maximum amount of energy.
Firstly, no differences have been found for the scenario 1 and 2 since there is only one
electrolyzer. For the rest of scenarios, a small improvement is observed by using the load
sharing algorithm (shown in orange in Figure 4.1). Less consumption rate, more hydrogen
production and more energy captured from the wind farm is achieved with this method.
In addition, the difference on the usage of the electrolyzers is insignificant between one
or the other, with a very little more operating hours in the load sharing method. Thus,
the number of stacks that need to be replaced is the same by using one method or the
other. However, where this control system stands out is in the scenario with 50 x 20 MW
AEL electrolyzers (scenario 7 and 8). In this situation, the energy consumed and the total
hydrogen produced by using load sharing control outperforms the sequential control, with
a 1.15 % and 1.29 %, respectively. The simultaneous activation of all the electrolyzers
allows to aggregate all the ramp rate, and therefore, can follow better the wind power
fluctuations.
In general, there is not a big difference between one strategy or the other. The load
sharing control shows better results, but the truth is that are quite insignificant because
there are only few times that the fluctuation on the wind power is so high that only the
load sharing method can fully harness it. Other parameters such as the size of the stack,
the technology used or the associate operational cost are more relevant considerations
to take into account for the decision­making.

Operating hours

PEMEL exhibits longer operating hours compared to AEL due to their ability to operate at
a wider range of power, increasing their flexibility.

this threshold. When multiplying the stack usage by the project’s duration (set at 20 years),

technologies require stack replacement.

4.3.5
Economical analysis
CAPEX and fixed OPEX (set at 3% of CAPEX) consistently tend to be higher for PEM
stacks compared to alkaline stacks. This difference is particularly more noticeable with
the current prices of both technologies. However, in this report, the CAPEX gap between
AEL and PEMEL narrows significantly, because it has been considered the outlook prices
for projects commissioned in 2030, which include some cost reduction measures. The
reduction on CAPEX for PEM technology is expected to be greater, because historically,
the cost decrease in recent years for this technology remained high, and there is expected
continuation of this downwardtrend due to rapid technology development and increased
manufacturing volume towards 2030. Conversely, alkaline electrolysis technology has
already reached a high maturity level, and cost reduction is more moderate, primarily
driven by scaling up the plants through improvements in the supply chain. [2]
Furthermore, CAPEX and fixed OPEX also show an uptrend when the size of the stack
decreases. This can be attributed to economies of scale, where the manufacturers can
achieve cost reductions by producing and installing larger stacks. The utilization and
optimization of certain components also play an important role in this trend, as the balance
of plant equipment and infrastructure can be shared among larger stack configurations.
By increasing the size and reducing the number of the stacks, the complexity involved in
design, manufacturing and installation may be reduced. Thus, a higher cost­effectiveness
is achieved resulting in cost savings.
The replacement of the stack can result in significant OPEX for the project, primarily due
to two main factors.
1. The costs associated with acquiring a new stack and consequent labour costs.
2. During the replacement process, the electrolyzers being replaced are non­operational,
resulting in a loss of production during that period.
3. Proper disposal or recycling of the old stack components may incur additional costs.
The costs associated with the stack replacement can be included prorating proportionally
the amount of hours that each electrolyzer has been used, or consider the cost of replace­
ment, only when the lifespan has been reached. For this work, it is only considered the
latter, where this type of expenses are included only when the usage of the stack exceeds
its lifetime.

5
Sensitivity Analysis
In this section, a sensitivity analysis will be conducted to explore the impact of various
parameters on the performance and economics of a hydrogen plant combined with off­
shore wind to produce green hydrogen. The focus of the sensitivity analysis will be on the
configuration with 5x200MW capacity using the sequential control mode.
The sensitivity analysis comprises four variables that will be studied:
1. Total annual hydrogen production
2. Consumption rate (kWh/kg)
3. Average usage in hours of the stack over the entire project duration
4. Lifecycle cost
To analyze this variables, all the parameters will be modified in two different scenarios:
the low case and the high case. For every scenario, only one parameter will be altered at
a time in order to examine their effects, allowing for a comparison with the base case.
In the first three sensitivity analyses, three different parameters have been selected for
modification to observe the corresponding changes in the analyzed variables. First, the
installed capacity of the hydrogen plant is compared between two configurations: the low
case with 4 stacks x 200MW and the high case with 5 stacks x 160MW. Both configura­
tions share a combined capacity of 800MW, resulting in a reduced ratio between the total
electrolyzer capacity and total offshore wind capacity.
Then, the minimum load power, expressed as a percentage of the nominal power and the
ramping rate will be adjusted to assess its influence on the system.
The fourth analysis is done from an economic perspective, specifically focusing on the
lifecycle cost. However, this analysis is simplified and has limitations as it does not dig
into the financial details, such as the interest rates. Despite that, the objective is to un­
derstand the scale of influence that different parameters can have on the lifecycle cost.
The parameters modified in this economic sensitivity include in addition to the previous
analysis (except the ramp rate, that does not affect), the CAPEX, fixed OPEX, OPEX
associated with stack replacement throughout the project duration and lastly, the whole
length of the project.
By conducting these sensitivity analyses, we aim to gain a valuable understanding of how
key parameters impact both the performance and economic viability of the electrolysis
plant. It is important to acknowledge its simplified nature and the need for deeper financial
considerations.
5.1
Annual hydrogen production
From the results depicted in Figure 5.1a and Figure 5.1b, the most influencing parameter
is the installed capacity. Logically, if the total capacity of the plant is reduced by 20 %,

The last sensitivity analysis examines the impact of various parameters on the lifecycle

while the high case configuration with 5x200 MW has lower OPEX for stack replacement.
The high case also shows longer average usage per year for all electrolyzers except
the last one, potentially leading to more stack replacements and increased project costs.

whereas the high case demonstrates reduced replacement costs. At PEMEL scenarios,

replacement costs (OPEXstack is given in €/kW) due to smaller stacks and same quantity

6
Limitations and Future Research
The main goal of this master thesis is to understand and compare the different behaviors
of various configurations of electrolyzer stacks under a fluctuating power source, such as
offshore wind power, in order to imitate a potential layout for the future energy island in
Bornholm. However, there are several limitations to be considered due to the unprece­
dented scale of the real project, making it challenging to obtain real values and technical
specifications. As a result, many assumptions have been made based on reliable data
from other studies, manufacturers, or transmission system operators.
Table ?? summarizes the simplifications done in this study and the potential future re­
search to improve the precision on the results.
The economic analysis aims to provide an understanding of the magnitude of a gigascale
hydrogen plant and assess the economic implications associated with different configura­
tions of electrolyzers. However, it does not dig deeply intro high financial levels, as it does
not involve speculations on revenues from selling hydrogen or detailed investment analy­
sis such as interest rates. These variables are subject to significant uncertainties and the
reliability on the result would heavily rely on the precision of electricity price forecasts in
the following decades, which may introduce potential inaccuracies into the analysis.
Instead, the obtained figures in the economic analysis serve to illustrate the magnitude
of the capital expenditures (CAPEX) and operating expenditures (OPEX) involved in the
project. Sensitivity analysis is employed to gain a general perspective of the financial ex­
penditures associated with such a project and to identify which parameters have a higher
sensitivity.
By addressing these limitations and conducting further research in these areas, we can re­
fine the understanding and analysis of electrolyzer stack behavior under fluctuating power
sources. This will contribute to the development of more accurate models and designs
for the future energy island in Bornholm.

7
Conclusion
The Techno­economic analysis of the 1GW electrolyzer portfolio for the Energy Island
Bornholm project aimed to evaluate the technical and economic potential of a green hy­
drogen plant under different electrolyzer setups while maintaining a constant total installed
capacity, simulaing a potential layout for the Bornholm energy island porject. Through
simulations, sensitivity analysis, and the examination of various variables, the following
conclusions have been drawn.
Firstly, despite the higher ramp rate of PEMEL compared to AEL, and supposedly be
a better option for capturing the fluctuations from the wind, the thruth is that the wind
fluctuations observed barely exceeds the ramp rate of the AEL, making both technologies
equally competitive in capturing the fluctuations.
The comparison also reveals that there is minimal difference between the two control
strategies proposed. The sequential mode activates the electrolyzers when the preced­
ing one reaches its maximum load whereas the load sharing mode activates all the elec­
trolyzers simultaneously, aggregating the ramp up of all the individual electrolyzers. The
results show that the load sharing has very small improvements compared to the former
one. This happens because the fluctuations of the wind barely exceeds the ramp rate
of one electrolyzer, and therefore the ability to aggregate the ramp rate of the individual
electrolyzers from the load sharing method is barely leveraged. However, in the case
of small stacks, load sharing method demonstrate bigger improvents in AEL scenarios,
because it reduces the standby time of the last electrolyzers. PEMEL systems show no
significant difference in this aspect because of its higher ramp rate.
Furthermore, increasing the number of stacks without the option to turn off worsens the
system’s performance. Provided a system designed with the same capacity as the wind
farm, more stacks result in a higher possibility of having stacks in standby mode, consum­
ing a residual power without producing hydrogen. However, reducing the ratio between
installed capacity of electrolyzer and installed capacity of wind power, achieves lower
consumption rates and improves stack utilization.
Using the current state­of­the­art’s technical parameters, the main significant difference
found between the different scenarios are related to load flexibility and the efficiency
curves. While the load flexibility is more favorable for PEMEL systems, the consumption
rates makes AEL a better solution. With a 5.8 % lower consumption rate, AEL exhibits
more hydrogen production for every scenario. Another outcome is that PEMEL always
captures more energy than AEL. Therefore, if the efficiency is improved and the con­
sumption rate is lowered to match same level as AEL, then PEMEL will outperform AEL’s
performance.
Since the efficiency curve determines that the most efficient point of the electrolyzer is
given for a partial load between 17­25 %, a different control approach that could possible
enhance the plant’s production would be to keep all the stacks close to these levels and
only if there is surplus of power, ramp up sequentially the electrolyzers.
The economic analysis shows that the CAPEX represents a big share of the total cost.
The OPEX due to replacement is more remarkable for PEM technology as the lifetime of
the stack is 44.4 % shorter than AEL. Larger stack sizes prove more favorable in terms
DTU Wind and Energy Systems

of CAPEX and fixed OPEX, as scaling up reduces upfront costs and operation and main­
tenance expenses. Implementing additional control strategies that adjust the order of
electrolyzer activation can potentially reduce costs by minimizing the frequency of stack
replacements.
Setting a 5x200 MW system as the base case, the sensitivity analysis determines that the
most sensitive parameter is the installed capacity. Decreasing the hydrogen plant size
makes a better use of the electrolyzers, reducing their standby time. At the same time,
the consumption rate is reduced due to reduced standby consumption, and although the
total hydrogen production is lower, the efficiency of the plant is improved.
When modifying the ramp rate, no much variation is observed. only in the case where
the AEL’s ramp decreases from 10 % PN/min to 5 % PN/min, a 3.31% reduction in the
hydrogen production is observed, represented by wind gusts that produces large step
increase that exceeds the electrolyzer’s adaptation capabilities. This also means that the
addition of a battery to supply a more flat and smooth power input might not be required
since it will not bring much benefits and could increase the total cost of the plant.
In terms of economic sensitivity, the variation on the project length together with the OPEX
due to stack replacement are the most sensitivy parameter after CAPEX. These two pa­
rameters determines the frequency at which the stacks have to be replaced upon reach­
ing the lifespan limit. However, if the an additional control systems manages the order
at which the electrolyzers run, so the usage of each stack can be equally balanced, the
frequency of replacement can be reduced and potentially, the lifecycle costs.
While doing this research, some improvements have been realized to further improve
the performance of this plant. Reducing the ratio, use the load sharing method that can
aggregate the ramp rates, altering the order at which the control runs the electrolyzers
and an algorithm that optimizes the load of the electrolyzers to their optimal point and
can switch off the stacks when low winds are forecasted, could improve drastically the
performance and operations and maintenance cost of the whole system.
