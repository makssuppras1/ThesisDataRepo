Abstract 
Lactococcus Lactis (L. lactis) MG1363 grows more slowly on maltose than on glucose. 
An adaptive laboratory evolution (ALE) experiment was set up to look for and explain 
the bottleneck of MG1363 growth on maltose and to better understand the reasons for 
controlling bacterial growth on specific sugars. The mutants obtained by ALE over 
several months showed a faster growth rate on maltose. After 850 generations, CS3155, 
one of the mutants was isolated and used in later research experiments. Growth 
experiments showed a 27% increase in specific maltose growth rate, but more 
surprisingly, on galactose (25%) and glucose (5%), there was a faster growth rate as 
well. In all three sugars, the formation of the products and by-products became more 
homogeneous. In CS3155, the faster the growth rate of maltose was, the higher the 
turnover rate of maltose was, while the consumption flux of maltose increased by 27%. 
The sugar flux of galactose and glucose decreased by 30% and 7%, respectively. 
Analysis of the medium after fermenting showed that arginine was metabolized on 
maltose by CS3155, while MG1363 only consumed arginine on maltose. The arginine 
consumption rate was also tested. It showed that when growing on maltose, CS3155 
consumed arginine at a significantly faster rate than MG1363. By deleting the arcA 
gene, it was possible to eliminate arginine catabolism. After deleting arcA, MG1363 
and CS3155 had similar growth states on galactose, while CS3155 derivatives still grew 
faster on maltose and glucose. Selected enzyme activities, including three specific ones 
==PAGE:3==
ii 
 
for maltose metabolism, were determined and only the β-phosphoglucomutase was 
found to be increased (by 70%). To further characterize the mutant, its genome was 
sequenced, and transcriptome analysis was performed. These findings suggest that 
some mutations were accumulated and in which the gene argJ and phoU may be 
responsible for the change of the phenotype. One of the maltose specific genes, pgmB, 
have been up regulated in CS3155. At the same time, it was seen that malE and malF 
were 2.0 and 1.7-fold up-regulated on all three sugars.

1 Introduction 
1.1 An overview of the project 
Lactococcus lactis (L. lactis) MG1363 grows slowly on maltose compared with that on 
glucose. After an adaptive laboratory evolution for several months, we isolated a mutant 
CS3155 that was able to grow faster on maltose. Meanwhile, CS3155 showed a faster 
growth on galactose and glucose as well. Meanwhile, in CS3155, the by-product 
formation on maltose was more homolactic, more lactic acid was produced instead of 
format, acetate, and ethanol. At the same time, the maltose consumption flux became 
higher.

1.2 Lactococcus lactis 
Lactococcus lactis(L. lactis) is one of the most comprehensively investigated lactic acid 
bacteria (LAB) due to its important features in application in dairy industry.[1] LAB 
==PAGE:9==
3 
 
are generally regarded as safe (GRAS) and consist of various important genera: 
Enterococcus, Streptococcus, Lactococcus, Oenococcus, Leuconostoc, Pediococcus, 
Lactobacillus, ect.[2] Among these genera, L. lactis belongs to Lactococcus and is 
facultative anaerobic, Gram-positive and non-sporulating.[3]  
 
Currently, L. lactis comprised L. lactis subsp. lactis, L. lactis subsp. cremoris and L. 
lactis subsp. hordniae.[4] In this project, L. lactis subsp. cremoris MG1363 is the wild-
type strain we used, which is widely studied throughout the world. MG1363 is plasmid-
free and constructed from L. lactic NCDO712, and the plasmid was removed by 
applying UV treatment and protoplast-curing strategies.[5] The genome of MG1363 
has been completely sequenced since its long time research. [6]  
 
L. lactis is one of the most commonly used starters in the fermentation, especially has 
an extensive use in dairy industry. At present, L. lactis is usually applied to the 
production of sour cream, yogurt, soybean yogurt, milk drinks and other dairy products. 
In spite of this, during the fermenting process, L. lactic also plays an important role in 
the development of the sensory characteristics of the product.[7] For example, in the 
fermentation of the dairy product industries, the main function of L. lactis, in addition 
to converting the lactose in milk to lactic acid by fermenting, is producing flavor 
substances such as diacetyl and acetaldehyde. Nevertheless, during the cheese maturing 
process, the intracellular peptidase and extracellular protease of L. lactis can promote 
the hydrolysis of protein in cheese, and such a process takes a critical part in in the 
==PAGE:10==
4 
 
formation of the mature cheese flavor substances. In spite of the dairy industry, through 
the metabolic activities, such as proteolysis during fermenting soymilk, proteins are 
broken down into amino acids and peptides. This process of decomposition can remove 
the unpleasant odors in soy products. Moreover, during the process of making kimchi, 
ethyl lactate produced by esterification reaction can increase the flavor and sour taste. 
Meanwhile, L. lactis also does benefit to the formation of texture. For example, through 
the production of exopolysaccharides (EPS), a satisfying texture can be obtained.[8] In 
addition to all the above functions, as to the main characteristic of L. lactis, is 
metabolizing glucose or lactose to produce lactate.[9] The forming of lactate is quite 
benefit in prolonging the shelf-life of the products which are produced by fermentation, 
for the accumulation of lactate can drop pH value. A relatively low-pH environment is 
able to suppress the growth of the other microorganisms.[10] 
  
Despite of the important effects in food industry, based on several features on L. lactic, 
such as its fast growth rate, with relatively simple metabolism, the separate metabolism 
of decomposition and anabolic, no producing of endotoxin or toxic substances,[11] 
fully sequenced small genome, no formation of inclusion bodies nor extracellular 
protease, its monolayer membrane which is conducive to the secretion of exogenous 
proteins, and relatively few intracellular and extracellular proteins, along with the 
successful development of compatible genetic engineering tools and etc., all of these 
mentioned characteristics make L. lactic a model LAB with genetic engineering.[12] 
Genomes of L. lactis are small but contain enough biological information, and

make L. lactis of great research value in metabolic regulation. 
 
1.3 Homolactic Fermentation and Heterolactic Fermentation in 
Lactococcus lactis 
Lactic acid fermentation, which is a process by which sugars are decomposed and 
utilized by anaerobic fermentation to produce lactate. According to the difference of 
strains, metabolic pathways and products, lactic acid fermentation is classified into 
homolactic fermentation and heterolactic fermentation.  
 
1.3.1 Homolactic Fermentation 
Homolactic fermentation occurs through the Embden Meyerhof Pathway (EMP) which 
is shown in Figure 1-1. One molecule of glucose is converted to 1,6-Fructose 
Diphosphate (FDP) and consumes ATP to release energy. Then, catalyzed by acetal

bacteria can conduct homolactic fermentation through EMP pathway, and the main 
fermentation product is lactic acid.

1.3.2 Heterolactic Fermentation 
Glucose is oxidized by the hexose monophosphate pathway (HMP), producing a large 
amount of NADPH and H+ and a variety of important intermediate metabolites. After 
the metabolization of glucose and the production of glucose-6-phosphate (G6P), the 
HMP pathway is cleaved into D-Xylulose-5-phosphate (DX5P) and CO2 under the 
catalysis of glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G6DP). Now, pentose phosphate is 
metabolized in two ways. 
 
In the first pathway, hexose phosphate and PGAL are generated, and triose phosphate 
is further converted to pyruvate by using some enzymes of the EMP pathway. The 
second pathway involves a series of reactions to produce ethanol as the final product. 
The HMP pathway can be found in Figure 1-2.  
 
The percentage of HMP pathway in total microbial metabolic energy is related to the 
product requirements between the metabolic activities of cells. Many important 
coenzyme, fermented products can be produced by this method. Such as nucleotide, 
amino acid and lactic acid. Meanwhile, HMP can also provide pentose-phosphate for 
nucleotide and nucleic acid biosynthesis. 
 
Lactic acid bacteria produce lactate, formate, ethanol, acetate, and other products 
through HMP fermentation. Some lactic acid bacteria also need to rely on the HMP 
pathway for sugar degradation due to the lack of aldolase and isomerase.

1.4.1 Metabolic pathway on maltose and glucose 
When L. lactis grows on maltose, the sugar in the medium is taken up by an ATP 
dependent permease and enters the cell as maltose. After entering the cell, maltose is 
split into glucose-1-phosphate (G1P) which can be found in Figure 1-3 and glucose by 
maltose phosphorylase.[38] Glycolysis takes place after phosphorylation of glucose by 
glucokinase (GK). At the same time, the isomerization of G1P into glucose-6-phosphate 
(G6P) by β-phosphoglucomutase (β-PGM).[17]  
 
The growth rate of MG1363 on maltose is quite slow when compared to when growing 
on glucose [18]. This phenotype can indicate some differences and bottlenecks when 
growing on glucose and maltose. During the transport at the beginning of the 
metabolization. References show that maltose is transported by an ABC type permease 
while glucose is taken up by a PTS which can be seen in Figure 1-3 due to it is more 
readily fermentable. Glucose enters the cell as G6P. [40] Compare the energy spent on 
transport when entering glycolysis, there is no difference whether growing on maltose 
or glucose, when equal amounts of both sugars are added for metabolizing. However,

1.4.2 Metabolic pathway on galactose and lactose

catabolism (Figure 1-6) and that is followed by the producing glucose and galactose or 
galactosephosphate. Although glucose entering glycolysis is quite easy, another product, 
galactose, is less efficient to be utilized during fermentation in some strains. Due to the 
low utilization of galactose, the galactose component may even appear in the growth 
medium.[19][20]

1.5Adaptive Laboratory Evolution  
As a scientific method, adaptive laboratory evolution (ALE) is very important for the 
analysis of evolutionary phenomena in a controlled laboratory environment. ALE was 
completed by William[25] about a century ago and by the middle of the last century.[26] 
In the process of biological study, ALE is a common approach to research the basic 
mechanisms of molecular evolution and adaptation changes which are accumulated in 
microbes through long-term selection under specific growth conditions.[27] When 
microbes evolve under designed and controlled conditions, it provides a way to select 
phenotypic changes that exhibit better function and may carry spontaneous mutations 
that are beneficial under given conditions.[27][28] 
 
In the ALE experiment, a selected target microbe is required to be cultured and 
generated under specific conditions. Over a period of time, say, weeks to years, the 
==PAGE:23==
17 
 
selected phenotype of the microorganism will improve. ALE studies microbes exhibit 
some important comparative advantages, including the fact that most microbes have 
only simple nutritional requirements. In addition, it is very convenient to conduct and 
operate microbial culture in the laboratory. At the same time, and importantly, 
microorganisms generally grow rapidly, with typical specific microbial cell growth 
rates ranging from 0.05 to 1.0 h-1,[27] and that means hundreds of generations can be 
cultured in weeks or months. In contrast to comparative genomics, ALE experiments 
show that the altered microbial traits are closely related to the specific growth 
conditions designed.[29] The ALE experiment provides important experimental 
evidence for evolutionary biology. Due to applying of new technologies, whole genome 
re-sequencing (WGS) is able to test phenotype-genotype correlations with the help of 
transcriptional profiling[30] and next-generation DNA sequencing (NGS).[20][21]  
 
ALE experiments are generally easy to set up, and two approaches which are common 
to be applied in the lab are shown in Figure 1-7. By using shaking flask to culture, 
microorganisms can be generated in parallel continuous culture. Usually, a part of the 
culture needs to be moved to a fresh medium each day for a new round of growth and 
then generates. This method is quite simple to set up, inexpensive to use, and allows for 
a large number of parallel experiments. If we can use a smaller culture volume, such as 
a deep well plate, [33] hundreds of microorganisms can grow in it simultaneously. What 
is more, some environmental factors can be more easily under control. But over a long 
period of time, the population density of microbes will change. In addition, the growth 
==PAGE:24==
18 
 
rate of microorganisms also varies with different growth periods. In the process of 
microorganism growth and the consumption of culture medium, the nutrient supply in 
the growth environment and environmental conditions, such as the change of pH value, 
may cause environmental complexity.[27] However, in most experiments, these factors 
may not have such critical effects on the setup of the experiments. 
 
Continuous culture in a bioreactor vessel is the second ALE experimental 
method.[34][35] In this way, the growth rate and population density of microorganisms 
are basically at a constant level. In addition, the nutrient supply in the medium and 
environmental conditions for microbial growth can be precisely controlled, such as pH 
value and oxygen content. But the biggest drawback is the high operating costs 
compared with the culture in shaking flask.  
 
In this study, we selected the first batch continuous culture method to conduct ALE 
experiment on MG1363. Since MG1363 showed a relatively slow growth rate and low 
cell density when on maltose than that on glucose, the sugar for growing was controlled, 
instead of adding glucose, we used maltose as the substrate for fermentation. And as a 
result, a mutant, CS3155, was obtained with good phenotypic improvement after 850

generations.

2.4 Adaptive Laboratory Evolution 
MG1363 was used in ALE experiment. The experiment was carried out at 30℃ in vitro 
under the condition of batch culture, anaerobic and no shock. First, microorganisms 
were cultured in a medium containing 0.2% (W/V) maltose and grew with maltose as 
the sole carbon source, initiating liquid culture. The next day, part of the bacterial 
solution was transferred to the fresh medium for dilution and new growth. This was 
repeated for several generations to look for mutants with improved traits, i.e., to exhibit 
better growth characteristics on maltose. 
2.3 Genome Sequencing and Data Analysis 
The genome sequencing data of L. lactis MG1363 was obtained at NCBI portal. Single 
colonies were cultured in 5 ml M17 medium with 0.02% (W/V) sugar (glucose, maltose 
or galactose) at a temperature of 30℃. The microorganisms were cultured overnight, 
diluted with M17 culture medium containing corresponding sugar by 50 times. After 
dilution, bacteria continued to be cultured to make the bacteria grow to the exponential 
stage. The cells were centrifuged at 10000 RPM for 2 min and the DNA was isolated 
and extracted using the GenElute bacterial genomic DNA kit (Sigma-Aldrich) in 
accordance with the procedure. These genomes were sequenced by Illumina HiSeq 
sequencing system. During the assembly phase, the sequence reads were aligned with 
the previous sequence to assemble the Genome sequence of L. Lactis MG1363 for

3.1 Isolation of the fast growing mutant CS3155 
L. lactis CS3155, a fast-growing mutant which was obtained from L. lactis subsp. 
cremoris MG1363. CS3155 was isolated after MG1363 being cultured for an extended 
period in the defined SALN medium with added 0.02% (w/v) maltose as the only 
carbon source. In this medium, the wild-type strain, MG1363 grows with a doubling

36 minutes. This difference in growth rate was the incentive for mutating. Surprisingly, 
there was a mutant with a doubling time around 55 minutes which was relatively faster 
compared with MG1363, was obtained after several months and this mutant was named 
as CS3155.

4 Discussion 
4.1 The adaptive laboratory evolution 
ALE is designed to produce desirable properties during continuous culture and 
generation of bacteria over a long period of time under specific environmental selection. 
In this study, the carbon source of L. lactis MG1363 was controlled. 0.02% (w/v) 
maltose was added to culture medium SALN as the sole carbon source of MG1363, and 
the bacteria were continuously cultured. During this process, 850 generations were 
performed, and we obtained mutant CS3155 with good growth characteristics. 
 
The reason for this ALE experiment was that the growth characteristics of MG1363 
were quite different when growing on different carbon sources. According to the 
obtained experimental data, MG1363 grew at a faster rate on glucose, and the final 
fermentation product conformed to the composition of homolactic fermentation. Most 
of the products were lactic acid, and a small part of the by-products may appear. 
However, the growth status of maltose showed a completely different trend. First, the 
growth rate of the bacteria and the cell density at the exponential stage were greatly 
reduced. This indicated that growing on maltose, MG1363 was not well consumed and 
utilized the substrate. Not only that, fermentation of the final product also appeared a 
large number of formate, acetate, etc., lactic acid content decreased. The composition 
of the final product indicated that MG1363 was heterolactic fermentation when maltose 
was used as carbon source. In order to explore the reasons for the growth retardation of 
==PAGE:50==
44 
 
MG1363 on maltose, we need to obtain a strain that can show a good growth state on 
maltose. Therefore, the ALE experiment was established to allow bacteria to gradually 
adapt to the growth conditions in selected cultures and accumulate the desired 
characteristics in the generation. 
 
Compared with the parent strain CS3155, the growth rate of maltose and the rate of 
sugar consumption of the isolated strain were 27% higher. There was a 24% increase in 
the final cell density on maltose. Moreover, the final product of CS3155 had more lactic 
acid, and the amount of the remaining by-products is significantly reduced. This 
indicates that CS3155 had a more homogenous fermentation metabolic pathway. The 
growth characterizations of CS3155 on glucose were also improved. This phenomenon 
indicates that CS3155 may not only undergo changes in maltose metabolic pathways 
during ALE experiment. In general, the mutant CS3155 obtained by ALE experiment 
has the desired good characteristics. 
 
4.2 The arginine performed a positive effect on the growth of L. 
lactis 
Arginine takes an important part in the urea cycle. It is metabolized to citrulline and 
ornithine through the ADI pathway in bacteria.[55] Surprisingly, lactic acid bacteria 
have an ADI pathway to produce citrulline and ornithine from arginine as well. For L. 
lactis, arginine serves as an energy source for bacteria growth at the same time. In many 
==PAGE:51==
45 
 
cases, lactic acid bacteria utilize arginine and citrulline to obtain energy via the ADI 
system.[56] Ammonia produced by ADI system is a critical nutrient source for bacteria 
growing and in the synthesis of produce proteins and nucleic acids. 
 
In this study, from the data, we have found that CS3155 can utilize the added arginine 
completely when grew on maltose. Even though MG1363 still obtained a much lower 
cell density compared with CS3155 when adding same amount of arginine, MG1363 
still consumed the arginine for growth. When using glucose as the only carbon source, 
both MG1363 and CS3155 did not consume arginine. To further prove the positive 
effect of arginine addition on bacterial growth, different concentrations of arginine were 
added to culture medium SALN and after cultured for a certain time, the cell density of 
MG1363 and CS3155 were detected and compared. We only added 10 mM as the 
maximum amount of arginine, and within this range, both strains showed an increasing 
trend in cell density. Nevertheless, the data indicated that the growth rate has also been 
improved. It seemed that the increased cell density of CS3155 was significantly 
correlated with the increased concentration of arginine, which means the addition of the 
arginine affected the growth of CS3155 much more than that on MG1363. 
 
The arginine deaminase pathway includes arginine conversion to ornithine, NH3, CO2 
and ATP.[57] The route involves three enzymes for catalysis. In the presence of ADI, 
arginine is broken down into citrulline and ammonia.[57] The ADI pathway alkalinizes 
the cytoplasm and produces metabolic energy by releasing NH3. This is because ATP is 
==PAGE:52==
46 
 
produced during substrate phosphorylation catalyzed by carbamate kinase. Ornithine 
carbamoyl transferase converts citrine to ornithine and tranexamic acid.[57] And then 
finally carbamate kinases, and the final products are ammonia, carbon dioxide and 
ATP.[57] These three enzymes are encoded by acrA, arcB and arcC, respectively. 
 
According to literature, 1 mole of arginine is consumed, and 1 mole of ATP is produced. 
Combined with existing experimental data, it can be preliminarily assumed that the 
addition of a small amount of arginine indeed provides a certain positive catalytic effect 
for cell growth. The growth and reproduction of cells are dependent on the energy 
supply of ATP. However, theoretical studies have shown that during the catabolism of 
arginine, some ammonia is released into the bacterial growth environment. Based on 
the experimental results obtained by some previous research, in L. lactis, during the 
process of arginine consumption, the bacterial growth environment will gradually 
become alkaline, which means that pH will increase gradually when more arginine is 
consumed. An overly alkaline environment can induce inhibition of the glutamic acid 
decarboxylation system. 
 
4.3 The effect of gene arcA & the upregulated arcA, arcB and arcC 
It has been mentioned in section 4.1 that arcA encodes ADI. The ADI is the first step in 
the decomposition of arginine. The ADI converts arginine to citrulline and releases 
ammonia. Theoretically, if the arcA gene is deleted, the ADI pathway will not be able 
==PAGE:53==
47 
 
to continue, and subsequent catabolism, such as the breakdown of citrate, will also be 
blocked. Based on the literature, we already know that large amounts of energy are 
released in the process of decomposition and consumption of arginine. Adequate ATP 
supply makes the growth and reproduction of bacteria active, which can not only 
produce higher cell density but also show faster growth rate. 
 
In order to verify the importance of gene arcA, we deleted the target gene in this 
experiment. The bacteria MG1363 and CS3155 with the target gene deleted, were 
cultured again, and the growth culture medium were conducted in different carbon 
sources at the same concentration, and the growth characteristics of different strains 
were compared. The experimental results showed that deletion of arcA did have a great 
impact on the growth of bacteria. CS3155 grew on glucose after the deletion of the gene, 
which grew only 2% faster than MG1363 and 4% slower than before the deletion. The 
growth rate of CS3155 on maltose deletion was 5% higher than that of MG1363 and 
16% lower than that before deletion. The growth rate of MG1363 decreased slightly 
before and after deletion of the target gene. 
 
The e data showed that the better growth characteristics obtained by the mutant CS3155 
were largely affected by the arginine metabolic pathway. Combining the data gained 
from the transcriptomes, gene arcA, arcB and arcC all present a upregulation trend, that 
is to say, three enzymes encoded by these three genes, ADI, Ornithine carbamoyl 
transferase and carbamate kinases can get higher expression levels. The entire arginine 
==PAGE:54==
48 
 
metabolic pathway will involve more key enzymes, allowing more nutrients to be 
produced and more energy to be released. However, the pH of the medium may be 
affected by the accumulation of ammonia in the medium.

5 Conclusion 
In this study, we focused on the factors affecting the growth retardation of L. lactis 
MG1363 on maltose. In order to find the bottleneck of bacterial growth more accurately, 
ALE was first adopted to evolve the parent strain. The carbon source in the design 
experiment is only maltose. In the process of long-term selection, strains that can better 
adapt to the growth of maltose are left behind, and the good traits obtained during the 
period are accumulated and transferred and retained. After 850 generations, we 
successfully obtained a well-shaped mutant CS3155. CS3155 not only showed 
excellent characters on maltose, but also showed small improvement on glucose. 
 
According to the later experiments, we found that the growth of mutant CS3155 was 
closely related to the addition of arginine. When the concentration of arginine increased 
gradually, CS3155 also showed a faster growth rate and higher cell concentration. 
Therefore, we preliminarily believe that the arginine metabolic pathway is of great 
significance. The metabolism of arginine provides the bacteria with a large amount of 
ATP. When the gene arcA (encoding ADI) was deleted, we found that the growth rate 
of CS3155 on maltose was indeed significantly limited, while the growth rate on 
glucose was less affected. We believe that the addition of arginine has a great influence 
on the growth of CS3155 in maltose, and meanwhile, the growth of CS3155 in maltose 
requires more energy support.

By genome sequencing we found a number of mutations in the CS3155, through the 
existing cognitive, we think argJ and phoU mutations may be a good shape with the 
mutant strains have greater relationship, later can knock out target genes, growth 
character comparison, further analysis whether mutations and character changes have a 
direct relation. By transcriptome analysis and enzyme activity test, we found that only 
pgmB, an important gene directly related to maltose metabolism, was up-regulated, and 
the enzyme encoded β-PGM showed higher expression. β-PGM plays an important role 
in the maltose pathway, transforming G1P into G6P for further glycolysis.
