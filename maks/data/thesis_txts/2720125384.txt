Abstract
With a continuously growing focus on sustainability within the Architecture, Engineering,
Construction, and Operation (AECO) industry, there is a need for accelerating the competence
development within Life Cycle Assessments (LCA) across the entire industry. According to
the International Energy Agency, the manufacturing of cement, steel and aluminium used for
building constructions amount to around 6% of the total global energy- and process-related
CO2-eq emissions in 2021 [1], underlining the urgency of reducing embedded carbon emis-
sions from load-bearing structures.
The use of advanced Building Information Modelling (BIM) can enhance the sustainability of
construction projects. Implementing a Common Data Environment (CDE) can facilitate bet-
ter integration of BIM and LCA through a centralised platform that utilises a single source of
truth for project data, providing stakeholders access to the most accurate and up-to-date project
information.
This thesis introduces a toolchain capable of representing LCA calculations based on structural
3D models through automated processes. The toolchain is based on a microservice architec-
ture for easy implementation in a cloud-based CDE. A class hierarchy for structural elements
was developed to create a foundation for exporting data from proprietary 3D models into an
object-oriented database. The class hierarchy was utilised to create structural elements parsers
from Revit and FEM-Design. A microservice was created to depict LCA calculations based on
the structural elements data obtained from Revit and FEM-Design. Furthermore, a microser-
vice was created to investigate how environmental data could be implemented as a design
parameter in a parametric study of structural design in FEM-Design.
This thesis reveals some barriers associated with automating the workﬂow between structural
3D models related to both technical aspects and human behaviour. Despite these barriers, this
thesis demonstrates that providing structural engineers with valuable feedback regarding en-
vironmental performance through an automated workﬂow based on the developed toolchain
is possible. This eventually creates possibilities for the engineers to assess embodied carbon of
the load-bearing structures in the early phases, enabling the potential for reducing the overall
climate impact from buildings.

INTRODUCTION
==PAGE:11==
According to the International Energy Agency (IEA), the building sector is responsible for
around 33% of global energy- and process-related CO2-eq emissions in 2021 [1]. According
to United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP), it is critical to decarbonise the global
buildings sector to prevent catastrophic climate change [2]. To reach the goals of the Paris
Agreement, UNEP states that all new buildings must be net-zero carbon from 2030.
In 2021, the Danish Ministry of Interior and Housing presented an action plan for
sustainability in the building and construction industry called the National Strategy for
Sustainable Construction [3]. Following this new strategy, a change in the Building
Regulations 2018 (BR18) was suggested, which became effective as of the 1st of January
2023. The changes resulted in two new requirements: (1) The climate impact of all new
buildings, regardless of size, must be calculated, and (2) New buildings above 1000 m2 are
required to have a climate impact below 12 kg CO2-eq per m2 per year – a requirement that
will be further tightened in the coming years [4]. These requirements accelerate the need
for competence development within the ﬁeld of Life Cycle Assessments (LCA) and
sustainability across the entire construction industry.
A Danish study from 2020 [5] showed that the greenhouse gas emissions from building
materials are around 2-4 times higher than that of the energy use in the operational phase.
This underlines the importance of reducing the embedded carbon footprint of buildings –
including the load-bearing structures. The new BR18 requirements necessitate structural
engineers to become better informed on the environmental performance of different design
options. However, life cycle assessments are usually not an area of expertise for structural
engineers. Structural optimisation studies seldom investigate the impacts on the carbon
footprint of the entire building which results in a risk of creating a design that is not
environmentally optimal when looking at a full life cycle assessment of the whole building
[6].
To avoid a situation where the construction project is ﬁnished but does not comply with
BR18 requirements, it is essential to implement LCA early in the design process when
optimisation is still possible. Furthermore, if the LCA is introduced too late, the potential
for optimisation is limited or non-existent, and in that case, the LCA will only serve as an
accounting tool. A difference between the early-design LCA and the ﬁnal LCA is described
in the literature as an LCA performance gap [7]. This is caused by lack of information in
BIM models, conducting LCAs as an isolated task, lack of design tools, and the need for a
common framework [8, 9, 10, 11, 12, 13, 14]. To avoid this, a more holistic approach that
involves more stakeholders in the LCA process is necessary. Building Information
Modelling (BIM) can facilitate the integration of LCA into the design process by providing
a platform for multiple stakeholders to collaborate and make informed decisions based on
data-driven analysis. The information needed for conducting an LCA through BIM
integration demands a high level of complexity which could be assisted through
programming and multidisciplinary inputs from LCA specialists, architects, engineers, and
programmers [15]. An interdisciplinary approach has the beneﬁt of targeting
environmental problems from different perspectives and therefore provides more
signiﬁcant opportunities for optimisation already in the early phases of a building project.
In a study by Jusselme et al. (2020), it was found that the majority of respondents believed
that the integration of life cycle objectives into the design process should be a collaboration
between architects and engineers. However, the study found that in more than half of their
projects, architects did not involve engineers in the conceptual design stage [14]. This
indicates that there may be issues with the collaboration of integrating LCA into the
building process.
When dealing with sustainability in the built environment, it is important to break out of
the silo thinking in terms of mindset, processes, and technological solutions [16]. One
2
==PAGE:12==
approach is to work in a cloud-based Common Data Environment (CDE) acting as a single
source of truth [17]. Interoperability between BIM and LCA is based on the concept of
utilising the strengths and capabilities of both concepts. BIM can be used as a tool to reduce
the many assumptions as well as the manual and time-consuming tasks associated with
LCA calculations. When multidisciplinary contributors put an effort into supporting the
process with data or knowledge, the challenges of integrating LCA into the building
process can potentially be overcome [13, 18]. This is also backed up by UNEP (2022) [2]:
To decarbonize the building materials sector, all stakeholders need to take
greater responsibility to understand the environmental impact of their decisions
regarding material selections across the life cycle. Doing so requires getting the
right data to the right stakeholders at consequential stages of decision-making.
This can be aided by using high-level BIM in a common data environment as it enables
multiple stakeholders to work on a shared digital representation of the building and
thereby form a basis for design decisions [19].
This thesis argues that automating the workﬂow between life cycle assessments and
structural analysis of buildings through a cloud-based common data environment enables
structural designers to reduce the LCA performance gap. This thesis develops a toolchain
capable of calculating the climate impact of buildings based on 3D BIM models and
Structural Design and Analysis Software (SDAS) models. The developed toolchain aims to
aid engineers in creating structural designs with reduced carbon footprints. The reduction
of embodied carbon emissions from structures supports the reduction of the overall climate
footprint of the entire building.

2.1
Introduction to Building Information Modelling
According to ISO 19650 part 1, BIM refers to the "use of a shared digital representation of a
built asset to facilitate design, construction and operation processes to form a reliable basis
for decisions" [20].
BIM is a wide term with different levels of collaboration and technology. BIM maturity
levels represent the degree of maturity of the collaborative process between the various
construction stakeholders [21]. The term BIM maturity levels was ﬁrst presented by Mark
Bew and Mervyn Richards [22]. The levels range from level 0 to level 3 and can be
introduced as follows.
Figure 2.1 illustrates the BIM maturity levels adapted from Bew and Richards [23].

Level 0 indicates basically no level of collaboration, and information exists in 2D drawings
with no information stored in the graphic representation of the elements.
Level 1 presents partial collaboration. At this level, 2D and 3D CAD technologies are used
and a document-centric CDE is present in the project for managing shared documents and
information. However, despite the existence of a CDE, 3D models are not shared between
the different design disciplines.

2.2. INTRODUCTION TO LIFE CYCLE ASSESSMENT
Level 2 indicates full collaboration where project data are shared among project members
using a common ﬁle type like the IFC (Industry Foundation Class) format. At this level,
two new dimensions are introduced: 4D and 5D indicating time management and
economic calculations respectively.
Level 3 is the highest BIM Maturity Level indicating full integration of information. The
information sharing takes place in a cloud-based common data environment where all
participants can work on the same model simultaneously. Furthermore, yet another
dimension is added: 6D, which refers to the management of the building’s life cycle. [23].
Another important term within BIM is Level of Development (LOD). The AIA (American
Institute of Architects) deﬁned LOD as describing graphical and informative detail of the
model. The LOD informs you with what accuracy and how detailed the data provided in
the project are elaborated [24]. Table 2.1 below corresponds to the fundamental LOD
deﬁnitions by BIMForum [25].

very small part of LCA principles will be introduced in this thesis which are the parts that

Interoperability of BIM-LCA
Interoperability between BIM and LCA is based on the concept of utilising the strengths
and capabilities of both concepts. A high maturity level of BIM allows multiple
stakeholders to work on a digital model that includes all the information needed to design,
build, and operate the building. LCA is a method for evaluating the environmental
performance of the building over its life cycle. The integration between these tools can
assist in identifying hot spots related to environmental impacts and thus ﬁnding
opportunities and improvements to reduce the environmental impacts.

were different approaches to conducting an LCA. The study found that most companies
used a quantity take-off approach, similar to method one illustrated in ﬁgure 2.2 and that
these approaches were either manual or semi-automated. According to the participating
companies, all of them used LCAbyg as LCA software. However, only one of the
respondents had developed a plug-in solution for the 3D software Revit, that allows for the

dynamic display of LCA results as the user edits the Revit model. All of the companies

Authors Safari and AzariJafari (2021) found that almost 78 % of the investigated BIM-LCA
articles in 2020 used Revit as BIM software [8]. From Dalla Mora et al. (2020) it is found that
Revit is frequently chosen as the BIM modelling software due to the availability of plugins
and APIs, which makes it possible to create tools that can be directly connected and
integrated into the main software [38]. As a result, plugin programs such as Tally,
PLEIADES and OneClickLCA have been developed to facilitate the integration of BIM and
LCA through the plugin solution in Revit.
Safari and AzariJafari (2021) explained that three BIM-LCA integration methods were
mainly used in the literature. The ﬁrst method is a manual mapping between BIM software
and an LCA database, similar to method 1 in ﬁgure 2.2. The second method is similar to
methods 2, 3 and 4 in ﬁgure 2.2, where an API is used to create a plugin or extract data
enabling data from the BIM model to be transferred to an LCA calculation. The third
method is similar to method 5 in ﬁgure 2.2, where LCA data is implemented into the BIM
environment. This is achieved by using the parametric capability of BIM, visual
programming, and by displaying results in a user-friendly environment of BIM. Safari and
AzariJafari (2021) postulates that while these approaches may minimise the need for
manual input in the future, BIM is still used as a ﬁxed database, and the data embedded in
the BIM environment is still limited. Therefore, there is a need for a common and
comprehensive framework to improve interoperability and address the current limitations
of BIM. The study found that over half of the BIM-LCA publications focused on conducting
a life cycle assessment during the early design phase. However, collecting data during this
phase was identiﬁed as problematic.
The increasing focus on BIM-LCA integration has initiated research and comparison
between different BIM-LCA integration tools. Such studies have been done by Alwan et al.
(2021), where the advantages and disadvantages of e.g. Tally and OneClickLCA were
compared. Alwan et al. (2021) address the issue that existing BIM tools have limited access
to LCA databases, making it difﬁcult to conduct an LCA by BIM-LCA integration without
manual mapping. Manual mapping of building materials with environmental data is both
time-consuming and complex. Another aspect which makes the integration between LCA
and BIM difﬁcult is the LOD of the model, which is typically LOD 100 or 200 in the early
design phase and LOD 500 as built. For the LCA to be effective, it needs to be created in the
early stages, where changes can still be made in the project, but Alwan et al. (2021) state
that studies typically cover models with a LOD 300 [39]. However, early-phase models are
often lower than LOD 300, and people might interpret "early" differently, which will be
further discussed in the following section.

In the literature study by Safari and AzariJafari (2021) on the integration of BIM and LCA,

standardisation and harmonisation of the interoperability of BIM and LCA which is one
thing that could signiﬁcantly improve the accuracy of the LCA by ensuring that data and
processes are consistent and compatible.
2.3.3
Need for standardisation and harmonisation
It is evident that several studies point to the need for standardisation and harmonisation of
the BIM-LCA approaches [10][38][9][7] and the BIM model [11] [41].
Authors Roberts et al. (2020) emphasised that standardised reference data for both building
elements and environmental data need to be developed to aid the integration of LCA
within the BIM environment to provide adequate design support at early design phases [7].
Roberts et al. (2020) deﬁne the difference between the impacts from the LCA in the early
phase and the LCA as-built as a performance gap and that there are no means of
addressing the environmental impacts when the building is built [7].
Nilsen and Bohne (2019) used LOD to aid the calculation of the LCA in the early design
phases when detailed data is unavailable [41]. The results showed a considerable
discrepancy between GWP in an early LCA (LOD 200) and the ﬁnal LCA (LOD 350).
Therefore it was concluded that addressing LOD in the early design process will positively
impact the GWP, by enhancing information and detail in the BIM objects. Using generic
values and best practices when the LOD is low was further suggested.

tools that create value for all actors in the digital construction process. The aim is to
strengthen processes and collaboration in the construction process through a variety of
standardised IT tools [42]. Through this collaboration, a guide for deﬁning a part of the

In the BIM2LCA approach presented by Horn et al. (2020) [10], LCA is integrated into BIM

approach entails that while quantities are derived from a BIM model, the LCA data is
provided on a server that is reviewed by experts and can be adjusted without making

2.3.4
Challenges with the interoperability of BIM and LCA
Authors Safari and AzariJafari (2021) argue that the main issue with establishing a
dynamic LCA using BIM integration is hindered by a lack of detailed information on
materials and composition of components in the databases [8]. Some data gaps that were
identiﬁed by Safari and AzariJafari (2021) are (1) the lack of a naming convention between
the LCA database and the existing BIM database, (2) the need for a common modelling
structure to automate data exchange, (3) innovation in the BIM database, (4) development
of regional LCA databases and building materials standards, (5) application of a wider
range of environmental impacts, and (6) integration of hypothetical life-cycle scenarios into
the design.
The inaccuracy and lack of data have been identiﬁed by several studies as one of the
biggest challenges we face in automating BIM-LCA integration [7, 11, 12]. Lack of data in
the models was identiﬁed as problematic in the study by Zimmermann et al. (2021) where
it was stated that this led to poor quality of the quantity take-off [11]. According to a
respondent in the study, certain materials, such as the amount of reinforcement and steel in
plaster walls, are not presented in the 3D models. The respondent also noted that there is a
lack of standardisation in the models in relation to LCA and a lack of a method for quantity
extraction. Additionally, the respondent pointed out that there is a need for inputting
material information and developing models to a sufﬁcient level. DiKon (2022) explains
that the needed information for conducting an LCA is larger than what is represented in
3D models. At the same time, not all 3D material can be used directly for LCA analyses,

Another issue that is presented by Naneva et al. (2020) is the variability between LOD for

modelled as concrete, resulting in the reinforcing steel not being considered in the LCA
[12].

2.3.5
Roles and responsibilities
In a literature review of academic papers, researchers Roberts et al. (2020) investigated
when and how LCA is incorporated into the design process of buildings, and where
improvements are needed. It was demonstrated that LCA is typically separate from the
design process, rather than being an integral part [7]. Roberts et al. (2020) concludes that
one of the main areas of focus should be environmentally-led computational design
because computational design has the ability to guide practitioners towards
environmentally beneﬁcial solutions. However, limited studies have focused on the
capability of LCA being a guiding tool for the design process instead of only an additional
tool.
In a survey by Thais et al. (2022), the goal was to explore options for software developers to
create a framework that more efﬁciently integrates the LCA of the whole building into the
project process. Thais et al. (2022) found that the design team plays a vital role in
communicating decisions with all stakeholders. This communication will be more efﬁcient
when fast or instant results and technical support are attributed through software based on
interdisciplinary inputs [13].
In a survey by authors Jusselme et al. (2020), responses from 500 architects and engineers
showed that practitioners spend more time on the LCA calculation when using computer
software compared to using the rule of thumb or excel spreadsheets [14]. The reason for
this, Jusselme et al. (2020) stated, is that the practitioners prefer to make decisions based on
already acquired knowledge rather than simulations and because the software is not well
adapted to the design process. The study further showed three major obstacles regarding
the gap between the research intensity and its low beneﬁts on the practice; (1) A clear lack
of LCA performance requirements from the clients, (2) A high cost of use and, (3) Major
issues in the integrated design process. According to the study, the issue with the
integrated design process is that architects are not very good at including engineers in their
process. When respondents were asked who should be in charge of integrating life cycle
objectives into the design process, 74 % of the respondents stated that is should be a
collaboration between architects and engineers. However, the study showed that architects
do not involve engineers at the conceptual design stage in more than 54 % of their projects.
Jusselme et al. (2020) argue that engineers ﬁnd it difﬁcult to integrate their technical
knowledge into the design process because the architects have difﬁculties understanding
the technical language and the complexity of the performance of the LCA. The study
further demonstrated that one-third of the engineers do not believe that architects can
comprehend their constraints.
Jusselme et al. (2020) proposed mixed approaches in the communication on the
performance of the LCA between architects and engineers as the need for both quantitative

In approach (a), the architect needs a small amount of knowledge about technical aspects.

the design. However, this may result in the architect rejecting the modiﬁcation of the
design, leading to a second iteration and ultimately increasing the cost of the project.
Approach (b) focuses on both aesthetic and technical aspects simultaneously, with technical
aspects serving as design drivers rather than barriers. This approach is effective when the
hybrid practitioner has extensive knowledge in various technical ﬁelds and expertise in

hybrid practitioner to work in multiple programs, which can be time-consuming and may
not be so suitable during the initial design phase.

calculation software. Klitgaard et al. (2006) argues that one way of promoting
interdisciplinary collaboration in the design process is to use the potential given by various
computer programs [47]. Negendahl (2015) concludes that; “most studies suggest that
mixed design teams, consisting of both engineers and architects, representing the expertise
within their respective disciplinary ﬁelds, will achieve the best performance outcome” [46].

and require manual entries, but an ideal next-generation tool should focus on supporting
data exchange automation and a more collaborative design process [16]. The presentation

It was suggested by Bari et al. (2022) that an ideal next-generation tool should support
benchmarks through databases that are extended with statistical records to allow for
benchmarking [16]. Most benchmarks are developed for sustainability certiﬁcation of
buildings, or to deﬁne a target for the whole building [48], or as a national strategy, like the
new BR18 regulations. Benchmarks like these can support that the environmental impacts
of the building fall within the required values, but cannot identify potentials for
optimisation during the design process. Hollberg et al. (2019) suggested incorporating the
benchmarks within the LCA tools to provide direct feedback to the designers [48]. It was
argued that benchmarks on the element level are necessary for providing a designer with
suggestions for improving the environmental performance of the building through the
choice of different materials. Hollberg et al. (2019) investigated the dual-benchmark
approach combining top-down targets for the whole building and bottom-up targets for
building elements. They found that using a dual benchmark approach can facilitate the use
of LCA-based tools for design support and promote the optimisation of building-related
environmental performance.
The dual benchmark approach has also been considered in a Danish context by
Zimmermann et al. (2020), where different options for choosing benchmarks have been
discussed [5]. The new BR18 regulations are established from a bottom-up approach since
it is based on existing building practices, and the reference value is based on the common
solutions we have today. The baseline is calculated based on the study of the climate
impact of 60 existing case buildings [5].
2.4
Interoperability of SDAS-LCA
With the new requirements in the Danish Building Regulation, two new deliverables will
be essential in all larger building projects: (1) Documentation of the environmental impacts
of the whole building in its entire life cycle, and (2) reduction of impacts in relevant
building elements and processes in order to actually meet the limit values set for the
documentation deliverable. The documentation requirement will demand technological
help as there are currently not enough LCA experts in the industry to meet this new
demand if LCA calculations are performed manually as they currently often are, see

emissions from the materials are around 2-4 times higher than that of the energy use in the
operational phase [5].

Structural optimisation is not a new area of concern emerging with the fairly new interest
in mitigating the embodied carbon of construction projects. Optimising building designs
have been done since the 1970s with a focus on developing efﬁcient and economic designs
[6]. However, structural optimisation studies seldom investigate the impacts on the carbon
footprint of the entire building which results in a risk of creating a building design that may
not be environmentally optimal when looking at a full life cycle of the whole building [6].
The new sustainability requirements for buildings are demanding structural engineers to
become better informed on the environmental performance of different design options [18].
However, life cycle assessments are usually not an area of expertise for a structural
engineer. In order to aid the structural engineers in making informed decisions on design
options, it is necessary to implement information about the environmental impacts of the
structures in the general tools that the engineers use.

2.4. INTEROPERABILITY OF SDAS-LCA
During most bigger construction projects, it is common to use Finite Element Analysis
(FEA) tools to handle structural calculations of complex structures. This could be widely
used software programs like Autodesk’s Robot Structural Analysis [49], Strusoft’s
FEM-Design [50] and CSI’s ETABS [51]. These commercial structural design and analysis
software (SDAS) does not currently provide a direct and real-time linkage with LCA
calculations in the user interface and therefore do not offer any direct guidance to the
structural engineer on environmental footprint. This indicates a need for integration
between structural analysis and LCA calculations, which is also supported by researchers
Oti and Tizani (2015); “For the structural engineer, tools dedicated to depicting
sustainability to inform design decisions on options are generally lacking.” [18]. Authors
Ching and Carstensen (2022) also state: “There is an increasing need for automated design
processes that can help guide structural design towards lower embodied carbon solutions.”
[52].
2.4.2
State-of-the-art
Multiple researchers have investigated different ways of optimising structures on the basis
of environmental impact. In the articles by Eleftheriadis et al. (2018), researchers
introduced a BIM-enabled framework which could effectively integrate the assessment of
optimised structural designs based on life cycle concepts at building level [6, 53]. They
developed an optimisation algorithm in C# using the Autodesk Robot Structural Analysis
API which interacts with BIM models that can provide the building data to the Finite
Element Model (FEM). The created algorithm was a modiﬁed version of the multi-objective
optimisation NSGA-II algorithm and was based on two objective functions that involve
embodied carbon emissions and cost of the structure. This was done to explore optimal
design solutions considering both economic and environmental aspects. The study focused
mainly on reinforced concrete slabs and columns and investigated the optimisation of the
structural layout, the slab and column sizes, and the reinforcement detailing of the
elements in relation to an already existing design.
The case study found that "...the embodied carbon emissions of the structure could be
reduced by approximately 16-19% through the effective design optimisation of the
structural ﬂoors and columns." Furthermore, the studies found that the topology of the
structural grid seemed to have the largest impact on the cost and carbon performance [53].
The study concluded that the structural engineers should consult design teams in the early
design stages, as the structural system has a high impact on the ﬁnal life cycle assessment
of the whole building.
In a journal article by Oti and Tizani (2015), researchers aimed to assess the sustainability of
structural design options by using process and data modelling techniques [18]. An
integrated framework was made to depict the sustainability of the conceptual design of a
steel-framed building. The framework was developed by the use of object-based modelling
techniques in C#.NET environment to create a sustainability assessment model.
Statistic-based algorithms like Monte Carlo applications and multi-criteria decision
analysis were used to explore what-if-scenarios of material combinations and to perform
the risk and sensitivity analysis. The prototype was then connected with the BIM tool Revit
Structures to run as an add-in tool allowing the designer to call the program during the
structural modelling of the building.
The authors’ suggestion for further work includes extending the framework to other
existing BIM platforms.
In a journal article, authors Zenísek et al. (2020) investigated multi-criteria optimisation of
load-bearing reinforced concrete (RC) structures focusing on economical- and
environmental aspects as well as structural performance [54]. The main purpose of the
article was to investigate whether low, medium or high-strength concrete is the most
economically and environmentally favourable for the construction of load-bearing RC
18
==PAGE:28==
2.4. INTEROPERABILITY OF SDAS-LCA
structures. They created an optimisation program in Visual Basics for Application in MS
Excel and connected it to the structural analysis program RFEM [55], which was chosen
due to its programmable interface. The optimisation was done by supplying the program
with different values within three variables: (1) Column cross section, (2) ﬂoor slab
thickness, and (3) concrete strength class. The environmental impacts of the different
designs were analysed using the life cycle assessment method according to ISO 14040 and
ISO 14044. The environmental datasets were based on the thinkstep database and
calculated using the Gabi Software.
The focus was to optimise the particular structure stated in the paper with regard to
concrete strengths and their environmental- and economic impacts. The paper concluded
that high-strength concrete, which is usually neglected, performs better in relation to
construction cost and environmental impact for certain structure types. The optimisation
performed in the paper focused on already deﬁned structural variables and does not
connect the FE-model to a 3D model nor connect automatically to LCA calculations.
Authors Ching and Carstensen (2022) investigated topology optimisation of truss
structures with two materials. This was done for a range of objectives and constraints
including embodied carbon [52]. The researchers drew inspiration from continuum
topology optimisation to formulate a problem with a minimum compliance objective. Two
different constraints were used in the optimisation process: (1) a weight constraint, and (2)
a GWP constraint. The different designs were made in MATLAB using the nonlinear
problem solver fmincon as the gradient-based optimiser. The framework developed in this
research allows designers to automatically generate topology-optimised truss designs that
incorporate two different material systems. The embodied carbon of the structures was
only calculated for the ﬁrst life cycle stages A1-A3. The technique proposed in the paper
contributes by providing designers with a way to automatically generate
topology-optimised truss designs using GWP as a design constraint. However, the
approach for calculating the embodied carbon emissions of a structure does not give a full
and equitable result as the entire life cycle is not considered which may result in burden
shifting.
Commercial companies are also investigating how to incorporate environmental data in
structural calculations. The Swedish software company Strusoft has launched a web
application called FEM-Climate [56], which calculates the CO2-eq emissions from a
FEM-Design model. The climate footprint is based on the Swedish climate database
Boverket and covers life cycle phases A1-A5. A FEM-Design struXML ﬁle is uploaded to
the web application where the structures are mapped to relevant materials from the climate
database. The user can see and edit the mapping before downloading the results ﬁle as an
XLSX ﬁle. FEM-Climate is a simple application allowing structural engineers to estimate
the carbon emissions of a FEM-Design ﬁle. It is transparent and easy to use, which is an
obvious advantage for structural engineers with none to little experience with life cycle
assessments. However, it only covers phases A1-A5 and is based on Swedish
environmental data meaning it is not very comprehensive and does not comply with the
requirements in BR18. Furthermore, this method is a very static approach and is not
directly linked to a dynamic FEM-Design model, which allows for ﬁle version errors.
Furthermore, the results are downloaded and stored locally on the user’s computer having
no connection to other platforms, programs, or project stakeholders.
In the highlighted articles, different approaches have been taken in an attempt to evaluate
and optimise structures based on carbon emissions. Some articles have mostly focused on
performing comparative life cycle assessments of a deﬁned structural concept as seen in [6,
52, 53, 54], which is important research for collecting knowledge on which parameters
inﬂuences the environmental performance of building structures. However, this approach
is not necessarily easy to upscale and make usable for structural engineers in everyday
19
==PAGE:29==

design and project work. Only one of the highlighted articles [18] focused on the process of
developing a framework for sustainable assessment of structures through integration
between a BIM-based structural analysis tool and material databases including cost and life
cycle information. If we want to build more sustainably, it is important to keep a holistic
approach. This applies to many different areas of the design process. One of the important
topics is the life cycle stages included in the LCA. In the articles [6, 52, 54], the analysed life
cycle stages only cover the product and construction stages. When performing an isolated
comparative analysis to evaluate the environmental performance of e.g. the structural
system, material choice for an element or detailed concrete/reinforcement design, it might
seem natural to only cover the production phase. However, this only presents the impacts
related to cradle-to-gate and does not cover the entire life cycle of the analysed elements.
This can result in burden shifting and a misinterpretation of the different analysed options.
Furthermore, by analysing the structural elements as an isolated topic, it is not investigated
how the structural design choices are affecting other parts of the building and its life cycle.
An example is how the material choices of the structure can impact the building’s energy
demand due to heat accumulation of the structure [57].
It is not a novel idea to perform multi-criteria optimisations of structures as seen in the
described literature. There are multiple ways of approaching the topic of environmental
optimisation of structures through a BIM-enabled framework. Sustainability, structural
analysis, and information technology are all individually vast subjects and the
interconnection between them is an important but complicated topic. When dealing with
the topic of sustainability in the built environment, it is important to break out of the silo
thinking in terms of mindset, processes, and technological solutions [16]. One approach is
to work in a cloud-based common data environment acting as a single source of truth.
2.5
Cloud-based Common Data Environment
To enhance the sustainability of construction projects, it has been argued that the
integration of BIM results in multiple beneﬁts. Researchers Olawumi and Chan (2018) have
ranked 36 beneﬁts of the integration of BIM and sustainability practices in construction
projects. Some of the highest-ranked beneﬁts are: facilitate sharing, exchange, and
management of project information and data (ranked nr. 6), facilitate accurate geometrical
representations of a building in an integrated data environment (ranked nr. 8), and
real-time sustainable design and analysis early in the design phase (ranked nr. 9). The
highlighted beneﬁts all underlines the relevance of this thesis.
In order to utilise the many possible advantages of BIM, multiple criteria need to be met. In
section 2.1, the terms BIM Maturity levels and Level of Development were introduced.
Going towards BIM maturity level 3, there is an emerging focus on the development of the
common data environment which is seen as the next evolution of BIM. A CDE is a
collaborative platform based on the idea of linking a collaborative project space with cloud
technology [19]. The CDE provides project stakeholders with a centralised platform for
collecting, managing, evaluating and sharing information. The primary task of the
platform is to provide a place for information exchange as well as ensure a consistent data
model [58]. As described by Preidel et al. (2018), using a CDE can result in a higher rate of
reusability of information as well as serve as a central archive for documentation. By using
a cloud-based CDE with full integration and interoperability of information, the quality of
the building project could be greatly enhanced. However, the BIM Maturity levels and
CDE mainly deals with the collaboration between stakeholders and the managing of
information during the life cycle of the building. The maturity level does thereby not
necessarily indicate how detailed a model is. This is where the LOD becomes important as
described in section 2.3. Making the models more detailed enables more accurate
estimations of the environmental performance of the buildings.

2.5. CLOUD-BASED COMMON DATA ENVIRONMENT
The international standard series ISO 19650 1-4 [20] deﬁnes a common uniﬁed framework
for BIM-centred collaboration and information management related to built assets
throughout their life cycle. One of the main recommendations of the standard is the use of
a common data environment for information management. Figure 2.8 simplistically
illustrates the information ﬂows when taking a traditional approach and an integrated
approach respectively. The traditional information ﬂow is a complex system where the
different project stakeholders and participants typically exchange information manually,
making it difﬁcult to ensure that the right information is with the right people at the right
time. The traditional information exchange can easily lead to audit control errors and
duplicate work which can be costly and time-consuming. Furthermore, different
stakeholders risk working on different bases which results in discrepancies in the project
material. The integrated approach adopts the use of a CDE. The general vision of the CDE
is to make the information ﬂow simpler by the use of a central repository that stores all
relevant and up-to-date project information. With a CDE, every project member should
have access to the same information (if they are approved for that speciﬁc information)
meaning that e.g. the structural engineer designs on the same basis as the mechanical
engineer [20].

The literature review disclosed a gap in LCA performance, reﬂecting a discrepancy
between the environmental impacts identiﬁed in the early LCA and those identiﬁed in the
ﬁnal LCA. This issue is attributed to various factors, which will be further discussed in the
following.
It was emphasised through the literature study, that integration of BIM and LCA could
facilitate faster and more accurate LCAs. However, it was pointed out that the required
information is not always available in the models at the desired phase of the project. In
ﬁgure 2.10, the ability to make changes in the project in relation to the cost of the changes is
shown through the building phases. The early stages of a project often involve numerous
uncertainties and unmade decisions, which means that the cost of making changes is
relatively low at this point. As the project progresses, however, certain constructions and
materials become more ﬁxed, making it more expensive to make changes later on.

In the early stages of the BIM model, speciﬁc building components and detailed material
can only be approximated using generic data, and the model typically has a low LOD. An
issue related to this is that current approaches are not adequate to account for un-modelled
or complex construction elements. In order to accommodate for the incomplete building
information modelling, there is a need for a common and comprehensive framework to
improve interoperability and address the current limitations of BIM.
Often, the work around a life cycle assessment of a building is handled by a sustainability
specialist who documents the environmental performance of the building. However, the
sustainability specialist might not have in-depth knowledge about everything and may
base some choices on assumptions, especially in the early design phases. This can lead to
inaccurate initial environmental impact results. If some of the work relating to
environmental performance instead fell to the disciplines whom it concerns, it could lead
to fewer mistakes. To accommodate this, there is a need for greater collaboration between
the various stakeholders in the design process, and environmental aspects should concern
all disciplines. This does not remove the time needed to perform an LCA, but it relocates
the tasks to someone with better conditions to solve them. An example of this is
un-modelled reinforcement in a BIM model. A sustainability specialist might not have the
right competencies to judge a relevant assumption for reinforcement in concrete structures,
whereas a structural engineer has a more solid foundation on which to base the

3.1. PROJECT DESCRIPTION
In the preceding chapters, the topics of LCA, BIM, SDAS and how they are currently
connected, have been investigated. The aims and objectives of this thesis are based on the
knowledge obtained in chapter 2. Through the literature review, the main research gaps
have been identiﬁed. The following research gaps are a short recap of section 2.6 and will
form the basis of the problem statement.
As described in the introduction, the construction industry accounts for a signiﬁcant
amount of the total global carbon emissions [1]. This underlines the importance of
reducing the embedded carbon footprint of buildings by engaging the right stakeholders at
the right time. The early stages of a building project offer a greater potential for
incorporating changes and thereby reducing the carbon footprint. However, the literature
study revealed a discrepancy between early-design LCA and the ﬁnal which is described as
an LCA performance gap. The main causes for the LCA performance gap can be
summarised in the following arguments:
• The lack of information in BIM models reduces the possibility to perform accurate
LCAs.
• Conducting LCAs is often an isolated task performed by only one project participant,
even though it is the decisions made by all disciplines that inﬂuence the LCA results.
• There is a lack of design tools for structural engineers that introduce environmental
aspects to the design process.
• There is a need for sustainability to be assessed more holistically through an
object-centric common data environment.
• There is a need for a common framework to improve interoperability between BIM,
SDAS and LCA.
3.1
Project description
The main objective of this thesis is to investigate how structural engineers can make more
informed decisions on their structural design based on environmental footprint and
especially climate impact. The general idea is, that by creating a two-way information
exchange between BIM models, SDAS models, and LCA calculations connected to a
common data environment, an optimised workﬂow of structural design and real-time
feedback on environmental performance can reduce the embodied carbon footprint of
structures.
The new BR18 requirements push engineering consultants to focus much more on the
entire life cycle of the building. This study can potentially help save companies time and
thereby money through the automation between structural design and LCA calculations.
Furthermore, the reduction of embodied CO2-eq emissions from structures aids in
reducing the overall climate footprint of the entire building.
This master’s thesis aims to automate the workﬂow between life cycle assessments and
structural analysis of buildings through a cloud-based common data environment.
3.2
Problem statement
Based on the project description, the following problem statement has been deﬁned:
How can engineers make structural design decisions based on environmental
performance through an automated workﬂow between structural analysis and LCA
calculations in a cloud-based common data environment?
To assist in answering the main problem statement, the following tasks will be carried out:

A toolchain is created to aid structural engineers in making design decisions based on
climate impact. The toolchain intends to automate the workﬂow between BIM, SDAS, and
LCA calculations. This toolchain is a part of a CDE platform, as explained in chapter 3,
utilising its microservice architecture. Two microservices are developed. One which
calculates the LCA performance and returns the results to a database based on object
information obtained from Revit and FEM-Design. The second microservice runs
parametric studies based on object information from FEM-Design, by utilising the
FEM-Design API.
Four main tasks are carried out:
1. Creation of a parser which extracts and serialises object data related to structural
elements from Revit (Revit Structural Elements Parser).
2. Creation of a parser which extracts and serialises object data related to structural
elements from FEM-Design (FEM-Design Structural Elements Parser).
3. Creating a parametric study by utilising the FEM-Design API (Parametric Study
Microservice)
4. Creating an LCA calculator based on the serialised object data (LCA Microservice).
Figure 4.1 illustrates the system architecture of the described toolchain. The details of the
different tasks of the service will be elaborated on in the subsequent sections of this chapter.

Introduction to the Revit API
Autodesk Revit provides a .NET API that can automate repetitive tasks, extend the core

Windows operating system. The Revit API allows for creating software plug-ins in form of

the command is executed by a user it is then loaded into Revit. Revit .NET API allows
programming with any .NET compliant language including VB.NET, C#, and C++.
C# is an object-oriented programming language, which means that it is based on objects
containing data and code. Object-oriented programming revolves around deﬁning the
rules for the kinds of interactions their objects have [62]. When using C# it takes advantage
of the underlying .NET Framework, which makes it a good choice for programming within
the Revit API. In this project C# was used as the programming language for working within
the Revit API.

4.5.1
Introduction to Python-Flask framework
Flask is a micro web application framework written in the programming language Python.
Flask comes with a solid core with the basic services needed for creating a web application
with the main dependencies being (1) the routing, debugging and Web Server Gateway
Interface, (2) the template support, and (3) the command-line integration. All other key
services, like e.g., accessing databases, are provided through extensions that integrate with
the core packages [65].
The Python-Flask framework supports the creation of RESTful API endpoints. An API is a
set of rules that enable communication between different applications. RESTful APIs, in
particular, conform to the architectural style of Representational State Transfer (REST). The
REST architecture is widely used because it is ﬂexible and follows the client-server model
of the World Wide Web [65].
A RESTful API consists of requests sent from client to server and responses received from
server to client, see ﬁgure 4.14. The main request methods used are the HTTP operations:
GET, POST, PUT, and DELETE. The data exchanged between the client and server can be in

4.5.3
LCA data library
Environmental data
One of the core foundations of a reliable LCA is the environmental data input, as this data
is one of the main determining factors of the outcome of the environmental performance.
For setting up the LCA calculation service, an environmental data library needs to be
established. Environmental data for construction materials can be found on many different
platforms such as ÖKOBAUDAT [67], ECO Platform [68] and EPD Danmark [69].
The Danish Building Regulations (BR18) §297 item 5 dictates that for the calculation of the
climate impact from materials, appendix 2 table 7 (BR18) ’Generisk Grundlag’ [64] should
be used and can potentially be combined with data from relevant and valid product
speciﬁc or industry-speciﬁc EPDs. Table 7 (BR18) is based on a combination of generic data
from the German database ÖKOBAUDAT and Danish industry EPDs from EPD Danmark
47
==PAGE:57==
4.5. LCA MICROSERVICE
and consists of around 460 environmental datasets. As of January 2023, table 7 (BR18) only
contains information on GWP and can therefore only be used for information on climate
impact as currently required in the Building Regulations. Applying table 7 (BR18) makes it
relatively easy for users to navigate the environmental data, as there are not too many data
entries, and the authority already approves the data.
To comply with the Building Regulations, it was chosen to use table 7 (BR18) as the
environmental data library for the microservice. The table is read as a CSV ﬁle in the
Python script and can easily be modiﬁed if the user wants to add speciﬁc EPDs.
Life cycle phases
Following the rules of BR18 §297 item 2, the life cycle phases to be included in the LCA
microservice are A1-A3, B4, B6, C3-C4, and D. In section 2.2, table 2.3 shows all life cycle
phases, where phases marked with bold indicates the requirements for the LCA on climate
impacts according to the BR18.
Since the focus of this service is based on the impacts from material use, B6 is not included
in the microservice. Referring to the requirements by BR18, the climate impact should be
calculated during a 50 years period. This means, that phase B4: Replacement is furthermore
not relevant for this speciﬁc service, as load-bearing structures made in concrete, timber
and steel all have a lifespan above 50 years as given in BUILD’s Lifespan table [70].

4.5.5
Mapping the LCA data to the structural elements
As the included building parts have been deﬁned and the environmental data library is
determined, the mapping between them is carried out. The speciﬁc products used for
different elements in different buildings will always vary. This means that to automate the
process of LCA calculations, it is necessary to make qualiﬁed assumptions and use generic
data. The choices made in this project for the mapping between structural elements and
corresponding environmental data are described in this section.
Ready-mixed concrete
Ready-mixed concrete is a relevant building material for all of the structural elements
classes, as concrete is widely used in the Danish construction industry. The microservice
only covers concrete strengths that are within the normal-strength classes (12-50MPa). The
instances of ready-mixed concrete in table 7 (BR18) are all based on industry data based on
information from manufacturers of around 80% of the total Danish production of
ready-mixed concrete. For most of the strength classes, there are different choices for
environmental data. The choices made for each strength class will be described in the
following and can be seen in table 4.2.
C12/15, and C16/20 are the lowest concrete strengths that are normally used. Table 7
(BR18) does not provide any environmental data for these two strength classes meaning
that they are mapped with the same data as C20/25, which is the next lowest class. There is
only one option of environmental data for C20/25 which is dataset #B1425 which covers
Self Compacting Concrete (SCC) and is applicable for elements categorised in passive
environmental class.
For C25/30 there is only one option. This is dataset #B1433 which covers normal concrete
and is applicable to elements categorised in passive environmental class.
For C30/37 there is only one option. This is dataset #B1477 which covers normal concrete
and is applicable to elements categorised in moderate environmental class.
For C35/45 there is a choice of either CEM I SR-5, CEM I, or grouting concrete - all of which
are made for aggressive environmental class. Another choice for C35/45 is the Self
Compacting Concrete. The EPD which covers SCC is applicable to moderate
environmental class. As the moderate environmental class is common for the structural
elements covered in this microservice, dataset #B1485 is mapped with elements modelled
with concrete strength C35/45.
C40/50 has two options, either CEM I SR-5 or CEM I, which are both applicable to extra
aggressive environmental class. As CEM I SR-5 is usually used for bigger civil construction
works, CEM I #B1461 is mapped with strength C40/50.

The Results microservice is mainly a part of a front-end solution which is not within the

building types.
4.7
Parametric Study Microservice
Many decisions impacting building performance, are made during the early stages of the
design process, often with only a limited amount of information. In order to make qualiﬁed
choices that are based on multiple factors (e.g. GWP and structural performance), it is
necessary to thoroughly evaluate multiple options at a detailed level. One way that
engineers can consider environmental performance in their structural design decisions is
through the use of parametric studies, which will be described in the following section.
Parametric design is a subset of computational design, where parameters and rules are
applied in order to create different design solutions [72]. A parametric study is ideal for
identifying the environmental impacts of structural elements because it allows for
exploring multiple options based on predetermined parameters.
In this project, the parametric studies are created within the FEM-Design API in the
programming language C#. The auto design function of FEM-Design is utilised to
investigate the performance of the structural elements. The system architecture of the
parametric study microservice is illustrated in ﬁgure 4.26. The idea of the Parametric study
microservice is that it utilises the LCA microservice for calculating LCA and sends the
results back to FEM-Design.

4.7.1
Reinforced concrete deck
This parametric study is able to investigate a reinforced concrete deck by varying the
thickness and strength of the deck.
The structure of the created script is similar to the FEM-Design Structural Elements Parser
in the way that it is running with the use of struXML ﬁles, FdScripts, and BSC ﬁles, see
section 4.3. The outputs consist of quantity estimation of concrete and reinforcement, and
RC shell utilisation. The GWP value of the different concrete strengths and reinforcements
is implemented into the script. The script runs two loops where one is embedded into the
other. The outer loop will loop through the concrete strengths and assign the concrete
strength parameter to the deck and the inner loop will loop over the interval of thicknesses
and assign a thickness to the deck. Then, the deck is analysed and the GWP is calculated
from the analysis results.
In the following example, the parametric study is set up for a deck with a width of 4 m and
a length of 12 m. The deck is loaded with an 8 kN/m2 surface load on the entire deck and
supported by line supports on each end, see ﬁgure 4.27.

stirrups according to their user-deﬁned design parameters. Initial reinforcement

deck is required or in situations where the element requires signiﬁcant reinforcement.

Columns
This parametric study is able to investigate the inﬂuence of using different strength classes,

The structure of the created script for the parametric study of columns is also similar to the

cross-section to the column, according to the material. The output from the analysis of the
column is utilised for calculating GWP.

cross-sections are restrained to rectangular concrete cross-sections (C20/25), steel IPE

4.7. PARAMETRIC STUDY MICROSERVICE
The FEM-Design’s auto-design function can give a good indication of the most suitable
cross-section or amount of reinforcement, but this is only in relation to load-bearing
performance. The environmental performance is dependent on the quantity, however, a
larger proﬁle (increased volume or weight) does not necessarily result in a better
load-bearing capacity. The aim of the parametric studies was therefore to give valuable
inputs to the design of structural elements that contains information on both load-bearing
capacity and environmental performance.
The issue with using the auto-design function in FEM-Design is that some parameters
cannot be auto-designed. For example, when auto-designing with either timber design or
steel design, the FEM-Design auto-design function ﬁnds a suitable cross-section from a
range of different cross-sections, but it cannot vary between different steel or timber
qualities. This means that the variation between different qualities is still a manual task of
modifying it in the struXML ﬁle. It should further be noted that while the auto-design
function of FEM-Design can give a good indication of size and material, it is crucial that the
user understands and are able to interpret the results.

5.5
Remarks on the tests
The presented test cases show only inconsiderable deviations between the derived
quantities from the developed structural elements parsers and the software quantity
schedules, as well as between the GWP results from the LCA microservice and the
manually calculated results. This indicates that the developed toolchain performs as
intended. However, obstacles have emerged doing the testing of the toolchain. The main
obstacles are highlighted in the current section.
The developed LCA microservice runs on object input structured as JSON ﬁles meaning
that it accepts information from all types of 3D software, as long as it follows the general
information requirements and class hierarchy as described in chapter 4. However, working
with JSON ﬁles extracted from Revit and FEM-Design respectively means that inputs like
"Material" and "Quality" are not necessarily described in the same manner. An example of
this is the concrete quality which is described as for example "C25/30" in FEM-Design but
for some elements in Revit are described as "Concrete, C25/30". This is because some
elements in Revit are system-families and therefore cannot have Material for Model
Behaviour assigned to them, this was also described in section 4.2.4. Therefore, the
LCA-script have to embrace multiple ways of describing the same material. If more
software were to be connected to the service, it would be necessary to adapt the LCA
microservice further according to the way material and quality are assigned in the speciﬁc
software added to the toolchain.
In Revit, the user has a lot of freedom regarding how they model the objects. For example,
many companies will create their own material library, which might not match the material
deﬁnitions in the developed toolchain. Furthermore, it might not be customary to include
all the LCA-necessary material information in a 3D model – especially not in the early
design phases. This is problematic when trying to automate the process of mapping 3D
68
==PAGE:78==
5.5. REMARKS ON THE TESTS
objects with LCA data. The contrary to this is the material-assigning for objects in
FEM-Design. One of the main advantages of obtaining LCA information based on an SDAS
like FEM-Design is that the core of these models is the structural design calculations which
naturally require a speciﬁcation of the structural elements’ material properties. This results
in more precise object information regarding structural elements, as the FE model is the
basis for the structural design and should be accurate to what will be built.
There are, however, some obstacles associated with the use of FEM-Design. The toolchain
utilises the quantity estimation schedules from the FEM-Design software. This means that
the quantities and materials are not necessarily organised in a way that is meaningful in
relation to mapping the quantities with LCA data. Moreover, some quantities like ﬁre
protection material are not included in these quantity schedules in FEM-Design, meaning
that ﬁre protection is currently not covered by the developed toolchain.
The main goal of this toolchain is to automate the process of LCA calculations based on
data that should already be available in models. However, when the data is unavailable in
the models, the user must manually specify it. This could be an architect that has designed
concrete beams but not the details of the concrete strength and the reinforcement ratio
which is of course not the job of the architect. It is, however, the job of a structural engineer,
which might have speciﬁed the element details on a general note on a drawing instead of
applying this information directly to the 3D objects. In this case, the person responsible for
the LCA must ﬁnd the information, which already exists, somewhere else and apply it in
the right way to make the toolchain run as intended. An example of this is the data
enrichment done to Revit test model 1 - Building 118. The necessary information was
unavailable in the original 3D model, meaning the building needed to be enriched with
material and quality data. The biggest problem with the model deﬁciency was the lack of
concrete strengths and reinforcement, which, if modelled incorrectly, can cause signiﬁcant
deviations in GWP from the ﬁnished building. In section 2.3 it was described that the
amount of reinforcement is often not modelled in the Revit 3D model. Reinforcement is
typically modelled at LOD 400, but early LCAs are usually conducted at lower LODs. It
would support the LCA to specify that some building components require a higher LOD,
delivered by the structural engineer, as suggested by DiKon [43].

6.1. CONTRIBUTIONS
The aims of this master’s thesis were to automate the workﬂow between life cycle
assessments and structural analysis of buildings through a cloud-based common data
environment. This was achieved by creating a toolchain that is capable of calculating the
climate impact from a structural model. The toolchain intends to aid structural engineers in
creating structural designs with reduced carbon footprints. This will contribute to reducing
the inconsistent results between early and ﬁnal LCA described as an LCA performance
gap. In the following, the contributions from this thesis will be highlighted, followed by a
discussion of the limitations, and lastly, suggestions for future development of the tool will
be stated.
6.1
Contributions
We developed a toolchain which automates the processes of calculating LCAs for structures
based on BIM and SDAS models making many manual procedures redundant. This can
save labour hours, making more time for parametric studies and deeper investigations of
how different design choices performs based on multiple criteria, including GWP.
We developed an open-source class hierarchy for structural elements based on the
information needed to perform a life cycle assessment according to the Danish Building
Regulations. The class hierarchy was developed as a common way to export data from
proprietary BIM and SDAS formats into an object-oriented database which means that the
class hierarchy can be used as a foundation for connecting additional software other than
Revit and FEM-Design to the toolchain.
Using this class hierarchy, we created a tool which parses structural elements and their
LCA-relevant parameters from a FEM-Design 3D model to a CDE. Additionally, we created
a similar tool which parses structural elements and their LCA-relevant parameters from a
Revit 3D model to a CDE.
We created microservices which calculate and display the GWP of structural elements. The
microservices run on open formats meaning the inputs could be based on theoretically any
relevant software providing that the elements have been serialised to a JSON conforming
to the right data structure.
Furthermore, we exempliﬁed how the developed parametric study in FEM-Design can
assist structural engineers in reducing the carbon footprint of their designs, thereby
providing insights into how structural engineers can work more actively to reduce the
environmental impacts of the structural design.
The developed toolchain is based on microservice architecture for easy and beneﬁcial
implementation in a common data environment opening the opportunity for conducting a
comprehensive life cycle assessment of the built asset. In chapter 2, it was mentioned, that
the studies on carbon-optimisation of structures lacked a broader connection and
integration to the other discipline teams and the building as a whole. When analysing the
structures on their own, it might be possible to identify a carbon-reduced structural design
of different options, but how this option impacts the rest of the building is not being
addressed. It can be dangerous to divide the LCA effort into discipline silos as this
excludes a holistic approach and thereby risking that efforts to reduce the impacts from one
discipline will lead to increasing the impacts from another. To accomplish collaboration on
a higher level it is suggested to achieve BIM Maturity Level 3, where integrated
collaboration takes place in a cloud-based and object-oriented CDE. The purpose of this is
to provide a platform where all stakeholders have access to a single source of truth. When
BIM data is centralised and interconnected, there is an opportunity to thoroughly
investigate the environmental impacts from a holistic life cycle perspective while not
burden shifting through the different disciplines.

6.2. DISCUSSION OF INTEROPERABILITY
6.2
Discussion of interoperability
Integration between BIM and LCA shows great potential for reducing errors and reducing
the time spent on LCA calculations. Furthermore, the integration between SDAS and LCA
shows great potential for actively designing carbon-reduced structures. However,
interoperability issues between BIM/SDAS and LCA have been identiﬁed through the
work of this thesis. Some issues can be classiﬁed as a question of technology while others
can be classiﬁed as a question of human behaviour.
One of the main issues of automating the processes between BIM/SDAS and LCA
calculations is the unsystematic ways of modelling object information in a building model.
When there are no clear rules of what information should be stored, and in which way it
should be stored, automation becomes challenging and might not have the desired beneﬁts
of saving time and reducing errors. This problem can be seen as related to human
behaviour and can be very difﬁcult to act on. When there are no set rules for how to
implement the information in the BIM objects, it becomes difﬁcult to write an intelligent
script for mapping the objects to environmental data automatically as materials could be
stored under an unlimited number of names and combinations. A way to address this
problem is to develop a framework with a set of clear rules and deﬁnitions on how to
create LCA-ready BIM models. This problem has also been addressed in the literature,
where the need for a common and comprehensive framework has been requested as
described in chapter 2. The unsystematic ways of modelling can be aided by specifying the
requirements for the object information in the building model. This could be done in
relation to LOD and LOI as suggested in section 2.3.3. In addition, it is necessary to have an
understanding of what information is needed to be able to create as accurate an LCA as
possible. In table 6.1, examples of what information should be available for structural BIM
objects, are provided.

phases and impact categories. The developed tool incorporates only the LCA phases and
impact categories required by BR18. None of the eight tools presented in table 2.4, runs as a
fully automated tool. Some of the tools offer a semi-automated interoperability between
BIM and LCA but require a user to map environmental data with geometry. This project
succeeded in creating a toolchain capable of automating the workﬂow between LCA
calculations and structural models. It can be adapted to suit a given project, but it is at
present only adapted to the materials and constructions described in chapter 4.
FEM-Design and Revit have been investigated as 3D modelling software, and some
advantages of using one of the software over the other have been discovered. For instance,
Revit can give a more realistic picture of volume because joints are modelled more
precisely, whereas FEM-Design models from centre to centre. On the contrary, FEM-Design
has an advantage as it already has a ruleset for the materials deﬁned in the software. As
structural calculations and detailed design require a speciﬁcation of elements’ size and
material, objects in structural analysis and design software contain, by default, the main
information necessary to calculate the climate impact of the elements. This is very useful
when automating the mapping between elements and the corresponding environmental
data.
6.3.2
Need for manual mapping
The created toolchain includes the main elements for calculating the GWP of load-bearing
structures. However, some important details are currently omitted as it was not within the
scope of the project to include all possible types of building elements and materials but
rather to investigate the possibilities related to extracting relevant parameters for LCA. An
example of excluded building parts is the ﬁre protection of the structural elements. Taking
the classic building materials steel, concrete, and timber, the ﬁre treatment of these differs
signiﬁcantly. For concrete, there is usually no need to treat the elements with any ﬁre
protection as the elements are mostly ﬁre-designed with the "reduced cross-section"
method. Steel is on the other hand extremely sensitive to ﬁre, and would usually be
protected with either ﬁre-retardant paint or insulation in the form of e.g. ﬁre-resistant
gypsum boards or stone wool plates. For wood structures, ﬁre protection can, like steel, be
in the form of ﬁre-resistant plates or insulation, or ﬁre-retardant paint. In FEM-Design it is
possible to calculate the amount and material of the ﬁre protection material. However, it is
yet not possible to extract the quantities of the used ﬁre protection material through the
FEM-Design API. For this reason, it is not yet a part of the toolchain. Other examples of
omitted building parts are TT-decks, wooden cassette elements, and other types of
prefabricated elements. Some building materials have likewise not been included in the
toolchain including masonry and light aggregate concrete. This should preferably be
included if the tool is to be developed further.

6.3. DISCUSSION OF THE DEVELOPED TOOLCHAIN
As this toolchain has automated the mapping between structural elements and
corresponding LCA data, the user has no option to choose between alternatives within a
material category. An example of this is the use of construction wood. In table 7 (BR18), it
is possible to choose from construction wood made from coniferous tree or pine and spruce
tree. Additionally, it is possible to choose between the treatment methods sawn and planed
wood. However, these alternatives are not available when using the developed toolchain.
The choices made in the toolchain regarding environmental data are either based on the
materials that are commonly used or are the most conservative materials. However, when
conducting a ﬁnal LCA, it is important to consider which materials and treatment methods
are used as there is a signiﬁcant difference in the GWP.
In the early design phases, LOD and LOI might be low, meaning that the necessary data for
the LCA calculations is unavailable. A way to address this problem could be to adjust the
tool to accept objects that do not comply with the ruleset for the object class by ﬁlling out
the necessary details with a set of predeﬁned generic data. However, auto-designing BIM
objects in the manner described here can lead to modelling errors. To avoid these errors,
manual mapping of structural elements and materials should be enabled. This is especially
crucial for the ﬁnal LCA, as it requires a detailed assessment of the climate impact. It is,
therefore, essential to provide users with the ability to control how information is assigned
to an object. However, providing manual mapping slows down the process, which is not
ideal. On the other hand, the process would be more transparent by excluding a ’black-box’
automated solution. A solution could be to include an option for manual mapping of
elements where the toolchain cannot classify the material. This would reduce the
requirements for the preparation of the BIM model.
6.3.3
Limitations due to using table 7 (BR18)
The tool is limited by the choice of using table 7 (BR18) because there are some materials
which are not included, e.g. low- and high-strength concrete, as described in section 4.5.5.
The tool maps low-strength concrete with the lowest available strength in table 7 (BR18)
which causes an overestimation of the GWP. High-strength concrete is similarly mapped
with the highest available strength in table 7 (BR18) leading to an underestimation of the
GWP results.
In relation to the LCA microservice, the use of CSV ﬁles as an input for the environmental
data library is a very static approach and requires ongoing maintenance to keep the library
updated as mentioned in section 4.5.3. If a library was accessed through an API, the
maintenance and update checks would be reduced if not eliminated. This has however not
been implemented because the current way to access table 7 (BR18) is through a static table.
The two platforms ÖKOBAUDAT and ECO Platform both have large databases and they
provide access to environmental data in an open digital data format freely accessible either
through a web interface or through an API for access by other software systems. This
makes them suitable for software development and could be interesting to investigate for a
tool which did not have to comply with the requirements in BR18.
Table 7 (BR18) is based on a mix of generic environmental data from Ökobaudat and
industry data from EPD Denmark. As the LCA microservice is currently built exclusively
on table 7 (BR18), there is no product-speciﬁc data available in the microservice.
Product-speciﬁc data allows the designers to choose the most optimal manufacturer and
product relative to other products in the same category, which can help promote a proper
search for a low-emission option. Product-speciﬁc data should be implemented in the LCA
calculations and documentation in the late design phases to make the LCA as accurate as
possible for the ﬁnal building. However, in the earlier design phases, it can be misleading
to use a product-speciﬁc EPD, as many changes occur during the long process of designing
and constructing buildings. For this reason, using generic and industry data can be
advantageous in the earlier phases of the building process. The developed LCA

6.4. AIDING STRUCTURAL ENGINEERS IN DESIGN DECISIONS
microservice is therefore mostly advantageous in the earlier stages when the speciﬁc
manufacturers and products have yet to be decided. It is possible to expand the
environmental data library with product-speciﬁc EPDs, but the user would have to rewrite
some of the scripts. This could cause problems when mapping from elements to speciﬁc
environmental data, and the time it takes to do this might exceed the time saved by trying
to automate the process. However, if a company for example always uses the same
concrete manufacturer, it would be easy and advantageous to add the right environmental
data to the library and remap the concrete materials to the new data.
Using table 7 (BR18) as a data foundation can lead to a streamlining of LCAs on buildings.
This can provide a basis for comparing different building projects. In addition, it provides
opportunities for setting limit values for GWP as there is a common basis.
In the Building Regulations, §297 and §298 are only dealing with the climate impact of
buildings. Therefore, the generic dataset table 7 (BR18) solely contains information on the
GWP from materials, as this is enough to meet the requirements. The toolchain caters to the
Building Regulations by conforming to table 7 (BR18) and calculates only the GWP of the
load-bearing structures. This means, that even though the term life cycle assessments have
been widely used in this report, it is not quite an accurate deﬁnition to use. The problem
with only using GWP as a parameter for environmental performance is the clear risk of
burden shifting through the many different environmental impact categories. Climate
change is one of the main environmental focus areas in society and politics at the moment.
And while it is crucial to reduce the greenhouse gases from human activity, there are also
other extremely critical ecological challenges like biodiversity loss and land-use change
among others. Showing only the CO2-eq emissions of the structural elements indicates that
the "right" choice is always the option with the lowest GWP, which does not embrace a
holistic perspective of sustainability.
6.4
Aiding structural engineers in design decisions
This project has sought to investigate how knowledge on climate impact from load-bearing
structures can become an integrated part of the decision-making of structural design. The
primary task of a structural engineer is to deliver safe and durable structures which are
structurally optimised. However, considerations on e.g. execution, architectural
requirements, ﬁre, and acoustics can also impact their design process and therefore need to
be taken into account. The new LCA requirements introduce yet another consideration out
of their expertise area. It is important to aid the structural engineers in a good way when
imposing this new concern on them. When developing the LCA microservice, simplicity
and transparency have been important factors. The materials have been mapped with
attention, to make the tool as reliable as possible. The developed toolchain has automated
the process of calculating the GWP of load-bearing structures. This enables structural
engineers to gain insights into the CO2-eq emissions directly related to their ﬁeld of
responsibility and expertise. With this information readily accessible, it becomes easier and
more compelling for engineers to incorporate it into their consulting practices.
This project has also investigated how SDAS-LCA interoperability can be used for active
carbon-reduced design processes. Parametric studies can lead to an increased focus on the
sustainability of the load-bearing structures, as it provides valuable inputs, for example, in
the early design, where opportunities to improve environmental performance can be
identiﬁed and incorporated into the design. The parametric study microservice developed
in this project shows a proof-of-concept for ways to make tools that can guide the structural
engineer in lowering the embodied carbon of the structures. A parametric tool, which
depicts the GWP for each option, directly provides the engineer with an insight into the
parameters inﬂuencing CO2-eq emissions. Setting up a parametric study that runs fully
automatically is challenging as many factors can affect the calculations. However, the

6.5
Toolchain further development
The contributions of the developed toolchain are described in section 6.1. However, the
toolchain is not fully developed and suggestions for further work will be explained in the
following.
Relating to section 6.3, the idea of automating the mapping between environmental data
and objects from 3D models proposes many issues. The ﬁrst issue that occurs in the
process, is the lack of rules for modelling in Revit or similar 3D software. The differing
object information makes it difﬁcult to fully automate the mapping process between object
materials and the right corresponding environmental data. In chapter 2, the need for
standardisation and harmonisation of the interoperability of BIM and LCA was
highlighted. A clear framework of how to enrich objects in the BIM environment with the
necessary data for LCA calculations could improve the accuracy of the LCA as well as
enhance the process behind it. The new BR18 requirements can potentially provide a
greater incentive to follow the same modelling rules. It is challenging to fully automate the
mapping between the elements and the accurate environmental data before a clear set of
modelling rules has been deﬁned and implemented. Therefore, an argument could be
made that there is still a need to include an option to manually map object instances with
environmental data. This would preferably happen in a front-end solution or directly in
the 3D software with a user interface.
In section 4.5, the included building parts for the LCA calculations have been stated. The
toolchain includes the main parts for calculating the GWP of load-bearing structures.
However, to enhance the toolchain, incorporating ﬁre protection of structural elements is
crucial, as the method of ﬁre protection varies based on the type of structural material
used. Furthermore, bigger connection details like bolted connections should likewise be
included in the future development of the toolchain along with other omitted building
parts as described in section 6.3.
Many of the methods to guide GWP-based impact decisions are related to a communicative
front-end solution. As a front-end solution is not within the scope of this project, it has not
been developed. However, discussions on communicating the results have been an
important part of the tool development. An important feature of a possible front-end is to
compare the results against a baseline. An obvious and inevitable baseline for comparing
the total emissions of the building would be the CO2-eq limits from BR18. On a more
detailed level, it would be interesting to have the baseline for CO2-eq emissions from
load-bearing structures only, in order to make it easier to compare the footprint from the
structures directly. This baseline could for example be calculated on the basis of the
buildings in the case library from VCBK (Videnscenter om Bygningers Klimapåvirkning)
[73] or it could be a baseline decided internally in the company, or following the
dual-benchmark approach as described in section 2.3.6.
Currently, the GWP for each material in the LCA microservice is based on a single input
from table 7 (BR18). As a building project develops, some of the environmental data from
table 7 (BR18) could be replaced with product speciﬁc EPDs. This means that the actual
GWP could be higher or lower than what was originally displayed and which the decisions
were based on. An idea could be to implement GWP values from other environmental
datasets and thereby display an interval of CO2-eq emissions rather than a single value.
These guidelines have not been made for the current toolchain. For further development of
the tool, it would be advantageous to develop a proper front-end solution with clear
guidelines for the structural engineer.

6.5. TOOLCHAIN FURTHER DEVELOPMENT
For further development of the tool, it is also necessary to include more environmental
impacts, as only focusing on GWP can lead to burden-shifting. This would be relevant for,
e.g., stakeholders wanting a more complete picture of the environmental impacts of their
projects.
Another important aspect in relation to LCA of the building is the service life of the
structure, which was brieﬂy touched upon in section 4.5. At present, the service life of the
structure has not been implemented in the tool, but it should be a focus area for further
development. Similarly, in section 4.5.3, the life cycle phases were introduced. Here, it was
mentioned how the replacement phase B4 is overall evidently very relevant., but as the
LCA is calculated over a 50-year period and all the included structural elements and
materials have a lifespan above 50 years, there is no replacement of the elements.
Therefore, it has not been included in the service. However, replacement should be a part
of the service, as it is not unlikely that some users would include special materials or
elements with a lifespan below 50 years.
The further development of the tool should additionally focus on expanding the CDE
framework to such that it is possible to get a full LCA of the entire building project. For
instance, it should be possible to calculate and implement the environmental impacts from
the operational and construction phases.
Another valuable feature to include in further development of the toolchain is a real-time
feedback loop between the LCA results and the structural calculations. A direct feedback
loop feeding the LCA results back to FEM-Design could provide the designer with a very
visual representation of the GWP of the structure. An example could be to colour-map the
elements on the magnitude of their CO2-eq emissions, thereby providing a visual hot-spot
analysis directly in FEM-Design.
The development of the parametric study microservice should focus on integrating it into a
CDE platform, as described in section 4.8. Creating a tool, which depicts the GWP for each
option, directly provides the engineer with an insight into the parameters inﬂuencing
CO2-eq emissions.
In the toolchain, a service that checks the results has currently not been developed. This
means that there is no way to detect if any modelling mistakes were made in the 3D
software, if some 3D objects were not included in the structural elements parser, or if the
LCA microservice has miscalculated the GWP. Therefore, it is crucial that the user has
general insights into the project basis, structural modelling, and LCA parameters to assess
if the results seem rational. To develop the toolchain further, it is essential to include a way
to check the results – either implemented in each service or as a separate microservice.

CONCLUSION
==PAGE:88==
This thesis aimed to investigate how engineers can utilise an automated workﬂow between
structural analysis and LCA calculations in a CDE to include environmental performance
in structural design decisions.
This project introduces a toolchain capable of representing LCA calculations based on a
structural 3D model. The automation between BIM/SDAS and LCA calculations allows
structural engineers to base their design decisions on environmental performance. This
allows for assessing embodied carbon in the early design phases, resulting in a potential
for decreasing the overall environmental impact of the built asset.
It is demonstrated that the toolchain works with models from both Revit and FEM-Design
based on the developed class hierarchy for structural elements. However, for the toolchain
to work, the models must comply with speciﬁc requirements, emphasising the need for a
common ruleset for BIM-LCA integration. Some issues regarding the lack of LCA-ready
BIM objects in the Revit model can be solved by using the FEM-Design model as a base for
the LCA calculation because it already has a ruleset for the materials deﬁned in the
software.
The automation of the toolchain has been discussed, and it is suggested that there is still a
need to include an option to manually map structural elements to the desired
environmental data.
A parametric study microservice was created using the FEM-Design API to investigate the
incorporation of environmental impacts as a parameter for structural design decisions. The
parametric study shows great potential for further developing a tool that depicts
sustainability in a structural analysis environment. A tool like this could help engineers
reduce the climate impact through the whole design process.
The toolchain utilises a common data environment, where BIM data is centralised and
interconnected, which can support the opportunity to thoroughly investigate the
environmental impacts of a built asset from a holistic life cycle perspective.
