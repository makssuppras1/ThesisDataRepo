Abstract
This study investigates the wear behaviour of polymer-polymer contacts. A correlation
of wear rates with materials parameters is sought. It is investigated whether the relation
described by Lancaster in 1968 can be used for polymer-polymer contacts. In this relation
the wear rate ˙W is proportional to 1/(σtεt), where σt is the strength at break and εt is
the strain at break. This is investigated by a series of wear tests using various polymers
including semicrystalline polymers and amorphous polymers. Some of the tested polymers
are carbon or glass ﬁber reinforced. The polymers are tested using the Novo Nordisk
Tribotester. A correlation between 1/(σtεt) and the wear rates of polymers could not be
identiﬁed. It was observed for one material combination that the wear would separate
in two distinct groupings. For experiments involving glass ﬁber ﬁlled polymers against
carbon ﬁber ﬁlled polymers a wear pattern involving a sudden transition from low to high
wear was observed.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
This project investigates the wear of polymers. In many applications of polymers wear is a
limiting factor on product lifetime. This is especially an issue since only very limited tools
exist for estimating and predicting polymer wear in the design phase. This is becoming
increasingly relevant as sustainability comes to the forefront of product design where
increased product lifespans and reduced carbon-footprints are key.
Novo Nordisk A/S (NN) is a major pharmaceutical company headquartered in Denmark
engaged in the development and production of several drugs and devices for treatment of
diabetes and other diseases. One such device is the Novopen, which is used to dose insulin
quickly and accurately. The Novopen was introduced in 1985 and has since though many
improvements and iterations, with a total of more than one billion devices being produced
[1, 2]. NN devices are split among two primary lines, single use and durable. Single-use
are only used once and therefore wear is not a major issue. On the other hand durable
devices are typically used for several years. For instance the NovoPen Echo is designed to
last up to 5 years in use[3]. Such a device contains points where polymer materials slide
against each other. This can produce wear which might negatively impact the lifetime of
the device.
Being able to predict the wear of materials without running lengthy and costly tests would
be very beneﬁcial, especially in the early stages of development and materials selection,
where only a technical data sheet (TDS) might be available. Ideally one would like to
predict the wear properties based on commonly available bulk material properties.
Most of the materials used in Novo Nordisk today is thermoplastic polymers because
they can be injection molded, which enables cheap, high volume production of complex
geometries. Wear of these polymers can be an issue that limits the lifecycle of durable
devices. The particular usage and load pattern for these devices also plays a signiﬁcant
role in how wear occurs. Signiﬁcantly for wear concerns, the load is intermittent, being
used for a short period several times a day. As a result of this loading pattern frictional
heating is negligible and does not contribute to wear.

1.2
Prior research
Tribology is the study of two surfaces in and experiencing relative motion. This ﬁeld can
be divided into two main research areas: friction and wear. Some of the most foundational
work in friction research is the the work of Amontons who created two laws of friction:[4]
• The frictional force is proportional to the applied load
• The frictional force is independent of the apparent area of contact
To this Coulomb added his law in 1781:[5]
• The frictional force between two bodies in motion is independent of the relative
velocity between the two bodies
These laws allow the deﬁnition of the coeﬃcient of friction (CoF), µ [-], as:
µ = Ff
Fn
(1.1)
Where Ff [N] is the frictional force and Fn [N] is the normal force. This concept continues
to be widely used.
The other branch of tribology is wear, which is the main focus of this thesis.
Wear
encompasses a wide array of diﬀering mechanisms that all remove material by mechanical
or chemical action[6]. In this thesis the focus is on wear by relative sliding of materials
in contact.
A signiﬁcant contribution of the work relating to wear and tribology in
general can be traced back to the work done by Tabor and Bowden in "The Friction
and Lubrication of Solids", originally published in 1950[7]. This, along with the work by
Holm, was the starting point for Archard in his 1953 paper where fundamental contact
mechanics were developed [8, 9].
These developments lead to the 1956 paper where
Archard and Hirst proposed a theory of wear where the volumetric wear (W) follows this
relationship[10]:
W[m3] = Ks P
Pm
(1.2)
in this equation K [-] is a constant is based on the probability of an encounter of two
surface asperities (i.e. surface roughness peaks) producing a wear particle, s [m] is the
sliding distance, P [N] is the applied load and Pm [Pa] is the "ﬂow pressure", a term
roughly corresponding to hardness. A major feature of this theory is that the wear rate
should be independent of the apparent area of contact.
Since then a number of modiﬁcations and alterations have been proposed to Archard’s
model. One such suggestion is that for a polymers on metals the wear would be dependent
on the speed, load and time such that the wear volume could be predicted by:[11]
W = KvaP btc

particular to any given material combination. While this model might produce more ac-

Hardness is often included as a parameter in wear modelling. In a 1974 paper by Khr-

was found to have excellent correlation with hardness, with harder metals being less
worn. However the correlation was less clear when alloying elements and heat treatment
was involved[13].
Another interesting phenomenon was documented by Lacey and Shaver in 1999 [14]. This
study documented how for a series of tests done on a reciprocating steel-on-steel setup
lubricated by fuels were studied. The results did not appear to be normally distributed,
but instead separated into two groups, one where minimal wear occurred and one with
substantial wear. This was attributed to one of two distinct wear processes occurring
randomly.
1.2.1
Polymer wear
Polymer wear has some unique mechanisms that distinguishes it from metal wear. Some
of these factors include the low thermal conductivity and melting point, which when
combined makes thermal considerations much more important than in metal contacts.
Another important phenomenon of polymer wear is transfer ﬁlms [15]. Transfer ﬁlms
are a phenomenon where a thin ﬁlm of one material face transfers to the opposite face
during wear. This can have marked consequences on the friction and wear properties of
the material [16]. Wear testing of polymers are often made with a metal counterface, this
poses a problem when testing polymer-polymer contacts, as standards and literature are
often focus on polymer-metal contacts.
One way of describing wear of polymers is by PV-limits [17]. The PV-limit is the maximum
product of pressure and velocity of the contact before extreme wear is seen. PV-limits are
based on a thermal analysis. The PV limit is the point where, in steady-state conditions,
the polymer in the interface will start to melt. This is used as an upper bound for the
load that a given contact can sustain. As an example the PV-limit for ABS (Acrylonitrile-
Butadiene-Styrene) with a steel counterface is given as 490 MPa m/s, and 35 MPa m/s
against itself. These limits are commonly used[18], and listed by some manufacturers in
their data sheets and guidelines [19, 20]. However PV-limits, being based on thermal
analysis, are not descriptive for wear in the regime of NN devices. Still the PV concept
is a useful tool for describing wear conditions and when comparing diﬀerent tests. In a
typical contact in a NN device a typical speed is 20 mm/s and typical contact pressure of
3 MPa, this gives a PV of 0.06MPa m/s. For this study somewhat higher PV values will
be used in order for the tests to produce more substantial and measurable wear.

The Ratner-Lancaster Relation
One of the ﬁrst papers that introduced the idea of using bulk material properites as a
means of estimating wear rates was that of Oberle in 1951[21]. He proposed that wear
rates of metals in contact could be estimated by the ratio:
˙W ∝E
H , that is the elastic
modulus over the hardness. This and other results inﬂuenced a 1964 paper where S. B.
Ratner et Al. proposed the following relationship between material properties and wear
rate ˙W for polymers[22]:
˙W[m3] ∝
µ
Hσtεt
(1.4)
Where H [Pa] is the hardness, σt [Pa] is the ultimate tensile strength and εt [-] is the
elongation at break. This is based on the following reasoning:
• The wear should be proportional to the work done on the contact divided by the
work needed to deform the material until break.
• The force is proportional to the coeﬃcient of friction.
• The work required to deform the polymer until break is proportional to the product:
σtεt
• The contact area is reduced by the hardness, H reducing the wear

tionship between polymer materials characteristics and wear rates[23]. In the proposed
model the wear rate should be:

components is not nescessarily random. The addition of internal silicon oil lubrication to
polyimide with carbon ﬁber reinforcement has also been studied [34]. This paper found
that the addition of silicon oil lubrication gave lower wear rates at room temperature
but with a detrimental eﬀect at elevated temperatures. The wear performance of CF
reinforced PEEK composites in self-mated couples were tested by Scholes and Unsworth
[35]. During this test the specimens were lubricated by diluted calf serum. Here unﬁlled
PEEK was found to have greater wear rates than CF reinforced PEEK. The results of
these studies on the eﬀects of CF reinforcement can be seen in Table 1.1, along with
approximate PV-values of the experiments. While most of these papers ﬁnds a decrease
in wear rates when carbon ﬁber is added, the eﬀect is still up for debate.
The eﬀect of roughness on the wear of PEEK and CF reinforced PEEK has been studied
[37]. This study used steel specimens of varying surface ﬁnish against PEEK specimens.
The study found that the wear was relatively constant across the tested range of 0.02-1µm
Rq (Root mean square roughness), but with a minimum at 0.15-0.25 µm Rq.
However most of these studies test polymer-metal contacts and not polymer-polymer con-
tacts, which limits the usefulness of the existing when design polymer-polymer contacts.
Another important factor in this study is to create wear conditions that mimic those found
in Novo Nordisk devices. This means that local heating should be kept to a minimum as
the operational cycle of NN device means that it does not get hot. The wear of self-mated
ABS contacts have been studied[38]. However the speeds in the contact are much higher
than are used in Novo Nordisk devices and local melting is observed, making the results
incomparable with wear in NN devices.

1.3.1
Thesis statement
The main questions which are adressed in this thesis are:
• Does the Ratner-Lancaster relation hold for multi-pass polymer-polymer contacts?
How should the the two diﬀerent material properties be handled? Is it only the
most wearing material that "decides" the wear rate or is the opposite face material
important?
If so what is the proportionality constant?
• Is the microstructure of the polymers important? Do semi-crystalline polymers wear
more than amorphous? Or vice versa?
• Which polymers and methods are best suited for further study of wear phenomena
in polymer-polymer contacts?

Chapter 2
Background and materials
2.1
Polymer characterization
Polymers are large molecules composed of several monomers.
There are several ways
of characterizing and grouping polymers. In this report the focus is on thermoplastic
polymers. This group of polymers can be heated up and molded in a liquid state, this
allows for rapid production using injection molding.
Some thermoplastics form regular crystalline regions. These regions can have a marked
eﬀect on the properties of the the polymer[41]. As these regions only form part of the
microstructure these are called semicrystalline. Semicrystalline polymers in general are
harder and stronger than amorphous ones[41, 42]. Another distinction is between ho-
mopolymers and copolymers. Homopolymers are polymers formed from a single type of
monomer. Copolymers on the other hand consist of two or more diﬀerent monomers.
Depending on the copolymer this allows the properties of the end material to be tuned
by adjusting the relative content of the diﬀerent monomers. The two copolymers which
are used for testing in this thesis are POM-copolymer and ABS. ABS is a copolymer con-
sisting of acrylonitrile, butadiene, and styrene. POM is commercially available as both
a homo- and copolymer, the POM-copolymer has worse mechanical properties than the
homopolymer. However POM-copolymer oﬀers improved chemical resistance and better
long term dimensional-stability and low temperature properties[43, 25]. The POM grades
used in this study are all POM copolymers.

2.3. Hertzian Contact mechanics
2.2.1
Creep
When a thermoplastic material is loaded for an extended period of time the strain of the
polymer will increase over time while the load is applied[42]. This phenomenon is called
creep. The eﬀects of creep on this study is twofold. First and foremost it means that
when a thermoplastic is loaded in our test setup it will decrease in height slightly. This
means that the measured wear tends to be overestimated because the wear is estimated
by the height of the specimen. Another eﬀect of creep is that it makes an exact deﬁnition
of ultimate tensile strength, elongation at break, yield strength and similar parameters
diﬃcult as they depend on the applied strain rate. The tensile tests for the base materials
used in this report are tested following ASTM D638 or ISO 527 according to the data
sheets from the manufacturers.

3.1
Experimental Setup
The tests are conducted on the Novo Nordisk Tribotester[39]. The setup is sketched in
Figure 3.1. The speciments are mounted at point (1), the geometry and mounting of the
specimens is described later. The load on the contact can be set by moving the dead
weight (2) using the stepper motor (3), these are mounted in a slide (4) which contains
a toothed belt that connects the stepper motor and the weight. The slide tilts about a
pivot (5). The entire slide height can also be adjusted using a threaded rod (6) in order
to level the slide. A servo motor (7) rotates the lower part of the contact through an
angle gearbox (8). The wear is measured during the test using a laser distance sensor (9).
This is also depicted in Figure 3.2, where the laser distance sensor which does not move
and the reference plate which is mounted to the slide is pictured. As the specimen wears
the slide will rotate about the pivot leading to the reference plate moving closer to the
laser distance sensor. The frictional force is measured using a pair of strain gauges (10)
mounted on either side of the arm protruding from the slide. This arm holds the upper
specimen in place, and the frictional force is transferred through this arm. The entire
setup is secured to a base (11).
The output of the sensors (9,10) are logged using a LabVIEW program. This program is
also used to control the two motors (3) and (7), that sets the load and sliding speed of the
contact.The setup is controlled via a desktop computer through a LabVIEW interface.
In LabVIEW the load, rotational speed and data sampling rate can be set. For all of the
tests the load is 387 N, except where otherwise noted, the sliding speed is 10 mm/s and
the sampling rate is 1 Hz. The test setup is described in more detail by Ruby et. al [39].

3.2
Wear test Procedures
The specimens are ﬁrst wiped oﬀwith ethanol. Care is taken to not touch the specimens
with bare hands and cotton gloves are worn when handling the specimens. This is to
prevent grease from the hands of the person conducting the test contaminating the sur-
face of the specimens. Once the specimens are mounted in the Tribotester, the slide is
leveled by adjusting the height and using a spirit level. The test program is initialised
in LabVIEW. The test program can be seen in Figure 3.6. The test starts by initialising
data acquisition and apply the prescribed normal load, which unless otherwise stated is
set to 387 N. The rotation is started such that the relative motion of the contact is 10
mm/s. The program then has 30 minutes of runtime followed by 3 minutes of standstill.
This is done to prevent excessive heating of the contact. The data recording is halted
when the rotation is stopped. This cycle is repeated 22 times resulting in a test length of
12 h 3 min, as 22·30 minutes+3·21 minutes=12 h 3 minutes. The total sliding distance
is s = 10mm/s·11h= 396 m. After the test has run 22 sections of 30 minutes, the load
is removed and the data collection is stopped. The data is logged in a text ﬁle which
subsequently is analysed in MATLAB.
The room where the wear experiments take place is not temperature or humidity con-
trolled, however normal indoor room temperature was observed by the operator. Re-
garding the eﬀects of humidity on wear rates, in 1990 Lancaster in a review article noted
that: "Not a great deal of information is available concerning the inﬂuence of atmospheric
humidity on the wear of unﬁlled polymers but, as with friction, the eﬀects do not seem
to be particularly large."[48]

4.1
Repeatability of tests and POM against PBT30CF
In order to investigate the repeatability of the test setup 7 identical tests were run for the
same material pair. With POM as the small R10 and PBT30CF as the large, ﬂat part.
This combination results in a PV value of 0.17 MPa·m/s.
In Figure 4.1 the wear of PBT30CF against POM over the course of 12 hours is shown. The
experiment is repeated 7 times, with all of them shown in Figure 4.1. The 7 experimental
runs appear to follow one of two wear patterns, with 3 runs ending at about 0.1 mm of
wear and 4 runs clustered around 0.16 mm. In both cases the wear is most signiﬁcant at
the start of the experiment, with a more steady wear rate as the experiment progresses.
The wear over the ﬁrst 1800 seconds (half hour) is shown in Figure 4.2. For three runs, a
sudden increase in wear is seen after about 500 s. In one run the sudden change in wear
however a gradually higher wear is seen, so it ends up joining the "high wear" group.
The experiments seem to indicate that this pair undergoes one of two possible wear
patterns, as none of the experimental runs had wear lying between these two clusters.
This phenomenon of two distinct wear groups has previously been observed by Lacey
and Shaver in a 1999 paper, which found that for some wear tests the results where not
normally distributed, but that the distribution of wear results had two distinct peaks,
indicating that one of two processes occurred at random[14].
Table 4.1 shows the wear in mm hw, the speciﬁc wear, η, for each run. Along with the
mean, ¯η, standard deviation σN and coeﬃcient of variance, CoV for each parameter. The
coeﬃcient of variance for the speciﬁc wear is 36%. Compared to the µ where the CoV is
only 1.2%. The wear therefore appears to be subject to great variance while the friction
stays the same. These ﬁndings make a estimation of the repeatability of the results in
general quite uncertain. Within each of the two blocks the repeatability is much better
than for the experiment as a whole. It is an unresolved question whether this pattern is
common across polymer-polymer contacts or if this is a special case. For this thesis it is
assumed that the CoV found for this test holds as a prediction for the variance for the
other tests.

4.3
Unﬁt tests
For a combination of materials to be suitable of testing it should fulﬁll the following:
• The contact should not fail during the test, i.e. the specimens should remain intact
during the test
• The friction force should not be exceed the capacity of the Tribotester. This limit
is set at 400 N
For some conditions these criteria are not met. A small R10 ABS was tested against a
large ﬂat POM. however during the test, the ABS specimen collapsed. While this did not
damage the tribotester, it was decided to stop testing with ABS specimens. The runs
completed with ABS specimens are still included. PBT was tested against PBT30CF. The
wear and friction coeﬃcient as a function of time is seen in Figure 4.10. Here the friction
is seen to increase dramatically over the course of the test. The frictional force ultimately
reaches 400 N which is the limit for the machine and the test is stopped. On the basis of
this test it is decided to not continue tests with self-mated contacts. This phenomenon has
previously been observed, where self-mated contacts over time "fuse together"[25]. These
contacts are because of this characteristic unsuited for usage in practical applications,
and so less relevant for the study at hand.

4.7. Glass ﬁber reinforced against Carbon ﬁber reinforced polymers
Figure 4.27 shows a sketch of the observed phases of wear in these tests. First in the A
phase, the wear rate is low, then the wear rate increases dramatically in the B phase,
after which the wear rate decreases somewhat and becomes stable in what is here called
the C phase.
In many applications however it is not the wear rate itself that would
determine the usable lifetime of the contact. Instead the dramatic increase in wear and
friction seen in phase B would likely be unacceptable before the steady state of phase
C was reached. Therefore, what is important in terms of an engineering perspective is
therefore not necessarily the wear rate, but primarily the time, tlife, until the transition
from low to high wear.
The results suggest that as the carbon ﬁber content of the
counterface increases, the lifetime of the contact decreases. However, as no repetitions of
these tests were made and as was observed in Section 4.1 a given contact might have very
diﬀerent responses when repeated. The fact that the tests were performed with a polymer
containing silicon oil might have an eﬀect, however the impact of this is not studied in
these tests.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
The wear of several polymer-polymer connections were tested. The repeatability of the
wear process of POM against PBT30CF was tested. It was found that the wear split into
two groups one with less wear and one with more. The split between these two groups
was at the beginning of the wear process. It was found that both the PBT and POM
specimens were worn. The creep behaviour of ABS against POM was analysed. The
Ratner-Lancaster relation was tested with a POM, a semi crystalline material and PC,
an amorphous, against several materials. The Ratner-Lancaster model did not correlate
to the wear rates.
The wear rates also did not appear to correlate to opposing face
hardness. The results were impacted by the initial mounting mechanism for the PEEK
specimens, double adhesive tape, proving to have major issues. POM-PS connections
were studied with higher contact pressures. Increasing the contact pressure, and thus the
PV-value, did not appear to cause an increase in wear rates. PBT40GF2Si was tested
against PPS30CF, this combination showed in interesting wear pattern, with a short initial
period with limited wear followed by a spike, after this initial period the wear rate seemed
to settle. This wear pattern was also seen for PBT40GF2Si against PEEK with carbon
ﬁber. The length of the low wear period was longer for PEEK20CF than for PEEK40CF.
Against neat PEEK the transition to high wear was not seen. This indicates that the
lifetime of the contact could be negatively impacted by higher contents of carbon ﬁber.
5.1
Perspective and Future Work
This study has not been able to produce a model that can generally predict wear rates.
One area where results did seem relatively useful is when studying the life-times of ﬁber-
ﬁlled polymer connections. This area might prove promising by switching from a focus
on wear rates to life-times, might not only be more accurate, this could also move wear
modelling in a direction where it could be more directly applied to design and material
selection in device design.
