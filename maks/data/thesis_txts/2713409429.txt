Abstract
Current policies and new upcoming taxes on CO2 encourage electrification of heating systems. Because of
the evolution of the political context and the current instability of gas prices, Energinet decided to explore
new investments in eco-friendlier solutions to replace the gas boilers currently operating in its meter and
regulator stations.
This study aims to evaluate the current performance of the gas boilers, create a tool that would help
Energinet to determine the best option for each station and test it to give recommendations for the stations
located in Brøndby, Ellidshøj and Taulov. The gas boilers’ efficiencies were evaluated in Python with the
CoolProp library. The explored solutions include electric heaters, ammonia ground source and air soucre
heat pumps with free heating options and possible solutions for evaporator thawing. They were modelled
and dimensioned on Python with the CoolProp and TESPy libraries. The economic model was made in
Excel. Some of the investment cost models could be further optimized in Python.
The study showed that the efficiency of the boilers ranges from 30% to 70%. This, coupled with the size
of the stations, determine how pressing renovation is. Overall, all of the solutions are economically feasible
and more eco-friendly. Among the possible solutions, heat pumps without a free heating system are more
cost-effective. Ground source heat pumps are the best option if Energinet wants to remove the gas boiler
entirely. With more limited resources allocated to the project, a hybrid system coupling the current gas
boiler as a backup and an air-source heat pump is the most cost-effective option. The solution is also more
easily generalised, which maximizes overall avoided CO2 emissions.
More practical issues come into play as the possible need for new facilities to host the heating system and
the comfort of the technicians with the new systems. The actual installed system design, refrigerant and
efficiency may differ from the COP estimated for the ground source heat pump: 5.13 and the air source
heat pump: 3.08. Despite the possible fluctuations, the sensitivity analysis reinforces the reliability of the
results.

Introduction
With rising concerns over global warming and the depletion of fossil fuels, a transition towards greener
energy sources is inevitable. The COP26 that occurred in November shows general commitment from all the
countries to make a sustainable future. Denmark is a leader in terms of energy transition and sustainability.
Indeed, Denmark occupies the top position in the 2020 Environmental Performance Index [1]. Thus as a
partly public company, Energinet attaches great importance to setting an example of sustainability and
taking action to make the world a better place [2]. Energinet is in charge of the electricity network as well
as the gas network. It is responsible for ensuring that every dwelling has access to electricity and gas when
they need it. The gas is transported at high pressure (80 bar) [3] when it needs to travel long distances
to limit the size of the pipes and reduce the costs. However, the gas needs to be expanded before being
delivered to households. This is what the 42 Danish meter and regulators (M/R) stations are for. One is
shown below:
Figure 1: A regulation station [4]
The gas is preheated before expansion. Then, the gas is ready to be sent to distribution centres. Currently,
most of the M/R stations date back to 1983, when climate change prevention was not considered as crucial
as it is now. The preheating system consists of a boiler that burns gas to heat water. Then, a heat exchanger
allows the heat to be transferred to the gas. The regulation heating system is depicted below:
4
==PAGE:10==
Figure 2: Scheme of the regulation station heating system
In this project, three M/R stations are investigated emission-wise and cost-wise: Brøndby, Ellidshøj and
Taulov. The aim is to build a reliable and flexible economic model that allows Energinet to predict the
economic and environmental performances of eco-friendly heating systems in their stations. An additional
electric heater, ground source heat pump and air source heat pump with a free heating option and two
solutions for the thawing of the evaporator of the air source heat pump: sensible heat storage and a sec-
ond evaporator are explored.
The key parameters are the net present value(NPV), the internal rate of
return(IRR), the payback time(PBT), the avoided emissions of GHG gas and the minimal nominal price of
CO2 emissions. It will allow Energinet to make decisions for any station it sees fit. The three aforementioned
stations serve to give general guidelines about what the best investments are according to the model, as they
are of different sizes.
6
Background
6.1
general introduction to Energinet
Energinet is an independent public enterprise owned by the Danish Ministry of Climate, Energy and Utilities.
They own and operate the Danish gas transmission system onshore within Denmark including marine belt
crossings. Energinet’s core gas activity is transporting natural gas through the national transmission system
made of 862km of pipeline buried 1 meter below the surface. They are also responsible for the security of
supply and physical balance in the Danish natural gas system. The company has its headquarters in Erritsø
[5]. The key to their engagement is the ISO 9001 certification. It is a guarantee that Energinet is committed
to delivering products and services that meet the customer needs and improving their process while trying
to identify and minimize the risks [6].
6.2
The case
The gas must be delivered at least 2°C and a pressure that ranges from 20 to 40 bars depending on the local
distribution station. Since the gas is transported at around 80 bars in the transmission grid, it needs to be
expanded down to the proper pressure in the 42 M/R stations owned by Energinet. However, the expansion
comes with a significant drop in temperature of about 20°C, which compromises the requirements of the
delivered gas. Therefore, each M/R station includes a heating system to preheat the gas. Most of them are
gas boilers that are 30 to 40 years old. New CO2 taxation announced by the Danish government [7] call for
a feasibility analysis of alternatives to the current gas boiler. The three most popular electrical technologies
that produce heat are electric boilers, heat pumps and recovering excess heat from a process. For the latter,
the current supply of gas and the absence of nearby industries around the M/R stations push the choice
towards a gas turbine, delivering electricity while the excess heat would be provided to the process gas. A
5
==PAGE:11==
benchmark revealed that many different versions of heat pumps and recovery processes from gas turbines
existed. Some are listed below:
• heat pumps
– Reversible heat pumps
– Adsorption Heat pumps that are used to operate at around 150°C up to 300°C.
– Chemical heat pumps that can recover heat from a 30 to 100°C heat source.
– LiBr absorption refrigeration that can recover heat from an 80 to 150°C heat source.
– Ammonia-water absorption heat pump
– Adsorption refrigeration works at 100 °C on the cold side.
• recovery heat from gas turbines
– Brayton cycle
– Kalina cycle
– regenerative Brayton cycle
– organic Rankine cycle (solar)
Among these technologies, a classic reversible heat pump was chosen, for the temperature lift is similar to
the one of a residential building.

Methods and materials
The main project steps are designing the alternative systems according to the data provided before carrying
out a feasibility study and a carbon balance on the new solutions.
• Data processing from the M/R stations
Energinet provided data of their M/R stations. From these and the analysis of the system presented
in figure 2. The flow of process gas, the inlet temperature, the outlet temperature after expansion,
the inlet pressure, the outlet pressure after expansion and the fuel gas consumption are provided.

file, Pandas for data processing and CoolProp to access a database of fluid properties.
• Selection of the potential replacement systems
Once the power that needs to be transferred to the fluid and the temperatures are known, new energy
sources can be envisaged. Anton was introduced only to the viable solutions within the power and
temperature range calculated beforehand. Due to the capacity range of the new heating system and
the electrification objective, recovering excess heat from a gas turbine is excluded. Therefore, it was
decided to continue with an electric boiler, a ground source heat pump and an air source heat pump.
Keeping the current boiler as a backup is considered as well.
• Designing the systems
The design is made so that the energy requirements are met while complying with general design rules
regarding heat exchanges and hydraulic circuits. This part is done in Python with the TESPy library.
The gas boiler is kept as is. The heat pump systems are given a free heating alternative system that may
reduce their energy consumption over time. The electric heater system remains without this option.
The aim is to determine the efficiency of each so it can be translated in terms of energy consumption
and then into operational costs. The main design features of the heating system are determined as
well. It, however, is a personal objective.
• Comparing the systems Once each scene is set, the final results will be put in the comparative
table shown in section 8.5. The comparison is based on key economic and environmental parameters
that were decided by Energinet. The Net Present Value(NPV), the internal rate of return(IRR), the
payback time(PBT) the overall amount of CO2 and methane emissions saved with each alternative
help Energinet to decide which to invest in. A sensitivity analysis is then carried out to observe the
changes in NPV, and PBT due to the unforeseen changes in capital costs, changes in electricity costs
and gas costs. Different scenarios regarding the gas boiler utilisation and thus the new heating system
size are explored. The changes in overall saved emissions are quantified versus the utilization of the
existing boiler.

7.1.3
Calculation of the efficiency
The need for replacement of the boilers is prioritized according to their efficiency. The boilers that show the
lowest efficiency should take precedence.
The calculation consists of several steps. First, the heat that is provided to the process gas is calculated. It
will be calculated based on the process gas flow (in Nm3), the inlet pressure, the outlet pressure, the inlet
temperature, the outlet temperature and the gas composition.
The actual gas composition contains a wide range of hydrocarbons. However, only the three main components
are considered. The proportions are rounded up in a way that the sum is 100%. The gas composition is
90.66% of methane, 6.67% ethane and 2.67% propane. However, CoolProp cannot handle the mix of methane,
ethane and propane. Furthermore, considering that the three components circulate in the pipe witout mixing
result in unrealisticly low needs for heat and thus an overall efficiency of the boilers of 1.3. This can be
explained by the pressure, enthalpy charts of ethane and propane. Both are in liquid state, if pure, at the
given inlet pressure and temperature. Their enthalpy decreases between the inlet and the outlet although it
is supposed to increase because of the pre-heater while the expansion preserves enthalpy. The corresponding
graphs can be fond in Appendix. As a consequence, the natural gas is assumed to be pure methane.

7.2
TESPy and modelling of the systems
Modelling the different systems in Python requires a specific library.
Thermal Engineering Systems in
Python or TESPy is used. It allows the user to create a network by setting components such as pumps,
heat exchangers or pipes. They can then be connected the way that the user wants. Each component and
connection possesses attributes that can be fixed or left for calculation. These attributes are involved in
equations that are also inherent in the components. Once the network and the attributes are set, the set
of equations is solved by using the Jacobian matrix as an intermediate [10]. If the system can be solved,
all of the attributes of all of the components and connections are available. All of the fluid properties and
equations are handled by another library called CoolProp.
The final aim is to be able to size the different components depending on the utilization of the gas boiler as
a backup. Thus, each hour is input as if it defined the designing conditions of the systems. Once put into
the Excel model, the size of the components will be known as well as the efficiency of the newly implemented
system.

7.5.1
evaporator thawing
If the temperature of the ambient air is approximately 4°C and is somewhat humid, water will condensate on
the walls of the evaporator and freeze [22]. It insulates the heating surface of the evaporator and hinders its
performance. The two main options for thawing are electric heaters at the level of the evaporator or operating
the heat pump in cooling mode to heat the evaporator. The latter option is adequate for residential buildings,
for the indoor temperature serves as a heat source while the ice melts. This results in a decrease in COP
but the heat pump still heats the building overall. However, since the process gas needs to be heated at all
times, reversing the cycle is impossible, even for a short time. Electric heaters can be placed at the inlet of
the evaporator and allow hotter air in when frost is spotted. During the thawing period, the COP decreases
to 1. It can be assumed that the consequences of reversing the cycle described in the literature [22] should
be similar to the other method that involves electric heaters. On average, the thawing period lasts 5 minutes
and occurs every hour. Tests proved that this affects the seasonal COP by 15 %. Moreover, mal-defrost could
make the COP decrease by 40.3%. To avoid it, a second evaporator can be installed. The second evaporator
operates when the first one is thawed with electric heating. It allows the system to maintain a constant COP
throughout winter. The design of the system allows another solution to be envisaged. It consists in storing
the equivalent of 5 minutes of energy for the water loop so that the evaporator can be thawed. Since the
intermediate loop before gas already operates on hot/cold water, it is appropriate to consider a water tank
as sensible heat storage. Quantifying the extra cost of a thawing system is further explained in the economic
section 7.9.4.
The second evaporator size is the same as the main one operating in the air source heat pump.
The sensible heat water storage is designed based on the heat demand.
Since the thawing cycles last
approximately 5 minutes, the heat storage must provide one-twelfth of the maximum heat demand in kWh.
Then, based on the specific heat value of the water at 40 °C : 4.18kJ/kg, the density of water: 1000kg/m3 and
the difference in temperature of the hot and cold water, the storage tank volume can be calculated.

7.8
Quantification of start-stops
The operation of gas boilers results not only in CO2 but also in methane emissions that come from the
imperfect combustion of the fuel gas. Methane may become a major issue since it impacts 80 times as
much as carbon dioxide over 20 years [30]. While the normal operation of the boilers releases an almost
non-measurable amount of unburnt gas, starts and stops are the main sources of methane emissions, if any.
It is thus crucial to quantify these to assess properly the environmental impact of keeping the boilers as they
are. As the burners have a minimum power output, start-stops are expected when the gas demand is low,
especially in summer. Start-stop cycles are a few minutes long. 3 minutes-data of the fuel gas consumption
in Brøndby, Ellisdhøj and Taulov are provided by Energinet. Unfortunately, the figures from where Brøndby
and Ellisdhøj are given with a precision of +-1 Nm3 and are consequently not accurate enough since the
start-stops occur when the consumption hits low values. Therefore, hourly values were used for these two
stations and it was assumed only one start-stop could occur per hour. Only a lower boundary for the two
stations can then be calculated. To identify a start-stop, the minimum gas burner power, that is found on
the technical data sheets, is compared to the energy released through the combustion of natural gas. The
latter is calculated as defined in equation 7.1.3. If the minimum gas burner power is greater than the average
power delivered by the burner, one star-stop cycle is counted for Brøndby and Ellisdhøj. As far as Taulov is
concerned, the same occurrence could mean that the boiler stopped and then started again. This is further
discussed in the scenarios below.
Each station are evaluated under eight scenarios, defined by 3 differentiating factors:
• The efficiency of the burner is assumed to fluctuate between 90% in the worst scenario and 100% in
the best scenario. If the efficiency is 90%, the burner needs more gas to reach the minimal output
mentioned in the technical data sheet.
• A threshold is set to identify the cases where the burner delivered more than the minimum before
shutting down, bypassing the aforementioned test.
It is a way to find an upper boundary to the

7.9
Economic Model
To compare the value of the different options explored, several key parameters are considered: the NPV and
the minimum nominal tax per ton of CO2 that would make the NPV null, the IRR and the PBT. The savings
or expenses are calculated hourly, for the electricity price changes on the same basis, and then summed up
to make yearly values.
The inflation rate is assumed to be 2%. The discount rate is set to 3,5%, confirmed by Energinet.
All of the alternatives considered are systematically compared to what would happen if the gas boilers
were kept alone. The savings made by choosing an electrical device consist of emissions that are avoided
and therefore not taxed as well as the avoided gas consumption. The new costs consist of the electricity
consumption, the investment cost in the new solutions and the internal costs associated with the conduct
of the project by Energinet team members. Should one or several alternatives include the gas boiler as
a backup, the new gas consumption cost will be quantified as well. However, due to the newly acquired
flexibility of a dual system, the start-stop cycles and their associated methane emissions are not considered
anymore in the new scenarios. The start of operation is calculated for 2025 but can be modified, should the
decision to invest be made earlier or later.
As far as the demand pattern is concerned, it will be assumed that the demand profile is the same as 2019,
2020 and 2021 in a repetitive pattern.

90.7%, 6.7% and 2.6%. MCO2 is the molar mass of CO2: 0,044kg/mol. The second fraction can be interpreted
as the mass of CO2 emitted per kg of gas. The coefficients 1,2 and 3 are directly related to the number of
CO2 molecules that result from the combustion of methane, ethane and propane.
Then, the hourly gas consumption is calculated based on the utilization of the boiler that is input, the
hourly need for heat to the process gas and the efficiency of the stations calculated at the beginning of the
study. Multiplying the sum over the year of the natural gas consumption with the cost of natural gas and
adding the product to the total cost of emission gives the total operational cost related to gas. It should be
mentioned that the yearly savings are calculated as the operational cost of the system with utilization of the
gas boiler of 100%.

9
Discussion
The study was carried out based on three years of data. The forecast of the performance and the load may
differ from what is presented in this paper. The sensitivity analysis helps to take some of these changes into
consideration especially when the sensibility to electricity prices and gas prices are explored. Thus the study
results remain valid.
The efficiency of the gas boilers varies depending on the station. The cause of heat losses may be either at
the level of the boiler itself or the level of the circuits. In the first case, start-stops or isolation flaws may be
the core of the problem. Start-stops are expected to plunge alongside the implementation of the additional
electrical heating system. Thus, keeping the same boiler efficiency in the model is a conservative assumption.
Should the heat losses be located at the level of the water circuit itself, the insulation should be improved.
If not, it will lower the efficiency of the newly designed system. On the contrary, if the heat losses are due
to the boiler itself and start-stop cycles, then the consumption of the new system that keeps the boiler as a
backup will be even lower than predicted in this study.
The choice of ammonia as a refrigerant is motivated by environmental considerations. Traditional refrigerants
like R32 and R410A are being withdrawn from the market [37].
The new solutions under development
consist of mixing CFC with other substances to comply with new environmental policies. However, since the
CoolProp/TesPy combination of modules does not handle mixed fluids well, ammonia was chosen. According
to side simulations, choosing traditional refrigerants like R410A does not change the COP of the heat pumps
significantly. The change in COP would result in a decrease in electricity costs. Fortunately, the deviation
remains within the range of the changes in electricity prices tested in the sensitivity analysis. Thus, the
conclusions made in this study remain valid.
It should be mentioned that an off-design simulation would be appreciated, for it helps to have a more
representative idea of the performance of the designed system. This only concerns the heat pump systems
because the operation of the electric heater system fixes the inlet and outlet temperatures. Monitoring the
heat pump operation consists in controlling the gas temperature after expansion and the temperature of the
water that comes out of the condenser. The pump and the compressor ensure the response of the system. If
the temperature of the gas is too low, the volume flow rate of water increases. If the hot water temperature is
lower than expected, the compressor works more. I had the chance to visit the DTU heat pump installations
thanks to Emil from Campus Service. He gave me access to the heat pump and allowed me to see how the
components were wired. As a consequence, when it is not operating at maximum load, the temperature
of the cold water and the temperature of the refrigerant exiting the condenser are expected to be lower
while the refrigerant exiting the evaporator is expected to be hotter. Hence, the performance of the heat
pump is expected to be higher, and heat exchanges at the level of the heat exchangers are favoured in these
conditions. Keeping the COP constant in the model is a conservative assumption.
Moreover, the thermal response of the ground should be further investigated. If the borehole provides more
or less heat than assumed in this study, the evaporator may be smaller or even bigger. It will also give a
different temperature outlet at the level of the evaporator on the refrigerant side. Since most of the heat is
absorbed through phase change by the refrigerant and the cost of the evaporator is included in the cost of
the ground source heat pump, the results of this study are not affected. However, differences in the design
of the ground source heat pump are expected. Lastly, the operating pressure of the fluids in the heating
system should be subject to a balance. Indeed, a noticeable difference in pressure at the level of the heat
exchangers will shorten their operating time. The allowed pressure difference depends mainly on the design
of the heat exchanger. When the pumps operate on liquids, they need a minor amount of power compared
to the compressor and the electric heater. The COP of the heating system is likely to be lower than foreseen.
However, the sensitivity analysis made on electricity prices should be able to cover this possibility.
The company that carries the installation and the actual design of the heat pump must choose a non-
inflammable refrigerant for safety reasons. Alternatives to replace the current high GWP refrigerants are
being developed. A different kind of heat pump may be proposed by the company that installs the system.
53
==PAGE:59==
For instance, the CO2 heat pump shows promising results in space heating.
Then, mix handling by TESPy also made the implementation of brine difficult in the loops. It was substituted
with water instead. For the borehole head loss evaluation, the pressure was set in a way that water does not
freeze in the model. The actual system is more likely to perform better due to the use of brine. It shows
higher specific enthalpy and density[38]. Thus, lower volume flow rates are expected for the same amount
of heat to deliver. It will reduce the load on pumps and the size of the designed pipes.
To increase the efficiency of the heat pump systems, a second compression stage could be added. It increases
the isentropic efficiency of the whole compression stage. The response time would remain unchanged while
the system would reach a steadier state of operation slower than in a single stage configuration[39]. It was
discussed with Brian and Jierong that this would not be necessary for small heat pumps as in Taulov and
Ellidshøj. Furthermore, an internal heat exchanger could be implemented to recover heat from the refrigerant
coming out of the condenser to provide it to the refrigerant coming out of the evaporator. Thereby, the
average refrigerant temperature at the condenser would be higher and lower at the level of the evaporator.
Hence, both heat exchangers could be designed smaller for the same performance.
As mentioned in the study, only the saved direct emissions were evaluated. However, even though electricity
production is one of the most eco-friendly, it is not carbon-free yet. Energystyrelsen estimated that 200
grams of CO2 were emitted per kWh in 2020 but continues to decrease significantly over the years. It was
291 grams of CO2/kWh in 2017. The sole combustion of the natural gas emits 203grams of CO2 per kWh.
This number does not account for extraction and transport. This may be of interest to Energinet for the
choice of the technology to implement. A lot more indirect emissions shall be avoided with heat pumps
compared to the electric heater although the direct emissions are the same. There will also be a difference
in indirect CO2 emissions between the ground source heat pump and the air source heat pump whose COP
is lower. However, regarding the changes in the emissions per kWh of electricity and the global political
directions, the discrepancy between the heat pumps is expected to shrink soon. Besides, installing a ground
source heat pump may cost more than expected. Indeed, the technology catalogue from Energystyrelsen
mentions a 700000 DKK increase in the installation price for boreholes. It is taken into account if the size
of the heat pump exceeds 160kW as presented in the catalogue. However, the heat pumps considered in the
base case scenario do not exceed 21kW in the two smallest stations. It is hard to quantify how this extra cost
ramps up from one borehole to 14. This may impact the key parameters if the extra cost starts approaching
300 000DKK for a couple of boreholes. At this level, the investment cost starts being comparable to the
internal cost of conduction of the project, the main driver of investment in Ellidshøj and Taulov. Short
terms savings are impacted as well, for the investment cost play a more important part. Simulations showed
that advancing the end of operation to 2030 favours smaller electrical heating systems.
In addition to the economic and environmental parameters presented in this study, practical issues must
be considered while choosing the new system. First, since most of the M/R stations are withdrawn in the
countryside, adjustments may be needed regarding the possible electricity supply. It has been confirmed that
installing an extra 20kW capacity device would not be a problem. However, the current state of stations may
not allow having much more. It is worth mentioning that a heat pump has a lower electricity consumption
than an electric heater and thus allows the system to provide more than 20kW of heat without adapting the
electrical connection to the grid. Then, whatever the chosen option, the local technicians in each station will
have to learn how to ensure the proper operation of the new system. Since heat pumps are more elaborate
systems, becoming comfortable with maintenance procedures may take longer than an electric heater. It
should be mentioned that the Nybro M/R station is already equipped with an electric heater so the company
has experience with this kind of system. As far as the two types of heat pumps are concerned, the air source
heat pump requires more maintenance than the ground source heat pump. It shows in the costs given in the
technology catalogue from Energystyrelsen [33]. Practically speaking, in addition to the annual maintenance,
air filters and air pipes must be cleaned every 1 to 3 months [40][41]. Finally, the heat pump systems take
up more space than an electric heater or the gas boiler. Thus, additional investment may be required for
new facilities to host the systems. It is up to the Energinet teams, to determine how much more they are
54
==PAGE:60==
ready to invest for a new building, based on the NPV difference between the electric heater and the heat
pumps and an on-site assessment.
Regarding how to read the economic parameters, although ground source heat pumps show the highest NPV,
they also require more investment from Energinet. Furthermore, the difference in NPV with air source heat
pumps is not significant enough to make it steadily stand out in the sensitivity analysis. On the contrary,
the air source heat pumps present a higher IRR than the ground source heat pump in most cases. As a
consequence, if Energinet is ready to invest enough money to equip all of the stations with ground source
heat pumps, they will most probably have the highest return on investment in the long term. Otherwise, it
is economically and environmentally better to generalise the installation of air-source heat pumps to as many
stations as possible. The stations would be equipped with both the gas boiler as a backup with the air source
heat pump as a main source of heat. If Energinet wants to remove the gas boiler entirely, ground source heat
pumps are, by far, the best option. It must be kept in mind that the best size of the new installation depends
on the load duration curve, the technology and the parameter that is chosen to be maximized.
Alongside the air source heat pump, a second evaporator or sensible heat storage is necessary for the proper
operation of the heat pump during winter. Sensible heat storage shows more cost-efficient in most cases.
However, its size is highly dependent on the temperatures that are set for the system and may require an
electric buffer to allow Energinet to save some space. Furthermore, the heat exchanger cost seems overes-
timated. There were indeed some cases where the second heat exchanger happened to be more expensive
than the whole heat pump. Energinet can expect lower costs than predicted if the second evaporator is
chosen.
If the end of the contract of Energinet is advanced to 2030, the payback time should be observed more
closely, as all alternatives are not equal. Most options remain cost-effective if they start operating in 2024
but it can become more delicate if the heat systems are built later. The gas price developments should be
paid special attention to due to the political disagreements between Russia and Ukraine and their impact
on future energy strategies. Current policies lead to electrification and are expected to persevere in this
direction, which increases further the cost-effectiveness of the alternatives presented in this study.
10
Conclusion
The current gas boilers have efficiencies that are between 60 and 75%. Some like Taulov can drop down to
30%. Ensuring that the piping isolation is not the cause of the heat losses that occur is necessary to ensure
the reliability of the results of this study.
Based on the economic and environmental potential analysis of the different alternative solutions, in can
be stated that any electrical heating system would be beneficial to both Energinet and the environment.
The heat pump solutions stand out regarding the key parameters under scrutiny. They also present greater
profitability stability regarding changes in electricity prices. It is although difficult to discriminate the ground
source heat pump and the air source heat pump based on these factors. If Energinet can afford to implement
ground source heat pumps in its stations, they will more likely have more returns on investment and easier
maintenance. If their budget is more limited, air source heat pumps with the old gas boiler as back-up are
preferred, as these can be more generalized and be more profitable under these conditions. If Energinet
prefers to remove the gas boiler entirely, the ground source heat pump is the best option.
If a limited number of stations can be investigated and changed before it becomes too expensive to do, the
largest stations with the poorest gas boiler efficiency should be identified and prioritized. The size may
become a more minor criterion of choosing if the internal costs of project conduction scale down.
Depending on the choice of Energinet, the optimal installation size changes depending on the station. Ground
source heat pumps are favoured by higher capacities than air source heat pumps.
Should the air source heat pump be chosen as the new heating system, a system for evaporator thawing must
55
==PAGE:61==
be implemented. Installing a second evaporator tends to be a more cost-effective option than a sensible heat
storage.
Whether Energinet selects the ground or air source heat pump. Free heating is not a viable option in terms
of economics. It also allows little electricity savings.
