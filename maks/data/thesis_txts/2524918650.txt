Abstract 
Background: Treatment of cancer has been challenging in the recent years. One of the 
successful immunotherapies is neutralizing antibodies targeting immune checkpoint proteins 
(CTLA-4). T cells are the prime immune cells that response to cancer, by recognizing 
mutations in peptides. However, some regions of TCRs, which is not encoded in the genome 
but generated by random recombination, can, by chance produced as epitope and be recognized 
as non-self by the immune system. Therefore, analyzing the T cell epitopes repertoire and the 
mode of action of the immune system towards the T cell epitopes is the main focus of this 
study.  
Objective: The goal is to analyze the amount of T cell epitopes before and after 
immunotherapy. Furthermore, to examine whether the smaller number of epitopes that T cells 
in the repertoire are reacting to is the result of deletion by the immune system. Alternatively, 
investigate whether the expansion of T cells in the repertoire could be an outcome of protection 
by the checkpoint proteins.  
Method: T cell epitopes repertoire of four NSCLC patients, patient 4, 32, 36 and 38,  whose 
alleles are known and are received Ipilimumab, which is a CTLA-4 inhibitor, is analyzed in 
the pre and post-treatment of immunotherapy, using bioinformatic tools and MHC prediction 
epitopes method, NetMHCpan. Patient 4 had a CR, patient 32 has SD, patient 36 and 38 had 
PD.   
Result: The results vary among four different patients and alleles. A large amount of T cells 
repertoire is observed in the pre- and post-treatment of the patient, who has recovered from the 
disease. In addition, the amount of T cells repertoire is increased after immunotherapy. For two 
patients, who have stable disease and progression in cancer, the amount of T cells repertoire is 
low before and after the treatment. Besides, the number of T cells repertoire remains unchanged 
during the process. For one patient, where the disease has progressed, a large amount of T cells 
repertoire is observed in pre- and post-immunotherapy; however, the number of T cell epitopes 
decreases after immunotherapy.  
Conclusion: Increase of the T cell epitopes repertoire after receiving CTLA-4 inhibition in the 
recovered patient is congruent with other studies finding [1]. This might be due to the fact that 
checkpoint proteins protect the T cells during the treatment or before they accumulate in the

site of the tumor.  Decreasing of the T cell epitopes repertoire is affected by many mechanisms, 
such as impairment in the process of epitope's degradation before presenting the epitopes to the 
T cells, which results in unstable T cells - MHC complex and thus, elimination of the T cells. 
Moreover, the interaction of T cells with epitopes with a high binding affinity causes the 
deletion of the T cells repertoire. Furthermore, patients with different allele response differently 
to immunotherapy. This finding raises the theory that T cell epitopes might be immunogenic 
to some patients with certain alleles, which results in inducing the immune system and deletion 
of T cells. Because the immune system is continually learning, recognition of TCRs region as 
self or non-self might be time-consuming for the immune response.

1- Project description 
Tumor cells, which would normally be recognized by T cells, develop ways to exploit the 
immune checkpoints as a way to evade immune detection or elimination. Therefore, immune 
checkpoint inhibition therapy has significantly increased interest in the treatment of cancer 
patients [2].  
During immunotherapy, patients receiving checkpoint inhibitor, especially as cytotoxic T-
lymphocyte antigen-4 checkpoint inhibitor, experience changes in their T cells repertoires [3]. 
The inhibition of checkpoint proteins leads to the expansion of the T cell receptor repertoire 
because of the new T cell epitopes.  
T cell receptor through VDJ recombination process creates regions that are not encoded in the 
genome but are produced through random Deoxyribonucleic acid rearrangement, especially 
complementarity-determining 3 region; thus, the T cells may be recognized as immunogenic 
and eliminated by the immune system. However, T cells might express some signals that mark 
themselves as not immunogenic to the immune system, the same signals that are released by 
the tumor cells, and therefore, the immune system does not eliminate them.  
In order to understand the modes of action of this type of immune system, the complementarity-
determining 3 regions of the T cell receptor beta-chain in the T cell repertoire before and after 
checkpoint inhibition for the major histocompatibility complex alleles of non-small cell lung 
carcinoma cancer patients are analyzed using computational approach and standard 
bioinformatic T cell epitope prediction tool.  
1.2- Aim of this study 
This project provides an overview of how checkpoint inhibition immunotherapy leads to the 
change of T cell receptor repertoire. 
There are two hypotheses:  
1- Observing few epitopes before and after immunotherapy indicates the fact that there is 
self-deletion of the T cells, and the immunogenic T cells are eliminated by the immune 
system.

2- A large amount of the T cell epitopes in pre- and post-immunotherapy illustrates the 
theory that the T cells are protected by some checkpoint proteins before 
immunotherapy.

2-Introduction 
In the recent years, cancer immunotherapy has proven to be challenging in the development 
and implementation, as it depends on overcoming multiple mechanisms that prevent the 
therapy.  
A growing understanding of immune response has been a foundation for improving new 
methods of cancer immunotherapy. T cells are the prime immune cells in response to cancer, 
although they may be aided or hindered by other immune cells. Immune checkpoint inhibitors 
have led to long-lasting effect in the cancer patients [4]. 
This study, therefore, is designed to focus on the T cell epitopes repertoire and how the 
checkpoint proteins affect the change in the T cells repertoire.   
 
2.1- Background 
Cytotoxic CD8+  T lymphocytes are the immune cells that are effective at recognition and 
thereby elimination of the tumor-specific antigens displayed in major histocompatibility 
complex (MHC) class I molecules, which are presented on the surface of antigen-presenting 
cell (APC) [5].  
The persistent high antigenic load leads to the T cells exhaustion, which occurs during cancer, 
in which the T cells become functionally unresponsive to further antigen stimulation [6]. To 
prevent multi autoimmune reactions, the activation of the T cells is normally suppressed by 
multiple mechanisms of peripheral tolerance, a pathway called immunological checkpoint. 
Negative immunological checkpoints are provided by inhibitory receptors such as cytotoxic T-
lymphocyte antigen-4 (CTLA-4) and programmed death-1 (PD-1) [7]. Immune checkpoint 
inhibitor target CTLA-4 can promote clinical response. 
CD8+ T cells epitopes generate through random rearrangement of complementarity-
determining 3 regions (CDR3). When these CDR3 regions are present through the cloning, 
there will be enough copies to trigger the immune system response; however, the CDR3 regions 
have not been demonstrated to induce the immune response. 
==PAGE:13==

13 
 
Finding a way to increase the immune system’s capacity to mount a large, tumor-specific T 
cells is crucial to defend the body against cancer. Recognition of the T cell receptor (TCR) 
CDR3 region as immunogenic will result in an immune system that is not targeted the tumors.  
Furthermore, there are many shared T cells between the normal tissue and the tumor, which 
are fighting the viruses and not the cancer cells. Therefore, tracking the change of T cells 
epitope repertoire and understating the mode of action of the immune system can provide high-
value information for clinical monitoring of immune function status of cancer patients and 
additionally, helps to reduce the side effect of the CTLA-4 blockade monoclonal antibody 
(mAB) drug, by removing the immunogenic epitopes. 
 
2.2- Non-small-cell lung carcinoma cancer 
Non-small-cell lung carcinoma (NSCLC) is a type of epithelial lung cancer accounts for about 
85% of all lung cancers 29. The disorders which inhabits the anti-cancer immune response are 
correlated to age with the most significant risk in the oldest patients, group of both sex. Other 
environmental factors that affect the immune response are such as dust, allergens, and microbe, 
with resulting oxidative stress and hypoxia [8]. A study examined by Jianjun Zhang [9] 
indicated that the tumors have both greater density and richness of the T cells than normal lung 
tissue; however, the normal tissue has more activated T cells.   
 
2.3- Structure of T cell  
TCR consists of two different polypeptide chains. In human, the majority of T cells express a 
TCR composed of TCR-alfa and TCR-beta chains, linked by a disulfide bond. A smaller subset 
of T cells expresses a TCR with gamma and delta chains. The extracellular portion of each 
chain consists of a variable (V) region, a constant (C) region, and a short stalk segment 
containing a cysteine residue that forms the interchain disulfide bond. Both chains have a 
carbohydrate side chains attached to each domain.  Each chain spans the lipid bilayer by a 
hydrophobic transmembrane domain and ends in a short cytoplasmic tail (figure 1) [10].

2.4- T cell development 
TCRs are generated through a random process of Deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) 
recombination, which involves rearrangement of V (variable segment), D (diversity segment), 
and J (joining segment) and the deletion and insertion of nucleotides at the V(D)J junctions. 
 When the progenitor T cells migrate from the bone marrow into the thymus, they lack CD4, 
CD8, and TCR complex molecules, and additionally, receptor genes are not rearranged. 
The first stage of development of the T lymphocytes is the double-negative cell, where CD4 or 
CD8 are not expressed.  Rearrangement of beta/delta chain locus begins in the double-negative 
stage through VDJ recombination on the DNA of the beta/delta-chain, where the beta/delta-
chain locus undergoes DJ rearrangement before VD recombination (figure 2). Following the 
process, the rearranged cells interact with thymic epithelial cells and become thymocyte and 
thereby progress to the double-positive stage, in which they proliferate. Whereas the cells that 
fail to make a successful rearrangement do not survive.  In the double-positive stage, the 
alfa/gamma chains locus’s VJ rearrangement begins. Recombination of alfa/gamma and 
beta/delta chains is regulated by recombinase-activating genes RAG-1 and RAG-2, which are

strand breaks at the recombination signal sequence (RSS), which flank all TCRs locus

The purpose of recombination is to increase the diversity among the CDRs. The variable 
domain of TCRs alfa and beta chains consists of CDR1, CDR2, and CDR3 loops. The center 
of the antigen-binding site is formed by CDR3 of the TCR alfa and beta chains, to which the 
D and J locus segments contribute. The highly variable CDR3 region contacts mainly the 
unique peptide on MHC molecules [13]. Whereas the CDR1 and CDR2 regions that are more 
conserved, and the top of MHC contact the surrounded parts of the CDR3-antigen region. TCR 
V segment region is specific for MHC molecules; thus, most of the binding interface engages 
in contact with the CDR1 and CDR2 TCR regions, regardless of the peptide presented [14].  
The thymocytes initially express CD4+ , CD8+ TCR, and CTLA-4 at the double-positive stage, 
which have specificity for MHC class II and I respectively [15]. During and after the double-
positive stage, thymocytes undergo negative selection. The T cells that recognize self-peptide: 
self-MHC molecular complex, will be positively selected and go on to the mature stage,

2.5- MHC and alleles  
MHC class I present the peptides derived from proteins synthesized in the cytosol, and thus, 
they are able to display the epitope of viral peptide on the surface of the cell to CD8 cytotoxic 
T cells. When a pathogen invades a cell, it makes its cytosolic protein in the cell. These 
cytosolic proteins are degraded by proteasome complex into small peptide fragments, in which 
they produce the proper sized antigens for loading into MHC class I [20]. Thereby, the peptides 
created by proteasome are transported by a peptide Transporter associated with Antigen

Processing (TAP) into the gate of endoplasmic reticulum (ER). At this stage, the ribosome 
produces MHC class I molecules, which is not complete yet, but only has alfa1, alfa2 and alfa3 
domains, where alfa1 and alfa2 are the variable regions of MHC class I, which increase the 
specificity of the antigen-binding site. This incomplete protein of MHC class I molecule binds 
to a protein called calnexin, which helps MHC class I to go through the ER. Following this 
process, another protein called macroglobulin Beta2m attaches to the incomplete MHC class I. 
The MHC class I is subsequently completed and is consisted of alfa1, alfa2, alfa3, and beta 2 
domains. The protein ERp57 is attached to MHC class I, which covers the binding sides of 
MHC class I in order to prevent loading of peptide antigen to the binding site of MHC class I. 
Furthermore, this protein helps MHC class I to move towards the gate of ER by TAP 
transporter, where another protein called tapasin interacts with MHC class I. After the 
interaction with tapasin, ERp57 protein is removed, which will allow the peptides load onto 
the binding site of MHC class I.  When an antigen attaches to MHC class I, the complex 
antigen: MHC class I leave the ER and move towards the surface of an infected cell, and present 
the antigen to CD8 T cells [21].   
Based on the fact that viruses can infect any nucleated cell, MHC class I molecules are 
expressed on most cells of the body. The level of constitutive expression varies from one cell 
type to another one. For instance, cells of the immune system express abundant MHC class I 
on their surface, whereas liver cells (hepatocytes) express relatively low levels. 
The genes encoding MHC class I molecules are linked within the protein complex. This protein 
complex is encoded by human leukocyte antigen gene complex (HLA). The proteins encoded 
by HLAs are unique for each person, and thus, the immune system uses HLAs to recognize 
self and non-self-cells. The differences in MHC molecules for each person are based on the 
polygenicity and polymorphism. Polygenicity means that MHC class I molecules are coded by 
three independent genes called HLA-A, -B, and -C. The term allele frequency is referred to the 
relative frequency of an allele at a genetic locus in a population. Moreover, polymorphism 
means that multiple stable form of each HLA gene exists in the population [22]. Polymorphism 
helps to increase the diversity of interaction with many different epitopes and, thus, protect the 
population.

While beta 2 macroglobulin domain of MHC class I molecule is not polymorphic, the alpha 
domain is highly polymorphic and encoded in the HLA gene family and therefore, there are 
hundreds of different alleles of MHC class I molecules in the human population [23].  
The association of MHC genotype with disease is assessed initially by comparing the frequency 
of different alleles in patients with their frequency in the normal population. Depending on 
each patient carrying protective or susceptibility alleles, human antibody therapeutic can be 
immunogenic for some and non-immunogenic for the others [24].  
 
2.6- T cell epitopes  
TCRs bind to short continuous amino acid sequences called peptides, which are presented on 
the surface of MHC. These sequences are often buried within the native structure of the protein 
and, thus, cannot be recognized directly by TCR. TCR does not recognize the antigenic protein 
itself, but a peptide fragment of the protein called epitope. Therefore, the protein needs to be 
unfolded and processed into peptide fragments, and thereby bound to an MHC molecule and 
create a complex of peptide and MHC molecule. There are some positions in the peptide: MHC 
complex called anchor, in which the interaction is very strong and consist of some specific 
amino acids that are conserved enough to tolerate the capture of epitope into MHC class I. The 
studies demonstrated that anchor position of each peptide is usually on position 2 and position 
9.[25]. 
T cell epitopes presented by MHC class I have a length of 8-11 peptides; however, the vast 
majority of the peptide has a length of 9 [26]. Many properties define a T cell epitope; it must 
be degraded by proteasomal cleavage, it must bind to MHC molecule with a strong binding 
affinity, the MHC: peptide complex must be stable enough to be able to transfer to the surface 
of the cell and interact with the specific T cells repertoire [27].  
T cell epitopes of cancer fall into two categories; the epitopes that can be seen by T cells 
repertoire and are identical between normal and tumor cells, and second, the epitopes that are 
specific to tumor cells and cannot be detected in normal cells, due to the tumor-associated 
mutation or other genetic events [28].

2.7- Immunological checkpoint 
The studies show a decrease in tumor growth in NSCL cancer with CTLA-4 pathway blockade. 
Ipilimumab is a mAB that binds to CTLA-4 and, thus, blocks its interaction with B7 and allows 
T cells to proliferate [29].   
CTLA-4 is rapidly expressed on the surface of T cells and imposed a checkpoint by binding to 
B7 molecules on APC, which leads to inhibition of cell cycle progression and decreasing IL-2 
production, a cytokine known for stimulating T-cell proliferation. Thereby CTLA-4 delivers a 
negative signal that must be overcome by other signals before T cells can get activated [30].  
T cells co-stimulatory molecule CD28, provides a co-stimulatory signal that is critical for the 
proliferation of naïve T cells. CD28 interacts with the ligands B7-1(CD80) and B7-2 (CD86) 
on the surface of APCs, which results in the dephosphorylation of TCR signaling protein such 
as CD3, leading to T cell inhibition. CTLA-4 shares the ligands B7-1 and B7-2 with CD28 
[31]. 
The co-stimulatory signal released by CD28 primes upregulate the expression of CTLA-4 and 
PD-1 (4). CTLA-4 expression on the T cells can also perform an inhibitory effect on the 
activation of other T cells. The action of this process is not yet explicit, but it might result from 
CLTA-4 binding to B7 on the APC, which steals the effect of CD28 required by the other T 
cells to bind to the APC [32].  
The CTLA-4 blockade impacts two stages of T cell activation. The first stage is in the draining 
lymph nodes, where CTLA-4 is expressed by regulatory T cells [33], and the second blockade 
takes effect at the tumor microenvironment as exhausted T cells express CTLA-4 [34]. Notably, 
the regulatory T cells that are needed to suppress the activation of the immune system in the 
presence of non-self cells, express high levels of CTLA-4 on their surface, and they require 
CTLA-4 to function normally [35]. 
Similarly, PD-1 has also been described as a negative checkpoint and interacts with 
programmed death ligand (PD-L1) and PD-L2 and B7 [36]. Most of circulating T cells do not 
express PD-1; however, they can be induced to express the receptor through exposure to 
cytokines such as IL-2, IL-7, IL-15, and IL-21[37] . The study by Wang et al., 2011 suggested 
that CD8 express substantial levels of PD-1 in the primary stage of cancer. The expression of

2.8- Cancer cells evade the immune system 
Lung cancers are unstable and are defined as a result of multiple genetic alterations. The tumors 
evade the immune response by low antigen presentation and low co-stimulatory molecule 
expression [40] . Cancer cells adopt a mechanism to evade the immune system, in which they 
take advantage of immunological checkpoint, that the body has evolved and prevent 
immunotherapy [41].  
Recent evidence indicated that tumors can occasionally express their immunogenic antigen, 
which results from the oncogenic driver mutations that initiate tumor progression [42]. The 
mouse models have demonstrated that CD8 T cells can recognize these tumors associated 
antigens, thus, one of the ways in which tumors can evade the immune system is by alteration 
of antigen-presenting and downregulation of MHC class I expression on their surface. Analysis 
of MHC class I expression on tumors demonstrated that between 40% and 90% of tumors 
displayed total or partial loss of MHC class molecules [43]. Complete loss of MHC class I is 
most often associated with mutations in the gene encoding beta2-microglobulin subunit or 
mutations in members of the HLA gene family. In addition, a complete loss would also occur, 
where the tumor cells decrease the function of MHC class I by downregulating the expression 
of HLA genes in the absence of mutations [21]. Interestingly, many studies suggest that 
alteration in the regulatory mechanism of MHC class I, where antigen loads onto MHC class 
in ER, led to an impairment of the immune system, rather than a mutation in the genes 
themselves.    
Another common mechanism by which tumor cells evade the immune system is by 
manipulation of antigen processing and, thus, alteration of their antigen presentation, which 
results in reducing the immunogenicity. This process does not change the expression of MHC 
==PAGE:21==
  
 
Alla H. Dahir 
 
21 
 
class I [44] . Tumor cells evade T cells by alteration in tumor-associated antigen expression, 
modifying their epitopes or masking them using "immunodominant" antigens, to which the 
majority of the immune response is directed [45].  
Using immune checkpoint pathways by tumors is a significant mechanism for resistance to the 
immune system. Activated T cells secrete the inflammatory cytokine IFN-gamma, which 
promotes PD-L1 or PD-L2 expression in the tumor cells. After the recognition of tumor 
antigens by T cells, PD-L1/PD-L2 of tumor cells bind to the PD-1 receptor on the activated T 
cells and block TCR signaling by immunological checkpoint, and thereby inhibit T cell 
survival, proliferation and immune function [46], figure 4.

2.9- Epitope prediction  
T cell epitopes are presented in the binding groove of MHC class I molecules [48]. The primary 
step of T cell epitope prediction is to predict the likelihood of a peptide to bind to MHC 
molecules.  
==PAGE:22==
  
 
Alla H. Dahir 
 
22 
 
The most straightforward rule for epitope prediction of each specific peptide is based on the 
analyzing the properties and finding the HLA motif of each amino acid in the peptide. For 
instance, the amount and the position of hydrophobic amino acids in the peptide indicate the 
high frequency of these amino acids. By finding these preferences and conserve amino acids 
in each peptide, it will be possible to screen through the pathogen genome and find the peptide 
that has the same properties and predict that these peptides will be likely binders to the specific 
MHC molecules.  Calculation of information content (I), which identifies essential amino acid 
for function and structural stability and defines the frequency of amino acid type in a given 
peptide and thereby, creating a frequency matrix, and a sequence logo, is a simple way to 
predict the position of each amino acid, in which the conserved amino acid has a high value of 
information content. Based on this information, the prediction score can subsequently be 
calculated.  
However, it is challenging to predict the raw sequences of peptides with bias. There are some 
approaches that help to improve the prediction of each peptide in a given database. Weights 
are computed for each peptide segment contained in the database. The weights are given to the 
redundant and overrepresentation sequences, which are resulted from the bias in the lab, that 
for instance investigate the impact of mutating in an amino acid on the binding strength. 
Sequence weighting, which means taking highly similar peptides and count them as one is an 
excellent approach to reduce the bias and data redundancy. Another approach is based on 
finding the best substitute amino acid in each specific position. This can be calculated by 
pseudo counts, where the similarity of the structure of side chains of different amino acids are 
defined, and thereby, by creating the BLOSUM (substitution frequency) matrix, the most 
similar amino acid, which still maintains the protein function and structure can be replaced. 
Furthermore, by creating a weight matrix applying sequencing weighting and pseudo count 
approaches, it is possible to estimate the frequency of each amino acid in each position in the 
peptide. It should be noted that, a large number of a frequency of a specific amino acid, does 
not mean that this amino acid is more important for the interaction compare to other amino 
acids, because in nature all amino acids are not found with equal frequency. Therefore, the final 
frequency must be calculated as the frequency of a specific amino acid that is resulted from all 
the mathematical approaches divided by the frequency of that amino acid in the nature.  
 However, these tools of epitope prediction are proper for a small number of peptides.  
==PAGE:23==
  
 
Alla H. Dahir 
 
23 
 
In the peptide, the binding affinity of an amino acid at a specific position can be influenced by 
other amino acids at the next positions, a process called sequence correlations. ANN can take 
high-order sequence correlations into account [27]. In order to apply ANN, a mathematical 
model is trained using statistical methods, which are guided by minimizing the errors on the 
so-called training dataset and predict the peptide based on the target measurement. Creating a 
model is a crucial matter because the model must capture the signal and not the noise, and if 
the model is overfitted, where the noise is zero, the model will not be useful for new data [27]. 
 
2.9.1- Artificial neural network  
Artificial neural network (ANN) is the best data-driven bioinformatics approach to recognize 
the patterns. ANN is a particular type of computing system that consists of connected units 
called artificial neurons, which are modeled based on the human brain. Each of these neurons 
receives a signal and transmit the signal to the neurons connected to it. The ANN is able to 
learn from the data, without being programmed with task-specific rules and thereby provide 
responses in the form of predictions. There are three layers in ANN; input layers, which receive 
the input information, the hidden layers, which perform the mathematical computation, and the 
output layers, which obtain the result. All these layers are connected in a given structure 
through weighed connection; these synaptic weights define the importance of the function to 
the input layer in the way that if the sum of the weight of each neuron is larger than the 
threshold, the neuron activated [49]. 
 
2.9.2- NetMHCpan 
 A large number of T cell epitopes-mapping algorithms have been developed to rapidly identify 
the T cell epitopes [50] . Among numerous tools for MHC class I binding predictors included 
NetMHCpan, which uses two methods; allele-specific approach, where separate predictors are 
trained for each specific MHC allele [51] and pan-specific, where a single approach is trained 
on data covering multiple MHC molecules. Each MHC molecule has a structure that is highly 
conserved among species. Two similar MHC molecules would likely share the same function, 
although they have different binding affinity. Based on this information, it is possible to define 
the NetMHCpan trained data based on the binding affinity of the peptide sequence and, 
==PAGE:24==
  
 
Alla H. Dahir 
 
24 
 
additionally, based on the information, which is dictated by the peptide sequence environment. 
Pan-method is a very powerful tool, considering the huge polymorphism of HLA genes, as they 
predict all MHC molecules, even those with limited or no binding data [52]. 
The peptide length differs between MHC molecules. NetMHCpan uses neural networks trained 
on any length other than nine mers. Furthermore, it uses allele-specific method, which creates 
an alignment allowing insertions/deletions in the peptide data and thereby integrate information 
of peptide with different lengths into one prediction method [52].  
NetMHCpan predicts peptide binding to MHC class I and II molecules from the protein 
sequence or sequence alignment using ANN, which is trained based in a five-fold cross-
validation manner. Each of these five different models has different training and test 
performance subsets for binding affinity prediction.  
Furthermore, the data-driven method for NetMHCpan binding prediction is based on the 
affinity measurement deposited in the immune epitope database (IEDB) [53].

3.2- Preprocessing the raw sequence data 
The data was sequenced on an Illumina HiSeq 2500 using paired-end sequencing method [55]. 
The raw RNA-seq data have been deposited at the NCBI Sequence Read Archive (Accession 
number SPP136187. The length of the left and the right reads of the raw data is 101 base pairs 
(bps). SPR files are downloaded using specific bash commands, and the data is converted to 
fastq format using fastq-dump. The result of running fastq-dump is as interleaved Fastq format. 
It means that the left and the right reads alternate in the file and are in order of the left reads 
are first printed (4 lines), followed by the right reads; then the second 4 left reads, then the 
second 4 right reads. Due to the fact that the order of the right and the left reads is known, a 
bash command is used to separate them. (Appendix 1). 
In RNA-seq, the idea of merging before or after trimming is depend on the purpose of the 
function of the analysis. When merging occurs before trimming, due to the bad quality, many 
of the bps are removed. For some studies, for instance, identifying variance, an error in one bp 
gives many false positives, and additionally, it is difficult to examine whether the error is a 
mutation or not. However, in merging, the software can handle one error in 100 bps by 
overlapping the bps. Moreover, trimming before merging cut the bp, and thus, there will not be 
==PAGE:29==
  
 
Alla H. Dahir 
 
29 
 
enough data to analyze the epitopes in the CDR3. Based on this knowledge, the data is merged 
before trimming.   
Fast Length Adjustment of SHort reads (FLASH), which is a high-speed and accurate software 
tool to find the correct overlap between paired-end reads and merging them, is used for merging 
[56]. Generally, the minimum required overlap length between the left and the right reads, is 
ten bp. The maximum allowed mismatch ratio is 0.25. However, 25% mismatch of 10 bp is a 
large number when the reads contain repetitive regions. When trimming occurs, the minimum 
quality threshold must be between 10 and 20, where for instance, 15% is equal to 1% error, 
which means that in every 100 bp, one bp might be wrong. Nevertheless, due to the purpose of 
the function of this study, 0.25 is a decent number (Appendix 1). The data is trimmed by 
removing the wrong bases and adaptors from the left and the right reads. Due to the lack of 
time, it is not possible to trim the whole data.  
To assess the quality of the raw sequence data, FastQC is applied (Appendix 1). However, for 
lack of time, we did not use the data filtering procedure presented here, but we used a publicly 
available pre-processed dataset for further analysis.  
 
3.3- Preparing the processed data 
In order to obtain a more reliable result, the T cell epitopes repertoire are examined in the 
TCRB CDR3 region and, additionally, in ten amino acids before and after the CDR3 region. 
The TCRB CDR3 sequence data collected from ImmunoSEQ ANALYZER [57], which 
contains only the TCRB CDR3 regions of the human gene. The VJ segments of the ten amino 
acids before and after TCRB CDR3 regions in the sequencing reads were defined according to 
ImMunoGeneTics (IMGT/GENE-DB) collaboration. A script is used to identify which V&J 
segments of the ten amino acids contributed to before and after each TCRB CDR3 regions. 
(Appendix 2). Subsequently,  based on the name of the gene, the matched sequences of the 
TCRB CDR3 regions and V&J segments data of 20 amino acids are appended, in which the 
first sequences are from V segment, which are assembled to TCRB CDR3 and thereby aligned 
to the sequences of J segment (Supplementary 1). The result is a Datafile containing 76 
different filenames. A script is used to extract each filename from the Data file. (Supplementary 
2). Afterward, the 76 files are converted to a fasta file (Appendix 2).

5- Discussion 
Insufficient understanding of how the immune system reacts towards the T cells is a barrier to 
advancing immunotherapy for cancer. 
This study is conducted to investigate the elimination or survival of CDR3 in T cells beta-chain 
repertoire before and after immunotherapy for NCLSC patients. The study included four 
patients. To increase the reliability of the epitope detection, ten amino acids before and after 
TCRB CDR3 are also analyzed. The idea was to create an artificial T cell to compare the results 
of the amount of T cells repertoire of the patient with, but unfortunately, due to the lack of 
time, it was not possible to create one and therefore, the average of the strong and weak binders 
is used as a comparison of the results. 
The variation of results between the cancer patients is high. There is only one patient that 
recovered from the disease, and the data of this patient revealed an expansion of T cells before 
and after immunotherapy in 4 of 5 alleles. Moreover, T cells repertoire are increased in one 
allele during the immunotherapy. Whereas the data of the patients 32 and 36 who have stable 
and progressed disease, respectively, revealed a low number of T cells repertoire in pre- and 
post-treatment. Previous studies support this finding, showed that the number of T cells 
repertoire is relevant for the anti-tumor response after CTLA-4 inhibition [1]. 
On the other hand, an expansion of the T cell epitopes is observed in the data of patient 38, 
who has a progression in the disease. However, the number of T cells repertoire decreases in 3 
of 6 alleles of this patient after the immunotherapy. This finding suggests that T cells repertoire 
are protected by immune cells before the immunotherapy but are eliminated during the 
immunotherapy by the immune system, considering the immunogenicity of TCRB CDR3. 
However, the analyzed data of patient 38 was collected at day 22, where the course of treatment 
was not completed yet. Thus, there might be an increase of T cell epitopes at the later stage of 
the immunotherapy.  
A study published by Mok et al., 2015 suggests that an increases in the diversity of TCR V-
beta chain CDR3 in peripheral blood might indicate the theory that blockade of CTLA-4 is 
more related to the general ability of the patient's immune response to be activated, rather than 
a correlation with tumor response [59]. Thus, gaining knowledge about how the immune

system impact on each point of immunotherapy is essential. According to a study published by 
Huang et al., 2015 the expression of checkpoint proteins in cancer appears to help the tumor 
escape host immune response, while the expression of checkpoint proteins in the immune cells 
around the tumor, appears to be a host response to the tumor [60]. The samples for this study 
were collected from the peripheral blood, which means that the T cells repertoire have an 
adequate response and somehow needed to be protected by immune system.  
Tumor antigens include normal proteins overexpressed by cancer cells and new proteins that 
are generated by mutations and gene rearrangement [61]. When analyzing the immunogenicity 
of TCRB CDR3, the mechanism of antigen-presenting has to be considered.  
 
5.1- Shared epitopes 
The theory of the ability of shared epitopes to immunize and generate T cell responses against 
cancer is essential. The fact that these shared epitopes are not tumor specific may lead to the 
result that these epitopes are not immunogenic enough to induce the immune system and are 
protected by the immune system. This indicates that the T cells presented these shared epitopes 
are also protected by the immune system and not eliminated, despite the great diversity of VDJ 
recombination of TCRB CDR3. 
Conversely, the epitopes, which are tumor-specific by mutation in normal sequence, is 
problematic as well. 
 
5.2- Mutation 
The degree of mutation of proteins by tumor cells is essential among the cancer patients. It is 
known in the field of cancer disease that an individual amino acid within peptide can affect 
immunogenicity by altering peptide-MHC or peptide- TCR repertoire interactions [62]. The 
random mutation of the normal sequence by tumor cells yields unique antigen; however, 
mutations in the antigen lead to overexpressing of T cells. The altered and overexpressing 
amino acids from a mutation can result in a non-self-peptide that are more likely considered as 
"self" [63]. The number of mutant tumor-specific antigens increases exponentially with 
increasing the size of the tumor. The large amount and increasing of the T cells repertoire 
during the immunotherapy indicate that the T cells are protected from the immune system in 
==PAGE:43==
  
 
Alla H. Dahir 
 
43 
 
patient 4. However, in the patient 38, although there is a large amount of T cells repertoire, but 
the T cells are decreasing during the immunotherapy. This finding indicates that the 
characterization of a tumor itself may also influence the expansion of the T cells repertoire. 
The mutational burden of tumor cells may increase their antigenicity but may also result in 
enhancing the immunogenicity of patient 38 tumor cells, which result in overexpressing of T 
cells and, thus, eliminated by the immune system. 
Interestingly, a study published by Matzinger et al., 1994 [64] suggests that there are always 
cells that are able to respond to immunized self-antigens if presented at high enough levels by 
APC. Furthermore, he suggested that the highly concentrated antigens with a high binding 
affinity may induce clonal deletion of T cells repertoire, rather than T cells expansion during 
the immune response. Based on these facts, it is difficult to argue whether the immune system 
eliminates the T cells repertoire because of interaction with highly concentrated antigens or 
due to overexpressing of the possible epitopes which are generated from a great diversity of 
VDJ recombination of TCRB CDR3.  
 
5.3- Mechanism of antigen-presenting   
Defect in the mechanism of peptide degradation pathway and antigen presentation to CD8 TCR 
by tumor cells also result in the deletion of T cells. The peptide epitopes present on the surface 
of tumor cells with impaired TAP function derived from self-peptide, act as immunogenic non-
self-peptide [65]. The T cells that present these peptides can be recognized as non-self-proteins 
and be eliminated by the immune system. Thus, a self-deletion of T cells might be due to the 
presence of these epitopes on TCRB CDR3.    
Another critical aspect for the deletion of the T cells epitopes is the tumor environment. 
Stimulation of APC by tumor cells, resulting in inadequate expression of MHC class I and thus, 
APC cannot fully engage with TCR, which results in T cells deletion [66].    
 
5.4- Mechanism of checkpoint proteins 
In this study, the expression of checkpoint proteins CTLA-4 is inhibited; however, the 
checkpoint proteins PDl-1 is still expressing on the T cells. A theoretical point of potential 
interest is whether CTLA-4 protects the T cells before the immunotherapy or by PD-1 during 
==PAGE:44==
  
 
Alla H. Dahir 
 
44 
 
the immunotherapy. The possible theory might be that because the immunosuppressive 
proteins express in the thymus, they protect the T cells before immunotherapy. A study made 
by a group of researchers shows that the expansion of T cells repertoire does not derive from 
pre-existing tumor immune cells; instead, the expended clones consist of new T cells that have 
not previously been observed in the same tumor [67]. Moreover, in a study published by 
Arakawa et al, 2019. the T cells repertoire, which were detected before the initiation of CTLA-
4 checkpoint, respond well against tumor epitopes in immunotherapy. These studies suggest 
that immune checkpoint proteins protect the pre-existing T cells repertoire before the 
immunotherapy [1].  
This finding arises this theory that TCR may express some kind of unknown cytokines or 
receptors in the absence of CTLA-4, which help them to be protected from the immune system. 
 
5.5- Mechanism of PD-1 
PD-1 is constitutively expressed on the surface of T cells. Tumor cells express PDL-1, which 
they use to escape from the immune system. This fact indicates that the expression of this 
checkpoint protects the tumor cells from the immune system. Consequently, the expression of 
PD-1 checkpoint protects the T cells from the immune system. According to a study published 
by Hopkins et al, 2018. the T cells repertoire change in the periphery induced by CTLA-4 
blockade is larger than the change induced by PD-1 blockage [3]. This finding indicates that 
the influence of PD-1 expression on the surviving of T cells is larger than CTLA-4 checkpoint 
proteins, and therefore the number of T cells with CTLA-4 blockade increases. 
 
5.6- Alleles 
The degree of CD8 T cell repertoires varied among different alleles in different patients.  This 
finding suggested that the ability of interaction of the T cells with epitopes is associated with 
the structure of the HLA molecules. Hence, the T cells epitopes might be immunogenic to 
cancer patients with specific alleles, as differences are apparent between HLA A, B, and C loci. 
This finding suggests that patients response differently to T cells epitopes, and random 
recombination of VDJ segments might be immunogenic to some patients, which results in 
inducing of the immune response against the T cells.

A study published by Ishida et al., 2017 demonstrated that the patients with HLA-A26 respond 
twice to the blockade of immune checkpoint than patients negative for the allele [68]. Hence, 
HLA evolution, which is due to the size and the affinity of epitopes, influence the level of 
immunogenicity of the epitopes [69]. This arises the theory that whether the presence of 
different MHC allelic variants within a population, which is associated with different epitope 
repertoire size, prevent the escape of cancer cells epitopes or, dilute the focus of the immune 
response, which results in overzealous T cells deletion. 
 
5.7- T cells immunity 
According to Ishida et al., 2017  the blockade of CTLA-4 in the immunotherapy appears to be 
more effective in cancer patients with preexisting anti-tumor immunity [69]. Due to the fact 
that the immune system is continually learning, recognition of TCRB CDR3 as self is a time-
consuming process for the immune system. 
Alternative checkpoint proteins have been identified that may also play a role in the variation 
of T cells repertoire. According to a study published by Dummer et al., 2018 inhibition of 
CTLA-4 may cause upregulation of other checkpoint proteins receptors [70]. More researches 
needed to examine the role of a combination of immune checkpoint proteins in the protection 
or deletion of T cells repertoire.  
There are some aspects that had to be analyzed furthermore in this study.  
It was essential to analyze the epitopes; for instance, the expressions level of these epitopes 
could be examined.  According to a study, increasing the diversity change of epitopes has an 
association with the development of toxicities after receiving the mAB drug [1].  This finding 
arises the theory of whether the increasing in the epitopes is because of an increase of tumor-
specific epitopes or because of the immunogenicity of mAB drug. Analyzing the mAB epitopes 
and thus, reduction of the likelihood of side-affect may result in producing a drug with a better 
outcome. 
Another essential aspect that needs to be considered is where the cancer occurs. Lung is an 
open organ, which is constantly exposed to its environment, which may result in smoking and  
air pollution infections. Hence, it is possible that the analyzed T cells epitopes are not related 
to cancer.

Moreover, analyzing of CDR3 length distribution provides additional evidence of differences 
in T cells repertoire before and after immunotherapy. The shorter CDR3 is likely to be more 
observed by the immune system. 
Checking the similarity between the tumor epitopes and self-epitope would have helped to gain 
a better understanding of the T cells repertoire response.  
There are several limitations in this study, including the lack of information about the TCR alfa 
chain CDR3 sequences and their pairing with beta chains. Moreover, the small sample size is 
potentially limited this study. More CR patients will reveal a better view of understanding the 
effect of the degree of T cells repertoire expansion before and after immunotherapy. 
Nevertheless, data showing that immunogenic epitopes persist after treatment with expanded 
T cells repertoire suggest that they are possibly are protected by the immune system over time.

6- Conclusion 
Although the elimination of T cells repertoire has no immediate clinical effect on the well-
being of the patients, but it is still essential to examine how immune system responses during 
the immunotherapy. 
This study demonstrated the change of T cells repertoire before and after immunotherapy. The 
results vary between different patients. A high amount of T cells repertoire and an increase in 
the number of epitopes is observed in the patient who had CR. Two patients, who had SD and 
PD, had a less amount of T cells repertoire. Moreover, a high amount of T cells is observed in 
a patient, who had PD; however, the number of T cells decrease. Many critical questions about 
whether the T cells repertoire is protected by checkpoint proteins or deleted by the immune 
system remain.    
Many of the processes that contribute to immune tolerance take place in the tumor 
microenvironment. Learning about these immune process and how CDR3 regions can be 
recognized as self or non-self is essential, in which, it helps to gain a better understanding of 
how tumor microenvironment react against the immune system, and thus, by altering the 
microenvironment develop a higher systemic T cells immune response.  
This may contribute to improve the understanding of why some patients do not respond to the 
mAB drug because the wrong population of T cells epitopes are expanded, or there are some T 
cells deletion that prevents the immunotherapy.

Perspective 
 Many clinical trials of CTLA-4 blockade have been conducted in different cancer diseases. 
While some patients achieved a positive result, and other patients do not. There are many 
aspects that needed to be analyzed furthermore. Are there any certain tumors that cause the 
deletion of the T cells repertoire? Do the T cells express some unknown signal that protect 
them from the immune system, even in the absence of checkpoint proteins? 
To date, two checkpoint inhibitors have been approved for lung cancer.  Examination of the 
effect of other checkpoint proteins on the protection/deletion of the T cells and discovering the 
exact role of checkpoint proteins in the immune system will help to inhabit the checkpoint 
protein that protect the T cells less.

2.2- Assembling the CDR3 and VJ data 
There are three files: TRV file, TRJ file and the 76 CDR3 files.  
Assembling occurs based on the name of the gene that matches between all three files. The 
number which belongs to the TCRBV (in the CDR3 files) must match the number of TRBV 
(in the TRV file) and the number which belongs to TCRBJ ( in the CDR3 files) must match 
the number of TRAJ ( in the TRJ file).  For example, the first nucleotide sequence in file Pt1-
Day22, is:  
ATTCTGGAGTCCGCCAGCACCAACCAGACATCTATGTACCTCTGTGCCAGCACCC
GAACAGGCTATTCACCCCTCCACTTTGGGAAC 
The V-gene name of this sequence is: TCRBV28-01*01 and the J-gene name is: TCRBJ01-
06*01. 
The first sequence is the sequence, which is in TRV file, the second sequence is the sequence, 
which is in the CDR3 files and the third sequence is the one, which is in TRJ file. I have to 
append all these three sequences and make them as one sequence.  
The translation of nucleotide sequence is: 
ASTNQTSMYLCASTRTGYSPLHFGNGTRLTVT 
The V-gene ends with c, it matches the TRBV in TRV file, which is: ASTNQTSMYL   
The CDR3 is : CASTRTGYSPLHF 
The J-gene starts with G, which matches the TRBJ in TRJ file, which is: GNGTRLTVT
