Camps are the main living spaces of the refugees stranded in the di↵erent temporary
hosting countries. Therefore, play an important role to people’s life in many aspects.
The thesis aimed to understand the degree and manner on which the design of infras-
tructures a↵ects the well being of the refugees in a camp.
A ﬁeld trip was done to
Nea Kavala Camp, in Northern Greece, to collect data trough participant observation,
interviews and surveys. The study showed that women in New Kavala Camp were the
most a↵ected by the infrastructure. The design of the water supply, latrine and shower
had a direct relation to their physical and mental health. Due to the fact that women
are the responsible for the house duties in which infrastructures play an important part.
The improvement of infrastructures could help to improve the refugees well being up to
a certain extend, but they will never reach the highest level of well being inside of a
camp.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Camps are the main living spaces of the refugees stranded in the di↵erent temporary
hosting countries, and therefore play an important role to peoples life in many aspects.
The thesis aims to analyze how the distribution and design of the infrastructures a↵ects
the refugees’ well being in a camp. There are many theories of quality of life that try
to ﬁnd the relation between the physical environment and the well being of the people
living in that space. The extent into how the infrastructure and space design inﬂuence
human well being is not yet deﬁned.
Global forced displacement increased in 2015, with record-high numbers, 65.3 million
individuals were forcibly displaced worldwide as a result of persecution, conﬂict, gener-
alized violence, or human rights violations, 21.3 million people were refugees. More than
the half (54%) of all refugees worldwide came from just three countries: the Syrian Arab
Republic (4.9 million), Afghanistan (2.7 million), and Somalia (1.1 million) (UNHCR,
2016a).
About 10% of Syrian refugees have sought safety in Europe since the conﬂict started
(UNHCR, 2016g). Greece has been the arrival point for most refugees and migrants
trying to enter in Europe. As a result, the need for fast construction of refugee camps
had to be covered and consequently the conditions in Greece are failing to meet European
standards and legal norms.
When designing and managing a refugee camp many aspects have to be covered. The
primary goal is to meet the basic human needs, including food, shelter, sanitation,
education, accessible drinking water and etc. To accomplish this, the infrastructure and
spaces have to meet the functional expectations, providing technical services such as
electricity, running water, housing, latrine and shower facilities.

In 2015, over 1 million people, refugees and migrants, have arrived to Europe by sea
(UNHCR, 2015a). At the end of 2015, migrants also increasingly used road and rail
transport to access the EU. About half of the people were originally from the Syrian
Arab Republic, but there was also a signiﬁcant amount of people from Afghanistan and
Iraq.
The Balkan Route (ﬁgure 1.2) was the main channel for refugees to enter Europe in 2015.
The route consists -in sequence from Turkey- of Greece, Macedonia, Serbia, Croatia,
and Slovenia. In March 2016 more than 75,500 refugees and migrants were stranded
in Greece after Balkan countries shut their borders, closing o↵routes to central and
western Europe (Batha, 2016).
1.1.2
Greece
With the implementation of the EU-Turkey agreement and the closed borders of the
Balkan countries people travelling through Greece could no longer continue their jour-
neys.
Figure 1.2: Balkan route used by refugees pre-March 2016. Source: Eurostat, Frontex.
The possibilities available for the refugees in Greece through the legal path are either
asylum in the country or relocation elsewhere in Europe. Both of them take a long time,
due the fact that authorities do not have enough sta↵to process asylum claims quickly.
As a consequence they are waiting in temporary camps.
Greece Refugee population is over 62,000 people (Committee, 2016). The UNHCR is
labouring to get as many refugees as possible into hotels and apartments, but most are
still in camps.

1.1.3
Case Study: Nea Kavala Camp
A ﬁeld trip was done by the author as a part of the research in order to understand the
interaction between the refugees and infrastructure. The ﬁeld trip, in March 2017, had
a duration of twelve days in which ten were spent in the camp.
Nea Kavala camp housed during 2016 one of the largest number of people in the camps
across Greece (Here, 2016). It is now a medium-term to long-term camp for refugees
waiting to process their asylum application (Sandvold, 2016). Most of the residents have
had their interview for relocation, but they are still waiting.
The status of the camp as a medium/long-term camp made it ideal to study the inﬂuence
of the infrastructures on the everyday life of the residents, since some of them have been
there for more than a year now.
Nea Kavala was set up as an oﬃcial camp when the unoﬃcial camps at the border (e.g.
Idomeni) were growing. After the border to Macedonia was closed, the large unoﬃcial
camps were closed down and refugees were relocated to camps like Nea Kavala.
Nea Kavala camp is situated in an old airport runway 5 km East from Polykastro in the
rural area of Northern Greece, 66 km away from Thessaloniki (ﬁgure 1.3). Because of
this reason the area is completely ﬂat and is subjected to large wind gusts.
Figure 1.3: Nea Kavala location.
==PAGE:17==
Introduction
5
The camp opened oﬃcially the 28th of February 2016 (UNHCR, 2016e). The Greek
Military opened the camp with 600 tents for around 3,500 people. The tents did not
have any ﬂooring and in case of rain the area is partially submerged. In addition, they
did not have any kind of heating system (from section A.1).
At the beginning, NGOs did not have access to the camp and therefore humanitarian aid
from them was not accessible for the residents. The camp is run by the Hellenic Army,
they were the only responsible and distributors of food and drinkable water. Nowadays
the military also controls the access to the camp through the main entrance with an
identiﬁcation check.
Due to the number of residents during the early stages of the camp settlement there was
a shortage in the food distribution. As a result tension was created among the residents,
that struggle to cope with the situation.
Figure 1.4: UNHCRs family tents set in May 2016 (UNHCR, 2016c).
With funding by the European Commission Humanitarian Aid in May 2016 the army
tents were substituted by UNHCRs family tents with 16 m2 main ﬂoor area, plus two 3.5
m2 vestibules, for a total area of 23 m2, double-fold with ground sheet (ﬁgure 1.4). The
new tents were suitable for a family of 5 people, following the recommended minimum
living area in hot and temperate climates (3.5 m2 per person), and provided additional
space for cold climates. The technical speciﬁcations for these tents were developed by
shelter specialists, to guarantee a product ﬁt for use in all climates, with appropriate
outdoor life span, at a minimum cost (UNHCR, 2016c).

1.2
Problem Statement
The aim of the research is to ﬁnd the relation between infrastructures and well-being in
a refugee camp.
Since people living in camps had already experienced extreme su↵ering and accumulated
e↵ects of trauma, the ideal would be to create infrastructures with positive e↵ects on
their well-being, helping them to deal better with everyday life.
Unfortunately, the
situation in refugee camps is far from good. It is known that living conditions in some
camps are unacceptable, leading to diseases and su↵ering.
In order to improve the infrastructure e↵ectively, and so the living conditions in camps,
it is important to understand the degree and the manner on which they a↵ect the well-
being of the refugees. Therefore, the main question that will lead the research process

is:
”What is the e↵ect of infrastructure design
on refugees’ well-being in camps?”
This will be the research question use to analyze the infrastructure’s present situation

To obtain the information of the present situation in the camp and the interaction of

2.1
Environmental quality and quality of life
In order to analyze the a↵ection that the design of infrastructures have on the residents
well being it is necessary to understand the role of the physical environment in quality
of life. This a complex and multidimensional concept, and physical environment is just
one of the many factors.
The environment and the individuals are interrelated, but there is no uniform view on
causes and e↵ect (Wang, 2012). Therefore, there are many models trying to explain
quality of life and the factors integrated on it.
Environmental quality was deﬁned as the resultant of the quality of composing parts of
a given region but yet more than the sum of parts, it is the perception of a location as
a whole. The composing parts (nature, open space, infrastructure, built environment,
physical environment amenities and natural resources) have their own characteristics and
partial quality (RMV, 1996). From this deﬁnition it is assumed than infrastructure and
distribution of spaces design plays a part on the environmental quality of the residents.
In the quality of life model from Shafer (ﬁgure 2.1) it can be seen how the interaction
between physical and social domain creates the concept of livability. Furthermore, the
interaction between the physical and economic domain creates the concept of sustain-
ability. In a temporary scenery, like a refugee camp, the economic domain is most of the
time suspended, besides the occasions were they became long term camps where this
aspect could be develop.

2.2
Infrastructure & Well Being in Refugee Camps
Physical environment is the part of the human environment that includes purely physical
factors such as soil, climate, and water supply; therefore infrastructures can be consid-
ered as one of the factors of the physical environment in which someone lives. In order
to understand the inﬂuence of the physical environment in human well being previous
studies will be discuss in this section.
==PAGE:24==
Theoretical Framework

2.2.1
Physical Health
In the research of Al-Khatib et al. (2003) the impact of housing conditions on the health
of the refugees at a Palestinian camp is studied.
In this research the poor housing
conditions that the Palestinian refugees were living was clearly noticed. Moreover it
was found that the prevalence and incidence rates of upper respiratory tract disease was
directly related to these conditions.
A study conducted in 8 camps inhabited by Liberian refugees in Sierra Leone Bonner
et al. (2007) showed that the risk of Lassa infection (severe febrile illness transmitted to
humans by the rodent Mastomys natalensis) in the camp depended on individual housing
quality (building materials) and external hygiene (vegetation and rubbish surrounding
the house). The analysis also showed no association between the occurrence of Lassa
fever in a household and the distance to human waste centers.
Both of the studies mentioned above succeed to ﬁnd a relation between housing con-
ditions and health. Direct factors include site of residential area, building materials,
natural lighting and ventilation, crowding, availability and access to water and sanita-
tion (Al-Khatib et al., 2003).
In a review of water and sanitation provision in refugee camps in association with selected
health and nutrition indicators Cronin et al. (2008) showed how the quality of services
or gaps in poor water and sanitation were a↵ecting refugees’ well being and health.
Figure 2.4: Conceptual outline of the relationship between water & sanitation and
other sectors.(Cronin et al., 2008)
==PAGE:25==

The inter-linkages between access to water and the other basic life sustaining sectors
are shown in ﬁgure 2.4, a conceptual outline of the relationships between the water
& sanitation, nutrition and health sectors. The insuﬃcient service provision in these

Khan and Shahidullah (1982) documented that in one refugee camp in Bangladesh,

2008).
2.2.2
Other factors of well being
The studies mentioned previously are mainly related to the physical health of the refugees
and not so much to the mental health or other aspects that also integrate well being.
In the case of the study by Cronin et al. (2008), it was concluded that the impact of
one factor, access to water, not only inﬂuenced the physical health of the refugees but
also the education of children. Children collecting water had adverse a↵ects on their
education, but an improvement on their health. It was shown that households reporting
a case of diarrhea within the past 24 hours collect 26% less water on average than those
who did not report any diarrhea cases.
There are many papers studying the socio-economics, mental health and education inside
refugee camps. But there is a lack of studies relating infrastructures to this factors. It
is in this gap where the author tries to connect from a multidisciplinary approach all
the factors to the infrastructure design.

3.2.1
Participant Observation
Before arriving at the camp the information regarding contact with the residents and
possible language barriers was insuﬃcient to ensure that interviews would be possible, so
it was chosen to consider participant observation as one of the ways to collect qualitative
data.
Once on the site it was found that personal interviews were possible but participant
observation was kept as a method to collect qualitative data. What people say they
believe and do are often contradicted by their behavior. Given the frequency of this
very human inconsistency, observation can be a powerful tool to analyze what people
report about themselves during interviews (Mack et al., 2005).
The method is distinctive because the researcher approaches participants in their own
environment rather than having the participants come to the researcher.
Generally
speaking, the researcher engaged in participant observation tries to learn what life is
like for an insider while remaining, inevitably, an outsider (IBID).
Being that the arrangement of the camp only enables NGO’s to access the facilities, the
interaction with residents was possible through being part of Drop in the Ocean (DiH)
an NGO working at Nea Kavala responsible for distribution of clothes, other non-food
items, weekly food packs and vegetables. The set up of the organization made it possible
to have contact with the residents as a part of the community. For instance, the families
would often invite volunteers to their houses for warm meals and co↵ee.
Therefore it was easy to be able to observe the residents in their daily activities. During
the time of the research, objective notes about what was seen were made, and this
together with informal conversations were recorded as ﬁeld notes in a ﬁeld notebook.
The aim of the participant observation was to check against residents subjective report-
ing of what they do. Participant observation is also useful for gaining an understanding
of the physical contexts in which study participants live; peoples behaviors and activities
what they do, how frequently, and with whom (IBID).

3.2.2
Qualitative Semi-Structured Interviews
The purpose of the interviews was to gather information of past and presents experiences
of the residents in the camp related to the infrastructures and spaces.
Interviews can be placed on a continuum of structure, from unstructured to highly struc-
tured. Embedded in this continuum is the idea of how much control the interviewer will
have over the interaction (Harrell and Bradley, 2009). When the control of the inter-
viewer over the interaction is medium the interview is named semi-structured interview.
Semi-structured interviewing, using a guide, with questions and topics that must be
covered was chosen. Due to the fact that the situation in the camp was unstable. And
being that the conditions in which the interviews would be conducted were unclear before
the ﬁeld trip.
There was some discretion about the order in which questions were asked, but the ques-
tions were standardized. This kind of interview collects detailed information in a style
that is somewhat conversational (IBID). In order to keep the ﬂow of the conversation
instead of taking notes, the interviews were recorded with verbal authorization of the
interviewed.
Semi-structured interview method was chosen to delve deeply into the topic of infras-
tructure and spaces, and to understand thoroughly the answers provided.
In order to cover the di↵erent aspects of the infrastructure and space design di↵erent
questions were asked depending on the interviewee age and gender. The questions were
divided into di↵erent topics. The assumption was made to chose this topics as main
factors for the rest of the study and keep water supply separated from WASH. The
questions were as follow:

For the interview and also for the Likert Scale survey two kind of sampling were used:

• Opportunity sampling is just what its name implies, making use of opportunities

• Snowball sampling occurs when the research beneﬁts from one participant sug-
gesting or introducing another participant to the researcher. This is a dangerous
primary sampling design, as the risk inherent in snowball sampling is the over
representation of a single, networked group (IBID). Since the number of people in
the camp at the time of the visit was not large, this type of sampling was the most
used. One of the residents in the camp, that volunteered as a translator introduced
to many of the residents and explained the purpose of the interviews.

Likert Scale
For seeing that communication issues may occur during the interviews due to the lan-

A Likert scale is a psychological measurement device that is used to gauge attitudes,
values, and opinions. Unlike a simple yes / no question, a Likert scale allows to uncover
degrees of opinion. This can be particularly helpful for sensitive topics. Having a range
of responses will also help to more easily identify areas of improvement (Monkey, 2016).

be identiﬁed and this will be map as in ﬁgure 3.2.

needs, etc.
This tool is used to identify new business opportunities but in this case the purpose is

an activity.
The UACs created for this case of study to analyze the di↵erent activities could be used

Case Study 1: Young woman
I met this young lady through DiH, she was quiet, looked at me with kind eyes, and had

was broken but good enough to carry a conversation.
She had an older sister and two younger siblings, a boy and a girl. During the interview,
she talked about her mom, but never mentioned the father ﬁgure. I explained to her
the nature and purpose of the interview, a bit about my research, and the fact that I
am a student of Architectural Engineering. She looked at me amazed with her big eyes
wide open and told me, she also wanted to be an engineer and construct houses. She
told me that she was 2 months away of ﬁnishing high school and the following fall she
was supposed to start university before they had to ﬂee.
Water Supply
She told me her mom does all the cooking, which is a very diﬃcult task, due to the
amount of water needed to prepare a meal and to wash and clean afterwards. The water

Chapter 6
Discussion
6.1
Findings
The results of the research conﬁrm that the design of infrastructure and spaces in camps
have an inﬂuence on refugees well-being. Suggesting that the e↵ect is bigger on woman’s
well being.
To the questions stated in section 1.2.
What is the scale of the e↵ect on the well-being?
The study shows that the diﬃculties encountered by the residents during everyday life
activities in the use of infrastructure and spaces have an impact in their well-being.
Being infrastructures a part of the physical environment, and physical environment only
one of the disciplines of a broad range related to quality of life.
It can not be measured the degree in which the di↵erent factors (WS, WASH, electric-
ity,...) inﬂuence the well-being, but it can be seen that is bigger from one subject to
another. For this reason, it is important for human well-being to take into account the
concept of quality of life into infrastructure and space design.
Are factors of equal importance to everyone?
Through the research a gender di↵erence was seen in the way the residents interact with
the factors. The amount of times that women interact with some of the factors was
greater than in men.
In the three cases of studies (section 4.2) it is mentioned that women are the ones who
take care of the house duties where the use of water and electricity is needed, and that
46
==PAGE:59==
Discussion
47
they are more uncomfortable with shower and toilet situation related to the WASH
facilities.
This di↵erence is also visible in section 5.2, where women grade much lower the WS,
latrine and shower.
The di↵erence in the WS grade must be due to the fact that women make use of the
water much more than men do, as they are responsible for the cooking and cleaning
activities where water is needed.
The gap related to the shower and toilet could be due to the reason that they are also
responsible for the kids, that need help when using the facilities. With the hot water
restriction times it is diﬃcult for them to have enough time to shower, as they need to
cover all the previous activities in comparison to men who have more time available for
their own needs.
The limitation of this ﬁnding will be discuss later in section 6.2.
Which aspects of well being are a↵ected by infrastructure design?
As was shown in the case studies (in section 4.2.1 and 4.2.3) their hygiene and physical
health (they always feel tired) was a↵ected but also the design of the infrastructures
contributed negatively to their level of stress and therefore be one more cause to anxiety
and depression, a↵ecting women’s mental health.
It was also seen that the health of the kids in the camp was threat by the design of
infrastructure, being that during the summer 2016 an outbreak of Hepatitis A occurred
due to the bad sanitation and housing conditions of the camp (table A.12).
Is there a tool to measure the inﬂuence of the environmental quality on the
well-being?
There is not such a tool, as mentioned previously in the ﬁrst question the inﬂuence can
not be measure. But a tool to grade the di↵erent factors and compare the design of
camps related to infrastructure and spaces could be created.
UNHCR has already created a tool as survey form that can be used to monitor and
evaluate the condition of communal and public toilet blocks in a refugee setting, this tool
is the ”F-400 Toilet Infrastructure Sanitary Survey Form (UNHCR, 2015)” (Appendix
E). The main purpose of this tool is to rapidly identify public health and protection
related risk (UNHCR, 2015b). In this case the risk is measured in a scale from 0 to 12.
A similar tool can be created in which every factor could be evaluated from 0 to 9. The

6.2
Study’s Limitations
One of the limitations of the studied is the sample size, due to the time restriction only
one camp was study and because of the ﬂuency of the residents in English, the number
of interviews was not high.
Another issue to take into account is the fact that the result of the study is based in big
proportion on the cultural values and beliefs of Middle Easter countries. Origin from
most of the refugees stranded in Greece. The result of the study might vary in camps
in which the gender di↵erences are least noticeable due to the cultural background.
An important fact to consider is the variable situation of the camps and the speed of
the changes.
In contrast with cities were planning can be made with time and the
infrastructures last for many years, camps are temporary and the processes inside them
are constantly changing, as well as the number of residence and other factors that could
inﬂuence the design of the camp. For example during the time of the ﬁeld trip (March
2017) the electricity supply was good and the residents gave a high grade during the
Likert Scale Survey (Appendix C), but two months later (May 2017) the residents were
left with no electricity (this information was given by PoI 1 via Facebook Messenger).
Also during the interviews the authors self perception of the situation was challenged
by the biases of her gender and age, and probably also by some others like religion,
nationality, race and ethnicity. Also an important factor to consider is the line of study
of the author as an engineer and the lack of background knowledge in certain aspect of
the study. The biases of the author could have an inﬂuence on the interpretation of the
results.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
From the di↵erent theories of quality of life can be seen that infrastructures play a role
in the human well being. During the research trough the di↵erent interviews it was seen
how the di↵erent factors inﬂuence the residents’ everyday life.
An important ﬁnding during the research was that the di↵erent factors of infrastructures
inﬂuence di↵erently depending on the gender of the person. Being women the most
negatively a↵ected by it.
Women in Nea Kavala Camp are the responsible for the house duties in which infras-
tructures play an important part. The factors that inﬂuence women the most and where
the di↵erent was made were WASH facilities (latrine and shower) and the water supply.
The design of the WASH and water supply a↵ected directly into women’s physical health.
They had to carry the water from the taps to the containers and they are the main
responsible for the kids, so they also had to shower them, cook for them, etc. All of this
inﬂuence in their level of stress, consequently also to their mental health.
Due to the infrastructure design the physical conditions of the refugees were threaded
in certain seasons. In summer there were cases of Hepatitis A, during the winter the ﬂu
was common around children.
The improvement of infrastructures could help to improve the refugees well being up to
a certain extend, but they will never reach the highest level of well being inside of a
camp.
The linkage between hygiene and infrastructure was studied by other authors as well as
the inﬂuence of the infrastructure design on the physical health of the refugees. As the
study has shown the design not only a↵ects hygiene and physical health it also a↵ects
other parts of their well being, such as mental health.

==PAGE:63==
Conclusion
51
For the entire study was hard to get reliable data. The information available before the
ﬁeld trip and also the information that the stakeholders in the camp were willing to give
was limited. The interviews and ﬁrst hand visual experience were the main source of
information. Therefore, it is diﬃcult to standardize the study or create and implement
policies.
The next step, to better understand the inﬂuence of the design in the well-being, could
be a boarder investigation taking samples from camps located in di↵erent countries with
residents from di↵erent backgrounds and similar number of residents.
In addition, regarding the technical aspect of the study, the factors related to infras-
tructure design that determine environmental quality, could be better determine as in
this investigation an assumption was made from the beginning that the factors were:
WS,WASH, electricity, housing and PS.
Finally, to include all aspect that integrate well being and give a sense of dignity, from
the well-being and Infrastructure tool a more complex tool could be created in which
other factors could be include like access to schooling, food distribution, distance to
medical facilities, etc.
