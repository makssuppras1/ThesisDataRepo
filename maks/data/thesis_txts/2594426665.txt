Outline
The thesis studies the evolution in the last phase of cholera in the Caribbean country of Haiti.
The effect of WaSH (Water, Sanitation & Hygiene) interventions that have been carried out is
evaluated through a new variable that is incorporated into an epidemiological model. This
piece of writing is organized into six chapters. Chapter 1 introduces cholera as a disease and
its outbreak in Haiti; Chapter 2 presents the epidemiological deterministic model in details;
Chapter 3 shows the geographic context of Haiti; Chapter 4 provides a protocol for the model

Abstract
Cholera is a waterborne disease triggered by the pathogen Vibrio cholerae which induces
watery diarrhoea that might quickly lead to death if untreated. Of the current seventh cholera
pandemic, the worst epidemic has ravaged Haiti, where roughly a million people have tested
positive and the death toll has exceeded 10,000. After the dreadful earthquake that struck
Haiti in early 2010, the water, hygiene and sanitation (WaSH) conditions of the country
have dramatically plummeted, laying the groundwork for a disease that mainly transmits
through contaminated water and food to establish. The health authorities, notably the World
Health Organization and its branches, and the Haitian Ministry of Health, alongside with
a few non-governmental organizations, have rolled out a plan to tackle the epidemic with
the deployment of oral vaccination campaigns and specific targeted interventions, aimed
at improving the hygiene conditions. Several spatially explicit epidemiological models have
obtained remarkable results in forecasting and predicting the evolution of the disease in the
Haitian communities. These compartmental models consist of grouping communities into
classes according to their health status e.g. if they are susceptible, currently infected or if they
have recovered, plus an additional class which represents the environmental concentration of
V. cholerae. Rainfall has been proven to be a driver of the incoming flux of pathogens into the
aquatic reservoir. This thesis focuses on the effectiveness of the WaSH interventions in the
response to the cholera epidemic framework, through their inclusion into the epidemiological
model by means of two main mechanisms. The first one hypothesizes a modification of the
people’ behaviour and thus a likely reduction of their exposure to the disease; the second
one considers an action on the pathogenic environmental concentration instead. These
assumptions result in a variety of epidemiological sub-models that are calibrated separately
by dint of a Monte Carlo Markov Chain (MCMC) algorithm. Once these models have been
calibrated, they are ranked according to the Akaike Information Criterion which evaluates their
performance in terms of their goodness-of-fit to observed data and number of parameters. The
model that achieves the best score considers a temporary reduction of the people’ exposure by
virtue of the WaSH interventions. This tool can potentially support forecasting studies aimed
at assessing to what extent a given fashion of deploying WaSH interventions — spatially and
temporally— may help control and eventually eradicate cholera. This thesis also presents a
discussion concerning the additional work which is needed in order to expand the stability and
reliability of the aforementioned model and its ability to be employed into cholera forecasting.

Résumé
Le choléra est une infection causée par le pathogène Vibrio cholerae qui provoque une diarrhée
aiguë très contagieuse et peut entraîner la mort. Après le terrible tremblement de terre qui a
frappé Haïti en janvier 2010, les conditions sanitaires du pays se sont rapidement détériorées,
ce qui a favorisé la propagation de la maladie dans les eaux usées et la nourriture contaminée.
Résultat, un million de personnes touchées par cette bactérie et plus de dix mille morts.
Les autorités sanitaires, notamment l’Organisation Mondiale de la Santé, le Ministère haïtien
de la Santé Publique et de la Population, ainsi que diverses organisations non gouvernemen-
tales, ont approuvé un plan pour lutter contre l’épidémie : des campagnes de vaccination
nationales ont été cumulées à des interventions spécifiques dans le but d’améliorer les condi-
tions sanitaires de la population.
Les modèles épidémiologiques spatialement explicites apportent des résultats concluant dans
différentes prévisions, concernant les communautés haïtiennes, ces dernières se composent
de différents groupes de personnes organisés selon l’état sanitaire avec une classe spéciale
liée à la concentration du pathogène dans l’environnement aquatique.
Pour évaluer l’efficacité des interventions, nous avons utilisé et modifié ces modèles afin d’y
inclure deux processus d’action : d’abord des interventions de prévention qui permettent de
réduire l’exposition de la population. Ensuite, nous postulons qu’un deuxième mécanisme
pourrait apporter une réduction directe des pathogènes dans l’environnement aquatique.
Ainsi, différents modèles épidémiologiques sont formulés conformément à ces hypothèses,
une recalibration selon un algorithme Monte Carlo Markov Chain est ensuite apportée. En
outre, le classement de ces nouveaux modèles selon le critère d’information d’Akaike évalue
leur performance en fonction de leur adéquation avec les données observées et le nombre
de paramètres. Le modèle avec le meilleur classement énonce une réduction temporaire de
l’exposition au choléra à la suite du déroulement des interventions sanitaires.
Ainsi, cet outil peut potentiellement avoir une application directe dans des études de prévi-
sions épidémiologiques pour rédiger des scénarios de déploiement d’interventions, aussi bien
dans le temps que dans l’espace géographique, afin d’analyser leur capacité de contribuer à
l’élimination du choléra. Cette thèse examine ainsi le travail additionnel qui est nécessaire
pour améliorer la stabilité et la fiabilité du modèle calibré et son potentiel d’être utilisé dans
les prévisions du choléra.
Mots clefs : Ecohydrologie, Épidémiologie, Maladies hydriques, Choléra, Optimisation, Mo-
dèles compartimentaux

CHAPTER1
Cholera in Haiti
1.1
Cholera: an introduction
Cholera is a waterborne infective disease that causes severe watery diarrhoea and is triggered
by the infection with Vibrio cholerae, a Gram-negative rod, comma-shaped bacterium that
thrives in coastal waters and river estuaries (Colwell, 1996; Lipp et al., 2002; Ryan et al.,
2018). Knowledge about cholera dates back to the nineteenth century, although the Indian
subcontinent has been ravaged by the disease for centuries (Ramamurthy & Sharma, 2014).
However, the causative agent of cholera was only discovered in 1854 by the Italian anatomist
Filippo Pacini, who firstly isolated and observed the bacterium (Lippi & Gotuzzo, 2014).
In symptomatic cases, cholera is associated with severe diarrhoea, which may lead to dehy-
dration and, eventually if untreated, to death (Swerdlow et al., 1997). A study from the World
Heath Organization (hereafter WHO) shows that in 2016, diarrhoea diseases feature in the top
ten causes of death globally, with roughly 1.4 million fatalities in 2016 alone, and the second
place in low-income countries (World Health Organization, 2018). Cholera is still endemic in
African and Asian countries (Ali et al., 2015), but local epidemics, such as the Haitian cholera
outbreak which started back in 2010, are still violently outbursting nowadays.
Humans contract cholera when infected food or waters are consumed (World Health Orga-
nization, n.d.-c). The majority of ingested V. cholerae are typically killed by gastric acids, but
survivors pass to the small intestine and colonize it. The infectious dosage is reported to
be around 105 and 108 organisms (Nelson et al., 2011). Once settled, the bacteria secrete
the cholera toxin, which causes additional chloride and sodium excretion through the apical
chloride channel and thus lead to watery diarrhoea. In severe cases, the discharge can be as
high as 1L per hour and contain roughly 1011 to 1012 pathogens per litre (Nelson et al., 2011).
Acute dehydration and electrolytes loss can lead to death within 12 hours from the onset of
symptoms, if the patient is not promptly treated. The therapy consists in a first intravenous

rehydration of the patient with supply of carbohydrates and salts, needed to balance the elec-
trolytes loss during the acute stages of diarrhoea, when the patient may not be able to respond
due to hypovolemic shock. The oral rehydration solution (ORS) should be administered as
soon as the patient can drink. Antibiotics can be prescribed to counteract the effects of cholera
and reduce the numerousness of living bacteria shed into the external environment (Centers
for Disease Control and Prevention, n.d.-b). As treating cholera is fairly easy and yet it is a
leading cause of death in low-income countries, detecting the onset of symptoms during the
early stages of the illness is crucial to both save the patient’s life and hinder the dispersion of
pathogens into the aquatic reservoir.
Nowadays, the global burden of cholera still remains characterized by broad ranges of uncer-
tainty. A study by Ali et al. (2015) documented an estimation of almost three million cases
worldwide and the World Health Organization itself reports a 1.3 to 4 million cholera infection
every year (World Health Organization, n.d.-a).
1.1.1
Linkages to scarce levels of sanitation
As the World Health Organization outlines (World Health Organization, n.d.-b), the onset and
the diffusion of cholera is frequently associated with the lack of adequate levels of sanitation,
water quality and hand hygiene, with a population-attributable fraction of the disease inci-
dence that can go up to 0.61 in Sub-Saharian Africa, as estimated by Prüss-Ustün et al. (2014).
A study by Swerdlow et al. (1997) describes how the lack of running improved water in refugees
camps in Malawi, Africa, has led to more frequent infections from V. cholerae. Commonly, not
all households were equipped with more than one water tank and refugees used to wash their
hands after defecation by dipping them into the drinking water containers. This was proved to
be an efficient mean of spreading the disease within the household. The same study reports
wide foodborne transmission when the food was consumed without being cooked beforehand
or leftovers were eaten.
A successful tackle of cholera in affected regions can thus be pursued and eventually achieved
through coordinated oral vaccination campaigns and the deployment of tailored interventions
aimed at improving the water, sanitation and hygiene (WaSH) conditions. Specifically, these
tools may be applied at separate stages: an early deployment of cholera vaccination campaigns
during the epidemic phase of the disease is not advisable as it might divert resources away
from measures that are immediately needed (Swerdlow et al., 1997). A study by Lilje et
al. (2015), addressed the behavioural factors connected to the prevention of cholera and
diarrhoeal diseases in Chad: they surveyed the population and found a generally low risk
perception across the it, which demonstrated the need to increase people’s awareness as a
key factor to reduce the risk of infection. They also suggested how targeted interventions

should specifically be designed to tackle the population’s lack of relevant knowledge about
water treatment and sanitation. In addition, there are studies within the literature highlighting
specific technologies that have been adopted in several countries affected by cholera to
combat it, e.g. well chlorination (Garandeau et al., 2006) that should, however, be provided
together with educational sessions for the local users (Cavallaro et al., 2011). Cholera in the
Sierra Leonean outbreak of 2012 has been also addressed through WaSH strategies involving
residents’ educational sessions concerning household chlorination and the distribution of
chlorine tablets (Von Nguyen et al., 2014). The authors of this last piece of research also
suggested how street food vendors should be included in such educational sessions and
given access to safe water, as street food shops typically constitute a non-neglectable risk in
spreading diarrhoeal diseases.
To conclude, WaSH interventions worldwide have been characterized by improvements of the
access to water and its quality (both at source and at the point of use); improvements con-
nected to sanitation and to the reduction of the exposure to faeces; promotion of behavioural
changes related to personal, domestic and food hygiene; distribution of hygiene materials
and disinfection and cleaning product; disposal of dead bodies; and general improvements in
wastewater and solid waste management (D’Mello-Guyett et al., 2020). These effects are aimed
at interrupting the human-to-human and domestic transmission, reducing the discharge of
pathogens into the environment and the halting of the human exposure to a contaminated
environment. The measures are to be applied and deployed both at household and community
levels. Guidelines for handling cholera cases through WaSH intervention exist (Lamond &
Kinyanjui, 2012); however, D’Mello-Guyett et al. (2020), urge policymakers to standardize the
existing guidelines to provide coordinated guidance in operating such WaSH interventions.
Nonetheless, a thorough evaluation of the effectiveness of these WaSH interventions is still
missing, as stressed by Taylor et al. (2015), in a systematic review of the existing literature on
WaSH interventions.
1.1.2
Seasonal variability of cholera
A strong physical connection between seasonal patterns and insurgences of cholera worldwide
is endorsed by a variety of studies. For instance, Lobitz et al. (2000) focused on the cholera
spread in Bangladesh and the estuarine and coastal areas facing the Bay of Bengal, a region
where cholera is endemic, observing a strong similarity between the sea surface temperature
and the observed epidemiological trend and explained this pattern through the annual repro-
duction cycle of the plankton colonies which host the pathogen. However, rainfall trends also
seem to control the transmission of the disease, as found out by Jutla et al. (2013) in a study
that examined the cholera presence in the Indian region, specifically in the Indus basin, which
is characterized by a monsoonic climate with heavy rainfalls between June and September.
3

Notably, they found an increase in the occurrence of cholera during the monsoon season
and hypothesized that heavy precipitations can simultaneously reduce the availability of safe
drinkable water, bring severe damage to the sanitary infrastructures supplying water, and the
subsequent floods can result in overflowing sewers. They also found a connection with the
temperature, which are generally higher during the summery monsoon season, pointing out
how warmer climate help bacteria colonies grow. The remarkable linkages between seasonal
factors (e.g. rainfall and temperature) have been spotted in Haiti as well, where cholera has
become epidemic in 2010. Eisenberg et al. (2013) have indeed found a strong correlation
between rainfalls and cholera cases in a study that also investigated the lag between a heavy
precipitation event and the corresponding increased risk of getting the disease. They docu-
ment this lag to be around 4-7 days in areas where the disease has an epidemic behaviour,
but this value can be higher in estuarine and coastal settings. A short lag, as outlined by the
authors of the study reflects the saturation conditions that occur in sewage systems after a
rainfall-related flood as also hypothesized by Rinaldo et al. (2012) and Hashizume et al. (2008).

mean precipitation. A study by Emch et al. (2008) has unravelled the cholera seasonal patterns
across the globe, finding out that tropical regions are typically more affected by severe cholera
outbreaks. In particular, they warn climate change has the potential to influence the seasonal
patterns of cholera, subsequently rendering the disease’s outbreaks more severe for countries
that have higher latitudes.
1.2
2010 Haitian Cholera Outbreak
The largest modern cholera outbreak in terms of deaths afflicted Haiti. Even though the
epidemic that has been affecting Yemen since 2016 is comparable in severeness (World Health
Organization, 2019), the Haitian one remains the deadliest, with almost 10,000 causalities as of
January 2020. The forerunner of the Haitian cholera outbreak was a catastrophic earthquake
that struck the Western part of the island of Hispaniola on 12th January 2010 with a magnitude
of 7.0. Many facilities, including governmental offices and sanitary infrastructures, were
severely damaged after the quake and its sequential aftershocks. The actual death toll is
disputed, but almost all sources place it on the order of 105 lives, a number boosted up by the
precarious socio-economical prior conditions of the country. The aftermath of the disaster
laid the grounds for the epidemic: the sanitary standards plummeted after people lost their
home and access to clean water.
A paper by Lantagne et al. (2013) enlists the main hypothesis that were considered at the time
as the reasons why cholera, a disease that was absent in Haiti, has outbursted so violently.

1.4. Humanitarian efforts towards cholera eradication
1.4
Humanitarian efforts towards cholera eradication
1.4.1
Introduction
Since the begin of the outbreak, many efforts aimed at eradicating cholera from Haiti have been
made by national and international players. Several governmental and non-governmental
bodies and companies activated to address the epidemic through a coordinated response
involving other players as well. After the first massive propagation of the epidemic in 2010-
2012 that caused most of the total infections, the incidence has eventually decreased, except
for seasonal mild flare-ups linked to periodic rainfall. This caused the international funding
to drastically plummet despite many organizations were still struggling to promote proper
hygiene standards into the Haitian households. However, in February 2013, the Ministry of
Public Health and Population (MSPP) of the Haitian Republic, together with the United Nation
Children’s Fund (UNICEF), the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), and the
Pan American Health Organization (PAHO), rolled out a National Plan for the Elimination of
Cholera in Haiti 2013-2022 (Ministry of Public Health and Population et al., 2013; Rebaudet
et al., 2019) that provided a snapshot on the epidemiological situation at the time in Haiti, the
WaSH (Water, Sanitation, and Hygiene) conditions in the country as well as the long term goals
and the envisaged means to achieve these objectives. The response can be categorized in two
main branches: an oral vaccination campaign (OCV) and specific targeted interventions aimed
at improving the local levels of sanitation and hygiene. Amongst the vaccination campaigns
that have been deployed over the 2012-2017 period, around 1.4 million people have been
vaccinated (Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, n.d.-a). The role of oral vaccination
campaigns in eradicating cholera from Haiti has been studied by Lee et al. (2019) (preprint) in
a multi-scenario study that analyzed the probability of eliminating cholera and the number of
averted infection under several vaccination strategies.
1.4.2
WaSH Case-Area Targeted Interventions (CATIs) in Haiti
The other pillar of the humanitarian response to the cholera epidemic in Haiti was the deploy-
ment of specific case-area targeted interventions by the sanitary operators. These interven-
tions have been activated through a specific alert system: when cholera cases were suspected
(after infections were reported or specific rumours in a given area were heard), WaSH response
teams (appointed by UNICEF and MSPP) carried out targeted interventions to the affected
households and the neighbourhood. Their deployment included, as reported by Rebaudet
et al. (2019):
• educational sessions aimed at promoting awareness and specific knowledge about

1.5
Research question and purpose
This piece of research is aimed at understanding how water, sanitation, and hygiene in-
terventions have contributed towards the eradication of cholera in Haiti and whether an
epidemiological models that embeds such interventions is attainable. The outcome of this
study may be further applied into developing a robust epidemiological model to be employed
in future forecasting studies. The following chapters illustrate the methods that are envisaged
to address this research question.

CHAPTER2
Mathematical epidemiological model
Compartmental models are frequently employed in epidemiology to classify and define in-
teractions between pre-defined groups of individuals, depending on their health status. In
technical terms, these models are also referred to as SIR models, an acronym for Susceptible,
Infected, and Recovered which are three main demographic compartments, including healthy
people that might get the disease if exposed, ill patients that are currently infected, and, re-
covered patients that are potentially immune to the illness for a defined period. The union
of these three compartments forms the total population H. The rates at which people move
between these compartments stem from medical, virological, epidemiological, and socio-
demographical studies and are typically introduced in the underlying model as parameters
that can be assumed from the literature or calibrated against empirical data.
The SIR models can either be spatially implicit, meaning that the geographic structure is
not considered and that there is only one analyzed spatial dimension (which can be e.g. a
city under lockdown, a region, a small country, etc.), or spatially explicit, thus considering
the geographic matrix as multiple interconnected nodes. In this case, the epidemiological
compartments, as well as the nodal population, are explicitly evaluated for each node. The SIR
models that are implemented in this piece of research are spatially explicit and run on two
scales defined upon the Haitian watersheds and the ten administrative departments. More
about the spatial structure of the model is presented in the following chapters.
This chapter introduces the epidemiological models that are implemented in this work. The
compartmental model for cholera includes an additional compartment, which is that of
environmental vibrios (B) i.e. the concentration of Vibrio cholerae in the aquatic reservoir.
Furthermore, there might be the need to treat the asymptomatic patients separately from
those who manifest symptoms as the former move freely between different communities yet
shed bacteria through their faeces and thus contribute to the spread of the disease.
The mathematical model builds on the previous work developed by the EPFL Laboratory

2.2
SIARB models specifics
The SIARB epidemiological model shares most of its features with the original SIRB model.
However, it distinguishes between two types of infected: those who are symptomatic people
(compartment I) and the asymptomatic infected individuals (compartment A). The model
considers the following main differences between these two collections of people:
• asymptomatic infected individual do not display any symptom and, as a result, do travel,
thus contributing to the spread of the disease towards other nodes;
• asymptomatic carriers of the disease shed less bacteria than the symptomatic, because
there is no increased discharge. The model considers the asymptomatic shedding rate
θA as a fraction of the symptomatic one θ, yielding the following relationship: θA = rθ,
where r represents the ratio between the asymptomatic and the symptomatic shedding
rate and has to be calibrated;
• asymptomatic patients, due to the lack of symptoms, do not dehydrate and therefore do
not die because of the disease. However, they can still die for other natural reasons.
The model disregards the differences in the recovery time of the asymptomatic patients, which
is considered equal to that of the symptomatic individuals; and it neglects that the immunity
period of the asymptomatic patients could be shorter than that of symptomatic people. The
immunity period length is calibrated, and thus averages amongst all infected individuals,
while the recovery time is assumed from the literature. The SIARB model thus accounts for the
differences illustrated above and displays the ODE system presented in equation (2.19).

CHAPTER3
Geographic context
This chapter introduces some of the features that characterize Haiti geographically and there-
fore have an impact on the spread of the epidemics. Specifically, the administrative divisions
are firstly introduced. Humanitarian data (e.g. the epidemiological bulletin but also the popu-
lation records and the WaSH interventions database) is made available on various levels that
follow the administrative boundaries. The physical geography also has a significant linkage to
the cholera spread as rivers, which are ultimately influenced by the elevation profile and the
local climate, act as reservoirs for the pathogens and as pathways towards further localities.
The population distribution and the road networks are also hereby presented.
Haiti is a Caribbean country located on the Western portion of the Hispaniola Island, the
Eastern part being the Dominican Republic with whom Haiti shares its sole land border. The
total population is estimated to be slightly over 11 million people with a median age of 24 years
(Central Intelligence Agency, n.d.). There are two co-official languages, which are French and
Creole. The country is administratively divided in 10 departments (French: Départements) and
140 (now 144) municipalities (French: Communes). The administrative subdivisions, featuring
the several layers of power, are shown in figure 3.1. Haiti is mostly surrounded by the sea
except for the border with the Dominican Republic. Its terrain is diversified and rugged and
there are no flatlands. As a consequence, there are many rivers, albeit generally short and
non-navigable, the longest and most important being the Artibonite river.
According to the Köppen-Geiger climate classification (Beck et al., 2018; Rubel et al., 2017),
Haiti has an equatorial tropical climate, spanning from a tropical rainforest (Af) and monsoon
(Am) climate in proximity of the coast and a tropical savanna with dry-winter (Aw) climate
in the hinterland. Higher mountains are characterized by a milder climate, specifically a
temperate dry winter climate (Cwb) and a temperate climate with no dry season (Cfb). A
smaller part of the country, geographically overlapped with the coastal portion of the Artibonite
Department, is characterized by an arid climate with high temperatures.

CHAPTER4
Methods
This chapter deals with the calibration of the SIR models previously introduced. Several
formulations of the model either including or not the asymptomatic infected individuals
with their own compartment and with different formulations concerning the inclusion of the
interventions into the model were defined. After being calibrated, these models undergo a
formal comparison which assesses their ranking for forecasting purposes.
4.1
Model calibration
The calibration process is done through the Python package spotpy (Houska et al., 2015). The
calibration quality is assessed through the comparison of the observed and simulated new
cases, arranged in a weekly basis (on all Saturdays). Three numerical indicators, properly
called objective functions, are implemented to assess the goodness-of-fit of the calibrated
model to the empirical data: the likelihood L and log-likelihood ℓ, the residual sum of squares
RSS, and the Nash-Sutcliffe Efficiency Index NSE. Each one of these indicators are used where
appropriate.

4.1.3
Calibration methods
The model is calibrated through several techniques that are made available in the package
spotpy:
• the Latin HyperCube Sampling (LHS);
• the Differential Evolution Adaptive Metropolis Algorithm (DREAM) (Vrugt et al., 2010);
• the Differential Evolution Markov Chain Algorithm (DE-MCz) (Ter Braak & Vrugt, 2008).
While the first and the third methods have been used on exploitative calibrations, the final
results are obtained through the DREAM algorithm. It consists of a stable implementation of a
Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) method that runs several parallel chains simultaneously
to achieve a good exploitation of the parameter hyperspace. Furthermore, it implements a Dif-
ferential Distribution and a Metropolis-Hastings update step to adjust the jumps between two
subsequent steps. The algorithm is first initialized with a prior broad flat uniform distribution
as reported in table 4.3, it runs six parallel chains up to a O(106) total realizations.

5.3
Bayesian inference of the results
5.3.1
Outline
The DREAM algorithm that has been adopted to calibrate the model consists in a Markov
Chain Monte Carlo method that runs several chains sampling the parametric hyperspace.
The process starts from a given prior distributions and develops a posterior distribution
through the aforesaid sampling. This section studies these posterior distributions as well
as the evolution of the algorithm’s Markov Chains. As suggested by Vrugt et al. (2010), the
maximum number of iteration has been chosen so high (O(106)) to allow a convergence before
this number is exceeded. However, convergence could not be achieved and the sampling
maintains a random jumping pattern throughout the entire simulation. The simulation has
been stopped when the algorithm could not find a better solution through the sampling of
the hyperspace and the likelihood of this happening was deemed reasonably neglectable.
However, at this time, the algorithm has not converged and a better solution, which could
likely be characterized by different parameters and, potentially, a different ranking in reference
to the other epidemiological models, could still exist. The Markov chains are shown in figure
5.7, where — due to the strong oscillatory nature of the sampled parameters, which results in
a statistical noise that hinders a thorough interpretation of their random walk — the Savitzky-
Golay quadratic filtering algorithm with an averaging window width of 2500 points has been
used to smooth the data and eliminate the associated noise. The comparison between the
data before and after the smoothing is shown in figure 5.8.
Furthermore, Markov chains have been sorted according to the sampled realizations’ likeli-
hood, to check whether those realizations that are characterized by a better goodness-of-fit
converge towards a common solution. The sorted Markov chains are illustrated in figure 5.9,
where it does emerge a convergence towards a common parametrization of the model. The
sorting depicts how the top realizations, in terms of their associated likelihood, are described
by a fluctuation around the same set of parameters, which has been presented in the previous
section. In addition, as the figure describes, the six chains commonly converge towards the
top-performing set of parameters, which suggests that the chosen parametrization is the
actual global optimum.

5.3.2
Discussion
The six Markov chain have explored the parameters’ hyperspace and yielded several different
realization of the epidemiological model, each one connected to a different set of the governing
parameters. Although the DREAM method is generally expected to converge towards the global
optimum within a reasonable number of interventions, this was not the case. As figures 5.7
and 5.8 show, the chains maintain their oscillatory behaviour without reaching a common
final value. This issue could be potentially connected to the following reasons:
• the calibration window is quite broad, which results in a greater number of points to
evaluate, given that the epidemiological output is compared to the empirical data for
each one of the ten departments;
56
==PAGE:75==
5.3. Bayesian inference of the results
• the a priori distributions as well as the parameters’ bounds have a large support;
• there is an high number of parameters that require calibration and are not referenced to
the literature;
• on a more epidemiological note, the period of interest covers the endemic phase of
cholera in Haiti, where the cholera incidence is generally low and thus is subject to
statistical noise that might alter the quality of the overall calibration. Additionally, the
low incidence of the disease results in a higher sensitivity of the governing parameters.
Addressing the reasons introduced above does not necessarily result in a convergence of the
algorithm. For instance, the calibration window cannot be restricted, because the model
involves a progressively changing exposure coefficient. Any temporal restriction would affect
the ability of correctly calibrating the model that includes the WaSH interventions. On the
other hand, the parameter bounds can be further restricted. This could be the case for θ,
φ, and σ, where the mode of the distribution is strongly skewed towards the lower bound.
Referencing these parameters to the literature could lead to a faster convergence, although it
might cause a loss of information and a potentially underfit model. Another option could be
varying the number of Markov chains that run simultaneously. However, while increasing the
number of chains allows a better exploitation of the parameters’ hyperspace, thus increasing
the probability of finding the global optimum, a convergence might be harder to achieve
due to the greater number of chains. On the contrary, having less chains could achieve a
convergence faster, but in a local maximum.
The main outcome of the lack of a convergence of the Markov chains is the model non-
identifiability, which is an issue that arises where different sets of parameters might result in
similar model outputs and an analogue likelihood. When a non-identifiability problem exists,
the model prediction might be inaccurate and the determination of the confidence interval is
not possible (J. H. Guillaume et al., 2019). While an identifiability analysis was not possible
due to time constraints nor it was possible to recalibrate the model by choosing narrower
parameters’ intervals, the model realizations converge towards the best, as illustrated in figure
5.9. This might indicate that the optimum that has been found by the algorithm is actually
a global optimum, thus supporting the calibration that has been completed. As a matter of
fact, the six chains simultaneously converge towards the best realization when they are sorted
according to the likelihood, despite not coming to a common solution at the end of their
random walk. In addition, the plots shown in figure 5.9 also report that the best realizations
are located in a reduced interval, thus implying that a recalibration of the model could be
characterized by more restricted parameters’ bounds.

CHAPTER6
Conclusions
While Water, Sanitation, and Hygiene (WaSH) interventions constitute a substantial humani-
tarian action that is widely implemented worldwide to tackle cholera, little is known about
their actual effect and effectiveness in driving the number of new infections down. Haiti is
one of the countries that have been hit by the current seventh cholera pandemic the most. To
address this exceptional epidemic insurgence, the Haitian health authorities, alongside with
a few worldwide organizations, rolled out a decennal plan that paved the way for a coordi-
nated response against cholera in Haiti. This plan consisted in an oral vaccination campaign,
coupled with WaSH case-area targeted interventions focused on promoting awareness and
distributing specific sanitary materials. To date, the 2010 epidemic of cholera has been de-
clared extinct (Pan American Health Organization & World Health Organization, 2020). As
the health institutions report, cholera eradication was achieved through the joint action of
vaccinations and interventions, which require and provide prompt action when a new cholera
infection is detected or rumored. The aim of this thesis is understanding and assessing how
the interventions have acted on the control of cholera i.e. which mechanisms of improve-
ment of the epidemiological conditions in Haiti are activated through the deployment of such
interventions.
The WaSH interventions are therefore taken into account by introducing a new variable, which
is the relative proportion of interventions deployed in a given day, or week, by the nodal popu-
lation. The resulting variable is spatially explicit and is considered on a departmental scale. It is
thus imported in an compartmental epidemiological model of cholera that has been developed
by the researchers of the Laboratory of Ecohydrology at EPFL. Well referenced mechanisms of
infection as well as interactions between the population compartments are considered. Like
most epidemiological models of cholera, the one implemented within this study considers
an additional compartment representing the environmental pathogenic concentration. It is,
in fact, well documented that the presence and the subsequent concentration of bacteria in
the hydric environment is strongly correlated with the possibility of getting infected by the
63
==PAGE:82==
Chapter 6. Conclusions
pathogen that provokes cholera. These organisms enter the aquatic reservoir through the
faeces that are being shed by the infected people. Healthy susceptible individuals, if exposed
through the consumption of contaminated water or food, can become infected and start, in
turn, shedding. The models run on a departmental scale i.e. develop the aforementioned
epidemiological classes for each one of the ten Haitian departments and establish a mobility
matrix between the several nodes through the road network. The transmission of the disease
through riverine pathways is here neglected because of the choice of the nodes’ matrix. A
seasonal behaviour driven by rainfall is implemented by means of a coefficient that enhances
the shedding rate of pathogens into the aquatic reservoir. To answer the research question and
elucidate the ultimate effect of the WaSH procedures, the existing model has been modified by
considering and implementing several mechanisms of action of the interventions within two
main setups for the compartmental model. A first mechanism (labelled with ME) hypothesizes
the reduction of the people’s exposure by raising their awareness on the means of infection
and distributing hygiene products. Within this hypothesis, two further possibilities on the
temporal effectiveness of these interventions are evaluated separately. A second process intro-
duces the WaSH interventions within the environmental concentration of Vibrio cholerae (MB
models). Specifically, two ways of action are developed, of which one produces an increase
of the bacterial mortality and the other one reduces the shedding rate. The interventions are
considered within a term featuring a power law. This tool, through its parameters, effectively
highlights the behaviour of the interventions. Precisely, the scale parameter denotes whether
the action does positively contribute to the reduction of the cholera infection or not, and the
exponent characterizes the behaviour as sublinear, linear or superlinear. While models MB
adopt an instantaneous effect of the interventions, which is similar to that of rainfalls, models
ME record interventions back in the past both in an unlimited and limited time window. Their
action has been evaluated together as well (MBE models).
These models have been calibrated through the DREAM (DiffeRential Evolution Adaptive
Metropolis) Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) algorithm that runs several chains simul-
taneously to explore the parameters’ hyperspace and computes the jumping between two
sampled points by analyzing the previous realizations altogether. Due to the high number of
parameters that require a calibration and the wide support of the prior distributions for these
parameters, six chains have been ran up to O(106) overall iterations. The calibration on the
models has achieved Nash-Sutcliffe Efficiencies ranging between 0.5 and 0.56. According to
the final likelihood of the best realization of each one of these models (i.e. the one that best fits
the empirical data), these propositions have been ranked by an implementation of the Akaike
Information Criterion (AIC) that evaluates the goodness-of-fit to the available information, but
penalizes the model with the highest number of unnecessary free parameters. The resulting
score is a trade-off between the loss of information of a given model (when underfitting occurs)
and the needed simplicity of the model, to avoid overfitting. When overfitting arises, the model
tends to have more parameters than those that could be justified by the data and, therefore,
64
==PAGE:83==
has an inferior predictive performance. The AIC score is availed of to rank the models and
select the one with the best performance. In agreement with this analysis, the model that
considers an action upon the exposure parameter with a limited temporal effectiveness of the
interventions is selected.
The study therefore suggested that the WaSH interventions have had a positive effect in
reducing the epidemiological recurrence of cholera in Haiti. Furthermore, the calibrated
exponent of the power law, which is greater than the unity, indicates that the effect of the
interventions act superlinearly on the reduction of the exposure parameter and thus more
interventions produce an even greater fallout. However, the calibrated width of the moving
time window implies a temporally restricted effectiveness of such interventions, which might
be detrimental for the success in eradicating cholera if the interventions are deployed too far
apart. Additionally, because the contagion does not mainly occur through human-to-human
transmission, but through an aquatic reservoir, interventions in Haiti need to be maintained
for at least two more years and provide a swift response if cholera alerts arise (Pan American
Health Organization & World Health Organization, 2020).
The lack of a final convergence of the DREAM algorithm constitutes the main limitation of
this piece of research, despite having achieved a satisfactory goodness-of-fit of the model.
Although the results have indeed highlighted the importance of the WaSH interventions, which
was the main research question, the application of the calibrated model for forecasting may
lead to unstable and uncertain predictions. In fact, credible intervals could not be yielded due
to the noisy posterior distributions and the behaviour of the Markov chains. While sorting the
Markov chains has shown a virtual convergence towards the best solution, which supports
the set of parameters that have been selected, to expand the purposes of the research further,
additional effort focused on the model calibration and the resulting Markov chains would
be required. In addition, the vaccination campaign that has been carried out along with the
interventions should be embedded within this research. This would ultimately clarify the
respective importance of the vaccinations and WaSH interventions, thus promoting a more
efficient management and allocation of the resources with an optimization of their effect from
an epidemiological standpoint.
Nowadays, new cholera infections have reportedly disappeared in Haiti. Nevertheless, the
research on cholera control can be developed further by investigating aspects that are beyond
the scope of this study. First of all, this study does not contemplate the oral vaccination cam-
paign, which is of fundamental importance when it comes to analyzing an elimination plan of
the disease to its fullest. Vaccinated people are healthy individuals that are not susceptible of
getting the disease. Therefore, they would belong to additional compartments, which might be
more than one in the case of decreasing immunity to the disease, and therefore removed from
the other main epidemiological classes. Having the vaccinations integrated with the WaSH
65
==PAGE:84==
Chapter 6. Conclusions
interventions would create a substantial asset in epidemiological forecasting and achieve a
more efficient final plan. In addition, the current research can still be improved by achieving
a convergence of the calibrating algorithm. With the current results, the non-identifiability
issue that arose still constitutes the greatest limitation of the research. Although the cali-
brated model can be used to produce scenarios through which the WaSH interventions are
deployed to control cholera, confidence intervals cannot be produced and the optimization
of the governing parameters is rather unstable. The issue can be addressed through a better
exploration of the posterior distributions that can be attained by modifying the settings of the
DREAM algorithm (e.g. reducing the parameters’ bounds, increasing the number of iterations
and reviewing the number of active Markov chains, etc.). The research can also be enriched
through a downscaling of the WaSH intervention data to a watershed scale and an involve-
ment of the pathogenic transport through the river network. To do so, watershed should be
intersected with the municipal boundaries and interventions should be transferred according
to a demographic proportionality and the model recalibrated. This might, however, slow the
code down and the presence of an additional parameter might require more time to achieve
a fully converged algorithm. Moreover, a future development of this research could be the
achievement of a stochastic formulation of the epidemiological model, which would lead to a
redefinition of a few aspects of the model and a subsequential recalibration of it.
This piece of research has justified the implementation of the WaSH procedure by the Haitian
and international health authorities and by the non-governmental organizations. Specifically,
the study has disentangled the most likely patterns through which interventions might control
the prevalence of cholera and progressively bring it towards a complete disappearence and
their performance in the model calibration. Furthermore, the model can be used to forecast
and predict the unfolding of cholera in the future. While rainfall, which remains a strong driver
of cholera incidence, especially in a endemic phase of the disease, can easily be bootstrapped
with climate forecasting techniques, future interventions to be deployed can be considered by
the policymakers as part of several scenarios and their effect potentially evaluated in ad-hoc
plans intended to control cholera, not only in Haiti but in other affected areas as well.
