Abstract
This thesis compares various decentralized PID tuning methods to evaluate their effective-
ness and performance in multivariable process systems. The objective of these methods is to
address the fundamental problem of multivariable systems: the presence of process interac-
tions, where each manipulated variable affects all controlled variables.
Five multi-loop tuning methods from the literature were applied to two case studies of in-
creasing complexity. The ﬁrst case studied the quadruple tank system by Johansson (2000), a
2×2 linear system with adjustable interactions. Decentralized controllers met the performance
speciﬁcations for this case. The second case analyzed the four-stage spray dryer by Petersen
(2016), a nonlinear system with 12 states and 7 outputs, of which 4 are controlled resulting
in a 4 × 4 system. Although the performance speciﬁcations were achieved, the study revealed
limitations of decentralized PID controllers in some aspects.
Results in both the time and frequency domains highlight the strengths and weaknesses of each
method on each case. Decentralized PID controllers can be an effective and practical solution
for processes with diagonal dominance and low interaction between loops. However, their per-
formance might be limited in systems with higher coupling or complex dynamics. A thorough
analysis of the process dynamics combined with techniques to study the loop interactions, as
seen in this study, is essential to assess the viability of the strategy.

Introduction
1.1
Process industry
Manufacturing industry encompasses a wide range of industries that are involved in the
transformation of raw materials into valuable products. These industries include sectors such
as chemical, petrochemical, pharmaceutical, food and beverage, oil and gas, pulp and paper,
among others.
Global competition, a rapidly changing economic conditions, faster product development, and
more stringent environmental and safety regulations have made process control increasingly
important in the manufacturing process industry [1].
The chemical manufacturing industry has been affected for this tendency and control sys-
tems are necessary to match product speciﬁcations, safety, operational constrains, economical
constraints and use of resources [1].
Additionally, with the increased interest on new technologies and software, automated pro-
cesses have gained interest and are widely used, resulting in higher production yield and higher
quality of the products but also a higher manufacturing complexity.
Thus, modern plants have become more challenging to operate due to the trend towards
complex and highly integrated processes, which require the control of numerous parameters,
leading to multivariable systems.
From its deﬁnition, a system is named a multivariable system if it has more than one variable
to control [2]. However, the need to control parameters means also that some other have to
be manipulated, becoming then multiple input and multiple output (MIMO) systems.
This is more easily understood if an example from literature is taken. Figure 1.1 shows a
distillation column where several parameters needs to be controlled. In the example in Figure
1.1, there are ﬁve controlled variables (CV’s) and ﬁve manipulated variables (MV’s). The
controlled variables are distillate composition, xD; bottoms composition, xB; column pressure,
P; and the liquid levels in the reﬂux drum and column base, hD and hB. The ﬁve manipulated
variables are distillate and bottoms ﬂow rates, D and B; reﬂux ﬂow, R; and the heat duties for
the condenser and reboiler, QD and QB. [1].

1.2
Process control problem
Due to the increased emphasis on more efﬁcient plant operation, process control has gained
interest as one of the solutions. The primary objective of process control is to maintain a
process at the desired operating conditions, while satisfying all the other constraints and re-
quirements [1]. In essence, the goal of process control is to ensure that the output y behaves
as desired by adjusting the plant input u. This means that the manipulated variables (MVs)
are used to absorb disturbances and maintain the controlled variables (CVs) at their desired
setpoints by changing their signal level, ensuring this way an stable and efﬁcient operation of
the system.
The simplest control loop consists of two primary components: the controller and the
plant or process. See Figure 1.2 for an example. Here, the process output is fed back and

1.2. PROCESS CONTROL PROBLEM
The objective of the controller is that the process output y is stable at a given setpoint.
The setpoint is also called reference signal and is denoted by r or ysp.
When the process is capable of reaching stability i.e., y = r the system is stable and that
operating point is called steady-state.
1.2.1
Process dynamics
To correctly understand a system, process dynamics have to be addressed. Process dy-
namics refer to the unsteady-state (or transient) process behavior. It shows how the process
responds to changes in inputs, disturbances, or setpoint changes. Unlike steady-state behavior,
process dynamics focuses on the time-dependent evolution of the process variables.
Understanding the process dynamics of each process is crucial as it directly inﬂuences
stability and performance. Controllers are designed to handle not only the steady-state per-
formance but also dynamic behavior to avoid oscillations, instability, or poor response times.
It is usually advisable to consider process dynamics early in the process design. From the dy-
namics behavior of the system, a set of equations that allows the user to predict the behavior
of the process are obtained [2]. Additionally, transfer functions for the process can be derived
from ﬁrst-principle models or identiﬁed from input-output data to obtain a linear model.
1.2.2
Stability
The task of a feedback control system is thus to ensure the stability of the process, minimiz-
ing the inﬂuence of disturbances and perturbations and to optimize the overall performance.
These objectives are achieved by maintaining some variables (temperature, pressure, concen-
tration, position, speed, quality, ...) close to their desired values or using setpoints which can
be ﬁxed or time-dependent [3].
In process control, it is essential to consider disturbances. Disturbances are inputs to the pro-
cess that alter the manipulated signals, eventually creating an offset between the setpoint,
r, and the output, y. As a result, steady-state conditions are disrupted, and without a feed-
back control design, the steady-state conditions will not be achieved; therefore, the process is
unlikely to meet the performance requirements.
To compensate the effect of disturbance on the process, a feedback loop is added to the
system where the output y is recirculated back to the controller node. Consequently, a new
parameter which is the error, named e, is created as the difference of the output y and the
setpoint r as e = r −y. This simple feedback loop with constant update on the input u is called
closed-loop. Closed-loop control is the basis for process control and gives the controller the

1.3
Tuning objective
Once the process is understood, the next step is to design and tune the controller. In many
cases, the design and tuning phases can be carried out simultaneously. A Proportional-Integral-
Derivative (PID) controller design procedure should include these three aspects: process model
identiﬁcation, controller structure design and PID tuning [4]. Design mainly involves deter-
mining the structure and form of the controller and ensure that the chosen control system
and architecture work well with the speciﬁc process being faced. Tuning of a PID controller
refers to the process of adjusting the controller’s parameters to meet the desired performance
speciﬁcations, such as stability, speed of response, and robustness.
At steady-state, most closed-loop systems present stability; however, when a disturbance
(e.g., a feed ﬂow rate variation) affects the system, it can become unstable. Tuning the con-
troller means adjusting the inner settings of the controller so that the desired closed-loop
system performance is obtained. In other words, when the process conditions change or the
controller is not properly tuned, the closed-loop system can become unstable or perform poorly,
leading to inaccuracies in the process.
A typical controller has three basic parameters that determine its function, namely the gain,
Kc; the integral time, τI; and derivative time, τD. These are the parameters that should be
selected so that the controller is tuned for the particular process. There is a variety of methods
that can be used to determine these parameters; there are simple techniques that require little
process knowledge as well as more elaborate methods that use more information about the
process [5]. It all depends on the demands and nature of the process. These methods will
be discussed later for both single-input single-output (SISO) and multiple-input and multiple-
output systems. However, it is worth mentioning that the task of tuning gets signiﬁcantly more
challenging and complex when interactions between loops have to be taken into account.
The function of a feedback control system is to ensure that the closed-loop system has
desirable dynamic and steady-state response characteristics. Ideally, a well designed and tuned
closed-loop system would satisfy the following performance criteria [1]:
• The closed-loop must be stable
• The effects of disturbances are minimized
• Rapid and smooth responses to setpoint changes

• Steady-state error (offset) is eliminated
• Excessive control action is avoided
• Robust control system i.e., insensitive to changes in process conditions and inaccuracies

The tuning of a controller often involves compromising some of the early mentioned criteria.
For a given closed-loop system, achieving a faster control response may require more aggres-
sive tuning, which is likely to result in overshoots or signal ampliﬁcation. Conversely, priori-
tizing robustness typically needs slower responses to both disturbances and setpoint changes
and sometimes the objectives levels might not be met [1].
There are mainly three types of external signals which can affect the stability of the con-
troller. Those are load disturbances, measurement noise and setpoint changes. In the process
industry, load disturbances and noise are very common, while setpoint signals often remain
constant for long periods of time [6]. Tuning objective in process control refer to the desired
outcomes achieved by adjusting controller parameters to meet system requirements. The most
frequent desired outcome for a closed-loop system are:
• Good tracking performance: Ensure that the controlled variable (CVs) follows the set-
point closely, critical for processes where deviations are unacceptable.
• Disturbance rejection: Minimize the effect of external disturbances on the CVs.
• Offset removal: Eliminate persistent deviations between the CVs and the setpoint, en-
suring steady-state accuracy.
Seborg (2016) chapter 12.6 [1] illustrates how these objectives are prioritized differently
based on the type of process variable handled. For instance, ﬂow and pressure control loops
are characterized by fast responses with frequent but generally small disturbances (generally
due to noise). In these cases, disturbance rejection is the primary focus. Liquid level control
loops face two control problems with different objectives: tight level control (control at or
near a setpoint) and averaging level control which manages levels between high and low limits
to dampen ﬂuctuations in inlet ﬂows. Contrarily, general guidelines for temperature control
loops are difﬁcult to state because of the wide variety of processes and equipment involving
heat transfer. Due to their complex dynamics, temperature loops usually require a balance of
all the mentioned objectives.

1.5
Problem statement
The primary objective of this thesis is to explore and evaluate different methods for tuning
decentralized PID controllers in a multivariable system environment. Speciﬁcally, it aims to
ﬁnd effective methodologies for tuning multi-loop systems where individual controllers are
designed and tuned based on single-loop techniques. This work seeks to address whether
decentralized PID tuning can be a viable strategy for industrial applications based on perfor-
mance speciﬁcations. Each approach will be studied in terms of performance plots and some
metrics to ease the comparison.
To investigate this, two study cases will be studied: the linear quadruple tank system by
Johansson (2000) [8], and the GEA four-spray dryer [9].
The goal is to study each system, evaluate and compare the performance of these different
methods, potentially ﬁnding scenarios where they fall short.

1.5. PROBLEM STATEMENT
Multivariable algorithms like LQR (Linear Quadratic Regulator) and MPC (Model Predic-
tive Control) are receiving a lot of attention in industry because they are well-suited for systems
where multiple inputs and outputs interact simultaneously. However, this thesis focuses on de-
termining whether multi-loop systems with decentralized controllers can provide a viable and
efﬁcient alternative to this more complex methods.
While single-loop tuning methods are well-established and widely understood, MIMO-PID con-
trol remains less mature in comparison. This thesis aims to develop a stronger knowledge on
this speciﬁc techniques.
The outcomes of this thesis are expected to beneﬁt both academia and industry. On the
academic side, it will offer more knowledge on techniques for MIMO systems, potentially
discovering new useful methods.
For industry, it aims to provide alternative approaches based on the design and tuning of
multi-loop controllers in decentralized structures, which aim to reduce computational and
engineering effort while achieving the performance demands.

2.1
PID Control
The PID controller is by far the most common control algorithm. Most feedback loops are
controlled by this algorithm or minor variations of it [5]. PID controllers have been used in
industrial control applications for a long time. First PID controllers date to 1890s but it was
not until the 1940s that the production of general purpose PID-controllers gained momentum
[6], [10]. Despite being in the industry for a long time, the popularity of PID controller in
feedback control system is unmatched, the PID controller still give a performance that are up
to the expectations and hardly beatable for other types of controllers in terms of cost/beneﬁt.
This widespread use of PID in the industry can be attributed to the instrument simplicity, ease
of re-tuning it, easy implementation and maintenance. In control system applications, 90-95%
of control loops are of PID form. [10], [11].
As the name indicates, the PID controller employs three modes: proportional, integral
and derivative term. However, PID controllers can be used in proportional only (P-mode),
proportional-integral (PI mode), proportional-derivative (PD mode) and proportional-integral-
derivative (PID mode) depending on the process requirements.

Frequency Domain
It is also relevant to introduce the frequency domain and the frequency response analysis.
Frequency domain analysis is fundamental in control systems due to its ability to reveal system
characteristics that are not easily observed in the time domain. The frequency domain is a
representation of a system or signal in terms of frequency rather than time. It focuses on an
alternative way to analyze dynamic systems by using frequency response analysis. Frequency
response concepts and techniques play an important role in stability analysis, control system
design, and robustness assessment [1].
In the frequency domain, signals and systems are characterized by their magnitude and
phase responses. When a process is fed with a sinusoidal input u(t), its output y(t) is also a
sinusoidal wave of the same frequency [1]. However, the output undergoes two key changes:
• Phase Shift (ϕ): The output sine wave lags or leads the input by an angle ϕ, known as
the phase angle.
• Amplitude Ratio (AR): The output amplitude ˆA is scaled relative to the input amplitude
A, and their ratio deﬁnes the amplitude ratio, such as AR =

As the input frequency ω increases, the amplitude ratio typically approaches zero, indicat-

of stable systems, regardless of their complexity [1]. Both the phase shift and amplitude ratio
depend directly on the input frequency ω, making them fundamental to understand speciﬁc
system behaviors.
Bode Diagram
The Bode diagram (or Bode plot) shows the frequency response characteris-
tics in which AR and ϕ are plotted as a function of ω. These plots are particularly useful for
rapid analysis of the response characteristics and stability of closed-loop systems. Normally,
the frequency is expressed in units of radians/time, the amplitude ratio in decibels and the
phase angle in degrees (rather than radians) [1]. Each controller or transfer function model
has a characteristic behavior on the Bode plot, with distinct patterns in magnitude and phase
that reﬂect its dynamic properties. These responses, such as the magnitude of the slope or
phase lag of a certain system, make of Bode plots a powerful tool for identifying and analyzing
systems.
Bode diagrams also give name to the Bode Stability criterion. The Bode Stability criterion
provides a measure of the relative stability by merely analyzing the characteristic equation
of the closed loop transfer function. The Bode Stability criterion says that for an open-loop
transfer function that is strictly proper (more poles than zeros) and has no poles located on
or to the right of the imaginary axis, with the possible exception of a single pole at the origin
(integrator pole), the closed-loop system is stable if the open-loop amplitude ratio AROL(ωc) <
0dB [1]. Where, ωc is known as the critical frequency and it is the frequency for which the
phase of the open loop transfer function reaches an angle of −180◦.
Gain and Phase margins
Two more metrics can be obtained from the Bode plot. Those are
the gain and the phase margin. Gain Margin (GM) is the amount by which the system’s gain can
be increased before it becomes unstable, measured at the phase crossover frequency (where the
phase is −180◦). It is typically expressed in decibels (dB). Phase Margin (PM) is the additional
phase lag that can be introduced before the system reaches instability, measured at the gain
crossover frequency (where the gain is 1 ≈0dB). It is expressed in degrees. Both metrics
quantify how close a system is to instability and are essential for designing robust and well-
behaved control systems. In general, large values of GM and PM correspond to sluggish closed-
loop responses, whereas smaller values result in less sluggish, more oscillatory responses [1].

Variable pairing
From a system with multiple variables, the choice of controlled variables is
usually fairly intuitive. Engineering judgment, based on a good understanding of the process,
leads usually to a logical choice. Considerations of economics, safety, constraints, availability
and reliability of sensors, etc., must be factored into this decision [29]. Choosing the MVs
based on process dynamics is desired but all the possible pairings should be study. RGA is a
good technique to choose the pairings but can also be used to create subsets of possible MVs
and CVs.
Once the nxn of CVs and MVs is obtained, techniques to pair those are recommended to
study the interactivity levels.
2.4.1
Relative Gain Array (RGA)
The RGA method is used to pair inputs and outputs measuring the process interactions.
RGA measures two-way interactions between the outputs and inputs, indicating which input

The sequential loop tuning involves tuning one loop at the time, each step in the design
procedure involves designing only one SISO controller until all loops have been designed.
The ﬁnal controller design depend on the order in which the controllers are designed and, as
only one controller is considered is considered at a time, the closing of subsequent loops may
alter the response of previous loops making iteration necessary [28]. When doing the sequen-

loop k is being tuned at that time, the matrices used in the design of the k’s controller will
include the information of the previously designed controller, aiming to get a more accurate
tuning for the on-going controller. For the undesigned controllers in the k + 1 loop and so

decouplers to the system. Decouplers are additional controllers designed to compensate for
process interactions and reduce control loop coupling. However, decouplers are not commonly
used, because the ideal decoupler is not physically achievable and model inaccuracies can limit
their effectiveness [1]. Adding two extra controllers to the system increases its complexity and
cost, without necessarily achieving the desired performance. For these reasons, decouplers will
not be considered in this project. The goal is to maintain the original closed-loop structure,
where there are n controllers for n CVs and MVs.

Having a look at the transfer functions of the process in Equation 3.2, it can be seen that both
diagonal transfer functions have a similar τi value, which means that the control response

bigger interactions or to be constantly trying to correct the error caused by the other loop
giving sluggish and poor control response. A simple adjustment to avoid this unnecessary

stable for all values of Kc, providing that its sign is correct.[46]. Based on these observations,

Disturbance rejection and noise sensitivity
Disturbance rejection and noise sensitivity are critical elements that deﬁne a system’s stabil-
ity. The sensitivity function S(jω) measures disturbance rejection, while the complementary
sensitivity function H(jω) measures noise sensitivity [52].
In complex control systems, with multiple delays and varying noise levels, the study of distur-
bance attenuation and noise sensitivity becomes essential [41].
The relationship between both output-to-plant sensitivity function is linked by the equation
S + H = I. Here, S is the Output-to-plant sensitivity function and H is the Output-to-plant
complementary sensitivity function [53], [54]. Both were deﬁned during the sequential loop
closing tuning method where they are used to estimate the tuning parameters (see Equations
2.36 and 2.37).
This equality equation underscores the trade-offs between disturbance rejection and noise
attenuation [52]. In both the continuous time domain and the frequency domain, the rela-
tionship S + H = 1 holds as a fundamental property of feedback systems.
Ideally, in a SISO system, S should be small to obtain the beneﬁts of feedback, and H
should also be small to avoid sensitivity to noise which is one of the disadvantages of feedback.
Unfortunately, these requirements are not simultaneously possible at any frequency as it is
clear from Equation S + H = I. This equality implies that at any frequency either S(jω) or
H(jω) must be larger than or equal to 0.5 [30]. Additionally, systems with time delays and
right-half-plane (RHP) dynamics exhibit peaks in S(jω), indicating reduced robustness and
increased sensitivity to disturbances at those speciﬁc frequencies [30]. Special attention has
to be given to those cases.
On the other hand, for a MIMO system, deriving the identity equation S + H = 1, it is
obtained:

or tracking error across all directions [53], [54]. Thus, while in a SISO, the sensitivity function
S can be high while the complementary sensitivity function H is low (or vice versa). It is more
complex for a MIMO system, where both S and H can have high values simultaneously due to
loop interactions; here the trade-off between disturbance rejection and noise attenuation gets
more critical. Nevertheless, the sensitivity functions are still governed by the identity S+H = 1
which will be clearly seen at high and low frequencies.
In this case, the robustness of the control system will be evaluated by using a combina-
tion of SISO loop-based analysis and MIMO maximum singular value analysis to check for
robustness in both the individual loops and the global overall system.
The decentralized PID controllers are designed based on a SISO framework, treating each
loop independently. For the individual tuning methods, the Bode plots of sii(jω) and hii(jω)
provide valuable insights into the performance and robustness of individual loops.
However, while the SISO analysis ensures the performance of individual loops, it does not
capture the interactions and coupling on the global system. These interactions can lead to poor
robustness globally, even if the individual loops perform well. To address this, the maximum
singular value of the complementary sensitivity (¯σ(H(jω))) matrix is analyzed. This analysis
complements the SISO results by providing a global measure of robustness.

Biggest-Log Modulus tuning method
The BLT method is also used for comparison for the Spray Dryer case. Oppositely to the
quadruple tank system, since the process transfer function do have delay, the ultimate gain
and the ultimate frequency can be calculated.
To determine the ultimate gain (Ku) and ultimate frequency (ωu) of the process transfer
functions, a systematic procedure is followed using frequency response analysis.
Step 1. Compute the frequency response using the bode plot data, the magnitude and
phase responses of the diagonal transfer function Gii are calculated across the frequency range.
Step 2. Identify the ultimate frequency. The ultimate frequency (ωu), is the frequency
where the phase lag of the system equals −180◦. This is achieved by ﬁnding the frequency at
which the absolute difference between the phase response and −180◦is minimized.
Step 3. Calculate the Ultimate Gain. The transfer function Gii is evaluated at the ultimate
frequency to compute its complex response. Then, the ultimate gain (Ku) is determined as the
reciprocal of the real part of the transfer function evaluated at the ultimate frequency [31].
The ultimate gains (Ku) and ultimate frequencies (ωu) of the diagonal transfer functions

This multi-loop tuning method without proportional kick is also included in the document.
This multi-loop tuning method proposes an easy tuning method including a modiﬁcation
on how the input signal is calculated. This was explained during Section 2.5.4 but the main
difference lies in how the controller computes the controller input signals. For a PI controllers
as the ones being used for this case, controller input’s signal is calculated for the controller
such as:
u(s) = Kc{−y(s) + (1/τIs)e(s)}
The main consideration of the system is that controller needs to work on deviation vari-
ables, otherwise the absolute −y(s) signal would cause a disproportionate signal. However,
the ESDIRK solver needs the absolute value to compute the correct states and outputs given
the conditions. Thus, after the calculation of the outputs, y has to be manipulated such as
y = y −yss where yss is the nominal value at t = 0. Then, after the computation of u, u is
converted back to the absolute value by u = u + uss, ensuring the correct states calculation.
Following the guidelines explained on Chien (1999), the design parameter τc can be cal-
culated using the ratio between τ/θ. However, as observed in previous methods, common
guidelines frequently result in overly aggressive tuning for the real system. Following strictly
the guidelines described on the paper, resulted in sustained oscillations with high-amplitude.
As a result, more conservative rules to choose τc must be used.
Last step of the tuning method is the RGA-based detuning factor. This de-tuning rule
applies if any value of RGA(λii) < 1. Thus, looking at the G plant matrix (see 4.12) the ﬁrst
loop parameters must be de-tuned using a factor of 0.97.

4.5.1
Single-loop robustness analysis
The robustness of the individual loops can be analyzed by plotting the frequency response
of the S sensitivity function and the H complementary sensitivity function. In this ﬁrst part,
each loop is treated as a SISO system.
A good closed-loop performance requires minimization of the effects of both disturbance
rejection and noise attenuation [52]. As mentioned, this is indeed impossible and a trade-off
have to be done. |S(jω)|) is desired to be low at low frequencies and |H(jω)|) is desired to be
low at high frequencies [30].
In the plots, individual sii and hii are plotted for each loop, showing its behavior and allow-
ing the comparison between the used methods. This will help check the desired disturbance
rejection and noise attenuation while showing any deﬁciency on the controller design, based
on the frequency response.

Looking at Figure 4.27, |S(jω)|) remains low at low frequencies. For any proper system,
|S(jω)|) will approach unity as the frequency becomes large [55]. At the same time, |H(jω)|)
is equal to 1 following the |H(jω)| + |S(jω| = 1 equality. At mid frequencies range, both sen-
sitivity functions start to variate, |S(jω)|) increases while |H(jω)|) decreases simultaneously.
When both functions transition, it implies a critical balance point, where disturbance rejection
start to degrade and noise attenuation becomes more relevant [52]. Then, at high frequencies
|S(jω)|) = 1 and |H(jω)|) = 0.
The bandwidth (ωb) of each loop is deﬁned by the frequency where |S(jω|) crosses for
the ﬁrst time the point −0.707 ≈−3 dB [30]. Bandwidth shows the frequency range in
which each system effectively rejects disturbances, beyond that point the disturbance rejection
worsens. For frequencies larger than ωb, the system doesn’t effectively reject high-frequency
disturbances and may amplify them [1], [30]. Increased bandwidth implies less attenuation
of outputs signal i.e., the system follows the reference signal more closely, more effective
disturbance rejection and faster responses [55].
Loop 1 and Loop 2 present higher bandwidths, which results on a better disturbance rejec-
tion over a wider frequency range and faster responses but resulting in poorer high-frequency

It is possible to observe small peaks at mid frequencies in most loops where the equality
|H(jω)| + |S(jω| = 1 is not conserved. This is due to the presence of time delays which cause
the system to act as if it had RHP-zeros. Original G plant transfer functions do not have any
RHP-zero; however, this computation assumes that all internal delays are set to zero. To better
assess the system behavior a Padé approximation has to be done, which adds an RHP-zero.
Processes having time delays may be assumed to have inﬁnite number of RHP-zeros. For this
reason, it is fair to say that the time delay term acts as a RHP in terms of the expected behavior
on the S function bode plot [56].

respective transfer function. The magnitude of these peaks i.e., the excess over 1, is given by
the controller gain. Skogestad (2001) demonstrates that increasing Kc, increases the system
bandwidth, resulting in a better low-frequency disturbance rejection. However, this comes at
the cost of reduced robustness and higher peaks in S see in Figure 4.27. A loop with very high
Kc may push the system closer to instability and cause oscillations.
These peaks in the S plots reﬂect zones in the frequency range with reduced robustness. This
is explained by the waterbed effect which says that if sensitivity is pushed down at some fre-
quencies then it will have to increase at others [30].

methods, it is an indicator of whether the decentralized control could yield similar results

maximum singular value ¯σ of the complementary sensitivity function ¯σ|H(jω)|.

frequency range to ensure nominal stability and good performance. Ensuring that ¯σ|H(jω)|
remains low is generally indicative of a well-behaved and robust system.

(≈0). However, at mid-frequencies, ¯σ|H(jω)| experiences a pronounced spike, suggesting
that there could be issues with nominal stability and performance at this range using the
current tuning methods and the G plant. At high frequencies, the system acts as expected and
drops until a magnitude of ≈0.

Results Evaluation
After the convey of the results for both cases, some discussion can be done. Overall, ac-
ceptable PI control performance is obtained with all techniques.
The main objective of this thesis was to demonstrate whether decentralized PID tuning
methods could work as a lower complexity alternative to multivariable controllers. The ini-
tial approach was to identify methods that would prove effective and distinguish them from
those that would not. However, although slightly additional adaptations had to be done, their
performance was tolerable and individual setpoints were met for all cases and methods. This
changed the initial objective perspective to the identiﬁcation of the best performance, and later
comparison of the different methods.
The used methods were found in the literature. The vast majority were old, showcasing a
trend towards multivariable controller strategies. However, their results were similar to the
obtained here and usually end in a comparison between methods. This shows that the most
recognized literary multi-loop methods do provide solutions to multivariable process systems
even though they can lack for certain systems or in certain aspects (overall robust performance,
etc,).
There were more methods on the list but due to the time limitation only the most widespread
were considered.
Throughout both cases, the single loop SIMC technique was used with tolerable perfor-
mance depending on the needs. This shows that, SISO methods could be used to get a starting
point for further tuning if some previous knowledge on process control and/or the process is
possessed.
However, it is demonstrated that obtaining a perfectly-tuned multivariable system using SISO
method is, a priori, a poor approach since interactions are not addressed simultaneously at
any point. Multi-loop methods address this fact, making them more convenient. Taking a look
to the non-constrained system at Appendix A.3, SIMC based performance cause oscillations to
propagate across loops, worsening the signal before eventually reaching steady-state.
Looking at Tables 3.15-3.16 and 4.6-4.7, multi-loop tuning methods give an overall im-
provement in metrics with IAE and TV mitigation. A higher IAE value indicates larger and/or
prolonged deviations from the setpoint, whereas a higher TV implies larger and more frequent

The ﬁrst case describing the quadruple tank system from Johansson (2000) was based

The spray dryer was presented in the second case. The spray dryer model described a non-

account for un-modeled dynamics and environmental perturbations.
Model uncertainty is usually added to the model G; uncertainty is bounded but otherwise
unknown, creating several possible plant scenarios [30].

satisﬁes performance speciﬁcations without model uncertainty.

the other hand, some methods were added in the Spray dryer case thanks to the presence of

during the quadruple tank. Instead, a simple stability analysis based on the Bode plots of the

was decided not to develop it for the spray dryer case and look instead for the Gain and Phase
margins for a relative stability check.

sitivity and robustness following different approaches, trying to give the clearer and more
understandable results.

Another approach in terms of robustness analysis would be to compare the decentralized

6
Conclusions
The objective of this thesis was to test various decentralized PID tuning methods to evalu-
ate their performance on multivariable systems.
The aim was to identify effective methodologies for tuning multi-loop PI controllers. 5 tech-
niques were studied using two different cases: the quadruple tank system (Chapter 3) and the
Spray dryer system (Chapter 4).
The quadruple tank system demonstrated that decentralized methods can be viable so-
lution for low-order systems with mild interactions. The quadruple tank case represented a
linear system with adjustable interactions; when the interactions were reasonable, decentral-
ized PID controllers met nominal performance requirements, providing a simpler alternative
to complex techniques.
Robust and stable systems were achieved. However, their similar responses pointed the need
for comparison with a more complex system.
In contrast, the spray dryer case revealed challenges when high-order systems with com-
plex and/or unobserved dynamics are involved. Although the performance objectives are met,
transient performance often deviated from linear-based expectations, requiring additional de-
tuning or adjustments during the controller design. Aggressive time-domain performance ap-
peared overly conservative during the single-loop frequency-domain analysis based on the gain
and phase margins, indicating a potential mismatch between the used tuning approaches and
the real system behavior. Issues such as the lack of robustness shown on Figure 4.29 of the
¯σ(H(jω)) plot, suggest that advanced control methods might be more appropriate.
This thesis also demonstrated that SISO methods like SIMC can be effective for tuning
low-coupled systems. Subsequent ﬁne-tuning through detuning or iterative adjustments, gave
acceptable results on the quadruple tank. However, relying solely on SISO methods isn’t rec-
ommended, as numerous iterations are required to achieve the desired performance.
In summary, decentralized PID controllers could potentially represent an alternative in
some systems; or, at least, offer initial settings for further tuning or benchmark for comparative
studies. However, strong prior knowledge on the process and its dynamics is crucial to assess
whether multi-loop PID would be a viable solution.

The following formula shows the determinant of I + GC used during the development of

This equation includes interactions up to the fourth order for all four control loops. At the
fourth-order level, a reduced list of terms is used since almost all signiﬁcant interactions are
already captured by the previous order terms. This approach simpliﬁes the expression and
reduces its complexity, making it more manageable.
