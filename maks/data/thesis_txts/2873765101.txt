Abstract
This thesis investigates a time-adaptive recursive quantile regression (RQR) framework for
short-term supermarket refrigeration load forecasting. Accurate and adaptive forecasts are
essential for energy-eﬀicient operation, cost savings, and enabling demand-side flexibility
in smart grids. Unlike classical mean-based models, quantile regression estimates the full
conditional distribution of the load, capturing asymmetric uncertainty and tail risks.
The RQR model is implemented in a rolling, online fashion, allowing it to adapt continu-
ously to new data. It builds upon a baseline autoregressive model with exogenous variables
(ARX), which provides deterministic forecasts using recursive least squares. The RQR ex-
tends this baseline by generating conditional quantiles and prediction intervals.
Both
models are evaluated on real supermarket data across multiple forecast horizons, using
deterministic metrics (RMSE, MAE) and probabilistic metrics (pinball loss, coverage, and
interval sharpness).
Results show that RQR offers modest but consistent improvements over ARX, particularly
at longer horizons, with better calibrated intervals and enhanced representation of uncer-
tainty. These probabilistic forecasts can support operational strategies such as demand
shifting or participation in dynamic pricing schemes, contributing to smarter and more
flexible energy use in supermarket refrigeration systems.

1
Introduction
The increasing electrification of society and the rapid integration of renewable energy
sources are fundamentally changing the dynamics of modern power systems. While re-
newable generation offers clear environmental benefits, it also introduces greater variability
and uncertainty into electricity supply. To balance these fluctuations, the demand side
must become more flexible, enabling loads to be shifted or curtailed in response to grid
conditions or price signals. Supermarkets with their extensive refrigeration systems rep-
resent one of the largest and most controllable types of commercial loads. Refrigeration
typically accounts for up to 50% of a supermarket’s total electricity consumption, making
it both economically and environmentally important to manage the load in a more eﬀicient
and intelligent way.
Accurate and reliable load forecasts are essential for enabling advanced control and op-
timization strategies. For example, when combined with dynamic pricing, forecasts can
inform pre-cooling strategies, shifting consumption to cheaper hours while maintaining
temperature thresholds. Probabilistic forecasts are even more valuable, allowing opera-
tors to assess risks and make uncertainty-aware decisions—such as scheduling load shifts
without violating food safety constraints. In the broader context of smart grids, supermar-
kets could even participate in demand response programs, but such participation requires
reliable forecasting over multiple horizons, including an understanding of uncertainty.
Traditional short-term load forecasting has often relied on autoregressive models with
exogenous variables (ARX). These models are simple, eﬀicient, and effective when system
dynamics are stable.
However, they are inherently deterministic and only predict the
conditional mean of the load. This can be limiting, especially for systems like refrigeration
where behavior is influenced by nonlinear effects, operational regimes (e.g., defrost cycles),
and asymmetric residuals. Moreover, point forecasts alone do not quantify uncertainty,
which is crucial in risk-aware optimization and load shifting.
Quantile regression provides a natural extension beyond mean-based forecasting by di-
rectly estimating conditional quantiles, enabling richer modeling of asymmetry, heteroskedas-
ticity, and tail behavior. Unlike distributional assumptions used in probabilistic ARX-type
models, quantile regression does not assume symmetric or Gaussian errors. It also allows
the construction of calibrated prediction intervals, which are particularly useful for oper-
ational decisions in energy systems.
Another key challenge is model adaptivity. Real-world load patterns evolve due to sea-
sonal effects, operational changes, or behavioral shifts. Static models trained on historical
data may quickly become outdated. Recursive estimation methods, which update model
parameters as new data arrive, offer a practical solution. Recursive Quantile Regression
(RQR), first introduced in the energy context by Møller, Nielsen, and Madsen [1] and
developed further in Møller, Nielsen, and Madsen [2], combines the benefits of adaptive
online learning with distributional forecasting. This makes it especially well-suited for
applications like refrigeration load forecasting, where both adaptivity and uncertainty
quantification are critical.
The goal of this thesis is to evaluate whether a time-adaptive RQR model can improve
the reliability and informativeness of refrigeration load forecasts compared to a traditional
ARX model. While ARX provides a strong point forecast baseline using recursive least

squares, the RQR model enriches these predictions with conditional quantiles and predic-
tive intervals. Forecast performance is assessed across multiple horizons (k = 1, 2, 6, 12, 24
hours ahead), using both deterministic metrics (RMSE, MAE) and probabilistic metrics
(pinball loss, coverage, interval width, interval score). The comparison is grounded in real
data from Danish supermarkets, using a rolling online forecast scheme.
Beyond predictive accuracy, the practical motivation is to support energy-eﬀicient super-
market operation through better forecast-driven decisions. Probabilistic forecasts allow
store operators to manage risk more effectively, respond to electricity prices, and partic-
ipate in flexible load programs. This has potential to reduce energy costs, enhance food
safety assurance, and support the integration of renewable energy.
The main contributions of this thesis are threefold. First, it demonstrates how Recursive
Quantile Regression can be implemented in a fully online, time-adaptive setting for re-
frigeration load forecasting. Second, it provides an empirical evaluation of RQR versus
a recursive ARX baseline, highlighting differences in point and interval forecasting per-
formance across multiple time horizons. Third, it discusses how distributional forecasting
enhances the operational relevance of supermarket load forecasts, particularly in contexts
where uncertainty and flexibility matter.
The remainder of the thesis is organized as follows. Chapter 2 introduces the refrigeration
system and the available dataset. Chapter 3 presents the theoretical background on ARX
models, recursive estimation, and quantile regression. It also outlines the rolling forecast
setup and the evaluation metrics. Chapter 4 presents the results and their interpretation.
Finally, Chapters 5 and 6 summarize the findings, reflect on limitations, and suggest
directions for future work.

Data & Structure
2.1
Data Overview
The dataset applied to this study captures the electrical energy usage of supermarkets
over the course of an entire year (2023) at hourly resolution. In total, it consists of 8,760
hourly observations, providing a compact and information-rich basis for analysis. In the
dataset there are data for 38 different stores as well as an aggregated total representing
all stores combined. For this project, the total will be used to model. The data includes
measured power consumption and key exogenous variables known to influence energy de-
mand, particularly ambient temperature and time-of-day indicators. All data streams are
time-aligned using a common timestamp index, ensuring that each hourly entry includes
the corresponding consumption and environmental measurements.
Context and Scope: The focus on supermarkets is motivated by their significant and
time-varying energy consumption patterns which combine to create a complex daily pro-
file that is sensitive to both human activity patterns and weather conditions. Accurate
forecasting of such loads is crucial for demand planning, energy eﬀiciency measures, and
integration with renewable energy sources [3]. It also extends beyond this into possible
energy market participation, as mentioned earlier. By employing a recursive quantile re-
gression approach, we aim to capture not only the expected energy usage but also the
uncertainty in those forecasts, which is essential for risk-aware decision making in energy
management. The data used here is representative of the key features and challenges in
short-term load forecasting for commercial buildings. In particular, supermarkets exhibit
pronounced daily cycles and weather-driven variability, making them a good case for test-
ing advanced forecasting methods.
Structure of the Dataset: For each hour of 2023, the dataset records the actual power
consumption and associated features. Table 2.1 provides a summary of the variables in-
cluded and their dimensions. The primary target variable is the power consumption P (in
kW), recorded as a vector or a single time series. Accompanying this are several predictor
variables, notably the ambient temperature in various forms. The ambient temperature is
included both as an observed historical series and as forecast scenarios from a numerical
weather prediction (NWP) model. The forecasted ambient temperature is arranged in a
matrix form with 36 parallel series, which represents multiple forecast horizon steps ahead.
In our case, the 36-column structure is used to incorporate up to 36 hours of lead-time
for temperature forecasts. In addition, the dataset contains a time-of-day indicator for
each forecast horizon (as a 37 parallel columns, where there is a 0 step ahead entry for
the current hour and 36 steps into the future), and an alternate ambient temperature
measurement for certain stores (where available). All variables are described in detail in
the next section.
Data Sources: The power consumption data was collected via the store’s energy man-
agement system (hourly electric meter readings). The ambient temperature (denoted Ta)
originate from the observed ambient temperatures, while the forecasted ambient temper-
atures (Tanwp) were obtained from an NWP model feed (Numerical Weather Prediction).
Details of how these forecasts and measurements are obtained are beyond the scope of

2.2
Description of variables
2.2.1
Time index - t
The time variable has its format as a POSIX timestamp (hours since epoch). This is a
numeric time index for each hourly observation in 2023. It provides a unique identifier
for each time step and allows synchronization of all variables. In the model context, the
timestamp itself is not used as a direct numerical predictor (since it would just produce
a nearly linear trend); however, it is crucial for generating derived time-based features
(such as hour-of-day or day-of-week) and for splitting the data into training/validation
periods. The time index ensures that when looking at any given hour, we can retrieve the
corresponding power usage and associated weather conditions. It spans from 2023-01-01
00:00 to 2023-12-31 23:00 with no gaps except those addressed in preprocessing. This
index underpins the chronology of the recursive forecasting process – as we move forward
in time, predictions for the next hour(s) are conditioned on the current index and its
associated known information.
2.2.2
Power Consumption - P
The target variable and most important measure of the dataset is the power consumption.
It is the measured electrical power demand of the supermarket (kW), as a vector of
length 8760. This is the dependent (target) variable we aim to forecast. Each entry in
P represents the average power draw over the corresponding hour. Figure 2.1 illustrates
the time series of P across 2023, showing how consumption varies over hours, days, and
seasons. The values are obtained from the total in the dataset which is the combined
power for all 38 stores. This is simply the sum for all stores at each hour throughout the
year.
In the context of recursive quantile regression, P is the variable for which we want to
predict quantiles (e.g., median, 90th percentile, etc.) for future time horizons. Its rele-

2.2.3
Forecasted ambient temperature - Tanwp
This variable serves as a predicted ambient temperature from a numerical weather pre-
diction model (°C). The structure of Tanwp is on the form illustrated by the forecasting
matrix in figure 2.2. This means that for each time point t there is a temperature predic-
tion k steps ahead into the future. This gives the matrix its dimension of 8760 × 36, as
k = 36 in this case. For all time points, we have a temperature prediction 36 hours into
the future. However, for the end of the matrix, the forecast will start to predict outside
the time horizon of 8760 hours from the start of the year and into the next year of 2024.
This will not be an issue as there will not be made any predictions outside 2023.
Tanwp provides the exogenous forecast inputs needed for making forward predictions of
energy usage. Each column corresponds to a forecast scenario for ambient temperature,
alike the structure in Ta. There are 36 parallel series, which can be interpreted as 36
distinct lead times (e.g., 1-hour ahead, 2-hours ahead, …, up to 36-hours ahead) from a
single deterministic forecast model. In our supermarket case, it is common to use day-
ahead forecasts, so one might expect 24 hourly steps ahead; the presence of 36 columns
means that multiple days are included. Indeed, the dataset contain 1.5 days of hourly
forecasts for each issuance, covering beyond the next day into part of the second day. For
concreteness, one can imagine that at each noon, a new ensemble forecast is issued for

2.2.5
Hour of day - tday
tday serves as a cyclical hour index for each prediction horizon (integer 0–23, 37-column
matrix). This is an important time-of-day indicator engineered as a feature. For every
forecast lead in the 37 column structure, we record the corresponding hour-of-the-day (in
local time) that that forecasted period represents. Essentially, tday is a matrix where each
column indicates the horizon (0 through 36) of the target time for that time point. For
example, suppose the second column (k = 1, as the first column is for k = 0 as in the Ta
matrix) corresponds to a 1-hour ahead forecast from a given origin time. If the forecast is
made at 17:00 (5 PM), then 1-hour ahead corresponds to 18:00, which is hour 18 (in 0–23
notation). The third column (2-hours ahead) would correspond to 19:00 (hour 19), and
so on. By the time we reach, say, the 7th column, if it wraps to the next day, the hour
value will cycle accordingly. This variable is repeated for all 37 scenario columns.
The reason for including tday is to explicitly account for the strong diurnal pattern in
energy usage. Supermarkets have very regular daily schedules: they typically open in the
morning, experience peak customer activity around midday to early evening, and then
see reduced activity or closure at night. This is very clear from the bottom plot in figure
2.1. Consequently, power consumption follows a daily cycle with higher values during
opening hours and lower values overnight. By providing the model with the hour-of-day
for each forecasted interval, we allow it to adjust predictions based on known routine
variation (even for multi-step forecasts). In a quantile regression, this could manifest as
different intercepts or distributions conditioned on hour-of-day – for instance, the model
can learn that at 3 AM (hour 3) the distribution of P is narrow and centered around the
refrigeration-baseline, whereas at 3 PM (hour 15) the distribution is wider and centered
higher due to operational load. Including tday ensures that the recursive forecasts respect
daily seasonality: even if the recent observed P was low at night, when forecasting 8 hours
ahead into the next morning, the model knows a ramp-up is expected due to opening time.
Technically, tday can be represented as a categorical variable (24 levels) or via a cyclic
encoding (e.g., sine and cosine of hour angle) in the model. In our analysis, we keep it
in integer form for interpretability and because our regression framework can handle it
through fixed effects for each hour. The hour-of-day feature is one of the most influential
predictors for patterning the forecasts, essentially gating the model’s predictions to be
higher in daylight hours than at night, all else equal.

Method and Theory
This chapter presents the theoretical background and the methodology for forecasting the
electrical load of a supermarket refrigeration system. We first discuss the context and
motivation for short-term load forecasting in the smart grid paradigm, and review rel-
evant literature on forecasting methods. We then describe the unique characteristics of
supermarket refrigeration load that inform our modeling approach. Next, we detail the
structure of the forecasting models, including the time-series modeling framework, adap-
tive parameter estimation, regime-switching strategy for different operating conditions.
Finally, we outline how the models are implemented and validated, including discussion of
parameter tuning and residual analysis to ensure model adequacy. Throughout this chap-
ter, references to prior work are given to position the approach in the context of existing
research.
3.1
Background and Motivation for Load Forecasting
Integration of renewable energy sources into power systems has created a need for more
advanced load forecasting techniques to maintain grid stability. In Denmark, for example,
the national goal was that 50% of electrical consumption by 2020 should be covered by
wind energy, which was met, necessitating the development of a flexible Smart Grid [4].
In a Smart Grid, forecasting plays a key role in balancing the fluctuating generation from
renewables with consumption [4]. The ability of power consumers to provide flexibility is
critical; therefore, accurate load forecasts are needed to implement control schemes that
can shift or modulate loads in response to supply conditions. Industrial consumers such as
supermarkets are prime candidates for providing such flexibility, due to their significant
and relatively predictable energy usage on refrigeration, lighting, HVAC, etc. Many pro-
posed Smart Grid architectures incorporate load forecasting as an integral component of
energy management and demand response algorithms [4]. In particular, if a supermarket
refrigeration system is equipped with thermal energy storage (e.g. an ice storage tank),
load forecasting can enable predictive control strategies to shift cooling loads in time while
maintaining product temperature constraints. This concept has been demonstrated in the
literature: for instance, Halvgaard et al. show that heat load forecasts can be used in an
economic Model Predictive Control (MPC) scheme to optimally operate thermal storage
for demand response [5]. Similarly, the prospect of using refrigeration load flexibility for
grid support has been explored by researchers [6, 7], indicating the large potential of su-
permarket refrigeration systems to act as controllable loads. These motivations form the
backdrop for our focus on forecasting the refrigeration load in a supermarket as a step
toward smart, flexible energy systems.
Short-term electrical load forecasting (STLF) is a well-established field, and numerous
methods have been developed over the years. Traditional approaches often use statistical
time-series models such as Autoregressive Moving Average (ARMA) or Autoregressive In-
tegrated Moving Average (ARIMA) models and their variants. For instance, Chen et al.
developed an adaptive ARMA model for 24-hour load forecasting and found that intro-
ducing adaptivity significantly improved accuracy [8]. Classical ARIMA models capture
linear correlations in the load time series and have been successful in many applications,
but they may struggle with non-linear effects and changing system dynamics. To address
these issues, researchers have turned to machine learning and non-parametric methods.
Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs) have been widely applied to load forecasting due to
Probabilistic Load Forecasting for Supermarket Refrigeration
15
==PAGE:24==
their ability to approximate non-linear relationships. For example, an ANN model was
used to predict the total electrical load of a supermarket with good accuracy [9], which
is highly relevant since refrigeration constitutes a major portion of a supermarket’s elec-
tricity consumption. Support Vector Machines (SVMs) and related kernel-based methods
have also been explored for load forecasting. Espinoza et al. applied an SVM-based ap-
proach to forecast the electrical load at a power substation, demonstrating the feasibility
of machine learning for complex load patterns [10]. Hybrid and combined methods have
additionally been proposed to leverage the strengths of multiple techniques. One example
is the work by Tan et al., who combined wavelet transform with ARIMA and GARCH
(Generalized Autoregressive Conditional Heteroskedasticity) models to improve forecast
accuracy by decomposing the time series and modeling both mean and variance com-
ponents [11]. Likewise, Kavousi-Fard and Akbari-Zadeh presented a hybrid short-term
load forecasting method that integrates wavelet-based feature extraction with ANN and
ARIMA modeling, achieving better accuracy than individual methods [12]. These studies
underscore that no single approach is universally best; rather, the choice of method should
consider the characteristics of the load, the available data (including exogenous inputs like
weather), and the need for adaptability.
Another important thread in the literature is the use of regime-switching or state-dependent
models for systems that exhibit distinct operating modes. Regime-switching models allow
different model parameters or structures under different conditions (e.g., day vs. night,
weekdays vs. weekends, etc.). They have been widely used in energy forecasting: for in-
stance, to capture on-peak and off-peak dynamics in electricity price forecasting [11] and
to model weather-dependent modes in wind power prediction [4]. The underlying idea is
that a single set of model parameters may not be adequate to describe the process across
all regimes, so the model “switches” between parameter sets depending on some indicator
(which could be time-based or state-based). Given that many energy systems inherently
operate in multiple modes, regime-switching has proven effective in improving forecast
accuracy for those cases. The approach of this thesis leverages this concept by recognizing
that a supermarket’s refrigeration load behaves differently during business hours versus
off-hours, as will be detailed shortly.
In summary, the forecasting methodology developed in this work builds upon prior research
by combining elements of statistical time-series modeling. We aim to incorporate known
physical and operational characteristics of the system, such as its daily operating schedule,
into a data-driven model that can adapt over time. In the next sections, we discuss these
system characteristics and then describe the modeling approach in detail.
3.2
Characteristics of Supermarket Refrigeration Load
Before formulating the forecasting model, it is important to understand the key factors that
influence the refrigeration load in a supermarket. The system under study is a refrigeration
system in a supermarket. The electrical load Pt (in kW) at time t is primarily the power
drawn by the compressors. Based on analysis of the data and domain knowledge done in
the prior chapter, the most significant drivers of the refrigeration load are: (1) ambient
temperature, (2) store operation schedule (open vs. closed hours), and (3) internal events
such as defrost cycles and product loading.
Ambient Temperature Influence: The heat load on the refrigeration system increases with
higher ambient (outdoor) temperature because of greater heat ingress into the refrigerated
areas and reduced eﬀiciency of the condensers. Consequently, the compressor power tends
to rise on hotter days. The relationship between ambient temperature and refrigeration
power is not strictly linear across the full range of temperatures. At lower temperatures,

the refrigeration system has a lighter load, while at high ambient temperatures the load can
increase sharply as the system works harder to reject heat. Prior studies on supermarket
energy consumption have noted this dependence on temperature. For example, Braun et
al. found that for a supermarket in the UK, electricity usage (dominated by refrigeration)
correlated strongly with outdoor dry-bulb temperature, far more than with humidity [13].
In our data, we similarly observe that daily peaks in refrigeration power often coincide
with daily high ambient temperatures. However, the sensitivity of load to temperature can
vary at different temperature levels (indicating a non-linear relationship). This motivates
the inclusion of a non-linear function (such as a spline) of ambient temperature in our
model, allowing the effect of temperature on load to be modeled more flexibly than a
simple linear coeﬀicient.
In some recent electricity load forecasting literature, spline transformations are used to
capture non-linear effects of temperature, particularly in cases where a U-shaped or asym-
metric relationship is expected between outdoor temperature and electricity consumption
[14, 15]. However, in this thesis, splines are deliberately excluded from the modeling pro-
cess. This decision is based on a combination of theoretical and empirical considerations.
First, the temperature forecast variable Tanwp used here already reflects smoothed and
aggregated information over time, which may naturally mitigate the need for further non-
linear transformations. Second, recursive modeling techniques such as rolling ARX and
recursive quantile regression benefit from parsimony and numerical stability, both of which
can be compromised by the inclusion of spline-based regressors. Additionally, preliminary
experiments showed only limited gains in accuracy from spline-based modeling. Finally,
the key objective of this thesis is to compare model structures under recursive learning
schemes and distributional advantages, and simplicity in the feature space improves both
interpretability and comparability. For these reasons, splines were not included in the
final models.
Daily Operation Regimes (Open vs. Closed Hours): A supermarket does not maintain
the same refrigeration load 24 hours a day; instead, it operates in two distinct regimes
corresponding to business hours and off-hours. During opening hours, customer activity
(frequent door openings, restocking, etc.)
and lack of thermal covers on open display
cases lead to higher cooling loads. During closing hours (typically overnight), many stores
implement energy-saving measures such as covering open display cases with insulation and
reducing indoor lighting, which significantly lowers the refrigeration load. In the dataset,
this regime behavior is clearly visible: at night (when the store is closed) the compressor
load drops to a baseline level, whereas in daytime it rises to a higher plateau with more
variability. The bottom plot in figure 2.1 from the data illustrates a seven-day period where
the load each night is consistently lower than during the day, confirming the presence of
two regimes. We also observe high-frequency spikes during some daytime hours, which
correspond to scheduled defrost cycles for the freezer units. Typically, defrost heaters are
activated in early morning hours or late in the day for each freezer case, causing short
bursts of additional load. These spikes are superimposed on the general day/night pattern.
Given this behavior, we categorize the system into two regimes: Opening hours (high load
regime) and Closing hours (low load regime). Each regime may have its own characteristics
in terms of baseline load level and sensitivity to inputs. Incorporating regime distinctions
in the model is expected to improve forecast accuracy by allowing different behaviours for
the two periods.
It is worth noting that the boundary between opening and closing hours can usually be
determined by the store’s schedule. However, this is not known for all stores, but from plots
it is clear that a spike appears in the morning at 5AM and a fall in consumption appears

follow changes in ambient temperature or other inputs; there are dynamic effects due to
thermal inertia in the system. For instance, if the outdoor temperature rises suddenly,
the refrigerated temperature (and load) will respond with some lag, as the building and
products absorb heat gradually. Similarly, after a door opening event or a large new stock
of warmer goods is loaded into a cooler, the compressors run at a higher level for some
time to remove the added heat, then return to steady operation. These dynamics suggest
that a pure instantaneous mapping from inputs to load is insuﬀicient; we must account
for the time-dependent behavior. In time-series terms, the load has an autocorrelated
structure and the effect of an input perturbation decays over time. We address this by

3.3.1
Regression Models
Regression models are fundamental tools in statistics and time series analysis, used to
describe and quantify the relationship between a response variable and one or more ex-
planatory variables. In its most basic form, regression aims to explain the variation in a
dependent variable Yt by linking it to a vector of covariates xt = (x1t, . . . , xpt)⊤at time t
through a deterministic function f and an error term εt:
Yt = f(xt, t; θ) + εt
where θ is a vector of unknown parameters. In this formulation, f could be linear or
nonlinear in θ, and may or may not depend on time explicitly.
The most commonly
used version of this model is the general linear model, where the function f is linear in
parameters:
Yt = x⊤
t θ + εt
This model provides a simple yet powerful framework for statistical modeling, forecasting,
and inference, and serves as a basis for many of the more advanced methods used in
adaptive and recursive estimation.
The classical linear regression model relies on several key assumptions. First, the linearity
assumption states that the relationship between the covariates and the outcome is linear in
the parameters. Second, the exogeneity assumption requires that the explanatory variables
xt are non-random or at least uncorrelated with the error term εt, ensuring that the
regressors contain no endogenous information that would bias the parameter estimates.
Third, the model assumes homoskedasticity, meaning that the variance of the error term
is constant across all observations, i.e., Var(εt) = σ2. Fourth, it is assumed that there is
no autocorrelation among the errors, implying that Cov(εt, εs) = 0 for all t ̸= s, which is
especially relevant in time series settings. Finally, in many inferential contexts, a stronger
assumption of independence is imposed, where the errors are considered independently
and identically distributed (i.i.d.), allowing for more robust statistical inference.
Violations of these assumptions do not invalidate the model, but they do affect the prop-
erties of the parameter estimates and the reliability of inference (e.g., confidence intervals
and hypothesis tests).

Least Squares (OLS), which minimizes the sum of squared residuals:

influence different parts of the distribution of Yt (e.g., extremes). These issues motivate
more flexible models, such as quantile regression or recursive methods, which we return
to later [18].

Quantile Regression
While ordinary least squares (OLS) regression aims to estimate the conditional mean of a
response variable given some predictors, quantile regression focuses instead on estimating
conditional quantiles. This provides a much richer understanding of how the distribution
of the outcome variable changes across different parts of the predictor space.
Quantile regression was introduced by Koenker and Bassett in 1978 [19]. The key idea
is to model not just what happens “on average,” but also how the predictors affect the
lower, middle, or upper tails of the distribution of the response variable. This makes the
method especially valuable in settings where the data exhibit skewness, heavy tails, or
heteroskedasticity—common in areas like energy forecasting, finance, and environmental
modeling [20].
To understand quantile regression, it helps to start with the definition of a quantile. For
a random variable Y with cumulative distribution function FY (y), the τ-th quantile is
defined as:
qτ(Y ) = inf {y ∈R : FY (y) ≥τ}
This means that a proportion τ of the distribution lies below qτ(Y ). For example, the
0.5-quantile corresponds to the median, and the 0.1-quantile describes the point below
which 10% of the observations fall. Quantiles are less sensitive to outliers than the mean,
which is a key reason for their robustness in regression.
In regression settings, we are interested in conditional quantiles, i.e.,
qτ(Y | X = x)
which describe the τ-quantile of Y given the covariates x.
In the linear quantile regression model, the conditional quantile function is assumed to be
linear in the predictors:

Quantile regression offers several advantages that make it particularly useful in applied
forecasting contexts. First, it is inherently robust to outliers and skewed distributions,
since it does not rely on assumptions of normality or homoscedasticity. This makes it
well-suited for modeling energy loads, which may display heavy tails or sudden fluctua-
tions. Moreover, quantile regression captures heterogeneity in how covariates influence
the response variable across different parts of the conditional distribution. This allows for
a more nuanced understanding of the relationship between inputs and outputs, especially
when the effect of a predictor varies between low- and high-demand periods. Another
important advantage is that quantile regression naturally facilitates the estimation of
prediction intervals, rather than providing only a single point forecast. This feature is
especially valuable in risk-sensitive domains where understanding the uncertainty around
predictions is critical.
Nevertheless, quantile regression also entails certain trade-offs.
When multiple quan-
tiles are estimated independently, it is possible for the resulting quantile curves to cross,
which violates the logical ordering of percentiles and may complicate interpretation. Fur-
thermore, statistical inference in quantile regression is typically more involved than in
linear regression. It often requires resampling techniques such as bootstrapping or re-
lies on asymptotic theory, particularly when standard errors or confidence intervals are
needed. Finally, while quantile regression is relatively straightforward in static settings,
the method can become computationally demanding when extended to recursive or time-
adaptive frameworks, especially in the context of high-frequency or streaming data.
3.3.5
The rq() Function in R: Implementation and Optimization Details
In practice, quantile regression is typically solved using specialized software that can eﬀi-
ciently handle the non-smooth nature of the optimization problem. In this work, baseline
quantile regression estimates are obtained using the rq() function from the widely used
quantreg package in R [21]. This function implements the original formulation of Koenker
and Bassett [19] and estimates the conditional τ-quantile of the response variable given a
set of predictors.
Although the quantile regression problem is convex, it lacks a closed-form solution because
the check loss is not differentiable at zero. Classical methods, such as the simplex algo-
rithm, can solve this problem exactly but may become computationally expensive and nu-
merically unstable for large datasets or ill-conditioned design matrices [22]. Jan Møller has
also contributed work on simplex-based methods for quantile regression in time-adaptive
settings, where sequential updates can be leveraged to reduce computational cost.
To address these challenges, rq() uses more modern optimization techniques. By default,
it relies on a primal–dual interior point method that follows a path through the interior
of the feasible region rather than moving along its edges as the simplex algorithm does.
Intuitively, this approach replaces the sharp inequality constraints with smooth barrier
functions, allowing the solver to “glide” through the interior of the constraint set while
progressively tightening toward the optimal solution [23]. At each iteration, a Newton-
type step updates both the primal variables (the regression coeﬀicients and slack variables)
Probabilistic Load Forecasting for Supermarket Refrigeration
23
==PAGE:32==
and the dual variables (associated with the constraints), steadily improving feasibility and
optimality. Because it does not jump between corner points of the polytope, the interior
point method tends to converge faster and is numerically more stable, particularly in
high-dimensional or ill-conditioned problems.
Alternative solvers are available in rq(), such as a Frisch–Newton variant optimized for
sparse problems, or a classical simplex solver for very small datasets where exact vertex
solutions are needed. The function also supports regularized quantile regression through
an ℓ1-penalized formulation, which is helpful when the number of predictors is large.
Another practical feature of rq() is its ability to estimate multiple quantiles simultane-
ously by passing a vector of τ values. Each quantile is estimated independently, which
can lead to crossing quantile curves; while post-processing methods exist to address this,
they are not enforced by default. For statistical inference, rq() provides standard errors
and confidence intervals via bootstrapping or sandwich variance estimation, which are
essential for quantifying the uncertainty in estimated quantile effects.
Overall, rq() offers a reliable and eﬀicient way to obtain batch quantile regression esti-
mates. Understanding its optimization structure is helpful when comparing traditional
batch estimates with recursive extensions, which must approximate these solutions in an
online setting. There is plenty of theory and work on both the interior point method and
simplex method, but for this work the main focus in not on the optimization and com-
putational aspects of the regression models, and therefore deeper theory into how rq()
works is left out.
3.3.6
Recursive Quantile Regression (RQR)
While standard quantile regression is a powerful tool for estimating conditional quantiles,
it is fundamentally a batch method that requires all data to be available before fitting the
model. In many real-time applications, however, data arrive sequentially and there is a
need to update estimates continuously. Recursive Quantile Regression (RQR) extends the
quantile regression framework to this online setting by combining its robustness and distri-
butional flexibility with the adaptive updating mechanisms familiar from Recursive Least
Squares (RLS). This makes it suitable for applications such as energy load forecasting,
financial risk assessment, and other non-stationary time series problems.

3.3.7
ARIMA Models
The ARIMA class of models—short for Autoregressive Integrated Moving Average—is one
of the most widely used frameworks for modeling univariate time series with potential
non-stationarity and temporal correlation [24, 25].
An ARIMA model is specified by three non-negative integer parameters ARIMA(p, d, q),
where:
p = order of the autoregressive (AR) part,
d = order of non-seasonal differencing (integration),
q = order of the moving-average (MA) part.
The operator form of the ARIMA(p, d, q) model is given by:
ϕ(L) (1 −L)d yt = θ(L) εt,
where:
L yt = yt−1
(lag operator),
ϕ(L) = 1 −ϕ1L −ϕ2L2 −· · · −ϕpLp
(AR polynomial),
θ(L) = 1 + θ1L + θ2L2 + · · · + θqLq
(MA polynomial).
Here, εt denotes a white-noise process with zero mean and constant variance [26].
Differencing and Stationarity
The integrated component (1 −L)d removes non-stationary trends or unit roots by differ-
encing the series d times. After this, the resulting series is assumed (weakly) stationary,
meaning its mean, variance, and autocorrelation do not change over time [27, 26]. Typical
values are d = 0 or 1, rarely larger.
Autoregressive (AR) and Moving Average (MA) Parts
Once stationarity is achieved, the autoregressive part explains the current value based on
its own past:
yt = ϕ1yt−1 + · · · + ϕpyt−p + εt.
The moving-average part models current noise as a function of past error terms:
yt = εt + θ1εt−1 + · · · + θqεt−q.
Combined, the ARMA(p, q) model captures both dependence on past values and past
shocks [28, 26].
Autocorrelation and Partial Autocorrelation
Before identifying an ARIMA model, it is important to understand the correlation struc-
ture of the time series. Two fundamental tools for this purpose are the autocorrelation
function (ACF) and the partial autocorrelation function (PACF).
The autocorrelation function at lag k, denoted ρk, measures the linear dependence between
the current observation yt and its lagged value yt−k. Formally, it is defined as

pure autoregressive model of order p, the PACF cuts off exactly after lag p, while the ACF
decays gradually. Conversely, in a pure moving-average model of order q, the ACF cuts
off after lag q, while the PACF decays gradually. When both ACF and PACF display
gradual decay, it typically indicates a mixed ARMA process where both autoregressive
and moving-average components are present.
In practice, the sample ACF and PACF are visualized as correlation plots with confidence
bands, and these empirical patterns provide initial guidance for selecting candidate orders
p and q. Pure AR(p) models exhibit slowly decaying ACF and cutoff in PACF after lag
p. Conversely, MA(q) models have ACF cutoff after q and PACF that decays smoothly.
Mixed ARMA(p, q) models show decay in both ACF and PACF, making selection less
straightforward when both p > 0 and q > 0 [28].
Quantile–Quantile (Q–Q) Plots
A quantile–quantile (Q–Q) plot is a graphical tool used to compare the empirical distri-
bution of a dataset to a theoretical distribution, most commonly the normal distribution.
Each point on the plot represents a pair of quantiles: the theoretical quantile from the
reference distribution on the x-axis, and the corresponding sample quantile from the data
on the y-axis. If the data follow the specified distribution, the points should fall approxi-
mately along a straight diagonal line.
Q–Q plots are particularly useful for diagnosing departures from normality, such as skew-
ness, heavy tails, or outliers. Unlike summary statistics, Q–Q plots provide a visual and
intuitive understanding of where deviations occur—whether in the center or at the ex-
tremes of the distribution. This is especially relevant in time series modeling when check-
ing whether residuals behave like white noise and follow a normal distribution, which is
an assumption in classical ARIMA-based inference and forecast intervals.
Once p, d, q are chosen, parameters ϕi and θj can be estimated by fitting the model, often
via least squares or maximum likelihood. Forecasts are generated through iterated sub-
stitution of predictions into the model. Prediction intervals account for parameter and
noise uncertainty. ARIMA models are most effective for short- to medium-term forecasts
of stable processes [25]. They are less suited for long-term predictions, sudden structural
breaks, or processes with strong seasonality (without inclusion of seasonal terms). The
selection of p, d, q is partly subjective and requires careful validation of model assumptions
[25, 29].

3.3.8
ARX Models
While ARIMA models capture the internal dynamics of a univariate time series by explain-
ing its current value using its own past values and past shocks, many practical forecasting
problems require incorporating additional information from external or explanatory vari-
ables. Such variables may represent known drivers of the process, such as weather data
for energy demand forecasting or macroeconomic indicators for financial time series.
The Autoregressive model with eXogenous inputs (ARX) extends the AR framework by
explicitly including such covariates. Intuitively, ARX retains the temporal memory struc-
ture of ARIMA but allows the model to respond directly to external signals, thereby
improving interpretability and forecasting accuracy when relevant exogenous information
is available.

exogenous regressors:

This rolling estimation approach allows the ARX model to behave as an adaptive fore-

capture evolving dynamics in the power load. Only a relatively small estimation problem
is solved at each time step, which makes the method computationally eﬀicient while still
flexible enough to handle time-varying conditions.
Parameter selection
Before finalizing the ARX model structure in section 3.4, an exploratory model identi-
fication process was carried out following the ACF and QQ plot inspection, and a gen-
eral inspection against actuals. The goal was to understand the inherent autocorrelation
structure of the refrigeration load and decide on an appropriate autoregressive order and
seasonal treatment.
First the most obvious approach were attempted: Fixed-order ARX models. Simple ARX
models were fitted with increasing autoregressive order p = 1, 2, 3, 4. At each setting,
the residuals were inspected using the sample autocorrelation function (ACF). As illus-
trated in Figure 3.1, increasing p beyond 2 provided negligible improvement in removing
autocorrelation. While short-term residual autocorrelation decreased for higher orders,
a persistent spike at lag 24 hours remained in all cases. This spike corresponds to the
daily operational cycle of the supermarket and suggests a structural seasonal component
rather than a short-memory process that can be absorbed by a small AR order. This spike
remained even when introducing a 24-hour lagged AR part, Pt−24.
To further explore the residual behavior, quantile–quantile (Q–Q) plots were generated
for the residuals of each ARX model order. As shown in Figure 3.2, all four plots exhibit
clear deviations from the normality line, especially in the tails. These deviations reflect
the presence of skewness and heavy-tailed behavior in the residuals — consistent with the
complex and volatile nature of supermarket load. However, the non-normality is fairly
stable across different orders and does not clearly diminish as p increases. Thus, while the
Q–Q plots confirm the non-Gaussian nature of the noise component, they do not motivate
increasing model complexity beyond p = 2.
This raised the concern that the spike in lag 24 in the ACF plot might be diﬀicult to explain
using only short-memory AR terms. Therefore another approach was used: automatic
model selection with seasonal ARIMA. To allow for a potentially more complex structure,
an auto.arima() model with exogenous regressors was also tested, enabling seasonal terms
with period s = 24 hours. This automatic model selection tries various ARIMA(p, d, q) ×
(P, D, Q)24 combinations and chooses the best according to information criteria. This was
done to investigate how well the model can fit the data if we don’t restrict anything and
try to find the best fitted model structure regardless of complexity.
However, even with seasonal differencing enabled, the resulting residual ACF (see Fig-
ure 3.3) still showed a remaining correlation around lag 24 hours.
The output was a
ARIMA(2, 0, 2) × (1, 0, 2)24 model, which is a rather complex structure and took a long
time to run. This suggests that even a more flexible seasonal ARIMA structure did not
fully eliminate the daily periodicity.

3.5.3
Choice of Window Size and Quantile Levels
To ensure a fair comparison, the same rolling window length ω is used for both ARX and
RQR. This means that at any time t, both models are trained on the identical subset of
past observations, making their outputs directly comparable. In particular, this avoids
giving an unfair advantage to one model by allowing it more or less historical context.
When selecting window size, there is a trade-off between responsiveness and robustness.
The selected value is 200(hours), which corresponds to 8.33 days, a little over a week.
The value is selected as we wish to have at least a week to train the model due to weekly
changes, plus an additional day. One could argue that a bigger window size, such as two
or three weeks, would make the model more robust to week-by-week changes. However, a
whole week plus one day of memory is assumed to be suﬀicient. The model does not take
weekly changes into account, so having several weeks to train the model on does not add
much in that sense. If, for example, the model was trained on weekend and weekdays,
this would make sense, but since the model is only trained as one day after the other with
no modeling difference between them, this should not be necessary. On the other end, a
lower window size would make the model less robust to changes throughout a week.
The quantile regression can be fitted for multiple quantile levels τ ∈{0.05, 0.1, 0.5, 0.9, 0.95},
allowing for an empirical description of the conditional load distribution rather than just
its median. In this work, we focus primarily on the median (τ = 0.5) for direct compari-
son with ARX point forecasts, and also report the 5th and 95th conditional quantiles for
assessing the coverage of the resulting probabilistic forecast intervals.
3.5.4
Relation to the ARX Baseline
It is important to emphasize that the RQR model is not a replacement but an augmentation
of the ARX baseline. The ARX forecast serves two roles: 1. As a deterministic point
prediction that is easy to interpret and computationally eﬀicient. 2. As an informative
input variable for the quantile regression, enabling it to eﬀiciently capture any residual
asymmetries or heteroskedasticity in the forecast errors.
In this way, the RQR model provides a distributional layer on top of the ARX framework.
It preserves all interpretability of the baseline model (same regime and temperature drivers,
same daily persistence structure), while enriching the forecast output with conditional
quantiles and prediction intervals.

3.6

across all forecast horizons. The evaluation serves two complementary goals: first, to as-
sess how well the models predict the central tendency of the refrigeration load; and second,
to evaluate how well they characterize uncertainty in the form of prediction intervals or
quantiles. To ensure a fair and transparent comparison, both deterministic and proba-
bilistic metrics are computed on the same test set. This section describes the methodology
for evaluating the forecasts produced by the ARX and RQR models.

4
Results
This chapter presents the empirical results of the proposed forecasting framework. The
aim is to demonstrate the performance of both the baseline ARX model and the time-
adaptive recursive quantile regression (RQR) model, highlighting their respective strengths
and limitations. To maintain a clear structure, we begin with an intuitive Simulation
Study which illustrates key properties of the RQR approach in a controlled setting. These
examples are designed to make the effect of window size, quantile level, and adaptation
dynamics immediately visible before moving on to the real supermarket refrigeration load
case study.
After the Simulation Study, the forecasting results are reported on the real dataset. The
evaluation follows the validation methodology described in Section 3.6, comparing ARX
and RQR across multiple forecast horizons, quantile levels, and performance metrics.
4.1
Simulation Study
Before applying RQR to the actual refrigeration load, it is instructive to first investigate
how the algorithm behaves in a controlled environment where the true data-generating
process is known. We simulate a simple time-varying linear model of the form
yt = βi(t) xt + εt,

estimated quantiles behave.
This approach allows us to visually assess three important aspects of the recursive quantile
estimator. 1) Quantile-dependent parameter estimates — How the estimates differ for
lower, median, and upper quantiles. 2) Impact of window size — The trade-off between
stability and responsiveness. 3) Update time — How quickly the model reacts to a sudden
regime change.
These simple illustrations build an intuitive understanding of the algorithm before ana-

majority of the window to represent the new regime. Another observation is that the
higher quantiles are more responsive to sudden positive changes, while the lower quantiles
are more conservative, and vice versa with sudden negative changes.

Summary of Results
The analysis included qualitative illustrations of model behavior, as well as quantitative
metrics assessing accuracy, calibration, sharpness, and bias. Taken together, the results
provide a well-rounded picture of how the two models behave across different forecast
horizons and performance criteria.
The visual inspection of one-hour-ahead forecasts demonstrated that both ARX and RQR
track the short-term load dynamics well, benefiting from the strong autocorrelation in
refrigeration load. The models produced nearly identical centerline forecasts and similar
90% prediction intervals, confirming that at very short horizons, recent historical val-
ues dominate the predictive structure. Extending the horizon to 2, 6, 12, and 24 hours
revealed a more nuanced difference between the models.
As shown in Figure 4.5, the
RQR prediction intervals widened more gradually and were generally tighter than those of
ARX—especially during off hours—while still capturing the key uncertainty dynamics of
the data. This reflects RQR’s ability to adapt its conditional quantiles to observed regime
patterns and heteroskedasticity.
The quantitative metrics further confirm and extend these visual impressions. In terms
of point forecast accuracy, the RMSE and MAE for RQR were slightly lower than those
for ARX at all horizons, with the gap becoming more pronounced beyond 6 hours. For
probabilistic evaluation, RQR outperformed ARX on both median and upper-tail pinball
loss, especially at longer horizons. This advantage highlights the value of directly modeling
the predictive distribution rather than relying on a symmetric Gaussian approximation.
Evaluation of empirical coverage and overrate offered further insights. While both models
showed some undercoverage at long horizons, RQR consistently produced sharper intervals,
and ARX compensated with wider, more conservative bands. Interestingly, the overrate
metric revealed mirrored tendencies: ARX leaned slightly toward underprediction, while

This symmetry suggests complementary bias tendencies that could be useful in ensemble

5
Conclusion
The primary objective of this thesis was to investigate the performance of a time-adaptive
Recursive Quantile Regression (RQR) model for short- to medium-term refrigeration load
forecasting. RQR was evaluated against an Autoregressive model with exogenous inputs
(ARX), which served as a strong baseline. While the aim was not solely to outperform
ARX, the comparative framework was essential for assessing whether RQR could enhance
forecast quality and provide well-calibrated probabilistic predictions.
The results demonstrate that RQR offers consistent improvements over the ARX model,
particularly at longer forecast horizons. For short-term predictions (k = 1, 2 hours), both
models performed almost identically, leveraging similar recent information and autocor-
relation structures. As the forecast horizon increased, the RQR median forecast began
to slightly outperform the ARX mean forecast in terms of RMSE and MAE, with the
gap growing more pronounced at horizons of 12 and 24 hours. This suggests that RQR’s
adaptive structure is better suited to handling growing uncertainty over time.
More significantly, the RQR model proved its strength in probabilistic forecasting. Its
prediction intervals were sharper yet maintained better calibration, especially in the upper
tail. Pinball loss metrics consistently favored RQR at τ = 0.95, highlighting its ability
to capture asymmetric uncertainty. The interval score results were particularly telling:
RQR delivered more eﬀicient intervals with lower penalties, outperforming ARX both in
sharpness and reliability.
These advantages stem from RQR’s ability to directly estimate conditional quantiles,
rather than relying on distributional assumptions. However, because the RQR model was
built around the ARX structure, it could only refine—not replace—the underlying forecast
dynamics. This design limited the potential for larger improvements but ensured a fair
comparison. In this context, RQR served as a valuable extension of an already capable
baseline, improving its probabilistic performance with only modest increases in complexity.
From a methodological perspective, the recursive design allowed the model to adapt over
time and remain responsive to new data. This is particularly relevant for load forecasting
in dynamic environments, where non-stationarity and temporal patterns evolve frequently.
RQR’s ability to update estimates sequentially makes it suitable for real-time forecasting
applications, without requiring full model retraining.
From a broader perspective, this thesis demonstrates the viability of using time-adaptive
quantile regression in energy forecasting. Even when gains in point accuracy are small, the
ability to deliver robust, uncertainty-aware forecasts offers real operational value. Accu-
rate intervals support better decision-making in contexts where over- or under-prediction
carries risk—such as load scheduling, resource allocation, and energy trading.
Overall, the thesis shows that RQR improves forecast performance relative to a well-
specified ARX baseline, particularly in probabilistic terms and at longer horizons. While
the improvements are evolutionary rather than revolutionary, they point to the practical
benefits of incorporating quantile-based forecasting in load prediction pipelines. The added
ability to model uncertainty explicitly represents a meaningful step toward more informed
and risk-aware energy forecasting. Future research may extend this work by exploring
more flexible model structures, richer input data, and broader applications within energy
systems and beyond.

6
Discussion
This chapter reflects on the findings of this thesis, discussing the hindsight gained from
the modeling process, the limitations of the chosen approach, and potential directions for
future research. It also briefly outlines how the developed forecasting framework could be
applied in practice within the context of energy optimization.
6.1
Hindsight and Model Comparison
An important consideration in this thesis is the way the RQR model was constructed on
top of the ARX baseline. While this design choice ensured that the RQR forecasts were
well-calibrated around an already reasonable point forecast, it also made the comparison
between ARX and RQR somewhat asymmetric. Since the RQR model explicitly uses the
ARX prediction as an input, it is inherently constrained by the ARX model’s structure. In
other words, the RQR model could only refine, but not fundamentally surpass, the ARX
forecast. As a result, it is unsurprising that the performance differences were modest,
particularly for short horizons.
In hindsight, this layered structure limited the potential to demonstrate larger gains from
RQR. A more independent comparison—where RQR directly models the conditional quan-
tiles without relying on ARX outputs—might have yielded more distinct differences in
performance. Nevertheless, the probabilistic validation of the RQR intervals remains valu-
able, since it confirms that the model can capture the conditional distribution around the
ARX forecast, improving interval calibration and providing more meaningful uncertainty
quantification.
It is also worth noting that while the RQR slightly outperformed ARX in terms of median
accuracy at longer horizons, the magnitude of improvement was smaller than initially
expected, except for the interval score, which truly showed their difference. This suggests
that the ARX model was already suﬀiciently strong for this particular problem, leaving
limited room for improvement. In this sense, the thesis highlights an important lesson:
when extending an already well-calibrated linear model, the incremental gains from more
complex methods may be subtle rather than dramatic.
6.2
Limitations and Future Work
Several limitations of the current study point towards directions for future work. First,
the forecasting was performed on aggregated refrigeration load data for all stores in the
dataset. While this simplification was necessary to maintain a clear scope, it would be
highly relevant to investigate whether pooling data across multiple stores—or alterna-
tively, training specialized models for each individual store—would improve predictive
skill. Exploring the trade-off between pooled versus store-specific forecasts could reveal
more about the generalizability of the RQR approach.
Second, the chosen ARX structure was relatively simple and did not incorporate more
sophisticated regime-switching behavior. A day/night regime was included through an
intercept adjustment, but more flexible approaches could be tested.
For example, in-
troducing interaction terms between the regime variable and all other predictors would
allow the model to learn different day/night coeﬀicients for each covariate. Alternatively,
the regime could be made endogenous, with the model selecting its own regime structure
dynamically, akin to GARCH-type or hidden Markov models. These approaches could

improve the modeling of volatility clusters or nonlinear dependencies that are present in
the refrigeration load.
Third, the thesis primarily relied on linear relationships, with only limited use of splines to
capture nonlinear effects. Expanding the RQR model to include more spline terms could
better capture seasonal and diurnal variations, which are known to have nonlinear impacts
on refrigeration demand.
Related to this, another potential extension is to introduce
hour-specific effects, where each hour of the day has its own coeﬀicient. While this would
dramatically increase the model complexity and require a much larger estimation window,
it could reveal more fine-grained patterns in the load.
Another key direction for future research is to explore the effect of window size on forecast
performance. In this thesis, the window length was investigated on simulated data to make
an informed decision, and then fixed for computational tractability. However, different
window sizes may trade off between responsiveness to recent changes and statistical stabil-
ity. Further investigating the relationship between window size and forecast accuracy—
especially for quantile forecasts—would be highly relevant for practitioners seeking to
balance computational cost with predictive power.
Finally, while the focus was on the median (τ = 0.5) and 95th percentile (τ = 0.95)
quantiles, it would be valuable to examine a broader set of quantiles. This would allow
for a more complete evaluation of the conditional distribution and could further improve
decision-making in downstream optimization tasks.
6.3
Practical Applications and Use Cases
Beyond purely evaluating forecast skill, it is important to consider the practical value of the
developed forecasting framework. One key application lies in energy price optimization.
By knowing the refrigeration load forecast for the next 12 or 24 hours, and combining it
with electricity price forecasts, it becomes possible to shift consumption toward cheaper
hours while maintaining temperature constraints within the required thresholds.
This
could directly translate into cost savings for store operators. The probabilistic nature
of the RQR forecasts is particularly useful in this context, as it provides a quantified
measure of uncertainty. For example, if the model predicts a high probability of exceeding
a certain load threshold during peak price hours, pre-cooling could be scheduled during
cheaper hours to mitigate cost risks.
A related avenue is temperature-aware optimization.
While this thesis focused solely
on the load, incorporating the actual cooler or freezer temperatures as additional state
variables would allow for model-based control strategies. Knowing not just the expected
load, but also the expected thermal behavior of the refrigeration systems, would make it
possible to optimize energy usage more dynamically without compromising food safety.
Beyond refrigeration load forecasting, the approach explored here can generalize to other
demand-side management problems in energy systems. Any application where uncertainty-
aware forecasts are required—for example, in HVAC systems, building energy manage-
ment, or even electric vehicle charging—could benefit from the RQR methodology. The
main contribution of this thesis in this regard is the demonstration that time-adaptive
quantile regression can enhance uncertainty quantification even when built upon a simple
baseline model.

regression. At the same time, the thesis demonstrates the practical value of uncertainty-
