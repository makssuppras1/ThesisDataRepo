Abstract 
 
Despite increased interest and research in Alzheimer’s disease (AD), no effective treatment for 
slowing down the disease progression has yet been developed. In recent years a strong connection 
between AD and diabetes has been established. As type 2 diabetes is associated with peripheral 
insulin resistance, AD is associated with brain insulin resistance, why it is now named “diabetes of 
the brain” or “type 3 diabetes”. Therefore, treatments with glucagon-like peptide 1 receptor agonists 
(GLP-1RAs) of the brain have now been proposed but the lack of drug delivery techniques that 
efficiently transport drugs across the brain barriers is limiting. Research aiming at developing brain 
drug delivery techniques has mainly focused on the blood-brain barrier (BBB). But new emerging 
knowledge of the brain entry of the neurotropic mumps virus (MuV) through the crossing of the 
blood-cerebrospinal fluid barrier (BCSFB) opens the possibility to explore the BCSFB as a new target 
for drug delivery to the brain. Therefore, a peptide-drug conjugate (PDC) consisting of the MuV SH 
peptide as a shuttle and an acetylcholinesterase (AChE)-cleavable linker to ensure brain-specific drug 
release could present an improved brain drug delivery technique. 
This project demonstrates the design of a PDC containing an AChE-cleavable linker and an 
investigation of the optimal linker structure through a structure-activity relationship (SAR) study. 
Different dye-functionalized AChE-cleavable linker analogs with varying electronic and steric 
properties were synthesized to gain insight into the enzymatic cleavage and release of a GLP-1RA. 
The analogs were designed to be a substrate for AChE and were conjugated with the fluorophore 7-
amino-4-methylcoumarin (AMC) as a model drug. The SAR study investigated the release of AMC 
by fluorimetry under conditions representative of the CSF compartments. The SAR study showed 
that the ortho position to the enzymatically cleaved carbamate is very sensitive to steric changes and 
substituents larger than fluorine completely terminate the enzymatic cleavage. With the knowledge 
from the SAR study, future research directions aiming to increase the rate of drug release are 
proposed.

Resume 
 
På trods af øget interesse of forskning i Alzheimers sygdom (AD) er der endnu ikke nogen effektiv 
behandling til at bremse sygdomsudviklingen. I de senere år er der blevet etableret en stærk 
sammenhæng mellem AD og diabetes. Da type 2-diabetes er forbundet med perifer insulinresistens, 
er AD forbundet med insulinresistens i hjernen, hvorfor det nu kaldes ”type 3 diabetes”. Derfor er 
behandlinger med glukagon-lignende peptid 1-receptoragonister (GLP-1RA'er) i hjernen nu blevet 
foreslået, men manglen på lægemiddelleveringsteknikker, der effektivt transporterer lægemidler over 
hjernebarriererne, er begrænsende. Forskning, der sigter mod at udvikle teknikker til frigivelse af 
lægemidler til hjernen, har hovedsageligt fokuseret på blod-hjerne-barrieren (BBB). Men ny viden 
om inficering af hjernen af den neurotropiske fåresygevirus (MuV) via krydsningen af blod-
cerebrospinalvæskebarrieren (BCSFB) åbner muligheden for at udforske BCSFB som et nyt mål for 
lægemiddellevering til hjernen. Derfor kunne et peptid-lægemiddel-konjugat (PDC) bestående af 
MuV SH-peptidet som transporter og en acetylcholinesterase (AChE)-kløvbar linker der sikre en 
hjernespecifik lægemiddelsfrigivelse repræsentere en forbedret løsning til levering af lægemidler til 
hjernen. 
Dette projekt demonstrerer designet af et PDC med en AChE-kløvbar linker og en undersøgelse af 
den optimale linkerstruktur gennem et struktur-aktivitetsforholdsundersøgelse (SAR). Forskellige 
farvestoffunktionaliserede AChE-kløvbar linker analoger med varierende elektroniske og steriske 
egenskaber blev syntetiseret for at få indsigt i den enzymatiske spaltning og frigivelsen af et 
lægemiddel. Analogerne blev designet således, at de er et substrat for AChE og konjugeret med 
fluoroforen 7-amino-4-methylcoumarin (AMC) som modellægemiddel. SAR-studiet undersøgte 
frigivelsen af AMC ved hjælp af fluorimetri under forhold, der er repræsentative for CSF-miljøet i 
hjerne. SAR-undersøgelsen viste, at ortho-positionen til den enzymatisk kløvende carbamat er meget 
følsom overfor steriske ændringer, og substituenter større end fluor stopper fuldstændig den 
enzymatiske kløvning. Med den viden fra SAR-studiet er fremtidige udforskningsmuligheder for 
linkeren der sigter mod at øge frigivelseshastigheden af lægemidlet også præsenteret.

1 Alzheimer’s Disease 
 
Alzheimer’s Disease (AD) is the most common cause of dementia defining 60-70% of the cases and 
is ranked as the fifth leading cause of death globally [1]. It is a neurodegenerative disease with 
progressively worsening symptoms beginning in patients typically over the age of 65. The expected 
survival time after diagnosis is around 8 years for patients diagnosed in their 60s and only around 3 
years for persons diagnosed in their 90s [2]. Today, there are around 50 million AD patients 
worldwide and due to the increasing life expectancy, this number is predicted to double every 5 years 
resulting in 152 million AD patients by 2050 [3]. Symptoms in cognitive behavior such as memory 
loss, disorientation, learning problems, and behavioral issues are just some of the symptoms that 
worsen over the cause of the disease. The cognitive decline is a result of neuronal loss and nerve cell 
atrophy, which is triggered by the so-called senile plaques and neurofibrillary tangles (NFTs) [4]. 
This has led to increased interest and research in AD treatment, but despite great efforts in the last 
decades, there is today still no effective treatment for AD patients.  
In the explanation of AD pathogenesis, several hypotheses have been put forward such as the 
cholinergic hypothesis, amyloid-β hypothesis, tau hypothesis, and the inflammation hypothesis. 
However, the amyloid-β hypothesis is still the most acknowledged for describing the pathogenesis of 
AD, and thus it will be the only one described in this thesis.  
1.1 The amyloid-beta hypothesis 
 
In 1984, the discovery of amyloid-beta (Aβ) protein in the blood of AD patients suggested a link 
between those two, which was later strengthened by findings in 1987, showing that the Aβ protein 
was the major component in the senile plaques. Soon after, in the early 1990s, specific mutations in 
the Aβ gene on chromosome 21 that promote excessive production of Aβ protein were found to 
increase the risk of developing clinical AD. This gave rise to the amyloid cascade hypothesis which 
states that the AD pathology is caused by an aggregation of Aβ protein resulting in plaque formation. 
The accumulation of Aβ protein then initiates the other pathophysiological factors of AD, like NFT 
formation [5].  
Aβ is a small protein of 39-43 amino acids with two major isoforms: soluble Aβ40 and insoluble Aβ42. 
Misregulated cleavage of the amyloid protein precursor (APP) leads to increased formation of Aβ 
which aggregates in form of plaques (Figure 1).   
APP is a transmembrane protein that is mainly present in neuronal and glial cells. Under normal 
physiological conditions, 90% or more of the APP is cleaved by α-secretase and γ-secretase leading 
to two extracellular fragments sAAP-α and P3, and one intracellular fragment AICD which is rapidly 
==PAGE:12==
Page 2 of 116 
 
degraded [4]. These soluble fragments are believed to regulate synaptic plasticity, the excitability of 
neurons, and to protect the neurons from oxidative and metabolic stress [6], [7]. It is believed that 
during pathological conditions, an increased cleavage rate of APP by β-secretase instead of α-
secretase occurs. This results in an increased release of the Aβ peptide. The Aβ monomers oligomerize 
and arrange in sheet-like conformations like β-sheets that aggregate to form the senile plaques. The 
plaque formation is further be accelerated by an increased ratio of Aβ42/Aβ40 since Aβ42 is more 
hydrophobic than Aβ40 [4].  
 
Figure 1: In the non-amyloidogenic pathway, α- and γ-secretase cleaves the APP to yield two extracellular 
products; sAAP-α and P3, and one intracellular product; AICD. In the amyloidogenic pathway, β- and γ-
secretase cleaves the APP to give sAAP-β and the Aβ peptide, which is released extracellularly, and AICD is 
released intracellularly. Figure created with BioRender and inspired by [4].   
 
The extracellular build-up of Aβ plaques induces oxidative damage and promotes tau 
hyperphosphorylation which results in toxic effects on synapses. Tau has an important role in the 
neuronal cytoskeleton by stabilizing the microtubule network. Phosphorylation of tau regulates its 
binding to the microtubules and hyperphosphorylation of the tau protein leads to dissociation from 
the microtubules and aggregation into NFTs [8].

2 Current and future strategies for the treatment 
of Alzheimer’s disease 
 
Today, only symptomatic treatment options are available for AD patients, and they all focus on 
reversing the neurotransmitter imbalance. Currently approved drugs mostly target the cholinergic 
system with only one drug targeting the N-methyl-D-aspartate (NMDA) receptor. Cholinergic 
neurons are mainly found in the cerebral cortex, striatum, and hippocampus which are areas of the 
brain involved in critical physiological processes, such as attention, learning, memory, and sleep [9]. 
Because of the important role in cognitive processes, the cholinergic system has been pointed to as 
an important factor in many forms of dementia, including AD. Especially, the cholinergic synapses 
are affected by the Aβ plaque neurotoxicity in the early stages of AD. It is further believed that the 
loss of cholinergic neurons causes reduced dopamine transmission which could be the main factor 
triggering AD-related symptoms [10]. As a result of those findings, the first generation of AD drugs 
has focused on the cholinergic system. The cholinergic targeting drugs are Donepezil, Galantamine, 
and Rivastigmine which all are acetylcholinesterase inhibitors (AChEIs) (Figure 2).  
 
Figure 2: AChEIs are highlighted in the black box and the NMDA receptor inhibitor is highlighted in the blue 
box. 
Inhibition of AChE will increase the concentration of acetylcholine (ACh) in the synaptic cleft 
leading to a prolonged neuronal signal and thereby reducing cognitive symptoms (Figure 3). The 
efficiency of the drugs, however, decreases over time and the average period of treatment is 14 
==PAGE:14==
Page 4 of 116 
 
months. Besides the development of tolerance towards the treatment, the AChEIs are not effective in 
slowing down the disease progression [11].  
Other therapeutic agents like Memantine aim to control glutamate levels. Glutamate is an excitatory 
neurotransmitter in the brain and spinal cord which is involved in learning processes. High 
concentrations of glutamate lead to excessive NMDA receptor activity which consequently leads to 
excitotoxicity and neuronal death [12]. Memantine is used to treat moderate to severe AD patients, 
whereas AChEIs are typically given to mild to moderate AD patients [13]. Memantine is a non-
competitive antagonist for the NMDA receptor. It binds preferably to the open ion channel (open 
channel blocker) and blocks the flow of Ca2+ ions (Figure 3).   
 
Figure 3: The mechanism of action of AChEIs and NMDA antagonists. Figure created with BioRender. 
For the past 20 years, the only new drug approved by the FDA for the treatment of AD is the 
monoclonal antibody, Aducanumab. Passive Aβ immunotherapy is a new tactic in the pursuit of 
finding new effective treatments. The IgG1 antibody binds aggregated Aβ and assists in clearing the 
plaques from the brain. Aducanumab received accelerated approval by the FDA in 2021 and is still 
waiting for full approval. The fast approval of Aducanumab has received criticism because the clinical 
trials gave conflicting results on efficacy. Critics suspect that it can give false hope to patients and in 
combination with the high cost, they do not endorse the treatment [14], [15].  
Besides passive immunotherapy, certain other areas like stem cell therapy, anti-inflammatory agents, 
neuroprotection, and metabolic deficiencies are being investigated but so far without a breakthrough 
[16].

2.1 A connection between AD and diabetes? 
 
Current evidence suggests that diabetes may play an important role in accelerated brain aging. 
Diabetic patients are at greater risk of developing dementia including AD compared to non-diabetic 
individuals. Elderly people with type 1 diabetes (T1D) have an 80% higher risk of developing 
dementia and for patients with type 2 diabetes (T2D), the risk is 73% [17], [18]. T1D results from the 
destruction of β-cells in the islets of Langerhans in the pancreas leading to no or very little production 
of insulin. T2D, on the other hand, is caused by insulin resistance and consequently insufficient 
insulin production. Interestingly, peripheral insulin resistance also leads to decreased insulin 
signaling in the CNS, and with the high energy demand of the brain, insulin resistance in the CNS 
displays similar pathogenesis as AD. Because of that, researchers have proposed a new type of 
diabetes, type 3 diabetes (T3D), that is associated with dementia and AD because of the shared 
pathogenesis [19].  
Insulin is produced in the pancreas by β-cells in the islets of Langerhans and is transported across the 
BBB into the brain through a receptor-mediated active transport system [19]. However, the brain also 
has its own biosynthesis of insulin. A subpopulation of neurons in the cerebral cortex and 
hippocampus do supplement the peripheral supply of insulin [20]. However, how much this 
production contributes to the overall insulin pool in the brain is still unclear [19]. 
Insulin receptors are widely expressed and distributed across different areas of the brain. But, 
especially the hypothalamus, hippocampus, and cerebral cortex have high expression. These areas of 
the brain are involved in important functions such as the regulation of food intake, energy 
homeostasis, neuronal development, and cognitive functions [21]. There are two features of an adult 
brain that makes it particularly vulnerable to a diseased state. Neurons are permanently in a 
postmitotic state which means that they do not regenerate, and in combination with the incredibly 
high energy demand of neurons, they are more vulnerable during energy crises or oxidative stress. 
This neuronal degeneration or loss will cause a predisposition to neurodegenerative diseases [19].  
A direct connection between insulin resistance and the build-up of Aβ has been proposed. T2D 
patients have Aβ deposits both in the pancreas and in the brain like those seen in AD patients [19]. 
Whether the T3D and brain insulin resistance is a consequence or a cause of the abnormal Aβ protein 
processing is still a debate. However, with T3D as the underlying cause of AD, the impaired insulin 
signaling with oxidative stress and neuroinflammation is believed to promote the Aβ accumulation. 
The degradation of insulin and Aβ share the same degradative pathway through the insulin-degrading 
enzyme (IDE) [19]. Reduced activity of IDE has been shown to induce hyperinsulinemia and glucose 
intolerance [22]. The elevated levels of insulin can result in less degradation of Aβ since insulin will 
compete with Aβ binding to IDE, thereby, contributing to the build-up. Besides that, insulin could 
stimulate the secretion of Aβ by accelerating the trafficking of Aβ from Golgi to the plasma 
membrane [19].  
==PAGE:16==
Page 6 of 116 
 
To sum up, the consequences of impaired insulin signaling are decreased metabolism in the brain 
which leads to neuronal degeneration with the same pathology seen in AD patients.  
 
2.2 Gut-derived vs brain-derived GLP-1 and its role in AD 
 
Glucagon-like peptide-1 (GLP-1) is a 30 amino acid peptide hormone that plays a crucial role in the 
regulation of blood sugar levels. GLP-1 is an incretin that enhances the secretion of insulin and 
thereby decreases blood sugar levels. GLP-1 is mainly produced in the distal small intestines by L-
cells and subsequently secreted into the bloodstream. Although, the main production of GLP-1 takes 
place in the small intestines the brain has its own GLP-1 biosynthesis [23]. GLP-1 is derived from 
the proglucagon gene by posttranslational processing through prohormone convertase 1/3 (PC1/3) 
(Figure 4). The proglucagon peptide consists of the glicitin-related polypeptide (GRPP), glucagon, 
GLP-1, GLP-2, and two intervening peptides (IP-1 and IP-2), and depending on the cell type the 
peptide is processed differently. In pancreatic α-cells, the processing yields glucagon and Major 
Proglucagon Fragment (MPGF) but in the intestines and the brain the processing yields glicentin, 
oxyntomodulin, GLP-1, GLP-2, and IP-2 (Figure 4) [23]. 
 
Figure 4: The PC 1/3 processing of proglucagon which happens in the L-cells, in the small intestines, and the 
NTS.  
In the CNS GLP-1 is produced by certain neurons in the nucleus tractus solitarius (NTS) within the 
brainstem upon food intake. There are two pathways in which GLP-1 signals to the brain; the humoral 
and the neural pathway (Figure 5). Through the humoral pathway, gut-derived GLP-1 acts directly 
on the hypothalamus receptors by crossing the blood-brain barrier (BBB) following intestinal 
==PAGE:17==
Page 7 of 116 
 
secretion to the bloodstream. But, due to rapid degradation by dipeptidyl peptidase-4 (DPP-4) and a 
low half-life (2 min), gut‐derived GLP‐1 most likely signals to the NTS by the neural pathway [24]. 
This signal happens via the vagal afferent nerves in the intestinal or hepatic portal area that stimulates 
the NTS to start the synthesis of GLP-1 [24]. The brain-derived GLP-1 is transported to the axon 
terminals of the NTS neurons which extends to the hypothalamus and spinal cord. Thus, brain-derived 
GLP-1 function much like a neurotransmitter. In this environment, GLP-1 is believed to regulate 
numerous metabolic and cardiovascular functions such as satiety control, energy homeostasis, 
thermogenesis, blood pressure control, neurogenesis, and neurodegeneration [25], [26]. The GLP-1 
signal transduction happens through the GLP-1 receptor (GLP-1R) which is a G-protein coupled 
receptor (GPCR) that upon activation leads to the triggering of adenylyl cyclase, protein kinase C 
(PKC), and mitogen-activated protein (MAP) kinase [27]. These pathways are associated with 
neuronal protection, growth, and stem cell differentiation in the hypothalamus, hippocampus, and 
cerebral cortex [27].   
 
Figure 5: GLP-1 signaling has two pathways; the humoral and neural pathways. The humoral pathway 
involves the secretion into the bloodstream where GLP-1 will cross the BBB and enter the brain to directly 
deliver the signal to the hypothalamus. In the neural pathway, GLP-1 activates a neural signal that goes via 
the vagal afferent nerve to the NTS which then synthesizes its own GLP-1. GLP-1 is stored in vesicles in the 
axon terminals than can be released in the hypothalamus. Figure created with BioRender.

2.3 GLP-1 receptor agonists for the treatment of AD 
 
GLP-1 receptor agonists (GLP-1RAs) like exendin-4 and liraglutide have shown promising results in 
the treatment of neurodegenerative diseases, especially AD [28]. GLP-1RAs have a neuroprotective 
effect in the CNS by reducing Aβ plaques, preventing loss of synapses, and thus slowing down 
memory impairments [29]. Moreover, GLP-1RAs increase insulin sensitivity and improve energy 
homeostasis in the brain. Besides that, GLP-1RAs stimulate the differentiation of neuronal stem cells 
to promote neurogenesis and prevent brain atrophy in AD patients [28].  
 
Figure 6: The structure of GLP-1, Liraglutide ®, and exendin-4. Amino acids that deviate from GLP-1 are 
marked in red.  
Another interesting GLP-1RA is the small molecule 6,7-dichloro-2-methylsulfonyl-3-N-tert-
butylaminoquinoxaline (DMB) which is an agonist and an allosteric modulator of the GLP-1R that 
can increase the affinity of GLP-1 to its receptor [30]. Therefore, it can act alone or in combination 
with peptide GLP-1RAs.

3 Drug delivery across the brain barriers  
 
The increased prevalence of CNS disorders just underlines the urgent need for improved CNS-active 
drugs. Despite, the urgent need for CNS-active drugs the development time is longer, and the attrition 
rate is higher compared to other therapeutic areas [31]. The complexity of the brain and poor 
permeability of the brain barriers are some of the reasons why CNS drugs have a lower success rate. 
Especially the lack of efficient drug delivery techniques complicates the development of CNS-active 
drugs. The brain is arguably the most important organ of the human body. Thus, it is well protected, 
not only by the skull but also by unique physiological barriers that regulate the transportation of cells, 
molecules, and ions from the blood into the brain. These barriers are vital for maintaining the narrow 
homeostatic window of the brain [32].  
3.1 The blood-brain barrier 
 
The blood-brain barrier (BBB) is a semipermeable cellular barrier that tightly regulates the entry of 
substances to the brain from the bloodstream. It is comprised of highly specialized endothelial cells 
which are connected by tight junctions to construct a continuous layer that restricts the diffusion of 
molecules between neighboring cells (Figure 8). Additionally, the brain capillaries are not fenestrated 
because of the tight junctions which limit the diffusion of polar molecules and macromolecules such 
as proteins. The endothelial cells are surrounded by pericytes and astrocytic end feet which constitute 
an additional regulatory layer. Together, these cells are part of the neurovascular unit (NVU), and 
they define the physical barriers of the BBB [32]. They prevent the influx of unwanted molecules and 
cells from the blood into the extracellular fluid of the CNS while mediating the active transport of 
nutrients and hormones necessary for CNS functions. Another important feature of the BBB is the 
presence of efflux pumps that can remove a huge variety of hydrophobic molecules and drugs from 
the brain.   
The BBB endothelium is the tightest in the body, and thus the BBB represents the main obstacle to 
drug delivery to the brain. For this reason, CNS drug delivery is a crucial and tough task in CNS drug 
development [33].  
==PAGE:21==
Page 11 of 116 
 
 
Figure 8: The BBB is comprised of specialized endothelial cells, pericytes, and astrocytes that make up the 
NVU and they strictly regulate the entry of molecules. Figure created with BioRender.  
 
3.2 Crossing the BBB 
 
Biopharmaceuticals and 98% of all small molecules do not cross the BBB [34]. The average 
molecular weight of a CNS-active drug is 357 Da and they form less than 8-10 hydrogen bonds in 
water. [35]. Thus, the size and hydrophilicity are crucial for BBB penetration of small molecules. 
There are generally two mechanisms by which molecules can cross epithelial barriers: transcellular 
and paracellular transport (Figure 9). However, paracellular transport across the BBB is highly 
restricted because of the tighter endothelial junctions [36]. Small molecule drugs that fulfill the 
requirements mentioned earlier have an increased chance of entering the brain by transcellular passive 
diffusion across the BBB. This transport is gradient-dependent, and it is the major route for current 
CNS-active therapeutics [35]. However, the BBB can regulate the entry of small lipophilic molecules 
by efflux pumps. The high versatility of the efflux pumps makes them capable of returning a high 
variety of unwanted molecules back to the bloodstream. Thus, many molecules that can cross the 
BBB by passive diffusion never reach the brain because of a subsequent transport back into the blood 
[37]. Although most drugs utilize a passive transcellular transport mechanism, the transport of 
biomolecules like nutrients, ions, and proteins occurs through active transcellular transport. Small 
polar solutes like glucose and amino acids can cross the BBB by carrier-mediated transport, also 
called solute carriers. It is an indirect ATP-dependent transport, and the carrier can be specific for 
more than one molecule. Larger solutes such as peptides and proteins are transported by receptor-
mediated transport (Figure 9) [38].  
==PAGE:22==
Page 12 of 116 
 
 
Figure 9: Transcellular and paracellular passive diffusion are gradient-dependent transports whereas 
carrier- and receptor-mediated transport is not gradient-dependent since they require an energy source. Efflux 
pumps are also ATP-dependent and can transport molecules against the gradient. Figure created with 
BioRender and inspired by [38].     
Today, there is not a single FDA-approved biopharmaceutical for CNS disorders, that can cross the 
BBB [39]. This only highlights the need for new strategies for brain delivery as well as exploring 
alternative routes for brain entry, such as through the blood-cerebrospinal fluid barrier (BCSFB).  
3.3 The blood-cerebrospinal fluid barrier 
 
The BCSFB is far less studied than the BBB. The BCSFB is localized in the choroid plexus (CP) of 
the brain ventricles and separates the CSF from the blood, compared to the BBB that separates the 
brain interstitial fluid from the blood. An important function of the BCSFB is the removal of toxic 
metabolic waste, and thus, the transport taking place in the BCSFB is bidirectional [40]. Besides that, 
the capillaries of the BCSFB are fenestrated and surrounded by connective tissue containing immune 
cells. On the ventricular side, a single layer of cuboidal epithelial cells linked together by tight 
junctions makes up the main barrier (Figure 10) [41]. The cuboidal epithelial cells have a villous 
structure and are responsible for the production of CSF [40]. The CP falls into the class of leaky 
epithelia, similar to the kidney and gut epithelia that can secrete large volumes of fluid [41].  
==PAGE:23==
Page 13 of 116 
 
 
Figure 10: The BCSFB is localized in the CP of the brain ventricles. The BCSFB contains a CP cell layer as 
the main barrier and a stromal layer with immune cells. The BCSFB is leakier than the BBB due to the 
fenestrated endothelium. Figure created with BioRender.  
3.4 Crossing the BCSFB 
 
While the BBB has a more restricted passage of substances, the BCSFB is fenestrated and serves as 
a gatekeeper for many nutrients and immune cells [41]. The BBB and the BCSFB share a similar 
functional organization of transport systems. They both express proteins, so-called efflux pumps, that 
mediate the transport of nutrients by carrier-mediated transcellular transport of solutes.  
While researchers have focused on the BBB for developing drug delivery techniques, the passage 
across the BCSFB appears to be a preferential pathway for immune cells to gain access to the CNS 
both in physiological conditions and during the early phase of neuroinflammation [42]. But not only 
do immune cells utilize the BCSFB as an access point to the brain, many neurotropic viruses, 
including the Mumps virus (MuV), infect the brain by crossing the BCSFB. Therefore, exploiting the 
BCSFB for drug entry into the brain could be an attractive strategy.  
3.4.1 The Mumps virus peptide as a shuttle for brain delivery 
 
The MuV is an RNA virus that causes mumps which is characterized by painful inflammatory 
symptoms, such as parotitis and orchitis. However, the MuV is also highly neurotropic meaning that 
once the MuV enters the bloodstream it can effectively travel to the brain and cause infection which 
occurs in approximately half of the cases [43]. The genome of the MuV contains seven proteins one 
of which is the small hydrophobic (SH) peptide. The MuV uses the SH peptide to cross the BCSFB

Page 14 of 116 
 
and would therefore be interesting from a drug delivery perspective. The SH peptide is, as the name 
indicates, a rather small peptide (57 amino acids) with hydrophobic features.  Recent studies by M. 
Rosenkilde have discovered that the MuV-SH peptide acts as a ligand for the GPR125, an adhesion 
G-protein coupled receptor expressed on the surface of the CP epithelium. Additionally, the GPR125 
undergoes clathrin‐dependent endocytosis upon binding (Figure 11).

Targeting the BCSFB for drug delivery is a novel approach for the delivery of drugs to the brain, 
which may be beneficial for the treatment of CNS diseases. Not only will drugs be delivered to the 
ventricles containing high expression of receptors involved in cognitive functions but targeting the 
BCSFB will allow drugs to reach deep cerebral structures via CSF circulation [42]. The BCSFB is 
less restrictive than the BBB and represents an attractive alternative. But no efficient transport system 
has yet been developed [42]. Intriguingly, new results from K. Qvortrup in collaboration with M. 
Rosenkilde have shown that combining a fragment of the SH-peptide (2-11) and exendin-4 does 
increase the delivery to the brain in vivo. But to further expand the delivery technique and also include 
small molecule drugs, a brain-specific drug release mechanism is necessary. In this regard, enzyme-

4 Acetylcholinesterase 
 
Acetylcholinesterase (AChE) is a cholinergic enzyme that catalyzes the hydrolysis of the 
neurotransmitter acetylcholine in the synaptic cleft (Scheme 1). The primary role of AChE is to 
terminate the neuronal signal and stop transmission. When acetylcholine is released from the 
presynaptic nerve it reaches the postjunctional receptors but as soon as acetylcholine is released from 
the receptor, it will immediately be hydrolyzed by AChE. It happens so fast that normally one 
molecule of acetylcholine only reacts once with a postjunctional receptor [45]. The turnover rate of 
AChE approaches 1.5·10-4 s-1 which makes it one of the most efficient enzymes of the human body 
[46].  
  
Scheme 1: The enzymatic hydrolysis of acetylcholine yields choline and acetic acid. 
AChE is a serine hydrolase with an active site containing two subsites; an esteratic and an anionic 
subsite (Figure 13). The esteratic site contains a catalytic triad Ser203, His447, and Glu334 which 
resembles the catalytic site of other serine proteases [47]. The serine is activated by histidine and 
glutamic acid in a proposed mechanism shown in figure 12. The serine then makes a nucleophilic 
attack on the carbonyl in acetylcholine creating a tetrahedral intermediate where the oxyanion is 
stabilized by amide hydrogens from the peptide backbone of Gly121, Gly122, and Ala204 (Figure 
13). A proton transfer from the protonated His447 to choline allows the release of choline and the 
reformation of the carbonyl which leaves Ser203 acetylated. The acetylation is hydrolyzed with the 
same proton transfer mechanism by His447 and Glu334 to release acetic acid (Figure 12). In the 
==PAGE:27==
Page 17 of 116 
 
anionic site, Trp84 has been identified to be crucial for the binding of the quaternary amine by a 
cation-π interaction [48].

Figure 13: The active site of AChE contains an esteric site with the catalytic triad and an anionic site with 
Trp84. Gly121, Gly122, and Ala204 are important for the stabilization of the oxyanion intermediate. Figure 
inspired by [48].

The high catalytic turnover and brain selectivity of AChE makes it highly relevant to investigate for 
brain-specific drug release.  
4.1 Other cholinesterases 
 
AChE is a type B carboxylesterase enzyme and is synthesized in nerve, muscle, and certain 
hematopoietic cells. Other cholinesterases such as butyrylcholinesterase (BChE) are largely 
synthesized in the liver and subsequently exported to the bloodstream. It displays a more diverse 
range of substrates than AChE and is thought to play a role in the metabolism of dietary esters [46]. 
AChE is distinguished from BChE by its catalytic selectivity for ACh over butyrylcholine.

5 The project 
 
This project aims to develop an AChE-cleavable linker for a peptide-drug conjugate (PDC) that can 
be used in the treatment of AD. An effective linker shall display high stability in circulation and a 
rapid drug release in the presence of AChE. To identify the most suitable structure for efficient drug 
release a small selection of AChE-cleavable linkers will be synthesized and evaluated in a structure-
activity relationship (SAR) study. It is hypothesized that the electronic and/or steric effects of 
different R substituents will affect both the cleavage rate and stability of the linker (Figure 14).   
The structure of the PDC is illustrated in figure 14 below. The PDC consists of three moieties; the 
SH2-11 shuttle peptide, the AChE-cleavable linker, and an amine-functionalized CNS-active drug.  
 
Figure 14: The structure of the peptide-drug conjugate. 
 
The linker is designed to conjugate amino-functionalized drugs such as the small molecule GLP-1RA 
DMB or even peptide drugs such as Exendin-4. Importantly, the design releases the drug in its native 
form to ensure no alteration in activity. Upon entering the CSF, the AChE will cleave the linker and 
release the drug through spontaneous 1,6-elimination when the phenolate is exposed after AChE 
cleavage (Figure 15).

Figure 15: Upon AChE cleavage the linker undergoes a spontaneous 1,6-elimination to release the drug. 
 
The structure of the AChE linker is slightly modified compared to ACh (Figure 16). The acetyl group 
is exchanged with a dimethyl carbamate and the distance between the two recognition moieties is 
increased from two to three carbons. The introduction of the aromatic ring introduces some rigidity

6.3.2.2 The Second strategy 
 
Due to the low yields of the first strategy, the second strategy was instead investigated. The major 
concerns for this reaction were the nucleophilicity of the aromatic ring in the presence of the high 
EW nitro group and whether the benzylic alcohol would interfere with the reaction. The alcohol could 
act as a nucleophile and attack the imine to form a hemiaminal. However, the reaction is reversible 
in contrast to the last step in EAS. In addition, the pKa of an ortho-nitrophenol is around 7 whereas 
the pKa of a benzylic alcohol is around 15, so under the moderate basic conditions, the aromatic ring 
is expected to be the best nucleophile because of electron donation from the phenolate to the π-system. 
The reaction was carried out under similar conditions as for 1a-c, however, the choice of solvent for 
this reaction was DMF instead of DCM because of its higher boiling point. It was expected that the 
nitro group would slow down the reaction rate significantly because of the deactivation of the ring 
and therefore, a higher temperature could be needed for the reaction to occur.  
 
Scheme 19: Conditions affording 1-1c. 
Rewardingly, the reaction proceeded in an excellent yield. Substituting the aldehyde for a benzylic 
alcohol does not seem to affect the regioselectivity of the reaction. The benzyl alcohol is expected to 
display a similar directing effect as the CH2N(CH3)2 group from the nitration because of a likewise 
polarization of the benzylic carbon. Therefore, the expected positioning of the CH2N(CH3)2 is the 
desired one since the existing substituents direct to the same position. This is confirmed by 1H-NMR 
with a meta-coupling (~2 Hz) between the aromatic protons (See experimental section 9.2.3).  
With the excellent yield of 1-1c, the second strategy seems to be the better option. However, the 
benzylic alcohol could pose a problem in the next step.

6.3.5 Attachment of drug 
 
Conjugation of drugs and model drugs to benzylic alcohols is a well-established technique within the 
KQ group through a carbamate moiety that is stable during circulation but capable of releasing the 
drug after enzymatic cleavage. The conjugation can be performed by activation of either the benzylic 
alcohol from the linker or the amine from the drug. In this project, AMC was introduced as a model 
drug to study the enzymatic cleavage. The conjugation was achieved by activating AMC using 
triphosgene which creates the reactive isocyanate, see scheme 23 below. The synthesis of carbamates 
from isocyanates is a well-known synthetic strategy, where the isocyanate is formed in the reaction 
between an amine and phosgene [51]. As phosgene is a highly toxic gas, it is usually substituted with 
the more stable and safer derivative in solid form; triphosgene, which forms phosgene in situ.  
  
 
 
Scheme 23: Proposed reaction mechanism for the formation of the AMC-isocyanate. 
The AMC-isocyanate is highly reactive because of the polarization of the carbon induced by the 
electronegative nitrogen and oxygen atoms. To further increase reactivity, the reaction was performed 
in the presence of Dibutyltin dilaurate (DBTDL), a Lewis Acid, that additionally decreases the 
LUMO of the carbonyl carbon in a suggested mechanism shown in scheme 24. The DBTDL 
coordinates with the lone pair of the nitrogen from the isocyanate [52] and the benzylic alcohol then 
attacks the isocyanate carbonyl to form the carbamate.

6.3.6 Methylation 
 
The final step for the synthesis of the AMC-functionalized linkers is the introduction of the quaternary 
ammonium group, which is crucial for enzymatic recognition. The quaternization was done by 
alkylation of the tertiary amine with methyl iodide. The reaction is an SN2 reaction that proceeds 
through a nucleophilic back-side attack by the lone pair of the amine on the methyl iodide, thereby 
forcing the departure of iodide (Scheme 25).  
 
Scheme 25: Proposed mechanism for the formation of 5b-c. 
As mentioned earlier in the retrosynthetic analysis, section 6.1, methylation after AMC/drug coupling 
could be problematic since the drug most likely contains multiple nucleophilic groups susceptible to 
alkylation. However, for the synthesis of the AMC-functionalized derivatives, the introduction of the 
quaternary ammonium as the final step will simplify purification as quaternary ammonium 
compounds suffer from poor solubility in organic solvents and challenging flash chromatography 
purification.

6.3 Enzyme assays 
 
With the successful synthesis of II and III, the small library now consists of five analogs (I, II, III, 
IV, and V) containing different groups in the R position with varying electronic effects (Figure 20). 
Compound I, with hydrogen in the R position serves as a ‘neutral’ analog since hydrogen is the 
smallest substituent with no steric or electronic effects. Compound II has a more electron-rich ring, 
which may influence enzyme recognition, while also a steric effect may come into play. Furthermore, 
a higher stability of the conjugate is expected as the methoxy group will lower the leaving group 
ability of the phenolate. Compound III is very interesting because of the high EW effect from the 
nitro group and the ability to stabilize the phenolate anion through resonance, making the phenolate 
formation more favorable. This could potentially increase the cleavage rate. Compound IV with the 
trifluoro methyl group is likewise expected to favor the phenolate formation because of an inductive 
effect. Lastly, compound V with fluorine adds an inductive effect but without the steric effects since 
the size of fluorine is very similar to hydrogen.   
 
Figure 20: An overview of the current library comprised of five analogs with varying electronic and steric 
properties. 
==PAGE:61==
Page 51 of 116 
 
To investigate the AChE cleavage rate of the analogs, the enzymatic assay was performed using 
recombinant human AChE. And to mimic the pH 7.3 of the CSF, a PBS buffer with pH 7.4 was used.  
The AChE-cleavable analogs have AMC attached through a carbamate bond, identical to how a CNS-
active drug would be attached, therefore the release of AMC is an indicator of drug release. Upon 
enzymatic hydrolysis of the dimethyl carbamate, a 4-alkoxybenzyl carbamate will be revealed and 
undergo spontaneous 1,6-elimination of the amine coupled AMC, which releases native AMC, a p-
quinone methide moiety, and carbon dioxide, see figure 21.  
 
Figure 21: The AChE hydrolysis leads to a spontaneous 1,6-elimination of AMC, a p-quinone methide moiety, 
and carbon dioxide.  
A key advantage of using AMC as a fluorophore is the dramatic increase in fluorescence intensity 
(FI) upon the liberation of the amine. The free amine donates electrons to the ring, and thus increases 
the freedom of the π-electrons [56]. Besides that, AMC has a good separation between excitation and 
emission maxima which is important for efficient detection [56]. The release of AMC is measured by 
fluorescence spectroscopy because the FI is proportional to the concentration of released AMC.  
Before conducting the SAR study of the library some preliminary assays were needed. This includes 
an enzyme validation assay, an assay to study the proportionality of the FI and AMC concentration, 
and an assay to investigate the optimal enzyme concentration. All these initial assays will be 
elaborated in the following sections.

7 Conclusion 
 
During the project, two AChE-cleavable linker analogs (II and III) with different electronic and steric 
properties were successfully synthesized with the AMC fluorophore as a model drug. The syntheses 
were planned and performed experimentally. The synthesis of III was investigated with alternative 
synthetic strategies, with one of the strategies being superior to the other with the best overall yield 
for both the synthesis of the key building block for the SAR study, 3c, and the PDC, 2c. The synthesis 
of II proceeded as planned and both analogs were isolated as the acetate salt and added to the small 
library of AChE-cleavable linkers.    
 
The drug release profile of the five analogs (I-V) from the library was studied through fluorimetry 
under conditions representative of the CSF compartment. The SAR study indicated that the R-
position, ortho to the enzymatically cleaved carbamate, is very sensitive to steric changes, which 
eliminates any potential correlation between enzymatic hydrolysis rate and stability of the revealed 
phenolate. The only substituents that allowed enzymatic hydrolysis were fluorine and hydrogen, with 
hydrogen displaying the best release profile. The fraction of released AMC from analog I was found 
to be 33% over 8 hours at an AChE concentration of 5 U/mL with excellent stability in the absence 
of AChE. However, the AChE concentration required for an optimal drug release was 5 U/mL which 
is considerably higher than the AChE concentration in CSF of AD patients, 20-30 mU/mL [59]. This 
suggests that more investigations are needed to further optimize the linker structure to enhance the 
release rate and fraction of the drug released.  
 
With the current challenges in CNS-drug development and with the expected tremendous increase in 
the prevalence of AD over the next decades, there is no doubt that novel technologies for brain-
specific delivery are needed. Our AChE-cleavable linker has shown high stability at physiologically 
relevant pH and the possibility of releasing a drug in the presence of AChE. Although the drug release 
requires optimization, it still holds promising potential in a PDC for the treatment of AD.

8 Future work and perspectives 
 
Future work will initially focus on the expansion of the library to extend our knowledge of the AChE 
cleavage rate and model drug release. Interesting positions to investigate could be the suggested R1 
position which is ortho to the benzylic carbamate that could potentially enhance the drug release rate. 
Furthermore, various bioisosteres of the dimethyl carbamate could also be interesting to investigate 
how this will affect the enzymatic cleavage. Finally, the stability of the library linkers in human and 
rodent plasma with the presence of other cholinesterases like BChE should also be evaluated to ensure 
a brain-specific drug release.  
When suitable linker candidates are found they will be incorporated in a PDC by attaching an alkyne 
handle through the Grignard reaction suggested in section 6.1 followed by the attachment of a GLP-
1RA either the small molecule DMB or exendin-4. Finally, the synthesized SH2-11 peptide with spacer 
will then be attached through CuAAc to complete the PDCs. The pharmacology of the PDCs will 
then be assessed in relevant in vitro and in vivo models by the lab of M. Rosenkilde.

9.1 General Methodologies 
 
All starting materials, reagents, and solvents were commercially available and used without further 
purification. All solvents used were of HPLC grade. Anhydrous solvents were obtained from a 
PureSolv™MD-7 Solvent Purification System, Innovative Technology with Al2O3 as the stationary 
phase. Human recombinant AChE was purchased from R & D Systems, Sigma-Aldrich, and ProSpec-
Tany TechnoGene Ltd.  
Reactions were monitored using thin-layer chromatography (TLC) and/or UPLC-MS. Analytical 
TLC was conducted on Merck Aluminum TLC plates, silica gel 60 coated with fluorescent indicator 
F254. TLC plates were visualized under UV light. Flash column chromatography and dry column 
vacuum chromatography (DCVC) were performed using glass columns of varying sizes packed with 
Merck Geduran 60 Angstrom silica gel (40-64 μm particles) as the stationary phase. Eluents and 
volume ratios are specified under each reaction procedure. Solvent removal was done under reduced 
pressure (in vacuo) or blow-dried when suitable. Traces of solvents were removed in vacuo with the 
use of a membrane pump also referred to as house vacuum.  
Analytic UPLC-MS analysis was run on a Waters AQUITY UPLC system equipped with PDA and 
SQD electrospray MS detector. Column: Kinetex 1.7 μm XB-C18, 2.1 x 50mm. Column temperature: 
50 °C. Flowrate: 0.50 mL/min. Eluent A (0.1% HCOOH in H2O) and B (0.1% HCOOH in MeCN) 
used in a linear gradient (5% B to 100% B) in 2.4 or 4.8 min, hold 0.1 min, total run time 2.6 or 5.0 
min. Alternatively, Eluent C (15 mM NH4OAcin H2O) and D (15 mM NH4OAc in MeCN) used in a

9.3 Procedures for Enzyme Assays 
 
The enzyme unit (U) is defined as the amount of enzyme that catalyzes the conversion of 1.0 μmol 
of substrate per minute at pH 7 and 37 °C. All enzyme assays were performed in a microtiter plate 
with 96 wells and analyzed using the Infinite M200 PRO Tecan instrument. All fluorescence 
spectroscopy assays were performed in Greiner ® 96-well flat bottom black and all absorbance assays 
were performed in Corning ® 96-well flat bottom transparent.

buffer and then performing the 2-fold serial dilution in Eppendorf tubes before transferring to the 
microtiter plate with a total volume of 200 μL in each well. The fluorescence intensity was examined 
at 37 °C with the parameters of λex 387 nm and λem 470 nm according to [56]. A negative control

curve was conducted from the average of the triplicates subtracted the background fluorescence from
