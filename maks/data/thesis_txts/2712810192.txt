Abstract
Glass has been used for centuries in many different fields. A popular field of glass
use is building envelopes. Since glass is a brittle material which possibly can do
fatal damage to humans in case of fraction, several restrictions have been made. In
order to fulfill these restrictions tempered glass, also called safety glass, is often
used in buildings. To check whether the residual stresses are suﬀicient according to
production standards a SCALP instrument can be used.
This thesis takes the basis of calculation in question since it has been assumed that
the photoelastic constant does not vary over the depth of glass plates. Recent studies
have found a tendency in tempered glass to the density and this is wished for to be
further investigated. If there is a dependency on the residual stresses as found in
other papers it is possible to adjust the constant, hereby yielding in more accurate
stress measurements. In order to investigate this further, an experiment which has
not been used for this purpose yet, is developed on the basis of the standard co-
axial double ring bending test. Since the test described in the Eurocodes has a
ring diameter which is too narrow for the measurement instrument to fit inside, an
extended version of the test setup has been developed. In order to develop the test
setup a Finite Element Model has been modelled in the commercial software Abaqus
and SolidWorks.
The SCALP uses an immersion liquid to have better contact to the specimen when
measuring. In order to check whether an outflow and therefore a change in the
measurements over time a preliminary test was carried out. This was not the case,
and the execution of the main experiments were done.
By comparing the theoretically calculated stresses and the measured stresses an ad-
justment of the photoelastic constant can be found. This was done for all specimens
and for all depths herein.
The experiments lead to a clear variation of the photoelastic constant throughout
the depth of the specimens. By comparing to the literature and to different factors
which could affect the photoelastic constant it was not possible to find an exact
dependency.
Because of this it has been suggested to do the experiment again
but with multiple adjustments. Other types of experiments are also suggested to
investigate the photoelastic constant further.
Keywords: Glass, photoelastic constant, stress optical coeﬀicient, co-axial double
ring bending test, plates, Finite Element Method.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Glass has been produced for multiple centuries and dates back to 3600 BC in Egypt.
As the technology and production methods have advanced better glass can be pro-
duced and it is now possible to use glass as structural elements of buildings. As glass
has the advantage of letting more daylight into buildings it is coveted, but as the
area of the building facades increases, challenges in regard to other indoor climate
factors occurs. In the summer large glass panels can lead to overheating and vice
versa in the winter. This gives extra requirements for HVAC systems. As technology
makes it possible to build at new heights there are also increased requirements from
the European Standards and the strengths of glass have to be suﬀicient. This has to
be documented in the same way as for e.g. steel and concrete structures when it is
used as structural elements. As stronger glass was needed, multiple alternative ver-
sions to float glass have been invented. These count heat strengthened, tempered,
laminated and mechanically reinforced glass. In tempered glass the stresses vary
over the cross-section creating a surface with large compressive stresses. In order to
ensure the quality of the tempered glass different techniques can be used. One of
the methods for determining the stresses through glass plates is by measuring with
a so-called SCALP, which sends a laser ray through the glass. A camera at the side
of the laser can then record the retardations from the laser at all depths through-
out the glass. This can then be used to determine the stresses through the glass
plates. In order to calculate the stresses in the glass, formulas which is converting
the retardation from the laser into stresses, are used. In these formulas the so-called
photoelastic constant is used.
In this project the aim is to investigate the influence of multi-axial bending on the
photoelastic constant in tempered glass. In the literature the photoelastic constant is

CHAPTER 1.

1.2
State of the art
This section describes the research done within the area of photoelasticity and the
photoelastic constant. It is described in chronological order where the main results
are summarised shortly.
The photoelastic constant has until 1953 only been measured at low stresses up
to 0.0098 MPa. Bartenev (1953) decided to expand this since glasses usually can
withstand much larger stresses. By using a four point bending test it was found that
glass which were chemically toughened with acid had a photoelastic constant which
didn’t vary, from 0-245.5 MPa. He noted that the photoelastic constant might be
constant for even higher stresses.
Yokota (1979) investigated different types of glass and experimented with chemical
hardening of the surface of the glass. He noted that the usually used photoelastic
constant 2.60 TPa−1 wasn’t correct for all compositions of glass and could lead to
wrong result by using this.For different compositions of chemically hardened glass he
found constants with values from 2.81-2.98 TPa−1. The test was done by observing
the photoelastic fringes generated by the compressive stress which was applied to
a circular glass plate. This was in 1984 expanded further by himself and Matusita
et al. (1984). 37 different compositions of silicate glasses was used to determine the
photoelastic constants. They ranged between 2.24-4.00 TPa−1 and were determined
by using rectangles of about 10 by 12 by 100mm in a four point bending test. It
was concluded that they were highly dependent on the shear modulus. For some
compositions an increase in shear modulus would increase the photoelastic constant
and for other compositions it would decrease. A simple relation could not be found.
In 2010, an investigation carried out by Nielsen, Olesen, and Stang (2010) focused
on toughened glass. By using a scattered light polariscope together with a four point
bending test it was possible to calibrate the photoelastic constant to 3.01 TPa−1.
The experiment was carried out by measuring change in stress in the top surface of a
glass specimen during the bending test. Since the photoelastic constant is dependent
on the density, the variation from the float glass, which is 2.72TPa−1, could be a
part of the reason that the toughened glass had a higher value.
According to Ismaili and Ejlersen (2012), who made an experiment to determine
the photoelastic constant from using a horizontal tensile test, a variation of the
photoelastic constant was present. From the tensile test it was possible to investigate
the photoelastic constant under the same conditions throughout the depth. They
used a Scattered Light Polariscope to measure the stresses in a glass plate for varying
uniaxial stress states. They found a variation of the photoelastic constant from 3.05

In 2013 an investigation of two different methods for determining the photoelastic
constant was performed by Ramesh, Ramakrishnan, and Dora (2013). The methods

When a glass plate is subjected to a load it will fail as soon as the stress intensity due
to tensile stresses at the tip of a single flaw reaches its critical value. The loading
of the plate will induce crack growth from the flaws and this depends on several
different parameters. Besides the flaws the tensile strength of glass is dependent on
various factors such as the surface condition, the history of the actions in regard to
intensity and duration, and the size of the element. This means that if a glass is
loaded, an increase in the duration and load will lead to a deeper the initial surface
flaw and this leads to lower effective tensile strength. It is important to note that
the flaws will only increase when tensile pressure is present. Compression forces
will compress the flaws and therefore in practice for structural applications only the
tensile strength is used since the tensile strength will be exceeded long before the
glass is loaded to its compressive strength.
1.5
Tempering of glass
Float glass is the most common way to produce glass panels. If a higher strength of
the glass is needed there are multiple methods to strengthen the glass. These count:
Chemical strengthening, acid etching, lamination, reinforcing or thermal tempering.
This project is based on tempered glass, which is produced by reheating float glass.
Before reheating some steps have to be done. The first step is to drill holes, cut
geometry and chamfer edges since this is not possible after tempering, due to the
equilibrated residual stress state. After the pre-treatment the glass is cleaned be-
fore moving to an oven heating the glass to around 620-675◦C, which is above the
transmission temperature. This changes the glass from a solid to a soft material.
The glass is then cooled fast by quenching with air jets to around 20◦C. During the
quenching the glass is moved back and forth on the rollers to minimize the isolating
effect from the rollers. This process will often cause tempered glass to be slightly
curved due to the asymmetric cooling and to have roller-waves caused by the roller.
The tempering process will typically yield residual surface stresses from 80 MPa
to 170 MPa. The three main factors of tempering are the temperature dependent
viscoelasticity, the structural volume relaxation and the temperature history. The
tempering process can be seen in fig. 1.4.

stresses are present during the cooling but disappears when the temperature is
constant throughout the thickness. When the temperature is above the transition
temperature Tg the glass is relatively soft and cannot carry any stresses. When the
temperature reaches the transition temperature the glass will solidify and stresses

2.1
Photoelasticity
All structural materials have the property of elasticity. When the material is sub-
jected to a load inside a given range deformation appears. If the external load does
not exceed the elastic limit it will get the original shape back when unloaded. In
this thesis the elastic regime of glasses is investigated and the glasses are assumed
to be homogeneous and isotropic, meaning that the elastic properties are equal in
all directions (Aben and Guillemet (1993)).
The basic concept of photoelasticity is that a material which is under deformation
will have a change in optical properties. This change in optical properties is highly
useful to determine the stresses in a material. The indication that the material is
photoelastic is when there is an interaction of light with matter. This appearance

Polarized light
To describe the photoelastic effect or photoelasticity the electromagnetic theory due

therefore a number of arbitrary transverse vibrations. The waves are propagating
in all directions, so the rays are unpolarized. If the waves instead are restricted

propagation, the beam is polarized. The light can be polarized in several different
ways which is seen in fig. 2.1.

Birefringence
A lot of materials which are non-crystalline, but are ordinarily optically isotropic
become an-isotropic and will display some of the same optical characteristics as
crystals when they are stressed. Sir David Brewster (Aben and Guillemet (1993))
was the first to discover this phenomenon and it is usually known as artificial double
refraction. The concept of photoelasticity is based on this. The birefringence is the
reason to the double refraction. By double refraction a light ray which is approaching
a birefringent material, will split into two rays by polarization. Theses two rays will
have slightly different paths. When a material has a three-dimensional stress state

2.1.3
The Lattice effect
The electron density distribution governs the optical properties of solids which are
deformed. When the solid is deformed in e.g. a standard co-axial bending test,
where the stresses are compressive in the top and tensile in the bottom, the electron
density increases at the top and thereby more light propagation is retarded. The
opposite is the case in the bottom of the solid where the separation of electrons
will decrease the retardation of the propagated light. By this principle a solid with
isotropic optical properties and an isotropic electron density which is exposed to
bending and therefore is being deformed, the mean distance between the atoms in
the top and bottom will deviate from the unstrained state. An example is seen in
fig. 2.4, where the graphics show atoms exposed to uniaxial tension. This effect is
known as the lattice effect.

A constant stress field is created at the surface of the specimen and this stress field
is unaffected by the direction. This gives an advantage in relation to the other test
methods which include the four point bending test. This is due to the flaws on the
surface of the glass which is randomly distributed and with random orientations.
When the glass is subjected to the load and therefore a bending moment, the micro-
cracks will stretch and evolve into larger cracks which eventually will led to failure.
Cracks where the tensile stresses appear perpendicularly to the edge of the cracks
will cause the greatest stress intensity and therefore the orientation is important.
This test is usually used for small specimens where the thickness is between 1 and
9 mm and with side lengths from 66 to 100 mm. If larger specimens need to be
tested, the usual practice is to use gas pressure.
This is because of the larger
deformations that will appear due to the larger dimensions. The larger deformations
will then result in a behaviour which is strongly geometrically non-linear. The gas
pressure will then counteract this behaviour. Even though this is the procedure to
get accurate results, this setup is complex and is vulnerable to application errors.

3.1
Finite Element Method - FEM
A lot of physical issues can be described with partial differential equations.
In
simple cases with simple boundary conditions and geometry these can be solved
analytically. When the geometry and boundary conditions gets more complicated it
is often necessary to solve it numerically. A general method is the Finite Element
Method (FEM) where the problem can be solved by subdividing the system into
smaller and simpler elements. These elements are the result of a mesh of the system.
In each element simple equations are made and these equations are then combined
into a large system of partial differential equations that represents the whole problem.
The FEM software approximates a solution for the system. This thesis takes use of
the commercial FEM-software Abaqus/CAE to compute stresses and deflections of
different glass plates and test setups.

Determination of slopes
In order to show the process of finding the calibrated photoelastic constant fig. 5.3
is shown. In the figure the measured stress and the calculated stress are shown for
different depths. The change in measured stress ∆σmeasured is found as all the mea-
surements subtracted individually from the first measurement which is without any
load. The first measurement will then depict the stresses caused by the tempering
process. By subtracting the other measurements, only the applied stresses are left.
The change in the calculated stresses is found with eq. (2.17). It is then possible
to compare the measured stresses to the theoretically calculated stresses. By insert-
ing this data into a plot, a regression through the points can be made. The first
regression is made where the line is forced through the point (0,0). This is done
because when there is no stress applied the change must be 0. Though this seems
to be the most correct way, another method has been used as well. By applying the
function ”polyfit” in MATLAB it is possible to make a line through the data points
without forcing it through origo. This is done to make a more accurate regression
line. Theoretically speaking, the line should always go through origo, but it is possi-
ble that some inaccuracy in the load cell at low loads gives a bit higher values at the
first load steps. From the regression lines, the slopes are been found. By utilizing
the fact that the measured stresses should be equal to the calculated stresses it is
possible to check whether the photoelastic constant should be higher or lower than
the one used for the calculation of the measured stresses from the retardation. The
photoelastic constant used for the measurements is multiplied with the slope. If
the slope is higher than 1, it yields in a higher photoelastic constant and vice versa.
This is done for every measured point through the thickness of the glass. Depths
from 0-8mm with 1mm spacing are shown in the figure.

6.8.3
Load cell
For the load cell there are two possible sources of error, fluctuations and stress
relaxation, respectively.
Fluctuations
When loading the specimens the measured load with the load cell fluctuated a lot.
It was considered whether there was a defect in the load cell or if other factors were
present. If the load cell had a defect it could have affected all of the conducted exper-
iments. It has later become known that the load cell had problems with calibrating
and doing measurements after this experiment. It is possible that the load cell had
been defect during the experiments, but also that it had occurred after the experi-
ments. This thesis and the results and conclusions will be based on the assumption
that the load cell was fully functional. Another possibility to the fluctuations is the
load rings which were made of rubber. During the experiment the rubber would
deform and slowly adapt and shape after the load. This gave a long waiting time
before it was possible to take a measurement without too much fluctuation.
Stress relaxation
Due to the long waiting time because of the load cell or the rubber rings it is
possible that the glass has been affected by the phenomenon of stress relaxation
where the stress in the specimen will decrease over time. This could have led to
stresses measured to lower magnitudes which have been affected by this.
6.8.4
Change of immersion liquid
Another source of error could be the execution of the experiment. At experiments
done in the past by Nielsen (2009) and Ismaili and Ejlersen (2012) the procedure for
measuring with the SCALP had a slight variation. They had at each load step after
each measurement lifted the SCALP, cleaned the surface of the glass with ethanol
and placed a new drop of immersion liquid. At each load step the SCALP was
also rotated 90◦for measurements at the exact same load. When doing this thesis’
experiment it has not been possible to rotate the SCALP and clean the specimen at
each load step due to the steel cylinder surrounding the SCALP. This is a possible
source of error but it has been assessed that it should only have a little influence on
the final results.

6.9
Discussion
It is clear that some factors have influenced the experiment as the results vary as
much as they do. Even though this must be the case the following discussion is
made with the assumption the all the experiment has been performed correctly and
with equipment living up to the expectations.
Ismaili and Ejlersen (2012) came with the conclusion that for an experiment per-
formed as an uni-axial tension test, a dependency between the photoelastic constant
and the density in the tempered glass was present. A nice curve of the photoelastic
constant could be seen to follow the change in density. In the case of this thesis’ ex-
periment a temperature distribution found in Abaqus and the photoelastic constant
has been found but no apparent correlation could be found. Ismaili and Ejlersen
(2012) did two experiments which yielded in different C-values in the specimen. It
was from 3.02-3.05 in the top, which is relatively close when comparing to the exper-
iments in this thesis with differences of up to 1.26 TPa−1. One of the key differences
between Ismaili and Ejlersen (2012) and this thesis’ experiments is that when using
a uni-axial tension test the applied stress in the middle of the cross-section will not
be zero at any point. This is therefore more accurate than the bending test where
the center will be zero.
Gardon (1978) looked at the change in density through the depth of a glass plate,
where the densities from the surface to the middle were found to: 2506-2513 g/cm3.
This is seen in fig. 1.7. The variation was not found to relate to the density, even
though theoretically speaking a higher density should lead to closer atoms and there-
fore measured retardations and stresses were expected to decrease from the surface
towards the middle.
According to Schwiecker (1957) the photoelastic constant is affected by the compo-
sition of the glass. At 100% SiO2 the photoelastic constant should be 3.3TPa−1. As
the float glass used had a value between 3.25−1 and 3.57−1 it is possible that the
glass in this thesis’ experiments had a composition consisting of 100% SiO2, possibly
with up to 25% B2O3 added or with up to 50% PbO.
As for the other specimens, at least two different compositions are present. First
the specimens with thicknesses from 6-10mm had a greenish tint. The color comes
from iron from the production process. The color difference is seen in fig. 6.9. The
photoelastic constant was found to be between 2.05-3.31TPa−1. Due to this the
composition of the glass could be SiO2 with up to 50% of BaO or 30% of PbO for
the lowest value. The high value of 3.31TPa−1 could be 100% SiO2. The values in
between could be from Na2O or CaO.

table and even extends further in both directions of the scale. For the values in the
table different types of glass is used, from float glass to different degrees of tempering,
as well as chemical strengthened glass. The tempered glass has C-values ranging
from 3.01-3.10TPa−1, where the experimentally found in this thesis ranged from
2.29-3.04TPa−1 within the standard deviation in the top surface. When using these
statistics the C-values are also lower than the expected values of 3.01-3.10TPa−1.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
The work within this thesis is the result of multiple tests on different types and sizes
of glass plates in a co-axial double ring bending test. The experiment was conducted
based on knowledge from standard co-axial tests but the setup was changed in order
to make room for the SCALP measurement instrument. The aim of the thesis was
to investigate the photoelastic constant through the thickness of glass plates and
observe whether it can be assumed to be constant or varying through the depth.
At the reference specimen a small decrease from the top surface to the center was
found. At the tempered specimens the photoelastic constant varied through the
thickness.
In the first couple of mm an increase was present but at around 0.7
of the height the slope changes and the photoelastic constant will decrease to the
center of the specimens. It was not possible to find a clear correlation between the
photoelastic constant and other properties of the glass, but it was suggested that
the composition could be one example of why this experiment deviates form the
theory. When comparing the float glass to the tempered glass a clear difference is
seen in the shape of C through the depth. Float glass is approximately a straight
line and tempered glass is curving.
One of the goals in this thesis was to learn about the experimental procedure under-
lying the SCALP measurements and adjusting the procedure to ensure that measure-
ments were possible. In order to ensure that it was possible to make the necessary
measurements, a setup developed from the standard co-axial double ring bending
test was made. During the experimental phase an ample experience of using the
SCALP instrument and a solid understanding hereof has been achieved. The exper-
iments was done successfully, but unfortunately multiple causes of error appeared
during and after the experiments. One major suspicion was the load cell, which can
have lead to the in some sort inconclusive results. Event though the results did not

If the results had been more consistent a script to correct the SCALP measurements

7.1
Future work
Since the results were not as promising as it would have been expected suggestions
for future work will be described.
First of all the machine used in these experiments works mechanically. This resulted
in severe waiting time for the load cell to stabilise. A suggestion for further work is
therefore to use a hydraulic test machine in order to keep the force on the specimen
at a constant level. In addition to this it is proposed to use more dense rubber rings
since since it also took time for them to compress and decompress at the different
load steps. In addition to this measurements on the tension side of the specimens
could also be done.
In the past experiments with the SCALP cover four point bending tests (Nielsen
(2009)) and uni-axial tension tests (Ismaili and Ejlersen (2012)), and in this thesis
the multi-axial bending has been utilized. It could be interesting to investigate uni-
axial tension, since this will not create the difference in the stress distribution to be
zero in the middle of the cross-section.
Moreover the experiment done could be repeated more times in order to find better
statistical values, and afterwards to make function for stress correction to ensure
more precise measurements, hence closer to the real stresses.

Description of work process 
A CO-axial bending test is performed with two rings. A load ring and a support ring. The load ring 
is applying the force to the specimen creating a field in inside the smallest ring with a constant 
bending moment.  
Process: 
- 
The load cell is mounted to the test machine 
- 
The support cylinder is mounted to the top of the machine 
- 
The load ring is mounted to the bottom of the machine 
- 
Put the glass plate on top of the load ring 
- 
Start the machine and apply the maximum load (up to 50 MPa) 
- 
Unload the specimen 
- 
Clean the glass with ethanol 
- 
Apply a drop of immersion liquid 
- 
The SCALP instrument is mounted so the glass prism makes contact with the immersion 
liquid. 
- 
The specimen is loaded to the maximum load (up to 50 MPa) 
- 
The specimen is unloaded in steps of 10 %. At each step a measurement with the SCALP 
instrument is made. The retardation is recorded.  
- 
The SCALP instrument is turned 90°  
- 
The specimen is unloaded in steps of 10 %. At each step a measurement with the SCALP 
instrument is made. The retardation is recorded.
