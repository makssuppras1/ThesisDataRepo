Abstract
The development of a novel nanostructured electrochemical sensor for the detection of a plant
growth hormone, trans-Zeatin, is described in this study. The biorecognition mechanism was
developed by functionalizing the gold surface with methylene blue-labeled aptamers (MB).
Nanograss (NG) nanostructured electrodes had a large surface area with improved porosity,
allowing more aptamers to bind. The aptamers were immobilized using non-thiolate bonds
through nucleic bases, in this instance a ten-adenine chain (polyA10). This method is simpler
to operate since it does not require any additional steps, such as thiolate bonds, which must
be broken before bonding on the gold surface. Following aptamer immobilization, the biosen-
sor was characterized using the electrochemical technique of Diﬀerential pulse voltammetry,
which revealed a dynamic range of 10 nM to 10 µM of trans-Zeatin. The NG biosensor de-
tected at least 10 nM of trans-Zeatin while being tested for selectivity with the 2-isopentenyl
adenine (2ip) hormone.

Dansk abstrakt
Dette studie beskriver udviklingen af en ny nanostruktureret elektrokemisk sensor som har
til formål at detektere plantevæksthormonet trans-Zeatin. Biogenkendelsesmekanismen blev
udviklet ved at funktionalisere guldoverﬂaden af en elektrode med methylenblåt-mærkede
aptamerer (MB). Elektrodeoverﬂaden er samtidig og struktureret med nanograss (NG) som
giver forbedret porøsitet og større tilgængeligt overﬂadeareal, hvilket tillader immobiliser-
ing af ﬂere aptamerer.
Aptamererne blev fastgjorte ved brug af non-thiol bindinger via
nukleinbaser- i dette tilfælde en kæde bestående af ti adeniner (polyA10). Denne metode
er nem at arbejde med fordi den ikke kræver yderligere trin, såsom tilstedeværelse af thi-
olatbindinger, hvilket ville kræve et yderligere trin for at bryde bindingen, før den bliver
fastgjort på guldoverﬂaden. Efter aptamer immobiliseringen, blev biosensoren karakteriseret
ved brug af den elektrokemiske teknik – Diﬀerentialimpuls voltammetri – som afslørede at
tilstedeværelsen af trans-Zeatin kunne bestemms med en dynamisk rækkevidde mellem 10
nM og 10 µM. Den laveste koncentration trans-Zeatin biosensoren kunne detektere var 10
nM og den blev testet for selektivitet med hormonet 2-isopentanyladenin (2ip).

Chapter 1
Introduction
Since the discovery of cytokinins (CKs) approximately ﬁfty years ago scientists have
focused their interest on their isolation and functionalities.
CKs are described as small
molecules that are being secreted from main metabolic pathways and play a crucial role in
plant root cell division and proliferation while their biological actions diﬀer. Moreover, in the
last two decades, CKs have shown to be signiﬁcantly important for the plant’s growth as they
are acting as regulators and mediators of biotic and abiotic stress. Furthermore, they extend
the plant’s life by intercepting protein degradation while simultaneously stimulating protein
synthesis adjacent to the plant’s tissues. trans-Zeatin (tZ), cis-Zeatin (cZ), and isopentenyl
adenine (iP) are the most prevalent cytokinins produced during plant development and hence
the most relevant for subsequent detection and quantiﬁcation analysis using ultra-sensitive
biosensors [1–7].
Among the cytokinins, trans-Zeatin is one of the most signiﬁcant since it can be ex-
tracted naturally while also being mobile near plant tissues. For the ﬁrst time, zeatin was
discovered in immature maize kernels of the genus Zea. Zeatin is a plant growth hormone
that is produced from the purine adenine and comes in two isomers: cis and trans. More-
over, 6-alkylaminopurines are a class of organic compounds that zeatin is being included and
constituted [8, 9]. It has been observed that trans-Zeatin is one of the phytohormones that
regulates the growth and development of the plants (ﬂower bud diﬀerientation) and promotes
the cell expansion [9, 10]. However, because of its small structure, as can be seen in Figure
1.1, with a molecular weight of 219.24 gmol−1, it is diﬃcult to detect and quantify. Although,
as a result, researchers from all around the world are interested in detecting this molecule us-
ing speciﬁcally electrochemical sort of biosensors. Until today, the literature on determining
the Zeatin using electrochemical techniques such as (Cyclic Voltammetry, Diﬀerential Pulse
Voltammetry and etc) is still fairly limited [11].
Detecting phytohormones can be diﬃcult since most of the times it necessitates the de-
velopment of a bioassay or an immunoassay, which leads to more complex and time-consuming
procedures and methodologies. Sample pretreatment occurs before the detection begins and
it can be challenging enough to achieve high speciﬁcity and sensitivity in the whole system
since electrochemical biosensors functionalized with antibodies, aptamers or peptides may
interact with other hormones during the detection. So far, as mentioned previously, there
is extremely limited scientiﬁc literature focused exclusively on the determination and quan-
tiﬁcation of the phytohormone trans-Zeatin. What makes it more challenging is the small

structure of the molecule that requires preferably any sort of nanostructure in order to be
detected. It is known that nanomaterials enhance the sensing performance of the sensor by
increasing the active surface area, allowing for greater space for antibody, aptamer or peptide
binding. At the same time, nanostructured sensors can be extremely costly and that has as
a result, many research groups are focusing on the development of low-cost and sustainable
nanostructures for determining this kind of small molecules.
Figure 1.1: Chemical Structure of the target trans-Zeatin.
Till now, high performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) [12], liquid chromatogra-
phy mass spectrometry (LC-MS)[13], and liquid chromatography–tandem mass spectrometry
(LC-MS/MS)[14] have all been used to detect Zeatin. These type of approaches, are highly
costly because it usually requires expensive machines while also being time demanding due to
sample preparation and extended running operations. An electrochemical approach, on the
contrary, can deliver fast results in a more cost-eﬀective manner without compromising the
results and, in most cases, without the requirement for sample pretreatment. In addition,
more recent studies have shown that nanostructures can provide an extremely low detec-
tion limit usually in a concentration range of pico-molar using photoelectrochemical methods
(PEC)[15]. Wang et.al, in 2018 has ﬁrst introduced a photoelectrochemical method (PEC)
for the determination of Zeatin using graphene quantum dots on carbon nitride nanosheets
(GQDs/g-C3N4) reaching a low detection limit of 0.031 nM [16].
Zhou et al. similarly presented the ﬁrst simple and sensitive electrochemical method
for detecting Zeatin using MoS2 nanosheets functionalized with aptamers in 2018, with a
detection limit of 16.6 pM [11].
However, nanostructured biosensors functionalized with
antibodies, aptamers or peptides are ﬁnding application not only in the determination of
small molecules such as trans-Zeatin but also in the detection of foodborne pathogens such
as Vibrio parahaemolyticus, since they provide increased sensitivity and selectivity[17].
Aptamers are widely used in the design of electrochemical biosensors because they specif-
ically attach to particular targets such as small molecules, increasing the sensor’s selectivity.
Aptamers are short, single-strained DNA or RNA structures that are incredibly stable and
reusable. Most of the times, aptamers are conjugated with a reporter molecule, which causes
a change in the electrical signal depending on whether or not the target is captured. [18–20].

In recent years, scientists have primarily focused on the development of electrochemical
biosensors for the determination of various phytohormones because electrochemistry oﬀers
simplicity, low cost, and high sensitivity when compared to other methods such as HPLC or
mass spectrometry (MS). Many recent studies have revealed that nanostructured biosensors
perform better in terms of detection limit and sensitivity [21–24].
1.1
Deﬁnition of a Biosensor
According to Thevenot et al.
[25], an electrochemical biosensor is deﬁned a device
that can provide quantitative and semi-quantitative analytical information, as an integrated
device.Electrochemical sensors are favoured over other types of biosensors due to their better
sensitivity and simplicity of use in sensing applications. The electrochemical sensors also
allow for sensor module downsizing, making them appropriate for on-site analysis. Usually,
electrochemical biosensors are using a three electrode system which consist of a working
electrode (WE) where the bioreceptors bind, a reference electrode (RE) which measures the
kinetics of the electrons on the WE and a counter electrode (CE) which is always larger than
the WE because donates electrons to WE when the reaction takes place on it. However, a
detection mechanism it could be any element that works as a bioreceptor for an analyte, such
as DNA, aptamers, enzymes, antibodies and etc. Lastly, all the current changes on the WE
are being recorded by a potentionstat.
1.2
Aim of this project
This project aims to fabricate a novel nanostructured sensor for detection of plant
growth hormones, more particularly trans-Zeatin, using labelled aptamers as biorecognition
elements. The nanostructured electrochemical sensor, a so-called nanograss (NG) sensor,
would be based on a three electrode system which consists of a working electrode (WE),
a reference electrode (RE) and a counter electrode (CE) for fulﬁlling the electrochemical
sensor’s requirements. The next step, is the functionalization of the surface properly for the
detection of the analyte. This would be achieved by binding Zeatin aptamers on the gold
surface. Lastly, after the fabrication and functionalization of the sensor with aptamers were
accomplished in this study, the system would be assessed using the electrochemical technique
of diﬀerential pulse voltammetry.
In Figure 1.2 is presented the novel nanograss (NG) biosensor, which is functionalized
with aptamers coupled with the methylene blue (MB) reporter molecule for the detection
of the trans-Zeatin target.
The NG sensor will be compared to commercial gold screen
printed electrodes (SPAuE), which have previously proved their electrochemical behavior
in the market and have been employed by numerous research groups for many years. As a
result, it was simple to establish the electrochemical behavior of the innovative NG sensors by
comparing their performance, as will be discussed analytically in the next chapters. Finally,
the sensor’s selectivity will be investigated by using other growth plant hormones such as
2ip.

Lithography
Optical lithography (UV lithography) is a photon-based process that begins with a
substrate, such as Silicon, being covered with a photoresist (photosensitive gel) and exposing
a shadow cast of an image or a speciﬁc design. UV lithography typically consists of three
major processes. These phases are the spin coating of the photoresist on the substrate of
interest, the exposure of a speciﬁc pattern under the speciﬁed wavelength, which also speciﬁes
the exposure resolution, and ﬁnally, the development of the photoresist [26, 27].
The spin coating process (Figure 2.2) begins with the deposition of the appropriate pho-
toresist on a substrate that is put on a holder in a spin coating machine. When the substrate
with the applied photoresist is ready, it begins to spin. Through centrifugal forces, the spin-
ning speed determines the thickness of the photoresist on the substrate. A few micrometers of
photoresist are usually suﬃcient to proceed. It is noteworthy to point out at this point that
pretreatment with Bis(trimethylsilyl)amine (also known as HMDS) is necessary for improved
photoresist adherence on the substrate, known as adhesion promotion (soft bake). There the

2.3
Reactive Ion Etching (RIE) Etching
Etching is a technique for patterning the surface of a substrate on a micro or nano scale.
Unfortunately, there are not that many techniques to pattern a surface at the micro/nano
scale, hence plasma is employed. Plasma is now regarded the fourth state of matter and
is produced by heating a gas at a high temperature (4000 to 20000K) until it becomes
completely ionised. Plasma is mostly made up of free-moving particles like positive ions
and electrons. In etching techniques, plasma with an ionization range of 10−6 to 10−4 is
electrically driven rather than created by heat due to apparatus diﬃculties reaching extremely
high temperatures [29, 30].
Figure 2.3: Overview of generating plasma [31].
Generally, researchers seek to etch particular regions of a substrate. To accomplish this,
a lithography process is required ﬁrst to delineate those regions. As a result, the etching
will only occur on the exposed areas. There are two types of dry etching: anisotropic and
isotropic. An anisotropic etching occurs when the gas etches vertically and in all directions,
whereas an isotropic etching occurs when the gas etches evenly in all directions and oﬀer
great uniformity. These factors are critical for achieving the desired layout. Another critical
component in etching is the proper gas selection, because incorrect etching gas selection puts
not only the structure at risk, but also chamber contamination and non-etching surface eﬀect
[29, 30].
Figure 2.4: Overview of dry etching process in Reactive Ion Etching (RIE)[32].

As indicated in the above paragraph, selecting the correct type etching is critical. There
are various prerequisites for selecting the proper process gas.
The ﬁrst are the radicals
produced by the chemical breakdown in the plasma, as this is the only way to carry out
etching without contaminating the substrate’s surface with undesired byproducts. Most of
the time, radicals are not charged at ﬁrst and are permitted to roam (diﬀusion). However,
some radicals are deposited on the surface of the substrate and are adsorbed. Adsorbed
radical particles on the surface begin a reaction and desorb the reaction product from the
surface, leaving an etched spot. As a result, the reaction’s product must always be volatile
at chosen temperature and pressure, and it must also contain atoms from all of the process
gases employed, as well as atoms from the substrate’s surface. It should be noted at this time
that all of these considerations must be completed before beginning the etching, otherwise
the result after etching will not be desirable [29, 30].

The chosen etching characteristics might be the most essential aspect of etching since
they are the factors that deﬁne the ﬁnal outcome, repeatability, and uniformity. To begin,
the ’Etch Rate’ determines the etched material at a certain time in the etching process and
is critical in regards to the amount of wafers that must be etched. Furthermore, the Loading
Eﬀect must be evaluated. The ’Loading Eﬀect’ is dependent on the equal distribution of
radicals throughout the whole surface of the substrate, which maintains homogeneity (limited
ion bombardment). Finally, selectivity is essential for a successful etching procedure. The
ratio of etch rates for the material to be etched vs the remaining material is deﬁned as
selectivity, as shown in Equation 2.2 [33].

The Reactive Ion Etching (RIE), which is a capacitive connected plasma system with

2.4
Thin ﬁlm deposition
Thin ﬁlms are ﬁnding applications in a variety of materials in today’s society. In general,
they are used to improve the physical and chemical characteristics of numerous material sur-
faces. Thin ﬁlms are typically employed in electronics, optical communications, and biosys-
tems, but they are ﬁnding additional uses in industry all the time. Thin ﬁlm is often deﬁned
as a material deposition with a thickness ranging from 1 nanometer to a few micrometers.
A thin ﬁlm can be created in two ways: via growth (the thin ﬁlm grows with the substrate,
perhaps using a thermal growth process), or by usually metal deposition on the substrate’s
surface using a deposition technique such as Atomic Layer Deposition (ALD) or Sputtering.
However, before beginning to grow or deposit a thin layer on a substrate, a few parameters
must be evaluated. Because the growth or deposition of the thin ﬁlm requires appropriate
step coverage which means good surface coverage, the correct method is chosen based on the
application and the surface on the substrate [34–36].
ALD and Sputtering were employed for this work. So because nanograss (black sili-
con) structure is highly irregular and non-uniform, only those procedures could accomplish
adequate step coverage and side wall coverage on the etched region. The medium vacuum
that these processes employ help the ions to travel to any direction into the chamber. The
techniques will be discussed in the sections that follow.
2.4.1
Sputtering - Lesker
Sputtering is a Physical Vapour Deposition (PVD) method. In contrast to other PVD
methods such as E-beam evaporation, it employs a medium vacuum (10−3 to 10−4). Sputter-
ing deposition occurs randomly on the wafer, which results in greater step coverage and side
wall coverage on the substrate’s surface. Sputtering techniques include DC (Direct Current)
sputtering, RF (Radio Frequency) sputtering, Magnetron Sputtering, and others. It was the
DC Sputtering technique that was employed in this study since the nanograss structure is
irregular as stated previously [37].

2.4.2
Atomic Layer Deposition (ALD)
Atomic Layer Deposition (ALD) is a thin ﬁlm growth technology that has evolved
considerably over the last several decades and is classiﬁed as a chemical vapour deposition
(CVD) process. Two of the most signiﬁcant advantages of this approach are the ability to
deposit a thin layer on practically any type of substrate, including 3D structures (such as
nanograss), while also achieving high step coverage with uniformity throughout the whole
surface of the substrate. The ALD technique employs gases or liquids as precursors, which
are injected into the chamber where the precursors adhere to the surface of the substrate,
a process known as chemisorption. When the ﬁrst gas is pumped into the chamber and the
chemicals have been absorbed to saturation on the surface, the purge phase occurs, which
extracts the excessive gas. The second gas is then introduced and binds to the ﬁrst gas
monolayer. This process is known as a ’cycle,’ and it can continue indeﬁnitely until the
desired thickness of the thin layer is reached, as illustrated in Figure 2.8 [38]. It should be
noted at this point that the purge phase, which occurs between the precursors, is performed
using inert gases that will not interfere with the process or the surface of the substrate.
Depending on the process the deposition rate of one cycle is approximately 0.5 to 1 Å/cycle
[35, 38, 39].
Figure 2.8: Overview of one cycle in the atomic layer deposition process [38].
There are various advantages to an ALD process that distinguish it from others. The
self-limiting growth process is one feature, which implies that the thickness of the thin ﬁlm
being deposited is determined only by the number of cycles. As a result, the thickness can be
determined precisely and with high reproducibility. Another key aspect of ALD is the ability
to separate the dosages of each precursor, which ensures that no gas phase reactions occur
between the deposition steps. Last but not least, the temperature window in an ALD process
is often broad. That means that is a cycle the processing conditions of diﬀerent materials are
matching and that gives the privilege for multilayer thin ﬁlm deposition of various materials
[40–42].
However, there are several disadvantages in a method like ALD. Running an ALD

number of substrates. However, a procedure like this uses a lot of energy because it normally

Fabrication Steps
Silicon (Si) wafers were employed for this project’s aims. The wafers were single-sided

UV lithography – First step
UV lithography was the initial step in the fabrication process. In that section, a thin
ﬁlm of photoresist was placed on the surface of the wafer. A positive photoresist, AZ MiR
701, with a thickness of 1.5 m and the HMDS surface modiﬁcation, was employed (Recipe
1411 in Spin Coater: Gamma UV). The photoresist was exposed in a machine called as the

For a positive photoresist, the settings used for optimal exposure of the Mask A (Figure
2.10) on the substrate were λ = 375 nm, Dose = 200 mJ/cm2, and Defocus = -6. With
those parameters, the exposure had 675 stripes and lasted around 9 minutes to complete on
a single wafer’s substrate.
Figure 2.11: Spin Coat of the AZ MiR 701 photoresist (a) and exposure with Aligner: Mask-

Development
When the exposure is completed, the development process begins. The wafers are placed
in a machine called TMAH UV-lithography (Süss MicroTec model Gamma 2M), which dilutes

minute. The ﬁnal stage in the development is to rinse the wafers with DI water to wash away

Oxide passivation
Since gold (Au) is set to be employed as the sensor’s covering material, it is essential
to ensure that there is through current leakage on the Silicon’s substrate. To avoid this, an
insulator thin ﬁlm must be deposited. The ALD 1 machine was used to deposit an Al2O3

the number of cycles determines the eventual thickness of the thin ﬁlm, and each process
requires at least two precursors to operate. In this work, 20 nm of Al2O3 was deposited,

precursors used in that procedure were H2O with a 0.1 second pulse duration and TMA

the chamber has been set at 200 degrees Celsius since the TMA chemical may not be able
to react at temperatures below 150 degrees Celsius, while temperatures beyond 300 degrees
Celsius may entirely destroy the TMA. After the deposition, the thickness of the Al2O3 was
measured with an Ellipsometer. An Ellipsometer is measuring the change in polarization of
the light while the light interacts with the sample’s surface. Thus, the actual thickness of

Aptamer functionalization
This chapter will provide a basic overview of aptamers. In addition, given some exam-
ples, an overview of how aptamers are employed in biosensing will be addressed. Following
that, the methods which were followed for this project’s purposes, will be explained and
analytically described. Finally, the functionalization protocols of the NG and SPAuE sensors
will be given analytically.
3.1
Aptamers in biosensing
From the moment that aptamers have been discovered in 1990, they have met great
applications in the scientiﬁc and industrial world. Aptamers, as previously noted, are short
DNA or RNA sequences [18, 45] .
Because of their functions and structures, aptamers
provide more advantages than disadvantages, and hence more diagnostic devices use them as
detecting mechanisms. Aptamers have a high aﬃnity for the target because they fold when
the target is captured, reducing interference with other irrelevant small molecules. Aptamer-
based sensors, on the other hand, have improved ﬂexibility, which means they are faster to
respond and reusable because aptamers are rather stable structures by their own [46–48].
Aptamers outperform antibodies in terms of stability at room temperature while at the same
time are being easier to be modiﬁed and that is also a reason for are being used as target
recognition structures [49, 50].
However, it should be mentioned that working with aptamers can sometimes be more
challenging than expected. In that case, one of the disadvantages that aptamers have is the
negative signal. It means, for instance, when the target is being captured by the aptamer
and it folds, then the signal response is decreasing. According to this work but also to some
recent publications where aptamer-based sensors were developed, the same signal behavior
was observed. Where the signal was decreasing instead of increasing when the analyte was
binded [50, 51].
For many years, nevertheless, aptamer immobilization on an Au surface was accom-
plished through thiol bonding (S-Au). However, in recent years, attention has been drawn
to the use of nucleotide bases, speciﬁcally Adenine [52–54]. According to a publication, the
Adenine base shows signiﬁcant binding to the Au surface at ambient temperature [55]. Fur-
thermore, the use of several adenine bases (polyA) enhances the spacing between aptamers
27

on the Au surface which reducing the possibility of aptamer-aptamer interaction, while also
providing excellent stability and adsorption [56]. However, one of the most signiﬁcant ad-
vantages of using a polyA chain is that it takes less time to bind to the surface due to low
pH, reducing the need for overnight incubation in most cases. Furthermore, their low cost is
critical in their use, as the development of a biosensor may be rather costly [52].

3.2.1
Aptamer immobilisation process
The heat shock occurs after the aptamer solution has been prepared to the required
concentration. The heated water at 93 degrees Celsius (◦C) in water-bath allows the aptamers
to denature and unfold to their maximum length before attaching to the gold surface. The
aptamer dilutions were placed in a warm bath for 10 minutes before being immediately
immersed to a cold bath for another 4 to 5 minutes in order to maintain their unfolded
shape. The aptamers were then deposited on the sensor’s active surface, as shown in Figure
3.1. As a result, 6 µl of 1000 nM of aptamer solution is deposited on the working electrode of
both the NG and SPAu sensors. The electrodes are incubated overnight at room temperature.
Figure 3.2: The heat-shock procedure to properly fold the aptamers.
The electrodes are thoroughly washed the next day with milli-Q water to ﬂush out any
aptamers that did not bind on the surface. After washing, the electrodes with the immobilized
aptamers on top were kept at room temperature before use. It is important to mention at this
point that when the electrodes are functionalised with aptamers they are preserved better in
a moist environment.

Working principle of labeled aptasensor
During the years diﬀerent sort of biosensors have been developed to fulﬁl the market’s
demands. In this work a MB-labeled biosensor was developed since they are using aptamers
as a detection mechanism which makes them very selective and sensitive. A labeled biosensor
employs aptamers as a biorecognition element and they are conjugated with a mediator label,
most of the time methylene blue (MB), for signal generation and ampliﬁcation [50].
When measuring with a labeled biosensor the initial signal is being increased since the
MB is quite close on the electrodes surface but when the analyte is captured by the aptamer,
then it changes the MB position further away from the surface and that cause the decrease
in the current signal when measuring. This phenomena will be discussed in the following
chapter, where the electrochemical analysis will be given.

4.1
Diﬀerential pulse voltametry (DPV)
Diﬀerential pulse voltammetry (DPV) is a potentiostatic method which is extensively
used in electrochemistry. Diﬀerential pulse voltammetry comes with several advantages over
other electrochemical techniques, such as Cyclic Voltammetry (CV), and generally other
sweep linear methods. An important advantage that DPV provides is the low capacitive
current. At this point it should be mentioned that when the DPV is performed also DC
current can be observed.
The recorded signal that is being produced from the DPV is
actually the diﬀerence between two signals. The ﬁrst signal is the voltage just before the
pulse begins and the second signal is the voltage after the pulse has been applied [58–60].
Pulse techniques are ﬁnding great application since they can provide higher sensitivity
than other electrochemical techniques. Most of the times pulse methods are being used for
quantitative determination of small molecules. In addition, they are easy to handle, since
they only require a few parameters to be considered, with most important one, the amplitude
potential which regulates the voltage of the pulse. However, when DPV is performed and set
to a speciﬁed range of potential, it can aid in decreasing the measurement of the background
current, which is important because it provides a better insight of what is actually happening
on the WE during the measurement [58–60].

Finally, one of the most essential tests of the NG aptasensor was to investigate at its

Chapter 5
Conclusions
A novel nanograss sensor functionalized with aptamers for the determination of the
growth plant hormone, trans-Zeatin, is reported in this study. When the analyte is captured
or not, the suggested biorecognition process is based on the current change of the labeled
aptamers with methylene blue. The non-thiolated technique for immobilizing aptamers is
less time consuming and less expensive. The variation between the electrodes in the NG
sensor fabrication process was lower than in conventional screen-printed electrodes, making
the procedure reproducible. The NG sensor’s surface was highly porous, and the spike-nature
of the electrode’s surface resulted in increased surface area, which allowed more aptamers to
bind. The NGE sensor outperformed the SPAuE sensor in a greater dynamic range of 10 nM
to 10 µM, with a LOD of 10 nM. Last but not least, after the interference test with the 2ip
hormone, the NGE sensor demonstrated high selectivity to trans-Zeatin hormone. Finally,
this sensor has the potential to be employed for real-time measurements of trans-Zeatin
determination.
5.1
Future work
This study requires optimization. Several factors must be evaluated. To begin, changing
the thickness of the deposited Au might improve the overall performance of the NGE sensor.
Only 20 nm and 40 nm were tried in this project, with the 20 nm Au thin ﬁlm peeling oﬀafter
one electrochemical reading, suggesting that increasing the thickness might be more eﬀective.
During the NGE manufacturing process, it was discovered that the pressure in the etching
phase had a signiﬁcant impact on the ﬁnal formation of the nanograss. Diﬀerent pressures
were examined, and it was observed that when the pressure in the chamber is high, short,
dense nanograss is formed. As a result, optimizing the pressure might enhance the surface
structure. Furthermore, calibration of the NGE and SPAuE must be done in triplicate for
more precise data. Finally, in addition to the 2ip, the sensor’s selectivity would need to be
evaluated with additional hormones.

2. UV lithography (defining nanograss areas) 
3. DRIE etching (etching out nanograss) 
4. Oxide growth (Isolating silicon) 
5. Metal deposition (Electrodes)  
6. Cutting chips
