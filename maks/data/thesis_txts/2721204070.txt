Abstract
This thesis was initiated with Helios Power Trading A/S to explore tools to forecast the spot
price in France. The spot price in this context refers to those of the day­ahead market.
Here, the price of power for every hour the following day is determined by matching supply
and demand. The thesis uses the constraints that come with the day­to­day power market
as a trader, meaning that a 24­hour horizon will be used for every forecast.
First, a foundation was built to understand the dynamics of the power market and the
characteristics and limitations of working with financial data and time series. Then several
different pricing models were explored and used to model the day­ahead electricity prices
on a 24­hour basis.
The forecasting models explored in this thesis are seasonal ARIMA models, seasonal
Lucia­Schwartz­inspired models, deep neural networks in the form of LSTM, gradient
boosting, and finally, combined forecasts where several of the previously created pre­
dictions are combined to mitigate the errors. To examine the performance of the models,
a period of 60 days of hourly spot prices was used to evaluate the fit of the forecasts.
Two models had the best performance on the test set, which consisted of 60 days of hourly
data. The gradient boosting with the previous day’s hourly prices, the hour’s consumption,
and the hour’s residual as input, produced the best MAE score, while a combined forecast
created from two SARIMA models and the previous week’s prices for the same day, had
the best RMSE and daily MAE score. This indicated that the gradient boosting had a few
larger errors than the combined model. The combined model came closer to the overall
daily mean of the prices. The difference in the score of the two models is however small
and more test data is needed to give a more definite conclusion.
Both the seasonal Lucia­Schwartz­inspired model and the SARIMA models improved per­
formance when the previous day’s price at the same hour was used as input and a high
correlation was found with a given day’s and the previous day’s price. For the mixed
models that also took explanatory variables as input, the residual load improved the per­
formance more than other explanatory variables.

1
Background
Knowing what the future, even just the near future, might look like has always given a com­
petitive advantage. In power trading, it can be used in cross­border trading to buy energy
from one country and sell it to a neighboring country to earn a profit if the future price
in both countries can be predicted. Here spot price forecasts are particularly useful for
trading power across interconnected European countries, such as France and Germany.
The traders will look at multiple spot price forecasts for the various interconnected Euro­
pean countries in order to determine if one country will have a lower clearing price at the
spot market for a specific hour compared to a connected country. If that is the case the
trader will submit a bid for the given hour and if the speculation is correct, the payoff for
the trader will be the spread between the two countries’ spot prices, minus the capacity
costs to transport the power from one country to the other.
1.1
Forecasting
When making a forecast the goal is to estimate future events. The predictability of an
event depends on several factors. Understanding which factors contribute to the outcome
and having the needed data available is important to make a good forecast. The task of
forecasting will be made easier the more similar the future is to the past (Hyndman and
Athanasopoulos 2018). There are different approaches to making a forecast. The model
to be used in forecasting depends on the resources and data available, the accuracy of the
competing models, and the way in which the forecasting model is to be used (Hyndman
and Athanasopoulos 2018).
A simple time series forecasting method uses historical data to predict the future without
including explanatory variables. Trends and seasonal patterns will be extrapolated from
the data. An explanatory model helps explain what causes variation in the electricity
prices through explanatory variables only, also called predictor variables. This could be
information such as calendar data, like the date, and day information like a working day,
weekday, or time of the day (Christen et al. 2020). Historic variables of the data that will
be attempted to be predicted will be used in the model. A third type of model combines the
features of the two above­me mentioned models. This mixed model combines historical
values with explanatory variables. The variables used in the model will in general not be
able to account for all changes found in the electricity demand. Therefore an error term is
often added to the models, which allows for random variation and the effects of relevant
variables that are not included in the model.
Using an explanatory model or mixed model requires the system to be well understood.
But even if it is well understood, it can be extremely difficult to measure the relationship
that is assumed to affect the behavior of the electricity price. Furthermore, when incor­
porating more information about the future, it is needed to also make a forecast of those
explanatory variables, which might also prove to be difficult. It is a good approach if the
goal is not only to know what happens but also why it happens, but a simple time series
model may give more accurate forecasts than an explanatory or mixed model (Hyndman
and Athanasopoulos 2018).
There are at least two kinds of information required to make a good forecast: Statisti­
cal data and the accumulated expertise of the people who collect the data and use the
forecasts. It will often be difficult to obtain enough historical data to be able to fit a good

1.2
The power market
The electricity market is a system for effecting the purchase and sale of electricity using
supply and demand to set the price. The premise for this market is that electrical energy
can be treated as a commodity. A commodity is defined as a basic good used in com­
merce that is interchangeable with other goods of the same type (Fernando 2022). There
are however fundamental differences between electrical energy and other commodities.
The most important difference is its very limited storability and transportability (Lucia and
Schwartz 2000). It needs to be produced at the same time as it is consumed since it
currently does not make sense to buy electricity cheaply for one delivery period and at­
tempt to sell it expensively for a later delivery period as it cannot be stored without too
great expenses. This means that supply and demand ­ generation and load ­ need to be
balanced on a second­by­second basis. This causes rapid cyclical variations in the cost
and price of electricity.
In the power market, there can be periods with regular price activity followed by spikes.
This makes electricity a volatile asset class. Especially meteorological events and sea­
sonality have a high effect compared to other financial markets due to the production of
renewable energy and the need for cooling or heating (Huurman, Ravazzolo, and Zhou
2012). But the market for other energy commodities such as gas and fuel also has an
effect on the price as can be seen in figure 1.1.
1.2.1
The importance of matching supply and demand
Europe’s synchronous grid works on alternating current (AC). One of the benefits of this
system is that it makes it easy to transform the voltage. The frequency in this case refers
to the number of times the current changes direction each second. This is measured in
hertz.
Electricity networks need to have supply and demand matched on a continual basis.
Power can currently not be stored at a large scale in the electricity system. To keep the
power grids in Europe running stable at an average frequency of 50 hertz, the demand
for power and the amount of electricity that is being fed into the system, have to be kept
equal. The power grid can handle small fluctuations as long as these deviations are no
more than 0.2 hertz above or below the target. Straying too far from this tempo can lead
to electricity outages, even blackouts.
This balance has to be achieved by closely matching supply and demand – which the
power market has to deliver, apart from its other purpose of shaping the trading price for
electricity. Here Transmission System Operators (TSOs), who are the entities entrusted
with transporting energy on a national or regional level, need to estimate the consumption
patterns of consumers in order to match the production schedules.
The fact that the grid has a frequency of precisely 50 hertz (i.e. that the current changes
direction 50 times a second) is rooted in history rather than technical necessity. In the
USA, for instance, they work with 60 hertz. But once this ‘pulse rate’ is set, every power
plant, transmission line, electrical plant, and device has to work to the same rhythm.

1.2.2
Meeting demand
The power demand can be divided into base load and peak load (Harris 2011). Base load
refers to those where the power is supplied overall 24 hours. The energy comes from
plants that can generate dependable power at a low price to fulfill the minimum level of
demand. The peak load market fulfills the remainder of demand that cannot be met by
the base load market and is only supplied for a fraction of the day. The definition of when
peak load varies from country to country. A commonly accepted convention for ”on­peak”
energy is 8 a.m. to 8 p.m. whereas the rest of the time is ”off­peak”. The generation
supplied by peak load plants can be turned up or down quickly to meet the exact demand.
Base load plants include nuclear power plants and coal power plants due to their low­cost
fuel and steady­state power they can produce. It can also be hydro­power and geothermal
power if the resources are regionally available.
Peak load plants include gas plants, solar power plants, and wind turbines. Those are
more unreliable or more expensive than the base load plants and will be the ones that
can affect the price of energy when the demand cannot be met by the base load plants
as can be seen in figure 1.1.
1.2.3
Selling electricity
Quantities of electrical energy in financial markets are usually denominated in MWh since
electricity contracts usually are delivered for a certain number of megawatt (MW) for a
certain number of delivery hours (h), at prearranged times (Clark 2014).
In the electricity exchange, supply and demand determine the final price. The market­
clearing price is the lowest bid that is still accepted in the auction. The power plant that has
the highest marginal cost determines the price for all the power plants that sell electricity,
as illustrated in figure 1.1. This means that the pricing is uniform. Therefore it is also
more attractive for consumers to buy cheaper energy such as energy from renewable
power plants. This possibility is however highly dependent on weather conditions. The
electricity demand that renewable energy sources cannot cover, defined as the residual
load, must be provided by conventional power plants. This will later often be referred to
as residuals.

During peak hours the demand for electricity pushes up the bidding prices for electric­
ity, which results in higher prices unless a high quantity of cheap renewable energy is
available. But during periods with low demand, such as weekends, holidays, or night­
time, negative prices can occur. This is due to the need of matching supply and demand,
which means that the energy produced needs to be consumed. It can be more expensive
for the producer to stop and then restart the power plant, than sell for a negative price for
a shorter period. This is especially true when wind and solar power cover a large share
of the demand.
Electricity is traded several times from the first producer sale to ultimate consumer deliv­
ery. Some electricity companies sell the electricity from their power plants several years in
advance at a fixed price through a financial agreement like Power Purchase Agreement.
The company’s future production of electricity is known so by fixing the price future, price
risks can be minimized. The buyers can then secure a guaranteed energy supply at a
predictable price. This allows both sides of the transaction to accurately estimate their
revenues or costs two to three years ahead, giving them a high degree of certainty. Such
transactions are usually made on energy exchanges. To secure buyers and sellers, in
the event that one of the two is no longer able to fulfill their obligations, the deposit of
cash collaterals ­ initial margin or variation margin ­ is stipulated by government regula­
tions. These deposits are returned in full as soon as the transaction has been settled as
planned.
In spot trading, there are two different markets according to the lead times when the elec­
tricity is meant to be delivered. In the day­ahead market, contracts are swapped which
have a delivery or acceptance date one day after the business transaction is done. In
the intra­day market, electricity is traded very short­term. It can be traded 15­45 minutes
before the delivery at a certain hour or quarterly­hour, where more variables are known
which makes real­time production and thereby the price easier to predict. The spot market
is an essential tool to balance the grid. Day­ahead prices differ significantly from the cor­
responding real­time price. This difference is due to misestimations made before traders
know all the details of the real­time conditions (Stoft 2022). The purpose of the intra­day
market is to correct the prediction errors of the past.
The power prices are determined through a closed auction that is conducted once a day.
The final agreements are spot contracts. At the auction for the day­ahead market, the
buyers and sellers have until 12 CET to submit their bids for each of the delivery hours
the next day. The orders for each of the 24 separate auctions are registered in an order
book. Then the orders are matched and the market price is calculated. The final hourly
clearing prices are typically announced at 12.45 CET. Since the trades result from a large,
open, and transparent competition between the orders of the exchange members, they
reflect the best information available at the time under the market conditions (EPEX SPOT:
Basics of the Power Market 2023).
1.2.4
Influences on price
While contracts have a more stable price, the spot electricity price has higher volatility.
The spot electricity price is the outcome of a vast number of variables including fundamen­
tals (like loads and network constraints) but also unquantifiable psycho­ and sociological
factors that can cause an unexpected and irrational buyout of certain contracts leading
to price spikes (Weron and Misiorek 2006). Spikes in the electricity prices can also be
due to positive demand shocks or negative supply shocks (e.g. catastrophic equipment
failure) causing auxiliary power plants with very high marginal costs of production need to
be brought into the intra­day market. Prices are strongly dependent on electricity needs

(demand) and their determinants at every precise moment. The determinants can be
business activities, meteorological conditions, or temporal weather conditions together
with their derived uses or production of electricity. Temperature variations have an espe­
cially high effect on the demand during the winter months due to the need for heating while
the effect is less significant but still there in the summer due to the use of air conditioning.
In France, in winter variation of 1◦C corresponds to a variation of consumption of 2500
MW due to the widespread use of electric heating, and in summer about 400 MW due to
the use of air conditioning (RTE Electricity Report 2020: Consumption 2023). Since wind
energy and solar energy have a very low marginal cost, they will drive up supply during
peak hours at a lower cost and drive down the overall spot price. On the flip side, droughts
or low wind speed can put upward pressure on other energy sources which drives up the
price.
1.2.5
Delivery
Retail electricity prices are usually highest for residential and commercial consumers be­
cause it costs more to distribute electricity to them. Industrial consumers use more elec­
tricity and can receive it at higher voltages, so supplying electricity to these customers is
more efficient and less expensive. The retail price of electricity to industrial customers is
generally close to the wholesale price of electricity.
It is infeasible to route transmission networks across oceans due to the loss of energy
related to transporting it over large distances. Thus electricity markets tend to be more
regional compared to other energy markets. Because of these transportation constraints
for electricity, electricity contracts and prices are highly local, i.e. strongly dependent
on the local determinants of supply and demand. The price are however still influences
by cross­border trading. Transportation is a natural monopoly and hence is subject to
regulated prices. In Europe, the power transmission is coordinated by European Network
of Transmission System Operators for Electricity (ENTSO­E).

1.3
Time series
A stochastic process is an indexed family of random variables {Xt}t∈I such that for all
t ∈I, the random variable Xt is defined on some given probability space. The family of
indexes I can be discrete or continuous, e.g. Z, R respectively.
A time series represents one observation of the stochastic process. From a standard sta­
tistical point of view, a realization of a stochastic process, i.e. time series data, represents
only one sample. To make a meaningful statistical analysis, it is necessary to assume that
the observed part of the realization is typical for the time series as a whole. This idea is
related to the concept of stationarity.
Time series can have different forms of non­stationarities and non­normality which make
time series forecasting and evaluation a more difficult problem in comparison to other
prediction tasks. The problematics includes (Hewamalage, Ackermann, and Bergmeir
2022):
• Non­stationarities
– Seasonality
– Trends
– Stochastic trends/unit roots
– Heteroscedasticity
– Structural breaks (jumps or spikes)
• Non­normality
– Non­symmetric distributions
– Fat tails
– Intermittency
– Outliers
• Series with a very short history
Let {xt} be a time series. A time series is (weakly) stationary if the following conditions
hold:
1. E[xt] is constant for all t.
2. Var[xt] < ∞for all t.
3. γ(t, s) = γ(t + r, s + r), ∀r.
Given xt is a stationary time series, the auto­covariance function does not depend on
the points in time t and s but only on the number of periods t and s are apart from each
other, i.e. t −s. Strong stationarity is defined as the distribution of a finite window (sub­
sequence) of a time series (discrete­time stochastic process) remaining the same as the
window is shifted across time (Cox and Miller 1965).
Assessing the stationarity of a time series can be a bit more complex. Neither seasonality
nor trend in a time series does not have a formal definition (Hewamalage, Ackermann,
and Bergmeir 2022). Seasonality usually means that the mean of the series changes
periodically over time, with a fixed length periodicity, and hence depends on time. A trend
6
A survey on electricity price forecasting
==PAGE:19==
stationary time series does not have a mean that is constantly increasing or decreasing
with time.
A trend can both be deterministic, where it is the change of the mean of the time series,
or a stochastic trend, which is due to a unit root. Both kinds of trends change the mean
and the variance of a time series (Salles, Ogasawara, and González Silva 2021). If a time
series has a unit root, there are no reversions to a historical level and the time series acts
as a random walk, wandering in any direction.
Heteroscedasticity is when the variance of a time series is not constant. Heteroscedas­
ticity arises from environmental changes that make the volatility of time series increase or
decrease over time (Hanssens, Parsons, and Schultz 2003).
A structural break is an abrupt shift in a time series.
1.3.1
Manipulating the data
When working with time series it is important to make sure that data leakage does not
happen. Data leakage can happen if the training set and the test set have an overlap or
if the test set is in between periods of the training set so the model ”already knows the
future” that comes after the test set. Data leakage can happen by performing smoothing,
decomposition (mode decomposition), normalization, etc. over the whole series before
partitioning for training and testing (Hewamalage, Ackermann, and Bergmeir 2022). Oth­
erwise, if the data is normalized by calculating the minimum and maximum values for the
entire dataset before using these values to scale the data and afterward split the data
into training and test set, the training dataset knows something about the data in the test
dataset; they have been scaled by the global minimum and maximum values, so they
know more about the global distribution of the variable then they should. It is therefore
important to split the data before any manipulation that depends on the properties of the
data happens.

Unit root
To figure out if a time series is best modeled as stationary or non­stationary, it is useful
to check if it has a unit root. A unit root, also called a unit root process or a difference
stationary process, is a stochastic trend in a time series, sometimes called a random walk
with drift. If a time series has a unit root, it means that it is not stationary and therefore
some transformation is needed, in order to remove the unit root from the time series,
before it can be modeled using AR, MA, or ARMA.

are the KPSS test (Shin et al. 1992) and the ADF test (Dickey and Fuller 1979). A major
difference between KPSS and ADF tests is the capability of the KPSS test to check for
stationarity in the presence of a deterministic trend.
For the KPSS test the null hypothesis is that the data is stationary and the H0 is attempted
to be disproven.
For the ADF the null hypothesis is that the data is not stationary or that it has a unit root.

2.4
Long Short­Term Memory (LSTM)
Neural networks are inspired by the neurons and connections of the human brain. It is
able to recognize underlying relationships in data in order to learn and model the rela­
tionships between input and output data that are nonlinear and complex. There are many
variations of neural networks. The general form is having an input layer, a number of
hidden layers, and an output layer. The strength of neural networks is their activation
functions and multiple connections through a number of hidden layers which can form the
complex relations between the input and desired output.
LSTM is a variant of a recurrent neural network (RNN) that has a structure with an added
long­term memory. An example of the network structure can be seen in figure 2.1. It
was first introduced in 1995 and the final paper was published in 1997 (Hochreiter and
Schmidhuber 1997). It can deal with long sequences of related data, such as an image
or long text, while still being able to detect long­term dependencies across the data.
Figure 2.1: From (What’s the difference between ”hidden” and ”output” in PyTorch LSTM?
2018)
In a vanilla RNN the input is processed sequentially. Given a time series, the input at time
t will together with the input data, xt, also include the hidden state, from the previous time
step, Ht−1, which can be referred to as the short­term memory. The problem with RNN
arises for the back­propagation during training of the network. Since it requires continuous
matrix multiplications due to the chain rule, the gradients will either shrink exponentially
and thereby vanish, or blow up exponentially which results in exploding gradients. This
either causes the weights of the model not to be updated and no learning occurs, or the
model will end up being unstable. This problem is referred to as the vanishing gradient
problem
In a LSTM network an additional input is added at time t ­ the long­term memory, also
known as the cell state. The gates are trained to filter out the relevant information from
the cell state at each time step and make sure the needed information can flow through

2.5
Gradient Boosting
Gradient boosting machine (Friedman 2001) relies on the intuition that the best possible
next model, when combined with previous models, minimizes the overall prediction error.
Boosting methods iteratively combine weak learners in order to build a strong learner
with higher accuracy. The gradient refers to the error or the residuals of the model. The
goal of gradient boosting, is to gradually reduce the errors of the model. Decision trees,
often decisions stumps, meaning that they only have a single split, are here used as weak
learners. It was originally designed for solving classification problems but has now been
adapted to also be able to solve regression problems.
2.5.1
Decision Trees
Decision tree learning is a supervised learning approach that can be used for classifica­
tion or regression. Trees used for regression and trees used for classification have some
similarities – but also some differences, such as the procedure used to determine where
to split (Breiman et al. 1984). To get a basic understanding of decision trees a simple
example is used. The basic and most common way of constructing decision trees is a
top­down recursive tree induction strategy which has the characteristics of a greedy pro­
cess (Yannakakis and Togelius 2018), where the split chosen is that which will minimize
the error of the predictions. For a binary decision tree that predicts accommodation pref­
erences, a split could be if the budget is above a certain level denoted by θ1, given the
budget is an input feature denoted by x1. This condition is then used to create the first
split given by x1 > θ1. After the first split, if the tree is not a stump with depth=1, then
another condition is added to the two new branches as illustrated in figure 2.3a. New
branches will be added until the desired depth of the tree is reached.

4.4
Long Short­Term Memory (LSTM)
The network is created using the PyTorch library (Paszke et al. 2019). To calculate the
error of the model the Mean Squared Error (MSE) are used, which essentially is the RMSE
without taking the square root. This is together with the Adam optimizer (Kingma and Ba
2015) used to update the parameters of the model during training and fine­tuning.
Since the training data to a certain extent have different properties than the test data and
overfit wants to be avoided, a high dropout will be used. It is in general recommended to
keep it below 50%. Therefore the dropout was set to 35% which means that 35% of the
layers will be deactivated and not be used nor updated when a batch is being fed to the
network during training.
The weights in the initial network are randomly set but made sure they are reproducible
by setting a seed in PyTorch (Paszke et al. 2019).
Before the price data is fed to the LSTM model it is scaled using the standard scaler given
in equation 1.6. This is done since the output of the model is limited by the range that it
was been trained on and feeding in scaled data shrinks the difference between the training
data and the test data. After scaling the minimum of the training data is −1, 8586 and the
maximum is 5, 6254. Meanwhile, the scaled test data has a minimum and a maximum of
0, 4693 and 7, 4371 respectively.
Various inputs to the LSTM model will be applied
• Input:
– Price (Pd−k−1,t=0, ..., Pd−1,t=23) with k ∈[1, 2, 3]
– Day of the week w ∈[1 : 7]
– If it is a holiday h ∈[0, 1]
– Consumption (Cd−k,t=0, ..., Cd,t=23) with k ∈[1, 2]
– Residuals (Rd−k,t=0, ..., Rd,t=23) with k ∈[1, 2]
• Output: ( ˆPd,t=0, ..., ˆPd,t=23)
Given the input and the output the structure of the model is many­to­many. The con­
sumption and residual are likewise scaled using the standard scaler from equation 1.6.
The training set is used to estimate the parameters of the scaler to avoid data leakage.
The input size of the model is given by
input_size = (k −f) × e + f
(4.19)
where k is the number of days of data, and e is the number of hourly variables included
in the model given by P, C, and R. f is the number of additional information given by w
and h.
The seven last days of the training data are removed from the training set and used as a
validation set.
The training set has a batch size of 60 which gives 12­13 iterations per epoch run depend­
ing on which dataset is used. The training data will be shuffled after each epoch, which
will result in different updates to the network.
After 1000 epochs the validation will every 100 epochs evaluate the network to see if the
fit of the validation data still improves. If the validation results do not show improvements

compared to the two earlier validations the training of the model will be terminated. To
predict ( ˆPd,t=0), ..., ˆPd,t=23) the 30 days prior given by (Pd−30,0, ..., Pd−1,23) are used to
finetune the full network before a prediction is made and training set and test set is shifted

learned during training.
The first network is created with a hidden size of 64 and 5 layers. This is only used

day(s) denoted by (Pd−1−k,t=0, ..., Pd−1,t=23) are being used as input. As can be seen from
equation 4.19, for every additional hourly information parameter added, the input size
naturally increases. Therefore, to accommodate more complex relations, the network
used when residuals and consumption are added to the input, the second network will
have a hidden size of 128 and have 6 layers.

4.6
Forecast combinations
Building on the same idea as Gradient Boosting was based on, the results from the dif­
ferent model will be combined. In general, although exceptions exist, combining different
models has proven to lead to a higher accuracy since it averages out unsystematic errors,
although not systematic biases (Armstrong 2001).
When combining different models it is important to employ diverse forecasts that have
used different techniques or data to to generate the predictions (Lago et al. 2021). Com­
binations or ensembles of forecasts with equal weights have been shown to perform well
(Armstrong 2001). The accuracy of a mathematically aggregated forecast is a function of
the accuracy of the individual forecasts and the correlations among their errors (Maines
1990). It is therefore important that the correlations among forecast errors is as low as
possible
In order to choose which forecasts to combine the Pearson correlation coefficient will be
used. The Pearson correlation is a measure of linear correlation between two sets of data.
It is given by
ρX,Y = cov(X, Y )
σXσY
(4.20)
The forecast will be combined by taking the mean of the output and thereby ensuring
equal weights to all the forecasts. This means that if three forecasts are combined the
accumulated results of those will be divided by three. To ease the notation it will be
denoted by the sum.
4.6.1
Selecting combinations
To find out which models to combine to achieve the best forecast, the Pearson correlations
between the different residuals of the various models given by Pd,t −ˆPd,t is used.
Since SARIMA(0, 1, 0)×(0, 1, 3)24 produced the best results, except for the gradient boost­
ing, this will be used as the first starting point. The model has an almost perfect correlation
with the similar SARIMA models with Q > 0, where all are above 99% correlation in the
residuals. The lowest correlation was found with the naïve forecast given by ˆPd,t = Pd−7,t
with a correlation of 0, 234641.
Therefore the first forecast combination is to combine SARIMA(0, 1, 0)×(0, 1, 3)24 and the
previous week naïve forecast. They will be combined by taking the mean of the two out­
puts. To improve it further the residual correlation is again assessed but with the models
using other approaches to forecasting. The gradient boosting trained on Data 1 with set­
ting (3) has a correlation of 0, 648433, which is higher than would be preferred. Combining
those forecasts gives a slight improvement to the results, but significantly added to the
complexity of the model.
Lastly to add a LSTM model it was found that the lowest residual correlation with the

this model to the combined model ended up making the forecast slightly worse.
It can also be beneficial to add a model trained on new information such as the residuals

5
Discussion
LSTM did not respond well to the volatility of the prices, which could be due to a too­high
complexity of the model. The training data used is relatively small compared to the stan­
dard training data size used for training neural networks. This results in the network either
being overfitted to the training data or did not gain enough insight into the underlying struc­
ture of the data. Therefore models with lower complexity such as the gradient boosting
performed better, in particular with even smaller training data. If more training data was
obtainable it could be possible that the flexibility of the LSTM would prove to work well on
historical data and manage to capture the underlying pattern of the price data better.
When comparing the LSTM score with other models it can be seen that the RMSE score
is better relative to the MAE score. This is a result of the error function of the model being
given by the MSE.
The seasonal­Lucia­Schwartz­inspired model assumed that the future would look similar
to the past and revolve around an overall mean. As could be seen from the assessment
of the data in section 3.1.2 the properties of the price data have changed significantly
over the last few years and the data did not exhibit a static behavior. Therefore more
parameters were added to the model in an attempt to better capture the pattern of the
price. The performance of the model improved with a term to compensate for the previous
day’s predictions made by the deterministic part of the model by assuming the error made
by a given day’s predictions would be similar the following day. But the model failed to
capture any systematic pattern and explain forward prices. Incorporating the OU process
as the non­deterministic part of the model significantly worsened the performance since
the entire model then assumed a systematic pattern and fixed volatility. Giving too much
weight to the OU process made the model performance even worse because of the high
volatility of the process. Therefore, the κ parameter ended up giving close to zero weight
to this term which made it almost redundant.
The best results were produced by the gradient boosting and the combined forecast,
where gradient boosting in its essence is a version of combined forecasting. The gradient
boosting is able to model non­linear relationships in the data and has less complexity than
the LSTM which means that it can perform well on less training data. The model is able to
take in explanatory variables such as consumption and residuals, which for the test data
made the model performs better. The gradient boosting algorithm is also quick to train and
does not require a lot of resources, which means that training it from scratch every day
can help it adapt fast to the changing pattern of the data. Choosing an appropriate time
frame for the training data is a variable parameter that someone with domain knowledge
should re­evaluate periodically.
Since financial data is highly dependent on outside factors and due to the changing con­
ditions of the power market, the models that perform better on the current data might not
be able to capture the patterns as well if the volatility change. But having a model that can
work with explanatory variables might prove to be more flexible. The influence of outside
factors on power prices can change throughout the years. But continuously fine­tuning or
re­estimating the models takes this variation into account and produces better results.

6
Conclusion
The objective of this thesis was to gain insight into the world of power traders and potential
approaches to forecasting power prices in the day­ahead market.
First, to motivate the case, the characteristics of the power market were explored. It was
found that the price depends on the highest price of the available supply. If renewable en­
ergy sources had a high production it would help bring down the price. If the demand was
high more expensive energy sources would have to supply the energy which increases
the price.
Afterward, the historical spot prices of France were inspected. It was found that the price
data exhibited a range of non­stationarities including seasonality, trends, having a unit
root, and structural breaks, and non­normality such as non­symmetric distributions, fat
tails, and outliers. Furthermore, since it was found that the price depends on the demand
and the energy sources available, the consumption and residuals were also explored.
The data was then adjusted to use for models whose input is not limited to historical data.
Information about the day given by the day of the week and if a day is a workday were
also found to have an influence on the price. These were created as potential explanatory
input variables.
Five different approaches to predict the day­ahead market price were then examined:
Various SARIMA models trained on historical data only, two different seasonal Lucia­
Schwartz­inspired models trained on explanatory variables, and their own historical errors.
Next, a LSTM of various complexity and with historical data and explanatory variables
as input. Gradient boosting models, consisting of 500 or 1000 tree stumps or trees of
depth three, were trained on historical data combined with explanatory variables. Lastly,
combined models where various of the previously produced models were combined with
equal weight to mitigating the errors of the forecasts were explored. To evaluate the per­
formance of the models, a baseline was created using two naïve approaches to predict
the prices.
Most of the models did not handle the structural changes in the data well and performed
better when trained on a smaller dataset than the available data.
The LSTM had the overall worse performance and was not able to outperform the baseline
of the previous day naïve approach. The main reason is the relatively small training set
and the validation set and test set having different properties than most of the training
data.
The seasonal Lucia­Schwartz­inspired model did not perform as intended as no general
properties of the data were captured.
The SARIMA models did not handle the abrupt changes in the data well. Some of the
SARIMA models did however manage to model a conservative price forecast by using
the seasonal MA to create a smooth estimate. The SARIMA with the fewest seasonal
components and with seasonal AR instead of seasonal MA, showed the worse perfor­
mance since the fluctuations of the forecast became too great.
The best result was produced by the gradient boosting with the previous day’s hourly
prices and the same hour’s residual and consumption, as input and the combined forecast
consisting of two SARIMA models and one naïve forecast. Two out of the three models
A survey on electricity price forecasting
53
==PAGE:66==
used in the combined forecast did not perform well on their own, together they managed
to capture a general pattern of the data. The gradient boosting had the best score for
the MAE while the combined model had the best results for the RMSE and daily MAE.
This indicates that the gradient boosting has a few estimations with a larger error, which
the RMSE penalizes harder, but overall has a good fit. Both approaches would be worth
going forward with.
By exploring the feature importance of the gradient boosting algorithm it was found that
the residual did provide valuable information that helped with a more accurate prediction.
Since the consumption, in general, are more expensive when it cannot be covered by
renewable energy sources, this seems reasonable. Adding variables for the consumption
and residuals for the energy did in general produce better results for the gradient boosting
algorithm and the LSTM.

7
Future Work
7.1
Improving Long Short­Term Memory (LSTM) model
For the LSTM there is potential for improving the performance by training it on more data.
This can either be done by using transfer learning, meaning that the network is trained
on similar data from similar European countries, and then fine­tuning the model on the
French data. Otherwise, if it is not possible to obtain more data, synthetic data can be cre­
ated either by Synthetic Minority Over­sampling Technique (SMOTE) or modified SMOTE.
SMOTE created new data points from the existing data by creating new data points which
lie between any two nearest data points joined by a straight line.
Since LSTM is able to detect long­term dependencies, using longer sequences as data
input to utilize its abilities is ideal if more data is available. Having an input of a week
or more of daily energy prices together with explanatory variables such as the weather
forecast would allow the LSTM to distinguish itself from the other methods explored. The
error function of the LSTM was also found to be sensitive to outliers. Training the model
with a different error function could potentially improve the overall fit.
7.2
Forecasting residual load
Since the consumption and residual data were the actual data and not a forecast, these
values are not possible to use in an actual trading setting. Since using residuals as input
increased the performance of the gradient boosting and the LSTM it is worth looking into
methods of creating a forecast of residual load.
The residual load depends on consumption and the production of energy from renewable
energy sources. Therefore the first step is to predict the consumption. The consumption
is highly seasonal dependent and showcases a more regular pattern as can be seen from
figure 3.5. Therefore it might be possible to model it by capturing seasonal systematic
patterns like that of the seasonal Lucia­Schwartz­inspired model. However, creating mul­
tiple models and making a combined forecast has been shown to outperform individual
forecasts. Therefore using several models to predict the consumption and combining the
forecasts would likely give the best result.
Next, the production of renewable energy needs to be predicted. This is highly correlated
with the weather. Creating weather forecasts required gathering a significant amount of
new data and training new models. Therefore a pre­made weather forecast would be
preferred to optimize the process.
Then, the relationship between the weather and energy production needs to be assessed
and a model created depending on the variables from weather forecasts and historic data
from renewable energy production. It will likely be best to create multiple independent
forecasts for the different renewable energy sources.
Lastly, consumption prediction and renewable energy production should be combined to
predict the residuals. However, since both predictions will create certain errors it might be
better to use both as input to the final model that predicts the day­ahead market price. If
enough data is available a deep neural network might prove to be useful for this complex
relation, but gradient boosting might also be enough to capture the underlying pattern.
Both models can potentially compensate for the errors made by the forecasts of con­
sumption and of renewable energy production.

7.3
Creating a trading strategy
Due to the small test set, this can be seen as a proof of concept. Therefore, before
applying it in a business setting, tests should be run with more data, to make sure that the
forecasts of the day­ahead market price do not involve high risk.
The next step from the perspective of a trader is to see if the forecasts can be used to­
gether with those of an interconnected country like Germany. If both countries have rea­
sonable forecasts they can be used to predict which country will have the lowest price. It
can then be determined if it is economically beneficial to buy from one and sell to the other.
Given the predictions are good enough to turn a profit, the forecast can be implemented
into a trading strategy.
