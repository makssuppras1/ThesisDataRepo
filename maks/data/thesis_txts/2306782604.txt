ABSTRACT 
In this project liposomes loaded with antibiotics used for cystic fibrosis symptoms treatment 
were studied. Hydrophilic antibiotics were encapsulated into liposomes passively by 
hydration of lipid or lipid mixtures. Large unilamellar vesicles (LUVs) were prepared by 
extrusion through filter with 100 nm porous size. Each of five antibiotics (Penicillin G, 
Meropenem, Ceftazidime, Vancomycin, Colistimethate sodium) was first encapsulated into 
POPC liposomes. Then the most promising ones were tested in liposomes of different 
compositions, when Stealth mixture was also involved. Dialysis was used for washing the free 
non-encapsulated drug. Drug content was quantified either by UV-VIS spectrophotometry or 
by HPLC. Low encapsulation efficiency (below 1.5%) of all prepared liposomes suggested 
drug leakage. Therefore, stability of the antibiotics was tested over the conditions occurring 
during liposome preparation. Penicillin G was relatively stable during heating (70 °C, 1 hour), 
over time (10 days of storage at 5 °C) and after extrusion through filter with 100 nm porous 
size, when dissolved in phosphate buffer (pH = 7.0). Instability of Penicillin G was found in 
Stealth liposomes over time, though no similar degradation was observed in POPC liposomes. 
These stability experiments led to suggestion of drug interaction with one or more 
components of the Stealth mixture. This interaction was studied by UPLC-MS showing effect 
of ethanol on Penicillin G, when in all degraded solutions peak with m/z = 381 was found. 
Meropenem dissolved in three different buffers (pH = 4.0; 7.4; 8.0) was highly instable when 
heated at 65 °C for 3 hours. This led to degradation of the antibiotic in Stealth liposomes. 
Vancomycin dissolved in aqueous solutions in high concentration (115 mg/ml) tended to 
precipitation, disabling liposome preparation. Stability of the buffer solutions pH after adding 
the drug was also tested.

1. INTRODUCTION AND MOTIVATION 
Liposomes are widely studied drug delivery systems because of their ability to improve 
therapeutic efficacy of the drugs. Liposomes can accumulate to a high extent in inflamed 
tissues, which is one of the main reasons why liposomes could improve antibiotic delivery.  
Using liposomes could also help to overcome the bacterial resistance problem, since drug 
distribution depends primarily on the properties of the liposomes, not on drug properties. 
These advantages could be used in treatment of cystic fibrosis symptoms leading to the 
original scope of the project, which was formulation of bioresponsive liposomes loaded with 
antibiotics and their potential in vitro study. Cystic fibrosis is also studied by Professor Søren 
Molin from Biosustain DTU, whose collaboration was suggested for examining of 
successfully prepared liposomes. The original scope was adjusted after findings of liposomal 
drug leakage and antibiotic instabilities resulting in studies of non-encapsulated drug stability 
and encapsulated drug interaction with liposomal membrane components. 
Outline of the Thesis 
The project background, performed experiments, obtained results and their discussion are 
presented in following five chapters. 
Chapter 2 summarizes theoretical background regarding liposomes, antibiotics used in this 
project and cystic fibrosis. 
Chapter 3 describes experimental part of the project. Involved materials and methods of 
liposome preparation, characterization and antibiotic quantification are presented. 
Chapter 4 presents the results obtained by experiments described in Chapter 3. Liposomal 
size and polydispersity index, encapsulation efficiency of liposomes, as well as drug stability 
in its free form and encapsulated in liposomes are reported. 
Chapter 5 discusses the challenges and obstacles addressed while performing individual 
experiments. Drug leakage and drug instability are primarily deliberated. 
Chapter 6 concludes the results and potential explanations of their meaning.

2. THEORETICAL BACKGROUND 
2.1 Liposomes 
Liposomes are spherical vesicles formed by one or more lipid bilayers surrounding an 
aqueous core. Liposomes emerge spontaneously when lipids compatible with lipid bilayer get 
into contact with water. Since lipids are constituted of hydrophilic polar head and 
hydrophobic lipid tail, the hydrophobic interaction between water and hydrophobic part of the 
lipid molecule leads to directing the polar heads towards the water environment and contrary, 
the hydrophobic tails are assembled in the opposite direction. This bilayer resembles of 
cell-membrane structure (see Figure 1).1,2,3  
 
Figure 1. Liposome structure: polar heads (A) and hydrophobic tails (B) of lipids surrounding aqueous 
core with encapsulated hydrophilic drug (C) and entrapping hydrophobic drug (D) in the bilayer 
 
Liposomes were discovered by Alec D. Bangham research group in 1961 in Cambridge.4 
They are widely studied as potential drug delivery systems because of many advantages they 
possess. Liposomes used for drug delivery are usually composed of lipids, which are 
biocompatible, biodegradable and non-immunogenic.1,2,5 This enables them to be transported 
quickly and without fast degradation within the body. Because of their amphiphilic character, 
they can encapsulate a lipophilic drug into their aqueous core and entrap either a hydrophilic 
molecule at the membrane interface or a lipophilic drug inside the membrane. Improving of 
drug therapeutic index can be established by modified drug absorption, reduction of drug 
metabolism, lowering drug toxicity or protecting body tissues, for which the drug is toxic, and 
==PAGE:9==
3 
 
also by prolonging drug half-life.1,2,3,6 The drug distribution is primarily dependent on the 
properties of the liposomes. Liposome surface can be also easily modified, which is 
preferably used to target the vesicles specifically. These advantages are shaped depending on 
the physicochemical properties of liposomes. Among the most important characteristics are 
liposome composition, their size, drug load efficiency, liposome stability and their interaction 
with cell-membranes. Liposomes can be simply adsorbed, transported by endocytosis, fused 
with cell-membrane or lipid exchange between liposome bilayer and cell-membrane can 
occur. The latter two ways are rarer than the former two. 1  
The drug load into liposomes is influenced by preparation method of liposomes. In this 
project the solvent evaporation method consisting of four main steps was used. When working 
with lipid mixtures, the lipids are firstly solubilized in an organic solvent together with lipid 
soluble compounds (if any are added). This solvent is then evaporated. This can be done 
spontaneously in a glass container but is preferably done by using either rotary evaporator to 
get thin lipid film or by using freeze-dryer. Tertiary buthanol (t-BuOH) is one of the most 
suitable organic solvents for freeze-drying. Organic solvent evaporation can be skipped if 
one-lipid liposomes are prepared. The lipids are then hydrated by aqueous medium containing 
hydrophilic drug if encapsulation of this drug is desired. The hydration is performed above the 
phase transition temperature of the phospholipid. During this process, the solution is mixed 
manually or mechanically using either vortex-mixing or bath sonication. This results in 
a milky suspension giving multi-lamellar large vesicles, which can be further processed to 
reduce their size and the number of lamellar. A very gentle method of size reduction is an 
extrusion using polycarbonate membrane filters of defined porous size. This technique allows 
choosing the upper limit of liposome size and producing almost completely unilamellar 
vesicles (LUVs).1 
Pure lipids can exist in two phases, either in a gel phase (solid-ordered) below the transition 
temperature or in a fluid phase (liquid-disordered) above this temperature. When lipids are in 
mixture, the phase separation can occur. This means one lipid in solid phase can exist next to 
the liquid phase lipid retaining phase equilibria. Incorporating cholesterol into lipid bilayers 
leads to introduction of a new phase called liquid-ordered phase. Cholesterol makes the lipids 
in the bilayer more packed and stabilizes the liquid-ordered phase over a wide range of 
temperatures and compositions. On the other hand, the bilayer fluidity necessary for 
a membrane function remains the same.7   
==PAGE:10==
4 
 
Stability of liposomes is important to obtain sufficient shelf-life of the vesicles. Instability of 
liposomes can lead to leakage of the encapsulated drug. This can be caused by problems with 
membrane permeability, size distribution problems or by hydrolytic and oxidative 
degradation. Oxidative degradation occurs in unsaturated acyl chains. This can be avoided 
either by keeping the level of oxidation at minimum or by using saturated or 
mono-unsaturated lipids instead of the poly-unsaturated ones. Hydrolysis of the ester linkages 
of fatty acid to the glycerol backbone occurs least at neutral pH and low temperature. The 
fusion of the lipids occurring at transition temperature can be prevented by adding cholesterol 
into the structure, which can eliminate the transition. Regarding the encapsulated drug, the 
most stable lipid membranes are formed by saturated lipids with a higher ratio of cholesterol. 
Biological stability of liposomes depends on presence of biological agents, which are able to 
interact with the vesicles. Neutrally charged liposomes seem to be more stable, since they 
have low affinity to charged proteins. The long-term stability of liposomes is most often 
ensured by coating the surface of liposomes with poly(ethylene glycol), PEG leading to 
prolonged circulation of liposomes in the body. An example of clinically approved and stable 
liposome formulation using the pegylation is the liposomal doxorubicin Doxil.1 
The composition of liposomes can be formulated with respect to target selectivity. In this 
project, the charge of the liposomal membrane was proposed to ensure the electrostatic 
interaction with the target. Since bacterial cell walls are negatively charged, cationic 
liposomes exhibit strong liposome-bacterium interaction.3 Bacterial cell wall negative charge 
is given by presence of teichoic acid in Gram-positive bacteria and by presence of outer layer 
in Gram-negative bacteria. The outer layer is composed of phospholipids and 
lipopolysaccharides, which are highly negatively charged.8 To enable the electrostatic 
interactions between liposomes and bacteria, cationic lipids such as DOTAP can be added 
into the liposomal mixture.

2.2 Antibiotics  
In this project, five antibiotics were studied in terms of the possibility to use them as 
liposomal drug carriers. These antibiotics were chosen because of their usage in treatment of 
cystic fibrosis symptoms.  
Penicillin G (PenG), Meropenem (Merop) and Ceftazidime (Ceft) are β-lactam antibiotics. 
They inhibit cell wall synthesis by acting on enzymes transpeptidases and carboxypeptidases. 
The β-lactams simulate part of the natural substrate of these enzymes and then covalently 
modify the target protein, which is involved in the cell growth. These proteins are unique for 
bacteria, which makes β-lactams targeted directly to these organisms.9  
Vancomycin (Vanc) is a glycopeptide antibiotic, which inhibits cell wall synthesis in 
Gram-positive bacteria. Vancomycin behaves as a substrate specific inhibitor of 
peptidoglycan synthesis. The antibiotic binds specifically to the residues of lipid II, which 
carries   dissaccharide-pentapeptide units along the membrane. Then sterically hinders the 
enzyme transglycosylase, which normally incorporates the dissaccharide-pentapeptide unit 
into developing peptidoglycan. This inhibition is a slow, concentration-independent process.10 
Colistimethate sodium (Colist) is one of the forms of colistin, which is a cationic polypeptide 
antibiotic interacting with anionic lipopolysaccharide in Gram-negative bacteria outer layer. 
Colistin displaces calcium and magnesium cations stabilizing the lipopolysaccharide 
membrane, which results in increased permeability of membrane. Colistin is used in the 
treatment of infections caused by multidrug-resistant pathogens.11

2.3 Cystic fibrosis 
Cystic fibrosis (CF) is a lethal, inherited autosomal recessive disease. CF appears only in 
individuals who receive two copies of mutated cystic fibrosis transmembrane regulator 
(CFTR) gene (one from each parent), which is located on a chromosome 7 long arm. The 
disease affects epithelial organs, primarily respiratory tract. In CF patients, the viscid mucus 
is developed leading to secondary infection. A pulmonary exacerbation, an episode of 
increased respiratory symptoms, is the most often cause of CF patients morbidity and 
mortality (approximately 90%).14, 15, 16 
The pathogenesis of CF is not fully understood, but it is widely accepted that CFTR gene 
expression is linked to the epithelial ion transport, particularly chloride anions. In healthy 
airways, apical membranes of epithelial cells possess cilia covered by approximately 7 um 
high periciliary liquid (PCL) layer, which is together with mucus a part of airway surface 
liquid (ASL). PCL helps to hydrate and transport mucin molecules in the mucus layer and 
ensures sufficient ciliary movement by low-viscosity environment. In a CF patient, the PCL is 
depleted leading to thicker mucus layer of high tonicity and direct apposition of the apical 
membrane.14 Figure 2 shows the difference between epithelia in a healthy individual and in 
a patient suffering from CF. 
 
Figure 2. Difference between healthy epithelia (A) and cystic fibrosis epithelia (B) showing depleted PCL 
(blue) not ensuring sufficient space for ciliary movement and thick layer of mucus (green) 
 
==PAGE:14==
8 
 
This ASL dehydration is regulated either by passive or by active forces. The passive 
regulation depends on hydration state of PCL, when water is either added or extracted from 
the liquid layer. But active ion transport has much higher influence in this case, because 
airway epithelial cell membranes are relatively water-permeable. Salts can be transported in 
either a mucosal (secretion) or a serosal (absorption) direction. Secretion occurs via active 
movement of chloride anions in a mucosal direction followed by passive transport of sodium 
cations and water up a chemical and osmotic gradient. During absorption, sodium cations 
active transport in serosal direction is accompanied by passive transport of chloride anions 
and water down the osmotic gradient. CFTR also interact with other components of the 
ion-transport system, primarily with epithelial sodium channel (ENaC). In a healthy 
individual, interaction between those two channels leads to tonic inhibition of sodium cations 
absorption. Once CFTR gene is not expressed sufficiently, the ENaC is released from this 
inhibition, which promotes high absorption of sodium cations leading to depletion of PCL, 
followed by adherence of concentrated mucus in the airways. These mucus plaques favour 
bacterial colonization and persistence.14 
In CF patients, bacteria cannot be removed mechanically by their entrapment in the mucus 
followed by movement in cephalad direction via cilia forcing their clearance by cough. The 
mucus is also thick enough to prevent penetration by host immune cells, predominantly 
neutrophils, which response immediately to bacterial infection by release in high 
concentrations, phagocytosis of bacteria and lastly apoptosis.17 Plus hyper-absorption of 
sodium cations causes a high energy consumption required by Na+ -K+ -ATPase, leading to 
hypoxic environment, which favours bacteria capable of anaerobic biofilm formation.14 High 
bacteria concentration induces high release of inflammatory mediators (cytokines and 
chemokines) as well as bacterial products.16 All this results in a chronic infection and 
inflammation leading to permanent airway wall and lung parenchyma damage.14  
There are two dominant bacteria strains infecting CF epithelial cells – Staphylococcus aureus 
(S. aureus) attacking in the first two decades of CF patient life and Pseudomonas aeruginosa 
(P. aeruginosa) for the rest of CF patient life. Aggressive and long-term antibiotic treatment 
is in CF patients necessary, since it is not common for CF airways to be completely sterile. 
One of the general principles for antibiotic treatment is to maintain lower airways sterile. The 
antibiotics are delivered in high doses – in children at the top end of the dose range, in adults 
two to three times higher dose than recommended. In the CF patient childhood, the 
prophylactic antibiotic treatment against S. aureus is common.16 S. aureus is Gram-positive 
==PAGE:15==
9 
 
bacterium, capable of small colony and biofilm formation. Formation of colonies is enhanced 
in presence of P. aeruginosa exoproducts. The biofilms are associated with bacteria resistance 
to antibiotics.18 Two types of S. aureus are recognized. Methicillin-sensitive S. aureus 
(MSSA) is found mostly in sputum of children and also as a co-infecting bacterium together 
with P. aeruginosa and other Gram-negative organisms. Methicillin-resistant S. aureus 
(MRSA) is not resistant only to methicillin but also to other β–lactam antimicrobials. By the 
third decade of CF patient life, over 80 % of patients suffer from chronic infection with 
P. aeruginosa, which is also the most common cause of chronic lung disease. Once this 
bacterium is established in patient’s airways, it is impossible to get rid of it. Bacterium-host 
relationship results in constant inflammation. P. aeruginosa is a complex bacterium, which 
can adapt in the relatively hypoxic environment by forming of colonies and biofilm. The 
biofilm protects the bacteria from host’s neutrophils, which get frustrated then either apoptotic 
or necrotic. Biofilms also increase bacterial resistance to antibiotics, since it protects the 
bacteria against drug penetration. P. aeruginosa can also change its phenotype from 
non-mucoid to mucoid during time. The treatment of P. aeruginosa infection depends on its 
stage. Therefore, the accurate identification of the bacterium is necessary. In the first stages, 
aggressive antibiotic treatment is used. During pulmonary exacerbations, β-lactams and 
aminoglycosides are usually prescribed. Chronic infection is then usually treated by long-term 
nebulized antibiotic therapy, when e.g. Colistin can be used.16

3.3.3 Liposome characterisation 
Liposomal size was characterised by dynamic light scattering (DLS) using a characterisation 
of the temporal structure of particles formed in liquid solution by Brownian motion. Since the 
particles are moving randomly, the frequency and the phase of scattered light are changed. 
The frequency is changed because of Doppler effect. The phase of light is difficult to measure 
directly, but the interference of changing phase and large amount of scattering leads to 
fluctuation in intensity, which can be measured simply. In DLS, laser is used as 
a monochromatic source.20  
Buffers used for measurements were filtered through the filters with 0.45 μm porous diameter.  
Plastic cuvettes were washed three times by filtered buffer to remove any dust, which could 
interfere with the measurement. Then 2 ml of filtered buffer was transferred to the cuvette and 
appropriate volume of liposomal sample was added to get concentration of 50 μM. Plastic 
cuvette was closed by the lid and sample was homogenized manually and allowed to stand 
until there were no air bubbles. Each sample was measured at least three times to get the 
average effective diameter and polydispersity index of measured liposomes. 
3.3.4 Drug stability and quantification by UV-VIS spectrophotometry 
PenG was quantified by UV-VIS spectrophotometry, which is based on ability of molecules 
to absorb electromagnetic radiation of specific wavelengths. This ability is possessed by 
molecules containing a chromophore such as conjugated double bonds. Absorption of 
ultraviolet (190 – 400 nm) or visible (400 – 800 nm) light leads to valence electron transfer 
from non-excited to excited state. Quantitative analysis of solutions containing one 
compound, which absorbs light, is based on Beer-Lambert law. Absorbance at certain 
wavelength is directly proportional to molar concentration of solution, when the product of 
length of light path and molar absorption coefficient is the constant of proportionality.21  
All measurements were performed in a 1 cm wide quartz cuvette, when 400 μl of sample was 
measured. After each single measurement, the cuvette was washed with MQ water and EtOH 
and dried with air flow. As a blank MQ water was used for all samples, when buffers and 
other solvents without PenG were measured to see if their absorbance does not influence 
spectra of the antibiotic. To measure standard curves for PenG in HEPES, PBS and TRIS 
buffers and EtOH, 50 mg of PenG were dissolved in appropriate solvent and then 11 2-fold 
dilutions were done. Concentration of drug encapsulated in POPC liposomes prepared in PBS 
buffer was measured using 20-fold dilution of liposomal sample with EtOH or 11-fold 
==PAGE:21==
15 
 
dilution with 10% Triton X-100. Obtained spectra were compared with same dilutions of 
POPC liposomes prepared the same way but without any drug. To see if the PenG spectra are 
influenced by pH, 45 mg of PenG was dissolved in PBS buffer and either 15 μl of 1 M HCl or 
3 μl of 5 M NaOH was added to 400 μl of this PenG solution. The same stock solution was 
also used to see how 20-fold dilution with EtOH influences PenG absorbance. Then 400 μl of 
0.003 M solution of PenG in EtOH was measured after adding 1 μl of TFA. POPC liposomes 
loaded with PenG were diluted 20 times with EtOH and measured after adding of 1 μl of TFA 
to 400 μl of this solution. Last experiment using UV-VIS spectrophotometry to quantify PenG 
was measuring release profile of free drug. One millilitre of 0.2 M PenG in PBS buffer was 
transferred into dialysis tubing and this was inserted in a glass vial with 10 ml of PBS buffer, 
which was stirred by magnetic stirrer. Dialysis was performed in triplicate. Every 15 minutes 
1 ml of sink buffer was taken from each vial and 1 ml of fresh buffer was added back into the 
glass vials with dialysis tube to keep the volume of sink buffer the same during whole dialysis 
experiment. This was repeated up to 6 hours after start of the dialysis. Last sample was taken 
after 24 hours. Samples of sink buffer were measured in an appropriate dilution to keep the 
absorbance of PenG below 1.  
3.3.5 Drug stability and quantification by HPLC 
PenG and other antibiotics were then quantified by HPLC. Liquid chromatography is one of 
the separation methods, when column with sorbent is used. The sorbent, which can be reached 
by the sample is called stationary phase. Between particles of sorbent, mobile phase (eluent) 
flows. When the sample is in contact with sorbent, each component of the solution is retained 
by the solid phase to reach thermodynamic equilibrium. By applying high pressure, high 
resolution is obtained. In this project reversed phase chromatography was used, since the 
column was non-polar and mobile phase polar.21

chromatograms). Flow rate: 1 ml/min. Injection volume: 50 μl. UV wavelengths: 220 nm and 
240 nm. The method22 and wavelengths23,24 were based on literature search.

detector at 220 nm and QDa electrospray MS detector. Injection volume: 5 μl.

4.2.1 UV-VIS spectrophotometry 
All UV-VIS experiments were performed only with PenG. At first, spectra of different 
concentrations of PenG in PBS, TRIS and HEPES buffers were measured in a quartz cuvette. 
A non-shouldered broad band at 324 nm was observed in all three buffers as well as shoulders 
at 257 and 264 nm belonging to the band, which shifted with different PenG concentration. 
This shouldered band also possessed absorbance higher than 1 for all PenG concentrations, in 
which the peak at 324 nm was observed. Standard curves were plotted for all three buffers and 
all three mentioned wavelengths. The absorbance was linearly dependant on concentration 
only in certain ranges. The best trade-off between measurable concentration range and 
goodness of fit was obtained for 324 nm in all cases. Next experiments requiring use of buffer 
were performed in PBS buffer. Spectra of PenG in PBS buffer and its standard curves can be

5. DISCUSSION 
Liposome characterisation 
Size of all prepared liposomes reflected the diameter of pores in the filters used for liposome 
extrusion.33 Size varied with liposome formulation, encapsulated antibiotic and also used 
buffer. The polydispersity measurements showed the liposomes as predominantly 
monodisperse vesicles. 
Free drug stability 
At first, PenG was studied by UV-VIS spectrophotometry in this project. Searching existing 
literature, bands seen at 257 and 264 nm belong to non-degraded PenG.34 On the other hand, 
the absorption seen at 322 nm is ascribed to either an isomerization product formed in neutral 
conditions over time or a degradation product of PenG formed by heating in acidic pH.35,36 In 
the spectra measured in this project, a band at 324 nm was observed even in freshly prepared 
samples in neutral pH. But the statement regarding the degradation product would be in 
agreement with protonation experiments performed in this project, when the signal at 324 nm 
was enhanced by adding HCl into aqueous or TFA into EtOH solutions. Since the band at 
324 nm probably belonged to a product of PenG degradation, absorbance at 257 and 264 nm 
was proportional to concentration in small concentration ranges only and repeatability of 
measurements was low, the HPLC technique was used to further quantification of PenG, since 
it enables separation of the lipids and drug during detection. By liquid chromatography, the 
stability of PenG solution in buffer of neutral pH was observed over time (10 days of storage 
at 5 °C) as well as over short periods of heating even in relatively high temperatures (1 h, up 
to 70 °C). The stability over time is supported by existing research, which claims PenG 
highest stability in buffers of neutral pH.37,38,39 By extrusion through 100 nm cellulose filter, 
approximately 10% of the drug could be lost.  
Other antibiotics were quantified by using HPLC technique only. Stability test of Ceft or 
Colist were not performed, since these two antibiotics were discarded based on their low 
encapsulation into POPC liposomes and also their high baseline noise in HPLC spectra 
chromatograms compared to other studied antibiotics. Vanc solutions of 115 mg/ml tended to 
precipitate, which made their extrusion together with lipids difficult and led to gel formation. 
Merop in neutral buffer solution was relatively stable over time. On the other hand, solutions 
in acidic or basic pH were degraded up to 57.9% of original drug content. Merop was 
==PAGE:45==
39 
 
significantly degraded by heating at 65 °C. These stability experiments data are supported by 
existing literature.40 A certain similarity pattern between Merop and PenG degradation can be 
found, since both antibiotics belong to β-lactam group. Adding Merop or Vanc into buffer 
solutions led also to pH change. pH of the former antibiotic solutions was most stable in 
basic, pH of the latter on the other hand in acidic conditions.  
Liposome leakage 
Liposomes loaded with antibiotics prepared in this project had in general very low 
encapsulation efficiency. The leakage of liposomes was supported by data obtained by 
measuring drug content in liposome solutions after several dialyses, which were supposed to 
wash the free drug from liposome surface. There was no more than 1.5% of the original drug 
content left after second dialysis in all cases.  
There are many studies using microbiological methods to measure efficacy of liposomes 
loaded by PenG. By labelling the antibiotic with [14C]-PenG, the encapsulation efficiency was 
detected in these studies. Hee-Jeong Kim et al.41 hydrated the DPPC-Chol-DC-chol 
(1:0.49:0.43) liposomes with 10 mg/ml PenG solution and extruded them through a filter with 
140 nm pore size to get LUVs. The concentration of hydration solution corresponds to 
approximately 30 mM solution, which compared to 27 mM concentration of PenG detected in 
liposomal solution suggests almost 90% encapsulation efficiency. On the other hand, the 
article also mentions drug leakage. However, this was not elaborated further. Gregory 
Gregoriadis42 found 2 - 8% encapsulation efficiency depending on used formulation, when the 
liposomes were prepared by sonication after hydration of thin lipid layer and the 
measurements were done in vivo. Even though Helene E. Schaeffer and David L. Krohn43 
suggest secondary association of PenG and liposome membranes, their in vivo radioactivity 
measurements show 2 – 18% encapsulation efficiency, when the lowest amount of drug 
entrapped into multilamellar neutral liposomes and the highest into unilamellar positive ones. 
Meropenem 3.7 – 7.2% encapsulation efficiency depending on formulation was described by 
Zuzanna Drulis-Kawa et al.44. In this case, the Merop content was detected by HPLC. The 
highest encapsulation efficiency was found in PC:DOTAP (8:2, m/m) and DMPC:Chol:PS 
(4:3:4, m/m) LUVs of approximately 130 nm and 115 nm size, respectively. The free drug 
was removed from liposomes by gel filtration in all mentioned studies.  
The liposome leakage is dependent on lipid bilayer structure and also on liposome preparation 
method. At first, POPC liposomes were prepared and loaded with each antibiotic. POPC is 
==PAGE:46==
40 
 
a model lipid used in biophysical experiments. It is a mono-unsaturated lipid, therefore its 
transition temperature is low and no phase transition occurred in the working temperature 
range. On the other hand, POPC can be at risk of oxidative stress. This can be avoided by 
using saturated lipids, which was the reason to work with DSPC in the case of Vanc loaded 
liposomes. Furthermore, adding more than 30% of cholesterol into the lipid mixture leads to 
elimination of phase transition of saturated lipids. Cholesterol also secures mechanical 
integrity of the membrane. The Stealth mixture contains HSPC, a natural version of synthetic 
DSPC. Its phase transition is removed by cholesterol present in the mixture. Moreover, the 
Stealth mixture contains PEG, which extends the liposome blood-circulation time, since the 
uptake of mononuclear phagocyte system is reduced. Even though, adding cholesterol into 
mixtures should make the membranes impermeable, no significant difference between drug 
load into liposomes with and without cholesterol was observed. Actually, PenG was loaded 
more efficiently into POPC liposomes without cholesterol than into Stealth liposomes. This 
led to suggestion of some kind of interaction between the antibiotic and one or more of the 
components of Stealth mixture. 
From the preparation point of view, the size of the liposomes and the pH could affect the 
leakage. In this project, large unilamellar vesicles (diameter higher than 100 nm) were 
prepared. The LUVs do not suffer from curvature strain as much as SUVs, which are prone to 
increased permeability of the membrane leading to drug leakage. There was also a pH change 
observed after adding Vanc or Merop into the buffer solution. This could lead to change of 
drugs properties in terms of their ability to cross the lipid membrane. 
Drug stability in liposomes 
The main difference between using POPC and Stealth mixture was the drug stability in these 
liposomes. Preparing POPC liposomes, only minor antibiotic degradation was observed for 
Ceft and for Merop. In the latter case, the degradation occurred after second dialysis, which 
was performed 7 days after the first one. This corresponds to the slight degradation of Merop 
in neutral pH over time found in free drug solution. In the former case, the acidic pH, in 
which the liposomes were prepared, could play a role, since Ceft is also β-lactam antibiotic. 
Significant drug degradation was observed in Stealth liposomes loaded with Merop or PenG. 
In the case of Merop, the degradation could be caused by heating, since the percentage of 
drug content measured in liposomes before extrusion and in free drug solution were similar. 
The different degradation profile in liposome chromatograms could be ascribed to extrusion, 
which can affect the degradation process. On the other hand, in case of PenG, the drug 
==PAGE:47==
41 
 
degradation is significantly higher in liposomes than in free drug solution, even though 
liposomes were prepared in the neutral pH, in which PenG is considered as the most stable. 
This proposed some drug interaction with one or more of the lipids contained in Stealth 
mixture occurred. These potential interactions were studied by LC-MS. The mass spectra of 
degraded samples shown predominantly peak at m/z = 381, which was suggested to belong to 
a complex between PenG and EtOH. But no peaks potentially belonging to lipid-antibiotic 
interaction were determined. Literature in this case reports a hydrophobic interaction between 
phosphatidylcholines and PenG. John M. Padfield et al. compared this interaction to the 
behaviour of sterols in the phospholipid mixture, since it also lowered the configurational 
freedom and kinetic motions of the phospholipid hydrophobic chains.45 On the other hand, it 
was also said, that the PenG degradation products possessed higher diffusion coefficient than 
non-degraded antibiotic.46 Higher permeability of degradation products through phospholipid 
membranes could correspond to the drug leakage from liposomes.

6. CONCLUSION 
Liposomes prepared in this project suffered from drug leakage. All antibiotics loaded into the 
liposomes are hydrophilic. Each antibiotic was first encapsulated into POPC liposomes, 
serving as a standard lipid for biophysical experiments. Then different liposome formulations 
loaded with the most promising antibiotics (PenG, Merop, Vanc) were prepared. But the 
problem with low drug load was not overcome with different liposomal composition. 
Therefore, conditions of the antibiotic loss were studied. PenG was relatively stable in buffer 
solution of neutral pH and in POPC liposomes. On the other hand, high PenG degradation 
was observed in Stealth liposomes. The possible interaction of the antibiotic and one or more 
component of Stealth mixture was studied by UPLC-MS. PenG degradation was described in 
the samples prepared in EtOH, which probably forms a complex with the antibiotic. Unlike 
PenG, Merop was highly unstable in buffer solutions of different pH (4.0, 7.0, 8.0) at higher 
temperatures (65 °C) and over time. Thus, degradation of Merop in Stealth liposomes was 
probably caused by heating during the liposome preparation. Vanc in higher concentrations 
(115 mg/ml) tended to precipitate in Citrate buffer (pH = 4.0), 150 mM NaCl (aq) solution 
and HEPES buffer (pH = 7.0). This disabled preparation of liposomes, when hydration 
solution of this high drug concentration was used.  
Studies using microbiological tests to describe liposomes loaded with antibiotics and their 
effect on bacteria show enhanced activity of the drug and possibility to overcome bacterial 
resistance, when most frequently encapsulated antibiotics were fluoroquinolones and 
aminoglycosides.3 Since bacterial resistance is a tremendous problem of present time, 
liposomes could be a promising solution. But this project revealed several obstacles, which 
need to be overcome in preparation of liposomes loaded with antibiotics. This leads to 
question the liposomes as the antibiotic delivery system. Only antibiotics which are stable in 
aqueous solution during all conditions of liposome preparation as well as in presence of lipids 
or cholesterol can be used for liposome preparation, unless the degradation products are also 
active. This is not a case of β-lactam antibiotics degrading by hydrolytic β-lactam ring 
opening leading to generation of microbiologically inactive products.37,47 Overall, stability of 
the drug should be tested further before their encapsulation into the liposomes.
