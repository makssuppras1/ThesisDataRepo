Abstract
This thesis, conducted in cooperation with Br┬иuel & Kj├жr, concerns the investigation of a math-
ematical method for performing structural health monitoring and damage detection og struc-
tures. The method, in this thesis referred to as the covariance method, uses the covariance of
multisensor vibration signals to detect structural damage within structures.
To investigate the covariance method, lab experiments have been performed on a short thin-
walled PVC pipe and a virtual environment have been created to perform additional experiments
by simulations in ANSYS. The lab experiments provided results which can be analysed with
respect to the sensibility of the method and its applicability for indicating the occurrence of
damage in the structure.
By diямАerent signal processing the investigations have lead to the
beneямБts of band pass ямБltering the accelerations measured on the pipe, as the covariance method
provides more useful damage indicators within some frequency ranges than others. Also the lab
experiments show that measuring accelerations in diямАerent directions (perpendicular and axial)
can have beneямБts when using the output for damage detection. The results of the experiments
and signal processing have also implied that computing secondary damage indicators can be
beneямБcial as a supplement for the damage detection. The experiments performed in the virtual
environment was used for investigating the inямВuence of changing the material parameters.

1 Introduction
The purpose of this master thesis is to investigate a method desired used on wind turbine blades
by Br┬иuel & Kj├жr (B&K) for detecting structural damage. The method, here referred to as the
covariance method, is developed by Parker and Fraizer [1] and uses covariance matrices in the
signal processing of measured accelerations of the potentially damaged blade and compares it
with a mean covariance matrix of the undamaged blade, called reference matrix. By taking the
2-norm of the diямАerence between covariance matrices and the reference matrix, a damage index
can indicate structural damage in the blade. The scope of this thesis is to dig into and gain
further understanding of why and how this method is working and what aямАects the outcome,
still having damage detection in mind.
1.1
Motivation
As the desire to produce sustainable energy increases, the need for improved eямГciency of the
wind turbines increases as well. High eямГciency usually means large wind turbines and blades. As
the wind turbines are exposed both to the dynamic behaviour of the system and the weather over
a long period of time, damages are unavoidable. One of the most common types of damage in
wind turbines is blade damage [2]. Undetected damage in the blades of a wind turbine can have
catastrophic consequences to the whole system and its surroundings. Therefore it is desirable
to ямБnd defects in the blades as early as possible, so they can be rectiямБed. This is where damage
detection by structural health monitoring (SHM) comes in. For structures in use, the damage
detection and SHM should be non-destructive in order to maintain a structure and not destroy
it. The covariance method, recently tested by B&K, is a way to ensure that by applying a force
large enough to make the structure (blade) vibrate at the proper frequencies and small enough
not to damage the blade during the SHM. From prior experiments the covariance method seems
to be working eямГciently for SHM and damage detection.
However, which parameters, that
inямВuence the structural dynamics creating the damage indicator, appears to be yet unknown
and is desired uncovered in this thesis.
1.2
Literature Review
Many articles regarding SHM of diямАerent structures have been read, to gain a general background
knowledge within this ямБeld. As this project concerns the investigation of the covariance method,
the gained knowledge regarding SHM is not documented in this thesis. Written documentation
regarding the covariance method has been hard to ямБnd except for two articles from Parker and
Fraizer [1] and [3]. In the former, they refer to a prior article where the covariance method is
developed [4], which has not been possible to procure. For this reason the background regarding
the covariance method in Chapter 2 is written based on [1] and [3] plus a youtube video [5] and
conversations with Tcherniak [6]. To provide the reader with suямГcient background knowledge
to understand the general idea of the covariance method, the underlying basis and general signal
processing is described in Section 2.1 along with an applied example in Section 2.2.

2 Background
In this chapter, the background theory of the covariance method will be explained. First by
shortly stating the connection to other structural health monitoring (SHM) methods, which are
more well-known in the ямБeld of damage detection of wind turbines. Then the general theory be-
hind the covariance is documented to provide the reader with suямГcient knowledge to understand
how to generate the damage index, which can indicate the occurrence and increase of damage in
a structure. At last a demonstration of the covariance method is shown by applying the method
on a small test blade in a physical lab experiment performed previously by Tcherniak [5].
As mentioned in the introduction, the covariance method is a non-destructive SHM method for
detecting damage. Many non-destructive SHM methods exist using diямАerent sensors and signal
processing: Modal analysis, guided wave analysis, strain monitoring, acoustic emission, thermal
imaging, etc. [2] and [7]. Even though the covariance method is applicable with almost any
kind of sensors [1], it cannot be classiямБed as any of the above. Possibly though, the covariance
method contains some elements which correspond to modal analysis and guided wave analysis.
For guided wave analysis, an occurrence of a damage is detected as the damage causes the
wave propagation to scatter in all directions from the point of damage. This scatter of waves
is possibly why the measured accelerations in the covariance method changes the damage index
(DI) when a damage occur or grow. In any case, the covariance method can detect changes in
the dynamic behaviour of the structure by using multisensor vibration signals, much like SHM
by modal analysis.
2.1
The Covariance Method - Theory
When using the covariance method for SHM of a structure, the computed test stage covariance
matrices of the accelerations is compared with a reference covariance matrix, which is computed
from the undamaged structure in the training stage.
A DI that can indicate the existence
of damage in the structure is computed from the reference covariance matrix and the test
stage covariance matrices. The underlying basis of the mathematical model that describes the
physics of the structure of investigation is explained by Parker and Frazier in [1]. In this thesis
however, only experimental investigations are performed for which reason the generation of DI
by experiments are explained.

2.2

For easing the understanding of how the covariance method is used for SHM and damage de-

test blade.
The covariance method was tested by Tcherniak [5] on a small test blade of a length of ap-
prox. 1 meter, see Figure 2.2. The measurements are performed as a single input, multiple
output system, where an actuator (input) is placed at one point on the blade and multiple uni-

4.1
Creating the Virtual Environment
The creation of a virtual environment for a ямБnite element (FE) analysis in ANSYS requires
several considerations and choices.
In reality it is a iterative process, where element type,
geometry, mesh and type of analysis all are dependent of each other. This iteration process is
not described here, but some considerations are listed and brieямВy discussed.
Element Type:
For any FE analysis, the number of elements (and nodes) are desired big
enough to provide valid results and small enough to limit computational time. The main choice
in this analysis regarding element type is whether to choose solid or shell elements.
From
experience gained in the course 41812 - FEM Light at DTU, the element type SOLID186 was
validated as a good choice of element for a cylinder subjected to a torque. For the purpose of
this thesis, the option of doing transient analysis was tested as well as the possibility to retrieve
results of accelerations in the desired directions. As the SOLID186 lives up to these requirements,
the corresponding shell element SHELL281 is compared to SOLID186 by doing a convergence
study of the natural frequencies in a very high frequency range (FR), as the frequencies in the
lowest FRs was identical. In the end, the choice of element for this analysis fell on SHELL281,
which is a 3-dimensional 8 node element. The main reason for this is the computational time
saved by having a shell element compared to a solid, and the average deviation of the natural
frequencies of the two elements was less than 0.05 %, see Appendix A.8 of the convergence study.

As the element type is a shell, the pipe is modelled as a line revolved around the
y-axis creating an area. The revolution around the y-axis ensures the use of the rsys-function
(circumferential coordinate system) when retrieving results. The revolved area is divided in 120
circumferential areas to control the element size and to ease the simulation of damage.

When doing transient analysis in ANSYS where the equilibrium is the point of origin, no bound-
ary constraints are necessary. This means that only loads must be applied for this speciямБc ex-
periment, enabling a free-free boundary condition. To simulate an impulse load, two load steps

ramped loading. Together, the ямБrst and second load step simulates a hit on the structure. The
third and last load step neither apply nor remove load, but allows the analysis to continue for
a proper amount of seconds (or in this case 2000тИЖt) after the "hit". The force and load step
length values are found by trial and error and are chosen to provide vibrations which relatively
quickly returns to the equilibrium where the accelerations have converged to zero.

4.4.1
Optimization of the Virtual Environment
Each stage in each experiment must be analysed (computed in ANSYS) separately, and on the
computers available at the data bar in building 414 at DTU this takes approx. 6-8 hours. As
mentioned in Section 4.1.2, retrieving data is also time consuming and takes approx. 3-4 hours.
This means that each stage of each experiment costs 9-12 hours of computational time before
having data applicable for generating DIs by the signal processing described in Section 3.3.2.
As each experiment consists of 6-7 stages of damage sizes, one full virtual experiment can take
a week to perform if using only one computer.
Such a time consuming experiment calls for an optimization. A relatively easy way to optimize
this experiment is to have bigger elements and fewer time steps.
This of course requires a
validation of how much inямВuence it will cost to perform these changes. Initially, having only one
computer available, the need for results for the project was prioritized higher than performing a
transient analysis convergence study of element size, and the number of time steps was chosen
so the acceleration values would converge to zero. More experiments were initially planned, but
as the computational power available caused unforeseen challenges, the experiments performed
in the virtual environment had a small change in purpose to enable the use of the experiments
for investigations of the covariance method.
4.4.2
Usability for Investigating the Covariance Method
As each stage of the pipe is simulated in a perfect environment, the analysis in ANSYS is only
performed once for each stage. This induce DIs which do not scatter in the same way as the
physical lab experiments.
A way to make the virtual environment more comparable to reality (lab experiments), is to
simulate an imperfect environment. This can be done with diямАerent means; e.g. small changes
of force value for each impulse could provide a scatter more comparable to what would happen
in reality. Simulation of rubber bands with small changes of stiямАness could do the same trick.
These means however will require many more runs of analysisтАЩ in the program in question (in
this case ANSYS). Another way to spread the results of DIs in each stage to simulate reality
better could be to add noise to the output, e.g. Monte Carlo simulation.
The virtual experiments performed for this project, can not stand alone when investigating
diямАerent parametersтАЩ inямВuence on the DIs as damage indicators. However, it can support or
challenge the theories made from the lab experiment and FRF from above. Also, as it is a
controlled environment material properties can be changed, and the investigations of this can
suggest which parameters to be aware of and thereby indicate which materials that will be most
applicable to the covariance method.

5.4
Discussion of Results
In this chapter, the results of three experiment types have seen investigated: The test blade
experiment performed previously by Tcherniak, the experiments performed in the lab at DTU
and the experiments performed in a virtual environment. The purpose of the investigations
diямАers from experiment to experiment.
Investigating the reproduced DIs from the test blade experiment was mainly done to validate
the signal processing created for this project. However, as the secondary damage indicators was
mentioned by Tcherniak in [5], the test blade experiment also served a purpose of investigating
the inямВuence of computing the secondary DIs.
The main purpose of performing experiments on the pipe in the lab was to test the covariance
method on a simple structure and to investigate whether the covariance method could be applied
on the test set-up and used for damage detection. As this was established, the investigation of
the inямВuence of measuring accelerations in diямАerent directions was desired, as it was suspected
to show a big diямАerence when computing the DIs. In the lab experiments, no assumptions are
required, so the outcome of the covariance method is a direct indicator of what actually happens
in the structure. However, as the mass of the accelerometers and actuator combined almost cor-
respond to 1/3 of the pipe mass, this might have an inямВuence on the structural behaviour of the
pipe. For this reason, the inямВuence of inямВicting damage might be more easily detected if reducing
the relation between the mass of the measuring equipment and the mass of the structure.
The results obtained in the lab showed that a magnitude of DIs could obtain the same value as
the noise from other experiments, which makes the covariance method very sensitive to external
noise. This is important knowledge when using the method for SHM, as the surrounding envir-
onment can have a great eямАect on the outcome of the DIs. This surrounding environment is not
simulated in ANSYS, for which reason it can be diямГcult to know from the virtual experiments
what will beneямБt the usefulness of the covariance method the most, just by analysing the results
from the virtual experiments.
As mentioned in Section 4.4.2, the virtual experiments cannot stand alone when investigating
diямАerent parametersтАЩ inямВuence on the DIs as damage indicators. However, it can support or
challenge the theories made from the lab experiment. This was done by comparison with the
results obtained in the lab, and investigations of the coherence between the FRFs and the DIs.
The latter are more simple in a virtual environment, as each stage of damage only consists
of one sample of data compared to the lab experiments, in which 100 samples were used for
each stage. Also, as it is a controlled environment material properties can be changed and as a
comparison can be made to the reference DIs, the investigations in the virtual environment can
be used suggest which parameters that aямАect the outcome of the covariance method the most,
when changing material.

6 Conclusion
The aim of this project has been to systematically investigate the parameters which inямВuence the
outcome of the covariance method for detecting damage. It has been important throughout this
project to investigate what can provide the best damage indication when using the covariance
method for structural health monitoring (SHM) and damage detection.
The methods used for the investigations are experiments performed in a laboratory and in a
virtual environment created in ANSYS, plus the signal processing of the obtained data. The
experiments performed in the lab and the virtual experiments served diямАerent purposes for the
investigations.
In the lab it was possible to reckon in the inямВuence of the surrounding en-
vironment and how much the computed damage indices (DIs) scatter in each test stage as a
consequence of this. This surrounding environment was not simulated in ANSYS which enabled
the investigations of changing material parameters in a controlled and systematic way in the
virtual environment. Also the virtual experiments served as a comparative investigation to the
physical experiments.
From the lab experiments, it was discovered that the direction and position of the measured
accelerations have a great inямВuence on the DIs. Also it was found that ямБltering the accelerations
for the generated DIs had a great impact on the outcome.
Of the investigated parameters
which inямВuence the DIs, there is no clear tendency of what provides the best DIs for damage
detection. However, investigations of the lab experiments show that measuring in both axial
and perpendicular directions can be beneямБcial for the damage indication, especially in frequency
range (FR) [800;1600], where a diямАerence between all three types of DIs (general, perpendicular
and axial) is most clear.
From the noise test and the scatter of DIs in each test stage in the lab experiments it can be con-
cluded that the covariance method is sensitive to the surrounding environment. This sensitivity
can be a great problem when doing SHM, as surrounding noise can indicate damage or pos-
sibly hide an important indication of damage. The latter is not investigated in this thesis, as the
experiments were performed in a relatively silent lab to reduce the external inямВuence on the DIs.
Both lab experiments and virtual environment support the theory from the results of the test
blade experiment, that computing the DI by the second highest singular value of the matrix
(Qref тИТQy,i) can provide a more clear damage indicator than using the 2-norm, which origin-
ally is used by Parker and Frazier [1]. However, this should be used as a supplement when dong
SHM, not as a substitution. The results from both lab and virtual experiments also support the
hypothesis that the positions of the measured accelerations have an inямВuence on the generated
DIs, and that accelerations close to the damage constitute better damage indicators.
The virtual experiments showed that it has some eямАect on the DIs, whether the material prop-
erties are changed. E.g. changing the YoungтАЩs modulus showed to have an inямВuence on the DIs
in the FR [800;1600], but not a signiямБcant inямВuence in the other FRs. The biggest diямАerence
from the reference DIs is found when having pure mass proportional damping (╬▓ = 0, ╬▒ ╠╕= 0),
especially in the higher FRs. From that investigation it seems that a smaller value of ╬▓ will
provide a better damage indication within these FRs.

CHAPTER 6. CONCLUSION
In general it is hard to give just one suggestion of what provides the most clear damage indica-
tion, when using the covariance method for SHM. All in all it is suspected that each structure
should be tested beforehand for choosing the best directions and positions of measurements, plus
frequency range which provides the most clear damage indication. This is especially important
if the output is monitored by a human operator, as there are limitations to how many graphs
a human can monitor at the same time. This can be automatized by e.g. choosing the highest
value of DI in the reference stage and making the program give notice when DIs of higher values
occur. In this way the DIs can be computed by any suggested signal processing and still be
manageable for a human operator.
To further investigate the covariance method it is suggested for more lab experiments to be
performed using structures of diямАerent geometry, dimensions and materials, probably corres-
ponding to the structure desired for SHM. Or, the lab experiments could be simpliямБed even
further using fewer accelerometers on e.g. a simple beam. It would also be interesting to invest-
igate the covariance method in a virtual environment, which is programmed to simulate external
inямВuences.
