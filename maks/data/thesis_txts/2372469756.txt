Abstract
This work aimed to compute and visualize the response of Oscillating Water Col-
umn (OWC) Wave Energy Devices (WECs) by means of a generalized modes de-
scription of the water motion inside the chamber, in order to study the wave pattern
generated by the OWC.
Firstly, a fixed double-chamber OWC was studied in the frequency domain, and
the wave pattern around the body was visualized. Sloshing effects were included in the
WAMIT simulations and compared with the existing experimental data. Moreover,
the effect of nonlinear external damping on the responses was studied in the time-
domain analysis.
The responses derived with time-domain calculations were in a
better agreement with the experimental data than the frequency-domain results. The
idea of replacing the bi-directional air turbine with a conventional turbine was studied
in the time domain and indicated that a conventional turbine yields higher energy
absorption near the resonance.
In a similar manner to the fixed body, a 23 degree-of-freedom floating model was
analyzed using the generalized modes method and the wave field was characterized.
A platform is assumed behind the structure and the change of the wave amplitude
is computed.The calculations suggested that the OWC can generate almost 50% de-
crease in the wave amplitude for short waves and approximately a 10% decrease in
long waves.
Finally, an array of three full models with lateral separations was considered, and
the changes in the wave height were computed for a specified domain behind the array.
In this analysis, the two additional OWCs were defined as images of the central body
so that the generalized modes responses of the images were the same as the central
attenuator. It was seen that the largest energy absorption was found when the OWC
structures were placed with a separation distance of 1/3 of their length.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Climate change is arguably the most important issue that our world is facing today.
Given the current population growth rate, the consequent increase in energy demand
will require more renewable energy. Although in recent decades a large amount of re-
search has been carried out on sustainable energy, further developments are necessary
to enable large-scale production of low-cost renewable energy.
Ocean waves are a significant energy resource and the potential for extracting
energy from the waves is considerable. Waves are produced by wind action and are
therefore an indirect form of solar energy. The assessment of this huge resource is
not easy. Ocean waves exhibit strong variability at all time scales (e.g. from wave
to wave, sea state to sea state, season to season and year to year), thus requiring
detailed data sets of long-term time series.
Innovative ideas for wave power conversion have been developed over the last
three decades, resulting in thousands of patents in recent years.
However, wave
energy is relatively immature compared with other energy technologies. At present,
a number of different wave energy concepts are being investigated by companies and
academic research groups around the world. Although many working designs have
been developed and tested through modeling and wave tank tests, only a few concepts
have been tested at sea.
1.1
Oscillating Water Columns
Oscillating water column (OWC) devices contain a pneumatic chamber where there
is an opening at the immersed bottom. The internal free surface is surrounded by
the rigid structure of the chamber and air is trapped above the internal free surface.
The incident waves result in oscillations of the water column which drive the air back
and forth through an air turbine functioning as the power-take-off mechanism (PTO).
The energy extraction from the oscillating air column is completed by a self rectifying
air turbine for example a Wells turbine.
In order to model the air turbine in the laboratory, an orifice is created in the top
of the chamber. By varying the size of the orifice, the damping induced by the viscous
losses associated with the orifice can be adjusted to match the turbine characteristics
at full scale.
==PAGE:18==
2
1 Introduction
In the last decades, considerable efforts have been made to exploit the energy of
the ocean waves. The low frequency of the motion, of the order 10−1 Hz, provides
a challenge to the designers to convert this motion into electrical energy with a fre-
quency of 50 Hz. The OWC, with its use of high speed air turbine, is a type of wave
energy converter (WEC) that has been studied extensively and seeks to avoid this
problem. [14]
OWC devices can be built on shorelines in bottom–fixed models, near shore in
either bottom–fixed or floating structures, or in offshore sites as floating OWCs. The
first use of a floating OWC is recorded as a whistling buoy used for navigational
aids. In the 1970s, a significant development was made by Masuda [20] in Japan who
designed the OWC using an air turbine to generate electricity. Kaimei was a ship-
like structure noted for its relatively easy construction and good energy conversion
efficiency. Moreover, from tests in the wave tank, it was confirmed that the ship-
shaped structure is more suitable for mooring than other buoys. The floating OWC
devices are slackly moored to the sea bed and free to oscillate.
Full sized bottom standing OWC wave energy converters have been built in Japan
(Sakata, 1990), India (Vizhinjam, 1990), Scotland (OSPREY, 1995), Portugal (Pico,
1999), and UK (the LIMPET, 2000). A common problem reported from the recent
full-scale models was the difficulty of construction. OSPREY, the largest of all the
mentioned devices was destroyed, and sank near the Scottish coast. Thorough reviews
of OWC technologies are presented by CarbonTrust[1], Heath [19], Falcão[3], and
Drew et al [13].
1.2
Purpose and Objectives
Wave-structure interaction is an important field in marine hydrodynamics. Extreme
waves are considered as one of the main loading sources on oil and gas platforms. Off-
shore wind turbines are another type of marine structure where wave loads influence
both the design load and the fatigue life. In both cases, near to the free surface, the
wave forces may reach to their maximum values, exposing the platforms to extreme
impact forces. The impact force can result in serious damage to the wind turbine
and oil platform substructures. Moreover, high waves restrict access to the platform
for operation and maintenance processes. Wave energy converters are a group of ma-
rine structures which extract or absorb incoming wave energy and this is a potential
solution to the aforementioned problems.
Recent work by Bingham et al.[12], numerically modelled and experimentally mea-
sured the OWC internal pressure and surface elevation of both a fixed single degree
of freedom OWC and an I-beam attenuator OWC device with 26 degrees of freedom.
The comparison showed broad agreement between the experimental and numerical
models.
==PAGE:19==
1.3 Content of this work
3
In this thesis, the difference between the experimental results and the numerical
analysis noted in [12] was the motivation to investigate the sloshing effect and the
finite width of the tank used in the laboratory. Moreover, it is attempted to analyze
the device performance in the time domain with a nonlinear air pressure on the water
column. Once the mathematical problem is established, the last and the clearest goal
of the present work is to investigate the wave field exerted by the OWC chambers
behind the structure. This could give a better idea of how to deploy the WEC to
reduce the load on the desired offshore platform.
1.3
Content of this work
This report consists of four sections. Chapter 2 provides an overview of the OWC
system. A theoretical background of the OWC and its equation of motion in both
time- and frequency-domains are presented. Chapter 3 introduces the numerical im-
provements implemented to study a fixed double chamber OWC and their validation
via the experimental data, as well as the generated wave field behind the fixed device.
In the time-domain analysis, first, the WEC is studied with both linear and nonlinear

2.1
Frequency Domain Analysis
In the frequency domain, to solve the linear hydrodynamic problem in OWCs, several
approaches have been developed. In the early theories, rigid body models were used,
both for two and three dimensional problems, to predict the trapped air pressure and
the water column elevation (Evans.1976 [17]). Using this approach, the internal free
surface is considered to be a massless rigid body with zero thickness. Here, the air
pressure inside the chamber is only coupled with the mean free surface motion or the
piston displacement. It is assumed that the chamber width is smaller than the incident
wavelength, and the spatial variation, at least for long waves, is ignored. Later, the
theory was developed by Falcão and Sarmento [2] and generalized by Evans.1981 [18],
for a non–plane internal wave surface by providing a model to obtain the increased
pressure of air for a fixed rigid body. A further generalization was made for moving
rigid bodies enclosing an internal free surface by Falnes and McIver [11].
Relatively, the physical meaning of the former method is more obvious compared
with the other. Thus, in this work the massless rigid body approach is applied in
both the WAMIT calculations and post-processing analysis. Linear potential theory
is used to compute the hydrodynamic coefficients of the structure and the Haskind
relations are applied to determine the wave exciting force. [10].

The hydrodynamic interaction of the OWC and the fluid is described by linear poten-
tial theory where the flow is assumed to be inviscid and irrotational. The body and
free surface boundary conditions are satisfied to the first order. The velocity field is
represented by the gradient of the velocity potential, satisfying Laplace’s equation,
∇2ϕ = 0, where ϕ is the frequency domain velocity potential of the flow around the
structure.
An earth-fixed Cartesian coordinates system is defined with x and y in the horizon-
tal plane on the calm water surface, and z pointing upward in the positive direction.
The rigid body degrees of freedom of an OWC device with respect to its equilibrium
position will be denoted as ξk = ( ξ1, ξ2, ξ3, ξ4, ξ5, ξ6), corresponding to surge, sway,
heave, roll, pitch and yaw, respectively. For the fixed OWC, the responses associated
with the six degrees of freedom are zero.
In contrast, for the floating OWC, the
structure is free to translate and rotate.
In addition, the motion of the internal
chamber adds generalized modes. The main one contributing to generating the power
is the piston mode parallel to heave, denoted as ξ7.

This chapter provides a brief description of the scaled double-chamber OWC used

The numerical results obtained by WAMIT are validated by the data from the
laboratory tests for regular waves presented in [8]. The simulations with WAMIT

Solutions have also been obtained

It has been suggested by people working with OWC devices that more power can
actually be extracted by applying the air turbine only over one half of the oscillation
cycle instead of using the complete cycle [15]. This strategy allows for the use of a

bi-directional turbine. To investigate this idea, an OWC system with a uni-directional
turbine is also considered. The performance of the new model is numerically analyzed,
both in the frequency and time domains, and compared with the device in which a
two-way turbine is employed.

3.6 OWC Performance with a One-way Turbine

3.6
OWC Performance with a One-way Turbine
A Wells or impulse turbine rotates in a single direction in an oscillating airflow and
is therefore self-rectifying and does not require a system of rectifying valves. Further-
more, this turbine is one of the simplest and probably the most economical turbines
for wave energy conversion. However, according to previous studies, such turbines
have inherent disadvantages: low efficiency at high flow coefficient and for the Wells
turbine, poor starting characteristics in comparison with conventional turbines be-
cause of a severe stall [16].
A possible solution for these drawbacks could be to replace the self-rectifying air
turbine with a conventional turbine which only works when the pressurized air escapes
the chamber via the turbine or the lowered water level, drives the air to the chamber.
Then, for the second half of the wave period, when the turbine is idle, the air will
flow freely in or out via a venting system.
To model the performance of the OWC with a one-way turbine, the frequency
and time domain analysis, have been carried out and compared to the case using a
self-rectifying turbine. Note that the linearized relation of the damping coefficient
will be modified as the turbine is only functioning for half of the period. Eq.(2.16) is
again used where the time average period is either from 0 to T/2 or T/2 to T. Hence,
the linear relation for the external damping coefficient in this case is modified to:

4.1
The KNswing Attenuator
The KNswing WEC is an attenuator OWC consisting of 40 chambers, inspired by
the Kaimei project by Masuda [20]. The power conversion system of this machine
requires further study. As shown in the previous chapter, the PTO mechanism can
be a self-rectifying turbine, a conventional turbine absorbing the power only for half a
period, or a larger turbine connected to all the chambers by rectifying valves. Model
tests have been conducted by Pors and Simonson [9] which assessed the performance

by modeling the air turbine with an open orifice. Ducasse [6] and Bingham et al.[12],
numerically modelled the internal pressure using calculations based on WAMIT and
compared to the experimental results from [9]. From these early works, it can be
concluded that the capture width ratio of the KNswing attenuator is satisfactory.
Therefore, further studies are needed to fully develop the concept.

are the same as described for the double-chamber discussed in Chapter 3, Fig.3.1. The
floating OWC, in contrast to the fixed model, has 26 degrees of freedom including
the six rigid body motions and 20 piston modes, and exploiting symmetry along the
centerline of the hull so that the sway (ξ2), roll (ξ4) and yaw(ξ6) responses can be
ignored. The experimental model of the KNswing attenuator is presented in Figs.4.1a

CHAPTER 5
An Infinite Array of
Floating OWCs
Deploying multiple wave energy converters as wave farms will lead to considerable
advantages in terms of economical aspects. If the wave farms, considering the inter-
action of the converters, are well designed, higher power absorption can be expected
than a single wave energy converter installed at the same area. Moreover, there would
be significant cost reduction in the power transmission by developing a grid connec-
tion hub. However, the spacing between the WECs should be planned carefully to
sufficiently utilize the wave energy in the site and reduce the cost of electrical ca-
bling and maintenance. Thus, the number of WECs in an array is affected by all the
aforementioned factors.
Within the scope of this work, an infinite array of WECs is analyzed where the
devices are defined as images for the floating OWC located at the center. Thus, the
hydrodynamic interaction is defined such that the water column elevation inside the
chambers of the image bodies are the same as the central body in the array.
Since the WAMIT calculations of the full model described in the last chapter are
computationally demanding, the geometry model has been simplified. This simplifi-
cation will be described briefly in the first section. With the simplified geometry, the
memory and time needed for WAMIT calculations are reduced substantially, allowing
calculations to be performed for small arrays of floating OWCs.
First, the performance of the isolated device is analyzed according to its capture
width ratio and compared with the original model. Having established the simplified
geometry model, an array of three floating bodies with different inter-spacing is con-
sidered. The wave pattern around the array and the change of the wave height are
also studied.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
This Master thesis describes numerical simulations performed to understand the wave
pattern around a fixed, double-chamber OWC, a 40 chamber floating full model, and
an array of three floating OWCs. In addition, important information regarding the
power absorption characteristics of single and multiple chambers has been produced.
As a part of this work, it is attempted to reproduce the previously-obtained laboratory
results corresponding to the fixed single chamber model with scale of 1 : 50.
The air pressure inside the chamber is computed with a set of cosine-shaped gen-
eralized modes. The main mode contributing to the power extraction from the water
column is regarded as the displacement of the internal water surface parallel to heave,
known as the piston mode. To model numerically the single chamber inside the tank
in the frequency domain, the transverse sloshing mode as the second generalized mode
is also introduced, together with an array of images for the single chamber to capture
the finite width of the utilized flume. With the image bodies, the walls of the flume
are modelled resulting in standing waves inside the channel. The sloshing inside the
both chamber and the flume is predicted in the computed responses in piston and
sloshing modes. The experiments and the numerical calculations with sixteen images
were compared.
However, still a discrepancy between the two sets of results was
noted.
Given the hydrodynamic coefficients in the frequency-domain, the time-domain
analysis was performed for the generalized-mode responses. The external PTO damp-
ing coefficient in the time domain is computed with both linear and nonlinear rela-
tions. The comparison between the test and the nonlinear time-domain simulations
revealed a satisfactory agreement in the piston mode responses. However, for the
sloshing mode responses, large relative differences were observed.
The performance of the OWC with both bi-directional and uni-directional air
turbines was studied. Using a one-way turbine resulted in an increase both in the
piston mode responses and the capture width ratio near the resonance wave period
of the OWC.
The wave pattern around an array of three fixed OWCs was also analyzed and the
changes in the wave height in a domain behind the array was computed. The wave
pattern and the changes of the wave height around a floating OWC with 40 chambers
was simulated. The sloshing effect inside the chambers on the piston mode response
==PAGE:86==
70

was examined and no critical changes in the responses were seen. Furthermore, the
same analysis was performed for the attenuator with double the size of the original
model. This modification had the advantage of increasing the wave periods where
the OWC is effective at absorbing wave energy.
To simulate the wave field exerted by a small array of the floating OWCs, first
the geometry model was simplified in an attempt to decrease the panels to save
computational time. An array of three bodies was analyzed with the new geometry
file under the assumption that these three bodies approximate an infinite array, where
the generalized mode responses are identical in all three OWCs. Regarding the tested
lateral distances between the attenuators, the highest power absorption was obtained
by an array with 50 m inter-spacing (1/3 of the WEC length).
Further investigations could focus on the improvement of the numerical model by:
• Finding the best approach to solve the singularity problem in computing the
infinite frequency added mass for a time-domain analysis,
• Considering both lengthwise and transverse sloshing modes inside the chambers
of the floating OWC,
• Studying the array of the floating OWCs with both piston and sloshing modes.
• Analyzing the array where each attenuator responds independently to the waves
In summary, the acquired experience and numerical results in this project can be
used in the future development of new and more precise models as well as experimental
setups.
