Abstract
The study quantifies the efficiency of Greater Copenhagen’s public transport system service
among different population groups and identifies both spatial and demographic disparities
in accessibility. Integrating the Danish TU Travel Survey with General Transit Feed
Specification (GTFS) OD matrices and zone-level accessibility scores enables the linkage of
observed trip patterns to income, car ownership, and distance from stations. Spatial cluster
analysis reveals zones where low-income, car-less residents face systematically poor access,
distinguishing in general transport exclusion from self-selected travel choices. Combined
with results from equity-weighted regressions three patterns emerge: (1) Participation
deserts are concentrated in the outer-western suburbs (Høje-Taastrup, Ishøj), where
47% lower accessibility coincides with 0.8 fewer daily activities than in the central belt.
(2) When car availability and accessibility are controlled for, household income loses
significance—indicating that mobility resources, rather than income itself, mediate exclusion
in accordance with existing literature. (3) Accessibility shows a threshold effect: zones
with scores below 0.4 on the normalized index do not lead to more trips, while zones with
scores above 0.6 tend to reinforce existing travel patterns instead of creating new ones.
The results highlight areas of potential exclusion and offer practical recommendations and
guidelines for transport research to explore in depth and promote social inclusion in a
car-dependent society.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Transport systems are actively shaping the opportunity landscape that can be either
inclusive or exclusive for different population groups. Over the past two decades, research
and studies have bridged this argument through the aspect of transport justice [1, 2].
Highlighting the importance of evaluating fairness of transport through the people’s ability
to participate in daily activities rather than only checking average connectivity [3]. Despite
this progress in research there is still lack of empirical understanding on the way that
exclusion occurs, what segments of the population are mostly affected, if there is a specific
pattern on the location that exclusion is met and what are the key factors to drive these
inequalities.
Especially in Nordic countries where high aggregate provision can hide
local-level disparities
Greater Copenhagen illustrates this tension well. Although the region is accounted to offer
one of the Europe’s densest public transport networks, recent research prove that 37% of
S-train platforms and 45% of bus stops still do not meet basic universal design standards
[4]. Simultaneously, peripheral municipalities tend to experience in general significantly
lower job accessibility compared with the urban core [5, 6]. These gaps contribute to an
ongoing car-dependent culture among the population - especially low-income parts of them-
and highlight that lack of accessibility can limit participation in essential activities [7]
Therefore, it is crucial to understand the reasons and the geography of this exclusion
in order to develop transport policies that promote equal access to social life for all the
residents.
The study attempts to bridge this challenge by combining research perspectives that
have often been studied separately focusing on the Greater Copenhagen Region. First,
distinguishing horizontal and vertical equity [8] [9] to clarify that accessibility is not a supply
indicator. Secondly, through the observation of social exclusion and inclusion literature in
transport that is connected to reduced mobility and participation [10, 11], and using trip
frequency and activity diversity as indicators for inclusion. Lastly, the study applies the
accessibility–activity participation framework [3] to identify participation deserts—areas
where observed activity levels are lower than expected given local accessibility—and to
examine whether these areas align with sociodemographic and spatial disadvantages in
Copenhagen.
In terms of methodology, the study constructs the first micro-spatial, schedule-based
accessibility layer for Greater Copenhagen by combining General Transit Feed Specification
(GTFS) data with the Danish National Travel Survey (TU) from 2020–2024. Network-based
Dijkstra’s algorithm on zone to zone matrices is used to address the coverage bias typically
1
==PAGE:12==
found in stop-to-stop GTFS data network [12]. Based on this network, a gravity-based
accessibility indicator is measured to estimate the number of opportunities that each zone
can reach on 45 minutes. The number of opportunities is addressed by the number of public
transport stations from origin zone to destination zone. The measure is later included in
an individual-level modeling, where input on household structure of the person as well as
income, car ownership, age, gender, and mobility impairments are derived from TU data.
The marginal effect of accessibility in daily trips is then estimated controlling for spatial
and socio-demographic factors through a multivariate count model.
The analysis consists of two stages:
1. Spatial hot-spot detection.
A Getis-Ord G∗
i statistic is computed for each of the 921 traffic-analysis zones and
then filtered in the target area, identifying areas where residents engage in fewer
out-of-home activities.
2. Vertical-equity regression.
Regression modeling then tests whether accessibility still has a significant association
with daily trip frequency after controlling for vertical-equity variable such as income,
car ownership, age, gender, and mobility limitations and thereby separating transport
exclusion from personal travel preferences.
Scope of the project
The scope of this project is to explore how accessibility is distributed across the Greater
Copenhagen Region and to understand how it relates to social inclusion in everyday
mobility. The study aims to identify whether there are existing population groups and
areas that experience limited access to opportunities and to explore the factors driving
these inequalities. By combining accessibility measures with individual travel behavior
data, the analysis tries to uncover spatial patterns of exclusion and evaluate the role of
income, car ownership, and public transport availability. The scope of the research includes
both the spatial and socio-demographic dimensions of accessibility, providing evidence that
can support fairer and more inclusive transport planning in the region.
Research Questions of the project
1. What are the key factors that influence the relationship between accessibility and
transportation equity in public transit?
2. How equitable is the current public transportation system in terms of access for
different socio-economic groups in Greater Copenhagen?
3. What policy interventions could improve public transport accessibility in undeserved
areas?

Chapter 2
Background
2.1
Problem Statement
Accessibility is a central concept in transport research, as it captures the ease with which
people can reach opportunities such as jobs, education, healthcare, and social activities.
Since Hansen’s early formulation of accessibility as the “potential of opportunities for
interaction” [13], a large body of literature has refined and expanded the concept [14].
propose a widely used framework distinguishing between land-use, transport, temporal,
and individual components of accessibility.
Beyond measuring opportunities, accessibility is closely tied to questions of equity and
justice. Martens [1] argues that transport systems should not only maximize efficiency
but also distribute opportunities fairly across social groups. This perspective connects
accessibility with broader concerns about transport-related social exclusion, where limited
access to mobility restricts individuals’ ability to participate fully in society [10, 15].
Recent studies have increasingly linked accessibility with participation in activities. Pot,
Heinen, and Tillema [16] show that both objective and perceived accessibility shape activity
participation across spatial contexts, while Mehdizadeh et al. [17] highlight how accessibility
can affect life satisfaction through participation. These findings underline the importance
of connecting accessibility metrics with observed travel behaviour.
In the Danish and European context, several studies have assessed accessibility patterns,
but often with a focus on aggregate indicators such as average travel times or connectivity
at the regional level [6]. Kaplan [5] demonstrates that connectivity-based measures can be
used to evaluate equity in public transport provision in Greater Copenhagen, showing clear
differences between population groups. However, few studies have integrated accessibility
measures with survey-based activity participation data, leaving a gap in understanding the
distributional impacts of transport provision in Denmark.
This thesis addresses that gap by linking accessibility measures derived from public transport
network data (GTFS) with travel behavior and socio-demographic information from the
Danish National Travel Survey (TU). In doing so, it contributes to the literature on
transport equity by focusing on how accessibility interacts with income and car ownership
to shape opportunities for daily participation in Greater Copenhagen.

2.2
Regional Info
The study is focused in Greater Copenhagen metropolitan region, covering the Capital
Region and Region Zealand (≈2.8 million inhabitants)—which functions as an integrated
labor market connected to southern Sweden via the Øresund fixed link [18].
Public-
transport governance is multi-layered: while national governments have the responsibility
for financing and regulating rail infrastructure, regional authorities such as Movia in
Denmark and Skånetrafiken in Sweden integrate bus and local-rail services. In parallel,
state-owned companies such as Metroselskabet and Hovedstadens Letbane manage the
development of the driver-less metro and the forthcoming Ring 3 light-rail (27 km, opened
in 2025), under boards that include the Danish state, Copenhagen and Frederiksberg
municipalities, and 11 surrounding councils. This arrangement has been described as a
hybrid “decentralized-contracting” arrangement that preserves municipal influence over
land-use and station-area development [19].
Recent network extensions—including the Cityringen M3, M4 South Harbour line and
the under-construction light-rail—have been financed through land-value capture and
municipal cost-sharing formulas, with surpluses from metro redevelopment projects ( DKK 5.8 billion in North Harbour) reinvested into further expansion. This has created a
self-reinforcing cycle where transport infrastructure both drives and benefits from urban
growth. While accessibility standards are high on new infrastructure—metro stations are
fully step-free and 99 % of trains run on time—persistent barriers remain. Audits indicate
that 37 % of S-train platforms and 45 % of bus stops fail to meet minimum wheelchair
accessibility standards [4], and fare integration across the Danish–Swedish border remains
incomplete. Moreover, spatial inequalities persist: although the metro expansion improved
network centrality by 7 %, peripheral municipalities such as Frederikssund continue to face
limited connectivity [20]. Area-based accessibility analysis shows that residents of western
Region Zealand can access fewer than 20 % of the jobs reachable by inner-Copenhagen
commuters within 45 minutes by public transport [21]. Perceived accessibility is also lower
among elderly passengers and those with functional limitations, particularly on Øresund
services, where vertical gaps and inconsistent real-time information are major barriers
[22]. Consequently, high-capacity, efficient services remain concentrated in the urban
core, and the distributional impacts of future accessibility investments on low-income and
mobility-restricted populations are still poorly understood—an evidence gap this study
addresses [23].

Chapter 3
Literature
The systematic review of previous publications and research provides the foundation
for understanding the current state of knowledge on the project’s topic. This review
examines theories and models relevant to the study, focusing on the importance of equity in
transport, the definition of accessibility, and the concepts of social exclusion and inclusion.
By comparing and contrasting these perspectives continuously but also reviewing several
approaches the objectives and scope of the project are formed.
This chapter outlines both the procedure followed in conducting the review and the main
insights derived from it. The search primarily emphasized literature published after 2014,
while also including earlier influential works where necessary to capture key conceptual
developments. No strict geographical limitations are applied; however, a significant portion
of the relevant studies originated from Nordic contexts and European, where similar
approaches and case studies provided useful points of comparison .
3.1
Theoretical Foundations
Equity by definition is related to justice and fair distribution of goods in society. Lewis [24]
explored the connection between different existing theories of justice from social psychology,
philosophy and economics that have been applied in transport research and practice over
the past decades.
3.1.1
Transport Equity
Transport equity can be translated into the fair distribution of benefits and disadvantages
among all groups of different population groups. The main idea is to ensure that everyone
has a reasonable chance to reach different kinds of daily activities like jobs, school, healthcare,
leisure [2, 8]. An important consideration is to examine whether any group is unfairly
burdened by the system and to assess potential risks it may create [25]. No population
should carry a disproportionate share of the costs, risks, or exclusions associated with
transport. This includes ensuring that negative impacts such as expenses, pollution, safety
hazards, and social exclusion are distributed fairly [8]. The most dominant frames in
transport equity are vertical and horizontal.
• Horizontal Equity
According to Litman [9] the term refers to ”equal treatment among equals”, where
individuals that belong to groups that share common characteristics like mobility
5
==PAGE:16==
needs or income they get the same transport advantages. More precise, in transport
studies people that live in comparable locations should have equally accessible service.
• Vertical Equity
Recognizing that not all groups have equal needs, opportunities, or capabilities [24],
transport justice requires differentiated planning and policy responses. In this context,
vertical equity emphasizes that fairness is achieved not through identical treatment
but through consideration of structural differences across populations. This indicates
that design in transport policies and systems should be done with respect to income
and social class, as well as individual needs and abilities, [9] to ensure that people
within these groups are not disproportionately disadvantaged.
The study of equity in transportation extends beyond policies and resource availability, as
it is a multidimensional outcome shaped by various factors. These factors are often closely
linked to the specific variables chosen for analysis [8].
3.1.2
Social Inclusion
The concept of social exclusion has become a central theme in transport studies over the
past decades, focusing on how unequally distributed access to goods, services, and essential
activities can lead to exclusion, particularly among disadvantaged or less privileged groups
defined by income, ethnicity, gender, or age. Limited transport resources, along with
exposure to air and noise pollution, have been shown to reduce quality of life—conditions
especially prevalent in urban areas. According to Lucas [26], limited access to employment,
services, and key activities defines social isolation and exclusion, while negative externalities
of road traffic, such as accidents and pollution, further decrease well-being. Transport
inequalities are therefore closely linked to broader social disadvantage, as certain groups
face greater risks and health burdens arising from unequal transport service.
3.1.3
Transport Accessibility
As mentioned by Di Ciommo and Lucas [25] transport accessibility studies primarily
focus on developing measures that assess both equality and inequality of access to the
transport system, while at the same time emphasizing access to the key life activities and
opportunities that such systems enable. Attraction and opportunities through accessibility
can be grouped and measured into two key categories. The first is mobility, defined as the
ease of movement, often measured by the area a person can reach within a given time using
available modes. And the second refers to people’s ability to to reach key destinations
such as education, work or any other activities improving resident well being [14]. Both
dimensions are shaped by individual resources, abilities, and perceptions of safety or
comfort, which may limit the extent to which transport systems can be effectively used [7].
Accessibility can be linked to housing geography of people, spatial, or their income rate,
financial [27]. Approaching spatial accessibility often involves measuring travel time or
distance to public transport services or to activity destination [25] By contrast, financial
6
==PAGE:17==
accessibility focuses on the share of income that households must spend on transport,
with affordability identified as a major determinant of transport poverty [25] According
to Martens et al. [8], accessibility can be assessed through various indicators related to
resources, outcomes, opportunities, and well-being. These include measures such as car
ownership and public transport availability, often disaggregated by factors like income,
gender, or age. Table A.6 in the appendix summaries the four core dimensions used to
measure transport equity same as Lucas and Jones [11] they distinguish three scales on
these dimensions the micro level, which relates to individual circumstances; the meso level,
which concerns neighborhood or local area characteristics; and the macro level, which refers
to broader strategic or system-wide factors As they argue that a failure at any of these
levels can disrupt accessibility and, if sustained, result in social exclusion.
3.1.4
Transport Affordability
Transport affordability describes the percentage of household income that must be spent on
commute around activities. While transport spending generally increases with income, con-
sistent with transport functioning as a normal good, it often consumes a disproportionately
large share of the budgets of low-income households [28].This means that mobility can
become a financial strain for disadvantaged groups, even when higher-income households
spend more in absolute terms. The built environment ,particularly the location that the
household is, has also a key role in this condition. For example, dense areas with good
public transport service and walkable design most of the times are connected to reduced
car dependence and overall costs [29]. On the other hand areas with poor job–housing
balance tend to have long trip distances and greater reliance on cars, a thing that increases
the financial pressure(fuel cost,maintenance, insurance) but also indirectly considering the
time traveling on road. Since travel is a derived demand, need for people to reach key
activities, affordability can also improve when the need for long journeys is reduced [30].
Both time and money matter—different groups experience different trade-offs depending on
their income and available time [3]. Policies that shorten trips, expand transit, and support
mixed-use neighborhoods are especially helpful for low- and middle-income households,
making transport more equitable.

3.2.3
Research
The literature search is conducted across several academic databases and online platforms.
The primary sources included DTU Findit, Research Gate, Scopus, and Google Scholar,
which provided peer-reviewed journal articles and conference papers in transport field.
Additionally, specialized databases such as Science Direct are consulted for transportation
and social science literature. Reports and working papers from organizations such as
Ministry of Transport for Denmark (Transportministeriet), and European Commission
mobility initiatives are also reviewed to capture relevant policy perspectives.

In addition to database searches, the literature review also followed an author-based
approach. Influential scholars in the field of transport equity and social exclusion, most

3.2.4
Methodological Inspiration
The methodological framework is mainly inspired by three key studies that incorporate
social equity aspects into transport analysis. Each of them is thoroughly proofread and
analyzed in depth, focusing on their methods and approaches, as well as the similarities in
research scope to this study.
Planning transport for social inclusion: An accessibility-activity participation
approach, Allen and Farber, 2020 [3]
This paper formed a major part of the study, as its approach is particularly interesting
and introduced a new concept compared to previous research, participation deserts. It
is based on a regional context, using a large-sample travel survey from the Greater
Toronto and Hamilton Area to define participation deserts—neighborhoods where activity
participation is lower than expected given local accessibility levels. This term added a
behavioral dimension to the analysis by linking accessibility with the actual level of activity
participation observed across neighborhoods. The authors then applied multivariate models
to better understand which social groups are most likely to benefit from improvements in
transit accessibility, while also identifying areas at higher risk of social exclusion. Their
research is not only oriented toward increasing participation in daily activities but also
toward finding where accessibility investments can most effectively prevent and reduce
existing inequalities within the population.
Modeling the relationship between travel behaviors and social disadvantage,
Lucas et al., 2016 [7]
In this research, the authors focus on improving existing transport models to better
capture key insights into people’s everyday use of the transport system. Their goal is
to highlight differences in travel patterns across society based on factors such as income,
ethnicity, gender, and other socioeconomic characteristics that influence mobility. The
study uses regional data from the UK National Travel Survey to model the travel behavior
of socially disadvantaged populations. Indicators such as trip frequency, travel distance,
mode choice, and trip purpose are analyzed alongside variables like income, employment
status, gender, age, and ethnicity through both simple and expanded linear regression
models. This approach helps explain disparities in travel behavior and identify the most
influential factors shaping accessibility. The results emphasize the importance of developing
transport policies that respond to the needs of diverse population groups rather than
10
==PAGE:21==
those of dominant or average users, as overlooking these variations can lead to inaccurate
predictions and inequitable investment decisions in transport systems.
Using connectivity for measuring equity in transit provision, Kaplan et al.,
2014 [5]
This study is the only reference that shares the same study area as this thesis—the Greater
Copenhagen Region—and directly addresses issues of transport equity. The authors assess
equity in the public transport system through measures of transit connectivity, considering
factors such as in-vehicle time, service reliability, and the availability of multimodal
connections between zones. Their approach combines GIS-based representations of the
transport network with level-of-service indicators. Connectivity is calculated at the zonal
level, and three types are distinguished: (a) origin connectivity, which measures how easily
residents of a zone can reach all other zones; (b) destination connectivity, which reflects
how accessible the opportunities in a zone are to people from other areas; and (c) pairwise
connectivity, which captures the level of connectivity between specific origin–destination
pairs. In addition, the study introduces a potential accessibility measure—the Public
Transport Connectivity Provision (PTCP)—which accounts not only for travel times but
also for the scale of opportunities available within each zone. To evaluate the fairness
of accessibility distribution across the region, the authors use the Gini coefficient, where
values closer to 0 indicate equality and values closer to 1 reflect greater inequality. Their
results show that Copenhagen’s transit-oriented development promotes both spatial and
vertical equity, providing strong connectivity in dense urban areas and across income
groups, though the northwestern part of the region remains less equitable, with limited
access to jobs and education. This study served as a key methodological and empirical
reference for comparing the outcomes of this thesis and validating its results, as well as an
inspiration for future methodological improvements.

the reported movements between consecutive activities, excluding incomplete or invalid

the survey. The rule to define the number of activities undertaken after each session filters
out all trips ending ”Home” since activities are defined by visiting other destinations. For
each session, all distinct combinations of destination category and purpose are identified,
ensuring that identical types of activities are counted only once. Trips that represented a
return to the home location (e.g., Holiday →Home) treated as a single activity. The total

Methodology
The following chapter provides a detailed explanation of the methodology adopted for this
research. It outlines the critical decisions made throughout the study and describes how
these are addressed to effectively connect theory with practice. The chapter also explains
the analytical procedures and reasoning behind each methodological choice, enabling to

5.1
Network Design
5.1.1
Zone-to-zone Network
As discussed in the previous chapter, a new public transport network model developed to
estimate scheduled travel times across Greater Copenhagen. The process consisted of two
main stages: (1) an initial zone-to-zone aggregation model, and (2) a refined network-based
shortest-path model. Both are constructed from GTFS data provided by Rejseplanen, that
contain detailed information on routes, stops, and timetables for all public transport modes
in Denmark.
First, the zone-to-zone travel time matrix is computed from GTFS data, and all public
transport stops are mapped to their corresponding zones [32]. Each station is assigned
to a zone if it is located within its boundaries; otherwise, it is linked to the nearest zone
within 500 meters to ensure complete coverage of the Greater Copenhagen area. Next, all
stop-to-stop travel times are extracted and computed from the timetables, and the results
are filtered to keep only those connections where both origin and destination stops are
successfully linked to zones. The connections are aggregated to zone-to-zone scheduled
travel times, by calculating the median of all valid links between each pair of zones. The
median is preferred over the mean to reduce the influence of outliers caused by irregular
schedules or infrequent service [33]. The result of this procedure is the average scheduled
zone-to-zone travel time and serves as a baseline for better understanding of network
performance. However, this approach proved to have limitations. Although only one
station failed to be assigned to a zone (as it is located outside the zoned area) and 4,036
stations remained in the network, the model produced 2,498 zone pairs and covered only
23.2% of the trips in the TU travel survey after merging. Due to the way this model is
built, it represents only trips with a direct route connection served by public transport
from zone to zone, and therefore the travel time is only considered, when there is a direct
link between origin and destination [12]. The Python code is available at A.2.
To address these limitations, the model is extended to a stop-level representation of the
24
==PAGE:35==
network, allowing for a detailed and realistic estimation of public transport travel times.
5.1.2
Final Approach: Network-Based Shortest Path
The improved model represents the entire public transport system as a network. Every
public transport station is assigned to a node, and all the scheduled connections are
represented through edges where the travel times in minutes are the weights of the edges.
Negative and zero travel times are excluded. For network analysis and path computation,
the NetworkX library in Python is used [34]. A dictionary to link all the stops to the
corresponding zones achieved the aggregation from stop to zone level. Following that, for
every stop, Dijkstra’s shortest path algorithm applied to compute the minimum travel
time to all the zones that are possible to reach within 120 minutes. The threshold used to
represent a realistic upper bound for feasible trips using public transport in the metropolitan
area of Copenhagen. The algorithm ended up having a stop-to-stop travel time matrix
that is used for the zone-to-zone aggregation, keeping the minimum travel time for every
origin–destination zone pair. In this way, all the different travel times within the zones are
taken into account, as well as how the stops are distributed or how well they are connected,
and it is more representative as a way to check whether there are parts of zones that are
easier to reach than others (Python code available at A.3).
The model covered 80.8% of all origin–destination pairs, compared to only 23.2% in the
previous zone-based approach, a pattern consistent with the bias documented by [12]. This
improvement shows that the network-based shortest-path model gives a better picture
of how the public transport system actually works and provides a stronger base for the
accessibility analysis that followed. Table 5.1 summarizes the key differences of the two
methods.

One of the main objectives of the study is to explore whether certain characteristics are
associated with areas where people appear to have lower levels of activity participation.
To account for spatial dependence, variation, and the effects of scale the need for spatial
modeling arose. Having information on the number of activities and trips that people

statistic, following the approach of [3].
Getis-Ord Gi* is a measure of spatial association that detects areas of concentrated activity
or deprivation [35]. Knowing the coordinates of each location, the measure compares the
value at a given point with the values of its neighbors within a specified distance and with
the overall mean of all values in the dataset. A hot spot indicates a location surrounded by
high values that are significantly higher than expected under spatial randomness, whereas
a cold spot indicates a location surrounded by low values. The statistical significance of
these clusters is expressed through z-scores or p-values. It is important to note that the
variable used in the computation should be positive and have a natural origin, as the Gi*
statistic is not location-invariant—adding a constant to all values affects the outcome,
while multiplying by a constant does not. Equation (5.1) shows the formula of the Gi*
metric, where x j is the mean activity participation rate in zone j (positive and with natural

method compares the local sum for each zone and its neighbors to the global sum across all
zones [35]. When the local sum is substantially different from the expected value, and the

5.3
Modeling Framework
As discussed in Chapter 3, transport equity research distinguishes between horizontal
equity—equal treatment regardless of personal characteristics—and vertical equity, which
indicates preferential treatment for groups with greater need or disadvantage [2, 9]. Trip
generation and count-data models are commonly used to quantify how socio-economic
attributes and spatial conditions influence individual mobility [7, 3].
The analysis uses the cleaned travel-diary panel described in Chapter 4. After removing
records with missing essential fields, the estimation sample contains 14 062 observations
collected over five survey years 2020–2024. The unit of the analysis in individual diary
day and the dependent variable is the number of out-of-home trips reported in the 24-hour
period.
Three estimators are compared:
1) Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) – treated as a naive benchmark;
2) Poisson Generalised Linear Model (GLM) – default count-data specification;
3) Negative-binomial GLM (NB-2) – relaxes the Poisson equi-dispersion assumption.
The number of trips is used as the dependent variable, while various socio-demographic
characteristics of individuals serve as independent variables on a baseline model. To
capture potential vertical equity effects, the baseline model is extended to include indicators
related to social disadvantage such as mobility difficulty, elderly status, income group, and
unemployment, inspired by Lucas et al. [7]. The baseline model to predict trip frequency is
specified in Equation 5.3. This baseline model is later expanded to examine the additional
influence of household-level characteristics beyond individual-level attributes.

5.4.1
Specification testing
Count-data models are typically compared in a nested sequence that balances model fit and
parsimony [40]. Firstly, OLS estimation on a non-negative count outcome is inconsistent
because the error term is heteroskedastic and the dependent variable is discrete and right-
skewed. Therefore, residual plots are used to reject the linear regression specification [40].
In principle Poisson GLM assumes equi-dispersion, that is, Var[y] = E[y], meaning the mean
and variance of the dependent variable are equal so the variance function is not estimated.

6.2.1
Core patterns
Income effects
Once car ownership, having a license and accessibility are held constant, household income
shows no monotonic relationship with the outcome; none of the income categories reaches
statistical significance, suggesting that income operates mainly through car availability and
locational choice. In accordance with Lucas et al. [7], the behavioral differences observed
among low-income households disappear once car ownership and accessibility are controlled
for, suggesting that transport-related social exclusion is driven primarily by disparities in
mobility resources rather than by income.
Social disadvantage and demographic composition
Household structure has an important role, having families with children under 18 making
8-9% more trips.Surprisingly, people experiencing mobility difficulties perform slightly

more trips than others. Age has a significant contribution on the number of trips, the age
groups of people less than 18 years old and above do 6-16% less trips than the reference
age group (30-54) while the age groups right before and after do not differ significantly,
revealing a U-shaped pattern as expected from literature where accessibility difficulties are
larger for adolescents and retirees [16].
Spatial and temporal effects
The area of residence affects trip frequency. Residents in outer suburbs and regional areas
make fewer trips compared to those in inner suburbs, likely due to longer distances between
destinations and fewer available opportunities. Similarly, individuals in Semesters 3 and 4
(post-summer) make slightly more trips than those in Semester 2, indicating seasonal or
academic-period variations in travel.
Mobility resources and land-use accessibility
Driving license ownership significantly increases trip frequency by approximately 7% ,
confirming that access to private mobility resources enables more active travel behavior. In
contrast, living in areas with high public transport accessibility is associated with an 11%
reduction in trips, suggesting that good accessibility allows individuals to consolidate their
activities within shorter distances and indicates that accessibility extends beyond simple
proximity; it reflects the genuine freedom of individuals to select among different activity
locations as argued by Martens [1]. Bicycle availability shows a small and statistically
insignificant effect, indicating limited influence on overall trip frequency.
Occupation
Part time occupation indicates 4%-5% more trips while all the other categories do not
diverse form the baseline, full-time employees.
Gender
Males exhibit 4% more trips than females — even after accounting for all other factors —
there still appear to be inherent and unexpected gender differences in travel behavior that
cannot be explained by measurable resources or social roles.

Chapter 7
Discussion
In transport research, it is important to distinguish between stated data and modeled data.
Stated data are collected directly from individuals, usually through surveys, interviews, or
travel diaries, where respondents self-report their socio-demographic characteristics, travel
behavior, and preferences. While this type of data provides valuable first-hand insights, it
is often limited by issues such as recall bias, non-response, and the relatively small scale of
collection.
Modeled data, by contrast, are generated through simulation models that integrate various
data sources to estimate or predict behavior. Activity-based models, such as COMPASS, fall
into this category: instead of relying on respondents’ reported trips, they simulate complete
daily activity patterns of individuals and households across a region. The advantage of
modeled data is that it allows for comprehensive coverage and scenario testing, but its
accuracy depends heavily on model assumptions, calibration, and the quality of input data.
The findings of this study are based on stated data and indicate that mobility factors such
as car availability and accessibility affect daily mobility behavior. When controlling for
income, there is a significant loss in the strength of income effects. This suggests that the
impact of income is mediated by mobility resources such as car ownership or possession of a
driving license, which influence people’s participation in daily activities. When individuals
have comparable access—either through private cars or public transport—the inequalities
in trip frequency by income almost disappear.
This argument supports the idea that ensuring access for all population segments, rather
than implementing simple income-based policies, forms the foundation for transport equity.
This aligns with previous research highlighting the role of resources and accessibility in
promoting social inclusion [1]. Instead of focusing on eliminating transport inequalities
through horizontal planning (offering equal service to everyone), a vertical equity approach
is preferred when planning for diverse populations—bridging inequalities by improving
accessibility for socially disadvantaged groups.
Policies should promote high-quality,
affordable, and reliable public transport services that advance transport justice.
Spatial and statistical analyses of the Greater Copenhagen Region’s transport system
reveal uneven treatment among residents. Certain peripheral municipalities simultaneously
exhibit lower accessibility and lower participation rates. These areas represent places where
residents engage in fewer daily activities, even though the distance from the metropolitan
area is not substantial.
The population analyzed in this study includes specific demographic groups such as youth,
44
==PAGE:55==
elderly individuals, and low-income households—with and without access to private vehicles.
This inclusion supports the study’s goal of assessing whether accessibility correlates with
actual participation for all. The results indicate that even when high accessibility is ensured
in terms of travel distance and time, some population groups still experience exclusion.
This occurs either due to spatial distribution or individual privileges that create inequalities,
such as car ownership. Car dependency has also been highlighted in transport equity
research as an important factor in areas outside metropolitan centers, including Greater
Copenhagen [5]. One more, consistent yet unexplained, finding relates to differences in
travel behavior between genders.
Transport equity is not achieved only through infrastructure improvements but is closely
linked to maintaining affordability, inclusion, and accessibility simultaneously. Policies
should focus on groups with limited mobility resources—ensuring affordable public transport
pricing relative to income and promoting community-based mobility options such as car-
and bike-sharing schemes to reduce exclusion among those without private vehicles in
car-dependent areas. Such spatial exclusion can also eliminated by improving public
transport connections between peripheral areas, potentially through the implementation
of smaller, publicly owned vehicles instead of extensive infrastructure investments, which
may not be justified by the relatively low participation rates in those areas.
Overall, the findings of this study are consistent with previous research, suggesting that
the observed phenomena reflect a broader pattern rather than being unique to this region.
However, despite Greater Copenhagen’s widely recognized and efficient transport network,
the results underscore that even well-performing systems can experience equity challenges.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
The results of this analysis the ability to participate in essential daily activities—measured
by both trip frequency and activity diversity—is shaped more by car ownership and
transit accessibility than by income. Although income shows some influence, suggesting
inequalities among population groups, its effect becomes less significant once mobility and
sociodemographic factors are controlled for. This finding indicates that transport equity
depends more on structural and accessibility-related factors than on income distribution
alone.
The Greater Copenhagen Region is served by an efficient public transport network, offering
extensive coverage in terms of travel time, distance, and timetables. However, despite
this efficiency, certain geographic areas—such as Ishøj and Høje-Taastrup—still experience
social exclusion. These residential areas are insufficiently connected to the transport
network and essential services. Accessibility here depends on reliable access to education,
green spaces, and housing. Residents in these areas exhibit notably low participation in
daily activities and high levels of care dependency, revealing that the current network does
not fully meet their needs.
Social exclusion becomes more pronounced when individuals’ daily activities are not
concentrated in the metropolitan core but rather in nearby areas that lack strong public
transport connections. This is particularly critical for people without access to private cars,
even if they possess a driving license. Disadvantaged social groups who face such barriers
often experience reduced participation in out-of-home activities, which can negatively affect
regional welfare. The heavy reliance on car ownership also highlights the exclusion of
groups who typically do not have access to cars—such as the elderly or youth—who still
depend on mobility for essential purposes like school, healthcare, and other activities.
To mitigate these disparities, public transport must remain a feasible and attractive option,
ensuring safety, affordability, and independence from household structure. Addressing both
social and spatial inequalities requires a combination of transport policies. Enhancing
transit connections in low-access areas could involve implementing community-based
mobility options such as car-sharing, reducing dependence on private vehicles. Additionally,
introducing income-sensitive pricing policies and improving public transport infrastructure
and station environments could enhance the overall commuting experience.

8.1
Limitations
Several limitations should be acknowledged. First, it is difficult to confirm clear causal
relationships, as unmeasured differences between people and areas may affect the estimated
impact of accessibility. Second, the accessibility measure is transit-only and does not
account for mode competition (e.g., car or cycling) or the probabilistic nature of route
choice, which may overestimate the role of public transport in a multi-modal urban
environment. Third, small sample sizes in peripheral zones limit spatial detail and the
precision of local estimates. Fourth, the single day travel diary may not reflect weekly
travel patterns, as some individuals may have chosen to travel less on the survey day.
8.2
Future Research Directions
Future research could strengthen these findings through several extensions. Accessibility is
currently modeled as a linear effect; applying a non-linear specification (e.g., a sigmoid
transformation) could better capture potential threshold effects. Furthermore, it could be
refined by incorporating the attraction or opportunity component, accounting for both
origin and destination land-use characteristics. Including job- and education-related data,
as well as testing the impact of reduced travel times between origins and destinations,
would provide deeper insights into how accessibility improvements affect participation.
Expanding the analysis to multimodal networks—including car, bicycle, and e-scooter
travel—would offer a more comprehensive view of accessibility patterns.
Integrating
COMPASS model data could extend spatial coverage, while combining the analysis with
smart-card or smartphone data would help validate the diary-based findings and capture
multi-day mobility rhythms and mode substitution effects. Finally, testing combined
policy measures—such as fare limits, transit-oriented development (TOD), and accessible
design—would allow for a better evaluation of how integrated strategies can promote fair
and inclusive mobility in Greater Copenhagen.
