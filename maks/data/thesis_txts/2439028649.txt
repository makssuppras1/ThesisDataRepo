Abstract
Hepatitis C virus is a major health burden worldwide. It is estimated that between 130 and 170 million
people worldwide are chronically infected with HCV. At least 350,000 people die each year due to liver
failure caused by HCV. Therefore treating HCV has become one of the most prioritized tasks on the
health agenda. The last recent years the so called DAAs have shown great promise against HCV by
speciﬁcally targeting viral proteins that are critical for its survival. Among the DAAs, Sofosbuvir and
Velpatasvir are some of the most consumed. However, a study under the Copenhagen Hepatitis Program
at Copenhagen University Hospital, Hvidovre showed that these medications are not effective on long
terms as they causes HCV to form resistant mutations eventually. In this project fastq data was provided
from the study in order to analyze the data for the purpose of identifying SNPs of relevance that could
indicate drug escape selection. Moreover, after identiﬁcation of the SNPs the next step was to characterize
and identify the corresponding mutations that the SNPs caused. Several SNPs were identiﬁed as being
exposed to selection. Some of the corresponding mutations could potentially be drug escape causing but
lack of literature and research in the area only made it possible to determine with certainty two drug
escape mutations, S282T for NS5B and L31V for NS5A.

Resumé
Hepatitis C Virus er en kæmpe sundhedsbyrde verden over. Det estimeres at omtrent 130 til 170 millioner
mennesker på verdensplan er kronisk inﬁcerede med HCV. Mindst 350 tusind mennesker dør hvert år på
grund af leversvigt forårsaget af HCV. Derfor er behandling af HCV et af de mest priorierede opgaver
på den sundhedsmæssige dagsorden. I de seneste par år har de såkaldte DAAs vist stort potentiale mod
HCV ved at speciﬁkt angribe virale proteiner som er kritiske for HCVs overlevelse. Blandt DAAs er
Sofosbuvir og Velpatasvir nogle af de mest anvendte af disse. Dog har en undersøgelse lavet under
Copenhagen Hepatitis Program ved Hvidovre Hospital vist at disse medikamenter ikke er effektive på
lang sigt da det i sidste instans forårsager resistens mutationer i HCV. I dette projekt er fastq data blevet
tildelt fra denne undersøgelse med henblik på at analysere dataen for at identiﬁcere relevante SNPs som
kunne indikere resistens. Ydermere var det næste skridt efter SNP identiﬁkationen at karakterisere de
mutationer som disse SNPs forårsagede. Flere SNPs blev identiﬁceret som vidste tegn på selektion.
Nogle af de tilsvarende mutationer viste potentiale til at være resistensfremkaldende men mangel på
literatur på området gjorde det kun muligt med sikkerhed at bestemme to resistensmutationer, S282T for
NS5B og L31V for NS5A.

Aim
Viruses such as HCV that show resistance to antiviral medicine is quite a serious challenge health wise
on a global scale. HCV is able to develop various different drug escape mutations to be able to resist
antivirals. At Copenhagen University hospital, Hvidovre under the Copenhagen Hepatitis C Program a
study was made (L. Pham et al.) and documented in the article: "HCV Genotype 6a Escape from and
Resistance to Velpatasvir, Pibrentasvir, and Sofosbuvir in Robust Infectious Cell Culture Models" [1].
In the study Huh 7.5 cells were cultivated and infected with a prototype of the HCV 6a genotype called
HK2. Some of these cells were among others experimentally treated with single inhibitor therapy either
with Sofosbuvir or Velpatasvir. The study concluded that the HCV strains within the infected cells were
able to form drug escape mutations over a couple of weeks.
• The aim of this project had been divided into three objectives. Primary objective was ﬁrst of all
for me to learn to use bioinformatic tools and methods in order to work with bioinformatic related
problems.
• Secondary objective was by being handed fastq data from (L. Pham et al.) to be able to process
and analyze this data through different programs and eventually being able to ﬁnd single nucleotide
polymorphisms (SNPs) that could cause the drug escape mutations and locate them in the provided
HCV reference genome.
• Tertiary objective was to further analyze the SNPs in order to determine the actual mutations they
caused in order to characterize and if possible determine the drug escape mechanism.

Corrections
1. The timepoint "endpoint" in both treatment frequency matrices (see appendix 6.11) has been ex-
cluded for further analysis in this project because it has not been possible for me to ﬁnd information
about the exact time for which the "endpoint" sample was taken for either treatment.

ever, the cell cultures were drug treated throughout the whole time frame. Hence the correct indi-

Hepatitis C Virus
Figure 1.1: The Flaviviridae family. Figure adapted from [2].
Hepatitis C virus (HCV) is a spherical enveloped, positive-sense, single-stranded RNA virus. HCV is
of the Hepaciviurs genus (from the greek "hepatos" meaning liver) in the Flaviviridae family shown in
Figure 1.1. This family also include yellow fever virus, west nile virus and dengue virus. It is one among
ﬁve known hepatitis viruses that are known as hepatitis A, -B, -D, and - E virus. Even though HCV
was ofﬁcially identiﬁed in 1989 its existence was already discovered in 1975 when a study found that
transfusion associated hepatitis was not caused by either hepatitis A - or hepatitis B virus. Therefore it

was ﬁrst simply referred to as non-A, non-B hepatitis. Later studies in chimpanzees revealed that non-A,
non-B hepatitis was caused by a small agent [3]. Despite being able to infect chimpanzees for studies
HCV is a human pathogen as it has not been observed in animals. However, a few examples of HCV
homologues have been identiﬁed in dogs and horses. Yet, the origins of HCV still remain obscure [3,
4, 5]. The HCV particle is made up of a core of RNA that is surrounded by a protective protein shell
and encased further in a lipid envelope of glycoproteins. Apolipoproteins can also be found on HCV
particles. The lipid composition consist of very low density lipoproteins and low density proteins with
cholesterol esters. HCV particles have shown to have a rather smooth and even surface. The size of HCV
varies but is in the range between 40 and 180 nm in diameter but typically around 65 nm i diameter [6].
An illustration of the HCV particle can be seen in Figure 1.2. HCV strains are classiﬁed into 7 major
genotypes (1 to 7), among which genotypes 1 and 2 cause most infections worldwide. Under these 7
genotypes there are identiﬁed more than 108 subtypes. Despite all this divergence all different subtypes
are hepatotropic and pathogenic. It has been estimated that the nucleotide diversity of HCV genomes
is about 32.4% between HCV genotypes and 14.6% within HCV sub-genotypes. Among each different
HCV infection there is still a nucleotide variation of 1 to 5 percent [7]. HCV genotype 1-4 are the most
common world-wide. In Western Europe the most common sub-types are 1a and 1b. In India, Nepal,
and Pakistan genotype 3 is the most frequent. In Hong-Kong and Southeast Asia genotype 6 is the most
common. Genotype 5 accounts for less than one percent of the world infections, where most of these
have been observed in South Africa. Genotype 2 is mostly observed in West Africa. Genotype 4 is most
frequent in Africa and the Middle East where Egypt is that country in the world with the highest HCV
prevalence [8, 9].

1.1.1
HCV infection (Hepatitis C)
Hepatitis C is the complex liver disease following infection of HCV. Hepatitis C is a global health burden.
Despite that 1012 HCV particles are generated per day in infected patients it has been named the silent
epidemic due to its asymptomatic behavior decades after infection. Accounting for 15 to 20 percent of
all acute hepatitis infections, it is believed that between 130 to 170 million people are infected worldwide
with an estimated 3 to 4 million new infections every year[11]. It is estimated that on a global scale
HCV is responsible for at least 350,000 deaths annually with survival rates declining rapidly. [12] Severe
complications with liver diseases such as ﬁbrosis, cirrhosis and heptocellular carcinoma are often the
results of a long term chronic Hepatitis C infection and lead in many cases to death. Therefore, hepatitis
C is the most common cause for liver transplantation in developing countries [7]. The diagnosis of HCV
is given by the observation of the presence of HCV antibodies in the blood. It has been observed that
15-40% of infected patients were able to clear HCV spontaneously meaning their immune systems was
able to respond to the HCV infection by producing neutralizing antibodies. Unfortunately despite a
strong immune response this is generally insufﬁcient in order to eradicate the HCV infection [4]. Less
fortunate patients develop chronic hepatitis C. Approximately 70 percent of these patients will develop
a liver disease and 10 to 20 percent of these will develop liver cirrhosis. The number of patients in
risk of progressive liver diseases is projected only to increase signiﬁcantly over the next decades [13].
Currently there are no vaccines available for HCV due to the tremendous strain variation. The main
route of transmission is direct or indirect exposure to contaminated blood. This can happen through
transfusions of blood products, sexual contact, consumption of intravenous drugs, vertical transmission
from mother to child, or poorly sterilized surgical materials [14]. Total health care costs associated with
HCV therapy increased from 77 million dollars in 2009 to 18.4 million dollars in 2015 with the approval
of HCV antivirals driving the expenses [8]. HCV infections typically present smaller symptoms such
as neusea, guadrant pain and malaise. Often Patients are not even aware of the infection. Center for
disease control and prevention in the United States recommends any person born between 1945 and 1965
to receive a screening test for HCV and also people why have had exposure of other risk factors such as
intranasal drug use, injections, birth by an HCV infected mother or recipient of a blood transfusion or
organ transplant before July 1992 [15].

Genome and molecular biology
The genome of HCV amounts approximately 9600 nucleotides, depending on the genotype, and contains
one open reading frame coding for a poly-protein of approximately 3000 amino acid (AA) residues. The
precursor poly-protein is cleaved into 10 different mature proteins. These are the core (C), E1, E2, which
count for the structural proteins and the nonstructural proteins p7, NS2, NS3, NS4A, NS4B, NS5A, and
NS5B [14]. The genome with the respective protein coding regions are illustrated in Figure 1.3. In
appendix 6.4 a larger version can be seen of the genome. Also a scheme has been made for this project
that gives an overview of the genome and locations of the different proteins and regions in the genome
used for this study. This can be found in appendix 6.1. In the following the 10 HCV proteins and the
untranslated regions are described:

C
The core consist of the ﬁrst 191 amino acids and has a size of 23 kDa. The core encapsulates the RNA
genome. It has been reported to act with the 5’ UTR of the RNA genome. C is a multifunctional protein
and highly basic. It has 3 domains based on hydrophobicity. Of the 3 domains, domain 1 have many basic
whereas domain 2 is more hydrophobic and domain 3 is very hydrophobic and acts as a signal sequence
for E1. C forms the structural component of the HCV virus. When expressed in mammalian cells the core
protein is located on the membranes of ER. The core protein interacts with many cellular proteins to affect
the host cells functions such as apoptosis, signalling pathways gene transcription and lipid metabolism.
Its interference with the metabolism leads to oxidative stress and also eventually hepatocellular carcinoma

E1 and E2
E1 and E2 are type I transmembrane envelope proteins. Together they surround the HCV particle by
making an envelope. They play a key role in virus entry via receptor binding and fusion. They are highly
glycosylated with 6 and 11 glycosylation sites respectively. E1 and E2 are anchored to the double lipid
layer derived from host cell. being at the proximity of the assembly site E1 and E2 carries different
responsibilities. E1 is responsible for fusion between cellular membranes and the HCV parts and E2
is responsible for the cell attachment. E1 and E2 form a non covalent heterodimer retained in the ER.
E2 contains two hypervariable regions that are under constant selection for mutation because they are
targeted by antibodies [18, 19].
p7
p7 is a approximately 63 AA long polypeptide which is located between the genes of E2 and NS2.
Intracellularly P7 is located in the ER and spans the membrane thus being an integral membrane protein.
The cleavage is mediated by signal peptidases of the ER of the host cell. p7 has an essential role in the
virus particle assembly in a genotype speciﬁc manner. It is also a viroporin which are viral proteins that
form calcium ion channels [18].
NS2
NS2 is an approximately 21-23 sized kDa transmembrane autoprotease essential for completing the viral
replication cycle. It plays an important role in HCV assembly as it mediates the cleavage of NS2 and NS3
from each other. The N terminal of NS2 is very hydrophobic and forms 3 or 4 transmembrane helices
in the ER membrane where as the C terminuses play a role in the NS2/NS3 auto protease activity. it is
known that NS2 interacts with E1, E2 and p7 which is essential for the migration of the E1/E2 dimer to
the virion assembly site. The crystal structure of NS2 C terminus has revealed it to be a dimeric protease
with two active sites. It works together with NS3 for its viral applications [18].
NS3
NS3 is around 67 kDa of size and has multifunctional activities. The N terminus has serine protease
activity while the C terminus has helicase activity. It is bound with the ER membrane through its inter-
actions with NS4A. The activity of NS3 has shown to be vital for RNA replication. It is known to cleave
downstream of the polyprotein at the sites NS3/NS4A, NS4A/NS4B, NS4B/NS5A, NS5A/NS5B. Addi-
tionally it also cleaves TLR3 adaptor protein TRIF and another mitocondrial antiviral signalling protein
called MAVS. Therefore NS3 has become one of the antiviral targets in drug development [18].

NS4A
NS5A is approximately 54 amino acids long. It acts as a cofactor for NS3. It has a N terminal that is very
hydrophobic. Deletion analysis linked it to be involved in targeting NS3 to the ER membrane. It is as-
sumed that around 20 amino acids for a transmembrane helix that anchors the NS3/NS4A complex to the
ER membrane. Residues in the core of NS3 interact with the NS4A terminal to form the complex. NS4A
has also shown to be responsible for the phosphorylation of NS5A and interacts with NS5A directly. An
AA region in the centre of NS4A (residues 2135-2139) has shown to be essential for the phosphorylation
of NS5A [18]
NS4B
NS4B is a small 27 kDa sized protein important for recruiting other HCV proteins to form a replication
complex. NS4B contains 4 transmembrane domains and interacts with NS3. It has found to be an integral
membrane protein located in the ER membrane. It induces morphological changes to the ER by forming
a structure called the membranous web, which has been correlated to the site where all the HCV proteins
are localized making it the site for replication. The structure consist of many small vesicles embedded
in a membranous matrix. NS4B has appeared to have reduced mobility in the membranous web due to
possible oligomerisation [18]
NS5A
NS5A is a hydrophobic phosphoprotein plays a vital role in the replication of HCV. It has no transmem-
brane domains. Studies have indicated that NS5A contains an essentiual zinc coordinated motif that play
a vital role in the structural integrity. It is assumed that NS5A has numerous interactions with proteins to
affect cell signalling where it regulates the signalling with both pro and anti apoptotic mechanisms. NS5A
is divided into 3 domains. domain I contains a basic dimer involved in RNA binding. Domain II and I
are responsible for the RNA replication. Domain III participates in the assembly of the HCV particle.
The phosphorylation state regulates the balance between RNA replication and downstream processing.
NS5A is also one of the HCV target proteins for antivirals [18]
NS5B
NS5B is a 65 kDa RNA dependent RNA polymerase that synthesises new RNA. It is the workhorse of the
HCV replicase complex with a right hand structure with ﬁnger, thumb, and palm domains. The replication
goes through a via synthesis of a complementary negative RNA strand that uses the genome as a template
and a subsequent synthesis of a positive RNA strand making a genomic copy. NS5B has emerged as
the major target for antiviral drug intervention due to its central role. NS5B lacks proofreading in the

5’ UTR
The 5’ UTR also known as the untranslated upstream region contains around 341 nucleotides and contains
the internal ribosomal entry site. It is shown to be the most conserved region in the HCV genome. It
contains speciﬁc secondary RNA structures required for replication and translation. It has been shown
that elements of the 5’ UTR is used as the internal ribosome entry site thereby passing the need for
processing events such as capping. So far 4 domains of 5’ UTR have been identiﬁed each with highly
structured RNA elements that include stem loops and a pseudoknot. Domain I and II are involved in the
replication where domains II, II and IV contains the coding region for the internal ribosomal entry site.
The 5’ UTR interacts with a liver speciﬁc microRNA called miR122 which is critical for efﬁﬁcent RNA
replication. This process is proposed to be necessary for protecting the 5’ terminal of viral sequences
from nucleic degradation or innate immune responses [20, 14]
3’ UTR
The 3’ UTR or the downstream untranslated region. It varies between 200 and 235 nucleotides in length.
It has 3 distinct regions. The variable part of 3’ UTR is located in the beginning of the sequence. All
these regions have appeared to be involved in the replication where two of these act as cis replicative
elements within the NS5B coding region. Furthermore, it stimulates HCV translation in cis. 3’UTR is
also involved in stabilization and packaging of the RNA [20, 14].
Frameshift protein F
The F protein or alternate reading frame protein is generated with a -2/+1 frameshift in the N terminal
core encoding domain of the polyprotein. It’s existance is known due to detected antibodies speciﬁc for
F peptides located in infected patients. Its mechanisms are still unknown but have been proposed to be
involved in viral persistence as it has shown to supress type 1 interferon responses [14, 21].

Life Cycle
Figure 1.4: Illustration of the lifecycle of HCV. Figure adapted from [4].
The life cycle of HCV can be categorized into a series of steps displayed in Figure 1.4. The different
steps will be characterized in the following:
Attachment
In the bloodstream HCV particles are transported around until they get into contact with hepatocytes.
This allows the attachment to take place between attachment factors and the host cell’s surface receptors.
The attachment is speciﬁc to receptors on hepatocytes. These include lipoprotein receptor scavenger
receptor class B type I, tetraspanin CD81, occludin, and tight junction protein claudin 1. From the HCV
side glycosaminoglycans and low density lipoprotein receptor is thought to attach to the mentioned host
receptors. It is believed that E2 binds to the external loop of CD81 [20, 22, 18].
Entry
The actual entry of HCV into the hepatocytes is only partially understood. It involves a series of speciﬁc
cellular entry factors. It is known to be an interaction with more speciﬁc receptors in the attachment
process that actively promote the entry of HCV by inducing conformational changes of the HCV particle
or by activating signalling pathways [23, 18, 22]. Figure 1.5 depicts an overview of the entry process.

Endocytosis
After the entry the HCV particle gets inside the host cell through endocytosis. The process is pH depen-
dent and clathrin and dynamin mediated. It is believed that this process is mediated by the initial receptor
interactions although the exact role the HCV co receptors in endocytosis remains undetermined [20, 22].
Fusion
The low pH in the endosomal compartment eventually leads to major conformational changes in E1
which leads to membrane fusion and release of the capsid into the cytoplasm [20, 24].
Uncoating
After the release the HCV particle uncoats itself in order to release RNA. This process involves the
Rab5a positive endosomes along actin stress ﬁbers. Here the uncoating process takes place where the
decapsidation of viral nucleocapsids liberates the RNA [23, 20].
RNA translation
Translation is initiated through help of cellular factors. The internal ribosomal entry site on the 5’ UTR
region allows the initiation of the translation into the approximately 3000 AA long polyprotein. Domains
I and II of 5’ UTR are relevant in this process. domain II, III, IV together with the ﬁrst 40 nucleotides of
the core region comprise the entry site. The translations initiation take place through the formation of a
complex between the 40s ribosomal unit and the entry site [23, 20, 16].

Polyprotein Processing
Host signal peptidases process the junctions between C/E1, E1/E2, E2/p7, and p7/NS2. NS2 then cuts
the NS2/3 junction and the NS3/NS4A junction. NS3 then processes the preferreded order of cleavage:
NS5A/5B, NS4A/4B, NS4B/5A. These are mediated intermolecularly [16].
RNA replication
RNA replication is believed to occur in association with ER derived membrane spheres induced by NS4A
and NS5B. The replication only takes place if there is enough viral protein. The process takes place in
the membranous web where NS5B replicates the positive sense strand into a template mediate negative
sense strand. Many viral as well as host proteins take part of the replication process through regulation.
Cyclophillin A and B have shown to have an important role through the regulation of the binding of
NS5B to the RNA template. Since NS5B has no proofreading activity HCV has a high mutation rate. It
is also known that NS3 has an important role in the replication process even though its exact mechanism
is unknown. It is proposed that its activity is important for the separation of nascent and template RNA
strands, unwinding RNA secondary structures or displacing RNA binding proteins. It is also known that
domain I and II of NS5A are essential for RNA replication. Figure 1.6 illustrates the replication and
assembly steps in the HCV life cycle [23, 20, 16].

Assembly
The assembly process is a tightly regulated process coupled to the host cell lipid synthesis. The mature
core protein relocates from the ER membrane to cytoplasmic lipid droplets with assistance of diacylglyc-
erol acyltransferase-1. The nucleocapisid formation is shown to involve interaction between the core and
NS5A where either is located on the the cytoplasmic lipid droplets. The RNA genome is relocated from
the membranous web to lipid droplets. This process mechanism is poorly understood but is known to
be dependent on NS5A on core coted lipid droplets. The transportation of the core protein and NS5A is
assumed to be partly regulated by a host protein called diacylglycerol O-acyltransferase-1. Other proteins
playing a role in the assembly are tail interaction protein and seipin. Afterwards capsin relocates to the
ER lumen where the viral envelope is acquired through budding with the ER membrane. Here E1 and E2
are anchored at the proximity of the assembly site. NS2 is known to control this ﬁnal step in the assembly
process [23, 24, 22]
Release
The release of HCV particles is a further process step coupled to the host cell lipid synthesis. The virions
are released from hepatocytes by transit through the secretory pathway. In this process the virions acquire
the characteristical low buoyant density and glycans located on the envelope proteins are modiﬁed. The
HCV is eventually released by exocytosis [23, 24]

1.2
Antiviral Drugs
Antiviral drugs/antivirals are compounds or agents that are designed to stop the development and prop-
agation of a virus without causing relevant damage to the host cell. Even though many antivirals have
made it to the shelves over the years, the number of available agents against viruses is still limited and
only effective against a small range of viral pathogens. This is because the development of antiviral drugs
is particularly difﬁcult. First of all viruses are intracellular parasites. Therefore, it is difﬁcult to interfere
in the virus growth without having a negative impact because every step in the viral life cycle engages
the functions of the host. Also viruses that cause fatal diseases have to be handled by experienced, well
trained staff in facilities with strict contaminent requirements. These facilities are expensive and difﬁcult
to maintain. The potency requirements for antivirals are also very high making them difﬁcult to develop.
Another problem also worth mentioning is the short duration of several viral infections. Since symptoms
typically appear after the virus has stopped replicating it is necessary to administer drugs early in the
infection, but this could potentially harm healthy patients. Antiviral drug development is often fuelled by
two considerations: the requirement for the drug against the speciﬁc virus infection and which drugs that
already are available to treat that speciﬁc virus infection. It takes typically around 5 to 10 years to ﬁnd
a compound active against a virus infection. One approach that is investigated is to use small interfering
RNAs to silence viral genes as these kind of RNA have lots of interactions with viral RNA. Many viral
diseases are self limited meaning they do not require antiviral therapy. However, the development of
antiviral drugs is still required to combat other more serious viruses. Circa 90 antiviral drugs are ap-
proved to treat the following 9 viruses: HIV, Human Cytomegalovirus, Herpes Simplex Virus, Inﬂuenza
Virus, Respiratory Syncytial Virus, Varicella Zoster Virus, Human Papilloma Virus, Hepatitis B Virus,
and HCV. Most antiviral drugs have been chemically synthesized. However, natural products have con-
tributed to the development of these drugs by giving insights into the synthesis of chemical compounds.
Treatment with antiviral drugs is typically only relevant in case of chronic or persistent viral infections.
Even though the drug aims at eradicating the viral pathogen from the host this is not achieved very often.
Studies have considered for future treatments to focus either on a broad spectrum antiviral that have a
broad spectrum of antiviral activities or the many for one approach which uses multiple antiviral drugs
for one target [25, 26, 27, 28, 29, 30, 31, 32].

1.2.1
History of Antiviral Drugs
Figure 1.7: Timeline of approved antivirals between 1959 and 2016. Figure adapted from [33].
The era of antiviral drugs initially started in the early 1950s when thiosemicarbazones were quoted to
have antiviral activity against vaccinia virus. Some of these compounds had potential enough to be
led into clinical studies for prophylaxis of smallpox. However, at the same time the smallpox vaccine
took over and the need to further attempt to develop an antiviral drug became superﬂuous. However, in
1959 W.H. Prusoff described the thymidine analogue idoxuridine. Although it initially was described
as an antitimor agent, its antiviral activity was described in 1961 by by Herrmann who proposed the
drug to be used in rabbits and humans against Herpes Simplex Virus. It was approved in June 1963
as the ﬁrst antiviral drug against Herpes Simplex Virus as a topical treatment for eye infection. Today
Idoxuridine is still used for topical treatment against epithelial keratitis caused by Herpes Simplex Virus.
Another compound amantadine shown to be effective against inﬂueza A Virus and was approved as an
antiviral drug in 1966. In 1970 it was discovered that some viruses posses a reverse transcriptase able
to translate RNA into DNA. This led to many attempts during the 1970s to ﬁnd inhibitors of this protein
even though its true important to human pathology was determined. In the late 1970s acyclic nucleosides
analogues were discovered. Of this discovery the nucleoside analogue acyclovir became a successful
treatment option because it was highly selective and did cause any adverse side effects. In the early
1980s HIV came to attention due to its fatality and seriousness. Based on the success of acyclovir this
provided encouragement to face HIV in course of searching for new inhibitors. Azidothymidine was
discovered as a successful inhibitor of HIV and was approved for the market in 1987. In the 1980s
genome sequencing and structure based drug design became new aspects to include in a new approach
to target viral proteins. This new method allowed the elucidation of the HIV life cycle led eventually
to the structure of HIV protease inhibitors in the mid 1990s. Ever so, in the late 80s the so called non-
nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitors were discovered but their effectiveness was limited and today

1.2.2
Types of antiviral drugs
Antiviral drugs can target any step in the viral replicative process from adsorption to release from the
host cell. Antiviral drugs have been developed with different mechanisms of action (MoA). The most
common MoAs will be elucidated in the following.
Nucleoside analogue
This type of antiviral drug targets viral DNA polymerases. It is dependent on phosphorylation to become
a substrate of a viral DNA polymerase. Viral enzymes are responsible for the transformation into the
monophosphate devirate. Further phosphorylation is carried out by host cell kinases. The triphospate
derivative mimic the natural substrate of the viral DNA polymerase and is therefore incorporated into the
growing DNA chain. This leads often to termination of the viral replication due to the lack of the 3’ OH
in the ribose ring. Examples of nucleoside analogues are Vidarabine, Entecavir, and Telbivudine [25, 35,
33, 36].
Acyclic guanosine analogue
Examples of Acyclic guanosine analogues are acyclovir, valganciclovir, penciclovir, ganciclovir, valacy-
clovir, and famciclovir. They are particularly effective at targeting Herpes Simplex Virus. Acyclovir was
the ﬁrst acyclic guanosine analogue developed intially for targeting adenosine deaminase. However it
was pointed out that it showed potent activity against Herpesviruses by targeting the DNA polymerase.
Subsequently other acyclic guanosines were developed to target other viruses such as Vaciella Zoster
Virus and Human Cytomegalovirus. These antiviral drugs are dependent on the phosphorylation by viral
thymidine kinases in order to be activated [25, 33].
Nucleoside/Nucleotide reverse transcriptase inhibitor
This antiviral drug type targets the reverse transcriptase enzyme to prevent viral reverse transcription.
They are also known as 2’-3’-dideoxynucleoside analogues. After the phosphorylation to the 5’ triphos-
phate they act as chain terminators for which they compete with the deoxynucleotidetriphosphates. The

Nonnucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitor
Nonnucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitors target the allosteric site of a viral reverse transcriptase.

Protease inhibitor
This drug is designed after the peptidomimetic transition state principle. This means the replacement of
the peptide linkage serving as substrate for the protease by a transition state analogue. This analogue
cannot be cleaved by simple hydrolysis unlike the transition state peptide. Chemically, The concept is to
replace a peptide linkage with a hydroxyethylene group. Examples of protease inhibitors are Saquinavir,
Ritonavir, Indinavir, Nelﬁnavir, Amprenavir, Lopinavir, Atazanavir, Fosamprenavir and Darunavir [25,
33, 37].
Integrase inhibitor
This antiviral drug type is speciﬁc for HIV. The integrase of HIV is responsible for the insertion of viral
genome into the DNA of the host cell. The integrase inhibitor target the catalytic site thus preventing the
virus integration. This step is quite vital for HIV so the inhibition will lead to a reduction of viral loads.
Examples of integrase inhibitors are Raltegravir, Dolutegravir and Elvitagravir [25, 33, 37].
Pyrophosphate analogue
This antiviral drug has only one of its type on the market namely Foscarnet. Foscarnet is used exclusively
for treating Herpes and cytomegalone viruses that have shown resistance towards nucleoside analogues.
Pyrophosphate is a product of the nucleotide incorporation reaction so hence foscarnet acts as an inhibitor
for the DNA polymerization. Forscarnet is unlike the classical antiviral agents because it does not have
to be phosphorylated before it can bind [33, 25].
Entry inhibitor
Entry inhibitors block viral entry. Entry inhibitors can either be fusion- or co receopter inhibitors. Fusion
inhibitors such as enfuvirtide mimicks a coil coil interaction with the heptad repeat regions of the viral

1.2.4
HCV drug escape
Figure 1.11: Typical drug escape mutations in HCV. Figure adapted from [56].
HCV displays high genetic diversity. This leads to selection of drug resistant mutants. This can also
be referred to as drug escape as it evades the mechanism of the drug. HCV drug escape has especially
become a huge challenge for HCV treatment strategies. The drug resistance of HCV is based on the
selection of certain HCV mutants in which speciﬁc nucleotides in the genome result in an AA change in
the subsequent HCV protein. Figure 1.11 gives an overview of the most common drug escape mutations
for the 3 targeted HCV proteins NS3, NS5A and NS5B. Speciﬁc resistance mutation selection falls
typically on mutations Y93 or L31 against NS5A inhibitors and S282 or L159 for NS5B. The high
mutations rate of HCV has lead to many cases of resistance making it quite successful. This gives a
critical issue in the treatment aspect especially after the discontinuation of the combination treatment with
pegylated interferons which evoked an effective immune response. Polymorphisms of target structures
can provide optimized resistance as they pre-exist in patient strain naive to the speciﬁc DAAs. The
emergence of resistance associated variants is related with drug factors such as potency and exposure and
susceptibility. Combining DAAs with different modes of action usually provide an increased potency
and higher resistance barrier. Parameters determining selection are variant ﬁtness and drug resistance.
Fitness is likely to impact on persistence and the ability to compete with the original virus wild type strain
at the end of treatment. However, NS3 resistance mutations often leads to adversely affects of assembly.
Resistance mutations in HCV can be divided into two categories: primary and suppressor mutations.
Whereas primary mutations are responsible for the survival or the drug evasion, suppressor mutations
reverts the original phenotypic effect. Drug resistant HCV variants cannot be detected with molecular
methods because they usually have a lower replication ﬁtness. Resistant mutants tend to regress over
weeks if not months after the cessation of drug treatment. This allows the virus to reemerge as much as
two years later. The exact escape mechanisms are still undetermined. This makes HCV drug escape an
even more serious concern in the ﬁght against HCV [56, 57, 58].

1.3
Next Generation Sequencing
Next Generation Sequencing (NGS) also known as high throughput sequencing or second generation
sequencing is a term describing different sequencing technologies. Common for all these technologies
is their ability to do parallel or deep sequencing. This means that they sequence on millions of small
fragments in parallel. After the process it is possible to map these individual reads to a reference genome
for determining the genome locations. The important advantages of NGS data is the quality, robustness
and low noise, even though high expertise on both the wet-lab and the bioinformatic side is required. Ad-
ditionally, the clinical advantages of NGS is the ability to capture a broader spectrum of mutations and
genomes that can be investigated without bias. Also NGS has shown great advantages in microbiology
as well when determining pathogens. This is a great beneﬁt when tracing infection outbreaks. Disadvan-
tages of NGS is the required infrastructure of laboratories such as computer capacity, storage, and the
expertise required to analyze and interpret the data output. A state of the art NGS platform can generate
150,000,000 reads for approximately £ 1,000. Consequently, large sample batches would be have to run
to make it cost effective. [59, 60, 61, 62].

Prior to 2004, Sanger sequencing, developed by Frederick Sanger in 1977, was the go to technology
when sequencing DNA. Today Sanger sequencing is known as "ﬁrst generation sequencing". Despite that

1.3.2
NGS Technologies
Generally, NGS comprises 4 different major technologies. These are 454, ion Torrent, Illumina/Solexa
and Solid. All NGS methods use a three step sequencing process: library preparation, ampliﬁcation
and sequencing. For NGS technologies DNA is used for making libraries. Therefore, viral RNA would
have to be inversely transcribed to DNA before preparing the library. The approaches can either be
amplicon (from one single gene) or shotgun (random sequences across the entire genome). To choose
one technology over another depends on the available resources, the personal experience, ﬁnances and
what applications that are considered [68]. In the following the different technologies will be described:
454: Pyrosequencing
Introduced in 2005 by 454 Life Sciences was as the ﬁrst NGS technology brought to the market. It is
known as 454 GS20 pyrosequencing platform (Roche). This technology has been used for many years due
to great variety of applications and its long reads. However, as better technologies have emerged on the
market it was decided in 2016 to slowly phase the technology out of the market. In this technology a DNA
fragment is sheared with enzymatic digestion and ligated with oligonucleotide adapters. Afterwards,
it is attached to beads, ampliﬁed with PCR in an oil-water emulsion. The sequencing is conducted
with 3 enzymes: DNA polymerase, ATP sulfurylase, luciferase and apyrase along with adenosine 5’

1.3.4
NGS Analysis
This section gives a brief overview of NGS analysis which has been the workﬂow of this project. In
certain rare instances the ﬁrst required step is to make a de novo genome assembly which is typically
a low quality draft so forth the genome is not available. This is done through improvements in data
generations and in the algorithms analyzing the data. A single genome can often be the work result of
large groups of scientists and many years of data curation. After the genome assembly it is possible to
annotate regions that corresponds to biological relevance. The output of NGS systems result in gigabases
of raw sequence data. Hence, extensive analysis in-silico using different algorithms and applications is
necessary. The steps include genome assembly, preprocessing, mapping of reads, identiﬁcation of SNPs
and detection of insertions and further downstream processing. For preprocessing fastq ﬁles the ﬁrst thing
is to remove adaptor sequences and sequences of low quality. In case of paired-end sequences it should be
ensured that the trimmed reads is reﬂected in both its forward and reverse sequence. It is also necessary
to screen for host sequences especially for viral data because contamination of host sequences is a routine
scenario. Mapping to a reference genome is searching for positions in the genome that corresponds to
the reads. Mapping algorithms can either be hash based or compression based. Hash based algorithms
identify all words of a certain length in the reference genome and makes a table with a unique ID. The
programs of hash algorithms have large memory requirements. Compression based algorithms transform
the reference genome sequences into a tree like structure that contains all the possible sub-sequences
allowing a faster way to access and perform sequence comparisons on the reference genome. Mapping
is extremely important for the further downstream analysis. If the reference genome is of poor quality
it will have great impact on the results. Following the mapping the next step is to call for variants.
Deviations from the reference genome are known as variants which are determined by single nucleotide
polymorphisms (SNPs). SNPs are different from Single Nucleotide Variant (SNV) because SNPs refer
to a percentage of a whole population where as SNV does not have a corresponding frequency. SNPs can
tell something about a tendency of a population which is very important for the analysis. Before running
the calling program it can be necessary to sort and index the mapping output for the algorithm to run more
efﬁciently. It is important to have a full sequence coverage supported by several reads before executing
this step. Tools to identify variants fall into 4 categories: germline callers, copy number variants, somatic
callers, and identiﬁcation and structural variants identiﬁcation. The use of either of these tools depends on
what to look for. After identifying variants it is possible to go further and trying to genotype the mutants
on basis of the SNPs by identifying the mutations. Data analysis of NGS data is just an important factor
as the sequencing itself. The analysis makes a foundation of exploring and understanding mutations in
genomes in order to have a greater understanding of the organism [83, 63, 64].

AdapterRemoval v2 is a program that searches for and removes adapter contamination from NGS data

eventually wrong SNP calls. Therefore, AdapterRemoval is necessary to run on the dataﬁles to prevent
this from happening. AdapterRemoval v2 is able to run on any UNIX based platform. AdapterRemoval
v2 performs ungapped semiglobal alignment by using a variation of the Needleman-Wunsch algorithm
[89]. It can thus look for matches between the 3’ read end and 5’ adapter sequence end. The overall
principles of the program is shown in ﬁgure 2.2.

2.5
Genome Mapping with BWA
Burrows Wheeler Alignment tools (BWA) is a software package for mapping sequences against a large
reference genome. It comprises three algorithms: BWA-backtrack, BWA-SW and BWA-MEM. BWA-
backtrack is designed for Illumina sequence reads up to 100 bp, while BWA-SW and BWA-MEM for
longer sequences ranged from 70bp to 1 M bp. BWA-MEM and BWA-SW have similar features like long-
read support and split alignment. However, BWA-MEM, which is the latest, is generally recommended
for high-quality queries as it is faster and more accurate. BWA-MEM also has better performance than
BWA-backtrack for 70-100bp Illumina reads. Therefore, BWA-MEM was used in this project to allign
the fastq sequence fragments to the provided HCV 6a HK2 reference genome. BWA is based on backward
search with Burrows-Wheeler Transform which is a compression based algorithm that aligns sequencing
reads against a large reference sequence efﬁciently and allowing mismatches and gaps. An example of
the principle can be seen in Figure 2.3. The output of BWA is the standard SAM/BAM format. BWA
has shown better performances than several state of the art aligners and it scales well to large genomes.
BWA only processes a batch of reads at a time. it ﬁrst seeds an alignment with maximal exact matches
(MEM) by ﬁnding the longest exact match that covers the speciﬁc query position. [91, 92, 93]

2.6
Sorting and Indexing BAM ﬁles with SAMtools
SAMtools is a software program that provides various utilities for manipulating alignments in the SAM
format. It is widely used in genomics work ﬂows for post processing sequence alignment data. This
includes sorting, merging, indexing and generating alignments in a per-position format. It can store
alignments for reads up to 128 Mbp. SAM is short for Sequence Alignment/Map and is the general
format for storing sequence alignments. For the improvement of performance another format BAM
has been developed short for Binary Alignment/Map. BAM format keeps the exact same information
as SAM just in a binary compact representation. The SAM/BAM format have a header section and one
alignment section. The header section starts with character ‘@’, whereas lines in the alignment section do
not. The BAM ﬁles for the different timepoints and treatments were sorted and indexed with SAMtools.
Sorting is typically done by coordinate and is used to streamline data processing and to avoid loading
extra alignments into memory. The sorting can be quite computationally intensive but fortunately not for
Computerome in this case. Afterwards the BAM ﬁles were indexed after they have been position-sorted,
which is only possible for BAM ﬁles. Indexing the BAM ﬁles was for the only purpose of was to allow
fast random access to the BAM ﬁle making the SNP calling more rapid [95, 96, 97]. See appendix 6.2
for commands.
2.7
Call of Variants with LOFREQ
LOFREQ is a software program that models sequencing run error rates to call variants that occur in less
than 0.05 percent of a population. It has shown to have almost perfect speciﬁcity. Lofreq treats each
base in a coloumn through a Bernoulli trial based on the success/failure concept where failure equals
variant base. LOFREQ runs almost any type of aligned sequencing data since no dependent thresholds
for either machines or sequencing technology are used. It automatically adapts to changes in coverage
and sequencing quality and can therefore be applied to a variety of data-sets e.g. viral/quasi-species,
bacterial, metagenomics or somatic data. It is quite sensitive as it is able to predict variants that are below
the average base call quality. The output of LOFREQ is called variant call format (VCF). VCF is a text
ﬁle format that contains meta information lines, a header line, and data lines that contain information
about different positions and alleles with their respective frequencies which is useful for the workﬂow of
this project. LOFREQ was used on the BAM ﬁles that had been sorted with SAMtools[98]. See 6.6 for
script used.

2.9
Creating genome table for prototype HCV 6a HK2
In order to go from having SNPs of interest and be able to localize them in the genome to further localize
the coding AA required many steps. First of them was to blast the HCV 6a HK2 reference genome
from (L. Pham et al.) provided by Ulrik Fahnøe. The blasting was done with the NCBI blast tool [104].
The blast had a 97 percent identity with the standard HCV 6a genome on Uniprot [105]. Therefore the
reference genome on Uniprot was used to localize the AA residues of the respective HCV proteins in the
HCV 6a HK2 genome. The position spans were noted in the table. The total length of the polyprotein
and the genome was obtained through the DNA and Protein grouping tools [106], [107]. The 5’ and 3’
UTR genome regions were determined with help of the ExPaSy Translation tool which could identify
the coding region held up against the nucleotide genome sequence. The ExPasy (Expert Protein Analysis
System) Translation tools is an open source online portal operated by the Swiss Institute of Bioinformatics
[108, 109, 110]. The corresponding coding regions of the proteins were determined by multiplying the
polyprotein AA length from the ﬁrst AA residue to the start of the relevant protein with 3 to obtain
number of nucleotides and then adding the nucleotide length of the 5’UTR. The reference genome and
the polyprotein can be found in appendix 6.1 and 6.2. The genome table can be found in appendix 6.3.
2.10
Identifying mutations with ExPaSy translation tool
The SNPs of interest were inserted into the ExPaSy translation tool in the relevant fraction of the reference
sequence with enough nucleotide margins to avoid cut of the involved amino acid (AA) residue in relation
to the codon frame. The SNP positions were found manually in the reference genome (see appendix 6.1).
The same procedure was done for the HCV polyprotein in order to ﬁnd the position of the variant AA.
Afterwards the variant AA residue was located and compared to the reference AA. If the AA variant was
identical to the reference it was noted as a silent mutation.

3.7
Further discussion
In order to choose the right mutations for further research it is crucial to certify that the mutations do
lead to drug escape. Therefore it is necessary to consult previous studies to determine if any of the mu-
tations have been observed before with regards to persistence. If not there is not enough evidence to
conclude the identiﬁcation of the escape mutations then no conclusions can be drawn and no mutations
can be determined. Looking at the mutations found in this project it is likely that many of the mutations
were genetically hitchhiking on other mutations as they follow the same pattern. Pattern group 1 under
Sofosbuvir treatment could indicate suppressor mutations that rescues the phenotype after the S282T
mutation. The same goes for Velpatasvir and the L331V mutation. As pattern group 4 increase more
slowly over the time frame but maintains a rather high frequency at 70 percent this could go for a more
phenotypic selection and not necessarily for Velpatasvir escape. The 4 group 3 mutations under Sofosbu-
vir/Velpatasvir does not indicate any speciﬁc adaption as they seem to increase independently from the
other SNPs in both cases. Also the fact that the mutations occur for both treatments indicate that they
adapt to something that does not necessarily in relation to the drug treatment. It is possible that the muta-
tions could adapt against immunological responses by mutating on a genomic RNA level hence allowing
frame shift protein F to be translated or allow new properties to the secondary RNA structure. However
not enough evidence can be held up to this and further study is necessary. Regarding the mutations in
group 1 and 2 under the Velpatasvir treatment that had a very high frequency at day 9 could indicate a
sudden selection that was subsequently decreased and ultimately disappeared. This could either be on be-
half of a more effective variability mutation or because this mutations were fatal for the HCV generation
leaving the phenotype to vanish. It has been observed with the S282T phenotype before that it ultimately
disappeared after the selection pressure was removed. This could likely be the case for group 1 and 2
under Velpatasvir. Another important aspect to take into a count is the noise from the NGS readings. It is
impossible to avoid noise reading but for this project it can have had an impact on the interpretations. As
the noise seems to have appeared all over the reads it is likely that it could have affected the reads of the
selected SNPs. Nonetheless, the noise reads were of a quite low frequence and the ultimate impact on the
absolute results would be rather low. One great limitation for mutation analysis has been the small offer
of HCV protein crystal structures at PDB. This has limited the ability to display and try to understand
the mutations. This most likely lies in the fact that not enough research has been conducted in the area
and has therefore left a lot of unanswered questions. It is hence necessary for further studies to elucidate
the HCV proteins in order to get a greater understanding in the molecular biology of these in order to
subsequently determine resistance mutations as well as viability mutations.

4
Conclusion
In this project fastq data from (L. Pham et al.) was provided for bioinformatic analysis with the aim of
visualizing SNPs to eventually identify selected mutations that caused the HCV 6a HK2 prototype to
escape Sofosbuvir and Velptasvir. Throughout the study different tools have been applied in order to go
from raw fastq ﬁles to obtain VCF ﬁles visualizing variants. These VCF ﬁles were possible to further
analyze in R to obtain allele frequency matrices of all occurring SNPs from the treatments. Various SNPs
showed an interesting frequency pattern which could be used for further investigation. However many of
the mutations showed to be silent and seemed at ﬁrst not to have any effect on the drug escape mechanism.
Two well documented escape mutations were identiﬁed: S282T in NS5B against Sofosbuvir escape and
L31V in NS5A against Velpatasvir. S282T has been reported several times in studies to be the typical
resistance mutation towards sofosbuvir. Other escape mutations of interest were Q514R and Y555C
which also had been observed before working as resistance mutations for sofosbuvir. Other missense
mutations of interest that were observed were I585V in NS5B. The presented data in this project showed
noise for many SNPs which could have inﬂuenced the validity of the conclusions drawn. Furthermore, the
protein entries in PDB were of a limited selection as only 5 out of a potential 16 visualizable mutations.
This is a limitation to the project as it has not been possible to get a proper idea and impression of the
mutations. It has not been possible to determine escape mechanisms for the drug escape mutations as
little knowledge and studies have been conducted in this area.

4.1. FUTURE PERSPECTIVES
4.1
Future Perspectives
For future work it would be necessary to minimize the noise by improving the work ﬂow for the NGS
system to obtain more valid evidence. Nonetheless, it is likely that there always will be sources of errors
in experiments and therefore unavoidable. Besides the already documented resistance mutations S282T
and L31V it could be interesting to investigate the signiﬁcance of the mutations found in this project that
were not able to be visualized but have been documented before as drug escape mutations. Therefore
more research is needed in order to get a better understanding of the HCV proteins and their mechanisms
both as references but also as mutant variants as this would beneﬁt a fuller understanding of the actual
escape mechanisms. This is relevant especially for NS5A as the actual mechanism of this protein is barely
understood and yet targeted by multiple DAAs. The mutations found in this project before that were not
documented before but showed indications of selection could also be interesting to further investigate
to get an idea of their potential mechanisms. This project has showed that single inhibition treatment
of either Sofosbuvir or Velpatasvir is not an optimal solution as HCV is able to drug escape after a few
weeks for Sofosbuvir (S282T and as soon as 4 days for velpatasvir (L31V). Therefore this has afﬁrmed
the necessarity for combination treatments of DAAs. For future HCV treatments it could be an interesting
aspect to further investigate and research what DAA combinations are more efﬁcient than others with or
without the addition of standardized antiviral drugs in order to see if these have an improving effect. As
metioned earlier it is crucial for future studies to get a better insight into HCVs molecular biology and
interactions and escape mechanisms. This also includes the frame-shift protein F in order to clarify its
mechanisms as studies indicate that this protein could serve as an important factor for HCV persistence.
By understanding the escape mechanisms of the drug escape mutations it can help improving the counter
drug treatment for more efﬁcient eradication. Future perspectives of this study would be to look into
what individual DAAs that are most effective as they can effect the drug escape and if their is a possible
higher efﬁciant potential type of medicine against HCV that could replace DAAs in the futre.

6.7
FASTQC Modules
Module
Description
Basic Statistics
Provides simple composition statistics such as Encoding, Total Sequences,
length and overall %GC
Per
Base
Sequence
Quality
Draws a Box-Whisker plot.The red line is the median value,
the yellow bars are the inner quartil ranges (25-75%), the whiskers
represent the 10% and 90% points, and the blue line is the median quality.
Per Sequence Quality
Scores
Visualizes if subsets of sequences have universally
low quality values. Normally only a small percent
of the total sequences will have low quality.
Per
Base
Sequence
Content
Plots the proportion of each base position to what nucleotide
has been called. For a perfect random library there is no
difference in the proportions. The module raises an error if the difference
between A&T or G&C is greater than 20%.
Per Base GC Content
plots the GC content for each position. Random libraries
should have the plot line run horizontally.
Per Sequence GC Con-
tent
Measures the GC content across each sequence and compares
the average to a modelled normal distribution of GC content.
The module will raise an error if the deviations are larger than 30%.
Per Base N Content
Plots the percentage of base position on which N was called.
N represents a base call that could not be determined. The module will
raise an error if the N represents more than 20% of the content.
Sequence Length Dis-
tribution
generates a graph of the distribution of the different fragment sizes in the ﬁle.
The module raises an error if any sequences have zero length
Duplicate Sequences
Counts the degree of duplication for every sequence in the set and makes a plot
that shows the relative amount of sequences and their degrees of duplication
Overrepresented
Se-
quences
List sequences that make up more that 0.1 percent of the total. The module
raises and error if any sequence represents more than 1 percent of the total.
Overpresented Kmers
Counts the enrichment of every 5-mer in the library. It will plot the top 6 hits
to show the pattern of enrichment. Raises error if any k-mer is presented more
than a 10 fold at any base.
