Summary
The ﬁlamentous fungus Stachybotrys chartarum has been isolated in damp and water-
damaged buildings. The association of fungal growth of S. chartarum and potential
human health issues has brought much attention to the identiﬁcation of the chemical
potential of S. chartarum. This has lead to an understanding that two exclusive chemo-
types exist producing either macrocyclic trichothecenes or atranonens. Both chemotypes
have the production of a compound class called spirocyclic drimanes in common.
Spirocyclic drimanes have a chemical scaﬀold that is very homologous to that of ci-
chorine and aspernidine A produced by Aspergillus nidulans. Much eﬀort has been put
forward to elucidate the pathways leading to the production of cichorine and aspernidine
A in A. nidulans. Not only does the spirocyclic drimanes share much of its structure
with cichorine and aspernidine A, but some of the isolated spirocyclic drimanes share
chemical structure with some of the proposed intermediates in the biosynthesis of ci-
chorine and aspernidine A in A. nidulans.
Because of this chemical homology, the purpose of this study was to investigate the
biosynthetic pathway that leads to the production of spirocyclic drimanes in S. char-
tarum. The investigation utilized knowledge of previously isolated compounds from S.
chartarum, knowledge of secondary metabolic pathways in A. nidulans, and general
knowledge of secondary metabolism in combination to propose a pathway for synthesis
of the spirocyclic drimanes. This hypothesis was tested via heterologous expression of
genes from putative secondary metabolite gene clusters, found in the recently genome
sequenced S. chartarum. Furthermore, CRISPR-Cas9 gene targeting was attempted in
S. chartarum with a recently developed vector proven functional A. nidulans.
Via a bioinformatic investigation of the S. chartarum genome, two putative sec-
ondary metabolite gene clusters were found to have the genes thought to be capable
of producing spirocyclic drimanes. These clusters were designated clusters A and B.
Two genes from these clusters were successfully heterologously expressed in A. nidulans.
This was a putative transcription factor, stbR (cluster B), and a putative PKS, staA
(cluster A). The heterologous expression of the transcription factor induced production
of 7-hydroxy-4-methoxy-5-methylcoumarin, which have not been identiﬁed in A. nidu-
lans before, but has been identiﬁed in Aspergillus niger. The heterologous expression
of the PKS revealed that the product was orsellinic acid, also thought to be the initial
compound in the proposed pathway. The transformation of S. chartarum with CRISPR-
Cas9 vectors were possible, as S. chartarum transformants were able to grow under the
selection pressure of the antibiotic hygromycin B, to which S. chartarum was found to
be sensitive without the transformed vector. The success of gene targeting by CRISPR-
Cas9 could not be conﬁrmed as no altered phenotype compared to the wild-type was
found.
To my knowledge this is the ﬁrst study investigating secondary metabolic pathways
in S. chartarum, and the ﬁrst study attempting transformation of S. chartarum.

Sammenfatning
Den ﬁlamentøse svamp Stachybotrys chartarum er blevet isoleret fra fugtige og vand-
skadede huse. Forbindelsen mellem vækst af S. chartarum og mulige skadelige eﬀekter
for det menneskelige helbred har bragt megen opmærksomhed på identiﬁkation af den
kemiske diversitet som S. chartarum er i stand til at producere. Dette har ført til iden-
tiﬁkationen af to separate kemotyper, der producerer makrocykliske trichothecener eller
atranoner. Begge kemotyper kan producere en klasse af stoﬀer, der hedder spirocycliske
drimaner.
Spirocycliske drimaner har et kemisk skelet, der er meget lig det man ﬁnder i ci-
chorin og aspernidine A, der begge bliver produceret af Aspergillus nidulans. Der er
blevet brugt meget tid på at løse de biosyntestiske veje, der fører til produktionen af
cichorin og aspernidine A i A. nidulans. Spirocycliske drimaner deler ikke kun struk-
tur med cichorin og aspernidine A, men nogle spirocycliske drimaner deler også delvist
kemisk struktur med nogle intermediater i den foreslåede biosyntese af cichorin og as-
pernidine A i A. nidulans.
På grund af denne kemiske homologi er formålet med dette studie at undersøge den
biosyntetiske vej, der fører til produktionen af spirocycliske drimaner i S. chartarum.
Undersøgelsen kombinerer viden om tidligere isolerede stoﬀer fra S. chartarum, viden
om sekundær metabolisme i A. nidulans og generel viden om sekundær metabolisme til
at foreslå en syntesevej for spirocycliske drimaner. Hypotesen blev testet ved hjælp af
heterolog ekspression af gener fra foreslåede sekundær metabolisme genklustre, der blev
fundet i den for nyligt genomsekventerede S. chartarum. Derudover blev CRISPR-Cas9
gen targeting testet i S. chartarum, der for nyligt blev fundet funktionel i A. nidulans.
Via bioinformatisk undersøgelse af S. chartarum genomet, blev der fundet to mulige
sekundær metabolisme klustre, der havde den nødvendige sammensætning af gener til
at udføre den foreslåede biosyntesevej. Disse klustre blev navngivet kluster A og B. To
gener fra disse klustre blev heterologt udtrykt i A. nidulans. Dette var en formodet tran-
skriptions faktor stbR (kluster B) og en formodet PKS staA (kluster A). Den heterologe
ekspression af transkriptions faktoren inducerede produktionen af 7-hydroxy-4-methoxy-
5-methylcoumarin, der ikke tidligere var blevet isoleret fra A. nidulans, men er blevet
fundet i Aspergillus niger før. Den heterologe ekspression af PKSen gav produktion
af orselin syre, som også var foreslået som et udgangspunkt for den foreslåede biosyn-
tese. Transformationen af S. chartarum med CRISPR-Cas9 vektorer var mulig, da S.
chartarum kunne gro med antibiotikaen hygromycin B, som S. chartarum var sensitiv
overfor uden den transformerede vektor. Det kunne ikke bevises at transformationen af
CRISPR-Cas9 gav ændringer i DNA, da der ikke var nogen ændringer i fænotypen for
S. chartarum sammenlignet med vildtypen.
Så vidt jeg ved er dette det første studie, der undersøger sekundære metaboliske
biosynteseveje i S. chartarum, og det første studie, der forsøger transformation af S.
chartarum.

Introduction
Filamentous fungi have long been the interest of research and one focus has been on the
detrimental eﬀects of fungi. These include food and feed spoilage along with contamina-
tion of water-damaged buildings. To get a fungus to grow water and a growth substrate,
e.g. plant material from dead or living plants, is needed. The latter is always available
in buildings, why it is desirable to keep the former absent to prevent contamination
of fungi. Unfortunately, it is virtually impossible to prevent fungal contamination if a
building is water-damaged or very damp. There is a wide array of fungi, which have
been associated with water-damaged buildings (Andersen et al., 2011; Jarvis, 2003).
Basidiomycetes like brown- and white-rot have been found to compromise the building
integrity because of degradation of building materials (Schmidt, 2006), while several
ascomycetes do not break down building material to a great extent, but might have a
negative eﬀect human health (Andersen et al., 2011).
Due to the suspected negative health eﬀects of fungal contaminations, much eﬀort
has been put into elucidating chemistry, identiﬁcation and taxonomy of such fungi. A
fungus which has been found often in building material, and has therefore also been
investigated thoroughly, is Stachybotrys chartarum (Andersen et al., 2002; Jarvis, 2003;
Wang et al., 2014). The investigation of chemistry has been on isolating new compounds
as many of these have been assigned biological activity (Jarvis, 2003; Wang et al., 2014).
The biosynthesis of these metabolites have not been investigated as a molecular tool-
box for Stachybotrys chartarum has not been developed. An obstacle has been that it
is only recently that a genome of S. chartarum has been assembled (Semeiks et al., 2014).
Some metabolites of S. chartarum named spirocyclic drimanes have high homology
with metabolites isolated from Aspergillus nidulans, i.e. cichorine and aspernidine A. A.
nidulans is used as a model fungus to explain eucariotic molecular biology. Due to this
a good molecular biologic toolbox has been developed for A. nidulans (Nielsen et al.,
2008; Nødvig et al., 2015; Oakley et al., 1987). This toolbox has been used to eluci-
date secondary metabolism in A. nidulans, which has been studied extensively (Nielsen
et al., 2011b; Sanchez et al., 2012; Yaegashi et al., 2013), but heterologous expression
of secondary metabolism genes in A. nidulans from other fungi have also been tool for
elucidating secondary metabolism (Hansen et al., 2011; Nielsen et al., 2013).
Secondary metabolism has been and still is investigated on a broad spectrum of
organisms as it has oﬀered a wide array of bioactive compounds, and some drugs used
nowadays have been derived from natural sources, e.g. penicillin and statins (Dewick,
2011).
The discipline of understanding secondary metabolism is important, as this
could lead to reprogramming of secondary metabolism pathways and development of
new active compounds. Understanding biosynthetic pathways can also lead to uncov-
ering unknown metabolites as only a fraction of the chemical capability of a fungus is
expressed in laboratory conditions (Frisvad et al., 2008; Nielsen et al., 2011b).
The purpose of this study was to investigate the metabolic pathway leading to the
production of spirocyclic drimanes. Heterologous expression of genes from S. chartarum
in A. nidulans was used to understand their activity. Also, a development of a genomic
toolbox for S. chartarum was begun by transformation of gene targeting CRISPR-Cas9
vectors. To my knowledge this study will be the ﬁrst doing biosynthetic pathway eluci-
dation in and transformation of S. chartarum.

1.1
Stachybotrys chartarum, the gypsum fungus
The ﬁlamentous fungus Stachybotrys chartarum is often isolated when examining indoor
fungi in water-damaged buildings, especially on gypsum wallboards (Andersen et al.,
2016). The majority of Stachybotrys in this group is the species S. chartarum and this
species has been associated with idiopathic pulmonary hemorrhage in infants (Andersen
et al., 2002; Jarvis, 2003). For this reason, much research to identify the pathogenic fun-
gus has been done, as not all isolates identiﬁed as S. chartarum has been found highly
pathogenic. Furthermore, eﬀort has been put towards ﬁnding the pathogenic metabo-
lite or metabolites (Andersen et al., 2002; Jarvis, 2003). Through this eﬀort, especially
Satratoxin H, one of many macrocyclic trichothecenes produced by S. chartarum, has
been found to cause severe health problems (Jarvis, 2003). Very interesting is it that not
all S. chartarum produce macrocyclic trichothecenes, but instead produce a variety of
atranones (Andersen et al., 2002). As can be seen in ﬁgure 1, except from both being of
terpenoid origin, these two types of compounds are of very diﬀerent biosynthetic origin
(Semeiks et al., 2014).
Figure 1. The two chemotypes of S. chartarum strains produce either atranones or macrocyclic tri-
chothecenes like satratoxin H (Jarvis, 2003).
It is only recently in a study by Semeiks et al. (2014) that the whole genome of
S. chartarum has been assembled. The study lead to a genomic understanding of the
diﬀerent chemotypes and which genes were involved in the production of the diverse
chemotypical compounds. The study by Semeiks et al. (2014) compares the exclusive
and common trichothecene genes found the two chemotypes of S. chartarum and the
exclusive atranone genes, which were only found in S. chartarum and S. chlorohalonata
of the atranone chemotype. The genomes has been uploaded to genbank.

1.2
Aspergillus nidulans, the model fungus
In recent years much eﬀort has been put into investigating the metabolism of A. nidulans
and to ﬁnd and link genes to secondary metabolites (Lo et al., 2012; Nielsen et al., 2011b;
Sanchez et al., 2010). A study by Nielsen et al. (2011b) investigates the metabolic po-
tential in A. nidulans. They ﬁnd that more metabolic potential is nested in the genome
of A. nidulans as only nine out of 32 putative PKS genes have been linked to known
metabolites. Some of the investigated pathways produce metabolites from derivatives
of orsellinic acid. These are orsellinic acid produced by the PKS orsA initiating the
synthesis of lecanoric acid, violaceol I/II, and F9775 A/B, and 3,5-dimethylorsellinic

acid produced by ausA initiating the synthesis of austinol and dehydroaustinol.
The biosynthesis of cichorine was ﬁrst investigated by Sanchez et al. (2012) in which
they identiﬁed the PKS (AN6448/pkbA) as the ﬁrst step in the biosynthesis. Further-
more, they found four tailoring enzymes (cicB, cicC, cicE and cicH), a regulatory gene
(cicD) and a transporter gene (cicA). Another study of the cichorine pathway by Nielsen
et al. (2016) also investigated the biosynthesis of cichorin and identiﬁed genes by over-
expression of a transcription factor (pkbR). Deletions of identiﬁed genes upregulated by

1.3
Secondary metabolism of fungi
Fungi produce secondary metabolites to increase the ﬁtness of the organism.
Their
function can either be protection against other fungi or bacteria (antibiotics), toxins to
harm or suppress substrate (e.g. plants or animals), protection against the environment
(e.g. pigmentation) etc. Many of the known compounds do not yet have any assigned
bioactivity (Dewick, 2011; Frisvad et al., 2008). The high diversity of compounds is not
always linked to a high diversity in biological activity, but rather a sign of biological
evolution. The evolution has produced a high level of chemical diversity. This high
diversity is an evolutionary attempt to beneﬁt the organism. There is a big diﬀerence in
primary and secondary metabolism. Primary metabolism has been perfected to produce
only one product every time while secondary metabolism to some degree is made to be
imperfect (Firn and Jones, 2000).
The secondary metabolism uses compounds from primary metabolism as building
blocks. There are generally three main classes of compounds: terpenes, non-ribosomal
peptides and polyketides. Terpenes are composed of isoprene units (C5 units), non-
ribosomal peptides are joined amino acids, while polyketides joins units of a ster unit
acetyl-Coenzyme A and extends with decarboxylated malonyl-CoA units (C2 units).
Therefore, it is often possible to identify the origin of many compounds, but there are
numerous examples of mixed origin in compounds, e.g. both terpene and polyketide
parts (named meroterpenoids) in the structure. Furthermore, intermediates of primary
metabolism (e.g. amino acid biosynthesis) can also be integrated in synthesis of sec-
ondary metabolites (Dewick, 2011).
With the basic building blocks assembled secondary metabolites are usually tailored
to give even more diversity. This is in the form of alkylation, reduction/oxidation, halo-
genation, rearrangements, glycosylation etc. (Dewick, 2011).

Polyketides
Polyketides are produced by a starter unit of acetyl-CoA linked together with decar-
boxylated malonyl-CoA units, to form a chain. The reaction is catalyzed by an enzyme
class named polyketide synthases (PKSs). As the units are joined together they form a
carbon chain, in which every second carbon is a carboxyl. This is only the case if the
PKS does not have domains that reduce the carboxyl to an alcohol (ketoreductase), dou-
ble bonded alkene (dehydratase), or even a single bonded alkyl chain (enoyl reductase).
These diverse options are programmed into the PKS enzyme and the individual units
appended in the polyketide chain are often diﬀerentiated between level of reduction. An
overview of the most important domains in a PKS can be found in table 4 (Cox, 2007).
Much eﬀort has been put forward in ﬁnding an bioinformatic analysis method to
determine the products of unknown PKSs. Fungal PKSs are usually iterative, meaning
that each domain is used several times. Because of this it is not straight forward to
determine the length of the polyketide chain produced by an unknown PKS. This is op-
posed to the modular structure of bacterial PKSs, in which the sequence of modules in
the PKS dictates how many chain elongations are done to produce the ﬁnal polyketide,

Methodology
To investigate the proposed pathway of spirorocyclic drimanes heterologous expression
in A. nidulans of genes thought to be a part of the biosynthesis was done, along with
attempts to target the same genes in S. chartarum. Also, chemical analysis of extracts
from fungi were essential to investigate the hypothesised pathway and to evaluate het-
erologous expression and gene targeting.
The methodology behind these assays are
explained in this section.
2.1
Molecular biology
Many studies have investigated and developed a molecular toolbox for A. nidulans
(Nielsen et al., 2008; Nødvig et al., 2015; Oakley et al., 1987).
Rapid development
of the molecular understanding of A. nidulans in recent time has been largely aided by
the assembly and availability of the full genome on "The Aspergillus Genome Database"
(www.aspergillusgenome.org). Furthermore, the availability has also aided the eluci-
dation of biosynthetic pathways for many metabolites, which is also updated on the
database. Not only has the database the full A. nidulans genome, but also the full
genomes of A. niger, A. oryzae, and A. fumigatus are available. Also, genes from sev-
eral other Aspergilli are searchable through the many investigative tools on the webpage
(Cerqueira et al., 2014).
2.1.1
Heterologous expression
Heterologous expression of biosynthetic enzymes is one of the basic principles in biosyn-
thetic pathway elucidation. This method is especially useful when elucidating pathways
in an organism in which the genetic toolbox is poorly developed. Therefore, by trans-
ferring the genes to an organism with a developed toolbox, e.g. A. nidulans, one can
have more control over the process of expression (Hansen et al., 2011).
Some of the important preliminary work to make heterologous expression eﬃcient
in A. nidulans is presented in the article by Nielsen et al. (2008). In this, the gene
nkuA, which is part of the mechanism to create non-homologous end joining (NHEJ),
is transiently disrupted. Thereby, the primary method of DNA repair is homologous
recombination (HR). If HR is the only functional DNA repair mechanism, heterologous
expression of genes via directed integration is possible if ﬂanks of the transformation
substrate is homologous to the section of genome where integration is desired. Tran-
sient disruption instead of deletion of nkuA gene was desired in the article because
absence of the NHEJ repair system results in shortened telomeres and susceptibility to
γ-irradiation in A. nidulans. The transient disruption gives the possibility of reconstitu-
tion of the nkuA gene by direct repeat elimination of the fungal marker. This marker is
pyrG that makes A. nidulans uracil and uridine prototroph, but also makes it sensible
to 5-ﬂuoroorotic acid (5-FOA) (Oakley et al., 1987). If transformants with the disrupted
nkuA gene are grown on plates with 5-FOA, only isolates with the pyrG eliminated and
nkuA reconstituted, will grow (Nielsen et al., 2008).
A recent example of heterologous expression is the study by Hansen et al. (2011)
in which they heterologously express a PKS from Penecillium brevicompactum in A.
nidulans. The aim of the project was to ﬁnd the product of the putative PKS gene
mpaC, but the study also aimed to develop an expression platform for elucidation of
genes. This was achieved by directed integration by HE in A. nidulans in a pre-speciﬁed

2.1.2
Gene targeting - CRISPR
With heterologous expression being one of the basic principle of biosynthetic pathway
elucidation, gene targeting is another. This method disrupts or deletes a gene and an
alteration in the phenotype, e.g. metabolic proﬁle or pigmentation, if this is observed,
can be linked to the disrupted gene. An example of gene targeting has already been
presented in the study by Nielsen et al. (2008). This is done by inserting a fungal marker
inside the gene to be disrupted. This gives a functional integration of the marker, but
disrupts the targeted gene.
Another important method of gene targeting, which was not fully developed for
ﬁlamentous fungi until recently by Nødvig et al. (2015), is the clustered regularly inter-
spaced short palindromic repeats-Cas9 (CRISPR-Cas9) system. In this system the gene
targeting is carried out by transformation of a vector carrying the gene for the Cas9 nu-
clease, single chimeric guide RNA (sgRNA) sequence, fungal plasmid replication region
(AMA1) and a fungal marker. This vector is not meant to integrate and are therefore
not cut prior to transformation. As long as the transformant is grown on the selective
media they will retain the plasmid, but if selective pressure is stalled the transformant is
likely to get rid of the CRISPR-Cas9 plasmid (Doudna and Charpentier, 2014; Nødvig
et al., 2015).
The mutagenesis of CRISPR-Cas9 is directed by a sequence in the sgRNA named
the protospacer, which is 20bp long. The protospacer is a copy of the sequence which
is desired to target. The only requirement for the target sequence is the presence of a
palindromic adjacent motif (PAM) sequence adjacent to the protospacer. For the Cas9
nuclease system this motif has to be NGG, while NAG is also possible, but with lower
eﬃciency. With this in mind it is possible to target virtually every gene in the genome
as NGG is a very frequent motif (Doudna and Charpentier, 2014; Nødvig et al., 2015).
When the CRISPR-Cas9 vector is transformed into the host the mode of action
is that the Cas9 nuclease is produced along with the sgRNA. The protospacer of the
sgRNA will basepair with the target region. The other part of the sgRNA is recognized
by Cas9 and the nuclease does a double stranded blunt break of the DNA sequence.
Afterwards, when Cas9 and the sgRNA is not present the host has to preform DNA
repair. This is done by either HR or NHEJ. If the sequence is correctly assembled by
HR or NHEJ the CRISPR-Cas9 system can repeat the directed restriction, but if the
double stranded break is repaired with errors, e.g. insertions, the CRISPR-Cas9 system
can no longer make the double stranded break, as the protospacer sgRNA cannot an-
neal to the target sequence. Thereby, if the target sequence is an open reading frame,
the translation will have a frameshift, and most likely a premature stop codon. The
annealing and restriction of the CRISPR-Cas9 system is depicted in ﬁgure 13 (Doudna
and Charpentier, 2014).
The CRISPR-Cas9 vector can be combined with a gene targeting vector similar to
the one used by Nielsen et al. (2008). The CRISPR-Cas9 vector will then carry out
the double-stranded break and the co-transformed vector will be integrated by HR and
the target gene will be disrupted. The co-transformation of the CRISPR-Cas9 vector
greatly increases the frequency of successful gene targeting as compared to transfor-
mation without it. This technique can be used with an cut or uncut gene targeting
vector, with the circular having much higher successrate (Nødvig et al., 2015). This is

2.2
Chemical analysis - HPLC
The goal of chemical analysis in natural product chemistry is to separate and identify
single components in an extract from e.g. a fungal culture. One of the most important
tools, to do separation of a complex extract containing numerous compounds, has been
liquid chromatography. In liquid chromatography a solid stationary phase and a liquid
mobile phase interacts with the extract in order to separate the individual components.
The most often used liquid chromatography nowadays is that of high-performance liquid
chromatography (HPLC). Many diﬀerent methods can be used in HPLC, but the one
used in this project is reverse phase (RP) HPLC, as this is very versatile and can be
used to separate compounds with a broad range of polarity (Meyer, 2005).
RP-HPLC forces the polar mobile phase through a tightly packed apolar column,
which is ﬁlled with extremely small particles, which acts as the stationary phase. The
compound extract is then added to the mobile phase in the inﬂow of the column and the
mixture of compounds in the extract interact more or less with the stationary phase.
As the stationary phase is apolar it will retain such compounds more than polar com-
pounds, and polar compounds elute before less polar or apolar compounds. Hereby it is
possible to separate an extract with a wide array of compounds to its single components
(Shalliker and Kayillo, 2005).
If compounds coelute in a speciﬁc setting the stationary and mobile phases can
be customized to optimize separation of a speciﬁc extract. The small particles in the
stationary phase are commonly made of porous silica and decorated with an organic
compound such as octadecyl (C18), phenyl hexyl, nitrile, or other similar compounds.
Compounds having very similar polarity and molecular weight can be separated if one
of these has e.g. an aromatic ring, which interacts well with a phenyl hexyl stationary
phase instead of a C18 stationary phase (Shalliker and Kayillo, 2005).
The stationary phase is one parameter, which can be altered while the mobile phase
is also very much customizable. As previously mentioned the mobile phase in RP-HPLC
is polar, but by altering the polarity and nature of the mobile phase, separation is also
altered. The mobile phase in RP-HPLC usually consists of a mixture of water and an
organic solvent like methanol or tetrahydrofuran, but the usual organic solvent of choice
in RP-HPLC is acetonitrile (ACN). Methanol, tetrahydrofuran and ACN all have in
common that they are less polar than water, but more polar than the stationary phase.
Therefore, the ratio of water and ACN is essential to how the separation will commence.
The mixture of ACN and water can either have a constant ratio (isocratic elution) or
change throughout the separation (gradient elution). In isocratic elution the retention
of compounds are not changed, thus compounds interacting heavily with the stationary
phase will take a long time to elute from the column. Therefore, gradient elution is
usually preferred when there is little or no previous knowledge of the compounds to be
separated, as is the case for natural extracts. In gradient elution, increasing the ACN
ratio speeds up compounds which are retained in the stationary phase, while separation
of similar compounds is still possible (Snyder et al., 2010).
2.2.1
HPLC detection methods
With the compounds eluting from the column they are detected by some sort of detec-
tor. This detector can be of many sorts, but the ones utilized in this project is diode
array detection (DAD) and mass spectrometry (MS).
18
==PAGE:30==
In DAD the eluting ﬂow from the column is lead through a chamber in which a poly-
chromatic light is lead through it. The wavelengths of the light are in the UV and visible
light spectrum (∼200-700 nm). After the light has been lead through the chamber a
detector separates and detects the magnitude of each detected wavelength and records
a spectrum. This way if a compound absorbs light in the UV or visible area it can be
detected by the DAD, and it is possible to identify the compound by the UV-spectrum,
as this is usually very speciﬁc for any given compound. It can sometimes be hard to
distinguish two very similar compounds as conjugated double bond are what gives rise
to absorption of light. Thus, small changes, e.g. methylations, are not always detectable
by DAD, but will need other methods for detection (Snyder et al., 2010).
A challenge in utilizing UV spectrums for identiﬁcation is that previous identiﬁcation
is needed. Thus, identiﬁcation of unknown compounds by UV spectroscopy is not an
easy task. Therefore, MS spectroscopy is currently used more often when working with
identiﬁcation unknown compounds, but UV and MS used in combination is a powerful
tool in chemical analysis (Nielsen et al., 2011a).

MS is used to detect a wide array of compounds various origin and are highly sen-
sitive to even small variations of compounds.
One MS analysis technique is named
time-of-ﬂight (TOF). In this gaseous charged compounds ﬂy through a magnetic ﬁeld
and depending on their weight and the number of charges on them, their m/z ratio, they
will ﬂy faster or slower through the magnetic ﬁeld. By detecting when the charged com-
pound is given a kinetic kick and when it hits the detector the MS analyser can calculate
the mass of the compound. The charge applied to the compounds can be either positive
or negative, and depends on the settings of the MS detector (Niessen, 2005). The widest
detection of compounds is found in positive ionisation, but some compounds are only
detected in one of the two ionisation modes (Nielsen et al., 2011a). When the charge is

fact can also be used as a ﬁngerprint, as it is speciﬁc for any compound (Niessen, 2005).

2.2.2
HPLC data analysis
In HPLC data analysis it is important to distinguish between chromatogram and spec-
trum. The spectrum is recorded at any moment by the detection method and gives a
presentation of the detection range at that speciﬁc moment. A chromatogram, on the
other hand, is detection through time, and can be thought of as multiple spectrums
beside each other. Therefore, in a HPLC-UV dataset, the x-axis can be deﬁned as time,
y-axis as the level of detection, and the z-axis are the diﬀerent detected wavelengths.
The zy-plane will then give spectrums, while the xy-plane will give chromatograms.
The default DAD chromatogram is usually a representation of a speciﬁc wavelength,
e.g. 210nm or 254nm. The reason for this rather low wavelength in the default chro-
matogram is that almost all optically active compounds, other than the mobile phase,
usually will absorb light at these low wavelengths (Snyder et al., 2010).
In MS detection there are two ways of making the general chromatogram. One is
the total ion chromatogram (TIC), which plots the total number of ions received of all
recorded masses at a speciﬁc moment. The other is the base peak chromatogram (BPC),
which plots the intensity of the most intense ion at a speciﬁc moment. A BPC is usually
better than TIC for detecting elution of compounds in extracts with many compounds,
as it takes away all less prominent peaks. Since an eluting compound will have high
intensity at the time of elution a BPC will be able to show this. A last type of MS
chromatogram is the extracted ion chromatogram (EIC) which is a chromatogram of a
chosen ion. This is a xy-plane in the HPLC-MS dataset (Snyder et al., 2010).
A fungal extract is a mixture of numerous compounds, hence the data will also be
somewhat hard to interpret as several compounds might elute simultaneously. A sin-
gle compound, eluting solitarily, usually results in a more or less symmetrical peak in
the chromatogram while co-eluting compounds will result in asymmetrical peaks in the
chromatogram. The most eﬀective method, to distinguish which masses in a spectrum
belongs to which compound, is deconvolution. This technique is most eﬀective in MS
detection as one can use EIC to very speciﬁccally identify masses in the spectrum. Two
EIC of two diﬀerent masses in a spectrum will determine if these masses belong to the
same compound. If retention time of the maxima of the two EIC is diﬀerent the two
masses belong to diﬀerent compounds. This method has wide application when analysing
HPLC-MS data (Snyder et al., 2010). An example of deconvolution is shown in ﬁgure 15.

Molecular work with S. chartarum
As previously mentioned there has not been published any research on molecular work
with Stachybotrys, and therefore some initial tests of sensitivity were carried out. One
of the most often used selection markers in molecular work with A. nidulans is the AF-
pyrG marker and absence of its activity can be checked by inoculation on media with
5-FOA. This requires that the organism is sensitive to 5-FOA. Also, a marker is needed
for initial selection of transformations. Such a marker can be resistance to a antibiotic
like hygromycin B, but this requires the organism to be sensitive to it. Finally, the for-
mation of protoplasts might be possible, but viability and competence of the protoplasts
are not a guarantee when working with a new organism. The viability of the protoplasts
after being frozen (-80◦C) and thawed was checked by plating these on transformation
media (TM). The assays of sensitivity were done by inoculating spores on the selection
plate (5-FOA or hyg) and then inoculating the second plate without selection with the
same inoculation needle. This ensured that spores on the inoculation needle were able
to grow if the selection pressure was not present. The sensitivity towards 5-FOA and
hygromycin B as well as a test of viability of protoplasts are shown in ﬁgure 29.

Discussion
The results from the heterologous expression in A. nidulans and investigation of the
metabolic capability of S. chartarum supports the proposed pathway of spirocyclic dri-
manes. A PKS proposed to be part of the biosynthesis staA was heterologously ex-
pressed in A. nidulans and the product was found to be orsellinic acid, which was also
proposed to be a possible initial substrate of the pathway. Furthermore, CRIPSR-Cas9
gene-targeting was tried in S. chartarum, but no assays were proven to be successful.
5.1
Biosynthesis of spirocyclic drimanes
The hypothesised biosynthesis of spirocyclic drimanes was found to have some support
in the bioinformatic study of S. chartarum, with some gene clusters having what might
be the correct combination of genes. The found homology of cluster A with the genes
found in A. nidulans, which carries out similar reactions, give conﬁdence in the proposed
biosynthesis and the putative cluster. Furthermore, previous studies have isolated many
of the intermediates thought to be part of the proposed biosynthesis. The molecular ions
of these intermediates have also been identiﬁed by HPLC analysis of the extracts of S.
chartarum. All these conclusions have given conﬁdence in the hypothetical biosynthesis.
The hypothesised biosynthesis did not take stereospciﬁcity into consideration in or-
der to make a simple proposal. The diversity of spirocyclic drimanes (Wang et al., 2014)
propose that diﬀerent stereocenters can arise from the biosynthesis. Some stereocenters
are the same in all isolated compounds. These are the carbon #10 spirocenter, the car-
bon #15 proton and the carbon #17 methyl. These conﬁgurations are shown in ﬁgure
34. This ﬁgure also shows how the diversity of the stereospeciﬁc centers on carbon #11
and #20 might be formed. Due to the Wagner-Meerwein rearrangement via the carboca-
tion the attack of the proton the stereocenter on carbon #11 can be formed as an α or β
conﬁguration of the methyl. Furthermore, the carbocation which ends the ringformation
of the terpene moiety allow unspeciﬁc attack of the water molecule to create both the
α or β conﬁguration of the alcohol. These possible conﬁgurations are all shown ﬁgure 34.

5.2
Heterologous expression in A. nidulans
The heterologous expression of the staA PKS in A. nidulans found orsellinic acid as
the product of the PKS. Furthermore, cross-feeding between the native A. nidulans
pathways utilizing orsellinic acid and the orsellinic acid produced by the heterologously
expressed staA, was not observed. An explanation of this could be the localisation of
the expressed genes to synthesize the compounds (Kistler and Broz, 2015). If the het-
erologously expressed staA produces high amounts of orsellinic acid in a compartment
which is not the same as the tailoring enzymes, conversion of orsellinic acid will not
commence and it will accumulate as is observed in the extracts. A way for A. nidulans
to utilize orsellinic acid, which is produced in the wrong compartment, would be if a
transporter existed that could transport orsellinic acid to the correct compartment, but
this does not seem to be the case.
Another explanation for the absence of cross-feeding could be that tailoring enzymes
in A. nidulans that carry out the production of lecanoric acid, violaceol I and II, and
F9775 A and B does so by docking enzymes together. If the heterologously expressed
PKS that produces orsellinic acid cannot dock to the tailoring enzymes as the native A.
nidulans PKS does, then the conversion of orsellinic acid to the products might not be
carried out (Cox, 2007).

5.3
CRISPR in S. chartarum
The gene targeting done by Nødvig et al. (2015) was very eﬀective and the gene tar-
geting was almost simultaneous with the transformation. This is seen as some of the
transformants targeting yA were yellow as they appeared on the transformation plate.
The targeting of the pigmentation gene in S. chartarum did not produce any changed
phenotype for any of the four targeted genes during a period of more than two weeks.
Therefore, targeting was concluded not to be successful.
The attempt to introduce CRISPR-Cas9 mutations in S. chartarum was done by
utilizing the plasmids synthesized in the article by Nødvig et al. (2015). As mentioned
earlier these have been optimized for usage in A. nidulans, thus promoters and ter-
minators were chosen to accommodate this. These promoters were Ptef to promote
transcription of Cas9, Pgpd to promote production of the sgRNA fragment and PtrpC
to promote production of the resistance marker for hygromycin B (Nødvig et al., 2015).
These all have to be recognized by S. chartarum for the CRISPR-Cas9 system to work.
The hygromycin resistance marker was functional, as S. chartarum transformants were
able to grow. Also, the transcription of Cas9 was found positive, as the Cas9-eGFP
transformants appeared to have ﬂuorescence in the mycelium. This leaves the conﬁr-
mation of transcription of the sgRNA to be found. If the sgRNA is not transcribed
CRISPR-Cas9 will not be successful.
Another important factor is the localisation of Cas9. This was investigated in sec-
tion 4.5.1 and some localisation was found, but the low level of eGFP signal made it
hard to conclude. A more thorough analysis could be done if the mycelium was also
stained with DAPI to illuminate the nucleus. If the signal from the DAPI staining and
the signal from the Cas9-eGFP protein are co-located, the localisation of the expressed
Cas9-eGFP would be in the nucleus.
Finally, the designed protospacers might not function. If the sequence has an error
in the part where the protospacer should anneal, or another unknown factor inhibits an-
nealing, designing new protospacers might carry out the gene targeting. It has previously
been found that designing new protospacers in the same gene could get CRISPR-Cas9
to function (Hoof, 2016).
The two chemotypes had diﬀerences in transformations with the CRISPR-Cas9 vec-
tors. The atranone chemotype (IBT40288) was very easy to transform and grew well
and remained resistant to hygromycin throughout the time of the study. The satratoxin
chemotype (IBT40293) was often not able to grow when transferred from the transfor-
mation plate to a new plate. It is possible that there is a diﬀerence in the sensitivity
towards hygromycin in the two chemotypes of S. chartarum. The concentration of hy-
gromycin in the initial tests of sensitivity were 0.1 mg/ml while the concentrations in
transformations were 0.075 mg/ml. This alteration was due to diﬀerent transformation
protocols used in the lab. If the satratoxin chemotype of S. chartarum were more resis-
tant to hygromycin, this might allow for some of the protoplasts to grown without the
transformed CRISPR-Cas9 vector on the transformation media.

5.4
Future perspectives
As previously mentioned this is to my knowledge the ﬁrst attempt to do elucidations
of metabolic pathways and carrying out molecular biologic alterations in S. chartarum.
This work has been supported by the array of compounds isolated and identiﬁed from
S. chartarum, but more thorough investigation is needed in order to better determine
the exact mechanism of the pathway to produce spirocyclic drimanes.
An initial step would be to create a pyrG deletion in S. chartarum as this might en-
able the AFpyrG selection marker to be used, which is also often used in transformation
of A. nidulans. This is a recyclable marker and can therefore be used for several iter-
ations of molecular biology alterations. Also, over expression of a transcription factor
found in cluster A or B in S. chartarum would reveal if the proposed pathway actually
belongs to one cluster or the other. If none of these over expression studies induce pro-
duction of spirocyclic drimanes another homologous cluster might be tested.
Also, further investigation of CRISPR-Cas9 gene-targeting in S. chartarum is needed.
More protospacers could be tested, or alterations in the cloning vector might get the
system to function in S. chartarum.
Finally, an heterologous expression of more genes from cluster A or B in A. nidulans
and ultimately the whole putative cluster producing spirocyclic drimanes would help
elucidate the nature of the metabolic pathway. If all intermediates and derivatives of
the pathway are also produced in A. nidulans, it would indeed support the proposal of
the matrix like pathway, which would induce production of several diﬀerent compounds
instead of one or few.

6
Conclusion
The goal of this study has been to investigate the pathway of the spirocyclic drimanes
in S. chartarum. Through knowledge of A. nidulans metabolism, the chemical potential
of S. chartarum, and general knowledge of secondary metabolism of fungi, a pathway
for production of spirocyclic drimanes has been proposed.
To investigate the biosynthetic pathway the S. chartarum genome was explored,
which lead to identiﬁcation of two putative secondary metabolic gene clusters contain-
ing the genes thought to be necessary in the proposed pathway of spirocyclic drimanes.
Genes in these clusters had homology with genes in clusters in A. nidulans, which pro-
duces compounds that are highly homologous to the spirocyclic drimanes. Two genes
from the S. chartarum gene clusters were successfully expressed in A. nidulans. One be-
ing a putative transcription factor (stbR) and the other a putative PKS (staA). The tran-
scription factor induced production of 7-hydroxy-4-methoxy-5-methylcoumarin, which
has not been identiﬁed in A. nidulans before. The heterologous expression of the PKS
lead to identiﬁcation of the product of the PKS, which was orsellinic acid. Orsellinic acid
was also proposed to be the initial compound in the proposed pathway, thus supporting
the initial part of the proposed pathway. Even though orsellinic acid is also produced
as an initial compound in secondary metabolism of A. nidulans, no crossfeeding of the
heterologously expressed PKS and the native A. nidulans tailoring genes was observed.
Extraction of metabolites from S. chartarum were analysed by HPLC-MS and inter-
mediates of the proposed pathway were identiﬁed. Some of these intermediates had been
isolated from S. chartarum before. One of the ﬁnal products of the proposed pathway,
stachybotrylactam, could not be identiﬁed, as the standard of the stachybotrylactam
did not have the same retention time as other compounds in the extracts. Many iso-
mers and derivatives of stachybotrylactam exist, and many of these were identiﬁed and
thought to be possible products of the pathway instead of stachybotrylactam.
CRISPR-Cas9 gene targeting was attempted to target genes in S. chartarum. Trans-
formation of S. chartarum has not been reported, thus initial assays were done to develop
a transformation strategy.
After successful transformation of CRISPR-Cas9 vectors,
gene targeting by CRIPSR-Cas9 was not proven eﬀective in any of the transformed S.
chartarum, as no altered phenotype was not observed.
It is proposed that the hypothesised pathway is more or less a matrix of compounds
instead of an assembly line. If indeed the pathway produces a matrix of compounds
these compounds could be used as various building blocks in development of drugs or
other bioactive compounds, as many of the spriocyclic drimanes already has an observed
biologic activity.
The results found in this study could only partly conﬁrm the hypothesised pathway.
A more thorough study of the pathway would give more insight into the chemical di-
versity of the pathway. This could be investigated by heterologous expression of the
whole pathway in A. nidulans. Also, alteration of the CRISPR-Cas9 vector might ac-
commodate CRISPR-Cas9 gene targeting in S. chartarum. Finally, knowledge of the
molecular toolbox available in A. nidulans could be used to develop a similar toolbox
for S. chartarum.

Plug extraction procedure 
 
 
The plug extraction procedure is made for screening of secondary metabolites 
(extra- and intracellular) from semi solid (agar) fungal cultures grown for 
normally 7 days (up to 14 days) on selected media.  
 
This method is not suited for liquid media. 
 
This procedure is not suitable for media containing large quantities of 
interfering material (e.g. cheese, meat agars) or agars where the cultures are 
very stressed by addition of e.g. glycerol. This is a qualitative metabolite 
profiling method used for HPLC, LC-MS, and direct injection-MS. This 
procedure is not for quantitative measurements! 
 
 
If natural material samples - such as cheese, meat etc. - are to be 
analyzed consult with KFN before extracting samples. Extra care must 
be taken during clean-up to avoid clogging up the HPLC.

Safety 
 
Besides common sense and normal laboratory safety 
rules – the following things are required 
 
All tubes and vials must be labelled (see below) with name, date, and unique 
number. 
 
As many fungal cultures contain toxic substances including carcinogenic 
compounds (known and unknown) all extracts must be considered toxic. 
Gloves (Neopro gloves, not latex) must be worn and the work must be 
performed in a fume hood. 
 
All work must be performed on a stainless steel surface which can be cleaned 
with ethanol after use. 
If spill occurs on table or rack - wash with ethanol and dry with paper towel at 
least two times. If your gloves are contaminated be sure to change the 
gloves immediately. The gloves will only protect against the chemicals for a 
few seconds. 
 
Used tubes, Pasteur pipettes and pipette tips should be collected in a plastic 
container and discarded in one of the bags for medical waste – remember to 
carry down the bag in the containers in front of the building. Never place 
Pasteur pipettes or pipette tips in a glass or plastic beaker (mycotoxins 
will stick to the surface and will cause a health risk if not properly cleaned!). 
 
Plugs can be cut out from the cultures in a LAF-bench or a fume-hood, 
whereas the work processes involving organic solvents must be done in a 
fume-cupboard (higher ventilation rate).

Procedure 
 
Extracts should not be exposed to direct sunlight (many compounds are 
light sensitive). Also try to minimize the time from cutting the plugs until 
extraction, unless the tubes with the plugs are frozen (gives slightly better 
extraction for many compounds). It should be noted that the samples - at any 
time in the procedure - can be frozen down and stored in the freezer. 
 
1. Start labelling the tubes the printed labels (figure 
2). Tubes should be kept in a rack to prevent spills 
and mixing up the samples. 
 
 
2. The plugs are taken from the areas which are 
intended for examination. For standard screening the 
plugs are taken from 1) the 
centre of the colony, 2) the 
rim near other colonies and 
3) the rim as far away from 
other 
colonies. 
This 
procedure is illustrated in figure 3. 
 
 
 
 
The plugs are transferred to the falcon tubes. The plug drill must be cleaned 
twice in two glasses with ethanol (if not stamping out plugs with a reversed-
pipette tip) and then flamed before taking plugs from a new culture. 
 
3. The extraction solvent (1.5 or 0.8 ml depending on if Falcon or 
Eppendorf tubes are used) and eventual internal standard solution (20 µL 
chloramphenicol, 500 µg/mL in ethanol) is added to the falcon tubes. 
The lids are put on the tubes and they are placed into the ultrasonic bath in a 
special rack (to be found by the ultrasonic bath) at full effect for 60 min. Make 
sure that there is water in the ultrasonic bath and that the level of the water 
only reaches app. halfway up the tubes (the labels should not be submerged, 
hence the label may fall off). 
 
Alternatively the samples can be placed in the freezer over night after the 
addition of the extraction solvent – and the next day ultrasonicated for 60 min.  
 
4. If using a 1 step extraction (method A) the extract/supernatant is 
transferred to a new labelled new tube with either: 
x Pasteur pipette (glass)

5. The tubes (without caps) are then placed in the Nitrogen evaporation setup 
at 40˚C in B221 room 119, 129, and 206. 
 
It is very important that all ethyl acetate has been evaporated as these 
chemicals are highly detectably in the HPLC analysis and will alter the 
retention time for polar metabolites. 
 
Evaporation time is normally < 30 min  
 
If using a two step extraction (method B and C) it is now time to add the 
second extraction solvent (internal standard should not be added 2 times) to 
the tubes with the plugs and place the tubes one more time in the ultrasonic 
bath for another 60 minutes while the first extraction solvents are being 
evaporated. 
 
After ultrasonication the second extraction solvent is transferred to the tube 
containing the now dry extracts from the first step and the solvent is 
subsequently evaporated as described above. 
 
 
6. After evaporation 300 μL of methanol or acetonitrile-water (6:4; if methanol 
labile compounds like lactones are to be analysed) is added to the tube with 
the dry extracts. To redissolve the pellets the lids are put on and the tubes are 
ultrasonicated for 20 min. or placed in the fridge over night and then 
ultrasonicated for 20 min. the following day. Sometimes it can be necessary to 
wash the pellet and tube sides using a Gilson pipette (200 µL tip) or a Pasteur 
pipette to get the pellet completely redissolved.  
 
 
7. The extract now has to be purified for particles before it can be injected into 
the UHPLC, HPLC or LC-MS instruments. 
 
Place the vials in the centrifuge and centrifuged at max g-power for 2-3 
min.  
After centrifugation 150 µl from the top is carefully transferred (avoiding the 
particular matter at the bottom of the tube) to a labelled plastic vial using a 
200 μL Gilson pipette, the extracts are now ready for analysis. 
 
Is sample appears to contain particles consult with KFN.
