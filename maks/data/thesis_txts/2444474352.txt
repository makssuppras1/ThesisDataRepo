Abstract
The main objective of this study is to investigate deposit formation in a maritime urea-
SCR system by developing a mathematical model for the decomposition of a urea droplet.
The project examines the reaction paths leading to byproduct formation during the urea
decomposition. Furthermore, diﬀerent approaches to the modelling of the decomposition
process are assessed in a thorough review.
Previous research shows that the urea decomposition is a complicated process with
many reactions. A model describing the decomposition of a urea sample heated with a
constant heating rate is used as a basis for the modelling of the droplet. This model
consists of 15 reactions and includes the byproducts biuret, CYA, ammelide and triuret.
The results from the model showed some agreement with experimental TGA data, as
the mass loss curves generally have the same shapes. However, there were also devia-
tions as the model predicted smaller amounts of byproducts and lower decomposition
temperatures. Furthermore, the simulation results showed that a higher heating rate led
to more ammelide as well as less biuret and CYA. As the heating rate was increased,
the formation of ammelide exceeded the formation of CYA, which was not expected.
The model was modiﬁed in order to describe a droplet urea decomposition, where
the temperature was modelled either with a heating rate or an energy balance. Results
from the heating rate model predicted a larger formation of ammelide at high heating
rates. Next, the decomposition of a urea droplet was simulated with the energy balance
model, and comparison with experimental data showed that the model underestimated
the decomposition rate. Furthermore, the energy balance model was used to simulate
byproduct formation during droplet decomposition at ambient temperatures similar to
those in a maritime SCR system. The results from these simulations showed that am-
melide was the only byproduct formed in signiﬁcant amounts. It was also found that
smaller droplets led to less formation of byproducts.

Project description
Background
Selective catalytic reduction (SCR) of nitrogen oxides has become a leading technology
in ﬂue gas cleaning of the exhaust from maritime diesel engines. In the maritime indus-
try, urea is the preferred reducing agent in SCR processes. Present mixing chambers
have volumes comparable to those of the engine itself, and there is a strong incentive to
signiﬁcantly reduce the chamber size. Size reduction is expected to increase problems,
known from automotive urea-SCR systems, with formation of undesired solid byprod-
ucts during urea decomposition that may clog the injection nozzles or form deposits
downstream, causing restrictions in passage as well as enhancing corrosion. A better
understanding of the urea decomposition chemistry, in particular the formation and de-
composition of secondary products constituting the undesired deposits, is necessary to
facilitate a design of an evaporation system that secures stable operation and a high NO
reduction eﬃciency for the SCR process.
Project objective
The objective of this project is to characterize the decomposition process of urea-water
solutions in SCR systems and identify conditions for byproduct formation. The aim
of the project is to develop a mathematical model for the decomposition process of an
isolated urea droplet. The model should describe the thermolysis of urea droplets, as well
as byproduct formation. The model will be established based on elements from literature
and previous work conducted at DTU Chemical Engineering. Finally, the model will be
validated against experimental results, and evaluated through a sensitivity analysis.
Content
 A literature survey on the urea decomposition process.
 Establishment of a mathematical model for the urea decomposition process includ-
ing byproduct formation.
 Validation of the model by comparison with experimental data.
 Evaluation of the model by a sensitivity analysis.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Emissions from diesel engines are composed of several harmful substances, such as hydro-
carbons, carbon monoxide, particulate matter and nitrogen oxides (NOx). Given this,
it is important to have an eﬃcient aftertreatment of the exhaust gas in order to reduce
emissions to the environment. In the maritime industry, the focus of the aftertreatment
is the nitrogen oxides, since shipping is a signiﬁcant contributor to NOx pollution [1, 2].
Emissions of NOx gasses are known to cause ground level ozone and acid rain [3].
The regulations for NOx emissions in maritime applications are provided by Annex
VI of the International Convention of Pollution From Ships (MARPOL) developed by
the International Marine Organization (IMO). Annex VI includes emission standards for
both NOx and SOx gasses and these standards are known as Tier I-III. In table 1.1, the
emission limits for NOx are shown.
Due to these regulations, it is necessary to eﬃciently remove NOx from the exhaust
gas and for this purpose, the selective catalytic reduction (SCR) is the leading technology.
In the SCR process, NOx is reduced to N2 by the use of ammonia [5–7].
However,
since NH3 also poses an environmental hazard, having pure ammonia onboard should
be avoided in order to prevent the release of ammonia into the environment. For that
reason, it is beneﬁcial to use urea which is non-toxic and thus allows for a safer storage
[3, 8, 9]. Urea decomposes into NH3 in the hot exhaust stream and on the catalyst, and
during this decomposition, isocyanic acid (HNCO) is formed as an intermediate. HNCO
is hydrolysed to ammonia and CO2 over the catalyst, but it can also react either with

2.1
Selective catalytic reduction (SCR)
In order to comply with the regulations for NOx emissions, an eﬃcient deNOx process
is required. The leading technology in the ﬁeld of NOx removal from diesel exhaust
is the selective catalytic reduction (SCR), in which NOx is reduced to N2 by the use
of ammonia [5–7]. This reduction can take place without the use of a catalyst in the
temperature range of 800-1000 ◦C [12]. But since the temperatures in an SCR system
is much lower (see table 2.1), a catalyst is needed in order to decrease the required
temperature for the process.
Three diﬀerent types of catalysts can be used for the SCR process; vanadium (V-
SCR), copper zeolite (CuZ) or iron zeolite (FeZ). The zeolite catalysts are often preferred
because they show better durabilities at high temperatures and higher NOx conversion at
low temperatures than the vanadium catalyst [6]. They are however also very sensitive to
sulfur poisoning, whereas V-SCR has a signiﬁcant sulfur tolerance. Due to this property,
vanadium catalysts are used in maritime applications because maritime fuels contain
high amounts of sulfur [15].

The reduction of NOx by ammonia in any of the SCR catalysts is governed by three
global reactions [1, 2, 5, 6, 8].
4 NH3 + 4 NO + O2 −−→4 N2 + 6 H2O
(R 2.1)
2 NH3 + NO + NO2 −−→2 N2 + 3 H2O
(R 2.2)
4 NH3 + 3 NO2 −−→3.5 N2 + 6 H2O
(R 2.3)
The ﬁrst reaction (R 2.1) is the standard SCR reaction, because NO comprises > 90% of
the NOx in diesel exhaust [1, 6, 8]. Since NO2 is also present in the exhaust gas, reaction
(R 2.2) will occur in the catalyst as well. This is called the fast SCR reaction due to a
faster reaction rate. Lastly, reaction (R 2.3) with pure NO2 is the slow SCR reaction,
which will rarely take place, since an excess of NO2 is required. In order to increase the
occurrence of the fast SCR reaction, a diesel oxidation catalyst (DOC) can be installed
upstream of the SCR, since a DOC oxidises NO to NO2 [5]. This procedure is mostly
used in the automotive industry.
Ideally, the ratio between NH3 and NOx should be 1 as to avoid ammonia slip. How-
ever, in order to ensure a high NOx conversion, an excess of ammonia is often used.
This can result in emissions of unreacted ammonia and to avoid this, an ammonia slip
catalyst (ASC) can be placed downstream of the SCR to oxidize the NH3 into N2 [16].
Ammonia is known to be harmful to the environment and the use of pure NH3 is
therefore generally avoided in SCR systems for mobile applications.
Instead, urea
(NH2−CO−NH2) is commonly used as the reducing agent as it is non-toxic and de-
composes into NH3 in the hot exhaust gas [3, 8, 9].
At standard conditions, urea is a solid powder which is pressed into spherical pellets
when it is to be applied in an SCR system [9]. However, the use of solid urea poses
challenges such as aggregation of pellets and diﬃculties with a dynamic dosing to match
the dynamics of the NOx emissions [9]. Due to these complications, urea-water-solutions
(UWS) are generally used in SCR systems. In the automotive industry, the commercially
used solution is called AdBlue. It is composed of 32.5 wt% urea and 67.5 wt% water,
and this exact composition has been chosen because it is liquid at standard conditions
[9]. However, there can also be complications associated with the usage of AdBlue, as it
has a relatively high freezing point of −11 ◦C, which can cause problems during winter
[8]. In maritime SCR, a urea concentration of 40 wt% is often used. This UWS will have
a higher freezing point, but this is not a problem since the temperatures in the engine
room are generally higher [13].
2.2
Decomposition of urea
UWS is sprayed into the system upstream of the SCR where it is heated by the hot
exhaust gas. As the temperature increases, water ﬁrst evaporates and subsequently urea
starts to decompose after the temperature reaches its melting point of 133 ◦C [9, 17, 18].

The evaporation of water (R 2.4) and thermolysis of urea (R 2.5) are both endothermic

proposed to place a dedicated hydrolysis catalyst upstream of the SCR which would
hydrolyse the isocyanic acid into ammonia before it enters the SCR. However, since the
HNCO hydrolysis has a much higher reaction rate than the actual SCR reaction on an
SCR catalyst, it is seemingly more favourable to simply increase the volume of the SCR
catalyst [21].

2.3
Byproduct formation
The formation of byproducts during the urea decomposition is undesired since they form
solid deposits which can be located on the exhaust pipe walls, urea injector, catalyst
entrance, catalyst surface, and mixing unit [23–25]. The deposits may lower the NOx
conversion, due to an increased backpressure and a decrease in the lifetime and activity
of the catalyst [7, 25, 26].
Byproduct formation in a urea-SCR system is a complex process, which is dependent
on several factors. The composition of the deposits is very dependent on temperature
as diﬀerent byproducts form at diﬀerent temperatures. Furthermore, there are several
factors which inﬂuence the amount of deposits formed. Understanding the process of
urea decomposition and byproduct formation is of great importance in order to reduce
deposits in the urea-SCR system, and it will therefore be examined in this section.
2.3.1
Temperature dependence
The main byproducts of the urea decomposition are CYA and biuret followed by am-
melide. Unreacted urea can also be present in the solid deposits in larger amounts, while
ammeline and melamine are usually only found in small quantities [10, 27]. However,
the exact composition of the deposits is dependent on the temperature. This is evident
in ﬁgure 2.2, which shows deposits formed at four diﬀerent wall temperatures. Even
though this is only a qualitative experiment, it clearly shows that the temperature af-
fects the composition of the byproducts. Since this temperature dependence is of great
importance when it comes to understanding byproduct formation, several studies have
been conducted on this subject.

vessel by performing high performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) and thermogravi-

Based on their results, they divided the decomposition into four reaction regions

produced in small amounts and only traces remain of urea and biuret.
In this region, a white precipitate was observed at 210 ◦C which transformed into

diﬀerent from those in an exhaust system. For example; the urea is in the form of UWS
droplets in an SCR system, and the heating rates are signiﬁcantly higher. Though the
results presented in this section give a basic understanding of the urea decomposition
process, the results cannot be used to accurately predict the byproduct formation in a
urea-SCR system.

Kinetic reaction schemes
In order to model the formation of byproducts during urea decomposition, a kinetic
model is needed. Since many reactions take place in the decomposition process, it is
advantageous to neglect some of the reactions and products to decrease the complexity of
the model. It is logical to include the products which are present in signiﬁcant amounts,
i.e. urea, HNCO, biuret, CYA and ammelide, and neglect the rest. By neglecting other
products such as ammeline, melamine and triuret, several reactions will automatically
be excluded. However, as there are still many reactions associated with the remaining
products, it would be beneﬁcial to neglect more reactions from the model. This could
be done by evaluating the signiﬁcance of the diﬀerent reactions based on experimental
results by e.g. Schaber et al. [10] and Bernhard et al. [18], but it will not be done here,
since setting up a new kinetic model is out of the scope of this project. Instead, two
kinetic reaction schemes from literature will be presented and discussed in this section.

2.3.3
Factors inﬂuencing the quantity of deposits
Deposit formation in a UWS-SCR system is believed to happen as a consequence of
the wall wetting phenomenon [24, 27, 32], which occurs when the UWS spray impinges
on the wall resulting in a decrease in the wall temperature at the impingement area.
If the temperature falls under a critical temperature, wall ﬁlm is formed, and the wall
temperature decreases further as a result of evaporation from the ﬁlm [7, 27].
The formation of wall ﬁlm is dependent on several diﬀerent factors, and in order
to reduce wall wetting and consequently deposit formation, it beneﬁcial to understand
these factors.

Deposition happens when both the wall temperature and the droplet velocity are low,

vapor layer between droplet and wall. The size of the droplet remains unchanged. Ther-

Temperature
As explained in section 2.3.1, the temperature greatly inﬂuences the composition of
the formed byproducts. However, the temperature also has a signiﬁcant eﬀect on the
quantity of the deposits. Higher exhaust temperature will accelerate the evaporation of
the urea and a faster evaporation leads to less wall wetting [7]. Furthermore, as discussed
above, the spray/wall interaction is aﬀected by the wall temperature, which is naturally
dependent on the exhaust and ambient temperature due to heat transfer.
The eﬀect of the exhaust temperature on the wall ﬁlm was investigated by Gan et al.
[23] who reported that an increase in the exhaust temperature will lead to a decrease in
the ﬁlm thickness. Strots et al. [24] tested both the exhaust temperature and the ambient
temperature, i.e. the temperature of the air directly surrounding the exhaust system
and found that an increase in either temperature will lead to a lower deposit yield. Their
results showed that the exhaust temperature has a larger inﬂuence on deposit formation
than the ambient temperature. The ambient temperature was also tested by Zhu et al.
[25] who observed that a decrease of 10 ◦C (from 36 ◦C to 26 ◦C) almost doubled the
deposit yield.
Exhaust mass ﬂow
Gan et al. [23] reported that a higher exhaust mass ﬂow leads to a faster ﬁlm evaporation.
Simulations showed that an increase in the mass ﬂow decreases both the ﬁlm length and
the ﬁlm thickness which thus leads to reduced deposit weights. Higher ﬂow rates also
improves the heat transfer from the gas to the wall which resulting in higher and more
stable temperatures of both and, as discussed, higher temperatures decrease deposit
formation [23].
Injection rate
Zheng et al. [28] studied the eﬀect of injection rate on wall wetting and observed that
as the urea injection rate increases, wall wetting and deposit formation occurs. Further-
more, Gan et al. [23] simulated the ﬁlm formation at diﬀerent injection rates and found
that an increase in the urea injection rate will cause both ﬁlm length, ﬁlm thickness
and ﬁlm length growth to increase. This is because a lower injection rate will result in
smaller droplet diameters which causes the urea to evaporate faster and thus less wall
ﬁlm is formed [25].

The size of the droplets is also a contributing factor to the amount of wall wetting, since

Since faster droplet evaporation leads to reduced wall wetting, it is clear that smaller
droplets leads to a decrease in wall ﬁlm. The initial size of the droplet can be reduced

Injection conﬁguration
The injection of UWS into the exhaust pipe can be carried out in several diﬀerent ways.
As a result, the trajectory of the spray will be altered and thus also the impingement
on the walls. This will in turn aﬀect deposit formation.
Zheng et al. [28] tested two diﬀerent injector mounting geometries, which are shown

mounted on the bend. In the S-pipe, the injection is in the axial direction in order to
improve the distribution of droplets. The results from the test showed that the S-pipe
design performs better with regards to reducing deposits, especially at low temperatures.

variations of this option was investigated by Zhu et al. [25] by performing vehicle road
tests. They tested the nozzle position and found that placing the nozzle in the center of
the pipe reduced the deposit yield by 95% compared to when the nozzle was 10 mm from
the center. Furthermore, they also compared radial and axial injection from the nozzle.
Solid deposits on the pipe walls were only found when the injection was in the radial
direction. However, the axial injection can possibly result in deposits on the catalyst

Mixer
In order to improve the urea decomposition and deNOx performance, mixing units can
be installed in the SCR system [5, 32]. In addition to providing an enhanced performance
of the system, mixers placed downstream of the UWS injector can also help reducing
deposit formation as is reported by Zheng et al. [28]. They tested two diﬀerent mixers
and found that both reduced the amount of deposits signiﬁcantly.
Additionally, Zheng et al. [28] found that the temperature of the outer wall was
∼20 ◦C hotter with a mixer installed. As the temperature change between the inner and
outer surface of the wall can be neglected, it is assumed that the inner wall temperature
is also ∼20 ◦C higher with a mixer. This will be a contributing factor to the decrease
in deposits, since a higher wall temperature leads to less wall wetting.

Review of urea decomposition
models
Modelling the decomposition of urea is complicated and the subject has therefore been
the focus of much research. The most common approach is to model the evaporation
and thermolysis of a single UWS droplet. This method is based on the assumption of a
spherical droplet with a surrounding gaseous ﬁlm region located in a hot gas mixture. An

3.1
Modelling of evaporation
When modelling the evaporation of the droplet, the mass ﬂow rate of evaporation and
the heat transfer rate are evaluated based on mass and energy balances. The models
described in this section consist of analyses of the gas phase, the liquid phase and the
gas-liquid interface. In the gas phase analysis, the above mentioned mass and energy
balances are set up. The analyses of the liquid phase and gas-liquid interface are used
to describe the temperature and species concentrations in the droplet and ﬁlm region,
respectively.
Abu-Ramadan et al. [36] and Gan et al. [7] have diﬀerent approaches to the modelling
of the evaporation process. The model by Abu-Ramadan et al. [36] is a multicomponent
vaporization model as they assume evaporation of both water and urea, whereas Gan
et al. [7] assume that only water evaporates. Furthermore, droplet dynamics are only
included in the model by Gan et al. since they do not treat the surrounding air as
stagnant. There are however similarities between the two modelling approaches as well,
and the following assumptions are common for both models:
 Spherical symmetry of the droplet.
 There is one-way diﬀusion (Stefan ﬂow), since the gas is insoluble in the liquid.
 Thermodynamic equilibrium at the gas-liquid interface.
 The following phenomena are negligible: Soret eﬀect (mass diﬀusion due to tem-
perature gradient), Dufour eﬀect (heat ﬂux due to concentration gradient) and
pressure diﬀusion (diﬀusion due to pressure gradient).
 The gas ﬁlm surrounding the droplet is in quasi-steady state.

is given in table 3.1. First of all, Abu-Ramadan et al. [36] developed a multicomponent

et al. [7] who developed two separate models for the evaporation and the thermolysis

Both models include convection and diﬀusion in the mass ﬂow rates of evaporation,
but the expressions are not identical to each other. In the model by Gan et al. [7], a log-
arithmic term is included, and this is not the case for the model by Abu-Ramadan et al.
[36].
Furthermore, since the Abu-Ramadan model is a multicomponent vaporization
model, the evaporation rate is found by summation of the mass loss of all evaporating
species. The heat transfer rates are based on similar energy balances which state that
the heat transferred to the droplet equals the heating and evaporation of the droplet.
However, Gan et al. [7] neglect heat transfer from radiation which is included by Abu-
Ramadan et al. [36]. Additionally, the rate of convective heat transfer are calculated
diﬀerently by the two author groups as Gan et al. [7] include heat transfer from both
convection and conduction, whereas Abu-Ramadan et al. [36] neglect convection.
The modelling of the thermolysis process is done in signiﬁcantly diﬀerent ways by the
author groups. Abu-Ramadan et al. [36] use two diﬀerent approaches; the vaporization
approach and the direct thermolysis approach. In the vaporization approach, urea is
assumed to evaporate and subsequently decompose in the gas phase, and this is modelled

3.5
Summary of literature survey
Based on the discussion in section 2.3.2, it can be concluded that the deposit formation
during the urea decomposition is a complicated process.
Diﬀerent studies show the
presence of diﬀerent products and reactions. However, there is an agreement that the
most signiﬁcant byproducts are biuret, CYA and ammelide, since these are formed in the
largest amounts. Therefore, these are usually included when modelling the byproduct
formation while the rest are neglected, as is done by Gan et al. [7] while Brack et al. [30]
also include triuret.
Determining the importance of the diﬀerent reactions is more complicated and would
require an extensive analysis of several diﬀerent experimental results. Since there is no
general agreement about this subject in literature, there are diﬀerences in the reactions
included in the two kinetic schemes by Gan et al. [7] and Brack et al. [30], which are
presented in section 2.3.2. However, there are also some reactions which are common
for both schemes as these are essential for the process.
There are several diﬀerent models for the decomposition of urea, and a few of them have
been presented in this chapter. The evaporation of water from a UWS droplet has been
modelled by Gan et al. [7] and Abu-Ramadan et al. [36]. The evaporation model by Gan
et al. [7] is the most simple, because Abu-Ramadan et al. [36] assume multi-component
evaporation, where water and urea evaporate simultaneously. Both models show good
agreement with experimental data.
The thermolysis of urea has been modelled by Gan et al. [7], Abu-Ramadan et al.
[36] and Brack et al. [30]. The Abu-Ramadan model shows good results, but since the
focus of the present project is the byproduct formation, this thermolysis model by Abu-
Ramadan et al. [36] is not particularly relevant. Gan et al. [7] and Brack et al. [30]
model the byproducts by including the kinetic reaction schemes presented in section
2.3.2. The two models diﬀer as Brack et al. [30] model the decomposition of urea in a
reaction vessel and Gan et al. [7] model the decomposition of UWS droplet and are thus
including an energy balance in the model. Both author groups use the CSTR approach
in the model, but in the model by Brack et al. [30], the reactions take place in the entire
volume, whereas Gan et al. [7] assume that they only take place on the droplet surface.
Since it does not seem likely that the reactions would only take place on the surface, the
model by Brack et al. [30] is used as a starting point when developing a new model.

However, a high formation of ammelide at 500 ◦C/min could possibly explain the
incomplete decomposition in the experimental results seen in ﬁgure 4.2. Even as the
temperature reaches 600 ◦C, there is still a small amount of remaining mass present.
It has been reported by Krum [42] and Bornhorst et al. [47] that the decomposition
of ammelide results in the formation of thermally resistant species, which could be the
species still present at temperatures above 500 ◦C. This is only speculation, since the
experiments by Krum [42] did not include any analysis of the byproducts and it is
therefore not possible to determine the composition of the byproducts.

temperature of 300 ◦C and 2.14·105 ◦C/min for an ambient temperature of 400 ◦C, both
with an initial diameter of 70 µm. This means that if the model predictions are correct,

4.1.3
Eﬀect of surface area
In this model, the urea decomposition is dependent on the surface area of the sample,
i.e. the area of the surface which is in contact with air. This dependence is due to
two reactions; B5 in which CYA decomposes into gaseous HNCO, and B15 which is the
evaporation of condensed HNCO. The CYA decomposition is modelled as a zero order
reaction and the rate is thus not dependent on the concentration, but it does however
depend on the surface area. The HNCO evaporation is modelled with the Hertz-Knudsen
equation in which the surface area is included. This is also described in section 3.2.3.
Figure 4.4 shows that the surface area has a signiﬁcant eﬀect on the urea decomposi-
tion according to this model, especially at low heating rates. A larger surface area leads
to a decrease in the amount of byproducts and shifts the CYA decomposition to lower
temperatures. As the heating rate increases, the inﬂuence of the surface area decreases
and at 500 ◦C/min, the results are almost identical with the three diﬀerent surface areas.
This is most likely due to the small amounts of CYA formed at higher heating rates.
However, to the knowledge of this author, it has not been reported anywhere in
literature that the CYA decomposition should be dependent on the surface area. As
this dependency does not seem to have been determined experimentally by Brack et al.
[30], it could be relevant to investigate it by performing TGA experiments with diﬀerent
crucibles in order to vary the surface area while keeping all other conditions the same.

Chapter 5
Modelling of urea thermolysis in
a droplet
As explained in section 3.2.3, the model by Brack et al. [30] describes the decomposition
of urea in a TGA experiment. However, in an actual SCR system, the urea is in the
form of droplets and it is therefore interesting to modify the model in order to be able to
simulate the decomposition of a urea droplet including byproduct formation. Since the
urea is sprayed into the exhaust gas in a urea-water solution, the modelling of the droplet
decomposition should include both the evaporation of water as well as the decomposition
of urea. However, as explained in chapter 3, the process can be separated into two steps,
where the water evaporation takes place ﬁrst followed by the urea decomposition. The
model presented in this chapter will focus solely on the thermolysis of urea, and the
starting point will thus be the pure urea droplet when all water has evaporated. In
order to model the decomposition of a UWS droplet, the presented model could be
combined with a model for the water evaporation e.g. one of the models described in
section 3.1 developed by Abu-Ramadan et al. [36] or Gan et al. [7].
The temperature development in the droplet during urea thermolysis will be imple-
mented in two diﬀerent ways; 1) with a ﬁxed heating rate and; 2) with an energy balance.
The two approaches are described in section 5.2.1 and 5.2.2, respectively. Modelling the
droplet decomposition with a heating rate is useful, because it makes it possible to inves-
tigate the eﬀect of the heating rate on the byproduct formation. However, in an actual
urea-SCR system, the droplet is heated by the exhaust gas and the heating rate is not
known. A model with an energy balance will therefore describe the conditions in an
SCR system more closely.
5.1
Assumptions
When modelling a process like the urea decomposition, it can quickly become overly
complicated if every detail is to be included in the model. It is therefore necessary to
make some simplifying assumptions, in order to reduce the complexity of the model.
The assumptions made for this model is described in this section.
49

Spherical droplet This assumption is commonly used and simpliﬁes the modelling sig-
niﬁcantly, since it is complicated to take the distortion of the droplet into account.
No gradients in the droplet Naturally there will be temperature and concentration
gradients inside the droplet. Since the heat is provided by the surrounding gas,
the temperature will be higher towards the surface and lowest at the center of the
droplet. Concentration gradients will occur due to the temperature gradient but
also due to the evaporation of HNCO taking place at the surface. However since
the droplets are small, these gradients are neglected.
Constant relative velocity In an actual SCR system, the velocity of the urea droplet
will be aﬀected by the exhaust gas velocity and will thus change over time. These
droplet dynamics have been modelled by Gan et al. [7] as described in section 3.1.2.
However, in this model, the droplet velocity, and thus also the relative velocity, is
assumed to be constant. This is a reasonable assumption for ship engines, where
the engine load is rather constant during operation.
Ambient gas is atmospheric air Since the ambient gas in an SCR system is the ex-
haust gas, it will not have the exact same composition as atmospheric air. However,
this assumption has been made in order to simplify the modelling process since
the composition of the exhaust gas is changing over time since the combustion is
a dynamic process. Furthermore, the model will be validated against data from
experiments performed in atmospheric air. This assumption is reasonable since
both diesel exhaust and air mainly consists of nitrogen. The concentration of wa-
ter vapour and CO2 are signiﬁcantly higher in diesel exhaust compared to air while
the oxygen concentration are lower. Due to the similar compositions of the two
gasses, using an approximation of atmospheric air generally leads to an error below
2% [48].

5.4.3
Conclusion on droplet simulation
Based on the results discussed in section 5.4.1, it can be concluded that the heating
rate has a large eﬀect on the composition of the byproducts in this model. The model
predicts that a higher heating rate leads to less formation of biuret and CYA but an
increased amount of ammelide. This is consistent with the simulation results presented
in section 5.4.2 where an increase in the ambient temperature (which equals a higher
heating rate) also leads to more ammelide and less biuret and CYA.
Furthermore, a higher heating rate results in a faster decomposition as seen in ﬁgure
5.1 but it also shifts the decomposition to higher temperatures as seen in ﬁgure 5.2.
Increasing the ambient temperature will also lead to a faster decomposition which is due
to both the higher heating rate as well as the droplet reaching a higher temperature.
Lastly, ﬁgure 5.5 shows that smaller droplets generally result in less byproducts,
which in turn lead to a faster decomposition.
5.5
Sensitivity analysis
There are uncertainties associated with some of the properties used in the model. This
is primarily due to the lack of reliable data for many of the products; this has resulted
in several of the properties being estimated with diﬀerent methods. Since the accuracy
of these methods are not known, it will lead to uncertainties in the model. In order
to determine if these uncertainties will result in large deviations, a sensitivity analysis
is performed. This is done by investigating how the results change when the diﬀerent
properties are varied.
In this section, the sensitivity of the density and heat capacity of the droplet and the
enthalpy of urea thermolysis will be evaluated, as these are all related to the properties
of the products present in the system. However, the properties of the ﬁlm region will
not be considered in this analysis since the gas is assumed to be atmospheric air, whose
properties are generally well established.

5.5.1
Enthalpy of urea thermolysis
Ideally, the heat of thermolysis should be calculated based on the enthalpy changes of
each of the reactions taking place. However, this is not possible since it would require
thermodynamic data for all of the products. For this reason, a constant value of 3096
kJ/kg has been used for the enthalpy change.
This value is given by Gan et al. [7]
and is based on the sum of the heat of thermolysis and sublimation. Since none of the
underlying data or calculations are given, it is not possible to give an estimation of the
uncertainty, but it is most likely not exceedingly large as the ﬁctive enthalpy presumably
originates in some data of the urea thermolysis. A variation of ±60% is therefore chosen
for the sensitivity analysis.
Changing the thermolysis enthalpy greatly inﬂuences the decomposition as seen in
ﬁgure 5.6. The thermolysis is an endothermic process and when the enthalpy is de-
creased, the droplet is heated faster, which will result in a faster decomposition. A lower
enthalpy also yields a shorter heating period, and this leads to a larger mass when the
mass loss starts to stagnate. Varying the enthalpy with ±60% results in the heating
period varying from 13 seconds with the droplet reaching a mass of 19% to 26 seconds
reaching a mass of 13%. A lower enthalpy will thus result in a faster decomposition but
also a larger mass when the droplet temperature has reached the ambient temperature
and vice versa.

Conclusion
The purpose of this project was to investigate the decomposition of urea-water solu-
tions in an SCR system with focus on the formation of deposits. This investigation
was intended to include an examination of the possible reaction mechanisms leading to
byproduct formation. Furthermore, the objective was to develop a mathematical model
describing the decomposition of a urea droplet as well as the byproducts formed during
this process.
The study of the byproduct formation showed that it is a very complicated process as
it includes several products and reactions. Due to this complexity of the decomposition
process, it can be helpful to only include the relevant reactions and products when the
byproduct formation is modelled. The most relevant byproducts are biuret, CYA, and
ammelide, as these are formed in the largest amounts. It is considerably more diﬃcult to
establish the most relevant reactions, as diﬀerent authors have reported diﬀerent ﬁndings
regarding this subject.
The decomposition of urea has been modelled several times but many of the models
do not include byproduct formation. A model developed by Brack et al. [30] was chosen
as a basis for the modelling work in this project. This model describes the decomposi-
tion of pure urea in a thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) experiment and includes the
byproducts biuret, CYA, ammelide and triuret. First, the model was implemented in
Matlab in order to evaluate it against experimental results. The decomposition of urea
was simulated at four diﬀerent heating rates; 5, 10, 50 and 500 ◦C/min, and the de-
compositions of the products biuret, CYA and ammelide were simulated as well. The
numerical results were compared to experimental data by Krum [42] and even though all
the simulations showed the expected tendencies, there were several deviations between
the numerical and experimental results. On this basis, it can be concluded that the
model can only be used cautiously for the conditions used in these experiments.
The model interestingly predicted an increase in the formation of ammelide and a
decrease in the formation of CYA at higher heating rates. This tendency was not ex-
pected since it is generally agreed in literature that CYA is formed in large amounts while
ammelide is formed in considerably smaller amounts. Since there are no experiments
which can validate this, it is not possible to conclude if it is accurate. It would however
63
==PAGE:75==

be interesting to investigate this further since the heating rates in an SCR system are
considerably higher than 500 ◦C/min, which could mean that ammelide might be the
only byproduct of interest, if the model is correct.
Subsequently, a model for the byproduct formation in a urea droplet was developed
based on the model by Brack et al. [30]. The droplet decomposition was modelled with
the temperature change described in two diﬀerent ways; with a heating rate and with
an energy balance developed by Gan et al. [7].
Four diﬀerent heating rates were used in the simulations; 10, 100, 1000 and 100 000
◦C/min, and the results showed the same trends for increasing heating rates as in the
modelling of a TGA experiment. Higher heating rates resulted in an increased production
of ammelide and at 100 000 ◦C/min, the only byproduct was ammelide. Furthermore,
increasing the heating rates also shifted the decomposition to higher temperatures.
When the energy balance was implemented, the model could be validated against
experimental data by Wang et al. [41], and it was shown that the model underestimated
the decomposition rate. The decomposition was then simulated with four diﬀerent am-
bient temperatures; 300, 350, 400 and 450 ◦C. These results showed that ammelide was
the primary byproduct and that the decomposition time decreased drastically as the am-
bient temperature was increased. Another simulation was run with smaller droplet sizes,
which better represented the sizes in an actual SCR system, and this showed that smaller
droplets led to signiﬁcantly less byproduct formation. An initial droplet diameter of 1
µm resulted in no byproduct formation which signiﬁcantly reduced the decomposition
time of the droplet.
In conclusion, the model can be used to give a general idea of the formation and decom-
position of byproducts during the urea decomposition both in a TGA experiment and
in a single droplet. The simulation results can furthermore provide an overview of what
could be relevant to investigate further, either by optimizing the model or by conducting
experiments.
6.1
Future work
The model is currently producing results which show some agreement with experimental
data but there are also deviations. It would therefore be beneﬁcial to further optimize
the kinetic model in relation to experimental data in order to produce more accurate
results. When performing this optimization, the TGA model should be used since there
is limited data available for the decomposition of urea droplets.
In order to have a
suﬃcient amount of data, TGA experiments with urea, biuret, CYA and ammelide
should be conducted with both low and high heating rates.
The results from these
experiments can then be used to ﬁt the kinetic parameters. During these experiments,
it would also be valuable to analyse the byproducts and thus determine if the model
prediction of a high formation of ammelide at increased heating rates is accurate.
Furthermore, an evaluation of the diﬀerent reactions and products included in the
model could be performed with regard to reducing the complexity of the model. For
64
==PAGE:76==
CHAPTER 6. CONCLUSION
example, it could perhaps be advantageous to remove triuret and the reactions related
to this product from the model, since it is formed in insigniﬁcant amounts. It is thus not
particularly relevant to consider triuret and including it in the model might just increase
the complexity unnecessarily. This can, however, not be determined without a thorough
analysis.
Additionally, experiments have shown that some amount of urea evaporates, and this
urea is thus not decomposed into HNCO and NH3. This will naturally aﬀect the decom-
position process as well as the amount of byproducts formed, and it would therefore be
interesting to incorporate this evaporation into the model. In order to do this, it would
be necessary to perform quantitative experiments determining several factors such as;
the amount of evaporating urea; at which temperature the evaporation start and; how
fast the evaporation is.
In the optimization, it would also be relevant to experimentally investigate if the urea
decomposition is dependent on the surface area as the model suggests. By performing
TGA experiments with diﬀerent size crucibles while keeping all other conditions the
same, the surface area dependence could be evaluated.
Besides the optimization of the kinetic model, there are a few extensions which could be
added to the droplet model. The ﬁrst is to include a model for the water evaporation,
which would make it possible to simulate the decomposition of a UWS droplet instead of
a pure urea droplet. This is more relevant since UWS droplets are used in actual urea-
SCR systems. The evaporation of water in a UWS droplet has been modelled several
times and a model by e.g. Gan et al. [7] or Abu-Ramadan et al. [36] could be used.
Furthermore, the droplet dynamics and the spray/wall interaction could be included
in the model in order to better evaluate the byproduct formation in an actual exhaust
system. This could possibly be done by implementing the model into CFD simulations.
A valuable contribution to the TGA model is to include an energy balance in order
to account for the heat transfer in the sample. As the model is now, the sample is heated
with a constant heating rate and all heat transfer is neglected. However, in reality, it
is the crucible which is heated with the heating rate and there may be a delay in the
heating of the sample. Implementing an energy balance will therefore result in a more
accurate description of the temperature in the sample.

% Load parameters
par = constants;
% Initial conditions
d0
= 0.6e−1; % Droplet [cm]
m0
= 1/6*pi*d0ˆ3 * par.rho urea; % Mass [g]
T0
= 100+273; % Temperature [K]
ini = [0 m0/par.W urea 0 0 0 0 0 0 T0]; % Initial conditions
% Heating rate [K/min]
beta = 10;

tspan = 0:1:2000;

T = x(:,9)−273; % Temperature [C]
% Calculate mass of each species in percent of initial mass
urea m
= x(:,1)*par.W urea/m0*100;
urea s
= x(:,2)*par.W urea/m0*100;
HNCO
= x(:,3)*par.W HNCO/m0*100;
biuret
= x(:,4)*par.W biuret/m0*100;
biuret mat
= x(:,5)*par.W biuret/m0*100;
CYA
= x(:,6)*par.W CYA/m0*100;
ammelide
= x(:,7)*par.W ammelide/m0*100;
triuret
= x(:,8)*par.W triuret/m0*100;
% Total mass [%]
mass = urea m + urea s + HNCO + biuret + CYA + ammelide...

dxdt = [dUreadt;dUreasdt;dHNCOdt;dBiudt;dBiumatdt;dCYAdt;...

% Energy balance
% −−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−
% Calculate mole fraction of each species
Y = zeros(1,8);
for i = 1:8
Y(i) = x(i)/sum(x(1:8));
end
% Reference temperature
T ref = T + 1/3*(T inf −T);
Tvec ref = [T refˆ3; T refˆ2; T ref; 1];
% Properties of gas film region
lambda g = sum(Tvec ref .* par.lambda'); % Thermal conductivity [W/(m K)]
Cp g
= sum(Tvec ref .* par.Cp g'); % Heat capacity [J/(kg K)]
mu g
= sum(Tvec ref .* par.mu'); % Viscosity [kg/(m s)]
rho g
= P*par.W air / (R*T ref)*10ˆ(−3); % Density [kg/mˆ3]
% Dimensionless numbers
Pr
= Cp g*mu g / lambda g; % Prandlt
Re d = rho g*d d*10ˆ(−2)*u rel / mu g; % Reynolds
Nu 0 = 2.0 + 0.552*Re dˆ(1/2)*Prˆ(1/3); % Nusselt
% Heat capacity of droplet
Tvector = [Tˆ3; Tˆ2; T; 1];
Cp
= sum(Tvector .* par.Cp d');
Cp d
= sum(Y.*Cp); % J/(K kg);

dTdt = 1/(m*10ˆ(−3)*Cp d) * (pi*d d*10ˆ(−2)*Nu 0*lambda g*(T inf−T)...
