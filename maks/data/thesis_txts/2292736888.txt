Abstract 
 
Slotted cold-formed steel members are used more and more as a replacement for other 
types of load bearing external wall systems such as wood or concrete, but can also be 
used as internal infill walls. Due to the fact that slotted cold-formed steel members is 
being used more frequently for load bearing exterior wall systems in buildings of up to 
two storeys more research is needed on this subject, as the current European Standards 
for cold-formed steel members does not included slotted profiles. The load bearing 
construction in a light steel frame consists of a vertical C-profile connected at the top 
and bottom to a horizontal U-profile by self-tapping screws. The slots reduce the load 
bearing resistance of the section compared to normal cold-formed steel members. 
 
The bending stiffness perpendicular to the direction of the slots of a thin-walled slotted 
steel web has been investigated using a numerical analysis and the results from the 
analysis of the stress achieved in the web shows that the deformation of a slotted web 
happens both due to bending in some areas of the slotted web and due to torsion in other 
areas. This discovery was used to create an analytical model for calculating the angular 
rotation of the web near the supports using the principle of virtual work. The angular 
rotation is needed for calculating the rotational spring stiffness of a slotted web. The 
calculated rotational spring stiffness can be used directly in the calculation method for 
distortional buckling of cold-formed steel members provided by the European 
Standards.  
 
The behaviour of the joint between the slotted C- and U-profile during compression has 
been investigated through a numerical short column analysis carried out using the finite 
element software: ABAQUS. A thorough description of the model has been made, and 
special emphasis has been put on modelling the interactions between the profiles in the 
model so correct behaviour of the joint is obtained in the analysis. A linear perturbation 
eigenvalue buckling analysis has been made to investigate the buckling modes of the C-
profile for use as initial imperfections in a static nonlinear analysis. The static nonlinear 
analysis has been carried out using the Riks method for solving the post buckling and 
collapse analysis. The result of the nonlinear analysis has been used to analyse the 
behaviour of the joint and find the distribution of load transferred through contact 
pressure between the elements and as shear through the screws. 
 
Last the performance curve from the nonlinear post buckling analysis has been 
compared to that from short column experiments performed by (Lyng & Persson, 2014), 
carried out on a model using the same setup as in the numerical analysis.

Slidsede koldformede stål profiler der bliver brugt i lastbærende ydervæg systemer 
bliver oftere og oftere i byggerier i op til to etager som erstatning for træ- og

området da de nuværende Europæiske Standarder for koldformede stål profiler ikke

profilerne sammenlignet med normale koldformede stål profiler.

1. Introduction 
 
1.1 Background of thesis 
 
The use of slotted cold-formed steel members in single family houses and other types of 
two-storey buildings such as schools or office buildings has been growing in the Nordic 
countries in recent years. Slotted cold-formed steel members are used more and more as 
a replacement for other types of load bearing external wall systems such as wood or 
concrete, but can also be used as internal infill walls. Due to their light frame and 
relatively high lead bearing capacity the slotted cold-formed steel members are very 
efficient in keeping down the self-weight of the structure whilst maintaining a good load 
bearing capacity.  
By adding the slots in the web the profiles achieve good thermal properties as the 
thermal bridge through the profile is reduced. The thermal properties of slotted cold-
formed steel members in interaction with gypsum board are as good as e.g. that of 
timber stud walls.  
Furthermore the nature of how these walls with cold-formed steel members are build 
makes them well suited for prefabrication because the skeleton doesn’t necessarily have 
to be built on site. 
 
One of the fabricators of cold-formed steel profiles who are also making slotted profiles 
is Gyproc A/S. The system with slotted profiles by Gyproc A/S is called 
THERMOnomic, and is as already mentioned a flexible, sustainable and energy 
efficient lightweight building system which are in line with future requirements. The 
profiles investigated and tested in this thesis will be from the Gyproc THERMOnomic 
system. 
The THERMOnomic system comes in widths varying from 120 mm to 250 mm and 
thicknesses from 0.7 mm to 2.0 mm. Furthermore the number of slots also varies with 
thickness. For thicknesses between 0.7 mm to 1.5 mm there are 10 slots across the 
width of the web and for 2.0 mm thick profiles there are only 8 slots in the web, which 
is shown in Figure 1.1 b) and c) respectively.  
 
In the wall system the slotted C-profiles are used as vertical studs connected to two U-
profiles, or rails, at the top and the bottom, which is shown in Figure 1.1 a). The studs 
are connected to the rails using 2 screws in each flange in both ends of the stud, giving 8 
screws needed per stud in total. The studs are usually placed with a spacing of 600 mm, 
but the spacing can be changed depending on the external loading and the width of the 
gypsum plate. The skeleton formed of the C- and U-profiles are then clad with two or

It was previously mentioned that the benefits from having the slots included smaller 
thermal bridges. A downside to the slots is that they alter the stiffness and the resistance 
of the web significantly; meaning that the usual design methods no longer applies. The 
slots have a considerable effect on the behaviour of the profiles during axial 
compression, by changing it into a very anisotropic design, where the axial stiffness in 
the longitudinal direction is different from the axial stiffness in the perpendicular 
direction. The same is also the case for the bending stiffness of the slotted web plate in 
the C-profiles. This has an influence on the local and distortional buckling behaviour of 
the stud and thus also on the behaviour of the joints, e.g. by reducing the local and 
distortional buckling stress of the C-profile.  
 
As it stands now there are no design guidelines available in the codes or standards for 
cold-formed steel profiles with a slotted web and the design of the slotted profiles has 
mainly been based on tests, but around the millennium a few studies have been made 
with regards to the load bearing capacity of slotted profiles. These studies are presented 
shortly in chapter 2. For it to be possible to use the slotted cold-formed steel members 
for external load bearing walls in larger or more complex building projects in the future 
a better understanding of the slotted profiles is thus essential. 
 
 
1.2 Objectives of the thesis 
 
There are two primary objectives of this thesis. Common for both is that they focus on 
gaining an improved understanding of how slotted C-profiles act when subject to axial 
loading of the C-profiles. 
The first task of this thesis has been made in collaboration with fellow Master of 
Science in Civil Engineering students: Jonas Hjorth Jacobsen and Mustafa Shaker. The 
slotted web is isolated and subjected to a pure bending simulation using a finite element 
model, with the objective of developing an analytical method for predicting the 
rotational spring stiffness of a slotted web, which is needed for calculating the critical 
distortional buckling load of an axially compressed C-profile with slots. 
The objective of the second task of this thesis is to gain a better understanding of how 
the joint between the C- and the U-profile acts during axial compression of the C-profile 
by investigating how the forces are being distributed from the C-profile to the U-
profiles through the joint. This will be achieved by building as precise a finite element 
model as possible to help further the understanding of how the joint behaves, and 
analysing the results from this model as well as comparing these results with results 
from short column compression tests performed on slotted C-profiles.

1.3 Outline of the thesis 
 
A basic introduction of the slotted cold-formed steel members and their use has already 
been given earlier in this chapter. Moving forward in this thesis chapter 2 presents the 
reader with a literature study of previous work with respect to the analysis of 
compression of slotted cold-formed steel members and their design methods in order to 
obtain a basic knowledge on the subject. Chapter 3 describes the creation of the

the numerical analysis of the slotted web to investigate the reduced stiffness of the 
slotted web plate. Furthermore chapter 3 also includes a comparison of the results from 
the analytical and numerical models. Going forward to chapter 4 a description of the 
compression test performed on slotted C-profiles is made, and the results from those 
tests are presented as well as the method and results from the tests performed on the 
material of the slotted C-profiles. Chapter 5 describes the numerical analysis of the test 
subject including how the model was made and reasoning’s behind the choices made 
when creating the model. Furthermore chapter 5 includes an eigenvalue buckling 
analysis as well as a nonlinear analysis of the numerical model. The results from the 
nonlinear analysis is also analysed and compared with the results from the tests done in 
chapter 4. In chapter 6 the results from the compression tests are compared to the results 
obtained from the numerical analysis. Chapter 7 gives the conclusion and chapter 8 
describes further studies needed.

2. Literature study 
 
As mentioned in the introduction, the slots alter the stiffness and the resistance of the 
web significantly meaning that the usual design methods no longer apply. Only a 
handful of papers have been made regarding the design and load bearing capacity of 
slotted cold-formed steel members subject to compression so far.  
 
Höglund (Höglund, 1998) has developed a method for analytical design/calculations of 
slotted light-gauge steel studs mainly based on Swedish Code for Light Gauge Metal 
Structures 79 (StBK-N5). Höglund describes that several types of failure may occur in 
such profiles, depending on the load conditions, with the most important being 1) 
Buckling in the plane of the web and 2) Lateral buckling of the flanges in compression, 
which are treated in detail, whereas the rest are described more shortly or just with a 
calculation method. Common for all failure modes is that the resistance is affected by 
the modulus of elastic shear support of the slotted web or by the reduced transverse 
bending stiffness of the web. The first is expressed by a reduced shear modulus, which 
Höglund also suggests a way of determining. The reduced transverse bending stiffness 
of the web is mainly governed by an effective area approach. When the studs are under 
eccentric compressive or transverse loading, Hölunds suggests a way of determining the 
stress distribution across the whole section by taking the effect of the shear 
deformations of the slotted web into account.  
The method presented for finding the stress capacity ratios for lateral buckling also 
takes into account whether the flanges are braced or unbraced. 
For concentric axial loading Höglund assumes that the lateral support from the slotted 
web is negligible and only uses the flanges for the strength calculations. For eccentric 
and transverse loading the shear strength of the web has an influence on the total load 
bearing capacity of the stud. 
Höglund also compares the results from his analytical calculations with already 
performed tests on slotted steel studs with plasterboard bracing performed by the Danish 
Technological Institute and the Department of Structural Engineering, Steel Structures, 
Royal Institute of Technology, Stockholm and he concludes that for both tests his 
calculated results are in quite good agreement with the tests, despite being a bit on the 
safe side.

Kesti (Kesti, 2000) performed short column tests on web-stiffened C-sections with a 
slotted web and fixed-end conditions to investigate the compression strength of slotted 
steel studs. The tests showed that the restraint created by the perforated web with 
respect to distortional buckling mode is important, as the failure loads of the whole 
sections were approximately 10% higher compared to those of the tested sections with 
only the flanges (no web part) when the lips of the whole section was failing outwards, 
and 27% higher when the lips was failing inwards. The tests thus also showed that the 
direction of the distortional buckling mode has a direct impact on the failure loads of 
such profiles. It was also discovered that the initial imperfection direction has an 
important impact on the compression capacity and the direction the lips buckled. 
Gypsum board braced column tests were also carried out, and showed that the screw 
pitch had no influence on the buckling mode or the number of buckling half-waves. The 
screw pitch did however have an influence on the ultimate failure load of the wall, with 
the densest screw pitch providing the load resistance. Furthermore the tests showed that 
the thinner the wall thickness of the section, the bigger the influence of the gypsum 
board has on the failure load of the wall-stud. 
Furthermore a model was suggested for taking the restraints provided by the gypsum 
sheathing into account, when determining the compression capacity of the slotted steel 
wall stud. The model takes the flexural stiffness of the sheathing and rotational stiffness 
of the connection between the sheathing and the stud into account, which improves the 
distortional buckling strength of the slotted wall stud. 
 
By using FEM and comparing this to the tests Kesti (Kesti, 2000) found that the 
reduced perpendicular bending stiffness of the web seemed to be the most important 
factor to describe the behaviour of the slotted web and its influence on the distortional 
buckling strength of the section. A FE analysis for gypsum-sheathed columns also 
showed that the stiffness values used for the springs (modelled gypsum board) had an 
influence on the number of buckling half-waves in the distortional buckling mode. 
 
A method for calculating the analytical compression strength of slotted wall-studs was 
also suggested by using a modified version of the EC3 method, which was shown to be 
in good compliance with the results from the tests.

A study of the support strength of walls with slotted steel studs under different axial 
loading conditions was performed by Costa (Costa, 1999). In this study a series of tests 
were performed on simply supported slotted studs with seven different connection types 
between the stud and rail in order to study different load paths from the studs to the 
supports. The tests with only axial loading showed that the general failure mode was 
due to local buckling near the joint, no matter the connection type used for the supports. 
To calculate the ultimate loads of the wall with slotted studs Costa used the theoretical 
approach and model developed by Höglund (Höglund, 1998), which has been described 
earlier. The calculations showed that the test results were in general in compliance with 
the results from the analytical calculation, with the analytical calculations being slightly 
on the safe side. 
It might be worth noting that only two tests were performed for each support condition, 
meaning that the sampling pool is probably not large enough to conclude anything 
decisive, although the results are good indicators of how the failure mode might develop 
in the joint between a slotted steel stud and rail when loaded axially.  
 
 
Thöyrä (Thöyrä, 2001) has made experiments regarding the shear load capacity of 
slotted steel studs with the purpose of comparing these results with the analytical 
calculation method suggested by Höglund (Höglund, 1998). By doing experiments on 
samples with both large and small slots in the web Thöyrä discovered that the studs with 
large slots failed by flexural buckling of the strips between the slots even though global 
shear buckling occurred initially. The studs with small slots failed by global shear 
buckling without local flexural buckling of the strips.  
The experiments also proved it difficult to find a method which measures the shear 
deformations of the slotted webs precisely. 
Thöyrä also used a FEM analysis to investigate whether it can be used instead of tests 
for finding the ultimate shear stress and the shear modulus of the slotted web, which is 
needed for the analytical model. By comparing the results from the shear load tests with 
FEM it was seen that FEM analysis estimated the maximum load rather well. 
Furthermore it was seen that all three methods (test, analytical and FEM) gave almost 
the same shear stress results for small deformations, but as the deformations became 
larger so did the deviation in stress between the methods. Thöyrä concludes that this 
suggests that the analytical model is well suited for calculating the shear modulus. 
Finally different reductions of the shear modulus were found for studs with large and 
small slots.

2 Literature study 
 
 
8 
 
Lyng and Persson (Lyng & Persson, 2014) have performed a series of 40 short column 
compression tests on slotted steel studs with a C-profile in a setup made to resemble 
how a wall would be built in reality, excluding the gypsum boards. This means that the 
C-profiles were fastened to slotted rails with a U-profile by self-tapping screws. To 
imitate the rigid support of a floor or foundation, which these walls would be placed on, 
Lyng and Persson used thick steel plates which were bolted to the web of the U-profile. 
The C-profile studs used had varying geometry with 20 tests performed on 1000 mm 
long studs and 20 tests performed on 500 mm long studs. Each stud length was then 
tested using a thickness of 0.7 mm and 1.0 mm. In half the test subjects a web stiffener 
was placed in each end of the web of the C-profiles before it was mounted to the U-
profile. The web stiffeners were distributed evenly among the varying lengths and 
thicknesses to create 8 different test series with 5 tests in each series.  
The results from the tests show that an increase in thickness from 0.7 mm to 1.0 mm 
about doubles the load carrying capacity of the C-profiles for both lengths tested. 
Whereas the decrease of length from 1000 mm to 500 mm doesn’t have nearly as large 
an effect on the maximum load carrying capacity of the C-profiles as the increase in 
thickness does.  
Besides providing a buckling strength of the C-profile the behaviour of the test results 
from (Lyng & Persson, 2014) were later analysed in (Andreassen & Jönsson, 2015) in 
which they tried to describe the observed failure type of each individual test. The tests 
showed that for the 1000 mm long C-profiles the failure type observed were exclusively 
distortional buckling. Both distortional closing and opening of the edge stiffener was 
observed, with distortional opening only being observed for the 0.7 mm thick test 
specimen.  
The tests on the 500 mm long C-profiles showed both distortional buckling and local 
buckling at the joint between the C- and U-profiles. For the test specimen with a 
thickness of 0.7 mm more failure types with local failures at the joint was observed than 
for the test specimen with a thickness of 1.0 mm. 
Furthermore it was discovered from the tests that the web stiffener had no noteworthy 
effect on the load carrying capacity, nor the type of failure observed for each individual 
test. This is because the slotted web in the C-profiles could best be described as acting 
like springs when axial load was applied to the C-profile. This means that most of the 
applied axial load is transferred through the edge stiffener, flanges and solid part of the 
web close to the flanges. Therefore any attempt to stiffen the web might seem obsolete 
with regard to the load carrying capacity or failure type of the slotted C-profiles.

3. Analytical model for rotational spring 
stiffness 
 
As a step to get a better understanding of the distortional buckling for slotted C-profiles 
an analytical model for calculating the rotational spring stiffness of the slotted web has 
been derived. Knowing the rotational spring stiffness of a web in a thin-walled profile is 
important because the distortional buckling of a compression member such as the C-
profile generally involves rotation of each flange and flange stiffener around the flange-
web junction, creating a bending moment in the web. A typical distortional buckling 
mode of a C-profile is shown in Figure 3.1. 
 
 
Figure 3.1 Distortional buckling mode of a C-profile2 
 
This chapter will describe the creation of that new analytical model for calculating the 
rotational spring stiffness of slotted thin-walled webs. This is done because profiles with 
slotted web parts in general aren’t considered in the current version of DS/EN 1993-1-3, 
and as it stands now there is no precise method for including profiles with slotted web 
parts in the Eurocode calculations for thin-walled profiles without the use of finite 
element programs. 
The way the Eurocode calculates the critical distortional buckling load is described in 
chapter 5 of DS/EN 1993-1-3. The Eurocode uses the rotational spring stiffness of the 
slotted web part to calculate the spring stiffness of the edge stiffener in a C-profile. This 
in return is then used to calculate the distortional buckling of axially loaded C-profiles.  
The rotational spring stiffness of the web in a C-profile characterises the perpendicular 
bending stiffness of the web part of the section. Because of the correlation between 
rotational spring stiffness and the perpendicular bending stiffness of the web part of the 
section, and the fact that the perpendicular bending stiffness is severely reduced due to 
the slotted area in the web, it is necessary to find a way to analytically calculate the 
rotational spring stiffness for web parts with slotted areas. As mentioned it is possible to 
do so by using finite element programs to calculate the deflection of the slotted web

Even though the slots in the web part also reduce the axial stiffness of the web, besides 
the already mentioned bending stiffness, the former is going to be ignored for the 
analytical model. Even though the local buckling of a slotted plate is dependent on both 
the longitudinal axial stiffness as well as the longitudinal and perpendicular bending 
stiffness of the slotted plates it will not have much effect on the load bearing capacity of 
an axially loaded slotted C-profile. This is because the slotted part of the web is not a 
part of the effective area of the web when considering local and global bucking due to 
axial loading in the longitudinal direction when using the calculation methods presented 
in DS/EN 1993-1-3, and there is no axial loading of the web in the perpendicular 
direction. This then means that the only effect a reduction of either the axial stiffness or 
bending stiffness has on the load carrying capacity of the C-profiles is during 
distortional buckling where the perpendicular bending stiffness of the web plate is 
relevant. Therefore this analytical model is only going to be focused around predicting 
the rotational spring stiffness as a result of the decrease in the perpendicular bending 
stiffness of the slotted web part.

3.1 Numerical analysis 
 
The first step in the creation of the analytical model was to do a numerical analysis, 
using the finite element program Abaqus, to get a better understanding of how the 
slotted web part of the C-profiles act when loaded with a constant bending moment.  
In the numerical analysis three different C-profiles from Gyproc have been analysed for 
later comparison with the analytical model. These sizes are: THR145, THR200 and 
THR245 meaning that the distance from one flange to another in the profiles are 145 
mm, 200 mm and 245 mm. Furthermore for each C-profile four models have been made 
with different thicknesses: 0.7 mm, 1.0 mm, 1.5 mm and 2.0 mm. This has all been done 
to get as many different finite element models as possible to compare with results from 
the analytical model. This will ensure that the validity of the analytical model is tested 
in a wide range of dimensions.  
The models created in Abaqus are all 1 m long deformable 3D shell extrude models 
with the same boundary conditions as described in Figure 3.3. Shell elements have been 
chosen over solid elements because the slotted cold-formed steel members considered in 
this thesis are very thin compared to their length and width. The elastic material 
properties used for all models were Young’s modulus:

And the Poisson’s ratio:

The applied load is a moment of the magnitude 1 Nmm/mm applied to the shell edges in 
the longitudinal direction of the slotted web part. 
 
For the entire model the element type chosen in Abaqus is called ‘S3’, which is a linear 
three-node triangular shell element type with six degrees of freedom, also referred to as 
dof’s for future reference. This type of element has been chosen because it best 
accommodates the complicated geometry of the web due to the slots without getting 
major distortions in the initial element geometry. It is chosen as linear over quadratic 
because the linear mesh provides sufficient integration points for the task ahead. The 
entire 1 m long model is meshed with seeding for which the deflection of the mode has 
converged. In the centre of the model a more refined mesh is specified compared to the 
coarser mesh everywhere else in the model. This refined mesh is created to be able to 
investigate the stress in the web more precisely in that area. 
The analysis type chosen for the Abaqus models of the slotted webs is a linear static, 
general analysis. The analysis is linear because only the linear elastic material properties 
have been specified (E-modulus) and nonlinear geometry, called ‘Nlgeom’ in Abaqus, 
is not turned on because only small displacements are expected.

From Figure 3.10 it can be seen how the normal stress in the x-direction is distributed in

the slender plates between the slots it can be seen that the normal stress is very close to 
being 0, which must mean that the outer forces applied to the model is transferred 
through these parts as something else than bending moment. 
It is also worth noting that the normal stress is largest near the edges of the connective 
areas next to the short edge of the slots, and then the normal stress decreases towards 
the middle. Around the long edges of the slots the normal stress is close to zero. So all 
in all it can be concluded that the normal stresses runs in a zigzag pattern from one end 
of the web through the slotted area to the other end of the web, increasing towards the 
connective areas, then decreasing to the middle of the slender long plate parts between 
the slots and then increasing again towards the next connective area and so on.

When looking at the rotational spring stiffness calculated using the FEM model and the 
analytical method presented in Table 3.4 it clearly shows that for the analytical method 
there is only a small difference in rotational spring stiffness between the THR145, 
THR200 and THR245 profiles, whereas for the FEM model the difference in rotational 
spring stiffness are noticeably larger. This means that the analytical method estimates 
the rotational spring stiffness of the slotted web of the different C-profiles to be much 
closer to each other than what the FEM model shows. 
From Table 3.4 it can be seen that a pattern in the deviation of the analytical model from 
the FEM model becomes clear for the webs with 10 slots, no matter the thickness of the 
web. The tendency for the analytical model is that it underestimates the rotational spring 
stiffness for all the THR145 C-profiles meaning that they are on the safe side, except the 
2 mm thick one with 8 slots. In the rest of the calculations the analytical model is on the 
unsafe side when predicting the rotational spring stiffness. The results for the THR200 
profiles are generally closest to the rotational spring stiffness calculated using the 
deflection found using Abaqus, and the deviations of the rotational spring stiffness for 
the THR245 profile lies between the range of 12-15 % on the unsafe side.  
For the profiles with 8 slots in the web the deviation pattern looks different. The 
deviation is generally larger for the profiles with 8 slots, with the maximum deviation of 
23.99 % recorded for all 12 models investigated being for the THR245 profile with 8 
slots and a web thickness of 2 mm. The deviation of the THR145 profile is actually the 
smallest recorded with a deviation of just 3.72 %; however this is most likely 
coincidental that the analytical model almost got the rotational spring stiffness right for 
just this web.  
 
It is difficult to know whether the increase in thickness or the increase in the solid 
outmost parts, so, between the slots and the flanges, is the reason behind the model 
becoming more imprecise for the C-profiles with only 8 slots in the web, and it is 
probably a bit of both. From the results in Table 3.4 it can be seen that the larger the 
thickness of the web becomes, the more the deviations of the analytical method from the 
FEM model shifts towards being more on the unsafe side. This can e.g. be seen for the 
THR145 profiles where the deviation becomes less on the safe side as the thickness 
increases, and is actually a bit on the unsafe side for the 2 mm thick THR145 profile. 
This tendency is recurring for the THR200 and THR245 profiles as well. The same 
connection can be made to the increase in the width of the web, and thus the increase in 
the solid outmost parts, so. 
 
Another observation which can be made from the results in Table 3.4 is that the 
thickness of the web plates has the largest impact on the rotational spring stiffness, 
compared to e.g. the span length. This can be seen from both the analytical model and 
the FEM model, where the rotational spring stiffness approximately triples as the 
==PAGE:45==
3 Analytical model for rotational spring stiffness 
 
 
35 
 
thickness increases from 0.7 mm to 1.0 mm, from 1.0 mm to 1.5 mm and so on, whereas 
when the web width decreases the increase in rotational spring stiffness is just a fraction 
of the increase from increasing the thickness. 
 
Table 3.4 also shows that the stiffness ratio increases as the width of the web increases. 
This is because as the entire width increases, so does the solid outmost parts of the web 
because the slotted part of the web has a constant width. This means that even though 
the width of the web, and thus the span of the web, increase, so does the stiffness 
because the width of the stiffer solid parts, so, increases. 
 
 
A possible source of error for the analytical method is that in the model presented in 
Figure 3.13 many plate areas overlap each other, meaning that areas are counted twice 
in the analytical calculation. However it’s difficult to avoid the overlapping of areas 
since all the plates in the model have to be connected through the centrelines. Another 
possible source of error is that the analytical model doesn’t have the exact same 
boundary conditions as the FEM model, since the FEM model is modelling a web which 
is 1000 mm long and the analytical model only models a 49 mm long part of the web. 
This means that the analytical model is missing the adjacent plates of the web in its 
longitudinal direction which is accounted for in the FEM model, and which most likely 
has an influence on the calculated rotational spring stiffness of the slotted web. 
 
 
It can be concluded that the analytical model as it stands right now is best at predicting 
the rotational spring stiffness for slotted webs with 10 slots and a width at around, or a 
bit less than, 200 mm. 
With a maximum deviation at approximately 15 % for the web model with 10 slots the 
analytical calculation method is best used as a way of calculating the rotational spring 
stiffness of a slotted web with 10 slots. Furthermore it can be concluded that the 
analytical calculation method when used on slotted webs with 8 slots is less precise than 
that if it was used on a web with 10 slots.

4. Experimental research 
 
Experimental research also plays a role in investigating how the joint between the stud 
(C-profile) and rail (U-profile), used for external load bearing wall systems consisting 
of slotted cold-formed steel members, behaves when subject to compression, which is 
the main objective of this thesis. 
As mentioned earlier in chapter 2, Costa (Costa, 1999) has made some few experiments 
in order to study the support strength of walls with slotted steel studs under different 
axial loading, but the sampling wasn’t large enough to conclude anything decisive. 
 
In this chapter a series of tests performed on slotted cold-formed steel members will be 
described. Two different tests will be described and discussed: Short column tests and 
material tests. The short column tests will be used to investigate the behaviour and load 
bearing capacity of the studs and joints between slotted cold-formed steel members used 
for external bearing walls in compression. The results from the short column tests will 
also be used in a comparison with the results from the numerical analysis, which will be 
described further in chapter 5. The material tests are done to be able to specify the 
elastic and plastic material properties of the profiles tested. The material properties will 
also be used further on in the numerical analysis in chapter 5.  
The tests and test results described in this chapter has originally been presented in (Lyng 
& Persson, 2014) and then they have been analysed further in (Andreassen & Jönsson, 
2015). 
This chapter will describe only the tests performed which have relevance to this thesis. 
This means that all the short column tests with a web stiffener originally performed in 
(Lyng & Persson, 2014) will not be described further in this chapter, only those without 
web stiffeners will be discussed. 
 
 
4.1 Short column test 
 
As briefly mentioned above, the investigations of short columns are performed in order 
to observe the strength and behaviour of the joint and the slotted C-profile column used 
for external load bearing walls during compression. The best way to do this is by using 
short columns for the tests, which are more likely to also buckle locally than longer 
columns are. With short column tests it is also possible to load the column with a bigger 
load before failure which benefits the investigation of the joint.

5. Numerical analysis 
 
The main objective of this thesis is to investigate and gain a better understanding of how 
the joint between the stud (C-profile) and rail (U-profile) used for external load bearing 
wall systems consisting of slotted cold-formed steel members behaves when subject to 
compression. To help investigate the behaviour and load bearing capacity of the joint 
and stud during compression experiments made by (Lyng & Persson, 2014) were 
described in chapter 4. In the following chapter a numerical analysis of the system will 
be carried out to further investigate into more detail how the joint behaves during 
compression and expand on the knowledge gained from the tests. 
The numerical analysis is used for the investigation of the joints in the slotted cold-
formed load bearing system as well as the stability. This is done by using a combined 
method consisting of a linear buckling analysis and a nonlinear post buckling analysis. 
The numerical analysis will be carried out using the finite element analysis program 
‘Abaqus’.  
The documentation of the numerical analysis is split into three sections: 
 Model creation: The model created in Abaqus will be described and the different 
choices made in the process of creating the model will be discussed. 
 Linear buckling analysis: A description of the eigenvalue buckling analysis 
performed on the model. The results of the analysis is presented and analysed. 
 Nonlinear analysis: A description of the nonlinear post-buckling analysis 
performed on the model with imperfections obtained from the eigenvalue 
buckling analysis. The results from the analysis will be discussed and the 
behaviour the joints connecting the C- and U-profiles are analysed. 
 
The main focal point of the numerical analysis will be to investigate the behaviour of 
the joint in a model with a 1000 mm long THR145 profile with a thickness of 1.0 mm.  
 
 
5.1 Abaqus model 
 
As shortly mentioned above in the introduction to the numerical analysis the finite 
element program ‘Abaqus’ has been used to model an example of the test specimen 
used in the short column tests discussed in chapter 4. The Abaqus model has been made 
so it resembles the test setup as much as possible, meaning that in addition to the C- and 
U-profiles, which are the components of the slotted cold-formed external wall system, 
the fixed support blocks are also modelled and incorporated into the Abaqus model.  
 
==PAGE:63==
5 Numerical analysis 
 
 
53 
 
Before the model ended up looking and behaving like it do in the final version, a lot of 
analyses have been run to test all of the choices made. The process of getting to the final 
model started with creating some very simple models containing just two parts with a 
simple geometry. The purpose of these simple models was to test e.g. the contact 
interactions between parts or if the constraints used worked as intended. The contact 
interaction is needed to make sure that the different parts in the model doesn’t penetrate 
each other, but apply pressure to the parts they come in contact with when load is 
applied. The constraints in the Abaqus model have the purpose of imitating the 
connective parts in the real system from the experiments, such as bolts, screws and 
welds in a simple way. When these simple tests were done and an understanding of how 
the contact interactions and constraints worked was acquired the full model was 
assembled. 
During the process of making the full Abaqus model there has been a lot of 
complications with it, and difficulties with getting the nonlinear analysis to converge 
towards an acceptable result. This has resulted in a process of changing and adjusting 
the input to the model bit by bit in different versions of the model. Some of the 
important tests the model has gone through in the different versions include the 
following: 
 Testing different constraint methods such as tie constraints and coupling 
constraints. The constraints used in the final model will be discussed further in 
5.1.6. 
 Testing different contact interactions for the model as well as assembly positions 
relevant to the contact interactions. This will be discussed further in 5.1.5. 
 Small changes to the geometry of the slotted cold-formed steel members have 
been tried, such as reducing the radius of the curved corners or removing them 
entirely and replacing them with 90 degree corners. 
 In the latter versions different types of elements have been tried as well as the 
refinement of the mesh has been tested. 
 
On the USB provided with the thesis a log note describing all the changes that has been 
made for each version throughout the process can be found together with some selected 
older versions of the model made before the final one, as well as the final version of the 
Abaqus model. 
 
In the following sections a description of the creation of the final Abaqus model used 
for the eigenvalue buckling analysis and the nonlinear analysis is presented.

5.1.1 Material properties 
 
For the Abaqus model two different isotropic materials have been specified. One 
material which is to be used for the slotted cold-formed steel members and one which is 
to be used for the fixed support blocks. Since the fixed support blocks have to be very 
stiff so they stay rigid only the plastic material properties is specified, and a large 
modulus of elasticity is used. This is to make sure that the only deformations obtained 
from the analysis happen in the slotted members. The material properties for the slotted 
members are taken from the material tests which were described in chapter 4, and in 
contrast to the material properties specified for the fixed support blocks the material 
properties specified for the slotted members have to contain both the elastic and plastic 
properties. The plastic properties are necessary for the nonlinear post-buckling analysis 
which will be carried out for the model. Material test 14 has been chosen as the material 
properties which will be used to specify material of the slotted members in the Abaqus 
model; however some modifications have to be made on the results from the material 
test before they are ready to be submitted into Abaqus.

be specified correctly as to what Abaqus expects. Abaqus expects that the stress strain 
data to be entered for the plastic material properties, the yield stress and its

region of plastic behaviour of the material curve shown in Figure 5.1. Furthermore

correspond to the slope defined by the first point in the true stress and true plastic strain 
graph entered. The data from the tests described in chapter 4 originally performed by

obtainable through the testing equipment. The engineering stress and strain can be

5.1.2 Geometry 
 
The entire Abaqus model is made up of four different parts. The geometry of these parts 
will be described further in this section. All the parts are made as 3D deformable shell 
extrusions, meaning that the cross-section of a part is designed in Abaqus and then the 
depth is specified. Shell elements are chosen over solid elements because the slotted 
cold-formed steel members considered in this thesis are very thin compared to their 
length and width.  
 
The two main parts for the model are the THR145 (C-profile) and THS145 (U-profile) 
which were also used for the short column tests. The geometry of the cross-section is 
almost the same as shown in Figure 4.1; however a few changes have been necessary to 
make to the cross-sectional geometry of the U-profile in order for the contact interaction 
to work better. The geometrical change made to the U-profile is: a reduction in the

5.1.3 Element type 
 
As previously mentioned in 5.1.2 all the parts are made as 3D deformable shell 
extrusions, which means that the type of elements chosen for the model are quadrilateral 
4 node elements, S4, or triangular 3 node elements, S3. Both are linear shell element 
types with eight and six dof’s respectively. Both element types use a fully nonlinear 
formulation which is required for large strains to be calculated and to run a nonlinear 
analysis. It has been chosen to not use quadratic elements because the linear elements 
will provide precise enough results while keeping the computation time down. For the 
quadrilateral elements reduced integration is not used to form the element stiffness. By 
comparing the stress data results from tests run in v10.4 which are with S4R elements 
(with reduced integration) to those from v10.5, which are with S4 elements, a clear 
convergence in the stresses are observed for the version with S4 elements, as well as a 
better stress continuity due to it having four integration points instead of 1 (S4R).  
 
For all parts in the Abaqus model the mesh control is defined as quad-dominated. The 
quad-dominated mesh creates a mesh with a majority of quadrilateral elements, mixed 
with few triangular elements in places where placing a quadrilateral element would 
cause large distortions to quadrilateral elements. This is not wanted as the most optimal 
shape if a quadrilateral element is a square. This means that the majority of the elements 
in the model are S4R elements supplemented by some few S3 elements.

has been used. The mesh for the two plates is shown in Figure 5.7. A finer edge seeding 
is used around the two holes in the horizontal plate marked red in Figure 5.7 (left). An 
approximate element size of 4 is applied to the inner most edge of the red area and an

5.1.5 Interactions 
 
To get the best understanding of how the joint behaves during compression it is 
important to make sure that the Abaqus model is modelled as similar to the real 
behaviour of the slotted cold-formed steel member’s wall system as possible. This e.g. 
means that the elements of the different parts cannot penetrate each other and that 
specific parts of the slotted profiles, such as the web, must be able to move as they 
would in reality without being constrained. The contact interaction algorithms in 
Abaqus provide just that. By implementing contact interaction between the different 
parts in the model, constraints are made without limiting the movement of the model 
unnecessarily. 
Defining the contact interaction in Abaqus is done in two steps: Defining the contact 
interactions and defining the interaction properties, which will be described below. 
 
 
5.1.5.1 Contact interactions 
 
Abaqus provides two different algorithms for modelling contact interaction problems: 
The general contact algorithm and the contact pair algorithm, which are both defined by 
specifying surface pairings. These two algorithms can be used either separately or 
simultaneously and share many of the same underlying algorithms. Equations involving 
groups of nearby nodes from the respective surfaces are formed in Abaqus to enforce 
the contact conditions. 
 
 
Defining general contact: 
The general contact algorithm is specified as a part of the model definition and can only 
be applied to the initial step of the model. The general contact algorithm in Abaqus 
defines the possible contact between surfaces automatically and allows very simple 
definitions of contact. Furthermore the general contact algorithm uses the finite-sliding 
and surface-to-surface contact formulation as the primary formulation and can use edge-
to-surface as a supplementary formulation. Their meaning will be described further 
down in this section. General contact is easy to use in simple models where the contact 
pairs are easily defined as the algorithm automatically defines the possible contact 
surfaces. Figure 5.10 shows the “Edit Interaction” window for general contact in 
Abaqus. Here it can be seen that the default settings is to include all exterior faces and 
feature edges. However more advanced controls for more complicated models can be 
used to exclude surfaces and specify contact properties, surface properties and contact 
formulation.  
 
==PAGE:75==

Since the contact surfaces in this model are more complex it’s easier to use the contact 
pair algorithm as the contact surfaces are easier to control when defining them yourself 
than with the general contact algorithm. For the model created for this thesis both 
general contact and contact pairs were tested in the very early stages to see what yielded 
the best results. The conclusion from these tests was that using contact pairs yielded a 
more precise representation of the contact than the general contact did for the model 
used in this thesis.

applied to any step of the model. For this model the contact pairs are applied to the 
initial step and are then propagated to the other steps in the model, instead of having to 
create them for all the steps individually. Contact pairs are defined manually by 
specifying the two surfaces which may interact with one another. All contact pairs 
specified are assigned a contact formulation and interaction property which will be 
discussed in more details further down in this section.

Master and slave roles: 
The master and slave roles have to be defined for the two surfaces in a contact pair. In 
the general contact algorithm the roles are assigned to surfaces automatically, but for the 
contact pair algorithm it has to be manually assigned. When choosing the master and 
slave roles for the two surfaces in a contact pair the following rules must be met 
according to (Abaqus Manual v6.12): 
 Analytical rigid surfaces and rigid-element-based surfaces must always be the 
master surface. 
 A node-based surface can act only as a slave surface and always uses node-to-
surface contact. 
 Slave surfaces must always be attached to deformable bodies or deformable 
bodies defined as rigid. 
 Both surfaces in a contact pair cannot be rigid surfaces with the exception of 
deformable surfaces defined as rigid. 
This basically means that the stiffer body should always have the master role, and that 
the slave surface should have a more refined mesh than the master surface.

Contact discretization: 
When simulating contact conditions, Abaqus applies some conditional constraints at 
different locations on the two interacting surfaces. These conditions as well as the 
location of the constraints depend on the contact discretization specified in the contact 
formulation. There are two different contact discretization options in available in 
Abaqus: Node-to-surface and surface-to-surface. 
In the node-to-surface discretization each contact condition traditionally involves one 
slave node and a group of master nodes close to the slave node. Values are then 
interpolated to a projection point on the master surface which lies within the group of 
master nodes. The slave nodes in a node-to-surface discretization are constrained so that 
they cannot penetrate into the master surface. The nodes of the master surface can 
however penetrate into the slave surface is the mesh on the slave surface is coarser than 
on the master surface, as shown in Figure 5.15 (top right). This is because the nodes in 
the slave surface relates to a projection point on the master surface which has the 
shortest distance to the slave node. 
 
 
Figure 5.15 Contact discretization6 
 
In the surface-to-surface discretization both the shape of the master and slave surface is 
considered in the region of contact constraints. Instead of enforcing the contact 
conditions at individual nodes the surface-to-surface discretization enforces the contact 
conditions in an average sense over an area of nearby slave nodes. Some small 
penetrations may possibly be observed, however large penetrations as seen for the node-
to-surface discretization does not occur. In Figure 5.15 (bot) it can be seen that the

master and slave role less influence for the surface-to-surface discretization compared to 
the node-to-surface discretization, when talking about penetration of surfaces. 
 
As can be seen from Figure 5.14, the choice of discretization method for the model is 
surface-to-surface. This is because the surface-to-surface discretization method provides 
more accurate stress and pressure results than node-to-surface does. Due to the nature of 
how these two discretization method works the node-to-surface method tends to have 
forces concentrated around the nodes leading to an uneven pressure distribution, where 
since the surface-to-surface uses an average force over an area of nearby slave nodes 
which leads to a more smooth pressure distribution. 
 
 
Contact tracking approaches: 
In Abaqus two different contact tracking approaches can be chosen. These tracking 
approached take the relative movement of two interacting contact surfaces into account 
in the contact simulation. The two approaches are: Finite-sliding and small-sliding. 
Finite-sliding allows for arbitrary relative separation, sliding and rotation of the contact 
surfaces, where small-sliding assumes that there will be relatively little sliding of one 
surface along the other. The finite-sliding tracking approach is the most general tracking 
approach and is also what is used for this model since because larger sliding is expected 
in the model.  
 
 
Accounting for shell thickness: 
Accounting for shell thickness is on for this model, and means that the surface thickness 
of the shells is taken into account when calculating the contact constraints.  
 
 
Slave adjustment:

formulation for contact pairs at the start of the simulation. Using slave adjustment the 
position of the slave nodes are moved so they precisely contact the master surface 
without creating any strain in the model. The common use of it is to eliminate small 
gaps or penetrations between the surfaces caused by numerical round off, but shouldn’t 
be used to correct large errors in the mesh or assembly design.

5.1.5.2 Interaction properties 
 
In a mechanical contact simulation the interaction between surfaces in contact is defined 
by assigning a contact interaction property model to a contact interaction. Many 
different contact properties can be specified in Abaqus, but this section will only focus 
on the two mechanical contact interactions relevant to the model used in this thesis. The 
two mechanical contact interactions are: Tangential behaviour and normal behaviour. 
 
 
Tangential behaviour: 
For the tangential behaviour a friction formulation is chosen. For this model frictionless 
behaviour is chosen as the friction between the slotted profiles is negligible. There are 
also friction formulations such as: Penalty and Lagrange Multiplier, where it’s possible 
to add a friction coefficient, but they will not be discussed further. 
 
==PAGE:84==
5 Numerical analysis 
 
 
74 
 
 
Figure 5.19 Tangential behaviour, Abaqus 
 
 
Normal behaviour: 
By default, the surfaces interact (have constraints) only in the normal direction to resist 
penetration. The normal contact behaviour is defined by the contact pressure-
overclosure relationship and the contact constraint enforcement method. 
 
Pressure-Overclosure relationship: 
The default contact pressure-overclosure relationship used by Abaqus is the ““hard” 
contact” model. This model implies that the surfaces only transmit contact pressure if 
the nodes of the slave surface are in contact with the master surface. Furthermore no 
penetration is allowed, however depending on the chosen constraint enforcement 
method this condition will either be strictly satisfied or approximated. Other contact 
pressure-overclosure relationship available in Abaqus is the three “softened” contact 
relationships using a linear law, a tabular piece-wise-linear law or an exponential law. 
The “softened” contact relationships are specified in terms of overclosure (or clearance) 
versus contact pressure, meaning that the surfaces transmit contact pressure when the 
overclosure measured in the normal direction between the nodes in the slave surface and 
the master surface is greater than 0. The “softened” contact pressure-overclosure 
relationships can e.g. be used to model a soft, thin layer on one or both surfaces.  
For the model in this thesis the “hard” contact relationship has been chosen as it best 
fulfils the requirements of the model, where no penetration is wished and contact should 
only be transmitted when the nodes of the slave surface are in contact with the master 
surface. 
 
 
Constraint enforcement methods: 
As explained above, the choice of constraint enforcement method for the “hard” contact 
relationship affects whether no penetration allowed is strictly satisfied or just 
==PAGE:85==
5 Numerical analysis 
 
 
75 
 
approximated. In Abaqus three constraint enforcement methods are available: Direct 
method, penalty method and augmented Lagrange method, as illustrated in Figure 5.20. 
 
 
Figure 5.20 Constraint enforcement methods for “hard” contact pressure-overclosure, 
Abaqus 
 
In the direct method Abaqus tries to strictly enforce the specified pressure-overclosure 
behaviour for each constraint. The direct method can be used for the “hard” contact 
pressure-overclosure relationship and also for the “softened” contact pressure-
overclosure relationships where it actually is the default and only method available. The 
direct method has its limitations as “hard” contact simulations using the direct method 
are susceptible to over-constrainment issues because of the strict application of the 
contact constraints.  
 
In the penalty method, which is only applicable for the “hard” contact pressure-
overclosure behaviour, Abaqus makes a stiff approximation of the “hard” contact 
behaviour. With the penalty method some penetration of the nodes in the slave surface 
through the master surface will occur as the contact force is proportional to the normal 
penetration distance of the slave nodes. An advantage of the penalty method is that due 
to some of the mechanics behind the calculation it reduces the number of iterations 
needed in an analysis, driving down the computation time. The penalty method can both 
be linear and nonlinear. The difference in the two is that for the linear penalty method 
constant penalty stiffness is applied making the pressure-overclosure relationship linear, 
and for the nonlinear penalty method the penalty stiffness varies. The linear penalty 
method is the default in Abaqus. 
 
As with the penalty method the augmented Lagrange method also just applied to the 
“hard” contact pressure-overclosure behaviour. The augmented Lagrange method uses 
==PAGE:86==
5 Numerical analysis 
 
 
76 
 
the linear penalty method, but then uses an augmentation iterations scheme to reduce the 
normal penetration distance. This means that compared to the penalty method, the 
augmented Lagrange method uses more computation time.  
 
Comparing the direct method to the penalty and augmented Lagrange method the latter 
two are more efficient due to their reduced computation time, as the calculation cost per 
iteration is lower and less total iterations are necessary per analysis. Although some 
small sacrifice is made in the solution accuracy may be expected.

5.1.6 Constraints 
 
The purpose of creating constraints in the Abaqus model is to create a realistic model 
for the connective parts used in the test setup which the Abaqus model imitates. These 
connective parts in question are the screws of the type QPBT 16 from Gyproc A/S used 
to connect the C- and U-profiles with each other in the flanges. Other connective parts 
in the Abaqus model are the M12 bolts used to fasten the U-profiles to the fixed support 
blocks and the 5 mm fillet welds connecting the vertical and horizontal plates in the 
fixed support blocks. However since only the QPBT 16 screws have any effect on the 
behaviour of the joint a description of these will be in focus in the section.  
 
The QPBT 16 screws are modelled in Abaqus as tie constraints. A tie constraint ties a 
master and a slave surface or node region together so there is no relative motion 
between them. The tie constraint prevents slave nodes from separating or sliding 
relative to the master surface. The dialog box in Figure 5.21 shows the options chosen 
for the tie constraint modelling the QPBT 16 screws.

profiles and the slave surfaces for the ties are chosen as node regions around the holes in

discretization optimizes the stress accuracy for the specified surface pairing.

which for Abaqus/Standard (which is used) means that the surface-to-surface 
discretization method is used. This is because it provides optimized stress accuracy 
around the holes of the profiles, which is desirable.

5.2 Eigenvalue buckling analysis 
 
The linear eigenvalue buckling analysis will be performed on all three models 
mentioned in the introduction to chapter 5:  
-Model 1: 1000 mm long THR145 profile and a thickness of 1.0 mm. 
-Model 2: 1000 mm long THR145 profile and a thickness of 0.7 mm. 
-Model 3: 500 mm long THR145 profile and a thickness of 1.0 mm. 
 
The purpose of running a linear eigenvalue buckling analysis is to find the probable 
buckling modes which may lead to collapse of the model. It was seen from the short 
column tests presented in chapter 4 that the main buckling mode observed for the slotted 
C-profile was distortional buckling. Therefore this is also the buckling mode which is 
sought to be found by the eigenvalue buckling analysis.

step prior to the linear perturbation step, or the state of the initial conditions of the 
model if no prior general analysis step exists. The linear perturbation models response is 
purely decided by the linear elastic material properties specified for the base state.

5.3 Nonlinear post buckling analysis 
 
The nonlinear post buckling analysis will be performed on all three models mentioned 
in the introduction to chapter 5:  
-Model 1: 1000 mm long THR145 profile and a thickness of 1.0 mm. 
-Model 2: 1000 mm long THR145 profile and a thickness of 0.7 mm. 
-Model 3: 500 mm long THR145 profile and a thickness of 1.0 mm. 
 
For the analysis of the behaviour of the joint between the C-profile and U-profile, and 
the investigation of the load bearing capacity, a static nonlinear post buckling analysis 
will be used to find the solution to the stability problem. The method used to achieve 
this is the Riks method, which can be used for static post buckling and collapse analysis.  
Because the Riks method is a general analysis step it can include sources of 
nonlinearity. For this model there are three sources of nonlinearity present:  
The boundary nonlinearity is present because of the contact interactions build into the 
model.  
The material nonlinearity is present due to the plastic material properties specified. 
The geometric nonlinearity is present because large displacement effects are activated 
by the “Nlgeom” function as shown in Figure 5.30. By having large displacements 
activated the analysis uses the node positions from the previous increment for the next 
calculation. This means that for the analysis using large displacement elements distort 
from their original shape as the deformations increase. 
 
In a post buckling analysis the load can reach a maximum value and then decrease again 
as failure happens, due to the negative stiffness shown by the load-displacement curve 
which means that the structure must release strain energy to remain in equilibrium. The 
way the Riks method works is that it finds static equilibrium at the end of each 
increment, and uses the load magnitude as another variable meaning that the Riks 
method solves for load and displacement simultaneously. 
 
The settings chosen for the general analysis: static, Riks step can be seen in Figure 5.30. 
Nonlinear geometry is on because in the post buckling analysis the inclusion of large 
displacement effects will ensure that the elements distort from the original shape of the 
model as the deformations and load increases, so buckling can occur. More details about 
using the Riks method for post buckling analysis can be found in 5.3. 
The load is applied to the model as a shell edge load applied at the top of the vertical 
plate in the support block as shown in Figure 5.25 and is:

The post buckling analysis in Abaqus can be solved both with and without geometric 
imperfections. However if the imperfections are omitted, and the geometry stays 
perfect, the post buckling analysis response might exhibit a sharp bifurcation at the 
ultimate buckling load, which may be missed by the Riks method according to 
(Łodygowski, Rakowski, & Litewka, 2014). By introducing the geometric 
imperfections to the perfect geometry it smoothens the discontinuity so you get a more 
continuous response at the point of buckling and allows the solution to follow the 
response more easily.

5.3.1 Implementing imperfections 
 
The imperfections are needed in the model to make it more true to reality. In the Abaqus 
model the geometry is modelled perfectly but in reality elements have a lot of 
imperfections. This is especially true for thin-walled profiles which this thesis is dealing 
with. These imperfections were also described by (Lyng & Persson, 2014) in relation to 
the short column tests described in chapter 4.  
The imperfections found in the eigenvalue buckling analysis in 5.2 are implemented 
into the three post buckling analyses through the node file of the deformed model 
created from the eigenmodes found in the eigenvalue buckling analyses. The 
imperfections are introduced as perturbations in the initial perfect geometry of the 
model. The following has to be written into the “Edit keywords” tool in Abaqus to 
implement the eigenmodes from the eigenvalue buckling analysis as imperfections in 
the post buckling analysis:  
 
*IMPERFECTION, FILE=Buckling-job, STEP=1 
“Number of eigenmode”, “Scale factor”

the eigenvalue buckling analysis and the scale factor is 5. FILE refers to the eigenvalue 
buckling analysis file with all the logged coordinates of the deformed mesh, STEP 
refers to the post buckling analysis step, “1” if it placed right after the initial step. 
More than one eigenmode can be used to specify the imperfections. If more than one 
eigenmode is specified Abaqus uses linear superposition to create the imperfection in

5.3.2.4 Load distribution through the joint  
 
The distribution of the forces transferred through the joint between the C- and U-profile 
has been investigated. The flanges in the C- and U-profiles are connected with screws 
which transfers load between the profiles. Besides this, load is also being transferred 
through contact interactions between the two profiles.  
 
The force transferred through the screws in the joint between the C- and U-profiles has 
been found using the field output request “NFORC” in Abaqus, which calculates the 
element nodal forces that can be attributed to the stresses within that specific element. 
The specific nodal force used is “NFORC2” which is the nodal force in the global y-
direction of the model. A path is created around each hole at the bottom of the C- and 
U-profiles, containing all nodes in the holes, and the “NFORC2” output is requested as 
“XYData” in Abaqus. The total force transferred through one screw is the absolute 
value of the forces measured in the holes of the C- and U-profile for a given screw.

5.3.3 Sources of errors 
 
As was shortly mentioned in the analysis of the behaviour of the joint, there was a small 
inaccuracy in the forces transferred through the screws. This error of uncertainty is due 
to the mesh not being refined enough. Because Abaqus extrapolates the stress values 
from integration points to the nodal locations, and because each node in the model is 
shared by more than one element, the extrapolated values will have to be averaged in 
order to produce a smooth contour plot in Abaqus. It is due to this averaging that there 
is a small deviation of the forces in the screws. This problem can be solved with a more 
refined mesh which was tried, but unfortunately the model wouldn’t run with a fine 
mesh than used for the analysed model. 
 
Another source of error could be the way the screws are modelled in the Abaqus model. 
The screws are modelled as ties, tying the all nodes around the holes of the C-profile to 
all the nodes of the holes in the U-profile so they can’t move relative to each other. This 
isn’t a precise way of modelling a screw, which can’t transfer tension as it can be seen 
the ties does. 
 
Furthermore a possible source of error could be that the ties are “infinitely” strong and 
will never fail as real screws would at some point.

that small deformations happening in the machine e.g. around the clamps is logged as 
well during the test. These reasons could very well be why the vertical displacement of 
test 14 is larger than the one from Abaqus, which is just the vertical deformation of the 
test elements.

7 Conclusion 
 
 
106 
 
7. Conclusion 
 
From the numerical analysis of the perpendicular bending stiffness of the central part of 
the web plate where the slots are placed it was found that the perpendicular bending-
stiffness is reduced to 0.065 or 6.5% of that of a solid web which spans the same 
distance. This means that the slots in the web have a large effect on the distortional 
buckling behaviour of the C-profile during axial loading, because the distortional 
buckling is dependent on the perpendicular bending stiffness of the web. It can also be 
concluded that the numerical analysis carried out with Abaqus is a very precise tool to 
use for determining the perpendicular bending stiffness of a slotted web. However it 
does have its limitations as a new model has to be created every time a mesh with new 
dimensions is analysed.  
A numerical analysis was also made of a full size slotted web, including the solid web 
parts, so, on both sides of the slotted web part. From this analysis the stress distribution 
throughout the slotted web was analysed and it could be seen that the normal stress in 
the perpendicular direction of the web was concentrated around certain parts of the web, 
while being zero at other places. It was discovered that in the parts of the web where the 
normal stress in the perpendicular direction was zero there was a large shear stress in 
the xy-direction. This shows that the deformation of a slotted web happen both due to 
bending in some areas of the slotted web and due to torsion in other areas. 
The results from the stress analysis of the web were used to create an analytical model 
for calculating the angular rotation of the web near the supports using the principle of 
virtual work. The rotational spring stiffness calculated using the analytical model for 
angular rotation showed that for the analytical method there is only a small difference in 
rotational spring stiffness between the different profiles tested with the same thickness, 
whereas for the FEM model the difference in rotational spring stiffness is noticeably 
larger. The deviations of the analytical model for rotational spring stiffness compared to 
the FEM model varies from being 10 % on the safe side to 15 % on the unsafe sides for 
webs with 10 slots. Larger deviations up to 24 % are observed for webs with only 8 
slots in them. This shows that the analytical model is best suited for predicting the 
rotational spring stiffness of webs with 10 slots. 
 
The results from the eigenvalue buckling analysis shows that the first local buckling 
mode in the web of the C-profile and the first distortional buckling mode for the C-
profile happens within 2 kN of each other. The local buckling of the slotted web starts at 
8.32 kN axial loading, and then the distortional buckling starts at 10.25 kN. Furthermore 
it was seen that the wavelength of the first distortional buckling mode was a half 
wavelength.  
 
==PAGE:117==
7 Conclusion 
 
 
107 
 
For the nonlinear buckling analysis it wasn’t possible to achieve a large enough load for 
collapse to happen in the structure. A total axial load of 22.515 kN was achieved in the 
analysis before it stopped and didn’t reach collapse. Due to the same reason it wasn’t 
possible to compare the ultimate load from the numerical analysis to that of the 
experiments. 
The results from the nonlinear buckling analysis were also used for the analysis of the 
behaviour of the joint between the C- and the U-profile, and it could be seen that forces 
was transferred from the C-profile to the U-profile in two ways: Contact pressure and 
shear through the screws/ties. 
It was observed that contact forces were transferred from the C-profile to the U-profile 
in both the flange stiffeners, the web and the flanges. The contact forces were mainly 
concentrated around the flanges, with the largest contact forces being observed at the 
end of the flange stiffeners and in the solid part of the web close to the flanges. It was 
furthermore seen that the contact forces in the web where the slots were very small, 
which confirms that the web in the end of the C-profiles acts like springs which just 
deform when small axial forces are applied to it. A total load of 9.795 kN was 
transferred from the C-profile to the U-profile as contact force, with 8.986 kN of that 
being from the flange stiffeners and web of the C-profile, and just 0.809 kN being 
transferred through the flanges. Lastly it was observed that the contact forces between 
the flanges in the C-profile and the bended corners of the U-profile didn’t occur until 
late in the loading stage, at around 18 kN of total load applied to the structure. 
The force transferred through the screws connecting the flanges of the C-profile with 
the flanges of the U-profile in the joint was also investigated by looking at the 
“NFORC2” output given by Abaqus, which is the nodal force due to the stress in an 
element. Here it was seen that at the maximum load in the nonlinear analysis (22.515 
kN) 12.457 kN were transferred through the screws. These forces were almost evenly 
distributed in the two flanges, with 6.272 kN transferred through the two screws in the 
large flange and 6.185 kN was transferred through the screws in the small flange. It was 
furthermore observed that the load in the bottom screws in both flanges transferred 
approximately 4 kN whereas the top screws only transferred approximately half of that. 
A deviation of 1 % was observed between the total load transferred from the C-profile 
to the U-profile through the screws and contact force. This was due to an uncertainty in 
the forces at the screws given by Abaqus, which was due to the mesh being too coarse. 
Finally it was observed by plotting the development of the forces in the screws and the 
contact forces relative to the total force on the structure, that below a total load of 15 kN 
most of the load is transferred through the screws. When loading above 15 kN the 
contact forces starts to increase a lot, as the forces in the screws are smoothened and 
stay at a constant load. This means that the larger the deformations become in the two 
profiles the more load will be transferred as contact instead of through the screws.

8 Future studies 
 
 
108 
 
8. Future studies 
 
For future studies the analytical model for calculating the rotational spring stiffness of 
the slotted web should be improved so it better accounts for variations in the slotted web 
geometry besides the thickness. 
 
The Abaqus model used for the numerical analysis should be improved so the nonlinear 
analysis reaches the ultimate load where collapse occurs and the load bearing capacity 
of the model should be analysed. 
Furthermore the current connection between the C- and U-profiles should be improved 
to better imitate real like behaviour of a screw. This should be done by implementing 
the screw as a solid part in the joints to connect the C- and U-profiles with each other in 
the Abaqus model, and the results from the model should be analysed to gain an even 
better understanding of how the joint behaves during compression. 
 
The web stiffener which is sometimes used in the slotted cold-formed load bearing 
systems made by Gyproc A/S should be added to the Abaqus model used to investigate 
the behaviour of the joint during compression. The effect of the web stiffener on the 
load bearing capacity of the construction and the behaviour of the joint should be 
analysed for C-profiles of different lengths, in relation to the discoveries made in 
(Jacobsen, 2015) about the forced eccentricity created by the web stiffener. 
 
The behaviour of the joint between the C- and U-profiles, and the distribution of forces 
between the screws and contact should be analysed for a model with distortional 
opening deformations as well. 
 
Gypsum board should be added to the sides of the C-profiles in the Abaqus model and 
the effect of the gypsum board on the load bearing system and capacity during axial 
loading should be analysed. The effect of the gypsum board in the behaviour of the joint 
should also be investigated. 
 
Lastly the possibility of implementing improvements to the joint, with the purpose of 
improving the load the load carrying capacity of the joint, should be investigated.
