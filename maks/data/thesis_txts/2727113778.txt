Abstract
The following thesis focus on the topic of state estimation via machine learning. State esti-
mation in this context refers to the prediction of acceleration responses of bottom-fixed steel
jacket serving as the substructure for the reference wind turbine, IEA Wind 15-Megawatt
Offshore Reference Wind Turbine, when subjected to waves.
The main objective of this research has been to develop a reliable model combining Finite
Element Method and Machine Learning to predict structural response. The aim was to predict
acceleration response in one part of the structure, based on acceleration response recorded
by sensors in different locations.
The structure of interest was modelled using OpenSeesPy. A total of 24 different wave
events were generated using a given wave generator. These waves were subjected to the
structure, and response was recorded in all free nodes.
The recorded data was utilized as visual sensors in the research. To determine the optimal
number and locations of sensors, three different sensor setups, namely sensor setup Case
1, Case 2, and Case 3, were designed. In addition, all three sensor setups included the
desired locations for predictions, which were consistent across all setups.
The 24 generated wave events were categorized into four distinct groups based on their wave
characteristics. These groups consisted of three different experiments, namely experiment
1, experiment 2, and experiment 3, each used for training data. With an additional group
specifically designated for testing purposes. The tree different experiments were utilized to
construct three separate Neural Network dedicated to prediction purpose. To ensure a fair
comparison of the Networks, all three were evaluated using the same testing data.
The results obtained from the three Neural Networks mainly showcased a sufficient level of
accuracy, with small differences. Regarding the three different sensor setups, improvements
in the accuracy were observed when transitioning from sensor setup Case 1 to setup Case
2 and setup Case 3. These alternative sensor configurations demonstrated an improved
performance, resulting in more precise and reliable predictions.
Keywords: Finite Element Method, Neural Network, State estimation, Wave loads, Re-
sponse prediction, OpenSeesPy

Chapter 1
Introduction
Construction is a continuously evolving field. Structures and civil infrastructures are of vital
importance in modern societies, supporting their prosperity and growth. With infrastructure
systems subjected to extreme loadings, such as earthquakes and intense sea waves, it is
essential to continuously maintain high standard of the structures. Damaged structures can
cause several consequences, including economic and environmental losses, and in worst
case human casualties. Structural Health Monitoring (SHM) is therefore a key task in the
field of civil engineering, due to health and safety.
SHM is a system that uses non-destructive inspection (NDI) techniques to provide continu-
ous information about a structures state. The system enables the possibility of conducting
structural integrity evaluations at any given time, allowing for timely implementation of nec-
essary remedial measures [1].
In the field of SHM, a range of procedures can be implemented, such as sensor application,
visual inspection, ultrasonic testing, and other non-destructive techniques. However, sensor
utilization emerges as the most practical approach due to its affordability, ease of installation,
and ability to enable continuous monitoring.
Various quantities, including strain, displacement, velocity, acceleration, temperature, and
humidity, describes the state of a particular location within a structure at any given time.
These quantities can be utilized to detect the structural state over time through the employ-
ment of sensors like accelerometers, strain gauges, and geophones. Accelerometers are
commonly employed in this procedure, as they offer the potential to detect changes in the
structural response, thus indicating the presence of damage [2].
Damage can occur both intentionally and unintentionally. In a general term, it is defined as
alterations within a system that have detrimental impact on its present or future performance.
Regarding structural systems, damage is referred to as changes to the material and/or ge-
ometric properties of the structural system. Explained in another way, the behavior of the
structure transitions from the linear regime, to the non-linear regime. The non-linear regime
is characterized by non-linear material properties, resulting in permanent plastic deforma-
tion. The presence of a non-linear state indicates a reduction in the system’s stiffness, as
well as the accumulation of plastic deformations. Based on this, the identification of dam-
age involves a comparison of the current state of the structure to an initial state, typically
characterized by an absence of damping [2].
To obtain applicable quantities of the structure for the SHM through sensor measurements,
it is necessary to determine the appropriate number of sensors to be deployed. The ap-
propriate number of sensors depends on various factors, including the type and size of the
structure, as well as cost-efficiency and accessibility.
For instance, in the context of bridges, the Tsing Ma Bridge in Hong Kong has been instru-
mented with more than 1000 sensors. However, this an exceptional case. Normally bridge
health monitoring as utilized by less than 100 sensors [2]. But even with this amount of sen-
sors, the method won’t be either practical or cost-efficient. Studies reported to date focus on

A study titled Nonlinear State Estimation via Machine Learning, was conducted as a part of a
previous thesis at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). It focused on utilizing Machine
Learning (ML) techniques to extrapolate the recorded state from sensors placed at specific
nodes of a structure to location without sensor coverage [2]. Building upon this previous work,
the present thesis aims to extend and refine the research by exploring additional structures
and aspects of Machine Learning based state estimation in structural analysis. This method
will conduct a reduction in cost of the SHM, allowing monitoring of locations that are not
accessible. This can be in the case of offshore structures, where the corrosive saltwater
environment adversely affects the sensors and their wiring systems, meaning the elements
below water-line can not be monitored.
Offshore structures have long been relevant. First within oil and gas, and later offshore wind.
With a sustainability point of view, it is inevitable that offshore wind is forward-looking. Hav-
ing them offshore gives the opportunity to use bigger wind turbines, using the better wind
conditions and have less environmental disadvantages [6]. This makes them a subject of
serious interest, and the focus for the current thesis, as their continuous and safe operation
is essential to be maintained during their design lifetime. To obtain safety, the turbines foun-
dation is essential when constructing. Through the years, different types of foundations for
offshore wind turbines were developed. Which one suited for the turbine, is chosen depend-
ing on the depth and substrate. The current project focus on the fixed based foundation,
known as jacket foundations, which is connected to a Reference Wind Turbine (RWT). The
jackets are truss-like constructions, which comprises three to four anchoring points driven
into the seabed. The majority of the jacket structure is located below the water-line, which
makes monitoring using ML techniques relevant for this type of structure.
The objective of this study is to develop a reliable model combining finite element method
(FEM) and ML for predicting the acceleration response in the bottom-fixed jacket structure
and its connected RWT, which is subjected to wave loads. This prediction is specifically
aimed at locations where sensors are not directly positioned. The model relies on data col-
lected at locations where sensors are present.
There have been performed multiple studies on offshore jackets using machine learning
approaches for structural damage detection and fatigue estimation. Where especially the
study performed by [7] is of interest. The paper focuses on damage detection of offshore
platforms by using measured vibration response applied by random excitations. Applicability
of the numerical random wave excitation of the jacket was verified through convolutional
neural network (CNN), long short-term memory (LSTM) networks and CNN-LSTM method,
combined with random decrement technique (RDT). Laboratorian tests of a jacket under
random loads showed that the proposed combination provided good results in structural
damage detection. With a potential in industrial process safety.
Environmental conditions are of key importance in regards of being able to estimate the sea
state based on the vibrations from the sensors. As the external wave conditions are affecting
the dynamics of the structure. In 2021 there was published a paper by [8] where the aim of
the study was to estimate the encountered sea state using machine learning from measured
data of a 14,000 TEU container ship. By creating a convolutional neural network (CNN)
using the provided sea state data, the neural network was able to estimate the directional
wave spectrum of encountered sea based on hull responses. With outputs for sea state
including the significant wave height, modal wave frequency, wave direction and kurtosis.
Examining the performance from the CNN, it was a capable of accurately estimate the sea

Chapter 2
Theoretical Background
The following chapter provides theory of two key components: the finite element method
(FEM) within structural dynamics, and the neural network in the field of machine learning.
Relevant aspects within both fields are described to establish a theoretical foundation for
understanding these methods and their application to the current project.
2.1
Structural Dynamics: The Finite Element Method
Structural dynamics is the study about the response of a structure subjected to dynamic
loads, such as wind, waves and earthquakes. In engineering, it is crucial to understand the
theory of structural dynamics and behavior to effectively withstand and safely resist dynamic
loads.
Within the field of structural dynamics, mathematical models are used to represent the struc-
tures behavior. The field is extensive, with the theory explained in the current project focuses
only on the relevant aspects. In the current project, the mathematical model is implemented
by the numerical method, FEM. It is used to simulate and analyze the behavior of the struc-
ture subjected to dynamic loads. The strength of this method is the ease of which complex
geometries, anisotropic materials, and boundary conditions can be handled, as well as the
possibility to include non-linearities in the numerical simulation [11].
The explanation of the structures motion reduces to solving differential equations, such as
the equation of motion (EoM). However, an exact solution is usually difficult to obtain, which
results in the goal of finding a computable approximation to the solution [12]. The formulation
of the equation of motion can be described for different degree of freedom.
2.1.1
Degrees of Freedom and Natural Frequencies
In the case of mechanics, degrees of freedom (DOF) is defined as the number of independent
variables, which defines the possible positions of a system in space [13]. Within the field of
structural engineering, the DOFs is represented by a displacement or rotation of the system.
Since velocity and acceleration is both derivatives of displacement, they can also be used
to describe the same DOF system. They are also referred to as free-DOFs, which either
illustrate a translation in a straight line or a rotation around an axis. However, DOFs that are
not designated as free do not exhibit any deformation or rotation. This is typically observed for
DOFs located at nodes connected to the structural support, due to its support conditions. A
distinction is usually made between a single degree of freedom (SDOF) and multiple degrees
of freedom (MDOF).
The SDOF system is referred to as a simplified model, described by only one independent
displacement or rotation, by moving or vibrate in one direction. Figure 2.1 illustrates an
example of an internal damped mass-spring, with a single degree of freedom. The system
is built by a mass m, a mass-less spring with a stiffness of k, and a mass-less dash-pot with
the damping coefficient cv.
The dash-pot refers in this case to a viscous damper, with a coefficient proportional to the
viscosity of the liquid in the dash-pot. Given c > 0, energy is extracted from the system. This

Different types of NN can be distinguished based on how they process input data and their

Finite Element Model
Using the theoretical concepts described in section 2.1, a FE model of the bottom-fixed jacket
structure, in conjunction with the RWT, is developed. The model is constructed by elements
and nodes, as depicted in Figure 3.3. Following, essential components involved in the mod-

The finite element model is modelled and analyzed in OpenSeesPy. OpenSeesPy is a
Python interpreter of the original OpenSees, and makes it possible to utilize the framework
of Python.
The Open System for Earthquake Engineering Simulation (OpenSees) is an
open source software, originally developed to simulate the seismic response of structural
and geotechnical systems [20]. The system is continuously developing, and is capable for
modeling and analyzing the nonlinear response of systems using a wide range of material
models, elements and solution algorithm. Thus, the software may be used for dynamic cases
not only subjected to earthquake.
The three-dimensional model of the structure consist of elements that posses six degrees
of freedom (DOFs) per node. These DOFs include translational movements along the three
axes, namely, x, y, and z, as well as rotational movements around each axis. With a total of
326 elements, the structure comprises 163 nodes, resulting in a total of 978 DOFs. However,
it is important to note that the four nodes located at the bottom of each leg are fixed supports,
which implies that these nodes do not experience any deformation or rotation in any of their
DOFs. As a result, out of the 978 DOFs, 954 are considered free-DOFs, available for analysis
and modeling.
Elements and Masses
As mentioned above, the model is built by a total of 326 elements. 316 of these are in the
bottom-fixed jacket structure, and defined in OpenSeesPy as forceBeamColumn elements.
This element type offers the opportunity to encompass distributed plasticity using a range of
integration methods, thereby accounting for non-linear behavior [21]. Distributed plasticity
provides the means to obtain yielding at any given integration point along the element.
It exist a diverse range of integration methods. For the given structure, the Gauss-Lobatto
integration method is used. It is utilized with a total of ten integration points along each
element, including one at each end of the element. Gauss-Lobatto integration is the most
common method, due to it placing an integration point at each end of the elements where
the bending moments are largest [22].
For the elements defined as forceBeamColumn, the geometric transformation is defined.
This is used to transform the local elements’ stiffness and forces to the global system.

tational. The linear transformation neglect the effects of axial forces and member rotations,
in addition to it not account for any non-linearities. On the other hand, PDelta transforma-

Dynamic Loads: Waves
To obtain response for the upcoming state prediction of the structure described in section 3.1,
external loads are applied. In this case, the external loads are dynamic wave loads. For the
development of wave events subjected to the structure, a wave generator developed by Luigi
Caglio is utilized. This wave generator is a script made in Python to generate wave events
given certain inputs, such as the peak period and the characteristic wave height. In this
study, a wave event refers to specific waves subjected to the structure within a certain time
duration.
The wave generator generates waves using the ’airy’ wave theory. The airy wave theory is
often referred to as linear wave theory. This is the simplest theory, assuming small wave
steepness and small waves relative to the water depth [24]. The relative water depth is
defined as the ratio H/d, where d is the water depth and H is the waves’ peak to peak
amplitude, as illustrated in Figure 3.4. In linear theory, the wave profile can represented by
the sine wave expressed as,
η(x, t) = A · sin(ωt −kx).
Here, A represents the wave amplitude, ω is the angular frequency, and k corresponds to
the wave number, which represents the number of waves within a distance of 1 meter. The
wave profile is often approximated using a trigonometric function, specifically a sine or cosine
function. However, it is important to acknowledge that this is an approximation. In real-world
scenarios, the actual shape of a wave can deviate from a pure sine function due to various
factors such as wind effects, non-linear interactions, and other environmental conditions.

3.5.1
Noise
In reality, the acceleration response obtained using measurement instruments will in fact
contain som level of noise. In the context of sensor-derived structural response, noise refers
to unwanted or random fluctuations not representative of the actual structural behavior or
signal of interest, in the measured data [28].
There are several common noise sources, such as environmental noise, electrical noise and
sensor noise. Considering for example environmental noise, external factors such as nearby
traffic or wind vibrations might introduce noise into the measurement data. The presence of
noise can affect the accuracy and reliability of analyses. It can distort the true dynamic char-
acteristics, and potentially lead to erroneous conclusions if not properly addressed. Based
on this, mitigate the effect of noise is of interest in many cases. This can be performed
employing various techniques, including e.g. filtering and signal averaging. However, it is
important to note that it is not feasible to completely eliminate noise from the response data.
Therefore, a certain level of noise is intentionally introduced into the response data used for
future predictions in the current study. Noise is incorporated into the responses generated
by the finite element analysis (FEA) that serve as input to the future neural network model.
However, when comparing the predicted output responses with the actual responses, no
noise is added. This approach ensures that the predictions reflect the true responses of the
structure, without the influence of noise.

method, the noise introduced to the response signals is adjusted based on the sensor’s noise

quantity of measuring sensors. Conversely, the output layer contains a number of neurons

4.3
Optimizing the Neural Network
The NN models and their optimization were set in Python by using the module PyTorch. The
hyper-parameters are identical for each model, and were defined as following [20].
In the neural network models, several optimization parameters are considered to fine-tune
the training process. First, the concept of epochs is introduced in section 2.2 as the num-
ber of complete iterations through the entire dataset during training. It aims to minimize the
error by repeating the iteration process a specified number of times. The batch size repre-
sents the number of sub-vectors propagated through the network before updating the model
parameters. It allows for more efficient computation by processing data in smaller subsets.
Additionally, the learning rate determines the magnitude of parameter updates during each
batch or epoch. A smaller learning rate value results in slower learning but provides stability,
while larger values may lead to unpredictable behavior during training.
For the specific optimization of the models, the following values are chosen: 15 epochs,
batches consisting of 50 sub-vectors, and a learning rate of flr = 1 · 10−4. During training,
the process is stopped when the difference in error between two consecutive iterations falls
below 1 %, ensuring convergence and avoiding excessive training.

are learning and whether they are suffering from issues such as overfitting or underfitting. By

Discussion
The results presented in chapter 5 demonstrate that the three neural network models used
in this study indicate a high level of accuracy in their predictions.
According to the results presented in Table 5.1, Table 5.2, and Table 5.3 in chapter 5 they
indicate that the neural network models trained with different datasets achieved relatively
high prediction accuracy in most of the nodes. When comparing the performance of each
network, there is no significant difference observed in terms of errors or TRAC results based
solely on the values. The largest difference is observed between the network trained with
data in experiment 1 and the network trained with data in experiment 3. In this scenario, the
network trained with data from experiment 3 yields better outcomes. This could be attributed
to the broader spectrum of wave characteristics included into the training process of the
network.
By examining the means of the TRAC values from experiment 1 and experiment 3, the dif-
ference amounts to approximately 4 %, which does not seem significant. However, in the
context of predicting acceleration responses in a bottom-fixed jacket structure subjected to
wave loads, a 4 % difference in the TRAC value might be considered significant. This is
because the acceleration responses are directly related to the dynamic behavior and safety
of the structure. Even small deviations in the predicted acceleration values can have impli-
cations for the structural integrity and performance.
To decide whether the difference of 4 % is significance, it depends on several factors. These
factors include the specific requirements and tolerances of the structure, which must be
further described in the case of real structures. An acceptable range of errors and deviations
should be assessed in the context of structural design standards, safety regulations, and
overall objectives of the analysis.
Upon analyzing the error and TRAC values for each node, it becomes evident that node 11
and node 75 exhibit significantly poorer predictions compared to node 13, node 14, and node
16. This outcome is expected considering the utilization of sensor setup Case 1, which lacks
sensors along the RWT tower and below the water line. The absence of sensors along the
tower inhibits accurate prediction of the tower’s behavior, especially given the stiffness of the
TP. Additionally, the distant placement of sensors from node 11, located close to the seabed,
further contributes to the challenges in accurately predicting its response.
Table 5.4 provides an overview of the results obtained using sensor setup Case 2, and the
corresponding figures in Figure 5.6 and Figure 5.6 visually demonstrate the improved pre-
diction of responses in node 75 compared to sensor setup Case 1. The TRAC value shows a

Among all nodes, node 11 stands out with the highest errors, represented by RMSE and

expected. The TRAC value only improved by 0.0728, resulting in a TRAC value of 0.7429.

In the case of poor predictions observed in node 11, which is located near the seabed, it
is indeed somewhat unexpected considering the placement of additional sensors in close
proximity to the seabed. One would anticipate that the inclusion of sensors in such a loca-
tion would enhance the accuracy of predictions in that area. However, it might be noteworthy
to consider the influence of the structure being bottom-fixed, as indicated by the findings in
the literature study by Kawai et al. [8]. A bottom-fixed structure results in reduced move-
ment and displacement, particularly in closeness to the seabed where node 11 is located.
Consequently, the accuracy of predictions in this area is adversely affected, leading to lower
levels of accuracy compared to nodes situated higher in the structure. This observation sug-
gests that predicting high responses in the structure may be relatively easier due to larger
displacements and more discernible patterns, whereas predicting responses in regions with
lower movements, such as near the seabed, poses greater challenges. The lower accu-
racy in node 11 suggests that the structural behavior and dynamics near the seabed may be
more complex than initially anticipated. It is possible that factors such as sediment interac-
tions, wave reflections, or other underwater phenomena contribute to the challenges faced
in accurately predicting responses in this specific location.
When considering the feasibility and economic aspects, achieving accurate predictions in
locations close to the seabed, including other underwater locations, is of significant impor-
tance. These regions are critical for understanding the structural behavior and ensuring the
safety and integrity of the entire system. Therefore, accurate predictions in these areas can
provide valuable insights for design considerations, maintenance planning, and risk assess-
ment.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
This thesis has proved that the utilization of acceleration responses as input to predict re-
sponses of a bottom-fixed steel jacket structure, serving as a substructure for a reference
wind turbines is possible. The framework employed for the state estimation is a Neural Net-
work.
A preliminary analysis, which included developing a Finite Element Model, generating differ-
ent wave events and time-history analysis with noise added to the signals, was performed.
This was part of the preliminary project to obtain responses for future predictions.
In order to record the desired responses, the locations of interest was decided. These lo-
cations was used to develop the three different sensor setups, locations of sensors, three
different sensor setups, namely sensor setup Case 1, Case 2, and Case 3. In addition, all
three sensor setups included the desired locations for predictions, which were consistent
across all setups.
Three different Neural Network models was trained using training data from its respective
experiment as input. The results obtained from the three Networks, by utilizing sensor setup
Case 1, demonstrated a sufficient level of accuracy in prediction acceleration responses for
this specific structure. However, the network developed through experiment 3 exceeded
the two other networks in performance. This was due to a higher TRAC value along with
lower RMSE and SMSE values indicating lower errors, which overall a better accuracy of
predictions.
Node 75, positioned at the midpoint of the reference wind turbine tower, and node 11, located
at one of the jacket legs in close proximity to the seabed, displayed the lowest accuracy in
their predictions.
Using the sensor setup Case 2, with additional sensors along the reference wind turbine
tower, the prediction accuracy showed a notable improvement for node 75. The TRAC values
increased by approximately 12 %, giving a TRAC of 0.9986. Simultaneously, the RSME and
SMSE decreased to virtually zero.
The sensor setup Case 3 was developed to improve the accuracy of node 11, by attaching
additional sensors below water line. This setup did not achieve substantial improvements
in accuracy for node 11. Therefore further investigations must be performed to improve the
accuracy of locations at the jacket leg close to the seabed.
In conclusion, although predictions at some locations resulted in a not so sufficient accuracy,
the main objective of this thesis have been achieved. The main objective was to develop a
reliable model combining Finite Element Method and Machine Learning to predict structural
response. The aim was to predict acceleration response in one part of the structure, based
on acceleration response recorded by sensors in different locations.

7 CONCLUSION
7.1
Future Studies
Due to limited time, some limitations was made. The reference wind turbine was investigated
in stand-still condition, where the wind loads also were neglected. In reality, this would not be
a real situation, whereas the purpose of wind turbines are to be exposed to wind, such that
is rotates. In a future study, an investigation of the structure subjected to wind, and taking
the rotational forces into considerations would be of interest.
The waves generated in this study are developed in the production state, given a wave height
of 8 - 10 meters. For future work, investigations using different sea states might be relevant.
These states could be in the transition between the production and cut-out (wave heights of
15 - 20 meters) and a 50 years condition (wave heights of 25 - 28 meters).
Regarding the relatively poor accuracy of the predictions generated in node 11, some changes
could be done to the network and data. It could be an idea to use a bigger amount of train-
ing data, preferably with a larger range of wave characteristics. Another suggestion is to
extend the architecture of the Neural Network by adding more layers, as well as extend the
layers using more neurons. Parameters such as learning rate, epochs, and batch might be
changed.
Further investigations are necessary to gain a deeper understanding of the complex dynam-
ics near the seabed and address the challenges associated with accurate predictions in these
specific locations. Additional data, sensitivity analyses, and alternative modeling approaches
may help improve prediction accuracy and enhance the reliability of assessments.
Lastly, for the current thesis, the objective was as mentioned performed only on a bottom-
fixed jacket structure, meaning that the findings of this thesis are only valid for this type of
structure. A future study could also investigate the methods subjecting to a different structure.
