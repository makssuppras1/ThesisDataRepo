Introduction
The capacity demands of optical communication systems are growing year on year.
This rapid growth creates the need for new communication systems technology.
In the late 1990s and early 2000s, the capacity of optical communication systems
was scaled using wavelength division multiplexing (WDM) by adding more and
more WDM channels. However, adding more WDM channels inevitably used up
the readily available transmission bandwidth [1]. This led to the requirement for
more eﬃcient use of the available bandwidth. The introduction of coherent optical
communication systems allowed for the dimensions of light to be taken advantage of
for advanced modulation formats. Information can be transmitted in the amplitude,
phase, wavelength and polarisation of the light [2].
Using advanced modulation
formats allows for multiple bits to be transmitted at the same time, which increases
the possible capacity achievable in a given bandwidth. The capacity per bandwidth
is known as the spectral eﬃciency and is given by,
SE = C
B = log2(1 + SNR)
(1)
where the SNR is the signal to noise ratio and is described by the ratio of the signal
power to noise power.
SNR = P
N
(2)
Multilevel modulation formats, such as quadrature amplitude modulation (QAM),
require higher optical signal to noise ratio (OSNR) the more levels are added [3].
This increase in optical power leads to nonlinear eﬀects in the transmission ﬁber
due predominantly to Kerr nonlinearities. This introduces a limit to the possible
SNR as the signal distortion deteriorates the signal quality. This limit is known
as the nonlinear Shannon limit [4]. The nonlinear induced distortion to the signal
is a major hurdle for increasing the capacity of optical communication systems.
Therefore, it is highly desirable to be able to compensate for the nonlinear signal
distortions and increase the achievable SNR.
Figure 1: SNR versus signal power for a linear and nonlinear channel.
There have been diﬀerent methods of compensation proposed, both digital and opti-
cal. The method of digital back propagation (DBP) has attracted a lot of attention
3
==PAGE:5==
for nonlinearity and dispersion compensation [5]. In DBP, after the signal is re-
ceived, it is propagated through a virtual ﬁber in the electrical domain. DBP adds
computation time to transmission and is limited by the bandwidth of the coher-
ent receiver. Although eﬀective for compensation of intrachannel distortions, it has
proven to, so far, be impractical for compensating interchannel nonlinear eﬀects in
multichannel systems [6]. Optical Phase conjugation (OPC) has regained interest
since its initial proposal in 1979 for dispersion compensation [9]. OPC reverses the
phase of the complex pulse envelope. OPC has the potential to avoid some of the
disadvantages of DBP. As it is an all optical process it provides compensation in
real time and has a much larger bandwidth [7]. One of the popular methods of using
OPC for compensation of nonlinearity and dispersion is mid span spectral inversion
(MSSI). In MSSI, OPC is performed in the middle of the transmission link so that
the distortions in the ﬁrst half of the link are compensated by the propagation in
the second half of the link.
However, MSSI requires both power and dispersion
symmetry which can be challenging to obtain in a real system [8].
The aim of this study is to numerically investigate potential approaches to move
towards a more robust compensation module based on OPC, that does not rely
on highly symmetric system conﬁguration. The compensation module design puts
focus on practicality of implementation and use of commercially available compo-
nents. This work focuses on applying OPC based compensation in unrepeatered
transmission systems.

3
Nonlinearity Compensation in Optical
Communications
Any signal propagating through an optical ﬁber will be subject to distortions due to
nonlinearity and dispersion. The impact of nonlinearities has become increasingly
signiﬁcant with the use of higher order modulation formats as they utilize higher
powers, smaller channel spacing’s and larger bandwidths. As linear impairments to
the signal are eﬃciently compensated by digital signal processing (DSP) techniques
in modern coherent communication systems [3, 21, 22], the nonlinear impairments
have become the limiting factor. There have been many techniques proposed to
compensate the nonlinear impairments accumulated by a signal during transmis-
sion. The methods for compensating nonlinear impairments broadly fall into two
categories, optical compensation or digital compensation. In the digital domain,
digital back propagation (DBP) [6,23–25] has been of interest for compensation of
nonlinear impairments.In the optical domain, optical phase conjugated twin waves
(OPC-TW) [26–28] and optical phase conjugation (OPC) [23,24,26,29–32] are two
approaches to all optical nonlinearity compensation. Each approach has its strengths
and weaknesses. DBP involves the propagation of a received signal through a virtual
ﬁber with the inverse parameters to the transmission ﬁber. DBP is eﬀective at com-
pensating deterministic nonlinear distortions involving few channels but suﬀers from
excessive computational complexity for larger bandwidth signals [6,33]. OPC-TW
compensated for nonlinear distortions by simultaneously transmitting the signal and
its conjugated copy. These are typically stored in orthogonal polarizations. If the
dispersion maps are symmetric then the original signal can be restored by superposi-
tion of the signal and its conjugate at the receiver [26]. However, using one polariza-
tion for the conjugated signal halves the possible spectral eﬃciency (SE) [34]. OPC
in the ideal case can provide perfect simultaneous nonlinearity and dispersion com-
pensation. This is only possible with perfect power and dispersion symmetry [29].
In real systems, the presence of noise [35], third order dispersion [26], polarisation
mode dispersion [23] and imperfect power symmetry [36] aﬀect the symmetry of the
system which reduces the possible compensation. The case of OPC will be explored
in more detail in the following sections.

Lumped Nonlinearity Compensation
Traditionally nonlinearity and dispersion compensation using OPC relies on very
high levels of symmetry in the link. The scheme known as mid-span spectral inver-
sion (MSSI) places the OPC unit at the mid-point of the communication link [29,59].
However, MSSI can be impractical in real optical links as the exact midpoint can be
hard to deﬁne as well as diﬃcult to access. This also requires that the placement of
the OPC unit will have to be tailored to be at the mid-point of every link. Lumped
nonlinearity compensation diﬀers from MSSI in that it does not require access to
the middle of the link to place an OPC unit. Instead a nonlinear element, or com-
bination of nonlinear and dispersive elements, for accumulating a nonlinear phase
shift equal to that of the link, is combined with an optical phase conjugation into a
discrete compensation module.This is often referred to as optical back propagation
(OBP) due to it being the all optical counterpart to digital back propagation. There
are two conﬁgurations for placement of the compensation module, transmitter side
(TS-OBP) and receiver side (RS-OBP) placement.
Figure 11: Diagram of optical back propagation setup. (a) Transmitter side optical back propaga-
tion (TS-OBP) and (b) receiver side optical back propagation (RS-OBP)
There have been various designs for the compensation elements block shown in ﬁgure
11. In [60], a single length of SSMF at the transmitter side was used as the nonlinear
medium in combination with dispersion management in the link to account for the
positive dispersion in the compensation SSMF. The dispersion management took
the form of dispersion compensating ﬁber (DCF) at the end of each transmission
span. A diﬀerent approach was investigated numerically in [61], where RS-OBP was
used with the compensation element consisting of alternating highly dispersive ﬁber
(HDF) and highly nonlinear ﬁber (HNLF) to compensate each span of the trans-
mission link. This approach is analogous to the split step Fourier method (SSFM)
being performed optically. These HDFs need to have large positive dispersion if a
SSMF is for transmission which is not a commercially available option. The use of
ﬁber Bragg gratings (FBG) was suggested instead in [62]. In this section, guidelines
for the design of a TS-OBP compensation scheme using dispersive and nonlinear
elements to compensate for signal distortions in an unrepeatered link. The scheme

Multi-Channel Analysis
The analysis of the ATS-OBP compensation to this point has been performed on
single channel systems. In high capacity systems, wavelength division multiplexed
(WDM) transmission is used, making it an interesting case to analyse the compensa-
tion performance. In single channel systems the dominant nonlinear eﬀect is SPM.
With the introduction of new wavelength channels, the eﬀects of FWM and XPM
between the channels (inter-channel) become a signiﬁcant limiting factor [36]. First
a ﬁve channel systems is used to investigate the performance of the previously de-
signed compensation module. The three compensation span system with At = 90%,

ECLs in the transmitter to make up a 5 channel WDM dual polarization signal.
Each channel is modulated with 16QAM data at a symbol rate of 32Gbaud per
polarization. The central channel is centered at a wavelength of 1550nm. A chan-
nel spacing of 37.5GHz was used. Figure 26 shows the SNR performance with and
without compensation. The SNR is the mean average SNR of the two polarizations
of the central channel. The transmission system with compensation is simulated
both with and without dispersion in the system. In the case without dispersion, the
FBGs are removed from the compensation module leaving just the HNLF. The back
to back SNR for the system was measured as 22dB.
From ﬁgure 26, it can be seen that in the absence of dispersion in the transmission
system, perfect compensation is achieved. The SNR is seen to act linearly up until
it starts to approach the system back to back SNR. Without dispersion, the system
reduces to matching two lumped nonlinear elements, one for the compensation mod-
ule and one for the transmission link. With the introduction of dispersion into the

Nonlinearity Compensation with Partial Power

5.1
Theory
In section 4, a lumped nonlinearity compensation module was investigated that
used a combination of highly nonlinear elements and dispersive elements to provide
compensation.
It was seen during the numerical simulation of the system, that
the majority of the nonlinear distortion is accumulated in the ﬁrst 50km to 60km.
This is further analysed here in the form of a partially symmetric transmitter side
compensation module. This approach is similar to MSSI but, rather than placing
the OPC in the middle of the transmission link, a shorter piece of the same type
of ﬁber is placed with an OPC unit on the transmitter side.
As the ﬁber type
before and after the OPC are the same, the symmetry is determined by matching
the power proﬁles. To achieve power symmetry with a link with an exponentially
decreasing power proﬁle, the power proﬁle in the compensation module must have an
exponentially increasing power proﬁle, given by exp(−αL), for ideal compensation.
Distributed Raman ampliﬁcation can be used to approximate power proﬁle required
for power symmetry [37,58,72–74].
A diagram of the system conﬁguration can be seen in ﬁgure 28(a) and the required
power proﬁle in ﬁgure 28(b). For the purpose of this analysis it is assumed that the
compensation module can provide an ideal exponentially increasing power proﬁle
while the transmission link has an exponentially decreasing power proﬁle. This is
given by the loss in the compensation module having the opposite sign to the trans-
mission ﬁber. The compensation module will provide compensation of the nonlinear
and dispersive signal distortions that are experienced for a length of Lc into the
transmission ﬁber. The dispersion experienced beyond this length is compensated
in the DSP chain. The input power Pc into the compensation module must be set
using an EDFA to match the the power a distance Lc into the transmission ﬁber.
This is given by,
Pc = P0exp (−αLc)
(118)
where P0 is the launch power into the transmission ﬁber. This results in the power
being ampliﬁed up to the power P0 after propagation through the compensation
ﬁber.

6
Conclusion
The goal of this work was to investigate numerically the compensation capability
of OPC based nonlinearity compensation without power symmetry. In section 3.1,
the symmetry requirements associated with ideal compensation using OPC were
presented. For perfect compensation there must be both power symmetry about the
point of phase conjugation and dispersion with the same sign, given that ﬁber type
before and after conjugation are the same. In this report, lumped compensation
modules are investigated as they do not rely on being able to access the mid-point
of the link but rather can be used at either the transmitter or receiver of the link.
In particular, transmitter side compensation is focused on.
In section 4, transmitter side optical back propagation (TS-OBP) compensation
module designs are investigated. The modules consist of alternating steps of highly
dispersive elements (ﬁber Bragg gratings) and highly nonlinear steps (highly non-
linear ﬁbers).
Three diﬀerent modules were initially compared, that diﬀered in
the placement of the dispersive steps.
Through numerical simulation, one com-
pensation module layout was determined to provide the best performance. This
module layout was termed asymmetrical transmitter side optical back propagation
(ATS-OBP). This layout determines the FBG dispersion based on accumulation of
a deﬁned amount of nonlinear phase, as opposed to symmetrically dividing the ﬁber
length. This results in asymmetric dispersive steps. It was found that this approach
provided better compensation than the other conﬁgurations, particularly when the
number of nonlinear and dispersive steps (compensation spans) in the compensation
module are limited to low numbers. A variety of design parameters were numerically
investigated to study their aﬀect on compensation capability and to determine some
design rules for such non-symmetric compensation. It was found that the single
channel compensation was eﬀective for designs utilising very few HNLFs and FBGs.
However, in the case of multi-channel compensation, signiﬁcant deterioration to the
achieved compensation was observed for compensation modules limited to few com-
pensation spans. It was also shown that eﬀective compensation could be restored for
multi-channel systems by using large numbers of compensation spans. This would
likely be impractical to implement using HNLF and FBG but could possibly be
implemented on an integrated platform.
In section 5, a transmitter side compensation module was investigated that employed
a shorter length of ﬁber of the same type used in transmission. This was designed
such that it had power symmetry with only a portion of the transmission ﬁber at
the beginning of propagation in the link. This was continued from an observation
made in section 4 that eﬀective compensation can be achieved by combining optical
compensation of the ﬁrst 50km of transmission with digital compensation of residual
dispersion. It was shown that using 50km of SSMF with power symmetry to the
ﬁrst 50km of transmission could provide compensation for a range of transmission
beyond 50km. A simple design of such a module could provide a practical lumped
compensation unit for a range of unrepeatered transmission distances.
