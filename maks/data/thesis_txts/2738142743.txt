Abstract
The world experienced the effects of COVID­19 in 2020, making the importance of supply
chain resilience even more realized. The efficient operation of the healthcare supply chain
is crucial for ensuring the availability of The efficient operation of the healthcare supply
chain is crucial for ensuring the availability of essential medical supplies and drugs, es­
pecially during the pandemic. The main objective of the study is to evaluate the solutions
to supply chain disruptions and to propose optimal solutions for different types of drugs.
Based on the collected data, the supply chain model was developed using AnyLogistix
software, and computer simulation was used to derive the data under different scenar­
ios. By analyzing the data, the mitigation strategies applied to three different types of
medicines in case of supply disruption were obtained. The study shows that due to the
specificity of medical products, different disruption mitigation strategies should be applied
to different drugs in order to enhance supply chain resilience at low cost and high quality.
Keywords: healthcare supply chain, simulation, supply disruption, redundancy solutions,
mitigation strategy.

Introduction
1.1
Background
Supply chain management is becoming increasingly complex due to several factors such
as globalization, technological advances, and socio­political uncertainty. The healthcare
supply chain (HSC) is particularly complex in terms of its criticality and special impor­
tance. Unlike traditional supply chains, healthcare supply chains involve high­value med­
ical materials and, more importantly, the lives of patients as consumers. The healthcare
industry faces a unique set of challenges and pressures. The quest for efficiency and
cost­effectiveness in the international healthcare system must be balanced with the need
to ensure the highest level of service.
The world has experienced the disruption of patient care and supply chain operations,
even the collapse of global supply chains, which was caused by COVID­19 in 2020. This
eventually led to a sharp slowdown in global economic activity.[1] According to the data
from the International Monetary Fund[1], the impact of the epidemic on economic activ­
ity in the first half of 2020 exceeded expectations. This series of emergencies and their
consequences have led people to pay more attention to supply chain disruption and miti­
gation strategies, and related research has surged in the past two years. People are more
deeply aware that any disruption in the supply chain can have serious consequences and
endanger human life. Disruptive events affecting the healthcare supply chain are ubiq­
uitous. Natural disasters such as earthquakes, floods, and tsunamis, man­made disrup­
tions such as terrorist attacks or industrial accidents, as well as the phenomenon of the
spread of influenza viruses, all pose significant threats to the stability and functioning of
healthcare supply chains.
Given the critical nature of healthcare and the potential impact of disruptions on patient
prognosis, it is important to study and understand how disruptions affect healthcare supply
chain performance. This understanding can help develop effective strategies and mitiga­
tion measures to ensure resilience and continuity of healthcare delivery even in the face of
adversity. By addressing the complexity of healthcare supply chain disruptions, we aim to
develop strategies and solutions that improve the resilience and reliability of these critical
systems. Our ultimate objective is to ensure that healthcare providers consistently deliver
high­quality care, even in the face of unforeseen challenges, and to minimize unnecessary
cost pressures and improve revenue management.
In order to solve this complicated problem, this project is completed by modeling and
simulation. In order to complete the simulation experiment more efficiently, the project
used AnyLogistix software produced by Anylogic Company, which solves the problem
of variable decision­making by optimization and simulation. AnyLogistix is an easy­to­
understand tool that can be used by researchers, students, and professionals to solve
many problems related to supply chain management.[2] Its application makes it possible to
create supply chain models and distribution networks, perform experiments, and analyze
results. Due to its almost non­parametric requirements, users without programming skills
can use it well and enjoy the convenience of the software. This allows the projects to be
entrusted to medical professionals for further research and optimization.
The healthcare system in northern Italy was selected for this project. This is due to the
supply chain, which consists of 21 hospitals of all sizes. The system has been upgraded,

1.2
Objective
The objective is to identify recovery strategies for different classes of drugs in the event
of disruptions within the healthcare supply chain. It also explores methods to optimize
costs while ensuring a high level of service. The model is constructed using AnyLogistix
software, utilizing data from the healthcare supply chain in Northern Italy. Based on simu­
lation results under various scenarios, three mitigation strategies were developed. These
strategies were then evaluated for their effectiveness across different types of drugs. Fi­
nally, a cost optimization approach is proposed, focusing on enhancing the flexibility of
the supply chain.
1.3
Research Questions
This project aims to investigate and propose effective mitigation strategies to manage
disruptions in healthcare distribution networks. Disruption scenarios, like natural disas­
ters and supplier bankruptcies for different pharmaceutical classes, evaluating mitigation
strategies such as inventory optimization and technology adoption, are analyzed. The
objective is to develop a comprehensive framework enhancing an organization’s resilient
supply chain and maintaining high service levels for multiple pharmaceutical classes. The
study is based on the following research question:
• RQ1 ­ How do disruptions impact the efficiency and reliability of healthcare delivery?
• RQ2 ­ How can simulation models, specifically using AnyLogistix software, be em­
ployed to analyze and improve healthcare supply chain resilience?
• RQ3 What mitigation strategies can be implemented to enhance the resilience of
healthcare supply chains?
• RQ4 ­ How do different classes of drugs within healthcare supply chains impact the
choice and effectiveness of mitigation strategies during disruptions?

2.1
Supply Chain Management Overview
With the progress of human technology, the products produced are becoming more and
more complex, and people’s pursuit of efficiency has produced a demand for supply chain
management. Supply chain managemen t is very difficult to define. Here is the definition
of the Council of Supply Chain Management Professionals (CSCMP).
Supply chain management encompasses the planning and management of all activities
involved in sourcing and procurement, conversion, and all logistics management activ­
ities. Importantly, it also includes coordination and collaboration with channel partners,
which can be suppliers, intermediaries, third­party service providers, and customers. In
essence, supply chain management integrates supply and demand management within
and across companies. [3]
Supply chain management has also been changing in this process. Initially, supply chain
management was about managers saving money in a series of activities that cost money,
such as purchasing, selling, manufacturing, assembling, warehousing, transporting, and
delivering goods. As a result, the mindset of supply chain managers revolved around
reducing costs. But at the end of the last century, the purpose of the supply chain changed,
and people realized that the supply chain itself could also be a part of competitiveness.[4]
For example, through excellent supply chain management, Dell made it possible to deliver
computers that meet customer requirements within a few days, which changed the rules
of the computer market.[5] For another example, the American retail giant Costco has
made it possible to provide high­quality goods at low prices through large­scale supply
chain and warehousing integration innovation.[6]
With the practice and development of supply chain management, it has become a subject
with many related studies. In recent decades, a lot of software tools have been developed
specifically for it, and Anylogitix used in this paper is one of them. At the same time, the
research on supply chain management has also become more detailed, and different
studies have been carried out in view of the characteristics of different industries. The
next section will focus on the characteristics of the medical supply chain and medical
supply chain management.
2.2
Characteristics of The Healthcare Supply Chain
From the perspective of a supply chain, a hospital is an organization that brings together a
series of different products (medication, consumables, and cleaning supplies) to support
its normal operations. These products come from different suppliers, which are collec­
tively called external supply chains. [7]The medical flow is an example of an internal sup­
ply chain. In every department of a hospital, there is a storeroom for storing 150 to 400

dles, syringes, and medical gloves).[8] The central warehouse of the hospital provides the
storage room and usually stores 1500 to 2000 different products, in some cases, 8,000
or 9000 different products are only medical supplies. [8]
The management of medical supplies is more complicated than that of pharmaceutical
products. This is because drugs are usually defined by their chemical formula, and in the
category of medical supplies, various products can achieve the same function (meet the
same needs of different people). For example, a supplier’s catalog can contain 140 dif­

on product selection [9], and it is extremely difficult to change their preferences, which
makes it difficult for hospitals to reduce the types of products they purchase. The com­
plex processes and product diversity in the healthcare industry make the logistics costs
of healthcare 8 to 20 times higher than those of other industries.[10][11] Since 2018, re­
searchers have been working on measures to improve the performance of the supply
chain in this field [12]. However, recent surveys show that the performance of different
links in the logistics chain of the healthcare industry is stagnant. [13]
2.3
Healthcare Supply Chains Management
As mentioned in the previous section, the healthcare supply chain is different from other
supply chains. It has high complexity, high product value, and the characteristics of human
life. The healthcare supply chain is divided into two chains: the internal chain is related to
patients, hospital warehouses, etc. Moreover, the external chain has a relationship with
dealers and manufacturers.[14] This makes the management of the healthcare supply
chain different from traditional supply chain management. The chain not only includes the
flow of medical drugs, medical supplies, and other physical objects but also involves the
flow of patients, the establishment of relationships, the distribution of rights, organizational
processes, and so on. [15] Based on these characteristics of the medical supply chain,
special management practices are required to manage it.
The spread of an increasingly severe competitive environment finally affected the health­
care industry: the medical system is under increasing pressure, and it needs to provide
high­quality and consistent medical services for patients while reducing costs. [16]The
cost of the supply chain may account for more than a quarter of the total budget of hospi­
tals. The healthcare industry has made slow progress in reforming its supply chain, lead­
ing to high costs. For example, inventory management: the health care supply chains
usually maintain a high level of inventory to ensure a high level of service, but this is con­
sidered inefficient. This is because the healthcare industry prioritizes the level of service
over costs. After all, human life is paramount.
Due to the particularity of the products and services in the healthcare industry, managing
its supply chain is very complicated. Take the most common products in the supply chain
as an example, such as drugs. It is difficult to predict the demand for drugs. This is
because patients expect various services from healthcare institutions, which makes it very
complicated to predict demand. The shelf life of drugs is not long, and eventually, they
will be wasted because of expiration. Therefore, it is necessary to design a new medical
care supply process to improve customer service, and at the same time improve overall
quality management and flexibility to meet the changing needs of patients and reduce
waste. [17][18]

2.4
Related Research
Healthcare has always been one of the most important industries, and protecting lives is
very important for every government and individual, but it has not been paid enough at­
tention for a long time. In the past 20 years, due to several different disasters, the medical
supply chain has been taken seriously. This section reviews the theories, mathematics,
and MCDM methods of health care and resilience, and the latest research on COVID­19
and long­term disasters.
Balcázar­Camacho et al. [19] discussed the interaction and cooperation among the or­
ganizations that make up the supply chain. They proposed a mixed integer programming
model for coordinated planning of healthcare services. In the first scenario, patient care
is carried out under the assumption of coordination, involving a free flow and cooperation
of information to meet the needs. The second scenario assumes that there is no coopera­
tion. Mathematical research shows that the supply chain is improved through coordinated
planning and the total cost is reduced by 16.33%.
Jochmann and León­González [20]proposed a mathematical approach to decision­making.
They used a semi­parametric Bayesian method to estimate healthcare demand. This ap­
proach aims to control for different behavioral attributes or genetic diversity between sim­
ilar individuals that could affect healthcare demand. Based on this Bayesian approach,
more potential outcome models can be developed to understand the impact on demand.
A model was developed by using a fuzzy TOPSIS decision matrix by Mehralian et al. [21].
Used to improve the agility of the healthcare pharmaceutical supply chain. This model
focuses on delivery method, speed, planning, reordering, and trust development. Phar­
maceutical companies should learn how to manage unexpected interruptions and pay
more attention to supply chain operations. This exemplifies how multi­criteria decision­
making methods are used in the healthcare supply chain. The goal is to sort important
variables through optimization methods and give priority to those more important stan­
dards, thus helping to support decision­making. Multi­criteria decision­making methods
have also been used in sustainable development. Chadian and Singh[22] used a hybrid
multi­criteria decision­making method to find the location of healthcare waste disposal fa­
cilities. The TOPSIS method was used to rank the locations based on variables such as
distance, cost, public exposure, and road conditions. This provided decision­making help
for finding a suitable location.
COVID­19 is a global emergency, which is spreading all over the world at an unprece­
dented speed. Compared to other disease outbreaks in the past, a possible link to COVID­
19’s rapid spread is the global trade in various products [23]. Supply chains span borders,
and the supplies needed to fight the epidemic rely on rapid communication and material
transfer between countries. Lichtenwald [24] pointed out that there is a real risk that med­
ical staff around the world lack the necessary supplies. During the epidemic period, there
was a large­scale shortage of necessary supplies around the world, which made countries
pay a heavy price. This makes the supply chain resilience and the recovery method after
supply interruption Become a popular field. This is also the reason why the author chose
to change the direction of the research direction.

2.6
Disruption Management
Supply chain disruption is considered as the overall process of the occurrence of un­
expected events and their subsequent consequences, which can seriously affect the
progress of materials and normal business activities [28]. Supply chains are usually faced
with a variety of risks. These risks can be divided into operation risks and disruption risks
.[29][30] Operational risk refers to the daily interruption in the operation of the supply chain,
such as delivery time, and disruption risk is a low­frequency but high­impact event. Dis­
ruption risk can be divided into two types: natural factors (earthquakes, floods, volcanoes,
COVID­19) and human factors (terrorist attacks, accidents, supplier shutdowns). These
risks, like temporary disruptions of one or more links in the supply chain, will have a di­
rect impact on the entire supply chain network. As described in the previous section, the
complexity of the healthcare supply chain is extremely high, which leads to the disruption
event having a great impact on the supply chain. Healthcare organizations need to take
measures to prevent and reduce the harm caused by disruption events.
In order to study the influence of disruption events, researchers usually build models
to simulate the occurrence of disruption events for further study.
Hishamuddin et al.
(2015)[31] established a discrete event simulation model, and a three­stage supply chain
with multiple sources was analyzed.
They considered the interference of supply and
transportation through a random interference generator in the model. The most impor­
tant finding of this study is that the number of out­of­stocks and the duration of recovery
are strongly positively correlated with the total supply chain cost, compared with the im­
portance of sales loss.
2.7
Mitigation Strategy
In modern global supply chains, disruption risk has become a risk that cannot be ignored.
The low probability and high impact of supply chain disruption and the losses caused
by it can threaten the financial situation of an organization. In order to minimize losses,
companies take different measures, such as redundant inventory or helping suppliers
restore supply. In 2009 Toyota North America prevented supply chain disruptions due to
supplier failures by warehousing critical parts [32]. Using alternative suppliers is also a
method that companies will adopt in the face of disruption.

3.1
Case Background
The case study of this project is situated in northern Italy, especially in the Emilia­Romagna
region. This region represents significant developments in the Italian healthcare sector in
recent years.
In order to take a more direct approach to influencing public spending, some countries
have centralized the procurement of goods and services through the introduction of spe­
cialized national or sub­national bodies, such as Central Procurement Bodies (CPBs).
This practice is strongly encouraged in a number of European countries such as the UK,
France, Sweden, Denmark, Austria, and Italy (Dimitri et al, 2006) [35]. Over the past 15
years, Italy’s healthcare system has transitioned from a traditional decentralized model to
a modern centralized approach. Compared to the decentralized model, the centralization
in Italy’s healthcare system through regional has been effective in reducing costs without
compromising the quality of healthcare services (Ferraresi et al, 2021)[36]. Figure 3.1
shows the main processes and connections between the two supply chain models, with
each entity clearly identified.

to the central warehouses, where they are stored until needed. However, there has been a

3.4.1
Central Logistic Hub
This hub acts as the primary receiving and distribution center for the entire network, man­
aging the flow of medical supplies from over 300 suppliers. It occupies an area of 5000
square meters and handles approximately 6200 different stock keeping units (SKUs), in­
cluding drugs and medical devices. [38]
The facility features 11 loading bays, of which 5 are designated for receiving goods and
6 for dispatching. Its design is based on the AVEN warehouse, adhering to the most ad­
vanced logistical standards in Italy and Europe. The interior is divided into distinct zones,
including areas for receiving goods from suppliers, storage (both general and specialized
such as refrigerated rooms, flammable material shelters, and pharmaceutical storage),
picking and shipping, and quarantine. [39]
In the model established in this paper, the central hub plays the role of transit and storage
between the hospital and suppliers. All medicines are sent to the central hub at one time
from the supplier, and then the hub sends the medicines to the hospitals according to the
different needs of the hospitals. Since the warehouse is large enough and only a few
drugs are involved in the model. Hence it can be assumed that the warehouse space is
infinite and no cost considerations are needed.

Product Category Analysis
Hospital drug management involves the intricate task of handling a vast array of pharma­
ceutical products, each with its own unique demand characteristics. Efficient drug man­
agement is essential for ensuring the smooth operation of hospitals and delivering quality
healthcare services. Variations in drug utilization exist not only across different hospitals
but also among various wards within the same hospital, necessitating a holistic approach
to drug management. Rather than addressing drugs individually, a more practical strategy
involves classifying them into distinct categories based on their demand characteristics.
The concept of classifying pharmaceutical products stems from the research of Azzi et al.
(2013) [40], where data from collaborative projects with AVEN provided insights into the
complexities of drug management. This classification system hinges on two pivotal fac­
tors: the standard deviation of item demand and the commonality of item demand across
the healthcare network. In essence, drugs are categorized based on their consumption
Analysis and Improvement of Healthcare Supply Chains Subject to Disruptions

==PAGE:26==
variability (high or low) and their commonality within the healthcare network (high or low).
This classification yields four distinct classes:
Class 1: Products characterized by high commonality in the network and low demand
variations, indicative of regular consumption patterns across all hospitals.
Class 2: Products with lower diffusion but regular consumption. It means that they are
not very popular or utilized in the target market. Despite their low market penetration,
these products have stable consumption patterns. Once consumers accept and use such
products, they purchase and use them on a regular or recurring basis.
Class 3: Products exhibiting high diffusion but irregular consumption patterns. It means
this popular product does not have a stable consumption pattern. Consumers buy and
use them irregularly and erratically.
Class 4: Products with low diffusion and irregular consumption.
To gain deeper insights into the management requirements and best practices for each
class of pharmaceutical products, representative drugs from Classes 1, 2, and 3 were
selected for analysis in this project. The detail can be shown in figure 3.6. A compari­
son of categories 1 and 2 shows the impact of the commonality of medicines within the
health network. In contrast, the comparison of categories 1 and 3 shows the impact of
consumption regularity.

other painkillers such as paracetamol or ibuprofen alone. Painkillers are popular in mar­

Class 1 pharmaceutical products with widespread diffusion in the network and stable de­

Clenil 0.8 mg/2 ml is used for the treatment of Asthma. It is not widely distributed due to

Pantorc 40 mg is used to treat disorders associated with acids in the stomach and in­

alongside irregular demand fluctuations. Focusing on this medication allows for an in­
vestigation into how widespread demand, coupled with irregular consumption patterns,
impacts overall demand variability.
Through the comparative analysis of these three pharmaceutical products, insights into
the nuanced relationship between demand commonality and demand variability within
hospital drug management are gleaned. Understanding this relationship informs the de­

care outcomes.

3.6.1
Order Interval
Order interval is a regular, re­occurring period of time between consecutive orders placed
to replenish inventory in supply chain management. The order interval is determined
based on factors such as demand patterns, lead time for delivery, and inventory holding
costs. Effective management of ordering intervals helps to maintain optimal stock lev­
els. If the intervals are too short, excess costs and overstock may result from frequent
ordering. In contrast, if the intervals are too long, there is a risk of stock­outs, which can
affect customer satisfaction and sales. Due to the different classifications of these three
medicines, the ordering intervals exhibit varying distributions.
Class 1
Class 1, Tachidol 500mg, is characterized by high demand in the target market and main­
tains a stable and regular consumption pattern. This reflects its reliability, with short in­
tervals between orders due to the robust demand. Consequently, hospitals are expected
to place orders with the central hub once every day to maintain adequate stock levels.
The requirement for daily ordering ensures that hospitals can meet patients’ needs for
medicines in a timely manner and minimize the risk of shortages. This frequent ordering
process guarantees that essential drugs, such as Tachidol 500 mg, are always available
for patient care. Additionally, placing orders with the distribution center on a daily ba­
sis facilitates efficient inventory management, enabling accurate demand forecasting and
timely replenishment. This systematic approach not only supports the high demand for
Tachidol 500 mg but also enhances the overall effectiveness and reliability of the supply

Class 2

stock levels ensure that there are no stock­outs, while maximum stock levels prevent
stock build­up. Min­max policy with safety stock improves the efficiency and reliability of
inventory management by balancing inventory levels with fluctuations in demand, help­
ing the healthcare supply chain maintain stable operations and a high level of customer
service.

3.6.4
Shipping
The shipping policy should clearly define how products are sent. First, it should specify the
type of vehicle to use for different shipments. Additionally, it should determine the order in
which shipments are prioritized. Finally, the policy should establish the schedule for when
to ship the products, including the frequency and timing of shipments, to optimize delivery
efficiency and meet demand in a timely manner. By clearly defining these aspects, the
shipping process becomes more efficient, ensuring prompt and cost­effective delivery of
products.
Vehicle Type
Two types of transport vehicles are involved in this case, the truck and van. The detailed
parameters of transport can be seen in Figure 3.8. The speed limits in Italy to follow are
50km/h on urban roads, 70 km/h on urban highways, and 90 km/h on secondary extra­
urban roads [47]. Therefore, the speed distribution is set triangular with a minimum value
of 20 km/h, a maximum value of 80 km/h, and a mode of 50 km/h. The van is mainly
used to transport all products from the central hub to hospitals. The truck starts from the
Suppliers and the terminal at the central hub.
We assume an unlimited number of vehicles, and this setup ensures the most efficient
delivery times. Every order is dispatched in a timely manner without having to wait for
a vehicle to be dispatched. This arrangement not only improves logistical efficiency but
also effectively avoids transport bottlenecks during peak demand periods.

Figure 3.8: Vehicle Type
Shipping Policy
The shipping policy we chose is Full Truck Load (FTL) with periodic checks. The order(s)
will not be shipped until at least one vehicle is fully loaded but it is checked once a defined
period. The policy has parameters with the minimum load ratio and period.
The minimum load ratio sets the threshold for the ratio between the ordered product
amount and the vehicle capacity. If this threshold is exceeded, the vehicle is considered
fully loaded. Typically, in every shipment from the Central Logistics Hub to hospitals,
an average of 400 to 800 different types of drugs are delivered [38]. However, in the
model, we are focusing on a scenario where only three types of drugs are transported by
a Healthcare Supply Chain (HSC) vehicle. During periods of low transport demand for
the three types of drug, it is not desirable to have a backlog of goods waiting to be fully
loaded in order to maintain service levels. Therefore, although we chose FTL, we set the
minimum load ratio to 0. This means that the vehicle will be considered fully loaded as
long as there is a cargo to be transported, regardless of the vehicle’s load capacity. This
means that even if the vehicle is loaded with only one product, it will be considered fully
loaded and transported.
Period defines the time period during which the policy is triggered. The value is set as 1,
meaning the policy is triggered every day.
Order Priority
The shipping policy sets the priority according to which the orders are shipped. In this
case, ELT (Earliest Lead Time) is chosen, meaning that orders with the shortest lead
time are given the highest priority. This decision is particularly important in the medical
field, where timely delivery of pharmaceuticals and medical supplies can be critical. By
prioritizing orders with the least lead time, it ensures that essential drugs and supplies
reach hospitals and patients as quickly as possible, minimizing the risk of shortages and
ensuring continuity of care.
Shipping Schedule
Typically, hospitals operate from 8 am to 6 pm. Consequently, we have aligned the ship­
ping schedule for vans from the central hub to hospitals to accommodate these hours. The
central hub itself operates from 6 am to 9 pm, providing a window for managing logistics
effectively.
During this operational period of the central hub, vans are utilized to transport goods from

cessed within the hub’s operational timeframe, facilitating seamless distribution to hospi­
tals according to their opening hours.
Shipping paths
Figure 3.9 demonstrates the transport path of a healthcare supply chain that includes
suppliers, central hubs, and hospitals.
Figure 3.9: Shipping Path in HSC Network
Suppliers use trucks to deliver via different routes to the central hub, which then distributes
and delivers supplies to individual hospitals using vans as needed, This network structure
ensures stability and reliability of supply.
In the transport process, each van departs independently from the central hub (DC). This
means that each van operates individually after loading its cargo and does not form a
convoy with other vans or make a group transport. Each van has a destination. In each
transport mission, the van has to deliver its cargo to a specific hospital. For example, if a
van departs from a central hub with a destination of Hospital 1, it delivers goods to Hospital
1 only during this trip. After delivering the load to the specified hospital, the van does not
move the load between hospitals. Instead, each van returns directly to the central hub
after completing its delivery.
Taking this approach helps to keep a high level of service. It avoids the need for vans
to transport between multiple hospitals and ensures simplicity and efficiency in transport
routes. In this way, each transport task can be focused on one hospital at a time, thus
simplifying transport management and ensuring the efficient operation of the supply chain.

follows a uniform distribution for transportation time. Transporting all products from sup­
plier 1 to the central hub takes between 3 to 5 hours. Transport goods from the central
hub to a group of hospitals takes between 1 to 2 hours.
The capacity of the vans is not large and the shipping policy does not require full loads.
Therefore, the processing time for both the loading process (which takes place at the

3.7
Key Performance Indicators
To evaluate the effectiveness of the healthcare supply chain. Three main aspects are
focused on: financial, service, and operational performance.
3.7.1
Financial Performance
Financial performance indicators will help assess cost­effectiveness. Key metrics include:
inventory carrying cost, transportation cost, and inventory spend. The formula is:
Inventory Carrying Spend = Number of Units × Cost per Unit per Day × Number of Days
Transportation Cost = Number of Kilometers × Cost per Kilometer
Inventory Spend = Number of Products × Cost per Product
Total Cost = Inventory Carrying Spend + Transportation Cost + Inventory Spend
(3.5)
A detailed cost analysis identifies opportunities to optimize inventory management. These
financial indicators provide an important basis for decision­making. By analyzing and
comparing the cost­effectiveness of different strategies, the most cost­effective supply
chain management strategy can be selected.
3.7.2
Service Performance
Service performance indicators will measure customer satisfaction and service levels.
Key indicators include: ELT service level by orders and lead time.

ELT service level by orders: shows the ratio of on­time orders to the overall number of
outgoing orders. The value is defined as:
ELT Service Level by Products = Products in the on time orders
Products in the outgoing orders
(3.6)
Where:
Products in the on­time orders — the amount of products from the orders that were deliv­

By analyzing ELT service levels, it is possible to identify the causes of delayed deliveries,
such as insufficient stock, transport problems, or supplier delays. High ELT service levels
indicate that the supply chain has a high level of reliability and efficiency to meet the
hospital’s needs on time. It reduces the risks associated with drug shortages.
3.7.3
Operational Performance
Operational performance indicators will help assess the efficiency and resilience of health­
care supply chain processes. Key metrics include: Available inventory including backlog,
and Fulfillment of received products by customers.
Available inventory including backlog shows statistics on the quantity of product items
available in stock less the quantity of processed products for the incomplete orders (orders
that currently lack the required amount of products).
Fulfillment received products by customers shows statistics on the total quantity of product
items delivered to the customer. The data is defined as the sum of products that were
delivered as late products and fulfillment received.
Analyzing these two KPIs can reflect the efficiency of inventory management. By monitor­
ing the number of products actually available in stock, it is possible to understand whether
inventory management is effective and whether orders can be fulfilled in a time­efficient
manner. The indicator also takes into account the impact of backlogged orders, which can
help companies to identify the problem of insufficient inventory, optimize inventory replen­
ishment strategies, and avoid delayed or unfulfilled orders caused by inventory shortages.

By setting up these scenarios, we aimed to comprehensively analyze the effects of sup­

ensure continuous supply in future disruptions.
3.9
Mitigation Strategy
The mitigation strategies aim to ensure a continuous supply of drugs and minimize the
risk of shortages during future disruptions. In this case, three strategies are set to mitigate
the disruption.
3.9.1
Long­term Backup of Suppilers
The first mitigation strategy involves establishing a long­term backup supply of drugs.
This approach focuses on creating a strategic reserve of essential drugs to buffer against
supply chain disruptions. By maintaining a sufficient stockpile, we can ensure that criti­
cal medications remain available even when primary suppliers are temporarily unable to
deliver.
3.9.2
Increase Inventory Level
Increased inventory levels have enabled the central warehouse to better respond to fluc­
tuations in demand and supply disruptions. Adequate inventory reserves can improve
on­time delivery of orders and reduce stock­outs and delays. However, the strategy also
brings with it increased inventory holding costs and management complexity. Therefore,
when implementing this mitigation strategy, the central hub needs to consider the costs
and benefits and optimize inventory management to ensure effective cost control while
improving healthcare supply chain resilience.

Substitution of Similar Drugs
When the supply disruption exists for a long time, the original product in short supply
can be replaced with a comparable product without affecting the overall performance of
the drug. Meanwhile, emergency purchasing strategies minimize disruption losses and
ensure that each hospital’s needs and service levels are met.[49]

Discussion
6.1
Representativeness
In this case, we analyzed three mitigation strategies to respond to supply disruptions: in­
troducing alternate suppliers, increasing safety stocks, and introducing substitutes. These
strategies exhibit their respective advantages and shortcomings in different healthcare
supply chain environments. In this section, each mitigation strategy will be discussed in
depth, and its effectiveness and applicability in real­world applications will be evaluated in
conjunction with experimental data. By comprehensively comparing the three strategies,
we aim to provide practical guidance for hospitals to develop effective response plans in
case of healthcare supply chain disruptions. At the same time, we will explore possible
future research directions to further optimise supply chain management and enhance the
resilience of supply chain in the face of uncertain market environments.
The following is a discussion and comprehensive comparison of each strategy. As well
as a discussion of the representativeness and applicability of each strategy.
6.1.1
Backup Suppliers
Bringing in Backup suppliers is an effective strategy for coping with healthcare supply
chain disruptions, and is particularly applicable in situations where rapid restoration of
supply is required. However, higher procurement costs and start­up delays are factors
to be considered. Hospitals should establish partnerships with alternate suppliers well in
advance in their healthcare supply chain planning to shorten start­up times and minimize
the impact of supply disruptions. Some drug costs can then be appropriately reduced
through negotiation strategies.
This strategy is applicable to most types of drugs, and in this simulation, it is particularly
applicable to Class 2 drugs represented by Clienl. Since the spread of this drug is lower
and the demand is less, the alternate supplier can be activated directly in the face of an
outage. Even if there is a delay in activation, the intervention of the backup supplier can
respond effectively to the disruption, maintaining high service levels and meeting patient
needs. With this strategy, hospitals can quickly restore supply in the event of a healthcare
supply chain disruption, ensuring continuity and patient satisfaction.
6.1.2
Increase Inventory
Increasing safety stock can improve supply chain stability, especially in response to supply
disruptions. However, the increase in inventory carrying costs and the need for inventory
storage space are the main challenges in implementing this strategy. The central hubs
should plan appropriately to provide adequate storage space for the increased inventory.
Hospitals should rationalize safety stock levels based on specific needs and risk assess­
ments to ensure stock protection while effectively controlling costs.
The strategy does not work well for most drugs. As analyzed by experimental simulations,
the drugs were not restored to the desired level of service under any of the mitigations of
this strategy. We thus obtained from our analysis that the strategy of increasing inventory
is only applicable to drugs with very low demand. Furthermore, it is only applicable in
case of short­term disruptions. Other mitigation strategies should be adopted when faced
with long­term disruptions.

6.1.3
Drug Substitutions
Introducing substitutes is a flexible and effective supply disruption mitigation strategy, es­
pecially when demand is elastic. Hospitals need to assess the market acceptance and
supply chain integration of alternatives in advance to ensure a smooth transition during
supply disruptions. In addition, attention should be paid to the cost of sourcing alternatives
to avoid financial stress due to increased costs.
This strategy is particularly appropriate for Class 1 medicines, represented by Tachidol.
Due to the high demand and stable consumption that characterize this group of medicines,
they are the most responsive to supply disruptions. The impact of shortages can be well
mitigated by finding good substitutes in advance. Although we did not implement such
a mitigation strategy for pantorc in this experiment, it would be equally applicable to the
drugs like pantorc with high demand. Specifically, hospitals should identify suitable substi­
tutes in their supply chain planning and build strong relationships with substitute suppliers
to ensure that substitutes are available quickly when needed. At the same time, hospitals
should conduct detailed cost analyses to ensure that the introduction of alternatives does
not put undue pressure on their overall financial position. In this way, hospitals can main­
tain a high level of service in response to disruptions in the supply of Class 1 medicines
and meet the needs of patients, while effectively controlling costs.
6.2
Limitations of The Model
The most important thing in constructing a model to simulate the real situation is to make
the model as close as possible to the real situation. The model constructed in the paper
is deficient in the following aspects:
• In order to simplify the model, the setup of suppliers can provide 3 different drugs,
which is usually done by different suppliers in reality.
• In reality, hospitals are consumers and small warehouses in the healthcare supply
chain, and their role is closer to that of distributors. However, in the model, it is
simplified to become a consumer, so that it loses the function of storing medicines.
• In the model, the route for delivering drugs is such that a vehicle can only go from
the central warehouse to one hospital at a time. In reality, however, it is common
for a vehicle to deliver to several hospitals at a time, depending on the amount of
goods being delivered.
• The model also assumes that there are no restrictions on the number of vehicles,
but in reality, there are restrictions on the number of vehicles and personnel to be
considered.
• The model does not have any penalty mechanism for late deliveries. This does not
correspond to the real situation and creates a bias in the results.

fluenced by the following errors.
• Data error: Because the demand for drugs in hospitals is unpredictable and chang­
ing at any time, the data input to the model may be different from the facts, which
may lead to errors in the results.

6.4
Future Work
6.4.1
Activating Lateral Transshipment
Introducing Lateral transport between hospitals and setting up the hospital’s own storage.
In the current simulation model setup, hospitals do not have storage and each demand
is shipped directly from the central hub. Future research could explore the setting up of
hospitals’ own warehouses and inter­hospital lateral transport in case of supply disrup­
tions. With this strategy, the flexibility and responsiveness of the supply chain could be
significantly improved. It could also ensure that in the event of a supply disruption, hos­
pitals are able to quickly obtain needed medications from other hospitals, maintaining a
high level of service quality and patient satisfaction.
6.4.2
Multi­strategy Combination and Optimization
In this case, only a single mitigation strategy be analyzed. In further work, the effective­
ness of the combination strategy can be evaluated by modeling different healthcare supply
chain disruption scenarios. Compare the performance of single and combined strategies
in different scenarios and analyze their impact on inventory levels, order fulfillment rates,
holding costs, and total costs. Sensitivity analyses are used to assess the impact of dif­
ferent variables (e.g., disruption duration, demand fluctuations, supplier responsiveness,
etc.) on the effectiveness of the portfolio strategy. Strategies can also be analyzed for ap­
plication to hospitals of different sizes (e.g., large medical institutions, small clinics, etc.).
Targeted recommendations can be provided through the analysis.
6.4.3
Exploring the Impact of Different Metrics on Strategy Selection
Future research should also focus on how various metrics influence the selection of miti­
gation strategies. Factors such as order intervals, demand, drug type, and transport mode
play a key role in determining the most appropriate strategy. Given the limited number of
mitigation methods and the unique nature of the healthcare supply chain, not all methods
are universally applicable. The key is to quickly find the most appropriate method and
identify the relevant data needed to make informed decisions. To this end, the following
methods and techniques can be used: data analytics and machine learning techniques
such as predictive analytics and cluster analysis to predict future supply chain disrup­
tions and demand patterns; optimization techniques such as Linear Programming (LP)
and Mixed Integer Linear Programming (MILP) to optimize costs and service levels; and
sensitivity and scenario analyses to assess the impact of changes in key parameters on
strategy performance. Integrate the above techniques to provide real­time strategy selec­
tion recommendations; and identify effective ways to manage supply chain disruptions by
comparing the performance of different strategies in similar industries or regions through
benchmarking and best practice studies. Through these approaches, future research
could create an integrated framework that helps supply chain managers make quick and
informed decisions in the event of a disruption, maintain high service levels, and optimize
costs.

Conclusion
In conclusion, this study comprehensively analyzed the impact of disruptions on the health­
care supply chain and assessed effective mitigation strategies to improve the resilience
and continuity of healthcare services. The study was conducted using simulation model­
ing with AnyLogistix software, focusing on the healthcare supply chain in Northern Italy,
specifically in the Emilia­Romagna region. The main methodology included the creation of
the supply chain network structure, the processing of input data, the conduct of simulation
experiments, the setting of key performance indicators (KPIs), and the scenario setting.
Experimental simulations were also carried out under different scenarios to evaluate solu­
tions to supply chain disruptions and propose the best solutions for different medicines. It
was found that due to the specificity of healthcare products, different disruption mitigation
strategies should be used for different pharmaceuticals to achieve supply chain resilience
at low cost and high quality.
The principal conclusions can be summarised as follows:
• AnyLogistix software was used to simulate the operation of the supply chain under
different scenarios. The analysis of the results showed that the disruption has had
a great impact on the level of medical services. After the interruption, when the
inventory in the central warehouse was exhausted, the service level would drop to
0. A very short power supply interruption has little influence on the system, but with
the increase in disruption time, the negative influence would become greater.
• By using AnyLogistix software for simulation modeling, this study is able to ana­
lyze in detail the impact of supply chain disruptions in a controlled environment and
assess the effectiveness of various mitigation strategies. By constructing an accu­
rate supply chain model, the researcher was able to simulate different disruption
scenarios, such as port lockdowns due to natural disasters or supplier shutdowns
due to manmade accidents, and observe the specific impact of these disruptions on
various segments of the supply chain. Simulation modeling provides a way to test
and compare different mitigation strategies such as alternate suppliers, increased
inventory, and alternative medicines in a virtual environment, and the data gener­
ated provides strong support for decision­making. Simulation modeling can identify
weaknesses and potential risk points in the supply chain in advance, improve supply
chain responsiveness optimize resource allocation, and reduce losses due to dis­
ruptions. The dynamic simulation feature of AnyLogistix software also allows real­
time adjustment of parameters, enabling supply chain managers to react quickly and
take optimal countermeasures when faced with actual disruptions. This approach
demonstrates the great potential and utility of simulation modeling in supply chain
disruption analysis and mitigation strategy evaluation, providing valuable tools and
methods for future supply chain management.

characteristics and consumption patterns. For medicines with high demand and sta­

is particularly important to meet patient demand and avoid shortages by introduc­
ing a substitute medicine with similar efficacy and responding quickly to disruptions
in the supply of the original medicine. For medicines with moderate demand and
