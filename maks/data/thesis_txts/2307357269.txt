Abstract
The objective of this thesis is to investigate the method of ’¼ünding the column stability of thin-
walled column described in EN 19931-1: 2005. When analysing the stability of thin-walled
columns, local buckling plays a large factor in ’¼ünding the buckling resistance. EN 1993-1-1.
2005 classi’¼ües the column pro’¼üles in 4 di’¼Ćerent classes. Thin-walled column are classi’¼üed as
Class 4. For these pro’¼üles itŌĆÖs necessary to ’¼ünd an e’¼Ćective area of the pro’¼üle.
In this thesis the analytically known equations used for ’¼ünding the buckling resistance are in-
vestigated. The method described in EN 1993-1-1: 2005 assumes that the column is in pure
bending, giving an e’¼Ćective area from constant compression along the cross-section. In real
life no columns are perfectly straight, because of some form of imperfections in the column,
geometrically or otherwise. These imperfections give an added moment contribution to the
resistance of the column. Therefore two new methods of ’¼ünding the buckling resistance of the
column are found from modifying the Ayrton-Perry/Perry-Robertson formula.
From the ’¼ünite element program Abaqus the buckling resistance of columns have been found
from running a non-linear analysis and the buckling curves for both the analytical method and
the numerical method using Abaqus have been compared.
Keywords:Column stability, buckling, Class 4 columns, non-linear analysis

Introduction
5
Introduction
Steel as a structural material has been widely used over the last many years. Therefore it is of
great importance to have accurate standards and norms to use, when calculating the bearing
capacity of columns, beams etc. Most countries have well established standards, which are a
collection of descriptions and equations that are to be used.
In Europe most countries used The European Standard commonly known as the Eurocodes.
This standard is collection of experimental and theoretical work from di’¼Ćerent European coun-
tries. Most countries do also have national standards which can be implemented in the Eu-
rocode. This is because in some cases assumptions have to be made, which can di’¼Ćer from
country to country. In Denmark the standard known as Danish Standard, or DS is used, while
in Britain for example the national standard is known as BSi or British Standards.
This thesis investigates the known methods of ’¼ünding the column stability of thin-walled
columns, also known as Class 4 columns. Class 4 is a description of the column pro’¼üle. The
di’¼Ćerence between thin-walled columns and normal columns is the increased probability that
the column will buckle locally before the column will reach its maximum buckling resistance.
The method of calculating Class 4 columns is described in EN 1993-1-1: 2005 and EN 1993-1-5:
2006. This standard will be used for analysing the columns in this thesis. A general description
of thin-walled steel structures is found in EN 1993-1-3: 2007.

6.1
Column Stability
In this chapter the beam theory is modi’¼üed to include the e’¼Ćect from an axial load. If the axial
load is su’¼āciently large, the column bends and buckles. This load at the time of buckling, is
known as the critical load. In real-life columns are not ideally straight, with a centrally placed
axial load. Therefore buckling of real columns is a gradual process, where the displacement
increases and eventually becomes virtually unbounded, (S. Krenk & J. H├Ėgsberg, 2011).
The displacement magnitude can be found from either adding imperfections to the initial ge-
ometry or by adding transverse loads to the column, leading to initial curvature of the column.

In the next module called Step a new step is created which will start after the initial step.
In the initial step the boundary conditions will be created later. This step has the procedure
type known as Linear perturbation. Under this step type Buckle is selected. This step makes it
possible to ’¼ünd eigenvalues for the column. Using the eigensolver subspace the lowest eigenval-
ues can be found. The value found for the lowest eigenvalue will be used later in the non-linear
buckling analysis of the column.
It should be noted, that for some values of the column slenderness the eigensolver known as
Lanzos has to be used. This eigensolver makes it possible to select which values of the eigen-
value one would like to investigate. The reason for this, is because for some of the short column,
i.e low ╬╗-values, the global mode shapes will not occur at the lowest eigenvalues. The Lanzos
eigensolver makes it possible to ’¼ünd this global mode shape.
After the step has been created, the loads and boundary conditions can be applied to the
model. Because the loads will be applied as a concentrated force, some adjustment have to be
made to make the model able to buckle from a single force without having local buckling at the
placement of the concentrated force. In the Interaction module itŌĆÖs possible to add constraints
to the model. First two reference points are created at each end of the column. They are placed
at the center of the cross section. This is also where the load and boundary conditions will be
applied.
Under Constraint>create di’¼Ćerent constraint can be chosen. All the models used in this thesis
have a constraint called MPC (Multiple-Point Connection) type BEAM. This type of constraint
creates a rigid beam between two nodes to constrain the displacement and rotation at the ’¼ürst
node to the displacement and rotation of the second node. This corresponds to a rigid beam
between the two nodes.
In the Load module the boundary conditions are applied to each reference point at each end of
the column. The boundary conditions are chosen such that the column is simply supported at
each end, see table 5. The load is applied at end, which is free to move in the axial direction.
A unit load of 1 is applied to the model in the linear analysis.

Conclusion
In thesis the method of ’¼ünding the column stability of thin-walled column described in EN
1993-1-1: 2005 has been investigated.
First the analytical method found from the known
