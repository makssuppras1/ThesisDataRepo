Abstract 
Stroke is a severe problem in today’s society, advances in technology therefore need to be 
accessible to help with stroke rehabilitation. The following thesis addresses a challenge defined 
in collaboration with Technical University of Denmark as part of the European Union Horizon 
2020 ReHyb project. The challenge is around the design of interactive dashboards for therapists 
and patients with respect to optimal user experience during rehabilitation. By solving the 
challenge, the user experience during rehabilitation could be optimized.  
The project was conducted using design thinking theory and human-centered UX design 
framework focusing on user needs and user experience. The design process started with product 
definition, then user research methods were used to research users and the market. User 
personas, user stories, and storyboards were obtained to do need analysis. After that, the design 
process was proceeded to design and validation by using methods from brainstorming, user 
story mapping, and prototyping to final evaluation. The final deliverable were interactive 
dashboards, which could make therapists be more efficient at work and monitor patient’s 
training conditions, engage stroke patients gain more motivation during stroke rehabilitation, 
and help the third-party better assist with the patient. It provides a meaningful UX solution 
combining with digital twin technology in stroke rehabilitation.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Background & motivation 
Stroke is one of the most critical long-term diseases around the world. In the European Union 
(EU), it is the second-largest cause of death and a primary cause of adult disability [1]. Only in 
2017, there were 1.12 million incident strokes, 9.53 million stroke survivors, 0.46 million 
fatalities, and 7.06 million disability-adjusted life years lost due to stroke in the European Union 
[2]. In the United States, stroke afflicts 795,000 people each year. The first or new strokes 
account for approximately 610,000 of these. To be more precise, a stroke occurs every 40 
seconds in the United States. And someone dies of a stroke every 3.5 minutes [3]. A study shows 
that about 75 percent of strokes occur in people 65 or older [4]. There’s no doubt that stroke 
has become a serious problem in today’s society, especially for the health of elderly people. 
 
With the increasing aged population and the prolongation of life expectancy, stroke events and 
their long-term sequelae are expected to increase dramatically. As a result, the costs of stroke 
have increased with time, both for society and for families. Stroke is not only one of the primary 
causes of disability, but it also results in an increased use of health and social care resources. 
According to the findings, strokes cost European countries an average of €60 billion every year, 
with Germany, France, Italy, and the United Kingdom — the most populated countries – 
accounting for 63 percent of total expenses (€38 billion) [5]. Furthermore, stroke has a 
detrimental impact on many survivors' lives by altering various functions and limiting their 
capacity to carry out daily activities [6], which also causes a burden on the lives of family 
members. 
 
Despite stroke impairment being frustrating for stroke patients and their families, therapies 
are available to assist individuals to rehabilitate after a stroke. Thus, rehabilitation after a stroke 
becomes relatively important. Nowadays, the growing up of technologies, such as robot-
assisted therapy, digital twin, serious games, AR, and VR technologies, have made the industry 
see the great potential and possibilities in stroke rehabilitation. The combination of those greatly 
promotes both the interest of patients in the treatment process as well as the efficiency of the 
rehabilitation. As these promising technologies have started to be applied during the 
rehabilitation after stroke, it also makes it possible for the therapist to know the overview of the 
patient’s physical conditions and rehabilitation process. However, the existing dashboards for 
stroke patients and therapists have not been well designed with attractive user experiences 
which could promote the recovery of stroke. Both stroke patients and therapists are expected to 
have satisfying experiences during rehabilitation. Designing user-friendly interactive 
dashboards, could not only help patients do rehabilitation exercises in an immersive and 
interesting way with self-motivation but also help therapists do the patient management more 
efficiently. 
 
Nowadays, Rehyb, a project funded by the Horizon 2020 Framework Programme of the 
European Union, is researching on rehabilitation based on hybrid neuroprosthesis [7]. The 
==PAGE:9==
 
2 
ReHyb project is aimed to create a hybrid upper-limb exoskeleton that generates the user's 
digital twin using its own sensors and innovative actuation capabilities, and intelligently 
supports the user's physical movements. In stroke rehabilitation, this upper limb exoskeleton 
can effectively assist stroke patients in their movements. It also provides excellent technical 
support for new treatment modalities and designs. For therapists, the patient's physical data can 
be monitored and captured in time through the digital twin technology. For stroke patients, they 
are able to be assisted with support to do some AR or VR games for training during 
rehabilitation. The introduction of UX design in the healthcare industry also opens up space for 
new design opportunities.  
1.2 Added value 
This project is a master thesis project carried out in the collaboration with Rehyb. The needs of 
different users including stroke patients, therapists, and third-party people in stroke 
rehabilitation have been analyzed through user research. Based on the user's needs and 
combined with UX design principles, the interactive dashboards are designed for an optimal 
user experience. This allows the therapists to easily monitor the patient's physical information 
and rehabilitation training. The dashboards also assist therapists with daily tasks, patient 
management, etc. The patient dashboards make it easy for patients to carry out their daily 
training and motivate them to do exercises to help them recover better. The patient's family can 
also use the 3rd-party dashboards to know the patient's progress and accompany the patient in 
his or her rehabilitation. If the design ideas and solutions in this project are brought to the 
market, they may inspire other designers and researchers in the field of stroke rehabilitation to 
produce more better-designed products. 
 
1.3 Research framework & design methods 
The most important part of this project is the design research process, which makes having a 
research framework following design thinking become crucial. Usually, there are five stages in 
a design thinking process: empathize, define, ideate, prototype, and test [17]. This project 
follows the design thinking process (figure 1.1) to make sure the thinking is continuous and 
provide a good theoretical foundation for the final design. The empathize phase is about 
understanding the users and focusing on user-centered research. In the define phase, the users’ 
needs are defined and analyzed with the statement of problems. In the ideate phase, the project 
gradually creates an initial design idea based on the requirements. In the fourth phase, the 
prototype, a clearer understanding of the user's interaction behavior is developed and solutions 
are built. Finally, in the test phase, the design is evaluated and validated in dialogue with users 
and stakeholders. 
 
 
Figure 1.1: Design thinking process 
In the actual design practice, design thinking is not always a linear process. It could be flexible

3 
and iterative for innovation. During this project, the design process is combined with many 
design methods and the main research framework is shown in figure 1.2. Before the formal 
design starts, the product is defined firstly. The context is analyzed, stakeholders are mapped 
out and an intuitive understanding of the value of the design is gained. The first stage of the 
design is product research, where user research and market research will be conducted. User 
interview, survey and competitive research are used to help build empathy for users and a 
preliminary understanding of the product market. The next stage of design is need analysis, 
using user personas, user stories and storyboards to further analyze users, from understanding 
them to understanding needs. This is because a good product can only be designed by truly 
understanding the needs of the users. The next phase is design and validation, which is the main 
part of the design process that contains the most design methods and is the focus of the project. 
In this part, ideate, prototype and test are done to bring the design from zero to one. Firstly, 
brainstorming and user story mapping are used to develop the idea. Then the information 
architecture of the dashboards is constructed and the wireframes are drawn. Next, by using the 
moodboard, inspirations are gained, and colors and design style of dashboards are chosen and 
defined. After that, the prototypes are made. In the end the iterative design is based on the test 
results in after prototyping, resulting in the final UX design. The process of the design is critical 
as it incorporates many different considerations and directly influences the output of the design.

2.1 Stroke 
A stroke is a life-threatening disease that affects the arteries leading to and within the brain. It 
is also known as a transient ischemic attack or cerebrovascular accident, which happens when 
blood flow to the brain is blocked [8]. If the patient does not have prompt treatment, a stroke 
can cause lasting brain damage, long-term disability, or even death.  
 
2.1.1 Types of stroke 
There are two main types of strokes: ischemic, due to lack of blood flow, and hemorrhagic, due 
to bleeding [8]. Ischemic stroke occurs when a blood vessel supplying blood to the brain is 
obstructed, accounting for 87 % of all strokes. However, hemorrhagic stroke occurs when a 
weakened blood vessel ruptures. Uncontrolled elevated blood pressure is the most prevalent 
cause of hemorrhagic stroke. 
 
Besides, transient ischemic attack (TIA) is sometimes called a “mini-stroke.” It is different from 
the main types of strokes, because blood flow to the brain is blocked for only a short time—
normally no more than 5 minutes. Another form of stroke is known as cryptogenic stroke. In 
most cases, a stroke is caused by a blood clot that blocks the flow of blood to the brain. In some 
situations, the cause of a stroke can’t be determined after an investigation which is defined as 
a cryptogenic stroke. What’s more, brain stem stroke is a stroke occurs in the brain stem, which 
can affect the patient’s body of both sides and may cause someone in a “locked-in” state [9]. 
 
2.1.2 Effects of stroke 
Brain is the extremely complex and essential organ that controls a variety of body functions of 
people. If a stroke occurs, it will influence numerous nervous systems and stop parts of the 
brain functioning. The effects of stroke may include paralysis on the one side of the body, 
speech or language problems, vision problems and memory loss [9]. Most of the effects are 
long-term which have great impacts on the patient themselves both physically and mentally. 
Some of the patients may also experience bladder or bowel control problems, depression, and 
difficulty controlling or expressing their emotions [10]. At the same time, stroke influences the 
families of stroke patients and social care systems. There is no doubt that it gives a burden to 
the society worldwide. 
 
2.2 Stroke rehabilitation 
With the development of science and technologies, the effects of stroke are being known by 
==PAGE:13==
 
6 
more and more people. Therefore, further attention has been paid to the prevention and 
rehabilitation of stroke. Effective stroke rehabilitation approaches have become a topic of 
constant exploration by people from different fields all over the world. According to the 
findings and research, there are some new technologies now being used. Most 
neurorehabilitation approaches are based on a task-oriented motor learning model, which is 
mainly characterized by intensive training for tasks in a specific environment. In order to have 
a better understanding what is involved in stroke rehabilitation, and considering about research 
areas during of Rehyb project, some approaches during stroke rehabilitation are shown in the 
following sections. 
 
2.2.1 Physical therapy 
Physical therapy is the most common approach of rehabilitation for stroke patients. It aims to 
help stroke patients recover basic motor skills including walking, sitting, standing, and lying 
down, as well as the process of transitioning from one kind of movement to another [11]. 
Physical therapy activities might include [12]: 
l 
Motor-skill exercises: by doing the exercises, it helps the patient strengthen the muscles 
and enhance coordination all over the body.  
l 
Mobility training: a walker, canes, wheelchair, or ankle brace may be used to help the 
patient move around. 
l 
Constraint-induced therapy: while the patient practices moving the afflicted limb to help 
enhance its function, an unaffected limb is constrained. This approach is also known as 
forced-use therapy. 
l 
Range-of-motion therapy: certain exercises and treatments can help the patient restore 
range of motion and relieve muscular tension (spasticity). 
 
In some instances, physical therapy is combined with other technology-assisted physical 
activities so as to make stroke rehabilitation more efficient. 
 
2.2.2 Robot-assisted therapy 
Rehabilitation robotics is a scientific discipline concerned with better understanding and 
enhancing rehabilitation via the use of robotic devices. Patients with motor problems caused by 
stroke may benefit from robotic rehabilitation therapy because it can provide high-intensity 
training. For patients with subacute stroke, research found that upper limb motor function 
training using robot-assisted therapy was equivalent to or better than traditional therapy if the 
robotic device is well-designed [13]. In Rehyb, the upper limb exoskeleton is designed to 
intelligently assist the user’s motion. In this design project, there would be some dashboards 
for therapists related to the upper limb exoskeleton in order to complete some device setup and 
relevant interactions. 
 
2.2.3 VR/AR-assisted therapy 
In the realm of rehabilitation for post-stroke patients with motor problems, robot-assisted 
therapy has emerged as a promising approach in stroke rehabilitation. With contemporary 
technological breakthroughs, virtual reality (VR), augmented reality (AR), serious games, or a 
mix thereof, have been introduced to assist robotic treatment in keeping or enhancing the 
==PAGE:14==
 
7 
interests of patients so that they continue to execute their exercises [14]. The use of serious 
games and other computer-based therapies in VR or AR-assisted therapy entails engaging with 
a stimulated and real-time environment. Since VR and AR technology allows for constant 
repetition, the patient could receive real-time feedback on whether he is executing activities 
correctly in the virtual reality environment, which is critical for learning and relearning new 
physical abilities and motions. Finally, to make training more enjoyable, the exercises can be 
made more game-like by including points and additional levels [15]. These technologies can 
effectively eliminate the limitation of space in rehabilitation, which also bridge the gap between 
the clinical environment and at-home sitting. They provide the stroke patients an opportunity 
to practice everyday activities through gamification. 
 
For example, AppAttic (figure 2.1) is a VR stroke rehabilitation app, developed by 4Experience 
[16]. It aims for assisting stroke patients making progress in an game-based way. The content 
is always engaging and could encourage the patients to do the physical exercises while the 
design itself is easy to use and understandable for everyone aged 50+. 
 
Figure 2.1: AppAttic user interface 
With the applications of VR and AR technologies, combining with other technologies such as 
robot-assist technology in stroke rehabilitation has also become a topic of great concern. A 
study shows the effectiveness of VR, AR, or gamification when used in conjunction with an 
exoskeleton or robotic device for the rehabilitation of post-stroke patients [14]. In this design 
project, patients can access the patient dashboards via VR and AR technologies so as to have 
an immersive experience during stroke rehabilitation. 
 
2.2.4 Others 
Except the therapies mentioned above, therapies for the cognitive and emotional activities 
might also be an essential part during stroke [12]. Therapy for cognitive disorders including 
occupational therapy and speech therapy can help the patients with lost cognitive abilities, such 
as memory, processing, problem-solving, social skills, judgment and safety awareness. Therapy 
for cognitive disorders, like speech therapy, for example, can assist a patient in regaining lost 
abilities in speaking, listening, writing, and comprehending. Psychological evaluation and 
treatment, as well as medication, might also be used to assist stroke rehabilitation for recovering 
cognitive problems. During stroke rehabilitation, a variety of approaches are often used for 
patients in different situations. The cognitive health of patients is as important and worthy of 
attention as physical and mental health.

2.3 Healthcare UX design 
User experience (UX) refers to any interaction between a user and a product or service. Every 
element that defines this experience, how it makes the user feel, and how simple it is for the 
user to perform their intended activities are all taken into account by UX design [18]. To create 
seamless user experiences for products, services, and processes, UX designers include market 
research, product development, strategy, and design in the UX design process. UX designers 
construct a bridge between customers and their expectations, starting with understanding their 
needs and ending with the final design. 
 
As UX is applied in the healthcare industry, it provides a convenient and efficient solution in 
the clinical environment, both for doctors and patients. Technology and medical software have 
grown increasingly important in today's healthcare industry. While medical software has gone 
a long way in recent years, many users still find it difficult or confusing. Furthermore, the vast 
range of people who use the software tools (doctors, nurses, patients, administrative employees, 
people with impairments, etc.) makes strong user experience (UX) design in medical software 
even more crucial [19]. Therefore, healthcare UX, which is about the experiences people have 
with healthcare technology and services, is attracting more and more attention. Good healthcare 
UX is intended to provide the user with optimal usability, accessibility, and pleasure of 
healthcare technology and services [20]. However, because there are so many challenges to 
solve in healthcare, healthcare UX is still an extremely broad topic. Clinical UX, a niche within 
healthcare UX focused on clinicians’ and their patients’ interactions with healthcare technology 
and services (figure 2.2). 
 
Figure 2.2: Relationship between UX, Healthcare UX, and Clinical UX 
In order to capture more design possibilities and have a better understanding of the healthcare 
UX and clinical UX, by having a variety of research and analysis, the remarkable design trends 
are summarized in this section. 
 
2.3.1 Interactive virtual reality 
As mentioned in previous section, virtual reality creates an immersive, simulated environment 
in which people can experience the virtual sensations of real life. It's for this reason that virtual 
reality (VR) has sparked key UX trends in healthcare. Furthermore, it has vastly enhanced 
clinical outcomes and the entire user experience in healthcare by recreating triggering 
environments and allowing professionals to better assist patients. 
 
==PAGE:16==
 
9 
2.3.2 Skeuomorphism & neumorphism 
One of the most common UX trends in healthcare is the skeuomorphic design approach. It 
requires creating an interface that looks and feels like a real-world object. A well-known 
example is the recycle bin icon used for discarding files. Affordances in digital user interfaces 
are represented via skeuomorphism. In a digital environment, it corresponds to the user’s 
natural interpretation of objects [21]. Skeuomorphism could make a healthcare dashboard more 
interactive. By adopting this design style, the user experience could be intuitive so people can 
spend as little time as possible learning how to interact with healthcare technology [22]. 
 
Another design style trend is neumorphism, which was born from skeuomorphism and went on 
to create an entirely new UX style [23]. This style focuses on removing any flashy elements of 
the interface and replacing them with a soft look that is consistent throughout the product. It is 
not concerned with duplicating reality in the digital realm, like skeuomorphism is. Instead, it 
aims to create something entirely new in the world of user interface design. Its clean, simple, 
and interactive features have a softer visual affordance, thus it's a good fit for medical devices 
and healthcare dashboards. Queue Up healthcare app is an example with neumorphic design on 
the concept (figure 2.3). 
 
Figure 2.3: Neumorphism in a healthcare app [24] 
2.3.3 Data Visualization 
Since there are a lot of data been produced in healthcare and clinical circumstances, finding a 
good solution for visualizing the medical data is essential. A good medical data visualization 
system can effectively display the data, make the data easier to understand, and ensure the 
effective transmission of information. For this reason, doctors and medical researchers can get 
significant insights into people's health by looking at the visualization of patient data and 
making decisions faster [22]. Now, data visualization has become one of the most common 
healthcare UX trends. 
 
2.3.4 Telemedicine 
Telemedicine serves as an extremely solution for doctors to consult, diagnose, and treat patients 
remotely which breaks the physical boundaries [25]. By using telemedicine, the patient can 
make an appointment and converse with doctors conveniently over the internet. The most 
common technologies used by medical companies to offer clinical services at a distance are 
video conferencing solutions and remote health monitoring apps. There are a plenty of possible 
==PAGE:17==
 
10 
fresh ideas in this area. However, since the potential telemedicine users are various, different 
categories of patients should be considered in the UX design of the software. UX designers 
should take into account the age, psychological condition, technical capabilities, and other 
characteristics of users while creating an inclusive telemedicine application. 
 
2.3.5 Interactive chatbots 
As chatbots have become vital in customer support, interactive chatbots have now been used in 
many healthcare UX solutions as the first line of primary care [25]. Interactive chatbots, which 
are powered by artificial intelligence, can assist medical organizations in providing patient-
centric services which could lower doctors’ workload. They can respond to consumer inquiries 
in a matter of seconds thus the patient can feel cared for even if they talk to a machine. Newer 
UX trends in healthcare hint to the growing popularity of AI-powered chatbots that display 
some emotion in their responses. In other words, users expect healthcare software's user 
interface to contain the human element for improved engagement [20]. 
 
2.3.6 Patient-centric UI design 
The majority of emerging healthcare UX trends emphasize putting the patient at the center of 
the user interface design. Elegant visualizations, adjustable text, easy-to-use functionality, and 
minimal latency are all examples of healthcare UX design trends that improve the usability of 
healthcare apps [22]. While designing the user interface of healthcare apps, it’s important to 
consider the adaption and accessibility of the users, especially the elderly and disabled. 
 
2.4 Digital twin 
A digital twin is a virtual model of a physical thing that is meant to exactly mirror it. After being 
given some information (such as data), the virtual model may be used to conduct simulations, 
investigate performance concerns, and generate possible improvements, all with the purpose of 
gaining important insights that can later be applied to the actual physical thing [28]. As digital 
twin technology has started being used in healthcare, it’s been reported that over the next three 
years, 66% of healthcare executives intend to increase their investment in digital twins, which 
shows people's expectations for the future of digital twins. Digital twins in healthcare are used 
to construct individualized medication and treatment plans by modeling organs and single cells, 
as well as an individual's genetic composition, physiological characteristics, and lifestyle habits. 
With advanced modeling of the human body, doctors are able to do personalized diagnosis and 
treatment planning for patients [29]. There's no denying that digital twins offer the healthcare 
industry and healthcare UX more possibilities.

3.1 User-centered design 
User-centered design (UCD) is an iterative design approach where designers pay close attention 
to users' demands at every stage of the process. To design products that are incredibly useable 
and accessible for users, UCD design aims to incorporate users into every stage of the design 
process using a range of research and design methodologies [30]. It is actually a user-oriented 
perspective that could prompt designers think about the user’s real requirements after 
understanding the context of use. Then the design solutions are carried out and the evaluation 
will be based on the whole user experience. User-centered design will be applied in this project 
as a design thinking way throughout the design process since the users’ needs and the whole 
context would be considered. A few basic design principles that may be used for the product 
design process are the foundation of user-centered design are summarized below: 
l 
From the very beginning, users are included or considered in the design process. 
l 
It’s important to clarify the requirements. The product team continually strives to match 
user needs with project requirements. And the team members should also reach a 
consensus. 
l 
The user feedback loop should be introduced in the product life cycle. 
l 
The design process is iterative, which means the design and validation shall be conducted 
step by step and reflect on each other [31]. 
 
Based on the user-centered design, the design process is usually embodied in the five-stage 
design process proposed by the Hasso-Plattner Institute of Design at Stanford. That process 
includes the following stages: empathize, define, ideate, prototype, and test. As mentioned in 
the first chapter, it serves as a solid theoretical foundation for the design process of this thesis.

3.2 Accessible design 
Since one of the target user groups in this project is stroke patients who are elderly people with 
daily disabilities, having the consideration of accessible design could somehow lower barriers 
that exclude people from using digital products effectively. As accessibility is not just for users 
with certain abilities, such as those with visual, movement, auditory, linguistic, or cognitive 
impairments. It also applies to everyone who has a disability, whether it be permanent, 
temporary, or situational. Therefore, the goal is to improve users’ overall usability of interactive 
dashboard content through design [32]. Accessible design, as a human-centered strategy that 
aims to care for the needs of special groups, has been involved in the design in order to create 
a better product. In ISO’s guide 71, accessible design is defined as “design focused on principles 
of extending the standard design to persons with some type of performance limitation to 
maximize the number of potential customers who can readily use a product, building or service, 
which may be achieved by: 
l 
Designing products, services, and environments that are readily usable by most users 
without any modification. 
l 
Making products or services adaptable to different users (adapting user interfaces). 
l 
Having standardized interfaces to be compatible with special products for persons with 
disabilities.” [33] 
 
Therefore, in the scope of UX design, the key focus of the accessible design is to make the UX 
dashboards adaptable through the design elements, such as text and colors. According to the 
research and WCAG guidelines, there are some principles that should be considered while 
designing for accessibility: 
l 
Avoid unnecessary elements that can cause distractions [34]. 
l 
Avoid making the text smaller than 14–16px. 
l 
Avoid color combinations that are known to cause problems for the colorblind. 
l 
Avoid specifying important information by color alone. Use a combination of text, color, 
or graphical objects. 
l 
Use adequate color contrast. The text should have a color contrast ratio of at least 4.5:1 
against its background. The contrast of icons and graphical objects should be at least 3:1 
against adjacent colors. 
l 
Keep the consistency of components and navigation [35]. 
l 
Use a page title that is informative and descriptive. Informational page headers and labels 
for interactive controls on forms are needed. 
l 
Avoid complex gestures. Choose to use simple gestures. 
l 
Be aware of visual bias. Make the visuals aesthetic. 
l 
While performing user research, take into account issues with accessibility and inclusive 
design. Considerations for accessibility should also be included in the personas or user 
stories.

3.4 UX design principles 
There are many UX principles, according to various classification methods. However, Don 
Norman’s seven fundamental design principles, as a set of most widely used design principles, 
not only guides the direction of UX design but also reflect what factors can bring a good user 
experience to users. By following the design principles, the design could be more efficient, 
effective, and delightful. Since it’s deeply relevant to designing digital products today, the 
design principles will be applied in this design project so as to assist in designing intuitive and 
user-friendly interactive dashboards. The design principles are shown below: 
l 
Visibility: as a basic principle, visibility means that users are more likely to be aware of 
and understand an element if it is more visible. Despite this is a very simple principle, it 
entails the designer should prioritize what interface elements are the most important for 
the user experience and prioritize their visibility because not everything should be visible 
otherwise the interface will be disordered. 
l 
Feedback: the idea of feedback is to let the users know what action was performed and 
what was completed. They are many kinds of feedback in interaction design such as visual 
and audio. Appropriate feedback for users should be comfortable and the users will not 
need to guess the consequence of interactive behaviors. 
l 
Affordance: an object's affordance is an attribute that enables people to understand how to 
use it. The uses and interactions should be based on the mental model of the users and 
should fit their previous experience and cognition. So, the design should be intuitive 
enough that the users can quickly know how to access their desired information just by 
looking at the interface. 
==PAGE:22==
 
15 
l 
Mapping: mapping is about having a clear understanding of the relationship between 
controls and effects. The users will feel natural while using a design with good mapping. 
The slider and stove tops are very good examples that have strong mapping. 
l 
Constraints: constraint refers to limiting the range of user interaction possibilities to 
simplify the interface and guide the user to take the appropriate next action. There are 
many elements in a user interface, if there are no constraints, then the various possibilities 
will make the users feel confused. 
l 
Consistency: consistency is to have similar operations and similar elements to accomplish 
similar tasks. It is the key that the users could recognize the design components so as to 
perform the correct action. Keeping the consistency of design will make the user 
experience easier to use [38].

5.1 User interviews 
As an intuitive user research method, user interviews can effectively help designers understand 
users' thoughts. Since the users of this design project are mainly patients and therapists, they 
were interviewed briefly to understand their ideas in the process of stroke rehabilitation. 
 
The first person interviewed was a female therapist. When asked about the requirements for the 
interactive dashboards, she replied that she hoped the interfaces could be simple and not too 
complicated. Secondly, she said that in her work, there are many types of work to be done, such 
as helping patients move their limbs, rebuilding muscle strength, and giving them regular 
assessments once a week. Some of the work will be trivial. So, if there is a relevant design, she 
hoped it could be as simple as possible and can help them with their work, rather than making 
an extra burden. When asked what she thought was important for stroke rehabilitation, she said 
that continuous exercises were very important. Many patients might lack the motivation to do 
exercises initiatively, but they may be motivated to do exercises if accompanied by friends or 
relatives. Besides, when asked about patients' acceptance of new technologies, the therapist 
mentioned that many patients were elderly and unfamiliar with digital products. So she hoped 
the product should be simple and easy to learn and use. What’s more, when asked whether AR 
and VR glasses have been adopted by the current treatment, the therapist said that she knew 
these were good technologies, but they might be too much for some patients, so the technologies 
are not used in the stroke center where she worked at. 
 
The second interviewee was an elderly male patient. He showed a strong interest in the new 
treatment technology and design and expressed his willingness to try new treatment methods. 
His optimistic attitude made me impressive. He said he usually played golf with his friends and 
did some simple exercises. If he could do rehabilitation training by playing games, he would 
definitely have a try.

7 Design & validation 
Once having a firm understanding of what users want, need, and expect from a product, the UX 
process has then proceeded to the design and validation phase. This is almost the most critical 
and constructive stage of the whole design formulation process. From brainstorming to final 
UX design, this chapter includes various of design activities and methods. All the methods are 
applied in actual design practice with reflections. The design principles especially accessible 
design principles and UX design principles mentioned in chapter 3 will be considered through 
the UX design of this chapter. At the very beginning, brainstorming is used to map the ideas 
from the co-creation meeting. Then user story mapping (USM) helps define the goals, user 
activities and tasks in a user experience design process. Next, information architecture (IA) 
serves as a good tool to map the structure, content, and functions of the dashboard design. After

will then be validated through focus group interviews. Once gaining more insights from users, 
final iterative design dashboards are made based on user-centered design principles. Finally, 
these designs will be evaluated by other users and stakeholders who will provide valuable 
feedback and suggestions for future designs. The detailed design process will be described in 
this chapter.

7.2 User story mapping 
After gaining some ideas, the next step is to combine ideas with UX design for further 
refinement. As a lean UX mapping method, user story mapping (USM) is practiced to illustrate 
the interactions that a user should have with a digital product in order to achieve their goals 
[49]. It is a visual design thinking tool used to define the goals, user activities and tasks in a 
design. It helps break down big stories into smaller ones which is helpful for establishing a 
shared understanding in the design team. 
 
Typically, top-down and bottom-up design strategies are used to arrange the USM. In bottom-
up design, tasks are pieced together to create general activities and all-encompassing objectives. 
The user interactions become the primary focus. The designer can use it to list the individual 
tasks and then think about a prioritized framework and high-level goals for the product or design. 
When using a top-down design, the overall goals are divided into smaller, more manageable 
goals known as activities, and the activities are then divided into tasks. It appears that using this 
==PAGE:39==
 
32 
strategy will help move the design thinking process from abstract to concrete. The USM might 
combine the top-down and bottom-up design approaches from design thinking. Although they 
could be used simultaneously, the designer should make sure that they line up with each other 
and intersect in the center of the USM. 
 
In this project, the interactive dashboards' objectives and user activity backlogs are specified 
using USM as guideline, which helps define the design's key functional features. Since the user 
needs and goals have already been established previously, the ideas are gathered as well. Thus, 
top-down strategy is executed in the USM. While using it, the first thing is to define the users. 
For example, in USM of therapist’s dashboards (figure 7.4), the goals such as “having an 
overview of the patients”, and “knowing detailed information” are then written down as 
backbones. Around the goals, the actual activities that are expected to happen on the website 
are mapped out. According to the goals, more detailed tasks are specified step by step.

7.4 Wireframes 
In UX design, wireframes are usually used to connect the information architecture in different 
interfaces. A wireframe is a low-fidelity tool of a page's interface that focuses on the allocation 
of content space and priority, available features, and desired behaviors [51]. For these reasons, 
styling, color, or images are often absent from wireframes. Wireframes can help designers 
determine intended functions and organize the design elements. A website's numerous template 
associations can also be established with the aid of wireframes. 
 
Although there are numerous good tools for drawing wireframes, paper wireframing is still a 
great way to quickly illustrate the potential solution. First of all, it is relatively easy to use and 
very helpful for the initial considerations of the design interfaces. It allows designers to focus 
more on design features and design itself, such as constraints, rather than spending too much 
time on visuals. Secondly, paper wireframes can be used to iterate quickly, and if they are not 
ideal, they can just be thrown away and drawn again since it is very cost-effective. Wireframes 
are an efficient way to help people understand design ideas in the communication of design 
team. 
 
In this design project, wireframes are used to simply visualize how to organize the design 
elements in different dashboards (figure 7.10). As far as the therapist dashboards are concerned, 
there are many functional elements, so it is very important to visualize the data and functions 
reasonably and make the appropriate layout. Because in the various design elements, not only 
how to clearly realize the functions should be considered, but also what the therapist wants to 
see in the actual clinical practice should be understood. Before starting paper wireframing, 
some literature research is conducted, and some data visualization ways worthy of reference 
are found to assist in the design layout. For the dashboards of patients and the 3rd-party, 
accessible design principles have been kept in mind to do design as simple as possible. For 
example, the text should be easy to read and the font size should and visible. No matter in which

have been taken into consideration. Using wireframes is beneficial for noticing a lot of

7.5 Moodboard 
After wireframing, the design process is moved to making a moodboard to gain some 
inspiration for prototyping. Moodboard is usually the splicing of a series of images, words and 
intention design style pictures. It is a visual method commonly used in the design field to help 
define the intended design style. Adopting the moodboard in the design process can enable 
designers better seek the design direction, polish the design process, fully integrate the 
inspiration and design ideas, and deepen the design concept. Its essence is to visualize emotions 
and bring inspiration to design. In this project, the moodboard is used as the basis for subsequent 
visual design to help define the color and style of the design system. 
 
Starting using the moodboard, it was considered that colors have a big impact on the interfaces. 
Desk research about the use of color has been made. Firstly, as the context is in hospitals or 
stroke centers, the interactive dashboards for the therapist should be as simple, clean and 
reassuring as possible in such a medical scenario. At the same time, blue is found that it is a 
very comfortable and widely used color for healthcare user interfaces. And the theme color of 
the Rehyb project itself is blue. Therefore blue and white are decided to be used as the main 
colors for the therapist's dashboards, maybe with small areas of other colors. 
 
Secondly, for stroke patients, many of whom are elder and have reduced vision.  Older eyes 
are less able to distinguish low-contrast patterns. According to the US National Eye Institute, 
contrast sensitivity begins to decline from the age of 40 and may drop by up to 83% by the age 
of 80 [52]. This has far-reaching implications when designing for older people. This is why it 
is important to use colors with a high degree of contrast in the design, as well as colors that 
create emotion and feeling. Research has shown that blue is the first color of choice for all age 
groups, all genders and all ethnicities. Navy blue, sky blue and aquamarine are particularly 
==PAGE:46==
 
39 
favored by older people. Blue is a calming color and has a calming effect. It can help build trust 
and confidence and can even lower blood pressure. Orange, a warm color, represents 
friendliness and confidence and is preferred by some older people. In color psychology, yellow 
represents warmth and optimism and can make people feel secure. However, bright yellows can 
be tiring and trigger headaches.So once yellow is used in dashboard design, it should be used 
in small areas. Another interesting finding is that older people find purple very exciting and 
interesting. But it can be polarising, with some people loving it and others hating it. Based on 
the above research, for the 3rd-party interfaces, blue and orange will be used as the main colors, 
with yellow and purple as secondary colors. Hence, the moodboard is made and shown below 
(figure 7.11).

7.7 Focus group interview 
After design prototyping, the design process is moved to the design validation phase. To 
understand what users think of the design prototype, focus group interview was used as a 
qualitative user research method in this phase. This method usually involves a facilitator talking 
to a small group of representative users in an unstructured, naturalistic format. From there, an 
in-depth understanding of the issues involved is gained. In this design project, two focus group 
interviews were conducted. Participants in the first interview were a group of therapists from 
Schön Klinik in Munich, Germany. In the second interview, the participants were physicians 
and therapists from Glostrup hospital in Denmark. After the design prototyping was shown and 
well explained, discussions and suggestions were given on the prototypes, thus providing 
valuable input for the final design iteration. 
 
7.7.1 Schön Klinik meeting 
Participants from Schön Klinik thought the prototypes were relatively interesting. The overall 
of the design was accepted. Since they were the actual user of some therapist dashboards, they 
provided a lot of ideas and suggestions for therapist dashboard design. On the "overview" page, 
they thought it was a little bit unserious to have a small "smile" icon on the top since it’s in the 
clinical situation. On the "patients" page, they felt it was not necessary to search for information 
on patients by age. Usually in their work, there were be some regular assessments of patients. 
It would be more convenient if the dashboards could assist them to do the assessments. In 
addition to this, for the “general information” interface, they felt that that kind of progress 
visualizations should be shown for the patients, not the therapists. The therapists would like to 
know more about the data and the actual movements. They also felt that the interface was a bit 
cluttered with too much information and some information was not very clear. For the 
“rehabilitation history” interface, they thought that activity analysis was unnecessary and not 
very useful in practice. However, they found the 3D avatar very interesting and it would be 
great if it could be combined with the heatmap. For the “communication” function, the 
therapists said it was not necessary because they use the chat software they are used to in their 
work. 
==PAGE:59==
 
52 
 
For the patient dashboards, the overall design looked good. However, there were still a space 
for improvements. While the patient doing exercises, it’s better to keep the game interface full-
screen. And it would be more motivating if there was something like "I hope to see you again" 
at the end of the training in the “exercises” page. The text on some of the interfaces was not 
particularly easy to read, so the colors could be adjusted. For the “friends” feature, they think 
that some patients may not like it, and whether they will like the design or not is based on the 
personality of the patients. So perhaps having them compete with themselves rather than with

7.9 Feedback 
After the final design is finished, the design process has been moved to the final design 
validation phase. The goal of validation is to show that the design is consistent and 
comprehensive regarding the demands of the users and stakeholders [61]. At this step, the 
designer shows the design product to get the feedback if it meets the user requirements. In this 
project, two design validation methods are conducted for gaining insights. One is stakeholder 
interview with team of Syncsense company, another is questionnaire with stroke patients from 
Roskilde golf club. Their feedback provides impressive inputs for for future design. 
 
7.9.1 Syncsense 
In the final feedback session, a stakeholder interview was conducted with Syncsense founding 
team. Syncsense team introduces VR technology that enables physical and cognitive training 
for the elderly and disabled [62] . It enables users to have access to virtual nature experiences 
through VR technology. As people involved with stroke rehabilitation technology, the 
Syncsense team was invited to a stakeholder interview to give their opinions on the design of 
==PAGE:72==
 
65 
the therapist dashboards. In general, participants found the current design interesting, especially 
the combination of the digital twin with the heatmap, which was very innovative. They thought 
that it would be better if the presentation of information on the heatmap was more visual. For 
example, the motion range would be better represented by more specific curves and angles with 
the 3D avatar. The data for cognitive information should also be presented in a more visual way. 
They also mentioned that currently there were many healthcare apps, and that it would be a 
more systematic and comprehensive solution if therapists could also monitor patients on their 
mobile phones. Future designs could be further optimized with these comments to the final 
dashboard design. 
 
7.9.2 Roskilde golf club 
The Roskilde golf club is a self-organized club for stroke patients. Patients usually get together 
for golf training to promote stroke recovery through exercise. After the design team had 
introduced the patient dashboards, a user survey was conducted using a questionnaire. A total 
of seven responses were collected online and physically. Six of them were stroke patients and 
one was a relative of a patient, the youngest being 65 years old and the oldest 81 years old. The 
participants thought the interfaces were very clear and easy to understand. When asked about 
their views on the “ranking” function at the end of training of the “exercises” dashboard, four 
said they wanted to compare themselves with others and two said they only wanted to compare 
with their own training history. As to whether they would like to share their training results on 
a social platform, four people said they would like to share them, while three said they would 
only like to see them for themselves. For avatar customization, half of the participants wanted 
to develop their own avatar through this function, and the other half said they did not want to. 
In the questionnaire, the interface of the main page was presented in two color combinations, 
one in blue-orange and the other in blue-yellow. The vast majority of people preferred the blue-
orange paired interface, regardless of whether there were other cartoon elements. This confirms 
that the choice of color scheme for the user interface design was the right one. It is also worth 
noting that the participant mentioned that many stroke patients might need help to complete 
their training exercises. Overall, the design was very well accepted and the information 
obtained from the questionnaire has been very helpful for the subsequent design.

8 Discussion 
As mentioned in section 1.4, the overall question of this project is around the question: “How 
can interactive dashboards for therapists and patients be designed to provide the optimal user 
experience during rehabilitation?” Therefore, this chapter will be based on the research 
questions discussing about the research results. 
 
8.1 The interactive dashboard design 
In this project, all user dashboards have been designed with human-centered design principles. 
The design is user-driven, keeping the principles of ease of use, simplicity, consistency and 
"less is more" in mind. In the design, the users are guided through the interface with sensible 
navigation bar, rather than being lost in a multitude of interfaces. Logical information 
architecture is used, categorizing information in a hierarchical manner. When there is a lot of 
information, the layout of the interface gives visual priority to the key information by 
highlighting it. Also, the dashboards are designed with constraints so that user behavior can 
happen in the way it was designed, rather than getting out of hand. The design also takes into 
account the users’ mental model and design elements are presented in a way that is familiar to 
the users. The dashboards have been designed to minimize the learning curve for the users, 
making the design easy to accept. There may be a lot of space for improvement, but based on 
user feedback, it is at this point easy to use and very acceptable. 
 
8.2 Design elements 
This section will address the first research question: “Which design elements should each 
interface obtain in terms of user-friendliness, user-motivation, trust and functionality?” 
 
One of the the most important design elements for designing an interface is color. This is 
because color influences the user's perception of the product and the acceptance of its use. For 
example, in the case of stroke patients, as most of them are elderly, some of them have vision 
reduction. Therefore, when designing the product with accessible design, the colors are chosen 
according to the people with poor eyesight. Red and green colors were avoided because of the 
possibility of red-green color blindness. Low-contrast colors were also avoided and high-
contrast colors were chosen. In this way, the user can not only use the product feeling 
comfortable, but also be motivated. 
 
Another design element is the soft shape. Shapes can be felt by people very intuitively. Sharp 
shapes can be dangerous and unsettling, while soft shapes can be reassuring and comfortable. 
In the design, sharp shapes were avoided to use, so that all shapes chosen have a sense of affinity. 
For example, in the therapist dashboards, neumorphism to used to make the interface elements 
softer. For the patient interfaces the design is also based on rounded corners avoiding right 
angles. While achieving functionality, this will make the user feel friendliness and trust. 
 
Scale is also an important design element. It refers to the size of one element in relation to 
==PAGE:74==
 
67 
another. Scale affects the visual balance and hierarchy of an interface. For example, in therapist 
dashboards, a large element can be obvious to the user that this is a focused section to be used 
and viewed. On the contrary, smaller text may be ignored. It is therefore essential to pay 
attention to the scale of design elements in interface design and layout. 
 
8.3 Digital twin & avatar 
The second research question “How could a Human Digital Twin (HDT) model be connected 
to a patient avatar to increase the patient's motivation?” will be discusses in this section. In the 
design, the patient can create an avatar with a therapist before he or she is ready to start their 
stroke rehabilitation training. This interesting design promotes motivation for the patient to do 
exercises. In the patient interface, they can see their avatar, which is actually a mapping design, 
once they get used to seeing the image of their avatar and they will be more willing to continue 
the exercises. In the 3rd-party dashboards, the patient's family member can do the avatar 
customization together with the patient, all of which makes the avatar effective in facilitating 
the patient's training. 
 
8.4 Data in dashboards 
According to the research question”How could the sensor data, visualizations and other relevant 
information provided by the HDT be included in the dashboards in a meaningful and user-
friendly way?”, this section will give an answer. HDT can capture a very large amount of data 
and it is a very important question which data is actually needed for the therapist. During the 
design process, literature research had been referenced and some data visualization had been 
used in the therapist dashboards. However, in the focus group interview, it became clear that 
that data was not really of interest to the therapists. What they were most interested in was a 
3D heatmap of the patient's movements, so combining the 3D heatmap with the HDT data was 
a great way to do this. This also gave me the important insight that desk research is not enough 
for design. The most viable way is to really get in touch with the users and understand their 
needs. 
 
8.5 User interface evaluation 
As for the research question “How could the resulting user interfaces be evaluated regarding 
the user experience?”, several design methods were applied during the evaluation of the 
dashboard design. After the prototypes were completed, two focus group interviews were held 
and the design was presented to the therapists at Schön Klinik and Glostrup hospital to get their 
opinions. As the therapists are real users, their comments were very specific and constructive. 
Based on their suggestions, the design has been optimized and iterated. The final dashboards 
were then presented to the Syncsense founding team through a stakeholder interview to get 
feedback from their perspective. A survey of patients at the Roskilde golf club was also carried 
out and their feedback was gathered through a questionnaire. There is no doubt that these are 
good design methods of evaluating the user interface. Whether it is design or validation, it is 
always useful to keep everything user-centered.

9 Conclusion 
This thesis aimed to design interactive dashboards for therapists and patients with respect to 
optimal user experience during rehabilitation. Finally, the research questions were answered by 
creating a design solution that took the needs of the users including therapists, stroke patients 
and the 3rd-party people, into consideration. 
 
The design followed the principles of user-centered design, where the real needs of the user 
have been put in the first position of consideration. And the design process was informed by 
design thinking. The final design allows the therapist to better monitor the patient's physical 
conditions during stroke rehabilitation, to see the range of motion in a 3D heatmap combined 
with a digital twin, and to view the patient’s progress. The dashboards also help the therapist to 
carry out weekly assessments of the patient and make settings to the exoskeleton device. 
Feedback sessions reviewed that therapists said the resulting dashboards were helpful for their 
daily work. Through patient dashboards, stroke patients can use the high usability dashboards 
for their daily training. Many of elements are designed to be motivated. For 3rd-party people, 
the 3rd-party dashboards allow them to check on the patient's progress and training schedule 
efficiently. 
 
Generally speaking, it is hoped that this design solution will help improve the users’ experience 
during stroke rehabilitation, increase the motivation of stroke patients in their rehabilitation 
process and help them to better recover from the stroke. However, the focus of this thesis is 
strongly on the therapist dashboards, the patient dashboards and the 3rd-party dashboards still 
need further elaboration and feedback.
