Abstract
This thesis examines how the Time Slotted Channel Hopping (TSCH) protocol can
be modified in order to further accommodate applications using mobile nodes. In the
context of mobility within TSCH networks the join process is particularly interesting,
as it has previously been shown that it is a very time and energy costly process, and
that mobile nodes will have to perform the process more frequently.
Thus, the main focus of this project is on the joining and re-joining processes of nodes,
where additional network topology data is propagated throughout the network in the
form of Information Elements appended to the the already existing Enhanced Beacon
mechanism. The topology data is used by joining and re-joining nodes to calculate
the channels of future Enhanced Beacon broadcasts as well as for a simple analysis of
the network schedule in order to more eﬀiciently receive Enhanced Beacons and thus
reduce the time and energy consumed by the process of joining a network.
Furthermore, in order to address the issue of clock drift and time de-synchronization
between mobile nodes and TSCH networks, new mechanisms are proposed and tested.
The modifications done to the mechanisms of the TSCH protocol are tested thoroughly
and the results are quantified and commented upon, in order to ultimately assess the
impact of the modifications.
The obtained results show that it is indeed possible to significantly reduce both the
time and energy spent on joining or re-joining a TSCH network. For the initial joining
process to a network the results show an average reduction in join time of approximately
50%, whereas for the re-joining process the results show an average reduction in re-
join time of upwards from 90%.
Lastly, the introduced mechanisms for clock drift
mitigation show that the solution can remain reliable for up to 12 minutes without
time re-synchronization, given a maximum clock drift of ±10 PPM.

Terminology
• Contiki-NG: An open-source operating system for IoT devices.
• Cooja: A simulation software able to simulate networks and interactions between
nodes.
• Drift: The pace in which two time sources are de-synchronizing, usually mea-
sured in PPM.
• Enhanced Beacon: A type of message in TSCH used to advertise the existence
of the network as well as join new nodes to the network.
• Information Element: An isolated and specific piece of information in an
Enhanced Beacon conforming to a format set by the IEEE 802.15.4e Standard.
• Join-priority/Rank: The hop count from any node to the coordinator-node of
the network.
• Mote: A simulated device in a Cooja simulation.
• Node: An individual device in a network, in this work specifically a TSCH
network.
• Shift: The total delta between two time sources.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
This chapter aims to familiarise the reader with the project and the environment in
which it was conducted. That is, provide the reader with a general understanding of
the project, why and how it was done as well as set the scene for the project and the
limitations herein.
1.1
Motivation
While Internet of Things is currently emerging and continues to gain more widespread
use in consumer markets with products such as Wearables and Smart Home articles,
it is also doing exactly so, just even more rapidly, away from public knowledge in the
industry. Industry 4.0 has ushered in and with it a new industrial revolution based
on the usage of cyber-physical systems interconnected through the Internet to become
part of the Internet of Things[9]. Many industries hereby see a great potential in utiliz-
ing the Internet of Things, e.g. to gather data and ultimately boost productivity and
gain. Examples hereof are Healthcare and Agriculture, both of which could utilize the
Internet of Things and sensors to gather all sorts of necessary and additionally relevant
data. In Agriculture some data is already being gathered manually for necessity, such
as testing the earth for appropriate mineral, chemical and water levels before deciding
to plant. With the use of sensors and the Internet of Things these manual probings
would no longer be necessary as the data could be gathered automatically and in much
larger sample sizes. Additionally, manned machines such as tractors and harvesters
could be controlled from afar or be made fully autonomous by specialized software.
This, however, still presents a hurdle in today’s Industrial Internet of Things. Since a
cyber-physical system can be connected to the Internet either by a wired connection or
wirelessly using e.g. WiFi, any IoT device will inherit the characteristics of the method
used to connect it to the Internet. Most notable among the characteristics is, that a
wired device will have highly reliable communication but low degree of freedom in phys-
ical movement due to being wired, whereas for a wireless device it is the exact opposite;
reduced communication reliability, due to interference and less sense of direction, but
a high degree of freedom in physical movement. Incidentally, devices employing a fully
wireless configuration in order to gain the freedom of physical movement must deal with
many more drawbacks stemming from their wireless nature, such as lower bandwidth
on the connection and being battery-driven, to name a few. It is, however, apparent
that for applications where physical movement in a larger area is necessary, such as
autonomous harvesters and tractors, a wired connection is not viable at all as the net-

1.2 Problem statement
2
work coverage would depend entirely on the length of the wire. In such applications
a wireless Internet connection is therefore an absolute necessity and the downsides it
brings are a necessary evil. There exist, nonetheless, communication protocols for the
network stack aiming to solve the issue of reliability in wireless communications. One
such protocol is called Time Slotted Channel Hopping and it is a MAC-Layer protocol
aiming to provide reliable wireless communication between low-power devices while si-
multaneously trying to maximize and share bandwidth equally between devices in the
networks. Furthermore, extensive research has shown that the TSCH protocol can in-
deed provide reliable wireless communication in small and large networks of low-power
devices alike, offering a good solution in terms of reliability, bandwidth and power con-
sumption for the area of Industrial Internet of Things[7]. Although this would indicate
that the TSCH protocol could be a good fit for the aforementioned agricultural appli-
cations, the research backing TSCH mostly concern application where devices remain
stationary throughout their lifetime. For applications where physical movement is nec-
essary research have shown that TSCH performs adequately, at best, if the amount of
other devices within coverage is high[16], and inadequately when the number of other
devices within coverage is low[15]. Specifically, the issue in such applications is that
the time taken for a device to join a TSCH network is unpredictable and theoretically
has no upper limit. The time taken depends on the amount of other devices within
coverage and the configuration of the network. However, despite ideal circumstances
the joining process to a TSCH network can take several minutes, which for low-power
autonomous devices puts a strain on battery-life and in most applications introduces
ineﬀiciency in the form of a significant period of time where the device must idle before
continuing. It would therefore seem that the TSCH protocol is a promising prospect
for movement-heavy applications utilizing low-power devices if the aforementioned is-
sues can be solved or lessened significantly. This project aims to assist in doing exactly
that. The downsides of the TSCH protocol will be explored, propositions for possible
solutions will be put forth and a proof-on-concept solution will be implemented and
tested in order to ultimately assess its viability, applicability and contribution to the

1.2
Problem statement
Currently the TSCH protocol has been proven to provide reliable wireless communi-
cation between devices, although only for applications utilizing primarily stationary
devices[7][16][15]. Furthermore, the TSCH protocol offers other benefits too such as
power-eﬀiciency as well as maximization and equal sharing of the available bandwidth.
These benefits are, however, lost to applications utilizing devices requiring physical
movement. This is due to the significantly time consuming joining process to a TSCH
network, which bodes ill for moving devices that will be leaving and entering the range
of the network continuously, which may result in several idle periods spent on joining
the network. Naturally, in order for devices to benefit from the services offered by
TSCH they must be joined to the network, meaning that enduring the taxing joining
process is inevitable. This presents a problem in movement-heavy applications effec-
tively lessening the benefits of the TSCH protocol, i.e. spending a long time joining the

2.1
Background
This section will explore the TSCH protocol as it is described by its standard as well

2.1.1
Time Slotted Channel Hopping
The TSCH protocol was introduced to the MAC section of the IEEE 802.15.4 standard
in 2012 in the IEEE 802.15.4e amendment to the aforementioned standard – later on
in 2015 it was added to the standard in succeeding versions. TSCH is a MAC-Layer
protocol aimed at providing a reliable MAC layer for reliable communication between
wireless devices, as seen by the upper layers of the network-stack. Furthermore, TSCH
targets only wireless devices, meaning that the MAC layer is a shared-medium network
where the shared-medium is the combination of radio bands and channels on which
communication is enabled. A common characteristic for all shared mediums is that
they serve more than one user at the same time, thereby being shared.
However,
since most channels can only handle transmission of data in a single direction at the
same time, it is crucial that a channel access method is effectively governing which
user may transmit at any given point in time.
TSCH combines frequency-division
multiple access and time-division multiple access mechanisms to allow several network
nodes to share the same radio medium by dividing the available bandwidth of the
medium into frequency sub-channels. Furthermore, TSCH allows nodes to share the
same frequency sub-channel by dividing access to the sub-channel into different time
slots and allocating individual time slots to individual nodes, respectively. Thus TSCH
divides the available bandwidth on a radio frequency by time and sub-channels and
generates a coherent schedule, a so-called slotframe, to which all nodes in the network
subscribe and adhere. An example of such a slotframe is shown in figure 2.1 which
shows a TSCH slotframe dividing the available bandwidth into 8 sub-channels and 4
time slots, resulting in 32 slots which can then be allocated to individual links, a link
==PAGE:25==
2.1 Background
9
being a permitted one-way transmission of data from a source to a destination node.
As it can be seen from the figure, some links have been allocated a slot such that a
node may transmit to another node in a specific time slot on a specific sub-channel, e.g.
in time slot 1 node 3 may transmit to node 4 on channel 7, if the channel range was 1
through 8. Thereby, TSCH governs medium access control by only allowing one node at
a time to transmit data on a specific sub-channel on any given time slot. Hence, TSCH
is essentially a channel access method for governing correct shared-medium access in
shared-medium networks such as any IoT network like e.g. a wireless sensor network.
Figure 2.1: Simplified example of a TSCH slotframe using 8 frequency channels and
4 time slots with some slots being allocated a link specifying a transmitter and receiver
node marked by ID.
While the slotframe is front and center in the operation of TSCH, several other mecha-
nisms are employed in order to generate the slotframe and facilitate the correct usage of
the schedule dictated by the slotframe. One such mechanism is for time-synchronization
between nodes of the network and ensuring that every node knows which is the current
time slot. Naturally, a slotframe cannot be infinitely long due to memory constraints,
therefore TSCH creates a slotframe with a fixed length of time slots and reuses this
very slotframe continuously. A time-keeping property called the Absolute Slot Number
(ASN) has therefore been introduced in the protocol in order for individual nodes to
keep track of and agree on which time slot is the current one at any given time. The
ASN is defined by the standard as the total number of timeslots that has elapsed since
the start of the network[10], i.e. it can be computed as follows:
ASN = N ∗Sl + Tc
(2.1)
where N is the current slotframe cycle starting from 0, Sl is the length of the slotframe
and Tc is the current time slot. The ASN is therefore used globally in the network
to keep track of time and increments with each passing of a time slot. It is, however,
not only used to synchronize nodes across the network. An important property of the
ASN is the fact that it is always the same across all nodes in the network and thus,
taking advantage of this, it is also used for calculating which sub-channel to transmit
or receive on in any given slot in the slotframe. Although oversimplified in figure 2.1
==PAGE:26==
2.1 Background
10
the slotframe does not specify the exact sub-channel for a link to use. A time slot
is instead divided into different channel offsets and globally in the network a channel
hopping sequence is agreed upon, thereby effectively making the slotframe agnostic to
the channels used by the network. In order to choose a sub-channel to transmit or
receive on at any given link in the slotframe, the channel offset from the slotframe
and the current ASN is used to calculate which sub-channel to use from the channel
hopping sequence, which in itself is merely a set of channels arranged in a sequence.
The specific sub-channel to use for a given link is calculated as follows1:
C = CHS[(ASN + Coffset)%CHSl]
(2.2)
where CHS is the channel hopping sequence, Coffset is the channel offset for a link
and CHSl is the length of the channel hopping sequence. The unique pairs of the ASN
and channel offset at any time slot will thereby result in different channels chosen from
the channel hopping sequence for each link in any one time slot. As the name of the
protocol implies, nodes in the network will therefore ”hop” or switch to a new channel
according to this equation every time they are part of a link in the schedule.
Other important mechanisms in TSCH are the generation and propagation of the slot-
frame as well as the joining and synchronization of new nodes in the network. A TSCH
network is initially created by a coordinator node which decides the channel hopping
sequence to be used. The coordinator node then begins following the schedule of the
network’s slotframe as well as broadcasting Enhanced Beacons (EBs), which contain
information about the network such as the current ASN and the join-priority/rank of
the node sending the EB, which is measured as the hop count from the receiver to the
coordinator of the network. Depending on the TSCH configuration, the EBs may also
contain information such as the channel hopping sequence and any network schedules,
in the form of slotframes and links. In TSCH this is called advertisement, since nodes
that are already part of the network will advertise the network by broadcasting EBs
for unfamiliar nodes to find. Advertisement may be part of the slotframe schedule, in
which case a slot is specifically allocated to an advertisement link for a specific node,
or it may be based on a max time period EBtimeout and a random element such that
any node will always at least broadcast and EB once every EBtimeout period. The
channel on which an EB is broadcast depends on the scheduler; if advertisement links
are allocated to a slot in the slotframe they, also have a channel offset according to
their slot, but if advertisement is purely based on a timeout, a channel offset value of
0 is generally used, as it is the case with the Minimal Scheduling Function[2]. Nodes
that are currently not part of a TSCH network, and are looking to join one, will be
scanning for EBs in order to find TSCH networks and possible parent-nodes to connect
to. Scanning nodes will be scanning for EBs while ”hopping” from channel to channel
randomly in a default configured channel hopping sequence. Upon receiving an EB
the scanning node will synchronize to the ASN from the EB and proceed to scan for a
configurable amount of time further, in case EBs from other possible parents may be
found. When the scanning node has exhausted the scan timeout it will join the TSCH
network by associating to the best parent-node found during scanning, based on the
1Equation adapted from page 17 in [10].
==PAGE:27==
2.1 Background
11
join-priority of all parent-nodes found; the lesser the join-priority the better the parent.
Association involves informing the chosen parent-node that the joining node is joining
the network and that it has chosen the parent-node as its parent, or direct connection,
as well as the construction of the slotframe locally in the joining node. How to go
about the construction of the slotframe, and thereby the schedule, is left answerable
to the specific implementation of the TSCH protocol. Thus the construction of the
slotframe can vary from implementation to implementation and be tailored for specific
performance needs. Furthermore, it can be either centralized or decentralized, mean-
ing that it can be either constructed by e.g. a coordinator-node and be broadcast to
the network in the EBs or it can be constructed locally on any joining node, based
on other information available about the network. Upon association the newly joined
node will, likewise, begin following the schedule of the slotframe as well as broadcasting
EBs. Naturally, in order for nodes to keep adhering to the schedule and continuously
transmit and receive data at the right time, they have to remain synchronized. Many
schedulers therefore instruct nodes to listen in advertisement slots of other nodes in
order to regularly receive EBs and be able to resynchronize their ASN in the case of
desynchronization. In this manner the nodes of the TSCH network are able to work to-
gether to join new nodes into the network and keep each other synchronized throughout
the lifetime of the network.
Combined, the mechanisms highlighted previously in this section, enable TSCH provide
the following properties for nodes in a TSCH network: reliable communication, low
power consumption and optimized usage of available bandwidth. Nodes in a TSCH
network are able to reliably communicate with each other, despite communicating
over a traditionally unreliable wireless shared-medium of radio frequencies. This is
due to the slotframe schedule preventing multiple transmissions of data simultaneously
on the same channel, thereby effectively reducing interference on the shared-medium.
Furthermore, due to the fact that the slotframe schedule is known to all nodes in
the network, nodes are able to turn off their radio and only turn it on and transmit
or receive data when scheduled to do so, thereby lowering the power consumption of
nodes. And lastly, for any given application using TSCH, the scheduler can be tailored
to the application’s needs in order to optimize the schedule, and thereby the available
bandwidth, for the application.
2.1.2
Contiki-NG
As described by the Contiki-NG project itself, Contiki-NG is an open-source, cross-
platform operating system for Next-Generation IoT devices[3]. The Contiki-NG OS
thus implements a stack of network protocols in order to support devices for various
IoT applications. Networks in IoT applications differ greatly in the protocols they opt
to use since each application presents different circumstances and goals. Contiki-NG
therefore aims to implement relevant protocols such that it may support the needs of
many applications and be a top choice in the area. Naturally, as Contiki-NG imple-
ments a slew of network protocols, many of which are not relevant for all applications,
the project also provides the possibility to configure which protocols to include in a
compilation of the OS. Additionally, since the platforms used as IoT devices are many
==PAGE:28==
2.2 Related work
12
and different, Contiki-NG also supports compilation into firmware for the most com-
monly used platforms. In the context of this project, Contiki-NG also implements the
TSCH protocol in its network stack, and in that area it is considered as one of the top
choices. Furthermore, Contiki-NG comes equipped with the Cooja Simulator for simu-
lating networks with nodes running the OS. For this project Contiki-NG has therefore
been used as the baseline for the ideas and changes implemented.
It should be noted, however, that Contiki-NG implements the TSCH protocol slightly
differently to what is described by the IEEE 802.15.4e Standard. The difference is
primarily seen in the implementation of the scanning-process of the protocol.
The
TSCH Standard describes that a node seeking to join a network must scan at least
for a set amount of time or until a set amount of nodes have been discovered, before
it should associate to the most suitable parent-node found. This is included in the
TSCH Standard to ensure that new nodes at least try to find the best parent-node to
associate to, although not by all means, with the aim that it will generally result in
better network connections where the hop-count between node and coordinator is as
low as possible. On the contrary, Contiki-NG’s implementation of the TSCH scanning-
process will skip the timeout, for which to scan as a minimum, and always associate
to the first parent-node discovered.

Related work
This section will explore relevant related work to this project to establish a knowledge
of the state of the art regarding the TSCH protocol and mobility. Related work includes
research papers analyzing and assessing the impact of mobile nodes in TSCH networks
as well as proposed changes to the TSCH protocol in order to improve performance of
TSCH in mobile applications. Hence, it will explore and consider previously conducted
research related to the area of mobility in TSCH.
Impact of mobility on the IoT MAC infrastructure: IEEE 802.15.4e TSCH
and LLDN platform
In [14] the authors compare the TSCH and LLDN protocols regarding what impact
mobile nodes have on the performance of the network. They focus especially on the
process of association and disassociation to and from the network, which they show
incur significant overhead and communication downtime for mobile nodes, due to the
fact that mobile nodes will disassociate more often and therefore need to associate once
more when in range. The authors demonstrate that the association, and re-association,
process is costly in both energy and time and that an increase in channels used by
the protocol also increases these costs accordingly, as nodes will generally have to scan
more channels and longer before EBs are found and association can be done. These
results are produced by simulating networks with different configurations. The authors
also argue that changes to the scheduling of EBs would be able to alleviate the costly
process of synchronizing to the network. The argument is made that fixing the schedul-
ing of EBs to a single channel will lessen the impact of the aforementioned costs, as
==PAGE:29==
2.2 Related work
13
nodes will know in advance which channel to scan on, thereby significantly reducing
the time to find an EB and simultaneously also the energy consumption of the scanning
process. This proposition, however, also comes with its downsides such as increased
risk of interference on the channel assigned to EB broadcasts.
Mobility Aware Framework for Time-slotted Channel Hopping IEEE 802.15.4e
Sensor Networks
The same authors, of the work mentioned above, have also developed a framework
for TSCH, described in [13], which aims to shorten the joining process to a TSCH
network.
The framework is called Mobile TSCH (MTSCH) and it works by utiliz-
ing passive beacons for advertisement of the network instead of the tradional EBs
of TSCH. The passive beacons are ACK messages transmitted on a fixed channel by
the coordinator-node, meaning that nodes seeking to join only has to scan a single
channel rather than multiple channels blindly. The result is that nodes receive these
synchronization messages faster compared to in TSCH, effectively reducing the power
consumption and time-cost introduced by the TSCH joining process. The authors also
evaluate the MTSCH framework using simulation and find, summarily, that it is able
to reduce the radio duty cycle of mobile nodes, on average, in the range between 7%
and 50% when using between 6 and 15 nodes respectively. Furthermore, they also
show that they are able to increase the total connectivity time per node between 10%
and 50% or between 3% and 50% depending on the effective communication range of
the nodes.
Schedulers
Another approach to improving the performance of a TSCH network is adapting the
schedule to be favorable for the application. On the subject of TSCH schedulers lots
of research and propositions have been made towards reaching an optimal scheduler
for most general applications. The IETF has in RFC 9033[2] described the 6TiSCH
Minimal Scheduling Function (MSF), which is the standard scheduler used in many
TSCH implementations including Contiki-NG. Currently though, the general notion
in the TSCH community is that schedulers such as Orchestra[6] and Alice[11] are the
golden standard for network performance, at least in static networks, and they have
also been shown to perform better than MSF in certain applications in [15]. There
have, however, also been made attempts at schedulers specifically designed for mobile
nodes. An example of such a scheduler is Instant which have been proposed and de-
scribed in [8]. Generally speaking, Orchestra proves to improve network performance
on many parameters over MSF[6] and Alice claims to improve on all parameters com-
pared to Orchestra[11] for general application purposes. Instant, on the other hand,
also shows a significant improvement over an optimized Orchestra implementation,
although specifically for mobile nodes[8].
As is immediately evident, from the amount of proposed schedulers and their claims
of improvement over one another, is that a lot of work have already been done in
the area of TSCH schedulers. This is warranted since the schedule is arguably one
==PAGE:30==
2.2 Related work
14
of the most impactful factors of network performance, but it is also partly the reason
for the focus of this project not being on scheduling. Although a lot of research have
studied and proposed different scheduling mechanisms, not much work have been done
on improving the scanning- and joining process of nodes to the TSCH network, which
is the focus in this project.
A Reliability Analysis of TSCH Protocol in a Mobile Scenario
Another work in [16] studies the impact of mobile nodes on network performance in
multiple scenarios. One scenario is what they call a typical industrial deployment[16]
where the network space is fully covered in static nodes and mobile nodes roam around
freely, and another is where the network space is fully covered by mobile nodes. The
authors focus on the joining and synchronization process of nodes to the network and
primarily measure performance based on the parameters downtime and energy con-
sumption. In order to gather results the test-scenarios are simulated using the Cooja
Simulator and the results are compared to a standard TSCH network having only
static nodes. The study finds that in networks where all nodes are mobile, and the
space is fully covered, the behavior and performance of the mobile nodes are similar
to that of a network consisting of only static nodes. That is, their results show that
power consumption and downtime did not deviate significantly from a standard net-
work. For the scenario where mobile nodes roam around static nodes, the study finds
similar results. The amount of mobile nodes does not impact the downtime of the
network and power consumption of static and mobile nodes are the same. The authors
therefore conclude that node mobility presents an insignificant impact on the joining
process to a TSCH network in scenarios where the network space is either fully covered
by static nodes or where the network contains a suﬀicient amount of mobile nodes to
guarantee consistent coverage. Whereas the conclusions of this study heavily rely on
a closed network space where mobile nodes are consistently within coverage of other
nodes and cannot realistically deviate from the network, this project focuses on the
joining and synchronization process in a much more general sense. This project aims
not to demonstrate an improvement for specific network topologies and settings but to
improve the joining process regardless of the topology and settings used.
TSCH Evaluation under Mobile Scenarios
Yet another and more recent study in [15] has explored the impact of node mobil-
ity on the performance of TSCH in two scenarios representing different applications.
Contrary to the study in [16] this study does not explore TSCH performance in a net-
work space fully covered with nodes. Instead, the network space is sparsely populated
with only the nodes necessary to fulfill the goal of the application, and nodes may move
out of range of other nodes. The two scenarios examined represents an autonomous
agricultural application and a smart warehouse application. In the agricultural sce-
nario nodes are moving in a predefined and predictive pattern and in the warehouse
application nodes are moving randomly in a given space. This study also simulates
the scenarios using the Cooja Simulator in order to gather results, and the focus pa-
rameters for the study is the overall downtime of nodes in the network, the percentage
==PAGE:31==
2.2 Related work
15
of all transmitted packets that are also received as well as the initial join time of all
nodes in the network. The two scenarios are simulated using different state of the art
schedulers and node counts and the results are compared. Regarding the parameter of
percentage of packets received, the study finds that this metric is generally very low
in all scenarios and that TSCH cannot be provide a reliable and robust performance
in a mobile scenario[15]. On the matter of downtime, the study finds all scenarios
and schedulers to result in a downtime close to 0, but with a measurable increase in
downtime in the agricultural application, where one node purposely moves out of range.
This indicates that the size of the network space used for the tests generally allows the
nodes to be within coverage of each other and that if node were to be more frequently
move out of range for longer periods, the total downtime in the network would increase
substantially. Finally, with respect to the initial join time metric, the study argues
that in mobile scenarios it is especially important for the initial join time to be as low as
possible, since nodes may be expected to perform tasks on the outskirts of the network
and deliver data or update its instructions when moving close to a base station. In
such a case this means that the window of opportunity might be too small, and a high
initial join time could therefore severely impact said node’s ability to deliver its data
to the system or receive new instructions. The study generally observes a very high
initial join time for the warehouse application and a much lower initial join time for
the agricultural application. The authors conclude that the current state of the TSCH
protocol yields inadequate performance in mobile applications, due to the fact that
mobile applications differ greatly in their setting and that the observed performance
vary greatly depending on mobility pattern of the application. While some mobility
patterns may show favourable results, others may be in the other end of the spectrum,
but ultimately the results should be compared to the requirements for the application.
Therefore, since many mobile applications will require nodes to move out of range
regularly, and that this would incur another join time period for a node entering the
network later on in time, the current approach of the TSCH protocol is best utilized
in application spaces where the risk of nodes moving out of range is low.
Reliable Mobility in Industrial IoT Networks
Finally, a recent work studying TSCH in a mobile setting is the Master Thesis in
[18] aiming to improve the process of re-joining a mobile node to a TSCH network.
That is, it does not consider the initial scanning- and joining process of TSCH nodes,
but only the scanning- and joining process of nodes that were joined to and have re-
cently disassociated from the TSCH network. The author aims to improve this process
by retaining information about the TSCH network even after disassociating from it,
such that it may be used by a mobile node to re-join more quickly when within range of
the network once again. Specifically, this is done by retaining the ASN of the network
at the time of disassociation and begin an ASN tracking process to keep the node’s
local ASN time-synchronized to the network’s ASN.
Using the ASN along with a timestamp of when an Enhanced Beacon was last received,
the author uses the average EB period (14 seconds) to estimate how many EBs have
been broadcast since the last received EB, as well as when the next EB will be broadcast.

Using the ASN number for the next estimated EB broadcast, this solution will scan
continuously on the corresponding channel in order to capture the EB packet. However,
this solution relies heavily on the ability to accurately predict the ASN of the next EB,
but as the delta between EBs is inherently random between 12 and 16 seconds and
the reliability of the estimate thus decreases with time, this method is suspected to be

However, the results presented by the author shows a slight performance increase. This

3.1.1
Versatility
Individual solutions may focus on different aspects of the TSCH protocol and may be
intended for specific node types, network topologies and/or use cases. The versatility
property is an evaluation of how specific or general a network or use case may be in
order to gain the benefits of the given solution. I.e. if a solution only benefits particular
networks and/or particular use cases, the solution will score low in versatility, compared
to a solution where the benefits apply more generally across the integral functions of
the protocol, or in a broader range of network topologies or use cases.
3.1.2
Communication downtime
Some systems may require communication channels to be established quickly in order
to communicate time-critical data. Thus, it is a favourable trait of a network if nodes
generally have fewer and shorter periods of downtime, meaning spans of time where
they are unable to communicate with the coordinator of the network. The communi-
cation downtime property is thus an evaluation of how a proposed solution will impact
the downtime of nodes in a TSCH network; a low score meaning that nodes generally
will have more and longer downtime periods, and a high score meaning that nodes
generally will have fewer and shorter periods of downtime.
3.1.3
Data overhead
A solution may have a positive or negative impact on the data overhead of a network
given a specific network topology, condition or configuration. For example, if the so-
lution adds a large overhead to all communication, the solution’s score on the data
overhead property will be lower compared to a solution with a smaller overhead. How-
ever, the data overhead property can also be affected by other factors depending on
the use case. Hence, the data overhead property essentially evaluates solutions based
on how fast the necessary data is generally transmitted and received between nodes
in the network, thereby rewarding solutions that transmit less data and solutions that
transmit data faster and more reliably.

3.1.4
Energy eﬀiciency
Variations of the TSCH protocol may affect the power consumption of the individual
nodes in the network, whether this variation be on a configurational or implementa-

affect this property independently and thus specific use cases and network topologies
may have a great impact on the power consumption. Therefore this property is not
easily quantifiable at this stage, meaning that generalizations and assumptions must
be made in order to compare and evaluate this property between solutions. Essen-
tially, the Energy eﬀiciency property evaluates the impact a given solution has on the
power consumption of individual nodes in the network. Specifically, a low score in this
property is given to solutions that are thought to increase the power consumption and
a high score is given to those that are thought to decrease the power consumption,
relative to the TSCH protocol as described by the IEEE 802.15.4e-2012 Standard.

Feasibility
The feasibility property is a subjective estimation of the feasibility of a proposed so-

mechanisms of the solution work as intended. For example, if a solution relies on unrea-
sonably complex computations or unrealistically precise measurements, the feasibility
of said solution should be thoroughly discussed and evaluated before one attempts to
implement it, since a half-done implementation yields few useful results. The feasibil-
ity property thus evaluates if a solution is actually realistically possible to implement
and test successfully within the time frame given to the project. A low score in this
property would therefore indicate that a solution is thought to be complex and/or
time consuming to implement, whereas a high score would signal that a solution is less
complex and/or less time consuming to implement.
3.2
Proposed solutions
Taking a closer look at the TSCH protocol it is clear that the process of joining a
network is very ineﬀicient. A joining node will listen for EBs from nodes in a network
while constantly switching between random channels. Depending on the network size
and the timing of the EB broadcasts, it may take several minutes for the joining node
to capture an EB. Furthermore, the joining node should listen for multiple EBs in
order to select the best parent, interrupted only by a set timeout after which the
node will associate to the best known parent. This means that the energy-consuming
radio is activated constantly during the entire joining process and that more suitable
parent-nodes may be overheard.
In order to introduce mobility into the TSCH protocol this project therefore focus on
the joining-process of the protocol.
==PAGE:36==
3.2 Proposed solutions
20
If a mobile node relocate out of range of the network it must disassociate from its
current parent and re-associate to a new parent-node when within range of one again.
If it is possible to reduce the time and energy required for a node to join a network, the
benefits will therefore be more significant for mobile nodes, as these will be expected to
disassociate from their parents more often than static nodes. For the sake of simplicity
the general context is assumed to be networks where a single node is mobile while the
coordinator and other nodes are stationary.
The following subsections will explore different proposed solutions thought up during
the project. Common for all of them is that they aim to improve the process of joining
a node to a TSCH network, regardless of it being by the time spent joining or some
other factor like the power consumed.
3.2.1
Fixed EB broadcast channel
In order to decrease the number of missed EBs during the scanning phase of the TSCH
protocol, one could change the pseudo-random order of channels on which the beacons
are broadcast. For example, a single fixed channel could be selected for EB broad-
casts, in which case a scanning node would never miss a beacon due to listening on
another channel. This is illustrated in figure 3.2 which shows the available slots for EB
broadcasts colored in blue, meaning that only blue slots may be used for broadcasts
but are also available for transmission and receiving links. This solution is expected
to decrease the duration of the scanning phase significantly and thus in turn slightly
decrease power consumption and communication downtown. Therefore, those proper-
ties all score positively. Furthermore, this solution is very feasible, however, it also
introduces severe disadvantages regarding channel redundancy. If the amount of in-
terference on the selected broadcast channel is too high to reliably communicate, it
effectively prevents new nodes from joining the network and the existing nodes from
re-synchronizing. I.e. the entire network becomes completely dependant on a single
network channel. This would be a direct contradiction to the extensive measures TSCH
takes in order to ensure channel redundancy and this results in the solution receiving
a very low versatility score, as it would only be performant in select network settings
and scenarios.

3.2.2
Post association evaluation
A way to improve the joining-process could be to associate to the node of the first EB
observed. This would decrease the joining-period by up to the set amount of time for
which a node should scan for alternative parent nodes. However, it would also likely
result in poorly constructed networks with nodes being joined to sub-optimal parents
compared to the join-priority of other nearby nodes, which were just not discovered
first.
In order to alleviate this issue, the joining node could continue to scan for EBs even after
associating, and switch its parent-node in case a more suitable node was discovered.
This way the joining node would be able to transmit data during the scanning timeout,
while searching for a better parent node. This solution would decrease communication
downtown and would therefore score positively on this property. However, depending
on the implementation, the node may not be able to eﬀiciently listen for EBs and
communicate with its parent simultaneously, due to its duties of maintaining network
functionality according to the schedule.
Some implementations of TSCH may also
implement distributed scheduling, where the timeframe schedule is agreed upon locally
by neighboring nodes. In this case the joining node would not be able to predict when
and where other branches of the network might broadcast an EB. Thus the node would
either suffer an increased risk of missing beacons or neglect the communication with
the temporary parent in favor of constantly listening for EBs. While issues like this
is only present in some implementations of TSCH, it does count negatively for the
versatility of the solution. Furthermore, some TSCH scheduler implementations are
centralized, meaning that the schedule is generated by a central participant in the
network and propagated to member-nodes through EBs. In implementations using
such schedulers this solution would result in a negative scoring on the data overhead
property, due to the fact that each time a node would find a better parent after joining
the network, the schedule might need to be re-generated and updated at the member-
nodes. This increases the amount of information which has to be relayed between the

nodes throughout the network. Additionally, this is further negatively impacted by the
possibility that a node switching parent may also result in its child-nodes switching
to a different parent-node, effectively causing a chain-reaction of parent switches, also
known as hysteria. Finally, the successful implementation of this solution is regarded
as being quite feasible and therefore scores positively in this property.
3.2.3
EB channel prediction
Another approach one might take to improve the joining-process is being able to predict
when and where the next EB will be broadcast.
The general idea of EB channel
prediction is that if the joining node can predict which channel the next EB will be
broadcast on, it can make sure to be listening on that channel only. If the joining
node is within range of another node and the channel is free from interference, the
joining node can be certain to obtain the EB. Additionally, nodes could also be able to
predict when the next EB might be broadcast. Thereby a node would be able to direct
its scanning to select channels and timeslots where an EB could be broadcast. Both
of these capabilities, however, are dependent upon some information about the target
network. In order to predict the channel of a slotframe timeslot, on which an EB might
be broadcast, the node must know the ASN and channel offset of the slot, as well as
the channel hopping sequence used by the network. Also, regarding the prediction of
when an EB could be broadcast, the node must also know the schedule of the network.
This means that EB channel prediction is only possible after the joining node has
received the necessary information about the network, all of which can be included in
an EB. Nonetheless, after receiving an EB this solution would improve the performance
of the remainder of the joining-process significantly and would, additionally, also be
applicable for re-joining a node to the network after disassociation, which is a common
problem for mobile nodes specifically.
Being able to reliably predict the broadcast channels of EBs of the target network
should reduce the joining time significantly, as the number of EBs missed would be
reduced by a large margin during scanning. This solution therefore scores positively
in versatility for all types of networks. Networks with stationary nodes will experience
a faster join time and especially mobile nodes will reap the benefits of this solution, as
they are more likely to disassociate and re-associate and could therefore benefit from
the shortened join time several times.
The solution is also expected to significantly reduce communication downtime for mo-
bile nodes changing position in the network, as the scanning process is expected to be
shorter in most cases. The solution therefore also scores positively in the communica-
tion downtown property.
Considering that the solution would make it much faster for joining nodes to discover
more possible parent-nodes, it would make sense to maximize the amount of parent-
nodes discovered, in order to be able to make a qualified selection of a parent among
a larger pool of options. To enable this in the scanning process the EB of the TSCH
protocol could be extended to include information about the network topology, such

as how many nodes are currently in the network. Scanning nodes would thereby know
how many possible parent-nodes are part of the network and might be able to assess
whether to scan for a longer or shorter period of time. Regarding the data overhead
property this solution is therefore expected to have a slightly negative impact because
EBs may include more data to be transmitted in the network. However, it could lead
to the solution resulting in the creation of stronger networks, as nodes are expected to
evaluate more parent-nodes and thus are more likely to select a better candidate. The
solution may in turn also result in less data-forwarding among branches in the network
and fewer packages lost during transmission, due to poor connections between parent
and child nodes.
Finally, the solution is expected to affect the energy eﬀiciency property positively as
it would reduce power consumption by multiple means. Firstly, since the scanning
process will be shortened, nodes will in turn use less power and secondly, since it will
be possible to predict on which timeslots EBs could be broadcast, scanning nodes will
be able to turn off their radio on all irrelevant timeslots.
All thing considered this solution scores slightly negatively in feasibility since, despite
the individual components of the solution being feasible enough, it is composed of a
multitude of components that must all work and cooperate in order for the solution to
demonstrate its real improvement.
3.2.4
Physical location triangulation
The general proposal of physical location triangulation is that if a joining node can
determine where in the network it is located relative to the existing network nodes,
it can use these coordinates to select a subset of acceptable parents, thus being able
to evaluate the join-priority of nearby nodes against nodes which have not yet been
discovered – and if discovering a particularly nearby node, be able to stop the scanning
altogether, without otherwise meeting the criteria for parent selection. In other words,
this solution sets out to reduce the amount of network nodes to discover before a parent
can be decided upon. It does therefore not deal with how the joining node coordinates
timing in regards to communication with other nodes.

4.2
Enhanced Beacon timeslot & channel prediction
When initially listening for Enhanced Beacons during the joining process, a node will
have no information about the network and thus it has no way of knowing which chan-
nel to listen to, when to listen and even the timings of the timeslots. Furthermore, the
length of the slotframe is also unknown. Therefore the only way to discover Enhanced
Beacons is to blindly listen on random channels for a set amount of time before at-
tempting a new channel. This process is quite ineﬀicient as the radio must be on at
all times, even when no nodes in the network are broadcasting beacons. Additionally,
the majority of beacons will be missed, especially in networks where the number of
configured channels is higher, since the joining node can only listen on a single channel
at a time.
As the probability of aligning the scanning with the timeslot and channel offset of a
beacon broadcast does not increase with each missed beacon broadcast, (similar to the
probability of a coinflip resulting in heads not increasing if previous attempts failed)
there is no theoretical upper limit to how long the joining node may have to scan.
However, the average duration depends on how often nodes in the network transmit
Enhanced Beacons, how many network nodes are within range and how many channels
are configured. This behaviour is illustrated in figure 4.3, where an Enhanced Beacon
may be broadcast on any channel at any point in time.

network node and is able to synchronize to the ASN of the network. The joining node
can then predict which channel the network node would broadcast its Enhanced Beacon
on, if it were to broadcast in a given timeslot. While the joining node cannot predict
when the broadcast will occur, it can eliminate the risk of missing the broadcast due
to scanning on the wrong channel. While the Enhanced Beacon may still be lost, due
to interference or other external factors, it should significantly increase the probability
of receiving the Enhanced Beacon once it is transmitted. Such a scenario is illustrated

each slot is assigned to a specific communication link, e.g. between two specific nodes
(multiple links can be scheduled within a single timeslot). These can include advertise-

the slotframe schedule, it can determine a set of specific ASNs where said network
node may broadcast its Enhanced Beacon. For all other ASNs the joining node can

power. Such a scenario is illutrated in figure 4.5 where Enhanced Beacons can only be

4.3
Topology data
In order to share and distribute network topology data between nodes, the Enhanced
Beacons of the TSCH protocol were extended to include this information as a MLME
Vendor Specific Nested Information Element1. This type of Information Element was
used since it, as the name implies, allows for any vendor specific information to be
serialized and added as an IE to the EB. It is therefore also up to the vendor, which
in this project is we the authors, to make sure that any such information is parsed
correctly, such that it is meaningful on the receivers end. Hence, adding information
about the network topology to the EBs meant obtaining the data, serializing it in the
format conforming to an MLME Vendor Specific Nested IE, adding it to the EBs and
parsing it on the receiving end when detecting the presence of this specific MLME IE
type.
The benefit of using the Enhanced Beacons to transmit the topology data is that En-
hanced Beacons are already an integrated mechanism of all implementations of TSCH,
thus requiring no modification to the transmission and routing of EBs, if this solution
were to be adapted to other TSCH versions. Furthermore, this reduces the introduced
overhead significantly, as no additional timeslots must be dedicated to the topology
data transmission, but the transmission instead takes place in already allocated times-
lots for EBs. While overhead in respect to dedicated topology transmission timeslots
is avoided with this solution, it may still increase the interference in the network as
more data is transmitted during the EB timeslots. Also, while a receiving node will
keep its radio ON for the entire duration of a timeslot, a transmitting node will turn
off its radio after transmitting the scheduled package. Therefore, the topology data
introduces a slight energy consumption overhead, as the receiving node must process
more data and the transmitting node must transmit more data before its radio can be
turned off.
The scheme, however, works such that when a node broadcasts an Enhanced Beacon,
it will also attach its own knowledge of the network topology as an array of the data
structure displayed in listing 4.1. Just before broadcasting, the node will make sure to
update the entry in the topology data concerning itself. This structure must contain
various relevant node information depending on the TSCH version and implementation,
e.g. channel offset. In the current implementation each node in the topology requires
12 bytes of memory and space in an IE packet. As the maximum payload of a MLME
Long IE is 255 bytes, each topology IE in an Enhanced Beacon package can therefore
contain 20 topology node entries, as some storage must be reserved for management
overhead.
As mentioned, any data put into an IE must be serialized into a 1D-array of bytes. In
the case of the topology data, its organization in the MLME Vendor Specific Nested
IE is illustrated in figure 4.6.

network to continuously communicate changes to the ASN is unrealistic, the joining
node must be able to track the network ASN locally to a certain precision determined
by the guard times of the timeslots.

4.4.2
Shifted timeslot scanning
Even though drift correction has been implemented in the ASN tracking, this solution
requires reliable timing synchronization with multiple nodes at once. Thus, the joining
node may be drifting relative to its most recent time-source neighbor - which in turn
may be drifting from other network nodes.
For example: A network node A has synchronized its time source to coordinator node
B 10 seconds ago, but has since been drifting relative to coordinator B by X PPM. It
then broadcasts an Enhanced Beacon which is received by joining node C. The joining
node C now synchronizes its timesource to that of node A, which is shifted relative
to the coordinator B. Node C itself is drifting by Y PPM, so when node C tries
to listen for the Enhanced Beacon of node B 15 seconds later, the total shift equals
10s ∗X + 15s ∗Y , where X and Y are the fractions by which the respective nodes are
drifting.
There may be multiple sources of drift, and for the purpose of this application the
drift direction is not irrelevant. If the shift exceeds the guard times of a timeslot it
is significantly safer to experience positive shift rather than negative shift. In figure
4.8 we see that even though the time source of the joining node had shifted beyond
what the guard time could absorb, the Enhanced Beacon transmission was still received
because the transmitting node inexplicably delayed the beginning of the transmission
and thus no data was lost.

4.5
Re-joining after disassociation
As the solution proposed in this project can only improve the joining process once it
has obtained network information, it is still ineﬀicient for mobile nodes to discover
the first Enhanced Beacon.
It is expected that in an application utilizing TSCH
and mobility, nodes will disassociate from the network much more often than other-
wise. Thus, if a mobile node can retain the network information once it disassociates,
it can further optimize its re-association process, as it is no longer required to ran-
domly scan for the first Enhanced Beacon. As the default implementation of TSCH
in Contiki-NG tracks the timing and value of the network ASN up until the point
of disassociation, the tsch_disassociate()9 method was modified to initialize the cus-
tom_asn to the last known value of the ASN upon disassociation. Furthermore, the
time_since_packet_pending variable was also set upon disassociation, in order to in-
dicate the last time the ASN was updated. Thus, the disassociated node will be able
to track the network ASN using the update_custom_asn() method immediately after
disassociating from the network, just as it is done after receiving the first EB as an
initially joining node.
This will allow recently disassociated nodes to scan more eﬀiciently for enhanced bea-
cons from the established network, and thus re-join the network faster, once re-inserted
into the network range.
4.6
Classic TSCH version
As previously mentioned in section 2.1.2, Contiki-NG implements the TSCH protocol
slightly differently than what the IEEE 802.15.4e Standard describes.
Therefore a
so-called Classic TSCH version has been developed, changing only the joining-process
in the Contiki-NG implementation to follow the description of the TSCH Standard.
Instead of always associating to a network node upon receiving the first EB, as in the
default implementation of Contiki-NG, it instead continues to scan for a set timeout
or until having discovered a set amount of distinct nodes through EBs. Only hereafter
will the node associate to the most suitable parent found, based on the join-priority of
the discovered nodes. Hence, in order to facilitate fair comparison between the TSCH
version developed during this project and a standard TSCH version, the Classic TSCH

CHAPTER 5
Results
This chapter aims to present and comment upon the results obtained from testing
the implementation of this project. Furthermore, it will also describe the methods
and approaches used to test the implementation, as well as how and what data was
gathered and how data was processed to produce the final results.
5.1
Evaluation
To ultimately validate the contributions made to the TSCH protocol, the altered ver-
sion produced during this project, which is documented in section 4, had to be tested
thoroughly. This was done in order to evaluate whether or not the changes did in fact
contribute positively to the protocol. The approach used to arrive at this end was a
stepwise process. Firstly, simulations of various network configurations of TSCH nodes
were carried out, each called a test in the following. Common for each test is that they
were conducted using both the classic TSCH version and the altered version. Secondly,
relevant data was collected from each test and processed into test results, demonstrat-
ing certain relevant performance properties of nodes in a TSCH network. Lastly, the
test results for identical tests were compared between the two TSCH versions, classic
and altered, in order to evaluate which version performed better in various network
configurations, situations and on different properties.
5.1.1
Cooja Simulator
To simulate network configurations and the interaction between TSCH nodes in a
network, the Cooja Simulator was used. The Cooja Simulator comes bundled with
the Contiki-NG OS source code as an optional tool, which one may use to test the

simulations, with the classic and final altered version, in order to gather the results
presented in this chapter.
In addition to using the barebones Cooja Simulator the Cooja Mobility Plugin was also
used to facilitate movement of TSCH nodes during simulations. This plugin enables
precise movement of individual nodes in both space and time during an ongoing sim-
ulation. In the context of this project it was used to simulate network environments

where nodes were suddenly added to the network, as well as environments where nodes

A Cooja simulation in this context consists of a set of nodes arranged in an arbitrary

5.1.2
Automated tests
Since networks are variable by nature and the performance of a network depend heavily
on several factors, such as timing and positioning, it was imperative that tests repre-
senting different network topologies were also carried out many times. This in order
to account for different timings in node startup-times and EB broadcasts. To ensure
that the variable nature of such factors was taken into account, identical tests were run
multiple times on different seeds, thereby resulting in different communication timings
between the test runs.
With many tests to run on different seeds and different TSCH versions, an automated
solution to run the tests was essential. For that purpose an automated simulation
script, simulation.py1, was developed. The script made it possible to run the same test
with several configurations on the aforementioned properties, seed and TSCH version,
autonomously for an arbitrary amount of tests and configurations. For any automated
test run, the script is configured with which test to run, which TSCH versions to test
and the amount of seeds to test on. The script then picks the configured number of
seeds at random and starts running the tests with the different configurations. For
each test the script updates the test file with the configuration to test on, runs the test
using Cooja’s headless run-configuration, logs the results and proceeds to configure and
run the next test configuration. This enables testing hundreds of test configurations
in a relatively short amount of time, contributing to more reliable results as they are
based upon many more tests.

5.1.4
Network topologies
In order to ensure uniform conditions for all tests a few well-defined network topologies
were designed. All used topologies therefore fit into two main categories: ellipse and
group. These two categories of topologies are shown in figure 5.1 with a total node
count of 6 and in both possible states; with the mobile node having moved in and out
of the network respectively. Commonly for both topology categories, as illustrated by
the figure, is that they form a circle with a variable number of nodes. It is important
to note that regardless of the number of stationary nodes in the circle, the radius of the
circle will never exceed the range of the mobile node. Although they look the same it
can be seen, albeit with a closer look, that the difference between the ellipse and group
topologies is the distance between the stationary nodes. This difference in distance
means that in the ellipse topology each network node is only within range of its two
neighboring nodes, whereas in the group topology all nodes are within range of each
other. The mobile node, however, is within range of all nodes in both topologies once
it has moved to the center of the network.
This makes the group topology better suited for re-joining scenarios as the stationary
nodes should always prioritize to associate to the coordinator, ensuring a more stable
network once the mobile node is moved out of range, as it should have no children.
Assuming all nodes, including the mobile node, associate to the coordinator, this also

5.2
Testing
As this project focuses on the association process of the TSCH protocol, tests were per-

The following subsections present for each performed testing scenario how the test was

5.2.1
Test: Optimal scanning timeout
As mentioned in section 5.1.5 the TSCH protocol can be configured to customize the
behaviour and properties of the nodes and network. A configurable variable which is
likely to have a large impact on the following tests is the MAX_EB_DELAY. This
variable determines the maximum amount of time a node will scan for possible parents,
after discovering the first potential parent. A larger MAX_EB_DELAY of a joining
node is likely to result in more parents discovered and thus a parent with a lower
rank/join-priority. As is also described in section 5.1.5 IETF recommends configuring
the MAX_EB_DELAY to 180 seconds when using the minimal mode of operation.

6.1
Result takeaways

6.1.1

focus of this project, the expectation in this regard also was to improve it considerably,

6.1.2
Power consumption
One of the expected benefits of the modified solution was the reduction of power
consumption, as mentioned in section 3.2.3. This expectation was tested in both an
initial joining scenario and a re-joining scenario in sections 5.2.4 and 5.2.6.
In the initial joining scenario the results showed that the implementation of the solution
did reduce the power consumption by roughly 50% compared to Classic TSCH, however,
only a small percentage of the total time spent joining the network was spent with the
radio turned off. Thus, the 50% improvement in power consumption did not stem from
the Custom node actively turning off the radio but from the reduced join-time of the
node. This was further showcased in figure 5.8 where the time spent scanning for the
first EB was illustrated as a portion of the total time spent joining. In the re-joining
scenario, on the other hand, a comparison of figures 5.9 and 5.10 showed that Custom
nodes only had their radio turned on for approximately 33% of the total time spent
==PAGE:81==
6.1 Result takeaways
65
re-joining on average, thereby benefiting greatly from the node actively turning off the
radio when there is no chance of an EB broadcast.
From the results provided it can be argued how much of an impact the solution actually
has directly on the power consumption of the TSCH protocol. Specifically for stationary
nodes, the direct improvement on power consumption by the mechanism for turning
off the radio when not needed, is negligible because they most likely will not have
to re-join the network ever. Considering mobile nodes, who will most likely have to
frequently re-join the network, the aforementioned mechanism will result in a positive
direct impact corresponding to approximately 66% reduced power consumption during
re-joining scenarios.
In addition, the Custom version will indirectly impact power
consumption positively for all node types due to the significantly shortened join- and
re-join time, which indirectly improves power consumption by approximately 50% and
92%, respectively. Still there is at least one mechanism in the Custom version which
negatively impacts the power consumption of the protocol by a currently undetermined
factor. This is the addition of network topology data in the EBs. Increasing the size
of an EB will in turn have a negative impact on the power consumption of an EB
transmission and reception, due to the node having to spend more time with the
radio on transmitting and receiving individual EBs. Of course, the TSCH protocol
already allows for the addition of several optional Information Elements in an EB,
containing information such as the channel hopping sequence or a number of slotframe
schedules1. Thus the size of an EB is already implementation- and network-specific
and the addition of yet another Information Element, in the form of network topology
data, might in fact present a negligible impact on power consumption. However, as
the effect has not been studied, the point still stands and the addition of the network
topology Information Element in the EBs therefore currently presents an unknown,
but surely negative, impact on the power consumption of the protocol.
6.1.3
Clock drift
In section 5.2.7 the impact of intentional shift2 was examined. The results in table
5.1 and table 5.2 show that the tolerance was increased by more than 1000%. This
is a very important feature in order to ensure reliability when using the proposed
mechanisms to re-join a network, as a re-join sequence will require drift tolerance of
at least 120 seconds – the time it takes a node to disassociate. However, the results
still show a reliability limit of roughly 12 minutes given a drift of ±10 PPM. This
means that any implementation of this solution should consider a time-threshold for
time-synchronization, after which the mobile node should revert to a standard blind
channel scan, as the reliability of the solution can no longer be guaranteed.
If no
threshold is implemented, the mobile node risks shifting its time-source more than the
tolerance of the solution, at which point the solution will effectively prevent the mobile
node from discovering EBs for a long period of time.

6.2
Obsolete topology data
As the tolerance for drift increases, a mobile node may leave the network for longer
periods, while still retaining the ability to quickly re-join. However, as the ability to
leave the network for longer periods improves, so does the risk of changes in the network
topology. Nodes may change their channel offset, their parent or time source. Local
schedules may change and other nodes may leave the network. These changes cannot
be communicated to any mobile node outside of the network’s coverage, and thus, once
the node once again is within range of the network, it may be storing obsolete topology
data. Depending on the situation this may decrease the re-join performance or entirely
prevent the node from re-joining using the topology data. In the latter case, the node
would eventually consider its topology data obsolete due to potential drift and revert
to random scanning.
6.3
TSCH Configuration
The configuration of the TSCH network has a significant impact on the performance of
mobile nodes. Thus one should consider the benefits and disadvantages of the relevant
settings, as different network use-cases may benefit from different settings.
Keep in mind the equations from section 6.1.1. These show how a reduced EBIavg, the
average EB broadcast frequency, could reduce the join time for mobile nodes. However,
for an established network this will increase power consumption and interference in the
network as nodes must transmit the EBs more often.
Likewise, configuring the channel hopping sequence to a smaller amount of available
channels will improve the join time of new nodes, but in turn result in fewer available
communication slots for each ASN. This would reduce the available bandwidth and
increase interference - especially in dense networks.
However, as seen in figure 5.3
and equation 6.2, a unique trait of the Custom implementation is that the number of
parents considered is not dependant on the number of channels configured.
Finally, Dmax or MAX_EB_DELAY can be configured to achieve a similar effect
as the aforementioned settings.
The disadvantage, however, would be that joining
nodes would consider fewer parents before associating, as is shown in figure 5.2. Using
the Custom implementation, this would in turn result in less optimized networks if
configured with a value less than 16 seconds.
Hence, as it is apparent from the aforementioned configuration-decisions, every config-
urable trait of the TSCH protocol is essentially a trade-off. It is therefore crucial to
configure TSCH wisely and target the configuration for the intended application by
evaluating what is important and what is not.

6.4 Exclusion as a parent
67
6.4
Exclusion as a parent
In order to improve the general stability of the network, it may be beneficial to create
a tag for mobile nodes in the topology data, such that they are recognizable to other
nodes when broadcasting their EBs. One could argue that a mobile node would serve
as a very unreliable parent for any other node, due to it potentially moving out of
range. Therefore, if joining nodes were able to identify which of the potential parents
were mobile, they would be able to select another parent. Of course this could also be
reflected in a rank-multiplier for mobile nodes, such that the joining nodes could make
a more nuanced decision. Generally, these considerations are likely influenced by the
use case of the specific network and its nodes. However, these are solely speculations
and no tests have been conducted during this project that could show the impact on
network stability and reliability of using mobile nodes as parents.
6.5
Constraints
6.5.1
Topology data size
While keeping track of the network topology provides significant benefits to the im-
plemented solution, it is not without drawbacks or challenges. Most IoT platforms
are for example extremely resource constrained and thus their memory must be used
sparsely and wisely. When the network grows, it increases the demand for allocated
memory on all nodes in the network, currently by 12 bytes per node. Furthermore, the
amount of data which can be transmitted with a single Enhanced Beacon is dependent
on timeslot length, guard-times, data rate and more, but is in all cases finite. As there
is no defined limit on the size of TSCH networks, the implemented solution will inher-
ently need some modification in order to accommodate large networks. In section 7.1
a solution for this problem has been proposed.
However, if the implemented TSCH network is simple enough to use a constant slot-
frame with locked broadcast slots, a constant broadcast channel offset and a constant
channel-set, the topology information is not strictly necessary for a joining node to ob-
tain the vast majority of the benefits provided by the solution. Instead these variables
and configurations should be hard-coded into the firmware of the nodes in the network,
including mobile nodes. In such a network the added overhead of the topology tracking
likely outweighs the additional benefits which topology information still provides.
6.5.2
Local schedules and multiple channel offsets
It is argued that the solution presented in this report is relatively versatile - meaning
that it can be useful for many differently configured TSCH-networks in various use-
cases. While this is still considered to be true, the solution is not particularly versatile
with respect to portability. The solution currently needs to be modified to support any
specific scheduler, such as Orchestra or Alice, in order to correctly identify in which
slots of a slotframe an EB may be broadcast.
==PAGE:84==
6.6 The Cooja simulator
68
Furthermore, if the network is not configured with a global channel offset, channel set
and scheduler, it may require a more sophisticated scanning-mechanism. For example,
if the topology data shows that one third of all network nodes have channel offset
1, and two thirds have channel offset 0, a joining node cannot listen for EBs from
both groups simultaneously, since they could broadcast in the same timeslots, albeit
on different channels. Thus the joining node must either prioritize one offset or scan
using alternating offsets. This may cause significant changes to the performance of the
solution, but has yet to be evaluated, as support for schedulers other than minimal
schedule, has yet to be implemented. A potential solution for these issues is further
discussed in section 7.3.
6.6
The Cooja simulator
As mentioned in section 1.3.1.1, various problems were encountered while using the
Cooja simulator. This in turn raises the question of the reliability of simulations in
Cooja, compared to a real-world context, and the trustworthiness of results obtained
from using it. However, as the Cooja simulator is a widely used and accepted tool
within the WSN/IoT industry[17], the results of the simulations are deemed relatively
trustworthy.

CHAPTER 7
Future Work
7.1
Managing large topologies
As discussed in section 6.5.1 the current implementation does not account for the
possibility of topologies larger than what the resource constrained nodes can practically
store in memory or communicate to neighbors. A suggested solution to this issue is
to remove information from the local topology dataset when resource limits are met.
In essence, if a node can only allocate memory for 20 nodes in the local topology, it
should discard nodes from the topology when receiving information about more than 20
nodes. A suggestion is to implement a jump-counter which counts how many nodes the
topology information has passed through, and to discard nodes with a high jump-count,
i.e. to discard nodes which are distant in the topology structure. While it cannot be
guaranteed, it is expected that nodes that are topologically distant (high jump-counter)
are likely to be physically distant as well. This will naturally result in an incomplete
local topology for any particular node, and potentially also nearby nodes relying on
information from this node. However, it is assumed that given a realistic configuration,
the topology information required to eﬀiciently join a network is significantly less than
what the typically resource limited IoT platforms can handle.

7.2 When to abandon ASN tracking in re-join scenarios
70
7.2
When to abandon ASN tracking in re-join scenarios
While it has been shown in section 5.2.7 that this solution improves the tolerance for
clock drift, reliability is still an important issue. Thus, any node leaving the network
should have a defined maximum duration without re-synchronization, for which to rely
on the ASN-based channel prediction. After this duration the node should regard the
mechanism as non-reliable and abandon any reliance on it. If the issue of unreliable
channel prediction is not addressed, the joining node risks de-synchronization between
its ASN and the network ASN, and thus will always scan for EBs in timeslots where
none are scheduled. Once the potential total drift of the joining node reaches a level
where accurate ASN-timings can no longer be guaranteed, the node should revert to
scanning randomly for EBs in order to ensure reliability. A similar feature is currently
implemented in TSCH, used for determining when a node should disassociate from the
network, and from which inspiration could be drawn.
7.3
Support of various TSCH schedulers
As mentioned in section 6.5.2, this solution suffers the issue of being very dependant on
the TSCH schedule implementation and thus requires modification in order to support
various schedulers. If this solution is to be used in a wider context, the implementation
should be able to automatically analyze the implemented schedule in order to deter-
mine for which ASNs an EB may be broadcast. This task may or may not be trivial
depending on the scheduler to be supported. E.g. it will probably be more challenging
for autonomous link-based schedulers such as the ALICE scheduler1.
7.3.1
Multiple channel offsets in topology
Section 6.5.2 mentions the issue of non-network-wide channel range and channel offset
configurations. While a simple implementation randomly selecting between the pos-
sible EB broadcast channels should be relatively easy to implement, this issue opens
up the possibility for more advanced network analysis. For example, if the network
does not contain an equal amount of nodes for each present offset, should there be
a bias towards scanning with the most densely represented offset? Perhaps a jump
counter, as discussed and illustrated in section 7.1, could be introduced. From this
the joining node could be able to estimate the offset densities of the nodes that were
likely to be nearby, based on the network location of a previously received EB. Fur-
thermore, it may also be relevant to re-evaluate the optimal setting for the scanning
timeout (MAX_EB_DELAY) if multiple channel offsets or schedules are present in
the network.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
Throughout this thesis multiple aspects of mobility within TSCH networks have been
examined. Multiple ideas and solution have been put forth, all aiming to increase the
performance of mobile nodes in TSCH networks in various aspects. Individual ideas
were selected for implementation and evaluation in order to scope the project.
The focus of the project has been on utilizing network data in order to improve the net-
work association process, by predicting channel offsets and ASN values for Enhanced
Beacon broadcasts in the network. For this purpose, additional network data is contin-
uously collected and propagated throughout the existing network in the form of MLME
Information Elements appended to the Enhanced Beacons. While this, unavoidably,
introduces a slight overhead in the form of larger data transmissions between nodes,
using the already allocated Enhanced Beacon timeslots alleviates the need for intrusive
changes to the slotframe scheduling mechanisms. Thus, the aforementioned overhead is
solely in the form of interference and power consumption and does not directly exacer-
bate the data transmission rates of the network.The project also asses the possibility of
retaining network information upon disassociation from a network, in order to further
improve an eventual re-joining process.
The evaluation of these modifications show promising results for both the initial joining
process and the re-joining process. The modified initial joining process reduces the
join time by approximately 50% or more compared to a classic TSCH implementation,
depending on the network topology and configuration. The re-joining process shows
even better results, with a re-join time reduction of 90% or more, depending on the
network topology and configuration.
Additionally, the solution deals with the issue of clock drift and the suggested coping
mechanisms have shown great promise, increasing the tolerance for clock drift by more
than 1000% while presenting no apparent drawbacks.
While this report conclusively suggests that TSCH certainly can be modified and made
more performant for mobile applications, the topic still requires further work before
any industry-wide implementations are realistic. Good examples of further research
questions are therefore: What is the optimal way of mitigating resource constraints
when topologies grow too large to eﬀiciently store or communicate? and How will more
advanced TSCH versions and schedulers impact the performance of the solution?.
