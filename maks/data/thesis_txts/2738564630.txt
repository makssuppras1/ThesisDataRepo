ABSTRACT 
 
Organic Solar Cells have been the subject of research in recent years due to their low cost, 
flexibility, low weight and low environmental impact. Over the past few years, the power 
conversion efficiency (PCE) of organic solar cells (OSCs) has improved rapidly with the 
development of polymeric donors and non-fullerene acceptors.  However, OSCs present stability 
limitations when compared to technologies based on crystalline silicon or inorganic thin film.  
Thus, improving the lifetime of OSCs is the top priority to promote practical applications.  
Therefore, it is crucial to study the stability and life cycle of NFA Organic Solar Cells. There are 
different factors that affect the stability; besides the material and morphology of the active layer, 
the hole/electron carrier layer and the device structure also determine the stability of the OSCs. 
One of the most commonly used electron transfer layers in OPV is ZnO. ZnO is often chosen due to it 
high mobility, transparency and its multiple deposition procedures already studied; however, ZnO 
present a photocatalytic behavior that induces the degradation of the active layer greatly shortening the 
lifetime of the solar cell. In this work, tannic acid is explored as a possible solution to this problem due 
to its characteristic as an antioxidants. 
Most of the main learning objectives have been achieved: Study and Understanding of the 
structure and functioning of an organic solar cell; Fabrication of an organic solar photovoltaic 
device (OPV) device; Understanding of the function and need of antioxidants for OPV devices with 
ZnO as electron transfer layer (ETL); Measure, study, and comparison of the performance of OPV 
devices with and without an antioxidant between the ZnO ETL and the active layer. Study of the 
antioxidant layer passivating the ZnO. Compare OPV devices with the ZnO ETL deposited through 
solution-based processing versus sputtered. Work and experiment in the glovebox environment 
However, the optimization and improvement of stability of the OPV devices could not be 
demonstrated. The procedure was completed, but the results were not satisfactory. Probably 
because the measurements were inaccurate, or the contact was not established correctly.

INTRODUCTION 
 
MOTIVATION 
Organic Solar Cells have been the subject of research in recent years due to their low cost, flexibility, 
low weight and low environmental impact. Over the past few years, the power conversion efficiency 
(PCE) of organic solar cells (OSCs) has improved rapidly with the development of polymeric donors and 
non-fullerene acceptors. Recently, ladder-type non-fullerene acceptors (NFA) have resulted in OPV 
power conversion efficiencies (PCE) of ~18% in opaque cells, and 10% in semi-transparent cells with 
50% visible transparency [1]. 
This increase in efficiency as well as the ability to scale up to industrial manufacturing has made them 
attractive to reach the market, making them viable for many applications. However, OSCs present 
stability limitations when compared to technologies based on crystalline silicon or inorganic thin film.  
Thus, improving the lifetime of OSCs is the top priority to promote practical applications.  
Therefore, it is crucial to study the stability and life cycle of NFA Organic Solar Cells. There are different 
factors that affect the stability; besides the material and morphology of the active layer, the 
hole/electron carrier layer and the device structure also determine the stability of the OSCs. 
In this work, we aim to study the effect of electron transport layer and antioxidants on the life cycle of 
non-fullerene based organic solar cells.  
RESEARCH FACILITIES 
This research was performed at SDU CAPE, the Centre of Advance Photovoltaic and Thin-film Energy 
Devices directed by Prof. Morten Madsen. CAPE is a part of the mads Clausen Institute (MCI) at the

OBJECTIVES 
The objective of this research is to study the use of antioxidants in the interlayer between the electron 
transfer layer and the active layer to prevent the degradation of the active layer induced by the 
photocatalytic reaction of ZnO, therefore, improving the stability of the solar cell. The main learning 
objectives of this master thesis are: 
1. Study and Understanding of the structure and functioning of an organic solar cell. 
2. Fabrication of an organic solar photovoltaic device (OPV) device. 
3. Understanding of the function and need of antioxidants for OPV devices with ZnO as electron 
transfer layer (ETL). 
4. Measure, study, and comparison of the performance of OPV devices with and without an 
antioxidant between the ZnO ETL and the active layer. 
5. Measure, study, and comparison of the stability of OPV devices with and without an antioxidant 
between the ZnO ETL and the active layer. 
6. Study and optimization of the antioxidant layer passivating the ZnO. 
7. Compare OPV devices with the ZnO ETL deposited through solution-based processing versus 
sputtered. 
8. Work and experiment in the glovebox environment.

STATE OF THE ART AND RESEARCH CONTEXT 
Solar cells are energy converters that transform electromagnetic waves, photons (from sunlight); into 
DC electricity, electrons. To do so, the absorbed electromagnetic waves generate free charge carriers 
that are transported through the cell and are collected into electrodes. This process is called the 
photovoltaic effect. 
In the photovoltaic effect, absorbed photons with an energy equal or higher than the bandgap of the 
photoactive material promotes an electron from the valence band to the conduction band generating a 
voltage. Then, electrons and holes are separated through a p-n junction, with the electrons moving to 
the n-type side and the holes to the p-type side [3].  
For solar cells where the active layer is not a semiconductor but a molecular component, like organic 
solar cells, the photon promotes an electron from the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) to 
the lowest occupied molecular orbital (LUMO). If a photon has a higher energy than the difference 
between HOMO and LUMO, it will be promoted to an orbital with higher energy and then is falls to the 
LUMO through relaxation. 
In the case of organic photovoltaics organic semiconductors are employed. These semiconductors are 
based on conjugated carbon structures (alternating single and double bond. This pi-conjugated 
structures form band structures and are strong light absorbers and emitters. For OPV devices two 
organic semiconductors are needed, a donor and an acceptor. This is because the charge generation via 
photoexcitation is very inefficient in pristine organic SC, excitons with a large binding energy are formed. 
Using two organic SC helps to separate the charges. 
OPV solar cells are part of the third generation of solar cells (emerging technologies), this generation of 
solar cells includes technologies that can be solution-processed, with active layers mostly obtained 
chemically instead of physically. They are normally more cost effective since different expensive 
process requiring doing a vacuum are avoided.[4], [5], [6],[7] . 
 
ZINC OXIDE AS ELECTRON TRANSFER LAYER. 
Zinc Oxide (ZnO) is a commonly used material as electron transfer layers for different devices including 
flexible electronics and different types of solar cells structures like perovskite solar cells and organic 
solar cells. This layer can be deposited through different methods obtaining different morphologies: 
compact, mesoporous or both with the compact layer facing the electrode and the mesoporous part 
facing the photoactive material. The mesoporous layer is normally obtained by spin-coating a solution 
of nanoparticles and then annealing. Such types of layer have a larger area of interaction with the 
photoactive material but also have lower stability [10],[11]  
Zinc Oxide nanoparticles layer are commonly used as ETLs due to its high mobility, transparency and 
high efficiency, because of these it is the dominantly used electron transport material in high 
performance non-fullerene organic solar cells. Despite the ease of fabrication and high efficiency, the 
ZnO ETL suffers from photo instability due to its intrinsic ultraviolet (UV)-response that could result in 
poor stability of the organic solar cells. Bulk ZnO’s band gap is at 3.37 eV, any radiation with higher

energy can interact with the ZnO inducing an exited state, the generated electron-hole pair can induce 
a redox reaction photodegrading the organic active layer and destroying the cell.[8],[12],[13]. 
 
TANNIC ACID. 
In order to prevent the photodegradation of the active layer different strategies have been studied. In this 
case the deposition of an antioxidant in the interlayer between ETL and active layer is studied. The 
employed antioxidant is tannic acid. Tannic acid, one of the most famous polyphenols, has attracted 
widespread interest in membrane society [14]. Tannic acid is a high molecular weight molecule with 
molecular formula C76H52O46 and molecular weight of 1701.2 g/mol [15]. This acid is a type of 
polyphenol, which gives it reducing agent properties. Tannic acid is a naturally occurring molecule 
making it more affordable however it is also considered a carcinogenic agent, which should be 
considered during the synthesis and fabrication of the device as well as in case of destruction and 
management of the components after its lifetime. 
 
 
ACCEPTOR AND DONOR 
In organic solar cells the active layer is composed of two organic semiconductors instead of a single 
semiconductor like 1st generation solar cells, CIGS or perovskite solar cells. For this research the two 
studied organic semiconductors are PBDB-T (PCE12) and ITIC-F (IT-4F). The PCE12 is a 
semiconducting donor polymer while the IT-4F is a non-fullerene acceptor.

SPIN-COATING AND ANNEALING 
Spin-coating is a very common solution-based technique that allows us to obtain flat thin films over a 
substrate. In these techniques a solution with our desired material is deposited over the substrate, then 
substrates are spined at high-speed obtaining thin films thanks to the centrifugal force. The main factor 
effecting the final thickness of the film is the rotation speed.

Commercially bought glass substrates with patterned ITO are employed. The substrates are cleaned by 
sonicating in acetone and isopropyl alcohol for 15 minutes each. The cleaned substrates are 
UV.ozonized for 15 minutes to activate the surface.

ELECTRON TRANSPORT LAYER 
Zinc oxide is employed as electron transport layers. Two differently deposited zinc oxides are 
compared: (i) solution processed and (ii) sputtered.  
For the solution processed zinc oxide ETL a commercially bought solution of zinc oxide nanoparticles 
(Avantama N-10-flex, 2.5 wt% ZnO in mixture of butanols) is deposited through static spin-coating.  30 
L of solution are deposited over the sample. Once the solution has spread through the sample is spin-
coated at 3000 rpm for 30 seconds. Afterwards, the samples are annealed at 120 ºC for 15 minutes. 
For the sputtered ZnO, one of the recipes used in the lab was employed, this recipe is more focused on 
improving the efficiency than the stability. The ZnO oxide was deposited at a work pressure of 5.00·10-3 
mbar in Ar without O2. The Zinc oxide was deposited at a work power of 80 W at room temperature till 
reaching a thickness around 30 nm. 
 
ANTIOXIDANT DEPOSITION 
Tannic acid (TA) is used as antioxidant to passivate the interlayer between electron transfer layer and 
active layer. The TA is deposited through static spin-coating. 30 L of TA in ethanol (Thermoscientific, 
99%) are used per sample. Different concentration of TA is assessed ranging from 1 mg/mL to 0.1 
mg/mL. Different spin-coating speeds were also compared ranging from 1000 rpm to 6000 rpm. All the 
sample were spin-coated for 30 seconds and then annealed at 100 ºC for 5 minutes. 
 
ACTIVE LAYER 
The photoactive layer consists of two components PBDB-T (PCE12) (Brilliant Matters), polymer donor, 
and ITIC-4F (IT-4F) (Brilliant matters, Purity   99.9%), acceptor. The active layer solution is prepared by 
mixing both components at a 1:1 weight ratio in chlorobenzene at a concentration of 10 mg/mL each 
plus a 0.5 % in volume of diiodooctane (1,8-Diiodooctane, 99% purity with cooper as stabilizer). Then 
the solution is stirred overnight at 80 ºC.  
The active layer is deposited through dynamic spin-coating (the solution is dropped while the sample is 
already rotating. 25 L of active layer solution are used per sample and the samples are spin-coated at 
2500 rpm for 30 seconds. Then, the samples are annealed at 110 ºC for 10 minutes. 
 
HOLE TRANSPORT LAYER AND ELECTRODE 
Molybdenum oxide is used as hole transport layer and silver (Ag) as the anode. Both layers are 
deposited through thermal evaporation.  
MoO3 is used as material for the hole transport layer, however, during evaporation the content of 
oxygen may have changed. The deposited layer has a thickness of 10 nm and is deposited at a 0.02 
nm/s rate with a rotation speed of 5 rpm in a base pressure of 5.00·10-6 mbar. 
For the contact a 100 nm layer of Ag is deposited at a rate of 5.00E-6 mbar with a rotation speed of 5 
rpm.

RESULTS AND DISCUSSION 
 
The following experiments were performed in order to assess the use of an antioxidant, in this case 
tannic acid, as a passivating agent between the electron transfer layer and the active layer, preventing 
the photocatalytic degradation of the active layer induced by the ZnO ETL. 
OPTIMIZATION OF THE ANTIOXIDANT INTERLAYER. 
For the first step of the optimization process two concentrations (1 mg/mL and 0.5 mg/mL) were 
assessed at different spin-coating speeds ranging from 1000 rpm to 6000 rpm. For the two 
concentrations at all the different assessed speeds, the JV curves show s-shapes indicating that not all 
the generated carriers can be extracted. Light soaking is needed in order to prevent the s-shape. In all 
the cases a big improvement could be observed after 5 minutes of light soaking. For the samples 
deposited with TA solution 1 mg/mL around 30 to 35 minutes were needed to reach a state without s-
shape curves.  
For the samples deposited with a TA solution 1 mg/mL, the average efficiency for the samples deposited 
at different speeds is in between 1 to 2 % except one case where the efficiency was higher but the 
associated uncertainty is very high. It must also be noted that the uncertainty associated to the 
efficiency of the control is quite high. It can be observed that the factor that affected the most the 
performance is the FF (Table 1).

STABILITY MEASURMENTS 
Stability measurements were attempted for three different batches of solar cells with antioxidant. 
However, no coherent data was obtained. This was because of different problems: technical, 
experimental and temporal. 
CONCLUSIONS 
In this research was able to achieve solar cell devices with a tannic acid interlayer that don’t reduce the 
efficiency significantly; however, it was not possible to successfully measure the stability of this cells. 
The incorporation of tannic acid in the interlayer between the ZnO electron transfer layer and the organic 
photoabsorbers was studied to try to prevent the photodegradation of the active layer due to the 
photocatalytic behaviour of the ZnO. Extensive testing was conducted to try to optimize the interlayer 
of tannic acid. 
Most of the main learning objectives of this master thesis have been achieved: 
1. Study and Understanding of the structure and functioning of an organic solar cell.  
Through detailed analysis and extensive experimentation, the study elucidated the 
architecture and operational mechanisms of these cells. This involved examining the 
various layers that constitute the solar cell, including the active layer, the electron transport 
layer, and the hole transport layer. 
2. Fabrication of an organic solar photovoltaic device (OPV) device. Successfully 
fabricated an organic solar photovoltaic (OPV) device. This achievement involved the 
precise assembly of multiple layers. The resulting OPV device demonstrated effective 
performance, validating the design and materials used in its construction.

3. Understanding of the function and need of antioxidants for OPV devices with ZnO as 
electron transfer layer (ETL). Conducted research about the photocatalytic behaviour of 
the ZnO oxide in different fields and understood how it negatively effects solar cells devices 
and the need to prevent such reaction through different means like passivation. 
4. Measure, study, and comparison of the performance of OPV devices with and without 
an antioxidant between the ZnO ETL and the active layer.  Successfully measured, 
studied, and compared the performance of OPV devices with and without an antioxidant 
between the ZnO ETL and the active layer. The analysis demonstrated that the presence of 
an antioxidant improves device performance.  
5. Measure, study, and comparison of the stability of OPV devices with and without an 
antioxidant between the ZnO ETL and the active layer. The improvement of stability of 
the OPV devices could not be demonstrated. The procedure was followed but not 
enough coherent data could be gathered. 
6. Study and optimization of the antioxidant layer passivating the ZnO. The antioxidant 
layer passivating the ZnO was carefully studied and optimized reaching conditions where 
no s-shape characteristics were obtained. 
7. Compare OPV devices with the ZnO ETL deposited through solution-based processing 
versus sputtered was studied. Compact ZnO devices presented a higher efficiency, if 
stability is proven to also be superior, an alternative viable method to the one used in the 
lab could be employed moving forward. 
8. Work and experiment in the glovebox environment. Extensive work and experimentation 
were conducted in a glovebox environment to ensure the precise handling of materials and 
prevent contamination. This controlled environment was crucial for maintaining the 
integrity of the materials and achieving reliable experimental results. The use of the 
glovebox enabled accurate fabrication and testing of the OPV devices, contributing to the 
overall success of the research objectives. 
9. Measurement and study of the absorption of the active layer. The UV-vis spectra of the 
active layer was measured and studied. Tr5ansmittance and reflectance were compared.
