Abstract
Transmission lines have been suspended from insulators between steel lattice towers for
decades. Bystrup has proposed a new design utilizing the non-conductive properties of glass
ﬁbre reinforced plastics. In the proposed design, the conductors are directly attached to the
power pylon, meaning dynamic forces are also directly transferred to the pylon. The new
pylon design must be able to withstand these dynamic forces, of which the worst case, is the
phenomenon known as galloping.
A numerical galloping model is further developed and compared to an experimental study
in order to extract the forces acting on the pylon. Furthermore structural parameters of
the pylon are examined and their impact on the galloping motion is analyzed. This project
shows the diﬃculties of accurately comparing numerical galloping models with experimental
data, and illustrates the chaotic nature of galloping.
Another numerical model, describing the conductor-bearing part of the pylon, is used in
order to simulate an experiment, that will be performed at DTU. This numerical model
applies the forces extracted from the galloping model, and use them as input, in order to
determine the eﬀect of galloping on the pylon. The dynamic forces induced from galloping,
yields a maximum displacement of ∼8.5cm on the 11m long conductor-bearing part, of the
new pylon design.

1.
Introduction
1.1
Introduction
High-voltage transmission masts have been constructed as steel lattice towers for decades,
with no signiﬁcant change in appearance. Steel lattice towers are generally considered to be
visual pollution due to both the height and aesthetics of them.
In 2008 the danish government decided that all 400kV transmission lines should be converted
into underground cables if technically possible. However, that plan was changed in 2016
where it was deemed too expensive to make 400kV underground transmission lines. Instead
projects aiming to reduce the visual pollution of the transmission towers were resumed[1].
Bystrup has proposed a T-pylon design where the cross arms are made of glass ﬁbre reinforced
plastic (GFRP). This design would allow the transmission cables to be attached directly on
the cross arms instead of being suspended from the arms in insulators. Avoiding the need
of insulators would lead to a signiﬁcant reduction in height of the pylon.

transmission towers. The T-pylon consists of two cross arms made of GFRP and a tower of
steel, the three parts are connected using a T-section made of steel mounted at the top of

Having the cables connected directly to the cross arms introduces some concerns regarding
dynamic forces. The cross arms have a higher stiﬀness than insulator strings which may
introduce higher dynamic forces to the structure under certain circumstances. The most
severe dynamic loads happens when the transmission cables experiences galloping. Galloping

These galloping
motions will, in the proposed design be directly transferred to the pylon instead of through
the insulators.
The aim of this project is to determine the loads applied to the cross arms during cable
galloping and their eﬀect on the cross arms. A numerical model investigating cable galloping
on Bystrups T-pylon, will be further developed in order to extract these. Furthermore the
numerical galloping model will be tuned based on experimental results in order to validate
the model.
This project will also introduce the forces transferred to the cross arm during galloping into
a diﬀerent numerical model. This model will simulate an experimental setup that will be
performed at DTU in the fall 2018. By using the same forces as input in the numerical model
and the experimental setup, the model and experiment should be able to validate each other.
However, the validation and comparison between the model and the experimental data will
not be available for this project.

1.2.2
GFRP properties
When ﬁbre reinforced materials are used it is always important to orient the ﬁbres in a way
that makes sense. The ﬁbres gives the structure additional strength in the ﬁbre direction.
This means that if the ﬁbres are oriented in the wrong direction they might not contribute
to the material strength at all. Furthermore GFRP is made of many thin layers of material
which makes it possible to have multiple ﬁbre directions in the cross arms. In this case the
cross arms are designed in order to have high stiﬀness in the x- and y-direction in order to
resist deformation. The high stiﬀness also increases the natural frequency of the cross arms
which helps to avoid an over lapse with the galloping frequency. This is crucial because the
transmission cables are attached directly to the pylon which means that the galloping motion
is also transferred directly to the cross arm, instead of through an insulator string. If the
natural frequency of the cross arms are the same as the galloping frequency this could lead
to critical damage to the pylon during galloping. The speciﬁc lay-up used in this project is

1.3
Galloping
This section explains what galloping is and shows an example of the damage it can cause to
a transmission line.
1.3.1
The galloping phenomenon
Galloping is an extreme form of cable vibrations with low frequency and high amplitude.
Due to the very violent motions of galloping, it can have devastating consequences for the
transmission tower, power grid and in some cases the surroundings.
==PAGE:10==
5
1.3. GALLOPING
Figure 1.3.1: Damages caused by galloping during a winter storm in North Dakota (2010) [4]
Figure 1.3.1 shows the damage galloping can cause to transmission towers. However, gallop-
ing can cause damage to the power grid without doing damage to the transmission towers.
When galloping occurs on multiple transmission lines in the same span, the cables might
be out of phase with each other. If the cables are out of phase, the distance between them
during galloping is reduced and might lead to short circuits if not dimensioned correctly.
Galloping occurs in cold climates where ice accretion on the transmission cables results in
an asymmetrical cross section. When the asymmetrical cross section is then subjected to a
relatively strong cross wind it can cause an aerodynamic instability. This instability results
in the cables vibrating in elliptical patterns with large displacements in the vertical direction
and small displacements in the horizontal direction these vibrations are known as galloping.
Galloping can occur with many diﬀerent modes, however, it does not happen at a speciﬁc
mode but rather a mixture of modes. In this mixture of modes one mode is dominant but
will be greatly impacted by other modes.
1.3.2
Den Hartog galloping
In 1932 J. P. Den Hartog released a paper describing galloping for the ﬁrst time. He described
it as an aerodynamic instability which occurs when the eﬀect of the negative slope of the lift
curve is greater than the damping action due to drag [5]. This also explains why the ice
accretion is critical when talking about galloping, without ice accretion making the circular
cable asymmetrical no lift force is present thus making galloping impossible. Den Hartogs
statement is also expressed through an inequality stating that the system is dynamically
unstable if:

2.
Numerical Galloping Model
2.1
Numerical cable galloping model
In this section the numerical galloping model made in ABAQUS, which is further developed
in this project is described. The model was initially made by Daniel Johansen as his master
thesis at DTU and afterwards expanded and ﬁne tuned by Mathias Kliem. This section acts
as a brief introduction to the model based on Daniels and Mathias’ work([6],[3]).
2.1.1
Static loads
The numerical model describing cable galloping is comprised of both static loads and dynamic
loads. The static loads include gravity and pretension of the cables and the dynamic loads
consists of the aerodynamic loads induced by the wind.
The numerical model made in
ABAQUS have three spans where the middle span is the one experiencing galloping. The
two spans next to the middle span are not subjected to the aerodynamic loads and thus do
not experience galloping.
Figure 2.1.1: Cable galloping model in ABAQUS
In Figure 2.1.1 the pylons with the attached transmission line is shown with a span length
of 300m. The two cable ends and the bottom of the pylons are constrained in all 6 degrees
of freedom. The ﬁrst forces that needs to be applied to the model are the pretension and
the gravity. The pretension force controls the sag of the spans, a high pretension force leads
to a smaller sag between pylons. This is also evident from the analytic expression[7].

a density is assigned to the cable and the gravitational acceleration applied to the model.
However, the gravity also have a contribution to the pretension which makes it necessary to
ﬁne tune αt after gravity is applied in order to get the desired sag. This way of modelling
the sag/pretension is only possible as long as the pretension contribution of the gravity is
smaller than the desired pretension.

cable movement changes the angle of attack which introduces new lift, drag and moment co-

equation[5].

2.1.3
User-deﬁned Element
The aerodynamic forces in section 2.1.2 can not be applied using any of the standard forces
in ABAQUS. Therefore it is necessary to make an user-deﬁned element UEL, capable of
inducing the aerodynamic forces to the cable.
An UEL, is a Fortran code which recieves an input from ABAQUS and uses it to determine
a set of forces/moments/pressures/displacements and then use that as an input for the next
iteration of the ABAQUS model. In order to get the necessary forces and moments for this
project the Fortran code needs the angular velocity ˙θ and the vertical cable velocity ˙V . In
order to extract these values an element with no stiﬀness and mass is deﬁned in the UEL,
these elements are then applied to the cable in a way where they share the nodes of the cable
elements. Because these elements do not have mass or stiﬀness they will not interfere with
the dynamics of the model. They are used purely as a way to get the variables needed to
calculate α and afterwards apply the aerodynamic loads to the cable using the user-deﬁned
elements.
2.2
Parametric analysis
The work performed by Daniel Johansen[6] and Mathias Kliem[3], focuses on the dynamic
response of the cable during galloping and how it can be mitigated. the focus of this project
is to predict the forces acting on the T-pylon if galloping occurs. In this section some key
parameters in the model are changed in an eﬀort to understand their eﬀect on the forces
applied to the pylon.
Before a parametric analysis can begin it is important to understand which parameters in the
model that are important and changeable. In order to determine which parameters which
are of importance many diﬀerent methods can be used, including design of experiments.
However, due to both the nature of the galloping phnemenon and the computational cost of
the model, two parameters known to be of importance are changed. In this case the stiﬀness
of the pylon and the span length will be investigated and analyzed. The eﬀect of the changes
to the parameters will be compared using the forces on the pylon seen in ﬁgure 2.2.1.

2.2.1
Pylon stiﬀness
The stiﬀness of the pylon has a great inﬂuence on how the forces from the cable are transferred
to the cross arm. The stiﬀness and mass of the cross arm dictates the natural frequency. A
high stiﬀness leads to a higher natural frequency which ideally is far away from the galloping
frequency. Galloping is a low frequency phenomenon and usually lies around a frequency of
1Hz[9]. Furthermore an increase in stiﬀness without changing any other parameter would
lead to an increase in damping. This can be seen from the following expression:
C = αM + βK
(2.5)
Where C, M and K are the damping, mass and stiﬀness matrices respectively and α and β
are damping constants relating to the mass and stiﬀness respectively. If K is increased and
the rest stays constant it must result in an increased damping for the system. The forces
acting on the pylon cross arm with increased stiﬀness can be seen in ﬁgure 2.2.2.
Figure 2.2.2: Forces acting on the cross arm where the galloping cable is attached where the
pylon has a 50% increased stiﬀness.
Figure 2.2.2 shows that a signiﬁcant increase in cross arm stiﬀness (50%) does not change
the force response. This indicates that the natural frequency of the cross arm is relatively
far above the galloping frequency. Making the gap even larger between the two frequencies
do not aﬀect the forces acting on the cross arm.

parameters like mass, geometry of the cross arm and possibly material.

2.3
Validation
A part of making a numerical model is to compare it to other work if possible, which could
be either another numerical model or an experiment. In this section the numerical model
will be compared to an experiment performed in 2006. The numerical model will be changed
in order to ﬁt the experimental parameters, and the results will be compared.
2.3.1
Description of experiment
A full scale test facility in Canada was used to conduct a galloping experiment in 2006[10].
The test facility is placed in agricultural surroundings which have optimal wind conditions
in order to induce the galloping motion to the test line. Pictures of the test line and the

of the parameters. The wind velocity used in the numerical model is 8.5 m/s, even slight
changes to this parameter will make the model fail to produce galloping or change the mode
of the galloping motion. Another concern is that the experiments have not been compared to
work performed by others i.e. numerical models or other experiments. This is likely because
such work have not been performed before at least not in that scale. Furthermore the article
does not provide the wind direction, which may also aﬀect the results. In the numerical

experiment, as small variations due to the experimental setup being outside are expected.

for. These are just some of the reasons why adjusting and ﬁne tuning numerical galloping
models in order to ﬁt experimental work is very diﬃcult, the literature also shows that
comparing numerical models with other numerical models is diﬃcult [6].

many parameters in the model leads to dramatic changes in the response. Galloping is not
completely understood, and Den Hartog galloping used in the numerical model presented

assumptions might be too rough when the model is compared to experiments, where variables

Numerical Cross Arm Model
The forces from the galloping motion are directly transferred to the T-pylon instead of
through insulators. This means the cross arms must be able to withstand the direct response
from the galloping forces. In order to investigate the impact of the galloping forces found in
section 2.4, a numerical model in ANSYS is developed. The switch in software is due to a
shell model of the cross arm made by Jacob Waldbjørn already exists.
Furthermore the author is more experienced in ANSYS and none of the parts used in the
ABAQUS model are reusable. The cross arms in the ABAQUS model are assigned a custom
material with average properties of the GFRP layup (smeared properties). However, in order
to account for the layers interactions with each other, each lamina have to be deﬁned with
the right orientation.
In this chapter the galloping forces will be applied to the ANSYS model as a time history,
which makes it possible to evaluate the cross arms response if galloping occurs.

4.
Conclusions
4.1
Galloping model
The comparison between the numerical galloping model and the experiments[10], shows
that the numerical model predicts a galloping amplitude that is ∼2.2 times larger than the
experimentally found amplitudes. The chaotic behaviour of galloping makes it diﬃcult to
pin point the reason for the discrepancy in results, between the model and the experiments.
The mode of the galloping motion is unpredictable, however the numerical model showed,
that one mode is typically stable for a speciﬁc range of damping coeﬃcients α and β. It
is also important to note, that galloping does not vibrate at a speciﬁc mode, it is rather a
mixture of many modes with one mode being dominant. This mixture then changes when
the damping or any other parameter in the system is changed. This have great inﬂuence on
the amplitudes and forces transferred to the transmission tower, which is why it is hard to
compare diﬀerent galloping models or experiments.
4.2
Cross arm model
The forces transferred from the galloping motion to the transmission tower is extracted from
the galloping model, and used as an input in the cross arm model made in ANSYS. The
results of the cross arm model indicates, that the transient eﬀects are negligible, due to the
high stiﬀness and lightweight nature of the GFRP. Furthermore a modal analysis conﬁrms,
that the natural frequency of the cross arm does not overlap with any of the displacement
frequencies during galloping. When forces representing three conductors during galloping
were applied to the cross arm, the maximum displacement was ∼8.5cm in the end of the
11m long cross arm.
4.3
Further work
This thesis and much of the literature concerning galloping, focuses on one span, with dead
spans next to it. This is probably due to the complexity of accurately describing a single
span, but it is unlikely that the neighbor spans do not interfere with each other. This could
be a concern if the galloping motion of neighbor spans ampliﬁed the forces transferred to the
pylon. In order to use the forces extracted from the galloping model, in the experiments that
will be performed at DTU. They need to be translated into a code which can be understood
