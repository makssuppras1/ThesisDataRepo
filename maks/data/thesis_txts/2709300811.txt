Abstract
The current Master Thesis concerns the investigation of the mechanical properties of
Copenhagen Limestone, and how they are affected by the physical properties of the material.
This investigation was based on the analysis and interpretation of laboratory data in order
to derive the intact rock strength parameters of the Copenhagen Limestone, the upper
layer of Copenhagen’s bedrock, and through the investigation of the available RQD data,
characterize its rock mass. The acquired data were analysed to plot and investigate the
failure mechanism of the rock through the Hoek - Brown failure criterion. Additionally, a
numerical simulation was executed, based on Roesen and Trankjær’s (2021) article, where
the behaviour of uplift anchors grouted on Copenhagen Limestone was examined according
to the strength parameters of the rock material.

Introduction
1.1
Project Objectives
This thesis provides a framework of interpretation of the Copenhagen Limestone rock-
mass based on a set of laboratory experiments and borehole data. In more detail, three
different laboratory test data sets were analysed, in order to derive the strength parameters
of Copenhagen Limestone.
Additionally, numerical analyses through a finite element software are executed. Through
these analyses, the behaviour and shear capacity of uplift anchors grouted on Copenagen
limestone on a real-scale structure are examined, based on several bedrock conditions.
1.2
Stimuli and Motivation
Underground structures have been present in the human civilisations for thousands of
years. They mostly advanced through mining for precious metals and minerals, and the past
hundreds of years through transportation and commercial industries. Nowadays, the soaring
development of construction activity and expertise, along with the lack of space in modern
urban areas, are generating specific challenges due to the intricate site conditions in which
they are executed.
The past twenty years, Copenhagen witnessed a large growth, and several large infras-
tructure and transportation projects were built, such as the Cityringen Metro Line, and
currently the extension of the Harbour (M4) line. This project’s motivation is the familiar-
ization with the subsurface and bedrock of Copenhagen. An understanding of the physical
and mechanical properties of Copenhagen Limestone would offer precious knowledge in the
design of future geotechnical and civil engineering projects.

2.1
Physical and Mechanical Properties of Rock
Tunnels, mines and rock shafts are constructed inside rock masses. Dams, bridges and
large infrastructure projects are founded on top of rocks. Buildings and cultural monuments
are constructed by using rock as a building material.
It is evident that the study and
understanding of the physical and mechanical properties of rock is of paramount importance
in the design of engineering structures.
Rocks can be subjected to three primary types of stresses, namely the compressive,
tensile and shear. The compressive stresses cause a decrease in the volume of the rock, as
the forces are acting inwards and opposite to each other. The tensile forces tend to equally
pull a specimen apart by acting outwards on its surface. Finally, the shear stresses are caused
when a pair of two equal forces act on opposite directions. For each type of stress applied
on a rock, a specific type of failure occurs, and the resistance of the material depends on the
mineral composition and and the way the compounds are bound together.
In order to determine the resistance of rocks against the types of failure mentioned above,
specific laboratory or in situ testing procedures are conducted on either intact rock samples,
or rock masses. The three most common laboratory tests to determine the resistance of
a rock against failure are the Unconfined Compressive Strength test, the Splitting Tensile
Strength (or else the Brazilian) Test, and finally the Triaxial test.
Uniaxial Compressive Strength
Unconfined Compression Strength (UCS) is a destruc-
tive strength test which applies uniaxial load in unconfined state. The compressive strength,
the Young’s modulus of elasticity, and the Poisson’s ratio of the test specimens can be mea-
sured from the UCS test. It is used for classification and characterization of intact rock. The
rock strength is classified using the principal UCS of dry rock. In the case of a saturated
rock then its UCS is less that of dry rock. The sample’s diameter is 54 mm while cube or

Triaxial Compressive Strength
The triaxial compression test should be used to measure
the strength of cylindrical rock speciments which are subjected to triaxial compression.
Generally, the strength increases with an increase in confining stress. This strength is rarely
measured in rocks, more important for soil.
Multiple test must be executed, as their values are needed to determine the strength
envelope in several failure criteria such as the Mohr-Coulomb and Hoek-Brown. Triaxial
compression tests are performed on rock specimens to determine how the rock will behave
under different confining pressure values. For example the results from the Hoek-Brown
failure criteria can be used to define the intact’s rock compressive strength.
Tensile Strength of Rocks
The Brazil test is used to capture indirectly the uniaxial
tensile strength of a cylindrical rock specimen. When testing shales or other anisotropic
rocks, it is advised to cut test specimens parallel to and perpendicular to bedding. For
the specimens that are cur parallel to bedding then the direction of the load related to
the bedding should be specified.
The tensile strength of rock is usually in the range of
UCS/20 to UCS/8. Generally, when examining the rock stability, the tensile strength is not
as important as the UCS.
2.2
Rock mass vs. Intact Rock
When studying the mechanical properties and behaviour of rock materials, an important
distinction must be made between the intact rock and rock masses. The term intact rock
indicates an unfractured rock block with no fractures, between discontinuities and it is
composed by minerals and grains. The volume of an intact rock block may vary between a
few millimeters up to several meters. The mechanical properties and behaviour are greatly

2.2.1
Hoek - Brown Failure Criterion
The Hoek - Brown failure criterion was firstly introduced in 1980 as a method to provide
input data for the analysis required for underground excavations in hard rocks.
It was
an empirical approach, and its results were derived from data that were acquired from
previous researched based on the brittle failure of intact rock.
Even though the failure
criterion provided satisfactory results, a number of uncertainties and inaccuracies rendered
it problematic to be applied on numerical analysis and limit equilibrium problems. This
stemmed from the fact that in the original equations that described the criterion did not
take into consideration the geological parameters and in-situ conditions [2].
The criterion has been updated ever since, with the latest issue being published at
2018[3]. The updated Hoek-Brown criterion now takes into account both the intact rock

properties as well as rock mass discontinuities.
The procedure to characterize the rock mass parameters of a specific site has been out-
lined as follows. Initially, borehole locations need to be decided efficiently to understand the
areaâs geology. After the core samples are drilled and recovered, they are processed according
to specific standards so that laboratory testing to extract the intact rock strength parame-
ters can be determined. In addition to the core drilling, field observations and estimates are
executed by geologists to provide a qualitative estimation of the rock mass.
According to the field observations and estimates, two significant parameters can be
assessed: the geological strength index (GSI) and the disturbance factor (D). GSI was ini-
tially introduced by Hoek (1994)[4] and afterwards further developed by Hoek and Marinos
(2005)[5] in order to classify poor quality rock masses in respect to the structure and surface
conditions. GSI classification is a careful engineering geology description of the rock mass
which is essentially qualitative because previous rock characterization systems (RMR
Q-
system) were not suitable for weak or heterogenous rock massesâ (Marinos et al. (2005)[6]).
When tunnels, slopes or foundations are excavated in rock masses, removal of the rock
results in stress relief which allows the surrounding rock mass to relax and dilate (Hoek
et al.[3]). The disturbance factor D was developed in order to minimize rock failure and
the dilation caused by the stress relief when excavating or drilling in a rock mass. For this
reason, depending on the type, shape and the method of excavation a specific value of the
disturbance factor is selected .

2.3
Copenhagen Limestone
2.3.1
Material Description
Limestone is classified as a sedimentary rock, which is primarily composed of the
minerals calcite (CaCO3), or the double carbonate of calcium and magnesium, dolomite
(MgCO3, CaCO3). Its formation is a result from the deposition of carbonate sediments,
and several physical and chemical processes enable the sediment to become a coherent rock
[7].
The Copenhagen limestone was established by Stenestad (1976) [8]. It was formed in the
late Danian stage of the Paleocene epoch, and it overlays the bryozoan limestone formation.
Copenhagen limestone can be divided in three distinct areas, namely the upper, middle
and lower units. This distinction was based on macroscopic visual criteria, such as bedding
structures, bioturbation and clay content (Galsgaard, 2014) [9].
The composition of Copenhagen limestone is about 20%-30% carbonate sand and silt,
along with a small content of clay that gives the limestone a light gray colour (Stenestad,
1976) [8]. Through face logging, it has been observed that Copenhagen limestone has a sub-

horizontally layered structure, with alternate extremely weak to extremely strong beds of
variable thickness. The fluctuations in the thickness and strength of the layers is thought to
be climatically controlled (Jakobsen et al., 2015) [10]. Additionally, parallel to the layering
of the limestone, flint layers are observed.
2.3.2
Physical and Mechanical properties of Copenhagen Limestone
The porosity values in a Copenhagen Limestone varies depending on the induration
level of it. In slightly indurated to strongly indurated limestones the porosity varies between
52 vol % to 3 vol %, where the lower end of the porosity can be located in the very hard
uppermost layer in the Copenhagen Limestone. The average grain density values of the
Copenhagen Limestone is 2.66g/cm3. It has been observed that with the increase of the
induration, the grain density is also increasing. The bulk rock density, which reflects the
grain density and the porosity, varies in limestones with values between 1.85 and 2.65g/cm3.
The bulk density value also reflects the uniaxial compressive strength within the limestone,
while there is an inverse relationship between the porosity and the rock strength, as this is
caused by the cementation of the particles. Furthermore, the cementation type itself affect
the strength, as coarse cement produces higher strength values rather than fine-grained.
In an engineering geological context, limestone can be described using the degree of
induration. The induration is correlated with the degree of cementations, as the cement binds
the particles together. For a degree of induration H1, the limestone can be characterized as
an unlithified as there is an absence of cement binding the particles together. A limestone is
characterized as slightly indurated when it has a degree of induration of H2, and this occurs
when only a limited degree of cementation took place, in this case the porosity is between 35
to 50 %. For induration degrees of H3 and H4, the limestone is characterized as indurated
to strongly indurated, and this stems from the progressive precipitation of calcite cement in
the pore space, which has strengthened the bonding between the various particles (Knudsen
et al. (1995)[11]).

2.3.3
Engineering Geology of Copenhagen Limestone
The Copenhagen limestone formation is of late Danian age. It is a sandy and silty
carbonate mudstone defined by Stenestad (1976) [8]. It has sub-horizontal layering with
pronounced flint layers parallel to the layering. Usually the limestone is covered by not
more than 2-5m of glacial sediments. However, they have been areas mapped that show
erosional scars containing clay till and meltwater sand that reach up to 10 m. Although,
at the navigational channels which have water depths that reach up to 13 m, due to the
periodic very high energy currents present, the glacial sediments are eroded leaving only a
thin layer of coarse grained lag sediments that contain gravel, cobbles and large boulders
that were washed out from the glacial till. There are areas, where thin layer of sands and
peat or organic mud have been detected. (Knudsen et al (1995) [11]).

Data Analysis
The current chapter concerns the data analysis of specific laboratory tests and rock mass
evaluations that were executed on Copenhagen Limestone samples during the execution of
Cityringen metro line. The laboratory test data are comprised of Triaxial (TX) tests, Uncon-
fined Compression (UCS) tests and Splitting Tensile Strength - or else known as Brazilian
(BRZ) - tests. In addition to the laboratory experiments, the Rock Quality Designation
index (RQD) of the extracted borehole samples was determined. The data were provided by
Rambøll.
The specimens that were tested were acquired from geotechnical boreholes that were
created in specific areas, related to the Cityringen Metro Line. In Appendix A, the boring
identification number of each location is presented, along with the test data.
In the acquired laboratory testing data sheets, the stress conditions at failure are pre-
sented. Additionally, further information about the tested specimens are provided, such as
the depth of the collected sample, the degree of induration, the bulk density, the initial void
ratio and porosity. The raw data can be found on Appendix A.

Numerical Modelling
In the past two decades, various underground projects have been constructed in Copen-
hagen. The bedrock in the area is comprised of Danian limestones, which are characterised
as weak sedimentary rocks with varying strength properties. Another issue the established
underground structures are encountering is the high ground water levels, as the phreatic
water is hydrostatic from roughly the ground level. To resist the uplift forces acting on the
structures, passive uplift anchors or tension piles are installed to ensure structural stability
(Jackson et al., (2021) [15]).
As stated by Roesen and Trankjær (2021) [16], a new urban area in the former Postal
Service Centre in central Copenhagen is developed, and it will be comprised of service and
retail trade, housing apartments, and a two-level underground parking area. Due to the
high pore pressures and the generated uplift forces, approximately 950 ground anchors were
installed in the basement.
The anchors were subjected to investigation testing, aiming
to achieve failure in the grout-limestone interface. None of the anchors failed during the
investigation testing, raising questions about the possible parameters that may affect the
behaviour of the anchor grouted in Copenhagen limestone.
In this chapter, a numerical simulation of an anchor subjected to pull out testing will
be approached, based on the information provided in the Roesen and Trankjær (2021) [16].
The software that will be used to simulate the problem is PLAXIS 2D.

4.3
Pull out test simulation
The anchor pull out test is an investigation test that establishes the ultimate load resis-
tance of the anchors in respect to the ground conditions and the construction materials used.
It is used to provide sufficient information on whether the installed anchors are competent
to withstand the required loading, and gain insight on whether a new type of anchor can be
used to avoid failure between the ground-anchor grout interface [19].
In the case described by Roscoe and Trankjær (2021) [16], twenty three investigation
tests were executed on vertical ground anchors throughout the base slab area, where 9 of
the anchors had a grouted length of 4 m, 7 of them had a grouted length of 5 m and the
last 7 had a grouted length of 6 m. The anchors were tested in accordance to the Danish
Standard DS 1537, where the Test Method 1 was applied.
Test Method 1 suggests that the tested anchor should be loaded incrementally in cycles,
from a datum load to a proof load [19]. Two sets of measurements are acquired from this
test; the displacement of the anchor is measured in respect to the applied load, and the
displacement of the anchor versus time, at the peak of each loading cycle.
In order to review the performance of the anchors, the creep rate is calculated through
the acquired measurements. For each anchor, the creep rate ks was calculated as shown
in Equation 12 below, wheresi is the deformation and ti is the time. A threshold value of
ks ≥5mm was set to signify anchor failure, as prescribed in the Danish Standard DS 1537
[20].

Discussion
5.1
Data Analysis
The analysis of the data sets of tests performed on the Copenhagen Limestone samples
provide a good understanding of the strength properties of the material, and how specific
physical parameters affect them.
Initially, the stress data points of the UCS and BRZ testing procedures were plotted in
respect to the depth that the sample was acquired, as shown in Figure 2 and Figure 3. From
this primary analysis, it was established that the depth of the sample had no relation to the
strength properties of the rock. This can be justified by the varying degrees of limestone
diagenesis, which resulted in varying degrees of cementation and silicification [21].
According to the diagrams presented in Figure 9 and Figure 10, it is evident that both
the uniaxial compressive strength and the tensile strength of the Copenhagen Limestone is
influenced by the bulk density of the sample, as well as from the degree of induration. For a
higher bulk density and for a higher degree of induration the samples seem to have a higher
resistance to compressive and tensile stresses. The degree of induration is an expression of
the degree of calcite cementation and silicification (Larsen et al. (1995) [22]).
After establishing which physical properties of the limestone affect its mechanical ones,
the Hoek - Brown failure criterion was implemented to determine the strength parameters of
the intact rock. The derived intact rock parameters from the Hoek - Brown failure criterion
are summarized and presented below, on Table 8.

Finally, the study of the RQD data acquired from the boreholes were examied. By
observing the resulted RQD values of the analysed data set as seen in Figure 13, it is
apparent that the rock is of poor quality.
However, these results could be pertinent to
several external factors that may have been introduced to the limestone prior or posterior
to the extraction of the core samples.
Hansen et al. (2015) [24], during the evaluation of face logging data, found that the
core analysis was underestimating the rock mass parameters of Copenhagen Limestone, as
in many cases the face logs revealed excellent rock mass surfaces, equivalent to RQD values
of 90%-100%.
Limestone in general can be characterized as a weak rock with a brittle
behaviour, this could affect the resulted core samples as the drill could have broken up the
rock in situ.
5.2
Numerical Simulation
In this numerical simulation of the pull out anchor testing the two main outputs of
this parametric analysis were the relation between the rock mass properties of Copenhagen
Limestone with respect to the creep ratio and to the mobilizing shear stresses generated on
the surface of the grouted length of the anchor. The rock mass parameter that was altered
throughout the parametric numerical analyses was the geological strength index.
It has been observed that as the GSI values were increasing for the same anchor loading
the displacements on the top of the anchor were decreasing. This is attributed to the fact
Page 45 of 54

that a larger GSI value suggests a stronger rock mass. In order to evaluate whether the
anchor can pass the pull out test a creep ratio graph was plotted in respect to the applied
loads, as shown in Figure 24. By inspecting this graph it can be seen that even though the
vertical displacements are staying at low levels the creep ratio increases and after the load
step of 2200 kN the anchor reaches failure as the creep ratio overcomes the 5 mm threshold
value as described in the Danish Standards [19]. By comparing the creep results of the
numerical analyses with the creep results presented on the Roscoe and Trankjær (2021) [16]
it can be seen in the Plaxis simulation the anchors reach failure whilst in reality they have
not. A possible reason is that Roscoe and Trankjær may have underestimated the intact
rock strength and the rock mass parameters at the site of construction.
The next aspect that was examined in the numerical analyses was the mobilising shear
strength on the grouted part of the anchor.
With an increase to the GSI values in the
analysis, it was observed that the values of the mobilising shear stresses were reducing. As
the higher GSI values suggest a more robust rock mass, it is sensible that the interface
between the anchor grout and the limestone can withstand higher stresses. The anchor does

6
Conclusion
The current thesis concerned the data interpretation of available data sets of specific
laboratory tests, in order to establish the strength parameters of Copenhagen Limestone.
Through the data analysis and interpretation of three distinct laboratory experiments, the
strength parameters that characterize the examined rock material were defined. Additionally,
a statistical analysis was implemented in order to sort out any irrelevant data and outlier
values. Finally, a numerical analysis was executed by simulating a real scale construction as
realistically as possible, in which permanent uplift anchors were examined under a pull out
test.
Through the interpretation of the laboratory rock experiments, it was established that
the physical properties of Copenhagen limestone, such as the bulk density and induration
highly affect the strength parameters of the material. More specifically, it was concluded
that the intact rock samples with the higher induration degrees and bulk densities could
withstand higher compressional stresses. As there were no further data about the geological
conditions of the rock mass, it was not possible to establish the strength parameters of the
rock mass.
Additionally, a set of borehole drilling reports of specific sites throughout Copenhagen
was examined and analysed over the rock quality designation (RQD) value. From the bore-
hole data analysis, a high number of examined cores had very low RQD values, implying
that the quality of Copenhagen Limestone’s rock mass was of poor quality. However, by
comparing the RQD results with established bibliographical research of core logging, it was
understood that due to the highly brittle nature of limestone, the retrieved borehole cores
may have been fractured during drilling operations.
Finally, a numerical analysis simulating the establishment of permanent uplift anchors
in Copenhagen Limestone was attempted, according to the data provided in the report of
Roscoe and Trankjær [16]. The numerical analysis shed light on the importance of having
a proper background on the geological conditions of a construction area, as the defining
Page 48 of 54
==PAGE:56==
parameter on the numerical analysis was the Geological Strength Index (GSI). The numerical
analysis results were deemed reasonable, as a higher GSI value resulted in a stronger rock
mass, resulting in lower displacements and lower mobilizing shear stresses on the surface of
the grouted part of the anchor.
