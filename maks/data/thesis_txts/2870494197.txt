Abstract
Autoimmune diseases such as Psoriasis (PsO) and Psoriatic Arthritis (PsA) present complex
immunological profiles that can be explored at single-cell resolution using single-cell mRNA se-
quencing. This thesis develops two complementary computational frameworks, Zero-Inflated
Poisson Latent Distance Models (ZIPLDM) and Poisson Archetypal Analysis (PAA), to directly
model scRNA-seq data without normalization, thereby capturing intricate cell–gene relation-
ships and uncovering novel insights into disease mechanisms and treatment responses.
In ZIPLDM, both cells and genes are embedded into a shared latent space, modeling expression
counts through Poisson assumptions while accounting for random effects and zero-inflation.
This approach inherently accounts for sparsity and variability in scRNA-seq data while pre-
serving its full dimensional complexity. Evaluations demonstrate that ZIPLDM yields robust
representations, accurately predicting gene expression rates and identifying subtle differences
between clinically relevant subgroups. By leveraging the latent representation of cells, it also
facilitates annotation denoising, correcting potential biases from static, reference-based anno-
tations.
Meanwhile, PAA captures high-level variation and identifies archetypes that represent distinct
functional states across cell populations. When guided by pathway analysis, PAA distinguishes
responders from non-responders to methotrexate (MTX) treatment, even at baseline, highlight-
ing potential mechanistic drivers of non-response embedded in specific cell subsets. These
archetypal patterns suggest that some patients may harbor immunologically distinct subpopu-
lations not apparent through conventional analyses.
By combining ZIPLDM and PAA, this thesis presents a unified framework for analyzing scRNA-
seq data in PsO/PsA. The methods reduce reliance on extensive preprocessing while revealing
clinically actionable insights, such as biomarkers of treatment response. These findings un-
derscore the value of unsupervised approaches for scRNA-seq data and guiding therapeutic
strategies in autoimmune diseases.

Introduction
This thesis aims to explore advanced computational methods for analyzing single-cell mRNA
sequencing (scRNA-seq) data to improve the understanding and treatment of Psoriasis (PsO)
and Psoriatic Arthritis (PsA). While these methods are applied to rheumatological diseases in
this study, they are also applicable to the analysis of any cellular composition assessed through
scRNA-seq. This flexibility allows the methods to be utilized across a wide range of biological
and medical research domains, facilitating a deeper understanding of diverse cell types and the
underlying mechanisms of various diseases.
Understanding and treating autoimmune diseases such as Psoriasis (PsO) and Psoriatic Arthri-
tis (PsA) remain a significant challenge due to their inherent heterogeneity and unpredictable
responses to treatment. These diseases involve complex interactions between immune cells,
genetic predispositions, and environmental factors, resulting in highly variable disease mani-
festations across patients. Consequently, these complexities demand more personalized ther-
apeutic approaches that can account for individual differences [1] [2].
scRNA-seq provides an unparalleled ability to examine cellular gene expressions and functions
at a high resolution, offering critical insights into the diverse cell populations involved in au-
toimmune responses. By enabling the identification of rare cell types and states, scRNA-seq is
instrumental in uncovering novel disease signals and guiding personalized treatment strategies.
However, despite its potential, current computational methods often fall short in fully capturing
the intricate heterogeneity present in scRNA-seq data. This limitation makes it difficult to con-
sistently identify novel cell types, states, and functional insights essential for advancing both
disease understanding and the development of effective, targeted therapies [3] [4].
Graph Representation Learning (GRL) offers a promising approach to address these limitations
by learning low-dimensional representations of cells and genes in a structured, informative man-
ner. By modeling the intricate relationships between cells and genes, GRL shows promise for
uncovering biologically meaningful patterns within scRNA-seq data [5]. These patterns are crit-
ical for understanding cellular phenotypic diversity, which are fundamental to identifying novel
therapeutic targets. The potential of GRL to capture both local connectivity patterns and global
structural properties within cellular data makes it a powerful framework for modeling the intricate,
network-like characteristic of scRNA-seq datasets [6] [3].
Graph-based methods have proven effective across various fields, including biological data
analysis. In structural biology, DeepMind’s AlphaFold utilizes graph-based models to predict
protein folding [7]. Similarly, graph-based approaches have been applied to tasks such as
chemical compound classification [8], network community detection [9] as well as scRNA-seq
data analysis [10].
For scRNA-seq data, graph-based methods are well-suited due to the intrinsic relational struc-
ture of biological systems [5]. Common approaches involve constructing cell-cell graphs using

1.1
Research Question & Operationalization
The challenges and opportunities outlined above lead to the following research questions:
Research Question 1: How can Graph Representation Learning (GRL) be used to identify
novel cell types and states associated with treatment response in PsO/PsA patients that
are not covered by existing methods?
To investigate this, the study will apply Poisson Latent Distance Models (PLDMs) to embed cells
and genes into a shared latent space that captures complex relationships within single-cell data.
By extending the LDM to account for phenomena such as zero-inflation and incorporating ran-
dom effects, the model will more accurately be able to represent the underlying distribution of
the data, capturing the sparsity and variability typical in scRNA-seq datasets. Hierarchical clus-
tering will be performed on these latent representations in an attempt to identify novel clusters
of cell types and states. These clusters will then be compared with known cell types and states.
This approach aims to uncover novel or previously uncharacterized cell types and states that
may be linked to differential treatment responses in PsO/PsA.
Research Question 2: Can Archetype Analysis of single-cell gene expression signatures
reveal novel cell states and functions?
This research question will be addressed by identifying and characterizing archetypes within
single-cell gene expression data.Archetype Analysis seeks to identify extreme patterns of gene
expression that characterize the archetypes, which represent distinct cell states within the dataset.
By comparing these archetype-defined cell states with existing annotations, the study aims to
uncover novel cell functions or states that have not been previously characterized. The analy-
sis will focus on gene expression patterns to explore the functional implications of these novel
archetypes, offering new insights into cellular behaviors and their potential roles in disease
mechanisms.
Research Question 3: Can predictive models based on invariant latent representations
of single-cell transcriptomic data consistently predict optimal treatment strategies for
PsO/PsA patients across varying treatment conditions?
To address this question, the study will examine treatment response models using latent repre-
sentations derived from the PLDM. The predictive framework will integrate immunoprofiles with

In this thesis, we propose a novel approach for representing immono-cellular profiles and treatment-
relevant states in complex autoimmune diseases, specifically PsO and PsA. Our framework
integrates Poisson Archetypal Analysis (PAA) and Zero-Inflated Poisson Latent Distance Mod-
els (ZIPLDMs) to capture distinct cellular archetypes and underlying latent structures in high-
dimensional single-cell mRNA sequencing data. Importantly, the framework is theoretically ag-
nostic to the origin of the tissue, allowing its use in various scRNA-seq applications.
In particular, we develop a ZIPLDM framework that accounts for sparsity in single-cell data, en-
suring that zero-inflated observations do not disproportionately affect our latent representation.
To complement this, we adjust traditional AA to better align with the distribution of count data,
provinding a highly interpretable representation of scRNA-seq data.
To evaluate model performance, we apply link prediction to assess cell-gene relationships and
node classification to infer biologically meaningful cell types. We use Jensen-Shannon diver-
gence to quantify differences in immunoprofiles across patient groups and treatment condi-
tions. Furthermore, we employ Single Cell Pathway Analysis (SCPA) to compare pathway ac-
tivity between baseline and follow-up samples, providing a functional validation of the inferred
cell annotations. We also apply hierarchical clustering to the learned latent representations for
community detection, facilitating the identification of distinct immune cell populations and their
hierarchical relationships.
Finally, we demonstrate the utility of the proposed framework in downstream analyses by iden-
tifying candidate biomarkers associated with differential treatment response. The results high-
light the potential of PAA and ZIPLDMs in deriving clinically relevant insights from single-cell
data and inform future efforts in precision medicine for autoimmune diseases like PsO and PsA
as well as other scRNA-seq applications.

Background & Material
2.1
PsO & PsA
PsO and PsA are interconnected chronic inflammatory disorders, both driven by shared au-
toimmune mechanisms that affect the skin and joints [15]. PsO is recognized primarily as a
skin condition marked by red scaly plaques that typically appear on the elbows, knees, scalp,
and lower back. These lesions arise from an overactive immune system that drives excessive
proliferation and inflammation of skin cells [15]. PsA is a form of inflammatory arthritis that de-
velop in approximately 3 out of 10 individuals with PsO. It is characterized by inflammatory joint
pain, stiffness, swelling and enthesitis resulting from the same dysregulated immune responses
that cause skin manifestations [15]. PsO & PsA often also present with comorbidities such as
inflammatory bowel disease, uveitis, as well as cardiovascular (CV) comorbidities, such as hy-
pertension, dyslipidemia, insulin resistance, and obesity [16] [17]. PsA affects approximately
133 individuals per 100,000, with an increased prevalence seen in Nordic regions, where it
reaches 200 per 100,000 [15] [18]. Despite the high prevalence of psoriatic disease, significant
knowledge gaps remain in understanding its pathogenesis. While the past decades have seen
the introduction of powerful biologics targeting key inflammatory pathways, non-response con-
tinues to be a challenge. This is largely due to the lack of predictive therapeutic biomarkers
and an incomplete understanding of disease mechanisms. In practice, this leads to a hetero-
geneous response to treatment in patient groups and complicates treatment strategies [19] [2].
2.2
Single-cell mRNA sequencing
Single-cell mRNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) is a transformative technology that allows for ex-
ploring the transcriptomes of individual cells, providing unprecedented insights into cellular het-
erogeneity and dynamics. Unlike traditional bulk RNA sequencing, which averages gene ex-
pressions across a population of cells, scRNA-seq captures the unique gene expression profile
of each cell, enabling the identification of distinct cell types and states within complex tissues
and diasease states [20] [21].
Since its introduction in 2009 [22], subsequent refinement in scRNA-seq has reduced cost and
increased sequencing capacity [23] [24] [25]. scRNA-seq has become a crucial tool in various
fields, including immunology, neuroscience, and developmental biology [26] [27]. As a result of
its application, the detection and understanding of disease mechanisms has advanced signifi-
cantly, allowing the identification of rare cell types, mapping of cellular states, and uncovering
gene expression patterns associated with pathological conditions. These capabilities are driving
innovations in precision medicine and fostering the development of novel therapeutic strategies
[21].
Single-cell mRNA-seq data can be sampled from a wide range of tissues and organs across

expression individually rather than in bulk. There are currently various and competing ways to
acheive this [12] [23] [24] [25], however this thesis will primarily handle data sampled with the BD

into micro-wells or droplets, each designed to hold only one cell. Tiny polymer beads called
nano-beads, loaded with barcoded oligonucleotides, are then introduced to these partitions.
Each bead has a unique cell barcode that allows the identification of the cell it is paired with.
Additionally, these barcodes contain unique molecular identifiers (UMIs) that tag every mRNA
transcript captured from the cell. In essence, the nano-bead functions as a tool for capturing
and uniquely tagging mRNA transcripts, enabling accurate quantification of gene expression.
The outcome is a count of gene expression for every gene in each cell, where each count is
determined using UMIs to eliminate potential duplicates. After this process, raw gene counts
data are generated and represented as a gene count matrix, where each row corresponds to
a cell, and each column corresponds to a gene, with the entries reflecting UMI counts across
thousands of genes. The sampling process from the BD Rhapsody can be seen in Figure 2.1

2.3
MTX treatment data
For the purpose of this thesis, we will focus on applying computational methods on data from
patients with PsO/PsA. Specifically, we will consider scRNA-seq samples from PBMC of pa-
tients in a methotrexate (MTX) treatment regimen.
If a patients present with moderate to severe PsO/PsA, they are usually started on MTX as
a first-line treatment [30]. MTX is generally safe, cost-effective, and provides broad-spectrum
treatment targeting both joint and skin manifestations of PsO/PsA [31] [32] [33].
The data was generated at The Parker Institute, as part of a research initiative to investigate
the response to various treatments at the cellular level coupled with clinical outcomes. The
data from The Parker Institute also includes other treatment regimens such as TNF and IL-17
inhibitors, as well as healthy controls.
For each treatment, PBMC cells were sampled at baseline (0 months) and follow-up (4 months)
to assess the treatment’s effect on gene expression. At both baseline and follow-up, clinical
markers were extensively collected and emcompass skin and joint manifistations of the disease,
as well as general health. Using the clinical markers, a Disease Activity index for Psoriatic

2.4
Literature Review on Computational Methods in Single-Cell
mRNA Sequencing
The field of computational methods for single-cell analysis has expanded significantly, now en-
compassing over 1,400 distinct approaches [36] [37]. These methods address various modal-
ities, including scRNA-seq, protein surface markers, chromatin, and even spatial imaging [36].
They also target diverse tasks such as annotation [11] and clustering [38]. For scRNA-seq, the
two primary tasks of interest are cell annotation and cell clustering.
2.4.1
Reference Annotations
Defining and annotating cell types is an extremely challenging task [39], which remains an open
problem in many contexts [40]. Best practises in the field recommend using a mix of manually
calculated marker genes and utilizing reference datasets to annotate cells cluster [41]. These
reference annotations are themselves derived using a plethora of computational methods [37].
Reference annotation tools, such as Seurat [11] and scCATCH [42], have been developed to
automate this process. Seurat is particularly known for its ability to provide reference-based an-
10
Towards Precision Immunoprofiles Using Graph Representation Learning for Modeling Single-Cell RNA-Seq Data
==PAGE:21==
notations by leveraging a reference atlas, enabling consistent mapping of cell types across dif-
ferent datasets. This method is reproducible, computationally efficient, user-friendly and avoids
the explicit clustering step often otherwise required as a premliminary step for cell annotation
[41].
While reference annotation approaches are scalable and accessible, they have notable lim-
itations. They may not account for systemic differences, such as variations induced by dis-
ease states which can alter cell behavior and identity [43] [13]. Additionally, reference datasets
themselves may be influenced by factors like treatment regimens or experimental conditions,
introducing potential noise [44].
To address these challenges, tools like ScType [43] and scMRA [45] have emerged. ScType
utilizes extensive databases of gene markers, while scMRA employs deep learning to integrate
multiple reference datasets, mitigating batch effects. These innovations highlight the critical
role of computational advancements in improving cell annotation workflows and addressing
structural challenges and methological limitations.
2.4.2
Cell Clustering Approaches
Clustering is a fundamental step in analyzing scRNA-seq data, enabling the identification of cell
subtypes and inference of cell lineages. This process, however, is fraught with challenges due
to the unique characteristics of scRNA-seq data, including high dimensionality, dropout events,
and biological heterogeneity [46]. Traditional clustering methods such as hierarchical clustering,
k-means, and graph-based clustering have been adapted to address these complexities [47],
while deep learning-based methods aim to learn latent representations of the data to uncover
meaningful clusters [48].
A common approach to clustering involves constructing a cell-to-cell graph from the cell-gene
matrix [11] [12]. High dimensionality and variance complicates the measurement of cell-to-cell
distances and are therefore often handled by preliminary normalization and dimensitonality re-
duction [38] [49]. This is further complicated by dropout events, which introduce noise and
zeros, further obscuring true biological signals [13]. Combined with batch effects arising from
experimental variability, these issues can mask meaningful biological signals, making it chal-
lenging to construct clusters that capture the underlying biological structure [38] [13].
As an example, Seurat employ some of these computational conventions in the creation its
own reference dataset. It uses Principal Component Analysis (PCA) to reduce, both mRNA
and protein data, to the top 40-50 dimensions. It constructs k-nearest neighbors (kNN) graphs
separately for gene expression and surface protein data, combines these using a Weighted
Nearest Neighbors (WNN) approach, and refines the result through a Jaccard-based shared
nearest neighbor (SNN) method. Clustering is performed using the Smart Local Moving (SLM)
algorithm, and clusters are annotated through differential expression (DE) analysis. Reference
annotations are subsequently derived by projecting new data into this PCA space and assigning
labels based on the most similar cells [11]. This workflow is similar to other cluster-based anno-
tation approaches, such as those presented in Ding et al. 2020 [12] and Ekiz et al. 2020 [50].
While common approaches, the reference annotations inheret the limitations of the methods
Towards Precision Immunoprofiles Using Graph Representation Learning for Modeling Single-Cell RNA-Seq Data

==PAGE:22==
used to generate them. PCA is sensitive to the large variations in gene expression magnitudes,
making it prone to being dominated by highly expressed genes and can even be dominated by
techinical artifacts [51] [52]. This obviously affect the downstream clustering that itself is based
on arbitrary parameter choices, such as the k in kNN and can lead to varying cluster sizes [38].
2.4.3
Archetypal Analysis in scRNA-seq
Archetypal Analysis (AA), while sparringly, has been employed to address the complexity and
heterogeneity of scRNA-seq data. Nonlinear extensions of AA, incorporating variational autoen-
coders, allow for the parameterization of the challenging geometry inherent to scRNA-seq data,
building on previous work in modeling these data’s structure [53][54]. Furthermore, PANDA in-
troduces a probabilistic framework that applies AA to scRNA-seq data under a Poisson assump-
tion. It defines cell types based on reference annotations and integrates spatial transcriptomics
data to estimate spot-specific cell type proportions and cell-type-specific gene expression. This
approach captures both spatial and transcriptional heterogeneity within tissues, offering a com-
prehensive view of cellular organization [55].
In summary, identifying and labeling cell types in single-cell RNA data remains challenging de-
spite the availability of tools like Seurat, scCATCH and emerging methods like ScType and
scMRA. While these tools streamline the annotation process and improve consistency, they
are limited in their ability to discover novel cell types and account for variations within the data.
Similarly, clustering approaches face significant hurdles due to the inherent complexity and vari-
ability of scRNA-seq data. Continued advancements in computational methods are essential to
enhance the accuracy and reliability of both cell type annotation and clustering with GRL and
AA being a strong candidates [5] [53].

Methods
In this chapter, we outline our GRL-based approach for modeling the scRNA-seq count matrix
directly, eliminating the need for any preprocessing or direct normalization steps. We begin by
demonstrating how the cell-gene counts can be epxressed as a weighted bipartite graph and
then extend this framework to Latent Distance Models. To capture the overdispersion and spar-
sity often found in scRNA data, we introduce Poisson and zero-inflated formulations, enhanced
with random effects for technical variability. Building on these representations, we apply hi-
erarchical clustering in the latent space and explore Poisson Archetype Analysis for improved
interpretability. Finally, we present our evaluation approach for model performance covering
techniques such as link prediction, node classification, and Single Cell Pathway Analysis.

3.2.1
Poisson LDM for Bipartite Graphs
To uncover the latent structure underlying the cell-gene interactions, we employ the Latent Dis-
tance Model (LDM) as introduced in Hoff et al. 2002 [57] specifically adapted for bipartite graphs.
In the LDM framework, each node in the graph is associated with a position in a low-dimensional
latent space. For our bipartite graph:
• Let zi ∈Rd denote the low-dimensional latent position of cell i, for i = 1, . . . , N.
• Let wj ∈Rd denote the low-dimensional latent position of gene j, for j = 1, . . . , G.
The fundamental assumption of the LDM is that the probability of an edge between a cell and
a gene is a function of the distance between their latent positions. Such that a small distance
translates to a large probability of an edge. In our weighted bipartite graph, this distance also
determines the weight of the edge.

While the standard Poisson Latent Distance Model (PLDM) aims to effectively capture the un-
derlying structure of cell-gene interactions, it operates under the assumption that the observed
counts follow a Poisson distribution, where the variance equals the mean. However, scRNA-seq
data often exhibit overdispersion, where the variance exceeds the mean. This overdispersion
can arise from both biological heterogeneity (e.g., cell state differences, stochastic gene ex-
pression) and technical noise (e.g., batch effects, sequencing depth variability, or time-of-day
effects during sample processing) [58] [49].
Overdispersion can reflect unobserved variability in the data that cannot be fully explained by
the modeled rates λij. For example, cells processed in different batches will show systematic
differences in gene expression levels unrelated to biological variation, or stochastic sampling
effects during sequencing will inflate variability. Failing to account for these effects can lead to
the model fitting to noise and systemic differences rather than the biological variation which we
are actually trying to represent [58] [49].
Furthermore, the count matrix is often sparse, with a large proportion of zeros due to dropout
events (where transcripts are not captured during sequencing) or simply that some genes ap-
pear extremely infrequently or only in certain cell populations. These zeros may however not
always represent true biological absence but rather technical artifacts, as the expression of
genes in a cell can almost be seen as a Monte Carlo approximation from the underlying dis-
tribution of the genes expression in a given cell. This sparsity motivates the incorporation of
zero-inflation into the model, allowing for a flexible treatment of zero entries as arising from

3.2.4.1
Relation to the SBM Framework
In a standard Stochastic Block Model (SBM), each node is assigned to a discrete block, and
edges form with block-specific probabilities [6]. In contrast, the LDM assigns each cell and gene
to a position in a continuous latent space, with distances dictating the existance and magnitude
of expression. By subsequently applying hierarchical clustering to the learned latent positions,
we effectively recover a “block-like” structure. Nodes that lie close in the latent space form clus-
ters with higher intra-cluster rates than inter-cluster rates. Thus, this approach can be viewed
as a continuous analogue to the discrete SBM, preserving the key property that intra-block
connectivity higher than connectivity between different blocks.

3.4
Model Evaluation
To evaluate our model performance we employ several approaches, some of which are standard
graph evaluation metrics and others more tied to the problem-specific biology. As discussed
previously, there is no real ground truth for some of the attributes we are trying to predict, such
as cell type annotation. As a consequence the evaluation of such attributes will air to the side
of qualitative evaluation.
3.4.1
Link Prediction
Link prediction is mathematically formulated as predicting the likelihood P(e(u, v)) of an edge
e existing between two nodes u and v in a graph. For our bipartite graph, where u represents
cells and v represents genes, the task is reduced to determining whether a given gene v ∈V2
is expressed in a cell u ∈V1. Positive edges represent observed expressions, while negative
edges are sampled from unconnected cell-gene pairs.
To evaluate the performance of the model, we use ROC-AUC, which captures the trade-off
between true positive and false positive rates, and PR-AUC, which is particularly suited for
imbalanced datasets. Additionally, Micro F1 scores evaluate performance across all edges,
offering a holistic view of the model’s predictive ability. The threshold for the precision-recall
ratio is chosen to maximize the Micro F1 score. These metrics provide a robust assessment of
the model’s ability to generalize.
Our evaluation involves splitting the dataset into training and testing sets, withholding a 10%
subset of edges for testing. Negative edges are sampled randomly from non-existing edges,
while ensuring an equal class distribution.

3.4.2
Node Classfication
Node classification is a machine learning task aimed at predicting the labels or categories of
nodes in a graph based on their features and/or the graph’s structure.
In our case we do not handle node-based features since we consider gene counts from cells as
weighted edges and therefore represent all features from a cell as one position in a latent space.
For node classification we only consider cells and not genes, although they both have a learned
latent position. The point of interest is to infer biological functions of the cells and thereby a cell
type annotation, with no clear classification task for genes other than what is already covered
by Link Prediction.
As touched upon in Section 2.4, we do not consider the reference annotation as a ground truth.
Our evaluation approach for classification is therefore less orthodox and involves correcting
or denoising the reference annotation based on the learned representation. It contains the
following steps:
1. Extract the latent representations zi for each cell
2. Overlay the reference annotations in for each cell
3. Perform kNN leave-one-out in the latent space to correct the label for each cell
Following this procedure we will have two annotations, a reference, and a corrected version
based on the latent space. By comparing these, we can quantify the discrepancy and therefore
the level of disagreement between the annotations.

3.4.7
Pareto Task Inference
We consider Pareto Task Inference (ParTI) as introduced in Hart et al. 2015 [68] to assign
functional biological task to the archetypes identified using the AA model presented earlier.
Although AA in of itself is a dimensionality reducing technique, the components will still contain
Towards Precision Immunoprofiles Using Graph Representation Learning for Modeling Single-Cell RNA-Seq Data
25
==PAGE:36==
the same amount of features as we original data. Since our features are genes expression,
approximately 30.000, our archetypes will also have this dimensionality.
ParTI seeks to identify functionally important features for each archetype, i.e. what genes are
most enriched near an archetype, compared to the rest of the population. This is acheived in
the following way:
1. Each datapoint (cell) is binned into bins of equal size based on distance to the archetype.
2. For each bin, the enrichment is calculated as the median value of the bin compared to the
median value of all other bins.
3. The enrichment of a feature in bin 1 (the bin closest to an archetype) is used for continous
significance test using the Mann-Whitney U test
4. Then false discovery rate (FDR) is applied as we are tested to control for multiple hypoth-
esis testing.
5. A feature is only considered defining for an archetype if the maximal enrichment occurs
in the bin 1. For this beta distributions are fitted to each bin and the probability of a peak
in bin 1 is computed.
The result is a list for significant genes for each archetype with accompanying enrichment, p-
valus for the feature peaking in an archetype. This can be used to describe biological tasks.
To get an even more aggregate view of biological function, pathways can be used instead of
pure gene expression, using the same apporach as above.

5
Discussion
In this thesis we set out to apply GRL and AA to obtain unsupervised representations of scRNA-
seq data to reduce bias and enhance understading of disease pathways in autoimmune dis-
eases such as PsO and PsA. The following discussion will consider how these tools can be
used and improved in the analysis of scRNA-seq data, as well as limitations and proposals for
future work.
5.1
Latent Representation: Interpretability and Applications
The results from the ZIPLDM-RE offer a high-resolution view into the underlying structure of
single-cell data for PsO and PsA. By embedding both cells and genes in a shared low-dimensional
space, the model attempts to capture and represent functional biological relationships. We show
that adding problem specific model parameters to the standard PLDM, such as random effects
and zero-inflation, reflected strongly on performance in Link Prediction tasks and, especially, in
rate estimation of gene expression providing a better representation of the data. Our approach
demonstrated the best representation of cells in 32 dimensions, however even in ultra-low di-
mensions, the model exhibited impressive initial performance. Link prediction performed so
well, that the π parameter might be able to correct for technical dropout event, however that is
to be explored further.
5.1.1
Annotation Denoising
The latent representation provide functional relationtships between cells based on distance.
Cell annotations also provide function based groupings for cells, and are often critical for down-
stream analyses, as mechanistic insights frequently depend on isolating specific subpopulations
to detect important patterns [65].
While most scRNA-seq literature relies on reference-based annotation obtained through rigor-
ous analysis of a single or mutiple datasets, this widely adopted approach has several limita-
tions. First, reference-based annotation does not allow for the discovery of novel cell types,
states, or functions, as it assumes a predefined set of cell types. Second, it is inherently static,
anchoring cell expression profiles to the dataset from which they were derived, and therefore
cannot account for systemic variations such as disease states in new data or variability intro-
duced by different sequencing methods. Third, reference-based annotations are typically gen-
erated through computational pipelines that involve normalization, dimensionality reduction, and
multiple similarity steps to infer cell-to-cell relationships, ultimately relying on differential expres-
sion analysis to identify known biomarkers for annotation. This apporach can lead to overfitting
on to the reference dataset and introduce bias and noise into the cell clusters [13].
We provided a procedure for mitigating the above concerns by denoising annotations based on
the unsupervised latent representation of the ZIPLDM-RE model. It showed a significant dis-
agreement between the reference and denoised annotations with an F1-score of 0.854. Further
investigation revealed that the majority of the denoising happened in a few cell-types that often
Towards Precision Immunoprofiles Using Graph Representation Learning for Modeling Single-Cell RNA-Seq Data
47
==PAGE:58==
was a part of the same supertype, however cross supertype migration did also occur.
Using SCPA as a benchmark revealed that the denoised annotation led to more detected sig-
nificant pathways and higher q-values accross cell types. This was surprising, as the kNN with
k = 10 remained fixed regardless of the population size of cell types. A more adaptive and cau-
tious approach could involve adjusting k based on the population size of the specific cell type,
since smaller cell populations may be more sensitive to the choice of k. Moreover, if the kNN
probability falls below a certain threshold, it could be prudent for the denoising to preserve the
original observation, however that would need further exploration.
While basing evaluation on SCPA is not a definitive measure of annotation quality, it serves as a
useful proxy for evaluating this challenging task, particularly given the author’s limited expertise
in biology.
This approach, while valuable, does have its limitations. For instance, similar cell types often
exhibit comparable pathways in response to treatment [70], which could result in q-values not
reflecting wrongly corrected labels.
Additionally, while q-values provide a measure of statistical certainty, they do not always cor-
respond directly to biological relevance or importance. Annotations with high q-values may not
necessarily highlight the most biologically meaningful pathways, leading to a potential overem-
phasis on statistical significance rather than biological relevance. Incorporating log-fold change
(LFC) could address some of these concerns, as it is more directly related to biological function.
Ultimately, more biological expertise is needed to evaluate and assess robustness of the de-
noising procedure presented in this paper.
5.1.2
Treatment Response Insights (NRPs vs. RPs)
One of the more notable observations from the latent representation was that NRPs displayed
a higher JS-divergence between baseline and follow-up. This higher divergence suggests that
NRPs may have more pronounced shifts in their immune profiles compared to responders. In
other words, the pronounced latent space response in NRPs may reflect a negative reaction to
treatment, driven by persistent or worsening pathological processes, unlike the modest changes
seen in responders.
Interestingly, patient 8 remains an outlier, showing minimal latent space changes despite being
categorized as a NRP. Notably this patient also had the lowest DAPSA scores at baseline and
was the only patient that had an increased follow-up score.
The difference in treatment response and different immunoprofile reaction in NRPs, might point
to the existence of subtypes within PsO and PsA, where distinct immunological or genetic pro-
files could result in differential responses to treatment [71].
Nonetheless, it further supports the potential of latent representation approach, revealing that it
can distinguish cells not only on heterogenous supertypes, but also on closely related subtypes
48

and treatment response.
5.1.3
Perspectives on Hierarchical Clustering
Since we argue that the latent representaion is good at distinguishing treatment response and
cell subtypes, using hierarchical clustering directly in the latent space, shows promise as a
foundation for novel cell type discovery. While not thoroughly investigated in this thesis, sev-
eral approaches could be viable. One approach could be to employ hierarchical clustering
on already identified cell types e.g. CD4 Naive, in an effort to further identify subpopulations
in an unsupervised manner. In a scenario with no prior annotation for the data, hierarchical
clustering could even be used to identify the initial cell groups, as we show stark similarities
between etablished cell types and the hierarchical clustering in Figure 4.5. This could in theory
act as a completely unsupervised approach to cell clustering with no risk of introducing bias
on predefined biomarkers for specific cell populations, while avoiding explicit normalizations,
dimentionality reduction and deriving a cell-to-cell relations. However, labeling these clusters
would still be a challenge without established markers.
5.2
AA & Identifying Biological Drivers
As expected, both BAA and PAA applied to all cell types in the MTX baseline data, capture the
heterogeneity among different cell types rather than signals indicative of treatment response.
These archetypes effectively capture the intrinsic variability across the different cell types, high-
lighting that the primary sources of heterogeneity arise from cellular differences in types rather
than treatment-induced changes or patient-to-patient variability.
By utilizing the results from the SCPA, we guided the PAA to analyze cell groups with high
change in biological function after exposure to MTX. Surprisingly, PAA successfully distin-
guishes treatment response profiles in both CD4 Naive and CD8 Naive cells at baseline, despite
the absence of any observable clinical differences between RPs and NRPs prior to treatment.
This highlights that removing heterogeneity from broad cell types allowed for other patterns to
arise, further cementing the importance accurate cell type groupings.
Measuring a z-score in the archetype allowed us to identify the genes driving biological function
in archetypes compared to the rest of the cell population. The z-scores also hinted at functional
differences in immunoprofiles at the archetypes. In cases where treatment response clusters
strongly at specific archetypes, these archetypes may help explain if and why a PsO/PsA patient
does not respond to MTX. However, the mean/variance assumption for the z-scores is likely not
upheld, as explored earlier. More robust methods analogues to z-scores are needed for more
reliable in depth analysis.
The differences in treatment response explored in this thesis also highlights that individual dis-
ease trajectories may not always align neatly with the standard DAPSA-based categorization
for PsA. While the DAPSA score is clinically practical and assists in identifying clinical response
rates, it does not account for the heterogeneity of immune profiles in psoriatic disease that may
Towards Precision Immunoprofiles Using Graph Representation Learning for Modeling Single-Cell RNA-Seq Data

critically influence treatment response trajectories.
Considering the pathway enrichment identified by SCPA reveal that pathways related to transla-
tion and metabolism are strongly downregulated in NRPs when exposed to MTX treatment. By
comparing archetype-specific gene expression to the global average, we identified genes that
stand out as potential functional drivers within each archetype at baseline. In CD4 Naive and
CD8 Naive cells, genes associated with translation, ribosomal activity, and protein synthesis
are more highly expressed in within-celltype archetypes linked to NRPs.
These patterns of gene-level signals may reflect hyperactivated or stress-related states within
NRPs immune populations more so than in RPs. This could indicate that the oxidative and
metabolic stress induced by the MTX treatment overwhelms cells that are already at high stress
level at baseline, forcing them to shut down part of their transtional output to save resources.
This would be in line with current views on how cells react to excessive stress [72].
5.3
Methodological Considerations & Limitations
A notable feature of our approach is that we directly model scRNA-seq data without any prepro-
cessing or normalization steps. Typically, normalization is a crucial step in scRNA-seq analysis
due to variability in sequencing depth and technical noise, and developing robust normalization
methods is an active research area in the field [49]. However, our modeling approach inherently
accounts for normalization by assuming a Poisson distribution for the observed counts, where
the rate parameter λ reflects the expected expression levels of a gene in a cell. This allows us
to bypass explicit normalization procedures, as the Poisson framework implicitly handles vari-
ability through the Poisson Log-Likelihood. Additionally, random effects in our model effectively
account for the technical variation commonly observed in scRNA-seq data, with zero-inflation
showing statistical support for inclusion in our model, further ensuring robustness without re-
quiring external normalization steps.
For the PLDM models deployed in this thesis, our current modeling of rates assumes that the
baseline expression rate is determined solely by Euclidean distances in the latent space and
random effects. Incorporating an explicit intercept term, β, could refine the model, especially
for the PLDM without random effects, which is highly constrained to produce rates in the range
[0,1], as exp(−0) = 1 which is the maximum value λ can take in equation 3.6. The global param-
eter β would act as a baseline expression rate in the latent space, which could allow the random
effects to only handle technical variability, as they are currently also responsible for increasing
the expression rates for genes in cell that are expressed above 1 in PLDM-RE and ZIPLDM-RE.
Another modeling choice relates to the choice of distribution. The negative binomial distribution
is often used when modeling scRNA-seq data [73]. It closely relates to the Poisson distribution
used in this thesis but includes an extra parameter, r, to handle dispersion. This allows the
negative binomial to accommodate the overdispersion commonly observed in scRNA-seq data,
where the variance exceeds the mean. It is also frequently paired with zero-inflation to model
the excess zero counts in scRNA-seq datasets [14].
50
Towards Precision Immunoprofiles Using Graph Representation Learning for Modeling Single-Cell RNA-Seq Data
==PAGE:61==
We did not adopt a ZINB (Zero-Inflated Negative Binomial) approach because the negative bi-
nomial distribution does not align with our methodology. Specifically, we model each gene count
within a cell as an independent Poisson distribution, making it impractical to estimate the dis-
persion parameter at such a granular level. Additionally, a combination of Poisson distributions
should sufficiently capture the global overdispersion typically addressed by a ZINB model [74].
This is because the Poisson mean-variance relationship is inherently tied to each observation
in the count matrix and not the the overdispersion seen at an aggregated level.
Our implementation of Archetypal Analysis does currently not take into account the exces of
zeros in scRNA-seq data.
While the time constraints of this thesis did not allow us to ex-
plore an explicit parameterization approach for modeling zero-inflation, other methods, such
as AANet [54] and scAAnet [53], employ deep learning to address non-linearity, heterogeneity,
and zero-inflation. They use an autoencoder to infer a latent archetypal space where they inde-
tify archetypes. They seem to provide a promising approach to AA for scRNA-seq data when
compared to standard approaches.
Both ZIPLDM-RE and Poisson AA demonstrated robustness across various random initializa-
tions. Evaluation metric errors for ZIPLDM-RE consistently ranged between 10-3 and 10-4,
except for MSE, which was higher due to its quadratic nature. For PAA, NMI exhibited some
variation depending on the subset of data used, but it generally remained high, despite the lack
of an initialization method. To improve the consistency of PAA, especially in cases where noisy
data could neutralize the benefits of initialization methods such as FurthestFirst and Furthest-
Sum [61], it is crucial to explore and identify robust initialization techniques. Although invariant
representations between the two models cannot be mathematically guaranteed, both models
displayed significant robustness and stability across multiple runs, with ZIPLDM-RE showing
particularly strong robustness.
In this thesis, we demonstrated that combining GRL and AA can offer new insights into the
structure and function of scRNA-seq data in psoriatic disease. By embedding both cells and
genes into a shared latent space, our ZIPLDM-RE approach effectively bypasses many typical
preprocessing steps and pitfalls while capturing intricate relationships that standard pipelines
may overlook or distort. Importantly, the latent representation enabled us to denoise reference-
based annotations and identify previously unrecognized differences between responders and
non-responders to MTX treatment. AA, when guided by pathway enrichment analysis, demon-
strated the ability to distinguish between treatment responses even before treatment adminis-
tration, effectively uncovering response differences within specific cell populations in PsO/PsA
patients.

5.4.1
Refined Denoising and Annotation
The proposed denoising procedure highlighted the potential pitfalls of relying on static, reference-
based annotations by revealing substantial shifts in cell annotations. However, several practical
improvements remain open. An adaptive nearest-neighbor approach, where k is scaled based
on cell type population size, may yield even more reliable denoising outcomes. Additionally,
employing probabilistic thresholds, such that annotations remain unchanged if neighborhood
confidence is low, could further mitigate overcorrection. These refinements, combined with
deeper biological expertise, could help validate whether altered annotations consistently align
with meaningful differences in cellular function.
5.4.2
Hierarchical Clustering in Latent Space
As shown in Figure 4.5, the latent space exhibits strong alignment with known cell types, sug-
gesting that it could support purely unsupervised clustering. A fruitful direction would be to apply
hierarchical clustering methods directly in the ZIPLDM-RE latent space for novel cell-type dis-
covery, either starting from broad cell classifications (e.g., T cells) or from scratch, completely
bypassing reliance on previously established markers. This could reduce biases arising from
predefined reference panels and potentially uncover previously unrecognized subtypes.
5.4.3
Further Archetypal Analysis Extensions
While PAA successfully detected treatment-response patterns within subsets of cells, it did not
explicitly account for zero-inflation. Deep-learning-based methods like AANet or scAAnet have
shown promise in modeling nonlinearities and excess zeros, potentially offering a more nuanced
capture of scRNA-seq heterogeneity. Future work should explore integrating zero-inflation or
autoencoder-based approaches into the PAA approach.
5.4.4
Improved Initialization Strategies
Both ZIPLDM-RE and PAA showed consistency in their representations across diverse random
initializations; however, robust initialization remains critical to ensuring model stability, espe-
cially with noisy or highly heterogeneous data. Future extensions could focus on tailored strate-
gies such as FurthestFirst, FurthestSum, or domain-specific heuristics designed to select initial
archetypes/cell embeddings that are both representative and robust.
5.4.5
Biological Validation and Clinical Translation
Finally, while pathway enrichment metrics (e.g., q-values) serve as a convenient proxy for bio-
logical relevance, more refined analyses are needed to confirm whether these statistical mea-
sures correspond to true mechanistic importance. Furthermore, expert biological knowledge
is essential to contextualize these results and validate their relevance in disease specific sce-
narios. For AA, the ParTi approach outlined in section 3.4.7 would greatly enhance the inter-
pretability of the archetypes, being that it serves as a more robust and conservative approach
than the z-scores. It would be especially useful the integrate the MSigDB of pathways to tie
each archetype more to overall biological task function rather than relying on enrichment at the
gene level. The Pareto optimality assumption also ensures that task function is only assigned
to archetypes if the task peaks at the archetype preventing false positive tass assignment.

Conclusion
This thesis investigated advanced computational methods for analyzing single-cell RNA se-
quencing (scRNA-seq) data to address the complexities of autoimmune diseases such as Pso-
riasis (PsO) and Psoriatic Arthritis (PsA). The proposed framework integrates two distinct yet
complementary models, Zero-Inflated Poisson Latent Distance Models (ZIPLDMs) and Pois-
son Archetypal Analysis (PAA), to uncover biologically meaningful patterns, improve annotation
quality, and explore treatment responses.
The ZIPLDM framework models cell-gene interactions in a shared low-dimensional latent space,
capturing complex relationships while accounting for the sparsity and zero-inflation that char-
acterize scRNA-seq data. The incorporation of random effects and problem-specific parame-
ters significantly enhances performance in tasks such as Link Prediction and rate estimation
of gene expressions. This makes the ZIPLDM framework particularly effective in representing
structures in scRNA-seq data that reflect biologically and clinically relevant variability, such as
the differential treatment response of PsO/PsA patients. Moreover, the latent representation
generated by the ZIPLDM demonstrates its ability to denoise reference annotations, reducing
biases introduced by predefined annotaitions.
In tandem, PAA serves as an exploratory tool for capturing high-level variability and heterogene-
ity within scRNA-seq data. By analyzing gene expression patterns, PAA identifies archetypes
that represent distinct cellular states and functions. When guided by Single Cell Pathway Analy-
sis (SCPA), the PAA framework provides a nuanced view of biological processes within specific
cell populations. For example, examining immune cell subsets revealed archetypes linked to
treatment response, highlighting potential functional drivers of non-response or poor outcomes
in PsO/PsA on MTX treatment.
Together, these models complement each other by addressing different aspects of single-cell
data analysis. ZIPLDM excels in constructing functional latent representations of cells, while
PAA identifies boundaries and archetypes that reveal distinct cell states and functions that could
drive subtypes of PsO/PsA. Their combination demonstrates the potential for more comprehen-
sive and flexible approaches to understanding cellular heterogeneity and disease mechanisms
in autoimmune disorders.
This work lays a foundation for future extensions, such as exploring more specialized initializa-
tion strategies, more robust model selection, or explicit zero-inflation in PAA. While focusing on
PsO and PsA, the methods are theoretically agnostic to the source of tissue, allowing broader
applicability in scRNA-seq studies across diverse diseases and biological systems. By enhanc-
ing interpretability of scRNA-seq data, this thesis contributes to the growing field of precision
medicine, offering tools to bridge the gap between single-cell data analysis and actionable clin-
ical strategies.
