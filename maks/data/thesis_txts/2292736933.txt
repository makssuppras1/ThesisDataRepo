1 Introduction 
1.1 Sustainable development, Life-cycle assessment 
 
 The urban population went from representing 34% of the total global population in 1960 to 54% in 
2014 (WHO). Around the world, cities are confronted with the challenge of meeting the needs of this 
increasing urban population. This increases the pressure on the supply of energy, water, food , which 
are major services a city has to deploy to sustain its population. At the same time, global awareness 
of the need for a switch to a sustainable development has been taken place these last decades with, 
among others, the Rio Earth Summit Agenda 21 actions signed by 179 countries, including France, 
and the 1994 Aalborg Charter. Sustainable urban development has to take into account not only 
environmental aspects, but also social and economic aspects. And the large variety of environmental 
problems and a city’s activities effects on the environment alone render decision-making on urban 
development difficult. To support local public policies and actions that transcend traditional national 
top-down planning, the environmental impacts from urban systems have to be comprehensively and 
consistently quantified.  
Life-cycle assessment (LCA) can be a powerful tool for such purposes, allowing a holistic 
environmental impact assessment of a given socio-technical system. LCA adopts a life-cycle 
perspective, quantifying all relevant environmental impacts from the extraction of the raw materials 
through the production and use/operations of the system to its final disposal stage. 
1.2 Bibliography / State-of-the art 
1.2.1 Urban  LCA studies 
 
To this date, few LCA studies have been performed on a city and/or territory scale.  
 
Among those, Goldstein(2012) investigated the application of the LCA framework combined 
with urban metabolism on five case cities around the world. In this study, the systems studied 
were the cities as a whole, represented by their annual metabolic activities, contrary to this 
project were only the energy systems are examined. 
 
The flows modelled were: food, water, transport fuels, energy, construction materials, metals, 
plastics, paper, glass, rubber, and electronic goods. 
 
Data came primarily from previous UM studies, then national or regional statistics scaled down 
to the city, or inferred from waste statistics. 
Both midpoint and endpoint impact potentials were assessed. Also, the results were compared 
to previous mass and energy accounts from urban metabolism methodologies.  
 
Another study made on the overall operation of a territory has been reported by Loiseau(2013) 
made by Siquiera Lemos(2011) on a Portuguese territory. 
 
Others focused their LCA studies on sub-systems of a territory 
 
For instance, Saner(2013) worked on the housing and mobility demands of individual 
households of a small village in Switzerland. The data was mostly resulting from advanced 
modelisation using statistics of population, climate data, and statistics. The application of the 
methodologies used in this study, although fitted to the goal of that paper, will likely proved 
==PAGE:2==
to be difficult at a larger scale. In fact, the inventory for the village is an aggregation of house-
scaled inventory. Moreover, the study focuses only on GHGs emissions, thus not utilizing LCA 
to its fullest potential/missing to use one the advantages of using LCA. 
 
Ramaswami(2008) also focused on GHG accounting for the city of Denver, Colorado from 
direct energy use, airline travel, and embodied energy of key urban materials such as food 
concrete, water. LCA was used to quantify the latter while the American Environment 
Protection Agency protocol for greenhouse gas emission accounting was used along with EIO 
tables. 
 
Loiseau(2013) also mentions Björklund(2010) ‘s paper where he applied LCA to evaluate 
different energy resources management scenarios in Sweden municipalities and Lundie & 
all(2004) who worked on Sidney’s water systems. 
Few studies have been found who used LCA to assess the impact of a city’s metabolic activities, and 
most of them used LCA partially: either using the concept behind LCA for inventory purposes and/or 
accounting for GHG’s emissions. 
 
 
1.2.2 Methodological considerations on the application of LCA on an urban scale 
 
The use of the LCA framework in the urban sustainability context is recent and still little developed. 
While studies used selected aspects of LCA on case studies to account for the upstream processes 
involved in the urban life mechanisms, the LCA framework is rarely applied as such. Indeed, some 
changes have to be implemented in order to obtain adequate and relevant results for environmental 
planning decisions/monitoring.  
Thoughts have been conducted in that direction. Chester M. (2010) suggested an hybrid approach 
combining urban metabolism to LCA divided into six steps from the identification of urban metabolism 
indicators of interest, followed by system boundary selection and establishing sustainability indicators. 
The steps left consists in conducting the usual stages of an LCA study. Goldstein( 2012) also crafted an 
hybrid UM-LCA framework and used EIO-LCA for missing data. Loiseau and all(2012) acknowledged the 
difficulties of directly applying the LCA framework as a land planning decision tool and highlighted the 
need for a standardized methodology for urban LCA. Four methodological issues were identified and 
they suggested methodological improvements for each of them: functional unit definition, system 
boundaries, data collection, the local context representation. Loiseau and all focused on adapting the 
LCA framework for the purpose of Strategic environmental assessment. For such use, they suggested 
that the results of this revised LCA tool are to be divided following two criteria. The indicators resulting 
from the study can be first divided into direct or indirect impacts to differentiate between the impacts 
resulting from activities occurring inside the territory and those occurring outside the spatial territory 
under study.  Along with the potential environmental impacts, the paper suggested the use of a land 
use functions vector as an indicator of the environmental, social and economic functions provided by 
the system under study.

From the bibliography research carried out for this project, it appears that no study on the energy

 Moreover, both midpoint and endpoint impact potential categories will be used, extending beyond

The system definition being a sensitive step of the LCA approach, the project will aim at defining the

study under a LCA software, SimaPro and the processes from ecoinvent that one could employ to 
represent it. 
 
1.3 Relevance of the thesis 
This aim of this project is to investigate the possibility of applying LCA to an urban-scaled energy 
system and to prospect what difficulties one might encounter doing so. 
It will also provide a first evaluation of Paris energy systems environmental impacts over from the 
extraction of raw material to the distribution to the end-user. Moreover, potential impacts other 
than on climate change will be estimated

2.7 LCI modelling framework and handling of multifunctional processes 
 
For decision context C1, the ILCD handbook (EU, 2010) recommends using the attribution principle as 
LCI modelling framework. 
The attributional modelling in LCA is also known as “descriptive” LCA as it describes the potential 
environmental impacts that can be imputed to a system over its life cycle while consequential 
modelling’s goal is to identify the consequences of the decisions made on a process on other systems 
( other processes, the economy, markets…) Average or generic data is generally used when the 
products originate from a mix of producers or technologies as it is the case for the electricity and 
heat fed to an urban area. 
Some of the processes describing our system are multifunctional, i.e. they provide more than one 
valuable output. They are mainly the co-generation of heat and electricity processes. In attributional 
modelling, the ILCD handbook recommends subdivision or virtual subdivision til only single operation 
unit processes are obtained. Here, it is not possible, the system expansion approach is then applied 
here, in accordance with ISO 14044.  
As the ecoinvent processes are employed in the modelling of the energy systems under study, some 
transgressions to the LCI modelling framework had to be made. In fact, in Ecoinvent, the 
consequential system model is the only option applying system expansion to multi-product datasets.  
While the data for consequential processes are of the marginal nature, this option was chosen as it  
complies with the ISO requirements. Furthermore, as the data entered in the process in order to 
model the energy systems of Paris and the Ile-de-France region were averaged data, the notion of 
average data in attributional modelling is secured. 
As stressed by Schmidt(2013), the attributional modelling as it is in Ecoinvent may render a 
“misleading” recommendation support as it includes constrained supplier, thus being not 
representative of historical investments in the capital goods or the limitation in the energy supply. 
In case of a multifunctional process, mainly co-generation of electricity and heat, system expansion 
was used using the most probable alternative of providing the secondary functions.

situation.

3.3 Hypothesis  
3.3.1 Electricity 
The theoretical mix has been implemented in SImapro with slight modifications. For instance, most 
of the co-generation plants in France produce medium voltage electricity1. That’s why co-generated 
electricity was not taken into account in the national mix. Moreover, several source of energy for the 
electricity production were not taken into account because not identifiable or could not be described 
properly from the Ecoinvent processes. The sum of those accounted for less than 0.7% of the total 
national production, the exclusion of those is thus consistent with the cut-off criteria. The 
pourcentages of each remaining primary energy have then been recalculated. 
Information about the origin of the biogas was found, but the decision was made to take the  
ecoinvent process process “Biowaste  treatment of manure and by anaerobic digestion | Conseq, U” 
It was also assumed that all the electricity consumed in Paris and IDF is low voltage. 
3.3.2 Heat 
A generic heat production process has been created for the representing the average national heat in 
order to customize some of the electricity production processes. As natural gas has the largest share 
in the national French heat mix (44%) by far2, the process is based on “Heat, district or industrial, 
natural gas {Europe without Switzerland}| market for heat, district or industrial, natural gas | 
Conseq, U”. 
In order to calculate the different share of each primary energy in the heat mixes considered for 
Paris, it was assumed that every primary energy consumption mentioned in  “Bilan énergétique 
Paris”(CEREN,2009) other than the “electricity” share was used for heating purposes.

5 Discussion  
 
One of the goals of this LCA report is to identify “environmental hotspots” .  
The heat consumption in Paris is the most obvious point to start with. Although it’s the 
predominance of its contribution in the impacts differ from one method to the other, the large use of 
natural gas in individual or small scale boilers has significant consequences on the city’s 
environmental impacts. The district heating system can gain to be generalize, starting with the 
tertiary sector, which is mainly consist in offices. It would probably help to reduce Paris’s score in 
freshwater eco-toxicity. 
As regards to the electricity systems, the regional electricity mix which mostly uses fossil fuels 
impacts on Paris energy systems environmental performances. A development of renewable energy 
sources might render better environmental scores. 
No critical difference was observe between the sectors that were studied here. It might be 
interesting to refine the investigation to smaller categories such as: types of dwellings, economic 
activity for instance. 
As mentioned in some LCA papers( Goldstein,2013) spatial differentiation is lacking is LCA nowadays 
and adding spatial information, such as the localisation of the plants in our case,  could yield 
information on the local environmental impacts they entail.  
In fact, the results yield in this study could be refined. It would be interesting to take into account the 
actual technology used, at least in the region, the transport routes of the fossil fuels used to produce 
heat and energy, and to account for the actual state of the network distribution ( heat losses, 
length…). 
The collection of data for a study on energy systems on a urban scale is cumbersome. Even is data on 
energy consumption and production is more easier to find now than 10 years before, they are not 
precise enough to correctly depict the system. The information needed exists, but are often 
protected by commercial confidentiality. 
6 Conclusion 
 
The energy system of Paris is bound to a rapid growth in the years to follow with the “Grand Paris” 
development project with a 20% increase in energy consumption from 2011 to 20303 and new 
infrastructures are to be built to accommodate the changes. Another LCA study would be beneficial 
for such projects. 
The application of LCA on an urban scale can be used as a decision tool but is not a research tool as 
applied in this paper. More refinement might lead to better results of course. And the inclusion of 
the energy consumption for the urban transport scheme would lead to more relevant results and 
conclusions
