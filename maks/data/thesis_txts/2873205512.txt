Summary
This thesis investigates how the spatial distribution of workplaces and residences shapes
commuting behaviors in Denmark by disentangling geographical constraints from intrin-
sic behavioral preferences. Using a comprehensive dataset of over 77 million home-work
location pairs spanning multiple decades, a novel framework [BML24] based on the pair
distribution function is applied to capture the commuting behavior preferences indepen-
dently of spatial arrangement of locations.
By normalizing observed commuting distance distributions with the pair distribution
function, an intrinsic distance cost function is extracted, revealing a universal, geography-
independent scaling law. This intrinsic cost follows a robust piecewise powerlaw decay
across distances from approximately 10 meters up to 500 kilometers, exhibiting distinct
regimes for intra-city and inter-city commuting. The lower decay exponent characterizes
shorter, urban trips, while the steeper exponent applies to longer, inter-city commutes,
with a transition aligned to typical city radii.
Analysis across demographic groups, employment types, and geographic subdivisions
confirms the universality of this scaling behavior, with variations in parameter values
but consistent piecewise form. Notably, the Copenhagen metropolitan area exhibits a
distinct pattern where the first power-law regime dominates almost the entire distance
range, reflecting its dense urban fabric and integrated commuter structure.
The results provide strong empirical evidence for a universal behavioral scaling law
governing commuting distances, independent of the underlying spatial distribution of
residences and workplaces. This insight offers a novel perspective on commuting model-
ing by clearly separating geography-driven opportunity effects from intrinsic commuter
preferences.
The findings have practical relevance for urban transport planning and
policy by informing models that predict commuting flows and infrastructure demand
under varying urban forms and socio-demographic conditions. Furthermore, the distinct
metropolitan pattern observed in Copenhagen highlights the need for tailored modeling
approaches in dense urban regions, underscoring the complex interplay between urban
structure and mobility behavior.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Commuting is a fundamental aspect of human mobility, constituting one of the most
regular and quantitatively significant forms of spatial behavior in modern societies. Daily
travel between home and workplace accounts for a substantial proportion of total trips
within urban and regional transport systems [Jan93; VBL21] and plays a critical role
in shaping infrastructure demand, land use patterns, and environmental impact [Hor04].
Beyond its infrastructural implications, commuting length and time have been shown
to influence individual well-being, modal choices, work-life balance, and labor market
accessibility [Cha+20; SF08]. As such, commuting patterns not only reflect urban spatial
structure but are also closely linked to socio-economic and psychological dimensions
of everyday life. Understanding the mechanisms underpinning commuting behavior is
therefore essential for developing effective transport planning, urban policy, and mobility
theory.
A major challenge in analyzing commuting behavior lies in disentangling the roles of spa-
tial constraints and individual preferences. Traditional models of human mobility, such
as distance-decay or gravity-based frameworks, often embed assumptions about the cost
of distance without explicitly separating these from the influence of geography [GJ80;
HFM21]. In response to this limitation, a recent study by Boucherie et al. [BML24],
titled Decomposing geographical and universal aspects of human mobility, introduced a
novel framework based on the pair distribution function. Adapted from techniques in
statistical physics, the pair distribution function quantifies the distribution of all possible
origin-destination pairs as a function of distance, thus capturing the spatial configuration
of opportunities in a region. By comparing the empirical distribution of trips with this
geographical baseline, the authors identified an intrinsic distance cost function, which
represents the normalized probability of travel as a function of distance, after removing
the confounding effect of spatial structure.
This framework offers a powerful methodological contribution by isolating behavioral
preferences from the spatial opportunity structure. The resulting geography-normalized
mobility distribution has the potential to reveal universal features of human movement,
particularly those that remain consistent across varying geographic and socio-economic
contexts. Although demonstrated on generalized human mobility datasets, this approach
has not yet been fully applied to commuting specifically, despite commuting being among
the most structured and policy-relevant forms of everyday mobility.

The objective of this thesis is to apply and extend the framework introduced by Boucherie
et al. in the context of commuting behavior. Using high-resolution data on residential
and workplace locations across Denmark, this research will construct the pair distribu-
tion function for all possible home-work location pairs, derive the empirical commuting
trip distance distribution, and compute the intrinsic commuting attractiveness as a
function of distance. By normalizing the observed trip distribution with the underlying
spatial structure, the study aims to extract a commuting-specific intrinsic cost function
that captures distance preferences independent of urban geography. This contribution
will help to clarify the extent to which commuting behavior in Denmark is shaped by
physical layout versus behavioral or systemic preferences, and it will provide new insights
that can inform both theoretical models of human mobility and practical applications
in urban transport planning.

2.1
Commuting as a Distinct Yet Dominant
Form of Human Mobility
Commuting refers to regularly recurring travel between a place of residence and a work-
place or educational institution.
This repetitive movement distinguishes commuting
from other types of human mobility, such as leisure trips, irregular shopping errands,
migration or relocation. While work-related commuting is most common, the definition
can be expanded to any regularly repeated travel between fixed locations.
Commuting exhibits temporal and spatial regularity. Trips tend to occur at similar times
and follow set routes, making them distinguishable from other mobility forms with irregu-
lar timing and destinations [Kun+13], [Pap+16]. Decisions about commuting mode and
route are often highly routinized and habitual, sometimes overriding conscious decision-
making [Gue+12]. The need to commute arises from the spatial separation of home and
workplace, deeply linked to the development of metropolitan areas, suburbanization,
and broader urban structures [Pap+16]. Unlike more autonomous forms of mobility,
commuting is shaped by job requirements, social norms, and constraints such as family
obligations and school or work schedules [Gue+12].
Commuting, particularly home-work trips, constitutes a major share of everyday move-
ments in urban areas and has a significant impact on transport systems, urban planning,
and infrastructure [Kun+13]. The importance and volume of commuting are observed
across countries, regardless of differences in transport infrastructure or stage of develop-
ment [Kun+13].
Research suggests that people across diverse contexts dedicate a relatively stable amount
of time each day to commuting, a phenomenon sometimes referred to as ”Marchetti’s
constant” [Mar94]. While commute durations fluctuate by city and era due to infras-
tructural and societal variations, the general trend remains stable. It is observed that
commuting typically accounts for about 10% of working time [SD02], which corresponds
==PAGE:10==

to single trip duration of 28 minutes for an 8 hour working day. Various studies [SV00;
Hup82; Vil99] have reported the average daily travel time to be between 1.1 and 1.3
hours. Collectively, these findings support the ”constant travel time budget hypothesis”
[Kun+13] which states that, since the Neolithic era, humans consistently allocate around
one hour per day to travel, irrespective of geographic, modal, or lifestyle differences.
2.2
Decomposing Geographical and
Universal Aspects of Human Mobility:
Core Paper Review
2.2.1
Introduction
Recent work by Boucherie, Maier, and Lehmann (2024) [BML24], titled Decomposing
Geographical and Universal Aspects of Human Mobility, explores the complex interplay
between universal behavioral patterns and geographical constraints in shaping human
mobility. Their study highlights the importance of disentangling these two aspects to
gain a more nuanced understanding of how mobility emerges in diverse contexts. Since
human movement both influences and is influenced by the built environment, such in-
sights are particularly relevant for applications in urban planning, epidemiology, and
the broader social sciences.
The central research question explored in this study is:
• How do geographical constraints and intrinsic human movement choices interact
to shape patterns of human mobility?
This research aims to decompose observed human mobility into two distinct components.
The first component comprises constraints imposed by geography, including features such
as landmasses, urban structure, and the spatial distribution of settlements. The second
component consists of universal principles that are independent of geographic context
and govern intrinsic human movement choices.
Mobility studies have traditionally been shaped by two predominant theories regarding
human movement. One theory proposes that physical distance is a primary deterrent to
mobility [Car56; Wil67], a relationship commonly modeled using gravity models. In con-
trast, the alternative perspective suggests that the key factor influencing movement is
not distance itself, but rather the abundance of opportunities available in the surround-
ing area [Sto40; Nou+12]. This research addresses the longstanding debate between
==PAGE:11==
2.2 Decomposing Geographical and Universal Aspects of Human Mobility: Core Paper Review
5
these viewpoints by examining whether a unified framework can be established through
explicit consideration of geographic structure and inherent movement costs.
By isolating the effect of geography, the research seeks to uncover whether there are
consistent, universal laws that govern human mobility across different contexts and
scales, independently of local geographic features.
2.2.2
Methodology
To accomplish this, the authors utilized a comprehensive dataset comprising more than
39 million residential moves in Denmark, collected over 36 years, and distinguished by
exceptional spatial precision at the address level. The Danish registry’s near-complete
population coverage minimizes common biases associated with sampling or self-selection.
The study’s empirical scope is further extended by the inclusion of large-scale French mi-
gration data and urban mobility records from Foursquare for cities in North America and
Asia. This diversity of data sources strengthens the generalizability of the conclusions.
The core methodological innovation of the paper lies in the introduction and adaptation
of the pair distribution function. This function quantifies, for any given origin, the ex-
pected number of possible destinations at different distances, thereby capturing how the
spatial organization of settlements and urban structures shapes the raw distribution of
mobility distances. By normalizing the observed probability of moves over each distance
by the corresponding pair distribution, the authors extract the intrinsic distance cost
function. It quantifies the fundamental ”cost” or diﬀiculty associated with traveling a
distance r between two locations, independently of the spatial arrangement or geography
of those locations.
2.2.3
Results
Empirical results demonstrate that, after this normalization, the intrinsic probability of
making a move of distance (see Figure 2.1) r follows a simple inverse power law (∼1/r), a
relationship that remains robust across five orders of magnitude and in multiple national
and urban contexts (see Table 2.1). Within urban environments, a piecewise structure is
observed: for intra-city moves, the decay of movement probability is characterized by a
lower exponent (approximately 0.6), whereas for longer, inter-city moves, the exponent
increases sharply (near 2.0).
The transition between regimes aligns closely with the
empirical radius of cities, as inferred from the distribution of locations.

2.2.4
Limitations and Future Work
The scope of the study is most comprehensive for residential migration, with additional
analyses extending to short-term mobility. However, systematic commuting, specifically,
routine home-to-work movements, remains outside the immediate scope of the work,
though the authors identify it as a critical avenue for application and extension of their
methodology. At finer spatial resolutions, the results may be sensitive to how specific
locations (e.g., building centroids or door positions) are defined. Furthermore, while the
analytical models robustly account for spatial opportunity, demographic and economic
==PAGE:13==

7
drivers of movement are not explicitly modeled, particularly at the city and neighborhood
levels.
For future research, particularly in domains such as commuting, the framework offers a
rigorous tool for disentangling the effects of geography from behavioral regularities. Ap-
plying the pair distribution normalization to home–workplace data may reveal whether
the universality of the intrinsic distance cost persists in the context of routine, purpose-
driven mobility.
Extensions incorporating demographic or socio-economic variables
could further refine the explanatory power of the model, enabling a deeper understanding
of how global mobility laws interact with local heterogeneity.
In summary, the work by Boucherie et al. provides a methodological and conceptual
foundation for the study of human movement, demonstrating that after correcting for
spatial structure, global and cross-contextual regularities in mobility emerge. This con-
tribution bridges major paradigms in the field and defines a clear path for subsequent
empirical and theoretical developments in human mobility research.

CHAPTER 4
Methods
4.1
Data processing
4.1.1
Creating the Main Commuting Distance Dataset
Consecutive working periods in the workplace address dataset for each person, where the
workplace location (commune, street code, house number) remained the same and peri-
ods directly followed each other, were consolidated into a single continuous period. This
step reduces fragmentation caused by repeated yearly or monthly entries that represent
the same workplace over contiguous time intervals.
To avoid repetition caused by internal moves within the same building (e.g., changing
apartment or floor), the floor and door numbers were removed from the home address
data. Subsequently, consecutive residence periods with identical commune, street, and
house numbers were merged into single continuous periods.
Both the cleaned home and workplace address datasets were enriched with geospatial
coordinates by joining with the building location dataset using the commune, street, and
house number identifiers. The resulting east and north coordinates have an expected
spatial accuracy of ±2 meters.
The datasets for workplace and home periods were merged on person_id using an outer
join. This operation combined each residence period with all corresponding working
periods for the same individual, irrespective of possible overlaps.
To ensure that home and work locations relate to the same time frame, the merged data
were filtered so that only periods where the home and work intervals overlapped were
retained. This ensures the calculated commute distances relate to actual concurrent
living and working locations.
For each overlapping home-work period pair, the Euclidean distance between the re-
spective geospatial coordinates was computed. This distance serves as a proxy for the
commuting distance traveled by the individual.
These steps together produced the main commuting distance dataset, which captures

each person’s concurrent home and work locations along with the associated commute
distance. In total, the processed dataset contains 77 million home-work pairs and corre-
sponding commute distances for 6.3 million people. This dataset forms the basis for all

When required for specific analyses, the main commuting distance dataset was aug-

4.2
Intrinsic Distance Cost
4.2.1
Computation of the Observed Commute
Distance Distribution
The observed commute distance distribution was derived from the main commuting dis-
tance dataset, which contains paired home and workplace coordinates for individuals.
All commute distances were extracted and aggregated into a histogram representing
their empirical distribution. To capture a wide range of scales while preserving detail
at shorter distances, the histogram bins were logarithmically spaced into 100 intervals,
covering distances starting from approximately 10 meters upward. This choice of log-
arithmic binning allows for effective visualization and analysis of patterns that span
several orders of magnitude in distance.
4.2.2
Estimation of the Pair Distribution Function
Calculating the pair distribution function for the entire dataset by considering all pos-
sible distances between every home-work pair would be computationally prohibitive,
given the large number of points. Instead, the pair distribution function was approxi-
mated. Columns of work locations and home locations were separately extracted from
the dataset. The home locations were then randomly shuffled to break any temporal
or spatial pairing with the work locations, thereby simulating a spatial distribution of
locations independent of commuting behavior.
The pairwise Euclidean distances were computed between each work location and a
randomly shuffled home location. This procedure effectively estimates the spatial density
of location pairs and captures the underlying geographic structure of the set of locations,
precicely because of the large scale of the dataset (77 million rows). A histogram of these
==PAGE:18==
4.3 Powerlaw Estimations of Intrinsic Distance Cost
12
distances was then constructed using the same logarithmic binning scheme of 100 bins
starting at 10 meters, resulting in the empirical pair distribution function.
The randomization approach described above, which involves shuffling the entire set
of home locations to estimate the pair distribution function, is valid and effective for
the full, large-scale dataset. However, when analyzing filtered subsets or subgroups, for
example commuters living within a single city, the reduced sample size can make this
approach less reliable due to limited data points. In these cases, a modified method
was employed: a random sample of 2000 home locations were drawn from the subgroup
and 2,000 work locations were independently drawn from all of Denmark. All pairwise
Euclidean distances between these sampled points were computed. This sampling pro-
cedure was repeated 20 times, and the resulting distances aggregated to construct a
histogram estimation of the pair distribution function for the subgroup. This approach
balances computational feasibility with statistical representativeness when applying the
pair distribution normalization at finer spatial or demographic scales.
4.2.3
Derivation of the Intrinsic Distance Cost
The intrinsic distance cost represents the geometry-independent tendency or cost associ-
ated with traveling a given distance, disentangled from the constraints imposed by the
spatial distribution of locations. It is obtained by normalizing the observed commute
distance distribution by the pair distribution function on a bin-by-bin basis:
π(r) ∝f(r)
p(r),
where f(r) is the observed commute distance histogram value at distance r, and p(r)
is the corresponding pair distribution function value. This ratio effectively removes the
influence of geographic layout and density from the observed mobility patterns, isolating
behavioral or cost-related effects on commuting distances.
4.3
Powerlaw Estimations of Intrinsic Distance
Cost
A power law distribution describes a relationship between two quantities where one
varies as a power of the other, typically expressed as a probability distribution following
p(x) ∝x−α for x ≥xmin. The parameter xmin represents the lower bound above which
the power law behavior is assumed to hold. The scaling exponent α determines the rate of
decay and typically lies in a range greater than 1. Estimation of α is commonly performed
[CSN09] using maximum likelihood estimation, which provides statistically consistent
==PAGE:19==
4.3 Powerlaw Estimations of Intrinsic Distance Cost
13
and eﬀicient estimates without requiring data binning. Choosing an appropriate xmin is
critical to isolate the portion of the data that follows the power law.
It is known that the standard maximum likelihood estimation method for power laws
exhibits bias particularly when the exponent α is less than 1.5.
To address this, a
maximum likelihood estimator specifically developed for power laws with α < 1.5, which
incorporates a bounded power law distribution to ensure a proper fit and reduces bias
in parameter estimation [Han+17] was employed. This approach is especially relevant
in the context where geographical constraints naturally bound the range of distances.
Through this method, a more reliable estimates of the powerlaw exponent in the low-
exponent regime are obtained.

4.3.2
Practical Details of Powerlaw Fitting
The maximum likelihood estimation approaches implemented in the software packages
developed by [Han+17; Mai23b] require unbinned data as input to accurately estimate
parameters of powerlaw and piecewise powerlaw models. However, due to the nature of
the data processing pipeline, we only possess binned data in the form of histograms over
logarithmically spaced distance bins.
To overcome this limitation, we reconstruct an approximate unbinned sample from the
binned data using an inverse transform sampling method inspired by [VC14]. The es-

sential idea is to treat the histogram as a discrete approximation of the underlying
distribution and generate synthetic raw samples consistent with the bin probabilities,
while carefully accounting for the variable bin widths due to logarithmic spacing.
This procedure is implemented in the Python function below:
def sample_from_histogram(x_vals, weights, n_samples=10000):
"""
Sample from histogram with log-spaced bins
Accounts for variable bin widths in log space
"""
x_vals = np.array(x_vals)
weights = np.array(weights)
# Calculate bin widths (assuming log-spaced)
if len(x_vals) > 1:
log_x = np.log10(x_vals)
d_log_x = np.diff(log_x)
avg_d_log_x = np.mean(d_log_x)
# Bin widths in linear space
bin_widths = x_vals * np.log(10) * avg_d_log_x
# Weight by bin width to get proper density
density_weights = weights * bin_widths
else:
density_weights = weights
# Normalize to probabilities
density_weights = density_weights / np.sum(density_weights)
# Create cumulative distribution
cdf = np.cumsum(density_weights)
# Generate random samples
random_vals = np.random.random(n_samples)
# Find corresponding x values using interpolation
sampled_x = np.interp(random_vals, cdf, x_vals)
return sampled_x

Using this approach, multiple synthetic unbinned samples from the binned intrinsic dis-
tance cost histogram are generated. For each sample, the powerlaw parameter estimation
via the maximum likelihood estimation based fitting package [Mai23a] is applied. To
account for sampling variability, we repeat this procedure 20 times, each time estimating
the model parameters independently. The final parameter estimates are then derived as
the averages over these repetitions.
This method enables leveraging existing unbinned maximum likelihood estimation fitting
tools while preserving the statistical characteristics of the binned data. It balances com-
putational feasibility and accuracy, ensuring reliable inference of powerlaw parameters
for the intrinsic distance cost in the study.
4.3.3
Plotting the Powerlaw Fits
In this study, the intrinsic distance cost obtained as the ratio between the observed
commute distance distribution and the pair distribution function is not a probability
density function in the strict statistical sense. Therefore, plotting the fitted piecewise
powerlaw curve requires careful consideration to ensure meaningful visual comparison.
For plotting the fitted piecewise powerlaw curve, we anchor the fitted function at the
median bin of the empirical ratio distribution. This anchoring aligns the model curve
with a meaningful reference point in the data, enabling a clear visual comparison of the
fit over the range of commuting distances.
It is important to emphasize that this plot is primarily illustrative. The evaluation of
the powerlaw fit quality and the accuracy of parameter estimates is conducted using
likelihood metrics and goodness-of-fit tests during the fitting procedure.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
This thesis has applied and extended a novel analytical framework based on the pair dis-
tribution function to disentangle geographical constraints from intrinsic behavioral pref-
erences in commuting patterns across Denmark. By leveraging a high-resolution dataset
of over 77 million concurrent home-work location pairs spanning multiple decades, the
study has advanced understanding of how spatial structure and human behavior jointly
shape commuting distance distributions.
The core finding is that after normalizing the observed commuting distance distribution
by the geographical spatial structure encoded in the pair distribution function, an in-
trinsic distance cost emerges that follows a piecewise powerlaw scaling over distances
ranging from tens of meters to hundreds of kilometers. This intrinsic cost reveals distinct
regimes: a relatively shallow decay for intra-city commuting, reflecting greater willing-
ness or necessity to travel shorter distances within urban areas, and a steeper decay for
inter-city commuting, reflecting increased cost or reduced tendency for long-range trips.
The characteristic transition distance aligns well with empirically derived city radius
scales.
Interestingly, the Copenhagen metropolitan area exhibits a unique pattern where the
first, shallow exponent dominates nearly the entire commuting distance range and the
second, steeper exponent loses practical significance, highlighting the impact of dense
urban morphology and integrated transport infrastructure on commuting behaviors.
The findings have implications for theoretical mobility modeling, providing a unified,
geography-free scaling law that operates continuously at fine spatial granularity. For
urban planners and policymakers, recognizing the fundamental behavioral scaling laws
underlying commuting can inform transport infrastructure design, zoning regulation, and
sustainability strategies by anticipating mobility responses independent of local spatial
layouts.
While this study has provided novel insights into the universal and geography-dependent
components of commuting behavior, opportunities for future work remain. Extensions
could include consideration of multimodal transport, temporal dynamics, 2and exten-
sions to other mobility forms such as leisure or migration.
Additionally, integrating
behavioral data from surveys or mobile tracking may further deepen interpretation of
intrinsic distance costs.
==PAGE:31==
6 Conclusion
26
In conclusion, this thesis contributes a rigorous, data-driven decomposition of commuting
patterns into spatial and behavioral drivers, uncovering universal powerlaw scaling of
intrinsic distance costs and complex urban scale effects. It advances mobility science by
bridging spatial structure and human choice, providing a robust foundation for modeling
and managing urban mobility in contemporary and future cities.
