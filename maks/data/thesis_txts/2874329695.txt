Abstract 
Cleaning-in-Place (CIP) is essential in pharmaceutical manufacturing to ensure 
effective cleaning without equipment dismantling. This project focused on designing a 
laboratory-scale CIP module to simulate hygienic design challenges, particularly dead 
legs and pipe orientation. Cleaning trials were performed at DTU Pilot Plant on the 13F 
CIP unit using mustard as a model soil, with tests varying flow regime, temperature, 
soil condition, and dead-leg geometry. Cleaning effectiveness was evaluated through 
grid-based quantification and penetration distance analysis. The results showed that 
turbulent flow was significantly more effective than laminar flow, dried soils were more 
difficult to remove than fresh soils, and dead-leg orientation strongly influenced 
cleaning, with downward branches performing best. These findings align with EHEDG 
and ASME BPE standards and provide practical recommendations for CIP design and 
validation. The study demonstrates how a pilot CIP module can be used to investigate 
hygienic design challenges and serve as a valuable tool for both research and 
education.

1 Introduction 
Cleaning is a fundamental requirement in the pharmaceutical and bioprocessing 
industries, where strict hygiene standards must be met to ensure product quality, 
patient safety, and regulatory compliance. Contaminants such as residues from active 
pharmaceutical ingredients (APIs), excipients, or microbial growth can compromise 
product safety and lead to cross-contamination between batches if not effectively 
removed. Traditionally, cleaning was performed manually, which was labor-intensive, 
inconsistent, and difficult to validate. As production systems have become more 
complex and multipurpose, there has been a growing need for automated, reliable, and 
verifiable cleaning methods. 
Cleaning-in-Place (CIP) has emerged as the industry standard for addressing these 
needs. CIP enables the internal cleaning of tanks, vessels, and pipework without 
dismantling equipment, ensuring efficient, reproducible cleaning cycles that reduce 
downtime and operator variability. Its development has not only improved process 
efficiency but has also supported compliance with increasingly stringent international 
regulations and hygienic design standards. 
1.1 Cleaning-in-Place (CIP) 
Cleaning-in-Place (CIP) is an important technology that enables the automated 
cleaning of equipment, such as tanks, pipes, and reactors, without needing to take 
them apart [1]. Its significance lies in ensuring that equipment is free of product 
residues and contaminants between batches, thereby preventing cross-contamination 
of drug products [1], [2]. CIP systems circulate cleaning solutions through internal 
surfaces under controlled conditions, reducing the need for labor-intensive manual 
scrubbing and minimizing operator-related variability [1], [3]. Effective CIP directly 
supports high product quality and patient safety, since residues from potent active 
ingredients or biologics can present serious health risks if not fully removed [1], [2]. 
The concept of CIP originated in the dairy industry during the mid-20th century and 
later advanced with automation technologies such as programmable logic controllers, 
valves, and sensors, making processes more reliable and verifiable. By the late 20th 
century, CIP was widely adopted in pharmaceutical manufacturing, particularly in 
facilities requiring rapid changeovers and efficient equipment utilization [1]. 
==PAGE:18==
 
 
2
1.2 CIP in Pharmaceutical Manufacturing 
The need for effective cleaning, as introduced in the previous section, is particularly 
critical in the pharmaceutical industry because the same equipment is often used to 
manufacture several different products. This shared use creates a risk that residues of 
active pharmaceutical ingredients (APIs), excipients, or microbiological contaminants 
can remain on surfaces, leading to cross-contamination or batch failures previously 
mentioned [4]. To mitigate this risk, regulatory bodies like the U.S. Food and Drug 
Administration (FDA) and the European Medicines Agency (EMA) require 
manufacturers to validate their cleaning methods, proving they consistently reduce 
residues to acceptable, safe levels [5], [6]. 
The operational impact of cleaning is significant, with research showing it can consume 
up to 50% of all manufacturing hours in some facilities [7], [8]. This highlights the need 
for the efficient, automated CIP systems described in section 1.1, as they can 
substantially cut down on downtime, reduce water and chemical consumption, and 
ensure high cleaning performance. Therefore, CIP is not just a regulatory necessity 
but also a crucial component for achieving the efficient and sustainable operations 
required in modern pharmaceutical manufacturing. 
1.3 Hygienic Design 
The key factors to consider when developing equipment for pharmaceutical processing 
industries are its performance and cleanliness. The European Hygienic Engineering 
and Design Group (EHEDG) provides design advice and conducts tests to ensure that 
equipment parts are easy to clean. Modern CIP systems use a combination of 
parameters—such as time, temperature, flow velocity, chemical concentration, and 
mechanical force—to remove soil and microbial contaminants from equipment 
surfaces [9]. 
According to EHEDG, effective cleaning depends not only on the chemicals used but 
also on the proper design of the equipment and installation layout to avoid dead legs, 
low-inflow zones, and areas that cannot be drained appropriately [10]. 
==PAGE:19==
 
 
3
The ASME BPE standard provides comprehensive guidelines for constructing 
pharmaceutical and bioprocessing equipment. Tanks, pipes, internal angles, and 
corners must be designed to allow complete drainage or fitted with drain valves placed 
as low as possible. Dead spaces should be avoided—or at a minimum, be easy to 
clean and disinfect [11], [12]. However, many production plants are not fully hygienic, 
especially when new systems are added to existing layouts. Therefore, it is 
recommended to regularly inspect equipment and piping to identify defects or 
weaknesses early [10]. 
1.4 Hygienic Design Challenges 
Despite CIP’s advantages, designing CIP-capable equipment and systems presents 
several hygienic engineering challenges. Among them, dead legs are particularly 
critical. A “dead leg” refers to a portion of a pipe or equipment where fluid flow is 
minimal or stagnant—such as an under-used branch or dead-end tee—where cleaning 
solution can linger. These zones can retain residues and foster microbial growth, 
compromising cleaning efficiency [13]. 
Industry guidelines—including ASME BPE—recommend that the length-to-diameter 
(L/D) ratio of these branch legs be 2:1 or less for systems used in high-purity 
applications [14]. This aligns with standards such as WHO TRS 929 (1.5D) [15]  , 
WHO TRS 970 (3D) [16] , and ASME BPE-2009 (2D)  
Drainability is equally critical for CIP compliance—equipment must self-drain 
completely to avoid residual liquid post-cleaning [17]. Design strategies to facilitate full 
drainage include sloped tank bottoms and pipe inclines of 1–3%, which help ensure 
runoff to outlet points [18]. Without proper drainage, residual soils or cleaning solutions 
can accumulate, increasing batch-to-batch cross-contamination risk and facilitating 
microbial regrowth [17]. Therefore, eliminating dead legs and ensuring efficient 
drainage are essential for validated, hygienic pharmaceutical systems.

1.5 Objective 
The main objectives of this project were defined to link theoretical understanding with 
practical evaluation of Cleaning-in-Place (CIP) in pharmaceutical applications. The 
study aimed not only to assess current cleaning challenges but also to contribute 
design considerations and experimental evidence that could support the development 
of a pilot-scale CIP module for educational and research purposes. Specifically, the 
objectives were to: 
• Advance the understanding of Cleaning-in-Place (CIP) in pharmaceutical 
applications by outlining the design considerations for a pilot module intended 
for educational and research use. 
• Design a modular CIP system incorporating representative hygienic design 
challenges, including dead legs of varying length-to-diameter ratios and 
different branch orientations. 
• Experimentally investigate the impact of operating parameters — flow regime 
(laminar vs. turbulent), temperature, and soil condition — on cleaning 
performance within the module. 
• Quantify cleaning efficiency using representative test soils and appropriate 
assessment methods with evaluation of cleaning performance. 
• provide practical recommendations for improving hygienic design and CIP 
module performance, as well as outlining directions for future research and 
industrial applications.

2 Background and Theory 
2.1 Regulatory Frameworks, Standards, and 
Research Gaps 
Designing and validating hygienic equipment cleaning in the pharmaceutical industry 
must align with GMP-lifecycle expectations and supporting guidance. Freely 
accessible regulatory documents emphasize a risk-based approach: EU GMP Annex 
15 on qualification/validation, the FDA Process Validation Guidance (three-stage 
lifecycle), and WHO TRS 1019 Annex 3 with TRS 1033 Annex 2 (HBELs in cleaning 
validation); PIC/S PI 006-3 provides pragmatic expectations for cleaning validation 
programs [15], [16], [19], [20], [21]. Although guidance is extensive, open literature still 
shows comparatively few peer-reviewed, pilot/bench-scale studies designed for 
training or to intentionally probe hygienic-design challenges; recent open-access 
examples include a PAT-equipped film-flow cleaning rig (Heliyon) and digital/inline 
monitoring approaches (Frontiers in Microbiology; MDPI Sensors) [22], [23], [24]. This 
thesis, therefore, specifies CIP-module criteria consistent with these GMP 
expectations while deliberately incorporating diagnostic design challenges to support 
learning and investigation. 
2.2 Cleaning Mechanisms 
Pharmaceutical soils are diverse (e.g., sticky gels, creams, powders) and can be more 
challenging to remove than typical food soils. Inadequate drainability may also allow 
microbial films to develop. Because of these risks, cleaning in pharmaceutical 
manufacturing is strictly regulated under GMP, and each cleaning process must be 
verified to ensure effective soil removal [19].   
At a fundamental level, deposits can be removed by mass transfer and/or by fluid-
mechanical forces. Long-timescale, soluble deposits can be removed by diffusion and 
reaction even without significant shear, whereas flow-induced forces primarily act by 
overcoming cohesive strength within the soil and/or adhesion between the soil and the 
surface. A comparative overview of four relevant mechanisms is widely used in the 
literature: (i) diffusive dissolution, (ii) cohesive separation, (iii) viscous shifting, and (iv) 
adhesive detachment [25], [26], [27]. 
==PAGE:22==
 
 
6
The dominant mechanism for a given soil can change across cleaning phases and 
operating conditions. For example, when cleaning mustard-like yield-stress residues 
in pipelines, an initial high-shear phase may promote cohesive separation with bulk 
detachment, followed by gradual erosion and diffusion-driven dissolution as the deposit 
thins [28], [29]. Under even higher local shear—such as impinging jets or falling films—
adhesive detachment can dominate, where fluid forces directly break the soil–surface 
bond [30], [31]

2.3 Factors Affecting Cleaning Performance 
The principal factors influencing cleaning effectiveness are traditionally summarized 
by Sinner’s circle—mechanical action, chemistry, temperature, and time. The 
Nederlands Instituut voor Zuivelonderzoek (NIZO) subsequently expanded this model 
to incorporate equipment design and product-related factors, acknowledging that 
geometry, surface condition, and soil properties strongly modulate the original four 
parameters. The updated concept is shown in Figure 2.2  [26], [31], [32].

2.3.1 Process-related factors (Sinner’s original four) 
Mechanical action 
Hydrodynamics govern the near-wall shear and momentum transfer that detach or 
erode deposits. Higher flow rates and wall shear generally increase removal rates; 
impinging jets and falling films can transition the dominant mechanism from cohesive 
erosion to adhesive detachment when local stresses exceed soil–surface adhesion 
[26], [28], [33].   
Chemistry 
Alkaline solutions (e.g., NaOH with builders/surfactants) saponify and solubilize fatty 
soils and swell/solubilize many protein/carbohydrate deposits; acids address 
inorganic/mineral 
films 
(e.g.,“milkstone”) 
and 
heat-derived 
mineral 
scales. 
Disinfectants (oxidizing or quaternary-ammonium systems) are applied where 
sanitization is required. Over-dosing chemistry does not linearly improve outcomes and 
can restructure certain proteinaceous soils, making them harder to remove, so optimal 
concentration is context-dependent [31], [34].   
Temperature  
Elevated temperature accelerates diffusion and reaction kinetics, often shortening 
cleaning time; however, it can also harden specific soils (e.g., protein 
denaturation/cross-linking), increasing the mechanical or chemical energy required for 
removal. Thus, temperature must be optimized with chemistry and time rather than 
maximized by default [26], [31].   
Time  
Contact/circulation time interacts with all other parameters. “Soak” phases can pre-
weaken cohesive soils, while excessive exposure under harsh conditions may 
restructure soils adversely. Industrially, time also trades off with productivity and 
resource consumption, motivating lifecycle optimization (e.g., cleaning maps, CPV) 
[26], [31], [32], [35].   
==PAGE:24==
 
 
8
2.3.2 Product-related factors 
Matrix and composition  
Soil rheology and chemistry (e.g., fat/protein/starch ratios, emulsified vs. particulate 
phases) determine removal mechanisms and the efficiency of alkali, acid, enzymes, 
and surfactants. Yield-stress or highly viscous residues (e.g., condiments, ointments) 
typically require higher local shear or pre-wetting/soaking to initiate removal [26], [28], 
[36].   
Heating history and ageing  
Drying, thermal exposure, Maillard reactions, and mineral precipitation can strengthen 
deposits over time, raising the energy needed for removal—particularly for protein-rich 
soils. Consequently, cleaning recipes often differentiate fresh vs. aged soils and 
include steps that limit deleterious restructuring (e.g., prompt pre-rinses, controlled 
temperatures) [26], [31], [34].   
2.3.3 Equipment-design factors 
Geometry and drainability  
Crevices, dead legs, poor slopes, and areas of stagnant flow impede mechanical 
action and promote soil retention and biofilm formation; hygienic layouts aim to avoid 
such zones and ensure full wetting/coverage in tanks and piping. Surface condition 
(e.g., roughness, weld quality, material finish) influences adhesion and cleanability; 
smooth, adequately finished stainless steel (e.g., 316L in pharma) is standard. 
Effective tank cleaning additionally requires coverage and appropriate spray device 
selection/positioning [26], [31], [32].   
In practice, process, product, and equipment factors must be tuned together. Tools 
such as cleaning maps and pilot-rig experiments help visualize interactions and set 
operating windows that achieve cleanliness while minimizing resource use [32], [35].

2.4 CIP System Configurations and Protocols 
Developed in the dairy sector in the 1950s, CIP was rapidly adopted across brewing, 
beverages, food, and later biopharma because it delivers repeatable cleaning under 
controlled hydrodynamic and chemical conditions while minimizing operator exposure 
[26], [34]. 
2.4.1 System configurations 
Two archetypal configurations are widely used (see Figure 2.3): 
• 
Single-use systems. Detergent solutions are prepared fresh for each cycle and 
discarded (or sent to waste treatment) after use. Skids are typically located 
close to the user equipment, and distribution is via dedicated supply/return lines 
and mix proof valve manifolds. Benefits include high hygienic assurance, 
simplified validation, and minimal cross-contamination risk; trade-offs are 
greater water/chemical consumption and operating cost [21], [22], [26]. 
• 
Reuse systems. Detergent solutions are recovered and recycled partially or 
fully through holding tanks (e.g., caustic and acid tanks). Inline sensors 
(conductivity, temperature, turbidity/UV) enable stage transitions and solution 
quality control; periodic make-up (concentration/temperature) maintains 
effectiveness. Reuse reduces resource consumption and cost but requires 
tighter monitoring and lifecycle control to prevent deterioration or carryover [21], 
[22], [24], [26]. 
In both designs, skids include a CIP pump, heat exchanger for temperature control, 
and instrumentation for flow, temperature, and conductivity to verify stage endpoints 
and support batch records. Spray devices or return loops are engineered to ensure 
complete coverage/wetting of tanks and sufficient wall shear in pipelines [20], [22], 
[24], [26], [31]. 
2.4.2 Representative CIP protocol 
A typical automated recipe comprises sequenced stages governed by flow rate, 
temperature, contact time, and chemical concentration (the Sinner elements).

10
Parameters are tailored to soil type and equipment design, but the structure commonly 
follows: 
1. Product recovery / drain — empty equipment via process pumps/valves to 
minimize soil loading. 
2. Pre-rinse (water) — remove bulk residues and condition surfaces; often 
controlled to a conductivity or turbidity endpoint. 
3. Alkaline wash — circulate an alkaline detergent (e.g., NaOH with 
builders/surfactants) to saponify fats and solubilize proteins/carbohydrates; 
temperature and concentration are set from prior validation/DoE studies. 
4. Intermediate rinse — flush detergent until a defined conductivity threshold is 
reached. 
5. Acid wash (if required) — dissolve inorganic/mineral scale (e.g., 
carbonates/phosphates) and help restore metal surface passivity. 
6. Intermediate rinse — remove acid to threshold. 
7. Disinfection / sanitation (if required) — apply an approved biocide (e.g., 
peracetic acid) or thermal sanitation. 
8. Final rinse — achieve “rinse-to-clean” endpoint (conductivity close to feed 
water) and document return-to-service criteria [20], [22], [24], [31].

2.5 Performance Testing and Validation Tools 
In GMP-regulated industries (pharmaceutical, dairy, food), CIP performance is 
demonstrated through a lifecycle approach to validation and verification. Regulatory 
and consensus guidance expect documented evidence—typically including ≥3 
successful, representative runs—that established operating conditions reduce product 
residues, microorganisms, and cleaning agents to predefined acceptable levels and 
that the process remains in a state of control. Acceptance criteria, sampling plans, and 
recordkeeping are defined prospectively, and routine verification/continued process 
verification (CPV) uses in-line measurements and periodic analytical testing to confirm 
ongoing performance [5], [15], [19], [21].   
2.5.1 Common Verification Methods 
• 
Visual inspection — Post-cleaning inspection for visible residues, ideally after 
relevant disassembly. EU GMP Annex 15 recognizes visual checks as an 
important criterion but not sufficient on their own; visual inspection should 
==PAGE:28==
 
 
12
complement analytical limits and documented acceptance criteria [19]. Practical 
industry guidance echoes this position.   
• 
Riboflavin test (spray-coverage/cleanability aid) — A fluorescent riboflavin 
solution is applied to internal surfaces prior to cleaning; UV inspection after the 
cycle highlights shadows and poor coverage. Open procedural descriptions and 
best-practice cautions (e.g., it evaluates coverage, not residue clearance) are 
available in technical notes and application guides [38], [39].   
• 
Conductivity measurement — In-line conductivity provides real-time detection 
of phase transitions (e.g., product → water → detergent → rinse), supports 
concentration control of caustic/acid solutions, and helps verify rinse endpoints 
to prevent detergent carryover [40], [41]. Peer-reviewed work also demonstrates 
multi-sensor/PAT approaches for monitoring cleaning progress.   
• 
Microbiological assessment — Surface swabs and/or rinse-water samples 
are analyzed to verify microbial control of the CIP cycle and to document 
bioburden/endotoxin limits where applicable. WHO validation guidance and 
industry guides describe sampling plans, locations, and reporting expectations; 
recent open-access studies discuss swab/rinse recovery and method 
performance [15], [42], [43].

2.6 Challenges in CIP Optimization 
Even with mature guidance and extensive operating experience, CIP performance 
often fails for practical, system-level reasons—typically a combination of design 
constraints, 
hydrodynamics, 
chemistry/temperature 
control, 
and 
weak 
verification/monitoring. Under GMP, these weaknesses must be identified and 
controlled through validation, routine verification, and continued process verification 
(CPV) [15], [19], [21], [52]. 
• 
Poor drainability or dead legs.  
Stagnant zones (horizontal runs with inadequate slope, long dead legs, poorly 
positioned nozzles) reduce coverage and shear, trap soils, and hinder rinsing—
raising the risk of microbial persistence. Hygienic layout and demonstrable 
coverage (e.g., spray-device testing) are therefore foundational to validation 
[15], [19], [52]. 
• 
Insufficient flow velocity / weak hydrodynamics.  
Cleaning efficiency depends on near-wall shear and full wetting. For water-like 
CIP media in typical process lines, turbulent flow (Re ≳ 4000) is preferred to 
enhance mass/heat transfer and detachment. In practice, pharma processors 
often target ≈1.5 m·s⁻¹ or higher (diameter-dependent) to achieve turbulence 
and displace entrained air; pilot- and plant-scale studies report effective 
cleaning in the ~1.1–1.7 m·s⁻¹ range for representative soils [28], [31]. Where 
velocities are too low (laminar/transitional flow), mechanical action falls and 
cleaning time rises sharply. 
• 
Incorrect chemical concentration and/or temperature.  
Over or under-dosing alkali/acid, or running too hot/cold, can impair soil removal 
(e.g., protein hardening on overheating) or damage equipment (e.g., corrosion, 
passivation loss). Operating windows should be established during 
development/qualification and maintained with in-line checks and periodic 
assays [15], [52]. 
==PAGE:31==
 
 
15
• 
Incomplete rinse verification.  
Missing a rinse endpoint risks detergent carry-over and out-of-spec residues. 
Regulators and WHO emphasize routine verification using the same 
sampling/analytical approaches defined at validation (e.g., conductivity-traced 
phase changes, “rinse-to-clean” thresholds, swab/rinse testing) and trending of 
results as part of CPV [15], [16], [21], [43]. 
• 
Sensor miscalibration / weak instrumentation control.  
Mis-ranged or uncalibrated sensors (flow, temperature, conductivity, pressure) 
can trigger sequence errors (e.g., cutting a wash short or failing to reach 
temperature), undermining validated conditions and data integrity. Calibration 
status and alarms must be embedded in routine monitoring [15], [52]. 
2.7 CIP Cleaning Process Lifecycle and Control Flow 
Evaluation and control of a CIP process span three complementary layers—validation, 
verification, and monitoring—implemented within a documented lifecycle. The 
objective is to demonstrate that the process is fit for purpose (validation), continues to 
perform in routine use (verification), and remains in control day-to-day (monitoring) 
[19], [21], [52], [53]. 
• 
Validation — Confirms the cleaning process design is appropriate before 
routine use and after significant changes. Typical outputs include: user 
requirements (URS), risk assessment (e.g., FMEA/HACCP-style), a validation 
plan/protocol (VMP), IQ/OQ documentation, a PQ report based on ≥3 
successful, representative runs, a sampling plan (locations, swab/rinse 
methods), and predefined acceptance criteria for residues, detergents, and 
bioburden [19], [21], [53]. 
• 
Verification — Confirms, at defined intervals (e.g., per batch or campaign), that 
residue removal and microbial control meet expectations under routine 
conditions. Evidence includes completed batch records, cleaning logs, 
rinse/swab analytical results, and stage-endpoint checks (e.g., conductivity for 
==PAGE:32==
 
 
16
detergent clearance). Triggers for enhanced verification should be defined (e.g., 
atypical soils, extended campaigns, or borderline results) [19], [42], [53]. 
• 
Monitoring — Provides routine, often real-time checks (e.g., flow, temperature, 
pressure, conductivity) to confirm stage endpoints and maintain process control. 
It also covers instrument calibration status, alarm handling, and electronic data 
integrity for parameters that can be monitored over time [19], [52], [53].

3 Methodology and Experimental Setup 
This chapter outlines the experimental methods and instruments used to evaluate 
Clean-in-Place (CIP) cleaning performance, linking back to the theoretical background 
of fouling and cleaning described in earlier chapters.  The methodology is structured 
to evaluate how variations in flow and temperature influence soil removal from 
stainless-steel surfaces. In doing so, it supports the thesis objectives of assessing 
hygienic cleaning performance while designing and configuring a laboratory-scale CIP 
system that replicates common hygienic design challenges. This chapter therefore 
links theoretical aspects of hygienic design with practical experimental aspects.   
A pilot-scale mobile CIP unit “13F” module as shown in Figure 3.2 & Figure 3.3  was 
used, allowing both manual and automated cleaning cycles under controlled 
conditions.  Cleaning protocols were developed to vary key parameters systematically, 
and performance was evaluated using a standardized test plate and visual inspection 
through scoring.  Hygienic design standards (EHEDG, ASME BPE) are referenced to 
contextualize the dead-leg studies.   
The chapter proceeds by (1) detailing the cleaning protocols and experimental design, 
(2) describing the evaluation method with a grid plate, and (3) presenting the CIP 
system and test-section configuration. All cross-sectional schematics of the test 
sections (Figures 3.5–3.8) were prepared in SolidWorks 2024 Student Edition . These 
CAD drawings illustrate the geometry of the test pipe, grid plate, and dead-leg 
configurations used in the experiments. 
3.1 Cleaning Protocols and Experimental Design 
The cleaning experiments were designed to explore the effects of flow rate, 
temperature, and soil condition on cleaning efficiency.  Stainless-steel test plates were 
coated with a food-grade soil (mustard paste) and subjected to CIP cleaning under a 
range of conditions.  Specifically, flow rates were chosen to span laminar to turbulent 
regimes in the test pipe: low flow velocities resulting in laminar flow, and higher flow 
velocities to ensure turbulent flow.  As turbulent flow produces higher wall shear stress, 
it is generally more effective at dislodging soils than laminar flow.  Temperature was 
also varied between ambient conditions (approximately 20 °C) and an elevated level 
==PAGE:34==
 
 
18
(approximately 40 °C), as increased thermal energy is generally associated with 
enhanced cleaning kinetics. In addition, the effect of soil condition was examined by 
comparing plates that were tested immediately after soiling (“fresh” soils) with those 
that were allowed to dry prior to cleaning. The dried mustard layers adhered more 
strongly to the stainless-steel surface and exhibited reduced solubility, thereby 
representing a more challenging cleaning condition and serving as a proxy for worst-
case fouling scenarios. 
The study employed a factorial experimental design.  For each trial, one or more 
parameters were changed while others were held constant to isolate effects.  Flow 
conditions (laminar vs turbulent), soil status (fresh vs dried), and temperature (low vs 
high) were systematically combined. Flow regime was characterized by Reynolds 
number: turbulent flow was targeted (Re > 4000) to ensure sufficient mixing and shear 
in the pipe, whereas laminar flow (Re < 2100) was used as a comparative baseline. In 
summary, the cleaning protocol matrix included multiple flow rates, two temperature 
levels, and two soil conditions, allowing assessment of their individual and combined 
impact on cleaning effectiveness. 
3.2 Evaluation Method 
Cleaning effectiveness was quantified using a stainless-steel plate that had been 
manually gridded to create equal sections. This gridded surface served as a visual 
inspection tool, enabling systematic assessment of soil removal across the plate. The 
surface of the plate was subdivided into equal sections by permanent marker lines, 
providing a consistent framework for evaluating soil removal. Before each run, a 
uniform layer of mustard paste (soil simulant) was applied over the grid 
surface.  Mustard paste was chosen as a standard soil because it contains proteins 
and oils that adhere strongly, making it a conservative test of cleaning. The paste was 
spread evenly approximately 3 mm thick (mistakenly labeled 4mm) using a stainless 
steel applicator (Figure 3.1 top right) and in some trials was allowed to air-dry for 
several hours. Figure 3.1  shows an example of a grid plate coated with mustard paste 
and the plate after cleaning cycle.

4 Analysis Method 
All cleaning efficiency experiments were conducted on a laboratory-scale Clean-In-
Place (CIP) system using water as the cleaning medium. No chemical detergents were 
added in order to isolate the effects of temperature and mechanical action (flow 
velocity) on soil removal. Water was pumped through a fouled pipe system for a set 
duration, after which flow was stopped and the system was drained. The cleaning 
process then proceeded according to the standard CIP rinse cycle. Time, temperature, 
and flow rate (mechanical action) were key cleaning parameters that were 
systematically varied in these trials, as they are known to affect cleaning performance 
in CIP processes [56]. 
All graphs in this thesis (cleaning efficiency vs. time, penetration distance vs. time, and 
comparative plots for fresh/dried soils and dead-leg orientations) were generated using 
MATLAB Student Version R2024b. The data tables were processed and plotted within 
MATLAB to ensure consistency in axis scaling, legends, and labeling. This software 
was chosen because of its wide use in engineering research, reproducibility, and 
compatibility with matrix-based data analysis. The MATLAB scripts calculated 
Reynolds numbers, flow velocities, and cleaning efficiency percentages, and then 
automatically generated the figures presented detailed in Chapter 5.

6 Discussion 
6.1 Laminar vs. Turbulent Flow Cleaning Performance 
The cleaning trials clearly demonstrate that the flow regime has a dramatic impact on 
soil removal efficiency. Under laminar flow conditions (Reynolds number Re ≈ 1410), 
cleaning was much slower and less complete compared to turbulent flow (Re ≈ 7047). 
For example, at 20 °C with fresh mustard soil, the laminar flow (100 L/h, Re ≈ 1410) 
removed only ~72% of the soil after 10 minutes where the turbulent flow (500 L/h, Re 
≈ 7047) achieved 100% removal in just 5 minutes (Figure 6.1). This disparity is 
expected, as turbulent flow generates higher wall shear stress at the pipe surface, 
which promotes rapid detachment of soils [58]. Laminar flow produces a smoother, 
less disruptive boundary layer near the wall, which limits momentum transfer to the 
surface and impedes soil removal. Turbulent flow is therefore generally preferred in 
CIP processes for its superior ability to dislodge and carry away residues [11]. 
The Reynolds number calculations confirm the intended regimes – for water at 20 °C, 
100 L/h corresponds to Re ≈ 1410 (laminar) and 500 L/h to Re ≈ 7047 (fully turbulent). 
Equivalent flows at 40 °C (66 L/h and 328 L/h, respectively) yielded similar Re values, 
ensuring a consistent comparison across temperatures. In summary, the turbulent 
conditions consistently yielded faster and more complete cleaning than laminar 
conditions, underscoring the importance of maintaining turbulent flow in CIP systems 
to effectively remove soils [26].

6.2 Effect of Temperature at Constant Reynolds 
Number 
6.2.1 Fresh Mustard Soil 
The experiments holding Re constant between 20 °C and 40 °C revealed effects of 
temperature on cleaning efficiency as shown in Figure 6.1. Interestingly, for fresh 
mustard soil, the lower temperature (20 °C) performed slightly better or on par with 40 
°C when Re was the same . At laminar Re ≈ 1410, the 20 °C run reached ~72% soil 
removal by 10 minutes, whereas the 40 °C run (adjusted to 66 L/h for the same Re) 
reached ~62% in 10 minutes . A similar trend was observed under turbulent conditions: 
at Re ≈ 7047, the 20 °C case achieved full cleaning in 5 min, while the 40 °C case (328 
L/h for equal Re) took ~10 min to reach 100%. These results suggest that simply 
increasing temperature does not always accelerate cleaning if the flow dynamics 
(shear forces) are reduced to keep Re constant. 
6.2.2 Dried Mustard Soil 
By contrast, dried soil showed a strong positive temperature effect at constant Re . 
Dried mustard was much more difficult to clean (as discussed below), and in this case,

the higher temperature significantly improved the cleaning efficiency. Under turbulent 
conditions (Re ≈ 7000) as shown in Figure 6.2, 40 °C water removed the dried soil 
almost twice as fast as 20 °C water. At 20 °C and Re ≈ 7047, virtually no dried soil was 
removed in the first 10 min, and even after 20 min, only ~26% was cleaned. It took 30–

A similar trend occurred in laminar flow as shown in Figure 6.3 : at 20 °C, no dried soil

6.3 Fresh vs. Dried Soil Cleaning Comparison 
A major finding of this study is the stark contrast in cleanability between fresh (wet) soil 
and dried soil. Under equivalent flow conditions, fresh mustard soil was removed much 
faster and more completely than the same soil after drying. This was evident in both 
laminar and turbulent regimes. For instance as shown in Figure 6.4 ,  laminar flow at 
20 °C (100 L/h, Re ≈ 1410), the freshly applied mustard was ~72% cleaned within 10 
min and likely would reach near 100% with only a few more minutes. Meanwhile, the 
dried mustard under the same flow showed no detectable cleaning for the first 10 min 
and only ~25% removal at 20 min. Even after 60 min of laminar flow at 20 °C (100 L/h, 
Re ≈ 1410), the dried soil was still not fully removed as ~70.0% cleaned .  
In turbulent flow (Figure 6.5), the difference was equally pronounced: fresh soil was 
completely removed in 5 min at 20 °C (500 L/h, Re ≈ 7047), whereas dried soil at 20 
°C took on the order of 45 min to achieve ~92% removal. At 40 °C fresh soil cleaned 
to 100% in 10 min (328 L/h, Re ≈ 7047), and dried soil cleaned to 100% in 20 minutes.

6.4 Balancing Thermal and Hydrodynamic Factors 
for Soil Removal 
The results make it clear that temperature alone cannot compensate for inadequate 
shear; high flow velocity (turbulence) is still required. In practice, CIP systems often 
combine high turbulence with elevated temperature (e.g., hot cleaning solutions) to 
maximize cleaning efficacy. However, studies have shown diminishing returns beyond 
a certain threshold: for instance, in a water-only CIP test system, cleaning performance 
did not significantly improve when temperatures rose above 45 °C, provided that wall 
shear exceeded 0.65 Pa, implying that once mechanical cleaning is optimized, further 
heating yields limited benefit [62]. 
The improved performance at higher temperatures is likely due to thermal softening or 
enhanced dissolution of the dried residue. Heating the cleaning fluid reduces its 
viscosity, which in turn lowers flow resistance and facilitates higher shear and better 
mass transfer into the soil layer. This helps break down hardened or congealed 
deposits and allows a transition toward more turbulent flow regimes, enhancing 
scrubbing action [59]. For a soil like mustard, which contains proteins and 
polysaccharides, warmer water increases solubility and swelling. This aligns with 
established CIP practice: higher temperatures improve surface wetting and the 
solubilization of protein–carbohydrate residues, which facilitates their detachment 
under flow [59], [60], [61]. Increased cleaning power with temperature has been 
observed in both industry-focused guidelines and experimental studies [60], [61]. 
In the present study, within the investigated temperature range of 20–40 °C, the 
increase in temperature provided a measurable improvement in cleaning efficacy, 
particularly for the removal of hardened soil deposits. This implies that for dried or 
strongly adhered residues, using warmer cleaning solutions (or longer soaking at 
temperature) can significantly enhance soil detachment at a given Re. Conversely, for 
fresh soils that are easily sheared off, priority should be given to ensuring sufficient 
flow shear (turbulence) rather than high temperature. Overall, maintaining the same 
Re at different temperatures showed that mechanical force (hydrodynamics) was the 
dominant factor for fresh soil, whereas thermal effects became important for 
challenging dried soils. 
==PAGE:75==

6.5 Dead-Leg Cleaning Performance (Length and 
Orientation) 
Dead-leg experiments showed that both the length of the dead leg and its orientation 
have profound effects on cleaning efficiency. 
6.5.1 Dead-leg length  
The 12 cm dead leg (internal diameter 2.5 cm, L/D = 4.8) consistently cleaned more 
completely than the 25 cm dead leg (L/D = 10) under comparable conditions. In the 12 
cm horizontal dead-leg at 20 °C, high turbulence (1500–2000 L/h, Re ≈ 21180–28180) 
was able to penetrate and clean a substantial portion of the branch. For example, at 
2000 L/h (Re≈ 28180), about 63.9% of the 12 cm leg was cleaned in 20 min, 
corresponding to ~7.7 cm penetration depth (Figure 6.6). At the same flow rate and 
time, the 25 cm horizontal dead-leg was only ~13.5% cleaned – a mere 4.5 cm 
penetration, even at 1500 L/h (Re≈ 21180) for 20 min, the short dead-leg had ~22% 
cleaned (~2.7 cm), whereas the long dead-leg showed only ~6.7% (~1.7 cm) of 
cleaning (Figure 6.6). Clearly, the cleaning fluid struggles to reach deep into longer 
blind branches.  
Figure 6.6: Comparison graph of penetration distance vs. time for 12 cm and 25 cm dead legs at 1500 
L/h (Re≈ 21180) and 2000 L/h (Re≈ 28180) on horizontal orientation at 20 °C. 
==PAGE:76==

These findings are in line with hygienic design principles that demand minimization of 
dead-leg lengths. Industry guidelines (ASME BPE, EHEDG) recommend a dead-leg 
L/D ratio of ≤ 2:1[64], [65]. The present results provide experimental support for the 
stringent length restrictions prescribed in hygienic design standards, as dead-leg 
branches of approximately 5–10 pipe diameters in length (corresponding to the 12–25 
cm sections tested) could not be effectively or reliably cleaned under typical CIP flow 
conditions. Even at very high Reynolds numbers , complete soil removal did not occur 
in the 25 cm branch. This implies that in actual CIP systems, dead-legs significantly 
longer than a few pipe diameters may harbor unremoved residues after cleaning, 
representing a contamination risk. 
6.5.2 Dead-leg Orientation 
Orientation of the dead-leg (horizontal, vertical-up, vertical-down) was another key 
factor impacting cleaning performance. Tests on the 12 cm dead-leg revealed a clear 
trend: vertical downward orientation was the easiest to clean, followed by horizontal, 
and vertical upward was the most difficult. At 2000 L/h (Re≈ 28180) and 20 min (20 
°C), the vertical down dead-leg was completely cleaned – 100% of squares removed, 
i.e., full 12 cm penetration (Figure 6.7). Under the same conditions, the horizontal 
dead-leg was ~56% cleaned (about 6.7 cm penetration), and the vertical-up leg was 
only ~37% cleaned  with ~5.83 cm penetration (Figure 6.7). 
It was further observed that at lower flow rates, the vertical-up orientation exhibited 
minimal to negligible cleaning performance, with little or no measurable soil removal. 
Additional images showing the cleaning results can be found in Appendix A (Table 
A.10). The poor performance in the upward-facing dead-leg is likely due to two issues: 
air entrapment and gravitational counteraction. An upward blind branch can trap an air 
pocket at its top end, preventing cleaning fluid from fully filling and wetting the surface 
[64], [65], [66]. In contrast, a downward-facing dead-leg tends to fill completely with 
cleaning solution (gravity helps keep it flooded), and any air can escape upward into 
the main line [64]. Fluid flow can thus reach the dead-end and actively wash the entire 
surface, as evidenced by the 100% efficacy in vertical-down orientation. These results 
support design recommendations that dead-legs should be oriented to be self-draining 
and free-venting (i.e., sloped or vertical-down whenever possible) [64], [65]. 
 
==PAGE:77==
 
 
61
 
Figure 6.7: Combined comparison graph of penetration distance vs. time for all three orientations  at 
20 °C with different flowrates. 
In summary, the dead-leg experiments highlight two practical points: (1) Keep any 
piping branches as short as possible (ideally L/D < 2) to ensure flow can reach the end 
[64], [65]; (2) Orient branches favorably – arrange piping so that cleaning solution can 
naturally flood and drain the branches, avoiding upward stagnation points. 
The key findings regarding non-uniform cleaning are discussed below; a 
comprehensive record of all observations can be found in Appendix A. 
6.6 Uneven cleaning observations 
The cleaning trials showed that removal was not always uniform across the test 
surfaces. In several turbulent flow tests, soil detachment began on the side opposite 
to the incoming flow direction, rather than directly at the inlet. For example, during the 
500 L/h , (Re≈ 7047) 20 °C run, the first visible removal after 1 min occurred on the 
==PAGE:78==
 
 
62
back wall of the plate (12.5% efficiency) while the front-facing side remained fouled 
until 3 min (33.3% efficiency). Similar patterns were observed in dead-leg experiments, 
where cleaning sometimes initiated from the middle of the branch rather than the 
entrance. At 1500 L/h (Re≈ 21180) in the 12 cm horizontal dead leg, ~11% were 
cleaned at 5 min, corresponding to a narrow patch mid-branch, while the inlet zone 
remained soiled. By contrast, the vertical-down dead leg at 2000 L/h (Re≈ 28180) 
showed cleaning progressing more evenly, with complete removal (100%) at 20 min. 
In other trials, top–bottom asymmetry was evident: for example, in the 40 °C laminar 
case (66 L/h, (Re≈ 1410), the upper region cleared by 7 min (≈47% efficiency), while 
the lower squares were still contaminated until ~10 min (≈62% efficiency). 
These irregular cleaning fronts might be explained by localized flow hydrodynamics. 
Flow separation and recirculation zones downstream of pipe entries or expansions 
generate regions of low shear stress, delaying soil removal in those areas [58], [67]. 
Turbulence eddies and velocity fluctuations can cause higher wall shear in unexpected 
regions, which is why removal sometimes begins in the middle of a plate or on the back 
side [26], [55]. The grid-based visual method proved valuable in detecting these 
effects, since it not only quantified the percentage cleaned but also captured the spatial 
pattern of removal square by square. Without such mapping, early non-uniform 
cleaning could easily be overlooked if only outlet turbidity or conductivity were 
monitored [58], [67]. 
6.7 Reproducibility of Dead-Leg Cleaning 
To assess the consistency of the dead-leg cleaning results, three tests were conducted 
for the 12 cm dead-leg at the highest flow (2000 L/h, 20 min, 20 °C) in each orientation. 
The outcomes, reported as mean ± standard deviation, provide insight into the 
variability of cleaning in these geometries. The horizontal 12 cm dead-leg had a 
cleaning efficiency of 55.6 ± 7.7%. The vertical-up orientation showed 36.6 ± 11.2%, 
with a much wider spread. In stark contrast, the vertical-down orientation consistently 
achieved 100.0 ± 0.0% in all three repeats. 
The zero standard deviation for vertical-down indicates a robust and reproducible 
cleaning process. The horizontal dead-leg had moderate variability, while the vertical-
==PAGE:79==
 
 
63
up was highly variable and unreliable. Regulatory guidelines for cleaning validation 
emphasize that processes must be consistent and reproducible [52]. The data here 
show that vertical-up dead-legs pose significant CIP risks due to poor reproducibility, 
aligning with EHEDG concerns [64].

7 Conclusion 
This study investigated the cleaning performance of a pilot-scale clean-in-place (CIP) 
system using mustard-based test soils, with systematic variation of flow regime, 
temperature, soil condition, and dead-leg geometry. 
The influence of flow regime was decisive. Under turbulent flow (Re ≈ 7047, 500 L/h, 
20 °C), complete cleaning of fresh soils was achieved within 5 minutes, whereas under 
laminar flow (Re ≈ 1410, 100 L/h, 20 °C) ~ 72% of the plate area was cleaned after 10 
minutes. At 40 °C, turbulent flow (328 L/h, Re ≈ 7047) achieved full removal within 10 
minutes, while laminar flow resulted in ~62% cleaning in the same period. These 
results confirm that turbulence provides the shear and mixing required for effective soil 
removal, whereas laminar conditions promote boundary layer formation that restricts 
cleaning. 
Temperature effects were strongly dependent on soil condition. For fresh soils, 
increasing the temperature from 20 °C to 40 °C did not accelerate cleaning at constant 
Reynolds number: both conditions achieved complete cleaning under turbulence, in 5 
minutes at 20 °C and in 10 minutes at 40 °C. By contrast, for dried soils, temperature 
substantially enhanced cleaning. At 20 °C and turbulent flow, ~26% cleaning was 
achieved after 20 minutes, increasing to ~92% after 45 minutes. At 40 °C under 
turbulent flow, however, full cleaning was achieved within 20 minutes. These findings 
underline the importance of cleaning before soils dry, or applying pre-soaking 
strategies to mitigate drying effects. 
Dead-leg studies demonstrated the impact of both length and orientation on cleaning 
penetration. In the 12 cm horizontal branch (L/D ≈ 4.8), cleaning penetration averaged 
~64% (7.7 cm) after 20 minutes at 2000 L/h, while in the 25 cm horizontal branch (L/D 
≈ 10) penetration dropped to 13.5% (4.5 cm). Orientation effects were equally critical. 
In the vertical-up branch (12 cm), cleaning penetration reached only 36.6% ± 11.2% 
(3.5 cm) due to air entrapment and poor fluid exchange. By contrast, the vertical-down 
branch (12 cm) achieved 100% cleaning across the full length (12 cm), supported by 
favorable drainage. Replicate tests in the horizontal configuration confirmed the trend, 
with penetration averaging 55.6% ± 7.7% (6.7 ± 0.9 cm). These results experimentally 
==PAGE:81==
 
 
65
validate the EHEDG and ASME BPE guidelines, which limit dead-leg L/D ratios to ≤1–
2. 
The grid-counting method used in this study proved to be an effective semi-quantitative 
approach for evaluating cleaning performance. It revealed spatial cleaning patterns, 
including preferential removal from central or downstream regions of the plate and 
delayed cleaning at edges, likely arising from turbulence distribution, recirculation 
zones, or variations in soil thickness. This diagnostic capability offers greater resolution 
than binary “clean/not clean” checks and provides a practical tool for CIP validation. 
As part of the study, an initial riboflavin coverage test was performed on the 13F CIP 
module to assess spray and flow distribution. A stainless-steel plate coated with 0.2% 
riboflavin solution was subjected to rinse cycles at 500 L/h and 1000 L/h. After the 500 
L/h cycle, residual fluorescence was still visible under UV light, indicating incomplete 
cleaning coverage due to insufficient turbulence. By contrast, no fluorescent residues 
were detected after the 1000 L/h cycle, demonstrating that higher flow velocity 
improved contact and coverage. Photos of the test samples under UV light are shown 
in Appendix C. 
While riboflavin provided useful qualitative information, its limitations were also 
recognized. In particular, the method is sensitive to uneven distribution of the riboflavin 
film, lighting conditions, camera settings, and user interpretation, all of which can lead 
to variability in the results. Riboflavin is therefore suitable for verifying coverage 
(contact) but does not measure actual cleaning effectiveness. These insights underline 
that riboflavin should be considered a complementary verification tool rather than a 
stand-alone measure of cleaning. For future work, more systematic riboflavin trials 
combined with realistic soils are recommended to strengthen CIP verification and link 
coverage data with actual soil removal performance. 
In summary, this work demonstrates that effective CIP requires a holistic approach: 
turbulent flow (Re > 4000), moderate heating (~40–50 °C), timely cleaning to prevent 
soil drying, and hygienic design to minimize stagnant zones. The results provide 
quantitative evidence for the combined impact of flow regime, temperature, soil 
condition, and dead-leg geometry, while also offering practical recommendations for 
==PAGE:82==
 
 
66
CIP cycle design, equipment layout, and cleaning validation in compliance with 
industry standards.

8 Future Work 
Future research should expand beyond the current scope by testing a wider range of 
soils, including pharmaceutical excipients, biological residues, and mixed soil systems, 
to improve the applicability of results. The use of chemical cleaning agents at different 
concentrations and higher temperatures should also be systematically studied to better 
reflect industrial cleaning protocols. 
Scale-up studies are recommended to validate the laboratory findings in pilot- and full-
scale CIP systems, accounting for larger pipe diameters, more complex layouts, and 
realistic process conditions. Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) could be employed 
to visualize flow distribution, shear stresses, and dead zones within geometries, 
thereby complementing experimental observations. 
The original project description envisioned both the conceptual and detailed design of 
a new CIP pilot unit, supported by experimental work. Due to time constraints and 
fabrication delays, the design phases could not be completed within the thesis 
timeframe. Instead, this work focused mainly on experimental investigations of key 
hygienic design challenges, particularly dead-leg cleaning behavior and the influence 
of flow regime and temperature. For future work, these findings could be incorporated 
into the conceptual and detailed design of a new CIP pilot unit. 
A further aspect that should be investigated is the observed similarity in cleaning 
performance between 20 °C and 40 °C in the straight-pipe tests. While this study 
suggested that hydrodynamics dominated over temperature effects under these 
conditions, more detailed experiments are needed to determine whether this trend is 
reproducible or specific to the present setup. 
Finally, future work should focus on applying the insights from this study to the 
development of CIP strategies tailored for the pharmaceutical industry. In this sector, 
where cleaning validation is a regulatory requirement, future investigations should 
emphasize the integration of representative hygienic design challenges—such as long 
dead legs, complex piping networks, and heat-sensitive soils—into pilot- and industrial-
scale studies. Particular attention should be given to quantifying cleaning performance 
with advanced monitoring tools (e.g., inline sensors, digital twins, and image-based 
validation) and optimizing CIP cycles for efficiency, reproducibility, and sustainability. 
==PAGE:84==
 
 
68
Such efforts would not only support compliance with regulatory frameworks (e.g., FDA, 
EMA, GMP, and ASME BPE) but also provide industry with validated approaches to 
reduce cleaning times, minimize chemical and water consumption, and ensure patient 
safety through robust contamination control.
