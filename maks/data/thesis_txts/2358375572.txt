Abstract
This master thesis addresses high temperature heat pumps and their possibilities for
application in the pulp and paper industry.
Initially the pulp and paper industry is examined with regards to locating waste heat
sources that can be utilized in order to improve energy eﬃciency and reduce fossil fuel
consumption. Heat pump technology is examined as well, with regards to uncovering
what parameters are important for high temperature heat pump operation.
By performing a case study, it can be seen that hot waste air from drying processes
are the best sources for use with the heat pump.
After potential sources are located, a case study for simulation of the heat pump is
presented. The heat pump is simulated in EES, in order to uncover what is required
in order to make the heat pump work.
The simulations show that feasible COPs are obtainable at both 110 ◦C and 150 ◦C.
Based on the simulation results, the compressor will be the main concern, when oper-
ating a heat pump at high temperatures. The compressor needs to be lubricated and
cooled properly, in order to keep it running. Based on information from compressor
manufacturers, it appears possible to construct a compressor that is able to deliver the
required conditions, while lubricant oils that remains stable under the given conditions
are used.
There are however still several parameters that needs to be taken into account and an
experimental setup should be set up in order to test whether the simulations and real
life conditions are similar.
If the calculated results can be validated, a high temperature heat pump should have
great potential within the paper industry.

1
Introduction
Heat pumps have been proven a good solution for increasing energy eﬃciency in sev-
eral applications both commercial and private. Heat pumps are normally applied at
relatively low temperatures and with low temperature lifts as well. They are composed
of a closed circuit with a compressor that moves refrigerant between a condenser heat
exchanger (HEX) and an evaporator HEX. By applying diﬀerent pressures in the sys-
tem, it is possible to utilize diﬀerent boiling points for the refrigerant and thus moving
energy. When done correctly, heat pumps are several times more eﬃcient than e.g.
electrical heating when comparing input energy against the output energy, also deﬁned
by the coeﬃcient of performance (COP).
Since many industrial applications require large temperature lifts which heat pumps
traditionally can not deliver eﬃciently, it has not been customary to implement heat
pumps in high temperature industrial applications.
In the Industry, large amounts of waste heat are often wasted due to the temperature
being too low to be eﬀectively used in a way that is feasible.
If it is possible to apply heat pumps to raise the temperature to a level that can
make it useful with a relatively low amount of technical work, the waste heat to the
environment can be decreased and the overall mill eﬃciency improved.
For this concept to be useful in industrial applications, the heat pumps will be required
to deliver temperatures above 100 ◦C.
In order to discover whether it is possible to realize the proposed concept, an explo-
ration of existing state of the art technologies must be performed.
This will be followed by simulations of diﬀerent types of heat pumps with diﬀerent
refrigerants, in order to ﬁnd the most viable solution.
Apart from what is more eﬃcient, the scale of the application will also be taken into
consideration, as the size of industrial application will have an inﬂuence on how to
implement the solution if possible.
Technological advances and the development of the energy market and politics makes
heat pumps potentially very attractive for the industry, both environmentally and
economically. High costs of fossil fuels as well as a desire and a need to consider envi-
ronmental impact paired with potential operational cost reductions are all arguments
for exploring heat pump technology further.
There are many potential industries for which heat pump technology can be interest-
ing. In order to maintain focus, it is however to limit the scope of the study, therefore
the pulp and paper industry has been chosen.
The Pulp and paper industry is the fourth largest industrial consumer of energy in
Europe. With its energy intensive production processes it accounts for 5.7 % [1] of the
total industrial energy consumption. The production involves considerable amounts
of high temperature steam as well as water and air heat exchanging, making it good
candidate for this study.
This industry is under a lot of attention when it comes to the environmental impact
of its production both with regards to deforestation and recycling. Since the paper
production is such an energy intensive process, improvements to the production eﬃ-
ciency with regards to the environment are also sought after. The production of paper
requires both large amounts of both energy and resources. In modern paper mills the
majority of chemicals and water in the process is recovered and recycled in order to
reduce pollution.
1
==PAGE:12==
There are already several proposals for increasing energy eﬃciency in paper mills in
several diﬀerent smaller areas that can return the investment within a year.
For a high temperature heat pump to be desirable in a paper mill, it needs to perform
well enough to be able to compete with the alternatives.
1.1
Aim of the Thesis
The work included in this report aims towards answering the following questions:
• Is it possible to obtain improved energy eﬃciency in a pulp and paper mill by
implementing a high temperature heat pump based on state of the art technol-
ogy?
• Where in a pulp and paper mill would a high temperature heat pump be appli-
cable?
• Are there any limiting factors that need to be taken into account and how do
they aﬀect the implementation possibilities?
• Is there a potential market for high temperature heat pump implementation in
the pulp and paper industry?
The main reason that these questions are asked, is due to potential energy eﬃciency
improvement. Energy consumption in the Paper Industry is related to the consump-
tion of both biomass, fossil fuels and electricity. By reducing energy consumption
it would be possible to reduce operational costs as well as reducing the use of fossil
fuels to reduce the level of greenhouse gas emissions. If fossil fuel consumption can
be reduced in favor of electrical energy to a heat pump, this would be a preferable
solution when considering cost beneﬁt of investments.
The goal is to ﬁnd areas in a modern Pulp and Paper mill where high temperature
heat pumps can bring value to the production as well as improve overall feasibility.
The majority of the consumed energy is in the form of thermal energy in various
water, steam and air ﬂows, making it a viable candidate to examine whether high
temperature heat pumps can be implemented to increase energy eﬃciency. This will
however require a heat pump that can perform well. It is therefore necessary to have
a heat pump that is able to and optimized for high temperature operation.
Diﬀerent parts of the heat pump cycle will therefore be investigated with regards to
possible limitations in components and refrigerants.
By doing this, it will be possible to see whether there are any real motive for imple-
menting high temperature heat pumps as an alternative to at least some of the fossil
fuel consumption.

1.2
Thesis outline
There will be obvious energy eﬃciency diﬀerences between newer and older mills which
necessitates the question of where high temperature heat pumps might be relevant.
To simplify the study, it is chosen only to focus at modern Pulp and Paper mills only,
thus considering state of the art mills.
First the pulp and paper industry and heat pumps will be investigated in relation to
obtain an overview of processes and technology involved.
When pulp and paper production and heat pumps have been described, this knowledge
will be used to locate areas of interest with regards to implementing heat pumps.
Based on the performed survey of pulp and paper production and heat pumps, a
case study is formulated with regards to creating a simulation model. The simulation
model is then created, so that it is known what needs to be focused on with regards
to realizing the simulation model.
Using the results from the simulation model, usable refrigerants will be located and
compared. Whether there is a market potential or not, the technology for a potential
implementation will need to be present.
An evaluation will therefore be performed to uncover whether the technology to use
high temperature heat pumps in the desired application is available and feasible.
Components for the heat pump will also be speciﬁed, as it becomes known, what the
requirements to them will be.
When looking at the technology used in the heat pumps, it is relevant to look for
potential bottle necks in the technology, limiting factors that deﬁnes how high working
temperature is achievable in a heat pump.
When looking at this, the compressor
will be an obvious component of interest, as it is the main moving component in a
conventional heat pump system.
When working outside the normal ranges of heat pumps, it will also be relevant to ex-
amine which refrigerants can be used in order to perform a comparison and evaluation
of these with regards to determining the refrigerant most suited.
When the results are found, they will be compared and a brief analysis of the economy
in implementing a high temperature heat pump in pulp and paper production will be
performed.
1.3
Political Framework
Due to improvements in technology and increasing prosperity, the global energy con-
sumption is rapidly increasing and majority of energy consumption today is based on
fossil fuels. [14]
There are several concerns with the use of fossil fuels, as they are being used today.
There is a ﬁnite amount of fossil fuels available and as consumption increases it be-
comes harder and more expensive to extract them, while new fossil fuel sources are
forming signiﬁcantly slower than they are currently being consumed.
Another concern with fossil fuels is the impact on the climate in the form of pollution
and release of greenhouse gasses.
3
==PAGE:14==
By continuing on this path will severely aﬀect the climate and future generations that
will have to live with the consequences.
In order to avoid this, there are several steps that can be taken. Fossil fuels can be
used more eﬃciently and in many cases be replaced by renewable energy sources.
Industry accounts for 25 % of global energy consumption and in many cases, signiﬁcant
savings can be obtained by implementing solutions that can be payed back within short
period of time. This means that many energy eﬃciency improvements are actually
feasible and can help to reduce long term costs.
In Denmark an energy eﬃciency policy has been in place since the 1990’s that focuses
on climate and energy goals.
It has been based on CO2 taxation as well as tax
reimbursements and subsidies for energy saving initiatives.
This policy utilizes the ’carrot and stick’ idea, in that it rewards those that chose to
invest in energy improvements and punishes those that do not. [15]
A continuous eﬀort is required in order to improve performance every year in order to
reach 100 % renewable energy and to be subject for continued tax beneﬁts.
This is used as an incentive for the companies to continuously look into and invest in
energy eﬃciency improvements.
1.4
Readers guide / Structure of paper
This thesis will be written after the IMRAD structure and will thus be composed of
four general parts.
1. The ﬁrst part contains the introduction which contain basic information regard-
ing the theoretical foundation and importance of the chosen topic. The aim of
the paper and related research questions is also described here as well as any
relevant limitations in the study.
2. The second part is the theoretic investigation, where theory and literature ﬁnd-

Pulp and paper industry
The following section on pulp and paper industry is primarily based on CEPI Key
statistics 2015[14], Best Available Technologies (BAT)[1] and Energy Eﬃciency Im-
provement and Cost Saving Opportunities for the Pulp and Paper Industry[16].
CEPI is a non proﬁt organization that represents the pulp and paper industry in Eu-
rope. They provide information on and for the industry in order to share experience
and knowledge while BAT is a product of the European commission.
Based on this, they are considered as credible sources of information for this report.
Paper as it is today, is the result of an evolution in production techniques throughout
the last two millennia.
Production techniques and materials for production have been altered and improved
several times to better accommodate increasing demands for paper.
Despite the digital age, paper is still widely used for a wide variety of applications such
as printing and packaging. The paper industry in Europe directly employs 179,000
people and has a turnover of e79 billion adding e16.5 billion to the EU GDP which
is a contribution of approximately 0.1 %.[14]
In the nineties awareness about the paper industry arose, as the impact it had on the
environment became a concern.
This has resulted in several signiﬁcant changes in how paper is produced today.
An increase in the use of recycled paper of up to 46% of the total raw material con-
sumption have reduced deforestation of rain forests.[14]
Paper production also requires large amounts of water and is an area where major
improvements have also been implemented. At present, it is now possible to clean and
return the vast majority of water used in the processes.
Beside raw materials, paper production is very energy intensive, using 1.3 million TJ
annually. The majority of this energy comes from the combustion of fossil fuels and
biomass within the mill.[14]
A large part of the energy consumption in a Pulp and Paper mill is in the form of ther-
mal energy. If this energy consumption can be reduced and/or become more eﬃcient,
this would result in both ﬁnancial savings as well as a more sustainable production.
Today, paper is commonly a thin material that is produced by pressing moist ﬁbers
of pulp into sheets and then drying them in order to produce the ﬁnal paper.
The production consists of two processes that can be located within the same mill
or the pulp can alternatively be produced and dried in a separate mill and then
transported to the paper mill.
Even if the pulp and the paper production is co-located, the pulp can still be dried
and stored or sold as market pulp.

Pulping
The pulping process frees the ﬁbers in the wood from the lignin that binds the ﬁbers

When the lignin has been removed, a watery slurry remains which makes up the raw

To produce pulp, there are three diﬀerent processes.
• Mechanical pulping, where the ﬁbers are separated from the wood by grinding
it. This process gives high yields of up to 95%, but does not dissolve the lignin
which causes the ﬁber quality to become low. It is mostly used for low-quality
products.
• Semi-chemical pulping is a combination of mechanical and chemical pulping,
where the wood chips undergo a mild chemical process before being mechanically
pulped. The yield from this process is also somewhere in between the yield from
the mechanical and the chemical pulping.
• Chemical pulping is the most used production method, as it is being used by
almost 85 % of the european industry. It has a lower yield than mechanical
pulping, but the quality is much higher and can be used for printing paper etc.
It separates the ﬁbers from the lignin by dissolving the lignin bonds. This is
done primarily with either sulfate or sulﬁte processes. The sulfate process is also
known as the Kraft process[17] and is used in around 80 % of the production.
The name kraft is derived from the german word for strength, due to the superior
strength of paper produced with this method.

Wood preparation
Wood is the main source of raw material for ﬁber in pulp production. When the
wood arrives at the mill it is ﬁrst debarked by rotating logs in large steel drums, by
using friction or with high pressure water jets. It is however the ﬁrst two mechanical
methods that are most common, partly because they require less energy but also due
to that the bark is also often used as fuel in the mill. The bark will need to be dried
if the water jet debarking process is used, before it can be used as fuel.

==PAGE:18==
After the logs have been debarked they are chipped into small pieces and sorted for
optimal size depending on type of wood and pulping method.
Waste material from sawmills are often used for pulp as well.
The wood chips are treated with steam and saturated in a solution of black and white
liquor. Blac, white and green liquor will not be described into detail, but are diﬀerent
chemical solutions used for the treatment of the wood.
The wood chips are cooked in batch- or continuous cookers called digesters. This leaves
a combination of chemicals dissolved in water, lignin and raw pulp, known as brown
stock. The brown stock is then blown to collection tanks, where steam and volatiles
are separated. Before the brown stock is being used for anything, it is screened for
debris and other mechanical contaminants.
Chemical recovery
When debris has been removed from the brown stock, it is washed, usually in several
steps.
This is done to separate and recover the cooking chemicals from the pulp.
This allows as much as 98 % of the chemicals to be recovered and reused whilst the
remaining ”black liquor” can be used as fuel for the steam production, improving
overall energy eﬃciency.
The Kraft chemical recovery process consists of four main stages.
• Black liquor is the waste product that remains after separating the ﬁbers from
the lignin in the wood. It consists mainly of lignin hemicellulose and the inor-
ganic chemicals that are used in the process. The composition of black liquor is
approximatly 15% solids and 15% chemicals in a watery solution. To recover the
chemicals in the black liquor is concentrated in multiple-eﬀect evaporators, that
evaporates and removes water from the mixture through multiple steps. Some
organic chemicals are separated and recovered during this process.
• When the black liquor reaches a concentration of 65-80 % it is burned in the
Recovery boiler to recover any inorganic chemicals. This process also supplies
thermal energy for driving a turbine that produces electricity. Depending on the
amount of solids in the black liquor,if the content is low it can be a signiﬁcant
supplement to the energy or if the content is high it can make the mill self
sustaining in the means of electricity.
• The recovery boiler is also used to generate high and low pressure steam as well
as condensate for the mill, which contributes to the mills energy demands.
• The solid remains from the recovery boiler are dissolved in water and added
calcium oxide to make the solution become calcium hydroxide, also known as
white liquor.
Precipitating Calcium carbonate is taken from the white liquor and is recovered
by calcination to produce calcium oxide for the recausticizing.

Pulp preparation
When the raw pulp comes from the pulp mill, it has a varying brownish color due to
remaining lignin. To achieve bright white paper with resistance to color change over
time, the pulp must be bleached. Approximately half the produced pulp is bleached.
Depending on the pulping process, there are diﬀerent methods for bleaching due to
the varying lignin content.
The chemical pulp is bleached in several stages that alternate between washing and
chemical treatment. The chemical process used in approximately 95 % of the cases is
called Elementally chlorine free (ECF) bleaching.
When the pulp is sold as market pulp or if the pulp production is too high compared
the paper mill production, the pulp is dried. The pulp water content is reduced from
approximately 50 % to 10 %, by ﬁrst pressing the pulp into sheets and then drying it
with multiple stage evaporators.
When placing the pulp production and the paper production adjacent to each other,
this step can often be avoided, unless the production outputs are not matched.
This is a highly energy intensive process and it is preferably avoided if possible due
to large amounts of waste heat.
Paper pulp generally consists of 28 % secondary ﬁbers apart from wood ﬁbers, that
consists mostly of recycled paper.
Recycled paper often contains ink and other contaminants, that must be removed
before using it for high-quality paper and paperboard. It is however very normal that
the recycled paper is simply used for low-quality paper products without de-inking.
The bleached pulp and the recycled pulp are mixed together before being processed
into paper.

dried by hot air and then coated in one or more steps. The coating is applied as a
uniform liquid with rollers and then dried with hot air after each coating.
The drying process removes excess water from the paper by evaporation. The moist
air is then removed by fresh air circulation.

2.3
Areas of interest in the pulp and paper industry
When looking at the diﬀerent process steps in both pulp and paper production, there
are several areas of interest to locating potential waste heat sources where energy
utilization can be optimized by implementation of a heat pump.
Energy consumption
Biomass and fossil fuels are used to produce high pressure steam that drives turbo
generators in order to produce electricity for the mill. When the steam has been used
for electricity generation it is typically considered medium - low pressure and is then
used in the production.
Throughout both the pulp and the paper production steps, several of these contains
varying levels of heating of either the product or the treatment chemicals used on
the product as well as removal of water from the product. This heating demand is
supplied by the extracted steam from the electricity generation.
Since the focus area of this paper is heat pumps and their implementation, the energy
consumption of the pulp and paper mill will not be covered to any great extent except
for parts of the production where it is relevant. That means that a more detailed
description of fuel consumption and electricity and steam generation will be omitted.
[19]
Pulp Production
There are many areas in the pulp production that have potential for heat pump inte-
gration.
The resulting ﬂue gas from the burning of black liquor that has passed through the
turbine, is used for high and low pressure steam through direct heat exchanging. Re-
maining thermal energy in the ﬂue gas could however be utilized by implementing a
heat pump in order to recover energy, that can not be used in direct HEXs for steam
production due to the too low temperature.
Furthermore the drying of the pulp is a very energy intensive process, that may can
contain a large potential for waste heat recovery. This is however a process that is
avoided if possible, due to the large energy demand of the process. Most pulp and
paper mills are placed in adjacency to each other, thus making this process superﬂuous
and therefore less interesting. The process is similar to the one used in paper drying,
and will be further described later.
Paper drying
Paper drying by far accounts for the largest thermal energy consumption in paper
production, as it uses large amounts of steam to dry the paper sheets. The steam
is passed through rolls that the paper is passed between, in order to heat the paper
so that the contained water evaporates. This is illustrated in ﬁgure 6 and 5. Steam
is also used to heat dry air that is passed through the drying chamber, in order to
further help with evaporation and to remove the moisture from the paper.

3.1
Types of cycles
In most refrigeration cycles, the principle of vapor compression is being used and
will therefore also be the main focus when looking at heat pump components and
technology.
3.1.1
Subcritical cycle
The critical point is of great importance for a refrigerant. When it is reached, it is no
longer possible to separate liquid from gas. The critical point is deﬁned by both the
temperature and the pressure.
The typical refrigeration cycle operates at sub-critical conditions, meaning that the
refrigerant is cooled to below critical temperature and kept below the critical pressure,
in order to allow condensation to occur. The refrigerant is then passed through a
throttling valve and evaporated at a lower temperature before being re-compressed.
In this way the subcritical cycle relies heavily on the transfer of latent energy in both
the condenser and the evaporator.
Refrigerants have traditionally been chosen or created to operate optimally under
given conditions. For this to work, the critical temperature must be above the desired
condensation temperature, otherwise phase change of the refrigerant is not possible in
the condenser.

Supercritical cycles does not operate with condensation, thus the heat transfer occurs

The term supercritical is derived from that the cycle operates above the critical point,
which is the highest point on the phase curve in the log(p),h-diagram. The diﬀerence

are similar to those in the sub-critical cycle. The isenthalpic cooling of the refriger-

Transcritical refrigeration cycles originates with the use of R744 (CO2) in heat pumps.
R744 boils at -87.8 ◦C and reaches the critical point at 31 ◦C, making it impossible
to condensate in typical heat pump cycles.
In order to use R744 in heat pumps, the supercritical cycle was invented. The super-
critical cycle utilizes a gas cooler instead of a condenser and does not use latent heat
from condensation in the heat sink, only cooling of the superheated refrigerant.
Supercritical cycles are preferably used, when the properties of the refrigerant does
not allow sub-critical conditions, when there are no better alternatives for the given
conditions.

3.2
Heat pump components
3.2.1
Heat exchangers (HEX)
Heat exchangers (HEX) transfer thermal energy to and from the refrigerant and the
ﬂuid that is being cooled or heated. Diﬀerent designs exist, but the basic idea is to
create as large a heat transferring area between the two ﬂuids as possible, with as
large a heat transfer coeﬃcient as possible.
When designing a HEX, the following equation must be taken into consideration:
19
==PAGE:30==
˙Q = UA∆T
(3.1)
where:
˙Q = Heat transfer
U = Heat transfer coeﬃcient
A = Area of heat transfer
∆T = Temperature diﬀerence
˙Q is the heat transfer and is deﬁned by the application.
U is dependent on the material being used in the HEX and is commonly copper or
aluminum due to high values. This leaves two variable parameters, A or ∆T.
It is preferable to have a small ∆T, in order to minimize heat losses, when reducing
∆T, the area however have to increase, meaning that the HEX would become very
large. This means that there will always be a compromise between size and eﬃciency.
Due to this, there is a general rule of thumb in the energy industry, saying that ∆T=5-
10K.
Larger values of ∆T are typically used in larger industrial applications, whereas lower
values are used in smaller and more precise applications.
Types of HEXs
A common type of HEX is the ﬁnned tube HEX, which can be seen in ﬁgure 18. It
consists of several turns of tubing in a ﬂat zig zag pattern with many thin ﬁns, in
order to maximize the heat transferring area.
The plate HEX, can be seen in ﬁgure 19. It consists of several diﬀerent plates with
patterns and holes that leads the refrigerant across the plates in several layers.
Shell and tube HEX are composed of a shell with a large amount of tubes going
through it. This is illustrated in ﬁgure 20.
Shell and tube HEXs can be separated into two types, based on how the ﬂow is di-
rected. In dry expansion, the refrigerant ﬂows through the tubes and the cooled ﬂuid
ﬂows in the shell. In ﬂooded evaporation, the ﬂows are opposite.

3.2.2
Throttling valve
The throttling valve is positioned between the condenser and the evaporator and
throttles the ﬂow in order to cause pressure loss. By lowering the pressure, it enables
the the cooled and condensed refrigerant to evaporate at a lower pressure.
When throttling the refrigerant, both the temperature and pressure decrease, and the
process is often considered isenthalpic.
3.2.3
Compressors
Compressors are utilized in heat pumps to move the refrigerant in the system and to
create a pressure diﬀerence in the system that enables condensation and evaporation
at diﬀerent temperatures. The compressor sucks in evaporated refrigerant from the
evaporator and compresses it discharges it towards the condenser.
The compressor is the most expensive component in the heat pump, as well as the
only moving part that can be aﬀected by temperatures and vapor quality.
Depending on the application, there will be diﬀerent requirements for the compressor
in terms of capacity, eﬃciency, operational range as well as noise and vibration in
some cases. Due to these varying demands to the compressor, there are also several
diﬀerent types of compressors.[22]

In order to narrow the scope for the selection of compressor types, it is chosen to focus

Positive displacement compressors are separated into two subgroups, reciprocating
and rotary.
These types of compressors can be one of three types, depending on the application;
hermetic, semi-hermetic or open. [11]
Smaller compressors that are used in refrigerators and air condition is usually of the
hermetic type, since they are cheap to produce in large quantities and can be easily
replaced in case of breakdowns. They incorporate the compressor as well as the elec-
tric motor within a hermetically sealed casing that is most commonly welded together.
When using larger compressors, production costs increase and it therefore makes sense
to build compressors that can be opened and serviced or repaired in order to increase
the lifespan of the compressor, thus the semi-hermetic compressor. They share the
similar properties with the hermetic compressors, the casing can however be opened,
in order to perform maintenance and repairs.
In an open compressor, the motor is not placed inside the casing. The crankshaft
protrudes from the casing so that it can be driven by an external motor and the
casing can be opened.
By keeping the motor outside the compressor casing, it is
possible to run the compressors with larger motors and other power sources than
electricity. By positioning the motor outside the compressor casing it will not transfer
any excess heat to the refrigerant. Due to the protruding crankshaft there is however
a necessity for seals, that have to be inspected and replaced at regular intervals. Open
compressor types are most commonly used in large scale industrial applications. [11]

Reciprocating compressors
Figure 30: Reciprocating piston compressor type [7]
Reciprocating compressors as can be seen in ﬁgure 30, also known as piston compres-
sors have volumetric eﬃciencies that are very similar to internal combustion engines
due to the similarities in construction.
The movement of the piston in the compressor cylinder produces suction and com-
pression of the refrigerant.
Much like combustion engines, the cylinder chamber also utilizes inlet and outlet
valves, that open and close. This ensures that refrigerant is being moved through the
compressor as eﬃciently as possible.
The construction is relatively simple and takes advantage of the experiences from the
combustion engine, which makes the reciprocating compressor relatively cheap.
Due to the cyclic movement of the pistons, this type of compressor is generally both
noisy and vibratory.

Rotary compressors
Rotary compressors consists of rotary vane, scroll, screw and centrifugal compressors.
They are used in several diﬀerent applications and operating ranges.
Rotary vane compressors
are composed of a cylindrical casing with an opening
in each end for suction and for discharge. Inside the casing there is an eccentrically
positioned rotor. The eccentric rotation moves the refrigerant through the casing and
due to a gradually decreasing volume, the refrigerant is compressed.
Figure 31: Rotary vane compressor type [8]
The rotary vane compressor is both simple, cheap and also quite compact. It is often
used in household appliances. The function of the rotary vane compressor can be seen
in ﬁgure 31
The rotary vane compressor also exist in a version with two rotors that rotate towards
each other to increase eﬃciency while maintaining low cost.
More advanced versions uses inverters and brushless motors to improve eﬃciency.
Rotary compressors are able to apply the highest capacity in comparison to dimension
ratio and are typically also able to reach more than 15 % higher engine speeds than
e.g. scroll compressors.
They are suitable for applications up to 50 kW with good results, both domestically
and industrially.
Screw compressors
Screw compressors are based on either a single or two intermeshing screw rotors in a
casing. A depiction of a dual screw compressor can be seen in ﬁgure 32.
As the screws rotate, they draw the refrigerant in and as it moves between the lobes,
the volume between the lobes decrease and causes compression.
After compression, the refrigerant is directly released towards the condenser.

3.3
Refrigerants
Refrigerants are the working ﬂuids used in vapor compression heat pump cycles.
When selecting refrigerant, there are certain criteria that needs to be considered.
29
==PAGE:40==
The refrigerant should have properties that are suited for the application, both chem-
ically and physically.
Another aspect is safety and the risks involved in the use of the refrigerant. This is
a necessary consideration, in case of leakages. If the chosen refrigerant is ﬂammable
or toxic, certain precautions have to be taken into account, to ensure the safety of
personal working around, or with the heat pump.
Apart from personal safety, there is also the consideration of the eﬀect on the envi-
ronment if the refrigerant escapes the cycle.
In the beginning, any available and usable refrigerant was used without regards for
toxicity, ﬂammability and potential environmental concerns. These refrigerants were
mainly hydrocarbons (HC) and natural refrigerants (NR).
When the market for refrigeration expanded during the 1930’s due to new and in-
creasing application areas, it was necessary to develop new and safer refrigerants.
This lead to the development and usage of chloro-ﬂuoro-carbons (CFC) and hydro-
chloro-ﬂuoro-carbons (HCFC). They were stable, safe, eﬃcient and quickly gained
popularity.
Time passed and knowledge of the environment increased, along with awareness of
global warming potential (GWP) and ozone depletion potential (ODP) of refrigerants.
Research into refrigerants and their properties resulted in the introduction of hydro-
ﬂuoro-carbons (HFC), that are currently one of the most common types of refrigerants
on the market.
Due to the GWP and ODP, many refrigerants are being or have been phased out.
CFCs was completely phased out in 2005 due to that they were classiﬁed as class I
ODS in the clean air act.[29] HCFCs will be phased out completely by 2030, as they
are classed as class II ODS. They have been regulated heavily during the last 10 years,
as they are being gradually phased out.[30] The use of HCFCs have been reduced with
90% in 2015 and will be reduced by 99.5% by 2020.
Due to the phasing out of CFCs and HCFCs, they will not be considered for applica-
tion in this report, as they are not available in the near future.
Many of the older refrigerants have been replaced by modern HFCs and NRs, which
with modern technologies have been able to replace the old refrigerants.
HFCs still have a high GWP which has caused concerns about using them as a replace-
ment for CFCs and HCFCs in the future.[31] New regulations have been put in place
for HFCs, initiating a phase out, beginning in 2015 to reach a ﬁfth of the production
in 2014 by 2030.[32]
The european union’s ﬂourinated gas (f-gas) regulations prohibits the use of f-gasses
with a GWP of over 150 in vehicles and there is an increased focus on unintentional
release of f-gasses into the atmosphere from the year 2017.
Regulations on HFCs beginning in 2015 also meant limiting the amount and types of
HFCs on the market, as well as banning the use in many new products.[32]
This means that when looking at suitable refrigerants, the choice of HFCs will be
limited and that they in the future will be phased out as well.

Alternatives to HFCs could be Hydro-ﬂuoro-oleﬁns (HFO), HCs, and other NRs.
HFOs are a subgroup of HFCs but with a diﬀerent chemical composition that makes
them more reactive than HFCs. This helps to lower the GWP of HFOs and have
caused HFOs to be excepted from the planned HFC regulations. [33]
HCs and NRs are not ODS and most of them have a GWP rating of around 3.
There are several advantages by using HCs and NRs in that they have low environmen-
tal inﬂuence, they have good thermodynamic properties that can increase the COP
and they are cheap and easily available. They are often easy to implement in existing
systems, since they can operate under the same conditions as many older refrigerant
types. HCs are however ﬂammable and are therefore subject to safety legislations.[34]
Refrigerant properties and selection criteria
When choosing the right refrigerant for a heat pump, there are several necessary
considerations that inﬂuence the decision.
Environmental considerations
As mentioned in the beginning of the thesis, it is necessary to consider environmental
impact and regulations on the use of refrigerant. ODP and GWP are crucial, since
they directly aﬀect whether they will be available future.
The lowest possible values of ODP and GWP are desirable and in this project it is
chosen to set the limit at 150, which is also the applied limit for vehicles. This is
meant as a precaution to ensure that there will not be any immediate environmental
concerns.
Safety considerations
Depending on the application of the heat pump, safety concerns will be relevant to
consider.
Refrigerants are classiﬁed according to ASHRAE standard 34 [11]:
”Class A: Toxicity not identiﬁed at concentrations below 400 ppm by volume.
Class B: Evidence of toxicity identiﬁed at concentrations below 400 ppm.
31
==PAGE:42==
Group 1: No ﬂame propagation in air at 18 ◦C and 101 kPa.
Group 2: Lower ﬂammability limit (LFL) greater tham 0.10 kg/m3 at 21 ◦C
Group 3: Highly ﬂammable as deﬁned by LFL less than or equal to 0.10 kg/m3
at 21 ◦C and 101 kPa or heat of combustion greater than or equal to 19,000 kJ/kg.”
Toxicity and ﬂammability is something to take into consideration especially if the
circuit is to be placed in locations where leaks might aﬀect workers or products. If the
refrigerant is toxic and/or ﬂammable, precautions must be taken, in order to ensure
safety. With modern refrigeration systems and the proper safety precautions, this
is however not something that should cause any major concerns for the considered
application.
Refrigerant properties considerations
Diﬀerent refrigerants do not have the same properties and cannot be applied optimally
in all conditions.
When either the critical temperature or pressure is reached, gas can no longer liquefy.
This has signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the cycle and the conditions the heat pump can
operate under.
The critical temperature aﬀects whether the system is able to operate under subcrit-
ical conditions, or if it is necessary to operate under supercritcal conditions.
A high enthalpy of vaporization or latent heat is the energy required for the refrigerant
to undergo a phase change. When the refrigerant is evaporating or condensing in the
respective HEXs, there is no temperature change in the refrigerant. This means that
there is a more eﬃcient heat exchange. In order to achieve a high latent heat exchange
in the HEXs, a high enthalpy of vaporization is desirable.
Price and availability is something that is relevant to consider, but is not something
that should have any major impact, unless the system leaks signiﬁcantly. [35]

3.4
Limiting factors in heat pumps
Heat pumps normally operate at relatively low temperatures, making them ideal for
air conditioning, refrigerators and other similar applications.
When temperatures
above 100 ◦C are desired, there are several parameters that need to be considered.
The refrigerant must be able to handle high temperatures and pressures, as well as
the components in the heat pump must be able to handle these conditions. When
constructing the piping and HEXs, it is a question of using appropriate materials and
material dimensions as the same principle are used in several other types of high tem-
perature application, e.g. power plants and exhaust gas heat recovery.
The compressor must be able to operate with high discharge temperatures, without
being unreliable.
High discharge temperatures for compressors are caused by the need for high con-
densation temperatures, low evaporation temperatures and high PRs. [45] These are
34
==PAGE:45==
all conditions that will be present in the simulation model and the choice of compres-
sor must therefore be suitable for the conditions under which it will operate under.[46]
In order to enable a compressor to deliver high discharge temperatures, it needs to be
cooled and lubricated in a way that enables continuous operation.
When the compressor operates at high temperatures, loss of oil ﬁlm, carbonization
and oil decomposition can occur, causing a lack of lubricant in the compressor and
ultimately a breakdown. [47]
”Machinery lubrication” [48] states:
”Premium-grade ISO VG 32 turbine oils are used more often than the heavier viscosity
grades. The typical viscosity index is 97, with a pour point around -37oC (-35oF).
Oxidation stability (per ASTM D943) should exceed 5,000 hours and the ﬂash point

3.5
Experimental technologies
This section brieﬂy describes experimental technologies that could be interesting to
take into consideration in the future, when they are further developed and more sta-
tistical data is available on the performance.
Since all of these technologies are currently on a experimental level, it is diﬃcult to
say whether they will be relevant or not. It is however interesting to compare their
claimed results with those from the simulations performed throughout this project, to
see whether they are competitive.
Absorption-compression
The absorption compression heat pump is a hybrid tech-
nology in the sense that it uses a mixture of both H2O and NH3 as the refrigerant. The
system incorporates a gas/liquid tank to separate the mixture and send it through
a compressor and a pump respectively. The refrigerant will be a mixture of gas and
liquid through most of the cycle, but other than this, the technology is similar to
regular heat pumps and operate with much the same components and pressures.
It is claimed that this technology is able to raise the temperature from 65 ◦C to 110
◦C at a COP of at least 4.[49]
Stirling
A heat pump based on the stirling cycle that uses helium as a refriger-
ant. The concept is basically double-acting piston compressors with integrated heat
exchangers that are connected to the external energy system.
The temperature range for this technology is 80 ◦C to 200 ◦C.
The working medium never undergoes any phase change, which is claimed to reduce
restrictions on source and sink temperatures. It is also quick to ﬁnd new equilibrium
when temperatures change, making the system more robust.[50]
Rotation heat pump
The rotational heat pump is based on a closed fan propelled
process. The process is being rotated around an axis at high speeds which causes
the gas to be compressed and increases the temperature. The process is 1-phase only
which increases the eﬃciency of the system. When the gas has been compressed and
thus heated, it is passed through a heat exchanger which transfers the heat to a heat
sink. By varying the rpm, it is possible to to alter the compression ratio.
The working area of the rotational heat pump is −20◦C < T < 150◦C and claims a
theoretical COP=10.3 and a practical COP=6. Due to the only movement in the heat
pump being the axial movement, it requires little maintenance.[51]
MVR (Mechanical Vapor re-compression)
Turbo-compressors meant for air
have been converted to work with steam instead and can work in several stages for
improved eﬃciency.
This is currently not a fully developed technology, but some ﬁgures are available.
It is currently possible to upgrade heat energy from a 100 ◦C with a lift of 12-15◦C.
It is small and simple to install, making it ideal for implementation wherever one sees
ﬁt.
The aim is to make it possible to use waste heat in the range of 60 ◦C < T ≤150 ◦C
and to be able to upgrade it as high as to 200 ◦C. There are however no long-term
tests as of yet.[52]

gains in implementing a high temperature heat pump in a real process.

Economical analysis of market
The economical analysis of the market will primarily be based on statistics from CEPI
and BAT. BAT describes several opportunities for signiﬁcant energy savings in the
paper industry. There are many areas in pulp and paper mills, where waste heat can
be utilized or the utilization can be improved. A large part of the waste heat are
water, air and steam which are ideal sources, when it comes to upgrading low quality
waste heat, so that it can be better utilized.
The pulp and paper industry is very diverse as several diﬀerent technologies are used
throughout the production, based on which kind of paper is being produced. To be
able to reach the largest possible market segment, the dominant technologies and
production methods are chosen.
CEPI
According to CEPI, in 2015, there were 763 paper and board mills and 154 pulp mills.
The total pulp production is 36.3 million tonnes and the paper and board production
is 90.9 million tonnes.
Energy consumption
The energy consumption within CEPI is 1.126 million TJ
of fuel and 100,518 GWh of electricity.
The two primary sources of fuel are gas and biomass from e.g. waste materials in the
pulp and paper production. 35% of the fuel consumption consists of gas and 57.6%
consists of biomass. 13.4% of the total energy consumption is electricity and 51.4% of
that electricity is produced by Combined heat and power (CHP) on site.
Pulp production
71.7% of the total pulp production is sulphate pulp. Approxi-
mately 95% of the sulphate pulp is produced with the Kraft process.
39.3% of the pulp production is new wood pulp, whereas 47% is recycled paper and
the remaining 14.7% consist of other non-ﬁbrous materials.
Paper production
The two main types of paper are packaging paper and graphic
paper, which contributes with 50% and 36% of the total paper and board production,
respectively. [14]
BAT - Best available technology
According to BAT, there are many areas of application for heat pumps in the pulp
and paper industry. However the use has been limited by high costs and potential
environmental problems with conventional working ﬂuids. Heat recovery and heat
exchange is however an area where a heat pump could be generally applicable, when
considering the paper drying and coating section. [1]

Discussion
The COP for several of the refrigerants does not decrease signiﬁcantly, when the tem-
perature is increased from 110 ◦C to 150 ◦C. A temperature increase up to 150 ◦C
should cause a larger decrease in COP, due to increased irreversible losses in the sys-
tem.
R717 requires signiﬁcantly lower ﬂow rates than the other two refrigerants, however
at much higher pressures and temperatures. R717 is an aggressive substance, which
requires the piping in the system to be made of stainless steel instead of copper and
will also require special lubricants that does not react with the refrigerant.
When looking at the performance of R717 in the subcritical cycles, it performs at
the same levels as the other refrigerants. When taking this into consideration along
with the potential complications with the use of R717, there will have to be very good
reasons to choose this refrigerant over the others.
When considering the implementation of a heat pump in a paper mill, there are several
important factors to take into account. The heat pump must be economically feasible
in long term use, as well as short term use. The installment of the heat pump need to
be able to return the investment, preferably within a year, according to BAT kr [1].
This must be achievable, while providing a lasting decrease in energy consumption,
without requiring considerable maintenance costs.
Even though the heat pump might be feasible, it may still be dismissed, in favor of
other solutions that provides a better cost beneﬁt.
The beneﬁts from implementing a heat pump and diverting signiﬁcant portions of
waste heat from other processes, will likely provide a better utilization of the energy.
The exhaust air still contains large amounts of energy after leaving the heat pump
evaporator, but the consequences of redirecting the energy in the exhaust air should
be considered and further investigated.
When considering the implementation of a heat pump of this scale, size will have
inﬂuence on whether it is possible to implement, due to available space in the mill. If
it is necessary to erect an additional buildings to house the heat pump, the solution
will no longer be feasible.
When considering the sheer size of the components, mainly in compressor along with
the high temperature conditions, material expansion due to the temperature changes
needs to be considered.
The operational conditions for the heat pump has very high demands to the capa-
bilities of the compressor and the lubrication. It has not been possible to locate any
one manufacturer that can deliver compressors that are able to fulﬁll all of the re-
quirements. Due to this, some information from diﬀerent manufacturers have been
combined. Aerzen uses oil [56], that is able to withstand the high temperatures and
their compressors should therefore be able to withstand the calculated temperatures.
Aerzen compressors however have an upper pressure limit of 25 bar, which is not suf-
ﬁcient for all of the carried out simulations.
The temperature loss from compressor cooling, caused by the oil being removed from
the compressor at the discharge, can have an impact on how the heat pump performs.
60
==PAGE:71==
A reasonable assumption is, that the oil has the same temperature as the discharge
temperature of the refrigerant. The ﬂow of the oil may vary, depending on how high a
temperature, the compressor is able to handle. Depending on how high the ﬂow is, the
oil temperature may decrease, which in turn will decrease the discharge temperature
of the refrigerant. The oil then removes too much energy from the system. The energy
in the oil can be used in a HEX to heat the supply air, so that the energy in the oil
is utilized and does not represent a pure loss.
How to best utilize the energy from the compressor is something that can be studied
further, to discover, how to utilize it. There can be several opportunities, such as ap-
plication in the heating of the supply air or for various other heat requiring processes
in the mill.
The survey on whether it is possible to ﬁnd a compressor that can cover the require-
ments for the heat pump is based on combining information from diﬀerent manufactur-
ers of compressors and lubrication oil. There are no guarantees that this compressor
characteristics are possible to combine in real life applications.
To obtain a more clear picture of the losses in a heat pump of this scale, a more
thorough analysis of the subject should be performed. Due to the scale of the heat
pump, it is likely that the heat losses are signiﬁcantly larger than the calculated 8 %.
When comparing the simulated results to the experimental technologies in section
3.5, the COPs are very similar. This could indicate, that there is no need to wait for
experimental technology to go through testing and become commercially available,
before investing in high temperature heat pumps.
When comparing the results in [20] with the simulated results, it can be seen that the
simulation COPs are slightly lower[20], operating at lower temperatures. The results
are however similar enough, that it can be considered as a slight validation of the
simulation results, assuming that the obtained results in [20] are correct.
The simulated results should be tested in an experimental setup, to verify whether
the results can be reproduced in real life and validated against the simulations.
The paper industry is constantly looking for energy improvements with short payback
time and low investments, in order to reduce costs and fulﬁll environmental require-
ments. A heat pump might therefore represent a preferable choice when considering
the return of investment rate. Whether there is a potential market for high temper-
ature heat pumps in the pulp and paper industry, seems very likely, but will depend
on several diﬀerent factors, that can be diﬃcult to account for. This all depends on
the alternatives to a heat pump and how market prices for resources vary etc.

Conclusion
An exploration of the processes in a pulp and paper mill, uncovered multiple oppor-
tunities for implementation of heat pumps where waste heat can be used as a heat
source for a heat pump. The waste heat can be upgraded with a heat pump, in order
to produce high quality steam or heat supply air. This is to reduce the need for fossil
fuels, such as natural gas. The best opportunity for heat recovery is in the paper
drying section, where the exhaust air from paper drying, can be used to heat supply
air to obsolete the use of natural gas heated steam.
Based on the simulation results, there are several refrigerants that can be used in high
temperature applications while providing good COPs.
The best results for the simulation models were achieved by using supercritical heat
pumps with HFO refrigerants.
The results show that a heat pump can deliver COP of 3-5, depending on whether the
heat sink temperature is 110 ◦C or 150 ◦C.
With investment costs of 20 million kr for the 110 ◦C heat pump, it is able to return
the investment within almost 1.5 years, whereas the 150 ◦C heat pump costs 40 million
kr and takes 2.8 years for the investment to be returned.
The primary limiting factor to consider, when designing a heat pump for high tem-
perature application, is the compressor. The compressor needs to be lubricated and
cooled in order to operate reliably, which means that it will be of the oil ﬂooded screw
type, so that it can run continuously, without requiring frequent maintenance. The
screw compressor can deliver the necessary ﬂow and pressure, for the heat pump to
deliver the desired heat sink.
Based on a price of 3,750 DKK
kW , the 110 ◦C heat pump provides a return of investment
within 1.5 years, with a price of 20 million DKK and an annual saving of 13.5 million
DKK. The 150 ◦C heat pump will take 2.8 years to return the investment, with a
cost of 40 million DKK and an annual saving of 14.3 million DKK. Based on that the
tendency in the paper industry is to choose the cheaper options with short return of
investment, the 110 ◦C heat pump will be the most feasible. There are however still
relevancy in the 150 ◦C heat pump, due to the high quality of energy that it is able
to deliver, while still maintaining an acceptable COP.
8.1
Area of application
Due to the scope of the research, the usable area of this study is focused on the paper
industry, but could easily be converted to suit other industries with similar waste heat
sources. The waste heat sources in this thesis consists mainly of hot exhaust air, which
can easily be applied for exhaust gasses and other similar gas ﬂows. When considering
e.g. exhaust gasses, carbon deposits and fouling will have to be taken into account to
maintain the eﬃciency of the heat pump. The hot air that is considered in the case
study contains signiﬁcant amounts of water, which gives it a very high energy density
compared to dry air. To assure that the heat pump concept will work in any given
situation, the heat source must be able to provide enough energy for the evaporator.
If this can be fulﬁlled, the heat pump can be applied in many diﬀerent scenarios across
many diﬀerent industries.
