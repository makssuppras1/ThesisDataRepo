Abstract
This master’s thesis explores the automation of hydrological time series data cleaning
by utilizing machine learning models. The focus is on unsupervised anomaly detection
in water level time series data with the deployment of Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)
neural networks. Four distinct anomaly types, including spikes and drops (point anoma-
lies), level-shifts (collective anomalies), and flat-lines (clustered contextual anomalies),
are identified. The study explores different single-station and multiple-station LSTM neu-
ral network architectures for detecting these anomaly types. A batch size of 32 and a
look-back window of 16 were chosen for both single-station and multiple-station models,
while 64 and 128 LSTM cells proved more effective for the respective approaches. The
findings showcase an exceptional water level fitting performance for high-quality water
level time series data, with Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) values on the order of 10−3
to 10−2 water level meters.
Both single-station and multiple-station approaches proved sufficient in detecting point
anomalies, achieving average F1-Score values above 0.91. However, the detection of
level-shifts and flat-lines posed more challenges for these setups. Further testing re-
vealed that the expanded multiple-station approach, leveraging water level data from more
neighboring measuring stations as input features, demonstrated particular effectiveness
in detecting level-shifts. This method achieved a 95.1% average Duration Accuracy (DA)
for artificially generated level-shifts, and after further calibration, reached an average DA
above 60% for artificially generated flat-lines.
In summary, there are still challenges, accompanied by required preliminary and post-
anomaly detection processing steps. Nonetheless, this study provides an unsupervised
method for detecting anomalies in hydrological time series data, opening the possibility
of future advancements in achieving complete data cleaning automation.

Introduction
Water is a fundamental element of life and a central focus of Sustainable Development
Goal (SDG) 6 within the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development [1]. The digitaliza-
tion of the water sector and the utilization of big environmental data play crucial roles in
efficiently managing this vital resource. In the field of hydrology, the availability of ex-
tensive time series datasets has transformed our understanding of water systems and
their behavior. This transformation has been driven by advances in sensor technology,
remote sensing, and computational capabilities, resulting in the generation of voluminous
hydrological data [2]. While this data manifests unprecedented research and practical
opportunities, it also poses significant challenges. Foremost among these is ensuring the
quality and reliability of these datasets. Hydrological time series data is inherently vul-
nerable to various sources of anomalies and missing values related to sensor errors, that
can compromise the accuracy and validity of analytical results [3].
The necessity for robust hydrological data cleaning methods is emphasized by the scarcity
of domain-specific structured techniques. Frequently, developed methods either general-
ize more broadly to environmental data [4] or rely on manual data labeling [5], presenting
efficiency challenges and consistency issues across different researchers [6]. In the case
of the few systematized unsupervised approaches related to hydrological data [7, 8], the
architecture of the developed models is to a certain degree obscure, making it challeng-
ing to ascertain the extent of neural network architecture comparison. Another significant
limitation involves determining the specific types of anomalies that have been identified
and clarifying which ones have been successfully detected or not.
To bridge this gap, the primary objective is the development and assessment of an au-
tomated algorithm designed to detect anomalies in hydrological time series data. This
includes a specific focus on addressing the issue of water level data quality through ef-
fective unsupervised anomaly detection. The developed method aims to enhance the
usefulness and reliability of big hydrological datasets, leveraging the growing capabilities
of LSTM models for forecasting and decision-making in hydrology [9, 10, 11, 12, 13, 14].
The specific research questions are:
• Which LSTM neural network architectures are suitable for detecting anomalies in
hydrological time series data?
• What type of anomalies can be identified in hydrological time series data?
• Are there differences in how well different types of anomalies can be detected?
• Can we detect anomalies in a completely automated and unsupervised manner,
without manual data labeling of the involved time series?
The master’s thesis comprises eight chapters, each serving a distinct purpose. Chapter
1 introduces the thesis’s aim. Chapter 2 describes the essential theoretical background
necessary for comprehending the subsequent chapters and provides a brief overview of
other relevant studies. Chapter 3 focuses on the case area under study, while Chapter
4 introduces the data employed throughout the thesis. Chapter 5 outlines the methods
used to yield the results expounded upon in Chapter 6. Chapter 7 covers the discussion
of these results and gives a preview of potential future research directions. Lastly, in
Chapter 8, the central findings of the thesis are summarized.

Theoretical Background
This section provides the theoretical background for the methods applied in my research.
Firstly, the concept of Artificial Neural Network (ANN) is introduced, and I describe how
neurons form hidden layers and explain the functionality of activation functions. Following
that, I briefly introduce the concept of Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) and then focus on
its subcategory, Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) neural network. Furthermore, I explain
how the training process works by choosing appropriate loss functions and optimization
method. Subsequently, I offer an overview of relevant studies where hydrological model-
ing has been conducted using LSTMs. Finally, I summarize this section by underscoring
the applications of LSTMs in anomaly detection within hydrological data that are most
relevant to my research, discussing the valuable insights gained and the limitations iden-
tified.
2.1
Artificial neural networks
The inception of an ANN can be traced back to the pioneering work of Warren S. Mc-
Culloch and Walter Pitts [15], who initially conceptualized them as mathematical models
simulating the information processing capabilities of neurons. Contemporary understand-
ing acknowledges notable differences between ANNs and their biological counterparts.
However, despite these distinctions, a fundamental structural resemblance remains ap-
parent [16, Chapter 15]. The key components of ANNs, including neurons, hidden layers,
and the complexities associated with activation functions, are detailed in the following
section.
2.1.1
Neurons and hidden layers
Various types of ANNs exist, with Feed-Forward Neural Network (FFNN) being the most
prevalent. An FFNN can consist of one or more hidden layers, and within each layer, there
are one or more neurons or nodes, defining the hidden size of each layer [17, Chapter
1]. Consequently, hidden layers determine the ’depth’ of a neural network, while neurons
determine the ’width’. Additionally, there are the input layer with one or more input features
and the output layer with one or more output features, as illustrated in Figure 2.1.
Every node in a neural network is a crucial element with the ability to execute mathematical
operations. These nodes connect through weights, and as mentioned forming a layer
when multiple nodes receive the same input. Each node processes input by applying
weights and a bias, followed by an activation function. This mechanism allows nodes
to interconnect, facilitating the flow of information through the network layers, based on
Equation 2.1,
o = a(Wx + b)

where o is the output, W are the weights connecting the previous layer with the current
layer, x is the input or the activation from the previous layer, b is the bias, and a is the

2.1.2
Activation functions
The activation function in a neural network is like a tool that changes the way information
is combined and processed. It takes the total of weighted inputs and transforms it into a
non-linear output. This transformation is crucial because it enables the network to grasp
and learn more intricate patterns in the data. Without this step, the network would only
handle linear relationships. So, picking the right activation function helps the network get
better at understanding and dealing with the complex connections between inputs and
outputs [18, Chapter 6].

2.2
Recurrent neural networks
RNNs emerged as a noteworthy category of ANNs specifically designed for managing
sequential data. Their significance has grown across diverse applications, particularly in
tasks that involve sequential inputs like natural language processing and speech recogni-
tion. What distinguishes RNNs from FFNNs is their capacity to process input sequences
in a sequential manner, handling one element at a time, all the while maintaining a dy-
namic ’state vector’ within their hidden units. This ’state vector’ adeptly encapsulates
information regarding the historical elements within the input sequence, allowing RNNs
to effectively capture temporal dependencies in data [22]. By conceptualizing the outputs
of hidden units at various time steps as analogous to separate neurons within a deep
multi-layer network, RNNs enhance the efficacy of training through the implementation of
backpropagation through time. In this approach, the error is systematically propagated
backward through both the hidden layers and a temporal dimension [23].
Figure 2.2: Illustration of an RNN with shared parameters (U, V , W) over time. Neurons,
grouped under node nt, receive inputs from previous time steps, allowing the RNN to map
input sequence xt to output sequence ot. The unfolded computational graph on the right
demonstrates the sequential processing (sketch inspired by LeCun et al. [22]).
2.2.1
LSTM neural networks
RNNs, however, have a significant limitation in their training process. Training these net-
works has proven challenging due to the complexity of handling backpropagated gradi-
ents, which tend to either amplify or diminish at each time step. Consequently, over an
extended sequence of timesteps, these gradients commonly encounter problems of either
exploding or vanishing, limiting their ability to capture long-term dependencies in the data
[24].
LSTM neural networks, a type of RNN developed by Hochreiter and Schmidhuber [25], ef-
fectively handle the problem of exploding or vanishing gradients. The unique structure of
LSTMs, as depicted in Figure 2.3, allows them to proficiently manage and preserve infor-
mation over extended sequences, representing a significant improvement in addressing
gradient-related challenges during training [26].
LSTM cells are specifically designed to capture and learn long-term dependencies in data
by employing gating mechanisms that facilitate the storage of information over multiple
timesteps. Although the overall structure of an LSTM cell resembles that of a traditional
RNN, each recurrent neuron in the RNN can be substituted with an LSTM cell, wherein

2.5
Anomaly detection in hydrological time series data
Anomaly detection involves identifying unforeseen or unexpected data samples in the
time series datasets. This can be automated through two primary methods. Firstly, by
employing supervised machine learning models that require labeled datasets for calibra-
tion and utilize known instances of anomalies. Conversely, with unsupervised machine
learning models which operate without reliance on labels, proving valuable when labeled
data is limited or unavailable [4].
According to Russo et al. [4], anomalies in environmental data can be classified based
on their context. A ’point’ anomaly refers to a singular measurement deemed anomalous,
regardless of when or where it occurred. In contrast, a ’contextual’ anomaly designates
8
Harnessing Machine Learning Algorithms for Automated Data Cleaning of Hydrological Time Series
==PAGE:19==
a single data point as anomalous, taking into consideration the time or location of the
measurement. For example, a data point might fall within the acceptable range of sensor
measurements but could still be considered unusual for a specific location, time of day, or
time of year. Finally, a ’collective’ anomaly is characterized by a set of measurements that,
while appearing normal on an isolated basis, reveal anomalous patterns when analyzed
in the context of the entire time series.

2.5.1
LSTM applications for anomaly detection
In the past, researchers across diverse fields have frequently turned to data-driven meth-
ods to tackle challenges related to anomaly detection and the imputation of missing data
within time series datasets. In the specific context of hydrology, Zhao et al. [7] have effec-
tively used data-driven algorithms to address these dual objectives. Their methodology
involved a fusion of two LSTM forecasting models and a statistical control model to re-
construct anomalies and fill in missing data points. This process entailed the generation
of new values, each associated with distinct confidence intervals as computed by their re-
spective models and it proved successful when dealing with continuous hydrological data.
Furthermore, their research explored spatial correlations between neighboring monitoring
stations, revealing its potential to enhance the quality of hydrological data with disparities
in both time and space.
Another noteworthy research effort, carried out by Kulanuwa et al. [8], involved explor-
ing anomaly detection in hydrological time series and experimenting with data imputation.
They employed a weighted combination of forward and backward LSTM models, specif-
ically designed to capture patterns and dependencies in sequential data. An interesting
step in their methodology included artificially generating 10% of anomalies in the last 50%
of the available data for each time series. However, the anomaly detection was explic-
itly conducted based on two statistical methods, primarily relying on a ’sliding window’
technique where the distance of the median value in this window marked a data point as
anomalous or not.
In the study conducted by Maleki et al. [29], the authors focused on the unsupervised
labeling and treatment of anomalies more generally in time series data. Their approach
involved training a deep LSTM Autoencoder and using the reconstruction error - the dif-
ference between the input and the output of the Autoencoder - as a metric to identify
anomalies. In the architecture of LSTM Autoencoders, an encoder built on LSTM princi-
ples compresses the input sequence into a latent vector representation of lower dimen-
sion. Following this, a decoder, also implemented as an LSTM network, employs the
Harnessing Machine Learning Algorithms for Automated Data Cleaning of Hydrological Time Series
9
==PAGE:20==
latent representation to reconstruct the original input sequence [29]. Still, it is important
to note that their anomaly detection method made an assumption about the distribution of
data, specifically assuming normality, which does not hold true for hydrological time se-
ries. Hydrological time series data exhibit non-normal characteristics, including significant
skewness [30], which cannot be overlooked when applying anomaly detection techniques.
Adopting a similar methodology, Zhuang et al. [5] performed anomaly detection in flow
data from the Næstved sewer system using LSTM models. More specifically they exper-
imented with both single-sensor and multiple-sensor LSTM model architectures. Despite
not constructing an LSTM Autoencoder, they applied the reconstruction error logic in a
relative residual version. This process involved dividing the absolute value of the resid-
ual - the difference between observation and model prediction - by the predicted value to
obtain the relative residual. Nonetheless, they utilized quantiles of this metric to identify
anomalous data. Additionally, they conducted experiments involving these quantiles and
fine-tuned the anomaly detection threshold for different sensors. Similarly to a previous
study, they as well artificially generated anomalies to evaluate the detection performance
of their method. It should be mentioned, though, that a limitation in their approach regard-
ing scalability and efficiency in the developed anomaly detection method derives from the
preliminary conduct of data labeling.

To ensure consistent training and prevent issues stemming from input values varying sig-

as features with larger values can lead to more substantial gradients during the train-
ing process, potentially causing training instability or non-convergence. Min-max scaling
standardizes each feature to fall within a predefined range, in this case, [-1, 1], as com-
puted using Equation 4.1,
Xscaled =
X −min(X)

where Xscaled is the scaled version of X, and minr and maxr are the lower and upper
boundaries of the scaling range, i.e. -1 and 1, respectively. This scaling procedure is
applied to all input time series data, ensuring consistency between the training and test
datasets. It relies solely on the minimum and maximum values obtained from the training
data, thereby maintaining the model’s independence from information in the test data.

Data split
Effective data splitting is a fundamental practice in machine learning, involving the division
of a dataset into three key components: the training set, validation set, and test set.
The training set serves as the basis for model learning, exposing it to diverse inputs and
enabling the extraction of hidden patterns. Simultaneously, the validation set plays a
crucial role in fine-tuning the model, providing an independent measure of its performance
and guarding against overfitting. Finally, the test set serves as the ultimate benchmark,
offering an unbiased evaluation of the model’s ability to generalize to new, unseen data.
The data split procedure ensures that the model is not only well-trained but also capable
of making accurate predictions in real-world scenarios, contributing to its robustness and
reliability [33].

More specifically, the tuning of the anomaly detection threshold is semi-automated. Vari-
ous quantile values of the residual error during the model fitting in the training data of the
reference station are explored, and these values are used iteratively as anomaly detec-
tion thresholds to label the test data of the same station. The objective is to minimize the
generation of false labels, operating under the assumption that there are no anomalies
in the test data of this station. However, to avoid ’overfitting’ during threshold tuning, a
quantile value is accepted as a threshold, even if it almost results in no false labels. This
approach is taken to prevent becoming overly station-specific in the choice of threshold,
with the ultimate goal of using this quantile percentage as the initial choice for the anomaly
detection threshold ’hyperparameter’ in any future station. It is important to note that the
actual threshold value will vary in each future time series since it is not a fixed number, but
a statistic derived from the fitting distribution. This implies that full automation regarding
threshold tuning can be achieved for time series with similar characteristics.
In summary, the logic behind anomaly detection with LSTM model configurations lies in
their training process. These models are fine-tuned to effectively capture patterns in high-
quality data while exhibiting diminished performance in the presence of anomalies. By
leveraging the distribution of residual error generated after the model fit phase, a distinct
threshold can be strategically chosen based on the distribution’s quantile values. This
threshold serves as a decisive marker, enabling the identification of unlabeled data as
either anomalies or normal data, as illustrated in Figure 5.8.

Reconstruction assessment
Following the detection of potential anomalies, consideration arises regarding the recon-
struction of various anomaly types. Here, I explore whether certain anomaly types can be
directly reconstructed. However, I only briefly touch on this aspect and do not extensively
conduct additional research in this regard, as it is not the current study’s aim.
It can be stated that point anomalies, such as the spikes and drops defined in my re-
search, can be directly reconstructed. This involves removing the detected anomalies
and replacing the resulting one-timestep gap either through interpolation methods or by
imputing the missing value using the LSTM model’s one-step prediction. However, as
mentioned in Section 6.4 and illustrated in Figure D.20 the way point anomalies are de-
tected leads to the creation of these ’shadows’ of point FP in each successfully detected
anomalous event.
To address this side effect and accurately reconstruct only the anomalous timestep for
each point anomaly, I propose a method for encoding all labeled anomalies after the ini-
tial application of the anomaly detection method in the examined time series. Specifically,
I assign the first point of each detected event a label of 1, while labeling the subsequent
consecutive points as 0. I then selectively remove only the points labeled as 1 and recon-
struct them using a simple interpolation technique. This approach effectively addresses
single-point anomalies without the risk of replacing FP, and thus preserving good quality
observations. Subsequently, to address the possibility of consecutive point anomalies
or other types of anomalies, a second ’loop’ of the anomaly detection tool can be imple-
mented, even with a lower anomaly detection threshold quantile choice. This allows the
remaining anomalies to be re-detected and penalized even more, especially challenging
ones like flat-lines, while simultaneously avoiding the excessive generation of FP due to
point anomalies, as most of them will have already been reconstructed.
Figure 6.13 illustrates an example of reconstructing two events of point anomalies as
described above. In this process, only the first point of each anomalous event is removed,
which is the TP point and replaced with a new value, while the ’shadow’ of the point FP
remains untouched, retaining the observed water level values.

Discussion
In this chapter, I elaborate on different components of the developed anomaly detection
method. To begin, I provide a summary of the developed method with the key takeaways
regarding the results. Following that, I discuss the method’s limitations, concluding with
an outlook on potential future research orientations.
Summary
The feasibility of training LSTM neural networks for unsupervised anomaly detection in
hydrological time series data is underscored by the conducted analysis. The initial step
involves training a neural network on high-quality water level data, fitting it properly. Sub-
sequently, based on the residual error distribution between the model’s one-step pre-
dictions and actual observations, an anomaly detection threshold is established. This
threshold is crucial for labeling future data as normal or potential anomalies. To assess
the method’s efficacy, different anomaly types are identified in preprocessed water level
time series data. Artificial anomalies, varying in types, lengths and magnitudes, are then
generated on unseen data. The anomaly detection process is conducted based on the
predefined threshold and the residual error of the model fit on these data.
With regard to the water level modeling phase of the developed method:
• The multiple-station LSTM Model No.8 achieved the best fitting performance across
all examined model architectures with an RMSE score equal to 0.00329 meters for
the test data of the reference Station ID: 22000057.
• The single-station LSTM Model No.5, which achieved the best performance for this
approach, was tested on the data of all available time series. The achieved RMSE
score was below 0.012 meters for all stations except Station ID: 22000059, where
there is a level-shift in the test data.
With regard to the anomaly detection phase of the developed method:
• The anomaly detection threshold in all conducted experiments is determined by the
quantile value of 99.80% from the distribution of the residual error in the training
data. This value is derived from the initial calibration on the high-quality data of the
reference Station ID: 22000057.
• The average F1-Score for both the single-station and multiple-station approaches
exceeded 0.91 in detecting artificial point anomalies, while achieving an average DA
of around 80% for the artificially generated level-shifts. Despite their partial success
in identifying flat-lines equal to zero, both approaches struggled to detect flat-lines
close to the time series intercept, revealing a lack of robustness for this anomaly
type.
• The expanded multiple-station LSTM model leveraging water level data from addi-
tional measuring stations outperformed the previous models, achieving the highest
DA in detecting level-shifts across all experiments with an average DA of 95.3%.
However, for level-shift magnitudes closer to the range of time series values, the
performance decreased. Nonetheless, this model configuration exhibited signifi-
cant improvement in detecting flat-lines as well and with stricter anomaly detection
threshold calibration, the DA for flat-lines increased to values above 60%.

Anomaly detection method limitations
At this point, it is crucial to highlight the limitations of the anomaly detection method. The
applied method is not flawless, and there are aspects that still require further research
and improvements to enhance the method’s scalability and applicability.
An important limitation of the applied method is its dependency on high-quality data avail-
ability during training. The algorithm relies on high-quality data segments to properly
tune the model fit and may struggle to operate effectively if significant anomalous data
segments dominate the training set. Additionally, the choice of the quantile value deter-
mining the anomaly detection threshold is based on the distribution of the residual error
during the training phase. As a result, it is crucial for the model to accurately fit the data
to distinguish future anomalies.
Another significant limitation is the tuning of the anomaly detection threshold. This process
can be challenging, and even slight changes can have a substantial impact on detection
performance. Despite efforts to enhance scalability and recommend a quantile metric
to avoid value and station-specific choices, the proposed quantile is not necessarily an
optimal ’hyperparameter’ choice for all future cases. The increase of noise in training data
might necessitate lower quantile values to effectively distinguish between anomalies and
good data quality.
It should also be noted that data preprocessing is crucial for the applied method, pri-
marily due to data gaps that can span extensive time periods and cannot be adequately
addressed by simple interpolation techniques. However, this implies that the method can-
not be directly applied to the raw data extracted from the sensors. From this perspective,
the approach still has a considerable distance to cover before achieving full automation
of the entire data cleaning process.
Within this preprocessing constraint, there is also the generation of flat-lines equal to the
previously observed value in each interpolated timestep or small cluster of timesteps. As
demonstrated by the poor results of Experiment No.11, this anomaly type is the most
challenging to detect, leading to a decrease in the method’s anomaly detection perfor-
mance and the generation of additional noise in our data. Consequently, employing a
machine learning or other imputation approach could offer a more effective means of im-
puting missing timesteps compared to interpolation, before progressing to the anomaly
detection phase.
Finally, in the conducted research, despite the unsupervised nature of the anomaly de-
tection, the method’s artificial generation of anomalies and the subsequent evaluation
are based on the identified anomaly types in the available water level time series data.
However, this approach introduces the possibility of overlooking hidden anomaly types
that might be challenging to discern through visual inspection. Additionally, future data
extraction may reveal new anomaly types, such as slower or more gradual time series
curve drifts due to sensor deterioration, necessitating further research on their detection
efficacy.
Expanding on this limitation, it is crucial to recognize that water level data inherently dis-
play certain time series characteristics. It is essential to acknowledge that these char-
acteristics may not necessarily represent all hydrological time series data. Specifically,
alterations in water level time series data manifest differently compared to, for example,
precipitation time series data. Thus, diverse time series characteristics have the potential
to significantly impact the performance of the anomaly detection method.

Research outlook
Here, I present my ideas for future research. Figure 7.1 illustrates the complete con-
ceptual schema for automating hydrological time series data cleaning. Three potential
research pathways come into view, highlighted in blue.
The first is relevant to the the preliminary phase where data are being preprocessed, and
consideration of how this could be done automatically is crucial to ensure readiness for
the application of the conceptualized data cleaning algorithm. Automating this process
would enable the direct application of the developed method to raw hydrological time
series data, extracted either through available data lakes, data warehouses, or directly
via APIs, without necessitating manual data preparation by the user.
The second is based on the identified model limitations, where further research can ad-
dress anomaly types that were not fully detected. This may involve configuring a new
model, such as an LSTM Autoencoder, where anomaly labeling is based on the recon-
struction error rather than the residual error, as described in Section 2.5.1. Additionally,
improving the model architecture to discern whether a previous data point is anomalous
or not could enhance the detection of more challenging anomalies (Figure D.22). Alter-
natively, researchers could explore an entirely different category of deep learning model,
such as a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN), treating a univariate time series as a one-
dimensional image where the only dimension is temporal, and framing anomaly detection
as marking a desired object [37].
The third research pathway is relevant to the reconstruction assessment of more challeng-
ing anomaly types than point anomalies. This could be feasible with a machine learn-
ing model specifically developed to reconstruct the identified anomalies, at least those
deemed valuable for reconstruction. In the end, by integrating various developed models
into a combined tool, an automated data cleaning algorithm can be formulated.

Conclusion
Four types of anomalies have been identified in the available water level time series data:
spikes and drops, categorized as point anomalies; level-shifts, categorized as collective
anomalies; and flat-lines, categorized as clustered contextual anomalies.
Various single-station and multiple-station LSTM architectures were tested for detecting
these anomaly types. In both cases, a batch size of 32 and a look-back window of 16 were
preferred. However, 64 and 128 LSTM cells proved more effective for the single-station
and multiple-station cases, respectively. Overall, the expanded multiple-station approach
is recommended, leveraging water level data from additional stations as input features.
Notably, precipitation data did not enhance the predictive power of the developed models.
The selected models though, utilizing only water level data, exhibited exceptional fitting
with an average prediction accuracy of approximately 0.003 meters in cases involving
high-quality water level time series data.
In general, both single-station and multiple-station approaches proved effective and suffi-
cient in detecting point anomalies. However, the identification of level-shifts and flat-lines
posed more challenges. A major constraint observed in both approaches is the difficulty
in detecting flat-lines near the intercept of the time series. This limitation also extends to
detecting level-shifts of small magnitude in close proximity to the value range of the wa-
ter level time series data. Nevertheless, the expanded multiple-station LSTM approach,
utilizing more water level stations to feed the anomaly detection algorithm, demonstrated
robust results in detecting level-shifts and promising results in detecting flat-lines.
In conclusion, the objective of detecting anomalies in hydrological time series data in an
unsupervised manner, without preliminary data labeling, has proven successful. How-
ever, there are still challenges, along with required preliminary and post-anomaly detec-
tion processing steps to achieve complete automation. Thus, it cannot be stated that I
have fully harnessed a machine learning algorithm to automate the entire data cleaning
process of hydrological time series. Nonetheless, I have succeeded in developing an
anomaly detection method that can enable the possibility of achieving this.
