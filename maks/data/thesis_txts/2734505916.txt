Abstract
In this thesis the generalized nonlinear Schrödinger equation is studied.
At first numerical methods are derived for the pure NLS equation and
compared with an analytical solution. The numerical methods used in
this thesis are the split step Fourier method and a semi-discretization.
Later the numerical methods are extended for various perturbations
of interest.
Finally the numerical methods are compared with each
other, but also to approximate analytical solutions that arise from the
collective coordinate approach.
Some of the perturbations lead to a constant velocity different from that
of the pure NLS equation, others cause an acceleration of the soliton
solution. The effect of two other perturbations is a modulation of the
amplitude, either by damping or by a laser gain, while the modulation
of the amplitude for one perturbation act as a random walk.
The
approximate analytical solution captures the overall behavior of the
soliton solution but not radiation.

Introduction
Let us consider a short laser pulse propagating through an optical fiber. Under normal conditions the pulse
will gradually get wider and wider. This is what is known as dispersion. The dispersion restricts how fast two
pulses can be transmitted after one another, and thereby also the transmission speed. Therefore the amount of
information transmitted through the optical fiber is reduced because of dispersion. This phenomenon happens
as a short pulse contains multiple frequencies with a different speed of propagation, since the refractive index
in the fiber varies with the frequency. By introducing nonlinear effects the effect of dispersion is countered,
causing the pulse to preserve its shape. Such a shape preserving pulse is also known as a solitary wave or a
soliton1. This can be modelled by the generalized nonlinear Schrödinger equation, in short the generalized NLS
equation.
In this thesis we will have a closer look at the generalized NLS equation. This includes a derivation and a
non-dimensionalization of the NLS equation followed by an analytical solution and a numerical solution found
using the split step Fourier method. Of special interest are perturbations of the NLS equation and how they
affect the soliton solution compared to the pure NLS equation. In this context we will investigate whether a
perturbation changes the velocity or the amplitude of the solution. For this purpose we will extend the split
step Fourier method to the given perturbations and introduce a semi-discrete method for comparison. Another
question of interest is whether we can solve the perturbed NLS equations analytically. To do so we will assume
that the parameters of the analytical solution (to name one parameter we have the amplitude of the soliton)
to the pure NLS equation vary through the optical fiber. In other words we assume that an analytical solution
is on the same form as that of the pure NLS equation. By the use of two approaches - energy calculations and
the collective coordinate approach - we will try to come up with an expression for each parameter as function
of the spatial parameter. For the energy calculations we will find the total Hamiltonian of each perturbed NLS
equation, from which we will find the change of energy of the soliton. The collective coordinate approach2 gives
us an expression for each parameter through manipulation of the Lagrangian. Doing so will give us a solution
that we can compare to the numerical solutions.

Conclusion
We have been looking at two different numerical methods to solve the generalized NLS equation, namely, the
split step Fourier method and the semi-discrete method, where the NLS equation is split into a real part and
an imaginary part. Overall the split step Fourier method is faster. However for nonlinear perturbations, that
causes a constant velocity different from that of the initial condition, we see that the semi-discretization is
faster. Of special interest was to come up with an approximate analytical solution that models the solution
to the given perturbed NLS equation well. To do so we considered the analytical solution to the pure NLS
equation, which has 4 parameters. The goal of the approximate analytical solution was to describe how each
parameter varies with z. Our first attempt was through energy calculations. Here the problem is that we only
get one equation, whereas we can only model one of the four parameters. This was improved by the collective
coordinate approach, as this method yields an expression for each parameter. The expressions acquired from
this method also satisfy the energy equation that arose from the energy calculations.
Now let us talk about the different perturbations and how they affected the soliton solution. For the pertur-
bations with β1, β3 and µ we see a constant velocity. For the perturbation with β2 we see that the soliton
accelerates. For these perturbations the numerical methods get the same solutions for small step sizes. The
approximate analytical solution captures the general shape of the solution, but not the radiation. The per-
turbations with Γ and G(p) cause a modulation of the amplitude. Here the approximate analytical solution
visually looks similar to the numerical solutions, but comparing the amplitude shows that it is slightly off. The
approximate analytical solution does not capture the oscillations caused by radiation and for Γ we have a small
error due to periodic boundary conditions. For the perturbation with the Raman term we see an acceleration
of the soliton similar to that of β2. For the Raman term the split step Fourier method is much more efficient
than the semi-discrete method. For the perturbation with β4 we see a modulation of the amplitude, which act
as a random walk. The approximate analytical solution clearly does not give an exact estimate of the solution.
It does give an idea of the shape of the solution, but is does not describe the modulation of the amplitude at
all.
With this exception the expressions for the collective coordinates describe the overall solution for each pertur-
bation well. The downside of the approximate analytical solution is, that it does not capture the radiation of
the solutions. The problem is that we assume a sech-shaped pulse. For a better approximate analytical solution
a term should be included to capture the radiation. In general the approximate analytical solution is a good
tool to quickly describe the overall behavior of the solution. From this we can then choose Z and T properly
for the numerical method of choice.
