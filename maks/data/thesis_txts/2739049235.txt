Abstract 
Over the past decades, new methods such as bulk RNAseq have made it possible to study gene 
expression and the role of alterna?ve splicing in a high-throughput seAng. Around 95% of all mul?-
exon genes are es?mated to undergo alterna?ve splicing, and alterna?ve splicing has been found to

Despite the impressive technological achievements within scRNAseq, many limita?ons s?ll exist. In

isoform expression counts. However, such methods are much more costly than those covering only

1. Introduction 
RNA splicing is one of the key steps in processing pre-mRNAs into mature mRNAs by splicing out mainly 
introns. The mechanisms of RNA splicing were already characterized in the late 70s [1]. Splicing 
involves mul?ple biochemical processes catalyzed by the spliceosome [2], where introns are removed 
with the cleavage of pre-mRNA splice sites found at the 5’ and 3’ ends of introns. 
Most cells of a human body (or any mul?-cellular organism) carry a copy of the same genome. Thus, 
what dis?nguishes a cell from other cells is not the genome. Instead, the phenotype of cells can be 
characterized by the genes they express, measured as the transcrip?on of genes into RNA molecules, 
specifying the iden?ty of a cell. The sum of these RNA molecules is deﬁned as the transcriptome. 
The data used in this study is based on detec?ng gene- and isoform mRNA expression levels through 
primarily single-cell RNAseq (scRNAseq) techniques.  
 
1.1. Increased Protein Diversity Through Alternative Splicing 
With the comple?on of the Human Genome Project in 2003, the human genome was es?mated to 
encode ~22,000 protein-coding genes, which are translated into ~100,000 diﬀerent proteins [3]. The 
large discrepancy between the number of protein-coding genes and observed proteins violated the 
previous ‘one gene, one-protein’ paradigm. One biological process known to play an important role in 
increasing protein diversity is the restructuring of exon paWerns during the splicing process of mRNA 
molecules, generally referred to as alterna?ve splicing. This exon restructuring allows one gene to be 
translated into diﬀerent amino acid sequences called protein isoforms with poten?ally diﬀerent 
func?ons. The mechanisms of alterna?ve splicing can be broken down into ﬁve major types: 1) Exon

in diﬀerent mRNA isoforms [4]. 
 
The important role of alterna?ve splicing in gene regula?on and disease-mechinasms is steadily 
becoming more accepted. Around 95% of all mul?-exon genes are es?mated to undergo alterna?ve 
splicing [4], and at least 15% of disease-causing point muta?ons are es?mated to result in alterna?ve

3.2. Predicting Isoform Expression 
This study's main aim was to determine whether isoform expression counts for scRNAseq data can be 
predicted when gene expression counts are given as input. The following sec?ons will try to answer 
this ques?on through a prac?cal approach, where a predic?ve model is ﬁrst trained and then 
evaluated. The goal of this study was not to end up with a ﬁnal model to be used in future predic?ons 
of isoform counts. In fact, this study limits itself to train and evaluate a rela?vely simple predic?ve 
model while gathering valuable insights for guiding future aWempts at predic?ng isoform expression 
counts. 
 
3.2.1. Distribution of Target Values 
To gain a beWer understanding of the values that the model will have to predict, all target values 
(isoform count ra?os) were ploWed in a histogram, as seen in Fig. 10. ScRNAseq data is known to be 
especially sparse, which is also seen here with ~ 83 % of all target values being zero, while ~ 6 % of all 
target values were one. The high occurrence of zeros compared to ones can be explained with a simple 
example. One such scenario could be a gene with, e.g., ﬁve known isoforms where only one of these 
isoforms is expressed. This gene would contribute with four target values at zero and just one target 
value at one. Local peaks in the histogram can be observed around target values at 0.25, 0.5, and 0.75, 
which makes sense since these are common frac?onal values, describing scenarios where an isoform 
accounts for a quarter, half, or three-quarters of the total expression.  
Simplifying the predic?on of target ra?os to a binary classiﬁca?on problem could make 
performance interpreta?on easier and reduce computa?onal resources. However, since around 12% 
of all the target ra?os fall between 0 and 1, this would indisputably limit the model's biological 
relevance and the performance of poten?al downstream tasks.

4. Discussion 
To the knowledge of the author, no other publicly available descrip?ons of aWempts to predict isoform 
expressions for scRNAseq experiments (or for any other types of RNAseq experiments) exist. 
Therefore, the results presented in this study are postulated to be the ﬁrst publicly available results for 
predic?ng isoform expressions working with RNAseq data. 
As this study focuses on u?lizing gene expression counts to predict isoform expressions, the success 
depends on the assump?on that gene-level expression counts carry meaningful informa?on for 
predic?ng isoform expressions. As the central dogma states, informa?on is transferred from DNA to

level expression counts could be to use DNA sequences as input for the predic?ve model. Interes?ngly, 
Scwessinger et al. [44] describe the value and challenges of their aWempt to predict single-cell gene 
expressions from DNA sequences. Given the more sta?c nature of DNA compared to RNA, the closer 
biological rela?onship between gene- and isoform expression counts, and the availability of suitable 
data, this study chose to use gene expression counts obtained from scRNAseq to predict isoform 
expressions. 
 
Among the many public repositories containing RNAseq data, ARCHS4 [45] was recognized to be the 
best choice, mainly due to the incorpora?on of isoform- and gene-level quan?ﬁca?ons for all samples, 
but also due to the uniform preprocessing ﬂow, resul?ng in ready to use expression count matrices. 
Because this project did not focus on op?mizing the processing of scRNAseq data, further processing

For feature representa?on of the gene expression counts, PCs were obtained using all samples for

Limi?ng the model to only consider protein-coding genes might have a dual impact on performance. 
ARCHS4 provides gene expression counts for ~67k diﬀerent Ensembl gene IDs [46], and out of these, 
only ~23k are labeled as protein-coding. Excluding all non-protein coding genes thus limits the amount 
of data available to the model, which may lower the upper bound for what any predic?ve model can 
learn. Some non-protein-coding genes, such as long non-coding RNAs, are known to regulate gene 
expression, and much has yet to be learned about the non-coding parts of the human genome [47]. 
On the other hand, including all non-protein-coding genes would increase the size of the predic?ve
