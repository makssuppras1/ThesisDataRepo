Abstract
This work investigates the technical and economic feasibility of a laboratory-developed pro-
cess for green methanol production via one-step electrolysis, using a phosphate-based proton-
conducting electrolyte.
In this system, water is oxidized at the anode, and the resulting
protons pass through the membrane to the cathode, where they form hydrogen and sub-
sequently react with CO2 over a copper-based catalyst to synthesize methanol. The system
operates at 28 bar and 240 °C, conditions selected to support both electrolyzer’s and methanol
synthesis requirements.
A detailed techno-economic analysis was conducted for a 1.06 MW electrolyzer system. The
technical evaluation, performed through simulations using Aspen Plus and validated by man-
ual calculations, defined the upstream and downstream components surrounding the elec-
trolyzer and identified optimal operating conditions. A CO2 recirculation loop was introduced
to overcome methanol conversion limitations, increasing methanol output to 116.6 kg/h. The
electrolyzer’s energy demand was 9.09 kWh per kilogram of methanol, with additional heating
and cooling requirements highlighting the potential for internal heat recovery. Heat integra-
tion strategies demonstrated that up to 99% of the system’s thermal energy requirements
could be recovered internally by utilizing both the cathode outlet stream and waste heat from
the one-step electrolyzer. A cooling strategy involving internal channels was also proposed to
manage one-step electrolyzer’s cooling.
The economic analysis included calculation of the Total Capital Investment (TCI), Operating
Expenses (OPEX), and Levelized Cost of Methanol (LCOM). A bottom-up cost analysis
in stacks cost identified the bipolar plate fabrication and catalyst materials as the primary
capital cost drivers. In addition to the stack, costs for power electronics and Balance of Plant
(BoP) components—such as heat exchangers, recycling compressors, and installation—were
included in the TCI and significantly contributed to the total capital cost of the system.
Strategies to reduce capital costs were explored, revealing that scaling the system to 35 MW
could reduce equipment costs by up to 70%. Further savings could be achieved through bulk
procurement of bipolar plates and reducing membrane thickness. The LCOM was estimated at
1136 USD/ton under baseline assumptions, with potential to reduce to 900 USD/ton through
improved stack design and system scale-up. Sensitivity analysis identified electricity price,
stack power consumption, and CO2 feedstock cost as the most influential economic variables.
To validate the techno-economic findings, specifically the impact of membrane improvements
on cost reduction, a series of laboratory experiments were conducted. These experiments
tested membrane stability and configuration under the system’s operating conditions and
introduced a new membrane preparation method. The new method excluded the PVB binder
and incorporated a PBI felt support, resulted in thinner membranes with improved mechanical
robustness. Chronopotentiometry and Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS) tests
demonstrated voltage stability and ionic conductivity comparable to the previous design,
confirming the feasibility of the proposed membrane configuration.

Introduction
International shipping is at a pivotal moment for decarbonization, with the International Mar-
itime Organization (IMO) having set the ambition to achieve net-zero emissions by or around
2050, alongside intermediate goals for adopting zero or near-zero greenhouse gas (GHG) fuels
by 2030. [1] Policies such as the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS) and FuelEU Maritime
can double the cost of conventional fuels by 2030 and increase the cost of Low Sulfur Fuel
Oil (LSFO) more than fivefold by 2050. At the same time, the FuelEU pooling mechanism
can provide significant time-limited benefits for alternative fuels from 2025 through 2044, in-
centivizing near-term investment. [2] Bio- and e-methanol remain among the most promising
alternative shipping fuels for scaling up within this decade due to their emission-reduction po-
tential, compatibility with existing infrastructure, and ease of handling and storage. However,
a significant cost gap exists between conventional fossil fuels and low-GHG alternatives. More-
over, challenges such as the complexity of biomass gasification technology, the cost of green
hydrogen production, and high project acquisition costs further complicate the transition. [3]
Producing e-methanol directly from carbon dioxide and water in a single-step operation rep-
resents an alternative pathway with fewer barriers compared to traditional methods. This
approach could reduce energy consumption, simplify equipment and therefore costs, provid-
ing a viable solution for accelerating the adoption of green methanol in the shipping industry.
In this work, a techno-economic analysis was conducted to investigate the technical and eco-
nomic feasibility of one-step green methanol production, as well as potential cost-reduction
pathways. The simulation and theoretical calculations were supported by laboratory exper-
iments to practically demonstrate the potential for improving one-step methanol production
technology.

Description of the system
3.1
Process overview
It has been shown that e-methanol can be produced in a closed electrolyzer system by feeding
water to the anode and carbon dioxide to the cathode. [4] In this setup, water undergoes
electrolysis and is split into hydrogen and oxygen. The hydrogen formed at the cathode can
then react with the carbon dioxide, also introduced at the cathode, as illustrated in Figure
1. This reaction, known as CO2 hydrogenation, occurs over a traditional methanol synthesis
catalyst.
The electrolysis system is a solid-state system and resembles a Proton Exchange Membrane
(PEM) electrolyzer, as it utilizes a proton-conducting membrane.
However, it operates
based on the phosphate electrolysis process, which enables super-protonic conductivity at
elevated temperatures. Conceptually, the system integrates two key processes—electrolysis
and methanol synthesis—into a single unit. Both of these processes will be described in detail
in the following chapters.
Figure 1: High-level overview of the one-step green methanol production of the electrolyzer
system.
3.2
Electrolysis
A typical proton exchange membrane (PEM) electrolyzer system uses electricity to split wa-
ter (H2O) into hydrogen (H2) and oxygen (O2) using a solid polymer electrolyte membrane.
It operates under near-ambient temperatures (typically 50–80°C) and is powered by direct
current (DC) electricity. [5] The system or cell is composed of two electrodes in which the
electrochemical reactions take place. The electrodes are separated by the membrane, which
keeps the two compartments isolated and enables the transfer of protons (H+). At the anode,
water is oxidized to produce oxygen gas, protons, and electrons. The protons travel through
the membrane and are reduced at the cathode by the electrons that have traveled through an
external circuit, resulting in the production of hydrogen gas. The reactions are sustained by
an externally applied current, which is driven by a voltage supply that exceeds the thermody-
namic cell voltage. [6] The operating principle of the cell is illustrated in Figure 2. The anode
and cathode traditionally include Ir-based oxides and Pt-based catalysts respectively, while
the electrode substrates are titanium mesh or carbon paper. The proton exchange membrane
is a perfluorosulfonic acid polymer (Nafion-based) which conducts protons and is impermeable
to gases. [7]

Compared to PEM electrolyzers, the difference of the examined system lies in the choice of
materials, which enable it to operate at elevated pressure and temperature, specifically the
choice of electrolyte, electrodes, as well as the addition of the copper-based catalyst. The
electrolyte is a solid acid membrane made of cesium phosphate (CsH2PO4, CDP). [8] This
membrane is solid under ambient conditions, but transitions into a super-proton-conducting
phase above 232 °C, during which its crystal structure changes from monoclinic to cubic.
This phase transition results in increased proton conductivity. [8] In addition, the Faradaic
efficiency—which denotes the fraction of current used for hydrogen production—was nearly
100%, meaning there was no loss of current to side reactions. However, the high conductivity of
CDP in its super-protonic phase depends critically on maintaining a high water vapor pressure
(typically 3-8 bar). [8, 9] Without sufficient humidity, the electrolyte begins to dehydrate,
leading to a loss in proton conductivity and overall performance. On the other hand, Nafion
has typically only been used at temperatures up to just above 100 °C, as it tends to dehydrate
when heated due to water evaporation. [10]

In case the feed consists only of CO2 and H2, which is the case for the one-step electrolyzer
system, the predominant reactions are the CO2 to MeOH and the reverse water gas shift
reaction (RWGS) to CO and H2O. [17, 18] However, it has been shown that CO is only
present at very low current densities, in the scale of mA, therefore the most possible pathway
is assumed to be reaction 1, direct conversion of CO2 to MeOH and the RWGS reaction
is not taken into consideration. [4] Based on that, it is evident that this reaction produces
a significant amount of water in the outlet, therefore care should be given in the design of
catalyst, as this could decrease its catalytic activity. [18]
Concerning the conversion rate, which is an important system parameter and denotes the
amount of CO2 converted into MeOH or other products, it is dependent on both the thermo-
dynamic limitations and on the kinetic/catalytic performance. [19–22] The process is exother-
mic; therefore, according to Le Chatelier’s principle, it is favored in lower temperatures and
higher pressures. The latter is attributed to the stoichiometric amount of moles which is
decreased from 4 to 2 in the reactants. The typical conditions of methanol synthesis through
CO2 hydrogenation are at 220-270 °C and 50–80 bar, with conversion rates up to 25 % per
pass. This is a trade-off between kinetics and thermodynamics as very low temperatures do
not favor the kinetic rate. [19–22].

In the specific conditions of one-step electrolysis, 28 bar and 240 °C, the conversion rate is close
to 16% moles/moles implying that the system has reached equilbrium conditions by making
use of an efficient catalyst. The choice of those operating conditions for methanol synthesis
using a solid phosphate electrolyzer is a compromise between electrolyte performance, reaction
thermodynamics, and product stability. The system operates at 240 °C and 28 bar, conditions
that match the beginning of superprotonic conductivity in the CsH2PO4 (CDP) electrolyte,
which begins around 232 °C. While higher temperatures improve ionic conductivity and fa-
cilitate reaction kinetics, they also risk methanol decomposition, making 240 °C a practical
upper limit. [4].
For the purposes of the technoeconomic analysis, the system is assumed to operate at equi-
librium conditions; therefore only the thermodynamic limit is considered without detailed
catalyst design.
3.5
Unit stack design and basic system assumptions.
The core component of the electrolyzer system is the stack which is usually made up of multiple
individual cells stacked together in series to increase the voltage and therefore hydrogen
production. The electrolyzer’s skeleton is the bipolar plates, which connect the anode of one
cell with the cathode of the next allowing current to flow through the stack. The bipolar
plates have also channels or grooves to guide reactants to the electrode surfaces and products
aways from the cell. The material of the bipolar plates is stainless steel with the anode coated
with RuO2 for corrosion resistance. [4] One stack was assumed to consist of 100 cells which
is a common stack design.

The electrodes and electrolyte are layered between the flow plates in a gasket comprised of
Papyex and Kapton layers. [24] The Papyex layers are compressible and provide gas sealing
between individual gasket layers. The Kapton layers provide gas sealing between metal sur-
faces and gasket, as well as electrical insulation. The ratio of gasket thickness (combined
Kapton and Papyex thickness) compared to electrodes and electrolyte has to be carefully
controlled. A too thick gasket will result in insufficient pressure on the electrode-electrolyte
interface and a weak contact between the two. If the gasket is too thin, the pressure on the
electrolyte will be too high and it can form cracks and result in gas crossing over between the
electrode compartments. Several layers of Papyex and Kapton can be combined to achieve
an overall thicker layer. The ratio between the electrolyte and gaskets should be 1.0 : 1.1 for
the electrolyte and electrodes. [24]
The technoeconomic analysis was performed for the unit stack design which is the base case in
which the sensitivity analysis and optimization was performed. The voltage and the current
were set to 1.8 V and 1 A/cm2 respectively, which are typical values for a PEM electrolyzer
system.
The loading of the catalysts follow the values of the experiments conducted by
Christensen et al. [4]. The unit stack design is comprised of three stacks to increase the power
of the electrolyzer to 1.06 MW, since one unit made of 100 cells equals to 350 kW. This
was chosen for the shake of simplifying the comparison with literature values. The choice
of temperature and pressure conditions was further explained in Section 3.4. The unit scale
design specifications along with the stack dimensions are illustrated in Figure 3.

Carbon dioxide (CO2) can be transported via pipelines or trucks. The transportation of CO2
via pipelines can occur in three primary phases—gas, liquid, and supercritical—each suited
to specific conditions and operational objectives. Gas-phase transportation involves CO2 in
its gaseous form at low pressures, making it safer and more appropriate for short distances
and densely populated areas. However, it requires larger pipelines and entails higher invest-
ment per unit of throughput. Liquid-phase transportation compresses and cools CO2 into a
liquid state, which is ideal for short-distance, low-throughput applications, particularly when
CO2 is already available in liquid form at the source. This method demands insulation and
incurs higher infrastructure costs.
Supercritical transportation, where CO2 is maintained
above 31.1◦C and 73.8 bar, is the most cost-effective and efficient method for long-distance,
high-volume transfer. However, it requires rigorous control of temperature and pressure and
is typically routed through sparsely populated regions due to safety concerns.Across all trans-
portation methods, CO2 exhibits unique physical properties, including its tendency for rapid
phase change, corrosive behavior in the presence of water, and sensitivity to both pressure and
temperature. These characteristics necessitate careful design considerations, such as selecting
appropriate materials, managing impurities that may affect phase stability, and conducting
comprehensive thermodynamic analyses to ensure pipeline integrity and operational safety.
In this analysis, it is assumed that CO2 enters the system at the required operating pressure;
therefore, no additional compression is necessary prior to utilization.
Another critical parameter is the final destination and intended application of the CO2, which
determines the necessary outlet pressure. For instance, pressures of 20-30 bar are typically
sufficient for CO2 liquefaction and subsequent short-term storage or transportation. In con-
trast, underground geological storage generally requires significantly higher pressures, ranging
between 90-170 bar. For pipeline transportation, regional distribution networks often operate
at pressures of 10-15 bar, whereas long-distance transmission pipelines are typically designed
for operating pressures of 70-90 bar, depending on pipeline length, elevation profile, and
throughput requirements. [25, 26]. In the specific investigation, CO2 is considered to enter
the plant at 28 bars which is teh operating pressure of the electrolyzer.

4.2
Model with recirculation
Recirculation is essential in this process because the conversion of CO2 and H2 to methanol
is thermodynamically limited, meaning that only a fraction of the reactants is converted in a
single pass through the reactor. By recycling the unreacted gases back into the system, overall
CO2 conversion is improved, methanol yield is enhanced, and waste is reduced. This not only
increases resource efficiency but also lowers operating costs and minimizes the environmental
impact of the process. After separation from methanol and water, the unreacted gases are
recirculated to the cathode and mixed with fresh CO2 feed until a steady-state condition is
achieved.

4.2.2
Recirculation with non-ideal separation
In a real design, since the system involves a mixture of components whose separation is
not solely governed by their boiling points, part of the CO2 dissolves into the liquid phase
containing water and methanol, while a portion of water and methanol remains in the gas
phase with CO2 and H2. Hydrogen is non-condensable and is entirely recirculated back into
the system.
However, the recirculation of water and methanol acts as inerts, which can
accumulate over time if no vent stream is implemented. Moreover, the solubility of CO2 in
methanol and water at high pressures can disrupt the ideal CO2:H2 ratio of 1:3, as CO2 is
partially lost to the liquid phase while H2 continues to recirculate. This imbalance can lead
Page 17 of 94
==PAGE:19==

to hydrogen accumulation, which is undesirable both due to its explosive nature and because
hydrogen has the highest mass-specific heat capacity among the gases involved. The latter
would increase the energy demand for reheating the mixture to the operating temperature.
Therefore, a vent stream is necessary to maintain mass balance and prevent accumulation.
However, the vent flow should be minimized, as the loss of valuable reactants would negatively
affect the economic efficiency of the process.

4.4.2
Electrolyzer thermal management and heat integration
The stack temperature should be carefully controlled to avoid high temperature gradients.
This is essential because the membrane operates within a specific temperature range, and large
gradients can induce cell degradation or thermal cracking. [31] For solid oxide electrolyzers
(SOEC), the stack temperature is usually regulated by controlling the sweep gas flow, by
adjusting the excess air flow (air ratio), which allows cooling or heating of the cell to avoid
large temperature differences (approximately 100 K) between gas inlet and outlet. [31] On the
other hand, lower-temperature electrolyzers such as PEM or alkaline systems absorb thermal
energy from inefficiencies via the unconverted water, which is then cooled through an air-
cooled radiator. [32] Figure15 shows the process diagram of electrolyzer cooling system.
Figure 15: Process flow diagram of the electrolyzer internal cooling and heat recovery loop
with the feed water.
For the specific system under investigation, a conceptual design was proposed in which the
electrolyzer maintains a stable temperature through internal cooling. This internal cooling is
achieved via channels inside the electrolyzer that contain heat pipes. [33] Heat pipes are tubes
or cavities filled with small amounts of heat transfer liquids; they enable high heat transfer
rates due to the evaporation-transport-condensation cycle of the liquid within the pipe. [33]
In this case, water is used near its boiling point: it absorbs the latent heat of vaporization,
vaporizes, transports heat, and releases it upon condensation in another part of the system.
Figure 15 show the illustration of the closed loop system with vaporizing and condensing
steam.

Total capital investment
The total capital investment, or CAPEX, accounts for the full cost required to manufacture,
purchase, deliver, install, and commission a system or facility. It consists of the direct costs
which include expenses for equipment supply, installation, mounting, interconnecting piping
and the indirect costs, which cover engineering, project management, construction, super-
vision, management, and commissioning costs, as well as contingencies to cover risks and
uncertainties that may lead to higher costs. [40], [41]
The direct costs of an electrolyzer system are determined by the system configuration and de-
fined boundaries. A typical PEM electrolyzer system includes the electrolyzer units, which are
composed of multiple stacks, each consisting of multiple cells. However, in addition to the elec-
trolyzer itself, the system requires several auxiliary components to operate effectively. These
include power electronics, such as rectifiers and DC-DC converters to convert incoming AC
electricity into DC current, as well as sensors, valves, piping, and control units. Furthermore,
the system incorporates thermal management systems, purification units, and compressors.
Together, these components represent a significant portion of the total system cost, meaning
that the stack itself may not be the dominant cost contributor. [42–45]
The one-step electrolyzer system for water-to-methanol production is a hybrid configuration
that incorporates most of the components mentioned above, along with additional equipment
related to methanol separation and purification. The cost structure and the methodology
used to calculate the cost of each component in this system are illustrated in Figure 17.

5.4
Electrodes
Regarding the electrodes, the cathode consists of a stainless steel felt electrode, which serves
both as the porous transport layer (PTL) and the electrode itself, diverging from conventional
PEM designs that use separate titanium (Ti) or stainless steel PTLs beneath catalyst-coated
membranes. A layer of platinum (Pt) is then coated on top of the strike layer. On top of
this, a sandwich-type layer is applied, consisting of two plates of copper foam filled with a

(316L) felt, followed by electroplating with ruthenium (Ru). An additional layer of ruthenium
is then applied by ink spraying on top of the electroplated layer. [4]

Gaskets
The electrodes and electrolyte are layered between the flow plates in a gasket composed
of Papyex and Kapton layers. The Papyex layers are compressible and provide gas sealing
between individual gasket layers. The Kapton layers provide gas sealing between the metal
surfaces and the gasket, as well as electrical insulation. The ratio of gasket thickness (combined
Kapton and Papyex thickness) to the thickness of the electrodes and electrolyte must be
carefully controlled: it should be 1.0 : 1.0 for the electrolyte and 1.1 : 1.0 (gasket : electrode) for
the electrode. [8,9] A gasket that is too thick results in insufficient pressure on the electrode-
electrolyte interface and weak contact between the two.

5.9
Economy of scale
The capital cost of the methanol production system is influenced by a combination of dif-
ferent factors, such as technological innovation, learning effects, market penetration, as well
as external drivers such as inflation and the rising cost of inputs like building materials and
commodities. Among these, economies of scale could have a significant effect in cost reduction
particularly in early stages system.
Although the modular design of electrolyzer stacks limits the extent to which traditional
economies of scale could apply, it could still be achieved a cost reduction through volume-
based procurement, where unit prices decline as order quantities increase. In contrast, balance
of plant (BoP) components, such as heat exchangers, pumps, and compressors, offer greater
scalability, as larger equipment can replace multiple smaller units, thereby maximizing scale-
driven cost savings. And this particularly important for the stacks, where they could share
the same large equipment.
The influence of economies of scale in the Balance of the Plant is assessed using a framework
similar to that applied in conventional petrochemical plants, where larger production units
typically benefit from reduced capital costs per unit of capacity. The CAPEX scale-up effect
is quantified using the Power Law Cost Function, which describes how capital costs evolve as
a function of installed capacity. The equation is given by:

OPEX
6.1
Fixed costs
The operating expenses of the plant are categorized into fixed and variable operating expenses.
The fixed costs include labour, administrative, and maintenance costs, which are typically
estimated at 5% of the CAPEX per year. The variable costs include electricity and feedstock,
covering the consumption of utilities and raw materials.
An important part of the fixed cost is the stack replacement. It is defined as the moment
during operation when the electrolysis power consumption increases by 10%, marking the end
of life of the stack [77]. The European Union summarizes the degradation rates for SOEL,
PEMEL, and AEL as 1.9%/kh, 0.19%/kh, and 0.12%/kh, respectively, as of 2020 [71, 77].
Consequently, the stack’s full load hours combined with the plant’s capacity factor determine
the replacement schedule, making stack replacement a recurring cost over the plant’s lifetime.
The fraction of the stack that requires replacement depends on the technology and degradation
mechanisms involved. In the case of PEM electrolyzers, replacement typically involves the
electrochemical cells, while retaining components such as bipolar plates, manifolds, and end
plates. For the purposes of this analysis, only membranes and electrodes are considered to
be replaced, accounting for 20% of the initial stack CAPEX. This percentage is assumed to
remain constant for each replacement cycle. A stack lifetime of 70,000 operating hours was
assumed, which is a typical value reported for PEM electrolyzers [78–80].
The replacement schedule is determined by dividing the stack lifetime by the plant’s annual
operating hours, which are influenced by the capacity factor. For a capacity factor of 100%,
the replacement interval is calculated to be approximately 8 years. Therefore, over a plant
lifetime of 20 years, two replacements are anticipated. The present value of future replacement
costs is calculated using the following equation:
PVstack replacement,i = Replacement Cost in Year t

Finally, the total present value of replacement costs is annualized using the annuity factor,
allowing this cost to be consistently distributed over the full lifetime of the investment, similar

6.3
CO2 feed
The cost of carbon dioxide (CO2) is highly dependent on its origin and the level of processing
required to purify and compress it for methanol synthesis. The lowest-cost CO2, typically
ranging from USD 15-35 per tonne-comes from facilities that emit concentrated biogenic or
industrial CO2 streams as by-products, with minimal capture required (e.g. ethanol fermen-
tation, natural gas sweetening, fertilizer production) [81–84]. These sources, however, offer
limited overall capacity. In contrast, capturing CO2 from flue gases of power plants, steel
mills, cement factories incurs higher costs typically USD 40-120 per tonne due to the need for
more complex capture systems like solvent absorption or membranes [83,85,86]. These higher
costs stem from integrating capture units into facilities with more dilute CO2 streams.
Among biogenic sources, ethanol fermentation is especially attractive, producing very high-
purity, 99% of CO2, requiring minimal purification and compression generally placing capture
costs near the range of 15-30 USD/ton [81,82]. Other biogenic sources such as biogas upgrad-
ing or municipal waste incineration typically yield moderate-purity CO2 streams, requiring
more sophisticated separation and slightly higher costs. Overall, biogenic CO2 sources remain
substantially more economical compared to Direct Air Capture (DAC), which currently incurs
costs in the range of 400-800 USD/ton [84], and cheaper than capturing from industrial flue
gases.

Electric power supply
The electric power supply for the process can be sourced from various energy systems, each
differing significantly in cost structure, technical requirements, and reliability.
Renewable
sources such as wind and solar power generally offer lower long-term electricity prices, typically
ranging from 0.02 to 0.06 USD/kWh, depending on resource availability, geographic location,
and project scale. [81,87] In contrast, electricity from the public grid is often more expensive,
typically ranging between 0.06 and 0.15 USD/kWh, reflecting market tariffs, grid fees, taxes,
and other regulatory charges. While grid electricity provides stable, continuous availability,
wind and photovoltaic (PV) power are intermittent, depending on weather conditions and
time of day. As a result, utilizing renewable electricity may require additional measures, such
as flexible electrolyzer operation, energy storage systems, or hybrid configurations, to ensure
stable operation of downstream processes.
In recent years, the levelized cost of electricity (LCOE) for renewable technologies has declined
substantially. Solar PV has experienced a reduction in LCOE of approximately 90% since
2010, while onshore wind has seen a decrease of around 70% over the same period. According
to recent data, in 2023 the global average LCOE reached 0.044 USD/kWh for solar PV and
0.033 USD/kWh for onshore wind [88]. These LCOE figures include the full costs associated
with electricity generation—such as capital expenditure (CAPEX), operating expenditure
(OPEX), maintenance, and financing costs—but exclude any additional costs related to grid

8.3.2
Chronopotentiometry & Polarization curve
Chronopotentiometry is an electrochemical technique where a constant current is applied to
the electrochemical cell between the working and the counter electrode while the potential
is measured as a function of time, relative to the reference electrode. The applied current
forces electrochemical reactions to proceed, and the resulting potential response reflects the
kinetics, mass transport, and electrode processes involved. Redox-active species diffuse to the
working electrode surface to balance the applied current, until the diffusion-limited concen-
tration of redox species reaches zero at the electrode surface then the potential changes to
Page 70 of 94
==PAGE:72==
Maria Grammatikou
Master thesis
a value at which the flux of the electroactive species is sufficient to supply the applied cur-
rent.In systems where mass transport limitations become significant, characteristic features
such as potential plateaus or rapid potential changes (due to depletion of reactive species at
the electrode surface) can be observed. Chronopotentiometry is particularly useful for eval-
uating reaction stability, determining diffusion coefficients, and studying electrode processes
under controlled current conditions. [99,100] Following the Chronopotentiometry a polariza-
tion curve is constructed by doing step chronopotentiometry. In this method, a sequence of
constant current steps are applied to the electrochemical cell, and the corresponding potential
response is recorded over time. For each current level, the steady-state voltage is extracted by
averaging the potential after the initial transient response. The voltage values are then plot-
ted against the corresponding current densities to generate the polarization curve. This curve
provides insight into the kinetic, ohmic, and mass transport characteristics of the electro-
chemical system. [12,101] In this study, chronopotentiometry and step chronopotentiometry
was employed to investigate the stability and response of the membrane-electrode assembly
under both electrolysis and methanol production conditions.
8.4
Preparation of single cell
8.4.1
Membrane Preparation
The solid electrolyte, CsH2PO4 (CDP), is synthesized by reacting Cs2CO3 with 85% H3PO4
under stirring, followed by precipitation in methanol. The resulting solid is filtered, washed,
and dried before being recrystallized in water and again dried at 110°C. To prepare membranes,
then CDP is mixed with 7% polyvinyl butyral (PVB) as a binder. First, 32 g of CDP is ball-
milled with 25 mL of isopropanol for two days to form a uniform paste. Then, 2.5 g of PVB
is added, and the mixture is ball-milled for one additional day. The resulting slurry is the
casted and dried. [8]
For the preparation of the new membrane electrolyte, CDP is not recrystallized in water,
instead, precipitation is carried out in methanol. The resulting material is ball milled without
the addition of PVB binder, using larger volumes of isopropanol (e.g., 30 mL and 50 mL
compared to the standard 25 mL) which promotes the dispersion.
Following milling, the
CDP powder is applied to a PBI felt substrate, which is subsequently wetted and hot pressed
to form the membrane.
After ball milling the mean particle size is 15µm, with no particles larger than 40µm (in
contrast to 22µm and up to 100µm) [8]
8.4.2
Hot Pressing of CDP Electrolyte
After drying, membrane sheets are cut into 41 mm diameter disks. To prevent sticking during
pressing, the disks are sandwiched between two 41 mm diameter Teflon discs. This stack is
placed within a metal ring between two metal plates. The assembly is placed between two
heating blocks of a hydraulic press, and compressed at 3000 kg (151 kg/cm2) at 160°C for 45
minutes. Cooling water is circulated to prevent overheating. After pressing, the assembly is
cooled to room temperature, and the electrolyte disc is gently separated using a Kapton sheet
to avoid cracking. The final thickness is measured at multiple locations for documentation.

8.4.3
Electrolysis Cell Construction
The hot-pressed CDP membrane is assembled between two metal felt electrodes (typically
Pt- or Ru-coated stainless steel). The full cell is constructed by sandwiching the electrodes
and electrolyte between two stainless steel flow plates, with the anode plate coated in Ru
for corrosion protection. Gas sealing and electrical insulation are provided by layered Papyex
(compressed graphite) and Kapton gaskets. The gasket thickness is selected carefully to match
electrode and electrolyte dimensions: typically a 1:1 thickness ratio for electrolyte and a 1.1:1
ratio for electrodes to maintain proper compression. Assembly is performed layer by layer.

Experiments 6 & 7
Experiments 6 and 7 refer to a new membrane formulation that excludes the use of PVB
binder and replaces methanol with isopropanol as the solvent. It has been shown that using
isopropanol as a solvent could induce a more uniform distribution of components. [103] Instead,
the membrane material is dispersed into a PBI felt matrix, which is a very chemically and
thermally stable material, with the purpose of enhancing membrane’s flexibility. Additionally,
since the CDP is not recrystallized in water, it results in finer particle formation. These finer
particles are more uniformly distributed within the PBI felt, contributing to enhanced activity.
This method also yields membranes with the lowest thickness observed among the samples
studied.

Summary and conclusions
This thesis presents a detailed techno-economic analysis of the lab-developed method for one-
step e-methanol synthesis. The process is based on a novel electrolyzer that integrates water
electrolysis and CO2 hydrogenation in a single unit, enabled by a super proton-conducting
membrane (CsH2PO4, CDP). The system employs ruthenium (Ru) as the anode catalyst,
platinum (Pt) at the cathode, and incorporates a copper-based methanol synthesis catalyst
within the cathode side. Protons generated from water oxidation at the anode migrate through
the CDP membrane and react with the CO2 feed at the cathode to form methanol. Operating
conditions of 240 °C and 28 bar were selected to support both super-protonic conductivity and
favorable methanol synthesis kinetics and thermodynamics.
The analysis was carried out for a 1.06 MW system composed of three electrolyzer stacks.
Catalyst loadings were taken from laboratory experiments, while stack dimensions and system
specifications were based on design assumptions. The process was simulated using Aspen Plus,
Page 84 of 94
==PAGE:86==
Maria Grammatikou
Master thesis
employing the RK-Soave equation of state, and validated through manual calculations. The
technical evaluation included the design of upstream and downstream the electrolyzer units,
investigation of CO2 recirculation and selection of optimal conditions for condensation and
separation. In addition, the methanol yield was determined along with the system’s energy
consumption.
Initial simulations on the single-pass configuration identified 30°C as the optimal condensation
temperature at 28 bar, balancing methanol recovery at 90% and minimal CO2 loss of 0.25%
in the condensed phase. However, due to the limited CO2-to-methanol conversion in a single
pass at 16%, a gas recirculation loop was introduced to increase methanol yield. The non-
ideal separation model, identified the CO2 solubility in condensed methanol-water phase and
showcased the need of a small vent stream to avoid H2 accumulation in the loop.
This
recirculation strategy increased methanol production to 1.01 mol/s or 116.64 kg/h.
Energy analysis revealed the electrolyzer stacks require 9.09 kWh/kg MeOH. The recirculat-
ing compression for 1 bar of pressure recovery adds 0.03 kWh/kg MeOH. Heating demands
amounted to 3.03 kWh/kg MeOH, split between water evaporation and CO2 preheating,
while cooling requirements were 3.24 kWh/kg MeOH for stack thermal management and 2.47
kWh/kg MeOH for product condensation. These figures highlight significant potential for
internal energy recovery. Since the electrolyzer operates above the thermoneutral voltage, it
generates excess heat, which must be removed in order to keep a stable operation tempera-
ture. Heat integration was implemented between the hot cathode outlet and the incoming
CO2 stream (including recirculation), and partially with the water feed, yielding a 58% re-
duction in heating duty. Further recovery through internal water evaporation cooling loops
within the electrolyzer reduced external heating needs by nearly 99%, with only 1% remain-
ing due to pinch point limitations in heat exchange. Compared to literature estimates of
12–15 kWh/kg MeOH for green methanol production, the examined system demonstrates
competitive performance.
The economic analysis aimed to quantify the Total Capital Investment (TCI), Operating Ex-
penses (OPEX), and Levelized Cost of Methanol (LCOM). LCOM represents the breakeven
selling price of methanol while the TCI was calculated based on both direct and indirect
costs. Direct costs included the electrolyzer stack, Balance of Stack (BoS)—covering power
electronics and piping—and Balance of Plant (BoP), such as heat exchangers, pumps, com-
pressors, piping and installation. The indirect costs include the engineering, procurement and
development.
Stack cost estimation was performed using a bottom-up methodology based on vendor quotes
and literature values for materials and fabrication. Bipolar plates were the most expensive
component, costing 204 USD/kW, primarily due to the CNC machining manufacturing tech-
nique. This was followed by the anode and cathode catalysts. Although used in minimal
quantities, 0.003 and 0.005 kg/kW, the Pt and Ru catalysts were the most expensive in terms
of raw material cost, at 105 and 101 USD/kW, respectively.
Stainless steel raw material
although used in larger quantity, 5 kg/kW it contributed less to the cost at 11 USD/kW.
Overall, the total stack cost was estimated at 488 USD/kW, with a sensitivity range from
460 to 513 USD/kW depending on material, design and cost variations. The design included
reducing the loading of either the cathode or anode catalysts or introducing a new membrane
configuration that reduces membrane thickness.
Page 85 of 94
==PAGE:87==
Maria Grammatikou
Master thesis
BoS was estimated at 398 USD/kW, while BoP cost, calculated using standard chemical
engineering cost equations, added 191 USD/kW. Despite the fact that electrolyzer stacks
are modular systems and less sensitive to scale-up, significant BoP cost reductions, up to
70% to the purchased price, could be achieved through integration of more stacks across
larger components. In addition, scaling to 10.6 MW and 35.3 MW showed that also bulk
manufacturing of components such us the bipolar plates could reduce their cost by 20%. The
combined impact of all the above improvements could bring total the direct costs to 908
USD/kW.
OPEX included the fixed costs categorized into maintenance, and stack replacement and the
variable costs categorized into electricity, CO2, and water cost. The LCOM was calculated
using an annuity factor based on a Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC) of 5% over
a 20-year plant lifetime.
This yielded a base-case LCOM of 1136 USD/ton of methanol.
Electricity was identified as the largest cost contributor accounting for 72% of LCOM and
followed by CO2 feed and CAPEX. Sensitivity analysis of ±20% on key parameters showed
LCOM was most sensitive to electricity price and specific power consumption. CAPEX even
though contributing to the cost less than CO2 feed due to the amortization effects over time, it
showcased slightly higher sensitivity. Improvements in catalyst loading, membrane thickness,
and stack materials, as well as integration at larger scales, 35.3 MW, could reduce LCOM to
1100 USD/ton. WACC, stack lifetime, and plant lifetime had comparatively smaller impacts.
The above findings highlight the importance of stack improvements, particularly membrane
configuration, for achieving short-term cost reductions in the stack and, consequently, low-
ering the LCOM. To validate the feasibility of such improvements, a series of laboratory
experiments were conducted to evaluate system performance and CDP membrane stability
under operating conditions, as well as to test a new membrane preparation method. Initial
experiments (1-4) employed membranes fabricated with a polyvinyl butyral (PVB) binder
revealed overvoltage instability, membrane fluidity inside the electrode structure, and signs of
gas crossover and ionic conductivity loss due to membrane’s mechanical cracking. To address
these issues, a new membrane preparation method was introduced, by excluding PVB binder
while using polybenzimidazole (PBI) felt as a mechanical support. This approach, tested in
Experiments 6 and 7, led to the fabrication of thinner, more elastic, and mechanically robust
membranes. Specifically, Experiment 6 demonstrated voltage stability over time, comparable
with literature benchmarks and indicative of low ohmic resistance and minimal mass transport
limitations.
Overall, the one-step electrolyzer system for e-methanol synthesis demonstrates strong tech-
nical potential, owing to its efficient energy requirement, effective internal heat recovery, as
well integrated production and consumption of hydrogen in one-step. Economically, the sys-
tem offers promising cost improvement opportunities through optimized membrane design,
economies of scale in the Balance of Plant (BoP), and bulk manufacturing of key cost-driving
components. Laboratory experiments validated the impact of membrane design improvements
and confirmed the critical role of continued research and development in enhancing system
performance and reducing costs. These findings offer a promising pathway for sustainable
methanol production and could support decarbonization efforts in energy-intensive sectors
such as shipping.
