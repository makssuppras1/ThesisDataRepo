Abstract
Structural interactions are always present to some extent in complex systems so it is of
great importance to know how to model and deﬁne contacts when trying to predict the
behavior of the system. Current investigation is an attempt to ﬁnd out what kind of
contact geometry between the parts is desirable and easy to predict. Two models with
diﬀerent contact geometries are proposed. The lightweight parts needed to be tested
with non-intrusive methods so at ﬁrst an experiment with acoustic excitation was
used to extract modal parameters. The new experimental setup with electromagnetic
shaker is presented and validated to investigate a lightweight bodies as an attempt to
improve the experimental study. The study of the frequency response variability due
to changing contact conditions was carried out with both experimental methods. The
results from the measurements are compared with the solutions obtained by the ﬁnite
element method and the main observations are summarized. The geometry with small
line contacts showed smaller variability when compared with the model with unilateral
contact geometry. Finally, an attempt to utilize the numerical transient analysis is
documented and the conclusion was made that the proposed structure geometry was
not suitable for the numerical analysis to reproduce experimental results.

Preface
This project is the culmination of the work done over the period spanning from the
23rd of January to the 23rd of June 2017. The thesis has been prepared as a partial
fulﬁllment of the requirements to obtain a MSc degree in Engineering Design and
Applied Mechanics at the Technical University of Denmark. The project has been
carried out following the project plan and represents a work load equivalent to 30
ECTS points.
The thesis was made in collaboration with Oticon A/S. The project was a con-
tinuation of the study carried out by Ester Creixell Mediante where the variation of
structural response due to uncertainty of mechanical contacts in hearing aids where
investigated.
At the moment there is a lack of precise tools to model the contacts in hearing
aids and estimate how it inﬂuence the overall performance of the hearing instrument.
So the present study aims at investigating how the variations of structural response
could be reduced and overall structural response could be modelled by including the
behavior of the mechanical contacts between plastic parts in hearing aids.
The thesis has been supervised by Niels Aage, Ester Creixell Mediante and Martin
Larsen. I would like to thank all my supervisors for their valuable contributions by
consulting, suggesting and discussing the topic at any moment when needed. Also, I
am grateful for their enthusiasm towards the topic, support during the project and
proofreading the thesis report.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation and objectives
Traditionally, a design of complex micro-mechanical systems, such as hearing instru-
ments in the health care industry, involves the use of prototyping and a testing ap-
proach with engineering tools such as computer aided design (CAD) systems and
numerical analysis. While CAD software helps to create and specify the design, the
numerical modeling aims to explain the physical processes within the structure [1].
Explanation leads to reconstruction and prediction, which in turn gives useful data
for decision-making and improvement of the design. Combined with an experimen-
tal validation, the numerical analysis is a powerful tool for creating new solutions in
various industries such as automotives, aviation, space and, as mentioned, heath care.
With ongoing technological advancement, hearing instruments became smaller
and at the same time more powerful, meaning that microphones and loudspeakers are
closer to each other. This enforced feedback problems due to internal transmission of
sound and vibrations [2]. This acoustic-structural interaction becomes very diﬃcult
to investigate experimentally when combined with small dimensions, very complex
geometry and diﬀerent interfaces between components, as in Figure 1.1. Thus, the
traditional prototyping-testing approach is not an eﬃcient means of improving the
vibration response of a structure and the overall performance.
Figure 1.1: General view of parts in a hearing aid [3].
As a tool to solve structural problems and advance the design of new instruments,
the numerical simulation needs to be accommodated in order to investigate and iden-
tify possible improvements. It is clear that nearly all structural problems deal to some
extent with interacting bodies [4]. Therefore, numerical models of the devices must
be improved to account for dynamic contacts between diﬀerent parts that aﬀect a
structural response and inﬂuence the feedback problems of hearing aids.

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
However, in order to use numerical models, it is necessary to ﬁnd a fundamental
and clear deﬁnition of desired contacts. Figure 1.2 shows the current state of the
design in modern hearing instruments. On the left of Figure 1.2 the CAD design is
created in such a way that the parts would have constraints in the desired locations and
directions also leaving clearances between components for manufacturing tolerances.
However, when the device is manufactured and assembled, the components are found
to be tightly ﬁt, thus some the clearances are not maintained. The additional contacts
are in unknown locations and aﬀect the structure’s behavior in unpredictable ways
(simpliﬁed situation is shown in Figure 1.2 right).
From a robust design point of view, the actual design is overconstrained because
there are multiple surfaces limiting each other in the same direction. This makes it
diﬃcult to control design parameters such as mechanical contact force. The lack of un-
derstanding and clariﬁcation of mechanical contacts leads to designs with a vibration
response variability between diﬀerent instruments in serial production. This creates
a problem of controlling the quality of the hearing aids and accurately predicting the
response of the structure and, ultimately, the performance of the device.

Although the vibration transmission through structure cannot be avoided, it can
be minimized to improve the worst-case scenario. What is more, the clear deﬁnition
of contacts and accurate predictive mathematical models would help to optimize and
improve current designs in order to reduce the response variability and gain more
control over the performance of the hearing aids.
This thesis was inspired by previous work in reference [5]. The project aimed at
experimentally investigating the variability of structure vibration response and de-
veloping the mathematical contact models that would incorporate the uncertainties
caused by mechanical connections between hearing instrument parts. The current
investigation focuses on experimental testing of the structural behavior in hearing
aid-scale assemblies, and of the response variability reduction and on deﬁning math-
ematical models that could predict the vibration behavior of these structures. The
main goal of this project is to investigate the eﬀects of the mechanical contacts to
the structure vibration response and create the mathematical simulation model that
could capture these eﬀects.

1.2
State of the art
Any viable modelling technique needs to be supported by an appropriate experimental
method in order to validate the result. The experimental approach to measuring the
dynamic behavior of structures (modal testing) relies mostly on extracting the vibra-
tion characteristics of a structure from various measurements. Common techniques
of exciting a lightweight ﬂexible structure include using pure impact, impact trough
force sensor and electromagnetic shaker with stinger setup as in [6] and [7].
While mechanical vibration measurement theory is well established and described
as in [8], there can be quite a big inconsistency in modal testing practice as stated
in reference [9]. The reason is that the errors can be caused by various factors, such
as mechanical device errors, noise, signal processing errors, non-linear behavior of a
structure and so on [9]. There are documented attempts to overcome some problems
to achieve high precision modal testing of lightweight structures as in [6]. In the article
the author presents a method that compensates for the eﬀect of an accelerometer mass
acting on a test structure in order to obtain accurate modal parameters. There are
also examples, such as in [10], where dynamic characteristics of the excitation system
(shaker, stinger and transducers) can contaminate the frequency response function
(FRF) of the structure being tested. The study in [10] aims at predicting the scale of
contamination and reducing systematic errors in measurements.
However, the smallest test specimen mass in the relevant articles were 600g which
is massive compared to a hearing aid industry parts, which typically have a mass of 1g
and below. There is a noticeable lack of documented investigations on experimental
testing of such light and small structures. This means that typical conﬁgurations of
experiment setups need to be used with extreme caution and, if possible, an alternative
solution should be explored, for instance, an acoustic excitation as used in the study
in [5].
Since this project aims to investigate the vibrating structures in contact, it is
appropriate to look into the work done in the ﬁeld of vibroimpact mechanics. The
published experimental and mathematical investigations range from analyzing simple
phenomena of a bouncing ball [11] to a cantilever beam impacting a constraint [12].
In general, studies of periodic impact seek an analytic solution that approximates the
measurements fairly well. Mostly, one degree-of-freedom (DoF) models are used and
this greatly limits the complexity of a structure that can be analyzed. For instance,
besides the aforementioned ball and beam studies other researchers described pendu-
lum systems [13] and construction platforms stacked together under base excitation
which were still analyzed analytically as a 1 DoF system [14]. In relation to acoustics,
a model to study rattle noises in automotive cockpits by using generic system of a
beam and an aluminum base was proposed in reference [15]. Despite its uniqueness,
the system could also be modelled by a single DoF impact oscillator. It is established
that vibroimpact problems are highly non-linear because of sudden change between
contact and non-contact states. Therefore, analytic solutions can only predict the
behavior of relatively simple models and in order to simulate a complex system, a
more sophisticated ﬁnite element method (FEM) needs to be employed.
The ﬁrst notable attempt to solve the contact mechanics problem was done by
Hertz in 1882 by applying elasticity theory [16]. Hertz derived expressions for pres-

Based on the NTS discretization and contact element shape functions, the contact

The Technique demonstrates stability and passes the contact
patch test. Some Mortar method-based procedures rely on the intermediate surface
formulation as illustrated in Figure 1.3c and other techniques describe one of the

family of methods are still being developed for various problems. Although they are
reportedly diﬃcult to implement.
An alternative method for contact domain discretization would be the Nitsche
method, analyzed in [37, 38], where the Gauss points of one surface play the role of
slave nodes. Just to explore a few more alternative methods, the recently introduced
isogeometric contact formulation [39] and the virtual element method combined with
NTN contact formulation in [40] show good performance. These procedures are out
of the scope of this project so, the reader is invited to use the indicated sources for
further study.
Ongoing academic research shows that the computational contact mechanics meth-
ods are improving to be more generalized in order to be adapted to various industries.
As the hearing aid industry is increasingly utilizing FEM tools, methods from contact
mechanics are required to simulate and evaluate new designs in suﬃcient accuracy.

Chapter 2
Theory and methods
While physical phenomena of the contact are easy to understand and observe, nu-
merical procedures to solve a contact mechanics problems are not generic and are not
easily interpreted. The main challenge in solving these problems is the fact that a
contact and non-contact state changes as a step function which is a hard non-linearity
that cannot be diﬀerentiated or integrated in a trivial way. The problem involves not
only computing correct displacements of the bodies but also a means of incorporating
constraints of contacts which simply create a non-linear behavior of a model. The
literature review showed that there have been and still are a lot of eﬀort to solve
these problems computationally. New approaches that are being developed show that
a generic algorithm for this kind of problem is yet to be found. Bearing this in mind,
the ANSYS commercial software will be used in this project. But even when using
commercial solutions, it is important to understand the underlying principles to make
the right choices on the many parameters that must be adjusted.
The following describes the basic principles of how mechanical contact problems
are solved and what solutions will be used in this study.
2.1
Contact formulation
Simple examples are shown to explain the basic principles used for algorithms and
methods utilized in this investigation.

2.1.2
Penalty method
The ﬁrst method that is going to be used is the so-called Penalty method.
The
procedure is based on utilizing a penalty factor kn which acts just like a spring stiﬀness,
therefore, the mechanics of the method can be represented as a non-linear spring acting

The spring stiﬀness/penalty factor is resisting an external contact body that loads
in normal direction, producing a resistance force, equal to those loads. It is clear that
a general expression for the spring behavior can be expressed as:
Fn = knxp;
(2.6)
Where xp is penetration value. An array of such springs over the contact interface
represents a force ﬁeld which is the equivalent of contact pressure, which is one of the
main parameters in computational contact mechanics.
The main property of a penalty method is that the penetration xp is always present
to some extent. This is due to the numerical limitations of penalty factor/spring
stiﬀness. While analyzing equation (2.6), it is clear that for a ﬁnite contact force
Fn there must be some non-zero values of kn and xp. The ideal case is when the
stiﬀness of the spring kn equals inﬁnity, then the penetration would be inﬁnitely
small. However, the inﬁnite values are not possible numerically. Also, too high a
penalty value kn would cause ill-conditioning of a system and result in convergence
problems. Thus, penetration must be allowed but as long as xp values are small or
negligible, a result will be accurate enough. The penalty method is easy to implement
and only a relatively small computational power is required to reach the solution.
2.1.3
Lagrange Multiplier formulation
Another highly used method is a Lagrange Multiplier formulation which, in contrast
to penalty method, enforces zero or nearly zero penetration of the contacting bodies.
The method does not require a normal contact stiﬀness and treats the normal contact
force as an additional degree of freedom. So the procedure adds an extra equation
with the λ term into the system of equations and when solved, this term represents
the contact force:
Fn = λ;
(2.7)
For comparison with other methods, the illustration in Figure 2.3 shows a simpli-
ﬁed result of the Normal Lagrange procedure.

Despite the fact that it produces an accurate result, the Lagrangian adds addi-
tional unknowns into the system that must be solved, thus the method can be more
computationally expensive. Nevertheless, with no penetration a contact status can
be either open or closed, and with such a step-change requirement the procedure can
bring problems in convergence because contact points may oscillate between open/-
closed status. Such an oscillation is called chattering and, when encountered, can be
avoided by allowing a negligible penetration.
2.1.4
Augmented Lagrange formulation
The last method is the Augmented Lagrange method which is categorized as a penalty-
based method, but as the name indicates, it is also related to the Lagrange multiplier
method. An equation that describes the principle of the Augmented Lagrange method
can be expressed as:
Fn = knxp + λ;
(2.8)
The illustration of this procedure is essentially the same as in Figure (2.2), but
the computational procedure is a combination of both previous methods as seen in
equation (2.8). In general, the augmented Lagrange combines the advantages of both
methods - Lagrange Multiplier and Penalty - and avoids their drawbacks. It converges
precisely to an exact solution xp for a ﬁnite value of the penalty coeﬃcient kn and the
additional computational eﬀorts are relatively low [21]. The term λ in equation 2.8
mimics the usual Lagrange multiplier as in equation (2.7) and represents the extra
DoF in the system as a reaction force, also this term is the reason why the augmented
Lagrange method is less sensitive to the magnitude of the contact stiﬀness kn. This
allows the use of higher penalty factor values, which results in a much smaller allowed
penetrations combined with the exact Lagrange solution. That yields better accuracy
for the whole procedure. Because of its eﬃcient use of both methods, the augmented
Lagrange method is widely used to solve computational contact mechanics problems.

detected between bodies. The contact detection must be performed at each solution
step and, given the usual big problems, the detection of interactions between surfaces
through all of the surface nodes leads to an excessive time requirement for problems

3.1
Geometry
As stressed in [5], designing for a big contact surface between bodies does not mean
that the whole surface is touching. This is due to irregularities in a manufactured ﬂat
surface in micro-scale where contact and non-contact zones are formed. These irregu-
larities increase uncertainty and variation of contact performance in serial production.
One of the aims of this project is to reduce variability and study possible ways of con-
trolling it. Therefore, two basic geometries were made in order to investigate if better
deﬁned contacts can reduce variability.
One model is chosen to be a structure with unilateral contact where two parts
are connected through relatively big ﬂat surfaces touching each other, a cross-section
and illustration of specimen can be seen in Figure 3.1a. An opposing model, which
is expected to be an improvement, has the same outer geometry but the contact is
changed to have a ﬂat-to-round surface interface. The contacts are made by rounded
geometrical features that have a height and length of 0.3mm that form line contacts
when touching a ﬂat surface. In this case the contacting surfaces are much smaller
than in the previous model and are assumed to be strong enough in order not to be
crushed during measurements, assembly and dis-assembly. All other close surfaces
of the line contact model parts are separated by 0.3mm gap which is suﬃcient to
separate parts bearing in mind that typical vibration amplitude of hearing aid parts
is on a nanometer scale. The illustration of line contact model can be seen in Figure

Chapter 4
Experiments
The following chapter contains a description of the experimental setups that were used
throughout this study and the obtained results. The investigation primarily focused
on the behavior of the structures in a frequency range 1000 - 10000Hz because this is
the common range in which the vibrational problems in hearing aids arise.
At ﬁrst, individual components from Figure 3.2 were tested with acoustic ex-
citation and natural frequencies with mode shapes were obtained. The data were
compared with the simulation results to validate the experimental setup. At the next
step, the assemblies described in section 3.1 were tested with validated experimental
setup. The same geometry was simulated using the FEM model with bonded contacts
and numerical results were compared with the experimental ones.
Furthermore, the variability of the structural responses was determined for the
both assemblies and a diﬀerences between unilateral and line contact models were
discussed.
Later on, an electromagnetic shaker setup was proposed for a next set of measure-
ments. After an evaluation of the setup the variability of assembly responses again
was obtained. Also, numerical solutions were obtained by using the FEM model with
bonded contacts in order to check if there is a match between the results.
Finally, the pull-out test was performed multiple times with diﬀerent pulling
speeds in order to obtain an average friction force acting in the assembly with line con-
tacts. Data collected in the experiment is used to estimate how big is the geometrical
overlap between connected parts.
4.1
Vibration test with acoustic excitation
This section presents an experiment with acoustic excitation. The purpose of this
experiment is to determine if there is a mismatch between the measurement and the
simulation results from the FEM model with bonded contacts. The bonded contacts
option assumes that there is no separation, penetration or sliding of the connecting
surfaces. The mismatch would indicate that there is a separation between contacting
surfaces during the measurement and this would make this case a non-linear problem.
In that case, a more sophisticated FEM model would be required in order to obtain
a correct solution.

4.1.5
Acoustic excitation experiment result discussion
The measurements of individual components have provided data that could be pre-
dicted by the modal FEM analysis with maximum deviation of 5.37%, thus proving
the validity of the experimental setup and used material properties. In turn, the as-
sembled structure measurements gave the results that were signiﬁcantly diﬀerent from
the numerical ones with maximum deviation of 24.69%. This shows that the contacts
inﬂuence the structural response of the assemblies and the FEM model with bonded
contacts is not representing the test specimen frequency response accurately. This
encourages the conclusion, that contacting surfaces touch and separate periodically
when the structure is excited at diﬀerent frequencies. This imposes a non-linearity
to the problem. Therefore, a more elaborate FEM analysis needs to be performed,
namely transient analysis, in order to capture the non-linear structural interaction
between parts and to obtain a measured frequency response function.
When performing variability analysis due to dis-assembly, it was found that the
line contact geometry produces smaller variability than the unilateral contact geom-
etry. This means that smaller but clearly deﬁned contact surface can produce less
varied and more reliable results. However, the high variability was observed in higher
frequency for both cases so there is an open question about the better geometry in
higher frequency ranges.
Furthermore, it is clear from Figures 4.3, 4.5 and 4.6 that there are additional
peaks in FRF of the specimen that was found to have mode shapes with a rigid body
motion. This raises a question about the eﬀect of the acoustic test setup to the results.
There might be an acoustic resonance inside the acoustic chamber since the front door
of the box is made of glass that can produce echoes, which can be reﬂected back to
the chamber. Also, due to sound wave physics and test specimen placement variation,
it is not clear what kind of input forces are acting on the test specimen so the pure
frequency response of the structure alone is diﬃcult, if not impossible, to reproduce.
This is also a reason why the used experimental measurements cannot be called a
modal analysis even though it is suitable for measuring natural frequencies and mode
shapes.
Since one of the objectives of this project is to be able to model part interactions
in the structure, it is important to match the input loads in the experiment and in the
numerical model to reproduce the actual system behavior. Investigating the eﬀects of
an acoustic chamber is out of the scope of this project. So in order to investigate if
the variability observed at higher frequencies is due to measurement errors introduced
by resonances in the acoustic box, a new measurement setup where the input forces
can be measured is introduced.

4.3
Pull-out test
Before a FEM transient analysis could be performed, the computer aided design
(CAD) model geometry needs to be modiﬁed to represent an actual tested structure.
The test specimen geometry presented in Chapter 3 were designed in such a way
that the assembled parts would be touching without any virtual overlap. However,
when manufactured, the assemblies were staying together when assembled, meaning
that there is enough friction force to hold them together. The test specimen was
designed in a way that the friction force could be created only by parts pushing each
other sideways (refer to Figure 3.1). Therefore, the presence of friction force shows
that there is a dimension mismatch, namely, the width of the parts. The correct
dimension needs to be estimated because it inﬂuences forces acting in contacts and
consequently, the frequency response of the structure. Including this part interaction
would result in a more accurate numerical model.
It was decided to experimentally investigate the friction force and then perform
numerical analysis iterations in order to obtain a theoretical overlap of the parts. A
pull-out test was chosen for the study.

Chapter 5
Transient analysis
A FEM transient analysis is performed to model a vibrational behavior of the assembly
with line contact geometry. The transient analysis was chosen because it would allow
to deﬁne a frictional contact between parts and use all of the contact formulations
described in chapter 2. Therefore, the FEM model would allow for contacting sur-
faces to separate and collide with each other over time, meaning that this non-linear
behavior could be captured in the ﬁnal solution. Thus there is a chance that the
measurement results could be replicated by this FEM analysis. A frequency response
function is derived from the transient solution by using the Fast Fourier Transform
(FFT) procedure.
After validating the FEM model for transient analysis and FFT procedure, the
main modelling started by incorporating frictional contacts and the obtained solutions
were compared with the measurement result.

5.3
Transient response with frictional contact formulations
Since the simulation with bonded contact does not match measurement results, it
is desirable to use it with diﬀerent options, namely, Lagrange multiplier, Penalty
and Augmented Lagrange contact formulations which were described in chapter 2.
These formulations would allow separation of the contacting surfaces, thus reduce the
stiﬀness of the system. Therefore, it was decided to use the aforementioned contact
formulations as an attempt to reproduce the measured FRF shown in Figure 5.4 with
higher accuracy.
The FEM model with mentioned contact formulations will use the CAD model
with virtual overlap of 0.041mm. It was predicted by performing a pull-out test in
subsection 4.3.3 with intention to increase accuracy of the numerical scheme. This
is because the measured FRF already incorporate geometrical overlap that produces
forces which hold the parts together.
Unfortunately, when running the FEM transient simulations with model param-
eters described in section 5.1 the computational storage requirements exceeded the
available resources so the full solution were unavailable with primary conﬁguration. It
was decided to reduce the numerical model scale by cropping the frequency range that
needs to be simulated. The frequencies f0 = 1700Hz and f1 = 5000Hz were selected
for the new frequency sweep inspired by the measured FRF shown in Figure 5.4 which
indicates that the desirable resonance peaks are between the new f0 and f1 values.

Chapter 7
Discussion
This chapter starts by discussing the chosen test specimen geometry in chapter 3 and
proceeds by elaborating on the experimental setups and the obtained measurement
results in chapter 4. The discussion is continued by considering a chosen numerical
modelling cases in chapters 3, 4 and 5 and in the end a future work prospects are
identiﬁed.
7.1
Test specimen
During the validation of experimental setups the FEM simulations of separate parts
accurately predicted the behavior of the test specimen thus the material properties
were estimated correctly.
The assemblies showed clear mode shapes and natural frequencies could be identi-
ﬁed from the obtained FRFs. But the assembly experiment results were signiﬁcantly
diﬀerent than the predicted ones by the FEM simulations which assumed bonded con-
tacts. This indicated that there are separation events between the surfaces of the
contacting parts, thus the assemblies have a non-linear behavior and could be inves-
tigated in order to reproduce the contacting surface behavior while also determining
the characteristics of diﬀerent contact types.
However, the proposed test specimen geometry were complex and only modal
parameters could be measured. It would be diﬃcult if not impossible to measure
the transient response of the structure, close to the contacting points meaning that
the non-linear behavior can only be assumed and not observed directly. The direct
observation would allow to identify the stiﬀness of the contact, therefore, allowing to
compare diﬀerent types and geometries of contact. In addition, the direct observation
would allow to capture separation and connection of the contacting surfaces thus
allowing to compare the result directly with the FEM simulation without intermediate
steps. Direct comparison would allow to evaluate the FEM procedures in terms of
suitability for the problem.
The problem of limiting observation could be overcome by having a simpliﬁed
test specimen geometry. It would be easier to measure a primitive shape body which
have a clearly visible contact that could be measured by the Scanning Laser Doppler
Vibrometer. Also, reduction in size and complexity of the geometry would lead to
smaller and faster FEM simulation cases.
7.2
Experimental study
The test specimen were measured with the acoustic excitation in the acoustic box and
the electromagnetic shaker. The frequency response functions were obtained in both
experiments. The mode shapes with natural frequencies of the individual parts were
obtained and it matched with the FEM modal simulation predictions meaning that

the experiments are suitable for extracting the mechanical vibration parameters.
However, the acoustic excitation cannot be easily quantiﬁed or recorded, therefore,
the experiment with acoustic excitation cannot be considered as modal analysis. The
obtained FRF of the test specimen in this setup can be aﬀected by the specimen
placement in the box, acoustic modes and the results showed additional resonance
peaks with unknown origins. Because of these possibilities the ﬁnal output of the
experiment need to be interpreted with caution.
The modal analysis with the electromagnetic shaker proved to induce only me-
chanical modes thus producing a relatively clear FRF of the test specimen.
The
measurement of the input force and clearly deﬁned excitation location in the struc-
ture allowed to obtain the coherence plot and easily model the test case by the FEM
modal, harmonic and transient simulations. Despite that the measurement setup com-
ponents were detected to interact and inﬂuence the ﬁnal measurement result. The
stinger proved to have own vibration modes in the measured frequency range and even
if it was moving in much lower magnitudes than the test specimen itself, the stinger
was concluded to induce the additional modes in the test part. The stingers inﬂuence
could be avoided by having a diﬀerent stinger geometry which would increase stiﬀness
and have the modes outside of the measurement frequency range.
In addition, the bottom of the stinger that was ﬁxed was found to be oscillating
in the lateral direction in distinct frequency ranges. This suggested that there is an
additional test ﬁxture lateral movement caused by shaker suspension. Unfortunately,
this cannot be controlled in any way by using the same excitation device, therefore, the
assumption of purely vertical excitation of the test structure is faulty to some extent.
The results need to be interpreted with caution that the obtained modal parameters
might be shifted to lower frequencies because the additional lateral movement can
aﬀect the total stiﬀness of the test ﬁxture. Nonetheless, the connections between the
stinger, base, screw and the test part were assumed to be completely rigid which might
be not true thus for further use the eﬀect of those connections should be evaluated in
terms of their eﬀect to the ﬁnal result.
7.3
Numerical modelling
The FEM modal and harmonic analysis was proven to be accurate enough for the
individual components in the both test cases but lacks in precision when modelling
assembled structures. This is due to the fact that these FEM routines treats interfaces
between the parts as bonded contacts which do not account for non-linear contact
eﬀects such as separation or sliding of the contacting surfaces. The bonded contacts
assumes no separation, penetration or sliding of the contacting surfaces so the system
becomes more stiﬀthan it should be. That is what was seen during the project -
when assemblies where simulated by the FEM modal or harmonic analysis most of
the numerical resonance peaks where predicted in the higher frequencies than the
measured ones. Even though, the mode shapes where predicted in suﬃcient accuracy,
the numerical natural frequencies need to be interpreted with caution when using the
bonded contact option.
The aforementioned problems can be avoided by using the frictional contact for-

schemes. However, after running simulations the obtained transient responses and
resulted FRFs were diﬀerent than the experimental ones. It was concluded that the

7.4
Future work
Understanding the eﬀect of mechanical contacts in a hearing aid structure would allow
to predict the performance of the instrument with higher conﬁdence. Predictability
would lead to a faster development of the new devices and the increased robustness
of the device against the mechanical vibrations. Therefore, it is worth to continue the
research on this direction.
The future work could include a more simple test specimen geometry. The part
could have a beam-like geometry with a speciﬁc contact geometry. Such conﬁguration
would allow to directly observe and measure the frequency and the transient responses
which could be easily compared with the numerical models. This approach could
help investigating diﬀerent types of contacts and ultimately, would help to relate the
contact stiﬀness parameter to the contacting area while also revealing the inﬂuence
of the diﬀerent contact types to the structural response of the whole system.
Besides that, the shaker setup could be further developed by investigating what
kind of stinger geometry would be suitable to test the lightweight parts without pol-
luting the frequency response and having a minimal eﬀect to the stiﬀness of the tested
structure. The study could aim at creating an eﬀective test system for investigating
the mechanical vibration behavior of the lightweight components. Alternatively, the
shaker setup without a stinger could be investigated to obtain the experimental setup
that would allow an easy measurement of contacting surfaces.
Furthermore, frictional contact formulations could be further investigated with
new geometry of test specimen. Also, the new knowledge about the contact types
could be utilized by using an alternative FEM contact modelling approaches. An
example of such approach could be implementing the non-linear springs in the con-
tacting surfaces that have the contact stiﬀness estimated by the measurements of the
simpliﬁed geometry. Successful implementation could lead to a fast and eﬀective way
to predict the vibrational response of the assembled structure.
Finally, the FEM transient analysis techniques could be further investigated by us-
ing hardware that has more of computational resources than was available throughout
this study. While the additional computational resources could be a costly alternative
it is also of great interest to implement the model reduction techniques into the FEM
transient analysis that would allow to perform a ﬁne mesh computations without
suﬀering the increasingly demanding computational requirements.

Chapter 8
Conclusions
This project aimed at experimental investigation and numerical modelling of contacts
in hearing aids.
Two structures were proposed in order to replicate a shell of a
hearing aid. Each structure had diﬀerent contact geometries allowing to compare
how it inﬂuences the frequency response function. One structure included a unilateral
contact geometry where two relatively big surfaces touch each other while the opposing
model included line contact geometry where ﬂat surface touches a strip of round
surface.
The main challenge of experimental investigation was that the lightweight test
specimen needed to be measured by using a non-intrusive excitation method. An
excitation with broadband sound signal was proven to be suﬃcient while extracting
natural frequencies and mode deﬂection shapes. But the lack of understanding about
the actual load input into the specimen and polluted measured responses encouraged
to look for a diﬀerent testing method. A measurement setup with electromagnetic
shaker was proposed and proven to be suﬃcient to obtain natural frequencies and
mode shapes. The shaker setup allowed to measure a load input and obtain a coher-
ence plots while also producing the response without pollution from non-mechanical
sources.
Both experimental setups were used for investigating the response variability due
to dis-assembly in each structure. The model with line contacts showed a smaller
natural frequency variability, thus suggesting that the smaller contacting area can be
preferable when designing contacts between parts. However, the variability of the
same line contact model was increased in higher frequencies and the reasons for that
are unknown. Thus giving motivation to further investigate the problem at hand.
The numerical models with bonded contacts showed to be inaccurate when trying
to predict a structural behavior of the structures showing that there is an interaction
between the parts that could include a non-linear behavior, such as separation or im-
pact. Thus it needs to be approached with a more advanced numerical analysis. The
eﬀorts to implement frictional contacts into the proposed model resulted in big dif-
ference between the measurement and numerical analysis results. Therefore, diﬀerent
contact formulations could not be evaluated. It was concluded that current geometry
fails to distribute an input load throughout the structure when using frictional con-
tact formulation. Therefore, diﬀerent structure geometry should be created in order
to evaluate the numerical models for non-linear contact problem.

Appendix B: Evaluation tests of FEM contact formulation
The formulation of contacts numerical model can be tested in two ways: contact patch
test and Ladyzenskaja-Babuska-Brezzi (LBB) stability condition.
The contact patch test incorporates a numerical model of the elastic block forced
into a ﬁnite elastic foundation by means of a uniformly distributed force ﬁeld or a
constant pressure. Both bodies can have the same or diﬀerent material properties,
in either way the exact solution involves a constant pressure throughout the area of
contact. The type of connecting meshes does not matter and the test directly show
if the contact formulation technique is robust and working correctly. The cases of
failing and passing techniques are illustrated in Figure 8.1.
(a)
(b)
Figure 8.1: Comparison of the stress distribution on the contact patch test with
two blocks of the same material and the upper block loaded with constant down-
ward pressure: (a) Contact constraining technique fails to produce unilateral pressure
distribution; (b) The technique produces unilateral pressure distribution [40].
Failing algorithms (example in Figure 8.1a) may introduce solution errors at the
contacting surfaces, and these errors do not necessarily decrease with mesh reﬁnement.
Reader is invited to refer to the text in [19] for a more detailed patch test application.
Another way to validate a contact formulation technique is to check the LBB
stability condition. The condition is generally applied to mixed ﬁnite element meth-
ods, thus can be used to evaluate the discretization with non-conforming meshes and
contact ﬁnite elements. The LBB condition is used to asses the reliability and the
solution eﬀectiveness of the ﬁnite element discretization in terms of convergence. As-
sessment is based on the computation of multiple parameters related to the boundary
conditions, physical parameters of the system and the element size. These parameters
are checked in the LBB condition and if the criterion is fulﬁlled, the ﬁnite element
discretization is assumed to be reliable and eﬀective. Since this study is using a com-

Appendix D. FEM elements
Here is a short description of each ﬁnite element (FE) type used in the ﬁnite element
method simulations. The names of the FE are native for ANSYS software.
For modelling solid bodies the SOLID187 element were used throughout the study.
SOLID187 is a quadratic higher order 3D, 10-node element of tetrahedra geometry.
It is well suited to modeling irregular meshes produced by CAD systems.
The element consists of 10 nodes having three DoFs each which are translations in
x, y and z directions. It is capable of including plasticity, hyperelasticity, creep, stress
stiﬀening, large deﬂection, and large strain. Besides that it can be used for simulating
deformations of nearly incompressible elastoplastic materials, and fully incompressible
hyperelastic materials. The illustration of this element can be seen in Figure 8.2.
Figure 8.2: The geometry of the SOLID187 ﬁnite element. Each node is names by
a letter and circled numbers indicate faces [42].
The contacts between bodies are modelled by using CONTA174 element for con-
tact surface in pair with TARGE170 element which is used for a target surface.
The CONTA174 element is used to represent contact and sliding between 3D
target surfaces and a deformable surface deﬁned by this element.
The element is
located on the surfaces of 3D solid or shell elements.
CONTA174 has the same geometric characteristics as the solid or shell element
face it is connected to. The illustration of this contact element can be seen in Figure
8.3. Contact occurs when the element surface penetrates an associated target surface.
Coulomb friction or shear stress friction can be allowed for this FE.
71
==PAGE:85==
Figure 8.3:
The geometry examples of the CONTA174 contact element.
Final
geometry is deﬁned by the solid element to which the CONTA174 is attached. R
is axis for isotropic friction, x0 axis is parallel to global X axis and x is axis is an
example of custom deﬁned axis direction [42].
TARGE170 can represent various 3D target surfaces for the associated contact
elements (in this study CONTA174). In general, the contact elements overlay the
solid, shell, or line elements describing the boundary of a solid deformable body so
geometry of this element is deﬁned by geometry of the overlaid FE. An illustration in
Figure 8.4 show example of pairing between TARGE170 and CONTA174.
Figure 8.4: The geometry example of the TARGE170 contact element. The element
is paired with TARGE170 contact element [42].

Appendix H: Fast Fourier Transform function
The MATLAB script provided below uses time series of structural response to cal-
culate frequency response. The algorithm uses MATLAB’s built in function ﬀt that
calculates and conditions the frequency spectrum. The calculation requires the time
series to be uniformly sampled, i.e. the time increment ∆t should be the same through-
out the transient response. Since the transient response is made by numerical analysis,
no ﬁlters are applied to the time series before FFT.
The script below is based on the routine written by Jon J. Thomsen.
%
−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−
function FFTdB test
% Function for computing the frequency
% spectrum (=FFT) of time response. The function fft dB (defined below
this
% test program) uses MATLAB's built−in function fft to calculate and
% condition the spectrum into a form typically used in vibration
analysis.
%
−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−
clear all;
close all;
disp('−−−>');
% load data file. It shoudl contain two columns: time & response values
load('data.mat');
t = data(:,1); % time
r = data(:,2); % response
f0 = 1700; % starting frequency [Hz]
f1 = 5000; % target frequency [Hz]
T = 0.1173; % Total sampling time
N = size(r,1); % length of time data series
% Plot time series
subplot(2,1,1);
tPlot = 0.1*T;
nPlot = round(tPlot/T*N); % Define part of the time series to plot
dt = T / (N−1); % Sampling interval
fs = 1/dt; % Sampling frequency
fNyq = 1/2*(N−1)/ T; % Nyquist frequency
disp(['Nyquest frequency: ' num2str(fNyq)]);
plot(t(1:nPlot),r(1:nPlot)); axis([tstart tPlot −3 3]);
xlabel('Time {\itt} [s]'); ylabel('{\itx}({\itt})');
title('Part of time series');
% Calcultate FFT
dBmax = 100; % Scales output to max at this value

[xFFTdB, fFFT, nFFT, fNyq] = fft dB(r,N,T,dBmax); % Defined below
% Plot FFT
subplot(2,1,2);
plot(fFFT,xFFTdB(1:nFFT),'−b');
axis([f0 f1 0 1.1*dBmax]);
xlabel('Frequency [Hz]'); ylabel('FFT({\itx}) [dB]');
title('Frequency spectrum of time series');
disp('<−−−');
%
−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−
function [xFFTdB, fFFT, nFFT, fNyq] = fft dB(x,N,T,dBmax);
% Compute the FFT of a uniformly sampled time series, in the form
typically
% used for vibration analysis.
%
% INPUT
%
x: Vector containing the uniformly sampled time series to be FFT
'ed
%
N: Length of x
%
T: Total sampling time
%
dBmax: Max dB−value wanted for the FFT
%
% OUTPUT
%
% xFFTdB: Vector containing the FFT of x, given as magnitude (i.e. | FFT(
x)|),
%
and scaled to [..;100dB]
%
fFFT: Vector of frequencies corresponding to the elements in xFFTdb
%
nFFT: Number of elements in xFFTdb and fFFT
%
fNyq: Nyquist frequency (=largest value in fFFT)
%
% JJT / 12.3.2004
%
−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−−
%
dt = T / (N−1); % Sampling interval
fs = 1/dt; % Sampling frequency
fNyq = 1/2*fs; % Nyquist frequency
df = 1/T; % Frequency resolution
nFFT = N/2 + 1; % (useful) Length of the FFT (the remaining N/2−1 points
are not used)
xFFT = fft(x); % Use MATLAB's function fft to Fourier transform the time
series
dBnorm = 100 −20*log10(max(abs(xFFT)));
% Constant needed for calculating dB
% Definition: [dB] = 20*log(SignalValue/SignalReferenceValue)
xFFTdB = 20*log10(abs(xFFT)) + dBnorm; % For displaying the FFT:
calculate | xFFT | in dB.
fFFT = 0 : df : (nFFT−1)*df; % Frequency lines corresponding to the FFT−
components
