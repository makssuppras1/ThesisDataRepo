Abstract
Addressing the serviceability limit state (SLS) requirements is a central concern in the
design process of timber structures. The lightweight nature of timber can result in un-
comfortable vibrations even in low to mid-rise structures, creating a need for detailed
analysis of the fundamental frequency. This parameter has significant influence on the
resulting dynamic response of a structure, along with perceived occupant comfort.
Currently, few buildings exist from which empirical equations predicting frequency can be
derived. For this reason, a numerical approach is taken to develop an analytical model
that can estimate the fundamental frequency of timber buildings, specifically with cross
laminated timber (CLT) core walls. The scope of the study includes the simulation of
buildings ranging from 6 to 24 storeys, and footprints from 375 m² to 1350 m².
A framework has been developed to facilitate a parametric study of 75,000 building reali-
sations. This includes a finite-element (FE) modal analysis to determine the fundamental
frequency. Some ultimate limit state (ULS) and SLS criteria are evaluated to confirm the
feasibility of the simulated building layouts.
Comparing the fundamental frequency results from this study with predictions made using
current analytical expressions proposed by various building codes, a tendency of existing
analytical models to overestimate the fundamental frequency is observed. This may lead
to unconservative estimations of the top floor accelerations leading to excessive peak ac-
celeration responses.
A regression analysis, utilising a nonlinear least squares optimisation, was performed to
create a straightforward expression able to predict the fundamental frequency of CLT core
buildings using basic mechanical and geometric variables.

Introduction
As urbanisation grows, the challenge of sustainable building development persists, with the
global construction sector alone accounting for 10% of the total greenhouse gas emissions
(UNEP, 2020). Engineers seek innovative solutions that balance climate responsibility
with urban growth. In recent decades, advancements within engineered wood products
and connection design have enabled the development of multi-storey timber structures,
making them a viable option for more sustainable construction. Notably, several pioneering
structures have embraced these advancements, reaching new heights. For instance, the
18-storey timber building Mjøstårnet was entirely constructed using timber structural
members (Abrahamsen, 2017) while the 18-storey Brock Commons building showcases a
mass-timber hybrid design, combining concrete and timber (Fast et al., 2016).
The increasing height of timber buildings presents new challenges. Highlighted by Abra-
hamsen et al. (2020), multi-storey timber structures often demonstrate suﬀicient strength
in the ultimate limit state (ULS), however, the challenge of meeting serviceability limit
state (SLS) requirements, such as deflection and vibration, often becomes the primary
concern. Compared to conventional materials, timber’s inherent lightness and reduced
stiffness can result in heightened sensitivity to vibrations. The fundamental frequency is
a significant parameter influencing the vibration behaviour, which is directly related to
the comfort level of the building (Hafeez et al., 2014).
The frequency is determined by the building’s stiffness and mass. Global mass and stiffness
of timber buildings differ from that of conventional concrete or steel buildings. This is
often influenced by a variety of factors, examples of which include connection stiffness and
geometrical properties (Zhang et al., 2021). Predicting the frequency during the design is
vital to determine the response to wind as well as seismic actions. Empirical forecasting
models are proposed by various international building regulations for the prediction of
fundamental frequency for use in early design (Edskär and Lidelöw, 2019). EN1991-1-4
(2007) presents an equation for multi-storey structures, based on data from 163 concrete,
steel and concrete-steel buildings developed by Ellis (1980) while the Canadian NBCC
(2020) presents an empirical formula based on measured fundamental periods of several
reinforced concrete buildings (Hafeez et al., 2014).
Current fundamental frequency forecast models rely on operational vibration measure-
ments, from structures built with conventional materials and structural typologies. It is
currently not fully understood how well these existing models predict frequency in modern
mass-timber structures, meaning caution should be exercised when using these analytical
equations for timber design (Vilguts et al. (2021), Edskär and Lidelöw (2019)).

Since multi-storey timber construction above six storeys is still relatively uncommon, lim-
ited vibration measurements exist, complicating the creation of an empirical equation to
predict the fundamental frequency. This motivates the use of numerical parametric tools
to develop analytical models utilising more inputs than just the height. This field of study
is currently in development, with Cao and Stamatopoulos (2021) conducting a parametric
study of the dynamic response of planar moment-resisting timber frames (MRTF) where
multiple parameters such as connection stiffness, loads, geometry etc. were investigated.
Furthermore, Vilguts et al. (2021) presents an expression for estimating the fundamental
frequency of MRTF by considering multiple geometry and stiffness parameters.
Given its strength and stiffness properties, cross-laminated timber (CLT) has experienced
growing adoption as a lateral force-resisting system (LFRS) (Ilgın, 2024). Consequently,
there is a compelling motivation to conduct a similar study for multi-storey CLT buildings,
exploring the fundamental frequency by considering relevant influential parameters.
1.1
Research questions
Ensuring that timber structures meet vibration requirements in SLS is a general issue
in the design process. As mass-timber construction is a relatively new method, a better
understanding of its dynamic behaviour is essential.
As such the lack of a generally accepted expression to predict the fundamental frequency
of multi-storey timber buildings motivates a parametric numerical study to develop an
analytical model.
This study aims to address the problem by investigating the following research questions:
• How accurate are existing prediction models in estimating the fundamental frequency
of multi-storey timber buildings with CLT cores as the main LFRS?
• How can the fundamental frequency of timber buildings be estimated accurately in
the early design phase?
• What are the most influential parameters affecting the frequency for buildings with
CLT cores?

Objectives
A parametric study of the fundamental frequency in multi-storey timber buildings will be

• Develop a framework enabling a parametric study of various feasible buildings with

Literature Review
2.1
State-of-the-art
Research within performance of timber structures in SLS (mainly deflections and vibra-
tions) has only gained traction within the last decade.
Use of CLT panels as slabs is
becoming more common, resulting in a large amount of research focused on vibration as-
pects of CLT slabs (Aloisio et al., 2023). Utilising timber in construction is recognised
as a significant step towards more sustainable buildings (Arehart et al., 2021). However,
significant design issues need to be solved before widespread adoption can be expected.
Building taller structures has also emerged as a potentially sustainable response to ur-
banisation and growing populations. There is however no defined term for when a timber
structure can be considered tall. Foster et al. (2016) discussed the terminology, acknowl-
edging that taller timber structures could not be considered tall had they been made from
concrete or steel, but proposed that lowering the bar for timber would underestimate the
potential. Even so, current trends seem to propose 12 storeys and above as a tall timber
structure. Taller timber structures exist, with most of these built in the form of concrete-
timber hybrids, taking advantage of the high lateral stiffness a concrete core can provide
(Ilgın, 2024).
For use in this project, the following terminology for use within modern timber structures
is established:
1. Multi-storey refers to all structures of more than 2 storeys
2. Tall timber buildings refers to buildings of more than 12 storeys.
Abrahamsen et al. (2020) established that multi-storey timber buildings (MSTBs) can be
designed to have suﬀicient strength to resist lateral loads such as wind and earthquakes
for ULS. Thus, the deflection and vibration aspects govern design choices. The along-wind
vibrations at the top floor is typically, in the early design stage, calculated using methods
based on the gust factor approach developed by Davenport (1961). The approach is used
in a variety of forms in many international building codes such as the Eurocode and
ASCE7, with quite varying results for timber structures (Landel et al., 2021).
These
approaches do not consider the wind loads as a time-varying load, but rather uses a
statistical representation of the wind load in the form of a power spectral density (Reynolds
et al., 2011). This means wind is described based on its frequency content, and the load (or
energy) applied to the structure is dependant on its natural frequency. Taller structures
typically exhibit bending vibration behaviour in the along wind direction. Here, responses

Theory
This section presents the relevant theories required to answer the research questions.
Specifically, it explains the use of mass-timber in modern structural engineering and
presents the calculation methods for stability verification of CLT panels. The basics of
modal analysis, which can be directly applied to timber structures, are also included. Ad-
ditionally, this section provides a brief overview of the least squares optimisation routine,
with the aim of using this procedure for model optimisation with a large dataset.
3.1
Timber as a structural material
Timber is a naturally grown material, which has been used for structural purposes through
centuries. It is characterised by its sustainable advantages, high strength-to-weight ratio
and aesthetic appeal. The strength properties of timber are influenced by the loads that
the tree experiences during its growth. Timber exhibits anisotropic behaviour, with greater
strength and stiffness parallel to the grain direction compared to perpendicular to it (Harte,
2009). Due to its exposure to wind loads during growth, wood demonstrates high elastic
strength under instantaneous loads.
Various technologies are commonly used to enhance the strength and stiffness of structural
timber products (Karol et al., 2018). A common type of engineered wood products is glued
laminated timber, also known as glulam. Glulam is manufactured by gluing individual
lamellae, with a typical thickness of 33mm and 45mm. By cutting the larger dimensions
of wood into thinner lamellae, knots and other irregularities are removed, enhancing the
strength and stiffness properties. Examples of glulam elements are shown in Figure 3.1.
In MSTBs glulam is often used as columns and beams due to their compressive and

(2013)

Cross laminated timber (CLT)
CLT is an engineered wood product manufactured by gluing longitudinal and transverse
lamellae, with layers oriented perpendicularly each other. By laminating the layers in
perpendicular directions, these elements can function as shell elements, including slabs
and shear walls.
The CLT panels are often used in modular construction, where the
panels are assembled on-site by use of mechanical fasteners.

Although the CLT element itself is very stiff in its own plane, connections between the
elements are typically a point of weakness.
The overturning moment and sliding can

the requirements in both ULS and SLS. The strength of the CLT panels, are according to

Load distribution of CLT systems
The stiffness of the slab has a large influence on the load distribution between the shear
walls. Since the CLT slabs are neither stiff nor flexible Petersen (2020), the load distribu-
tion can be more complex to determine compared to concrete structures. The distribution
of the horizontal loads depends on the relationship between shear wall stiffness and the
slab stiffness (Petersen, 2020, section 4). If the slab is suﬀiciently rigid, the load distri-
bution will be determined based on the stiffness of the shear walls. However, if the slab
is considered as flexible, the load will be distributed based on the load area attributed to
each shear wall.
In the context of multi-storey buildings using shear walls, the walls accumulate lateral
loads from the floors above, whereas the slabs are only exposed to the lateral load from
a single floor. Consequently, the CLT slabs can be considered as significantly stiffer than
the CLT shear walls, due to the much higher load the shear walls experience. Therefore,
the load distribution can be determined based on the relative stiffness of the shear walls,
as outlined in Petersen (2020) section 4. Effectively this means that the slab is considered
as a rigid diaphragm in the analytical calculations.

3.3
Response to dynamic loads
Timber is by nature a lightweight material and the combination of building to a greater
height, low mass, presence of connections and relatively low stiffness, can lead to lower
natural frequencies compared to similar concrete structures. For some lateral loads, like
seismic events, lower natural frequencies (and in turn higher periods) may lead to less
inertial forces being applied to the structure. Ground motions often exert the most energy
in the period range of 0-1 seconds, and increasing the natural period of a structure to
avoid the high energy levels may be a benefit of using CLT structural systems.
As northern Europe is not a seismically active region, wind loads are typically the lead-
ing lateral load. Wind loads with low wind pressure has higher spectral density for low
frequencies (Figure 3.9) resulting in structures with lower frequencies being more prone
to excitations from wind. This especially affects timber buildings compared to concrete
and steel buildings. Predicting the dynamic response of a timber building is therefore
crucial during the design phase. However, this task can be challenging due to the limited
knowledge of non-linear behaviour and the diﬀiculty of modelling damping (Aloisio et al.,
2023).

4.3.1
Modelling CLT shear walls
As the study aims to investigate full timber structures, the FE model from the previous
sections is developed further by modifying the lateral resisting system to represent CLT
shear walls using the extensive CLT library in FEM-Design. Glulam columns providing
vertical load transfer are set as pinned meaning the core is the main lateral resisting
system. The core walls are stacked directly on top of each other and connected. Slabs are
attached to the side of the core wall using pinned connections. This is due to high vertical
loads causing issues perpendicular to the grain had the slab been incorporated into the
core.
Unlike concrete cores, which can be continuously cast, multi-storey CLT walls cannot
be considered monolithic due to the nature of the mechanical fasteners used to assemble
the elements.
A model including the stiffness of the connections will thus result in a
lower overall stiffness compared to a model with monolithic elements. A common way to
account for this loss in stiffness of a multi-storey CLT shear wall is to modify the material
parameters by reducing Young’s modulus for the CLT element, and then use a continuous
element from the ground floor to the top of the building. Connolly et al. (2018) proposed
a reduction of 50% of the stiffness properties for a continuous CLT shell element, however
this approach does not reflect the actual slip occurring in the connections.
To that extent, developing an FE model that accurately reflects the behaviour of the of
CLT shear walls is deemed necessary. The connections can effectively be modelled as a
spring placed in a single point between the connected elements, representing the actual
connection group including fasteners. It is then possible to pre-define stiffness values in
all three dimensions (StruSoft, 2010) corresponding to calculated stiffness values of the

5
Method
This chapter outlines the methodology of the study. The research strategy to investigate
the research questions is as follows:
1. Define potential input parameters based on literature review and set up a parametric
model to investigate their sensitivity.
2. From the sensitivity study and practical reasons, define an input library and develop
an algorithm that allows for simulations with all combinations of input.
3. Screen the generated models (using methods presented in section 3.1.2) to ensure
they meet select ULS and SLS requirements and run modal analysis.
4. Transform the saved data for use in regression analysis and compare frequency pre-
dictors from codes and studies to establish a basis for developing an analytical model.
5. Develop an analytical model based on the simulation results using non-linear least
squares.
5.1
Definition of Input Parameters
As described in section 3.2, the natural frequency of a structure depends on the mass
and stiffness matrices of the structure. A suitable library of parameters influencing these
matrices must be constructed, and realistic ranges within which to vary these parameters
must be established. The two main approaches for determining significant input variables
are through literature and sensitivity testing.
To test the significance of each parameter, three basic FE models are constructed with
Grasshopper and the FEM-Design API. All three models are timber structures featuring a
CLT core and slab, glulam beams and columns with pinned supports. This configuration
implies that the lateral resistance is solely provided by the core. The three models (Figure
5.1) are created at either end of the input space, to ensure a parameter does not only
affect a specific type, size or geometry. Each parameter is investigated by locking all other
input parameters and incrementally increasing or decreasing the numerical value, with 1
simulation being run for each increment.
5.1.1
Sensitivity evaluation of input parameters
Parameters that may have an impact on the fundamental frequency are identified based
on the literature review and the design decisions engineers face during the early design
phase. These parameters are tested in the base models, and both the parameters and the
corresponding results will be presented in the following sections. Finally, the complete
library of inputs will be presented in tabulated form.

Investigation of hold-down connectors as an input parameter

connectors are often a governing criteria in ULS, especially when the number of storeys

frequency, the influence of the hold-down stiffness is investigated.

Mass
It is common knowledge that mass has an influence on the fundamental frequency (Cao

A strategic distribution of mass can also result in reduced accelerations (Cao and Stam-

of additional loads placed on selected floors is analysed.

in smaller foundations due to less vertical dead loads. It is currently not fully understood
to what degree this difference affects stiffness.

timber buildings. This encompasses a complete and autonomous program able to generate
thousands of model combinations based on a predefined library of inputs, while correctly
modelling connections, loads, boundary conditions etc. and running modal analysis. The

5.3.1
Framework description
The Grasshopper script works by using components included in the software package to
create 3D geometry. Ground floor geometry is created first using a planar surface created
with dimensions based on footprint input. The surface is meshed with a grid sizing of five

at each mesh intersection as line elements with length equal to the storey height.
The core is also placed using the grid mesh created on the ground floor surface. Core
walls are created based on pre-defined core sizes from the input library, with core position
being randomised within the building footprint. The wall elements are created as surfaces
extruded from the lines making up the perimeter of the core. Columns cannot be placed
within the core. Wall and columns are then converted to timber panel shell elements and
column bar elements respectively using the FEM-Design API.

same way as in section 4.2.2.

5.3.2
Brute force iteration
The focus of the project is not on optimisation. For the problem at hand, a brute force
algorithm (BFA) offer a solution to create a large simulation space by combining all pos-
sible iterations from the input library. The BFA is a straightforward, versatile and easily
implementable solution to creating a large number of input combinations.
Despite some drawbacks (such as very large solution spaces and slow runtime) the BFA
is deemed a viable approach to the problem at hand. This is because regression analysis
requires a substantial amount of data, preferably randomised, to accurately capture the
structural behaviour of a wide variety of differently designed buildings. Note that we are
not necessarily seeking the best solution, but rather a comprehensive understanding of the
structural behaviour and patterns.

5.4
Data processing
As is evident in Table 5.2, some values increase with height due to the higher wind loads
experienced in ULS for greater building heights. This results in higher connection capacity
and stiffness and more shear walls for taller buildings. Additionally, the footprint of the
building can increase with the height.
For this reason, it is necessary to normalise the inputs to get a common scale, as taller
buildings will generally have lower frequencies even with higher connection stiffness. The
aim of this project is to create an intuitive expression to predict the frequency based on
readily available inputs. This rules out normalisation by standard score or z-score, as the
intuitive physical meaning of a given property is lost in the transformation.
A way to circumvent this issue is by normalising to a common size variable. The total
height of the structure is straightforward to scale to, creating a common ratio for param-
eters that are varied over the height of the structure. This way, it is easy for the end user
to scale common engineering values for use in the model developed during this project.
The independent variables transformed by normalisation with regards to the height is:
1. Connection stiffness at ground floor, kser →kser/h
2. Shear wall length in vibration direction Lshear →Lshear/h
Additionally, some building models generated by the framework do not exhibit bending
behaviour in the first mode. This is the expected behaviour, and often checking the mode
shapes to find orthogonal bending in each of the first two modes with the third being
torsional, is a check to see if the model is behaving correctly.
Since the framework does not currently support saving the mode shapes, checking the
direction of modal mass participation (described in 3.2) serves as an alternative method
for assessing the modal behaviour of the models.
For each run, the modal mass participation along each of the principal axes is saved as
output. This enables the filtration of results showing vibration along the Z-axis, as this
is not a typical outcome. Additionally, it is desirable to observe a large part of the mass
vibrating along one principal axis, allowing for minor torsional effects.

8
Discussion
A model to predict vibration properties in the early stages of design has been proposed,
useful for the design of MSTBs. The model has direct practical use for designers. Possible
shortcomings and limitations along with validity of the results will be discussed in this
section.
8.1
Reliability of numerical results
A model based on results from FE analysis results depends greatly on the quality of the
numerical results. This study is centred on dynamic properties of timber buildings, an
area with limited empirical data. To evaluate the credibility of the numerical results, we
will draw upon findings from existing structures and papers.
Firstly, all simulations are run within the linear-elastic range. This means that any kind of
non-linear behaviour the structure can exhibit, for example in connections or by excessive
loading events, is not included in this study.
Figure 8.1: Numerical frequency calculations for a large number of tube-in-tube systems,

from 52.5 to 147 meters, for varying building widths. nfloors from 15-27 results in building
heights comparable to our study.
Various authors have studied frequency of full timber buildings, and found similar tenden-
cies with predicted frequency from EN1991-1-4 (2007) too high. Frequency calculations

(2022) investigates a different lateral resisting system. This means that the comparison
is not entirely 1:1, but provides an indication based on material. Our study encompasses
lower buildings than those included in Figure 8.1, meaning we see higher frequencies. Nev-

8.2
Limitations and assumptions
The proposed model relies on several assumptions leading to some limitations, which will
be discussed in this section.
Verification of structural elements
A screening system is implemented to automatically check some critical parameters for
tall CLT structures, namely connection capacities and building deflections.
The remaining part of the generated models are not verified fully. The vertical structural
system is consciously chosen with large elements to be conservative, however it is noted
that the remaining building is not verified.
Input parameter population
The parameters tested in this work defines the proposed regression model. Using geometric
or mechanical parameters significantly different than those presented in Table 5.2 will
reduce the accuracy of the proposed model. Only two CLT wall thicknesses were used due
to computational cost, and choosing a thickness other than 240mm or 320mm will lead to
larger deviation from the numerical results. Moreover, changes in geometry inputs, like a
footprint area, falling outside these ranges will also lead to greater discrepancies compared
to those shown in Table 7.3.
Additionally, only two different load levels (representing the live load) are included in the
parametric study. Further populating the input library with additional dead loads could
enable a better use of the mass as a measure to control the frequency.
To apply the model to a broader range of buildings, further work should generally explore
a larger library of input values, such as additional wall thicknesses and loads.
Applicability to different structural systems
The proposed model is applicable only to timber buildings with CLT shear walls.
It
is essential to recognise that structures using truss systems or moment-resisting frames,
have their own distinct parameters (such as axial or rotational stiffness in connections)
that significantly influence the fundamental frequency (Vilguts et al., 2021). This is not
Parametric Study of the Fundamental Frequency in Multi-Storey Timber Buildings
73
==PAGE:83==
accounted for in the proposed model. Parametric analyses should be carried out on these
systems as well to develop system specific models.
Additional shear walls
When designing a MSTB, engineers may consider incorporating additional shear walls
alongside the CLT core to add stiffness.
The proposed model considers the length of
shear walls in the vibration direction. However, since only buildings with a central core
were simulated, the regression model has not been exposed to results from models with
additional shear walls. More research involving the inclusion of extra shear walls, especially
for the tallest structures, is necessary.
Openings and construction methods
The inclusion of an opening in the core accounts for doors and HVAC access. In this
project, these openings are modelled as a single 3-meter-wide void in the longest side of
the core. In practical construction, the openings would constitute smaller holes incor-
porated into the CLT panels before transportation to the site. Panels with integrated
openings would exhibit greater stiffness than two separate panels, as modelled in this
project, consequently increasing the global stiffness. Additionally, the framework is not
configured to randomly place openings in the core. A single large opening in one wall is a
straightforward way to represent the loss of stiffness due to all openings.
The shear panels span horizontally using a platform technique (section 3.1.1). As a result,
floor-to-floor height is limited by the width of the elements. As CLT panel manufacturing
evolves, the possibility of creating wider panels emerges. Further research could explore
how different construction methods such as the balloon technique impacts the fundamental
frequency.
Vertical corner connections
The vertical corner connections between shear walls (Figure 4.10) impact the interaction
between the core walls and thereby the lateral stiffness. For the modal analysis, common
connections from manufacturers were used ensuring only partial interaction between core
walls. Investigating the influence of vertical connections in core walls would be beneficial
for the modal analysis results as this could be a design parameter for engineers.
Vibration direction
The proposed model in Equation 6.1 requires the engineer to input the summed length of
the shear walls in the vibration direction. The number describes a total length of shear
wall, meaning a 10x10 meter core will have 20 meters of shear wall in each direction.
The proposed equation requires the engineer to have prior knowledge of structural dy-
namics, as a conscious decision about assumed vibration direction must be taken. This
74
Parametric Study of the Fundamental Frequency in Multi-Storey Timber Buildings
==PAGE:84==
is assumed to be possible for users with relevant experience, as core structures will typi-
cally vibrate along the axis with the shortest dimension of the core (depended on opening
geometries). A way to improve the equation for the end user is to take this into account
directly in the model.
Moisture content and stiffness
Another factor not accounted for in this project is changes in stiffness due to the moisture
content. Moisture requirements for structural timber is defined in (EN1995-1-1, 2007, ch.
5), meaning structures are defined based on these predefined requirements. The kmod and
kdef factors account for changes in material properties and load duration for ULS capacity
and SLS deflections, however typically modal analysis is carried out using mean values..
This is also the method used here.
Larsson et al. (2022) highlighted seasonal changes of ±0.2 Hz for a 4 storey mass-timber
concrete hybrid structure in Sweden. This is attributed to the moisture content of the
wood. This means AVT results have slight dependence on the season. Some authors have
also observed very slight increases of natural frequency in structures having completed the
initial dry out process of the timber, again not accounted for in this project.
8.3
Mode shapes
The mode shapes of the complete dataset was not able to be captured in the duration of
this project. Although FEM-Design API is capable of extracting mode shapes, the 3D
models with meshed shell elements create large point clouds, often with more than 50,000
nodes. Saving this data is computationally very heavy, making it unpractical for creating
a large dataset.
At present, the framework only supports use of mass participation to describe vibration
direction. More detailed data on the mode shape would be advantageous, as the slid-
ing occurring in shear connections is not captured by standard mode shape calculations.
EN1991-1-4 (2007) suggest using ϕ(z) = (z/H)ζ for fixed cantilevers to describe the vi-
bration shape, with ζ depending on the type of structure and z being the height.
The difference in vibration behaviour is highlighted in Figure 8.4, with the slip in the
joints found in 8.4a not captured by the code in 8.4b.
This agrees with the findings
of Edskär and Lidelöw (2019) where mode shapes of CLT and post-and-beam buildings
did not correspond well with EN1991-1-4 (2007) equations. Further development of the
framework could lead to the extraction of a simplified mode shape by retrieving a small
number of nodes per floor. This is a common way to simplify a large system (done by
multiple authors, for example Miyazu and Loss (2024)). Inclusion of data like this could
lead to creation of simplified analytical equations able to predict mode shapes, similar to
Eurocode 1 approaches.

9
Conclusion
This study presents a framework for conducting a parametric analysis of multi-storey tim-
ber buildings. The framework includes FE modal analysis to determine the fundamental
frequency.
To address the three central research questions (listed below), a numerical
approach was used to explore various parameters related to CLT core structures.
• How accurate are existing prediction models in estimating the fundamental frequency
of multi-storey timber buildings with CLT cores as the main LFRS?
• How can the fundamental frequency of timber buildings be estimated accurately in
the early design phase?
• What are the most influential parameters affecting the frequency for buildings with
CLT cores?
Through simulations of 21,752 feasible MSTBs with CLT cores, a consistent trend was
observed; existing prediction models tend to overestimate the fundamental frequency. Our
findings align with those of other researchers who conducted parametric studies on MSTBs
of different structural typologies.
Notably, none of the existing predictors accurately
captured the frequency of timber buildings with CLT cores, investigated in this project.
It was found that several parameters had an influence on the fundamental frequency, and
using the building height as the sole predictor was not accurate. Through the use of non-
linear regression optimisation, we found it possible to estimate the fundamental frequency
of a MSTB with a CLT core using a simple analytical equation with the following seven
inputs; number of storeys, floor height, CLT wall thickness, shear connection stiffness,
length of shear walls in vibration direction, live load and footprint.
The study explored the impact of the listed parameters, revealing a strong negative cor-
relation between the number of storeys and the fundamental frequency. In contrast, the
stiffness of shear connections and various geometry parameters of shear walls had a sig-
nificant positive effect. The proposed model shows high performance as demonstrated by
an R2 value of 0.97, RMSE of 0.046 and MAE of 0.032.
Existing forecasting models proposed by various building regulations are only valid for
conventional building types. This research demonstrates that these models are not ac-
curate for MSTBs with CLT core layouts. Consequently, a simple analytical equation is
presented allowing engineers to predict natural frequencies in early design stages.

9.1
Further work
This section presents proposals for further work, both to increase performance of the model
and developing the framework within the scope of SLS performance of timber structures:
1. Further population of the input library. Due to limitations in computational time,
only a limited population of input was included. Extending the input library will en-
compass more possible combinations of buildings for the regression model, increasing
accuracy.
2. Inclusion of additional stiffness parameters in the numerical model will lead to more
realistic results reflecting real-world buildings. These could include stiffness from
partition walls and semi-rigid column connections.
3. Investigating the addition of shear walls beyond just a core, will lead to a more
varied population of timber buildings with CLT shear walls. Often, adding extra
shear walls is beneficial for resisting lateral loads, and is a choice often taken by
engineers.
4. Including vertical span direction of CLT walls and balloon type construction will be
beneficial to investigate this change in stiffness.
5. Modify the framework to be compatible with other lateral resisting systems of timber.
This could lead to a more general expression for timber buildings, or different models
for each system.
6. Several timber buildings utilises concrete cores as LFRS. Investigating the accuracy
of existing forecasting models for such types buildings and potentially propose a
model could be beneficial.
7. Expanding the presented framework to include response analysis of lateral deflection.
Proposing models that predict inter-story drift and top-story drift would be a useful
tool assisting engineers in SLS design.
8. Investigating incorporation of added mass could substantially enhance the usefulness
of the model. The ability to make strategic decisions about adding extra mass could
be particularly beneficial during peak acceleration analysis.
