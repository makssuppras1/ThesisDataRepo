Abstract
Vertigo is a symptom of dysfunction in the central or peripheral vestibular system. Di­
agnosis of vertigo and its root cause is essential for prevention of further complications.
Accurate detection and differentiation between different types of root causes is difficult
with conventional methods like performing clinical maneuvers or measuring velocity, am­
plitude, duration, direction and eye displacement patterns because the sharp eye move­
ments, known as nystagmus, is not perfectly sinusoidal in real world and contains a lot of
noise, nonlinearity and is subjective to clinical interpretation, which is often inconsistent.
This study introduces a framework that analyzes suitable deep learning models for accu­
rate gaze tracking and custom trains models such as Transformer and CNN models for
automated detection of vertigo and its root cause diagnosis. Synthetic datasets of nys­
tagmus episodes representing Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV), Ménière’s
Disease (MD), and Vestibular Migraine (VM) were created to overcome the scarcity of pub­
licly available clinical data. The proposed system is able to process raw video recordings
collected from an IoT­based headset provided by the EyeKnow company to the Region
Zealand Hospital, converts them into temporal waveforms, and classifies them using the
deep learning­based diagnostic model.
Comparative analysis between the Transformer model and CNN model demonstrates
that the CNN­based model performs slightly better than the Transformer based model.
SHAP analysis was employed to interpret model predictions, where the CNN based model
showed distributed feature attribution and the transformer based model showed strong fo­
cus on key features. Moreover, an architecture for how to use this system in a pipeline
was also proposed.

Introduction
1.1
What is vertigo?
Vertigo is a clinical symptom that is characterized by a false sense of movement. It is often
described as dizziness or feeling that the surroundings are spinning, according to [1]. This
symptom can arise due to dysfunction in either the peripheral (inner ear and vestibular
nerve) or central vestibular systems (brain and cerebellum). Vertigo is a common reason
for emergency department visits, and the causal disease is also frequently misdiagnosed.
Epidemiological studies indicate that the prevalence of vertigo increases with age and is
two to three times more common in women than in men [2]. Approximately 15­20% of the
people in the world experience dizziness, and 5% of these cases are attributed to vertigo
attacks [2].
Figure 1.1: A man having a vertigo attack [1]
1.2
Challenges in diagnosis
There are a few pitfalls in identifying whether the dizziness in the patient is caused by ver­
tigo or other underlying conditions. These include overreliance on dizziness as a symptom
for diagnosis, underuse of timing­based differentiators such as the amount of time the at­
tack occurred, underuse of hallmark eye exam observations, overreliance on head CT to
eliminate the possibility of neurological causes, etc. [3]. Apart from this, there are sev­
eral other conditions that commonly cause dizziness; vascular diseases such as stroke
(non­vestibular) or transient ischemic attack (TIA) commonly present acute dizziness [3].
1.3
Quality of life impact and clinical motivation
Apart from the discomfort caused by dizziness, vertigo can have a profound impact on
one’s quality of life. It can lead to loss of balance, feelings of anxiety , avoidance of move­
ment, and reduced mobility. Among the elderly, vestibular dysfunction is closely linked
with higher incidences of falls, which are a major cause for injury and loss of indepen­
dence. A recent study found that 61% of individuals who experienced a fall had under­
lying vestibular dysfunction [4], and older adults have a higher probability of recurrent
falls compared to those without dizziness [5]. Therefore early and accurate detection of
root cause and clinical treatment are essential for improving patient safety and long­term
outcome.

Background
To assess whether a patient is experiencing vertigo based on gaze behavior, it is es­
sential to first track eye movements accurately and then interpret the resulting patterns.
Achieving this requires a foundational understanding of ocular anatomy and the neuro­
physiological mechanisms underlying different types of vertigo. This section introduces
the key anatomical and functional concepts that support the identification and classifica­
tion of vertigo episodes based on eye movement patterns.
2.1
Eye anatomy
Figure 2.1: Eye Anatomy [6]
The surface of the eye is made of tear film, which comprises three layers: a mucous
layer called the conjunctiva, a watery layer produced by the lacrimal gland, and an oily
layer produced by the meibomian gland, as shown in 2.1 part I. 2.1 part II shows light is
focused into the eye through the cornea, the iris is located behind the anterior chamber,
and the pupil is located in the middle of the iris. It widens or contracts the pupil to control
the amount of light entering the eye. The lens sits behind the pupil and is responsible for
focusing light towards the back of the eye. The light passes through the vitreous humor
(jelly­like space) and is projected onto the retina (light­sensitive tissue). It has special
cells called photoreceptors (rods and cones), which are responsible for sending electrical
signals through the optic nerve to the brain, which is responsible for our sight. A very
specialized area of the retina called the macula is responsible for giving us our detailed,
central vision. The macula is a broader, oval­shaped area near the center of the retina
responsible for central, high­resolution, color vision. Within it lies the fovea, a small pit
that provides the sharpest vision, as shown in 2.1 part III. [6]

2.2
Peripheral Vestibular system
Figure 2.2: Peripheral Vestibular System [7]
The peripheral vestibular system is important for balance and spatial orientation. This
system is located in the inner ear and is responsible for detecting angular and linear ac­
celeration if the head. This system is also responsible for transmitting signals to the brain
to maintain control of the posture, co­ordinate eye movements, and stabilize gaze dur­
ing motion [8] [9]. The system consists of two main components: semi­circular canals
and otolithic organs (utricle and saccule). These components are embedded in the bony
labyrinth of the temporal bone and filled with endolymph, which is a special type of fluid
that is responsible for sensory transduction. The bony labyrinth hair cells that convert
mechanical movements into electrical signals.
Semi­circular Canals: There are three canals: superior, posterior, and horizontal, and
they are located orthogonally to each other as shown in 2.2, which allows detection of
angular acceleration in all three spatial planes. Each canal consists of a region called
ampulla, which is expanded; this contains sensory hair cells embedded in a gelatinous
structure called cupula. When the endolymph moves within the canal, this deflects the
cupula; this causes bending of the hair cells and alters their firing rate [7].
Otolith Organs: The utricle and saccule detect the linear acceleration and head tilt rel­
ative to gravity. Their maculae have hair cells topped with calcium carbonate crystals
called otoconia; when the head moves, the otoconia shift, which bends the hair cells and
triggers the vestibular nerve signals [7].

2.3
Central Vestibular system
Figure 2.3: Central Vestibular System [10]
The central vestibular system integrates the signal received from the peripheral vestibular
system. It also plays a key role in maintaining balance, posture, and vision. The central
vestibular system interprets the information received from the peripheral vestibular system
and generates an appropriate motor response. It is composed of the vestibular nuclei
complex in the brainstem and the cerebellum. These components receive signals from
the vestibular nerve and project them onto the different regions of the brain to regulate
eye movements, posture, and spatial orientation. The picture 2.3 represents the central
vestibular system.
Vestibular Nuclei:Four major nuclei are present on each side of the brainstem which are
superior,medial,lateral, and inferior vestibular nuceli. They are located in rostral medulla
and caudal pons. They serve as the initial relay station of the vestibular information. They
integrate signals from the inner ear, visual system, and proprioceptors and then send the
output to oculomotor nuclei, the spinal cord, the cerebellum, and higher cortical centers.
[11].
Cerebellum: The Cerebellum and especially the flocculonodular lobe fine­tunes the vestibu­
lar signals, adjust VOR and help maintain balance and posture [11].
2.4
Eye movements
Accurate and coordinated eye movements are essential for clear and stable vision. They
allow us to fixate on objects of interest, rapidly shift gaze between targets, follow moving
stimuli, and stabilize the visual scene despite head or body motion. These movements

result from a complex integration of ocular muscles, neural control circuits, and sensory
feedback from vestibular and visual systems.
The eye movements can be broadly categorized into several types, each serving a distinct
functional role in vision and gaze control:
Fixation: It can be described as steady focus of the eye on an object for a longer time(0.2–
0.6 s). It makes use of the fovea, which is responsible for pinpoint high­resolution vision.
The eye perceives and processes new information in this phase[12].
Saccades:These are rapid movements (20­100 ms) of the eye to shift focus from one
fixation point to another [12]
Smooth pursuits:They are also proper eye movements like saccades; however, they are
continuous movements used to track a moving object. [12]
Vestibulo Ocular Reflex (VOR): It is an involuntary reflex that stabilizes the gaze during
head movement by moving the eye in the opposite direction to the head movement and
is equal in magnitude. It is used to stabilize the visual scene on the retina even when the
head moves. This reflex works even when the eye is closed or in complete darkness as
well. Damage to this reflex can cause nystagmus [13].
Vergence: It is a nonconjugate eye movement that is used to track or merge objects
located at different distances. The aim is to achieve binocular vision [14].
Optokinetic Response:
It is a reflexive movement that is used to stabilize the retina
when the entire visual field moves across it; for example, when viewing out of a window
of a moving train, the eye follows the motion and then resets back, which creates a jerk
nystagmus­like pattern [15].
2.5
What is Nystagmus?
Nystagmus is an involuntary rhythmic eye movement. It can be from side to side, up and
down or circular. It is a common symptom of vertigo attack. It affects both eyes and may
last for few seconds . there are generally two types of nystagmus Pendular nystagmus
causes eye to swing like a pendulum back and forth in both directions equally.
Jerk nystagmus is the most common type wherein the eye drifts slowly in one direction
and then jerks back to the other direction. Jerky nystagmus is most common in vertigo
patients and pendular nystagmus is usually caused due to brain diseases such as multiple
sclerosis or it can also be a congenital issue. [16]. Vertigo attacks causes blurry vision
in patients, meaning they cannot really see anything clearer than Oscillopsia which is a
feeling of the world around them spinning[16].

problems in the vestibular nerve present in the inner ear.
The Central vertigo is caused by the cerebellum and is usually caused by an injury to

3
Literature Review
3.1
State of the Art
Vertigo, a debilitating symptom that arises from vestibular dysfunction and requires a pre­
cise clinical evaluation. The current state of the art in diagnosing vertigo and its causes
is based upon structured clinical maneuvers, special vestibular laboratory tests, and the
usage of advanced eye­tracking instrumentation. This section reviews various diagnostic
techniques and highlights their strengths and weaknesses, which defines the need for
automated and data­driven methods considered later.
3.1.1
Clinical Maneuvers
Dix­Hallpike Maneuver
The Dix­Hallpike maneuver is a common test performed to detect vertigo caused by be­
nign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV) and has been a gold standard since 1952. It
is caused by calcium carbonate crystals floating (otolith crystals) in the inner ear canals,
which are semicircular tubes responsible for balance. The patient is told to follow the
following steps: [18]
1. The patient sits on the examination table with the head turned to one side at a 45◦
angle and legs stretched in the front and eyes wide open.
2. The healthcare worker aids the patient to lean back with one ear pointing towards
the ground, and they are asked to stay there for 1­2 minutes.
3. While in this position the patient’s eyes are checked for nystagmus.
If the test is positive, there is an 83% chance that the patient has vertigo caused by
BPPV. However, it correctly rules out BPPV only 52% of the time. Moreover, this test
is not safe to perform on patients with major vascular diseases, Down syndrome,
spinal cord injuries, morbid obesity or on pregnant women [19].
HINTS
The aim of the HINTS exam is to assess a patient presenting acute vestibular syndrome
(AVS), which is characterized by symptoms such as dizziness, vertigo, and nystagmus.
It is a crucial test often used in emergency departments for detecting whether the AVS
stems from peripheral or central causes; however, it fails to identify the exact cause within
the peripheral/central causes. It is an acronym for three tests.
• Head­Impulse Test (HI): This test cannot be performed on patients with neck pain
or spine injury. Below are the steps to perform this test.
1. Hold the patient’s head with both hands on either side and tilt the head slightly
forward.
2. Ask the patient to relax and direct them to keep their eyes on your nose at all
times.
3. Gently tilt their head to the left/right side and back to the center again. Repeat
this unpredictably.
4. Look for loss of visual focus on your nose and a corrective saccade; this indi­
cates a positive test and confirms the existence of peripheral vestibular dys­
function.

If the patient loses focus with quick head movements, it could indicate the VOR
(vestibulo­ocular reflex) is not intact and could indicate it is of peripheral origin; else,
if the patient is able to keep focus, it could be of central origin.
• Characterization of spontaneous Nystagmus(N)
1. Observe the patient’s eyes when at rest and identify if there is any nystagmus.
­if so, observe the direction of nystagmus
2. Ask them to look to the left side and then to the right and observe if there is a
presence of nystagmus.
If the nystagmus is bi­directional or vertical, it could be of central origin, but if it is
none or unidirectional, then it is of peripheral origin.
• Test of Skew
1. Ask the patient to focus their gaze on the practitioner’s nose.
2. Cover one eye of the patient with an object or hand for a few seconds and then
uncover the eye.
3. Observe whether the eye was able to stay focused or it drifted and realigned
itself once the eye was uncovered.
If any abnormal correction is observed,
it means it can be of central origin; otherwise, it is due to peripheral causes. This
test has 98% specificity

Video Head Impulse Test
In this test the clinicians move the patient’s head abruptly while the eyes are recorded via
video goggles to access the vestibulo­occular reflex of all semicircular canals. It detects
nystagmus if there is a dysfunction in the peripheral vestibular system [20].
Caloric Test
The ear canal is introduced with warm or cold air/water; this stimulates the horizontal
semicircular canal, which causes nystagmus. The hot water causes endolymph to rise,
and the nystagmus beats towards the stimulated ear. If the water is cold, it causes en­
dolymph to fall, and the nystagmus beats in the opposite direction of the stimulated ear
[21].
Electronystagmography/VideoNystagmography (ENG/VNG)
These are a battery of tests measuring eye movements created due to the stimuli (posi­
tional testing and caloric testing). The movements can be traced using small electrodes
on the skin around the eyes or using video goggles [22].
Rotational Chair Testing
The patient is seated on a motorized seat, and they are rotated at various speeds. The
associated eye movements are recorded and analyzed. This is particularly helpful if both
vestibular systems (horizontal and vertical) are impaired and the caloric test is inconclu­
sive [22].

3.1.4
Limitations and Gaps
While above methods describe the state of the art for vertigo detection and root cause
diagnosis there are several limitations associated with it:
• Many techniques described above required specialized training. The diagnosis de­
pends heavily on manual observations which introduce variability in observation and
diagnosis.
• The laboratory methods require specialized equipment and resources which limits
accessibility
• The commercial eye tracking hardware requires rigid calibration , degradation with
occlusions and lack of transparency of algorithmic diagnosis
• Most methods only detect whether the cause of vertigo is central or peripheral or
provide information such as velocity , amplitude and duration but do not detect the
precise causes. Due to the lack of availability and access to sufficient condition
based nystagmus data these models are trained for small datasets, thus they do
not generalize well.

Methods for Eye tracking
Scleral Search Coils

Infrared Oculography
Infrared oculography measures eye movements by detecting changes in the intensity of

in reflected intensity indicate the eye positions. It is sensitive to external light changes

Video Oculography
Video oculography, or video­based eye tracking, is one of the most popular and common
techniques used in commercial systems for eye tracking. One or more cameras are used
to capture images of the eyes, and gaze direction is later determined using glints reflected
on the pupil. There are two main categories [26]:
• Invasive (head mounted): Cameras are attached to wearable devices.
• Non­Invasive (remote): Cameras are placed externally; this is widely used in the

Following are various types of hardware setups used:
• Single Camera Systems: This uses a single camera and multiple light sources to
track pupil glint positions. They are simple but limited by field of view and image
resolution, e.g., Tobii, Pupil Labs, etc. [26] [12] [24].
• Multi­Camera Systems: This uses multiple cameras and light sources to capture
wide fields of view. They can compensate for head movements and use stereo
camera setups to compute 3D gaze positions, e.g., Zhu et al. (2006), Beymer and
Flickner (2003), and Ohno and Mukawa (2004) [26].
3.2.2
Methods for Gaze Estimation and tracking
.
Feature Based Gaze estimation
The feature­based gaze estimation methods identify key features such as pupil glints,
pupil center, and eye contours to determine where a person is looking. They are less
affected by lighting and viewpoint changes but perform poorly when eye features are
unclear.
There are two main types of feature based gaze estimation methods [26]:
• Model­based approach:
It uses a geometric eye model to determine 3D gaze direction. It requires camera
calibration and knowledge of the scene’s geometry. It reconstructs the optical and
visual axes of the eye, then finds gaze points by intersecting the visual axis with the
scene. The gaze direction is estimated by intersecting the visual axis with scene
geometry. Reconstruction of the optical axis is done using estimation of the cornea
and pupil center [26].
• Interpolation­based approach
This method maps image features such as pupil centers to gaze coordinates using
either parametric models such as polynomials or nonparametric models like neural

networks. The polynomial mappings use calibration data and regression to find
coefficients, where as the neural networks learn the mappings directly from the facial
feature co­ordinates to gaze points without modeling the eye’s geometry [26].
Appearance Based Gaze Estimation
Appearance­based gaze estimation techniques determine gaze direction directly from the
visual appearance of eyes in the images. This does not need any geometric calibration.
These techniques map image features to screen coordinates using approaches such as
morphable models, grayscale unit images, appearance manifolds, Gaussian interpolation,
and the cross­ratio method. These rely on image data rather than physical eye or camera
models. This makes them simpler and more flexible for gaze tracking [26].
3.2.3
Machine Learning Methods For Diagnosis
Classical Machine Learning Techniques
Initially classical machine learning techniques were explored for nystagmus detection.
The analysis of the suitability of the methods considered is described as follows:
• Support Vector Machines (SVM): SVMs try to find the best boundary (hyperplane)
to separate data points into different classes with the largest possible margin [27].
They can work on small/high­dimension data such as SPV, amplitude, axis domi­
nance, frequency, etc. However, features need to be extracted and prepared to be
used as input to the model.
• Random Forest (RF): It is an ensemble of decision trees [28]. The random forest
method has been widely used for classification and is less prone to overfitting. It
requires carefully prepared feature sets as well.
• Hidden Markov Model (HMM): HMM can model a nystagmus episode as a se­
quence of hidden physiological states that produce observable eye patterns over
time [29]. It can provide probabilistic state transitions, which can aid in differential
diagnosis.
Compared to deep learning methods, all these methods seem to have limited feature
representations, as relevant features need to be extracted and given as an input to these
models, thus missing out on being able to learn latent patterns. Plus, they struggle with
handling noisy data.
Deep Learning Based Techniques
Deep learning models can also be used for classifying nystagmus patterns by directly
using VOG data. The analysis of various methods is described as follows:
• 1D CNN on raw VOG traces: Raw VOG waveform/video input is provided to this
model. It learns various filters through training to be able to detect nystagmus pat­
terns directly from x/y positions [30].
• LSTM / BiLSTM (RNN): Recurrent models capture sequential dependencies in
VOG time series or per­frame eye features; BiLSTM variants often improve detec­
tion/recognition of nystagmus types. However, models like RNN have vanishing
gradient issues and cannot track long­range dependencies. [31]
• Transformer Model: This can handle long temporal dependencies better than CNN
and RNN without the vanishing gradients problem through its attention mechanism.
The Transformer model can process multichannel eye­movement sequences (hori­
zontal, vertical, and torsional components) as tokenized input vectors.
The self­attention mechanism enables the model to capture both local waveform
characteristics (e.g., individual nystagmus beats) and long­range temporal depen­

3.2.4
Synthetic Data Generation Methods
Scarcity of publicly available disease specific nystagmus datasets has led to exploration
of various synthetic data generation methods available. Some of the approaches are as

requested to the author.
Thus, the major barrier is data availability: without large, labeled collections of pathological
eye movements, retraining pretrained GANs remains infeasible within the current scope.
Future work could combine parametric simulation of nystagmus waveforms (e.g., syn­
thetic generation based on vestibular physiology models) with transfer learning on small
clinical datasets, enabling GANs to learn both the rhythm and variability of involuntary eye
oscillations.
7.4
Challenges Encountered
Following are the challenges which emerged during the data acquisition and creation
phase:
• Lack of nystagmus dataset: Both real and synthetic datasets such as various
gaze images and video recordings of eye movements overtime lacked involuntary
eye movement sequences.
• Scarcity of data that is temporal in nature: Most data was static or it had volun­
tary eye movements, unsuitable for modeling the fast and rhythmic oscillations of
nystagmus episode.
• Ethical as well as regulatory bottlenecks: acquiring clinical data of real patients
having nystagmus episode was difficult due to legal and ethical constraints.
• Mismatch with pre­trained GAN models: Pretrained models which were publicly
available were not trained on nystagmus data and thus they did not aligned with the
objectives of simulating nystagmus for clinical applications.

7.5.3
Pre­processing and Resampling
1. Cleaning Data: Rows with NaN or fewer than three samples were removed in both
x and y co­ordinates.
2. Construction of Timebase: A uniform timebase with sampling frequency of 1000
Hz was formed.
3. Apply anti­aliasing: It is a way to smooth out a signal before down sampling. If it
is not done, the fast changes in the signal can fold into slower signals which creates
noisy/fake patterns. Anti­aliasing method such as Centered moving average [61]
was applied where each data point was replaced with an average if its neighbouring
points in an 10 ms window.
4. Resampling to 70 Hz: All the signals were resampled using linear interpolation
with 1/70 s spacing. The interpolation was independently applied on both x,y axis .
The resampled signals timeline starts and ends at same times as the original signal
which means that the entire duration of the original signal was preserved.
5. Final data preparation: After pre­processing each signal was saved in an .npz file
with x, y, t as keys and a manifest metadata.csv which contains npz_path column
was emitted to enable easier programmatic data loading.

each type observed. Higher the frequency of a type , more likely a patient with the
disease depicts this particular type of nystagmus
Spontaneous nystamus:This type of nystagmus occurs without any change in head
position or without any stimulus.This is commonly observed in conditions like VM
and MD.
Positional nystagmus:This type of nystagmus is triggered by change in head posi­

benign condition such as BPPV
• Duration of nystagmus: This is the length of time the nystamus episode lasts for.
In BPPV is it short (< 60 sec), but the nystagmus episodes caused due to MD and
VM are longer and persistent in nature.
• Slow phase velocity (spv): A nystagmus cycle is made of slow drift and a fast
corrective reset. The velocity with which the eye drifts in slow component is called
slow phase velocity and is measured in deg/sec. Slow phase velocity dictates the
intensity of the nystagmus episode.
• Direction (horizontal, vertical, torsional): the nystagmus beating can occur verti­
cally(up beating/down beating) or horizontally(left beating/right beating). The direc­
ction of beating can also help localize the type of affected canal in case of peripheral
vestibular issues.

10.2
What Are Neural Networks ?
Neural networks are computational models which are inspired by human brain .They
are the most influential algorithms in modern machine learning and AI and are em­
ployed in complex domains such as Computer vision, Natural Language Processing
(NLP) and Speech Recognition. It is inspired by how biological neurons communi­
cate through electrical signals. The earliset model of the artificial neuron was pro­
posed by Warren McCulloh and Walter Pitts where it could perform functions of a
single function. Later Frank Rosenblatt introduced a perceptron model which is a
linear model which has bounded output [75].
A Neural Network consists of layers of nodes (neurons/perceptrons), these are con­
nected to each other. Each connection has a weight and each neuron has a bias.
These weights and biases act as a dial which controls which input feature influences
the final output. During training the network learns by adjusting these weights and
biases to minimize errors while making predictions [75].

SHAP Analysis
Deep Learning models such as the CNN and Transformer based models used in
this project are extremely complex and essentially are black boxes according to
[84], hence it is hard to understand how the model has predicted a particular output.
It is critical to find the input features that contribute most to models output since it
impacts medical decision making and improves trustworthiness of the models.
This requirement of interpretability falls under the field of Explainable AI (XAI). The
aim of this field is to make machine learning models more transparent and under­
standable. SHAP(SHapley Additive exPlainations) analysis is deemed to be the
most widely used method amongst various XAI techniques. In this section both
CNN and Transformer models described in the section 10 have been evaluated us­
ing SHAP analysis method to evaluate an interpret their decision making process.
11.1
What is SHAP analysis?
SHAP analysis is a model agnostic method. This method is based on SHAP values
. It uses a game theory approach which measures each players contribution to the
final outcome. In context of machine learning / deep learning, an importance value
is assigned to each feature which represents its contribution to the output. SHAP
value provides information about significance of each feature compared to other
features [84].
SHAP values have several properties:
• Additivity: Shap values are additive meaning contribution of each feature can
be computed independently and the summed up [84].
• Local Accuracy:Sum of all SHAP values equals the difference between model’s
expected output and predicted output [84].
• Missingness: SHAP values are zero for missing or irrelevant features for pre­
diction [84].
• Consistency: Higher the feature contribution to the outcome , higher the SHAP
value assigned to it [84].

13
Discussion
As stated earlier the current methods of detecting vertigo and its root cause relies
on clinical maneuvers. This is not suitable for patients with pre­existing conditions
such as pregnancy, patients with spine disorders and neck injuries. The generated
nystagmus can be fatiguing, difficult to reproduce and relying heavily on clinical
expertise. Moreover, the classical techniques of waveform analysis, uses many in­
termediary pre­processing steps, and rely heavily on the correct calibration of the
equipment. The choice of preprocessing strategies can sometimes affect the perfor­
mance of the model. Subjective interpretation of parameters such as the slow phase
velocity (SPV) profile, duration, direction, and amplitude derived from recorded nys­
tagmus waveforms often leads to differences in diagnosis. Most of the AI models
for vertigo detection and root cause diagnosis such as Tobii with NeuroEars [23] are
trained on relatively smaller datasets due to scarcity of rich nystagmus datasets,
and thus, may not generalize well.
To address some of these challenges, this study presents an automated diagnostic
pipeline for detecting vertigo attacks and identifying their underlying causes. The re­
cent success of Neural network models to provide end­to­end learning, has resulted
in their application for a wide range of use­cases. Moreover, it has been seen that
neural network models provide a certain amount of tolerance to noisy data. This
is very beneficial in a real world application, and more so in our use­case, as we
can reduce the pain of calibration of equipment, and allow some amount of error
in this process. In recent times, we are also observing a rise in application of the
transformer neural network architecture, in many real world use cases outside the
classical NLP text generation domain. This was also why we chose to apply this
architecture for our problem.
The following sections discusses the results in the various phases of the project.
We shall look at the key aspects and challenges in each section and analyze our
results in comparison with the original problem statement.
13.1
Dataset Creation
The Lack of publicly available disease specific labeled datasets and regulatory con­
straints in accessing real patient data present major challenges in training data­
driven diagnostic models. Moreover, there are currently no pre­trained GANs or
generative models capable of producing disease specific nystagmus data, and de­
veloping such models would itself require access to large volumes of labeled clinical
data.
This was successfully addressed by developing a synthetic dataset using math­
ematical modeling to simulate nystagmus characteristics associated with Benign
Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV), Ménière’s Disease(MD) and Vestibular Mi­
grane(VM). These simulated nystagmus waveforms were generated based on char­
acteristic parameter values (some of which include frequency, amplitude, and slow­
phase velocity, SPV T50 halving time) reported in scientific literature [33] for Benign
Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV), Ménière’s Disease (MD), and Vestibular Mi­
graine (VM). The dataset generation phase also focused on ensuring that the gen­
erated dataset has a good amount of variance, and avoids bias within each disease
Gaze tracking with transformer model based methods for accurate detection of vertigo attacks
59
==PAGE:69==
class. Gaussian noise was also added to the dataset to simulate more real world
conditions. Variance was induced on the various parameters mentioned above. The
control samples were obtained from the publicly available GazeBase dataset [42].
The implementation was done in Python along with some of the popular machine
learning and statistics libraries like pandas, scipy and numpy. Git version was used
and a modular object oriented code design was used for this implementation.
Our analysis of this phase shows that we were successful in simulating and gener­
ating real life nystagmus waveforms. We were successful in doing so by drawing
inspiration from the techniques mentioned in [33]. The generated dataset was com­
pared with the same study as a sanity check. On an average about 70 samples were
generated per class. The number of samples per class is varying because the range
of the parameter settings for amplitude, SPV profile, duration etc. varies largely per
disease. The process of each sample generation involved building a velocity profile
of the nystagmus episode, and thereafter taking the integral to get the displacement
profile. The spv profile was saved for backward traceability and troubleshooting.
Our analysis of this phase shows that we have been successful in generating a var­
ied dataset for all the 4 classes. The algorithms used were successful in simulating
real world nystagmus episodes. The balanced dataset is an asset for the machine
learning stage as this would avoid class imbalance issues, thereby avoiding the
need for creative cross validation techniques in the model training phase.
13.2
Model Training and data pre­processing
The model training phase focused on developing and training two deep learning
architectures Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN) and Transformer Model each
one integrated with late fusion mechanism for combining temporal and directional
information in the dataset. Both of these models were designed to carry out clas­
sification of the underlying disease characterised by the nystagmus episode. The
output classes were more formally called BPPV, Ménière’s Disease (MD), Vestibular
Migraine (VM), and voluntary eye movements.
The nystagmus direction was one hot encoded in the model input, along with the
waveform signals, in the training phase. This vector is derived in the inference phase
using a PCA based algorithm. The data was standardized to center the mean and
get a unity std. deviation. The size of the input vector was reduced by reducing the
sampling frequency of the signal and applying linear interpolation. The idea behind
this reduction was to reduce the input space of the NN model. This helped further
reduce the overall parameter space of the models.
The choice of the CNN model was not only to establish a baseline, but also because
of the benefits of parameter sharing, and internal pooling layers. CNNs are also a
good choice because of their ability to capture local patterns such as oscillations,
local nystagmus specific patterns, waveform shape within short time, oscillations
and frequency. Transformer models on the other hand were used to capture global
patterns through its attention mechanism, enabling it to learn long range temporal
dependencies.
The late fusion strategy [79] was employed in both model, where the temporal em­
beddings learned from waveform inputs were concatenated with static directional
information before the final classification stage. This ensured that critical spatial
cues, such as the direction of nystagmus, contributed to the final prediction without
disrupting temporal feature extraction.

Training employed the cross­entropy loss function with the Adam optimizer (learning
rate = 1 × 10−3, weight decay = 1 × 10−4), using mini­batches of 64 samples across
40 epochs. Dropout regularization was used with a rate of (0.1–0.2) to mitigate
overfitting. Five­fold cross­validation was used to ensure a true unbiased estimate
of the models generalization error. The model performances were also compared
with the performance of a possible ensemble model that is made up of the CNN and
transformer model.
The training and inference pre­processing pipelines standardized synthetic and con­
trol waveforms to a uniform sampling rate, duration, and representation, ensuring
consistent feature learning. The inference pipeline applied the same transforma­
tions to real recordings from the EyeKnow [17] headset, enabling seamless deploy­
ment without calibration requirements. This modularity directly resolved one of the
core problems of diagnostic systems on device calibration and variability across ac­
quisition hardware.
13.3
Performance of CNN vs Transformer
The performance evalutaion techniques such as 5 fold Cross validation, confusion
matrix as well as the accuracy we employed to get an unbiased estimate of models
error rate.
The results of all three methods demonstrated that the 1D CNN model with late
fusion demonstrated slightly better performance than the Transformer model with
late fusion. The 1D CNN model with late fusion seems to show convergence with
the 5­fold cross validation method where as the Transformer model with late fusion
never converges and displays overfitting as the validation loss increases with each
epoch. This result can be attributed to characteristics of both models, while CNN
is better at identifying local patterns and can generalize better even when trained
on relatively small labeled dataset, while in contrast to this, Transformer model has
higher number of trainable parameters and they rely on large and diverse datasets to
learn meaningful patterns through attention mechanism. Since the dataset is limited
and synthetic in nature the Transformer might have memorized the training patterns
rather than generalizing on unseen data which could be a cause to the observed
overfitting. One could thereby also hypothesize that a hybrid model architecture
with early convolutional layers, and later attention blocks for global context, could
perform significantly better for this task. A simple sanity check for this idea was
made by measuring the performance of a ensemble model arrangement using the
same 5 fold cross validation folds. Although we did not observe a significant change
in the performance of the ensemble model, we did observe a change in the logits
distribution, which can be seen as, that both the models are able to focus on diffent
aspects of the signal.

explainability in decision making commonly associated with deep learning and AI­
based diagnostic systems.
The top five most influential features contributing to
the final predictions were identified and analyzed to gain insights into the models’
decision­making behavior. The results reveals that the CNN based late fusion model
shows distributed feature attribution which is robust to noise whereas Transformers
based model shows strong focus on certain key features. In general, it was ob­

13.5
Model Output Verification
The correctness of the model’s predictions must be verifiable by clinical practition­
ers to ensure safe and trustworthy deployment in diagnostic workflows. To achieve
this, the inference pipeline was designed to generate visual diagnostic graphs de­
rived from the raw waveform data produced by the video­to­waveform deep learning
model. These visualizations illustrate key temporal and spatial characteristics such
as fixation periods, instantaneous velocity profiles, and signal dispersion through­
out the nystagmus episode. By examining these features, clinicians can compare
the waveform behavior against the known clinical signatures of each vertigo class
(e.g., BPPV, MD, VM) and validate whether the model’s predicted output aligns with
physiologically consistent patterns.
13.6
Video to waveform generation
The raw eye­tracking videos need to be converted into temporal waveforms before
they can be used as input for the diagnostic deep learning models. To achieve this,
several deep learning based video to waveform conversion models were analyzed.
Given that the EyeKnow headset records monocular eye videos, the most suitable
models identified were Meye [72] and DeepEye [74]. These models were specif­
ically chosen because they are deep learning architectures trained on large and
diverse datasets, which makes them capable of handling variations in eye shape,
pupil size, camera to eye distance, and partial occlusions. As a result, they can ac­
curately segment the pupil and estimate gaze position without requiring manual cal­
ibration. This capability directly addresses the challenge of calibration dependence
caused by inter­patient anatomical differences and thereby supports the project goal
of developing a robust and generalizable system suitable for real­world clinical de­
ployment.
Despite achieving promising outcomes, this study is subject to several limitations
which are discussed below:
13.7
Limitations
• The limited availability of publicly accessible, disease­specific nystagmus record­
ings significantly restricts the model’s generalization capability when applied to
real­world clinical data, as the training process relied primarily on synthetic and
control datasets.
• While the synthetic dataset was designed using research­based parameters
reflecting disease­specific nystagmus behavior, it lacks validation against ac­
tual clinical ground truth data, which may limit its ability to represent the full
clinical diversity and inherent noise observed in real patients.
• The use of mono ocular data, limits analysis as the models miss out of binocular
specific patterns.
• torsion detection needs to be modeled as it is a key differentiator between
BPPV and the other two categories such as VM and MD as it might improve

• The video­to­waveform conversion models integrated into this framework were

• The Transformer model showed signs of overfitting, which can be attributed to

Future Work
While the proposed pipeline shows promising results, several directions can strengthen
its clinical relevance and performance:
• Real­world data collection: Data Augmentation of the dataset with real pa­
tient nystagmus recordings to improve model generalization.
• Data diversity: Inclusion of binocular and torsional eye movement data to
capture more complex clinical patterns.
• Model improvements: Hyper parameter tuning on both models as well as in­
clusion of further features such as gaze angles along with pupil position. This
will increase the feature space, thereby adding context to the model for clas­
sification. Usually this should be followed up by data augmentation to avoid
suffering from the curse of dimensionality.
• Video­to­waveform refinement: Video to waveform models as of now are
trained on rather generalistic datasets, and might not be ideal for capturing
the waveform constructs required for nystagmus classification. Fine tuning
the waveform generation models on disease specific vertigo attack data, could
bring use closer to application of this pipeline in real world scenarios
• Clinical validation: Testing and validation of the system in hospital settings
with real patient data.
• Real­time deployment: Optimization of the pipeline for onsite or cloud deploy­
ment for practical, on­site diagnosis.
• User interface: Development of clinician­friendly dashboards and user inter­
faces for visualization and interactive result verification.
• Regulatory considerations: Addressal of ethical, privacy, and regulatory re­
quirements for medical AI systems.
The above listed aspects, can help evolve the project from a proof­of­concept to a
clinically deployable aid tool for clinical practitioners thereby aiding fast, accurate,
and explainable vertigo diagnosis ’

Conclusion
This Thesis has presented an automated pipeline for detecting vertigo attacks and
their underlying causes using gaze tracking and deep learning. By using synthetic
data modeling to combat scarcity of publicly available disease specific data of real
world patient having a vertigo attack and displaying nystagmus eye movements.
Finding suitable video to waveform conversion models, constructing modular pre­
processing pipelines and training and interpretive deep learning models. This demon­
strates the feasibility of a non­invasive AI assisted diagnostic tool to support vertigo
and root cause detection.
The 1D CNN model showed better performance with respect to generalization and
stability compared to the Transformer model with late fusion, which suffered from
overfitting due to data limitations. The SHAP analysis further highlighted how the
models made their predictions, which is essential for clinical acceptance.
The results confirm that a synthetic data­driven approach can effectively train base­
line models capable of identifying disease­specific nystagmus patterns. Although
the system has not yet been clinically validated, it lays a strong foundation for de­
veloping scalable, explainable, and adaptable vertigo diagnostic tools.
