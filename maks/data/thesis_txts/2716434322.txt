Summary
In the last two centuries, human activities have been fueling climate change, due to
burning fossil fuels, and we have reached a point where we must take quick actions
to stop it. It is clear that society needs to shift to a carbon-neutral future and, as we
do so, new challenges arise.
Renewable energy is of significant importance to the transition to fully sustainable
energy generation. This is possible, but because of the intermittent availability of
these sources (like wind and solar), it is fundamental to build reliable and optimized
energy storage systems, which ensure continuity in the power output.
In this thesis, we formulate mathematical models for wind and solar power, based
on the physics and the scientific literature. Using stochastic differential equations,
the uncertainty of the wind speed and the cloud cover (for the solar power model)
are modelled. Afterwards, the power curve of a wind turbine is computed, expressing
it as an optimization problem that regulates tip-speed ratio and pitch blade angle.
The effective solar radiation on PV panels, and therefore the power output, is instead
computed by combining models for direct and diffuse radiation.
Secondly, models for energy storage systems are developed. We consider batteries,
thermal storage and water electrolysis. Each of them stores energy in different forms:
electricity, heat and hydrogen, respectively.
Eventually, all the models are combined and an optimal control problem is for-
mulated, such as to balance electricity demand and production, and the economic
revenue of the overall energy system is maximized. The program achieves this by
controlling where the power from wind and solar is stored or sent and how and when
the energy is sold to the market, according to the prices of electricity, heat and
hydrogen over time.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
This chapter introduces the reader to the general motivation of this thesis and to the
energy storage technologies. The goal of the thesis and how the problem is formulated
mathematically are also introduced.
1.1
Motivation
As society as we know it today has progressively become industrialized and the use of
energy has been increasing over the last century, greenhouse gas emissions have been
rising at an alarming pace, making us face one of the biggest problems of our times:
climate change caused by global warming. The biggest contribution to greenhouse
emissions is carbon dioxide, which accounts for around 75%. The annual CO2 emis-
sions time series is shown in Figure 1.1. As well known, climate change is a threat
to our society and to Earth, and therefore needs to be addressed immediately. As
shown in Figure 1.2, more than 73 % of the global greenhouse gas emissions is caused
by the energy sector. This is due to the use of fossil fuels, which release CO2 in the
atmosphere when burned. Because of that, it is clear that a future with fossil fuels is
not sustainable anymore and we need to transition to renewable energy sources, such
as wind and solar power. In order to speed up the transition, the European Union
has established a long-term goal: to be climate-neutral by 2050, i.e. an economy with
net-zero greenhouse gas emissions. Furthermore, Denmark is particularly ambitious,
and wants to cut down CO2 emissions by 70 % by the year 2030 and also to be com-
pletely independent of fossil fuels by 2050. Already today, Denmark’s energy mix is
made by up to 80% of renewable sources (where 57 % only comes from wind).
Transitioning to a completely green energy mix is possible, but it brings new
challenges. Sustainable energy sources like solar and wind rely on something that
cannot be controlled and is volatile/intermittent. In other words: what do we do
when the sun does not shine and the wind does not blow, but the energy demand
is high? Because of this, renewable energy sources have to heavily rely on energy
storage systems, that serve as a buffer for when the power production is low or
absent. Moreover, it is essential that these energy storage systems run as eﬀiciently
as possible.
In order to do so, unlike for traditional power sources based on fossil fuels, we
have the need for forecasts of the energy production in the future. Making reliable

The second key aspect that needs to be taken into account when running renewable

power to the market.

1.2
Introduction to energy storage systems
An energy storage system (ESS) is a device that allows to capture energy, to store it
and release it at a later time. As of today, the energy storage technologies in use are
many, and they differ in the form of energy they can store. According to this, the
most important energy storage systems can be classified into
• Chemical ESSs, which include all the Power-to-Gas systems, such as produc-
tion of hydrogen, and methane.
• Thermal ESSs, which include all the types of thermal energy storage (e.g. via

molten salts). These can be latent or sensible, according to whether change of
phase of the material is allowed or not. This storage technology is relatively
new and consists in storing energy as heat by transferring it to a material that
can hold it for a long period of time and then it is released when needed.
• Mechanical ESSs: the most important one is pumped-storage hydroelectric-
ity, which stores energy in the form of gravitational potential energy of water,
pumped from a lower elevation reservoir to a higher elevation. In the discharg-
ing phase, the water flows down naturally from the higher reservoir and power
can be produced using a turbine.
Other mechanical ESSs are flywheel and
compressed air.
• Electrochemical ESSs: this includes all the forms of batteries.
• Electrical ESSs, like capacitors and supercapacitors.
While some systems are exclusively used to store and release electricity (like batteries),
others make use of excess power for different purposes. All the processes that use
excess power produced from renewable sources to convert it to something different
are called Power-to-X, where X stands, for example, for an e-fuel. This is the case
for hydrogen and methane.
The energy storage systems can also be classified according to their storage ca-
pacity and their discharging time. These features are essential to consider, because
they determine whether a technology is appropriate or not for a certain setup. Figure
1.3 illustrates these features for the most common energy storage systems.
The most worldwide deployed energy storage technology is pumped hydroelectric-
ity, see Figure 1.4 which shows the total operational energy storage market. Moreover,
batteries and thermal storages are rapidly increasing and gaining interest around the
world, as the technology gets better and less expensive.
In this thesis we are going to consider and study 3 types of energy storage systems,
which are
• batteries,
• thermal storage,
• hydrogen production via water electrolysis.
These systems are intrinsically different from each other: we will use batteries to store
electricity, thermal storage to store heat and electrolysis to store hydrogen. Moreover,
they have very different capacities, as shown in Figure 1.3, since thermal storage and
hydrogen are intended for long-term use.

We will model energy production from wind turbines and solar PVs, by describing

problem which takes both technical constraints and energy demand into account, and
maximizes the economic profit from the whole system.

CHAPTER 2
Methods
In this chapter, we introduce the mathematical framework used throughout the thesis.
First of all, we define what a stochastic differential equation is and present a simple
simulation method: the Euler-Maruyana scheme. Subsequently, a prediction scheme,
the continuous-discrete extended Kalman filter (CD-EKF), is presented. Finally, the
economic optimal control problem is explained. This represents the core of thesis.
The main goal is to optimize the charge and discharge of the energy storage systems,
taking into consideration energy demand, production and electricity price from the
grid.
2.1
Stochastic differential equations (SDEs)
A stochastic differential equation (SDE) is a differential equation in which one or
more terms are stochastic processes. An SDE is the result of adding some random
white noise to an expected slope. An SDE is usually presented in the differential form
dx(t) = f(x(t), t)dt + σ(x(t), t)dw(t)
(2.1)
The function f(x(t), t) is the so called drift coeﬀicient, while σ(x(t), t) is the diffusion
coeﬀicient. Notice that the diffusion may also depend on the state variable x(t). The
drift defines the slope of the process, while the diffusion indicates the variance around
the mean expected behaviour. The process w(t) is a standard Brownian motion (also
called Wiener process). (2.1) can also be expressed in the integral form

2.2
Euler-Maruyama simulation method
The Euler-Maruyama scheme is one of the most known and simple simulation methods
of SDEs. It can be seen as the extension of the Euler method for ordinary differential
equations to SDEs.
Let us consider a time interval [0, T] and its discretization 0 = t0 < t1 < ... < tn <
... < tN = T (with time step ∆t = T/N). Given a stochastic differential equation in
the differential form (2.1), we can approximate the solution x in the given discrete
time interval with the following scheme [3]
x(ti+1) = x(ti) + f(x(ti), t)∆t + σ(x(ti), t)∆wi
(2.3)
for i = 0, 1, 2, ..., N −1 with given initial condition x(t0) = x0. The smaller the time
step ∆t is, the better approximation of the solution is obtained. The Euler-Maruyama
method comes from the truncation of Ito’s formula of the stochastic Taylor series after
the first order.
2.3
Continuous-discrete extended Kalman filter
We will consider a non-linear system described by some equations given in the
general form (1.6). With this setup, the natural tool used to perform predictions is the
well known continuous-discrete extended Kalman filter (CD-EKF). The name
indicates that the underlying state model is expressed in continuous time, but the
measurements are available at discrete times. The EKF is the non-linear extension
of the linear Kalman filter. The Kalman filter estimates the system state and its
uncertainty (covariance), at each time step. The EKF is a useful and widely deployed
tool, because of the fact that a lot of real life systems involve non-linearities. The
EKF works using linearizations of the nonlinear transformations. This implies that
linearized transformations are only reliable if the error propagation is linear. If this
condition does not hold, the approximation can be poor, and could even make the
filter diverge [4].

EKF. The variable x indicates the state, while the matrix P is its covariance. The
notation ˆxn|m indicates the estimate of x at time tn given the observations up to and
including time tm. The CD-EKF consists of 2 parts: the one-step prediction step and
the filtering step. The explanation follows as in [5].

CHAPTER 3
Wind power model
This chapter deals with developing a model for wind power production from wind
turbines. This problem can be divided into 2 sub-problems: creating a model for the
wind speed dynamics and another one that describes how a wind turbine converts
the wind speed into generated electrical power. We will tackle the first problem con-
sidering a simple model based on stochastic differential equation from the literature,
while the wind power production problem is considered here in a steady state manner,
and solved considering the optimization problem on the power coeﬀicient of the wind
turbine.
3.1
Introduction: the wind
Modeling the wind dynamics is not an easy task. This is due to the huge variability
and stochasticity intrinsic to the nature of the wind.
Winds are movements of air originated by temperature gradients on the Earth
surface, due to the uneven solar irradiation. The warmer and lighter air from the
equatorial regions flows towards the colder poles and goes back with a lower tem-
perature (geostrophic winds). The Coriolis forces also affect the air circulation on
the Earth. Moreover, in the lower part of the atmosphere (surface layer) the winds’
speed and direction are altered by obstacles and frictional forces, originating turbu-
lent flows of different amplitudes and frequencies (local winds) [15]. Therefore, wind
“depends on the geographic location, the climate, the height above ground level, the
roughness of the terrain and the obstacles in the surroundings. These are the winds
the wind turbines interact with”. Characterization of winds can be made studying
their kinetic energy distribution in the frequency domain (van der Hoven spectrum).
When considering the frequency spectra of winds in different locations, over a long
period of time, we discover that the energy in the wind, regardless of the site, appears
to always contain 2 distinct frequency peaks:
• The first one is found to be at low frequencies (4 days cycle). This is associ-
ated to the mean wind speed, or geostrophic wind, which depends on the
geographical position.
• The second one is found at high frequencies (1 minute cycles) and it is due to
the local turbulent winds.

3.3
Introduction to wind turbine energy capture
The problem of forecasting the production of wind power is not just limited to esti-
mating the speed of the wind in the future, since we also need a way of translating
wind speed in real generated power by the turbine. Each single model of wind turbine
has a different way of reacting to the wind speed, and how much energy it can capture
from it. The relationship between wind speed and generated power is given by the so
called power curve, which can be considered one of the most important feature of
a wind turbine model. Figure 3.3 shows how a generic ideal power curve looks like.
The cut-in and cut-out speeds (Vmin and Vmax in the figure) are the speeds below
and above which the wind turbine is shut down. In the former case this is due to the
too low available energy, and it would not compensate for the operational costs. In
the latter case, the turbine is stopped to avoid structural overloading. In Region I
the turbine operates in order to capture as much energy as possible from the wind.
Region II is a transition area that might not exist in a lot of wind turbines. In this
region the rotor speed is limited. The rated speed Vn is the limit after which the
turbine does not generate more power, but remains constant (region III). Building
wind turbines with higher rated speed is possible, but economically unfeasible, since
the average wind speed is usually around the rated speed, while higher speeds are
not frequent enough to justify the extra sizing of the turbine to capture more power
[15].

CHAPTER 4
Solar power model
In this chapter we deal with modeling our second and last source of green power,
the one obtained from solar radiation. This is technologically achieved, as well know,
with photovoltaic (PV) panels. The diﬀiculty of modelling power production from
solar energy does not lie in the PV technology itself, but rather in modelling the
real sun radiation that hits the Earth at a specific location and time. This is due to
its high level of stochasticity. In order to create a robust model for solar power, we
therefore need to model the dynamics of the solar radiation in depth, which is the
scope of this chapter. We will achieve this goal building stochastic models for cloud
cover state, direct radiation and diffuse radiation, as proposed in the literature.
4.1
Introduction
The dynamics of the solar radiation is described considering stochastic models for
• the sky cloud cover,
• direct radiation,
• diffuse radiation.
The cloud cover model is the first element of our model. It is essential because it
quantifies the percentage of radiation that goes through the atmosphere and what is
instead reflected by the clouds (hence, how much radiation is blocked by the clouds).
What we are ultimately interested in is the so called global radiation, which is the
total amount of shortwave radiation received from the sun by a surface horizontal
near the ground. This quantity is what eventually determines the energy production
from photovoltaic cells. The radiation that a surface receives per surface unit is called
irradiance and its SI unit is
W
m2 . It is defined as the derivative of the radiant flux
received with respect to the surface area.
The global radiation IG is made up by two contributions
IG = ID + IN
(4.1)
where ID is the diffuse radiation and IN is the direct radiation. The former
accounts for the radiation that hits the surface of the Earth without finding any
==PAGE:44==
34
4 Solar power model
obstacle (both objects and particles) and therefore does not lose energy. This means
that the radiation is not scattered in the atmosphere and has a fixed direction. The
latter, instead, accounts for the the sunlight that has been scattered by particles in
the air, and even objects. In clear sky conditions, most of the global radiation is direct
radiation, while when the sky is very cloudy, the direct radiation drops drastically
and most of the radiation is diffuse, because of the clouds blocking the sunlight and
scattering the radiation.
This is the reason why the direct and diffuse radiation
tightly depend on the cloud cover index. Direct and diffuse radiation also depend on
the solar elevation of the sun, i.e. the angle that the sun forms with the horizon at a
specific location and time of the year. This model is not stochastic but deterministic.
Once the global radiation is obtained, from direct and diffuse radition model and
cloud cover model, the generated power by a solar PV farm can be computed with a
simple deterministic relationship.
The overview of the structure of the whole solar power model is presented in Figure
4.1 as a sketch. Notice that the blue boxes in the chart represent the stochastic parts
of the model.

represents the biggest source of stochasticity when modelling and predicting solar

When the sky coverage is high, very low levels of direct radiation make it through
the atmosphere, whilst a lot of the global radiation is diffuse.
The cloud cover state is an index and it is measured in Okta scale. An okta is an
integer value from 0 to 8, where 0 represents completely clear sky and 8 represents full
overcast of the sky. 9 could also be possible to indicate the impossibility to observe
the cloud cover (for example in foggy conditions).
Different cloud cover models have been proposed in the scientific literature. A
popular choice is a discrete state Markov model. This kind of model is quite straight-
forward and suits this type of data. The idea simply consists in defining a 10-states
Markov chain (one state for each Okta) and then estimating the transition probabil-
ities between states, using maximum likelihood. The model becomes quite simple if
we assume that the state at time t + 1 only depends on the state at present time t.
This means that the Markov chain is first-order and that we have a 10x10 transition
probabilities matrix. This modelling approach is nice, but for the purpose of this
thesis, we would rather use novel stochastic models based on SDEs, since they are
more convenient to handle when building a Kalman filter to make predictions.

CHAPTER 5
Battery model
This chapter presents the first and simplest energy storage system, i.e. the one using
battery. We will consider a very simple linear model to model the energy accumulated
in the battery, using an ordinary differential equation.
5.1
Overview and technology
The energy storage system that utilizes batteries is know as Battery Energy Stor-
age System (BESS). With this system, the energy is accumulated in batteries, and
when the need arises, it is released back as electrochemical energy. The main types
of batteries are
• Lithium-Ion (Li-Ion) batteries, which are with no doubts the most com-
monly deployed nowadays. These batteries represent around 90% of the global
market of BESS. The reason why they became so popular is that they are light
and compact, they have high capacity and energy density, low maintenance,
and a long lifetime. Li-Ion batteries are easily and quickly charged and have a
low self-discharge rate [10]. Moreover, they are becoming relatively cheap, as
shown in Figure 5.2.
• Lead-Acid batteries. They are the oldest battery technology but the cheapest.
They drawbacks are slow-charging, heavyweight and low energy density.
• Sodium-Sulfur (NaS) batteries. They have high energy and power density
and a long lifetime, but they require high operating temperatures, which makes
them also dangerous.
• Flow batteries. These type of batteries are different than the others because
they store the energy using a liquid electrolyte solution. They have low energy
capacity but long lifespan.
The global total operational BESS capacity is shown in Figure 5.1.

Thermal storage
model
The next energy storage system that we are to consider is the one through thermal
energy. Thermal storage is somehow similar to a battery, but while a battery works
exchanging chemical energy, thermal storage works through heat exchange. When
using thermal storage, excess power from renewable sources can be converted into
heat, which can be used to heat up some material, and then released back when
needed. In this chapter we will consider molten salts as material for thermal storage,
and we will build a simple 1-tank model as well as a 2 tanks model.
6.1
Overview
The increasing necessity to rely as much as possible on renewable (but volatile) energy
sources, going towards a more sustainable and green future, in order to limit the use
of fossil fuels, has pushed engineers to explore and implement new ways of storing
energy.
Thermal energy storage (TES) is a quite straightforward, (relatively) inexpen-
sive and eﬀicient way to achieve this. Even though the TES systems can differ quite
significantly, they all use the ability of a material to accumulate energy, through
some heat exchange mechanism. Then when energy is needed, the material releases
heat (cooling down), which can be used in several ways. The most obvious one is to
(re-)generate electrical power. This technology is known as Power-to-heat-to-power
(PtHtP), and it is made of PtH component for charging, a TES and another device
(HtP) for discharging (for example Rankine cycle).
TES allows excess thermal energy storage to be stored for several hours, days or
even months, depending on the technology and the medium used. TES systems are
divided into 2 big categories, differentiating whether phase transformations of the
material are permitted or not.
In the former case, the system can hold the energy for longer time, and it is
known as latent thermal storage. In this setting, the latent energy associated
with a phase transition of the material is exploited, in order to retain/release heat,
without increasing/decreasing its temperature (isothermic transformation). The ma-

TES mechanism. In this case, a heat exchange with the material always implies a

CHAPTER 7
Electrolyzer model
In this chapter we are going to explore a different possibility of energy storage, that
is through electrolysis, the process of splitting water into oxygen and hydrogen, using
electrical power. This is a Power-to-Gas method. We are going to explore the thermo-
dynamics related to the chemical process, and then we will build an electrochemical
model, relating the current intensity and the voltage of an electrolytic cell. With
such a model, the hydrogen flow rate is computed easily. The model then finally
implemented in a steady state manner.
7.1
Overview and technology
The last energy storage method that we are to consider is water electrolysis, which
is the chemical process of decomposing water into oxygen (gas) and hydrogen (gas).
The general reaction of electrolysis of water (also called water splitting reaction)
is
2H2O(l) + Electrical power −→2H2(g) + O2(g)
(7.1)
Electrical power has been included among the reagents of the reaction, in order
to underline the fact that the reaction is not spontaneous in standard conditions, and
it needs electricity to happen. This will be explained further in the next section.
As explained in the Introduction chapter of the thesis, the hydrogen produced
from electrolysis can be used in many ways: it can be converted back into electrical
power via a fuel cell, or it can be used a product for other chemical reactions (for
example for the production of ammonia or methanol).
The main state-of-the-art electrolysis technologies are three:
• Alkaline Electrolysis.
This represent the most common electrolysis tech-
nology along with PEM. It is the oldest and most mature technology. It is
characterized by having two electrodes (cathode and anode) operating in a liq-
uid alkaline electrolyte solution of potassium hydroxide (KOH), usually 20-40%,
or sodium hydroxide (NaOH), separated by a diaphragm (see Figure 7.1). It
operates at relatively low temperatures (max 90◦C). This is the type of elec-
trolysis that we are going to consider for our model, therefore it will explained
more in depth in the next pages.
==PAGE:70==

• Polymer-Electrolyte Membrane (PEM) Electrolysis. This technology
utilizes a thin polymer membrane as solid electrolyte, rather than a solution.
PEM water electrolyzers also work at low temperatures (max 90◦C). See Figure
7.2.
• Solid-Oxyde Electrolysis. This is the least developed and not widely com-

The most important aspect of the electrolysis that we have to model is the kinetics

intensity and voltage in an electrolyzer (I −U).

optimal control problem. We want to simulate for an entire day because energy mar-
kets usually work on a day-ahead basis [7]. Electricity is traded everyday (typically
at noon) for the day after in pools, where producers and customers present bids for
the energy delivery. The power exchange market in the Nordic countries is Nord-
Pool [8]. The day-ahead market is the most important one, because it allocates the
most energy for the day. Adjustments in real-time of the energy are needed though,
==PAGE:91==

based on the real demand. Because of this, real-time or regulation markets are also
established, in order to correctly balance the demand.
As it happens in NordPool, we assume to know the price of the electricity (and
heat and hydrogen) the day before, and that this changes every 1 hour. The whole
time horizon is 24 h, and we assume a sampling period of 10 min for the power
production. Since the time horizon is way too long and so is the time step, we cannot
take into account the turbulent local variations of the wind speed, therefore these
need to be neglected and we assume to know a numerical weather prediction of the
average wind speed vm(t). The turbulent variations in the wind profile will be
absorbed by the battery or the other storage systems, during operation. Using the
mean values of the disturbances corresponds to considering the expected profit. This
is also the reason why properly sized energy storage are essential for renewable energy
systems. The simulated wind and solar power are shown in Figure 8.3, along with
the total power.
Notice that the time step for the discretization of the control horizon and of the
sampling horizon are different! This is because we assume that we decide how much
power we sell every hour, even though we sample the power every 10 minutes. This
needs to be properly accommodated in the implementation of the optimal control
problem, choosing different discretizations for the variables.
The prices are generated randomly from normal distributions. The storage sys-

systems.
The program controls the distribution of the produced power to the storage sys-
tems in such a way that the most profit is made, i.e. electricity/heat/hydrogen are
sold only when expensive, while they are stored when the selling prices are low.

8.3
Reformulation of the optimal control problem
(Scenario 2)
In the previous formulation and setup of the system we used the thermal storage
and the electrolyzer only to convert the power to heat and hydrogen, and then sell it
(Power-to-X). The electricity demand was therefore only handled by the battery.
However, in reality, thermal storage and hydrogen storage are also used to regen-
erate power (Power-to-X-to-Power). This is because these storage systems allow to
store an enormous amount of energy for a long period of time, while batteries do not.
In the event of a cloudy and non-windy day, the battery would not be enough to
deliver the desired power for a long period of time. We implement this through the
following modifications of the system
• In order for the thermal storage to also support the demand of electricity, a
(generic) steam turbine is added to convert heat into electrical power. This
is not modelled in detail here but we assume a 70% eﬀiciency in the energy
conversion.
• The hydrogen can also support the grid, using a fuel cell, that produces power
(and water) using hydrogen and oxygen. This is also not modelled in detail
but we assume a conversion eﬀiciency of 60%. Since 1 kg of hydrogen contains
141800 kJ of energy, we have that the power (in kW) produced by some hydrogen
flow (in mol/s) dH2 is
dfuel cell(t) = 141800 · 0.6 · MMH2 · dH2.
(8.7)
• Now the power produced by the wind turbine and the solar PV farm directly
goes into the grid to accommodate the demand, and only the surplus over the
demand is sent to the storage systems. The optimal control program controls
automatically all the power flows, both to the markets and to the storage ancil-
laries.
The new overall structure of the system can be seen in the diagram in Figure 8.8.
The optimal control problem is therefore slightly changed to accommodate the
new features. The demand of electricity (power) is denoted by del(t). The power sent
to the storage is simply

(8.8)

(when Ptot(t) < del(t)) is dsto(t). A new constraint is therefore introduced in the
optimization problem for managing the electricity demand with all the storages. The
reformulated optimal control problem is

8.6
Discussion of the results
Concluding, we just demonstrated how it is possible to control optimally the power
flows in a renewable energy system with many storage systems, in order to satisfy a
power demand and maximize the profit, selling excess energy. The solutions given
by the optimal control program in the simulations are as expected: the program
smartly charges the storage systems before a low power production is expected, and
discharges them to satisfy the demand. The excess power is sold when possible in
order to make profit, when the prices are high. The program also successfully stores
energy for the future (after the control horizon), thanks to the profit to-go terms in
the profit function.
We demonstrated that having multiple storage systems is essential in order to
guarantee continuity in the power output. Moreover, we showed that optimization is
fundamental to run renewable energy systems smoothly.

CHAPTER 9
Conclusion
This thesis encompasses a series of mathematical models for renewable energy sys-
tems, consisting of a power generation part and an energy storage part. The power
generation is provided by wind turbines and solar PV panels, while for the energy
storage we have considered batteries, thermal storage and water electrolysis.
In the first part of the thesis, we have developed a simple SDE model for the wind
speed, which is based on the physical properties of the phenomenon (average and
turbulent wind). Afterwards, we have seen how wind speed is converted into power
by a wind turbine. Formulating a simple optimization problem, the power output of a
wind turbine is obtained controlling tip speed ratio and pitch angle, using the power
coeﬀicient values. Then, we have built a model for solar power generation.
This
comprehends a model for: cloud cover state (SDE), direct radiation, diffuse radiation
and PV power output.
Afterwards, the energy storage systems have been modelled. We described the
energy flux in a battery and in a thermal storage using simple differential equations,
coming from physical intuition. The water electrolysis model is, instead, rather com-
plicated and required much more study and multiple literature sources.
The core of the thesis is then addressed: an economic optimal control problem
is defined on the whole energy system. The program smartly sends the produced
power where it will make the most profit. Energy (in the form of electricity, heat or
hydrogen) is rather stored or sold according to the cost functions. This program is
then modified to allow re-conversion of heat and hydrogen in electricity, in order for
them to support the electricity demand, along with the battery.
9.1
Considerations and future work
Finally, some considerations on what could be improved in this work, maybe for the
future:
• The wind speed model is rather simple, and maybe not optimal for forecasting.
More complex models shall be used.
==PAGE:114==
104
9 Conclusion
• The wind turbine model is a simplification of a more sophisticated controller,
since we assumed steady state conditions on the wind speed, which is unrealistic.
The real output of a wind turbine might therefore differ. For future work, a
dynamic controller shall be considered. Furthermore, wind power measurements
shall be considered to validate such a model.
• Other different improved models from the scientific literature for the direct and
diffuse radiations could also be investigated.
• There is clearly a correlation between wind speed and solar irradiance, which
was not investigated nor considered in this work, but could be interesting to see
if there is some related scientific work on the topic.
• There is an abvious correlation between electricity prices and wind speed and
solar irradiance, which is not considered here.
• In order to not complicate too much this work, the thesis focused heavily on sim-
ulation, rather than forecasting. Nonetheless, we introduced in Chapter 2 the
framework for making predictions (the continuous-discrete extended Kalman
filter). This shall be used on the SDE models of cloud cover state and wind
speed, in order to establish a prediction scheme on the generated power. We
leave this for future work.
• The constraint on the power demand in the optimal control problem should be
soft, just to avoid the problem to be infeasible when too little power is produced
by the wind and solar overall.
• The model for water electrolysis is robust, but for future work, one should not
assume steady state conditions and allow the temperature to change. In this
regard, we must stress that this has been tried but, in order not to complicate
too much the thesis, it has been decided to assume the temperature constant
anyway. In spite of this, the reader must be aware that this does not represent
a huge approximation, since in real-life industrial electrolyzers the temperature
is controlled and kept very close to a target value. Therefore, the hydrogen flow
is not really influenced by the change in temperature and assuming constant
temperature does not represent an oversimplification.
• The steam turbine and the fuel cell show be modelled in more detail.
• The models for thermal storage and battery are rather simple, and they probably
need to be more sophisticated for real-life applications. Nevertheless, the scope
of this thesis is to establish a framework that combines different renewable
energy sources with multiple and different storage systems, in order to carry
out an optimal control problem, which automatically optimizes how the whole
systems runs, according to a profit measure and a power demand. If needed,
the models can be complicated and improved, but the framework remains the
same. Therefore, the author believes that the core scope of the project has been

of the work.
