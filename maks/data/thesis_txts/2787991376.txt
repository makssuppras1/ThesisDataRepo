Abstract 
Auditory spatial awareness is one of the many facets of the human auditory system, its 
prominent feature is sound localization. Humans and some animals have been observed 
to be able to estimate the locations of objects such as walls and trees solely from 
reflected sounds. This ability, also known as echolocation, has been shown to be 
significantly developed in people with visual impairment as a means for navigating the 
environment. Biomimicry of such ability could be used in machines such as humanoid 
robots as a means of enhancing their localization and navigation capabilities. The 
generally complex acoustic environment is for the sake of this study simplified to a single 
large hard wall, a single sound source and a binaural receiver. Recordings are obtained 
from simulation and in measurements using a Head and Torso Simulator (HATS) and a 
single speaker inside an anechoic room with one large acoustically reflective wall. This 
paper proposes and evaluates a novel method for localization of reflected sound that 
estimates the distance and direction of the HATS to the wall from the obtained binaural 
recordings using known head-related transfer function (HRTF) and echo-ranging.

1  Introduction 
One of the most essential abilities of human hearing is sound localization. Humans have 
just two ears yet can locate the direction from which a sound is coming from. Thanks to 
our ears we can estimate the direction of arrival (DOA) as well as the distance of the 
sound source. While most people rely on sight as a means of navigating the environment, 
hearing helps with mapping the environment in addition to sight or as an alternative when 
sight is lost. In echolocation the task is to localize the source of reflected sound, that 
being the reflective surface, while the sound source location is relatively close to the 
receiver and is known. As observed in a study involving blind people [1], when in a room 
or near large hard surfaces, both blind and sighted people can estimate their location 
within the room and locate large objects using self-generated sounds. The topic of sound 
localization is a broad area of acoustics with practical applications in marine navigation, 
aeronautics, and noise control. Binaural localization has gained attention in recent years 
as new technologies utilizing binaural localization are developed for robotics, hearing 
aids and sound scenes from virtual and augmented reality. Having a clear signal 
processing method for binaural echolocation could prove useful in advancing all these 
technologies and perhaps in learning more about the natural ability of humans and other 
living creatures.

1.1 Distance Estimation from Time Delay 
Distance of the reflective surface can be calculated from the total time it takes the sound 
to travel from the source to the receiver at the speed of sound, which under normal 
conditions is constant. Hence, to find the distance the sound travels all that needs to be 
found is the time of fight (ToF). Time delay estimation (TDE) of sound is a well-studied 
problem [2]. When a recording contains a source sound and a delayed reflected sound, 
it is possible to find the time difference of arrival (TDOA) between the two. The biggest 
challenge in TDE is that most methods have low performance in the presence of 
reverberation and noise.

A simple TDE algorithm is the cross-correlation method (CC) [3], it can be used to find 
the delay between two correlated signals by finding the peak in the correlation function. 
In the case of a single signal with echo, autocorrelation (AC) can be used to find the 
TDOA. A common practice for TDE is to use the generalized cross-correlation (GCC) 
function with phase transform weights (GCC-PHAT), it is a reliable method and it can be 
used in single path situations [3, 4, 5]. GCC analyzes signals from several receivers and 
a reference signal, it makes use of pre-filters to improve performance in noise and uses 
cross-correlation between all the signals for the TDE. 
1.2 Sound Localization 
Conventionally, devices built specifically for sound localization use microphone arrays 
and beamforming for DOA estimation [6, 7]. A beamformer is essentially a spatial filter 
that uses the recordings from the microphone array to form a directivity pattern. Since 
each receiver is at a different position if they are at different distances from the sound, 
there is TDOA of the sound between the receivers. Conventional methods use 
microphone arrays consisting of many receivers. By having many microphones, accurate 
beamforming is possible and is the basis for many sound localization algorithms [8, 9, 
10]. 
Contrary to this, humans have only two ears. Sound localization ability in humans has 
been explored and the working principles were proposed in psychoacoustic studies [11, 
12]. Taking into consideration the shape of the head and the outer ear, the sound signal 
that gets to the middle ear is significantly changed from the original sound propagating 
through free space. This is described by the head-related transfer function (HRTF) and 
the resulting signal includes clues the auditory system can use for sound localization. 
HRTF is representation of the transfer function in frequency domain, in time domain it 
becomes the head-related impulse response (HRIR). As proposed by Lord Rayleigh [13], 
there are two main cues for sound localization that are involved in estimating the azimuth 
angle between the source and the head, and these are interaural time differences (ITD) 
and interaural level differences (ILD). Furthermore, spectral cues which come from the 
asymmetrical sound reflections from parts of the body, mainly the pinnae, help with 
direction estimation and are essential for elevation detection and front and back 
disambiguation.

Source localization from binaural recordings is also a well-studied problem and usually 
makes use of known HRTFs of the binaural receiver to localize the sound source [14, 
15]. In one binaural localization study [16] binaural cues of ITD and ILD are utilized along 
with binaural spectral magnitude difference standard deviation in a gaussian mixture 
model for source localization with convincing results. This hints at the possibility of 
utilizing multiple cues extracted from the receiver signals together for improved 
localization performance in reverberant or noisy environments. A simple algorithm for 
binaural localization was proposed in a study [17] in which cross-correlation is used to 
find the ITD and convolution with cross-channel HRIR is used for more accurate 
localization.  
Many sound localization studies limit themselves to direction angles within 180°, by 
which they avoid front-back ambiguity. This front-back disambiguation problem might be 
resolved by movement or rotation of the sound source or the head as suggested by some 
studies [18, 19, 20]. To allow for full 360° direction estimation without movements of head 
we need to look at the spectral cues. Research suggests that the HRTF can be used for 
front-back disambiguation without relying on movement [21, 22]. 
1.3 Echolocation 
When it comes to localizing reflected sound, there are sonar and similar systems utilizing 
microphone arrays [23]. A system consisting of two microphones to localize reflected 
sound has also been explored in a study [24], however in this study the two microphones 
were without head or pinna and therefore did not mimic human sound localization 
enhanced by the HRTF. Nonetheless, their experiments verified a technique for 
localization of the reflective surface using two receivers.  
Echolocation essentially involves multiple sound sources, which is a complex topic 
explored for both microphone arrays [25, 26] and for binaural receivers [27, 28]. Most of 
the studies of multiple source binaural localization have multiple uncorrelated sound 
source signals. In our study we have a single sound source of known position and one 
sound reflector with unknown position. The sound coming from the reflector is correlated

with the source sound and furthermore we can utilize a known excitation signal that we 
play into the sound source. 
In this study we propose a novel approach to binaural echolocation using HRTF. We 
consider ITD and other cues used for source localization and combine them with 
methods specific to localization of reflected sound. We create binaural recordings using 
simulation and we obtain recordings from measurements using HATS. We use a Head 
and Torso Simulator (HATS) to obtain binaural recordings and known HRTF datasets 
obtained with permission from another study [29] using the same HATS equipment. The 
environment is simplified to one large rigid wall inside an anechoic chamber. We assume 
a large vertical wall, so we only need to know the azimuth of the direction and are not 
concerned with finding the elevation as we might need for a point source localization. 
Furthermore, we study only a single source and singe reflector scenario and are not 
concerned with any other sound sources or reflections except for uncorrelated noise and 
for room reverberation. The main task for this research is to propose a signal processing 
method to be used for binaural echolocation and evaluate its performance.

2  Methods 
In this section, we explain all the methods we employed for estimating the location of the 
sound reflector and the data acquisition methods. There are just two values the estimator 
needs to find, the distance and the direction. What is available are the binaural recording, 
the excitation signal, the HRIR dataset and known values such as dimensions of the 
HATS-speaker setup and the speed of sound.  
 
Figure 2.1 Method overview block diagram 
Our employed method is a process consisting of five major steps. In the pre-processing, 
the signal is cut and filtered to improve the SNR. We use initial delay estimation to find 
the TDOA. Next is HRIR analysis, in which we use deconvolution and convolution 
combined with crosscorrelation to obtain dataset for direction and distance estimation. 
Direction is estimated from ITD, ILD and crosscorrelation coefficient, all three are 
combined in best-match estimation. Finally, the distance is estimated using the found 
direction and a new estimation of TDOA.

The acoustical model of our study consists of a HATS, a speaker and a reflective wall 
and takes place in a room. The sound travels from the sound source, the speaker, to the 
receiver, the HATS, by direct path and by reflection from the reflector, the wall.

2.2 Direction Estimation 
Estimation of the direction to the wall is done in an approach similar to binaural sound 
source localization methods [15, 16, 17], it relies on cross channel convolution and 
correlation. However, to localize the reflected sound instead of the source sound, we 
utilize deconvolution and windowing of the correlations. We use a combination of three 
localization cues for direction estimation. 
2.2.1 HRIR deconvolution and crosscorrelation 
In this study we use a dataset of measured HRIRs obtained in a previous study [29]. This 
dataset has binaural HRIRs for azimuth angles from 0° to 360° in 5° steps and elevation 
angles from -40 to 90° in 5°steps, but for this study we are interested only in the 0° 
elevation angle, therefore we use 72 binaural HRIRs. 
We employ a process similar to one described in [15] in which receiver signals from left 
and right ears are deconvolved with a HRIR dataset to obtain a signal approximating 
original signal, then the deconvolved signals from left and right channels are 
crosscorrelated to find which HRIR produces highest correlation magnitude and the 
azimuth corresponding to that HRIR is the estimation of the direction. However, we

2.4 Excitation Signal 
In this study we use a linear sweep for the excitation signal. As described in an HRTF 
research [31], using frequency sweep has some advantages as compared to other 
signals. We chose to use linear sweep for its deterministic nature, which is good for 
reproducibility of the results, and for the fact that linear sweep has a flat frequency 
spectrum, therefore no frequency normalization is required in the signal analysis. The 
signal length used was 0.5s. We studied the frequency range to find the effective 
frequency range of the estimation.  
2.5 Preprocessing 
The recording is cut to start of the source signal, since the two receivers are synchronized, 
both channels are cut to the same time. A band-pass filter is used to treat the signal, the 
cutoff frequencies are set by the frequency band of the excitation signal. Furthermore, 
we explore the option of using a time-variant bandpass filter to treat the signal, making 
use of the known change in frequency of the sweep signal with time. The upper and 
lower cutoff frequencies are set by the excitation signal and the maximum TDOA of 
interest, which is based on the maximum wall distance set for the experiment. We used 
a sixth order Butterworth filter for the band-pass filter and a second order Butterworth 
filter for the time-variant band-pass filter, both were applied with zero-phase filtering by 
forward-backward filtering [32].

4  Discussion 
The experiment of this research relies on data obtained from simulation and from 
measurement. The measurement data is limited, and the measurement was done in just 
one room with a certain reverberation and with a wall that could have been closer to a 
large and rigid wall. That along with using a speaker which may have had inaccurate 
frequency response furthers the difference between the simulation and the measurement. 
The correlation of the excitation signal and the reflected signal is much lower in the 
measurement than in the simulation, which leads to high errors in methods using the 
excitation signal, the GCC shows significant improvement over just the CC using 
excitation signal. In the end autocorrelation is sufficient for the simple TDE of a single 
sound reflection. GCC would likely be the better choice for an excitation signal that does 
not have a flat frequency spectrum. In future studies, more attention should be given to 
the properties of the reflective wall to make sure it can be matched with the simulation. 
In the direction estimation the ICP estimator seems to provide the most accurate 
estimations compared to the other two. This is likely due to the front back ambiguity the 
other two estimators are limited by. The slight difference in the peaks of the ILD estimator 
in Figure 3.6 is most likely caused by the attenuation of the pinna having a different profile 
for front-facing and for back-facing orientations. But the combined estimator has better 
performance than just the ICP estimator which shows that the ITD and ICD estimators 
have a positive impact on the combined estimator. The most optimal way to combine 
them might be something worth investigating. In one study [16] a similar combination of 
direction estimators for source localization was employed in gaussian mixture models. 
As for the excitation signal exploration, this study is limited to a single signal type. For 
the specific HRIR set we have found a frequency range that has good performance. 
However, if this study was done using recordings from measurements the result might 
differ. The distance estimation becomes accurate for frequencies above 3.5kHz. This is 
likely due to the fact of large wavelength of lower frequency sound. The direction has 
more effective ranges. The accuracy is good around 4kHz and around 12kHz. Since the 
signal is convolved with the HRIR, the attenuation caused by the transfer function is likely 
the reason for this result. A study [30] also makes a clear link between frequency of the 
signal and use of different cues (ITD, ILD and spectral cues) in the direction estimation. 
The signal used does not have to have a flat frequency spectrum. A signal with optimized 
frequency spectrum could perhaps be created for maximal effectiveness.

5  Conclusion 
This study was set to propose and evaluate a method for binaural echolocation using 
HATS. Existing methods for binaural sound source localization and microphone array 
echolocation were researched and used as a basis for a novel method. The proposed 
method proved to work with performance comparable with SOTA methos for binaural 
sound source localization. We described a method using three distinct estimators for 
direction. The combination of the three proposed direction estimators was shown to have 
better performance than each of the estimators alone in most cases and can detect the 
reflected sound direction in full 0 to 360° azimuth including front-back disambiguation.  
We also estimated the frequency range of the linear sweep excitation signal that results 
in higher performance of the direction and distance estimation than a broadband sweep. 
We proposed utilizing the time dependent frequency of linear sweep excitation signal for 
filtering out some of the noise and reverberation from the recorded signal by using a 
time-variant bandpass filter, using the filter shows an increase in estimation performance 
in low SNR conditions. 
While this study does propose a robust method for binaural echolocation of a single wall, 
it is far from being a full method for mapping the environment using sound. Next step 
could be to move on from a single reflective surface to multiple surfaces. Also, more 
information could be extracted from the echo, such as the elevation angle, the reflection 
properties of the material and the shape and velocity of the object.
