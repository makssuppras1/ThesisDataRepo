ABSTRACT 
The purpose of this study was to explore a gap in the literature concerning the evaluation of 
entrepreneurship education programs with a non-venture intention objective. To gather the insights 
into this subject a case study methodology is applied. In this exploratory case study the success of two 
of the student innovation support programs at DTU Skylab are evaluated: Skylab Ignite and the Skylab 
incubator.  
The case study consists of two phases: the data collection phase and the evaluation phase. In the data 
collection phase all the information needed for the evaluation is gathered through interviews, 
observations and document review. Apart from the information needed, it was found that no concrete 
objectives for the Ignite and incubator programs were formulated, as well as no content-based 
evaluation methods used to assess the programs.  
In the evaluation phase the product-customer fit between the programs and the identified target 
groups is assessed during sessions run in collaboration with the program coordinators. This was done 
using the developed evaluation method based on the Value Proposition Canvas. In this adaptation the 
implicit and explicit needs of the target groups are compared to the implicit and explicit learning 
outcomes of the programs. With implicit needs representing the learning outcomes students 
themselves might be unaware of that they need to learn about. And explicit needs representing the 
learning outcomes students have expressed they want to learn about. 
The practical results of this evaluation of Ignite showed a global fit between the needs of the target 
groups and the learning objectives offered, indicating a successful program design. The evaluation of 
the incubator program showed a misfit between the learning objectives offered and the needs of its 
participants. This is a reflection of the lack of objective for this program. This can cause the program to 
offer the wrong features to offer community, which do not fulfill the need the students express. 
Another result of the evaluation is the insight into the effect of communicating the right learning 
outcomes to the right target groups. By using the explitic learning objectives  in the communication 
concerning the program, target groups looking for ways to meet their explicit needs can see if this 
program fulfills them.  
As a result of the study, practical value was provided for DTU Skylab in the form of concrete 
recommendations on how to improve their programs and create concrete missions and content based 
evaluations.  
 
The main result of the study is found in the insights addressing the a gap in the literature concerning 
the evaluation of entrepreneurship education programs with a non-venture intention objective. As a 
case study methodology was used, the results of the study are not generalizable. They do provide 
insight into the topic of successful innovation support design and offer subjects for potential future 
studies. The main general insights are the confirmation of the importance of setting concrete 
objectives. Furthermore, taking into account both implicit and explicit needs when designing a 
program allows for both the content to be relevant and the students to feel heard and motivated. The 
explicit learning outcomes identified using this method can also be used to ensure that the 
communication about the features offered appeals to the intended audience by matching their explicit 
needs.  
==PAGE:4==
 
 
 
4 
Ultimately, the adapted VPC model provides a new perspective on the assessment of educational 
programs. This perspective allows the definition of success to be the confirmation that the right 
learning outcomes were offered. To evaluate the success of the program in regard to the resulting 
skills and knowledge instilled in the students, the combination of product-customer fit and classical 
measurement tools could be considered. This would require further exploration of the potential of the 
adapted VPC model as an evaluation method.

1. INTRODUCTION 
Sometimes a little bit of help goes a long way. When it comes to innovation and the development of 
new products, getting the right support can accelerate the creation of new companies and products. 
This kind of innovation support happens in different ways: ranging from advice through mentorship 
programs, courses focusing on specific skills and brainstorming sessions with an industry partner, to 
more tangible resources such as office space and money. An example of an innovation support service 
is a business incubator. This type of facility aims to promote new venture creation and early-stage 
growth by offering space, office services and business development assistance (Allen & McCluskey, 
1991). In other words, an incubator is successful when it helps create successful new ventures. 
 
There are also innovation support programs within educational institutions contributing to teaching 
students about entrepreneurship. With entrepreneurship education being a developing field of 
research, there is a lack of agreement as to the definition of entrepreneurship education, and its 
objectives (Duval-Couetil, 2013; H. Y. H. Wong & Chans, 2021). However, the creation of new ventures 
can be argued to be one of the main ones, creating economic and social value through technological 
development and new jobs (Ndou et al., 2019a; H. Y. H. Wong & Chans, 2021). This is further 
emphasized by the available field-specific program evaluation methods, which aim to measure the 
intention a student has to start a new venture (Moberg, 2014).  
 
Most scholars view other educational objectives than venture creation as more important, such as 
increasing the entrepreneurial spirit or helping entrepreneurs grow (Mwasalwiba, 2010). However, 
literature regarding designing and evaluating successful programs around these objectives is in short 
supply. This follows the lack of consistency in pedagogical approach, learning objectives and learning 
outcomes in course design across programs (Duval-Couetil, 2013; Streeter et al., 2002). This indicates a 
gap in the current literature that this study aims to explore: assessment methods for non-venture 
intention objectives in entrepreneurship education programs.  
 
DTU Skylab 
To explore the topic of assessing entrepreneurship education programs with a non-venture intention 
objective, a case study was performed. In this case study the student innovation support programs at 
the Innovation Hub Skylab at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) are observed to gather further 
insights into how the design of their programs can be evaluated. In 2020 the Skylab Innovation Hub 
was opened with over 5000 square meters of facilities such as prototyping labs, meeting rooms and 
open spaces for events.  
 
Skylab offers different programs to both students and researchers: Skylab Ignite, an acceleration 
program; Skylab Funding, which helps apply for external grants; The incubator, a place for start-ups to 
work on their products; and Skylab Pilots, where companies, researchers and students are matched to 
work on the development of innovative solutions.  
 
The mission is to help students develop an entrepreneurial mindset by having them test out ambitious 
ideas in an environment where it is safe for them to fail and learn from this. Management at DTU 
Skylab has expressed their interest in evaluating their programs to examine if they are offering the 
educational value they intend. This suggests venture intention is not the intended outcome.

Research questions 
To explore the research gap concerning assessment methods for non-venture intention objectives in 
entrepreneurship education programs within the context of the case offered by DTU Skylab, the 
following research question has been formulated.  
 
How can educational student innovation support programs without a venture intention perspective be 
evaluated? 
To gather the insights needed to answer the research question, case study methodology is applied. In 
this exploratory case study, the success of the student innovation support programs at DTU Skylab is 
explored. To do so an evaluation method based on an adaption of the Value Proposition Canvas was 
developed.  
 
The case study consists of two phases: the data collection phase and the evaluation phase. In the data 
collection phase, all the information needed for the evaluation is gathered through interviews, 
observations and document review. The information needed is divided into four sub-questions: 
 
1. What are the Skylab programs and goals? 
2. What are the target groups for each program? 
3. What are the explicit needs of each target group? 
4. What are the implicit needs of each target group? 
With the outcomes of these questions programs chosen can be evaluated. With this evaluation 
recommendations for a potential re-design will come forward, as well as a general view of the success 
of the programs. With this, the study will provide practical value for the organization. 
 
The outcomes of the case study will then be used to determine insight gathered on the topic of 
assessment methods for non-venture intention objectives in entrepreneurship education programs. 
Here the potential benefits of the designed evaluation model will be discussed, as well as future 
studies suggested.  
 
The objective of this study is to provide insight into what are important aspects of educational-focused 
innovation support programs for pre-startups and start-ups. As well as ideas on what future research 
can be done on the topic. Furthermore, practical value is created for DTU Skylab through the results 
and recommendations following the evaluation of its programs. 
 
 
Before diving into the case study, however, a literature study to explore the gap concerning 
assessment methods for non-venture intention objectives in entrepreneurship education programs is 
presented in the next chapter.

2. LITERATURE STUDY 
 
This literature study consists of three separate parts. In the first section, the research gap described in 
the introduction is explained. By researching the topics of entrepreneurship education, program 
design, objectives, and assessment methods the research gap concerning the assessment methods for 
non-venture intention objectives in entrepreneurship education programs is corroborated. The second 
section dives into the topic of the potential societal value created through entrepreneurship education 
without a venture intention objective. This study is executed to show the importance of further 
research into how to successfully design entrepreneurship education programs for other objectives 
than venture creation. This is to indicate the relevance of the following case study. In the third section, 
a high-level overview of teaching methods and tools used in entrepreneurship education is provided, 
as to show the understanding that lies at the basis of the interpretations performed in this thesis. 
 
2.1. 
Evaluation of educational programs on entrepreneurship 
In the introduction, the initial question about the design of successful innovation support programs 
was posed as the basis for this thesis. This question came up based on the empirical experiences of the 
researcher and the interest of the case study company. Therefore, it was important to evaluate how 
much is already known on the subject, and what type of research in this area would potentially provide 
new insights or new avenues of inquiry for following studies. To do so, first, the concept of 
entrepreneurship education is explored, as well as the importance of goal-oriented program design. 
This concludes with an observation of the difference in objectives of entrepreneurship education when 
looking at different perspectives. Assessment tools to determine how effectively these objectives are 
reached are discussed, leading to a conclusion on what gap there is in this area of research.  
 
2.1.1.    Entrepreneurship education 
Educating in entrepreneurship is described as a relatively new field. In the 1970s a few business 
schools started teaching entrepreneurship courses. By 2005 over 1600 colleges and universities have 
entrepreneurship programs, with these numbers only continuing to grow(Fellnhofer, 2019; Kuratko, 
2005). Across the world, governments, universities, and other institutions are spending large amounts 
of money on the development of entrepreneurship education programs (Aboobaker & Renjini, 2020). 
In the 1990s a movement started that aimed to expand entrepreneurship education and move it to 
other disciplines than just business, with the academic interest following (Duval-Couetil, 2013; Streeter 
et al., 2002). However, the research into the discipline is still growing and it is suggested that as an 
academic field it is still striving for more maturity as a field of study, with a consensus on its conceptual 
and methodological principles (Brazeal & Herbert, 1999; Duval-Couetil, 2013; Kuratko, 2005; Neck & 
Greene, 2011a, 2011b; Solomon et al., 2002).  
 
As recent as 2021 a review on learning outcomes in entrepreneurship education concluded that there 
is a lack of agreement as to the definition of entrepreneurship education, and how it should be taught 
and assessed(H. Y. H. Wong & Chans, 2021). The core of this debate would be the understanding of 
whether entrepreneurship education is supposed to teach the skills to be able to create a venture or 
to instill a mindset of opportunism, proactiveness, innovation, and risk tolerance(Caird, 1991; Duval-
Couetil, 2013; Henry et al., 2005; Kirby, 2004). This has resulted in a lack of consistency in course 
design across programs when it comes to pedagogical approaches, learning objectives, and learning 
outcomes(Duval-Couetil, 2013; Streeter et al., 2002). 
==PAGE:12==
 
 
 
12 
Learning outcomes can be defined as what the learners know or can do after completing a specific 
program(Adam, 2004; Allan, 1996; H. Y. H. Wong & Chans, 2021). In other words, these learning 
outcomes show what the program has achieved in terms of its educational goals and are essential 
when it comes to demonstrating the value created(Duval-Couetil, 2013). Assessing this value or impact 
of a program is important in all educational fields, but with the limited consistency and diversity in 
approach in the field of entrepreneurship it is even more essential to be able to provide evidence of 
the obtained knowledge and skills by its students(Duval-Couetil, 2013). Regardless of the direction the 
program design takes, the assessment of the learning outcomes will allow determination and 
demonstration of the value created. 
 
2.1.2.    Importance of program design  
With the growing emphasis on educational evaluation(Duval-Couetil, 2013),  Pittaway et al. (2009) 
state that assessment should be based on the implication With the growing emphasis on educational 
evaluation(Duval-Couetil, 2013), Pittaway et al. (2009)states that assessment should be based on the 
implication“that educators know what it is that students are supposed to learn and how students learn 
it” (Pittaway et al., 2009). 
 
This again hints at the connection to program design which is part of the assessment: the program is 
designed to teach students a certain learning objective in a certain way, which should then be the basis 
for the assessment. In the same way, the perspective of the program has to be considered in its 
evaluation. A course designed with a focus on social entrepreneurship versus multidisciplinary 
collaboration as its perspective may have very different outcomes or differ in pedagogical techniques 
and intended participants(Duval-Couetil, 2013). Having clear program-level objectives throughout the 
design stage ensures that courses within the same organization work towards similar outcomes, even 
if their students, methods, and stakeholders differ. It also ensures that the needs of the students, 
employees and other stakeholders meet the objectives of the relevant organizations i.e. department 
or university. This consensus on learning outcomes throughout the different levels is particularly 
important within the field of entrepreneurship education due to the diverse approaches to teaching it 
contains(Aboobaker & D, 2020; Duval-Couetil, 2013). 
 
This shows the importance of understanding the learning outcomes and creating fitting learning 
objectives in the program design phase, also from an organizational perspective (European 
Commission: Enterprise and Industry, 2008a). The degree to which it achieves these objectives as 
learning outcomes can then be used as a measurement of its effectiveness, defined as its ability to 
successfully produce the desired result(Effectiveness Noun - Definition| Oxford Advanced Learner’s 
Dictionary at OxfordLearnersDictionaries.Com.). 
 
2.1.3.    Entrepreneurship education objectives  
Entrepreneurship education programs can have different objectives. The European Committee names 
the following as examples: “developing entrepreneurial motivation among students, training students 
to set up a business (planning, networking, selling, finding resources, etc.), developing the 
entrepreneurial skills needed to identify and exploit opportunities” (European Commission: Enterprise 
and Industry, 2008). 
 
 
==PAGE:13==
 
 
 
13 
The overlap between these objectives would be that they have to do with preparing and motivating 
students to set up a company. Entrepreneurship has been identified as a large driver in economic 
growth (Almodóvar-González et al., 2020; Pradhan et al., 2020; Prasetyo & Kistanti, 2020)and the 
European Union has included raising the level of entrepreneurship and innovation in their Europe 2020 
Strategy for economic growth(Pradhan et al., 2020) through new ventures and job 
creation(Mwasalwiba, 2010). This perspective explains that, from a governmental policy point of view, 
the increase in venture creation is one of the main objectives of entrepreneurship education, which 
heavily influences universities and other educational institutions(Aboobaker & D, 2020; Estrin et al., 
2016). The educational objective that would lead to an increase in entrepreneurial activity is 
mentioned as venture intention. Venture intention indicates the likeliness of the student to create a 
venture or engage in entrepreneurial activity after they have completed the educational program. 
 
This objective relies on the positive correlation between entrepreneurship education and 
entrepreneurial activity, which multiple studies have indicated (Aboobaker & D, 2020; Barba-Sánchez 
& Atienza-Sahuquillo, 2018; Unger et al., 2011). However, some scholars point out problems with the 
research done(Duval-Couetil, 2013; Gorman et al., 1997; Matlay, 2005; Solomon et al., 2008). The 
results have been indicated as being mixed, inconsistent, and inconclusive. Problems include the need 
for more longitudinal studies, a lack of control groups in the research design, and an inherent bias in 
the samples chosen. Most studies base their conclusions on the study of students who have chosen to 
enter entrepreneurship focussed programs. Therefore, they show at least a pre-existing interest in 
venture creation if not venture intention(Duval-Couetil, 2013). This shows that the correlation 
between entrepreneurship education and venture intention has not irrefutably been proven. 
However, venture intention still forms the basis for many programs, as well as its evaluation methods. 
Other sources would suggest there are variations in objectives for entrepreneurship education are 
emerging, showing entrepreneurship education can have other positive effects than the creation of 
new start-ups(Gibb, 2002a; Hannon, 2005; Moberg, 2014; Sarasvathy & Venkataraman, 2011). 
 
A highly cited literature review has evaluated different articles on entrepreneurship education and 
found the distribution(Mwasalwiba, 2010): 34% of scholars researched see the objective of 
entrepreneurship education as creating or increasing entrepreneurial attitudes, spirit, or culture. 27% 
view new venture creation and an increase of jobs as the objective, while 24% associate 
entrepreneurship education with helping local entrepreneurs grow and improve . Only 15% see the 
objective as a way to pass on entrepreneurial skills to its students.  This shows that among scholars the 
venture creation objective is a lot less important than among governmental institutions.  
 
Even that last statement seems to be subject to change. The European Commission has stated the 
following in their Expert Group report on ‘Entrepreneurship in Higher Education, Especially In Non-
Business Studies’: “While the creation of graduate start-ups is therefore a desirable outcome, it should 
not be forgotten that entrepreneurship is also (and equally) about successfully managing innovation 
and growth. In existing business and entrepreneurship programmes very often only the start-up aspect 
is considered, while the skills and knowledge needed to manage the growth phase of a small business 
are neglected” (European Commission: Enterprise and Industry, 2008).  
This suggests a new avenue of learning objectives, namely growth management, which would require 
a potentially different educational approach, although it can be argued this would fit better in 
management-oriented education.  
 
==PAGE:14==
 
 
 
14 
Even though most scholars seem to see an emphasis on teaching attitudes, skills and helping existing 
entrepreneurs improve, the majority of programs still seem to focus on venture creation(Mwasalwiba, 
2010). This is followed by growth management, entrepreneurial financing, and other programs with 
business model creation at its core(Gibb, 2002b; Küttim et al., 2014). This makes sense, as the 
governmental perspective and the money connected to it will probably have a big influence on policy-
making within educational institutions(Mwasalwiba, 2010). That venture creation is a big objective can 
also be found in the assessment methods that have been reviewed(Kolvereid & Isaksen, 2006; Moberg, 
2014; Norris F.  Krueger & Brazeal, 2017). With cognitive psychology becoming a bigger influence in 
how entrepreneurship programs are evaluated, the main topic again has been how entrepreneurship 
education can raise students’ intention to create new ventures(Kolvereid & Isaksen, 2006; Moberg, 
2014; Norris F.  Krueger & Brazeal, 2017). Though without long-term impact research it is complicated 
to evaluate the effect of a program on the actual venture creation.  
 
2.1.4.    Assessment methods  
Before diving into the venture intent assessment, a quick general view at assessment methods. To 
determine the success of a program, or its effectiveness, a fitting assessment method has to be used. 
Program specific assessment methods can be created, following six steps as suggested by Palomba & 
Banta (1999):  
 
1) Agreeing on the goals and objectives for the program;  
2) Designing a planning for the evaluation;  
3) Involving different stakeholders;  
4) Choosing and re-designing data collection methods and assessment instruments;  
5) Adapting the program based on the findings from the first assessment round; and  
6) Reexamining the assessment process.  
But even within this process one can see that general data collection and assessment instruments are 
chosen based on the direction taken from the program objectives and input from stakeholders. 
Traditional evaluation methods exist which can be chosen from, depending on the program objective. 
For example, measuring cognitive skills achieved in finance or strategy by asking students to develop 
and present a business plan. However, when it comes to specific entrepreneurial non-cognitive skills or 
soft-skills such as creativity and self confidence in managing uncertainty, it is more difficult to measure 
the effect of the education received(Danish Foundation of Entrepreneurship, 2016; European 
Commission: Enterprise and Industry, 2008a). In 2009 Pittaway et al. concluded a review of the 
literature by saying assessment practice was a major gap in the field of entrepreneurship education 
research, with only few of the developed assessment instruments validated or widely used(Duval-
Couetil, 2013; Pittaway et al., 2009). However, there is one concept multiple studies have determined 
to be a useful basis for an assessment method.  
 
Self-efficacy is a person’s belief that they can accomplish a certain task and is rooted in social cognitive 
theory(Moberg, 2014; Newman et al., 2019). This has proven to be a fitting theoretical perspective to 
measure the successfulness of educational programs(Mauer et al., 2017; Moberg, 2014). It plays a big 
part in a person’s choice, level of effort and perseverance: the more an individual believes they can 
accomplish a task, the more inclined they are to pursue and persist in performing the task(Moberg, 
2014). The reason that it is determined to be a good educational evaluation tool is that it fits the 
perspective that education is supposed to provide individuals with knowledge and skills within a 
==PAGE:15==
 
 
 
15 
specific area(Biggs et al., 2022; Moberg, 2014). This suits one of the two definitions of entrepreneurial 
education as well: providing the skills and knowledge to be able to create a venture. While some 
researchers use general self-efficacy (GSE) in their measurement tools(Judge et al., 2001; Newman et 
al., 2019; Scholz et al., 2002), most experts agree that self-efficacy is domain specific: this way the 
measured confidence in the accomplished task can be field specific(Bandura, 1997; Newman et al., 
2019). This type of entrepreneurial task-specific self-efficacy is called entrepreneurial self-efficacy, also 
known as ESE. 
 
2.1.5.    Entrepreneurial self-efficacy (ESE) 
ESE can show the influence entrepreneurial education has on the students’ entrepreneurial behavior. 
Multiple studies indicate that a person with high ESE has higher entrepreneurial intentions and 
behaviors than someone with low ESE(Barbosa et al., 2007; Mauer et al., 2017; Mcgee et al., 2009; 
Moberg, 2014; Zhao et al., 2005).  ESE shows an individual’s perceived behavioral control, which is an 
indicator for their intention to perform a certain behavior, following the theory of planned 
behavior(Newman et al., 2019). This makes it a fitting assessment method to indicate the effectiveness 
of a particular program in increasing the venture intention of its students(Newman et al., 2019). It has 
also been specifically suggested that performing a pre- and post-measurement with ESE can give 
educators better insights into how to improve their programs’ effectiveness(Mcgee et al., 2009). 
ESE is measured using surveys which inquire about the self-efficacy of specific tasks and/or concepts.  
 
The most widely used ESE measurement tools is a 22-item scale developed by(Chen et al., 1998; 
Moberg, 2014). This scale consists of questions within 5 sub-dimensions that are supposed to measure 
the self-efficacy in terms of entrepreneurship tasks: marketing, innovation, management, risk-taking 
and financial control. However, a comment on this scale is that it is relatively general within the field of 
management, not specific to entrepreneurship (Bandura, 1986; Newman et al., 2019).  
 
The following often used scale tried to develop a more domain specific tool. The scale of DeNoble, 
Jung, and Ehrlich (1999) is supposed to capture“the entrepreneurial skills that are uniquely different 
from managerial skills”, covering six sub-dimensions: developing new product and market 
opportunities, building an innovative environment, initiating investor relationships, defining core 
purpose, coping with unexpected challenges, and developing critical human resources  
 
The most recent version is developed by McGee, Peterson, Mueller and Sequeira (2009). This was 
developed as it was argued that“there remain inconsistencies in the definition, dimensionality, and 
measurement of ESE”. The scale takes different phases of the start-up process into account and is 
aimed for both nascent as experienced entrepreneurs, resulting in the following four sub-dimensions: 
searching, planning, marshalling, and implementing(Mcgee et al., 2009; Moberg, 2014). 
 
These three scales all have different perspectives on what the essential dimensions of 
entrepreneurship are, but all use the ESE to evaluate the entrepreneurship education in terms of 
venture intention(Markman et al., 2017; Mcgee et al., 2009). However, it is argued that venture 
intention should not be of concern for university education. Their main purpose should be to enhance 
knowledge and equip students with relevant skills, not to enhance intentions(Biggs et al., 2022; 
Karlsson & Moberg, 2013; Moberg, 2014). Researchers argue that with societal changes in the last 
decade, entrepreneurial skills are a necessary ability for all individuals, having a growing influence on 
career development and vocational behavior. These skills are necessary both when they choose to be 
==PAGE:16==
 
 
 
16 
self-employed, but also as employees. For example when working on corporate entrepreneurship and 
innovation or in the social sector(Charney & Libecap, 2000; Moberg, 2014; Newman et al., 2019). 
There is universal use for individuals with qualities taught when educating in the entrepreneurial 
mindset, such as creativity, proactivity, and risk-tolerance (European Commission: Enterprise and 
Industry, 2008a). These qualities would currently not be measured effectively by a scale that focusses 
on venture intention.  
 
2.1.6.    Research gap 
With a lack of consensus on the definition of entrepreneurship education and its objectives, the 
importance of program design with specific learning outcomes is more crucial to show the value 
offered. To measure the effectiveness of these programs, field specific measurement tools are 
necessary. However, the existing tools such as ECE only work to measure venture intention, which is 
not always relevant to the objective of the program. This confirms the gap in the research as to 
assessment methods for non-venture intention objectives for entrepreneurship education programs.  
With venture intention having been one of the biggest objectives in entrepreneurship education, 
especially due to the governmental interest in this, this gap would not be much of an issue. However, 
as described, with societal changes in the last decade, there is an increasing interest in teaching 
entrepreneurial skills without the objective to increase venture intention. This perspective shows the 
importance of doing more research on non-venture intention objectives. Therefore, it will be further 
explored in the following section.  
 
2.2. 
Societal value through entrepreneurship education 
One of the main outcomes of the last section, 2.1.6, was the dominance of venture intention as a 
program objective within entrepreneurship education and its assessment methods. This was linked to 
the connection between venture intention and venture creation, which in turn is suggested to have a 
large positive influence on the economy. But due to societal change it seems venture intention might 
no longer be the main objective of entrepreneurial education, something researchers agree with. 
However, the question remains what positive societal influences entrepreneurial education has when 
not aiming for venture intention. In other words, why is it important to do more research on non-
venture intention objectives? This topic is what will be explored in this section.  
 
2.2.1.    Changing perspective 
The main tendency still seems to be that entrepreneurship education results in graduates with higher 
venture intention, who then go on to create economic and social value through starting new 
enterprises, create more jobs and cause technological development (Ndou et al., 2019b; H. Y. H. Wong 
& Chans, 2021). This contributing to society then has a positive effect on national development, which 
would be why governments should support entrepreneurship education and influence its learning 
objectives(H. Y. H. Wong & Chans, 2021). For long this has been the view of a lot of scholars and policy 
makers. However, recent reports from the European Commission and local governments such as the 
Danish ‘the Strategy for Education in Entrepreneurship’ suggest that this perspective is changing.  
 
In 2008 the European Commission presented a final report from their expert group titled: 
Entrepreneurship in higher education, especially in non-business studies(European Commission: 
Enterprise and Industry, 2008b). This report advises that boosting start-ups is not the only benefit to 
==PAGE:17==
 
 
 
17 
be had from entrepreneurship education. It helps students become more creative and boosts overall 
self-confidence, which is in line with what the principle of self-efficacy suggests. Developing 
entrepreneurial capacities and mindsets would then help them successfully enter the labor market.  
 
The Danish government agrees with this and takes this vision one step further. It aims to develop 
students' knowledge of entrepreneurship and their ability to act entrepreneurially(Danish Ministry of 
Science et al., 2010), with a broad definition of what entrepreneurship entails. It is rooted in the 
principle of value creation, whether it is economic or not. The aim is for more people to understand 
the process of innovation, that is, where the observation of an opportunity leads to the development 
of an idea, implementation and ultimately value creation. Ultimately, the Danish Government works 
from the perspective that“Our future competitiveness is strengthened by the fact that through the 
education system we stimulate young people's ability to think innovatively, see opportunities and 
translate ideas into value”(Uddannelses- og Forskningsministeriet, 2013). 
 
 This can happen in the form of starting one’s own business, or in an existing company by creating new 
processes or products that can be of value to the organization (Danish Ministry of Science et al., 2010).  
This change in perspective might also signal an opportunity for change in the focus of most programs, 
or at least allow for other objectives to get more opportunities. When most focus from government 
and policy makers is on venture creation, creating programs with a different objective would lack 
support. But change starts with a vision (Gill, 2002). With support or even leadership from 
governmental institutions to define the purpose of new programs with these objectives, there is also 
the opportunity for more research in these fields. Both into the following objectives, and into how to 
create effective policy in this regard (Ratten & Usmanij, 2021). 
 
2.2.2.    Positive effects of entrepreneurship education objectives 
As mentioned before, scholars have defined multiple objectives behind designing an entrepreneurship 
education program. Mentioned are the following objectives: creating or increasing entrepreneurial 
attitudes, spirit or culture; venture and job creation; helping local entrepreneurs grow and improve; 
passing entrepreneurial skills on to its students (Mwasalwiba, 2010).  These are relatively general 
terms, and hard to translate to practical contributions, especially when it comes to creating or 
increasing attitudes. In the same paper these objectives were concluded on, the researcher translated 
them into three categories of education based on what educators intent to achieve: Educating for, 
about, and in entrepreneurship (Co & Mitchell, 2006; Hytti & O’Gorman, 2004; Kirby, 2004; 
Mwasalwiba, 2010).   
 
Educating for entrepreneurship would aim to create an entrepreneur, or in other words to prepare 
someone to start a new venture. It is aimed at both current and nascent entrepreneurs (Co & Mitchell, 
2006; Hytti & O’Gorman, 2004; Kirby, 2004; Mwasalwiba, 2010)and the objective is to provide them 
with the skills and attitude to start a business(von Graevenitz et al., 2010). Therefore, this category 
covers all of the general objectives mentioned before: creating or increasing entrepreneurial attitudes 
or mindset, helping existing entrepreneurs grow, passing on skills and most of all, venture creation. As 
discussed before, this is considered by policy makers the most desired outcome and has the potential 
of creating economic growth and technological progress(Acs & Szerb, 2007; Stel et al., 2005; Sternberg 
& Wennekers, 2005; von Graevenitz et al., 2010; P. K. Wong et al., 2005), through, for example, job 
==PAGE:18==
 
 
 
18 
creation, high investment readiness of founders and a better performance of university spin-
offs(Dietrich, 1999; Harhoff, 1999; Reynolds et al., 1994; Shane, 2004; von Graevenitz et al., 2010).  
 
Programs focused on teaching about entrepreneurship aim to create a general understanding of the 
concept of entrepreneurship(Hytti & O’Gorman, 2004; Mwasalwiba, 2010). This is aimed to make 
students have a better understanding of what entrepreneurship entails, which would be in line with 
understanding the entrepreneurial spirit or culture. Therefore, this objective would be adjacent to the 
first one mentioned in the initial categories: creating or increasing entrepreneurial attitudes, spirit or 
culture. The potential benefit of this would be to make non-entrepreneurs more understanding to the 
challenges and opportunities of entrepreneurship. The World Bank states that these people will be 
able to influence both an entrepreneur’s opportunities as their ability to effectively use their skills and 
resources(Islam et al., 2001; Mwasalwiba, 2010). In practice, this would mean policy makers being able 
to make more fitting grant-policies for example, or financers being better able to understand business 
proposals. On its own it might not have a large impact, but it is a supporting function to the ones being 
educated for entrepreneurship and would potentially expediate the entrepreneurial process for some 
founders.  
 
The aim of educating in entrepreneurship would be to use some of the positive traits of entrepreneurs 
within an existing organization(Dreisler et al., 2003; Henry et al., 2005; Hytti & O’Gorman, 2004; Kirby, 
2004; Mwasalwiba, 2010). It takes the objectives of passing on entrepreneurial skills and the increasing 
of entrepreneurial attitudes and culture and applies it to jobs that are already in place in their 
organization. The benefit of this would be that individuals take more responsibility of their learning 
and career life (Charney & Libecap, 2000; Hytti & O’Gorman, 2004; Moberg, 2014; Newman et al., 
2019). This would include attitudes such as creativity, pro-activity and risk-tolerance and skills 
including product development and marketing(Mwasalwiba, 2010). This fits the argument of other 
researchers that entrepreneurial skills are becoming a necessary ability for all individuals(Charney & 
Libecap, 2000; Moberg, 2014; Newman et al., 2019). This also could be a way to create intrapreneurs: 
entrepreneurs who are not self-employed but work within a larger organization. Ultimately, having 
these skills as an employee might open up options throughout their career, and according to Hytti & 
O’Gorman (2004) makes individuals take more responsibility of their learning and career 
life(Mwasalwiba, 2010).  
 
One more term fitting this list was suggested by Kirby (2004), educating through entrepreneurship. 
This describes the process where students acquire the necessary skills and understanding of the 
entrepreneurship process through the creation of a new venture(Mwasalwiba, 2010). However, this is 
less of an objective as it seems to be a teaching method, therefore it is not often used in the same 
group. 
 
2.2.3.    Societal impact 
Jones and Matlay agree that there is an opportunity to broaden the perspective of entrepreneurship 
education and move beyond economic impact as main objective(Jones & Matlay, 2011). New forms of 
entrepreneurship are starting to be recognized, which include social entrepreneurship and sustainable 
entrepreneurship. These provide different perspectives on what entrepreneurship means, but all 
provide positive societal impact.  
 
==PAGE:19==
 
 
 
19 
Successful entrepreneurship does not have to be about creating jobs and revenue. Social 
entrepreneurship can be defined as the field in which entrepreneurs tailor their activities to be directly 
tied with the ultimate goal of creating social value (Abu-Saifan, 2012). This is often done without profit 
being a motive for the organisation. This form of entrepreneurship per definition provides societal 
value and is getting more attention(Abu-Saifan, 2012). 
 
However, as a research topic it lacks consensus on its definition and theoretical framework (Abu-
Saifan, 2012; Barendsen & Gardner, 2004; Bruyat & Julien, 2001; Weerawardena & Sullivan Mort, 
2006). As stated before, without clear consensus on the learning outcome and how students will learn 
it, it is tough to create an effective educational program (Duval-Couetil, 2013; Pittaway et al., 2009).  
Although there is theoretical overlap between regular entrepreneurship and social entrepreneurship, 
there are specific topics that are only taught when concerning the latter. For example, management of 
non-profit organisations, social innovation and what challenges one has to overcome when creating a 
business model that is not centred around revenue increase(Abu-Saifan, 2012).   
 
One way to aim for revenue but still create societal value is sustainable entrepreneurship. This is an 
emerging concept that has been gaining more research attention over the last decade, with scholars 
connecting traditional entrepreneurship with the needs of society and the environment to establish 
this new field of study (Cohen & Winn, 2007; Terán-Yépez et al., 2020). Sustainable entrepreneurship 
can be defined as “the discovery, creation, and exploitation of opportunities to create future goods 
and services that sustain the natural and/or communal environment and provide development gain for 
others”(Patzelt & Shepherd, 2011; Volkmann et al., 2021) although there is no consensus on this 
definition.  
 
These opportunities mentioned in the definition often overlap with the UN’s Sustainable Development 
Goals (SDG’s) and tend to aim for -partial- solutions for fundamental societal challenges(Pacheco et al., 
2010; United Nations General Assembly, 2015; Volkmann et al., 2021).  
For example, climate change, gender equality or the availability of clean water and sanitation. 
Sustainable entrepreneurship combines the economic, ecological, and social dimensions of 
sustainability into the core of its business models, for example using the Triple Layered Business Model 
Canvas tool(Joyce & Paquin, 2016; Schaltegger & Wagner, 2011). This shows that sustainable 
entrepreneurship differs in both intentions as in use from traditional entrepreneurship.  
 
These two forms of entrepreneurship show how a different approach can achieve beneficial results. 
However, they also require a different educational perspective to create the necessary skills for an 
individual to take this approach, which required more research to fitting pedagogical methods and 
tools. 
 
2.2.4.    Individual impact 
The effects of entrepreneurship education on an individual’s development and career potential are the 
last perspectives to explore when diving into the reasons why different approaches to 
entrepreneurship education deserve more research. This is done from the perspective of human 
capital. According to Schultz: “Human capital is the set of skills, knowledge and other attributes that 
contribute to an individual’s capacity to perform productive work” (Aboobaker & D, 2020; Schultz, 
1980). Human capital can be viewed as a societal thing, where it describes the total economic value of 
==PAGE:20==
 
 
 
20 
the workforce, or as an individual factor. In this case we look at the individual knowledge and skills 
created by entrepreneurship education that are not related to venture creation.  
 
There is a lot of knowledge to be gathered from entrepreneurship education that would later on be 
applicable to other career paths. General business knowledge, such as business strategy, risk, 
marketing, finance, and business idea development (Matlay, 2008; H. Y. H. Wong & Chans, 2021). 
Students can develop knowledge in a more specific field, such as engineering management or legal 
knowledge (Pluzhnik et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2019; H. Y. H. Wong & Chans, 2021). It is also suggested 
students gather knowledge on decision making and problem solving techniques, as well as 
interpersonal communication and negotiation(Rafiei et al., 2019; Tang et al., 2014; H. Y. H. Wong & 
Chans, 2021). These would also be reported as skills learned. 
 
Other multi applicable skills include but are not limited to: initiative-taking, strategic thinking, 
negotiation capacity, opportunity identification and opportunity evaluation (Pluzhnik et al., 2018; H. Y. 
H. Wong & Chans, 2021). These are identified as entrepreneurial competencies, but could be 
applicable in a number of professions. The same goes for analysing, networking, adaptability, 
creativity, risk-taking propensity and sense of initiative (Kissi & Somiah, n.d.; H. Y. H. Wong & Chans, 
2021). More generic competencies would include creativity, communication skills, teamwork and 
critical thinking (H. Y. H. Wong & Chans, 2021).  
 
These lists of knowledge and skills show all the learning outcomes that students can gain from 
entrepreneurship education which could benefit them in a non-venture creation career. This human 
capital can be suitable in multiple available career paths, both as described for about entrepreneurship 
and in entrepreneurship.  It shows that the learning outcomes of entrepreneurship education can be 
beneficial in many ways, even though creating objective specific programs would be a more efficient 
way to achieve this human capital. There is even a positive influence found of entrepreneurship 
education on the graduate’s employability. There is one more benefit for students who follow 
entrepreneurial education: Discovering they might not be suited for a life as an entrepreneur after all 
(von Graevenitz et al., 2010). Successfully matching students with a fitting career and giving them the 
right tools to start that career is also one of the goals of education. Rae and Woodier-Harris even 
suggest this offers students an advantage in terms of identifying and developing their career options 
and goals (Matlay et al., 2012; H. Y. H. Wong & Chans, 2021). 
 
2.2.5.    Research importance 
This section shows the outcome of the research done on the benefits of different objectives on 
entrepreneurship education, other than venture intention. With the current changes in perspective 
from policy makers creating an opportunity for change in program objectives, this would be the right 
moment as well to do more research into what these programs should entail and how they should be 
taught. Changing the objectives from a for entrepreneurship perspective to about or in 
entrepreneurship would allow for other parts of the workforce to benefit from the skills and 
knowledge that come from entrepreneurship education. Social and sustainable entrepreneurship are 
new fields that fall inside the for objective, to create entrepreneurs, but require a different approach 
which necessitates its own further research on teaching methods. The societal benefits of these new 
approaches to entrepreneurship would substantiate this. And ultimately, the individual human capital 
created for non-entrepreneurs through entrepreneurial education seems beneficial enough to allow a 
wider audience to benefit from this. In the form of fitting education that is.

All these objectives create new benefits, other than economic growth. This shows that it is important 
to do more research into the distinctive objectives for entrepreneurship education other than venture 
intention and how to teach them, assess them and use them to societies benefit. 
 
 
2.3. 
Main teaching methods of entrepreneurial education  
Now that the gap in research in terms of assessment of non-venture intention entrepreneurship 
education is determined, and the importance of further research has been shown, this section will 
explore the state of art in teaching methods. Linking back to the statement “that educators know what 
it is that students are supposed to learn and how students learn it”. 
 
It is suggested that next to the objective of the program, the method of teaching can also influence its 
effectiveness. While the teaching method is not under investigation in this thesis, as it is focused on 
evaluating the fit between a program’s objective and outcome, its pedagogical methods are relevant 
context to this process.  
 
2.3.1.    Teaching methods 
In education there are many different pedagogical methods used to pass on knowledge or skills to the 
receiving students, teaching key concepts, providing hands-on experience, and studying real-world 
examples of successful and unsuccessful businesses (Bae et al., 2014; Klein & Bullock, 2006). Each method 
can be more or less suitable to achieve certain objectives than the other. These methodes were 
divided into three categories further discussed here: Theory-based learning, learning-by-doing, and 
case studies.  
 
Concerning theory based methods. All approaches mentioned here are theory based, but these 
pedagogical methods focus on passing on the concepts and principles of entrepreneurship through 
lectures, the reading of research or performing research through for example business model analysis. 
The objective these methods serve is to pass on knowledge on entrepreneurship. This then can be 
used in identifying market opportunities, developing a business plan, and understanding key financial 
concepts(Fiet, 2001). Theoretical frameworks and models such as Porter's Five Forces(Porter, 1989), 
the SWOT analysis (Bell & Rochford, 2016), and the Business Model Canvas (Osterwalder & Pigneur, 2010) 
would be tools that could be discussed when using such methods. 
 
The second category would be learning-by-doing. When using these methods the focus is on passing 
on skills and knowledge through experience. This is done by simulating a practical, real-world 
experience as closely as possible in a environment where it is safe to fail. Methods can be simulation, 
role-playing, mentorship, and action learning together with other people, whether they are classmates 
or external stakeholders (Lackéus, 2015; Rasmussen & Sørheim, 2006). The goal is to give students an 
experience as close to reality as possible, such as a sense of what it would be like to develop a start-up 
from idea to business. A lot of different tools can be used depending on the situation to be simulated. 
For example Disciplined Entrepreneurship(Aulet, 2013) and the Lean Startup methodology (Ries, 
2011). 
 
==PAGE:22==
 
 
 
22 
The last category are case study based methods. When applying these methods students analyse real-
world cases and aim to learn from their successes or failures. With this students can see the effect of 
certain choices, or can compare theories with reality. The objective is to combine theory with reality 
and teach students to analyse and develop alternative solution for management- or organisational 
problems (Farashahi & Tajeddin, 2018). Tools often used for these methods are for example the Business 
Model and Value Proposition Canvas (Osterwalder & Pigneur, 2010), Cost-Benefits analysis, and PEST 
Analysis (Aguilar, 1967). 
 
While different in their approach, all methods to contribute to one or multiple of the earlier described 
entrepreneurship education objectives. All teach skills, pass on knowledge or aim to instill a mindset 
and understanding either usable for the creation of a venture or to be beneficial in other career paths. 
 
2.3.2.    Tools and frameworks 
When discussing the different methods, various tools and frameworks used in entrepreneurship 
education were already mentioned. These tools are not bound to one method, but can be part of or 
even the basis of an entire course. There are many tools out there, coming from the business and 
management perspective such as the SWOT and cost-benefit analysis. But also tools specifically 
designed to help navigate the entrepreneurial process. There was no research found on which 
frameworks or models are used most.  
 
As there are too many to describe them all, the choice was made to discuss the following three: The 
Business Model Canvas (Osterwalder & Pigneur, 2010), the Lean Startup(Ries, 2011) and the Disciplined 
Entrepreneurship 24 Steps to Succes (Aulet, 2013). These were chosen as they are some of the most 
recenty developed tools, assuming they therefore represent the most recent developments in the field 
of research.  
 
Business Model Canvas 
The Business Model Canvas (BMC) is a visual tool that helps entrepreneurs and business leaders map 
out their business models. It is presented in the book ‘Business Model Generation’ by Osterwalder & 
Pigneur (2010). The canvas consists of nine building blocks that represent the key building blocks of a 
business model, which are: Customer Segments, Value Proposition, Channels, Customer Relationships, 
Revenue Streams, Key Resources, Key Activities, Key Partnerships and Cost Structure.  
The BMC can help understand the current business model, working out all the segments of a 
functioning business and putting them in one overview. This can then be used to detect potential gaps 
or opportunities for improvement. It can also be used to communicate the business plan to others. The 
flexibility of this tool makes it applicable in any stage of a business, from idea generation to scaling up.  
 
The same writers used the BMC as the basis for a second book, describing another visual tool: the 
Value Proposition Canvas. In this book the building blocks Value Proposition and Customer Segments 
from the BMC are  expanded upon. The tool helps to understand the value a product or service offers 
to its customers, how it addresses their needs and problems, and evaluate to what degree it fits the 
current market(Osterwalder et al., 2015). The two tools are designed to be used together and 
complement each other. However, they require some knowledge on how to use them right. Although 
the books offer a good guide through this process, it is not a overarching framework to approach the 
entire star-up process, like the approach described in the next book. 
 
==PAGE:23==
 
 
 
23 
Lean Startup 
The Lean Startup is a methodology for developing and launching new products or businesses. It was 
first introduced by Ries (2011) in his book "The Lean Startup: How Today's Entrepreneurs Use 
Continuous Innovation to Create Radically Successful Businesses". This methodology is based on the 
idea that startups can't afford to spend a lot of time and resources developing a product or service 
that then might not fit with what customers want. Therefore, this approach advocated creating an 
Minimal Viable Product (MVP) which is a simplified but functional version of the product. This then can 
be used for testing with customers and figuring out if it fits their needs and if the business model 
works. This gives the start-up valuable feedback that can be used to adapt the product if necessary 
without having to have spend money on a a fully developed product first. This translates into the 
following three steps: build, measure, learn. Build an MVP based on the business idea, use customer 
testing to gather data that measures the success of the idea, and then use that data to analyse the 
business plan and adapt where needed. The process then loops back with a new build phase until the 
result is a fully validated business. 
 
Though this loop entrepreneurs are encouraged to continuously experiment with their ideas and 
validate their assumptions about the customer, product and market. And embrace making mistakes, 
especially if they result in better products. Through this, the Lean Startup methodology provides an 
approach and a mindset to its users that is used throughout the early-stage start up process, moving 
from idea to product. The last framework covers the process up until scale-up, and has devided this 
process up into 24 steps. 
 
Disciplined Entrepreneurship 
Disciplined Entrepreneurship by Aulet (2013) provides a 24-step process that guides entrepreneurs 
through the process of creating a successful start-up, from idea generation to scaling up. It covers 
topics such as product design, customer development, and fundraising and is divided into four main 
stages: Idea Generation, Customer Development, Value Proposition Development and Business Model 
Development. The purpose of these stages is to help entrepreneurs stay focused on the next step to 
take without losing sight of the whole process. 
 
The Disciplined Entrepreneurship methodology is based on the idea that starting a successful business 
is a disciplined and structured process, that can be learned and improved. Through its steps it provides 
a set of tools and frameworks to help entrepreneurs to create a viable business model. It also 
emphasizes the importance of understanding custome needs and problems and creating a value 
proposition that addresses them. Furthermore, it follows the view of the Lean Startup in the 
importance of creating an MVP and testing it as soon as possible.  
 
In conclusion, it can be seen that there is some overlap between the tools described. The preface of 
the Disciplined Entrepreneurship book even acknowledges the influence of both the BMC and the Lean 
Startup, among others. However, they differ in their focus and their level of structure. The BMC is used 
specifically for the business model process, and provides a visual tool to use to create one. The Lean 
Startup focusses on the early stage of the start-up process, and provides a general iterative process 
description. Disciplined Entrepreneurship goes into more detail and addresses the whole process from 
idea to scaling the business through a step-by-step process guide. It shows they serve different 
purposes, and understanding when to use which tool is an essential part of learning them.

entrepreneurship education, as to show the understanding that lies at the basis of the interpretations 
performed in this thesis. With this, the literature study is complete.

3. METHOD  
This chapter present the method and steps taken to create a fitting research design, taking into 
account case study methodology, the context of the case study and the development of a fitting 
evaluation method. 
 
3.1. 
Case study 
Once the initial research question was established, a fitting methodology was to be found to use as a 
basis for the research design. The objective of the study is to explore the topic of assessment methods 
for non-venture intention objectives in entrepreneurship education programs within the context of the 
real-world example offered by DTU Skylab. 
 
Using (Yin, 2018) dual definition of the method, a case study seems to fit this objective:  
“A case study is an empirical method that investigates a contemporary phenomenon (the “case”) in 
depth and within its real-world context, especially when the boundaries between phenomenon and 
context may not be clearly evident.” 
and:  
“A case study copes with the technically distinctive situation in which there will be many more variables 
of interest than data points, and as one result benefits from the prior development of theoretical 
propositions to guide design, data collection, and analysis, and as another result relies on multiple 
sources of evidence, with data needing to converge in a triangulating fashion.” 
 
In other words, using a case study approach means observing an actual real-world example or ‘case’ 
that is relevant to the research goal, taking into account the context of the example. Triangulating 
fashion refers to the need to gather data from multiple sources to compare. This can lead to a myriad 
of insights, where a theory guided methodology and multiple data points are needed to come to a well 
substantiated conclusion.  
 
This leads to the choice to use the case study methodology as an overarching approach. Due to the 
nature of the question, only observing the outcome of the current program design would give limited 
insights into what makes a program successful and what not. By observing a real-world situation and 
its context, a general understanding of innovation support design and an evaluation of the specific 
programs considered in this case is pursued. This can be indicated as an exploratory case study, fitting 
the interest expressed by the case organization to evaluate their programs.  
 
Some arguments against using a case study approach include that the investigation of a single case 
study cannot lead to generalizable insights, or that it creates a bias towards the researchers pre-
conceived understanding of the concept that is to be observed (Denzin & Lincoln, 2011).  In this 
instance these arguments are negated as there was limited prior knowledge on the subject, nor is the 
aim to create generalized theories based on this case study alone. The main scientifical value will be 
found by using the insights from this study as the basis to form hypotheses for further research. 
Furthermore, a practical value is provided to the case study organization in the form of an evaluation 
of the efficiency of their programs. When discussing biases later in this chapter, the methods chosen 
to deal with biases will also be discussed. As certain biases are case-related, an initial case description 
will give the context to help explain the choices made.

3.3. 
Evaluation method 
In this chapter the development of the evaluation method used as a basis for the research design is 
discussed.  
 
3.3.1.    Grounded theory 
As mentioned in section 2.1.2 of the literature study, an assessment should be based on the programs 
objectives. Therefore it was important to first start the data collection phase to gather information on 
the relevant programs within Skylab and their objectives. This was then used in an inductive process of 
data collection and theory building to create a fitting research design, which is centered around the 
developed evaluation method.  
 
This qualitative research method is called grounded theory (Glaser & Strauss, 2017). It is a strategy 
that allows to discover theory by systematically obtaining data or working the data ‘from the ground 
up’ (Yin, 2018). This is a way to arrive at a theory suited to the needs of the study and it allows to 
explore the subject and related excisting theory simultaneously. This exploration allows for the 
recognition of concepts and hypothesis which are then backed up by research and excisting models. 
There have been doubts to the credibility of this method, as it is a very flexible approach and prone to 
bias (Oktay, 2012). To remedy this, potential bias has been assessed as part of the research design. 
This will be presented in section 3.4 
 
Through this inductive process it was decided to approach the programs offered as a product or 
service. This opened up the opportunity to address the success of the programs as one would do with 
a product evaluation, by exploring the product-customer fit. To do this, the Value Proposition Canvas is 
used. 
 
3.3.2.    Value Proposition Canvas 
The Value Proposition Canvas (VPC) is an visual analytical framework created by Alexander 
Osterwalder which is used in product design. This tool is used to test if the value proposition fits the 
needs of the customer segment chosen. This fit is an essential part of a successful business model 
when following the Business Model Generation method(Osterwalder & Pigneur, 2010). In general, a fit 
happens when what is being offered through the product ‘fits’ what the customer wants from a 
product.  
 
A VPC consists of two parts that are positioned on opposite sides of each other: the customer profile 
and the value map. The customer profile describes what a customer wants from a product divided in 
three categories: Jobs, Pains, and Gains. Jobs describe what the customer is trying to get done in their 
==PAGE:28==
 
 
 
28 
lives. Pains are the bad outcomes, risks and obstacles related to these jobs, or in other words, 
problems the customer could solve using your product. Gains are the outcomes a customer wants to 
achieve, or the benefits they are looking for when considering a product. Each customer group can 
have their own customer profiles with specific jobs, pains and gains. 
 
The value map shows the Products and Services, which contains the features that the value proposition 
is built around. The Pain Relievers describe how the product relieves the pains of the customers. The 
Gain Creators show how the product creates the gains the customer is looking for. The customer 
profile and map are then compared to evaluate if the features described offer the right pain relievers 
and gain creators to satisfy those pains and gains indicated in the customer profile.  
 
This can be used as a tool to build a new product, but also to evaluate an existing one. By comparing 
the VPC that is created based on the profiles of the current customers to one that is created to 
describe the current product, one can evaluate if the current features are necessary or not, and which 
aspects of the product the customer is missing. In other words, this method can help evaluate the 
effectiveness of a product. 
 
Therefore this method was considered to be used as the evaluation method for the Skylab innovation 
support programs, to measure how well the program design fits the customer profiles of the students 
participating. However, there was one perspective missing in this approach.  
 
3.3.3.    Student needs 
When observing the programs at Skylab, one instance stood out in relation to the customer profiles. 
Students had joined one of the programs based on assumptions of what they would learn by doing so 
but later it turned out they were being taught things they did not know they needed. This would show 
a customer pain they themselves were not aware of. As shown with a quote from one of the 
participants: “I did not know that I needed this”. The insight this student refers to turned out to 
completely change their perspective on product development. At the same time, certain topics they 
came to learn about in the program were insufficiently covered. This shows although students might 
know what they want to get out of an innovation support program, they may not know what they 
should be learing, and what would provide them with the most value. Both aspects can be used to 
formulate their pains and gains.  
 
Someone who would know might be a specialist in the field of education offered. The statement 
“Educators know what it is that students are supposed to learn and how students learn it” (Pittaway et 
al., 2009) as discussed in the literature study in section 2.1.2 emphasises this. This would suggest an 
specialist in the field would be best suited to determine the students pains and gains, or at least the 
ones they should have. However, as the educational programs discussed here are voluntary, seeing 
their self-expressed learning needs met motivates them to continue working (Williams, 2011). Even if 
these are different from the pains and gains a specialist would choose.  
 
Looking into students needs and course design revealed a precedence for using customer needs as 
evaluation input. In the study of English as a foreign language course, the students were actively 
involved in the continuous development of the programs as to ensure they were being offered 
learnings fitting their needs(Holme & Chalauisaeng, 2006). This analysis of needs was later further 
discussed in another study in the same field. Here it is questioned whether a specialists’ analysis can 
==PAGE:29==
 
 
 
29 
really identify the problems learners face (Liu et al., 2011). As (Long, 2005) suggests: “There is an 
urgent need for courses of all kinds to be relevant—and to be seen to be relevant—to the needs of 
specific groups of learners”. These studies were in the field of language education, but the precedence 
of using students’ self-expressed needs in such a way does seem to provide an opportunity to take into 
account both the specialists interpretation of the students pains and gains, and the students.  
  
3.3.4.    Adapted Value Proposition Canvas 
To be able to evaluate for both the specialists view on the students pains and gains, and the students, 
the excisting VPC is adapted. Instead of separate pains and gains, there will be one term for what a 
customer aims to get out of the product: needs. There are two types of needs: explicit and implicit 
needs. 
 
Explicit needs describe what is voiced by the students they want to learn about. These are often the 
reason students join a program, or look for specific input. Getting these needs met will help motivate 
them. On the product side these needs are met by Explicit learning outcomes, which represents what 
the students think they will learn from this product. In the data collection phase these needs are 
identified through observations and interviews with the student participants. 
 
Implicit needs describes what students themselves might be unaware of that they need to learn about 
in their specific development stage. These are often rooted in theory, and are identified by a specialist. 
On the product side these needs are met by Implicit learning outcomes, which represents what the 
specialist thinks the students will learn from this product. In the data collection phase these needs will 
be identified through interviews with the coordinators, who will pose as specialists. They will also 
emerge from observations and interviews with the students by identifying which needs they did not 
know they had.

3.4. 
Data collection and biases 
As a last step in research design before the data collection process could get started, the right 
programs and participants to observe have to be chosen. When using a grounded theory approach, 
selecting the right participants is important to collect relevant data as to observe things that will 
actually help to answer the research question(Glaser & Strauss, 2017). This is called a purposive 
sampling technique (Yin, 2018). Furthermore, certain choices were made to avoid bias in the research 
design as much as possible, both concerning the sampling choices as well as data collection methods 
(Yin, 2018).  
 
The main methods chosen for this data collection are direct observations and unstructured interviews. 
Both suit a case study methodology well for different reasons: the real-time observations allow for 
context to be part of the analysis, while not manipulating the events that are being observed. The 
strength of doing interviews lies in the fact that explanations for behavior can be found, as well as 
personal views and experiences. Both have weaknesses too, for example the presence or questions of 
the researcher can influence the actions and answers of the subject, also called reflexivity. This is dealt 
with by spending more time with the subjects than just doing the observations and interviews, with 
the goal of them getting used to the presence of the researcher while they are working or participating 
in events. Potential inaccuracies due to poor recall will be dealt with by taking notes while interviewing 
and observing, as well as recording meetings whenever possible.  
 
However, as only one researcher will be performing the data collection, choices in who to include have 
to be made to avoid selectivity(Yin, 2018), i.e. have a broad enough coverage of the experiences in the 
programs and the different customer groups. To still get enough data on the programs and customer 
groups that are going to be evaluated, the following choices were made: 
 
- 
The evaluation will cover the programs Ignite and the incubator. Within Skylab Funding there is minimal 
to no interaction between the organization and the students, as the program consists of an application 
and an interview. After this the main service is actually provided to the connected funds, who receive 
recommendations on who should receive the fund. This is why the Funding program is less relevant for 
this thesis. Skylab Pilots is also ruled out for this investigation, as it includes influences from researchers 
and SME to direct the students in their start-up journey. As it will be difficult to deduce what the effect 
of the Skylab program is and what comes from experiences with other influences, the choice was made 
to eliminate this program from consideration. Both Ignite and incubator are programs where active 
interaction with the students is part of the features, and outside influences are limited, this will give the 
best insight into the effect of a program design on its own.  
- 
Within the programs there are multiple groups participating. For Ignite the Spring 2022 group consists 
of 7 teams, while the Incubator has 17 teams throughout the spring semester. It was deemed doable to 
follow 2 teams per program, to observe their whole journey throughout their participation. With each 
team meeting once or sometimes twice per week, this would create ample opportunities for 
==PAGE:31==
 
 
 
31 
observations. Furthermore, sporadic unstructured interviews can take place to take into account 
experiences outside of those of the chosen teams.  
- 
The Ignite teams were chosen due to the fact that there were only two teams who had English as the 
language used during their coach meetings. As the researcher does not speak Danish, nor was it the 
intention to influence the meetings by requesting a language change, this seemed a logical conclusion. 
Unfortunately, one of the groups available was also enrolled in the Pilot program. This causes a bias, as 
it is impossible to fully split the influence of Ignite and that of the Pilot program.  
- 
Within the Incubator a similar choice was made, where the factor of choosing teams with the same 
coach was also considered. This was to take out the difference in experience due to the coaching style, 
as to keep the source of influence to the program itself as much as possible and observe comparable 
experiences. Furthermore, the intention was to choose groups that have had minimal other interactions 
with Skylab and its programs, so as not to combine influences. Also, teams including researchers were 
ruled out as to keep the focus on students’ educational experiences. 
- 
One last adaptation was made due to the fact that the coordinator of the incubator changed three 
times throughout the observation period. As coincidentally this person was also the assigned coach for 
the chosen groups, the observations were influenced. One of the groups chose this as a moment to take 
a hiatus from the incubator as well. This does result in a slightly skewed data collection, which is 
something that will be discussed in chapter 6 .  
This is what was done to minimize the bias going into the data collection process. Acknowledging that 
unknown bias is always present in research, considering the exploratory nature of this case study it is 
deemed to be enough to create the intended insights into the topic.  
 
3.5. 
Research design 
To explore the research gap concerning assessment methods for non-venture intention objectives in 
entrepreneurship education programs within the context of the case offered by DTU Skylab, the 
following research question has been formulated 
 
How can educational student innovation support programs without a venture intention perspective be 
evaluated? 
 
To gather the insights needed to answer the research question, case study methodology is applied. 
The case study consists of two phases: the data collection phase and the evaluation phase. Following 
the need for multiple sources mentioned in the  case study methodology (Yin, 2018), the conclusions 
will be based on three different types of data: Information from the students, information from the 
coordinators, and independent information from documents and theory. Three different data 
collection methods are chosen: direct observations, unstructured interviews, and documentation 
about the organization. Through these methods the information is gathered to use as input for the 
evaluation of the programs.  
 
 
==PAGE:32==
 
 
 
32 
The information needed has been divided into the following sub questions: 
 
1. What are the Skylab programs and goals? 
2. What are the target groups for each program? 
3. What are explicit needs for each target group? 
4. What are implicit needs for each target group? 
The data collection phase takes place throughout the period that the Ignite program runs: 10 weeks in 
the spring semester. This allows for a full analysis of this program, as the rest of the programs will run 
continuously. 
 
The second phase is the evaluation phase. This will consists of sessions organized in collaboration with 
the coordinators to evaluate their programs together. This will be done following the adapted VPC 
evaluation method as described in section 3.3.4.. The collaborative approach will allow for both their 
practical insights into the organization of these programs to be taken into account, as well as to create 
value for the organization in the shape of an initial adaptation of the programs based on the fit found. 
This does create a potential bias when the coordinators do not agree with the findings as input. 
However, this method was chosen to maximize the potential operational impact of this analysis on the 
organization. 
 
The objective of this study is to is to provide insight into what are important aspects of educational 
focused innovation support programs for pre-startups and start ups. As well as ideas what future 
research can be done on the topic. Furthermore, practical value is created for DTU Skylab through the 
results and recommendations following the evaluation of its programs.

Data collection process

4.2. 
What are the Skylab programs and goals? 
Throughout the data collection phase, a lot of time was spend at Skylab. Being present, observing the 
interactions and diving into the documentation that was made available, have brought the following 
insights into the organisation and its programs.  
 
4.2.1.    Skylab: a post-growth organisation 
As mentioned when describing the case study, Skylab as an organisation has gone through a period of 
fast growth in the last couple of years. It started off as a location for students to work on their 
innovative projects, and has grown into much more than that. Now catering to both students and 
researchers, they still offer fascilities to work on projects, but also multiple services and programs to 
follow. They state that their mission is to help develop an entrepreneurial mindset through thinking 
big, failing fast and scaling quicker.  
 
However, it seems there is no definition of what this entrepreneurial mindset entails, nor which part of 
Skylab contributes to this in what way. The premise for this study included a statement from one of 
the main managers within the organisation, that they would like to measure the educational value they 
offer their students with their programs. Little is documented on the practical goals the organisation 
has in this regard, nor is there a definition of the objective of the different programs availabele. 
 
This seems to fit the image of an organisation that has recently grown from a start-up like initiative to a 
full-fledged organisation with over 50 employees quickly. This proces is recognised as the scaling of an 
entrepreneurial venture as described by Picken (2017). The organisation of a start-up has to be 
adaptable, loosely structured and is often informal. When scaling up the organisation more structure 
and discipline is required, with policies and defined processes instead of ad hoc descicion making. 
==PAGE:34==
 
 
 
34 
Picken also describes typical hurdles organisations have to overcome in this process, including the 
setting of  a direction and maintaining focus(Picken, 2017). This means having a clear organisational 
goal, communicating this and determining concrete objectives that will contribute to this goal.  
 
Skylab seems to be in the middle of this transition, and running into the hurdle of having loosely 
defined goals and objectives. This makes it hard to evaluate for the organisation as a whole, if the 
value that is being created is the right one. Currently a semi-formal organisational structure exists, but 
without an overarching goal, each department determines its own place within the organisational 
direction. The direction for each program is roughly agreed upon, but when asking for program goals 
and objectives, each employee communicates different aspects. Therefore, the coordinators 
individually put their direction to the program, or take over the direction determined by the previous 
coordinator. The 2nd Ignite coordinator describes that Ignite was run the way it was because of a 
combination of factors: it is how it has always been, and due to operational concerns – such as limited 
resources or time. With three different employees fulfilling the role of coordinator for the incubator 
within the period of research, it was difficult to determine what the goal of this program was. 
However, in the following sections a summary of the direction and content of both programs will be 
given.  
 
4.2.2.    Ignite program details 
Skylab Ignite Spring 2022 started on the 24th of February and ran for 10 weeks. 7 teams of between 2 
and 5 people go through four workshops to gather knowledge and work on customer & value creation, 
team readiness, business readiness and funding & pitch readiness. It is aimed at teams between the 
idea and start-up phase. Between each workshop they can book a 1-on-1 coaching session with both 
their business coach and their technology coach, to give them personalised feedback and help them 
take the right steps in developing their idea. Ultimately during the finals all teams will pitch to have a 
chance to win one of the monetary prizes up to 25.000 DKK.  
 
During the observation period there were two coordinators involved, as the main coordinator was on 
maternity leave. Both put a different emphesis on the goal of the program. The temporary coordinator 
organised two seasons of Ignite, and after the first adapted one of the workshops to add more focus 
on customer validation. Her vision is for Ignite to accelerate start-ups through a customer centric focus. 
The goal is to have students do early testing with customers to validate their idea, and teach them to 
pivot. This helps achieve the secondary goal: help them create a fit between product and market, 
giving the best chance for success in her eyes. 
 
The main coordinator has a slightly different view. Ignite has to help students build a mindset about 
entrepreneurship. For her this means being able to take in feedback and adjust their ideas. This would 
fit the first coordinators approach, except with less of an emphesis on customer focus. She also 
indicates that due to her maternity leave she had time to think on the program, and realised it was run 
on the basis of how it used to be and operational concerns. When asking who the program is designed 
for, she replies that she does not know. She is convinced an evaluation of the program would be 
useful, and wants to figure out why it is designed the way it is and how it should be. Therefore, she 
added to the data collection phase by organising her own interviews and participating in the evaluation 
sessions.  
“I need to rethink how we do this”   
- 2nd Coordinator Ignite  
==PAGE:35==
 
 
 
35 
This shows the lack of why – the goal – and its influence on making the how – program design - 
uncertian. This makes it hard to communicate what the program is about and offers its participants. 
This can be observed when talking to the participants in the two teams that were followed; one team 
signed up for Ignite by accident, both did not know what to expect of the program. Even before a 
workshop they often did not know what they were going to learn or do, which caused them to make 
assumptions, be unprepared and at times be disappointed. 
 
4.2.3.    Incubator program details 
A similar situation is observed at the incubator. The incubator offered space to 17 teams throughout 
the observation period. These teams differ in size, field and development stage, but are between 
student-startup and real-world start-up. Each team is either assigned their own desks or can use the 
flex-desks in the incubator space. Furthermore there is free coffee and the teams can book meeting 
rooms and use the prototyping fascilities Skylab offers. These last two are also available to all other 
students at DTU.  
There is a weekly one hour meet-up where all teams perform a 1 minute pitch as to practise and keep 
each other up to date on development. Otherwise, this moment is used for external guests to give 
presentations on various topics, such as other incubators or funds. This moment can also be used to 
organise a short workshop, such as on legal issues or accounting. This is the primary contact moment 
for the program, and participation is encouraged but not mandatory. Each team is also assigned a 
coach, who they can book 1-on-1 sessions with. 
Teams get to stay at the incubator for a maximum period of 16 months, as long as they are engaged 
and present. At the kickoff it is said that there is supposed to be an evaluation of their participation in 
the program. However, the first coordinator indicates that in reality this does not happen, nor do any 
of the following coordinators mention wanting to do this.  
 
The incubator is run by one incubator manager, here called the coordinator. Within the observation 
period, 3 different employees fulfilled this role. This had an effect on the continuity within the 
program, but also made evident the lack of defined goal for the incubator, independent of who is 
currently program coordinator. The first coordinator aims to help them get out of the incubator as 
prepared as they can be, and does this by asking the teams to create an exit plan as soon as possible. 
The second coordinator says that the incubator should teach students how to swim on their own. In 
other words, offer a safe environment for them to try and fail before they do so in the real world. This 
is done by giving feedback and motivation through the confirmation they are doing something legit, 
but mosly allowing them to work and be there if they have questions.  
The third coordinator agrees with the goal to create a real-life experience where it is still safe to fail. 
However, he sees the way to reach that is through bringing together start-ups with different 
backgrounds and letting them learn from each other through a sense of community. 
 
These three approaches are not mutually exclusive, and the goals behind them are not extremely 
different. However, they put the emphasis on a different part of what the incubator has to offer. And 
that shows that there is no clear independent definition of this. This shows in the communication 
about the incubator, as what is offered tends to change or be relatively vague. This makes it unclear 
for the students applying, but even the students already in the incubator, what more the program has 
to offer than just the desk they have been assigned. 
 
==PAGE:36==
 
 
 
36 
Throughout the observation period the attendency at the weekly meet-ups started to drop. The 
interaction with the guests happened mainly between them and the Skylab employees present. The 
teams waited until the presentation part of the meet-up was over so they could ask if the guest would 
be willing to answer questions one on one. This negates the ‘learning by community’ principle as 
described. At the end of the observation period, one team showed up to the last meet-up. To use a 
quote from the second coordinator:  
 
“If we do not give a good view of the content or the content is not good, we will see that in the 
attendance” 
 
4.2.4.    Skylab overall 
The previous paragraph show that both programs struggle to set a defined goal and concrete 
objectives to indicate they are achieving this goal. Also, when putting the two programs and the 
outcomes as experienced by the students together it seems there is no clear path from Ignite to the 
incubator: a lot of teams are not ready for the incubator after Ignite. At the same time, there are 
teams in the incubator who have not taken part in Ignite and are now asking for input that is covered 
in that program. However, they are not being redirected to then attend the Ignite workshops. This 
shows the lack of an overall organisational goal and centralised direction. This makes it difficult to 
determine the success of Skylab as a whole. 
 
One thing that came forward during the data collection phase was that although Skylab communicates 
its success in terms of visitors, pre-startups and start-ups in their yearly fact sheet. The fact sheet of 
2021 can be found in Appendix I.  
Internally they use a different tool, the Net Promoter Score (NPS) (NICE Satmetrix et al., 2023). This is 
what the programs use to report to management. It is obtained by asking students to fill out a survey 
with questions that determine how likely this person is to recommend the experience to a friend. 
Although this does measure customer satisfaction, as a success measure it provides two problems: 
First of all, there is a chance the score is influence by a reflexivity bias. This means that people will 
answer in a way that they think is what the interviewer wants to hear(Yin, 2018). Especially since the 
amount of the people answering the survey is quite small, the feeling could be that the coordinator 
will know who sent in which survey. As a lot of students mention that being part of the community is 
important to them, it is unlikely they will risk upsetting one of the Skylab employees. 
The second issue is that although it might give a good idea of the overall customer satisfaction, it does 
not help indicate the areas of improvement as it does not take into account the content of the 
program. Nor is it a goal specific method, unless the only goal is customer satisfaction. Therefore it is 
not a useful method to gather data for evaluation of the success of the program.  
 
There is one other measurement tool used in both programs: the KTH Innovation Readiness Levels(IRL) 
(KTH Innovation, 2023). This is a visual model designed to determine the current position of a team in 
the innovation development stages, akin to the Technology Readiness Levels (TRL). However, this is 
currently only used sporadicly as a conversation starter with the teams on where they think they could

4.3. 
What are the target groups for each program? 
With a more complete understanding of Skylab as an organisation and the individual programs goals, 
the next sub question to answer concerns who the programs are designed for. Determining the right 
target groups is important when evaluating the program design: different groups of people can have 
different needs. As stated by the 2nd Ignite coordinator as well, there is a lack of clear target groups for 
the programs. This makes it hard to know what the program should be offering. Now that Skylab also 
wants to serve researchers with its programs, this has only become more complicated. As the 
programs globally aim to serve teams in different development phases, each program gets their own 
target groups.  
 
4.3.1.   Ignite target groups 
Two student target groups were identified within Ignite, with a clear difference between them. A third 
group was indicated by the 2nd coordinator, the researchers, but as that is outside of the scope of this 
study, they will not be further discussed here. 
 
The two target groups are identifiable by their background in entrepreneurship. A difference in 
experience was reported by students who had already followed entrepreneurship courses or even 
were following a full entrepreneurship focused masters program, opposed to students without this 
background. This distinction between entrepreneurship students and non-entrepreneurship students 
is one that can also be found in the literature(Mwasalwiba, 2010), and it is even stated that the De 
Noble ESE scale can differentiate between the two(de Noble et al., 1999).  
 
The group containing mostly students with a background in entrepreneurship courses found the 
workshops repetitive and got most of the value out of the opportunity to work with the coaches on 
specific questions they had. Team H even stated that they did not feel they were the target group for 
this program due to this. 
 
“The workshops were used as conversation starters more than anything else.”  
Teammember Team 2 on the value the workshops offered 
 
The teams where most participants did not have experience or a background in entrepreneurship 
indicated they got most from the knowledge obtained in the workshops, which they then discuss and 
make case applicable in the coaching sessions.  
 
“I did not know that I needed this” 
Teammember Team 1 on the first workshop 
 
Within the data collection, several teams were clearly part of the group coded S.W.EB. (Students With 
Entrepreneurship Background). This included Team 2 and for the extra interviews observed it included 
teams B, C, G and H. T 
The second group was coded as S.WO.EB. (Students Without Entrepreneurship Background). This 
group was found in Team 1, and in the extra interviews with teams A and E.  
The teams D and F showed a mix of the two groups, with D having some background and F having one 
member with a masters in entrepreneurship and 2 with no background at all. These teams show that 
the groups as indicated are not necessarily the absolute only types of teams participating in Ignite. 
However, they represent a simplification for the sake of the evaluation.

4.3.2.    Incubator target groups 
The groups within the incubator were less clearly distinguishable. As most teams were in a further 
development phase once in the incubator, they would already have some entrepreneurial background 
found in the process until then. The difference turned out to be less between the teams, but between 
different team members.  
 
As some team members, and sometimes entire teams, focus on the the technologicalside of the 
business and the practical challenges, such as getting specific answers to deeply 
technologicalproblems they are having and having a space to work on their prototype. Especially 
within a technologicaluniversity as DTU there are enough people who work on their start-up by 
focussing on perfecting their technology. This brings certain needs with it, but also a challenge in the 
coaching process in terms of getting these individuals to not forget the value of, for example customer 
validation.  
 
Other teammembers would be working on developing the business side of the start-up and focus on 
working out the business model and talking to customers. These are students either from a business or 
entrepreneurship educational program at the university, or technologicalstudents who have realized 
the importance of the business development and put their focus in this direction. They ask for help 
with determining the best next steps in how to grow, but might forget to develop the technology along 
side it. 
 
A wellrounded team in the incubator might have both types, but then the incubator still needs to be 
able to offer value to both. Therefore, it is about identifying the needs of the individuals within the 
team who have a different perspective.  
 
The first group was coded as having a Technical Perspective. Of both teams observed, Team 2 was the 
most focused on the technologicaldevelopment. However, the extra interview done showed the most 
clear person of this type. He claims he worked “more on technology than it should be” during his time 
in the incubator – extra interview. 
The second group was coded as the Business Perspective. This was mainly found in one of the 
members of team 1, who was also the most vocal. He would be the voice of his team in meetings with 
more people present, or would come alone to the incubator. His perspective is indicated in the 
meeting between team 1 and the 2nd coordinator: they ask for feedback on the next strategic steps 
and how to make a realistic development roadmap.  
 
One thing to be acknowledged is that the different needs within the incubator were less visible due to 
the lower amount of intense interaction moments and the lowering attendance throughout the 
observation period, including one team going on hiatus. Another factor is the switch in coordinators 
for three times, which also meant that the coaches for the observed teams switched. This will be 
further discussed in chapter 6.

5. PROGRAM EVALUATION 
With all the data collected, it was possible to put together customer profiles and product profiles to be 
able to do a evaluation. This was done together with the coordinators by facilitating multiple sessions 
using the adapted VPC model. This collaboration did influence the results, for example with the 
coordinators adding needs that did not come forward in the data collection process. This choice of 
method will be further explored in the discussion.  
 
5.1. 
Evaluation process 
The evaluation is split into three sessions per program, which were planned to fit into the schedules of 
the coordinators. For Ignite both the 2nd coordinator and the student assistant were present. The 
incubator sessions were run with the 2nd and 3rd coordinators. 
  
In each first session the focus was on identifying the customer groups and creating the customer 
profiles. Customer profiles are a visual representation of each target group containing the customer 
jobs as well as the implicit need in the top bracket and explicit needs in the bottom.  
In the second session the focus was on creating the a visual representation of the current product 
profile. Working from the identified features of the service offered, the value offered is split in a similar 
way as the needs. On the top part are the learning outcomes that represent the learnings offered by 
the program. On the bottom are the learning outcomes that represent the value that students come to 
the program looking for. 
 
In the last session the evaluation took place, where the comparison between the needs of the 
customers and the learning outcomes offered by the program was made. This resulted into insight 
concerning which needs are being met or not, and which parts of the product provide value or not. A 
visual representation of this method can be found in Figure 1 Adapted Value Proposition CanvasFigure 
1 in section 3.3.4.  
In the conclusion of these sessions the mission of the programs is adapted and further defined, which 
will be presented in section 5.4. 
 
The main outcome is an overview of the product profiles, where for every learning outcome it is 
indicated whether or not it fits a need of one or both of the target groups, using color coded crosses 
and checks. This shows whether the learning outcomes offered, provide a value fitting the needs of the 
customers. Main needs that were not being met were used to create new learning outcomes. 
  
Ultimately the insights gathered were used to review the features in the left window, resulting in a 
better understanding of what the product design should be. Another result were the customer profiles 
with crosses and checks indicating whether or not the identified need was taken care of.  
 
With the outline of the sessions and evaluation process explained, the results of the sessions for each 
program will be presented in the following sections.

cover the explicit need for more networking, and potentially being able to find the right technology 
connections. But it also could be organized in a way to find more potential co-founders, fitting the 
need for more employees or co-founders.  
 
The last realization from this evaluation comes in the shape of the features that are available for the 
incubator teams, but apparently not clearly enough. This is where the incubator manager, identified as 
the coordinator role in this study, needs to connect his program with the rest of Skylab. As for Ignite, 
there are technology specialists available who can be technology specific coaches, and there are other 
experts to provide active help with the specific tasks mentioned in the basic business skills cluster. 
Improving the use of this will improve the learning objectives offered, and therefore improve the fit 
between the program and the target groups. 
 
This concludes the outcomes of the evaluation of the incubator program. The conclusions of this 
evaluation and the resulting new mission definition will be discussed in the next section.

6. CONCLUSION AND DISCUSSION 
With both the evaluation based on the adapted VPC method completed, the case study, and with it 
this thesis, can come to a conclusion. In this chapter, the conclusion of the case study is discussed, 
with the specific results concerning the design of the Skylab innovation support programs found 
presented.  
 
Next, the conclusion concerning the research question will be presented, discussing general outcomes 
on the topic of program design and evaluation. This is followed by the discussion and limitations.  
 
Finally, the areas of future studies following this exploration will be described. 
 
6.1. 
Case study conclusion 
In chapter 5 the outcomes of the evaluation were presented. In these outcomes the following case 
specific conclusions are found. 
 
The first conclusion considering the evaluation of the student innovation support programs at Skylab is 
that there is a general fit observed between the learning outcomes and objectives of the program 
Ignite and the educational needs, both explicit and implicit, of its identified target groups. Although 
there are a few missed needs, minor adaptions to the program seem to make it possible to meet them 
all. This shows that Ignite’s program design is effective. The proposed improvements have been 
identified and used to design a second iteration of the program: Ignite 2.0 has been presented by the 
coordinator on the 17th of June 2022, showing the influence this study has had on the organization.  
 
One remark concerning the evaluation of the Ignite program however is the contradictions observed in 
the needs of its target groups. There are needs which are essential to one group, but where the 
content of the connected learning objective would be repetitive or even redundant for the other. 
Specifically, the basic business knowledge offered in the workshops is an essential part of the program 
for the S.WO.EB. teams, but students within the S.W.EB. group have expressed this created limited 
value for them or that it made them feel they were the wrong target group for the program.  
This shows that even though the conclusion of the evaluation showed it reached both target groups 
needs effectively, trying to serve mutually exclusive needs at the same time still will cause 
disappoinment among the target groups. It is recommended to revisit the question who Skylab wants 
to serve with its programs as to ensure fully effective results for the chosen target group.  
 
The evaluation of the student incubator program at Skylab, showed a misfit between the learning 
objectives offered and the needs of its participants. By looking at the misfit concerning “community 
feel” it is pointed out that there is an explicit need for this, and the coordinators claim to offer it. 
However, students still experience it as missing and the program does not communicate it is part of 
being in the incubator. This connects to the information found in the literature study about the need 
for a clear objective when designing entrepreneurship education. Without a clear, concrete objective, 
the understanding of what the learning outcome community exactly needs to be is missing. This can 
cause the program to offer the wrong features to offer community, which do not fulfill the need the 
students express. This can be found in the example of the meetups: where coordinators aim to create 
community by getting together and listening to content-focused presentations, the students have 
expressed an interest in success stories from other start-ups to learn from and get motivated.  
==PAGE:56==
 
 
 
56 
This also shows the importance of knowing who you design for and of understanding their needs: the 
coordinators have a different view on what offers community value than the students.  
Another result of the incubator evaluation is the insight into the effect of communicating the right 
learning outcomes to the right target groups, and with it managing expectations. The coordinators 
communicate what they think students are looking for when coming to the incubator as what they are 
offering. If this definition misses an important need that is indeed met in the program but not viewed 
by the coordinators as important, this causes unnecessary mismatches and misunderstandings of what 
it is that the incubator has to offer. This can result in the wrong target groups applying or teams 
getting demotivated and leaving the incubator due to unfulfilled needs. This fits the observed receding 
attendance and active participation of students in the weekly meetups.  
 
These results can be remedied by redesigning the incubator program from the perspective of its new 
mission, and by viewing this development as an iterative process. Just as when working on a start-up, 
following the cycle of designing, testing, and learning makes sense here. Basing the next evaluation 
cycle on the main needs and learning outcomes identified in the previous cycle will make each 
iteration more accurate, as it is more clear what to look for. The same goes for both the formulated 
mission statement: it represents the start of objective-based program evaluation, but can still use 
some improvements.  
 
A recommendation for the improvements at the incubator is to not try to adjust every slight mismatch 
between needs and learning outcomes all at once. Instead, focus on first adding the learning outcomes 
covering the main missing needs, represented by the colored boxes. These will increase the product-
customer fit regardless, and allow for the next iteration cycle to focus on smaller mismatches each 
time.  
 
The last conclusion on the case study concerns the complete organization of Skylab. From the 
organizational structure and its history of fast growth it became clear that Skylab finds itself in a 
transitional period at the start of scaling up. The lack of concrete program objectives was part of that, 
reflecting the missing organizational consensus on its educational goals. Without an overarching 
educational objective each program designs its own mission and learning outcomes, causing a 
potential gap in development stage between students who have finished Ignite and teams who are 
ready for the incubator. To ensure a logical passage from the first program offered to the second, it is 
recommended to further investigate this potential gap and decide on the intended alignment of the 
student innovation support programs.   
 
6.2. 
Research conclusion 
With the case study specific conclusions complete, it is time to zoom out and revisit the motivation for 
performing this case study. This was a case study on the topic of successful student innovation support 
with a non-venture intention objective in an educational context. This was reflected in the main 
research question:  
 
How can educational student innovation support programs without a venture intention perspective be 
evaluated? 
==PAGE:57==
 
 
 
57 
As the research to answer this consisted of a case study, the conclusions of this study are not 
generalizable. However, they do provide insight into the topic of successful innovation support design 
and offer topics and potential hypothesis for future studies.   
 
The first insight is the confirmation of the theoretical perspective, covered in the literature study, on 
the importance of setting concrete objectives, both concerning programs as well as overarching 
organization goals – when relevant. Evaluating the success of a design means measuring how effective 
it attains its goals. Without an objective that is connected to concrete and measurable learning 
outcomes, this is impossible. With the current field specific evaluation methods indicated in the 
literature study being aimed at venture intention as an objective, a different approach to assessment 
was succesfully developed and executed.  
 
The adapted VPC model provides a new perspective on the assessment of educational programs. 
Understanding what the learning outcomes fitting the objective are supposed to be, means 
determining what the student needs to learn, called implicit needs in this study. However, this study 
suggests it can also be approached from the perspective of the program as a service. Through this, the 
evaluation is designed as a product-customer fit: by determining what the student needs to learn, the 
features of the program can be evaluated on meeting these needs.  
This perspective allows the definition of success to be the confirmation that the right learning 
outcomes were offered. This does not take into account an individual’s ability to absorb the offered 
knowledge. A potential way to remedy this would be combining this adapted VPC method with 
classical measurement tools such as general self-efficacy surveys. This would both indicate if the right 
learning outcomes are offered and to which extent the teaching was successful in instilling confidence 
in the ability to perform a related task. A further exploration of the potential of this model adaptation 
and possible expansion of the approach is required. 
 
Viewing the program as a service can be used to take the customers’ needs as direction for the 
program design and thus its evaluation. This can be based partially on what an educator knows are the 
right learning outcomes, taking into account the educational objective, for the chosen customer or 
target group: the implicit needs. But the also takes into account the learning outcomes the customer is 
looking to obtain: the explicit needs. Understanding both what your customer is looking for and what 
they might need, from an expert’s perspective allows for both the content to be relevant and the 
students to feel heard and motivated.  
 
This would put emphasis on choosing and understanding the target groups when designing a program 
as to determine its learning outcomes, which are then used as metrics in following iterations of 
continuous evaluation and adaption. Target groups will be looking for their explicit needs to be 
reflected in the communication on what the program is about. Without understanding these needs, 
the wrong assumptions can be made about why students join this program. This will lead to 
miscommunication and potentially attracting target groups who are a mismatch with the value offered 
in the program. Consequently, when using the explicit needs of the misaligned target groups as basis 
for an evaluation you will fail to properly achieve a product-customer fit.  
 
To answer the research question; according to this study educational student innovation support 
programs without a venture intention perspective can be evaluated by first determining the objective 
and current learning outcomes. This is followed by identifying the target group for the program and 
==PAGE:58==
 
 
 
58 
their implicit and explicit needs. These needs are then compared with the offered learning outcomes. 
In other words, if the program effectively achieves what it was meant to do: offer students the learning 
objectives they need from this specific innovation support program.  
  
6.3. 
Discussion & limitations 
In this section the research design and outcomes are reflected upon, discussing the effect of the 
change in research design, limitations in data collection and the influence of the chosen collaborative 
method for the sessions.  
 
The premise of this study included the question posed by Skylab; if the educational value offered in 
their programs was what they aimed it to be. In other words, they wanted to evaluate their innovation 
support programs without a venture objective, looking at the educational learning results. This 
suggested that they had defined what the concrete objectives of these programs and the organization 
were in terms of the entrepreneurial mindset they wanted to create and which aspects they aimed to 
teach. Therefore, the study was intended to look into the evaluation of a specifically non-venture 
intention objective from an organization perspective, showing how well the objectives and outcomes 
of the programs fitted the organizations intent. 
 
However, when starting the study, it turned out that aside from individual visions there were no 
defined objectives nor learning outcomes available, neither on program- nor organizational level. This 
means that the outcome of this case study is less about evaluating an organization without venture-
intention objective then it is about evaluating programs without a clear objective, other than that it is 
not venture intention. This is what the research design was adapted to. This still provided insights for 
the organization into the performance of their programs as well as an understanding of the non-
venture intention evaluation process and potential evaluation methods.  
However, to fully explore the gap in research found in the literature study, performing a similar study 
with defined non-venture intention objectives on both program and organization level would be a 
necessary step.  
 
Due to the change in research design certain theoretical perspectives that would have been relevant 
have had to be left out. The initial literature study focused on entrepreneurship education, venture 
intention and the state of art in evaluation methods. However, the adapted research design shifted the 
emphasis more towards the content and practices of the programs to be evaluated. This would have 
benefitted from an expanded understanding of learning styles and pedagogic techniques in 
entrepreneurship education. This as to better understand what choices in learning objectives and their 
execution have which impact on the outcomes. However, there was a limitation on how much time 
could be spent to do a new literature study and this perspective was set aside for this study. Future 
studies on the subject of entrepreneurship education evaluation should take these subjects into 
consideration.  
 
Another limitation encountered in this study was the amount of interactions possible to acquire in the 
data collection process, especially considering the incubator program. Due to the lower amount of 
intense interaction moments and the reclining attendance throughout the observation period, 
including one observed team going on hiatus, the data on the needs of the incubator was less 
extensive than for the Ignite program, making the outcome less robust. Another factor influencing 
these insights is the switches in coordinators of the program, which also were the coaches of the 
==PAGE:59==
 
 
 
59 
teams observed. This led to a haphazard coaching process for them as well, potentially influencing 
their overall experience in the incubator, but definitively the number of opportunities to observe 
coaching sessions. More coaching sessions would have benefitted the understanding of the target 
groups, and potentially helped further define their specific needs. Therefore, it would make sense to 
view this evaluation as the first iteration, used to define which metrics need to be taken into account 
for future assessment. 
 
The last discussion point is to reflect on the collaborative method chosen for the evaluation sessions. 
By creating the results together with the coordinators of the programs the result was skewed towards 
their opinions and practical needs. This can be viewed in the added needs, such as those concerning 
regulations; they were added, not deemed as main needs, but still had to be addresses in the 
evaluated product profile. Although their insights were invaluable, it did create a result that would be 
practically usable for them, where the ideal theoretical model following the method chosen might 
have been different.   
 
For this research design this choice makes sense, with the following arguments:  
As the case study was executed following a research request by the organization itself, the aim of the 
study included providing value for the organization through the evaluation. Through the collaboration 
in the sessions this was assured to be achieved.  
 
The second argument is that with only one observer, the downside of the chosen data collection 
method was that it took a lot of time to gather enough data to be able to get a complete picture. For 
example, the entire program of Ignite had to be completed to fully understand the students’ 
experience. With this in mind, the coordinators’ input on validating the data found and potential 
missed perspectives taken from their experience proved to be invaluable in producing complete 
customer profiles. Furthermore, both researcher and coordinators got to challenge each others’ 
assumptions in these sessions, resulting in a more robust outcome then if the evaluation was 
performed alone.  
 
6.4. 
Future studies 
With the discussion concluded, the last result of the study is presented: suggestions on relevant topics 
for future studies.  
 
The first suggestion would be the further exploration of the potential of the adapted VPC model as an 
evaluation method, and the perspective of determining the success of the program’s design by 
confirming the right learning outcomes were offered. To evaluate the success of the program in regard 
to the resulting skills and knowledge instilled in the students, the combination of product-customer fit 
and classical measurement tools could be considered. Regardless, the perspective of approaching an 
educational program as a service could provide new angles on both program design and evaluation. 
 
Furthermore, although this exploration provided interesting insights, to fully explore the gap in 
research found in the literature study, performing a similar study with defined non-venture intention 
objectives on both program- and organization level would be a recommended step.  
 
A second suggestion would be to follow up on the theoretical perspectives that are suggested in 6.3 
pedagogic techniques in entrepreneurship education. This would provide a better understanding of 
==PAGE:60==
 
 
 
60 
what choices in learning objectives and their execution have which impact on the outcomes. In 
addition to that the influence of individuals’ differences in start-ups on the learning process could be 
researched. In this study their educational background was taken as an indicator for splitting up target 
groups. However, there are multiple individual factors influencing one’s learning experience, such as 
learning styles and group dynamics.  
 
 
 
All the future studies mentioned above are relevant to entrepreneurship education, as would be fitting 
for the conclusion on a master thesis with this focus. However, zooming out, there is a lot more to be 
researched concerning the factors of success in innovation support. Enough to dedicate a PhD degree 
to.

meeting  
Team 1 
Situation: students present their idea to their coaches and get first feedback.  
Observation: students have a clear idea of what they think they will be doing and getting out of 
this process, plus what they want from the coaches. Coaches have a different view and try to 
direct them. Students go into defence mode on their plans. They plan to go from idea to 
production in 10 weeks. 
Students want contact with the right people to help them with specific business questions, such 
as outsourcing and testing processes. They have multiple assumptions about who would buy their 
product but need research, and they want the coaches to tell them which is the right one 
(Customers). They also want to recruit another member or hire someone for a specific task 
outside their skillset and ask how to do that (team development). 
Coaches offer specific answers to their questions, but the primary value comes from them trying 
to guide the students towards what they know the students need. Value is in individual input

09/03/2022 Workshop 1 
(Customer and 
Value creation) 
Teams 1 & 2 
Situation: First workshop, full day. I followed part of the day with a 2x 1-hour break to do 
observations for the incubator. The workshop consisted of one day leader presenting 
information/teaching for between 20 and 45 minutes and then giving the teams a practical 
assignment to apply to their start-up idea. The focus is on customers, how to understand them 
and how to test assumptions—connected to the Lean Canvas.  
Observation:  
Team 1: Quote from member team 1: “I did not know that I needed this”. The other member 
mentioned that it changes how he thinks. Refers to the learnings on understanding your 
customer/thinking from their perspective/making sure you contact them and test your 
assumptions. Their viewpoint is relatively practical in that they did not know talking to a customer 
was something you could do, nor that you could test your product in specific ways even when it is 
not ‘done’. Team 1 actively takes in the information, and during the workshop, they adapt their 
planning/add aspects of the workshop to their to-do list. The perspective continues to be that 
their product is correct; how do you show this to the customer? Perfecting their solution is their 
objective, not the customer’s perspective. This perspective is changing: “The most important 
thing is getting out and asking people”. And: “We need to break with our idea of not telling 
anybody’ [about our product] >> “If we stay in the cage we are working on what we think people 
want”. “It is not just about technology” >> realisation by Team 1 member.  
They understand that it is essential to validate in this stage but see it as an additional task to their 
plan instead of a step they need to figure out first. This might show a lack of understanding of 
business action plan/lack of problem definition as a ground for the development.

Team 2 has taken the workshop information and adapted it to their product, which has a 
customer and a user side—asking who will pay for the product, the user or someone else (critical 
thinking). They are already in a customer validation process, talking to potential customers and 
testing the technology (working on technology). Now they start to wonder, what aspect do we 
need to validate? (customer validation). 
17/03/2022 Workshop 2 (Team 
Readiness) 
Teams 1 & 2 
Situation: The workshop focus is Teams. It starts with theoretical input (phases of team 
development) followed by a task for all teams to share their team experiences. 2nd exercise is to 
identify the most important motivations for starting your own business and discuss them with the 
team. This is to help talk about what is essential for the team. And if someone joins, how would 
their values fit in. For the third exercise, there was a choice of which exercise to do last: write a 
persona for a new member or a job ad. 
Observation: 
Team 1: Say that they find the change between input (knowledge) and exercises useful as it gives 
them a practical way to figure out what they have learned. Also interesting to them to know this 
as they wanted to think about adding a team member. Mainly for practical reasons: they need to 
gain the skills to do software work. Interesting that they use the exact words from the second 
exercise/knowledge part in the third part without being instructed to do so: information is 
sticking and being turned into practical use. Using the motivations in creating the persona, they 
realise that to have a manageable team fit, things low on their motivation should be different 
from the new person’s primary motivation. This fits the principles of the first exercise: 
norming/storming > To ease the norming stage, it is helpful to have similar views on things, 
although it is also important to have teammates be able to challenge those views. They are, in a 
way, pre-norming. This realisation was discussed within the team but later not translated onto 
the worksheet. The questions focussed on more practical aspects of the persona. 
 
One of the members appreciates the visual representation of the motivations, which helps to 
understand those of his teammate and become closer as a team (team bonding/alignment). 
 
Team 2: had a hard time starting; it needed to be clearer what to do and why. They do feel an 
exact need for this information, and even more on team alignment: with our education ending, 
where do we stand individually and together? The motivation exercise: if what you want from the 
journey is the same thing, it is good to realise that.

The expectation of this workshop was to talk about team expansion and how to determine if 
you’re ready. They say these are valuable tools they are learning and they knew the knowledge

distracted? Effect of mismatch in target groups? 
In the meantime, they do not finish the third exercise. They turned into their version of team 
bonding by using the 2nd exercise and focusing on where each of them is about each other and 
talking about it. The workshop brings them together and allows them not to be distracted by day-
to-day things; this helps them move along together (team development).  
What they describe they could do with the new knowledge and tools is that they want to analyse 
where they are now, how to get through it and how to invite others in (Next steps). While the 
knowledge describes the stages, it does not tell how to move to the next stage. This indicates an 
unfulfilled need : move through the team/start-up process, which are the right next steps? (Next 
steps, entrepreneurship process) 
18/03/2022 Coaching meeting 
Team 2 
Situation: 2nd meeting of the team with coaches. The same coaches help them within the Pilot 
program, but they will split their meetings using one for each program. However, some spillovers

questions on both.

overlap with X-technology, the course they had before (shows non-fit for all target groups). Does

knowing the material the last workshop and were not able to follow the exercises due to that.  
They also note the workshops were good, but a bit long and general, the leader did not know 
about the projects which made them miss specific feedback. It did promote discussion and mare 
them reflect and re-align, which was good.
