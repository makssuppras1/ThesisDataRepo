Abstract
This thesis investigates whether high┬¡resolution phase┬¡contrast micro┬¡Computed Tomog┬¡
raphy (┬ÁCT) combined with biologically informed image analysis can be used to detect
and characterize inducible bronchus┬¡associated lymphoid tissue (iBALT) in human lung
biopsies from patients with severe asthma.
To explore this, an iterative image┬¡guided workflow was developed, consisting of four
steps: biopsy acquisition, ┬ÁCT imaging, pixel classification using ilastik, and validation
through histology and expert annotation. A three┬¡class pixel classification model (iBALT,
tissue, background) was trained using a Random Forest classifier on morphological fea┬¡
tures extracted from ┬ÁCT volumes. The model was applied to large volumetric datasets
(>200 GB), and post┬¡processing was used to identify and quantify potential iBALT regions.
In one high┬¡resolution scan, two major iBALT regions (Region┬¡1 and Region┬¡2) were iden┬¡
tified. Region┬¡2 was used for training and later validated through expert annotation and
histological staining, showing strong spatial correspondence with model predictions. This
region measured approximately 500 ┬Ám in width and 250 ┬Ám in depth, and accounted for
3.29% of the biological volume (iBALT + tissue). Notably, the model identified a second
region (Region┬¡1) that was not known prior to training, demonstrating its ability to gen┬¡
eralize and detect previously unseen structures. Feature importance analysis revealed
that iBALT was defined by a focused subset of features, while the tissue class was more
heterogeneous. The model generalized well to background tissue in a second sample
and did not detect iBALT, consistent with its anatomical composition.
While the identified regions fulfill relevant morphological and anatomical criteria for iBALT,
final confirmation requires immunohistochemical staining to verify cellular composition
and organization. These results nonetheless demonstrate the feasibility of combining
┬ÁCT and machine learning for non┬¡destructive, volumetric detection of immune structures
in lung tissue. The workflow provides a foundation for future studies aiming to improve
biopsy targeting and immune structure analysis in pulmonary disease.

Introduction
Asthma is a chronic inflammatory disease of the airways characterized by recurrent wheez┬¡
ing, shortness of breath, and coughing, affecting more than 262 million people worldwide1.
Given its high prevalence and global economic burden, asthma remains a worldwide
health concern with a reported global mortality rate of 455000 in 2019 alone2. Asthma
affects individuals across all age groups, imposing a substantial burden on their daily
lives. This burden is reflected in activity limitations, sleep disturbances, and poor mental
health, all of which significantly reduce quality of life3ÔÇô5. Although prevalence for children
is decreasing, the opposite trend is observed in adults, highlighting the need for a vigilant
approach and improved strategies in diagnosis and treatment of asthma6.
While asthma affects individuals across a spectrum of severity, a small proportion of peo┬¡
ple with asthma accounts for a disproportionately large amount of mortality, morbidity,
and economic burden7,8. These are the individuals living with severe asthma. In the pop┬¡
ulation of adults with asthma, they make up only 3% to 10% but account for 60% of the
cost associated with asthma9,10. Therefore, a deeper understanding of the underlying
pathology and the improvement of treatment strategies is especially important for those
living with severe asthma, where better asthma control is correlated with improved quality
of life3.
One research area of interest in understanding the immunopathology of severe asthma
is the role of inducible bronchus┬¡associated lymphoid tissue (iBALT) ┬¡ a tertiary lymphoid
organ in the lungs. In patients with severe and very severe Chronic Obstructive Pul┬¡
monary Disease (COPD), iBALT is more frequently observed compared to mild and mod┬¡
erate COPD11,12. Despite the known association between iBALT formation and pulmonary
pathology in severe COPD, the role of iBALT remains a topic of discussion11,13. Although
much of the research is often put on COPD, higher organization of iBALT is also closely
linked with higher severity of asthma symptoms11. The presence of iBALT, however, does
not necessarily indicate disease14.
The role of iBALT in the pathology of severe asthma remains incompletely understood.
One approach to gaining deeper understanding in severe asthma is through histologi┬¡
cal analysis of lung biopsies15. The volumetric tissue samples are often examined in a
histopathological workflow that provides subcellular resolution of distinct slices of the sam┬¡
ple. Microscopy techniques are conventionally considered the standard in this workflow
due to the high specificity when paired with appropriate staining techniques16. However,
this method presents challenges, including time required, poor conservation of tissue, and
only partially accessing the full volumetric information in the sample17. To address these
limitations, high┬¡resolution X┬¡ray tomography is a potential complementary technique that
provides non┬¡destructive full volumetric access to the tissue sample18.
To overcome the limitations of traditional histology and unlock the full potential of vol┬¡
umetric tissue data, this project explores an iterative, image┬¡guiding workflow centered
around phase┬¡contrast micro┬¡Computed Tomography (┬ÁCT). This technique enables non┬¡
destructive, high┬¡resolution imaging of lung biopsies, while preserving the sample for
downstream histological validation. By leveraging this capability, virtual histology can be
performed to identify candidate regions of interest prior to physical slicing. Image analysis
plays a central role in this process, enabling the detection, segmentation, and character┬¡
ization of these structures directly from the volumetric data. This not only improves the

2.2
Immune activation and airway remodeling in asthma
The lung is not only a site of gas exchange but also a critical interface for immune surveil┬¡
lance. Its epithelial surfaces are continuously exposed to airborne antigens, necessitating
a tightly regulated immune presence. Under homeostatic conditions, this involves a bal┬¡
ance between innate immune cellsÔÇösuch as macrophages, dendritic cells, and neutrophilsÔÇö
and adaptive immune cells, including resident lymphocytes. These cells are distributed
throughout the epithelial lining, lamina propria, and interstitial spaces, where they monitor
for pathogens and maintain tissue integrity22,26.
Many of these immune cells originate or become activated in lymphoid organs, which are
specialized structures that support immune cell development and coordination. There are
different types of lymphoid organs: primary, secondary, and tertiary. Primary lymphoid
organs, such as the bone marrow and thymus, are where lymphocytes are generated
and undergo maturation. In the thymus, for example, immature T cells migrate through
distinct cortical and medullary zones, where they are selected based on their ability to
recognize antigens without triggering autoimmunity. Secondary lymphoid organs, includ┬¡
ing lymph nodes, the spleen, and mucosa┬¡associated lymphoid tissues (MALT), are sites
where mature immune cells encounter antigens and initiate adaptive responses. Tertiary
lymphoid organs, by contrast, are not present at birth but form in non┬¡lymphoid tissues
during chronic inflammation or persistent immune activation. These structures resemble
secondary lymphoid organs in organization but arise de novo in response to pathological
stimuli22,26,27.

7
Discussion
The aim of this thesis was to investigate whether an iterative, image┬¡guided workflow com┬¡
bining high┬¡resolution phase┬¡contrast ┬ÁCT and biologically informed image analysis could
enable accurate detection and characterization of iBALT in human lung tissue affected by
severe asthma. The overarching hypothesis was that this approach would provide suffi┬¡
cient contrast, spatial resolution, and analytical power to identify potential iBALT structures
in a non┬¡destructive, volumetric manner, with validation provided by expert annotation and
histology.
7.1
Bridging imaging and biology
The phase┬¡contrast CT scans revealed a range of tissue compositions across samples,
including regions dominated by smooth muscle, cartilage, and glandular structures, as
well as vessels and connective tissue (See Figure 6.1a). This structural variation aligns
well with the anatomical expectations for bronchus biopsies, which acquires distinct sub┬¡
types of tissue composing the dense network surrounding the bronchi exactly like shown
in Figure 2.2. Bronchial biopsies typically also include respiratory epithelium with its basal
membrane22.
The identification of iBALT structures was most prominent in the high┬¡resolution scan of
Cryo2 DK32 vi, where two large regions were visible. One of them, Region┬¡2, is a large,
relatively elongated spheroid┬¡like region localized between glandular and muscular tissue.
This region exhibited high model confidence, with many voxels exhibiting iBALT probabil┬¡
ities ranging from 60ÔÇô80%, and was consistently highlighted across multiple slices. The
same region was also annotated by both experts with high certainty as iBALT, resulting
in a combined label of iSS in the central area and iPS toward the periphery (See Fig┬¡
ure 6.6c). This agreement between model prediction and expert annotation supports the
biological plausibility of the detected structure being iBALT.
Histological validation further confirmed the presence of a dense lymphoid aggregate in
the corresponding region, as seen in section 120 of the H&E┬¡stained slices (Figure 6.11b).
The aggregate measured approximately 500┬á┬Ám in width and 250┬á┬Ám in depth, aligning
well with the volumetric thickness estimates derived from the 1D projections. The dark pur┬¡
ple staining, typical of lymphocyte┬¡rich regions, was spatially consistent with the modelÔÇÖs
predicted iBALT mask, reinforcing the validity of the detection. For comparison, the study
with Bactrian camel lung by He et al.33 reported iBALT regions ranging from 200ÔÇô600┬á┬Ám
in width and 200ÔÇô300┬á┬Ám in depth. The region identified here fits comfortably within these
ranges, suggesting that its size and appearance are consistent with established descrip┬¡
tions in the literature31,33. In terms of volumetric size, iBALT is estimated to be 0.134 mm3
making up 3.29% of the total biological volume.
In terms of anatomical context, the region is located on the basal side of the bronchial
epithelium, adjacent to pulmonary blood vesselsÔÇöan arrangement that aligns well with
the major findings discussed in Section 2.3 regarding typical iBALT surroundings11,27,30,31.
A zoomed┬¡in view of H&E┬¡stained section 120 is shown in Figure 7.1, where multiple blood
vessels are marked with arrows. Notably, these vessels appear to consistently traverse
the lymphoid aggregate, supporting one of the criteria for iBALT proposed by Ji Young
Hwang et al.11. However, the second criterionÔÇönamely, the presence of a nerve running
through the aggregateÔÇöcould not be confirmed in this volume.

7.2
Annotation uncertainty
Defining the precise boundaries of lymphoid aggregates, especially at the voxel level,
remains a persistent challenge in both histopathological annotation and computational
analysis53,54. Notably, both expert annotators in this project expressed lower confidence
near the edges of annotated iBALT regions as seen in Figure 6.6, reflecting the biological
and morphological ambiguity inherent to these transitional zones. This uncertainty is not
unique to the annotators: the modelÔÇÖs probability maps also reveal lower probability at
these boundaries as evident from Figure 6.8, suggesting that the algorithm is attuned to
the same ambiguities that challenge expert interpretation.
Beyond individual regions, the overall distribution of iBALT probabilities within each an┬¡
notation label offers further insight into the relationship between model predictions and
expert confidence. In regions labeled iSS, where both annotators agreed on a strong
iBALT presence, the probability distribution was approximately symmetric and unimodal,
but flatter than a normal distribution. This suggests a relatively homogeneous structure,
where the model consistently detects iBALT┬¡like features across the region. In contrast,
other labels, such as iPS, iPP, iAS, iAP, and iAA, exhibited right┬¡skewed distributions.
These were dominated by low┬¡probability voxels, with only a minority showing high iBALT
probability. This pattern likely reflects greater biological heterogeneity or annotation un┬¡
certainty, with these regions containing a mix of iBALT and surrounding tissue.
Among all annotation categories, the iSS label is particularly noteworthy, not only for its
distinct, nearly normal probability distribution, but also for its elevated median iBALT prob┬¡
ability (approximately 43.92%). This convergence of high model confidence and strong
annotator agreement could suggest that iSS regions represent a ÔÇÿprototypicalÔÇÖ iBALT for┬¡

Conversely, the lower medians and more skewed distributions observed in other labels
likely reflect a combination of partial iBALT presence, transitional tissue zones, and areas

Beyond the well┬¡defined iSS regions, the model also identified additional candidate areasÔÇö

Phase┬¡contrast ┬ÁCT plays an essential role in this project, not only as the imaging modality
used throughout, but as the enabling mechanism for morphological segmentation of im┬¡
mune structures such as iBALT. As discussed in the theory chapter, phase┬¡contrast ┬ÁCT
generates contrast by exploiting variations in the refractive index of tissueÔÇöspecifically
the ╬┤ component in the relation for refractive index shown in Equation (3.4). This stands
in contrast to conventional CT, which relies on attenuation coefficients ┬Á governed by
Lambert┬¡BeerÔÇÖs law (see Equation (3.2)).
This distinction is critical. As shown in Table 3.1, the contrast ratio between soft tissue
types such as lung and skeletal muscle is close to unity, making attenuation┬¡based CT
poorly suited for differentiating between them. In theory, this implies that conventional CT
would struggle to act as a contrast mechanism between distinct soft tissue types. Our
results confirm this: phase┬¡contrast ┬ÁCT provides the necessary sensitivity to resolve
structural boundaries and support segmentation in unstained tissue.
The observed contrast in phase┬¡contrast ┬ÁCT arises from direction┬¡dependent phase
shifts caused by refractive index gradients. These gradients manifest as distinct intensity
patterns in the raw images. Bright regionsÔÇösuch as those shown in Figure 4.2ÔÇötypically
correspond to sharp interfaces with large differences in ╬┤, indicating abrupt changes in
refractive index. These are often associated with membranes or densely packed cel┬¡
lular boundaries. Large gray regions, such as muscle fibers, suggest more gradual or
anisotropic changes in ╬┤, potentially arising from fibrous structures. For example, if a
muscle fiber runs parallel to the imaging plane, the refractive index may vary minimally
along its axis but significantly across it, producing directional contrast. Darker regions,
especially those appearing as long black stripes, may represent areas of low ╬┤ contrastÔÇö
possibly homogeneous fluids or sparsely structured tissue. These considerations are
important when looking at the phase┬¡contrast ┬ÁCT in Figure 6.1, where the intensities are
reversed to mimic microscopy of tissue, which is a compromise against the underlying
physical properties that the raw intensities reflect.
These patterns are not merely visual artifacts but reflect underlying tissue architecture.

7.6.1

The model demonstrated several strengths. It enabled non┬¡destructive, volumetric ex┬¡

Future perspective
This thesis has demonstrated the feasibility of using high┬¡resolution phase┬¡contrast CT

Conclusion
This thesis has explored the use of high┬¡resolution phase┬¡contrast ┬ÁCT and machine
learning┬¡based image analysis to detect potential iBALT regions in preserved human lung
tissue. Through an iterative imaging┬¡guided workflow, it was possible to identify, seg┬¡

that imaging┬¡guided analysis can support high┬¡specificity questions in tissue immunology,
even in the absence of staining.
Based on the findings of this project, the following criteria are proposed as a practical
framework for identifying candidate iBALT regions in human lung tissue:
(1) The region should lie in a peribronchial and perivascular location.

Histology
Histological validation was performed using standard hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) stain┬¡
ing, adapted from the protocol used in the J. Erjef├ñlt lab (Eriksen, 2025). This method
provides high┬¡contrast visualization of cellular and tissue architecture, enabling identifica┬¡
tion of lymphoid aggregates such as iBALT in preserved lung biopsies.
Histological Sectioning Workflow
Prior to staining, formalin┬¡fixed lung biopsies were embedded in paraffin and sectioned
using a standardized slicing protocol. This process was designed to preserve tissue ori┬¡
entation and ensure consistent depth sampling across serial sections.
ÔÇó Freezing and mounting: The paraffin block was gently frozen to stabilize the tissue.
It was then mounted onto a HM355S microtome, ensuring alignment for consistent
slicing.
ÔÇó Sectioning: Tissue was sliced into 4 ┬Ám┬¡thick sections, with 10 consecutive slices
cut per batch. After each batch, the block was temporarily refrozen with the tissue
still mounted to prevent misalignment or remounting artifacts.
ÔÇó Water bath transfer: The freshly cut slices were floated onto a water bath, allowing
them to flatten and relax. Each section was then carefully mounted onto a labeled
microscope glass slide, with the slide number corresponding to the slicing depth.
ÔÇó Depth control: This process was repeated until the target depth of approximately
152 slices was reached, corresponding to 608 ┬Ám into the tissue. This depth was
calculated based on prior imaging and anatomical estimates to ensure sampling of
regions with potential iBALT formation.
Following sectioning, the slides were processed using the H&E staining protocol described
below.
Staining Workflow Overview
The protocol consists of four main stages: deparaffinization, hematoxylin staining, eosin
counterstaining, and dehydration and mounting.
This protocol yields high┬¡quality histological sections suitable for morphological assess┬¡
ment and comparison with volumetric imaging data. The differential staining of nuclei
(blue┬¡purple) and cytoplasm (pink┬¡red) facilitates identification of lymphocyte┬¡rich regions
and supports validation of candidate iBALT structures identified in micro┬¡CT scans.
