Abstract 
Therapeutic cancer vaccines aim to deliver adjuvant and antigens to antigen-presenting cells 
that activate tumor-specific CD8+ T cells, which are significant mediators of antitumor 
immunity. The efficacy of therapeutic cancer vaccines in the clinic has been limited, but novel 
delivery platforms, successful combinations of vaccination with other immune therapies such 
as ACT, and improved antigen selection has sparked renewed interest. This thesis investigates 
a novel therapeutic vaccine platform in the form of a liposome that delivers the model antigen 
Ovalbumin (OVA257-264) in conjugation with a potent TLR7 agonist. The peptides delivered 
are in the form of high-affinity (SIINFEKL) and lower-affinity (SIITFEKL) ligands that are 
recognized by CD8+ T cells from TCR-transgenic OT-I mice. 
 
We demonstrate that intratumoral vaccination effectively delivers liposomes to dendritic cells 
(DCs) within the tumor and tumor-draining lymph nodes. The liposomes induce maturation 
and antigen presentation by DCs and cross-presenting type-I conventional dendritic cells 
(cDC1s) in vitro and in vivo. We also show that liposomal vaccination outperforms non-
formulated antigens in the context of inducing tumor infiltration of adoptively transferred 
CD8+ T cells. Vaccination with high-affinity antigens was compared to immunization with 
low-affinity antigens to determine their influence on exhaustion of tumor-infiltrating 
lymphocytes, which is often portrayed by an increased expression of programmed cell death 
protein 1 (PD-1). Vaccination with high- and low-affinity antigens increased tumor infiltration 
of adoptively transferred CD8+ T cells and reduced their PD-1 expression, compared to 
delivery of adoptively transferred T cells without vaccination. A minimal effect on tumor 
growth and survival was achieved when combining the adoptive transfer of OT-I CD8+ T cells 
with low-affinity antigen vaccination. Overall, we present a highly flexible vaccine delivery 
system, capable of delivering high- and low-affinity antigens. Future studies should further 
examine vaccination with high- and low-affinity antigens in relation to therapeutic effects and 
T cell responses.

1 Introduction 
Cancer is a leading cause of death worldwide, accounting for millions of deaths globally every 
year [1]. The development of cancer occurs through uncontrolled cell growth of the progeny 
of transformed cells through a multistep process [2]. Several acquired biological capabilities 
can allow cancer to form, one of them being the ability of cancer cells to avoid immune 
destruction [3, 4], which makes targeting the immune system an attractive way to fight cancer. 
 
Cancer immunotherapy aims to boost the immune system to eliminate malignant cells. The 
basis of harnessing the immune system to combat cancer can be traced back to the 19th century. 
In 1892 William Coley, often titled as the father of immunotherapy, first attempted to leverage 
the immune system to treat cancer by injecting a cocktail of heat-inactivated bacteria into 
patients with inoperable sarcomas, providing tumor regression [5, 6]. Coley’s discoveries 
provided a proof of concept for utilizing the immune system to treat cancer, but unknown 
mechanisms of action hindered further progress [7]. In the past decades there have been 
tremendous advancements in understanding the recognition and elimination of malignant cells 
by the immune system, which has led to a breakthrough within the field of cancer 
immunotherapy [8]. Immunotherapy can be used alone or in combination with other treatments 
and can help patients who don’t respond to traditional treatments such as surgery, radiation 
therapy, and chemotherapy. Additionally, these more conventional therapies often fail to 
successfully eliminate residual malignancies and cancer metastasis. At the same time, 
immunotherapy can provide long-term protection [9], and has been able to cure previously 
untreatable cancers [10]. 
 
Today several cancer immunotherapy platforms are used in cancer treatments. The most widely 
used are immune checkpoint inhibitors such as cytotoxic T-lymphocyte associated protein 4 
(CTLA-4) and programmed cell death protein 1 (PD-1) blocking antibodies. Ipilimumab, a 
CTLA-4 immune checkpoint inhibitor, was first authorized to treat advanced melanoma in 
2011 [11, 12]. However, the suppression of T cell responses through immune checkpoints is 
just one of many mechanisms that tumor cells utilize for immune evasion for immune evasion 
[11, 12], which emphasizes the need for other immunotherapeutic approaches. Numerous novel 
immunotherapies are currently being explored. These include cancer vaccines, oncolytic 
viruses and cell-based therapies [13], with several being clinically validated for cancer 
treatment  [14-16].

1.1 Cancer and the immune system 
For a successful anticancer immune response, a stepwise process needs to take place that has 
been termed the cancer-immunity cycle. During cancer formation, genetic mutations 
accumulate due to genomic instability. This results in the expression of neo-antigens by cancer 
cells that can be presented by major histocompatibility (MHC) molecules on the surface of 
cancer cells. Tumor antigens are sampled by dendritic cells (DCs) that migrate to tumor 
draining lymph nodes (tdLNs), where they prime and activate tumor-specific cytolytic CD8+ 
T cells. This results in an anticancer T cell response which potentiates the cycle further [17]. 
This model provides a rationale for targeting different steps within the cancer immunity cycle 
to maintain its functionality. DC antigen presentation and T cell priming can be facilitated by 
cancer vaccines delivering adjuvant and antigens. The adjuvant aids in maturing the DCs, 
increasing the strength of antigen presentation, and combating immune tolerance. Additionally, 
adoptive cell therapy can aid in the trafficking of CD8+ T cells to the tumor [18], further 
illustrated in Figure 1. 
 
Figure 1: The cancer immunity cycle. Upon cancer cell death, neoantigens are released and taken up 
by DCs that migrate to the secondary lymphoid organs and present the antigen to naïve T cells through 
MHC interactions with T cell receptors (TCR). Upon maturation, effector T cells enter the circulation 
where they traffic to and infiltrate tumors. Activated cytotoxic CD8+ T cells recognize cancer cells 
based on the antigens they release and kill them. Cancer cell death releases more antigens that will 
propagate this cycle and amplify the anticancer response. The cycle can be targeted to aid an immune 
response against the tumor by a) Cancer vaccines and b) Adoptive cell therapy, among other cancer 
treatments and immunotherapies. Adapted from Chen et al [17].

1.1.1 Dendritic cells in cancer 
Although dendritic cells (DCs) are a rare population within tumors and lymphoid organs, their 
ability to initiate an antigen specific immune response by activating T cells is crucial for an 
antitumor immune response [19], which forms the basis of the previously described cancer 
immunity cycle [17].  
 
DCs bridge the gap between the innate and adaptive immune systems [20]. Immature DCs 
sample antigens and are activated upon recognition of damage-associated molecular patterns 
(DAMPs) or pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) via their pattern recognition 
receptors (PRRs). DAMPs are released by or expressed on the surface of dead or dying cells 
within the tumor [21]. DC activation can also be driven by stimulation with type I interferons 
(IFNs) released by cells within the tumor microenvironment [22]. The now mature DCs 
upregulate the C-C chemokine receptor (CCR)7 and migrate from the periphery to lymphoid 
tissues to activate their cognate T cells and initiate adaptive immunity [23].  
 
Several DC subpopulations have been characterized based on ontogeny and functional factors, 
both in mice and humans. Those include conventional DCs (cDCs), plasmacytoid DC (pDCs), 
and monocyte DCs (moDCs) [19]. Although several professional antigen-presenting cells 
(APCs) exist, cDCs are particularly effective at generating a T cell response. They can present 
endogenous and exogenous antigens on both MHC-I and MHC-II molecules [22]. cDCs in 
humans and mice express MHC-II and CD11c and can be further divided into two sub-
populations, cDC1 and cDC2s. cDC1s can be recognized by their expression of the chemokine 
receptor XCR1 and the C-type lectin receptor DNGR-1/CLEC9A, while cDC2s express CD11b 
and CD172a [24]. Both subsets can present antigens on MHC-II to CD4+ T cells, although 
cDC2s may be more efficient in that process. However, only cDC1s can present exogenous 
antigens in MHC-I through a process called cross-presentation [25], this makes cDC1s crucial 
in eliciting cytotoxic CD8+ T cell responses against tumor cells [26-28].   
1.1.2 CD8+ T cells in cancer 
The generation of a potent cancer response and therapeutic effect of cancer immunotherapy 
often relies on cytotoxic T lymphocyte (CTL) responses against tumor antigens [29], with high 
levels of tumor-infiltrating T cells correlating with good prognosis in several cancer types [30-
33]. 
 
==PAGE:11==
 
4
APCs activate naïve CD8+ T cells in secondary lymphoid organs [34]. Their activation depends 
on TCR:MHC-I interactions (signal 1) and co-stimulation through CD28 interacting with 
CD80 or CD86 on APCs (signal 2) [35, 36]. Cells that receive suboptimal TCR stimulation or 
co-stimulation become anergic [37, 38]. After activation, the previously naïve CD8+ T cells 
expand and give rise to a pool of effector CD8+ T cells that exit the lymph node and home 
towards target tissue via chemokine receptor and adhesion molecule interactions [39]. 
Inflammatory cytokines such as interleukin (IL) 12 and type I IFNs increase the effector 
functions of T cells (signal 3). In their absence, T cell tolerance in the form of deletion or 
anergy can occur [40]. 
 
These functional CTLs have two primary killing mechanisms, granule exocytosis and Fas 
ligand (FasL) mediated induction of apoptosis. Killing via granule exocytosis occurs very fast 
and is mediated by the release of granules with cytolytic mediators such as granzyme and 
perforin, resulting in cancer cell destruction [41]. The FasL pathway is slower, mediated by 
cross-linking of the cell surface death receptor Fas, expressed on the cancer cells, with FasL, 
expressed on the CTLs. This triggers caspase activation resulting in apoptosis [42]. CTLs also 
indirectly kill tumor cells through the secretion of cytokines such as tumor necrosis factor 
(TNF) and interferon (IFN)-γ [43]. TNFs and IFN-γ have immunomodulatory effects, in 
addition to acting as cytotoxic cytokines and initiating apoptosis in tumor cells [44-46]. Upon 
cancer clearance, a subset of the CTLs is maintained as memory CD8+ T cells, ready to carry 
out effector functions upon reencountering the antigen [47]. 
1.2 Immune suppression in the tumor microenvironment 
Many tumors have strategies of immune evasion and minimizing the effectiveness of CTL. 
Here, immunosuppressive mechanisms exerted by cancer cells, and the presence of suppressive 
and regulatory immune cells within the tumor play a big role [48]. These immunosuppressive 
networks interfere with nearly every step needed for effective immunity, limiting the success 
of immunotherapies [49]. 
 
Immune evasion is exerted by cancer cells through several mechanisms. These include the 
release of immunosuppressive mediators such as IL-10 and transforming growth factor β 
(TGF-β), downregulation of tumor antigens and MHC-I, and upregulation of inhibitory ligands 
such as programmed death ligand-1 (PD-L1) [49, 50]. Several of these immune suppressive

pathways cause defective antigen presentation by suppressing the function of DCs and other 
APCs and impairing tumor infiltration by T cells [51]. 
 
Several cell types, of both the innate and adaptive immune systems, contribute to immune 
suppression in the tumor microenvironment [52]. These include regulatory T cells (Treg), 
tumor-associated neutrophils (TANs), tumor-associated macrophages (TAMs), and myeloid-
derived suppressor cells (MDSCs) [53]. These cells promote tumor growth and immune 
suppression through numerous mechanisms such as inducing angiogenesis, providing survival 
signals to cancer cells, fostering metastasis, and aiding in evasion of immune destruction by 
suppressing APCs and lymphocytes [50, 54-56], further represented in Figure 2.

1.2.1 Myeloid-derived suppressor cells  
The observation of myeloid cells with suppressive abilities was first described over 20 years 
ago in patients with cancer [57-59]. These cells were later termed myeloid-derived suppressor 
cells or MDSCs [60]. MDSCs can be divided into two subgroups, granulocytic MDSCs (g-
MDSCs), also termed polymorphonuclear myeloid-derived suppressor cells (PMN- MDSC), 
and monocytic MDSCs (m-MDSCs) [61].  
 
In healthy individuals, MDSCs are present in low numbers in the circulation and have a role in 
immune regulation and tissue repair. However, this population rapidly expands in the context 
of infections, inflammation, and cancer [62]. Activated MDSCs promote tumor growth through 
several processes, one of them being immune evasion. MDSCs can hinder the tumor infiltration 
of T cells by interfering with their trafficking through interference with adhesion molecules. 
MDSCs also inhibit T cell activation and proliferation by depleting amino acids necessary for 
those processes [63]. Additionally, MDSCs can adversely affect DC functions by suppressing 
their differentiation, maturation, and antigen presentation [63, 64], which represents a major 
immune escape mechanism in cancer [65]. 
1.3 Therapeutic cancer vaccines 
With the surge in cancer immunotherapeutics in the past decades, there has been great interest 
in developing efficient therapeutic cancer vaccines. However, many have shown suboptimal 
efficacy in the clinic [66, 67]. Significant developments in cancer vaccine platforms have been 
made in recent years, leading to advances within the field [68]. In the past decade, several 
vaccines have entered phase II and III trials [69, 70]. The United States Food and Drug 
Administration (FDA) approved the first therapeutic cancer vaccine, Sipuleucel-T 
(Provenge®) in 2010 for treatment of prostate cancer [14]. 
 
Cancer vaccines are an attractive class of immunotherapeutics. By delivering antigen and 
adjuvant to APCs, they activate CTLs against tumor antigens, allowing for direct killing of 
tumor cells and lasting immune protection [66, 71, 72]. Multiple cancer vaccine platforms have 
been developed, including DC vaccines, engineered cellular vaccines, oncolytic- or 
recombinant-virus vectored vaccines, and peptide- or protein-based vaccines [73]. Tumor 
antigens that cancer vaccines target can be broadly split into two categories, tumor-specific 
antigens (TSAs) and tumor-associated antigens (TAAs). TSAs are neoantigens that arise from 
==PAGE:14==
 
7
cancer-specific mutations, or oncogenic viral antigens, and are therefore regarded as foreign 
by the immune system with high immunogenicity. TSAs can be highly immunogenic but are 
often unique to each patient’s tumor and require personalized treatments. TAAs are aberrantly 
expressed self-antigens and have been used in most preclinical cancer vaccines to date. They 
are often shared between patients, which makes them a good candidate for cancer vaccine 
development. However, there are challenges involved with using TAAs for vaccine design as 
high-affinity T cells that recognize self-antigens are deleted through the mechanism of central 
or peripheral tolerance, resulting in the remaining T cells being low avidity [74]. This poses a 
challenge for TAA-directed vaccines since they need to stimulate low-affinity or rare self-
reactive T cells to elicit an antitumor response, often failing to induce robust T cell proliferation 
[74, 75].  Several approaches have been developed that attempt to break this self-tolerance and 
improve vaccine immunogenicity to increase the expansion of T cells with high avidity for 
tumor antigens [76-78]. However, the resulting T cell expansion has not consistently been 
linked to greater antitumor responses [79-81].   
1.3.1 Low-affinity antigens for cancer vaccinations 
 
When successfully primed, low-affinity T cells or high-affinity T cells primed with low-affinity 
ligands show some beneficial characteristics for immunotherapy such as lower expression of 
inhibitory molecules, less exhausted phenotypes, and decreased autoimmune reactivity [79, 82, 
83]. Epitopes presented in chronic viral infections are similar to tumor antigens since they are 
present for a long time, often resulting in T cell exhaustion. In models of chronic viral infection, 
it has been described that high-affinity CD8+ T cells are initially rapidly expanded and 
activated but then decline in numbers or become senescent, while lower-affinity T cells expand 
later and persist in late stages of infections [84-86].  A study conducted by Zahm et al. showed 
that vaccination with high-affinity epitopes and a high antigen dose had less antitumor efficacy 
than low-affinity epitopes. Additionally, they report an increase in the expression of T-cell 
checkpoint molecules, notably PD-1, following vaccination with high-affinity antigens [83]. 
High PD-1 expression on T cells has been connected to an exhausted phenotype, both in the 
context of chronic viral infection and cancer [87-89]. In addition to upregulating inhibitory 
receptors, exhausted T cells display loss of proliferative abilities, cytolytic activity, and effector 
functions such as the production of IL-2 [90]. This new insight raises the question of whether 
low-affinity T cells should be considered favorable in chronic or long-lasting diseases such as 
cancer [79, 91].

1.3.2 Liposomes as vaccine delivery systems 
The use of liposomes as immunological adjuvants for antigen delivery was first reported in 
1974 [92]. Since then, the science has been further validated. Several liposomal vaccines are 
in clinical trials, and liposomal vaccines against human papillomavirus (HPV), influenza virus, 
and hepatitis A virus are commercially available [93]. One of the current limitations of cancer 
vaccines is delivering sufficient amounts of antigens to DCs to induce proper maturation and 
antigen presentation and avoid immune tolerance [94]. Nanocarriers such as liposomes loaded 
with antigens and targeted towards DCs are an interesting approach to overcome this problem 
[95]. Liposome-based delivery systems have additional benefits as they are highly flexible and 
versatile, making it easy to change their charge, size, and location of antigens [96].  
1.3.3 TLR7 agonists as adjuvants 
Successful cross-presentation, which is crucial for antitumor CTL responses, depends on the 
activation status of DCs [19, 97]. Antigen presentation by immature DCs can result in 
immunological tolerance, either through T cell deletion, T cell anergy, or induction of Tregs 
[98-100]. Toll-like receptor (TLR)7 agonists have been used as adjuvants in immunotherapy 
due to their ability to induce DC maturation and antigen presentation [101]. TLR7 expression 
on murine cDCs has been debated [102, 103], but it has been well documented that pDCs 
express high levels of endosomal TLR7 receptors that recognize single-stranded RNA. Upon 
activation through TLR7 receptors, pDCs release large amounts of type I IFNs, such as IFNα 
[104, 105], that have major effects on cross-presentation through two mechanisms, activating 
DCs and stimulating CTLs via their IFN receptors [27]. This immune regulation exerted by 
TLR7 agonists makes them intriguing adjuvants for cancer vaccine formulations, further 
illustrated in Figure 3. 
1.4 Adoptive T cell transfer 
Adoptive cell transfer (ACT) of antitumor T cells is a promising and tailored cancer treatment 
that has shown impressive efficacy in the clinic [106-110]. ACT using autologous tumor-
infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs) was pioneered at the National Cancer Institute in the late 1980s 
[111]. Since then, it has been used successfully against metastatic melanoma in multiple 
clinical studies, with complete response rates ranging up to 50% and complete durable tumor 
regression observed in up to 20% of patients [106, 112]. The therapy has also been promising 
in patients with metastatic colorectal or breast cancer when using TSA-specific TILs [113, 114]. 
==PAGE:16==
 
9
 
Figure 3: Immune regulation exerted by TLR7 agonists. TLR7 agonist binds to intracellular TLR7 
receptors on pDCs, which upon activation release type I IFNs. These IFNs mediate tumor cell lysis by 
directly killing tumor cells and activating CD8+ T cells and DCs that further interact, causing effective 
CTL mediated tumor cell lysis. 
 
TILs used in ACT are harvested from patient tumors and are activated and expanded ex vivo 
before being reinfused to the patient. Before adoptive transfer, patients receive chemotherapy 
which induces lymphodepletion, and the treatment is followed by a high dose of IL-2 [107, 
111]. Lymphodepletion is an essential component of the treatment since it promotes 
engraftment of the transferred lymphocytes [107], and eliminates endogenous lymphocytes 
including regulatory T cells that compete with the adoptively transferred cells for cytokines 
such as IL-7 and IL-15 [115]. Lymphodepletion can also cause immunogenic cell death of 
tumor cells, resulting in the release of tumor antigens and pro-inflammatory cytokines, further 
improving the antitumor response [116]. IL-2 regulates key aspects of T cell biology as it 
promotes proliferation and effector functions on CD8+ and CD4+ T cells [117] and is therefore 
administered to support their proliferation in vivo [118].  
 
With recent advances in T cell engineering came the generation of TCR-engineered T cells or 
T cells carrying synthetic chimeric antigen receptors (CAR). While TCR-engineered T cells 
are MHC restricted, CAR T cells allow for high-affinity recognition of antigen without MHC 
recognition [119]. CAR T cell therapies offer a wide range of targets as they can be designed 
to target any surface antigen of choice [120], and have been very successful in treating B cell 
malignancies [121]. However, their success has been limited by a high toxicity rate [122, 123], 
sometimes having deadly consequences [124-126]. Another limitation of CAR T cells is that 
==PAGE:17==
 
10
they are limited to recognizing surface antigens, while TCR-engineered T cells and TILs 
recognize all proteins bound to MHC [127]. TCR-engineered T cells have shown some 
therapeutic potentials but face many limitations, such as problems with target selection and 
mismatch of histocompatibility antigens that can result in graft-versus-host disease [128]. A 
comparison of TIL and CAR T cell therapies is portrayed in Figure 4. 
 
The successful use of CAR-T cells against solid tumors has thus far been limited [128].   
Although the transfer of TILs is an attractive therapy against solid tumors, it also has its 
limitations. A percentage of patients don’t respond or stop responding to treatment due to 
innate or acquired resistance. This resistance is often due to a lack of functional, tumor-specific 
T cells within the tumor and the immunosuppressive tumor microenvironment [118]. This 
makes combination treatments with other immunotherapies such as checkpoint inhibitors [129-
131], and cancer vaccines an exciting option.

1.4.1 Combining vaccination and adoptive cell therapy 
One of the hallmarks defining successful TIL therapy is the persistence of tumor-reactive T-
cells [132, 133]. TIL persistence is greatly improved by preconditioning and administration of 
IL-2 following ACT. It could be further enhanced by augmenting T cell survival and 
proliferation with antigen stimulation in vivo, providing a rationale for pursuing combination 
therapy with ACT and vaccination [134].  
 
The combination of ACT with vaccination has shown promising results in preclinical studies, 
mediating tumor regression in mice, and improving the efficacy of adoptively transferred T 
cells [135-137]. The combination treatment has also been tested in the clinic. In a recent clinical 
trial, patients carrying NY-ESO-1 positive advanced sarcoma or melanoma were treated with 
ACT of NY-ESO-1 specific T cells combined with NY-ESO-1 DC vaccination. Two out of six 
patients showed an objective response, and one showed complete tumor regression. The study 
showed safety and feasibility but the effects of the booster vaccinations in the weeks after TIL 
therapy were considered minimal [138]. Another recent trial reported enhanced persistence of 
CAR T cells when given in combination with vaccination [139]. Cancer vaccination can also 
be administered before TIL harvest to prime CD8+ T cells prior to harvest [140-142]. Further 
studies are required to investigate the synergistic effects of combination therapy with ACT and 
therapeutic cancer vaccination.

2 Aim 
The research lab of Thomas L. Andresen at DTU Health Technology has developed a cancer 
vaccine platform that allows for systemic delivery of TAAs in conjunction with a potent TLR7 
agonist, co-formulated into a liposome. The TAAs are linked to the liposome through a 
reducible linker which releases antigen upon cellular uptake. The research group has gathered 
abundant amounts of data validating the platform in the context of the high-affinity model 
antigen ovalbumin (OVA) (OVA257-264, SIINFEKL) linked to a liposome through a cholesterol 
linker, both as a monotherapy and in combination with ACT of CD8+ T cells.  
 
This thesis will focus on further validating the platform using a new DSPE-PEG linker that is 
more easily reducible and could therefore deliver more antigen upon cellular uptake. The 
platform will be validated in the context of the high-affinity antigen SIINFEKL, and the low-
affinity antigen SIITFEKL. Experiments are conducted in the preclinical, syngeneic murine 
B16-OVA melanoma model with transgenic OVA expression. The aim of this thesis can be 
divided into the following categories: 
 
1) Validating whether antigen presentation and DC maturation in vitro can be improved 
using a reducible linker system.  
 
2) Comparing whether intravenous or intratumoral vaccination provides better antigen 
delivery to cDCs within the tumor and tdLN in vivo. 
 
3) Evaluating the effects of liposomal vaccination on the immune environment within the 
tumor and secondary lymphoid organs, with focus on cDC1 maturation and antigen 
presentation, as well as immunosuppressive MDSCs. 
 
4) Evaluating whether vaccination with low-affinity antigens improves T cell responses 
in the setting of therapeutic vaccination in vivo.  
 
5) Evaluating the therapeutic effect of low-affinity antigen vaccination in combination 
with adoptive T cell therapy.

3.3.1 Isolation and generation of BMDCs

4 Results 
This thesis focuses on evaluating a liposomal vaccine platform, established in the research 
group, in vitro and in vivo. Experiments will focus on validating the platform in terms of DC 
maturation and antigen presentation, T cell proliferation and infiltration, and antitumor effects. 
This platform enables systemic delivery of tumor-associated antigens (TAAs) in conjunction 
with the potent TLR7 agonist TMX, co-formulated into a liposome (Figure 5A). TLR7 
agonists have been extensively researched as adjuvants in immunotherapy [143], and have been 
demonstrated to activate APCs and induce antigen-specific CD8+ T cell responses [144]. The 
liposomes are PEGylated and thus, classified as ‘Stealth’, which has been shown to extend the 
blood circulation time of liposomes [145]. The TAAs are linked to the liposome through a 
reducible linker, which upon cellular uptake, release the antigen intracellularly (Figure 5B.). 
For activation of CD8+ T cell responses, the TAAs need to be presented in MHC-I, which 
cDC1s are capable of through a process called cross-presentation [28]. 
 
As mentioned above in section 2, the vaccine platform has been extensively validated in terms 
of the high-affinity antigen SIINFEKL and CD8+ T cell immunity. We will compare liposomes 
using two linker systems. One being a cholesterol linker system, also termed “old linker” and 
the other being a DSPE-PEG linker system, also termed “new linker”.  The liposomes carrying 
the cholesterol linker will hereafter be named Stealth:TMX:MK062, and the liposomes 
carrying 
the 
DSPE-PEG 
linker 
will 
be 
named 
Stealth:TMX:AC067 
and 
Stealth:TMX:AC068. Stealth:TMX:MK062 and Stealth:TMX:AC067 liposomes carry the 
high-affinity antigen SIINFEKL (OVA257-264), while Stealth:TMX:AC068 liposomes carry 
a lower affinity version of the OVA antigen, SIITFEKL (Figure 5C). Liposomes that do not 
carry antigen are termed Stealth:TMX. The physicochemical characteristics of liposomes used 
in this thesis are presented in Figure 5D.

4.5 Liposomal vaccination with high- and low-affinity antigens induces 
proliferation and tumor infiltration of adoptively transferred CD8+ T 
cells 
For an effective immune response to take place, a robust expansion of effector CD8+ T cells 
is required. In cancer, antitumor T cells include CD8+ T cells with a high affinity for mutated 
TSAs and CD8+ T cells with low affinity for nonmutated TAAs. Since TSAs are often patient-
specific, cancer vaccines usually focus on TAA vaccination and therefore face the challenge 
of activating low-affinity T cells  [82]. Several approaches have been developed to try to 
engage T cells with high avidity for tumor antigens [76-78]. Even though high-affinity antigens 
==PAGE:39==
 
32
are highly effective in inducing T cell expansion [74, 162], studies have shown that low-affinity 
antigens can have improved characteristics for immunotherapy. It has been reported that CD8+ 
T cells activated by low-affinity antigens show a lower expression of inhibitory molecules such 
as PD-1 and less exhausted phenotypes [82, 83, 163]. T cell exhaustion poses a real problem 
in cancer immunotherapy and is characterized by high expression of inhibitory receptors, 
decreased effector functions, and impaired proliferation [164]. In this context, we wanted to 
examine the engraftment, proliferation, and exhaustion of CD8+ T cells primed with high- or 
low-affinity antigens. We decided to compare our low- and high-affinity liposomes, 
Stealth:TMX:AC068 and Stealth:TMX:AC067, respectively, to delivery of unformulated 
TLR7 agonist and either the low-affinity antigen (SIITFEKL) or the high-affinity antigen 
(SIINFEKL). Since the TMX ligand is not soluble in PBS, we used Resiquimod (R848) as a 
control. R848 is a potent TLR7/8 agonist that has been thoroughly investigated as an adjuvant 
in immunotherapy [165]. While activating TLR7 and 8 in humans, it is a selective ligand for 
TLR7 in mice [166, 167], making it ideal for comparing to TMX which is a selective TLR7 
ligand. 
 
We harvested spleens from OT-I donor mice that have a transgenic TCR designed to recognize 
the SIINFEKL peptide bound to MHC-I. We stained the harvested splenocytes with CTV to 
be able to track their divisions and injected 5x106 splenocytes i.v. into B16-OVA tumor 
carrying female C57Bl/6JRj mice. The mice were preconditioned with CPX 24 hours before 
receiving the adoptive transfer. Mice were vaccinated 24 hours after receiving ACT. All groups 
received 10 µg of peptide either in the form of unformulated peptide or liposomes. Low-affinity 
groups received a TMX or R848 dose of 14 µg and high-affinity groups a TMX or R848 dose 
of 17 µg, due to the properties of the liposomes. Mice were either euthanized 48 hours after 
vaccination, allowing us to look at the engraftment and proliferation of the adoptively 
transferred CD8+ T cells in the tdLNs, or euthanized 120 hours after vaccination to look at 
engraftment of adoptively transferred CD8+ T cells in the tumor via flow cytometry (Figure 
10A). Adoptively transferred CD8+ T cells were gated as VD-, CD45+, CD3+, CD8+ and 
MHC-I:SIINFEKL dextramer+ (see gating strategy Supplementary Figure S5). The 
dextramer allows for identification of antigen-specific CD8+ T cells. PD-1 was utilized as a 
marker of T cell exhaustion [83, 168, 169], and CD44 as a marker of T cell activation [170]. 
The proliferation of adoptively transferred CD8+ T cells was estimated by a proliferation tool 
in the software FlowJo V.10.7.1.

5 Discussion 
This thesis has explored the effects of therapeutic liposomal cancer vaccination delivering 
high- or low-affinity OVA antigens in combination with a TLR7 agonist. The liposomal 
nanoparticles are highly flexible, allowing for the insertion of different antigens. The delivery 
of high-affinity antigen was evaluated in vitro by examining maturation and antigen 
presentation of BMDCs and in vivo, where optimal vaccination route was explored in regard 
to liposome delivery and DC maturation. The platform was further evaluated by determining 
the effects of high- and low-affinity antigens on T cell expansion, engraftment, and exhaustion. 
Additionally, the therapeutic effects of low-affinity cancer vaccination were explored. In this 
section, findings from this project, clinical relevance and proposals for future studies will be 
discussed. 
5.1 Intratumoral vaccination activates cross-presenting cDC1s 
5.1.1 DCs in the tumor and tdLNs take up liposomes following intratumoral 
vaccination in vivo  
Several injection routes have been studied for cancer vaccine delivery. These include 
intramuscular delivery, s.c. delivery, and i.v. delivery, each of them with their benefits and 
limitations. I.t. delivery of cancer immunotherapies can deliver significantly higher drug doses 
to the tumor than other injection routes [171, 172], triggering even more potent anti-tumor 
immune responses with less risk of toxicity than systemic delivery [173]. In the past few 
decades, i.t. delivery of TLR agonists has been widely tested and has been shown to enhance 
recruitment and activation of immune cells in tumors. Furthermore, i.t. delivery of peptides can 
enhance tumor cell antigenicity as peptide can be loaded onto MHC-I molecules of tumor cells, 
enhancing tumor cell recognition by antigen-specific CTLs [174]. Based on these benefits of 
i.t. vaccination reported in the literature, we decided to compare i.t. and i.v. delivery of 
liposomes by tracking DiI labeled liposomes via ex vivo flow cytometric analysis. We report 
that i.t. liposomal vaccination allowed for high uptake of liposomes by DCs within the tumor 
and tdLN, 4 hours and 24 hours after vaccination, providing antigen for presentation to T cells 
while i.v. injected liposomes accumulate in the spleen and tdLN but don’t reach the tumor 
within 24 hours (Figure 8). This is in line with other studies that report that i.t. injected 
nanoparticles concentrate in phagocytes in the tumor and tdLNs [175], and that only a fraction 
of i.v. injected nanoparticles end up in tumors, as they need to pass organs of the 
==PAGE:47==
 
40
reticuloendothelial system with the majority of them ending up in the spleen and liver [176, 
177]. 
5.1.2 Stealth:TMX:AC067 vaccination increases frequency and maturation of cross-
presenting cDC1s 
The presentation of exogenous antigens on MHC-I molecules by cDC1s is crucial to activate 
CD8+ T cells and generate an immune response against tumors. High tumor infiltration by 
cDC1s has been reported to have positive prognostic value in several different cancer models, 
including melanoma  [19, 178, 179]. DCs can be classified into two functional states, mature 
and immature. The hallmark of mature DCs being the ability to activate antigen specific naïve 
T cells in secondary lymphoid organs. Upon maturation, DCs upregulate MHC-I, MHC-II and 
costimulatory molecules, such as CD80 and CD86 [180]. Insufficient levels of antigen 
presentation by DCs, and antigen presentation by immature DCs can result in immunological 
tolerance through induction of T cell anergy and deletion, or through promotion of Treg 
differentiation [181].  
 
Due to this importance of DC maturation in cancer immunotherapy, we set out to analyze DC 
maturation in vitro and in vivo. We report that vaccination with Stealth:TMX, 
Stealth:TMX:MK062, and Stealth:TMX:AC067 induced DC maturation, evaluated by CD86 
expression, in vitro (Figure 6C and Figure 7D). The same was observed in vivo with both 
DCs (Figure 8) and cDC1s (Figure 9), showing a pattern of increased maturation in tdLNs, 
spleen, and tumor 24 hours after i.t. vaccination, although not significant in all organs. This is 
in line with other publications that report that TLR7 agonists activate cDC1s and enhance their 
cross-presenting abilities [45, 182, 183]. TLR7 expression on pDCs and their IFN release upon 
TLR7 agonist encounter has been well described, and results in DC and T cell activation [143]. 
However, TLR7 expression on murine cDC1s is debated [102, 103]. Stealth:TMX:AC067 
seems to induce cDC1 maturation to a slightly greater extent than Stealth:TMX, which suggests 
a possible antigen-dependent maturation effect (Figure 9). However, this difference was not 
significant and would therefore need to be further evaluated.  
 
Furthermore, we confirm that delivering SIINFEKL linked to the liposomes through a DSPE-
PEG linker (Stealth:TMX:AC067) instead of a cholesterol linker (Stealth:TMX:MK062) 
increases the duration and degree of antigen presentation in vitro (Figure 6D  and Figure 7E), 
and the frequency of antigen-presenting cDC1s in the tumor in vivo. The frequency of cross-
==PAGE:48==
 
41
presenting cDC1s is increased in the tdLN 24 hours after vaccination while decreased in the 
tumor (Figure 9). This is likely due to the migration of mature DCs from the tumor to the 
tdLNs to activate antigen-specific T cells. TLR7 activation and type I IFNs have been reported 
to increase CCR7 expression on DCs and enhance their migration to lymphoid tissue [184-
186].  
 
Collectively, we demonstrate that, by co-delivering tumor antigen and adjuvant via 
intratumoral immunization, our liposomal vaccine platform induces cDC1 maturation in the 
spleen and tdLNs and induces antigen presentation by cDC1s in the tumor and tdLNs.  
5.2 Vaccination increases the frequency of m-MDSCs with a mature 
phenotype 
One of the major barriers of successful immunotherapies is combatting immunosuppressive 
pathways and cells within the tumor microenvironment where MDSCs [63], and more 
specifically m-MDSCs play a significant role [187]. We observed an increase in m-MDSC 
frequency in the spleen, tdLN, and tumor following i.t. vaccinations, to a greater extent with 
Stealth:TMX:AC067 than Stealth:TMX  (Figure 9 and Supplementary Figure S2). This is 
in contrast with numerous studies reporting that administration of TLR7 agonists correlates 
with a decrease in intratumoral MDSCs. This has been reported for m-MDSCs [188], and g-
MDSCs [189] in the CT26 tumor model, as well as for MDSCs cultured from spleens of 4T1 
bearing mice [161]. However, expansion of MDSCs by adjuvants may not always be bad [190]. 
We also observe an upregulation of the costimulatory molecule CD86 on m-MDSCs in the 
tdLN and spleen and upregulation of MHC-II on m-MDSCs in the spleen (Figure 9 and 
Supplementary Figure S2). In the literature, it has been reported that TLR7 agonists induce 
m-MDSC differentiation into macrophages and DCs, and reduce their immunosuppressive 
activity [161]. Upregulation of costimulatory molecules and other molecules associated with 
an APC phenotype, such as CD86, MHC-II and CD11c, on MDSCs have been reported to 
correlate with less immunosuppressive functions. Instead, these mature m-MDSCs represent 
an antigen-presenting phenotype, capable of inducing T-cell proliferation [158, 161, 188, 191].  
 
The effect of this m-MDSC increase and possible phenotypic change would need to be 
investigated further. For future studies it would be interesting to carry out an in vitro assay 
==PAGE:49==
 
42
using MDSCs pulsed with the liposomal vaccine formulations and look at their suppressive 
abilities in a coculture with T cells. 
5.3 Evaluation of CD8+ T cell responses to vaccination with high- and 
low- affinity antigens 
5.3.1 Liposomal vaccination improves tumor infiltration of antigen specific CD8+ T 
cells 
To validate the benefits of delivering TLR7 agonist and peptide via our liposomal platform we 
wanted to compare the benefits of liposomal delivery of antigens to delivering unformulated 
antigens along with a TLR7 agonist. The TLR7/8 agonist R848 has been used as an adjuvant 
in several explorative immune therapies [192-195]. However, when given systematically, R848 
has a short in vivo half-life [196], and is quickly cleared by the renal system so insufficient 
drug concentrations can reach the targeted area [197]. Additionally, toxicities are often 
observed due to systemic cytokine release [198-200]. Both limitations can be combatted by 
nanoparticle delivery of TLR agonists [195, 201].  
 
We decided to compare liposomal antigen delivery to the delivery of R848 and unformulated 
peptide in combination with ACT of antigen-specific OT-I splenocytes. We report that 48 hours 
after i.t. vaccination Stealth:TMX:AC067 and Stealth:TMX:AC068 induce proliferation of 
adoptively transferred, OVA-specific CTLs in the tdLN, to a similar extent as delivery of R848 
in combination with SIINFEKL and SIITFEKL respectively. Additionally, liposomal 
formulations induced extensive tumor infiltration of CTLs, which was not observed to the same 
extent when delivering R848 and unformulated peptide (Figure 10C and G). This increase in 
tumor infiltration of adoptively transferred cells provides evidence for the benefits of delivering 
therapeutic cancer vaccines in a nanoparticle system. Nanoparticles have a long retention time 
at the site of injection and enhance the uptake of antigens by APCs, allowing for sustained 
release of both adjuvant and antigen to APCs that may have synergistic effects [197, 201]. 
Others have reported potent T cell responses and immune cell infiltration following 
nanoparticle vaccination, attributed to prolonged and improved antigen presentation by DCs 
[202, 203].

43
5.3.2 Therapeutic vaccination with high- and low-affinity antigens reduces PD-1 
expression on antigen-specific CD8+ T cells in the tumor 
Antigen affinity greatly impacts T cell responses to cancer vaccines. Based on beneficial effects 
of low-affinity cancer vaccination reported in the literature [82, 83], we set out to compare T 
cell proliferation and exhaustion when adoptively transferred OT-I CTLs were activated with 
low-affinity peptide (SIITFEKL) or a high-affinity peptide (SIINFEKL). High-affinity 
vaccination either in the form of liposome (Stealth:TMX:AC067) or R848 + SIINFEKL 
induced CD8+ proliferation and expansion to a greater extent than low-affinity vaccination in 
the form of liposomes (Stealth:TMX:AC068) or R848 + SIITFEKL, preferential expansion of 
high-affinity T cells or T cells stimulated with high-affinity antigens has been reported by 
others [204, 205]. However, this increase in T cell expansion has not consistently been linked 
to greater antitumor responses [79-81]. Of interest is that even though high-affinity antigens 
led to greater expansion, the frequency of adoptively transferred T cells within the lymph nodes 
was lower. This poses the question of whether the highly proliferating T cells had started to 
migrate out of the lymph node (Figure 10 B and C).  In the context of infection, activated T 
cells leave the lymph nodes and migrate to inflamed non-lymphoid tissues a few days after 
initial antigen exposure [206]. A study examining tumor infiltration of adoptively transferred 
CD8+ T cells reported their presence in the tumor within 24 hours of transfer, with cross-
presenting APCs within the tumor, and tumor cells presenting antigen to the transferred T cells 
and activating them [207]. It would be interesting to investigate this further by excising tumors 
and tdLNs at this early timepoint and look at the distribution of adoptively transferred cells.  
 
Adoptively transferred T cells in the lymph node stimulated with high-affinity antigens also 
presented a higher PD-1 expression (Figure 10E).  PD-1 expression on naïve T cells is induced 
upon activation, and its level is related to the strength of TCR signaling. However, in chronic 
diseases, such as cancer, PD-1 expression on effector T cells is maintained, often leading to T 
cell dysfunction via PD-L1 negative signaling in the tumor [208].  
 
As discussed in section 5.3.2, we saw a greater infiltration of adoptively transferred antigen-
specific T cells in the tumor following high-affinity vaccination. Of interest is that adoptively 
transferred TILs from unvaccinated mice showed a major increase in expression of PD-1, 
suggesting that an adjuvant in the form of vaccination greatly influences the phenotype of 
adoptively transferred cells. Additionally, T cells activated with high-affinity antigens showed 
a lower expression of PD-1 than T cells activated with low-affinity antigens (Figure 10F, G, 
==PAGE:51==
 
44
I),  which is in contrast to what has been reported by others [82]. A study by Wu et al. reports 
that PD-1 upregulation induced by T cell priming in the tdLNs has no direct effect on the 
functionality and can be used as a marker of activation. In contrast, PD-1 expression on CD8+ 
T cells in tumors represents an exhausted or dysfunctional phenotype due to the high expression 
of PD-L1 in the tumor [209]. Other studies also report that CD8+ T expressing high levels of 
PD-1 are exhausted and lose the ability to eliminate cancer [88, 169]. The successful 
reinvigoration of T cell functions by PD-1 or PD-L1 blockade highlights the importance of this 
axis in T cell dysfunction [210]. Surprisingly, CD44 expression was downregulated on CD8+ 
T cells stimulated with SIINFEKL (Figure 10H). CD44 is upregulated ∼24-48 hours after 
activation of naïve T cells by antigen stimuli and remains high on effector and memory T cells 
[211]. A study from 2011 described that rapid expansion of CD8+ T cells by in vitro 
stimulation resulted in significant loss of CD44 expression [212]. However, CD44 
downregulation on TILs has not been well described in the literature, and would be interesting 
to examine further.  
 
To conclude we observed that high-affinity antigens induce proliferation of antigen-specific T 
cells and their infiltration into the tumor to a greater extent than low-affinity antigens. T cells 
stimulated with high-affinity antigens displayed a lower expression of PD-1 and lower 
expression of the activation marker CD44, suggesting that they are less activated. Based on 
these results, we cannot determine whether low-affinity vaccination provides benefits in the 
context of CD8+ T cell responses. However, we report a lower expression of PD-1 on 
adoptively transferred CD8+ T cells that have been stimulated with a TLR7 agonist in vivo, 
which demonstrates the promise of combining ACT with therapeutic cancer vaccination. In a 
recent study Zahm et al. also report that TLR agonists led to decrease in expression of PD-1 on 
antigen-activated CD8+ T cells. These effects were mediated by IL-12 and shown to improve 
antitumor immunity [213]. 
5.4 Therapeutic vaccination with low-affinity antigens does not 
significantly improve survival  
We decided to conduct an efficacy study to see if our liposomal platform could improve the 
efficacy of low-affinity cancer vaccination. In the B16-OVA tumor model, which is poorly 
immunogenic, making growth retardations challenging to achieve [214, 215], none of the 
treatments, R848 + SIITFEKL, Stealth:TMX, or Stealth:TMX:AC068, improve survival or 
==PAGE:52==
 
45
delay tumor growth as a monotherapy. There is a tendency for improved survival and tumor 
growth delay when Stealth:TMX:AC068, and Stealth:TMX are administered as a co-therapy 
with adoptive T cell transfer. Interestingly, coadministration of OT-I and Stealth:TMX 
performs just as well as coadministration of OT-I and Stealth:TMX:AC068 both in terms of 
survival and control of tumor growth. However, the delivery of unformulated SIITFEKL with 
R848 does not perform as well. This suggests that there is not necessarily an antigen-specific 
effect, but rather the liposomal delivery of the TLR7 agonist that has a therapeutic effect in this 
setting (Figure 11). Due to a small tumor size the day before adoptive transfer of OT-I T cells, 
mice did not receive lymphodepleting preconditioning. Lymphodepletion has been established 
as an important component of ACT as it eliminates competing endogenous lymphocytes, 
promotes engraftment of transferred cells, and causes immunogenic cell death of tumor cells 
[115]. It would be interesting to conduct another efficacy study combining ACT with the 
delivery of low- and high-affinity liposomes following lymphodepletion. 
5.5 The future of therapeutic cancer vaccines and clinical relevance  
Cancer vaccines aim to activate CTL responses against tumors, facilitating a cure for the 
current disease and protection against tumor relapse. Despite extensive interest in therapeutic 
cancer vaccines, their success in the clinic has been limited [68]. Novel vaccine platforms and 
combination strategies are required to enhance the clinical efficacy of therapeutic cancer 
vaccines. Indeed, combination with checkpoint blockade [216-218], and ACT [219-221] have 
achieved effective clinical responses, synergistically enhancing antitumor T-cell responses, 
and combatting immune evasion.  
 
T cell-based cancer immunotherapies face several challenges, such as dysfunctional T cell 
responses and immune suppression in the tumor microenvironment [121], which were explored 
during the course of this thesis. This novel liposomal vaccine platform has potent adjuvant 
activity in the form of a TLR7 agonist, is highly versatile, and can deliver any tumor antigen 
of choice.  
 
Stealth:TMX:AC067 liposomes induce cDC1 maturation and antigen presentation, critical for 
effective CD8+ T cell activation. Liposomal delivery of high- and low-affinity antigens greatly 
induces tumor infiltration of adoptive CD8+ T lymphocytes, battling an established problem 
in ACT that a low number of TILs manage to reach the tumor [222].  Collectively this shows 
==PAGE:53==
 
46
the clinical relevance of liposomal cancer vaccine platforms to elicit a more effective TIL 
response and promises in combination therapy with ACT. Even though we did not provide 
evidence for the benefits of vaccination with low-affinity antigens, we report that our liposomal 
vaccine platform successfully delivers the low-affinity antigen SIITFEKL and induces T cell 
proliferation and tumor infiltration of adoptively transferred cells. This is important since 
cancer vaccines in the clinic often need to activate T cells with low affinity against TAAs [74]. 
We report that vaccination with high- and low-affinity antigens greatly reduces PD-1 
expression on adoptively transferred cells compared to ACT without T cell activation by 
vaccination, which provides evidence that T cell stimulation with low- or high-affinity antigens 
in conjunction with a TLR7 agonist can battle the problem of TIL exhaustion. Future 
investigations are required to further illustrate the effects of antigen affinity on CD8+ T cell 
phenotypes, effector functions and therapeutic abilities. 
5.6 Potential future studies 
More studies would need to be conducted to determine whether low-affinity antigens provide 
improved characteristics for cancer immunotherapies.  
 
For future studies comparing CD8+ T cell phenotypes in relation to exhaustion, more markers 
would need to be included, such as CTLA4, TIM-3, LAG-3, and TIGIT. By looking at multiple 
exhaustion markers and inhibitory receptors we could more confidently conclude whether 
antigen affinity influences T cell exhaustion since PD-1 expression alone cannot correctly 
discriminate between exhausted and activated T cells. It would also be interesting to include a 
later time point, as in the context of persistent antigen stimulation in chronic infections it can 
take up to 2-4 weeks for T cells to develop an exhausted phenotype [89]. Furthermore, it would 
be interesting to look at the effector functions of TILs by examining their cytokine production 
since it has been reported that adoptively transferred high avidity CD8+ T cells lose their CTL 
effector functions following tumor infiltration [223]. This could be achieved by measuring 
effector cytokine production such as TNF-α and IFN-𝛾 by ELISA or cytotoxicity by chromium 
release assay [224].  To try to elucidate why T cells stimulated with high-affinity antigens had 
a lower expression of CD44, it would be interesting to include more T cell activation markers 
such as CD69 and CD25, and the adhesion molecule CD62L and see if the expression of those 
markers also confirms a less activated phenotype when T cells are stimulated with high-affinity 
antigens.

liposomes carrying the high-affinity antigen SIINFEKL have been shown to significantly 
improve the survival of B16.OVA and EG7.OVA tumor carrying mice [225]. A study

antitumor responses. In contrast, intermediate-affinity peptides promoted antitumor immunity

6 Conclusion 
The aim of this thesis was to evaluate a novel therapeutic cancer vaccine platform that allows 
for delivery of low- or high-affinity antigens in conjunction with a potent TLR7 agonist in the 
syngeneic B16-OVA melanoma model. The functionality of the liposomal particles was 
evaluated based on uptake by DCs and induction of DC maturation and antigen presentation in 
vitro and in vivo. Stealth:TMX:AC067 liposomes induced continuous DC maturation and 
antigen presentation in vitro. Furthermore, Stealth:TMX:AC067 liposomes were taken up by 
DCs in the tumor and tdLNs following i.t. vaccination and induced maturation of DCs and 
cDC1s, as well as cDC1 antigen-presenting abilities and migration to tdLNs. An increase in m-
MDSCs frequency that upregulated CD86 and MHC-II was detected in the tdLNs and spleen, 
with unknown effects on treatment response. To determine whether vaccination with low-
affinity antigens showed improved characteristics for cancer immunotherapy we compared 
vaccination with high- and low-affinity antigens, SIINFEKL and SIITFEKL, respectively. 
Adoptively transferred CD8+ T cells activated by high-affinity antigens showed an increase in 
PD-1 expression in the compared to those activated by low-affinity antigens in the tdLN, but 
not in the tumor. This suggests that they were activated to a greater extent but not showing an 
exhausted phenotype. Low-affinity antigen stimulated T cells in the tumor expressed higher 
levels of CD44, suggesting that they are more activated. In general, all vaccine formulations 
led to a decreased expression of PD-1 on TILs compared to unvaccinated mice, showing the 
promise of therapeutic vaccination as a combination therapy with ACT. Vaccination with 
Stealth:TMX:AC068 did not significantly improve survival, and only provided modest 
retardation of tumor growth.  
 
Findings from this study highlight the potential of this therapeutic cancer vaccine platform to 
induce maturation and cross-presentation by cDC1s, proliferation of CD8+ T cells, and their 
engraftment into the tumor. We present a highly flexible system, that can effectively deliver 
high- and low-affinity antigens to APCs. Future studies should aim to further compare 
vaccination with high- and low-affinity antigens in relation to therapeutic effects and CTL 
effector functions.
