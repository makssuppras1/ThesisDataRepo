Abstract
This thesis studies the Pickup and Delivery Problem with Time Windows (PDPTW), with a set
of additional side-constraints given by the real-life problem studied. These constraints include
an heterogeneous ﬂeet of vehicles and truck drivers’ break regulations. We propose a two-stage
algorithm to solve this problem, the route minimization is performed by a Guided Ejection
Search (GES) metaheuristic, and the cost minimization by an Adaptative Large Neighborhood
Search (ALNS). The main contribution of this thesis is the integration of the heterogeneous
ﬂeet into these metaheuristic frameworks.
The implementation is parameter tuned, and then tested on several industrial-scale instances
provided by AMCS. A comparison is done between the GES and an adapted version of the
ALNS for the route minimization phase. These results are analysed and some suggestions are
made on how to improve the heuristic in further research.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Context Description
During the last decades the global economical context has put more pressure on companies
and states to aim at a better utilization of their resources, and an optimization of their oper-
ations. This pressure is increased by the ecological crisis that also pushes decision-makers and
companies to use the available resources in the best way possible. Most economical sectors are
concerned by this global context, which has become the main concern of planners. Fortunately,
the technical evolution of computers, as well as the proliﬁc research performed during the last
decades, have increased and improved the portfolio of data-driven decision-making tools avail-
able for the diﬀerent aspects of planning.
This is especially true for logistics, where the need of eﬃcient transportation services is increas-
ing worldwide. It is indeed not unusual for the transportation cost to represent 20% of the total
cost of a product (Hoﬀet al. 2010). Moreover, for every economy worldwide, the transporta-
tion sector weights for a signiﬁcant part of the gross domestic product (Hoﬀet al. 2010) and
employs a signiﬁcant part of the active population (13 millions of people in the U.S (TET 2017
Chapter 4 – Transportation Employment) and 10 millions in E.U. (Transport Sector Economic
Analysis)). The increasing competition in the transportation sector has put more pressure on
its actors to improve eﬃciency, cost, service quality and agility. Environmental issues are more
and more becoming a major concern at a societal scale, which reﬂects on the transport sector,
responsible for 14% of global greenhouse gas emission, with three quarters of this amount from
road transport (Piecyk and McKinnon 2010). As such, logistic decision-makers and planners
are left with no choice than optimizing their operations.
1.2
Research Focus
The Vehicle Routing Problem (VRP) has been extensively researched since its ﬁrst description
by George Dantzig and John Ramser in 1959 (Dantzig and Ramser 1959) as an extension of the
Travelling Salesman Problem. Despite its simple and elegant mathematical formulation, the

VRP has been proven to be NP-hard (Lenstra and Kan 1981). The research on the VRP has
been divided into several categories depending on the diﬀerent characteristics and constraints
included. The VRP taxonomy will be developed further in section 2.4.
The problem studied through this thesis is provided by AMCS Denmark. A detailed description
will be presented in section 2. The problem consists of a list of requests that need to be moved
from one location (pickup) to another location (delivery) within a given time window. A list of
vehicles with diﬀerent properties is available to perform these pickups and deliveries. The aim
of this thesis is to design and implement an algorithm that creates a feasible routing plan of the
vehicle ﬂeet to complete all orders within their given time windows. A routing plan consists of
the list of vehicle used, and for each vehicle in the solution, the ordered list of operations to
perform. The main objective is to minimize the number of vehicles used in the routing plan,
associated with a secondary objective of minimizing the total cost of the routing plan. Some
real-life constraints are taken into account in this thesis, especially the EU regulations on trucks
drivers’ working time.
1.3
Methodology
Despite its apparent simple description, the problem considered is rather complex, especially
due to the Pickup and Delivery structure and the real-life side constraints that make the so-
lution evaluation more complex and increase the number of variables. Moreover the instances
provided by AMCS are rather large, between 200 and 3000 requests (these instances will be de-
scribed further in section 7). Thus, exact solution methods can not be used to solve eﬀectively
this problem in a reasonable computation time. In this thesis a two-phase approach is used to
tackle the problem, which is a common method used in state of the art related literature (see
section 3 for further description). In the ﬁrst phase of the algorithm the number of routes is
minimized, then the total cost is optimized in the second phase. This approach allows us to
independently develop performing algorithms for the route minimization and cost minimization.
Two metaheuristic frameworks are presented in this thesis to solve the two objectives :
• A Guided Ejection Search (GES) for the route minimization. The initial solution for
the GES is created by assigning every request to a diﬀerent route. At each iteration a
random route is deleted and its requests are inserted in an ejection pool. The requests
in the ejection pool are then inserted again in the solution, potentially by ejecting other
requests into the pool. There is a penalty associated with each request, this penalty
increases every time a request is ejected, labelling it as "complex" to insert, and thus
more diﬃcult to eject again. A perturbation procedure is called when a request is ejected
to avoid local optima, it consists of several iterations of local moves. The implementation
of this algorithm is detailed in section 5.
• An Adaptive Large Neighborhood Search (ALNS) for both route and cost minimization.
The ALNS starts from an initial solution generated by a simple heuristic. At each iteration
a relatively large part of the requests is removed, and then inserted again using diﬀerent

methods. The particularity of this framework is the large part of the solution that is
destroyed at each iteration (up to 30/40% of the requests). The adaptive feature consists
of the use of diﬀerent removal and insert methods with a roulette-wheel selection. The
probability of each method to be selected depends on the results of the given method
in the past iterations.
The design of this algorithm focus on cost minimization, the
optimization of the number of routes is implemented to have a comparison with the GES.
Each algorithm uses a set of parameters whose values have a signiﬁcant impact on the perfor-
mance, especially for the ALNS. Thus a stage of parameter tuning will be performed using the
instances provided by AMCS in section 7.

2.1
Detailed Description of the Problem
The aim of the problem is to generate a routing plan. A routing plan consists of a list of vehicles
used in the solution and, for each vehicle, the ordered list of tasks to be performed associated
with their starting time. The aim is to design a plan for one day, but since some routes can
cross midnight the time horizon considered is 48h. The length of each route is however limited
to 24h, to create a cyclic plan over several days. Figure 2.1 presents an illustration of the cyclic
plan. The order (b) that cross midnight only have one time window (TW), whereas the two
other orders (a) and (c) have a TW that does not cross midnight, they can therefore be served
on day 1 or on day 2. One can see that order (a) is served on day 1, but since the route (lasting
less than 24h) will be repeated (represented by dots on the ﬁgure) on the next day the order
will also be served on day 2. On the opposite, order (c) is scheduled on the route on day 2, but
it will also be served on day one in reality. Therefore, each order only needs to be scheduled
once, as long as the routes are limited to 24h. On some of the scenarios provided by AMCS
the logic of attributing an order to day one or two was already performed.
The nodes locations are separated in two categories: terminal nodes and customer nodes. The
only diﬀerence between the two is that routes must start and end at a terminal location. Figure
2.2 shows an example of node locations, with terminal locations in blue and customer locations
in red.
There are several diﬀerent types of vehicles that can be used in a routing plan, each associated
with diﬀerent properties. The number of vehicles of one type is limited, thus the choice of

2.2
Mathematical Formulation
In this section we will present the mathematical formulation of the problem as well as the
notations that will be used throughout this paper. A summary of the notations is presented
in section 2.3. The aim of this section is to deﬁne a rigorous mathematical model to represent
the problem. Since we are not using any method of mathematical programming, we made the
choice not to use a Mix Integer Programming (MIP) model, but a more abstract formulation.
This formulation has the advantage of being more elegant and easy to read, while remaining as
rigorous as a MIP model.
The problem is deﬁned on a directed graph G = (V, E) with a set of vertices V = {1, ..., M}
and a set of edges E = {(i, j) ∈V 2 | i ̸= j}. The nodes in V represent the diﬀerent loca-
tions (terminals and customers indiﬀerently). The problem is also deﬁned on a time horizon
T = {0, ..., Tmax}.
==PAGE:17==

8
Requests
The orders are separated in two categories : the pickup orders P = {pi, i = 1, ..., N/2} and
the delivery orders D = {di, i = 1, ..., N/2}, N being the total number of orders. The set
of orders is then O = P ∪D. It is assumed that P ∩D = ∅i.e. an order is either a pickup
or a delivery, but can not be both. Let H = {hi = (pi, di) ∈P × D} be the set of requests,
where each request is composed of one pickup order and one delivery order; each order o ∈O
is characterised by the following parameters:
• Its location loco ∈V .
• Its time windows [ao, bo] when the order should be serviced.
• Its quantity qo. A pickup order is deﬁned by qo > 0, and a delivery order by qo < 0.
Moreover, the two orders of an associated pair pickup/delivery have the same quantity,
with an opposite sign.
Vehicles
The vehicle ﬂeet is not homogeneous, which means there are diﬀerent vehicles available to cre-
ate a routing plan. Let W = {1, ...Nveh} be the set of available vehicles. In practice there is
a limited amount of diﬀerent vehicles, each of them available up to a certain amount. Each
vehicle v ∈W possess a maximum capacity Qv that should not be exceeded during a route. A
vehicle v ∈W is also associated with a starting terminal termv ∈V , where the vehicle must
start and end its route.
Furthermore, we deﬁne the following functions to deﬁne the constraints between vehicles and
orders:
• comp(o, v) represents the compatibility between a vehicle v ∈W and an order o ∈O.
comp(o, v) = 1 if the is order o ∈O can be serviced by vehicle v ∈W, 0 otherwise.
• hand(o, v) is the handling time of order o ∈O and vehicle v ∈W.
Other parameters
This paper takes into account E.U. regulations on truck drivers breaks, that will be explained
further in chapters 3 and 4. A break has a duration of Tbreak. The maximum accumulated
driving time without a break is Tdriving, and the accumulated working time without a break is
Tworking. Moreover there is a maximum time between two visits at the terminal for the vehicles,
to permit a change of driver, i.e. a route has to schedule one visit at its associated terminal
location at maximum Tsegment time units after the last visit of this terminal. There is also a
maximum total time for a route, which is Troute. In practice this time is 24h, to be able to
integrate the route in a cyclic plan.
Each directed edge (i, j) ∈E is associated with a distance disti,j and a driving time ti,j. We
suppose that both times and distances respect the triangle inequality and are non-negative.

Variables
The variables used in this problem are the diﬀerent routes present in the solution.
Let R be the set of routes, a route r ∈R is characterised by :
• The vehicle servicing the route rv ∈W.
• The sequence of orders to serve rorders =< o0, o1, ..., on, on+1 >.
Orders o0 and on+1
represent the terminal node associated with the vehicle servicing route rv, i.e. termrv. By
convention these two "dummy orders" are deﬁned with no handling time, i.e. hand(o0, v) =
hand(on+1, v) = 0 ∀v ∈W, no quantity, i.e. qo0 = qon+1 = 0, and with no time windows
[ao0, bo0] = [aon+1, bon+1] = T.
• The set of times where the service of each order in the route starts rserv = {so, o ∈rorders}
with so ∈T for all o ∈O. Throughout this paper, the service start of an order o ∈O
refers to the time when the handling of this order begins.
• The set of break times rbreaks. A break time tbreak ∈rbreaks is deﬁned as the starting time
of a break, thus no activity can be scheduled during the time interval [tbreak, tbreak +Tbreak]
on the corresponding route.
Let Σ be the set of solutions. A solution σ ∈Σ is deﬁned by the set of its routes : σ = {r1, ..., rk}
with ri ∈R, ∀1 ≤i ≤k, k being the number of routes in the solution σ.

2.4
Classiﬁcation
First introduced in (Dantzig and Ramser 1959) as an extension of Travelling Salesman Problem,
the Vehicle Routing Problem (VRP) has been extensively studied by the operation research
community. Many variants of the problem have appeared to model diﬀerent real-life constraints
and problem structures. In this section we will look at the common nomenclature for the diﬀer-
ent variants of the VRP and classify the present problem with respect to the usual taxonomy
found in the literature. Figure 2.4 shows the links between the main variants of the VRP.
Figure 2.4: Map of the main VRP subproblems (Map of VRP subproblems)
The classiﬁcation used is based on the state of the art classiﬁcation and review of (Braekers
et al. 2016), which reviewed the published literature on the VRP between 2009 and 2015 on 31
journals. They extended the work of (Eksioglu et al. 2009) on the VRP taxonomy.
Firstly the problem at hand is deterministic, as all the distances, travel times and orders are
known in advance and no stochasticity is involved in the problem deﬁnition, nor the data pro-
vided. The aim of this paper is to create a routing plan that is part of a cyclic master plan.
Thus, the problem is static as it will not be updated with the vehicles and orders status during
its run time, as opposed to dynamic problems that update the plan as soon as new information
about orders and driving times become available.
Each order in the problem possess a quantity and vehicles have a limited capacity that should
not be exceeded. The problem also integrates time windows for each order, that should be
enforced. These time windows are qualiﬁed as "hard" as they cannot be violated, opposed to
"soft" time windows whose violation leads to a penalty.
==PAGE:25==
Description and Classiﬁcation of the Problem
16
The requests in the problem consist of a pickup and a delivery that should be placed on
the same route, one after the other.
This structure of problem is called Pickup and De-
livery Problem (PDP). (Berbeglia et al. 2007) deﬁned a more precise classiﬁcation of PDP
which we will use in this paper to deﬁne our problem. The problems are classiﬁed using the
[Structures|V isits|V ehicles].
The structure can either be One-to-One (1-1) if each request has one pickup node and one
delivery node, Many-to-Many (M-M) where every node can serve as a source or a destination
for any commodities, or One-to-Many-to-One (1-M-1) where commodities are available at the
terminal and are destined to customer nodes. Additionally, commodities are available at the
customer nodes to be transported to the terminal. The structure of our problem is One-to-One
(1-1) as each request has one pickup node and one delivery node.
The visits are deﬁned as the way pickup and delivery operations are performed at the diﬀerent
nodes. It can be either PD where, for one request, the pickup node and the delivery node
are visited exactly once, P-D if these operations can be performed together or separately, and
P/D where each customer, for a request, has a pickup or delivery order, but not both. In our
problem a request consists of a pickup at a location node, and a delivery at another node, which
are associated. The visits is then categorized as P/D.
Finally there is a set of diﬀerent vehicles, with a limited amount available. This is referred as
an heterogeneous vehicle ﬂeet, and is not really considered in (Berbeglia et al. 2007); we will
denote it H. The structure of our problem is then [1 −1|P/D|H].
(Koç et al. 2016) presents a survey and review of the existing literature on the heterogeneous
vehicle routing. One of the main contribution of this paper is a classiﬁcation framework for
such problems. We will classify the present problem with respect to this classiﬁcation.
The two main Heterogeneous Vehicle Routing Problems categories are deﬁned as:
• Fleet Size and Mix Vehicle Routing Problem (FSM) where the heterogeneous ﬂeet is
unlimited.
• Heterogeneous Fixed Fleet Vehicle Routing Problem (HF) where the ﬂeet is predeter-
mined.
Our problem possess a limited amount of vehicles to choose from in order to build the solution,
thus it is straightforwardly classiﬁed as a Heterogeneous Fixed Fleet Vehicle Routing Problem.
The second layer of the classiﬁcation concerns the nature of the objective function. In both
aforementioned cases the objective is to minimize a total cost function including a ﬁxed vehicle
cost (F) and a variable vehicle cost (V). The ﬁxed cost represents the cost of using a vehicle in
the solution, and the variable cost depends on the route scheduled with a given vehicle, often
being the distance cost. The distinction is thus made on which cost(s) are included in the given
problem, it can be only ﬁxed cost (F), only variable cost (V), or both costs (F,V).
The problem presented in this thesis includes:
==PAGE:26==
Description and Classiﬁcation of the Problem
17
• A ﬁxed cost represented by the number of vehicles used in the solution, as well as the
starting cost of the diﬀerent vehicles.
• A variable cost represented by the distance cost term and the time cost term in the
objective function presented in equation 2.14.
The last layer of classiﬁcation concerns the presence of time windows. Therefore our problem is
classiﬁed - with respect to this classiﬁcation - as a Heterogeneous Fixed Fleet Vehicle Routing
Problem with Time Windows and both objectives, i.e. HFTW(F,V).

Chapter 3
Literature Review
This chapter presents a brief review of the literature related to the present problem. The review
does not aim at being exhaustive on the proliﬁc VRP literature. The objective is more to present
several state-of-the-art algorithms relevant for the scope of this thesis, and explain the thought
process behind the choices of design in our algorithm. Therefore we exclude exact solution
methods from the scope of this literature study. Since the instances provided are of very large
size, especially with all the constraints considered, exact methods would be too time consuming
to produce good quality results in a reasonable time, thus the focus is put on heuristic methods.
An interested reader can nevertheless refer to (Braekers et al. 2016), (Eksioglu et al. 2009) or
(Berbeglia et al. 2007) for good quality literature surveys and reviews on the vehicle routing
problem.
The description and classiﬁcation performed in chapter 2 has highlighted the diversity and com-
plexity of the constraints we have to handle in this thesis. Most academic papers study simpler
versions of the VRP, and do not include a large number of side constraints. Therefore we will
present the relevant literature related to the diﬀerent components included in the problem.
3.1
The Pickup and Delivery Problem
In the early work on heuristic methods to solve the PDP, TABU-search (Nanry and Barnes
2000) and simulated annealing (Bent and Van Hentenryck 2004) were proposed, and new neigh-
borhoods structures adapted to the pickup and delivery constraints were introduced.
The Guided Ejection Search (GES) is a relatively recent metaheuristic framework ﬁrst in-
troduced in (Nagata and Bräysi 2009) as a new heuristic to address the route minimization
problem. In the aforementioned paper the algorithm is used to solve the VRPTW. The GES is
based on the ejection pool mechanism, enhanced by powerful insertion and guided local search
strategies.
18
==PAGE:28==
Literature Review
19
The GES metaheuristic framework has then been successfully applied to diﬀerent problems,
including the Job Shop Scheduling Problem in (Nagata and Tojo 2009) and the PDPTW in
(Nagata and Kobayashi 2010b).
In their paper, Nagata Y. and Kobayashi S. (Nagata and Kobayashi 2010b) applied the GES
framework to the Pickup and Delivery Problem with Time Windows, with some changes to
adapt their previous algorithm to the new constraints that modify the structure of the problem
and increase the complexity. At each high-level iteration one route is randomly removed from
the solution and its requests are put in an ejection pool. Then the second low-level loop tries to
insert all requests in the solution. At each iteration one request is ejected from the solution and
a simple pair insertion is tested in every possible way. If the insertion was not successful then
a second procedure is called to insert the aforementioned request by allowing the ejection of
other requests with a penalty-based objective. The solution is then perturbed randomly using
the iteration of two local search moves in order to add diversiﬁcation to the heuristic.
Despite its relative simplicity and only minimizing the number of routes, the GES has been very
successful achieving this objective. The results presented show good performances compared
to other state-of-the-art methods on Li and Lim benchmark instances (Li and Lim 2003), and
the algorithm remains very competitive with most recent methods (Curtois et al. 2018). For
these reasons the algorithm we implemented in this thesis is based on the GES framework and
is largely inspired by the algorithm designed in (Nagata and Kobayashi 2010b). Some features
have been added and some mechanisms adapted as it will be presented in chapter 5.
Nadata Y. and Kobayashi S. applied their GES algorithm as the ﬁrst phase of a memetic algo-
rithm to solve the PDPTW in (Nagata and Kobayashi 2010a). The term Memetic Algorithm
refers to a Genetic Algorithm hybridized by the use of other metaheuristics to enhance per-
formances. This metaheuristic is population based : instead of updating one solution at each
iteration, a population of several solutions is updated. At each iteration parent solutions are
selected and a crossover procedure creates child solutions by taking good proprieties from both
parents. Then child solutions are improved by an education procedure, based on local search
moves, to enhance the search capabilities of the algorithm. A mutation procedure is randomly
called to perturb the solution. Then the population is updated with the child solutions.
The main feature of this article is the design of a new crossover mechanism (namely the Selective
Route Crossover Exchange SREX). In fact, in genetic algorithms the design of a good crossover
mechanism is one of the main features that aﬀect performances (Nagata and Kobayashi 2010a).
The pickup and delivery constraints makes the search space of the PDPTW much more con-
strained than the VRPTW, and thus the design of an eﬃcient crossover that aﬀects meaningful
solution blocks from the parents to the oﬀspring becomes very hard. In the SREX crossover
introduced in this paper, the child solutions inherit whole routes from their parents. Then the
requests present twice due the transfer of whole routes from two diﬀerent solutions are ejected,
and the requests absent from the solution are inserted with a greedy insertion mechanism. The
partition of routes transferred between the two parent solutions is performed by a local search
heuristic procedure to minimize the number of requests to insert after the crossover.
This algorithm was seriously considered through the work done on this thesis, but time did not

allow us to ﬁnish the implementation.
Another state-of-the-art method for the PDPTW is the Adaptive Large Neighborhood Search
(ALNS) introduced in (Ropke and Pisinger 2006).
This paper extended the Large Neigh-
borhood Search (LNS) metaheuristic framework with adaptive features to enhance the search
performances. The LNS was ﬁrst introduced in (Shaw 1998), even if the concepts behind the al-
gorithm has been used before. When faced with tightly constrained problems, local moves used
in most local search heuristics make little change in the solution, making it diﬃcult to avoid
local optima. One solution to tackle this problem is to allow the search to look into infeasible
solutions by relaxing some constraints. Another method is the idea behind the LNS: for each
iteration to destroy a large part of the solution (up to 30-40% of the requests), and then insert
the removed requests again. This approach makes iterations longer than local search strate-
gies, but is able to make very large moves through the solution space, and thus to eﬃciently
diversify the search by looking into diﬀerent promising areas. The LNS framework has been
used successfully applied to a large variety of problems. We can cite (Bent and Van Hentenryck
2004) where the LNS is integrated in a two-stages algorithm for the VRPTW.
The ALNS proposed by (Ropke and Pisinger 2006) uses the same destroy/repair mechanism
as the LNS at each iteration, but with several diﬀerent methods for both the destroy and
repair phases. One destroy method and one repair method are selected at each iteration by a
roulette-wheel mechanism. Thus each method is associated with a weight, i.e. probability to
be chosen, that evolve depending on the eﬀectiveness of the given method. Thus the diﬀerent
weights system adapts itself to the stage of the search and the speciﬁcity of the instance at
hand, increasing the robustness of the algorithm.
The destroy methods used include:
• Random removal where the requests to remove are selected randomly.
• Related removal heuristic that aims at deleting requests that are "related" to each other.
This is measured by a relatedness measure taking into account the distance, the service
time, the capacity load and the vehicle compatibility. This heuristic has been introduced
in (Shaw 1997) and (Shaw 1998).
• Worst Removal that tries to delete requests that are expensive to serve, and thus seem
to be at a sub-optimal place in the solution.
The insertion methods include:
• A basic greedy heuristic that insert one request at each iteration, by making the move
that seems the best locally.
• A regret heuristic that extends the greedy insertion method by taking into account, for
each request, not only the best insertion but also the second (and more) best insertion(s).
The ALNS has shown very good results on the PDPTW, outperforming most other heuristic
methods at the time of its publication. This algorithm is still considered as one of the most
performing methods for this problem.

The good results and the robustness of the ALNS motivated our choice to use it as the frame-
work to design the cost minimization algorithm in this thesis. Indeed robustness is a very
important criteria for real-life applications, to be able to produce good quality solutions on one
run, on instances with diﬀerent characteristics. Moreover the ALNS is a very ﬂexible frame-
work, enabling us to eﬃciently incorporate all the side-constraints of the problem.
The work of (Santini et al. 2018) on the acceptance criteria for the ALNS helped for the design
of this part of our algorithm. The authors made a comparison between the diﬀerent acceptance
frameworks for the ALNS, including Record-to-Record Travel (RRT), Simulated Annealing
(SA), Hill-Climbing, etc. They tested these frameworks on three optimization problems: the
capacitated vehicle routing Problem (CVRP), the capacitated minimum spanning tree problem,
and the quadratic assignment problem. The CVRP is close enough to the problem studied in
this thesis to use their results for our choices of design. The tests were performed on instances
coming from diﬀerent benchmark sets commonly used in the literature. They show that the
Record-to-Record Travel performs better than the other acceptance criteria, with respect of
the statistical tests used.
More recent papers include (Curtois et al. 2018) where the authors have developed a powerful
hybrid algorithm that iterates three diﬀerent methods one after the other until the stopping
criteria is met. The aforementioned methods are:
• A local search procedure that uses four diﬀerent neighborhood structures. It follows an
Hill-Climbing strategy until no further improvement is possible with respect of all four
neighborhoods. The main purpose of this mechanism is to produce quickly good-quality
solutions.
• A simpliﬁed version of the ALNS presented in (Ropke and Pisinger 2006). The main
simpliﬁcation is the removal of the adaptative mechanism.
• An adapted version of the GES presented in (Nagata and Kobayashi 2010b). The al-
gorithm is largely inspired from the aforementioned paper, the main diﬀerence being on
the stopping condition to adapt the degree of intensiﬁcation according to the state of the
current solution.
Their algorithm has shown very good results on the Li and Lim benchmark instances (Li and
Lim 2003). It outperformed most state-of-the-art methods, ﬁnding new best solutions on a large
number of instances, and equalising already known best solutions on the majority of them.
3.2
Heterogeneous Fleet
The interest for heterogeneous ﬂeet structures in the VRP literature has increased the last two
decades and the topic has become a rich research area. Indeed in a large proportion of real-life
distribution problems the ﬂeet is composed of diﬀerent vehicles (Koç et al. 2016). (Koç et al.
2016) presents a good quality survey and review of the existing literature on the topic.
==PAGE:31==
Literature Review
22
Solution methods to address the heterogeneous ﬂeet problem with both ﬁxed cost and variable
costs includes (Lia et al. 2007) which presents a record-to-record travel algorithm. The record-
to-record framework is a variant of the Simulated Annealing (SA) with deterministic threshold
acceptance criteria. Indeed a new solution is accepted if its cost is below a certain threshold
above the best solution. The value of this threshold is decreasing with time to allow a lot of
diversiﬁcation in the beginning of the search, and intensify it at the end. The neighbourhood
structure is composed of diﬀerent local search moves.
(Euchi and Chabchoub 2010) also proposes a hybrid TABU-search algorithm to solve the prob-
lem with variable costs only. TABU-search metaheuristic is a local search strategy that relies
on a list of forbidden moves to avoid being trapped into local optima. Indeed the TABU list
keeps track of the previously visited solutions and their attributes to avoid cycling. In the
aforementioned paper the framework is enhanced by the use of an Adaptive Memory Proce-
dure. This adaptive memory keeps track of diﬀerent components of the encountered solutions
and periodically builds a new solution with the data present in memory. This is motivated by
the fact that good solutions can be found by combining diﬀerent components of other good
solutions.
In this thesis the solution method we developed does not rely much on the methods seen in
the literature. Indeed in most of the paper we have seen, the heterogeneous ﬂeet structure is
integrated in a metaheuristic framework, and we have not found any particular "trick" to handle
the ﬂeet. Moreover, we judged this feature of our problem less constraining on the structure than
the pickup and delivery constraints. Thus, we decided to implement metaheuristic frameworks
that have shown good results on the PDPTW, and then design new mechanisms adapted to
these frameworks to handle the heterogeneous ﬂeet feature.
3.3
Drivers’ working hours
The regulations on drivers’ working hours, integrated in this thesis, can have a big impact on a
routing plan for the VRP. Despite their importance for real-life applications, these regulations
have only received little attention from the Operation Research literature.
The EC regulation 561/2006 voted by the European Union and entered into force in April 2007
enforces the regulations and restrictions that apply to trucks drivers’ working hours. This text
imposes restrictions on working and driving hours in road transportation reaching from single
driving periods up to periods over several weeks. This law enforces the dispatchers to schedule
break and rest times in their routing plans (Meyer 2010). Figure 3.1 displays the diﬀerent basic
and optional rules deﬁned by the aforementioned regulation. In our problem we only apply the
rules concerning our time horizon. Moreover, one might notice that the rules applied in the
present thesis are a bit more restrictive than the ones in the legislation.
In (Goel 2009) the author presented two methods to schedule driving and break periods:
• A naive method that goes linearly through the route, from the start to the end, and
schedules breaks and rest periods when they are needed to enforce the regulation. This
==PAGE:32==
Literature Review
23

method has the advantage of being simple and fast, but is not able to optimize the position
of breaks in a route.
• A multi-label method that generates diﬀerent labels (i.e. breaks schedules) for a route
with a recursive function. The set of labels is updated continuously by pruning infeasible
and dominated labels. This method has better search capabilities than the naive one, but
is more time consuming. Indeed, the number of generated labels is important, even with
pruning the dominated ones.
These methods are then integrated in a simple LNS framework to solve the VRPTW, integrating
the aforementioned constraints. In the present thesis we decided to use a simple method for
breaks handling. The method presented in chapter 4 works with a similar mechanism than the
aforementioned naive method. This choice is motivated by the fact that our problem is already
complex, with large instances, therefore our algorithm is already relatively slow. We decided
to focus on running time for the breaks scheduling method.

Chapter 4
Travel time and feasibility
This chapter presents the low level mechanisms used in both algorithms to schedule breaks and
compute the driving time between the service of two orders. It also presents a quick procedure
to evaluate the feasibility of an insertion.
We made the choice not to allow infeasibility in our model; therefore, only feasible moves are
accepted, and every time a route is found infeasible it is discarded. The partial solution is
always feasible, with potentially some requests unplanned.
4.1
Breaks scheduling
We have already introduced in section 3.3 the problem of breaks scheduling. In the solution
method presented throughout this thesis we decided to use a rather simple and straightforward
method to handle these constraints. Considering the complexity of the problem and the size of
the instances, we estimated that the gain in term of solution quality with a more sophisticated
method is not worth the slower running time it would incur.

• The route time, which is the time between the last visit to the depot of the vehicle

4.2
Choice of time windows
As we presented in chapter 2, some orders have two time windows: one on day 1 and one on
day 2. On one of the instances provided by AMCS the logic of scheduling an order on day
1 or on day 2 is already done, but not on the others. To tackle this problem we add some
considerations to the method presented earlier.
First the start of a route is always scheduled on day 1. Indeed, the duration of a route is limited
to 24h, then there are two possibilities:
• The route crosses midnight. In this case there is no choice to make, obviously the start
of the route is scheduled on day 1.
• The route does not cross midnight. In this case every order present in the route possess
the two TW, on day 1 and 2, which are identical: if the TW on day 1 is 14h →16h, then
it is also 14h →16h on day 2. If the route is scheduled on day 2 then all orders can be
served on day 1 by the exact same route. Therefore the route can be scheduled on day 1
(and thus starts on day 1) without loss of generality.
The second issue is to choose between the two diﬀerent time windows to check the feasibility
of a route with respect of the TW constraints. To solve this problem, we just transform the
condition with an OR statement: to be feasible a route requires to have all its orders serviced
during their time window on day 1 OR on day 2. In the large majority of the cases at most
one the two TW is feasible. Indeed a route lasts for less than 24h, it is therefore really seldom
to have an order placed in a route that is feasible in terms of time windows both on day 1 and
day 2. This case might happen on orders with a very large TW though. However, an order
can stay with its day unspeciﬁed in a route without causing problem. Indeed the case where
the two TW are feasible only happens on routes with very few orders scheduled, otherwise the
other orders constraint too much the route. In fact it only happens at the very beginning of
the search, where the routes contain few orders.
Thus the choice on which time window to choose for one order is not really a choice since in

necessary to specify which one is used.
4.3
Insertion feasibility
For a good implementation of a heuristic method, one of the most critical part is the design
of an eﬃcient delta-evaluation procedure. A delta evaluation is a mechanism that enables the
algorithm to evaluate the feasibility and change of cost of a move, without having to recompute
everything. In this section we present the method used to evaluate the feasibility of an inser-
tion. It is quite clear that, with the breaks scheduling procedure presented, the insertion of an
order might disturb the time when breaks are scheduled after this order, and thus aﬀect the
TW feasibility of the following orders. In some cases one route can even become infeasible after
removing one order. Therefore it seems very complicated to accurately assess the feasibility of
an insertion in constant time.
The ﬁrst consideration to improve the eﬃciency of this delta-evaluation is that the times in a
route are computed in the driving order. Thus when inserting (or ejecting) an order in a route,
all the orders scheduled before are unaﬀected, thus we only need to recompute the parameters
related to the orders coming after it.
The second consideration is that, even if an evaluation in constant time is not possible, a lot
of insertions are obviously infeasible. We implemented a procedure that checks if it is worth
to compute the feasibility. This procedure works in constant time and aims at discarding ob-
viously infeasible insertions. It is not 100% accurate, the tests we have made shows that the
procedure can be wrong in both ways: a feasible insertion labelled as infeasible, and an infea-
sible one labelled as feasible. The second case is not a problem since the times in the route are
recomputed if the insertion seems feasible, thus it will be discarded if it actually is not. The
second case is more problematic. Indeed it reduces the amount of feasible moves, and thus the
quality of the search. However, the tests performed shows that this loss of quality is more than
compensated by the gain in running time.

Guided Ejection Search
This chapter presents the Guided Ejection Search (GES) algorithm implemented in this thesis
to minimize the number of routes. The GES framework has been brieﬂy introduced in section
3, the present chapter aims at describing in details our implementation. This algorithm is
mainly inspired from (Nagata and Kobayashi 2010b). We will ﬁrst present an overview of the
algorithm, then describe the diﬀerent mechanisms used in the procedure.
5.1
Overview of the Algorithm
Figure 5.1 presents an overview of the GES algorithm.
The algorithm starts by creating an initial solution where each request to insert is placed on
a diﬀerent route. Then, routes are removed one by one until the termination criterion is met.
The procedure to create the initial solution is presented in details in section 5.2.
At each iteration of the main loop one route is randomly removed from the solution and its
requests are added to the Ejection Pool (EP). The EP is a list that stores the unserved requests,
temporary missing from the solution. Then, the inside loop inserts the requests from the EP.
One request is ejected at each iteration, as presented in (Nagata and Kobayashi 2010b). If this
request can be inserted normally in the solution, the iteration stops; if it cannot, the penalty of
the request is increased and the k-ejection procedure, presented in section 5.3, is called to insert
it. The k-ejection procedure tries to insert the request in the solution by ejecting at most k
requests. Then the solution is perturbed by the procedure presented in 5.4. If the termination
criterion is met then the best feasible solution found is returned. In every step the partial
solution remains feasible, with some of the requests temporary placed in the ejection pool.

5.3
k-ejection
The k-ejection procedure is the main feature introduced by the GES framework. This procedure
is called when no feasible insertion is found for request hin. It aims at ﬁnding the best insertion
position by removing (ejecting) at most kmax requests present in the solution. Every possibility
of insertion and ejection of at most kmax requests is tested. kmax is a parameter that will be
tuned in chapter 7. The number of possibilities increases exponentially with kmax and the size
of the instance. In (Nagata and Kobayashi 2010b) a subprocedure is implemented to eﬃciently
evaluate the feasibility of an insertion/ejection move in constant time, in order to limit the time
consumption of the k-ejection. In this thesis the constraints on drivers’ hours regulations makes
it complicated to compute the feasibility of an insertion in constant time, as we have seen in
chapter 4. Therefore the method employed in (Nagata and Kobayashi 2010b) does not ﬁt well
with the problem of this thesis, and will not be used. This section presents the method we
designed to implement this mechanism, with an aim on limiting the computational time of the
procedure. The algorithm relies on recursive programming, to prune as much branches as possi-
ble on the search tree, and reduce the overall number of evaluations compared to a linear search.

3.3
Pruning the search tree
The k-ejection procedure tries every possibility of inserting the input request, by ejecting at
most kmax other requests from the solution. This is a very large number of possibilities, es-
pecially considering that the pickup and the delivery positions have to be considered. The
algorithm we developed in this section is designed recursively: every time insertPU or insertDel
calls itself again the maximum number of requests to eject k is reduced by one, which ensures
the termination of the algorithm.

of search tree that will not lead to an improvement. Indeed a possibility of insertion/ejections
is not evaluated in the following case to speed up the search compared to a linear procedure:
• If the vehicle servicing the route is not compatible with the request.
• If it is not possible to improve the penalty by ejecting a request. In this the whole branch
of this ejection is pruned.

5.4
Perturbation procedure
This section describes the perturbation procedure. The perturbation is called every time one or
more request is ejected, and aims at escaping local optima. (Ropke and Pisinger 2006) suggested
that very large moves (up to 30/40% of the solution) are a good method to escape local optima
in tightly constrained problems like the PDPTW. In the GES this diversiﬁcation is performed
by the iteration of local moves. An incumbent solution σ can be moved from a promising area
of the search space to another by the iteration of small moves. Indeed the constraints on σ are
relaxed a bit because of the requests in the ejection pool that are temporarily removed from
the solution. It consists of two diﬀerent mechanism:
• A vehicle perturbation procedure that chooses one route in the solution, based on its
penalty, and changes its vehicle to a random vehicle available. Then it ejects all requests
that became infeasible and add them to the ejection pool.
• The iteration of local search moves. Two diﬀerent neighborhoods are used for the local
search: the pair relocation and the extended pair exchange. This procedure is called with
a probability ppertu every time a request is removed from the solution.

4.2
Vehicle Perturbation
This mechanism aims at bringing diversiﬁcation into the search in terms of vehicles selected.
Without any mechanism to address the heterogeneous ﬂeet, the algorithm would be very in-
consistent. In fact, a route is removed by random at each iteration, thus a request with a large
quantity can become totally infeasible if no vehicle with a large enough capacity is present in
the solution. A request with few compatible vehicles could also become impossible to insert if
no such vehicle is present in the solution anymore. In this case the search would be trapped in
this local optima where a request is just impossible to insert.
Furthermore selecting a set of random vehicles will probably lead to a low quality solution, es-
pecially for the instances with several terminals. These considerations pushed us to implement
a mechanism to address this problem. At ﬁrst a random vehicle mutation was implemented,
but the preliminary results (that will be presented in 7) showed that despite improving the
solution quality, this mechanism was still too inconsistent to ensure good performances. The
new procedure presented in this section was designed to use the penalties of the requests, al-
ready implemented in the GES framework, to guide the mutation of vehicles as it does for
the insertion/ejection of requests. The procedure is presented in algorithm 6. The function is

penalty reaches 10, 20, 30, etc. This case is implemented to deal with a request becoming
infeasible, and blocking the search because no vehicle is compatible. In this case the

5.5
Discussion on the objective function
The GES algorithm we have presented in this section only considers the number of routes in
the solution in its objective function. Indeed the objective is only to minimize the number
of routes. However the diﬀerent vehicles are associated with a starting cost whenever they
are used. One could argue that minimizing the total vehicle cost (i.e. the starting cost of all
vehicles in the solution) would be a smarter objective. We believe that the GES will probably
perform better in minimizing the number of routes if the notion of cost is not implemented in
the framework since no other cost is considered in this algorithm. The optimization of the set
of vehicles used will be part of the cost minimization phase performed by ALNS. Indeed the
==PAGE:50==
Guided Ejection Search

ALNS is a framework that oﬀers a lot of diversiﬁcation possibility, and is able to move widely in
the solution space. Thus we think the ALNS will be eﬃcient to optimize the overall cost of the
working plan, which includes the starting vehicle costs. On the other hand the GES framework
accepts every move performed if it is feasible, therefore big vehicle moves could perturb the
solution too much, and thus prevent a good intensiﬁcation.

4.1

The regret heuristic is a parallel insertion heuristic that build several routes at the same time.
It was ﬁrst introduced in (Potvin and Rousseau 1993) to solve the VRPTW. In most cases this
procedure is called to insert a set of requests in a partial solution, the only time this procedure
is used to build a solution from scratch is for the initialization of the route minimization pro-
cedure, detailed further in section 6.7.
The idea behind the regret heuristic is to extend a basic greedy insertion heuristic that will just
insert the request with the lowest insertion cost at each iteration. This is done by incorporation
of a "look ahead" of the next move(s). Indeed a basic greedy heuristic will insert ﬁrst requests
that do not increase the cost much. Such requests are probably fairly easy to insert, and in-
serting them ﬁrst will constraint much more the solution when the "diﬃcult" requests will be
inserted. This is a problem since the insertion of complicated requests will be postponed, and
thus become even more costly. The regret heuristic tries to overcome this issue by inserting
ﬁrst requests that will become more diﬃcult if postponed.

4.2
Applying noise to the cost function
The k-regret insertion heuristic as presented is purely deterministic. In (Ropke and Pisinger
2006) the authors proposed to add noise when computing the cost of an insertion in ∆. The
aim is to add some diversiﬁcation to the procedure, not to always perform the move that seems
the best locally. This way more diversiﬁcation is included in the search.
Every time the cost C to insert a request into a route is computed, a noise term is also com-
puted. This noise term is picked randomly in the interval [−maxN, maxN]. Then the modiﬁed
cost C′ = max(0, C + noise) is used instead of C. At each high-level iteration of the ALNS,
the algorithm decides if noise is applied to the insertion heuristic or not, with the same weight
adjustment procedure used to choose a destroy method and an insertion method. This way the
probability of applying noise depends on the how successful this noise mechanism has been in
the past iterations.
For this mechanism not to be instance-dependant, the parameter maxN needs to be scaled by
the characteristics of the instance. To solve this issue we set:
maxN = η maxi,j∈V,v∈W(disti,jcdist(v) + ti,jctime(v))
The parameter η represents the amount of noise in a way that is not instance-dependant. This
parameter will be tuned in chapter 7. To compute maxN, η is multiplied by the maximum
driving cost between two locations of the instance.

6.5
Adaptive Weights Adjustment
In this section we described how the method selection procedure works, and how weights intro-
duced in section 6.1 are adjusted through the search. The main idea behind the ALNS is that
diﬀerent methods allow the algorithm to perform diﬀerent moves, with diﬀerent neighborhood
structures, and thus enhance the search capabilities. Moreover the probabilities associated with
these diﬀerent methods are not ﬁxed parameters, but are adjusted through the search. This
mechanism makes the algorithm more robust since one method can be very eﬃcient on one
type of instance, yet ineﬃcient on another type of instances. On the same way, one method
can be very performing at the beginning of the search, yet loose its usefulness at the end of the
search. With an adaptive mechanism the algorithm will adapt itself to the instance at hand,
as well to the stage of the search.
5.1
Method Selection
When selecting the methods at each iteration, as presented in section 6.1, there are several
choices to make:
• The swap method, which consists of the choice of the parameter δ between 1 and δmax,
or no swap at all.
• The destroy method, two choices are available here: the random removal and the related
removal.
• The repair method, which consists of the choice of the parameter k between 1 and kLNS
max ,
or the m-regret with k = m.
• If noise is applied or not in the repair procedure.
These diﬀerent choices are independent from each other, and the same selection mechanism is
used to perform these diﬀerent choices. Therefore we will present the selection procedure for
one mechanism.

destroy, repair, or noise. Each method is associated with a weight wi, i ∈{1, ..., n}. The ALNS
is initialized with all weights equal to 1. The mechanism used to choose one heuristic in M is
called the roulette-wheel selection. In this mechanism the probability for a method to be chosen
is proportional to its weight, this probability is given by the following formula:

5.2
Weights adjustment
The adaptive feature of the ALNS modiﬁes the values of the weights introduced in the previous
section depending on how performing a given method has been in the past iterations. For this
purpose some new parameters are introduced. For a one method mi ∈M we deﬁne:
• The score of the heuristic scorei, which is a measure on the eﬀectiveness of the method.
The score starts at 0 and is increased in two cases: if the heuristic produced a new best
solution the score is increased by σ1. If it produces a solution that is worst than the best
one, but is still accepted by the acceptance criterion (see section 6.6) then the score is
increased by σ2. σ1 an σ2 are two parameters that will be tuned in chapter 7.
• The counter of the heuristic ci, which counts how many times the heuristic mi has been
called.
• The segment length slength. One segment is just an amount of iterations that are performed
with the same weights. Thus the weights are not updated at each iteration, in order to
reduce the amount of statistical noise, but every slength iterations. If slength = 10 then the
weights are updated every 10 iterations, depending on the performances of the diﬀerent
heuristics during these 10 iterations. This parameter will also be tuned in chapter 7.
Then when a segment is ﬁnished the weight of method mi is computed with the following
formula:
wi ←(1 −µ)wi + µ scorei

If ci = 0 then the weight remains unchanged. The parameter µ controls how quickly the weights
are adjusting to the performances of the heuristics. If µ = 0 then the weights are constant and
does not adapt to the search. If µ = 1 then the weights are only computed using the results of
the previous segment. The parameter µ will be tuned in chapter 7.
Every time a segment is ﬁnished, and the weights adapted, the scores and counters of the dif-
ferent heuristics are reset to 0.
6.6
Acceptance Criterion
The acceptance criteria framework refers to the way new solutions are accepted or discarded.
Indeed only accepting solutions that improve the objective is a simple way to address this is-
sue, however it is not the best way to do so (Santini et al. 2018). We have already introduced
some acceptance criteria in chapter 3. The design of this part of the algorithm relies on the
work presented in (Santini et al. 2018). In this paper several acceptance criteria, including the
most common ones, are implemented in an ALNS framework and then tested extensively on
three diﬀerent problems. These test problems include the Capacitated Vehicle Routing Prob-
lem (CVRP). The problem studied in this thesis includes more constraints than the CVRP,

however we believe the two structures are close enough to extend the conclusions they draw

6.7
Route Minimization
The main purpose of the ALNS algorithm presented through this chapter is to minimize the
cost of the solution once the number of routes has already been minimized. Indeed a two-phase
algorithm enables us to design independently two diﬀerent algorithm, each of them tailored for
one speciﬁc objective. However we adapted the ALNS to also handle route minimization, in
order to have a comparison with the GES and be able to evaluate its performances.
There are only minor changes to adapt our algorithm for route minimization: we just call the
ALNS procedure on an incumbent partial solution, and the algorithm stops every time a fea-
sible solution including all requests is found. Then one route is removed by random and the
heuristic is called again, with the aim of ﬁnding a solution that plans all requests with one less
route. One change is that the weights of the diﬀerent methods are not reset when the heuristic
ﬁnd a feasible solution. Indeed at the beginning of the search a feasible solution is found quite
quickly and the weights would not have time to adjust themselves if the weights are reset each
time a route is removed. The second change is that not only feasible solutions are accepted as
best solution in the selection mechanism.
The ﬁrst iteration of the ALNS for route minimization is seeded with an initial solution. This
initial solution is obtained by the k-regret insertion heuristic. A ﬁrst solution is created with
one empty route for each vehicle available in the instance at hand. All requests of the instance
are placed in the list of unplanned requests D. The k-regret heuristic is then called with these
two arguments, and returns an incumbent solution we use for the ALNS.

7.2
Methodology
This section presents the methodology we have used to perform the tuning of the diﬀerent
parameters of the algorithm presented in this thesis. The duration of the thesis did not allow
us to perform the tuning as accurately as we would have liked to. We are aware that a more
extensive tuning would have probably lead to more accurate values for the parameters, and
thus to better performances. However we tried to design a strategy to enhance the quality of
the tuning as much as we could considering the time available. The diﬀerent choices we have
made are presented and discussed in this section.
Considering the highly probabilistic design of the solution method, each conﬁguration of pa-
rameters needs to be run several times to obtain some accuracy on the results. For this reason
==PAGE:66==
Parameters Tuning
57
it was decided to perform 10 runs for each conﬁguration of parameters, on all the test instances.
The running time for the full algorithm is set to 8h. This computation time is shared equally
between the two phases of the algorithm: 4h for route minimization, and 4h for cost optimiza-
tion. Considering the amount of runs that has to be performed, a running time of 8h was
too long to allow us to test everything. For that reason we decided to create a set of tuning
instances, smaller than the instances provided, and to perform the parameter tuning on these
small instances. Six test instances were therefore created by sampling the requests from In-
stance 1 and Instance 2: three test instances were created based on each of these instances.
We will refer to the instances provided by AMCS as large instances, and the one we created as
test instances. We believe that testing the parameters on several test instances, with diﬀerent
characteristics, will avoid to "overﬁt" the parameters on one instance. Indeed if the algorithm is
tuned on only one instance, it will be very eﬃcient for this instance, but less robust on diﬀerent
problems. It was decided to sample 200 requests randomly for each test instance. 200 requests
is a compromise between not having a problem too small - and thus too easy to solve - and
not having a problem too large, requiring a lot of computation time to obtain accurate results.
The test instances sampled from the large instance 1 are called Instances 1.1, 1.2, 1.3, the ones
sampled from instance 2 are called Instances 2,1, 2.2, 2.3.
The implementation of the algorithm has been done with the Julia language. All the runs done
for the parameter tuning have been launched on DTU High Performance Computing (HPC)
cluster. The tests have been launched on two types of machines: an Intel Xeon E5-2680v2 with
20 cores and 2.80GHz and an Intel Xeon E5-2660v3 with 20 cores and 2.60GHz. These two
machines have been used, however all the runs concerning the tuning of one parameter have
been performed on the same machine, to ensure a fair comparison. The implementation of our
algorithm has not been written to use parallel computing, each run uses only one core.
The time limit for the parameter tuning on the small instances has been set to 20 minutes per
run. Since the GES and the ALNS are tuned separately, the time limit of one run of parameter
tuning is 10min.
Then the general strategy for the parameter tuning is to tune one parameter at a time. Some
parameters that seem highly connected will also be tuned together. We believe that tuning
one parameter at a time is not the optimal way of doing, since some parameters are probably
linked together, but time did not permit us to explore much these connections.
First we start with a set of initial parameters based on the preliminary tests we have made
and the results found in the literature. Then one parameter is tuned at a time, sometimes 2.
For each parameter conﬁguration we performed 10 runs of 10 minutes on the 6 test instances
presented above. This makes a total of 60 runs for each parameter conﬁguration. We believe
this amount will reduce the statistical noise and lead to reliable results.
To ensure a fair comparison between the diﬀerent conﬁgurations, we introduce the score of a
set of parameters, computed as follow. First we keep track of the best solutions found for each
instance. For each conﬁguration we compute the average value of the objective for the two types
of test instances: the average objective value for the runs on instances 1.1, 1.2 and 1.3, and the
average value for instances 2.1,2.2 and 2.3. Then we need to scale these two average values,
==PAGE:67==
Parameters Tuning
58
otherwise the one with the largest objective will dominate over the other. To do this scaling
we divide the two values by the average best objective found on the respective set of instance,
i.e. the average objective value of one conﬁguration for instances 1.1, 1.2, 1.3 is divided by the
average of the three best objective found so far: the best objective on instance 1.1, the best
objective on instance 1.2 and the best objective on instance 1.3. Then we have two scores -
scaled - one for test instances 1.x and one for test instances 2.x (x ∈{1, 2, 3}). The total score
of the conﬁguration is the sum of these two scores.
Then the conﬁguration with the lowest score is preferred. The value of the tuned parameter is
updated for the next tests.

Tuning of maxRemoval
The results for the tuning of maxRemoval are displayed in table 7.26. The interpretation of
these results is straightforward. One can see that, after a minimum at maxRemoval = 30, the
performances are worsening gradually with the increase of the parameter. Indeed an increase
of maxRemoval means that more requests are removed at each iteration, thus there are more
requests to insert, and at the end the number of iterations performed during the tests of 10
minutes decreases. In (Ropke and Pisinger 2006) the authors found the best performances with
maxRemoval = 0.4. However the termination criterion in this paper is deﬁned on the number
of iterations, making the time of each iteration less crucial than in the present case where a time
==PAGE:79==
Parameters Tuning
70
limit stops the heuristic. Moreover one should note that in our version of the ALNS the vehicle
swap mechanism also ejects requests from the solution. If the amount of requests ejected by
the swap procedure reduces the value of maxRemoval the destroy methods are called with,
explaining why very low values of maxRemoval are performing well. At the end the value
maxRemoval = 0.15 shows the best performances, and is chosen.

Tuning of T0
The results for the tuning of T0 are displayed in table 7.27. The interpretation of these results is
clear: the performances improve until a minimum at T0 = 0.03, and worsen afterwards. T0 = 0
corresponds to only accepting solutions that improve the best cost found so far in the search.
The results therefore show that a more sophisticated acceptance criterion, like the Record-to-
Record travel chosen in this thesis, is an important feature in the ALNS. Indeed it enables the
search to accept non-improving solutions, and thus to move more freely in the solution space.
One can see that this feature add diversiﬁcation to the search without compromising much the
intensiﬁcation mechanisms. The parameter is set to the value T0 = 0.03, which presents the
best performances.

Detailed presentation of one run

parison between the two heuristics is not fair. Indeed The GES is only designed to minimize
the number of routes, without any consideration about the cost, whereas the ALNS is mainly
designed for cost optimization. Moreover the results of the parameter tuning performed for cost
minimization have also been applied for the route minimization. Even if the general algorithm
works in the same way, the values of the diﬀerent parameters would probably change if the
tuning was performed again on the route minimization problem.
The second observation that can be drawn from these results concerns the cost. Indeed one
can see that, despite presenting larger vehicle costs, the ALNS performs better than the GES
+ ALNS in terms of cost. This can be explained by the fact that, in this case, the algorithm
is taking the cost into account from the beginning of the search. However the most proba-
ble explanation is that a solution with more routes is less constrained, and thus allows more
possibilities. To test this hypothesis we performed 5 runs of 6 hours on instances 1 and 2.
The six hours of the run include 2 hours for route minimization with the GES, and 4h of cost

Chapter 9
Discussion
This chapter presents a critical discussion on the work done on this thesis. The limitations of
the model are described, as well as possible improvements and suggestions for future research.
Firstly we have seen in chapter 4 that the breaks scheduling procedure is rather simple and
does not optimize the breaks’ positions. The choice not to implement a more sophisticated
procedure was decided in order to keep the running time at a reasonable level. Indeed we
have seen that the algorithm is not able to ﬁnd the optimal breaks schedule when some order
conﬁgurations appear, making the solution more constrained.
Despite being seldomly studied in the literature, some papers present alternate scheduling
methods, as we have seen in chapter 3, for example the multi-label method introduced in (Goel
2009). Testing the algorithm with diﬀerent scheduling methods could possibly enhance the
search signiﬁcantly since the size of the feasible solution space would increase. However these
new possibilities would come with an increased running time, already long considering the
industrial-size of the problem instances. The method chosen in this thesis is the most straight-
forward, and thus fast, yet we believe that a better balance between search capabilities and
running time is possible.
Another way to improve the algorithm would be to perform a clustering of the requests. In-
deed the approach chosen in this thesis is to consider terminal locations as normal locations.
However most requests in the test instances have either the pickup order or the delivery order
located at a terminal location. The algorithm could probably beneﬁt from a clustering of the
requests around the diﬀerent terminals. Indeed the assignment of each request to a terminal
would reduce signiﬁcantly the number of insertion possibilities and the size of the diﬀerent
neighborhood structures used in this thesis, and thus reduce the overall running time of the
algorithm since less possibilities would have to be considered when looking for a possible inser-
tion of a request. The requests that have one of their orders at a terminal locations are easy
to cluster, yet that is not the case for all of them. An opportunity for further research would
be the design of an eﬃcient clustering procedure to assign each request to a terminal.
The parameter tuning presented in chapter 7 have been performed on smaller test instances
than the ones provided by AMCS, with a shorter running time than the tests performed on

the large instances. This choice was decided to keep the amount of time required to perform
all the tests at a reasonable level. However it is obvious that the results would have been more
accurate if the tuning was performed with the normal run conditions. Indeed even if some pa-
rameters are unaﬀected by the size of the instance, this is probably not the case for all of them.
Moreover the test instances were created by sampling the requests from two large instances,
the rest of the data being unchanged. This method probably changed a bit the structure of the
problem and the importance of the diﬀerent mechanisms. Indeed the test instances present, in
proportion, more vehicles available and more locations compared to the number of requests.
This result in a modiﬁed structure of the instances that could lead to diﬀerent tuning results.
Another downside of this thesis is the lack of comparison to benchmark the performances of the
algorithm developed. It is indeed complicated to assess the performances without comparing
the algorithm with other methods. The GES and ALNS metaheuristic frameworks have already
shown their performances on several optimization problems. However, the main contribution
of this thesis is the integration of the heterogeneous ﬂeet feature to these frameworks, that
could not be tested against other state-of-the-art methods. If further researches are done on
this problem it would be beneﬁcial to adapt the algorithm to more classical problems where
benchmark instances are available, in order to see the relevance of the design choices we made.
Finally the implementation of the algorithm in Julia is far from being ﬂawless. Our program-
ming experience was limited at the beginning of this thesis, and the proﬁling of the code showed
several bottlenecks in the implementation, as well as a large amount of memory consumption.
Time did not permit us to investigate much the optimization of code performances. We believe
that a seasoned programmer could signiﬁcantly speed up the implementation, leading to an
increase of the performances.

Chapter 10
Conclusion
In this thesis we studied a real-life Pickup and Delivery Problem with Time Windows. The
diﬀerent features of the problem have been analysed and classiﬁed according to the usual VRP
nomenclature. After performing a brief review of the state-of-the-art methods for the PDP, we
designed a two-phase algorithm to solve the present problem.
The minimization of the amount of routes in the solution is done by a Guided Ejection Search
metaheuristic, enhanced by a vehicle perturbation procedure to ﬁt the scope of this thesis. Then
the cost minimization is performed by an Adaptive Large Neighborhood Search, also enhanced
by a set of vehicle swap methods that ﬁt in the ALNS framework. Then the parameters used
by both algorithms were tuned on smaller test instances. At the end our algorithm was tested
on the industrial-size instances provided by AMCS, and compared with a pure ALNS. The
results seem promising, even though a comparison with other state-of-the-art methods would
be needed to accurately assess the performances.
The main contribution of this thesis is the integration of the heterogeneous vehicle ﬂeet opti-
mization into the two metaheuristic frameworks. This was achieved by the design of eﬃcient
vehicles swap procedures that ﬁt well in these frameworks. The results show that these mech-
anisms lead to better performances than a random vehicle swap.
