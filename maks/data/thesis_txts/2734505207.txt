Abstract
Dataยญdriven hydrological models, such as flood forecasting models, rely on clean data,
as missing data can lead to faulty or no forecasts. However, cleaning data is a tedious
and timeยญconsuming task, especially with an increased number of sensors. Consequently,
there is an interest in developing modelยญdriven approaches that automatically impute missยญ
ing data. This thesis investigates the potential of machine learning models for data impuยญ
tation in water level time series of Danish streams.
A recursive imputation strategy is employed, in which missing data points are sequentially
imputed by using oneยญstep ahead predictions as input for subsequent oneยญstep predicยญ
tions. The strategy can be applied to any underlying model, and different configurations
of Feed Forward Neural Networks (FFNN) and Long ShortยญTerm Memory (LSTM) models
were tested consecutively aiming at improving the imputation accuracy and determining
the most sutiable model for this task. The performance of these models in imputing data
gaps of varying length was assessed using metrics such as the Root Mean Squared Erยญ
ror (RMSE) and the Persistence Index (PI), which compares imputation against a simple,
naรฏve model that fills data gap with the last observed value.
Among the models tested, LSTMs trained on imputing data gaps of 24 or 48 hours with
precipitation as an additional input, demonstrated the best data imputation across multiple
gap lengths. The imputed water levels from these models deviate from original observaยญ
tions on average only by 0.03 m and 0.09 m for data gaps with a length of 24 and 168
hours, respectively, indicating that reliable imputation for data gaps of up to one week
can be achieved. Adding additional input features such as an indication of the time of
the year or other water level time series were not found to improve imputation accuracy
substantially. However, even though performance scores implied robust imputation, qualยญ
itative evaluation showed that there are still notable drawbacks in the data imputation,
such as overestimation of magnitude and duration of peak water levels. Therefore, furยญ
ther research on improving the data imputation is needed before the model can be reliably
employed for data cleaning purposes.

Introduction
Hydrological time series data on water levels is an important input parameter for various
applications such as flood control or water resource management [1, 2, 3]. However, the
data, usually measured automatically by sensors, is often incomplete or anomalous due
to environmental and technical issues or human activities [4, 5]. This causes problems
for applications such as flood warning systems requiring precise and timely data on water
levels, as missing data can result in wrong predictions or no forecast in dataยญdriven models
[6, 7, 8].
Heavy rain events have intensified in many regions over the past halfยญcentury which can
for instance be clearly seen in Europe and NorthยญAmerica. Furthermore, the probabilยญ
ity of extreme weather events leading to severe floods has increased due to climate
change. [9] Therefore, creating reliable flood prediction models enabling to implement
early flood warningยญsystems is crucial for mitigating the severe consequences of floods.
However, for this, complete records on water levels are crucial and thus, data imputation
algorithms ideally working automatically are needed.
Currently, miscellaneous methods exist for imputing data in hydrological time series:
โข Deletion methods: The most simple approach to data imputation are deletion methยญ
ods, erasing records containing missing values. However, apart from introducing
bias in the dataset and reducing the amount of available data, the time series data
becomes incomplete and thus, not anymore suitable for prediction models [8, 10].
โข Statistical methods: Missing values are replaced with some statistical value of the
time series e.g., mean, median or mode [5, 11]. Being a conventional choice for
imputation due to their simplicity, statistical methods underestimate the variance of
time series as the derived metrics can only take into account the present values
and thus, parameter estimates might be biased [12]. Hence, statistical methods
canโt actually reproduce real data as they furthermore are not capturing any dynamic
changes in the data [11].
โข Interpolation methods: Interpolation imputes data by fitting a smooth curve of a
chosen polynomial degree to the data set [13]. For instance, linear interpolation fits
a straight line between two adjacent points of each data gap in a time series [14].
Being sufficiently accurate if only one or a few data points are missing, performance
of interpolation methods decreases with increasing length of the data gap being
unable to capture temporal variations [6, 8]. Hence, these methods are only suitable
for stable periods without significant rainfall or flood events [15].
โข Regression based methods: Another wellยญknown and commonly used statistical
method for filling gaps in hydrological time series data is the regression based Auยญ
toregressive Moving Average (ARIMA) method using temporal autocorrelation and
nearest neighbor methods for fitting a regression model to the data [11, 10]. Howยญ
ever, assuming linear dependence between neighboring data points, ARIMA fails
to capture nonlinear trends [11, 16]. As these are an inherent feature of hydroยญ
logical data, this method performs in particular bad for longer data gaps, where
multiple nonlinear processes are involved [17]. Furthermore, being limited to uniยญ
variate input, ARIMA canโt take advantage of spatioยญtemporal correlation to other
input features [11].

โข Physicalยญbased methods: These methods, e.g., the Soil and Water Assessment
Tool [18, 19], impute data based on hydrological models simulating physical atยญ

The downside of physicalยญbased methods are that they are timeยญconsuming and
labour expensive to set up, as they are highly dataยญintensive and require expert
domain knowledge for calibration [20]. Additionally, these methods have long comยญ
putation times, as e.g., a whole catchment needs to be modeled [14, 21].
โข Machine learning methods: In recent decades machine learning models have reยญ
ceived considerable attention and a number of methods were explored for imputing
hydrological data e.g., Random Forest [12], MissForest [22, 23, 6], Kยญnearest Neighยญ
bors [12, 6], Full Subsequence Matching [6] and Adaptive NeuroยญFuzzy Inference
System [24, 25]. Being both simple and effective, these machine learning models
have shown to outperform data imputation of statistical methods. [25]. However,
training of machine learning models remains challenging as they might overfit or
converge to local minima [26, 27].
โข Deep learning methods: Deep learning methods are a subset of machine learning
methods that are based on Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs) [28]. In contrast to
machine learning models, these dataยญdriven models do not require structured and
labeled data as input, they learn independently to identify pattern in the data and alยญ
low distinct, multivariate input. Recently, ANNs have become widely used for data
imputation, due to their ability to learn complex nonยญlinear patterns in the data withยญ
out needing aยญpriori knowledge on the underlying physical processes [15, 16]. ANNs
that were successfully applied for imputing hydrological data are FeedยญForward Neuยญ
ral Networks (FFNNs) [25, 21], Long ShortยญTerm Memory (LSTM) models [13, 16,
17, 6], Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) [29] and Self Organizing Maps [21,
15]. Here, Self Organizing Maps appear to perform well for imputing low flow data,
whereas FFNNs give good results for high flows [21]. However, in particular LSTMs
seem promising for data imputation, as they capture both periodic and chaotic beยญ
havior in time series data [26]. Furthermore, LSTMs were shown to learn effectively
highยญfrequency fluctuations and nonยญlinearity [16]. A common drawback of these
methods is that their training and the identification of the ideal set of hyperparameยญ
ters is timeยญconsuming rendering them less applicable for realยญtime applications [6].
Apart from the method applied, data imputation can be further distinguished with regard
to the strategy that is implemented for imputing multiple consecutive time steps. Several
approaches exist but the most common are:
โข Recursive strategy: Missing data points are imputed one after another by feeding
oneยญstep ahead predictions back as an input for the next oneยญstep ahead prediction
until the end of the data gap is reached [30]. Being well suited for realยญtime applicaยญ
tions, a drawback of the method is, that it is sensitive to the accumulation of errors
as these are propagated forward [31].
โข Multiยญoutput strategy: All time steps of the data gap are computed at once based
on some input, e.g., the last available data point(s) [30]. Avoiding error propagation,
the multiยญoutput strategy is less flexible regarding the length of imputation as it is
constrained to a specific, maximal time horizon that it is trained on [31, 26].

โข Correlation with external variables: Data is imputed taking advantage of physical

imputation of water levels, despite its advantage for imputing data in a live setting.

flood forecasting models while facing the variety of possible promising imputation apยญ

in Danish rivers for which up until now no study on the best imputation principle was conยญ
ducted. Due to their promising results ANNs are tested. Furthermore, as early warning
systems operate in a live setting, a recursive imputation principle is applied, examining
thus a novel ANN architecture and revealing insights in its suitability for data imputation.

1.1
Research questions
This thesis explores the potential of machine learning models for data imputation in waยญ
ter level time series of Danish streams. Therefore, the following research questions are
investigated:
1. Can we use machine learning models to impute missing data in water level time
series of Danish rivers?
(a) Is a simple forward or a recurrent architecture of the machine learning model
better suited for data imputation?
(b) How accurate are the models imputing data depending on the length of the
data gap?
(c) Can the data imputation be improved by including multiple water level series
in the model?
2. How does the training of the machine learning model influence its data imputation?
(a) Which training horizon leads to the most accurate imputation (across multiple
gap sizes)?
(b) Is data imputation of recurrent models improving with longer warmยญup periods?

Artificial neural networks
Artificial neural networks ANN are a subset of machine learning models whose structure
was inspired by the human brain, mimicking how neurons signal one another. An ANN
learns to understand and solve problems by passing information through interconnected
cells.
Figure 2.1: Illustration of an FFNN with 2 hidden layers. Each hidden layer consists of 4
neurons. The two inputs x1, x2, neurons and the output หy are connected by weights.
ANNs are composed of nodes, also known as neurons, single computational units that
can apply mathematical operations. Each neuron is connected to other neurons by a set
of weighted connections. The neurons are organised in layers such that all neurons reยญ
ceiving the same input form one layer. Due to this, information is passed sequentially
(layerยญwise) through the network: First, the input is presented to each neuron in the input
layer. These apply a weight and a bias to the input and subsequently an activation funcยญ
tion. The output is then propagated to one or several hidden layers until the output layer
is reached providing the final output of the model. In each layer, neurons apply a weight,
bias and activation to the output of the previous layer. In doing so, a neuron in a layer is
usually connected to all neurons in the previous layer and all neurons in the subsequent
layer representing a fully connected network. This process is also known as a forward

2.2
Recurrent Neural Networks
In FFNNs inputs and outputs are independent of each other, being thus unable to take
into account any sequential structure of the data. In other words, FFNNs canโt remember
anything from the past data. By introducing a so called hidden state that saves states
or information from previous input at each time step, RNNs overcome this issue as the
hidden states allows them to learn time dependencies in the data. RNNs are commonly
used for tasks as language modeling, speech recognition and machine translation in which
they outperform FFNN [32].
In constrast to an FFNN, parameters of an RNN are not only shared across each layer,
but also across all time steps of a neural network. Therefore, optimization of RNNs and
FFNNs differ, as errors are not only backpropagated through the networkโs hidden layers
but also throughout time. During this so called backpropagation through time which was
introduced by Werbos in 1990, errors are summed at each time step [33]. However, this
process causes challenges for learning longยญterm dependencies, as propagating gradiยญ
ents over many stages often results in vanishing or exploding gradients as the gradient is
multiplied many times by itself during training [34, 35]. Thus, gradients of shortยญterm deยญ
pendencies are exponentially larger than gradients of longยญterm dependencies [36]. This
problem could be addressed by either scaling very deep feed forward networks [37] or
keeping the parameter space such that gradients donโt vanish or explode [36]. However,
Bengio et al. (1994) [35] show that when using stochastic gradient descent, entering a
region of parameter space where gradients eventually vanish or explode is necessary for
achieving a robust learning of longยญterm interactions. Furthermore, the authors demonยญ
strate that the longer the sequences, and thus the dependencies that have to be learned,
the more challenging becomes the training of the RNN with stochastic gradient descent
such that the probability of successful training rapidly approaches 0 already for series with
10 or 20 items [35].
2.2.1
Long ShortยญTerm Memory
LSTM is a type of RNN that is capable to learn to remember information over very long
time horizons. It was first introduced by Hochreiter and Schmidhuber [38] in 1997 and by
overcoming the problem of vanishing gradients in RNNs has shown superior performance
over the RNN [39]. The structure of an LSTM cell is shown in figure 2.2.
The structure of an LSTM cell is similar to the structure of an RNN as each neuron of
the RNN could be replaced by an LSTM cell, but mathematical operations and internal
structures are distinct. Both models contain a hidden state, that is updated at each time
step. In addition, the LSTM cell has a cell state that it controls by so called gates enabling
the LSTM to decide at each time step if and which information should be removed or
added to the cell state. Thus, the cell state allows the LSTM to store information for
longer periods. Equations (2.2) to (2.7) outline how the hidden and cell state are updated
and the subsequent steps are described as follows.
At each time step, the current input and the previous hidden and cell state are presented to
the LSTM cell. First, the LSTM cell decides with the so called forget gate what information
should be deleted from the cell state. Next, the cell state is updated with new information.
For this, the input gate identifies the values that will be updated and the update gate
creates a set of new candidate values, that could be added to the cell state. The output of
the two gates is then combined for updating the cell state. Finally the output of the LSTM
cell, the new hidden state, is determined by the output gate, that decides which parts of
the cell state should form the output. The updated hidden and cell state are then passed
to the next LSTM cell where the process is iterated with the input of the next time step.

2.3
Training
Identifying the optimal set of parameters, e.g., weights of an ANN for performing a given
task is called training. During training, these parameters are adjusted by optimizing the
loss between the output, i.e., prediction, of the ANN and the corresponding desired outยญ
put, i.e., the target. Thus, the neural network learns from its mistakes. Backpropagation
in combination with a stochastic gradient descent algorithm is the prevailing training apยญ
proach for ANN [42]. While backpropagation is the algorithm for calculating the gradient
of the loss function, stochastic gradient descent is an optimization algorithm that updates
the parameters of the ANN based on the gradient in the direction that minimizes the loss
function [43]. The iterative training process is described in the following [36]:
1. Initialization: Random initialization of the parameters of the ANN.
2. Forward pass: An input is presented to the ANN and passed through the network,
creating an output with the current set of weights.
3. Loss calculation: The difference between the output of the ANN and the actual
or desired output is evaluated using a loss function. The goal of the training is to
minimize this loss.
4. Backpropagation: Computation of the gradients of the loss function with respect
to the parameters. Updating of the network parameters using an optimization algoยญ
rithm, e.g., stochastic gradient descent, such that the loss is reduced.
5. Iterative process: Steps 2 to 4 are repeated refining in each iteration the paramยญ
eters of the ANN until the model converges to a predefined, satisfactory level of
performance.

Data imputation principle
The illustration in figure 4.1 shows the conceptual approach of the data imputation that is
applied in this thesis, a recursive oneยญstep strategy. When imputing several time steps,
the imputation of the first time step is generated by the model, based on the last observed
value and additional input features. Subsequently, the imputed value, that was just preยญ
dicted by the model, is used as part of the input variables for imputing the next time step
by applying the same oneยญstep ahead model. This process is iterated in the same manner
until all missing time steps are imputed. Figure 4.2 provides an additional illustration of
the imputation process in a time series.
The recursive imputation approach is applied due to the following reasons:
1. In contrast to multiยญstep ahead approaches, where models predict the next consecยญ
utive time steps of a fixed horizon at once and being thus, limited to the length of
their horizon in the imputation, the recursive approach gives a large flexibility with
regard to the length of the data gap that needs to be imputed. Consequently, only
one model has to be constructed, whereas in a multiยญstep approach, several models
for different imputation lengths have to be created [26, 48]. Therefore, the recursive
oneยญstep approach appears superior for reconstructing time series as well as for
applying it in an operational setting, where a single model is able to impute values
until sensor errors are fixed.
2. The recursive approach enables to couple the data imputation model with anomaly
detection and extend it to an automatic data cleaning algorithm. For this, the model
could at every time step compare observations against its prediction and evaluate
based on an anomaly threshold if an observation should be replaced with its imputaยญ
tion. In addition, the model would flag the point as imputation or prediction, enabling
it to account for the uncertainty of its own prediction when creating a prediction at
the next time step. In doing so, anomalies could be removed and imputed simultaยญ
neously and likewise, when retraining the model or using the cleaned time series,
information on uncertainty of a specific data point could be evaluated by models.
Even though an automatic data cleaning algorithm is the ultimate goal, this thesis
will as a first step assess the possibility of data imputation without accounting for
anomaly detection.
3. Lastly, previous work showed that time series forecasting could benefit from a reยญ
cursive oneยญstep approach [29, 49, 30]. As forecasting and data imputation share
similar challenges, it is assumed, that a recursive oneยญstep approach could also be
beneficial for data imputation of water levels.

4.5
Training setup
The training setup is similar for all models and described as follows. First, the different
training approaches are explained and second, further adjustments of the input data that
are necessary for training the ANN are described. Lastly, the technical implementation of
the training is outlined.
4.5.1
Training horizons
Due to the recursive imputation approach, the models can in theory impute data gaps of
any duration, as the oneยญstep predictions could be infinitely iterated. However, for training
the ANNs, a discrete length of imputation, hereinafter called training horizon, has to be
determined, as one needs to compare the imputation to true observations for being able
to optimize the model (cf. section 2.3). Consequently, the models are trained on imputing
gaps of a specific length and it is one of the research questions of this thesis to analyse the
impact of the training horizon on the imputation quality. Therefore a range of imputation
horizons is chosen, namely 1, 12, 24, 48 and 164 hours (cf. also table 4.1).
4.5.2
Data pipeline for model training
For training the ANN, the input time series is arranged to sequences of so called data
windows resulting in a data structure as illustrated in figure 4.6. At each time step t, a
sequence is created by selecting the w previous time steps as input and the h subsequent
time steps as target, where w corresponds to the warmยญup window of the LSTM and
h to the training horizon (see sections 4.3 and 4.5.1, respectively). W and h are thus
dependent on the model configuration and model type, i.e., for the FFNN model w=1.
Data windowing is necessary as the model requires input data with a windowed structure.
Moreover, data windowing enables faster processing as data windows can be simulated
in parallel in batches [50]. Apart from that, the chosen windowing approach creates a
high variation of starting points for the imputation i.e., the model has to learn to impute the
chosen training horizon from almost any point of time in the time series. Furthermore, the
windowing assures that the model sees all kind of trends and seasons during training.
After the data windowing, the input data is further split into a training, validation and test
set, shown in figure 3.7 in section 3.4. Following, the input data for the model is randomly
split in chunks of data, so called batches, of a predefined batchsize B in each epoch,
enabling faster processing of the data through parallelism [50]. As the data is allocated
randomly into the batches at the beginning of each epoch, composition of batches varies
among epochs. Figure 4.7 illustrates this process. In this thesis, the batchsize is set to
1000, as this showed to provide a good balance between model accuracy and duration
of training time.
During training in each epoch, the model is trained successively on batches of the training
set constantly updating the model weights. At the end of each epoch, i.e., when all batches
have been exposed to the ANN, the final set of weights is used on the validation set and
a validation loss is calculated. This process is repeated for a preset number of epochs.
After the last epoch, the final set of weights is chosen based on the lowest validation error.
Following, the test set is used to calculate performance metrics and test the generalisation

The Adam algorithm, an adaptive learning rate optimization algorithm, is applied during
training. Adam is chosen, as it is a stochastic gradient descent algorithm that was deยญ
signed specifically for training neural networks and is due to its good performance comยญ
monly applied [51]. The standard Pytorch configuration is used and MSE as loss criterion.
Moreover, exponential decay of the learning rate with a decay rate of 0.01 is applied afยญ
ter 50 epochs meaning that the learning rate is lowered by 1% every consecutive epoch.
Furthermore, early stopping is applied, meaning that the optimization is stopped if the
validation loss doesnโt improve for 20 consecutive epochs. Also, the maximal number of
epochs the model could be trained for is set to 150.
Besides that, Dropout is implemented being proven to be an effective regularization method
by avoiding overfitting of ANNs [52]. During model training, dropout randomly deactivates
nodes or outputs with a given probability. In doing so, the model is forced to find new
paths in the model and not only rely on a single path. In this thesis, a dropout probability
of 0.2 was applied.

4.7.1
Quantitative evaluation
The Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) and Persistence Index (PI) are calculated for evalยญ
uating quantitatively the performance of the different models. In theory, the trained models
can impute data gaps of infinite length due to their recursive architecture. Therefore, for
calculating comparable evaluation metrics, it is necessary to define discrete imputation
horizons, i.e., the length of a data gap. In this thesis, the imputation horizons the modยญ
els are evaluated on are set to 1, 12, 24, 48, 164 and 672 hours. 164 and 672 hours
correspond to one week and a month of missing data, respectively.
The imputation horizons are determined based on a preliminary analysis of the distribution
of the data gaps (section 5.1). For most of the stations, data gaps have a length below
20 hours thus, the imputation horizons of 1, 12 and 24 hours. Furthermore, the increasing
length of the imputation horizon enables to identify up until which gap length a model
produces robust imputations. Moreover, being symmetric to the training horizons (cf.
section 4.5.1), the chosen imputation horizons allow to evaluate the hypothesis that a
model performs best on imputing data gaps having the length of its training horizon. The
imputation horizon of 672 hours was added for assessing how the ability of the models to
generalise their imputation on longยญterm horizons is and if exceeding the training horizon
results in lower robustness of the imputations as the model wasnโt trained for making
predictions in this time range.

6
Discussion
The following section discusses the data, methods, and results applied and obtained
throughout this thesis. First, the results and insights of development of the data imputaยญ
tion model are reviewed in section 6.1. Then, the limitations of the approach are critically
examined in section 6.2 followed by a research outlook in section 6.3 describing what can
be done to improve the existing approach.
6.1
Data imputation models
In summary, this thesis developed consecutively a model architecture that can be used for
imputing missing values in water level time series of Danish streams. A recursive imputaยญ
tion strategy was applied and it was tested if this could produce reliable data imputation.
Both FFNN and LSTM type models were tested in several variations. The main findings
from analysing the results have been the following:
โข Data imputation of LSTM models is superior to that of FFNN models, which is reยญ
flected by both evaluation metrics and visual inspection of the imputation. As LSTM
have proven to capture longterm dependencies in time series, this result is not surยญ
prising and in line with findings of other studies, e.g., Cheng et al.(2020) [26].
โข Including precipitation in the LSTM models improves data imputation allowing the
model to learn delayed responses and dynamic fluctuations of water levels.
โข A recursive imputation combined with the configuration of the LSTMยญP or LSTMยญPยญT
model provides the most accurate data imputation being reliable up to one week.
โข Dry periods and variations in water levels, that are not linked to precipitation or
seasonality, are most challenging for data imputation and not imputed correctly by
any of the tested models.
โข Training horizons and warmยญup windows govern the stability of the data imputation
models. The following findings confirm those of Janbain et al.(2023) [17] that reยญ
ported similar observations when applying an LSTM model for imputing water level
records of the river Seine:
โ Thereโs an optimum of training horizon between 24 and 48 hours, longer or
shorter training horizons result in erroneous data imputation.
โ The longer the warmยญup window of the LSTM models, the better the initialization
of the internal model states and the more accurate the data imputation even
for short training horizons.
Having complete time series is crucial when the data should be used for modeling purยญ
poses, i.e., to train other models. Taking only the metrics of the LSTMยญP or LSTMยญPยญT
models into account, it could be concluded that the models could serve for this objecยญ
tive. However, the qualitative inspections of the models showed that these still have large
drawbacks in their imputation, e.g., overshooting of peaks or overยญ and underestimating
water level changes during dry periods. If the cleaned water level data should be used as
input to models for that these characteristics are vital, the data imputation would still be
too inaccurate. For instance, flood prediction models, rely on precise data on peaks and
trends in water levels for correct predictions. Apart from that, it is likely, that the output
Machine Learning Models for (semiยญ) Automatic Cleaning of Hydrological Time Series Data
51
==PAGE:64==
from the imputation model is also too inaccurate for other modeling purposes, as good
and precise training data is key for functional models.
Therefore, for being able to apply the data imputation model to provide cleaned data to
other applications, the data imputation had to be further improved. In particular, the model
needs to be able to learn longยญterm variation in water level changes that are not linked to
precipitation or seasonality but, i.e., caused by saturated soils or changes in ground water
tables. One way to achieve this, would be to include information on longยญterm trends to
the model inputs. This could be at one hand other environmental or meteorological data
as groundwater levels, or on the other hand, data aggregations i.e., a sum of the water
level of the past months giving an idea of the long term trends of the water level. Further
improvements that could improve the model performance are discussed in the research
outlook in section 6.3.
6.2
Limitations
The limitations of the current setup are discussed in the following. First, it is critically
examined in which situations the conclusions of this study might not hold. Following,
limitations of the methodology and how results were obtained are outlined.
6.2.1
Cleaned input data
A major limitation of the data imputation model is, that the model was developed based
on an almost complete water level time series that was cleaned for apparent anomalies.
Applying the model to a sensor with an incomplete or erroneous time series, will most likely
result in a decreased imputation performance, as the model canโt distinguish between true
and anomalous observations and might reproduce anomalies in its imputation.
Apart from that, the prerequisite of cleaned data substantially limits the applicability of the
data imputation model, as sensors, as outlined in chapter 1, are prone to error and time
series are often incomplete. For instance, it is visible from figure 3.2, that already most
of the water level stations are located within the study area of this thesis, canโt hold this
condition even after some basic data cleaning was applied.
6.2.2
Assumption of natural hydrograph
The data imputation model was developed using a time series of a sensor that is located
in the more natural part of the river Gudenรฅ, cf. section 3.1, having thus a rather normal
hydrograph. Therefore, it was not tested, if the model is able to impute time series with
more humanยญphysical interventions such as gates, dams, or vegetation cuts. It is likely
that imputation performance of the model decreases in this case, as these phenomena
most often donโt follow regular cyclical pattern and are challenging for the model to learn.
Consequently, it is possible that the data imputation model might already not be applicable
to the downstream stations of Gudenรฅ as these are highly influenced by humanยญphysical
interactions.
6.2.3
Applicability to other hydrological time series and areas
The architecture and structure of the imputation model could in theory be used to imยญ
pute other (hydrological) time series data. However, hydrographs of, e.g., groundwater
or streamflows, have distinct pattern that differ from those of water level data and it is
highly uncertain, if the data imputation model would be able to impute these time series.
Therefore, the conclusions drawn from this study are limited for time series having simiยญ
lar behavior as that of the station used for the model development, ns Uldumkรฆr. While
multiple stations at the stream Gudenรฅ show a comparable behavior, other rivers might
have different patterns due to distinct climatic, geographic or environmental conditions
52
Machine Learning Models for (semiยญ) Automatic Cleaning of Hydrological Time Series Data
==PAGE:65==
such that also for the same type of hydroglogical time series, i.e., water level of rivers, the
conclusions of this study might not hold and model adaptations were necessary.
6.2.4
Data cleaning
The data was corrected for obvious spikes using a zยญscore (see section 3.2.1) assuming
normal distribution of the water level data even though this condition didnโt hold true for all
water level data. Thus, it is possible that observations were removed and consequently,
the model being trained on slightly biased data. Furthermore, the data was only cleaned
for spikes and not for other anomalies such as flatlines or level shifts. Even though it
wasnโt apparent from the results, it could be, that the model learned to reproduce errors
which is a limitation of the model due to its data preprocessing.
6.2.5
Model alteration strategy
The procedure applied for the successive updates of the input parameters to the LSTMยญ
model for identifying if imputation performance could be improved, gives rise to potential
shortcomings. Input parameters are changed based on analysis of deficiencies in the
imputation, however, due to the stepยญwise procedure not all possible combinations were
tested and furthermore, it might be that other sets of input parameters, could result in
even better data imputation.
Furthermore, the necessary adjustments to the input data, for assessing if including time
series of multiple water level stations into the model input improves data imputation, subยญ
stantially limits the comparability of the results of the LSTMยญAW model. It is uncertain, if
increased or decreased imputation performance could is caused by the differing training
and testing setup or by the additional input feature.
6.2.6
Training
With the current setup, the model receives during training apart from the starting point
only its own predicted values as input. However, if used in a life setting, the model would
need to process both observations, when they are available, and predictions, whenever
observations are missing. As the model is only trained based on its own imputations, it
might not work well with observations which can be noisy. One way of solving this could
be the implementation of another training method, i.e., professor forcing or scheduled
sampling where the model sees both observations and predictions during training. This
alteration is further discussed in section 6.3.3.
6.3
Research outlook
In this section, possibilities for further research are outlined. First, additions to the current
setup are outlined and following, possible new directions of the research are discussed.
6.3.1
Possible model architecture improvements
The model hyper parameters, e.g., learning rate, dropout rate, depth and width of the
neural networks. are currently not optimized. Thus, performance of the models would
likely increase, if these parameters were calibrated. A common method for optimization
are grid search or bayesian optimization [54]. For instance, Janbain et al.(2023) [17]
determined the optimal set of parameters of the LSTM they used for data imputation of
water levels with grid search.
6.3.2
Alternative network types
In this thesis, FFNN and LSTM network types are tested. However, other network types
might increase the performance of the data imputation. One such network type is a CNN.
Currently, CNNs are mostly used for image recognition and classification [55]. However,
Machine Learning Models for (semiยญ) Automatic Cleaning of Hydrological Time Series Data
53
==PAGE:66==
CNNs can also be applied on sequential data, using 1D convolutions as for instance
applied by Wunsch et al.(2021) [29] for groundwater level forecasting.
Another network type that could potentially improve data imputation is the Transformer
model [56] being applied in many stateยญofยญthe art deep learning models [57, 58, 59]. Transยญ
formers are based on an attention mechanism, that weighs each part of an input sequence
with respect to every other part of the input sequence allowing it to focus on specific parts
of the input when generating an output [56]. This mechanism enables Transformers to
capture dependencies between different parts of the input data and consider longยญrange
dependencies more efficiently [59].
An advantage of both Transformers and CNNs is that they not only scale well too large
data sets, but also do not need to be trained in sequences as LSTMs. Thus, the networks
can be trained with high efficiency on GPUs or TPUs.[36, 56]
6.3.3
Training strategies
Professorยญforcing and scheduled sampling
Currently, the model is trained only on its own predictions. If during training the first preยญ
diction is incorrect, errors are propagated and the model might not be reaching further
optima. Thus, longยญterm imputation could become less robust. Furthermore, in a life setยญ
ting, the model would need to run not only on its own predictions but also on observations
whenever they are available. However, being only trained on its own predicted input, the
imputation might not work well with observations which can be noisy. These issues could
be addressed by adjusting the model training as described in the following.
Professorยญforcing [60] and scheduled sampling [61] are training methods, where not only
predictions, but also true target values, i.e., observations, are fed back into the recursive
model during training. In scheduled sampling training, the model gradually transitions
from only feeding back true observations at the beginning of the training to only using the
modelโs own predictions [61]. Professor forcing on the other hand applies a stochastic
approach, where randomly true observations or own predictions are fed back into the
model [60]. The idea of this training techniques is, that model predictions become more
accurate if the model bases its predictions on true values while also taking into account
the uncertainty of its own previous predictions.
Flagging of predictions
Currently, imputing the first time step correctly, i.e., imputation horizon of one hour, is
challenging for the model and using simply the last available observation instead of the
model prediction is on average more accurate. One way to address this drawback could
be the implementation of professor forcing or scheduled sampling as introduced previยญ
ously. In addition to that, the model could be provided with flags indicating if the input is
original data or its own prediction, e.g., by labeling observations with zeros and model reยญ
sults with ones. This could in the case of the LSTM improve the initialization of the states,
as the model could learn to give a larger weight to the initial states, as they are based on
true observations. As a consequence, this could force the model to get its first imputation
more accurate and in a consequence reduce the propagation of errors.
6.3.4
Manyยญtoยญmany network
With the current data imputation model, it is necessary to build a model for each sensor in
the river network. This makes it impractical for an application in an operational setting, as
it requires time to configure each network individually. Extending the imputation model to
a so called manyยญtoยญmany network that imputes data simultaneously for multiple sensors
could address this issue.

A manyยญtoยญmany network describes a network with multiple sequences in input and output.
Instead of having a model for each sensor in the network, one could impute the data of all
sensors simultaneously by using the data of all sensors in the input for imputing the data of
each sensor in the output. This approach takes advantage of the correlation between the
water level stations and that multiple stations have similar behavior. Apart from increasing
practical applicability it might thus also increase model performance.
However, it would need to be tested if such a network is feasible and how its configuration
should look like. A model for the whole river, i.e., using all available water stations as
input and output, might become noisy, as the model is challenged by identifying patterns
in a large amount of input data. Furthermore, at Gudenรฅ, hydrographs are quite distinct
due to the differences in humanยญphysical influence along the river (cf. section 3.1), and a
manyยญtoยญmany model might not be able to reproduce these distinct pattern. However, this
could be solved by creating two manyยญtoยญmany models, one for each river stretch which
could be subject to further study.

Conclusion
The aim of this study was to explore the potential of machine learning models for data imยญ
putation in water level time series of Danish streams. Therefore, different models applying
a recursive data imputation strategy were tested consecutively, for identifying which arยญ
chitecture is best suited for data imputation. Furthermore, it was assessed what influence
the training setup has on the quality of the data imputation.
When testing the different model architectures for their data imputation performance, the
following conclusions with respect to the research questions were drawn:
When testing if we can use machine learning models to impute missing data in water level
time series of Danish rivers, the following conclusions were drawn:
โข A recurrent architecture of the machine learning model is better suited for data imยญ
putation than a simple feed forward one. LSTM models have superior imputation
performance when compared to FFNN models, demonstrated by lower RMSEs and
better PIs. The data imputation of an FFNN model is seldom better than replacing
missing data with the last available observation.
โข LSTM models with only precipitation (LSTMยญP) or precipitation and time encoding
(LSTMยญPยญT) as model input appear to be the best model configuration for data imยญ
putation, as reflected in their RMSEs and PIs being the best of all tested models.
โข The imputation performance of all models decreases with an increasing length of
the data gap. However, the LSTMยญP and LSTMยญPยญT models were found to provide
reliable data imputation for data gaps up to one week deviating on average not more
than 0.09 m from the observations.
โข Data imputation can be improved by including precipitation to the model inputs. Inยญ
cluding multiple water level time series or an indication of the season does not reยญ
sult in substantial improvements of the data imputation, but further validation with
respect to the incorporation of water level time series is necessary.
โข Data imputation is most accurate during periods with precipitation, where models
capture fluctuations in water level correctly.
โข All models fail to impute long term trends and changes in water levels that are not
directly linked to precipitation or seasonality. Another prevalent deficiency of the
data imputation models are highly dynamic fluctuations.
Generally, it was found that data imputation of the machine learning model is influenced
by the training setยญup, i.e., the training horizon and warmยญup window. The main findings
are presented below:
โข A training horizon between 24 and 48 hours leads to the most accurate data impuยญ
tation across multiple gap sizes, data imputation becomes erroneous if the training
horizon is too short or too long.
โข Longer warmยญup windows were found to improve data imputation for recurrent modยญ
els, however changes are the largest for those with very small and very long training
horizons. From this, it is deduced that it is important to choose a warmยญup window
such, that the recurrent model has enough time steps to initialize its internal states.

โข Warmยญup window and training horizon complement each other. The warmยญup winยญ
dow was found to influence the training process, even though itโs not included in the
loss calculation, as the model learns to understand more context which results in
stable predictions even for short training horizons. Conversely, if the right training
horizon is chosen, i.e., 24 and 48 hours for the models that were tested in this thesis,
model predictions are stable also for short warmยญup windows.
Overall, it was found that machine learning models, in particular LSTM models with precipยญ
itation as input can be used for imputing water level time series of Danish streams. Howยญ
ever, even though models showed very high performance when analysing their scores,
visual inspection of the model performance revealed that the models still have large drawยญ
backs in their imputation. Therefore, if the data imputation model should be used to clean
time series for the use in other models, i.e., a flood prediction model, imputation quality
had to be improved and it would be beneficial to couple the imputation with anomaly deยญ
tection. Hence, this research can be seen as a first step towards an integrated framework
for data cleaning.
