Abstract 
Corynebacterium glutamicum has been established as a promising host organism for heterologous 
protein production in the biotechnology industry, due to several factors such as its ability to secrete 
properly folded and functional proteins. However, further research is required before it can be applied 
for heterologous protein production at industry scale. In a previous study, a novel inverse IPTG-
inducible transcriptional switch was developed to control the production of cutinase. In this study, the 
switch and a modified version of the switch containing an enhanced PbetI promoter was characterized 
at different cultivation scales. The results showed that the switch was able to successfully regulate the 
production of cutinase. However, although the enhanced PbetI promoter was shown to cause 
increased expression levels, the elevated expression was unable to be sustained over time, indicating 
a potential issue of plasmid loss. Furthermore, the expression of the switch variant was revealed to be 
less stringently regulated under IPTG induction, indicating that the BetI repressor might have a 
reduced binding affinity toward the mutated PbetI promoter. To improve the stability of the system and 
reduce the heterogeneity of the population, the transcriptional switch was integrated into the genome, 
and verified by fluorescence measurements.

1. Introduction 
1.1 Background 
The biotechnology industry has become an important sector over the past decades and has now 
reached a market size of over 400 billion dollars, employing upwards of 1 million people worldwide 
(IBISWorld, n.d.). This is in large due to its important contributions of healthcare, food, and 
environmental products. One of the crucial aspects within biotechnological production is the choice of 
host cell. Escherichia coli is currently the main prokaryotic host cell used for heterologous protein 
production, but it lacks certain features, such as an effective secretion system and protein folding 
mechanisms. Consequently, many proteins are expressed in an insoluble form, which renders them 
difficult if not impossible to recover without the use of harsh denaturants and costly protein-refolding 
procedures. This is especially true for pharmaceutical products, where more than 90% of products are 
terminated in the early stages of clinical development due to their low solubility (Liu et al., 2016). 
In short, there is a need for alternative prokaryotic expression systems with effective secretion 
systems and protein folding mechanisms (Liu et al., 2016). One such alternative is C. glutamicum, 
which is a fast-growing Gram-positive bacterium (Liu et al., 2016; Nishimura et al., 2007; Ray et al., 
2022). C. glutamicum was originally discovered in Japan in 1957 where it was identified as a natural 
producer of L-glutamate (Kinoshita et al., 1957). Since its discovery it has been used for industrial-
scale production of amino acids, such as L-glutamate and L-lysine, producing over 10 million tons of 
amino acids per year, making it a crucial organism in the biotechnology industry (Sanchez et al., 
2018). C. glutamicum has a high G/C content making it a genetically stable organism (Liu et al., 2016; 
Nishimura et al., 2007; Ray et al., 2022), and due to its non-pathogenic nature and lack of endotoxin 
production, it is generally regarded as safe (GRAS) (Lee et al., 2016; Liu et al., 2016; Ray et al., 
2022). Furthermore, it has effective secretion systems and has minimal extracellular protease activity.  
As a result of these characteristics, it is a promising future host organism for heterologous protein 
production (Liu et al., 2016). 
 
1.2 C. glutamicum as host for heterologous protein production 
However, there are several challenges that C. glutamicum needs to overcome before it can reach its 
potential as host for industry-scale heterologous protein production. When developing cell factories, 
the availability of genetic tools and components is essential. The existence of such tools is one of the 
reasons that E. coli has remained the host organism of choice for heterologous protein production in 
the industry for decades. Unfortunately, transferring genetic tools and components from other host 
organisms such as E. coli directly to C. glutamicum has proven to be difficult. One example is 
promoters, as promoters that exhibit high activity in E. coli such as the lac promoter, has been shown 
to exhibit low activity in C. glutamicum. Therefore, there has been a need for optimization of existing

genetic tools and components, and development of new ones specifically for C. glutamicum. 
Fortunately, a lot of development has been done in this area in recent years, which has enabled 
research and development of new efficient expression systems for C. glutamicum (Liu et al., 2016). 
When designing expression systems, the choice of promoters is important. Many expression systems 
used for production of heterologous proteins are based on strong constitutive promoters, as this is a 
simple way of achieving high levels of expression and yields. However, continuous overexpression of 
protein can often lead to reduced cell growth due to the increased metabolic load on the cell and can 
therefore also negatively impact the yield. For this reason, these types of expression systems are not 
always as cost efficient as desired. Due to this issue, inducible expression systems are often desirable 
for industry-scale production systems (Donovan et al., 1996). 
Many commonly used expression systems are based on the Tac promoter which is inducible by the 
addition of isopropyl β-D-1-thiogalactopyranoside (IPTG). The Tac promoter is negatively regulated by 
binding of the LacI repressor, but as IPTG is added to the cell it binds to LacI, disrupting the LacI-
mediated repression of the Tac promoter thereby enabling transcription of target genes (Donovan et 
al., 1996). However, the use of inducer molecules such as IPTG can be expensive when applied at 
industry-scale production, with a price of $90 per gram (Sigma-Aldrich, 2023a). The addition of large 
amounts of IPTG can also cause an increased metabolic burden to the cell, harming the cellular 
growth rate, resulting in reduced yields (Donovan et al., 1996). In the case of C. glutamicum it is also 
important to note that it has been shown to have poor permeability to IPTG, which can cause further 
issues (Liu et al., 2016). Therefore, it is desirable to find ways to reduce IPTG usage while retaining 
the ability to regulate protein expression, to enable cost reductions and improved yields. 
The efficiency of the cell chassis is another important aspect that must be optimized, for C. 
glutamicum to reach its potential as host for heterologous protein production, as an inefficient cell 
chassis will hinder its use for any potential applications. One way of increasing the efficiency of the cell 
chassis is by deletion of non-essential genes from the genome, which can increase the robustness 
and productivity of the cell (Liu et al., 2016). A lot of work has been done in this area recently, by 
construction of genome-reduced strains based on the C. glutamicum ATCC 13032 wild type strain, 
such as CR099 and derivatives thereof (Baumgart M et al., 2018; Linder et al., 2021). 
 
1.3 The secretion system of C. glutamicum 
As previously mentioned, one advantage of C. glutamicum is its efficient secretion system. An 
effective secretion system is important, as the lack thereof could cause accumulation of proteins in the 
cytoplasm and physiological stress on the cell that could inhibit its growth. Secretion of the target 
protein can also make downstream processing significantly easier, as the product can be purified 
directly from the supernatant (Watanabe et al., 2013).

The secretion system of C. glutamicum is comprised of two distinct pathways known as Sec and Tat. 
The Sec secretion pathway is the main secretion pathway and mediates translocation of proteins from 
the cytosol to the supernatant in an unfolded conformation. The pathway consists of two different 
mechanisms, namely the co-translational and post-translational secretion mechanisms. 
The co-translational secretion mechanism of the Sec pathway begins during translation initiation when 
the signal recognition particle (SRP) binds to the signal peptide of the cargo protein while it is still 
inside the ribosome. After the binding event, the SRP, the ribosome, and the protein being translated, 
are directed to the Sec translocase with the aid of FtsY, a membrane-associated protein that acts as 
the SRP receptor (Figure 1). The following translocation process across the cell membrane is identical 
to the post-translational translocation process as described below (Liu et al., 2016, 2017). 
 
Figure 1: The mechanism of the Sec secretion pathway (Liu et al., 2017) 
 
The post translational mechanism of the Sec pathway begins with the chaperone SecB, which binds to 
the mature part of the precursor protein, keeping the protein in an unfolded state. The unfolded 
precursor protein is transported by SecB to SecA located in the membrane. Here SacB interacts with 
the positively charged and zinc-containing C-terminal, transferring the precursor protein to SecA. After 
the transfer, SecB is released from the precursor protein. With the help of SecA, the precursor protein 
is inserted into the protein-conducting channel (PCC) which is composed of the SecYEG complex. 
The signal peptide is inserted first, followed by the N-terminal domain of the protein. SecA then drives

the translocation of the precursor protein through a series of ATP-binding and hydrolysis events, which 
cause conformational changes and propel the movement of the protein through the PCC (Figure 1) 
(Liu et al., 2016, 2017). 
The Tat secretion pathway is an alternative pathway that mediates translocation of proteins from the 
cytosol to the supernatant in a folded conformation.  
The function of the Tat secretion pathway in C. glutamicum primarily depends on the two membrane-
integrated subunits, TatA and TatC, but the presence of other TatA-like proteins such as TatB and 
TatE can improve the secretion efficiency (Figure 2) (Liu et al., 2016, 2017). 
 
 
Figure 2: Components of the Tat secretion system (Goosens et al., 2014) 
 
Secretory proteins that depend on translocation via the Tat-pathway, must be properly folded prior to 
the translocation and contain an N-terminal signal peptide with a twin-arginine (-RR-) motif. The 
proteins TatC and TatB located in the membrane, forms a docking complex. As the signal peptide on 
the cargo protein interacts with the binding site on the docking complex, TatA-like proteins are 
recruited to the docking complex to form a transmembrane pore and the cargo protein is inserted into 
the pore by TatC. While the cargo protein is being translocated, it is still bound to the docking 
complex, but when the protein has moved across the membrane, the signal peptide is cleaved off by a 
type I signal peptidase, releasing the mature protein from the membrane (Figure 3) (Goosens et al., 
2014; Liu et al., 2017).

As previous studies have reported 3-10-fold increases in secretion efficiency by overexpression of 
components from the secretion pathways described above (Liu et al., 2016), optimization of the 
secretion pathways is another area where further research would be highly beneficial in the effort of 
optimizing C. glutamicum for the use as a host organism for heterologous protein production. 
 
1.4 Cutinase and its applications 
One of the proteins for which C. glutamicum would be a suitable production host, is cutinase. 
Cutinases is a group of serin esterases that belong to the α/β hydrolases family (Figure 4). They have 
hydrolytic activity against a range of substrates such as esters, polyesters, triacylglycerols and can 
catalyze esterification and trans-esterification reactions. These characteristics makes cutinases a very 
attractive group of enzymes for a range of industrial applications in various sectors (Martínez & 
Maicas, 2021; Pio & Macedo, 2009). 
 
Figure 4: Crystal structure of cutinase from Fusarium solani (Longhi et al., n.d.) 
 
One of the industries in which cutinase can be applied, is the textile industry. The surface of cotton 
and other fabrics is covered in a hydrophobic wax-like layer of cuticle scales that reduces wettability of 
the fabrics and inhibits staining of the textile. Today this layer is removed by alkaline hydrolysis using 
sodium hydroxide and other chemicals at high temperatures of upwards of 100°C. This process is very 
energy consuming and generates a lot of chemical waste that is expensive to get rid of. The 
application of cutinase is currently being studied as an alternative treatment method to avoid the use 
of harsh chemicals and high temperatures, as this would be highly beneficial in terms of both the 
economic and environmental impact of the process (Chen et al., 2013; Martínez & Maicas, 2021). 
==PAGE:14==
 
 
14

Another industry in which cutinase shows promise is in the detergent industry. As cutinase is highly 
efficient at hydrolyzing fatty acids and exhibits activity at water-oil interfaces within the temperature 
and pH range typically used for washings, it could be suitable for use in detergents to remove grease 
spots (Martínez & Maicas, 2021). Furthermore, it has an advantage over the lipases that are currently 
used in detergent, in that they are not calcium-dependent, which could enable reduced use of calcium 
and calcium chelators in detergent formulations (Dutta et al., 2009; Flipsen et al., 1998). 
Furthermore, cutinase could see use in the food industry for the production of esters such as ethyl 
butyrate, which is used for flavor enhancement of food products. Such esters can be synthetically 
produced by chemical reactions between alcohols and carboxylic acids, but the use of strong acids 
and bases, as well as high temperatures in these reactions restricts compounds produced in this way 
from being classified as “natural” products, which detracts from their value (de Barros et al., 2012; Su 
et al., 2016). The use of biocatalysts such as cutinase is therefore an attractive alternative as a way of 
enabling the production of these compounds under more moderate reaction conditions (Martínez & 
Maicas, 2021). 
The use of biocatalysts such as cutinase could ultimately enable production processes in the industry 
to be performed at lower temperatures and without the need for harsh chemicals, thereby reducing the 
environmental impact while enabling cost reductions at the same time. 
 
1.5 Design of transcriptional OFF-switch 
As previously mentioned, can the use of inducer molecules such as IPTG be very expensive when 
applied at industry-scale production. Therefore, it would be beneficial to find ways to reduce IPTG 
usage for production of heterologous proteins while retaining the ability to regulate the protein 
expression. To achieve this, in a previous study (Shen, J., unpublished) a transcriptional OFF-switch 
was designed, constructed, and inserted into the expression vector pEKEx2, for the control of gene 
expression in C. glutamicum by inverse IPTG induction (Figure 5). In the study cutinase was used as 
the target gene, while GFPuv and mCherry served as reporter genes for the ON- and OFF-states, 
respectively. In an attempt at improving the expression level of cutinase and GFPuv, mutagenesis was 
performed of the PbetI promoter, which resulted in the mutated PbetI(H12) promoter which was shown 
to exhibit elevated GFPuv and cutinase expression levels. 
==PAGE:15==
 
 
Optimization of genetic switches for dynamic metabolic engineering in Corynebacterium glutamicum 
15 
 
 
Transcription of cutinase is regulated by the PbetI promoter, which is negatively regulated by the 
betaine inhibitor, BetI. The transcription of BetI is controlled by the Tac promoter, which is repressed in 
the presence of LacI. Addition of IPTG results in the binding of IPTG to LacI, thereby removing free 
LacI from the cytoplasm, disrupting the LacI-mediated repression of the Tac promoter. This enables 
the transcription of BetI and thereby the repression of the PbetI promoter. Thus, in the absence of 
IPTG, cutinase should be expressed. As the Epr signal peptide has been added to the N-terminal end 
of cutinase, it should be secreted to the supernatant via the Sec secretion pathway. However, with the 
addition of IPTG to the media, the transcriptional switch should be triggered to shift from the ON-state 
to the OFF-state and further transcription of cutinase should be prevented. 
The application of this transcriptional OFF-switch for regulation of expression should in theory allow for 
reduced IPTG usage, as IPTG induction would only be required during the pre-culture stage. This 
should facilitate reduced IPTG costs, increased growth rates in the main-culture stage and improved 
yields, thus improving the overall cost efficiency of the system.

1.6 Aim of the study 
In this study two C. glutamicum strains carrying the described transcriptional OFF-switch with the 
regular and a mutated PbetI promoter, will be characterized at various cultivation scales. Furthermore, 
genomic integration of the OFF-switch will be performed in an attempt at stabilizing the performance of 
the OFF-switch.

2.7 Prepared media 
CGXII minimal media, 2xTY complex media, BHI complex media and BHIS complex media was used 
for cultivation of C. glutamicum, and LB complex media was used for cultivation of E. coli. 
CGXII minimal media was prepared by mixing MOPS (21 g/L), Ammonium sulfate (10 g/L), Urea (5 
g/L), KH2PO4 (1 g/L) and K2HPO4 (1 g/L). The minimal media was supplemented with 1 mL/L of stock 
solutions of Biotin (200 mg/L), CaCl2 · H2O (10 g/L), MgSO4 · 7H2O (250 g/L) and a trace element 
solution consisting of Fe(II)SO4 · 7H2O (10 g/L), CuSO4 · 5H2O (0.3 g/L), MnSO4 · H2O (10 g/L), NiCl2 
· 6H2O (0.02 g/L) and ZnSO4 · 7H2O (1 g/L). For shaking flask cultures grown in CGXII minimal media 
1% glucose was added as carbon source, while 4% glucose was added for bioreactor cultures.

3.11 Cloning of OFF-switch constructs 
To address concerns regarding possible loss of plasmid, several cloning strategies was designed to 
integrate the switch-construct into the genome. The first approach involved integrating the complete 
switch into the genome (Figure 7a), while the second approach would also incorporate a His-tag to the 
C-terminal domain of BetI to allow for future investigations into the regulation kinetics of BetI (Figure 
7b). The third approach involved only incorporating the regulatory component of the switch into the 
genome, with the cutinase and GFPuv genes maintained on the pEKEx2 expression vector (Figure 
7c). The aim of this strategy was to maintain an enhanced copy number of the gene of interest while 
maintaining easy access to the PbetI promoter and the gene of interest for genetic modifications in 
future experiments without requiring genomic integration.

integration vector. Even though all primers were designed and ordered, due to late delivery of some of

3.17 Genomic integration of OFF-switch constructs 
The non-replicative integration vector pK18mobsacB-int1 was used for incorporation of the OFF-switch 
construct into the genome of C. glutamicum CR099.  
==PAGE:33==

The strategy for integration of the transcriptional OFF-switch into the genome of C. glutamicum CR099 
was based on the occurrence of two homologous recombination events in cells transformed with the 
non-replicative integration vector pK18mobsacB-int1 harboring the OFF-switch construct, as shown in 
Figure 10 (Nakashima & Miyazaki, 2014). The integration vector was designed with the OFF-switch 
construct flanked by two regions homologous to the genome of C. glutamicum CR099 as well as 
containing two selection markers. The first selection marker provides resistance to the antibiotic 
kanamycin. As the integration vector is based on the pMB1 replicon, replication of the plasmid is 
restricted to E. coli and related species and is non-replicative in C. glutamicum (Schäfer et al., 1994). 
Cells should therefore only be able to grow in kanamycin containing media if they successfully 
integrate the vector and the resistance gene carried by it, into the genome. The second selection 
marker is sacB which is a gene that encodes for the enzyme levansucrase. This is a transfructosylase 
that catalyzes sucrose hydrolysis and levan synthesis. As the presence of levansucrase has been 
shown to be lethal to C. glutamicum (Jäger et al., 1992), the presence of the sacB gene can be 
selected for by growing cells in media containing sucrose. As cells that have undergone the first 
recombination event now contains the sacB gene in the genome, only those cells that have undergone 
a second recombination event removing the sacB gene again, should be capable of growing in 
medium containing sucrose. 
The genomic integration of the OFF-switch construct was carried out by streaking transformants onto 
BHI plates containing 25 ug/mL kanamycin and incubating them at 30°C for 48 hours to select for cells 
that underwent the first recombination event. To select for the second recombination event, colonies 
were then picked and streaked onto BHI plates containing 10% sucrose which were incubated at 30°C 
for 48 hours.

4.1 Characterization of OFF-switch in shake flask cultivation 
For characterization of the transcriptional OFF-switch, two different variants of the OFF-switch 
constructs were used, the first using the wild type PbetI promoter, and the second being a variant 
containing the mutated PbetI(H12) promoter that was previously shown to induce stronger expression 
in C. glutamicum (Shen, J., unpublished). 
 
4.1.1 Characterization of growth rate 
An experiment was set up using strains of C. glutamicum CR099 harboring the two variants of the 
plasmid construct as well as a negative control harboring the pEKEx2 plasmid without the OFF-switch 
construct. The three strains were first pre-cultivated overnight (~16h) in 5 mL 2xTY complex medium 
at 30°C and 180 rpm, and then transferred to 50 mL CGXII minimal media containing 1% glucose and 
incubated overnight at 30°C and 150 rpm. These cultures were then inoculated the main culture, 
consisting of CGXII minimal media containing 1% glucose, to an OD of 0.8 and incubated at 30°C and 
150 rpm. By the addition or absence of 0.2 mM IPTG to the CGXII pre-culture and main-culture, four 
different cultivation conditions were created for the strains (IPTG (-/-), (-/+), (+/-), (+/+)) to characterize 
the OFF-switch. The OD of the cultures was measured every hour and plotted on a semi logarithmic 
graph. 
As seen in Figure 11 the wildtype culture reached peak OD after 10 hours with an OD value of 25.85. 
The strain harboring the OFF-switch construct with the wild type PbetI promoter reached peak OD 
after 12 hours of growth in all cultivation conditions. The highest OD was measured in the non-induced 
culture (IPTG (-/-)) with an OD value of 22.45, while the lowest OD was measured in the fully IPTG-
induced culture (IPTG +/+) with an OD value of 18.45. The peak OD measured in the IPTG (-/+) and 
IPTG (+/-) cultures were 21.00 and 20.25 respectively. 
The peak OD values of the strain harboring the mutated PbetI(H12) promoter was also observed after 
12 hours of growth, but it generally reached lower OD values. The highest OD value was measured in 
the IPTG (+/-) culture with a value of 21.70, while the lowest OD was measured in the IPTG (-/+) 
culture with a value of only 15.95. However, the OD of this culture was increased to 17.35 after 24 
hours, indicating it had not yet peaked at the time of the measurement. The OD measured in the fully 
induced culture (IPTG (+/+)) was very similar to that of the non-mutant strain, with a value of 18.85. 
Interestingly, the non-induced culture (IPTG (-/-)) reached a peak OD value of only 18.30, which was 
significantly lower than the non-mutant strain reached under the same conditions, which could be an 
indication that the mutated PbetI(H12) promoter impose a burden on cell growth.

4.3 Cloning of the OFF-switch construct 
Initially, an attempt at isolating the pEKEx2_BetI-mCherry_PbetI(H12)-Epr-Cut-GFPuv plasmid was 
performed on a C. glutamicum strain harboring the plasmid using a Plasmid Miniprep kit, however,

layer present in gram-positive bacteria, lysozyme treatment was considered as a possible solution for 
the plasmid purification, but it was decided to use an existing E. coli strain harboring the same plasmid 
instead. The plasmid purification was successful, and the plasmid was run on a 1% agarose gel where 
it was found to have the correct size of ~10.8 kb (Figure 35 well 3). 
Initial attempts at PCR amplification of the insert fragment from the purified plasmid were 
unsuccessful, but the PCR protocol was optimized by increasing the extension time to ~60s/kb and by 
performing a 2-step PCR and a touch-down PCR. The PCR fragments were run on a 1% agarose gel 
and both PCR products were found to have the expected size of ~5.6 kb, but also showed off-target 
amplicons with a size of ~2.2 kb. The touch-down PCR showed higher amplification of both the PCR 
fragment and off-target amplicon (Figure 35 well 5) than the 2-step PCR (Figure 35 well 4).

5. Discussion 
In a previous study a transcriptional OFF-switch based on inverse IPTG induction was constructed 
(Shen, J., unpublished). This design allowed for induction of protein expression by dilution of IPTG. 
The intent of this design was to restrict the use of IPTG to the pre-culture stage, as a way of providing 
effective cost optimization to the production of heterologous protein production in C. glutamicum.  
The transcriptional OFF-switch was further improved by the addition of a mutation to the PbetI 
promoter, which resulted in the PbetI(H12) promoter mutant that had enhanced promoter activity and 
increased transcription of the target gene. The aim of this study was to continue this work by 
characterizing the performance of the PbetI(H12)-promoter mutant strain at different cultivation scales 
and to further optimize the performance of the strain. 
Characterization of the OFF-switch was done by cultivating the wild type OFF-switch strain C. 
glutamicum (pEKEx2_BetI-mCherry_PbetI-Epr-Cut-GFPuv) and the PbetI(H12) promoter mutant strain 
C. glutamicum (pEKEx2_BetI-mCherry_PbetI(H12)-Epr-Cut-GFPuv). The cultivation was carried out in 
50 mL shake flasks, under different pre-culture and main-culture conditions by the addition or absence 
of 0.2 mM IPTG to the media. 
From these experiments, the growth rate of the promoter mutant strain was observed to be slightly 
reduced as compared to the wild-type strain (Figure 12). To characterize the OFF-switch dynamics, 
spectrometry analysis of the GFPuv fluorescence (ON-signal) and mCherry fluorescence (OFF-signal) 
was performed. As expected, the analysis of the fluorescence intensity, showed that the promoter 
mutant strain was able to reach a significantly higher average level of GFPuv fluorescence intensity 
than the wild-type strain under non-induced conditions, as well as a higher GFPuv fluorescence 
intensity per OD (Figure 13 and Figure 15). However, while the wild-type strain was able to retain a 
high level of GFPuv fluorescence per OD for the entire 24 hours of cultivation, the GFPuv 
fluorescence intensity per OD of the mutant strain peaked after only 5 hours after which it declined. 
These results could indicate loss of plasmid, perhaps due to the metabolic burden from the increased 
expression obtained from carrying the enhanced PbetI promoter. To further our understanding of the 
OFF-switch dynamics, flow cytometry analysis was performed as well. The results revealed a larger 
decrease in the GFPuv fluorescent proportion of the cell population over time in non-IPTG-induced 
cultures of the promoter mutant strain than compared to the wild type strain, thus confirming the earlier 
findings (Figure 19 and Figure 23). In cultivations for which the pre-culture had been IPTG-induced the 
expectation was to observe a low amount of GFPuv fluorescent cells at the start of the main-culture 
and this was indeed the case for the wild-type strain. Interestingly the promoter mutant strain, showed 
significantly higher amounts, or even peak levels, of GFPuv fluorescent cells at the beginning of those 
cultivations. The results also showed many cells exhibiting GFPuv and mCherry fluorescence 
simultaneously (Figure 26). These results indicate a less stringent regulation by BetI on the PbetI(H12)

promoter than the wild type promoter. This could indicate that the BetI repressor has a reduced 
binding affinity towards the PbetI(H12) promoter mutant. 
As we were interested in characterizing the performance of the promoter mutant strain in large scale 
cultivations, a 500 mL bioreactor cultivation was performed as well. For this cultivation, the pre-culture 
was induced with 0.2 mM IPTG while the main-culture was carried out in the absence of IPTG. The 
cultivation was performed as a fed-batch fermentation with a single feeding after 24 hours of growth. 
This method of cultivation was chosen as it had been used in previous studies to achieve high cell 
densities and high yields (Sun et al., 2020). 
The growth rate from the bioreactor cultures was observed to be slightly increased as compared to the 
shake flask cultures cultivated under the same conditions (IPTG +/-), with a growth rate of ~0.30 h-1 in 
the bioreactor versus 0.25 h-1 in the shake flask. As previously, the OFF-switch dynamics were 
characterized by spectrometry analysis of the GFPuv and mCherry fluorescence intensities during the 
cultivation.  
The results showed a high variance in the initial GFPuv fluorescence per OD between the two 
biological replicates. After the initial 12 hours of the cultivation, the GFPuv fluorescence per OD of the 
two cultures became more comparable. After 24 hours the fluorescence per OD had increased 
significantly, but quickly dropped back to the previous level after the addition of antifoam and glucose. 
At this point significant foam-development had occurred and antifoam was added to both cultures, 
which likely explains the drop in fluorescence per OD, as biomass that had been stuck in the foam 
overnight were resuspended into the culture from where samples were taken.  
Results from flow cytometry analysis supported the spectrometry results, showing over 50% GFPuv 
fluorescent cells at the beginning of the cultivation, which was reduced to 10-20% later during the 
cultivation. Interestingly the GFPuv fluorescent part of the cell population was able to remain at this 
level throughout the entire 48 hours of the cultivation, contrary to the shake flask cultivation where the 
GFPuv fluorescent cell population saw a continual decrease with only 1.2% of GFPuv fluorescent cells 
remaining after 24 hours. As the shake flask cultivation was performed with only 1% glucose as 
carbon source, whereas the bioreactor used 4% glucose, glucose starvation might have been 
contributing cause for the lack of long-term GFPuv-expression in the shake flask cultures. 
When comparing the fluorescence intensities measured from the shake flask and bioreactor cultures, 
the values are significantly lower in samples taken from the bioreactor cultures. This seems to 
contradict the results obtained from the flow cytometry analysis, as a higher proportion of fluorescent 
cells were observed in bioreactor than in the shake flask (Figure 23 and Figure 32) as well as cells 
with a higher fluorescence intensity (Figure 22 and Figure 31). In this regard, it is worth noting that the 
RFU values from the fluorescence measurements are difficult to compare directly, as the fluorescence 
gain used to measure the fluorescence intensity was set automatically by the plate reader and they 
were set independently for each experiment.

To measure cutinase activity, an enzyme activity assay was performed on the supernatant obtained 
from each of the cultivations. As was expected, the activity from supernatant obtained from the shake 
flask cultivations showed elevated cutinase activity from cultivations not induced with IPTG. 
Furthermore, supernatant obtained from the shake flask cultivation of the promoter mutant strain 
demonstrated a significantly elevated level of cutinase activity compared to that of the wild-type strain. 
This was observed to be the case in both IPTG-induced and non-induced cultivation conditions, which 
further verifies the enhanced activity of the PbetI(H12) promoter. However, it also highlights the 
promoter's ability to exhibit enhanced activity in the presence of BetI.  
The same enzyme activity assay was performed on the supernatant from the bioreactor cultivations. In 
this case the results showed a cutinase activity of only 10.53 U/mL and 11.70 U/mL after 24 hours 
which was a significant reduction compared to the activity obtained in the shake flask cultivation which 
showed a cutinase activity of 90.64 U/mL after 24 hours of growth under the same conditions, with 
IPTG induction in the pre-culture stage, but not the main culture. Furthermore, after 48 hours the 
cutinase activity measured from bioreactor 1 and 2 was only increased by 14% and 26% to 12.05 
U/mL and 14.71 U/mL respectively. Based on these results, it appears that the cells had a significantly 
higher cutinase production efficiency during the initial 24 hours of the cultivation compared to the final 
24 hours.  
One of the challenges that presented itself in this study was the substantial variation within the cell 
population, where only a fraction of the cell population exhibited GFPuv fluorescence. An approach to 
address this issue could be genomic integration of the transcriptional switch. This strategy could 
minimize the loss of the plasmid caused by the increased metabolic burden, and potentially decrease 
the heterogeneity within the population. The work for this approach was initiated in this study with the 
design of a genome integration strategy for three different OFF-switch constructs. However, due to 
time limitations, only one of the OFF-switch constructs was integrated into the genome of C. 
glutamicum. 
Another issue was the reduced cutinase production efficiency observed from the fed-batch cultivation. 
This issue highlights the need for further research into optimization of cultivation conditions in large 
scale fermentations and is a challenge that needs to be addressed before scaling up to industrial 
production levels.  
The PbetI(H12) promoter mutant was showed to have leaky transcription when induced by 0.2 mM 
IPTG. This issue requires further attention, as this degree of leaky transcription is undesirable for 
industry scale production systems. It might be possible to reduce the leaky transcription somewhat by 
increasing the IPTG concentration used to induce the OFF-switch, however it is unlikely to fully solve 
the issue. Furthermore, increased IPTG usage would result in significantly increased production costs 
when scaled-up. Further research into the binding kinetics between the BetI repressor and PbetI 
promoter seems to be a more reasonable approach. It would be valuable to gain a better

understanding of how the H12 mutation affects the repressors affinity towards the promoter, as it 
might be feasible to optimize this aspect of the system through protein engineering. 
 
6. Conclusion 
In this study, C. glutamicum CR099 carrying the expression vector pEKEx2, harboring a transcriptional 
OFF-switch construct with and without a mutation in the PbetI promoter, was characterized. The 
PbetI(H12) promoter mutant was found to exhibit a slightly reduced growth rate compared to the wild 
type, but increased GFPuv expression. The cutinase activity of the supernatant was observed to be 
elevated as well. However, it was demonstrated that induction of the transcriptional OFF-switch with 
0.2 mM IPTG was insufficient to completely turn off GFPuv-transcription in the promoter mutant in 
contrast to the wild type, which could indicate reduced affinity between the BetI repressor and the 
mutated PbetI(H12) promoter. Additionally, the expression level of GFPuv and mCherry as well as the 
cutinase expression, was shown to be decreased over time which could indicate loss of plasmid. 
Furthermore, characterization of the promoter mutant was performed in an upscaled bioreactor 
cultivation. The cultivation conditions of the bioreactor were demonstrated to cause a significant 
decrease of the cutinase activity levels observed in the supernatant, highlighting the need for further 
research into optimization of bioprocess conditions to achieve higher yields. 
An effort at incorporating the transcriptional OFF-switch into the genome of C. glutamicum was made 
resulting in 40 transformants, with 21 of them exhibiting elevated GFPuv fluorescence. However, due 
to the time limitations of the project, successful genome integration of the OFF-switch construct has 
not yet been verified by PCR or sequencing.
