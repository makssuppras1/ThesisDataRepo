Abstract
This paper investigates the implementation of a Data Assimilation Framework in
a linear reservoir model with 3, 5, and 7 reservoirs and in a piecewise linear sur-
rogate model covering the Damhus ˚A catchment in Northern Copenhagen. The
DA Framework used a Ensemble Kalman Filter and a Deterministic Ensemble
Kalman Filter to update the models. The linear reservoir model is used to obtain
knowledge and the framework. Several diﬀerent simulations show that the frame-
work is able to update all three version of the reservoir model. The simulation
shows, that the placement of the observation point have a great inﬂuence on the
results, both of the reservoir it is located on and also on the surrounding reservoirs.
The piecewise model is updated in respect to observations provided by Greater
Copenhagen utility company HOFOR from 2016. Four measuring points are used;
Moseholmen, Tofteøjevej and Dæmningen 01 and 02. Results from a complex Mike
Urban model and the surrogate model are compared to the observations from the
four points. The Mike Urban model and simulations show it is able to capture the
general tendencies in the dry weather ﬂow and peaks, though with underestima-
tions in both cases. The surrogate model is calibrated for dry weather ﬂow and
then simulated before it is implemented with the DA Framework. The simulation
with this show that it captured both the dry weather ﬂow and peaks well. The
dry weather ﬂow generally with higher daily variation than the observed data and
underestimation of the peaks similar to the Mike Urban results.
Implementing the surrogate model with the DA Framework forced some changes
to the PRM and fairly some comprises with the updating parameters. The surro-
gate model is updated only with the EnKF for three diﬀerent periods, a short dry,
long dry and a long wet period. The short dry is the only one which have been
possible to simulate with a R = 1 and R = 50. The other two is simulated with
lowest possible R found, R = 5 or 6, and R = 50. Increasing the R factor have a
positive eﬀect on the stability of the model and the ensemble updates. A increased
R factor reduce the risk of ensemble collapse. Increasing the standard deviation of
the observation also have an positive aﬀect on the ensemble as it lowers the risk
of the ensemble updating to random unobserved peaks.
Comparing the Mike Urban and the surrogate model they both show that there are
able to simulate the overall tendencies in the observations. The diﬀerence in com-
putational time is noticeable. Mike Urban can not simulated a year continuously.
It is simulate one month at a time, which take several hours. The surrogate model
is able to simulate a whole year in ﬁve minutes. They both struggle capturing
the dry weather ﬂow and none of them are able to capture a yearly variation in
the model. This paper show a base of a further investigation of the use of simple
and quicker models on a daily basis and with DA Frameworks. However, it was
not possible to improve the updated model through the DA Framework as was
intended.

Sammenfatning
Denne rapport undersøger implementeringen af er Data Assimilering (DA) Frame-
work gennem en lineær reservoir model med 3, 5 og 7 reservoirer og i en stykvis
lineær surrogat model, som dækker Damhus ˚A-oplandet in the nordlige København.
DA Frameworket anvendte et Ensemble Kalman Filter og et Deterministisk En-
semble Kalman Filter til at opdatere modellerne.
Den lineær reservoir model
bruges til at opn˚a viden om frameworket. Forskellige simuleringer viser, at den er
i stand til at opdatere alle tre reservoir modellen. Simuleringer viser, at placerin-
gen af observationspunktet har stor indﬂydelse p˚a opdatering og sidst det endelig
resultat, b˚ade p˚a reservoiret, den er placeret p˚a, og p˚a de omliggende.
Den stykvis lineær model er opdateret i forhold til observationer fra Hovedstadens
forsyningsselskab, HOFOR, som dækker 2016.
Der anvendes ﬁre m˚alepunkter.
B˚ade resultater fra en kompleks Mike Urban model og surrogat modellen sam-
menlignes med observationerne fra de 4 pointer. Mike Urban modellen, som er
basis for PRM, viser, at det er stand til at fange tørvejr ﬂow retfærdigt og de
ﬂeste af toppene i strømmen. Men generelt har et lavere estimat af b˚ade tørvejr
ﬂowet og udsvigningerne. Surrogate modellen er kalibreret for tørt vejr ﬂower,
samt simuleret inden implementeret med DA Framework. Simuleringen viser viser,
at den fanger b˚ade tørvejrs periode og udsving meget godt. Tørvejrs Flowet er
generelt højere end observeret, særligt de daglig variation er højere. Dertil estimere
den udsvingene lavere end observeret ligesom Mike Urban modellen.
Implementering af surrogatet sammen med DA Framework medførte nogle æn-
dringer til PRM. Det blev nødvendigt at skrue lidt p˚a model parametrene i forbindelse
med opdatering. Surrogat modellen er kun forsøgt opdateret med EnKF. Dette
er gjort i 3 forskellige perioder, en kort tør, lang tør og lang v˚ad periode. Den
korte tørre periode er den eneste, der har været mulig at simulere med R = 1 og
R = 50. De to andre perioder er simuleret med lavest mulig R fundet (R = 5
eller 6) og R = 50. Forøgelse af R faktoren har en positiv eﬀekt p˚a stabiliteten
af modellen og ensemblets opdateringer. En øget R faktor fjerner risikoen for at
ensemblet kollapser. En øgelse af observationernes standardafvigelse har ogs˚a en
positiv eﬀekt p˚a ensemblet, da det mindsker risikoen for, at det ﬁnder tilfældige
uobserverede udsving.
En sammenligning af Mike Urban og surrogat modellen viser at de begge, kan
simulerer de generelle tendenser i observationerne. Der er dog en stor begræn-
sning forskel. Mike Urban kan ikke simulere et ˚ar kontinuerligt og simulere kun en
m˚aned ad gangen. Surrogate modellen er i stand til at simulere et helt ˚ar p˚a fem
min. Begge modeller har svært ved at fange tørvejr ﬂowet, og ingen af dem er i
stand til at fange den ˚arlig variation. Det er ikke klart om den ene bedre end den
anden p˚a nuværende tidspunkt. Dog har det ikke været muligt at forbedre den
opdateret model via DA Framework as var intentionen.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The Danish sewer system consists of large and complex structures, that handles
wastewater, everyday rain water and heavy stormwater. As the precipitation pat-
terns changes, and heavy precipitation occurs more often, the drainage system
is challenged. To get the maximum potential from the urban drainage system
it is important to understand, manage, optimise and control the system. There
are various methods to improve an existing wastewater system (Bechmann, 1999).
Therefore the expectations and requirements for drainage models, have through
the years become of a very high standard in order to meet the requirements and
legislation. Additionally the drainage models now require a large level of detail
alongside a reasonable computation time. Our increasing knowledge and the large
computer capacity that is nowadays available, allows us to accommodate this.
However, the aim that models are able to predict the behaviours of any given sys-
tem ﬂawless, still seems far away from us today. Nonetheless the models are widely
used and needed by for example the utility companies. They relay on these models
for management and are used heavily for rerouting, expansion and upgrading the
systems. Distributed urban drainage models, as they are called, have become very
large, covering entire cities with all pipes and manholes, basins and so on. There-
fore, there is a growing demand for simpliﬁed, faster models, which still obtain the
high accuracy we know today and thereby can improve our sewer system.
Bechmann (1999) mentions, among others, two approaches to improve the sewer
systems. One way is to extend the system with Low Impact Development instal-
lations or basins to store excessive water from heavy rainfall, another could be to
use modern real-time data, online measurements, which Hutton et al. (2014a) also
touches upon. The second method to improve and modify urban drainage models
could be by including Real Time Control (RTC), and thereby meet consumer and
regulatory demands (Hutton et al., 2014a). The method can also contribute to
reduce ﬂooding and combined sewerage overﬂow (Hutton et al., 2014b). There
are many diﬀerent RTC methods, used through an online platform. The infor-
mation about wastewater system can be used to control decisions, which can lead
to a intermittent improvement the performance and control change of the system

(Sch¨utze et al., 2004). A method for use of observed data could be implementing a
Data Assimilation (DA) scheme. DA can be used to reduce the model uncertain-
ties, by adjusting the model using measurements that are related to the modelled
quantities (Borup et al., 2014). DA methods are successfully used in oceanography,
meteorology predictions, oil reservoir modelling and groundwater modelling (Hunt
et al., 2004; Borup et al., 2014). It is a well known statistical based method which
uses previous observed data together with a current model step to update the next
model step. It is a way to improve the accuracy of the estimation for a next state.
The beneﬁts of using DA in e.g. weather predicting could be gained in hydrol-
ogy modelling as well, leading to a more correct description of the observed reality.
A major challenges in urban drainage modelling is, as mentioned, the compu-
tation time. The known physical-based distribution urban drainage models often
have a long simulation time, when operating large complex models. Many theses
and studies have in the last decade optimised these model to less complex, simpli-
ﬁed model that simulated the same catchment with less quantities. An option is to
simplify directly in the used program or use another platform. Using a simpliﬁed
drainage models one has the ability to move the same amount of water within
the system, through fewer nodes and links leading to faster simulation time. The
main challenge with a simpliﬁcation is not to compromise to much with the re-
sults. Keeping in mind, that when modelling there are always compromises that
will have to be made, as a model will always be a simpliﬁed representation of the
truth and not the truth itself.
This project investigates a simpliﬁcation of an urban drainage model and its abil-
ity to represent the observations from the true system. The aim is to improve
the model through update eﬀects with a DA scheme. This is done through the
Ensemble Kalman Filter (EnKF) and the Deterministic Ensemble Kalman Filter
(DEnKF). A large Mike Urban model is, prior to this project, simpliﬁed into simple
ﬂow volume relationship based PRM driven through a surrogate model. Observa-
tions from the Greater Copenhagen Utility Company (HOFOR) are compared to
Mike Urban simulation and the results obtained from the surrogate model. These
observations are used to improve the accuracy of the surrogate model through a
DA framework. The thesis is presented as follows, Chapter 2 gives an overview
of DA schemes along with the ensemble based Kalman Fliters. Two models are
used in this project Chapter 3 describes a linear reservoir model, which simu-
lates a conceptual linear reservoir model and a surrogate model. Both models
are implementing with the DA framework. The catchment and collected HOFOR
observation is presented in Chapter 4 and 5. Chapter 6 describes the surrogate
model, which covers a simpliﬁcation of the drainage system Western Copenhagen
and a dry weather ﬂow calibration. The performance of the surrogate model is
tried enhanced through the DA scheme. The improvements and results for this
will be presented in Chapter 7. Last the results and analysis are discussed and
concluded upon in Chapter 8 and 9.

2.1
Data assimilation
Data assimilation (DA) uses observations to update a model state and thereby
improve the ﬁnal model and its results. DA combines information from the past
with new information to predict the next step in the model. It is a method to
dynamically minimize the error in a numerical model. Through DA a system is not
only governed by the system equations but also by observations. DA is for instance
a key component in numerical weather and ocean predictions, and oil reservoir
modelling (Hunt et al., 2004; Borup et al., 2014; Evensen, 2007). In hydrology
it has been used in both groundwater modelling (Hunt et al., 2004) and for state
updating, for instances for characterizing of soil moisture, rainfall-runoﬀmodelling
and ﬂood forecasting (Pathiraja et al., 2016). DA is a analysis technique, which
accumulated observated information to the model state, by taking consistency
constraints with laws of time evolution and physical properties. (Bouttier and
Courtier, 1999). There are two basic approaches when assimilating (Bouttier and
Courtier, 1999):
• Sequential: Considers observation made in the past up to the time of analysis,
which is seen in real time assimilation systems.
• Non-Sequential: Only observations from the past can be used for instance in
reanalysis exercise
With the two options the analysis can either be intermittent or continuous. With
a intermittent analysis the observation can be processed in small batches, which
is usually technically convenient. In continuous analysis observation batched over
longer periods are considered, and the correction to the analysed state is smooth
in time which is physically more realistic (Bouttier and Courtier, 1999). Optimally
all the assimilation of the data sources provide information about the state of the
physical systems and therefore have the potential to improve model predictions
(Borup et al., 2014). Every time an observation is available the ensemble is updated
using the observations (Borup et al., 2014), and through every update the model
aim to become more accurate (Bouttier and Courtier, 1999). The meaning and

2.2
Kalman Filter
The Kalman Filter (KF) can be introduced as a method for estimating and pre-
dicting the unmeasured state vector (Madsen, 2008). The KF was ﬁrst introduced
by R. E. Kalman in 1960 (Kalman, 1960). From its introduction the ﬁlter has
been used for guidance, navigation, and control of vehicles, particularly aircraft
and spacecraft. The U.S Navy have used it for their nuclear ballistic missile sub-
marine navigation system. Since then it has grown and is now a commonly applied
approach in time series analysis and used in ﬁelds as signal processing and econo-
metric.
The KF is essential to understand the further work in this paper, but will not
be used in its original form. The ﬁlter is an mathematical process, which uses a
series of observations containing statistical uncertainties to estimate model vari-
ables. The KF is the optimal sequential assimilation method for linear models
(Evensen, 2007). The observations used are assumed to be stochastic and Gaus-
sian distributed. It uses Bayesian Inference to estimate the probability distribution
of the model variables. The statistical method of Bayesian Inference uses Bayes
theorem to update a distribution probability (Box and Tiao, 1973). The update
of the model is based on the prior distribution, a likelihood distribution and the
uncertainties or strengths of these. Normally the prior distribution is the previous
model state, the observations is the likelihood distribution and the updated state
is their uncertainty. The KF consists of a prediction, which is an estimation of
future values xk4, a reconstruction (ﬁltering), which is an estimation of the values
xk and an interpolation (smoothing), which is a estimation of the past values of
xk (Madsen, 2008).
The Bayesian update uses the error covariance P (2.2.1), which is calculated from
the KF itself. One derives P by propagating the estimate of it from the previous
analysis. It is done forward in time throug the model operator and addition of the
process error covariance Q (Borup, 2015).

2.3
Ensemble Kalman Filter
A method to use DA is through a Ensemble Kalman Filter (EnKF). This ﬁlter is
based on the principles of the KF, and is used for larger, more complex models,
which the KF cannot manage in its original form. The EnKF have gained its
popularity through its simple conceptual formulation and it has an eﬀective and
reasonable computation time (Evensen, 2009; Borup et al., 2014). It is optimal
for linear and Gaussian distributed systems. However, recently it have also been
tested on non-linear systems, e.g. Borup et al. (2014). With the EnKF, the ﬁlter
is built up by m models, an ensemble, in contrast to the KF where only one model
is propagated. The x vector in the KF, is replaced by X, which is a matrix of m
x x vectors. An ensemble is a set of individual models, which is used by ENKF
to update the model. The covariance matrix P is one of the reasons that the
EnKF handles larger systems so well. Here the sample covarinace is replaced with
a matrix based on an ensemble model. EnKF gives a statistical approximation to
EnKF by gathering the error of the forecast and analysis. It was ﬁrst formulated
by Evensen (1994) and is based on an updated state, analysed state and a model
forecast, which is described as the background state. The EnKF relies on the as-
sumption that an observed quantity can be regarded as a stochastic variable that
is Gaussian distributed with mean and variance that equals the measurement and
the measurement noise (Borup et al., 2014).

Ensemble
As mentioned this project has an ensemble based approach when implementing the
DA. The EnKF and the below described DEnKF are both ensemble based ﬁltering
methods. The addition of an ensemble is the essential thing that makes them dif-
ferent from the original KF. As mentions, is an ensemble a set of individual models,
or diﬀerent states of the same system. It is used to forecast, but instead of only a
single forecast, the ensemble one is provided with a set of diﬀerent models, which
gives a wider range of indications of possible future states of the modelled scenario.
It is important to keep in mind, when working with an ensemble, the size of
the ensemble, as it can have a great inﬂuence on the model state and results. An
ensemble size should be large enough to generate the information to the model
variables that the observation contains (Burgers et al., 1998). An ensemble size
too small can make the analysis error margin larger.
It can give a very poor
approximation of the initial ensemble (Burgers et al., 1998). However, a large
ensemble size can slow down the simulation time remarkably, without gaining the
wanted eﬀect.

2.3.2
Localisation
Localisation is a method that can minimise the correlations in the ensemble. When
having a limited ensemble size one can experience problems with spurious correla-
tion (Borup, 2015; Bocquet, 2017). An inaccurate correlation can also arise due to
wrong assumptions and speciﬁcations concerning the structure of the ﬁlter noise
on forcing, states and parameters (Borup, 2015). This could lead to unwanted
long-range corrections to the model. Such a problem can be dealt with by apply-
ing a localisation scheme in the model, which insure that the model adjustments
decreases with the distance to the observation (Hamill and Whitaker, 2001). Lo-
calisation will not be used in this project, as the ensemble size is considered to be
large enough for this not to be necessary.
2.3.3
Inﬂation
When depending on a Bayesian-based ensemble update one is continuously reduc-
ing the ensemble variance. Furthermore most errors related to ensemble based
ﬁlters tend to lead to underestimated error covaricenes. This can lead to a ensem-
ble collapse. If the variance of the ensemble becomes smaller than the variance
of the observation, the ensemble is in danger of collapsing. A minimised variance
means that the observations are ignored by the ﬁlter and the model is therefore free
to diverge towards any value far form the observations. To counteract this one can
11
==PAGE:26==
2.4. DETERMINISTIC ENSEMBLE KALMAN FILTER
use inﬂation. The method seeks to increase the ensemble spread by multiplying
the ensemble anomalies by a certain factor during the update cycle (Borup, 2015).
The method is directly used in the DEnKF, where the anomalies are multiplied by
0.5. This way the ﬁlter itself makes the correction, so no inﬂation is needed. As
with the localisation this method will not be used on the EnKF in this project.
2.3.4
Dampening corrections with the R-factor
The Kalman gain, Eq 2.3.2, is obtain through the updating phase have an impor-
tant inﬂuence on the next updating step. It is used to update both the model
state and the covariance. Damping the gain will results an lower eﬀect of the gain
and thereby a else eﬀective update of the next model state. A lowering of the
Kalman gain means that the update follow the model prediction more than the
observations leading to an less eﬀective update. The Peter Sakov DA Framework
used in this project formulates this damping through a so call R factor. R = 1
allow a complete Kalman gain and thereby a full update. Increasing the R factor
will lower the Kalman gain and thereby lower its ability to contribute to an full
update.
2.4

Another ﬁlter, which will be used in this project is the Deterministic Ensemble

paper.

Chapter 6
Surrogate model
This section will cover the surrogate model. This model is, as mentioned, created
from the MU model results of the Damhus ˚A catchment in the western part of
Copenhagen. The model is developed by Morten Borup, DTU Environment. The
surrogate model is a piecewise linear model and the structure is shown in Figure
6.1. The structure is derived from an original setup for a traditional hydrody-
namic model. The model is set up to simulate any given PRM. The PRM used is
derived directly from the result of the MU model. It consists of 165 compartments
describing the water volume ﬂowing between them. To simulate a whole year of
rainfall is approximately ﬁve minutes. The equivalent model in Mike Urban takes
several days to run, for the same period. It is primarily because it is not possible
to run the whole MU model at once and needs to be done one month at a time.
Figure 6.1: An illustration of the structure of the surrogate
model, derived from an original set up for a traditional
hydrodynamic model.

6.1. THE PRM
6.1
The PRM
The PRM contains information of the 165 compartments and 258 connections,
which represents the northern part of the Damhus ˚A catchments. The ﬁle lists
the allowed volume exchange between the diﬀerent compartments, through a ﬂow
volume relationship (VQ relationship). In Appendix D one can see an illustration
of how this relationship are calculated. The water is exchanged though connections
modelled as existing pipes in the drainage system, meaning that the ﬂows can be
directly coupled with observed data for the covered area. The PRM have been
modiﬁed several times during this implementation, ﬁrst to the surrogate model
and again when implementing the DA Framework, Section 7. In the surrogate
model small changes have been made to increasing last exchange volume, for given
connections. This increase allows more water through the system, preventing a
simulation stop. This is done as the model became unstable when applying rain
and then again when the dry weather ﬂow was applied. The historic rain volumes
were too large and it causes the original maximum volumes to be exceeded. The
changes means that the allowed volume in the last time step is increased so more
water can ﬂow through. During the implementation of the DA framework to the
surrogate model a lot of instability occurred leading to even more changes to the
PRM. Section 7 lists all second round modiﬁcations.
6.2
Dry weather ﬂow
A MU drainage model takes dry weather ﬂow into account directly in its simu-
lation. The surrogate model simulates by moving volumes around based on the
input it receives. The model does not generate dry weather ﬂow so this would have
to be applied manually. The raw results from the surrogate model can be seen in
Figure 6.2, where the zero dry weather ﬂow is illustrated.
11 rain inputs are applied to the model.
This caused the model to stop run-
ning in the initial simulation.
There were no indication of where the problem
were occurring, other than it caused ”a matrix to exceed it’s dimensions”. Trying
to detect the problem the main focus was on the rain input, as it was the only
thing changed after receiving the model. The input rain and length were modi-
ﬁed. These diﬀerent loads caused a stop, but no systematic pattern were found.
Finally it was discovered that the problem was not caused by the inputs, but by
the DLL setup, which runs the surrogate mode, and was due to the size of some of
the compartments. The DLL were not able to check for numerical stability. The
model can become unstable if a compartment is emptied too quickly or the ﬂow
becomes too large when compared to the volume. In Mike Urban one can adjust
for these challenges by changing the timesteps. However; in the surrogate model
the time steps are one minute and can not be changed. Therefore some setting in
the DLL were changed. The model is also stop before it reaches an unstable state
and will give a direct message, so one knows where the problem occurs.

Chapter 8
Discussion
Through this project the DA framework have been implemented to two diﬀerent
models, a linear reservoir model and the surrogate model. In between a calibration
have be done for the dry weather ﬂow in the surrogate model. This chapter will
look at the process in this paper and evaluate the performance of the two models.
The main focus will be on the work and simulations done with the updated model
in Section 7.
8.1
Linear Reservoir model
The linear reservoir model used in section 3 were used to get familiar with the DA
framework and the two ﬁlters, EnKF and DEnKF. Various diﬀerent parameters
have been modiﬁed to see how they impact the DA scheme. The linear reservoir
model implemented with the DA Framework have been a good tool to get an
understanding on how they work together and ﬁlters work and update.
It is
scripted in such a way, that is it easy to run and thereafter plot the results. Even
though it is a great tool, one should keep in mind that is it a very well performing
model, as well as it is a small linear set up. Therefore not all the results are one
to one comparable to what is obtained, when applying the framework to a larger
model, which also was the case with the updated model. However, the simulations
done show the importance of the observation point. The placement of the point
can have an great inﬂuence on the results of a linear model. It became quit clear
that placing the point in the inlet of the string will update the following reservoir
almost as good as where the observation point is placed. Placing the observation
point on the outlet instead, will update the reservoirs upstream for the placement,
but this update will come in second step and not straight away, due to the step
wise update. Working with the linear reservoir model and the implementation
have been usefull in the later wotk with the DA Framework.

8.3
Performance of the updated model
The updated model implementation were original set up to update a system for
only 12 connections. Therefore the script needed some alternations to ﬁt a catch-
ment with 165 compartments and 258 connections, as the one in this paper. The
original set up had a large output list, saving all connection details and model
covariance matrix, among others. When the larger PRM was applied the model
had a simulation time of several hours, depending on the chosen length of period,
and there were several simulation stop due to memory limitation. The memory
limitation were approached by not saving the model’s covariance matrix, which
can become a very large matrix. Further the simulation time were brought down
to a maximum of an hour and a half, by only saving ﬂow values for the 4 connec-
tions of interest. This means that is it not possible to see the performance of any
other connections. It could, however, be needed to save other connection if the
model has other purposes than this project. One should be advised to chose only
the needed connection and save then, as it have a big inﬂuence on the simulation
time.

PRM limitations
The PRM describing the VQ-relationship have played a large role in the challenges

some of the updating limitations is caused by the structure of the PRM. Diﬀerent
problems through this project have been caused by errors in the PRM, which have
delayed this process. The volume correction have been done more than once. Both
when loading the rain and dry weather ﬂow in section 6 but especially in Section
7. In the beginning the volume would be corrected right away till the model was
running the full simulation period. It was not possible when implementing the DA
as it is very consuming. With R = 1 it could be a continuously hourly correction
where one correction would lead to another error and so forth. It could take hours
to get the model to end an simulation. The problem with the VQ-relationship is
not solved yet. The PRM still leads to simulation stops and an thorough review
of it is to work problem-free. There is the unsolved issue with connection 93 and
98, which have a wrongful description of the ﬂow volume. An further investigation
of the area around these connection would be needed to correct the connections.
This could be done by examine the set up in the Mike Urban model once more.
The structure of the PRM causes a limitation to the ensemble update. At ﬁrst
it was unclear what caused the many simulation stops when working with the
updated model, but after some testing it became clear that it was the ensemble
updates. It is very clear in some of the ﬁgures illustrating connection 93 and 250
that the ensemble reaches a upper level which it cannot go above. This mean that
it is not possible to update the model to the correct ﬂow level, but continuous
push the upper limit, which will eventually cause the simulation to stop. This is
not ideal as it forces one to increase the R factor used for the update and thereby
lowering the models ability to update at full potential. The instabilities and the
precautions that had to be made to make the model simulating without stops,
could be one of the reason there none of the data assimilation attempts, which
improved the model remarkable.

Conclusion
The DA framework have successfully been applied to a the linear reservoir model
with both 3, 5 and 7 reservoirs. Through this model it is shown that the observa-
tion point have a great impact on the ensemble updates and results of the linear
model. Placing the observation point on the inlet reservoir of a string will lead to
a almost full update of all following reservoirs. Placing the observation point on
the outlet reservoir will lead to a later update, but with time it will both improve
the reservoir it is placed on and the previous reservoirs in the string. The linear
reservoir model also give an improvement when adding more than one observation
point to the system.
The DA framework implementation on a piecewise linear updated model have
proven to is a bit more complex. Here the observation point will update the point
it is located on, however varies diﬀerent tests shows that it cannot guarantee a suc-
cessful and improving update to the surrounding points. Connection 249 and 250
are not able to improve one another and will eﬀect each other negative, dragging
downwards or upwards, respectfully, to match their own observations. Further-
more the updated model is only able to simulate one short period under optimal
condition with R = 1. Any chances made after this run show that a higher R factor
is needed to obtain a stable simulation. An increased R factor comprise with the
models ability to update. That leads to a great deal of the results to be the same
as original or worse. Simulations with a long dry weather period show the best im-
provement for the connections upstream from the observation point. Connection
249 performance overall best and is able to improve connection 93 through all sim-
ulations. Observation at connection 250 are signiﬁcantly higher than at the other
connections, so when updating for this, it will worsen the model predictions at the
other connections. The worst performing period for the updated model is the long
wet period were little or non improvement is seen upstream from the observation
points. Nevertheless both changing the R factor and the standard deviation of
the observations have a positive eﬀect on the ensemble updates. Increasing the R
factor will secure that the ensemble does not collapse. An increase of the standard
deviation can smooth the ensemble, so it does not end up updating and creating

The PRM in the model needs to be further investigated to fully estimate how
the problems related to it can be solved. Before that is done, the simulations done
with the updated models will be too unstable. Right now the simulations stops so
random that even the smallest changes can cause the model to stop.
Compared to the results both Mike Urban Model, the surrogate model and the
updated model are able to simulate the same results. They both simulate the ﬂow
with the same tendencies, capturing the long dry weather period best and the long
wet weather period worse. An overall conclusion, on which of the two models are
performing best, is at this point hard conclude. But even without any updates
applied the surrogate model is able to capture both the peaks of the rain event and
the dry weather ﬂow. This shows that this simple, quick surrogate model is a good
base for further investigating towards using these simple models on a daily basis.
That said, the Data Assimilation done did not improve the model performance,
as one could have hope for. The indicates that it cannot be used as a easy ﬁt to
compensate for the model error.
