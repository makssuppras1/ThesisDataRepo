Abstract
This Master’s thesis deals with temporal modes which constitute a new method for data
multiplexing. To this day there is no known way of demultiplexing the temporal modes
using passive optical elements, and instead one must resort to nonlinear optics.
Asymmetrically-pumped Bragg scattering is a type of four-wave mixing that combines
a strong, broad pump and a weak, short pump, to enable frequency conversion of a much
weaker optical signal. The process, that takes place within an optical ﬁber, is highly
dependent on the temporal shape of the signal, and can therefore be selective with
respect to a set of temporal modes.
It is found that a single stage of frequency conversion performs non-ideally with the
ﬁgure of merit, the “selectivity” describing the ability to convert only a target temporal
mode, being limited to 0.83. By using the temporal-mode interferometer that incorpo-
rates an additional stage of frequency conversion, it is shown that selectivities in the
excess of 0.98 can be reached. This result is also supported by numerical work.

Resumé
Dette kandidatspeciale omhandler tidslige modes, der udgør en ny metode til at multi-
plekse data. Der er i dag ikke nogen kendt måde at demultiplekse tidslige modes på ved
brug af passive optiske elementer, og i stedet må gøres brug af ikke-lineær optik.
Asymmetrisk pumpet Bragg scattering er en form af ﬁrebølgeblanding, der kom-
binerer en stærk, bred pumpe og en svag, kort pumpe for at muliggøre frekvenskonver-
tering af et endnu svagere optisk signal. Processen, der foregår inden i en optisk ﬁber,
afhænger kritisk af signalets tidslige form, og kan derfor blive brugt selektivt i forbindelse
med et sæt af tidslige modes.
Det viser sig at et enkelt stadie af frekvenskonvertering resulterer i at “selektiviteten”,
som beskriver evnen til kun at konvertere en ønsket tidslig mode, er begrænset til 0.83.
For at forbedre dette gøres brug af det tidslige mode interferometer, som introducerer
endnu et stadie af frekvenskonvertering. Herved opnås selektiviteter der overstiger 0.98
— et resultat som underbygges ved brug af numeriske simuleringer.

Preface
This master’s thesis by the title “Asymmetrically-pumped Bragg Scattering for Tempo-
ral Mode Multiplexing” theoretically deals with the nonlinear optical process four-wave
mixing in connection to temporal mode (de)multiplexing.
The reader is expected to
be familiar with the concept of optical ﬁbers, and to hold knowledge about the ﬁelds
nonlinear optics and quantum optics on the master’s level.
The work related to this thesis has been performed in the time span from January 1st,
2015 to June 30th, 2015, and constitutes 35 ECTS points of the Photonics Engineering
M.Sc. Eng. education at the Technical University of Denmark. The work was initiated
and ﬁnished at the department of Photonics Engineering at the Technical University of
Denmark under the supervision of Prof. Karsten Rottwitt, while it in the period from
the middle of February to the middle of May was conducted at the Oregon Center of
Optics, University of Oregon, Oregon, USA, under the guidance of Prof. Michael Raymer.
I would like to start out by expressing my thankfulness to the many people I met in
Eugene, Oregon, for making my stay truly unforgettable. I consider myself extremely
lucky having made so many great friends in such a short amount of time.
At the University of Oregon, I was fortunate enough to be able to steal away some
time from graduate student Dileep V. Reddy. I hardly think I have ever encountered
a brighter mind, and besides providing guidance on my project, he helped me code in
Fortran90, and introduced me to the concept of MPI. I would also like to send my sincere
gratitude to Prof. Michael Raymer. Not only did he allow me to visit and be a part of his
research group, he also contributed with unique insight and a seemingly inﬁnite number
of ‘what’s-next’-ideas.
The stay in Eugene was made possible by virtue of externally received funding. I
therefore send my greatest thanks to Østsjællands Andelsvaskeri Fond, Knud Højgaards
Fond, and Henry og Mary Skovs Fond for being the ﬁnancial enablers of the trip.
At the Technical University of Denmark I would like to thank Niels Møller and Kasper

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Photons play an essential role in existing classical information networks as well as in the
majority of the schemes proposed for quantum information science [1, 2, 3]. It is therefore
of both fundamental and practical interest, to be able to manipulate, control, and sort
photons in each of the available degrees of freedom. The conjugate variables time and
energy (or time and frequency) constitute such a degree of freedom, and have already
been utilized with great success in classical optical information to achieve time-division
multiplexing and wavelength-division multiplexing.
There are alternative ways of exploiting the time-frequency degree of freedom, and
the most recently proposed is that of temporal modes [4, 5]. Temporal modes are contin-
uous orthogonal overlapping functions of time (frequency) [6], and thereby multiplexing
with temporal-modes is conceptually very similar to multiplexing using higher-order spa-
tial modes. As the theoretical and practical work concerning temporal modes is still in
its infancy, there are still many interesting problems to be solved. One of the challeng-
ing aspects are that of selectively choosing a particular temporal-mode component and
spatially separate it from the remainder of the orthogonal temporal-mode set. Recently,
nonlinear signal processing using sum-frequency generation in second-order nonlinear
crystal waveguides has been proposed for overcoming this issue [4, 7, 8, 9]. This thesis
deals with an alternative solution using four-wave mixing in optical ﬁbers.
Nonlinear optics is the main work tool used in this thesis, and we therefore start
out in Section 1.1 with a quick introduction to a research ﬁeld that has been in rapid
development over the past 50 years.
The central nonlinear eﬀect considered is four-
wave mixing Bragg scattering which is described in Section 1.2. Not until Section 1.3
do we again encounter the temporal modes, which are ﬁrst presented in the context of
classical information channels. Photon temporal modes have recently been shown to
form a complete framework for quantum information science, which is therefore visited
in Section 1.4. Finally, Section 1.5 states the project goals and presents an outline of
this thesis.

1.1
A Brief Introduction to Nonlinear Optics
Nonlinear optics is the research ﬁeld concerning the optical phenomena that originate
from the interaction between intense light and matter. The laser, having the properties
of creating directional and highly focused beams, allows the creation of the optical ﬁeld
strengths required to observe material responses depending on the ﬁeld strength in a
nonlinear fashion (quadratic, cubic, etc.).
1.1.1
Physical Origin
When an optical ﬁeld is applied to a material, the charged nuclei and electrons feel the
presence of the ﬁeld in terms of an electromagnetic (Lorentz) force. This force displaces
the bound electrons from their equilibrium position around the nuclei, resulting in the
creation of atomic dipoles — the material response. For small optical ﬁeld strengths,
the bound electrons are capable of keeping up with the variations in the ﬁeld, and the
response is said to be linear.
However, if the applied ﬁeld is suﬃciently strong, the
electron displacements become so large that the response becomes an-harmonic, and
thus the material response becomes nonlinear.

1.2
Four-wave Mixing
As the name suggests, FWM is an interaction between four waves of carrier frequencies
which we restrict to the visible and near infrared parts of the electromagnetic spectrum.
In particular, when three of these optical modes overlap, they create temporal beating
patterns inﬂuencing the third-order polarization P (3) at the beating frequency (the fourth

mode). As the induced third-order polarization behave as a driving source in Eq. (1.8),
energy can be transferred between the four optical modes in an energy conserving fashion.
The medium is therefore passively enabling the coupling of the optical modes.
Due to the diﬀerent ways by which the participating ﬁeld modes in FWM can be
permuted, FWM comes in multiple ﬂavors with various potential application possibilities
[15]. It has for example been subject to attention due to its applicability in relation
to signal regeneration needed in long haul ﬁber-optic transmission systems using both
degenerate [16] and non-degenerate pump conﬁgurations [17, 18]. The so called ﬁber-
optic parametric ampliﬁer is interesting by virtue of its broad possible gain region [16, 19]
and its low noise ﬁgure [20]. In addition, FWM enables frequency conversion (FC) of
optical signals [21, 22, 23], allowing for optimization of optical processes which are highly
wavelength dependent. This eﬀect has in later years even been shown to be feasible on
the quantum level, as quantum frequency conversion (QFC) of single photons have been
demonstrated [24, 25]. In particular, an explanation of the work in [24] serves nicely here
as it includes multiple FWM interactions. Initially, part of a pump at λP1 = 808 nm
is coupled into an optical ﬁber. Due to modulation instability, which is analogous to
degenerate FWM, signal and idler photons are created at wavelengths λs1 = 683 nm
and λi = 989 nm as tailored by the phase matching of the ﬁber. In this process, two
photons are annihilated from the pump, while photons are pairwise created in the signal
and idler ﬁelds. A measurement of an idler photon therefore heralds the presence of
the signal photon which is sent into an additional ﬁber together with optical pumps at
wavelengths λP1 = 808 nm and λP2 = 845 nm. In the second ﬁber, non-degenerate FWM
enabled QFC of the photon at λs1 = 683 nm to a wavelength of λs2 = 659 nm, with
an eﬃciency of nearly 30 %. This proof-of-concept experiment neatly demonstrates the
usefulness of FWM to serve both as a ﬁber-based single-photon source using heralding
and as a method of achieving QFC. The FWM interaction resulting in QFC is often
referred to as Bragg scattering [26], and is dealt with in greater detail in the following
section.

1.3
Multiplexing in Optical Communication
The development of ﬁber-optic communication networks has been fundamental in build-
ing the Internet as we know it today. Based on Internet Protocols data is transmitted
through the globe-spanning optical cables in the form of light pulses at carrier frequen-
cies of ∼193 THz (wavelength of 1.55 µm). Thereby, the Internet enables world-wide
communication in a seemingly limitless fashion. However, the exponentially growing ca-
pacity demands do not seem to saturate within the next decades [27], and therefore it
is an ongoing challenge for engineers and scientists to keep up with the evolution. One
straightforward solution to the increasing demands is the simple deployment of additional
ﬁber optical cables. Indeed, the world-wide ﬁber optical network annually grows with
tens of millions kilometers [27], but the simple linear scaling connected to installing addi-
tional cables is not considered a durable solution to the exponential demands of capacity.
For this reason, we require sophisticated ways of controlling and manipulating the light
degrees of freedom (DOF) which in a waveguide geometry comprises (i) polarization, (ii)
energy (frequency), and (iii) transverse-mode proﬁle [28].
The polarization state of light is a two-dimensional state-space representing two or-
thogonal polarizations, which are readily managed and sorted using a polarizing beam
splitter (PBS) cube. The polarization of light has been used to perform polarization-
division multiplexing (PDM), which allows doubling of the data rate [29]. The energy de-
gree of freedom has been utilized to accomplish wavelength-division multiplexing (WDM)
[30], and thus substantially increase bandwidth capacity per ﬁber. This is achieved us-
ing prism based setups to (de)multiplex signals discretely spaced in frequency. Also the
sorting and management of higher-order spatial modes in optical ﬁbers have received a
lot of interest in recent years. Especially focus has been directed towards the orbital

1.4
Quantum Information Science
Quantum information science (QIS) is the ﬁeld of study in physics and informatics con-
cerning using the rules of quantum mechanics to process information [34]. QIS most
fundamentally departs from classical information science by having the quantum bit
‘qubit’ as unit of information. The qubit can, in contrast to the classical digital bit, be
in any quantum mechanical superposition of the orthogonal states represented by |0⟩and
|1⟩, i.e.
|qubit⟩= α |0⟩+ β |1⟩,
(1.13)
where the complex coeﬃcients α and β must obey the condition |α|2 + |β|2 = 1. The
state of a qubit is often represented as a point on the Bloch sphere through the complex
values of α and β. It should however be mentioned, that a single qubit, even though
it in general can exist in a superposition state, can not carry more information than
a classical bit. This statement owes to the fact that the qubit must be measured in
order for it to convey its message — an action that projects the qubit onto one if its
two basis states. However, the unique features of QIS such as quantum entanglement
[35, 36] and quantum teleportation [37, 38], enable scalable QIS systems to solve certain
computational problems more eﬃciently than a classical computer. Examples of such
problems are the factorization of large prime integers which can be done on a quantum
computer using Shor’s quantum factoring algorithm [39] or the search of a database
which can be done using Grover’s quantum search algorithm [40].

1.4. QUANTUM INFORMATION SCIENCE
9
The qubit can in principle be comprised of any two-level quantum system and in
practice it is likely that quantum information schemes will be composed of various diﬀer-
ent qubit systems. Photons are viewed as one the most essential physical realizations of
the qubit, with the two orthogonal states represented as the photon being vertically- or
horizontally polarized. A measurement of the photon state is then easily performed by

1.4.1
Quantum Key Distribution
One of the most interesting applications in QIS is quantum cryptography. The rules of
quantum mechanics allow the distribution of a key between two distant parties conven-
tionally named Alice and Bob, in such a way that they are capable of detecting whether
an eavesdropper Eve has listened in. After having established a shared key, Alice and Bob
can communicate safely. We here brieﬂy review the BB84 protocol [50], which was the
ﬁrst protocol for secure QKD. The BB84 protocol was initially proposed using polarized
photon states, but can equally well be implemented using states of TMs.

space. The ψ-basis and φ-basis are rotated at an angle of 45-degrees with respect to
each other. Alice now generates two random bit strings of equal lengths — one deciding
the bit value and one deciding which of the two bases each bit should be send in. The
generated qubits are transmitted to Bob, who has created a third string of random bits
deciding his measurement basis. In the cases where Alice and Bob use the same basis,
Bob should acquire the correct bit of information in his measurements, while in cases
where they have used diﬀerent bases, Bob only acquires the correct bit with a probability
of 1/2. After revealing their used bases over a public channel, they therefore discard all
bits where the used bases disagree, and compare part of the bits where the used bases
agree. If the compared bits are consistent, Alice and Bob are conﬁdent that the remaining
bits are known only to them, so that these can constitute a key for subsequent secure
communication. It might however be, that Eve has tried to intercept the photons in
transit, in order to acquire knowledge about the key.
In order to do so, Eve must
measure each photon in either the ψ- or the φ-basis, and thus only 50 % of the times,
the qubit is measured in the basis in which it was prepared. The remaining 50 %, the
measurement performed by Eve results in a projection of the qubit onto the wrong basis.
Eve has no way of being aware of this, and due to the no-clonning theorem [51], has
no way of creating a copy of the original qubit state. The presence of Eve is therefore
readily discovered by Alice and Bob when they compare their bits. If the observed error
signiﬁcantly exceeds the error-tolerance of the quantum channel, they can with some
high probability (depending on length of string and number of compared bits) conclude
that Eve has listened in. They therefore discard the remaining bits and start over.
1.5
Statement of Project Goal and Outline
In this thesis we investigate the achievable ﬁdelity with which it is possible to demultiplex
temporal modes using frequency conversion based on non-degenerate four-wave mixing.
In particular we consider a setup where one pump is pulsed and weak while the other is
broad and strong — a scheme which we call asymmetrically-pumped Bragg scattering.
• Chapter 2: We present the coupled-mode theory used to describe asymmetrically-
pumped Bragg scattering, and use the Green function formalism to present ana-
lytical solutions. The Schmidt decomposition is subsequently used in the analysis
of the resulting Green functions, giving rise to natural temporal modes.
• Chapter 3: We introduce the concept of temporal-mode interferometry, and show
how temporal modes can be eﬃciently sorted using multiple stages of frequency
conversion based on asymmetrically-pumped Bragg scattering.
• Chapter 4: We exemplify a generic method which can be used to numerically
achieve the Green function describing frequency conversion.The method is used
to perform a more extensive analysis of the frequency conversion process while
including physical eﬀects that was not possible to incorporate in the analytical
model.
• Chapter 5: The feasibility of the setup proposed in the previous chapters is
considered, while including a discussion of various physical eﬀects that have been
neglected in the analysis.

Asymmetrically-pumped Bragg Scattering
Coupled-mode theory has been widely used to describe phenomena concerning energy
transfer between optical modes [52, 53]. We here expound on using coupled-mode theory
to describe the four-ﬁeld coupling for asymmetrically-pumped Bragg scattering [54]. A
signiﬁcant part of this chapter deals with the derivation of the set of Green functions
which describe the frequency conversion process in the limit where the pump depletion
can be neglected. The Green functions enable the unique determination of signal out-
put amplitudes given speciﬁc pump shapes and ﬁber parameters. Finally, the Schmidt
decomposition is used to analyze the Green functions, which immediately deﬁnes the
natural temporal modes of the frequency conversion process.

2.6
Summary of Chapter
The intention of this chapter was to present an analytical solution for asymmetrically-
pumped Bragg scattering — a four-wave mixing conﬁguration including one very broad
pump p which we treated as a continuous wave ﬁeld, and a much weaker and shorter
pump q.
Mathematically, the interaction between these two pumps and two signals
labelled r and s, was modelled by coupled-mode theory while neglecting pump depletion,
intra-pulse dispersion, and phase modulating eﬀects from the weaker pump q.
The Green function formalism enabled us to ﬁnd the four transfer functions that for
a given set of signal inputs allowed us to explicitly calculate the unique corresponding
signal outputs. These transfer functions, called the Green functions, had functional forms
depending on the pump amplitudes and the optical ﬁber parameters. The analysis of
the Green functions was simpliﬁed by the ﬁnding of two dimensionless parameters that
signiﬁcantly reduced the parameter-space.
Further insight was obtained by using the Schmidt decomposition (singular value
decomposition). This mathematical tool enabled us to decompose the Green functions
into the natural temporal modes (Schmidt modes) of the four-wave mixing process, while
simultaneously achieving information about the conversion eﬃciencies of each temporal
mode. In the search for a method to sort temporal modes, the Schmidt decomposition
thereby allowed for a straightforward assessment in terms of the deﬁned selectivity. By
a thorough search of the parameter-space, a selectivity of S = 0.83 was found to form
an upper bound.

Temporal-mode Interferometry
We begin by providing a short review of the Mach-Zehnder interferometer [71, 72] in
Section 3.1. The reason for this detour is the apparent similarity with a dual-stage QFC
device, which is presented in Section 3.2. Multiple stages of QFC have recently been
suggested in order to enhance the TM selectivity to near unity [9, 73] for sum-frequency
generation in second-order nonlinear waveguides. This scheme, called temporal-mode

3.1.1
Classical Interferometry
An example of the Mach-Zehnder interferometer (MZI) consists of two beam splitters,
two mirrors, and a detector at each of the two outputs. We here review the balanced MZI
where both beam splitters partition light at a 50:50 ratio. Thus light from a coherent

the coherence length of the light source. Depending on the relative phase between the

at either output arm. The phase sensitive operation of interferometers allow them to
e.g. perform temperature ﬂuctuation measurements [74] or act as modulators for com-
munication systems [75, 76], but is also essentially what makes them useful in diverse
ﬁelds as astronomy [77] and seismology [78].

the direction of propagation), and thus their polarization, spectral shape, etc. must be

TWM or FWM [63, 83, 84]. The quantum interference eﬀect has even been demonstrated
experimentally using microwave photons of diﬀerent frequencies [85].

spired the concept of temporal-mode interferometry (TMI) [9]. To understand how this
scheme works, revisit Eq. (2.82) which states the apparent analogue between conversion

3.3.1
Matching the Stages
The temporal-mode matching between the two QFC stages is a critical aspect of TMI.
As apparent from Eq. (3.22), inter-stage temporal mismatch inevitably degrades the
performance. Thus it is important that |µ11| and |ν11| are both as close to unity as
possible. Expressed in terms of the Schmidt modes, the output Schmidt modes of the
ﬁrst stage must overlap temporally with the input Schmidt modes of the second stage.
From Figs. 2.8 and 2.9, it was seen that output and input modes belonging to the same
channel were in fact very similar, though skewed oppositely in time. However, matching
the temporal shapes is not suﬃcient in order to observe the interferometric eﬀect between
the two stages. In fact, the temporal phase proﬁle of the output Schmidt modes of the
ﬁrst stage must be matched to the temporal phase proﬁle of the corresponding input
Schmidt modes of the second stage, as apparent from Eqs. (3.23). In the case where
the stages are temporally mode matched, it is by tuning the inter-stage relative phase
θ, possible to adjust the direction of power ﬂow in the second stage. Thus, as in regular
interferometry, the relative phase between the two signal channels plays a critical role
for TMI.
Returning again to the GFs in Eqs. (2.49)–(2.52), all four kernels have a phase factor
of the form exp [iΓp (t −t′)] in common.

input and output times, it is directly transferred to each of the Schmidt modes as was
seen in Figs. 2.8 and 2.9. From Fourier theory, it is known that an expression of the

3.4
Summary of Chapter
This chapter introduced the concept of temporal-mode interferometry, which bears close
resemblance to normal interferometry with light. Temporal-mode interferometry is based
on dual-stage frequency conversion, with each stage being analogous to the beam split-
ters in a Mach-Zehnder interferometer. Furthermore, in temporal-mode interferometry,
the light is in a color-superposition in between the two stages, rather than in a path-
superposition. The close resemblance, originates from the mathematical description of
the frequency conversion process which can be described as an active beam splitter for
each of the input temporal modes.
Two ﬂavors of temporal-mode interferometry was presented. In the direct collision
conﬁguration the two frequency conversion stages were composed of identical ﬁber de-
signs, and thus in order to obtain an additional ﬁeld collision in the second ﬁber, the
faster propagating signal must be relatively retarded in between the stages. In the re-
verse collision conﬁguration one instead reverses the group-slowness in the second stage,
allowing for a natural second collision of the signals. It was found that the reverse col-
lision conﬁguration allowed for perfect inter-stage temporal mode overlap between the
two stages, i.e. the output Schmidt modes of the ﬁrst stage had potential of perfect ﬁeld
overlap with the input Schmidt modes of the second stage. As this was not the case for
the direct collision conﬁguration, the reverse collision allowed for slightly better results.
We showed that using temporal-mode interferometry, temporal-mode selectivities
in the excess of 0.98 was achievable for both conﬁgurations. In order to obtain such
results, one must engineer each of the stages to 50 % frequency conversion of the target
temporal mode, and optimize the inter-stage variable phase to ensure a coherent addition
of conversion in the second stage. We even demonstrated how the inter-stage variable
phase could be tuned to allow for either complete back-conversion to the original signal
frequency, or complete conversion to the secondary signal frequency.

Width Analysis of pump p
So far in the numerical study it has been assumed that τp = 5|βrs|l, which is more
than enough to ensure that the interaction takes place within the region of approximate
constant amplitude of pump p. From an energy saving point of view, it is therefore
interesting to investigate how sudden the ﬁdelity decreases as we make the pump pulse
shorter. Figure 4.9 shows the selectivity obtained for varying pump widths using the
parameters Pp = 30 W, l = 30 m, τq = 1 ps, Eq = 43.4 fJ, and the signal wavelengths
λs = 656 nm, λr = 733 nm resulting in βsr = 2.3 ps m−1. The x-axis shows the pump
width in units of signal walk-oﬀ|βrs|l which in the present case is 69 ps.
For τp > |βrs|l, the selectivity is almost constant in both conﬁgurations (the selectiv-
ity actually decreases as the pulse width is made shorter, but the eﬀect is so tiny that
it is not apparent form the plot). At τp slightly beneath |βrs|l the selectivity reaches
a ‘threshold’ where it falls oﬀdramatically. Pump p is at these values no longer broad
enough to contain the other pump envelopes throughout propagation. One can therefore
consider τp = |βrs|l as the limit of asymmetrically-pumped Bragg scattering. For smaller
values of τp we are in the regime of two pulsed pumps, where the eﬀect of pump p is no
longer a constant change of the refractive index due to the optical Kerr eﬀect.

4.4
Summary of Chapter
We have here dealt with solving the coupled-mode equations numerically in order to
obtain a more thorough description of the four-wave mixing by including intra-pulse
dispersion and nonlinear phase modulation from the pulsed pump. The split-step Fourier
method with an embedded Runge-Kutta of fourth order was used to model the evolution
in a speciﬁc photonic crystal ﬁber with zero-dispersion wavelength of λ0 = 736 nm.
The method of numerically obtaining the system Green function (or more exact, the
SVD of the Green fucntion) was presented. The method was based on calculating the
signal output for a set of input basis functions, while decomposing the output frequency
converted signal in an appropriate output basis. The Hermite-Gaussian basis was chosen
for both the input- and output basis functions. As the output signals of the s-channel are
somewhat far from being Hermite-Gaussians, the characteristic time scale of the output
basis had to be chosen carefully in order to ensure convergence.
Finally, we performed an analysis on the attainable selectivity after introducing the
additional physical eﬀects. Nonlinear phase modulation from the pulsed pump was seen
to have a detrimental eﬀect on the selectivity in some cases (presumably through complex
imposed phase proﬁles on the signals). However, by making the broader pump stronger
allowed for weakening the shorter pulsed pump and thus limiting this eﬀect.
It was
shown that if the broader pump had a peak power of 30 W, a square-like amplitude of
duration in excess of ∼100 ps, and a single-stage ﬁber length of approximately 30 m,
selectivies in the excess of 0.97 for both the reverse- and direction collision conﬁgurations
were attainable.

CHAPTER 5
Discussion
We now brieﬂy discuss some experimental considerations in connection with the TMI
setup. First, the compatibility of TMM with other types of data multiplexing is in focus.
Secondly, we present a qualitative discussion of the potential issues related to some of
the physical eﬀects which were not included in the previous chapters.
5.1
Experimental Considerations
The most fundamental requirement for TMM is a generation scheme of TMs. Classically,
it suﬃces to be able to create diﬀerent TMs at distinct locations. Various orders of TMs
can be created using well developed techniques for pulse shaping as for example a 4f-
system [69, 118]. The TMs can subsequently be combined and propagate through a ﬁber
link. Since a speciﬁc set of TMs occupy the same spectral region, they are subject to
the same linear dispersion which greatly simpliﬁes operation. TM selective QFC can be
used to convert only a target mode with high ﬁdelity. The converted mode can then
be ﬁltered out using for example a dichroic mirror. In practice the square-like shape of
the s-channel output Schmidt modes may cause trouble due to their broad oscillatory
spectrum, and thereby limit the eﬃciency of the spectral ﬁltering. It should however
be noted, that smaller frequency shifts (small pulse walk-oﬀ) results in less square-like
output signals as apparent from a comparison between Fig. 3.9 and Fig. 3.10. Thus, a
small frequency shift (∆λ < 30 nm) may still be applicable due the smaller bandwidth
of the output pulse as compared to the bandwidth of the signal output obtained for a
larger frequency shift.

tiplexing (PDM) [29], and diﬀerential quadrature phase shift keying (DQPSK) [119, 120]
are highly developed methods that can be deployed as a unity to obtain unprecedented

the amplitude-phase state of the input signal.
5.1.2
Advantages of Temporal Modes
The main advantage of TMs is their immediate compatibility with existing optical com-
munication networks based on low-loss single-mode ﬁbers (spatial mode). On the other
hand, TMs are not limited to the fundamental spatial mode, and are therefore in prin-
ciple also compatible with higher-order transverse mode ﬁbers. Another feature of the
TMs is that they are stable. They do not, as polarization modes or transverse ﬁber
modes, couple to each other as a result of ﬁber inhomogeneities.
Without proof, we also suggest that deploying TMM may aid in getting closer to
the fundamental limit imposed by Shannon’s theorem [121]. Dense WDM systems are
reaching limits resulting from FWM-induced noise between the closely lying spectral
channels [122, 123]. Indeed as the temporal overlap between TMs of diﬀerent orders
is very small, and the nonlinear optical Kerr response time is extremely short (< 1 fs)
[13], the FWM induced noise between diﬀerent TM channels must be expected to be
negligible.

5.2. DISCUSSION OF ADDITIONAL EFFECTS
71
5.2
Discussion of Additional Eﬀects
We now list and discuss some additional physical eﬀects that are associated with the
QFC process which may to some extend aﬀect the results obtained in the thesis.
5.2.1
Optical Losses
We proposed the QFC to take place in an solid core PCF which, in the case of a 1 ps
pump pulse, was found to yield selectivities exceeding 0.97 for ﬁber lengths above l ≈
30 m. However, the performed analysis excluded optical losses resulting from for example
Rayleigh scattering or non-ideal coupling into and out of the two PCF based QFC stages.
With operation close to the ZDW of λ0 = 736 nm, it is questionable whether the λ−4–
dependence of Rayleigh scattering can be neglected. Simulations have been performed in
order to validate that optical pump losses on the order of 3 dB km−1 (this was arbitrarily
chosen) did not have a detrimental eﬀect on the performance. The selectivity did not
show a noticeable decrease, but the s-channel Schmidt mode became further skewed due
to the decreasing pump power throughout the ﬁber. However, as we did not have the
actual PCF attenuation proﬁle, a more in-depth analysis of the inﬂuence of optical losses
was not performed.
5.2.2
Incomplete Nonlinear Description
The CMEs considered in this thesis take into account the most important physical eﬀects
that inﬂuence the FWM process. In reality there are multiple additional eﬀects which
should be accounted for, in order to get a more complete description of the nonlinear
coupling of the four participating ﬁeld modes. This nonlinear coupling is described by
all the terms in the CMEs including the nonlinear coupling coeﬃcient γ. However, for a
more genuinely correct description, multiple coupling coeﬃcients should be introduced
in order to account for diﬀerent transverse ﬁeld mode overlaps (aﬀecting Aeﬀ) as well
as variation with wavelength [12]. Our model also assumes an instantaneous third-order
nonlinear optical response. This assumption suﬃces when describing the electronic part
of the response, but fails to take into account the molecular response describing the
coupling of light to optical and acoustic phonon modes.
5.2.2.1
Raman Scattering
When light, propagating in an optical medium, interacts with optical phonons the mech-
anism of photon-phonon interaction is called Raman scattering [124]. Thus, as opposed
to the process of FWM which is a photon-photon interaction passively enabled by the
medium, the optical medium plays an active role for Raman scattering. The process
requires energy to be conserved so that ωp = ωs + Ωph for the Stokes process where
photons are scattered from a pump ﬁeld at frequency ωp to a lower frequency Stokes
ﬁeld of frequency ωs while the excess energy goes to the vibrational optical modes of
frequency Ωph. The anti-Stokes case is governed by the reverse process ωp = ωas −Ωph
and requires that the phonon modes are initially populated. The response time of the
nonlinear coupling between the light modes and phonon modes is on the order of the
==PAGE:94==
72
CHAPTER 5. DISCUSSION
phonon lifetime. For silica the response function is often approximated by a damped
harmonic oscillator with a decay time of ∼100 fs [125]. In particular, we are interested
in stimulated Raman scattering (SRS), which is the eﬀect where the presence of Stokes
photons coherently stimulate the Raman scattering process. SRS can result in a large
growth of the Stokes ﬁeld, and consequently to some extent deplete one of our pump
ﬁelds used for FWM. As the eﬀect of SRS scales with length and power [13], we should
be mostly worried about inﬂuence from the strong pump p. SRS can be limited to a
large extend by separating the two pump ﬁelds with a frequency that exceeds the peak
of the Raman gain of ∆ν ≈13.2 THz for silica [13]. Furthermore, if the pump ﬁelds are
placed on the longer wavelength side of the ZDW as compared to the signal ﬁelds, the
eﬀect of cascaded Stokes scattering can not introduce any signal noise.
5.2.2.2
Brillouin Scattering
Brillouin scattering is the nonlinear interaction between photons and acoustic phonons,
and as opposed to Raman scattering which happens in a more or less random direc-
tion, Brillouin scattering is in ﬁbers mainly observed in the backwards direction as a
consequence of the low dispersion slope of acoustic phonons [92]. The frequency shift of
backwards scattered photons is small as compared to the Raman frequency shift and is
on the order of 10 GHz in silica. SBS can be observed at small power levels (< 100 mW)
for narrow bandwidth sources, but the threshold depends critically on parameters such
as ﬁber length, pulse duration, and spectral linewidth. In [93] a detailed analytical anal-
ysis based on square pulses results in a threshold light intensity which in the transient
regime is inversely proportional to the pulse duration squared. Indeed since the Brillouin
scattered light propagates backwards, the possibility for power build up is hugely limited
by the small temporal overlap between pump ﬁeld and scattered ﬁeld. It is therefore
expected that the square pump pulses of 30 W with duration of approximately 200 ps is
only weakly perturbed by SBS over a ﬁber length of 30 m. However, had we not changed
pump p from being a CW ﬁeld to a pulse, the eﬀect of SBS would without a doubt be
detrimental.
5.2.3
Polarization Eﬀects
Throughout this thesis we have assumed that the participating ﬁelds were co-polarized
along some well-deﬁned axis, in the entire ﬁber. In practice, even the best fabricated
isotropic optical ﬁber suﬀers from small birefringence due to asymmetric strain, bends,
and general inhomogeneities. For this reason, the polarization state of an optical ﬁeld
varies somewhat randomly throughout propagation, and sadly the eﬀect is not indepen-
dent of frequency in which case the four ﬁeld modes could have undergone the same
random variation. Since the nonlinear coupling between two optical modes depend on
the polarization state of the modes [13], polarization ﬂuctuations would induce statis-
tical noise to the QFC process.
This issue can be circumvented using either of two
methods: (i) If the polarization state varies quickly enough, one may observe an eﬀective
(reduced) nonlinearity which could replace γ in the CMEs. (ii) One can make use of
a polarization maintaining ﬁber, that has a built-in strong birefringence. Polarization
maintaining ﬁbers exhibiting strong birefringence has been reported for PCFs by advent
of a azimuthal variation in the airhole structure design [126, 127].
==PAGE:95==
CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
In asymmetrically-pumped Bragg scattering, the interaction between four (optical) ﬁelds
is mainly governed by one strong and broad pump, which, due to the optical Kerr eﬀect,
induces a modulation in the eﬀective refractive index of the medium. A secondary pump
is pulsed and assumed to have a small peak power, justifying the initial neglegtion of
nonlinear phase modulation. The Bragg scattering process allows, under phase-matched
conditions, the two pumps to interact with a much weaker signal ﬁeld.
During the
interaction, the weak signal is frequency converted, while photons are simultaneously
transferred from one pump to the other to obey energy conservation. This frequency
conversion process is highly dependent on the temporal shape of the signal ﬁeld, the
pulsed pump ﬁeld, and the parameters of the optical ﬁber passively enabling the interac-
tion. In fact, some signal shapes might be fully converted in the process, whereas other
signal shapes remain fully unconverted. The process is therefore said to be extremely
shape selective, and has for this reason been suggested to form the basis of a device
that can demultiplex a set of temporal modes — a temporal mode sorter. To investigate
the demultiplexing ability of asymmetrically-pumped Bragg scattering, we have for the
ﬁrst time provided the full set of analytical Green functions that describe the frequency
conversion process. The Green functions, depending only on the pump shapes and the
ﬁber parameters, allow us to transfer any set of input signal amplitudes to the corre-
sponding output signal amplitudes. In particular, the Schmidt decomposition was used
to partition the Green functions into the natural input-output temporal modes, with
corresponding weights that could be directly interpreted as the corresponding conversion
eﬃciency of the corresponding mode. The assessment was performed using the “selectiv-
ity” deﬁned as the square of the target mode conversion eﬃciency divided by the sum
of all the mode conversion eﬃciencies. For a Gaussian pulsed pump, the selectivity was
found to be inherently limited to 0.83. As this number would result in 100 % conversion
of the target temporal mode, but 25 % conversion of non-target modes, it rendered the
temporal-mode sorting ineﬀective, especially in cascaded operations.
As single-stage asymmetrically-pumped Bragg scattering resulted in inadequate tem-
poral mode sorting, the possibility of a dual-stage setup was investigated. The so called
“temporal-mode interferometry” resembles a Mach-Zehnder interferometer with the two
beam splitters replaced by two stages of frequency conversion, and the two possible
paths substituted by two diﬀerent signal wavelength channels. In fact, the mathematical
73
==PAGE:96==
74
CHAPTER 6. CONCLUSION
description of frequency conversion was shown to be analogues to an active frequency
shifting beam splitter for each of the temporal modes. Therefore, rather than maxi-
mizing the conversion eﬃciency of the target temporal mode in each stage, which was
known to impact also the higher-order non-target temporal modes, the two stages were
tuned to 50:50 for the target mode. As it was already known from the single-stage setup,
it was possible to reach 50 % conversion of the target mode while not converting any
higher-order mode with extremely good ﬁdelity (< 1 % conversion). Consequently, by
adding the eﬀect of two stages, almost ideal behavior with selectivies in excess of 0.98
was reachable. In order to achieve this, it was necessary to temporal match the two
stages by either retarding the faster propagating signal between the stages or by invert-
ing the dispersion proﬁle of the second-stage ﬁber. Whereas the former of these methods
stands out by being much simpler to realize, the second method results in slightly better
selectivies (diﬀerences of 0 −0.05) depending on the setup parameters.
The setup using asymmetrically-pumped Bragg scattering excels by resulting in sim-
ple linear phase shifts on the temporal modes — equivalent to simple frequency shifts.
This frequency shift is even possible to avoid by instead pre-frequency shifting the pumps
prior to each stage by the corresponding amount. In fact, if this pump frequency shift is
not performed, the temporal phase proﬁles of the signals do not result in matching of the
two frequency conversion stages, and consequently no coherent addition of energy is seen
in the second stage. However, the simple linear phase shifts are based on the assumption
that nonlinear phase modulation from the weaker pulsed pump can be neglected. To
investigate for what situations this assumption is justiﬁable, a numerical model based
on the split-step Fourier method together with an embedded Runge-Kutta-4, was used
to include the eﬀects of intra-pulse dispersion and nonlinear phase modulation from the
weaker pulsed pump. To limit the inﬂuence of stimulated Brillouin scattering on the
broad and strong pump, it was, instead of being a continuous-wave ﬁeld, modelled as
a square-like pulse that completely enveloped the remaining three optical ﬁeld modes
throughout both ﬁber segments. In order to assess the temporal-mode sorting ability, a
method to obtain the singular value decomposition of the Green function was presented.
This directly allowed us to ﬁnd the selectivity for a wide range of diﬀerent values of pump
powers, ﬁber lengths, and signal frequency shifts. In order to reach the required interac-
tion strength while simultaneously reducing the eﬀect of the nonlinear phase modulation
from the pulsed pump, the broader pump was compelled to have a peak power around
30 W at single stage ﬁber lengths of approximately 30 m. Using these parameters for
a small-core photonic crystal ﬁber, selectivities in the excess of 0.97 was still reachable
even after including nonlinear phase modulation from the shorter pulsed pump.
Thus, in conclusion, we have in this thesis laid the theoretical groundwork needed
to estimate the required pump powers, pump widths, signal shapes, ﬁber designs, as
well as further initiatives that would be needed in order to perform a proof-of-concept
demonstration of the demultiplexing of a set of temporal modes using four-wave mixing
Bragg scattering.
