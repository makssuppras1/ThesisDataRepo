Abstract
This thesis investigates virtual roomยญacoustic rendering for XR, with a focus on how much
geometric accuracy is neededโgiven only a singleยญpoint acoustic measurementโto achieve
sufficiently accurate room acoustics across multiple listener positions.
Leveraging Meta Quest 3โs onยญdevice room scan, we tried to build an endยญtoยญend pipeline
that can both acquire an approximate geometric model within minutes and perform a sinยญ
gle acoustic capture fully on the headset, which was not possible for technical reasons.
With an alternative equipment ARRAI, we conducted the measurement campaign across
16 various acoustic environments.
Utilizing the measured data, we constructed simulated scenes using two classic geometric
acoustic simulation โimage source method and ray tracing, under three levels of geoยญ
metric simplification. By a simple automatic iterative optimization, we tuned the model to
an adequate level of accuracy based on reverberation time. With this tuned simulation,
we can get the impulse responses across different rooms and simulation approaches.
After error estimation, we found that with this simulator, reverberation can be accurately
simulated without precise dimensional data. Whereas the detailed geometry with rayยญ
tracing should be applied if a precise reconstruction of an irregular roomโs acoustic feaยญ
tures is needed. Additionally, we identify several limitations in the audio rendering engine,
including nonยญphysical impulse response morphologies, random oscillations of data, and
insufficient spectral resolution across frequency bands.

Introduction
In recent years, the rapid development of Extended Reality (XR) technologies may give
many more people access to immersive content that includes virtual acoustic simulations.
XR is an umbrella term encompassing immersive technologies such as Virtual Reality
(VR), Augmented Reality (AR), and Mixed Reality (MR), all of which integrate digital and
physical worlds to varying extents. XR applications range from virtual assistants that
anchor sound to realยญworld surfaces to immersive games that adapt to oneโs surroundings.
Realistic acoustics play a crucial role in such immersive environments.
Perfectly reproducing an acoustic scene has been a longstanding objective in virtual
acoustics research. Softwareยญbased approaches could attempt to blindly estimate a room
impulse response (RIR) between a source and the receiver. Alternatively, one promising
direction lies in combining rapid geometry acquisition with singleยญpoint acoustic measureยญ
ments, which could enable dynamic, sixยญdegreeยญofยญfreedom (6ยญDoF) spatial audio with
minimal setup effort in theory. If conducting such a singleยญpoint measurement could be
done entirely with the headset, without additional equipment, users may decide to do it
for an ideal experience.
The release of consumerยญgrade hardware like Meta Quest 3 and an improved version
of 3D room scan in Meta Quest build 59.0 [1], allows users to acquire an approximate
geometric model of their environment with just a twoยญminute scan instead of a traditional
measureยญandยญsketch of the room. This provides a unique opportunity for capturing and
simulating room acoustics at scale. Rather than relying on laborious, multiยญpoint impulse
response measurements or manually creating a model for acoustic simulation, it seems
promising to harness the headsetโs approximate geometric model and a single measureยญ
ments to inform the audio renderer. But thereโs chance that the real situation is simpler,
that in some cases, even a simplified shoebox room model may suffice to achieve perยญ
ceptually plausible results.
When implemented with a stateยญofยญtheยญart audio renderer such as the Meta Audio XR
SDK[2], such an approach would seamlessly align visual and auditory cues, reduce caliยญ
bration effort, and enable more feasible, contextยญaware listening experiences in XR. This
thesis examines the influence of room geometry on simulation accuracy when only a
singleยญpoint acoustic measurement is available. Specifically, it addresses the following
research question:
โGiven only a singleยญpoint acoustic measurement, how accurate should the roomโs geoยญ
metric model be to achieve acceptable room acoustic simulation qualityโ
As a secondary goal, it is also interesting to find out the suitability of the Meta Audio SDK
for XRยญrelated acoustics research.

2.1
Fundamentals of Room Acoustics
Room acoustics deals with the behavior of sound in enclosed spaces, encompassing
sound generation, propagation, absorption, reflection, and diffusion. This section reviews
the fundamental theories and key parameters of room acoustics. Unless otherwise stated,
the definitions and explanations of the relevant concepts are primarily based on Kuttruffโs
โRoom Acousticsโ [3]. These core concepts are essential to discuss when modeling or
measuring a roomโs acoustic features in this project.
2.1.1
Impulse Response (IR) and its Measurements
The impulse response h(t) of a room is the acoustic output recorded at a receiver locaยญ
tion when an ideal impulse (Dirac delta function) is emitted from a sound source. It fully
characterizes the linear timeยญinvariant properties of the space by capturing direct sound,
reflections, and reverberation over time. Room impulse responses enable auralization of
any signal through convolution with h(t).
In practical measurements, obtaining the room impulse response typically involves the
following steps:
Excitation Signal: The excitation signal is emitted from the loudspeaker and used to
activate the acoustics characteristics of the room. The details and selection of it will be
discussed in section 3.1.
Playback and Recording: While the capture of acoustic responses at receiver locations
may appear straightforward in theory, in practical measurements, there are more to conยญ
sider including the selection of hardware and software parameters, relevant to the specific
application scenario.
For example, according to the ISO 3382 [4], one has to pay attention to the directivity
of the sources, diameters of the microphones, numbers of sourceยญmicrophone combinaยญ
tions, positions in the room, distance to the walls, etc., if they want to fulfill a specific
measurements purpose. The detailed measurements procedures and setups used in this
paper are discussed in chapter 3.
Deconvolution: The recorded signal is processed using deconvolution techniques (such
as inverse filtering or crossยญcorrelation methods) to extract the impulse response of the
room from the measured data.
Analysis and Processing: The resulting impulse response can then be analyzed in both
time and frequency domains to study acoustic properties, and the definitions of several
important attributes will be explained next.

2.1.3
Reverberation Time (RT) & Early Decay Time (EDT)
Reverberation time, commonly denoted as RT or T60, is defined as the time required for
the sound level to decay by 60 dB after the source has stopped. It directly characterizes
the persistence of sound in a space and strongly influences speech intelligibility, musical
clarity, and listener comfort. Within the context of XR, the representation of RT is crucial
for achieving auditory plausibility [5] and immersion. Consequently, RT is selected as
a key objective metric in this study to evaluate the performance of different geometrical
models in the acoustic simulator.
Assuming that the decay is linear on a logarithmic scale, it is sufficient to measure a drop
of 20 dB and multiply the time by 3, or a drop of 30 dB and multiply the time by 2. This
method is often used in practice, because in real measurements, it might be hard to get
an SNR of 60 dB. The resulting metrics are then called RT20 and RT30. Early decay time,
as a similar concept, refers to the decay time extrapolated from the interval [0, ยญ10] dB in
EDC. The auditory system is highly sensitive to the instantaneous changes in sound (i.e.,
the onset phase) [6], thus the discussion on EDT is also essential.

2.2.2
RayยญTracing Methods
Ray tracing models defined as straightยญline rays emitted from the sourceอพ unlike the imageยญ
source method (ISM), which enumerates all specular reflection paths exactly, ray tracing
stochastically samples many plausible reflection paths.
The core principle is to emit rays from the source according to a prescribed angular disยญ
tribution or in random directions, optionally weighted by the source directivity. Each time
a ray hits a surface, it is reflected and the tracing continues. Intersections with receivers
are recorded, but rays are not terminated at the receiverโthey pass through it. Each
ray stores bandยญlimited energy and is attenuated at reflections according to the bandยญ
dependent absorption of the encountered surfaces. Rays are terminated when their enยญ
ergy falls below a threshold or when a maximum propagation distance is reached. As an
alternative, one can employ Russian Roulette: at each hit, the ray is terminated with a
probability equal to the absorption coefficient. This approach is simple and does not reยญ
quire explicit perยญband energy bookkeeping, but it is less suitable for simultaneous multiยญ
band simulations.
Receivers in ray tracing must have a finite volume, as the probability of a ray intersecting
a mathematical point would be zero. Weighting by the path length traversed within the
receiver volume can improve accuracy.
A key distinction from ISM is that ray tracing natively accommodates diffuse reflections
rather than only perfectly specular ones. At each impact, one may spawn a specular
ray together with multiple diffusely reflected rays, which can improve fidelity but leads
to rapid growth in ray count. A lighter alternative is a singleยญray scheme with stochasยญ
tic scattering: at each hit, the outgoing direction switches to a random diffuse direction
with probability equal to the scattering coefficientอพ otherwise, it follows the specular law.
Another difference from ISM is coverage of geometry and physics: instead of assuming
an ideal shoebox room, ray tracing supports arbitrary geometries, material absorption,
scattering/diffusion, and edge effects.
Ray tracing also has limitations. With too few rays, results exhibit statistical fluctuationsอพ
the number of rays required depends on geometry and materials and is not known a priยญ
oriอพ volumetric receivers may inadvertently register invalid or duplicate paths if not handled
carefullyอพ ray splitting can be computationally expensiveอพ diffraction is typically approxiยญ
mated via scattering and edge heuristics rather than by fullยญwave modeling.
In summary, when exact specular arrival times and levels are crucial in simple or rectยญ
angular rooms, ISM is well suited for early reflections. When realistic late reverberation,
scattering, and complex geometry are required, ray tracing is commonly preferred, with
accuracy controlled by the number of rays and modeling choices.

3.1

3.4.2
Matched Volume Shoebox Model
In this method, the three dimensions of each shoebox has to be determined based on
the volume information of the corresponding room. Given the moderate number of rooms
involved, we set these dimensions manually. The general procedure is as follows:
For rooms with a shoeboxยญlike shape, the lengths of two side lengths of the room are
used as two dimensions of the shoebox. Usually the room height is often taken as one
of these, because it is typically straightforward to obtain and consistent across the whole
room. The third dimension is then derived using the actual room volume.
For highly irregular rooms, such as the one shown in Figure 3.7b, a simplified approach
is adopted: the height of the room model is used directly as the shoebox height, and the
remaining two dimensions are set equal, resulting in a square floor plan.
After defining the shoebox dimensions, we calibrate its translation and rotation to align the
shoebox center with that of the actual room geometry, while ensuring that all measurement
points lie within the simulated shoebox.

Limitations on Meta XR Audio SDK engines
Although the Meta XR Audio SDK is one of the cuttingยญedge XR audio rendering engines,
it still has certain limitations as a numerical simulation tool, bringing unreal shape of genยญ
erated IRs or results that do not conform to physics common sense. Here, we present a
few abnormal phenomena during the simulation process that we observed.
4.4.1
Nonยญsmooth Connection in IR at High Frequencies
Figure 4.25, Figure 4.26, and Figure 4.27 display simulated impulse responses and their
corresponding EDCs across low, mid, and high frequency octave bands, respectively. It
can be observed that at lower frequencies, the impulse response closely resembles physยญ
ically measured impulse responses with similar decay patterns. However, in higher freยญ
quency bands, a discontinuity emerges between early reflections and late reverberation.
This specific EDC pattern typically occurs only in exceptionally large architectural spaces
with prominent obstructions near the receiver position. The phenomenon is inconsistent
with our simulation conditions, as all rooms represented relatively small, unobstructed
rooms where such acoustic behavior would not be expected. This proves the limitation in
the rendering engineโs highยญfrequency reverberation modeling.

Discussions & Conclusions
5.1
Limitations of Meta Audio XR SDK
Figure 5.1 shows a temporal envelope gap between the onset of the IR and the emerยญ
gence of the late reverberation. The same pattern is observed across all three simulation
methods. We refer to this gap as a transition interval: in typical geometrical room simยญ
ulations, early reflections and late reverb are generated separately and then connected
together by fading out the early reflections and fading in the reverberant tail to avoid overยญ
lap [28]. The effect is more pronounced at higher frequencies, where early reflections
are shorter. Perceptually, this transition may be unobtrusiveอพ however, it affects metrics
derived from the EDC. During the transition interval, the EDC often exhibits an approxiยญ
mately flat segment, which biases the linear regression used to estimate RT, especially
at high frequencies.
Figure 5.1: Unity generated IR zoomed in the beginning filtered at 8kHz octave band
Given that the Meta XR Audio SDK is not open source, we can only hypothesize about
its internal design, including the interยญfrequencyยญband coupling relations. Along with the
random data oscillations, these limitations make it tricky to calculate the simulated RT, to
optimize it to a certain value and to reproduce exactly the acoustic environment that has
been tuned.
5.2
Error Evaluation
In an ideal scenario, the tuned reverberation time at the reference position would perยญ
fectly match the real measured values. However, inherent limitations of the software,
compounded by its closedยญsource nature, prevent the optimization process from achieving
perfect alignment. Despite these constraints, most tuning errors remain within acceptable
boundsโeither below 10% relative error or 0.1 s absolute errorโwith the exception of a
few specific rooms that fail to converge even after a large number of iterations.
Across the twelve rooms, a notable finding is that the shoebox simulation for the most irยญ
regular geometry still yields comparatively small errorsโabout 10% to 40% per frequency
band. For shoeboxยญlike rooms or arni, itโs better to use fixed shoebox rooms as the deยญ
tailed geometry doesnโt provide a significant improvement.

Across simulation methods, contrary to common sense, the Meta Audio XR SDK shows
little performance difference between the fixedยญsize and matchedยญvolume shoebox modยญ

Across frequency, performance is generally best in the mid band, whereas low and high

5.3
Summary
This study investigates the integration of room models acquired via the Quest platform
with geometryยญbased acoustic simulation, with a focus on evaluating the performance of
scanned models under varying levels of utilization. To support this goal, we developed a
comprehensive measurement system based on the headset to capture impulse responses
with ARRAI, positional data, and room models from 10 measurement points across 16
distinct acoustic environments. Using these data, we conducted acoustic simulations via
the Meta Audio XR SDK in three different approaches, resulting in a total of 48 modeled
acoustic scenes. We further established an automated and semiยญautomated framework
to optimize the sound absorption coefficients input for these simulations.
Although the closedยญsource nature of the Meta Audio XR SDK limits insight into its internal
logic, our results indicate that: in most cases, ISM based on a fixed shoebox that do not
incorporate real room geometry show little difference from those using volumeยญmatched
shoebox approximations. However, for irregularly shaped rooms, the use of real geomยญ
etry combined with rayยญtracing yields significantly better results, albeit at increased comยญ
putational cost. Additional findings include insights into the underlying theory and certain
limitations of the IR generation process in the Meta Audio XR SDK.
There are several directions remain open for future work, which includes: (1) more adยญ
vanced acoustic parameter optimization strategiesโcurrently, we employed a simple feedยญ
back network, but methods such as [29] could be integratedอพ (2) expansion to a larger
set of measured roomsโwhile 12 rooms sufficed for this study, a broader dataset would
strengthen generalizabilityอพ and (3) further automation of the workflow. Currently, scene
creation and data import into Unity require manual intervention. A fully automated pipelineโ
from room scanning and measurement with Quest to optimized acoustic simulation in
Unityโrepresents a promising direction for future development.
