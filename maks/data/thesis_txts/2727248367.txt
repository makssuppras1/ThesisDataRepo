Abstract
Be18 is a calculation software used to assess the overall energy performance of buildings
in accordance with the Danish Building Regulations. Based on EN ISO 13790:2008, Be18
currently calculates the energy needs for heating and cooling of buildings for a monthly
time interval. With the new hourly method proposed by EN ISO 52016­1 in 2017, the
choice for an hourly time calculation interval appears as an improved procedure to its
predecessor. This thesis presents the analysis, adaptation, and implementation of the
hourly method to calculate the energy needs involved in space heating and cooling of
buildings, for the application within the context of the Danish regulations. Three building
cases were analysed to evaluate the differences in heating and cooling by comparing
results calculated based on both the monthly and hourly methods. For the residential
cases, the hourly calculation estimated lower energy needs for heating and higher energy
needs for cooling than the monthly quasi­steady­state method in Be18. An opposite trend
was noticed for the office building case, with higher differences between the two assessed
methodologies. Differences in the time­step length and in how both models consider
the solar radiation through opaque constructions, the long­wave heat exchange with the
external environment, the heat capacity, and the total solar heat gains into the thermal
zone, were identified as the possible explanation for such deviations. Further studies will
need to be undertaken for future practice.

Introduction
This chapter briefly introduces the case of energy in the built environment and the rele­
vance of building codes and regulations as a key driver for mandating construction and en­
ergy efficiency measures. The main objectives and expected contributions of the project
are also presented. Finally, an overview of the structure of the thesis is provided.
1.1
Context and Motivation
The use of energy in the construction and operation of buildings is one of the key drivers
to achieving the green transition. In 2021, worldwide energy demand in buildings reached
135 exajoules (EJ), representing 30% of the global final energy consumption. In addition,
the buildings sector is responsible for around one­third of the global energy­ and process­
related carbon dioxide (CO2) emissions. About 8% of global energy­related and process­
related CO2 emissions resulted from the use of fossil fuels in buildings in 2021, with
another 19% from the generation of electricity and heat used in buildings [1].
At a regional level, buildings account for 40% of the energy consumed and 36% of the
energy­related greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions in the European Union (EU) [2]. There­
fore, improving the energy efficiency of constructions is crucial for cutting down on emis­
sions, tackling energy poverty, reducing people’s vulnerability to energy prices, supporting
job creation, and contributing to the achievement of the EU’s energy and environmental
goals.
New technologies for space heating and cooling, hot water production, lighting, and re­
newable energy systems contribute to more efficient energy use. It is, however, the im­
provement of buildings envelope the main driver of a reduction in the energy demand,
denoting how relevant building codes and regulations are for governments to mandate
constructions and introduce energy efficiency requirements [3].
Denmark has been noted as a country with an ‘impressive progress in decarbonizing its
energy sector’ [4] and also referred to as the country with the ‘strictest building regulations
in the world’ [5]. The first Danish Building Regulations (BR) were established during the
1960s with the introduction of insulation requirements for building envelopes. In 1977,
energy performance requirements were introduced, but only for newly built constructions
[6]. It was with the BR of the year 2006 that requirements became extensive to all buildings
­i.e. newly built and already existing ones, as an implementation of the EU Directive
2002/91/EC, namely Energy Performance Building Directive (EPBD) [7]. From then on,
and over the past four decades, BR have been increasing stringency and positioning
Denmark among one of the first countries to formulate the requirements for Nearly Zero­
Energy Buildings (NZEB) [6] [8].
The 2018 BR currently set the energy framework for all types of buildings. The limit is
mainly set in terms of the maximum allowed primary energy demand for space heating
and cooling, domestic hot water (DHW), and lighting (for non­residential buildings). As­
pects such as thermal bridges, solar gains, shading, infiltration, ventilation, heat recovery,
the efficiency of systems, and electricity for operating the building are included [9]. The en­
ergy frame also considers the contribution of renewable energies, the air tightness of the
building, the design transmission loss, and general minimum requirements for the build­
ing envelope. Compliance with the energy framework is, therefore, performed through

the tool Be18 [10], based on the monthly time interval calculation procedure proposed by
EN ISO 13790:2008 [11].
Nevertheless, with the revised 2018 EPBD [12], which requires EU Member States to
describe their national calculation methodology following the annexes of the standard
family EN ISO 52000, and the new specific hourly method proposed by EN ISO 52016­1
[13], the choice for an hourly time calculation interval appears as an improved procedure
to its predecessor.
The need for an hourly calculation procedure
Van Dijk [14] points out many of the advantages of choosing an hourly calculation method:
• ‘The thermal balance in buildings changes dramatically compared to the past: nowa­
days the solar and internal gains have a relatively much larger influence on the en­
ergy needs for heating and cooling.’ These dynamic effects are taken into account
more precisely with an hourly interval calculation; whereas the monthly method uti­
lizes adjustment factors that make it less accurate.
• A better picture of the heating and cooling needs in the same month can be deter­
mined. In contrast, months with both heating and cooling cannot be predicted with
the monthly calculation procedure. Therefore, results for transition months may not
be accurate, as only a monthly heating or cooling load can be determined, based
on the ratio between the energy needs for both modes.
• The influence of hourly and daily variations in weather and the operations of dynamic
technologies, such as solar shading, adaptive facades, free cooling, etc is better
estimated with an hourly time step. Correlation factors contained in voluminous
tables are needed to account for these influences in the monthly method.
• Transparency and robustness are no longer lost. Due to no need for adjustment
factors, the hourly calculation method becomes applicable to a wider range of cases
and remains more reproducible and verifiable.
Furthermore, the existence of an energy performance gap (EPG) has been proven by
many researchers. That is, ‘buildings’ expected (projected, simulated) energy use fre­
quently does not match actual observations’ [15]. A study performed for approximately
135,000 detached houses in Denmark with energy performance certificates (EPC) issued
between 2006 and 2014 shows that, despite some uncertainties, there is no coincidence
between theoretical and actual consumption [16]. Although this deviation can be ex­
plained by different user behavioral patterns and varied social and demographic back­
ground of the buildings’ occupants, in residential buildings at least, still around 50% of
the variance in heating consumption is due to the characteristics of the building itself and
other physical parameters [17].
In addition, buildings’ simulated energy use is of particular concern for life cycle assess­
ment (LCA) calculations. An analysis of 60 Danish building cases found that the climate
impacts from building operations vary from 0.3 to 4.9 kg CO2­eq/m²/year. For some build­
ings, these values go up to 16.7 times greater than others. Results are based on the
energy framework calculations, not on the actual energy consumption, which is typically
higher than the estimated in theoretical predictions [18]. Hence, the hourly calculation
method proposed by EN ISO 52016­1:2007 offers an improved alternative to predict build­
ings’ energy use, their interaction with the environment and modern dynamic technologies,
and the influence of occupants, which could help to narrow down the EPG, estimate the

energy policy to mitigate climate change and the transition to a low­carbon economy.
1.2
Objectives
The main objective of the project was to analyze, adapt and implement the hourly method
proposed by EN ISO 52016­1:2007 to calculate the energy needs involved in space heat­
ing and cooling of buildings and for the internal temperatures, for the application within the
context of the Danish regulations. For this, a programming model in Python to perform
the calculations was developed.
Following a validation of the programming model, the next aim was to evaluate the in­
fluence of hourly and daily variations in weather, the operation of the building, and their
dynamic interactions for heating and cooling by comparing the results of selected cases
calculated based on both with hourly and monthly method ­the latter, through the use of
Be18.
Likewise, the project intended to assess not only accuracy, but also transparency, ro­
bustness, and applicability of the hourly method, considering that increased complexity
should be kept at a minimum for potential implementation in the Danish BR. For a success­
ful assessment approach to work as a policy instrument to reduce energy consumption
in buildings, a balance between reproducibility, accuracy, level of expertise, and costs
needs to be found.
Finally, whether a shift to an hourly method is found to be worth the effort or not, it was
expected that the programming model could serve as a starting point for future improve­
ments of Be18’s current calculation core as, for instance, a validation of the correction and
correlation factors of the monthly method can be derived from the output of the hourly time
interval calculation procedure.
1.3
Thesis Overview
The overall structure of the present report takes the form of six chapters.
• Chapter 1 sets the context and motivation of the project, together with the objectives
and the overview of the thesis.
• Chapter 2 provides a review of the relevant literature investigating the monthly and
hourly calculation methods to assess the energy performance of buildings. Addi­
tionally, an insight of the calculation procedures in both EN ISO 13790:2008 and
EN ISO 52016­1:2017 is given, which serves as the conceptual framework for the
proposed methodology following next.
• Chapter 3 exposes the methodology and a brief description of the programming
model implemented.
• Chapter 4 presents the selection of study cases utilized for the validation of the
programming model and the assessment of the hourly calculation procedure.
• Chapter 5 explores the results and introduces the discussion according to figures
and assumptions, and to the positioning of the present work in the Danish context.
• Chapter 6 concludes with the main findings of the work, recommendations, and
future applications of the obtained results.

2
Literature Review
This chapter provides first, a review of the literature and research works done to inves­
tigate the monthly and hourly calculation methods to assess the energy performance of
buildings. Additionally, an insight of the calculation procedures in both EN ISO 13790:2008
and EN ISO 52016­1:2017 is given, which serves as the conceptual framework for the
proposed methodology following next.
2.1
Related Work
The EPG between predicted energy use and real consumption has been attempted to
be explained by numerous studies. It is interesting to note the findings of Mahdavi et
al. [15], who performed a systematic literature search to review the topic. Even though
the research was mainly focused on trying to identify the role of occupants in regard to
building energy simulations, the authors suggest: ‘there is no conclusive and sufficient
empirical evidence supporting the claim that occupants’ behavior is responsible for the
bulk of building­related EPGs’. Therefore, many other factors, such as uncertainties about
the building geometry or systems, and weather conditions, still remain contributors to the
observed discrepancies.
To try to deal with these uncertainties, Petersen [19] developed BeREAL, an add­on tool to
Be18 which allows minimum and maximum input values for the most critical parameters in
trying to assess the overall energy performance of buildings, offering output results within
a confidence interval. Although the tool’s final report [20] recognizes that neither Be18
nor BeREAL are intended to be energy consumption forecasting tools, they are probably
the only energy calculations performed during building design for most of the cases. In
the present work, BeREAL was not tested, as the link to access the tool on the web could
not be reached then. Nevertheless, several engineering consulting companies reported
achieving a more realistic picture of energy consumption in new buildings when using
BeREAL [21].
When it comes specifically to EN ISO 52016­1:2007, to date, the existing academic lit­
erature concerning the application of the calculation procedures described in it is not ex­
tensive. In 2016, Van Dijk et al. [22] pointed out the differences between the hourly
and monthly calculation methods and also remarked on the improved characteristics of
the newly proposed hourly interval procedure, compared to the previous one suggested
in EN ISO 13790:2008. By means of several test cases from the International Energy
Agency Building Energy Simulation Test (BESTEST) series [23], they successfully vali­
dated the calculation method and concluded that it is more powerful than its predecessor,
requiring no significant additional data input in comparison to the monthly method.
A number of authors have assessed the application of EN ISO 52016­1:2007 in Italy. Bal­
larini et al. [24] compared the hourly calculation method in EN ISO 52016­1:2007 with
both the hourly model of EN ISO 13790:2008 and the dynamic simulation software En­
ergyPlus by assessing a single­family house case located in Torino. Both methods were
found to be reliable, with EN ISO 52016­1 showing more similar results to the detailed
dynamic simulations. The authors suggest that the main deviations occurred due to the
differences in modelling the heat capacity of the building and, consequently, the thermal
time constant. In a similar study, Cirrincione et al. [25] analysed the energy behaviour

of a public building in Sicily by using EN ISO 52016­1:2017, EN ISO 13790:2008 and
EnergyPlus. Even though the EN ISO 52016­1 model seemed to be consistent with the
EnergyPlus results in regard to the heating loads, the cooling loads were found to be sig­
nificantly overestimated. The authors argue that such overestimation follows the way the
internal solar gains and the radiative and convective components are calculated in the
model. In addition, they point out that the way the HVAC system is modelled is another
source of discrepancy. The authors observe: ‘In the case of the EN ISO 13790 model
the set point temperatures are compared with the air temperatures returned by the model,
while in the EN ISO 52016 model, this comparison is made with the operative tempera­
tures. After all, the current sensors that enable the HVAC on­off regulation are all driven
by the air temperature and not by the operative one’.
In a sensitivity analysis of the thermal energy need of a residential building in Rome as­
sessed by means of EN ISO 52016­1 simplified dynamic model [26], Bianco Mauthe
Degerfeld et al. studied the impact of changes in the occupancy density, mass distri­
bution in external walls, indoor operative temperature set­points and solar transmittance
of windows of the building. The results claim that the operative temperature set­point
is absolutely determinant for both heating and cooling, followed by the occupancy den­
sity, particularly during the cooling period. Changes in the mass distribution of building
components do not introduce significant variation of the outputs, which leads the authors
to suggest two possible reasons: either the EN ISO 52016­1 model is too simplified to
describe the phenomenon, or the study case characteristics flatten the differences.
Likewise, two other follow­up studies investigated the application of the Italian National
Annex (NA) to EN ISO 52016­1 [27], aimed at improving the simplified hourly calcula­
tion method by introducing alternative modelling options of the heat conduction, the extra
thermal radiation to the sky, the shortwave radiation heat transfer, and the coupling of
thermally conditioned zones. In the first study, Bianco Mauthe Degerfeld et al. [28] com­
pared the results of the analysis of an office building in Northern Italy calculated with the
hourly method of the EN ISO 52016­1 standard procedure and the Italian Annex. The im­
provements proposed by the NA were applied one by one, and all at once. The findings
indicate that the improved models show considerable variations in the energy needs for
heating, regardless of the modelling alternatives being applied separately or all at once.
In terms of cooling, only the use of solar angle and time­dependent correction factors for
the total solar energy transmittance of glazing proved instead to be influencing the most.
The second work, performed by De Luca et al. [29], also attempted to investigate the
effects of the Italian NA but in this case, on a residential building archetype. The outputs
were compared to dynamic simulations in EnergyPlus. The most influencing factor was
found to be the modelling of solar gains through windows, complementing these results
to the outcomes of the first study.
Domenico et al. [30] contributed with another significant research by studying the applica­
tion of the hourly dynamic method of EN ISO 52016­1 for three building models comply­
ing with the NZEB requirements ­a detached house, a multi­family building, and an office
building, within the Italian context. The results were compared to the ones obtained by the
current monthly calculation method, specified by UNI/TS 11300­2:2019 and based on EN
ISO 13790:2008. For both residential building cases, considerable relative differences
between both methods were found. Energy needs for heating differed from 10 to 40%,

In the context of the project Enhancing storage integration in buildings with photovoltaics

effect of heat transfer through walls and windows was also studied. For glazed elements,

2.2
EN ISO 13790:2008
The EN ISO 13790:2008 was developed to support the implementation of the EPBD, and
it introduces the calculation methods for the assessment of annual energy use for the
space heating and cooling of buildings [11]. An overview of the procedures described
there are exposed in this section, as they base the calculation of the heating and cooling
demands specified in the SBi­instruction 213 [33], which Be18 uses.
The EN ISO 13790:2008 introduces three types of calculation procedures: (1) a monthly
quasi­steady­state methodology, (2) a simplified hourly dynamic methodology, and (3)
calculation guidelines for detailed dynamic simulations. Only the first two are addressed
below.
2.2.1
Monthly quasi­steady­state method
The essence of the quasi­steady­state method is the utilization of correlation factors to
account for the effects of dynamic interactions in the building. In this way, the energy
needs for space heating of the building and for each calculation step (in this case, a
month) are calculated as:
QH = QH,ht −ηH,gnQH,gn
(2.1)
where:
QH total energy needs for space heating for a given heating month, in kWh;
QH,ht total heat transfer for the same considered period, in kWh;
QH,gn total heat gains for the same considered period, in kWh;
ηH,gn dimensionless gain utilization factor.
The energy balance is, therefore, affected by ηH,gn, which takes into account the fact that
only part of the internal and solar heat gains is used to decrease the needs for heating.
Likewise, a utilization factor for losses ηC,ls is introduced when calculating the cooling
needs QC to account for the non­used transmission and ventilation heat transfer:
QC = QC,gn −ηC,lsQC,ht
(2.2)
where:
QC total energy needs for space cooling for a given cooling month, in kWh;
QC,gn total heat gains for the same considered period, in kWh;
QC,ht total heat transfer for the same considered period, in kWh;

The adjustment factors in this method, plus the fact that months with both heating and
cooling cannot be predicted simultaneously, can introduce large relative errors, especially
in months close to the beginning and end of the heating and cooling seasons.
2.2.2
Simple hourly dynamic method
The dynamic hourly method is based on the three­node resistance­capacitance model
shown in Figure 2.1, which simplifies the heat transfer phenomenon between the internal
and the external environment.
For each hour, the heating and/or cooling energy needs are found by calculating the
heating and/or cooling power ΦHC,nd that needs to be supplied to, or extracted from, the
internal air node θair to maintain a certain temperature set­point. Heat transfer occurs
by ventilation, Hve, and by transmission, split into a window part, Htr,w, and an opaque
part, Htr,op. The former is considered to have a zero thermal mass, whereas the latter
contains the thermal mass split into Htr,ms and Htr,em. The nodes in the system are the
air temperature node θair, the surface temperature node θs, and the node representing
the mass of the building zone, θm. θsup and θe stand for the supply air temperature node
and the outdoor air temperature node, respectively. Solar gains, Φsol, and internal heat
gains, Φint, are distributed amongst θair, θs and θm. The thermal mass is represented
by a single thermal capacity, Cm. A coupling conductance, Htr,is is defined between the
internal air node and the central node. Thus, for a given hour, all values are known except
for ΦHC,nd, which can be calculated.

2.3
EN ISO 52016­1:2017
The EN ISO 52016­1:2017 was introduced with the 2018 revised EPBD, which requires
EU Member States to describe their national calculation methodology following the an­
nexes of the standard family EN ISO 52000, developed under mandate M/480 given to
the European Committee for Standardisation (CEN) [12]. With this, EN ISO 13790:2008
was withdrawn.
The EN ISO 52016­1:2008 is intended to provide integration with other Energy Perfor­
mance Building (EPB) standards, outline a revision of the monthly calculation method and
replace its predecessor simple hourly calculation method with a more direct and transpar­
ent one, with no need for additional input data. Furthermore, it specifies the procedures
for the calculation of moisture and latent energy needs for (de­)humidification.
2.3.1
Monthly calculation procedure
The monthly calculation procedure in EN ISO 52016­1:2017 remains almost unchanged
­i.e. the thermal balance of the building is made up at a monthly time step, where the
dynamic effects are taken into account by correlation factors. However, some differences
are introduced. For the calculation of the energy needs, months are analysed under two
considerations, namely with or without long unoccupied periods. During months without
long unoccupied periods, two situations can arise:
1. QH;nd;ztc;m = 0, or
2. QH;nd;ztc;m = QH;ht;ztc;m −ηH;gn;ztc;mQH;gn;ztc;m.
where:
QH;nd;ztc;m monthly heating energy need, in kWh;
QH;ht;ztc;m total heat transfer for the heating mode, in kWh;
QH;gn;ztc;m total heat gains for the heating mode, in kWh;
ηH;gn;ztc;m dimensionless utilization factor.
Whether one or the other situation applies, depends on the value of the heat­balance ratio
for the heating mode, γH;ztc;m. In situations with long unoccupied periods, QH;nd;ztc;m is
linearly interpolated according to the results of the time fraction with unoccupied periods
versus occupied periods.
Similarly, depending on the value of the dimensionless heat­balance ratio for cooling,
1/γC;ztc;m, the monthly energy need for cooling is:
1. QC;nd;ztc;m = 0, or
2. QC;nd;ztc;m = aC;redztc;m(QC;gn;ztc;m −ηC;ht;ztc;mQC;ht;ztc;m)

2.3.2
Hourly calculation procedure
The hourly calculation method differs from its predecessor in the fact that building ele­
ments are no longer arbitrarily grouped together. Instead, each construction component
­i.e. walls, windows, floors, etc, is modelled as a series of nodes and resistances. Opaque
elements are split into four resistances and five nodes, like the one shown in Figure 2.2.
Windows and doors are modelled as only one resistance and 2 nodes.
Figure 2.2: Equivalent model of a generic opaque envelope component.
Generally speaking, the model of an opaque envelope component has two surface nodes
­external, θ1, and internal, θ5, and three inner nodes, θ2 to θ4. The energy balance is
performed on the way that heat is accumulated between layers (κ1 to κ5), conductance
occurs between nodes (h1 to h4), and convective and radiative heat transfer is considered.
For each component, the energy balance equation is established and solved as a matrix.
The number of equations describing the building elements is therefore 5 x the number of
opaque elements + 2 x the number of windows and doors. In addition, an energy balance
equation for the air within the building is formulated. This set of equations is solved for an
hourly step time interval, allowing to determine the internal temperatures, the heating and
cooling loads and needs ­sensible and latent, and the heating and cooling design loads
for the building.

details about the equations for the hourly calculation in EN ISO 52016­1:2007 and the
algorithm developed in Python to implement them are given.

a considerable impact. So while some of these deviations can be considered accept­

When it comes to heating, results show that the energy needs calculated by the hourly
procedure are below the estimated ones by Be18. This finding was also reported by
Domenico et al. [30] in a similar assessment for a detached house in Italy. The dif­
ferences, however, are lower in this particular case. Several factors could explain this
observation. Firstly, Be18 does not consider the heat solar gains through opaque con­
struction elements, which may be a cause for the higher needs observed. Nevertheless,
heat exchange due to long­wave radiation to the sky is not included either. Therefore,
both heat fluxes might compensate for each other, explaining why the differences in the
hourly calculation are not large. Secondly, even though the heat exchange through the
ground was not analysed in detail in this work due to its complexity, the differences could
also be attributed to that. In Be18, elements in contact with the ground are modelled as
opaque elements affected by a temperature factor, which considers the temperature dif­
ference over the construction in relation to the difference between inside and outside tem­
perature. In addition, transmission losses are then assessed by considering a constant
ground temperature of 10°C throughout the whole year, in accordance to the Danish Stan­
dard DS418 [37]. In the case of the hourly procedure, the heat flow through the ground
was obtained by the methods exposed in EN ISO 13770:2017, which characterizes heat
transfer via the ground by the heat flow related to the area of the floor, the perimeter, and
the annual periodic heat exchange resulting from the thermal inertia of the ground [38].
Moving forward, the difference in the time­step length is perhaps another reason for the
observed results. Avoiding the use of monthly average values for outer temperature and
solar irradiance, may explain the lower figures. Finally, yet while the effect of the heat
capacity is not visualized in the exposed results, the way both methodologies deal with
it differs from each other. In Be18, the total heat capacity of the building is indicated as
a single value accounting for all construction elements. Conversely, in the hourly proce­
dure, each individual building component is modelled in accordance with its specific heat
capacity and distribution of mass.
With regard to cooling, the predicted energy needs calculated by the hourly procedure
26
Implementation and assessment of the hourly calculation method for potential application in Be18
==PAGE:36==
are higher than the ones obtained with Be18. The deviations may be caused, partly, by
the reasons stated above. In fact, the influence of solar radiation is larger during the
summer period. Therefore, it is not surprising that the cooling energy needs from the
hourly procedure are higher. It should also be noted that cooling is not set by definition in
Be18. Instead, an overheating penalty is calculated as a fictive energy demand, equal to
the energy demanded by an imaginary mechanical cooling system, in order to keep the
indoor temperature at 26°C [9]. This fact introduced some uncertainties when reading the
output values.
One interesting finding from the results reported is the cooling energy needs obtained with
the hourly calculation for the months of February and March. Although both values are low
­around 18 and 61 kWh, respectively, they were somewhat surprising. Nonetheless, an
hourly detailed output of the outer temperature, the solar heat gains, and the cooling load
for this period is presented in Figure 5.2, which suggests that the occurrence of peaks in
the solar heat gains of around 10 kW combined with the fact that the building has no solar
shading devices, could be a possible explanation. The increases in temperature do not
seem to be correlated.

Other compelling results are the ones found for the months of April and October. In these
transition months ­and perhaps even in March, the differences are the highest. This could
be attributed to the limitation of monthly averaging when both heating and cooling modes
occur in the steady­state method. When performing the monthly average, a negative
cooling load can balance out a positive heating load. The results table in Be18 is shown
in Table 5.4. For the months of April and October, the Rel. supplement ­i.e. the ratio
between heat gains and heat losses, is closer to 1. This suggests the coexistence of both
heating and cooling needs, which might offer an explanation for the observed differences
between the two methodologies.

in this case was from Monday to Friday, from 8 to 17. In terms of occupancy density, a
number of 1.0 was utilised. This condition of use can be modelled more accurately by the
hourly procedure; whereas in Be18, although a start and end time can be defined, it is the
average usage time of 45 hours/week that is used for the calculations.
Turning to the cooling energy needs, the outcome is also contrary to the residential cases.
The hourly procedure shows lower results than the quasi­steady­state method, with larger
differences as well. Overall, it emerges from these results that a more detailed modelling
of the occupancy of the building has an influence on the thermal balance. This could be
particularly interesting in the dynamic interaction between the occupants and systems.
Also, if these results were to be used for the design heating or cooling load calculation,
small differences could have a significant impact, especially on monetary costs.

General Comments
5.4.1
Potentials of the hourly calculation procedure
The possibility of having an hourly time interval calculation procedure offers many advan­
tages in comparison to the monthly quasi­steady­state method. Even though the building
cases tested in the present work are fairly simple, the hourly output from their analysis
was used to show how the variations in weather and operation of the building influence
the energy needs for space heating and cooling and for the internal temperatures. Thus,
a critical winter and summer week was defined, based on the maximum values for the
heating and cooling loads, respectively. Figure 5.7 and Figure 5.8 present the variation of
the outer temperature, internal operative temperature, solar heat gains, and heating and
cooling loads, over the critical week, for the case of the office building. Only this case was
selected to show such variations due to the fact that the influence of changes in both the
weather and the operation of the building can be visualized. The figures presenting the
critical winter and summer week, and the critical winter and summer day, for all the Be18
examples analysed are included in Appendix C.
Figure 5.7: Overview of the critical winter week for the office building.
In Figure 5.7, it can be seen how the peaks in heating loads occur at the start of the
occupation of the building, after the long unoccupied period, which match with the points
in time when the outer temperatures are lowest and solar heat gains null. The effect
of the heat capacity also stands out. On the fourth day of the week, when the building
has been through some occupied periods already, the peak in the heating load is lower
compared to the second day. The solar gains on the third day and the slight increase
in temperature, combined with the heavy mass of the building, contribute to achieving

a lower heat load. In addition, the graph shows how the internal operative temperature
drops during the weekend, when the building is not operated. Only some increases are

A similar behaviour is observed in the summer situation, but with peaks in the cooling
loads occurring in coincidence with the highest solar heat gains and outer temperatures.

the quasi­steady­state method to a certain level. Nevertheless, additional variables, such
as the surface temperature of elements or the mean radiant temperature of the air in the
thermal zone, for instance, can be read out.

5.4.4
Potential implementation in the Danish building context
The findings reported here shed new light on how the hourly procedure described in the
Standard could be put into practice in the Danish context and particularly, for the future of
Be18.
In reviewing the literature, a note on the stringency and positioning of Denmark among
one of the first countries to formulate the energy requirements for buildings was made.
Hence, the implementation of the addressed methodology could contribute to an initia­
tive to improve the building stock or an update of the current calculation procedure in the
energy framework. The results from the building cases analysed here highlight the ro­
bustness and accuracy of the methodology. While these are not expected to forecast the
real building energy consumption, the more detailed results are valuable for narrowing
down the EPG and the uncertainties in LCA calculations quoted at the beginning of this
report.
Likewise, the increased complexity of the assessed approach is minimum, which suggests
that the application for the future development of Be18 may not be troublesome. Potential
users would not need much deeper knowledge in the areas of energy design and operation
of buildings, their interaction with the environment and technical installations, and the
methods for controlling them.
Yet questions remain on how hourly modelling of the building systems and technical in­
stallations could look like. The work presented here only accounts for the calculation of
the basic energy needs. To model the interaction between the systems and the thermal
zone significantly more and perhaps arbitrary input data might be required, introducing
procedural complications and uncertainties to the results.
Furthermore, whether a shift to the hourly calculation procedure is decided or not, the
potential implementation will also depend on how big of an impact this could have within
the energy labelling scheme and the fulfillment of the energy framework in the Danish BR.
In future investigations, it might be possible to undertake a study of already issued energy
performance certificates to be able to assess these differences.
5.4.5
Suggestions for future work
Despite the promising results, additional research is needed to better understand how the
calculation procedure applies to more complex building cases. A limitation of this study
is that the influence of the building technical systems and the effect of the interaction with
dynamic technologies were not evaluated. The effect of shading devices and obstacles
was not included in the programming model. Therefore, further studies, which take these
into account, will need to be undertaken. Furthermore, thermal zoning was not addressed.
The influence of adjacent thermally conditioned and unconditioned zones is suggested as
a possible future investigation. Finally, additional research could also include the bench­

6
Conclusion
This project was undertaken to analyse, adapt and implement the hourly method proposed
by EN ISO 52016­1:2007 to calculate the energy needs involved in space heating and
cooling of buildings and for the internal temperatures, for potential application within the
context of the Danish Building Regulations, namely Be18. For this, a programming model
in Python was implemented, and a series of building cases were assessed by both the
hourly calculation procedure and Be18.
The main findings of the investigation performed are:
1. For residential cases, where permanent occupation and operation of the building
were considered, the hourly calculation estimated lower energy needs for heating
and higher energy needs for cooling than the quasi­steady­state method in Be18.
The reasons behind this may be varied, but the fact that solar radiation through
opaque construction elements and long­wave heat exchange with the external en­
vironment is not considered in Be18 was pointed out as one of the main differences
between both methodologies. Also, the heat capacity of the building is considered
differently. Likewise, differences in the calculation of heat transfer via the ground
and the time­step length were identified as possible explanations for the observed
results.
2. In the case of the office building, an opposite trend to the residential cases was
noticed. In fact, the differences in the results of the two assessed methodologies
were higher. Even though further investigations with more complex building cases
need to be done to come to a better conclusion, results suggest that a more detailed
modelling of the occupancy and operation of the building can significantly influence
the calculation of energy needs.
3. Yet when the comparison of the hourly method with dynamic simulation was not the
main scope of this project, the results from the benchmark with BSim indicate that
the Standard overestimates the energy needs for heating. On the contrary, energy
needs for cooling were found to be lower with the hourly method in EN ISO 52016­
1. This was attributed to the way the model from the Standard calculates the total
solar heat gains into the thermal zone. Nevertheless, the findings are rather difficult
to interpret, as the outcomes from other studies comparing the hourly method with
several dynamic simulation software are varied. Thus, the results barely stand as
another contribution to the already existing studies found in the literature review.
Overall, this project contributes to understanding how the hourly procedure described in
EN ISO 52016­1:2017 could be put into practice in the Danish context, particularly, for the
future of Be18. The added value of the hourly calculation balances out the extra needed
input and the increased complexity, which are kept at a minimum.
Notwithstanding the limitations of the present study, this work highlights the potential of
the hourly method for upgrading the current requirements and outlining future energy
efficiency initiatives. Challenges remain on the hourly modelling of the building systems
and technical installations, and on the impact that the change from one methodology to
the other could have on the EPCs already issued, and on the fulfillment of the energy
framework in the Danish BR. Therefore, further studies are recommended to understand
these important implications in future practice.
