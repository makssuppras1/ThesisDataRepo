Abstract
Unspecific peroxygenase (UPO, EC 1.11.2.1) has a high potential for industrial appli-
cations due to its capability of using a wide range of substrates. One of the main challenges
in the utilization of UPO is the risk of chemical inactivation by its oxidizing agent, hy-
drogen peroxide. It is therefore important to study the stability of UPO. Stability studies
of UPO often employ spectrophotometric assay methods, for example the 2,2’-azino-bis(3-
ethylbenzothiazoline-6-sulfonic acid) (ABTS) method. However, sources often vary on the
procedure of the ABTS assay and do not mention important parameters.
This thesis aims to standardize the ABTS assay procedure and utilize the standard-
ized procedure to study the stability of UPO against H2O2, 1-propanol, and propionaldehyde.
The standardization involved running different procedures in triplicates and evaluating the
reproducibility and linearity of the progress curves. Seven aspects of the assay were evalu-
ated: the reagent volume, the assay container, the concentration of the enzyme stock, the
temperature, the order of addition, the concentration of H2O2, and the ABTS-to-H2O2 ra-
tio. Afterwards, the stability study was conducted using the exposure approach. UPO was
incubated in the inactivating agent and sampled at two different time points: right after
addition and after 45 minutes. The sample was used in the ABTS assay to determine the
enzyme activity.
An ABTS assay procedure has been developed as a result of the standardization. The
most crucial aspects of the standardization are using a cuvette, equilibrating all reagents,
storing the enzyme at concentrated solutions, and using a 2 mM concentration for both ABTS
and H2O2. The stability study confirmed the inactivation mechanism by H2O2 and raised
the possibility of a protective effect by 1-propanol. However, the possibility of a protective
effect means that the exposure approach is not suitable for studying the stability of UPO.
Instead, an experiment combining stability study with a kinetic experiment in fed-batch
mode was proposed.

Background
Aided by advances in protein engineering and bioprocessing technology, enzymes are
increasingly utilized in industrial-scale manufacturing [1]. Sheldon and Woodley (2018) iden-
tified several benefits of enzymes in comparison to conventional catalysts [2]. Enzymes are
produced from renewable sources instead of increasingly scarce metals, and are biodegrad-
able and nontoxic. Their reactions are usually conducted at ambient temperature and at-
mospheric pressure, lowering their energy demands and forgoing the need for specialized
equipment.
Various industrial processes have transitioned from conventional chemical reactions to
biocatalysis for quality or environmental reasons. For example, nitrile hydratase has replaced
copper-based catalysts in the production of acrylamide from acrylonitrile, as the enzyme-
catalyzed process requires a milder operating condition and produces less by-products [3].
The pulp industry has substituted chlorine in the bleaching process with enzymes such as
xylanases, lignin peroxidase, and laccase. This substitution manages to reduce the levels of
toxic and bioaccumulating organochlorine compounds in the wastewater [4]. Other industries
such as pharmaceuticals exploit the stereoselectivity of enzymes.
Enantiomerically pure
amino acids, used as ingredients in parenteral nutrition and antibiotics, are produced using
acylase and hydantoinase [5] [6].
An enzyme with a large potential for industrial applications is unspecific peroxygenase
(UPO, EC 1.11.2.1). UPO is an oxidoreductase (an enzyme that catalyzes redox reactions)
that uses peroxide as its oxidizing agent. The term "unspecific" refers to the wide range
of reactions that UPO can catalyze [7]. Meanwhile, the use of hydrogen peroxide as an
oxidizing agent instead of molecular oxygen simplifies the bioreactor design and allows for
easier continuous operation [8]. As a result, UPO is a promising candidate for widespread
industrial use.
1.1
Unspecific Peroxygenase
The enzyme subclass EC 1.11 encompasses oxidoreductases that use hydrogen per-
oxide as the electron acceptor. This subclass is divided into two groups: the peroxidases
(EC 1.11.1) and the peroxygenases (EC 1.11.2). The difference between the two is that in
peroxygenase reactions, the oxygen in the oxidation product originates from the hydrogen
peroxide instead of the substrate [9]. The differentiation between the two groups can be
carried out by radioactive labeling [10] [11].
As of 2025, six members of the peroxygenase group have been identified.
A rep-
resentative enzyme of this group is unspecific peroxygenase (UPO, EC 1.11.2.1). It was
first discovered in 2004 from the fungus Agrocybe aegerita [12]. Since then, other fungi of
the phylum Basidiomycota have been shown to produce UPO, such as Marasmius rotula
and Coprinellus radians, though with slightly different structures [13]. Research by Molina-
Espeja et al. (2014, 2015) has also managed to express UPO from Agrocybe aegerita in
Saccharomyces cerevisiae and Pichia pastoris [14] [15]. Its biological function in those fungi
has yet to be known; one hypothesis is that it allows wood-decay fungi to utilize aromatic
compounds and lignocellulose as their carbon source [7].

1.1.1 Biochemistry
Figure 1.1 shows the structure of the active site of UPO. UPO is a heme-dependent
enzyme, which means that it contains a heme ring as the cofactor. Heme is a coordination
complex of an iron ion coordinated to a porphyrin acting as a tetradentate ligand [16]. More
specifically, it is a heme-thiolate enzyme, which means that one of the axial ligands of the
coordination complex is a thiolate (R−S−). In the case of UPO from Agrocybe aegerita
(AaeUPO), this thiolate comes from cysteine-36, which is part of a proline-cysteine-proline
(PCP) motif. Finally, glutamate-196 on the distal side of the heme ring and arginine-189
form the acid-base pair required to cleave hydrogen peroxide. This system constitutes the
active site of UPO [17].
Figure 1.1: Structure of the active site of AaeUPO [18]
The mechanism of an UPO reaction has been explained by Wang et al. (2017) [18].
The first step of the reaction is the replacement of the water ligand in the heme complex with
H2O2. Then, glutamate-196 deprotonates the complex and cleaves the O−O bond, creating
an active compound called Compound I (Cpd I). Cpd I interacts with the hydrogen atom
of the substrate in a process called hydrogen abstraction. This creates Compound II, which
is the heme ring with a hydroxy ligand, and a radical of the substrate. The hydroxy ligand
and radical quickly recombine to generate the product. The product dissociates from the
enzyme, regenerating the active site and restarts the reaction cycle for another H2O2 and
substrate molecule. Figure 1.2 illustrates this entire sequence.
Kinne et al. (2009) discovered that the main reaction of UPO follows a Ping-Pong
Bi-Bi mechanism [10]. "Ping-Pong" means that a product (in this case water) is released
before all substrates have bound to the enzyme. Meanwhile, "Bi-Bi" signals the number of
substrates in both the forward and reverse reactions, in this case two ("Bi") forward and two
("Bi") reverse [19]. In their research paper, Kinne et al. proved this mechanism by obtaining
parallel double reciprocal plots typical of a Ping-Pong mechanism [20]. This research paper

1.1.2 Potential Industrial Applications
Most of the interest surrounding UPO concerns its ability to catalyze the oxygenation
of organic molecules, particularly of inert C−H, C−C, and C−−C bonds. Oxygenation reac-
tions are of major interest in industries such as fine chemicals and pharmaceuticals. Table
1.1 is a nonexhaustive list of potential applications of UPO curated from several sources.
Over 300 substrates have been identified for UPO, giving rise to the term "unspecific" in its
name [7] [21].
Currently, these reactions are conducted with metal catalysts. For example, cyclo-
hexanol and cyclohexanone are currently produced from liquid-phase oxidation of cyclo-
hexane catalyzed by either cobalt or chromium depending on the desired cyclohexanol-to-
cyclohexanone ratio [28]. However, as is typical for metal-catalyzed processes, the energy

also produce 2-naphthol [25] [29].
As a consequence of these drawbacks, industries began to explore biocatalytic path-
ways to supplant conventional catalysis in oxygenation reactions. Prior to the discovery of
UPO, the often-explored enzymes to substitute metal-catalyzed oxygenations are cytochrome
P450 monooxygenases (P450s). This group of enzymes performs the same oxyfunctionaliza-
tion as UPO with a nearly identical mechanism [30]. However, even after decades of research,
its application has been very limited [31].
The difficulty in developing P450-based processes stems from its use of molecular
oxygen as the oxidizing agent [32]. Biochemically, the use of molecular oxygen requires a
long electron transport chain dependent on auxiliary coenzymes (flavoproteins and nicoti-
namides), which can be expensive to provide [18] [30] [32]. From an engineering point of
view, it also adds complexity to bioreactor design and operation. Because of its low sol-
ubility in water, oxygen needs to be continuously supplied through a sparger system [33].

This supply involves multiphase mass transfer, whose rate (oxygen transfer rate, OTR) is
dependent on a multitude of factors such as the physical properties and the geometry of the
reactor [34].
In contrast, the ability of UPO to utilize H2O2 allows for the simple catalytic mech-
anism seen in Figure 1.2 [18]. Moreover, at atmospheric pressure and ambient temperature,
H2O2 is liquid and miscible in water [35]. H2O2 can be added directly into the bioreactor
and mixed into the reaction broth, although the cost of H2O2 is naturally higher than that
of air. Moreover, without the consideration of oxygen transfer, the reactor can be designed
for optimum kinetics and conversion, for example by using plug flow reactors in continuous
operations [36] [8]. Nowadays, UPO has overtaken P450s in enzymatic oxygenation research
by virtue of these benefits.
The utilization of H2O2 is not without drawbacks. As a strong oxidizer, H2O2 can
destroy protein structures in a process called denaturation [37]. In enzymes, denaturation
often causes a loss of activity, which is termed inactivation [38]. Despite using H2O2 as
a substrate, UPO is not immune to being inactivated by it, as seen from the heme ring
destruction reaction in Figure 1.3. Indeed, Monterrey et al. (2023) identified inactivation as
one of the issues to be resolved towards large-scale applications of UPO [30]. Therefore, it
is important to have a study focused on UPO stability against inactivation by H2O2.
The study of enzyme stability is usually conducted by exposure experiments. An
enzyme solution is exposed to the inactivating condition for a certain period of time. After-
wards, the remaining activity of the exposed enzyme is assayed. The enzyme reacts with a
predetermined reagent, and the rate of the reaction is recorded. As the exposure period is
extended, the reaction rate will also decrease. This workflow has been employed by studies
such as Rotilio et al. (2021) to determine the thermal stability of UPO and Arnao et al.
(1990) to study the inactivation of horseradish peroxidase by H2O2.
Interestingly, the exposure approach has not been applied to study UPO inactivation
by H2O2. Instead, contemporary research on UPO often treats inactivation by H2O2 as a
"side effect" in kinetic studies. For example, Mireles et al. (2021) conducted a batch kinetic
experiment on UPO-catalyzed ether oxidation, then assayed the residual activity after the
main reaction halted [39]. The same approach on inactivation was taken by Karich et al.
(2016) and Bormann et al. (2020) in their aforementioned studies on UPO’s catalase activity
and benzylic hydroxylation, respectively [22][23]. The drawback of this approach is that H2O2
was consumed throughout the experiment, which means that the enzyme exhibited a different
stability profile at different time points. The substrate’s protective effect could not be clearly
described either, as the substrate concentration also decreases. The exposure method allows
for a stability study at constant concentration independent of the enzyme’s kinetic activity,
which will provide more valuable information on the enzyme’s stability profile.
1.2
The ABTS Assay
The activity assay method in an enzyme exposure experiment is usually based on the
principles of spectrophotometry. Spectrophotometry measures the concentration of a certain
analyte by passing light through the sample. Due to the interaction between photons and
the analyte, the intensity of the light is attenuated, which is termed absorbance [40]. The

higher the concentration of the analyte, the more intense the absorbance. The absorbance

which can be correlated with time to measure the reaction rate.
There are several spectrophotometric methods for assaying the activity of peroxide-
utilizing enzymes such as UPO. Based on the publicly available database of UPO functional
data, the three most utilized methods are the 2,2’-azino-bis(3-ethylbenzothiazoline-6-sulfonic
acid) (ABTS) assay, the 2,6-dimethoxyphenol (DMP) assay, and the 5-nitro-1,3-benzodioxole
(NBD) assay [41]. For example, the research by Molina-Espeja et al. (2014), which developed
new variants of AaeUPO through directed evolution, used both the ABTS and NBD assay to
measure activity of the evolved mutants [14]. Another study from the same research group
utilized the DMP assay when engineering an AaeUPO variant to produce 1-naphthol [25].
The stability studies by Rotilio et al. (2021) and Arnao et al. (1990) also used the ABTS
assay [42] [43].
Of the three aforementioned assay methods, the ABTS method is both the most
common and the most advantageous. The ABTS reagent is nontoxic, inexpensive, stable,
and water-soluble, making the assay robust, safe, and simple to conduct. In comparison,
DMP is both toxic and light-sensitive, and the resulting colored compound often precipitates
[44]. Meanwhile, NBD is insoluble in water and thus must be dissolved in acetonitrile [44].
For that reason, this thesis project focuses on the ABTS assay.
The reagent of the ABTS assay is 2,2’-azino-bis(3-ethylbenzothiazoline-6-sulfonic acid)
or ABTS. When oxidized, ABTS produces a metastable cation, as seen in Figure 1.5. This
cation emits a blue-green color, which allows for a convenient spectrophotometry analysis
at a wavelength of around 415-420 nm [44]. The wavelength absorbance of the reaction
solution changes as the oxidation proceeds and more of this blue-green cation is produced.
For the purpose of quantification by the Beer-Lambert Law, the value of ϵ for ABTS is 36
mM−1cm−1 [44].

1. The assay container.
Based on the volumes in Tables 1.2 and 1.3, it can be inferred that the Molina/Dolz
procedure utilizes a microplate, while the Hilberath procedure uses a cuvette. Microplates
allow for multiple runs to be complete concurrently, but present a complication when con-
verting the absorbance into concentration through the Beer-Lambert Law. In contrast to
the cuvette, for which the pathlength l is standardized as its width (usually 1 cm), the path-
length of a microplate assay depends on the total volume of the assay as well as the curvature
of the meniscus [48]. This complication makes microplate assays less robust against natural
volumetric variations than cuvette assays.
The Molina/Dolz procedure was developed for high-throughput screening in directed
evolution research, whose purpose is rapid qualitative characterization of multiple enzyme
mutants. As such, conversion by the Beer-Lambert Law is often not needed. However, a
stability study requires quantitative measurement of enzyme activity, and the pathlength
variation might prove to be a significant disturbance.
Nevertheless, if this issue can be
proven to be negligible, using a microplate is favored over using a cuvette because of its
higher throughput and smaller amounts of reagents.
2. The temperature control method.
Temperature, alongside pH, is one of the most important parameters to specify when
conducting an enzymatic reaction.
Temperature affects enzymatic reactions in multiple
ways, for example by changing the activation energy, altering the enzyme’s affinity towards
the substrate, and thermal denaturation [38]. Despite its importance, only the Hilberath
method specified a temperature (25°C), while the Molina/Dolz method did not. It is likely
that the lack of specified temperature in the Molina/Dolz method also pertains to its main
purpose in screening, at which temperatures may be varied to test thermal stability.
A more pressing issue that both methods did not specify is the temperature control
method, regardless of what that temperature may be. The Hilberath method mentioned an
"incubation" at 25°C, but details such as incubation time and method were not given. It
is possible that both methods assume that the spectrophotometer machine has an adequate
thermostat to control the reaction temperature and precise pre-equilibration was not needed.
On the other hand, Punekar (2018) recommended temperature equilibration using a water
bath.
An equilibration step offers a more certain and monitorable temperature control,
though with the risk of denaturing the enzyme.
3. The order of reagent addition.
The two procedures disagree about the order of reagent addition. The Molina/Dolz
procedure recommends mixing the buffer, ABTS, and H2O2 first in a "reaction solution",
before adding some of the reaction solution into an aliquot of the enzyme. On the other
hand, the Hilberath procedure mixes the enzyme and ABTS first and initiates the reaction
with the addition of H2O2.
Both orders have their own strengths and pitfalls.
According to Punekar (2018),
typical enzyme assays usually add the enzyme last to prevent prior inactivation due to the
Page 9 of 41
==PAGE:19==
s230009 - Master’s Thesis
assay conditions [20]. This is in agreement with the Molina/Dolz procedure, which probably
aims to prevent prolonged exposure to H2O2 and acidic pH. However, H2O2 is a very strong
oxidator. It is possible that the reaction will proceed prominently even without the enzyme,
which will compromise the accuracy of the assay. The Hilberath procedure may have been
formulated with this consideration in mind.
4. The concentration of H2O2.
For an assay to accurately portray the extent of the enzymatic activity, the reaction
rate should be limited only by the enzyme availability. That means the substrate concentra-
tion should be sufficiently high, which for enzymes can be quantified as being significantly
higher than the expected Michaelis constant, KM. When considering a typical Michaelis-
Menten curve, a concentration above KM also means less variation in rate, which makes the
assay more robust against minor volumetric errors. However, too much substrate can cause
substrate inhibition. In the case of H2O2, there is also the problem of chemical inactivation.
Both procedures agree on using an H2O2 concentration of 2 mM. It would be prudent
to check whether this concentration causes inactivation. On the other hand, it is also interest-
ing to explore whether it is possible to increase the concentration to achieve a concentration
"safer" from substrate limitation.
5. The ABTS-to-H2O2 ratio.
The same consideration for H2O2 concentration applies to the other substrate in the
assay, ABTS. As seen in Figure 1.5, ABTS and H2O2 react in a 1:1 molar ratio. However,
both the Molina/Dolz and Hilberath procedures have H2O2 in excess; at a 1:2 ratio for Dolz
et al. 1:4 for Hilberath et al., and 3:20 for Molina-Espeja et al. This is to ensure that
the H2O2 does not become limiting from side reactions, both from UPO’s catalase activity
(see Figure 1.4) and accidental impurities. Unfortunately, the maximum H2O2 is limited by
inactivation concerns. A lower ABTS-to-H2O2 must be achieved by decreasing the ABTS
concentration instead of increasing the H2O2 concentration. This presents a risk of ABTS
becoming limiting, as well as losing robustness due to operating closer to KM.
Based on the analysis above, the ABTS assay procedure still has several issues to
resolve to ensure its accuracy and precision. It is necessary to start the stability study of
UPO by establishing a standard assay procedure.

Project Description
The previous chapter has outlined two important questions to address towards large-
scale application of unspecific peroxygenase in industry. First, a standardized and repro-
ducible ABTS assay procedure should be established. Second, the stability of UPO against
inactivating agents should be studied. This thesis project was established to answer these
two questions experimentally.
2.1
Objective Statements
The goal of this thesis can be synthesized as the following objective statements:
1. Standardize an ABTS assay procedure for the determination of the activity of unspe-
cific peroxygenase.
2. Determine the stability profile of unspecific peroxygenase against chemical inactiva-
tion using the standardized ABTS assay procedure.
2.2
Experimental Plan
The objective statements in Section 2.1 were answered sequentially. The standardiza-
tion of an ABTS assay procedure was carried out first, in which the five conflicts and issues
listed in Section 1.2 were resolved. The assay was conducted with different treatments. Sub-
sequently, the results of the assay were compared to determine which treatment gives the
most reproducible and accurate results. At the end of the procedure standardization, the
final procedure was evaluated against statistical measures for reproducibility and accuracy.
The standardized ABTS assay procedure was used to evaluate the stability of UPO.
The stability study followed the exposure experiment workflow discussed briefly in Section
1.2. UPO was exposed to the inactivating agent for a certain period of time and diluted
to reduce the concentration of the inactivating agent.
The remaining activity was then
determined using the ABTS assay.
The stability study of UPO in this thesis was contextualized in the study of a model
reaction, that is the oxidation of 1-propanol to propionaldehyde. Consequently, the stability
of UPO was examined not only against H2O2, but also against 1-propanol and propionalde-
hyde. This contextualization serves two purposes. First, the wider scope of the stability
study ensures that the developed assay procedure is applicable for a wide range of purposes.
Second, the results of the stability study can be used for further research, for example in
kinetic studies.

ABTS was supplied in its diammonium salt form. The salt was solubilized in dH2O

2.3.2 Preparation of Buffers
The citrate buffer had a pH of 4.4 and a concentration of 100 mM. It was made by
mixing 56.49 mM citric acid and 43.51 mM sodium citrate dihydrate in dH2O [49]. The pH
was adjusted by adding NaOH 1000 mM. The citrate buffer was used in the ABTS assay to
maintain pH.
The phosphate buffer had a pH of 7.0 and a concentration of 50 mM. It was made by
mixing 28.89 mM disodium hydrogen phosphate heptahydrate (Na2HPO4 · 7H2O) and 21.11
mM sodium dihydrogen phosphate monohydrate (NaH2PO4 · H2O) in dH2O [50]. The pH
was adjusted by adding NaOH 1000 mM. The phosphate buffer was used to solubilize the
enzyme, as well as necessary dilutions of the concentrated enzyme solution.
2.3.3 Preparation of Enzyme Solution
The enzyme used was a recombinant AaeUPO (PaDa-I mutant) created by Molina-
Espeja et al. (2014) [14]. It was provided by a research group from the Delft University of
Technology as a lyophilized enzyme powder with an enzyme content of 0.81 nmol/mg [46]. To
make a solution, the powder was solubilized in phosphate buffer to a concentration of 2 µM,
and dilutions were made as necessary. As per the research group’s recommendation, enzyme
solutions were stored in a refrigerator at 4°C for a maximum of 7 days after solubilization.

3
ABTS Assay Procedure Standardization
The procedure standardization involves several verification experiments to compare
different ABTS assay procedures found in literature. Based on the discussion in Section 1.2,
five factors have been identified for the procedure standardization. In order of execution,
these factors are the assay container, the assay temperature control method, the order of
reagent addition, the concentration of H2O2, and the ratio of ABTS to H2O2. Within each
factor, two to four different procedures were evaluated.
In addition to the five aspects above, two unforeseen factors were found to influence
the assay. These factors are the volume of the reagents and the storage concentration of
the stock. These unforeseen factors will be discussed in the same manner as the five known
aspects. Their placements in this chapter are based on the chronological order in which these
factors were discovered during the study.
3.1
General Methodology
The evaluation of all aspects followed the same workflow. Three replicates of each
procedure were run on a UV-Vis spectrophotometer at a wavelength of 420 nm. The assay
runtime was 300 seconds.
The relatively long runtime was used because the microplate
reader requires a longer transition time between readings. A longer runtime was therefore
necessary to ensure that enough data points were obtained for the reaction rate calculation.
The cuvette reader does not have this limitation. Hence, if the cuvette was chosen as the
assay container, the runtime would be reduced to 90 seconds, which was recommended by
the Hilberath procedure.
The output of the spectrophotometer would be a progress curve plotting absorbance
against time. The absorbance readings were converted to the concentration of the ABTS+
cation using the Beer-Lambert Law (Equation 1.1). Afterwards, the slope of each triplicate
was calculated from start to end regardless of linearity.
The first criterion for evaluating the procedures was the variability of the slopes.
These slopes represent the reaction rate, and a reproducible procedure will return the same
rate every time.
Variability was quantified by the coefficient of variation, as suggested
by Centore (2016) [51]. The coefficient of variation (CV) is the ratio between the sample
standard deviation and the mean, as stated in Equation 3.1. A lower CV means a lower
variability and thus better reproducibility.

Enzyme Instability in Diluted Solutions
The cuvette’s progress curve in Figure 3.5 showed a prominent drop in activity for
Replicate 3.
Replicate 3 was executed three days after the other two replicates, which
suggested a loss of activity during storage. This was despite the execution day still being
within the seven-day storage period mentioned in Subsection 2.3.3. Moreover, the enzyme
was stored in the refrigerator at the specified temperature of 4°C. In conclusion, there was
another storage condition that caused the loss of activity.
One possible cause was that the enzyme was stored in a dilute concentration directly
fit for the assay (2 nM). Generally, it is recommended to store enzymes in concentrated
solutions and dilute right before the assay. This is because enzymes in dilute concentrations
are prone to adsorbing to the tube walls, causing reduced activity in storage [20]. Even
though this experiment had taken precautions against this phenomenon by using PCR-grade
tubes and pipette tips, it was decided to explore this possibility.

stock was made from it. The 2 nM stock was used to perform the procedure in Figure

assaying enzyme activity, such background reaction is undesirable. This is a justification for
not choosing the ’UPO Last’ procedure.

Stability Study
With the ABTS assay procedure established, the stability study can finally be con-
ducted. As mentioned in Section 2.2, the stability study was given an additional context,
which is the model reaction of a 1-propanol oxidation to propionaldehyde (propanal). There-
fore, the inactivating agent tested against UPO included also 1-propanol and propionalde-
hyde, in addition to hydrogen peroxide.
Prior to the stability study, the assay’s enzyme concentration was selected to ensure
the reaction progressed at the appropriate rate. This section is included in this chapter
instead of the previous chapter on procedure standardization. The enzyme concentration
of an assay depends on the enzyme stock and is therefore specific to the experiment. A
standard assay procedure does not have to specify the enzyme concentration.
4.1
Selection of Enzyme Concentration
To calculate the activity of an enzymatic reaction, the substrate should not be de-
pleted immediately so as to obtain enough data points in the linear regime of the progress
curve. Moreover, the Beer-Lambert Law is limited to low concentrations of analyte, so if
the reaction progresses to quickly, the conversion from absorbance to concentration will be
inaccurate [40]. On the other hand, the reaction should show an appreciable progress within
a reasonable time period. This is especially important in this stability study, where an inac-
tivating agent will be present. Consequently, the ABTS assay should use a suitable enzyme
concentration: low enough to give an accurate reading of the progress curve but high enough
to keep the experiment duration brief. The goal of this section is to determine that enzyme
concentration.
The selection of enzyme concentration follows the workflow of Figure 4.1. The enzyme
concentration was changed by changing the amount of 2 µM enzyme stock and phosphate
buffer in the pre-dilution step, which can be seen in Table 4.1. Otherwise, the procedure
followed the established ABTS procedure from Chapter 3, restated here as Table 4.2.
The selected enzyme concentration would be the highest concentration which, at
the end of the 90-second runtime of the assay, maintained a PCC at minimum 0.999 and
achieved an endpoint absorbance below 1.000. These two metrics ensure that the "highest"
concentration would not be too high. Linearity ensures that the substrate is not depleted
too quickly. Meanwhile, the absorbance endpoint below 1.000 is a common rule-of-thumb at
which the Beer-Lambert Law most likely still applies.

Methodology of the Stability Study
After the enzyme concentration was established, the stability study was conducted.
The workflow can be seen in Figure 4.3. The concentrated enzyme was exposed to different
concentrations of the inactivating agent. Then, two samples were withdrawn, one right after
exposure (0 minute) and one after 45 minutes of exposure. The sample was assayed with
the same ABTS assay procedure established in Chapter 3.
A "control" experiment was
also conducted in this manner, with the inactivating agent replaced by additional phosphate
buffer.
The absorbance readings were converted to the concentration of the ABTS+ cation
using the Beer-Lambert Law (Equation 1.1). Afterwards, the reaction rate was determined
by calculating the slope of the progress curve in concentration terms. Finally, the reaction
rates of the 0-minute and the 45-minute exposure experiments were compared to the rate
of the control experiment to determine the stability profile of UPO against the inactivating
agent.

4.5
Discussion
The stability study described in the previous section discovered several interesting
findings. Most importantly, it presented the possibility that the substrate protection effect
in UPO extends beyond inactivation by H2O2. The protective effect means that the stability
study against H2O2 is meaningless without the accompanying substrate of the reaction. For
example, Figure 4.4 showed complete loss of UPO activity after 45 minutes of exposure at
20 mM H2O2. However, the protective effect of 1-propanol may prevent this loss of activity.
A study by Hilberath et al. (2023) showed the extent of this substrate protective effect
on UPO. Using the same UPO source as this thesis (PaDa-I mutant), the study conducted
the oxidation of cyclohexane into cyclohexanol/cyclohexanone with an H2O2 concentration
of 150 mM. The reaction was able to proceed for about 2 hours before a significant loss of
activity occurred [52]. This result indicated that this UPO stock has the stability to conduct
reactions at an H2O2 concentration much higher than 20 mM, as long as the other substrate
is present.
The substrate protection effect on UPO depends on the concentration of the substrate.
However, the substrate itself may be inactivating, and UPO also has a substrate inhibition
mechanism. This challenged the common approach in stability in kinetic studies. Exposure
experiments conducted are inadequate to describe the stability of UPO due to the lack of
reaction context.
However, as explained in Subsection 1.1.2, the non-constant substrate
concentration in typical batch Michaelis-Menten experiments means the data from these
experiments cannot be used for stability studies.
The solution to this dilemma is pairing stability study with kinetic experiments in
fed-batch operation, where the H2O2 and substrate concentrations can be kept nearly con-
stant.
An example experiment with this approach has been conducted by Bormann et
al. (2020) [23]. In this study, the UPO-catalyzed oxidation of 4-ethylbenzoic acid (EBA)
into 4-(1-hydroxyethyl)benzoic acid (HEBA) was performed in a fed-batch reactor coupled
with an electrochemical H2O2 production. A mechanistic model was developed using the
King-Altmann method, then the parameters were determined through curve-fitting against
experimental data.
The resulting model described the complete reaction system shown
in Figure 1.4 as a system of four ordinary differential equations, which incorporated both
Michaelis-Menten, substrate inhibition, and inactivation terms.

5
Conclusion
This chapter summarizes the key findings of this thesis. Based on those findings,
possible further research directions are suggested.
5.1
Key Findings
This project has established a standardized ABTS assay procedure, which can be
referred to in Figure 3.17 and Table 3.15.
This procedure harmonized different sources
with regards to the assay’s reaction container, temperature control method, order of reagent
addition, and reagent concentrations. The most crucial points from the assay procedure are
using a cuvette, equilibrating all reagents, storing the enzyme at concentrated solutions, and
using a 1:1 ABTS-to-H2O2 ratio.
An important takeaway from the procedure standardization is that UPO might be
very sensitive to small changes in the procedure, to the point where traditional best practices
do not work for it. Several assay best practices and rules-of-thumb have been rejected in this
procedure standardization in the name of robustness. For example, the procedure developed
here uses large reagent volumes and does not recommend the use of a reaction cocktail.
The developed ABTS assay procedure was then used to study the stability profile
of UPO against H2O2, 1-propanol, and propionaldehyde. The stability study confirmed the
inactivation abilities of H2O2. It also uncovered the possibility of the substrate protective
effect of UPO extending to other inactivating agents. However, the stability studies against
1-propanol and propionaldehyde were inconclusive. The stability study concluded that the
traditional "exposure experiment" method could not be used in studying the stability of
UPO due to the substrate protective effect.
5.2
Suggestion for Further Research
There are several findings of the procedure standardization that may be of importance
in the actual operation of UPO-catalyzed reactions. For example, it would be interesting
to study the instability of UPO in dilute solutions that was described in Section 3.4. This
issue persisted in this experiment despite the best-practice prevention of using PCR-grade
equipment. A deeper look into this issue may change the standard procedure of enzyme
handling or the types of equipment that can be used in UPO research.
An outstanding issue arising from the procedure standardization is related to reaction
temperature. The procedure standardization has proposed a more stringent temperature
control method. This method can be used to determine the actual ideal temperature of the
ABTS assay, instead of relying on the typical value of 25°C.
Finally, as mentioned in Section 4.5, the stability study of UPO should be conducted
simultaneously with the kinetic study. When doing this study, higher concentrations (>
20 mM) of 1-propanol should be tried. The study conducted by Bormann et al. (2020)
can be used as a template [23]. However, when conducting the oxidation of 1-propanol to
propionaldehyde, the possibility of continued reaction from propionaldehyde to propionic
acid should be anticipated.

Description of safe work process  
Necessary precautions and safety arrangements (Relevant issues from KBA, section C) 
General Remarks 
Lab coat and safety glasses are worn at all times and put on at the entrance of the laboratory. Disposable nitrile 
gloves are worn at all times and changed after any contamination. Eye wash bottles, sink with soap and emergency 
showers are easily accessible. Spills are cleaned up immediately.  
 
Making of Buffers 
Mixing conducted under the fume hood. Stored in capped laboratory glass. 
1. C/P Buffer (pH 4.0): Used for stability assay. Citric acid solution + Na2HPO4 solution. Stocks created 
from citric acid crystals and disodium hydrogen phosphate.  
2. Phosphate Buffer (pH 7.0): Used for kinetic experiments. Potassium hydrogen phosphate + disodium 
hydrogen phosphate, directly dissolved into ddH2O.  
3. If necessary, add NaOH or HCl to adjust pH until desired. 
 
Making of UPO Stock 
Freshly made every working week. Lyophilized enzyme powder concentration: 0.81 nm UPO/mg.  
 
Making of Reaction and Exposure Stocks 
Mixing conducted under the fume hood. Stored in capped laboratory glass. Concentrations listed are maximum. 
1. H2O2 Stock: H2O2 + ddH2O 
2. 1-Propanol Stock: 1-propanol + ddH2O 
3. Propionaldehyde Stock: propionaldehyde + ddH2O 
 
Making of ABTS Screening Stocks 
1. 20 mM H2O2 Stock (20 mL): H2O2 stock + ddH2O 
2. 20 mM ABTS Stock (10 mL): Available in the specified concentration.  
3. Reaction Solution (90 mL): 5 mL ABTS stock, 10 mL buffer, 10 mL H2O2 reaction stock, 65 mL ddH2O 
 
Stability Assay - Enzyme Exposure 
Mixing conducted under the fume hood. Incubated in an Eppendorf tube. The amount of exposure stock added is 
adjusted to reach the concentration variation needed. The amount of C/P buffer added is adjusted to reach total 
volume. Maximum concentration at 2 M (propanol and propionaldehyde) and 5% (H2O2).  
 
Stability Assay - Spectrophotometric Screening 
UV spectrophotometry at 418 nm, conducted at 37°C. 180 µL of the reaction solution + 20 µL of exposure solution 
are pipetted periodically into the microplate wells.

Reactor setup: Borosilicate glass (Pyrex/Schott-Duran), 250 mL working volume. Temperature not higher than 
30°C, atmospheric pressure. The reactor has probes for H2O2 measurement and temperature control, as well as a 
port for enzyme addition and sampling. Temperature control and agitation are with a  tap water bath on a magnetic 
hotplate stirrer. No pH control method. 
 
Reactions will be run at different concentrations of propanol and H2O2. Exact values are to be decided after the 
stability assay but will not be higher than 2 M propanol and 5% H2O2. When propanol is varied, H2O2 is in excess, 
and vice versa. The amounts of H2O2 and propanol adjust to the run’s concentration variation. Phosphate buffer is 
added to reach total working volume. 
 
Kinetic Experiment - Gas Chromatography Analysis 
Sampling vials 1 mL, ethanol-to-reaction broth ratio depending on substrate concentration. Dodecane or n-butanol 
is added as internal standard. Conducted outside the fume hood for ease of sampling but capped immediately. Gas 
chromatograph setup is instructed in the video example. 
Enzyme Inactivation 
Add HCl to reaction broth and all analytes until the pH drops below 2.  
 
Cleanup 
Waste is disposed of according to its waste group. The reactor and its probes are rinsed with distilled water and 
dried. All electrical connections unplugged. Nitrile gloves are disposed of as chemical waste. Lab coat and safety 
glasses are taken off at the entrance of the laboratory. Wash hands before leaving the laboratory.
