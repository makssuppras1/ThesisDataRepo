Abstract
Male reproductive health requires adequate androgen signaling in fetal life to promote the development
of secondary male sex organs and general masculinization of the fetus. Endocrine disruption during
this critical stage of life may lead to irreversible reproductive disorders such as genital malformations,
testicular cancer, and impaired semen quality affecting the wellôÙbeing and reproductive function of
the male. This thesis examines the ability of quaternary ammonium compounds (QACs) to disrupt
androgen signaling in vitro to gain an understanding of whether this group of substances can contribute
to male reproductive disorders. This was investigated by using an androgen receptor reporter gene
assay to examine the ability of the compounds to agonize or antagonize the androgen receptor in
vitro. A selection of 15 QACs with diverse structures was included in the experiments to investigate
what chemical properties might contribute to the potential endocrine disrupting effects. The results
presented in this thesis revealed that none of the tested compounds led to effects on the androgen
receptor activity and thus, no structural traits determining of the effects could be identified. These
results suggest that QACs do not have the ability to disrupt androgen receptor signaling and that the
compounds do not contribute to male reproductive disorders through this mechanism of action.

Introduction
During the last century, growing evidence from epidemiological studies has revealed increasing rates
of endocrine related disorders. These include sexual development abnormalities, decreased fertility,
diabetes, obesity, and endocrine related cancers such as testicular, prostate, and breast cancer [1ã
7]. While some causal relationships have yet to be established, the rate of these incidences makes it
evident that environmental and lifestyle factors, rather than genetic factors, must be the most likely
contributing factors [8]. This has raised concerns about the ability of chemical compounds in the
environment to act as endocrine disrupting chemicals (EDCs). Humans are extensively exposed to
various chemicals from a multitude of sources on a daily basis, e.g. food packaging, cosmetics, pharôÙ
maceutical agents, plasticizers, pesticides, and cleaning agents, and if these chemicals can interfere
with the endocrine system there is a risk of endocrine related disorders affecting human reproduction
and development [8].
After the emergence of the COVIDôÙ19 pandemic in recent years, there has been an increase in disinôÙ
fectant practices in households, hospitals, and in the industry around the world [9]. This has resulted
in increased use of disinfectants and cleaning agents among which many contain quaternary ammoôÙ
nium compounds (QACs). These compounds have broadôÙspectrum antimicrobial properties and have
the ability to inactivate enveloped viruses such as the severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus
2 (SARSôÙCoVôÙ2) [10]. Despite the wide use of these compounds during the pandemic, only little is
known about their endocrine disruptive potential and their potential contribution to endocrine related
disorders. Consequently, there is a need for further evaluation and screening of the compounds to
investigate their potential to affect the endocrine system and subsequent endocrine related disorders.
1.1
Aim
The aim of this thesis was to gain insight into the endocrine disruptive potential of QACs and a better
understanding of whether this group of substances may contribute to male reproductive disorders. This
was investigated by the screening of 15 QACs for the potential to disrupt androgen receptor activity in
vitro. The QACs have diverse chemical structures and were selected to investigate what properties of
the chemical structure might contribute to the disruption of the androgen receptor. The generated data

form the basis for future prioritization of experiments in animals, if needed.
More specifically, the following two questions were addressed:
ãÂ What is the potential of QACs to agonize or antagonize the androgen receptor in vitro?
ãÂ What properties of the chemical structure of QACs contribute to the effects on androgen receptor
activity?

To investigate the ability of the QACs to disrupt androgen signaling in vitro, a method from the OECDôÙ

Background
This chapter will give a brief introduction to the field of endocrine disruption followed by an introducôÙ
tion to the male reproductive system and how disruption of androgen signaling during development
may lead to male reproductive disorders. It will also give insight into the chemical group of quaterôÙ
nary ammonium compounds (QACs) as well as the methods that can be used to assess the endocrine
disruptive potential of chemicals.
2.1
The endocrine system and endocrine disruption
Endocrine systems comprise networks of glands responsible for regulating almost any biological proôÙ
cess in the human body by the production and secretion of hormones. The human body contains
several specialized endocrine glands, including the pituitary gland, thyroid gland, adrenal glands, and
the gonads secreting hormones into the blood system [8]. The endocrine systems are responsible for
the regulation of several metabolic processes as well as nutritional and behavioral processes in adult
life but also for important reproductive and developmental processes in fetal life [12].
In recent years, concerns have been raised regarding the ability of environmental chemicals to act
as endocrine disrupting chemicals (EDCs). These are compounds that can interfere with endocrine
systems leading to adverse effects on the exposed individual or the offspring. The World Health
Organization (WHO) uses the following definition of an endocrine disrupter:
ãAn endocrine disrupter is an exogenous substance or mixture that alters function(s) of the enôÙ
docrine system and consequently causes adverse effects in an intact organism, or its progeny, or
(sub)populationsã [12].
A wide range of substances is suspected to possess endocrine disrupting properties including both
natural and synthetic compounds such as dioxins, phthalates, parabens, polychlorinated biphenyls,
plasticizers, and pesticides [13]. EDCs can interfere with endocrine systems through several mechôÙ
anisms of action, e.g. through estrogenic, androgenic, thyroid, and steroidogenic (EATS) modalities
[14]. Fetal life is considered particularly vulnerable to endocrine disruption due to the critical role of
endocrine signaling in reproductive development [8].

2.2
The male reproductive system
To understand how endocrine disruption may lead to male reproductive disorders, letãs have a look
at how the male reproductive system is developed under normal conditions. Normal development
requires adequate androgen signaling in fetal life which involves key processes such as sexual differôÙ
entiation of the testes as well as androgen synthesis, secretion, transport, metabolism, and androgen
receptor activity. Androgen signaling is important throughout male life and also in female reproducôÙ
tive health but the focus here will be on the role of androgen signaling in the development of the male
reproductive system in fetal life, exclusively.
2.2.1
Sexual differentiation
In the initial stage of the development of human embryos, there are no morphological differences
between the male and female fetuses [15]. The sexual differentiation of the gonads into either ovaries
or testes is initially determined by the expression of the sexôÙdetermining gene Sry encoded on the
YôÙchromosome [15]. Since females have two X chromosomes instead of an XY pair, they will not
express Sry, which allows for ovaries to develop. In the XY gonads, the presence of Sry gene induces
Sox9 expression in preôÙSertoli cells leading to the differentiation of supporting cells to Sertoli cells.
These cells have important functions in the organization of the testis cords and secrete antiôÙmû¥llerian
hormone (AMH) which prevents the development of a female reproductive tract [16]. Following the
differentiation of Sertoli cells, steroidogenic cells start to differentiate into fetal Leydig cells [15].
Leydig cells produce insulinôÙlike 3 (INSL3) mediating testicular descent and become the main site of
androgen synthesis driving the general masculinization of the fetus and the development of secondary
male sex organs [16]. This means that, while female sexual differentiation is largely independent of
estrogens and androgens, male sexual differentiation is entirely dependent on androgens for normal
reproductive development [17]. A simplified overview of the sexual differentiation of gonads into
either testes or ovaries can be seen in Figure 2.1 below.

2.2.2
Androgen synthesis and regulation
Following the sexual differentiation of the gonads into testes, the testes will initiate androgen syntheôÙ
sis. Androgens are synthesized by a sequence of enzymatic steps from cholesterol within the Leydig
cells located in the interstitial compartment of the testes [18]. This process is also termed steroidogeôÙ
nesis. The first step of steroidogenesis requires the translocation of cholesterol to the inner mitochonôÙ
drial membrane which is facilitated by steroidogenic acute regulatory protein (StAR) [19]. Here, the
first enzyme reaction occurs which involves the conversion of cholesterol to pregnenolone catalyzed
by the enzyme CYP11A. Subsequently, pregnenolone passes from the mitochondria to the smooth

endoplasmatic reticulum, where the remaining enzyme reactions occur [19]. Here, pregnenolone is
converted to androstenedione which is the precursor of testosterone. This conversion requires comôÙ
bined enzymatic actions of 3öýôÙHSD and CYP17. The final step in the androgen synthesis involves
the reduction of androstenedione to testosterone catalyzed by 17öýôÙHSD. Notably, a small percentage
of plasma testosterone is also derived from precursors of cortisol synthesis in the adrenal cortex [20].
Steroidogenesis during early pregnancy is induced by human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG) produced
by the maternal placenta. The production of hCG is estimated to peak between 8 and 12 weeks of gesôÙ
tation after which the levels begin to decline and the levels of luteinizing hormone (LH) begin to rise,
and the steroidogenesis comes under the control of the male hypothalamusôÙpituitaryôÙgonadal (HPG)
axis [19].
Following synthesis, the testosterone is released into the blood circulation by diffusing down a conôÙ
centration gradient across cell membranes into the bloodstream [18]. Small amounts of testosterone
are released into the lymphatics and tubule fluid. In the bloodstream, most testosterone is transported
bound to circulating plasma proteins such as albumin or globulin. The transfer of testosterone into
target tissues is presumed to also occur through passive diffusion across the cell membrane [18].
2.2.3
Androgen metabolism and action
After testosterone enters the target tissues, testosterone is now ready to interact and mediate its funcôÙ
tion via the androgen receptor. Testosterone may directly interact and activate the androgen receptor
or it may be converted into its more potent metabolite, dihydrotestosterone (DHT) by 5öÝôÙreductase
before the interaction [14]. The conversion of testosterone to the more potent metabolite is essenôÙ
tial for many masculinization processes to ensure sufficient androgen receptorôÙmediated responses.
The androgen receptor is part of the steroid hormone nuclear receptor family [21] and is a hormoneôÙ
inducible DNAôÙbinding transcription factor expressed in a diverse range of tissues including bone,
muscle, prostate, and adipose tissue, with the highest levels observed in male reproductive organs
[22]. In the absence of a ligand, the receptor is localized in the cytosol complexed with heatôÙshock
proteins and other chaperone proteins [21]. When a ligand binds to the receptor, it triggers a conôÙ
formational change leading to dissociation from the heatôÙshock proteins and subsequent translocation
to the nucleus. In the nucleus, the androgen receptor dimerizes and stably binds to specific DNA
sequences called androgen response elements (AREs) within target gene promoters to initiate a reguôÙ
latory cascade [22]. The ligandôÙactivated androgen receptor typically acts in cooperation with other

2.3
Male reproductive toxicity
Now that we have seen how the male reproductive system develops under normal conditions in reôÙ
sponse to adequate androgen signaling, letãs have a look at how the system may be disrupted by
chemicals leading to male reproductive toxicity. This section covers potential mechanisms of enôÙ
docrine disruption followed by the adverse effects associated with disruption.
2.3.1
Mechanisms of endocrine disruption
Compounds that disrupt the development of the male reproductive system may act by interfering with
androgen signaling during any of the processes mentioned in the previous section and may even interôÙ
fere with several of these processes simultaneously. The mechanisms of endocrine disruption can be
broadly classified into the following two categoriesëƒ (i) NonôÙreceptor mediated processes including
interference with androgen synthesis, secretion, regulation, protein transport, metabolism, or eliminaôÙ
tion to indirectly modulate androgen function, and (ii) ReceptorôÙmediated processes including direct
interference with the androgen receptor to modulate androgen function [17].
The receptorôÙmediated disruption is the focus of this thesis and can be further classified into disrupôÙ
tion by agonists and antagonists. An agonist mimics the action of a naturallyôÙoccurring androgen by
binding to and activating the androgen receptor, while an antagonist inhibits the action of naturallyôÙ
occurring androgens by binding to and blocking the androgen receptor transactivation. An antagonist
might also anchor or keep the androgen receptor in the cytoplasm [22]. A brominated flame retarôÙ
dant, tetrabromoethylcyclohexane (TBECH) is an example of a compound that has been identified as
an androgen receptor agonist in vitro [23], while diethylhexyl phthalate (DEHP) is an example of an
androgen receptor antagonist [24]. The most common mechanisms of endocrine disruption include
disruption of the androgen synthesis, metabolism, and androgen receptor activity, and examples of
chemicals affecting these targets can be seen in Figure 2.3 on the next page [23ã26].

2.3.2
Adverse effects linked to disruption
If an environmental chemical disrupts any of the aboveôÙmentioned processes during fetal life, alterôÙ
ations in the subsequent gene transcription of androgen responsive genes occur which are responsible
for the male fetus to fully masculinize. This includes genes instructing the androgen sensitive tisôÙ
sues to differentiate into male external genitalia such as the growth of the genital tubercle into a penis
and fusion of the genital swellings to form the scrotum [16]. Such disruption may in turn lead to
the development of several reproductive disorders, such as cryptorchidism, hypospadias, or impaired
semen quality [14]. Cryptorchidism is the failure of one or both testes to descend into the scrotum
and is probably the most common developmental malformation, while hypospadias is a condition in
which the urethral opening of the penis is misplaced [27]. Cryptorchidism and hypospadias is typiôÙ
cally discovered at the time of birth, but impaired semen quality does not become evident until in the
later stages of life. Despite physiological differences and the varying time of onset, these reproducôÙ

==PAGE:18==
tive disorders often occur simultaneously and have been hypothesized to share a common origin in
fetal life, termed the testicular dysgenesis syndrome (TDS) [28, 29]. This hypothesis proposes that
normal male sex differentiation and subsequent male fetal development are sensitive to disruption by
numerous causes of both genetic and environmental factors which may result in a testicular dysgenesis
syndrome contributing to increased risks of the development of one or several of the aboveôÙmentioned
reproductive disorders. The male reproductive system is especially sensitive to disruption during the
ãmale programming windowã where the masculinization of the fetus takes place which has been sugôÙ
gested to comprise 8ôÙ14 weeks of gestation in humans [30]. Disruption occurring during the male
programming window often leads to irreversible and lifelong implications for the affected individual
and, potentially, also for their offspring [8].
The problematic extent of male reproductive disorders was highlighted by Danish health statistics in
2000, where it was found that more than 40% of young adult men had subnormal sperm counts, 5ôÙ6%
of school boys had undescended testis, and almost 1% had penile abnormalities [31]. Moreover, WHO
reference values for human semen parameters were adjusted to lower values in 2010 based on semen
samples from over 4500 men from four different continents with unknown fertility status suggesting
a global trend of decreased reproductive health in males [32].
2.4
Assessment of male reproductive toxicity
Due to the growing evidence of increasing incidences of reproductive disorders worldwide, a variety
of in vivo and in vitro test methods have been developed to assess the potential of chemicals to disrupt
endocrine systems. Regarding the male reproductive system, several endpoints such as anogenital disôÙ
tance and nipple retention are used as biomarkers for endocrine disruption in rodent toxicity studies.
Anogenital distance refers to the distance between the anus and the external genitalia which is about
twice as long in newborn males than females [16]. Nipple retention refers to the detection of nipples
in male rat pups appearing as relatively small dark spots in the positions where the female nipples
are located [33]. A short anogenital distance and nipple retention in male offspring are considered
retrospective biomarkers of demasculinization linked to insufficient androgen action during the male
programming window in fetal life [33]. Other physical biomarkers include developmental malformaôÙ
tions such as hypospadias and cryptorchidism which are also linked to disruption of androgen action
and/or testis function during fetal life [14].

included in the same test guideline called ãTest Guideline No. 458 Stably Transfected Human AndroôÙ

Activity of chemicalsã from 2020 [11]. The assay performed in this thesis is part of this test guideline,
and the principle of the assay will be described in the following section.
2.4.1
Principle of the androgen receptor reporter gene assay
The androgen receptor reporter gene assay is used for the detection of potential agonistic and antagôÙ
onistic activity of chemical compounds on the androgen receptor. The principle of the test method
is based on the stably transfected ARôÙEcoScreenãÂcell line deriving from a Chinese hamster ovary
cell line with three inserted constructs: (i) the human androgen receptor expression construct enôÙ
coding the fullôÙlength human reporter gene, (ii) an androgen receptorôÙresponsive firefly luciferase
reporter gene construct for the detection of agonistic or antagonistic activity, and (iii) an androgen
receptorôÙindependent renilla luciferase reporter construct that is constitutively active under the SV40
promoter in viable cells which is used to assess the cell viability to distinguish pure antagonism from
a cytotoxicityôÙrelated decrease in luciferase activity [11].
The endpoint of the assay is the activation or inhibition of the androgen receptor which can be assessed
by measuring luminescence. If the androgen receptor is activated by an agonist, the receptorôÙligand
complex translocates to the nucleus where it activates the transcription of the firefly luciferase gene.
This leads to increased levels of the luciferase enzyme which can transform the substrate luciferin into
a bioluminescence product that can be quantitatively measured on a luminometer. This results in an
12
==PAGE:20==
increased light signal. For the detection of antagonistic activity of chemical compounds, a wellôÙknown
agonist will be added to the system to induce androgen receptor activity. Hence, if an antagonistic
chemical is added to the system, the activity of the androgen receptor will terminate leading to a
decrease in light signal. A simplified overview of the assay principle can be seen in Figure 2.4 on the
next page.
Cell viability is assessed by adding a reagent that stops the firefly reaction and activates the renilla
luciferase reaction. The renilla luciferase reporter construct is constitutively expressed in viable cells
so the generated light signal from reagent addition corresponds to the cell viability. If a decrease in
light signal is observed for exposed cells compared to controls after the addition of this reagent, this
indicates cell cytotoxicity.

2.5
Quaternary ammonium compounds
This section will introduce the chemical group of interest in this thesis, QACs. This will include an
introduction to the compounds in terms of chemistry and use as well as exposure routes and biomoniôÙ
toring. Moreover, reported in vitro and in vivo effects of the compounds related to endocrine disruption
and reproductive toxicity will be presented.
2.5.1
Chemistry and use
QACs constitute a group of cationic substances consisting of a central positively charged hydrophilic
nitrogen atom (N+) and a hydrophobic tail including four aliphatic and/or aromatic radicals [37]. QACs
also have a negatively charged anion portion, usually chloride or bromide, which is linked to the
positively charged nitrogen to form a salt [38]. The general structure can be seen in Figure 2.5.
QACs were classified into four groups by the United States Environmental Protection Agency (U.S.
EPA) in 1988 based on structural differences to build a database that will support the continued regôÙ
istration of the compounds [39]. The Group I cluster is composed of alkyl or hydroxyalkyl (straight
chain) substituted QACsëƒ the Group II cluster is composed of nonôÙhalogenated benzyl substituted
QACsëƒ the Group III cluster is composed of diôÙ and triôÙchlorobenzyl substituted QACsëƒ and the Group
IV cluster is composed of QACs with unusual substituents (i.e. charged heterocyclic ammonium comôÙ
pounds). Structural differences in the hydrophobic tails of the compounds may affect the antimicrobial
activity and solubility of the compound and may also possibly affect the bioactivity [38, 40].

hydrophobic radicals (R). The radicals are typically alkyl chains, benzyl rings, or aryl groups [40].
An anion portion (XôÙ), which is usually chloride (ClôÙ) or bromide (BrôÙ), is linked to the positively
charged nitrogen atom (N+).

The unique amphiphilic nature of QACs makes them attractive as antimicrobials with reported effects
against a range of bacteria and fungi [41, 42] including methicillinôÙresistant Staphylococcus Aureus
(MRSA) [43]. The broadôÙspectrum antimicrobial properties of QACs make the compounds ideal as
sterilizing agents for use in households, hospitals, and in the industry, and the compounds are comôÙ
monly found as active ingredients in cleaning products, disinfectants, and hand sanitizers [40, 44].
Interestingly, QACs are also able to inactivate enveloped viruses and are mentioned on the U.S. EPA
list of disinfectants for use against SARSôÙCoVôÙ2 (List N: Disinfectants for Use Against SARSôÙCoVôÙ2)
[10]. As of today, 652 products are mentioned on the list among which there are 301 products containôÙ
ing QACs as the active ingredient. QACs thus constitute the highest percentage of biocidal agents in
U.S. EPAôÙapproved disinfectant products for COVIDôÙ19. In addition to this, the antimicrobial propôÙ
erties of the compounds make them ideal in wood preservation, wastewater treatment, and pesticides
as well as for use as preservatives in many personal care products including cosmetics, body lotions,
and shampoos [16]. QACs are also used as antimicrobial agents in pharmaceutical drugs and oral care
products such as mouthwashes and toothpaste [45, 46].
The most widely used compounds include didecyldimethylammonium chlorides (DDACs) and alkylôÙ
dimethylbenzylammonium chlorides (ADBACs), which are subclasses from the Group I and Group II
cluster, respectively. The DDAC subclass consists of 5 structurally similar compounds among which
one is tested in this thesis (DDAC, CAS: 7173ôÙ51ôÙ5). The ADBAC subclass consists of 19 similar
compounds among which 3 are tested in this thesis (benzalkonium chloride, benzethonium chloride,
and alkyl(ethylbenzyl)dimethylammonium chloride). Both subclasses are registered as active ingreôÙ
dients for antimicrobial products by the U.S. EPA [47, 48]. Moreover, DDAC and ADBAC (C12ôÙ16)
are approved by the European Chemicals Agency (ECHA) for use in wood preservatives and for disinôÙ
fecting agents in human and animal hygiene products, algaecides as well as for equipment and surfaces
that may come in contact with food materials [49ã56].
Other uses of QACs include, but are not limited to, applications as surfactants, electrolytes, and antiôÙ
static agents. The antistatic properties of the compounds have made them popular in fabric softeners,
antistatic fabric sprays, and hair conditioners [57]. Due to the extensive use in commercial and houseôÙ
hold settings, the production of some QACs has exceeded 500,000 tons/year worldwide which has
placed them on the list of highôÙproductionôÙvolume chemicals by the OECD [58].

2.5.2
Exposure routes and biomonitoring
The widespread use of QACs in various settings results in daily exposure in the general population.
Human exposure can occur through dermal contact, inhalation, or oral ingestion [59]. The exposure
is often related to direct dermal contact with cleaning products, cosmetics, disinfectants, and several
personal care products or through inhalation of aerosolized droplets produced by spray products [60].
Moreover, ingestion may occur from food products that have been in contact with pesticides or disinôÙ
fected equipment. QACs have for example been detected in fruits and vegetables such as oranges and
cucumbers [61] as well as in food additives, milk, and other dairy products [62]. In addition to this, it
has been suggested that QACs may transfer from mother to child during pregnancy providing a potenôÙ
tial exposure route for the developing fetus. This suggestion came from a study that measured levels
of ADBAC in the mouse neonatal brain after maternal dietary exposure, demonstrating the ability of
the compounds to cross the bloodôÙplacental barrier and enter the developing brain in mice [63].
Small children have been suggested to be the most vulnerable group to QAC exposure from cleaning
products and disinfectants due to their frequent contact with treated surfaces and handôÙtoôÙmouth acôÙ
tivity leading to mouthingôÙmediated ingestion of the compounds [64]. Notably, an involatile nature
of the compounds gives them a tendency to persist on hard surfaces and to favor the transfer from the
surfaces to human skin which increases the exposure to small children [64].
The presence of QACs in the human body has recently been monitored in several epidemiological
studies. QACs have been detected in human blood samples in two different studies reporting the
presence of the compounds in the blood of 80% and 95% of the study participants, respectively [60,
65]. One of these studies observed a low clearance of the compounds from the liver indicating high
bioaccumulation potentials of the compounds in the body [65]. The frequency of different QACs in the
blood samples were also evaluated and it was found that ADBACs were the most frequently detected
compounds in the blood samples [65]. In a recent study from 2022, QACs were for the first time also
detected in human breast milk, and breastfeeding was identified as a major exposure pathway to QACs
for nursing infants [66].
17
==PAGE:25==
The biomonitoring of QACs during the COVIDôÙ19 pandemic has been of special interest due to the inôÙ
crease in disinfectant practices in households and various public settings, including hospitals, offices,
and childcare facilities [9]. An increase in indoor exposure to QACs during the COVIDôÙ19 pandemic
has been documented in households in the United States in 2020 [62]. The increase in concentration
was estimated to be a rise of 62% which was suggested to increase the daily intake in small children
by ten times compared to adults in the same residential units [62]. Higher concentrations were found
in households that generally disinfected more frequently suggesting a link between increased use of
disinfectants during the outbreak and increased indoor exposure to QACs. Additionally, human blood
levels of QACs have been shown to significantly increase during the pandemic [65].
2.5.3
Endocrine disruption in vitro and in vivo
QACs have been used for more than 50 years and are generally considered relatively safe by regulatory
authorities (i.e. the U.S EPA and ECHA) stating that their primary critical effects are pointôÙofôÙcontact
effects such as skin and eye irritation [47, 48, 51]. However, only little is known about their ability
to disrupt endocrine systems. The few available in vitro and in vivo studies assessing the endocrine
disruptive potential and reproductive effects of the compounds, respectively, will be presented in this
section.
An in vitro assessment of the agonistic and antagonistic properties of widely used oral care antimicroôÙ
bial substances has been performed including hexadecylpyridium chloride (also known as cetylpyriôÙ
dinium chloride which is one of the compounds tested in this thesis and which is listed in Table 3.1)
[67]. Here, the compound was found to antagonize the androgen receptor suggesting endocrine disôÙ
ruptive potential of the compound. QACs have also been shown to inhibit cholesterol synthesis. This
has been observed for ADBAC in both an in vitro and in vivo study [63, 68]. As cholesterol is an
important component in the androgen synthesis, this suggests that ADBACs may have the ability to
indirectly disrupt testosterone synthesis important for male reproductive health. In addition to this,
a human epidemiological study has also demonstrated an association between blood levels of QACs
and disruption of cholesterol homeostasis in a doseôÙdependent manner [60].
The ability of ADBAC and DDAC to affect fertility has been investigated in two animal studies by
Melin et al. [69, 70]. In the first study, mouse breeding pairs were exposed to formulations containing
mixtures of ADBAC and DDAC through their diet for six months, and their reproductive performance
was assessed in terms of pregnancy intervals, time to first litter, pups per litter, and the number of
18
==PAGE:26==
pregnancies [69]. All these parameters indicated decreased fertility following QAC exposure. In the
following year, the research group investigated the sexôÙspecific reproductive effects from these QACs
and found that female mice spent significantly less time in estrus and had reduced ovulation compared
to nonôÙexposed mice, and male mice exhibited declines in sperm concentration and motility followôÙ
ing the exposure suggesting that the observed decreased fertility was mediated through reproductive
disturbances in both male and female mice [70]. This suggests that QAC exposure may affect mouse
fertility. However, these studies have been heavily criticized for being insufficient to provide evidence
for reproductive toxicology in several publications based on the level of methodological flaws [44, 59,
71]. These findings also conflict with evaluations performed by regulatory agencies (i.e. the U.S. EPA
and ECHA) concluding no evidence of reproductive harm from ADBAC and DDAC [47ã50]. This
will be addressed further in the discussion of this thesis.
The aboveôÙmentioned reproductive effects in mice suggest endocrine disruption following adult exôÙ
posure but further studies will be needed to validate if the observed effects are indeed associated with
endocrine disrupting properties of the compounds. To my knowledge, no studies have reported alôÙ
terations in endocrine sensitive endpoints such as anogenital distance and nipple retention following
prenatal QAC exposure.
The United States Food and Drug Administration (FDA) has recently requested additional safety data
on certain QACs, including ADBAC, in medical and consumer antiseptic products [72]. In addition
to this, QACs were reported to be under consideration as potential priority chemicals by the Scientific
Guidance Panel for Biomonitoring California in March 2021 based on the degree of human exposure
to the compounds, the likelihood of the compounds being toxic based on peerôÙreviewed health data as
well as the toxicology of chemically related compounds among other criteria [73].

5
Discussion
5.1
Androgen receptor activity of QACs
In this thesis, 15 different QACs were screened for their androgen receptor activity in vitro and it
was found that none of the compounds had the ability to agonize or antagonize the androgen receptor.
Several of the QACs led to reductions in response in agonist and antagonist mode but these effects were
in all cases suggested to be cytotoxicityôÙdriven because corresponding decreases were observed in the
cell viability mode. The aim of this thesis was not only to screen for androgen disruptive potential
of QACs but also to gain knowledge on whether specific chemical structures were contributing to the
observed effects. Since none of the substances led to effects on the receptor activity, no structural
traits determining of the effects could be identified. Generally, the benzyl substituted compounds all
exhibited cytotoxicity from concentrations of 25 ö¥M and below, whereas the cytotoxicity for the other
groups of QACs investigated were more heterogeneous. The assay provided consistent results with
low standard deviations of the means, and the responsiveness of the assay was verified by significant
changes in receptor activity followed by exposure to the positive controls. The EC50 and IC50 values
of the controls were determined to be 0.15 nM and 164.3 nM, respectively, which is compliant with
previously inôÙhouse historical data (data not shown).
Only a few of the tested QACs have been tested for androgen receptor activity previously and to my
knowledge, this is the first report of lack of effect for many of the included substances. Interestingly,
however, the lack of in vitro androgen receptor activity observed for the charged heterolytic QAC,
cetylpyridinium chloride, in this thesis is in contrast with effects observed in a previous study [67].
The reason for this discrepancy remains unclear but may be partly explained by assay and cell type
differences. The study used the soôÙcalled AIZôÙAR cell line which is a human prostate carcinoma epôÙ
ithelial cell line expressing an endogenous androgen receptor instead of the ARôÙEcoScreenãÂcell line
that was used in this thesis. The different cell lines may have affected the observed outcomes as they
may react differently in terms of cell viability. Other assay differences include different compound
solvents and different agonists used in the antagonist mode. However, the true extent of the ability
of cetylpyridinium chloride to affect androgen receptor activity in vitro remains unknown and furôÙ

5.2
Test concentrations in human context
As previously mentioned, QACs have been measured in human blood and breast milk, and the meaôÙ
sured levels in the blood have been shown to increase following the COVIDôÙ19 pandemic. The tested
concentrations of the QACs varied in this thesis due to different levels of cytotoxicity but the concenôÙ
trations were generally approximately 1000ôÙfold higher than the reported levels in the blood and breast
milk from the biomonitoring studies. Since no effects were observed in androgen receptor activity at
these concentrations, it is suggested that human exposure to single QACs should not be problematic
in terms of androgen receptor disruption. To my knowledge, not all QACs included in this thesis have
been measured in humans, and the extent to which these substances are present in humans remains
unknown. They were included, however, in this thesis because of the possibility that hazard data for
these compounds might be lacking and needed. As we have seen previously, QACs have the ability
to cross the bloodôÙplacental barrier in mice which is suspected to be a possible route of exposure in
human fetal life. Nevertheless, to my knowledge, there are no reported human biomonitoring data on
measured levels of QACs in the placenta or umbilical cord. Therefore, the concentrations of QACs
reaching the developing fetus in humans remain unknown but the levels will be expected to be lower
than the measured concentrations in the blood, and fetal exposure to single QACs is suggested to be
nonôÙproblematic in terms of androgen receptor disruption.
The QACs in this thesis were tested as single compounds. However, it should be considered that
humans are likely exposed to several QACs simultaneously in concert with many other chemicals
affecting the same targets. Such mixtures of chemicals may be additive or even synergistic leading to
effects that are different from the sum of the individual components [60]. Thus, a specific compound
may seem innocuous when individually tested but may cause hazardous effects when in association
with other EDCs [63]. In order to obtain a broader picture of the chemical exposure risk to QACs,
mixtures of several QACs should be considered. However, since we did not observe any androgen
receptor activity in any of the tested compounds in this thesis, it seems unlikely that the compounds
could contribute to effects on androgen receptor activity in mixtures.

5.3
Assessment of other endocrine modes of action
As emphasized previously, the androgen signaling pathway comprises several key endocrine processes
important for male reproductive health. These included androgen synthesis, regulation, secretion,
transport, metabolism, action, and elimination which are all sensitive to disruption by EDCs. From
the results obtained in this thesis, we gained insights into the ability of QACs to affect androgen
signaling through agonism or antagonism of the androgen receptor in vitro and found that androgen
receptor disruption is unlikely to contribute to male reproductive disorders. However, the ability of
the compounds to disrupt any of the other processes remains still to be investigated. This means that
we have only covered a subset of relevant endocrine modes of action for the male reproductive system
and that we cannot evaluate the overall endocrine disruptive potential of the QACs from the results
obtained in this thesis.
To provide a broader picture of the endocrine disruptive potential of QACs affecting the male reproôÙ
ductive system, additional assays should be conducted to investigate the ability of the compounds to
disrupt any of the other aboveôÙmentioned processes. This includes for example an H295R steroidogenôÙ
esis assay to assess the ability of the compounds to affect androgen synthesis. Moreover, another assay
of relevance would be a 5öÝôÙreductase inhibition assay to test the effects on testosterone metabolismëƒ
that is, the conversion of dihydrotestosterone from testosterone, which is a much more potent ligand
for the androgen receptor and very important in male reproductive development. In fact, to elucidate
the full endocrine disruptive potential related to male reproductive health, one needs to include all relôÙ
evant endocrine modes of action as specified in the background section. To provide an even broader
picture of the endocrine disruptive potential of QACs not necessarily related to the male reproductive
system, one would also need to consider endpoints related to other endocrine systems such as estrogen
signaling, thyroid hormone signaling, and retinoic acid signaling.

5.4
Predicting in vivo outcomes from in vitro assays
The androgen receptor reporter gene assay performed in this thesis provided mechanistic knowledge
on the endocrine mode of action of QACs in vitro but the results cannot be directly extrapolated to
the complex in vivo situation in animals and humans. NonôÙanimal experiments remain constrained by
certain factors limiting the clinical translatability of the data which will be discussed in this section.
An important consideration when assessing in vitro data is that the performed assay does not account
for effects on the whole organism. This includes the toxicokinetic parameters, absorption, distribution,
metabolism, and excretion (ADME). These parameters are important for the chemical availability at
the target site and might indirectly affect the ability of the compounds to cause endocrine disrupting
effects in vivo. Other general limitations of in vitro studies include the lack of consideration of imôÙ
munological parameters, protein binding, speciesôÙtoôÙspecies differences, and feedback mechanisms.
This means that effects observed in vitro may not correspond to the effects observed in vivo. Such
an example was provided in a review by Svingen et al. from 2022 [14], where in vitro and in utero
effects of two novel antagonists, pyrifluquinazon and bisphenol C, were found to be contradicting
[77]. Pyrifluquinazon displayed weak androgen receptor antagonism in vitro but caused severe antiôÙ
androgenic in vivo effects including a short anogenital distance and nipple retention in male offspring.
On the contrary, bisphenol C was found to be a potent androgen receptor antagonist in vitro but did
not induce reproductive malformations effects in the male offspring in vivo [77]. Pyrifluquinazon
was actually found to be 380 times less potent than bisphenol C in vitro despite causing more severe
antiôÙandrogenic effects in vivo [77]. This emphasizes that translatability of in vitro data is still a maôÙ
jor challenge in current research and that the findings reported in this thesis should not be directly
translated to the complex in vivo situation.
Regarding in vitro studies related to reproductive toxicity, specifically, a major limitation is that the
studies do not consider the required complex processes to establish the adversity on human health
following chemical exposure. The studies do not consider the importance of the sensitive window
of exposure during the male programming windows which may affect the severity of the adverse
effects, nor the long time period that may occur from chemical exposure to potential adverse effects.
Additionally, in vitro reproductive toxicity studies are unable to predict associated adverse effects of
longôÙterm exposure to compounds. This makes the studies unable to predict the adversity of effects
36
==PAGE:44==
caused by many environmental chemicals to which humans are exposed on a daily basis throughout
their whole life.
Generally, extensive mechanistic knowledge of the endocrine disruptive potential of chemicals is reôÙ
quired to predict in vivo outcomes. However, the number of relevant in vitro assays is limited, and only
four endocrine related in vitro OECDôÙvalidated guidelines are so far available providing only limited
coverage of mechanisms relevant for endocrine disruption [11, 35, 36, 78]. In relation to this, in vitro
studies usually assess only one relevant mechanism of action, despite the fact that several mechanisms
may contribute to endocrine disrupting effects of single compounds. Moreover, most assays focus on
the soôÙcalled EATS modalities despite the fact that many nonôÙEATS modalities such as the HPGôÙaxis
and retinoic acid signaling are under concern for their ability to affect normal development [14]. The
current knowledge on how these nonôÙEATS modalities may contribute to in vivo effects remains limôÙ
ited which was highlighted by the OECD in 2012 [79]. This calls for additional test assays targeting
nonôÙEATS modalities and more validated endocrine related in vitro test guidelines in general.
5.5
Evidence on the endocrine disruptive potential of QACs in
literature
The results obtained in this thesis did not reveal an endocrine disruptive potential of the QACs. On
the contrary, studies from the literature have reported effects suggesting endocrine disruption and
reproductive toxicity related to the compounds. This section will discuss what is currently known and
unknown regarding the endocrine disruptive potential of the compounds from the available literature.
Generally, the amount of available data on endocrine effects of QACs is limited and the reported efôÙ
fects leave doubt about the ability of the compounds to contribute to male reproductive disorders. To
my knowledge, there are no in vitro data on the ability of the compounds to affect androgen syntheôÙ
sis, androgen metabolism, or any of the other nonôÙreceptor mediated processes of androgen signaling,
and only a few QACs have been tested for their ability to agonize or antagonize the androgen recepôÙ
tor with results that are contradicting to the results obtained in this thesis. Regarding in vivo data,
reproductive toxicity animal studies have been performed for ADBAC and DDAC which have been
submitted to and reviewed by the U.S. EPA and ECHA concluding no risk of reproductive harm from
the compounds [47ã50, 80, 81]. However, the majority of these studies are unpublished and it remains
unknown whether any of the studies have assessed endocrine sensitive endpoints such as anogenital
37
==PAGE:45==
distance and nipple retention. In addition to this, in vivo data for other subclasses than ADBAC and
DDAC could not be found. This indicates that the currently available data is insufficient to provide
evidence on the endocrine disruptive potential of the compounds. The results obtained in this study
contribute to this existing knowledge gap.
In contrast to the reproductive toxicity animal studies submitted to the regulatory authorities, two
studies by Melin et al. reported significant reductions in mice fertility after exposure to ADBAC and
DDAC which was suggested to be linked to endocrine disruption [69, 70]. These findings do not
correspond with the conclusions from the regulatory authorities nor the observed results in this theôÙ
sis. The authors claim that the results illustrate the importance of assessing the toxicity of mixtures
whose components have only been evaluated individually. However, the high level of critical methodôÙ
ological flaws and nonôÙrobust designs of the experiments, in my opinion, make them insufficient to
conclude a direct association between the compounds and reproductive toxicity. Notably, the studies
were conducted by the same research group leading to many of the same methodological flaws being
evident in the studies among which many are nonôÙcompliant with U.S. EPA guidelines [82, 83]. For
example, none of the studies provided sufficient measures of acute toxicity such as body weight which
leaves doubt whether the decreased fertility was caused by direct effects on the reproductive system
or secondary effects from general acute toxicity. One of the studies observed clinical signs such as
labored breathing and cyanosis in the female mice indicating that acute toxicity might have been eviôÙ
dent. Several other limitations exist but will not be discussed in more detail here. In my opinion, these
limitations make the studies unable to contribute to the existing knowledge gap in the literature, and
hence, there is a need for further research on the topic to provide evidence on the endocrine disruptive
potential of QACs.

5.6
Future perspectives
In light of the increasing disinfecting practices worldwide following the COVIDôÙ19 pandemic, it is
currently more relevant than ever to investigate potential toxicological effects of QACs. The experiôÙ
ment performed in this thesis is part of a larger study aiming to investigate potential endocrine modes
of action of QACs. This thesis provided knowledge on the androgen receptor activity and the potential
of the compounds to contribute to male reproductive disorders. Another endpoint of interest relevant
to the male reproductive system is the ability of the compounds to affect testosterone synthesis. Due
to time constraints, however, it was not possible to perform an H295R steroidogenesis assay within
the time period of this thesis, but this will be a relevant next step for the future. While this thesis has
focused on the male reproductive system in particular, future in vitro assays will also assess the enôÙ
docrine disruptive potential on other endocrine systems. These include an estrogen receptor reporter
gene assay, CYP19A1 inhibition assay, transthyretin displacement assay, retinoic acid reporter gene
assay, as well as retinaldehyde dehydrogenase assay. Data from these experiments in combination
with the results generated in this thesis will ultimately form the basis for future prioritization of exôÙ
periments in animals if relevant findings are revealed. These assays will contribute to the limited data
on the endocrine disruptive potential of QACs from the literature.
General challenges in predicting in vivo outcomes from in vitro assays are still evident. The is emôÙ
phasized by contradicting results from studies investigating in vitro and in vivo effects of the same
compounds. In order to enable the proper use of in vitro studies to successfully predict in vivo outôÙ
comes in the future, there is a need for additional mechanistic knowledge on the causal relationship
between measured parameters in vitro and adverse outcomes in vivo [14]. To address these challenges,
the adverse outcome pathway (AOP) framework may be a promising tool. The concept of AOPs was
introduced around 2010 and provides a simplified description of the sequence of mechanistic events
that lead to a particular toxicological effect from initial trigger to adverse outcome [84]. By continuôÙ
ing working on establishing AOPs connecting modes of action observed in vitro with specific adverse
outcomes, in vitro studies may become more robust in predictive toxicology by providing more preôÙ
cise predictions of adverse effects. This will also require the development of additional in vitro assays
in the future to provide a broader coverage of endocrine mechanisms. More robust predictions of in
vivo effects will allow for a reduction and replacement of animal experiments in accordance with the
3 Rãs principle in future toxicity testing.

6
Conclusion
The overall aim of this thesis was to gain insight into the endocrine disruptive potential of QACs
and a better understanding of whether this group of substances may contribute to male reproductive
disorders. This was investigated by the screening of 15 QACs for the potential to disrupt androgen
receptor activity in vitro through the conductance of an androgen receptor reporter gene assay. This
experiment included structurally diverse compounds to investigate what chemical properties of the
compounds could contribute to the effects on androgen receptor activity.
The results obtained in this thesis showed that none of the tested QACs had the potential to agonize or
antagonize the androgen receptor in vitro, and therefore no structural traits determining of the androgen
receptor activity were identified. The fact that none of the substances caused effects on the androgen
receptor suggests that the compounds do not contribute to male reproductive disorders through this
mechanism of action. Nevertheless, whether the compounds possess other endocrine modes of action
contributing to male reproductive toxicity remains still to be investigated. These findings in combiôÙ
nation with results from a multitude of other endocrine related in vitro assays will form the basis for
future prioritization of experiments in animals if needed. The results obtained in this thesis alone do
not suggest a need for animal studies assessing antiôÙandrogenic properties of QACs.
