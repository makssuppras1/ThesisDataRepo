Aim 
The purpose of this study is to provide further data on the prey composition of Baltic 
grey seals (Halichoerus grypus). Scat content was evaluated from a data set of 282 scats 
collected over a three-year period (2014-2016) from two separate haul-out sites located in the 
Western Baltic. The scats’ contents included primarily otoliths and other skeletal remains 
recovered from the faecal material. Atlantic cod (Gadus morhua), garfish (Belone belone), and 
Atlantic herring (Clupea harengus) were the primary prey species, with the three species 
accounting for approximately 92% of the total biomass calculated. This study will examine the 
geographical, seasonal, and yearly differentiation between the prey species in the diet, as well 
as estimate the total consumption of the primary prey species by grey seals in the Baltic Sea. 
These estimated consumption amounts will be based on the biomass estimates obtained from 
the study. It will also offer a comparison between two common methods for assessing prey 
composition: DNA analysis and scat analysis.

Introduction 
During the past few decades, the Baltic grey seal (Halichoerus grypus) population size 
has risen significantly (Graves et al. 2009). The grey seal is known to consume a range of 
species, including commercially valuable species such as cod (Gadus morhua) (Smout et al. 
2013). As grey seal numbers grow this has raised concerns over competition with fisheries 
(Westerberg et al. 2000). Therefore, understanding the diet composition of top predators, such 
as grey seals, in an ecosystem is critical to understanding how predation may influence prey 
populations. By gaining a better understanding of the Baltic grey seal’s diet, a top predator in 
the Baltic Sea, one is able to better determine the role that grey seals play in the ecosystem. 
This is particularly relevant when evaluating how they may affect commercially valuable 
species. In the Baltic Sea these include Atlantic cod, Atlantic herring (Clupea harengus), and 
sprat (Sprattus sprattus) (Sparholt, 1994). In the case of this thesis, the focus will be to provide 
data on the prey composition of Baltic grey seals, highlighting the variation between locations, 
seasons, and years.  
The Baltic Sea 
The Baltic Sea and its accompanying Gulfs of Bothnia, Finland, and Riga, are part of a 
semi-encompassed body of brackish water consisting of very low salinity, extending to around 
400,000 km2 (Hook and Johnels, 1972). The salinity of the surface water ranges from 8‰ in 
the southern waters to 3.75‰ in the north of the Gulf of Bothnia (Hook and Johnels, 1972). 
The Baltic Sea receives an influx of water from the North Sea (average salinity 35‰) through 
narrow, shallow Danish Straits and Sound (Öresund) where saline water enters the Baltic and 
mixes with freshwater from the catchment areas (Hook and Johnels, 1972). It is almost 
landlocked with many rivers discharging into the sea. This makes the Baltic Sea particularly 
vulnerable to runoff from surrounding agricultural areas, which can lead to algal blooms and 
eutrophication (Hansson and Rudtam, 1990). There is also very little tidal action in the Baltic 
Sea, instead the Baltic depends on wind action, differences in water temperature and pressure, 
and barometric pressure changes to create currents that occur at varying depths (Hook and 
Johnels, 1972).

Fish stocks in the Baltic Sea 
Due to the brackishness conditions of the Baltic Sea, the species diversity is limited to 
organisms that are adapted to the saline conditions. The brackish nature of the Baltic allows 
for a mixture of marine and freshwater species (Hansson and Rudtam, 1990). The waters 
support several valuable fisheries, including cod, salmon, sea trout, eel, herring, sprat, and 
others (Hook and Johnels, 1972). In the Baltic Sea, the main species targeted by commercial 
fisheries include cod, herring, and sprat, which makes up 95% of the total catch in the area 
(Sparholt, 1994).  
The cod stock in the Baltic Sea has two separate subpopulations - the eastern cod stock 
(Gadus morhua callarias) located east of Bornholm and up the Bothnian Bay and the Gulf of 
Finland (ICES sub divisions (SD) 25-32), while the western cod stock (Gadus morhua L.) is 
located west of Bornholm and in the Danish Belts (ICES SD 22-24) (Bleil et al., 2009). During 
the late 1980’s the eastern Baltic cod stock has been greatly reduced as the Baltic Sea 
experienced a regime shift (Möllmann et al., 2009). This shift resulted in the Baltic changing 
from predominantly cod to predominantly sprat (Möllmann et al., 2009). In recent years, cod 
abundance in the eastern Baltic has begun to increase, however the rise has been observed in 
individuals less than 30 cm while those greater than 38 cm continue to decline (Eero et al., 
2015). The decrease in the larger individuals and the decrease in the nutritional condition of 
the stock is attributed to various environmental and ecological factors (Eero et al. 2015). 
Hypothesized factors for the decline in health include: (i) low availability of prey species (Eero 
et al. 2012); (ii) increasing size of low oxygen areas affecting cod growth (Plambech et al., 
2013); (iii) rising infestation of cod by parasites (Mehrdana et al., 2014); and (iv) size 
selectivity of fisheries removing many of the large cod specimens from the stock (Svedäng and 
Hornberg, 2014, Eero et al., 2015).  
General background of the grey seal 
The grey seal is a large phocid seal that is found widely distributed throughout the North 
Atlantic Ocean, ranging from regions with cold temperate to sub-arctic waters (Olsen et al. 
2016). The grey seal can generally be found residing on rocky shores and has populations 
located on both sides of the Atlantic and in the Baltic sea (Bonner, 1971). There are three main 
geographically isolated populations that are currently recognized (Klimova et al. 2014). These 
three populations include: the Western Atlantic population that resides along the waters off the 
coast of the United States and southern Canada; the Eastern Atlantic population that is 
==PAGE:7==

primarily based off the coasts of the United Kingdom and Ireland, as well as colonies off the 
coasts of Iceland, Faroe Islands and northern mainland Europe; and finally, the Baltic 
population found in the Baltic Sea (Figure 1) (Klimova et al. 2014). Across this wide 
distribution the various populations show significant variation in genetic composition, breeding 
habitat, and skull morphology (Olsen et al. 2016). They can also be distinguished by their 
geographical isolation and variations in breeding season (Bonner, 1971). All three populations 
are currently displaying increasing trends in population size, with the total world-wide 
population estimated at approximately 632,000 individuals (Bowen, 2016).

The Baltic Grey Seal 
The Baltic grey seal is believed to have colonized the Baltic region after the last glacial 
retreat, moving north as the ice retreated and more area became available (Fietz et al. 2016). 
Over time the Eastern Atlantic and Baltic Sea populations have become morphologically and 
genetically different (Klimova et al. 2014; Fietz et al. 2016). Hence, they are considered to be 
two independent subspecies: H. grypus grypus in the Baltic Sea and H. grypus atlantica in the 
North Sea (Fietz et al. 2016). One significant difference that has established between these two 
neighboring populations is a difference in their breeding times (Fietz et al. 2016). The eastern 
Atlantic population in the North Sea breeds from November to December while Baltic 
population breeds from February to April (Härkönen et al. 2007; Fietz et al. 2016). The shift 
to a spring breeding time for the Baltic population has been attributed to the adaptation of 
breeding on sea ice (Fietz et al., 2016).  
Distribution and recolonization 
The Baltic grey seal is the largest and most abundant of the three seal species found 
within the Baltic, the other two being the ringed seal (Phoca hispida) and the harbour seal 
(Phoca vitulina) (Lundström et al. 2007). The grey seal can be found throughout the entire 
Baltic Sea, with the majority of the population found to reside above a latitude of 58° N (Hiby 
et al., 2007; Lundström et al. 2007). In the early 20th century the species had been highly 
abundant over the entire area, including the southern latitudes of the Baltic Sea (Oksanen, 
2015). However, due to anthropogenic pressures (discussed below), their numbers were greatly 
reduced and their recolonization of their former distribution areas south of 58° N has occurred 
at a much slower pace than those located further north (Figure 2) (Härkönen et al, 2013). In a 
recent report, HELCOM has determined that the distribution of grey seals in the south-western 
Baltic has not yet reached ‘good environmental status’, contrary to the populations further north 
(Figure 2) (Härkönen et al, 2017). In order to reach ‘good environmental status’ three 
parameters must be fulfilled: 1) the distribution of seals is close to ‘pristine conditions’ (e.g. 
100 years ago), 2) or where all accessible haul-out sites are occupied, and 3) when no reduction 
in the range of occupation occurs (Härkönen et al, 2017).

Nevertheless, the dispersal of grey seals has continued to move south over the past few 
decades (Figure 3). Grey seals have been observed recolonizing former haul-out sites located 
at Christiansø and Rødsand in Denmark, as well as off the Polish coast (Härkönen et al. 2017). 
The colonization process involves a specific pattern, in which new sites are visited by single 
individuals for up to ten years, with colony numbers slowly accumulating (Härkönen et al. 
2017). After a period of time a point is reached in which adult seals then begin to increase 
dramatically and pups begin to be born (Härkönen et al. 2017). This process can be observed 
in various stages at colonized sites along the North Sea coastal areas, Måkläppen in southern 
Sweden, and Rødsand in southern Denmark (Härkönen et al. 2017). In 2007, it had been 
observed that one to two pups were being born annually at Måkläppen in February and March, 
indicating that the area was in the process of recolonization (Härkönen et al. 2007). In addition, 
further south at Rødsand, in February and March 2003 and 2004, the first live grey seal pups 
(two each year) were observed, marking the return of the species breeding in Denmark (Edrén

et al. 2005). Due to its status as a seal sanctuary, Rødsand is protected from public access for 
a portion of the year and is therefore believed to be the most likely location for re-establishment 
of the grey seal population in Denmark (Härkönen et al. 2007).  
Abundance  
Overall, the grey seal population in the Baltic Sea has been increasing steadily, with 
30,285 individuals counted in 2015 (HELCOM, 2016). This number is based on annual aerial 
surveys and photographs taken of seal haul-out sites across the entire Baltic during peak 
moulting season from late May to early June (HELCOM, 2016). However, these annual census 
results should be considered to be a relative value of grey seal abundance, since weather and 
high water levels can decrease the number of seals spotted through aerial surveys. Thus, the 
actual population size is estimated to be larger. The largest abundance of grey seals was spotted 
in the Swedish Baltic proper between the Gulf of Bothnia and the northern tip of Gotland with 
9,422 individuals (HELCOM, 2016). In Danish Baltic waters, 906 individuals were identified 
(HELCOM, 2016).  
Breeding and Movement Patterns 
Grey seals gather together for breeding, moulting, and hauling out at exposed rocky 
shores (Härkönen et al, 2013). The main breeding season for Baltic grey seals occurs from 
February to March and they moult on ice and haul-out sites from April to June (Härkönen et 
al, 2013). During this moulting period, the majority of the Baltic grey seal population aggregate 
to the Northern Baltic Proper and the Archipelago Sea (Härkönen et al, 2013). However, during 
all other times of year the species distribution is less accumulated and can be found throughout 
the entire Baltic Sea (Härkönen et al, 2013). In addition, Baltic grey seals have the ability to 
travel long distances and therefore have the potential to haul out anywhere along the Baltic Sea 
(Figure 3) (Sjöberg and Ball, 2000). A study performed by HELCOM (2016) using Groupe 
Spécial Mobile (GSM) transmitters have revealed that grey seals travel and forage across the 
entire Baltic Sea, whereas harbor seals are much more stationary (Figure 4).  
Nevertheless, studies have also shown that they seem to spend much of their time 
(approximately 75%) around particular haul-out sites (Sjöberg and Ball, 2000). In the Southern 
Baltic there is only a limited number of known current haul-out sites, these include Rødsand, 
Tat, Måkläppen, and Utklippan. The haul-out sites can be identified using satellite telemetry 
revealing where the highest densities of seals are congregating (Figure 3). Additionally, grey

seals were also historically known to haul-out at sites south-west of Samsø, as well as around 
Fyn (HELCOM, 2016).

Grey seal Diet 
The grey seal is a top predator in the marine ecosystem, preying on primarily fish 
(Grellier et al., 2006; Lundström et al., 2010; Härkönen et al., 2013). Multiple studies have 
shown grey seals to be generalist feeders, consuming a wide range of species depending on 
prey availability, the geographical area, and the season (Prime and Hammond, 1990; Ampela 
and Ferland, 2006). The grey seal is known to be a primarily benthic forager, feeding on a large 
variety of fish, including both benthic and demersal species (Härkönen et al. 2013; Vincent et 
al. 2016). Additionally, they have also been found to feed on smaller quantities of cephalopods 
and crustaceans (Bonner, 1971). An adult seal is estimated to consume approximately 4.5 kg 
of fish per day (Eero et al. 2011). However, this can depend on what the seal is eating, for 
example due to the high energy content in sandeel, only an average of 4 kg is required per day 
compared to an average of 7 kg of cod (SCOS, 2009). 
The diet of the Baltic grey seal typically consists of around 20 prey species (Eero et al. 
2011). Kroner (2016) found fish 23 taxa in the south-western Baltic grey seals diet, while 
Scharff-Olsen (2015) identified 9 taxa. The diet varies when comparing the grey seals residing 
in the Baltic proper to those further north in the Bothnian Sea and Bay. In the Baltic proper 
55% of a grey seals’ diet consists of clupeoids, with the most common prey species comprising 
of Baltic herring, sprat and cod (Eero et al. 2011). While in the Bothnian Sea, clupeoids make 
up 70% of the diet, with Baltic salmon, herring, and sea trout making up the most common 
prey items (Eero et al. 2011). Multiple prey species consumed by Baltic grey seals are 
commercially valuable species such as cod and herring.  
 
Due to the fact that grey seals repeatedly return to rest at certain haul-out sites, their 
access to prey far offshore is limited, as they must remain in the vicinity of these sites (Smout 
et al. 2013). This results in limitations on the prey available to them, as indicated in studies that 
reveal a relation in seal diets to prey abundances near haul-out sites (Prime and Hammond, 
1990; Hammond et al., 1994).  
Anthropogenic Influence 
The lifecycles of many seal species are currently affected by multiple anthropogenic 
influences and pressures. Interactions between humans and marine mammals can be 
categorized into two categories: direct and indirect. In the case of the Baltic grey seal, direct 
interactions include hunting, culling, bycatch in fishing gear, and the destruction of fish and 
gear by seals (Vincent et al. 2016). While indirect interactions include environmental 
==PAGE:13==
13 
contamination, the competition for shared resources between the seals and the fishermen, and 
the impacts of climate change (Vincent et al. 2016). Grey seals were once highly abundant in 
the entire region of the Baltic Sea (Hiby et al. 2007). However, the population in the Baltic has 
experienced fluctuations and serious declines in the population over the past centuries. This 
can be greatly attributed to anthropogenic interactions such as unrestricted hunting and 
exposure to environmental toxins (Lundström, 2012).  
Exploitation: hunting and culling  
Grey seals have been hunted and exploited in the Baltic Sea for over 10,000 years 
(Härkönen et al. 2005). In fact, the colonization of the coastal areas around the Baltic was 
greatly perpetuated by the hunting of seals and salmon (Harding and Härkönen, 1999). Sealing 
remained very important for the local economies in the Baltic from 1300 AD to 1800 AD, 
making the Baltic one of the most significant producers of seal oil, which had been used in the 
production of leather, soap, and paint in Europe (Harding and Härkönen, 1999). However, 
during the end of the 19th century, cheaper alternatives to seal oil became available, resulting 
in a shift in the view of seals as a product to a competitor by the local fishermen (Harding and 
Härkönen, 1999).  
The estimated population size during the early 20th century was approximately 100,000 
seals (Harding & Härkönen, 1999). As grey seals came to be viewed as a competitor they fell 
victim to culling campaigns (Fietz et al. 2016). For example, in the Skagerrak, Kattegat and 
the Baltic Sea, the grey seal population was reduced by 90% due to a coordinated culling 
campaign between the neighboring nations (Fietz et al. 2016). This resulted in the elimination 
of grey seals from Danish waters, as well as along the Baltic coasts of Poland and Germany, 
during the 1930’s (Harding & Härkönen 1999; Fietz et al. 2016). The size of the Baltic grey 
seal population rapidly declined from an estimated 90,000-100,000 individuals in the 
beginning of the 1900’s to about 20,000 individuals in the 1940’s due to substantial culling 
efforts (Figure 5) (Harding et al. 2007). However, in 1974 a ban on hunting was introduced 
(Harding et al. 2007).  
Environmental Contaminants  
During the end of the 1960’s, studies reveal that the Baltic Sea had become seriously 
contaminated with DDT and PCB pollutants discharged from industrial coastal areas (Jensen 
et al. 1969). Studies found that these toxins caused low fertility rates and reduced reproduction 
rates. This has been suggested to be a major contributor to the decline of the Baltic grey seal 
==PAGE:14==
14 
population during that period, decreasing the population to fewer than 4,000 individuals (Hook 
& Johnels, 1972). The main cause for the decline was reproductive failure as a result of 
occlusions and stenosis of the uterus in the female seals (Bergman, 1999).  
In the 1970’s and 1980’s most countries bordering the Baltic placed a ban of these 
toxins, which resulted in the steady recovery of the population (Lundström et al, 2007). A 20-
year study performed by Bergman (1999), from the period 1977-1996, revealed that the 
decrease in DDT and PCB pollutants in the Baltic Sea has resulted in a positive trend in seal 
health. The prevalence of uterine obstructions decreased from 42% to 11%, while the 
prevalence of pregnancies increased from 9% to 60% (Bergman, 1999). However, the study 
did find an increasing prevalence of colonic ulcers in young seals (1-3 years old), with 
perforating colonic ulcers being the second leading cause of death (7%), second to drowning 
in fishing gear (72%) (Bergman, 1999). The increase in colonic ulcers, despite the fact that 
concentrations of known contaminants have decreased, may indicate that “new” or increased 
amounts of unidentified toxic contaminants are present (Bergman, 1999). These may include 
polybrominated diphenyl ethers (PBDE) which are used as flame retardants (Sellström et al. 
1993) or bis (4-chlorophenyl) sulfone (BCPS) which is used to produce high-temperature 
polymers (Olsson & Bergman, 1995). However, knowledge of the impacts of these compounds 
is limited and requires further study.

Management of the Baltic Grey Seal 
The grey seal gained protected status in Denmark in 1967, with any disturbance or 
hunting of the species strictly banned (Härkönen et al., 2007). In 1992 the grey seal was listed, 
along with the ringed seal, as an annex II species in the EU Habitat Directive, which dictates 
that conservation areas should be set aside to maintain their vital habitat (Oksanen, 2015). Grey 
seals have also been included in the Baltic Sea Action Plan of the Baltic Marine Environment 
Protection Commission (HELCOM BSAP) with the aim to achieve good ecological status in 
the Baltic (HELCOM, 2013B). Due to these protections sanctuaries have been created. 
However, the majority of the protected areas are aimed at protecting terrestrial haul-out sites 
along the Baltic Sea. Little focus has been placed on foraging areas which are more difficult to 
identify but where much of the interactions with fisheries occur (Oksanen, 2015). With the help 
of these protections, the average annual growth rate of the Baltic grey seal population has been 
approximately 7.5% annually during the last decade (Eero et al. 2011). The grey seal has also 
been listed as ‘least concern’ on both the IUCN and HELCOM Red Lists, with no future threats 
listed (IUCN, 2008; HELCOM, 2013).  
Interactions and conflicts with fisheries 
Damage to gear and landings 
Over the past several decades, due to the rise in population size, the damage to catch 
and fishing gear in the Baltic due to seals has increased greatly, particularly for the Swedish 
coastal fisheries (Lunneryd et al., 2003). In 1964 seals were estimated to cause damage to 
Swedish fishing gear estimated at 41,000 Swedish crowns and catch of 401, 000 SEK 
(Sönderberg, 1971). This damage to the fishery was estimated to be 1-2% of the entire catch 
landed annually along the Swedish east coast (Sönderberg, 1971). Since the 1960’s, the value 
of estimated damage caused by seals has risen dramatically.  
Of the three seal species residing in the Baltic, the grey seal is known to cause the 
majority of the seal-induced damage to fishing gear (Lunneryd et al., 2003). Due to the current 
rise in grey seal population numbers in the Baltic Sea, the conflict between seals and coastal 
fisheries has only escalated (Königson et al. 2007). In 1999, the damage caused by seals in 
Sweden was estimated to be greater than $3.5 million US dollars (Westerberg et al. 2000; 
==PAGE:16==
16 
Lundström et al. 2007). In 2004 that number rose, with the annual cost of seal damage to fishing 
gear and catch estimated to be greater than $5 million US dollars (Westerberg et al. 2008).  
Fisheries that have had considerable conflict with grey seals include the coastal 
fisheries located in the Swedish Baltic, where fishing operations often consist of a single 
fisherman in a small boat (Königson et al. 2007). Between 2000 and 2005 the interactions 
between grey seals and the Baltic gillnet fishery for herring (Clupea harengus) was 
investigated, using 3 data sources: EU official logbooks, voluntary fisherman logbooks, as well 
as field studies (Königson et al. 2007). This was done in an attempt to investigate the interaction 
between fishermen and seals. Figure 6 reveals the relative frequency of seal interactions with 
herring gillnet fisheries by taking the average over several years. The results from all three data 
sources reveal a significant negative impact of grey seals on herring fisheries in the Baltic, with 
at least 30% of the reports from the EU official logbook claiming seal interaction and 60% 
from the voluntary logbook (Königson et al. 2007). Grey seals have also greatly affected the 
Swedish set trap fishery for salmonoids (Salmo salar, Salmo trutta, Coregonus lavaretus) in 
the northern Baltic (Lunneryd et al., 2003). The grey seal enters the traps through the main 
entrance, damaging both the catch and the gear’s netting (Lunneryd et al., 2003). It has been 
estimated that over half of the capable catch of this fishery is lost to seals (Lunneryd et al., 
2003).

Increasing prevalence of parasites in the Baltic cod 
The infection of larvae from parasitic seal worms (Pseudoterranova decipiens), also 
known as cod worm, in commercial fish stocks has become an increasing concern in the Baltic 
Sea. Due to the increase in grey seal abundance and their role as final hosts for these parasites, 
several studies suggest that grey seals are causing the increasing rate of infection of commercial 
species such as cod (Buchmann and Kania, 2012; Lunneryd et al. 2015).  
Pseudoterranova decipiens are anisakid nematodes that go through a complex lifecycle, 
with one free-living stage and three host stages required for it to fulfill maturation (Lunneryd 
et al. 2015). The eggs are first released in the faeces of a seal and sink to the sea bottom where 
they hatch into free-living larvae (Lunneryd et al. 2015). The parasitic larvae are then ingested 
by their first host: crustaceans and polychaetes, which are then eaten by a range of marine fish 
(Buchmann & Kania, 2012). In the fish the larvae travel from the stomach of the fish to the 
mussel tissue (Lunneryd et al. 2015). The seals then become the final host by ingesting the 
infected fish and their stomach is the pivotal location of the final two moults, where the worm 
obtains maturity (Buchmann & Kania, 2012). The lifecycle is then completed with the eggs 
released through the seals’ faeces.  
This anisakid nematode is found worldwide, including the North Sea, East and West 
Atlantic, and Antarctic waters (Buchmann & Kania, 2012). Previously the parasite had been 
rarely found in the Baltic cod population (Myjak et al. 1994). However, studies have found that 
in recent years the occurrence of cod worm has increased significantly in the Baltic Sea. A 
study by Lunneryd (1991) found 1,777 nematodes from 165 harbour seals found in the 
Skagerrak, Kattegat, and southern Baltic. Of these cod worm (Pseudoterranova decipiens) was 
the most identified species, making up 71.0% of those identified (Lunneryd, 1991).   
The increasing prevalence of the cod worm is of high concern for fishermen. The 
presence of nematodes in fish can cause several illnesses in humans if the fish is consumed raw 
or undercooked, including a disease called anisakidosis (Nadolna and Podolska, 2014). The 
parasite also economically impacts fishing industries as the larvae destroys the quality of the 
fillet and increases the labour cost for filleting (Lunneryd et al. 2015). With the grey seal 
population in the Baltic expected to continue to increase, the prevalence of parasitic worms 
infecting commercially valuable fish stocks is also expected to rise (Lunneryd et al. 2015).

Various Methods for Diet Analyses 
Molecular Analysis 
Genetic analysis of the prey remains from faecal material is one method that can be 
used to identify the consumed prey species for marine mammals, such as grey seals. The DNA 
could also be collected from blood or saliva etc. DNA techniques for identifying prey from 
scats requires specific detection of DNA sequences that are distinctive to the species being 
identified (Casper et al. 2007). The advantage of this method is that it provides results that are 
independent of the bony remains (Lundström et al. 2012). Thus species that do not leave behind 
hard part remains, or prey remains that are fragile and/or highly digested can be identified 
(Casper et al. 2007). This method also allows for the potential to identify a wide variety of prey 
(Jarman et al. 2006). However, there are also several disadvantages for using this method, as it 
does not give information regarding the size of the prey or the number of prey consumed 
(Lundström et al. 2012). It is difficult to quantify the biomass of different prey items since one 
cannot accurately relate it to the number of prey DNA molecules recognized in the scats 
(Deagle et al. 2010).  
Stomach Content Analysis 
A commonly used method to examine the diet of the grey seals is by examining the 
stomach contents of animals found dead, killed during fishing operations, or hunted 
(Lundström et al. 2007). This method measures the hard parts, such as otoliths as well as 
accounts for the volume of prey remains found in stomach.  
Otoliths are found in the inner ear of the bonefish (teleostes), in the part called the maze, 
and is the body in the fish that detects gravity and acceleration (Härkönen, 1986). Fish have 
three otoliths in each ear: the lapillus, asteriscus and sagitta (Härkönen, 1986). The sagitta is 
the largest of the three otoliths and it is shaped differently in different species and therefore 
used for determination (Härkönen, 1986). Species are determined on the basis of the visual 
characteristics of the otolith, such as the shape of, for example, rostrum, antirostrum, sulcus, 
cauda, and ostium. (see Appendix 1). Otoliths consist mainly of calcium (CaCO3) and 0.2% - 
10% protein (Härkönen, 1986). They grow in line with the growth of the fish, which makes it 
possible to estimate the fish size based on the width and length of the otolith (Härkönen, 1986). 
There are several biases that occur when utilizing this method. Seal stomachs can be 
found to be empty, as studies such as Rae (1968) found 45% of grey seal stomachs from the 
study were found empty when sampled. Partial or complete digestion of the otoliths can also 
==PAGE:19==
19 
bias the study as prey items which remain longer in the stomach can be overestimated while 
those that have a quick digestion rate can be underestimated. However, the ratio of eroded 
otoliths is usually lower when performing stomach analysis compared to faecal analysis. 
Faecal Analysis 
Faecal content analysis has been a popular method for analyzing and reconstructing the 
diets of seals and it has been used in numerous studies conducted around the world (Prime and 
Hammond, 1990; Bowen and Harrison, 1994; Ampela and Ferland, 2006; Bowen and Harrison, 
2006). Scat analysis is an extremely useful tool for examining mammalian diets because it is 
non-invasive, low-cost, and allows for the collection of large sample sizes (Bowen, 2000). 
Unlike stomach analysis, it is able to provide a wide array of information while generating a 
low level of disturbance and being relatively easy to collect. This method analyzes the hard 
parts of prey remains in the faeces which are then used to reconstruct the prey species type, 
prey size and length, and the number of prey consumed (Ampela and Ferland, 2006). Fish 
remains that allow for identification include sagittal otoliths, vertebrae, cranial bones and other 
skeletal parts (Ampela and Ferland, 2006).  
However, despite the common use of this method, estimating species composition and 
the size of the prey from recovered otoliths and other hard parts also has potential biases. One 
of the main sources for the bias is to the erosion caused by the digestion of the otoliths and 
bony remains. Otoliths become partially digested when they pass through the seal’s stomachs. 
Each prey species will have a different digestion rate. Some species such as herring and sprat 
have very small and fragile otoliths than can become completely digested. In addition, prey 
species without otoliths or very small otoliths will be under-represented. Thus, due to the 
differing rates of digestion among the prey species, scat analysis may be biased towards species 
with large and robust hard parts (Bowen, 2000). Another cause for uncertainty when using this 
approach, it that the composition of the diet of grey seals living near coastal mainland areas, 
where the scats are largely collected, may differ substantially from those residing offshore 
(Bowen and Harrison, 1994).   
In addition, to be able to use otoliths in feeding analysis, it requires that the piscivorous 
animal being studied ingests the whole fish or at least the head. It has been found that some 
seals may avoid eating the head of large fish, depending on the species of seal and the 
composition of the fish stocks in the area (Härkönen, 1986). If the head is discarded to a large 
degree then the study can become heavily biased. However, there are only a limited number of 
==PAGE:20==
20 
studies examining grey seals feeding habits in the wild. One example by Sönderberg (1971) 
found that grey seals often fragment large fish such as salmon, and only pieces are eaten.  
Partial or complete digestion of otoliths 
Studies have found that otoliths and eye balls (coagulated lenses) are able to resist 
digestion the most when compared to other bony part remains (Härkönen, 1986). However 
partial or complete digestion of otoliths does occur and can highly bias the estimated prey size 
distribution, as well as the diet composition (Lundström et al. 2007). For example, complete 
digestion of otoliths can create erroneous values in the estimated number of prey consumed, 
while partial digestion can create miscalculations when calculating the fish size based on the 
eroded otoliths. In order to combat this, multiple studies have created captive feeding 
experiments in order observe the reduction in otolith size and thereby calculate the digestion 
rate of various otoliths. Prime (1979) found up to 86% of the otoliths fed to a captive harbor 
seal, in which a majority were reduced in size and the remaining 14% completely digested. 
Since otoliths can be quickly digested or the head may not be ingested, it is particularly 
essential to identify the bones and other identifiable hard parts as well.

Background of this study 
This thesis arose from four years of collaborative work examining the diets of grey 
seals (Halichoerus grypus) implemented by the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) 
National Institute of Aquatic Resources, University of Copenhagen (KU), and the Swedish 
University of Agricultural Sciences (SLU). The collaboration has allowed for the collection of 
approximately 600 scat samples from three separate locations at present date. The focus of this 
thesis will be to use a portion of the data, collected from 2014 to 2016, and examine the 
regional, seasonal, and yearly variation in the diets of grey seals. This thesis will use the 
traditional approach of identifying otoliths and other hard part remains found in faeces 
collected from two haul-out sites. It will also compare the results found through this method to 
a previous Master’s thesis in which DNA analysis was performed on the same scat samples 
(Kroner, 2016). This previous study was performed by Anne-Mette Kroner from the University 
of Copenhagen.

Materials and Methods 
Sampling location 
This study has used the method of analyzing the prey remains found in grey seal faecal 
scats. This study analyzed 282 scats collected from haul-out sites located on the island of Tat 
(Christiansø), Denmark (N=103) and Måkläppen (Falsterbro), Sweden (N=179) in the years 
between 2014 and 2016. Scats were collected every month, except for December. Therefore, 
months were combined according to season: winter (December, January, February), spring 
(March, April, May), summer (June, July, August), and autumn (September, October, 
November). The samples were collected in cooperation with the Swedish University of 
Agricultural Sciences (SLU). The sites were accessible by boat (Tat) and by foot (Måkläppen) 
from the mainland at low tide. The faecal material was collected by hand and put in separate 
labelled plastic bags and frozen at -20°C until processed.  
Måkläppen (ICES SD 24) is a sandbar located on the Falsterbro peninsula in southern 
Sweden. It is a popular haul-out site for grey seals and harbour seals. In 2007, it had been 
observed that one to two grey seal pups were being born annually at Måkläppen in February 
and March, indicating that the area was in the process of recolonization.  
Tat (ICES SD 25) is a small uninhabited rocky island that is 1.1 kilometers northwest 
of the island of Christiansø. It is a member of an island archipelago called Ertholmene that is 
located approximately 20 km northeast of the island of Bornholm. It is approximately 100 
meters long and 75 meters wide at its greatest width. It is a common resting place for grey seals 
moving throughout the Baltic. The waters surrounding Tat are also a known spawning location 
for Baltic herring (Aniansson and Hägerhäll, 2007).  
Seal species determination 
The first 23 scats from Tat and all scats from Måkläppen were confirmed to come from 
grey seals using Sanger DNA sequencing. The rest of the scats from Tat were assumed to be 
from grey seals as they are the only species present. These results are from Kroner (2016).  
Bony hard part extraction 
Before analysis each scat sample was thawed at room temperature. They were then left 
in a jar filled with water in order the help break down the material and to allow for easier 
==PAGE:23==
23 
disintegration. Each sample was then washed under running water through a number of sieves 
decreasing in size from 1 mm to 0.25 mm to extract the bony hard parts of the prey remains 
from the faeces. The otoliths were removed from the sieve as soon as possible to minimize any 
possible erosion or further damage and stored dry for further identification. The hard parts 
collected were then stored in polythene bags. Of the 282 faecal samples, 225 contained food 
remains. The most frequent remains that were collected include fish otoliths, vertebrae, and 
eye lenses. Parasites were also retained and frozen for later identification. 
Diet Analysis  
The bony hard parts of the prey collected from the scats, such as otoliths and vertebrae, 
were collected and identified to the lowest taxon possible. Of the otoliths recovered, only 
sagittae were found, counted, and measured. All otoliths were identified to the prey species, 
when possible. This was done using the published otolith identification guide “Guide to the 
Otoliths of the Bony Fishes of the Northeast Atlantic” by Tero Härkönen (1986). In order to 
identify the premaxillae and vertebrae, the published guide by Watt et al.  (1997) titled “Guide 
to the Identification of North Sea Fish Using Premaxillae and Vertebrae” was used. 
Subsequently, the number of individual otoliths and other bony parts was counted and grouped 
into species or family. To estimated the number of prey in each scat sample, I looked at each 
scat individually. If the otoliths recovered were very broken, for example only the tips of 
otoliths recovered, they were not counted as they were difficult to identify to a taxon and/or be 
measured. Unmatched otoliths were counted as a single prey, while two otoliths with the same 
size and width were counted as a single prey. If the otolith was broken in half and both halves 
clearly matched each other, the otoliths were counted as one individual. If they were not 
matched they were counted as two individuals. When calculating the number of individuals 
from vertebrae and premaxillae, if they were located in a scat with otoliths of the same species 
they were not counted. If there were no otoliths matching the same species in the scat, a 
vertebral column was used to represent 1 fish and 1 premaxil represent 0.5 fish (Pierce and 
Boyle, 1991). When identifying garfish, 15 or less vertebrae was identified as 1 fish, as 
estimated from images of garfish vertebral columns.  
In order to estimate the prey size, the otoliths were measured and converted to their 
estimated size pre-consumption. To measure the length and width of the otoliths, a Leica 
microscope was used to capture photographs of each of the otoliths (see Appendix 2). Then an 
image analyzer (ImageJ) was used to measure the length (OL) or width (OW) of the otoliths. 
==PAGE:24==
24 
The otolith length was measured from the anterior tip of rostrum to the posterior edge through 
the center (nucleus) of the otolith. This was done when possible, as multiple otoliths were found 
broken at the tips. Otolith maximum width was measured perpendicular to the length. All 
identifiable otoliths were measured and identified, equaling a total of 343 otoliths. A small 
number of otoliths (n = 33) were too eroded or broken to be identified and they were therefore 
impossible to classify, as well as to measure.  
In order to take into account the size reduction of the otoliths due to digestion, size and 
numerical correction factors were used (SCFs and NCFs) (Tables 1 & 3). Since digestion is not 
equal for each of the various prey species, use of the correct species-specific digestion 
correction factor is essential (Bowen, 2000).  
Erosion Class Identification 
All recovered otoliths were examined and classified by the degree of digestion they had 
encountered based on their external morphological features. I classified all of the identified 
species otoliths, except sandeel, into three erosion classes based on the otolith morphology and 
degree of lobation, as defined by Lundström et al. (2007).  
 
•! Class 1: otoliths are in pristine condition with a very low amount of erosion and clearly 
defined lobation.  
•! Class 2: otoliths are moderately digested and show evidence of erosion with partial, 
less pronounced lobation.  
•! Class 3: otoliths are highly digested and eroded and reveal no evidence of lobation with 
surfaces smooth.  
 
Due to the small and less defined nature of sandeel otoliths, I classified the otoliths into two 
erosion classes, as performed by Tollit et al. (1997).  
 
•! Class 1: otoliths reveal the characteristic oval shape and the pointed rostrum is clearly 
visible.  
•! Class 2: otoliths lack the characteristic oval shape and the rostrum is not discernable.  
 
Due to the long erosion exposure time the otolith faces when passed through the 
gastrointestinal tract, there were no otoliths identified as Class 1. Only erosion Classes 2 and 3

Diet Indices 
Four different diet indices were used to analyze the prey species composition found in 
the scats. Frequency of occurrence (FOi) of prey species i was calculated as the number of seal 
scats containing species i (si) divided by the total number of seal scats containing prey remains 
(st). I calculated the FOi  using both otoliths only (A) as well as all hard part structures (B). 
 
(1) 
FOi = (si/st)  x 100 
 
The relative numerical contribution (Ni) for a prey species was calculated as the number 
of individuals of the prey species in relation to the total number of individuals of all species. ni 
signifies the total number of individuals of taxon i and nt represents the total number of 
individuals of all taxa.  
(2)  
Ni  = (ni /nt) x 100 
 
In addition, the relative biomass contribution (Bi) of a prey species was also calculated. 
It reveals the total weight of the prey species in relation to the total weight of all species. bi 
signifies the total weight of taxon i and bt represents the total weight of all the taxa. 
 
(3) 
Bi = (bi/bt) x 100

Fish Abundance  
To relate the diet of Baltic grey seals to the availability of potential prey, I obtained 
data regarding the catch per unit of effort (CPUE) of various length classes of the main prey 
species from DATRAS (the Database of Trawl Surveys). CPUE is defined as the catch of fish 
in numbers, or weight, caught during a defined period of effort. In the case of this database the 
data was in numbers caught in one hour. The CPUE is used in this study as an indirect measure 
of the relative abundance of the target species. The database DATRAS is composed of data 
collected from primarily bottom trawl surveys coordinated by ICES expert groups. The data 
used from this database were collected for cod (Gadus morhua) from two separate Baltic Sea 
sub-divisions, 24 and 25, and averaged over the years 2012 to 2017.

Discussion 
Sources of error and bias 
It is important to point out that while scat analysis is a valuable and important method 
in examining the diets of grey seals, some caution must be applied when making conclusions 
about their diet composition and size of their prey. Studies investigating the diets of pinnipeds 
based on the contents of their scats are subject to several sources of error and bias.  
Prey size 
A large proportion of the bias is caused by the erosion of the otolith and the other hard 
parts used in the analysis, due to its presence in the gastro-intestinal tract. This reduces the size 
of the otoliths to varying degrees depending on the prey species and size of the otolith. In order 
to reduce the possible bias in prey size, previous studies have applied size correction factors in 
an effort to make up for the erosion of otoliths due to digestion (Prime and Hammond 1990, 
Tollit et al. 1997, Lundström et al. 2007, Lundström et al. 2010). This was done through SCFs 
calculated as the ratio of mean eroded otolith size to mean un-eroded otolith size from captive 
feeding studies (Tollit et al. 1997). However, if the SCFs calculated were too large or too small, 
they may result in overestimating or underestimating the size and the weight of the species. 
For example, in this study a size correction factor of 1.8 for the otolith width of an erosion class 
3 cod otolith, determined by Grellier and Hammond (2006) through a feeding study using grey 
seals, may have been too large and overestimated the size and weight of the cod. The option of 
using SCFs from a study done on harbor seals was considered, but due to the differences in 
physiology and digestive strategies between the two species, this approach would have 
introduced additional bias (Wilson et al. 2013) 
Published species-specific otolith size correction factors (SCFs) were available for all 
prey species found in this study, however three different studies were required to cover all six 
species and all erosion classes. The use of three different studies could have introduced bias as 
all three had different conditions for the feeding trials used to calculate the SCFs. According 
to Andersen et al. (2007), it may introduce bias if the digestion coefficients are derived from 
carrier experiments, in which a “carrier,” such as a herring, is used to feed a number of otoliths 
from other species. Grellier and Hammond (2005) found that digestion coefficients from carrier 
experiments tend to result in higher digestion coefficients, compared to in situ experiments 
which replicates feeding behavior in wild populations. From the sources used in this study, 
==PAGE:49==
49 
Tollit et al. (1997) used carrier species to deliver the otoliths to the grey seals. The use of size 
correction factors obtained from this study may have introduced some level of bias, which 
could have had an effect on the size distribution of prey.  
The application of SCFs has a tendency to move the distribution of prey consumed 
towards larger individuals (Lundström et al. 2007). In order to offer a comparison, this study 
has also calculated the fish length without applying the size correction factor. It can be assumed 
that the actual fish lengths would be higher than the length estimated without the SCF applied 
but lower than those calculated from SCFs obtained from captive feeding experiments.  
Diet composition estimation 
Another source of error includes the complete erosion of hard remains that results in a 
reduced recovery rate of more susceptible, faster-eroding hard parts. This can create an 
underestimation of prey species with small and/or fragile otoliths (Tollit et al. 1997). In order 
to minimize this source of error this study used other hard prey remains, in addition to otoliths, 
to identify prey presence. If this had not been performed, garfish would not have been found 
to constitute a large portion of the grey seal diet. To further reduce bias, this study also applied 
published species-specific NCFs to the number of otoliths identified for each species in order 
to compensate for the probability of complete digestion of some of the otoliths. The use of 
NCFs primarily increased the presence of species with small otoliths, such as sandeel and 
herring, which would have been more susceptible to complete erosion in the seal’s 
gastrointestinal tract.  
Additionally, it is also possible that a seal may not ingest the heads of certain fish 
species. For example, when the seal feeds on larger fish such as cod or salmon, they may only 
take bites from the flesh and leave the head behind (Lunneryd, 2001). This leads to an 
underestimation of the larger prey species and the results will therefore be biased towards 
smaller size classes. However, it is not possible to determine the extent of this source of error 
in the case of grey seals due to limited observations regarding feeding behavior. Additionally, 
one is not able to identify the sex, age, or other characteristics of the seals whose scats had been 
collected, which could have an effect on the variation in diet composition. 
 
 
==PAGE:50==
50 
Collection of scat samples 
Another source of bias in this study is the uneven collection of samples across the 
seasons, locations, and years. There was a significantly higher number of scats collected in the 
spring, compared to the other seasons. There was also a higher number of scats collected at 
Måkläppen, compared to Tat. Therefore, this study is unable to say for certain if a higher 
occurrence or biomass of a species in the spring, for example, was due to the seal’s predation 
or due to the number of scats collected. In order to combat this, the frequency of occurrence 
was used as the primary method to compare the locations and seasons, as it is not affected by 
the number of scats collected.  
Additionally, the samples collected at Måkläppen may contribute to a slight bias in 
relation to seasonal variation. This is due to the fact that scats located at Måkläppen are located 
at a higher elevation and are not necessarily exposed to the tide. Additionally, the scats at 
Måkläppen were collected approximately every couple of months, on average. Therefore, the 
scats may have been left by the seal weeks to months before being collected. This bias is not 
present at Tat however, as the haul-out site is very close to the tidal action and therefore the 
scats would be swept away within days.  
Secondary Predation 
Large fish, such as cod, prey on smaller fish and therefore it is likely that the otoliths 
of small prey species discovered in seal scats may come from the stomachs of larger fish. These 
prey items can also be called secondary prey. In the Baltic Sea, common prey species of cod 
include sprat, herring, and sandeel (Neuenfeldt and Beyer, 2006; Ojaveer, 2017). This bias 
cannot be accounted for as it is difficult to definitively say if the identified species had come 
from another prey or had been consumed directly by the seal. However, studies have found that 
even in extreme cases, the impact of secondary prey on the assessment of the diet composition 
is marginal- less than 1% (Hammond and Grellier, 2005).  
Overall findings 
The general findings of this study, based solely on the hard parts identified, reveals that 
the diet of the grey seal in the Baltic Sea is not as varied as previous studies have found, with 
only 7 prey taxa identified. This may be due to various reasons, as previously discussed above. 
However, this study does allow for the main prey taxa to be accounted for and their length and 
biomass to be estimated. The main prey species identified in this study were identical to those 
==PAGE:51==
51 
identified to be the primary prey through previous evaluation using DNA analysis (Kroner, 
2016). These prey species include Atlantic cod (Gadus morhua), which in the previous study 
had a frequency of occurrence (FOi) of 64.8%, garfish (Belone belone) with an FOi of 38.6%, 
and Atlantic herring (Clupea harengus) with an FOi of 37.24% (Kroner, 2016). Excluding 
garfish, these results are not surprising as cod and herring make up a majority of the biomass 
of Baltic fish communities (HELCOM, 2017). Garfish was a surprising result both in the 
Kroner (2016) study and this study, as previous studies on grey seal consumption conducted in 
the Baltic Sea had not discovered the presence of garfish (Lundström et al, 2007; Lundström 
et al. 2010). However, it should be noted that these previous studies were conducted further 
north in the Baltic Sea and this may account for the discrepancy. Additionally, this species was 
not identified through otolith recovery but rather through the identification of their green-tinted 
vertebrae and premaxillae. The length of a garfish otolith can range from 1.4 to 5.95 mm 
(Furlani et al., 2007). The small size of the otolith could make it more susceptible to being 
digested within the seal’s gastrointestinal tract and provide justification as to why no otoliths 
were discovered.  
Furthermore, DNA analysis detected a total of 23 fish taxa in the grey seals’ scats, 
including species such as viviparous eelpout (Zoarces viviparous), fourbeard rockling 
(Enchelyopus cimbrius), and lumpsucker (Cyclopeterus lumpus) (Kroner, 2016). These species 
had a relatively low frequency of occurrence but their presence in diet was observed (Kroner, 
2016). A possible reason for why these species were not found through scat analysis could be 
as a result of how the prey was consumed. Particularly with lumpsucker, seals have been 
observed to strip away the skin in order to obtain the underlying flesh and eggs (Couch, 1878; 
Kindt-Larsen, per comm.). In the case of eelpout, the otolith size ranges from 0.8 mm to 2.1 
mm (Härkönen, 1986), thereby making it quite susceptible to digestion or loss during scat 
rinsing. Additionally, no freshwater species were identified in the diet as previous studies have 
found, this may be due to the fact that freshwater species are more prevalent in the northern 
and eastern parts of the Baltic Sea due to salinity requirements (Sparholt, 1994).  
Prey occurrence 
This study has found that cod plays an important role in the diet of grey seals, providing 
the highest biomass overall and located in 25.8% of the scats that contained prey remains. This 
result is similar to that discovered by Kroner (2016). While herring has been found to be the 
dominant prey species of grey seals in the central and northern Baltic (Lundström et al. 2007), 
cod has been shown to be the dominant prey in the southern Baltic (Kroner, 2016). In this study 
==PAGE:52==
52 
herring’s biomass contribution was quite low at 6.4%, and only found in 7.6% of the scats 
containing prey. Garfish, on the other hand, had the second highest abundance and was found 
to play a surprisingly larger role in the diet as it was identified in 28% of the scats.  
Sandeel was also found to play a substantial role in the diet with the highest number of 
individuals ingested (n=239), after numerical correction factors were applied. In a previous 
study, sandeel had been observed to be actively selected by grey seals despite their lower 
abundance (Bowen and Harrison, 2006). Sandeel is an important prey item because it provides 
a high amount of energy (Evans and Bjørge, 2013). Nevertheless, sandeel could potentially be 
a secondary prey item, consumed by another prey such as cod. While inspecting the specific 
scats where sandeel otoliths were found, some with up to 23 otoliths, no other otoliths 
belonging to cod or another species was located. Sandeel also had the highest prey specific 
abundance (Pi) of 93%, revealing that in those scats where sandeel otoliths were found sandeel 
represented 93% of the prey individuals. This is not definitive but it may suggest that sandeel 
is a primary prey species for grey seals.  
Sprat was found to play a small role in the diet of the grey seals from this study and 
only discovered in two scats. Lundström et al. (2007) identified sprat as the second most 
common prey item for grey seals in the central and northern Baltic, occurring in 26.9% of scats. 
Sprat has a high abundance in the central Baltic Sea, where the sprat population has increased 
significantly since the 1990’s, partly due to the collapse of a main predator, cod (Möllmann et 
al. 2004). Due to the generalist nature of the grey seal and the high abundance of sprat, it is 
understandable why sprat would be a main prey species in the central Baltic region. 
Comparatively, in the southern Baltic sea, near the Bornholm Basin, there is a high 
concentration of adult cod (Eero, 2014). The predation pressure, as well as exploitation by 
fisheries, has resulted in a low abundance of sprat in the southern Baltic (Eero, 2014). This 
may explain why sprat was not found to be a significant prey source.  
Prey Size 
The size of prey species consumed in this study varied greatly, from a 1.8 cm sandeel 
to 83.9 cm cod (SCFs applied). The seals consumed prey with a mean length of 30.1 cm (SCFs 
applied). Overall, the mean prey size is slightly larger than those reported in grey seals residing 
in Newfoundland, Canada which had mean prey length of 20.4 cm (Hammill et al. 2007). The 
majority of fish consumed ranged between 30-40 cm (SCFs applied). This prey size is slightly 
larger than what some other studies have found. For example, Mikkelsen (2007) found the 
majority of grey seal prey to be between 15-30 cm in length on average. Though, the same 
==PAGE:53==
53 
study also found that in areas where larger species such as wolfish or catfish were important 
prey items, the average length of the prey items tended to be larger (>50 cm) (Mikkelsen, 
2007). The largest fish consumed this study was a cod with an estimated length of 83.9 cm. 
While this estimated size is quite large it is not the largest fish ever documented to be consumed 
by a grey seal. For example, Hammill et al. (2007) discovered a wolfish with a length of 99 
cm. The detection of such large fish does not necessarily mean that the whole fish was 
consumed. For example, the seal may have bitten the head off of a large prey while attempting 
to catch it or it could have been trapped in fishing gear.  
In order to relate the diet of grey seals to the availability of size lengths of cod, I 
extracted data from the ICES database DATRAS. The data collected from DATRAS included 
the catch per unit of effort for the length classes of cod located in the areas where the grey seals 
were hunting. The most commonly caught length class of cod by grey seals at both Måkläppen 
was 40-49 cm (SCFs applied). This is only slightly larger than the length class with the highest 
CPUE value which was between 30-39 cm. While at Tat, the most commonly caught length 
class was 60-69 cm. This length class is significantly larger than the most abundant length class 
estimated through CPUE, which was 30-39. These results could either signify that the SCF 
applied to the cod otolith was slightly too large or it could mean that seals were actively preying 
on larger sized cod than those that were more readily available. 
Additionally, the most consumed length of herring without size correction factors 
ranged from 15 to 19 cm, while with size correction factors applied the majority consumed size 
was 25 to 29 cm. Atlantic herring can attain a maximum length of 40 cm, however, most adult 
fish range from 20-30 cm (ICES, 2006). Assuming the size correction factors are correct, the 
seals are primarily consuming adult herring ages 3-6 years old (ICES, 2006).   
Variation between locations 
The results indicate that there are some geographical differences in the grey seals diet 
between the two locations. At Måkläppen garfish was found to dominate the diet, while at Tat 
cod dominated the diet. Additionally, at Måkläppen, the seals had a more diverse diet with all 
7 species identified, while at Tat only 5 species were found. Smout et al. (2013) states that grey 
seals are adaptable generalist predators and consequently their diet may differ significantly 
between locations. This could be the case in this study. There was a considerable difference in 
the composition of prey between the two locations. This may be attributed to the fact that grey 
seals consume prey that is readily available to them in the locations they occupy. As Sjöberg 
==PAGE:54==
54 
and Ball (2000) found, the diet of grey seals has been found to reflect species found near the 
particular forage areas that they reside at.  
The most significant difference in feeding behavior between the two locations was the 
much larger presence of garfish at Måkläppen, compared to Tat. This may be attributed to the 
fact that garfish come from the North Sea to Swedish coastal waters to spawn (Dorman, 1991). 
As they migrate they swim in areas with shallow waters and subsequently spawn in areas with 
eel grass (Otterlind, 1985). This makes it likely why they would be found at Måkläppen and 
not at Tat, as Måkläppen has shallow waters and eel grass located around the sandbank, while 
Tat is surrounded by rocky surfaces and the deep waters of the Bornholm Basin. The 
opportunistic feeding behavior of the grey seals becomes apparent as garfish makes up a much 
larger proportion of their diet during the spring, when they are available.  
Another variation between the two locations was the presence of sandeel solely at 
Måkläppen, with no presence identified in the scats obtained from Tat. This may be attributed 
to the fact that the preferred habitat of sandeel are coastal waters ranging from mid-tide level 
to depths of 30 meters, in areas containing sandy sea-bed sediment (IUCN, 2016). They are 
rarely found offshore (Nielsen, 1994). The waters surrounding Tat are deep, with the Bornholm 
Basin east of Tat reaching up to 120 meters (Geus map). Therefore, the shallow and sandy 
habitat around Måkläppen would be more suitable for sandeel.  
Additionally, herring and cod made up a greater proportion of the diet of grey seals at 
Tat, compared to Måkläppen. For the seals hauling out on Tat, cod was the most common prey 
species. Adult cod migrate to deep basins where they aggregate and spawn, requiring a specific 
salinity level for their eggs to have a neutral egg buoyancy (Tomkiewicz and Köster, 1999). 
One of the three deep basins in the Baltic Sea that provide the correct level of salinity for cod 
is the Bornholm Basin (Tomkiewicz and Köster, 1999). Currently there is a high density of 
adult cod concentrated in the Bornholm Basin (Eero, 2014). The high abundance of cod around 
Bornholm reveals why their frequency of occurrence would be higher at Tat than Måkläppen. 
Additionally, the higher abundance of herring at Tat may be attributed to the fact that the waters 
surrounding Bornholm are a known spawning site for Baltic herring (Aniansson and Hägerhäll, 
2007).  
Dab was also found to have a slightly higher prevalence in the diet of grey seals located 
at Tat, compared to Måkläppen. While dab can be found both in SD 24 and SD 25, dab requires 
deep basins, such as the Bornholm Basin, to spawn (Nissling et al. 2002). This could account 
for the slightly higher frequency of occurrence in the scats obtained from Tat.

55 
Seasonal Variation 
The seasonal variation in grey seal diets is believed to be driven primarily by changes 
in the abundance and distribution of prey (Bowen and Harrison, 2006). The seasonal variation 
is an important indicator as it reveals the changes in abundance of fish species throughout the 
seasons. However, the sex and age of the seal can also cause changes in diet composition over 
the year (Bowen and Harrison, 2006). For example, females require more energy during 
pupping season (Beck et al. 2007).  
This study found that during the spring the seals consumed a larger variety of prey, 
compared to all other seasons. Additionally, the highest total biomass estimated to be 
consumed was found in the spring. This may be attributed to the fact that after moulting grey 
seals forage intensively in the spring, from April and June to July, in order to reestablish their 
energy reserves for the next winter (Hammill et al. 2014; Oksanen, 2015). However, this may 
also be attributed to the fact that more scats were collected during spring (n=166), compared 
to any other season.  
In the spring at Måkläppen, garfish replaced cod as the primary species consumed by 
the grey seals. The consumption of garfish in the spring was much higher compared to all other 
seasons. This corresponds with the timing of garfish migration from the North Sea to Swedish 
coastal waters in the late spring (April-May) to spawn (Dorman, 1991). Herring was found in 
higher amounts in spring and summer. This could be due to the fact that herring in the western 
Baltic spawn during the spring and migrate to the Kattegat-Skagerrak to feed during the 
summer months (Sparholt, 1994). Cod had the highest frequency of occurrence in the grey 
seal’s diet during the winter, this may be due to the fact that winter, along with spring, is the 
time period when Atlantic cod spawn. It may also be due to the fact that there is less species 
available and as an opportunistic predator, the grey seal consumed what was available.  
Sandeel in the Baltic Sea have two populations, one that spawn in the spring and one 
in the autumn (Nielsen, 1994). Insufficient data is available regarding whether the ranges two 
spawning groups are different (Nielsen,1994). In this study sandeel had a higher frequency of 
occurrence in the spring, which may suggest that the grey seals were primarily feeding on the 
spring spawning stock.   
Yearly Variation 
The diet did not differ significantly between the years (2014 to 2016) in terms of species 
composition (P=0.206). One trend that was discovered throughout the three years was an 
==PAGE:56==
56 
increase in cod’s frequency of occurrence during this period. This could signify that the 
abundance of cod is rising in the Baltic Sea and becoming more available as a prey item for 
the grey seal. Studies have shown that while the number of larger eastern Baltic cod (>38 cm) 
are decreasing in abundance, smaller cod (<30) have increased fivefold from 2004 to 2014, 
with the highest numbers since the 2000’s (Eero et al. 2015). Additionally, the average catch 
per unit effort for cod (30 cm) over the years 2010-2016 has increased (DATRAS). This could 
explain the rise in cod’s importance as a prey item, compared to studies performed in 2007, 
when cod numbers had just begun to rise (Lundström et al. 2007). If the SCF for cod was over-
estimated, this rationalization would be more applicable.   
Comparison with DNA analysis study 
A previous study by Kroner (2016) performed an analysis on the same scats used in 
this study, however, they were analyzed using another method- DNA analysis. A table 
comparing her results to those found in my study can be found in Appendix 5. All of the major 
prey species found in my study were the same major prey species identified in Kroner (2016). 
When Kroner (2016) identified a high number of DNA sequences for a prey species, those 
same species were often found as an otolith or hard part in the scat (see Appendix 5). On 
average, species with a very low number of DNA sequences were not usually found as a hard 
part in the scat. Additionally, some scats that were excluded from her study because they did 
not contain DNA sequences, were scats that were found to contain otoliths or prey remains. 
The two studies were able to complement each other, as they both provided additional data on 
the prey consumed by grey seals. The study using DNA analysis was able to find a much larger 
range of species, including eelpout, fourbeard rockling, lumpsucker, and multiple other species. 
However, using otolith and hard part recovery this study was able quantify and estimate the 
amount of prey consumed. Together both studies provide a comprehensive overview of the diet 
of grey seals hauling out in the western Baltic Sea.  
Biomass consumption 
I estimated that adult grey seals on average consume 37,804 tonnes of cod per year in 
the Baltic Sea. I estimated a total prey consumption of the primary prey species at 49,438 
tonnes of fish over the year for the entire grey seal population of the Baltic Sea. These results 
could of course be skewed due to the fact that the sex and age of the grey seals was not taken 
into account. Knowledge of the population structure would result in more accurate estimates. 
Additionally, the average population sizes estimated by HELCOM (2016) are likely 
==PAGE:57==
57 
underestimations due the fact that they were taken using aerial surveys. Therefore, these results 
provide a simple estimation of the average of biomass consumed by adult seals in the Baltic 
Sea for the various prey species. 
Of the prey species consumed, three species represent those that are substantially 
removed by both the grey seal population and the fisheries within the Baltic Sea. These three 
include cod, herring, and sandeel. Annually, I estimated that grey seals removed 1,131 tonnes 
of cod and 95 tonnes of herring in Danish Baltic Sea waters (Table 8). The annual Danish 
commercial fishery landings of cod in the Baltic Sea in 2016 was 10,560 tonnes and 10,328 
tonnes of herring (statisikbanken.dk). In 2011 the total reported landings in the Baltic Sea was 
estimated to be 750,000 tonnes (Lassen, 2011). These numbers reveal that when looking at 
grey seals in Danish Baltic waters and in the Baltic Sea as a whole, they remove a negligible 
portion of the biomass of cod and herring, compared to commercial fisheries. 
Conflicts with fisheries  
In the results of this study, cod was discovered to be a principal food source for the grey 
seal, making up the majority of the biomass consumed. Cod is also a key species for fisheries 
in the Baltic Sea, which has resulted in increasing tensions between seals and fisheries 
(Westerberg et al. 2000).  Grey seal consumption is believed to be capable of causing a 
reduction in fish stocks, or in the very least, negatively effect vulnerable species going through 
stock recovery (Smout et al. 2013). However, studies have shown that, when looking at fish 
consumption from a historical perspective, seals have removed much less cod than fisheries in 
areas where cod was once abundant (Smout et al. 2013). This suggests that it is likely not seal 
predation of cod that has been the primary factor reducing the size of the stock to dwindling 
levels (Smout et al. 2013).  
Garfish made up the second most substantial portion of the grey seals diet. However, 
this would not cause a significant conflict with fisheries as garfish is a relatively unimportant 
commercial fish species. In 2016, there were only 219 kg of garfish landed in the Baltic Sea by 
Swedish commercial vessels (SwAM statistical database). It has also been suggested due to the 
fast swimming nature of garfish, that the species’ presence in seal diets may be a result of 
discards by fisheries (Brown and Pierce, 1998).  
Due to the decline in cod, herring has becoming the largest Swedish fishery in recent 
years, with a total of 55,669 tonnes landed in the southern Baltic over the past 6 years (SwAM 
database). This fishery made 123,361,000 SEK in 2016, making it also the highest-earning 
==PAGE:58==
58 
fishery from all Swedish fisheries in the southern Baltic (SwAM database). Herring was the 
third most common prey species identified in the grey seals’ diet. Through the use of predator-
prey distribution patterns, acoustic estimates, and multispecies analysis (SMS), Eero et al. 
(2011) discovered that the effect of predation on Baltic herring, as well as sprat, stocks is very 
small. Therefore, when deliberating stock management of herring in the Baltic Sea, the impact 
of seal predation can be disregarded (Eero et al., 2011). However, at a local level the overlap 
of grey seals foraging areas and herring fishery activities may result in a higher number of 
interactions and therefore spatial planning should be taken into consideration (Eero et al., 
2011). 
Dab is primarily a bycatch species for demersal fisheries fishing for plaice and sole in 
the North Sea and Baltic (ICES, 2015). The discarding rate of dab is 90% of the total catch 
(ICES, 2015). Thus, grey seal predation of dab should have very little to no impact on the 
surrounding fisheries landing dab. Small quantities of sandeel is also reported to be landed in 
the Baltic Sea, with the catches used for the production of fish meal and fish oil (Lassen, 2015). 
Thus, predation by grey seals on the sandeel population should have very little impact on the 
fishery.  
Implications for management 
The recovery of grey seal stocks throughout much of the Baltic signifies a conservation 
success story. However, this success introduces new challenges, such as an increase in the 
number of interactions with fisheries (Königson et al. 2007). One of the major issues continues 
to be the destruction of the fisheries’ gear and their catch by grey seals (Königson et al. 2007). 
In an attempt to mitigate the damage to fisheries caused by seals, Finland and Sweden have 
used lethal removal as a key management strategy (Oksanen, 2015). Together the two countries 
annual quota for grey seals is around 1,850 individuals (Oksanen, 2015). However, despite this 
strategy, the damage to catch and gear continue to be a significant problem for coastal fisheries 
(Varjopuro, 2011). Therefore, other possible solutions need to be considered. One such solution 
could be to increase the distance of fishing activity from haul-out sites. Sjöberg and Ball (2000) 
found that 10 out of 11 seals will spend more than 75% of their time within 50 km of the nearest 
haul-out site. Therefore, the magnitude of the seals’ impact on fisheries should decline with a 
decreased spatial overlap between seals and fishing activities (Sjöberg and Ball, 2000; 
Oksanen, 2015). If this is not possible, due the close proximity of key fishing areas near haul-
out sites, another solution could be the use of specialized fishing gear.  
==PAGE:59==
59 
In traditional trap nets seals are able to enter into the fish bag of the net and eat and 
damage the catch (Suuronen et al. 2006). In an attempt to reduce these losses, pontoon traps 
were created. Pontoon traps are fishing traps with modified fish chambers, which the seals 
cannot get into (Oksanen et al. 2015). The pontoon trap is commonly applied to fisheries 
targeting herring or salmonoids (Lundin et al. 2010). The use of these traps have shown a 
considerable decrease in the losses of catch due to seals (Suuronen et al. 2006). For example, 
in one study 30% of salmon was observed to be damaged using the traditional trap, compared 
to just 1% using the pontoon trap (Suuronen et al. 2006). The use of this gear provides a 
practical and sustainable solution for the select fisheries applicable for its use.   
 
Additional fisheries that are particularly susceptible to seal-inflicted damage include 
the gill-net and longline fisheries targeting cod (Königson et al. 2009). In recent years these 
fisheries have experience extensive damage due to grey seals (Königson et al. 2009). This has 
led to the conception of baited cod pots, compartments which enclose the caught fish making 
it harder for the seals to access it (Königson et al. 2014). While the pots’ catching efficiency is 
dependent on several variables such as season, depth, and placement in relation to the current, 
the device has been found to produce commercially viable catches (Königson et al. 2014). As 
the grey seal population in the Baltic continues to grow, the use of cod pots may provide a 
viable alternative for landing cod. 
Further studies 
In this study it was discovered that relatively small number of prey species were 
represented in each individual scat. 84.7% percent of the scats were found to contain prey 
remains of only 1 species, while 2 and 3 or more species were consumed by 13.7% and 1.5% 
of the seals. Further study could include DNA testing the seal scats to link the seal with its 
scats. By doing this one may be able to identify if there is a preference shown by individual 
seals for specific prey. Additionally, it could show a pattern if certain scats belonging to one 
seal contain multiple large otoliths, signifying that the individual is a specialized and talented 
hunter.  
Parasites were also found in 19.6% of the scats collected. They are becoming an 
increasing problem in Baltic Sea, particularly for the cod fisheries. The parasites collected in 
this study could be further analyzed and identified to species level. This could provide further 
data on the prevalence of the various parasite species occurring in the seals’ gastro-intestinal 
==PAGE:60==
60 
tracts, as well as if there is a pattern between their prevalence between the locations, seasons, 
and years. 
 
Concluding remarks 
The increasing Baltic grey seal population introduces new challenges for fisheries and 
for managers. Therefore, understanding the diet of the Baltic grey seals is vital in identifying 
their role in the marine ecosystem. This understanding allows managers to better understand 
the grey seals’ impact on commercial fisheries. This study helps provide an understanding of 
the primary species grey seals are consuming surrounding the haul-out locations of Måkläppen 
and Tat in the southern Baltic Sea. It reveals that Atlantic cod provides the highest amount of 
biomass consumed for grey seals at both locations. The results also indicate differences in the 
diet between locations and the seasons. The seals around Måkläppen were found to primarily 
consume garfish and sandeel over the spring season. While cod and herring were the primary 
prey species for seals around Tat. The variance in the diet between the two locations implies 
that grey seals forage in the areas near their haul-out sites. Seasonally, the diet varied, with 
more species and biomass consumed during the spring. However, more scats need to be 
collected and more evenly over the seasons in order to reduce the bias in the samples collected 
and provide a more accurate picture.
