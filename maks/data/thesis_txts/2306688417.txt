xix
Abstract
Osmosis is the ﬂow of solvent (e.g. water) from regions of low solute concentration
(e.g. sugar) to regions with higher via semipermeable membranes. It is of great technical
importance in various industrial applications including in desalination and water puriﬁ-
cation facilities. It also plays a key role in all living cells. For instance, sugar transport
is driven by concentration diﬀerences of sugar across the cell membrane of phloem cells.
Previous work has shown that the presence of unstirred boundary signiﬁcantly aﬀects the
osmotic pumping eﬃciency by lowering the concentration near the membrane. Moreover,
it has been shown that in perturbed boundary layers, the concentration overshoots its
equilibrium value for suﬃciently large Peclet numbers resulting in concentration behaving
as a damped oscillator. We show numerically that in the more realistic two dimensional
geometries such as the cylinder, these oscillations are induced for smaller Peclet numbers,
in the presence of weak bulk ﬂows. We propose a modiﬁed one dimensional model, in-
cluding spatial dependence of advective velocity in which we provide numerical estimation
of the oscillatory properties of the ﬂow and we propose a model to tackle the problem
analytically. Lastly, we prove both numerically and analytically that the existence of these
damped oscillations are not uniquely associated with osmotic phenomena but may be also
found in various other systems that can be described by a concentration dependent velocity
ﬁeld.

Introduction
All living cells are known to have semipermeable membranes separating the interior of
the cell from the environment outside. These membranes allow for solvent (e.g. water)
to ﬂow from regions of low to high solute concentration. This phenomenon is know as
osmosis. Osmosis plays a major role in cell function since it regulates the cell volume
in both animal and plant cells and controls bulk water ﬂow inside and between cells [1].
However, the behaviour of unstirred boundary layer adjacent the membrane is yet not fully
understood and still a topic of active research. What are the physical properties of these
layers?
Unstirred layers constitute a region of reduced concentration due to the introduction of
water in the domain. Let us consider a solute diﬀusing from the bulk region of the domain
(see Figure 1.1) towards the membrane. If no advective inﬂux were present, concentra-
tion would be uniform throughout the whole domain. However, a solvent ﬂux through

Introduction
3
In 1932 Ernst Münch published his pressure ﬂow hypothesis, otherwise known as the
Münch mechanism, which states that osmotically driven ﬂows are the main contributor for
transporting sugar from the leaves towards the bulk opart of the tree [17]. It has been the
most prevalent theory throughout the years and just recently experimentally proven to be
true [18]. Sugar is produced in the mesophyll cells found in leaves of plants by absorbing
energy harvested from sunlight via photosynthesis. This sugar then needs to be transported
throughout the body of the plant which is made possible via a vast network of tubular cells
called phloem cells that spans from the leaves to the roots of the plant. Another type of
transporter cells are the xylem cells which pump up water from the roots to the leaves and
provide suﬃcient hydration to sustain the plant. Phloem cells located in the leaves load
sugar acquired from the mesophyll cells and thus a concentration gradient between the cell
interior and the surrounding aqueous solution is established, leading to water ﬂow into the
phloem. These osmotic ﬂows have been studied extensively in the past [19]. For instance,
workers have studied the optimal concentration of sugar that can transfer the maximum
amount of energy throughout various plants [20] and other biological systems [21].
Most of the workers above, however, have treated osmotic systems that have already
established a steady state. In the present work, we investigate numerically, using a simple
ﬁnite diﬀerence method [23], the transient behaviour of osmotic ﬂows and how unstirred
layers aﬀect the temporal evolution of concentration for various one and two dimensional
geometries. We demonstrate in a simple one dimensional model, for suﬃciently high solvent
inﬂux (e.g. the Peclet numeber is suﬃciently large) that steady state is reached via an
overshoot of concentration that leads to a damped oscillation, which is veriﬁed analytically
by [3]. Expanding into two dimensions, we ﬁrst compare our numerical model with the
steady state analytic solution proposed by [10], we then show that the oscillatory behaviour
is present even in the presence of bulk ﬂow in a cylinder, while absent for parallel plates
for a wide range of Peclet numbers. Based on the results of the two dimensional models,
we propose a revised one dimensional model with a modiﬁed velocity ﬁeld, in the search
of a ﬂow regime with a pulsatile behaviour which is solved numerically, while a method to
an analytical solution is given but still remains to be solved.

Introduction
In the last chapter of the present work we brieﬂy explore the idea of oscillatory be-
haviour in ﬂow systems with non osmotic dynamic coupling of concentration and velocity.
For instance, ﬂuctuations in ionic concentrations in xylem cells lead to an increase/decrease
of vessel diameter by which regulates the hydraulic resistance of the channel [24] and ul-
timately the velocity of the ﬂow. Responsive hydrogel coated materials can change their
thickness based on the concentration of a substance or other stimuli (p.H., speciﬁc antigens
etc.) around the hydrogels. In microﬂuidic channels these may act as valves controlled
by solute concetration and are able to regulate the ﬂow [25]. Figure 1.3 shows ﬂow in a
microchannel in which pillars are coated with a responsive hydrogel. Since the thickness of
the pillars is controlled directly by regulating the concentration, one may control the ﬂow
rate and the resistance of the channel by means of biological markers [22]. We propose a
simple one dimensional model in which solute diﬀusion is counteracted by advective ﬂow
that is driven by an external pressure gradient but also regulated by the concentration at
some point within the domain and prove both numerically and analytically the existence
of damped oscillations in this type of velocity - concentration coupling. Finally our results
may prove relevant to a number of systems with non linear couplings. For instance, honey-
bees form swarm clusters around a fertilized queen. The density of the cluster is regulated
so that the queen stays at a constant temperature [26]. Coupling to thermal convection
and heat exchange with the environment leads to a feedback system similar to that shown
in Fig. 1.3.

In chapter 2 it was shown that the presence of an unstirred layer near the semiperme-

secondly, a cylinder with semipermeable walls. What are the conditions under which these
oscillations occur? First, a derivation of the governing equations will be presented. Then
we will attempt to show an approximate analytic solution for the steady state and lastly
numerical simulations are also presented and investigated in order to obtain a complete
picture of the ﬂow properties.

Modiﬁed one dimensional model
In chapter 2 we introduced a simple, one dimensional model of osmotic ﬂow in an osmotic
boundary layer. Both a complete analytic as well as numerical description were provided
to show the existence of oscillations of concentration within this boundary layer under
speciﬁc circumstances. This circumstances where fulﬁlled by making certain assumptions,
of which the two most prevalent are worth pointing out. We have to recall the form of
the non dimensional governing equation (2.12). The assumption that was made is that
throughout the whole domain, the solute is subject to a velocity (since we are considering
a one dimensional problem, there is only one velocity component and it alone constitutes
the velocity ﬁeld) that is proportional the velocity at the osmotic boundary. Thus there is
no spatial dependence of that velocity, but only temporal. The validity of this assumption
can be accepted if one considers a suﬃciently small and well deﬁned boundary layer, in
which spatial variations of velocity can be considered negligible. This leads us to the second
assumption being that the boundary layer has a ﬁxed thickness δ, which is true in the case
δ →0. In reality the exact thickness of the boundary layer is not as simple to deﬁne, since
the complexity of three dimensional systems does not allow for such arbitrary assumptions.
Thus, we expanded onto the second dimension by introducing in the next section 3 two
special cases of osmotic ﬂow between two parallel osmotic plates and in a cylinder. These
models show a more realistic description of a physical system where the whole domain
thickness is considered to act as a boundary layer. Numerical investigation showed the
existence of oscillations in two dimensions in the case of a cylinder, but a analytical solution
of (3.70) for either case (parallel plates or cylinder) is not readily available. Lubrication
theory allows us to approximate the velocity ﬁeld for both cases given from (3.20) - (3.22)
and (3.67) - (3.69) for the parallel plates and cylinder respectively. These velocity ﬁelds
suggest that it is possible to introduce a modiﬁed one dimensional model, which now has
a spatially varying velocity that drives the concentration according to the aforementioned
ﬁelds.

So far we have extensively studied the eﬀect of osmosis in shaping the ﬂow in osmotic

6.1
One dimensional model
A simple one dimensional model of an osmotic boundary layer was presented. In this model,
advective inﬂux of solvent at the osmotic boundary is counteracting diﬀusion throughout
the layer and concentration reaches a steady state. We show both numerically and ana-
lytically that when the ﬂow of solvent into the domain via the semipermeable membrane
is high enough, that is, the Peclet number is large, then the equilibrium is reached via an
damped oscillation of concentration around the steady state value. These oscillations are
initiated for critical value βc ≈27 (or β1,c = 2.4 for the modiﬁed Peclet number). At this
value, as it can be seen in Figure 2.6, the imaginary part of the wavenumber, Im(k), has
values close to zero but rapidly increases as β (or β1) becomes larger. Due to the fact that
Im(k) ∝1/T, where T is the period of oscillation, when these oscillations are initiated,
they have inﬁnitely long period,

there is an is a rapid increase in the damping rate until the critical value is reached, when
the undulations seem to partially slow it down. Given that β = LpRTC0δ/D and assuming
that in a given system the only controllable parameter is the permeability, Lp, one may
associate the change in the advective ﬂow of solvent with the change in permeability, e.g.
an increase of Lp results in an increase of β. One may argue that temperature can also
be modiﬁed, which holds true in the laboratory but not in biological systems where the
temperature ﬂuctuations within cells are negligible. That being said, when a steady sate
is developed and a perturbation disturbs the equilibrium, once this perturbation travels

towards the semipermeable membrane and builds up a considerable concentration diﬀer-
ence across the membrane, then if the permeability is large enough (meaning the amount
of aquaporin channels is large), it will allow for large amounts of solvent to ﬂow through
and push the solute quite "violently" towards the steady concentration boundary, creating
larger gradients along the domain, that in turn reproduce the same phenomenon, though
with a reduced amplitude hence the observation of damped oscillations.
The analysis above has been based on the assumption that the unstirred boundary
layer studied is of constant thickness, ˆx = δ and that the value of the concentration at
the end of the boundary layer is also constant, C(δ) = Cb. In reality one must be careful
with the deﬁnition of these quantities. The thickness of the layer depends the magnitude
of osmotic ﬂow. The larger the advective velocities introduced by the solvent inﬂux, the
larger the boundary layer thickness, while on the contrary, a diﬀusion dominated system
would restrict the boundary layer in a region close very close the membrane. But the
thickness of the layer is also dynamically linked with the concentration source stemming
from the bulk region ˆx > δ. Diﬀerent regions within a cell have diﬀerent concentrations,
leading to diﬀerent concentration gradients that could potentially drive the ﬂow in the
boundary layer, which would consequently be constantly reshaped based on the topology
and physiology of the region of the cell that is studied.
6.2
Two dimensional models
In the search for osmotically induced oscillations in more realistic geometries, we expanded
our study in two dimensions, namely, for ﬂow between parallel plates and ﬂow within a
cylinder.
6.2.1
Parallel plates
A bulk ﬂow between parallel plates drives the solute of some concentration between the
osmotically active parallel plates.
We showed that the velocity ﬁeld can be described
with the aid of lubrication theory as a two dimensional squeeze ﬂow. As solute is swept
along the domain it comes in contact with the semipermeable walls and solvent inﬂow
is accelerating the ﬂow. We proposed an analytical model for the steady state solution,
the validity of which was conﬁrmed numerically. The said solution assumed a parabolic
concentration proﬁle throughout the domain. Two major limitations impose restrictions
on the range of validity of the solution. The ﬁrst limitation is that the contribution of
longitudinal diﬀusion must be considered negligible, while the second is that the osmotic
velocity through the membranes should be signiﬁcantly smaller than that of the bulk ﬂow in
order not to aﬀect the shape of concentration and create an osmotic boundary layer. Since
the ﬂow is controlled by the ratio of perpendicular advective with diﬀusive transport, β,
and the ratio between longitudinal and perpendicular advective transport, α, the solutions
hold when α ≥β and β ≥1 (see Figure 3.2). According to this solution, solvent that is
accumulated through the walls, is shifting direction and ultimately accelerates the ﬂow in
the longitudinal direction. Given that the plates (or cylinder) are suﬃciently long, then one
may assume that the bulk velocity (and thus the parameter α) becomes larger towards the
==PAGE:68==
48
Conclusion and Discussion
latter sections of the domain of whilst being small near the inlet. However, the numerical
solution of the transient problem reveals that no oscillations were observed for moderate
and high Peclet numbers. This can be attributed to the deceleration of the perpendicular
advective velocity after entering the domain, losing a portion of the momentum necessary
to overshoot the equilibrium value. Likely, for higher Peclet numbers it should be possible
to observe oscillatory behaviour, but the numerical solver would require signiﬁcantly more
computational resources and time.
6.2.2
Cylinder
As in the case of parallel plates, axial ﬂow of parabolic shape sweeps concentration along
the domain enabling inward solvent ﬂow through the porous walls. A steady state solution
is also provided under the assumption of a parabolic concentration proﬁle throughout the
cylinder with a range of validity similar to that of the parallel plates. The velocity ﬁeld
that describes squeeze ﬂow reveals that solvent accumulated by the mainstream ﬂow ﬁrst
accelerates radially before losing its momentum in the radial direction and start ﬂowing
axially. Numerical simulations show that in cylindrical geometries oscillations occur due to
solvent inﬂux. For β ≈3 an unexpected "bump" in the exponential decay was observed for
both cases α = 0.01, 0.1. It is unclear whether this "bump" is a result of numerical issue or
some irregularity of the ﬂow that could take place in the chosen regions of measurement of
the wavenumber properties with the former to be less likely true since this slight increase
in the damping factor was observed in various re-runs of the code for varying temporal
and spatial discretization. Oscillations are observed for β ≥βc ≈19 which is signiﬁcantly
lower that the value observed in the one dimensional case. This is of course attributed to
the fact that squeeze ﬂow in a cylinder accelerates the radial velocity component which in
turn intensiﬁes the eﬀect of advection allowing perturbations to more easily overshoot the
equilibrium value.
6.3
Modiﬁed one dimensional model
We presented a modiﬁed one dimensional model in which the advective term in the govern-
ing equation does not depend solely on the concentration at the osmotic boundary but has
also a linear spatial dependence. Decelerating ﬂow (i.e. ϵ < 0) corresponds to geometries
such as the parallel plates where we showed that radial velocity is monotonically decreas-
ing, though not linearly. Accelerating ﬂow (i.e. ϵ > 0) corresponds to cylinder shaped
geometries in which radial velocity close to the walls is increasing. But the model can also
be representative of other geometries. One can imagine that in a circular diverging channel
with a semipermeable inlet, the ﬂow would decelerate along the path since to compensate
for the expansion of the channel. In contrast, if the channel was converging, ﬂow would
accelerate in a similar manner so that ﬂow rate would be conserved. As far as oscillations
are concerned, the numerical solver showed a direct dependence of the critical value of β for
which the oscillations start being observed to the geometric parameter ϵ is found. For the
extreme case of ϵ = 5 oscillations were initiated for β ≥βc ≈9 (or equivalently in terms
of the modiﬁed Peclet number, β1 ≥β1,c ≈1) and when ϵ = −1, no oscillations were ob-

served. Analytically, we were unable to solve the eigenvalue equation for the wavenumber
k, which would provide analytic solutions, so the problem still remains open.
6.4
Beyond osmosis
The transient evolution of a ﬂow between a concentration sink and source, acting as con-
stant concentration boundaries, where advective velocity is proportional to concentration
at some point in the domain was studied. Constant boundary conditions were applied
and the coupling of velocity and concentration takes place in a region (in our case, sin-
gular point) within the domain. We were successful in extracting the ﬂow properties of
the wavenumber k both numerically and analytically using perturbation theory. We also
found a strong correlation between the position of forcing and the intensity of the oscil-
latory behaviour. For instance, at x0 = 0.5 oscillations are found when β ≥βc ≈37
(the corresponding modiﬁed Peclet number is β1 ≥β1,c ≈4.1) and we computed a maxi-
mum frequency Re(kmax) ≈80 (in non dimensional units). Comparably, when forcing is
dependent on concentration at x0 = 0.3 we observe a much earlier onset of oscillations,
namely when β ≥βc ≈20 (β1 ≥β1,c ≈2.1). Moreover, the rate of frequency increase
is substantially lower, peaking at Re(kmax) ≈45. On the other hand, damping is also
reduced, leading to prolonged vibratory concentration transport. The aforementioned fact
of non osmotic related oscillations, opens up new possibilities and a wider range of systems,
(natural or man-made) that can be studied.
6.5
Biological and technological implications
Osmotically induced oscillations may be desirable or not depending on the application,
biological or technological. The main focus is on systems that are subject to frequent
change of ﬂow conditions, that is, steady state is perturbed frequently. The numerical and
analytical studies carried out in the present work can provide a theoretical basis for the
development and optimization of devices that are able to perform biological experiments on
osmotically driven ﬂows. In Table 6.1 shows the values for the onset of oscillations (when
they exist), for the various domains considered. For a cylinder the value is βc ≈19 when
α = 0.01. Diﬀerent cell types have have diﬀerent osmotic properties. According to [30]
permeability in various plant cells varies by several orders of magnitude. For example, the
species glycence max has cells located in the hypocotyl, the stem of a germinating seedling,
which have permeability ranging between Lp = 0.7 −17 × 10−12 m/(s Pa). Similar perme-
ability is found at the membrane of subepidermal bladder cells in the fruits of Capsicum
annuum. A reasonable value for osmotic pressure found in plant cells is RTC = 106 Pa
for sugar concentration of approximately 0.5 M [20] while the diﬀusivity of sugar in water
is D = 5 × 10−10 m2/s [27]. Cells can vary signiﬁcantly in diameter, ranging from 10 µm -
1 mm thus, if we assume that the whole cell diameter acts as a boundary layer, one may
estimate the Peclet number β = LpRTCδ/D ≈0.34 −34 depending on the thickness of
the layer. Hence, it is possible to observe oscillations in larger cellular structures once the
threshold of β = 19 is surpassed. Let us assume a ﬂow within the range of Peclet numbers
mentioned above, say β = 25. Then, the dimensionless frequency according to Fig. 3.13
