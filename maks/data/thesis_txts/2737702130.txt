Abstract 
Ecological momentary assessment (EMA) has traditionally been used to study hearing-aid benefits and 
outcomes, as well as to explore the auditory reality of hearing-aid users. However, EMA can also deepen 
our understanding of individual listening intentions and contribute to the development of more intelligent 
hearing aids. However, there is currently no evidence as to how well self-reported listening intentions, 
activities, and outcomes via EMA represent real life. By examining self-reports collected via EMA, we can 
enhance our knowledge of real-world listening activities, intentions, and outcomes. 
 
The project expands upon the Common Sound Scenario (CoSS) framework (Wolters et al., 2016) by 
including objective and subjective data acquired by conducting a week—long field trial with 21 
participants. Normal hearing individuals were recruited to validate the study’s methodology and to set 
the stage for future applications with hearing-impaired participants. Objective data include the 
characteristics of the ambient sound environment (sound pressure level (SPL) and signal-to-noise ratio 
(SNR)), and heart rate (HR) measurements collected through hearing-aids and smartwatches respectively. 
Subjective data were acquired by multiple EMA completions per day and per participant using the SEMA3 
app. Through these surveys, participants self-reported their listening activities, intentions and hearing-
related stress levels, and were asked to evaluate how much they aligned with their actual listening 
experiences.  
 
The collected HR and EMA data presented good face validity  and had comparable patterns to previously 
conducted EMA studies. Participants predominantly reported non-specific listening intentions, followed 
by speech communication and focused listening. These intentions were evenly distributed throughout the 
day, with peaks during lunch and dinner hours—times when conversations are likely. Interestingly, even 
younger individuals with normal hearing employed coping mechanisms to enhance their listening 
experience during conversations. The comparison of consistency and real-life match ratings revealed high 
overall ecological validity, although non-specific intentions received lower ratings. While our selections 
aligned with the CoSS framework, deviations were observed, suggesting partial applicability of the 
framework. Participants reported low hearing-related stress levels, consistent with their normal hearing 
status. Notably, the choice of activity and intention influenced self-reported listening experiences, with 
listening to live sounds and media device use being the least stressful activity. We conclude that EMAs 
provide high ecological validity especially for specific listening intentions and activities.

1 Introduction 
1.1 
Purpose and Background 
Until recently, researchers evaluated the real-world listening experiences of hearing aid users through 
retrospective questionnaires, interviews, and tests under controlled conditions (Holube et al., 2020). 
Meanwhile, objective assessments of hearing aid benefits primarily relied on laboratory experiments 
conducted in controlled environments. Unfortunately, these controlled conditions do not fully capture 
the complexities of real-world situations, where conditions are constantly shifting (Andersson et al., 
2023). In laboratory settings, the impact of daily-life activities, social interactions, and specific listening 
intentions on people’s actual experiences in the real world may not be fully understood (Pichora-Fuller et 
al., 2016). When using retrospective questionnaires, responses related to specific experiences may be 
influenced by memory retrieval limitations, errors, or biases due to intervening events between the 
experience and the questionnaire’s completion (Holube et al., 2020).  
  
Hence, researchers have suggested experience sampling methods to capture real-world listening 
experiences more accurately. Specifically, ecological momentary assessment (EMA) involves collecting in-
the-moment self-reports from individuals in their natural environments, covering aspects such as 
behaviour, motivation, experiences, thoughts, and feelings (Shiffman et al., 2008). Studies using EMA have 
given us insights into how hearing aid users experience their listening environments (Andersson et al., 
2023). In their study introducing EMA to hearing research, Wu et al., (2015) demonstrated good construct 
validity. The data pattern obtained from EMAs conducted in diverse, uncontrolled real-world 
environments, aligned well with establish audiology knowledge. EMA methodology has inherent 
limitations. These include the variability in individuals’ lives and listening situations, as well as a selection 
bias in the situations people choose to experience and evaluate (Holube et al., 2020). 
 
EMAs have traditionally been applied to investigate hearing-aid benefits or outcomes, or to better 
understand the auditory reality of hearing-aid users (e.g. Andersson et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2018; 
Christensen et al., 2024; von Gablenz et al., 2021). However, EMA can be used to further understand the 
listening intentions of individuals. Understanding listening intensions, such as wanting to understand 
speech in a conversation or monitor ambient sounds, can help fine-tune a range of hearing aids features. 
In order to enhance our knowledge about listening intentions, it is important to establish how ‘ecologically 
valid’ we can capture listening activities, intentions and outcomes using EMA. 
 
Ecological validity is a term often referred to when discussing how laboratory outcomes of e.g., hearing 
tests can be reflected in real life. Advancing ecological validity in hearing science helps understand 
everyday listening situations and the specific challenges that make listening difficult and, ultimately, 
develop new technologies for more integrated and individualized care (Keidser et al., 2020). Here, we use 
the term to reflect how well self-reported listening experiences via an EMA app capture real life. The self-
reported listening experiences are anchored to the Common sound scenario (CoSS) framework (Wolters 
et al., 2016). The three-level framework was established to group auditory reality by categorizing daily 
hearing experiences according to listening intentions and tasks (von Gablenz et al., 2021). 
 
==PAGE:14==

The CoSS framework has been extensively used in hearing research to reduce the number of variables and 
make outcomes from different studies comparable (e.g. Andersson et al., 2023; Jensen, 2019; von Gablenz 
et al., 2021).  Since there are not yet sufficient data in the hearing research field to propose a specific set 
of survey items, it is recommended to use the CoSS framework to group the listening situations (Schinkel-
Bielefeld et al., 2023). There are three intentions, divided into seven tasks and each task is further divided 
into two scenarios, leading to 14 scenarios in total (Figure 1). Each scenario has an indicator (occurrence, 
importance, difficulty) and the darker its colour, the higher its value (Wolters et al., 2016). The listening 
intentions used in this project are the same as those in the CoSS, and the listening activities are based on 
the framework (for more information see 2.3.3 Questionnaire Design). 
 
 
Figure 1 The CoSS framework: three intention categories, seven tasks, 14 scenarios & their indicators (Wolters et al., 2016). 
By combining EMAs with acoustic data-logging from the hearing aids, individual assessments can be linked 
to real-world acoustic contexts (Andersson et al., 2023). According to them, in young adults with normal 
hearing, collecting acoustic and heart rate (HR) data can enhance the accuracy of predicting real-world 
self-reported listening experiences or outcomes. In our project, self-reporting of listening activities, 
intentions, and outcomes in daily life is the subjective data collected using EMAs daily. The ambient sound 
environment characteristics as well as HR measurements is the objective data logged using hearing aids 
and a smartwatch, respectively. Objective data may be used to replace some subjective assessments, or 
to categorize the environment in which individuals provide a self-report (Schinkel-Bielefeld et al., 2023). 
Beyond the work of Andersson et al., (2023), there appears to be limited existing research in exploration 
of the potential associations between self-reported listening experiences, acoustic data, and HR data, 
emphasizing the need for further exploration. 
 
From research literature, the terms listening situations, acoustic or sound environments are used to 
describe situations in which hearing might play a role. These terms are often used interchangeably or 
together as generic terms in research (Wolters et al., 2016). Acoustic or sound environment refers to all 
the sounds that can be heard in a specific place at a particular time. The characteristics of the sound 
environment that are of interest in these projects are signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) and sound pressure level 
(SPL) (Christensen et al., 2024).  
 
==PAGE:15==
 
 
3 
 
SNR represents the spectral dimension of sound by comparing the level of background noise to the signal 
level in decibel (dB). An SNR greater than 0 dB indicates that there is more signal than noise, and the more 
positive the value, the less noise relative to the signal (Christensen, Saunders, Porsbo, et al., 2021). In the 
hearing systems field, SNR plays a crucial role. It is an extra auditory physical attribute that impacts 
people’s ability to understand speech in noisy environments and engage in conversations. Achieving an 
optimal SNR—where the speaker’s voice stands out from the background noise—is essential for effective 
communication (Long, 2014; Plack, 2018). According to (Long, 2014), SPL serves as a common indicator of 
acoustic wavelength. It can be easily measured using basic tools like a sound level meter and is closely 
related to how individuals perceive loudness. SPL represents the logarithmic measure of sound’s effective 
pressure relative to a reference value, expressed in dB. The reference pressure is set at the threshold of 
human hearing (around 1000 Hz for young normal-hearing individuals), and when sound pressure equals 
this reference, the level is 0 dB. Human hearing ranges from 0 dB SPL (threshold) to 120-140 dB SPL (pain 
threshold), with sensitivity varying by frequency. While SPL does not directly correspond to loudness, it 
plays a crucial role in understanding sound intensity (Long, 2014; Plack, 2018). High SPL and low SNR 
indicate an acoustically demanding situation (Holube et al., 2020). 
 
The effect of sound level on HR has been investigated already in hearing research. For example, 
Shoushtarian et al., (2019) used functional near-infrared spectroscopy (fNIRS) to collect HR measurements 
from 28 normal hearing participants and concluded that changes in HR were linked to the sound level 
showing a distinct response between near threshold sounds and comfortably loud sounds. Christensen et 
al., (2021) and Andersson et al., (2023), investigated the relationship between the everyday sound 
environment characteristics and HR measurements using real-world data collected from hearing aids and 
smartwatches. Christensen et al., (2021) emphasized the influence of ambient environmental sound in 
models of human ecophysiology. To understand this influence, subjective hearing-related stress reports 
complement objective measures, such as monitoring the cardiovascular reactions toward changes in the 
environment. While the effect of sound level on HR has been studied, there remains a need to investigate 
how different listening experiences influence HR and hearing-related stress levels in individuals. In this 
study, smartwatches are used to collect HR measurements. 
 
In conclusion, this project aims to investigate the ecological validity of self-reported listening activities, 
intentions and hearing-related stress. This is achieved by comparing the self-reported activities and 
intentions to consistency and real-life match ratings, while also assessing their correlation with ambient 
acoustics. Additionally, self-reported stress ratings are compared to activities, intentions and HR 
fluctuations. The ultimate goal is to evaluate and validate the methodology used, and to assess its 
applicability in future studies with hearing-impaired populations. The aim is to contribute to the research 
around the development of smarter hearing aids, with adaptable processing techniques that will optimize 
the hearing experience.

2 Materials & Methods 
2.1 
Procedure Selected 
The chosen approach in this study to collect data is to conduct a week-long field trial on normal hearing 
adults. 
 
The decision to conduct a field trial, rather than a controlled laboratory test, stems from the study’s 
objective: to evaluate how self-reported listening intentions and outcomes reflect typical listening 
experiences in daily life. By observing participants in their natural environments, valuable insights into 
real-life conditions can be obtained, capturing the complexities and social interactions of everyday life 
(Aziz, 2017). 
 
The rationale for selecting a seven-day trial duration is that the duration of a field trial should be a balance 
between the time required to address the research question and the burden placed on participants 
(Schinkel-Bielefeld et al., 2023). One week comprises five working days and a weekend, during which 
various interesting listening experiences can be captured. Moreover, it is crucial to account for the overall 
time needed for data collection and subsequent analysis. Considering that this is a MSc thesis, extending 
the data collection period would pose challenges for data analysis and discussion within the limited 
timeframe. Other audiological EMA studies have employed a 2-week duration (Christensen et al., 2024; 
Andersson et al., 2023) or approximately 3 weeks (Schinkel-Bielefeld et al., 2020). However, one week or 
even less might be sufficient to establish individual patterns, like the von Gablenz et al., (2021) study that 
used 3-4 days. In this type of study, which aims to capture a diverse range of auditory experiences, one 
week of data collection is as informative as two weeks (Schinkel-Bielefeld et al., 2023). 
 
Each participant was instructed to wear one Oticon Intent™ hearing aid (HA) on each ear, an Oticon 
More™ hearing aid (HA) attached at a clip on their blouse collar, and a smartwatch continuously for one 
week seven days, from 09:00 to 21:00. They were used to collect objective data: The Oticon More™ HA 
was used to collect the characteristics of the ambient sound environment (SPL and SNR), and the 
smartwatch measured the HR of the participant. The analysis of the Oticon Intent™ HA data is out of scope 
of this project. Additionally, each participant was requested to complete seven scheduled EMA surveys 
daily, prompted on their phone by the SEMA3 app. They were also encouraged to initiate self-triggered 
surveys whenever they experienced new listening activities or missed a scheduled survey. Through these 
surveys, they self-reported their daily listening activities, intentions, and outcomes.

2.3.1 
Software Choice 
In previous EMA studies, Evaluate HA app was used with Oticon devices to collect both sound data from 
the hearing aid and facilitate participant surveys for self-reporting listening experiences (Andersson et al., 
2023; Andersson et al., 2021; Christensen et al., 2024). However, for this study, a different technical 
platform was intentionally chosen, SEMA3 (O’Brien et al., 2023), and the rationale behind this choice is 
explained below. The study will also address the usability of the SEMA3 app (see 4.6 Methodology). 
 
SEMA3, also known as Smartphone Ecological Momentary Assessment, is specialized software designed 
to conduct research using daily life survey methods. Researchers can use the free, opensource platform 
for designing and administering intensive longitudinal daily life surveys. The rationale behind choosing 
SEMA3 for the current study, is that it offers flexibility, supporting both schedule-triggered (at fixed or 
random intervals) and participant-triggered surveys. Additionally, SEMA3 accommodates various question 
types, including multiple choice, slider, text, grid, and graphic questions. Also, it operates in offline mode, 
eliminating the need for constant internet connectivity during surveys (O’Brien et al., 2023). SEMA3’s 
biggest advantage is that it can be installed easily to both Android and iOS smartphones, unlike Evaluate 
HA. This means that Android users can use their personal phones to participate. Receiving notifications 
on their personal devices may enhance attention and increase compliance.  
 
2.3.2 
Sampling Schedule 
To enhance randomness, a dynamic pseudo-random schedule was devised, ensuring that participants 
would not anticipate prompts at specific times during the day. The survey prompts started from each 
participant’s initial day. A total of seven surveys per day were distributed across a time span from 09:00 
to 21:00. The survey window spans 60 minutes, during which, each survey was randomly scheduled 
between a designated start and end time (e.g., between 09:00 and 10:00 in the morning). The 
unpredictability of the prompt timing is beneficial, since this approach prevents participants from 
modifying their behavior based on prompt expectations and avoids sampling activities that occur regularly 
at fixed times (e.g., same time every day) (Schinkel-Bielefeld et al., 2023; Higgins et al., 2023). Only the 
first prompt was scheduled to go out precisely at 09:00 in the morning of the starting day for each 
participant. This timing ensured that the process was properly initiated, as evident from the SEMA3 
website where the survey was designed. Once prompted, participants had a 30-minute window to 
complete the survey, during which they received additional reminders. This way they could ‘snooze’ the 
prompts which happened every 5 minutes (Jenstad et al., 2021). The sampling schedule is depicted in 
Figure 6 Day 0 corresponds to the first day of the trial and Day 6 to the last.

Question #1: Are you using headphones now?  
In this question, participants were asked whether they were streaming media or talking through the 
Intent™ HAs or headphones. The purpose of this inquiry is to account for situations where there the 
ambient sound would not have impacted participants naturally due to streaming or using external devices, 
which would not be captured by the microphone of the More HA. In this manner, the responses can be 
filtered: when the EMA responses were correlated with sound characteristics during the analysis, only the 
‘Yes’ responses would be considered. After starting the experiment, it became apparent that the phrasing 
of this question only addressed headphone use and did not account for streaming through hearing aids. 
Despite this, consistency was maintained throughout the trial, leaving the question phrased as is. Its 
meaning was explained clearly during the initial introduction meeting with each participant, as well as in 
the instructions sheet provided to guide them through the trial.  
 
Question #2: What is your main listening activity?  
Participants were asked to choose what was the main source of sound around them. When participants 
are asked to identify their primary listening activity, it can enhance our understanding on how the activity 
impacts their listening experience and the benefits of hearing solutions. This approach aims to simplify 
complex situations and allows researchers to interpret daily-life listening experiences more effectively 
(Schinkel-Bielefeld et al., 2023). When designing this question, the first step to choose the five choices 
was to collect as many as possible different scenarios someone can listen to through brainstorming and 
literature research, and group them into few major categories. This process was based on consultation of 
the CoSS framework  (Wolters et al., 2016) (see Table 3). The final and fifth activity choice was selected 
‘None of the above’ to enable participants to select it when none of the other four felt suitable enough to 
describe what they were listening to.

Question #3: How consistent was your listening activity during the last 5 minutes? 
Participants evaluated the consistency of their chosen listening activity over the last 5 minutes. They

or sounds. The response scale ranges from 0 (not at all consistent) to 10 (consistent to a great extent).

Participants were asked what their intention was, what sound activity they were trying to pay attention 
to. The three provided choices aligned with the CoSS framework (Wolters et al., 2016):

Question #4c: Are you aware of using any coping strategies to improve your listening experience?  
Participants were asked whether they have noticed any body movements or other mechanisms to 
improve their listening while engaging in conversation. The rationale behind this question is that head 
and body movements during suboptimal hearing situations are part of everyday life for all individuals, 
normal or impaired hearing. If speech understanding is so difficult during a conversation, the listener may 
either seek more favorable conditions to continue or withdraw from the conversation. Until recently, in 
EMA studies that explore listening intent, it is not often evaluated the modifications people need to make 
during a listening situation to help them (Borschke et al., 2024). Understanding how full body kinetics, 
head movement and hearing health are related could improve the signal processing strategies in hearing 
devices development. However, there is limited understanding of how head movement kinesthetics relate 
to a listener’s communicative goals (Higgins et al., 2023).

HA users tend to consciously or unconsciously adapt their situation based on their hearing ability. During 
the trial, participants were requested to report what modifications they did every time they encountered 
an acoustic situation. They concluded that on average the modification increased the feeling of 
pleasantness of the hearing situations. The final options mentioned in Table 2 were customized based on

Question #5: How well did your selection of listening activity and intention match your real-life listening 
situation? 
This question aimed to assess the degree of alignment between the selected activities and intentions and 
the actual listening context participants experienced. The response scale ranges from 0, meaning that it 
did not reflect at all the real-life conditions (low real-life match) to 10, meaning it reflected fully (high real-
life match). Understanding the alignment between selected activities and intentions and real-life 
conditions is crucial for advancing our understanding of auditory behaviors and improving assessment 
methodologies. 
 
Question #6: How did this listening experience make you feel?   
This question aimed to capture participants’ mood state (e.g., stress, frustration) during the listening 
situation. Frustration may arise due to excessive background noise, difficulty focusing on what you want 
to hear, lack of control over noise levels, or general hearing-related stress or anxiety. The response scale 
ranges from 0 (felt very frustrated or stressed) to 10 (felt very good). A higher score indicates that the 
listening situation did not cause discomfort or stress.

total number of questions was kept to a minimum, to reduce participant burden and encourage 
compliance (Schinkel-Bielefeld et al., 2023). Each question’s purpose, along with clarifications and 
examples, was thoroughly explained in the Instructions document provided to participants (see Appendix

The study involved 21 normal-hearing participants recruited from both Oticon A/S and Danmarks Tekniske

participants is sufficient. For instance, other EMA studies in hearing research have successfully used 15

2.5 
Data Analysis and Interpretation 
In this project, the R statistical software package is employed to generate results. All statistical analyses 
were conducted using R v.4.3.2 software. Visualization of descriptive statistics was done with the 
“ggplot2” package (v.3.4.4). Additionally, the LME analyses were performed using the lmerTest package 
in R.  
2.5.1 
Data Processing 
The HR, SNR and SPL data were averaged over a 5-minute time window before each EMA completion. HR 
data is filtered to remove outliers, thus, extreme values. For each participant, the values that are greater 
than 95% of their HR measurements are excluded to decrease the effects of physical exercise on the 
measurements. HR values below 40 bpm were also excluded since they are either suggesting an error or 
an individual having bradycardia. Following the data filtering and cleaning and transforming the data into 
a suitable data frame format for R, the descriptive analysis is performed. This includes calculating 
summary statistics (mean, standard deviation), and perform visualizations to present how the data is 
distributed across the hours of the day and across the whole trial. The purpose of this analysis is to get an 
overview of all the collected data within-subject and across the whole trial and prepare the ground for 
addressing later the research questions one by one. 
2.5.2 
Statistical Approach 
Statistical models are applied to investigate the association between selected EMA listening activity and 
intention options, HR and ratings of consistency, real-life match and hearing-related stress. Analyzing EMA 
data presents challenges due to repeated observations within individuals, the adaptive survey design, and 
the multilevel data. Each person contributes correlated observations, which, if treated as independent, 
could lead to substantial and incorrect reductions in standard error (SE). To take into consideration the 
correlation between the repeated observations from each individual, it is recommended to use statistical 
approaches which account for the nesting of repeated observations within individuals (Oleson et al., 
2022). 
2.5.2.1 
Aggregating Data 
A widely used approach for handling repeated observations in EMA data involves computing within-
participant averages. Thus, each participant has a single value derived from their averaged responses to 
each question. The central limit theorem states that for a sufficiently large sample size, the averages of 
data tend to follow an approximately normal distribution. As a result, statistical techniques that rely on 
the assumption of normality to analyze these averaged values can be confidently applied (Oleson et al., 
2022). This technique of computing a summary statistic first (within-participant average) and then 
analyzing the summary statistic has been successfully applied in multiple studies (Schinkel-Bielefeld et al., 
2020; Sabin et al., 2020).

2.5.2.2 
Linear Mixed Effect Model 
Mixed or random effect models or multilevel analysis have typically been used by a lot of EMA studies in 
hearing research field (Christensen et al., 2024; Oleson et al., 2022; Andersson et al., 2023; Christensen et 
al., 2021). Those models are well known from lab experiments and can be used to disentangle the effect 
of different factors on the outcomes (Holube et al., 2020). In this case, the models allow to analyze the 
effect of listening experience factors on the EMA responses. Following the above-mentioned 
recommendations in EMA literature, linear mixed-effects (LME) models were applied to investigate the 
associations between different EMA questions. The LME models considered the multilevel structure of 
the data, where observations are correlated and nested within each individual and condition due to 
repeated measurements (Oleson et al., 2022). To address individual baseline variability, the participants 
are treated as random effects in the models (Barr, 2013).

intention level) with a 95% confidence interval. If the p-value from the F-test is <.05, null hypothesis is 
rejected. This means that the activity or intention have a statistically meaningful impact on the EMA rating 
of the questions (Winter, 2013). In the context of LMEs, ANOVA evaluates whether the categorical fixed 
effects explain a significant portion of the variance in the response variable. For LME (2-6) with the HR as 
a continuous fixed effect, statistical significance was evaluated by inspecting the coefficient for 5-minute 
mean HR.

Effect Sizes 
In all analyses except the last one (HR vs self-reported hearing-related stress relation), apart from 
evaluating the statistical significance, it is also important to calculate the effect size and clinical 
significance (Wu et al., 2019). ANOVA is performed to assess whether there are significant differences in 
the mean EMA ratings between different activity or intention levels. The ANOVA gives you an overall test 
of significance. An effect size represents the magnitude of the difference between two populations. Unlike 
statistical significance, effect sizes emphasize clinical significance—whether the observed difference is 
practically meaningful (Oleson et al., 2022).  
To explore pairwise differences between the different levels of activities or intentions at each LME model 
described above, first the marginal means for each level is calculated using the Kenward-Roger’s 
approximation method. Estimated marginal means (EMMs) for specified factors or factor combinations in 
a linear model are the average response for each level of a categorical predictor, while accounting for 
other covariates or factors in the model. Then, a pairwise comparison based on the estimated marginal 
means is performed using Tukey’s method for comparing a family of 3 or 5 estimates (3 intentions or 5 
activities respectively). The p-value calculated for each EMMs paired comparison indicates whether the 
difference between the two levels is statistically significant. A small p-value (typically < 0.05) suggests a 
significant difference.

Bobbitt, 2020):

Pearson’s correlation coefficient 
The correlation between the responses to real-life match question (EMA Question #5) and consistency 
question (EMA Question#3) is investigated by calculating the Pearson’s correlation coefficient. First the 
data is aggregated for each participant for the two variables. Since the variables are averages for the two 
EMA ratings, normally distributed data is used, which is one of the assumptions for calculating Pearson’s 
correlation coefficient (Oleson et al., 2022). Coefficient (r) values between 0 and 0.3 indicate a weak 
relationship between x and y, between 0.3 and 0.5 suggest a moderate relationship, and above 0.5 a 
strong relationship strength between the variables. If r is negative, it indicates an inverse relationship: 
when one variable (x) increases, the other variable (y) decreases linearly. If r is positive, it signifies a direct 
relationship: as one variable (x) increases, the other variable (y) also increases linearly (Bobbitt, 2020b; 
Turney, 2022).

4 Discussion 
4.1 
Face Validity 
In this section, the overview and patterns of the subjective and objective data collected and analysed in 
the Results chapter is presented. First the analysis of the collected sound and HR data is discussed 
followed by the responses to the EMA questions and their patterns. The visualized data is interpreted to 
derive meaningful insights, to evaluate if the test has overall good face validity with the goal of preparing 
to address the research questions. 
4.1.1 
Overview of Sound and HR Data 
The sound data collected by the participants represented only 5.41% of the expected total amount. This 
is significantly less than what would be expected by a population of hearing-aid users, which have been 
shown to be around 85% (Christensen, Saunders, Havtorn, et al., 2021). Compliance issues are expected 
to lower the collected data, i.e. participants moving more than 10 meters away from the phone with the 
app. As previously stated, during the first meeting all participants were encouraged to check the 
connectivity through the Evaluate app every morning and often during the day. Also, during the last 
meeting with a lot of participants, they mentioned that they indeed checked the Evaluate app often and 
it seemed that it was connected. It was also reported that the app pushed some notifications by itself, 
urging the participants to check the connectivity. Based on this information, it appears that the sound 
data loss is unlikely to be solely due to compliance issues. Technical problems, such as poor connectivity, 
likely contributed to the issue. Additionally, data loss affected all participants, with two of them having 
no recorded data at all.  
The mean SPL was calculated around 60 dB. In Andersson et al., (2023) study, the SPL calculated was 60.6 
± 9.6 dB, 56.7 ± 10.9 dB and 48.7 ± 9.8 dB for the three listening intents ‘speech communication’, ‘focused 
listening’ and ‘non-specific’ respectively. Additionally, Christensen et al., (2021) measured SPL to be 48.79 
± 5.27 dB for ‘Quiet’, 65.97 ± 7.34 dB for ‘Noise’ and 72.47 ± 3.87 dB for ‘Speech’ sound environments.  
The equivalent continuous sound level for this study was calculated at 60 dB, very similar to our findings, 
suggesting good face validity for the logged SPL. 
 
The mean SNR was calculated around 1 dB. When the SNR is above 0 dB, it means that the signal level 
surpasses the noise level and a higher SNR corresponds to better signal quality (Sheldon & Burke, 2024). 
The calculated mean is low (signifying increased level of noise), but positive, meaning that most logged 
SNRs are above 0 dB. In comparison to other hearing research studies, Christensen et al., (2021) measured 
SNR to be 4.89 ± 3.86 dB for ‘Quiet’, 3.81 ± 2.50 dB for ‘Noise’ and 12.50 ± 5.14 dB for ‘Speech’ sound 
environments. In Wu et al., (2018) study 63% of the speech listening situations fell between 2 and 14 dB 
and only 7.5% of 894 typical daily listening situations had SNRs below 0 dB. Our calculated SNR is much 
lower than other studies. Similarly to Christensen et al., (2021), the sound data in this project was 
obtained using commercial hearing devices. These devices are typically designed for low power 
consumption, that may result in reduced resolution and accuracy in their sound estimators.  
 
In total, SPL measurements spread from 35 to 90 dB and cluster around 60-65 dB. SNR values range from 
-25 to 15 dB, with most of them being positive and cluster within the -5 to 5 dB range. When comparing 
==PAGE:65==
 
 
53 
 
the SPL and SNR distributions between all logged data and the data corresponding to EMA completions, 
it becomes evident that the high concentration around the median primarily stems from the latter 
dataset. Regarding the SPL and SNR distributions through the day, SPL showed great variability in contrast 
to SNR that remains more stable during the day. From the work of Christensen et al., (2021), similar 
distributions of SPL and SNR were made for each hour of the day (Figure 2 (a) and (c) respectively). Highest 
SPL observed around lunchtime (12:00) and dinner time (18:00). SNR appeared to be stable and around 
2.5 dB during most hours of the day and started increasing in the afternoon at 16:00, reaching its peak at 
21.00. 
 
In conclusion, having less than 20% of the total SNR/SPL measurements has an important impact on the 
overall analysis. The limited number of SNR and SPL measurements corresponding to only 184 EMAs 
renders them insufficient for meaningful results and analysis. Although the sound environment during 
various listening experiences was a focal point of this project and research questions, further exploration 
was not possible to conduct. 
 
Obtaining HR data for 931 out of the 1060 EMA responses, indicates capturing a great amount of HR data 
(87.83%). Our dataset includes 90,034 HR measurements. In comparison to Christensen et al., (2021) ’s 
study on the influence of everyday sounds on cardiovascular stress, the researchers analysed 25,193 data 
records from 56 participants for 971 unique participant-days. This amount of data corresponds to a subset 
of their total data with overlapping acoustic and HR information. In our study, the main issue related to 
the HR data loss is participants’ compliance. Some participants did not wear the smartwatch continuously, 
either taking it off for a few hours or not wearing it at all for a day or two (Feedback Questionnaire, 
Appendix B - Results). Additionally, one participant used a Garmin device, which posed a challenge for 
data collection since the existing method could not collect HR measurements from Garmin watches. 
 
The calculated mean HRs of this study are closely similar (78.54 bpm from all logged data and 79.27 bpm 
corresponding to EMAs). The mean real-world HR—derived from 66,788 participants in the Health eHeart 
Study—stands at 79.1 bpm ± 14.5 (Avram et al., 2019). This study investigates real-world heart rate norms 
and includes participants with an average age of 43.3 ± 14.8 years, with 47.0% being female. Thus, the 
calculated mean HR is consistent with the mean real-world HR, suggesting good face validity for our HR 
measurements.  
 
HR is spread out from 50 to 105 bpm. The participants’ measured HR when completing the surveys were 
more evenly distributed around the mean of approximately 79 bpm. HR appears to be relatively evenly 
distributed during most hours of the day, but with some variability. The increase in HR values notable 
around noon could be attributed to lunchtime leading to a temporary elevation in HR due to digestion 
after eating (Høst et al., 1996). Additionally, the elevation of HR in the afternoon may be due to 
participants having dinner or engaging in exercise, household chores, or social activities after work hours. 
4.1.2 
Overview of EMA Responses 
75% of the completed EMAs were scheduled and the rest were self-triggered by the participants, raising 
the total number of completed surveys to 1060. The scheduled EMAs are distributed evenly among 
participants and there seems to be an inverse relationship between self-initiated surveys and adherence 
to the scheduled ones. Throughout the day, self-initiated surveys are evenly distributed, and scheduled 
==PAGE:66==
 
 
54 
 
surveys account for more than 50% of the total completions (see Figure 12). In Schinkel-Bielefeld’s, (2020) 
work, participants were also encouraged to initiate EMAs by themselves, apart from receiving triggered 
ones. They concluded that self-initiated EMAs complemented triggered EMAs by capturing a broader 
range of diverse listening situations. 
 
The total compliance rate including both scheduled and self-initiated EMAs was calculated at 74.38% and 
participants completed 7.21 surveys on average per day. The lower compliance rate of scheduled surveys 
calculated at 65.38% ± 18.13 is maybe related to participants avoiding completing some scheduled surveys 
due to inconvenient timing or being unable to do it at the time of the prompt. Activities like work, social 
events, being in a class or meeting can make participants skip surveys (Xu et al., 2020). According to 
comments from the Feedback Questionnaire in Appendix B - Results, some participants opted for self-
initiated surveys due to technical issues of the SEMA3 app. They either did not receive the notifications 
for the scheduled surveys, or the scheduled surveys were not displayed properly in the SEMA3 app. 
 
In comparison to other EMA studies in the hearing research, 19 participants, both normal and impaired 
hearing, submitted 1,295 surveys in one week, reaching the same compliance rate as ours, 74.4%. In von 
Gablenz et al., (2021) study with 24 HA users during a period of 3-4 days, the compliance rate of scheduled 
surveys was rather low, 50%. The HA users were also encouraged to complete surveys on their own, 
increasing the total compliance rate to 73%, very close to our findings. Additionally, during the two-week 
duration of Andersson et al., (2023) study, the 15 normal hearing participants were also encouraged to 
self-initiate surveys. They completed in total 1,521 EMAs with an average of 7.2 ± 4.4 EMAs per day and 
per participant, very close to ours (7.21). Both studies provided similar results to ours, indicating good 
compliance. A sampling rate between 4 and 10 surveys per day is considered acceptable for a field trial of 
1-2 weeks (Xu et al., 2020). According to Schinkel-Bielefeld et al., (2023), the compliance in hearing 
researchers including normal hearing participants is challenging since these participants may lack the 
motivation derived from research directly improving their own quality of life.  
 
Regarding the distribution of listening activities across participants and during the day, interesting results 
are derived from Figure 13 and Figure 14 respectively. Similar patterns emerge from the work of 
Christensen et al., (2024) concerning hearing aid users. Figure 1c illustrates the relative frequency of EMA 
responses as a function of self-reported listening activity vs the hour of the day. Speech occurs most 
frequently during lunch hours (12:00 – 13:00), consistent with our own findings. However, in their study, 
participants appeared to listen less to speech throughout the day (27%) compared to our participants 
(42%). This difference could be attributed to our participants’ younger age and the fact that they are either 
working or studying during the day, rather than staying at home like older HA users. Additionally, ‘Sounds 
around me’ (18.2% of the total EMAs) and ‘Nothing in particular’ (17.1%) maintain dominance until 16:00 
which aligns with the ‘Ambient sounds’ (31%) category in our results. In their work, they showed that 
streaming, television, and music activities dominate the afternoon and night hours from 18:00 to 23:00, 
which can also be seen from our Figure 14. In Christensen et al., (2024) study, only watching TV made up 
27% of the total EMAs, in comparison to listening to ‘Live sounds or sounds through media device’ that 
made up only 24% of the EMAs in our study. 
 
These parallels between the two studies suggest that participants’ sound exposure, reflecting their daily 
routines and activities, have a lot of similarities for both HA users and younger individuals with normal 
==PAGE:67==
 
 
55 
 
hearing. Nevertheless, it seems that HA users would stay more at home alone during the day listening less 
to speech and more to the TV or similar. 
 
The ‘None of the above’ activity was selected when participants did not find a suitable option to describe 
their listening activity and is less than 0.1% of the total responses. If the percentage of ‘None of the above’ 
selections was substantial, these cases could be re-classified based on the other listening activities. In 
future studies, participants should be explicitly instructed to select the ‘Ambient Sounds’ option when 
faced with very quiet scenarios or a new option labeled ‘Silence’ could be added to the list. This is similar 
to ‘Nothing in particular’ option from the studies of Christensen et al., (2024) and Andersson et al., (2023). 
4.2 
Listening Activity and Intention (RQ1) 
RQ1 aims to evaluate the correspondence between self-reported listening activities and intentions. Since 
exploring the acoustic environment was deemed out of scope for this project, this part of RQ1 was not 
further explored. Contrary to the null hypothesis, our findings indicate that there is indeed a significant 
relationship between the self-reported listening activities and intentions. 
 
When participants listen to speech from one or more people, the dominant intention choice is speech 
communication (observed in over 75% of responses). However, approximately 25% of these instances are 
linked to ‘Focused listening’ or ‘Non-specific’ intentions, suggesting that not all speech-related listening 
(e.g. podcast) is necessarily conversation-oriented. Similarly, ‘Live sounds or sounds through media 
device’ was primarily associated with ‘Focused listening’ as intention, but around 25% of the responses 
suggest ‘Non-specific’ intention. This great percentage shows that participants may listen to music or 
some form of streaming but did not pay attention to the source of sound. Additionally, when encountering 
ambient sounds, participants’ intention is almost always non-specific. In the remaining 10%, the intention 
was either ‘Focused listening’ or ‘Speech communication’. This differentiation is more challenging to 
interpret, but maybe their main listening activity was very loud and distracting from their intention which 
was either ‘Focused listening’ or ‘Speech communication’. 
 
As previously mentioned in 2.3.3, the activities and intentions were chosen based on the CoSS framework 
(Wolters et al., 2016). The associations described above highlight deviations from the CoSS framework 
and the correspondence between the framework tasks and our chosen activities (see Table 3). If the 
framework was fully applicable, whenever participants selected speech as their listening activity, their 
intention would align with ‘Speech communication’. Similarly, for ‘Live sounds or sounds through a media 
device’, the intention would correspond to ‘Focused listening’. Finally, when encountering ambient 
sounds, the intention would predominantly be ‘Non-specific’. 
 
Comparably, from the study of Andersson et al., (2023), in the very similar to ours EMA question: ‘What 
are you listening to at the moment?’, the researchers provided 8 activities or listening intents the normal 
hearing participants could choose from. During the analysis, the researchers classified the listening intent 
‘nothing in particular’ as ‘non-specific’ and the intents ‘one person talking’ and ‘people talking’ as ‘speech 
communication’ of the CoSS framework. The other possible listening intents were classified as ‘focused 
listening’. The validity of the selections and the CoSS framework will be further discussed in 4.4.

4.4 
Ecological Validity of Activities and Intentions (RQ3) 
To investigate if our study design aligns better with the individuals’ actual listening environment (RQ3), 
the self-reported consistency and real-life match ratings from the EMA responses as well as the feedback 
from the participants are discussed. Addressing these concerns will enhance the accuracy and reliability 
of future studies using EMA assessments. Contrary to the null hypothesis, our findings indicate that there 
are some deviations between the listening activity and intention selections and the real-life listening 
conditions. 
 
Overall, participants generally reported consistent main listening activities. Most activities have high 
consistency ratings (above 8) and low variability, except ‘None of the above’ which is less than 0.1% of the 
total responses. ‘Live sounds or sounds through media device’ had significantly higher consistency ratings 
compared to ‘Ambient sounds,’ suggesting fewer interruptions during the former. The high consistency 
ratings enhance the reliability of the responses to subsequent survey questions. 
  
Regarding the real-life match ratings of activities and intentions, around 80% of the options were marked 
as 8 or higher, reflecting very well the real-life listening conditions. However, the 10% of low real-life 
match ratings mean that some participants felt that the EMA questions were unclear to them. ‘Ambient 
Sounds’ and ’Non-specific’ were the least representative activity and intention respectively. This indicates 
that for some of these responses, the participants might have had specific listening activities or intentions 
not represented by the categories they could select between. 
 
Upon examining the relationship between consistency and real-match ratings of the EMA self-reported 
listening activity and intention, it becomes evident that many EMAs received high ratings in both cases. 
High ecological validity is desirable for any self-report measure. Moreover, finding a positive association 
between these two ratings suggests that participants who report more representative experiences also 
tend to exhibit greater consistency in their responses, and vice versa. Such findings enhance our 
confidence in the questionnaire’s quality and interpretation. Additionally, this is a good measure to filter 
==PAGE:70==
 
 
58 
 
data in future studies. The EMAs with both low real-life match and consistency ratings, indicate low face 
validity. From the results it was interesting to notice that their correlation was not perfect; for example, 
there is a cluster of ratings with high consistency (=10) and low real-life match (=2-3), which would suggest 
that there might be a need for additional listening activity/intention category. 
Since the choices were based on the CoSS framework (Wolters et al., 2016), evaluating the real-life match 
of the selections employed in this study parallels assessing the CoSS framework, its applicability and 
effectiveness. Our results presented high overall ecological validity but with some lower ratings for non-
specific intentions, lowering the framework’s validity. 
 
In the Feedback Questionnaire, participants highlighted the need for more tailored choices for activities 
and intentions. Furthermore, participants expressed difficulty distinguishing between EMA Question #2 
(activity) and #4 (intention) and how they are different. Additionally, some participants struggled to 
quantify their responses to EMA Question #5 (real-life match) due to its unclear relation to meaning and 
purpose. As a result, these challenges may have contributed to the lower real-life match ratings. 
Requesting participants to evaluate their feelings in real-time can be complex. 
4.5 
Listening Situation and Hearing-Related Stress (RQ4) 
RQ4 investigates how the participants’ overall listening experience is affected by the self-reported 
listening activity and intention (i.e. listening situation) and how HR varies related to that. Contrary to the 
null hypothesis, our findings indicate that there is an association between an individual’s self-reported 
listening situation and their listening experience and physiological stress. 
 
Overall, participants’ listening experiences predominantly resulted in low self-reported hearing-related 
stress levels, consistent with their normal hearing status. According to Ohlenforst et al., (2017), HA users 
typically experience greater listening effort compared to normal hearing individuals in laboratory studies 
assessing speech intelligibility in noisy conditions. This suggests that in difficult listening situations in real 
life, the increased listening effort could manifest as elevated stress. For high self-reported stress levels 
(over 70%), an increase of HR was observed but for low and moderate stress ratings, HR was stable below 
average. Notably, hearing-related stress ratings did not exhibit a linear relationship with HR 
measurements and a nonlinear model should be tested. In Andersson et al., (2023)’s work, HR did not 
show a consistent association with self-reported listening experiences based on EMA ratings related to 
the pleasantness of surrounding sounds. 
 
Among all the listening activities, participants reported the least stress when engaging with ‘Live sounds 
or sounds through media devices’. This finding is consistent with the idea that listening to live music, 
watching TV, or streaming content often occurs during leisure hours, such as in the afternoon, when 
people enjoy hobbies after work. These activities are typically associated with less stressful situations. 
Additionally, the finding that ‘Focused listening’ led to the highest relaxation further validates the CoSS 
framework (Wolters et al., 2016), especially considering its division into the tasks of ‘Live sounds’ and 
‘Through media device’ (as shown in Figure 1). In conclusion, our listening intention can indeed influence 
self-reported listening experiences, a finding that aligns with the research by Andersson et al., (2023).

4.6 
Methodology 
The EMA study was designed with the focus of obtaining high quality data while minimising participant 
burden and maximising compliance. The SEMA3 app offered several advantages, including flexible survey 
creation, easy modification, offline functionality, and user-friendly operation by the survey administrator. 
Participants reported high usability but encountered specific issues, such as missed scheduled surveys due 
to notification glitches, error messages on the SEMA3 app screen, and compatibility problems with android 
smartphones. The pseudo-random EMA collection during the day, decreases the inherent bias of EMAs 
and increases the ecological validity of the reported listening situations (Andersson et al., 2021). 
 
The high overall ecological validity of self-reported activities and intentions signifies that participants’ 
reports using EMA can be highly trusted. The surveys seem to capture the people’s real-life listening 
experiences in a high degree, with some limitations regarding non-specific intentions. The 74.4% 
compliance rate was comparable to other studies and validates the methodology chosen. 
4.7 
Limitations and Future Work 
The major limitation during this study was the great sound data loss, preventing us from investigating the 
acoustic environment for different self-reported listening experiences. Further investigation into these 
technical factors that led to losing so much data is needed to identify the root cause and enable preventive 
measures for future studies. Thus, in the future, when proper sound data logging is ensured, the validity 
and consistency that was rated during every EMA, can be predicted by the sound data itself, thus 
eliminating the rating during every EMA. 
 
Regarding the selections of activities and intentions that were extensively discussed in this project, their 
number could be enriched. A suggestion could be to split these low validity choices (i.e. ‘Ambient sounds’, 
‘Non-specific’) to more options and investigate if the validity ratings increase. Increasing the number of 
choices could potentially increase the ecological validity of the methodology and improve the question 
design. Future studies could explore additional dimensions within the CoSS framework or refine existing 
ones to enhance its utility. Else, additional examples could be provided to assist participants in making 
more accurate choices. Visible examples could be incorporated within the app interface, displaying them 
under each option. Participants could view them by using a button, only when they needed guidance. 
However, balance is needed to not make the questionnaire design too complicated and affect usability, 
especially for older HA users who may not be as technologically adept. This option was not supported by 
the SEMA3 app currently used. Another suggestion to improve validity is by identifying EMAs with both 
low validity and consistency ratings, those that lack face validity can be isolated. Removing these less valid 
EMAs ensures that the remaining data better reflects users’ actual experiences. 
 
Finally, since the methodology was validated for normal hearing individuals, it can be applied during a trial 
with a hearing-impaired population. The ecological validity of the reported daily life listening activities 
and intentions using EMA, can then be compared between different populations. Further comparisons 
can be made regarding the HR fluctuations, self-reported hearing-related stress ratings and activities and 
intentions. Additionally, as mentioned above, the sound environment characteristics can be properly 
assessed and connected to participant’s listening experiences. Additionally, exploring HR responses to 
different self-reported listening experiences, is a promising research direction for further investigation.

5 Conclusion 
The aim of this study was to explore the ecological validity of self-reported listening activities, intentions and 
hearing-related stress. The comparison of consistency and real-life match ratings for the different activities 
and intentions showed high overall ecological validity, although non-specific intentions showed lower ratings. 
In addition, participants’ HR seemed to relate to hearing-related stress, which further suggests high ecological 
validity of the EMAs. The Common Sound Scenario framework guided the design of activity and intention 
selections, and deviations from the framework were observed, indicating partial applicability to EMA studies. 
 
Participants primarily chose non-specific listening intentions, followed by speech communication and 
focused listening. Intentions were evenly distributed throughout the day, with peaks of speech 
communication during lunch and dinner hours when conversation likely occurs. Overall, participants 
reported low hearing-related stress levels, consistent with their normal hearing status, and their HR related 
to the reported stress levels in a non-linear fashion. Listening intention influenced experiences, with live 
sounds and focused listening reported being the least stressful. 
 
In comparison to other EMA studies involving both normal hearing and hearing-impaired individuals, good 
face validity was demonstrated by our results. Specifically, HR measurements presented good face validity, 
and completed EMAs showed high compliance rates, as evidenced by both scheduled and self-triggered 
responses. However, due to technical connectivity issues and potential participant non-compliance, an 
analysis of sound environments was not included. 
 
Despite encountering a few technical issues reported by participants, the SEMA3 app, used for survey 
delivery, was easy to use by the survey administrator and demonstrated high usability. EMAs offer strong 
ecological validity, particularly when assessing specific listening intentions and activities. Overall, the study’s 
methodology was validated and can set the stage for future applications with hearing-impaired participants.

Test flow in detail/ Protocol 
The test flow following the participant recruitment is presented below in detail. It includes all the steps 
from preparing for the first meeting until the last meeting with each participant and the data collection. 
The 
test 
flow 
is 
summarized 
in  
Figure 32. 
 
1. Preparation for the First Meeting with the Test Participant (TP) 
• 
Checklist #1 (see attached in the Appendix) is meticulously completed during this preparation phase to 
ensure that all necessary steps are executed accurately, setting the stage for a successful initial meeting 
with the TP. 
• 
Email Communication: Send an email to the TP, requesting them to download specific apps onto their 
personal phone before the scheduled meeting. This proactive step streamlines the meeting process. 
• 
Equipment Readiness: Assemble a bag of essential equipment, including: 
o Intent™ Hearing Aids (HAs) 
o iPhone for Intent™ HAs 
o More HA Clip 
o iPhone for More HA (provided only if the TP does not already possess an iPhone) 
o Fitbit Smartwatch 
Additionally, it is important to ensure that the hearing aids, iPhone(s) (if provided), and smartwatch 
are charged and that their chargers are also provided to the TP. 
• 
Document Preparation: 
o Consent Form: Participants are required to sign this form before commencing the trial. It grants 
consent for their participation and the utilization of their data. TPs from Oticon sign the Oticon 
Consent Form (see attached in the Appendix). TPs outside of Oticon sign the DTU Consent Form (see 
attached in the Appendix). 
o TP Characteristics & Criteria: This document collects TP-specific information, including age, gender, 
and lifestyle. It also outlines inclusion and exclusion criteria, crucial for determining whether the TP’s 
data can be included in the final analysis. 
o Checklist #2: A comprehensive guide that ensures no critical details are overlooked during the first 
meeting with the TP. 
o Instructions: Participants receive this document for reference throughout the trial. It provides 
clarifications for each EMA survey question, guidance on handling the smartwatch, Intent™ HAs, and 
More HA, as well as key considerations to keep in mind. 
 
2. First Meeting with TP 
The scheduled first meeting with the test participant (TP) takes place at KBN’s Technical Room, situated 
in Smørum (Oticon’s headquarters). The meeting is allocated a duration of 75 minutes. During this time, 
the TP is provided with all the necessary equipment and receives detailed instructions regarding their role 
in the trial. Additionally, the fitting of the hearing aids occurs. The meeting follows the outlined schedule: 
 
• 
Practicalities: In the initial segment of the meeting the TP is presented with an overview of the trial and 
the purpose of the trial and the specific tasks are briefly explained. The TP proceeds to sign the Consent 
==PAGE:98==
 
 
86 
 
Form, granting permission for participation. The TP Characteristics & Criteria document is completed, 
capturing essential TP-specific information. 
• 
Task Explanation: Next, the tasks are elaborated upon in detail. First, the TP receives comprehensive 
guidance on completing the EMA surveys using the SEMA3 app. Then, detailed instructions are provided 
to the TP regarding the proper use of the HA clip and the Evaluate HA app. The TP is afterwards informed 
about the usage of the Fitbit smartwatch and the associated Fitbit app. 
• 
Intent™ HA Fitting: The clinician performs the HA fitting and activates data logging. Additionally, guidance 
is provided on the correct usage of the Intent™ HA and the Oticon Companion HA app. 
• 
Conclusion: Towards the end of the meeting the TP completes a final EMA survey for practice. Then, the 
date and time for the last meeting are selected and any remaining questions or concerns are addressed. 
The TP receives a bag containing all the necessary equipment and printed Instructions. They are 
encouraged to reach out to the researcher via phone or email if they have any questions or encounter 
issues (Schinkel-Bielefeld et al., 2023).  
 
3. During the 7-Day Trial 
Checklist #3 is filled out to ensure that the TP receives EMA survey notifications promptly on their first 
day of the trial. Throughout the 7-day trial regular communication and check-ins with the TP occur to 
verify that all aspects of the trial are proceeding smoothly (Schinkel-Bielefeld et al., 2023).  
 
4. After the 7-Day Trial 
On the first day following the 7-day trial it is confirmed that the TP has successfully initiated the trial. 
Regular contact is maintained with the TP to ensure continued smooth progress. 
 
5. Right After the 7-Day Trial 
The TP is requested to complete a feedback questionnaire. 
 
6. Last Meeting with TP 
During the final meeting all loaned equipment is collected from the TP. Moreover, sound and HR data 
collected during the 7-days is gathered. Finally, a comprehensive discussion takes place, focusing on the 
TP’s experiences and insights.

Feedback Questionnaire - Intent HAs feedback 
Figure 34 illustrates the responses of the 16 participants who wore the Intent HAs during the trial to the 
Question #4: On a scale from 1 to 5, please rate the following sentence: Wearing the Intent Hearing Aids 
from 9AM to 9PM was very comfortable.  
 
Figure 34 Feedback Questionnaire, responses to Q#4: Intent HAs. 
Based on the figure, it is evident that a significant portion of participants experienced discomfort while 
wearing and using the hearing aids. Connectivity issues were present often and for a lot of participants, 
despite fully charging the HAs. Troubleshooting steps included turning them off, connecting to the 
charger, and disconnecting and reconnecting Bluetooth. Restarting the phone also helped in some cases. 
Wearing glasses simultaneously with the hearing aids proved difficult for some participants and the HAs 
caused discomfort during physical activity. Some users experienced heaviness in the head or ear pain after 
wearing the aids for extended periods. Finally, a few participants noticed a slight voice delay (echo) and 
struggled to hear conversations at their own table in noisy places like the canteen or restaurants.
