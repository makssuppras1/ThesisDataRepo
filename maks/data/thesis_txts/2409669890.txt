Abstract
This thesis investigates cost savings for a fleet of electric vehicles based on different
market participation and charging strategies while allowing to act in the regulating
market. A two-stage stochastic optimization problem is formulated. It includes uncertainties
from driving behaviour from a local business in Frederiksberg, Copenhagen area, and
regulating prices as well as a battery degradation model. Reductions in cost from smart,
unidirectional and bidirectional charging are compared to the current state of the art,
dumb charging, and hence are assumed to be profits. Resulting from this, the business
opportunities for two different market models are investigated. The current standard
market model is characterized by the existence of an aggregator executing market actions
for the vehicles. The second model is a possible future DSO market allowing small scale
trades by individual vehicles.
Compared to dumb charging, smart charging provides already an adequate reduction
with a very small effort while unidirectional strategies are able to more than double
the previously achieved savings.
The bidirectional charging strategy yields into the
most dominant profits.
However, when contractually limiting availability to allow
participation only before and after working hours, significantly less savings can be
expected.
Smart charging and unidirectional charging are nearly unaffected by that
change since their bids are usually not during the working hours. Further, it is identified
that driving behaviours have a large influence on savings.
In addition to that, this
thesis also shows that calendar aging, when verifiable implemented, is one of the largest
possibilities to decrease cost due to shortened battery lifetime.
In the end, it is concluded that it is currently a challenge to implement bidirectional
charging in a business model because additional charger expenses are expected to be
covered only after 14 years. Having an aggregator as the fleet manager using unidirectional
charging strategies, a large fleet is required to run profitably. Due to the fragmentation
of savings between the company and the EV user, the most favourable approach for a
fleet manager is owning the battery and offering rental reduction. In contrast to that, it
is expected that a new DSO market allowing single vehicles to participate provides the
largest incentives for electricity market involvement. Savings can be passed more directly
to the EV user and moreover, additional services can be traded to raise profitability.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
During the past decades, governments as well as organizations are working on reducing
negative impacts caused by global warming. In this process, green house gas emissions
(GHG) are determined to be the prime reason for the climate change [1]. As a result,
the European Union has introduced legislation and policies to approach this problem. A
roadmap has been implemented containing several interim targets in 2020, 2030 and 2040
to achieve a 80-95% reduction compared to the 1990 level in 2050 [2]. The largest sectors
with the highest potential in Europe are the energy and transport sectors. Combined,
they are responsible for over 50% of the total GHG emissions while the road traffic alone
accounts for approximately 18% [3]. Since the production of power and heat is still, to
the largest extent, based on fossil fuels, the transition to renewable energy technologies
has by far the largest potential. In terms of road transport however, the development
to vehicles with an electrical powertrain do not grow as quick as expected due to the
high investment costs. As a result, it is only in Norway and the Netherlands where

1.1
Context and methodology
In the future, the view on vehicles will change rapidly. Currently, they are only seen as a
means of transportation. However, due to the integration of vehicles into the electricity
market, new possibilities and challenges arise. Subsequently, an EV acts not only as
a conservative energy consumer, it can further evolve to be an energy supplier. The
large storage capacity can be used as a flexible unit to utilize fluctuations in the energy
market for purchasing cheap power, its storage and afterwards selling for profit. The
strong relationship between the energy prices and power penetration linked with the
electricity demand allows to utilize beneficial situations by active market participation.
It follows that efforts are made to optimize the integration of EV’s under different point
of views.
Nevertheless, a beneficial implementation needs to deal with several key issues.
At
first, electric vehicles are nowadays charged right away when they are plugged into a
socket [5]. This so called dumb charging is not only harmful for the grid because of

to support with additional capacity. Hence, smart market participation strategies have
to be implemented and restricted to the current market rules. It will, if well managed,

supporting, GHG emission reducing and profit earning assets. Thus, this thesis aims
to propose and optimize different charging and market participation models to decrease
charging cost.
Although the advantages for the power system and the revenues for the owners of electric

When speaking of technical constraints of EV it is most of the time restricted to the
fear of running out of power before reaching the destination (range anxiety) which is

2.1
Day-ahead market
At first, this section is providing some general information about the danish power
system. Afterwards, the Day-ahead market operator Nord Pool is introduced and facts
about the operation are stated including parts of the framework which are considered in
this study.
Due to geographic reasons, Denmark has divided its energy system into two different
transmission grids. Western Denmark corresponds to DK1 and is especially integrated
into the continental European power system. DK2 refers to the eastern Danish energy
system and is in particular connected to the Nordic grid. Both areas, DK1 and DK2,
have a special 600MW DC interconnection since 2010 through the Great Belt which
separates both systems [14]. The power systems are not only physically divided, they
have also their own bidding markets to account for technical constraints.
To run a more efficient electricity market with a higher security of supply, Nordic
countries started deregulating and merging marketplaces in the early 1990s.
Hence
it resulted into the today’s known marketplace Nord Pool [15]. Over the years, more
countries in the Baltic area and the UK have joined the market. An overview of the
current bidding areas can be seen in the Appendix A in figure A.1.
Thus, DK1 as
well as DK2 participate both in the market of Nord Pool.
Further initiatives like
the Price Coupling of Regions (PCR) project try to involve even more countries in
Europe to reduce prices, increase social welfare and harmonize the overall energy systems.
Therefore, a higher integration of Denmarks bidding areas into the European market is

The Day-ahead market is the most important marketplace for trading power and determining
buyer and seller for actual energy delivery and consumption. Hence, the mechanism
for deciding who gets the energy from which provider is crucial for a reliable and
cost effective energy supply.
This dispatch is done with other European states, the
EUPHEMIA algorithm within PCR is used [17].
The goal of the algorithm is to
maximizes social welfare and to prevent situations where the capacity of relevant network
elements are exceeded. Consequently, the most competitive price arise and an efficient
capacity allocation is provided [18]. Main inputs are special network data specific for
each region, topology data to distinguish between different bidding and network areas as
well as orders from market participants. A simplified illustration of the market clearing
can be seen in figure 2.1.

After receiving all bids regarding supply and demand, the algorithm sorts the production
offers from low to high prices and the consumption from high to low price offers. In
here, the producers are usually bidding their marginal production cost and the consumer
their willingness to pay for energy[19]. The intersection of both curves determine the
equilibrium price and production. The producers with bids below the equilibrium price
are accepted and receive the highest accepted bid as a compensation for the generated

price are accepted as well and have to pay the equilibrium price for the energy they
receive. In addition to this simple presentation technical constraints need to be added

In the Day-ahead market of Nord Pool, participants have to place their bid until gate
closure time which is set to be at 12:00 before operation day[20]. Three different offer
types are available. Hourly orders, Block orders and Flexible orders. Block orders are
especially suited for, and used from generation units where the ramp up is an important
factor. Flexible orders are block orders with a maximum span of 24 hours where the
algorithm decides the best time for acceptance of offers. The advantage of EV’s are
that they do not have the issue of start up constraints. Hence, they can participate
with hourly orders and bids and utilize their flexibility and capability for dynamic
operation. In addition, it increases flexibility due to the uncertain availability of the
vehicles. Therefore, the smallest time frame is chosen to be modelled in the optimization.

Market for ancillary services
In this section, it is at first introduced which kind of services for can be provided.

In every area, the transmission system operator (TSO) is responsible to maintain physical
properties like frequency and voltage stability and furthermore, balance supply and
demand within its region until the time of delivery.
Imbalances can be caused by
differences between the actual demand, the predicted and scheduled supply or unexpected
failures within generation or the grid infrastructure[5]. Since more distributed energy
resources, which are volatile, will be integrated to reduce GHG emissions, the energy
production will become more uncertain and remaining power quality and safety will be
even more important. The approach is to provide a common regulating power market
where upward and downward regulating is bought to tackle imbalances in demand and
supply[20]. In Denmark the TSO Energinet.dk is running the market for eastern and
western Denmark. However, Energinet.dk has a special situation in here since it has to
deal with two different bidding areas. Thus the market for DK1 has a structure closer
to central Europe and DK2 is more integrated into the Nordic one which resulted in a
special collaboration with Svenska Kraftät in Sweden. In the following the framework for
provision of balancing power in eastern Denmark is described since this study will cover
this particular participation. In appendix A on can also read about alternative power
markets and products like the Frequency controlled normal operation reserve (FNR) and
Frequency controlled disturbance reserve (FDR).
Balancing power is activated by Energinet.dk for upward and downward regulating to

3.1
Charging strategies
In this section, basic charging strategies are introduced and their characteristics are
outlined. This will help to understand the potential degree of participation and the
opportunities which follow from them.
To begin with, the state of the art EV charging strategy is introduced: dumb charging.
The basic characteristic is that the vehicles are charged right away when they are plugged
in. It represents the user preference to have a the largest possible enrgy level at all
times. The power is furthermore the maximum charger capacity. Subsequently, the
time for the energy exchange is only dependent on the arrival of the vehicle. The most
common return time is between 16 and 18 pm. Considering the general daily trend of
electricity prices, it is trivial that these periods are the ones with the highest prices
since energy consumption is already high. From this it follows that purchasing power
is costly during that time. In addition, some specific battery degradation factors are
especially unfavorable in this strategy which is further mentioned in chapter 4. Since
dumb charging is currently used for nearly all EV’s the assumption is that any reduction
in cost are seen as profits.
The second charging strategy is smart charging. The key characteristic is in here that
the power purchase is delayed to time periods where prices are low. The user preference
is here to generate savings with a very very low effort. This requires an optimization with
price forecasts as an input. Also degradation is a factor which has its influence on the
charging strategy. Compared to dumb charging, smart charging optimizes already the
cost by being able to decide when and how much to charge. Moreover, the optimization

while dumb charging does not leave an option for it.
The last basic strategy is charging with regulating market participation.
The user

unidirectional and bidirectional which are explained later.
While having the basic

temperatures between 0-20 ◦C reasonable trade offs [Leng2015] [26] which has also
been considered in this thesis.
The next important effect is due to the charge and discharge current. For applicability
it is usually mentioned in terms of C-rates. Corresponding to Ohm’s law, the larger
the charge or discharge C-rate, the higher the loss due to internal resistance. Thus, the
losses inefficiencies are converted into heat in the cell which increases the temperature
level.
Not only that higher C-rates cause temporary losses during operation due to
a higher heat production, also long-term effects are resulting from that by the same
degradation effects described beforehand. High continuous C-rates should be avoided.
But on the other hand, short pulses do not damage the cell in all situations as long
as the recovery time is sufficient enough. The state of charge is besides an important
factor for battery degradation. The higher the SOC is, the larger the degradation gets

The relevance of the degradation model can be acknowledge when comparing two cases
of charging. The dumb charging which is right now the state of art and bidirectional
strategies.
The characteristic of the currently used pattern is that the vehicle will always be charged
to the upper boundary. Consequently, the battery aging will be significantly accelerated
as described in section 4.2.3.
Hence, the lifetime savings are already very intuitive
when implementing smart charging which reduces the average state of charge over the
whole lifetime. As well, long consecutive charging with high DOD also damages the
battery significantly. Breaks between charging itself, and discharging, cycles help the
electro-chemical system to rest which is highlighted by [31]. Hence, situations will occur
where dependent on the chosen time resolution the charging occurs in a step pattern.
Furthermore, when charging in a smart way, feasible situation can occur where it is
even beneficial to use the flexibility of the SOC to participate in the regulating market.
Consequently, a beneficial situation for the EV owner can occur.
Many studies have used a constant cost for V2G application.
However, due to the
complex battery lifetime behaviour this approximation can lead to major errors and do
not give any indication which situations are beneficial or not. The outcome of that could
be more or less always a maximum power discharge and a maximum depth of discharge
to utilize the the feasible price area. Consequences are only shown with an average cost
which do not replicate the damages. Due to the fact that the EV owner has an interest
to use the battery for transportation primarily. Moreover, to prevent bad marketing it
is important to face degradation realistically. Therefore, the implementation of a more
advanced degradation model needs to be acknowledged. Since the scope of this thesis

CHAPTER 6
Stochastic optimization
In this chapter, the optimization method is introduced. After reading the literature it
was decided to use two-stage optimization programming. The most common approach
in optimization is taking deterministic forecasts to predict optimal points of operation.
However, this is only an acceptable method if the predicted parameters are not too
much influenced by uncertainty. Admittedly this is rarely the case. Furthermore, the
investigations of [11] show evidence that implementing uncertainties into the optimization
have significant improvements for regulating market participation of electric vehicles. In
this study, electricity prices as well as driving patterns can not be predicted with full
certainty. Thus a deterministic approach contains a lot of shortcomings since it always
takes just one deterministic prediction. A different, though more complex method is
stochastic programming. In there, several approaches are available. Robust optimization
is commonly used to always guarantee feasibility since it covers the minimum and
maximum possible scenarios but yields into more conservative outcomes. However, this
approach yields into conservative outcomes and reduced regulating market participation
in this thesis significantly. Whereas a two-stage stochastic optimization model takes the
probability distribution into account and yields into better objective values which also
is why it was chosen. A first stage ”here and now” decision, commonly the Day-ahead
charging, based on uncertain second stage scenarios which are the driving and regulating
price scenarios in this case. A set of scenarios including the respective probabilities are
used as an input
Hence at first, the method is introduced with a small example comparing it to a
deterministic optimization.
After that, scenarios are built based on the previously
introduced data which are an input for the optimization problem.
Since large sets
of scenarios are resulting from that, a scenario reduction method is presented to reduce
calculation time.

the basic difference of these studies are the assumptions compared to this thesis. When

by a lower probability. As a result, a possible improvement would be the extension of
the calculation period to take the expected consumption into account to distribute the
charging cycles. But on the other hand, the cost were reduced as expected for all cars
apart from V1..

8.6.1
Degradation cost adjustment factor
Since the degradation cost from degradation is a large factor for the degree of participation,
this factor needs to be investigated further. in addition, due to data scarcity described
in section 4.3 the influence has to be checked. Therefore, a sensitivity analysis has been
performed and the results are shown in this section.
This analysis was performed especially for the bidirectional market participation since
the influence of the degradation is the largest in this strategy. Figure E.7 in the appendix
E.4 shows for that the outcome for altering the degradation adjustment factor αDeg,veh
from 0.4 up to 0.8.
The resulting cost for the fleet are 102 DKK, 671 DKK, 1192 DKK and 1442 DKK
respectively. 951 DKK is the value for αDeg,veh = 0.596. Therefore a clear trend can
be seen.
When lowering the degradation factor gradually, the cost decrease at first
relatively constant. However, the slope increases and with a factor of 0.4 the expenses
falls noticeable more.
This behaviour can be explained by the influence of the factor on the non linear cycle
degradation function.
When multiplying the aging while having shallow cycles, the
impact of the factor is relatively tiny because the value itself is comparably small. The
deeper the cycle gets, the degradation is rising non linear. Thus the effect of multiplying
the same adjustment factor with larger values of the function shown in figure 4.2 results
in an even steeper curve. The model will consequently decide to reduce charging when
it is getting too expensive and cuts also regulating participation. That fact explains the
smaller slope when increasing the degradation factor.
In the opposite direction, the reduction in cost gets even more dominant. When decreasing
the adjustment factor, the non linear degradation function flattens out. As a result, the
vehicle can decide to charge deeper while having the same degradation cost and sell
more energy to utilize price peaks.
The modification of the adjustment factor can also be seen as a price development.
The current price range as described in chapter 4.3 is located within a realistic margin.
When battery production cost are lowered in the next few years, it can ensure a more
profitable participation for electric vehicles. Therefore, regulating market participation
have potentials in the future.

9.1
Conclusion
In the following, the outcomes of this study are presented by at first introducing the
inputs and afterwards, summarizing the cost savings for possible business opportunities.
Four different basic market participation strategies are used in this study. These are
dumb charging which serves as the reference case and smart, unidirectional and bidirectional
charging. Savings in cost compared to dumb charging are assumed to be profits which
are increasing the opportunities for business models. In particular, two different models
are considered. In the first case, there is an aggregator who manages the charging for
a complete fleet of vehicles and acts in the electricity markets in their place.

own charging.

In regards of new business opportunities, frequency regulating was also highly considered
in the beginning of this study.
The possibility of large profits was leading to the
assumption that it is already a very promising option. While studying battery degradation
in this matter, it became clear that fuelled by the data scarcity on battery degradation,
assumptions on cost could not be justified enough. Comparable studies showed furthermore
to a large extend highly questionable outcomes. It is however very likely that profits can
be earned by providing frequency regulation. A first possibility would be to let day-ahead
charging react to frequency deviations. This could simplify the complexity and provide
a simple first solution. But to what extend the battery lifetime will be affected should
be tested in real world experiments. While studying that issue, it became likely that the
battery aging is increasing significantly with full bidirectional participation which could
lead to the outcome that on the one hand, profits are very high but on the other hand,
the battery needs to be exchanged way earlier than expected. Therefore, it is important
to dose frequency regulating offers. To not endanger the transition to electric mobility
by too optimistic ancillary market participation, the battery degradation has to be taken
into account and examined further. As a result, this study highly suggests experiments
to gain real world data for researching the actual impact of frequency service provision
to develop accurate models and help EV to be a successfully implemented into the power
system.

Frequency regulation market frameworks and

The frequency controlled normal operation reserve (FNR) is for the DK2 regulation
market the equivalent for the primary reserve. The FNR must be within 49.9-50.1 Hz
and has to be supplied in frequency deviations of +\−100 mHz. It needs to be supplied
linearly between deviations of 0-100 mHz and after 150 s the full activated reserve power
has to be supplied [24]. Furthermore, frequency measurements have to have a sensitivity
of +/- 10 mHz as well as a Supervisory control and data acquisition control system
(SCADA) with a resolution of less than 1 s to respond accurately. Communication to
the control center of Energinet.dk in Erritsø ist required containing status of production,
grid connection, up and downwards FNR as well as net production at the connection
point. More importantly 1.3.1.4 states that the aggregation of smaller units is allowed
as long as the requirements are fulfilled[24].
Auction deadlines are separated into two different types.
Energinet.dk and Svenska
Kraftnät collaborate in the daily purchase auction where the required amount is purchased
two days before (D-2) operation for block bids up to six hours at 15:00 and the remaining
part one day before (D-1) 19:00 for single and block bids up to three hours. The bids are
selected due to the lowest prices and they are accepted entirely or not at all. Notification
times for acceptance are for D-2 16:00 two days before the day of operation and for
D-1 at 21:00 on the day before operation.

regards to EV fleet aggregator, is that the minimum power to enter the market is 0.3

B.1
Review of EV participation for frequency
services and regulations also in regards of
future DSO markets
Participation in frequency regulation has certain requirements. Several electric vehicles
need to be merged to a fleet to act as a fully recognized market participant [52]. Technical
feasibility has been proven by [51]. The measured response times for 3 EV from the
type Nissan Leaf without any special V2G capability showed evidence that the response
time is while using droop controls within the required ranges for frequency controlled
disturbance.
However, the power steps used are in 1 A steps so that the frequency
support is not linear on small scale. Hence, it has been suggested to investigate an EV
fleet of at least 10 cars to ramp-up together yielding in a linear supply growth. Also the
control via internet has been proven successful and within requirements.
Moreover, [23] investigated the regulatory framework with respect to the participation of
EV in the frequency control and voltage support. For a monitoring the system, the DSO
must have a clear overview over the EV in the system. Therefore, the installation of
smart meter capable of a 2 way communication is necessary. Admittedly, some European
member states have not entirely implemented the regulatory of having a penetration of
having equipped 80% of the consumers with devices.
Thus, the penetration of this
technology can not be assumed to be the same in each country. Also the regulation that
everyone has to have a non-discriminatory access to the grid can be a challenge. Because
of the high consumption of EV and the higher need of reactive power compensation, the
DSO has to adapt its grid corresponding to the new requirements. Comparably to laws
which force already some PV owners to supply reactive power, EV owner also could
fall under this constraint [53]. In this case however, the DSO can save investments into
hardware providing voltage stability. Additionally with communication requirements
that could lead to more investments needed by the EV owner, the distribution of cost
must be performed in a fair way between the DSO and the EV owners. Hand in hand
goes with that also the standardization of communication interfaces. Nowadays, efforts
have not been successful in this regards so that this issue is still very important to be
solved [23]. When working as an aggregator, DSO’s have different frameworks sometimes

B.2
Review of EV participation for reactive power
services and regulations
Voltage support by providing reactive power can be very advantageous for the DSO
without significant losses nor operational costs of the DSO. Since EV’s are placed
all over the distribution grid, they are especially beneficial to sustain voltage levels
even in remote buses, ensuring with their hardware to react in real-time.
Hence,
this would reduce furthermore the DSO’s investment cost for stationary components.
However, converter in the EV need to be oversized which result in additional costs for
the manufacturer and as a matter fact, the consumer. Furthermore, since there is still
a lack of communication infrastructure and the converter can operate autonomously, it
is unlikely that a compensation system for the service can be implemented during this
time[5]. Taking the existing regulatories into account, reactive power support can be
introduced as a mandatory support since for PV units in distribution grids in Germany
larger than 4.6kV ar already need to compensate in certain circumstances[53]. It can
be furthermore justified because this services does not require any support by the most
expensive component of the EV, the battery, and is moreover not a limiting factor while
charging when the converter is sized large enough.
In the end, it is highly dependent on the distribution grid’s strength if the reactive
power support service is sufficiently helping the system. Taking into account the cost
for installation additional components within the EV which will be passed to the owners,
a market for compensation could be implemented to justify this service. On the other
hand, the additional cost on the EV could be compared to the saved investments by
the DSO not having to provide stationary capacitor banks. Hence, social welfare can be
maximized by implementing, however a fair compensation system needs to be developed
[5]. Also starting a new market platform could for these services can connote significant
costs which will maybe not be feasible when the market only contains a small financial
volume.

implemented to apply it within the current model and reduce calculation time. Thus,

it was decided to use SOS2 to implement the piece-wise linear functions.
