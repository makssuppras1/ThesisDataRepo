Abstract
Desphenyl-chloridazon (DPC) and methyl-desphenyl-chloridazon (MDPC) which are metabolites of the se-
lective herbicide chloridazon (CLZ) are among the most commonly found pesticides in Danish groundwater.
CLZ degrades relatively fast to DPC under aerobic conditions, whereas degradation under anaerobic condi-
tions are slower. Degradation of DPC to MDPC has been observed in aerobic conditions, however further
pathways are unclear. Only a limited amount of studies have investigated the degradation of CLZ and
metabolites in water/sediment systems. This project assess biodegradation in shallow groundwater contam-
inated by a point source of CLZ and its metabolites. This includes a characterization of the degradation
at two contaminated sites (Site 1 and Site 2). A laboratory experiment was conducted using water and
sediment from Site 1 spiked with CLZ or DPC. The eﬀects of biostimulation with CH4 and O2 and bioaug-
mentation with methane oxidizing bacteria (MOB) were evaluated.
Water samples from two sampling campaigns and previous investigations indicated that contamination at
Site 1 and Site 2 consisted primarily of DPC. Only low concentrations of MDPC were found, indicating
slow degradation. MDPC was detected only to a depth of 14 m bgl., and the molar ratio indicated that the
degradation occurred primarily under aerobic-nitrate reducing conditions in the upper 6 m.
CH4 and O2 were measured in all microcosms in the degradation experiment, as a decrease is a sign of
biological activity and an indication of degradation. Consumption of was highest in the microcosms with
MOB. Growth was visually evident in the biostimulated and bioaugmented microcosms as the water became
unclear, indicating cell growth. In the degradation experiment, the spiked CLZ was degraded to DPC and
a small amount of MDPC in the microcosms containing sediment from the originally aerobic sediment
(D1: 2.5-3.0 m bgl.). This was observed after a lag phase of 25 days, and DT50 values of 46.3-58.9 days
were determined. The microcosms containing sediment that was originally anaerobic (D2: 7.5-8.0 m bgl.)
and were spiked with CLZ did not show a clear degradation curve.
An incomplete mass balance was
observed, which could originate from a continuous sorption, bound residues, or a slow degradation. Limited
degradation occurred in microcosms spiked with DPC (9.2% compared to initial concentrations). The results
of the degradation experiment did not indicate that biostimulation with O2 and CH4 or bioaugmentation
with MOB enhanced the degradation of CLZ and DPC. Based on these results, it is not considered a
viable remediation strategy for the two sites. Further investigations is required to assess the potential for
bioremediation with other types of amendments and bacteria.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Pesticides are frequently detected in soil and groundwater, mainly due to the extensive application in conven-
tional agriculture (Torrentó et al. 2019, Melsbach et al. 2020). Groundwater is an important water source in
Denmark, as it accounts for 99% of all drinking water and is therefore frequently screened for contaminants
(Miljøministeriet, 2014). Detection of pesticides in groundwater poses a problem for the groundwater re-
source and is often a reason for closing abstraction wells when concentrations exceed the groundwater quality
criteria. In Denmark, the groundwater quality criteria are 0.1 µg/l for individual pesticides (also valid for
relevant and non-relevant metabolites) and 0.5 µg/l for the sum of individual pesticides (Retsinformation,
2021). Pesticide contamination can originate from point sources characterized by high concentrations in
small areas or from diﬀuse sources where lower concentrations in larger areas are observed (Miljøstyrelsen,
2020a).
Some of the most frequent found pesticides in both groundwater and surface water in Denmark and the rest of
Europe are desphenylchloridazon (DPC) and methyldesphenylchloridazon (MDPC), two known degradation
product of the herbicide chloridazon (CLZ) (Loos et al. 2010, Schuhmann et al. 2016).
CLZ is a systematic soil and leaf herbicide used mainly for weed control in cultivation of sugar beets, both as
fodder beets and sugar beets, along with onions, bulbs, and beetroots (Miljøstyrelsen, 2020a). CLZ was sold
in Denmark from 1963 to 1996, where it was phased out and has been banned since 2012. However, it is still
approved and used in some European countries and in Australia (PPDB, 2021). CLZ and its metabolites
have high solubility and low sorption tendency, indicating high mobility in soil and a great potential to enter
and pollute surface and groundwater (EFSA 2007, Khan et al. 2012). DPC has however been found to be
long-term stable in soil, and reservoirs have been detected in the unsaturated zone, acting as a long-term
source for leaching to groundwater (Schuhmann et al. 2016, Hintze 2020).
The Danish Environmental Protection Agency and GEUS conducted a national screening in 2020 as part of
the Danish groundwater surveillance (GRUMO), where DPC and MDPC were among the most frequently
found pesticides. 31.5% of all sampled GRUMO water wells contained DPC, with 16.8% exceeding the
quality criteria (GEUS, 2020b). MDPC was found in 16.4% of all sampled screens and 6.4% exceeded the
quality criteria. Similar results have been found for water well screens in 2020 for DPC and MDPC.

==PAGE:18==
Degradation of pesticides can be divided into abiotic and biotic, with the biotic process often being the
dominant. The ﬁrst report on biodegradation of CLZ was described by Engvild and Jensen (1969), where
bacteria using CLZ as its sole carbon source was isolated from aerobic topsoil. MDPC was ﬁrst discovered
in 2007 in a non-target screening of groundwater and surface water under aerobic conditions (Schuhmann
et al. (2016), with reference to Weber et al. 2007). Several studies show that CLZ degrades relatively
fast into DPC under aerobic conditions in topsoil and water, whereas a slower degradation rate is found
under anaerobic conditions (Schuhmann et al. 2016, Buttiglieri et al. 2009, EFSA 2007). The degradation of
DPC to MDPC has been observed in aerobic conditions, however further degradation pathways are unclear
(Melsbach et al., 2020).
For sites contaminated by point sources, where the groundwater quality criteria are often exceeded, an inves-
tigation, risk assessment, and consideration of possible remediation types are often relevant (Miljøstyrelsen,
2014). Bioaugmentation and biostimulation are two types of remediation which can enhance degradation
of for example pesticides. While biostimulation involves adding nutrients and other supplementary com-
ponents to the natural microbial population to initiate and enhance degradation, bioaugmentation is the
introduction of microorganisms into the subsurface or aquifer.
Using methane oxidizing bacteria (MOB) has shown to improve the degradation of some types of microp-
ollutants including pesticides (Benner et al. (2015), Verhagen (2015), Hanson and Hanson (1996)). This is
due to the MOB possessing enzymes with broad substrate speciﬁcity, enabling them to oxidize, degrade and
transform organic molecules co-metabolically (Wendlandt et al., 2010). Studies of the degradation of CLZ
using MOB is limited but have been conducted in laboratory studies in growth media and in ﬁlter sand
from water treatment plants (Verhagen 2015, Hedegaard 2018).
As ﬁndings of CLZ and metabolites threaten groundwater as a resource for drinking water, there is a need
to investigate possible remediation techniques for contaminated sites to mitigate the risk. Furthermore,
laboratory studies of CLZ and DPC degradation in water-sediment environments are important to conduct
to understand the fate of contamination. Two contaminated sites are studied, Site 1 and Site 2, which
have been investigated by WSP A/S and Region Zealand since 2015. The sites have primarily been used
for farming and by agricultural contractors. The sites have been contaminated with herbicides, primarily
phenoxy acids and CLZ. Several point sources with DPC and phenoxy acids have been found at both sites.
The overall scope of this project is to assess biodegradation in shallow groundwater contaminated by a point
source of CLZ and its metabolites. The speciﬁc objectives are:
• Characterize and assess degradation of CLZ and DPC based on data from previous investigations and
new data of CLZ and metabolites from groundwater samples near the contaminant point sources
• Plan and conduct ﬁeld work, including water samples and sediment samples from the sites
• Design and perform laboratory experiments to study degradation of CLZ, DPC and formation of
MDPC in batches with water and aquifer material spiked with CLZ or DPC
• Assess the degradation of CLZ, DPC, and study the eﬀects of biostimulation with CH4 and O2 and
bioaugmentation with Methane Oxidizing Bacteria (MOB)

Chapter 2
Chloridazon & Metabolites
The following chapter describes chloridazon and its metabolites along with the ﬁndings in groundwater in
Denmark. In addition, the transport in soil and groundwater is presented in connection with the physio-
chemical properties of the compounds.
Chloridazon (sometimes called pyrazon) is a herbicide used primarily for weed control in the cultivation of
sugar beets (fodder beets and sugar beets for sugar production), onions, bulbs, and beetroots (Miljøstyrelsen,
2020a). It acts as a systemic soil and leaf herbicide, either alone or in mixtures, and belongs to the group of
photosynthesis inhibitor herbicides (EFSA, 2007). It is applied pre-emergence or early post-emergence and
may only to be used until two months before harvest (Miljøstyrelsen (2020a), Schuhmann et al. (2016)).
Chloridazon (CLZ) is the active ingredient in the products Alicep, Expander, and Pyramin, which are avail-
able in powder form (as a salt) or as a liquid solution. CLZ was banned in Denmark in 2012, however it is
still approved and used in several EU countries and in Australia (PPDB, 2021).
CLZ has two known degradation products, desphenyl-chloridazon (DPC), which is the primary degrada-
tion product, and methyl-desphenyl-chloridazon (MDPC). A pan-European survey on persistent pollutants
in groundwater has shown, that DPC and MDPC are among the most frequent found pesticides in both
groundwater and surface waters in Europe (Loos et al. (2010), Schuhmann et al. (2016)). Table 2.1 shows the
common names, CAS number, IUPAC name, chemical formula, and structure of CLZ and its two metabolites.

CLZ in Denmark
In Denmark, CLZ was sold from 1963 to 1996 where it was phased out and possession became prohibited
in 2012. Sales occurred mainly in 1970-1980 with quantities ranging from 60,000 kg/year to over 100,000
kg/year (Miljøstyrelsen, 2020a).
Since 1997, water works have tested for CLZ when pesticide contamination is suspected (Lenschow et al.,
2016). DPC was added to the Regions of Denmark extended pesticide analysis for groundwater in 2012 and
became a part of the standard analysis of groundwater in 2014 (Lenschow et al., 2016). MDPC has since
late 2017 been a part of the standard analysis for water works during well control (Miljøstyrelsen, 2020a).
Since DPC and MDPC became a part of the standard analysis controls, more sites with DPC and MDPC
contamination have been discovered.
In 2020, the Danish Environmental Protection Agency and GEUS conducted a screening for DPC and
MDPC as part of GRUMO (Danish ground water surveillance), which was among the most frequently found
pesticides. Table 2.2 shows the ﬁndings (%) of DPC and MDPC and ﬁndings above the quality criteria of
0.1 µg/l in all active sampled GRUMO wells and water abstraction wells.

CLZ is generally not found in large concentrations in groundwater when investigating point sources, GRUMO
wells, or in groundwater extraction wells. DPC, on the other hand, is the most commonly found compound,
either as the sole metabolite or together with CLZ and MDPC. DPC is most often found at sites with farming

is both valid for GRUMO wells and extraction wells (GEUS, 2018). However, DPC has been found in
groundwater from screens at 100 m bgl. and below (GEUS, 2018).

2.1
Transport & fate of CLZ and metabolites
Pesticides, depending on their mobility and persistence, can migrate within but also outside of the applied
soil and contaminate air, surface water, groundwater, and soil (Børgesen et al., 2015). The main processes
that determine the behavior and fate of pesticides in soil are adsorption, degradation, and movement by
several transfer processes. Transfer processes of pesticides includes volatilization, diﬀusion, runoﬀ, leaching,
assimilation by microorganisms, and uptake into plants (Pérez-Lucas et al., 2018).
2.1.1
Physio-chemical properties
The fate and transport of CLZ, DPC, and MPDC are linked to their physio-chemical properties (Table 2.3).
Physio-chemical parameters and their classiﬁcations can be seen in Appendix A.1.
The low vapor pressure (< 5 Pa) and Henry’s law constant (KH) below 0.1 Pa· m3· mol−1 indicate that
CLZ and DPC are non-volatile (DPC is slightly more volatile) (PPDB, 2020). This is also consistent with
laboratory studies showing that after application to soil and plant surfaces, volatilization is considered
negligible under ﬁeld conditions (EFSA, 2007). Data on vapor pressure and Henry’s law constant is not
available for MDPC.
The logKow values (octanol-water partitioning coeﬃcient) are low for both CLZ, DPC and MDPC, which
indicate that the compounds are mainly found in the water phase and have low bioaccumulation. A log Kow
value < 2.7 indicate low bio-accumulation values (PPDB, 2020)).
The solubility expresses the mass that can dissolve in water, which is temperature dependent (Nazaroﬀand
Alvarez-Cohen, 2001). CLZ has the lowest solubility compared to its degradation products, however it is still
considered highly soluble in water (PPDB, 2020). DPC and MDPC are 100 times more soluble, indicating
that the extent of sorption to organic material in soil or sediment is low. The high water solubility and
low tendency to bind to soil also indicate that DPC and MDPC can be transported into groundwater and
surface waters by water driven transfer processes such as recharge and runoﬀ. Lysimeter experiments have
also shown, that concentration of CLZ signiﬁcantly decreased with depth, whereas DPC and MDPC had
a continuous downward migration in soil (Melsbach et al. 2020, Schuhmann et al. 2016). The topsoil has
furthermore shown to have a great importance for retaining CLZ (Melsbach et al., 2020).
The leaching capacity of pesticides can be categorized by GUS (Groundwater Ubiquity Score) and can be
calculated using the half-life (DT50) of the compound and its organic carbon- water partitioning coeﬃcients
(Koc) (Gustafson, 1989) (Equation 2.1).

Generally, CLZ and its metabolites have high solubility and relatively low sorption tendencies, suggesting
that the compounds have a great potential for entering and polluting surface and groundwater (Khan et al.
(2012), Buttiglieri et al. (2009)). The physio-chemical properties of high mobility and polarity also favor
an easy migration between surface water and groundwater (Hintze et al., 2020). Even though DPC and
MDPC have higher mobility compared to CLZ, the metabolites have been found to be long-term stable,
and metabolite reservoirs have shown to be present in the unsaturated zone which act as long-term source
for leaching to groundwater (Schuhmann et al. 2016, Hintze 2020).
Both CLZ and DPC can become bound residues in the soil. EFSA (2007) reported that bound residues of in
a 14C-labelled CLZ soil laboratory study counted up to 9.7% in sandy clay loam. A laboratory study under
aerobic conditions in soil at 25 ◦C (reported in EFSA (2007)) shows that DPC consisted predominately
in the formation of bound residues (up to 42% of initial concentration after 92 days) in a soil degradation
experiment. Only a small amount was degraded to MDPC (EFSA, 2007). Higher TOC value could increase
the aﬃnity for bound residues (Barriuso et al., 2008).
Toxicity
CLZ is considered to have low toxicity with no highly speciﬁc response in mammals (Buttiglieri et al., 2009).
It has low acute oral, dermal and inhalation toxicity and has neither mutagenic nor genotoxic properties
(EFSA, 2007). Based on the available information, the European food safety authority (EFSA) has con-
cluded that a long-term risk to birds, freshwater ﬁsh and invertebrates cannot be excluded (EFSA, 2007).
CLZ is moderately toxic to aquatic animals (Chabera et al., 2021). Algae are sensitive to CLZ (Bisewska
et al., 2012), and a study conducted on crayﬁsh shows that both CLZ and DPC exhibit adverse eﬀects at
environmentally relevant concentrations (0.45 µg/l and 2.7 µg/l) for 30 days (Chabera et al., 2021). How-
ever, DPC and MPDC are generally considered less toxic to aquatic organisms compared to CLZ (EFSA,
2007). Riedo et al. (2021) showed that the amount of pesticide residues was negatively linked to the mi-
crobial biomass. These pesticide residues included a mixture of herbicides, including CLZ, fungicides and
insecticides. The results also suggest that the mixture in general had harmful eﬀects on beneﬁcial soil life
(Riedo et al., 2021).
In European regulations, the drinking water limit of metabolites diﬀers between relevant metabolites and
non-relevant metabolites. The distinction is based on, among other things, whether the biological activity is
comparable to that of the parent substance, its risk to organisms, and toxicological properties (Laabs et al.,
2015). DPC and MDPC are classiﬁed as non-relevant metabolites. In general, European regulations apply
a drinking water limit of 0.1 µg/l for relevant metabolites, which apply to all member states (EC, 2006).
Quality criteria for non-relevant metabolites vary, and only Denmark has a restrictive limit of 0.1 µg/l
(GEUS, 2020b). In other member states, the drinking water limit for non-relevant metabolites varies, but
0.75 µg/l is the general limit (Laabs et al., 2015). This non-consistent deﬁnition of non-relevant metabolites
and the drinking water limit lead to diﬀerent crop protection products being available in diﬀerent member
states, and a non-harmonized legal clarity for drinking water producers across Europe (Laabs et al., 2015).

Chapter 3
Degradation
The following chapter introduces the degradation of CLZ including previous degradation studies, and methane
oxidizing bacteria. Furthermore, biostimulation and bioaugmentation is explained along with factors that in-
ﬂuence degradation.
Degradation of pesticides can be divided into abiotic and biotic degradation, with the microbial process
often being the dominant one (Quintela and Varrone, 2019).
Abiotic degradation consists of chemical
degradation (such as hydrolysis) and photochemical reactions.
Photodegradation is the light-mediated
chemical degradation, which can be divided into direct and indirect photodegradation, the latter being the
main pathway. Photodegradation should be considered mainly on the soil surface or in surface waters where
light is available (Quintela and Varrone, 2019).
Microbial degradation occurs via two main pathways: metabolic degradation and co-metabolic degradation
(Chowdhury et al., 2008). In metabolic degradation, microorganisms use pesticides as a primary source of
nitrogen, carbon, and energy. In the co-metabolic pathway, the pesticide is not used as a direct growth
substrate, but another organic material is present to serve as the primary energy source.
In most cases where pesticides are cometabolized, they are only partially degraded, which generates new
xenobiotic compounds with new properties (Madigan et al., 2015). If the pesticide is fully mineralized,
it is transformed into CO2 and H2O and possibly salts (Lindhardt et al., 2000). Using bacteria with co-
metabolic reactions to degrade micropollutants such as pesticides requires enzymes with broad substrate
speciﬁcity. Methane oxidizing bacteria represent classes of microorganisms that have been shown to catalyze
co-metabolic reactions leading to biotransformation of micropollutants (Benner et al., 2015).
3.1
Degradation of CLZ
CLZ has, as mentioned in Chapter 2, two known degradation products, DPC and MDPC, which appear
to be mainly microbially mediated (Buttiglieri et al., 2009). The microbial degradation of CLZ to DPC
was ﬁrst described by Engvild and Jensen in 1969 in aerobic topsoil. This study isolated bacteria capable
of growing on CLZ as the sole carbon source (Engvild and Jensen, 1969), and the study concluded that
the ﬁnal decomposition product from a biological breakdown was DPC. Around the same time, another
study independently isolated a CLZ-degrading bacterium with similar properties from soil under aerobic
9
==PAGE:26==
conditions (Fröhner et al., 1970). Lingens et al. (1985) further described the degradation pathway, which
included further degradation of DPC to pyruvate and acetaldehyde. The study also described the isolation
of the 22 strains already identiﬁed in 1985 and classiﬁed the new genus and species with the proposed
name Phenylobacterium immobile. The CLZ degrading bacteria are aerobic, Gram-negative, and immobile
(Lingens et al., 1985). Phenylobacterium immobile is not closely related to other Gram-negative bacteria
(Eberspacher and Lingens, 2006). MDPC was ﬁrst discovered many years later by Weber et al. (2007) in a
non-target screening of ground- and surface water under aerobic conditions (Schuhmann et al., 2016).
Figure 3.1: Steps of the degradation of CLZ to DPC (a) and DPC to MDPC (b). The illustration is adapted
from (Melsbach et al., 2020).
Figure 3.1 shows the formation of DPC and MDPC from CLZ. The part of CLZ which is microbially at-
tacked is the phenyl-ring which is opened and mineralized to CO2. CLZ ﬁrstly gets an O2 molecule in the
benzene ring by dioxygenase and is then oxidized by dehydrogenase. Subsequently, O2 is incorporated into
catechol by metapyrocatechase. Finally, amidase cleaves the 2-hydroxymuconate from the formed DPC. The
degradation of DPC occurs by methylation to MDPC. A laboratory study with 14C labelled DPC conducted
under aerobic conditions in soil showed a shift in equilibrium between the conversion from MDPC back to
DPC (EFSA (2007), Miljøstyrelsen (2020a)). Figure 3.1 therefore has an arrow showing the possibility of a
reversible reaction. CLZ, DPC and MDPC can also be degraded directly by photolysis, where the metabo-
lites have been found to degrade faster under the inﬂuence of light (EFSA, 2007).
Several studies show that CLZ degrades relatively fast to DPC in aerobic soils and water (Schuhmann
et al. (2016), Buttiglieri et al. (2009)), and DPC is therefore often the major component found in soil and
groundwater (Miljøstyrelsen, 2020a). Further degradation of DPC is not well understood, but it is known
to be slowly transformed into MDPC in aerobic soils (Melsbach et al., 2020). It is unclear whether there is
a broader range of degradation pathways (Melsbach et al. (2020), Miljøstyrelsen (2020a)).

3.3
Methane oxidizing bacteria
MOB possess enzymes that enable them to oxidize, degrade, and transform organic molecules such as
pesticides by a co-metabolic pathway. In a study by Verhagen (2015), conducted in serum bottles with a NMS
growth medium, a MOB culture was able to degrade the pesticides chloropropham, metazachlor, benalaxyl
and ethofumesate. The co-metabolic properties of MOB have also been shown in similar experiment in
serum bottles with NMS medium to degrade micropollutants such as sulfametoxazole and benzotriazole
(Benner et al., 2015). It is therefore possible, that these properties could co-metabolize CLZ and DPC as
well.
MOB uses methane as the electron donor and carbon source, where oxygen is the electron acceptor. As the
MOB utilizes the growth substrate, enzymes are produced that simultaneously provide energy to drive re-
actions to transform the co-metabolic substance (Wendlandt et al., 2010). There are two types of microbial
methane oxidation, by aerobic bacteria (methanotrophs) or by a consortium of methanotrophic archaea, and
sulfate and nitrate reducers in anaerobic conditions (Wendlandt et al., 2010). Aerobic methane oxidation
often occurs at the interface between aerobic and anaerobic zones, particularly in soils or sediments, but also
in oxic aquatic environments (Wendlandt et al. (2010), Madigan et al. (2015)). Bacteria are predominantly
attached to the sediment compared to the aqueous phase (Griebler and Lueders, 2009), which is also true
for methanotrophs, where both the abundance and diversity have been shown to be greater in sediment
compared to groundwater (Kuloyo et al., 2020). The optimum temperature for methane oxidation is around
25 ◦C, but oxidation can occur at 0-35 ◦C (Hanson and Hanson, 1996). The pH should be between 5-8 for
the best growth conditions (Hanson and Hanson, 1996).
14
==PAGE:31==
Methane oxidation is catalyzed by the enzyme methane monooxygenase (MMO), which occurs in two forms
in the cell: a soluble form of MMO (sMMO), which is dissolved in the cytoplasm, and the particulate MMO
(pMMO), which is membrane bound (Wendlandt et al., 2010). Copper concentration is the main regulator
for the expression and activity between sMMO and pMMO, as genes encoding for sMMO only are expressed
at low levels (Benner et al., 2015). The presence of nitrate as a nitrogen source is also required for the
expression of sMMO (Wendlandt et al., 2010).
Bacteria expressing pMMO have been shown to be more robust to toxic metabolites and consequently grow
faster than cells expressing sMMO (Benner et al., 2015). Verhagen (2015) showed that both types of MMO
were able to degrade pesticides, but pMMO degradation eﬃciency was highest.
3.4
Biostimulation & bioaugmentation
To enhance biodegradation of pesticides, in situ bioremediation can be applied, which can primarily be
performed in three ways: natural attenuation, biostimulation and bioaugmentation (Quintela and Varrone,
2019). While natural attenuation allows natural occurring processes to remove contaminants, biostimula-
tion involves initiating and/or stimulating the natural microbial population by supplying nutrients and other
amendments, speeding up the degradation (Tyagi et al., 2011). Lastly, bioaugmentation is the introduction
of bacteria into soil and/or groundwater to enhance biological activity either in situ or ex situ (Stroo et al.,
2010). The combination of biostimulation and bioaugmentation have yielded good results and improved
the degradation rates of pesticides when the bacteria were inoculated along with additional carbon sources
(Quintela and Varrone, 2019). Laboratory experiments with groundwater and/or aquifer material is a good
way of investigating diﬀerent experimental approaches, and generate insights into the potential for pesticide
degradation by bioremediation (Albrechtsen et al., 2001).

shows relevant factors that can inﬂuence biostimulation and bioagumentation of pesticides. For bioaug-
mentation, one of the biggest challenges is ﬁnding suitable pesticide degraders with metabolic properties to
utilize pesticides, and sustain the growth of the bacteria with substrate (Helbling, 2015).

Bioaugmentation is a promising method for removal of pesticides in contaminated soils and groundwater,
and has been successfully applied for many types of pesticides (Cycoń et al. 2017, Helbling 2015). CLZ
is an organochlorinated pesticides, and several other types of pesticides belonging to this group have been
successfully degraded by bioaugmentation. This include lindane which during a 45-day experiment in soil
was bioaugmented with bacteria isolated from a lindane-contaminated soil, and 70-100% of the initial dose
was degraded (Cycoń et al., 2017).
Mixed bacterial and fungal communities have also shown to improve bioaugmentation of pesticides (Hel-
bling, 2015). This has been shown to be the case for diuron and 2,6-dichlorobenzamide (BAM) which was
found to mineralize more rapidly when bacterial and fungal were simultaneously present than when bacterial
degraders were present alone (Ellegaard-Jensen et al., 2014).
If bacteria which have the capabilities to degrade pesticides are already present at contaminated sites,
biostimulation is often the optimal remediation type compared to bioaugmentation. Biostimulation can
overcome limitations in soil and groundwater by creating new conditions, stimulating biodegradation. (Luo
et al., 2019). As redox conditions play a part in the microbial degradation activity, traditional biostimulation
strategies therefore often include amendments with electron acceptors such as nitrate or oxygen to enhance
degradation, the latter often used due to its high oxidation potential (Aldas-Vargas et al., 2021).
A study by Aldas-Vargas et al. (2021) investigated biodegradation of the herbicides MCPP, 2,4-D and
BAM in microcosms with groundwater. The setup was in microcosms, anaerobic or aerobic with nitrate or
16
==PAGE:33==
oxygen and/or dissolved organic carbon. Oxygen coupled with dissolved organic carbon was most successful,
resulting in complete degradation of 2,4-D in all microcosms after 42 days. BAM and MCPP were not
biodegraded. The study furthermore concluded, that biostimulated microorganisms from shallow aquifers
had faster degradation rates of pesticides than those from deep aquifers (Aldas-Vargas et al., 2021). This
could be due to the microorganisms being exposed to high concentrations of DOC (dissolved organic carbon)
in the upper soil layers and micropollutants (Aldas-Vargas et al., 2021).
Levi et al. (2014) also investigated the aerobic degradation potential for the herbicides bentazone, mecoprop
and dichlorprop in anaerobic groundwater samples from three drinking water wells and one drinking water
abstraction well. The experiment was conducted in microcosms with concentrations of 1 µg/l and lasted 200
days. The addition of oxygen, even adding small amounts of oxygen, was found to stimulate degradation of
bentazone, mecoprop and dichlorprop.
The environment in which bioremediation is planned should be fully characterized in terms of the natural
biodegradation capacity and the conditions necessary to stimulate degradation activity (Aldas-Vargas et al.,
2021). This include characterization of the redox conditions which are important in determining the extent
and rate of contaminant biodegradation (Maier and Gentry (2014), Miljøstyrelsen (2014)).
There are several ways to determine redox conditions. One is to use redox sensitive species to characterize
redox conditions present in water samples (Christensen et al., 2000). For aerobic conditions, free oxygen
needs to be present at 0.5-1 mg/l or above. It is possible to measure oxygen levels below 0.5 mg/l, but it
is not always known whether the readings represent free oxygen or are caused by an intrusion of oxygen
during pumping and sampling (Christensen et al., 2000). Characterization of denitrifying conditions does
not necessarily require the presence of measurable nitrate, as the process may limit the concentrations
below the detection limit. However, a decrease in nitrate concentration is a sign of denitrifying conditions
(Christensen et al., 2000). An indication of iron reduction includes the presence of dissolved iron (Fe(II)),
as this is the end product generated by the iron reduction itself. Sulphate reduction can be indicated under
anaerobic conditions by a decrease in sulphate concentration and/or the presence of hydrogen sulphide.
Nitrate reduction is characterized by the presence of nitrate and may also be indicated based on detected
nitrate. However, nitrate may also be present in aerobic conditions. The presence of ammonium in water
samples may indicate reduced conditions as it is oxidized at high oxygen concentrations. It can also be the
result of high dynamics of organic matter degradation (Christensen et al., 2000). Anaerobic ammonium
oxidation (annamox) is a reaction in which ammonium is oxidized to dinitrogen gas under anoxic conditions
using nitrite as an electron acceptor (Hu et al., 2011). Annamox is indicated by the detection of dinitrogen
and nitrite. If a high nitrate concentration is present, it leads, by the annamox process, to a higher nitrate
reduction and a greater release of nitrite (Hu et al., 2011).

4.1
History of the sites
The two sites, Site 1 and Site 2, have been investigated since 2015 and are both contaminated by multiple
point sources. Point sources are characterized by high concentrations in small areas, while non-point sources
have low concentrations in larger areas (Miljøstyrelsen, 2020a).
Two types of herbicides were used at both sites: phenoxy acid and herbicides with CLZ as the active
ingredient. The main ﬁndings at the sites are phenoxy acids and DPC. At Site 2, DPC have been found
in both an upper aquifer and in shallow groundwater, however the main contaminants are phenoxy acids.
Site 1 was found to have higher levels of contamination with DPC present in the deep, upper, and shallow
aquifers compared to Site 2.
Site investigations were initiated when contamination with phenoxy acid- pesticides, primarily MCPP and
dichlorprop, was detected in a drinking water well belonging to a nearby waterwork. The contamination had
been detected since early 1990 and it was later decided to investigate where the contamination originated
from. Since then, it has become apparent that the source of this contamination is the two sites where
signiﬁcant contamination with the same pesticides has been detected.
North of the water work is a large well ﬁeld belonging to HOFOR where phenoxy acid contamination has
been detected in the southern monitoring wells.
None of the water wells or wells associated with the well
ﬁeld had concentrations of DPC, only phenoxy acids. The water work has temporarily closed one water well
(212.861) due to phenoxy acids and is now only extracting from one well (212.683). These sites are located
south of the extraction and were investigated by WSP (Figure 4.1).
The two sites, both with the same owner, were used by agricultural contractors. Site 2 was used by an
agricultural contractor from 1952-1980, while Site 1 was used for the same purpose from 1966-1980. In
addition, normal farming activities were also conducted at both sites. The main part of the activities was
moved from Site 2 to Site 1 in 1966, where a washing area, storage and workshop were established. Figure

4.2.2
Hydrogeology
The deep aquifer in the area consists of Danian-Bryozoan limestone and the Grønsand formation. The
majority of the abstraction wells are located in the latter, as it has favorable water ﬂow properties due to
the fractures in the limestone. The Maastrichtian limestone is not used as a primary aquifer in the area.

A shallow aquifer consisting of a sandy clayey till is also present at both Site 1 and Site 2, primarily in the

the deep aquifer. It is not known, if there is a downward gradient from the upper aquifer to the deep aquifer

contamination is present in deeper layers. The groundwater ﬂows towards the water wells belonging to the
water work.

4.4
Fate of the contamination
Based on the investigations at the two sites, a conceptual model was created that includes a quantiﬁcation
of pesticide masses, calculated ﬂuxes, and estimated leaching and transport times from the sites to the
extraction wells. Overall, the vertical ﬂux at Site 1 and Site 2 is estimated to be 1.5-2 g/year respectively.
By the time contamination reaches the upper and deep aquifers, the primary ﬂux will be horizontal.
It is estimated that the majority of the contaminant mass is found in the upper aquifer located in the sandy
clayey till. This is the result of leaching through the upper part of the clayey till. The conceptual model
also indicates that a large portion of the phenoxy acids have already leached through the clayey till into
the aquifer and been transported downstream. The mass of phenoxy acids in the plume is not expected to
increase in the future.
However, there is still a signiﬁcant mass of DPC present at the two sites, primarily at Site 1, having a
greater vertical ﬂux in the clayey till than the phenoxy acids. The leaching is expected to las for at least 70
more years. This could potentially lead to a rising concentration in the deep aquifer. The slow leaching of
DPC compared to the phenoxy acids may be due to two reasons: CLZ was used after the phenoxy acids at
the two sites, or the fact that the degradation of CLZ to DPC is slower than that of the phenoxy acids.

5.3
Cultivation media & inoculum
MOB was used in the degradation experiment, however, an existing culture present at 4 ◦C ﬁrst had to
be activated and adapted to CLZ and DPC. The used MOB is a mixed culture of Methylococcus and

==PAGE:45==
Methylophylus. The cultivation was done in three batches in series using synthetic cultivation media i.e.,
diluted ammonium salt (dAMS) with ammonium as nitrogen source (Appendix B.1).
The optical density (OD), which measures the absorbance and scattering of light, was used to measure the
microcosm with MOB at 600 nm by a Cary 50 Bio UV-Visible Spectrophotometer (Varian). The growth of
MOB in dAMS was quantiﬁed with a TSS-OD relationship from Goonesekera (2021) (Appendix B.1). The
ﬁrst cultivation batch was inoculated with the inactive culture and no CLZ or DPC was added. The volume
of the inoculum in the following batch was adjusted based on the TSS-OD relationship. In this way, the
same amount of cells was used from the ﬁrst batch to each of the series. The composition of the cultivation
media and the laboratory protocol can be found in Appendix B.1. Oxygen and methane were provided in
a 60:40 ratio.
The glassware and stoppers used in the experimental setup of the cultivation batch were autoclaved at 121
◦C for 20 minutes before use. The OD was measured on the pure dAMS medium as a zero, and then the
MOB before inoculation before starting a new batch. 250 ml serum bottles were ﬁlled with dAMS medium
and sealed with butyl rubber stoppers and aluminum lids. The total volume of liquid was 100 ml with 3
% v/v of inoculum for the ﬁrst batch. Prior to inoculation, the microcosms were ﬂushed with nitrogen for
six minutes. After inoculation, 100 ml of headspace was removed and 60 ml 100% O2 and 40 ml 100% CH4
were added. Methane and oxygen were added every 2-3 days to maintain the growth of MOB. The second
and third batches each consisted of four microcosms, two of which were spiked with DPC and the other two
with CLZ, corresponding to a concentration of 100 µg/l in the culture medium. This allowed the MOB to
adapt to the pesticides for two weeks before being used in the degradation experiment. Microcosms were
placed in a shaker at 125 rpm and at 25 ◦C.
Samples were collected from the four microcosms after the end of Batch 3 by ﬁltering 1 ml sample with
a 0.20 µm ﬁlter into a 2 ml glass vial. Samples from the second batch were collected two weeks after the
addition of the pesticides and samples from the third batch were collected one week after the addition of
the pesticides. The samples were stored in a freezer before analysis to inhibit any further degradation. The
samples were extracted to get a ﬁrst impression of whether the MOB was able to degrade the pesticides
after a short period of time. It was furthermore used to determine the amount of CLZ and DPC present,
which was used to calculate the initial concentration in the degradation experiment. The results showed a
slight overall increase in DPC where CLZ was added, but no MDPC was detected in any of the microcosms.
The results are shown in Appendix B.2 and are not discussed further.
5.4
Degradation experiment
The scope of the degradation experiment was to study the potential of biodegradation of CLZ and DPC

Addition of methane and oxygen
Methane and oxygen were added to all microcosms, except the unamended controls, to stimulate microbial

should be between 10-15% to have a suﬃcient level of methane. The ratio of methane and oxygen when

5.4.2
Sampling the microcosms
Microcosms were sampled for pesticides, methane, oxygen, and pH during the experimental period (Ap-
pendix C.1). Only half of the microcosms were sampled each time, i.e., duplicates were not sampled. Prior
to sampling, all microcosms were rolled carefully 5-10 times and allowed to stand until the sediment had
settled and the water was clear. Methane and oxygen were measured in headspace samples prior to sampling
for pesticide analysis the ﬁrst 44 days. It was decided that additional samples for pesticide analysis should
be collected after the 44 days. All water samples were stored in a freezer between sampling and analysis.
Methane and oxygen
Methane and oxygen levels in the headspace (vol %) were measured with gas chromatography (GC) (Thermo
Scientiﬁc Trace 1310). The GC was calibrated each time before sampling the microcosms. Methane was
calibrated using 5%, 40% and 60% standards and oxygen was calibrated using standards of 2.5%, 10% and
100%. After each calibration, a test of atmospheric air was performed, which should be about 21%, and a
variation in oxygen content of 21-24% was observed during the experimental period. Following calibration,
0.2 ml of air from the headspace of each microcosm was injected into the GC. All microcosms were sampled
twice at one point, and the methane and oxygen content varied up to approx. 2.5%.
When the vol % of methane was approximately 10%, additional methane and oxygen was added to make
sure the methane concentration in the water phase stayed at the optimal level of 2-5 mg/l methane in the
water phase. The amount of additional CH4 and O2 added to each microcosm can be found in Appendix
E.4. As 2 ml of water was extracted each time a sample for pesticide analysis was taken, the volume of
air increased. The volume of headspace was therefore considered before calculating how much methane and
oxygen was added. Figure E.1 and E.2 in Appendix E.4 shows the measurements from GC expressed as vol
% of CH4 and O2 in the microcosms.
Pesticide
Prior to sampling for pesticide analysis, 2 ml of air was injected through the rubber stopper with a syringe
to compensate for the 2 ml of water removed. To avoid the biomass and suspended material, 1 ml of water
was ﬁltered with a 0.20 µm ﬁlter into a 2 ml glass vial. The glass vials were stored in a freezer until analysis
to avoid further degradation of the compounds. 16 samples per microcosm was send to analysis over the
experimental period (Appendix C.1 shows when samples were collected). Samples were analyzed 5 times
during the experimental period by LC-MS /MS by laboratory technicians.

Chapter 6
Results & Discussion
This chapter describes the results of the investigations at Site 1 and Site 2 and the ﬁndings from the degra-
dation experiment. First, a characterization of the redox conditions and contamination of the two sites
is presented, followed by a discussion of the vertical distribution and assessment of the degradation. The
consumption of oxygen and methane in all microcosms is discussed. The results of the degradation exper-
iment of CLZ and DPC are presented and discussed. This includes whether a relation between biostimu-
lated/bioaugmented and the unamended controls is seen, and how this study compares to previous degradation
experiments. Lastly, the ﬁndings of the experiment is set in context to the two sites.

the clayey till around 11-16 m bgl. indicates an accumulation of DPC in this layer down to the bottom of
the clayey till.
At the two sites, a sand/gravel layer is typically seen around 16 m bgl. When the DPC reaches this layer,
which has a high hydraulic conductivity, the concentrations are expected to get diluted and horizontally
distributed with the water ﬂow. This is most likely also the reason for the lower concentrations in the
limestone deep aquifer, as only a small amount of the DPC reaches the deep aquifer. Nevertheless, some
ﬁndings of DPC in the deep aquifer are above the quality criteria of 0.1 µg/l and the highest concentration
in the deep aquifer found at the two sites is 10 µg/l.

The DPC found in the water samples were converted to concentrations in the soil. Three diﬀerent Kd values
were used (see Appendix D.3 for details). This shows that the total DPC concentration in the clayey till
could be as high as 81.9 µg/kg.
A master thesis by Nielsen (2021) examined two sites with DPC point sources on Lolland and Femø and
evaluated the extent of CLZ, DPC, and MDPC in the unsaturated zone. Soil samples showed that 21 -
93 µg/kg DPC was present in an unsaturated clayey till. This was determined to be a potential reservoir
of both CLZ, DPC, and MDPC contributing to a long-term leaching to deep aquifers. Schuhmann et al.
(2016) conducted a lysimeter study of CLZ, DPC, and MDPC over more than two years. This showed a
high retention of CLZ in the soil, and the permanent presence of CLZ in the lysimeter formed reservoirs for
DPC and MDPC. It is likely that a DPC reservoir is also present at Site 1, however further investigations
of the site are needed to clarify this.
Molar ratios of DPC and MDPC
Figure 6.4 (b) shows the molar ratio of DPC. This data set includes 61 points obtained from the sampling
campaign and previous water samples at both sites. MDPC is included in the sum in samples where it was
part of the analysis. Figure 6.4 (c) shows the vertical distribution of the molar ratio of MDPC with data
from both sites and includes 17 water samples.
Normally, this ratio might give an incorrect picture of the
degradation if CLZ were present, as a constant formation of DPC would be seen (Melsbach et al., 2019).
However, CLZ concentrations are low to undetectable in this case and therefore probably do not play a role.
DPC is the main component in all samples where CLZ was detected. CLZ was detected to a depth of 7 m
bgl., whereas DPC was detected down to 25 m (in the deep aquifer) at the two sites. This again indicates
that almost all of the CLZ has been degraded to DPC. Although CLZ degrades relatively quickly to DPC,
it has been shown to have high retention in the topsoil, higher than for DPC and MDPC (Schuhmann et al.
(2016), EFSA (2007)). Since CLZ was only found in water samples in the shallow groundwater and not in
the upper or deep aquifer, this might be valid for the two sites.
As seen on Figure 6.4 (c), the MDPC ratio is highest in the ﬁrst 6 m bgl. and then decreases rapidly with
increasing depth and has only been found down to 14 m bgl., and was therefore not found in the primary
aquifer. The highest concentration of DPC was found in B102-1 (screen placement at 11-14 m bgl.), but no
MDPC was found, indicating that it is not the amount of DPC which governs the degradation of MDPC.
This is also valid for B101-1 where high concentrations of DPC was found, but no MDPC. The ﬁrst 6 m
bgl. corresponds to the shallow aquifer where redox conditions were characterized as aerobic to nitrate
reducing. This indicates that the degradation of DPC to MDPC occurs primarily under aerobic to post-oxic
conditions, and as the concentration of MDPC is low compared to DPC, this degradation occurs slowly.
This is consistent with previous laboratory studies in which MDPC was detected in aerobic conditions
(Schuhmann et al. (2016), Buttiglieri et al. (2009), Melsbach et al. (2020)) and not in anaerobic soil (EFSA,
2007).

DPC was found down to 25 m. at the sites, however this is not the case for MDPC. It is not known if
MDPC is further transformed before it reaches the lower layers, and therefore is only found in the upper 14
m. MDPC could also be diluted during horizontal and especially vertical transport, and since concentrations
are relatively low, it is easily diluted below detection limit. Under aerobic conditions, a shift in equilibrium
between the conversion from MDPC back to DPC has been demonstrated (EFSA (2007), Miljøstyrelsen

potentially also happen under anaerobic conditions.
If DPC is only degradable to MDPC under aerobic conditions, this also indicate that once DPC reaches
anaerobic conditions, further degradation to MDPC is unlikely. Thus, DPC will potentially leach to the
deeper aquifer before further degradation (with the known degradation pathways) and be transported fur-

sediment. The molar concentration of MDPC is slightly higher in the microcosms with sediment from D1
than in those with sediment from D2. The increase in MDPC is likely the result of DPC degradation.
The CLZ found in the microcosms spiked with DPC increase slightly in the microcosms with sediment from
D1 throughout the experiment. This increase is thought to be due to slow a desorption from the sediment
(Appendix E.6).
The naturally occurring bacteria in sediment D1 that appear to degrade CLZ are unable to signiﬁcantly
degrade DPC during the experimental period.
The bacteria present in the microcosms containing the
sediment from D2, did not show any ability to degrade DPC either.
Relation between biostimulation, bioaugmentation and the unamended controls
The molar concentrations of DPC were very similar in all six microcosms, and it was not possible to detect
a clear diﬀerence between the biostimulated, bioaugmented, and the unamended control. This was also true
for the molar concentration of MDPC, indicating that biostimulation and bioaugmentation did not enhance
degradation.

6.5
Potential of biostimulation & bioaugmentation at the two sites
The degradation experiment, using groundwater and sediment from two diﬀerent depths at Site 1, can be
used to simulate and predict the processes occurring at the sites. As shown by the investigations of Site 1
and Site 2, CLZ is almost completely degraded, with the main metabolite being DPC under aerobic con-
ditions. Although the experimental period was only 74 days, the same trend was observed in microcosms
54
==PAGE:71==
with sediment from D1 spiked with CLZ. Only a small amount of MDPC was found at both sites, and
mainly in the aerobic part of the soil. The condition of the sediment from D2 was changed to aerobic in the
microcosms, however, a slightly higher concentration of MDPC was found in the sediment that originally
came from the aerobic conditions. This indicates the presence of naturally occurring bacteria capable of
degrading CLZ and DPC in sediment from D1. This is also consistent with the ﬁndings at the site.
A report on Site 1 and Site 2 estimated the mass of CLZ, DPC, and MDPC present in the clayey till, and
used this to estimate that the leaching will last at least 70 years more. These calculations are based on
water samples alone, as the compounds were not considered to sorb signiﬁcantly. However, in the degrada-
tion experiment, a continued sorption in both sediment types supports the idea that the clayey till has the
potential to accumulate DPC at the two sites. In Schuhmann et al. (2016) and Hintze et al. (2020) DPC
and MDPC has been found to be long-term stable and metabolite reservoirs have been shown to be present
which acts like a long term source for leaching to groundwater. If this is the case for the two sites, it is
possible that the leaching of DPC could last for a much longer time than ﬁrst anticipated. Furthermore,
if DPC is only degradable to MDPC under aerobic conditions, which the results of the investigation of the
two sites indicate, DPC would remain at the two sites if left alone.
The microcosms containing sediment from D2 can provide a sense of how biostimulation with O2 and CH4
aﬀects degradation at the two sites. CLZ and DPC were not degraded in microcosms with D2 sediment
during the experimental period. This indicates that biostimulation of anaerobic sediment with O2 and CH4
is not a viable method for enhancing the degradation at the two site. The use of MOB for bioaugmentation
of CLZ and DPC for both types of sediment did not enhance degradation in the microcosms either and this
type of bacteria is not a solution to the problem at the two sites either.
Bacteria which are able to degrade CLZ almost completely to DPC and MDPC within 74 days are present in
the aerobic sediment used in the degradation experiment. It would be interesting to investigate and isolate
the speciﬁc type of bacteria that are able to degrade CLZ. However CLZ in itself is not the problematic
compound, but rather DPC as it is slowly degradable. This is not only the case for the point source con-
tamination at the two sites, but generally in Denmark and Europe when a CLZ containing herbicide has
been spilled (Miljøstyrelsen 2020a, GEUS 2020b, Loos et al. 2010).
To further investigate the potential for bioremediation of DPC, other types of amendments could be added to
similar water/sediment batch experiments such as nitrate or ammonia. Like the MOB, ammonium oxidizing
bacteria have also shown co-metabolic transformation of micropollutants in biological wastewater treatment
(Men et al., 2017), and could therefore have the properties to degrade CLZ and DPC. However, in order for
bioremediation to be a success, the degradation rate should be high enough for the degradation to happen
within a foreseeable period of time. If this is not the case, other types of remediation should be looked
at. For instance, studies have investigated the adsorption of CLZ and metabolites by diﬀerent types of
technologies including graphene oxide in water (Yan et al., 2021) and soil amended by biochar (Dechene
et al., 2014), however with mixed results. An advanced oxidation processes in a UV-reactor combined with
H2O2 has shown good result, as DPC was degraded in up to 60% in water. It is still a novel technology,
and further studies of possible intermediate products is still needed (Miljøstyrelsen, 2020b).

Chapter 7
Conclusion
Degradation in groundwater from two sites, Site 1 and Site 2, contaminated by point sources of CLZ and its
metabolites was investigated. Water samples from two sampling campaigns and relevant data from previous
investigations were used to characterize and evaluate degradation at the two sites.
A degradation experiment was conducted with groundwater and sediment from Site 1 to evaluate the poten-
tial for biostimulation (O2 and CH4) and bioaugmentation with MOB in two types of sediment. Sediment
was collected in soil cores from aerobic (2.5-3.0 m bgl.) and anaerobic conditions (7.5-8.0 m bgl.) and used
in microcosms under aerobic conditions. A total of 12 microcosms were sampled during the 74 days, 4
biostimulated microcosms, 4 bioaugmented and 4 unamended controls.
The point source contamination at the two sites originated from a CLZ containing herbicide. The initial
contamination was almost completely degraded to DPC, which was found to be the main metabolite at both
sites. CLZ was found only in the top 7 m, indicating high retention in the topsoil. Elevated levels of DPC
in the water samples collected in screens placed at the clayey till could indicate an accumulation. Only low
concentrations of the further degradation product MDPC were found in the water samples, indicating slow
degradation. MDPC was detected to a depth of 14 m bgl., and the highest MDPC concentrations were
present in the ﬁrst 6 m bgl., corresponding to aerobic-nitrate reducing conditions at the sites. This indicates
that further degradation of DPC to MDPC occurs mainly under these conditions at the two sites.
In the degradation experiment, O2 and CH4 were measured in the microcosms. Since a decrease is a sign of
biological activity, this may be an indication of degradation. The consumption of O2 and CH4 was highest
in the microcosms containing MOB. In the biostimulated microcosms, the consumption of CH4 was almost
absent, indicating that the naturally occurring bacteria do not utilize CH4. Higher oxygen consumption was
observed in the biostimulated microcosms than in the unamended controls. The low oxygen consumption in
the unamended controls could be due to low consumption by naturally occurring bacteria. No clear diﬀer-
ence was observed between the two sediment types used. Bacterial growth was visually evident as unclear
water indicated growth. This was most pronounced in the microcosms containing MOB after the lag phase
of 20 days. However, bacterial growth was also observed in the biostimulated microcosms, which became
more unclear than the water in the unamended control.
57
==PAGE:74==
In the microcosms spiked with CLZ using sediment from D1 (originally aerobic sediment), only a small
amount remained in the water phase. After a lag phase of approximately 35 days, the decrease in CLZ
followed the known degradation pathway in which degradation of CLZ led to the formation of DPC as the
major metabolite and a small amount of DPC was further degraded to MDPC. This is consistent with the
results at the two sites. DT50 values of these microcosms ranged from 46.3-58.9 days, which is comparable
to previous studies in water and aerobic soils that were not biostimulated or bioaugmented.
The three microcosms spiked with CLZ and with the original anaerobic sediment D2 did not show a clear
degradation curve. An incomplete mass balance was observed, which could be due to a continuous sorption,
bound residues, or a slow initial degradation. Since the two types of sediment originates from two contrasting
redox conditions, a diﬀerence in the naturally occurring bacteria are present. The absence of degradation
in microcosms with D2 could be attributed to the bacteria requiring a longer adaptation time when redox
conditions change from anaerobic to aerobic.
Limited degradation occurred in microcosms spiked with DPC (up to 9.2% compared to the initial concen-
tration), and only a small increase in MDPC was observed. No clear diﬀerence was seen between a lag phase
and a degradation phase, but a slow degradation that was slightly faster after 50 days. It was not possible
to distinguish between the biostimulated, bioaugmented microcosms and the unamended controls.
In conclusion, the results of the degradation experiment do not indicate that biostimulation with O2 and CH4
or bioaugmentation with MOB improves the degradation of CLZ and DPC. Therefore, based on these results,
it is not considered a viable remediation strategy. Further investigation should be conducted to determine if
other types of biostimulation and bioaugmentation are viable strategies not only for the two sites, but also
for other sites contaminated by DPC point sources. Recommendations for further investigations in context
of this work have been provided.

Chapter 8
Recommendations for further investigations
In the context of the work conducted in this thesis, it has become clear that additional studies of point
source contamination are needed to better understand the degradation pathways of CLZ and DPC, and to
identify viable remediation strategies. Recommendations for further investigations includes the following:
• At Site 1 and Site 2, additional sampling of existing wells not previously analyzed for MDPC, as well
as pore water sampling, will provide a better overview of MDPC distribution. This will also investigate
if the molar ratio of MDPC in and near the shallow groundwater is higher than current data indicate.
• Previous studies have shown that each time experiments are spiked with CLZ, the lag phase decreases.
This has been shown in water (Buttiglieri et al., 2009) and soil (Engvild and Jensen, 1969). It would
therefore be interesting to spike the microcosms used in the degradation experiment again with CLZ or
DPC to see if this development is also observed in the water/sediment system, and see the diﬀerence,
if any, between the two types of sediment.
• Using 14C-labelled CLZ or DPC in similar water/sediment batch experiments could be used to study
the mineralization quantiﬁed by formed 14CO2 (Lindhardt et al., 2000). This would especially be
beneﬁcial if the experiments ran for a longer period of time, compared to this study, increasing the
possibility that a full mineralization takes place. 14C-labelled CLZ or DPC would furthermore make
it easier to follow the fate and the degradation pathways. As 14CO2 is measured, it increases the
accuracy of the mass balance. This could identity if the incomplete mass balance for microcosms
spiked with CLZ and containing D2 sediment is due to mineralization.
• To investigate the potential for biodemediation, other types of amendments than O2, CH4 such as
nitrate or ammonia could be added. The potential for CLZ and DPC degradation could furthermore
be investigated with ammonium oxidizing bacteria which also possesses co-metabolic properties and
have been shown to degrade herbicides (Men et al., 2017).
• Finally, the degradation experiment was conducted at 20◦C. However, to simulate ﬁeld conditions and
groundwater temperatures at 10◦C to a higher degree, microcosms should be optimally stored at lower
temperatures. 20◦C was chosen to accelerate degradation. If the microcosms were stored at lower
temperatures, the experiment would likely need to be run for a longer period of time, which was not
possible within the time frame of this study.

E.7
Mass balance
A closed mass balance was considered where the total mass in the system at day 0 equals the sum of CLZ,
DPC and MDPC, Equation E.5.
The changing volume of the water phase due to sample extraction is
considered. The mass balance is excluding mineralization in the system.
Ctot · Vw = (CCLZ + CDPC + CMDPC) · Vw
(E.5)
Where the concentration of CLZ, DPC and MDPC is in µmol/l, and Vw is the volume in the microcosm.
The fraction of mass at each day compared to the total mass at day 0 can then be expressed by the sum of
CLZ, DPC and MDPC divided by the total mass.
Figure E.6 and E.7 shows the mass balance, where 100% means total mass conservation in the system.
