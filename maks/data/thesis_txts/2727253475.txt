1 Abstract 
Enough literature studies available today have implemented the occupant behaviour model and 
also sighted the benefits of ventilative cooling during the cooling periods in warm summer 
months. One of the challenges is to effectively address the ventilative cooling potential of the 
venting hatch and roof windows in conjunction with the occupant behaviour model since many 
of the studies available have demonstrated ventilative cooling and occupant behaviour but 
concerning the windows. Also, another challenge is to demonstrate the same by creating a 
workflow using Grasshopper parametric tool.  
The study presented henceforth aims to present a framework to implement the behaviour model 
and analyse the ventilative cooling potential of the venting hatch and roof windows for the 
three summer months of June, July and August, using Grasshopper as a tool. The analysed 
model is a single-story family house, in Copenhagen, Denmark. The project's objective was to 
utilize Grasshopper to create a parametric analysis script, focusing on exploring the impacts of 
draught, overheating, and thermal comfort. 
The results indicated that the angular opening of 60⁰ of the venting hatch and roof windows 
contribute to keeping the thermal environment inside the house at an optimum level without 
breaching the overheating limit. This proposed study makes future Grasshopper 
script development possible concerning the ventilative cooling potential.

Introduction 
 
3 Introduction 
 
 
 
Climate change is an ever growing phenomenon of this century. It is estimated that if the 
current pace of climate change continues, global warming will most likely reach 1.5⁰C between 
2030 and 2052 (IPCC) (<i>Summary for Policymakers — Global Warming of 1.5 oC</i>, n.d.). With 
such an increase in global temperatures, it will directly affect the heating quotient of residential 
houses, forcing the occupants to adopt more cooling to nullify the effects of rising 
temperatures. Overall, the need for energy would only increase, posing a challenge to 
governments and consumers alike. High indoor temperatures for extended periods pose 
significant challenges to the health of the occupants, affecting the productivity, satisfaction, 
well-being and morale of the inhabitants (Yan et al., 2015).  
Increase in demand for cooling energy: 
A study (Jylhä et al., 2015) demonstrated that the future annual energy demand for heating of 
spaces and ventilation supply air decreased by 20-40% by year 2100, while the energy demand 
for cooling increased by 40-80%. Many studies have concluded that climate warming has the 
potential to reduce the heating demand, but the rising cooling demand may offset or even 
exceed the savings in heating energy.  
Examples of increase in cooling energy: 
The paper (Frank, 2005) also argues similarly to the study (Jylhä et al., 2015) by stating that 
the annual heating energy demand for Swiss residential buildings for the period 2050-2100 will 
decrease by 36-58% and on the other hand, annual cooling energy demand will witness an 
increase by 223-1050%. Extreme heatwave events such as those witnessed in the summer of 
2003 will become more frequent in future, and over half of all European summers are likely to 
be warmer than in 2003 (Frank, 2005). A study from the Hadley Centre in the United Kingdom 
(Pope et al., 2007) suggests doubling atmospheric carbon dioxide concentrations in Europe 
over the next 50-100 years will, with a probability, spell an average global temperature rise 
between 2.4 and 5.4⁰C. 
Climate change and overheating risks: 
Climate change and overheating risks rating scheme such as passive house is influenced to 
push for high thermal comfort standards. To put this into perspective, a paper (Mcleod et al., 
2013) investigated whether passive house dwellings can provide high standards of thermal 
comfort in the future or are inherently vulnerable to overheating risks. The authors quoted Zero 
Carbon Hub (ZCH), by stating, there is some anxiety that homes build today may be at risk of 
overheating even in the current climate. Given the prospect of significant warming, well within 
the expected lifetime of homes, this risk will increase with potentially serious consequences. 
The authors concluded that thermal mass played a clear role in reducing the overall duration of

overeating in the passive house dwellings and was also correlated with a reduction in solar heat 
load.  
One of the factors causing overheating – weather conditions:  
Weather conditions play a vital role in influencing the overheating risk inside dwellings. Warm 
weather has different impacts than hot, tropical, or cold weather. To investigate weather factor, 
the study (Taylor et al., 2014) examined the overheating risk in London dwelling archetypes 
when simulated under different UK climates, both in the present and under ‘hot future’ 
conditions, intending to identify whether the conclusions drawn from location-specific studies 
can be generically applied to different cities. 3456 dwellings variants and six different Design 
Summer Year (DSY) climate files were used from locations within the UK. The results indicate 
that weather files can influence the ranking of relative overheating risk between dwelling types, 
with significant variations in the relative ranking between London, Scotland and the North of 
England, and the rest of England. Hence the authors suggested that the variability of building 
performance needs to be considered based on each regional weather condition. Further, 
people's behaviour influences overheating through internal heat gain and window opening 
habits (Ponechal & Jurasová, 2018).  The authors concluded that thermal mass played a clear role 
in reducing the overall duration of overeating in the passive house dwellings and was also 
correlated with a reduction in solar heat load.  
Global scope of thermal comfort performances through PMV/PPD: 
A study (De Dear et al., 2013) studied the progress in thermal comfort research over the last 
twenty years. According to them, one of the most sweeping changes across the field of thermal 
comfort research in the last 20 years is the acceptance of a fundamentally different, but not 
new, model of comfort. At the beginning of the two-decade period, there was little doubt in the 
literature about what was the model of thermal comfort–Fanger’s seminal (1970) Predicted 
Mean Vote (PMV) and Predicted Percent Dissatisfied (PPD). PMV/PPD reduces thermal 
comfort to a steady-state heat balance equation that, according to its author, could be used 
without modification anywhere in the world. This global scope was further reinforced by the 
inclusion of PMV/PPD in various comfort standards, most notably ISO7730 (1984) and 
ASHRAE 55-1992, lending the model an authority that HVAC engineers and others were 
responsible for delivering thermal comfort inside buildings (De Dear et al., 2013).  
The primary objective of building designers and regulations is to prioritize the thermal comfort 
of occupants within the occupied areas. It is essential to establish this fundamental requirement 
and continuously update the existing guidelines to align with changing occupant behaviour and 
climate conditions. Considerations such as overheating, draught effects, and internal heat gains 
contribute collectively to the overall thermal comfort, which ultimately determines the success 
of these efforts. The concept of ventilative cooling is applied to this thesis. Ventilative cooling 
refers to the usage of natural or mechanical ventilation strategies to cool indoor spaces. This 
effective use of outside air reduces the energy use of cooling systems while maintaining 
thermal comfort (EBC Annex 62 Ventilative Cooling Project Summary Report, n.d.)

Introduction 
3.1 Problem statement  
 
Overheating, draught, window opening habits, occupant behaviour, and outdoor and indoor 
temperatures are all interconnected, which outputs one variable known as thermal comfort. If 
any of these factors deviate from their desired state, it directly impacts occupant thermal 
comfort. While many research papers exist on studying these effects with facade windows, 
rarely any of the papers available today investigated these factors with venting hatch as the 
main focus and also using Grasshopper as a simulation tool. This project attempts to study 
optimal venting openings needed for the residential summer comfort through a Grasshopper 
workflow. A benchmark has been tried to setup using Grasshopper that is relevant to the 
existing Danish building regulations and European standards. Thus, a proper investigation has 
been carried out with all the available data through existing literature papers and appropriate 
assumptions wherever deemed necessary.

Literature review 
 
4 Literature Review 
 
 
 
This chapter provides presents literature review regarding ventilative cooling, draught and 
occupants window opening behaviour. 
 
4.1 Ventilative cooling  
 
According to the UN Environment Programme 2022 (2022 Global Status Report for Buildings and 
Construction | UNEP - UN Environment Programme, n.d.), despite a substantial increase in 
investment towards lowering the energy intensity of buildings, the total energy consumption 
and CO2 emissions have only increased. Building energy demands have shot up by around 4% 
from 2020 to 135EJ, and CO2 emissions have reached an all-time high of 10GtCO2, a 5% 
increase from 2020.  
The ventilative cooling potential depends on many variables such as internal gains, solar gains, 
use of solar shadings, window’s U and g values, heat loss coefficient of the building, ventilation 
flow rate, and indoor and outdoor temperatures (Justo-Alonso et al., 2017). In colder climates, 
generally, houses are built with super-insulating materials with the aim to save heating energy 
demands, but it also creates thermal discomfort during summers when the outdoor temperatures 
are at their peak. The removal of excess heat becomes necessary to reduce thermal discomfort 
and reach an acceptable indoor climate. Hence the potential of ventilative cooling becomes 
larger (Finocchiaro et al., n.d.). The authors (Justo-Alonso et al., 2017) state that ventilative 
cooling in Norway can cover 100% of the cooling needs. According to the authors, an 
additional consideration for cold climates is overcooling, as this opts for additional limitations 
on the use of window openings for cooling. 
There are many research studies published on the approach of summer comfort and ventilative 
cooling for residential houses. One of the papers (Jylhä et al., 2015) demonstrated that the 
demand for heating of spaces and ventilated air supply reduced by 20-40%, while the energy 
demand for cooling increased by 40-80% by the year 2100, illustrating the devastating impact 
of climate change and the uncertainties from future greenhouse gas emissions. Like other 
studies published, the authors believe that the increase in cooling demand load will most 
eventually offset the decrease in the heating demand and even exceed depending on the site 
locations.  
A study (Psomas, Fiorentini, et al., 2017) warns that mechanical and passive ventilation and 
shading systems, if manually controlled, cannot assure optimum indoor environment 
conditions during summer. Here the authors argued for a developed window system with 
automated algorithms to improve the indoor environment during cooling seasons. Authors of 
the papers (Martin et al., 1996) and (Schulze & Eicker, 2013) concluded that complex algorithms

Literature review 
in many buildings do not perform better than simple ones, and setting parameters proved more 
important than the control strategy itself. The key findings suggest that uncomplicated 
strategies for ventilative cooling, which prioritize interactions between the site and the 
dwelling's structure, yield optimal performance (O’Sullivan, 2021). 
Previous studies have shown that passive climate change adaptation measures can positively 
affect thermal comfort in summer described in the study (van Hooff et al., 2016). Many 
published studies have considered the future climate year and simulated the overheating risks 
like the authors (van Hooff et al., 2016) and (Jylhä et al., 2015). (van Hooff et al., 2016) 
simulated a typical Dutch terraced house concerning passive climate change adaptations

defined passive measures as those that do not use energy once implemented.

Germany and Denmark. But the authors did not include future climate change into 
consideration. It was found during their experiment that ventilative cooling through window 
opening plays a crucial role in maintaining thermal comfort in all three houses, and that window 
was frequently used. The hypothesis that low-energy buildings have an increased risk of

4.2 Draught 
 
Draught is defined as an unwanted local cooling of the human body caused by air movement. 
It is known that the risk of draught increases with an increase in mean air velocity and 
decreasing air temperatures (Melikov & Nielsen, n.d.). Many studies have performed experiments 
to assess draught risks like the authors (Yu et al., 2006). The work mainly aimed to investigate 
the effects of temperature gradient and room air temperature on local discomfort due to air 
movements at different thermal sensations. Their results indicated that vertical temperature 
gradient had an insignificant impact on draught perception. Draught perception at the foot, calf, 
thigh and arm was affected by both overall and local thermal sensations. Similarly, (Melikov 
et al., n.d.) measured airflow characteristics in the occupied zone of 12 spaces served by a 
displacement ventilation system. Correlations were found between mean air velocity and 
turbulence intensity, maximum air velocity and length of scales of turbulence.  
==PAGE:20==
20 
Literature review 
 
Fig 4.2: Draught rate for different body segments (Yu et al., 2006) 
Likewise, the study (Pitchurov et al., 2002) that surveyed occupants' responses to the thermal 
environment in eight office buildings reported that about 24% of the respondents complained 
they were bothered daily by the draught, mainly on their lower legs. According to the study 
(Fanger et al., 1988), complaints due to draught continue to arise, even though air velocities 
are within the prescribed limits. They state that airflow with high turbulence causes more 
complaints of draught than air flow with low turbulence at the same mean air velocity and air 
temperature. 
The paper (Heiselberg, 1994) presented a study of downdraught created by cold-glazing 
surfaces. The authors noted that downdraught from glazed surfaces might cause thermal 
discomfort. A study (Lyons et al., 1999) used the empirical algorithms developed by the authors 
in the study (Heiselberg, 1994) to estimate an upper bound for the velocities of air currents. 
The velocities were evaluated as a function of glazing temperature, window size and distance 
between the window and an occupant. The authors stated that solar load dominates the 
perception of comfort during summer. The results indicated discomfort increases with clothing 
insulation and solar heat gain coefficient.  
A study (Schiavon et al., 2016) experimented with evaluating ankle draught risks for women 
with uncovered ankles. Thirty university students participated in the experiments. The authors 
demonstrated that the subjects were more sensitive to ankle draught than expected. Between 
23% and 57% of the subjects found air movements at ankle height unacceptable, compared to 
20% and 37% of the subjects who found the overall thermal environment unacceptable. 
Similarly, one of the study (Rueegg et al., 2001) investigated several configurations of window 
sizes, insulating standards and outside temperatures and their effect on down draught and 
thermal comfort in the occupied zones in Zug, Switzerland. The conclusion was that the 
window frame, not the windowpane, is the critical element and that there will not be 
unacceptable draught risks in the occupied zone as long as the window frame is highly 
insulating. 
 
4.3 Occupant behaviour on window opening  
 
Authors (R. V. Andersen et al., 2009) published a paper in which they studied occupant 
behaviour in different Danish households from September to October 2006 and again from

A study (Nicol, 2001) carried out surveys throughout Europe and analysed how the use of each

that physical conditions clearly influence the use of controls, but their use tends to be governed

which everyone opens a window, but as the temperature rises, there is an increased probability 
that they may have done so. The occupants of naturally ventilated buildings can open their 
windows for two primary purposes: to improve indoor air quality or to help improve comfort 
by reducing indoor temperature and encouraging air movement. They find out that the 
proportion of open windows tends to increase significantly as the outdoor temperatures rise 
above 10⁰C. 50% of European offices open windows at 22⁰C, rising to 80% at 33⁰C.

An experiment was conducted concerning the occupants’ behaviour for windows and door

window open time increases dramatically and is greater than door open times. Another 
interesting thing the author observed in his results is that during summers, exterior temperature

door open time. This indicates that window opening is an essential method of summer cooling, 
but that door opening does not increase with exterior temperature in the summer. Door and 
window openings show no correlation with each other during the summer. The author states 
that window opening is related to summer, while door opening seems to be driven by other 
factors.

probability of opening and closing windows. For naturally ventilated houses, the results

positively correlated with the window opening probability, while outdoor temperature was 
negatively correlated with the probability of closing windows. In the bedroom, the CO2 
concentration was the most crucial variable for the probability of opening windows, while it 
did not significantly affect the living room. The indoor relative humidity had the biggest effect 
on the closing probability in the living room but did not significantly affect the bedroom. Like 
similar studies conducted, the authors agree that both the opening and closing probabilities 
were influenced by the season and the time of the day.

4.3.1 Window-operation habits in different rooms  
 
According to the study conducted by IEA-ECBCS (Dubrul, 1988), occupant behaviour 
concerning ventilation involving Belgium, Germany, Switzerland, the Netherlands and the 
United Kingdom showed actions. The study showed that the type of dwelling (house or 
apartment) influences the time windows are open and also affects how wide windows are left 
open. According to the study main ventilation zones are bedrooms, while the most significant 
percentage of windows never opened are in living rooms and bathrooms. The investigations 
showed that the maximum of window openings occur in the morning. During the early 
afternoon (when cooking), the number of open windows is still relatively high but gradually 
decreases until the return home of working inhabitants (about 5 p.m.). 
In a study by authors (Verbruggen et al., 2021), a detailed description of window-use habits in 
different rooms is presented in their survey conducted in 499 Belgian households between 
March 19 and May 6, 2019. Most occupants (70%) leave the living room windows always 
closed during wintertime. While one-fifth regularly opens the windows in the morning or when 
present. Only 10% keep the windows closed, while 19% leave the windows always open. Many 
occupants (57%) regularly open the windows for a shorter period in the morning, evening, at 
night and when present. 
The kitchen window remains always closed in 66% of the households in wintertime. In the 
summertime, this decreases to only 16%. Almost 35% of the households leave the kitchen 
window always open in summertime.  The windows are often open in the bedroom, and most 
window habits are present. Only 29% of the occupants leave the windows always closed in 
winter; this drops even further to a mere 5% in summer. More than 10% leave the bedroom 
windows always open in wintertime; this increases to 37% in summer.

4.4 Conclusions from the literature review 
 
Few takeaways from the above-described literature papers which is summarized by brief points

• Ventilative cooling can meet the cooling needs and maintain the comfort level.

5 Aim and research questions 
 
 
 
The primary goal of the project, accomplished within a span of five months, is to create a 
Grasshopper workflow that incorporates the occupant behaviour model in conjunction with the 
use of venting hatches and roof windows as key elements for achieving ventilative cooling. 
The project seeks to implement an occupant behaviour model using Grasshopper workflow 
with the aim to identify optimal venting openings needed for residential summer comfort to 
address thermal discomfort risks leading to problems like draught, overheating and 
overcooling.   
The aim was achieved by developing the parametric study workflow in Grasshopper, but with 
some limitations, which will be discussed in later chapters. The workflow is developed in 
accordance with all relevant Danish building standards and appropriate assumptions wherever 
needed. Consequently, by thoroughly reading, analysing and comprehending all available 
literature studies, a well-curated and equitable research gap was identified, leading to the 
formulation of research questions that effectively address and fill the identified gap in the best 
possible way. The formulated research questions stated are as follows: 
 
1. Can an occupancy behaviour model with real-time simulation be implemented in 
Grasshopper? 
 
2. Can draught risks be taken into account for the intended occupancy behaviour 
model? 
 
3. Can thermal comfort be achieved in single-story houses using the venting hatch 
system? 
 
4. What is the contribution of roof windows to achieving thermally comfortable indoor 
environments in residential buildings?

Ladybug imports standard EnergyPlus Weather files (.EPW) into Grasshopper. It provides a 
variety of 2D and 3D interactive climate graphics that support decision-making during the early 
stages of design. It also simplifies the analysis process, automates and expedites the 
calculations, and provides easy-to-understand graphical visualizations in the 3D modelling 
interface of Rhino/Grasshopper. Integration with visual programming environments allows 
instantaneous feedback on design modifications and a high degree of customization. It also 
allows users to work with validated energy and daylighting engines such as EnergyPlus, 
Radiance and Daysim (Roudsari & Pak, 2013).

6.4.1 Converting Rhino model into Honeybee (HB) zones    
 
After creating a Rhino model, the second step is to import it into the Grasshopper workflow 
for the parametric simulations through the Geometry component. All the Grasshopper 
components mentioned or described from here on will be in italic fonts for the reader's ease of 
understanding. The Geometry component contains a collection of generic geometries from 
Rhino. Users can import single geometries, meaning models consisting of single zones or 
multiple geometries, meaning having multiple zones. Another important thing to understand is 
that the geometry created in Rhino has to be closed Boundary representations (Breps) or poly 
surfaces because Honeybee (HB) only takes closed Breps for parametric simulations, or else it 
is bound to give an error.   
 
 
Fig 6.14: Importing the Rhino model into Grasshopper canvas 
 
The next step for creating HB zones is to apply the HB Intersect Solids component. This 
component takes a list of closed Breps or polysurfaces and splits their faces to ensure matching 
surfaces between them. The matching room faces are required in order to construct a multi-
room energy model since conductive heat flow will not occur correctly across interior faces 
when their surfaces do not match.  
After the solids are intersected, the next process is creating an HB room by using HB Room 
from Solid component. It is an essential step to create HB rooms; without it, the simulation 
cannot identify the zones required for the smooth running of the recipe. Fig 6.15 provides a 
close-up representation of the inputs to create a successful HB room. Each room or zone is 
identified by a unique name and the geometry of that particular room.  
 
==PAGE:37==

Fig 6.15: Generating HB rooms 
 
Adjacencies between matching faces are solved by HB Solve Adjacency. One must note that 
adjacency between adjacent and similar faces must be solved for the program to identify and 
make distinctions if there are more than one zones. Adiabatic boundary conditions have been 
set to “False” because, in reality, there will be some heat exchange between adjacent zones and 
setting it otherwise will amount to over-simplifications of the modelling process. Adjacencies 
have been solved for interior surfaces, exterior surfaces and between kitchen and roof windows.  
It is interesting to note one more thing, during the modelling process in Rhino, the kitchen and 
living room were designed as separate zones because if it had been modelled as one zone, there 
would be issues arising due to equipment heat gain as it would consider an entire zone as 
kitchen including the living room and the program would apply load values evenly, and this 
would be far from reality. To overcome this issue, an air boundary condition is applied. Air 
boundary is a condition wherein free-flowing air and heat exchange can occur. Even though in 
the Rhino model, the kitchen and living room are two separate zones, by applying air boundary 
conditions, HB will treat these as zones with air exchange happening between them. The 
condition for applying air boundaries is that the zones should be adjacent. The same procedure 
is applied by creating an air boundary between the roof windows and the kitchen.

6.4.2 Adding HB apertures 
 
Glazing: 
Window glazings can cause significant thermal energy gains or losses in buildings. (Manz & 
Menti, 2011) presented eight case studies in Europe: Bucharest, London, Madrid, Moscow, 
Rome, Stockholm, Warsaw and Zurich and concluded by stating that modern triple glazing 
performs best and enables net gains at south facades in December, even in Moscow and

In terms of energy flows, glazings can be characterized by two parameters: Firstly, the total 
solar energy transmittance g, which denotes the share of the incoming solar energy, is 
converted into heat inside the indoor space. Secondly, the thermal transmittance U describes 
how much heat is transferred through the glazing per square meter and the Kelvin temperature 
difference between inside and outside. Therefore, the parameter g characterizes the solar gain 
of a glazing, whereas U stands for the thermal loss of a glazing (Manz & Menti, 2011). The 
authors state that an increasing number of panes leads to decreasing U and g values. This is 
because more cavities provide better thermal insulation, whereas more light is reflected due to 
more glass-gas interfaces.  
Additionally, similar to U glazing characteristics, Solar Heat Gain Coefficient (SHGC) plays a 
crucial role in the window's energy performance. It is the fraction of solar radiation admitted 
through a window, door or roof window, either transmitted directly and/or absorbed, and 
subsequently released as heat inside a home. A product with a high SHGC rating is more 
effective at collecting solar heat during the winter. A low SHGC rating product is more 
effective at reducing summer cooling loads by blocking sun heat gains (Energy Performance 
Ratings for Windows, Doors, and Skylights | Department of Energy, n.d.).  
The amount of visible spectrum of light transmitted through the windowpane is referred to as 
visible transmittance (Tvis). An object with higher Tvis transmits more visible light, reducing 
the dependence on lighting requirement, and is usually expressed as a number between 0 and 
1 (Energy Performance Ratings for Windows, Doors, and Skylights | Department of Energy, n.d.)  
Moreover, it is essential to revisit one of the points mentioned in the literature review section. 
Authors (Rueegg et al., 2001) concluded in their studies that the window frame, not the 
windowpane, is the critical element and that there will be no unacceptable draught risks in the 
occupied zone as long as the window frame is highly insulating.

It is worth mentioning again that this thesis focuses on occupant behaviour and venting hatch

Let us revisit some of the points regarding the opening/closing mentioned earlier in the 
“Literature review” chapter. (Nicol, 2001) investigated and analysed how window opening 
control varies with outdoor temperature through surveys. The author mentioned that the 
proportion of windows open tends to increase significantly as the outdoor temperatures rise 
above 10⁰ C. 50% of European offices have windows open at 22⁰ C, rising to 80% at 33⁰ C. 
There is no precise temperature at which everyone opens a window, but as the temperature 
rises, there is an increased probability that they may have done so, according to the author.  
(R. Andersen et al., 2013) performed measurements in 10 rented Danish apartments and five 
privately-owned single-family houses. The authors investigated the probability of opening and 
closing windows. CO2 concentration, indoor temperature and solar radiation were positively 
correlated with the window opening probability, while outdoor temperature was negatively 
correlated with the probability of closing windows. In the bedroom, the CO2 concentration was 
the most critical variable for the probability of opening windows, while it did not significantly 
affect the living room.  
The authors (Verbruggen et al., 2021) present a detailed study of window use based on habits 
and activities. They state that most occupants (70%) leave the living room windows always

6.4.6 HB Airflow Network (AFN) 
 
Airflows form a critical part of the study. Airflows can significantly impact the occupants' 
energy consumption, space conditioning loads, indoor air quality, and indoor thermal comfort. 
The airflows can be divided into two types: controlled and uncontrolled. The controlled 
airflows are mainly driven by fans. Uncontrolled airflows are driven by a combination of wind 
==PAGE:56==
56 
Methodology 
and forced airflow through the building envelope, leaky air distribution system ducts, and 
unbalanced return and exhaust air flows. Since fan flow rates are a function of external 
pressures, uncontrolled airflows also impact controlled airflows (Gu, n.d.).  
A study (Gu, n.d.) provides a detailed guide on the use of EnergyPlus Airflow network (AFN) 
implementation, and some interesting points mentioned in his paper are as follows. Simulating 
airflow impacts can be done by establishing an airflow network consisting of a set of nodes 
connected by airflow elements. A relationship between airflow and pressures must be specified 
for each element. Since this approach assumes that air flows from one node to another, it 
simplifies airflows through its pathways and cannot predict internal air circulation within a 
thermal zone.  
Through the HB Airflow Network component, uses EnergyPlus AFN to simulate airflows in a 
zone or multizone. AFN models airflow from one zone to another, accounting for pressure 
changes induced by wind and air density differences. The most important Energy Management 
System (EMS) program will be written and executed in the simulations by passing the HB 
model through the HB Airflow Network component. The EMS uses EnergyPlus runtime 
language (Erl) for custom programming of control routines. HB uses this program in 
conjunction with EnergyPlus and outputs the results. The EMS program is a ventilation control 
setpoint in which the user has input in HB Ventilation Control. It dictates when to open the 
operable apertures in the room based on the temperature setpoint provided. EMS is briefly 
discussed below. 
 
Energy Management System (EMS):   
The Energy Management System (EMS) is one of the high-level control methods available in

types of control actions. The concept is to emulate, inside EnergyPlus, the types of controls

objects use individual fields to store the statements for an Erl program. As with most 
EnergyPlus objects, each field is separated by a comma and typically given a separate line of 
text for readability. In this case, each field can be considered a separate line of Erl program

In this chapter, we shall be discussing the ventilative cooling algorithm. During the occupied

discomfort risks during summers in Denmark using ESP-r and coupling with a Building

air temperature is lower than the indoor operative zone temperature and when the indoor

The hatches in the zones are designed to open with a single-step angular opening of 60⁰. An 
angular opening of 60⁰ is selected since it translates to the sliding operable area of 50%. The 
calculation of the same is described in fig 6.27. The opening setpoint (Tset (opening)) of hatches is 
24⁰C chosen because ventilative cooling setpoints often range around this temperature band 
(Psomas, Heiselberg, et al., 2017)  and the closing setpoint (Tset (closing)) of 20⁰C is selected, 
based on the Danish standards DS 474 (1995) (Dansk Standard DS 474 E Norm for Specifikation Af 
Termisk Indeklima Code for Thermal Indoor Climate, 1995) tolerance requirements criteria.  
The algorithm follows a series of conditions to determine whether the venting hatches should 
be open or closed. The algorithm first checks the condition for indoor temperature (Tin) if Tin 
<= Tset (closing). If the condition is “Yes”, the hatches close, but if the condition is “No”, it moves 
==PAGE:60==
60 
Methodology 
to check the opening setpoint criteria (Tset (opening)). If Tin is still not above the opening setpoint, 
i.e., between 20⁰C and 24⁰C, the status for the hatches remains unchanged and will remain 
closed. To explain this to the reader, suppose Tin is 22⁰C; thus, it passes the first criteria Tin > 
closing setpoint of 20⁰C but is less than the opening setpoint of 24⁰C. Hence hatches will remain 
closed.  
The next condition to open the hatches is when the indoor temperature is between 24⁰C and 
27⁰C. In this temperature range, hatches will open 50%, as shown in fig 7.2. If the indoor 
temperature does not meet this condition and remains below 27⁰C, the hatches will remain open 
at 50%, and the algorithm proceeds to the next timestep. However, if Tin > 27⁰C indicating 
higher indoor temperature resulting in discomfort, occupants are expected to open the roof 
windows to offset the discomfort quotient. The threshold of 27⁰C is chosen because, as the 
temperature increases, there is a higher likelihood of occupants opening the windows (Nicol, 
2001). Additionally, the BR18 overheating criteria indicate a risk of overheating in the 
occupied space above this limit. Once the venting hatches and roof windows are opened, the 
simulation progresses to the next timestep. 
The reader might have questions about why there are no criteria for checking the outdoor 
temperatures in the algorithm. The reason for not contemplating the outdoor condition is 
because of the assumption that the criteria for occupants to open or close will depend first on 
the indoor comfort and, later, the outdoor conditions. If a window is open because the occupants 
feel too warm, it will probably stay open until they start to feel cold (R. V. Andersen et al., 
2009). Irrespective of the outdoor temperature, occupants will respond to their perceived 
warmth or coldness and take appropriate actions to alleviate their discomfort. The occupants 
of naturally ventilated buildings can open their windows for two primary purposes: to improve 
indoor air quality or to help improve comfort by reducing indoor temperature and encouraging 
air movement (Nicol, 2001). Thus, concluding by stating that indoor comfort is assumed to be 
the first criterion in the above-described algorithm as it will have a big say in deciding indoor 
comfort.

7.2 Draught  
 
Draught is the unwanted local cooling of the body caused mainly due to air movement. The 
discomfort due to draught may be expressed as the percentage of people predicted to be 
affected. There is no minimum air velocity that is necessary for thermal comfort. However, 
increased air velocity can be used to offset the warmth sensation caused by increased 
temperature (DS 7730) (DS-Publikationstyper Dansk Standard Udgiver Forskellige Publikationstyper. 
Typen På Denne Publikation Fremgår Af Forsiden, 2005a). With summer indoor comfort, the design 
limit for air speeds is maintained at 0.2m/s (Fink, 2015) to reduce the risk of draught discomfort 
for the occupants.  
Let us revisit some of the points mentioned in the literature study. A study (Schiavon et al., 
2016) experimented with evaluating ankle draught risks for women with uncovered ankles. 
Thirty university students participated in the experiments. The authors demonstrated that the 
subjects were more sensitive to ankle draught than expected. Between 23% and 57% of the 
subjects found air movements at ankle height unacceptable, compared to 20% and 37% of the 
subjects who found the overall thermal environment unacceptable. Likewise, the study 
(Pitchurov et al., 2002) surveyed occupants' responses to the thermal environment in eight 
office buildings and reported that about 24% of the respondents complained they were bothered 
daily by the draught, mainly on their lower legs. Thus, occupants are more prone to draught 
effects on the lower part of the body as compared to the upper part.

8 Occupant behaviour model scenario 1: 
intermittent occupancy with venting hatches 
 
 
 
In this chapter, a proposition is developed on how occupants are more likely to behave in terms 
of opening venting hatches and roof windows. When the dwellings are occupied, the users will 
behave instinctively so as to keep thermal sensations within comfortable range by opening or 
closing the windows. Due to software limitations, this chapter outlines a manual approach 
involving the adjustment of setpoints to determine the status of venting hatches, roof windows, 
and the operative temperatures of the zones. Further, to let the reader know, no ventilation 
schedule is applied in this scenario, wherein ventilation is always ON by default. Whereas, in 
scenario 2, which is discussed in the following chapter, a window ventilation schedule is 
applied from 17:00-08:00 and compared with scenario 1 results to know the efficiency of the 
investigated venting hatches.   
In this context, let us revisit some of the points in the study (Verbruggen et al., 2021) in the 
literature review sub-chapter 4.3.1. The authors stated that in the summertime, the number of 
occupants leaving the bedroom window open when they leave dwellings increases significantly 
to 41%. Only 54% of the occupants close all windows when leaving the dwelling in winter, 
with an additional 22% that close only the accessible windows. In the summertime, the 
percentage of occupants that close all windows are reduced to 39%. Naturally, during summers, 
when the outdoor temperatures are at a higher level and if the occupants chose to leave all the 
windows and roof windows closed, they are more likely to experience higher indoor 
temperatures when they return. To address this situation, a hypothesis is developed to predict 
the current day status of venting hatches and roof windows when they leave for work, based 
on the occupants' experience of the previous day's operative temperature. By predicting the 
status, occupants can make informed decisions to maintain the operative temperatures to an 
optimum level, ensuring thermal comfort.     
The hypothesis operates along the following pathway:  
To predict venting hatch and roof windows opening/closing status, occupants would base their 
decision on the current day's (d0) average outdoor temperatures and the previous three days (d-
1, d-2, d-3) peak indoor operative temperatures. Where d0 refers to the current day, d-1 refers 
to the day before the current day. For example, to be more precise and clearer, if it is supposed 
the current day is the 16th of June, d-1 would normally be referred to as be 15th of June, d-2 
would be the 14th of June, and d-3 would be the 13th of June. Peak operative temperatures would 
be based on previous days, and average outdoor temperature will be considered from the 
current day. 
A human tolerable setpoint of 22⁰C and 24⁰C is assumed in the hypothesis. According to this 
assumption, if the current day's (d0) peak operative temperature <= 24⁰C, the tolerable limit is 
24⁰C. However, if the peak operative temperature >= 25⁰C, the tolerable limit shifts to 22⁰C.

contrary, if the indoor conditions are low, they would typically prefer warmer environments. 
The value of 22⁰C and 24⁰C is chosen because the thermally optimal indoor temperatures are 
around these values. The choice of 22⁰C and 24⁰C is driven by the fact that these values align 
closely with thermally optimal indoor temperatures.

11 Discussion  
 
 
 
In modern times with technological advancements and improvements, with the advent of smart 
housing tools, energy leakages and over-energy use has decreased to a great extent. Right from 
the conceptual stage to the design stage to the handover of the end product to the end users, a 
great deal of smart tools and technology are in play. This has helped in bringing down total 
energy use, thus improving energy savings. On the flip side, due to climate change, the global 
environment is warming faster than ever thought. Energy saved during winter seasons is getting 
offset due to rising energy demand during summers thanks to above-than-normal temperatures.  
The parametric tool is a critical aspect nowadays for building designers, architects and all the 
relevant stakeholders. It provides flexibility, future thinking ability and a three-dimension view 
of the proposed model even before the beginning of the construction. The current Danish 
building regulations BR18 stipulate requirements that old and new buildings should adhere to. 
But the main focus of these regulations has been chiefly on heating seasons. It underestimates 
the potential issues generated during the cooling phase in warmer periods leading to unhealthy 
indoor temperatures and thermal discomfort (Psomas, Fiorentini, et al., 2017). With the climate 
becoming warmer and every summer being more intense than the previous, emphasis has to be 
also diverted on the same. The parametric workflow developed in Grasshopper for this thesis 
is an attempt to study the comfort conditions in relation to the venting hatches during the 
warmer months.  
This chapter addresses the research questions formulated at the beginning of the process.  
 
1. Can the occupancy behaviour model with real-time simulation be implemented in 
Grasshopper? 
 
An attempt has been made through this thesis to implement the occupant behaviour model 
through the Grasshopper workflow. Much of the research papers have studied the behavioural 
model and its impact on indoor comfort but rarely has anyone done the same using Grasshopper 
as the tool. Also, there is a lack of study on the venting hatch and roof window and its effect 
on optimal indoor comfort. Hence the study conducted for this thesis tries to address and fill 
this gap to the best of the abilities.  
Coming to the question, the occupant behaviour model can be implemented, but simulating 
real-time has some limitations. The methodology employed in Chapter 8 is executed manually 
rather than dynamically. As mentioned, the Grasshopper uses the EnergyPlus simulation 
engine to run the simulations. To achieve real-time simulation, which calculates ∆T based on 
the previous day's peak operative temperature and the current day's average outdoor 
temperature, as well as outputting the venting hatch and sky-window status for each day, 
==PAGE:105==
105 
Discussion 
integration with a tool called the Building Control Virtual Test Bed (BCVTB)  (Pang et al., 
2011). BCVTB is basically a platform to synchronize EnergyPlus simulation to real-time mode 
and exchange the data in a feedback loop. This is virtually outside the scope of the present 
study. A study (Psomas, Fiorentini, et al., 2017) also used the BCVTB tool, which the authors 
integrated with ESP-r simulation software and ran the simulation iteratively to achieve the 
results.  
Overall, the behaviour model can be implemented manually within Grasshopper, although 
limitations imposed by the software should be acknowledged. Simulating it in real time is 
necessary, as this would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the results. On the 
other hand, the results obtained for the behaviour model during full occupancy, as discussed in 
sub-chapter 10.1, demonstrate the implementation and execution of the algorithm using the 
Grasshopper workflow because the results align with the existing building regulation standards.  
 
2. Can draught risks be taken into account for the intended occupancy behaviour 
model? 
 
The Draught quotient is considered for the full occupancy model during the occupied hours. 
Five measuring heights from the floor surface level at a distance of 1m from the venting hatch 
are studied, analysed and presented in sub-chapter 10.1.4. As expected, at a lower height from 
the surface, there are substantial risk factors that occupants may experience due to increased 
air velocity, with some even touching speeds of close to 0.9m/s (fig 10.14). The result data of 
air velocity as a function of the temperature difference between indoors and outdoors in fig 
10.16 shows some peculiar data. Air velocity is shown only in the temperature difference range 
of 5⁰C and 10⁰C. Outside this range, the opening of the venting hatch does not take place and 
is relatively closed. Strangely, no hatch opening is indicated even when the outdoors gets warm, 
i.e., when ∆T gets smaller and smaller. This may be because even though ∆T gets smaller, the 
operative temperatures stay within the opening setpoint control of 24⁰C and do not breach the 
opening setpoint, thus triggering the venting hatch to remain close.   
Colder air travels closer to the surface; hence the discomfort risks are higher. But as the height 
increases, the air velocity and discomfort decrease. Other factors, such as lifestyle, also play a 
huge role in draught discomfort. People with sedentary lifestyles are more prone to draught, 
clothing habits, metabolic rates, outdoor temperature, humidity, air temperature and airspeed. 
A standard clothing and metabolic rate are considered for the draught analysis for the whole 
summer period, which may not be an ideal real-life scenario since occupants would change 
clothing, and not every day would be the same, and the same assumptions would go for 
metabolic rates as well. A more realistic approach with changing clothing is not considered for 
the study. Studying the pattern of airflow and air speed requires a more detailed CFD study 
which is computationally heavy software compared to Grasshopper and would give detailed 
insights into directions and speed of airflow. We obtained the expected results, but as 
mentioned, a more detailed approach focusing only on the draught aspect would yield even 
better data in terms of quality.

3. Can thermal comfort be achieved in single-story houses using the venting hatch 
system? 
 
Upon analyzing the results data, it is evident that all zones consistently meet the BR18 
overheating limit during the full occupancy, as presented in table 10.2 for the analysed summer 
months of June, July, and August. The majority of the operative temperatures in each zone 
remain below 26⁰C, with minimal temperatures exceeding the overheating limit of 27⁰C and 
no instances surpassing 28⁰C. Furthermore, the category II summer comfort range of 23⁰C - 
26⁰C, corresponding to the PMV of -0.5 < PMV < +0.5, 0.5 clothing and with the sedentary 
activity of 1.2 MET, is also achieved through the results displayed in fig 10.9.  
Among the zones, the living room and kitchen, exhibit the highest percentage of occupied hours 
within the comfort range category, while bedroom 4 displays the lowest, as illustrated in fig 
10.10. This observation may also explain why bedroom 4, along with another east-facing 
bedroom 1, has the highest percentage of cold sensation, as shown in fig 10.11. The intermittent 
occupancy comparison yields that the venting hatch, when it is 100% operational, does help 
maintain the indoor conditions within optimal level compared to the windows when it is opened 
only between 17:00-08:00 hours.   
It is pertinent to note that this study only considers a single venting hatch angular opening of 
60⁰. Exploring different angular openings based on indoor conditions at various stages may 
yield interesting data not investigated in this thesis. Considering the scarcity of available data 
on the implementation of venting hatches and their overall impact on indoor thermal comfort, 
this aspect should be taken into account for future studies. Also, the implementation of the 
venting hatch and roof windows in varied climate aspects should be considered in future 
studies. The results obtained in this thesis are obtained from small sets of analysed months, 
which may not give a complete picture. Hence a more extensive data set is required to come to 
any tangible outcome.   
 
4. What is the contribution of roof windows to achieving thermally comfortable indoor 
environments in residential buildings? 
 
The kitchen zone in the model house does not have access to venting hatches due to the absence 
of external walls. Hence, it is prudent to provide access to ventilation, irrespective of its 
location. Thus, two roof windows are provided for ventilation instead of venting hatches. From 
the results analysed, roof windows effectively contribute to ventilative cooling providing a 
comfortable indoor environment during the occupied hours, as indicated in table 10.2. The 
presence of roof windows also impacts the comfort level in the living room, as it shares airflow 
with the kitchen. The percentage (94.9%) of occupied hours between 23⁰C - 26⁰C in the kitchen 
is the highest compared to any other zone, with the living room coming next, as displayed in 
fig 10.10. Moreover, the kitchen also exhibits the highest percentage of occupants experiencing 
thermal comfort during occupied hours, as depicted in fig 10.13. In totality, it demonstrates 
roof windows' effect in maintaining a thermally favourable environment.  
==PAGE:107==
107 
Discussion 
In this project, venting hatches used in conjunction with the roof windows for ventilative 
cooling for single-story house fulfils the regulations mentioned in the Danish standards. 
However, it is essential to note that this project focuses on the use of venting hatches and roof 
windows in a single-story house, and the results are limited to the summer period. Further 
investigations are warranted to assess the performance of roof windows in combination with 
venting hatches throughout the year and in double-storey houses, where stack ventilation plays 
a significant role in influencing airflow and indoor temperature. Such studies would provide 
valuable insights into the effectiveness of these strategies in different scenarios.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:108==
108 
Conclusion 
 
12 Conclusion  
 
 
 
With climate change rapidly progressing and each passing summer month becoming warmer 
than the previous, there is a need to evaluate the occupants' indoor comfort quotient. Building 
design and construction is a holistic process requiring inputs from various stakeholders like 
architects, engineers, contractors and ultimately, the end-users. Currently, not much emphasis 
is led on the summer comfort, thereby underestimating the potential issue arising from the 
same. Modelling an occupant's behaviour is a complex task. How the individual will react to 
certain situations varies from person to person, and there are no specific guidelines on how a 
person would react in a particular situation. An attempt has been made to implement an 
occupant behaviour model and study the potential impact of the venting hatch and roof window 
on the residents inside the house during the peak summer months in Denmark using a 
Grasshopper script. It will be helpful to reiterate that the venting hatch serves a purpose to be 
used in conjunction with the windows and roof windows, and this thesis does not attempt to 
replace the venting hatch with the windows. Thus, this thesis tries to answer these questions 
and fill the gap arising due to the lack of available data.  
Through the analysis of the obtained data, it can be concluded that the occupant behaviour 
model can be implemented and executed in the Grasshopper tool. However, the software does 
pose certain constraints that affect the results, indicating the potential for further improvements 
if these constraints are addressed in future developments. Regarding the venting hatch, the 
results do provide an encouraging lead in terms of overall indoor comfort for the occupants.  
All the zones achieve overheating conditions; the indoor summer comfort category II 
temperature is also fulfilled. Venting openings of 60⁰ do serve the purpose of providing the 
thermal comfort required during the summers. But the data reflects only a limited number of 
months, specific locations and specific weather data, as studied for this thesis. A more 
comprehensive analysis should encompass an entire year and different locations with varying 
weather patterns to truly understand the efficacy of venting hatches. This will provide more 
robust data. For example, an angular opening of 60⁰ may not prove efficient in a location with 
weather conditions that differ slightly from Denmark. Additionally, a more detailed 
investigation using computational fluid dynamics (CFD) simulations is necessary to assess the 
impact of venting hatches on airflows and draughts, providing a deeper understanding of 
airflow patterns across different zones. 
This thesis offers guidelines, limitations and a well-defined workflow that can serve as a 
foundation for further development within the Grasshopper environment, thus eliminating the 
need to start from scratch. 
 
 
==PAGE:109==
109 
Conclusion 
12.1 Limitations and future work 
 
The project undertaken has several limitations that should be acknowledged. The methodology 
employed in Chapter 8 is executed manually rather than dynamically. As mentioned earlier, 
the Grasshopper tool utilizes the EnergyPlus simulation engine for running simulations. To 
achieve real-time simulation with a feedback loop, as demonstrated by the authors (Pang et al., 
2011) and (Psomas, Fiorentini, et al., 2017), integration with the Building Control Virtual Test 
Bed (BCVTB) is required. However, the use of BCVTB falls outside the scope of this study. 
Future work could involve implementing a similar mechanism by coupling EnergyPlus with 
the BCVTB tool, as this would yield more refined results. The model used in this study is a 
single-story house, and future work could involve investigating two-stories houses. Further, 
this thesis assumed that a house model is a stand-alone object considered for the analysis. 
Ideally, in a real-world scenario, there would likely be some objects besides the house 
obstructing or influencing the airflow entering the house. This could, too, be considered for 
any future analysis.  
Furthermore, it is essential to note that only one venting hatch opening is considered in this 
study. Further exploration could involve studying multiple openings based on indoor 
conditions at different times of the day. Another significant limitation is the analysis period. 
Since this project only examines three summer months, a proposition for future studies is to 
extend the analysis to encompass an entire year, providing results that account for the variations 
throughout the seasons.
