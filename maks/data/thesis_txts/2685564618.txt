Abstract
Magnetic bearings enable contact free support for rotors. Such a trait is advantageous in a
variety of applications. A downside of this bearing type is the inherent unstable behavior,
and stabilisation through an active control system is inevitable. The aim of this thesis
is to develop a mathematical model capable of accurately describing the rotor-bearing
system as well as designing and implementing a state space controller to accomplish a
stably levitated rotor.
The rotor is supported radially by two Permanent Magnetic Bearings (PMBs) and axi-
ally by an Active Magnetic Bearing (AMB). The forces from each bearing type have been
modelled analytically/numerically and validated experimentally. This procedure provided
satisfactory results for both types of bearings.
The dynamics of the rotor-bearing system were described using the Newton-Euler equa-
tions along with a linear model for the electrical dynamics of the AMB. The equations
were simplified via a linearization to markedly simplify further modelling and gain insight
in fundamental traits of the system. As anticipated, an eigenvalue analysis revealed in-
herent instability withing certain modes of the system. A state space controller with a
full order observer were then designed using a feedback law of the estimated states. The
observer were needed as the current measurements were deemed too noisy to use in a
feedback system.

Motivation
This project is motivated to achieve theoretical and practical knowledge within the technol-
ogy of magnetic bearings in a rotor-bearing system. Usually such systems are controlled
via classical PID controllers, but this project aims at designing and implementing a model-
based controller.
Another important aspect of this project was to experimentally validate the numerically
and analytically calculated bearing forces to obtain a realistic mathematical model.
Lastly, it was also sought to expand the sensoring system with the intend of retrieving ad-
ditional information of the system in operation and build a more robust controller. With
this information, the quality of the mathematical model can be assessed and perhaps
improved by adjusting certain parameters.

Introduction
Magnets posses a very advantageous trait within several mechanical applications - the
ability of contact free repulsion and attraction of magnetic material. In the field of rotat-
ing machinery, magnetic bearings enable contact free support for rotating shafts through
magnetic forces. The contactless support eliminates resistance between rotating and non-
rotating parts. In fact, using magnetic bearings, the sole resistance originates from friction
between the surrounding fluid and rotating shaft surface.
The technology of magnetic bearings constitute two types of bearings - Permanent Mag-
netic Bearings (PMBs) and Active Magnetic Bearings (AMBs).
This project aims at
modelling, implementing and testing a vertical rotor supported radially by PMBs and
axially by an AMB. PMBs provide passive suspension via repulsive forces between perma-
nent magnets attached to the rotor and the foundation. The PMB technology is relative
simple and cheap to implement, but comes with limited stiffness and damping abilities.
AMBs provide support via attractive electromagnetic forces. An AMB is essentially a pair
of electromagnets being actively managed by a feedback control system allowing stiffness
and damping abilities to be varied and optimized. A functional control system is key,
since AMBs are inherently unstable. To succesfully build an AMB, a system containing
several electronic units are needed. This is a demanding and expensive task, which re-
quires comprehensive theoretical and practical knowledge of dynamical systems within
multiple physical domains [1].
Across a variety of industries, the contact free support of magnetic bearings offer ad-
vantageous attributes such as:
• Removal of lubricant
• Reduction of wear and tear
• Elevation of rotational speed
The absence of oil lubricant makes the magnetic bearing technology suitable in food and
medical industries where a high level of cleanliness are demanded. The reduction of wear
and tear can reduce operational downtime of machinery markedly, as maintenance has
to be executed less often. For rotating machinery installed in a hard-to-reach environ-
ment such as the sea bed or in space, the reduction of downtime is highly beneficial
from a practical and economical point of view. Magnetic bearings allow for very high
rotational speeds to be realized. This ability is useful when storing energy in a rotating
mass, a flywheel. In such an application, minimizing energy losses crucial making the
magnetic bearings even more attractable. In Flywheel Energy Storage Systems (FESS),
the rotor-bearing system is often encapsulated inside a vacuum chamber to completely
remove frictional forces and reduce energy losses even further [2].
Although PMBs are generally used to carry static loads, radial PMBs have been succes-
fully implemented and tested in a rotor-bearing capable of reaching 5500 rpm without
significant vibrations [3].

Current Sensors
To monitor the current in the electromagnets, a pair of current sensors are soldered on to
the MXP A board. The sensors are of the type ACS712 from the company Allegro. The
sensors detect the strength of a magnetic field generated when current passes through a
wire through the Hall effect. They are capable of measuring currents between +/ −5
A with a sensitivity of 185 mV/A. In the absence of current the sensors will output 2.5
VDC. In this system they will only encounter currents in the range of 0 −0.6 A, which is
a relatively small range when comparing to the full measuring range of the sensors. This
led to concerns on whether adequate measurement resolutions could be achieved. During
testing, it was found that the sensor signals contained a significant amount of noise. The
noise was evenly distributed across the measurable frequency spectrum and a reasonable
filter could therefore not be applied. Raw current measurement signals resulting from
sending constant currents of 0.25 and 0.50 A to the electromagnets are seen in Fig. 10, p.

Rotor-bearing dynamics
In this chapter a mathematical model of the system is developed. This includes establish-
ing dynamic equilibrium of the rotor, modelling bearing force and setting up the equations
governing the coil dynamics. To ensure that the mathematical model reflects the actual
system as much as possible experiments has been performed to validate the bearing forces.
Equations of motion
The dynamics of the rotor are mathematically modelled using the Newton-Euler equa-
tions [7]. In the formulation of these equations the rotor is assumed to be rigid. This
assumption disregards flexible effects and greatly simplifies the modelling. Furthermore,
it is assumed that small rotor movements will be present, allowing the equations to be
linearized. These assumptions action simplifies the equations significantly and reduces
them to linear differential equations suitable for designing a linear controller.
(a) Reference frames
To apply the Newton-Euler approach different frames of references are defined. To set
up moment equilibrium using Newton-Euler, the moments acting on the body have to be
expressed in a frame where the inertia is constant. Additionally, accelerations must be
absolute with respect to a fixed reference.

coincides with the rotor center of mass (CM) in static equilibrium. Frame B1 and B2 are
attached to the rotor CM and follow the rotor motion. Frame B1 follows the first tilting
motion α, and B2 follows both first and second tilting motion, α and β. The definition
of the reference frames can be seen below:

(c) Dynamic equilibrium
With the kinematics defined, dynamic equilibrium can be set up. The task here is to

Design of Controller
The aim of the controller is to target the inherently unstable behaviour of the system,
and thus enforce that the real part of the eigenvalues becomes negative.
When designing the controller it is important to aim at reducing the responses of the
states as much as possible under a realistic set of disturbances. The requirements are:
1. Obtain asymptotic stability
2. Restrict maximum possible vertical displacement
3. The current in the coils must be kept below 0.58 A to prevent bursting the electro-
magnets.
4. The commanded voltage u must not exceed the available power from the amplifier.
5. The control current i must not exceed the bias current

After implementing the control system, levitation attempts revealed that the rotor tilting
affected the electromagnetic forces significantly. The effect was especially interfering for
the small air gap of 2 mm. The tilting caused the thrust discs edges to get relatively
close to the electromagnets causing an asymmetric magnetic field to be formed.
The

the tilting amplitudes and produce even more uncontrollable motion. After realizing this
larger thrust discs were build and mounted on the rotor, with the intend of limiting the
undesired tilting behaviour. The modification reduced tilting marginally, but rather large
amplitudes would develop again.

Discussion
A stably levitated rotor could not be achieved. The reasons for this are discussed in this
chapter.
Mathematical Model
A multiple degree of freedom mathematical model was developed using the Newton-Euler
approach along with a linear formulation of coil dynamics for electromagnets. The intend
of the modelling of the system was to develop a linear model being appropriate for linear
control theory to be applied and being able to capture the system dynamics satisfactory.
Although some components of the model has been analytically/numerically and experi-
mentally showed to be predominantly linear, there might be some non-linear effects that
can not be neglected. While attempting to levitate the rotor, the electromagnets seemed
to trigger tilting large motion. The effect of the large tilting on the electromagnet forces
are considered to have a significant contribution on the system dynamics. The theory
governing the electromagnetic forces presume the interacting surfaces, i.e. the thrust disc
and electromagnet, to be parallel and the accuracy of the theory becomes questionable in
the presence of large tilting motion. How to model such effects has not been investigated,
but can be explored experimentally using a similar setup as seen in Fig. 21. The large
tilting also limits the accuracy of the small angle approximations used to obtain the rotor
equations of motion. To retain linearity the strength of the PMBs can be increased to
reduce tilting.
To further expand the accuracy of the mathematical model radial forces from the elec-
tromagnets can be included. These forces will to some extend stiffen radial modes. The
radial forces has not been examined in this project.
Test Rig
The test rig was rebuild to add 3 extra proximity sensors and 2 current sensors. This
required a complete re-structuring of the electronics cabinet. The sensibility of the prox-
imity sensors proved to be of sufficient quality so that the corresponding signals could be
both differentiated and integrated in time. It was found that the current sensors could
only be used for monitoring the system, as the sensibility of the sensors was too poor.
This placed additional importance on the accuracy of the mathematical model as an ob-
server were needed to provide quality current estimates. Without an accurate observer,
the system is hardly going to function. Installing more sensitive current sensors, would be
beneficial for the test rig as the controller implementation will become markedly simpler.

Conclusion
Future work
To begin with a stable, levitated rotor has to be accomplished. Once levitation is achieved,
several theoretical and experimental improvements can be carried out.
Setting up a system for measuring and controlling the spin velocity would be a natu-
ral step towards a more advanced test rig. To spin the rotor a suggestion is to install a
single or a set air nozzles aiming at the turbine. A compressor can be used to provide
the required pressure to drive the air jet(s). Additionally, the RPM sensor has to be
installed to monitor and control the spin velocity via a feedback loop. Once the rotor
can be rotated, the unbalance response can be explored by attaching a small mass to the
shaft. The centrifugal force acting on the mass will transfer to the rotor and trigger radial
movements.
Implementing a motor/generator is considered to be unsuitable as rather large radial de-
flections appear due to the low damping and stiffness of the PMBs. It could be tried
to restrict these movements by increasing the stiffness of the PMB. This can either be
achieved by reducing the stator size or by changing the bearing design. A more compact
bearing can be designed using block magnets [3]. The block diamonds can be arranged
in a pattern to increase the magnetic field on the inside of the PMB while reducing the
field on the outside. Such a pattern is called a Halbach array.
Regarding the radial modes, one can investigate mathematically and experimentally if
these modes can be detected via the four proximity sensors. If this is the case, a compari-
son between the experimental and theoretical natural frequencies would provide valuable
insight in the accuracy of the mathematical model developed during this project. Given
large discrepancies are found, the mathematical model can be improved by including the
radial forces from the electromagnets and torques from PMB torques. As a last resort a
non-linear model with controller could be developed. Such a model can perhaps increase
accuracy even further, but will inevitably increase model complexity.
The robustness of the system can be tested by sending different excitation signals to
the AMB. Hereby relevant knowledge of the system performance under the influence of
disturbances can be achieved.
