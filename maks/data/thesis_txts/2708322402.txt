Abstract
A significant part of the earths infrastructure is comprised of seaborne vehicles and one
of the United Nations goals for a sustainable future, is to provide resilient infrastructure
[1]. Improving the resilience of infrastructure is a manyยญfaceted problem, and as such,
one improvement is in the form of autonomous ships.
To accelerate the development of autonomous ships, this thesis approaches the problem
of ensuring reliable and precise localisation of marine surface vehicles in harbour areas.
The standard solution for localization in harbour areas is to use the Global Positioning
System (GPS). This project aims to replace the GPS based solution with a localization
solution using a Wยญband radar and Intertial Measurement Unit (IMU) Building on this, the
fault tolerance of the GPS will also be increased using the fault diagnosis methodology
[2], by providing a measurement of when a fault have been detected in the system.
All components of the proposed system are tested on real data collected in connection
with this thesis. The results using the Wยญband radar and IMU for localization were not
found to be particularly promising. However, as a means of GPS error detection, the
redundancy provided by Wยญband radar and IMU sensors is promising for error detection.

Introduction
A lot of modern society depends on reliable and efficient transportation infrastructure.
Most of the modern infrastructure is comprised of trains, trucks and ships. The main inยญ
frastructures to support these are large roads, railways and harbours. While they are
all options when moving goods, different vehicles are often used in different situations,
where trucks are often used for relatively small distances, such as inside of a country, and
railways are used whenever available, for international transport, goods are most often
packed into large container ships, which can transport obscene amounts of containers
using a relatively low amount of fuel and personnel. The reliability of shipping as a transยญ
portation method is generally high, due to a focused effort on improving the infrastructure
around these methods, such as larger harbours, larger ships, and specialised training for
the crew.
A reliable infrastructure is a must for any modern society, not only to support the industries
of a country but also to allow people to get around easily, increasing personal freedom.
The importance of a reliable infrastructure is mirrored in one of the United Nations sustainยญ
able development goals, โ9: Build resilient infrastructure, promote inclusive and sustainยญ
able industrialization and foster innovation.โ This thesis will be related to subgoal 9.1, with
the overall description: โ9.1: Develop quality, reliable, sustainable and resilient infrastrucยญ
ture, including regional and transborder infrastructure, to support economic development
and human wellยญbeing, with a focus on affordable and equitable access for allโ [1]. Specifยญ
ically, the focus will be reliable and resilient infrastructure.
Autonomous ships could be a help in several infrastructure solutions. For container ships,
autonomous ships are already widespread, but the automation is constrained to only be
active in the long stretches on open water, where a simple controller using a GPS signal
and destination easily can perform the required steering actions. When navigating inยญ
harbour, all ships are steered by personnel, and while this is often reliable, automating
this area could free up people for other tasks, or reduce the cost of small scale operations,
such as those often seen in the form of ferries to less populated islands. With the docking
procedure handled automatically and reliably, more ferry departures could be handled
each day, dramatically improving the quality of this form of infrastructure.
Automation of ships is a common concept, and most commercial ships have access to a
simple GPS based system, where the desired course can be programmed, and the ship
will follow this route. The simple system will often not take any external information into
account, such as other ships, buoys and in very simple cases, landmasses. A course
tracking system will require personnel to maintain an overview of the area, as well as to
perform avoidance, and any docking manoeuvres.
To substitute this personnel, the system should be able to detect all other vessels and obยญ
stacles, calculate precise manoeuvres, and perform them with a high degree of precision
and accuracy.
When in harbour, the system will require precise estimates of the ship position and size.
Based on these requirements the main focus of the thesis is to improve the localisation
of ships and other marine surface vehicles. The main improvement will be an increase in
the reliability of the vehicle, by detecting faults in existing systems.

The reliability improvement will be investigated using a combination of easily accessible
technology which is already used in some marine settings, namely radar, GPS and IMUs.
By using popular and easily available sensors, existing research using these technologies
can be utilised, and further development and research will be more accessible.
The choice to use fault detection to improve the resilience of the infrastructure comes
from a scope reduction of this project. The ideal way to improve the resilience would be
to add several different sensors measuring the same things, where they in faultยญfree cases
could be fused to improve measurements, and in faulty cases would be separated from
the system, to ensure stability and longevity. From this, the scope could then be reduced
to either be focused on sensor fusion or fault detection. Localisation redundancy for fault
detection was chosen as the focus as it had the potential to improve the resilience of the
localisation.
The faults that could be expected in a navigation system on a ship are excessive noise,
sensor outages from loose cables, and violent weather. A more exotic one is proposed
by Nissov [3] is in the form of attacks to the GPS systems on board ships, by using fake
GPS towers to imitate satellites and using them to slowly turn the ships off course and
potentially into rocks or other hazards.
Neither violent weather nor fake satellite signals are expected in harbour, but it presents
the notion that the autonomous system should be able to detect disparities between difยญ
ferent sensors.
The system proposed in this thesis consists of a redundant system, with two groups of
sensors measuring the same ownยญship variables. One group is the GPS system and the
other consists of a combination of radars and IMUs. The redundancy of the measurements
is then used to detect any faults in the system.

2.2
Global positioning system (GPS)
The GPS is a positioning system maintained by the United States of Americaโs military.
A GPS system is made possible using a global network of satellites, broadcasting their
local time with a precision high enough to estimate the distance to them. By receiving 3
GPS signals, a system can theoretically estimate its position in space using triangulation,
if the position of the satellites and the distance to them are known.
Since the only way to estimate the distance using time measurements is if the receiver
has a clock synchronised with the broadcaster, a fourth signal is required to synchronise
the receiver clock.
The system broadcasts one publicly available carrier wave at 1575.2 MHz, called L1. The
data is 1023 bits broadcast at 1023 MHz, which enables synchronisation between the GPS
satellite and the receiver with a maximum difference of 1 millisecond. Approximately every
15 minutes, an โephemerisโ is broadcast, which is a precise description of the satellite
orbit, a requirement for localisation and synchronisation.
All of the required protocols and calculations are performed by specialised equipment,
and can sometimes be extended with additional software to try and improve the position
estimates by comparing newly received signals with previous ones, so that faulty meaยญ
surements can be detected and rejected.
2.3
Radars
Radar is an abbreviation of radio detection and ranging, and is used to describe systems
which uses radio waves to detect objects, the distance to them, and potentially their veยญ
locity. Radars are used to observe a large number of things, such as rain, aircraft, ships
and geography.
A radar system consists of a transmitter and a receiver, in some cases one antenna is
used for both purposes, and in some cases several receiver antennas are used. The
radar functions by emitting a radio wave, and then detects any returning deflections. The
distance to the object causing the deflection is then found from the flight time of the wave.
The size of and maximum distance to detectable objects is determined from the waveยญ
length used in the radar. The IEEE defines 13 frequency bands, which are shown in table
2.1.
The band of interest for this thesis is the Wยญband (75 โ110 GHz), which is often used for
autonomous vehicles and in meteorology for detecting small raindrops.

2.4
Inertial measurement unit (IMU)
An inertial measurement unit (IMU) is a device used to measure the forces acting upon
it, angular momentum and in most cases the electromagnetic field surrounding it. This is
achieved using a combination of accelerometers, gyroscopes and magnetometers.
An accelerometer measures the specific forces acting upon it. A stationary acceleromยญ
eter standing on planet Earth would measure the acceleration from the gravity, while an
accelerometer in free fall would not measure any acceleration.
All forces measured by the accelerometers are in the inertial frame, which is often closely
associated with the body frame of the vessel, since the sensor is often rigidly mounted in
the vessel.
The gyroscopes of the IMU measures angular velocity, which is often used in conjunction
with the other sensors to quickly determine the orientation of the IMU, and in turn, the
vessel.
The accelerometer and gyroscope sensors are all subject to noise and bias, where the
noise can be modelled as Gaussian, the bias is often constant within a timescale of 10 โ
60 min, enabling the bias to be modelled, such that,

In this chapter, a proposal for determining the ownยญship velocity will be presented in detail.
The two main components of the ownยญship velocity determination are the estimation and
the outlier removal. The intrinsics and tuning of both components will also be discussed.
The ownยญship velocity estimation is performed based on the doppler velocity estimated
by the radar. The ability to measure this property is unique for radars compared to other
methods of scanning the environment such as LiDAR and cameras.
The doppler effect is a result of a wave hitting an object with a different velocity than the
transmitter, resulting in a frequency change. When the effect is measureable, it can be
used to estimate the relative speed of the measured object. In the case of inยญharbour
navigation, a large portion of the measured objects are assumed to be stationary, and
thus the relative speed measured is a result of the motion of the ship, and the reverse
should then be the velocity of the ship. The concept is illustrated in figure 3.1, where it
can be seen how the measured relative speed of objects, noted as vr,n, is a function of
the objects angle from the radar, noted as ฮธn.
Figure 3.1: Figure showing how the radar measures the relative speed of objects (black
arrows), which is a projection of their relative velocity (blue arrows), which again comes
from the velocity of the ship (red arrow).
The algorithm for determining the ownยญship velocity has two main parts, sorting the meaยญ
surements into stationary and moving objects, and calculating a reliable velocity estimate
based on the stationary objects.

3.1
Linear velocity estimation
The automotive radar returns a collection of measurements on each radar scan, with
an estimate of the location of the reflecting surface, the relative radial speed, and the
strength of the returned signal. Since the speed measured by the radar is in relation to
the direction of measurement, a minimum of two measurements is required to find the
vessel velocity on a plane, and three for the velocity in 3D space. In an ideal case, where
all measurements are without fault and the ship is always level, with perfect mounting of
the sensors, two measurements are enough. With this in mind, the algorithm required will
have to find the best fit for the estimated velocity based on more than two measurements,
which all will have a variance in both position and reported relative velocity.
Several techniques for estimating ownยญship velocity using radar have been proposed by
other researchers, as was briefly mentioned in the literature review. One includes techยญ
niques found in computer vision, using Iterative Closest Point (ICP) algorithm to find the
pose transformation between each scan, and others use techniques found in existing
SLAM literature for LiDARs. Others use simpler techniques such as linear estimation or
just picking the measured velocities alike to what is expected. None of the found papers
shows any major gains from using highly advanced algorithms, so the one proposed in this
thesis is a linear fit on measurements where what is estimated to be wrong measurements
are sorted out.

Fault detection algorithm design
This chapter covers the design of a fault detection algorithm, with the main emphasis
being the preliminary statistical analysis required for creating it, as well as covering the
variables which requires tuning.
The main faultยญtolerance of the system stems from the ability to determine when a fault
occurs in the sensors.
Since multiple sensors in the system measure the velocity of the vessel, both the GPS and
the radar, a comparison of these estimated can be used to detect a fault in either of them.
To determine which of the systems is faulty using the proposed method, a third source of
velocity estimations would have to be provided. This is not seen as a major weakness, as
a detected fault can be communicated to either other autonomous systems on the vessel
or human personnel, which would then have the ability to either use alternative means of
navigation or determine which of the systems is faulty using external tools, and then turn
off or repair the faulty system.
Fault detection of the system is performed statistically using a generalised likelihood ratio
test (GLRT), which excel at detecting a change with an unknown magnitude. The test
statistic used is the residual found by directly comparing the velocity estimate provided by
the radar and the GPS, r = vradar โvGPS.
The goal of the algorithm is to determine if a collection of samples belongs to a statistical
distribution estimated to describe the system well, or raise a warning if the samples are
outside the estimated distribution.

which is any kind of positioning, and instead only an orientation estimate was performed.
This greatly increases the reliability of the experiment and obtaining the shown results
was made possible, but this is far from a complete solution.
A way to improve the reliability of any positioning could be done using existing SLAM
techniques, as in, [21], but it can be shown that sparse data is not optimal, and other
radar based SLAM methods are employing various techniques to increase the density
of the data [9], [14]. The data acquired in this experiment is still very sparse, even afยญ
ter compounding several seconds of data, as is shown in the results, figure 6.1. Any
reliable SLAM algorithm would require measurements which can be distinguished from
measurements taken at different positions, and without the background knowledge and
annotations which are provided in these results, recognising one of the point collections
as a car and the other as an unrelated nearby object is infeasible.
The result gained from this experiment is that this automotive radar could be used to
generate a rough map of an area, but it would not be detailed enough to reliably detect
medium to small objects.

6.3
Results of largeยญscale ownยญship velocity estimation
The route as recorded by the GPS, is shown in figure 6.3.
Using the developed velocity estimation method, the velocity was estimated using the
radar and compared to the GPS velocity it had varying results based on the type of outlier
rejection and noise reduction used.
The method used to estimate the correctness of the measurements was to generate a
residual from the two velocity measurements, r = vGPS โvradar. If the measurements
were ideal and without noise, this would be 0 at all timesteps. If only noise was present,
a mean near 0 is to be expected, but with a variance depending on the amount of noise.

For the table, it should be noted that the โrejectedโ column does not describe the amount

Different weight is put on the importance of the comparison metrics. The mean of the
residual is one of the highly weighted metrics, where a mean close to 0 would be considยญ

a high amount of extreme estimates, which is not desired. The variance should also be
kept relatively small, while the number of rejected points should be higher than using no
outlier detection, since no change signifies no measurements are being rejected.
The main difference between the two estimation methods shown here can be seen in the
mean and the variance, where the 3D method is closer to the GPS measurements than
the 2D method, but the 2D method shows a smaller variance. The estimation method
proposed as the best is the one operating in 3D. This method shows an average value
close to the expected one, while only showing slightly larger variances, which makes it
very easy to work with. In addition to this, by estimating the velocity in all three dimensions,
there are no requirements for the radar to be mounted perfectly vertical to ensure correct
measurements, which gives a lot of flexibility in implementation.
Comparing the individual methods, the result with no outlier rejection is used as a baseline.
Using RCS thresholding is immediately discarded, due to the obscene amount of rejecยญ
tions and extreme variance. Rejecting observations with a large distance to the mean of
all measurements is a viable outlier rejection method, with the added benefit compared
to the RANSAC method that it only has one tuning parameter and is very simple.
Performing a RANSAC with a sample size of 2 and a distance limit of 2 shows results
very close to those of the โdistance to meanโ method. Increasing the sample size of
the RANSAC method shows a smaller mean residual, but the increase in variance was
considered too large.
The method proposed is the RANSAC, since it is one of the two best methods and has
been used by others before, [14], [22]. The usage of the RANSAC method in the literature
is the main reason for the proposal of this over the โdistance to meanโ method, and an
implementation could reliably use either, as far as these results show.

In regards to the tuning of the GLRT variables, the main consideration is the window size,
where the main effect of increasing the window size is a longer detection time for faults.
In the context of marine transport, where distances and travel times are large, there is a
lot of room for testing data before any action is required, and as such, large window sizes
are generally acceptable.
The largest window size used here is 50 seconds, in which time, assuming the largest
tested step fault of 2m/s, the vehicle would have moved 100 meters off the planned route,
a negligible amount for a ship in open water. In inยญharbour situations, and if the technolยญ

an argument could be made that the window size should be decreased in those situaยญ

it provides a reliable deviation from the healthy case.

Conclusion
Performing faultยญtolerant inยญharbour localisation of marine surface vehicles was attempted
using GPS, radar and IMU systems.
The problem of localisation without using GPS was investigated, and it was found that
using an automative radar in combination with an IMU, only a low sparsity map could be
produced, which leads to the conclusion that automotive radars are not recommended
for mapping purposes. No conclusions are made in regards to the effectiveness of IMUs,
since the final result is too heavily influenced by the radar.
An increase in the fault tolerancy of the sensors was proposed using a generalised likeยญ
lihood ratio test, to detect faults in the existing localisation system, which uses the GPS.
The fault estimation was performed using the estimated velocity of the vehicle, found
using the radar compared to the velocity measured by the GPS system. The velocities
measured by the radar were shown to provide accurate results in the front facing direction
in smallยญscale testing, and results within 6 m/s in largeยญscale testing.
Using the estimated velocity for fault estimation, it was shown that step faults above 2 m/s
and ramp faults above 0.1 m/s2 could be detected within a reasonable time frame.
It is expected that in cases of GPS position faults, it will propagate to the velocity, resulting
in a functioning detection for several GPS faults.
In summary, localisation using nonยญGPS based methods was not possible, while providing
faultยญtolerancy to the existing localisation methods was possible.
