Abstract 
 
The aim of this project is to further contribute to the general understanding of narwhal 
(Monodon Monoceros) behaviour, while providing data for the energy expenditure 
estimation during movement. The narwhal is an understudied marine mammal,  with many 
of its swimming behaviours partly unknown. The wide use of compact, hydrodynamic 
technological devices attached to the bodies of marine mammals, has allowed researchers to 
study these animals even when they are submerged to great depths. In this project, two on-
animal tags where used to analyze speed and translate it to swimming behaviour: the 
AcousondeTM and the TDR10-DD-278TM tag. On the MatlabTM software environment, four 
different methods were formulated for speed estimation: speed from TDR10-DD-278TM, 
speed from depth, speed from acceleration and speed from sound. All methods focused on 
three selected dives and showed that narwhals travel with speeds of up to 6 [m/s] down to 
at least 900 meters deep. Each method was evaluated based on specific criteria defined by 
designer-client back-and-forth communication and was graded according to a concept scoring 
matrix created for this purpose. As the method speed from sound was the most preferable 
one with a score of 4.4, it was further analyzed and refined to estimate energy.  The energy 
estimate for each dive was calculated to be at around 656, 1492 and 881 kJ respectively. In 
conclusion, in respect to customer requirements satisfaction, all requirements have been 
fulfilled.

Chapter 1: Introduction 
 
The study of whales and dolphins is a challenging profession, as these animals often inhabit 
remote, harsh places and spend most of their lives swimming underwater. While ship-based 
observations can assist in counting numbers of individuals and study their behaviour, the rest 
of behavioural traits and patterns remain elusive or unknown, since they spend as much as 
95% of their time underwater and away from visual range. Luckily though for researchers, 
these animals use sound for locating food via echolocation, communication and other 
behaviours, therefore acoustic monitoring methods can provide useful data sets for study. 
In order to observe more extended spatial and temporal patterns of behaviours, researchers 
use technological devices, attached on the bodies of these animals, allowing them to record 
acoustic, positioning, movement and video data (Wiggins S. M., 2009). These tags and passive 
acoustic monitors, as they are called, allow for a  record of a lot of variables such as light 
intensity, pressure (depth), temperature, global positioning system (GPS). Moreover they are 
also equipped with multi-axis accelerometers to allow for monitoring of swimming dynamics, 
gyroscopes, hydrophones and other features. Some even allow real-time transmission of data 
over cabled or radio-linked hydrophones (although these hydrophones are not attached on 
the animal, they are instead hanging in the water column) (McDonald M. A., 2004).  
For longer periods of time researchers use typically acoustic monitoring. Monitoring both 
animal and potential anthropogenic sounds allows for establishment of response models 
(both ecological and behavioral). The passive acoustic monitoring tools can be described as 
more practical due to their ability to be remotely located worldwide, are cheaper and can 
unsupervised collect data (Sirovic et al., 2004). 
Since locomotion is an energy-demanding phenomenon, by using prolonged diving behaviour 
marine mammals are saving energy (relatively slow and steady swimming speeds, while 
utilizing changes between gliding and stroking gaits) (Skrovan et al., 1999, Williams et al. 2000, 
Tervo et al. in press). Their bodies glide through water with minimal effort due to the fully 
utilized propulsion energy created by said gait (Williams et al., 2000). As different rates in 
stroking translate into different energetic costs for an individual, alternations in stroking and 
swimming mode caused by disturbance yield an amount of excess energy expenditure 
(Williams et al., 2017a). To investigate the challenges imposed on narwhals, this project used 
compact, portable and hydrodynamic animal-borne tags that have been able to provide 
incredible amounts of new research on marine mammal behavior in the wild (Brown et al. 
2013; Tervo et al. in press). These tags are equipped with sensors that are able to collect a 
vast amount of data with sampling frequencies of hundreds to thousands of Hertz, providing 
a huge detail on the lives and behaviour of marine mammals. Even smaller animals, such as 
the narwhal, are now able to be tagged and studied with a combination of different tags (and 
sensors) in one deployment, supplying researchers with a more holistic view. 
For this project, the author coupled acoustic data on diving attempts with acceleration, depth 
and speed from animal-borne devices, with the aim of creating different ways to estimate 
their speed (and therefore power and energy).

Chapter 2: Problem definition 
2.1: Initial problem statement 
 
This project, as most project do, began when a client, Outi Tervo, Ph.D. (and by 
association, the Greenland Institute of Natural Resources) presented the author with 
a problem statement. The initial problem statement was expressed as follows: 
“Greenland Institute of Natural Resources is currently studying 
narwhals and their behaviour. That includes collecting data to 
study diet, communication, predation, stressors etc. We would 
like to know if speed can be reliably estimated using data from 
the two basic instruments part of our tagging scheme: GPS tag 
collecting positions or Acousonde collecting acoustics (flow 
noise), acceleration, direction (magnetometer) and depth-rate 
or a combination of these. Furthermore, we would like to receive 
an evaluation of which method is recommended. Being able to 
incorporate a new parameter like speed into the existing 
tagging scheme is cost efficient as well as better for the animal 
by minimizing both short- and long-term effects of tagging on 
an individual. It would also allow reanalysis of already existing 
data increasing the sample size substantially. “ 
Part of the client’s statement is an implied solution, and it is the client’s best guess at an 
answer. While implied solutions present a clear image, to the designer, on what the client is 
thinking, they can often limit the design space (and potential solutions). Initial problem 
statements must be refined so that errors, biases and implied solutions can be eliminated. 
 
2.2: Detailing Client’s Requirements 
 
After further analysis and consultation with the client the following requirements have 
been identified: 
 
1) Swimming energy estimate. When the narwhal swims through water, energy is 
dissipated due to drag on the narwhal’s body. The energy dissipated during swimming 
must be estimated. 
==PAGE:9==
 
3 | P a g e  
 
2) Precise energy estimate. The energy estimate must be free from bias (calibrated) and 
have a low uncertainty. As the swimming energy will be related to prey intake to 
determine energy balance, the precision must be on the order of the energy in a prey 
item. 
3) Reliable method. The cost of tagging, retrieving tag and post processing of data is very 
high. The method for energy estimation must therefore be reliable, to avoid failed 
tagging operations that results in no data. 
4) Easy calibration. The method should be easy to calibrate. Preferably once or in the 
field. 
5) Computational demand. The computational demand of the method should be low in 
order to limit requirements to post processing. 
6) Speed based estimate. The energy estimate must be based on an estimate of 
swimming speed. 
7) Long term measurement. The tagging process is very costly. It is therefore important 
that the tag can measure for a long time to  get as much data as possible from each 
tagging operation. 
8) AcousondeTM  tag must be used. The AcousondeTM tag is preferred by the client. At 
least in the short term this  tag should be used.

Chapter 3: General Background on Narwhals 
 
Narwhals are odontocete (or toothed) whales. Their most noteworthy characteristic is the 
spiraled tusk of the males (and rarely females), which can measure up to 3 meters in length. 
Six pairs of maxillaries and two pairs of mandibulary dental papillae are present in early 
narwhal embryos, but only two maxillary pairs persist and develop. Of these, the two anterior 
teeth will develop into an elongated tooth, which is the start of the tusk. The other two teeth 
remain vestigial. The left of the two elongated teeth grows and protrudes through the 
maxillary bones and the rostrum skin of the male whale. While the tusk is growing, it will spiral 
in the left direction. However, irregularities in the development of tusks are frequently seen; 
females sometimes have a small tusk, males occasionally have no tusk, and narwhals with two 
tusks, so-called “double tuskers,” are rare. While the tusks are an obvious secondary sexual 
characteristic, other functions have also been suggested such as salinity or water temperature 
examination (Nweeia et al., 2014). But since both females and males are thriving with or 
without a tusk, its sensory functions are not a necessity for survival. As per observations, 
narwhals do not utilize their tusks for fighting or similar aggressive behaviour. The main 
reason the narwhal remained a legendary animal for so long could potentially be due to its 
preference for isolated and inaccessible environments, usually in deep water areas, covered 
by heavy pack ice during the dark winter months.  
While polar cod (Boreogadus saida) and squid are often found in their stomachs, dietary 
studies indicate that narwhals do not feed a lot during the summer. Nonetheless, they feed 
primarily on Gonatus squid (including Gonatus fabricii), as well as on Greenland halibut 
(Reinhardtius hippoglossoides) during late fall and winter (Laidre and Heide-Jørgensen, 2005). 
Stable isotopes studies of stomach contents confirm that narwhals feed on a limited variety 
of prey. By examining stomach contents, the prey selection and feeding behaviour of 
narwhals has been studied between Canada and Greenland. Summer feeding activity records 
indicate little to no evidence of recent feeding, while samples collected in late fall and winter 
pointed out substantial levels of undigested material (providing evidence of recent feeding). 
During all seasons and locations, Gonatus squid (Gonatus fabricii mainly) remains were 
identified in stomachs, while Arctic (Arctogadus glacialis) and polar cod (Boreogadus saida) 
==PAGE:11==
 
5 | P a g e  
 
were occasionally detected during late fall and winter. Furthermore, the lipid-rich Greenland 
halibut (Reinhardtius hippoglossoides) was a considerable dietary part of narwhals (during 
winter) and was sometimes the only identifiable species in a full stomach (Laidre and Heide- 
Jørgensen, 2005). 
While for all animals foraging is crucial, the majority of studies have focused on terrestrial 
animals, as they are more easily manageable than marine mammals when it comes to 
studying predator-prey interactions (Scheel, 1993). Nevertheless, a rise in multisensor data-
logging devices have made it possible to study various marine species and their foraging 
behaviour (Chappell et al., 1993). 
Narwhals are regularly observed in small groupings of 5 to 10 whales. Up to hundreds of 
individuals can sometimes form larger herds. Males usually reside in separate teams, while 
females and calves are travelling with immature males (Marcoux et al., 2009). In the 
summertime, coastal habitats in many areas are occupied by narwhals and dive depth show 
a tendency  to  be  more  than  500  meters.  During  autumn,  some  populations  migrate  to 
wintering grounds in deeper waters, where they make 5–25 dives/day to depths exceeding 
800 meters, possibly in search of deep-water prey. Narwhals are positioned among the 
deepest diving marine mammals since their dive records indicate depths of more than 1500 
meters. It has been estimated that the average aerobic dive limitation of narwhals is twenty- 
two minutes, while the observed dives are most frequently below this limit; There are 
exceptions of dives exceeding thirty minutes (Laidre et al., 2003). 
In this project, a general energetic model was developed in an attempt to quantify the 
energetic needs of individuals per dive. Factors like growth multiplier, reproduction costs or 
digestive efficiency have not been taken into consideration. Instead this project analyses the 
energy spent by the recorded animal by calculating its diving speed and translating it into 
swimming energy.

Chapter 5: Description of tags and data 
5.1: AcousondeTM 3B tag 
 
The Acousonde™ 3B is a battery operated acoustic recorder, able to be full submerged 
underwater, consisting of one or two hydrophones, along with sensors for attitude, 
orientation, depth and temperature measurement, a digital recorder, and a field-replaceable 
battery. By being attached to an animal subject with the included suction cups (in the case of 
“Jonas” with additional harpoons), the tag is able to measure the body’s sound environment 
and vocal activity. The tag is additionally equipped with an accelerometer and a

available to all Acousonde units that released after 2011, if they use axis conventions. When

Chapter 9: Concept selection 
 
In this chapter we evaluate design concepts to determine if there are some worth realizing. 
With, now, four different design alternatives, we have to choose which one is the most 
preferable based on the criteria. This criteria or metrics will assist us in examining how close 
we are in meeting the client’s objectives. 
Before establishing those metrics, we need to address three potential issues that exist in this 
process. To begin with, these metrics should be limited to the client’s most important 
objectives. Secondly, the metrics we set must be as logical as possible to avoid over- or under- 
focus some of the results. Finally, the information on the examination of metrics includes 
some level of subjectivity, so they must be viewed as a guide and not as a numerical solution. 
Below we will introduce the concept scoring matrix method. This method uses a weighted 
sum of the ratings to determine concept ranking. Each criterion is assigned to a weight 
percentage that is analogous to their importance. The preferred design will be that with the 
highest score and will be chosen as the best solution.

Chapter 12: Discussion 
 
This project begun with the idea that two sensors attached on a narwhal would produce data 
suitable for the study of their swimming behaviour. And to a certain degree this has been 
achieved in various ways. While we managed to estimate speed, power and energy in one 
way, the rest of the data (light, temperature, magnetometer, etc) are not very useful either 
because of low sampling rates, or because they cannot effectively be related to speed.  
A very important sensor is missing from the AcousondeTM: a ring laser gyroscope. First things 
first, a  ring laser gyroscope is a device that is comprised of a ring laser. The ring laser consists 
of two independent resonant modes (counter propagating) in the same path. It detects 
rotation by operating in two different frequencies, one for each mode respectively (Macek 
and Davis, 1963). While both the accelerometer and gyroscope are used widely in various 
fields, they have one main difference. The gyroscope can sense rotation, where the 
accelerometer cannot. For example, when accelerating in a certain direction, the 
accelerometer is not able to differentiate between that and  arth’s gravitational pull. The 
gyroscope on the other hand is able to remain effective with its ability to measure the 
rotation’s rate around an axis. A gyroscope sensor would be able to provide with more 
accurate data regarding the animal’s movement, while it would also have assisted in 
calibrating the acceleration data. 
Another important factor for future consideration is how the drag coefficient value (CD) varies 
with depth. CD is not constant because it varies with various functions such as flow speed and 
direction, body position and size, fluid density and viscosity, while it can also be a function of 
the Reynolds number (a dimensionless quantity). CD is also influenced at the surface where  
the animal drags a wave (Clancy L.J., 1975). Therefore, an accompanying formula embedded 
into a software able to calculate CD in various depths, according to the sampling rate of the 
sensors, would be a very useful tool.

Moreover, this project was one of the early “victims” of the SARS-CoV-2 pandemic of 2020, 
and couldn’t go through with a calibration experiment. The early design plan of the project 
included a calibration experiment using a tow tank, but was abandoned due to the second 
lockdown of Denmark that started on early November 2020. During this experiment, we 
would have attempted to calibrate both sensors using a narwhal model, in various speeds 
simulating the swimming conditions of the animal in low depths. Such calibration would be 
useful for the  three out of four design alternatives, excluding the speed from sensor. 
Last but not least, a very useful tool would have been the Kalman filter, which is an algorithm 
that utilizes series of measurements (collected over time) and creates estimates of unknown 
variables that have the tendency to be more accurate than those supported by single 
measurements only. It operates by estimate a joint probability distribution over the variable 
for each separate timeframe. The Kalman filter is very useful in the field of guidance, 
navigation and control, a field under which this project is included at (Zarchan and Musoff, 
2000).
