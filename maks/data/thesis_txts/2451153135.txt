Abstract 
In this thesis, operational rules for a partially automated warehouse are set up. First, 
traditional warehouses and warehouse performance are studied as a groundwork for the 
study of the warehouse. This leads to the description of all the specificities of the 
processes of the automated warehouse. Thereafter a method to determine relevant KPIs 
to manage the warehouse is applied. In the third chapter, the warehouse is modeled in 
order to link the demand to the human resource and the equipment needed, by optimizing 
the utilization of those resources. This result constitutes a useful tool to adapt the 
resources quantities during the ramp-up phase. Finally, an optimization problem is 
formulated and solved to determine the leaving times of the delivery trucks. These data 
are used as inputs to decide the organization of the warehouse’s activities in term of team 
shifts.  
Keywords:  
warehouse; model; process; optimization; shift; ramp-up

Introduction 
Launching a new activity is a particularly critical phase for any company. In order to stay 
competitive, Groupe Casino, a huge actor of the retail sector in France, wishes to expand 
its e-commerce activity, namely the sale of grocery products through a website. A new 
business unit is being created to host this new activity, as part of the company Groupe 
Casino. The novelty of the activity is characterized by the fact that the product baskets will 
be prepared by robots in a large-scale automated warehouse. Then the baskets will be 
delivered to the customers by vans. The study case is located in France, and such a 
warehouse currently doesn’t exist in France. 
The purpose of this thesis is to study the logistics part taking place inside the warehouse 
and especially the operational rules which need to be set up. As part of launching a new 
business, any logistics study conducted will need to take into consideration the 
particularities of a ramp-up phase, i.e. a phase where the demand grows rapidly. In the 
case of a warehouse, optimizing the operational rules involves a combination of several 
tasks. As the scope of this thesis cannot contain all these tasks, some of them have been 
discarded, such as training and communication considerations. The focus was narrowed 
down to design of operational processes, performance management and resource-linked 
modelizations. First, the processes of all the departments will be decided and mapped: 
reception of products, preparation, pick up, quality control, shipping … The way the 
performance of the warehouse can be assessed will also be discussed. Finally, the size 
and the structure of the organizations will be set up in link with the evolving volume of the 
demand, due to the ramp-up phase. Both human and material resources will be examined. 
All the aspects of this problem will be investigated with the aim of setting the operational 
rules of the new warehouse. 
This thesis is structured as follows. First, the company hosting the case study is described 
in detail in order to explain the context in which the automated warehouse project is 
developed. Then, the methodology followed to collect the data and conduct the study is 
discussed. Thereafter, Chapter 3 gives an overview of the literature concerning 
warehouses, automation and performance. Chapter 4 constitutes the main part of this 
thesis as it summarizes the analyses of the problem and the results; The operational rules 
set up are explained. Chapter 5 gives recommendations to the company Groupe Casino 
and suggests further studies. Chapter 6 discusses the limits of the results.

Chapter 2. 
 
 
Methodology 
This masterthesis is interested in Ocommerce’s future warehouse. The methodology 
chosen is to do an internship in Ocommerce structure to get access to the data needed. 
Practical cases have been given by Ocommerce for the author to solve. They all concern 
the functioning of the logistics of the warehouse. Within this company, several 
methodologies have been followed depending on the purposes.  
The methodology to write the processes is first to conduct a broad literature review 
concerning warehouses to understand the basic principles of the warehouses’ processes. 
Documents from the logistics subsidiary Easydis from Groupe Casino have also been 
studied. Then an internal review of the literature concerning the processes of the existing 
automated warehouses have been made: most of them are confidential documents given 
by Ocado.  Then the first drafts of all the processes have been drawn. For this purpose, a 
template has been created: the goal is that the formalization is easy to understand so that 
a new employee could be trained very quickly. For the next step, some workshops with 
the project team have been organized to gather questions to ask Ocado on the field. The 
outcome of this phase is a so-called “Processes Book” with all the sequences drawn, 
questions, and information such as the identified pieces of equipment or the way the waste 
would be recycled. Then a team has been sent to visit one of Ocado’s CFC. The author 
personally didn’t get the chance to visit the warehouse as Groupe Casino wouldn’t allow 
it. However, after the visit, other workshops have been organized to summarize all the 
data gathered and adjust the processes written. The results of this methodology are the 
drawn sequences and the list of pieces of equipment that can be found in this 
masterthesis. Later, when the warehouse is built and the stations ready for use, tests will 
be made to try out the processes and further adjustments will probably be made. As soon 
as the first orders are picked, the employees will be asked to give feedback to improve 
the processes drawn. Every day, the teams will gather together and the employees will 
have the opportunity to express any remark they would like to share and any improvement 
they have pictured. 
Once the processes are written, the question of how they will be evaluated once set up is 
important to answer. The methodology chosen here is to look for a consistent framework 
in the existing literature. Wishes of the project team in term of operational KPIs have also 
been collected. Then the performance prism framework has been applied in order to 
develop relevant KPIs. These KPIs are kept in mind for further modeling.

Writing the processes also gives an insight into the functioning of the warehouse and 
enables to build consistent models for the warehouse. For the modeling and sizing of the 
organization of the warehouse, a model of the warehouse has been created and approved 
by the team members, whereas key metrics have been collected from Ocado. It has been 
tried to link all the quantities of resources to a number of orders per week so that the model 
can be applied for any forecast of the demand and thus can be used in a ramp-up phase. 
In order to size human resources, Ocado’s organization has been taken as a basis. For 
the equipment’s sizing, Ocado’s way of working has been observed, and workshops have 
been conducted to evaluate the missing items. The amounts of pieces of equipment have 
been sized related to the design of the areas or to the number of people working for a 
specific task. All these methods enable to evaluate the warehouse’s needs, which can be 
translated later into a recruitment plan and a material order schedule. After having sized 
generally the human resources needed, their organization in the warehouse has been 
studied in further details. A model of the successions of activities in the warehouse has 
been created. This model highlights the fact that other data were needed: the historical 
data from Monoprix in terms of delivery slots have been collected and hypotheses about 
the delivery slots offered by the future warehouse have been made. In order to create a 
valid hypothesis, a linear optimization problem has been solved.

Chapter 3. 
 
 
Literature review 
3.1. Warehouses 
3.1.1. Definition of warehouse 
According to the English dictionary (Lexico dictionary, 2019), a warehouse is “A large 
building where raw materials or manufactured goods may be stored prior to their 
distribution for sale”. It is a space dedicated to the storage of goods.  
Warehouses can be integrated into the value chain of a company in different ways, 
depending on the type of the company, as Emmett explains (2005): “In supply driven 
chain, warehouses hold stocks to feed internal department such as manufacturing and 
production. In demand-driven chain, warehouses store goods to fulfill the needs of 
external customers. “ 
3.1.2. Processes 
Frazelle model (2016) divides warehouses into five different activities: Receiving, Put 
Away, Storage, Order picking and shipping. Each activity is described in different tasks on 
the following image:  
 
Figure 1: Frazelle model 
==PAGE:20==
 8 
The different activities of a traditional warehouse are the following:  
• Receiving occurs when a transporter delivers goods from the suppliers. First, 
this activity needs to be scheduled with the transporter. When the truck arrives, 
the goods need to be inspected: do they correspond to the purchase order? Is 
there any damage? Did the transporter respect the cool chain? Then the goods 
are unloaded.  
• Putaway: the goods are identified by a product ID, and their storage location in 
the warehouse is also identified. A record of the stock is updated.  
• Storage: The products are moved to its storage location. Employees are often 
helped by Material Handling Equipment (MHE) such as forklifts.  
• Order picking: When the warehouse receives the order, an employee is 
designated to pick the order. In a traditional warehouse, the employee would 
walk through the warehouse according to a predefined journey. 
• Shipping: Upstream from the order picking, the deliveries are planned. Once 
they have been picked and brought to the outbound dock, the orders are packed 
and labeled. They are loaded into the delivery trucks. Then they are delivered. 
Focus on order picking 
“The objective of order picking is to collect items from warehouse to meet the requirement 
of various picking orders. It is one of the largest expenses in most warehouse Operations” 
says C.-T. Su (1995). Order picking requires a lot of operators and thus causes important 
labor costs. Due to that, the optimization of this process has been studied in many articles

3.1.3. Complexity 
A great part of retailers’ sales come from alimentary products. Selling food require special 
precaution and is a job in itself. R. Koster (2002) describes food as a complex product, 
meaning that they are “complex with respect to storage, handling, transportation or 
delivery.” In France especially, the assortment width of retailers is very large. A large 
assortment of complex products is for R. Koster (2002) a complex assortment. This 
complexity explains for instance why Amazon entered the food market in France only 
recently.  
3.1.4. Warehouses for retail 
Retailers in France 
Retailers are the companies which sell goods to the ultimate customers, usually in small 
quantities, according to the English dictionary ((Lexico dictionary, 2019)). They buy goods 
in important quantities to producers or wholesalers and then they sell them to personal 
customers.  
Grocery shops start their development in the 19th century. In the beginning, the main 
actors are isolated shops. In 1948, the first self-service shop opens in France. In the 
1960s, the consumption of French households increases rapidly, which transforms the 
market and create an enabling environment for larger companies. The first supermarket 
opens in 1957 in France. In the 1960s, the activity shifts from business activity to a 
distribution activity. Between 1970 and 2000, the role of retailers has known few changes. 
The new trends, namely e-commerce, and automation, are the latest changes.  
J. Fernie and L. Sparks (2018) give us some insight into the evolution of the role of retailers 
: “Retailers were once effectively the passive recipients of products, allocated to stores by 
manufacturers in anticipation of demand. Today, retailers are the active designers and 
controllers of product supply in reaction to known customer demand.” Logistics imply huge 
cost for retailers. It’s thus a key factor of their success. J. Fernie and L. Sparks explain 
==PAGE:23==
 11 
that retailers strive to reduce their inventory and to improve their efficiency. One example 
mentioned is the “composite distribution’, which is defined by “the distribution of mixed 
temperature items through the same distribution center and on the same vehicle.” It allows 
saving money on a single delivery, by being able to mutualize means.  
Small distribution warehouses VS big distribution warehouses 
When it comes to distribution warehouses, an important question to answer is the size 
and the number of distribution warehouses needed to maximize the profit.  
Having a great number of small warehouses could be seen as beneficial as it allows the 
company to offer local services, some might say that it should maximize the use of driver’s 
day and that multiplicating buffer stock would increase the level of service to the customer. 
However, carefully considering all these allegations by Bevington (1979), big warehouses 
are more beneficial than small warehouses. Firstable, Bevington explains that a company 
can easily be seen as local without having a local warehouse. It’s more a question of 
marketing than a question of localization of the warehouse. Secondly, the use of driver’s 
time is analyzed in the article, and it shows that in general, the driving time of a driver is 
only one-third of its total working time, inefficient time represents approximately 20% of 
the total working time. The writer concludes that it would be possible for the driver to 
deliver more orders (for instance by having a bigger truck), but not to deliver entirely a 
second route.  
Concerning the buffer stock, having one safety stock in a big warehouse instead of having 
individual safety stocks in small warehouses, enables to achieve a better customer service 
for the same total level of stock.  
Finally, the approach of Tom Bevington is to compare economically the solutions of a lot 
of small warehouses or few big warehouses, he concludes that few big warehouses are 
definitely the best choice from an economic point of view.

3.2.1. Definition 
According to the English dictionary, automation is “the use or introduction of automatic 
equipment in a manufacturing or other process or facility.” ((Lexico dictionary, 2019). 
Usually, operations formerly carried out by humans are now performed by machines or

3.2.2. Trends 
The growth in the quantity of items transactions increases the need for automation in 
logistics, according to J. Wang et al. (2009). The integration of information systems can 
improve drastically the efficiency of the logistics chain. Moreover, the expansion of e-
commerce has changed the traditional concept of trade. According to J. Wang et al., it has 
provided the distribution centers an “unprecedented opportunity for the development of 
logistics automation”. They explain however that the development of e-commerce has 
been slowed by the existing logistics, which is long to evolve because it requires huge 
investment and change of habits.  
Many articles testify of the expansion of automation, by studying specific technologies 
which have been in warehouses. Automation is an interesting topic for a long time already:  
for instance, Kapoor et al. presents in 1987 an automated warehouse with mobile robots. 
The movements of the robots enable the stock to be updated automatically. Other articles 
present technologies to help the employees to work operational tasks more efficiently, X. 
Yang et al. (2016) describes head-mounted devices using virtual and augmented reality 
technologies.  
Despite this apparently unstoppable growth of automation, X. Yang et al. (2016). 
recommend evaluating the production benefit versus the implementation cost before 
shifting toward automated technologies. Automated systems allow to save time, save cost 
and to improve reliability. However, the implementation costs of automated systems are 
often huge and thus, the benefit and the cost need to be balanced. Among the 
implementation costs, need to be taken into consideration the setup costs and the training 
cost.  
3.2.3. The emergence of e-commerce 
E-commerce involves a need for extra services: home delivery and picking of products by 
employees, in comparison to regular sales in a shop. E-commerce has made new 
activities to enter the retail world. R. De Koster explains that one of the main issues of an 
e-commerce company is the delivery of goods. Delivery is a very difficult activity, 
especially in big cities, and customers are demanding.  
When new e-commerce activity arises, the solution which involves less investment for a 
retailer is to pick the order in the store. However, several studies, including the article of 
R. De Koster shows it is not profitable in comparison to picking orders in a warehouse. 
Picking in a store is poorly efficient and involves high labor costs per order, whereas order 
picking in a warehouse can be mutualized and allows economies of scale. However, the 
volume of activity must be sufficiently high to justify the opening of a warehouse and

3.2.4. Robotics 
Human-robot cooperation 
Human-Robot cooperation is a challenge for employees working with robots. According to 
G. Charalambous et al. (2016), operators and robots collaborate better when the operator 
trusts the robot. For this purpose, the operator needs to understand the functioning of the 
robot. The operator needs to receive special training to understand “the robot’s key 
characteristics identiﬁed in the trust scale”, i.e.  “perceived safety, perceived reliability and 
robot motion, and pick-up speed”. After this process, the operator is empowered, and able 
to react appropriately and efficiently to the behavior of the robot. He or she is able to 
understand the causes of the events that occur related to the robots. G. Charalambous et 
al. call it developing a more realistic “mental model” of the robot.  
==PAGE:26==
 14 
Example of the introduction of new technologies 
The logistics chain of the retail sector is subject to the intensification of flows. It impacts 
all the activities in the value chain from cashiers to drivers. The information flow goes 
faster and faster and humans have to adapt. Cashiers and drivers are known by the 
general public. The logistics chain acting upstream is less known, but as well impacted by 
the increasing speed. The job of order pickers in warehouses is not an exception. Usually, 
the order picking operations are guided by warning lights. But some articles recommend 
using hands-free order selection processes. For instance, some new technologies have 
been implemented to increase the productivity of order picking. One of those is voice 
picking. It consists of providing the picker with a microphone. Picking instructions are then 
communicated to the picker orally. The picker has to validate every item picked by telling 
how much items he has put in the bag. The productivity by using this technology increases 
between 10% and 15% according to Govaere (2009). It can be explained by the fact that 
the eyes are not anymore used to read information, and the hands don’t have to validate 
operations anymore. This system is often associated with the instauration of productivity 
bonus, which encourages the employee to be more productive. 
3.3. Performance  
3.3.1. Definition 
According to the business dictionary (Online Business Dictionary, 2019), performance is 
defined as “The accomplishment of a given task measured against preset known 
standards of accuracy, completeness, cost, and speed”.  
It is interesting to look for a definition of performance management, which has been 
studied many times in the literature. Mentioned in the Glossary of Performance Terms 
(Cranfield School of Management), performance management involves “understanding 
and acting on performance issues at each level of your organization; from individuals, 
teams and directorates through the organization itself”.  
3.3.2. Methods 
A. D. Marco and G. Mangano (2011) teach us that: “Good warehouse performance will 
improve quality performance, delivery time, customer satisfaction and reduce cost in 
logistics system” 
Most companies use frameworks to develop their performance management strategy. The 
Balanced Scorecard has been widely used for this purpose. The Balanced Scorecard 
“suggests that we view the organization from four perspectives, and to develop objectives, 
==PAGE:27==
 15 
measures (KPIs), targets, and initiatives (actions) relative to each of these points of view” 
according to the balancescorecard website: financial aspect, customer point of view, 
internal processes and organizational capacity.  
Criticisms are formulated against the Balanced Scorecard framework, the main ones are 
the absence of consideration for the needs and wants of all the stakeholders and the lack 
of theory behind the model.  
Cranfield University has developed a framework called the Performance Prism inspired by 
the Balanced Scorecard, which includes better the needs and wants of the stakeholders. 
The starting point of the reflection shifts from "What is the organization's strategy?" to 
"Who are the organization's key stakeholders and what do they want and need?”. Further, 
in this study, the detailed methodology explained by Neely & al (2001) will be followed 
step by step. 
Most of the frameworks developed rely on Key Performance Indicators. These KPIs are 
“succinct measures that aim to describe as much as possible about a system in a minimum 
amount of points.” (Lecture 8 of Course 42371; DTU). 
The KPIs, also called indicators, have thus a key role for understanding, for performance 
and for accountability of a warehouse. As recommended for the first time in the book of 
G. Doran (1981) and many times since then, the KPIs should follow the SMART principles.  
It means that a good KPI should be : 
• Specific: it must be linked to the work of the employee who must achieve the 
objective 
• Measurable: it should quantify or qualify the objective 
• Acceptable: all participants should agree upon it  
• Realistic: the level demanded should encourage most of the employees and not 
frighten them 
• Time-bound: it should be delimited in time  
An important question to answer is: what should the KPIs measure? For many years, only 
the financial performance indicators were watched. But, Thomas Johnson and Robert 
Kaplan (1987) advocate that much information misses in order to manage a business with 
only financial measures. All the areas of performance that are important to the 
organization’s success should, therefore, be represented in the KPIs.  
==PAGE:28==
 16 
Specific performance management for warehouses has been studied many times. 
According to Staudt et al. (2015), all quantitative indicators applying to a warehouse can 
be classified among four categories commonly used: time, quality, productivity, and cost.  
The philosophy of E. H. Frazelle’s book (2016) in term of performance management is to 
set up objectives for warehouses, then to translate these objectives in KPIs. E. H. Frazelle 
considers that the objectives of a warehouse are to serve its employees, its customers, 
and its shareholders. Furthermore, an approach based on stakeholders enables alignment 
of traditional organizational strategies with social and ethical issues as explains Svendsen 
(1998). 
A warehouse should, therefore, provide the employees with a great place to work. First, it 
should be a safe place. Then, cross-functionality should be encouraged by training a 
maximum of employees to avoid boredom and turnover and to be more resilient to the 
variation of the demand. The operator-to-supervisor ratio should be reasonable: the book 
suggests between 8 and 12 employees for a supervisor. Finally, the retention of 
employees should be observed.  
In terms of serving customers, customers want orders to be accurate, on-time and in short 
cycle-time. The accuracy of each warehouse activity can be monitored. Damaged 
products delivery should also be tracked. On-time should be measured at different points 
of the process: supplier delivery, ready to be delivered to customers, and delivery to 
customers. The cycle time can also be measured and divided into subcycle times: inbound 
cycle time (from the inbound dock to stock) and outbound cycle time (from stock to 
outbound dock).  
The shareholders-facing KPIs won’t be mentionned as this is not directly related to the 
topic of this thesis.

4.3. Performance 
All the processes written precedingly will be evaluated regarding their performance. For 
this reason, the definition of performance for the warehouse studied needs to be set up 
and key indicators need to be chosen. 
4.3.1. Performance of the warehouse 
Inspired by Frazelle’s model, the methodology chosen is to put oneself into the shoes of 
all the stakeholders of the value chain. The framework of the Performance Prism (see 
Literature review Chapter 3.3.2) is used as a method to determine how to evaluate the 
performance of the warehouse. The methodology is explained in the article written by 
Neely et al. (2001). The result of this method applied to the present case can be read in 
Appendix B. The methodology used is explained in this chapter.  
The first step is to identify the main stakeholders. Let’s remind ourselves that we are 
interested only in the operational objectives of the warehouse and not in the commercial 
success of the overall activity.  
Having that in mind, the main stakeholders are :  
• the customers 
• the logistics operators 
• the logistics management 
• the suppliers 
For each stakeholder, the following questions are going to be answered:  
1. What do they want and need? 
2. What are the strategies Ocommerce requires to ensure the wants and 
needs of the stakeholders are satisfied? 
3. What are the processes Ocommerce has to put in place in order to 
allow the strategies to be delivered? 
4. What are the capabilities Ocommerce requires to operate its 
processes? 
5. What does Ocommerce expect from the stakeholder in return? This 
step is not explained in the text but only appears in Appendix B.

4.3.2. Performance management 
Once the processes are set up in the warehouse, their performance can be evaluated by 
using the KPIs found out in the precedent section. Typical performance management 
would include setting targets, meet regularly to compare the results to the targets and take 
actions to get closer to the objective.  
However, it has to be noticed that some performance indicators evolve in opposite 
directions. Improving a KPI might sometimes worsen another one.  
Some KPIs can apply directly to the processes. For instance, task productivity is the 
productivity for one process. However, the productivity of a task can affect another task. 
For instance, the decant activity and the picking activity are sometimes opposite. To 
increase decant productivity, one wishes to let as much package as possible. Whereas 
picking productivity is increased when the decant employees have removed as much 
package as possible. Both the global KPIs and the detailed ones are needed to take a 
good decision. In another category, reducing cost would often not be beneficial for the 
employees and thus decrease their job satisfaction, if it asks for them to work faster, for 
instance. Here again, a positive action for a KPI could be negative for another one. 
When looking at indicators, their importance in the global value chain should also be 
considered. The global productivity is an aggregation of several tasks’ productivities. 
When trying to improve global productivity, the impact of the subcomponents has to be 
analyzed. It would appear that some tasks are not worth improvement because they have 
almost no impact. It would at least give a priority order between the different improvement 
tasks which could be carried out.  
In the next sections, modelizations are developed in order to optimize the warehouse 
organization. The methods used aims to be performant, and thus to have the best possible 
score in terms of the KPIs described in this section, by respecting the constraints given 
(of the warehouse design, of the law, etc.). The goal of the resource optimizations 
proposed is to deliver the orders promised in time, with accurate contents, by using as few 
resources as possible but by maximizing the utilization of the resources engaged.

4.4.2. Organizing human resources 
Preliminary optimization problems 
The computations of Chapter 4.4.1 have given an insight into the number of FTE needed 
to make the warehouse work. However, this need is not equally divided between all the 
hours of a week. The activities of the warehouse are orchestrated by the delivery slots 
chosen by the customers and the delivery made by the suppliers.  
The warehouse of study is open 24 hours a day, seven days a week. 
Some activities are pulled by the delivery time of the customer:  
• picking of ambient, chill and frozen products 
• shipping 
• reverse flow activities are also considered as an activity pulled by the departure 
of the truck: this activity will indeed take place just before the next loading of the 
truck, to improve efficiency and limit the movement of trucks. 
Some other activities are pushed by the flow of goods received: 
• receiving activities 
• decant of ambient, chill and frozen goods 
Two different types of input data are thus needed to model the productive hours needed:  
• from the Supply Chain department: when are the goods received during a day 
and during a week? 
• from the Transport department and the Offer department, also named 
Commercial department: what are the slots defined for the customer orders, 
how is the demand divided among the time slots?  
After a discussion with the Supply Chain department, the distribution of the receptions is 
modeled by a uniform distribution between 3 AM and midnight every day of the week, 
except on Sunday. There are no suppliers’ deliveries on Sunday. The representation of 
this distribution can be read in Figure 64 and 65.

Chapter 5. 
 
 
Recommendations 
In this Chapter are explained change which are recommended to Groupe Casino and 
further studies that could follow this thesis.  
5.1. Processes of the automated warehouse 
Sequences describing the processes of the warehouse have been written by respecting 
the constraints given by the design of the warehouse. However, some improvements could 
be made to the sequences of action by changing the design of the working stations and 
the area. 
5.1.1. Receiving and Decant :  
As often, the contingencies are not integrated into the regular processes, as it can be seen 
in Figure A1. Any anomaly requires the use of a paper form. Moreover, the non-conforming 
products are not tracked by the system as they don’t match any known electronic situation. 
Currently, any missing parameter (it could be weight, dimensions, the method to open the 
package, the method to pick the product) would require for the operator to put the 
incriminated products on the side of the decant station. The products cannot be integrated 
into the hive and cannot go automatically to the area where the problem should be solved. 
I recommend that arrangements are made to allow the products to enter the hive even if 
a piece of information is missing, but that the bins with non-conforming products are sent 
automatically to an inventory management station, where the problem should be handled.  
Furthermore, all non-conformity should be declared on the field by using the hand device. 
This would require developing an additional menu on the device. The locations of these 
non-conforming products should be tracked. 
Some decant stations should be equipped with material to carry heavy products such as 
liquid. However, this implies that these specific decant stations are mapped into the 
system and that some pallets can be directed to the stations, so it isn’t only a question of 
equipment. Elevating tables are suggested to be used at 20% of the decant stations. 
 
==PAGE:112==
 100 
5.1.2. Order-picking 
Currently, the goods-to-man principle is applied, but the automation could go further by 
developing a robot to pick the products to avoid that this repetitive task is perpetrated by 
humans. Ocado currently works on such a solution8. Research and development in this 
field should be carried out. The existing solutions are quite slow for the moment. In the 
retail sector, the lead time between the order validation by the customer and the order 
shipping is key, that’s why such a solution has not been implemented yet.  
5.1.3. Shipping preparation & Shipping 
Shipping preparation and shipping are considered to be efficient as the frames used in the 
warehouse to move the totes are also put in the vehicles, which avoids handling 
operations. 
5.1.4. Reverse flow 
More conveyors could be used in the Bagging process. Currently, some tote moves are 
done manually, the operator has to squat down then to pick himself/herself up in order to 
put the totes on the high racking. As this is a tiring move, it is recommended that the 
operators don’t stay doing this activity more than 1 hour. There should be a frequent 
rotation. I recommend that some adaptations of this job station are made for ergonomics 
reason.  
Currently, to wash a storage bin, an operator has to call for the storage bin at an inventory 
management station. Then the bin is extracted from the system, put on a trolley and 
brought to the Washing area. Conveyors could also be added there to move the totes.  
 
Later in the project, all the processes will be tested in real working condition situation. For 
now, the stations are not installed in the warehouse and so the processes cannot be tried 
out yet. During this testing, it is suggested to make adjustments to the processes which 
have been written. It is also recommended to take notes of any discomfort that could 
appear during the work, to study further if improvements can be made on these topics.  
Moreover, especially during the first weeks, operators should be asked for feedback and 
workshops should be conducted to improve the working conditions of the employees.

5.2. Performance 
The method followed results in the first set of Key Performance Indicators which takes into 
consideration the point of view of different stakeholders. It is recommended that when the 
Management of the warehouse will have been hired, they take part in workshops on this 
thematic. The indicators have to become reports which will be used and become part of 
the routine of the warehouse. For each process, the most relevant KPIs among the list 
should be selected and used in order to pilot this process.  
5.3. Modelization of the warehouse 
5.3.1. Sizing resources 
Demand modeling 
The demand forecasted will be subject to change. New pieces of information will complete 
the forecast when the activity launch gets closer. Some biases can already be 
commented: it has been considered in Chapter 4.4.2 that the customers would adapt to 
the delivery slots offered by Ocommerce. Maybe they won’t and they will order to the 
competitors. This could be taken into account in the demand modeling.  
Key metrics 
In the future, at the time the warehouse opens, the key metrics should be carefully 
examined, and it should be considered to model the warehouse with more adapted 
metrics. Some design considerations are probably going to affect the metrics and make 
them different from Ocado’s metrics, which are currently used. For instance, the receiving 
productivity can be altered by the fact that some receiving locations are far from the dock 
doors. 
Human resource 
My study gives an overview of the Full-Time Equivalent needed for the activities of the 
warehouse. As a result of this part, a graph representing the number of FTE needed has 
been made. However, the fact that a new resource is not productive at the beginning 
needs to be added on top of that: I recommend that the employees are hired a few weeks 
or days before their official start date in order to receive proper training. 
Furthermore, only the productive employees are sized in the present study. It could be 
interesting to conduct the same study for the supervisors and the managers. For instance, 
the number of supervisors could be proportionate to the number of productive employees 
==PAGE:114==
 102 
they are responsible for. The number of managers could be decided on the number of 
different services by having one manager for each group of activities.  
Equipment management 
The quantity needed for each piece of equipment has been computed or a formula related 
to the number of orders per week. This represents a need. It is recommended not to stick 
to the calendar given by the calculations. First, the equipment needs to be planned to be 
received in advance, in case the supplier has production issues. Secondly, the supplier 
often requires ordering a minimum quantity each time, which means again that pieces of 
equipment will be ordered in advance. 
Another file or software needs to be used to take these new pieces of information, which 
are going to be gathered by discussing with the suppliers. 
Could also be considered how the equipment will be handled in the warehouse: where are 
they stored? How to avoid lost pieces of equipment? 
It could be pictured that only expensive pieces are tracked individually, as for instance RF 
Gun. For small and cheap pieces, only the overall stock would be tracked. The equipment 
handling will probably be centralized in a Central Store in the warehouse.  
5.3.2. Organizing human resources 
The idea here was to model the warehouse in order to have an accurate insight into its 
needs in terms of productive human resource. My recommendation is to use the results 
presented in Figure 73 as a tool for deciding the organization of the shifts. Some peaks of 
activity could be postponed or anticipated. For instance, some of the reverse flow activities 
can be done as soon as the vehicle is back, if this is convenient for the organization of the 
warehouse, if it happens in activity valleys and that some employees might be available.  
The next study to conduct is to address the planning problem: how can the teams be 
organized in order to maintain equity but to be as close as possible to the ideal needs 
which have been modeled.

Chapter 6. 
 
 
Discussion 
In this Chapter are discussed the strengths and the weaknesses of the results. It is also 
explained how the results of the present study could be integrated with other research 
results  
The present study aims to answer the following research question of interest: how to 
optimize processes and model organizations to set up operational rules in a new 
automated warehouse to support operational ramp-up phase? 
First can be discussed if the results exposed in the present study answers the question. 
All the results presented can be used for all the steps of the ramp-up-phase: the processes 
will be the same at any point in time, and if some small change occurs, they can be 
adapted, the models can be used no matter the number of orders. The study applies to 
the warehouse developed by Ocommerce, which can be called automated, as the 
processes have proven. The processes have been presented and optimized as well as 
possible with the available information.  
Organizations have been modeled in order to size the human resource and the equipment. 
Some rules in term of size of teams and quantity of resources, as well as hours, have thus 
been set up. However, organizations could have been modeled from other points of view: 
for instance, the organization architecture could have been studied and organization 
charts could have been made, but it has been let out of the scope of the thesis.  
6.1. 
Processes of the automated warehouse 
Results of the present study showed that the automated warehouse studied can fit into 
former models such as Frazelle’s model (2016) with some adjustments and that the same 
activities can be found. All these activities are necessary tasks perpetrated in a 
warehouse. 
The first step to understand and model a warehouse is to get an overview of the flows and 
processes occurring there. This study presents the main processes at a really detailed 
level of a state-of-the-art warehouse. The fact that it is a grocery activity makes the 
processes even more complicated. As it has been pointed out in the Chapter 5, even in 
such a warehouse, the automation could go further. However, the Groupe Casino had to 
build a solution for its e-commerce activity quickly and cannot wait for further 
developments. The originality of the warehouse lies in the storage system: the robots are 
==PAGE:116==
 104 
moving on top of the storage hive, and are not moving in alleys on the ground, as in the 
usual solutions chosen, whose illustrations can be seen in the Literature Chapter 3.2. 
The results presented in this section have the strength of having been reviewed by several 
stakeholders of the project and several iterations were made to write them. Moreover, the 
formalization used is quite easy to understand and the sequences will then be used widely 
for the training of the employees. 
However, the writer of the processes hasn’t seen the processes in by her own eye, which 
constitutes a weakness for the results. 
6.2. Performance 
The method of the Performance Prism has been applied. The strength of this method is 
that it takes many stakeholders point of views into consideration. This should enable to 
discover most of the indicators. However, many other methods exist and could have 
been used for performance management.  
6.3. 
Modelization of the warehouse 
6.3.1. Sizing resources 
Demand modeling 
The demand modeling used in the Study has the strength of having been created by the 
cooperation of all the main stakeholders of the project: Supply Chain and Offer and 
Executive Directors of the project. But this modeling is to be reviewed many times before 
the opening of the warehouse, which should be taken into consideration. Indeed, new 
pieces of information will become available when the opening is closer in time. 
Human resource 
The results of the human resource sizing part give the size of the operational productive 
team. The next step would be to do the same for the supervisor and the manager of the 
team. This would also require deciding which teams are going to be formed and what 
would be the architecture of communication in the warehouse. For instance, we could 
picture a manager of the morning operations and a manager of afternoon operations, but 
we could also imagine that those don’t exist and there are managers by functional 
departments only.  
==PAGE:117==
 105 
Equipment management  
The formula created allows computing the number of pieces of equipment necessary, 
which enables to be prepared for the ramp-up phase.  This step is important to have an 
overview of all the work that needs to be done to be able to start the activity in good 
conditions. 
6.3.2. Organizing human resources 
Preliminary optimization problems 
An optimization formulation has been used to size the number of vehicles needed and the 
time at which they should leave the warehouse. 
The demand model used is the orders currently delivered by Monoprix. It could be 
interesting to ask customers or potential customers what their expectations are in term of 
delivery slots. The number used here only represent the deliveries achieved by Monoprix. 
However, following Monoprix’s distribution is already difficult and damaging in term of 
profitability for Ocommerce, asking for customers real wishes would probably just be worst 
as the peak would be more irregular. 
The first strategy conducted was to meet all the calculated demand and to minimize the 
number of vehicles. It appeared to be a non-sense in term of warehouse optimization, 
which leads to looking into a second optimization problem: minimizing the unmet demand. 
This second problem is closer to reality. However, some other considerations could be 
added to the model: for instance, a multi-objective problem, also taking into account the 
utilization rate of the vehicle, could be made. Moreover, the optimization has been made 
for the peak day and it is considered that the organization will be the same on the non-
peak day, maybe with fewer vehicles, the reality might be different. Later, when the 
warehouse opens, vehicle routing problems, which have been studied in many papers, 
will be used to optimize the routes. 
Human resources organization 
The strength of the analysis of the present section is that a lot of warehouses constraints 
have been taken into account. The results of the study are quite realistic as a lot of 
parameters has been considered in the model. The model has indeed been created with 
the Logistics Director of the project. The weakness of the study is that some constraints 
applying to human resources need to be added on top of the results. First, employees 
won’t be interested in a part-time job, as it is not widespread in French culture. The salary 
of a productive employee is probably close to the minimum wage allowed in France. The 
employees thus want to work full-time to earn enough money to live. The jobs created 
==PAGE:118==
 106 
should, therefore, correspond to a full-time equivalent, or there is a risk of not being able 
to hire anyone for the job.  
Furthermore, French law dictates the following constraints:  
• It is forbidden to make an employee work more than 6 days in a row  
• Every day, an employee should have at least 11 hours of idle time 
This also needs to be taken into consideration. The present study is a preparatory work 
for a future employee scheduling problem, which consists of scheduling employees for 
weekly shifts by minimizing the cost while meeting the demand. As the warehouse is open 
7 days a week and 22 hours a day, the schedule of an employee probably won’t follow the 
pattern 5 days of work - 2 days of work. Innovations need to be made to have the most 
optimized schedule. 
A social dimension can be added to the results of this section. Flexibility could be 
introduced in the warehouse: the employees could be trained for several jobs, as the 
training is going to be quite short. Based on employees’ voluntariness, this practice would 
increase their satisfaction and prevent boredom. Moreover, this could also allow handling 
more easily the variability of the demand by shifting employees from an activity to another 
when needed. In the present study case, for instance, the Inbound area and the Outbound 
area are not aligned in the time. It means that there could be a peak activity in the Inbound 
area and not in the Outbound area. A skillful employee, who would have skills in both 
areas could be really helpful in such a case.  
From another point of view, the question of the duration and the hours of the shifts also 
takes part in the Health and Safety considerations. The results found in the study need to 
be compared to the recommendations in term of Health and Safety.  
Industries are often required to have activities 24 hours per day, 7 days per week. This 
requires employees to work at unusual hours and with irregularities. Those companies 
generally follow shift organization, to preserve equity between the employees. However, 
those organization of working time can be damaging to the health of the employee. 
Recommendations can be found to avoid risk for the employees’ health.  
In Kecklund et al., recommendations are made to combine shift organization and health 
and safety for the employees. The recommendations are the following:  
• A rotating shift system is preferred to a permanent shift system.  
• Regarding the speed of the rotation, the recommendations from different studies 
are not consistent. Some argue that the rotation should occur as fast as 
possible, as it would limit the negative consequences of early-morning shifts 
==PAGE:119==
 107 
and night shifts and that slow-paced rotation increase the risk of accident: the 
study shows that in comparison to the first night of the shift, the risk of accident 
is increased by 36% the fourth day of the shift. Others argue that the slower 
rotations allow adapting to the rhythm and that the sleepiness of the employees 
decreases with the amount of similar consecutive shifts.  
• The rotation of the shifts should be clockwise: first in the morning, then in the 
evening, then in the night.  
• Shifts longer than 11 hours are not recommended.  
• The morning shift should start later than 6 AM, preferably around 7 AM.  
The present study should be integrated into all these considerations to form an optimal 
organization.

Conclusion 
The goal of this thesis was to optimize processes and model organizations to set up 
operational rules in a new automated warehouse to support operational ramp-up phase. 
This research question could have been addressed in many ways; it was chosen to focus 
on certain aspects. The results are highlighted in this Chapter.  
The processes have been formalized in detail, they will allow all future stakeholders on 
this warehouse to understand quickly its functioning and to execute any operational task 
in the warehouse. A performance management strategy has been developed and KPIs 
have been chosen, integrating the point of view of all stakeholders. These KPIs will enable 
to evaluate the processes and the strategies and will be particularly useful when time for 
change and improvement will come. The KPIs also enable to understand the models built 
in this thesis. These models try to ensure short lead time and good utilization of resources, 
which is a hard-to-reach compromise. Several models have been developed. The first 
model sets up rule to compute the number of employees needed per activities from the 
number of orders. A similar approach was applied regarding the pieces of equipment, after 
determining the pieces needed. Then, an optimization model has been developed to 
determine the best times for delivery trucks to leave, knowing the number of orders, which 
are key inputs for the organization of the warehouse. The organization of the team during 
a peak day was thus deduced.  
These tools will ease the task of the future operational management in starting the activity. 
They will be able to know the composition of the team and of the set of equipment at any 
point of the ramp-up phase and thus to react quickly to unexpected increase of the 
demand, thanks to the study presented in this thesis.
