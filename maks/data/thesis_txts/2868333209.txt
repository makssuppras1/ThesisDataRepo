Abstract
Abstract
With the ambition of achieving climate goals, offshore wind energy plays a crucial
role in countries such as Denmark.
To accommodate this growth, offshore wind
farms and wind turbine generators continue to increase in size while moving farther
offshore, making efficient energy transport more challenging.
Offshore energy hubs
address this issue by centralizing offshore transmission infrastructure and transmitting
power to shore via HVDC cables.
The increase in wind energy production, with its
inherent fluctuations, presents challenges for grid integration. To ensure grid stability,
transmission system operators implement grid codes that specify parameters such as
voltage and reactive power capability requirements.
This thesis aims to explore the
economic potential of relaxing these grid codes and analyze the power flow of the systems.
The main focus of this thesis was offshore wind farms connected to offshore en-
ergy hubs. Three different offshore wind farm layouts were investigated: one operating
on a 66 kV system and two on 132 kV systems. The two 132 kV systems were differen-
tiated by their string configurations: one with shorter but more numerous strings, and
the other with fewer but longer strings. Each layout was evaluated using four different
grid codes with varying voltage and reactive power requirements.
The study showed that the more conservative 66 kV system was the most robust,
with the potential to accommodate the longest distance from the wind farm to the
hub. Conversely, increasing the voltage to 132 kV significantly increased capacitance,
limiting the possible distance between the wind farm and the hub without additional
compensation equipment.
The economic potential of the layouts was analyzed based on cost estimates for
cables and transformers, while other components were largely excluded due to limited
cost data.
The main objective was to compare the economic potential of the three
layouts under different grid codes rather than providing exact cost estimates.
Only
manufacturer costs were included; installation costs, which could significantly influence
total costs, were excluded from the analysis.
The findings demonstrated a potential saving of approximately $50 million when
transitioning from a 66 kV system to a 132 kV system. This saving was primarily due
to the higher voltage, which resulted in lower currents and enabled the use of thinner
cables. However, the 132 kV layout with longer but fewer strings required thicker cables,
increasing costs compared to the 132 kV layout with shorter but more numerous strings,
despite the former requiring less total cable. It was noted that including cable installa-
tion costs could significantly affect this result, but this was outside the scope of the thesis.
Testing various grid codes revealed that removing or relaxing reactive power re-
quirements had the most significant positive impact on system power loss and the design
power factor of wind turbine generators. Relaxing the voltage range also reduced system
power loss and allowed for smaller cables and transformers, though the economic impact
on manufacturing costs was relatively modest, in the range of $1–5 million.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation and Contribution
The global energy system is undergoing a significant transition to align with the Paris
Agreement, which aims to limit global warming to below 1.5°C and reduce greenhouse
gas emissions by 55% no later than 2023[35]. Moreover, the agreement sets the target
of achieving climate neutrality by 2050.
To obtain this, phasing out fossil fuels and
increasing the share of renewable energy are crucial.
Denmark has been a global pioneer in wind energy and intends to leverage its wind
potential to achieve climate neutrality by 2050. In 2022, a majority in the Danish Par-
liament set ambitious political goals, including the potential for a fivefold increase in
offshore wind capacity [25].
However, a major setback occurred in December 2024 when no bids were submitted
for three planned Offshore Wind Farms (OWF) in the North Sea. Energy companies cited
the unsustainable business case due to escalating costs, despite the great wind potential
and seabed conditions [27] [33].
Offshore wind energy remains a promising, cost-effective solution for decarbonization
[21]. With a significant increase in OWFs, but also the size of Wind Turbine Generators
(WTG) new challenges arise. The increase in capacity and size can already be seen in
the statics represented below, and it does not seem to stagnate anytime soon.
(a) Average size of commercial offshore wind
farms in the given year [42]
(b) Growth in size of wind turbines and
prospects [5]
Figure 1: Growth in size of offshore wind farms and wind turbines
To accomodate this growth areas further offshore are necessary which increases the
cost of connection. To obtain a cost-efficient energy system, Offshore Energy Hubs (OEH)
are considered an important solution.
OEHs centralize offshore transmission infrastructure, transmitting power to shore via
High Voltage Direct Current (HVDC) cables [22]. Multiple OEHs in Northern Europe
particularly in the North and Baltic Sea, are planned or under development, e.g., Energy
Island Bornholm (BEI). They are necessary to achieve the agreed wind power capacity by
2050, and they are among the largest infrastructure projects in Denmark. Hence, their
design must ensure seamless integration into the existing energy system while maintaining
cost efficiency. Research indicates that a 10 GW energy island could achieve potential
savings of 20-30 billion DKK through optimized design and implementation [32].
This thesis seeks to contribute to this effort by assessing the potential cost savings
associated with OWFs connected to OEHs.

1.2
Objectives and Scope
The main objective of this thesis is to assess the economic impacts of relaxing reactive
power Network Code (NC) requirements in the context of OEHs. This is achieved by
investigating different design choices for OWFs connected to OEHs and testing these
designs under varying NCs.
Three distinct OWF designs are considered, based on detailed plans from the BEI
project, TenneT’s 2 GW program, and suggestions from literature for next-generation
offshore wind systems:
• Layout 1: WTGs connected through a 66 kV array cable system
• Layout 2: WTGs connected through a 132 kV array cable system, maintaining the
same number and length of strings as in Layout 1
• Layout 3: WTGs connected through a 132 kV array cable system, with longer
strings and fewer number of single-point connections to the OEH
Each layout is modeled in the power system analysis software PowerFactory, with
balanced AC load flow executed via Python scripts integrated with PowerFactory. All
data analysis is also conducted using Python.
The thesis incorporates two different reactive power distributions:
• Equal distribution: Reactive power is allocated based on the rated power of the
WTGs. For identical WTGs, this results in a uniform reactive power output across
the OWF
• Weighted distribution: Reactive power is distributed based on the distance of each
WTG from the OEH. WTGs closer to the OEH are assigned a greater percentage
share, while those farther away less. This distribution is expected to reduce power
losses, enhancing the efficiency of the OWF.
The main focus of the thesis is on the OWFs connected to the OEH, rather than the
OEH itself. Furthermore, the economic analysis considers only the Capital Expenditure
(CAPEX), as Operational Expenditures (OPEX) lie outside the scope. An extensive lit-
erature review revealed that precise cost estimations for specific components are limited.
Therefore, the cost estimations primarily focus on the array cable system and transform-
ers.
The objective is to compare different layouts under various NCs, highlighting their
strengths and weaknesses rather than providing precise cost assessments. Additionally,
it examines how different NCs influence design choices for OWFs and how they affect the
power flow of the entire system.
The results and analysis hereof will contribute to answering the two following research
questions:
1. What impact do reactive power requirements have on operating conditions at both
the component and system levels?
2. What are the economical potentials associated with relaxing reactive power require-
ments for OEHs?

Background
2
Background
This section provides the background relevant to the project, focusing on the growth of
offshore wind energy, its current state of the art, and the relevance of OEHs. Addition-
ally, it explains the technical theory behind the electrical components used in the model
development.
2.1
Evolution of offshore wind energy
Offshore wind energy has undergone significant development over the years. The world’s
first OWF, with a capacity of approximately 5 MW, was built in 1991 in Vindeby, Den-
mark. It consisted of 11 WTGs, each with a nominal power of 450 kW, located 1.5–3 km
from the shore. The short distance to the coast made it feasible to connect the individual
WTGs to a 0.69/10 kV transformer and transmit power to shore via AC cables, where a
50/10 kV transformer was installed [30].
Since then, technology has advanced significantly, and OWFs have moved farther
from shore to take advantage of the greater wind potential. In 2002, Horns Rev 1 was
commissioned in the North Sea with a capacity of 160 MW. It was located 18 km from the
coastline and was the first OWF to have its own offshore substation. The OWF consists
of 80 WTGs, each with a nominal power of 2 MW. The WTGs are interconnected through
a 33 kV cable system, and at the offshore substation, the voltage is stepped up to 150
kV before being transmitted to shore via a 21 km long High Voltage Alternating Current
(HVAC) cable [40].
Until recently, OWFs were typically connected individually to the onshore grid via
HVAC cables. However, as more OWFs are built farther from shore, the average distance
to shore has significantly increased. From 2009 to 2019, the average distance to shore
for commissioned OWFs increased from 15 km to 60 km and continues to grow [18].
This increased distance and the growing number of installations have introduced new
challenges.
Transporting power over long distances through HVAC cables becomes less economical
feasible beyond a critical distance (typically 100–150 km) due to the limitations of reactive
power and the increased need for compensation equipment. This topic is explored in
greater detail in Section 17. One solution to these challenges is the use of HVDC cables,
which are more efficient for long-distance transmission [1].
Moreover, if all OWFs were connected individually to shore via HVAC cables, it would
require a significantly larger number of cables compared to utilizing OEH with HVDC
connections. As offshore energy production increases, the potential cost savings from
reducing the number of cables become even more relevant. Additionally, utilizing HVDC
technology enables the interconnection of multiple AC systems, minimizing the number
of point-to-point connections. This concept forms the basis for the development of OEHs,
which can centralize power collection.
2.2
Offshore energy hubs
OEHs are designed to connect multiple OWFs to a central hub, from which power is
distributed to surrounding areas. By installing OEHs far from shore, OWFs can benefit
from higher wind speeds in deeper waters. The overall concept of an OEH is visualized
in 2.

2.2.2
Inter-Array cable systems
Inter-array cables form the network that connects individual WTGs within the OWF.
Over the years, the system voltage has increased significantly. For instance, the first
OWF at Vindeby in 1991 operated at 10 kV, Horns Rev 1 in 2002 used 33 kV, and
the TenneT 2 GW standard now operates at 66 kV. Research already suggests potential
benefits by increasing the voltage level to 132 kV. This trend is primarily driven by
the increasing size of OWFs and individual WTGs. Keeping the array system voltage
constant while increasing WTG sizes would require higher current-carrying cables, leading
to thicker, more expensive cables or shorter strings with more strings overall, which would
also increase costs.
Studies indicate that higher voltage systems are more cost-effective than the current
66 kV systems.
These cost savings are mainly driven by CAPEX with regards to a
reduction of installed cable length due to fewer required strings. Another advantage of
higher voltage systems is the lower current, which reduces electrical losses [39].
The array cable voltage acts as a limiting factor for the length of each string. For
this thesis the maximum number of WTGs per string for a 66 kV system is defined by
DTU’s cooperation partners as six. For a 132 kV system, literature suggests this number
could increase to ten [39]. The array system of two OWFs each with a total capacity
of 510 MW consisting of 34*15 MW WTG could be arranged as illustrated in Figure 6
depending on the voltage level.

2.3
Network codes
This section explains the concept of NCs, their purpose, and the challenges they present.
Furthermore, a presentation of the NCs applied in the model will be provided.
With the significant increase in renewable energy generation, the associated fluctuat-
ing production introduces new challenges for grid integration. The TSO is responsible
for ensuring grid stability, which is achieved through the implementation of international
standards and NCs. However, as technology and the energy system continue to evolve,
these NCs and standards are also subject to continuous updates. This creates challenges
for manufacturers and developers, who must adapt to the changing requirements.
The increasing capacity of OWFs and their placement farther from shore raise ad-
ditional issues, such as power transmission over longer distances and weaker grid con-
nections.
The first issue is addressed by using HVDC connections instead of HVAC
connections, which is explained in Section 2.4.2 The latter issue is managed through
NCs.
NCs define the technical requirements for generators and loads connected to the trans-
mission or distribution system. These requirements vary by country, technology, and in-
dividual projects. In Europe, the European Network of Transmission System Operators
for Electricity (ENTSO-E) harmonizes and outlines these requirements across member
countries. The requirements cover aspects such as active power, frequency, voltage, and
reactive power control etc. [43]. However, this thesis solely focuses on reactive power and
voltage regulation.
Reactive power regulation contributes to the voltage stability of the grid.
These
requirements must be met at the PoC which is defined as the point where the OWFs
connect to the TSO’s network. NCs specify the required design Power Factor (PF) of the
WTGs, which determines the required size and capability of the converters to comply
with these requirements. The influence of these requirements regarding PF is discussed
in Section 2.5.
With regards to OEHs these are required to adhere to the NC on HVDC (NC
HVDC) established by the European Commission. These NCs specify properties such
as reactive power capabilities, voltage ranges, and fault ride-through requirements
[13]. OWFs, classified as DC-connected power park modules, are also subject to these
regulations [14] In addition to the NC HVDC, project-specific technical requirements
may apply, as seen in the case of the BEI. The BEI project includes tailored adjustments
to reactive power requirements, along with other project-specific parameters [10].
Four different NCs are investigated, an Outer Envelope with the highest range of
voltage and reactive power, the reactive power requirements for BEI, a relaxed version

2.4
Cables
This section outlines the technical background for the selection of cables and their impact
on the system.
First, an overall description is provided, including relevant parameters such as in-
ductance (L), capacitance (C), resistance (R), current (I), and voltage (V). Next, the
assumptions regarding material selection are justified. Finally, the cost estimations are
presented.
2.4.1
Electrical characteristics of cables
Since this project only considers the OWF up until the PoC all cables modelled are HVAC.
In the offshore wind industry, XLPE cables are widely applied, and therefore, this project
solely investigates this type. XLPE cables are three-core cables, where ”XLPE” refers
to the main insulation material. It should be noted that the insulation properties of the
cable are outside the scope of this thesis. The key electrical parameters investigated for
the XLPE cable are: L, R, and, C. A simplified equivalent circuit of the XLPE cable is
illustrated in Figure 12.
Figure 12: Equivalent circuit of XLPE cable [19]
The resistance of the cable influences power losses (Ploss = I2 ·R) along the cable and
contributes to voltage drop (V = R·I). The inductance of the cable represents its ability
to store energy in the magnetic field created by alternating current, and it contributes to
reactive power consumption. The capacitance, on the other hand, represents the cable’s
12
==PAGE:23==
Master thesis
Background
ability to store energy in the electric field between the conductors and the surrounding
environment, leading to reactive power generation.
For long HVAC cables, the capacitance is the dominant parameter, meaning that re-
active power increases along the cable’s length, while the active power decreases due to
losses. Reactive power must be compensated to maintain system stability and voltage
levels. This can be achieved by installing additional compensation equipment (e.g., shunt
reactors) or by using WTGs to operate in an inductive mode [19]. In this study, no addi-
tional compensation equipment is included; all reactive power compensation is managed
by the WTGs or transformers.
The XLPE conductor can be made of either aluminum or copper. Copper conductors
have a greater current rating per Cross-sectional Area (CSA) compared to aluminum.
Moreover, copper conductors have lower resistance, which means the system will experi-
ence smaller power losses during operation when using copper instead of aluminum. To
increase the validity of comparisons across different layouts, this project exclusively uses
copper conductors.
2.4.2
The challenges regarding HVAC
AC cables are generally preferred over DC cables due to their lower station costs. How-
ever, using AC cables can be challenging when it comes to increasing rated power and
distances, which is exactly what occurs with larger WTGs and OWFs located farther
offshore. The challenges arise from increased losses due to higher current and increased
capacitance with longer cable lengths. As a result, there is a critical distance for AC
cables, typically between 100 and 150 km depending on the cable type, beyond which
there is insufficient capacity for active power transmission [1].
The increase in reactive power with an increase in voltage level can be expressed as
[29]:
Q = 2π · f · C · V 2
(1)
From Equation 1, it can be seen that the reactive power generated by the capacitors
increases with the square of the voltage. This means that an increase in voltage would
require the WTGs and transformers to act more inductively to meet the setpoints from
the NCs. The influence of this effect is investigated in the results analysis.

2.6
Transformers
The last electrical equipment used in the modeling is the transformer. This project utilizes
transformers to step up the voltage from the individual WTGs to the array system, and
from the array system to the PoC. The purpose of stepping up the voltage is to transport
larger amounts of power with lower losses.
All transformers considered in this project are three-phase transformers. The ratings
of the transformers are given in MVA, which represents the total apparent power, encom-
passing both active and reactive power the transformer can handle. In this study, the
ratings of the transformers are defined by the generating unit connected to them and its
rated power, including the voltage range under which the system operates, as specified
by NCs.
The windings of the transformers, which dictate the step-up voltage, are made of
copper. Copper has inherent resistance, which leads to copper losses in the transformer.
Moreover, transformers behave as inductive components, meaning they absorb reactive
power. This is primarily due to their leakage reactance, which arises from the leakage
flux in the transformer [2] [9]. However, these aspects will not be discussed further in
details as they fall outside the scope of this thesis.

3.1.1
Layout 1
Layout 1 uses a 66 kV array system, which is the standard voltage rating for OWFs

strings with 6 WTGs each and two strings with 5 WTGs each.
As visualized in Figure 16, Layout 1 employs two cable types, represented by the colors

behavior. However, the lower voltage leads to higher current, resulting in greater power

3.1.2
Layout 2
Layout 2 operates at a 132 kV voltage level, while retaining the same number and lengths
of strings as Layout 1. The voltage level and current ratings of the cables are adjusted
accordingly.
This layout utilizes only one cable type, primarily due to the limited availability of
HVAC cables with low rated currents. The detailed diagram used in PowerFactory is

The higher voltage is expected to result in a more capacitive system, necessitating the
WTGs and transformers to operate more inductively. Additionally, the lower current is
anticipated to reduce power losses.
3.1.3
Layout 3
Layout 3 also operates at 132 kV but takes advantage of the higher voltage to enable
longer strings. Each OWF consists of four strings: two strings with 8 WTGs each and
two strings with 9 WTGs each.
Two cable types are used in this layout. The smaller cable, with a CSA of 300 mm2, is
utilized for a maximum of 7 WTGs per string. This cable is slightly under-dimensioned,
based on the assumption that power losses will occur and the cables can handle a small
continuous overload. The detailed diagram used in PowerFactory is provided in Appendix

With fewer strings, Layout 3 uses less amount of cable compared to Layout 2, which is
expected to result in a less capacitive system. Consequently, the WTGs and transformers
will be less inductive than in Layout 2 but more so than in Layout 1.
The reduced
cable material is also expected to contribute to smaller power losses. The actual system
performance will depend on the specific parameters of the cables used.

The first step in determining the required cable rating involves calculating the current
per string. The number of WTGs per string varies between 5 and 9 across the three
layouts.
Within each string, the highest current occurs at the cable segment nearest to
the OEH, as it accumulates the output from all upstream WTGs. This allows for using
multiple cable types within a string. For instance, as shown in Figure 18, a smaller cable
(purple) with a lower current rating and CSA may be used upstream, while a larger
cable (black) is used downstream closer to the OEH.
In a string of five WTGs, the current at the final cable section (iN) equals the combined
output of all preceding WTGs.

3.6
Control system
This section describes the control of the system regarding reactive power. The purpose
of the control system is to ensure the NCs are satisfied, the two parameters considered
are: voltage [p.u.] and reactive power per nominal power [Q/Pn].
To achieve this, a voltage setpoint is set at the external grid, a capability curve is
implemented for the WTGs, and a station control is included to achieve the reactive
power requirements and moreover also controlling the distribution of the reactive power
between the individual WTGs.
3.6.1
Station control
With the purpose of considering the reactive power limits, and ensuring that the capacity
of each WTG is not exceeded a station controller is implemented.
The station controller controls the reactive power setpoint, which is defined by the
NC. The reference point is the busbar connected to the grid, which serves as the PoC
(named terminal a in Appendix B).
Two different reactive power distributions are considered: According to rated power
and according to individual reactive power. The first distribution will in the following
sections be referred to as the Equal Distribution, since all WTGs have the same rated
power their dispatch will be almost identical. The second distribution will be referred
to as the Weighted Distribution, in this case the distribution is based on a weighting
accordingly to the distance from the OEH to the WTGs, this is explained in Section
3.6.2.
3.6.2
Weighted reactive power distribution
The purpose of a weighted distribution is to minimize losses, by ensuring that the WTGs
closest to the OEH contributes the most.

5.2
Assumptions and model limitations
Numerous assumptions were made throughout this thesis.
Some of these may have
a significant influence on the results, while others are unlikely to affect the overall
conclusions. Key assumptions are elaborated upon below.
Due to limitations in available cost estimations, only one source was used—namely,
NREL. Ideally, multiple cost estimations would have been obtained to define a repre-
sentative cost range. However, it was concluded that this was not feasible within the
available public literature.
Additionally, it is acknowledged that the 132 kV cables would, in practice, be more
expensive due to the additional insulation required. However, even if the cost of these
cables were doubled, Layouts 2 and 3 would still remain cheaper than Layout 1. Therefore,
neglecting this aspect is assumed to yield reasonable results for comparison purposes.
It was also assumed that the cost of transformers would not differ significantly between
a 0.69/66 kV transformer and a 0.69/132 kV transformer, provided the ratings remained
the same. Again, even if the transformer costs for Layouts 2 and 3 were doubled, these
layouts would still be cheaper compared to Layout 1. This is primarily because cable
costs are the dominant cost driver in this analysis.
Another limitation relates to the relationship between CSA and ampacity in the
obtained cable data. The cable data from ABB shows an exponential relationship, while
the NREL cost estimations are based on a linear relationship. Assuming an exponential
relationship would result in Layouts 1 and 3, which use the thickest cables, being
significantly more expensive than Layout 2. However, since Layout 1 uses the thickest
and greatest quantity of cables, followed by Layout 3, the conclusion that Layout 2 is
the cheapest remains valid, followed by Layout 3, with Layout 1 being the most expensive.
Finally, the potential savings were determined based on simulation results obtained
from the initial design. To ensure the new dimensions performed as expected, further
simulations should have been conducted using the updated input data, particularly for
cable properties where reactance, inductance, and capacitance would have changed. This

5.3
Recommendations for future work
From the results, it was observed that the biggest challenge with the 132 kV layouts
was reactive power compensation, particularly as the distance between the OEH and
OWF increased. Since OWFs typically include additional reactive power compensation
equipment, future studies could investigate how such equipment might address this
issue. Furthermore, the economic impact of adding compensation equipment should be
analyzed to determine whether the 132 kV layouts remain the most cost-effective options.
It was also observed that different NCs could allow for adjustments to the design
specifications of the WTG converters. Given that WTGs constitute the largest share of
the total CAPEX for OWFs, this presents significant potential for cost savings. Future
research could focus on evaluating the economic potential of reducing converter sizes.
However, this would require additional data that was not available for this thesis.
The weighted reactive power distribution showed some potential for reducing power
loss but was dismissed due to the minimal differences observed in this study.
It is
possible that the benefits of such a distribution would become more significant as
the distance increases. This thesis highlighted this potential and opened the door for
future investigations into the critical distance at which a weighted distribution becomes
advantageous.
Additionally, research could explore how the distribution might be
optimized to maximize its benefits.
6
Conclusion
This thesis concludes that there are significant potential cost savings associated with
shifting from a 66 kV to a 132 kV array system. The observed benefits primarily relate
to reductions in CAPEX and power losses.
The analysis showed that removing or relaxing reactive power requirements could
lead to reduced power losses and enable the possibility of decreasing the size of the WTG
converters. Since WTGs represent a significant portion of the overall CAPEX for OWFs,
this presents a substantial opportunity for cost optimization.
Furthermore, relaxing the voltage level requirements was shown to reduce power losses.
More importantly, it allowed for the selection of smaller cables and transformers with
lower ratings, thereby reducing the overall CAPEX. These findings emphasize the impor-
tance of exploring flexible NCs to optimize the design of OWFs.
The power flow analysis revealed that the capacitance of the system increased signif-
icantly for the 132 kV layouts, requiring the WTGs to operate more inductively. Among
the 132 kV layouts, Layout 2 faced greater challenges due to its shorter and more numer-
ous strings, while Layout 3, with longer but fewer strings, proved to be the preferable
design choice.
Additionally, the results demonstrated that increasing the distance between the OWFs
and the OEH amplified the differences in power loss between Layout 2 and Layout 3. For
longer distances, Layout 3 becomes increasingly advantageous compared to Layout 2,
suggesting that it is the more robust design for scenarios where greater distances are

In conclusion, this thesis highlights the significant economic and technical benefits of

follow along, and the robustness of a 66 kV Layout. Emphasizing the importance of
considering NC flexibility and design optimization strategies to ensure cost-effectiveness
