Abstract 
This project focuses on the Redlich-Kister equation, the implementation of the model, and how well 
this equation describes literature data of binary systems. The binary systems investigated in this 
project are 2-amino-2-methyl-1-propanol (AMP)-H2O, and piperazine (PZ)-H2O. Before the 
investigation of the binary systems, a fundament is built up with data from the literature on the pure 
chemical components; water, AMP, and PZ.   
It was found that the Redlich-Kister equation is a very good model when describing the behavior of 
the densities of the systems AMP-H2O, and PZ-H2O. This is especially seen in the excess molar 
volume curves of the AMP-H2O system at different temperatures. Here it was found that the model 
with four parameters was the one describing the literature data best as an average absolute deviation 
(AARD) of 12.48 was obtained. The fitted model parameters were 𝑎0 = −4.13, 𝑎1 = 2.32, 𝑎2 =
−1.26, 𝑏0 = 0.01. 
Pure AMP is solid at temperatures below 303 K (30 °C). It was therefore not possible to find literature 
data below this temperature. It was assumed that the obtained literature densities at 303 K and above 
can be extrapolated by linear regression to obtain the densities at temperatures below 303 K. It was 
expected that the lower the temperature of AMP the higher the density because the crystal structure 
becomes smaller at a lower temperature. It was also expected that AMP had no maximum density. 
All these assumptions were considered reasonable during the work with this project. 
Data on the PZ-H2O mixtures were obtained from the literature. Due to pure PZ being solid at room 
temperature, data were only obtained on mixtures with PZ mole fractions between 0.005-0.045 (2.3-
18.4 wt%) in the temperature range of 303-343 K (30-70 °C).  
The intention was to model and find the parameter values describing the PZ-water mixtures. 
Unfortunately, there do not exist enough data on the this systems for obtaining a full picture of the 
behavior of the excess molar volume. Therefore, it was not possible to fit the Redlich-Kister model 
with the literature data.

1 Introduction 
The climate, the greenhouse effect and carbon dioxide (CO2) are all factors, which need to be in 
balance with each other, whether to obtain a healthy environment here on Earth. For instance, there 
is too much CO2. Furthermore, there are too many factories. How are CO2 emissions limited and 
reduced then? For example, by using an absorber. There has been looking into how the absorber 
becomes most effective. This is achieved by using amines and water. How to optimize this liquid to 
capture as much CO2 as possible? Therefore, it is important to be able to measure the liquid's CO2 
content, called loading. This loading can be correlated with the density to make measurements on 
how much CO2 has been captured easier. The challenges in determining the density of the liquid are 
that the amines are solid at room temperature. Therefore, the densities of the pure components of the 
amines at the melting point and lower temperatures are estimated. This is done by extrapolation 
assuming linear correlation. Then models are set up for the amines and liquid mixtures. 
CO2 is an atmospheric gas, which can neither be seen nor smelled [1]. It is found naturally in the 
atmosphere and is excessed when humans breathe. CO2 is needed to obtain and maintain the 
ecosystem of the Earth. Plants and trees need CO2 for their growth, and in that process, they transform 
CO2 into oxygen. If there is not enough CO2 in the atmosphere, mammals and humans would get a 
problem getting enough oxygen. CO2 is not bad, but as with many other things, it can be a problem 
if there is too much of it [1].  
All combustion excess CO2 – e.g., from volcano eruptions to car driving and production of electricity 
[2]. The rising CO2 level has created an unbalance due to exponential growth. The content of CO2 in 
the atmosphere is rising from the production and extraction of natural gas for instance and when it is 
burned off e.g., during flaring. The emission of CO2 causes the greenhouse effect to increase, meaning 
the world climate changes [3]. 
The theory behind global warming caused by the release of greenhouse gasses (GHG) was made back 
in 1824 by the French mathematician and physician Joseph Fourier [4]. The quantification of the 
theory was made in 1896 by the Swedish mathematician and physician Svante Arrhenius [5]. Svante 
showed that global warming and climate change were triggered by man-made emissions of GHG. 
Despite this knowledge having been known for decades, it was ignored until recently when the 
Western media, politicians, and scientific community took notice of the topic because of 
environmental concerns. This culminated in December 2015 in the “Paris Agreement”, an agreement 
between the Parties of the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) to 
reduce GHG emissions. The aim of the agreement was to avoid a global average temperature increase 
of more than 1.5 °C compared to pre-industrial levels by sufficiently reducing GHG emissions [6]. 
Human-caused CO2 emissions are a huge contributor to GHG emissions. The increase in human 
population, the industrial revolution and technological advances all contribute to increasing CO2 
emissions. It requires energy to operate the expanded technology from the industrial revolution. Fossil 
fuels are greatly used to provide the required energy, but the combustion of these fuels generates CO2 
[7].

2 Carbon capture and storage 
Carbon capture and storage (CCS) is a technology where CO2 from flue gas is captured by absorption 
in a chemical amine solvent. Then the CO2 is stripped of the solvent by heating and collected to get 
shipped and put down in the empty oil fields. 
2.1 The carbon capture process  
A CO2 capture plant consists of an absorber with a solvent flowing inside. The absorber is filled with 
packing material e.g., Raschig rings to create as large a contact surface as possible to remove the CO2. 
The whole setting for a general CO2 capture process is illustrated in Figure 3. 
The flue gas enters the absorber with, e.g., 12% CO2, and as much as possible must be captured. It is 
possible to capture 100%, but it is usually not so energy efficient to do so. This is due to the last few 
percent of the CO2 are very energy costly. Instead, the bar is set slightly below 100%, e.g. 95% or 
98%. To comprehend to this, 90% is often decided as a limit.  
 
Figure 3: Basic flow sheet of an amine solvent-CO2 capture process. 
The following is a description of the principle of a carbon capture plant. The purpose of the facility 
is to obtain a cleaner emission from, e.g., a waste incineration plant by reducing the CO2 content in 
the flue gas. In a typical solvent-based capture process, a gaseous mixture is treated with a chemical 
solvent in an absorber column and CO2 is captured by the solvent and under input of thermal energy 
in the desorber column pure CO2 is released.  The heat from the re-boiler makes the chemical solvent 
in the desorber to release the pure CO2.  
In Figure 3 a simple process diagram of a CO2 capture plant is illustrated. Figure 3 shows an absorber, 
stripper, heat exchanger and reboiler. The fumes of the incineration plant must be cleaned of CO2,

and it is led into the lower part of the absorber. Here, the flue gas will meet a liquid flow. The flue 
gas moves up through the absorber, where it will meet the downward solvent flow on its way up. The 
solvent stream can contain an amine-based solvent, which has a special ability to absorb CO2.  
The decarbonised flue gas is led out through the top of the absorber and further into the atmosphere. 
The solvent flow is led from the bottom of the absorber to a heat exchanger that heats the solvent to 
120 °C. The solvent is then directed to the top of the stripper. Hereafter, the solvent stream will flow 
down through the stripper, where it meets the upward steam from the reboiler. The hot steam will 
strip the CO2 from the solvent.  
The pure CO2 is led further out of the top of the stripper to be collected and used or stored. The CO2-
stripped solvent will be returned to the heat exchanger to heat the aforementioned solvent stream to 
approx. 120 °C. After the heat exchanger, the cooled (approx. 40 °C) CO2-stripped solvent is led back 
to the top of the absorber where it meets a new stream of flue gas to once again absorb the CO2 from 
this. 
The pure CO2 that comes out is approximately 99% pure. The main impurity is H2O and minor 
individual gas molecules that originate from the flue gas such as N2, O2, unburnt CO, NO or SOx, as 
they are slightly soluble in water.  
2.2 System boundaries 
The following section describes the system boundaries on a typical CCS plant in order to determine 
the CO2 balance.  
If two flow meters are installed in the CCS plant, there are various things that can be considered. E.g., 
a flow meter could be placed at the outflow from the bottom of the absorber and a flow meter at the 
inflow at the top of the absorber. Notice that both flow meters are located where the flow is cold. The 
two streams between the desorber and the main heat exchanger are both hot. The flow from the 
reboiler is 120 °C and is cooled by the main heat exchanger to approx. 60 °C and then an additional 
cooler is placed to cool the liquid flow down to the desired 40 °C, which is led back to the top of the 
absorber.  
The important thing about the flow meters is when the CO2 content is to be determined. This can be 
done in different ways. For example, by titrating or by chromatography. It is also possible by 
measuring the density, which is a simple method to determine the CO2 content of the liquid. That is, 
if the loading is to be determined, then the density is measured at the two flowmeters placed in the 
two cold liquid streams. The CO2 balance can then be determined. This can be done in three different 
ways. Either a limit can be set around the flue gas flow to the absorber and the outflow at the top of 
the absorber. This creates a balance for how much CO2 comes in and how much comes out, which is 
how much is captured.  
In addition, a balance can be set up, where you look at how much CO2 comes in and how much is put 
into the liquid. The last way is to set up a balance that examines how much CO2 comes out after the 
condenser. The three methods should ideally give the same result when the system is in steady state. 
If it does not give the same result, then it is because the system is not in steady state.

2.3 Alkanolamine solvents 
The aqueous amine solutions are the most used solvents for the CO2 capture process. Amines are 
derivates of ammonia and can be sub-divided into three groups: primary, secondary, and tertiary 
amines. All amines have the characteristic that they at least contain one hydroxyl- and one amino 
group.   
Monoethanolamine (MEA) is often used for capturing CO2 and is typically referred to as the "base 
case amine" [14]. MEA has a relatively simple structure. In addition, there is piperazine (PZ), which 
has a ring structure with two N groups, so in principle it can capture twice as much CO2 per amine 
molecule. Furthermore, 2-amino-2-methyl-1-propanol (AMP) and methyldiethanolamine (MDEA) 
are amine, which can be used as solvents for CO2 capture.  
In this project, the focus will be on AMP, which is a primary amine, which means there is a nitrogen 
group with only one side chain and then there are two hydrogens (NH2). Furthermore, CO2 removal 
process by amine absorption can be improved by adding an activator PZ. This is a secondary amine, 
meaning that PZ has two carbons bonded to the nitrogen.

3 Project overview 
One of the most mature technologies for removal of CO2 from process steams such as biogas, 
cement production, and waste-to-energy plants is chemical absorption. The technology is also 
known as carbon capture and storage (CCS).  In this process CO2 is removed by an amine based 
aqueous solvent in an absorber and the solvent is regenerated by stripping off the CO2 at 110-120 
°C. The pure CO2 can be compressed and used for utilization or for CO2 storage.   
The desorption process is often associated with a high energy penalty as a sufficient amount of heat 
is applied in order to strip off the CO2. This leads to a broader focus in optimizing and designing 
new solvents, which can reduce the capital costs of the CO2 capture technology. 
In this project, fundamental knowledge on the solvent CESAR1, which is a mixture 2-amino-2-
methyl-1-propanol (AMP), piperazine (PZ), and water will be created. Correlations related to 
density, amine concentration and CO2 loading will be created in order to ease the experimental 
work related to determine these parameters. 
The objective of this project is to analyse binary mixtures as function of density and to construct 
correlations related to density. The amine concentration is based on real life solvents. The systems 
are AMP-H2O, and PZ-H2O.

6 Conclusion 
Due to the increasing levels of CO2 in the atmosphere it is necessary to capture and store it. The 
purpose of this project was to find an easy method to determine the amount of CO2 absorbed/captured 
in amine-based solvents used in a CCS-plant.  
This project investigated the Redlich-Kister equation in order to see if this model can be used to 
describe literature data of the different binary systems. In this project the binary systems AMP-water, 
and PZ-water was studied.   
From the investigations in this project, it was found that the Redlich-Kister equation is a very good 
model when describing the behavior of densities of AMP-water, and PZ-water. This was especially 
seen on the excess molar volume curves of the AMP-water mixtures. Here it was found that the model 
with four parameters was the one fitting the best with the literature data. The resulting model is 
obtained with the parameter values 𝑎0 = −4.13, 𝑎1 = 2.32, 𝑎2 = −1.26, 𝑏0 = 0.01 obtaining an 
AARD of 12.48. 
It is assumed that the measured densities at the temperature 303 K and above can be extrapolated by 
linear regression to obtain the densities at temperatures below 303 K. It is expected that the lower the 
temperature of AMP the higher the density. It is also expected that AMP has no maximum density. 
All these assumptions are considered reasonable during the work with this project. 
For the PZ-water mixtures, there were also found data in the literature. Due to PZ is solid at room 
temperature it was only found data on mixtures with PZ at a narrow span of mole fractions at low 
levels of PZ-concentration between 0.005-0.045 (2.3-18.4 wt%).  
The intension was also to model and find the parameter values describing the PZ-water mixtures. 
Unfortunately, there do not exist enough data on the PZ-water mixtures to obtain a fully picture of 
the behavior of the excess molar volume. Therefore, it was not possible to fit the Redlich-Kister model 
with the literature data.

7 Future work 
This section provides the ideas of what the next steps in the creation of a suitable correlation between 
solvent density and CO2 would be. 
It could have been interesting to investigate through experimental lab work, how fast the solvent 
containing PZ-AMP-H2O can be loaded with CO2. Unfortunately, there no literature data on this 
system was found during the period working on this project. There is a relatively easy method to load 
a given mixture with CO2.  
The CO2 loading (mol CO2/mol amine) can be determined by weighing a bottle containing the solvent 
sample before and after the exposure of CO2. For instance, CO2 could be bubbled through the solvent 
for respectively 5 min, 15 min, 30 min and 2 hours. Furthermore, there should be a reference solvent 
sample that has not been exposed to CO2. 
The solvents produced, could be used for further analysis. E.g., by measuring densities of the different 
solvents to find a correlation between the densities and the CO2-loading of the solvents. This 
correlation could be used to determining of the CO2-loading of a given similar solvent by simply 
measuring its density. These sets of data do not exist in the literature. 
Figure 34: Shows different CO2-loadings of a given binary solution with wt% CO2 as abscissa and density as 
ordinate.Figure 34 shows different CO2-loadings of a given binary solution with wt% CO2 as abscissa 
and density as ordinate. The times in minutes are durations of how long the solutions have been 
exposed to a constant gas flow of CO2. These are seen as 0 min CO2-loading (blue curve), 30 min 
CO2-loading (orange curve), and 120 min CO2-loading (grey curve), This is a thought example of 
how it possibly will look like if the data was found or the experiments were done. It is assumed that 
from the starting point at 0 wt% CO2 the density will increase linear proportionally with the increasing 
loading in wt% CO2. It is not known by certainty if the gaps between the three curves are identical. 
It could well be that the gap between blue and orange curve is bigger than the gap between orange 
and grey curve. It could also be opposite.
