Résumé 
 
 
Les réseaux électriques sont confrontés à de nouveaux défis, notamment à cause de 
la part croissante de la production renouvelable décentralisée. La méthode d’étude actuelle 
des réseaux HTA, basée sur un load-flow déterministe (LFD), pourrait donc ne plus permettre 
de les dimensionner de manière adéquate. Par conséquent, EDF R&D a développé un outil 
de load-flow probabiliste (LFP), qui a été implémenté et testé au cours de ce stage à l’aide de 
données de terrain, intégrées dans des modèles développés spécifiquement. Pour les nœuds 
du réseau ne disposant pas d’outil de mesure, le script Bagheera, autre outil développé par 
EDF R&D, a été utilisé. La conception des modèles de mise en forme des données d’entrée a 
constitué l’enjeu majeur de cette étude, avec en particulier la nécessité de prendre en compte 
la température et l’heure de la journée. Cependant, répartir les puissances active et réactive 
mesurées en tête de départ entre les charges s’est avéré essentiel pour l’obtention de résultats 
réalistes pour le LFP. Ce procédé réduit par ailleurs considérablement l’importance d’avoir des 
données de terrain en entrée ailleurs que pour les têtes de départ. Davantage de données 
auraient été nécessaires pour réellement améliorer la qualité des résultats. L’étude des 
résultats a surtout porté sur les chutes de tension, car l’outil de LFP a montré qu’il les calculait 
avec une bonne précision, et surtout parce qu’il s’agit d’indicateurs pertinents pour le 
dimensionnement des réseaux électriques. Le LFP montre que le LFD surestime légèrement 
les chutes de tension, mais son principal avantage réside dans le fait que les données 
aberrantes n’en impactent que peu les résultats. Le potentiel du LFP pour intégrer les 
nouveaux usages des réseaux électriques, ainsi que sa capacité à quantifier les risques 
d’apparitions de contraintes sur le réseau en font un outil, certes perfectible en l’état, mais de 
plus en plus pertinent pour le dimensionnement des réseaux électriques. 
 
Abstract 
 
 
Electrical networks are facing new challenges, mostly because of the increasing share 
of decentralized renewable production. To study Medium Voltage (MV) grids, the classical 
deterministic load-flow (DLF) methods may no longer be efficient enough to make accurate 
decisions about their dimensioning. A probabilistic load-flow (PLF) tool, previously designed 
by EDF R&D, was therefore implemented and tested in this thesis with field data and input 
models. The Bagheera tool, also previously developed by EDF R&D, filled in the inputs where 
no field data is available. Finding appropriate models for the inputs was the major stake of this 
work, and the most relevant parameters to take into account appeared to be the temperature 
and the hour of day. However, the average active and reactive power consumptions at all 
feeders’ heads needed to be dispatched on all connected stations without measurements in 
order to get realistic results for the PLF. This process lowers dramatically the importance of 
having input measurements except at the feeders’ heads. Input measurements were actually 
proven to be too few to make a real difference. The only outputs studied were the voltage 
drops, because they are well computed by the chosen PLF implementation, and because they 
are very significant for network dimensioning. In comparison with DLF, PLF shows that the 
voltage drops are currently slightly over-estimated, but above all it proved to be much more 
resilient to aberrant data. The potential of PLF to include all the new uses of electrical grids, 
as well as its capacity to also quantify the risk of appearance of constraints in the network 
makes it a yet perfectible tool, but a more and more relevant one for network dimensioning.

Introduction 
 
 
 
The share of decentralized renewable production is increasing. The electrical grids, 
designed for top-down power flows coming from conventional, centralized generation, need to 
be reevaluated to integrate this new intermittent production. However, the tools used so far to 
dimension the electrical networks are only based on extreme scenarios where there is no 
decentralized production. To avoid over-dimensioning the electrical networks and to better 
represent the actual requirements of electricity distribution, a new tool is needed. 
 
Modelling an intermittent, unpredictable and unreliable electricity production such as 
the one delivered by photovoltaic panels or wind turbines is quite a challenge because it 
corresponds to many different situations with very distinct constraints. Consequently, a 
probabilistic tool has been chosen, so that the occurrence of constraints is completely 
determined. 
 
There are mainly four types of constraints to take into account when studying electrical 
networks: power quality issues such as harmonics, which won’t be considered in this study, 
over-currents, which happen mostly in lines close to feeders’ heads, voltage drops, which are 
usually greater at the end of lines (a voltage drop below 5% is considered acceptable for MV 
grids), and high voltages, which can be caused by decentralized generation. The link between 
the injected active and reactive powers (both produced and consumed) and these constraints 
is made through a load-flow simulation. Hence the use of a probabilistic load-flow tool. 
 
This tool was previously developed and validated by EDF R&D. Nevertheless, it is still 
necessary to prove the relevance of its results when compared to actual measurements, and 
above all when compared to the current electrical grids dimensioning tool: the deterministic 
load-flow. Determining the probability of the extreme scenario used so far would for instance 
be a very relevant indicator. 
 
This Master’s thesis report will therefore aim at understanding the deterministic and the 
probabilistic load-flow tools in order to compare, analyze and discuss the results they provide. 
This study will incorporate measurements as inputs in both tools, to get comparable results for 
them, as well as to compare the simulated results with actual output measurements, and 
possibly validate their relevance. 
 
First of all, all the available field data will be presented, to set up the frame of the study. 
This will ease the presentation and the description of the implementation of the deterministic 
load-flow tool first, and then of the probabilistic load-flow tool. Finally, all the results will be 
compared and discussed, as well as their meaning for the electrical networks.

1.3 The Bagheera Tool 
 
1.3.1 For consumers 
 
The Bagheera tool, which will from now on only be referred to as “Bagheera”, is a 
PowerFactory script that was previously developed by EDF with one purpose. Bagheera has 
three input parameters: the temperature, the type of day (worked or holiday), and a risk 
parameter (0 or 1). The algorithm aggregates all LV consumers and producers after a given 
MV/LV transformer and make a consumption curve and a production curve which correspond 
to the consumption and production at each hour of an average day. 
The production curve doesn’t depend on any program inputs and finds all the information 
it needs in the PowerFactory network implemented by Enedis. However, the consumption 
curve depends on a few more parameters. All of them will not be discussed in this thesis, only 
the ones relevant to this study. Before discussing this parameter, it is useful to notice that the 
power factor is assumed to be constant and corresponds to tan(φ)=0.4. For each LV consumer, 
the consumption curve depends on the input temperature. This is extremely relevant because 
in France, a lot of consumers still have electric radiators, so the electricity consumption highly 
depends on the temperature as follows: 
𝑃ℎ= 𝐴ℎ+ 𝐵ℎ(𝑇𝑡ℎ𝑟𝑒𝑠ℎ𝑜𝑙𝑑−𝑇)+ 
where Ph is the active power consumption at each hour of day, T the input temperature, Ah and 
Bh two coefficients that depend on the hour of day, and Tthreshold is a threshold temperature 
below which the load starts increasing when the temperature decreases. The “+” indicates, as 
in all of this thesis, that all values below zero are considered to be zero. 
 
It is quite complicated to understand how Ah and Bh are calculated. Mostly, they depend 
on yearly readings of the consumer’s meter, but they also depend on the reliability of the history 
of the consumer, on the type of consumer, on the way the bills are calculated, etc. They also 
depend on the type of day: in this study, only working days are considered, in order to find 
regular patterns more easily. 
 
The threshold temperature is also an issue. For customers with a reliable history, it is 
equal to the non-heating temperature (TNC). This temperature, usually equal to 16°C, 
corresponds to the daily average temperature below which consumers start heating their 
houses, which involves an increase in the electricity consumption. Nevertheless, for many 
==PAGE:12==
 
- 11 - 
consumers, such a reliable history is not available and the threshold temperature is set by 
default to 6.8°C. This is presumably a source of error, because nobody would leave their house 
unheated at 10°C for example, but modifying Bagheera is not the objective of this study. 
 
Another parameter is called the “risk”, it corresponds to the incertitude on the active 
power consumption (because the consumed power can fluctuate a lot from one day to 
another). In Bagheera, enabling the risk means that the power is set to the 90th percentile of 
what it may reach. This probability was calculated by considering the uncertainty on Ah and Bh. 
The formula for the active power consumption of a LV consumer therefore becomes: 
𝑃ℎ= 𝐴ℎ+ 𝐵ℎ(𝑇𝑡ℎ𝑟𝑒𝑠ℎ𝑜𝑙𝑑−𝑇)+ + 𝐾90%𝜎ℎ√𝐸𝑛 
where K and σh depend on the type of consumer and of its history, and En represents the yearly 
consumption. It is important to notice that none of those three coefficients depend on 
temperature. 
 
There is one last noticeable parameter to take into account even if it isn’t one of the 
program’s input parameters: the saturation of the loads. In fact, in the electrical grid 
implemented in PowerFactory, Enedis put a maximum active power parameter for each LV 
load that its consumption can’t exceed. This phenomenon can be quite noticeable at very low 
temperature, but it will be neglected in all the calculations of this study, because its origin is 
quite uncertain and it doesn’t impact most of the calculations that are made. 
 
Once all these parameters are correctly computed by Bagheera, it aggregates all the 
LV consumers’ consumption curves into one global consumption curve which is the 
consumption at the MV/LV transformer. This aggregation is not a simple summation, it also 
incorporates some dispatching, as described in a totally different context in part 4.5. 
 
Figure 4 shows this daily average production curve for two stations where 
measurements are also available, in order to check their accuracy. Bagheera was run at 30°C 
to be above the threshold temperature, with the risk set to zero, and only measurements 
without temperature bias were selected for the comparison with the actual average day 
consumption curve.

1.3.2 For producers 
 
To generate the aggregated producers’ average production curve, the process is much 
simpler. First, the only decentralized producers on the studied electrical network are 
photovoltaic (PV) producers. Besides, the way it is implemented in Bagheera, their active 
power production only depends on the month of year, which represents the difference in 
sunshine hours. This means that it is not possible to have a probabilistic approach for 
producers (because a standard-deviation cannot be calculated as previously for consumers), 
but as no additional data is available, this is the best solution that was found during this thesis. 
In Figure 7, two examples of production curve are shown. 
 
 
Figure 7 highlights that the shape of the PV production curve is always the same (which 
is consistent with the fact that sunshine hours are the same throughout the network), only the 
peak power is different, and sunshine hours change according to the month. The effect of the 
weather (clouds) is completely neglected, as it is an average production curve. 
 
As for the reactive power produced or consumed, it is always zero, as the power factor 
is assumed to be constant and equal to 1.

1.4 Data pre-processing 
 
Not all the available data is used in this study. Only the total active power, the total 
tan(φ) and the average of the three line-to-line voltages are taken into consideration. Voltages 
will only refer to line-to-line voltages in the rest of this report. This is not an approximation, 
once the hypothesis of a balanced electrical network is made, this just corresponds to the 
needs of the study. Acknowledged measurements errors and time change were already 
discussed in 1.2, so only further data treatments will be discussed in this part. All data 
treatments are made using Matlab. The ones described in this part correspond to the Matlab 
script Pbrute2P1h_Pmaxj, which is available in appendix D. 
 
Once the data is extracted from the excel files, the first step is to remove points which 
are present several times so that their weight does not increase when probabilistic calculations 
are made. Then, aberrant data are removed: 
 
For tan(φ), this doesn’t apply because a good criterion for “aberrant” could not be found. 
Moreover, it will only be used in correlation with active power consumption data to 
calculate reactive power consumption, so non-aberrant data is validated through non-
aberrant active power measurements. 
 
 
For the active power, maximum and minimum values are set station by station, 
manually, by looking at the shape of the curve. Every point that doesn’t follow the global 
shape of the curve by far is considered aberrant and removed. In practice, a lot of 
values too close to zero are removed, but there aren’t any maximum values to set. 
 
 
For voltages, the treatment is the same as for active powers, except that there are fixed 
minimum and maximum values, which are applied to all stations. The selection is less 
restrictive, but accuracy is less needed than for active powers, since voltages are only 
used to check the results of the load-flows, they are not program inputs. 
 
 
Another criterion based on the derivative of the variables was also implemented, 
because it is not reasonable that they vary too fast, so tremendous variations must correspond 
to sensor errors. However, only a few points could be removed with this method and the 
calibration of the limit was uneasy, so, for simplification matters, this criterion was abandoned. 
 
Another issue here is to get the actual consumption at each station, to design a 
consumption model, despite the absence of data for solar production. This only impacts the 
active power data. In fact, it was explained in part 1.3.2 that Bagheera provides an average 
day solar production curve for each month. According to the month of the data, and the hour 
of day, the solar production given by Bagheera is therefore added to the data to get the actual

2. Deterministic Load-Flow 
 
2.1 Principle 
 
A load-flow is a simulation tool that links injected active and reactive powers at each 
station in an electrical network to the currents flowing in the lines and the voltages at each 
node. The kind of load-flow that is examined in this part is called deterministic load-flow 
because there is only one input value for the injected powers at each station of the network. 
As a consequence, there will only be one current value for each line and one voltage for each 
node as outputs. 
 
Enedis, the French DSO, has used until now a deterministic load-flow simulation to 
evaluate voltage drops in the MV grids, which is an essential part of network dimensioning. 
The value assigned to the powers at each station in the network is therefore crucial to 
determine, in order to be able to highlight if there can be constraints in the network, and if yes, 
where they are and how they could be solved. Two scenarios are considered by Enedis: the 
over-consumption or “winter” scenario, in which consumption is at its maximum (as defined 
below) and there is no production, and the over-production or “summer” case, in which 
production is at its maximum, and consumption is at 20% of its maximum. In this study, only 
the “winter” scenario was considered, therefore “constraints” means over-currents in line 
(currents above a certain threshold that depends on the line), and voltage drops between the 
feeder and the MV/LV station or the MV consumer above 5%. 
 
This “winter scenario” corresponds to an extreme situation for the network that hardly 
ever occurs, and that has to be precisely defined, and should correspond to very accurate, as 
well as relevant and adapted injected powers at each station.  
 
The first characteristic of this extreme situation is the fact that the production of 
renewables is neglected, as it should be expected that there will not be any wind or sun when 
the grid most needs it. The only production taken into account therefore comes from the HV 
grid, which is represented as constant source of voltage in this study (the S point in 
PowerFactory). 
 
The consumption, on the contrary, should be at its maximum in each station. The way 
it is calculated will be more thoroughly described in the next part 2.2. 
 
It was also underlined in the previous part 1.3 about Bagheera that the consumption 
was highly temperature-dependent in France. Consequently, the injected active and reactive 
powers have to correspond to a very low temperature. The temperature that was chosen by 
Enedis is called the minimum temperature (TMB). It is supposed to be calculated from day 
average temperatures over the last 30 years (the time step of the measurements being at least 
three hours). It then corresponds to the 30th coldest temperature, so that it is not reached more 
than once a year on average. Besides, it depends on the location of the network. For the one 
studied in this thesis, it is equal to -7.3°C. It can be noticed that, in the data available for this 
study, this temperature is never reached long enough to appear as a day average temperature. 
It can therefore be assumed that injected powers actually need to be interpolated. 
 
To properly dimension the network, an additional study incorporating the loss of one 
element of the network (such as a line) needs to be considered, but this thesis only focuses 
on the “winter” scenario, so with the network intact. 
 
==PAGE:20==
 
- 19 - 
 
 
From this description of the deterministic load-flow tool as a way to dimension the 
network, a few flaws can be noticed. Mainly, the production of renewables, even if it will always 
be equal to zero in extreme scenarios, dramatically impacts the network, causing voltage to 
rise locally, or adding currents flowing in lines that may not be designed for it. On the overall, 
the issue is whether or not the network is oversized to withstand this “winter” scenario. In other 
words, the level of risk of this scenario needs to be evaluated.

3. Probabilistic Load-Flow (PLF) description 
 
3.1 Principle 
 
The probabilistic load-flow is a new means of highlighting possible constraints in the 
network and their location, as well as a way to better take into account aspects neglected by 
the deterministic load-flow. 
 
Instead of making an extreme scenario which is represented by certain injected active 
and reactive powers at every station in the network, all situations are taken into account with 
a certain weight that corresponds to their probability of occurrence. In fact, the inputs of a 
probabilistic load-flow are probability density functions (PDF), and not one value, for all injected 
active and reactive powers at each station of the network. 
 
However, “injected” no longer means the power produced minus the power consumed 
at the station since consumed and produced powers have different PDFs. Consequently, for 
each station, there should be four PDFs, for active power and reactive power, for consumed 
power and produced power. 
 
Besides, even if the mathematical aspect of the PLF will be more thoroughly discussed 
in 3.3, the different PDFs used as inputs in the PLF cannot be correlated. However, the 
consumption of the different consumers is very likely to be correlated (because most people 
wake up at the same time, they cook at the same time, etc.), as well as the production of the 
different producers (sun and wind are homogenous in the geographical region studied). There 
are different ways to solve this issue: some of them are discussed in part 4.3. 
 
The probabilistic load-flow is then run, and from these inputs and the network’s data, it 
computes PDFs for output variables. For each station, it should give a PDF for the voltage, 
and for each line, it should give a PDF for the active and reactive power flows, as well as for 
the current flowing through the line. What the DSO is most interested in for dimensioning its 
network is the probability of occurrence of voltage or current deviations as a function of their 
magnitude: this is given by the quantiles of the output PDFs.

3.2 Interest 
 
 
Several advantages can be expected from using probabilistic load-flow in comparison 
to deterministic load-flow. First of all, the deterministic load-flow make up an extreme scenario 
that does not correspond to a real situation. If dispatching the feeders’ heads power flows helps 
lowering the consumptions to decent levels, the minimal temperature is only very punctually 
reached in the data, and not long enough to appear as a day average. What’s more, in Figure 
11, it is clear that P*max is calculated for station B6 in summer, put it is still extrapolated at the 
minimal temperature, setting the station at an active power consumption that is never reached. 
Even if the issue in that case was solved, it shows that the weight of some aberrant data can 
be way too important compared to its real consumption. Probabilistic load-flow, on the other 
hand, takes all situations into account, and they all have the same weight. The consumption 
of the agricultural consumer in summer is very likely to modify the shape of the PDF, but it will 
only be taken into consideration as a marginal trend. 
 
Another property is that the influence of temperature can be incorporated in the PLF. 
This is important when the PDF is created because, for instance, the average consumption 
depends on the temperature. Taking the total average would be less meaningful. 
 
In the extreme scenario used in the deterministic load-flow, renewable power 
generation is considered to be zero, because the coincidence of the absence of wind and sun 
and a consumption peak has to be withstood by the electrical network. However, renewable 
generation creates a production PDF at each station and consequently modifies the output 
voltage drops PDFs. As more and more decentralized production is likely to be connected to 
the electrical grid, reflecting its influence on it becomes more and more essential, not only to 
highlight the fact that some voltage drops may almost never happen, but also to show possible 
high voltages. 
 
The last major advantage of using probabilistic load-flow instead of deterministic load-
flow is that deterministic load-flow only determines the presence or not of constraints (as well 
as their amplitude) in the network in the extreme scenario. It gives no information about how 
often this scenario may occur, or how far it is from the average. On the contrary, probabilistic 
load-flow takes into account all possible scenarios and computes their probability. It will be 
interesting to find out to which quantile this extreme scenario corresponds to, so that its 
likelihood can be evaluated. 
 
Finally, the influence of many parameters can be incorporated in the probabilistic load-
flow, such as the hour of day, the number of customers connected to a MV/LV station, etc. 
This is a source of additional accuracy when it comes to network dimensioning, and enables 
the DSO to avoid oversizing lines because a parameter is set by default to its extreme scenario 
value.

3.3 Mathematical Implementation 
 
Once the independent input PDFs have been determined, there are several ways to 
run a probabilistic load-flow algorithm. Two of them are discussed here. 
3.3.1 Monte-Carlo Method 
 
When it comes to probabilistic analysis, the reference method is the Monte-Carlo 
method. In simple terms, a random number is simulated, according to the corresponding PDF, 
for each input active and reactive power for each producer and consumer in the network. The 
load-flow is then run on these simulated values, and one value is obtained for all output 
currents and voltages. The operation is repeated until there are enough output values to 
accurately compute a PDF from all these output values. 
 
This method has the advantage of being simple and accurate, but it takes many 
iterations to make good output PDFs. In this case, over twenty thousand iterations are 
necessary. This results in a very long calculation time (a few hours with a good computer), and 
therefore the method is not practical for Enedis, the DSO. 
 
However, this method was previously used to validate the tool that was finally used to 
run the probabilistic load-flow algorithm. The validation was made on the quantiles, especially 
the 90th one, because it corresponds to a norm defined by Enedis. 
3.3.2 Three-Point Estimation Method 
 
In order to get results with a decent computation time, a faster algorithm than Monte-
Carlo was developed and implemented in PowerFactory. It is called the Three-Point Estimation 
Method (TPEM), and consists in making discrete PDFs from the original continuous ones, so

Creating the PDFs for the different actors (consumers and producers) has been the 
main and most complicated focus of this thesis. The problem is to identify all the factors that 
modify the injected active or reactive power so that all is left is a “noise”, which will be called 
“error” from now on, only coming from the unpredictability of the consumers. Doing so, there 
shouldn’t be any bias left in the PDF that could hide a dependency between actors. At this 
point, it is possible to realize how complex finding the independent input PDFs can be. 
 
The TPEM algorithm was run with different input PDFs, to measure the influence of 
taking each new parameter into account. A version called TPEM_Brute exists, and does not 
consider any correlation between the variables. However, it proved to give results so 
inaccurate that they won’t even be discussed in the result section of this report. 
 
Producers’ electrical production (there is only PV in the electrical network considered) 
depends on many factors, such as sunshine hours, clouds, etc. However, no data is available 
on those matters. Consequently, a fixed value is used for each hour of day. This fixed value is 
the average on all months of the production curve given by Bagheera, as specified in 1.4. It 
would be fair to wonder why the influence of the month is not considered in this PLF: actually, 
the seasonal variations of consumption are very closely linked to the temperature and it is 
taken into consideration in the model described in 4.2. Running the TPEM for each month 
would multiply by 12 the number of load-flows to run and therefore dramatically slow the 
program for a limited interest since the influence of temperature is already incorporated. 
Besides, Bagheera only provides approximations of the solar production, refining such data is 
meaningless.  
 
The focus will therefore be on finding the dependencies of consumption. In the first 
interesting version of the TPEM algorithm, temperature and the hour of day are taken into 
account, as well as the influence of working days. Temperature not only impacts the average 
consumption but it also impacts the fluctuations that tend to be more important at lower 
temperatures, so at high consumptions. The hour of day also affects dramatically the 
consumption because every consumer has a routine that corresponds to working hours, meals, 
waking-up, etc. To be able to highlight that routine, only working days are taken into account 
in this whole study (calendar vacations such as Christmas, or New Year’s day are also 
removed from used data). The corresponding PowerFactory script is called TPEM_Hourly. 
 
However, the correlation common random variable (referred to as “correlation” in the 
rest of this report) between the different actors is still quite important, so it is assumed that 
some of the error depends on global trends. The second version of the program, 
TPEM_Hourly_correlated, splits the error into two parts: one common to all consumers, and 
the other one specific to each station. For simplification reasons, the common part is assumed 
to follow a normal PDF.

∀(𝑖,𝑗) 𝐶𝑜𝑣(𝑖, 𝑗) = 𝐶𝑜𝑣𝑎𝑟𝑖𝑎𝑛𝑐𝑒(𝜀′𝑖,ℎ,𝜀′𝑗,ℎ) 
 
It is therefore useful to use a principal component analysis to find the independent 
modes of the network. This basically consists in finding the eigenvalues and the eigenvectors 
of Cov. The eigenvectors would represent independent modes of the network, and the 
eigenvalues would be a quantification of their influence on the global behavior. 
 
This could even represent a source of optimization of the program since the principal 
component analysis only require to consider the biggest eigenvalues, so less load-flows would 
be needed. Instead of running two load-flows for each station plus two load-flows for the 
common part, plus one load-flow with all stations at their average power consumption, only 
two load-flows per eigenvalue considered would be needed (and five eigenvalues would 
probably lead to a good approximation of the results), plus the load-flow with all stations at 
their average power consumption. If all eigenvalues are considered, the results can be more 
accurate though. Anyway, the main advantage of this method is that all variables are 
independent with certainty. 
 
However, it is possible to calculate Cov(i,j) only if measurements are available for both 
station i and station j. Bagheera does not provide the time series it takes to make this 
calculation. Bagheera stations representing 90% of all stations, this solution was therefore not 
implemented. But in a network where measurements are available for all stations, it would 
surely be a more precise method that would lead to unbiased results.

Actually, implementing this calculation has proven to be quite unfruitful since it often 
gave aberrant values for the corrected moments (like complex values for the standard deviation 
for instance). This underlines the fact that the correlation between consumers was not 
completely taken into consideration with the model used, such as could be expected after part 
4.3. Consequently, as the random variables are not independent, their cumulants cannot be 
summed to get the feeders’ heads cumulant. This correction was therefore abandoned for the 
standard deviation, the skewness and the kurtosis. 
 
Nevertheless, the sum of the average of random variables being the average of the 
sum of random variables is still true even if they are not independent. Consequently, the 
average is still dispatched according to the formula written above. 
 
There are several MV consumers on the considered electrical network and for them, 
the problem is slightly different. In fact, only their subscribed power is available. Studying the 
MV consumers for which measurements are available, it was showed that their average active 
power consumption corresponds to their subscribed power divided by around 1.8, and that an 
intermediate standard deviation can be obtained by considering that their subscribed power is 
their 90th percentile, and then the actual standard deviation calculated from this assumption is 
divided by 10 to be more realistic. However, MV consumers cannot be assumed to have similar 
behaviors because their active power consumption corresponds to very different needs, so the 
correction on the average is also applied to them afterwards, as a way to reduce the influence 
of this approximation. 
 
4.5.2 For Reactive Power 
 
 
Dispatching reactive power consumption is only relevant if the model described in part 
4.4 is used. However, the mechanism described above to dispatch active power consumption 
cannot be applied in this case because reactive power consumption in the network also 
happens a lot in lines, and no linear relation between the reactive power consumed at the 
feeders’ heads and the sum of all the stations’ reactive power consumption exists. 
 
Consequently, a dynamic correction of this reactive power consumption was 
implemented directly in PowerFactory. Basically, the idea is to set a power factor to each 
station according to the model described in part 4.4 and then to increment or decrement it by 
very small steps in all the stations (where no measurements are available since measurements 
are supposed to be very reliable) and then run a load-flow calculation, until the right reactive 
power flow is achieved at the feeder’s head. 
 
This method only corrects the average of the reactive power consumption, but it is 
consistent with the active power consumption dispatching, plus correcting it more would not 
be relevant since the reactive power consumption model was purely empirical.

penetration in the electrical network, local voltage rises may become an issue.

that Bagheera is supposed to provide that quantile. This feature shows that the program is 
quite reliable and can be compared to the deterministic load-flow relevantly, since it is

5.3 Discussion 
 
On the overall, every new feature implemented in the model has increased the accuracy 
of the model. This may not appear clearly on the two examples previously shown, but, when 
all stations are considered, it becomes very relevant to keep them all. Even if they don’t always 
move the simulated results towards the measurements’ results, they improve the accuracy of 
the model. Their respective influence was shown in the previous part, and on the overall, what 
matters most is to correct the inputs with the feeders’ heads data. This somehow includes the 
influence of the hour of day and of the temperature. 
 
However, the influence of the temperature is not removed completely with this 
correction for the standard deviation, the skewness or the kurtosis. In fact, for the standard 
deviation, there remain inconsistencies due to the fact that the correlation was not completely 
removed. For skewness and kurtosis, inputs laws are either accurate because they come from 
measurements, or very inaccurate normal laws for which no correction is mathematically 
possible (they correspond to null skewness and normalized kurtosis). Consequently, the 
temperature dependency of the fluctuations should be better modelled: soothing the linear 
regression by making it a parabola for instance could avoid the peak in standard deviation 
around the non-heating temperature. 
 
The correlation does not impact much the results so far, but it may be mostly due to the 
fact that it is not entirely taken into consideration with the model that was chosen. This is 
probably one of the major and most complicated flaw of this study since it then prevents the 
correction of the standard deviation of the stations with the feeders’ heads data. A solution that 
was underlined in 4.3.2 is to have measurement devices set up at all stations, and then to do 
a principal component analysis. As Enedis is currently updating all electricity meters with new 
ones that will be able to collect hourly consumption data, this solution should become feasible 
in the years to come. The principal component analysis even has the potential to speed up the 
program by decreasing the required number of load-flows. 
 
The photovoltaic production connected to the network is also inappropriately modelled. 
So far, it is considered that there is a fixed production at each hour of day. Besides, the 
production level given by Bagheera is not confirmed by any kind of measurement. The best 
solution would be to have sunshine hours or even 10’ minutes data for each PV unit in the 
network, but another alternative is about to be implemented. In fact, data on the production of 
nearby PV plants was just found at the end of this study. It could be extrapolated to the PV 
units in the electrical grid considered in this thesis by multiplying the data by the ratio of the 
peak powers. This would only require the assumption that the weather is identical in locations

more or less in the same area, which seems reasonable. This feature has just been 
implemented, but won’t appear in this report because of the short time left. 
 
The reactive power injection model developed in 4.4 has also been proven to lack 
accuracy. If it fits the available data quite well for most stations, there are some stations with 
very different behaviors, and it is legitimate to assume that stations without measurements 
have peculiar behaviors as well. A deeper study on this matter, relying on more data is 
therefore needed. The main objective would be to find out the dependencies of the average 
value, because taking into account reactive power consumption fluctuations multiplies 
computing time by 2, and should therefore only be implemented if accurate enough. 
 
The previous paragraphs underline the great number of variables and functions that 
were implemented in the model. It is important to keep justifying their use because, if increasing 
the number of variables improves the accuracy of the model when compared to 
measurements, it could be due to an increased number of degrees of liberty, which may not 
correspond to physical phenomena. Consequently, it is essential to keep the model simple, 
because not only does it ensure that the phenomena taken into consideration are the ones 
actually significant for the study, but it also improves the calculation time. 
 
There remains one serious factor in the interpretation of the results that was mentioned 
before: the accuracy of the transformer ratio. This ratio is well-known to fluctuate with the power 
flow through the transformer, but it is quite complicated to find out how. According to some 
EDF researchers, the inaccuracy can reach between 1 and 2%. This corresponds to an 
imprecision in the voltage drops between 200 and 400V, which is the order of magnitude of 
the voltage drops! Nevertheless, according to the results shown in the previous parts, it is not 
likely that such high levels of fluctuations are reached. Otherwise, the simulated results should 
be much more different from the measurements. The important matter here is to remember 
that there exists this huge source of imprecision and that measurements cannot be relied on 
too much. Additional measurements on the transformer ratios could nevertheless solve this 
issue. Besides, during the presentation at their headquarters, Enedis researchers explained 
that the tap level could not be considered constant all the time, since it is managed separately, 
and without being recorded. 
 
If more data would be needed to check the results, it is not so sure that more input data 
are needed. Bagheera seems to provide simple but realistic inputs and then outputs when it 
comes to the interesting quantiles (80th, 90th and 95th). However, one of the main advantages 
of the PLF is that it doesn’t only provide the 90th quantile, which the DLF is already supposed 
to do. A finer management of the electrical network investments would therefore require 
measurements. Besides, the accuracy of Bagheera on the highest quantiles seems to come 
from an overestimation of the standard deviation, whereas it should correspond to the influence 
of other moments. 
 
If more accurate laws than normal distribution, or Gram-Charlier distributions are 
needed, the TPEM may prove to be limited since it only takes into account the first four 
moments. The TPEM can be compared to a Fourier decomposition, where only the first four 
harmonics are considered, because their magnitude is quickly decreasing with their order. 
However, for the very complex input laws observed in measurements, four harmonics might 
not be enough to consider all their complexity. This seems particularly true because for station 
A4, the kurtosis can reach values above 10, whereas it is expected to be around 3, which 
corresponds to a normal distribution. Besides, it was underlined in 3.3 that the TPEM made 
other huge approximations that could require more justifications. 
==PAGE:50==
 
- 49 - 
 
These are inner issues of the TPEM and they therefore cannot really be solved, but 
getting back to the Monte-Carlo method and using calculation servers may need to be 
considered. However, Enedis, which ordered this study, insisted that the PLF should be 
operational on an isolated laptop. 
 
Finally, all the information integrated in the PowerFactory model of the electrical 
network is unchecked. In particular, line parameters appear to be realistic when compared to 
the calculations methods’ results for such parameters [4], but there exist many studies 
highlighting that those parameters could be easily over- or under-estimated [5]. A sensitivity 
analysis proved that very small variations of those parameters lead to very different results. 
 
5.4 Perspectives 
 
 
The PLF tool was validated on voltage drops, but there are several other issues to take 
into account when dimensioning the network. The most important one is probably the currents 
in the network’s lines. The TPEM has been proven to provide poor results for currents, but 
there may be ways to improve the accuracy of the algorithm without slowing it too much. 
Moreover, if power quality issues are usually treated separately (yet they should be 
considered), currents and voltage drops are the determining factors when dimensioning the 
network. 
 
The results of this internship were presented to Enedis. They were satisfied with them. 
The results confirm to some extent the relevance of the current DLF method, since it gives a 
good approximation of the 90th quantile, when no unusual data wrongs the process. 
Consequently, they were not keen on implementing PLF in all their studies yet, but they would 
like to develop it, to make it more accurate to confirm these conclusions. 
 
However, with the development of decentralized production and the modern electric 
meter in households, the PLF will be the only tool able to provide a reliable information about 
the resilience of the network. If most of the development process is now done at the end of this 
internship despite the flaws highlighted in the previous part, there still remains work to do on 
the automation of the process, so that Enedis agents could only press run with the appropriate 
input in the appropriate folder. 
 
Once this automation step achieved, it will be possible to study the behavior of the 
network with the loss of one element. Only then decisions will be able to be made on the 
construction of new lines or the rearrangement of some parts of the network.

Conclusion 
 
 
The aim of this Master’s thesis was to make the PLF tool operational for network 
dimensioning and quantify its potential to change the way MV electrical networks are currently 
dimensioned by Enedis, the French DSO. There now exist functioning scripts that serve this 
function, and, despite the fact that they are not user-friendly yet, they still provide a lot of 
information as for how the electrical network modifications can be foreseen. 
 
The field data, which was collected every ten minutes over more than 4.5 years, was 
used to check the results as well as to get better inputs. Dealing with aberrant values was a 
large part of this study and still, more accurate definitions of “aberrant” are required for this 
step. 
 
The very little number of stations equipped with measurement devices does not enable 
them to make a significant difference in the results, and the increased accuracy they provide 
comes with complex computation issues. Consequently, using only data coming from 
Bagheera ensures an equal treatment of all stations, as well as savings in terms of complexity. 
However, as input distributions are highly unsymmetrical, which results are also expected to 
be, measurements inputs can be essential to introduce some asymmetry because they are the 
only ways to compute skewness and kurtosis. 
 
More measurements would also help dealing with the correlation. Whether it is by a 
principal component analysis or by another model yet to be designed, it has been proven to 
be a relevant feature of stations’ active power consumption. More measurements could also 
lead to a different way to deal with the temperature dependency of the fluctuations, which is 
still uncertain. The problem here is more to find a realistic interpretation of the model than to 
design one that fits data. 
 
At first sight, PLF seems to confirm the relevance of DLF for the “winter” scenario 
because only a slight over-estimation of the 90th quantile is shown for feeder A. However, DLF 
is quite vulnerable to aberrant data, because it is not easy to identify them, and because they 
can have a much bigger impact on the final results than in the PLF. This was proven to be 
extremely true for feeder B’s head data. 
 
PLF also provides more information, because the probability of extreme situations is 
calculated, and therefore a finer management of the electrical network becomes possible. If 
the over-dimensioning of the network does not look significant today for Enedis, new ways of 
studying networks may require such accuracy. 
 
The main advantage of PLF compared to DLF is that it is more adapted to the new uses 
of the network. The first reason being that it actually shows the impact of decentralized 
renewable production, whose share is increasing everywhere. It also has a better potential to 
incorporate electric vehicles or demand management, whose influence an extreme scenario 
would not reflect. PLF will therefore become more and more essential for electrical network 
topology analysis, so implementing it and automatizing it are crucial matters. This will be all 
the easier as new electric meters are currently being installed in all households in France, 
which will eventually provide enough data to run more accurate PLF. 
 
Nevertheless, PLF is only required until more precise forecast can be implemented. 
PLF gives statistics about the network, and with the increasing amount of information available, 
it is more and more plausible to think in terms of time forecast, possibly on large time scales.

𝜎(𝑉1)² = 𝑓1² ∗𝜎(𝑃1)² + 𝑓2² ∗𝜎(𝑃2)² 
 
Second case: the correlation is taken into account: 
 
 
In that case, the correlation can be written by writing (C, X1 and X2 are independent): 
 
𝑃1 = 𝐶+ 𝑋1 
𝑃2 = 𝐶+ 𝑋2 
So: 
𝑉1 = 𝑓1 ∗𝑋1 + 𝑓2 ∗𝑋2 + (𝑓1 + 𝑓2) ∗𝐶+ 𝑉0  
Finally: 
𝜎(𝑉1)² = 𝑓1² ∗𝜎(𝑋1)² + 𝑓2² ∗𝜎(𝑋2)² + (𝑓1 + 𝑓2)² ∗𝜎(𝐶)² 
 
𝜎(𝑉1)² = 𝑓1² ∗𝜎(𝑃1)² + 𝑓2² ∗𝜎(𝑃2)² + 2𝑓1𝑓2 ∗𝜎(𝐶)² 
 
 
An additional term can therefore be observed in the case of the correlation. It introduces 
some coupling between both stations because f1 and f2 are involved. With the same reasoning, 
it could be shown that all moments should be affected in a similar way (even if C has a normal 
distribution), except the average.

Appendix E: TPEM Pseudo-code 
 
 
This appendix aims at describing more accurately how the PowerFactory script 
TPEM_h_correl_solar_correc_reactive_reca works. It will take the form of pseudo-code but it 
will include some DPL code as well. Stars (*) indicate a reference to a PowerFactory script put 
at the end of this appendix. 
- 
Before running the actual TPEM, it imports all the useful data and store in matrices 
(IntMat) in a folder contained inside the script: 
 
Import of the standard deviation of the common probability density function for 
each hour of day. 
 
 
 
Import of the solar production of each station, at each hour of day, averaged on 
all the months of the year. 
 
 
 
Import of the average reactive power flow at the two feeders’ heads, for each 
hour of day. 
 
 
 
- 
Then, for each hour of day: 
 
Import of the first four moments of all the stations corresponding to the right 
hour of day. 
 
 
 
Initialization of matrices that will eventually store output moments, and of Lines, 
Loads and Terminals groups by turning them into maps (IntDplmap). 
 
 
 
Three-Point Estimation Method 
 
 
All actors (loads and generators with probability density functions) are 
set to their average value. This also generates a power factor according 
to the model described in part 4.4. 
 
 
The power factors of all stations are corrected until the right reactive 
power flow at the feeders’ heads is reached. These power factors are 
stored in a matrix*. 
 
 
The common part of all stations is set to the two other values of the 
TPEM input distribution calculated with input moments (dC variable)**, 
and load-flows are run each time. Results are recorded for all stations. 
Actors are set to their average value again. 
 
 
For each actor 
==PAGE:84==
 
- 83 - 
 
The TPEM active power distribution input is calculated with the 
input moments***. 
 
The value of the active power of the actor is then set to the lower 
point of the distribution (xi variable)**. 
 
A load-flow is run and results are recorded for all stations****. 
 
The value of the active power of the actor is then set to the higher 
point of the distribution (xi variable)**. 
 
A load-flow is run and results are recorded for all stations****. 
 
The actor’s active power is set again to its average value**. 
 
 
For each actor 
 
The value of the tan(φ) corresponding to the cos(φ) = power 
factor of the actor is set to the lower point of the normal 
distribution (yi variable)**. 
 
A load-flow is run and results are recorded for all stations****. 
 
The value of the tan(φ) corresponding to the cos(φ) = power 
factor of the actor is set to the lower point of the normal 
distribution (yi variable)**. 
 
A load-flow is run and results are recorded for all stations****. 
 
The actor’s power factor is set again to its average value (which 
is stored in a matrix)***. 
 
 
A final load-flow is run with all actors at their average value and the 
actors’ raw moments are calculated.
