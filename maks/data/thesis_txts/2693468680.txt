Abstract
In real-life situations such as the cocktail party scenario, listeners may experience diﬃculties focusing on dif-
ferent simultaneously presented stimuli, e.g. speech intelligibility or source identiﬁcation, and may prioritize
their attention to one or another. A complex situation like this could be properly achieved but could result
in an increase of cognitive load on the listeners, reporting fatigue at the end of the day. In current audiology
practice, these type of behavioral tests are commonly presented through a loudspeaker-array. However, these
may not be accessible to all. Virtual reality (VR) aims to be a tool for testing with a higher degree of immer-
sion by including both audio and visual information, with more available resources, in this case, replacing
the loudspeaker array for headphone-based audio presentation.
For this reason, this study ﬁrst focused on validating that localization abilities do not diﬀer signiﬁcantly
depending on the acoustic presentation method. Sixteen normal hearing subjects underwent a localization
test in which they had to identify the location of the source by pointing with a VR controller in the virtual
environment. The sources were spatialized between 29 loudspeakers placed around a half-sphere (0° - 350°
azimuth) at 4 elevation rings (0°, ±45° and 90°). The audio stimuli was chosen to be noise burst and continuous
noise to evaluate the eﬀect of using spatial cues on the responses. The results showed no signiﬁcant eﬀects
depending on the presentation method and best performance with continuous audio.
After this stage, a speech in noise and localization test was performed with spatialized binaural audio
presented through headphones.
Ten normal hearing subjects participated in a speech intelligibility task
(Danish HINT) in which the target source was presented from one of seven discrete positions between −90
and +90° azimuth. The subject’s task was to repeat the sentence, identify the source (single task), or to do
both (dual task). The performance of both speech and localization tasks was analysed by means of percentage
correct scoring and compared between single and dual task. Simultaneously, pupillometry was recorded and
used to investigate the additional cognitive demand on the listener when both tasks were presented as dual
task. While the percent performance showed no signiﬁcant diﬀerences on single and dual task, pupillometry
revealed an increase of pupil dilation for the dual task, suggesting an increase in cognitive load and listening
eﬀort. These results show good evidence that VR could be used as a tool for conducting behavioral and
physiological tests. Pupillometry gives an insight on another dimension of challenge and diﬃculty on the
subjects’ usage of cognitive resources, even when the behavioral measures show no diﬀerences in performance
level. However, to be able to have a reliable system, the spatialized audio played through headphones needs
further investigation. Even if the headphones are not as good as the loudspeakers in terms of perception and
performance, it gives more accessibility to these type of tests but further improvement needs to be made.

INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
Standard tests in clinical audiology, namely for spatial hearing or speech intelligibility, typically use a small set
of loudspeakers to present sound during the testing. However, not all clinics may have the space or resources
to setup or maintain these physical loudspeaker arrays. Virtual reality (VR) and virtual environments (VE)
provide new opportunities for clinics and researchers to test patients and to evaluate hearing abilities in
an audio-visual setup with a higher degree of immersion than in a normal physical setup [1]. By using a
head-mounted display (HMD), the patient can get a realistic simulation of almost any visual environment,
even while being in a physically small place, and the acoustic environments can be simulated with spatialized
audio presented over headphones, giving freedom to develop novel ways of testing patients.
In real-life situations like in the well-known “cocktail party” scenario [5, 4], sound sources may come
from diﬀerent directions and the listeners must direct their attention not only to the content of what is
spoken but to identiﬁcation of the speakers and their locations as well.
In optimal listening conditions
in a complex situation like this, NH listeners are generally able to understand speech and communicate
eﬀortlessly. However, when speech understanding is degraded by background noise or hearing loss, and there
is a deprivation of spatial cues, the listeners may experience greater diﬃculty understanding speech and
localizing sounds [2, 17]. In cases like these, speech comprehension becomes more reliant on explicit cognitive
working memory processes to ﬁll in the gaps [32, 30].
Individuals often report an increase in fatigue in
these conditions because it requires greater eﬀort to understand what is being said. The cognitive processes
required during speech comprehension are associated with more eﬀortful listening even at high performance
levels. Listening eﬀort, also known as cognitive load, has been deﬁned as “the extent to which the demands
imposed by the task at a given moment consume the resources available to maintain successful execution”
[25]. Therefore, the amount of eﬀort that a person is employing increases with an increase in diﬃculty in the
task, as long as the listener is engaged in the task.
Pupillometry has been commonly used to measure listening eﬀort. Pupil dilation reﬂects the amount of
processing demands associated with a task, with pupil size enlarging with increasing mental eﬀort, cognitive
load, and task complexity (e.g., speech in noise, degraded speech, etc.) [31, 32]. Although there is no direct
relation between the correct performance in a listening task with the listening eﬀort a participant exerts,
pupillometry may oﬀer insight into the cognitive demands and diﬃculties patients are experiencing even when
they are able to complete tasks with high accuracy [6]. Another commonly used method for investigating
listening eﬀort is the introduction of an additional cognitive demand during testing. Both tasks are presented
separately and their accuracy is evaluated. When presenting both tasks simultaneously, the performance of
the listener can be the same for a primary task (e.g., speech processing), but there could be a decrease on
accuracy on the secondary task when presented along with a primary task [8, 6]. We hypothesize that the
pupil dilation response will reﬂect the additional resources allocated when a listener must maintain a primary
task with the additional demands of a second, even when there is not any reduced task-accuracy; additional
demands and eﬀort can be also seen in slower response times, engagement, and overall performance [11].
In this study we will therefore use the VR equipment to investigate whether pupillometry can reﬂect the
listening eﬀort exerted under diﬀerent test conditions (primary and secondary tasks).
In current audiology practice, the testing methods may not capture the complexity of listening situations
experienced by hearing impaired (HI) patients in their daily life, such as spatial speech in noise [6]. Introduc-
ing realism in a speech intelligibility test may be more representative of real-life situations, and hence, may
be better for identifying hearing diﬃculties that are not detected in a normal laboratory environment [19].
HMD-based VR aims to to be a more ecologically valid clinical tool as it facilitates realism and pupillom-
etry measurements simultaneously, which makes it particularly suitable for clinical use; VR could therefore
give a better estimate of what patients are experiencing in their day-to-day life and ultimately, facilitate
individualized diagnosis and rehabilitation options.
There is currently little information available on the precision and performance of a spatialized audio en-
vironment compared to the more commonly used loudspeaker-based sound source when doing clinical testing
with a VR headset. For this reason, the ﬁrst step of the present study is to validate that the performance

of the VR sound environment is comparable and interchangeable when using binaural audio played through
headsets and with audio coming from physical loudspeaker arrays. To do so, spatial hearing tests will be
performed to asses localization abilities of NH listeners in a pre-built VR environment, evaluating and com-
paring the accuracy in both presentation methods. Since previous studies have shown an improvement in
sound localization scores when visual information is provided, as well as when showing the hand-pointer from
the VR controllers [26, 1], the participants will be provided with both during the tests. We hypothesize that
this test will show minimum and comparable sound localization error, regardless of the presentation method
or the type of stimuli.
After the validation stage, we will then use VR with spatialized binaural audio played through headphones
to asses the additional cognitive demands that localization may have when added to a speech perception
task. For this, the participants will be evaluated in three conditions: ﬁrst, a speech recognition task will be
completed where the target speech will be presented from diﬀerent locations and the participant’s task will
only be to repeat the speech; second, the listeners task will only be to identify the location of the sound
source; and a third condition, in which the subject will perform the same speech recognition task but also
will be asked to identify the location of the talker in the VE. The pupil dilation response will be measured
with pupillometry as the participant is completing each of the tasks using methods similar to the ones in
[28]. When comparing the pupil traces across the corresponding tasks (speech intelligibility and localization),
we expect that the pupil dilation will be larger when both tasks are presented simultaneously, reﬂecting the
additional demands that localization places on the listener during a speech recognition task.

Methodology
2.1
Visual and audio environment
For the VR, the visual scene was displayed on an HMD (HTC Vive system, HTC Corporation, New Taipei
City, Taiwan). For rendering the visual scenes, the Unity software was utilized (Unity Technologies, San
Francisco, California, USA) along with the SteamVR plug-in (Valve Corporation, Bellevue, WA) and the
HTC Vive Pro headset. The calibration of the visual scene was performed with the standard VR room setup
included with SteamVR. When using the loudspeaker-array, the alignment with the real world was done by
placing a HTC Vive tracker, correcting for any azimuth rotational inaccuracies and front-back oﬀset.
When listening to a sound in a natural environment, normal-hearing (NH) listeners make use of head
movement to pinpoint the exact location of a sound source in a three-dimensional space by using diﬀerent
localization cues and reducing any front-back confusion [16, 21]. The three primary spatial hearing cues are
interaural time diﬀerences (ITD) , and interaural level diﬀerences (ILD) , and spectral cues due to reﬂections
of sound waves around the body and pinnae. In VR, headphones are the easiest and most popular audio
solution for presenting the sounds. However, when using a HMD, the spatial cues can lose their accuracy as
the interaction between ITDs and ILDs relate to the head-related transfer function (HRTF) and are unique
to each listener [10, 13, 16]. A faulty headphone calibration or setup could cause front-back confusion and
localization inaccuracies, as well as reduce the immersive sensation of the VE for the listeners [27].
For the binaural reproduction, the 3D Tune-in toolkit was implemented for audio spatializitaion as a
plugin for Unity [7]. A set of HRTFs measured from a KEMAR dummy head microphone was used for the
acoustic spatialization [9]. In this database, 1550 positions were measured for each of the subjects in the
horizontal plane from 0° to 360°, and with elevations from −90° to +90°. This is an important annotation,
as these elevation angles give the range in which the sound sources should be localized.
The loudspeaker array consisted of 29 units of reference Genelec 8341 AP. The audio scene was rendered
with the TASCAR Toolbox for the Acoustic Scene Creation and Rendering [12]. The discrete positions of
the loudspeakers were equalized in level, delay, and magnitude response so that the sounds physical sound
could come from all around the half-sphere. The virtualized audio sources for the loudspeakers allowed the
sounds to be played from any virtual position of them. For virtualization of the sound sources, the ”nearest
speaker” mode was applied to playback the audio.
2.2
Validation of headphone-based reproduction
The validation tests were conducted in a semi-anechoic room of dimensions (7.9m × 6.0m × 3.5m). The
loudspeakers in the room are placed in a half-sphere of a 3-dimensional space, at 4 diﬀerent ring elevations:
0° (ear level), ±45°, and 90° (i.e., directly above the head), with 16, 6, 6 and 1 loudspeakers uniformly
distributed in the rings, respectively, with a total of 29 loudspeakers used. The listener position was centered
in the middle of the sphere of radius r = 2m, facing loudspeaker #1 at 0° elevation, adjusting the chair level
so that the subject’s ear achieved the height h = 1.62m. An illustration of the loudspeaker array setup can
be seen in Figure 1, in which the 4 elevation rings are shown with the loudspeakers numbered clockwise.

2.2.1
Subjects and procedure
Sixteen self-reported normal hearing subjects participated in the ﬁrst validation study, with ages ranging from
22 to 59 (33.7 years old on average). After conducting the experiment, headphone calibration was found to
be unbalanced, with the right side having a higher sound level and producing a bias towards this side. Due
to a uncertainty of the error that this could introduce in the results, a second validation study was conducted
by inviting back seven of these subjects (28 years old on average) for a re-test of this presentation method.
In this second part, the headphone balancing was ﬁxed and the 6 loudspeakers placed at the bottom were
4
==PAGE:14==

removed for simpliﬁcation of the test and thus, elevation at -45° was disregarded for the second validation
study.
The test consisted of a localization task in which modulated pink noise (20 Hz - 20000 Hz) as played
either as a noise burst (4 sinusoidal pulses, each having 100 ms duration, to make 400 ms in total) or as
a continuous noise (i.e., pulses playing repeatedly). The stimuli was calibrated for both headphones and
loudspeakers using an external SPL meter and a custom calibration procedure to have the audio ﬁle played
at 68 dB for noise burst or at 57 dB for continuous noise. These levels were chosen so they were comfortable
and audible to the subjects. While wearing the HMD, listeners were asked to identify the location of the
sound and point to the loudspeaker which they thought was the one playing the sound using a virtual laser
pointer (VR hand-controller). Before each stimulus, the subjects were to look at the center point (0º azimuth,
0 º elevation) to ensure they were facing forward. In the case of the noise burst, head movement was only
permitted after the audio was played. On the other hand, for continuous noise, the subjects were allowed
to move their head during the trial. This is because studies have shown an enhancement in localization
cues when the eye gaze is directed towards the sound source [1]; therefore, head movement was allowed in
the continuous noise condition so that the participants could make use of the spatial cues to localize the
loudspeaker.
A trial started when the audio began, and ended after the subject’s response. In one session, all the
loudspeaker sources were presented once in randomized order (29 trials per session). The ﬁrst two sessions
were done with the noise burst and the consecutive two with the continuous sound, with a total of four
sessions. The subjects performed this task over headphones and with the loudspeaker array (yielding a total
of eight sessions per subject); the diﬀerent test conditions are summarized in Table 2). The subject responses
were stored as spherical coordinates along with the correspondent loudspeaker number.
The subjects were given oral and written instructions prior to the test and no training session was
performed prior to the test in order to avoid bias or learning eﬀect from the subjects regarding the presentation
method.

2.2.2
Analysis of azimuth and elevation errors
The localization accuracy was computed across stimuli and presentation method for both the azimuth and
elevation, and the accuracy of the subjects’ responses was analysed by ﬁtting a linear mixed model (LMM) to
the azimuth and elevation errors. Responses in which the azimuth error was larger than 90° were removed from
further analysis. For the azimuth error, the sources located in elevations diﬀerent from 0° were disregarded;
thus, only the sixteen loudspeakers in the horizontal plane were considered in the model.
The type of
stimuli, presentation method, source location, as well as the two-way interaction of these were considered
ﬁxed eﬀects. For the elevation error, the type of stimuli, presentation method, source elevation and source
azimuth locations were treated as ﬁxed eﬀects, as well as the interaction between source elevation with
presentation method and type of stimuli. For both LMM, the subjects were treated as random eﬀects.

2.3
Speech in noise and localization test
The speech-in-noise test was conducted in an audiometric test room of dimensions (5m × 4m × 2.4m),
complying with the standard ISO 8253-2 regarding background noise [15], as only the VR system (HMD,
hand-held controller, trackers) and headphones were needed. The VE was set to be an egocentric interface,
where the subject was placed in a custom-built grey room with 7 semi-transparent unisex humanoid bodies
sitting on chairs around the space. These were placed at the locations in which the acoustic source positions
were, as previously done by [18]. Figure 3 shows the visual scene with the 7 possible talkers. The sources
were all placed at ear level (0° elevation) and in the frontal plane from -90° (left) to 90° (right) azimuth, with
30° separation between them. The distance from the listener to the humanoids was set to a radius of r =
2.5m.

2.3.1
Subjects and procedure
Ten normal-hearing Danish native speakers participated in the experiment, with ages ranging from 24 to
58 (34.9 years old on average). The mean audiogram is shown in Figure 4 in which the average pure tone
audiometry (PTA) threshold was 2 dB and 3.3 dB for the left and right ear, respectively. The subjects were
given oral and written instructions prior with a task familiarization session before the main test in which
they received feedback on the localization correctness.

Figure 4: Individual audiograms of 10 study subjects. Average values are shown in bold (34.9 years old
average).
For this experiment, the Danish hearing in noise test (HINT) corpus was used [22]. All audio ﬁles were
adjusted to have -20dBFS, and the babble noise was presented as a diﬀuse noise at a sound pressure level
(SPL) of 65 dB. The noise was presented 3 seconds prior to the target sentence, with randomized 0-1 second
augmentation, and ended 3 seconds after. The 5-word sentence was presented from either of the 7 possible
talker positions, and the subjects were scored on how many correct words were repeated back from 0 to 5.
The subjects were asked to listen to the audio while remaining still in the reference position and focus on a
cross in front of them (0° azimuth). During the audio playback, the pupil response was recorded. After the
audio had stopped, the subjects were asked to repeat the sentence they heard and/or to localize the talker,
depending on the test condition. One trial was referred to as the speech and noise presentation, as well as
the subject’s response.
Three test conditions were tested: (1) Speech-only, (2) Localization-only and (3) Speech and localization.
For the ﬁrst condition, the subjects were only instructed to repeat what they heard and disregard the location
of the sound, then continue with the next trial. For the second condition, the listeners were only instructed
to identify the sound source disregarding any speech recognition. Finally, for the third condition, aside from
repeating the sentence, they were also asked to move the head and localize the sound source by pointing
to one of the humanoids with the virtual laser pointer (hand-held controller). After this, they returned to
the reference position to proceed with the next trial. A summary of the conditions and short description
is provided in Table 3. There was no restriction in response time, and each trial ended after the subjects
submitted their answers. No localization or response feedback was given during the test.
Each test condition was repeated twice, each time with 20 trials, i.e. one list from the HINT corpus. A
total of 7 lists were presented to the subjects: 1 training list for the familiarization task and 6 lists for the
main experiment (2 per condition). These lists were randomized in order to avoid repetition of the same
list for the diﬀerent test conditions across all subjects. The presentation order of the three conditions were
also randomized for all subjects, with a balance presentation between them. However, the two lists of each
condition were presented subsequently. The location of the sources were repeated at least twice within a list,
with a total of at least 4 responses for each location per condition.
The signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) was ﬁxed for each individual throughout the whole test.
After the
familiarization task and prior to the main test, an adaptive speech-in-noise test was performed in order to
estimate the subject’s individual speech reception threshold (SRT). As it has been shown in previous work,
speech recognition is better for words presented spatially at ±90° than at 0° [3]; therefore, the SRT was
determined at a 50% correct sentence recognition measured from the front (0° azimuth), allowing a higher

speech reception threshold for the other talker positions. Maximum pupil responses are normally observed
at a 50% point [24], and as the SNR was chosen to yield on 50% correct sentence recognition (from the front)
or above (for peripheral positions), any ﬂoor or ceiling eﬀects can be avoided when adding the localization
to the speech-in-noise task [23]. In this way, there’s room for the extra cognitive load that the subject might
experience with the secondary task. In order to calculate the individualized SNR, one list was presented
adaptively to the subject so that the level was adjusted from trial to trial based on the subject’s correct or
incorrect response to the previous stimuli.

2.3.2
Pupillometry
With the aim of quantifying listening eﬀort while doing the speech-in-noise test, the pupil dilation was
recorded with Pupil Capture software (Pupil Labs GmbH, Berlin, Germany), where the cameras were mounted
on the lenses of the VR headset. Before starting a test, a calibration of the eye-tracker parameters was
performed for each subject to improve pupil detection.
As a ﬁrst step, the cameras were positioned as
straight and centered as possible in reference to the pupil by adjusting (tightening or loosening) the straps on
the top of the VR headset. The minimum and maximum pupil size settings were then adjusted to visualize
the full range for all eye movements, as well as the intensity range, in order to cover the pupil and minimizing
possible light reﬂections. These settings were veriﬁed by looking at the conﬁdence value, in which values
above 0.85 were considered suﬃcient.
As a ﬁnal step, a luminance control was performed in the visual
environment to reduce any pupil dilation changes caused by luminance instead of cognitive activity.
All the trials within a condition were time-aligned to the sentence onset, with a sampling frequency of 60
Hz. The pupil data from the ﬁrst 6 trials was removed from further analysis, as suggested by [31]. In the
post-processing, data point values that were more than 2.5 standard deviations smaller than the mean value
of the pupil dilation were categorized as eye-blinks. These were calculated by interpolating the values 50ms
before the blink and 150ms after. These values were set to Not-a-Number (NaN), and a linear interpolation
was done for the ﬁve points before and after the detected blink. When more that 35% of the data was missing
in a trial due to blinks, it was discarded and not considered for further analysis.
A baseline for each trial was calculated during the noise-only segment by calculating the mean pupil
dilation value, measured 600 ms prior to the sentence onset.
The trials which contained blinks during
the baseline time were removed from further analysis. The data was baseline corrected by subtracting this
averaged value from the data. After this correction, all the trials within a same condition were averaged across
subjects. Subsequently, the peak pupil dilation (PPD) was calculated for each subject and each condition.
The PPD was computed by taking the maximum value after the onset of the sentence, from seconds 3.5 to
5.5, as the response of the pupil is normally seen up to 1s after the end of the stimulus [31, 29].
2.3.3
Analysis of task performance and pupillometry
The performance for both speech and localization tasks was evaluated. For speech, the percentage of correct
word recognition in a sentence was calculated, and for localization, correct target choice was assigned 100%,
and any other choice was assigned 0%. The tasks were assessed as a single task and also when performed
simultaneously. To compare both situations, the subjects’ responses were analysed with a one-way ANOVA,
in which the independent variable was the condition. Furthermore, the eﬀect of the azimuth location on the
responses was also investigated in addition to the condition by ﬁtting a LMM to the performance score. The
8
==PAGE:18==
2
METHODOLOGY
condition and the azimuth target location were treated as ﬁxed eﬀects, as well as their interaction, whereas
the subjects were considered random eﬀects.
For pupillometry, a one-way ANOVA was applied to see the eﬀect of the condition on the PPDs. Paired
one-tailed t-test were then performed, as it was hypothesized that a higher pupil dilation will be seen for
Condition 3, when speech and localization tasks were done simultaneously, compared to conditions 1 or 2.
The PPD, however, is only calculated as the maximum value during a particular time interval and could
not show the changes or trends in the pupil response over time. For this reason, a growth curve analysis
(GCA) was used as the statistical method to investigate the changes in pupil dilation for all three conditions
over time, as measured in other studies [31, 20, 29]. This is done by ﬁtting a third-order polynomial to the
pupil traces during a certain time course, which is then used to analyse time-dependent diﬀerences over this
time among conditions and subjects. The GCA was calculated for the listening window (0.5-2.3 seconds
relative to sentence onset). The pupil dilation was modelled as a function of the ﬁxed term, condition and
the interaction with the independent linear, quadratic and cubic terms. The subjects were treated as random
eﬀects with the interaction of the independent terms.

DISCUSSION
4
Discussion
4.1
Validation of headphone-based reproduction
The main interest of this part of the study was to evaluate and compare the accuracy of two presentation
methods, loudspeakers and headphones. The analysis on the signed azimuth error showed overall comparably
small diﬀerences between both presentation methods, with a few exceptions for angles -45° and 90°, and no
important bias towards left or right. The stimuli that showed more favorable results (smaller azimuth errors)
was continuous noise, which can be attributable to the use of the localization cues. The accuracy was better
for azimuth angles than for elevation and the subjects chose loudspeakers close to the source, rather than
focusing on the elevation. This could also be connected to the use of ILD and ITD, which are normally not
enough to distinguish on elevation diﬀerences. Additional investigation could be focus on studying the eﬀect
having continuous audio but without allowing head movement. This could exhibit if the better results in
continuous noise are due to either the length of the stimuli, head movement or a combination of both.
From the spatial perception analysis, it can be seen that the azimuth error increases when the source
location is towards the back hemisphere.
This can be caused by the lack of visual information and the
diﬃculty to point at the source (as the chair was ﬁxed and the participants had to turn their torso in order
to point at any back loudspeaker). In terms of elevation, a LMM showed that the error was not signiﬁcantly
diﬀerent from 0° and 45°, regardless of stimuli. On the other hand, when the source was located at 90°,
this error was signiﬁcantly larger. The ﬁeld of vision could play an important role in these results, as the
participants could have forgotten about the existence of a loudspeaker above their heads, and just have
focused on the elevation rings that could be seen in the VR. The vertical ﬁeld of view with a VR-headset is
less than 90º which means that when the participant is looking straight ahead, not all loudspeakers are visible
for them which could potentially create some visual bias that could have aﬀected the results. For further
studies, it would be recommended to have the sources of interest in sight. [1] tackled this problem by having
the sources at elevations separated by around 25º, so the subjects could actually see all 3 loudspeaker-rings,
being more aware of the surrounding. The usage of a correct set of HRTFs and the valid range is of vital
importance as the perception of the spatialized audio could be aﬀected by it. The participants perception
on the sound direction could be diﬀerent from one to another, since generalized HRTFs were used. Here, the
perception of the source could be correct for each individual whereas the spatialized binaural audio could be
faulty. Making use of individualized HRTFs could help to reduce these errors, but to which extend it is an
advantage needs to be additional research. In any case, spatialized audio needs future investigation for being
able to correctly make use of headphone-presentation to play the sounds.
As a recommendation for future projects, it could be a good idea to randomize the order in which the
stimuli is presented to the participants. This is out of the scope of this study, but there could possibly be
a training eﬀect in the subjects response, as well as fatigue at the end of the test. For this experiment, the
headphone reproduction was always presented in the ﬁrst session, starting with the noise burst, which could
have led to participants getting used to the task and the spatialized audio, resulting in better results for the
continuous noise. The reason for non randomizing the presentation methods was for practical timing reasons
(equipment setup), but for future studies it could be a good idea to randomize the order within the subjects,
i.e., half of the subjects starting with headphones and the other half with the loudspeakers, and see if these
has any eﬀect on the results.
Overall, both the azimuth and elevation errors suggest that loudspeakers and headphones could potentially
be used interchangeably, depending on the purpose of the study, the desired level of accuracy and the relative
stimulus positions. The results of the second validation study showed considerable consistency, principally
when the sources were located in the horizontal plane and ideally, in the ﬁeld of view, i.e., in front of the
subject. Generally, the responses are good for headphones when a continuous stimuli is presented in the
frontal-hemisphere, with an azimuth error potentially caused by the generalized HRTFs, since the pinnae
reﬂections are not the same as in loudspeaker-based presentation.
Head movement allowance would be
advised if there’s a need for higher precision in the results since the subjects can make use of the spatial
cues. If a high accuracy in localization is needed, regardless of the stimuli, or if the test requires a variety of

4.2
Speech in noise and localization test
This second study made use of the VR with spatialized binaural audio to evaluate speech intelligibility
and localization abilities on the subjects. The goal of this study was to ﬁnd a way to assess, by means of
pupillometry, if there was any change of cognitive load when the subjects were presented with a single task in
contrast to two tasks simultaneously. From the task performance (i.e. percentage of correct words or correct
target choice) it was demonstrated that, on average, speech intelligibility does not present any meaningful
diﬀerence when presented individually or simultaneously to a localization task, and the same results are
observed for the localization results. The participants were not given any instructions to prioritize one task
or another, which could explain the individual diﬀerences on performance from one subject to another (see
Appendix Figure 17). Usually in a dual task paradigm, when the subjects are instructed to optimize the
primary task over the secondary, a decrease in performance for the secondary task is observed. Nevertheless,
in this experiment, it can be diﬃcult to prioritize one or another as both tasks are related. As an example,
for the speech-only condition, the subjects were instructed to disregard the location of the target, but they
could have been unconsciously thinking about the source in the process of understanding speech. As this is
not a formally dual task, this could explain the discrepancy between a normal dual-task outcome and the
results from this study, since no prioritization was instructed.
When analysing the azimuth inﬂuence on speech intelligibility, a small dip in performance is seen when
the target is in the front (Figure 13). Although not found to be statistically signiﬁcant, it suggests that
speech recognition is better at more peripheral locations, which could be caused by the better ear listening or
the SPL level. What is interesting to note is that localization shows the inverse behavior, meaning that, while
speech intelligibility decreases, localization abilities increase, at least when the target is placed at 0°. It would
have been interesting to compare this behavior with the pupil traces to see if this decrease in performance
depending on the target location is reﬂected in the pupil dilation. Future studies could include an analysis of
the pupil traces, e.g. PPDs, for each azimuth location, to see if the cognitive load of localization is dependent
on the source location, i.e., comparing how front and peripheral sources can aﬀect the results. In this study,
this could not be analysed due to the lack of trials per location, i.e. each location was only repeated 4 times,
resulting in only 4 data points to measure a pupil trace (before removing artifacts or blinks), which is not
a reliable amount of data to be able to conclude on. As a recommendation, the number of target locations
could be reduced or more repetitions of the source could be added to make it possible to conduct a meaningful
evaluation.
From the speech in noise and localization test on a group level, it could be concluded that, since there are
no signiﬁcant diﬀerences on individual and simultaneous task performance, the subjects are applying the same
listening eﬀort. Pupillometry, however, shows otherwise. The analysis on PPDs and GCA revealed an overall
higher pupil dilation when the two tasks were presented simultaneously than when presented individually,
even when the percent scoring did not change. This could be an indication of increased cognitive load of
the participants when having to focus on two tasks at the same time. It is important to note that the pupil
dilation was only analysed during the listening window, excluding any additional cognitive process after the
sentence oﬀset (e.g., thinking about the sentence or location, memorizing the answer and the moment of
giving the response). These results are according to the main hypothesis that the pupil dilation response
would reﬂect the additional resources allocated to maintain the performance in a single and dual task.
As there is no standard for pupillometry analysis, it can become diﬃcult and subjective to judge the
data. Choosing the right parameters for the post-processing is crucial for obtaining meaningful results, from
baseline window, type of baseline correction and PPD calculation range to criteria for blink removal and trial
removal. These parameters could potentially change the ﬁnal results and one must be, therefore, very careful
with their selection and when making conclusions from pupillometry data.
If large pupil dilation is observed, it is believed that it can reﬂect a higher cognitive load. Nevertheless,
these changes could also be due to other psychological factors such as motivation to complete the task or

attention [25, 32, 31]. Task arousal could also cause changes in pupil dilation, e.g. the novelty of the test
could have had an impact on the results as the subjects could have been stimulated by the VR platform,
or confused by the visual scene as the talkers could have been distractors. Future studies could include the
search for saccades by using the eye gaze position data from the VR as well as the head-tracking. This could
help to evaluate if some of the noise in a trial is caused by sudden movements of the eye (i.e., the subject
looking elsewhere than to the front), and avoid corrupted data by unwanted movement. Ensuring a good
calibration of the eye-tracker is highly recommended, since this determines the quality of the output data.
Despite the adjustments, the conﬁdence was not always constant throughout the recordings which indicates
that pupil detection did not always work correctly. Right after a blink, the cameras could have captured the
eyelashes or another area and detect it erroneously as the pupil area; this is specially the case if a participant
wears make up, which should be avoided. Light reﬂections could also have aﬀected and distorted the pupil
detection. Another aspect that could have contributed to unstable conﬁdence values is the subject-speciﬁc
eye characteristics such as iris color and pupil dynamic range, as these could make it easier or more diﬃcult
for pupil detection.
When looking at the individual level, the results showed that the task performance behavior is consistent
across subjects and, even if the values vary, the overall relationship between performance is the same as on
the group level, regardless if they are presented individually or simultaneously. As mentioned previously, an
overall higher cognitive load was observed for the condition in which the tasks are presented simultaneously.
However, although this aﬃrmation can be valid on the group level, it is not always the case for every
individual. There is considerable variation on the pupil responses and not all subjects show a clear higher
pupil dilation for the combined task, as seen in the group behavior. An example shown on a particular
subject that does not match the group behavior could suggest an increase in cognitive load for a single task,
however, the results of task performance did not give an indication of this, as the subject kept the same
performance level in single and combined task. Additional work could aim at identifying outliers in the
group and examine if the pupil response averaged across subjects remains the same. Moreover, investigation
on the relation between task performance and pupil dilation needs to be done; this would be of special clinical
relevance when considering the use of pupillometry along with the VR headset for testing cognitive load.
Further work on pupillometry could include analysis on reliability and repeatability of pupil features (e.g.
PPD, mean pupil dilation and GCA) when having diﬀerent test sessions, i.e., diﬀerent days, and investigating
if these results remain the same within and across subjects. Nonetheless, the analysis of PPD might not be
ideal when analysing the pupil response on an individual level as it is a single value found on the region
of interest; the pupil response is time-dependent and a peak value could appear at any point (due to eye
movements, faulty pupil detection, etc.)
for any condition, without representing or reﬂecting the actual
cognitive load over time.
As a ﬁnal remark, the experiment was conducted for only 10 subjects and any signiﬁcant conclusions
depend on their performance. To be able to have a bigger picture of how pupillometry could be an indicator
of increased listening eﬀort, testing more participants is recommended to average out all the subjective
diﬀerences.

CONCLUSION
5
Conclusion
In this study, the similarities on accuracy for two presentation methods was investigated. The azimuth and
elevation errors showed no signiﬁcant diﬀerences for audio presented through headphones and loudspeakers,
in the range of the HRTFs used (0 -360 deg azimuth and 0 to +80 deg elevation). A continuous audio stimuli
showed better results than a short duration audio stimuli, as the spatial cues can be used to better localize the
sound direction. The headphone-based presentation was further used for a speech in noise and localization
experiment. Speech intelligibility and localization accuracy did not show any signiﬁcant diﬀerence when the
tasks were presented individually versus simultaneously. However, signiﬁcant higher pupil dilation in the dual
task could indicate that the subjects used more cognitive resources in order to maintain the same performance
level as in a single task. Pupillometry shows the additional demands that a dual task places on a subjects that
in a standard speech in noise study could have been ignored or undetected from the performance assessment
only. Whether the observed increase in pupil dilation was only caused by the increase in cognitive load or
any underlying cognitive process still needs further investigation. Despite the results of the validation study,
the binaural spatialized audio from the headphones needs to be further improved to be used as a clinically
valid tool that correctly captures the subjects perception. The results of the speech in noise and localization
study showed that for the subjects, pinpointing at the exact source location is still challenging. Nonetheless,
this study demonstrates that a VR platform could be used to perform tests that are more realistic and
representative of real-life situations, e.g. a cocktail party scenario, in which the listening eﬀort and task
performance can be assessed. This could also improve life-quality of listeners as it could help reduce mental
fatigue and increase motivation to participate in listening-challenging social situations [14, 32].
