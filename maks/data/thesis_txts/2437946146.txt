ABSTRACT
Abstract
The current thesis describes models and methodologies for the structural optimization of
oﬀshore support structures and focuses on the design optimization of wind turbine towers
and monopile foundations. The motivations for this project arises from the interest of
the wind industry to achieve reductions in the capital expenses of the oﬀshore wind
turbines, which in turn leads in reducing the levelized cost of energy.
Load simulations are conducted in the DTU 10 MW oﬀshore wind turbine, by means
of the aeroelastic software HAWC2, based on realistic wind, wave and soil data. The
purpose of the simulations is the extraction of the ultimate and fatigue loads acting on
the turbine. The fatigue loads are simpliﬁed according to the damage equivalent load
methodology. Then, a similar FEM model of the support structure, based on Timo-
shencko beam elements, is formulated in a MATLAB package called MODOP (Monopile
Design Optimization), which produces optimal monopile-tower designs using structural
analysis and FEA. The loads are provided as inputs to MODOP.
The optimal design problem is formulated as a mass minimization problem solved by
a non-linear algorithm using SQP method. The diameter and the wall-thickness of the
monopile and tower sections represent the design variables of the problem. The problem
is subjected to structural integrity and geometrical constraints, as dictated by the rele-
vant industrial design standards. Among others, the geometrical constraints account for
the D/t restriction in the segments of the structure, while the structural constraints in-
clude fatigue damage assessment in the weldings, displacement and buckling evaluations
and yielding and vibration check. For the fatigue constraint, design-dependent stress
concentration factors at the weldings are also considered.
After performing the optimization it identiﬁed that, the fatigue constraint drives the
design along with the geometrical constraint related to the D/t ratio in the various
sections. It is concluded that, by optimizing the integrated monopile-tower system a
mass reduction up to 20%, with reference to the initial design, can be achieved.
It
should be highlighted that, according to the current analysis, the initial tower design is
infeasible. It is also observed that, the mass decrease can be accomplished by varying the
monopile dimensions, e.i. the thickness, but more importantly by using high D/t ratios
at the sections (up to 840). The limitations of such an endeavour are also considered
carefully. Finally, by performing a design-load iterative procedure based on a limited
number of simulations, the dependency of the loads on the design is quantiﬁed. The
results show that, at the 3rd iteration when the convergence is achieved, a 1.75 % increase
in the optimum mass is observed, which is inﬂicted by a 18 % increase in the FLS thrust
value and a 1.5 % increase in the FLS overturning moment.
These percentages are
with reference to the initial FLS load values that result in the initial optimum mass.
However, the more signiﬁcant change in the mass and the loads is observed during the
ﬁrst iteration.

INTRODUCTION
Introduction
The current M.Sc. thesis project aims in the development of analysis models and de-
sign methods for the optimal design of integrated monopile-tower models. Monopiles are
presently the most common type of oﬀshore foundation, mainly due to their straitforward
design. They are associated with many advantages including, their simple manufacturing
and installation, their low cost and signiﬁcant amount of accumulated experience related
to them, which renders them a secure solution for oﬀshore wind industry. However, as
turbines are installed in increasingly greater water depths, a strong interest in bringing
down the cost of the support structure arises. Cost reduction can be achieved, by uti-
lizing automatic design tools for the optimal conﬁguration of the support structure. In
light of that, the goal of the current project is to present an optimal-design methodology,
by means of which the structural optimization of the monopile-tower model is accom-
plished. Additionally, an extensive investigation related to the load extraction procedure
is conducted and the relationship between the design and the structural response is ex-
amined, by performing design-load iterations. Hence, it becomes feasible to quantify the
impact of the design-load relationship on the mass of the optimized support structure.
Report Summary
This section summarizes the current report. More speciﬁcally, the structure of the thesis
is formulated as follows:
• In Chapter 1, an extensive literature review is conducted. General information on
the oﬀshore wind industry developments in recent years is provided. Then, the sev-
eral types of support structures are presented and the focus is set on the monopile,
whose manufacturing and installation is described at length. Subsequently, the
principles of structural optimization are discussed along with the limitations in
wind turbine design optimization. Furthermore, the most common optimization
approaches are examined, as well as, numerous attempts on the design optimization
of monopiles and towers.
• In Chapter 2, the load extraction procedure by means of HAWC2 is explained.
At the beginning, the DTU 10 MW support structure model is described along
with the rest of the HAWC2 inputs, namely the wind, wave and soil parameters.
Subsequently, the fatigue and ultimate design load cases which are considered in
the simulations are presented in detail.
Finally, the extracted load values are
exhibited.
• In Chapter 3, the design optimization inputs and methodology, as well as, the
structural analysis models implemented in the MODOP package are described
in detail.
More speciﬁcally, the equations required for the computation of the
deﬂections, the stresses and for the modal analysis of the structure are provided,
along with the sensitivities needed as inputs to the optimization.

1. BACKGROUND
1
Background
1.1
Wind Energy and Climate Change
In recent years, global climate change has become a highly relevant topic. The EU has
decided to tackle this problem by reducing the carbon dioxide emissions, contributing
substantially to the phenomenon. More speciﬁcally, the EU goals for 2050 suggest a 80%
emission reduction, in relation to the respective emission values of 1990 [1]. Electricity
production from fossil fuels is a primary contributor to carbon dioxide (CO2) emissions.
Therefore, the role of renewable energy sources (RES) in electricity generation becomes
of utmost importance. In fact, the CO2 reduction policies suggest a higher penetration of
electricity produced from RES such as wind and solar, in the future electricity markets.
Although the goal for a higher integration of RES in the grid is a process still in progress,
several countries have already come a long way.
More precisely, the focus is set on
wind energy where in 2016 Denmark was identiﬁed as the EU Member State with the
highest wind penetration rate (demand covered by wind energy), namely 37%, followed
by Ireland with 27% and lastly Portugal with 25% [2]. In 2017, the ﬁgure increased even
more for Denmark, reaching 43% [3]. Therefore, as wind energy becomes increasingly
popular, oﬀshore wind farms acquire a very promising role. This can be justiﬁed be the
fact that, in comparison with onshore wind farms, they allow for a better utilization of
the wind resource taking advantage of the higher wind speeds oﬀshore. Additionally, the
lower shear permits the installation of shorter towers oﬀshore, while the visual impact is
also less signiﬁcant [4]. In the next section, a brief review of the oﬀshore wind industry
developments in Europe is provided.
1.2
Oﬀshore Wind Energy in Europe
Europe is a leader in the ﬁeld of oﬀshore wind energy alongside other large wind power
markets such as China, the US and India. The ﬁrst oﬀshore wind park in the world,
Vinderby, was erected in Denmark and had an installed capacity of 4.95 MW. Up un-
til 2001, more oﬀshore wind farms were built in northern European countries such as
Denmark, the Netherlands and Sweden, with Middelgrunden (Denmark) accounting for
the highest installed capacity at the time, namely 40 MW. From then on, the installed
capacity of oﬀshore wind has experienced an annual growth of 36.1 % on average. In
fact, between 2006 and 2010 the number of operating wind farms in Europe doubled,
while the installed capacity rocketed from 910 MW to 2933 MW, what constitutes an
enormous increase of 222%. From 2010 to 2015, an average annual installation of 1.6
GW has been estimated, while northern European countries have contributed to this
increase the most [5].

1.3
Support Structures for Oﬀshore Wind Turbines
1.3.1
Types of Oﬀshore Foundations
Oﬀshore wind turbines are complex structures due to the large variety of loads applied
to them. Establishing their stability oﬀshore is a challenging task, which has led in the
development of diﬀerent types of support structures. The support structure generally
consists of the foundation and the tower. This section aims to provide a good overview
of the existing oﬀshore foundations. Their categorization is based on the way they are
connected to the seabed and the choice of the appropriate foundation depends mainly
on the sea depth at the wind turbine site. The foundations can be categorized into 5
groups: the monopiles, the tripods, the jackets, the gravity-based structures and ﬁnally
the ﬂoating structures (see Figure 1.2).

Monopiles
Monopiles are the most popular type of foundation, mainly due to their simple produc-
tion and installation. The common practice so far has been that, monopiles are preferred
for depths up to 20 m. Nonetheless, eﬀorts to design monopiles in greater water depths
have been carried out, namely 20 m - 40 m [8]. The monopiles consist of slender cylindri-
cal sections, made out of steel plates, which are rolled and welded to each other. They
are driven deep in the seabed by means of hydraulic hammers. Additionally, they are
usually connected to the bottom of the tower through a transition piece (TP) that facil-
itates the installation of secondary structures (J-tube protecting the cables, platforms,
boat landings, etc.). The friction developed between the pile and the soil ensures the
stability of the structure [9]. However, the erosion at the base of the pile results in a
phenomenon known as scour. The scour eﬀect depends highly on the current and soil
conditions at the site and it causes the monopile to be more exposed to hydrodynamic
loading [7].
Tripods
Tripods and tripiles are less popular than monopiles, due to their relatively complex
production and installation. A tripod is a three-legged structure that is driven into the
seabed at the base of each leg. The tripod features a diﬃcult welding in the node where
the the three legs are connected. At that node, the structure is prone to fatigue. Even
though the tripod is supposed to be used for deeper water than the monopile [10], when
the depth increases signiﬁcantly the base of the tripod becomes very large and therefore,
the manufacturing and installation of the tripod is considered uneconomical [7].

Jackets
Jackets provide a solution for great water depths, where the monopiles become uneco-
nomical. They are complex four-legged truss structures made out of steel and can also
be referred to as space-frames or truss-towers. The members of the jackets are welded to-
gether with Y-, K-, and X-joints [9], while their installation is similar to the installation
of the tripods. They, generally, allow for a material reduction and thus, are relatively
light structures, while they are also transparent to waves. Despite the mass reduction
however, the jackets are still considered expensive [7].
Gravity - Based Structures (GBS)
The GBS are concrete structures with enormously large dimensions, which are placed
on top of the seabed. Building with concrete is generally considered a cheap option,
however with increasing depths and consequently dimensions, the GBS can become quite
expensive. Current GBS installed in operating wind farms weight up to 3,000 tn and
reach a height of 40 m. The future GBS are expected to be even larger reaching up
to 7,000 tn. They are transported by means of special vessels, that are in some cases
especially built to facilitate the GBS transportation. Moreover, their installation requires
a proper preparation of the seabed. Hence, the installation and transportation contribute
substantially to the overall cost of the GBS [11].
Floating Structures
Floating structures are considered the most suitable solution, when water depths be-
come greater than 70 m [7]. Floating structures remain attached to the seabed and are
stabilized by means of cables. Generally, ﬂoaters are still in development and the only
operational ﬂoating wind farm worldwide is the Hywind pilot park located in Scotland
[12]. Hywind accounts for an average water depth of nearly 50 m, which is twice the
average water depth, where work was carried out in 2017 [6].
1.3.2
Monopiles in Oﬀshore Wind Projects
In this section, the characteristic of the monopiles (MPs) are further analyzed and their
advantages and disadvantages are highlighted. Emphasis is also given in their manufac-
turing and installation processes. As already noted, MPs are the most commonly used
foundations due to their design simplicity. In fact, in the 2017 wind energy report of
Wind Europe [6], it is stated that MPs represent what corresponds to 87% of the total
installed substructures. Jackets and GBS follow, accounting for 9% and 2%, respec-
tively. The tripile and tripod represent only 1.8% and 2.9% of the installed foundations.
Finally, ﬂoaters account for relatively small ﬁgures, since their installation is only recent.

The installation process begins with the conﬁguration of the scour protection. The scour

connection free from any additional support in the axial direction, does not transfer
eﬃciently the loads from the tower to the MP [14].

1.3.3
Design Standards
The design of oﬀshore wind projects should generally comply with speciﬁc requirements.
Those requirements and best practices are dictated by design standards which can be
National, European or International. The recommended practices are useful tools for en-
gineers, as they tend to streamline the numerous design processes and provide guidance
with the aim of minimizing possible errors. The standards are also important for insur-
ance companies, investors and banks, as the compliance to them provides the security
that the project proceeds according to speciﬁc guidelines [9].
In light of this, several standards are consulted during the implementation of the current
project. First of all, the International Electrotechnical Commission (IEC) Standard is
studied thoroughly for the conﬁguration of the Design Load Cases (DLCs) in Ultimate
and Fatigue Limit states (ULS and FLS). In more detail, IEC 61400-1:2005 [15] for
onshore wind turbines and the IEC 61400-3:2009 [16] for oﬀshore wind turbines, are
both used.
For the fatigue analysis and the computation of the Stress Concentration Factors (SCFs)
at the welding areas, the guidelines of Det Norske Veritas (DNV) are followed and
more precisely, DNVGL-RP-C203 [17] related to fatigue design of oﬀshore structures.
Additionally, DNV best practices are also utilized in the buckling evaluation, which is
performed according the shell buckling guidelines provided in DNV-RP-C202 [18] and
[19]. Other relevant DNV reports are DNV-OS-J101 [20] for oﬀshore structure design
speciﬁcations, which is superseded by DNVGL-ST-0126 [21] as of April 2016. Moreover,
DNVGL-ST-0437 [22] for the loads acting on oﬀshore wind turbines is another useful
tool to the design process.
For the accurate estimation of the soil-pile interaction the distributed springs model,
described in the American Petroleum Institute (API) [23], is consulted. Implementing
the API method for the accurate estimation of the soil stiﬀness is a common practice
amongst geotechnical engineers.
Another standard related to the design of oﬀshore
wind turbines, is the Germanischer Lloyd (GL) [24], while the NORSOK standard [25],
developed for the Norwegian petroleum industry, is applicable to all types of oﬀshore
steel structures.

1.4.1
The Optimal Design Problem
The design or structural optimization is a discipline, which is closely related to the struc-
tural analysis and the ﬁnite element method (FEM) described in [26]. The formulation
of a design problem is a mathematically demanding process, which results in ﬁnding
Page 10
==PAGE:25==
1. BACKGROUND
optimal design solutions by means of semi-automatic and algorithmic tools. Typically,
the problem is formulated as a mass minimization problem and the optimal value of
the mass is found by varying the parameters that deﬁne the geometry of the structure.
In principle, there are three types of structural optimization problems depending on
the geometric feature: the sizing optimization, the shape optimization and the topology
optimization [27].
The formulation of the optimum design problem consists in converting a descriptive
statement of a problem into a well-determined mathematical statement. As stated in
[28], there are several steps to be undertaken. First of all, the objectives are speciﬁed
along with the requirements to be met.
Subsequently, all the required data such as
material properties and performance requirements, need to be gathered. In addition
to that, the analysis procedures and tools to be utilized should be speciﬁed. The next
step is the identiﬁcation of the design or optimization variables.
Any value can be
assigned to the design variables and each value results in a diﬀerent design. After that,
the criterion to be optimized a.k.a. the objective function should be determined. The
objective function is a scalar dependent on the design i.e. it is a function of the design
variable vector. The ﬁnal step includes the identiﬁcation and mathematical deﬁnition
of the design-dependent constraints. The constraints refer to the design and production
resource limitations and performance requirements. A ﬂow chart describing the optimum
design process is illustrated in Figure 1.8.

1.4.2
Design Optimization Algorithms
Generally, there are three main categories to which optimization algorithms are divided:
the gradient-based, the evolutionary and the hybrid methods [29]. In essence, the design
optimization problem is non-linear, non-convex and therefore laborious to solve. The
complexity of the problem makes it more diﬃcult to identify a suitable algorithm, as
all traditional optimization approaches (mainly the ﬁrst two categories) present both
advantages and disadvantages. For optimizing the design of a support structure of a
wind turbine, the ﬁrst two groups of algorithms are mostly employed as described in
section 1.4.4.
Gradient - Based Algorithms
The design optimization is, in many cases, solved by means of deterministic gradient-
based methods. As their name implies, gradient-based methods are highly dependent
on the availability of gradients (or ﬁrst derivatives or sensitivities), that at any point in
search space indicate towards the direction of the maximum change in the objective func-
tion. The importance of the accurate estimation of the design sensitivities is therefore,
highlighted. A number of gradient-based methods, i.e. sequential linear programming
(SLP), sequential quadratic programming (SQP), convex linearization and the method
of moving asymptotes, are well-deﬁned in [27]. Gradient-based methods are only suited
for continuously diﬀerentiable objective functions. If that is not the case, evolutionary
algorithms apply [29]. For convex problems, the gradient-based methods guarantee both
rapid convergence and optimal solution. In the case of non-convex problems, the basic
idea is that the problem is iteratively solved, while in each iteration a number of convex
approximations are resolved [30]. It should be noted at this point that for non-convex
problems, gradient-based algorithms are still fast, as they are for the convex ones, but
they are also likely to get stuck in nearest local optima, which constitutes an important
limitation of theirs. Another disadvantage is the fact that, their eﬀectiveness strongly
depends on the starting points of the algorithm [31].
Evolutionary Algorithms
The evolutionary algorithms are heuristic, stochastic algorithms that incorporate ran-
dom variation and selection.
They are based on the concept of natural selection or
in other words, in the tendency of a given population to evolve through selection and
creation of ﬁtter individuals.
More speciﬁcally, in each iteration, that constitutes a
generation, the algorithm generates oﬀsprings from parents. Each oﬀspring is created
by copying a parent and by adding mutation properties to the parameters of the parent
[29]. The goodness of the oﬀsprings is deﬁned by the ﬁtness function a.k.a. the objective
function, that summarizes the characteristics of the individual in a single number. A
number of evolutionary algorithms exist with the most popular being the genetic algo-
rithm (GA), the bacterial evolutionary algorithm, and the particle swarm optimization
[31]. The GA initiates with a set of random potential solutions and then evolves towards

==PAGE:27==

a set of rather optimal solutions after a number of iterations. As the iterations progress,
weak solutions tend to lessen, whereas better prevail [32]. It is noteworthy pointing
out that, even though many evolutionary algorithms are particularly resistant in getting
trapped in local optima, convergence to a global optimum is not always guaranteed. In
fact, in many large-scale problems, a priori knowledge of the global optimum is not fea-
sible and the best solution found by a evolutionary algorithm is accepted, until a better
one is found. Some additional limitations, are their not so straitforward conﬁguration
and the fact that they require a considerably large number of ﬁtness function evaluation
due to the randomness of the evolution, which tends to make them slow [33],[31].
Hybrid Algorithms
The hybrid or memetic algorithms combine the aforementioned methods and thus, ben-
eﬁt from the capability of the evolutionary algorithms to ﬁnd global optimum or quasi-
optimum solutions and from the ability of gradient-based methods to achieve fast con-
vergence.
In other words, they are, to a great extend, free from the disadvantages
linked to gradient and evolution based methods. This is achieved by e.g. applying local
searching techniques, as soon as, the ﬁtness value of the individuals reaches a speciﬁc
critical value, or by utilizing local searches between each generation of the evolutionary
algorithms [31]. A hybrid method is presented in [29], which is referred to as evolu-
tionary gradient-based procedure. Furthermore, another hybrid method is described in
[34]. This method is based on evolutionary algorithms and thus converges to global
optimums, but also utilizes the derivative information of the best individual at every
generation therefore, achieving rapid convergence.
1.4.3
Challenges of Wind Turbine Design Optimization
Complex Environmental Conditions
The design optimization of wind turbine structures is a rather rigorous procedure chal-
lenged by a number of issues. First, the wind turbines are prone to complex and highly
unstable environmental conditions, attributed to the stochastic nature of the wind turbu-
lence and the appearance of coherent transient phenomena e.g. gusts. Additionally, both
the roughness of the terrain, as well as, the wakes from other turbines, aﬀect the wind
ﬁeld signiﬁcantly. On top of that, oﬀshore wind turbines have to undergo loads due to
site-dependent waves and currents. Considering eﬀects of icing on blades, earthquakes,
scour and marine growth complicates even more the accurate estimation of the loads.
The irregular nature of the aforementioned phenomena, adds a considerable amount of
uncertainly in the calculations. It can be thus understood that, the complexity of the
environmental conditions dictates the simulation of numerous time-intervals in order to
acquire accurate results. Current standards suggest at minimum 60 min of simulations
per load case [16].

==PAGE:28==
1. BACKGROUND
Fatigue Damage Assessment
Another important issue is the identiﬁcation of fatigue as a design driver. Throughout
its lifetime, a wind turbine is subjected to a signiﬁcant amount of periodic loading due
to both wave and wind actions. The considerable number of cycles that a wind turbine
undergoes during its lifetime makes it susceptible to fatigue damage and therefore, fa-
tigue assessment becomes a major design requirement. In order to assess fatigue, the
loads have to be extrapolated to the lifetime of the wind turbine, namely 20 years. This
fact, combined with the long-term variability of the environmental conditions prescribes
the evaluation of a signiﬁcantly large number of design load cases. Finally, the necessity
of ﬁnding an adequate software for conducting the various load simulations, should be
underlined. Such a specialized software should include an advanced aerodynamic model,
as well as a hydrodynamic model in case of oﬀshore wind turbines.
Complexity of the optimization problem
An additional challenge is the complexity of the design optimization problem in wind
turbines. In many cases, there exists a large number of design variable and constraints
which in turn leads to various assumptions. Traditionally, MPs and towers are charac-
terized by a relatively limited number of design variables. Nevertheless, modern support
structure concepts such as jackets are usually represented by a very large number of de-
sign variables. Even though this does not constitute a problem for modern algorithms,
the optimization procedure becoming time-consuming can be a limiting factor. Addition-
ally, the majority of structural optimization problems are non-convex, thus displaying
various local minima. Due to the fact that non-convex problems are inherently compli-
cated to solve, convex approximations are used to obtain the solutions [27]. Therefore,
sometimes ﬁnding a truly optimal solution can become signiﬁcantly diﬃcult.
1.4.4
State of the Art in Wind Turbine Design Optimization
The design optimization of wind turbines is a very relevant topic nowadays. That is
because, it is closely related to the major goal of wind energy research and development
sector, namely the wind energy cost reduction. Cost reduction would allow a higher
penetration of wind energy in the energy markets. More speciﬁcally, EWEA’s central
scenario expects that the installed capacity in 2030 will be doubled with respect to
the capacity in 2014 and increased by two thirds from the expected capacity in 2020
[35]. In oﬀshore wind turbines, the support structure constitutes 25% of the total wind
turbine cost [4] and therefore optimizing its design exhibits high potential for overall
cost reduction.
Even though design optimization is highly used in industries such as the automotive
and the aerospace, its application is limited in the design of wind turbine structures.
More precisely, the knowledge from the aforementioned sectors has been utilized in the

optimization of the design of the blades and the rotor, however not the same applies for
the towers and the MPs. According to [30], this can be attributed to the traditional
tubular shape of the tower and the MP on the one hand, and in the introduction of the
modern, more complex support structures in greater depths, such as the jackets, on the

Onshore Wind Turbines
An early attempt in tower design gradient-based optimization is documented in [36].
The tower is assumed to comprise of sections that have uniform dimensions i.e. mean
diameter, thickness and height, which constitute the design variables of the optimization
problem. In the study ﬁve diﬀerent approaches are investigated, namely mass minimiza-
tion, stiﬀness maximization, maximization of stiﬀness to mass ratio, minimization of
vibration, maximization of natural frequencies. The problem is subjected to constraints
related to maximum deﬂection, yield strength of material and natural frequency limita-
tions. The buckling check is avoided by imposing a lower bound on the thickness. One
main conclusion is that, the last of the named optimization strategies (i.e. maximization
of natural frequencies) produces good results. The model is then tested on a 100-kW hor-
izontal axis wind turbine and an optimum solution is achieved, which displays signiﬁcant
improvement in the overall design of the structure.
Almost a decade later Microsoft Excel’s optimization capabilities are explored by opti-
mizing the design of an integrated system consisting of the tower and the foundation [37],
by means of a Generalized Reduced Gradients (GRG) solver [28]. The cross-sectional
dimensions of the system, i.e. the diameters of the tower base and top, the thickness of
the tower, the diameter and thickness of the outer edge of the foundation base, consti-
tute the design variables, while the objective function is the total cost of the structure.
The problem is subjected to constraints in regard to natural frequency, local buckling,
maximum displacement, bearing capacity and foundation stiﬀness and overturning. The
conclusions indicate that, considering the tower and the foundation together results in
a more expensive, but also in a more secure design. Additionally, the natural frequency
constraint is identiﬁed as the driver for the tower design, while the bearing capacity
constraint drives the design of the foundation.
To summarize, [36] presents the advantage of investigating multiple criteria to be op-
timized, while the model is suﬃciently accurate and easy to implement. On the other
hand, the buckling issue is only superﬁcially dealt with. As far as [37] is concerned,
it has the advantage of an integrated tower and foundation model, the optimization of
which leads to a safer and more accurate design. However, the assumption of uniform
thickness in the tower might lead to overestimating the cost of the structure. It should
be highlighted that, none of the aforementioned studies presents a detailed ﬁnite element
model, while the fatigue damage assessment is neglected.

Several years later, this research gap is identiﬁed by the authors of [38], who therefore
decide to combine ﬁnite element analysis (FEA) with the capabilities of the GA in order
to optimize the design of onshore wind turbine towers. The FEA model based on shell
elements is conﬁgured by means of ANSYS software [39]. The top and bottom diameters
of the tower and the thicknesses of the tower sections are selected as the design variables.
The optimization model minimizes the tower mass subjected to constraints referring to
deformation, ultimate stress, fatigue, buckling, vibration and the geometry of the tower.
The thrust and the overturning moment load components are considered both in ULS
and FLS. The model is applied on a 5MW wind turbine and the results demonstrate
that a mass reduction of 6.28 %, with reference to the initial mass, can be achieved and
that fatigue dominates the design.
Oﬀshore Wind Turbines
Several attempts on optimizing the design of oﬀshore wind turbines mounted on MPs
have been documented.
An early eﬀort is recorded in [32], where the authors carry
out a design optimization of a MP-tower system.
The model is conﬁgured as a 2D
multibody dynamic model, where the nodes between the bodies have two degrees of
freedom. Wind and regular wave loads are considered, even though uncoupled, in both
FLS and ULS. The problem is formulated as a mass minimization problem, solved by
a GA. For the conical or tubular segments of the support structure, top and bottom
diameters and uniform thicknesses are speciﬁed, which constitute the design variables
of the problem. The non-linear constraints are related to buckling, fatigue and natural
frequency limitations. By optimizing a 5MW oﬀshore wind turbine it is shown that, the
replacement of steel S235 with S355 results in weight reduction of 20 %. Nevertheless,
by coupling the wind and waves and by using irregular waves proﬁles in the analysis,
the load results can improve.
A more recent endeavour in optimizing the design of oﬀshore support structure (tower,
TP, MP) is documented in [40]. The model comprises of Timoshenko beam elements
(conical or cylindrical) representing the several sections of the support structure. Both
ULS and FLS are considered. In addition to the aerodynamic and the hydrodynamic
model, which is based on linear wave theory, a distributed spring model is implemented
for a better representation of the pile - soil interaction. The gradient - based optimization
problem aims to minimize the mass. It is ﬁrst solved by an interior point algorithm and
then, by an SQP algorithm that uses the interior point ﬁnal design as starting point.
The design variables of the problem are the uniform thicknesses of the sections, while
the problem is subjected to buckling, fatigue and natural frequency constraints. The
results of the research show that, a mass reduction of 12 % is feasible when the diﬀerent
components are optimized all together instead of being designed separately. Moreover,
the design is driven by the fatigue and the natural frequency. It should be noted however
that, a larger number of design variables (e.g. diameters and heights of sections) would
allow for further mass reduction.

During the same year the authors of [41], try to perform a gradient-based design opti-
mization considering the design of the rotor and the support structure simultaneously.
The aim is to maximize the levelized cost of electricity (LCOE) subject to constraints
regarding stresses, deﬂections, frequencies and fatigue on the blades and the tower. Both
FLS and ULS loads are imposed on the structure and the models used include an aero-
dynamic, a hydrodynamic and a control model, whereas a soil model is not considered.
The results show that, by optimizing the structure the LCOE decreases by 2.3 %. Ad-
ditionally, the fatigue is identiﬁed as the design driver in the case of the rotor as well as
the overall support structure. Finally, it is worth mentioning that, the study does not
take into account the buckling limitations and ﬁnite diﬀerences are used to deﬁne the
gradients.
A more recent approach is described in [42]. The MP structure is simulated using AN-
SYS, considering the interactions of wind, waves and currents. The design optimization
is based on ANSYS zero-order method, that uses only the values of the design variables
and not the derivatives [39]. The MP diameter and thickness are the design variables
and the volume is minimized under the premise of ensuring displacement, stress and
stiﬀness limitations. The buckling and fatigue assessments are not considered in the
research. The main conclusions include that, the volume of the pile is reduced by 33.5
% and that the displacement of the pile top drives the MP design.
Finally, the latest attempt in MP-tower optimization is described in [43]. A structural
optimization model for the integrated support structure (i.e. tower, TP, grout and MP)
of an oﬀshore wind turbine is conﬁgured combining FEA shell elements and GA methods.
The diameters and thicknesses along the sections of the support structure are selected
as the design variables of the problem. The chosen constraints include vibration, stress,
deformation, buckling, fatigue and design variable limitations. The optimization model
is applied to the 5MW NREL oﬀshore wind turbine mounted on a MP as described in
the OC3 project [44]. The results of the optimization indicate a 19.8 % reduction in
the total mass of the support structure, while the active constraints are associated with
fatigue and the ﬁrst tower frequency.

2.3
Wind and Wave Models
The aerodynamic section of the HAWC2 code, is based on a modiﬁed version of the classic
BEM (Blade Element Momentum) theory, described in detail in [54].
The extended
version includes numerous models such as dynamic and skew inﬂow, shear eﬀects, eﬀects
from large blade deﬂections, tip loss and dynamic stall. The wind conditions are based
on both deterministic and stochastic wind, which are expressed primarily by mean wind
speed and turbulence, respectively.
For the turbulence model, the Mann method is
utilized as described in the IEC Standards [15]. The tower shadowing eﬀects are also
accounted for.
As far as the wave kinematics are concerned, irregular Airy waves are used. Dependent
on the type of load state, fatigue or ultimate, either the Pierson-Moskowitz, or the JON-
SWAP spectrum is selected. The aforementioned spectra are the most frequently used
in wind generated sea states [16]. The former is related to a fully developed sea, whereas
the latter refers to a developing sea (e.g. storm situations). The hydrodynamic model
for calculating the wave forces, is based on the Morison’s equation [16], the general form

Design Load Cases
In the current thesis the loads acting on the wind turbine are evaluated in the ULS and
in the FLS. The employed DLCs are simpliﬁed versions of DLC 1.1 and 6.1 for ULS
and 1.2 for FLS, as these are described by the standards [16]. More precisely, DLC 1.1
and 1.2 incorporate the load requirements related to the atmospheric turbulence, which
is observed during the normal operation of the turbine throughout its lifetime. Thus,
these DLCs utilize the normal turbulence model (NTM) . In the case of DLC 6.1, the
rotor of a parked wind turbine is in a standstill condition and the extreme turbulence
model (ETM) is considered. As stated in the standards [15], an accurate load estimation
would require the realization of 60-min simulation per mean wind speed. In this report,
the simulations performed by means of HAWC2 software have a 10-min duration, which
is the minimum recommended duration. The instructions related to DLC simulations
given in [56] are utilized, while for the post-processing of the results with the Pdap
(Python Data Analysis Program) software, the guidelines of [57] are followed.

Design Optimization
In this section, all the optimization and structural analysis models and methods are
extensively described, as part of the MODOP package implemented in MATLAB. More
precisely, the optimization model assumptions, the FEA model conﬁguration and the
design variables of the support structure are presented. Moreover, the structural analysis
of the FEA model is thoroughly explained and the optimal design problem is formulated
along with all the geometrical and structural integrity constraints.
In regards to the notation used throughout the current section, bold capital letters refer
to matrices (M ⇒Matrix), bold non-capitalized letters denote vectors (v ⇒Vector),
and italic letters account for scalars (s or S ⇒Scalar). Before initiating, it is important
to provide an overview of the processes included in the MODOP package according to
Figure 3.1.
Figure 3.1: Structure of the MODOP package
Page 33
==PAGE:48==
3. DESIGN OPTIMIZATION
With reference to Figure 3.1 it can be seen that, the MODOP consists of two main parts
which are coupled to each other: the structural analysis and the optimization part. As
soon as the loads are extracted from HAWC2, their values are inserted into the MODOP.
In addition to the loads, the material properties (young modulus, yield strength, pois-
son’s ratio etc), the constraint limits (yield and fatigue strength, maximum displacement,
etc.), as well as, the initial design (diameters and the thicknesses) of the support struc-
ture should be speciﬁed in the package. The initial design provides a starting point for
the algorithm that performs the optimization. After that, the ﬁrst part of the MODOP
can be executed for the ﬁrst time and therefore the mass, the displacements, the stresses,
the natural frequencies etc and also the necessary derivatives with respect to the design
variables can be computed for the initial design, by means of structural analysis and
FEA. Therefore, the inputs to the optimization (objective function, constraints, ﬁrst
derivatives) are calculated and fed into the non-linear programming solver FMINCON
(ﬁnd minimum of constrained nonlinear multivariable function). Inside the FMINCON,
the constraints are evaluated, followed by the convergence criteria for the initial design.
If both requirements are satisﬁed the optimum design is found, else a new design is
produced by means of the SQP method [27]. The procedure is repeated until the design
with the minimum mass is found for which the constraints and the convergence criterion
are satisﬁed.
At this point, a brief explanation is given on how a new design is produced. As already
mentioned in section 1.4.2, the SQP method is used to obtain explicit, convex approxi-
mations of the non-convex structural optimization problem. Therefore for each design,
the SQP formulates an approximation, by solving which, the non-linear algorithm pro-
vides a search direction and thus a new design. The new design is used in case the
convergence criterion for the previous design is not satisﬁed. SQP is a state of the art in
regards to non-linear optimization methods and has proven to outperform many other
algorithms in terms of accuracy, eﬃciency, as well as, number of successful solutions [60].
In fact, its high performance is a main reason why it is selected in the current analysis.
3.1
Assumptions of the Optimization Model
The basic assumptions of the MODOP model are summarized below. It should be high-
lighted that, the HAWC2 model used for the load extraction is also mentioned in some
assumptions, however this is an entirely diﬀerent model from the MODOP. The HAWC2
model is merely used in the extraction of the tower top loads, while the assumptions are
strictly related to the model used in the structural analysis and optimization processes
included in the MODOP.
• The MP and tower cross sections are circular and thin-walled, which means that
D/t > 20 [61], [62].
Page 34
==PAGE:49==
3. DESIGN OPTIMIZATION
• The ULS and FLS loads extracted from HAWC2 are applied to the tower top of
the MODOP structural model, whereas no wave loads are implemented.
• The HAWC2 model of the DTU 10 MW assumes a steel density of 8500 kg/m3 to
account for secondary structures [46]. Therefore, the same density value is used in
the MODOP package.
• The MODOP tower is designed as a stepped column, as opposed to the tapered
column design of the tower in reality, as well as, in HAWC2. Instead of a bottom
and a top diameter at each tower section, the mean value of the two is computed
and used in the analysis.
• The normal stresses instead of the Von Mises are used in the MODOP analysis.
In other words, it is assumed that hoop and shear stress contributions can be
neglected. It should be stressed at this point that, according to [63], this stress
assumption is valid as the beam theory refers to the one-dimensional approximation
of a three-dimensional continuum. More speciﬁcally, the dimensions of the cross
section are signiﬁcantly smaller than the dimension along the axis of the beam
(thin-walled cross section assumption), which renders global the axial dimension.
• The nacelle/rotor structure is attached to the top of the tower as a concentrated
mass in the MODOP.

this time the mass is computed for a new design v −hei, where the small quantity h > 0
is subtracted from each design variable vi. The sensitivities of the mass can afterwards
be deﬁned according to equation (46) and compared to the analytical sensitivities. If the
diﬀerence between the two values is rather small, then the analytical sensitivities used
in the optimization are implemented correctly.

cost, should be also altered accordingly. Even though the optimal design is expected
to diﬀer from the one extracted from the mass minimization process, the simplicity of
the current cost model implies that the diﬀerence is expected to be insigniﬁcant. The
necessity of a detailed cost model in the design of the modern wind turbines should,
however, be highlighted given that the cost is generally a signiﬁcant design parameter.

Design - Load Iterations
A very important part of the structural optimization is to be able to demonstrate and
quantify the eﬀect of the design changes to the loads acting on the structure. An attempt
to provide answers to the following questions is presented in the current section of the
report:
• Do the design changes aﬀect the loads applied to the structure?
• How much do the design changes aﬀect the loads and vice versa?
• How many design-load iterations would be required to achieve load convergence?
Answering these questions can be accomplished by performing what could be described
as a design-load loop. The ﬁrst step of the process is to optimize the initial DTU 10
MW wind turbine design for simple ULS and FLS cases. Then, the optimum design is
imported back into HAWC2 and the new loads are once more extracted. If they do not
diﬀer substantially, it is safe to assume that the eﬀect of design changes to the loads
is insigniﬁcant, and hence, no more iterations should be performed. In case of large
discrepancies, the iterations should continue until the loads, and consequently the mass,
converge to a speciﬁc value. The iterative procedure is depicted in detail in Figure 4.1.

As far as the D/t constraint is concerned, a limited number of studies can be found in
the literature regarding the behavior of circular hollow sections with a D/t ratio over

150. However for wind turbine towers, diameter-to-thickness ratios up to 380 have been
reported, while a common range is between 100 and 300 [65]. For optimized towers,
even larger ratios are documented, namely up to 600 [66]. The main problem with the
very thin-walled sections is the fact that, the local buckling becomes more pronounced
as illustrated in Figure 5.16. As the structure becomes thinner, the initial geometrical
imperfections during the manufacturing process become increasingly important to avoid,
as they can amplify the local buckling eﬀect substantially [67].
Even though it is showcased in the above analysis that, an overly thin-walled structure
(D/t up to 840) can in fact withstand the local buckling eﬀect during the wind turbine
operation, there are several other issues which should be also taken into consideration.
First of all, the several sections of the MP and the tower are rolled and welded to each
other.
A high D/t would allow for more imperfections to appear during the rolling
procedure, which would make the welding more diﬃcult.
Generally, the thinner the
cross section the more prone it is to imperfections during manufacturing, which in turn
promotes the propagation of local buckling. Additionally, the simplicity of the current
model does not allow the consideration of secondary loads acting on the structure, such as
the existence of ladders or boat landings and possible hits caused by that. Concentrated
loads can greatly aﬀect the local buckling eﬀect.
Furthermore, it is shown that, in order to achieve the desired stiﬀness, the diameters
should be signiﬁcantly large, which renders both the transportation and the installation
of the wind turbine a diﬃcult endeavour. The dimensions of the vessels carrying the
several components of the wind turbine should increase as well, which could end up con-
stituting a signiﬁcant cost limitation for wind turbines with high D/t ratios. It should
be also highlighted that, large tower diameters increase the inﬂuence of the tower to the
wind ﬂow (tower shadowing eﬀect) which may aﬀect the production negatively. There-
fore, a necessity for more accurate wind turbine models is highlighted, the development
of which would lead to the evasion of conservative assumptions, such as conservative
limitations on the D/t ratio.

Design-Load Iterations
In the current section the results of the design-load iterations are presented. The ﬁrst
step is to perform the optimization, with the constraints of the basic design, by applying
the loads extracted from the simpliﬁed DLC simulations described in the section 4. The
optimization results show that, the mass is reduced from 1928 tn to 1584 tn. The value of
1584 tn is used as a reference mass value, therefore M0 = 1584 tn. The aim is to identify
how much will the results deviate from the initial optimal results after performing the
iterations and during which iteration convergence is accomplished.
For the accurate interpretation of the results, it is important to deﬁne the relationship
between the mass and the several load components. As in the previous section, fatigue
and D/t constraints dominate the several designs computed during the iterations. Thus,
the design is dependent on the FLS loads, while ULS loads do not aﬀect the results. In
other words, by changing the ULS load values the outcome of the optimization is not
altered, while the opposite occurs with the FLS loads. Nevertheless, not all the FLS
loads deﬁne the optimal mass. The maximum FLS stresses correspond to the moment-
force pair, for which the maximum moment value is achieved.
In wind turbines the
principle direction is the wind direction, or in other words the direction of the thrust,
namely the y direction in the context of the thesis. This can be easily veriﬁed by altering
the load values and observing the changes in the optimized mass, M0. By doubling each
load component, while the others remain constant the results depicted in Table 17 are
extracted.

Conclusions
In the current section the main conclusions in regard to the study are presented. More
speciﬁcally, numerous comments can be made with reference to the implementation and
the results and several suggestions can be brought forwards for future research. The
main conclusions are summarized below:
• The fatigue stress constraint holds a decisive role in the design of the support struc-
ture, along with the geometrical constraint related to the diameter-to-thickness ra-
tio in the various tower and MP sections. In terms of loads, the design is dependent
on the FLS values of the thrust Fy and of the overturning moment Mx.
• By optimizing the integrated MP-tower system, a mass reduction up to 20% can
be achieved with reference to the initial design, which is linked to two main factors:
the varying dimensions of the MP and the high diameter-to-thickness ratio in the
sections of the structure (D/t up to 840).
• Allowing the MP thickness to vary not only leads to lower overall mass, but also
to a lower MP diameter. However, MPs with varying dimensions account for more
complex stress and SCF distributions along their height, which may negatively
aﬀect the welding process.
• For the lightest design, the D/t range for the MP is found equal to 209-237, whilst
the D/t ratio corresponding to the tower varies between 242 and 840. These values
are much higher than the common D/t values found in the literature for the MP
(D/t = 100) [48], [42], [43] and for the tower (D/t = 100-300) [65].
• The design optimization can be used for the production of more cost-eﬃcient wind
turbine support structures especially when the D/t increases substantially. How-
ever, as the wall of the support structure becomes thinner, the eﬀect of local
buckling becomes more apparent and imperfections, which amplify the local buck-
ling eﬀect, are more likely to appear. Additionally, small thickness values come
at the expense of large diameters, which could cause problems in transportation,
installation as well as operation (tower shadowing eﬀect).
• The design loads that are used in the current analysis as design-independent, are in
fact design-dependent according to the ﬁndings of the design-load iterative process.
Additionally, performing this process is likely to lead to an increase in the optimal
mass. In the current report, the design-load loop leads to a mass increase of 1.75
% of the optimal mass, while the highest diﬀerence in the design-driving loads, i.e.
the FLS thrust Fy, equals 18 % of the initial FLS Fy value. This percentage values
are obtained after 3 iterations. Therefore, the design-load relationship should be
generally accounted for when performing structural optimization.
Page 77
==PAGE:92==
6. CONCLUSIONS
• The cost model is rather limited and thus the results from the cost optimization, do
not diﬀer from those of the mass optimization. The mass optimization approach is
therefore, appropriate in this context even though it should be kept in mind that,
the utilization of a more detailed cost model could lead to divergent results.
• Despite the inevitable assumptions, the optimization delivers realistic results in
terms of mass reduction and diameter and thickness distributions, which are in
accordance with the literature review presented in section 1.4.4.
• The use of the SQP solver achieves relatively fast convergence, namely converges
in about 5 min for 48 ﬁnite elements in total.
The utilization of an increased
number of ﬁnite elements slows down the optimization, although it increases the
accuracy leading to higher mass values (i.e. 2% mass increase for increasing the
MP elements from 5 to 20). Hence, a coarse mesh should be avoided as it results
in less conservative designs.
Recommendations for Further Research
In light of the aforementioned conclusions, numerous initiatives for further research and
model improvements can be recited. Regarding the assumptions, the cylindrical elements
could be replaced by conical ones for a better representation of the tower. Naturally,
by doing so, the design variables would increase as the mean diameter would have to be
replaced by a top and a bottom diameter at each segment. It could be also considered to
include the lengths of the segments as design variables. It is advised that an increase in
the number of the design variables is accompanied by an improvement in the performance
of the MODOP, possibly thought the vectorization of the existing loops. Moreover, it
is suggested that a realistic design of the TP is accounted for. The TP is an additional
stiﬀness component, whose implementation could aﬀect both the natural frequencies and
the stress distribution. Moreover, the axial stresses should be ideally replaced by the
Von Mises stresses. Von Mises stresses additionally account for hoop and shear stress
and thus correspond to higher, more conservative stress values. It is also advised to
replace the apparent ﬁxity length by a distributed springs model, for a more accurate
representation of the soil-pile stiﬀness. Moreover, it is recommended that a detailed cost
model is developed to facilitate optimal design problems based on cost minimization. It
would be rather interesting to identify the possible design deviations between a mass and
a cost minimization. However, gathering the realistic cost data which are necessary in
this endeavour, can be quite diﬃcult. Finally, it is important that MODOP is validated
with a commercial software in order to ascertain the accuracy of the results.
In regards to the aeroelastic simulations, more extensive FLS load analysis should be
performed possibly including idling and a larger number of simulations, for the accurate
estimation of the load values. Additionally, dynamic loads including the time parameter
could be implemented instead of static, which would render the optimization model and
thus the extracted results considerably more realistic. It should be also highlighted the
Page 78
==PAGE:93==

importance of implementing wave loads to the model. For the design-load iterative pro-
cess, it is suggested that further research on the matter is conducted. More speciﬁcally,
it is important to identify how the number of aeroelastic simulations aﬀects the results
of such a process and whether the design-load iterations always lead to more conserva-
tive designs. Finally, it is recommended to take steps towards the automation of the
design-load iterations, which in the current analysis is done manually. This could be
achieved by creating a tool able to couple the aeroelastic simulations to the structural
optimization.
