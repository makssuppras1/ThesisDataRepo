Abstract
This Master thesis investigates experimentally the properties and behaviour of stochastic
Riemannian manifolds, and how these can be learned from data. The thesis looks at dif-
ferent families of stochastic Riemannian manifolds in order to investigate the behaviour of
the geodesics and curvature. The thesis introduces Riemannian manifolds parametrized
by a set of eigenfunctions and gives a criteria for the convergence of these parametrization
and an analytical expression for the Christoffel symbol, geodesic equation and curva-
ture. Further the thesis investigates Riemannian manifolds estimated from data in both
the deterministic setting using Variational-Autoencoders and in the stochastic setting us-
ing Gaussian processes. For the Gaussian processes a novel way of sampling stochastic
geodesics and curvature is presented.
The eigenfunctions are applied to topographic data, where the coeﬀicients are estimated
using Bayesian regression. It was observed that the eigenfunctions are very good at mod-
elling the data, and that the curvature seems to be relatively close to zero. The Gaussian
processes and Variational-Autoencoder are compared on the MNIST and CelebA datasets,
where it was seen that the stochastic geodesics of the Gaussian processes provided the most
accurate representation of the data. Further it was seen that the Gaussian processes seem
to have high curvature in areas with no observations and close to zero curvature in areas
with observations. In general the properties of the expected metric do not necessarily rep-
resent the stochastic Riemannian manifold in the sense that the expected geodesic doesn’t
seem to be equal to the geodesic for the expected metric.

Introduction
This thesis investigates the behaviour and properties of stochastic Riemannian manifolds
through examples, which gives an insight to their behaviour, and how stochastic Rie-
mannian manifolds can be estimated via Bayesian modelling.
The thesis investigates
these properties in terms of using eigenfunctions on a rectangle to parametrize a class
of stochastic Riemannian manifolds, and the properties and behaviour of stochastic Rie-
mannian manifolds learned from data compared to estimating a deterministic Riemannian
manifold from data.
The first section will review the general notion of Riemannian manifolds. In this section
there will be examples of Riemannian manifolds that will be made stochastic. This section
aims to introduce the necessary theory for Riemannian manifolds as well as illustrate some
of the properties and behaviour of the stochastic Riemannian manifold. Finally in this
section the behaviour of expected length and expected energy for curves on a Riemannian
manifold is shortly reviewed.
The next section will study a general class of stochastic Riemannian manifolds, where the
manifolds are parametrized by a set of basis functions. The general computations for these
manifolds are derived. Similarly the behaviour of geodesics and curvature are studied to
gain a more general idea of the behaviour of Riemannian manifolds.
After this a section on learning Riemannian manifolds from data is described. This in-
cludes learning a deterministic Riemannian manifold through deep generative models using
neural networks as well as how to learn a stochastic Riemannian manifold using Bayesian
modelling. The framework introduced in learning Riemannian manifolds using Bayesian
modelling will also apply to the basis functions from the previous sections. Further the
connection between Gaussian processes and Riemannian geometry is described. In this
section two novel algorithms to sample stochastic geodesics and curvature from Gaussian
processes are introduced as well as a general remark on the energy functional for Gaussian
processes.
The final section illustrates the previous results on more general real-life data sets. This
section illustrates the algorithms to sample geodesics and curvature from Gaussian pro-
cesses as well as compare these results to the expected metric for Gaussian processes and
a learned deterministic Riemannian manifold using deep generative models. Further the
behaviour of the basis functions is illustrated by learning the parameters based on data.
The models will be applied to the MNIST dataset, CelebA dataset and topographic data
for the map of Denmark.
The main results and contributions of this thesis are
• The application of eigenfunctions to parametrize a Riemannian manifold to model
data and an analytical expression for the curvature, Christoffel symbols and geodesic
equation.
• A novel algorithm to sample geodesics and curvature from Gaussian processes.
• A comparison between learning a deterministic manifold and learning a stochastic
manifold based on the MNIST and CelebA dataset.

Riemannian Geometry
In this section the necessary theory regarding Riemannian manifolds will be described.
The main focus of this section is to briefly introduce Riemannian manifolds, geodesics as
well as the curvature of Riemannian manifolds in that order. There will be examples of
families of Riemannian manifolds, which will be made stochastic. The purpose of these
examples is to illustrate the behaviour of some simple stochastic Riemannian manifolds.
Finally the last section will describe the connection between expected length and expected
energy for stochastic Riemannian manifolds in general. The theory is based on [2], [12]
and [5].

Learning a Riemannian Manifold from
Data
In the previous sections the relevant theory and properties of Riemannian geometry has
been described as well as different classes of stochastic parametrizations of Riemannian
manifolds. This section will focus on how to actually learn a Riemannian manifold given
a set of observed data points. This section is based on [22], [10], [19] and [17]. Section
4.2.5 and 4.2.6 present a new method for sampling stochastic geodesics and curvature from
Gaussian processes as well as a remark on the energy functional for Gaussian processes.
A natural question at this point is why even learn a Riemannian manifold?
Clearly
some data is non-linear, e.g.
data on a sphere, which makes it diﬀicult to use linear
embedding techniques such as e.g. Principal Component Analysis (PCA). However, there
exists various non-linear embedding methods such as kernel methods that can be used for
non-linear data.
As described in [10] the learned manifold should have an operational representation. There
can be various operations that should be supported on the learned manifold, but the
following three seem to be a reasonable start
• Interpolation
• Distances
• Measure
Clearly interpolation and distances are well defined along a Riemannian manifold, at least
if it is geodesically complete. In [14] it is argued that a measure on a Riemannian manifold,
M, is
dM(x) =
p
|G(x)|dx.
The above is argued using a local representation of G(x) = AT A, where A consist of a
basis. Thus the above corresponds to infinitesimal volume element of the parallelepipedon
spanned by the basis vectors, and thus gives an infinitesimal volume element in each
tangent space, see [14] for further details, which also describes distributions, expected
value etc. on Riemannian manifold and their properties. Given measure it is now possible
to define integration and probability distributions on the manifold.
In [10] a further
analysis is done on the different aspects of learning a low dimensional representation of
data, and how various methods don’t have the above operations well defined.
There are different ways to learn a Riemannian manifold given the observed data. The
first section will give a brief description of how Riemannian manifolds can be learned using
Variational Autoencoders (VAE). Using a VAE one can learn a deterministic manifold.
The second section will focus on using Gaussian Processes (GP) to learn a stochastic
Riemannian manifold. The main purpose of the section for the VAE is to give an example
of learning a deterministic manifold and compare this to learning a stochastic manifold,

certainty of our estimates unlike usual OLS estimates.

6
Conclusion and Further Research
In this report the behaviour and properties of stochastic Riemannian manifolds were in-
vestigated through different examples. Further it was seen how Riemannian manifolds can
be learned from data in a stochastic and deterministic setting.
In this report it was seen that in general the Christoffel symbols and geodesics of the
expected metric don’t correspond to the expected Christoffel symbols and geodesics. This
could be seen from the analytical expression describing the difference between the expected
Christoffel symbols and the Christoffel symbols of the expected metric was given as well
as for the experiments. Note also that in the report it was seen analytically as well as
experimentally through the example of the stochastic plane that if the Riemannian metric
is suﬀiciently ’simple’, then Christoffel symbols of the expected metric and the expected
Christoffel symbols are equal.
A method for parametrization of a Riemannian manifold using eigenfunctions on a rect-
angle was also presented. It was shown how to scale the coeﬀicients to ensure almost sure
convergence assuming the weights are independent. It was seen that the use of eigenfunc-
tions on a rectangle can be applied in the context of Bayesian regression to estimate the
posterior distributions, where it was seen that the eigenfunctions were able to reconstruct
topographic data on the map of Denmark relatively well. However, it was also seen that
the proposed re-scaling of the coeﬀicients to ensure convergence is relatively restrictive in
the sense that the weight for the eigenfunctions come too close to zero to reconstruct the
data. For further research it should be investigated how to adapt the eigenfunctions to
data and at the same time ensure convergence for the Christoffel symbols, geodesics etc.
of the learned manifold. Ideally one would like that the eigenfunctions that explains most
of the variance of the observed data should have the highest weight similar to the theory
behind Principal Component Analysis. The analytical expression of the geodesic equation,
Christoffel symbols and curvature for the eigenfunctions are derived in appendix. It seems
that these are mixture of χ2 and multiplication of independent normal distributions. For
further research it could be a first step to describe the distribution of geodesics, Christoffel
symbols and curvature and look into the distribution highlighted in appendix to see, if
these can gain insight to the behaviour of stochastic Riemannian manifolds.
In this report two generic methods for learning Riemannian manifold from data were
presented: the Variational-Autoencoder to learn deterministic Riemannian manifolds and
the Gaussian process to learn stochastic Riemannian. It was seen that using the expected
metric to compute geodesics performed significantly better than using the Variational-
Autoencoder, even though both models had relatively good reconstructions of the CelebA
and MNIST data.
In general the VAE requires a relatively large dataset in order to
compute accurate reconstruction, and the complexity of the VAE is relatively high meaning
that it takes some time to train the model. However, when the model is trained the VAE
is relatively fast. The Gaussian process is relatively fast to learn from data given that
only some kernel matrices and matrix operations has to be computed. However, given
that there is a relative high number of matrix operations and inversions involved it was
seen in the project that for large amounts of data, the computations of Gaussian processes
can be relatively expensive in time.
82
==PAGE:90==
Two novel approaches to sampling stochastic geodesics and curvature from Gaussian pro-
cesses were also proposed. It was seen that the stochastic geodesics performed relatively
well in terms of estimating geodesics compared to the expected metric of the Gaussian
process, which indicates that it is preferable to use the stochastic Riemannian metric
rather than the expected metric. For further research it could be interesting to compute
an expression for the expected Christoffel symbols for Gaussian processes. Given that the
metric is Wishart distributed for Gaussian processes, which is (relatively) well-known in
terms of moments, it could be possible to find an analytical expression of the Christoffel
symbols. In this way the geodesics can be estimated using the expected Christoffel sym-
bols, which can be used to find the geodesics for the expected Christoffel symbols. This
might not correspond to the expected geodesics, but it can be closer to the behaviour of
the stochastic geodesics compared to the geodesic of the expected metric. Further the ap-
proaches presented to sample stochastic geodesics and curvature from Gaussian processes
are based on a relative high number of matrix operations and inversions making them
expensive for large datasets. Further since the sampling involves inverting the Cholesky
decomposition of the covariance matrix it was also seen during the project that this can be
relatively unstable if the covariance matrix consists of values close to zero. For further re-
search it should therefore be investigated if the algorithms can be made more numerically
stable.
For the Gaussian processes it was seen both for the expected and stochastic metric that the
curvature seems to be relatively close to zero in areas with observations and relatively high
in areas with few observations. This indicates that the curvature for Gaussian processes
in some way acts as a regulator to tell, where the observations are located. Further it
was also seen in the simple examples of the paraboloid that the Gaussian processes don’t
reconstruct the curvature. In this way Gaussian processes are not necessarily learning the
’true’ structure of Riemannian manifolds, even though the geodesics seem to be relatively
accurate.
In terms of the behaviour of the stochastic geodesics for both Gaussian processes and
the eigenfunctions on the plane it was seen that they differ the most at the midpoint of
the geodesics for a boundary value problem. Similarly it was seen that the stochastic
geodesics seem to diverge for an initial value problem. This raises the question of how
much the geodesics ’spread’ over time doing geodesic shooting, which could give an insight
by further study of ’stochastic’ version of the Exponential map. Similarly it was also seen
that there are some differences between the stochastic curvature and the expected metric.
For the example with the stochastic paraboloid it was seen that the expected metric had
zero curvature, which was relatively different from the stochastic curvature. Note also that
all stochastic Riemannian manifolds considered in the report are based on some ’version’
of normal distribution, and it could therefore be interesting to consider e.g. stochastic
coeﬀicients that are e.g. Gamma distributed or Pareto distributed to see, if this changes
the behaviour.
In general the behaviour of the considered examples of stochastic Riemannian manifolds
seem to be relatively controlled. Even though there were some difference between the
results of the expected metric and the stochastic metric, the results were relatively close
to each other. For further research it would be interesting to investigate the distributions
of geodesics, curvature, the Exponential map etc. This could at first be done in terms of
the moments, e.g. mean and variance and see how this can be used to gain insight to the
general behaviour of stochastic Riemannian manifolds.

Python scripts used in the report
The code used in this report is programmed in Python using JAX for Gaussian Processes
and numerical computations, while Pytorch has been used for the VAE. The code can be
found at Github under FrederikMR in the folder master_thesis at
https://github.com/FrederikMR
The files src consist of the general code used in the examples, where the most notable are
• AE_surface3d.py - Contains the implementation of an AE for three dimensional
data embedded into R2. Primary module used: Pytorch.
• bayes_learning.py - Contains an implementation of performing Bayes regression us-
ing basis functions to compute the posterior distribution of the weights and map.
Primary module used: JAX.
• gaussian_proces.py - Contains an implementation of Gaussian processes as well as
the Riemannian properties of the expected and stochastic metric such as curvature,
geodesics etc. Primary module used: JAX.
• ode_integrator.py - Contains an implementation of various methods for numerical
integration and ODE solvers. The boundary value problem is solved using the BFGS
algorithm in Python, where the gradient is estimated using automatic differentiation
in JAX. Primarily module used: JAX.
• rm_dgm.py - Contains an implementation for computing geodesics given a Deep
Generative model. Primary module used: Pytorch.
• rm_sympy.py - Contains an implementation for symbolically estimating geodesics,
curvature etc. on a general Riemannian manifold given either a metric matrix func-
tion or a parametrization. Primary module used: sympy.
• rm.py - Contains an implementation for estimating geodesics, curvature etc. on a
general Riemannian manifold given either a metric matrix function or a parametriza-
tion. Contains further also an implementation of the Riemannian geometry on rect-
angle using eigenfuncitons. Primary module used: JAX.
• sp.py - Contains among others an implementation of sampling multivariate normal
distributed variables in JAX. Primary module used: JAX.
• VAE_celeba - Contains the implementation of an VAE for the CelebA dataset em-
bedded into R2. Primary module used: Pytorch.
• VAE_mnist - Contains the implementation of an VAE for the MNIST dataset em-
bedded into R2. Primary module used: Pytorch.
• VAE_surface3d - Contains the implementation of an VAE for three dimensional
data embedded into R2. Primary module used: Pytorch.
The remaining files found in main are mainly applications of the above files. Any references
in the code can be found in the comment section, Sources, at top of the given .py file.

D.1
Convergence of Real-Valued Random Variables
A real-valued random variable is defined as [9]
Definition D.1.1. X is said to be a real-valued random variables on a probability space,
(Ω, F, P), if X : (Ω, F) →(RmB), where B denotes the borel-algebra, while F is a σ-algebra.
If P(X ∈A) = 1, then X is said to in A with probability 1 or almost surely. The definition
of almost sure convergence is [9]
Definition D.1.2. Let X, X1, X2, . . . be a sequence of real-valued random variables on
(Ω, F, P). If
P(Xn →X) = 1,
then Xn is said to converge to X almost surely.
Convergence in probability is defined as [9]
Definition D.1.3. Let X, X1, X2, . . . be a sequence of real-valued random variables on
(Ω, F, P). If
∀ϵ > 0 :
P(|Xn −X| ≥ϵ) →0,
n →∞,
then Xn is said to converge to X in probability.
Convergence in distribution is defined as [9]
Definition D.1.4. Let X, X1, X2, . . . be a sequence of real-valued random variables on
(Ω, F, P). If

then Xn is said to converge to X in distribution, where Cb(R) is the set of all bounded
and continious functions on R.
D.2
Jensen’s Inequality
Following [9] Jensen’s inequality is given by
Theorem D.2.1. Consider a real-valued random variable, X, defined on a probability
space, (Ω, F, P). Let E ⊂F be a sub-sigma-algebra. Consider a measurable and convex
function with probability 1, ϕ : R →R such that both X and ϕ(X) have first moments.
Then it holds almost surely that
ϕ (E [X|E]) ≤E [ϕ(X)|E)] .
Note that the inequality can be reverse if ϕ is concave almost surely.
