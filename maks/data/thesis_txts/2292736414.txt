Abstract 
 
This report contains the description of a project that I completed with the purpose of improving
 
 
 
 
 
   
 
   
 
 
 
 
 
 
gaze visualization, mainly for game developers. I spent my time research and working on my
 
 
 
 
 
   
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
techniques at the game developer company ​
Funday Factory​
. The project was successful in
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
producing a technique for rendering ​
very dense gaze data sets of 65,534 samples (18 minutes
   
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
continuous sampling at 60Hz) in real time (30 FPS) using only 3 draw calls. Comparisons are
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
   
 
 
 
 
made to previously developed techniques but direct testing on those tests is not conducted. The
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
   
 
 
 
result is a functional, proven technique for rendering gaze data which requires geometry shader
     
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
support as well as stencil and depth buffer support.

Problem Statement 
 
Current gaze visualization methods are heavily CPU dependent (CPU bound), becoming 
potential bottlenecks in real­time visualizations. 
  
Can dense gaze data sets be rendered smoothly, in real­time, by letting the GPU do most of the 
hard work? 
 
Chapter 1  - Introduction and Background 
Tradition 
Video game production in its entirety, with all its knowledge, tools and methods has emerged in
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
the span of a few decades. As with any production process, inspiration is naturally sourced from
 
 
   
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
   
 
 
 
other related or similar processes: video game production has a lot of its nomenclature from film
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
   
 
 
 
 
 
 
production which in turn has a large share of theater terms (e.g. the idea of a ​
Scene carries
 
 
 
 
   
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
   
 
 
through from theater to video game production). 
 
The current available body of knowledge on how to provide a compelling narrative or setting the
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
   
 
 
 
 
 
scene in order to evoke certain responses from the audience are the accumulated results of the
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
human race’s storytelling traditions. 
Methods of designing the decoration and lighting of a scene in order to focus the attention of the
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
   
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
audience also carry over from theater to film and in the last decades also to video games.  
 
A new era 
While a lot of information, knowledge and methods has carried over from similar fields to video
   
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
game production, it would have been very difficult to predict the possibilities that the spread and

technological opportunities for research.

Chapter 2  - State of the Art 
Here I will discuss the gaze visualization techniques presented by Stellmach et al (2010) and
   
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
then focus on the inspired technique presented by Maurus et al. (2014) and how the technique
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
seems to solve the problem of gaze visualization but has several drawbacks that I will seek to
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
   
 
 
 
describe solutions to. When it comes to visualization of gaze data, there are currently a few
 
 
 
   
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
   
 
trends, mainly in regards to the ratio of computations on the CPU compared to the GPU. 
 
For an introduction to the rendering pipeline of modern graphical applications, please refer to
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Appendix A. 
 
The Stellmach, et al. techniques 
Stellmach, et al. (2010) present three gaze visualization techniques that in union work well to
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
introduce the current state of the art. I will briefly describe their immediate positive and negative
 
 
 
 
 
 
   
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
attributes of each. 
Projected Attentional Maps 
Description: Taking inspiration from 2D heat maps, this method first projects all the gaze points
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
onto the target texture, most often the scene’s camera’s viewport, then renders additive,
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
gaussian textures on top and finally colors the texture pixels according to their intensities. 
Projected attentional mapping is a screen­space technique because it’s early use of gaze points
 
 
     
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
in a viewport space (typically the screen). 
 
 
Pros: 
●
Screen­Space: The heatmap works across multiple 3D model meshes, since it is
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
calculated independently of the scene models. 
●
Scene independent:​
 is not affected by scene size and object count. 
 
Cons: 
●
Back­and­forth: Rendering speed decreases severely as gaze points increase, since
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
the technique involves texture access on a per­gaze point level and more importantly to
 
 
 
 
 
   
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
this project: adds a lot of draw calls. 
●
The gaze visualization has to be recalculated if the camera is moved or rotated. 
Object-based Attentional Maps 
Description: For each object, the number of times the object has been hit by a recorded gaze
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
   
 
 
entries is saved. The objects are then fully colored according to how many associated gaze
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
points they have. 
Object­based attentional mapping is a model­based technique because it stores its results on a
 
 
 
   
 
 
   
 
 
 
   
per­model base.

Pros:  
●
Low data requirement:​
 Only requires gaze points as input. 
●
High reusability: the numbers of associated gaze points only need to be calculated
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
once per 3D model mesh. The color is also only assigned once. The camera can be
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
moved and rotated without triggering new gaze visualization calculations. 
●
Speed:​
 Performance is independent of render texture resolution. 
 
Cons: 
●
Mesh dependent: since objects can be rather large or complex, is a problem. The
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
   
 
 
fidelity of the technique is limited by the granularity of the scene’s objects. E.g. if a
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
     
human is represented using a single mesh, it will be impossible to tell from the
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
visualization if their head or shoes drew the most attention. 
 
Surface-based Attentional Maps 
Description: Ray casting is used for each gaze point to determine which particular triangle of
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
the hit 3D model mesh is hit. A gaussian distribution is used to apply some effect to the adjacent
 
 
 
 
   
   
 
   
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
triangles as well. In the end, a texture is created that maps to the model’s mesh, consisting of
 
 
 
 
 
   
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
colors that visualize the number of gaze points on particular triangles of the mesh. 
Surface­based attentional mapping is also a model­based technique because it also stores its
 
 
 
 
   
 
 
   
 
 
 
results on a per­model base. 
 
Pros: 
●
High reusability: The gaze map textures only need to be calculated once for each
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
viewed model. 
●
High (potential) precision: Unlike the Object­based technique, and to some extent the
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Projection­based technique, very precise interpretations are possible if the gaze data is
 
 
 
 
 
 
   
 
 
 
 
recorded well. 
 
Cons: 
●
3D model mesh dependent: Since this technique uses textures that map to the scene
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
model meshes, the quality depends highly on both the resolution of the mesh (the
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
number of vertices) and the topology (the relative placement of the vertices). If a low
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
     
 
resolution mesh is provided, or one with topology that has vertex­sparse areas, these will
 
   
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
provide less resolution for gaze interpretation.  
 
Combined Attentional Maps 
Description: Also described by Stellmach, et al. is a combination of the above techniques. The
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
   
 
 
 
 
 
 
idea is simple (if a bit vague): based on how far the scene camera is “zoomed in”, a technique is
   
 
   
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
   
 
   
   
picked similar to how working with Level of Detail models work.

The Maurus, et al. extensions 
In the article ​
“Realistic Heatmap Visualization for Interactive Analysis of 3D Gaze Data”,
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Maurus, et al. describe their technique for gaze visualization as inspired by the Stellmach, et al.
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
technique of texture projection (see the ​
Projected Attentional Maps section above). What they
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
add to the technique is the separation of the projecting space and the active camera space, i.e.
 
 
 
   
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
the texture is projected from where the camera was when the gaze recordings were made but
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
the scene can be viewed from arbitrary angles. They also use a shadow mapping technique to
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
   
 
 
 
 
alleviate the problem of rendering onto visually occluded objects which is inherent to simple
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
texture projection.  
 
Texture projection with shadow mapping technique 
Pros 
●
Perspective freedom:​
 An observer can clearly see what has been looked at and what 
was occluded by other objects. 
●
Screen­Space: The heat map works across multiple 3D model meshes, since it is
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
   
 
calculated independently of the scene models. 
 
Cons 
●
Very demanding on the CPU:​
 While some extensions are proposed in the article 
(Maurus, et al, 2014), there is no way around the incredibly high requirement of the 
technique in terms of expensive calls to the graphical API (mainly a lot of draw calls with 
relatively little data). 
●
High texture generation:​
 The shadow mapping technique alone requires one depth 
texture to be generated for each recorded gaze point/eye position pair. 
●
Frequent texture sampling:​
 Then there is the sampling of the gaussian texture used for 
attenuation of gaze value intensity.

Andrew T., et al. heat map research

Chapter 3 - Design and Tools 
Main design requirements 
In the design phase, I established a few requirements to follow during the implementation
 
 
 
   
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
phase. The implementation that I would end up with had to primarily be: 
 
●
Compatible and expandable with well­known methods currently employed in the field of
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
3D graphics. 
●
Fast enough to be used in a rapidly updated 3D environment. 
 
I was my intention to provide enhancements within the topic of gaze visualization that were
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
proven not just on abstract idea­level but supported with a tangible proof of concept. 
 
Test support requirements 
For testing purposes, I would also need control over the gaze data and the visualization
 
 
   
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
parameters, such that saving, loading and analysis the data would be easy. Secondary design
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
points that I kept in mind while developing the gaze visualization technique were:  
 
●
Loading and saving gaze data must be quick and hassle free. 
●
The detailed values of the gaze data must be immediately available for inspection, in
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
case invalid or corrupted data was loaded.

In the tests than ran successfully, the seemingly most performance heavy task was the

close to full capacity. 
After researching the topic however, I realized that it was not vsync or any other unwanted
 
 
 
 
   
 
   
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
feature I had forgotten to turn off but rather the reaction to having moved so much of the

was GPU bound. 
 
Now, being bound to either CPU or GPU for extreme times is not something one wants but I
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
   
was still getting reasonable FPS considering ​
65,534 vertices were sent to the GPU in 3 passes,

The problem of culling 
There is a special case where the mesh and geometry shader based technique I have
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
   
 
implemented is rendered unnecessarily: the player goes through many separate rooms over the
   
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
course of the gaze tracked session. No matter where the observing camera is placed during or
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
after the session, it will only be able to see a small part of the large gaze mesh. The problem is
 
 
   
 
 
 
 
 
   
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
   
that even if only a small part is viewed, all of it will be rendered since it’s still only one object,
 
   
   
 
 
 
 
 
   
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
and therefore can not be culled at all in regular fashion (​
culling refers to not rendering
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
something because it is outside of view or otherwise obscured). 
 
One solution to the problem is to use an octree data structure to break up a set of gaze data into
 
 
 
 
   
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
   
 
 
 
 
 
multiple gaze meshes that can be activated and deactivated depending on what part of the
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
scene (or room) the observer of the gaze visualization is in.

Chapter 7 - Conclusion 
In this project report I have described a technique for rendering very large gaze data sets using 
the GPU and freeing up the CPU to do other, more general purpose tasks. I can conclude that 
the technique incorporating a geometry shader pass and one big vertex/index buffer (in Unity:

Balancing 
Now that almost all of the computations needed for doing gaze visualization can be

Appendix A: The rendering pipeline 
Within the area of 3D graphics (and in many cases 2D), how model data that describes the
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
relative positions of the defining points of a graphically representable object are sent to the
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
graphics card and the encapsulation of the different stages is called the ​
rendering pipeline​
. The
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
   
 
 
 
 
 
metaphor is that input resources such 3D model data and textures will move through sections of
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
pipe and produce an image at the end. Each section of pipe has a purpose and some are
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
   
 
 
 
 
programmable such that they will do very different jobs depending on the input. 
 
In order to make use of some advanced features, I use the Direct3D graphics API in my
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
   
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
discussions in this report. Keep in mind, that the OpenGL API is similar in many ways but the
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
   
 
 
 
 
 
 
Unity engine does not support OpenGL’s geometry shaders that I use as a key pillar in my
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
   
 
   
 
 
 
 
technique. Therefor, I will describe the specific Direct3D version of the abstract concept of a
 
   
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
   
rendering pipeline.

Vertex Shader Stage: The first of the programmable stages, the vertex shader manipulates the
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
data of incoming vertices in parallel and outputs it.

The Hull Shader Stage: is the first optional tessellation step, which constructs control points
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
from vertices. ​
Unused in the technique described in this report. 
 
Tessellator Stage: Works when there is a hull shader connected in the pipeline. Its job is to
 
 
 
 
 
   
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
   
 
subdivide the incoming primitives into smaller primitives with the purpose of adding more detail
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
from less data. ​
Unused in the technique described in this report. 
 
Domain Shader Stage: Calculates vertex positions from the incoming hull shader stage and the
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
tessellator stage output. ​
Unused in the technique described in this report. 
 
Geometry Shader Stage: An important part of the technique I describe in this report, the
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
   
 
 
 
 
 
geometry shader has the unique ability to add new vertices to the vertex stream based on the
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
geometry shader program that the user writes. I use this to create icospheres around the
 
 
 
 
 
 
   
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
endpoints of the gaze samples. 
 
Stream Output Stage: Enables data to be reprocessed by different programmable stages of
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
the pipeline. ​
Unused in the technique described in this report. 
 
Pixel Shader Stage: Calculates the color value of each pixel from the incoming interpolated
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
vertex values calculated in the previous stages (mainly the vertex shader stage). Since these
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
calculations happen once per pixel, they are much more expensive than those performed in the
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
   
 
vertex shader. In the techniques I compare my own technique to, this stage is called once for
 
 
 
 
   
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
each pixel on the screen, per gaze point. 
 
Appendix B: Ray casting 
Used for many different purposes such as collision detection, light calculation, Ray casting is a
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
   
term that describes ray­surface intersection calculations. Ray casting is used extensively in both
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
   
 
   
 
game logic and rendering techniques as well as other fields within 3D graphics. 
 
Appendix C: Level of Detail 
LoD or Level of Detail is a description of techniques within computer graphics that involve
 
 
 
 
 
   
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
decreasing the complexity of 3D models (or other graphical artifacts), depending on metrics
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
such as (most often) distance to the camera, level of occlusion by other objects, visual
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
importance in the scene or others. It is quite common for 3D artists to create, manually or by
 
 
 
 
 
   
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
automated processes, several versions of the same model ­ each with relative lower complexity.
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
   
 
 
 
 
 
These models can then be swapped out in real­time dependent on the chosen LoD metric.
