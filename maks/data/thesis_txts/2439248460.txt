Abstract 
There have been increasing demands for cost-effective electrocatalysts toward oxygen 
reduction reaction (ORR) over the past decades, which can be used in metal-air batteries 
and fuel cells. In this project, a metal complex and graphene oxide were used for 
synthesizing a novel carbon-metal-nitrogen composite for ORR catalysis. A unique 
nitrogen-doped graphene anchored cobalt oxide nanoparticle catalyst (Co-G-15h) was 
successfully fabricated through a one-step hydrothermal process. Several controlled 
samples were also synthesized to investigate the origin of the ORR activity. Co-G-15h 
exhibits significantly high ORR activities in alkaline solutions among all the synthesized 
materials, but still not so comparable with the commercial Pt/C catalyst in terms of limiting 
current, electrochemical active surface area, onset and half-wave potentials and the number 
of electron transfer.  
 
To further exploit its catalytic performance, controlled annealing treatments at different 
temperatures were then applied. The one which was annealed at 800oC (denoted as Co-G-
15h-800), shows a well-defined mass-transport limiting region with the highest limiting 
current, the most positive onset (903 mV vs. RHE) and half-wave (744 mV vs. RHE) 
potentials among the series. Further, the rotating ring-disk electrode measurements reveal 
that Co-G-15h-800 favors a desirable four-electron pathway (3.8-3.86) for ORR. Moreover, 
Co-G-15h-800 displays a strong stability for ORR process, as demonstrated by almost no 
half-wave potential shift (-8 mV) after continuous CV cycles. Notably, the current density 
of Co-G-15h-800 shows no significant changes upon the injection of methanol. Under the 
same experimental conditions (500 seconds after injecting methanol), the current retention 
of Co-G-15h-800 is up to nearly 100%, comparing to 91% in commercial Pt/C, indicating 
its strong methanol tolerant capability.  
 
Also, we conducted oxygen evolution reaction (OER)-related measurements to extend the 
experimental research. Surprisingly, Co-G-15h-800 exhibits superior OER activities with 
a low overpotential (417 mV) for achieving 10 mA/cm2 current. The overall oxygen 
reaction activity of Co-G-15h-800 was evaluated by calculating the difference of OER and 
ORR metrics (Δ𝐸) in the full oxygen potential window. A small Δ𝐸 (805 mV) suggests 
that Co-G-15h-800 can be used as a promising bifunctional oxygen electrocatalyst with 
excellent catalytic nature for reversible oxygen reactions.

Motivation and Project Objective 
This century is expected to undergo unprecedented transitions and challenges in the manner 
of power generation, delivery and storage. Conventional energy sources based on oil, coal, 
and natural gas have been regarded as highly effective driving forces of economic progress. 
However, environmentally friendly power generation technologies are playing a more and 
more important role in future power systems due to increasing environmental problems 
caused by intensive utilization of fossil fuels and the so-called global energy crisis arising 
from rapid depletion of conventional energy sources. 
 
Renewable energies are defined as energy sources that can be continually replenished by 
nature. They are generally divided into three categories: derived directly from the sun (such 
as photo-thermal and photo-voltaic), indirectly from the sun (such as wind, hydropower, 
and photosynthetic energy stored in biomass), or from natural movements and other 
mechanisms of the environment (such as geothermal and tidal energy) [1]. Due to the 
unpredictable nature of renewable energies like wind or solar photovoltaic, currently it is 
still very difficult to directly integrate them into power grids. To address such issues, 
additional energy storage techniques are required to store the surplus electricity and release 
the stored electricity when needed. High-capacity energy conversion/storage systems like 
hydrogen fuel cells, metal-air batteries and water electrolyzers are considered as some of 
the most promising solutions for the utilization of renewable energies in future power 
system. For instance, a 100% green power system consisting of solar photovoltaic cells and 
wind turbines as basic electricity generators, water electrolysis and fuel cells as electricity 
conversion and storage components is depicted in Figure 1. 
 
 
Figure 1 Schematic diagram of a proposed renewable power system.

Implementing these advanced technologies in practical use highly depends on a series of 
electrochemical reactions involving oxygen evolution reaction (OER) and oxygen 
reduction reaction (ORR) for metal-air batteries, and hydrogen oxidation reaction (HOR) 
and hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) for water splitting and fuel cells [2]. These 
hydrogen-based and oxygen-based electrochemical reactions are, however, limited by slow 
kinetic, thus resulting in low efficiency in corresponding technologies. Among all these 
reactions, ORR and OER are regarded as bottle-necks since the multistep proton-coupled 
electron transfer processes are sluggish [3]. The normally used catalysts for ORR are the 
platinum (Pt)-based materials which are expensive and of scarce abundance on earth. 
What’s more, the long-term stability of ORR electrocatalysts under the corrosive operating 
conditions remains to be improved to meet the durability requirement for industrialization. 
 
This project aims to explore new Pt-free nanostructured ORR electrocatalysts with ease of 
synthesis, low cost, high catalytic activity and excellent stability. The morphology and 
composition of the as-synthesized catalysts are analyzed by a series of instruments. Their 
different catalytic performance toward ORR will also be compared with those of the 
commercially used Pt/C (20 wt%) as a benchmark via various electrochemical tests.

1. Introduction 
Global concerns about energy crisis and environment protection call for the development 
of clean and sustainable energy as substitutions of fossil fuels. Fuel cells and metal-air 
batteries have been considered as efficient energy conversion and storage devices due to 
their high energy densities, portability and zero emission [4]. In proton exchange 
membrane (PEM) fuel cells, fuel molecules like methanol or hydrogen are oxidized at 
anode, and in the meanwhile oxygen gets reduced at the cathode. However, the inherently 
sluggish kinetics of the oxygen reduction reaction (ORR) and the resulting utilization of 
precious metal-containing catalysts further limits the widespread commercialization of this 
promising technology. Compared with Hydrogen-based reactions like HOR and HER, the 
slow kinetics of oxygen-based reactions are mainly caused by the difficulties in O2 
adsorption on the electrode surface, O–O bond activation/cleavage and oxide removal. 
These are evidenced by much higher overpotentials in oxygen-based electrochemical 
reactions than those in hydrogen-based reactions as shown in Figure 2 [2]. 
 
 
Figure 2 Schematic of the overpotentials of hydrogen (HOR and HER) and oxygen (OER and ORR) 
electrocatalysis with the corresponding reactions [2]. 
 
Two major approaches of reducing catalyst cost have being actively pursued: one is to 
reduce Pt loading, and the other is to explore nonprecious-metal catalysts (NPMCs). The 
latter would always be better in the long run. At this juncture, how to reduce the dependence 
on noble metal catalysts or even fully eliminate the noble metal content without significant 
loss of ORR catalytic performance and durability is the crucial subject of most current 
electrocatalyst research studies. 
==PAGE:10==
Doping of graphene to synthesize carbon-metal-nitrogen composites as high-performance bifunctional catalysts 
 
10 
 
A variety of transition metal complexes with nitrogen-containing ligands have been 
explored as NPMCs due to their reasonable activity and remarkable selectivity toward 
ORR. In addition, this class of catalysts usually exhibit inertness to alcohol oxidation and 
many of them can catalyze ORR through a four-electron process without significant 
generation of hydrogen peroxide [5]. Nevertheless, a significant drawback of this kind of 
catalysts is the relatively low stability in acidic environment such as under the PEM fuel 
cell operation condition [5], [6]. The proposed reason for such degraded ORR catalytic 
performance could be the loss of the active sites caused by the attack of hydrogen peroxide 
generated during the incomplete oxygen reduction [7]. As reported in many studies, the 
stability of such catalysts can be significantly improved by heat treatment and the resulting 
ORR catalysts are much favorable for a direct four-electron pathway [7]. 
 
Recently, (nano)composites stabilized on carbon-supported metal compounds have been 
intensively explored as efficient NPMCs for ORR [8], [9]. Carbonaceous supports such as 
carbon nanotubes, nanofibers, and graphene have received great attention owning to the 
abundant free flowing π-electrons and large surface area. It is also well known that doping 
additional atoms (N, S, P) in carbon can efficiently modify the electronic structure of the 
ambient carbon atoms, leading to a controllable local charge density distribution [10]–[12]. 
Furthermore, carbon-based materials which are doped with dual elements can 
simultaneously catalyze ORR and OER with competitive activities because of adequate 
density of catalytically active sites [11]. 
 
Graphene oxide (GO), with an ultrahigh specific area about 740 m2 g-1 (measured from 
diluted solution with GO concentration of 50 µg mL-1) [13], have been widely used as 
electrocatalysts or electrode materials in fuel cells, metal-air batteries, supercapacitors, Li-
ion batteries and biological sensors [10], [14]. The abundant surface oxygen-containing 
function groups on GO make it possible to introduce non-precious metals on GO via 
electrostatic interaction. It is believed that the coupling of metal complexes and GO can 
enhance the catalytic performance toward ORR in terms of stability and conductivity [15].  
 
Having taken all above into account, in this project we have introduced a one-pot 
hydrothermal procedure for synthesizing M-N-C (M = Co) composite using Cis –
[Co(en)2Cl2]Cl precursor as both Co and N source and GO. Heat treatment was then carried 
out for the synthesized material to see if enhanced ORR performance can be achieved.

2. Theoretical Background 
2.1 Concepts in electrochemistry 
The term ‘electrochemistry’ is not a simple sum of electricity and chemistry. It is a study 
which deals with the interchange of chemical and electrical energy. In an electrochemical 
system, we mainly focus on the process of charge transfer between electronic conductors 
(e.g. electrode) and ionic conductors (e.g. electrolyte) and the possible factors that may 
influence this process.  
 
The charge transfer in electrode is completed by migration of electrons or holes. Typical 
electrode materials include metals, carbon materials, semiconductors and conductive 
polymers. The charge transfer is generally in the form of ionic motion in electrolyte phase. 
The commonly used electrolyte can be molten salts, ionic liquids and solution of any 
chemical compound which is dissociated into ions. Solid ionic conductors having one 
mobile ionic species which may be either anion or cation can also be used in some specific 
cases. For instance, yttria stabilized zirconia which allows O2- to migrate through its lattice 
in high temperature is used as electrolyte in solid oxide fuel cells (SOFC) [16]. 
2.1.1 Electrochemical reaction 
Electrochemical reactions are defined as heterogeneous reduction-oxidation (redox) 
reactions that occur at the interface of electrode/electrolyte. They are described by 
equations that differ from ordinary chemical equations by having normally two half 
equations and involving electrons apart from chemicals as reactants or products. The 
superposition of the two half reactions is the corresponding overall reaction. 
 
The interface between electrode and electrolyte is also called electric double layer (EDL). 
Compared with chemical reactions which usually release chemical energy in the form of 
heat, electrochemical reactions can convert most of the chemical energy into electricity. To 
complete such energy conversion, there are several features of electrochemical reactions: 
 
• 
Electrochemical reactions take place at the electrode/electrolyte interface. 
• 
Oxidation and reduction reactions are physically separated 
• 
Electrons travel across the interface and through external circuit during reaction, 
meanwhile ionic transportation occurs in electrolyte  
 
As mentioned in the first and third points, EDL is an extremely important region since it is

the place where different reactants interact, and electrons generated accordingly. The 
structure of EDL, the type and the concentration distribution of particles have a dramatic 
effect on the mechanism and kinetics of the electrochemical reaction. Many models have 
been proposed to describe this special region. Hermann von Helmholtz was the first 
scientist to introduce the concept of EDL and suggested a Helmholtz model. The basic idea 
of this model is that a charged surface immersed in an electrolyte solution will attract ions 
with opposite charge and repulse ions of the same charge. The layer of electronic charges 
at the electrode surface and the layer of counter-ions in the electrolyte constitute EDL. 
Helmholtz model assumed that the attracted counter-ions surrounded by solvent molecules 
form a monolayer on the electrode surface as seen in Figure 3a. This structure is 
comparable to the classic parallel-plate capacitor which is separated by a distance. Such 
distance in EDL is denoted as H in Figure 3 and normally equals to the radius of an ion 
[17]. 
 
 
Figure 3 Schemes of different EDL structures based on (a) the Helmholtz model, (b) the Gouy-
Chapman model and (c) the Gouy-Chapman-Stern model [18]. 
 
The Helmholtz model was then modified by Gouy and Chapman after considering that the 
ions distribution should be continuous, forming an extended layer of ions into the 
electrolyte solution. The motion of ions is determined by the combined effects of ion 
diffusion driven by concentration gradient and electrostatic migration driven by electrode 
potential. These factors result in a so-called ‘diffuse layer’ as shown in Figure 3b. However, 
the capacitance of EDL could be overestimated in Gouy-Chapman model since ions are 
treated as point-charges, giving rise to a relatively higher ion concentration compared to 
the real situation at the electrode surface [18]. 
 
To obtain a more realistic picture, Stern proposed a new EDL model taking account of the 
finite size of ions. He combined the Helmholtz model and the Gouy-Chapman model and

is termed as the Stern layer, and the outer region called the diffuse layer, as seen in Figure

2.2 Catalysts and electrocatalysis  
Catalysts are defined as substances which are capable of accelerating reaction rate without 
being consumed during catalysis reactions by forming temporary intermediates to decrease 
the activation energy of reactants. From the previous section, the electrochemical current 
depends strongly on the rate of the reaction, which in turn depends on the potential-
dependent activation energy barrier as seen in equations (2.10-2.16). The aim of 
electrocatalysis, especially for ORR reaction, is to achieve a specific current density at a 
low cost of activation energy or in other words, a low loss of polarization overpotential.

2.2.1 Mechanism of ORR catalysis 
The microscale understanding of the ORR mechanism is still in its development stage due 
to the highly complicated ORR kinetics. Compared to the anodic HOR in fuel cells, ORR 
is a multi-electron reaction that may comprise of a series of steps with the generation of 
different intermediates. A number of studies have shown that oxygen can be reduced 
following two paths, depending on the final products. One is the so-called four-electron 
reduction where O2 is completely reduced to OH- in alkaline media or to H2O in acidic 
media. The other is called two-electron reduction where O2 is partially reduced to hydrogen 
peroxide [12]. The former one is more favorable in ORR because of the adequate use of 
oxygen and a resulting higher output potential than the second one. However, the partial 
reduction of oxygen can be used in the industrial hydrogen peroxide production.

Regarding the ORR electrocatalytic activity measurements, the biggest problem is the mass 
transport issues arising from low solubility of oxygen in aqueous solution. The true kinetic 
current can hardly be obtained because the electrochemical process is limited by the mass 
transport process all the way through the potential window of polarization. To address such 
issue, special techniques like rotating disk electrodes (RDE) controlled by an electric motor 
are used to accelerate oxygen transport by getting electrolyte solution stirred during

that of the mass transport, and thus detailed kinetic properties can be studied with the help 
of such setup. 
 
On the other hand, the newly developed ORR catalysts should normally be assessed in a 
fuel cell environment and compared to a benchmark, for example, the commercial Pt/C 
catalyst. In most cases, such measurement is impractical since the complicated and low 
reproducible fabrication of membrane electrode assembly (MEA), and special skills and 
equipment are also required [28]. RDE has been one of the most effective techniques for 
fast screening evaluation of electrochemical behavior of newly developed materials at the 
lab scale.

2.3 Graphene 
Graphene is an emerging carbon material with a single atomic layer structure of carbon 
atoms arranged in a two-dimensional hexagonal pattern. This so-called two-dimensional 
(2D) carbon material was regarded to be non-existent because it is inconsistent with the 
quasi-two-dimensional systems until being discovered in 2004 by Andre Geim and 
Konstantin Novoselov from the university of Manchester [31]. Indeed, this single layer-
structured arrangement of carbon atoms is the starting form for all carbon allotropes such 
as carbon nanotubes, fullerenes and graphite. This unique structure also endows graphene 
==PAGE:27==
Doping of graphene to synthesize carbon-metal-nitrogen composites as high-performance bifunctional catalysts 
 
27 
with excellent physicochemical properties including high charge carrier mobility (up to 
105 cm2·V-1·s-1), high conductivity, ambipolar electric field effect, quantum hall effects at 
room temperature, high mechanical strength (130 GPa) and high surface area (theoretically 
2600 m2g-1) [32]. More and more applied studies about graphene-based materials or 
devices have attracted research interests worldwide. 
  
2.3.1 Preparation of graphene 
Several synthetic procedures have been developed to prepare graphene, but each one of 
them, besides possessing different scalability, produces graphene with very different 
characteristics. The preparation methods of graphene can be classified into two distinct 
approaches: (1) top-down and (2) bottom-up methods as shown in Figure 11 [33]. The top-
down methods are usually started with the exfoliation of graphite through mechanical or 
chemical processes, which aim at weakening the Van Der Waals forces existing between 
the graphene layers. The fabrication of graphene nanorods by opening carbon nanotubes 
through chemical or thermal routes is one of the typical top-down approaches. On the other 
hand, the bottom-up methods for the graphene preparation are comprised of assembling 
small carbon-containing molecules as building blocks into single or few layer graphene 
structures by means of thermal or chemical process.  
 
The top-down methods are mainly based on the fact that the graphene sheets are merely 
held by weak Van Der Waals forces along the c-axis of graphite crystal with an interlayer 
distance of 3.35 Å. A single graphene layer can be ultimately obtained by continuous 
mechanical or chemical exfoliation of graphite into small films. Mechanical cleavage of 
highly oriented pyrolytic graphite (HOPG) is firstly proposed as a physical top-down 
method for preparation of single layered graphene in 2004 [31]. Layers of graphene are 
repeatedly peeled from HOPG by an adhesive tape and subsequently transferred onto an 
appropriate substrate (e.g., silica wafer). Though this method is extremely simple, it 
enables the separation of single (or few) layer(s) graphene with an area up to 1 mm [34] 
and hardly causes any alteration or introduces any contaminations to the graphene sheets 
during the process. However, mechanical exfoliation can only be adopted for fundamental 
research due to their difficulty on reproducibility and high costs for large-scale production.

Based on the experience of the mechanical exfoliation of HOPG, attempts have been made 
to develop of a controllable chemical method to separate graphene sheets without 
significant alteration. One of the most well-known approaches to expand graphitic layers 
is through oxidative intercalation in highly oxidized graphite. The graphene oxide (GO), 
which is a chemically modified form of graphene, contains several types of oxygen-
containing functional groups within its surface like hydroxyl, epoxy, ether, diol, and ketone 
[35]. The formation of these functional groups helps to increase the interlayer distance from 
3.35 Å in graphite to ~6 Å in GO. The cohesion forces between graphitic layers are 
weakened as a result, and further separation of the layers can be done with a simple 
ultrasonication treatment. Graphene can be obtained by a following reduction of GO 
by reducing agents, thermal treatment or electrochemical methods. The chemically 
synthesized graphene is thus called reduced graphene oxide (rGO) because the 
oxygen functional groups cannot be completely removed from GO. The presence of 
residual oxygen functional groups and high density of defects created in the carbon 
matrix make rGO exhibit some better and improved properties than pristine 
graphene. Despite that, it is still a highly feasible and controllable way for 
synthesizing graphene material with desirable characteristics. As an intermediated 
product of chemical graphene synthesis, GO is also an attractive material owing to 
==PAGE:29==
Doping of graphene to synthesize carbon-metal-nitrogen composites as high-performance bifunctional catalysts

its interesting and unique properties. The oxygen functionalities on GO have been 
exploited for surface functionalization to make GO as novel supporting materials 
that can be used in electronic devices, energy conversion technologies, catalysis 
systems, biosensors, etc.  
 
The bottom-up methods of graphene preparation involve fabricating carbon-
containing molecules into a sp2-hybridized network. To achieve such unique carbon 
configuration, special conditions are usually required. Organic synthesis methods 
have been applied to build up small graphene-like molecules with well-defined 
shape and controlled edge structure using solution-based catalytic reactions if 
considering graphene as a polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon. The main drawback of 
this approach is that graphene molecules tend to be highly insoluble in most organic 
solvents when they are larger than a few nanometers. Further synthetic growth is 
therefore hindered, and uncontrolled side reactions may occur instead.  
 
Alternatively, carbon atoms can be deposited on the metal surfaces such as Pt, Cu 
and Ni to form planar structures via high-temperature chemical vapor deposition 
(CVD). Graphene can be achieved on SiC substrate through a process called 
epitaxial growth, during which Si atoms will sublimate at high temperatures under 
ultra-high vacuum conditions [36], leaving exposed layers of carbon atoms that will 
be rearranged to form graphitic layers. The thickness of the synthesized graphene 
layers can be precisely controlled by sublimation temperature and other synthetic 
conditions. The properties of graphene prepared on SiC wafer are somehow 
different from those of mechanically exfoliated graphene due to the substrate-
induced unevenness. Hence, the selection of SiC wafers is of great importance for 
the epitaxial growth of graphene. Moreover, the high cost of single-crystal SiC 
substrate as well as expensive instruments are the main issues that limit the large-
scale production based on this method.   
2.3.2 Doped graphene 
Doping of graphene with hetero atoms like nitrogen is an important approach to tailoring 
the electronic properties of graphene, which can greatly broaden its applications. There are 
usually two means for chemical doping of graphene: (1) heterogeneous adsorption of gas 
[37] and organic molecules [38] to the graphene surface and (2) homogeneous 
substitutional doping which introduces hetero atoms into the graphene lattice by solution-
based reactions. Among all the formulations of doped graphene, nitrogen is mostly similar 
in size of carbon, making it an easier fit into the carbon matrix. In this project, we constrain 
our focus on the topic of nitrogen-doped graphene (N-graphene) for the ORR application.  
 
==PAGE:30==

There are usually three common bonding configurations existing in the carbon lattice when 
nitrogen atoms are doped into graphene, including graphitic N, pyrrolic N and pyridinic N 
as seen in Figure 12. To be specific, pyridinic N bonds with two C atoms through a double 
bond and a single bond at the edges or defects of graphene. Pyrrolic N refers to N atoms 
bonded with C atoms through two single bonds in a five-membered ring. While the nitrogen 
incorporated in the hexagonal carbon network is designated as graphitic N. Apart from 
these three common nitrogen types, the oxidized states of pyridinic N, pyridine N have 
been observed in both the N-graphene and N-CNT studies and are believed to be capable 
to increase the capacitance of the material due to its positive charge [39].

Through doping of nitrogen atoms, N-graphene exhibits different properties compared with 
the pristine graphene. These alterations can be attributed to the spin density and charge 
distribution of carbon atoms influenced by the nitrogen dopants [10]–[12]. The sites on the 
graphene surface doped with nitrogen are called ‘activation region’, and they can directly 
participate in catalytic reactions or anchor metal nanoparticles acted in catalysis.

2.4.1 Ultraviolet–visible spectroscopy 
Ultraviolet–visible spectroscopy or ultraviolet-visible spectrophotometry (UV-Vis) is a 
spectroscopy technique where the adsorption of light in the region of ultraviolet–visible is 
measured and an electromagnetic spectrum is accordingly built. A spectrophotometer is 
used for measuring transmitted light signal through solutions, transparent solids and even 
gaseous samples. The UV-Vis spectrophotometer compares the light intensity before (𝐼0) 
and after (𝐼) passing through the sample as depicted in Figure 13 and this ratio is referred 
to as the transmittance which is expressed in percentage. The absorbance is correlated with 
transmittance through Lambert-Beer’s law [40]:

Where 𝜀 is the molar absorptivity (M-1 cm-1), 𝑙 is the light path length i.e., the dimension 
of a cuvette containing sample in cm and 𝑐 is the analyte concentration in mol/L. When 
the electron of analyte is excited by a light source form the ground state, specific 
wavelengths are absorbed while the rest are transmitted and recorded by the detector. 
However, the linear relation of the Beer-Lambert’s law has its limitations: (1) at high 
concentrations, deviation of molar absorptivity coefficient causes nonlinearity due to 
electrostatic interaction between molecules that are very closely surrounded by others; (2) 
the change or shift of refractive index in the chemical equilibrium may occur at high 
concentrations; (3) light scattering may be caused by contamination. To avoid these 
limitations, measurements require clean environment, proper sample dilution, optimized 
equipment, and calibration.

2.4.2 X-ray diffraction 
X-ray powder diffraction (XRD) is a high-efficient analytical technique used for the 
identification of crystalline phase composition of a material. It can provide information 
including unit cell dimensions, the extent of crystallinity and the size of crystallites.   
 
XRD measurement is based on constructive interference generated by the interaction of 
monochromatic X-rays and a crystalline sample. When a powder sample is hit by incident 
X-rays at an angle, depending on the arrangement of atoms and the distance between atoms 
in different planes, the diffracted X-rays may interfere in a constructive, destructive or 
partially constructive manner. The interaction between the incident rays and the sample 
produces constructive diffraction when satisfying Bragg’s Law [41]: 
 
𝑛𝜆= 2𝑑𝑠𝑖𝑛𝜃                                                                                                                              (2.49)  
 
This equation correlates the wavelength of electromagnetic radiation (𝜆) to the incident 
angle (𝜃 ) and the interplanar spacing (𝑑 ) in a crystalline sample. A simple illustration 
describing this condition is given in Figure 14. These diffracted X-rays are then detected, 
processed and counted. By scanning the sample through a range of 2𝜃 angles, all possible 
diffraction directions of the lattice can be attained due to the random orientation of the 
powdered material. The conversion of the location of diffraction peaks within the 2𝜃 
range to the d-spacings allows the identification of the sample by comparing d-spacings 
with standard reference patterns since each crystal has a set of unique d-spacings.

2.4.3 X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy  
X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) is a quantitative, surface-sensitive spectroscopic 
technique in which elemental compositions, empirical formula, and electronic states of the 
elements are measured. The surface composition at the depth up to 10 nm of the sample 
can be measured by XPS. During the measurement, a Monochromatic X-ray beam is 
generated by diffracting and focusing a beam on a thin disc of natural, crystalline quartz. 
The sample is then excited by this mono-energetic X-ray beam, causing photoelectrons to 
be emitted from the sample surface. An electron energy analyzer is used to measure the 
kinetic energy of emitted photoelectrons [42]. The electron binding energy of the emitted 
photon-generated electrons can be determined by using an equation that is based on the 
work of Ernest Rutherford: 
 
𝐸𝑏𝑖𝑛𝑑𝑖𝑛𝑔= 𝐸𝑝ℎ𝑜𝑡𝑜𝑛−(𝐸𝑘𝑖𝑛𝑒𝑡𝑖𝑐+ 𝜑)                                                                                    (2.50) 
 
where 𝐸𝑏𝑖𝑛𝑑𝑖𝑛𝑔 is the binding energy of the electron, 𝐸𝑝ℎ𝑜𝑡𝑜𝑛 is the energy of the X-ray 
photons used in measurement, 𝐸𝑘𝑖𝑛𝑒𝑡𝑖𝑐 is the kinetic energy of the electron as measured 
by the instrument and 𝜑 is the work function which is dependent on both the nature of the 
spectrometer and the specimen. High vacuum conditions are required to obtain XPS since 
the electrons’ kinetic energies can be significantly influenced by the gas molecules in air. 
 
A typical XPS spectrum is a plot comprised by the number of electrons versus the 
corresponding binding energies. Different elements can be detected through the XPS 
spectrum since each element produces a fingerprint XPS signal with a specific region of 
binding energy. The peaks in spectra correspond to the electron configurations of atoms 
[43]. The number of detected electrons correlates with the amount of a certain element, and 
valuable information like elemental compositions with the corresponding atomic 
percentage can be obtained as well from the XPS measurement.

2.4.4 Scanning electron microscopy 
The scanning electron microscopy (SEM) is an electron microscope in which images are 
generated by scanning a sample with a focused electron beam. The incident electron beam 
interacts with sample atoms, producing various signals containing sample’s surface 
topography and elemental composition through the emitted electrons from sample. 
Depending on the instrument, the finest resolution of SEM can be achieved is up to 1 nm, 
with a wide range of magnifications from 10 times to more than 500,000 times.  
 
The most common SEM working mode is detecting secondary electrons emitted by 
electron beam-excited atoms. Secondary electrons are usually emitted from the sample 
surface within the depth of 5-10 nm. Due to the nature of being surface sensitivity, 
secondary electrons can be used for inspection of the topography of the sample’s surface. 
For conventional SEM characterization, specimens must be electrically conductive and 
electrically grounded to prevent electrostatic charge accumulation on the surface. For the 
non-conductive samples, an ultrathin coating of conductive material should be done by 
either low-vacuum sputter coating or high-vacuum evaporation prior to the imaging.  
2.4.5 Transmission electron microscopy 
Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) is a microscopic technique where a beam of high 
energy electrons is transmitted through an ultrathin sample (~100 nm in thickness), 
interacting with the sample as it passes through. Transmitted electrons are then magnified 
and focused by electromagnetic lenses onto a fluorescent screen which emits light when 
hit by electrons. The images are finally recorded by a camera [44]. During operation, ultra-
high vacuum is required through the whole chamber to eliminate unwanted scattering and 
the sample must be very thin to allow the penetration of electrons. A cross-sectional scheme 
of TEM is given in Figure 15. We can have an insight on the structure, particle size 
distribution, porosity and even lattice parameters of the sample based on the corresponding 
TEM imaging.

2.4.6 Energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy 
Energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS, EDX, or XEDS) is an analytical technique 
used for elemental and chemical composition characterization of a sample. EDX 
microanalysis can identify the characteristic X-rays produced by different elements after 
bombarding the sample with high-energy electrons within an electron microscope like 
SEM or TEM. The generated characteristic X-rays from the sample are detected with an 
energy-dispersive spectrometer which is capable to distinguish element-specific X-ray 
energies. When the specimen emits X-rays, the detector converts the charge pulse from X-
rays into a voltage pulse. The voltage pulse is then converted into a digital signal, producing 
an EDX spectrum. EDX is especially useful in elemental concentration determination for 
a selected zone. The different elemental compositions on the core and shell for a core-shell 
structure, for example, can be determined through such characterization. 
 
It is worth noting that the SEM, TEM or EDX mentioned above are all techniques utilizing 
different products generated from the interaction between incident electron beam and 
atoms in the sample. All these products or signals convey useful information of the sample 
to the operator, including topography, elemental composition and distribution, micro-
structure and so on. Figure 16 summarizes different types of electrons generated from an 
electron-matter interaction.

The electrochemically active surface area (ECSA) of an electrode is a crucial parameter 
used for quantify electrocatalytic activities [68]. Many electrochemical methods based on 
the adsorption or stripping (oxidative removal) of an adsorbate to or from the electrode 
surface have been developed for the estimation of ECSA. For a platinum electrode, the 
hydrogen underpotential deposition (H-UPD) area resulted from hydrogen adsorption in 
CV measurements is commonly used to determine the ESCA. However, recent studies have 
shown that the total charge in H-UPD region may contain contributions from hydroxide 
adsorption on some specific Pt sites [69] and some current response from hydrogen-based 
reactions may also be improperly counted. In addition, the H-UPD effect cannot be found 
in many other catalysts as seen in Figure 29, making this method only applicable for Pt-
based catalysts. Therefore, a more universal ECSA determination method is required in this 
project. Through literature study, an electrochemical double-layer capacitance (Cdl)-based 
method [70] was employed in our case, where Cdl derived from non-faradic region was 
calculated to estimate the ECSA through CV measurements as seen in Figure 31 for Co-G-

appendix. All the calculated ECSAs are summarized in Table 2. The commercial Pt/C

The capability of efficiently catalyzing ORR and OER on a single electrode is of great 
importance, especially for rechargeable metal−air batteries and regenerated fuel cells that 
involve these two reactions. The development of efficient bifunctional oxygen catalysts

concern, we extended the potential of our electrodes to the water oxidation region for the 
investigation of potential OER electrocatalysis of the synthesized catalysts and the 
commercial Pt/C catalyst. As for the catalytic performance comparison among different 
bifunctional oxygen catalysts, it is reasonable and fair to use one single catalyst as the 
benchmark toward both OER and ORR. Therefore, despite the fact that Pt-based catalysts 
are not effective for OER due to the formation of Pt oxides on the surface at high potential 
conditions [75], we still used Pt/C catalyst as a benchmark for OER instead of the 
commonly used IrO2 catalyst.

5. Conclusion 
In summary, we have synthesized a carbon-metal-nitrogen composite (Co-G-15h-800) with 
a novel nanostructure via a hydrothermal process along with a follow-up thermal treatment. 
Physical characterizations revealed the co-existence of cobalt oxide species and nitrogen-
doped graphene. A porous texture, which is formed by the decomposition of thermally 
unstable species in precursor, is also confirmed by microscopic measurements. Most of the 
cobalt oxide nanoparticles are encapsulated in the nitrogen-doped graphene sheets and get 
well dispersed on the N-doped graphene surface. Owning to the positive synergistic effect 
of the two interfaces between the cobalt oxides and N-doped graphene along with the 
nitrogen-rich active sites, Co-G-15h-800 shows comparable ORR activities to the 
commercial Pt/C but far exceeds Pt/C in both long-term operation stability and crossover 
resistant ability to methanol. In addition, we have also demonstrated that Co-G-15h-800 
can be used as a powerful bifunctional catalyst for both OER and ORR. Considering all the 
measured catalytic performance, this new reversible oxygen electrode is promising for 
practically potential applications in metal−air batteries, fuel cells, water-splitting devices, 
and other key renewable energy devices.
