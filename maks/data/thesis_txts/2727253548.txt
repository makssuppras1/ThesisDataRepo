Summary
In the subsequent pages, the development of a method to manipulate the excitonic
fine structure splitting of a quantum light source based on epitaxially grown, self-
assembled quantum dots emitting in the telecom frequencies is presented. The ulti-
mate aim of the project is to enable the entanglement restoral of the photons emitted
by the biexciton-exciton cascade since, due to the lift in the degeneracy of the bright
excitonic states, entanglement is reduced in the presence of anisotropy in the confining
potential in the quantum dot.
In the first part of this thesis, an initial section is dedicated to introducing the re-
cent developments in quantum technology in relation to the project. In particular, an
introduction to the basics of quantum information is followed by a review of common
technological applications, after which a presentation of well-established techniques
for the emission of single photons is given. The successive part of this work entails the
description of quantum dots, focusing on the fabrication methods, the explanation of
the excitonic structure, and the description of the radiative cascade. After that, the
design of the devices is presented by first introducing the reader to semiconducting
junctions and then by discussing the structure of the devices. The theoretical anal-
ysis is concluded by a literature review in which the two papers [1, 2] that inspired
this project are discussed, together with four additional papers describing alternative
methods of entanglement restoral [3–6].
The second part of the following work entails the description of the cleanroom
fabrication of the devices.
The first chapter is dedicated to the discussion of the
process flow which was developed and optimized during the course of the project,
with particular attention to the procedures and process parameters for each step.
The last chapter describes the experimental realization of the devices, focusing on
the problems and diﬀiculties that were overcome by the author, and is concluded
with a description of the results from electrical characterization.

Introduction
”The science of today is the technology of tomorrow”
- Edward Teller
In the last century, information technology was completely revolutionized. It was
1965 when Gordon Moore pointed out for the first time how the miniaturization
of electronic components in integrated circuits would speed up exponentially in the
coming years [14]. The prediction came to be known as Moore’s Law and it dictated
the evolution of electronics from computers as large as an entire room to the small
and convenient devices that everyone knows today.
In more recent years the physical constrains of quantum mechanics have been
limiting the capability of the industry to follow Moore’s Law. In particular, since
the 1990s the oxide layer that constitutes the gate of CMOS transistors reached a
thickness low enough to allow a leakage current to flow between the metallic gate and
the semiconductor channel. This phenomenon is due to quantum tunneling and it is
a direct consequence of miniaturization [15].
The intrinsic limitation in the reduction of the size of electronic components led
researchers to pursue different solutions, one of which is a complete change in the
computational paradigm. In the last three decades a large effort was made not to
avoid the complications caused by quantum mechanics but to embrace them, thus
giving birth to quantum technology and the second quantum revolution [16].
In order to exploit many of the advantages of quantum mechanics, such as su-
perposition and entanglement, advancements in the technological front are needed.
In particular, quantum light emitters that are capable of emitting pure, indistin-
guishable, entangled single-photon pairs are of the utmost importance for many of
the applications involving quantum information such as quantum key distribution,
quantum metrology, and quantum computation [17].
This project aims to the development of a single-photon emitter based on epitax-
ially grown, self-assembled quantum dots (QDs) made of InAs/InP and emitting in
the long-wavelength telecom band. The QDs are placed in an optical cavity defined
by distributed Bragg reflectors and the structure is doped to vertically define a p-i-n
junction.
The process described in the following is for the fabrication of electrical contacts
with the objective of manipulating the confinement potential in the QD, with the
ultimate goal of tuning the fine structure splitting caused by anisotropy and allowing
for an enhanced degree of entanglement and single photon purity.

CHAPTER 1

This chapter aims to answer as to why this project came to be and explore the reasons
behind the current research endeavors in the field of quantum technologies, with a
particular focus on nano-optical solutions and photonic devices.
1.1
Introduction to quantum information
1.1.1
The QBit
As most are aware in today’s age, in classical computing the basic unit of information
is the bit: a binary abstract value physically implemented in processors through the
use of MOSFET transistors.
Theoretically speaking, the definition of the bit is not bound to its physical re-
alization. This is the reason why, during the history of computing, many different
solutions were found to implement a physical bit, with the main one being electri-
cal current but mechanical computing, vacuum tube computing (the ENIAC) [18]
and optical computing [19] have been explored and, together with more extravagant
physical media such as water [20] [21] and even dominos [22], have been shown to be
functional even though ineﬀicient solutions.
In quantum information, the basic unit of data is the quantum bit (Qbit or Qubit):
a binary quantum state physically implemented in quantum processors through the
use of a two-level system [10].
In the same way as the classical bit, the QBit’s definition is not related to its
physical implementation but it is instead an abstraction based on the mathematical
model that governs the quantum theory. Contrary to classical bits, for which CMOS
logic is the clear winner of all the possible implementations so far, QBits are currently
being fabricated with a wide variety of quantum systems.
As the world races toward the first scalable quantum computer, researchers are
striving to find the best physical medium to implement Qbits with some of the most
promising technologies being superconducting Josephson junctions, ion traps, nuclear
magnetic resonance, crystal defects such as color centers, and, last but not least, the
quanta of light: photons [23].
To better understand the Qbit and the concepts described in the following disqui-
sition, the basics of quantum mechanics must be explored.

The following work focuses on nanophotonic devices, therefore close attention

Among the different properties of photons, many can be exploited to implement a
Qbit. The most common and simple technique utilizes the polarization of the photon
[25] but time-bins [26] and dual-rail [27] implementations have been the subject of
research together with other techniques that employ the orbital angular momentum,
the occupation number or even continuous variables such as the quadratures of the
field [28].
In the following, the attention will be focused on polarization Qbit as the device
fabricated during the project is mainly oriented to such implementation.
1.1.2
Quantum key distribution
Of the many possible applications of Qbits in day-to-day technology, one of the most
promising is the development of secure and reliable cryptographic schemes: quantum
key distribution (QKD).
Current algorithms and technologies rely on a one-time pad to ensure complete
security but the need to share a key as long as the message limits the eﬀiciency of
the process. To avoid this, the main cryptographic techniques involve computational
assumptions about the possible eavesdroppers, the main one being the inability to
factor large numbers into their prime factors in a polynomial time (an assumption
that may soon be unfulfilled) [10].
In recent years, many different protocols employing Qbits and quantum channels
have been developed [29], the most well-known one among them being the protocol
BB84, published by Bennet and Brassard [30] which was then proven to be completely
secure more than twenty years later by Peter Shor and John Preskill [31].
At present times, the main limitations of the security and distance achievable
by QKD are presented by a lack of technology. The detector represents one of the
main limitations for distance since, as the signal is exponentially attenuated in the
optical fiber, the noise becomes the dominant source of errors [32]. On the other hand,
limitations in the single-photon purity allow the signal to be intercepted without the
necessity of observing the photon that will eventually reach the receiver. This leads
to the so-called ”photon number splitting attack”, evidencing the very concrete need
for eﬀicient sources of pure single photons [33]. As QKD often involves the need to
transfer a state from sender to receiver, a high degree of entanglement (described
in the following section) is also desired to implement protocols such as quantum
teleportation [10]
The first experimental proof of the feasibility of QKD was carried out over a small
distance of 32 cm by Bennet and Brassard themself in 1992 [34]. After that, many
experimental demonstrations using optical fiber [32,35,36] were implemented over a
distance of hundreds of kilometers and, in even more recent years, satellite-relayed
quantum key distribution was shown to be a viable and reliable implementation of
quantum cryptography [37,38].

1.1.3
Quantum computing
Often defined as the ”holy grail” of quantum technologies, quantum computing was
first conceived in a famous lecture by Nobel prize Richard Feynman in 1982. The
idea that sparked the second quantum revolution had the objective of simulating
quantum systems eﬀiciently, a task that is notoriously hard for classical computers as
the number of amplitudes that need tracking increases exponentially with the number
of particles [10].
For more than a decade, quantum computing remained a niche technology that was
thought to only have useful applications in the research field. It wasn’t until 1996 that
the industry realized the potential advantages and threats that quantum computing
presented, as the first substantial breakthrough of the field was the now famed Shor’s
algorithm for the prime factorization of numbers in polynomial time [39]. Since then,
many new algorithms that presented a significant speed-up in computational time
were discovered, some of the most notable being the Deutsch-Jozsa algorithm, the
Bernstein-Vaziran algorithm, and Grover’s Algorithm [40].
As classical computers employ logic gates to manipulate data and execute instruc-
tions, quantum computers utilize quantum gates to change the state of the Qbit. This
manipulation changes the coeﬀicient α and β of eq. 1.1, thus allowing the Qbit to
change into any of the states on the surface of the Bloch’s sphere [10]. Two common
examples of logic gates are the Hadamard’s gate and the CNOT gate, the first of which
maps the state |0⟩into the state |+⟩and the second (being a two Qbit gate) switches
the phase of a state if the control Qbit is in the state |1⟩. The Hadamard’s gate
supply one of the main capabilities of quantum computing: quantum superposition.
When followed by the CNOT gate (Fig 1.2), the Hadamard’s gate is able to
generate a peculiar two-Qbit state known as a Bell’s state, represented by |Ψ⟩=

2(|00⟩+ |11⟩). In such a state of the system, a measurement on one of the Qbit
changes the state of the system by making it collapse on either the |00⟩or the |11⟩
state. This means that a measurement of one Qbit can effectively alter the state of
the second Qbit, a phenomenon known as quantum entanglement, which can take
place even at large distances.

When in an entangled state, the Qbits are impossible to describe separately and
they act as a single system. This results in the measurements being correlated, if one
Qbit is measured to be in the state |0⟩, the state of the second Qbit will also collapse
accordingly, even at large distances [10]. Entanglement is a fundamental phenomenon
exploited by most of the quantum technologies (including QKD, which was treated
previously) and will be a primary interest when discussing the emission of quantum
dots (QDs).
As mentioned before, Qbits are currently being implemented in many different
physical forms. The most promising implementations at the moment are based on
superconducting Josephson junctions, as demonstrated in 2019 by the Sycamore pro-
cessor [41]. Nevertheless, a significant amount of research is going toward a photon-
based quantum processor [25–27], both using linear [24] and non-linear [42] approaches
together with newer computational schemes as Boson-sampling [43,44]

give as a result of g(2)(0) = 0 [17]. As pointed out before, purity is of particular
importance in quantum cryptography as the presence of multiple photons can lead
to security fallacies and expose the communication to the photon number splitting
attack [46].
Indistinguishability
In classical mechanics there is no underlying law that prevents experiments to
distinguish particles in a system. In the study of perfect gases, for instance, it is
always conceived to have the possibility of following a specific particle of the gas
as its position and momentum evolve in time. In quantum mechanics, this is not
the case anymore due to the impossibility of specifying more than a complete set of
commuting observables for each particle.
Even if such a phenomenon can be seen as a limitation, it has many surprising
consequences. In the theory of statistical mechanics, perfect gases are mostly mod-
eled with the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution of speeds but it can be shown that the
quantum property of indistinguishability leads quantum particles to have different
statistics based on their spins. Particles with semi-integer (Fermions) spin obey the
Fermi-Dirac distribution and are subject to the Pauli exclusion principle, while parti-
cles with integer spin (Bosons) obey the Bose-Einstein distribution and can exhibit
a phenomenon known as Bose-Einstein condensation [7].
In a similar way to how purity was measured, the indistinguishability between
two photons can be characterized employing the Hong-Ou-Mandel setup [47] where
two photons impinge on a beam splitter and, if they are truly indistinguishable, they
always leave the beam splitter from the same output due to quantum interference. In
the context of quantum information, indistinguishability is important for the imple-
mentation of two-photon quantum gates as the CNOT gate mentioned in the previous
section [17].
Brightness
As it was mentioned, electromagnetic fields can be described by a superposition of
number states using the Fock basis. As the photons propagate in free space or in an
optical waveguide, the evolution in time of the quantum state can be represented as a
variation of the coeﬀicients Cn in Eq. 2.1 as a function of time. Decoherence together
with dissipative interactions with the environment as photon absorption can lead to
variations in those coeﬀicients. In particular, it can be shown that optical losses lead
to the growth of the C0 coeﬀicient, meaning that the superposition of states leans
toward the vacuum state |0⟩where no photons are present.

1.2.2
Implementations
In the last decades, different quantum systems have been shown to be suitable single-
photon sources for quantum information, some of which are introduced in the follow-
ing. This does not aim to be an exhaustive analysis of all the current technologies,
whether it is supposed to be a review of the possible alternatives.
Infancy
The first instance of single-photon emission was demonstrated in 1974 employing
a cascade transition of a mercury atom [48], another corroboration came in 1977 by
utilizing a sodium atom [49]. One of the easiest ways to implement a single-photon
source is to use a faint laser [50], due to the fact that coherent states have a Poissonian
photon number distribution it is possible to reduce the intensity of the beam to limit
the probability of multiple-photon emission, even if this procedure does not produce
anti-bunched light since, for coherent states, we have g(2)(τ) = 1 [9]. This technique
has become obsolete when non-linearities started to be employed.
Spontaneous parametric down conversion
The electromagnetic properties of the majority of materials surrounding us can
be described using a linear approximation where only a first-order susceptibility is
taken into account. There is however a range of non-linear materials where a second-
order susceptibility χ(2) must be taken into account. By using the Hamiltonian of
the system, which describes the time evolution of the field, it can be shown that
in such medium two photons are created in two output modes (idler and signal)
at the expense of the annihilation of a photon (pump) frequency consistent with
conservation of energy [9]. The idler and the signal are two spatially separated modes
and the difference in the emission angle is governed by conservation of momentum.
A material with a third-order susceptibility can also give rise to a similar process
where, instead of one, two photons are annihilated at the input generating two cor-
related photons at the output. For this reason, this process has been denominated
”four-wave mixing” [50].
These processes are known to be non-deterministic, photons are generated at
random times, which limits the brightness of the source, but they are generated si-
multaneously. This problem has been solved by detecting one of the two emitted
photons with the objective of heralding with a high probability the emission of the
==PAGE:24==
12
1 Quantum technology
twin photon. This technique is, to date, the best performing in generating single-
photons and it is also capable of generating polarization entanglement [17].
Crystal defects
Colour centers are defined as optically active vacancies within an ionic crystal.
Some examples are F-centres, rare earth ions, silicon-vacancy centers, and nitrogen-
vacancy (NV) centers in diamond [8,17]. Of these, the last two are the most promising
technologies as they guarantee stable operation at room temperature and a long life-
time of the excited state [51].The main limitation of NV-centers is related to the
presence of a metastable energy state, referred to as a ”shelving” state, with a rela-
tively long lifetime that can thermally couple to the excited state leading to a decrease
of both the single-photon purity and the brightness [50].
NV-centers have been proven to be suitable single-photon sources with second-
order correlation functions ranging from g(2)(0) = 0.26 [52] to g(2)(0) = 0.07 [53],
but the main limitation preventing this technique from prevailing over spontaneous
parametric down-conversion sources is related to the randomness of position and size
of the sources due to the non-deterministic fabrication process.
Quantum dots
As stated a few times, this project focuses on single-photon sources employing self-
assembled QDs, therefore the discussion regarding this specific technology is carried
out more in detail in the next chapter.
Others
In more recent years, single-photon emission was observed in a wider range of
devices such as 2D materials, carbon nanotubes [51], single molecules [54], single
atoms, single ions and collective excitations in ensables [50]

2.1
Charachteristics of quantum dots
In general, for bulk materials the optical properties do not depend on the size of the
sample taken into account. This is true up to a certain scale where the effects of
quantum mechanics cannot be ignored any further. By reducing the size in one of the
dimensions of the material there is a point where the uncertainty of the position of
a particle is low enough to increase the uncertainty of the momentum, giving rise to
an energy contribution related to the confinement of the particle. This phenomenon
is known as quantum confinement and takes place when the confinement energy is
at least comparable to the thermal energy of the particle, which can occur in low-
dimensional structures denominated quantum wells, which were first proposed in 1970.
By limiting the size in two dimensions the structure takes the name of quantum wire
while, if the structure confines the particles in all three dimensions, it is called a
QD [8]. Among other effects, a result of confinement is the change in the density
of states of the material: while in bulk materials it is proportional to the square
root of the energy, in quantum wells and quantum wires it takes the shape of a
step function (with a hyperbolic profile for quantum wires). In QDs, the density of
states is represented by vertical lines, which corresponds to quantized energy levels
as described in the following chapter [12].
2.1.1
Quantum dot epitaxial growth
QDs are fabricated by means of epitaxial growth, a peculiar type of material depo-
sition in which layers are formed with well-defined crystal structures relative to the
underlying layer.
Inside a epitaxy reactor, the substrate wafers are inserted in a
chamber which is pumped down to vacuum and heated . The chamber is then filled
with precursor gases which then decompose in more reactive species, moving toward
the substrate through a stagnant layer where they diffuse and distribute across the
surface [11].
==PAGE:26==
14
2 Project analysis
Once the material reached the surface, three different growth regimes can take
place depending on which force dominates the deposition.
Frank–van der Merwe
Also known as two-dimensional growth, the VW growth mode arises when the at-
tractive force between the surface and the atoms of the deposited material is dominant
compared to the interaction between the atoms themselves. This leads to uniform
atomic layer formation and it can be used for the production of quantum wells.
Volmer–Weber
The FM growth mode takes place when the deposited atoms have a greater ten-
dency to attract each other than to interact with the substrate. This leads to the
formation of islands or clusters.
Stranski–Krastanov
The SK growth mode is an intermediate case where the layer grows two-dimensionally
up to a critical thickness where the material starts to nucleate in clusters due to a
mismatch between the lattice constants of the material deposited relative to the sub-
strate. By carefully controlling the deposition parameters, time in particular, the
formation of self-assembled QDs is observed [13].
The presence of three growth modes can ultimately be traced back to chemical
potentials and surface energies. Given σs the surface energy of the substrate, σe that
of the epilayer, and σi that of the interface, it can be shown that the energy difference
∆σ related to the formation of the epilayer is expressed as
∆σ = σe + σi −σs
(2.1)
and based on the sign of ∆σ the growth mode can be deducted.
∆σ > 0 leads
to the VW growth while ∆σ < 0 leads to the FM mode if no lattice mismatch is
present and it leads to the SK mode otherwise. The surface energy difference can
ultimately be traced back to film strain caused by the difference in lattice constant
of the mismatched layer.
Older methods for epitaxial growth, such as liquid-phase epitaxy, have been sub-
ject to research but do not provide a suitable candidate for the production of QDs.
Currently, two main techniques are employed for QD epitaxial growth: MOVPE
(metal-organic vapor phase epitaxy) which makes use of a chemical deposition mecha-
nism, and MBE (molecular beam epitaxy) which employs a purely physical deposition
mechanism. In MOVPE systems, precursor gases are injected into a chamber at high
pressure and diffuse toward the sample where they chemically react. Even though
the process is complicated by the need for complex chemical reactions, this makes the
system simpler to maintain and less expensive to produce. The speed of production

precise way, without the need for any chemical reaction. This enhances the control

2.1.3
Biexciton-exciton cascade and fine structure splitting
In the context of this project, two of the excitonic complexes discussed earlier are of
particular interest: the biexciton |XX⟩and the exciton |X⟩. In the case of strong non-
resonant excitation, carriers diffuse in the QD forming excitonic complices leading to a
radiative cascade. Two photons are emitted during this process: one in the transition
from biexciton to exciton |XX⟩→|X⟩and one in the transition from exciton to the
ground state |X⟩→|g⟩. The frequency of the two photons differs slightly due to an
energy contribution caused by the Coulomb interaction, which modifies the biexciton
binding energy compared to that of the exciton [17]. In the ideal case of a perfectly
symmetric QD, the two emitted photons are entangled and have circular polarization.
In reality, as the confinement potential symmetry is reduced, the relaxation from the
biexciton to the ground state can take place in two different ways due to a phenomenon
known as fine structure splitting (FSS).

phenomenon can be thought of as a consequence of the reduction of the symmetry of

linked to the morphological properties of the QD by use of a pseudo-potential [59].

2.2.1
Quantum dots epistructure
Among the different possible methods to manipulate the energy splitting of the exciton
that will be discussed in a later chapter, the project described in this context focused
on exploiting an electric field to affect the internal potential of the QD with the goal of
reducing the anisotropy and recovering the symmetry of the in-plane band structure.
The first step of this endeavor is to embed the QD in the center of an epitaxially
grown nanostructure characterized by three regions with different concentrations of
impurities: a p-i-n junction.
As semiconductors are doped with different elements, the concentration of carriers
in the conduction and the valence band changes, leading to a shift in the Fermi level
relative to the band structure. As electron donors are introduced, the semiconductor is
said to be n-doped, the concentration of electrons in the conduction band is increased,

As two adjacent regions of a semiconductor are doped with different types of
impurities, a p-n junction is formed and, at equilibrium, the gradient of concentration
of carriers leads to the diffusion of excess electrons from the n-doped region to the
p-doped region and the diffusion of excess holes in the opposite direction.

Electrical contacts
As the epistructure is doped to define a p-i-n junction in the growth direction, applying
a single bias to the junction results in a strong field in the z-direction. In the context

2.2.3
Device positioning relative to the emitters
Due to the self-assembling character of the epitaxial growth of QDs, the positioning
of the structures on the wafer is of random nature. Because of this, in the context of
this project, the positioning of the devices to tune the FSS is non-deterministic and
the presence of a QD in the center of the electrical contacts is not guaranteed for all
devices. For this reason, a large number of devices have been fabricated to improve
the chances of success. Although in recent years extensive research has been aiming
for the deterministic positioning of the QDs on a patterned structure [61, 62], the
presence of a processed surface still limits the quantum eﬀiciency and the brightness
of the emitted photons [17]. In a recent paper [63], a technique for the deterministic
positioning of a structure relative to a QD was developed based on a near-infrared
imaging method employing a thermo-electrically cooled InGaAs camera. The process
described in this thesis is mostly compatible with the deterministic positioning of the
devices, although some challenges are present: the positioning of the QDs is only
possible if the metallic reflector is present to enhance the emission.
This creates
complications in the process prior to the aluminum deposition, as it will be discussed
further in chapter 4.

In this chapter, a deeper explanation of how the project was developed is followed by
contextualization and comparison of the work with the current research and related
papers that inspired the project. The following is to be taken as a literature analysis
and a proof of understanding of the works associated with this thesis.
3.1
Previous research which inspired the project
The devices developed and described in this work are a direct evolution of two separate
papers: one from P. Holewa [1], who also co-supervised part of this project, about
the improvement in the brightness of quantum emitters, and one from H. Ollivier [2]
in which electrical control of the excitonic fine structure was achieved. As the project
described in previous chapters merges these two works, an in-depth analysis of both
is given in the following.
3.1.1
Extraction eﬀiciency enhancement
In a work from 2022 by Holewa et al. [1], the enhancement of brightness of InAs/InP
QD heterogeneously integrated on Si was demonstrated to be attainable with the
introduction of a metallic reflector underneath the emitters. Numerical simulations
were initially employed to investigate the electromagnetic field distribution, showing
a broadband increase in extraction eﬀiciency up to sixteen times and reduced leakage
of light into the substrate.
The experimental realization begins with the epitaxial growth of an array of low-
density, self-assembled InAs/InP QDs, emitting at 1.55 µm and embedded in a λ
-cavity followed by the deposition of a silicon dioxide layer and an aluminum layer,
acting as the metallic reflector. The sample is then flipped, chemically bonded to Si
and the substrate is removed making use of a sacrificial layer to stop the etch.
To verify the results obtained in the simulations, two experimental approaches
were pursued by the authors.
An initial estimate of the extraction eﬀiciency was

26

obtained by statistically analyzing the broad-band emission of the structure endowed
with the reflector in comparison with a reference structure. This method averages
over the emission of different QDs with relatively wide size distribution leading to
an average extraction eﬀiciency η ≈6%.
A more advanced method entailed the
fabrication of a 2-3 µm large mesa used to isolate a single QD with the objective of
quantitatively investigating the brightness enhancement of a solitary emitter without
the need for statistical analysis, which lead to an extraction eﬀiciency up to η ≈9.5%.
The paper concludes the discussion by reporting on the single-photon purity making
use of the second-order correlation function g(2)(τ) and pointing out the robustness
of the design and the compatibility with current fabrication techniques employed by
the industry. Further development was realized more recently by the same authors
by implementing an in-plane Bragg grating cavity to limit leakage and improve the
emission rate by Purcell enhancement [63].
The described paper was utilized as a starting point for the fabrication of the de-
vices described in this thesis. The implementation of a metallic reflector underneath
the emitters was employed and the use of chemically assisted bonding for the fabri-
cation of structures underneath the epitaxially grown QDs was utilized to fabricate
additional components below the emitter.
3.1.2
Electrical tuning of the fine structure splitting
This paper by Ollivier et al. [2] introduces the idea of controlling the FSS of In-
GaAs/GaAs QDs via three-dimensional control of the electric field making use of
electrical contacts. Three independent electric potentials are applied to large p-i-n
diode structures, connected to a micro-pillar cavity defined by distributed Bragg re-
flectors consisting of pairs of λ/4-thick GaAs/AlGaAs layers. The QDs are embedded
in the intrinsic region of the junction and a gold coating on the back of the substrate
act as a voltage reference as the three electric potentials are tuned.
Finite element method simulations were performed to get insight into the electric
field distribution at the center of the pillar, showing a large component of the field in
the growth direction when the diodes are reverse-biased (as pointed out in Sec. 2.2.1)
and a more peculiar behavior for the in-plane component based on the forward/reverse
bias combinations of the diodes. The authors then proceed to propose two schemes
of generation of an arbitrary in-plane electric field manipulating one voltage, based
on the forward or reverse bias of two of the other two contacts.
The FSS was experimentally probed by making use of polarization-resolved micro-
photoluminescence measurements at liquid helium temperature by exciting the QDs
with a continuous wave under quasi-resonant conditions. Even though the FSS was
too small to be spectrally resolved, a shift in the amplitude of the exciton photolumi-
nescence peak energy was observed as a function of the polarization. By measuring
the excitonic energy for different polarization and repeating the measurement with
multiple diode biases, a drastic reduction of the FSS energy was observed for certain
values of the voltages, together with a rotation of the exciton eigenaxis. This behavior

non-degenerate exciton levels swap when ∆Efss = 0.
This article inspired the shape of the mesa structure described previously and
fabricated during the course of this master project, with the main differences being
the absence of the distributed Bragg reflector cavity and the use of QDs emitting

In addition to what this paper presented, the use

3.2
Alternative techniques for entanglement restoral
Among different ways of tuning the FSS [64], the electrical method is one of the most
intuitive as it acts directly on the confinement potential of the QD with the aim of
removing its anisotropy. In this section, other techniques employed in research are
mentioned and discussed.
3.2.1
Magnetic tuning
Discussions of the use of a magnetic field to manipulate the excitonic energy structure
date back to 1999 [65], pointing out how the Zeeman interaction is capable of altering
the energy levels. The Zeeman effect is a peculiar behavior of fermions when subjected
to a magnetic field, it can be shown in terms of perturbation theory that a magnetic
field will lead to an energy splitting based on the spin and angular momentum of the
particle [7].
In 2006 it was demonstrated to be possible to use the Zeeman effect to alter and
reduce the FSS: since the biexciton state |XX⟩is spin-neutral, its interaction with
the magnetic field is limited, in contrast to the exciton state |X⟩which can have
total angular momentum of ±1 (bright states) or ±2 (dark states). The presence of
the magnetic field can lead to a coupling of the bright and dark states, this partially
allows optical transitions that would have been less likely in the absence of the Zeeman
interaction due to a need for a spin-flip. If the field is low enough, the bright-dark
separation induced by the Zeeman effect is proportional to the square of the magnetic
field and it can be shown that the separation in the bright exciton states, which is
also quadratic as a function of magnetic field, can have a negative sign for certain
properties of a QD, leading to a reduction of the FSS [3].
When compared to the electrical approach that was utilized in this project, mag-
netic tuning of the FSS offers an advantage of simplicity in terms of the fabrication
process. As a downside, since the field is applied by an external apparatus, the on-chip
integration is obstructed by the need for a magnetic field of several Tesla generated
by superconducting magnets. Moreover, the previously dark states become active
introducing more allowed transitions [64].

3.2.2
Optical tuning
In most cases, the excitation of the biexciton state in QDs is achieved by optical
pumping. In the context of this project, a laser may be employed to achieve the
excitation of the system while the electric field generated by the external bias will
be responsible for the tuning of the energy levels.
Instead of employing a static
electric field, a different approach entails the use of a second ligth source. In certain
conditions in the context of the Jaynes-Cummings model, an electron can be in a
particular set of states denominated ”dressed states” when interacting strongly with
an oscillating electric field. This introduces an energy splitting between levels, in
relation to the intensity of the field and the detuning, and such effect takes the name
of AC or dynamic Stark effect [9].
In a paper by Muller et al. [4] from 2009 the AC Stark effect was employed to shift
the excitonic energy levels of the bright doublet by controlling the intensity of the field
since the dressed states have a separation of
√
Ω+ δ where Ωis the Rabi frequency
(which is intensity dependent) and δ is the detuning. Setting a difference with previous
research, the authors proceeded to also establish the presence of entanglement by
reconstructing the two-photon density matrix via correlation measurements making
use of the g(2)(τ) function.
In comparison to the methods entailing externally applied fields, this kind of
optical tuning presents the advantage of allowing in-situ manipulation as the QDs
can be embedded in a waveguide, coupled with the laser.
The main downside is
the increased complexity of fabrication due to the presence of a second laser and,
when compared to the technique utilized in this project, optical Stark effect tuning
is expected to allow lower control on the excitonic structure as different states couple
with different coupling eﬀiciencies with the control light source [64].
3.2.3
Strain-based tuning
As pointed out previously, the FSS is a consequence of the asymmetric potential
of the QD over a certain in-plain axis. An intuitive solution to the problem is to
”mechanically” change the amount of strain in the structure, this is what was realized
in a 2015 paper [5] where the authors fabricated InGaAs QDs in a nanomembrane
integrated and aligned onto a piezoelectric crystal, a particular material that is able
to convert voltage differences into strain in the crystal lattice.
Together with optical tuning, strain-based approaches allow the in-situ manipu-
lation of the FSS and, thanks to well-established fabrication processes, it has the
potential to be integrated with other techniques, including the one employed in this
project. Complications are introduced by the need for stabilization of the piezoelectric
materials and the localization of the stress to small regions.

3.2.4
Post-emission method
One of the most recent and novel approaches to the problem of entanglement degra-
dation was proposed in 2022 by Varo et al. [6] based on a seminal paper [60] from
2008. As discussed before, once the photon-pair is emitted as a consequence of the
biexciton-exciton cascade, the reduced degree of entanglement is explained in terms
of which-way information but a more in-depth analysis [60] showed that the resulting
two-photon state is still an entangled one, although not a Bell state anymore.
Instead of acting on the source, Varo and colleagues [6] opted for an entanglement-
restoral protocol constituted by unitary operations on the state of the emitted photons
to change the phase and compensate for the difference accumulated before the emis-
sion of the second photon. It can be shown that if a device is assumed to impart a
phase difference depending on both frequency and polarization, a condition on the
parameters of the device can be found so that the phase difference is independent of
the random lifetime of the exciton state.
A proposed implementation of such a device involves the use of the Pockels effect:
a transversal electric field applied to a birefringent crystal is able to change the
phase of polarizations aligned along two different axes based on the intensity of the
field, allowing the phase-shift to be tuned. The paper concludes the discussion by
presenting Monte Carlo simulations to investigate the robustness of the solution and
argues about the simplicity of realization of such an approach.
Although experimental confirmations of the feasibility of this approach are still
lacking, a clear advantage is the possibility of recovering the entanglement without
affecting the fine structure at all. In a similar way to strain-based tuning, this method
also presents great potential in integration with other methods: as the FSS can be
reduced with other techniques, the degree of entanglement can be further enhanced
by acting on the emitted photons as described above. A possible complication arises
in the parameters of the setup, the FSS has to be known to compensate for the
phase difference and, if a tuning method is employed, the setup parameter must be
changed according to the tuning. The main downside of the proposed implementation
is the need for the manipulation of the photons after the emission, which introduces
challenges in the on-chip fabrication.

4.2
Epitaxial growth of the sample
The author of this work was not involved in the epitaxial growth of the QDs and
was handed the sample with a nano-structure already present. Nevertheless, a brief
description of the growth procedure is reported here for the sake of reproducibility.
The growth began with an epi-ready (001)-oriented InP substrate in the low-
pressure metalorganic vapor-phase epitaxy (MOVPE) TurboDisc reactor making use
of hydrogen as a gas carrier and four gas precursors:
arsine (AsH3), phosphine
(PH3), tertiarybutylphosphine (TPB) and trimethylindium (TMIn).The growth of
a InP buffer layer is followed by a 200nm-thick In0.53Ga0.47As lattice-matched layer

and AsH3, after which 50 nm of Si-doped InP was grown followed by 106.2 nm of
intrinsic InP. Consequently, a deposition of a nominally 1.22 ML-thick InAs layer at
a growth rate of 0.53 ML/s led to the nucleation of QDs in the Stranski-Krastanov
regime under a TMIn flow of 11.9 µmol/min and a AsH3 flow of 595 µmol/min (im-
plying a V/III ratio of 50) which were then annealed for 3.5 s. The QD growth was
then followed by another 106.2nm-thick layer of intrinsic InP after which 50 nm of
Zn-doped InP were grown to conclude the process.

4.7.2
InP and SiO2 etch
The layered structure consists of a stack of InP on top of SiO2 on top of Al. Due to the
selectivity of HCl, the InP layer can be easily removed in a solution of hydrochloric
acid and phosphoric acid (as in section 4.2.2) without affecting the underlying oxide
layer. On the contrary, wet etching of silicon dioxide employing hydrofluoric acid will
not ensure the survival of the aluminum layer underneath, as BHF dissolves both
SiO2 and Al. To solve this problem, dry etching is utilized instead. Making use of a
CHF3 plasma in III-V RIE ensures a good selectivity between SiO2 and aluminum,
ensuring a good ohmic contact after the metal deposition.
A simpler solution to this problem would be to create an opening in the silicon
dioxide layer prior to BCB bonding, during the deposition of the p-contact. This
would create some regions where SiO2 is replaced by metallic contacts, leading to the
need to only etch InP to expose the metallization instead of needing to remove a layer
of oxide.

4.10
Possible techniques of deterministic positioning of
the devices
As mentioned in section 2.2.3, recent studies [63] showed how the localization of the
QDs on the substrate is possible via near-infrared imaging, with the only problem of
needing a high emission intensity due to electrical noise in the camera sensors. The
solution that was adopted was to employ the increased extraction eﬀiciency of the
devices equipped with the metallic reflector described in prior works [1]. As was pre-
sented in this chapter, the fabrication of the aluminum reflective layer is followed by
BCB bonding, which allows the reflector to be between the emitter and the substrate.
This implies that the imaging is only possible subsequent to bonding and any fabri-
cation step prior to it has to account for the impossibility of localizing the QDs. The
only step that would lack the possibility of such localization is the lithographic fabri-
cation of the p-contact, presented in section 4.4.2, while every process step subsequent
to BCB bonding could easily be adapted to deterministic positioning.
In order to solve the problem, three possible solutions are hereby proposed by the
author, although none completely satisfying from the point of view of effectiveness
and eﬀiciency. The first solution would be to non-deterministically fabricate the p-
contact in such a way that the deterministic positioning of the mesa and n-contact
has a high probability of being aligned with the p-contact (e.g. a grid of adjacent
metallic rings covering the entire surface). This solution is the least plausible as the
alignment and centering of the QDs compared to the p-contact would be unlikely.
A second possibility would be a change of paradigm in the design of the structure:
instead of employing a p-i-n junction, an intrinsic layer of InP containing the QDs
could be covered with a SiO2 layer, followed by the aluminum layer and skipping
the deposition of the p-contact. This would lead to the formation of a metal-oxide-
semiconductor (MOS) structure that would be able to apply a field to the center of the
mesa, even though charge accumulation would take place at the SiO2/InP interface
causing interactions of the QD with the environment.
The third possible solution would increase significantly the complexity of the fab-
rication but with the advantage of allowing perfect alignment between the p-contact
and the mesa. This solution consists of performing a double BCB bonding: prior
to the p-contact deposition, a silicon dioxide layer and an aluminum layer would be
deposited as presented previously [1]. After that BCB bonding would be performed
onto a carrier, the InP substrate would be etched in HCl, and the position imaging
of the QDs would be carried out. After this, the process would resume as in section
4.4.1, meaning that an oxide layer deposition would be followed by the lift-off of the
==PAGE:67==
4.10 Possible techniques of deterministic positioning of the devices
55
p-contact and the deposition of a new aluminum reflective layer. At this point, a
second BCB bonding can be performed onto a silicon carrier and the previous car-
rier could be etched (making sure to protect the second carrier), and the oxide and
aluminum employed for the imaging would be etched in BHF after the layer of BCB
is removed via plasma ashing. This solution introduces many complications and the
choice of carriers wafers should be investigated further to ensure the possibility of
removing the first carrier once the second bonding is performed, without affecting
the second carrier. The use of nanoimprint lithography should also be considered to
be a valid candidate to perform a similar process.
A fourth idea, for which the author of this thesis does not take credit, is the use
of titanium dioxide for the fabrication of the p-contact: thanks to the high degree
of transparency, TiO2 would be a suitable replacement for the silicon dioxide layer
employed as a part of the optical cavity (section 4.4.1) while still providing a low
enough electrical resistance to form a conductive path to the p-doped region of the
InP nano-structure, effectively removing the need for the fabrication of the p-contact
and simplifying the process.

The devices were characterized by making use of the probe station and two source-
meters to measure the I/V characteristic curves of the semiconducting junctions (pre-
sented in section 2.2.1). The setup allows for the contact of four needle-shaped probes,
connected to the measurement tools with coaxial cables, and movable with precision
with the use of handles.
Two different kinds of measurements were performed, a first set of measurements
was taken to quantify the deviation in the I/V curve between neighboring devices
and the effect of the length of the bridges connecting the metallic contact to the
center of the device.
To do this, the voltage was swept between -12 V and 5 V,
the resulting current was measured, and the process was repeated for each device
on all three electrical contacts to verify the correct operation of the junctions. The
results from the three contacts were then averaged and compared to other devices,
with particular attention to the bridge length connecting the contact to the central
mesa. As shown in Fig 5.19, the profile of the characteristic curve is in reasonable
accordance with what was introduced in section 2.2.1. Unfortunately, as it appears
from the plots, no clear pattern is present in the characteristic curve of devices with
different lengths, even though a different intensity of the in-plane field is expected.
Nevertheless, the profile of the characteristic curve is in reasonable accordance with
what was introduced in section 2.2.1 and represented graphically in Fig. 2.6.

Conclusion
As stated in the Summary, the aim of the project was the development of a fabrication
process for a device to manipulate the excitonic fine structure splitting of epitaxially
grown, self-assembled quantum dots emitting in the telecom frequencies.
That goal was fulfilled by the process design and optimization, together with
experimental confirmation of the solidity of the process in the context of cleanroom
fabrication.
In this work, a theoretical discussion of the physics of single-photon sources and
their application to quantum information was related to the development of a bright
source of entangled single photons based on quantum dots with controllable excitonic
fine structure splitting.
The presentation of the device developed was then put in the context of state-
of-the-art technology. The relevant literature was first discussed by first presenting
research papers related to the structure of the devices, after which some alternative
methods of entanglement-restoral were compared; for each of them advantages and
problems were discussed, and they were compared with the device presented in this
work.
During the project development, multiple obstacles related to the fabrication had
to be overcome, which led to two iterations of the devices. Each step of the process
was tailored to be consistent with the cross-contamination restrictions related to the
cleanroom.
The final success of the process was proven by the electrical characterization of
the devices, measuring the current/voltage curves for all prepared devices. I/V curves
demonstrate that the profile of the characteristic curve is in reasonable accordance
with the one expected. In reverse, no clear pattern is present in the characteristic
curve of devices with different distances between the contact and the source.
The natural evolution of the project was introduced by describing the deterministic
positioning of the devices relative to the quantum dots and is expected to involve the
implementation of nanocavities based on circular Bragg gratings [63].
In conclusion, this research has shown the feasibility of the fabrication of elec-
trical contacts with the objective of manipulating the excitonic fine structure of a
quantum dot embedded in a p-i-n junction by controlling the in-plane electric field.
A reproducible fabrication process has been developed and the experimental results
show that the device meets the expected characteristics, giving the basis for further
experimental research.
