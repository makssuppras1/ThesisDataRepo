Abstract 
Calcium peroxide (CaO2) combined periodate (IO4-) process is a novel combination in 
degrading persistent micropollutants. Periodate-based advanced oxidation processes 
and periodate/hydrogen peroxide have been reported to be efficient in abating organic 
contaminants, the mechanism and reactive species also have clear report. But there’s 
limited research been conducted on the implementation of this restoration technique, 
which restricts the utilization of field experiences to further enhance comprehension 
and optimize restoration design practices. This research mainly focusing on the 
performance of CaO2/IO4- system in degrading carbamazepine and doing some 
optimization in operating the system. 
Result showed that carbamazepine could fully degraded in 5 min with 0.1 g/L CaO2 
and 1.0 mM IO4-, while the best operating initial pH was at 3. The degradation rates 
and kinetic rate of carbamazepine removal increased with the increase concentration 
of CaO2 and IO4-. The carbamazepine degradation was significantly influenced by the 
initial pH, when pH at 2 and 3 the removal rate of carbamazepine were both 100% 
and the kinetic rate constants were 0.9066 min-1 and 0.9247 min-1, when pH was 5 and 
11 the removal rate of carbamazepine were 76.66% and 43.97% after 5 min and the 
kinetic rate constants were rapidly decreased to 0.089 min-1 and 0.056 min-1. The 
natural water matrix including chloride ions (Cl-), nitrate ions (NO3-), phosphate ions 
(PO43-) and humic acid (HA) presented influence on the CaO2/IO4- system. NO3- and 
PO43- didn’t show significant influence on the system at all concentration addition, 
while chloride ions and humic acid showed significant impact on the degradation of 
carbamazepine. The radical scavenging experiments explained the generation 
mechanism of reactive oxygen species (ROS) and found out the main reactive species 
in carbamazepine’s degradation was ·OH. To further investigate the application of 
CaO2/IO4- system in real practice, the degradation of 24 micropollutants in real 
WWTP secondary effluent was tested which showed satisfactory efficiency.

1. Introduction 
The production and consumption of pharmaceuticals have grown rapidly in recent 
decades all over the world (Voulvoulis et al., 2015a). The pharmaceuticals are usually 
classified as antibiotics, antiviral, analgesic, and hormonal (Velempini et al., 2021). 
When the concentration of these pharmaceuticals is low, they possess biological 
activity and can be absorbed into the bloodstream. Once absorbed they may persist in 
the human body or other animal’s body for quite a long time (Mezzelani et al., 2018). 
With the high consumption of these pharmaceuticals, a big problem has been 
gradually beginning to emerge which is pharmaceutical pollution. Limited by 
economic and technical means, wastewater containing pharmaceutical ingredients 
from municipal wastewater, hospital wastewater, intensive animal husbandry, and the 
pharmaceutical industry will go into the natural waterbody and will finally threaten 
human health and aquatic lives (Mezzelani et al., 2018; Ondarza et al., 2019; Patel, 
Kumar, Kishor, Mlsna, Pittman, et al., 2019). The traditional wastewater treatment 
plants (WWTPs) are no designed to remove pharmaceutical compounds, which leads 
to the consequences that some chemicals have only 10% removal rate (Patel, Kumar, 
Kishor, Mlsna, Pittman, et al., 2019). There are several characteristics of 
pharmaceutical wastewater: it’s originating from highly concentrated antibiotic 
wastewater, exhibiting notable fluctuations in quantity, complex composition, low 
carbon-to-nitrogen ratio (C/N), high sulfate content and biological toxicity. (Guo et al., 
2017). All these characteristics make pharmaceutical wastewater hard to treat with 
traditional processing means. Carbamazepine is chosen as the representative 
pharmaceutical pollutant in this research, it’s widely used in the treatment of epilepsy 
and neuropathic pain (‘American Society of Health-System Pharmacists’, 2016). For 
decades of using, carbamazepine can be found in 62% of the 1052 sampling sites 
analyzed in 258 rivers worldwide and may cause potential environment problems 
(Wilkinson et al., 2022). 
==PAGE:8==
2 
 
In decades, how to treat pharmaceutical pollutants has gained quite a lot attention 
because the traditional treatment technology can’t fully degrade the persistent organic 
chemicals such as pharmaceuticals (Joss et al., 2006). Advanced oxidation processes 
(AOPs) have been promoted to degrade persistent compounds (Chen et al., 2022a; 
Oturan & Aaron, 2014). One of the earliest AOP technology was Fenton reaction 
which was discovered by H.J.H. Fenton in 1894 that he reported H2O2 could be 
activated by ferrous (Fe2+) salts to oxidize tartaric acid (Babuponnusami & 
Muthukumar, 2014). Then in later decades, the significance of ·OH reactions has been 
widely acknowledged, and a comprehensive compilation of over 1700 rate constants 
for these reactions with both organic and inorganic compounds in aqueous solutions is 
available (Buxton et al., 1988a). Then in a later investigation, some new oxidants 
were found to have the ability to generate reactive oxygen species (ROS) or other 
active radicals such as CH3C(O)O· and SO4•– (Ao et al., 2021; Caretti & Lubello, 
2003; Furman et al., 2010; Lubello et al., 2002). These investigations greatly 
promoted the development of AOPs technology. Peroxymonosulfate (PMS) or 
peroxydisulfate (PDS) based AOPs are popular in recent years, in sulfate radical-
based advanced oxidation processes (SO4•–-AOPs), the generation of highly reactive 
and short-lived sulfate radicals (SO4•–) occurs in situ through the cleavage of the 
peroxide bond within the persulfate molecule via energy and electron transfer 
reactions (Ike et al., 2018; Oh et al., 2016). But PMS and PDS based AOPs will 
produce high amounts of sulfate ions (SO42–) and when they are combined with 
calcium (Ca2+) or manganese (Mg2+) may cause fouling in further water treatment 
procedures (Y. Liu & Mi, 2012). Indeed, despite their advantages, these techniques do 
possess certain limitations to varying degrees. 
In this research, a novel periodate (IO4–) and calcium peroxide (CaO2) system was 
selected as the method to degrade persistent micropollutants. Calcium peroxide has 
been used as an amendment to supply external oxygen in agriculture, horticulture and 
sylviculture for decades in worldwide for its stable releasing of oxygen in soil (Thani 
==PAGE:9==
3 
 
et al., 2016). Comparing to hydrogen peroxide (H2O2), CaO2 has strong thermal 
stability, and less possibility to be influenced by the carbon dioxide and moisture in 
the air which made it has large benefits in industrial applications (Lu et al., 2017). So 
CaO2 is used as a substitute for H2O2. There’re also multiple advantages of using 
periodate (IO4–) in AOPs. Firstly, periodate is chemically stable and solid, suitable for 
long distance transportation and storage thus can save the cost in storage and 
transportation. Secondly, there’re multiple ways can activate periodate to form active 
free radicals such as ·OH, 1O2, IO3·, O2·, HO-2· to degrade undesirable pollutants in 
water (Bolong et al., 2009; Sirvio et al., 2011; M. Yan et al., 2018; Yun et al., 2017) 
such as using metal materials, non-metal materials or UV light. However, these 
activation methods still have limitations such as high consume of energy, and low 
efficiency in degrading micropollutants (Qian et al., 2015). 
Although there’re a certain number of studies have reported the combination of 
periodate and hydrogen peroxide, nevertheless, limited research has been conducted 
on the implementation of this restoration technique, which restricts the utilization of 
field experiences to further enhance comprehension and optimize restoration design 
practices. In this research, the combination of periodate and calcium peroxide shows a 
significant effect on the degradation of carbamazepine. To support the potential 
further use of this technology in real wastewater treatment process, the objective of 
our study is to address the following concerns: (1) finding the optimal operating 
conditions such as pH and dosage of periodate and calcium peroxide; (2) the impacts 
of environmental organic/inorganic matters on the reactivity of CaO2/IO4- system; (3) 
elucidate the reaction mechanism and identify the reactive species involved when 
utilizing this dual oxidant for the degradation of organic pollutants.

2. Theoretical background 
2.1 Pharmaceutical pollutants in the environment  
Pharmaceuticals are used as the representative micropollutants in wastewater 
treatment. With the development of medicine, the consume of antibiotics and the 
defense of multiple diseases all over the world, the use of various pharmaceuticals has 
increased rapidly. In 2015, revenue from the worldwide  pharmaceutical  market  
reached  1072  billion US dollar (Statista, 2016), and even in 2010 the EU’s 
pharmaceutical market reach to totaling 192 billion€ as the second biggest market in 
worldwide (IMS 2011a, BPI 2011). In this environment of heavy drug use, drug 
residues have been found in almost all environmental matrices (Patel, Kumar, Kishor, 
Mlsna, U. Pittman Jr., et al., 2019; Tran et al., 2018). One of the reasons is the existing 
wastewater treatment technology in WWTPs are not designed to treat pharmaceuticals, 
also the low concentration and complexity make pharmaceuticals hard to remove 
(Shirkoohi et al., 2020).  
As the existing treatment processes employed in wastewater treatment plants 
(WWTPs) fail to effectively degrade pharmaceutical compounds and the continuous 
input of these kind of wastewater (Roberts et al., 2016), these substances tend to 
accumulate in the effluent water and sludge of WWTPs (Kühne et al., 2001), 
subsequently entering natural water bodies and soil systems, thus impacting 
organisms such as fish and plants. Although the initial concentrations of these 
pharmaceuticals introduced into the environment may not be substantially high 
(Voulvoulis et al., 2015b), it is crucial to give due consideration to potential chronic 
toxicity, as aquatic organisms are exposed to these pharmaceuticals throughout a 
significant portion of their lifespans (Fent et al., 2006). Unlike pesticides, 
pharmaceuticals are designed to modify behavior or physiology rather than aim to kill 
organisms (Arnold et al., 2014), so pharmaceuticals are designed to be biologically 
active even at very low contents (Fabbri & Franzellitti, 2016). Considering all these

characteristics of pharmaceuticals, people know very little about the long-term effects 
of pharmaceuticals exposure (Fent et al., 2006) and actually when pharmaceuticals go 
into the environment and start to go inside of organisms even humans, the chronic 
effect may not become apparent for several years (Voulvoulis et al., 2015b).

2.3 Advanced oxidation processes (AOPs) 
Advanced oxidation processes, in general, are a series of chemical treatment 
procedures designed to remove organic or inorganic materials in water and 
wastewater by oxidation through reactions with hydroxyl radicals (·OH) (Glaze et al., 
1987). Usually in wastewater treatment there’re several applications to achieve AOP, 
using ozone (O3), hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) or UV light (Munter, 2001). 
·OH radical is widely recognized as the most highly reactive oxidizing agent, it has  
high oxidation potential between 2.8 V (pH 0) and 1.95 V (pH 14) and are non-
selective that can rapidly reacts with numerous species. (Deng & Zhao, 2015). 
==PAGE:13==
7 
 
Hydroxyl radicals engage in the degradation of organic pollutants through four 
pathways: radical addition, hydrogen abstraction, electron transfer, and radical 
combination (Ruppert et al., 1994). There are 3 main types of hydrogen peroxide 
based AOPs have been successfully used for wastewater treatment for decades which 
are Fenton, UV/Fenton and UV/H2O2 systems (Neyens & Baeyens, 2003).  
Fenton's reagent, discovered approximately 100 years ago, was not employed as an 
oxidizing process for the destruction of toxic organic compounds until the late 1960s 
(Huang et al., 1993). The Fenton based wastewater treatment processes are widely 
acknowledged for their high efficiency in eliminating numerous hazardous organic 
pollutants from water (Neyens & Baeyens, 2003). Fenton’s reagent is a mixture of 
hydrogen peroxide and ferrous iron (Fe2+), it generates ·OH according to Equation (1) 
and ferric iron (Fe3+) can be reduced to ferrous iron (Fe2+) again in the Fenton-like 
reaction releasing a hydroperoxyl radical (·O2H) according to Equation (2) (Sychev & 
Isak, 1995).  
Fe2+ + H2O2 → Fe3+ + ·OH + OH-                                  (1) 
Fe3+ + H2O2 → Fe2+ + ·O2H + H+                                   (2) 
Basing on Fenton process, some researchers found that Fenton reagent can be 
enhanced by UV radiation according to the mechanisms in Equation (3) and Equation 
(4) (Faust & Hoigné, 1990). 
Fe(OH)2+ + hv → Fe2+ + ·OH                                       (3) 
 H2O2 +hv → 2·OH                                               (4) 
It can be found that UV light induces the reduction of ferric iron, leading to the 
liberation of additional hydroxyl radicals and the formation of ferrous iron. This 
process serves to counteract the accumulation of ferric iron and prevent premature 
termination of the Fenton reaction (Rott et al., 2017).

8 
 
Some newly found AOP technologies have also been gained a lot of attractions. Such 
as using peracetic acid (PAA) or peroxymonosulfate (PMS) or peroxydisulfate (PDS) 
to active AOP. For example, PAA can be activated by UV light to generate active 
species (Caretti & Lubello, 2003; Lubello et al., 2002): 
CH3C(O)OOH → CH3C(O)O· + ·OH                                   (5) 
CH3C(O)OOH + ·OH → CH3C(O)OO· + H2O                             (6) 
As for the PMS or PDS based AOPs, some mechanism of active species generation 
are happened as below (Furman et al., 2010; J. Lee et al., 2020):  
HSO5– + SO52– → HSO4– + SO42– + 1O2                                (7) 
S2O82– + 2H2O → HO2– + 2SO42– + 3H+                                (8) 
S2O82– + HO2– → SO4•– + SO42– + H+ + O2•–                           (9) 
 
2.4 Hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) and calcium peroxide (CaO2)  
Hydrogen peroxide is a reactive oxygen species and the simplest peroxide with an 
oxygen–oxygen single bond. When exposed to light, hydrogen peroxide can 
decompose slowly and can rapidly react with organic or reactive compounds (Ref. 
from Wikipedia). For decades, hydrogen peroxide has been used to reduce 
biochemical oxygen demand (BOD), chemical oxygen demand (COD) and complex 
organic matters both in domestic and industrial wastewater (Ksibi, 2006). But there’re 
multiple disadvantages of using hydrogen peroxide. First, the storage condition for 
hydrogen peroxide is strict and need a high cost. It should be stored in a container 
composed of non-reactive materials such as stainless steel or glass (Ref. website), and 
due to its decompose characteristics under light, an opaque container or brown bottle 
is needed to store it (Ref. website). Second, it can form explosive matters when mix 
with organic compounds (Ref. website) such as sugar, starch, alcohols, petroleum 
products, also itself can explode under impact, heat or electric spark which made 
==PAGE:15==
9 
 
hydrogen peroxide hard and dangerous to transport  (Sheriff et al., 2007). Third, the 
price of hydrogen peroxide is high. Industrial grade hydrogen peroxide costs $390–
500 per ton (Zhu & Logan, 2013). These things indicate that using hydrogen peroxide 
still has lots of problems need to be solved. 
Calcium peroxide has been used as amendment to supply external oxygen in 
agriculture, horticulture and sylviculture for decades in worldwide for its stable 
releasing of oxygen in soil (Thani et al., 2016). Comparing to hydrogen peroxide 
(H2O2), CaO2 has strong thermal stability, and less possibility to be influenced by the 
carbon dioxide and moisture in the air which made it has large benefits in industrial 
applications (Lu et al., 2017). With the development of environmental technologies, 
calcium peroxide has been used in wastewater treatment especially in degradation of 
refractory organic compounds (Zhai & Jiang, 2014). When calcium peroxide 
dissolved in water via: 
CaO2 + 2H2O → H2O2 + Ca(OH)2                                 (10) 
it can release a maximum of 0.47g H2O2/g CaO2 (Vol’nov & Petrocelli, 1966). 
Considering about liquid hydrogen peroxide is easily to decompose under acidic 
condition (‘On the Mechanism of the Decomposition of Hydrogen Peroxide by 
Catalase’, 1938), the H2O2 liberated from CaO2 is auto regulated by the dissolution of 
CaO2 so the loss of H2O2 can be reduced (Xue et al., 2016). Also as the “solid H2O2”, 
calcium peroxide is more stable than liquid hydrogen peroxide so the cost of storage 
and transportation is much lower and the safety is much higher. 
 
2.5 Periodate 
In recent times, there has been a growing interest in periodate-based advanced 
oxidation processes (AOPs) because of their capability to effectively remove a wide 
range of organic contaminants. Moreover, the benign end product of periodate-based 
==PAGE:16==
10 
 
AOPs, iodate (IO3–), adds to the appeal of these processes (Chia et al., 2004; Du et al., 
2020; H. Sun et al., 2020a). The periodate ion (IO4–) exhibits strong oxidizing 
properties, possessing a reduction potential of +1.6 V (C. Lee & Yoon, 2004). 
Periodate can be activated through various means, including energy sources such as 
ultraviolet and ultrasound, the presence of reduced transition-metal ions like Mn2+ and 
Fe2+, metal oxides, and metal-free reductants such as hydroxylamine and H2O2 (Chadi 
et al., 2019a, 2019b; Chia et al., 2004; Du et al., 2020; Kim et al., 2022a; H. Lee et al., 
2014; H. Sun et al., 2020b; Zong et al., 2021). The activation methods in periodate-
based AOPs can yield a diverse array of ROS, including iodate radicals (IO3·), 
hydroxyl radicals (·OH), superoxide radicals (O2·–), singlet oxygen (1O2), and high-
valent iron-oxo species (such as Fe(IV)) (Chia et al., 2004; Choi et al., 2018; D. 
Bokare & Choi, 2015; Kim et al., 2022a; R. Li et al., 2022; F. Liu et al., 2022; Long et 
al., 2021; Zong et al., 2021). Indeed, these reactive oxidizing species (ROS) are 
widely acknowledged for their ability to augment the oxidizing capacity of periodate 
based AOPs. 
When compared to other traditional advanced oxidation processes (AOPs), the 
notable advantage of using periodate lies in its chemical stability. This characteristic 
grant periodate a significant edge over other commonly used oxidants, such as 
hydrogen peroxide or ozone. Furthermore, the utilization of periodate does not result 
in the formation of any solid sludge, which is a prevalent issue associated with one of 
the commonly applied AOPs, namely Fenton chemistry (Sukhatskiy et al., 2023). 
Comparing to peroxymonosulfate (PMS) or peroxydisulfate (PDS) based AOPs, the 
final product of periodate based AOPs will only generates iodate (IO3–) which is a 
non-toxic chemical in the environment (Chia et al., 2004). While PMS and PDS based 
AOP will produce high amount of sulfate ions (SO42–) and when they are combining 
with calcium (Ca2+) or manganese (Mg2+) may cause fouling in further water 
treatment procedure (Y. Liu & Mi, 2012). Peracetic acid (PAA) based AOPs has also 
attracted growing attention recently. It has some advantages on wastewater treatment 
==PAGE:17==
11 
 
such as no toxic by-products forming and high efficiency in the reaction (Ao et al., 
2021). But the disadvantages of using PAA are just as obvious. PAA solutions 
exceeding 15% begin to exhibit some degree of explosiveness, instability, and 
reactivity (Kitis, 2004) which will greatly increase its cost and safety in storage and 
transportation. Comparing to these traditional AOPs and newly found AOPs, using 
periodate has its unique advantages.

3.2 Experiment set-up 
The batch experiments of carbamazepine degradation are set in 250 mL cone bottles. 
carbamazepine is dissolved in methanol as stock solution, add carbamazepine stock 
solution in the cone bottle, after MeOH volatile put a certain amount of CP with DI 
water and keep the reaction solution constantly magnetically stirring during the 
reaction. After setting the initial pH as 3 by adding sulfuric acid except the experiment 
of investigating the influence of pH, add PI to activate the reaction. Take 0.95mL of 
sample at the pre-chosen time, add 0.05mL of sodium thiosulfate immediately as 
quenching agent. For quenching experiments, 100 mM TBA, 2 mM BQ or 2 mM FFA 
were added into the solution with other chemicals before the reaction started. All 
samples were sent to HPLC-MS to analyze the concentration of carbamazepine. 
 
3.3 Analytical methods 
The carbamazepine and other 24 recalcitrant micropollutants were analyzed by 
HPLC-MS system. For analysis, 900 µL of filtered samples was transferred into a 
HPLC vial and 100 µL of internal standard was afterwards added into the HPLC vial. 
Lastly, 10 µL of sample from the HPLC vial was injected and analyzed by HPLC-
MS/MS. 
The HPLC system consisted of a pump, a column oven, a degasser and an auto-
sampler and was from Agilent 1290 Infinity, USA. Chromatographic separation was 
==PAGE:19==
13 
 
performed using a C18 column (2.1x50 mm, 1.8 µm, Eclipse Agilent, USA). The 
temperature of the HPLC column oven was 35 ºC, and the constant flow rate was 0.6 
mL/min. The HPLC gradient of 0.1 % formic acid in ultrapure water (solvent A) and 
0.1 % formic acid in acetonitrile (solvent B) was used in ESI + mode, while 5 mM 
ammonium formate in ultrapure water (solvent A) and 5 mM ammonium formate in 
90 % Actonitrile/10 % ultrapure water was used in ESI- mode. The mass spectrometer 
equipped with the triple quadrupole was from Agilent (6470 series) USA.  
Dynamic multiple reaction monitoring (MRM), which is able to automatically 
separate the transitions of micropollutants into multiple MRM tables according to the 
retention time window for each transition, was implemented for ESI + modes. For the 
analysis under ESI + mode, the HPLC gradient was initiated with 0% B for 2 min, 
followed by increasing gradient B to 15 %, 20 %, 36 %, 45 %, 60 %, and 75 % after 
2 min, 3 min, 2 min, 1 min, 2 min and 2 min, respectively. Before each injection, 
initial gradient conditions were re-established for 3 min. Nitrogen was used as the 
sheath gas as well as the collision gas. While for the analysis under ESI- mode, a 
normal MRM method was implemented. The HPLC gradient was initiated with 0% B, 
followed by a linear increase of gradient B up to 60 % within 8 min. Initial gradient 
conditions were also re-established for 3 min. For both ESI + and ESI-, the sheath gas 
flow was 12 L/min with the temperature of 400 °C, while the gas flow was 10 L/min 
with the temperature of 260 °C. The voltage for the capillary was 4500 V. While the 
voltage for ESI + and ESI- was 500 and 0 V, respectively.

The inhibitory effect of Cl− at different concentrations can be elucidated as follows. 
The interaction of Cl− with ·OH results in a series of reactions that give rise to the 
generation of chlorine species and chlorine-containing radicals as shown in Equation

The generated chlorine-containing radicals including Cl·, Cl2-· and ClHO-·, exhibit 
lower redox potentials compared to ·OH, resulting in their comparatively reduced 
oxidation capacity when targeting pollutants (Lin & Lin, 2017). Also Cl· and Cl2-· can

Some researchers have explained the mechanism in the effect of PO43- in the Fenton-
like system. PO43- have been reported to act as radical scavengers in previous studies 
(Jalali et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2022). Additionally, their introduction into the system 
can lead to alterations in the solution's pH, subsequently influencing the stability of 
H2O2 or ·OH (X. Zhang et al., 2022). Usually PO43- are present as H2PO4−, HPO42− or 
PO43− in waterbody (Cheng et al., 2023). Basing on the investigation by (Wang &

H2PO4− + ·OH → H2PO4· + OH-                                     (31)

1010 M-1s-1) (X. Yang et al., 2016). In contrast, the rate constant for the reaction 
between ·OH and PO43− (1.1×109 M-1s-1) falls within a similar range as the reaction 
of ·OH with organic compounds (109-1010 M-1s-1). Consequently, PO43− exhibits a 
greater capacity for scavenging ·OH when compared with H2PO4− and HPO42−.

HA is one of typical natural organic matters, it contains multiple functional groups 
such as carboxyl, carbonyl and phenolic groups (Koumaki et al., 2015a; A.-Y. Zhang 
et al., 2018). Indeed, the role of HA in the degradation of organic pollutants is diverse 
and can exhibit varying effects, including inhibition, promotion, or negligible impact 
on the degradation process (Hong et al., 2022; A.-Y. Zhang et al., 2018). In the 
traditional Fenton or Fenton-like processes HA can absorbed on the surface of catalyst 
(Fe3+ or Cu2+), leading to the occupation or masking of reaction sites, which was not 
conducive to the adsorption of pollutants and activation of H2O2. However, the 
quinone moieties present in HA serve as electron shuttles, facilitating the accelerated 
rate of electron transfer between H2O2 and catalysts. Consequently, this electron 
shuttle effect greatly enhances the production of ·OH (Yu et al., 2021; A.-Y. Zhang et 
al., 2018; Y. Zhang & Zhou, 2019). But in this research, from previous investigation, 
only ·OH as main ROS generated in the CaO2/IO4- system, after adding HA it shows 
significant inhibition on carbamazepine removal, this is because HA can compete with 
pollutants to ·OH (Cheng et al., 2023). HA's reductive properties arise from the 
presence of various unsaturated functional groups, including -SH and C=C (X. Li et 
al., 2022). The reaction rate of HA with ·OH was found to be remarkably rapid, 
reaching approximately 1.3*108 M-1s-1 (Jiang et al., 2021).

Another interesting thing is, some researches have claimed that HA is capable of 
absorbing energy, such as light energy and γ-rays (Koumaki et al., 2015b; Q. Liu et al., 
2011). This will lead to the consequence of an excited triplet state of HA’s (3HA⁎) 
generation, and then forming various reactive species: 
HA + hν → HA*                                              (34) 
3HA* + O2 → HA + 1O2                                        (35) 
3HA* + O2 → HA•+ + O2•–                                       (36) 
But in this research, the experiment design didn’t prevent the reactor from light, so it’s 
hard to explain the influence of light in this part of investigation. In summary, HA 
plays a inhibition role in H2O2-based Fenton-like oxidation systems, and this behavior 
might be linked to the specific form of HA present in different systems.

With the addition of FFA, the degradation of carbamazepine was inhibited which may 
prove the existence of 1O2. But according to the Kim’s investigation, the generation of 
1O2 can only happen under alkaline condition through Equation (39) (Kim et al., 
2022a): 
H2O2 + O2•– → •OH + OH– + 1O2                                 (39) 
To figure out the reason for this contradiction, the mechanism of using FFA to 
scavenge active species need to be checked. From the research done by (Gao et al., 
2022), they claimed that the importance of 1O2 is overestimated. In Gao’s research on 
the test of various of scavengers, TBA and other scavengers are unreactive with PMS, 
whereas FFA with PMS can have reaction slowly. Adding high concentration of FFA 
may cause considerable PMS consumption in the process which may substantially 
change the production of various reactive species such as •OH and 1O2 because PMS 
is the source of these reactive species. Thus, in many research of investigating the 
reactive species the effects of using FFA as scavenger of 1O2 is often neglected (Y. Li 
et al., 2020; Y. Yan et al., 2021). With the observed reactivity and potential 
consumption of periodate ions in the CaO2/IO4- system, a trustworthy assumption is 
==PAGE:39==

that IO4- might be significantly utilized by FFA. This utilization could lead to results 
where the importance of 1O2 is not as high as initially suggested, resulting in 
outcomes akin to those reported in the PMS system, as indicated in Gao's research. In 
summary, the CaO2/IO4- system mainly generates •OH and 1O2, and these two ROS 
were the main species in carbamazepine’s degradation from Figure 11 result.

5. Conclusion 
This research aimed to comprehensively explore the performance of the CaO2/IO4- 
system in the degradation of carbamazepine, which served as a representative 
example of persistent micropollutants present in water. The existing technologies in 
removing persist micropollutants have some limitations such as needing longer 
reaction time, inconvenience of storage and transportation of oxidants, high cost of 
producing the oxidants, or generation of useless or toxic by-products after reaction. In 
our research, we observed that carbamazepine was entirely degraded within a 5-
minute timeframe using 0.1 g/L CaO2 and 1.0 mM IO4- at pH 3. These results indicate 
that the CaO2/IO4- system represents an efficient binary oxidant combination for the 
==PAGE:41==
35 
 
removal of micropollutants and holds significant potential for real-world wastewater 
treatment applications. After optimizing the operation conditions of CaO2/IO4- system, 
which was setting the initial pH at 3 and more reasonable dosage of CaO2 and IO4-, 
the carbamazepine’s removal rate was 100% in 5 min. In the CaO2/IO4- system, the 
presence of certain common ions, such as NO3- and PO43-, was found to have a 
relatively minor impact on the process. Even at the highest investigated 
concentrations of these ions, the kinetic rate constants only decreased by 19.4% and 
28.8%, respectively. Moreover, the final removal rate of carbamazepine remained 
relatively unchanged despite their presence. As for Cl-, widely acknowledged as 
scavenger in AOPs, didn’t show significant inhibition in the removal of 
carbamazepine even at high concentration (50 mM). Among all the investigated water 
matrix, HA showed an significant inhibition in carbamazepine’s removal. In the 
investigation of reactive species, it was figured that ·OH and 1O2 played the key role 
in degrading carbamazepine. But due to the limitation of experimental conditions and 
testing methods, the role of 1O2 in the system is hard to judge which needs further 
investigation. The most satisfactory result is in the real WWTP secondary effluent, the 
24 micropollutants were completely removed after 2 hours, which means the 
CaO2/IO4- system is applicable in real wastewater to remove micropollutants.
