Abstract
The Athena mission has been chosen by ESA to cover the topic of “The hot and energetic
Universe”. This will be done by studying the x­ray Universe with a space­based telescope
that is meant to be launched in the first half of 2030s decade. The development of the
optic mirrors is advancing thanks to the continuous effort of different entities, DTU Space
among them.
Several candidates have arisen to be the overcoating material for the silicon­pore optics
mirror plates developed for Athena; this thesis will cover the characterization of diamond­
like carbon in these type of plates.
X­ray reflectometry and x­ray photoelectron spectroscopy are the techniques chosen to
accomplish this goal. Reflectivity data of angle scans is taken at DTU Space, while energy
scans are taken in a synchrotron beamline at BESSY II facilities, in Berlin. X­ray pho­
toelectron spectroscopy experiments are done at DTU Nanolab. Measurements taken
before and during the time­span of the thesis are analyzed.
Diamond­likeness of the samples is obtained from x­ray reflectometry and x­ray photo­
electron spectroscopy, showing a result below 25% for both analysis. Also, an intralayer
between the coated film and the substrate ­thought to be composed by a mix of iridium,
silicon and oxygen [IrSixOy]­ is observed in x­ray photoelectron spectroscopy data.

Introduction
From the beginning, our place in the world is something that has fascinated humankind.
This fascination has made us turn our sight up and look into the eyes of the Universe; the
simple act of turning our face 90◦has unveiled a little bite of our history: what we are,
how we did get here or what thrives life in this particular location among all the immensity
of the space. However, there are still countless questions awaiting an answer that make
the career of a philosopher an exciting journey into the unknown.
One can think that the word philosopher is out of place in a scientific article or a master
thesis project.However, I like to think: what is philosophy but the branch of knowledge
that search for an explanation to the questions that don’t have, at the moment, a straight
forward answer? So it’s clear that nowadays the scientist’s job merges with the philoso­
pher’s job.
Primeval philosophers were just curious people that had the time to worry about these
fundamental questions, they could spend hours and hours thinking about the Sun, the
Moon and the stars. When the question didn’t have a clear answer with the understood
laws of nature at that time, these phenomena ­metaphysical on its nature­ were related
to deities. It is interesting to have a look into the curse of history and realize that even
ancient civilizations, like the Mesopotamia, the Maya or the ancient Greek had already
asked the same questions we are still asking ourselves today, but of course, today we
have more and better tools to answer them, being the answer more constrained than it
was then and giving a non­divine answer to some of these events.
It was Aristarchus of Samos the first person to suggest an heliocentric system and to
say that we weren’t the center of the Universe but that we were moving around the Sun,
putting humankind in a less anthropocentric position. However, not until Copernicus that
this idea really had an accurate mathematical approach behind. After Copernicus, astron­
omy started to evolve; we started to look into the Universe with new eyes: not only with
human eyes (in the visible range) but also in new different “colors” (wavelengths).
We now know that the Universe has more colors than just the visible ones. What is more
important, some of the features of it and the objects wandering in its immensity are only
observed in wavelengths very distant to the visible light in the electromagnetic spectrum,
like radio or x­ray light.
The history then continues with the development of the quantum theory: it was Max Planck
the first person who talked about energy being transmitted in packages, or quanta. This
led to the expression of radiation as packages of energy (the Planck equation, E = hν),
each quantum with an associated wavelength (or frequency). It is found that x­rays are
one of the most energetic types of radiation and one of the most interesting things about
them is that their wavelength is in the order of the size of atoms, which makes more prob­
able the interaction with them.
During the beginning of the second half of the XIXth century, x­rays were just a kind of
radiation occurring in experimental discharge tubes. It was Wilhelm Röntgen who first
described the nature of them in his famous article “On a new kind of rays” in 1896 [21].
However, it wasn’t obvious that there were x­rays coming from different parts of the Uni­
verse; when a detector pointed from the ground to the sky no signal was detected. Nowa­
days, we know that Earth’s atmosphere acts as a shield from these photons, protecting
us, humans, from high­energy particles.

1.1
Athena mission and current status
Athena, named after the Greek goddess of wisdom, stands as an acronym for Advanced
Telescope for High­ENergy Astrophysics. The mission was selected as the second large
class missions (L2) of ESA’s Cosmic Vision program [22] for the topic ”The hot and ener­
getic Universe” [23], with the aim of addressing two main questions:
• How does ordinary matter assemble into the large­scale structures and gas clusters
we can observe in space today?
• How do black holes and supermassive black holes grow and shape the Universe?
The telescope was intended to be sent into a halo orbit around the second Sun­Earth
Lagrangian point L2 [23], with a similar approach to the recently launched James Webb
Space Telescope (JWST). However, the mission design has recently been changed (early
2021) to L1 to avoid Non X­ray Background (NXB) resulting from high­energy particles:
the two main contributions of NXB to L2 and L1 are particles from Earth’s magnetotail
and solar wind respectively; it has been concluded that the effect is worse in L2 than
in L1 [20]. While JWST will observe the Universe in the infrared region [2], below 10−3
keV, Athena will observe between 0.3 ­ 12 keV [19]. The difference of three orders of
magnitude in the electromagnetic energy observed makes the design and functionality of
the two telescopes completely different, but at the same time complementary, aiming to
observe different features (with different characteristic wavelengths) of relevant objects.
The main characteristics of Athena telescope are collected on Table 1.1, being the Wolter
Type I telescope, and in particular certain characteristics of the silicon pore optics (SPO)
design, what will be further discussed in this thesis.

1.1.1
Athena mirrors
X­ray telescopes functionality principle lays on the reflection theory explained more in
detail in Section 2.1; this means the reflection on surfaces occurs at very small grazing
angles and, in order to focus photons, telescopes must have relatively long focal lengths.
The particular geometry employed for this kind of goal is often Wolter Type I, shown in
Figure 1.5. The incoming photon is reflected twice, first from the paraboloid surface and
later from the hyperboloid.
Figure 1.5: Sketch of the reflections on a paraboloid­hyperboloid mirror as the one
defined in Wolter Type I telescopes, [4].
One advantage of using the Wolter Type I geometry is its capability of nesting shells of
mirrors and hence achieving a substantially higher Aeff. The nesting of a big amount of
shells is done at the expense of sacrificing pure hyperboloid and paraboloid shapes, using
instead different approximations; this reduces its angular resolution. An already existing
instrument in orbit, NuSTAR telescope is a clear example of this application, [28].
The second way to improve the effective area is with the design and fabrication of an ap­
propriate reflectivity surface for the mirrors, what is commonly called coating. An excellent
coating design finely tuned to mission requirements is thus mandatory.

One of the challenges to consider when nesting many mirrors is the amount of total weight
that each of the rings adds up. With this consideration, SPO technology has been under
development by ESA for more than a decade, [3, 24–26]. SPOs can be described as a
pile of silicon wafers that are connected to each other through ribs, achieving a smaller

Coating design
After some investigations the main coating selected for the mirrors is a 10 nm layer of
iridium [29–31]. Iridium shows a higher reflectivity in the desired range compared to other
high­Z elements like gold (Au) or platinum (Pt) [29] and seems to be compatible with the
SPO manufacturing processes [32].
However, one of the problems of iridium is its relatively poor performance at low energies
(below 5 keV) because its absorption lines lay exactly in that regime. In order to solve this
issue, an extra overcoating of a low­Z material has been suggested [33].
The options considered for the overcoating material are: Silicon Carbide (SiC), Boron
Carbide (B4C) and Diamond­Like Carbon (DLC) [20]. They are currently being investi­
gated in parallel [9, 34–36]. In order to be accepted as overcoating materials, they shall
not reduce the performance on angular resolution (PSF) of the mirrors and be compatible
with the SPO manufacturing process.
The whole manufacturing process of Athena mirrors consists on:
1. Plasma cleaning of the silicon substrate surface to remove possible contamination
and ensure surface smoothness.
2. Iridium coating plus the overcoating material by DC magnetron sputtering [37].
3. Wet chemistry on the plate, consisting of two phases: (a) photoresist stripes are
lifted off with dimethylsulfoxide (DMSO) to allow bonding on the surface [38]; and (b)
a standard cleaning procedure (SC­1) for surface cleaning and activation of bonding
areas prior to stacking [32].
4. Automatic stacking of SPO plates.
5. Annealing procedure to improve silicon bond strength at 200ºC for 50 hours [39,40]
and reduce stress on the iridium layer [41].

1.2
Thesis scope and limitations
The focus will be put on investigating one of the possible improvements that are being
studied for the effective area enhancement, particularly, the usage of DLC as an over­
coating material for the mirrors. One of the first steps is to understand the chemical
composition of carbon in the thin films deposited at Cosine BU, in the Netherlands. The
characterization of the chemical bonding of carbon in these thin films is key and, to do so,
two main experimental techniques will be explored: X­ray reflectometry (XRR) and X­ray
photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS). With the latter supported by more literature in terms
of characterizing the chemical properties of carbon. One of the milestones of the project
will be to study how well DLC­ness can be characterized by XRR and therefore, material
density.
XRR is a non­destructive powerful technique used to characterize thin films; due to the
surface sensitivity of x­rays at very small grazing angles, information about thickness,
roughness and density of the reflective layers can be obtained by quantifying the reflected
x­rays. On the other hand, XPS main working principle lays behind the use of the photo­
electric effect to measure the binding energy of electrons in the atom or molecular orbitals;
this allows to study the elemental composition and chemical bonds of the material. How­
ever, surface sensitivity to x­rays challenges the study of the evolution of layers inside the
mirror, etching is hence required and, consequently, samples get destroyed.
XRR data will be taken at DTU Space facilities and at a synchrotron facility in Germany,
BESSY, while XPS measurements will be performed at DTU Nanolab. The analysis will
be carried out using Multifitting and CasaXPS, softwares for XRR and XPS respectively.
Due to time limitations and unexpected events (malfunctioning of LEXR instrument), the
1.5 keV measurements of the samples couldn’t be obtained. However, data previously
obtained of the same samples is used and analyzed.

Absorption processes
Together with the reflection and transmission from a surface, different kind of absorption
processes might occur. Some of these effects are observed in the Universe, while others
are more important in order to characterize and understand thin films properties.
When a photon impinges on an atom, part of the energy is used to excite the atom to
a certain level while the rest is transferred to the electron that gets thus emitted to the
continuum. This is called photoelectric effect and leaves the atom with a vacant position
in its electron shells, ionized and therefore with a local positive charge.
Figure 2.6: Sketch of absorption processes: a) photoelectric effecct, b.1) fluores­
cence of Kα line, b.2) fluorescence of Kβ line and c) Auger electrons.
When the vacant position is filled by electrons of some outer shell, the global energy of
the atom decreases because the inner shells are in a less energetic level. This causes
the atom to emit the extra energy in the form of a photon, the process takes the name of
fluorescence and usually the photons emitted lay in the x­ray regime. Since the energy

each atom has a particular signature when fluorescing and thus can be identified by its

when a electron, respectively from a L or M shell, fills a vacancy in the K­shell.

Diamond­like carbon (DLC)
Carbon is one of the most common elements in nature, and it is involved in a wide variety of
fields: from its contribution to fuse at high temperatures in the core of stars to be one of the
reasons why life arouse in this particular part of the Universe. Its electronic configuration
is the reason of its versatility and ability to bond in different ways with other atoms and it
is very important in material science.
C6 −→1s2 2s2 2p2
At first sight, its configuration would tell that will form bonds with its 2 valence electrons.
However, a diversity of carbon crystals and disordered structures are found in nature. The
hybridisation of the second level orbitals allows for this situation.

2.2.1
DLC properties
Diamond and graphite have typically ordered structures. Diamond σ­bonds make it the
natural element with highest hardness and thermal conductivity at room temperature. On
the other hand, graphite has strong intra­layer bonding thanks to the sp2­orbitals, but weak
Van der Waals bonding between the layers. DLC is known to have certain similarity to di­
amond properties the more the content of sp3­bonds: it has a high mechanical hardness,
chemical inertness and wide band gap [10]. Thanks to this, DLC has been considered for
several fields ­for example the automobile industry with satisfactory results­ [51].
DLC is a stable form of amorphous carbon (a­C) with a disordered structure, in contrast to
diamond and graphite. When coated for thin films, the structure of carbon is a mixture of
sp3 and sp2 bonds. The pyramid in Figure 2.9 shows different carbon materials and their

DLC deposition on thin films
It has been found that DLC can be grown in many methods, some of them more suitable
for material science research studies and some others to industrial manufacturing [53].
The physical process that makes the sp3 sites grow is known to be subplantation and it is
achieved thanks to the bombarding of the film with carbon ions C+ with a energy of about
100 eV [10].
The subplantation mechanism consists of three stages [10]:
1. Collisional stage of around 10−13 s in which a carbon ion penetrates the film, either
by a direct or a knock­on process. Sketched in Figure 2.10.
2. Thermalization stage of 10−12 s.
3. Relaxation stage of 10−10 s. Sketched in Figure 2.10.
High energies drive the ions to penetrate the film, while insufficient energy will cause them
to remain on the surface and form less energetic sp2­bonds. Once an ion penetrates,
during the first stage, the local density of the carbon bonding site increases; it is assumed
that hybridisation will adjust regarding to the local density of the sites, by forming sp3­
orbitals in higher density regions and sp2­orbitals in lower ones, as a way to reduce the
excess of energy more efficiently [10].
Even if the energy is high enough to penetrate the film, only a small fraction of it is spent
on the penetration, the rest is spent on atom displacements (around 30%) [54] and as
heat (by phonons emission) in the thermalization stage [10]. If the energy is still high, a
relaxation process follows the thermalization, resulting in atom displacements, with the
consequence of density decrease and preferential sp2­sites. Energies lower than 100
eV will not allow to penetrate sufficiently the surface whilst higher energies will result in
significant relaxation stages. Therefore, 100 eV is thought to be optimal for carbon ions
when growing DLC films [10].

Thin Film Characterization
The characterization of a thin film gives insight about the intrinsic properties of the film,
which are needed to understand how the film will perform under certain conditions and
over time.
There are several techniques to characterize thin films and all the methods available would
occupy a large amount of space in the report. Therefore, only the methods that have been

3.1
X­Ray Reflectometry (XRR)
XRR is a non­destructive technique that measures the reflectance of high­energy pho­
tons on a thin film based on reflection theory, Section 2.1. It provides different information
about the surface and the structure of the film depending on the set­up of the experiment.
The x­ray source is always fixed and pointing towards the sample; the position of the sam­
ple and the detector indicate the type of measurement. Different types of measurements
are possible, but the most typical one is a θ­2θ specular measurement: the sample is
moved together with the reflector in a ratio 1:2, a sketch is shown in Figure 3.1. However,
other measurements are also possible and can provide information of different features.
A specular θ −2θ measurement gives directly the values of reflectivity and indirectly thick­
ness, roughness and density of the material can be estimated.
Figure 3.1: Simple sketch of a θ −2θ XRR measurement set up.
The x­ray source is usually required to be monochromatic, and it defines the energy at
which the measurement is taken. Typically, the instrument uses a certain element that,
under excitation, radiates a continuum Bremsstrahlung spectrum plus its characteristic
emission lines due to the decay of excited ions. This results in the emission of x­rays
with different energies that must undergo through some steps in order to obtain a uniform
beam. These steps normally consist of the passage through a monochromator plus a
set of slits and mirrors, which filter scattered photons and shape a beam with a pure
spectrum.
Another measurement option is an energy­scan. For this scan, the angle is fixed and
photons are created by sychrotron radiation in a beamline facility. This allows to study the
performance of the film in a energy interval. The facility at which measurements analyzed
in this project were taken is explained more in depth in Section 4.3

X­Ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy (XPS)
XPS is a powerful technique to find the state of chemical bonds of the sample. The
chemical bonds are dictated by the electron binding energy, measured by the instrument.
The sample is irradiated with a beam of x­rays at a certain incidence angle θp, typically of
a size between 100­400 µm, which has to be monochromatic. Aluminium or Magnesium
are usually used as x­ray sources because their Kα emission lines are at 1486.6 eV and
1253.6 eV respectively [12].

experiment. Image from [11].
The interaction of x­rays with the atoms in the sample via the photoelectric effect results in
the emission of electrons from the surface with a kinetic energy Ek. By finding this energy
and subtracting it from the photon energy, the binding energy BE can be obtained
BE = hν −Ek −ϕspec
(3.1)
Where hν is the energy of the photon and ϕspec is the spectrometer work function con­
stant, intrinsic to each instrument.
Since all elements have a particular electronic configuration, the set of binding energies
of the electrons to the atom is unique and can be used to find the elements present in a
sample. The binding energies are measured from the Fermi level and each of the shell
electrons has a unique line. The particular spectrum of iridium is shown in Figure 3.3.
The high probability of interaction between x­rays and the atoms makes it necessary to

Quantitative in the sense that there is a deductive approach behind and thus, a clear

• Background: the choice must be done depending on the nature of the data.

• Components: elements can have different bonds resulting in different chemical bind­

Background
When an electron is ejected, if the collision of the photon with the atom takes place inside
the material, it can suffer from inelastic scattering due to interaction with other particles.
This results in a loss of energy and hence, a smaller kinetic energy detected or higher
binding energy estimated.
The concept of mean free path of the material is key to understand the contribution of
these electron to the background signal. The smaller the mean free path, the more the
electrons will be scattered. in Figure 3.3 it can be seen the systematic increase on back­
ground after the 4f and 4d peaks. The rest of the peaks also produce an increase of
background but it is less notorious since its intensity is lower.
To account for this feature in the analysis, a subtraction of background must be performed.
However, it is worth mentioning that a background is just an estimation of the noise and
will never represent 100% properly the data.
The simplest background choice is a linear one. However, it is only convenient for wide­
band gap materials, since in these cases the electron losses occur far away from the
region of interest and the background intensities on the high and low part of the peak
spectrum are very similar [65].
Another option is a Tougaard background type, based on the quantitative description of
the physical process behind the electron energy loss and not dependent on the initial and
final point [66]. However, in order to use this type in an accurate way, it is necessary to
extract instrumental contributions from the measurement, a situation rarely possible [67].
The last considered choice is a Shirley background type, which uses the information of
the spectrum to construct the background sensitive to changes in the data [68].

4.1
LEXR reflectometer
The Low Energy X­ray Reflectometer was designed and built by JJ X­ray and DTU Space.
It uses aluminium (Al) as x­ray source with its line emission at 1.487 keV.
Because of the relatively low energy of the x­rays produced, it needs to be operated
under ultra high vacuum ­otherwise some photons would be scattered by air molecules.
In order to do that, it operates in a vacuum chamber at a pressure equal or lower than
10−5 mbar [9].
Figure 4.1: Sketch of LEXR reflectometer. Image from [9].
The set of mirrors and slits is composed by: a first pair of mirrors KB1 and KB2 which
collimate the photons emitted by the source; then, the beam goes through two slits S1
and S2 reducing the background level before reaching a multilayer monochromator MC,
made of tungsten and silicon (Wi/Si), that cleans the signal of the thermal Bremsstrahlung
and Kβ emissions. Finally, an extra slit S3 is used to shape the beam to a spot size of
50×150 µm2 before impinging on the surface in stage TH (acronym in the sketch of Figure
4.1). A CCD detector, TTH in Figure 4.1, is placed in the end with the detector slit S4 that
filters out undesired scattering reflected from the sample [9].

BESSY II beamline
The instrument chosen to take energy scans is the Four Crystal Monochromator (FCM)
beamline of the Physikalisch­Technische Bundesanstalt (PTB) laboratory at the BESSY II
synchrotron facility. It can provide a energy range between [1.75 ­ 10] keV. Because of the
wide range of energies, it is necessary to operate the beamline under vacuum conditions.
A sketch of the beamline in shown in Figure 4.4.
Figure 4.4: FCM beamline sketched with the different mirrors and slits focusing the
x­ray beam. Image from [15].
The working principle is quite similar to that of the reflectometers discussed before: a set
of slits and mirrors are used to focus and shape the beam that will impinge on the sample
surface.
In this case, the FCM is the one in charge of reflecting the desired energy thanks to a
pair of Si(111) and a pair of InSb(111) mirrors, while the toroidal and plane mirrors are in
charge of focusing the beam, respectively horizontally and vertically [73].
The flux of the beam is not constant and the variation in intensity has to be considered,
that’s why ten beam intensity measurements are taken before and after the sample mea­
surement.
The statistical error is assumed to be of about 1% when performing energy scans in the
range [4­10] keV [74].

XPS Nexsa
The instrument used for the XPS measurements is a Thermofisher K­alpha Nexsa XPS
[16]. It operates under ultra­high vacuum and has the option of performing other mea­
surements: ISS (Ion Scattering Spectroscopy), REELS (Reflection Electron Energy Loss
Spectroscopy), Raman, UV Raman and UPS (Ultra­violet Photoelectron Spectroscopy).
A picture of the instrument is shown in Figure 4.5.
Figure 4.5: Image of Thermofisher Nexsa instrument [16]
The XPS mode uses as Al as x­ray source at 1.487 keV, with a variable beam spot size
between 200­400 µm. For electrically insulating samples, a flood gun is available to com­
pensate the charge during the analysis, what might cause a shift in the peaks of the
spectrum in case it is not used.
Depth profile
In order to obtain chemical information along the depth of the thin film and because of the
limited reach of the XPS technique, an argon (Ar+) ion gun MAGCIS (Monoatomic and
Gas Cluster Ion Source) is used to remove material from the top of the sample.
It is available with two options: monoatomic bombardment and cluster bombardment. The
cluster mode is used mainly for polymers and organic materials [16]. To perform etching
on Ir/C films, the monoatomic source is selected.
The ion gun can be adjusted to perform a smooth transition through the film by adjusting:
• Energy applied: between 500 eV ­ 4000 eV per atom.
• Etching time: the duration of the etching process. A higher time would obviously
mean a deeper step between levels.
• Current: two options available, the low one is designed to be three times lower than
the high one.
By iterating XPS scans in a loop with the etching process, a depth profile of the sample

Data Analysis
In this chapter the design considerations for the Athena mirrors are addressed. A first
look at the aimed models and effects due to variation of parameters will be followed by
the analysis of the acquired data.
The analysis of data has been performed using two different softwares, Multifitting and
CasaXPS for XRR and XPS measurements respectively.
• Multifitting: software aimed to model and fit optical properties of thin films. Consid­
ered by the author, Svechnikov, as the successor to IMD, developed by Windt [76],
allows to consider several reflectivity curves at the same time and offers a more
user­friendly graphic interface [77].

5.1
Multifitting vs. IMD
First of all, it is worth addressing the topic about differences between Multifitting and IMD.
IMD has been the common tool in research groups studying thin films since its devel­
opment in 1998 [76]; Multifitting instead, is a newborn software, launched only in 2020.
This age gap is the reason behind the huge difference in terms of literature support: as
of today, they count respectively 1117 and 13 citations (among them [78–80]) on their
descriptive papers [76,77].
There is a problem when using these kind of softwares: there is no way to access their
metadata and check the equations embedded in it, hence they work as black boxes. The
main components of calculation and fitting are discussed herein.
Regarding the calculation of reflectivity curves, both seem to use the same optical con­
stants data base, and thus, the material refractive index should be computed identically.
The optical constants used for the analysis are the ones of the Center for X­ray Reflective
Optics (CXRO) and Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory (LLNL).
The next checkpoint is the formalism used to calculate the curves. Both softwares are
built using the Parrat formalism to calculate the reflectivity coefficient of layer j. However,
Svechnikov doesn’t mention the use of Nevot & Croce modification to account for the
roughness and interdiffusion in his paper [77]. The latter might cause a small deviation
on the calculated results; however, due to time limitations of the thesis, it was not possible
to investigate deeply this issue.
Another factor that affects the performance of curve­fitting is the algorithm used to solve
the inverse NP­problem of finding the structure of a thin film from its reflectivity curve.
Both softwares offer several optimization algorithms. One of the most spread and used is

5.3.2
X­ray photoelectron spectroscopy analysis
Two sets of data were obtained and a composition study plus a quantitative analysis were
performed through a depth profile. The composition study consists of finding the elements
present inside the thin film and how they are bonded. The second part of the analysis
addresses the chemical bonds of carbon quantitatively with the goal of estimating the
sp3­content of the sample.
Both samples show very alike results, and unless it is specified, the considerations made
are the same for both.
The background used for the analysis is a Shirley type, supported by several literature [61,
64,68]. When the signal­to­noise is very small in regions where a peak is not present it is
decided to use a linear background type for the simplification of the model and quantitative
consistency.

on the carbon profile as well. The latter can be visually observed in Figure 5.20, on the

Carbon study
The study consists on a depth­profile analysis of the carbon signature. Carbon can bond
in two different ways: sp2­bonds (graphite­like) or sp3­bonds (diamond­like) with also dif­
ferent BE, pictured in Figure 3.4.
For the first layer, before any etching is performed (etch level 0), the presence of oxygen
justifies the addition of a component accounting for the bond of carbon with oxygen, C­O.
The line shapes used for the analysis on this level are simple Voigt­like functions GL(x) in
a region of BE between 280 eV ­ 292 eV.
Then, after 10 seconds of etching (level 1) the signal of oxygen disappears and no longer
is justified the use of an extra curve. From now on, the curve­fitting process is performed
using only two curves and the BE region is reduced to avoid the interference of Ir4d peak
signal. As Haerle et al. suggested previously [62], two curves are sufficient to explain the
chemical bonds of carbon, each of them accounting for a type of bond.
However, two symmetric lines are not sufficient to account for the physical processes
occurring. Some literature suggests the use of a component that accounts for the π­π∗
satellite peak of C­sp2 [9,60,71]. Nevertheless, this peak is usually located around [290­

292] eV [13], which lays outside of the region of analysis selected and thus cannot account
for the tail asymmetry of the C1s signal observed.
Therefore, it is decided to use an asymmetric line shape for the C­sp2 component based
on [13].

modified by the rising component, thus, contributing to the signal in that region and mak­

Thesis Conclusions and Summary
In this final chapter, a summary of the project steps and the conclusion of the data analysis
are presented.
The goal of this thesis was to understand and characterize diamond­like carbon thin films,
an overcoating candidate material for the SPO mirrors for the Athena mission.
In order to fulfill this goal, XRR measurements were taken and a XRR analysis was carried
out for four sets of data: two angle scans one at 1.487 keV and 8.048 keV; and two energy
scans at 0.6◦, one performed on October and another one on December of 2021.
At the same time, XPS measurements were taken and analyzed to assess the chemical
properties of SPO thin films.

6.3
Further work
To confirm the results obtained regarding the sp3­content, studies using other techniques
like AES (Auger Electron Specetroscopy), Raman spectroscopy and/or EELS (Electron
Energy Loss Spectroscopy) are suggested.
A further investigation on the deposition mechanism would be needed if it is meant to
achieve a higher DLC­ness in the films. It could be done by studying the parameters that
affect the coating conditions, and therefore, film quality and sp3­content.
If DLC films are selected as the overcoating candidate, a separate study should be carried
out to find out the stability of the carbonaceous structure due to the post coating processes:
wet chemistry and annealing.
The discrepancies observed at energies above 7 keV in the energy scans taken with two
months of difference should be followed up to discard an evolution with time of the film
that could affect the performance.
Finally, the formation of a IrSixOy layer has to be confirmed and further analyzed. How
does this affect the analysis performed and an unlikely evolution in time could be lines of
investigation.
