Abstract
The purpose of this Project is an extended research and performance anal-
ysis as well, of the wireless Communication Protocol LoRa. LoRa Technology
offers compelling features for IoT applications including long range, low power
consumption and secure data transmissions.
This technology, can easily be
plugged in existing infrastructure and enables low-cost, battery-operated IoT
applications in many ways, in almost every aspect of our lives.
To that extent, the Project focuses on the Interference problem that is in-
herent nowadays in this powerful technology, depending on different reasons. A
characteristic example could be a big number of devices concurrently sending
and receiving transmission data, within the same LoRa network. Furthermore,
the steps that have been followed in order to study and to possibly overcome
the Interference phenomenon, defined after using different simulation based sce-
narios which eventually examined in this Thesis by applying different Methods
for all EXPs. As far the latter are concerned, six(6) different experiments, each
with different transmission settings(SF, FREQ, BW, CR) studied and as far
as the former are, three(3) in total Spreading Factor Hopping Methods imple-
mented(i.e Section 3.4) and five(5) including the default LoRasim settings and
the Capture Effect technique, applied for each EXP(i.e Table 4.2). Moreover,
the Methods designed and used in the EXPs proved that the initially assump-
tions made(i.e Research Objectives, Section 4.1) end up being true, with the
results presented later in this report describing how and why this statement
applies. Finally, all settings(i.e Table 4.1) applied and every step taken in order
to study and overcome the Collision/Interference Problem LoRa faces today, is
explained in detail and precise, in this Master Thesis report.
** All EXPs tested in LoRaSim(i.e Section 3.1) and all SF-HOP Methods im-
plemented in Python 2.7(i.e Section 3.4, Appendix A2)

- Preface / Overview -
The Master Thesis presented in this paper, accounts for 35 ECTS and has been con-
ducted for the Applied Mathematics And Computer Science Department ,under the
supervision of the Assistant Professor Mr.Xenofon Fafoutis, for the time period be-
tween February and August, in 2019.
The Project focuses on the Performance Analysis and Enhancements of the LoRa
protocol for IoT Embedded Systems. More precisely, the motivation wheel behind
the ideas implemented and also presented later in this paper, is one of the most
crucial problems regarding this technology and digital communication in general. In
other words, this is the problem of the Interference. A more detailed explanation
and presentation of the problem that mentioned above, the solutions proposed and
the results extracted based on the research and work done, is made in the Chap-
ters following next in this Project. Different research studies examined throughout
this Thesis regarding the problem of Interference, but unluckily our findings was not
enough due to literature limitations on the Spreading Factor Hopping domain. This
is easily explained by the fact that it is a new technology in the IoT domain, thus it
is not yet fully been tested into many study case scenarios. Even though this was a
drawback for this research, still in Chapter 2 a discussion on relative studies made
similar to the one made in this Project, are presented. All the research results found
have been extracted, plotted and finally illustrated in this report work. Furthermore,
for the EXPs to be tested and the goals to be achieved, all work done with the usage
of the LoRasim simulator offered by the University Of Lancaster[1].
To conclude, as far as the Report’s structure(i.e
Section 1.6) is concerned, it has
been divided into six(6) in total Chapters. In the first Chapter an Introduction to
the Internet Of things(IoT) domain is made and after that an extended analysis on
the WAN Technologies along with the one this Thesis focuses(LoRa), are presented.
Moreover, the problem of Interference in LoRa and the solutions proposed in order
to overcome this phenomenon are also explained. In the Chapters following after
the Introduction part(i.e Prior Work, Approach, Experiments, Results, Conclusions),
initially a presentation of different cases regarding LoRa Technology shows some of
the limitations, problems and solutions may hide behind this upcoming technology.
Furthermore, in Chapter 3 is explained the proposed solution against the Interference
problem LoRa faces and in the next three(3) Chapters the Methodology we used to
succeed that, along with a detailed discussion and evaluation on the results taken
after those Methods applied on the EXPs, are presented too.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
IoT - Embedded System Devices
It is 2019 and even though many things that used to seem or sound in the past more
like science fiction movie scenarios, still technology’s continuous upward trend tend to
prove all assumptions made in the past, end up being correct. Due to the fast devel-
opments made in the latest years, almost in every aspect of the Technology domain,
our lives became even more secure and self-controlled. Moreover, the possibility for
every human being to stay in touch and to communicate in real time with anyone
around the globe, at anytime, opened a new door for a new era. This new age could
not been neither mentioned, nor analyzed, without discussing about the Internet Of
Things domain(IoT).
The idea behind the home automation initiated years ago, around the 1970s and
continued in 1988 by Mark Weiser who is considered to be the father of Ubiquitous
Computing, but the idea took a longer period of time until it turned to become a
realistic one. We have already entered a new world of smart collaborative systems
called the Internet of Things (IoT). ”Things” are intelligent because they incorporate
small, cheap and lightweight processors. They are collaborative thanks to wireless
communications, which enable the creation of spontaneous networks. Almost every
device now is enhanced with a memory and offers to the users the possibility to
remember specific events, to adopt context sensitive behaviors, to be are aware of a
specific location or situation and to communicate with other smart objects within an
environment. In the subsection coming next, different cases of IoT implementations
that they exist and operate today, shows how fast this domain is rapidly grows, both
technologically and demand speaking.

This subsection presents as an addition to the formerly mentioned, five(5) fields where

• IoT in Smart-Home Environments
For almost over a decade and especially nowadays, the number of houses which
are been equipped with smart embedded devices and gadgets connected to each
other, has become higher than ever before. Smart devices now have the ability
to observe and learn from the inhabitant’s behavior and to provide services more
easier. Every micro-controller and gadget included within a Smart Home Sys-
tem and not only, can be programmed specifically for the user’s needs, thereafter
convenience and customization are considered to be two of the most important
benefits coming with this technology. In a few words someone can say that, a
Smart Environment is a computationally enhanced environment with the ap-
propriateness to act autonomously and to support for the inhabitants, more
comforts than only just shelter from aggressive environmental conditions like
bad weather or cold. Also, Swedish research firm Berg Insight states that 63
million American homes will qualify as “Smart” by 2022, with everything from
Internet-connected light bulbs and attached real-time sensors, to cameras (i.e
there were nearly 130 million homes in the U.S. in total in 2018). This number
is quite high, considering the fact that the estimation was made just for the
United States.

To begin with, it is a natural process for humans to think bigger and more op-

tend to become bigger , consequently the need for more power(i.e energy) is an
inevitable aftereffect. IoT technology can offer an immense capability to reduce
the EC. Furthermore, for example in the cases where wasted energy lighting and
heating/cooling rooms that people are not currently using, can conclude to such
a result. To this extent, by focusing on zoned lighting/heating/cooling Smart
implementations, cities in the future could drastically decrease their demand
on the energy grid. To conclude, a study[2] made by Léan Doody, Nicola Walt,
Ina Dimirev and Anders Nørskov in 2016 and regarding the Smart Cities in
Denmark, stated that the smart cities concept provides an enticing opportunity
to national governments. The global market for smart cities solutions is large

1.1.2
Embedded System Devices
Smart environments refer to the combination and collaboration between both digital
and physical factors. By digital is meant every device existing and operating in the
environment(i.e network) and by physical, the living beings interacting with those
devices in the same area. Due to their functionality, those devices are knows as Em-
bedded System Devices(ESD).
An ESD is a highly specialized device used for specific purposes and can be included
within another object, or as a part of a larger system. Usually the device is a part of a
system that serves a ”greater purpose”, for example a heart rate monitor embedded in
a smartwatch, that can be connected to a smart phone to display the heart’s status in
real time, or an accelerometer embedded in shoes to monitor speed, distance traveled
and calories burned. Furthermore, the most commonly used POS and ATM machines,
are also examples of ESDs or Embedded Systems(ES). In more simple words an ESD
is an electronic system that is designed to perform a dedicated function within a
larger system and can be classified into four(4) different categories, based on their
functional requirements and performance. These types are the, Real Time ES (rtES),
Mobile ES (mbES), Stand-Alone ES (saES) and finally the Networked ES (nwES).
In the next paragraph, a small briefing about those types of ESDs along with their
most important characteristics, is presented.
Firstly, saES is a system which can operate on it’s own. By this meaning, no need
for a host system operating with an OS is needed. Next, rtES can be divided into
two(2) subcategories, the first one to be the Hard-Real Time System and the second
one to be the Soft-Real Time System.
No furthermore analysis will be made on
those subcategories, except the fact that the former one interacts with the hardware,
instead of interacting with physical factors and in both cases violating time scheduling,
could prove to be devastating as far as the ESD characteristics following in the next
paragraph are concerned. Moreover as an mbES, different examples could be given in
order to determine the exact type of the system. As a mbES is meant any portable
device used nowadays, such as mobiles and digital cameras.
Furthermore, two(2)
parameters could be mentioned as drawback to this type of ESD, with the first being
the memory and secondly the power limitations.
Last but not least, a nwES is
the system which can be connected to the internet, either via cable(i.e wired) or
air(wireless) and functions within different types of network interfaces such as, Local
Area Networks (LAN) and the Internet or Wide Area Networks (WAN). An extended
analysis on the Wide Area Networks (WAN) and more specifically on the Low Power
- Wide Area Protocols, is also presented later in the report(i.e Section 1.2).
1.1.3
ESD Characteristics
Initially a quick presentation of the characteristics that makes an ESD stable effi-
cient and user-friendly, highlights the most important of them and afterwards a more
==PAGE:17==

detailed explanation for each one shown here, is given too:
1. High Speed
2. Low Power Consumption
3. Smallest Possible Size/Weight
4. Highest Possible Accuracy
5. High Adaption/Accessibility
6. High Reliability
Based on common sense, if a device’s speed is high enough, then this device is
considered as a good one, but what does this really means? ESDs communicate with
one or more devices within a network, by sending and receiving transmissions. Those
transmissions include digital packets and in order for the communication to be as
much successful as it could be, packets have to be received or to be transmitted from
the device, with the best possible speed and accuracy they can. Secondly, energy is
something that everyone is concerned about, hence transmissions on one hand can
be fast, but on the other hand the fastest a transmission gets, the more energy the
device consumes.
Moreover, it is quite positive for a device to send and receive messages in the
best possible speed. In this case the power that the device has to consume in order
to transmit and to receive is high, therefore this makes the device not that much
appropriate, operationally and efficiently speaking. Nevertheless, every ESD’s func-
tionality depends on the purpose it serves, so its settings can be defined accordingly
to the operation needed and specified for its usage. Size and weight are also two(2)
more factors that should be taken in concern, given the fact that no one for example
could carry a wearable device censoring his own personal medical data, if the size and
weight are not convenient at all.
Finally, accuracy along with Low Power consumption, is also two(2) more of the
most important characteristics an ESD should support in the best possible way. It has
no meaning speaking about the characteristics mentioned already, if the device does
not meet Accuracy. Messages should be sent and to be received, not only in the fastest
way and by using the minimum energy the should, but also in the most accurate way
the could. Lets consider an example where a patient is medically observed within
such an environment. In this case if the data which transmitted and received from
the user’s wearable device are not accurate enough, it could be proven to even be fatal

1.3.1
Introduction To LoRa
LoRa is a CP based on the Chip Spread Spectrum(CSS) modulation technique, which
created by Semtech[6]. As its name implies, it stands for long range communication
and data exchange between nodes(i.e ESDs), operating within the network, as its
topology is based just like Zigbee on the star-topology in which ESDs transmit data
to a gateway(GW), otherwise also known as the basestation(BS). More precisely, a
GW is a powerful device which is capable of receiving and decoding multiple trans-
missions(i.e a GW listens to all SFs) and the devices operating in it can be classified
into three(3) categories, based on their functionalities. These categories[7] are the:
Class A, Class B and finally Class C. The main difference between those three device
classes is that in the first two, nodes can save power by transmitting when necessary,
in contrast to Class C where ESDs should stay in ”on-state” all the time, in order to
receive and to decode any transmission signals. More precisely, in the first Class, a
device transmits to the GW when needed and in the second Class nodes can trans-
mit data just like in the first one, except they support scheduled receive ”time-slots”.
Devices that belong to the third Class, have to continuously ”listen” to the GW in

Frequency Hopping
Speaking about narrowband systems, in order for collisions to be successfully avoided,
frequency hopping seems to be one of the solutions. This is succeeded by forcing the
nodes participating in the transmission, to regularly ”jump” from one frequency to
another, so repeated operations in the same frequency or channel are minimized.
However, the random selection of frequencies a node can jump to, can still lead to
transmission data loss. This can happen from either if the transmitter or the receiver,
hops to a frequency or a channel another device is using already. Another study[12]
made on 2017, examined the application of LoRa into different scenarios and con-
cluded to that, there is no standard way to efficiently use LoRa in a specific way for
all use case scenarios. The only way to do so, is that for each use case the SF, the
number of ESDs and the number of the channels, should all must be taken in concern
in order to determine if a LoRa fits each scenario, or not(i.e Similar Conclusions).
Moreover, if all ESDs are transmitting data at the maximum R, then the number
of packets to be successfully get received by the GW, decreases. To this extent, the
proportion of the packets received drops, because of the collisions that would possibly
occur in the process.

• Frequency Division Multiple Access - FDMA
FDMA is one of the most commonly used technology, regarding radio trans-
missions, today. By using it the FREQ band is divided into channels of equal
BW, thereafter every device can use different FREQs to transmit its data within
the networks coverage area. To this extent, this technique separates the trans-
mission channels used for the transmission based on the different frequencies
defined for each, so for example if five(5) users want to use five(5) channels for
their communication, they will have five(5) frequencies too. As an outcome by
using this FREQ division Method some of the advantages can be are, FREQ allo-
cation and band separation, simplicity to its implementation with respect to the
existing hardware and finally efficiency when constant highly traffic is observed
and required to be successfully managed by the system. On the other hand,
by using specified allocated FREQs permanently means that those FREQs will
be occupied continuously, thereafter spectrum will be wasted when a BS is not
transmitting neither receiving signals. Moreover, another drawback can be that,
even though it is simple to implement, yet it is expensive to do so.
• Time Division Multiple Access - TDMA
Next, another technology used for the same reasons(i.e Interference minimiza-
tion/elimination), is the well known today TDMA. It is more complex than
the one presented earlier(FDMA), due to the reason that this one requires high
accuracy between a node and a BS. As its name implies, this technology is
using time-slots in order to avoid collisions during the communication process.
In simple words, it offers the ability for a channel to be occupied by two users
at the same time by creating an ”illusion on time process”. Moreover, an ex-
ample explaining this illusion is described next. Lets assume that a system is
using a two(2) time-slot TDMA technology.
Then two(2) users can use the
same FREQ in a way where initially the first user, gets to use the FREQ for a
limited amount of time(50msec). Afterwards, the channel reverts to the second
user who gets also 50msec. Then it cycles again back to the first user again
who gets a further 50 milliseconds, and so on and so forth. This process is so
fast regarding time, where both of the users involving in the communication,
even though they use the same FREQ they think that they have exclusivity on
the channel. In order for a third user to participate in the communication with
no interference issues, one more radio channel should be needed. As negatives
to this technology it could be that, devices operating at high bit rates increase
their energy consumption and complex signal processing is required to synchro-
nize within short slots.

3.3
Collision Examination
Collisions in LoRa can occur in many ways, depending on different factors. Factors
like these can be the SF, BW, FREQ, CR and of course the time a node sends packets
to a GW. In LoRa, packet collision can happen when two or more devices transmit
packets in the same frequency or spreading factor concurrently. In the first case, the
Collision is characterized as FREQ-Collision and in the other case as SF-Collision.
Except that, a collision can also occur when two or more transmissions received by the
BS, are in the same strength and time(i.e STRENGTH-Collision, TIME-Collision).
To this extent, as the TIME-Collision is concerned, studies shown that by using di-
rectional antennas it can significantly improve a LoRa network’s throughput.
Moreover, collisions can be classified into two(2) categories. These are, the Intra-
Collisions and the Inter-Collisions.
On one hand, IAC concerns the case where,
collisions can occur when two or more devices transmit in the same FREQ and SF
and on the other hand, IEC is regarded when devices transmit in the same FREQ but
different SF. As far as the former case is concerned, only the strongest message will
be decoded by the GW and only in the case where its power at the BS, is more than
the other node’s power minus 6db(i.e Tpx1>Tpx2-6db). Moreover, in the second case
for the message to be decoded by the BS it should its PRx minus the PRx’(i.e other
node’s received power), to be higher than the ”Signal to Interference Noise Radio” of
the first message. Moreover, case studies shown that IEC are indeed an issue in LoRa
networks and thus, by giving higher SFs to devices operate far from the gateway,
might not necessarily improve the link capacity[23].
As far as this Thesis is regarded, only the case where SF collisions can occur between

5.7.1
Energy Consumption
During a transmission, for all devices operating within the network in order to send
and receive data, energy is needed. If no collisions appear in the transmission process,
then no need for extra energy is needed. On the opposite case, if packet collisions
occur then the device involved in the transmit process, should resend the packets,
hence it needs more energy for such action.
Subsequently, in this subsection of the Evaluation part, the project turns its focus
on the Energy Consumption before and after applying all the Methods, in the EXPs
presented so far in this Thesis. Moreover, for the Energy Consumption tests exam-
ined in this subsection only the case where the AvgSleepTime is constantly increased
has taken in mind. Finally, all the results taken from the simulation are illustrated
below in Figure 5.13, as also a brief explanation on them follows just after the Sub-
plot. From observing the plots above, EC seems to drop for all the EXPs tested as
the AvgSleepTime increases. This is easily be explained from the fact that, while the
”sleep time” for the devices was set to be low(i.e 500msec), all nodes operating in the
same LoRa, due to the Interference problem, have to transmit their data more than
one times(i.e resent data), as a consequence they have to use more Energy.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusions
In this Project, the Interference phenomenon in LoRa protocol was examined. All
devices operating in a LoRa network can transmit on specific radio settings, such as
the Spreading Factor(SF), Bandwidth(BW), Frequency(FREQ) and Code Rate(CR).
To this extent, if a device operates(i.e transmit data) in a LoRa network, where also
another one or more devices are using the same SF, then the packets transmitted from
one device will collide with the ones sent from another node, hence the packets will be
considered as lost since the BS will never ”listen” to those signals(i.e packet-decoding).
In order to eliminate in the best possible way the collisions may occur between de-
vices(i.e nrNodes), that concurrently operate in the same network and to achieve
the highest possible Data Extraction Rate(i.e DER), three(3) SF-HOP algorithms
designed(i.e ROT-HOP, RAC-HOP, EWMA-HOP) and five(5) in total tested in Lo-
RaSim(i.e NO-HOP, CE, SF-HOP algorithms). As described and shown in the pre-
vious Chapters, the Thesis examined six(6) Experiments(EXPs), where each EXP
transmits on different defined radio settings.
Moreover, the findings showed that by dynamically ”jumping” from a SF to a new
one can indeed improve transmission’s DER, thereafter LoRa’s throughput. Further-
more, even though all SF-HOP Methods did improved the DER for both of the two
cases tested for every EXP(i.e Increasing nrNodes, AvgSleepTime), yet each SF-HOP
Method behaved differently for all the EXP examined(i.e all extracted data plotted
and described in Chapters 4,5). Last but not least, in the Evaluation part(i.e Chapter
5, Subsection 5.7.1) the Project focused on the Energy(EC) aspect of the Interfer-
ence phenomenon, showing that by avoiding collisions, also the total EC is decreased,
hence making the SF-HOP techniques presented in this Thesis to be convenient if in
a LoRa transmission, the EC has to be taken in mind.

Feedback To Research Objectives
• The Performance Analysis of the LoRa Protocol shows that the Interference
problem, does exist and can be disastrous for the CP’s efficiency and accuracy.
At the time being, although LoRa seems to gain popularity in the IoT domain,
the scenario where an extreme amount of devices concurrently transmitting in
one network and high packet loss occurs, seems to be a bit far from reality.
This assumption is based on the fact that, today most of the existing LoRa

contrast to the formerly said, this will not be the case in the near future, where
the nrNodes concurrently operating with a single BS in a LoRa network, will
be much more higher than today.
• The Interference phenomenon, has significantly negative impact on the LoRa

each LoRa’s deployment can be adopted for different needs, hence the Interfer-

applications, even though Interference can affect both, still it is more crucial
to be taken in concern in the former case than the latter.

6.2
Future Work - Possible Scenarios
The final section of the Thesis Project presents two(2) more ideas, which their imple-
mentation and adoption in future research works, could be helpful for studying the
Interference problem existing in LoRa.
1. Time Scheduled Spreading Factor Hopping
In such a scenario, all nodes operate in the network use time-slots. By time-
slot, is defined to be the period of time in which a node is free to use a specific
SF. In such manner, all devices transmit and receive by using a SF only for
the specified time they are obligated to and ”jump” to a new SF after this
period of time exceeds the allowed. Moreover, if the initial time is set to be
the same for all nodes this can result to collisions because all nodes will use
the same SF, for the same period of time. A solution to this issue can be the
delay between the time-slot definition, for each device operating(i.e by giving
transmission priority).
2. Area Based Spreading Factor Hopping
Another idea is by dynamically changing the SF, when the area covered by
the BS is both known and precise. To this extent, two(2) more future work
scenarios are presented and described next.
Figure (6.1)
Topology BS/Nodes(LoRaSim)
a) Distance Based SF-HOP
In a LoRa network a BS transmits and receives within a determined area(i.e
Dist=150m for this Thesis). By using this Method a device can ”hop” to
another one available, after checking its distance from the GW. Afterwards,
==PAGE:78==

if the difference found to be more than the allowed one(i.e a distance
user-defined threshold) the device is forced to ”jump” to a new SF. This
technique can be beneficial in the cases where the users/nodes constantly
moving within the network. Moreover, although the SF-HOP Method just
presented also implemented and tested by the author, it did not included
in the Thesis report due to the External Interference problem(i.e A LoRa
network can Interfere with another one operating closely), while in this
Project the Internal Interference phenomenon was only taken in mind and
examined.
b) Grouped SF-HOP
The final solution could be by dividing the BS’s coverage area into four(4)
”quadrants”, since the BS is placed centrally in the simulator(i.e Figure
6.1). Moreover, its functionality is similar to the SDMA(i.e Section 2.4)
with the difference that in this scenario for each quadrant, different SFs
can be defined for usage. As a result collisions will drop, since all different
groups of nodes will transmit and receive with the SFs proposed for them.
