Abstract
PAMAM dendrimers have many biopharmaceutical applications due to the fact that they
can self-assemble into vesicles called dendrimersomes, that can encapsulate molecules and be
uptaken by cells. Due to their unique properties, they can also be modified at their core or
their periphery to improve cellular uptake and pharmacokinetics. They can also be modified
with fluorescent compounds to discover valuable information like activity of a potential drug,
as well as improve their ability to self assemble.
In this paper, a dansyl-modified PAMAM dendrimer was successfully synthesized to gener-
ation 3 (G3). G1, G2, and G3 were then self assembled into dendrimersomes in EtOH and
Milli-Q water respectively using direct hydration. The dendrimres were successfully able to
self-assemble into dendrimersomes. A concentration-size dependence for all generations, and
a generation-size dependence for G1 and G2.

Introduction
1.1
Characteristics of Dendrimers
Figure 1: Illustration of the basic structure of a dendrimer.
Fig 1 depicts the baseic structure of a dendrimer. These highly branched, monodisperse
macromolecles consist of a core (grey circle), branched macromolecular layers (orange cir-
cles), and multivalent periphery (green circles) [1]. Each new branch added from the core of
the dendrimer is known as a generation. As the generation of the dendrimer increases, the
size and number of reactive sites also increases [2]. At higher generations, dendrimers can
form globular structures due to an increase in sterics [3]. Moreover, their high number of
terminal groups allow dendrimers to have high solubility and binding affinity [4].
Dendrimers have been an interesting topic in biomedical applications due to their unique
features. Not only do they have greater mechanical and chemical stability compared to other
artificial vesicles like liposomes, they also have the ability to encapsulate macromolecules and
target cells[5][4]. Another interesting feature of dendrimers are their multivalent periphery,
which allows for several functional groups to be added onto the structure, and can improve
pharmacokinetics as well as site specific delivery of therapeutics[4].
While their globular structure, high degree of branching, and precise molecular weight makes
them very appealing as nanocarriers in biomedical applications, dendrimers also have a few
disadvantages [6]. One important obstacle to overcome when developing pharmaceuticals
is cytotoxicity. Dendrimer cytotoxicity is dependent on the size of the dendrimer and the
charge of the surface groups. Positively charged, high generation dendrimers (>G3) tend to
exhibit more cytotoxicity than lower generations due to their large size and large number of
positively charged surface groups [7]. However, these obstacles can be overcome by modifying

1.2
PAMAM Dendrimers
Polyamidoamine (PAMAM) dendrimers are a type of dendrimer with branches composed
of repeating amino groups.
These dendrimers are amphiphilic, with the branches being
hydrophilic, and the core being hydrophobic. They are typically syntheisized by divergent
synthesis, which is a step-wise synthesis process that starts with synthesis of a core and then
successivey adding branches until the desired generation is reached [8] [9]. Synthesis of all
half generations involve a Michael Addition with methyl acrylate, while synthesis of all full
generations involve ester amidation using excess ethylenediamine (EDA) [10].
PAMAM dendrimers have been used in many biopharmaceutical applications.
1.3
Self Assembly of PAMAM Dendrimers into dendrimersomes
Due to their amphiphilic nature, PAMAM dendrimers have the ability to self assemble into
vesicles known as dendrimersomes, that can aggregate in aqueous solutions.
[1].
These
vesicles can mimic biological membranes such as liposomes, and be very useful as drug
carriers [11].
Dendrimersomes can be assembled by dissolving the dendrimer in a water
soluble solvent like ethanol (EtOH) or dimethyl sufoxide (DMSO) then injected using direct
injection or direct hydration. In direct injection, the dendrimer sample is injected into water
while in direct hydration, water is injected into the sample [12] [13]. The shape, size and
stability of dendrimersomes are dependent on the method in which they are assembled, as
well as the generation of the dendrimer, and can be quantified using dynamic light scattering
(DLS) or Transmission electron microscopy (TEM).
1.4
Fluorescently Labeled Dendrimers
Dendrimers can be modified with fluorescent groups either at their periphery or at the core.
Fluorescence imaging is a powerful tool and can provide valuable information on the activity
of a potential drug and other biological processes [14]. When a dendrimer is modified with
a fluorescent group, it is possible to be able to observe biodistribution, cellular uptake, and
retention [15]. However, fluorescently modifying denrimers can also aid in their self-assembly
into dendrimersomes.

1.4
Fluorescently Labeled Dendrimers
Figure 2: Structure of a dansyl-chloride
Fig 2 shows the structure of the dansyl-chloride used in the synthesis of this paper. Dansyl-
chloride is an aromatic chromophore that is typically used to label proteins and amino acids.
Dansyl-chloride easily reacts with amines to give UV-active fluorescent derivatives [16]. Be-
cause of its aromatic property, dansyl-chloride is able to interact via π interaction, more
specifically π stacking. The use of π stacking has shown to be an effective strategy in the
self-assembly of other nanoparticles[17]. Therefore, it is hypothesized π stacking interac-
tions between dansyl groups can further improve self-assembly of the PAMAM dendrimer
synthesized in this paper.

Aim of Project
The aim of this project is to synthesize low generation PAMAM dendrimers modified with
a dansyl group and investigate how it can self assemble into dendrimersomes. This will be
followed by studying how different parameters effect the size, shape, and dispersity of the
dendrimersomes.

Methods and Materials
3.1
Synthesis of Dansyl-modified PAMAM dendrimers
Detailed synthesis for all compounds synthesized in this paper can be found in the appendix.
1H-NMR and 13C-NMR spectra were recorded on a 500 MHz and 101 MHz Bruker NMR
apparatus respectively. High resolution MALDI was measured in positive mode on a Bruker
SolariX XR instrument.
3.2
Dendrimersome Assembly
G1, G2, and G3 dendrimers were dissolved in water EtOH and Milli-Q water respectively to
a final concentration of 10 mg/ml (1% w/v) to create a stock solution. Dendrimersomes for
each sample were assembeled by rapidly injecting Milli-Q water into the stock solution and
vortexing for 5 seconds to give a final concentration of 2 mg/ml, 3.5 mg/ml, and 5 mg/ml.
900 µl of MQ water was added to the sample and the sample was filtered using a 13 mm
syringe filter with 0.2 µm PTFE membrane.
DLS was performed on a Malvern Zetasizer Nano S fitted with a 4 mW 632.8 nm ’red’ laser.
Samples were meausred using a disposable plastic micro cuvette. Instrument parameters
and measurement times were determined automatically and all experiments were performed
in triplicates.
3.3
fluorescence (methods and materials)
The fluorescence spectra of dendrimersomes was obstained utilizing a Tecan plate reader.
Milli-Q water was employed as the solvent, and the dendrimersomes were diluted to a con-
centration of 2 mg/mL unless stated otherwise. Fluorescence emission spectra were metic-
ulously captured at 20°C, employing an excitation wavelength of 320 nm, with emission
spectra ranging from 400 nm to 700 nm.

conditions and reacted with diethyl ether (Et2O) to form an amine salt. Subsequent addi-

significant signal broadening can be observed with aggregation sizes between 10 and 100 nm

the dendrimer’s core in both 1H-NMR and 13C-NMR also decreases. As the dendrimer begins
to aggregate, the core becomes protected from outside media by its branches. therefore, this
observation could be due to self-aggregation. branches

Dynamic Light Scattering
Once it was established that the dendrimers synthesized could self-aggregate into dendrimer-
somes, different methods of dendrimersome assembly were done to find the optimal assembly
method, and those dendrimersomes were measured using DLS. The best assembly method
was found to be direct hydration using EtOH for G1 an G2, and Milli-Q water for G3, and
vortexing for 5 seconds. As stated before, as the dendrimer generation increases, so does the
number of terminal amines on the surface of the dendrimer. This also causes the dendrimer
to become more polar. Therefore, due to their being significantly more terminal amines on
G3 than G1 or G2, the solvent used for G3 was Milli-Q water as it is more polar than EtOH.

earlier, the size of the dendrimersome is dependent on the solvent in which it is assembled
therefore, it was not expected for G3 to follow the generation-size dependence in this paper
as it is in a different solvent.

Conclusion
In this paper, dansyl-modified PAMAM dendrimers were successfuly synthesized up to G3.
TEM was used to confirm that the dendrimers synthesized could self-assemble into den-
drimersomes. G1 and G2 dendrimers were assembled in EtOH using direct hydration, while
G3 was essembled using Milli-Q water due to the sample not being soluble in EtOH.
DLS data showed that at the initial concentration tested (2 mg/ml, 3.5 mg/ml, and 5 mg/ml
for all generations) that the dendrimersomes had large size distributions, and were not very
monodisperse. Therefore, it was decided to filter the samples using a 13 mm syringe filter
with 0.2 (µ)m PTFE membrane to filter out the larger sizes in the samples. After filtration,
the dendrimersomes had a smaller size distribution, as well as a lower PDI that was consid-
ered monodisperse. A concentration-size dependence for all samples, and a generation-size
dependence for G1 and G2 were also observed.
It has been concluded that the PAMAM dendrimers synthesized in this project can self-
assemble into dendrimersomes, and that the size of the dendrimersome depend on the co-
nentration and the generation of the sample.
