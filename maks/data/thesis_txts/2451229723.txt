Abstract 
This master thesis project aims to juxtapose theoretical perspectives with market trends and 
industry data that outline an organization’s long-term strategy. The analysis is conducted for 
an SME (Small Medium Enterprise) that faces various challenges such as lack of profitability 
and long-term strategy, Kokotas Klimatismos Ltd (referred as KKLTD), that is specialized in 
HVAC (Heating, ventilation, and Air Conditioning), through projects and retail commercial 
activities. Initially, the theoretical analysis and literature review aims to outline the trends on 
indoor air climate and what’s the future towards sustainability on the particular sector. 
Primarily, the strategic and dynamic capabilities of the existing SBUs (Strategic Business Units) 
of the SME are evaluated, taking into consideration the market trends. The theoretical analysis 
entails a case study that challenges the effectiveness of the TMF (Technology Management 
framework) in an SME. Hence, an amplified technology roadmapping method was applied to 
KKLTD, and it has been proved to be an optimal tool for the technological evolution of the 
firm. Data that enabled the realization of the technology roadmap was collected from 
literature sources and by interviewing experts to get the technology and commercial 
perspectives. As the strategic sweet spot of the company has been framed, and a strong 
theoretical and empirical background of the case has been built, the TRM (Technology 
Roadmap) has demonstrated to be the ideal tool for the firm.  Consequently, the expected 
outcome is to shape an integrated package of solutions for the future, strategic 
differentiation, considering the capabilities of the company, aiming to develop its competitive 
advantage. 
Key words: Positioning Strategy, Competitive Advantage, Strategic Differentiation, 
Technology Roadmapping, Quadric Framework, Climate Change, SME, HVAC

Prelude 
The Fifth Assessment Report of the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change concluded 
that warming since the mid-20th century by the vast majority has been due to greenhouse gas 
emissions, as a result of human activities, and more accurately due to the combustion of fossil 
fuels, husbandry and land usage changes (1). Therefore, on April 22, 2016, in New York City, 
the Paris Agreement was signed—a global agreement to combat climate change— the core 
elements of this intergovernmental initiative are objectives and immediate actions for 
minimizing GHG (greenhouse gas) (2). This all-embracing agreement for climate change action 
is to hold “the increase in the global average temperature to well below 2°C above pre-
industrial levels and to pursue efforts to limit the temperature increase to 1.5 °C above pre-
industrial levels” (3).  
Currently, our planet is facing the numerous detrimental effects of climate change; 
temperature increase affects ecosystems of flora and fauna, water resources, public health 
and human activity. Inevitably, according to the European Environmental Agency, the energy 
needs for heating has increased in northern Europe, but on the contrary, the demand for 
cooling in south Europe has increased. As the European Environmental Agency underlines, 
there is a necessity to minimize global CO2 emissions gradually, to circumvent the most 
harmful effects of climate change (4). Undoubtedly, greenhouse gas emissions, linked with 
global climate change, have pinpointed a significant concern for the world community, and in 
parallel created the need for energy efficient products and services. Therefore, this trend has 
developed a series of business opportunities in multiple sectors, that can be utilized by 
offering sustainable solutions.

2 Introduction 
Building structures, essential elements of the world’s living societies, are indeed consuming a 
significant amount of energy and materials, and thus, being instrumental in global climate 
change (5). Globally, according to the Worldwatch Institute, it is estimated that building 
development and operation account for 40% of the world’s total energy consumption, 30% of 
raw materials consumption, 16% of potable water usage, and lastly, responsible for 35% of 
the world’s global CO2 emissions (6). As global warming necessitates new climatic conditions 
for the construction, it is projected that climate change will noteworthy affect the design, 
development, and operation of buildings along with occupants and their activity (7). These 
factors will lead to a higher energy consumption of buildings, as more extreme weather 
conditions (overheating summer) will demand different outdoor air conditioning settings to 
satisfy the increased occupancy by humans. In fact, according to current consumption and 
climate data, it has been projected that the energy consumption of residential buildings will 
be increased in various major metropolitan areas in Europe. Cities such as Athens, Rome and 
Paris, will need more energy to balance heat/ cool loads; an analytical graph of projections 
can be observed on Appendix A. Due to future regional temperature increase, cooling needs 
are rapidly growing, being predominant for all southern European climates and becomes 
higher than the heating loads for most European cities in 2050 and onwards (8). 
Nonetheless, not only the construction sector affects global warming; also, climate change 
has effects on building development and design. Climate change is expected to increase the 
concentrations of pollutants in the indoor air environment, and this might affect human 
health and wellbeing. These shifts are expected due to variations in outdoor pollution, as well 
as building reactions to extreme climate conditions (9). Therefore, entrepreneurship globally, 
and specifically the companies operating in the construction sector, need to focus on 
providing solutions that comply with regional regulatory policies considering climate change, 
such as INDCs (Intended Nationally Determined Contributions), and in parallel provide 
comfort and healthy indoor air environment for the occupants. Modern building design 
practices ought to be committed to reducing energy consumption and CO2 emissions.  
There is another crucial aspect that indicates the necessity of designing more energy efficient 
buildings, the energy price element. Worldwide, energy consumption in 2018 rose by 
approximately two times the mean growth rate since 2010, driven by a robust world economy 
and increased heating and cooling needs in some parts of the world, according to the 
International Energy Agency (IEA) (10). Consequently, fossil fuel needs risen, led by natural 
gas, even as Renewable Energy Resources (RES) reported double-digit growth. In parallel, CO2 
emissions increased by 1,7% in 2018, hitting a new record (11). Thus, it’s expected that the 
global energy prices will continue their overall upward spiral in the future, if comprehensive 
energy policies remain robust, as IEA mentioned. Moreover, energy prices are also dependent 
on regional norms and external factors. Nonetheless, energy cost in Greece from 2010 until 
the first half of 2016, has increased by 49%,  is the highest percentage for energy across 
Europe (12). 
Nowadays, the majority of energy produced in the EU (European Union), is supplied to 
buildings in order heat, cool and ventilate the indoor air environment. It is estimated by 
Eurostat that HVAC (Heating Ventilation and Air Conditioning) systems consumed in 2016 the 
65% of the final energy provided to residential buildings in EU, with similar data applying to 
other member states such as Greece, as it is demonstrated in Figure 1 (13). In parallel, 
according to other sources, roughly 50% of the energy production is used for HVAC systems

3 Literature review 
Numerous methods and techniques have been created that aim to approximate the future of 
technological developments. Several terms and approaches that constitute the umbrella term 
of Future-oriented Technology Analysis (FTA), methods such as technology forecasting, 
technology foresight, technology assessment and technology roadmapping. Cagnin et al. 
(2008) have examined FTA types in conjunction with the methods and aims to analyze which 
of these techniques suit better in specific situations (19). Cagnin et al. (2008) suggest that 
roadmapping method suits in cases when a particular future is considered as desirable, and 
the target is to identify to prepare the organization for this expected future state 
technologically. (19). Roadmapping could be considered as a separate form of FTA, and it 
entails planning stepwise through a series of milestones that aim for technology development. 
Furthermore, roadmapping is particularly beneficial for foresight studies aiming at broad 
communities of stakeholders. Hence, the method equally focuses on analysis and 
communication of the technological and decision-making pathways throughout the 
organization. This chapter juxtaposes the theory behind the technology roadmapping as 
chosen theoretical foundation of this thesis and describes how it is adjusted for the case.  
3.1 Technology Management framework 
Nowadays, many executives are acknowledging the strategic importance of technology in 
providing their companies, organizations and operating sectors with value and competitive 
advantage. These issues are becoming increasingly crucial, as the cost and complexity of 
technology change increases, and competition and technology supply is a global manner. A 
mean to incorporate the technology and commercial perspectives in the current business plan 
and develop a long strategic view is Roadmaps and the Roadmapping process, encapsulating 
the TMF (21).  
Technology management (TM) is related to the sufficient identification, selection, acquisition, 
development and protection of technologies (product, process, or infrastructural necessary 
to maintain and develop a market position and succeed, in conjunction to the corporate values 
and objectives (21). For technology management to add value to the company, it entails the 
implementation of effective processes and systems to make sure that current and prospective 
technological resources within the organization are aligned with the corporate strategy. 
Sustainable technology management entails creating the ideal knowledge flows between 
commercial and technological perspectives in the company, in order to equalize the market 
‘pull’ with the technology ‘push’. The consistency of these knowledge flows is related to the 
internal and external business framework, and factors as corporate targets, market dynamics 
and technological context (22).  
The technology management framework is illustrated in Figure 2 and it indicates the 
interaction between the five technology management processes, as ISAEP (Identification 
Selection, Acquisition, Exploitation, and Protection) , business processes (SIO: strategy, 
innovation, and operations), shaped by the market and technological forces. As Phaal et al. 
(2001) mention, the overall purpose of the particular framework is to demonstrate how a 
company incorporates technical and commercial knowledge to assist its strategy, innovation 
and operational processes, in the context of both the internal and external environment (23). 
The various activities and targets that are affiliated with technology management practice in 
companies (e.g., technology strategy, foresight, R&D management) are interconnected with 
the particular context and objectives.  
 
==PAGE:15==
14 
 
 
 Figure 2: The Technology Management Framework (24) 
At the core of the TMF is the technology base of the firm, which demonstrates the 
technological knowledge, competencies, and capabilities reflected by five TM processes 
(ISAEP) that empower the corporate objectives. The TM processes are: Identification of 
essential technologies for the firm, Selection of particular technologies that make successful 
products and services, and Acquisition, Exploitation and Protection of selected technologies 
by the company, as they are extensively presented and analyzed by Phal et al. (2001) (23). 
The ISAEP technology management processes do not function in seclusion with the core 
business processes, as various TM processes are dispersed in business processes, e.g., 
selecting technology is taking place while planning the business strategy and new product 
development. There are three core business processes, functioning in several business system 
levels in the company, namely the strategy, innovations and operations (SIO). Linking core 
business processes is essential as they ensure sustainable productive output for the company. 
Additionally, it is necessary to spotlight mechanisms of linking technological and commercial 
perspectives with core business processes, as they contain the dynamic element of knowledge 
flow between them. Hence, for effective TM, it is essential to develop an ideal equalization 
between market “pull” (e.g., requirements), and technology “push” (e.g., capabilities). 
Different 'mechanisms' can empower the conjunction of commercial and technical 
perspectives, including traditional communication channels (e.g., discussions or email), and 
cross-functional teams or meetings, management tools. (23). Conceptual frameworks such as 
the TMF, are interconnected and in parallel co-developed with practical tools and techniques 
that are necessary to apply these principles in practice. One tool that encapsulates the TMF is 
the “technology roadmapping”. 
3.1.1 Why technology roadmapping is a match for the business case 
This tool can enhance an organization’s “radar”, in terms of developing planning horizons, and 
in parallel identifying and assessing potential threats and opportunities in the corporate 
context. Technology Roadmapping is a widely utilized tool by the industry, as it supports 
strategic product and technology planning, e.g., in the case of Rockwell Automation (25). As 
Phal at al underline, TRMs are promising to empower the application and evolution of 
integrated strategic business, product and technology plans, given the fact that companies 
have the tools and resources to create them. TMFping is a technique that enables 
organizations to deal with these challenges, by being useful in both company and sector level, 
empowering TM. Because of the context-dependent nature of specific technology 
management challenges, business processes and management tools need to be somewhat 
adaptable and customized to suit the particular company's objectives, needs, resources and 
culture (23).The technology management and the roadmapping tool both integrate this 
==PAGE:16==
15 
 
flexibility prerequisite, and thus have been selected for the analysis of this master thesis case 
study. 
An advantage that the tool has, and played an important role to its selection for the case in 
this thesis among other TMFs, is the flexibility, and the multiple aims that it can address, as 
roadmaps can adapt and take various format (22). Among the positive aspects of 
roadmapping, is the applicability of the tool to all companies, no matter their size, in contrast 
to most of tools/frameworks that have been criticized for their incompatibility to SMEs (26). 
SMEs typically operate with no formal processes, and might not have in-house R&D 
department, as they are followers, so the framework is compatible to them. As Phal et al. 
(2009) mention, this tool is based on the concept that ‘technology is a resource and the 
technology base of a company represents the technological knowledge that needs to be 
turned into products, processes and services through technological capabilities developed by 
effective TM’ (27).  
Roadmaps are adjustable depending on the case, and can take different forms, ranging 
between the two poles of technology push (being divergent and seeking opportunities) and 
market pool (targeting on a customized product). A simplified version of Roadmapping has 
been developed by EIRMA (European Industrial Research Management Association) (28), and 
is the generic TRM, as demonstrated in Figure 3.  
 
Figure 3:Generic TRM, developed by EIRMA (28) 
This roadmap version is time-bounded, and includes layers with both commercial and 
technological perspectives, and in this way, it spotlights the development of market, products 
and technology, throughout time, while indicating the gaps between them (22). Several types 
of roadmaps have been developed, to serve multiple objectives and companies; they are 
diversified between purpose, format and use, and layers have been adjusted accordingly. The 
terms often used in roadmap layers are the product, innovation, business or strategy (22). 
After a thorough examination of roadmaps by Phal et al. (2004), several of them are 
demonstrated diversified into 16 main areas, serving different purposes (29). Nonetheless, a 
specific technology roadmapping type has been selected for the thesis case, in order to serve 
the firm’s purpose and in parallel, encapsulate the research question. 
3.1.2 TRM for service organizations; customized for the case 
The Service/capability planning type roadmap is more suitable to service-based enterprises, 
such as KKLTD, that indicates how technology supports organizational capabilities. This 
particular TRM is aiming at seeking organizational capabilities and spotlighting the gaps, as an 
interjection between technology and commercial perspective. Figure 4 demonstrates a Royal 
Mail TRM which was amplified, to seek the external factors that will influence a specific service 
department of the firm and its principal aims.

3.1.3 Challenges of technology roadmapping 
Several challenges are related with technology roadmapping method and are primarily 
associated with distinguishing the boundaries of roadmaps; it is often difficult to maintain the 
roadmap scope into the context of the organization (i.e. picking the relevant trends that are 
directly affecting the organization or a particular branch). The flexibility element that this tool 
has can potentially be a downside, as it has multiple uses, and can be misused, by not serving 
the planned goals of the workshop. Additionally, it might be an issue that participants of the 
workshop focus exclusively on one trend, neglecting other perspectives, and a holistic view 
about the future market environment. Hence, it is important that the participants are fully 
prepared to focus on the projected roadmap scope, and preparatory sessions have taken 
place. Also, it is necessary that the company executives will decide to what extent the firm 
should adopt the outcome and additionally, how to incorporate it to the rest of corporate 
processes (22).  
Another weak spot of roadmapping is that it potentially excludes substantial uncertainties on 
one future and it doesn’t control where the vision comes from, as Ricard and Borch mention 
(2011) (30). This element occurs because technology roadmapping has been positioned within 
TM where the vision is a given element, being contradictive with strategy management, that 
the concept is developed. A shared vision in the organization is a reliable driver for any 
process, and it is usually developed by top management, but is important to make it shared 
vision aiming to shared actions. As Ricard and Borch (2011) underline, a roadmap only 
operates with one single vision, bounding the company strategy with one only pathway, and 
this could potentially be an issue. Therefore, the scenario making as a foresight technique 
enables projections for multiple future perspectives (30).  Roadmapping and scenarios, are 
both widely used methods that help company executives shape the future of the technology 
development of the firm. They can be combined to assess multiple perspectives i.e. as it has 
been applied in a clean development case for metal manufacturing (31). 
Considering the particular TRM chosen, one limitation of the roadmap for a service 
organization is the enhanced complexity, having multiple layers, different arrows 
interconnecting various components. It might be confusing, as the particular roadmap focuses 
on how the company incorporates the future market state, and in parallel analyzing the 
missing capabilities that need to be developed. In case that the TRM is developed by a larger 
group of organization’s members, it is often challenging to select the ideal participants, 
relevant for the workshop, as they are shaping the interval of the tool, and thus being an 
essential factor of roadmaps successful method execution and implementation (20). 
Furthermore, the roadmap built, can potentially be unnecessarily complicated. Considering 
the challenges after the roadmap creation, the key issues entail maintaining these outcomes 
followed and updated by executives throughout time and communicating the changes in the 
organization either further down or bottom up (22).

4.1 Research method 
This particular thesis is a case study research has been guided by a series of research questions 
and not by learning objectives, that are answered in the Results chapter. According to Yin 
(2014), the “how” questions are seeking to explain a present circumstance, as the situation of 
the case company that emerges. The case study method is relevant, as the questions require 
in-depth analysis and diagnosis of the situation. It is indicated that research questions 
requiring explanatory answers, as this thesis case, might be optimally approached using case 
study methodology, as these particular questions need answers with operational links been 
traced over time (32). Also, the method is suitable if a researcher has limited control over 
behavioral events, and the scope of the research is a contemporary phenomenon, as Yin 
indicates. Meaning that the boundaries between context and phenomenon are not evident, 
alike the particular case, with multiple contexts (environmental, business) and the case of a 
company striving for long term survival. Additionally, case studies can respond optimally to 
hypothetical and unconcreted circumstances, with no clear outcome, as Yin mentions (33). 
Furthermore, this thesis aims to juxtapose theories such as TM, market analysis, responsibility 
and competitive advantage, to incorporate practical tools, such as technology roadmapping. 
Hence, the theoretical arguments introduced in this thesis are backed by practical methods, 
demanding data sources, and thus, the case study methodology has been chosen as a 
qualitative research method.

4.5.1 Quadric framework 
The Quadric Framework (Figure 6) is a management tool that enables organizations to grow 
their strategic differentiation and competitiveness and realize their role in the value chain. 
The framework is an integrated approach that bridges the gap between brand strategy and 
execution, strengthening the focus and alignment of branding related activities to build 
strategic differentiation over time. As it has been underlined by Michael Sherain, the creator 
of the Quadric framework, the tool cannot stand alone in case analysis and needs to be 
surrounded by other techniques and methods. Various companies have used the model as a 
compass, such as Haldor Topsoe and Arla, and on the website of Quadric consulting, it is 
demonstrated how companies progress positioning strategies by the showcase of Quadric

applied to Volvo, Nike, and Apple (38). The framework is divided into the central core idea, 
which is the organizations positioning strategy, as well as four main areas, the optimized brand 
portfolio, cultural uniqueness, operational evidence and aligned marketing as they are 
extensively displayed on the website (38). This tool is utilized in order to depict the company’s 
current strategy and spot misalignments in contrast to its current mission.

4.5.2 Value chain model: Firm level 
Every company is a synthesis of activities that are executed to design, produce, market, 
deliver, and support its product. All these activities can be depicted using the value chain 
model. According to Porter, ‘a firm’s value chain and the way it performs individual activities 
are a reflection of its history, its strategy, its approach to implementing its strategy, and the 
underlying economics of the activities themselves’ (39). This particular framework diversifies 
from the value chain on industry level, in a way that it focuses on the internal processes, 
whereas the other spotlights the different entities that apart the value chain, depicting the 
flow of a product from the manufacturer to the customer. In competitive context, value, 
measured by total revenue, is the amount clients are supposed to pay for what a company is 
providing them. If the revenue generation of selling a firm’s service exceeds the costs entailed 
in offering this service, then the company is profitable. The target of any corporate strategy 
ideally is creating value for client that exceeds the cost of the purchased good/service.  
The value chain represents the total value which entails the value activities and margin. Value 
activities are diversified between primary activities connected with the physical creation and 
sale of the product/service, and support activities that empower the primary ones, and each 
other, through developed functions such as technology, human resources and management. 
There are five main primary activities, related to any firm: Inbound Logistics, Operations, 
Outbound Logistics, Marketing and Sales, Service, as they can be observed on Appendix F, and 
an extensive analysis has been conducted by Porter (39). The support activities include Firm 
infrastructure, HR Management, Technology development and Procurement. This tool is 
necessary for the case organization, as it can depict shift between the firm’s current activities, 
and the updated ones, enabled by the integrated package of solutions for the future. 
4.5.3 Growth/share matrix by BCG 
The Growth/share matrix is a widely used tool in strategic management, developed by a Big 3 
consulting company, the Boston Consulting Group (BCG). The BCG Matrix tool is the most 
frequent portfolio management tool utilized to define which should be the firm’s priorities on 
a product portfolio of a business unit. As underlined by Afriyie et al (2018), the matrix aims to 
evaluate the strategic position of the business brand portfolio and its potential (40). The 
products or services are placed on matrix, accordingly, depended on two axes parameters: of 
the market share of a service or product and the overall growth rate of the market. The matrix 
==PAGE:23==
22 
 
differentiates the firm’s business portfolio into four categories, the Stars, the Question marks, 
the Cash cows or the Dogs, as it can be observed in Figure 7. 
According to Johnson et al (2009), a star is a business unit which has a high market share in a 
growing market, and in this case, the firm might be investing a lot to drive the growth but 
expects high profit level from this portfolio (44). Prospectively, a question mark is a particular 
business unit operating in an overall growing market that has not yet acquired high market 
share, and heavy investment is needs to potentially convert it to a star. On the contrary, cash 
cow is a business unit with a high marker share in a mature market, and this portfolio doesn’t 
require heavy investment, as the market has low growth, and the firm already has it as a 
constant cash provider. Finally, dogs are the business units with a low share in declining 
markets, causing cash drain to the firm, and BCG suggest divestment. The particular model 
has been utilized in in order to analyze KKLTD product portfolio, and all data that the matrix 
is based can be observed on Appendix J. The market growth has been justified by data 
obtained by GFK, a data analysis company, as well as the accountant department of the case 
company, whereas the market share has been defined by data obtained from the 
management and crosschecked by GFK.

4.6 Caveats and Research Validity 
In this part, a snapshot of the criticism affiliated with the methods chosen on this research, in 
all stages, is exposed. To begin with, undoubtedly the case study research method has the 
generalization limitation; meaning that the particular case is a unique approach and 
generalizing the outcomes in the end of the thesis is not ideal, as well as biasing participants’ 
conclusions. It is also critical that the majority of data in the case study approach are 
qualitative, and thus it is necessary to distinguish various data gathering methods and 
crosschecking ways. Considering the case selection, there is the fear of subjectivity, as the 
case company is affiliated with the researcher, being an ex-employ and family member of the 
owner.  
The subjectivity issue arises also on the data collection part, and particularly the interview. 
Despite the fact that the interviewees were people from various companies, there is a 
subjective part as they have similar background and business patterns, and have interpersonal 
relations with the case company management, and thus might have not been gully objective,

giving feedback to the researcher. It is suggested, in future reports, to have more 
interviewees, (more than 9, used in this research), and the researcher-interviewer to be from

objective perspectives. 
At last, concerns have been exposed, regarding the semi structured interviews, selected as a 
data gathering formula. Bogdan and Biklen have mentioned that with semi structured 
interviews it is likely getting comparable data across interviewees but is less possible to 
understand their perspective of structure of the topic (42). Also, Jick points out the thread of

flexible, and the researcher to be biased and lead to subjective outcome (43).

Large scale HVAC projects, residential HVAC projects, online retail (e-store), physical stores,

5.2.4.2 
Embedding IoT to HVAC systems 
HVAC systems and lighting systems are a major part of building energy usage, a fact which 
implies that energy efficiency is an important aspect of it. Jang et al. (2011) from Chung-Ang 
University in South Korea, suggest an IoT-based HVAC and Lighting (named I-HVAC&L) system 
for combinatory HVAC system and lighting management, that entails energy saving by 
providing ideal thermal comfort. This is the first evolutionary step of digital integration of 
HVAC systems. Based on the IoT network environment, the I-HVAC&L system manages the 
==PAGE:31==
30 
 
HVAC system and the lighting system based on the room-acquired indoor environment data. 
This suggested system with embedded IoT technology, enables the facility manager to 
proactively set up the system and develop an energy management strategy that considers 
energy efficiency and thermal comfort. This combinatory solution has been tested and 
demonstrated an energy reduction by 11,2% compared to the existing installed HVAC and 
lightning system (51).  
 The suggested system by Jang et al. (2011) includes the HVAC system components, sensor 
box, IoT device, gateway and intelligent central manager (ICM), as it can be observed in Figure 
8. An IoT-based device is connected to the HVAC system, and includes a smart electronic 
switch and a remote control, while it collects data such as control and energy usage and 
operating status of devices. Linking to the gateway, the IoT-based device transmits analog 
circuitry and data. The sensor box gathers data on temperature, humidity, lighting and air 
quality (e.g., CO2, CO) from the interior and exterior building environment, in order to be used 
for controlling the system. The gateway is connected via Zigbee communication to the sensor 
box and IoT-based device, and stores data collected from the sensor box. The ICM collects and 
manages data stored in the databases, as it is connected with all building gateways throughout 
the network. Hence, via the interface monitoring feature, administrators can evaluate the 
operation of the system and develop an energy management strategy. The central 
management unit is also capable of identifying handset malformations and alerting the 
manager in case of an error. This functionality enables administrators to identify device 
abnormalities rapidly and respond to them, and consequently, this proactive operating 
approach of both HVAC and lightning systems can lead to optimized energy consumption (51). 
The usage of the IoT system and exploitation to its full capability requires expertise affiliated 
with electronical and software engineering, as well as capabilities to embed this feature to 
existing HVAC systems.

5.2.4.3 
Computational intelligence techniques for HVAC systems and data management 
The initiative of minimizing energy consumption in buildings has acted as a catalyst in the 
development of advanced Computational Intelligence (CI) techniques for energy efficient 
design, operation and control of HVAC systems. There are various ways to improve energy 
efficiency of an HVAC system by applying computational intelligence, empowering the firm to 
the second evolutionary step of digital integration of its services. Researchers have 
==PAGE:32==
31 
 
accomplished to apply CI techniques to overcome time delay, system uncertainties by 
assisting on HVAC system prediction, optimization, control and fault detection and diagnosis. 
For HVAC systems, multi-agent systems (MAS) are mainly used for control purposes and are 
machine learning algorithms that do not require any interaction or feedback from the user. 
They are mainly based on stochastic based optimization algorithms, and their implementation 
to HVAC systems and Building management System (BMS) can optimize the operation and 
maintenance, and thus, mean energy saving (52). 
One method is Fault Detection and Diagnosis (FDD) of HVAC systems, which is essential for 
consuming less energy and reducing maintenance costs, as its basic principle is to detect 
deviations that might mean malfunction of the system. The FDD techniques could be 
distinguished in three clusters: the rules-based, model-based and data driven (53). Model- 
based methods by applying energy and mass balance phenomenon and residues can be 
estimated by comparing modeling outputs and actual indications. Rules-based methods are 
independent from any model and are based on expert’s knowledge to create rules. Data-
driven methods that are able to suggest a sensor warning in case of malfunction, do not entail 
any physical model or expert knowledge. HVAC system components are regulated by different 
controllers which can be standalone controllers or intelligent Building Automation Systems 
(BAS). BAS is a system of sensors and controllers that collects, and processes data from the 
HVAC system, and utilizes them in order to detect defective operation through its BMS central 
station.  
These systems are promising, due to their computational capabilities, and also more strict 
regulatory requirements for building energy efficiency and HVAC systems (52). Subsequently, 
these CI techniques are meant to be embed, by HVAC design firms, with existing HVAC 
components and offer a reduced energy consumption solution. But in order to implement this 
technological solution, firms like KKLTD ought to develop a series of skills and technology 
capabilities, before 2022, in order to offer data driven design, operation, and smart 
maintenance services. More specifically, KKLTD has the HVAC expertise, able to synthesize 
various latest technological system components, but lacks the data management expertise. 
Hence, this ability to apply these algorithms in the BMS, understand the inputs and outputs, 
and process them in order to get exploitable data.   
5.2.4.4 
Combining RES with HVAC; HybridGEOTABS 
In order to achieve the growing demand of nZEB building solutions in smaller applications, 
connecting HVAC system with RES, and potentially solar panels, is a promising choice. KKLTD 
currently is experienced with Heat pumps, heat recovery units, and applies RES (solar panels) 
in case the energy requirements are high. Though, in the future, there are demands for nZEB, 
and near zero energy emission buildings, which heat pumps cannot solely offer, and thus, 
KKLTD should add new features to its existing system design methodologies.  
As a first step, a new technology, that can be added, and has been developed by a consortium 
of REHVA (Federation of European Heating, Ventilation and Air Conditioning Associations), 
companies, and four universities, including DTU, is GEOTABS. The abbreviation stands for a 
GEOthermal heat pump in combination with a TABS (Thermally Activated Building System). 
GEOTABS integrates the use of geothermal energy, a nearly limitless and omnipresent source 
of energy, with radiant HVAC systems that can offer good quality indoor air environment. 
Specifically, the HybridGEOTABS acronym is referring th the combination of GEOTABS with 
secondary heating and cooling systems as well as other renewable and residual energy 
sources (R2ES), aiming to provide quality thermal indoor air environment, by minimizing 
energy consumption (54). A geothermal system is including a loop of metal pipe buried 
==PAGE:33==
32 
 
underground, that pumps heating fluid which absorbs heat and transferred through a heat 
exchange, as it can be schematically seen in Figure 9. 
By using Model Predictive Control (MPC), a new control-integrated building design method, 
integrated into the commercial system solution in HybridGEOTABS, the aggregate HVAC 
system energy consumption is optimized, in comparison to current GEOTABS systems. As a 
principle, MPC uses a mathematical optimization problem that minimizes a cost function e.g., 
the building energy usage, by choosing the control variables such as the supply water 
temperature, and constraints can be integrated such as boundary temperatures for zones, to 
achieve ideal air indoor environment. Lastly, MPC has an integrated internal forecast of the 
system state that can predict major factors affecting the thermal comfort, such as outdoor 
temperature (54). In order to embed this technology, a firm needs expertise about geothermal 
units, and the optimal integration would be in combination with IT HVAC components and 
data management, for ultra-energy efficiency.

5.2.4.5 
Novel configurations of HVAC system components 
Novel configurations of existing HVAC system components are demonstrated by 
Vakiloroayato et al. (2013), and it appears to be a successful way to minimize energy 
consumption in hotel buildings. Obtaining a more energy efficient HVAC system can be 
achieved by re-configuring the traditional systems to create a strategic utilization of existing 
system parts. Nowadays, researchers have proved that an optimized combination of existing 
HVAC systems are able to assure thermal comfort and energy efficiency (55). 
Recently, alternative control and optimization strategies have been utilized to minimize 
energy consumption but as the publication suggest, these techniques are either requiring 
large financial resources or are too complicated to be integrated into an HVAC system (56). 
Hence the alternative of combine different HVAC systems to generate a low energy 
consumption amplification is a less expensive solution. As cooling and heating load varies 
depending on time and day, an HVAC system could be created in tandem with an ingenuine 
design alternative that will maintain the process variables to their required set-point to 
preserve the desired comfort level in any circumstances. These innovative energy saving 
configurations of existing systems can be divided into the following design techniques; 
evaporating cooling techniques, vapor compression systems and different configuration 
techniques. 
Considering the evaporating cooling techniques, one can avoid the use of ozone-destroying 
chlorofluorocarbons and hydrochlorofluorocarbons by using water in the HVAC system as the 
working fluid. This system configurations have many advantages; easy maintenance, 
installation and operation, that entail CO2 and other emissions reduction. The idea is 
==PAGE:34==
33 
 
therefore to explore both the possibility of increasing the utilization potential of the 
evaporative cooling system by combining various components with this system and the ability 
to improve the performance of other HVAC systems when integrated with the evaporative 
cooling system (55). Recent research shows that mechanical vapor compression-based air 
conditioning systems consume substantial amounts of electricity. Therefore, increasing all 
such air conditioning systems ' performance coefficient with air-cooled condensers is a 
challenging issue. The condenser can reject more heat by pre-cooling the air before it reaches 
the condenser coil. As a result, cooling capacity increases as energy demand and use 
decreases. As condensing temperatures decrease, head pressure decreases, allowing the 
compressor to run less frequently, resulting in energy savings (55). 
Lastly, considering various configuration techniques, changing the design requirements 
sometimes allows air conditioning systems to operate more efficiently without additional 
costs being placed. However, this should be in accordance to the design criteria’s that 
construction manager of the Hotel/project has set; the interviewees majority ask for balance 
of comfort and energy consumption (Interview quantification, Appendix D). One example is 
an approach to liquid pressure amplification which leads to large energy savings for 
refrigeration systems for vapor compression. Another alternative is radiant ceiling cooling 
systems that can reduce energy consumption substantially but are unable to regulate indoor 
moisture. While chilled-ceiling systems can provide significant energy efficiency, the threat of 
collecting water condensation on the chilled panels of the system leads to water drop on the 
ceiling. A combination of a chilled-ceiling system with a desiccant cooling system was 
suggested by Niu et al. (2001), to resolve this issue (57). Currently, KKLTD is having specialized 
HVAC configuration expertise, expected to be evolved, throughout the years with specific 
actions. The novel configurations of existing HVAC system components are the evolution of 
KKLTD current expertise, expected to be a widely desired technology capability that can lead 
the creation of an enhanced energy consultancy services by KKLTD.

than the technology perspective, in the sense that the case company is mostly selling a service 
and is not producing technology. In fact, dealing with customers such as hotel or villa owners 
entails that they are not acquainted with technology, KKLTD role is to explain them the 
technological aspect and convince them that the suggested solution is the ideal one for them, 
namely a low cost, or an energy efficient indoor climate HVAC system. Therefore, technology 
perspective comes in down the value chain, communicated to the clients, in a context that 
they can precieve, and their overall requirement is a good quality indoor air environment. The 
case company should use new technologies to actively minimize energy efficiency in new 
buildigs (MAS, IoT HVAC etc) and also to prove energy efficiency to clients, with the use of 
tools co-developed by partners.  
The commercial perspective is indicating that the organization is selling a service as it has the 
ability to communicate and understand the needs of the end user, and sell a technological 
solution that doesn’t exist yet, and this demonstrates that the case company is strong working 
further down the value chain. Everyone can sell something that is available today, but it is 
necessary to sell something exactly servicing the company purpose, which eventually cross 
matches as its customers purpose also. As an experienced sales engineering company, the 
ability to understand the market and knowing well the product portfolio gives to the company 
a competitive advantage, an edge. As long as the company knows the market, and is able to 
set the standards, it has the ability to work further down the value chain and ask 
manufacturers to deliver specific solutions. Another idea that would demonstrate expertise, 
further down the value chain, by creating along with a credible institution such as the SBC 
Greece (Sustainable Building Council) the first ever Green Hotel Certification, that certifies 
hotels for their energy performance. A major hotel contractor and client of the case company 
indicated on the interview considering green buildings, that effectively summarizes the view 
of industry experts: “I think that hotel owners and villa owners are more concerned about 
sustainability issues, and with their individual initiative, they would agree for a highly efficient

want more sustainable systems, thus would proceed with a green HVAC building certification. 
“ (Appendix C). Subsequently, manufacturers and dealers (such as KKLTD, which is an 
integrated installer and dealer firm) should focus their R&D processes to this direction, 
because there is market for this specific solution, as it has been documented and proven by

6 Review of results and caveats  
This chapter aims to investigate the effectiveness of tools and methods utilized, by critically 
reviewing the outcomes from each aspect of this thesis, that enabled the case analysis. The 
target is to evaluate the success level of answering the general research question and the sub 
research questions, having in mind Yin’s research validity design scope.  
Initially, the first RC, considering the Identification of the sweet spot of the case company, and 
the evaluation of the firm's background was empowered by underpinning theories that 
converged the scope of the thesis. The strategy clock indicated the need of focused 
differentiation strategy, as the firm is not focused, striving to serve all segments with multiple 
strategies. Another issue that was reported, by attempting to seek the sweet spot, is that the 
company claims that focuses on giving energy-efficient solutions, but in practice, is not 
delivering this component, and the sustainability element is missing on Quadric. BCG Matrix 
indicated the exploitation of the promising, profitable SBU of Hotel solutions, and 
frontrunning after sales support SBU. As an outcome, the firm ought to focus on the SBU of 
boutique hotels and villas and aim to provide a service the state-of-the-art solutions and 
technology, they are willing to invest in more sustainable solutions, and in this market, there 
is decreased competition. The approach of investigating the company is proven to be a 
successful sequence of applied tools, as the clients are very positive towards this new 
direction, especially contractors, and additionally, secondary data sources.  
However, the primary data obtained that enabled the development of theoretical frameworks 
aiming to diagnose the situation often were based on expert’s viewpoint or estimated 
quantitative data. For example, while processing the BCG matrix, considering the data on 
market growth and market share of the firm, experts’ opinion was utilized, as well as financial 
statements, as there are no accurate statistical outputs by the company or the sector.   
However, to achieve credibility and construct validity, multiple validation sources were used 
for cross-checking the data, such as internal, external (suppliers, customers, experienced 
engineers in the market) specialists’ estimations, financial and internal documentation, and 
GFK’s statistical contributions. Additionally, according to Yin’s research validity design 
perspective, the key informants reviewed the case study draft and the development of 
theories, as a tactic to obtain correct operational methods and achieve and construct validity. 
As such, the selection of the Quadric framework of the firm was reviewed by company 
executives, and industry experts checked the growth and market share. Considering internal 
validity, it has been realized that causal relationships appear in the literature. Company case 
relationships between all entities and researcher have been deduced and analyzed to match 
in a logical model, thus leading to a high level of internal validity.  
Concerning the second RC, the TRM, as a core of methodological frameworks, was adjusted 
to serve and lead the case company towards the future positioning strategy. In overall, the 
methodology process was challenging, as the roadmap was not developed by a group of 
experts but was consulted by company employees and executives. However, the developer of 
the method was an employee for many years, having internal knowledge, and capability to 
deal with this procedure. The trends were based on the client’s opinion, experts and employee 
remarks and built upon a literature foundation. However, it would have been more optimal if 
there would be a broader band of interviewees and larger specimen, to get a more 
representative point of view. The result of the roadmap was validated by employees and 
experts, from a feasibility perspective. A challenging process was to coordinate all data from 
various sources and to coordinate data collection through interview meetings, necessary to 
the roadmap creation. Hence, the availability of data is considered sufficient, as the 
==PAGE:41==
40 
 
companies and institutions in this sector have available information on the internet. And the 
reliability of data is considered very high, as all information was crosschecked in multiple ways, 
and the sources were picked for their integrity and selected for their minimum bias potential.  
Technology Roadmapping for a Service Organization, as a hybrid of Service/capability planning 
roadmap and multilayer technological roadmap, was proven to be a successful tool selected, 
judged by the outcomes of the process. However, as there are multiple possible futures, 
currently not incorporated on the framework, there could be a change of neglecting a 
potential megatrend. Nevertheless, as technology is more rigid and there are viable options 
for the firm, by developing a TRM, and the megatrend picked, was validated by both literature 
and clients. Undoubtedly, climate change is a megatrend, affecting global economies, and 
especially modifying the HVAC sector radically. Thus, the case company will be indeed 
equipped with technological capabilities that will deal with a trend that is going to affect the 
specific industry. 
Referring to research validity, the external validity aspect of the specific case is more 
complicated, as the tool adaptations are customized for the firm, and the topic is closely 
affiliated with technology HVAC aspect, thus study findings may not be generalized to a 
broader theory than the incumbent study. From reliability aspect, the specific case study could 
be replicated by another researcher and results could be the same, as the interviewees were 
picked for their integrity, and throughout the research period, the bias avoidance was a 
primary target.  
In overall, considering the evaluation of the answer of RC3, the results of the roadmap were 
validated by company executives, and the implementation steps were underlined as realistic. 
Indeed, these technologies selected, incorporating IoT, digital novel configurations, and 
machine learning algorithms are validated and feasible to implement. Undoubtedly, it will be 
challenging to integrate all these technologies in one HVAC system module, but engineers of 
the company are quite convinced that the integration path is evident. Nonetheless, it would 
have been more optimal to progress a furtherly elaborated budget of the new technology 
acquisitions and financial future balance sheet. Additionally, there might be a case that one 
or two technologies will be potentially unrealistic or marginalized, and therefore, the 
company should have an alternative action plan, or another technology path, but this wasn’t 
projected due to time limitations and scope of this thesis.

7 Conclusions 
7.1 Conclusions 
The application of theory, consisting of the TRM, has proven to be successful in terms of 
drawing a positioning strategy for the SME. The methodology of picking a case company 
included the following sequence: examining its financial and performance background and 
spotlighting its focus SBU by underpinning theories on strategic positioning and applying the 
TMF to incorporate technology on its long-term strategy. This sequence of methods and 
theoretical frameworks is suggested to be a standardized approach when treating a 
technology SME, and thus, could be used for various industry cases. Initially, the speculation 
that domestic clients need more energy efficient, sustainable HVAC solutions and consulting 
services, was proven by the interview with selected hotel managers and contractors. 
Doubtlessly, climate change is a megatrend for the high energy consuming building and HVAC 
sector, and all sector enterprises ought to focus their actions towards this direction to survive. 
It is a commercial trend that KKLTD was ignoring, due to heavy workload and lack of 
positioning strategy, as the firm strives to serve every customer (low cost to high quality) 
instead of doing business out of the megatrend identified. 
Current clientele, industry trends, and global initiatives, all together pull into the direction of 
more sustainable energy consumption, and the case company should harmonize its future, 
converged strategy, into that direction. This vision was incorporated into the technology 
roadmapping, customized for a service organization, that converted the technology and 
commercial perspectives into sensible business decisions. Technology perspective was 
extensively explored through literature review and interviewing experts. Indeed, digitalization 
will affect the HVAC systems sector in terms of optimization and design techniques and RES^2, 
innovative design SWs, IoT systems, and machine learning technologies are aimed to be 
integrated. Undoubtedly, currently, the case company is not capable of integrating these 
technologies and thus, the TRM for a service organization exploits the gaps in terms of 
readiness and indicates decisions that bridge these capability or infrastructure gaps. These 
potential business decisions, validated by their feasibility by current clients and technology 
experts, are projected through the ISAEP processes of the TMF to create concrete 
implementation steps to acquire the technology. Lastly, the implementation guide is 
examined in terms of financial feasibility, by projecting a modest economic scenario, and it 
has been realized that these implementations are indeed on a realistic scope and will double 
the profits and increase the revenues. Nonetheless, the future of HVAC sector "is digital" and 
lies into the initiatives of facing climate change. And any SME, to survive, gain its competitive 
and grow, needs to achieve its sweet spot and build a strategy to correspond to market trends. 
7.2 Further Research 
In case of further research considering technological SMEs in the service sector, the adapted 
version of TRM for a service organization is proposed, along with methods that enabled its 
development. Methods entailing semi-structured interviews with experts, underpinning tools, 
and reviewing a megatrend extensively, could be replicated to obtain a TRM that adds value 
to the company. Hence, the roadmap is successfully drawing the implementation path of 
effective processes and systems to guarantee that the current technological resources in the 
company are enforced and the upcoming ones are aligned with the defined corporate 
strategy. The challenge lies in having even more ideal management tools and that diagnoses 
the SMEs current status, and new theories could be investigated. Also, the TRM adjusted was 
successful in terms of addressing the foresight elements and integrate them to the existing 
structure. Nonetheless, a further adjustment seeking for investigation could be to add or 
replace components to actively demonstrate the current competencies and technological 
resources.  
==PAGE:43==
42 
 
Additionally, a weakness of the TRM is affiliated with the aspect of examining a single future 
direction only, that leads to the development of a holistic strategy. Thus, a TRM, including 
multiple futures could be a further research element by examining the possibility of adding 
more viable options. Therefore, it would be interesting to investigate, beyond this thesis 
scope, the available methodologies in the literature and seek methods that could be 
integrated into the roadmap, such as scenario analysis as a foresight method, and probe how 
they can be implemented to SMEs and support them on their positioning strategy.
