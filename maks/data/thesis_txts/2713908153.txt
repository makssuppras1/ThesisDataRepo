Abstract
MuZero was introduced as a generalisation of the revolutionary AlphaGo Zero; an algo­
rithm that learned to defeat the best humans and computers in the game of Go only by
playing against itself. At the core of booth algorithms lies the idea of using a model of the
environment to plan many steps ahead. Planning is done by exploring a search tree for
advantageous future actions using Monte­Carlo tree search (MCTS). Appraisal of future
actions is computationally expensive, but MuZero only uses weak rules to exploit these
estimates.
In this thesis, a more powerful Bayesian version of MCTS will be discussed. The Bayesian
MCTS will be compared against similar MCTS methods in a synthetic experiment, where
Bayesian MCTS outperforms the competitors. Using a Bayesian framework for MCTS in­
herents many theoretical benefits. Improved parallel exploration of MCTS will be demon­
strated as an example. Bayesian MCTS will then be integrated into to a self­implemented
version of MuZero. This new algorithm is shown to solve simple environments, while
outperforming the baseline of MuZero in the more complicated Atari 2600 console game
Breakout. Bayesian MuZero suffers from instability in modelling the uncertainty of predic­
tions, suggesting a promising direction for future work.

Introduction
In 2016 AlphaGo shook the world by beating one of the world’s best players in the board
game of Go. AlphaGo had learned to play partly from Go games played by experts and
partly from playing against itself. In 2017 an improved version was released under the
name of AlphaGo Zero. This time the algorithm only learned from playing against itself and
required only the rules of Go in the form of a perfect model. This perfect model of the envi­
ronment enabled the algorithm to plan and explore many steps ahead through a technique
called Monte­Carlo tree search (MCTS). MCTS represents all future moves as a search
tree, and then iteratively explore promising moves while excluding poorer moves. The
improved move estimates improve move selection during play, but also crucially serves
as a source to learn from. In practice a perfect model is rarely known for complicated
problems, so a model of the environment needs to be learned instead. This was the cen­
tral idea behind the more general algorithm called MuZero. This model­based algorithm
outperformed other methods in playing Atari 2600 games [Brockman et al., 2016]. A field
that model­free methods had traditionally dominated.
When MCTS is performed in MuZero most of the computation time is spent evaluating
future states in the game, but exploring and updating the search tree builds on fast and
simple heuristics. This imbalance could discard performance, as improved rules might ex­
ploit the expensive evaluations better. Recently this was highlighted by [Grill et al., 2020]
who improved MuZero by updating a decision­making heuristic with a more theoretical
sound rule.
This thesis aims to improve the planning and decision­making in MuZero through a Bayesian
approach inspired by [Tesauro et al., 2012]. Using a Bayesian approach would not only im­
prove performance and sample efficiency, but also yield a more useful and well­understood
framework for other tasks such as uncertainty estimation, better control of exploration, and
even improved computational speed through parallelisation.
Concretely the objectives will be as follows:
1. Implement and compare different versions of MCTS agents in a controlled experi­
ment.
2. Implement a Bayesian version of MuZero and investigate the performance.
Because of the high computational cost of training MuZero, it would be infeasible to train
MuZero in the original setting. So two simplifications will be made. Instead of training
MuZero on Atari by observing the image output, the direct memory of the Atari console
will be directly observed. The size of the neural network will also be reduced. None
of these simplifications should impact the comparison of MCTS, as it is only the quality
of the MCTS that is being compared. Because the switch to Bayesian MCTS is simple
to implement and has low computational overhead, the benefits gained for MuZero are
essentially ”free”. Mainly the improved sample efficiency is important as reinforcement
learning agents are known for having low sample efficiency, which can be detrimental
to real­world uses such as robotics. As the Bayesian framework is more theoretically
grounded and well­understood, new studies could build upon and improve the MuZero
further.
The following theory will contain a general introduction to reinforcement learning and key
concepts. Then the specific reinforcement learning problem of MCTS will be introduced

MCTS will be presented. The theory section will end with the introduction of MuZero, and

The Bellman optimality equations have an interesting implication. If the number of states

policy.
We now have a scheme for finding the optimal policy:
1. Initialise with a policy π, for example, all action being equally likely.
2. Compute vπ using eq. 2.3 and qπ using eq. 2.6.
3. Update the policy with eq. 2.9.
4. Repeat step 2 and 3 until the policy does not change, i.e. the policy satisfies eq.
2.10.
This scheme is the concept of policy iteration. Practically this algorithm has many draw­
backs that make it infeasible. The algorithm requires perfect information about the envi­
ronment to compute the expectations to compute step 2. This assumption is rarely the
case in practice. Even with perfect information, the number of states can be so large that
it is infeasible to store and compute vπ(s) for every possible state. Finally, it might take
many iterations before the algorithm converges.
The above difficulties can be solved by introducing some methods that make up the core
of reinforcement learning. The quantities vπ, qπ and updating the policy can be performed
simultaneously, reducing the total number of iterations.
Secondly, instead of computing vπ(s) for every possible state, we could select the most
promising subset of the states. An intuitive choice that removes the assumption of perfect
information about the environment is to play episodes in the environment and restrict the
update to those states. In many cases, this is the only way we can interact with the
environment, and this choice has many advantages. Selecting actions from π during an
episode results in faster convergence, as this focuses on updating qπ(s, a) for actions with
high value instead of bad states and actions.
The environment must be explored in a way that has a chance to visit every state and
perform every action. Otherwise, a better policy might never be found, resulting in the
loss of theoretical guarantees of finding the optimal policy. A commonly used method is
to take uniform random actions during episodes with probability ϵ following π otherwise.
This strategy is known as the ϵ­greedy method. Large values of ϵ increase the chance
of exploring better states and actions but waste time exploring sub­optimal states and
actions. Low values of ϵ result in more time spent exploring promising states and actions,
but it might take a long time to find better states and actions. This dilemma of exploration
vs exploitation is a fundamental problem in reinforcement learning. The dilemma will be
explored in a concrete example in the section about multi­armed bandits. One algorithm
which uses these insights is the deep Q­learning algorithm.

completely deterministic environment, the expectation in equation 2.12 can be removed,

As a result, Q­learning is guaranteed to converge to the optimal policy and action­value
function with probability 1 when a ϵ­greedy method is used [Watkins, 1989], [Watkins and
Dayan, 1992]. The property that a different policy can utilised during episodes is called
offline learning. Q­learning is an off­policy method because the update only requires the
reward and state transition that are policy­independent from the environment. Conversely,
methods requiring their policies to be followed during an episode are called on­policy.
Storing every possible state­action in a table is infeasible for most problems of interest.
In chess the estimated1 number of states are around 5 · 1052. Even worse is Atari 2600
games, which can be upper bounded to have 256210·160 states. A well studied solution to
this problem is approximating the action­value function Q(St, At) instead of storing every
possible combination [Melo and Ribeiro, 2007], [Lagoudakis and Parr, 2003], [Tsitsiklis
and Van Roy, 1996]. If the approximating function can capture the underlying structure in
the problem, this will also improve convergence speed. The milestone work [Mnih et al.,
2015] showed impressive results on the Atari 2600 benchmark using neural networks as
function approximators for the action­value function in what they called deep Q­network
(DQN). The neural network allowed the model to process a down­scaled game screen
of 84 × 84 × 4 values. DQN is an important predecessor for MuZero as many of the
insights from DQN are transferred to MuZero. Later improvements to DQN [Hessel et al.,
2018] has also been applied to MuZero. These will be discussed in a later section about
MuZero.
2.1.2
Model­based Methods
Learning how the environment behaves is often easier than learning the optimal policy.
For example, it is easy to learn the rules of chess, but mastering the game takes many
years for a human. Having a model of the environment can bring many theoretical bene­
fits. Uncertainty estimation in stochastic environments, in­cooperation of problem­specific
information, superfluous information can be removed, and learning about the environment
indirectly through the model. Depending on the specific agent, the concrete details of how
the model is learned and used will vary, but a typical structure includes a model and plan­
ning part, as shown in figure 2.2.
When a model is learned, a problematic dilemma is what information should be captured
and learned. Depending on how the model is trained, the model might predict the exact
next state or only specific information. As a result, the model might capture superfluous
information neither needed for the agent nor the dynamics of the environments. An ex­
ample is the colours of chess pieces indicates which opponent they belong to, while the
texture is not helpful. Ideally, the model should remove unimportant information without
removing important information.
Having access to a model also enables the agent to look ahead to plan what actions
to take. Planning can refine the agent’s decision­making during play and improve the

2.2
Monte Carlo Tree Search
Rooted trees are a way to represent a decision problem, where each node represents
a state of the environment and each edge a decision made in the node. The objective
is to find a node that maximises a given utility function, i.e. maximising the reward. Ex­
haustively searching all nodes is usually infeasible because the search tree grows expo­
nentially as depth increases. A popular solution for adversarial games such as Go and
Chess is Alpha­Beta pruning [Knuth and Moore, 1975][Newell and Simon, 2007]. The
idea is to discard searching a node and its children when it can be guaranteed that non
of the children can be optimal. Because general decision problems are not adversarial
and the value of a state given by a utility function will be noisy, Alpha­Beta search is not
as suited for general decision problems. Another approach is Monte Carlo Tree Search
(MCTS). Here the tree is iteratively expanded by starting from the root node and travers­
ing the tree until an unexplored node or terminal state is found. The new node is assigned
a value based on a utility function, and the process is repeated. Historically [Abramson,
2014] the utility function has been feature engineered (e.g. board evaluation in chess)
or the expected return from random simulations of the environment called rollouts. Re­
cently AlphaGo Zero and MuZero have shown impressive results approximating the utility
function with a neural network. To get a theoretical understanding of MCTS to improve
reinforcement learning agents, we can start by looking at a single node in the MCTS. This
problem can be formulated as the multi­armed bandit problem.
Similarities to the multi­armed bandit problem
A fundamental and well­studied reinforcement learning problem is the k­armed­bandit
problem (see figure 2.3). Here an agent π can select among k different bandits. Selecting
bandit i provides a reward ri from the bandit’s distribution Bi, so ri ∼Bi. The goal of the
agent is to maximise the cumulative reward. This is done by maximising the number of
times the bandit with largest mean return is selected, i.e. maxi E[Bi]. The key difficulty

2.2.2
Upper Confidence Tree Search
UCB is formulated for bandits of arbitrary distribution Bi, so the distribution could also be
a UCB agent itself. This structure can be used to construct an Upper Confidence Tree
Search (UCT) [Kocsis and Szepesvári, 2006]. The tree search problem is complicated by
the fact that bandits improve over time, resulting in drift of their means. The drift is caused
by initially the uninformed UCB agent selects random children, resulting in a return equal
to the average of its children’s return. As the UCB agent improves, it converges to the max
of children’s returns. This effect is compounded by the depth of the tree, as exponentially
more UCB agents need to find their maximal child before convergence at the root can
happen. [Kocsis and Szepesvári, 2006] proves the drift does not hinder the average
asymptotic convergence but, in practice, vastly slows down convergence.
Expansion in UCT is performed at each iteration by, starting from the root node, a child
is recursively selected according to equation (2.19) until a leaf node is reached. During
the back­up phase, all bandit selected during expansion updates their mean value with
the new observed reward. UCT is also often used in trees, where non­leaf nodes can
have an associated value. Here the expansion stops at an unexplored node instead of a
leaf node. This way, deeper exploration of the tree can be prioritized for more promising
actions.

2.2.3
Contextual Tree search
UCT was formulated for the problem without prior knowledge of the bandit distributions
other than some assumptions. For many problems, additional information is available to
guide and improve the tree search. This class of problem is called contextual bandits.
This was first described by [Langford and Zhang, 2007], [C.­C. Wang et al., 2005], and
[Pandey et al., 2007]. Because the observations in a reinforcement learning problem
can be considered context, many reinforcement learning problems can be interpreted
as contextual bandit problems. Because contextual bandits exploit information gained
from the context, their performance depends on how well and robustly the context can be
exploited. In the worst case, where context is gravely misinterpreted, their performance
can degrade to be worse than context­free agents.

If a probability distribution of Bi is known or well approximated, this information can be

2.3
MuZero
MuZero was introduced as a generalisation of the celebrated AlphaZero [Silver et al.,
2018]. The central idea in MuZero is to use a learned model of the environment to enable
the usage of MCTS to bootstrap the accuracy of the action­value function. The core of
MuZero consists of two components:
1. A prediction function is used for estimating the value of a state f(s) ⇒vs. In the
previous theory, this is analogous to the reward observed from the bandit.
2. A state dynamics function estimates the transition from one state to another g(s, a) ⇒
s′. This model enables traversal in the search tree.
The 1. and 2. component is enough to learn to play games such as Go and Chess, where
there is no intermediate reward. For environments with intermediate rewards it is unfit,
such as the popular Atari 2600 library [Brockman et al., 2016]. For these environments,
an additional component is needed:
• The dynamic function g is also tasked with predicting the reward when going from

The following two tricks from DQN used to improve MuZero is:
• Only letting the agent perform an action every n frames to let the agent experience

• Storing old memories in a memory replay buffer [Lin, 1992] for training to allow

• More often sampling harder samples from the replay buffer during training to in­

• Running many agents with the same policy simultaneously to speed up experience

Bayesian MuZero
It is unclear how exactly the visit count distribution Πs = n(s, a)/N from MuZero is related
to the context P in PUCT. This has also been pointed out by [Grill et al., 2020], and
enhancing this should lead to improved performance. In addition, it will be demonstrated in
the experiment section that Bayesian UCT outperforms all other MCTS agents, including
the MuZero version of PUCT.
Inspired by Bayesian UCT, the idea is to implement a fully Bayesian framework for the
MCTS in MuZero. While MuZero only back­up the average return, this Bayesian will back­
up the whole return distribution. This enables the MCTS to explicitly model uncertainty,
allowing better exploitation of information and inheriting useful statistical tools. This con­
trasts MuZero’s version of PUCT, which only indirectly models the uncertainty through the
context vector P.
In Bayesian UCT, the leaf nodes observe the returns and compute the posterior of the
mean that is back­up through the tree. Because a leaf node becomes an interior node
when explored, the previous leaf node’s posterior is replaced by the new leaf’s improved
estimate. An elegant solution is to simply predict this posterior distribution using the con­
text head in MuZero.
As a result, the context head of MuZero will be converted from predicting the context P
to directly predicting the posterior distribution of the mean. For many environments, the
number of actions |A| is large, so it is not feasible to represent every action’s posterior
with a categorical distribution as is done for the value and reward. Instead, it will be
assumed that the mean is normal distributed, so only the associated mean and variance
of the posterior will be predicted. This only doubles the number of outputs compared to
the context vector in MuZero.
Changes of the Context Head
Predicting the posterior of the action­value function Q could be noisy, as the scale of the
action­value function is typically much larger than the value difference between actions.
This means the noise from predicting the action­value for each action could dominate
which action is deemed optimal. The advantage A of selecting an action will be predicted
instead to reduce the impact of noisy action­value estimates. This approach is inspired
by [Z. Wang et al., 2016] that improved performance for DQN. The advantage is defined

3.0.2
Related Works
There exists large amounts of literature [Browne et al., 2012] trying to improve MCTS in
general and for specific problems. Notably, an approach also using Bayesian methods to
improve estimates is proposed by [Bai et al., 2013]. Here the interior nodes are changed
from UCB to Thompson sampling nodes. They assume the returns from different arms
can be modelled as a Gaussian mixture model and impose a conjugate before obtaining
the bandit distribution. Crucially, their approach does not propagate the distributions from
the nodes up the tree like UCT; only the return value from a leaf is propagated up. This
effectively treats each node independently. They also do not use any context for their
method as the prior is set to a predefined distribution.
The trade­off between exploration and exploitation lies at the core of reinforcement learn­
ing. A sub­field of reinforcement learning seeks to optimally solve this trade­off by exactly
reason about uncertainty in a model using Bayes rule. Exact Bayesian inference is often
intractable because the number of states is often very large, so MCTS have been used to
only concentrate on states deemed important [Asmuth and Littman, 2011], [Guez et al.,
2013],[Vien et al., 2013].
As the MCTS in MuZero is based on a variant of PUCT it is implicitly assumed that the
context Πi on action i is proportional to action i being optimal. [Grill et al., 2020] formu­
lates the problem of matching context P to the visit count distribution Π as a regularised
optimisation problem. They show MuZero only solves this problem approximately and
observes improved performance when the problem is solved exactly. A recent publica­
tion [Danihelka et al., 2021] improves MuZero when the number of MCTS samples is low.
This is done by ensuring MuZero evaluates sparsely explored states while not sacrificing
performance when the visit count is high.

Comparison of MCTS Agents
Ideally, the different MCTS agents presented in the theory section should be compared by
implementing them in MuZero and comparing the performance across multiple runs. As
some agents require different model architecture, it is hard to compare directly and is pro­
hibitively computationally expensive. While all MCTS agents presented have comparable
asymptotic scaling 2.1, there might be a significant difference in practice. For example,
well­informed context­based agents should outperform non­context based.
Instead, the agents will be evaluated using two experiments in a complex environment: a
test of the MCTS agent’s resilience to noise, and a test of their ability to exploit information.
The MCTS will have a depth of 4, with each child having five children. The leaf distributions
will be Bernoulli distributions, where the parameter p is sampled as a beta distribution with
α = 2, β = 5. Because MuZero uses N = 50 explorations, the same amount will also be
used here. The interior nodes will also be given a value to mimic MuZero. This is done
by computing a node’s maximum distribution over its children, starting from the bottom.
The context distribution will be set to obey two constraints. The mean of the context
distribution will be pc, so for a beta distribution this corresponds to pc =
αc
αc+βc . Because p
is sampled from a beta distribution, the strength of the context parameter c can be set to
measure the number of ”prior” samples. This corresponds to the restriction c = αc + βc.
The noise will be varied by moving the mean pc way from the true mean p. This will be
done by first sampling a new point pnoise ∼Beta(αc, βc). Then setting the context mean
to pc = cnoise · pnoise + (1 −cnoise)p. To avoid the context mean falls outside [0, 1], cnoise
will also be restricted to [0, 1]. Finally, the context distribution will be recalculated with the
noisy parameters.
While this experiment construction seems complex, it allows for clear interpretation of the
parameters c and cnoise. The test environment construction is also listed in algorithm 3.
The entire context distribution is provided to agents requiring context to make the compar­
isons fair. The PUCT­based agents (PUCT, MuZero criteria) set their context according
to the chance of a bandit being the optimal choice as described in section 2.2.3. This
enables PUCT and MuZero criteria to have an unfair advantage, as it is unclear that the
context is presented this way in MuZero. The PUCT and MuZero­based criteria have an
added version (marked informed), where the mean estimates are also based on the con­
text. Intuitively the informed versions should perform better, as better information about
the mean return of the bandits. However, because the informed versions both rely on the
context for mean estimation and context vector P, the noise might further make the agent
over­confident, resulting in the agent becoming stuck in a sub­optimal node.
To measure the strength of each MCTS agent, one could use cumulative regret, but the
regret incurred during exploration is of no concern in MuZero; only the value of the pro­
posed best matters. A more related measure of performance is the difference between
the best next action and the identified best action. In the search tree, the next action is
which of the root’s children is chosen. The value of these children is set to be the maxi­
mum of any node below that child. Thus the measure of top­level regret is the difference
between the best node and the identified best node.

4.0.2
Parallel Exploration
UCT was introduced as a sequential planning algorithm, but it is possible to run it in
parallel to improve the exploration of the search tree. While parallel exploration is not
used in MuZero, it was used in AlphaGo and AlphaGo Zero to improve playing strength.
Parallel UCT faces problems in practice, as the parallel exploration muddies the back­up
operation. In contrast, Bayesian UCT avoids this problem. This benefit of Bayesian UCT
was not explored in the original paper but will be tested here.
UCT’s deterministic action selection would result in parallel threads exploring the exact
same nodes. Virtual loss [Chaslot et al., 2008] is a commonly used method that discour­
ages exploring the same nodes by introducing a penalty each time a node is explored.
Every time a thread selects a node, that node is virtually updated, as if the worst possible
return has been observed nvl additional times. This incentives other threads to explore
different paths when uncertainty is high but allows exploitation when a node is almost
surely optimal. In practice, virtual loss works well, but as more threads get added, the
benefit of additional threads diminishes. In the worst case adding more parallel threads
can even worsen the final accuracy [Segal, 2010]. This happens because searching in
parallel explores less promising nodes, but the value of a node in UCT is the average re­
turn from selecting that node. In the worst case, a promising node might not be explored if
one promising and one poor node were explored simultaneously. So it would take many
iterations before the promising node was further explored. The poor sibling essentially
muddies the back­up value of the promising node.
Because Bayesian UCT back­ups the entire return distribution, the above example cannot

node. It will not affect the distribution of the good sibling.
To measure the best possible speed­up from paralisation, the performance of a parallel
agent needs to be compared to the non­parallel agent nparallel = 1. The parallel efficiency
will be measured as follows. First the final value Rfinal of the top­level regret after Ntotal

parallel agent will then be the number of samples needed (Nneeded) for the non­parallel
agent to obtain the final regret of the parallel agent. The score will be normalised to
yield eff = Nneeded/Ntotal. To reduce the noise of the estimate of eff, the regret curve
will be fitted with a function. Because UCT and Bayesian UCT should follow O(ln N)
asymptotically, this bound can be used to derive a class of functions to use for curve
fitting. This is derived in A.0.6.

4.0.3
Performance of Bayesian MuZero
To measure if Bayesian MuZero is an improvement over MuZero it will be tested in two
environments The first environment is chosen to be the simple environment Cart­pole v1
from Openai’s Gym package. Cart­pole is simple enough so booth MuZero and Bayesian
MuZero can be trained to show their ability to converge with the computational power
available. For all tests, the same machine of 2 V100 GPUs were used. This enables
performance comparison against time so that MuZero can benefit from its faster MCTS.
Booth MuZero and Bayesian MuZero are self­implemented in Python and Pytorch for this
project found here1.
Notable the network architecture was reduced to allow for faster inference during environ­
ment play. In the original MuZero a residual structure [He et al., 2016] with 16 block was
used for f, g, h. Here a simple network with two 1D convolutions and four linear layers
was used for f and h. Because unrolling for prediction effectively increases the depth of
the neural network, it was important to allow the gradients to flow well to avoid vanishing
and exploding gradients. So g is chosen to remain a residual structure, but with a single
block instead. Because the network structure was changed, and the input dimensions of
the environments changed, the learning rate needed to be changed. The optimiser was
changed from Stochastic gradient descent to the more forgiving ADAM [Kingma and Ba,
2014] to reduce the time taken to do hyper­parameter search,
MuZero contains a large number of hyper­parameters, so most were kept the same as
the original MuZero on Atari games. A list of hyper­parameters for each environment
can be seen in the appendix A.0.7. Notably, prioritised sampling and the usage of the
MCTS mean estimate ν was found to hinder convergence. Both of these were disabled
for Bayesian MuZero, so these tricks were not included. The exploration parameter (tem­
perature parameter) was informally tuned individually for each agent.
For Cart­pole v1, the environment rewards were divided by 500 to reduce the scale of
variance. As will be discussed later, Bayesian MuZero has some problems with drifting
rewards. As will be discussed later, Bayesian MuZero has problems with variance feed­
back loops, so to stabilise learning, either a feedback loop around the variance prediction
of value V or the advantage A, will be disabled. Bayesian MuZero has some problems
with converging exactly because of the scale of the predicted variance. To show con­
vergence, a second run was done with the estimated variance of the value V fixed to
Var[V ] = 1/6. In Breakout, the variance of A was set to Var[A] = 1.

Discussion
5.0.1
Performance of Bayesian MuZero
The results from MuZero and Bayesian Muzero in the simple environment of Cart­pole
v1 are shown in figure 4.5. The figure shows MuZero solves the environment in around
1.5 hours (around 8k episodes), and maintains convergence. In comparison, Bayesian
MuZero is close to consistently solving the environment around 2 hours but struggles to
solve it every time. After 4 hours, the agent begins to diverge. As discussed later, this is
presumably due to problems with modelling the variance near convergence. To test this
hypothesis, the variance of the prediction head f is replaced with a variance of 1/6. Now
the Bayesian MuZero consistently solves the environment after 2.5 hours. The model
recovers from the slight drop in performance after 5 hours but is not shown in the graph.
While Bayesian MuZero was slower than MuZero to solve the environment, the overall
goal of the experiment was to show that both algorithms could converge, as such, the
hyper­parameters have not been tuned for fast converge.
This instability from predicting the variance was also present when playing Breakout.
Here it was found to stabilise the learning by overwriting the predicted variance of A with
Var(A) = 1. From the results in figure 4.6, it can be seen that MuZero initially improves
rapidly but begins to slow down. In comparison, Bayesian MuZero keeps improving and
even accelerates around 20 hours. This results in Bayesian MuZero obtaining a little
under three times the episode reward over MuZero.
Further hyper­parameter tuning might close the gap between the algorithms, but it sug­
gests Bayesian MuZero outperforms the baseline significantly. It is unknown if this gap
translates to larger neural network architectures and more complex environments. Some
work [Jones, 2021] suggests booth architecture size and complexity of the environment
scales predictably, but larger scale tests is needed to cement a difference between the
MuZero and Bayesian MuZero.
5.0.2
The sensitivity of MuZero and Bayesian MuZero
Booth MuZero and Bayesian MuZero are very complex algorithms with numerous hyper­
parameters. While not apparent in this report, most of the time in the project was spent
tuning and making small changes to the code to get the algorithm to learn anything. This is
because both models are very sensitive, and small errors can make them easily diverge.
For example, non­randomly breaking a tie during action selection in MuZero’s PUCT ver­
sion would result in MuZero not converging on Cart­pole. Especially the initialisation of
the parameters of neural networks was found to sensitive. In the MuZero paper, the way
the parameters are initialised is not mentioned. It was found here that the bias terms
of the last fully connected layer of f and g producing the categorical distribution should
be roughly normal distributed. The context prediction head f should be uniform over all
the actions. This is concerning as a potential better hyper­parameter configuration could
change the results of either MuZero or Bayesian MuZero. As the hyper­parameter values
shared by MuZero and Bayesian MuZero are either based on the original publication of
MuZero or based on own tuning of MuZero, this scenario is less likely, but the concern
remains.

5.0.3
Exploitation and Bias in MCTS
A general problem using estimates from MCTS is that the tree exploration operation bi­
ases the value estimates. [Shin et al., 2019] dissects the sources of bias from MCTS
into selection of nodes and back­up of the tree. They prove that UCT sampling nega­
tively biases the value estimates. This effect has also been observed during previous
experiments, where the bias of the top nodes estimate is shown in figure 5.1. From that
figure, it can be seen that also MuZero and PUCT suffer from negative bias, an effect not
diminished with improved context except for the non­informed PUCT, Bayes­UCT and
Thompson sampling. Interestingly, only Bayes UCT and Thompson sampling are pos­
itively biased. The positive bias is caused by the max operation during back­up and is
discussed (only for scalar and not distributional returns) by [Dam et al., 2019]. Using the
context head to predict these biased values from MCTS could pose a problem, as the
MCTS would further amplify any bias captured by the context. Predicting the advantage
partially reduces this amplification, as any bias present in booth V and Q is removed
because the advantage is calculated as A(s, a) = Q(s, a) −V (s). It was the primary
motivation for predicting the advantage instead of the action­value for this reason. The
bias amplification was observed to be a problem during initial testing where the context
head predicted the action­value Q instead of the advantage. The bias quickly caused the
algorithm to diverge.

Uncertainty Estimates
The sources of variance in Bayesian MuZero can be seen in figure 5.2. As the figure in­
dicates, the variance of the value and advantage estimate is bootstrapped from their own
estimates in MCTS. This can be a significant problem if the estimated variance is consis­
tently too large. This is because the large variance would be used as a training example,
further increasing the variance. Usually, this problem is dampened by the variance reduc­
tion from discounting the reward, and the MCTS also generally reduces the error. There
are, however, two cases that could start this feedback loop. A too uncertain initialisation
of the neural network parameters leads to a too large variance being predicted. The more
concerning case is when the model is rapidly improving. Here the increase in the scale of

5.0.5
Precision of Variance
A problem with using MuZero’s categorical representation of the reward and value head is
the precision. Any target mean can be represented in the interval between minimum plow
and maximum phigh support. This is not the case for variance, as the distance bounds
the minimum representable variance to the nearest support from a specific mean. This is
illustrated in figure 5.3 with MuZero’s original support of [−300, 300]. Here the support is
so coarse that distribution with a mean of 1 cannot have a variance below 2. This level of
inaccuracy is not acceptable for many applications, for example, the change to a support
of[−50, 50] for the Breakout and Cart­pole environment. This change only pushes the
minimum variance to 0.1 for a distribution with mean of 1. Another option is to increase the

6
Further work
PUCT Based On­policy Exploration
Bayesian MuZero uses a more stable off­policy estimate of mean and variance for explo­
ration. This estimate is a non­satisfactory heuristic, that could be improved. A promising
and elegant solution could be to perform exploration with PUCT like MuZero, but calcu­
late the context P explicitly from the off­policy (max operation) back­up. As observed
during experiments, the max operation positively biases the value estimates, while the
exploration using PUCT negatively biases the results, so using booth could decrease the
bias. It would also remove the need of predicting the visit count distribution, that is only
proportional to an action being optimal, as using the distributional information would allow
for the optimal choice of context for PUCT.
Improved Uncertainty Estimation
The precision of the predicted variance in Bayesian MuZero heavily affects the perfor­
mance. Ideally, unbiased and robust method for uncertainty estimation should be used to
predict variance. Two promising approaches include powerful calibration based methods
[Zhang et al., 2020], [Kumar et al., 2019], or a better and more robust uncertainty esti­
mation models such as Bayesian neural networks [Gull, 1989], [Neal, 2012]. Calibration
based methods would keep the neural network architecture intact, reducing the need for
specialised architectures and removing the need for architecture optimising. Bayesian
neural networks could be more powerful than calibration, but for complex environments
MuZero (and Bayesian MuZero) needs expressive large neural networks. These models
are evaluated many times during episodic­play, so the method would need to be computa­
tionally cheap. Large Bayesian neural networks are typically computationally expensive,
so so a middle­ground could be to a Bayesian neural network only for the top layer.
Stochastic Environments
Problems in the real world are usually either stochastic or so complex it virtually is stochas­
tic. MuZero have already shown impressive results in complex environments, but adding
Bayesian MuZero’s explicit modelling uncertainty would make it applicable to real­world
applications were risk­awarness is of crucual importance. Examples of this is automated
diagnosis in healthcare or finance where high risk choices generally are avoided even if
they have a high return. While the Bayesian framework of MuZero shows promising re­
sults, further work into stabilising the learning is needed for making it perform well across
many different real­world environments.

7
Conclusion
MuZero’s use of Monte­Carlo tree search (MCTS) to look many moves ahead has led to
impressive performance in complicated reinforcement learning environments. The back­
ground of the problem of MCTS have been introduced together with many variations of
solutions. Different variations of MCTS have been compared in a comprehensive experi­
ment. Here, a Bayesian MCTS agent was found to yield the best accuracy across varying
levels of informational context given and noise. Bayesian MCTS inherits favourable sta­
tistical properties. One such property was demonstrated in an experiment in the form of
improved parallel exploration of MCTS compared to its non­Bayesian counterpart.
An integration of Bayesian MCTS into MuZero was introduced and tested against a self­
implemented version of MuZero as a baseline. The Bayesian version of MuZero was
shown to solve the simple problem of Cart­pole v1, and outperform the baseline in the
complicated game of Breakout. While Bayesian MuZero outperforms MuZero, it is more
fragile than MuZero, mainly because of problems with modelling its own uncertainty. It is
hypothesised that feedback­loops from predicting variance was the largest source of this
problem. A promising future direction is improvement of uncertainty prediction that can
stabilise Bayesian MuZero and further improve performance.
