Abstract
The last few years of major advances in deep learning have made deep learning a hot
research topic. The substantial success in computer vision tasks, have encouraged many
researchers to compare the processing of visual signals in deep learning architectures with
the processing of visual signals in the human brain. The hope is, that by building brain
like models, we might shed light on and understand how the brain functions.
The aim of this project is to investigate if deep neural network models can be used as basis
for models capable of describing some of the attention mechanisms in the human brain.
Instead of developing a deep neural network model, we considered whether the features of
a pretrained ResNet-50 network could be of relevance in the prediction of an attentional
signal. Inter-subject correlations (ISC), which is a measure of attention obtained from
EEG recordings, were acquired from subjects watching video clips from "Sophie's Choice"
and "Bang! You're Dead". Linear shrinkage models were then trained to predict the ISCs,
based on activations extracted from dierent layers of the pretrained network for each of
the frames in the two video clips.
We found, that for four out of 16 layers, the extracted activations facilitated predictions
of ISC signicantly correlated with the true ISC. It was, furthermore, observed that the
majority of signicant activations were extracted among the rst 15 layers of the network.
This indicates that the network learns to capture attention related features in the rst
third of the network. This tendency should, however, be validated in future studies.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
In recent years, many elds of research have been subject to large improvements due to
major advances within the eld of deep learning and articial intelligence. The widespread
applications of these algorithms have made them an area of interest for researchers, com-
panies, and politicians [31].
The term Deep Learning is a relatively new term and was rst taken into use in 2006.
Before 2006 the algorithms were known as Neural Networks or Articial Neural Networks
(ANN). These algorithms were inspired by the attempt to encapsulate the way biological
systems process information mathematically [2]. The neural perspective on deep learning
is motivated by two main goals [13].
1. By reverse engineering the computational principles of the brain it might be possible
to build intelligent systems.
2. By building brain like models, we might shed light on and understand how the brain
functions.
Neuroscience remains to be a major source of inspiration for the development of new Deep
Learning architectures. An example of this is the convolutional and pooling layers in con-
volutional neural networks which are directly inspired by receptive elds of cells in the
visual cortex, and the overall architecture is an attempt to mimic the hierarchical process-
ing structure of visual cortical regions in the so-called ventral pathway [31].
However, much eort also goes into building intelligent models capable of simulating the
activity of the brain for an interpretational purpose. In fact, comparison of convolutional
network models and monkeys that were shown the same picture proved that the activa-
tions of high-level units in the convolutional network model explained half of the variance
of random sets of 160 neurons in the monkey's inferotemporal cortex [4].
In general natural signals are compositional hierarchies, in which higher-level features are
obtained by composing lower-level ones. In images, local combinations of edges form mo-
tifs, motifs assemble into parts, and parts form objects. Similar hierarchies exist in speech
and text from sounds to phonemes, syllables, words and sentences [31]. Scientists have
therefore suggested that the mammalian brain relies solely on canonical computations to

analyze sensory input [5]. In fact, neuroscientists, have shown that ferrets can learn to
see with the auditory processing region of their brain if their brains are rewired to send
visual signals to that area [58]. Studies like that and the fact that deep neural networks
have been shown to exploit the compositional hierarchies of natural signals e.g. images
have given reason to hope that deep learning algorithms might be able to resemble multiple
brain processes [59]. Although deep neural networks already have proven to form brain
like features both when used in image classication [4, 24] and speech recognition [35], the
models are unsuccessful to go beyond the sensory system [60].
It would be of great interest if deep neural networks could resemble attention-driven pro-
cessing in the human brain. This would be extremely applicable in domains like the lm
industry and the marketing industry as a supplement to user studies. Another area in
which such attentional state models would make a huge impact is in educational envi-
ronments. Imagine educators being able to test the degree to which their lectures evoke
attention. This would not only benet the educator but also the students.
As a step in the direction of building an attentional model, this thesis seeks to investigate
the applicability of the successful convolutional neural network ResNet-50 as a basis for
an attention model. The objective is to examine whether extracted features from dierent
layers of a deep neural network, can be used to predict inter-subject correlation (ISC),
which is a measure of attention obtained from EEG recordings [9].

State of the art
This chapter, describes some of the research and newest methods related to this thesis.
2.1
Convolutional Neural Networks for Image and Video clas-
sication
Much of the development in deep learning has been driven by the accessibility and amount
of data [1]. Large-scale image dataset and challenges like ImageNet Large Scale Visual
Recognition Challenge (ILSVRC) [43], Canadian Institute For Advanced Research (CI-
FAR) dataset [28], and MicroSoft Common Objects in COntext (MSCOCO) [33] and
large-scale audio dataset such as Switchboard [12] have made image recognition and speech
recognition some of the drivers for the development of new deep learning algorithms. Lately,
video dataset such as YouTube-8M Dataset [1] has emerged with the goal of accelerating
research on large-scale video understanding, transfer learning, etc.
One of the algorithms that have shown to perform well within a broad range of elds
is Convolutional Neural Network (CNN). The CNN architecture was proposed by Yann
LeCun et al. in 1989 [30] and is simply neural networks that use convolution in place of
general matrix multiplication in at least one of their layers [13].
In the following sections, we will go through some of the most popular and successful CNNs
for image and video classication.
AlexNet
The usages of CNNs was limited until the SuperVision team proposed the famous AlexNet
[29] and won the ILSVRC in 2012 with a top-5 error of 16.4% but with a margin of 9.8%
to the nearest competitor. The SuperVision team trained a deep CNN, with 60 million
parameters using an ecient GPU implementation to reduce training time. The paper also
introduced rectied linear units (ReLU) as non-linearities and the dropout trick to make
the network more robust [29, 19]. The performance of the AlexNet did not only make
CNNs increasingly popular, it also attracted more attention to the competition, making
it the benchmark within the eld of image recognition[43]. Inspired by the success of the
AlexNet, the vast majority of entries used deep CNNs for their submission in ILSVRC
2013.

Visualizing and Understanding Convolutional Networks
The winner of ILSVRC 2013 was Clarifai, who used an ensemble of several deep convo-
lutional networks for classication, achieving a top-5 error of 11.7% [64]. The team used
many of the same techniques introduced by the SuperVision team e.g. dropout technique
[19]. The main contribution, however, was the use Deconvolutional Networks [65] to ex-
amine and assist with selecting suitable architectures visually [64]. In the paper [64], they
visualize how the features of the fully trained model become increasingly more complex
with the depth of the network.
VGG
In contrast to previous networks, the VGG network architectures introduced smaller [3×3]
lters in each convolutional layers [45].
The Oxford group behind the VGG networks
showed that multiple [3 × 3] convolution in sequence could replace the larger receptive
elds used in earlier networks. The Oxford team also explored how the number of layers
aected the accuracy by training models with, 11, 13, 16 and 19 layers. The combination of
many features in each layer and many layers made the VGG networks both challenging and
time-consuming to train. As we will see in next section, there are clever ways to regularize
the model, to somehow restrict the massive search space promoted by many parameters.
Inception
In 2014 Google proposed a network named GoogLeNet [51], which achieved a top-5 error
of 6.7%. As opposed to the VGG network architecture presented in the same year, Google
showed that it was possible to increase the depth of the network was while keeping the
computational cost low by introducing the so-called inception layers. GoogLeNet heavily
relies on [3 × 3] just like the VGG network, but they also [1 × 1] convolution. Especially
the [1 × 1] convolutions in the inception modules functions as bottlenecks which reduce
the computational cost by shrinking the data size before the computational expensive
convolution operations. Google has further explored and optimized the inception network
architecture, and the newest version (Inception-v4 or Inception-ResNet [52]) uses both
batch-normalization [21] as well as residual connections originally introduced in the ResNet
architecture [16].
ResNet
In 2015 a team from Microsoft introduced a technique called Deep Residual Learning,
which made their ensemble of residual networks known as ResNets achieve a top-5 error of
3.57%[16]. Like with GoogLeNet the development of ResNet also was towards increasing
the depth of the networks to gain better results.
ResNet introduced residual connec-
tions, which were introduced to prevent gradients from vanishing during back propagation.

Residual connections are skip-connections allowing the output from previous layers to be
directly combined with outputs from future layers via identity mapping. The study proves
that residual connections simply and elegantly allow for deeper network architectures while
alleviating the problem of vanishing gradients.
Large-scale Video Classication with Convolutional Neural Networks
In 2014 a combined team of researchers from Stanford and Google decided to investigate
how to extend the usage of CNNs into video classication. The CNNs were tested on a
new dataset of 1 million YouTube videos belonging to 487 classes. The study provides
multiple approaches for extending CNNs to take the time domain into account. Their
best spatiotemporal networks display signicant performance improvements compared to
strong feature-based baselines (55.3% to 63.9%), but only a small improvement compared
to single-frame models (59.3% to 60.9%). Also, the study shows that the features learned
by their model in one data set are generic enough to be reused in another video classication
tasks, in this case, the UCF-101 [46] Activity Recognition dataset.
Beyond Short Snippets: Deep Networks for Video Classication
This study examined dierent ways of incorporating CNN architectures for use in video
classication [62]. The various architectures presented in the paper all uses a convolu-
tional base of either an AlexNet or a GoogLeNet architecture to extract relevant feature
information from the frames of the videos. To reduce training time, they used an AlexNet
and a GoogLeNet pretrained on ImageNet, and they then ne-tuned the networks on the
Sports-1M videos dataset. In all experiments, the GoogLeNet architecture proved best.
The features extracted from the CNNs were employed in various ways: some approaches
combined the features using pooling layers, another method combined the features in a
more sophisticated sequence processing architecture such as LSTM, and lastly, they tried
to capture motion information from each frame by incorporating optical ow in all the
approaches. The approach of employing LSTMs on both image frames and optical ow
yielded the highest published performance measure for the Sports-1M benchmark. How-
ever, they also conclude that using optical ow is not always helpful.
YouTube-8M: A Large-Scale Video Classication Benchmark
This recent paper, introduced YouTube-8M, the largest multi-label video classication
dataset, composed of approximately 8 million videos [1]. In contrast to other video datasets,
all videos have been preprocessed by extracting visual and audio features. The features
were extracted both at the video-level as well as the frame- and segment-level (at 1-second
resolution). All the visual features were extracted using Inception-V3 [53] image annota-
tion model, trained on ImageNet. The audio features were extracted using a VGG-inspired
acoustic model [18] on a preliminary version of YouTube-8M. Both the visual and audio

Attention-driven brain processing
Correlated Components of Ongoing EEG Point to Emotionally Laden Attention
 A Possible Marker of Engagement
This study provides evidence that maximally correlated components of neural activity
measured with EEG, across subjects reects attention and emotion-modulated cortical
processing. To extract the maximally correlated components within subjects (inter-viewing
correlation, IVC) and across subjects (inter-subject correlation, ISC) the authors employ
correlated component analysis. The correlated component analysis was applied on EEG
signals from 20 subjects watching short lm clips. The authors argue that by using EEG to
measure attention, it might be possible to shed light on the time scale of which engagement
is regulated in human subjects.
EEG in the classroom: Synchronised neural recordings during video presenta-
tion
The team behind this study explores if stimulus-evoked neural response data, known to
be modulated by attention, can be tracked in groups of students with simultaneous EEG
acquisition [38].
The experiments were conducted on 42 female subjects watching two
dierent movie clips. The authors show that it is possible to robustly track attention using
commercial-grade wireless EEG devices in a classroom setting. Furthermore, they report
that the recorded attention signal is correlated with the average luminance dierence in
the movies.

Using goal-driven deep learning models to understand sensory cortex
This review covers the development development caused by the introduction of deep neural
networks in computational neuroscience [60].
The review focuses on how deep CNNs
optimized for a given classication task e.g. ImageNet, can produce quantitatively accurate
computational models of the neural sensory processing. They provide evidence that the
rst hidden layers of CNNs contain a Gabor-wavelet-like activation pattern, which provides
eective models of neural responses in V1-V3. Moreover, they show that the layers in the
middle of the network resemble the activity of V4 and that the last hidden layers mimic

Exploring how deep neural networks form phonemic categories
The authors of this study examined how phonemic categories are formed in a deep neural
network trained for phoneme recognition [35]. The study presents a deep network to classify
phonemes as one of the 40 English phonemes or silence. The study proves that the deep
neural network is capable of normalizing the variability in the speech by forming separate
representations for dierent acoustic manifestations of phonetic features. Moreover, the
study shows that the phonetic features learned by the network are organized in a similar
way as the cortical representation of speech in higher level auditory areas of the human
brain.
Deep Neural Networks in Computational Neuroscience
This article presents a review of deep neural networks in computational neuroscience [26].
The article present some of the studies where deep neural networks successfully was use
to explain brain processes and compare these results with results obtained with shallower
models. Furthermore, they discusses some of the concerns associated with deep neural
networks in computational neuroscience. The rst concern is that the deep neural networks
are to complex to understand, so that we might not be able to correctly interpret the
proccess of the networks. Interestingly, the second concern about deep neural networks in
computer neureoscience, is that they might be too simple to capture the biological processes
in the brain. The authors concludes, that experimental neuroscientists in the future will
have to collaborate more closely with machine learning researchers to understand how the
brain works.

to divide the input space into distinct regions by decision boundaries or decision surfaces.

labels or even better posterior probabilities that lie in the range [0, 1] we nd it useful

The adjustability is the key to the success of the neural networks. It took many years to

neural networks, known as the perceptron algorithm [40] from 1957, primarily used for
binary classication problems, used a rather similar approach as explained above. Instead
of target values between zero and one, the perceptron used a step-function such that target
values were set to either +1 or −1. The perceptron algorithm works quite well, but it suers

schemes. It was rst in 1985 [42], when the idea of using dierentiable activation functions
in collaboration with error propagation or back propagation, that neural networks became
much more applicable. The dierentiable activation functions simply makes it possible
to update the weights of the network by allowing the error to propagate back through
the network, something that is impossible when using an undierentiable step-function as
activation function.

could introduce undesirable zig-zagging dynamics. However, since the gradients are added
up across a mini-batch of data, the nal update for the weights can have variable signs,
somewhat mitigating this issue. Therefore, this is an inconvenience, but it has less severe
consequences compared to the saturated activation problem above [22].

performing binary classication tasks. In this case, the outputs of the logistic sigmoid can

Local connectivity
Local connectivity builds on the idea, that when dealing with high-dimensional data such
as images, it is impractical to use separate parameters to describe the interaction between
each input neuron and each output neuron. Instead, each neuron in a hidden layer will
be connected only to a small region in its input layer. CNNs are therefore said to have
sparse interactions/connectivity, in contrast to full connectivity. The small region, known
as the receptive eld of the neuron or the lter size, is typically a square with dimensions
[s × s × d], where s is an odd number and d is the depth of the input. Note, that the
connections are local only in width and height, but always cover the entire depth of the
input volume. For the sake of simplicity, the depth dimension is sometimes omitted.

3.3.2
Common operations in CNNs
The next sections will elaborate on some of the most common operations used in CNNs,

Pooling
Pooling is a common operation that periodically is inserted in-between successive convo-
lution layers. As already explained the role of the convolutional layer is to detect local
features from the previous layer. Pooling layers can then be thought of as a layer which
role is to merge semantically similar features into one. To see this, note that the spatial
location of the features forming a motif might vary. By coarse-graining, the position of
each feature the motif can reliably be detected [31]. Also, pooling can be thought of as a
kind of downsampling. A lter, covering only a small area of the layer, is used to extract
one representative value for that area, it then moves on to a new location where it performs
the same computation. Note, that in contrast to the lters used in convolutional layers, a
pooling lter is swept across each depth layer and not the entire depth. Just like the lters
in the convolutional layers it is possible to add dierent stride lengths, typically of two or
three depending on the size of the lter.
There are dierent kinds of pooling operations, some of the most common are max-pooling
and average-pooling. Max-pooling simply extracts the largest value in the area covered by
the lter. As the operation is straightforward, max-pooling requires very little computa-
tion since it only needs to sort the values. Back-propagation is likewise very simple, as
the gradient just needs to be routed back to the input with the largest value during the
forward pass [22]. Average-pooling is, as the name suggests, an operation that computes
the average of the values covered by the lter. Typically people prefer to use max-pooling
since it has been proven to work better in practice [22].
Besides reducing the number of parameters, the pooling operation also controls overtting
by making the CNN more invariant to small translations of the input [13]. Figure 3.6 shows
how the hidden layer is reduced by applying a pooling operation on each of the individual
layers.
Dropout
Dropout [20] is a regularization method used to prevent the network from overtting. Often
when performing classications, we may not have access to the "full picture". Consider the
case of wanting to classify an image as a cat, but for some reason, the ears of the cat are
excluded from the picture. If the classier has learned to classify cats by the shape of the
ears, it might not perform very well on this task, even though the tail, and nose of the cat
are very distinctive. One way to account for this is by omitting dierent part of the image.

Batch-normalization
In general, one should be careful when initializing the weights of neural networks.
If
initializing all values to zero, then each neuron in the network is going to compute the
same output and have the same gradients during back-propagation. Initializing with small
random numbers could cause the gradient to become very small during back-propagation
which in turn is undesirable. One problem that arises from initializing with either zero
or random weights is that the distribution of the outputs of neurons is not taken into
consideration. One way account for this is to use Xavier initialization [11] which scales the
weights of the network to obtain roughly a unit Gaussian distribution. Another approach
is to use He initialization [17] which is an initialization method specically invented to use
along with ReLU activations.
A recent approach called batch-normalization [21] sometimes batch-norm alleviates many
headaches with properly initializing neural networks. It does so, by forcing the activations
throughout a network to take on a unit Gaussian distribution at the beginning of the
training. This procedure is possible since normalization is a dierentiable operation. When
implementing batch-normalization, we often insert a batch-norm layer immediately after
hidden layers, and before non-linearities just as in gure 3.6. In practice networks that use
batch-normalization are signicantly more robust to bad initialization. Additionally, batch-
normalization can be interpreted as doing preprocessing at every layer of the network, but
integrated into the network itself in a dierentiable manner. Moreover, batch-normalization
has been shown to alleviate the need for dropout, because it of it regularizing eects [21].

3.4.1
Residual Learning
One common problem when training deeper networks is that they often tend to face what
is known as the degradation problem. Deeper models are initially able to converge, but
as the depth increases the accuracy tends to saturate, and the degrades rapidly. Counter-
intuitively this is not caused by overtting, since also the training error increases [48, 15,
16]. Residual learning builds on the idea, that if the layers in the network were capable of
learning identity mapping, then a deeper model should not have a greater training error
than its shallower counterpart.
Let H(x) be an underlying mapping to be t by only
a few stacked layers, with x as input to the rst of these layers. [16] hypothesizes that
if multiple nonlinear layers are capable of approximating complicated functions, then it
must be valid to assume that multiple nonlinear layers are capable of approximating the
residual function: H(x)−x (assuming that input and output are of the same dimensions).
Instead of expecting stacked layers to approximate H(x), it is assumed that the layers
approximate a residual function: F(x) = H(x) −x. The original function are then given
by H(x) = F(x)+x. Using this formulation, we see that if identity mappings are optimal,
then in a residual learning formulation, the network just needs to drive the weights of the
nonlinear layer F(x) towards zero.

explained in section 3.3.2, to (3.23). Following the notation from above, a building block
in a residual network is dened as

3.6.1
Attention
Attention can be thought of, as the mechanisms that control the selection of stimuli, or
task, that are immediately relevant to behavior. Individuals normally have exibility as to
how and when they attend and what they attend to [47]. However, this is not always the
case, certain external stimuli such as large, bright, or loud objects, can summon attention
in and of themselves. Other stimuli have special learned signicance, such as one's name or
the face of a family member. This kind of attentional engagement, known as exogenous or
stimulus driven, ensures that salient and potentially relevant external events do not pass
unnoticed even if they are not actively sought out. Purposeful behavior, on the other hand,
often requires attention to be directed voluntarily, or endogenously. Purposeful behavior is
therefore based on internally dened goals and desires, that can help the individual ignore
potential external distractions. When writing a master thesis or watching a movie one
purposefully directs one's attention towards the keyboard or screen, while ignoring the
noise and other distractions [10]. In natural behavior, humans can pay attention to every
given location in space, no matter what might be at this place. It can also be directed to
a feature, as when one searches for a familiar face among a crowd of people. Lastly, atten-
tion can select whole objects. In each of these cases, attention has been found to facilitate
processing of the attended location, feature, or object, as assessed at the behavioral and
neural levels [39].
The attention mechanism is thus highly exible and can be used in the manner that best

3.6.2
EEG
Standard electroencephalography (EEG) refers to non-invasive recordings of electrical ac-
tivity from the cerebral cortex on the scalp. The electrical activity originates from action
potentials, post-synaptic potentials, and chronic neuronal depolarizations in the cortex.
Postsynaptic potentials are assumed to be the primary generator of electrical activity, due
to its greater eld and long duration [41]. In the normal functioning brain, action potentials
propagate down the axon to the nerve terminal where a neurotransmitter is released. At the
post-synaptic membrane, the neurotransmitter produces a change in membrane conduc-
tance and transmembrane potential. Depending on the neurotransmitter, the signal either
has an excitatory eect, depolarization, or an inhibitory eect, hyperpolarization, on the
neuron. It is the current ow associated with excitatory and inhibitory post-synaptic po-
tentials, which create potential elds sucient to be recorded on the scalp [41]. Due to the
volume conduction eect [3], the electrical signals measured at the scalp is of much lower
amplitude (10 µV to 100 µV) than when measured intracranial (10 mV to 20 mV) using
electrocorticography (ECoG) also known as intracranial electroencephalography (iEEG).
Because of the low amplitudes in voltage, the electrodes measuring electrical signals are
made up of highly conductive materials such as gold, platinum or silver/silver-chloride.
To further ensure low impedance, one often applies conductive gel between electrodes and

The low amplitudes of brain signals make the EEG prone to suer from distortion of
electrical signals of much higher amplitudes e.g. muscle contractions, eye blinking, power
outlet or movement [47]. The artifacts are usually dealt with by ltering the recorded signal
with a high-pass lter, which removes low-frequency artifacts, such as movement artifacts.
A low-pass lter which removes high-frequency artifacts, such as electromyographic signals

When recording the EEG, the signals from two active leads, are conducted to the same

specic electrode to the average voltage recorded by all the electrodes.

5.2.1
Transfer learning
The overall goal of this thesis is to examine whether a deep neural network for analysis
of visual signals, is capable of capturing information related to the attentional mechanism
of the human brain. Given the time it would take to come up with a suitable network
architecture and then train the network, it was decided that the time could be better used
on other areas. In oppose to developing a network from scratch one could use an already
proven and successful network, that would only require retraining the network. However,
the process of retraining a plain network can be very cumbersome and time-consuming.
Modern CNNs take two to three weeks to train across multiple GPUs on ImageNet [22].
Luckily, it is common to see people release the nal version of their trained CNN model for
the benet of others. These pre-trained models can then be used for initialization when
netuning the network on new classes more relevant to the new classication task. Using
and building on pre-trained neural networks is commonly referred to as transfer learning.
When netuning networks for new classication tasks, one usually retrain the last layers
of the networks, while leaving the weights of the previous layers untouched. The reason
for doing so is that the rst layers in neural networks trained on natural images seem to
learn features similar to Gabor lters, color blobs, and edge detectors [61, 63]. First-layer
features like these tend not to be specic to a particular dataset or task, but applicable to
many datasets and tasks. Later layers of the network tend to learn features more specic
to the task it is trained on [61, 63]. In practice, people often x the weights of the vast
majority of the network and then only retrain the last two or three layers, depending on
the size of the network. Transfer learning, with retraining of the last few layers, is thus a
much faster approach to building a new classier as fewer computations are required.
As previously stated, it is common to release pretrained networks. Some deep learning
frameworks even include the most popular network models in their source code to make it
even simpler to use transfer learning. As this thesis makes use of Keras, we will limit our
focus to the networks available in Keras. Keras comes with ve popular image classication
models with weights trained on ImageNet.
• Xception [6]
• VGG16 [45]
• VGG19 [45]
• ResNet-50 [16]
• Inception V3 [53]

networks is within a range of 0.044 %, with Xception being the best performing model, with

5.4.2
Linear models for prediction of ISC
To predict the ISCs of SC and BYD the three shrinkage models, Ridge Regression, Lasso,
and Elastic Net were trained on the extracted features. The content of the videos changes
much across the course of the clips. Some scene settings frequently appear while other
scene settings only appear once. This obviously greatly inuence the prediction accuracy
on the test set. If the test set consists of frames with almost identical scene settings as
some of the frames in the training set, then we would expect a high prediction accuracy.
On the other hand, if the opposite is true, then we would expect it to inuence the pre-
diction accuracy negatively. To account for this, we decided to train each of the models
three times, with three dierent temporal locations of the test set. In other words, for
each layer, we trained three distinct Ridge regression models, three distinct Lasso models,
and three distinct Elastic Net models. The rst of the models, used the rst one-tenth
of the windowed features as test data and rest for training, the second models used the
center most one-tenth for test and the rest for training, and nally, the third model used
the last one-tenth for test and all previous data for training. Before training all features
in the three training datasets were standardized by removing the mean and scaling to unit
variance. The standardization transformations were learned on the training datasets and
were afterward applied to the test data.
5.4.3
Permutation test for assessing of ISC prediction
To assess whether the predictions made by the models were signicant, we performed per-
mutation test on the prediction to obtain a p-value. The permutation test was performed
by rst computing the absolute value of the correlation between the predicted test ISC
and the true test ISC. Then the true test ISC was permuted, and the absolute value of
the correlation between the permuted ISC and the predicted ISC was calculated. The per-
mutation step was repeated 1,000 times, and the number of times, N, that the correlation
between the predicted ISC and the permuted ISC was higher than the correlated between
the predicted ISC and the true ISC, was counted. The p-value was then computed as
follows:
p =
N + 1
1000 + 1,

Conclusion
Previous research has shown, that deep neural networks learn to form brain like features
when trained on goal-oriented tasks e.g. image classication [4, 24] or speech recognition
[35]. However, the models used in these studies have been unsuccessful to capture features
related to other areas than the sensory system [60]. The objective of this thesis was to
assess whether features from dierent layers of a pretrained ResNet-50 network could be
used to predict inter-subject correlation (ISC) [9].
By successfully retraining the softmax layer of a pretrained ResNet-50 network on new
image classes, we conrmed that the pretrained network would be able to capture the
content in the two videos "Sophie's Choice" and "Bang! You're Dead". These ndings
align with previous studies related to transfer learning [61], however, the ndings do not
conrm whether the pretrained network has learned features related to attention.
To examine if the features learned by the pretrained ResNet-50 somehow resembles at-
tention in the human mind, we extracted features from 16 ReLU activation functions
throughout the network. The features were then used to train linear shrinkage models to
predict ISC. The ISCs were acquired from subjects watching the two video clips "Sophie's
Choice" and "Bang! You're Dead" [38].
We proved that for four out of 16 layers the extracted activations facilitated the three
linear shrinkage models to predict ISCs signicantly correlated with the true ISCs related
to "Sophie's Choice" and "Bang! You're Dead". Moreover, we observed that the majority
of activations, which yielded signicant results, were obtained from layers within the rst
15 layers of the network. To rule out whether this tendency is due to chance, further
investigations with more data are, however, required.
Nevertheless, the result holds promise, that convolutional neural networks in the future
can be of value in attention models.

Future Work
Even though the results presented in this thesis shows that our current model is capable of
signicantly make prediction correlated with ISCs, much remains to be investigated. Based
on the experiences gained from working on this thesis, the following section is devoted to
discussing approaches for future work on attention models.
At present, our model is a single-frame visual CNN model, which does not take into ac-
count the corresponding audio stream of the video. It is not hard to imaging that loud
noises or the underlying background music in movies contribute to the attentional state
of the viewer. It would, therefore, most likely be benecial to the prediction of ISC to
incorporate the audio stream into the model.
Due to the extremely memory demanding sizes of features, especially from the early layers
of the network, it was necessary to limit the number of extracted features. It might not
make a huge dierence, but models with access to all features should be explored. Also, we
only used features from a subset of the layers in our prediction, and it would, therefore, be
interesting to study the performance using the remaining features. Furthermore, it would
be interesting to implement optical ow as feature in the current model, which in some
situations have proven to yield better performance in video classication tasks.
Another interesting investigation would be to use other network architectures. This study
only employed ResNet-50 but other network architectures might be more suitable. Es-
pecially, it should be investigated if Recurrent Neural Network architectures, which have
shown promising results in other time-domain settings, would assist in improving the at-
tentional models.
Lastly, future studies should also investigate attentional models in other scenarios e.g.
radio content or for usage in education. It should also be explored if it is possible to im-
plement the model in a real time setting while recording EEG from subject at the same
time.
