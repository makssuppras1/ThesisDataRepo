Abstract
In vitro models is a popular choice for examining the effects of speciﬁc vaccine formu-
lations. However, improving the in vitro/in vivo correlation is required in order to get an
indication of the effectiveness of vaccine formulations. For the in vitro studies, cell mod-
els are utilized. Intestinal epithelial-like Caco-2 cells, macrophage-like THP-1 cells and
dendritic-like MUTZ-3 cells are investigated individually and combined into co-cultures
for a closer representation of in vivo conditions. Carrier-particles were produced to de-
liver antigens and adjuvants, thus initiating and empowering an immunological response.
One promising strategy was to produce monoolein based cubosomes with dextran as a
stabilizer, encapsulating antigens and adjuvants to enhance immunization.
Caco-2 cells were investigated under several different circumstances, including morpho-
logy and tight junction integrity using TEER values. After 11-15 days growth, TEER
values were measured at around 380-390 Ω/cm2, and different morphological changes
are found depending on the surface upon which they are grown. Caco-2 cells grown on
extracellular-like collagen show high levels of homogenicity while cells grown directly in
wells displayed net ﬂuid collections. When Triton-X-100 was introduced, the tight junc-
tions were disintegrated thus destroying the monolayer.
THP-1 cells were studied by adding different immunogenic substances, including cubosomes,
showing various phenomena. The levels of cytotoxicity were strongly varying depending
on the compound added to the cells, where cubosomes show cytotoxicity up to 55%.
A co-culture of THP-1 and MUTZ-3 cells is constructed and exposed to the most prom-
ising cubosome compound with OVA and Quil-A, but remains inconclusive even after
conducting a multi-analyte ELISA test.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Vaccination is one of the most crucial medical discoveries in modern medicine1, 2. The
basic idea behind vaccines is to prevent deadly diseases before any symptoms arise, thus
reducing the risk of morbidity and mortality. At the current state most vaccines are de-
livered by intravenous injections, which requires trained medical personnel and regulated
storage facilities3. Currently, there are other delivery options have been developed for
certain vaccines4 in the form of orally ingestible vaccine solutions which holds many ad-
vantages. However, the oral route in which for example the vaccine solutions are ingested
is complicated and contains many obstacles, chemically and physically. Ideally, when
the drugs are taken orally, the drugs survive the changes in pH and the harsh enzymatic
conditions in the stomach and reach the intestine where absorption will occur5. In vitro
cell models are commonly used to to get an indication of an effect of drug formulations
before testing the formulations in animal models6, 7, 8. In vitro testing in cell models has
been used for testing the effect of different speciﬁc oral vaccines, but a generalized model
has yet to be produced where the aim is to achieve a good in vitro/in vivo correlation.
Three types of cells are used to simulate the intestinal barrier and adjacent immune cells,
Caco-2 cells, THP-1 cells and MUTZ-3 cells. Caco-2 cells are simulating the intestinal
wall, while THP-1 and MUTZ-3 cells are expected to induce an immunological response.
Cubosomes produced from glyceryl monoolein with added dextran for additional stability,
are used as the primary carriers for antigens and adjuvants. Once the cubosome precursor
is exposed to aqueous solutions, the powder is dispersed into small particles available for
phagocytosis, hoping an immunogenic response is initiated.

2.1
The gastro-intestinal tract
Vaccine formulations produced as tablet or capsule are subjected to the full entity of the
gastro-intestinal (GI) tract, and most drugs are absorbed in the small intestine. Therefore,
it is crucial to acquire basic information of the GI tract in order to comprehend the uptake
of vaccine formulations. The GI tract is a crucial system which main purpose is to digest
and absorb nutrients for the body to function properly, in which many vital organs are
involved5. The following section will provide short a description of the GI tract and the
basic function of involved organs, as shown in Figure 2.1.
2.1.1
Route of ingestion
Food and drugs are inserted in the oral cavity and moistened with enzymatic liquids (amyl-
ase) from the salivary glands, dissolving carbohydrates5. The substance is swallowed and
transported through the oesophagus to the stomachs5. In the stomach, four types of cells
work in collaboration to produce a highly hostile environment, in which bolus and other
objects are broken down. The cells primary function is to produce hydrochloric acid,
pepsinogen and mucus to protect the inner lining of the stomach. Hydrochloric acid is
a strong acid, reducing the pH levels to around 2.0 when fasting, but in the fed state pH
levels can rise to around 4-55, 10, 11. In order to control the output of stomach content,
sphincters enclose the stomach in both ends. After a certain period of time, depending
on the stomach content, the lower (pyloric) sphincter opens and shuts sporadically, lead-

2.1.2
Cellular function of small intestine
Absorbing particles, such as various nutrients and micro organisms, is mostly done in the
small intestine. Therefore, knowing the cellular function and structure is critical in order
to understand the absorption pattern and reaction.

(IEL) cells. These cells are responsible for immediately secreting cytokines when come
into contact with foreign microorganisms. Epithelial cells, M-cells, IELs and Peyer’s
patches work in collaboration to ensure proper nutrient absorption from the bolus, as well
as enabling the body ﬁghting potential pathology-inducing microorganisms.
2.2
The immune system
The immune system is an internal and external defence system utilizing cellular and bio-
chemical agents to combat harmful bacteria, viruses, fungi and parasites. Through many
years of evolution, the immune system has developed into a complex and versatile system
combating otherwise lethal pathogens. The system is mainly divided into two primary
12
==PAGE:14==
categories; the innate immune system and the adaptive immune system.
2.2.1
The innate immune system
The innate immune system is the largest and most primitive of the two. It is comprised
of physical barriers such as skin, mucus and inner lining of the GI tract, and an internal
innate cellular-powered defence system. The physical barriers prevents most pathogens
from entering the body without triggering the rest of the immune system.5, 16 However, if
the barriers are breached a barrage of chemical and cellular reactions are trigged resulting
in inﬂammation. According to Figure 2.3, the innate immune system is activated from the
time of infection and remains activated until the infection is eliminated. During this time,
different cellular responses are activated depending on the type of infection (bacteria,
virus, parasite or fungus). Among the cells in action are phagocytes such as macrophages
and neutrophils that envelops and digests foreign objects, while macrophages also simul-
taneously displaying antigens on a Major Histocompatibility Complex (MHC) molecule
at the surface17. Macrophages will also secrete cytokines alerting other immune cells of
the presence of foreign micro organisms.5 16 Other immune cells such as basophils and
mast cells are there to induce an inﬂammatory response increasing blood ﬂow to the area
of infection. All of these functions are created to serve as a generic fast-acting response/-
prevention dealing very well with minor infections and as a damage control mechanism.
However, in some cases the innate immune system is not enough to combat an ongoing
infection which is when the adaptive immune system is necessary.5 16

2.2.2
The adaptive immune system
As stated in the name, the adaptive immune system is extremely adaptive and can deal
with almost any type of foreign pathological threat. Throughout an entire lifetime, the
body develops and expands the adaptive immune system simply by including a cellular
memory mechanism that remembers and combats previously encountered infections. The
most basic goal of the adaptive immune system is killing infected cells and/or produce
antibodies that helps neutralizing pathogens.5 16

phages from the innate immune system phagocytes a foreign micro organism, an antigen
is presented on MHC molecules of the surface of macrophages or dendritic cells antigen
presenting cells (APC). An antigen is typically a toxin or other foreign substance, unique
to that foreign micro organism. It serves as an identity badge that other cells can use
to recognize speciﬁc micro organisms. These antigens are introduced to T-lymphocytes

2.3
Vaccination
The invention and distribution of vaccines is one of the most radically important moments
in modern medical history22. Among the cases of successful vaccinations is the case of
measles. Measles is a high contagious airborne virus invoking strong symptoms such as
fever, blindness, body-wide white spots (Koplik’s spots) and diarrhoea23. If left untreated,
this illness can cause high levels of morbidity and mortality for the patient, while also
increasing the likelihood of transmission to other people.23 The introduction and licensing
of the measles vaccine in 1963 produced a signiﬁcant decrease in overall cases of measles
as shown below in Figure 2.6.24
Figure 2.6: This graph shows a time line of the occurrence of measles in the United States from 1944 to
2007.24
As shown in Figure 2.6, the X-axis is a time line from 1944 to 2007, while the Y-axis is
the number of registered cases of measles in thousands. The incidence of measles dropped
radically after the introduction of a vaccine and almost eradicated after a second dose was
recommended in 1989. Measles is just an example of the extraordinary importance of
vaccines. Other successful cases of vaccination are smallpox, polio and rinderpest which
are all eradicated or underway of eradication1.
2.3.1
Vaccine requirements
The attention will now be turned towards the underlying biological mechanisms behind
vaccination and immunisation. As described earlier, T and B-cells are the main drivers
for the adaptive immune system. In the case of vaccination, the end goal is to produce
memory cells that can recognize a speciﬁc microorganism that the body has previously
encountered. In order to gain access to the T and B-cells, one will have to surpass the
physical barriers of the innate immune system and go directly to the cellular immune re-
16
==PAGE:18==
sponse. From there on, the pathway is as described earlier in Section 2.2. The surpassing
of the physical barriers is usually done by intravenous injections, but recently new deliv-
ery systems are being developed, for example orally25, 26. However, in order for a vaccine
to function properly, several essential ingredients are required; An antigen, an adjuvant
and a particulate system.
2.3.1.1
Antigens
Antigens are the primary drivers for the immunogenic response required to produce memory
cells. Optimally antigens produce a powerful immunogenic response while simultan-
eously being completely harmless. There are several different types of vaccines that have
previously been produced produced, each with a set of advantages and disadvantages2.
2.3.1.1.1
Attenuated vaccines
Attenuated vaccines make use of live micro organisms
that have been signiﬁcantly weakened so that they lose their virulent properties27. This
type of vaccine typically produces a higher degree of immunogenicity. However, when
applying attenuated micro organisms there is a risk of reactivation which can cause seri-
ous harm, especially for immunodeﬁcient individuals.28. The measles vaccine described
earlier is based on attenuated microorganisms27
2.3.1.1.2
Inactivated micro organisms
Inactivated micro organisms are basically bac-
teria or viruses that have been been destroyed through heat, chemicals, radiation or an-
tibiotics. The leftover debris of the micro organisms is then applied to the vaccine, and
is considered safer than attenuated vaccines due to the inability to reactive. Polio is an
example of vaccine based on inactivated micro organisms.27.
2.3.1.1.3
Toxoid vaccines
Toxoid vaccines are made from inactivated illness-inducing
toxins rather than a micro organisms. Examples of toxoid based vaccines are diphtheria
and tetanus27.
2.3.1.1.4
Subunit proteins
Subunit proteins are fragments of proteins that individu-
ally induces an immunological response. These vaccines are typically safer than the pre-
viously mentioned types due to the fact that it contains no live components29. Therefore,
subunit protein vaccines are a popular choice for vaccine development30 Examples of
subunit protein vaccines are Hepatitis B and malaria2, 30.

2.3.1.2
Adjuvant
An adjuvant is a pharmacological or immunological agent, enhancing or modifying the
immunological response in vaccines, mainly when using subunit antigens as they do not
by them self provide a strong immune response. Adding an adjuvant to the vaccine for-
mulation usually boosts the number of cells involved and the level of antibodies, in order
to produce a sufﬁcient response. This boost can be invoked by increasing the presentation
time of the antigen or act as an irritant which ampliﬁes the immunogenic response31 32.
Adjuvants are required when antigens alone do not produce a sufﬁcient immunological
response, as seen for subunit antigens. Some of the most recognized adjuvants are cy-
tokines (interleukins), inorganic compounds such as aluminum hydroxide or detergents
like Quil-A.31
However, there are some general concerns when using adjuvants. With their toxic cap-
abilities, adjuvants has the risk of inducing an overreaction which can produce unwanted
side effects such as fever. It is therefore critical to ﬁnd the correct amount of adjuvant that
ampliﬁes the immune response while also ensures patient safety.
2.3.1.3
Particulate system
A particulate system is critically important, and without proper delivery no immunologic
response can be generated thus preventing immunisation. M cells only take up particles
and therefore, solubilised antigen will have no uptake hence, no effect. Currently, most
vaccines utilizes intravenous injections which creates a direct route for the vaccine for-
mulation to the blood stream. From this point, the vaccines will either be processed or
transported to intraepithelial pockets on the basolateral membrane which contains a large
portion of lymphocytes, thus initiating an immunological response.
It is previously shown that prolonged exposure of vaccine formulation produce a bet-
ter immunological response25, 32. An optimal oral particulate delivery system consists
therefore of a capsule keeping the formulation safe from the harsh acidic and enzymatic
environment in the stomach, and is able to release the formulation slowly in the small
intestine.

Cubosomes
As explained in Section 2.3 a well designed particulate system is required to trigger an
immunological response. Furthermore, THP-1 cells, as mentioned in Section 2.5, require

becomes evident that an object must be constructed that fulﬁls both requirements. In this

Cubosomes are lipid nanoparticles, and structurally, cubosomes are highly twisted lipid
bilayer with two non-intersecting water channels. These channels combined gives rise to
hydrophilic and hydrophobic domains, as described in Figure 2.7a. The cubic structure

drugs, including antigens and adjuvants25. It is desirable to hold a large amount of antigen
to ensure a sufﬁcient immunological response, and thus immunization. Cubosomes have
also shown great stabilising properties, even when exposed to strong light and heat35, and
so, cubosomes have been chosen as the drug delivery delivery system for this thesis.

2.5
In vitro cell models
Knowledge about relevant cells for in vitro experimentation is critical in order to produce
a proper cellular model. In vitro experimentation is a well-known widely used method
that has many advantages. First and foremost, performing in vitro experiments is much
cheaper than in vivo human or animal trials. Conducting in vitro experiments is a great
method for identifying which formulations advances to animal trials. Secondly, repro-
ducibility and speed is strongly improved when conducting experiments with cells com-
pared to animal trials42. The fast proliferation and constant replenishment of relevant cells
makes it easy to run a long list of experiments within a relatively short amount of time.
However, cells alone are vulnerable to external factors such as temperature, atmospheric
composition and pH.
Picking the right cell lines is critical in order to perform proper experiments. As explained
in section 2.1.1, when ingesting any substance orally the GI tract is responsible for break-
ing down and absorbing the substance. Since the small intestine is often the place for drug
absorption, the logical choice would be to choose cells from this type of tissue.
In this thesis, the cells selected for experimentation is three types of cell lines: Intestinal
epithelial-like cells (Caco-2), dendritic-like cells (MUTZ-3) and macrophage-like cells
(THP-1).
2.5.1
Caco-2
Originally harvested from a patient with colonal adenocarcinoma, Caco-2 cells have been
widely used to mimic intestinal epithelium for many years43. Even though Caco-2 ori-
ginate from colon tissue, they can under the right conditions undergo spontaneous differ-
entiation expressing phenotype similar to enterocytes. Caco-2 cells express tight junction
proteins as well as many enzymes and transporters, making them ideal for mimicking
enterocytes. It was previously explained that the small intestinal barrier contains many
different cell lines5, 7, but this thesis will be limited to Caco-2 cells as the intestinal epi-
thelial barrier. Caco-2 are derived from cancerous cells, meaning that they will continue
to grow even after a complete monolayer is constructed.
Caco-2 cells are adherent meaning that they stick to surfaces using proteins such as In-
tegrins and E-cadherins43. When adhesion is initiated, the cells branch, increasing the
surface area to improve adhesion. Additionally, Caco-2 cells produce tight-junctions, pro-
teins that tightly connects adjacent Caco-2 cells with each other44, 45. Combining these
two attributes enables Caco-2 cells to adhere to surfaces and continuously grow into a
tight monolayer, sealing the apical side from the basolateral side. The product is one
large united membrane, that only allows selected molecules and particles to pass. Ad-
ditionally, Caco-2 cells express the same cylindrical morphology and enzymatic proﬁle,
similar to real intestinal epithelial cells43. However, compared to THP-1 and MUTZ-3

cells, Caco-2 cells are relatively labour-intensive and expensive due to frequent changing
of cell-culture medium. Once seeded into transwells, full growth of a conﬂuent monolayer
is time-consuming, up to 21 days depending on the study7, 46 , 47, 48.
2.5.2
THP-1
THP-1 cells are one of the two immune cells involved in the experiments. They are de-
rived from a human leukemia monocytic cell line and have been extensively used for
monocyte/macrophage simulation studies. Such studies include nutrient and drug trans-
portation, immunogenic abilities and overall function of macrophages49. THP-1 cells
have two different stages; inactivated monocytes and activated macrophages. THP-1
monocytes are cells in suspension, but when activated they attain adhering properties50.
THP-1 cells can be activated by applying chemical "danger" signals to the medium in
which they are suspended. While activated the THP-1 cells enter a phagocytotic state, in
which they uptake foreign particles. Keep in mind, that THP-1 can only devour particles,
disabling them from consuming dissolved molecules alone32. Upon phagocytosis, they
excrete cytokines and interleukins such as TNF-α and IL-1β 51, detectable in their sur-
rounding medium5. Overall, using THP-1 cells is a great choice for modelling macro-
phage activity.

MUTZ-3
In previous in vitro models, dendritic cells have been used as antigen presenting cells

Spray drier
The next step is producing the dry powder precursor by spray drying. As mentioned

This equipment is particularly useful when observing cells over longer periods of time.
One option is to do a "segmentation kinetics analysis" in which the pictures are replicated
in certain time intervals. In doing so, time-lapse videos can be created, along with a seg-
mentation analysis for each repetition. Examining the morphological data can provide a
time-dependant evidence of the cellular characteristics, and is suitable for comparing the
environmental effects on the cells.

Aim
The aim of this thesis was to develop an in vitro cellular model that mimics the small
intestinal wall, in terms of barrier properties and immunological function. Cubosomes
containing different combinations of OVA and Quil-A are created for the purpose of
delivering immunogenic substances, potentially initiating an immunological response.
Cytotoxic assays will be conducted to detect the level of cellular damage when apply-
ing immunological agents. Firstly, Caco-2 cells will be investigated in terms of integ-
rity and morphology, and next, Caco-2 cells are used for transport studies of different
cubosomes and OVA dissolved in medium. Afterwards, a morphological investigation
of THP-1 cells is conducted using ﬂuorescence microscopy and an Ocelloscope. The
THP-1 cells are exposed to medium, medium with OVA and Quil-A dissolved separately,
lipopolysaccharides (LPS), commercialized oral vaccine Dukoral R⃝, the cubosomes and
Triton-X-100. Finally, THP-1 and MUTZ-3 cells are combined and subjected to medium
and cubosomes with OVA and Quil-A to investigate whether or not the vaccine formula-
tion induces an immunological response.

The morphology of the Caco-2 cells are expected to show various phenomena, depending
on layer upon which they grow and the agent added to them. In Caco-2 transport study,

Caco-2 monolayer and into the basolateral compartment, . The monolayer is expected to

and MUTZ-3 co-cultures, the cubosome vaccine formulation with OVA and Quil-A is
expected to contain produce a strong immunological response.

cell suspension is aliquoted from the well into a new well, along with 2 mL fresh medium.

well, along with 2mL fresh medium. For all other wells, 1mL cell suspension is removed

4.1.1.1
TEER values
When Caco-2 cells are left to grow, they will produce a monolayer, in which the cells are
interconnected with tight junctions. The number of cells and the amount of tight junctions
in the monolayer determines the integrity and strength of the layer. As described Section
2.10, the TEER equipment is used to quantify the integrity of the layer in Ω/cm2, and at
350Ω/cm2 the layer is considered operational. Permeability studies have previously been

having collagen and without collagen is important. The TEER study together with the
morphological Caco-2 study is used to examine whether or not the collagen layer is a
useful tool for simulation of epithelium.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
In this thesis, an evaluation of generic oral vaccines has been performed using relevant
cells cultures to mimic the intestinal environment. Caco-2 cells were grown to emulate
the intestinal epithelial barrier, while THP-1 and MUTZ-3 were grown to induce an im-
munological reaction. Cubosomes based on monoolein and dextran as a stabiliser were
produced with various combinations of antigen and adjuvant, in order to test the effect of
the different content.
TEER values were investigated for Caco-2 cells grown with and without collagen, in
which no signiﬁcant difference was found. Morphological studies have been conducted
to show the behavioural changes of Caco-2 over time when exposed to different immuno-
genic substances. Additionally, a protein transport study was conducted by ﬁrst growing
a complete monolayer on transwells, while simultaneously monitoring TEER values. The
transport was investigated for 240 minutes and found to increase the amount of protein in
the basolateral chamber over time.
Next, a THP-1 investigation was launched to provide adequate visualization of the effects
of the immunogenic substances. CFDA staining combined with ﬂuorescence microscopy
was used to snap pictures of different phenomena occurring. LDH cytotoxicity was con-
ducted along with the ﬂuorescence pictures and ocelloscope pictures to examine the cyto-
toxic effects of the substances.
Finally a co-culture was produced including THP-1 and MUTZ-3 cells, combined in a
layer of collagen and tested for cytokines after 24 hours exposure to cubosomes and me-
dium. The cytokine measurements were conducted with an ELISA assay, but was ulti-
mately found inconclusive.
Overall, an evaluation of oral vaccines has been performed using relevant cell culture
models, but future improvements and models are required to completely validate the use-
fulness of these models.
65
==PAGE:67==
Chapter 6
Future improvements
The aim of this thesis was to evaluate oral vaccines using cellular models, but there is
room for signiﬁcant improvements with new studies and experiments. Firstly, improve-
ments are possible in the Caco-2 experiments with regards to both growth patterns and
staining. Many experiments similar to the Caco-2 studies, monolayers were grown for an
increased amount of time to achieve better differentiation. Growing the Caco-2 monolay-
ers for an extended period of time may cause alterations in the protein transport patterns
and an increase in TEER values, thus improving the integrity of the monolayer. A staining
of the tight junctions, achievable with ZO-1 staining93, can also be part of the monolayer
investigation instead of TEER alone.
In the THP-1 investigation, the opportunities with the Ocelloscope have not been fully
utilized. There was an attempt to create a segmentation kinectics analysis, but ultimately
failed due to lack of harddisk storage space and memory. An additional attempt to con-
duct a segmentation kinetics analysis is beneﬁcial to generate more data, supporting the
investigation.
Finally, a triple-co-culture containing THP-1 and MUTZ-3 cells trapped inside collagen
with Caco-2 cells atop, is a reasonable model involving all three types of cell lines. With
the triple co-culture, a new multi-analyte ELISA test would be conducted along with spe-
ciﬁc ELISA testing for TNF-α, IL-1β and IL-8.
Another option is to expand the model by adding new content to the Caco-2 barrier, such
as new cell lines. Several studies have used a combination of Caco-2, HT29 and Raji-
B cells, ultimately creating a barrier with M-cells and mucus producing goblet cells79, 7.
This would create an improved emulation of the intestinal barrier.
