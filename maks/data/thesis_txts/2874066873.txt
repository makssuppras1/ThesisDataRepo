Abstract
As part of the ongoing effort to advance the development of next­generation Building­
Integrated Photovoltaics (BIPV), this thesis focuses on the design and characterization of
visually appealing, reliable, and electrically efficient solar modules. Particular emphasis is
placed on the use of structurally colored encapsulants, which offer promising alternatives
to other coloration technologies for BIPV.
A comparative analysis of 23 colored PV modules, incorporating various pigment and
filler combinations, explores their optical and electrical behavior under realistic angular
conditions. Special attention is given to the trade­off between color stability and photo­
voltaic efficiency. The results are intended to give better insights of how to evaluate color
stability for BIPV applications. The study systematically evaluates module performance
using IV flash, IAM, Hemispherical Reflectance and BRDF measurements, enabling a
rigorous assessment of the potential of structurally colored encapsulants in future BIPV
technologies.
The measurement results and accompanying analysis in this thesis offer key insights into
the relationship between optical appearance and electrical output in structurally colored
BIPV modules. The results reveal complex interactions between scattering composition,
pigment selection, and angular color variation, providing quantitative insights into color
stability and its relationship to electrical performance. These findings inform material se­
lection and design strategies that balance visual quality with energy generation, offering
a foundation for the development of commercially viable, aesthetically integrated BIPV
facades.

Introduction
Building­integrated photovoltaics (BIPV) are photovoltaic (PV) systems designed to be­
come an integral part of a building’s climate envelope, replacing conventional materi­
als in facades, glass and roofs, while generating electricity [1]. As BIPV becomes more
widespread, aesthetic integration into architectural designs is becoming increasingly im­
portant. Stakeholders, especially architects and building owners, are demanding solu­
tions that combine high energy efficiency with visually appealing and uniform surfaces [2].
Color is a key element in this integration, however achieving stable, uniform colors across
all viewing angles is particularly challenging due to iridescence, i.e. angle­dependent color
changes.
Iridescence arises from interference phenomena in micro­ and nanoscale structures, re­
sulting in angle­sensitive color changes. This phenomenon is widespread in biological
systems, where it serves functional purposes such as camouflage, signaling, and com­
munication, as seen for example, in butterfly wings and beetle shields [3]. In these natu­
ral contexts, iridescence is evolutionarily advantageous, but in technological applications
such as colored BIPV, it poses a significant challenge. Color variation as a function of
viewing angle or sun position can lead to visual irregularities in the facade, compromising
aesthetic design requirements in architecture. In this context, color stability and uniform
visual appearance become crucial parameters. [2]
Several recent approaches have explored structural coloration in BIPV using interference
pigments or multilayer coatings, such as dielectric stacks or photonic crystals. For in­
stance, Xu et al. (2024) [4] used dielectric multilayer coating and surface texturing on
cover glass to create color. The method achieved vivid colors with minimal current loss,
however it only partially mitigated iridescence through randomized surface texturing. Fur­
thermore, the reliance on external coatings raises concerns about long­term durability due
to potential degradation from UV exposure, mechanical wear, and environmental factors
[5]. To advance in BIPV it requires meeting architectural aesthetic demands while simul­
taneously maintaining high electrical performance. Previous studies, including Erban et
al.’s “Invisible­Solar Panel” [6], have demonstrated the potential of using structural colors
based on interference pigments, which are applied to the backside of the cover layer,
thus maintaining high efficiency. However, this approach still suffers from some degree
of iridescence which can compromise color uniformity in facades. [7]
A recent (2025) comprehensive review of structural coloration technologies for BIPV crit­
ically evaluates 131 studies, spotlighting the central importance of color standardization
and spectral optimization to reconcile energy efficiency with visual design constraints [8].
The authors highlight that structural color BIPV still faces “scalability challenges, efficiency
trade­offs, and the lack of unified standards” [8]. These recent reviews emphasize the
need for interdisciplinary collaboration, robust evaluation frameworks, and adaptive de­
sign strategies in order to position structural color BIPV as a central component of sustain­
able and architecturally integrated urban energy systems. Addressing these challenges,
this study investigates an alternative approach to structural coloration based on encap­
sulation materials with different scattering particles such as glass fillers, BaSO4, and pig­
ments. By integrating these materials directly into the optical path, their particle size,
refractive index, and concentration can be tailored to enhance diffuse reflection, thereby
mitigating iridescence and improving color stability under varying viewing angles [7].

A different method has recently been proposed in relation to the 41st European Photo­
voltaic Solar Energy Conference [9], where the use of micro­structured optical interlayers
to achieve structural coloration in PV modules was explored. In contrast to encapsulation­
based scattering strategies, this approach relies on interference and diffraction effects en­
gineered through sub­wavelength patterns within dedicated interlayers. While this tech­
nique can generate vivid and highly tunable colors, it raises questions regarding scalabil­
ity, long­term durability, and integration into established lamination processes. Compared
to such simulation­driven, structurally engineered designs, the present work emphasizes
material­based modifications that are more readily adaptable to industrial manufacturing.
By experimentally quantifying their impact on both optical appearance and electrical per­
formance, this study aims to provide a performance­oriented pathway towards reliable
and color­stable BIPV modules.
Recent advances in structural coloration have demonstrated promising strategies for achiev­

as performance ultimately depends on the intricate interplay between optical design and

This motivates the present study: To enhance the evaluation framework for BIPV mod­

thereby bridging the gap between aesthetic requirements and technical performance. As
highlighted in the 2019 IEA report on colored BIPV, the rapid growth of the BIPV market
is increasingly shaped by stricter building energy performance regulations and the push
toward nearly zero­energy buildings [11]. The report stresses that while a wide range of
technical solutions for colored modules already exists, challenges remain in balancing
aesthetics with efficiency and cost competitiveness. In particular, it identifies the opti­
mization of color appearance without compromising electrical output as a central barrier
for broader adoption. This underscores the need for systematic evaluation frameworks,
such as the one pursued in this study, to align material innovations with both architec­
tural acceptance and energy performance. Unlike many existing studies that focus on a
single parameter, such as optical transmittance, reflectance, or electrical efficiency [8],
this project undertakes an evaluation across multiple dimensions, including two different
reflectance measurement approaches, IV (Current vs Voltage) performance, IAM (Inci­
dent angle modifier) response, and angular color stability. By introducing a conceptual
framework for analyzing angular color variation, applied directly to the development of
structurally colored solar modules, this study provides an integrated perspective on how
structural coloration strategies affect both visual and electrical outcomes. By consolidat­
ing these aspects into a unified framework, the project contributes new insights into how
encapsulant design can be optimized to reduce iridescence and maintain high electrical
output, offering a practical route toward more visually consistent and architecturally in­
tegrated photovoltaic systems. The following section details the methodology used to
implement this integrated evaluation framework.

1.1
Project Scope and Methodology
The central objective of this thesis is to develop a practical, experimentally based method
for evaluating and improving color stability in PV modules with varying scattering char­
acteristics, using both non­pigmented and pigmented encapsulants. To achieve this, a
set of different encapsulants was first provided, from which a series of PV modules were
fabricated in­house. This process resulted in 23 modules with variations in filler con­
centration, pigment type, and particle size in the encapsulant, which were subsequently
evaluated for their color stability and optical performance. For the pigmented encapsu­
lant, the color is not applied externally, but is achieved through bulk dispersion within the
encapsulant. This internal coloring approach introduces new design possibilities, but also
presents unique challenges in how light is scattered and perceived at varying angles.
To address this, a series of optical and electrical measurements were performed, a total
of 4 experiments:
• IV (current voltage) flash measurements
• Total hemispherical reflectance measurements
• IAM (incident angle modifier) measurements
• Spectral BRDF (bidirectional reflectance distribution function) measurements, across
multiple incident and reflection angles
The dataset includes several modules with coextruded encapsulants, consisting of two
layers: a colored layer and a scattering layer. The optical response of these modules can
vary depending on which layer is oriented toward the light source. This became relevant
due to a confusion in manufacturing labeling, and a dedicated analysis was used to identify
layer orientation based on scattering signatures and electrical response.
Since color stability is not a single property, but the result of several interacting parame­
ters, a structured framework was developed to interpret and compare the results. Unlike
previous studies that focus primarily on optical transmittance and IV performance without
addressing visual color stability or perceptual metrics [12], this method integrates both
spectral and directional color data into a unified evaluation framework tailored for struc­
tural coloration in BIPV. It is a step­by­step approach to evaluate the angular color stability
of structurally colored solar modules. The method enables comparison both within one
color group (e.g. different blue formulations with varying pigment sizes) and across dif­
ferent groups (e.g. brown vs. green modules). It is designed to be adaptable: although
this thesis focuses on structural colors from pigmented encapsulants, the approach can
be extended to other color­producing technologies.
The outcome is not a single metric but a set of visual and analytical tools: IV curves
for electrical performance, CIELAB/CIELCH plots for chromatic variation, color stability
metrics, angle­resolved reflectance data, and cross­experiment comparisons. Together,
these guide material development. One of the key objectives is to identify the most in­
sightful types of analysis and the most effective ways to visualize the data; which aspects
of a measurement are most meaningful, and how these can be categorized. While cross­
comparisons between modules were necessary to development the method, the ultimate
goal is that it should be possible to evaluate a single module using the framework to deter­
mine how stable and visually consistent it is under realistic lighting and viewing conditions.

2.1.1
Scattering Phenomena
Scattering occurs when the path of light is altered by irregularities or particles within a
material. In PV modules, scattering plays a key role in controlling light distribution into
the cells and in minimizing reflection losses. Figure 2.2 illustrates incoming light at an
incident angle, showing its specular reflection (mirror­like) as well as diffuse reflection (in
dark orange) spread around the normal direction. The backward reflection is indicated in
blue, representing light reflected at angles below the incident beam. The portion of light
that is not reflected, indicated by the orange circles in the figure, is transmitted into the
material, where it undergoes subsurface scattering.
Figure 2.2: Schematic 2D depiction of the physical principles of light scattering at a ma­
terial surface [14]
The strength and direction of the scattering for PV modules strongly depend on the particle
size, density, and the difference in refractive index between the particles and the matrix.
In practice, these properties can be quantified through spectrally resolved reflectance
measurements, where the reflectance distribution as a function of angle and wavelength
reveals the degree of diffuse vs. specular reflection.
2.2
Color Theory and Perception
Color perception is a complex psycho­physical process in which the sensory cells of the
eye and the visual cortex of the brain work together to interpret electromagnetic radia­
tion as color. The human eye is particularly sensitive to light in the wavelength range
380–780 nm, which is called the visible spectrum. But colors are not only physically mea­
surable, they are also experienced in context. [15] Human color perception is affected by
ambient light, background colors, contrast, adaptation, and psychological factors. Conse­
quently, two spectrally different colors can be experienced as identical under one illumina­
tion, but appear different under another. [16] Therefore, it is important to use standardized
methods and color spaces to be able to quantify and analyze colors objectively, especially
in technical applications such as BIPV, where stable and predictable color rendering is es­
sential.

2.3
Structural colors and iridescence
Color in materials can arise in two fundamentally different ways: via pigments or via struc­
ture. Pigmented colors result from the absorption and selective reflection of certain wave­
lengths of light. They are relatively stable over angle and are widely used in paints, plastics
and traditional surface treatments. [7]
Structural colors, on the other hand, arise from interactions between light and micro­ or
nanostructures in the material. This can be in the form of interference, diffraction or scat­
tering. These colors are known, among others, from butterfly wings, peacock feathers
and oil on water, and they have the special property that their color impression changes
with the viewing angle, a phenomenon called iridescence.
In technical contexts such as BIPV, structural colors are used, for example, by adding par­
ticles with high refractive contrast (such as glass or BaSO4) to encapsulation materials.
These particles can create structural colors through scattering and microscopic interfer­
ence effects only when they fulfill the quarter­wavelength condition and exhibit sufficient
scattering order. This occurs when the particle size is comparable to the wavelength of
visible light (approximately 400­700 nm) and the particles are arranged with some degree
of spatial order, creating constructive interference patterns that selectively reflect specific
wavelengths without the use of traditional color pigments. [7]
The advantage of structural colors in BIPV is that they potentially allow higher light trans­
mission and thus lower energy loss. The disadvantage, however, is the angle depen­
dence, which can give a non­uniform appearance. Therefore, controlling iridescence and
color variation is a central challenge in the development of structurally colored solar mod­
ules. [9]
2.3.1
Iridescence: Origin and Visual Impact
Iridescence describes a color phenomenon where the color impression changes with the
viewing angle or light incidence. This is typically due to interference and/or dispersion in
structural materials, where different wavelengths are enhanced or suppressed depending
on geometry and optical conditions. From a study about ”Angle­dependent structural col­
ors in a nanoscale­grating photonic crystal” this phenomenon is demonstrated [21]. The
study increased the vertical observation angle from 45° to 70° under a constant horizontal
observation angle of 30°. Resulting, the peak wavelength originating from the photonic
crystal structure shifts from 620 to 542 nm, as shown in in Figure 2.5a. The color change
caused by the shift in the reflection wavelength peak, is clearly demonstrated in the chro­
maticity diagram in Figure 2.5b.
In technical applications, especially in BIPV, iridescence is undesirable. When a facade
changes color with the position of the sun or the viewing angle, it can lead to a non­uniform
and ”living” appearance that is perceived as disturbing in architectural contexts. A facade
field that appears uniform in color from one angle and varied from another can undermine
the visual integration into the building. Visually, iridescence is characterized by change
in hue over different angles, variation in lightness and saturation (chroma). As well as
gradients and color shifts in the reflection.
These effects are quantitatively measured by, for example, color distances in CIELAB or
CIELCH space, changes in BRDF profiles, and the distribution of specular points. In this
thesis, these tools are used to characterize and compare the degree of iridescence in
modules with different pigment formulations and structures. The goal is not to eliminate
iridescence completely, but to achieve a controlled and visually acceptable color variation
that can be harmoniously integrated into an architectural context.

2.3.2
Relevance in BIPV and Architectural Design
Unlike traditional solar cells, where efficiency and energy production are primary goals,
BIPV also needs to be visually integrated into buildings. Architects and developers often
require uniform color, low reflectance, and a stable appearance that does not change
significantly during the day or year.
Color variation with angle can lead to unwanted color shifts, which undermines the de­
sign intent and makes the facade inhomogeneous. A facade that appears bluish in the
morning, purple in the middle of the day, and gray in the afternoon can be perceived as
technically defective or aesthetically disturbing. Therefore, color stability over angle and
time is an important quality criterion in BIPV projects.
This thesis therefore seeks to contribute with methods to predict and control color varia­
tions in PV modules, for building integrated applications. This is a prerequisite for wider
acceptance of BIPV in architecture, where functionality and aesthetics must go hand in
hand.

3
Modules and materials
The evaluation of optical and electrical performance in Building­Integrated Photovoltaics
(BIPV) requires controlled, reproducible conditions that allow specific design parameters
to be isolated and studied. To achieve this, the study was conducted on purpose­built pho­
tovoltaic test modules, designed to replicate the structure of commercial BIPV products
while enabling systematic variation of material properties.
These test modules incorporate identical photovoltaic cells and glass substrates but dif­
fer in encapsulation layers with pigments, fillers, and surface treatments. This controlled
variation makes it possible to directly assess the influence of each material parameter
on angular­dependent performance, color stability, and overall optical behavior. By main­
taining all other module components constant, observed differences can be attributed with
high confidence to the intended material modification.
The use of dedicated PV test modules is essential for bridging laboratory­based optical
measurements and real­world facade applications. It ensures that the measurement data
reflects the complex optical interactions present in complete laminated PV devices, includ­
ing the combined effects of glass, encapsulant, and photovoltaic cell. This approach also
enables the evaluation of trade­offs between color appearance, light transmission, and
electrical output. Which all of which are critical for optimizing the design of architecturally
integrated PV systems.
3.1
Test module construction
The investigated solar cell modules are constructed as mini solar modules with a classic
layered structure, consisting of a glass front, encapsulation material, a solar cell, another
layer of encapsulation and a back sheet as demostrated in 3.1. Each of these layers has
a specific function and collectively contributes to the electrical and optical performance of
the modules. [22]

Glass layer
The front consists of tempered glass, which protects the active cell against mechanical
and weather influences. The glass also functions as an optical window, and its trans­
mission characteristics are therefore crucial for the amount of light reaching the cell. In
addition to bulk transmission, the micro­structure and surface texture of the glass influ­
ence how light is scattered or reflected before entering the module, thereby affecting both
optical losses and angular color stability. In this project, all modules are produced with
identical front glass to isolate the effect of variations in the encapsulation layer.
Encapsulation material (TPO/POE)
The primary encapsulation material between glass and cell is a thermoplastic polyolefin
(TPO), while the encapsulation material on the back of the cell is a layer of polyolefin
elastomer (POE). The encapsulation ensures electrical insulation, mechanical stability,
and protects the cell against moisture and UV (Ultraviolet) radiation [24]. While in this
study, the front encapsulant is provided by Lenzing Group Company and specifically en­
gineered with varying optical properties such as pigmentation, filler type, and scattering
behavior. This controlled variation in the front encapsulant composition forms the basis
for assessing how different material formulations influence light transmission, reflection,
and angular color stability in the manufactured PV test modules. Colored encapsulation
layers are achieved by adding pigments (e.g. brown or green) and/or optical fillers such
as barium sulfate (BaSO4) and glass particles with a defined refractive index and particle
size. These additions change not only the color but also the optical interaction between
the layers via scattering and interference, which is of great importance for the angular
dependence of the color [10]. Several of the modules are manufactured as coextruded
systems with two functional layers: A top layer with different scattering properties and a
back layer with colored additives, which enables differentiated optical functionality.
Solar cell and backsheet
The active component in all test modules is a monocrystalline silicon solar cell with a two
busbar front metallization and an aluminium back surface field architecture. A represen­
tative cell type used in this work is Motech XS156B2. The cells include a standard anti
reflective coating on the front surface. The cell is located between the two encapsulation
layers, meaning that incident light first undergoes reflection, absorption, and scattering in
the glass and encapsulant before reaching the cell. Upon arrival, additional optical inter­
actions occur at the cell surface due to its anti­reflective coating, textured morphology, and
metallization pattern. The backsheet is black, which minimizes rear­side light reflection
and prevents backscattered light from influencing the measured front­side reflectance and
color. While this can slightly reduce optical efficiency compared to a reflective backsheet,
it ensures that the measured spectra originate from the front­side optical stack alone. [22]
Variations in construction
The modules in this analysis differ in the formulation of the top encapsulation layer. Ex­
periments have been carried out with different types of pigments (brown, green, blue),
amounts of BaSO4 or glass fillers, as well as particle size and refractive index. This al­
lows a systematic investigation of the relationship between the optical properties of the
encapsulation material and the electrical performance and color stability of the modules.

Analysis Strategy
The proposed methodology for evaluating angular color stability in Building­Integrated
Photovoltaics (BIPV) was developed through an extensive, comparative study of 23 pho­
tovoltaic modules. The result is a guided evaluation framework from four experiments,
visualized as 5 rings, which captures the analysis in an intuitive structure.
The framework is rooted in three levels of comparative analysis:
• Within­module analysis: How much a single module’s color appearance (ΔE, hue
angle, chroma, etc.) changes over incident angles from 0◦to 60◦— i.e., how stable
or iridescent the color is across angles.
• Between­module analysis: Comparison of multiple modules within the same color
group (e.g., different blue formulations), to understand how changes in parameters
like particle size or filler concentration influence angular behavior.
• Between­group analysis: Identification of general trends across all module types,
helping define which metrics are the most insightful and which visualization methods
best reveal angular color dynamics.
This multi layered approach informed the selection of key metrics and led to the develop­
ment of a five ring evaluation framework, for assessing BIPV module performance.
Why this framework?
Color stability in structurally colored PV modules is inherently multidimensional. A single
value rarely captures the full performance, and different architectural applications may
prioritize different criteria: minimal ΔE shifts, flat incident­angle­modifier (IAM) curves,
low hue variation, or reduced specular glare.
Rather than hiding this complexity behind a single score or misleading precision, the
framework separates evaluation into five distinct yet complementary ”rings” or perfor­
mance domains illustrated in Figure 4.1. Each ring highlights a specific aspect of visual
or electrical performance, providing a nuanced view of trade­offs and strengths.
The framework supports two primary use cases:
• Comparative evaluation — to determine which encapsulant formulation performs
best across the five domains;
• Independent module testing — to evaluate the angular stability of a single module
using the same set of metrics.
Each ring relies on data generated through the optical characterization chain (BRDF,
spectral interpolation, CIE color conversion, etc.) and metrics such as maximum BRDF,
ΔE consistency, hue trajectory, chromaticity shifts, IAM slope, and lightness filtering. This
framework is designed to provide an evaluation from core color stability to electrical per­
formance trade­offs.
Core Research Question Addressed: How can the color stability of BIPV modules be
systematically evaluated through methods that capture both optical appearance and en­
ergy performance?

5
Methodology
The characterization of colored modules with structural colors requires a multi­faceted
experimental approach to assess both optical and electrical performance. This methodol­
ogy chapter presents four complementary measurement techniques designed to provide
a complete understanding of how different material compositions affect module behavior
under varying conditions.
The IV Flash Measurement (Section 5.1) quantifies the electrical performance and pho­
tocurrent generation efficiency of solar cells before and after encapsulation, enabling as­
sessment of how optical additives and color pigments impact electrical output. The Re­
flectance Measurement (Section 5.2) using an 8° incident beam in an integrating sphere
provides fundamental optical characterization, measuring both total and diffuse reflectance
components to understand light scattering behavior and surface optical properties. The
IAM (Incident Angle Modifier Measurement (Section 5.3) extends this analysis to angular
dependence, quantifying how electrical performance varies with incident angle. This pro­
vides insights into the module’s electrical angular stability, analyzing how consistently the
module generates current when illuminated from different directions. While IAM measure­
ments assess electrical performance stability, the fourth experiment, spectrally resolved
BRDF (Bidirectional Reflectance Distribution Function) Measurement (Section 5.4), ad­
dresses optical angular stability and potential iridescence effects by measuring how re­
flectance and color appearance change with viewing and illumination angles. BRDF offers
the most comprehensive optical analysis, mapping the complete angular distribution of
reflected light and enabling detailed color stability assessment through conversion to the
CIELAB color space. Together, these four experimental approaches provide a complete
framework for understanding the complex interplay between material composition, optical
properties, and electrical performance in colored BIPV applications, supporting the devel­
opment of materials that achieve both aesthetic integration and functional photovoltaic
performance.
5.1

To determine the electrical properties of the solar modules, IV (current–voltage charac­

The measurements were performed both before and after encapsulation of the solar cells,
allowing for the quantification of any effects of the encapsulation material on the perfor­

Understanding the optical behavior of PV modules requires separation of specular and
diffuse reflection components, as both influence visual appearance, perceived color sta­

were therefore carried out using an integrating sphere setup at normal incidence, which
provides a controlled method to quantify these components under standardized geome­

enables direct correlation of optical scattering characteristics with electrical performance
metrics such as the short­circuit current.
Measurements were performed on multiple PV modules, with three spatially distinct points

used as a calibration baseline for absolute reflectance. Two measurement modes were
applied:
• Total Reflectance: Capturing both specular and diffuse components with the spec­

• Diffuse Reflectance: Capturing only the scattered light component by placing a
light trap at the specular reflection port of the integrating sphere, thereby removing
the specular beam from the measurement.
Measurements were carried out using an integrating sphere with an internal diameter of
2 inches, coupled to a OceanOptics QE 65000 spectrometer. The light source was a
OceanOptics DH2000 deuterium­halogen lamp, delivering a fibre­coupled incident beam
to the sample through the measurement port.

5.3
Incident Angle Modifier Measurement
The Incident Angle Modifier (IAM) describes the variation in a module’s short­circuit cur­
rent (Isc) as a function of the angle of incidence. It provides a direct measure of the
optical response at oblique angles and is therefore a key parameter when evaluating col­
ored BIPV modules. In this study, IAM data are used as a proxy for angle­dependent
light transmission, complementing the optical characterization obtained from reflectance
measurements and color analysis.
The IAM results enable assessment of how encapsulation materials and optical additives
influence reflection losses and, indirectly, the perceived color stability of the modules.
Modules that maintain high IAM values over a wide range of incident angles are consid­
ered to have improved potential for consistent color appearance and seamless aesthetic
integration into BIPV facades [27].
5.3.1

a standard solar simulator, this setup has demonstrated good comparability with other IAM
measurement methods [28].

under constant irradiance conditions. The setup is illustrated in Figure 5.4. To ensure
repeatable geometry, the module center was aligned with the goniometer rotation axis so

A weighting system was implemented to account for the physical distribution of reflected

element is given by:

w(θr) = cos(θr) sin(θr)

due to the cos(θr) factor, while points at grazing incidence (θr ≈90◦) contribute less
despite having larger solid angle coverage. In practice, this avoids the unphysical situation
where the specular zone would vanish simply because sin(0) = 0.

Interpretation
This correction has two important consequences:
• The specular zone shows the highest average weight per point, because cos(θr) sin(θr)

• However, the total contribution of each zone also depends on the solid angle size.
Larger zones (e.g., backscatter, 45–90°) may integrate to significant values despite

coverage (sin θr). In this sense, the sin(θr) term directly represents the solid angle asso­

concept to a radiance­weighted form suitable for accurate BRDF­based analysis.

Color Data Processing from BRDF measurements
To evaluate angular color stability in Building­Integrated Photovoltaics (BIPV), a two­part
pipeline was developed: (1) conversion of BRDF spectral data into color coordinates in
perceptually uniform CIELAB color space, and (2) advanced statistical analysis and visu­

5.5.1
Spectral to CIELAB Conversion
BRDF spectral data were recorded for each module and viewing angle combination. Each
spectrum was interpolated to a standard 1 nm grid between 360–780 nm. The spectra
were then multiplied with the CIE 1931 2° color matching functions and a D65 illuminant
to compute XYZ tristimulus values using trapezoidal integration. These were converted
to CIELAB coordinates using standard nonlinear transformations.
Color Space Transformation Implementation
The spectral­to­color conversion was implemented using the colour-science Python li­
brary, which provides standardized colorimetric calculations:
1. Wavelength standardization: Raw spectral data interpolated to 360­780 nm range
with 1 nm resolution
2. Color matching functions: CIE 1931 2° Standard Observer color matching func­
tions applied
3. Standard illuminant: D65 illuminant (representing average daylight) used for all
calculations
4. XYZ calculation: Trapezoidal integration method applied for tristimulus value com­
putation
5. CIELAB conversion: Standard nonlinear transformations from XYZ to CIELAB
space
The conversion process followed the established colorimetric workflow:

The evaluation of the PV module performance includes a multi­dimensional approach, that
captures the complex interplay between electrical efficiency, optical behavior, and visual
stability. This chapter presents the results of a systematic analysis conducted across
five distinct analytical rings presented in Chapter 4, each addressing a specific aspect of

6.1
Ring 1 – Optical losses vs. Short­Circuit Current (Isc)
The first part of the analysis, Ring 1, quantifies optical losses in the colored PV mod­
ules and relates them to short­circuit current (Isc). Optical losses arise from reflection
at the module surface and within the encapsulation stack, reducing the photon flux that
reaches the photovoltaic cells. These losses include both specular (mirror­like) and dif­
fuse (scattered) components. In the following analysis of this section, the total and diffuse
integrated reflectance at 8◦incidence (Experiment 2) is directly compared with Isc from
flash testing (Experiment 1). The goal is to assess to what extent a single optical­loss
metric, integrated reflectance, explains variation in electrical performance.

material properties on optical losses at the incidence angle of 60◦.

Ring 5: Angular Color Stability
The final analysis address the angular color stability of the modules, a key factor for their
architectural acceptance. While ∆E, which accounts for the distance between two color
points, metrics are commonly used to quantify perceptual differences, they can overesti­
mate instability in BIPV due to glare­driven changes in lightness rather than true chromatic
shifts. To resolve this, two complementary approaches are applied: refined ∆E analyses
in CIELAB/CIELCh to maintain continuity with established methods, and a heuristic stabil­
ity formula that excludes lightness and emphasizes hue and chroma variations. Together,
these provide a balanced assessment of angular color stability and highlight systematic
differences between module types.

6.5.2
Color stability metric: Heuristic formula
Color stability is a critical parameter for Building­Integrated Photovoltaic (BIPV) modules,
as it determines both their long­term aesthetic performance and their acceptance in ar­
chitectural contexts. Traditional color stability metrics, such as ∆E, provide a general
measure of color change but fail to capture certain statistical and perceptual nuances. In
particular, the hue angle parameter is not directly incorporated in this analysis, despite its
significant contribution to color stability assessment. Moreover, conventional formulations
tend to mix lightness with chroma, despite lightness fluctuations being less relevant for
assessing actual color fidelity.
To overcome these shortcomings, a refined methodology was developed that combines

color stability to be assessed in a way that is both mathematically rigorous and intuitively
meaningful when comparing different module types.

perceptual instability. Furthermore, each parameter is weighted equally, even though hue
shifts are perceptually more disruptive than chroma changes.

1

This formulation introduces three key improvements:
1. Circular statistics for hue: ensures stable and correct handling of angular data,
eliminating artificial instabilities caused by boundary crossings. For example, a hue
distribution around 0◦is treated consistently rather than appearing artificially vari­
able.
2. Exclusion of lightness: reduces sensitivity to variations unrelated to pigment sta­
bility, focusing the metric on chroma and hue, which more directly affect perceived
color fidelity.
3. Weighted parameters: recognizes that hue instability has a stronger perceptual
impact than chroma instability. Weight factors wc and wh allow this balance to be
tuned, and were later optimized by grid search (Appendix A.3).
Together, these refinements yield a stability metric that better reflects perceptual changes
and discriminates effectively between stable and unstable designs.

non­pigmented modules (excluding an abnormally stable outlier, Module 6) was defined

Discussion
This chapter synthesizes the multi­dimensional experimental results, highlighting the in­
terplay between optical scattering, color stability, and electrical performance in BIPV mod­
ules. The discussion integrates findings across measurement methods: IV flash, hemi­

7.1
Comparison of Hemispherical Diffuse Reflectance and
Spectrally Resolved BRDF Diffuse reflectance Analysis
The comparison between hemispherical reflectance measurements (obtained using an
integrating sphere) and BRDF­based diffuse reflectance analysis reveals significant in­
sights into the characterization of BIPV module surfaces. The low correlation between
these two measurement approaches suggests fundamental differences in how they cap­
ture the scattering behavior of photovoltaic materials.
The hemispherical reflectance method measures the total light reflected into the entire
hemisphere above the sample, providing an overall assessment of the material’s re­
flectance across all angles. Using an integrating sphere, this method captures light scat­
tered in all directions (0°–90° from the surface normal) and can measure both total and
diffuse reflectance with different setups. In contrast, a spectrally resolved BRDF measure­
ment characterizes how light is reflected as a function of both direction and wavelength,
giving the intensity and color of reflected light per steradian per wavelength. BRDF analy­
sis quantifies the angular distribution of reflected light, allowing identification of the fraction
of incident light scattered into non­specular directions, such as forward scatter, backscat­
ter, and residual components.
The relationship observed between these methods suggests that materials with high hemi­
spherical reflectance may exhibit low diffuse reflectance if the reflected light is predom­
inantly specular, whereas materials with low hemispherical reflectance might show high
diffuse reflectance if the incident light is efficiently redistributed into non­specular direc­
tions. This relationship highlights the fundamental difference between total light collection
over all directions (hemispherical) and angular distribution analysis that measures how
light is scattered in specific directions (BRDF).

Overall, the methodology establishes a clear performance hierarchy: blue modules re­

This analysis demonstrates a methodology that accounts for the limitations of earlier ap­

7.2.2
∆E with Baseline Normalization for Lightness
The normalization of lightness emphasizes the true material color changes, as the nor­
malized values are lower while maintaining the same deviation patterns, reducing the
dominance of the lightness parameter in the overall color stability assessment. This pro­
vides a more accurate assessment of each module’s angular color stability over incident
angles for BIPV applications. It also makes it more feasible to compare across module
groups, which makes it possible to find general trends of the scattering parameters, such
as refractive index and filler amount. This kind of analysis can open up to understand
more general concepts, if the pigments are dominating or if the different colored pv mod­
ules needs different scattering parameters to obtain more color stability.
However, this approach introduces a fundamental uncertainty. Without a clear ground
truth, it is difficult to determine how much of the observed lightness variation is due to
glass glare versus genuine material color changes, making the absolute accuracy of the
normalized results challenging to validate. Therefore the analysis considered alternative
approaches, including the removal of the lightness component L* to evaluate only chro­
matic changes (e.g., using ∆C* or hue­chroma planes). However, L* contributes percep­
tually to overall color difference and is present in real facades under daylight conditions;
excluding it would understate visible appearance changes that are relevant to end users
and architects. For this reason, ∆E was computed in full L*a*b* space to capture both
chromatic and luminance shifts. Similarly, the analysis initially explored using the refer­
ence module (Module REF) as the baseline, as it is the darkest module and most of the
lightness should in theory stem from the glass, but then again the blue­tinted solar cell is
also impacting the lightness. The adopted per­module baseline approach that was used
(each module’s own 0° centroid), make a better attempt to remove bias from inherent color
offsets among materials and isolates angle­dependent variation within each module. The

7.3
Data Filtering Methodologies: Balancing Robustness and
Representativeness
The implementation of data filtering strategies was driven by the recognition that BRDF
measurements, despite their precision, are susceptible to environmental artifacts and
measurement limitations. The robotic arm’s operational constraints, combined with am­
bient lighting conditions, can introduce ”lucky hit” artifacts that skew results. Additionally,
the large dataset size (1141 data points per module) necessitates the use of mean values,
making the identification and removal of outliers critical for reliable analysis.
The filtering approach evolved through iterative refinement, beginning with basic light­
ness thresholds (15­95 L* range) and progressing to more sophisticated specular peak
detection. The selection of these thresholds was guided by systematic evaluation of how
different filtering parameters affected the positioning of mean values in LCh polar plots,
with particular attention to preventing systematic bias toward the blue region while main­
taining representation in the intended color domains.
The sensitivity of results to filtering parameters became evident during the development
process. Specular peak filtering with 2­4 standard deviation thresholds demonstrated
significant impact on data availability, with some modules losing all data at 0° incident an­
gle when overly aggressive thresholds were applied. This observation underscores the
delicate balance between data quality and representativeness that characterizes BRDF
analysis. The final filtering strategy adopted a conservative approach, prioritizing robust
and meaningful data while minimizing the introduction of systematic bias. This methodol­
ogy acknowledges that while BRDF measurements are inherently sensitive, appropriate
filtering can enhance reliability without compromising the fundamental characteristics of
the material under investigation.
The LCh polar plot visualization proved invaluable for understanding the impact of filtering
decisions on data interpretation. Figure 7.4 demonstrates how specular peak removal with
a 2D threshold reveals previously obscured angular features in the diffuse tail, highlighting
the importance of appropriate filtering for revealing underlying material behavior.

Diffuse Reflectance and Color Stability: Disentangling
Correlations
The relationship between diffuse reflectance characteristics and color stability reveals
complex, non­linear interactions from the non­pigmented modules that challenge simplis­
tic assumptions about material performance. From a theoretical perspective, one would
expect diffuse reflection to enhance color stability, since scattering light into many direc­
tions should reduce angular dependence of perceived color. However, from the compari­
son graph (Figure 7.5) between BRDF analysis diffuse reflection and color stability metric
∆E, shows that stronger scattering parameters does not necessarily yield higher color
stability. For instance, Gl2.1L20, with high refractive index, large particles and high filler
loading, exhibits greater diffuse reflectance than Gl1.5S5, yet its color stability is lower,
as reflected in the higher ∆E value.

The low direct correlation between diffuse reflectance and ∆E color stability metric repre­

than a simple trade­off, opening possibilities for optimizing both parameters simultane­

This finding challenges the assumption that high diffuse reflection is necessary for color
stability, suggesting that other material properties may play more significant roles in de­
termining angular color performance.

7.5.1
Discussion of results from the 5­ring analysis
The broader synthesis of the five­ring analysis emphasizes the trade­offs between elec­
trical performance, optical appearance, and visual stability. Encapsulation consistently
reduced short­circuit current, underscoring the fundamental tension between aesthetics
and efficiency. As expected, non­pigmented modules preserved efficiency far better than
pigment­based modifications, with brown pigments showing the strongest penalty. Yet
performance does not map neatly onto visual metrics—modules that retained current were
not always those with the most stable color. This indicates that design choices cannot be
optimized along a single axis but must balance competing priorities.
The diffuse­to­total reflectance (DTR) ratio, introduced here as a link between diffuse re­
flectance and electrical response, further illustrates this complexity. A moderate negative
correlation with Isc (R² = 0.54) suggests that scattering need not always be detrimental.
Instead, controlled diffusion may enhance visual uniformity while imposing only limited
electrical penalties, challenging the assumption that diffuse light redistribution should be
minimized in PV design.
Angular performance adds another dimension. Incident angle modifier analysis showed
clear pigment­dependent differences: brown modules exhibited relatively flat IAM slopes,
while blue and green modules degraded more strongly with angle. This implies that pig­
ment chemistry itself—not just the presence of pigmentation—drives angular response.
The application of the Martin and Ruiz model provided a useful reference, but also high­
lighted that normalized IAM curves can conceal real differences, reinforcing the need for
BIPV­specific benchmarks rather than borrowing from flat PV standards.
At the microscopic scale, BRDF zone analysis provided new insight into how particle size
and filler type influence scattering. Large particles enhanced diffuse redistribution but
retained forward scatter, whereas BaSO4 fillers reached high diffuse fractions without in­
troducing strong angular sensitivity. Interestingly, scattering in BaSO4 modules peaked
at intermediate loadings (10%) rather than maximum filler content, suggesting that over­
crowding may reduce efficiency of scattering interactions. This non­monotonic trend also
coincided with better color stability, pointing to the existence of optimal rather than maxi­
mal filler concentrations.
The color appearance maps make clear that pigmentation dominates angular variability,
with blue and especially green modules showing extreme ∆E shifts. The role of the solar
cell itself was also visible: blue­tinted cells shifted trajectories systematically toward cyan,
regardless of encapsulant. This underlines the importance of testing full modules, since
the cell can bias perceived color independently of the encapsulant design. The choice
of analysis space matters here: chromaticity diagrams provide intuitive visualization but
compress spectral information, whereas CIELAB analysis proved more sensitive to angu­
lar instabilities once filtering reduced glare contributions.

7.5.2
Limitations
The absence of a definitive ground truth for color stability assessment presents a funda­
mental challenge in BIPV characterization. The consistency of results across different pig­
ment groups and the alignment with theoretical expectations (e.g., non­pigmented mod­
ules showing highest stability) serve as internal validation. However, the methodological
choices in filtering and normalization introduce subjectivity that must be acknowledged.
Future work should establish standardized protocols and potentially develop reference
materials with known angular color stability properties to enable absolute validation of
these approaches.
The question of which measurement approach provides more accurate results becomes
particularly relevant when considering that different methods can yield substantially differ­
ent outcomes for the same material property. For instance, the hemispherical reflectance
measurements showed that the non­pigmented modules had lower diffuse reflectance
values than the pigmented modules, while BRDF measurements showed the contrary.
This discrepancy highlights that the choice of measurement method fundamentally influ­
ences the characterization of material behavior, therefore the ”accuracy” of results must
be evaluated through their consistency with theoretical expectations, cross­validation be­
tween complementary techniques, and their practical relevance to BIPV applications. This
methodological uncertainty underscores the importance of transparent reporting of mea­
surement conditions and the need for industry­wide standardization of characterization
protocols.
One methodological point is the filtering of BRDF measurement data.
Low­lightness
points and specular peaks were excluded to remove glare­related artifacts (Figure 7.4),
which otherwise exaggerated angular color shifts. The contrast between unfiltered and
filtered trajectories (Figure 6.40a vs. 6.40b) demonstrates the importance of data treat­
ment, and the heuristic metric that excluded lightness offered a complementary perspec­
tive by isolating chromatic effects. These steps improve robustness but also highlight that
methodological choices shape the interpretation of ”stability,” and care must be taken not
to over­filter and risk discarding genuine material behavior.
Finally, the study has limitations. Sample amounts within pigment groups were small, only
three modules for green and brown, restricting statistical power, and all measurements
represent laboratory snapshots rather than long­term behavior. Environmental exposure,
weathering, and durability remain open questions, especially since angular stability may
evolve differently from overall color fading. Expanding this framework to other coloration
strategies, such as interference coatings or photonic structures, would test whether the
observed trends are general to colored PV or specific to structural color strategies. Pre­
dictive modeling, possibly incorporating machine learning, could also accelerate optimiza­
tion by linking material inputs directly to optical and electrical outputs. Additionally, the
BRDF measurements were conducted under specific geometric conditions that may not
capture the full angular scattering behavior. The azimuth incident angle was fixed at φi
= 0°, limiting the analysis to zenith incident angle variations. This measurement configu­
ration captures variations in the vertical direction (how steeply light hits the surface) but
does not account for rotational variations around the surface normal. While this geomet­
ric constraint was chosen based on practical considerations, due to the substantial data
volume generated from zenith angle variations alone. Assuming zenith angle variations
dominate for BIPV applications is reasonable given typical solar geometry, but it may miss
important scattering effects in complex architectures or varying environmental conditions.
Therefore, it represents a simplification that may not fully represent the complex angular
scattering behavior encountered in real­world BIPV applications.

8
Conclusion
This thesis presents a systematic framework for the multidimensional characterization of
Building Integrated Photovoltaic (BIPV) modules, to address the core question:
How can the color stability of BIPV modules be systematically evaluated through methods
that capture both optical appearance and energy performance?
The presented methodology integrates four complementary measurement approaches:
IV flash measurements, Incident Angle Modifier (IAM) measurements, Hemispherical Re­
flectance and spectrally resolved BRDF measurements, together with visual performance
metrics for color stability. This multi­dimensional approach reveals complex interactions
between material composition, optical behavior, and electrical performance that single­
parameter analyses cannot capture.
The primary contribution lies not in identifying an ”optimal” module, but in providing the
analytical tools necessary for systematic BIPV evaluation. This work develops quantita­
tive metrics for color stability, establishes robust data filtering protocols, and introduces
visualization methods that capture angular color evolution. Together, these tools provide
a systematic basis for comparing BIPV materials, enabling performance and appearance
to be evaluated against clearly defined criteria rather than ad­hoc experimentation. Sev­
eral methodological innovations strengthen this approach: Robust BRDF data filtering,
including specular peak detection and lightness thresholding, improves reliability while
preserving angular information. Quantitative angular color stability metrics extend be­
yond traditional ∆E calculations, capturing both within angle uniformity and cumulative
angular variation. Integrated visualization techniques reveal subtle interactions between
scattering behavior, pigment composition, and incident angle effects.
From the cross­comparison of different measurements and metrics, a practical design
strategy emerges when comparing modules with and without pigments. Measurements
of non­pigmented modules are particularly insightful, as they reveal the influence of scat­
tering compositions in the encapsulant without the masking effect of broadband absorp­
tion introduced by pigments. The results suggest that optimizing scattering parameters
can increase diffuse reflectance while maintaining relative uniformity across incident an­
gles, providing a stable diffuse background that helps mitigate angular color shifts. Then
introducing pigments can selectively absorb off­peak wavelengths, enhancing color satu­
ration and suppressing residual iridescence. Together, scattering additives and pigments
play complementary roles: Scattering primarily controls the angular stability of diffuse
reflectance, while pigments provide spectral filtering for improved color stability.
The evaluation framework developed in this study addresses the fundamental gap be­
tween aesthetic demands and technical performance in BIPV applications. Evaluating
colored PV modules for building­integrated applications is a complex, multidimensional
challenge that extends far beyond empirical optimization. By combining quantitative met­
rics for color stability with electrical and optical characterization, this work demonstrates
that both energy efficiency and visual appeal can be assessed systematically, rather than
treated as competing priorities. The study establishes a foundation for BIPV technologies
that seamlessly combine energy generation with architectural integration, contributing to
advancement of sustainable urban design.
