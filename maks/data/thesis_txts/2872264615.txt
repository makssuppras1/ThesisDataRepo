Abstract
Personalised hearing­aid fittings aim to optimise user satisfaction across diverse acoustic
environments. Brightness, characterised by the amplification of higher­frequency bands
(typically ≥2 kHz), is a key determinant of perceived sound quality, yet it remains under­
explored in ecologically valid, real­world listening contexts. This thesis investigates how
the acoustic environment, listening intent, and physical location impact user preferences
for various hearing­aid brightness settings.
Twelve adults with mild to moderate hearing impairment participated in a two­week field
trial using their own Oticon hearing aids, programmed with two distinct brightness profiles
(Low and High). Each participant, blinded to their profile, alternated weekly between
the settings while conducting their usual daily activities. In­situ feedback was collected
through Ecological Momentary Assessment (EMA) using the Avicenna Research app,
which combined structured ratings by categories, open­ended responses, and GPS data
to provide a comprehensive picture of real­world listening experiences.
Quantitative analyses revealed that a combination of perceived environmental noise level,
listening intention, and the type of sound scenario influenced preferences for brightness
settings. Participants could reliably distinguish between the two profiles in daily life, with
satisfaction and perceived sound quality significantly affected by context. Mixed­effects
modelling showed main effects of brightness, noise, and intention. At the same time, a
semantic analysis of open­ended feedback revealed recurring themes, including the need
for clarity in speech communication and comfort during relaxation. Importantly, final user
preferences often reflected specific real­life challenges rather than a single “best” setting
for all situations.
This study demonstrates the feasibility and value of integrating EMA with semantic and
geospatial data to advance user­driven hearing­aid personalisation. The findings under­
score the importance of flexible, context­aware hearing­aid adjustments and support the
development of adaptive, data­driven fitting strategies that more accurately reflect users’
needs in everyday life.

Introduction
1.1
Background
Hearing aids are essential tools for individuals with hearing loss, enabling improved ac­
cess to speech and environmental sounds. Modern hearing aids are highly customizable,
offering a range of settings to accommodate diverse auditory needs and preferences.
One such parameter is the perceived ”brightness” of the sound, typically influenced by
the balance of gain applied to high and low frequencies. Adjustments to brightness can
significantly affect the clarity and comfort of sound, especially in speech­rich or complex
acoustic environments.
While clinical fitting sessions initially adjust hearing aid parameters, these do not always
reflect users’ everyday experiences. Real­world conditions are dynamic, and factors such
as the acoustic environment, user intent, and the type of listening activity significantly
shape auditory perception. As a result, users may develop different preferences for hear­
ing aid settings that better suit their real­life listening situations.
EMA has emerged as a powerful tool for collecting in­situ user feedback, capturing sub­
jective experiences in real­time and within real­life contexts. When combined with ob­
jective data such as Global Positioning System (GPS)­based environmental information
and open­ended responses, EMA allows researchers to gain deeper insights into user
preferences and the factors influencing them. This study uses EMA to investigate how
hearing­aid users experience and evaluate different brightness settings in their everyday
lives.
Despite technological advancements, a significant gap remains between hearing­aid per­
sonalisation in the clinic and the user’s real­world listening experience. Current fitting
strategies are typically static, context­agnostic, and often based on short­term feedback.
These limitations may prevent users from achieving the most comfortable and practical
settings for their actual daily life. Bridging this gap is essential for delivering user­centred
hearing aid solutions that respond flexibly to dynamic and contextual auditory demands.
This gap is particularly relevant when it comes to adjusting brightness. Users may toler­
ate or even prefer different brightness settings depending on whether they are speaking
with others, walking outdoors, commuting, or just trying to relax. A single “optimised” set­
ting may not serve all situations equally well. Therefore, capturing user experiences as
they occur, in context, is crucial for enhancing satisfaction and engagement with hearing
technology.
To illustrate the relevance of this approach, consider a typical hearing aid user navigating
multiple listening environments throughout the day. They may wake up listening to music
in a quiet kitchen, take public transport to a workplace meeting, then dine at a noisy restau­
rant in the evening. These contexts differ acoustically and socially. A single brightness
setting may not suit all these situations, and users may struggle to express their needs
in retrospect. EMA enables researchers to capture this feedback in the moment, provid­
ing a more detailed and dynamic understanding of listening preferences than traditional
methods, while reducing potential bias introduced by memory recall.

1.2
Research Motivation and Objectives
Despite advances in hearing aid technology, user satisfaction remains highly variable.
Personalisation of settings, especially brightness, can significantly impact the quality of
the listening experience. Yet, traditional clinical fitting methods may not fully capture users’
real­world needs, especially when contextual factors like environment or listening intent
vary throughout the day.
This thesis is motivated by the need to understand what drives user preferences for bright­
ness settings outside the clinic. By integrating EMA with semantic analysis and environ­
mental context data, this project aims to bridge the gap between lab­based fitting and
everyday auditory experiences. The insights gained may inform more adaptive, user­
centred hearing aid designs or algorithms, and help identify underlying patterns or themes
in the interaction between listening environment, intent, and user preference.
The main objectives of this study are:
• To investigate real­world preferences for hearing­aid brightness settings across di­
verse listening environments.
• To evaluate the role of listening intentions (e.g., focused listening, speech commu­
nication, or no active listening) in brightness preference.
• To analyse open­ended EMA feedback responses using semantic tools to identify
recurring themes related to auditory satisfaction and to evaluate how well these
themes capture the complexities of daily life.
• To link GPS­derived environmental context with listening preferences using data
integration techniques.
The use of EMA enables the collection of in­situ feedback in real­time, linking user re­
sponses to both their activity and environment. EMA reduces recall bias and improves
ecological validity by studying individuals in natural contexts. When paired with tools like
GPS tracking or open­ended feedback, it has the potential to provide highly contextualised
and personalised insights into hearing aid use.
Recent research has demonstrated the value of EMA in evaluating hearing aid features,
including noise management, listening effort, and device benefit. This thesis builds on
that growing body of work by exploring brightness preference through EMA, integrating
user­reported experience, context, and semantic interpretation.
1.3
Research Questions and Hypotheses
The overarching goal of this thesis is to explore how hearing­aid users experience and
evaluate different brightness settings in their daily lives. Building on the motivation to
personalise hearing­aid fittings better using real­world data, the study is guided by the
following research questions:
RQ1: What are the key factors driving hearing­aid brightness preferences in daily life?
RQ2: How do listening intentions moderate these preferences??
RQ3: Can environmental context (derived from GPS­based location data) predict bright­
ness preferences?
RQ4: What semantic themes emerge from open­ended EMA feedback, and how do they
relate to users’ preferences and satisfaction?

Literature Review
2.1
Brightness Perception in Hearing Aids
Brightness in hearing aids refers to the perceptual quality of sound linked to increased
high­frequency gain relative to the original fitting prescription. It significantly affects speech
intelligibility, particularly for consonants, and is often adjusted during the fine­tuning phase
of hearing aid fitting (Agnew, 1998; Bosman et al., 2021).
Users’ experiences with brightness vary widely. Some find increased high­frequency am­
plification helpful for clarifying speech, while others find it introduces harshness or distor­
tion, especially in noisy environments (Johansen et al., 2017; Narayanan et al., 2023).
Brightness is also closely linked to subjective listening comfort and clarity. Recent user­
centred studies emphasise that users’ perceptions of ”sharpness” or ”tinny” sound of­
ten stem from overemphasised high frequencies (Narayanan et al., 2023). A balance is
needed to preserve speech cues while maintaining a natural timbre (Andersson et al.,
2021).
Other studies show that complaints about excessive brightness are often context­sensitive.
For instance, brightness may help in soft­spoken conversations but be uncomfortable in
reverberant or noisy settings (Christensen et al., 2024; Glista et al., 2024). Adjusting
brightness typically involves manipulating gain above 1.5–2 kHz. Minor adjustments in
this range can significantly impact perceived timbre, especially for high­frequency conso­
nants like /s/ and /f/ (Bridges et al., 2012). Real­world studies show that user preferences
for brightness fluctuate across activities and environments (Johansen et al., 2017).
In real­world settings, preferences for brightness fluctuate based on activity and back­
ground noise. Bosman et al. (2021) found that bone­anchored hearing aid users preferred
more brightness in outdoor environments but reduced it in enclosed, noisy spaces. This
confirms that brightness preferences are dynamic and context­sensitive.
2.2
Real­World Hearing Aid Evaluation
Traditional evaluation of hearing aids in clinical environments lacks ecological validity.
Retrospective questionnaires often fail to capture real­world variability and are prone to
recall bias (Fourie et al., 2024; Galvez et al., 2012). In contrast, EMA allows users to
report experiences in real time and in their natural settings (Glista et al., 2024; Schinkel­
Bielefeld et al., 2023).
EMA provides richer, context­aware data than laboratory tests, capturing the temporal
and spatial dynamics of hearing aid use. Smartphone­based surveys and sensor data
logging are particularly valuable for understanding device performance in real­world set­
tings (Christensen et al., 2024; Schinkel­Bielefeld et al., 2023).
More recent studies have focused on combining objective acoustic measurements and
subjective EMA data. For example, Christensen et al. (2023) found that hearing aids
with DNN­based noise reduction maintained stable satisfaction across different noise lev­
els, while satisfaction with traditional noise reduction decreased as background noise
increased.
Bosman et al. (2021) also demonstrated that high­frequency gain adjustments in bone­
conduction devices were positively received when aligned with environmental logging.

2.3
Ecological Momentary Assessment (EMA) in Audiology
EMA involves repeated, real­time sampling of experiences and has been used to study
satisfaction, feature preferences, and listening effort (Shiffman et al., 2008; Timmer et
al., 2017). Its key advantage is reduced recall bias due to real­time reporting (Wu et al.,
2015).
Studies show high compliance among users, especially when surveys are context­triggered
or reasonably spaced (Jenstad et al., 2021). Challenges include participant burden and
noisy data, but adaptive survey designs and user­friendly interfaces have improved us­
ability (Xu et al., 2020).
Schinkel­Bielefeld et al. (2024) emphasises that EMA offers a more representative pic­
ture of user experiences in audiology than retrospective interviews and questionnaires.
Their findings highlight the value of EMA in capturing day­to­day preference variability
and fatigue.
Andersson et al. (2023) used EMA in combination with physiological monitoring (heart
rate) to show correlations between listening effort and environmental noise conditions.
2.4
Semantic and Behavioural Analysis of Feedback
With the addition of open­ended feedback in EMA, semantic analysis methods like Natural
Language Processing (NLP) help decode the hearing aid user’s experience (Vercammen
et al., 2023). Standard techniques include sentiment analysis, topic modelling, and named
entity recognition.
NLP methods have evolved to include sentiment­weighted topic models. Pasta et al.
(2022) demonstrated that topic modelling can identify context­dependent preference shifts,
especially in feedback referencing comfort vs. clarity.
Vercammen et al. (2023) used clustering to identify key themes in user feedback, including
speech clarity and noise management. They also observed that behavioural patterns such
as frequent program switching were associated with specific types of complaints found in
semantic clusters. Complementing this, Johansen et al. (2017) demonstrated that users
actively adjust brightness in real­time to enhance clarity and comfort.
2.5
Statistical Approaches and Methodological Insights
Linear Mixed Effects (LME) models are commonly used to handle repeated EMA data and
assess fixed and random effects (Christensen et al., 2024; Glista et al., 2024). Bayesian
modelling and clustering approaches are also used to correlate subjective preferences
with environmental and acoustic variables (Vercammen et al., 2023).
Compliance rates are often high, and EMA ratings are shown to align with controlled lab
results (Jenstad et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2015). Data preprocessing and survey design
remain essential for ensuring validity (Xu et al., 2020).
Oleson et al. (2021) provided guidelines on modelling EMA data in audiology, including
managing missing data and balancing prompt schedules.
Pontoppidan et al. (2023) introduced methods for learning user preferences from complex
listening scenarios using mixed models and personalisation heuristics.

2.6
Gaps in the Literature
Despite the growing body of EMA research, few studies focus specifically on brightness
perception and preference settings.
While many EMA studies collect environmental data (e.g., Signal­to­Noise Ratio (SNR),
sound class), they do not correlate this with spectral preferences, such as brightness, the
need for integrated multimodal analyses which combine GPS, sound features, semantic
feedback, and physiological metrics is evident (Andersson et al., 2023; Schinkel­Bielefeld
et al., 2024).
There’s also a lack of clear methods for automatically setting brightness levels, depending
on the situation and user feedback. This is an area that could be explored further in future
systems that adjust settings in real­time (Pasta et al., 2022).
Longer­term studies and adaptive survey designs are needed to understand better how
brightness preferences evolve over weeks or months, and hold promise for minimising
user fatigue and enhancing insights. Most existing research is limited to days or episodic
events, restricting generalisation (Pontoppidan et al., 2023).

Materials and Methods
3.1
Study Design Overview
The present study was conducted between January 9th and June 9th, 2025, as part of a
master’s thesis project at the DTU, in collaboration with Oticon and ERH. The study aimed
to investigate real­world preferences and satisfaction related to brightness adjustments in
hearing aid fittings, using EMA methods to capture daily listening experiences in natural
environments.
The study design followed a two­week EMA­based field trial using Oticon hearing aid
models owned by the test participants. A crossover design was implemented to allow
each participant to experience two different brightness settings: Low and High Brightness.
Each setting was assigned to one trial week, and the order was counterbalanced across
participants. Participants were asked to wear their hearing aids during daily life activities
and complete multiple EMA surveys per day via a dedicated software application installed
on their own smartphone devices.
Throughout the data collection period, participants were remotely supervised and sup­
ported via regular sms and email communication. This design enabled continuous mon­
itoring of technical issues, facilitated the quick resolution of troubleshooting needs, and
allowed for adjustments when necessary. The study workflow included participant recruit­
ment and briefing, hearing aid fitting and programming, EMA and GPS data collection,
multiple remote adjustments, final brightness preference selection, and subsequent data
processing and analysis.

Participant Recruitment and Selection
3.2.1
Recruitment Strategy
Participants for this study were primarily recruited from the existing volunteer pool main­
tained within the NOAH database at the Hearing Systems group at DTU. This internal
database includes individuals who have previously agreed to be contacted for hearing­
related research projects, typically MSc and PhD thesis studies or institutional research
collaborations. The database contains extensive participant profiles, including hearing
history, hearing aid usage, audiometric data, and relevant medical information. Impor­
tantly, all individuals within NOAH have provided informed consent to be contacted for
participation in further studies.
The recruitment procedure followed DTU’s internal guidelines for using the NOAH database,
as outlined in the Hearing Systems online Wiki platform. For participants already present
in NOAH, a recruitment log was created by adding an entry to the patient comments field,
which included the date, project description, and researcher’s name. Only individuals who
clearly expressed a willingness to remain in the database were updated accordingly and
kept in NOAH.
In addition to NOAH­based recruitment, a recruitment flyer (see Appendix B) was posted
on Oticon´s Inside online platform to recruit additional eligible candidates potentially. The
flyer outlined the purpose, eligibility criteria, and participation details of the study. Inter­
ested individuals could click a link or scan a QR code to fill in their details online, and they
would be contacted directly via email or phone for further screening and enrollment.
3.2.2
Eligibility Criteria
A total of twelve participants (four females and eight males) were enrolled in the study.
The participants’ ages ranged from 52 to 82 years, ensuring a broad representation of the
target population typically using hearing aids for age­related hearing loss. All participants
were experienced bilateral users of Oticon hearing aids and had stable audiograms within
the mild to moderate range of hearing loss.
The inclusion criteria were as follows:
• Current Oticon hearing aid users (models: Opn S, More, Intent, Real, and Ruby).
• Mild­to­moderate symmetric binaural hearing loss (up to approximately 70 dB HL in
the 2­8 kHz range).
• Have minimum hearing aid experience.
• Motivated to participate in daily­life field studies involving smartphone app usage for
two consecutive weeks, multiple times a day.
• Possess a recent audiogram (see Appendix B).
Exclusion criteria included:
• Severe or profound hearing loss.
• Major cognitive impairments or neurological conditions.
• Medical conditions contraindicating remote adjustments or study participation.
• Not being able to understand or communicate in English.
• Not being familiar or comfortable with smartphone app usage at an efficient and
reasonable level.

Part 1: Sound Description (Q1­Q3)

mended by the ITU­R BS.2399­0 report ”Methods for selecting and describing attributes
and terms in the preparation of subjective tests 2017” and operationalised via the Sense­

quality, making participant feedback more standardised and interpretable. In particular,

3.5
Additional Data Streams
In addition to self­reported survey responses collected via the EMA platform, the study
leveraged several additional data streams to provide a comprehensive and context­rich
assessment of participants’ real­world listening experiences and hearing­aid preferences.
• GPS location coordinates (through smartphone): At each EMA survey submis­
sion, participants’ smartphone GPS data were collected to record their geolocation.
This enabled the subsequent contextualization of each listening experience, allow­
ing for the analysis of environmental patterns.
• Google Places API enrichment: Each GPS coordinate was further processed us­
ing the Google Places API. This automated query enabled the classification of each
location into semantic categories (e.g., home, public transportation, restaurant, out­
door), facilitating the linking of survey data to real­world acoustic environments.
Note: There were some issues with the outputs given by the Google Places API
when crossed with pairs of latitude/longitude coordinates from the survey submis­
sions.
There appeared to be mismatches between the categories assigned by
Google for specific regions and areas in Denmark (e.g., SummerHouses in Nord­
sjælland were identified as outdoor environments rather than residential).
• Audio recordings (open­ended questions Q4 & Q6): For the two open­ended
questions in the EMA survey (Sound Scenario and Overall Sound Experience), par­
ticipants were encouraged to submit spoken (audio) or written responses. Audio
submissions were subsequently transcribed (see Section 3.6) to facilitate semantic
and sentiment analysis.
The integration of these additional data streams provided a multidimensional perspective,
linking subjective ratings and feedback to both the physical environment and the activity
context. This approach aimed to enhance the ecological validity of the findings and fa­
cilitate more detailed analyses of how listening situations, locations, and user intentions
influence hearing­aid sound quality and satisfaction in everyday life.
3.6
Data Processing Pipeline
After completion of the field trial, all data streams were systematically exported and pro­
cessed to ensure quality, consistency, and readiness for statistical and semantic analy­
ses. The key steps in the data processing pipeline were as follows:
1. Data export: Raw survey data from the Avicenna EMA platform and associated
GPS logs were exported in CSV format for further analysis. Each survey response
included a timestamp, participant identifier, survey responses, and GPS coordi­
nates. The individual datasets were identified by week number (1 or 2) and sorted by
brightness setting (high or low). They were then combined into a large master set,
which contained all the data to facilitate further analysis and extraction of results.
2. Time correction: All timestamps from GPS satellites, initially stored in UTC, were
converted to Central European Time (CET) to match the ”Record Time” of each
survey submission accurately.
3. GPS cleaning and processing:
• Only GPS points with a reported accuracy of 50 meters or better were retained
to ensure spatial reliability.

• A median smoothing filter was applied to correct for occasional location jumps

• Transcriptions were then translated from Danish to English using the GPT­4o

dataset forms the foundation for all statistical analyses described in Chapter 4.

Sentiment scores derived from open­ended responses to Q6 (see Section 3.7) were

various hearing­aid brightness settings, environmental noise levels, and listening inten­
tions, while accounting for within­subject dependencies.
Linear Mixed Effects Models (LME) for EMA Data
To appropriately model the repeated measures and nested structure of the EMA data,
where each participant contributed multiple responses under different conditions, LME
was used. LMEs allow for both fixed effects (e.g., brightness setting, perceived noise
level, listening intention) and random effects (participant­specific intercepts to account for
individual variability).
For each continuous or sentiment­related outcome (e.g., Q6 sentiment score), the LME
had the general structure:
Yij = β0 + β1Brightnessij + β2Noiseij + β3Intentij + b0i + ϵij
(3.1)
where Yij is the outcome for participant i at survey j, “Brightness” is the brightness setting
(low or high), “Noise” is the Perceived noise level category, “Intent” is the self­reported
listening intention, b0i is a random intercept for participant i, and ϵij is the residual error.

This approach allowed for robust testing of main effects and interactions while appropri­
ately accounting for the non­independence of observations within each participant.
Estimated Marginal Means and Group Comparisons
After fitting each model, Estimated Marginal Mean (EMM) for each level of categorical pre­
dictors (e.g., for low vs. high brightness or for different noise levels or listening intentions)
were computed using the emmeans package in R. EMMs represent the model­adjusted
means, providing an unbiased estimate of each group’s expected score while averaging
over other factors.
Pairwise comparisons between EMMs were conducted to determine whether, for exam­
ple, user satisfaction or sound naturalness ratings differed significantly between bright­
ness settings, across different perceived noise levels, or concerning various listening in­
tentions.
Statistical Significance and Group Comparisons
Model­based predicted marginal means and their 95% confidence intervals were esti­
mated for each experimental factor (e.g., brightness setting, perceived noise level, listen­
ing intention) using the fitted models. Pairwise comparisons between factor levels were
performed to test for statistically significant differences, with p­values below 0.05 consid­
ered significant. All model results are reported with relevant estimates, standard errors,
and confidence intervals, as appropriate.
Cross­Variable and Cluster Analyses
To further explore the interplay between the hearing­aid brightness setting, perceived
noise level, and listening intention, cross­analyses were conducted for combinations of
brightness, noise, and intent. In addition, clustering analysis and thematic extraction were
performed on open­ended responses (Q4 and Q6) using both automated and manual cod­
ing to identify recurring user themes and context patterns.

All data processing and analyses were performed in RStudio, with Python scripts used

the participant was offered the option to either restart the trial period or withdraw from the

and was therefore unable to continue participating in the study. Ocasional manual review
of incoming survey and audio data was performed to ensure completeness and reliability.
This ongoing supervision and adaptive approach ensured that participants remained en­
gaged, data integrity was preserved, and practical challenges inherent to real­world hear­

4.3
Ordinal Mixed­Effects Modelling Analyses
To analyse the influence of hearing aid brightness, environmental noise, and listening
intention on perceived sound quality, CLMM were fitted for two primary outcome variables:
Q1 (Sound Perception: Muffled, Balanced, Sharp) and Q2 (Naturalness: Natural, Slightly
Metallic, Metallic). Random intercepts were included for participant and hour of day to
account for repeated measures and diurnal variation.
CLMM were chosen for analysing Q1 and Q2, as these variables are ordinal ratings rather
than continuous scores. CLMMs enable the appropriate modelling of ordered categorical
responses with random effects for participants and time, unlike standard linear mixed
models, which are typically used for continuous outcomes.
Four nested models were tested for each outcome: (1) a baseline model with only ran­
dom effects (null model), (2) a main effects model, (3) a model adding the brightness ×
noise interaction, and (4) a model adding a further interaction with intention. Models were
compared using likelihood ratio tests.

5
Discussion
5.1
Main Findings and Interpretations
The primary objective of this thesis was to investigate how real­world factors influence
user preferences for hearing­aid brightness settings. The results consistently indicated
significant associations between brightness preferences and contextual variables such as
acoustic environment, noise levels, and listening intention. Participants preferred high­
brightness settings in noisy environments or when performing tasks that required clear
speech comprehension. Conversely, low brightness settings were chosen in quieter, more
relaxed environments or during passive listening scenarios.
The quantitative analyses demonstrated statistically significant differences in user ratings
for sound perception, naturalness, and noise level perception across brightness condi­
tions. The participants consistently rated high­brightness settings as providing more trans­
parent sound, but sometimes less natural, particularly in quieter contexts. Low brightness
settings, meanwhile, were appreciated for their comfort and natural sound quality, partic­
ularly in quiet and non­demanding listening situations.
These findings are consistent with prior research by Christensen et al. (2024) and Pasta et
al. (2022), reinforcing the notion of context­dependent preferences in hearing aid optimi­
sation. Our findings underscore the need for adaptable hearing aids that can dynamically
adjust settings according to real­world listening contexts (Pasta, 2021).
5.2
Detailed Interpretation of EMA Results
Participants reported clearer sound perception under high brightness, aligning with previ­
ous studies that found improved speech clarity at increased high­frequency gains (Bosman
et al. (2021); Glista et al. (2024)). Interestingly, however, increased brightness sometimes
compromised perceived naturalness, particularly evident in participants’ open­ended feed­
back (Appendix F). Several participants noted ”sharp” or ”tinny”/”Metallic” characteristics
in perceived sound, particularly noticeable in low­noise contexts, confirming prior con­
cerns regarding excess high­frequency amplification (Narayanan et al., 2023).
Listening intent further modulated brightness preferences significantly, with focused lis­
tening and active speech scenarios consistently favouring higher brightness. These context­
specific nuances underscore the complexity of hearing aid personalisation beyond simple
acoustic measures, a finding echoed by Vercammen et al. (2023).
5.3
Semantic Analysis of User Feedback
Semantic clustering analysis from open­ended responses (Q4 and Q6) identified clear
thematic distinctions between brightness settings. High brightness was consistently as­
sociated with improved speech intelligibility, but was criticised for harshness in calm en­
vironments. Low brightness settings were favoured for relaxed or passive listening due
to perceived naturalness and comfort, aligning with prior semantic findings from Pontop­
pidan et al. (2023) and Johansen et al. (2017).
Sentiment analysis further quantified these thematic insights, revealing higher positive
sentiment scores for high brightness in active listening contexts and slightly higher satis­
faction for low brightness in quiet contexts, consistent with recent results from Christensen
et al. (2023).

5.3.1
Q4: Semantic Themes in User­Described Sound Scenarios
The open­ended responses collected under Q4 offered rich insight into the types of sound
scenarios users commonly experience in everyday life.
When compared against the
CoSS, many of the emergent categories, such as social conversations, media listening,
and work­related scenarios, align closely with high­occurrence and high­importance sce­
narios in the CoSS matrix. Notably, categories like Home & Media Listening and Social
Interactions were frequently mentioned by participants and correspond well with CoSS
scenarios #1–#5 and #9, all of which were rated as highly important and/or frequent in the
original framework.
However, several participant responses highlighted natural or outdoor acoustic environ­
ments, such as bird sounds, rural quiet, and walking in nature, that are underrepresented
in the CoSS set. While CoSS includes a few passive listening or ambient monitoring
conditions (e.g., city walk, relaxing on the train), it does not explicitly capture the per­
ceived importance or affective significance of natural environments reported by users in
this study.
This comparison suggests that while the CoSS framework effectively captures structured
and socially relevant listening tasks, it may benefit from extension or adaptation when
applied to user­centred, ecological data collection methods such as EMA. Incorporating
contextually rich, affect­driven, and nature­related scenarios may enhance the ecological
validity of future hearing aid evaluations and personalisation frameworks.

These findings highlight the value of adaptive, context­aware brightness adjustments in
hearing aids. Semantic themes such as clarity, comfort, and sharpness suggest that
users frame their satisfaction in terms of both auditory performance and emotional tone.
A user­centred fitting strategy may outperform fixed prescriptions by optimising perceptual

Methodological Considerations
The use of EMA effectively captured real­time user experiences, substantially improving
ecological validity and reducing recall bias (Shiffman et al. (2008); Timmer et al. (2017)).
Although initially planned and intended, the integration of GPS location data via Google
Places API encountered substantial challenges due to accuracy and semantic mismatch
issues, limiting its practical applicability within this study. Future studies must address
these challenges or explore alternative geospatial contextualization methods.

RQ2: Listening intentions significantly moderated brightness preferences. Users actively
seeking clear speech comprehension consistently favoured higher brightness, whereas
passive or nonspecific listening intentions were associated with lower brightness settings.

5.7
Practical Implications for Hearing Aid Design
The findings underscore the need for adaptive hearing aids that can make context­sensitive
adjustments. Manufacturers can implement real­time adaptation algorithms informed by
user preferences and environmental contexts, potentially significantly enhancing user sat­
isfaction (Johansen et al. (2017); Pasta et al. (2022)).
5.8
Limitations
Despite many insightful outcomes, limitations included a modest sample size of recruited
participants (n = 12), which may have potentially limited the generalizability of the findings.
This was partly due to the strict recruitment criteria: brightness settings had to be adjusted
using Oticon’s Genie 2 software, which is only compatible with Oticon brand hearing aid
devices. As a result, although around 10 additional individuals expressed interest in par­
ticipating, their hearing aids, such as those from Bernafon, Widex, and Phonak, were
incompatible with the study protocol and could not be included in the study.
Furthermore, the subjective EMA responses may be influenced by personal bias, as par­
ticipants may interpret questions differently or fail to follow the study instructions consis­
tently, as can be verified through specific open­ended answers that were provided.
5.9
Suggestions for Future Research
Future studies should employ larger, more diverse samples, incorporating longer­duration
trials to observe the evolution of preference over extended periods.
Additionally, investigating other adjustable hearing aid parameters (e.g., compression,
directional microphones) alongside brightness could perhaps provide comprehensive in­
sights.
Experiment with different types of study designs that allow participants to alternate be­
tween brightness settings, enabling them to provide ecologically valid feedback from both
settings for the same listening conditions and auditory scenarios.
Incorporating physiological measures, such as heart rate variability, alongside EMA could
further enhance the understanding of interactions between auditory perception and emo­
tional response, building upon the methodology used by Andersson et al. (2023).

6
Conclusion
This thesis aimed to investigate the factors that influence user preferences for hearing­
aid brightness settings in real­world settings, employing a mixed­methods approach that
combined EMA, semantic analysis, and contextual data integration. By collecting in­situ
feedback from twelve participants over a two­week trial period, this research provides new
insights into the context­dependent and highly individualised nature of hearing­aid fitting
preferences.
The findings demonstrate that user preferences for brightness are not static but are shaped
by the complex interplay of environmental noise levels, listening intention, and the so­
cial or physical setting. Participants consistently preferred higher brightness settings in
acoustically challenging environments, particularly for activities requiring focused listen­
ing or speech comprehension. In contrast, lower brightness was chosen for comfort and
perceived naturalness in quiet, passive, or relaxed contexts. Quantitative EMA data sup­
ported these trends, revealing statistically significant differences in ratings for sound per­
ception, naturalness, and perceived noise levels. Meanwhile, semantic analysis of open­
ended responses revealed a rich tapestry of user experiences, framed by themes such
as clarity, comfort, and sharpness.
Notably, user satisfaction was found to be context­dependent, and neither brightness set­
ting universally outperformed the other. Instead, the optimal setting varied not only across
users but also within individual users, depending on their current activity and environment.
These results reinforce and extend findings from previous studies, highlighting the limi­
tations of one­size­fits­all clinical fitting strategies and the need for adaptive, user­driven
solutions in hearing aid design.
Methodologically, this study underscores the power of EMA in capturing real­life user ex­
periences with high ecological validity, while also noting the practical challenges of inte­
grating geospatial data for richer contextualization. The technical hurdles encountered
in linking GPS­derived environmental context to user feedback highlight a vital area for
methodological innovation in future work.
Despite a modest sample size driven in part by technical compatibility constraints and
strict inclusion criteria, the results align with and contribute to the growing consensus that
adaptive, context­aware hearing aids are essential for maximising user satisfaction. The
strong correspondence between participants’ final brightness choices and their qualitative
feedback further validates the value of integrating user­centred, qualitative perspectives
alongside quantitative data.
Looking ahead, future research should aim to broaden participant diversity, extend study
durations to observe the evolution of preferences over time, and investigate the interac­
tion between brightness and other hearing aid parameters. Moreover, the integration of
physiological metrics, such as heart rate variability, could deepen understanding of the re­
lationship between auditory perception, emotional response, and environmental context.
In conclusion, this thesis contributes by demonstrating both the feasibility and the value
of real­world, data­driven approaches to hearing aid personalisation. By foregrounding
the lived experiences and nuanced preferences of hearing­aid users, it paves the way
for more adaptive, responsive, and user­centric solutions, moving closer to the goal of
delivering truly individualised auditory rehabilitation in everyday life.

What Drives Preferences for Hearing-Aid Brightness Settings in the Real World? 
Who am I? 
My name is Robert Serrao, and I'm pursuing my MSc in Engineering Acoustics at DTU. 
I'm conducting my master’s thesis together with Eriksholm Research Centre & Oticon. 
The Project: 
This study investigates how auditory environments, listening intentions, and daily 
activities influence user preferences for hearing-aid brightness settings. Brightness 
settings refer to adjusting the gain of high frequencies to enhance sound experiences. 
By understanding these real-world preferences, we aim to improve the personalization 
of hearing aids. 
Who can participate? 
• Experienced Oticon hearing aid users (models: Opn S, Real, More, Intent, etc.) 
• Mild to moderate hearing loss (up to 70 dB HL at 2k-8kHz) 
• Binaural hearing loss 
• Ages 18–80 years 
What's involved? 
• Wear your own hearing aids and answer brief surveys on your smartphone after 
various daily listening experiences. 
• Study Duration: 2 weeks (14 days). Participants will complete at least 4 surveys per 
day, each taking about 3 minutes, totalling around 4 hours throughout the entire study 
period (including 1 hour for an initial briefing session at DTU).
