Summary 
Over more than 100 years of fire safety design, a predominant principle has been to mitigate the risk of fire 
spread by using passive fire separation. Thus, using separation units to mitigate risk of a fire spreading 
inside a building or between several buildings. 
As modern architecture has developed extensively over the same period to include more and larger areas 
of open space within public and commercial buildings, such as atriums, the opportunities for traditional 
passive fire separations design have been greatly challenged.  
High-pressure water mist is a new technology with a better performance than more traditional active 
systems such as sprinklers. High-pressure water mist technology provides an opportunity to rethink 
traditional ways of designing fire safe buildings. One example of this is to use a high-pressure water mist as 
active fire separation. 
The objective of this Master’s thesis is to perform a study through large-scale fire testing of the ability of a 
high-pressure water mist curtain to serve as an active fire barrier for the protection of building and human 
safety.   
Acceptance criteria have been determined for the level of critical temperatures and radiation according to 
performance requirements in Danish building regulations.  
The next objective is to determine an appropriate test scenario for large-scale fire testing, which is done by 
designing the test setup similar to applications of public and commercial building types. 
Fire testing has been carried out and results obtained of high-pressure water mist curtains’ attenuation 
effect of temperatures and radiation when acting as an active fire separation in a multi room building 
scenario, that is, a multiple room design similar to a typical apartment configuration. The test is set up with 
a fire in one room, with a door opening to an adjacent target room that is protected by an active fire 
separation using a high-pressure water mist curtain. 
The evaluation of the performance of the high-pressure water mist curtain acting as an active fire 
separation is based on the ability to attenuate temperatures and radiation through a door opening. 
The results of room and surface temperatures as well as radiation have shown that a high-pressure water 
mist curtain has sufficient attenuation effect to potentially eliminate risk of fire spread, thereby ensuring a 
safe building design. In addition, results have shown an extensive attenuation effect that can lower 
temperature and radiation levels to a point where human safety is ensured, thus providing opportunities to 
design escape routes etc. using active fire separations.  
The results have shown overall that building design with active fire separation of a high-pressure water mist 
curtain can potentially ensure a safe building design in relation to risk of fire spread and human safety.    
In addition, CFD simulations has been introduced in this report to reproduce the results of fire testing so 
that a suitable CFD model can be found moving forward for use in extended fields of application based on 
results from large scale fire tests.

Sammenfatning 
I mere end 100 år har en af de mest anvendt principper for afhjælpning af brandspredning i og imellem 
bygninger, været at inddele bygninger i brandsektioner eller celler for at afhjælpe brandsmitte. 
I samme periode har arkitektur gennemgået en voldsom udvikling med markante ændringer i 
bygningsdesign, til i dag at indeholde store åbne arealer med bl.a. atrium. Igennem denne udvikling er 
historiske præskriptive krav til inddeling af bygninger i brand sektioner og celle stærkt udfordret, specifik i 
forhold til design af sådanne adskillelser vha. traditionelle passive metoder. 
Højtryksvandtåge er en ny teknologi med en væsentlig bedre performance sammenlignet med traditionelle 
aktive teknologier som eksempelvis sprinkling. Højtryksvandtåge teknologien tilfører nye muligheder for at 
anvende en aktiv teknologi til at skabe brandsikre bygninger, herunder nye løsninger i forhold til sikring af 
bygninger mod brand. Et eksempel på en ny og forbedret løsning er anvendelse af 
højtryksvandtågegardiner som aktiv brandadskillelse for inddeling af bygninger i brandsektioner eller celler. 
Formålet med denne master opgave er at udføre et studie gennem fuldskala brandforsøg, at påvise 
funktionen af højtryksvandtågegardiner som en aktiv brandadskillelse til beskyttelse af mennesker og 
bygningssikkerhed. 
For vurdering af højtryksvandtågegardinets funktion som aktiv brandadskillelse blev der i opgaven fastsat 
nogle funktionskrav med reference til krav i det Danske bygningsreglement BR18, for at funktionen kunne 
vurderes mod anerkendte krav til brandsikkerhed.   
Næste step i opgaven var fastlæggelse af et egnet test set up for fuldskala brandforsøg med eftervisning af 
højtryksvandtågegardinets funktion, dette var nødvendigt da der ikke som for passive sektionselementer 
som eksempelvis branddøre findes en standard for prøvning af aktive sikringselementer. 
Fuldskala brandforsøg er udført i et bygningsdesign med flere rum for sikring af at forsøg og resultater, kan 
sammenlignes med moderne kompleks bygningsarkitektur. Brandforsøg er udført ved antændelse af brand 
i et rum med åben dør til et target rum, med krav om at branden ikke må skabe usikre forhold i target 
rummet. Døråbning imellem de to rum er beskyttet med et pendent monteret højtryksvandtåge gardin 
med funktion som en aktiv brandadskillelse. Måle- samt databearbejdningsudstyr er opsat for måling af 
strålingsniveauer samt rum og overflade temperaturer, for vurdering af dæmpning af strålingsintensitet og 
temperaturer. 
Evaluering af højtryksvandtågegardinets funktion som en aktiv brandadskillelse er baseret på resultater for 
dæmpning af strålingsintensitet samt temperaturer.  
Resultater fra brandforsøg viste højtryksvandtågegardinet til fulde kunne sikre funktionen for dæmpning af 
strålings intensitet samt temperatur til et sikkert niveau uden risiko for brandspredning fra brandrummet til 
target rummet.  
Resultaterne for dæmpning viste yderligere så udbredt effekt at strålings intensitet og temperaturer kan 
sinkes til et niveau hvor personsikkerhed er sikret med mulig anvendelse af target rummet som en 
evakueringsrute i bygningen.  
Resultaterne viste at højtryksvandtågegardin kan anvendes som metode for aktiv brandadskillelse og 
derved skabe et sikkert bygningsdesign i forhold til risiko for brandspredning samt personsikkerhed. 
I opgaven er introduceret CFD simulering for reproducering af resultater fra udførte fuldskala brandforsøg, 
med tanke for at sådan CFD model fremadrettet kan anvendes for validering af extended field of 
application.

1.0 Introduction 
Over more than 100 years of fire safety design, a predominant principle has been to mitigate the risk of fire 
spread by using fire separation. Thus, using separation units to mitigate the risk of a fire spreading inside a 
building or between several buildings.  
This approach is based on prescriptive building and fire regulations , for example, back to the earliest fire 
regulation in Denmark in the 1800s, which was made by the municipality of Copenhagen in 1856 in the 
aftermath of three great fires in Copenhagen (Jensen, 1856),(Sørensen, 2014).  
Today in 2018, the predominant approach is still to design fire separation units by using passive elements. 
For example, designs with closed fire doors or with fire parapets above the roof between two buildings. 
Fire separation design with active elements is still rarely seen in building designs even today.  
   
In comparison, modern architecture over the same period of more than 100 years has developed 
extensively. From buildings constructed primarily for providing simple shelter and space for industry to the 
present day, where building designs need to incorporate many more functions. These include architectural 
creativity, modern lifestyles on so on. This development in architecture has challenged the traditional way 
of planning fire safety from solely prescriptive methods. 
Modern building designs are challenging traditional ways of thinking about fire safety based on primitive 
prescriptive regulations including the highly detailed prescriptive requirements of fire separations using 
passive methods. The implementation of performance-based fire regulations in many countries has opened 
up a new approach to fire safety based on building performance rather than building design.  
Building regulations in many countries today allow performance-based fire safety building designs, for 
example, in Sweden, Denmark, Norway, Japan, Australia and New Zealand.  
Practical experience with prescriptive methods of passive fire separation shows a significant potential 
failure rate. Fire doors have up to 30 percent risk of malfunctioning (Byggestyrelsen, 2004). Such a failure 
rate results in significant risk of fire spread. 
As modern architecture develops to include more and larger areas of open space within buildings, such as 
atriums, there is a need to design safe escape routes that also allow for architectural innovation. All too 
often, when designing escape routes in compliance with traditional passive fire separation method this 
jeopardize architectural innovation for open spaces as well as for daily use of the building.  
Performance-based fire regulations have been implemented for building designs in Denmark since 2004. 
This has opened up to more freedom in architectural design but also meant that new solutions are needed 
in relation to fire safe designs. High-pressure water mist  is a new technology with a better performance 
than more traditional active systems such as sprinklers (FM Approvals, 2016). This new technology high-
pressure water mist offers new opportunities to rethink traditional ways of designing fire safe buildings. 
One example of these new possibilities is to use high-pressure water mist as an active fire barrier.  This 
Master’s thesis evaluates whether a high-pressure water mist curtain can be used as an active means of fire 
separation, thus ensuring safe performance in a building design that includes such a solution. From a 
performance-based approach, such an evaluation can be made by calculations and qualitative assessments 
based on theoretical knowledge(Sørensen, 2014), or as in this study, with a combination of theoretical 
==PAGE:12==

knowledge and an investigation of large-scale fire testing and qualitative assessment.   
The aim of this study is to implement methods of active fire separation using high-pressure water mist 
curtains as an alternative separation method to traditional passive methods. These curtains are designed 
for protecting open spaces in areas such as atriums, escape routes, hotels and hospitals, as well as for 
industrial applications with openings around conveyors and cable tunnels.    
By introducing active methods in addition to traditional passive methods of fire separation, the idea is to 
generate more flexibility in modern building designs by extending methods of fire separation.

1.1 Motivation 
 
The motivation for this Master’s thesis is to extend the options for using active fire separations with high-
pressure water mist curtains in modern architectural building designs. It can be a challenge to apply 
traditional passive fire separation in larger and more open sections of building design. For example, fire 
doors in openings that are very high or wide, or when process equipment has to pass through such 
openings. 
It is also an ambition to expand the options for adding active fire separations as a supplement to passive 
fire separations, enabling more fire separations than is possible with just with passive separation elements. 
At the same time, the aim is to add options for more fire separations in designs than is required by the 
maximum size of fire separation units according to building regulations. 
The challenges of applying traditional passive fire separations to modern architecture can be seen in public 
and commercial buildings, for  example in atriums, hospitals, hotels and theatres or in industrial 
applications such as transportation corridors, cable tunnels or areas with a high risk automatic fire doors 
malfunctioning. 
1.2 Master’s thesis - Research questions 
The aim of this Master’s thesis is to perform a study through large-scale fire testing of the ability of high-
pressure water mist curtains to serve as an active fire barrier, for the protection of building and human 
safety.   
This leads to the following research questions: 
Can a high-pressure water mist curtain mitigate the risk of fire, radiation and temperatures spreading from 
a fire compartment to an adjacent room, thus ensuring safe conditions for humans and buildings? 
In order to answer this overall question, the following research sub-questions are posed: 
• 
How can a high-pressure water mist curtain stop a fire from spreading from a fire compartment to 
an adjacent target room? 
• 
How can a high-pressure water mist curtain control radiation and temperature from a fire 
compartment in accordance with the building safety requirements of the Danish building 
regulations 2018 (BR18)?  
• 
How can a high-pressure water mist curtain control radiation and temperature from a fire 
compartment in accordance with the human safety requirements of the Danish building regulations 
2018 (BR18)? 
• 
How can an adjacent target room to a fire compartment be used as an escape route, with people 
passing by the unexposed side of a high-pressure water mist curtain as an active fire separation?

1.3 Master’s thesis - Limitation 
This study investigates two of the three main parameters in relation to meeting the performance 
requirements of a safe fire separation in accordance with Danish building regulations BR18 (Ministry of 
Transport, 2018). These three main parameters are: radiation, temperature and smoke. This study will 
examine the results of radiation and temperature from large scale fire tests, but not hazards to human 
safety from toxic smoke. An investigation of the possible effect of toxic smoke to human safety is not part 
of this study’s scope. 
The performance of the high-pressure water mist curtains in this study is compared to medium fire load 
and medium combustibility, classified as an “ordinary hazard” according to the classification of water mist 
and sprinkler systems with reference to EN12845 (Standard, 2009). 
High-pressure water mist curtains are not limited for use only in OH1 applications, but the results of this 
study is limited to a test setup similar to the application of test.     
During this study there were limitations on test equipment and logistics in relation to large scale fire 
testing, which resulted in a shortage of available water quantities during the test. Two minor pump units 
were available for the large-scale fire test with a maximum water supply capacity of 12.5 L/min. This is a 
low capacity for testing with a fire intensity comparable to the target segment of this study, and did not 
allow the investigators to increase water flow during testing if performance results showed this to be 
necessary. With such limited water quantities, it was not possible to test at equally the higher levels 
normally used in active firefighting systems only at lower levels. 
This report includes generating a FDS simulation model for use to evaluate extended field of application 
based on the results of large scale fire test in the project. A FDS model is setup for simulation of the results 
of test 0 in this project where as an FDS model adjusted for test 1-3 is to be setup in future work of this 
project. The model for test 1-3 has to include the active fire separation generated by the high-pressure 
water mist.  
The objectives considering risk of fire spread in relation to building safety conditions in accordance with 
Danish building regulation BR18 (Ministry of Transport, 2018) is limited to evaluated risk due to radiation 
and temperature. Thus, considering a risk of fire spread due to radiation from initial fire or smoke spread 
not by potential built up of combustible fumes.

1.4 Method 
In order to conduct the experimental study of this Master’s thesis, a set of acceptance criteria must first be 
established in relation to both human and building safety in accordance with Danish building regulations. 
These acceptance criteria will be used to evaluate the effect of the high-pressure water mist curtain as an 
active fire separation. 
The next objective is to determine an appropriate test scenario for large-scale fire testing, which is done by 
designing the test setup similar to applications of public and commercial types listed in chapter 1.1. Thus, 
the setup should be similar to applications classified as “ordinary hazard 1” according to VdS 3188en and 
EN12845 (OH class)(Standard, 2009). 
The test setup and procedure for testing are described with subjects for evaluating the effect of the high-
pressure water mist curtain. 
The test results from large-scale fire testing are presented, analyzed and discussed, after which a 
conclusion is presented on the findings of this study.

2.0 Danish building regulation and performance criterion. 
 
This chapter provides a study of the objectives to which the performance of the high-pressure water mist 
curtains can be compared, justifying whether such a curtain as an active fire separation barrier can ensure 
an equivalent or higher safety level than a passive fire door. 
A study of acceptance criteria in accordance with the human and building safety requirements of BR18 is 
presented first. 
This is followed by a study of design fires comparable to the application types for which the active fire 
separation examined in this study is intended. This study also aims to determine whether design fires of 
such applications are comparable with the test fire used in the large-scale fire test in this study.  
Lastly, this chapter will present a study of performance requirements for approval of fire doors used in 
passive fire separations, for evaluation and comparison of results from fire tests with the performance 
required of an approved fire door. 
2.1 Acceptance criteria in accordance with Danish building regulations 
This literature review aims to determine the basic performance requirements of a recognized method for 
designing fire separations in accordance with Danish building regulations. Thus, to set the acceptance 
criteria that an active fire separation with a high-pressure water mist curtain must fulfil in order for such a 
curtain to be proven as an effective supplement to passive fire separations methods.  
In order to understand the basic fire safety performance requirements in the Danish building 
regulation(BR18) a study of the background of these requirements is performed. The Danish building 
regulation included in 2004 a performance-based acceptance approach to fire safety design. The 
implementation was based on recommendations from a working group called the “Brandudvalg av den 
Nordiska Kommittén för Byggbestämmelser (NKB) (Pedersen et al., 1994). NKB is a cooperative body under 
the Danish, Finnish, Icelandic, Norwegian and Swedish authorities that works with building regulations. In 
1994, NKB prepared a working report intended as a draft document for the introduction of a performance-
based fire safety approach across the five Nordic countries of Denmark, Finland, Iceland, Norway and 
Sweden. This working report consisted of two parts, the first a draft of performance-based fire safety 
requirements and the second listing a draft of technical guidelines for calculation-based verification of 
performance-based fire safety requirements.   
After reviewing this report it is clear that it outlines many of the same basic requirements described in the 
Danish BR18 guidelines (Ministry of Transportation, 2018)  
During the time of writing this Master’s thesis, the new set of Danish building regulations (BR18) were 
implemented with a transition period from BR15 to BR18, meaning that not all the BR18 guidelines had 
been implemented at the time of this study. The literature review for determining performance 
requirements is based therefore on BR18 (Ministry of Transport, 2018) and a draft prescriptive guideline for 
hotels according to BR18 (Ministry of Transportation, 2018). As limited prescriptive design guidelines from 
BR18 had been released during the period of this study, a draft version of prescriptive design methods for 
hotels (Ministry of Transportation, 2018) has been used as the most applicable guideline released for 
complex building types. 
Overall performance requirements of §82 of BR18 state that a building has to be designed to ensure 
satisfactory performance in relation to human safety during a fire.

As Danish building regulations allow for both a prescriptive design approach and a performance-based 
approach, the following acceptance criteria will be used to document the performance of the high-pressure 
water mist curtain from a performance-based approach in accordance with BR18 (Ministry of Transport, 
2018). 
Acceptance criteria are split into three main groups, namely human safety, building safety and 
requirements for technical fire installations pursuant to BR18.   
2.1.1 Acceptance criteria for human safety 
With reference to §82, 91 2), 94 5) and 111 BR18 (Ministry of Transport, 2018), the building design must 
ensure safe conditions for occupants in relation to safe evacuation time (RSET, “required safe egress time”) 
through the design of escape routes, exposure to temperatures, exposure to radiation intensity and having 
building design split into separate fire safety units. 
With reference to §111 BR18 and prescriptive guidelines for hotels (Ministry of Transportation, 2018), a 
building design must be split into separate fire safety units to ensure that escape routes are safe to use 
throughout the complete evacuation time. Also with reference to §94 5), it must be ensured that conditions 
that jeopardize human safety do not occur during the time of evacuation in relation to critical levels of 
temperature, smoke concentration and heat radiation. 
Building designs can be divided into fire cells or fire sections, and building elements used for separating fire 
sections must be of EI60 / A2-s1, d0 classification.  
Acceptance criteria for human safety are based on commonly used criteria in performance-based fire safety 
engineering in Denmark (Sørensen, 2014), which are listed in Table 1. From this approach (Sørensen, 2014) 
the critical upper limit of air temperatures which humans can safely inhale is set at 60° Celsius. This limit is 
lower than that listed in(Byggestyrelsen, 2004), where the upper limit is set at 80° Celsius, this being 
another commonly used set of acceptance criteria which BR18 refers to (Byggestyrelsen, 2004). 
The acceptance criteria for human safety are set according to the maximum values of temperature and 
radiation intensity, both from short-time, long time exposure and total dose, from which occupants can 
evacuate safely. 
The acceptance criteria for human safety are shown in Table 1. 
 
2.1.2 Acceptance criteria for building safety 
With reference to §82, 99 and 104 BR18 (Ministry of Transport, 2018), the building design must ensure 
building safety throughout its total lifetime. This means that the building must be safe for occupants, 
sufficiently fire-resistant and designed to ensure that fire and smoke cannot spread from one fire 
separation unit to the next.  
Acceptance criteria for building safety are based on commonly used criteria in performance-based fire

2.3. Performance of approved fire doors used as passive fire separations. 
This literature study lists the performance requirements of an approved fire door for passive fire separation 
according to Danish building regulations BR18 (Ministry of Transport, 2018). 
With reference to Danish building regulation BR18, fire doors must be approved according to European 
standard EN1634-1 with classification of EI types, if these are to be used as an element of passive fire 
separation in Danish building designs. Such fire doors are approved by a fire test according to European 
standard DS/EN 1634-1, with acceptance criteria in relation to average and maximum temperatures and 
radiation at the unexposed side of the door (Standard, 2012).  
Doors used as passive elements in fire sections are typically of types EI60 or EI120 and for fire cells of type 
EI30.  
Acceptance criteria comply with DS/EN1634-1, DS/EN1363-1 and DS/EN1363-2 and are met by a proven 
performance in relation to the fire doors’ integrity, insulation and radiation, as described below: 
Integrity 
This acceptance criterion states that the performance of a fire door in relation to integrity must be proven 
throughout the time of testing according to the classification type of the door. For example, for an EI60 
door, its integrity must be proven for a minimum of 60 minutes during fire testing. 
The criteria by which the integrity performance of the fire door shall be judged are stipulated in EN1363-1 
(Standard, 2012) as the time for which the door continues to maintain its separating function during the 
test without: 
==PAGE:21==
11 
 
1. Causing the ignition of a cotton pad applied for a maximum of 30 seconds at a minimum distance 
from the door surface of 30 mm on the unexposed side. 
2. Permitting the penetration of a gap gauge of either 6 mm or 25 mm as follows: 
a. The 6 mm gap gauge can be passed through the door so that the gauge projects into the 
furnace, and can be moved 150 mm along the gap; or 
b. The 25 mm gap gauge can be passed through the door so that the gauge projects into the 
furnace. 
Thus, the integrity acceptance criteria do not stipulate full integrity, and allow some smoke and hot gases

Insulation

1. Maximum average surface temperature at unexposed side of the door set of 140°C 
2. Maximum surface temperature at unexposed side of the door set of 180°C 
Radiation 
With reference to EN1363-2 (Standard, 1999), there is no requirement to measure radiation intensity from 
a surface of a passive fire separation with a temperature below 300°C.  
Heat flux sensors must be of a type which measure total heat flux and must be placed 1 m from the 
unexposed side of a test specimen, if radiation is being measured.

3.0 Tests 
This chapter contains four subsections focusing on developing a test setup and test procedure for the large-
scale fire test in this study. The first part of this chapter describes considerations of fire load, followed by 
descriptions of the test facility, test setup and test procedure. 
 
3.1 Fire load 
In this chapter, the choice of fuel package and fire intensity will be decided for use in the large-scale fire 
testing of this study. The choice will be based on considerations of coherence with the target application 
(public and commercial, building OH types) and of fire development and intensity, as well as ensuring that 
the fire load is suitable for the test purpose. 
In order to ensure coherence with the target application of public and commercial buildings (OH risk class) 
and special risk from industrial applications, the decision will be based on a fire with similar characteristics 
to those typical of the target applications. Similarity in relation to types of fire and development is 
characterized as a t-squared fire with a fire growth at medium to fast (Quintiere, 1999) (Trust, 2011). In 
addition, the fire development should preferably reach the level of a fully developed fire relatively quickly 
in order to increase the number of tests possible to run in one day of fire testing. Thus, with a fuel type that 
develops rapidly to the fully developed fire phase, the total test time for each test can be optimized. 
In order to ensure the suitability of the fuel package according to the test purpose (namely to generate real 
risk of fire spread), three parameters are analyzed: the fuel type, position of the fuel package in the test 
room, and the heat release rate of the fire.  
High-pressure water mist is known to have an extinguishing effect by cooling as in traditional water-based 
systems, and from oxygen displacement similar to a gas-based extinguishing system. 
The purpose of this study is not to control the fire by extinguishing, suppression or control effect, but to 
generate a fire barrier between the fire room and target room with “a wall of water mist” attenuating 
radiation and temperatures. 
For the fuel package, a class B fire (Forsvarsministeriet and Beredskabsstyrelsen, 2016) with flammable 
liquid heptane with a flashpoint of - 4°C (Wikipedia, 2018) is used. This is to generate the most 
unfavourable conditions for the water mist extinguishing effect in relation to cooling effect. A heptane fire 
with a flashpoint below 0° Celsius cannot be extinguished by cooling, so heptane is used to minimize the 
water mist system’s extinguishing effect on the fire.  
A heptane fire is also considered suitable as it has a cleaner combustion and thus a higher share of 
radiation effect (𝑄̇ 𝑟𝑎𝑑𝑖𝑎𝑡𝑖𝑜𝑛) generating a maximum level of radiation. It also has a lower margin of error 
from cleaner combustion when compared to the numerical of CFD simulations in comparison with other 
fuel types such as diesel oil etc.  
 
To ensure maximum risk of fire spreading from the fire room to the target room, the decision was made to 
position the fire in line with the door opening O5 close to the target room (see Figure 10). The purpose of 
this was to generate direct exposure to the target room with maximum intensity. 
In relation to the choice of heat release rate, an analysis was performed as to what level of heat release 
rate would be needed to generate a sufficient risk of fire spread. This analysis was made with initial 
considerations of simplified calculations supported by thorough consideration of a CFD analysis.

In order to evaluate whether the heat release rate was adequate to generate a risk of fire spreading, the 
values of heat release rate from the fuel package were compared with the commonly used limit of 20 
kW/m² (Sørensen, 2014).

3.1.2 Determination of fire load by thorough considerations 
In order to validate the simplified calculations above, a method of thorough consideration is performed. For 
such analyses, a numerical study using computational fluid dynamics (CFD) is considered adequate to 
validate the heat release rate before starting large-scale fire testing. Fire Dynamic Simulator (FDS) version 
6.5.2 software is used for these considerations. 
A validation is considered necessary as the Modak equation used above in simplified calculations is 
developed for large pool fires, where the Modak equation sees the fire as a single point. However, the fire 
is not a single point but a surface, and this assumption on the part of the Modak equation results in some 
inaccuracy at close distances when used on smaller pool fires. This means that the results of a Modak 
equation are often to high when calculating a short distance to a fire with reference to Husted (Husted et 
al., 2009). 
In this chapter, the findings of the CFD simulations are presented.  
A CFD model was created in the software FDS version 6.5.2. similar to the building design of the test facility 
and with the fire load that was determined in chapter 3.1.1 The CFD model was created from the exact 
measurements of the test building and with the specification described in test setup chapter 3.3 and in 
chapter 3.4. 
The FDS simulations were used to validate the fire load estimate from chapter 3.1.1.  
These FDS simulations are used to evaluate if the fire load can be considered sufficient to generate a 
realistic risk of fire spread during large-scale fire testing, as well as to evaluate whether the fire of the 
actual test setup is expected to be fuel-controlled or ventilation-controlled. For this study, it is preferable to 
have a fuel-controlled fire in order to maximize the test fire intensity in relation to obtaining a maximum 
level of radiation and temperatures, i.e. the worst-case scenario in relation to risk of fire spread. In 
addition, as a full controlled fire is possible to run with higher accuracy according to chapter 5.1.2, this is 
considered preferable to a ventilation-controlled fire. As several tests will be run and the results of these be 
compared a set up with a fuel controlled fire is preferable to limit failure range in the results.  
The CFD setup and choice of devices used for measurement of radiation and temperatures was then 
evaluated and determined, see appendix C.  
The FDS analysis was performed from a total of eight simulations, divided into two simulation series with 
the first series having external doors closed and the second series having external doors open. 
 
Simulations 1-4 were performed with closed external doors, with the exception of openings in the staircase 
and opening O3, and with open internal doors between rooms 2, 3 and 4 as shown in Figure 2. This was to 
evaluate whether the fire could be expected to be ventilation-controlled and to what extent such 
conditions could influence the fire intensity (HRR).

similar rooms and one staircase room (see Figure 10). 
The test fire is located in room 3, named the Fire Room, at the centreline of the door opening to room 2, 
named the Target Room. A high-pressure water mist curtain is placed in door opening O5, separating the 
target room from the fire room. 
The test setup layout is seen in Figure 10 below and more shown in the Appendix D 
 
Figure 10: Layout of test level in test building 
The test building provides several options for ventilation openings to generate free airflow for natural 
ventilation of the fire. All inside doors, O2, O5 and O6, are open for free airflow during fire testing.  
External door O7 and the ceiling ventilation hatch in the staircase are open to generate an updraft in the 
staircase. At the same time, one external ventilation opening in fire room O3 and one external door O1 in 
room 4 is open (see Figure 10). These doors are open to generate free airflow during tests, ensuring a fuel-
controlled fire and to ensure relatively challenging conditions for the high-pressure water mist due to 
significant sideward airflow. Such conditions are considered less favourable for the high-pressure water 
mist curtain and are defined as unrestricted spaces with free airflow. These unfavourable conditions are 
generated to add a safety factor in the test setup to test the worst-case scenario, in contrast to a design 
scenario of restricted spaces with limited airflows. 
It is known that the high-pressure water mist extinguishing effect comes from influencing a fire on two 
sides of the fire triangle, with the effect of both cooling and oxygen reduction. Conditions for unrestricted 
spaces as opposed to restricted spaces reduce the high-pressure water mist effect of room extinguishing 
due to the inert effect, as free airflow often reduces such an inert effect.

The high-pressure water mist curtain consists of a door nozzle, with several micro nozzles installed. All 
nozzles are positioned with the centreline of the spray pattern downwards perpendicular to the ceiling.  
The high-pressure water mist door nozzle, which generates the high-pressure water mist curtain, is placed 
on the target room side of the door 30 cm above the door opening. The positioning of 30 cm above the 
door opening is chosen to physically shield the spray pattern of the curtain from targeting the fire by 
cutting off the part spreading towards the fire. The disadvantages of this positioning of the door nozzle is 
that an unknown water amount will condensate on the wall, resulting in a reduction of the effective water 
amount in the curtain than that specified in Table 3. When applying the results of this study, it must be 
taken into account that the actual water amount during testing was significantly lower than specified in 
Table 3, and therefore if this can be considered a significant safety factor in relation to the results.

3.4 Test procedure 
Large-scale fire testing is performed to provide performance data of the effect of a high-pressure water 
mist curtain used as an active fire barrier. This performance data is used for the evaluation of the research 
questions in this study (for research questions, see chapter 1.2) 
The results of the large-scale fire tests are used for the discussion and conclusions of this study. 
A test series of four tests is conducted for evaluating the performance of high-pressure water mist curtains 
used as active fire separation in door opening O5, between the fire room and the target room (see Figure 
10).    
A free burn test with no high-pressure water mist curtain, test 0, is conducted to establish a set of baseline 
values of performance parameters. The results from test 0 are then used as reference for evaluation of a 
high-pressure water mist curtain’s ability to mitigate the risk of fire spread.

D. Presenting results by thermal imaging: 
The results of temperatures at a specific point of interest within both the fire 
room and target room are presented using thermal imaging. Thermal imaging 
is used both for evaluation of room temperatures and to supplement 
temperatures measured by thermocouples, as well as for measuring surface 
temperatures at specific points of interest. 
Thermal imaging is not expected to have the same high accuracy as 
thermocouples measuring room temperatures, as FLIR measurements are 
taken through the air with an infrared lens measuring temperatures as 
radiation from a given object. This means that when, for example, the camera 
is pointed at the water mist curtains, it can from time to time measure 
particles of water droplets in the curtain, hot gases mixed in the curtain or hot 
gases on the other side of the water mist curtain. However, by remaining focused on where and 
what temperature is being measured, the FLIR imaging of room temperatures is considered 
accurate to use as a supplement to room temperature measurements. 
For temperature measurements on structural surfaces within the compartment, the accuracy of 
the FLIR imaging is expected to be high, and FLIR imaging is used for evaluating surface 
temperature at specific points of interest,  such as surfaces on the unexposed side of door opening 
O5 on the target room side. 
The general accuracy of the camera is specified by the equipment supplier as +-1°C. 
The temperature measurement with the FLIR camera is given as a single point temperature shown 
at the top of the image, with the location of the temperature measurement in the middle of the 
image indicated by a cross. Temperatures across the whole area of the picture are shown by a 
colour profile with the coldest areas in blue, moving to the hottest areas in white: blue-green-
yellow-red-white. 
Temperature measurement using both thermocouples and thermal imaging is recommended by 
FM Approvals, a member of the FM Global Group (FM Approvals, 2016), as these methods are 
considered accurate for temperature measurement in large-scale fire testing for approval of water 
mist products. With reference to FM Approvals, temperature measurement in combination with 
thermal imaging and of thermocouples is considered valid for this research project.

The highest temparature is seen in test 1, with a average of 87°C and an maximum of 101°C. Thus there is a

With maximum temperatures from test 1 close to 100°C from the top to the bottom of the curtain, this 
indicates that some water droplets at the front of the water mist curtain will evaporate, expanding

push hot gases in front of the mist on its way downwards. Upon hitting the floor, these hot gases are forced 
outward into the target room. Such movement of hot gases is the most likely explanation for the findings of 
increased radiation and temperature close to the floor in tests 1-3.

5.0 Discussion of results from large-scale fire tests 
This chapter presents a series of discussions on topics related to the completed fire tests and to the 
findings and results of these tests. 
Firstly, a discussion of results from tests 0-3 is presented, focusing on findings of the general effect of the 
high-pressure water mist curtains as an active fire barrier.  
This is followed secondly by a discussion of results and factors that could potentially affect test results, 
thereby constituting a potential risk of failure in results. 
Thirdly, a discussion of specific findings of radiation and temperatures from tests 1-3 is presented, as these 
findings indicate increasing levels of radiation and temperatures at floor level in comparison with test 0. 
Fourth, a discussion of critical conditions of human and building safety is presented based on findings from 
tests 0-4. 
Fifth, there is a discussion of whether the performance of a high-pressure water mist curtain can be 
compared to the performance of a fire door as a passive EI-type fire separation. 
5.1.1 Discussion of test results from tests 0-3 
Test 0 
Results show significant risk to both human and building safety. Temperatures and radiation are at 
significantly higher levels than the maximum limits according to the acceptance criteria presented in 
chapter 2.1, indicating a risk of fire spread to adjacent rooms and risk to occupants within the building. 
As room temperatures from inside the fire room reached a maximum of 454°C and averaged at 391°C at a 
height of 2 m, temperatures reached a critical level in relation to building designs with concrete hollow 
deck. With reference to Hertz temperatures above 374°C are critical to commonly used concrete elements, 
meaning that there is not only a risk of building damage but also a critical risk of complete damage to such 
building elements. To sum up the results from test 0, these show a risk in relation to fire spread damaging 
the building, occupant safety and risk of complete damage of concrete elements of a hollow deck type.    
 
Test 1 
By applying a high-pressure water mist curtain with capacity of 7.5 L/min. temperatures and radiation were 
significantly lowered to a level without the risk of fire spread and with some risk to occupant safety in 
relation to room temperatures, radiation dose and local radiation intensity in the target room. Thus, the 
results indicate some effect of the curtain in reducing both room temperatures and levels of radiation, but 
not however a sufficient effect to fulfil the acceptance criteria for human safety.  
Even if test 1 does not have the same effect on reducing room temperatures and radiation in the target 
room as in tests 2 and 3, results of HRR in Figure 22 indicate that test 1 has the most extensive suppression 
effect on the test fire. These results can be explained by just one or a combination of effects, as three key 
parameters affect the attenuation through the water mist curtain; volumetric concentration of water mist, 
the residency time of the droplets and droplet size. Firstly, as the quantity of water in test 1 is significantly 
lower then the volumetric concentration is also lower, and this could also reduce the force and velocity of 
the water droplets in a downward motion as a result of fewer droplets having to overcome the updraft. 
This means that the droplets can be more volatile with less residency time in the area of the curtain while 
also lowering the volumetric concentration still further.  
The second effect could be that, since outdoor wind speed had declined somewhat since test 0, the fire in 
test 1 may to some extent have become a ventilation-controlled fire. 
==PAGE:62==
52 
 
 
Test 2 
Although it is stated above that test 1 appeared to have the highest extinquishing effect by the attenuation 
level of HRR in Figure 22 it can be seen from observations during tests that test 2 had a higher extinguishing 
effect, as the fire was actually extingushed before being reignited during test 2. 
From an increase in the water amount of 29% from test 1 to test 2, results show significantly better 
performance by the high-pressure water mist curtains, with no risk to building safety and significantly 
better performance in relation to human safety. 
Test 3 
No signinficant improvements are seen from the results of test 3 compared to the results of test 2. Thus, 
even when water quantity is increased by 12%, this generates no further improvement in results. This was 
expected to some extent, as the water quantity in test 2 had already shown an extensive effect on reducing 
temperatures to a level close to ambient temperatures. The increase in water quantity of 12% is assumed 
insignificant in relation to further attenuation of radiation. As known deviation of reproducing results of 
large scale fire test in range of 10-35%, with reference to chapter 6.3.1, an increase of 12% is assumed to 
little to generate valid indications. 
 
5.1.2 Discussion of results and factors potentially affecting test results 
Large-scale fire tests are a primary method for researching fire theory and are used for the development 
and validation of methods of manual calculation, numerical methods, approval of water mist systems, fire 
theory etc. However, the conditions for experimental research using large-scale fire tests are very 
important to consider if such research is to be used for predicting results of fires under specific 
circumstances or for the approval of solutions. A fire in a single room is complex, however the level of 
complexity increases when applying a fire to a multiple-room scenario, which in most cases has the 
greatest similarity to the building structures in our everyday life. It is therefore important that the 
conditions of experimental research are chosen to correspond to conditions under which the test results 
are meant to be used. As the outcome of this study is intended to be used for fire safety engineering in 
complex buildings of public and commercial types, the test setup for this study is chosen as a multiple-room 
scenario. This is to mitigate variations from test variables and those typical to designs in the target 
applications of this study (target applications are described in chapter 1.0). 
Another important consideration is the variables stemming from reproducing experimental fires in such 
complex conditions. In this study, the facility used for testing is considered suitable for reproducing fire 
tests with a high level of accuracy. The consideration of high suitability for the actual test building is based 
on a thorough study of reproducibility of test fires in the exact same test building of this study (with 
reference to PhD thesis “Fire Dynamics of Multi-Room Compartment Fires”(Johansson, 2015)). The 
reproducibility of full-scale apartment fires is found in the order of 10-35%, depending on; ventilation 
conditions where well ventilated fires are easier to reproduce, positioning of equipment and the time after 
ignition.  
As the test procedure, ventilation conditions, positioning of equipment and time of ignition were fixed 
during all of the tests in this research project, the test setup is considered to have acceptable accuracy.  
==PAGE:63==
53 
 
In relation to the discussion above with regard to ventilation conditions having an influence on 
reproducibility, it makes sense to continue this discussion, as changes in outdoor wind speed were 
observed during the time that the test series of this study was run. Outdoor wind speed changed during the 
day of testing, potentially affecting the indoor ventilation conditions. This subject will be discussed here. 
Outdoor wind conditions had an overall effect on the test setup by applying a pressure profile 
perpendicular to the northwest side of the test building at openings 1 and 3. The indication of wind 
direction from the northwest and building position is seen in Figure 7. At the time of tests 0 and 1, outdoor 
wind speeds were relatively high at up to 8m/s (Table 3). As the supply of fresh air through openings O1 
and O3 at an initial pretest before test 0 showed high airflows in the fire room, resulting in fluctuating 
flames moving sideward from the fire pan, it was decided to leave O3 open and O1 closed during tests 0 
and 1. With this setup, and based on observations of the test fires, it was considered that the tests would 
be performed with stable conditions as well as ventilated fires. 
As outdoor wind speeds were lower during tests 2 and 3, these tests were performed with both opening O1 
and O3 open at the northwest side of the building. This was to compensate for the reduced air supply 
through opening O3, as the pressure profile on the northeast building side was lower due to a significant 
decrease in outdoor wind speeds compared with conditions in tests 0 and 1. 
All four tests were performed with similar setups in relation to other parameters which have a potential 
influence on ventilation conditions, for example indoor openings and outdoor openings in the staircase, 
etc. Outdoor openings in the staircase were placed on the southeast side of the building and on the roof of 
the building. The influence from changes in outdoor air speeds was considered insignificant to these. It is 
therefore considered that all four tests were performed under similar conditions as well-ventilated fires.  
All results and evaluations of the effect from the four tests were performed with data from the fully 
developed fire phase to mitigate the risk of findings being based on data from non-stable phases of the test 
fires, as phases of growth and decay are expected to have higher variance.  
Having a well-ventilated test setup with significant airflow is considered to have generated the most 
unfavourable conditions for the water mist curtain by minimizing the volumetric water concentration in the 
room. This subjected the curtain to significant airflows both upward from the updraft of the fire and 
sideward from airflow by ventilation. With this approach, the test setup is considered to include safety 
factors and be applicable for use in complex building types in well-ventilated conditions. 
 
5.1.3 Discussion of locally increased level of radiation and temperature in tests 
Results of radiation and temperature measurement indicate higher temperatures at a measurement height 
of 0.25 m for test 1 in comparison with the free burn test of test 0 (see Figure 21).  
It is surmised that these higher temperature measurements do not initially indicate that the high-pressure 
water mist has lower cooling capabilities for cooling the fire compartment  or blocking capabilities for 
attenuation radiation further away from the nozzles.   
The results of radiation and temperature measurements closest to the floor are assumed to indicate that 
one or a combination of effects explains why there was less attenuation effect furthest away from the 
nozzle. A comparison of results from test 0 and test 1 shows significant attenuation of radiation and 
temperatures with the highest factor of attenuation of temperature, approximately two times higher 
attenuation of temperatures than radiation. Three key parameters affect the attenuation through the 
==PAGE:64==
54 
 
water mist curtain; volumetric concentration of water mist, the residency time of the droplets and droplet 
size. Firstly droplet sizes are considered to have the highest attenuation effect with minor droplet sizes, 
thus, with choices of high-pressure water mist this parameter has been optimized compared to low-
pressure water mist and sprinkler having significantly larger droplet sizes. Minor optimization might be 
possible in relation to choice of nozzle technology but this variable is considerate insignificant for this 
project. Nozzle type used during fire test is type hollow cone with droplet size of approximately 50 my. 
Droplet size is considered to be the same for all three nozzle types used, as it involves similar technology 
and spray pattern with different k-factors.  
Secondly residency time of the water droplets in the curtain, as all three tests were performed at the same 
pressure, indicating that the similar droplet sizes and droplet speed in this combination gave the same 
residency time in the curtain. It is considered possible to extend residency time of the droplet if adjusting 
parameters of the high-pressure water mist system nozzles design. This from a changing circular spray 
pattern as used in test to a flat spray limiting the droplets orientation away from the centre of the curtain. 
Thirdly as key parameters, volumetric concentration is assumed to have the highest effect, as significant 
attenuation increase is seen from test 1 to test 2. As the main difference from test 1 og 2 is increased 
volumetric concentration, this assuption is concluded to be valid.  
Thus, the explanation for the significantly lower attenuation effect furthest away from the nozzle is 
assumed to be triplefold, that is: first and foremost, that there was insufficient volumetric water 
concentration in relation to attenuation of radiation, secondly that the water mist may have pushed hot 
gases in front of the droplets, and thirdly that droplets clashes together further away from the nozzles 
decreases in numbers and increases in size. When they hit the floor, these hot gases are then reoriented 
along the floor and measured where the heat flux sensors are placed. As the water mist is pushing hot 
gases in front of the droplets, the droplets will undergo a phase change from liquid to gas, generating hot 
steam which evaporates and expands more than 1700 times in volume.  
Volumetric concentration could be increased by adding more nozzles or nozzles along side the door 
opening sides, it would be considered beneficial increasing numbers of nozzles placed pendent in top of 
door opening as placed during tests but in extra row. Thus, increasing the volumetric concentration and 
even the concentration by overlapping nozzle rows. 
 
5.1.4 Discussion of critical conditions of human and building safety from findings 
of tests 0-3 
In this chapter, test results from fire tests 0-3 are discussed in relation to their ability to meet the 
performance requirements of Danish building regulation BR18 (Ministry of Transport, 2018) (requirements 
with reference to chapter 2.1)  
The test results are thus evaluated in comparison to the acceptance criteria of human and building safety. 
The results of fire test 0 show that the test fire will create critical conditions in adjacent rooms within the 
test building. The results also show that having a high-pressure water mist curtain activated will eliminate 
risks in relation to building safety and significantly mitigate risks in relation to human safety. Test results 
from tests 1-3 show that the individual settings of the high-pressure water mist curtain make a significant 
enough difference to ensure a high level of human safety. 
==PAGE:65==
55 
 
The test results of test 0 - a free burn fire without a high-pressure water mist curtain installed - show 
significant risk of fire spreading in the test building, as the acceptance criteria of maximum radiation 
intensity and temperature exposure to unexposed building elements are exceeded. There is a high risk of 
fire spreading due to conditions of flashover, as radiation intensity is higher than 20 kW/m², as well as due 
to heating of surfaces of unexposed building elements in target rooms to a level above 160°C [2]. At the 
same time, test results show significant risk to human safety, as the acceptance criteria of maximum 
radiation intensity, radiation dose and room temperatures are exceeded. 
The significant risk to both human and building safety indicates that a building design without a fire 
separation unit, that is where the fire room and any adjacent units are separated, cannot meet the 
performance requirements of Danish building regulation BR18 (with reference to requirements presented 
in chapter 2.1) 
The test results of test 1, with a high-pressure water mist curtain at a capacity of 7.5 litres of water per 
minute show that there will be no risk of fire spreading, as all acceptance criteria in relation to building 
safety are met. 
When compared with the results of test 0, radiation results show a decreasing factor of 38% in maximum 
radiation intensity from 27 kW/m² in test 0 to 17 kW/m² in test 1 and a decreasing factor of 36% in 
maximum average radiation intensity from 22.2 kW/m² in test 0 to 14.4 kW/m² in test 1. 
However, results in relation to human safety show some risk to human safety, as acceptance criteria of 
maximum room temperatures and to some extent of maximum radiation intensity and radiation dose are 
not met. 
This means that if a building with the actual test setup is protected with an active fire separation of a high-
pressure water mist at a capacity of 7.5 litres water per minute, there will be no risk of fire spreading from 
the fire room to an adjacent target room. At the same time, test results show that such an adjacent room 
cannot be used as an escape route without risk to human safety if occupants pass by opening O5. 
In comparison with the results of test 0, the average target room temperature at 2.0 m is lowered by 
approximately 70% from 298°C to 89°C and the maximum target room temperature is lowered by 
approximately 69% from 341°C to 107°C. 
 
The test results from test 2 with a high-pressure water mist curtain at a capacity of 10.5 litres of water per 
minute show that there will be no risk to building safety, as all acceptance criteria in relation to building 
safety are met. 
Test results also show that the risk to human safety is limited still further when compared to the results 
from test 1, as risk is somewhat reduced locally in relation to the level of maximum radiation intensity at 
0.5 m height.   
This indicates that the target room (with some limitations) can be used as an escape route if no occupant is 
passing by opening 5 with the risk of nearby exposure to radiation. 
In comparison with the results of test 1, the average target room temperature at 2.0 m is lowered by 
approximately 52% from 89°C to 43°C and the maximum target room temperature is lowered by 
approximately 52% from 107°C to 52°C. 
When compared with the results from test 0, radiation results show a decreasing factor of 52% in maximum 
radiation intensity from 27 kW/m² at 2.0 m in test 0 to 13.1 kW/m² at 0.5 m in test 2 and a decreasing 
factor of 51% in maximum average radiation intensity from 22.2 kW/m² at 2.0 m in test 0 to 10.9 kW/m² at 
==PAGE:66==
56 
 
0.5 m in test 2. 
The high-pressure water mist in test 2 not only had significant controlling capabilities for radiation and 
temperatures, but also extinguished the fire approximately 190 seconds into the fully developed fire phase, 
despite the positioning of the curtain and the fuel type having been selected to limit the cooling effect and 
to mitigate the water mist extinguishing effect on the test fire.  
However, it is assumed that some of the water mist was drawn into the fire room by the movement of hot 
air from the fire, and that water mist was then mixed with fresh air flowing into the fire plume. This helped 
extinguish the fire as water mist began to absorb heat in the fire plume, thereby decreasing combustion 
efficiency and by an inert effect from expansion to steam. These small high-pressure water mist droplets 
expand to a gas phase from its uptake of energy, expanding approximately 1700 times and pushing away 
oxygen at the fire plume, resulting in extinguishing by an inert effect. The combustion efficiency is lowered 
as the water mist cools the flames, resulting in lower radiation intensity back to the fire source and a 
decreasing pyrolysis rate.  
The extinguishing result in test 2 indicates that despite the low water quantity and obstructing measures 
taken to limit the extinguishing effect as much as possible, the performance of the high-pressure water 
mist curtain was significant in relation to extinguishing.  
Test results from test 3 with a high-pressure water mist curtain at a capacity of 11.9 litres of water per 
minute show, as they did in tests 1 and 2, that if a high-pressure water mist curtain is installed and 
activated, the risk of fire spreading from the fire room into an adjacent target room is eliminated. However, 
in comparison with results from test 2, no further lowering of room temperatures was seen in test 3. This 
was not a surprise, as temperatures from both tests 2 and 3 were lowered close to the level of ambient 
temperatures, indicating full temperature control from both specifications of water mist curtains. 
Results from test 3 showed that the average target room temperature at 2.0 m was 49°C and the maximum 
target room temperature was 55°C. 
When compared with the results from test 0, radiation results show a decreasing factor of 55% in maximum 
radiation intensity from 27 kW/m² at 2.0 m in test 0 to 12.2 kW/m² at 0.5 m in test 3 and a decreasing 
factor of 50% in maximum average radiation intensity from 22.2 kW/m² at 2.0 m in test 0 to 11.3 kW/m² at 
0.5 m in test 3. 
The results from test 3 therefore indicate no risk to building safety, as all acceptance criteria in relation to 
building safety are met. 
Tests results also show that risk to human safety is limited to a similar level as in test 2, as risk is somewhat 
reduced locally in relation to the level of maximum radiation intensity at 0.5 m height.   
This means that the target room can (with some limitations) be used as an escape route if no occupant is 
passing by opening 5 with the risk of nearby exposure to radiation. 
5.1.5 Comparison of the performance of the high-pressure water mist curtain 
and a fire door. 
The results of large-scale fire tests with a high-pressure water mist curtain incorporated in the building 
design as an active fire barrier show their ability to reduce temperature and radiation exposure into 
adjacent rooms. This chapter presents a discussion of whether the results from this active approach to fire 
separation can be compared with the approval criteria of passive fire separations such as fire doors. 
According to Danish fire regulation, fireproof doors are approved as a passive fire separation as fire doors 
of type EI. Such fire doors are approved by fire testing in accordance with European standard  
==PAGE:67==
57 
 
DS/EN 1634-1, with acceptance criteria in relation to maximum temperature and radiation on the 
unexposed side of the door. 
Approved fire separation doors must meet the demands of Danish building regulations by fulfilling the 
requirements described below. In order to compare the high-pressure water mist curtain’s ability to reduce 
temperatures and radiation with that of a fire door approved in accordance with DS/EN1634-1, the 
comparison is made using an EI60-type door. 
Acceptance criteria in accordance with DS/EN1634-1(Standards, 2018), DS/EN1363-1(Standard, 2012) and 
DS/EN1363-2(Standard, 1999) are listed below. 
Integrity: 
This acceptance criterion states that a fire door must prove its integrity during the time of testing 
corresponding to the classification of the door; for a EI60 door, the integrity must be a minimum of 60 
minutes. 
Insulation – Acceptance criteria: 
1. Maximum average surface temperature at unexposed side of the door set at 140°C. 
2. Maximum surface temperature at unexposed side of the door set at 180°C. 
Radiation 
If the above temperature criteria are not met throughout the time of testing, a measurement of radiation 
must be taken with reference to chapter 2.3. 
Integrity 
As the high-pressure water mist curtain exists as a “wall of water” continuously supplied from a fresh water 
supply, it will not, like a door, lose its integrity at a specific time from a fire burning through but rather from 
the time of water supply available. The integrity of a passive fire separation element such as a fire door 
must be proven, so that the time it takes before critical cracks or gaps are created in the door is longer than 
the classification of the door. This means that the time before critical cracks or gaps emerge in an EI60 door 
must be longer than 60 minutes. 
Thus, the overall criteria of integrity should ensure that an active fire separation solution continues to 
perform throughout the time equal to its EI classification. For the high-pressure water mist curtain, this is 
ensured if the time of water supply is equal to the requested EI classification, that is a minimum of 60 
minutes’ water supply for EI60, 120 minutes for EI120 classification etc. 
Insulation 
For the comparison of test results with the performance of approved passive fire doors, it is assumed 
acceptable to compare results of room temperature in the target room directly with acceptance criteria of 
surface temperatures on the unexposed side of a fire door. This is a assumption, as room temperature is 
measured approximately 1.4 m from the water curtain, however it is assumed that temperatures measured 
directly at the unexposed side of the curtain would not be accurate due to cold water droplets mixing with 
hot gases.  
With reference to the results presented in Figure 19, Figure 20 and Figure 21 no average temperature is 
measured above 140°C; all average temperatures from tests 1-3 are significantly below these acceptance 
==PAGE:68==
58 
 
criteria. 
At the same time, no maximum temperature is seen above 106°C. All maximum temperatures are 
significantly below the maximum limits in the acceptance criteria of 180°C. 
Test 1 shows a maximum temperature of 106°C and the highest average at 89°C, both at a height of 2.0 m. 
Test 2 shows a maximum temperature of 52°C and the highest average at 43°C, both at a height of 2.0 m. 
Test 3 shows a maximum temperature of 55°C and the highest average at 49°C, both at a height of 0.25 m. 
When comparing temperature, the tests indicate that the high-pressure water mist in all three tests 
reduces the room temperatures to a level significantly below the acceptance criteria of insulation. As no 
temperatures are seen above these limits, no evaluation of radiation is made with reference to description 
above.

6.0 CFD simulation. 
This chapter presents a numerical approach to reproduce the results from large scale fire tests using CFD 
software. This to generate a baseline CFD model for further numerical experiments and scaling of the 
results from the large-scale fire tests. This model is intended for use in similar applications as the large-
scale fire test performed in a multiple room design. Thus, the findings of this chapter are intended to be 
used as complimentary to the results of the fire test.  
6.1 CFD simulation model 
For this work the FDS 6.6.0 computational fluid dynamics software is used for numerical experiments 
reproducing the test results. 
FDS 6.6.0 is designed for fire related simulations and based on Navier-Strokes governing equations for both 
turbulent and laminar flows, thereby making it suitable for a numerical study of a complex fire in a multiple 
room scenario.  
FDS software is also considered to be suitable for numerical experiments with high-pressure water mist 
spray modelling in accordance with the work of Technical Research Centre of Finland (VTT) (Simo et al., 
2011) and (Jukka et al., 2012). 
6.2 FDS simulation of results from large scale fire tests. 
In order to reproduce the results of large scale fire testing using a numerical approach, this work is divided 
into two different scenarios, one scenario being the test without high-pressure water mist of test 0 and the 
second scenario being a test with high-pressure water similar to tests 1-3. As the high-pressure water mist 
curtain has significant impact on the fire, airflow etc. this is significantly different from the simulation setup 
for a scenario without such curtain.  
In this chapter an FDS model is presented according to the first scenario of the initial setup in test 0.  
6.3 Reproducing the test result by FDS simulation 
The possibility of reproducing the results of large scale fire testing using a numerical approach is affected by 
the levels of accuracy of both the results of repeated fire testing and of reproduced fds simulation. The 
accuracy of results from fire testing is affected by the complexity and condition of the fire, single room fires 
being somewhat complex with this complexity increasing in multiple room fires. 
At the same time, the variance in reproduced results using a numerical approach has an acceptable level of 
accuracy.  
6.3.1 Reproducing test result of full scale fire test. 
With reference to work by Johansson (Johansson, 2015) it is possible to reproduce results of repeated full 
scale fire tests for complex multiple room designs with an accuracy of 10-35% for temperature 
measurement in the adjacent room to a fire room. Significant factors in relation to this level of 
reproducibility are; ventilation scenario, location of equipment and time of ignition. As Johansson’s study 
was performed using a multiple room design in the exact same building used for this study, the level of 
accuracy of fire test results can be expected to lie within the interval of 10-35% with respect to significant 
factors. 
As none of the 4 fire test scenarios in this study could be replicated due to limited test time available, it is 
critical that the overall test setup was chosen for well-ventilated fires with similar conditions of placement 
==PAGE:70==
60 
 
of equipment and time of ignition. This was in order to give a similar level of accuracy to that of Johansson’s 
study. 
6.3.2 Reproducing test result by simulation. 
In order to ensure the accuracy of the reproduced results it was decided to use FDS for computational 
software with reference to that above explanation in chapter 6.1 as well as the following. The FDS 
simulation software is developed in a collaboration by NIST and VTT and the verification of FDS 6.6.0 is 
documented by the NIST and VTT in the Fire Dynamics Simulator Technical Reference Guide Volume 2: 
Verification (Kevin, Simo, Jason, Weinschenk, et al., 2016).      
6.4 Results of FDS simulation 
In order to reduce inaccuracies in the reproducing of large scale test results in the numerical analyses in 
FDS, the FDS model has been modelled to the highest extent comparable to conditions in the fire tests 
performed. 
An FDS model has been set up based on the exact measurement of physical dimensions of the second floor 
of this three-storey building with reference to Figure 10 and Figure 11. External wind conditions have been 
applied to this FDS model as a pressure profile on the northeast side of the building, i.e. the side with

7.0 Conclusion 
The purpose of this project was to determine if a high-pressure water mist curtain could be used as an 
active fire barrier mitigate the risk fire spread, and also to determine if such a curtain could be used as a 
complementary method to passive fire door separations of fire units in accordance with the performance 
demands of Danish building regulation BR18(Ministry of Transport, 2018). This overall aim was broken 
down into an investigation of four main objectives, which provided results for evaluating the performance 
of high-pressure water mist curtains. 
The conclusions is based on evaluation of the particular tests setup in relation to demand of Danish building 
regulation BR18. Conclusions is based on tests results and discussions in chapter 5. 
The first objective to be considered was the ability of the high-pressure water mist curtain to hinder an 
initial fire from spreading to an adjacent target room.  
During this study, the following parameters were identified as the primary contributing factors to potential 
fire spread: 
• 
Room temperatures 
• 
Surface temperatures 
• 
Radiation intensity 
The results in test 0 with a free burn fire revealed that without a high-pressure water mist curtain there 
was a risk of fire spread due to the surface temperatures of unexposed building elements and also due to 
radiation intensity at a level capable of generating a risk of flashover. However, no immediate risk of fire 
spread in relation to the room temperatures in adjacent rooms was identified. 
The results of tests 1-3, all with a high-pressure water mist curtain as a fire barrier, revealed no risk of fire 
spreading to adjacent rooms. 
Thus, for the first research sub question of chapter 1.2 it can be concluded that a pendent positioned high-
pressure water mist as type of tests 1-3 can stop a fire from spreading from a fire compartment to adjacent 
room by its attenuation effect of radiation and temperatures. 
The second objective to be considered was the ability to control radiation and temperature from a fire from 
one separation unit to other in relation to building safety conditions in accordance with Danish building 
regulation BR18 (Ministry of Transport, 2018).   
This objective was evaluated based on the same acceptance criteria of temperature and radiation control 
as used in the first objective. Thus, the active fire barrier of the high-pressure water mist curtain was 
considered placed in an opening of a fire separation unit.  
The tests 1-3 identified no risk to building safety in accordance with the performance requirements of 
Danish building regulation BR18. 
Based on result and limitation described above it can be concluded that a high-pressure water mist curtain 
of type as tested in tests 1-3 can control radiation and temperatures at acceptable level in accordance with 
building safety requirements of the Danish building regulations 2018. 
 
The third objective to be considered was the ability of the high-pressure water mist curtain to ensure 
control of temperatures and radiation intensity and dose in relation to human safety in accordance with 
the performance requirements in Danish building regulation BR18. 
==PAGE:82==
72 
 
The results from test 0 revealed a risk to human safety, in that the maximum limits of room temperature 
and radiation intensity were exceeded. 
The results of tests 1 revealed a risk to human safety in relation to room temperature and to radiation 
intensity nearest to the floor in the target room. 
The results of test 2-3 revealed no risk to human safety in relation to room temperature; they did however 
indicate some risk due to radiation nearest to the floor in the target room. 
it can be concluded that a high-pressure water mist curtain of type as tested in tests 2 and 3 can control 
temperatures at acceptable level and to high extend level of radiation in accordance with building safety 
requirements of the Danish building regulations 2018. 
The fourth objective to be considered was the possibility of using the target room as an escape route with 
people passing by the unexposed side of a high-pressure water mist curtain, as an active fire separation. 
Results of tests 1-3 indicated that whether the target room can be used as an escape route depends on the 
volumetric capacity of the high-pressure water mist curtain installed.  
Based on test results it can be concluded that the target room could not be used as an escape route with 
the type of curtain used in tests 1 but to some extend with type of curtain used in tests 2-3. It can as well 
based on tests results and discussions in chapter 5 be concluded that it is likely that a high-pressure water 
mist curtain can be designed to ensure full use of the target room as an escape route.

8.0 Future work 
As described in chapter 1.3 master thesis limitation, one main parameter has not been examined during 
this project namely the influence of toxic smoke spread in relation to human safety in accordance with 
requirements in the Danish building regulations BR18. Future work should include an examination of the 
high-pressure water mist curtain ability to control spread of toxic residues. This in order to fully evaluated if 
such curtain can be used as an active fire barrier between a fire compartment and an escape route.  
A relevant consideration in relation to such study are that a proven fire does (passive fire separation) does 
not fully protect an occupant from toxic gases and smoke spread as no demand for full integrity exist in 
approval of fire doors.  
Included in this project an FDS model has been validated for the fire scenario of free burn (test 0) without 
the high-pressure water mist curtain activated. Future work should include the completion of an FDS model 
for tests 1-3 with a high-pressure water mist curtain installed. This in order to complete validated FDS 
models (from test results) for evaluation of extended field of applications.  
At the same time, future work could include a further evaluation of radiation measurements from the FDS 
model generated for the free burn scenario in this project. This in order to generate more accurate FDS 
results, bearing in mind a finer mesh setup with more radiation angles included. 
It could be relevant in future work to investigate whether potential built up of combustible fumes between 
upper and lower limit of ignition can be expected to increase or decrease in a modern building design with 
and without active fire separations. Thus, thereby affecting potential risk of fire spread due to ignition of 
combustible fumes in adjacent rooms. Motivation for such study could be to investigate whether a 
decreasing area of leakage in modern building designs or changes in in potential spreading of fumes can 
affect the risk of fire spread. Acceptance of leakage area in building designs has decreased in latest update 
of Danish building regulation BR18(Ministry of Transport, 2018) due to a higher focus on energy efficiency, 
this relevant to both design with traditional passive separation and with high-pressure water mist curtains. 
At the same time an increase in use of active fire separations by high-pressure water mist curtains could 
affect the potential spreading of fumes. Potential either as increased spreading from replacing of physical 
separation or decreased by water mist potential of washing the smoke of residues. 
Future work should include further evaluation of assumption made during this project, namely that the 
performance of an high-pressure water mist curtains control of temperature (room) at fire separation can 
be compared to temperature (surface) requirements of approvals for fire doors (Standard, 2012). Or 
whether a new standard should be made for the approval of active fire separations. 
Future work should as well include extended field of application from the target segment of this project. 
Thus as; fire separation of larger open spaces as exhibition halls, outdoor protection of risk of fire spread 
between objects or buildings, shielding of high risk area inside building during potential building fires 
(weapon rooms, storage of flammable liquid, paint booths, protection of ammonia storage facility at 
accidental leakage “binding of ammonia particles”, etc.).

Devices 
Measurements and results of the FDS simulation are made using devices to measure temperature and 
several different contributions of heat fluxes.  
The risk of fire spread is assumed to be dominated by the radiative and convective contribution in a heat 
transfer from a fire source in one room to an exposed target of flammable material in an adjacent room. 
Heat flux sensors are placed in specific target positions to simulate a flammable material and thus measure 
the risk of fire spread by radiation. Both the FDS simulation and full-scale fire test devices for measuring 
heat fluxes substitute an actual flammable material in a specific target position, in order to provide a more 
accurate evaluation of radiation and to ensure that the high-pressure water mist curtain cannot cool or 
wetten the flammable material.

This is to evaluate room temperatures in relation to risk of fire spread.

Appendix F: Radiation dose calculations 
The radiation intensity is limited to a maximum of 10kW/m² for a maximum of 4 seconds and for a 
maximum of 1.5kW/m² throughout the evacuation time according to Sørensen (Sørensen, 2014).  
 
The radiation dose is limited to a maximum of 40kWs/m² when radiation is higher than 1.5kW/m² and less 
than 10kW/m² (Sørensen, 2014). 
The calculation is for the maximum level of radiation that a human is exposed to when passing by an 
opening O5 using the target room as an escape route. 
The radiation intensity that evacuees will be exposed to is calculated as an average exposure of radiation 
from the four different measurement heights in this project. The exposure of radiation is evaluated as 
being evenly distributed over an average body size.  
In order to calculate the maximum radiation dose a human can be exposed to when evacuating through the 
target room an evaluation of exposure time is needed. In order to evaluate the exposure time, an imagined 
evacuation route through the target room is used where the evacuees have to pass opening O5, with 
exposure, before exiting the target room by the adjacent staircase. Walking speed and density during 
evacuation are therefore set conservatively compared to the target room being an open, level corridor 
without the staircase as delaying factor.  
Walking speed down the stairs is set at 0.75m/sec with reference to Sørensen (Sørensen, 2014). 
Exposure time for an individual evacuee passing opening O5 through the target room is calculated from 
Equation 14 to Equation 18 as a total of walking and queuing time for this particular evacuation route. 
Walking distance is set for 2 metres, this being the width of opening O5 plus approximately 40 cm on each 
side, estimated as the critical position with exposure to direct radiation from the fire. Specific flow (Fs) is set 
at 55 persons/min/m at the staircase. Door width of O5 is 1.15 m and BL boundary layer of 0.15 (Sørensen, 
2014). N is the number of people in initial exposure at O5 during evacuation time and is estimated for 2 
persons with a specific flow rate of 0.75 m and walking distance.
