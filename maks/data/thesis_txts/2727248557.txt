SUMMARY 
 
The aggregation of -synuclein protein into amyloid fibrils is a main hallmark of Parkinson’s 
disease. In this work we have undertaken a comprehensive characterization of two distinct fibril 
polymorphs and characterized the influence of sonication on their lengths. Additionally, we have 
dived into a highly quantitative study of -synuclein aggregation behavior in vitro, using seed 
amplification assays. Such assays have allowed to determine the molecular rate constants of 
elongation and secondary nucleation under different conditions. In this thesis, we show that -
synuclein aggregation is dominated by elongation mechanism under neutral pH conditions while 
being dominated by secondary nucleation mechanism under highly acidic pH conditions. We also 
find that elongation rate constants are substantially enhanced at acidic pH values compared to 
neutral pH. Furthermore, we have studied the effect of human cerebrospinal fluid on elongation 
rate constants, corroborating its inhibitory effect. These findings provide new insights into -
synuclein aggregation in the context of Parkinson’s disease, in the pursue of developing efficient 
diagnostic tools that enable early detection and monitoring of disease progression.

INTRODUCTION  
 
1. Parkinson’s disease 
 
The term “Synucleopathies” encompasses medical conditions characterized by the presence of 
abnormal misfolded α-synuclein aggregates in both the peripheral and central nervous systems. 
These disorders exhibit diverse clinical manifestations due to differences in the specific cellular 
location and pattern of α-synuclein deposition. Examples include Parkinson’s disease (PD), 
multiple system atrophy (MSA) and dementia with Lewy bodies (DLB)1. PD, specifically, is a 
neurodegenerative disorder characterized by the progressive degeneration of neurons in the brain. 
It is estimated to affect 1-2% of individuals over the age of 65 worldwide, however, the incidence 
rate of PD increases with age, with a higher incidence in individuals who are over 80 years old2. 
According to a study published in the Journal of Parkinson's Disease, the global incidence of 
Parkinson's disease is projected to double from 6.9 million in 2015 to over 12 million in 20403.  
 
 
 
1.1 Pathophysiology: 
 
PD, in pathological terms, is characterized by the degeneration of dopaminergic neurons in the 
substantia nigra pars compacta (SNpc)4. Such neurons play a crucial role in the regulation of 
movement and coordination, as they are responsible for producing dopamine, a neurotransmitter 
that contributes to the transmission of signals between nerve cells5. Therefore, the loss of 
dopaminergic neurons in PD promotes clinical symptoms such as tremors, rigidity and 
bradykinesia6, further explained in section 1.2.  
 
PD’s onset is related to a substantial increase of α-synuclein expression and aggregation, 
assembling into amyloid fibrils,  ultimately result in the formation of Lewy Bodies (LBs) and 
Lewy Neurities (LNs)7. The presence of LB and LNs in different areas of the brain is believed to 
play a role in the appearance of additional symptoms associated with PD, including cognitive 
decline and autonomic dysfunction6, further explained in section 1.2.  
 
In terms of neurodegeneration progression, the disease comprises three different stages: the initial 
phase, referred to as “asymptomatic stage”, characterized by a substantial decline (10-20%) in 
dopaminergic neurons within the SNpc, followed by near-total loss of all dopaminergic neurons 
in the “early symptomatic stage”. Lastly, the disease enters the “clinical progression stage”, where 
neuron loss can also be observed in other brain regions8.

PD’s progression causes motor and non-motor symptoms. While motor symptoms are the ones 
that alter and hamper movement and physical tasks, non-motor symptoms affect organ systems, 
including the gastrointestinal and genitourinary systems9. PD’s symptoms are summarized in

1.3 Parkinson’s Disease diagnosis: 
 
PD’s diagnosis primarily relies on a thorough evaluation of the patient’s medical history and 
physical examination9. With the purpose of developing a robust criteria to increase diagnostic 
accuracy, the International Parkinson and Movement Disorder Society has presented a set of 
guidelines10. According to these guidelines, the diagnosis of PD requires the presence of 
parkinsonism, which refers to bradykinesia with rest tremor and/or rigidity. Additionally, 
individuals need to meet two of four of the following symptoms: rest tremor, dramatic 
improvement with dopaminergic therapy, presence of levodopa-induced dyskinesias or presence 
of either olfactory loss or cardiac sympathetic denervation11. 
However, PD’s diagnosis can be challenging due to the non-specific nature of its symptoms, 
which overlap with other neurodegenerative disorders, and the absence of a definitive biomarker. 
Moreover, variability and inaccuracies in diagnosis can occur due to the subjective nature of the 
clinical assessments. Considering the mentioned aspects, misdiagnosis or delayed diagnosis can 
occur, leading to delayed or inadequate treatment11. 
Another aspect that hinders PD’s diagnosis is the period that lasts from an individual’s first 
noticeable early symptoms to the actual timing of diagnosis, which has a duration of 10 years on 
average12. Furthermore, patients may not proactively disclose non-motor symptoms to a physician 
due to embarrassment, unawareness of its relationship with PD or because the primary diagnosis 
is focused on motor symptomatology9. 
Considering the aforementioned challenges, it can be inferred that current PD diagnosis 
approaches are marked by the inability to make a definitive diagnosis at early stages of the disease 
and the complexities in predicting the progression of the disease13. For this reason, the present 
research landscape is characterized by the pursuit of developing new and more efficient diagnostic 
tools14. Among the array of emerging techniques, biochemical tests stand out,  which will be 
further explained in section 2.3.  
 
 
1.4 Parkinson’s Disease treatment: 
 
Currently, there are no available treatments that cure Parkinson’s Disease. However, there are 
some therapies that slow down its progression. For example, to make up for the dopamine 
deficiency in PD patients, these are prescribed levodopa, a drug compound that once it has gone 
through the blood-brain barrier it decarboxylates, giving rise to dopamine. On the same line, PD 
patients are prescribed neuroprotectors (such as amantadine), to preserve the remaining functional 
neurons, making them viable for longer periods of time15.  
While the mentioned mainly treat the motor symptoms, other therapies are focused on the targeted 
treatment towards reducing non-motor symptoms. Some include pramipexol, to treat depression 
and rotigotine, to treat sleep disorders, among many others15.  
==PAGE:9==
9 
 
Taking everything into account, despite extensive research efforts, the precise causes of PD are 
yet to be elucidated and there is currently no cure. Consequently, deepening our comprehension 
of the disease is of outmost importance for scientists and healthcare professionals.  
 
 
2. α-synuclein seed amplification assays 
 
2.1 α-synuclein  
 
α-synuclein is a small, intrinsically disordered protein composed of 140 amino acids (see Figure 
1). Its monomeric form can be divided into three different segments: (1) amino acids 1-60, which 
include the acetylated N-terminal region, where mutations tightly related to PD’s onset are 
located; (2) amino acids 61-95 comprise the non-amyloid-b component (NAC), which plays a 
crucial role in the aggregation and formation of α-synuclein fibrils and (3) amino acids 96-140, 
which include the C-terminal domain, which has been identified as a calcium-binding domain. In 
fact, calcium ions have been proposed to modulate -synuclein aggregation, promoting it and 
accelerating the aggregation process16.

2.2 α -synuclein’s aggregation into amyloid fibrils 
 
As mentioned, α-synuclein fibrils are the primary component of Lewy Bodies and Neurities. The 
conversion of monomeric alpha-synuclein proteins to large fibrillar aggregates, known as amyloid 
fibrils, is tightly associated with PD´s onset17.  
The formation of α-synuclein aggregates entails the transition of a system originally in a 
homogenous solution phase to the coexistence of a newly formed aggregated phase with the 
original monomer solution phase17. The mentioned transition cannot be described as a one-step 
reaction, but occurs through the simultaneous interplay of four different events, these being 
primary nucleation, elongation, secondary nucleation and fragmentation, which will be explained 
in the following sections.  
==PAGE:10==
10 
 
2.2.1 
Primary nucleation 
 
Primary nucleation, shown in Figure 2, refers to the formation of nuclei from soluble monomers. 
In fact, this reaction is mostly surface-catalyzed, which could be, in vivo, the surface of a lipid 
bilayers or the air-water interface or nanoparticles, in vitro. Primary nucleation is a crucial and 
unavoidable step that can limit the overall reaction of fibril formation, acting as a bottleneck17. 
Nucleation events rely on the fact that monomers composing small aggregates have high free 
energy as they have a high interfacial energy towards water. Therefore, such clustered monomers 
tend to dissociate. However, with increasing cluster size, monomer-monomer interactions 
remarkably increase cluster stability, which leads to a higher probability of nucleus growth 
through monomer addition instead of nucleus dissociation. Critical nuclei are defined as the 
smallest aggregates sufficiently stable to proceed to form the new phase rather than dissolving.  
In summary, primary nucleation is the association of monomeric proteins without already formed 
aggregates participating17.

2.2.2 
Elongation 
 
While primary nucleation can be classified as a slow event with a high free energy barrier, 
elongation (shown in Figure 3) happens at a higher rate and has a significantly lower free energy 
barrier17. Fibril elongation consists of monomer addition to the fibril ends. In low monomer 
concentrations, there is a linear relationship between such concentration and the elongation rate, 
while at high monomer concentration the process saturates19. Therefore, elongation is always 
dependent on the number of available fibril ends. However, while at low monomer concentration 
elongation is not only dependent on the number of ends but also on monomer concentration. In 
contrast, at high monomer concentrations, elongation becomes monomer independent.

Integrating the aforementioned events that contribute to fibril formation, the kinetics of α-
synuclein aggregation from purely monomeric samples often follows a sigmoidal time course 
(graphically shown in Figure 6), which includes an initial lag phase, corresponding to a primary 
nucleation-dominated period where fibril concentration is below detection limits; an exponential 
phase, which corresponds to a elongation- or secondary nucleation-dominated period, and lastly, 
a plateau phase, which indicates the exhaustion of available monomers22.

2.3 -synuclein fibril polymorphs  
 
At the molecular level, amyloid fibrils are organized in a “cross-β” architecture, which includes 
β-sheets oriented perpendicularly to the fibril axis, shown in Figure 723. However, aggregation of 
α-synuclein monomers into amyloid fibrils exhibits heterogeneous conformations, which are 
referred to as polymorphisms. Among different polymorphs, different fibril diameter, twists or 
number of protofilaments per fibril can be observed24. Concretely, the variation in twists can be 
observed in the differences in pitch lengths, which refers to the distance of a full 360-degree 
rotation of the fibril. Different α-synuclein fibrils may present different morphology and 
pathology under different conditions25. In fact, evidence indicates that structural variations in 
amyloid fibrils associated to different pathologies26, including PD, MSA and DLB.  
 
In vitro, fibril growth conditions have been demonstrated to strongly affect -synuclein fibril 
morphology. These conditions include buffer composition, pH and temperature, among others26. 
For instance, Bousset, L et al., 2013 reported two different fibril structures when using two 
different buffers that differed in salt content: while the aggregates that were formed in higher salt 
concentrations resulted in twisted cylindrical fibrils, the ones formed in lower salt conditions had 
a flat, untwisted aspect.

2.4 α-synuclein seed amplification assays 
 
In amyloid diseases, generally, once the first aggregates are formed it is extremely challenging to 
inhibit or reverse the aggregation process19. Therefore, to fully elucidate the mechanistical 
properties of PD´s amyloid fibril formation and to be able to apply such to a clinical setting, α-
synuclein aggregation needs to be studied in vitro in the absence but also in the presence of 
aggregates, which are characterized and will be referred to in this study as (pre-formed) seeds. As 
==PAGE:15==
15 
 
mentioned in section 1.3 “Parkinson’s Disease diagnosis”, biochemical tests are the focus of many 
research groups that work towards developing robust and efficient PD diagnosis tools. In this 
study, the biochemical technique of focus is seed amplification assays (SAAs).  
 
α-synuclein SAAs can be defined as biochemical laboratory techniques used to study the 
formation and growth of α-synuclein aggregates in the presence of seeds. Seeds act as templates 
for the addition of monomers through rates significantly higher than de novo formation of fibrils 
through nucleation, accelerating the process of aggregation27. Amplification of such seeds in vitro, 
in the presence of α-synuclein monomer, can be monitored through kinetic studies, which allow 
to determine the rate constants for the microscopic events, explained in section 2.2, that increase 
the overall fibril mass19.  
 
As mentioned earlier, the kinetics of de novo α-synuclein aggregation can be described as a 
sigmoidal time course, described as an initial lag phase, followed by an exponential phase and 
finalized by a plateau phase, shown in Figure 8. However, by adding seeds in a SAA reaction it 
can be substantially accelerated as these act as catalysts for fibril aggregation, substantially 
decreasing or fully overcoming the initial lag time22, also shown in Figure 8. Moreover, there are 
other factors which can alter the length of the lag phase and fibril growth rates. Some of these 
include solution conditions, pH and temperature, among others26, some of such will be discussed 
in the results rection.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure 8 – α-synuclein’s aggregation into amyloid fibrils. Aggregation profiles of α-synuclein 
in the presence (dark blue trace) and absence (light blue trace) of seeds. Reactions are illustrated 
with an excess of α-synuclein monomer with respect to seeds. When seeds are present, they 
immediately start to grow (4) though binding of monomers, shortening or avoiding the lag phase. 
Elongated fibrils with an affinity of monomer to bind to their surface can promote secondary 
nucleation (5). When all available monomer has been converted to fibrils the reaction reaches a 
plateau. In the absence of seeds, primary nucleation (1-3) is the rate-limiting step and leads to a 
prolonged lag phase28. 
 
==PAGE:16==
16 
 
Being able to exhaustively explain the mechanistic steps contributing to the aggregation of α-
synuclein monomers into amyloid fibrils is necessary for understanding its origin and 
proliferation in the different synucleopathies. In a clinical setting, SAA could be used in PD, but 
also other synucleopathies, for diagnosis and monitoring as they allow the detection of minimal 
amounts of α-synuclein aggregates in a specific sample through its amplification22. Concretely, 
the application of SAAs holds the potential to enable quantitative determination of seed content 
in a specific sample of unknown composition. This capability is not only valuable for diagnosing 
PD but also for accurately assessing disease progression.  
 
Nevertheless, despite the immense potential of SAA, these face substantial difficulties, the main 
one being the immense challenge to obtain reproducible kinetic data for α-synuclein aggregation 
in vitro19. This supposes a substantial challenge when comparing studies conducted by different 
groups, laboratories, or even utilizing different equipment. Moreover, as SAA are based on 
indirect measurements of -synuclein aggregate mass, these rely on inferring aggregate content 
from observable parameters, such as thioflavin-T fluorescence. Therefore, these might not capture 
the full complexity of the aggregation process, potentially leading to limitations in data 
interpretation and analysis.  
 
 
All in all, it is of most significance to conduct exhaustive and comprehensive investigations to 
optimize SAAs and overcome the aforementioned challenges. To that end, in this study we aim 
to undertake a systematic exploration of the influence of diverse conditions on the kinetic 
parameters of seeded aggregation, providing insights on the mechanistic aspects contributing to 
-synuclein fibril formation. Through the research carried out in this study we aspire to make 
meaningful advances to the SAA field, ultimately contributing to the development of an accurate 
tool for diagnosis and monitoring of PD and other synucleopathies.

MATERIALS AND METHODS 
 
1. α-synuclein seed fibril formation  
Seed fibrils were produced in two different buffers, referred to in this study as “buffer A” and 
“buffer B” (see composition in Table 2). In such, 200 M of -synuclein monomer was added 
and incubated at pH 7.5 for 7 days, at 37 ºC under shaking conditions (1200 rpm) using 
Thermomixer C (Eppendorf). The fibrils were stored in Eppendorf tubes at -20 ºC until needed. 
-synuclein monomer was provided by DTU’s Bioengineering laboratory. The protein was 
obtained by expressing pT7-7 plasmid carrying the respective gene (Addgene plasmid 
#36046; http://n2t.net/addgene:36046) in E. coli BL21 (DE3). The transformed cells were grown 
in LB media with ampicillin and induced with IPTG. After harvesting the cells, they were 
resuspended, sonicatred and treated with benzonase to remove DNA. The cell-free extract was 
boiled and heat-precipitated proteins were removed. -synuclein was then precipitated using 
ammonium sulfate ((NH4)2SO4) and dialyzed. Anion exchange chromatography and size 
exclusion chromatography were performed to obtain the monomeric fraction of the protein. 
Expression and purification of -synuclein falls out of the scope of this project, this set of 
procedures were executed by another researcher within the Protein Biophysics group.

2. Study of fibril length distribution by AFM  
Seed fibril solutions were diluted to 10 M in Milli-Q water and 10 L of the solution was 
deposited on Muscosevite Mica sheets (Electron Microscopy Sciences, Pennsylvania, USA), 
rinsed with Milli-Q water, and left to dry at 25 ºC. Once dry, AFM images of the samples were 
taken using a DriveAFM atomic force microscope (Nanosurf, Switzerland) in tapping mode in 
air, using a PPP-NCLAuD-10 scanning probe (Nanosensors, Germany). Length distributions 
were extracted with an in-house written Python code (shown in Section 1 of the Appendix) and 
fibril length was manually selected using the Gwyddion (Version 2.62) software.

3. Study of fibril size by dynamic light scattering 
Seed (apparent) hydrodynamic radius was determined through dynamic light scattering (DLS), 
using the Prometheus Panta (NanoTemper, Germany) and standard grade capillaries 
(NanoTemper, Germany). The excitation power of the laser was automatically set by the software. 
The DLS measurements were performed automatically by the instrument at 25 ºC.  
DLS is a useful tool for studying the diffusive behavior of molecules, such as proteins and 
polymers, in solution. Concretely, DLS measures the Brownian motion of particles in dispersion 
and therefore allows to correlate the diffusion coefficient of a molecule or particle under study 
with its correspondent hydrodynamic radius, which depends on its size and shape30.  The 
scattering intensity scales with R6 31, which leads to very intense scattering from large particles 
that can mask the scattering stemming from monomers.  
 
 
4. Study of seeds by flow-induced dispersion analysis  
 
Flow-induced dispersion analysis (FIDA) using FIDA 1 (FidaBio, Denmark)  was used in this 
study with two different purposes: seed size determination and separation of monomer from 
fibrils. These are possible as the FIDA technique allows to separate molecules based on two 
different phenomena: (1) the Taylor dispersion theory, which considers the flow characteristics 
of a fluid and the diffusion of solute molecules, focusing on the idea that solute molecules in the 
fluid move at different speeds32. (2) Size-dependent diffusivity based on Brownian motion, which 
considers the random movement of particles resulting from molecular collisions in a fluid, 
disregarding external flow. It is through the Stokes-Einstein equation33 (Equation 5) that the 
hydrodynamic radius can be correlated to the diffusion coefficient of a certain particle33.

2. Wash 2 (milli-Q water): 45 s, 3500 mbar. 
3. Equilibration (A or B Buffer): 30 s, 3500 mbar. 
4. Sample application (-synuclein seed sample): 20 s, 1500 mbar. 
5. Measurement and detection (A or B Buffer): 30 min, 50mbar  
To separate -synuclein fibrils from -synuclein monomer the following protocol was used:  
1. Wash 1 (1M NaOH): 45 s, 3500 mbar. 
2. Wash 2 (milli-Q water): 45 s, 3500 mbar. 
3. Equilibration (A or B Buffer): 30 s, 3500 mbar. 
4. Sample application (-synuclein seed sample): 20 s, 1500 mbar. 
5. Measurement and detection (A or B Buffer): 75 s, 1500 mbar.  
All experiments were conducted at 25 ºC. The fibril’s intrinsic fluorescence was used for 
detection.  
When using the FIDA technique to determine the hydrodynamic radius of particles, three different 
profiles can be observed, each corresponding to a different range of particle sizes. The profiles 
are summarized below, starting from larger particle sizes and progressing to smaller ones.  
 
1) Profiles with spikes (Figure 10 A): These profiles are observed in samples containing 
significantly large particles. Taylor dispersion immediately separates large particles, 
which are unable to diffuse inside the capillary, resulting in such being individually 
detected. This gives rise to spikes in the intensity distribution profile.  
 
2) Profiles with a rapid increase followed by a prolonged shoulder (Figure 10 B): This 
profile is observed when particle size is small enough to not be detected as individual 
==PAGE:20==
20 
 
particles, while being too big to diffuse inside the capillary.  This type of intensity 
distribution maps the parabolic flow profile of the FIDA setup. The rapid increase in 
signal corresponds to particles located in the middle of the flow, which are rapidly 
transported to the detector. In contrast, particles located at the edges of the flow have a 
longer transit time, leading to the prolonged shoulder in the profile. Similar to the profile 
detailed in (1), this type neither allows particle size determination.  
 
3) Gaussian profiles (Figure 10 C): Gaussian peaks are observed when particles are small 
and homogeneous enough to freely diffuse through the capillary. A homogeneous 
solution that diffuses by Brownian motion results in a Gaussian curve that represents the 
distribution of flow velocities. These peaks do allow size determination.

5. Kinetic measurements of aggregation 
The kinetics of aggregation was monitored through Thioflavin-T (ThT) fluorescence assays. ThT 
is a benzothiazole dye that displays enhanced fluorescence upon binding to amyloid, as it has 
been shown to bind to -sheets, and it is consequently a useful tool for monitorization of 
aggregation kinetics in vitro. In the presence of fibrils, ThT shows an excitation maximum of 450 
nm and enhanced emission at 482 nm, while unbound ThT is not fluorescent at the mentioned 
wavelengths18. In our kinetic assays, excitation and emission wavelengths were set to 440 nm and 
480 nm, respectively.  
In the experiments performed in this study, ThT was used at 50 M. The increase of ThT 
fluorescence was measured in non-binding, black, flat clear-bottom, polystyrene 384-well plates 
(Corning, USA) using a FLUOstar Omega microplate reader (BMG Labtech, Germany) in bottom 
reading mode. Experiments were performed under quiescent conditions, with the plates sealed 
with adhesive sealing sheets (Thermo Scientific, UK) and at 25 ºC.  
==PAGE:21==
21 
 
Concretely, our approach was based on the systematic variation in the quantities of seeds, 
monomer concentration and solution conditions. Seed concentrations varied depending on the 
desired experiment conditions, ranging from 1 pM to 1 M (in monomer equivalents), as well as 
monomer concentrations, which varied from 10 M to 40 M. The rest of reaction components 
varied depending on the aim of the experiment and are detailed in the sections below.   
 
5.1 Study of pH’s effect on aggregation kinetics 
 
Experiments were conducted at two different pH, 7.4 and 2.5, to study pH’s effect on aggregation 
kinetics. Reactions contained all components mentioned above. Additionally, in experiments 
performed at pH 7.4, 20 mM of phosphate buffer (pH 7.4) and 150 mM of sodium chloride (NaCl) 
were added to the reactions. On the other hand, for experiments performed at pH 2.5, 40 mM of 
citrate buffer (pH 2.5) and 250 mM of sodium sulfate (Na2SO4) were added to the reactions. 
The choice of the two pH conditions used in this study was not arbitrary. We selected pH values 
to promote the mechanisms of interest in each experiment. For studies focused on elongation-
dominated aggregation, neutral pH (pH 7.4) was used, as in previous studies such as Buell, AK et 
al., 2014, it was shown to promote elongation. Alternatively, for experiments where secondary 
nucleation was the mechanism of study, pH 2.5 was used. In previous literature, including Buell, 
AK et al., 2014, acidic pH (pH 5) was demonstrated to significantly accelerate secondary 
nucleation. However, previous experiment conducted in our laboratory revealed that pH 2.5 
consistently lead to more reproducible secondary nucleation sigmoidal curves compared to pH 5 
(data not shown). For this reason, when aiming to study secondary nucleation, pH 2.5 was the 
condition of choice.   
 
5.2 Study of seed type’s effect on aggregation kinetics 
 
Two different seed polymorphs were used in the kinetic assays, formed in Buffer A (higher salt 
concentration) and Buffer B (lower salt concentration), see “1. -synuclein seed fibril formation” 
for details about composition. These seeds will be referred to in this study as A seeds and B seeds, 
respectively.  
 
5.3 Study of seed size’s effect on aggregation kinetics 
 
Three different seed sizes were used in the kinetic experiments, these being the resultant of the 
following sonication protocols: (1) non-sonicated, (2) sonicated for 20 minutes using a Vial 
Tweeter sonicator (Hielscher, Germany), 100% amplitude, 1 second on and 1 second off, these 
will be referred to as Vial Tweeter-sonicated seeds, and (3) sonicated for 1 minute effective using 
a probe sonicator (Hielscher, Germany), 20% amplitude, 3 seconds on and 12 seconds off, will 
be referred to as probe-sonicated seeds.  
 
==PAGE:22==
22 
 
5.4 Study of cerebrospinal fluid’s effect on aggregation kinetics 
Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) is a clear, colorless fluid that surrounds and protects the brain and 
spinal cord. It is mainly made up of electrolytes, such as Na+, Cl- and HCO3-, glucose, different 
vitamins, peptides and proteins (mainly albumin)36.   
To study the influence of CSF on the kinetic parameters of -synuclein aggregation, a series of 
samples were prepared as described earlier, with varying concentrations of CSF. Specifically, 
CSF concentrations of 0%, 2.5%, 5%, 10%, 20% and 40% were added to the samples for analysis.  
CSF used in this study has been provided by the company Lundbeck. 
 
6. Kinetic data analysis 
 
The data obtained from the ThT assays was normalized. The normalization process varied 
depending on the availability of FIDA data at the endpoint of the experiment. When FIDA data 
was available and indicated complete conversion of monomer to fibril (i.e., no detectable 
monomer in the sample), data was normalized between initial fibril concentration and final fibril 
concentration (in monomer equivalent). Alternatively, when FIDA data was not available for 
experimental limitations (e.g., experiments involving CSF, as it protein content could hinder fibril 
measurement), data was normalized through an alternative process. Concretely, the ThT 
fluorescence profiles were normalized between the highest and lowest fluorescence values. This 
normalization allowed to establish a relationship between fluorescence intensity and fibril mass. 
Specifically, the highest fluorescence value was designated as the maximum fibril mass 
concentration value (11 M), while the lowest fluorescence value was assigned as the minimum 
fibril mass concentration value (1 M). On a third case, non-seeded reactions were normalized 
between initial and final fibril concentration in monomer equivalent (0 M and 10 M, 
respectively).  
 
For elongation rate analysis, profiles were plotted and initial linear increase of signal was 
manually selected and linearly fitted.  Slopes (apparent elongation rate) were determined using 
Origin 2023 (OriginLab Corporation, Northampton, MA).  Apparent elongation rates were plotted 
against monomer concentration to obtain the elongation rate constant (further details in  Results 
section 3 “Effect of seed size on fibril aggregation profile”).  
 
The initial fibril number (P0) was determined by dividing the average fibril length values obtained 
from the AFM analysis for the different types of seeds (non-sonicated, vial tweeter-sonicated and 
probe-sonicated) by the average monomer length (obtained from RCSB Protein Data Bank, 
protein codes 6SST and 6SSX), which is 0.458 nm. This calculation enabled the determination of 
average number of monomer per fibrils and thus obtain the value of P0.  
 
For the analysis of secondary nucleation rates, aggregation profiles were plotted and fitted using 
the Amylofit amyloid aggregation fitting online program18. Amylofit facilitated the identification 
of the underlying molecular mechanisms dominating aggregation based on the ThT profiles, 
enabling the determination of secondary nucleation rate constants.

1. Effect of buffer conditions on seed morphology  
 
Seeds were formed, as described in Materials and Methods, in two different buffer conditions: 
Buffer A (A seeds, formed in high salt concentrations) and Buffer B (B seeds, formed in low salt 
concentrations). These had been previously described in Bousset, L et al., 2013 to have significant 
morphological differences. Concretely, A fibrils were described as twisted cylindrical fibrils, 
while B fibrils were described as ribbons, with a flat untwisted aspect29.  
 
In this study, to evaluate their differences in morphology, images of the two polymorphs were 
obtained though atomic force microscopy (AFM) (shown in Figure 9). Fibrils were manually 
selected and profiles were extracted, height and pitch lengths (distance of a full 360 degree 
rotation of the fibril) were analyzed using the in-house written Python script, found in Section 1 
of the Appendix. Through the mentioned script, we obtained the fibril’s heights (diameter), which 
were for A and B fibrils 8.32 ± 1.17 and 6.59 ± 0.87, respectively. On the same line, A fibrils 
pitch lengths were 204.37 ± 62.69 nm. 
 
Using the mentioned Python script, a pitch length value for B fibrils was obtained (116.90 ± 
27.24). However, we suspected that this result might be influenced by the criterion set in the script 
for defining a twist, which could be less stringent than desired. As a result, small irregularities in 
the fibril profile could be mistakenly identified as twists. To confirm this hypothesis, ten different 
fibril profiles from A and B fibrils’ AFM images were individually graphed (examples are shown 
in Figure 9 A) and manually analyzed to determine pitch lengths. The obtained result for A fibrils 
pitch length was 197.08  ± 53.79  . However, by observing the graphed fibril profile in Figure 9 
A, B fibrils did not show any twist pattern, and therefore pitch lengths could not be determined. 
This result correlates with the previously suggested idea, indicating a lack of stringiness in the 
definition of twists of the in-house Python script.  
 
Nevertheless, the differences in morphology and arrangement between A and B fibrils are evident 
in Figure 9. These not only differ in morphology, where A fibrils clearly show the pitches while 
B fibrils do not, but also arrangement, where B fibrils notably exhibit larger constructs and greater 
tendency to clump together. These observations highlight the divergent characteristics and 
aggregation behavior of A and B fibrils, underscoring the importance of understanding their 
unique properties in the context of the study.

3. Effect of seed size on fibril aggregation profile  
 
To evaluate fibril elongation profiles, kinetic experiments were performed at two different pH 
values: 7.4 and 2.5, using 1 M seed and monomer concentrations 10 M, 20 M or 40M. As 
mentioned in Materials and Methods, the choice of these two pH conditions is not arbitrary. These 
have been selected as they have been reported to promote elongation (pH 7.4)19 and secondary 
nucleation (pH 2.5). 
 
Data from all experiments was normalized between the values of 1 M and 11 M, 21 M  or 41 
M fibril mass, depending on the used monomer concentration. The normalization of the 
aggregation profiles relies on the analysis of samples at the experiment’s endpoint using FIDA. 
Such analysis revealed the absence of detectable amounts of monomers in the samples, indicating 
that complete conversion into fibrils had occurred. Therefore, normalization was necessary to 
analyze and compare the aggregation profiles accurately, ensuring the data reflected the relative 
differences in fibril formation among samples. For this reason, ThT signal was converted to fibril 
concentration in the profiles shown in Figure 16 (see raw data in Section 4 of the Appendix).   
==PAGE:31==
31 
 
 
Fibril aggregation profiles of A fibrils at pH 7.4 conditions are depicted in Figure 16 A. These 
profiles exhibit a notable pattern characterized by an initial linear increase in fibril concentration. 
The observed behavior suggests an initial phase dominated by elongation, where fibril 
concentration steadily rises over time. The difference slopes in such linear phases provides 
information about the kinetics of fibril growth of the three different types of seeds. Probe-
sonicated seeds exhibit the most pronounced and rapid increase in fibril mass during the initial 
elongation phase, followed by the vial tweeter-sonicated seed and lastly, the non-sonicated seeds. 
Therefore, the observed trend suggests a qualitatively correlation between seed length and 
elongation rate, with shorter seeds showing a more accelerated fibril growth. The relationship 
between seed length and elongation rate will be further quantitatively explored and analyzed later 
in this section.  
 
After the mentioned initial linear increase in fibril mass, fibrils A at pH 7.4 exhibit a subsequent 
decrease in their growth rate. This decline in growth rate implies the transition towards a plateau 
phase of the aggregation profile, where the reaction is approaching a state where the dissociation 
rate and the elongation rate are equal. This phenomenon suggests that the availability of 
monomers for fibril formation is becoming limited, leading to the observed gradual deceleration 
in aggregation rate.  
 
On the other hand, aggregation profiles of B fibrils at pH 7.4 are also shown in Figure 16 A. 
Similar to A fibrils at the same pH, B fibrils exhibit an initial linear increase in fibril concentration 
followed by a plateau phase. These profiles provide evidence that B fibrils also undergo an 
elongation dominated aggregation process at pH 7.4. However, it is worth mentioning that the 
data obtained for these fibrils shows less homogeneity, which could be a potential result of the 
fibrils having higher propensity to clump.  
 
Neither A nor B fibrils show an initial lag phase at pH 7.4, indicating the efficiency of these seeds 
to rapidly elongate as soon as they come to contact with monomer. In fact, an interesting 
observation is the ability of B fibrils to aggregate in high salt concentrations, despite being formed 
in low salt conditions.  
 
 
 
Fibril aggregation profiles of A fibrils at pH 2.5 are depicted in Figure 16 B. In contrast to A 
fibrils at pH 7.4, non-sonicated A fibrils at pH 2.5 exhibit an easily recognizable initial lag phase, 
which spans approximately 18 hours. Interestingly, such lag phase is not observed in the 
aggregation profiles of either vial tweeter- or probe-sonicated fibrils’ aggregation profiles. This 
discrepancy could be attributed to the observation that, at pH 2.5, fibrils tend to precipitate, as a 
consequence of the formation of clumps. Consequently, the absence of a lag phase in the sonicated 
fibrils suggest that sonication assists in dissolving the clumps, hence releasing free fibril ends for 
elongation. Notably, the aggregation profiles of vial tweeter- and probe-sonicated fibrils exhibit 
an initial elongation-dominated phase, emphasizing the role of sonication or seed size reduction 
in promoting fibril elongation.  
 
Following the observed lag phase, non-sonicated fibrils demonstrated a rapid and pronounced 
increase in fibril concentration. However, this rapid increase is unlikely to be attributed to an 
elongation-dominated aggregation phase. Instead, it is indicative of a secondary nucleation-
==PAGE:32==
32 
 
dominated mechanism occurring on the fibrils’ surface. Similarly, in the aggregation profiles of 
vial tweeter- and probe-sonicated A fibrils a clear distinction of two different phases can be 
observed. The initial phase corresponds to the elongation-dominated process and the second one 
corresponding to a secondary nucleation-dominated phase, as it has been previously reported that 
secondary nucleation  is enhanced at acidic pHs19. The presence of the latter phase suggests a 
significant divergence in the mechanistic growth process between pH 2.5 and pH 7.4. These 
results further support the pH-dependence of fibril formation, as solution conditions show to have 
a crucial role in influencing the kinetics of A fibril aggregation.  
 
Finally, Figure 16 B also depicts fibril aggregation profiles of B fibrils at pH 2.5. These exhibit 
an initial lag phase which are, for non-sonicated, vial tweeter- and probe-sonicated seeds, 19 
hours, 18 hours and 14 hours, respectively. These results suggest a decreasing trend in the duration 
of the lag phase with sonication. However, a t-test analysis using a 95% confidence interval 
indicated that the observed differences were not statistically significant. Similar to A fibrils, B 
fibrils at pH 2.5 also exhibited a tendency to clump under acidic conditions, which correlates with 
the presence of the mentioned lag phase. Following the lag phase, all B fibril profiles displayed a 
sharp increase in fibril concentration, indicating aggregation through a secondary nucleation-
dominated reaction, which is known to be favored at low pH concentrations.  
 
In conclusion, our findings align with the observations reported by Buell, AK et al., 2014, which 
highlighted distinct kinetic behaviors of fibril aggregation under different pH conditions. Firstly, 
at pH 7.4, we observed an elongation-dominated phase, consistent with the preferential increase 
on fibril concentration by monomer addition to fibrils. Contrarily, at pH 2.5 fibril aggregation 
mainly followed a secondary nucleation-dominated pathway. Hence, acidic pH promotes the 
formation of nuclei on the surface of preexisting fibrils, enhancing the importance of secondary 
nucleation on the fibril aggregation process. The study of the mentioned secondary nucleation-
dominated process will be further explored in the section “5. Effect of seed size on secondary 
nucleation rates”.

5. Effect of human CSF on elongation rates 
 
CSF is a biofluid that surrounds the brain and spinal cord, directly interacting with the central 
nervous system36, offering insights into neurodegenerative diseases like PD. For this reason, 
several groups have proposed CSF as a promising sample for quantifying -synuclein levels and 
determine disease state 39. However, the complex composition of CSF, which includes molecules 
such as proteins and electrolytes, requires a thorough study of its effect on seed amplification 
assays as it may influence aggregation kinetics. In fact, recent studies have provided evidence 
highlighting the inhibitory effect of CSF lipoproteins, manly high-density lipoprotein (HDL) and 
low-density lipoprotein (LDL), on -synuclein aggregation40. Interestingly, even at sub-
physiological concentrations, the mentioned lipoproteins show inhibitory activity in -synuclein 
aggregation.  
 
To study the potential inhibitory effect of  human CSF (hCSF) in the apparent elongation rate, a 
systematic variation of hCSF content in the kinetic reactions has been performed. 0, 2.5, 5, 10, 20 
and 40%  hCSF has been added to kinetic reactions that used probe-sonicated A seeds at pH 7.4 
and 10 M monomer. The reactions have been followed by ThT fluorescence and the resulting 
profiles can be observed in Figure 20 A (see raw data in Section 5 of the Appendix).  
ThT profiles were normalized between the highest and lowest fluorescence values. This 
normalization allowed to establish a relationship between fluorescence intensity and fibril mass. 
Concretely, the highest fluorescence value was designated as the maximum fibril mass 
concentration value (11 M), while the lowest fluorescence value was assigned as the minimum 
fibril mass concentration value (1 M).  
Apparent elongation rates have been calculated and are shown in Figure 20 B. As can be observed, 
the highest elongation rate correlates to the sample without hCSF. Furthermore, a sequential 
reduction of the apparent elongation rates can be observed from 0 % hCSF to 5 % hCSF. 
Concretely, apparent elongation rate reaches its minimum in samples with a hCSF content of 5% 
or higher. The observed inhibitory effect aligns with the findings reported by Bellomo, G et al., 
2023. Interestingly, the minimal apparent elongation rate values are not exactly zero; rather, they 
are approximately 0.05 M/h, this result will be further discussed in the Discussion section.

6. Effect of seed size on secondary nucleation rates 
 
To evaluate the effect of seed size on secondary nucleation rates, kinetic experiments were 
performed at pH 2.5, using seed concentrations ranging from 1 pM to 1 nM and 10 M -
synuclein monomer. The resulting data was analyzed using Amylofit, an amyloid aggregation 
fitting software which allows to determine the underlying molecular mechanisms dominating 
aggregation from the ThT time profiles18.  
 
Considering that the used experimental conditions (pH 2.5) are known to favor secondary 
nucleation (as explained in Section 3 “Effect of seed size on fibril aggregation profiles”), the data 
obtained from the kinetic experiments was fitted using a secondary nucleation-dominated model. 
In a first fitting attempt, profiles for every sonication type were individually fitted. The following 
parameters were used as initial values:  
 
- 
Initial monomer concentration (m0) was set as a global constant with value of 10 M.  
 
- 
Initial aggregate number (P0) varied for every seed concentration, it was set as a constant and 
the used values are shown in Table 3. Value for non-seeded reaction was 0.  
 
- 
Initial aggregate concentration (M0) varied for every seed concentration, it was set as a 
constant and values ranged from  1 pM to 1 nM.  
 
- 
Nucleation rate constant (kn) was set as a global constant, with a value of 10-6 M-1 h-1. This 
value was as an approximation to negligible nucleation rate constant, as Amylofit program 
can encounter difficulties in fitting the data when setting it to zero. For this reason, a small 
non-zero value was used as a nucleation rate constant.  
 
- 
Primary nucleus size (nc) was set as a global constant with value of 2. The selection of two as 
the primary nucleus size is a commonly adopted practice in the field18. 
 
- 
Secondary nucleus size (n2) was set as a global constant with value of 2. The selection of two 
as the secondary nucleus size is a commonly adopted practice in the field18. 
 
- 
Elongation rate constant (k+) was set as a global constant with a value of 2.84 · 106 M-1 h-1. 
This value was obtained from the experiments performed at pH 7.4 detailed in Section 4 
“Effect of seed size on elongation rate”.

DISCUSSION 
Parkinson’s disease (PD), multiple system atrophy (MSA) and dementia with lewy bodies (DLB) 
are diseases characterized by the presence of abnormal misfolded -synuclein aggregates and are 
named synucleopathies. Currently, synucleopathies do not dispose of efficient diagnostic 
techniques that allow early diagnosis and disease progression prediction. For this reason, different 
research lines, including this study, are dedicated to develop more robust diagnostic tools and 
techniques. To that end, in this study we conduct a comprehensive and highly quantitative analysis 
of -synuclein aggregation through seed amplification assays (SAAs). We believe that, by deeply 
understanding the microscopic events involved in -synuclein aggregation and their response to 
different conditions, we can take a significant step towards to the ultimate goal of applying SAAs 
for PD diagnosis in a real clinical setting.  
 
 
Most published studies exploring -synuclein SAAs employ vigorous shaking conditions41,42, 
leading to a fragmentation-dominated mechanism of fibril aggregation. In contrast, DTU’s 
Protein Biophysics group is in the process of developing and optimizing quiescent SAAs. By 
allowing the reaction to occur under quiescent conditions, other microscopic events such as 
elongation and secondary nucleation take dominance in the aggregation process. For this reason, 
study of elongation and secondary nucleation rate constants have been subjects of study in this 
report, as well as the effect of different seed morphologies and solution conditions in the 
aggregation of -synuclein.  
 
 
In this work, we have first determined the morphological differences in two different types of 
seed fibrils, A and B, formed in different salt concentration conditions using AFM imaging. 
Through this analysis, we have confirmed the findings published by Bousset, L et al., 2013, 
affirming that A seeds, formed under high salt concentration, exhibit a twisted cylindrical shape 
with a pitch length of 204.37 ± 62.69 nm. In contrast, B seeds, formed under low salt 
concentration, display a flat and untwisted morphology. Our observations align with the 
mentioned in previous studies, providing further support to the effect of salt concentration on seed 
morphology.  
 
 
Being able to identify morphological differences has significant clinical relevance, as fibrils 
causing different synucleopathies have been reported to have different morphologies26. Different 
-synuclein polymorphs arising from the same precursor protein have shown disease-specific 
biochemical properties and the ability to induce different pathological phenotypes, as stated in 
Mehra, S. et al., 2021. On the same line, in another study published in Shahnawaz, M. et al., 2020, 
it was demonstrated through protein misfolding cyclic amplification (PMCA) that fibrils derived 
from PD and MSA not only had distinct morphologies but also differences in seeding abilities43. 
This finding holds great significance when and if, in a hypothetical future, we aim to expand our 
work with SAAs from PD to other synucleopathies.  
 
 
 
==PAGE:44==
44 
 
In addition to the morphological characteristics analysis, seed-length was a studied parameter for 
A and B seeds that had undergone different sonication protocols, these being: (1) non-sonicated, 
(2) vial tweeter-sonicated for 20 minutes and (3) probe-sonicated for 1 minute effective. Such 
study was conducted using three techniques: AFM imaging, DLS and FIDA. Generally, the results 
obtained for A seeds consistently indicate that probe-sonicated seeds are the smallest and most 
homogeneous, while non-sonicated and vial tweeter-sonicated samples exhibit wider ranges of 
lengths and higher deviations. This fact aligns with our expectations, since probe-sonication, 
known for its higher efficiency in energy transfer and cavitation intensity compared to vial 
tweeter-sonication, proves to be a more effective method for material disruption. Conclusively, 
when aiming for short-sized homogeneous A seeds, probe-sonication would be the preferred 
technique. Alternatively, the analysis B seed length distribution presented additional challenges 
and unexpected discrepancies between the AFM and the DLS results, indicating the presence of 
human errors in the measurement of fibrils using AFM images. This observation highlights an 
inherent limitation associated with AFM fibril measurements, as the manual selection of fibrils 
introduces uncertainty regarding the reliability of the data, especially when working with 
heterogeneous samples. In both types of seed samples, DLS was determined to be the most limited 
technique when measuring seeds of the types and length ranges used in this study. In the scope of 
this work, DLS technique determined seed lengths to be notably bigger than the other techniques, 
due to a disproportionate scattering of the light intensity due to big aggregates and clumps in the 
samples. FIDA analysis further confirmed the overestimation of fibril size through DLS, but 
provides inconclusively size information for most samples. Overall, these findings affirm the 
challenges and limitations in accurately determining seed size through the different techniques 
and emphasize the need for careful data acquisition and interpretation. All in all, despite the 
inherent challenges and the possibility of human error, AFM imaging can be determined as the 
most suitable approach for determining the seed length of the specific seeds used in this study. 
While acknowledging the potential for human error, AFM has demonstrated its usefulness in 
length distribution determination in the scope of this work. However, it is important to admit that  
DLS and FIDA techniques could be significantly useful in the determination of seed size of 
smaller and more homogeneous fibril samples. 
 
 
Going further into the differences between seeds of distinct sizes and morphologies, we studied 
their effect on -synuclein’s aggregation profile using ThT fluorescence assays. Such were 
performed in two different pH conditions, 7.4 and 2.5. By closely examining the behavior of the 
seeds at different pH values, valuable insights of fibril aggregation behavior on different  
intracellular environments could be acquired. Considering the substantial pH variation across 
cellular compartments, ranging from the cytosol (neutral pH) to the lysosomes (acidic pH). 
Consequently, the findings derived from this study could elucidate distinct mechanistic properties 
of aggregation within different cellular compartments. Besides the biological meaning, the use of 
the mentioned pH values allow to have a certain degree of control over the dominant microscopic 
mechanisms of the aggregation reactions. As previous research by Buell, AK et al., 2014 
indicated, -synuclein aggregation at neutral pH is dominated by fibril elongation, while acidic 
pH enhances and promotes secondary nucleation by a four orders of magnitude. However, in this 
study we have extended the investigation to even lower pH, specifically 2.5, based on preliminary 
findings within our research group. These indicated that lower pH values offer the advantage of 
obtaining more homogeneous secondary nucleation data. The reason why acidic pHs promote 
secondary nucleation is related with the dramatic change in charge of the C-terminal residues of 
==PAGE:45==
45 
 
-synuclein, which are not present in the fibril core. This change in electrostatic properties is 
likely to promote surface-catalyzed nucleation events on the fibrils27.  
 
The mentioned tendency of certain microscopic mechanisms to dominate -synuclein 
aggregation was corroborated in our results. Kinetic profiles from A and B seeds at pH 7.4 showed 
an elongation-dominated profile, including an initial linear increase of fibril mass concentration 
followed by a plateau phase. We demonstrated that initial elongation rate increases with 
decreasing seed size, obtaining the higher results in apparent elongation rates from probe-
sonicated seeds. However, it is important to note that the elongation rates of fibril formation are 
proportional to the monomer concentration only at low monomer concentrations. In contrast, at 
high monomer concentrations, the elongation rate constant saturates and becomes monomer 
independent. Therefore, it would be interesting for future studies to investigate the specific 
monomer concentration at which the elongation rate no longer increases. Nevertheless, the data 
resulting from the mentioned kinetic experiments, allowed us to determine the elongation rate 
constant, k+, to be 2.84 · 106 M-1 h-1. In future studies, quantification of the elongation rate constant 
would be an interesting result to explore, as it will allow comparison of the elongation rate 
between different seed morphologies.   
 
 
At pH 2.5 fibrils exhibited a secondary nucleation-dominated profiles, where the initial linear 
increases observed at pH 7.4 were substituted by really rapid increases in fibril mass concentration 
at both pHs. Before that, in contrast to vial tweeter- and probe-sonicated seed samples, A non-
sonicated seeds showed a lag phase of approximately 18 hours. A possible explanation for this 
could be the fact that in the samples corresponding to assays performed at pH 2.5 two distinct 
phases were observed at the experiment’s endpoint: a dense layer at the bottom, where fibril 
clumps were evident, and a clearer layer on top. The presence of clumps is known to reduce the 
availability of fibril ends for elongation, thus resulting in delayed aggregation process, which can 
be reflected in the appearance of a lag phase. In fact, higher order assemblies have been reported 
to strongly decrease seeding ability19. The absence of a lag phase in the sonicated samples 
suggests that sonication plays an important role in dissolving fibril clumps. Therefore, sonication 
not only increases the overall number of fibrils by fragmenting them but also enhances the 
availability of fibril ends by untangling the clump-like structures. Ultimately, this results in 
sonication facilitating the initiation of fibril growth and promoting a more rapid aggregation 
process. Contrarily, B seeds exhibited a lag phase in all fibril types, with no statistically significant 
differences among them, appearing to be less efficient seeds at pH 2.5. A possible speculative 
reason for this is that, due to their differences in morphology, A seeds have a more suitable surface 
for secondary nucleation than B seeds.  
 
 
In this study, we also aimed to investigate the influence of cerebrospinal fluid, a heterogeneous 
biomolecular fluid, on fibril elongation in SAAs performed at pH 7.4. Quantifying and 
understanding the influence of hCSF in seed amplification assays is of outmost importance, as 
these assays, in a clinical setting, would use patient samples which would be CSF-based. 
Investigating the effect of hCSF in SAAs allowed us to do a comprehensive assessment of its 
impact on an elongation-dominated aggregation reaction. As was expected, our results confirmed 
that hCSF indeed has an inhibitory effect on fibril elongation that increased with increasing hCSF 
concentration, when such were low. As previously reported in Bellomo, G et al., 2023, the 
observed inhibitory effect is mainly attributed to the lipoprotein content of CSF, concretely to 
==PAGE:46==
46 
 
HDL and LDL. These lipoproteins were shown in the mentioned study to directly interact with -
synuclein oligomeric species, while not having any evidence of their interaction with HDL and 
LDL interacting with monomeric -synuclein. Unexpectedly, our study suggested that hCSF’s 
inhibitory effect reached a plateau phase from concentrations of 5%, however, the elongation rate 
throughout this phase was not 0 but 0.05 M/h. This unexpected finding challenges our initial 
assumption, as our expectation was that increasing hCSF percentage would eventually lead to 
complete inhibition of the elongation rate, as it is common for single-process inhibition. Instead, 
and due to the unexpected result, we formulated two preliminary hypotheses to explain this 
observation. Firstly, a possible explanation could be that hCSF may enhance ThT fluorescence, 
specially at higher hCSF concentrations, which would lead to the result of elongation rates 
obtained at 10%, 20% and 40% hCSF. If the mentioned event happened, it would explain the 
sustained signal at elevated hCSF concentration and indicate that the slope of the aggregation 
profiles no longer accurately represents the elongation rate. It is important to acknowledge that 
the impact of hCSF of ThT remains unknown and requires further investigation. However, a 
plausible hypothesis could be that hCSF induces flocculation of fibrils, followed by aggregation 
and sedimentation of the flocs, as some solution conditions have shown this effect in -
synuclein19.  Secondly, in a hypothetical scenario involving simultaneous dual modes of fibril 
growth, it is conceivable that only one of the modes is inhibited, leaving the remaining observed 
elongation rate. However, this proposition is highly speculative since our current understanding 
suggest that elongation does not occur through multiple processes. Nevertheless, it is possible that 
the introduction of hCSF to the reaction triggers other forms of aggregation.  
 
 
Lastly, in this work we also determined the secondary nucleation rate constants, k2, for non-
sonicated, vial tweeter-sonicated and probe-sonicated A seeds. This is, to my knowledge, is the 
first quantified numerical value of secondary nucleation of -synuclein. The obtained values 
obtained when fixing al the other parameters and fitting k2 globally were, 1.56 · 107 M-2 h-1 , 8.09 
· 106 M-2 h-1 and 1.61 · 107 M-2 h-1, for non-sonicated, vial tweeter-sonicated and probe-sonicated 
seeds, respectively. The obtained numerical values exhibit a high degree of similarity, with 
differences of 2-fold, aligning with the successful fitting of the data. We also observed that, when 
introducing an additional global fitting variable (elongation rate constant, k+), allowing for a 
greater system flexibility, the values obtained remained consistent with the previous results, 
despite having a slightly increased divergence of approximately 5-fold. This outcome was 
expected, due to the increased freedom given to the system at the time of fitting. Despite the 
unavailability of directly comparable k2 values, published data by Cohen, S. et al. in 2013 reported 
secondary nucleation rate constant of 3.6 · 107 M-2 h-1 for A protein, an amyloid protein 
associated with Alzheimer’s disease. This results suggest that our obtained k2, despite being 2.7 
times lower, is still within the same order of magnitude. Additionally, the result is consistent with 
the well-known fact that A protein exhibits remarkable efficiency in aggregating through 
secondary nucleation21.  
 
 
Regarding the elongation rate constant results at pH 2.5, a notable observation is the 
approximately 5-fold increase (concretely, 5.13-fold) when comparing the average value of k+ to 
the value obtained at pH 7.4. to the previously obtained value at pH 7.4. This finding aligns 
perfectly with the findings reported by Buell, AK et al., 2014, who reported a 5-fold increase in 
==PAGE:47==
47 
 
elongation rate when comparing aggregation at pH 7.4 to pH 5. Hence, it is evident that even with 
a substantial reduction in pH to 2.5, the enhancement of secondary nucleation remains consistent.  
 
 
While this experiment has provided valuable quantitative results for -synuclein’s k2, further 
investigations that expand the scope of this study could explore the impact of hCSF of the 
secondary elongation rate. Given its significant inhibitory effect on elongation, it would be 
reasonable to expect that hCSF may also influence secondary nucleation.  
 
 
On the whole, this study has provided valuable insights and advancements in understanding -
synuclein aggregation via SAAs using a highly quantitative analysis. The findings presented in 
this study make valuable contributions to the field of -synuclein aggregation research, as they 
have the potential to facilitate the accurate quantification of -synuclein in patient samples of 
unknown content in the future. Such advance could ultimately lead to early and efficient diagnoses 
of Parkinson’s disease and enable tracking of disease progression. Moreover, the insights gained 
into the behavior of different -synuclein polymorphs may also have relevance for extrapolating 
our findings to related disorders like multiple system atrophy and dementia with Lewy bodies.

CONCLUSIONS 
- 
A and B seed fibrils exhibit distinct morphologies.  
 
- 
Sonication leads to a reduction in seed size, with a greater reduction using probe-sonication 
compared to vial tweeter-sonication.  
 
- 
Under quiescent conditions, pH 7.4 promotes -synuclein seed aggregation dominated by 
elongation, while pH 2.5 promotes secondary nucleation-dominated aggregation.  
 
- 
B seeds demonstrate reduced seeding efficiency compared to A seeds at pH 2.5 conditions, 
although they still induce accelerated aggregation compared to de novo reactions.  
 
- 
Elongation rate shows an increase with monomer concentration at low monomer 
concentrations, as well as with decreasing seed size.  
 
- 
Human cerebrospinal fluid exerts an inhibitory effect on -synuclein elongation at pH 7.4.  
 
- 
The elongation rate constant increases approximately 5-fold at pH 2.5 compared to pH 2.5.
