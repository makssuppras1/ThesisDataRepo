Abstract
In the wake of the newest computer-aided design approaches, the levels of dimensional
complexity are constantly reaching new highs. In order to accommodate these advances
in design, the computational methods need to follow. It has been proven that in order
to reach the highest degree of sustainability and structural eﬃciency, engineering prac-
tices need to be introduced into the earliest stages of building design with constructive
analyitical feedback (Negendahl et al., 2016). Based on the concrete Footbridge over
Breiðholtsbraut highway in Iceland, designed by Studio Granda, an in-depth review of
the approaches, methods, and tools used for the initial design stages of the conventional
design is deﬁned, with an emphasis on the use of timber components in the superstruc-
ture of the bridge. In parallel to this, the parametric modelling environment of Rhino’s
Grasshopper is used to construct a generative dynamic algorithm which adds a structural
input into the early design stages. Through the use of visual programming, diﬀerent ap-
proaches of structural optimisation based on simpliﬁed calculation according to Eurocode
standards, are discussed and implemented within generative dynamic algorithms. The
feasibility of the proposed tools and methods, is demonstrated throughout the design
stages, leading to the project proposal, of the timber alternative to the concrete foot-
bridge over Breiðholtsbraut highway.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
The use of parametric modelling in the last decade has created new levels of complexity
within architectural design. By using parametrically driven geometry, designers are able
to explore endless varieties of geometric proposals and envision shapes, which would
seem impossible with conventional design methods. At the same time, modern climate
trends and increased environmental awareness dictate, that it is now more than ever
necessary to construct eﬃcient and sustainable structures. The major decisions about
both the geometry as well as function (and consequently eﬃciency) of the structure, are
made in the earliest stages of design, where the knowledge of the structure is usually very
generalised. The design in this stage is necessarily subject to rapid and sometimes drastic
changes. The consequences of these have a profound impact on the subsequent stages of
the project, the extent of which, is not always known in advance (Rolvink et al., 2014).
Structural eﬃciency however, is a multidisciplinary problem, which is best utilised
with early cooperation of engineering practices. Using integrated design processes, con-
siderations can, for example, be made, about the indoor climate and energy consumption
of the structure from the earliest design stages (Negendahl et al., 2016). Unfortunately,
this approach has not quite found its grip in the ﬁeld of structural analysis. The reason
for this being that structural engineers rely heavily on ﬁnite element method (FEM) cal-
culations in their work. In order to use these, the structure needs to have an advanced
level of deﬁnition.
Using parametric modelling however, it is possible to introduce structural engineering
earlier into the design process. By implementing simpliﬁed structural analysis into the
parametric modelling environment, the designer is able to obtain real time feedback on
the eﬃciency of a design. This can then be incorporated into the domain of form-ﬁnding
optimisation and evolutionary solving algorithms. The latter work on the principle of
using mathematically formulated constraints to converge towards the optimal numerical
solution (Mueller and Ochsendorf, 2013). In other words, the optimal ﬁtness of a chosen
parameter of the design.

Problem statement
Loosely based on the above described principles, the problem statement and the research
topics of the thesis can be deﬁned.
The thesis aims to examine in what extent integrated dynamic models can be imple-
mented into the early-stage design process of a timber footbridge. In order to do so, it is
split into two distinct parts, which take place in parallel and are essentially intertwined
to form the project proposal.
The ﬁrst part of the thesis regards the structural considerations in the design of a
timber footbridge.
This includes an extensive literature study into the methods and
decisive factors that lead to the choice of a structural system. Included, is the iterative
process of formulating project and material related problems and their solutions in the
deﬁnition of the bridge components, their function, and ultimately the structure as a
whole.
The second part regards the implementation of the integrated design tools, with the
goal to aid the designer throughout the design process. In order to do so, the stages of
the ﬁrst part need to be structured in a way where the entire process is clearly outlined.
The main goal of the thesis is answering the following set of questions:
• In what way and extent can parametric modelling environment aid the designer
throughout the design stages leading to a project proposal?
• Is it possible to create a parametric algorithm, which would on the premise of a set
pool of parameters through evolutionary solving approaches automatically arrive
at an optimal structural system solution?
• During which stage, and through which simpliﬁcations, is it feasible to implement
structural analysis into the parametric modelling environment of the project design
stages?
• Are the results of the early-stage structural analysis accurate enough to provide the
designer with constructive answers regarding the feasibility of the design proposal?

3.1
Timber bridges
3.1.1
History of timber bridge engineering
Bridge engineering has been an important aspect of human technological advancement,
enabling the crossing of rivers or other obstacles which would otherwise hinder movement.
Because of its ability to carry both compressive and tensile forces, wood was the ﬁrst and
prevailing material for bridge construction throughout human history. It was available
in abundance, easy to manipulate, process and join into advance forms, but it had the
disadvantage of being prone to degradation. To utilise timber structures to their full
capacity, bridge builders as early as 1st century AD used ox dung, lees and oils, and tar
to protect wood from rapid drying, worms and water respectively (Thelandersson and
Larsen, 2003). Since then, the use of timber for structural purposes has evolved and
improved but being a natural material, structures made of wood always had dimensional
restrictions set by nature imposed on them. With the introduction of man-made materials
such as steel and reinforced concrete, wood lost its role as a competitive structural choice
in Europe and most of the developed world by the 20th century (Sétra, 2007).
Wood-related innovations, however, were still evolving its use and bridging the limi-
tations of the past. In Switzerland and North America, for example, bridges were built
with roofs in order to shield the superstructure from the elements which increased the
life span of some bridges to over a century. In 1831, US, a patent was granted for a wood
preservative based on creosote which would, along with other chemical preservatives, lead
to a new step in timber bridge applications especially in the railway industry, which was
rapidly expanding at the time. The ﬁnal major step in historic wood advancements hap-
pened in the beginning of the 20th century with the ﬁrst patent on an engineered timber
product; more speciﬁcally glued laminated timber, or abbreviated as glulam (Sétra, 2007;
Ritter, 1990).
Even though the patent for glulam was issued in the beginning of the 20th century,
15
==PAGE:24==
it was only in the 1940’s that it was introduced as a bridge building material. This was
due to the rapid decrease in steel prices and the popularity of reinforced concrete, which
were dominating as bridge materials since the beginning of the century. Nevertheless,
engineered timber products were continuously upgraded. New fastening systems, along
with better preservatives and the invention of products such as stress laminated timber
(SLT), meant that timber was reintroduced as a valid alternative in bridge construction
to steel and reinforced concrete in the last few decades (Crocetti, 2016).
Created in controlled production conditions, the newly developed engineered timber
products were responsible for transforming wood from a heterogeneous natural material
to a more homogeneous or at least consistently made timber product. For the ﬁrst time,
timber engineering was not based solely on empirical experience and craftsmanship, but
could be classiﬁed and eﬃciently standardised. Adding to this the great possibility of
prefabrication, shorter construction times, consequently lower costs and most importantly
the modern environmental awareness and sustainability meant that timber bridges have
been on the rise since the 1980’s (Thelandersson and Larsen, 2003; Mettem, 2013).
3.1.2
Modern timber bridges
When distinguishing between the types of modern bridges, a reference is primarily made
to their structural system. Modern bridges come in a variety of structural system combi-
nations, but essentially, they are all a derivative of one of the six basic bridge structural
forms, which can be seen in Figure 3.1. Setting them apart is the principle they employ in
transferring applied loads into the substructure and the ground. In the following chapter
individual types are discussed through the way they work, their sub-types and notable
timber examples. The information in this chapter is combined from the following set of
references: Billington et al. (2017), Mettem (2013), Linville (2012), Ritter (1990).

Beam bridge
A beam bridge is the simplest and oldest form of bridge structure. The most primitive
of its kind was probably a tree trunk spanning a river or a ravine. The basic distribution
of forces is through the principle of a bending moment where tension occurs at the
bottom and compression at the top section of the spanning member. While this system
is usually not eﬀective for spans upwards of 30 metres, it commonly includes several
simply supported or continuous spans making it very economical and easy to construct.
Further variations of this system include slightly bowed or cambered beams and beams
under-strutted with steel ties or rigid sub-frames. A notable example of a timber beam
bridge is the Pont de Crest in France, seen in Figure 3.2a.
Truss bridge
Dating back to the 19th century, the truss bridges played a big role in the history of
American road engineering.
The structure of the truss is such that it only employs
straight elements carrying either compression or tension (depending on their orientation)
which are in theory connected with pinned joints. Holistically speaking, a single span truss
is not that diﬀerent from a beam element carrying compression at the top and tension
at the bottom members, but is in contrast much lighter, provides greater load carrying
capacity and lateral stability, and utilises material better. The downside of timber truss
bridges is the amount of connections which complicate the overall design of the structure
and increase maintenance costs. In history, this was also the biggest limitation of timber
trusses, but has since been overcome with modern connections incorporating steel plates
and dowel type fasteners. Spans achievable with timber trusses are quite remarkable,
an example of which is the 180m long Flisa Bridge in Norway with a main span of 70,3
metres, seen in Figure 3.2b.
Cantilever bridge
A cantilever is structurally deﬁned as a beam extending horizontally from a rigidly ﬁxed
support. Such design is very uncommon in modern timber bridges. Instead, a cantilevered
bridge is designed with a continuous section and a cantilevered section over the support
of the main span. The two sides meet in the middle through a simply supported section.
Forces acting on such a bridge are carried through bending moments with compression
on the bottom and tension on the top section of the cantilevered part as well as over
the supports. A beneﬁt of such an assembly is that the structure has a determinate
structural system, which means that the bridge can handle diﬀerential settlement of
the supports. Furthermore, splitting the bridge into prefabricated sections can prove
beneﬁcial for transport because of the reduced size of individual sections. A modern
example of a cantilevered bridge is the Bow river pedestrian bridge in Canada, seen in
Figure 3.2c. This timber bridge is 113 metres long and features an 80-metre main clear
span with a 34-metre suspended mid part.
Arch bridge
Tracing its origin back to the Antique, the arch is an eﬀective structural form which
transfers forces in the form of compression.
It is best utilised in steep terrain or in
17
==PAGE:26==
locations where clearance is a prerequisite and for spans between 10 and 70 metres.
Arches have been constructed in a parabolic, circular or even elliptical form with diﬀerent
levels of the deck structure. The deck can be positioned on top, bottom or through the
middle of the arch depending on the clearance, proﬁle, and ground criteria.
Timber
arches are always made as prefabricated elements. Because of excessive size for transport
the arches are conﬁgured into two or three hinged variations, which have a moment or
a pin connection at the crown. Because they create considerable horizontal reactions in
the supports, they are often undertied with steel. An example of a modern timber arch
bridge which was inspired by Leonardo da Vinci’s plans from the beginning of the 16th
century is the Leonardo da Vinci footbridge in Norway, seen in Figure 3.2d.
Suspension bridge
The principle of the suspension bridge is a deck structure suspended from vertical cables
or suspenders. These are in turn connected onto the main cables which are suspended
between the towers and anchored at the bridge abutments. All load applied to the bridge
is carried through tension by the cables and in turn through compression into the ground
by the pillars. Suspension bridges can achieve the largest spans and are typically only
used in such instances. The deck structure, which can be raised in the main span to
provide extra clearance, needs to be designed stiﬄy or heavyly enough to withstand
buckling and induced vibrations which may cause resonance. An alternative form to the
described suspension bridge is a tension-ribbon bridge. Following a catenary form, it
transfers forces in the form of tension into the supports. While the system may seem
simple in design, its anchoring at the abutments poses a signiﬁcant challenge. The very
ﬁrst tension-ribbon bridge was the Essing bridge in Germany constructed out of in-situ
longitudinally ﬁnger-jointed glulam beams. It can be seen in Figure 3.2e.
Cable-stayed bridge
The ﬁnal basic principle of the bridge form is the cable-stay. In this type, the loads are
transferred from the deck into tension cables connected to the pillars. When the design
of the cables on each of its sides is symmetric, the horizontal forces counteract each other,
which means the pillar is not subjected to bending moments but instead only experiences
compression. The arrangement of the cables is in a mono- (single row of cables on each
side), fan-, harp- or star-shaped conﬁguration. Because they are diagonal, they introduce
compression in the deck providing a much larger degree of the deck’s rigidity compared
to the suspension bridge. This structural type is used when spans larger than the beam
and shorter than those for suspension bridges are required. Along with diﬀerent cable set
ups, the design allows for several diﬀerent arrangements of the columns as well. These
can be in the form of single vertical or tilted columns in the middle or on the side of the
bridge (usually the case when the bridge is horizontally curved), double columns, and
portals.3An example of a multi-span cable-stayed timber bridge is the Georgian Anaklia-
Ganmukhuri pedestrian bridge, seen in Figure 3.2f.

3.2
Parametric modelling
Parametric modelling or a parametric model, is, as Patrick Janssen is quoted: ". . . an
algorithm that generates models consisting of geometry and attributes (e.g. material
deﬁnitions). This algorithm uses functions and variables, including both dependent and
independent variables. Some of the independent variables can be given a more prominent
status, as the interface to the parametric model – these are referred to as the parameters
of the model." (Frazer, 2016)
Designers have long gone from using pen and paper to envision their ideas. Through
the use of computer aided design (CAD) software, they were able to produce, visualise,
and communicate more complex designs. By implementing parametric design tools into
the equation, this was taken one step further and a completely new style of architecture,
called parametricism, was created (Schumacher, 2009). By the means of parametric tools,
architects are able to explore an endless variety of complex geometric solutions in a very
short time.
When talking about parametric modelling, a reference is made to the deﬁnition of the
algorithms or scripts that control the model. In a normal modelling environment, the
designer uses the tools available to him to create points, curves, and surfaces which are
then joined into desired shapes and geometries in order to graphically represent the built
model. These 3D models are a powerful visual aid at communicating the project with
those involved. But the ability to alter them once they are created can be a frustrating
process, since the elements have no deeper connection between them beyond how they
were graphically assembled. The idea in parametric modelling is no diﬀerent from this
basic explanation, with one very important diﬀerence. Instead of using simple geometric
features to assemble the model, this is done through the use of a scripting language which
controls the correlations of elements and objects in a history dependent manner. What
this means, is that the every element in the model is built based on parameters of the
previously deﬁned entity. In this way, changing one of the input parameters of a geometric
model updates all of the objects that were deﬁned based on this variable.
The great thing about this procedure is that it is very easy to use with nothing but
the very basic knowledge behind the logic of a programming language. This is because
platforms have been created which transform this procedure from a pure programming
to a visual programming approach. Environments, such as the add-on for Rhinoceros
called Grasshopper, allow the user to assemble a visual script using predeﬁned sets of
code, called components, which perform a speciﬁc function.
In the past few years, a shift has been initiated, to expand the principles of parametric
design beyond simple geometry. To provide the designers with tools for evaluating the
eﬃciency of a certain design based on more than just aesthetics, integrated dynamic
models were implemented into the early design stages. By introducing computational
engineering approaches into these stages, multidisciplinary performance feedback could
be obtained for the use in design exploration (Brown et al., 2016). This furthermore
provided a quantiﬁable basis for design optimisation, based on which a structure with
maximal eﬃciency or ﬁtness in regard to a certain parameter could be obtained using
automatic evolutionary solving techniques. The problem with this however is that the
ﬁtness of diﬀerent parameters is commonly inversely correlated, which means that the
freedom of the design will be impacted by the ﬁxation on ﬁnding a single optimal solution.
20
==PAGE:29==
As an alternative to this decidedly convergent process, a less than ideal solution can be
found for more than a single parameter (Von Buelow, 2007). The goal therefore, is to
establish the fundamental importance of individual optimisation parameters, which can
be used in correlation to achieve, what is called, multi-objective optimisation (MOO).
Naturally, such optimisation is only as good as the input that deﬁnes the weighing in
favour of a certain variable in comparison to another. Since the designers who use these
tools in the earliest stages of the design have a limited knowledge of structural concepts,
the algorithms need to be structured in a way where it is easy to use them. The best
approach for this, as was pointed out with visual programming, is making them highly
visual via a friendly graphical user interface (Mueller and Ochsendorf, 2013).
Such a tool is for example structureFIT, which uses an evolutionary optimisation
algorithm to generate designs of 2D truss structures and ranks them according to a ﬁtness
parameter. Through the graphical user interface, seen in Figure 3.3a, it than allows the
user to chose the preferred solutions, regardless of their ranking, and performs another
step of optimisation, arriving at a new set of proposed designs (Mueller and Ochsendorf,
2013). Another tool of this family which extends the use to spatial trusses is ParaGen. It
shares the same concepts of an interactive evolutionary framework through the graphical
user interface, seen in Figure 3.3b (Von Buelow, 2012).

Chapter 4
Timber in Bridge Engineering
In this chapter, the background information on wood as a structural material and its
properties are provided. This is followed by durability considerations for timber exposed
to the elements as well as products used in bridge engineering later on in this project.
4.1
Properties of wood
When living, wood is a growing and changing material that adapts to its surroundings.
On a microscopic level, it consists of several types of wood cells which act not only as
the medium transferring substances throughout the tree but also form wood ﬁbres. The
majority of these are oriented longitudinally and form the load carrying structure. This
is responsible for carrying the tree’s self-weight as well as all the external loads, resulting
in the wood’s excellent natural ability to carry compressive, tensile and bending forces.
In the following subchapters, the physical, mechanical and other beneﬁcial properties of
wood as a building material are presented.
4.1.1
Physical properties
It is important to note that during its lifespan, a tree is saturated with water and pro-
tected by bark, which is not the case when used for structural applications. When dry,
wood enters a cycle of permanent adaptation to the moisture levels in its surroundings.
These hygroscopic characteristics mean that wood is subject to shrinking and expanding
under the inﬂuence of changes in humidity. Furthermore, because the wood structure is
orthotropic in three diﬀerent directions as a consequence of ﬁbre orientation and uneven
seasonal growth, it reacts to moisture changes unevenly. The radial and tangential dimen-
sional changes are signiﬁcantly larger than the longitudinal ones. On average, tangential
changes have been found to be double those in the radial and from 20 to 50 times larger
than those in the longitudinal direction. Furthermore, the dimensional changes diﬀer in
the case of wetting and drying and also vary among the types of wood. All in all, they
can be compared to the thermal deformations in materials such as steel and concrete
under the inﬂuence of temperature variations (Sétra, 2007).
On the other hand, because wood is a very porous material, it has low heat conduc-
tivity. This means that it is not as aﬀected by the temperature shifts, and with the right
application often outperforms concrete and steel in extreme temperature environments.
23
==PAGE:32==
Even though it is classiﬁed as a combustible material, it structurally reacts better to
ﬁre. In the event of a ﬁre a carbonised layer is formed on its surface which protects the
inside of the solid wooden cross-section, making it practically unaﬀected. As a result, the
structural capacity of a timber element subjected to ﬁre is only reduced corresponding to
the amount of the cross-section reduction due to the progressing carbonization zone. One
thing to note in the case of ﬁre is that the weakest elements are usually not timber but
metal joints connecting the timber elements. (Sétra, 2007; Kuklik and Hansen, 2008).
Nevertheless, as stated previously, wood is a natural heterogeneous material and will
have defects such as knots, top raptures, bark, wane and resin pockets, and others. Knots
being the most common and serious defect are a consequence of the natural growth pattern
of tree branches and will occur multiple times on the same piece of sawn lumber, creating
a signiﬁcant local strength reduction of the element. Due to knots and other defects, the
overall performance of the element will be greatly aﬀected (Thelandersson and Larsen,
2003).
4.1.2

Wood is an orthotropic material, with its strength characteristics signiﬁcantly varying in
the three principal directions. In reality, only the longitudinal and transverse direction to
the ﬁbres are relevant since the radial and tangential directions share comparable values.

depending on the type of load action and the direction in relation to the ﬁbres. Comparing
the values, it can be concluded that the biggest weakness of timber’s strength is its
inability to carry stresses (especially tensile) perpendicular to the direction of its ﬁbre.

All materials have diﬀerent responses to the rate and duration of loading. As opposed
to most other materials, they are not negligible in timber. This can be credited to the
fact that wood is a viscous material subjected to creep, which means its properties,
most notably modulus of elasticity, are functions of time, humidity and length of load
application (Sétra, 2007). The loss of strength for solid timber under long term loading
can be up to 40% or even 80% in some engineered products, which is why the duration of

than normally (Thelandersson and Larsen, 2003).
4.1.3
Sustainability
When sourced responsibly, using wood as a construction material can play a big role in
tackling climate change. It grows with the sun’s energy and uses water and nutrients
provided by the environment. It is the only material that is perpetually renewable and
does not have a negative carbon proﬁle. Around 50% of its dry mass is essentially made of
carbon. A measure of a material’s impact on the carbon cycle is expressed in the embodied
energy, which tells how much energy was used in the production from raw material to
the ready-to-use building product. While the ﬁnished product is not completely carbon
positive due to the processing of structural products and transport, wood is by far the
most environmentally-friendly choice when it comes to structural materials. Furthermore,
with one third of the world’s waste coming from the construction sector, it is important
to note that timber products can be recycled, reused or disposed without signiﬁcant harm
to the environment when handled responsibly (Ferguson, 1997).
4.1.4
Human perception
An additional beneﬁt of using timber for structural purposes is related to its perception
by humans. We have an aﬃnity towards nature and consequently wood. Using timber in
structures gives a unique ability to reﬂect patterns and shapes occurring in nature, which
is why they are perceived by the majority as aesthetic and have positive psychological
eﬀects. This is not only important when in close contact with the material but also when
viewing a structure from afar. Bridges are generally very pronounced structures and can,
especially in a non-urban setting, clash with or overwhelm the entire scenery and appear
intrusive. When executed correctly, timber bridges have the highest potential to blend
in with nature and complement it.
4.2
Durability of wood
Durability of a material is expressed as its performance or resistance to deterioration.
The ﬁrst rule of wood preservation is to remove it from sources of water or moisture.
This is best achieved by designing structures for durability in the earliest design stages.
The timber durability considerations to be made in bridge construction are a careful
selection of timber species, surface and/or in-depth treatment with preservatives and most
importantly, physical protection of the exposed elements with emphasis on protecting the
non-replaceable load bearing elements of the structure (Mettem, 2013).
4.2.1
Choice of species
When deciding on the species of timber, several factors should be taken into account.
Firstly, it should be established what kind of environment the structure will be exposed
to. This is mainly in regard to the moisture and temperature levels and their ﬂuctuation.

Consequently, this includes the biotic factors which are likely to be present. Exposure
of a timber structure or its parts to attack is covered with use classes in standard EN
335:2013.
To be used for structural purposes however, are the corresponding service
classes according to standard EN 1995-1-1:2004, where they are classiﬁed in the following
way:
• Service class 1: Humidity levels below 65% and average moisture content in soft-
wood below 12%,
• Service class 2: Humidity levels below 85% and average moisture content in soft-

• Service class 3: Moisture content exceeds levels from service class 2.

4.2.2
Preservative treatment
Wood will last for centuries when kept dry, but in outdoor applications, it is exposed to
the unprotected environment. It is in this case that wood becomes susceptible to attack
by:
• Non-living agents (also called physical) such as heat abrasion, UV light, chemicals,
and
• Living agents (also called biotic) such as fungi, bacteria, insects and marine borers.
While the physical agents decay the structure of the wood through a longer time period,
it is the biotic aggressors that can cause signiﬁcant harm and rapid deterioration rates
in unprotected wood. The biotic agents that enter and decay wood use its structure and
the moisture above the ﬁbre saturation point as nourishment. By limiting their supply
of water, and more importantly introducing toxic preservatives into their main source of
food, the living organisms have a signiﬁcantly reduced chance of survival (Ritter, 1990).
The goal of a preservative treatment is to impregnate the timber’s structure to a
certain penetration depth with a toxic substance which will be retained in timber for the
duration of its use. Preservatives are classiﬁed either as oil-type or waterborne, depending
on the chemical composition, and are suitable for diﬀerent environmental conditions.
They are applied in two general ways. The ﬁrst is using pressure or vacuum to achieve
impregnation of the structure, and the second are the non-pressure methods such as
brushing, soaking and dipping. In bridge applications, the latter are usually used in the
case of bores or ﬁeld cuts, which happen after the initial pressure treatment. The choice
of preservatives mainly depends on the species of wood and the desired look of the ﬁnished
product. Furthermore, because most oil-type preservatives cause harm not only to biotic
aggressors but irritate human skin as well, regulations are in place specifying the types to
be used for handrails, ﬂoorboards and other elements of bridges which users have contact
with. As opposed to other structural materials, timber’s structural performance is in
most cases not as aﬀected by strong chemicals, which is yet another reason to consider its
use in cold-climate bridge applications, where de-icing agents and salts will cause damage
to concrete or steel (Sétra, 2007; Ritter, 1990).
28
==PAGE:37==
Additional treatment approaches include protective coatings such as varnishes and
paints, which are applied to the surface of the timber members via spraying or painting.
Their role is to protect wood against humidity and to provide an aesthetic function. Their
use in external applications is limited due to their rapid degradation caused by the sun.
Further issues regarding all toxic preservatives and ﬁnishes is the waste of treated timber
at the end of their usage. Treated wood is often classiﬁed as dangerous waste, which
limits the ability for its reuse. It is also the responsibility of the structure’s owner to not
cause harm to the environment by disposing the material according to the regulations
(Sétra, 2007).
4.2.3
Structural protection
Adhering to the principles of the structural protection measures according to Mettem
(2013), timber bridge elements must be designed ﬁrst and foremost so that they are
exposed to the minimal physical contact with water. When they are in contact with
water, it should be able to drain rapidly, and in a way that dirt and liquids cannot get
entrapped in the details. Another key element is that all timber parts of the structure
must be well ventilated.
Structural protection of timber bridges should be examined from a holistic as well
as a detail-oriented point of view. When talking about protecting the timber bridge as
a whole, protection can be applied in the form of covering or cladding. When applying
a cover, the idea is to design a protective element with enough overhang to shield the
members from direct contact with rain. Possible applications include designing a covered
bridge with a roof system (Figure 4.2a), or applying an overhanging deck that acts as
a cover for the timber element such as in a timber-concrete composite structure (Figure

enough to provide an angle of shelter of 30◦Another approach is to apply impermeable
covers on top and sides of the exposed timber elements (Figure 4.2c,d) (Simon and Koch,
2016).

4.3
Timber products
Advances in timber engineering have provided several modern engineered timber prod-
ucts expanding the possibilities of timber application greatly. These products have made
it possible to overcome the timber dimensional restrictions. Made under strictly con-
trolled factory conditions and following standards of practice, they transform wood from
a heterogeneous natural product to a signiﬁcantly more homogeneous engineered product
with potential for creating larger, more durable, and more diverse bridges which can be
considered as valid alternatives to their concrete and steel alternatives. But no modern
mid- or large-span bridge is constructed exclusively out of timber and especially not only
solid wood. They are best designed in composition with steel and concrete in critical
sections where they perform better than timber.
Instead of solid sections from the past, modern timber bridges and their components
are most commonly constructed of laminated veneer lumber (LVL), stress laminated
timber (SLT), and glued laminated timber (glulam). The latter is noticeably the most
important for this project and will be discussed in more detail.
4.3.1
Glued laminated timber
Glulam is deﬁned by ASTM International (2012) as “. . . a product made from suitably
selected and prepared pieces of wood bonded together with an adhesive wither in a
straight or curved form with the grain of all pieces essentially parallel to the longitudinal
axis of the member.”
Production of glulam
Manufacturing of glulam members, depicted in Figure 4.5, is executed according to stan-
dard EN 14080:2013. It begins with the choice of timber, which is in European countries
European whitewood, Spruce, Pine, Larch, Douglas ﬁr or in the case of hardwood, Euro-
pean oak, or a tropical species Iroko (GLTA, 2014). Because of diﬃculties associated with
gluing hardwood however, normally softwood is chosen. Laminations are ﬁrstly dried to
12-15% moisture content and secondly visually graded for strength by machine or human
31
==PAGE:40==
inspection. The thickness of laminations is generally from 40 and up to a maximum of
50mm for straight members, but needs to be reduced to the range of 20 to 30mm for curved
members. Before the curing process can start, the elements are planned, stacked, glued
and clamped under pressure into the desired shape by using clamping beds or hydraulic
presses. After curing, they are sanded and processed according to the requirements of
the ﬁnishing grades, classiﬁed as industrial, architectural or premium. During all stages
of the production, close control of quality and testing is performed, which is important
to achieve the result of a highly engineered product (Thelandersson and Larsen, 2003;
Forest Products Society and Forest Products Laboratory (U.S.), 2010; GLTA, 2014).
Figure 4.5 Production line of glulam elements (obtained from GLTA (2014)).
Adhesives and preservatives
Adhesives are essential for production of glulam. They are used for ﬁnger-joints, face
bonding of laminations, and when applicable, connection components, and threaded steel
rods. They are classiﬁed according to EN 301:2013 as:
• Type 1 adhesives, which are permitted for full outdoor exposure and temperatures
above 50◦C, and
• Type 2 adhesives for use in heated and ventilated buildings.
Further classiﬁcation regards the service class of the element where adhesives are used.
The three classes for glulam according to EN 1995-1-1:2004 cover applications regarding
the exposure to weather.
The main structural components should always be in the
second exposure class, while some secondary replaceable components are allowed in higher
classes. For bonding of all components in bridge applications Type 1 adhesives are used.
Adhesives for ﬁnger-joints and face gluing of laminations fall under the chemical family of
32
==PAGE:41==
phenolic and aminoplastic adhesives, while the connection components are usually glued
in-situ using an epoxy adhesive system. All the adhesives that are used for glulam and
fall under the phenolic and aminoplastic family have a gap ﬁlling capability of about 0,1
to 2mm (GLTA, 2014).
Although, laminations applied in outdoor conditions should have at least some natural
durability, this is not always possible due to the use of softwood in glulam. For that
reason, preservatives need to be applied during the production process.
Waterborne
preservatives are applied to the timber prior to lamination while oil solvents are either
applied to laminations prior to gluing or to the ﬁnished glulam element. All preservatives
must be such that do not aﬀect strength, stiﬀness and density properties of timber (Forest
Products Society and Forest Products Laboratory (U.S.), 2010).
Strength of glulam
The strength of glulam members is determined based on the strength of individual lam-
inations. There are several failure mechanisms for glulam, including failure due to high
tensile stress parallel or perpendicular to grain, shear or bending failure. The failures most
often originate at a knot or ﬁnger-joint. What distinguishes glulam from solid members
however, is that because the lamination are stacked in a speciﬁc way and ultimately have
fewer defects in the high stress zones, they display more reliable strength characteristics.
The strength of a glulam element is not necessarily higher than that of a solid one, but
the variability of results is lesser, which means the glulam elements can be utilised to
a much higher degree. This change is attributed to what is called the dispersion eﬀect.
Essentially, by gluing together smaller pieces, the defects in wood are smaller and more
uniformly distributed and each defect has less importance on the overall performance
(Thelandersson and Larsen, 2003).
The strength of a glulam beam is normally deﬁned by its bending stiﬀness. Failure
in compression in timber is very ductile and has a large yield capacity, while failure in
pure tension is brittle. Therefore, when talking about bending failure, the strength of the
element is actually deﬁned by the tensile strength of the outer laminations (Thelandersson
and Larsen, 2003). This is also why the European standard EN 14080:2013 classiﬁes it
according to the tensile strength ft,0,1,k of individual laminations parallel to grain. The
beam lay-up proﬁles are divided into homogeneous and combined section depicted with
letter “h” or “c” at the end of the naming (for example: GL32H or GL32C).
Glulam shape and sizing
In theory, a glulam member can be created in almost any height, length and shape.
Possible are members with straight tapered, curved, and a built-up or a varying cross-
section along its length. In reality, however, the sizing is limited by the factory layout,
transportation capabilities and in the case of very large or irregular members where
adhesive needs to be applied manually, the adhesive pot life.
On the other hand, normal glulam elements are very limited in width. There are
two reasons for this. Firstly, it is diﬃcult to obtain softwood boards wider than 225mm
used for laminations. Lamellas can be edge glued on their narrow face and assembled
in the cross-section in a staggered pattern, but this increases the obtainable width to a
maximum of 450mm. The second limiting factor is the manufacturing equipment which
33
==PAGE:42==
is not designed for boards in excess of 240 or in some cases 280mm (Thelandersson and
Larsen, 2003).
There are however ways of obtaining wider cross-sections of glulam elements. In the
case of very large spans, members can be designed by pairing two beams with concealed
spacing elements at intervals.
This is an eﬃcient way of creating large stable cross-
sections without any special manufacturing requirements. Taking this one step further,
using a steel framework for lateral stability, the pair of load carrying glulam beams can
be spaced considerably further apart. Using this system, a rigid “box” section can be
achieved with the deck providing stability on the top edge of the glulam beams and the
steel diagonals at the bottom.
4.3.2
Block glulam
An alternative approach is to create what is called a block-glued laminated element.
This is essentially gluing two or more glulam beams together edgewise. Its application is
particularly useful in the girder bridge designs where it opens the possibility of not only
creating stable rigid cross-sections but also creating members where the load bearing
structure and the deck are manufactured in one piece. This method can be applied to
straight, curved or even twisted members. Curved block glulam members are produced
by gluing curved individual glulam pieces, and have a curvature in top or side view.
Since laminations can only be bent around the weak axis, the orientation of the glulam
segments depends on the type of curvature, as seen in Figure 4.6.
Figure 4.6 Rectangular block glulam sections with vertical (left) and horizontal (right)
block glue lines (taken from DIN 1052:2004)
The strength of block glulam members is determined by the strength of the individual
glulam components with no size eﬀect adjustments speciﬁed. In the case of diﬀerent grade
components, the resultant strength is calculated based on the elastic composite theory
(Aicher and Stapf, 2014).
Regarding the eﬀects of moisture induced stresses, Franke
et al. (2013) found that with correct protection planning against moisture and decay, the
humidity variation in the solid cross-section does not pose structural issues in a block
glulam section and levels remain under 20% as demanded in structural service class 2.
Block glulam according to EN 14080
Even though this concept has been around for decades, it has never been included in
the Eurocode standards, until the predecessor of standard EN 14080:2013, which came

into eﬀect in 2005. According to it, block glulam is restricted to straight and uni-axially
curved solid rectangular sections (seen in Figure 4.6) under the condition that the glue
lines are planar. Special rules apply to the manufacturing process as well. Adhesives
must be of the phenolic or aminoplastic-polycondensating variety and must have gap-
ﬁlling capabilities of up to 2mm, while the individual glue lines should not exceed 1,5mm.
Further requirements are related to moisture content at bonding, and state that all glulam
components are to have less than 15% moisture content with the mean value of individual
component’s moisture content not exceeding 3%. Additionally, during curing, glulam
components should not be subjected to any type of bending. This last part therefore
rules out all sorts of twisted members.
Block glulam according to DIN 1052
Germany has had a longer tradition of using block glulam components, starting in 1989
with the Otto-Munz bridge in Reichenbach. Consequently, their DIN national standards
cover a greater variety of cross-section shapes, including built up sections, seen in Figure
4.7. Apart from the cross-section possibilities, the biggest diﬀerence is that according to
DIN 1052:2004, glue lines joining individual glulam sections can be curved out of plane.
This means that bi-axially curved or twisted beams can be achieved (Aicher and Stapf,
2014).

4.3.3
Other materials used in timber bridges
As mentioned at the beginning of this chapter, a timber bridge cannot be made exclusively
of timber. The design of structural parts is usually coupled with steel and/or concrete to
diﬀerent extents in medium or large span bridges. Most notably, the application of steel
can be seen in joints. Traditionally made of wood, the carpentry joint techniques, such as
mortise and tenon joints, joints using wedges and dowels, and so on, could only transfer
compressive forces eﬀectively. That is why, they were replaced by steel solutions. Uses
of steel in timber construction however, extend beyond simple fastening systems such as
nails, bolts, pins and rods. It is also found in post-stressing tendons of laminated decks,
35
==PAGE:44==
already mentioned protective plates of exposed member and end grain timber connections,
or on a larger scale for piers, cables in cable-stayed or suspended systems, and stiﬀening
systems increasing the overall rigidity of the structure. All structural steel components
must be designed with resistance to corrosion in mind and according to provisions of the
EN standards or more speciﬁcally the code for steel - EN 1993.
Concrete is another structural material which can be used in conjunction with timber.
It is used in composite timber-concrete bridges where the load carrying structure is made
of timber and the deck of reinforced or prestressed concrete. This can be a great solution
in the case of designing a vehicular bridge, where the concentrated forces are better
distributed through a concrete deck. Most commonly however, the use of concrete can be
seen in the bridge substructure – the abutments, piers or other types of supports which
are in regular or permanent contact with water. Design of concrete components must
comply with the provisions of the EN standards or more speciﬁcally EN 1992.

Chapter 5
Design Stages
The process of designing a bridge or any other structure for that matter is an itera-
tive practice, which progresses through several design stages. Each of them has high
importance for the development of the project and must be deﬁned precisely within its
outlined requirements. This is especially important in the design of timber structures,
where parameters such as durability and protective measures have to be introduced into
the earliest stages of design. In this chapter, the process of determining a bridge’s struc-
tural system throughout individual design stages leading up to a project proposal will be
discussed and the basis for the parametric modelling will be reviewed.
5.1
Pre-design stage
The very ﬁrst stage of the project and the basis of all the following is the pre-design stage.
In this stage, the project is outlined, and the preconditions are examined. Based on the
gathered knowledge, the project speciﬁcations are deﬁned. It is of utmost importance
that these are clearly communicated and agreed upon between all involved because they
represent the boundary conditions to the project and a foundation to all the subsequent
stages (Thompson, 2009). The most important consideration and questions that should
be answered can be seen in the diagram in Figure 5.1.
5.1.1
Topography
Project space understanding is obtained by reviewing maps, plans and photos of the
location. Naturally, it is beneﬁcial to visit the actual site in person, but when this is not
possible, inputs like Google Street View can be a substitute. Furthermore, when available,
having even a draft 3D model of the topographic conditions is a considerable advantage.
The goal is to determine what the bridge is going to cross. This could be a body of water,
road traﬃc, or a ravine. Each of these directly inﬂuences the bridge design through the
horizontal and vertical clearance requirements. Furthermore, when crossing traﬃc, it
also inﬂuences the allowed time of construction, meaning that prefabricated solutions are
preferred. Of great importance are also the elevations of the site and the surroundings.
This is because implementing a bridge structure extends further than simply designing
the bridge and its supports. Bridges are designed to aid in the transition over obstacles, so
normally they need to be connected into an existing network of approach paths. The rate
37
==PAGE:46==
Figure 5.1 Gathering project speciﬁc data in the pre-design stage (based on Almegaard
(2016))
of obstruction these create is dependent on how eﬃciently they are integrated into the
topography. If the site elevations have a minimal height diﬀerence, it means the bridge’s
structure needs to be raised accordingly, creating the need for steep or long approach
paths in the form of staircases and/or ramps.
5.1.2
Geography
Further investigation needs to be aimed towards the geographical conditions of the loca-
tion. The aim is to deﬁne how the structure’s environment will eﬀect the structure during
its entire life cycle. It could be in the form of immediate risks such as ﬂash ﬂoods, earth-
quakes, ﬁres, landslides, vehicle impacts, etc., or in the form of long-term damage from
biological factors due to moisture, and other factors of degradation (Sétra, 2007). This
is very important in outdoor timber applications, as explained in Sections 4.2 and 4.3 on
durability and protection considerations. At this stage, information is gathered on mete-
orological inﬂuences in the form of loads resulting from snow and wind at the location.
All of these act on the structure above ground level, but the substructure stretches below
as well, which is why ground conditions should be a topic of investigation. Speciﬁcally,
what is the composition of the ground proﬁle, at what level is the groundwater and what
conditions will aﬀect the design of the substructure. Naturally, not all of this will be
available before the start of the project, so some extent of on-site analysis is usually nec-
essary. However, since history tends to repeat itself, analysing historic data on extreme
natural occurrences at the site is always a good starting point (Almegaard, 2016).
5.1.3
User analysis
The subsequent data collection topic should be regarding the usage of the structure. A
pedestrian bridge can be used by four groups of users: pedestrians, cyclists, equestrians,
38
==PAGE:47==
and light motorised vehicles. The former is categorised into maintenance and emergency
vehicles. However, if these two are allowed, then the bridge is also at the risk of other
restricted uses of vehicular actions classiﬁed as accidental loads. Considering also the
frequency of use, each of these has its own set of limitations and design rules regard-
ing the load magnitude and the pattern of its distribution. Adding to that, the rules
diﬀer between users regarding the width requirements, approach and bridge inclination
levels, balustrade design and height, etc (Christer, 2017). Lastly, the client and other
stakeholders will normally have a set of wishes to complement the gathered boundary
conditions. This could be a preference to a structural system, material, complexity, or
speciﬁc architectural features.

Conceptual design
With the project speciﬁcations clearly deﬁned and with suﬃcient project data gathered
and analysed, the conceptual stage can begin. The main goal of the conceptual stage is
to choose a structural system of the bridge and develop a prototype of how the structure
will look and behave in relation to its components and holistically to its surroundings.
All of this needs to be done in conjunction with a feasibility analysis into the component
manufacture, transport, on-site assembly, and lastly the disassembly and disposal at
the end of the structure’s lifecycle (Thompson, 2009).
The latter, however, was not
considered in this project further than what was said in Chapter 4, about the harmful
eﬀects of preservative treatment, due to a lack of time.
The stage begins with a collection of examples of existing structures and identiﬁcation
of solutions employed in these projects, that relate to the project at hand. The larger the
space of examples is, the more solutions can be examined, so it is a good idea to thoroughly
commit to this stage. The design should be split into a set of problems, and solutions
should be identiﬁed for each of them.
This will result in several concepts regarding
the structural system and its components, which are assembled out of the solutions of
individual problems. It is at this point that time should be invested in creating diﬀerent
kinds of models that represent the concepts and can be used to evaluate and compare its
conformity to the project speciﬁcations and structural eﬃciency (Almegaard, 2016). The

5.2.2
Cross-section proposals
As established in Chapter 4.1.2, timber has low density and comparatively high strength.
It is for that reason, that timber bridges can be signiﬁcantly lighter than those made
of concrete or steel. This, however, is at the expense of stiﬀness. While the low weight
is a beneﬁt when designing the substructure of the bridge, the reduced rigidity can be
an issue when considering vibrations.
Therefore, natural frequencies of a pedestrian
bridge are a large concern and have to be considered at early stages. In this project,
the rigidity of the cross-section is of further importance because of the twisting, which
results from the overturning moments. Consequently, the cross-section design went in
two distinct directions, both using glulam elements as their core structural component.
The proposals and their variations were modelled into 3D parametric models, which can
be seen in Figure 5.7.
The ﬁrst proposal was a block glulam section based on the Otto-Munz bridge in
Reichenbach, Germany, described by Aicher and Stapf (2014). The beneﬁts of the solid
section are a high degree of rigidity and the fact that it can be manufactured with the
deck included in a single prefabricated element. Furthermore, the cross-section has a
large set of possible variations according to the cross-section shape. It can be adopted for
example into a rectangular (Figure 5.7-top left), trapezoidal (Figure 5.7-bottom left), or
T section (Figure 5.7-top right) and even with a varying proﬁle height along the bridge
span. At the same time, the section generates additional design considerations.
43
==PAGE:52==
Figure 5.7 Parametric 3D models of the proposed bridge cross-sections
• The glulam beams of this size, have to be bent uni-axially. In other words, the
curvature of the bridge must be in a single plane. Bending them bi-axially would
signiﬁcantly complicate the design, the manufacturing process, and ultimately the
price.
• Because the lamellas can only be bent around their weak axis, the individual glulam
beams that make up the block section need to be oriented on their side. This means
that the block glue-lines need to be horizontal.
• Creating connections between cross-sections of this size is considerably more chal-
lenging. They cannot, for example, be ﬁnger-jointed on site to create a continuous
bridge element as is the case in the Essing bridge, seen in Figure 3.2e.
The second proposal was a coupled glulam beam based on the cross-section of the
Bow river pedestrian bridge in Banﬀ, Canada (Epp et al., 2013), also seen in Figure
3.2c. This proposal achieves its lateral rigidity through the connection with the deck on
top (making this a double T section) and the steel bracing system at the bottom. The
parametric 3D model of this cross-section proposal can be seen in the bottom left part of
Figure 5.7. Its beneﬁt is mainly the simpler manufacturing of the glulam beams and the
more straight forward and researched principles of connections, but like the ﬁrst design
comes with a set of issues.
• As was the case with the ﬁrst section, due to the nature of the curvature, the
lamellas of the gluelam beams would need to be oriented vertically.
• Due to the reduced load carrying capacity compared with the ﬁrst design, the
glulam beams would need to have a signiﬁcantly higher proﬁle. This would raise
the entire deck of the bridge, which is undesirable, due to the need for approach
routes.
• The deck would need to be designed rigid enough to act as a diaphragm on top of
the two beams while at the same time providing suﬃcient levels of lateral stability.
This would probably result in a thicker CLT or glulam deck, which would potentially
need to be post-tensioned with steel cables.
44
==PAGE:53==
• The fact that a high degree of steel would have to be used to create the horizontal
bracing elements, somewhat defeats the purpose of using timber for the project in
the ﬁrst place. The stiﬀening elements could be created out of timber, but they
would still need to be interconnected with steel joints, which complicate the design.
Both designs utilise a timber deck structure that acts as a rain shield for the underlying
superstructure. In case this is not suﬃcient, side cladding is proposed at this stage. The
timber deck would in turn be protected by the wearing surface, which would include some
type of a bituminous hydrophobic layer.
5.2.3
Sub-structure considerations
The proposals for the supports systems are very much dictated by the structural types
discussed.
At this stage, they were made through structural sketches, drawings, and
ﬁnally, 3D parametric models. The latter were also used to better deﬁne the possible
locations of the intermediate pillars. Through the structural sketches, it could be seen
that the curved and tilted bridge would be subject to all six internal forces and moments.
It was also obvious, that the overturning moments would play a signiﬁcant role in the
design of the bridge substructure. The overturning moment and its size on individual
pillars is therefore the consequence of both the span length as well as the width of the
supports, as can be seen in the sketch in Figure 5.8.
Figure 5.8 Eﬀects of support width and span length on the overturning (the area that
causes the overturning is marked with red)
While the span is somewhat ﬁxed over the roadway (except in the case of the cable-
stayed system), the width of individual supports can be manipulated. By making the
supports wider the force applied on them from the twisting moment would be reduced.
An additional possibility instead of making a very wide support is to create a support
that in theory only attaches at two nodes on the inner and outer edge of the beam
element. Such a support could have two or four arms, which would be hinged on both
ends, eﬀectively only carrying axial compression loads - or possibly tension on the inner
arms if the overturning was large enough. Having pinned support connections that do
not transfer the bending moment into the pillars is also a preferred solution, because it
means the pillars do not have to be as bulky.
A completely diﬀerent approach was analysed with the second support type. In this
approach, instead of pillars, portal frames were introduced, onto which the cables connect
to form a cable-stayed structural system. This way, the eﬀective spans can be drastically
45
==PAGE:54==
reduced and the superstructure is carried through tensile forces in the cables. Conse-
quently, the static height of the superstructure proﬁle can be reduced compared to the
previous solution. But this change also brings further issues. Due to the slimmer proﬁle
and the non-linear behaviour of the cables, the bridge becomes much more ﬂexible and
could become prone to excitation from induced vibrations. Furthermore, the solution
complicates both the structural analysis as well as the construction of the bridge, due to
the need to tension each cable individually. And it should be noted that due to the curva-
ture there is no symmetry in the progression of the bridge, which means that every cable
will carry a diﬀerent eﬀective force creating uneven loading on the portal frames. These
will in turn have to be designed to carry moment loads and will have to be appropriately
larger.

5.2.4
Connection considerations
Optimal connection types and their locations are mostly aﬀected by the internal force and
moment distributions in the structure. Although, their shape can be estimated based on
engineering judgement and rules of thumb, they are in reality much more complicated and
aﬀected by several parameters in a bridge of curved and tilted design. Consequently, the
considerations into connections at this stage, produced only concepts, which would need to
be veriﬁed and dimensioned according to the results of a structural analysis. Nevertheless,
certain aspects of the connections can be envisioned in advance and potential problems
can be identiﬁed.
The design mainly focused on possible approaches to longitudinally connect prefab-
ricated segments of a block glulam section.
Three diﬀerent variations of connection
positions seen in Figure 5.10 were analysed. In the top part, the My bending moment
of a continuous structure is sketched, in the middle, one where each span is a simply
supported section, and in the bottom part, a cantilevered section approach.
Figure 5.10 Bending moment (My) diagram under a uniform load in systems with
diﬀerent longitudinal connections in the structure
To accommodate the continuous beam principle in the top sketch of Figure 5.10, rigid
connections would be required between the prefabricated sections.
It was previously
established that creating those is not possible through ﬁnger jointing with such massive
sections, and furthermore, any mechanical types of steel connections, would in reality not
be rigid due to the eﬀect of creep. A solution to this is using glued-in threaded rods. This
has been done to form rigid connections on the Sneek I & II bridges in the Netherlands,
as described in Miebach (2011).
Such rods would need to be glued into the end of the timber element using epoxy
or similar adhesives during the production of the beams and would with a metal end
plate form a multi-component connection. This is because gluing on site unnecessarily
complicates the assembly process. In literature (Bengtsson and Johansson, 2002; Jiang
et al., 2012; Tlustochowicz et al., 2011), these are described to carry axial forces best so
it is hard to estimate before performing any structural analysis whether the number and

to grain tensile strength of timber.

(right part). This connection is not limited to a ﬁxed moment type. It could as well have
been redesigned with an intermediate rotational part that transforms it into a pinned

which are fastened with dowel type fasteners. Due to the plastic behaviour of components,
a ductile failure of the connection is expected. The concept for connecting the block
glulam section is based on smaller pinned connections of the same type found in manuals
and standards such as for example Linville (2012) or EN 1995-1-1:2004. When considering
this connection, it becomes obvious that inserting the steel plates into the cut-in end slits
of the beam would not be the problem - the dowels would. Since the block glulam section
can be upwards of 2 metres wide, the same would apply to the dowels. But considering
that such a pinned connection would be placed at a location where bending moment My

do not need to span the entire width either, but can be inserted from both sides and only
up to a required length.

5.2.5
Maintenance planning
The aim of bridge maintenance is to prevent damage from occurring and spreading.
Throughout the report, mentions have been made on how the timber superstructure
would always be removed from eﬀects of liquid water and be kept in service class 2 to,
at the very least, fulﬁl its 50 year service life expectancy, but the same does not apply
for secondary components. Components like the railings, cladding, and wearing surface
have a shorter service life and should therefore be inspected, maintained and if the need
arises replaced.
It is for that reason that they should be designed so that it is easy
to detach them from the superstructure. Nevertheless, such inspections should only be
performed in predeﬁned intervals or when the need occurs. In the meantime, it is a good
idea to perform preventative measures such as ﬁtting the main structural components
with moisture sensors, that would signal any critical changes.
5.3
Developed design
The component concepts gathered in the conceptual stage now need to be analysed, eval-
uated, optimised, possibly redesigned completely, and assembled into the best solution.
It is at this stage, when the geometry, cross-section types, structural systems and loads
are outlined, that the analysis can begin. This is normally not detailed ﬁnite element
50
==PAGE:59==
method (FEM) analysis but instead somewhat simpliﬁed feasibility calculations. Never-
theless, these should be suﬃcient to identify the next level of problems in the design.
By deﬁning the most critical load combinations and performing limit state analy-
sis, answers regarding utilisation, deﬂection, and natural vibrations can be obtained.
Through these, the required cross-section sizes for typical as well as critical elements can
be estimated. The results of individual solutions can be evaluated based on structural
eﬃciency, material consumption, complexity, cost, etc. Most importantly, after the pre-
liminary calculations and the deﬁnition of critical forces and moments, the deﬁnition of
connection locations and their analysis can begin. It is only when these are outlined that
the sizing of prefabricated elements is known and the logistics of manufacture, transport,
and assembly can be determined (Almegaard, 2016).
The developed design stage serves as the gap-bridging stage between the conceptual
and detailed stage. Even so, the level of detail of the analysis and the ability to identify
problems in this stage reﬂects how well prepared the design is when it enters the detailed
stage after the project proposal is approved. Using parametric environments of Rhino’s
Grasshopper plug-in, algorithms were scripted through which the described analysis could
be performed on a series of parametrically driven models. It is in this stage that the most
work on visual programming has been done in this project. Consequently, it has an entire
chapter of the report dedicated to it. Before moving to the actual structural analysis,
a section is presented on the loads and load combinations that were taken into account
when analysing the bridge.
5.3.1
Loads acting on the bridge
The actions on the bridge considered in the structural analysis during the developed de-

Dynamic loads
According to Section 5.7 of EN 1991-2:2003, natural frequencies corresponding to vertical,
horizontal, and torsional vibrations should be tested. This must be done in order to
determine if any of the natural frequencies is close to one of the expected forced vibration
frequencies. If this is the case, the structure may enter into a state of resonance.
While forced vibrations were not considered in this project, natural vibrations were
analysed to determine if they fall into one of the frequency intervals of the periodic forces
that are characteristic to pedestrians crossing the bridge. These are the following:
• a single pedestrian exerts a simultaneous periodic vertical force with a frequency
in the range of 1Hz < fv < 3Hz and horizontal force in the range of 0, 5 < fh <
1, 5Hz,
• a group of joggers exert a periodic force with the frequency of fj = 3Hz.
Furthermore, according to EN 1990:2002/A1:2005, any vertical natural frequencies
below 5Hz and horizontal/twisting natural frequencies below 2, 5Hz should be veriﬁed
for comfort criteria of pedestrians.

Chapter 6
Parametric Modelling
Through the use of visual programming in the Grasshopper’s environment, visual as well
as computational models of components and structural systems were created. One of the
main goals of the project was to deﬁne how, and in what stages, this sort of approach
can assist a designer.
It should therefore be pointed out straight away that creating
parametric scripts should not be done for every step of the process. The time spent on
scripting the parametric model algorithms in the early stages of design should only be
justiﬁed when the design process would beneﬁt from a tool that can be used to visually
portray, analyse, and optimise large pools of design solutions. This chapter describes the
purpose and creation of the parametric models used in the project (an overview of the
parametric scripts can be seen in Appendix B). But ﬁrstly, a segment on the validation
of algorithms.

Because the plug-ins used to create the structural models are not primarily made for the
use with timber, a large part of the scripting process consisted of structural validations

6.3
Structural analysis with Karamba
Entering into the developed design stage, the most visual programming work has been
done on the project.
Normally during this stage, detailed structural analysis is not
performed. This is because in order to execute the analysis eﬃciently, the geometry,
materials, connections, support conditions, loads and load combinations, and others need
to be clearly deﬁned. Using parametric software however, models can be created where
one or more of these parameters are left as variables. Doing so enables the designer to
perform simpliﬁed analysis on the model in order to provide estimates for the design
questions that would normally be answered in the detailed stage.
On this project the described algorithm was created in a plug-in for Grasshopper called
Karamba.
In this section, the case speciﬁc considerations will be outlined regarding

constructed was of the continuous girder beam structural type.
6.3.1
Geometry
In order to construct the computational model of the bridge, the bridge curve and the
support locations deﬁned in Section 6.2.1 were used.
The cross-sections and support
deﬁnitions from the previous section were purely visual so this was the extent of geome-
try that could be transferred directly into the structural model. Nevertheless, since the
manipulation of the models in parametric models is not done through a viewport based
graphic user-interface, such as for example in RFEM, the geometry had to be structured
in a logical way that would enable simple deﬁnitions of the components. Because the
bridge assumes a curved shape, the curve needed to be approximated in Karamba with a
68
==PAGE:77==
reasonable number of straight lines. It was decided that 200 elements of approximately
0,5m length would be suﬃcient. The bridge is further characterised by four distinct sec-
tions or spans, which are deﬁned by the support locations along the curve. The segments
in the individual spans (named A,B,C,D) were therefore assigned unique identiﬁcations,
which adapt automatically to the change in support locations (A1, ..., Ai, B1, ..., Bj, ...).
Before these segments can be fed into the model assembly component, they need to be
transformed into native Karamba elements. The reason why beam elements were chosen
and not the shell ones was described in Section 6.1. Once the geometry, which is the
foundation of the model, was deﬁned, the components followed.

Support conditions
While the support locations were deﬁned along with the bridge curve via the number
sliders, these exact locations cannot be used for the structural analysis.
The reason

at approximately 0,5m length, the displacement of the approximated support position is

6.3.4
Cross-sections
Karamba is primarily made to be used with steel and concrete.
Although there are
outlines of a timber material, this is certainly not the case with cross-sections.
The
cross-sections oﬀered through Karamba components are those relating to steel beams (I,
box, circular hollow, trapezoid) or concrete sections (constant and variable thicknesses of
shells). When steel sections are used, Karamba oﬀers libraries of standard cross-section
sizes according to their country, shape, and family (for example: HEA, HEB, IPE sections
according to I proﬁles set to Europe).
Originally, the idea was to set up a similar library of cross-sections for timber proﬁles.
This however proved to be too diﬃcult so instead simpliﬁcations were made by adapting
70
==PAGE:79==
the steel and concrete sections to the use with timber.
Three stages of cross-section
development were made.
Rectangular section were initially created using steel thin walled box sections and
deﬁning the thickness of the walls to half of the outer width. This naturally turned out
to be a poor substitute due to the additional stiﬀness and weight of the overlapping walls.
Trapezoidal sections, which were created for concrete beams in Karamba, proved to
be a better ﬁt. Using these, the model could be approximated both as a rectangular
as well as trapezoidal section. The covering deck would be disregarded in the analysis
and only added as additional dead load on the superstructure as seen in the sketch in
Figure 6.12. This section performed rather well and displayed realistic deformations and
internal forces/moments. The problem however was that although the stiﬀness was not
much diﬀerent, the reduced self-weight of the section created a noticeable diﬀerence in
the natural frequencies of the system (not including the load of the deck). During further
analysis it was found that there is an option of adding masses for the purposes of dynamic
analysis, which would substitute the weight of the deck. Nevertheless, another aproach
was adopted before this realisation.

6.3.5
Loads
Deﬁning loads in Karamba is done with the "Load (Karamba)" component. The user
can chose between several load types, but in this section, only the gravitational and point
loads were used. Gravitational load automatically aﬀects all members and has an optional
input for the direction vector (by default this is the negative global Z direction). The
point type load component on the other hand needs to be deﬁned by a force/moment
vector, its position (with a point identiﬁer or coordinates), and an additional setting that
aﬀects if it will follow the deformation shape in large deformation analysis.
Using the point load component, all variable actions and the dead load described in
Section 5.3.1, were applied to the bridge members. In order to structure the model to
reﬂect all possible loading scenarios and later on the combinations, each of them had to
be applied separately according to their position, direction, and magnitude. A special
user deﬁned cluster was created in order to control the locations of the applied loads.
Its input parameters are the base curve, member and support points, and optionally
the joint points for later use. What this component does is allow for the possibility of
deﬁning speciﬁc loaded sections along the bridge. Each support deﬁnes a new section
similar to the deﬁnition of groups of members in the geometry deﬁnition. The diﬀerence
here is that additional sections can be created between supports and joints. The output
of this component, besides the member points inside the loaded section, are the local
coordinate axes along the bridge member (local x and y coordinates are oriented in the
bridge plane). An example of how this component works is shown in Figure 6.14, where
a ﬁctional load in the local y direction of the beam members is applied on segments 0, 2,
and 4. Start and end point of segment 2, marked with green crosses, are joints. Segments
are always numbered from left to right and automatically adapt to the locations and the
number of supports and joints. While in this case the load magnitude is applied directly
per node, this was done per metre length of the bridge in the project (seen in Figure
6.15). The value was later in the script automatically transformed into load per node.

6.4.3
Optimisation of cross-section
A large interest during the project was to deﬁne a cross-section for the superstructure
of the bridge, which could compete with the concrete solution by Studio Granda. The
initial misconception was that a much higher proﬁle would be needed in order to create a
system that is both rigid enough to withstand the deﬂections as well as strong enough to
carry internal actions. For that reason, creating a superstructure that would be utilised
as much as possible was a high priority.
It was also proven, through the utilisation
curves presented in the previous section, that the bridge is subject to large diﬀerence of
utilisation throughout its length.
81
==PAGE:90==
For the reasons stated above, settings were implemented with which the user is able
to set a varying height of the cross-section along the length of the bridge. This step is
done with the same procedure as initially deﬁning the cross-sections. The input controls
were set so that a base constant section is chosen along the entire length and is after-
wards modiﬁed on the selected sets of members. These are selected through their unique
identiﬁcation names, which were deﬁned in Section 6.3.1. The same principle can be
implemented for the width of the section, opening a large variety of shape conﬁguration
which can be analysed automatically, through the procedures described hitherto. The
beneﬁt of the entire Karamba algorithm therefore, is that the designs (within a set range)
can be analysed automatically, and more importantly evaluated on the spot.
Because the entire design is created in a parametric script and controlled through
input parameters, the user can simply change these and observe how they eﬀect the
results. Such a design approach, would however lose its meaning if the user would have to
manually review the results of each conﬁguration. Emphasis was therefore put on their
simplicity which is why the decision was made to create the ULS and SLS utilisation
outputs.
An example of this principle, can be seen in Figure 6.25, where three diﬀerent cross-
section design conﬁgurations are presented along with their SLS and ULS utilisation,
as well as the ﬁrst three natural frequencies of the model. They are all subjected to

load (gr1) is applied on the second and fourth span, and the corresponding characteristic

6.4.4
Evolutionary optimisation
There is however an additional reason for creating a single result parameter to determine
the eﬃciency of the entire design. Initially, the goal was to create an evolutionary solving
algorithm, which would run an analysis and based on the result ﬁtness propose the optimal
design. For this purpose, an add-on for Grasshopper, called Galapagos, would have been
used.
Galapagos can take several input parameters, but can only evaluate the ﬁtness of the
outcome based on a single one. In this case the input parameters would be the variation in
cross-section sizes along the bridge length and the output the greatest overall utilisation
value.
The solver arrives at the optimal solution by taking a pool of starting input
parameter combinations (parents) and pairing them into new solutions (children). The
highest ranking of these combinations represent the new generation of input parameters,
which are paired again to form yet another generation of oﬀ-springs. This iterative process
is repeated until it converges, a certain value of ﬁtness is achieved, or the user stops it.
While this sounds simple and straight-forward in theory, it gets quite complicated
when applying to the project. No diﬀerent from nature, evolutionary solving in parametric
modelling does not happen instantaneously. In terms of computing time, one iteration
means changing the input values, calculating or performing the function of the script,
evaluating the outcome, and choosing the highest ranked designs to feed into the next
iteration. This is where complexity of the algorithm comes into play. When something
very basic is being optimised, where an iteration takes a fraction of a second, running
82
==PAGE:91==
Figure 6.25 Comparison of the eﬃciency of various design proposals through the utili-
sation values
several thousand iterations does not mean much. The problem occurs when the algorithm
is so heavy, a single iteration takes a second or even more to complete. It is then, that
the user is left with hours upon hours of waiting time for a single optimisation procedure.
While already a signiﬁcant problem, this is only the beginning. The bridge, as it
is represented in the Karamba analysis, consists of 200 elements. This means that the
evolutionary solver would either have 200 input values for cross-section height to optimise,
or beam sets with constant heights would need to be created (similar to what is shown

that would need to be optimised as well. Add to this, the variations in bridge width

Cable-stayed solution
Along with the deﬁned algorithms for a girder beam bridge system, a cable-stayed alter-
native was considered. Of course, ﬁrst the validations needed to be done to prove that
Karamba can be used to analyse such elements. This proved somewhat harder to do for
the following reasons:
• deﬁning a parametric geometry for the pylons and cables and connecting them into
a structural system for the use with Karamba is signiﬁcantly more complicated and
time consuming,
• deﬁning several diﬀerent materials and cross-sections further complicates the script
because the interaction needs to be deﬁned in their joints manually,
• the cables need to be analysed with 2nd order theory,
• because there is no cable component in Karamba, they need to be modelled as
circular beam elements,
• the cable behaviour can be best approximated by setting them to only carry loads
in tension (they are ignored in the analysis in case of compression) and so that they
do not have bending stiﬀness - but that also means that they will only elongate and
not sag,
• optimising this analytical model goes further than only analysing the bridge deck.
87
==PAGE:96==
All of this means that when comparing these elements with models created in RFEM,
the internal forces, moments, and deﬂections were completely diﬀerent. The reason for
this is that the stiﬀness of each individual cable and also the pylons aﬀects the overall
stiﬀness of the model. There is also little room of simplifying the model by for exam-
ple assuming total rigidity of the pylons, because that does not represent the reality
adequately.
Possible solutions to this problem that were considered were to replace the cables
with spring supports, which was quickly discarded, since there is no way of deﬁning their
individual stiﬀness accurately. The second approach was to load each cable with a post-
tensioning force in order to minimise their deformation in the neutral state (only loaded
by self-weight). The problem is that the planned bridge curve is in no way symmetric so
each cable would need to have a unique post-tensioning force. They were modelled by
applying an initial strain load (ε0 [mm/m]) to each of the cables. The deformation was
converted into stress according to Hooke’s law for linear elastic materials.
The balancing of the forces was attempted with the evolutionary solver Galapagos. In
this case, the input parameters for the optimisation were the cable initial strain loads and
the output was the maximum deformation of the bridge deck, as seen in Figure 6.31. The
solver’s goal was to alternate the input parameters (the negative sign denotes tension) to
arrive at the minimal possible umax.

7.1
Connections
When talking about connections, there are two distinct types to consider. First are the
longitudinal connections, joining the individual prefabricated elements into the bridge
superstructure, and the second, the connections of the superstructure with the supporting
pillars. Both types however, have the same principles when designing their joints. The
connecting member will in both cases be a multi-component joint made out of metal. It is
not however the metal segment that is problematic when connecting timber elements, it
is how this component is ﬁxed onto the timber proﬁle. Transferring the actions from the
timber element to the steel connection means high local stresses, and depending on their
orientation according to the timber grain, these can result in several failure mechanisms.
In connection viability considerations, it was therefore this part that was the subject
of analysis. It has furthermore, been assumed that moisture induced variations in the
massive block glulam section do not cause problematic internal stresses. The only stresses
on the connecting members therefore, were the ones originating from the loading.
In Chapter 4, it has been established that perpendicular to grain resistance of timber is
much lesser than that parallel to grain. While both types of connections transfer stresses
perpendicular to grain, they act in diﬀerent directions. Supporting the superstructure
from bellow will mostly result in compressive, while the longitudinal connections will
almost always have at least some degree of tensile perpendicular to grain stresses. The
resistance of timber in the latter case is signiﬁcantly smaller, which is why the longitudinal
connections were taken as the basis for the validation calculations.
Principles of timber connection design are explained in standard EN 1995-1-1:2004
and EN 1995-2:2004 in the case of bridge elements. The design considerations in this
project however, are related to massive glulam block sections, for which connections are
not speciﬁcally mentioned in the Eurocode standards. A literature review had to be made

to ﬁnd design solutions and analysis procedures regarding the approaches discussed in
Chapter 5.

The design of the Breiðholtsbraut timber footbridge concluded with a segment on the

96

layer acting as the hydrophobic barrier to the timber. Such layers can have issues bonding

higher lateral loads, such as for example breaking and accelerating of vehicles on heavy
duty road bridges. As a result of the climate of the location, the topmost layer on the
bridge surface would also be ﬁtted with an anti-slip coating.
One of the most important parts of the bridge appearance is the balustrade. This is
not only an aesthetic feature however. It primarily acts as the safety barrier, but can be
incorporated in the form of parapet beams into certain structural systems as well. For

analysis. In this project the balustrade had no structural function so it was not a subject

Chapter 8
Conclusion
In this chapter, the concluding remarks are gathered, as well as the possibilities and pro-
posals for future research in this speciﬁc topic and generative design algorithmic modelling
in general.
8.1
Timber footbridge
During the project, a study has been made into the methods and principles applied in the
earliest design stages of conventional design. The importance of individual parameters
and distinct stages in the decision making process have been outlined on a study of a tim-
ber footbridge. It has been concluded that the nature of the curved bridge severely limits
the possible applications of timber structural systems, demanding that unconventional
structural components, methods, and considerations be used in its design. Nevertheless,
a viable timber solution has been found for the alternative superstructure design of the
concrete Breiðholtsbraut highway footbridge.
It has been proven through the use of Eurocode standards, that with implementing
the correct structural and preventative measures, the durability of the superstructure is
competitive with that of exposed concrete and steel bridges. Furthermore, the initial miss-
conception, that the static height of the bridge proﬁle would need to be signiﬁcantly higher
to account for the decrease in the rigidity of the system, has been proven as incorrect. In
fact, the proposed solution shows that the structure functions well within the limits of
serviceability and ultimate limit states according to provisions of the Eurocode standards.
Although the lowest natural vibration frequencies were identiﬁed as a potential issue in
the subsequent stages of the design, these are believed to be manageable through the
use of well established engineering approaches. In retrospect, it has been shown, that a
timber design of the footbridge is a viable and arguably a preferable solution for not only
the structural, but also logistical and environmental reasons.
8.2
Parametric modelling
In parallel with the conventional design methods, was the application of generative para-
metric algorithms in the computer aided design environment of Rhino’s plug-in Grasshop-
per. Two distinct uses of these have been made in the project. The ﬁrst was the creation
98
==PAGE:107==
of parametrically driven visual models created purely for the graphic visualisation and
communication of component concepts. The other more pronounced use was the cre-
ation of integrated dynamic models, which would by the means of structural calculation,
provide added value to the design procedure.
While the initial project goal was to establish an evolutionary algorithm, which would,
on the same principles as described by Von Buelow (2012); Mueller and Ochsendorf (2013),
provide a generative tool that would aid the designer in the choice of the structural
system, this was proven as completely unrealistic.
The reasons for this are that the
design of a structural system can go into so many diﬀerent ways, it would be impossible
to deﬁne the input parameters of such an optimisation procedure.
Furthermore, the
question whether that would be practical in the ﬁrst place is raised. Considering the
process of the initial design explained in the conventional design part of the project,
it is clear that the parameters inﬂuencing the decision-making process are so numerous
and diverse that it would take an enormous eﬀort to compare them and deﬁne their
individual importance. At the same time, the "state-of-the-art" approaches in this ﬁeld
show that signiﬁcant simpliﬁcations need to be made in order to even obtain some form of
constructive results. Implementing this on the bridge, the size of the one in this project
therefore proved to be impossible. It could however be done on a severely simpliﬁed two-
dimensional representation of the model with only certain optimisation variables, but
that would hardly be representative of the reality.
Nevertheless, integrated dynamic optimisation algorithms were implemented into the
visual programming script in the form of simpliﬁes structural calculations. These were
introduced at a later developed design stage, where the model was already deﬁned enough
to have the basic structural system, geometry, and components outlined. The program
was created however, in a way where all of these remained input variables which could
be altered at any given time. Importance was put on the simplicity of the results of
the automatic dynamic algorithms in order to be able to obtain instantaneous feedback
of the proposal eﬃciency as the design space was explored. The results were therefore
represented as utilisation values of the design according to calculation procedures deﬁned
by the Eurocode standards. The utilisation values were presented for the ultimate as well
as serviceability limit states based on the load combinations deﬁned by the user.
It has been proven through validation examples, that the parametric structural en-
gineering tool Karamba can be used with suﬃcient accuracy with timber, even though
it has primarily been made for concrete and steel materials.
Nonetheless, it showed
signiﬁcant setbacks and limitations in this regard as well. Because there are no native
cross-sections for timber elements in the program, these need to be modiﬁed from steel
or concrete ones. This limits the analysis to a small range of proﬁle possibilities, and
even those need to be double checked in order to prove that they behave correctly. Fur-
thermore, the extent of the use of Karamba with timber structures was the output of the
internal forces, moments, deﬂections, and natural frequencies, which is why in order to
obtain the mentioned utilisation results, these needed to be processed through additional
user-deﬁned algorithms.
The downsides of this entire procedure, is the shear amount of time it takes to create
an integrated dynamic algorithm. During the modelling a large number of components
had to be speciﬁcally made for the use with this particular project.
And while the
parametric scripts were written with the possibility of reuse in mind (and were reused for
99
==PAGE:108==
the purposes of a cable-stayed alternative to the girder beam bridge), it is questionable
how useful they would be on structural forms with more signiﬁcant diﬀerences such as
the truss- or an arch-type system. However, if enough time was invested into a creation
of a larger component library which would cover all structural systems of bridges, the
reuse of components on further projects would be a considerable advantage.
In conclusion, the use of parametric environments does add value to the design process
in a way where the project has a better deﬁnition of the feasibility during the transition
into the detailed stage, but the use of these tools is not economically justiﬁed in the
earliest pre-conceptual design stages, where the design freedom is too great and the
design is subject to very rapid and signiﬁcant changes.
8.3
Future work
As explained in Chapter 3, the most important aspect of computational design tools
created for the early design, apart from a comprehensive output of results, is a user
friendly graphical interface. The script created for the purposes of this thesis is in no way
a simple and user-friendly way of operating the algorithms. Therefore, for this script to
be useful for anyone else but the author, it would need to be thoroughly restructured,
and transformed so that the only instance of the script the user would be exposed to
would be the input parameter panel, a visual viewport of the resulting model, and the
numerical result panel. In this way, even individuals with limited or no knowledge of
visual programming in parametric environments would be able to beneﬁt from the use of
the program.
Along with this, the obvious topic for further research is the implementation of other
structural systems in order to construct a framework for the design freedom beyond just
a single type of structural form. But such research will not be economically proﬁtable
