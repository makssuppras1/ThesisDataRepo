Abstract 
Enteroaggregative Escherichia coli, commonly known as EAEC is a pathogenic E.coli associated 
with travelers diarrhea and endemic diarrhea.   It was characterised  in 1985 by its distinctive 
stacked-brick pattern and was first associated with diarrhea in 1987 during an outbreak in Santiago. 
The pathogenesis are reliant of the five types of aggregative adherence fimbriae which facilitates 
the colonisation of the intestinal tract.  
The  binding properties, of different EAEC strains, to colon cancer cell line T84 and bladder cancer 
cell line 5637 were examined in this study. 10 different strains were used in the study including 4 
EAEC strains. Two strains were constructed during the study by transforming pACYC184 and 
pACYC184-AAF/5-UK into MG1655. D-mannose were added to the experiments with T84 to 
investigate the role of type 1 pili in adhesion and invasion.    
The results from the celle-adhesion/invasion assay revealed that C555-91, C122-96 and ESBL-
2015-0001 are able to adhere to 5637, but unable to invade this cell line.  
C555-91, C338-14, C122-96, ESBL-2015-0001 and MG1655-pACYC184-AAF/5-UK are able to 
adhere to T84 regardless of the presence of D-mannose. C122-96,  ESBL-2015-0001 and MG1655-
pACYC184-AAF/5-UK are able to invade T84 cells when D-mannose are not present, indicating 
that type 1 pili plays a role in invasion of T84 cells.

Introduction 
Escherichia coli 
Escherichia coli(E.coli) is a gram-negative short straight bacillus which does not form spores. It is 
usually mobile by using peritrichous flagella and it often fimbriates. E.coli is present in both 
humans and animals as a part of the normal microbiota. Furthermore, it can be found in the nature 
in soil, water or other sites usually contaminated by faecal matter.(Sussman, 1997)    
As mentioned, most E.coli are harmless commensal bacteria living in the human gastrointestinal 
tract but some strains have deviated from this niche and have adapted specific virulence factors 
which enables them to cause disease in healthy humans. (Harrington, Dudley, & Nataro, 2006)  
Some of the clinical syndromes are urinary tract infection, septicemia, gastroenteritis, and 
meningiti(Harrington et al., 2006). Some of the pathogenic E.coli are: uropathogenic E.coli(UPEC),

Type 1 pili  
One of the most common appendages present on the cell surface for E.coli and many other gram 
negative bacteria are type 1 pili. Type 1 pili have been shown to promote adhesion to various cell 
types. Type 1 pili are a heteropolymer composed of FimA, FimF, FimG and FimH. The minor 
subunit FimH are responsible for the bacterias ability to adhere cells(Boudeau, Barnich, & 
Darfeuille-Michaud, 2001). Studies have revealed that type 1 pili plays a role in invasion of human 
bladder epithelial cells(Boudeau et al., 2001). The expression of type 1 pili are controlled by the fim 
switch. The fim switch is an invertible DNA element containing the promoter for fimA; the gene 
encoding FimA. When the switch is "on" the bacteria is in a fimbriated  state while when it is "off" 
it is in a nonfimbriated state.(Struve, Bojer, & Krogfelt, 2008) The fim switch are influenced by 
growth conditions e.g. in broth most bacterial populations are piliated while no or few pili are 
present when grown on agar plates(Hultgren, Schwan, Schaeffer, & Duncan, 1986). Furthermore, 
type 1 pili can be inhibited by mannnose by interaction of type 1 pili with receptor 
molecules(Sokurenko, Courtney, Abraham, Klemm, & Hasty, 1992).

Enteroaggregative Escherichia coli 
Enteroaggregative E.coli(EAEC) was first described in 1985 characterised by its distinctive stacked 
brick-like pattern when it adheres to HEp-2 epithelial cells. This adherence pattern are 
distinguishable from the patterns made by EPEC and DAEC (Harrington et al., 2006)(J. P. Nataro, 
2005).    
EAEC is a pathogenic E.coli known to cause mildly inflammatory watery diarrhoea often 
accompanied by nausea, fever and vomitting(Harrington, Strauman, Abe, & Nataro, 2005). It is 
involved in both endemic diarrhea and travelers diarrhea in industrialized and developing 
countries(Harrington et al., 2005)(J. P. Nataro, 2005). The first association of EAEC with diarrhea 
was during an outbreak of diarrhea among children in Santiago, chile in 1987 (Harrington et al., 
2006). An EAEC urinary tract infection-related outbreak have been reported in Denmark 
association EAEC with urinary tract infections  in addition to diarrhea(Jønsson et al., 2015a).  
  
Pathogenesis    
The pathogenesis of EAEC is performed by a stepwise process of adherence and biofilm formation 
followed by toxin release which leads to secretion of intestinal fluids (Boll & McCormick, 2012). 
The bacteria adheres to the intestinal mucosa of the terminal ileum and colon in the stacked brick 
pattern by means of one of the different aggregative adherence fimbriae. Then some strains 
elaborate enterotoxins including EAST1, ShET1 and cytotoxins which leads to mucosal 
inflammation(J. P. Nataro, 2005) (Harrington et al., 2006) The basic features of EAEC pathogenesis 
are illustrated in figure 1.

Aggregative adherence fimbriae 
The colonisation of the intestinal tract is facilitated by aggregative adherence fimbriae(AAF)(Boll et 
al., 2012). These fimbriae are related to the Dr adhesins of uropathogenic and diarrheagenic 
E.coli(Strauman, Harper, Harrington, Boll, & Nataro, 2010). The AAFs include an outer membrane 
usher protein, a dedicated periplamic chaperone, a major subunit and a putative cap subunit and 
aafB which encodes a minor subunit. The usher and chaperone genes display a high level of 
conservation while the fimbrial subunit genes diverse (Jønsson et al., 2015a). 
As of today there are described five variants of the AAF major structural subunit based on the 
sequence: AggA(AAF/I), AafA(AAF/II), Agg3A(AAF/III) and Agg4A(AAF/IV) and the novel 
AAF/V(Boll et al., 2012; Jønsson et al., 2015a).   
The AAFs's biogenesis are regulated by AggR which is a transcriptional factor. The transcriptional 
factor and the AAF genes are placed on a 60-65 MDa EAEC virulence plasmids collectively called 
pAA (Strauman et al., 2010)(Harrington et al., 2005). Each EAEC strain only expresses one of the 
AAF alleles(Harrington et al., 2006).  
 
AggR 
The trancriptional activator AggR is part of the AraC family of activators. This family of activators 
controls the expression of virulence factors in many enteric pathogens. AggR regulates several 
genes in addition to the AAF fimbriae. Some of these genes are located on a chromosomal island 
located 94 minutes of the EAEC chromosome. (J. P. Nataro, 2005)  
Studies suggest that AggR regulates itself by initiating a positive feedback loop which amplifies its 
own expression. The protein binds to a specific binding site close to the promoter -35 site. It is 
suggested that a second AggR binding site are present, but the role of this site have not been 
established in gene expression studies. The positive feedback loop are regulated by FIS which is an 
global regulator in E.coli.FIS binds to the promoter close to or overlapping the binding site for 
AggR. FIS mutants are not able to ativate aggR expression. AggR is mainly expressed in rich media 
in logarithmic phase.(Harrington et al., 2006)

Cell lines 
The fact that human enterocytes and goblet cells are difficult to isolate and maintain over time in a 
culture have complicated the investigation of the pathogenicity of enterovirulent bacteria. 
Furthermore, the results tend to vary depending on the particular donor. (Zweibaum, Laburthe, 
Grasset, & Louvard, 2011) 
This problem was addressed in 1983 where the first human colon carcinoma cell lines were 
established. These cell lines are able to express the characteristics, both structural and functional,  of 
mature enterocytes or goblet cells. Initially these cell lines were used to investigate the structure and 
function of polarized human intestinal cells like structure and function. The cells can form a 
monolayer that mimics an intestinal epithelium barrier both physically and functionally when grown 
in cultures. The human colon cancer cell lines have been used for investigating the cellular and 
molecular mechanisms of enteric pathogens.(Liévin-Le Moal & Servin, 2013)

Celle-adhesion/invasion assay 
Preparation of bacterial cultures and cell lines 
Bacterial cultures 
In this study 10 strains were used see table 1. Bacteria from -80oC were cultured in Luberia-
Bertani(LB) broth and grown overnight at 37oC with shaking at 250 rpm. The O/N cultures were 
subcultured 1:100 in Dulbecco's modified Eagle medium(DMEM) with 0.45% glucose and grown 
for 4 hours at 37oC without shaking to express AggR.  
D-mannose (0.1%) were added to DMEM when subculturing the bacteria to inhibit type 1 fimbriae 
when appropriate.  
Chloramphenicol (30µg/mL) were added to both the LB broth and DMEM when working with 
plasmids to maintain these.   
After incubation for 4 hours the bacteria were centrifuged at 4000 rpm in 10 minutes , washed with 
PBS and centrifuged again. The pellet were resuspended in cell media without antibiotics to an 
OD595 of 0.05.  
 
Cell lines 
The human bladder cancer-derived cell line 5637 and the human colon cancer-derived cell line T84 
were grown in RPMI 1640 medium and DMEM-F-12 respectively, containing 10% fetal bovine 
serum(FBS), 100 µg/ml penicillin and 100 µg/ml streptomycin. After achieving a confluence of 
90% in T-75 cm2  flasks the cells were splitted using Versene containing Trypsin and transferred to 
1½ 24-well plates(a total of 36wells). The cells were grown to 90-100% confluence in 24-well 
plates. 
Each well were washed 3 times with phosphate buffered saline(PBS), to remove antibiotics, and 
added 1 mL fresh cell medium without antibiotics.

Infection 
 Each well were infected with 25 µl bacterial suspension (approximately 2x106 CFU) and incubated 
at 37oC for 1½ hours.   
 
Quantification of total amount of bacteria  
To decide the total amount of bacteria the 12 wells containing both non-adherent and cell-associated 
bacteria are added Trypsin containing 2.8% Triton X-100 to a final concentration of Triton X-100 at 
0.5%. 
 
Quantification of amount of cell-associated 
To quantify the amount of cell-associated bacteria 12 wells were washed 3 times with PBS to 
remove non-adhering bacteria. Medium containing 20% trypsin and 0.5% Triton X-100 were added 
to each well. 
 
Quantification of amount of intracellular bacteria 
To quantify the amount of intracellular bacteria 12 wells were washed 3 times with PBS to remove 
nonadhering bacteria. Medium containing 100 µg/ml gentamycin or 100 µg/ml amikacin were 
added to the wells, followed by 1½ incubation at 37oC. After the incubation the wells were washed 
1 time with PBS and added medium containing 20% trypsin and 0.5% Triton X-100.  
 
Lysis and spotting 
After adding Trypsin and Triton X-100 to the wells a lysis step were performed by incubating with 
shaking at 50 rpm for 10 minutes. Ten-fold serial dilutions of the lysed cells were made by using 
PBS, 100-10-6 for the total amount of bacteria, 100-10-5 for the cell-associated bacteria and 100-10-3 
for the intracellular bacteria. The serial dilutions were  spotted on LB agar plates with 2 spots a 20 
µl for each dilution. The plates were incubated at 37oC overnight and counted the following day. 
The data shown are numbers of cell-associated and intracellular bacteria relative to the total number 
of bacteria.

E-test 
To determine whether ESBL-2015-0001 were sensitive to amikacin an E-test were used.  
ESBL-2015-0001 were plated on a blue agar plate and incubated overnight at 37oC. The turbidity in 
saline were adjusted to a 0.5 McFarland standard. The solution were distributed on a LB agar plate 
and the E-test were placed on the plate. The plate were incubated overnight at 37oC and inspected 
the following day. 
  
MIC sensititer determination 
To decide the concentration of amikacin in the celle-adhesion/invasion assay a MIC sensititer 
determination were performed.  
The strains were incubated in LB broth at 37oC overnight. The turbidity of the overnight cultures 
were adjusted to a McFarland value of 0.5(concentration of 108 CFU/ml) and further diluted in 
DMEM-F-12 to a final concentration of 5x105 CFU/ml.    
An amikacin stock dilution with a concentration of 18,5 mg/ml were made by dissolving amikacin 
in sterile filtered H2O. The stock solution were diluted to 500 µg/ml in DMEM-F-12. A two-fold 
serial dilution were performed in DMEM-F-12 until a concentration of 0.97 µg/ml had been 
achieved. 100 µl of each concentration were added to 8 wells in a 96-well microtitter plate.  
100 µl bacteria are added to each well halfing the concentration of amikacin in each well.  
As a positive control each strain were added to DMEM-F-12 without amikacin. DMEM-F-12 
without amikacin and bacteria were used as a negative control.  
The plate were incubated overnight at 37oC with without shaking. The plate were inspected visually 
for growth and OD595 were measured.

Transformation 
Preperation of competent cells  
After growing MG1655 overnight in LB at 37oC the culture were diluted in LB and grown at 37oC  
until an OD595 between 0.6-0.8 were achieved. The culture were then placed on ice for 15 minutes 
and afterwards spun at 4000 rpm for 10 minutes at 4oC. The pellet were resuspended in cold milli-Q 
water and spun again. This step were repeated 2 times. The pellet were then resuspended in milli-Q 
water and kept on ice until electroporation.    
 
Preparation of plasmid 
The plasmid were purified by using the Qiagen QIAprep® Spin Miniprep Kit(250) 
After growing HB101-pACYC184-UK overnight at 37oC in LB containing 30 µg/ml 
chloramphenicol the cells were centrifuged at 4000 rpm for 10 minutes. The pellet were 
resuspended in Buffer P1 and transferred to a microfuge tube. After adding Buffer P2 the tube were 
inverted 4-6 times. Buffer N3 were added and the tube were inverted 4-6 times again. The tube 
were spun again and the supertanant were applied to a QIAprep column. After spinning the column 
for 60 seconds the column were washed by adding Buffer PB and centrifuged for 60 seconds. The 
column were washed a second time with Buffer PE. To remove residual wash buffer the column 
were centrifuged again. Afterwards the column were placed in a clean eppendorf tube and the DNA 
were eluted by adding sterile Milli-Q H2O, waiting for 1 minute and then centrifuge for 1 minute.  
 
Electroporation  
40 µl of the competent MG1655 were mixed with 2 µl of the purified plasmid in a electro cuvette. 
The cuvette was placed in the electroporation apparatus which was set on E.coli 2(E.C 2). After 
applying the electric current 1 mL SOC medium were added. The mixture were gently mixed and 
transferred to a clean eppendorf tube.  
The transformation is done after 1 hour of incubation at 37oC. 20 µl was plated on an agar plate 
containing 30µg/mL to verify the transformation.

Biofilm assay 
To confirm the plasmids works a biofilm assay was performed. MG1655-pACYC184 and MG1655-
pACYC184-AAF/5-UK were grown overnight at 37oC in LB containing 30 µg/ml chloramphenicol. 
C122-96 was grown in DMEM. 
2 µl of each overnight culture were added to 8 wells containing 150 µl LB+30µg/ml 
chloramphenicol or DMEM. The plate was incubated at 24 hours at 37oC with mild shaking.  
After incubation the wells were washed 3 times with PBS and stained by adding 0.1% crystal violet 
to each well and incubate at room temperature for 15 minutes. Afterwards the plate was washed 3 
times with PBS and the crystal violet was dissolved by adding ethanol and incubate the plate for 45 
minutes at room temperature. OD595 was measured after incubation.   
 
Statistical analysis 
GraphPad Prism v5.00 (Graphpad Software, San Diego, CA) was used to perform Student's t test. 
To be considered statistically significant p-values should be <0.05.

Discussion 
Cell line 5637 
The celle-adhesion/invasion assay with 5637 revealed that the C555-91, C122-96 and C338-14 
were unable to invade 5637 since less than 0.01% of the total amount of bacteria invaded the cells 
for all of the strains. It is still unknown whether ESBL-2015-0001 can invade 5637 since it is 
resistant to gentamicin and the experiment was unfortunately not repeated with amikacin. If it 
follows the same pattern as for T84 it is not able to invade 5637, but this has to be tested.    
 All of the three strains were able to adhere to 5637. Furthermore it revealed that the positive 
control J96 could not invade  5637, even though  it has been found to invade 5637 cells with 
approximately 6% of the total amount of bacteria (Tsai et al., 2009b). It seems like it still can be 
used  as a positive control for adherence since an  observed 5.9% of the total amount of bacteria 
were adherent in this study. This number is based on  a single experiment and should be repeated.  
Furthermore, it indicated that there is a difference between the ability of C122-96 , C338-14 and 
ESBL-2015-0001 to adhere to 5637. The observed values were 4.7% +/- 4.95, 7.09% and 8.12% 
respectively. It is difficult to conclude which one is the best due to the variation between 
experiments. The same variation was observed for the positive control C555-91 which went from 
5.91% adherent to 1.81% adherent. C122-96 and ESBL-2015-0001 were tested with and without  
D-mannose present in the media. No clear pattern of the effect of D-mannose were observed. It 
differed from experiment to experiment whether the presence of D-mannose increased or decreased 
the amount of adherence.  
   
Cell line T84 
The celle-adhesion/invasion assay with T84 indicated that C555-91, C338-14, C122-96 and   
ESBL-2015-0001 were able to adhere to T84 cells. C338-14 and C555-91 were only tested once but 
the amount of adherent bacteria were 8.12% and 9.92% respectively, giving a strong indication that 
they were able to adhere to T84 cells. Furthermore C555-91 have been found to adhere to T84 cells 
in Boll et al 2013(Boll et al., 2013) indicating that this is correct.  
It is unclear whether the presence of D-mannose have an influence on the bacteria's ability to adhere 
to T84 cells. In this study it was different for each experiment whether D-mannose increased or 
decreased the amount of adherent bacteria.

Passage number     
Another thing to reconsider is the passage number of the cell lines. A cell line can be kept alive and 
growing by transferring it from vessel-to-vessel and thereby changing its passage number. When a 
cell line has a high passage number changes in morphology, growth rates, protein expression, 
response to stimuli and transfection efficiency can be altered(ATCC, 2010).  
For example a study by O'Driscoll observed significant differences between low passage(18) and 
high passage(40)(O’Driscoll et al., 2006). Unfortunately it is difficult to say how many passages are 
too many since it is highly dependent on factors such as the origin, the tissue, type of cell line and 
culture conditions(ATCC, 2010). This might have had an influence on the differing results from 
experiment to experiment. Therefore it is only reliable to examine the patterns and not the exact 
number from each experiment. 
 
Amikacin 
The second trial with C122-96 and ESBL-2015-0001 showed no invasion. This was unexpected 
since the first trial had shown invasion for both strains and C122-96 had shown invasion in a 
previous experiment. D-mannose was only added to half of the strains so this could not be the 
cause. This led to believe that something might be wrong with the amikacin since no information 
about storage had been found. To establish whether amikacin was the  problem an experiment with 
C122-96 was performed. In the trial D-mannose were added to half of the strains and gentamicin 
was used for half of the invasion strains and amikacin for the other half. Since C122-96 were able to 
invade T84 cells when D-mannose were not added both when gentamicin and amikacin were used it 
was concluded that amikacin was not the problem and that it could be used for the following 
experiments. It was also questioned whether amikacin could penetrate the cells and kill the bacteria 
but the gentamicin/amikacin test rejected this theory. Furthermore, amikacin had been used 
successfully in the first trial with ESBL-2015-0001 and C122-96. Other studies have also used 
amikacin on T84 cells without problems (“B-542. Virulence Comparison of Bacteremia-Producing 
Neonatal Escherichia coli Isolates in a Rat Model of Bacteremia.,” n.d.).

Binding to matrix proteins 
It was not surprising that there was a difference in the binding properties of the different strains. 
Previous experiments with the extracellular matrix proteins fibronectin and collagen IV had shown 
that O42, expressing AAF/II and C338-14 had different binding properties. The properties differed 
between the strains even though the same extracellular matrix protein were used, in this case 
collagen IV. It seemed like C338-14 were unable to bind to collagen IV and some binding might be 
due to type 1 pili. In contrast 042 were able to bind to collagen IV.  Furthermore differences within 
the same strain depending on the extracellular matrix protein were observed, see appendix 
"Fibronectin and collagen IV assay" for graphs with 042 tested on collagen IV and fibronectin. 
So it was not unexpected that the strains had different binding properties depending on the cell type 
and depending on which strains were used.  
 
Biofilm assay 
A hemagglutination were performed to verify that the plasmid containing AAF/V worked but the 
results were inconclusive since hemagglutination only took place when D-mannose were not added, 
see appendix "Hemagglutination assay". This indicated that the hemagglutination might be due to 
type 1 pili. To finally conclude whether pACYC184-AAF/V-UK worked a biofilm assay were 
conducted. Since MG1655 produces a lot of biofilm itself, even more than the positive C122-96, it 
was difficult to conclude whether the plasmid worked or not. An increase in biofilm production 
were observed for MG1655-pACYC184-AAF/5-UK compared to MG1655-pACYC184 with a      
p-value of 0.0353. This lead to the conclusion that the increase was significant so the plasmid 
worked.

Conclusion and future perspective 
The data from the celle-adhesion/invasion assay showed that C555-91, C122-96 and               
ESBL-2015-0001 were able to adhere to 5637. The amount of adherence differed between 
experiments but it was clear that they all adhered to 5637. None of the strains were able to invade 
5637. It is still unclear whether ESBL-2015-0001 can invade 5637 cells due to gentamicin 
resistance. Furthermore J96 does not  invade 5637 even though it has been observed in other 
studies. 
In case of T84 the celle-adhesion/invasion assay showed that C555-91, C338-14, C122-96 and 
ESBL-2015-0001 were able adhere to T84 cells. It is unclear whether D-mannose affects the 
adherence since the results varied from each experiment. C338-14 and C555-91 were unable to 
invade T84 cells. Furthermore the positive control LF82 did not invade T84 which might be due to 
inhibition of type 1 pili by D-mannose. 
C122-96 and ESBL-2015-0001 were able to invade T84 cells when D-mannose was not present 
with mean values of 1.18%+/- 1.20 and 0.9%+/-0.45 respectively. The last experiment with 
transformed plasmids indicates that MG1655-pACYC184 were unable to adhere and invade T84 
and MG1655-pACYC184-AAF/5-UK are able to adhere to T84 cells. When D-mannose was not 
present MG1655-pACYC184-AAF/5-UK was able to invade T84 cells. In conclusion type 1 pili 
might play an important role in invasion of T84 cells.  
Future investigations could be examining type 1 pili's role in adherence to bladder cells. Type 1 pili 
have been observed to adhere and invade bladder cells so the observed adherence might be due to 
type 1 pili and not aggregative adherence fimbriae. Furthermore, the last experiment with plasmids 
needs to be repeated to establish whether adding AAF/5-UK to a bacteria without aggregative 
adherence fimbriae makes it able to adhere and invade T84 cells. This will help understanding the 
pathogenicity of EAEC and the role of type 1 pili. To verify whether the observed invasion pattern 
were due to the fim switch a PCR could be performed to establish whether the switch is on or off.  
Furthermore, other intestine cell lines could be investigated to establish whether it is just T84 or all 
inestine cells C122-96 and ESBL-2015-0001 can invade.
