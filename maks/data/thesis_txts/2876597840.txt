Abstract 
This study investigates the responsible use of generative AI (GenAI) tools for the purpose of 
academic writing by researcher and doctoral students. It examines in context of GenAI tools 
about the declaration and disclosure processes, the benefits, challenges and what tools are used, 
as well as how they are used for different purposes in academic writing. A mixed method 
approach was used, that required survey using the framework UTAUT2, along with PLS- SEM 
in order to determine the behavioral intention and adoption of GenAI tools for academic 
writing.  
We have found that hedonic motivation and price value significantly predicted intention to use 
GenAI tools. At the same time, effort expectancy, social influence, facilitating conditions and 
habit were not significant to influence behavioral intention. It has been found that there is a 
lack of guidelines from institutions and publisher, and suggests ways that can be followed by 
institution, students, and teachers to make the declaration process clear, accountable and 
transparent.

Chapter 1: Introduction 
This chapter provides the background of the study by tracing the motivations, issues, and more 
extensive context of the use of Generative AI (GenAI) in academic writing. It opens with a 
discussion of the basic role of GenAI in academic writing and scholarly work, and the ongoing 
barriers to students. GenAI tool is presented as a multipurpose tool, that has come with new 
solutions. However, there are also important ethical and practical concerns with these GenAI 
tools. 
To investigate this phenomenon more deeply, the chapter draws upon established theoretical 
framework UTAUT2, that helps explain users’ motivations and perceptions toward AI-assisted 
writing. It also discusses growing institutional and publishing concerns regarding transparency 
and authorship, which have led to varied and still evolving disclosure practices. 
With this background in place, the chapter goes on to clearly articulate the problem this study 
is attempting to address. Writes out the important research questions guiding the inquiry, and 
defines the scope and limitations of the study. Together, these sections make a strong case for 
why this research is timely and necessary in the ever-changing landscape in academic 
communication. 
1.1 Background of the Study 
The recent years have witnessed more academic or research interest to the application of 
Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) programming tools in academic writing. The GenAI 
tools should be used to enhance productivity, the quality of writing, and help with various 
stages of creating an academic paper, and it may involve the idea generation, writing, editing, 
and summary. The issue concerning whether thousands of people negatively impact the 
academic integrity and quality of writing has become a key one since the usage of it becomes 
increasingly common (Li & Wu, 2025; Chanpradit, 2025) 
There are opportunities and challenges in GenAI in academic life. On the one hand, these tools 
have the ability to streamline writing and save time, as well as make work shorter. Conversely, 
there has been the problem of academic dishonesty and honesty, including the loss of critical 
thinking, skills. Moreover, due to the increased usage of these tools, it is an important ethical 
issue regarding transparency and the necessity to publish the use of AI in academic journals 
(Chanpradit, 2025). The study seeks to address the issue of applying and using GenAI tools in

academic writing, including how researchers and doctoral students use it and what ethical 
concerns it creates. The thesis will be a part of the current debate concerning the responsible 
use of AI in scholarly writing by exploring the advantages, issues, and traditions of GenAI use 
in writing(Van Niekerk et al., 2025). The study therefore focuses on researchers and doctoral 
students engaged in academic writing in higher education. Against this backdrop, the next 
subsection specifies the problem this thesis addresses and formulates the guiding research 
questions. 
1.2 Problem Statement and Research Question 
The emergence of GenAI in academic writing has caused much controversy regarding the 
impact it has on the quality and integrity of academic writing. These tools are very popular in 
facilitating the writing process, but as it is, there is little in-depth discussions on the use of these 
GenAI tools in academic setting, along with the ethical concerns. Our main research questions 
are: 
1. What Generative AI tools are adopted by researchers and doctoral students for 
academic writing?  
2. How are generative AI tools being used in academic writing by researchers and 
students?  
3. How should academic authors declare the use of generative AI in their scientific 
manuscripts?  
4. What are the perceived benefits and challenges associated with using generative AI for 
academic writing? 
By addressing these questions, we are addressing to fill these gaps in existing research on the 
topic of responsible and transparent use of GenAI tools for academic writing. 
1.3 Research Aim and Objectives 
Research Aim: 
This research is being conducted to understand the adoption and responsible use of Generative 
Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) tools for academic writing. Also, to understand the effect of 
these GenAI tools on academic integrity, writing quality, and transparency of academic 
communication. The aim of the study is to answer questions regarding the usage of GenAI 
tools by researchers and doctoral students in the writing process and the way they disclose the 
use of GenAI tools for their academic writing process.

• To evaluate the ethical implications and challenges of using Generative AI in academic

1.4 Justification of the Study 
The growing use of Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) tools in academic writing has 
brought about serious concerns about how this technology affects the standard, integrity, and 
clarity of academic writing. Although these tools have possibilities to improve the productivity 
and assistance to researchers in different writing, there also rises important ethical concerns 
(Chanpradit, 2025; Zhao et al., 2025). The challenges that are comprised of the problems of 
academic dishonesty, plagiarism, and the loss of critical thinking skills have not been discussed 
in the full extent in academic literature (Li & Wu, 2025; Lund et al., 2025). The importance of 
this study justifies it because of a number of reasons: 
Progression of Academic Writing Practices: GenAI tools have gained widespread use in the 
academic sphere in a very short period, and the need to conduct extensive research on the 
uptake and application of such tools at different phases of the writing process has not been 
fulfilled as of yet (Chanpradit, 2025). A deeper explanation of how exactly GenAI tools are 
integrated into the academic writing process will play its roles as very important source of 
information for the effectiveness and efficiency of academic work, in terms of the overall 
impact of these tools (Li & Wu, 2025). The study will help to understand more about the 
effective utilization of such tools and preserving academic rigor. 
==PAGE:13==

Ethical Implications and Transparency: Transparency and academic integrity remains one 
of the most burning issues when the phenomenon of AI is introduced into the academic 
environment (Rentier, 2025). Although GenAI tools are widely used, the information on how 
scholars have revealed to use these tools in scholarly publications is uncommon (Hsu et al., 
2025). The gap in the knowledge about the best practices in disclosing AI use will be bridged 
in this research, where the academic work will be transparent and comply with the ethical 
considerations. The research will inform the current debate of managing academic technology 
through fostering ethical concerns by understanding which technological innovation can be 
achieved in academic honesty; in other words, GenAI application (Fu & Weng, 2024 ). 
Institutional and Policy Development: With more institutions and publishers struggling with 
the nature of integrating AI into academic writing, there is an imminent need to have explicit 
guidelines and policies on how and where GenAI tools can be responsibly used and reported. 
This study will yield empirical evidence, which can guide the institutional policy, where 
universities and academic publishers will develop the standard on the ethical application of AI 
in writing and research. This study will be helpful to develop policies to facilitate the 
responsible use of AI tools, as well as reduce the risks that may arise in the activation of AI 
tools by identifying existing practices and issues (Hsu et al., 2025)(Ganjavi et al., 2023) . 
Knowing the User Experience and Adoption: Although an increased amount of literature is 
being published in the technical field of GenAI tools, there is a lack of literature investigating 
how researchers and students experience and adopt these tools (Chanpradit, 2025). This will 
be very useful in investigating the factors that influence the adoption of technology in 
academics, also looking at the motivations, benefits, and challenges of the user. The research 
will utilize the existing models of technology acceptance UTAUT2, to determine the 
performance expectancy, effort expectancy, social influence, facilitating conditions, hedonic 
motivation, price value, and habit influence behavioural intention and actual use of GenAI tools 
for academic writing, which will add value to the technology adoption research (Venkatesh et 
al., 2003 )(Venkatesh et al., 2012). 
Long-term impact on academia: There is an increasing worry regarding the long-term impact 
of GenAI tools use on the development of critical and creative thinking and the writing skills. 
(Chanpradit, 2025; Li & Wu, 2025. The study will add to the balanced picture in the way how 
GenAI can affect the future of academic writing by delving into the considered advantages and 
disadvantages of using these GenAI tools. It will also discuss how much dependency on AI

tools may be implemented in academia without jeopardizing the acquisition of the necessary

In this thesis, responsible use of GenAI in academic writing refers to using these tools in ways 
that support learning and research. At the same time, maintaining academic integrity, 
transparency about AI assistance, and respect for existing authorship and publication norms. 
1.5 Scope of the Study 
This study will be devoted to the introduction, use and ethical question of the generative 
artificial intelligence (GenAI) tools in academic writing. In particular, the study will examine 
the role of these tools in the academic writing process among researchers and doctoral students, 
and, in particular, the value of their application perceived, difficulties, and ethical issues linked 
to its usage. 
The following areas are considered the main aspects of the scope of this study: 
GenAI: The research will concentrate on diverse GenAI applications that can be employed by 
academic authors at various phases of their work, including drafting, editing, summarizing, 
grammar correcting and referencing, among others, like ChatGPT, Grammarly, DeepL Write, 
Elicit, Scite.ai, and others. The study will address the connection between these tools in the 
process of academic writing, in particular, their role and the magnitude of privacy in terms of 
productivity and quality of writing and academic achievements (Li & Wu, 2025) (Chanpradit, 
2025) 
User Adoption and Practices: The study will focus on the researchers and doctoral students 
in different academic fields, their experiences, their attitudes, and their levels of using GenAI 
tools. Academic backgrounds (e.g., STEM, humanities, social sciences) to investigate the usage 
of these tools by different fields will be taken into account. It will cover not only the 
introduction of GenAI tools into the lives of individuals and institutions but also analyse the 
behaviour of individuals who adopt them, reason why they do it, and frequency of using the 
tool in academic writing. 
Ethical Issues and Academic Honesty: The research to be conducted will examine the ethical 
issues related to the application of GenAI in academic writing, especially the problems of 
academic honesty, intellectual dishonesty, and the threat of taking deterrence to critical

transparency, and the study will examine how scholars and institutions can approach the issue

Declaration Practices: This paper will involve a review of the use of GenAI tools in academic

1.6 Significance of the Study 
The reason why this study is important is that it will add beneficial information on the changing 
role of Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) tools in academic writing. Since these tools 
continue to be incorporated in the research writing process of researchers, learners, and 
scholars, the effects of such tools on academic integrity, quality writing and communication 
are of utmost importance (Dwivedi et al., 2023).  
Overall, this research is important as it could contribute to the further development of the 
knowledge regarding the use of GenAI tools in academic writing, their ethical aspect, and the 
overall influence on academic communication. The results will be used to guide good practises, 
institutional policies and guidelines on the responsible use of AI in academia and make sure 
that the consequences of using such technologies are good and ethical towards promoting the 
quality and integrity of academization research and writing (Hsu et al., 2025).

Chapter 2: Literature Review 
2.1 Introduction  
In this chapter, we reviewed the current existing literature on GenAI tools and their use in 
academic writing. It is important to analyze these ads the use of GenAI tools is increasing for 
academic purpose (N. et el.,2024). This study of reviewing focuses on existing GenAI tools 
and their potential applications at different stages of the writing process. It is very important to 
understand these tools for assessing their influence on the quality and integrity of academic 
writing (Li & Wu, 2025). We will also look into the use of these GenAI tools in academic 
writing (Li & Wu, 2025). It will also detect the issues, especially with regard to ethical aspects 
such as plagiarism and the risk of losing critical thinking abilities. Lastly, the review will give 
an insight on how responsible use of GenAI can be sustained, transparency and ethical 
consideration in writing academic work (Fu & Weng, 2024). It also discusses about the 
theoretical framework used for the study, UTAUT, UTAUT2 and empirical studies related to 
this (Venkatesh et al., 2012). The results of this review will be used to promote a deeper insight 
into the changing role of GenAI in academics. 
2.2 Generative AI Tools in Academic Writing 
A number of studies have documented the ways GenAI tools are integrated throughout the 
academic writing process. Blevins (2025) and Lancaster (2025) report that tools such as 
ChatGPT, Grammarly and DeepL Write are commonly used to support idea generation, 
drafting, revising and summarizing. Chanpradit (2025) also discovered that these tools can 
assist the user to write clearer and more fluent pieces as they help correct grammar spelling 
form and punctuation and propose alternative sentence structure. Li and Wu (2025) maintain 
that the features are time and energy-saving to create publishable academic works. 
There are some specific GenAI tools that can help with writing particular tasks (Chanpradit, 
2025). Li & Wu (2025) demonstrate that Grammarly can automatically detect and correct 
grammatical, spelling and punctuation errors within seconds and (Lancaster, 2025) points out 
that ChatGPT has the ability to generate outlines, propose alternative formulations and 
summarize large volumes of text. Li and Wu (2025) conclude that such functionalities can 
make academic writing less time-consuming and enhance the overall quality of the written 
manuscripts. 
==PAGE:18==
9 
 
Beyond the scope of general purpose chatbots and writing assistants, more specialised GenAI 
tools are coming into contact with digital libraries. Khalid et al. (2025) investigate the use of 
Danish academic librarians of GenAI tools including Elicit, Scite.ai, Scholarcy, Litmaps and 
Scopus AI in support of systematic literature reviews. Their study shows that these tools help 
with literature discovery, citation tracking, summarization and post-publication analysis, and 
reveal concerns about data privacy, usability, cost and ethics. Librarians in their study 
emphasize on the need for institutional support and training in order to ensure that such tools 
are used responsibly (Khalid et al., 2025). The same information is provided by Dwivedi et al. 
(2023), who discuss the fact that ChatGPT can be used to make the process of peer review and 
data processing fast through paper summarisation and draft comment generation. 
GenAI tools are also used to deal with writer's block and for structuring the arguments. 
Lancaster (2025) reports that many students and researchers resort to GenAI when they have 
trouble getting started or continuing with an assignment. Hsu (2025) notes that AI tools can 
give suggestions for outlining a paper, organizing the arguments and rephrasing complex 
sentences, which is valuable for novice writers. Kim et al. (2024) find that students use GenAI 
throughout the writing process: first for idea generation and clarifying concepts; next to draft 
text with suggestions for phrasing and style; and finally for polishing the final document. 
Overall, according to these articles, GenAI tools are now part of numerous processes of 
academic writing. As the conception of ideas, and then drafting, and revising, then can aid 
literature review. The GenAI tools could also assist in a section of the scholarly writing that is 
proof reading and analysis. 
2.3 Benefits of Generative AI in Academic Writing 
In this subsection, we will be discussing four broad categories of benefits associated with the 
use of GenAI tools in academic writing. We will discuss concepts like improved linguistic 
quality, productivity gains, support for literature review and scholarly synthesis, and 
personalized feedback. 
Better quality of language: Lund et al. (2025) reports that students often feel that AI assisted 
writing is helping them to produce more polished texts, even though they are concerned about 
their academic integrity. Empirical studies of AI writing assistant tools, such as Grammarly 
and Wordtune, reveals that such AI writing assistants can automatically detect and correct 
grammar, spelling and punctuation errors. It can also offer alternative ways of expressing to 
==PAGE:19==
10 
 
improve clarity and coherence (Li & Wu, 2025; Zhao et al., 2025). Zhao et al. (2025) finds that 
Word tune appears to be employed not only for low-level error correction, but also for 
rephrasing sentences as well, overcoming "mental blocks" and attempting to offer the writer a 
more fluent and varied sentence construction. The systematic reviews conducted by 
(Chanpradit, 2025; Li & Wu, 2025) as well have reached the conclusion that GenAI tools are 
helping to produce more cohesive, precise and stylistically appropriate academic writings. Due 
to the fact that they indicate improved lexical options, reformulating sentences and modifying 
the writing tone. This helps in giving the academic tone to papers and written manuscripts. 
These features are very useful for non-native writers or multilingual writers who use English 
to publish their academic writing. Academics, who often take the assistance of GenAI tools to 
support them with vocabulary, syntax and sentence structures in high academic writing. That 
is, in the case of the academic writing in the higher education in the form of thesis or research 
papers, GenAI have been of great help (Hoomanfard & Shamsi, 2025). Studies of advanced L2 
and multilingual writers show that some users can have conversation with GenAI tools. These 
tools possess the power to modify wording in an iterative fashion and noticing meaning. Instead 
of seeing AI generated output as something that should be accepted, it means that such GenAI 
tools can be utilized as highly valuable tools in language learning (Sun, 2024; Wang et al., 
2025). Nonetheless, a majority of these investigations are based on small sample sizes within 
a particular institution and therefore, their results might not be applied to all academic writers 
and more so in the higher education field. 
Reduction of cognitive load: A number of studies focus on the productivity gains and 
reduction of cognitive load on the academic writers. Reviews of GenAI tools in academic 
writing, however, consistently report that tools such as ChatGPT, Grammarly and Wordtune 
have helped to automate certain tasks. Such tasks are rather the time-consuming parts of 
writing. This process of automation includes drafting that can be used as guidelines to build 
upon, for the main writing. It is usually very time consuming to rephrase sentences and 
summarize long passages of text. (Chanpradit, 2025; Li & Wu, 2025) and (Eager, 2024) argue 
that in strategic ways, GenAI tools can be used to to have better outcome in a number of stages 
of the research and writing process. From outlining to example paragraphs, to making sure that 
the same terminology is used throughout the entire manuscript. Thereby allowing researchers 
to become more focused and spend more of their energy on conceptual development and 
critical analysis. Similar results are found from a phenomenological study by Patac and Patac 
(2025). This study shows that students will use ChatGPT to reduce the cognitive load, and they

can make these GenAI tools do the mechanical tasks of the writing process. They can be used 
for reformatting or preliminary drafting. Students feel that this is freeing up resources in their 
minds. This gives them more time to understand the contents of their. Beyond individual 
writing, more general views of information systems and management point to the promising 
use of GenAI.  These tools can be used to speed up work in general, through aiding in rapid 
summarization, idea generation and drafting documents (Dwivedi et al., 2023; Haber et al., 
2025). These productivity gains are one of the most reported motivations for using GenAI tools 
in academic writing. Especially for researchers and students with tight deadlines (Li and Wu,

GenAI tools for literature review and scholarly synthesis: GenAI tools provide significant 
assistance for the literature review and scholarly synthesis. Dwivedi et al. (2023) notes that 
conversational agents such as ChatGPT have the capability to quickly summarize articles and 
extract key arguments and suggest related literatures. This noticeably helps in speeding up the 
early stages of topic scoping and familiarization. Haber et al. (2025) outlines the use of GenAI 
by researchers to create overviews of new fields, identify gaps and formulate research

and summarization can assist scholars to deal with information overload and to keep a better 
track of fast-growing literatures. Ganjavi et al. (2023) mentioned that the publishers are 
updating their journal guidelines to include ways of declaring AI use to give authors a clear 
instruction. So, this indicates that GenAI is being integrated. However, concerns about 
accuracy, hallucinated references and over-reliance on AI-generated summaries indicate that 
such tools must be used cautiously in literature reviews (Dwivedi et al., 2023; Li & Wu, 2025).

2.4 Ethical Implications of Using Generative AI  
The current literature regarding generative AI in education and academic writing converged on 
a few interconnected themes of ethics. These includes issues of academic integrity and 
plagiarism, and also the effect of AI on critical thinking and learning. The need for transparency 
with AI use, issues of authorship and responsibility, and broader issues of bias, fairness and 
institutional governance. 
Academic integrity and plagiarism: Resulting from a mix of factors, a first discussion can be 
the potential that generative AI will enable intentional and unintentional plagiarism in 
academic writing. According to (Fu and Weng, 2024), educational applications of GenAI tools 
should be used responsible practices. As GenAI systems can produce fluently sounding and 
seemingly original text can be turned into assignments by the user, without proper 
acknowledgement. (N. et al., 2024) similarly cautioned that there were new forms of 
misconduct in AI and chatbot applications in education. Things like students turning in essays 
from a GenAI and publishing or submitting it as their own work (N. et al, 2024). Attending 
specifically to academic writing, Rentier (2025) pointed to the dilemmas about the issue of 
whether the use of generative AI to produce substantial parts of a manuscript should be 
considered unethical, even if institutions had not yet formalized their policies. Empirical 
studies of the practices of students and doctoral candidates suggested that those risks were 
already existing. According to Lund et al. (2025), the students were asked about their 
perceptions of cheating with the help of AI, as well as misunderstood boundaries between 
legitimate support and academic misconduct. Even back then, there is still scarcely any 
empirical research that quantitatively assesses the rate of such misconduct in published 
research. 
Critical thinking and over-reliance: A second concern that emerged in relation to the possible 
erosion of critical thinking and the capacity for independent learning when writers became 
over-reliant on generative AI. (Lund et al., 2025) found that while many students found AI 
assisted writing efficient, some students found themselves less engaged in tasks like planning, 
drafting and literature integration. It was shown that there were more sophisticated multilingual

authors who could at times seek the assistance of generative AI as a tool to not only refine their 
language, but also to generate meaning and restructure arguments, which threatened to 
eliminate the deeper reflection on contents and sources (Wang et al., 2025). Same trend was 
seen in L2 learners, who sometimes paraphrased using these tools without understanf their 
meaning (Sun, 2024). According to (Deci and Ryan, 1985), this cognitive effort outsourcing 
would destroy intrinsic motivation and chances of growing into autonomy and mastery in 
academic writing. (Deci & Ryan, 1985) stated that such outsourcing of cognitive effort could 
ruin intrinsic motivation and opportunities to develop autonomy and mastery in academic 
writing. Studies of repeated behavior indicate that the dependency on technological aids could 
increase its level of automaticity with time. As Ouellette and Wood (1998) and Limayem et al., 
(2007) demonstrate, previous usage of technology is a strong predictor of future technology 
usage.Thus, once people start using any technology, their noral tendency is to keep doing this 
iver and over again. This is a problem, as this might actually affect the cricical thinking of 
students and they will not be able to work without these tools. 
Transparency, disclosure and policy: Another concern seen throughout several literatures is 
transparency in the use of generative AI. Ganjavi et al. (2023) conducted a bibliometric analysis 
of journal instructions to authors. Hsu et al. (2025) found that publishers already have a clear 
guideline and are trying to promote these, so that it is mentioned when authors take help from 
GenAI. Synthesized studies have shown that the journals and professionals are promoting the 
mention on GenAI use in reporting clearly. Also including in what stages of the writing process

complete disclosure was necessary to maintain integrity of scholarly conduct of 
communication. At the same time, he also emphasised that full disclosure was essential to 
preserve the integrity of scholarly communication. Medina (2024) likewise argued that 
transparent reporting practices were needed so that instructors, reviewers and readers could 
accurately assess the extent of human versus AI contribution to a text. Expanding on these 
findings, Van Niekerk et al. (2025) proposed that higher learning institutions ought to come up 
with institutional policies, which distinctly outlined the application of generative AI within 
their academic writing department. It must be noted in all occasions, even those to do with 
language assistance or brainstorming. Examples of practices that interfered with academic 
integrity must be stated. Any practices using GenAI tool that compromised academic integrity 
should be mentioned clearly.

Institutional governance: Governance of generative AI in academic contexts is another matter

2.5 Adoption and Acceptance of Generative AI Tools in Academia 
Research on technology adoption provided a useful lens for understanding how generative AI 
tools had been taken up in academic contexts. Building on models such as Technology 
acceptance model (TAM) and UTAUT/UTAUT2 unified theory of acceptance and use of 
technology (Venkatesh et al., 2003) and (Venkatesh et al., 2012) showed that performance 
expectancy, effort expectancy, social influence and facilitating conditions were central 
predictors of intention to use digital technologies. Studies of AI and chatbot adoption in other 
domains similarly confirmed the importance of these constructs, including enjoyment, 
perceived risk and habit. Drawing explicitly on this tradition, (Singh, 2024) examined 
generative AI through the lens of the TAM (Technology Acceptance Model and argued that 
perceived usefulness and perceived ease of use were likely to be key drivers of acceptance in 
educational settings. 
Perceived usefulness or performance expectancy: was consistently addressed as good 
predictor for the adoption of generative AI tools for academic work. In a systematic review of 
GenAI tools for academic writing, (Li and Wu, 2025) reported that researchers and students 
mainly adopted these tools to improve text quality, increase their productivity and support 
complicated writing tasks. (Chanpradit, 2025) similarly found that the users who are involved 
in higher education valued GenAI for grammar correction, idea generation and feedback on 
structure which they perceived as leading to improved academic outcomes. Focusing on 
specific tools, (Zhao et al., 2025) found that Wordtune users saw the system as useful for 
editing wording and clarity. (Lund et al., 2025) reported that students saw using GenAI tools 
for writing as efficient and useful for managing workload. From a pedagogical point of view, 
(Blevins, 2025) and (Lancaster, 2025) showed how generative AI could be integrated into 
writing pedagogy to support the writing process of drafting and revision. This reinforced the 
sense that such tools made positive contributions to learning and performance. 
Effort expectancy and ease of use: In these studies, by Venkatesh et al. (2003) and Venkatesh 
et al. (2012) it was found that technologies that were easy to learn were likely to be adopted 
and used. This pattern was also seen in studies related to AI. (Chen et al., 2021) found that user

2.7 Introduction to UTAUT and UTAUT2 Framework 
The Unified Theory of Acceptance and Use of Technology (UTAUT) was introduced by 
(Venkatesh et al.,2003). UTAUT proposed four core determinants of behavioural intention and 
technology use. performance expectancy, effort expectancy, social influence and facilitating 
conditions, together with moderating effects of gender, age, experience and voluntariness. They 
showed that this unified model explained substantially more variance in behavioral intention 
than its predecessors across a range of organizational settings (Venkatesh et al., 2003). 
Building on this work, (Venkatesh et al., 2012) extended the framework into UTAUT2 by 
adding hedonic motivation, price value and habit to better capture consumer technology use. 
In UTAUT2, hedonic motivation was the pleasure and fun of using a system, price value was 
the tradeoff between the perceived benefits and costs, and the habit was the degree to which 
the process of using technology has become habitual (Venkatesh et al., 2012). The addition of 
habit was consistent with earlier psychological and information systems research which 
emphasised the independent role of past behaviour and habit in predicting technology use 
beyond deliberate intentions. By incorporating these constructs, UTAUT2 was positioned as a 
comprehensive model for explaining technology adoption in consumer.

2.7 Summary  
This chapter reviewed existing literatures on the use of generative AI in academic writing, 
showing that GenAI tools and specialized research assistants are now embedded across the 
writing process, from brainstorming and drafting to literature review, editing and feedback. 
The chapter has summarised the evidence that these tools can be used for the purpose of 
improvement of the quality of linguistic, productivity and scholarly synthesis. This is seen 
especially among multilingual writers, and GenAI tools also act as interactive writing partners 
offering an iterative and personalised feedback (Li et al., 2025; Wu et al., 2025; Sun et al., 
2024; Wang et al., 2025). Meanwhile, the review pointed at serious ethical issues, among which 
are the possibility of plagiarism, over- dependency and loss of critical thinking, and 
unanswered questions regarding transparency, authorship and responsibility in texts mediated 
by AI (Fu and Weng, 2024; Rentier, 2025; Lee-Price, 2024). Drawing on technology-adoption 
models such as TAM, UTAUT and UTAUT2, the chapter showed that performance 
expectancy, effort expectancy, social influence, facilitating conditions and related constructs

Chapter 3: Methodology 
3.1 Research Design 
The research design adopted in this study is a mixed-methods approach. The study combines a 
quantitative UTAUT2- based survey analyzed with PLS-SEM and qualitative semi-structured 
interviews, allowing both generalizable patterns and in-depth accounts of GenAI use. This 
combination of method helps to find out the behavioral intention and adoption patterns. Also, 
concepts related to ethics and disclosure practices. It also looks into the ethical implications 
associated with the use of GenAI tools for academic writing purpose. Combining these two 
approaches, the study is expected to find out what tools are used by researchers and doctoral 
students, as well as explore the ethical issues that come up when using these GenAI tools 
(Chanpradit, 2025; Li & Wu, 2025). 
The qualitative part of the research is semi-structured interviews. This is done so that we will 
be able to develop insights based on the personal experience, perceptions, and ethical issues of 
the participants who are using GenAI tools. This method leads to exploring the subjective 
perspectives and gives the opportunity to modify questions in accordance with the answers that 
the participants gave, and understand the individual experiences and challenges during the 
application of these tools in a more elaborated way. The interviews will also give a scope to 
researchers to identify the themes of the ethical implications of GenAI applications, including 
transparency, authorship, the erosion of skills of critical thinking, and others. 
The quantitative aspect, in turn, entails the application of a survey to collect data, with a wide 
range of usage patterns, perceptions, and attitudes of a larger sample of academics. The survey 
was done using Likert scale questions that enabled us to measure the important variables like 
the frequency of use of the tool, perceived benefits, and ethical issues (Venkatesh et al., 2012; 
Yakubu et al., 2025). This helps us to detect trends and provide statistical contrasts and 
comparisons of different academic fields and different categories of people. 
==PAGE:34==
25 
 
The combination of these two approaches gives the research design the strength of responding 
to the research questions by first triangulating the results of the study using various sources of 
data to be more reliable and valid of the findings made. The mixed methods approach is, 
therefore, a way of defining the entire complexity of the use of GenAI tools in academic 
writing. The survey PLS- SEM addresses RQ1, RQ2 and RQ4 by by quantifying adoption, 
usage pattern and determining behavioral intention. RQ3 looks into benefits, challenges and 
ethical practices related to GenAI tools. 
 
3.2 Research Approach 
This study adopts a mixed methods approach, that is a combination of deductive and inductive 
elements. On the qualitative side, an inductive approach enables us to extract insights and 
patterns from the interview data, rather than only testing previously formulated propositions. 
Since the field of GenAI is still fairly recent in context of academic writing, the study will help 
to narrow down the conceptualization of the existing ideas and shed light on the new aspects 
of the GenAI usage as perceived and experienced by the participants (Li and Wu, 2025; 
Chanpradit, 2025). The quantitative strand, on the contrary, is based on deductive logic. It tests 
hypotheses derived from the UTAUT2 model (Section 3.5) using PLS-SEM on survey data. 
This dual logic is consistent with a pragmatic stance that values both explanation of 
relationships between constructs and understanding of participants’ meanings. 
The qualitative strand is informed by an interpretivist paradigm, which focuses on participants’ 
experiences when using GenAI tools. This approach will enable us to delve into the perceptions 
and the multiplied meanings of the participants. Including the way participants view the 
positive and negative aspects of the use of GenAI tools and the ethical concerns  (Fu & Weng, 
2024). The interpretivist methodology also acknowledges that such perceptions can be different 
and can be dependent on such elements as academic discipline, experience with the use of AI 
technology, and institutional support (Patac & Patac, 2025). 
In this way, the study would be able to reflect the complexity and richness of the experience of 
the participants with the use of the GenAI tools. This would subsequently enable an in-depth 
study of the use of the GenAI tools in academic writing and the ethical issues they bring up. 
The results will add to the existing literature on the responsible AI use in academic.

3.3 Data Collection Methods 
The two main data collection techniques employed in this study are the semi-structured 
interviews and the survey targeted at obtaining the in-depth information on the adoption, use, 
and ethical concern of Generative Artificial Intelligence (GenAI) tools in academic writing. 
3.3.1 Semi-Structured Interviews: 
The qualitative data were collected with the help of semi-structured interviews which were 
carried out using video or voice calls. Such an approach allowed a flexible interaction with the 
participants and gave a chance to find out their personal experiences and perceptions related to 
the use of GenAI tools (Panigutti et al., 2022). The semi-structured form of the interview 
provided uniformity in all the interviews, but allowed the use of free discussions that allowed 
us to dig deeper into individual experiences and ethical issues. 
Participant Selection: The sampling was done through purposive sampling (Tongco, 2007). 
The participants included researchers, doctoral students and academic librarians who were 
actively working with GenAI tools in their academic writing and research support. This was 
done to make sure that the experience of the participants was more directly related to the 
questions of the research. We also took the interviews of two librarians, which gave us a more 
in-depth perspective of how the different institutions encouraged or discouraged the use of 
GenAI tools for the purpose of academic writing.  The interview guide was structured around 
the four research questions presented in Chapter 1, namely: 
• What Generative AI tools are adopted by researchers and doctoral students for 
academic writing? 
• How are generative AI tools being used in academic writing by researchers and 
students?  
• How should academic authors declare the use of generative AI in their scientific 
manuscripts? 
• What are the perceived benefits and challenges associated with using generative AI for 
academic writing? 
Recording of the interview with the participants was conducted with the consent of the 
respondents, followed by the transcription of the responses. Sometimes Otter.AI was used to 
transcript the interviews, and later checked manually.  Transcripts were analyzed and themes 
were developed from them. This analysis technique entails the process of identifying and 
==PAGE:36==

analyzing patterns or themes in qualitative data. Through this analysis, we identified themes 
related to adoption and usage patterns (RQ1–RQ2) and ethical and disclosure practices (RQ3–
RQ4) of GenAI tools. 
3.3.2 Survey: 
A quantitative survey was conducted to examine the adoption and use of GenAI tools in 
academic writing. Google Forms was used in conducting the survey to collect the data 
presented by a wider circle of academic users. First, the survey included some questions about 
the demographics of the participants. This was followed by twenty-four Likert-scale items 
measuring the core UTAUT2 constructs used in the PLS-SEM model. The constructs are: 
performance expectancy (PE), effort expectancy (EE), social influence (SI), facilitating 
conditions (FC), hedonic motivation (HM), price value (PV), habit (HB) and behavioural 
intention (BI) (Venkatesh et al., 2012). Each construct was measured reflectively by three items 
adapted to the context of GenAI tools for academic writing. The online survey questionnaire 
was effective in gathering more data in a larger and more heterogeneous sample of respondents. 
The survey was distributed through academic e- mail addresses, University platforms and other 
scientific forums, to get as many responses as possible. For the analyses reported in this study, 
265 complete responses were retained. The survey link remained open for potential future data 
collection beyond the scope of the present study. The survey findings are later compared with 
the interview themes to provide both a statistical overview of adoption and intention to use. 
Also go get a better understanding of experiences that cannot be captured through Likert-scale 
items alone. 
Additional Collection of Data

institutional policy on AI use in academic texts. This enabled us to conduct a more profound 
study of AI tool recognition in the academic medium and determine the degree of transparency 
through the examination of these sources.

3.3.3 Sample size 
As this study follows a mixed-methods design, the sample size was different for the qualitative 
and quantitative components. For the qualitative part, the emphasis was on depth of information 
provided by the participants rather than the number of participants involved in the study. For 
the quantitative assessment, an a priori power analysis for structural equation modelling (SEM) 
was used to determine the minimum number of survey responses required to test the UTAUT2 
model for the adoption of Generative AI tools for academic writing purposes. 
Sample size for Quantitative analysis using structural equation modelling (SEM): 
For the quantitative analysis using UTAUT2, determining an adequate sample size is critical 
for the validity of structural equation modelling (SEM). We used Daniel Soper's a-priori 
Sample Size Calculator for the Structural Equation Model to estimate the number of 
participants required (Soper, 2023). This calculator requires specifying the number of latent 
variables, the number of observed indicators, the anticipated effect size, the desired statistical 
power, and the significance level. 
Our model includes eight latent constructs: performance expectancy (PE), effort expectancy 
(EE), social influence (SI), facilitating conditions (FC), hedonic motivation (HM), price value 
(PV), habit (HB), and behavioural intention (BI). It also includes 24 reflective indicators of the 
core UTAUT2 constructs. For this study, we assumed a medium to large effect size of (f 2= 
0.30). This represents common values in the research on technology adoption. The desired 
power was set to 0.90 to minimize Type II errors, and the conventional significance level of  α 
= 0.05 was retained (Cohen, 2013). Based on these inputs, the calculator produced two main 
results: It gave the minimum sample size for model structure to be 200 and a minimum of 218 
participants was required to detect the effects predicted in the hypothesis.  
To also take into account that people might not respond, or submit incomplete surveys and also 
that some might be excluded during data cleaning, we added a 10% safety margin. This gave 
us the target sample to be 240 respondents. This sample size is consistent with guidelines for 
PLS-SEM, that typically needs at least ten times of the maximum number of structural paths 
that is leading to  any construct (Hair et al., 2019). By considering target of 240 respondents, 
we can ensure adequate statistical power and model stability. The actual number of responses 
we used in the analysis was 265.

3.4.2 Quantitative Data Analysis (Survey): 
Descriptive statistics: The statistical analysis involved the mean scores, standard deviations, 
and frequencies to find the general themes of the utilization and assumed advantages of GenAI 
tools. As an example, the answers to the statements like “I think that using the Generative AI 
tools enhances the overall level of my academic writing, were examined to extract the shared

Measurement model (PLS-SEM): Partial least squares structural equation modelling (PLS-

reflective measurement model was first assessed in terms of indicator reliability (outer 
loadings), internal consistency reliability (Cronbach’s alpha, composite reliability and rho_A),

(e.g. discipline and role) to contextualize the model results.

3.5 Hypothesis Development 
Unified Theory of Acceptance and Use of Technology 2 (UTAUT2) is used for this part of the 
study because it explains the behavioural intention to use GenAI tools for academic writing. 
Prior research consistently shows that performance expectancy, effort expectancy, social 
influence, facilitating conditions, hedonic motivation, price value, and habit are the main 
factors behind the intention to adopt new technologies (Venkatesh et al., 2012). There are more 
studies related to AI adoption, that have helped us come up with these hypothesis.(Das & Datta, 
2024) (Venkatesh et al., 2012) (Nordhoff et al., 2021). Following these studies, our proposed 
hypothesis is: 
 
Hypothesis for UTAUT2 constructs that have influence on behavioral intention 
H1. Performance expectancy (PE) positively influences behavioural intention (BI) to use 
GenAI tools for academic writing (Venkatesh et al., 2012; Nordhoff et al., 2020) 
Performance expectancy is the construct that has most influence on the adoption of GenAI 
tools to improve the quality, efficiency, and productivity of academic writing. PE influences 
behavioural intention in UTAUT studies and has also been found to drive adoption of AI-based 
systems. 
H2. Effort expectancy (EE) positively influences behavioural intention (BI) to use GenAI 
tools for academic writing (Venkatesh et al., 2012; Nordhoff et al., 2020). 
When GenAI tools are considered easy to learn and use, with intuitive interfaces and low 
cognitive effort, users are more likely to adopt them. Prior UTAUT2 and AI-adoption studies 
show that when the effort needed to adopt a technology is lower, this increases the intention to 
use AI-based tools and platforms. 
H3. Social influence (SI) positively influences behavioural intention (BI) to use GenAI 
tools for academic writing (Venkatesh et al., 2012). 
==PAGE:41==
32 
 
Social influence is how others, such as supervisors and peers, encourage or support to use 
GenAI tools. 
H4. Facilitating conditions (FC) positively influence behavioural intention (BI) to use 
GenAI tools for academic writing (Venkatesh et al., 2012; Saihi et al., 2024). 
Facilitating conditions refer to the availability of technical and organisational support, 
including institutional access to tools, training, and related resources. When users have 
adequate resources and support, users are most likely that they will continue using this 
technology. 
H5. Hedonic motivation (HM) positively influences behavioural intention (BI) to use 
GenAI tools for academic writing (Venkatesh et al., 2012; Saihi et al., 2024). 
GenAI tools can increase hedonic motivation, as they provide a pleasurable and engaging 
experience. This enjoyment is expected to enhance behavioural intention to use GenAI tools. 
H6. Price value (PV) positively influences behavioural intention (BI) to use GenAI tools 
for academic writing (Venkatesh et al., 2012; Nordhoff et al., 2020). 
Price value is the balance between the monetary or time costs and the perceived benefits of 
using GenAI tools for academic work. Even when institutions provide free access, users may 
still weigh subscription costs or the time needed to learn the tool. UTAUT2 research suggests 
that favourable price value strengthens intention to adopt technologies and AI-based systems. 
H7. Habit (HB) positively influences behavioural intention (BI) to use GenAI tools for 
academic writing (Venkatesh et al., 2012). 
Habit is the extent to which using GenAI tools has become automatic. In UTAUT2, habit is an 
important factor influencing behavioural intention, particularly in contexts where users 
frequently rely on digital tools. Studies show that once someone starts using GenAI tools for 
academic writing, they tend to use them more often. 
Together, H1–H7 specify the structural paths in the PLS-SEM model, in which behavioral 
intention to use GenAI tools (BI) for academic writing is predicted by the seven UTAUT2 
constructs (PE, EE, SI, FC, HM, PV and HB).

3.5 Ethical Considerations 
This research is an important topic that needs to consider ethical issues. In order to make the 
study ethically sound, the following were done: 
Informed consent: All the participants were given an informed consent form. This included 
information on the aim of the study. It also had information on whether they were under 
compulsion or not to take part in the study, and also how their data is going to be used. It also 
had information about the length of the interview time. The participants of the study were 
requested to sign the consent form before starting the interview. 
Confidentiality: The identity of the participants was confidential. Anonymity was also upheld 
in the interview transcripts and final report by use of pseudonyms. The information obtained 
regarding the subjects was kept safe and was only available to the researchers. 
Right to withdraw: It was explained to the participants that they could leave the interview at 
any point with no effects. Their right to withdraw during the interview process was reminded 
to them. 
Data protection: All records, transcripts, and survey data were stored on password protected 
drives in accordance with institutional data protection guidelines. 
3.6 Limitations of the Study 
Though the present study is highly informative regarding the technology of using GenAI in 
research writing, some shortcomings are present. To begin with, the research was based on 
non-probability sampling (purposive interviews and a voluntary survey). That is why, these 
results cannot be generalized. Even though the sample represented scholars of various functions 
and fields, it might not reflect all researchers and students who use GenAI tools. Further 
research may utilize probability sampling and involve more different institutions and countries 
to compare practices in different contexts. 
Second, the study relies on self-reported data in both the survey and the interviews. This can 
be affected by biases such as social desirability, recall errors or the wish to give “acceptable” 
answers. The survey was anonymous to reduce this risk, and the interview questions were 
formulated in a non-judgmental, open-ended way. This was done to encourage participants to 
speak honestly about their practices.  
==PAGE:44==
35 
 
Finally, the study was capturing GenAI practices at one point in time. It cannot assess how 
adoption, attitudes or institutional policies change as tools and regulations evolve.

4.1 What Generative AI tools are adopted by researchers and doctoral students 
for academic writing? (RQ1) 
This subsection addresses RQ1: What generative AI tools are adopted by researchers and 
doctoral students for academic writing? using findings from the semi-structured interviews. 
Participants were asked to name the GenAI tools they use in recent academic writing projects. 
Their responses revealed a diverse tool landscape, which was grouped into five themes: 1) 
General purpose GenAI, 2) Grammar and writing-support tools, 3) literature search, 4) 
Reference management and citation, 5) Coding assistance 
Use of general-purpose GenAI tools for writing support: Across the interviews, participants 
reported that they frequently use ChatGPT and Google’s Gemini. Several participants 
described these as tools they used “on a regular basis,” with one noting that “most people are 
using ChatGPT and Gemini” for both writing and research tasks. Participants added, they use 
GenAI to explore topics, understand difficult articles and support brainstorming and decision 
making. Some participants also mentioned Microsoft Copilot, Claude, Perplexity, Monica and 
DeepSeek for academic work. Participants explained that the only GenAI tool officially 
provided was Microsoft Copilot through their University account. Many researchers and

students still preferred to “use the tools that they want,” especially ChatGPT and Gemini. 
Several participants also indicated that trust in particular providers shaped their choice of tools. 
Grammar and Writing- Support Tools: Grammarly and QuillBot were the most commonly 
used GenAI tools for better sentence structures. Several participants reported using Grammarly 
“for grammar issues” and checking spellings. QuillBot has been defined as a sentence 
paraphrasing tool, thesis writing text tool and a text-clearing tool. On the whole, the grammar 
and paraphrasing tools were found to be useful in rephrasing and simplifying text. Data indicate 
that individual tools were being adopted and at times even abandon the tools that failed to meet 
their expectations or there were other tools that were doing better. 
Literature search tools: Participants referred to Elicit, Research Rabbit, Litmaps, SciSpace, 
Scite, Semantic Scholar. These tools were described as particularly helpful for “finding the

Helping to get an overview of existing literature. One participant mentioned that they used

connected a to certain paper. These tools are often used alongside traditional databases such as 
Scopus and Google Scholar, and one interviewee anticipated that Google’s Gemini is expected 
to be more incorporated into Google Scholar in the future.  
Reference Management and Citation tools: Participants also employed AI enabled tools to 
manage notes, references and citations. Mendeley was used “for notes and reference,” helping 
to organize the litterateurs, that comes along with referencing facility. Zotero, often in

GenAI tools, that is very easily used for managing references. 
Coding Assistance tools: Microsoft Copilot was frequently mentioned “for my analysis

Some participants described using ChatGPT, Gemini and Copilot together for development. 
Indicating that generative AI is also used for data analysis and programming tasks within 
research projects. While Copilot was valued for generating code, it was not preferred to be be 
used in the writing process. This illustrated how the same AI tool could be perceived as 
effective in one domain such as coding, yet unsatisfactory in another.

4.2 How Generative AI Tools Are Used in Academic Writing (RQ2) 
This subsection addresses RQ2: How are generative AI tools used in academic writing by 
researchers and doctoral students? based on the semi-structured interviews. Participants were 
asked to describe the GenAI tools they used in recent writing projects and how they applied 
these tools at different stages of the writing process. The interview data were coded into four 
themes: (1) brainstorming and idea development, (2) language polishing and better sentence 
structure, (3) Literature navigation and research gap identification (4) Coding and analytical 
support 
Brainstorming and idea development: All participants involved in the study mentioned the 
use of generative AI tools early in the writing workflow, as it helps them in the idea generation 
process, or when participants sometimes felt “stuck” in the thinking process. These Large 
language models such as ChatGPT and Gemini were described as being used “a lot for 
brainstorming and for getting new ideas”. Several participants reported turning to GenAI tools 
to develop clear concepts and have alternative ways to approach a certain topic or problem. 
GenAI was also mentioned as a source of inspiration. Some participants said that they use 
GenAI tools to generate images or graphs for gaining inspiration, and later on they can build 
on that or take inspiration from their image to come up with their own graphs or drawings. 
They mentioned that they use it “in the ideation phase” and using them “as inspiration, not 
==PAGE:47==

directly copied”. In this sense, GenAI suggestions functioned as facilitators that helped 
participants think more clearly. 
Language polishing and better sentence structure: A second strong theme is the use of 
GenAI for language support. Tools such as Grammarly, QuillBot, ChatGPT and Gemini were 
used extensively to correct grammar, have better sentence construction and align manuscripts 
with academic style writing. Participants described using these tools to “fix grammar and 
wording,” for “paraphrasing my sentences,” and to ensure “proper grammar style” and an 
appropriate “academic tone.” 
For several international students, language-focused GenAI functions were particularly 
important for overcoming perceived linguistic disadvantages. One of the participants stated 
that the scientific ideas in the manuscript preserve their own, yet, they needed AI tools to be 
used in writing and structuring the manuscript, stating that the language barriers were the 
reason as to why the assistance would be useful. Some were also quoted saying that the tools 
have assisted them to be more confident in what they are handing in. Several participants 
emphasised that these tools helped them achieve better output with less effort and time. 
Literature navigation and research gap identification: Participants also used GenAI 
research tools for the purpose of literature review and conceptual understanding. Zotero, 
Semantic Scholar AI, SciSpace, Elicit, Research Rabbit, and Perplexity were used to locate and 
organize relevant publications, summarize key points, and explore potential research gaps. 
Some participants reported using Zotero and Semantic Scholar to “used to organize and manage 
references and support literature handling” while some participants relied on SciSpace and 
similar tools to “help me understand what those articles are saying” without reading the whole 
paper. 
GenAI tools were further described as providing better synthesis. Perplexity, for instance, was 
used to “find the gap and the contributions from that paper,” that is, to discover both the main 
contributions and possible openings for further research, and to suggest related work, 
effectively reducing efforts in searching papers, so that they can invest this time in their own 
projects. In several cases, these tools were said to make reading and reviewing feel more 
manageable and to “help finish a work in a limited time.” Such uses illustrate how generative 
AI tools are integrated into the literature review process as both discovery and review aids, 
while the ultimate decisions about relevance of the study and research gaps exploration is to be 
decided by the participants themselves.

Coding and analytical support: Along with text generation and editing, participants use 
GenAI tools to support coding aspects of their academic work. GenAI tool copilot is used, as 
its assistants in coding. It is also used to assist with statistical analysis. One participant that 
they used copilot extensively “to generate the code for R. This indicates that GenAI is often 
used for programming assistance. 
Some participants also experimented with using AI to produce visual outputs, including graphs, 
charts and images. As an illustration, they indicated having used AI to make graphs and charts 
but one respondent thought that it was not more effective than Excel, highlighting that some

conventional methods. Image generation tools were used to create illustrative figures, with one

specific editing, such as “changing the number or color of the bars in a graph” remained very 
challenging. Overall, GenAI is thus considered as a multipurpose helpful tool that extends 
beyond writing into programming and visual design, with mixed evaluations of its comparative 
advantages.

multiple stages of participants’ academic writing workflow. These GenAI tools are used for 
brainstorming and idea development through literature search to drafting, language polishing, 
coding, analysis and visualization. Participants generally adopted GenAI tools to make writing 
and research faster, more accurate and more manageable. Especially for those facing language 
barriers or complex analytical tasks. In this way, GenAI was integrated as a flexible, task-
specific assistant rather than used only at a single stage. 
The patterns are similar to previous research, showing that GenAI tools are used for idea 
generation, literature search, drafting, and writing process (Chanpradit, 2025; Li & Wu, 2025; 
Lund et al., 2025; Khalid et al., 2025). In Chapter 5, the interview findings on how GenAI is 
used will be compared with the quantitative results for performance expectancy, effort 
expectancy and hedonic motivation.

4.3 How should academic authors declare the use of generative AI in their 
scientific manuscripts? (RQ3) 
This subsection addresses RQ3: How should academic authors declare the use of generative 
AI in their scientific manuscripts? using findings from the semi-structured interviews. 
Participants were asked where and how AI use should be declared and what guidelines they 
were aware of in their institutions, journals or professional communities. Their responses were 
grouped into four themes: 1) Necessity of Disclosure, 2) Preferred Locations for AI 
Declarations, 3) Extent and Detail of Disclosure, 4) Institutional Policy for Disclosure 
1)Necessity of Disclosure: Participants pointed AI disclosure as a requirement rather than an 
optional mention. Students reported that whenever generative AI had contributed to 
methodological or formal documentation, they “have to mention” that it was used, emphasizing 
that using GenAI was “not wrong,” but that “it has to be mentioned” because the resulting text 
did not represent only their own thoughts. One interviewee went further and argued that “it 
should be mandatory” for authors to indicate when they had used generative AI, suggesting 
that undisclosed AI assistance would be incompatible with academic honesty. 
Participants often linked this sense of necessity to ideas of research integrity and responsibility. 
Some respondents said that that writer must admit the utilization of AI and that it must be 
conveyed that it was mentioned whenever GenAI had influenced the word choice or the 
composition of a document. Meanwhile, others have made the distinction between AI-aided 
phrasing and intellectual property, pointing out that they have taken help and offered it to AI 
but said that the thought is their own. This difference affected the level of commitment they 
had to reveal the use of GenAI tools, particularly when the policies attempted to vary. As stated 
by one participant, the majority of the journals are open to AI, a few do not and said she was 
not concerned when journals stated clearly whether they accepted or were not accepting AI, 
but that she was also concerned about her paper when the rules were ambiguous.  Overall, 
however, the dominant view was that normalizing open acknowledgement of AI use was 
preferable to concealing it. 
  
2)Preferred Locations for AI Declarations: Respondents cited various places in the 
manuscripts where AI application may also be proclaimed. The most mentioned included the 
acknowledgment section. Students and librarians suggested that authors could “acknowledge 
==PAGE:50==
41 
 
that they took help from AI” there, for example by stating that they “used AI tools for 
formatting or organizing the thesis,” while clarifying that “the research idea is our own.” 
Several interviewees referred to journal practices that have already formalized this expectation, 
such as Springer Nature, a publisher which required authors to acknowledge AI use for 
formatting or writing assistance. Others summarized their own practice simply by saying they 
would “mention AI in the acknowledgement”. 
Besides recognitions, there were also participants who suggested more discrete forms. One of 
the interviewees proposed that an author could include in a footer or endnote a declaration of 
using AI and the way it was used and leave it to the reader who would have preferred to see 
this information, but not to break the flow of the main store. These proposals showed how they 
wanted to offer a balance between readability and transparency although this is less frequently 
said. Concerning the information and sources, one of the participants emphasized that they are 
expected to write as per established patterns such as IEEE and, moreover, they possess great 
references, which are reasons why propositions written by AI, however, should be referenced 
in such conventional forms of writing scholarly papers. The participants, on average, put the 
acknowledgments section on the primary venue of brevity AI disclosures statements. 
3)Extent and Detail of Disclosure: Despite the general consensus that the use of AI should be 
disclosed, respondents stated that they are not confident that the non-disclosure should be as 
detailed as possible. One of the interviewees posed the question of how minute of a detail one 
should declare, especially in cases when it was applied to the language that had been run 
through tools like Grammarly to make it easier to read or comprehend. They stated that such 
language polishing, they needed to know, deserved recognition but that they need not take it to 
maddening extremes, such as enumerating every instance of synonyms proposed, every two-
lines-in-a-sentence rewritten, etc. It was sufficient to them to state that AI tools were applied 
to edit or format a language in general. 
Other respondents advised that statements should contain a note of the intention of AI 
assistance i.e. formatting, sorting out text or grammar support rather than attempting to 
reproduce all interactions. In the meantime, they admitted that the boundary between minimal 
and adequate disclosure may not be always strict. Other authors believed that publication of AI 
contributions is a requirement, when it is used beyond editing, but others considered their 
responsibility and journal requirements and policies. The absence of standardized requirements 
contributed to this ambiguity: one librarian noted that there were currently no fixed library 
==PAGE:51==
42 
 
guidelines on the level of detail expected and that such decisions were often worked out “in 
agreement with your professor.” These tensions indicated a shared commitment to 
transparency, but also highlighted the need for clearer guidelines about the scope and details 
of AI declarations. 
4) Institutional Policy for Disclosure: Participants’ views on how AI should be declared were 
strongly shaped by institutional, journal and professional frameworks. Librarians described 
how they advised students and researchers on copyright and moral rights, emphasizing the need 
to “reference a lot of stuff” because authors have a right to be named. They clarified that using 
an LLM that was trained on copyrighted work was not copyright infringement on its own, but 
recommended that users should carefully read terms of use, and adjust their behaviors as per 
the available referencing tools. One of the participants emphasized on the fact that, at least 
when it comes to logical mentions and references, one must keep a good record and use 
established papers only. Interviewees also mentioned that there should be formal guidelines 
from instituitions. They mentioned the Vancouver guidelines, and focused on the fact that “AI 
cannot be a co-author” and that its use must instead be declared as a tool. They observed that 
local research integrity frameworks were introducing European codes of conduct and revised 
EU guidelines. Librarians emphasized that that the key thing is that you mention it one way or 
another. Some even recommended saving prompts and sources “even if it feels not important” 
as a way of demonstrating “honesty, transparency and accountability” if questions arose, while 
also reminding authors that “the output you make is yours and your co-authors,” regardless of 
whether a spelling checker or generative AI had been used. 
Simultaneously, respondents recognized that there was still inconsistency in institutional 
mentoring. A number of sources indicated that the EU AI Act was being used as guide to 
emerging AI regulation, although also noted that local practices differed among departments 
and that educators were at disparate degrees of recognition and comfort when it came to 
generative AI. One interviewee argued that universities “should give students guidelines” 
because AI could be used, but “it should be within some limit,” while others reported that 
universities were increasingly “welcoming AI” and that professors encouraged its use. 
Participants expressed a desire for “some basic things” and “easy guidelines” (for example, 
short lists of recommended practices), but accepted that a single, stable rule would be difficult 
given how quickly tools such as newer versions of ChatGPT were evolving. Also, the use of 
GenAI tools for academic writing varies widely depending on the type of work the researchers 
are doing and which department they are from. As a result, students are often advised to choose 
==PAGE:52==
43 
 
a guideline “together with the supervisor” or to follow whatever the publisher or funder 
specified, as long as practices maintained reliability, honesty, respect and accountability  
(ALLEA - All European Academies, 2023) 
Summary for RQ3 
For RQ3, all participants agreed that the use of generative AI in academic writing should be 
disclosed and treated it as a requirement rather than an optional step. Especially when tools had 
influenced the wording, structure or methodological presentation of a manuscript. The 
acknowledgements section was the most frequently referenced in their recognition that they 
were assisted by AI, occasionally together with more finite foundations such as footnotes or 
endnotes to maintain readability (without associated ideas and intellectual input then being 
obscured under the authorship of the human authors).  At the same time, participants expressed 
uncertainty about how much detail to provide, and reported relying on supervisors, journal 
instructions or institutional policies when available. 
These opinions are consistent with the results of literatures regarding the transparent disclosure 
of GenAI usage and not explicit instituitional policies regarding transparent disclosre of GenAI 
usage (Ganjavi et al., 2023; Hsu et al., 2025; Lee-Price, 2024; Medina, 2024; Van Niekerk et 
al., 2025; ALLEA, 2023). Later, the ethical concerns, plagiarism and more issues will be 
discussed. 
 
4.4 What are the perceived benefits and challenges associated with using 
generative AI for academic writing? (RQ4) 
This subsection addresses RQ4: What are the perceived benefits and challenges associated with 
using generative AI for academic writing? based on the interview data. The participants were 
requested to narrate positive as well as negative experiences of using GenAI tools in their study. 
Their answers were categorized into two general themes namely perceived benefits and 
perceived challenges. These qualitative views can be used in the interpretation of the 
quantitative results on performance expectancy, hedonic motivation and ethical issues. 
Perceived Benefits: The respondents noted a number of perceived advantages of using 
generative AI in academic writing, especially when it comes to efficiency and assisting with 
language improvement. The tools were described by many as being very fast and convenient, 
==PAGE:53==
44 
 
to search papers and other papers and review written documents with the least amount of effort. 
In addition to speed, the participants also noted that AI support enhanced the structure and 
clarity of academic texts, assisting in creating the format of my paper and correcting grammar. 
This was considered to be of particular interest among non-native English speakers, as AI tools 
were seen to improve the readability and quality of writing and reduce the language barrier. 
Other respondents also indicated that a successful prompting could be used to generate results 
that appeared to be impressive and scholarly i.e. documentation and references that they found 
useful in sorting out the ideas and overcoming writer block. These advantages were commonly 
packaged as utilitarian additions that did not interfere with the intellectual property of the 
author but enhanced productivity and the quality of writing. 
Perceived Challenges: Participants described a number of challenges when using generative 
AI for academic writing. Mainly around reliability, accuracy, and its impact on their own 
critical thinking. The general concern was that artificial intelligence devices might alter or 
manipulate facts, a phenomenon a number of participants termed as hallucination. They 
described how AI might don a falsehood that is not actually true or propose sources that are 
nonexistent which they perceived to be highly detrimental within scholarly context. Because 
of this, they felt they had to “cross check everything,” adding extra work to verify what the 
system produced. Some also found the answers biased or too generic. Participants felt that this 
limited the usefulness of AI tools for more precise or rigorous academic tasks. 
Along with these issues, participants were concerned about what so much reliance on GenAI 
might do to their own critical thinking skills and writing skills. Several felt that frequent use of 
AI made them more reluctant to think on their own. One participant summed this up by saying 
that “the main problem is dependency,” warning that overuse of AI could “reduce your critical 
thinking or writing skill development”. These comments indicate a concern between the 
convenience and speed offered by AI and the perceived challenges. Participants therefore 
emphasized the need to pay attention to accuracy, originality and the continued development 
of core academic skills when using GenAI tools. 
Summary for RQ4 
Overall, the findings for RQ4 showed that participants experienced generative AI as both a 
powerful helper and a potential risk in their academic writing. On one hand, they valued how 
easily and quickly GenAI supported searching, structuring and polishing their texts, 
particularly for non-native English speakers. On the other hand, they were concerned about 
==PAGE:54==
45 
 
unreliable or fabricated content, possible bias and the risk of becoming dependent on AI in 
ways that might weaken their own critical thinking and writing skills. 
These impressions are in line with the results in the literature, where GenAI is said to improve 
the quality and output of linguistics, and are raising questions of hallucinations, plagiarism and 
loss of critical participation (Li and Wu, 2025; Lund et al., 2025; Sun, 2024; Wang et al., 2025; 
Fu and Weng, 2024; Rentier, 2025). In Chapter 5, these qualitative accounts of benefits and 
challenges will be discussed with the PLS-SEM results and the ethical debates reviewed in 
Chapter 2. 
 
4.5 PLS-SEM analysis of the survey results based on UTAUT2 
The quantitative data were analyzed using partial least squares structural equation modelling 
(PLS-SEM) to examine the determinants of behavioural intention to use generative AI (GenAI) 
tools for academic writing. All PLS-SEM analyses reported in this section were performed in 
R (version 4.5.2) using the seminr package. The analysis was based on the UTAUT2 
framework, in which performance expectancy (PE), effort expectancy (EE), social influence 
(SI), facilitating conditions (FC), hedonic motivation (HM), price value (PV) and habit (HB) 
were specified as direct predictors of behavioural intention (BI). In line with recommended 
PLS-SEM procedures, the results are presented in two stages. First, the reflective measurement 
model is assessed in terms of indicator reliability, internal consistency reliability, convergent 
validity and discriminant validity. Second, the structural model is evaluated by examining 
multicollinearity, path coefficients, coefficients of determination (R²) and the overall 
explanatory power of the model for BI. Sections (4.5.2–4.5.5) report the results of the reflective 
measurement model, and Sections (4.5.6–4.5.8) present the structural model outcomes. The 
moderating hypotheses for age, gender, and research experience were not estimated in the PLS-
SEM model, and the analysis therefore focuses on the direct effects H1–H7.

Chapter 5: Discussion 
5.1 Discussion overview 
This chapter will discuss about the survey results and interview findings, comparing them to 
the research questions and Generative AI (GenAI) tools used for academic writing and how 
they can be used with responsibility. The study is on GenAI adoption by researchers and 
students, benefits and challenges of using the tools, and the ethical implications of these tools, 
especially in regards to responsible use. This chapter discusses the findings of the study in 
relation to the research questions, the UTAUT2 framework and the existing literature on 
generative AI (GenAI) in academic writing (Venkatesh et al., 2012). It brings together the 
quantitative results from the PLS-SEM analysis and the qualitative themes from the interviews 
to explain how and why researchers and doctoral students adopt and use GenAI tools. Also, 
what benefits and challenges they experience, and how they view responsible and ethical use. 
The discussion highlights the role of performance expectancy, hedonic motivation and price 
value in shaping behavioural intention, and considers why other UTAUT2 constructs were less 
influential in this context (Venkatesh et al., 2012; Gansser & Reich, 2021). 
In brief, the study found that participants used a number of GenAI tools across multiple stages 
of academic writing (RQ1 and RQ2). They noticed considerable efficiency and quality 
improvements in their writing and were concerned about the problem of reliability and the 
detremental effect skills (RQ4). There was a high rate of support among the participants to be 
transparent about the disclosure of the use of GenAI despite the fact that practical guidelines 
were in many cases absent or vague (RQ3).  
Quantitatively, performance expectancy, hedonic motivation and price value are seen as 
significant predictors of intention to use GenAI tools. while effort expectancy, social influence, 
facilitating conditions and habit were not significant. The following sections elaborate these 
findings by research question, integrating survey and interview results with the literature. 
 
==PAGE:66==
57 
 
5.2 Summary of key PLS-SEM findings 
The findings of the PLS-SEM analysis patterns will later be compared with the interview 
themes on perceived benefits, challenges and ethical concerns. Three constructs of UTAUT2 
performances expectancy (PE), hedonic motivation (HM) and price value (PV) were found to 
be important and positive predictors of behavioural intention (BI). Of those, performance 
expectancy had the highest impact, which implies that the sense of writing quality and 
efficiency is a key predictor of GenAI adoption for academic writing. On the contrary, effort 
expectancy (EE), social influence (SI), facilitating conditions (FC) and habit (HB) did not have 
any significant effect on behavioural intention. This implies. that ease of use, external social 
pressures, available resources and current patterns of usage were not determining factors here. 
The model showed moderate explanatory power, that explains the variance in behavioural 
intention to be 47 percent (R² = 0.47) (Hair et al., 2019)(Cohen, 2013). The measurement model 
met recommended criteria for reliability and validity, with satisfactory composite reliability, 
average variance extracted and discriminant validity (Collier, 2020). Overall, the survey results 
indicate that respondents’ intention to use GenAI tools is primarily driven by perceived 
usefulness, enjoyment and value rather than by ease of use, institutional support or habits 
(Venkatesh et al., 2012). 
5.3 Interpretation of supported and non-supported hypothesis 
The PLS-SEM analysis tested seven hypotheses derived from UTAUT2 regarding the effects 
of PE, EE, SI, FC, HM, PV and HB on behavioural intention to use GenAI tools for academic 
writing (Venkatesh et al., 2012). Three hypotheses (H1, H5 and H6) were supported, while 
four (H2, H3, H4 and H7) were not supported. This subsection interprets these results in light 
of the qualitative findings and the existing UTAUT2 literature. Among the seven hypothesized 
relationships, three were confirmed: H1 (performance expectancy), H5 (hedonic motivation) 
and H6 (price value).  
Performance expectancy (PE) has turned out to be the best predictor of behavioural intention. 
This finding has the highest path coefficient in the model (β = 0.31, p <.001) meaning that the 
participants were more willing to use GenAI tools when they perceived that the tools enhanced 
the quality, coherence and efficiency of their academic writing. This is quite normal to 
background research on UTAUT2 which has determined performance expectancy to be the 
most significant factor that dictates technology adoption. Regarding academic writing, where 
clarity, accuracy and productivity are highly valued, it is natural that GenAI tool users, who 
==PAGE:67==
58 
 
feel that the tools can improve these parameters, are more willing to use them (Venkatesh et 
al., 2012; Nordhoff et al., 2021). The interview data supported this pattern, as participants 
repeatedly described GenAI tools as improving the quality, coherence and efficiency of their 
academic writing. 
Hedonic motivation (HM) was a strong predictor of behavioural intention (β = 0.21, p <.01). 
This observation underscores the importance of fun, interaction and the feeling of innovation 
to the adoption outcome. UTAUT2 highlights the fact that emotional and experiential factors 
might be especially important in the context of voluntary use (Venkatesh et al., 2012). GenAI 
tools, and conversational agents such as, ChatGPT, can make the writing process more 
interactive, less cognitive and more exciting, which further motivates users to use them. This 
is in line with the past works that have indicated that positive affective experience can help in 
attaching to digital writing and AI-enabled applications (Lund et al., 2025). This aligns with 
interview accounts of GenAI as making writing more engaging, less intimidating and 
sometimes even enjoyable. Especially when tools were used for brainstorming or creative 
support. 
Price value (PV) also had a significant positive impact on behavioral intention (β = 0.21, p 
<.001). The meaning of this is that the participants were more likely to adopt GenAI tools when 
they believed that the advantages outweighed the financial or resource-based expenses. 
Participants were more likely to adopt GenAI tools when they felt that the benefits- such as 
improved writing quality or saved time, outweighed any financial or resource-related costs. 
This positive attitude of value can be strengthened in the academic environment where a large 
number of GenAI tools can be used on the free level and seem to be of low threat to experiment 
with. Survey items on price value indicated that many respondents felt the time and effort saved 
by using GenAI justified the costs. The participants of the interviews also highlighted that it is 
easy to try various tools when it is free or relatively cheap. This observation aligns with 
UTAUT2, which defines the concept of price value as one of the essential factors when user 
thinks of using or adopting technology such as GenAI, which require paying a price to have 
siubscription (Venkatesh et al., 2012; Jain et al., 2022). 
Taken together, the supported UTAUT2 hypotheses indicate that the adoption of GenAI tools 
for academic writing was primarily driven by performance expectancy, hedonic motivation and 
price value. That is, by perceived usefulness, enjoyment and cost- benefit considerations. These 
constructs overshadowed the effect of effort expectancy, facilitating conditions, and habit in 
==PAGE:68==
59 
 
this study, which indicates that performance gains and perceived value played more important 
roles in ccase of GenAI adoption than usability, institutional support, and routine habit. 
Effort expectancy (EE) was not a significant predictor of behavioural intention. This implies 
that there was no significant impact of perceptions of ease of use on the GenAI adoption of the 
respondents. One reason is that GenAI technologies, especially text-based ones such as 
ChatGPT, are user-friendly by nature and do not need much learning. Effort expectancy is 
likely to become uninformed when the technology is seen by the majority of the users as easy 
(Venkatesh et al., 2012).. This understanding is consistent with the emerging results of GenAI 
research that suggest that the usability barriers are rather low particularly among students and 
researchers who use digital tools regularly (Saihi et al., 2024; Yakubu et al., 2025). This is 
consistent with the interview findings, where participants rarely described GenAI as difficult 
to use and instead focused on other concerns such as reliability and ethics.  
There was no significant influence of social influence (SI) on behavioural intention too. It 
means that peer influence, supervisor or virtual group support were not a determining factor in 
influencing respondent intentions to use GenAI. Qualitative data showed that there was some 
encouragement from peers and supervisors. However, there was no clear instructions, or 
warnings on how it can be used transparently. This gives us a better understanding of why 
social influence might not have been a strong predictor in the calculation (Van Niekerk et al., 
2025). But while GenAI is used in academic context, this might depend more on individual 
decision as to where and how they want to use GenAI tools, so social influence was weak. 
Moreover, GenAI technologies are still the subject of debate as far as academic integrity is 
concerned, which could lead the users to be less dependent on the explicit consent of the 
authorities or their peers (Van Niekerk et al., 2025). The t-value of SI (t = 1.74) indicates that 
some of the participants might have been subject to social cues, but they were not so significant 
to be significant. 
Facilitating conditions (FC) also had no significant effect on behavioural intention. 
Although access to technology and the necessary knowledge are generally important in 
adoption models (Venkatesh et al., 2012), most respondents in this study already had reliable 
access to computers, internet connectivity, and free versions of GenAI tools, which may have 
reduced the importance of FC. When enabling conditions to become widespread and no longer 
pose obstacles, their predictive power typically declines. Moreover, UTAUT and UTAUT2 
conceptualize FC as a stronger predictor of actual use than of behavioural intention, which may 
==PAGE:69==
60 
 
further explain the non-significant relationship observed here (Venkatesh et al., 2003; 
Venkatesh et al., 2012). The participants of the interview also said that access to computers, 
internet and GenAI along with infrastructural barriers as significant problems, implying that 
FC could be a necessary influencial factor, but not a significant source of intention in this 
aspect. 
Habit (HB) was also not a significant predictor of behavioural intention. It is probably 
indicative of the relative new exposure when it comes to the implementation of GenAI tools in 
academic writing. It is possible that users did not have enough time to have a habit and 
automatic usage patterns (Venkatesh et al., 2012; Li and Wu, 2025). Habit formation usally 
takes time and repeated action. Given that GenAI tools only became widely available for 
academic writing not very long ago, it is theoretically plausible that habitual use has not yet 
fully developed. Rather some users might interact with GenAI selectively or strategically 
during certain tasks or phases of the writing process, which also suggests that some users are 
selective in their application of AI, not dependent on it automatically, as also the interviews 
show.Given that GenAI tools only became widely available for academic writing not very long 
ago, it is theoretically plausible that habitual use has not yet fully developed. Rather, most users 
can also be seen to be using GenAI every now and then or deliberately to complete certain 
elements or steps of the writing process, which is also reported as being selective and task-
oriented and not automatic (not a dependency). 
Taken together, the non-significant effects of EE, SI, FC and HB suggest that GenAI use in 
academic writing is driven more by perceived usefulness, enjoyment and value than by 
usability concerns, social pressure, technical resource availability or existing behavioural 
patterns. This pattern is consistent with GenAI and chatbot adoption research, which also 
highlights benefits and value as primary motivators (Li & Wu, 2025; Saihi et al., 2024). Such 
results suggest that GenAI tools have several significant differences compared to more 
traditional educational technologies and capture the peculiarities of AI-based writing assistants 
use in the context of higher education (Chanpradit, 2025; Lund et al., 2025). Overall, the 
hypothesis testing suggests that UTAUT2 constructs related to perceived benefits (PE, HM, 
PV) are more informative for understanding GenAI adoption in academic writing than 
constructs related to ease of use, social context or habit. This pattern converges with the 
qualitative themes on perceived usefulness, enjoyment and value, while also highlighting 
concerns about reliability, ethics and skills development. These are factors not explicitly 
==PAGE:70==
61 
 
modelled in UTAUT2, but may be important for explaining why intentions do not translate 
into strong habitual use. 
5.4 The adoption and use of GenAI tools (RQ1 & RQ2) 
The first research question (RQ1) aimed at exploring the adoption of GenAI tools among 
researchers and doctoral students in academic writing. The qualitative findings showed that 
GenAI tools were used by participants from different age groups and study fields. The most 
mentioned tools were ChatGPT and Google Gemini; other tools were Grammarly, QuillBot, 
and Microsoft Copilot. They are mostly applicable in the academic writing process of 
brainstorming, grammar, finding literature and reference (Gansser & Reich, 2021). The 
flexibility and usefulness of GenAI tools are demonstrated by the fact that the participants used 
these for various phases of their writing process. As an example, content generation and editing 
were frequently performed with the help of ChatGPT and Grammarly. Literature review and 
gap identification were performed with the help of such tools as Elicit, Research Rabbit, and 
SciSpace. 
This is because the results show that GenAI tools are highly rated as tools that are beneficial 
when it comes to enhancing the efficiency and quality of writing. This is in line with the 
available studies which show that AI-based tools can drastically improve the productivity of 
writing through the automation of manual tasks that take a long time such as grammar 
correction and content creation (Amankwah-Amoah et al., 2021). These findings are also 
justified by the Performance Expectancy (PE) construct of the UTUAT2 model because the 
respondents continually said that the quality of their writing has increased due to working with 
GenAI tools. Descriptive survey results also indicated that respondents generally agreed that 
GenAI tools were easy to learn and use, which may explain why EE did not stand as a 
significant predictor of behavioural intention. Because ease of use was taken for granted rather 
than a distinguishing factor. These results are corroborated by the research works 
demonstrating that the acceptability and use of AI tools largely depend on how easy the tools 
are to use. 
5.5 Perceived benefits of GenAI tools (RQ2 & RQ4) 
RQ2 and RQ4 examined how participants perceived the advantages of using GenAI tools in 
academic writing. According to the research, GenAI tools are popularly believed to enhance 
its efficiency, quality, and productivity in the academic field (Li & Wu, 2025; Chanpradit, 
2025). Respondents claimed to have used the GenAI tools in creating content, correcting 
==PAGE:71==
62 
 
grammar, organizing thoughts, and simplifying the writing experience (Li & Wu, 2025). Also, 
the grammatical correction and language improvement feature of such tools as Grammarly and 
QuillBot were extremely valued by non-native English speakers (Lund et al., 2025; 
Hoomanfard & Shamsi, 2025). These perceived benefits are in line with the strong positive 
performance expectancy and hedonic motivation impacts on behavioural intention in the PLS-
SEM model, suggesting that users’ intentions are closely linked to their expectations of 
improved quality, efficiency and a more engaging writing experience (Venkatesh et al., 2012; 
Lund et al., 2025). 
The advantages of GenAI tools are in line with the corresponding literature, indicating that AI 
tools may be effective in substantially improving the quality of writing through the increase of 
clarity, coherence, and organization aspects (Chanpradit, 2025; Lund et al., 2025). The 
responses provided by the participants are dictated by the Hedonic Motivation (HM), a 
construct of UTUAT2, as many of them found the process of using GenAI tools to be 
pleasurable and entertaining. These tools were also seen as motivational as these led to a more 
favorable writing experience (Lancaster, 2025; Jose et al., 2024). This follows the fact that 
users that consider AI tools interesting are more inclined to keep using them (Jose et al., 
2024;Teo & Noyes, 2011). 
Besides, the respondents discovered that GenAI tools assisted them in overcoming writer’s 
block by generating ideas and offering them ways in which their writing could be structured 
(Medina, 2024). It is in line with the Performance Expectancy (PE) construct with the 
participants indicating that the use of GenAI tools enhanced the overall quality and efficiency 
of their academic work (Venkatesh et al., 2012). 
5.6 Challenges of using GenAI tools (RQ4 
RQ4 also addressed the challenges associated with GenAI tools in academic writing. The 
findings showed that there are a number of issues such as the accuracy and reliability of AI-
generated content. A significant number of participants raised concerns regarding the issue of 
hallucination in which GenAI tools may produce erroneous or fake information (Chanpradit, 
2025). This is the issue that is aligned with the earlier works that emphasize the risk of the AI 
tools generating unreliable results, especially in the academic environment (Dwivedi et al., 
2023; Rentier, 2025). 
==PAGE:72==
63 
 
The participants also mentioned that overuse of AI tools might impair their critical thinking 
and writing abilities. There were other participants who believed that the overuse of these tools 
would limit their therapeutic capacity to think creatively and immerse themselves in their 
writing (Lee-Price, 2024). This problem has been manifested in the Effort Expectancy (EE) 
construct in which some users complained that they felt that they were becoming too 
comfortable in using AI tools at the expense of their intellectual stimulation Venkatesh et al., 
2012; Yakubu et al., 2025). These concerns may help to explain why habit did not significantly 
predict behavioural intention. Even frequent users were cautious about allowing GenAI to 
become an automatic, unreflective part of their writing process (Venkatesh et al., 2012; Li & 
Wu, 2025). 
The Ethical aspects of the use of GenAI tools were likewise relevant when it came to the issue 
of plagiarism and properly referencing outputs generated by AI. (Van Niekerk et al., 2025; Hsu 
et al., 2025; ALLEA, 2023). Other participants were confused over the way to reveal the 
application of AI tools in their university writing and there were no standard methods to deal 
with AI-aided writing (Van Niekerk et al., 2025; Hsu et al., 2025). This shows why there should 
be clear policies and ethical frameworks on how GenAI tools should be used in academic 
writing. The lack of sound institutional guidance, as well as concerns regarding reliability and 
originality, could also assist in explaining the rather mediocre explanatory value of the 
UTAUT2 model as it suggests that such factors as ethics and trust are also involved, but not 
those that can be directly measured through the survey (Dwivedi et al., 2023; Van Niekerk et 
al., 2025). 
5.7 Ethical considerations and responsible use (RQ3) 
RQ3 specifically examined the ethical implications and disclosure practices associated with the 
responsible use of GenAI tools in academic writing. The research indicated that a significant 
proportion of the respondents agreed that the application of GenAI tools in academic writing 
must be reported. In particular, when such GenAI tools were used in content creation, grammar 
correction (Van Niekerk et al., 2025; Hsu et al., 2025). Many participants emphasized the issue 
of academic integrity by being transparent and responsible. 
Another reason that made participants apprehensive was that originality and authenticity of 
academic work could be undermined due to over-reliance on AI tools, which aligns with the 
literatures on the topic and obliges to note possible plagiarism and critical thinking loss as a 
result of writing aided by AI (Rentier, 2025; Kamila and Jasrotia, 2025).  
==PAGE:73==
64 
 
Although it was perceived that AI tools aid effectiveness of writing, some feared that excessive 
use of the tools may cause undermining of intellectual development of academic writing. This 
issue is correlated with the rising amount of literature on the topic of ethical issues of AI in 
education.Specifically, the threat of copying or worsening of critical thinking skills (Rentier, 
2025; Kamila and Jasrotia, 2025). These qualitative results supplement the non-significant 
survey effect of the social influence and facilitating conditions. formal policies and 
infrastructural support were not statistically significant in intention to use, but participants had 
a clear expectation that institutions and publishers should offer guidance on how to use GenAI 
responsibly, and transparently. 
Responding to these fears, respondents proposed that academic institutions and other 
publishers should come up with explicit references on how to disclose the use of AI, as well as 
how to credit AI generated work (Hsu et al., 2025; Van Niekerk et al., 2025). The findings 
demonstrate the need to develop a framework of ethical application of GenAI tools in academic 
writing, so that the ethical uses of those tools can be implemented in a responsible way to 
improve the quality of writing without jeopardizing the academic quality. 
5.8 Theoretical implications for UTAUT2 and GenAI adoption 
The results of this research project can be utilized in the application of UTAUT2 in the field of 
using GenAI tools for academic writing. The high, meaningful impacts of performance 
expectancy, hedonic motivation and price value on behavioural intention substantiate the value 
of perceived usefulness, experience value and price value add in voluntary technology adoption 
(Venkatesh et al., 2012). Contrarily, the insignificant findings on the effects of effort 
expectancy, social influence, enabling conditions and habit reveal that some of the traditional 
UTAUT2 predictors might be less effective at explaining the effect when technology is 
intuitive, and not routinely used in the institutions (Gansser & Reich, 2021; Das & Datta, 2024; 
Yakubu et al., 2025). The intermediate of behavioural intention (0.47) also shows that although 
UTAUT2 offers an effective core model (Hair et al., 2019; Cohen, 2013). More constructs, 
including ethical issues, AI output trust, AI literacy or disciplinary norms, might be necessary 
to represent GenAI adoption in academic settings more comprehensively. The qualitative 
themes of hallucinations, disclosure practices and concerns of skill erosion can be considered 
as candidates of such additional constructs and imply that the future development of UTAUT2 
in the context of GenAI should explicitly specify the dimensions of ethics and other concerns 
of trust (Dwivedi et al., 2023; Jose et al., 2024).

5.9 Practical and policy implications 
The practical effect of the results relates to how universities, supervisors, librarians and 
students can support the responsible use of GenAI tools in academic writing (Fu and Weng, 
2024; Van Niekerk et al., 2025). As performance expectancy, hedonic motivation and price 
value are the key influences on behavioral intention. Influence by institution is likely to be 
most effective in showcasing how GenAI can be used legitimately, meaningfully, and adding 
value. Simultaneously, the insignificance of the role of effort expectancy, social influence, 
facilitating conditions and habit indicates that the majority of users already have sufficient 
technical skills and infrastructure and that the choice of adopting GenAI tools is now more of 
an individual than an institutional one. This is also confirmed by recent findings of self-directed 
GenAI adoption among students and researchers (Chanpradit, 2025). This highlights the 
necessity of creating and disseminating explicit guidelines, principles, and ethical codes of 
conduct of GenAI use, instead of focusing on basic technical training, so that learners and 
scholars can use them in their writing in a responsible and transparent way (Hsu et al., 2025; 
Rentier, 2025). The desire of the participants of the interviews to get basic and easy rules, along 
with the mixed experiences of advantages and disadvantages, indicate the necessity of not only 
controlling the use of GenAI tools by the institutions, but also co-creating the pedagogy with 
outcomes of critical thinking and transparent application (Hsu et al., 2025; Rentier, 2025). 
5.10 Overall contribution and remaining issues 
Collectively, the results of the mixed method give a detailed view of the use of GenAI in 
academic writing. We found that researchers and doctoral students employed a broad range of 
tools throughout the writing process. Their intentions to keep using GenAI were largely 
motivated by the perceived performance advantage, enjoyment and value. They were also 
concerned about the reliability, disclosure, and maintenance of academic expertise. Using 
UTAUT2 to this new situation, along with PLS-SEM and thematic analysis, the study can help 
expand the current research on GenAI-assisted writing in higher education and reveal the 
importance of ethical and trust-related specifics that are likely to be included in the future 
models. Meanwhile, the blistering development of GenAI tools, differences in fields and 
institutions, and the absence of uniform regulatory guidelines imply that much of the question 
of responsible GenAI usage is open and will continue to be empirically and theoretically 
addressed.

Chapter 6: Conclusion and Recommendations 
This thesis examined the responsible use of generative AI (GenAI) tools in academic writing 
among researchers and doctoral students.  The study was guided by a mixed-methods research 
design and the UTAUT2 framework to investigate what (RQ1) GenAI tools are adopted, how 
(RQ2) they are used during the writing process, how (RQ3) they should be reported in scientific 
manuscripts, and what benefits and challenges were perceived by users (RQ4). The approach 
used by the study (semi-structured interviews and a PLS-SEM survey) is able to offer a unified 
perspective on the adoption of GenAI, practices and ethics in academic writing (Venkatesh et 
al., 2012; Li and Wu, 2025). 
6.1 Conclusion 
The overall aim of this study was to understand how researchers and doctoral students adopt 
and use GenAI tools for academic writing, and how such use can remain responsible and 
transparent. The research was carried out in the form of four research questions, which pose 
questions about the use of the GenAI tool (RQ1), its use during the writing process (RQ2), AI 
disclosure (RQ3), and a sense of benefit and challenges (RQ4). 
For RQ1, the study found that participants adopted a wide range of GenAI tools, including 
general-purpose chatbots (e.g., ChatGPT, Google Gemini, Microsoft Copilot). Grammar and 
writing-support tools (e.g., Grammarly, QuillBot). Tools for exploring literatures (e.g., Elicit, 
Research Rabbit, SciSpace). Reference management tools (e.g., Mendeley, Zotero) and for 
used for coding assistance (Microsoft CopilotThis indicate that GenAI tools are already 
integrated in the academic process (Li & Wu, 2025; Chanpradit, 2025). 
For RQ2, the qualitative findings demonstrated that GenAI tools are used at different stages of 
the writing process. These are used for brainstorming and idea development, literature 
navigation, drafting and revising text, language polishing, coding, and data analysis. The PLS-
SEM results supported this pattern by showing that behavioural intention to use GenAI was 
mainly driven by performance expectancy, hedonic motivation and price value. This indicates 
that users adopt these tools because they experience them as useful, engaging and worth the 
effort and cost (Venkatesh et al., 2012; Lund et al., 2025). 
In RQ3, the participants admit that application of GenAI in writing academic papers must be 
disclosed. Specifically in the case of tools that have an impact on wording, structure or 
methodological presentation. Participants prefer the acknowledgements page to declare their 
==PAGE:76==
67 
 
use of GenAI. They sometimes suggested also doing it with footnotes or endnotes.This 
transparency agrees with new guidelines and codes of conduct that are centered on honesty, 
transparency, accountability and proper attribution of the use in the writing process using 
GenAI tools (ALLEA, 2023; Van Niekerk et al., 2025) 
For RQ4, the study showed that participants experienced GenAI as both a valuable aid and a 
potential risk. Some of these benefits were more efficiency, grammar assistance, assistance 
with writer block and more effective organisation of ideas into word (Li and Wu, 2025; Lund 
et al., 2025). In the meantime, the users were concerned about the presence of hallucinated or 
falsified content, possible plagiarism, the loss of critical thinking and overreliance on AI tools, 
which strengthened the notion of using them cautiously and conscientiously (Hoomanfard and 
Shamsi, 2025; Rentier, 2025).  
Theoretically, the study contributes to UTAUT2-based research by showing that, in the context 
of GenAI for academic writing, performance expectancy, hedonic motivation and price value 
are the strongest predictors of behavioural intention. While effort expectancy, social influence, 
facilitating conditions and habit play a more limited role.It implies that traditional models of 
technology-adoption can be enhanced with an inclusion of new constructs like moral issues, 
trust and AI literacy as they are implemented to GenAI based academic processes (Dwivedi et 
al., 2023). In practice, the results indicate to the fact that iinstituitions, supervisors and 
publishers can all play a part to encourage responsible use of GenAI. They should provide 
guidance and also clear instructions about use and disclose of GenAI use for academic writing. 
6.2 Recommendations 
Taking into account the empirical findings and discussions in the discussion chapter, several 
recommendations can be made to support the responsible use of GenAI tools in academic 
writing. These recommendations target universities, supervisors and lecturers, students and 
researchers, publishers and policy makers. 
6.2.1 For universities and departments 
Universities and departments should develop clear institutional guidelines on GenAI use and 
disclosure. These guidelines should specify when and how GenAI tools may be used in 
assignments, thesis and publications. They should say clearly, how such use should be 
acknowledged. For example, in acknowledgements or footnotes (Lund et al., 2025; ALLEA, 
2023). In addition, degree programmes should integrate AI literacy into curricula and training. 
==PAGE:77==
68 
 
This should be done so that students and trainers or teachers understand both the capabilities 
and limitations of using GenAI for academic writing. This also includes includes informing the 
users og GenAI about bias, hallucinations and questions of intellectual ownership (Hsu et al., 
2025). Lastly, libraries, writing centers and IT services would be able to cooperate to provide 
services/workshops, online support and personal assistance regarding the responsible use of 
GenAI, with specific examples of how one should disclose and reference (Hoomanfard & 
Shamsi, 2025) 
6.2.2 For supervisors and lecturers 
Lecturers and supervisors are expected to demonstrate clear and ethical GenAI behaviors in 
their work and supervision. This involves a clear conversation with the students informing them 
about thae GenAI tools can be utilized. What will be regarded as acceptable assistance and how 
this application is supposed to be reported in the thesis and manuscripts (Rentier, 2025).  
Teaching should make his very clear to students that GenAI tools should be only used to take 
support. They can never replace the thoughts and the ability to. judge of a student. They should 
be made aware that final responsibility for the content is of the human author (Blades, 2025).  
Supervisors can also encourage reflective use by asking students to note how GenAI influenced 
their decisions during the writing process. 
They can promote practices like to briefly record prompts and outputs that were used in the 
writing process, where GenAI was used. 
 
6.2.3 For students and researchers 
Students need to understand that the genAI tools can only be used to get some help. In practice, 
this means using GenAI for idea generation, language polishing and text organization. Students 
should always have to come up with argumentations by themselves. Interpretation of literature 
findings and how they want to design the methodology should be coming from the students 
(Lund et al., 2025). 
Students and researchers should always verify and cross check each and every source of an 
information or reference, as GenAI tools have the tendency to hallucinate and make up 
information (Chanpradit, 2025). When GenAI tools have been used to write a line, generate an 
==PAGE:78==
69 
 
image or anything else, authors should mention it. The declaration about them being 
responsible should be there (ALLEA, 2023). 
6.2.4 For publishers and policy makers 
Publishers and policy makers should work on having a standardized disclosure method for 
declaring using GenAI in the process of writing their manuscripts.  Journals and publishers can 
also support this by providing explicit instructions on how to report GenAI assistance. There 
should be examples showing how and where to disclose. There should be mentioned explicitely 
that GenAI cannot be an author (Van Niekerk et al., 2025).  
Editorial policies should be aligned with broader research-integrity and AI-ethics frameworks. 
For instance, by requiring transparency, proper referencing and safeguards against plagiarism 
(Höpner & Uckelmann, 2024; ALLEA, 2023). 
On a broader level, publishers and policy makers shouls bring together academics to have 
discussions and more inter disciplinary research on issues related to ethics and AI disclosure 
with the avancement of technology (Panigutti et al., 2022; Tubin et al., 2022). 
 
6.3 Limitations and directions for future research 
Although this study provides useful insights into GenAI adoption in academic writing, several 
limitations should be acknowledged. These limitations can help to clarify the scope and point 
towards future research. 
Methodological and design limitations. 
First, the study conducted was done using a survey data that was self-reported. Such data may 
be affected by social desirability bias and inaccuracies in how respondents perceive and 
describe their own behavior. Observational or studies that required recording or interpretation, 
could add on to the biasness of the study in the context of representing GenAI use. 
Second, the study used a cross-sectional study design, which involved obtaining data at one 
point and yet, GenAI is an extremely dynamic field.  As a result, causal relationships among 
UTAUT2 constructs cannot be firmly established, and changes in perceptions and practices 
over time cannot be observed. Longitudinal studies would be valuable for examining how 
familiarity with GenAI, trust and habits develop as tools and policies evolve (Dwivedi et al., 
2023). 
==PAGE:79==
70 
 
 
Sampling and generalizability. 
Third, the quantitative sample was obtained using non-probability sampling and consisted 
largely of graduate-level students and early-career researchers. While this group is highly 
relevant to the topic, the findings may not generalize to senior academics, other professional 
researchers or disciplines with stricter norms regarding AI-assisted writing.  
Future studies in this context could use more diverse samples, with people from many 
backgrounds and study fields to compare patterns across disciplines and career stages. Fourth, 
the qualitative interviews involved a limited number of participants from specific institutional 
contexts.  
The identified themes are not representative of all the possible variants of GenAI practices at 
other universities and countries, thus being specific to a certain set of experiences. studies in 
several distinct locations may be of interest in terms of the contextual differences. 
 
Model specification and measures 
Fifth, although the UTAUT2 framework includes moderators such as age, gender and 
experience (Venkatesh et al., 2012), the present study focused on testing the direct effects of 
the core constructs (H1–H7). The moderating hypotheses were proposed conceptually but not 
estimated due to time constraints and concerns about measurement reliability. Future research 
should examine potential moderation effects to explore whether the influence of performance 
expectancy, enjoyment or price value differs across demographic subgroups.                             
Sixth, the PLS-SEM model lacked further constructs, which might be especially effective in 
GenAI academic writing, which include ethical issues, AI output trust, AI literacy or 
disciplinary norms (Dwivedi et al., 2023; Van Niekerk et al., 2025). The extension of the model 
to the consideration of such factors, as well as more advanced calculations (e.g. predictive 
validity indices), might offer a more detailed explanation of the behavioural intention and the 
actual use. 
Seventh, in the refined PLS-SEM measurement model some UTAUT2 constructs (EE, SI and 
FC) were represented by only two indicators, and the facilitating conditions construct 
included one indicator with a relatively low loading (≈0.56). Although composite reliability 
and AVE met the recommended thresholds, two-item constructs are generally less stable. So, 
the results for these constructs, especially FC, should be interpreted with some caution. 
==PAGE:80==
71 
 
Irrespective of these shortcomings, the research adds preliminary empirical data concerning 
the current integration of GenAI tools in the scholarly writing practice, how the said tools are 
perceived by their users, and how UTAUT2 can be utilized and adapted in the new setting. 
Future research that builds on and extends this work will be essential as GenAI technologies 
continue to develop and as institutions refine their responses. 
Closing remarks. 
Returning to the introduction, this study shows that GenAI tools can really support academic 
writing, but only when use critically and using ióur own judgement. By thinking about 
adoption and influence of GenAI, along with the ethical challenges, the study underscores 
that responsible GenAI use is not simply a technical issue, but a question of how academic 
communities choose to uphold integrity, originality and trust.  
It can be said that GenAI tools are already embedded in the academic writing process. As 
they become a more acceptable part of of being accepted in individuals and instituuitions, we 
should all be using them with rensibility and transparency.
