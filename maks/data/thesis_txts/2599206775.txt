Summary
Wave-force models for cylinders, such as the widely used Morison equation, captures the
overall time variation of the forces.
The quality of the model is known to depend on
the Keulegan-Carpenter number - which is a ﬂow characteristic. When the oscillatory
motion is small, relative to the cylinder size, the Morison prediction is accurate. But,
with increasing magnitude of oscillatory motion, the Morison equation does not capture the
underlying dynamics. The predictions are too smooth at the force peaks and miscalculate
the peak forces by more than just a couple of percent. The shortcomings of using Morison’s
equation have been addressed in the literature since its introduction in 1950.
In some cases, such as fatigue calculations, it is relevant and desirable to have a better
model of the force peak shapes and their relative magnitude.
Using data from a previous study, the in-line and transverse force-series were analysed.
The Keulegan-Carpenter numbers ranged from 2.6 to 39.6 with some results coming from
small scaled experiments and some from a CFD-model. The ﬁtting accuracy of Morison’s
equation to the in-line force was assessed and described by a goodness-of-ﬁt parameter.
Force measurements done at Keulegan-Carpenter numbers ≈13 display subharmonics
that are not well described by the Morison equation. In this thesis, the force coeﬃcients
are calculated and the lift forces are compared to the in-line force in an eﬀort to assess
the characteristics of the force time series.
Using Time series analysis methods, this project describes the error signal obtained
when the force series ﬁt, calculated from Morison’s equation, is subtracted from the data
signal. This provides a model that will predict the force peaks. The dominant harmonics
in the signal are identiﬁed using Fast Fourier Transform, thereby allowing a Fourier series
regression to be ﬁtted to the most dominant of the frequencies.
Even after the added Fourier series ﬁt there is a substantial remainder in the signal
not accounted for. This is treated as a stochastic part of the force series and is is ﬁtted
to an ARMA process. The ARMA method is demonstrated on a force series obtained
from the CFD-model at KC-number = 10.5. Once the order of the best ARMA process is
found, the most important input is the expected standard deviation of the residuals. The
residuals are the part of the signal not included in the Fourier series or the ARMA model.
Combining the Fourier series regression and ARMA process forms what is called ”the
Discrepancy Model”. The new force predictions are the combination of Morison’s equation
and the Discrepancy Model. When using the Combined model, the peaks are included in
the predictions made, and simulations can be made that have a time variation similar to
that of the original signal. Which performs better than Morison’s equation alone.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Oﬀshore structures are exposed to ocean waves. Good predictions of the wave forces are a
fundamental part of a good engineering design. Therefore models have been developed to
predict the wave forces on cylinders. Cylinders are used as representations of the monopiles
that are the foundations for oﬀshore constructions. Generally, it is appreciated that the
models are a simple yet accurate representation of the problem in order to be parsimonious.
This thesis investigates the current knowledge on such models and their prediction errors
with an ambition to suggest how to employ the vast possibility of statistical learning tools
that are becoming consistently popular.
1.1
Published work and state of the art
Calculating wave forces on monopiles is treated within the ﬁeld of vertical circular cylin-
ders under inﬂuence of oscillatory ﬂow and waves. Research on the topic gained increasing
interest in the sixties because of the expanding amount of oﬀ-shore construction projects.
This motivated Morison, O’Brien, Johnson and Schaaf (1950) to publish a paper on the
force exerted by waves on piles [11]. They introduced a wave force model which is widely
known today as Morison’s equation. Morison’s equation predicts the in-line wave force on
vertical circular cylinders given the wave kinematics and two dimensionless force coeﬃ-
cients, known as the drag coeﬃcient, CD, and the inertia coeﬃcient, CM. The coeﬃcients
are determined experimentally to match ﬂow conditions.
Keulegan and Carpenter presented a period parameter in 1958 [10] that relates the
water particle motion in oscillatory ﬂows to the cylinder diameter (or plate width). This
parameter is known as the KC-number. They found that the KC-number explains part of
the variation in the Morison force equations depending on ﬂow intensity and size of the
cylinder. Keulegan and Carpenter add an additional function to supplement Morison’s
equation in order to represent the force variation better.
Sarpkaya has published many papers since the seventies investigating hydrodynamics
around cylinders. His paper from 1976 and again 10 years later of the title ”In-line and
transverse forces on cylinders” presents an extensive assessment of wave force measure-
ment experiments [14], [15]. In the ﬁrst paper he reanalysed the data from Keulegan &

It is known that Morison’s equation predicts accurately at the relatively low values of

the nature of the vortex formations in this region according to Sarpkaya. In this region it
is not valid to assume the in-line force as an odd harmonic function.
Sarpkaya’s analyses include a description of the ﬁrst ten harmonics present in the
lift force.
The second and fourth harmonic dominate the drag-inertia regime.
In the
conclusions it is stated that for rough cylinders the drag and inertia coeﬃcients become
independent of Re at high β = Re/KC values 1. The distribution of the most dominant
frequencies change as the KC number increases [15].
Stansby has worked for many years on the topic and has also discussed the ﬁtting and
prediction quality of Morison’s equation. Stansby also addresses the problems regarding
force harmonics. The fundamental frequency is well described by the equation model, but
the higher harmonics are not. In some cases this gives rise to peak force under-predictions
and failing to properly include the high frequency components can be a serious shortcoming
in fatigue life calculations for oﬀ-shore structures.
Together with Billings, Stansby develops an extension to the equation using system
identiﬁcation techniques in order to balance model improvements while limiting the num-
ber of extra terms (1992 [17]). Either the extra terms are based on hydrodynamic knowl-
edge or purely on system identiﬁcation techniques, where the underlying physics are ne-
glected. Stansby tries both methods. First, by adding force terms in an equation similar
to Morison’s equation. Secondly, with (non-linear auto-regressive moving average with
exogenous inputs) NARMAX ﬁtting.
He argues that if the shortcomings of the Mori-
son’s equation cannot be improved by a history-term, then the discrepancy is caused by
something diﬀerent than vortex shedding. Their results from this paper motivated further
research into the importance of vortex shedding on wave forces on oﬀshore structures.
Later, a larger publication was published by Swain, Billings, Stansby and Baker (1998)
[19]. They compare the performance of Morison’s equation with other models tested on a
variety of data coming from diﬀerent sources. The new models are either extensions made
to Morison’s equation or models based on ﬁtting algorithms. It is an interesting approach
to identify the underlying system dynamics using the new tools for input-output data
equation models. The downside is the rather complex algorithms and methods they apply
to treat the problem. This makes their presentation of the results less clear to an ordinary
engineer with ﬂuid mechanic background. Performance improvements are obtained but a

Even though the shortcomings of Morison’s equation seem to have been known since

Time series analysis
The book ”Time series analysis and control” by Box, Jenkins and Reinsel oﬀers a
general introduction to time series analysis. It was ﬁrst published in 1970. A time when
research on time series analysis and forecasting was gaining large interest in economics.
This led to it’s own ﬁeld time series econometrics. Today the methods have found appli-
cations in many ﬁelds of science, industry and research.
A Scopus search using the search terms ”time series analysis” + ”hydrodynamics” OR
”ﬂuid mechanics” gives 558 results. A quick analysis of the publications per year shows a
positive trend. Very few of these deal with force time series and even fewer are of more
resent date.
Yet, time series analysis may prove to be a qualiﬁed tool for identifying and uncovering
information in the wave force data. And is certainly worth investigating.
Model development
As previously mentioned, it is accepted that there is no simple deterministic descrip-
tion of the forces induced on cylinders in oscillatory ﬂow that can completely capture the
inﬂuence of the many dependent variables. In recent years there has been an increasing
interest in using Machine learning and Statistical learning to identify the complex un-
derlying systems in many diﬀerent areas of research. The development of software and
inexpensive computational power has played a key role in making the methods more pop-
ular and accessible. As the amount of available data grows the methods become more
applicable.
Earlier this year Brunton, Noack and Koumoutsakos published a review on ”Machine
learning for Fluid Dynamics” in which they discuss the historical development of ﬂuid
dynamics and machine learning. Fluid mechanics is a ﬁeld that has a very large amount
of data available from full scale measurements, lab experiments, simulations etc. Making
the development of ﬂuid mechanics databases possible. Machine learning algorithms are
rapidly increasing in ﬂuid mechanics.
It is often important to precisely quantify the
underlying physical mechanisms in ﬂuid ﬂows in order to analyze them, and so Bruntan et
al. they believe that hybrid methods that combine machine learning and ﬁrst principles
models will show good possibilities for development [5].
Neural networks is one of the most well known methods of supervised learning. Gaus-

1.2
Project objective
The study by Petersen et al. [20] (2020) assessed the eﬀect of secondary structures on the
total load on the support structure. This thesis uses their data to study the diﬀerence
between the measured and predicted forces, that they found. This is called the discrepancy
signal, which is analyzed using signal processing and time series analysis. Focusing on
results in the range 10 < KC < 20, as it is a critical range for the ﬂow and prediction
quality. This is well known and was identiﬁed as far back as Keulegan and Carpenter’s
study [10]. Sarpkaya wrote that a dimensional analysis of what is needed to describe the
in-line and transverse forces leads to a very large family of plots [13]. This may have
seemed unmanageable in 1976, where as it should be reconsidered in light of the tools
available today, as discussed in Section 1.1.
Morison’s equation only deals directly with the in-line force. Yet it is clearly stated
by Sarpkaya that it is important to consider the transverse force when designing oﬀ-shore
structures for both smooth and rough cylinders [14]. One may think that the simplicity of
Morison’s equation is a temptation to neglect the transverse force during design. Bearman
et al. (1984) published a paper introducing a model equation for the transverse force
assuming a quasi-steady Strouhal number and lift coeﬃcient [2]. But such models have
not gained the same kind of attention as Morison’s equation for the in-line force.
This project includes measurements of both the in-line and transverse force to evalu-
ate whether the transverse force is of signiﬁcance to the total force and the relationship
between the transverse force and the discrepancy signal.
Due to the pandemic Covid-19 it was not possible during the project period to conduct
own experiments because the wave ﬂumes were closed and oﬀ-limits. Instead data from a
previous study was made available. The experimental study included data of the forces
exerted on the circular cylinder during waves ranging from small to large KC-numbers.
The goal of this project was to ﬁnd if there is a way that one could build a statistical

Hydrodynamics around circular cylinders
This chapter contains the relevant theoretical introduction to the hydrodynamics around
cylindrical structures. The topic of forces on vertical cylinders in oscillatory ﬂow is a well
known subject. Sumer and Fredsøe have published a book oﬀering an introduction to
the subject [18] This chapter is based on the theories and results presented in the book.
References are made to other publications, where they are mentioned.

change sides.

3.2
Validation and comparison [20]
The results from the physical model and the CDF model were compared qualitatively in
terms of force curve shapes and ﬂuctuations. The good agreement between the physical
model and the CFD-model in terms of determined CD and CM served as a validation of
the CFD model for cases with no secondary structures.
They state: In models using URANS there can be problems with predicting the drag
crisis. A main problem for the URANS models has been linked to the inadequate capa-
bilities in predicting the transition in the boundary layer from laminar to turbulent ﬂow,
which causes problems in ﬁnding the correct location of the separation point.
They ﬁnd that the good agreement between measurements and CFD can attributed
to: (1) conducting the study for an oscillating ﬂow in stead of a steady current will lessen
the dependence of the Reynolds number (2) The turbulent boundary layers were ensured
in the experiments by using the trip wires.
The lift coeﬃcient: The lift coeﬃcient was found to increase from zero at low KC
numbers to maximum values around KC = 10-20. The CFD model predicted higher lift
coeﬃcients than the measured results, though still within the order of magnitude. This
can partly be explained by having a two-dimensional model. It is described in the following
way: the vortices will typically be larger in 2D models and have a tendency to remain
large.
It is noted that the exact maximum obtained from the measured fore time-series can
be eﬀected by the ﬁltering in the post-processing. And this may have resulted in local
maxima reduction.
3.3
Comments from the study results [20]
Comments on results from small KC numbers: At KC = 5.2 the physical measure-
ments showed small high period oscillations that may be noise from using the carriage.
It is acknowledged that this could not be removed by standard ﬁltering. Yet it was still
deemed that the data was good for estimating the force coeﬃcients.
At low KC numbers (less than 5-6) the contribution from drag is very low (almost
negligible). Determining the drag coeﬃcient is therefore linked to some uncertainty at low
KC numbers.
At KC = 13 it is mentioned that the CFD data had a tendency to larger subharmonic
contributions than the measured forces.
The force-time series: The CFD calculations showed a higher degree of ﬂuctuations
than the force time-series from the physical experiments. No clear reason for this was
found.
It is suggested that it may come from the ﬁltering process of the physical re-
sults, whereby high frequency signals were removed. But it could also be the result of
shortcomings of the 2D URANS model.

4.1.3
Spline interpolation
The in-line force Fx and lift force FL are interpolated using Matlab’s built in spline inter-
polation.
The cubic spline interpolation was chosen because this is known to be good for ﬁtting
oscillating data.
Speciﬁcally it’s properties are demonstrated for ﬁtting trigonometric
functions. Cubic splines is a ﬁtting method that ﬁts polynomials between each data point
(’knots’). The ﬁrst and second derivatives are constrained to be continuous across the
knots. This avoids the problems experienced using linear interpolation.
The end condition for the spline is called ”not-a-knot”. The ﬁrst and last polynomial of
the ﬁt (meaning the polynomials connecting to either end point of the data set) must have
continuous third order derivatives as well at the points X1 and XN−1 (X is an arbitrary
data series). This constraint forces the ﬁrst and second polynomials to coincide as well
as the second-to-last and last polynomials. In other words it constraints both ends of the
data series. [1]
The velocity was also interpolated with spline functions. In the original data there
was one velocity measurement per ﬁfty force data points. The interpolation procedure
ensures that there are velocity and acceleration values for each force value. Even though
it is known that U(t) should be described by U(t) = Um sin(ωwt+φ) a visual investigation
of the plot shows that there is a drift in U. If the data from U(t) was replaced by a
parametric function one would gain a continuous description of dU/dt. A ﬁrst attempt
was to ﬁt a pure sine function to U(t). The original data and the ﬁtted data was plotted in
the same graph. This showed that the original data drifted sometimes. Since, sinusoidal
ﬁtting would loose the information of the drift, it was chosen also to ﬁt U(t) with spline
functions. But the sinusoidal ﬁtting was used to identify the value of Um and ωw which
are given in Table 4.3.

Chapter 5
Time series analysis and model identiﬁcation
Box & Jenkins deﬁne a time series.
A time series is a set of observations generated
sequentially over time. If the set is continuous, the time series is said to be continuous.
If the set is discrete, the time series is said to be discrete. Observations from a discrete
time series taken at times t1, t2, t3, ..., tτ, ..., tn can be called y(t1), y(t2), ..., y(τ), ..., y(n) or
yt1, yt2 such that yt is the observation made at time t and so on. Some time series may
have observations taken with varying time steps. But the rest of this chapter it is assumed
that the time steps are equidistant.
A deterministic time series is described by a mathematical function. Oppositely, a
time series which arises from a probability distribution is non-deterministic. The non-
deterministic time series are impossible to predict exactly and are called statistical time
series[3]. Typically in statistical analysis each observation is assumed to be independent
of the other observations. This clearly does not hold for certain observations made in
time, such as stock price, or an ECG measurement of the electrical activity of the heart.
Therefor time series analysis makes out is own area of statistical analysis. There are two
diﬀerent approaches to the time series analysis time domain approach or frequency domain
approach. Which one to use depends on what information in the data is most important.
[16].

Partial autocorrelation
The autocorrelation function takes nonzero values even past the lags that truly are present
in the underlying model. To illustrate, one can imagine a process where there is a strong
autocorrelation at lag 1. Meaning that the observation at time t is highly described by the
observation at time t −1. This eﬀect trickles such that it would seem that yt is correlated
with yt−2, but in fact it may be because yt−2 and yt−1 are correlated, due to their internal
distance of 1 lag. It is this treacle down inﬂuence that the partial autocorrelation function
tries to remove. Such that an AR(p) process has signiﬁcant autocorrelations up to lag p,
whereafter subsequent autocorrelations are zero.
One way of determining the the partial correlation coeﬃcients is to ﬁnd the autoregres-
sive coeﬃcients. The kth partial autocorrelation coeﬃcient is equal to the kth coeﬃcient
in a kth AR(k) model.

6.3.3
Harmonic modelling
Once the dominant frequencies have been identiﬁed a new vector is deﬁned containing
their equivalent integer values by rounding to the closest integer. If fr = 0 is a dominant
frequency this is skipped.
These frequencies are used to formulate a function familiar to a Fourier series. This
function is ﬁtted to the discrepancy data by least-squares-ﬁtting. The function consists
of harmonics in prioritized order by magnitude and also includes trend and intercept.
The function is called FSp for Fourier series of order p. The order determines how many
frequencies from the tables are included. FSp can be written in the following form
Fitp = c0 + a1 cos(m12πfwt) + b1 sin(m12πfwt) + ...
+ ap cos(mp2πfwt) + bp sin(mp2πfwt) + c1 · t
(6.3)
where
p is the number of harmonics included in the ﬁt
m is the integer deﬁning the harmonic. m takes integer values in prioritized order
c0 is the intercept
c1 is the trend
For example, an order p = 5 means that the 5 most dominant harmonics are ﬁtted to
the data which could be m = {3,
5,
2,
7,
16}.

Toolbox in Matlab uses. A classic Fourier-ﬁt series ﬁnds the ﬁrst harmonic and increases
the natural frequency by one integer at a time, thus not in a prioritized order. This series

Chapter 7
Conclusion
The widely used Morison equation, that calculates the forces exerted on cylinders during
oscillatory ﬂow, has been known to under predict the peak forces. Furthermore, the force
variation can sometimes be poorly described around the time where maximum force occurs.
This shortcoming can result in issues such as fatigue calculation errors.
The scope of this thesis was to investigate the possibility of using Time series analysis
to develop a model that can be added to Morison’s equation in order to have a better
description of the peak forces.
Using Time series analysis methods, this project described the error signal which is the
signal obtained when the Morison force predictions are subtracted from the data signal.
This provided a model that contains peak forces similar to the true values. The dominant
harmonics in the signal are identiﬁed using Fast Fourier Transform, thereby allowing a
Fourier series regression to be ﬁtted to the most dominant of the frequencies.
Time series analysis is a strong tool for describing the underlying process of obser-
vations made in time, such as a force time series. Therefore this project was a proof of
concept that successfully sought to assess whether using ARMA models, that are a tool
within time series analysis, was a proﬁtable approach to describing the errors made by
Morison’s equation.
The ﬁnal model found in this project was a combination of a Fourier series regres-
sion and an ARMA model. The Fourier series regression included the 6 most dominant
harmonics found in the discrepancy signal. And the ARMA model included three autore-
gressive terms and two moving average terms. Combining the Fourier series regression
and ARMA process forms what is called ”the Discrepancy Model”. The new force predic-
tions are the combination of Morison’s equation and the Discrepancy Model. When using
the Combined model we no longer saw continuous peak under prediction the way they
appeared in the ﬁt oﬀered solely by the Morison equation.

A.1.4
Leakage
A short introduction to the concept of leakage.
The Discrete Fourier Transform samples a discrete number of frequencies. If a har-
monic time signal of a speciﬁc frequency is sampled at frequency such that the signals true
frequency matches precisely the frequency of one of the ﬁnite Fourier series in transform,
then there will be a spike in the spectrum at this frequency. If on the other hand the true
signal frequency falls between two frequencies in the signal the spikes of the spectrum will
spread out. There will be spikes at those two frequencies. But there will also be spikes
of lower magnitude spread out to the neighbouring frequencies. This phenomena is called
leakage [6].
