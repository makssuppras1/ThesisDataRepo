Abstract
This thesis investigates two failure mechanisms occurring in a TimberВӯConcrete composВӯ
ite deck with notched connections. The two failures are occurring in the concrete part
of the deck, and is called concrete shear failure and concrete compression failure. In
the concrete shear failure, the entire notch is sheared off, and for concrete compression
failure, a corner of the notch fails. By using the upper bound method, it is possible to
calculate the capacity at failure, but the remaining capacity of the mechanism after failure
is unknown.
Based on the knowledge that the dissipation of the concrete is dependent on the crack
width, an incremental analysis using the upper bound method is used to estimate the
remaining shear capacity in the mechanism. For each increment in the analysis, the crack
width increases with 0.01 mm. When the analysis is completed, it is looked at how the
values has changed as the crack width increases, and the mechanism slips. In addition
to this, it was investigated what effect a change in the input or degree of preВӯstressing,
stiffness or capacity would have on the shear capacity. Lastly, the results obtained was
compared to results from an experiment performing pushВӯoff tests, to see if the results
corresponded.
The calculations showed that an increase in input, or increase in preВӯstressing, stiffness,
or capacity of the fastener, would result in the mechanisms to have an improved shear
capacity. An increase of stiffness and capacity would provide the best capacity if used on a
fastener with a higher preВӯstressing degree. Further, the calculated results was compared
to two pushВӯoff tests. The fasteners force in the experiment increased more slowly and
obtained a larger maximum capacity than calculated. When the shear capacities was
compared, the maximum shear capacity for the experiment was lower in the beginning. As
the slip increased, the calculated capacity decreased rapidly, but the measured capacity
maintained its capacity longer.
From the results obtained, it is seen that an increase in input or preВӯstress, stiffness, or
capacity, result in a larger shear capacity. Further, the results obtained for the calculation
does not comply fully with the pushВӯoff tests.

Methodology
The process of writing this master thesis started with reading up on relevant material and
obtaining more knowledge about the topic to be further investigated. Relevant material
in this case was composite materials, notched connections, plasticity and upper bound
method. Most of the relevant materials was research articles and literature, provided by
the supervisors. In addition to the other literature, a former thesis dealing with a relevant
experiment for this thesis was also provided. The results obtained from the report are to
be compared to the calculations performed later in this report.
While reading through the literature and getting a better understanding of the topic, good
and relevant information was underlined for later writing. Various questions that arouse
during the research, was written down and examined further afterwards. All questions
and uncertainties were sorted out before starting the calculations. After the gathering of
information was complete, the information was processed and written as bullet points at
first. The bullet points was then sorted into topics and relevance. By processing and
cleaning up the information gathered, it was easier to understand how everything was
connected, which further made it easier to write about the topics more properly afterwards.
After most of the writing was completed, the calculations started. At first, a symbolic calВӯ
culation was performed, based on the formulas defined in [1]. The work equations and
the equations for the angles, was recreated and derived for both of the failure mechВӯ
anisms investigated. This was done to gain a better understanding of how everything
was connected and why the formulas are expressed the way they are. After successfully
recreating the formulas in [1], the formulas was adapted to fit the specimens investigated
in the thesis. The formulas in [1] are written with the assumption that there are no tensile
forces, ft = 0, while the formulas defined and used in this thesis was written to apply for
cases both with and without tensile forces, ftвү 0 and ft = 0. Further, the final expression
for V was rewritten to display how much the concrete compression, concrete tension and
fastener each contribute with individually, before displaying the force they contribute with
combined.
Secondly, the calculations was performed numerically. The formulas defined in the symВӯ
bolical calculations was inputted with actual numbers, whereas the input numbers was
collected from the experimental report. The numerical calculations was also used as a
control, to check if the values obtained seemed reasonable and correct. In addition to
obtaining values for the angles and for the shear capacity, V , several plots was made to
see how changes in the different input values further changed the output for the angles
and V .
After the numerical calculations was completed, the same calculations was performed
again, but with an optimizer instead. Two different types of Scipy optimizer was used in

Literature study
3.1
Composite materials
A composite material is formed by the combination of two or more materials, all with differВӯ
ent properties. The different materials gives the composite unique properties. Although
the composite act as one material, the different materials are not dissolved or blended
into each other, but with distinct boundaries between the different components [3].
3.1.1
TimberВӯConcrete Composite decks
The composite structure further investigated in this report is the TCCВӯdeck. The idea beВӯ
hind composite structures is that they should utilize the strength of the different materials,
and compensate for the weaknesses of the other materials [4]. The TCCВӯdecks has an
upper flange of concrete, and lower part consisting of timber, with connections between
the two materials. This design exploits both of the materials, since the compression is
resisted by the concrete, and the bending and tensile forces are resisted by the timber
[5].
3.1.2
Advantages of TCCВӯdecks compared to pure timberВӯ or
concreteВӯdecks
There are many perks of using TCCВӯdecks over traditional timberВӯ or concrete decks.
When comparing TCCВӯdecks to a pureВӯtimber deck, one of the main advantages are an
increase in the thermal mass, which is important when looking at the energy consumption.
Other advantages includes an improvement with the challenges related to vibration [4] and
the acoustic performance. In addition to this, there is also an overall improved structural
performance of the deck [5].
When comparing a TCCВӯdeck to a pureВӯconcrete deck, there are several advantages.
This includes the reduced load that is imposed on the foundation, a reduces mass Вӯ which
further reduces the seismic action, and a reduced CO2Вӯemission due to the timber being
carbonВӯneutral [5]. Further, it also minimizes the required steel reinforcement needed,
due to the deck being partly substituted with timber [4].
In addition to the already mentioned advantages, the stiffness, load carrying capacity and
diaphragm bracing also are enhanced in the TCCВӯdecks compared to the pureВӯmaterial
decks [4].
3.1.3
Challenges with composite decks
Challenges and disadvantages related to the usage of composite decks over pure timber
decks is increased CO2Вӯemission, due to the substitution of timber with concrete. Further,
the occurrence of moisture problems at the timber, when the concrete is cast and curing.
The casting also influences the pace of the construction, since curing of the concrete
takes time. All these problems are easily solvable. The CO2Вӯemissions can be lowered

by using low strength concrete, and the moisture and pace of construction can be handled
by using preВӯfabricated concrete instead of cast concrete [6].
3.1.4
Notched connections
When looking at composite decks, the efficiency of the connection between the materials
determines the structural performance. The range goes from none to infinite stiff conВӯ
nections. No connection means that the materials will act independently, resulting in no

3.2
Plasticity
The theory of plasticity is a theory that studies the strength of different materials, and can
be applied when looking at ductile materials or constructions. It looks at materials that
are able to deform plastically when exposed to a constant load, once the load applied
has reached a value that is sufficiently high enough. The materials that often fit under
this description, is called perfectly plastic materials. By performing what is called a limit
analysis, it is possible to determine the loadВӯcarrying capacity of these materials [7].
3.2.1
Von MisesвҖҷs flow rule & rigidВӯplastic material
The definition of a rigidВӯplastic material is вҖқa material in which no deformation occurs (at
all) for the stresses up to a certain limit, the yield point. For stresses at the yield point,
arbitrarily large deformations are possible without any change in the stressesвҖқ [7].
A rigidВӯplastic material does not exist in real life, but it is possible to use it if the plastic
strains is much bigger than the elastic strains in the material. It is assumed that the
strains occurring in a rigidВӯplastic material are of plastic deformation, meaning that the
deformation is permanent and not reversible. If it were assumed elastic, the deformation
would be reversible [7].
When looking at rigidВӯplasticity, it is necessary to have a yield condition and a flow rule.
The condition for yielding can be defined by the function:
f(Пғx, Пғy, ....., П„xy) = 0

the stresses [8]. It is further assumed that the yielding surface looked at is convex, and
that it is observed as a homogeneous strain field [7].
This further results in an equation called the von MisesвҖҷs flow rule, which also goes by
the name the normality condition. This is because the flow rule defines the plastic strains
to be an outwardВӯdirected normal to the yielding surface. Meaning that the plastic strain
increments always are perpendicular to the yielding line or surface. This is due to О» Оҙf

The formula is expressed as:

3.2.2
Extremum principles for rigidВӯplastic materials
Extremum principles for rigidВӯplastic materials are based on the normality condition and
the curvature, or convex, of the yielding surface [8]. If the stresses appearing in the
structure or material is below the yielding point, there will occur no deformation at all.

This further means that it is not possible to determine the stress field in these structures
or materials until the stresses are at the yielding point.
When the load first reaches to the point where it only can be carried by the stresses at

3.2.3

An elastoВӯplastic material, can be deformed elastically and plastically when exposed to
loads. If the material has an elastic deformation, it means that the material returns to its
original shape when the loads decrease again. If the force were to increase, the material
would deform plastically, until it reaches its breaking point. A plastic deformation means
a permanent deformation, which is not reversible [9].

Modified Coulomb material
The modified CoulombвҖҷs failure hypothesis explains that the conditions for the failure ocВӯ
curring in concrete can be described by sliding failure in combination with separation failВӯ

comply with these two failure conditions [7].
The sliding failure is also expressed as the вҖқfrictional hypothesisвҖқ, which is based on obВӯ
servations showing that the failure in concrete often occurs along sliding planes or yield
planes. The sliding resistance is determined by two parameters, called the cohesion, c,
and a form of internal friction. The internal friction is given as a certain fraction of the norВӯ
mal stresses in the section looked at. The fraction is given as Вө and the normal stresses
as Пғ. If Пғ is a compressive stress, it contributes positive to the sliding resistance. If it has
a negative value, the contribution to the sliding resistance will be negative. The sliding
failureвҖҷs frictional hypothesis is fulfilled when the shear stress in the section is bigger than
the sliding resistance [7].

3.2.6
The Upper Bound Theorem
The upper bound theorem is defined as вҖқThe load determined from using the work equaВӯ
tion on a geometrically possible collapse mechanism will be greater or equal to the actual
collapse load of the structureвҖқ [11]. The geometrically possible mechanism is more specifВӯ
ically explained as a deformation that satisfies the geometrical boundary conditions and
plastic strains according to the normality condition [11].
The work equation is solved when external work is set equal to internal work. External
work is expressed as the external loads applied on the specimen, and internal work is
expressed by the dissipated energy [11].
Internal work, as stated above, is expressed by dissipation of energy. Further, the dissiВӯ
pation is dependent on the crack width of the concrete. The failure occurring in different
concrete structures often occurs along a line or surface. The surfaces are further interВӯ
preted as yield lines, which is where the plastic energy is being dissipated. The lines are
often lines of discontinuity. In order to calculate the dissipated energy it is necessary to
have the strains in the yield line, the failure condition and to impose the normality conВӯ
dition [12]. The dissipation per unit length can then be calculated using the formulas for
dissipation given in [7]. The formulas for dissipation are dependent on the assumptions
regarding if the concrete is treated as a Coulomb material or a Modified Coulomb material,
in addition to if its looked at as a plane strain or plane stress condition.

6
Conclusion
After investigating how changes in input, preВӯstressing, stiffness and capacity can influВӯ
ence the shear capacity for an elastoВӯplastic material, the results have shown that an
increase in the input will cause a larger shear capacity. Further, results have shown that
an increase in preВӯstressing will increase the maximum capacity of the mechanisms. In
addition, it will maintain a larger shear force capacity up until yielding, compared to lower
preВӯstressing degrees. After the occurrence of yielding for the various preВӯstressing deВӯ
grees, the shear capacity will remain the same. Changing the stiffness does not impact
the maximum shear capacity, but it affects how the shear capacity decreases. A larger
stiffness will result in a slower decrease in the capacity, until yielding occurs. Choosing
a higher capacity will result in a larger maximum capacity and ensure a higher capacity
throughout the slip for higher degrees of preВӯstressing. For lower preВӯstressing degrees
the impact of the increased capacity will occur later in the slip, and will not affect the shear
capacity in the beginning.
When the calculated results was compared to the measured results from the experiment,
it was seen that neither the start value nor the capacity throughout the slip corresponded
completely. The results however showed that the calculated maximum shear capacity was
greater than or equal to the actual collapse load, which corresponds with the definition of
the upper bound theorem. The measured shear capacity for the rest of the incremental
analysis is larger than the calculated capacity, resulting the upper bound theorem only to
be valid for the first increment.
