Abstract
This study uses the Neutron star Interior Composition Explorer (NICER) to examine the
source 4U 1820-30. All data collected with the instrument from its launch up to (and
including) 2022 are utilized. Using visual inspection, Lomb-Scargle Periodograms, and
Fourier transformation of light curves, temporal variations in the flux from the source
have been studied. The main focus has been on the detection of mHz quasi-periodic os-
cillations connected with marginally stable fusion. But a search for kHz quasi-periodic
oscillations and burst oscillations have also been carried out.
An mHz oscillation has been detected with 6.5σ significance in one observation where
the source is in its upper island state. The frequency of the feature here is estimated
to 3.82 ± 0.14 mHz. In this state, features around ∼4 mHz can be seen weakly (<3σ)
in several observations. But the features are too weak and significant evidence of them
leading to a burst has not been seen. With the available data, it can therefore not be
concluded that they are connected to marginally stable nuclear burning.
The kHz oscillations have been detected in other energy bands (Smale et al., 1997) but
can not be seen with NICER.
A significant detection (>3σ) of an oscillation during the rising phase of a burst at 718.5
Hz has been observed. But as the feature does not last very long and is only observed in
one burst, there is not enough evidence to claim that this feature is a burst oscillation.
Sustainability
This project has not had a direct focus on sustainability. The focus has been to widen
our understanding of the universe by analysing flux from a source and interpreting what
physical processes are able to produce that signal. However, on a longer timescale, the
work of understanding X-ray bursters can result in technologies that can contribute to a
more sustainable society.

Introduction
Low-mass X-ray binaries (LMXB) are systems containing a compact object (either a black
hole or a neutron star) and a companion with a mass comparable to or less than one solar
mass. Mass from the companion is transferred to the compact object via the inner La-
grangian point by filling its Roche lobe. As a result of the transferred material’s angular
momentum, the matter does not fall directly towards the compact object but forms an
accretion disc.
A subcategory of neutron star LMXB is bursters. These are sources wherefrom unstable
thermonuclear burning happens, creating sudden huge increases in the luminosity of the
source. As matter is accreted to the neutron star, a layer of helium can build up either by
accreted helium or as a result of stable nuclear fusion of hydrogen. When the density and
temperature of the helium reaches a critical point, an unstable nuclear fusion will begin
where the helium is rapidly consumed, thus creating an X-ray burst (Seward and Charles,
2010).
Besides the accretion disk, there is a corona in the system. An optically thin but geomet-
rically thick structure near the compact object that emits hard X-rays. The formation
and exact geometry of the corona is not fully understood yet, but figure 1.1 shows three
different models from a review paper by Degenaar et al. (2018).
LMXB are far from understood yet. It is therefore important to keep studying them to get
a better understanding of the physics that are dominant in their environment. As sources
behave differently, it is important to study the different sources to try to obtain the full
picture. Here the source 4U 1820-30 is really interesting as the companion star to the neu-
tron star is a white dwarf. Having a companion star where the composition of the material
transferred to the neutron star is very different, gives the opportunity to test one’s theo-
ries about the fusion processes happening at the neutron star and how it affects the system.
The study of LMXB is important as it is an extreme environment with powerful magnetic
fields, strong gravitational fields and extreme temperatures. How they all work together
to create the accretion disks, the coronas, and the fusion processes is a challenge to under-
stand, but working on these problems enhances our understanding of the physics of the
universe.
Furthermore, some LMXB give us the opportunity to study the most compact object in
the universe with a solid surface: The neutron star. The physics behind these objects are
far from understood, and LMXB are a probe that can be used to obtain more information
about them.
In this project, all data taken by the Neutron Star Interior Composition Explorer (NICER)
up to (and including) 2022 of the source 4U 1820-30 is used. A period stretching more
than 5 years.
Using this data, the source has been investigated for mHz quasi-period
oscillations (QPOs), burst oscillations and kHz QPOs.
The main focus has been on the search for mHz QPOs. As these oscillations have been
observed to be strongest in the low keV range, NICER is a good instrument choice as it
has a large effective area at those energies.

2.1
The Neutron star Interior Composition Explorer
NICER was launched in June 2017 and is onboard the International Space Station (ISS).
The ISS has an orbital period of ∼90 minutes which can limit the length of the uninter-
rupted observations of a source. Both because of the relative shifting position of the Earth
and as the station itself (especially its solar panels) can block the path to the source.
NICER contains 56 X-ray concentrators where 52 of them have been operational since
the launch. The concentrators have a field of view on ∼30 arcmin2 wherefrom photons
are concentrated on silicon drift detectors. The data is saved in an event list, meaning
the time, placement, and energy are saved for each event detected by the detectors. The
event lists can be used to create light curves using Xselect (Blackburn, 1995). The de-
tectors record the spectral resolution within a few percent and the detection times to 100
nanoseconds RMS precision. The energy range of the instrument is from 0.2 to 12 keV,
with an effective area of ∼1900 cm2 at 1.5 keV. The effective area of the instrument at
different energies can be seen in figure 2.1 (HEASARC, 2023).
Figure 2.1: The effective area of NICER compared to XMM-Newton PN from HEASARC
(2023).
2.2
Calibration and Filtering of NICER Data
The data from NICER can be obtained from the High Energy Astrophysics Science Archive
Research Center (HEASARC). The data is batched together in observations, each one with
a unique observation ID (OBSID). Each OBSID covers the observations for a day. In an
observation, the instrument can have an uninterrupted observation of the source over a
period, but it is much more common that the observation consists of several segments of
data. The data can be split up because there are periods where the instrument can not
observe the source (as explained in section 2.1) or because the instrument is simply set to
observe other sources. For each of these segments, the term pointing is used.

After retrieving the data from HEASARC, it can be transformed to level 2 data by using
the nicerl2 command (Blackburn, 1995). It calibrates, screens and filters the data and
gives an updated filter file with a cleaned event list wherefrom light curves and spectrum
files can be created.
In figure 4.21, a filtered light curve from NICER can be seen. Here it can be seen that
there is a pointing every ∼90 minute, and some of these pointings are chopped up due to
the filtering. The three peaks are X-ray bursts.
Just as data where the ISS passes the South Atlantic Anomaly are excluded, is it also
possible to exclude other observations where the background becomes too disruptive to
analyse the flux from a source. In nicerl2, the parameter cor_range can be used to
specify a range of magnetic cutoff rigidity to accept in GeV/c.
Optimally, the event files would only include data when the instrument has a clear line
of sight to the source. However, sometimes this is not the case. In some cases, it can be
seen when inspecting light curves that pointings start with a linear increase of flux, which
is probably because a solar panel has not moved out of the way of all the concentrators’
view of the source.
When such phenomena are seen, they should be removed from the data and the anal-
ysis. A way of detecting these phenomena is by creating a hardness-intensity diagram
(see section 2.5). As long as your data points are not average over a too long period, the
ones where there is an obstruction can be singled out from the overall shape of the diagram.
When observing a source, it should be done in reference to an inertial frame. If this is
not done, the arrival times of the photons from the source will be different because of the
changes in the frame. As ISS is in orbit around the Earth and the Earth orbits around
the Sun, NICER is not in an inertial frame. Therefore, when performing timing studies,
it is necessary to correct for this. This can be done by transforming the arrival times of
the events to the solar system’s barycenter.
As stated in section 2.1, the energy range of the instrument is 0.2−12 keV. However, when
observations are performed during the orbit day, low energy noise may increase and one
should be careful when interpreting events from energies below 0.5 keV (HEASARC, 2023).

He showed that the periodogram reduces to the classical form when the data is equally

When creating an LSP, one needs to define a frequency grid. When using Fourier Trans-
form, the frequency grid is given by the data used, but for LSP, one can search all fre-
quencies. However, it does not make sense to search for signals at frequencies that the
data poorly resolves. A common lower limit for the frequency range is 1/T as oscillation
at that frequency completes exactly one cycle in the data. The upper limit is a bit more
interesting. For Fourier transformation there is the Nyquist frequency, but when the data
is unevenly spaced, this frequency might or might not exist. Eyer and Bartholdi (1999)
proved that: Let p be the largest value such that ∀ti, ti = t1 + nip , where ni ∈N. p
is a kind of greatest common divisor (gcd) for all (ti−t1). The Nyquist frequency then is
1/(2p). If there is a pair of observation gaps whose ratio is irrational, the Nyquist limit
does not exist. Other approaches exist to choose the maximum frequency. One approach
is to set the limit based on a priori knowledge of the maximum frequency one expects a
signal from.
Having determined a range for the search for signals, one also needs to determine how
finely to sample the frequency range. One needs to have a good enough frequency resolu-
tion such that features can be seen. VanderPlas (2018) has investigated common frequency
resolutions and has seen that a resolution on 1/(n0T), where n0 is a factor around 5 to
10, is typical (VanderPlas, 2018).

2.6
Millihertz Quasi-Periodic Oscillations
Revnivtsev et al. (2001) discovered a new class of low-frequency QPOs from X-ray bursters
which they proposed originated from a special mode of nuclear burning at the surface of
the neutron star rather than from the accretion flow. Revnivtsev et al. (2001) discovered
this feature for the three sources 4U 1608-52, 4U 1636-53, and Aql X-1. Later the feature
has been detected for IGR J17480–2446 (Linares et al., 2010), 4U 1323–619 (Strohmayer
and Smith, 2011), IGR J00291+5934 (Ferrigno et al., 2017), GS 1826–238 (Strohmayer
et al., 2018), EXO 0748–676 (Mancuso et al., 2019), Swift J1858.6–0814 (Buisson et al.,
2020), 1RXS J180408.9–342058 (Tse et al., 2021), and 4U 1730–22 (Mancuso et al., 2023).
All of the mHz QPOs from the sources beside IGR J17480–2446 and IGR J00291+5934
can be seen as features that lead up to a thermonuclear burst whereafter the feature van-
ishes. For IGR J17480–2446 and IGR J00291+5934 this is not the case. Here the QPOs
exist where there are no bursts, and in the case of IGR J17480–2446, Linares et al. (2012)
have shown that the bursts transform into the QPO.
For 4U 1636−53, EXO 0748−676, 4U 1608-52, and Aql X-1, a drift of the mHz QPOs’
frequencies has been observed when the sources are in the intermediate state between the
island and banana state. Leading up to the burst, the frequency of the QPOs drops by
several mHz (Altamirano et al., 2008; Mancuso et al., 2019, 2021).
The theory that mHz QPOs are connected with the neutron star’s surface was further
strengthened by observations of anti-correlation between kHz QPO frequencies and the
luminosity variations during mHz QPOs. The frequencies for kHz QPOs normally increase
with the luminosity of the source. This supports the theory that kHz QPOs are created
in the inner part of the accretion disk (see section 2.8). When the accretion rate increases
(increasing the luminosity of the source), the inner part of the disk stretches further in-
wards, resulting in shorter orbit periods and thus higher frequencies for the QPOs. But
Yu and van der Klis (2002) showed that when the mHz QPO increased the luminosity,
the kHz frequency dropped. This could be explained by the mHz QPOs happening at the
surface of the neutron star, and thus they would produce a radiational pressure that could
push the inner edge of the disk outwards.
Heger et al. (2007) suggest that the mHz QPOs result from marginally stable nuclear burn-
ing of helium on the neutron star surface. However, the model they produced needs an
accretion rate close to the Eddington rate, one order of magnitude bigger than the global
accretion rate estimated from observation. They suggest that this discrepancy could be
fixed by that the local accretion rate onto the neutron star is close to the Eddington rate
even though the global accretion rate is much lower. And later, Keek et al. (2009) created
a model where turbulent chemical mixing with a higher heat flux from the crust was able
to create mHz QPOs at the observed accretion rates.
The frequencies for mHz QPOs lie in the range ∼3 −14 mHz, they have been observed in
the luminosity range L2−20keV ≃(0.05 −3.5) · 1037 erg/s, and they are more clear at low
energies (< 5 keV) (Mancuso et al., 2023; Tse et al., 2021; Lyu et al., 2020).

2.7
Burst Oscillations
Burst oscillations are, as the name suggests, oscillations that can be seen occurring during
thermonuclear bursts from LMXB. These were first discovered by Strohmayer et al. (1996)
with RXTE from the source 4U 1728-34. As of September the 17th 2021, burst oscillations
have been detected from 19 sources (Watts and Kini, 2021).
Burst oscillations result from asymmetric brightness patterns on burning surfaces of neu-
tron stars during a burst. Exactly what produces these asymmetries is not yet certain,
but it depends on the location of the ignition and how the flame spreads. It is generally
assumed the burst is ignited at one point on the neutron star surface wherefrom it spreads
(Shara, 1982), and that the oscillations frequency represents the spin frequency of the
neutron star (Ootes et al., 2016).
Three main categories of models are proposed to explain the physics involved in producing
the oscillations. These are hotspot models, surface wave models, and cooling wake models.
The hotspot models assume that the burst ignites at one place on the surface of a neutron
star, wherefrom it spreads in all directions. The hotspot can either spread to cover the
whole surface or be constrained to a region by magnetic confinement or Coriolis force
confinement. The hotspot models have problems explaining how the oscillations still exist
in the tail of the bursts, how burst oscillations are not present in all bursts from a source,
and the largest observed frequency drifts.
The surface wave models build on the assumption that the local ignition of the burst
triggers large-scale waves in the outer layers of the neutron star. The waves cause height
differences and thus brightness patches. The waves can exist after ignition has spread to
the entire neutron star and can thus explain that the burst oscillations can continue af-
terwards. However, surface wave models have issues reaching the height of the amplitudes
of the oscillations, and the frequency drifts in the models are too large.
The cooling wake models build on sequential cooling and positional dependencies of the
cooling timescales on the neutron star. Note that these models by themselves can not
explain the oscillations in the rising phase of the bursts when the flame of the ignition
spreads over the surface of the neutron star (Ootes et al., 2016).
The ignition and spread of the flame producing the bursts must depend on the accretion
rate. This has been investigated by Muno et al. (2004), Ootes et al. (2016), and Galloway
et al. (2020).
Muno et al. (2004) demonstrated that the burst oscillations are not directly determined
by the properties of the X-ray bursts but that they are affected by the accretion rate.
The results from the three analyses show that burst oscillations are most common when
a source is in its banana state, especially the upper banana. Still, burst oscillations have
also been observed from sources in the island state. The analyses show that oscillations
found in the islands state have low fractional RMS amplitudes, whereas the ones found in
the upper banana state can have both high and low fractional RMS amplitudes.
As the burst oscillation frequency is connected with the spin of the neutron star, it does
make sense to estimate an upper limit for the spin frequency such that the region wherein
one searches for the burst oscillations is limited.
Burgio et al. (2003) have investigated the limit of how fast a neutron star is able to spin.
These use three different equations of state, and the one enabling the highest spin fre-
quency sets this limit to 2 kHz.

So far, the fastest oscillation there is evidence for is in a burst from XTE J1739-285.
Kaaret et al. (2007) reported this feature and measured its position in frequency space to
1122 ± 0.3 Hz. It should be noted that Bult et al. (2021) later found a more significant
signal during a burst from XTE J1739-285 at 386.5 Hz (they caution against treating this
signal as a burst candidate until it can be independently confirmed). However, it still
makes sense to search up to the lower kHz frequencies.
During the burst, the frequency of the burst oscillation typically does not stay constant.
The frequency increases and goes up towards an asymptotic frequency. However, there
are exceptions to the rule. When examining a sample of 68 bursts, Muno et al. (2002)
found three that had spin-down episodes during the bursts. It should be noted that the
spin-down seen does not appear in the rising phase of the burst.
Burst oscillations have been observed in bursts with and without photospheric radius ex-
pansion (PRE). When a burst with burst oscillations contains a PRE, the burst oscillations
can be seen before and/or after radius expansion, but rarely during (Muno et al., 2002).
A photospheric radius expansion is an extreme X-ray burst where the radiation pressure
lifts the top layers of the neutron star. I.e. a burst where it will have reached its Ed-
dington luminosity. As the layers are lifted, the radius of the neutron star increases and
thus the temperature drops. When the burst decays and the energy emission drops, the
layers fall down again and when the radius obtains its original value, it is called touchdown.
2.8
Kilohertz Quasi-Periodic Oscillations
Kilohertz QPOs were first discovered in 1996 by van der Klis et al. (1996) and Strohmayer
et al. (1996). This feature has not been seen in any black hole candidates and is considered
a suﬀicient characteristic that the source contains a neutron star instead of a black hole.
kHz QPOs can be seen as two peaks in the power spectrum that together rise and fall in
frequency commonly in the band 300-1200 Hz in correlation with the state of the source.
Towards the edges of their observed frequency range, the peaks can also occur alone.
The two peaks are named the upper kHz QPO (vu) and the lower kHz QPO (vl) (which
have been seen with weak sidebands for several sources). The typical distance between
them is 300 Hz which usually decreases by a few tens of Hz when the peaks move up to
the high end of their range.
The frequencies where kHz occur are at the same frequencies expected for orbits in the
inner accretion, and almost all models interpret the phenomenon as this.
By assuming this, the observed frequencies set limits on the mass and radius of the compact
object as the orbit must be outside the object and outside the innermost stable circular
orbit (ISCO, which in Schwarzschild geometry is 6GM/c2).
Miller et al. (1998) proposed the sonic point model to describe the QPOs from orbital
motion at the inner edge of the disk. They assume that the inner edge of the Keplerian
flow is at the radius where the radial inflow velocity becomes supersonic. This model
explains the drift of the QPOs that is observed due to the source’s state, as a result of ra-
diative stresses that change the location of this inner edge. At the edge, clumps of material
form that accretes onto the neutron star in a fixed spiralling trajectory. There are many
clumps, and each will have a footprint on the neutron star where the material will hit (see
figure 2.4). Each footprint will eclipse with the rotational period of the pattern, which is
approximately equal to the rotational period of the orbital frequency at the sonic point. A
distant observed will then, from the combination of all the footprints, see a strong QPO.

So far, this explains one kHz QPO. The second, the lower kHz QPO, did Miller et al.
(1998) explain by proposing that it is a beat frequency between the upper kHz QPO and
the spin of the neutron star: vbeat = vu −vspin. This model then predicts that the distance
between the two QPOs is the spin frequency of the neutron star. But as the neutron star’s
spin can be considered a constant and the distance is not constant, Lamb and Miller (2001)
included in their model that the clumps at the inner edge of the disk could gradually spiral
down in a manner not the same as the matter that much faster spirals towards the centre
as described before. This flexibility of the clumps will make it possible for the observed
beat frequency to be higher than what it actually is, as the travel time of matter from
the clumps gradually decreases. Furthermore, because of the radial inwards drift of the
clumps, the footprints will move around the neutron star with a lower frequency, meaning
that the observed orbital frequency (vu) will be lower than the actual orbital frequency
as the angle between clump and footpoint gradually becomes smaller. This will further
diminish the distance between the two peaks. This decrease of distance would increase as
the luminosity of the source increases as the radiational drag would increase, causing the
spiralling down to become faster, just as observed.
However, the model still predicts that the distance between the peaks is approximately
the spin frequency, and observations show that for some sources, the distance is equal
to half the burst oscillations frequency, which is assumed to be the spin frequency. This
can be explained if the burst oscillations have arisen by two symmetric hotspots and are
thus not equal to the spin frequency. However, Wijnands et al. (2003) have reported that
for source SAX J1808.4-3658 the spin frequency is very likely 401 Hz, and the distance
between the kHz QPOs peaks is 196 ± 4 Hz.
Following this, Lamb and Miller (2003) abandoned this model and introduced a new
model where beat frequency arises by a resonant radius relatively far out in the disk,
where a resonant wave travels around the neutron star. The wave propagates around the
neutron star with a frequency of the neutron spin, but the matter orbits around half of
that. Depending on the accretion, one of these frequencies will dominate (and sometimes,
both will occur). Thus, the new model can explain that at some systems, the distance
between the peaks is the spin frequency, and at some, it is half of that (van der Klis, 2005).

Other mechanisms have also been suggested to explain the characteristics of the QPOs.
Troyer et al. (2018) carried out a systematic analysis of the lags in the kHz QPOs for 14

QPOs is the same for all sources but that mechanisms behind the upper and lower QPOs
are different. They find that the lower QPOs have a monotonic decrease in the soft lags

upper QPOs have near zero lags. The soft lag could result from the oscillations originating
in the corona, and the soft lag is a consequence of photons first interacting with the rel-

What causes kHz QPOs are far from understood, and more work is needed to better un-

3.2
Handling Millihertz Quasi-Periodic Oscillations
To begin with, the data was searched for mHz QPOs by making quick visual inspections
of the light curves for all the data. Besides having the potential to find clear QPOs in the
data, this search was also done to ensure that all bursts from that source that could be
detected were detected.
After this, a more throughout search was performed. This search for mHz QPOs was
also done without any bias from the theory towards some states of the source, where an
automated search through all the observations was made using LSP.
LSP was created for each pointing in each observation. Here it was very useful to use LSP
instead of Fourier transformation because the method still works if there are small data
gaps in the pointings. This automated search worked as follows:
By giving it a light curve from an observation, it divided the data into segments for each
pointing in the observation. The data given here was light curves with 1 s resolution and
from the energy band 0.2 −5 keV. As the search is for mHz oscillations, a resolution of 1
s is better than needed. And the energy range builds on the detected mHz QPOs being
clearer at low energies (see section 2.6). These are also the same parameters for the light
curves Lyu et al. (2020) used when they searched for mHz QPOs using NICER. Bursts
were removed from the light curves as they would disrupt the periodograms.
After dividing up the light curve, generalized Lomb-Scargle periodograms were produced.
The Gls function from the Python package PyAstronomy by Czesla et al. (2019) was used.
This function implements the generalized Lomb-Scargle periodogram described by Zech-
meister and Kürster (2009), which has the advantage over the classical LSP that it can
take the uncertainty of the measurements into account. The lower limit of the frequency
range used was one over the time length of the data. Thereby, one period of an oscillation
with that frequency would be present in the data. The upper limit is set to 1/2 Hz, so far
above the interesting range for mHz QPOs. The frequency resolution is set to oversample
with a factor of 10. The periodogram has been normalised using Cumming normalisation
(Cumming et al., 1999), which estimates the noise level a posteriori with the residuals of
the best fit and normalises using that. Zechmeister and Kürster (2009) write that this
normalization is suggested when the signal does not lie on top of Gaussian noise, but
intrinsic variability is present.
The generalized LSP does not have a fixed noise floor as the Fourier Transform, but a
noise floor still exists. Thus, an empirically measured noise floor is calculated by taking
the average value of all data points in frequency space above 0.1 Hz. If this value is equal
to the average of the data point above 0.2 Hz within a 5% interval, it is assumed that this
is the noise floor, and it is subtracted.
If the difference between the two values is greater, then the background is not subtracted.
This is not a problem, as the next step in the procedure is to fit a zero-centred Lorentzian
to the broadband feature and subtract it. From inspecting the data, it can be seen that
there always are broadband features in the spectra. However, it is a crude assumption
that it can always be fitted with just one Lorentzian. Therefore, when the fitting fails,
the data segments were flagged. In those instances, the script registers a warning in a log,
marking this data segment to be investigated manually later.
Lastly, it is checked if a power is great enough to be more than 3σ significant. If that is
the case, this is marked in the log to be examined manually.

This whole procedure is quite a crude approach to finding mHz QPO. Often there was still
a broadband feature below a possible QPO that gave it a too high power, or the signal
detected was the orbital period of the system, but this search enabled a quick reduction
of the large data set.
If at some point there was a weak feature, the pointing would be combined with the previ-
ous or next (or several neighbouring pointings) to see if the feature also existed there and
thus would create a stronger feature in the periodogram. However, as it can be seen in
figure 3.1, most of the pointings are under 1 ks long and often the recurrence of observing
the source is ∼5.4 ks (such that the source is observed one time per orbit of the ISS).
With these large data gaps compared to the exposure time, it becomes very diﬀicult to
see the true features hidden in the periodograms, which show the features from the source
and the window function.
Because of this strong influence of the window function, the search was continued using
Fourier transformations of the pointings instead. The LSP search had indicated some
data where QPOs could be present, which were reanalysed using Fourier transformation.
Furthermore, all data just before bursts (where most of the QPOs have been observed)
were reanalysed and closely inspected. Also, observation 4050300105 and 4050300106 (Au-
gust 2021), where Serino et al. (2021) reported a long burst from the source, was closely
examined to search for possible oscillations.
These searches were done by visually inspecting the power spectra from the Fourier trans-
formations. If it looked like there were a chance for a possible detection, the spectrum
was modelled with Lorentzians to examine it.
Just as in the LSP search, if a weak feature was observed, the neighbouring pointings were
also examined to see if the feature existed in several pointings. Using Fourier transforma-
tion, you can not combine the data the save way as for LSP. But the power spectrum can
be computed for each data segment, and their average can be calculated.

3.3
Handling Burst Oscillations
To search for burst oscillations, inspiration from Bult et al. (2021) who use data from
NICER to detect an oscillation during a burst from XTE J1739-285 has been taken.
The analysis of a burst begins 10 seconds before the rise, and the total length of the anal-
ysis is on 110 seconds which is examined with a sampling frequency of 8192 Hz and using
the energy band 0.5-10 keV.
The search for burst oscillations was made in the band 50-2000 Hz. The upper boundary
on 2000 Hz comes from the limit on the spin of the neutron star as described in section
2.7. The lower boundary on 50 Hz is set such that it lies above the high power variabil-
ities at the low frequencies. Thus, we can justify an assumption that the powers are χ2
distributed as expected from Poisson counting processes.
The 110 seconds of observation for each burst is divided into segments. The burst os-
cillations are expected to remain for a while during the burst. Therefore, three searches
in each burst are performed. One where segments lasting 2 seconds, one with segments
lasting 4 seconds, and one with 8 seconds are created. New segments were created every
second for the 1-second segments, every 2nd second for the 4-second segments, and every
4th second for the 8-second segments, making the segments overlap each other.
For each segment, a power spectrum was produced using Fourier transformation. The
spectra were Leahy normalized. Here the Powerspectrum function from the Python pack-
age Stingray (Bachetti et al., 2022) was used. It was chosen to switch over to use Python
for its flexible plotting abilities.
As described in section 2.7, the burst oscillations are expected to drift across frequencies.
Therefore, the procedure used by Bult et al. (2021) is followed where the powers are binned
together in sets of 4 across the frequency space.

Handling Kilohertz Quasi-Periodic Oscillations
Inspiration has been taken from Bult et al. (2018) in the search for kHz QPOs. They
use data from NICER to detect these features for the ultra-compact X-ray burster 4U
0614+09.
Here, data from observation 1050020101 to 1050020131 is used to detect the kHz QPOs.
Bult et al. (2018) cleans the data from high-background intervals by excluding data where
the count rate is above 1 per second in the energy band 12-15 keV (outside the nominal
passband of NICER). The same approach has not been used here. For 4U 1820-30, the
cor_range parameter is set to ”4−” when filtering the data using nicerl2 (Blackburn, 1995)
such that observations during high magnetic activity are cut-off. The cut-off region can be
made this large when analysing 4U 1820-30 as NICER has a long total observation time of
the source. However, this will cut off too much observation time when trying to produce
similar results for 4U 0614+09 to Bult et al. (2018). Therefore, HI and CC diagrams were
produced and inspected for outliers. In those cases, the data were removed. The outliers
can be spotted without any a priori knowledge of the specific shapes in the diagram for
the source, but with the HI diagram produced by Bult et al. (2018), it became easier.
All bursts have also been removed from the data.
As the source will move through different states over the observations, the possible kHz
QPOs will occupy different frequencies. Thus it is necessary to divide the data up in
segments depending on the state of the source. This has been done by producing HI and
CC diagrams and dividing the data according to their position.
When doing this for 4U 0614+09, only the HI diagram is used to divide the data such that
the segments contain the same data as in the paper by Bult et al. (2018). To replicate
Bult et al. (2018), the intensity is the flux from the energy band 0.5-6.8 keV, and the
hardness is the fraction of 1.1-2.0 keV over 0.5-1.1 keV.
For 4U 1820-30, the analysis of the source’s appearance using different energy bands by
Beck (2022) is used. From this, the following four bands is chosen: SC1 = 0.5 −1.25 keV,
SC2 = 1.25−1.75 keV, HC1 = 1.75−4.00 keV, and HC2 = 4.00−10.00 keV. The intensity
is then produced by the sum of them all, the hardness is the sum of HC1 and HC2 over
the sum of SC1 and SC2, and the hard and soft colours are respectively HC2/HC1 and
SC2/SC1.
The HI and CC diagrams are produced by dividing the data into segments of 128 seconds.
From the data corresponding to each point, light curves with a time resolution of 1/8192
s and energy band 2-10 keV are produced. These light curves are then used to obtain
power spectra using Fourier transformation. The limit on only using events above 2 keV
is chosen from the analysis produced by Bult et al. (2018) (see their figure 3). The power
spectra belonging to the same segments are then averaged to reduce error bars and make
the features clearer. When inspecting the frequencies above 2000 Hz, no high-frequency
signals can be seen. Here it can be concluded that the mean value of the power is 2 as
expected for Leahy normalized power spectra of Poissonian noise. Before fitting models to
the spectra, the noise floor of 2 is removed, the powers are RMS normalized, and powers
at frequencies below 0.1 Hz are ignored.

are the results from Bult et al. (2018).
There is also a small difference between the estimated significance of the kHz QPO made
here and by Bult et al. (2018). From their table, the fractional amplitude can be read.
The significance can be calculated by taking the value to the power of 2 and dividing it
by its error (which has been propagated according to the change of the value).
In segment 1, the significance of the detection of the kHz QPO made here is 2.4σ and
Bult et al. (2018) estimated it to 2.7σ. In segment 2, the significance of the detection of
the kHz QPO is 1.7σ and Bult et al. (2018) estimated it below 4.1σ. In segment 3, the
significance of the detection of the kHz QPO is 4.4σ and Bult et al. (2018) estimated it
below 4.7σ. In segment 4, the significance of the detection of the kHz QPO is 2.9σ where
Bult et al. (2018) estimated it to 3.2σ.
All the estimations are roughly the same except for segment 2 where there is a huge dif-
ference between our estimations.
The differences in the results can be explained by the fact that the data have not been
filtered the same way and that the data segmentation is not exactly the same. Thus, when
the models from Bult et al. (2018) are fitted to the data here, the fits are not good. But
the reason for this analysis was not to make good models of the power spectrum of 4U
0614+09. It was to show that the implementation works and that it can detect kHz QPO.

4.1
Search for Millihertz Quasi-Periodic Oscillations
When estimating the significance of a possible QPO, it is important to include the num-
ber of trials made to get a realistic value. As explained in section 3.2, different searches
have been performed. The searches go through some of the same data. For a very con-
servative estimation of the number of trials, each search of a data segment should count
as a trial. However, these searches are not independent of each other as there is a high
correlation between the searches. Going from the automated Lomb-Scargle periodogram
search to the manual using Fourier transformations, the same data is analysed in almost
the same way. The main difference is, that the results from the Fourier transformations
are closer examined. Therefore, the number of trials used to search for mHz QPOs has
been estimated to be the number of continuous data segments longer than 500 seconds
times 10. The 10 comes from an estimation of that there is room for 10 mHz QPOs in the
search range of ∼1-15 mHz (so far mHz QPOs have only been observed in the range 3-14
mHz, as can be seen in the theory section 2.6). To include time segments as low as 500
seconds could be argued to include too short intervals, as Fourier transformations from
these intervals would have a frequency resolution of 2 mHz and thus smear the QPO out.
However, because the number of trials also includes the analysis using LSP, where several
intervals can be joined together, it gives a fair estimate. Furthermore, it is better to have
a conservative number of trials to make the possible claims more trustworthy.
The number of segments with a length of 500 or above is approximately 600 (see figure
3.1). Thus the number of trials becomes:
Ntrials = 6 · 103

Broad Search for Millihertz Quasi-Periodic Oscillations
In the search for mHz QPOs in data that necessarily did not lead up to a burst, a signifi-
cant feature in the mHz range was discovered in some of the observations from June 2019
(OBSIDs starting with 205) where the source was in a bursting state. The observations
close to them, both in time and region in the CC diagram, were examined more closely to
characterize the feature.
This resulted in the observations of a feature in OBSID 2050300105, 2050300107, 2050300109,
2050300113, and 2050300114. The features are not present throughout the entire observa-
tions and can only be detected if segments of the data are used. Figure 4.7 presents power
spectra for all the data segments in the observations. The mHz feature in 2050300105 can
be seen in the first three spectra. In 2050300107, it can be seen in the second and third.
For 2050300109, it is in the two spectra at time 0.275 and 0.35. For 2050300113, it can
be observed in all of the observation segments long enough to resolve the low frequencies.
And for 2050300114, it can be seen in the last two segments that resolve the power spectra
below 1 mHz (around 0.8 days into the modified Julian date). Power spectra are also in-
cluded for observation 2050300115 as the feature in 2050300114 continues to the last part
of the day, and 2050300115 has data at the start of the next day. But the feature can not
be seen continuing into 2050300115.
It seems that the mHz feature observed in observation 2050300113 continues into 2050300114
where the second data segment have some high power at ∼3 mHz. This was examined by
combining the power spectra from observation 2050300113 and the two first data segments
in 2050300114. However, this resulted in the feature becoming less significant.
When inspecting figure 4.7, it seems that there are several data segments with oscillations
in the mHz domain.
However, the uncertainties on the powers at low frequencies are
very large. Thus it is necessary to combine at least 2 data segments to gain low enough
uncertainty such that the detection claim is trustworthy.

only one feature is visible at 3.3 mHz.
The feature at 1.5 mHz could arrive from the orbit of the system, which has a frequency
of 1.46 mHz. However, just as stated by Stella et al. (1987) is the seen amplitude from the
orbit fainter when the luminosity of the source is low. When dealing with power spectra
constructed in the timescales used to search for mHz QPO, the orbit feature is absent
when the source is in the island state. This is the only place a narrow feature at 1.5 mHz
is seen when the source is in the island state. Therefore, it is a crude assumption to claim
that the feature seen here originates from the orbit. If one assumes that the 1.5 mHz
feature is the orbit frequency, the frequency can be locked to 1.46 mHz when modelling
the spectra and thus obtain a better fit (if the assumption is true). But this is not assumed
here, and the frequency is let free to adjust to the data without this restriction.
The reason the two features are distinguishable when the first pointing is not used could
simply be because the first pointing is less than a thousand seconds long, which limits
the frequency resolution. As shown in figure 4.8, there is only one bin between the bins
covering 1.5 and 3.8 mHz in the power spectra for the first pointing. And that bin has
a large uncertainty. So instead of interpreting the results as a movement of a feature,
here it has been interpreted as just a result of poor frequency resolution and that the two
features are present throughout all three pointings.
Three Lorentzians are used to fit the data where one of them is zero-centred. The fit of
the model can be seen in figure 4.8 and table 4.2.
Using the model presented in table 4.2, both mHz features are estimated to have a signif-
icance of 7.7σ using the error command in Xspec (Arnaud, 1996). But this is without
considering the number of trials. When this is considered, using the number from equation
(4.1), the significances are reduced to 6.5σ.
If the 1σ errors for r2 instead are used to estimate the significance, the two features’
significances are estimated to 4.2σ and 7.6σ, leading to only the second feature having a
significance level above 3σ (6.4σ) when the number of trials is taken into account.

Common Characteristic for Millihertz Features
The observations 2050300105, 2050300107, 2050300109, 2050300113, and 2050300114 are
from 2019-06-08, 2019-06-10, 2019-06-13, 2019-06-19, and 2019-06-20, respectively. Through
the five observations, a feature appears in the frequency range between ∼3.3 and ∼4.5 as
it can be seen in table 4.7. The feature is not visible in the observations in between the
ones stated above, and furthermore, the feature is not apparent in the entire span of the
mentioned observations in all cases.
All of the observations lie in the same region in the HI and CC diagrams. They lie in
the low-hard state. This can be seen in figure 4.19 where only the data from these ob-
servations are shown. When comparing this to the complete HI and CC diagram for 4U
1820-30, shown in figure 2.3, it can be seen that these observations mostly lie when the
flux is at its lowest point observed from this source. However, not all of the data in these
regions can the feature be seen. And this is not just because of insuﬀicient data as the 2
longest periods of uninterrupted data in observation 2050300106 (which is the day after
observation 2050300105 and the day before observation 2050300107 where the feature has
been seen) are on 2.5 and 1.4 ks.
There have not been observed bursts in the observations where these mHz features have
been seen but they have been seen in the surrounding observation (see table 2.2), meaning
that the source is in a bursting state.
In table 4.7 the time distance between observed bursts and the pointing where the mHz
features can be seen. As NICER only observe 4U 1820-30 a small fraction of its time, it
is very likely that bursts have occurred outside of the observation time. Thus, the times
given in the table are upper limits for the times to bursts.

Millihertz Quasi-Periodic Oscillations in August 2021
The two observations from August 2021 (OBSID 4050300105 and 4050300106) were also
examined closely to see if any mHz QPOs could be seen during this special state of the
source (where the source should have been in its bursting state but wasn’t). However, no
feature is detectable in the data.
4.1.4
Burst Recurrence Time
When studying mHz QPOs connected with marginally stable nuclear burning, it is relevant
to know at what rate a source creates bursts. In table 2.2 all the detected burst for 4U
1820-30 by NICER can be seen. However, as a result of the limited observation time of
the source, there are undoubtedly several bursts which have occurred in the data gaps. It
is therefore not possible to use NICER data to estimate the recurrence time for bursts in
all the states of 4U 1820-30. But it is possible to do it for the source when it is in the state
observed in observation 1050300109. Here three bursts have been detected as can be seen
in figure 4.21. Under the assumption that the recurrence rate is relatively stable during
the observation, it can be seen that no burst occurs between the first and the second, as
there is a pointing at the time just between the bursts. Between the second and third
burst, a burst has most likely occurred during a data gap between them. From this, the
recurrence time can be estimated to 10.9 ks by finding the least squares solution to the
system:

Search for Burst Oscillations
A search was carried out for burst oscillation using the method described in section 3.3.
An overview of the results can be obtained by inspecting figure 4.23, where all the bursts
are shown with the significance of signals at frequencies from 50 Hz to 2000 Hz. It only
shows the region a few seconds around the ignition of the bursts, as no significant detection
was made elsewhere. It can be seen that it is only 8 out of the 15 burst which shows over
3σ significant signals.
As a burst creates a sudden change in the flux from the source, it is expected that an
increase in power, which is not connected with burst oscillations, will occur. Therefore,
only further examination of the bursts where a 3σ signal stretches over a longer period,
the 3σ signal can be seen in multiple segmentation (2, 4, and 8 seconds), or if a 3σ signal
is seen within 20 Hz of a 3σ signal seen in another burst will be conducted.
This reduces the remaining 8 burst down to three: 2050300110 with a signal around 720
Hz (centre plot in figure 4.23), 2050300119 with a signal at 62 Hz (4th row, second column
in figure 4.23), and 2050300124 with a signal at 63 Hz (5th row, second column in figure
4.23). The last two burst remains, as the detected frequencies are close to each other. The
two frequencies are detected using the 4-second long segments, and where the frequencies
are binned in pairs of 4, meaning that the frequency resolution is 1 Hz. So the signal could
be at the same frequency.
The strongest signal from 2050300110 occurs when 4-second segments are used and at a
power of 16.785 at 718.625 Hz. This means that the significance of the detection is 99.989%
(3.87σ). A fainter power can be seen above the 720 Hz feature in frequency space. At first
glance, one could think it is a harmonic, but it is located at 1180 Hz and therefore it is
not (the lower frequency feature is at 210 Hz).

5
Discussion
5.1
Interpretation of Millihertz Signals
As can be seen in the analysis of the mHz QPO from 4U 1636-53 in section 3.2, it was not
possible to obtain a significant detection of the QPO by fitting Lorentzians to the power
spectrum obtained through Fourier transformation. However, visually the feature could
be seen from the light curve, and the Lomb-Scargle periodogram shows a strong feature on
top of a low-powered broad feature. This advocates that the fitting of Lorentzians to the
power spectra should not solely be used as the tool for searching for mHz QPOs. Therefore
it is a good idea to make a combined search using Fourier transformation, Lomb-Scargle
periodograms, and visual inspections of light curves.
The results presented in this report for 4U 1820-30 are mainly those of the Fourier trans-
forms, as the 4U 1820-30 has very high-powered broad features at the low frequencies
making visual inspection hard (see figure 4.10 which is the one where the oscillation is
most clear). The results from the LSPs are excluded as it is necessary to include data
from several segments to gain strong signals and that means including large data gaps
with different lengths for the LSP. This makes it hard to interpret the signal behind the
convolution of the signal with the window function, especially in the presence of the high-
powered broad features at the low frequencies.
As can be seen in the power spectra presented in this thesis, 4U 1820-30 has significant
broadband features at the lower frequencies. As the power of the low frequencies in most
cases does not plateau out in the frequency range available using data from NICER, it is
very diﬀicult to fit zero-centred Lorentzians to them. Therefore, several of the model fits
presented are not very good. However, the focus has been on characterizing the possible
mHz QPOs and therefore the poorly determined zero-centred Lorentzians have been pre-
served not to overestimate the influence of the QPO in the power spectra.
When examining possible mHz QPOs leading up to a burst, further examinations were
carried out for bursts in observation 1050300109 and 2050300104.
In 1050300109, the
feature was very weak, and when earlier data was included with a small data gap of 3
ks, the feature vanished. In this observation, the recurrence rate of bursts is estimated
to be around 10.9 ks (see section 4.1.4). So the earlier data is most likely not far away
from a burst which will terminate mHz QPO, if present. That could explain why the
feature vanished when combining the data. However, it is much more likely that no QPO
is present here. If the source were in a state where mHz QPOs were present, it would be
expected that the weak feature would be visible in some of the other pointing, which there
are many of in this observation (see figure 4.21). And this is not the case.
The features seen around ∼3-4 mHz in the other observations can not be used to sup-
port the claim of presence for this feature. The reason for this is that the source is in a
very different state than when the other weak mHz features have been seen. Observation
1050300109 is in the intermediate state between the island and the banana state, where
the other features have been observed further up in the island state.
The feature in 2050300104 before the burst was a bit stronger, but unfortunately, no
closeby data were available to either strengthen it or disprove its existence. When includ-

ing the earlier data that occurred 25 ks before, the feature vanished, but this was expected
as 25 ks is far above the expected recurrence time of bursts.
2050300104 contains the burst where there is most data leading up to it before a data
gap. And for all bursts, besides the ones in observations 2050300119, 1050300108, and
1050300109, the data gap backwards in time is greater than 15 ks.
For 2050300119, there is a small data gap of 300 seconds. If the data before the burst and
the data before the gap is combined, around 1.2 ks of observation time is gained. However,
as a result of the gap, it is not possible to get very good frequency resolution. The next
data (backwards in time) is after a data gap on more than 30 ks.
Observation 1050300108 and 1050300109, where the data gaps before bursts are much
smaller, are when the source is in a very different state than where the other weak mHz
features have been seen. These two observations are at the two lower right triangles in
the CC diagram shown in figure 2.3 and thus in the intermediate state between the island
and banana state.
This means that with the current NICER data, there is not enough data leading up to a
burst when the source is high in the island state to be able to resolve the low frequencies
very well.
The feature in 2050300104 is around the same frequency where the features in the other
observations have been seen, and it is located close to them in the CC diagram. The data
before the burst has a soft colour of 0.81 and a hard colour of 1.27, so it is placed at the
lower right part of the points in figure 4.19.
If mHz QPOs exist for 4U 1820-30, it would be easiest to detect them and claim that
the oscillations observed are connected with marginally stable nuclear burning by having
longer data segments leading up to a burst. But just because no bursts have been detected
in an observation, it does not mean that the source has not produced any X-ray bursts
during that day.
Therefore, the weak mHz features studied in observation 2050300105, 2050300107, 2050300109,
2050300113, and 2050300114 are still interesting in the search for mHz QPOs. These obser-
vations lie around the observations where bursts have been detected by NICER (see table

QPOs have been observed from other sources.

5.2
Interpretation of Oscillations During Burst
As it can be seen in figure 4.24 and 4.25, the signal detected in observations 2050300119
and 2050300124 does not stretch over time, making it very unlikely that they are the
results of burst oscillations. However, the signal detected in observation 2050300110, does
stretch over some time, making it likely that it is not just noise (see figure 4.22).
The signal does not last very long, but that can be explained by the fact that it is a PRE
burst, and burst oscillations vanish during the radius expansion.
The feature’s shape in figure 4.22 could indicate a drift downwards in frequencies which
have not been seen before in the rising phase of a burst. The feature in the burst either
starts around 722 Hz and then has a very fast exponentially decreasing drop down to
718.25 Hz, or the higher signals 2 seconds before the ignition of the burst are indepen-
dent of the feature around 718 Hz, and the feature starts at 718.5 Hz and drops down to
718.25 Hz. The latter would mean that the drift is very small, if any. However, a possible
downward drift is atypical and would suggest that the feature is not a result of a burst
oscillation.

5.3
Interpretation of Kilohertz Signals
Interestingly the kHz QPOs is not visible in the data from NICER. The QPOs were ob-
served by Smale et al. (1997) and later studied by Altamirano et al. (2005) using the
RXTE, which has a spectral range from 2 to 250 keV (HEASARC, 2023). Unfortunately,
neither of their analyses contain an analysis of the energy dependence on the kHz QPO,
but from the results presented here, it can be concluded that the feature is more clear at
higher energies than what NICER is able to observe.
That the features are not visible in the energy range of NICER is quite interesting as Bult
et al. (2018) was able to detect kHz QPOs from 4U 0614+09 using NICER. 4U 0614+09
is also a system that consists of a white dwarf and a neutron star. The geometry of the
systems must differ to explain this discrepancy. A deeper analysis of this could improve
the understanding of these two systems.

6
Conclusion
This report presents the results of a temporal analysis made for 4U 1820-30. The data
used in the analysis are all the observations of the source by NICER since its launch up
to (and including) 2022.
Fourier transformation and Lomb-Scargle periodograms have been used to transform the
time series from NICER into the frequency domain to study the temporal variations from
the flux.
A study of mHz QPOs was made. It was the hope to observe such oscillations connected
with marginally stable nuclear burning. Only in observation 2050300105 a narrow feature
around ∼4 mHz could be detected with a significance above 3σ (6.5σ). The detection
of this and the observation of a narrow feature around the same frequency in multiple
observations, alas not with a significance above 3σ, indicate that there is an oscillation.
However, it can not be concluded that this is a mHz QPO connected with marginally
stable burning as the oscillation could arise by other means. With the current data and
without a detection of the oscillation where it ends after an X-ray burst, it would be in-
cautious to claim that the observed features are connected with marginally stable fusion.
That said, it is not unlikely, and observations of 4U 1820-30 with longer observation time
in the low keV range when it is high in its island state are encouraged. If 4U 1820-30 is
producing mHz QPOs connected with marginally stable nuclear burning, it would be the
first source to do so where the companion is a white dwarf. The possible discovery would
therefore be important as it could improve the models of these processes by including data
where the accreted matter is primarily helium.
In the search for burst oscillations, it was not possible to conclude that 4U 1820-30 ex-
hibits these oscillations. A significant feature at 718.5 Hz was observed during the burst
in observation 20503300110. This feature was detected with a significance of 3.87σ in a
power spectrum covering a 4-second long time segment, but it was also more than 3σ
significance in segments of 2 and 8 seconds lengths. The fractional RMS variation for the
oscillation was estimated to 3.203 ± 0.965 %. That the spin of the neutron star is around
720 Hz is not unrealistic, but more confirmation is needed to claim that this is a detection
of a burst oscillation from the source.
No kHz QPOs were visible in the data. This means that the oscillations are stronger at
higher energies as kHz QPOs have been observed from 4U 1820-30 using RXTE.

P,
dP
(A.1)
where f is the centre frequency for the bin, df is the span of the frequency bin, P is the
power, and dP is the error of the Power.
Xspec expect that the data comes in the form:
Emin,
Emax,
F,
dF
(A.2)
where Emin is the lower limit of the energy bin, Emax is the upper limit, F is the flux in
that bin, and dF is the error of that flux.
So to transform the data to the expected input for Xspec, the following operations are
necessary:
f −df,
f + df,
2Pdf,
2dPdf
(A.3)
The 2 in the last two operations is because df is the span of the frequency bin to one side.
