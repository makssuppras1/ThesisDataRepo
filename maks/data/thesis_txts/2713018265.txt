Abstract
With increasing awareness of the ecosystem and health in the past decades, people attach
more and more attention to environmental challenges. The microplastics issue is one of the
challenges that captures concerns recently that have been identiﬁed a threat to the terrestrial
environment and marine environment. Bio-reduction of metals is an important part of the
metal cycling in the nature. The effects of microplastics on the bio-reduction of metals by
microorganisms are therefore a hot spot to investigate.
In this study, the effects of microplastics on the bio-reduction of metals by microorganisms
were investigated by the PE microplastics. Four growth conditions were tested: 0, 1, 10 and
50 mg/L MPs. The microorganisms selected to perform the bio-reduction were Geobacter
sulfurreducens and Shewanella oneidensis MR-1, both belong to the model dissimilatory metal-
reducing microorganisms that involve the metals in extracellular electron transfer in their
metabolic activities. The metals selected as the electron acceptors in the bio-reduction were
Fe(III) from Fe-citrate and V(V) from NaVO3 and V2O5. The concentrations of Fe(II) and
V(V) were the indicators for judging the performance of bio-reduction. To further explain
the mechanism how microplastics effect the bio-reduction of metals by microorganisms,
the growth curves of Geobacter sulfurreducens and Shewanella oneidensis MR-1 with different
amounts of MPs were measured. As well, the effects of the incubation amount of culture
was investigated.
The bio-reduction of Fe(III) and V(V) by Geobacter sulfurreducens and Shewanella oneidensis
MR-1 performed better in the presence of MPs compared with in the absense of MPs. The
microplastics enhanced the performance of bio-reduction by promoting the growth of mi-
croorganisms. In addition, larger incubation amount of culture contributes to the promotion
of bio-reduction.

Introduction
Over the past 60 years, the global population has greatly increased from 3.03 billion in 1960
to 7.84 billion in 2021 (worldbank). It’s followed by massive resource consumption. Since the
1970s, humanity has been in an ecological overshoot that the annual anthropogenic resource
consumption has been higher than the planet can provide currently.
Figure 1: Global population from 1960 to 2020 (worldbank)
Since the introduction of plastics, its production and consumption have doubled numerous
times. Plastic is a synthetic or semi-synthetic material that contains polymers as the main
component. The ﬁrst manufactured plastics appeared in the middle of the 19th century,
and then in 1907, the ﬁrst fully synthetic plastics came out, which is a breakthrough in the
plastics industry (OECD, 2022). Due to lightness, affordability, plasticity and other good
characteristics, plastics have replaced numerous materials in product production since the
1950s, leading to incredible growth in plastic production. Plastics have been an indispens-
able part of modern society. The annual plastic production reached 381 million tons in 2015
while it was barely 2 million tons in 1950 (Geyer et al., 2017). Besides, the huge amount of
plastic production leads to a massive production of microplastics (MPs) which is the plastic
particles with a size smaller than 5 mm (OECD, 2022). Considering such a huge amount of
plastic production, plastics issues, including microplastics, have been a global sustainability
challenge, followed by a variety of environmental, ecology and health issues (OECD, 2022;
Haider et al., 2019; Dess`ı et al., 2021).

The metal cycle is an important part of the ecosystem. As a key element for life, iron is one
of the most abundant elements in the Universe. It can be found on the earth’s surface and
in the crust. Ferrous (Fe(II)) and ferric (Fe(III)) are the two statuses in that iron exists in the
environment (Gargaud et al., 2011). The iron cycle occurs in the cycle of ferrous (Fe(II)) and
ferric (Fe(III)). Fe(II) can be oxidized chemically and biologically, and Fe(III) can be reduced
geochemically or microbially. The microbial reduction of Fe(III) occurs in anaerobic respira-
tion, working as the electron acceptor via extracellular electron transfer (EET). Vanadium is
an essential trace metal in prokaryotic biochemistry that is relatively abundant on the Earth
(Schlesinger et al., 2017). The vanadium cycle in the environment is the biogeochemical cycle
of vanadium through the atmosphere, hydrosphere, biosphere and lithosphere.
Objective
The main goal of this project is to explore the effects of microplastics on the bio-reduction of
metals by microorganisms. It is achieved through the following objectives:
1. Evaluate the effects of MPs (PE) on the bio-reduction of Fe(III) and V(V) by Geobacter

Background
2.1
Plastics and Microplastics
Over the past few decades, with increasing awareness of the environment, health and sus-
tainability issues, a huge amount of attention has been attached to plastics and the inﬂuences
that plastics bring, from the public all over the world.
As a consequence of such a huge amount of plastic production introduced in section 1, mas-
sive plastic waste generates and most of the plastic waste that is known as a slowly degrad-
able material, will persist in the environment for centuries. It was reported that around
60% of the plastics ever produced are treated by landﬁll or discarded to the environment
directly without any proper waste treatment. Eventually, these plastics accumulate both in
the terrestrial environments and in the marine environments (Barnes et al., 2009). Going
through fragmentation and breakdown, those larger materials are leaded eventually into
microplastics that are deﬁned as plastic particles less than 5 mm. The associated dangers
from microplastics in the environment have long been reported (Fischer et al., 2015; Eerkes-
Medrano et al., 2015; Duis and Coors, 2016). Thereafter, it inevitably brings about social
concerns about microplastics in the ecosystem.
2.1.1

Based on the chemical composition of plastic, it can be classiﬁed into 2 categories. The ﬁrst
one is the plastics made up of carbon-chain polymers in which there are only aliphatic car-
bon atoms in the backbone chains (Ferdinand et al., 2016). The example structure, Polyethy-
lene(PE), is shown in Figure 3.

Another category is plastics made up of polymers with hetero-chain. In these hetero-chain
polymers, there are elements like oxygen(O), sulfur(S) and nitrogen(N) in their backbone
chains (Ferdinand et al., 2016). The structures of most of the hetero-chain polymers are

In terms of the classiﬁcation of plastics, there is hardly a sole basis. On the basis of the
permanence or impermanence of plastics’ form, they can be divided into thermoplastics

synthetic, or semi-synthetic polymers. Property, use, polymerisation processes and so on
can all be the basis of plastics classiﬁcation.
The composition and internal structure of plastics contribute to the signiﬁcant characteristic

on. These additives are used with the purpose of performance improvement on plastic prod-
ucts. For example, colorants are widely used in plastic products to show different colors for
diverse occasions.
2.1.2
Source and Classiﬁcation of microplastics
Microplastics commonly refer to plastic particles, solid synthetic polymers, with a size smaller
than 5 mm in diameter (OECD, 2022). It has captured a large amount of attention in plas-
tics pollution due to its threats to the environment, particularly to biota, and eventually to
humans.
Microplastics are existing in the environment in diverse forms, including fragments, spheres
and ﬁbres. The source of microplastics is hardly sole in the environment. Microplastics can
be devised into primary microplastics and secondary microplastics according to the reports

2.1.2.1
Primary microplastics
Primary microplastics include microplastics that come from two sources. Firstly, plastic
particles that are designed to have a size smaller than 5 mm belong to the category of pri-
mary microplastics. These microplastics are manufactured in millimetric or submillimetric
size and used in many products, for example, cosmetic scrubbing agents including facial
cleansers, toothpaste, biomedical uses and air blasting technology (Cole et al., 2011; OECD,
2022). In addition, the raw materials for the fabrication of plastic products are another source
of microplastics. Plastic pellets are accidentally lost during production or handling. When
these products are used and dumped, microplastics that are contained in them are disposed
meanwhile and then enter the environment. When these raw materials are used in the man-
ufacturing process, microplastics generate and then accumulate in the environment.
2.1.2.2

Secondary microplastics can be further devised into two categories. Firstly, secondary mi-
croplastics can be formed in the use of products. Microplastics formed from tyre abra-

particles(<5mm) coming from the deterioration and breakdown of larger materials. Under
the effects of physical, biological and chemical processes in the ecosystem, larger materials
gradually get easily broken down, eventually into microplastics and nanoplastics.

2.1.5
Effect of microplastics
Even though the beneﬁts of microplastics are undeniable, the impacts of microplastics on
the environment, ecosystem and human health cannot be ignored. When microplastics en-
ter marine environments, the consequence frequently reported is being ingested by a large
number of organisms. The documented organisms include ﬁshes, mussels, crabs, and plank-
ton like cnidarians, rotifers, ciliates, annelids and copepods (Browne et al., 2008; Avio et al.,
2015; Cole et al., 2013; Set¨al¨a et al., 2014; da Costa et al., 2017; Wu et al., 2019). Due to the
tiny size of microplastics, it’s quite easy to ingest them by organisms and thus accumulate.
Additionally, because of the properties of persistence and durability, plastics are essentially
the matter difﬁcult to be degraded, even in small sizes like microplastics. It has been long
reported that, even for the environment itself, it takes hundreds of years to fully degrade
plastics. Hence, it makes sense that microplastics gradually accumulate in the body of or-
ganisms. Furthermore, microplastics can be ingested in terrestrial environments.
Omidi et al. stated that microplastics are found in sheep and goats. Most of the organ-
isms mentioned above act as food in human daily life, which means microplastics can be
transported to human beings through eating and drinking. For all the organisms, direct
consequences of the ingestion of microplastics are proven to be negative impacts on the the
digestive system leading to insufﬁcient intake of nutrients, which eventually causes death
(Lambert et al., 2014; Geyer et al., 2017).
Microplastics can either act as secondary sources of adsorbed pollutants to the ambient envi-
ronment or pose joint biological toxicity with carried contaminants. For example, microplas-
titcs have been illustrated to have the potential to uptake and release persistent organic pol-
lutants (Bakir et al., 2014). In this case, creatures in the biological cycle, including humans,

2.2
EET
All lives including humans, animals, and bacteria survive through taking in energy from
chemical compounds. It has been demonstrated that microorganisms can produce energy in
a variety of ways. Microorganisms can obtain energy through respiration, photosynthesis,
as well as fermentation, to support their growth and maintenance. In depth the principal of
the three metabolic modes is basically the electron transfer reactions associated with oxida-
tion and reduction of chemical compounds (Kato, 2015). Among the three metabolic modes,
in terms of microorganisms, respiration is the most common way to acquire energy for con-
tinue normal metabolism activity. The way for most microorganisms conduct metabolic
activities is to involve soluble or gaseous chemical compounds in the redox reactions extra-
cellularly (Kato, 2015). However, microorganisms that metabolize and gain energy through
electrons transfer from and to extracellular compounds have also been found early. With
such an ability, these microorganisms present a better performance on living in some special
conditions like anaerobic conditions where the electrons are not rich.
Microbial respiration refers to the process in which microorganisms oxidize matter to release
electrons and couple the energy production of the respiratory chain (Yundang et al., 2016).
There is not only one sole mechanism in microbial respiration. As a type of microorgan-
ism respiration, EET clariﬁes the microbial bioelectrochemical processes in which electrons
are transferred between intracellular and extracellular regarding the metabolism of microor-
ganisms (Shi et al., 2016; Kato, 2015). EET has captured an astounding amount of attention
increasingly in recent years due to its practicability and applicability.
2.2.1
Mechanism of EET
Respiration utilizes the redox potential difference between the oxidation and reduction sub-
strates to transfer the electrons and thus gain the energy released from the process into a
bioavailable form. Utilizing oxygen (O2) as the electron acceptor in the oxidation and re-
duction reactions is the most common way for living organisms, which is so-called oxygen
respiration or aerobic respiration. Take humans as an example. Oxygen is utilized as the
electron acceptor, and the electron donor is usually organic compounds. The combination
of it in human metabolism processes is the respiration of humans, which is the process to
11
==PAGE:24==
Master thesis - s192128
Technical University of Denmark
gain energy for living. For the majority of living organisms, oxygen respiration works on
them as well. Figure 9 shows the conversion of energy in oxygen respiration where the
energy is eventually stored in Adenosine triphosphate(ATP).
Figure 9: Schematic diagram of oxygen respiration (Kato, 2015)
On the other hand, oxygen is not the only electron acceptor. There are other types of respira-
tion in the absence of oxygen, in which a wide range of substrates play the role of electron ac-
ceptors or donors. These types of respiration have been found in microorganisms. For some
microorganisms, nitrate, sulfate etc. are sources of electron acceptor and hydrogen(H2),
methane (CH4), sulﬁdes etc. are sources of electron donor. Thereby these oxidized inorganic
compounds and reduced inorganic compounds are the energy source. As well as, there are
some microorganisms that are able to conduct the metabolic process with metals extracellu-
larly, the principle of which is EET (Kato, 2015; Richter et al., 2012). The basic mechanism
of EET is illustrated in Figure 10. Cytoplasmic membrane (inner membrane) is the center
of electrons transfer and is the main barrier for cells to contact the external environment,
where the energy transferred from NADH to ATP occurs in oxygen respiration. Neverthe-
less there is not only the Cytoplasmic membrane in the cell envelope that there are always
some external structural compounds like the outer membrane shown in Figure 10. These
external structural compounds are electrically nonconductive (Shi et al., 2016; Albers and
Meyer, 2011). Redox carrier functions to overcome the nonconductivity of outer membrane
in EET. In contrast to oxygen respiration, in the microbial EET, microorganisms transfer elec-
trons between redox carrier (cytochrome c in Figure 10) in the cytoplasmic membrane (inner
membrane) and extracellular minerals electrodes.

2.2.1.1
Direct EET
When substrates contact microorganisms directly on the outer membrane, electrons are
transferred between them. Figure 11a presents visually and intuitively the mechanism of
this case that direct EET via outer membrane redox proteins. This mechanism has been
invested deeply in dissimilatory metal reducing bacteria (DMRB) (Shi et al., 2007). These
microorganisms utilize metal-containing redox protein, like c-type cytochromes in Geobacter
sulfurreducens and Shewanella oneidensis, to connect extracellular substrates to intracellular
respiratory chains.
By contrast, another sub-category of direct EET works when the substrates are not attached
to the outer membrane while contact each other via conductive matrix, for example, nanowires
of microorganisms, shown in Figure 11b. Microorganisms with these electric wires are able
to transfer electrons from or to the substrates distantly. Pili of Geobacter sulfurreducens and
outer membrane of Shewanella oneidensis both belong to conductive ﬁlamentous apparatus,
specially terms as ‘nanowires’ (Pirbadian et al., 2014), which help the electron transfer be-
tween the substrates and microorganisms.
2.2.1.2
Indirect EET
Particular microorganisms enable the electron transfer to or from extracellular substrates
through electron mediator compound (Kato, 2016), referring to indirect EET. In terms of
electron mediator, it functions as the electron carrier between the microorganism and ex-
tracellular substrates. The composition of electron mediators is diverse, such as quinone
derivatives and ferrocene derivatives (Nishio et al., 2013).
Figure 11c explains the mechanism, in which the electron mediator is reduced or oxidized
intracellularly and then the reduced or oxidized electron mediator diffuses out of microor-
ganisms to ﬁnd the extracellular electron donator and acceptor, after which the electron
mediator oxidized or reduced extracellularly goes back to the microorganisms. This elec-
tron transfer process is a cycle where the electron mediator goes out of and comes back to
microorganisms again and over again.

Recovery of useful metals
The reduction and oxidation reactions by EET can be applied in the recovery of useful met-
als. For example, EET plays a key role in bioleaching which is the technology to extract
metals from the ores by the metabolic processes of living organisms. Previous reports have
revealed that metals such as Pt(IV), Pd(II), Ag(I) and so on can be reduced to elemental
metals by the microorganisms with the ability of EET.
2.3
Dissimilatory metal reducing bacteria(DMRB)
DMRB plays an essential role in the natural cycling of organic matters and minerals. DMRB
is a group of microorganisms that can respirate in anaerobic conditions using metals as
the electron acceptors, which contributes to its wide distribution in various environments,

Technical University of Denmark
including aquatic sediments, submerged soils, and subsurface environments (Lovley, 2000;
Coppi et al., 2001). These microorganisms can transfer electrons to the surface of insoluble
metal compounds and extracellular terminal receptors in their natural habitat to support
their respiratory metabolism, in the process of EET (Santos et al., 2015). Dissimilatory metal
reduction activity only occurs in anaerobic conditions through dissimilatory metal reduction
microorganisms including obligate anaerobes that can be killed in the presence of O2 above
a certain concentration, as well as facultative anaerobes that perform aerobic respiration and
anaerobic respiration when there is O2 and there is no O2 respectively. The metal reduction
activity by dissimilatory metal-reducing microorganisms is essential in different aspects, for
example (Shi et al., 2007),
1. it involves in global carbon and nutrient cycling;
2. it contributes to the weathering and formation of minerals;
3. it can be applied in the remediation of metal contaminants
Geobacter and Shewanella are both the model dissimilatory metal-reducing microorganisms
that have been intensively detected on the focus of metal reduction via EET.
2.3.1
Geobacter
Geobacter is a genus of bacteria. The taxonomy and classiﬁcation of Geobacter are listed in
Appendix A. All Geobacter species are obligate anaerobes that can only grow in anaerobic
conditions. Geobacter species can use humic substances or metals as the electron acceptor
to acquire energy (Lovley, 2000) and they are the ﬁrst microorganisms found to have this
capability. These metals that function as electron acceptors include Fe(III), Mn(IV), V(V)
and so on. Based on the results of molecular analysis of Geobacter (16S ribosomal DNA),
it has been displayed that these Geobacter microorganisms are a prominent member of the
microbial community and the key drivers in the oxidation of organic compounds during
which a wide range of metals perform as the electron sink in diverse anaerobic environ-
ments (Tabares et al., 2020). Among the Fe(III)-reducing microorganisms in soils and sed-
iments, Geobacter is one of the most abundant and in these environments Fe(III) is a key
electron acceptor for the electrons from the organic matter oxidation (Lovley and Walker,
2019; Lovley, 2011).
There are quite many species in the genus of Geobacter. Among them, Geobacter metallire-
ducens is the ﬁrst species isolated while the ﬁrst to have a genetic system and a sequenced
genome is Geobacter sulfurreducens. Geobacter sulfurreducens was ﬁrst isolated from the sur-
face of a contaminated ditch in Norman, Oklahoma, USA. Geobacter sulfurreducens is a rod-
shaped microbe (See Figure 12) with a gram-negative cell wall. Geobacter sulfurreducens is re-
markable for the ability to facilitate metal reduction that relies on the metabolism of Geobac-
ter sulfurreducens, the core of which is the complete oxidization of organic compounds that

2.3.2
Shewanella
Shewanella is the sole genus of the family Shewanellaceae. Table 34 in Appendix B lists the
taxonomy and classiﬁcation of Shewanella. Shewanella species are facultative anaerobes that
can live in the absence and in the presence of oxygen. Shewanella can live in a diversity of en-
vironments, the majority of which are found widely distributed in aquatic habitats (Dikow,
2011). It has been identiﬁed that Shewanella has the ability to reduce a wide range of metals
including Fe(III), Mn(IV), V(V) and so on, making the genus critical for the biogeochemical
circulation of minerals (Wang and Quinn, 2010). In the anaerobic reseparation of Shewanella,
toxic substances and heavy metals can be the electron acceptors, which often become less
toxic after being reduced, thereby Shewanella can be applied in bioremediation.
The genus of Shewanella consists of a large number of species, among which Shewanella onei-
densis MR-1, is notable for its model anaerobic respiration of metals and other anaerobic
extracellular electron acceptors by EET. Shewanella oneidensis MR-1, a Gram-negative and
rod-shaped microbe (See Figure 13, was ﬁrst isolated in the sediment from Lake Oneida, NY
in 1988. As same as Geobacter sulfurreducens, Shewanella oneidensis MR-1 has a genetic sys-
tem and a sequenced genome itself. As a DMRB, Shewanella oneidensis MR-1 have the ability
to combine metal reduction with metabolism. In addition to metals, sulfates, nitrates and
chromates can be involved in its anaerobic resparation (Yi and Xie, 2021).

Mechanism of metal reduction by Geobacter and Shewanella
Geobacter sulfurreducens and Shewanella oneidensis are both the model microorganisms that
can perform EET to respirate anaerobically which have been intensively invested.
Both Geobacter sulfurreducens and Shewanella oneidensis reduce metals in anaerobic respiration
via direct EET. C-type cytochromes play a critical role in it. It has been identiﬁed that both
genomes of Geobacter sulfurreducens (111) (Methe et al., 2003) and Shewanella oneidensis (42)
(Heidelberg et al., 2002) contain numerous genes encoding for c-type cytochromes. Several
c-type cytochromes from the two microorganisms have been identiﬁed as the main con-
tributors to the process of EET and to distribute in inner-membrane, periplasmic and outer
membrane, as well as in the extracellular space (Santos et al., 2015; Ueki, 2021). Figure 14
illustrates the net work of c-type cytochromes in EET that electrons generated from the ox-
idation of organic molecules are transferred from the inner membrane to periplasmic and
then outer membrane, and eventually to the extracellular electron acceptors, on the other
hand that electrons from extracellular electron donors are transferred into the cell by these
c-type cytochromes by Geobacter sulfurreducens. As well, in the EET by Shewanella oneidensis
as shown in Figure 15, electrons are received from the menaquinone pool by the inner mem-
brane associated with cytochromes that is the redox hub where the electrons are distributed
to various cytochromes at periplasmic space, and then go through the cytochromes on the
outer membrane to the external environments.

specialized function of the protein nanowires is as electrical conduits.
Electrically conductive pili (e-pili) is the most intensively studied protein nanowires of Geobac-

envelope of Geobacter sulfurreducens (Strom and Lory, 1993) and can accept electrons from

As for Geobacter sulfurreducens, its type IV pilin, pilA, assembles into e-pili (Reguera et al.,

of Shewanella oneidensis MR-1 extend the periplasm and outer membrane (Pirbadian et al.,

Protein nanowires can protrude outside the cell envelope, thereby enhancing the cell’s reac-
tive surface (Cologgi et al., 2011). In other words, protein nanowires offer the possibilities for
microorganisms to contact extracellular electrons distantly, accelerating electron exchange.
Consequently, protein nanowires are critical for long-range direct EET in particular. Further-
more, Shewanella oneidensis can also conduct indirect EET via ﬂavin as the electron shuttle
(Edwards et al., 2020).

3.1.2
Cultivation
To ensure bacterial activity of Geobacter sulfurreducens and Shewanella oneidensis MR-1, they
are both subcultured every seven days. When culturing, 5% previous cultured medium are
added into the new clean medium - 3 ml of the previous culture medium with microorgan-
isms is added into 60 ml of a new medium. Geobacter sulfurreducens and Shewanella oneidensis
MR-1 are both subcultured twice every time – subculture into two reactors, to reduce the
likelihood of unsuccessful subculture. All these operations are done in strictly hygienic
conditions to prevent the risk of contamination: sterilized bench, unopened kit including
syringes and needles, operation close to ﬂame, gloves, disinfecting contact surfaces with
alcohol, etc. The subcultures of Geobacter sulfurreducens and Shewanella oneidensis MR-1 are
kept under shaking with a speed of 150 rpm at 37 °C and 27 °C, respectively.
3.2
Experiment design
The reactor in this project is where the microorganisms conducted metabolic activities and
where the microorganisms used the metals as electron acceptors leading to the bio-reduction
of metals. The main part of the reactor consists of the ﬂask and the medium inside. The ﬂask
is sealed and sparged with 100% N2 gas before the incubation of microorganisms to ensure
the anaerobic conditions. The reactor is placed on the shaker at the proper temperatures
for microorganisms, for example, in this project 37 °C for Geobacter sulfurreducens and 27
°C for Shewanella oneidensis. Bacterial liquid cultures should be always shaken. Shaker is
necessary in the experiments of this project in order to ensure the nutrient availability for
the microorganisms, as well as to avoid bacterial settlement in the reactor which may cause
cell death due to the lack of nutrient availability. It is important to be reminded that the
shaking is not supposed to be too vigorous because it will damage the cells and prevent

3.7
OD600 measurement
Optical Density (OD) measurement is a common method to detect the cell growth of mi-
croorganisms. The growth of microorganisms in liquid medium is typically detected by
measuring the optical density with the wavelength of 600 nm (OD600). In the lag phase the
value of OD600 will not change a lot, commonly followed by a sharply increase in exponen-
tial phase. It peaks at the top until stationary phase during which the OD600 of microorgan-
isms keep stable, and then starts decreasing which occurs in death phase. The method helps
harvest microorganisms at optical time. The data of microbial growth curves in this project
is carried out by spectrometer with a wavelength of 600 nm.
Procedure
1. Take 1 mL of sample from each reactor into cuvette;
2. Read in spectrophotometer with the wavelength of 600 nm;

4.1.10
Discussion
Form the above results, it can be seen that the bio-reduction performances of both Fe(III)
and V(V) by both Geobacter sulfurreducens and Shewanella oneidensis MR-1 are better in the
condition with the MPs of 50 mg/L.
This discovery can be explained that the presence of plastics can make an inﬂuence on the
growth of microorganisms.
It has been identiﬁed that microplastics can supply available carbon, which contributes to
the growth of microorganisms (Li et al., 2014, 2022). The carbon source can be the degraded
microplastics and the plastic additives released from the plastics during the degradation
process (Serrano-Ruiz et al., 2021; Flury and Narayan, 2021; Sintim et al., 2019). The micro-
bial biomass and enzyme activity are both affected by the content of carbon (Li et al., 2014).
The carbon from microplastics is likely to increase the richness and activity of microorgan-
isms.
Furthermore, the report by Chen et al. states that microplastics can facilitate the production
of N2O by increasing the N cycling functional gene abundances in microorganisms (Chen
et al., 2022a). Among the various microplastics, polyethylene (PE) accelerates the produc-
tion of N2O through nitriﬁer denitriﬁcation (ND) and heterotrophic denitriﬁcation (HD)
(Chen et al., 2022a). As an important intermediate product of microbial nitrogen cycling, the
promotion of N2O production by microplastics facilitates the growth of microorganisms.
In addition, the hydrophobicity and large speciﬁc surface area of microplastics may promote
the growth of microorganisms by suppling habitats for microorganisms (Li et al., 2022). Due
to these characteristics, the microplastics can be colonized by microorganisms effortlessly,
thereby forming bioﬁlms on the surface of microplastics. The substrates for the metabolisms
of microorganisms are enriched on the bioﬁlms, potentially resulting in the promotion of the
growth and reproduction of microorganisms.
As well, additives and other exogenous substances released from MPs during the biodegra-
dation process may effect the extracellular electron transfer in the metabolisms of Geobacter
sulfurreducens and Shewanella oneidensis MR-1. These substrates usually have more polar
groups, contributing to the increase of the activity of electron transfer belt (Chen et al.,
2022b). With the active electron transfer belt, the efﬁciency of bio-reduction of metals in-
creases in the metabolisms of microorganisms.
Besides, that microplastics can work as carriers of metals may be another reason. The ab-
sorbability of microplastics has been identiﬁed, including metals (Godoy et al., 2019). Mi-
croplastics, especially polyethylene (PE), have high afﬁnity to metals (Godoy et al., 2019), re-
sulting in absorbing the electron acceptors, Fe(III) and V(V) in this project. The enriched met-
als may involve in the extracellular electron transfer of microorganisms more, thus showing
a better bio-reduction performance of metals.

Hence, the promotion of microorganisms’ growth, increased activity of electron transfer belt
and enriched metals by microplastics lead to the better bio-reduction of metals, which ex-
plains the better bio-reduction performance in the condition of 50 mg/L MPs.

Conclusion
The effects of microplastics on the bio-reduction of Fe(III) and V(V) by Geobacter sulfurre-
ducens and Shewanella oneidensis MR-1 were studied and the PE microplastic was used as
the reference microplastic. The results showed that the presence of microplastics promoted
the bio-reduction performance of Fe(III) and V(V) by Geobacter sulfurreducens and Shewanella
oneidensis MR-1. Compared with the control group in the absence of MPs (0 mg/L MPs),
more Fe(III) and V(V) were reduced and were reduced faster in the experimental groups
containing MPs, particularly with the MPs of 50 mg/L.
In order to further explore the mechanism of the promotion of bio-reduction by microplas-
tics, the growth of Geobacter sulfurreducens and Shewanella oneidensis MR-1 was detected. It
is observed that both the two types of microorganisms grow better in the presence of MPs.
The growth curves indicate microplastics enhance the bio-reduction performance through
promoting the growth of microorganisms.
The effects of inoculum size on the bio-reduction was also investigated. The addition of
more microorganism culture contributes to the better bio-reduction performance of Fe(III)
and V(V) through decreasing the length of microbial lag phase. This result helps rule out that
the V(V) was not fully reduced in the experiment of the effects of MPs on the bio-reduction
of V(V) by Geobacter sulfurreducens and Shewanella oneidensis MR-1 due to the inoculum size
of microorganisms, not the microplastics.
The ROS level of Geobacter sulfurreducens and Shewanella oneidensis MR-1 with different con-
centrations of MPs was measured. The ROS result of microorganisms is signiﬁcantly lower
with the MPs of 50 mg/L compared with the control group (0 mg/L MPs). It further explores
the promotion of microplastics to the growth of Geobacter sulfurreducens and Shewanella onei-
densis MR-1.
The exploration of the effect of microplastics on bio-reduction of metals in this project is
limited due to many reasons. The future work can include the following:
The concentration of MPs set in this project is 0, 1, 10 and 50 mg/L, which doesn’t present
signiﬁcant differences of bio-reduction of metals. A wider range of microplastic concentra-
tions can be selected for further exploration.
The metal compounds, act as metal sources, are completely dissolved in the medium in
this project. However, Fe(III) and V(V) (hydr)oxides are are poorly soluble in water in the
nature. To further explore the bio-reduction of metals by microorganisms, metal contents
can be determined with reference to concentrations found in nature.
More morphological and genetic testing methods can be used on microorganisms to inves-
tigate the effects of microplastics on them, further exploring the effects of microplastics on
bio-reduction.
