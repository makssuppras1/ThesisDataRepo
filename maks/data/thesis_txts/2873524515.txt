Abstract
Cardiovascular disease is a major cause of death worldwide, and its most common manifestation is
coronary artery disease (CAD). The coronary arteries are the main blood vessels that supply the heart
with blood, allowing it to beat and pump blood. CAD develops as plaque accumulates in the coro-
nary arteries, reducing blood flow and causing stenosis and potentially a heart attack. Detecting and
analysing coronary artery stenosis from computed tomography (CT) scans is essential for improving
early diagnosis and treatment of CAD. This study presents a pipeline for analysing coronary artery
stenosis in the left coronary artery using deep learning and geometric modeling techniques in coronary
computed tomography angiography (CCTA) images.
The pipeline is developed and evaluated using anatomical annotations of the coronary arteries on the
publicly available ImageCAS (Image‑based Coronary Artery Segmentation) dataset, consisting of 1000
CCTA images. The images are transformed using curved planar reformatting (CPR) based on center-
lines to generate anatomical representations of the coronary arteries. Initially, two attempts at unsu-
pervised detection of calcium plaques are introduced. The first extracts a threshold for calcified plaque
based on placing a region of interest in the aorta between the right and left coronary arteries, while the
second is a convolutional autencoder trained to reconstruct normal artery cross-sections. The core of
the segmentation pipeline is built upon the nnU-Net (”no-new-Net”) framework, which self-configures
its architecture, preprocessing, and training based on the dataset, eliminating the need for extensive
manual design. From this, a 3D full-resolution network is detailed for accurate artery segmentation.
Continuous artery mesh surfaces are created by integrating the segmentation probability maps with a
graph cut segmentation algorithm, enabling slice-wise diameter estimation and morphological analysis.
Also, from the segmentation outputs, artery centerlines are re-centered using spline smoothing to op-
timize CPR alignment, and a centering score (Cscore) is proposed to quantify CPR quality and guide
spline optimization.
Analysis of the 3D full-resolution network revealed good performance, with an achieved dice score of
0.882 on a test set of 99 left coronary artery trees; however, inconsistencies were present towards the
end of the arteries. Artery meshes based on probability maps show great alignment with anatomical
expectations. Notably, high min/max diameter ratios and localized geometry deviations are found to
correlate with calcified regions, supporting the pipeline’s potential in stenosis localization. Finally, re-
centering the centerlines improved the Cscore by an average of 0.27 and was shown to improve artery
continuity but was also seen to introduce image artifacts in some cases.
This work highlights the potential of combining self-configuring segmentation with geometric analysis
for comprehensive and automated assessment of coronary artery stenosis in clinical imaging workflows.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Cardiovascular diseases remain one of the leading causes of mortality worldwide, with coronary artery
disease (CAD) at the forefront. CAD manifests as plaque forming inside the arteries, causing them to
narrow or become blocked, also known as stenosis. This results in a limited supply of oxygen-rich blood
to the heart, which can be fatal. Coronary artery stenosis does not have a simple cause but is linked to
risk factors such as high blood pressure, high cholesterol, smoking, diabetes, and obesity. The severity
of the stenosis depends on the amount of blockage; thus, early diagnosis is of high priority to ensure
timely treatment and improved patient outcomes [8].
Due to its low risk coronary computed tomography angiography (CCTA) has emerged as a powerful,
non-invasive imaging technique for evaluating coronary artery pathology. CCTA can provide detailed
anatomical information and is highly accurate in detecting coronary artery blockages [14]. However,
clinical analysis of CCTA scans is time-consuming, prone to inter-observer variability, and fundamen-
tally constrained by the complexity and variability of the coronary anatomy. For patients in high-risk
groups, it is vital to obtain a diagnosis quickly. However, manual segmentation requires expert radio-
logical interpretation of thousands of 2D slices—a process that is tedious, error-prone, and not scalable
for high-throughput diagnostics.
Recent advances in deep learning (DL) have shown great promise in automating medical image analy-
sis, particularly for tasks like segmentation and classification. Frameworks such as the nnU-Net have
enabled strong performance in diverse segmentation challenges by adapting to different datasets with
minimal manual tuning. Despite these advancements, the application of deep learning to CCTA data
presents several challenges.
Coronary arteries are small, highly curved structures that vary signifi-
cantly between patients. Advanced image processing techniques, when combined with deep learning
approaches, offer promising solutions to these challenges. The integration of traditional image process-
ing methods with state-of-the-art DL approaches represents a comprehensive strategy for addressing
the multifaceted nature of coronary artery stenosis analysis.
This thesis investigates a modular pipeline for automated coronary artery analysis in CCTA images,
with a focus on improving robustness in the presence of calcified plaque in the left coronary arteries.
The approach integrates deep learning-based lumen segmentation to accurately learn the shape of the
arteries, geometric centerline refinement, and diameter estimation using graph-based methods. The
pipeline is evaluated on a large, publicly available dataset and aims to improve the reliability of key
diagnostic measurements by addressing limitations in both data quality and geometric alignment.
The overarching aim is to experiment with and refine techniques that yield clinically actionable insights
into the localization and severity of coronary stenosis, with the goal being to aid in evaluating the risk
for the individual patient and improve future diagnostics. This work should be seen as fitting into a
larger ongoing framework in the analysis of the coronary arteries and its diseases. With the potential
to reduce manual workload and improve diagnostic precision, this research contributes to the growing
intersection of medical imaging and AI-powered healthcare.
1.1
Related Work
There are several traditional methods for segmenting coronary arteries. Region-growing techniques, as
described in [3] and [43], exploit voxel-wise intensity similarities and use prior anatomical or statistical

information to define criteria for how a region should expand. These methods are computationally
efficient and can be robust in clean datasets with high contrast and limited anatomical variability.
However, they are poorly suited for coronary artery segmentation in real-world CCTA data.
Other classical segmentation approaches include threshold-based methods [46] and active contour mod-
els [5]. Threshold-based methods rely solely on intensity values to differentiate structures of interest,
without incorporating any spatial or edge-based information. As a result, they perform poorly in regions
with low contrast, inhomogeneous intensities, or overlapping tissue classes, all of which are common
challenges in CCTA. Active contour models, while capable of incorporating edge information and shape
priors, are highly sensitive to initialization.
In the context of coronary artery segmentation, where
vessels are narrow, elongated, and embedded in complex anatomical surroundings, poor initialization
often leads to convergence on incorrect boundaries or failure to detect fine structures.
Recent advances in deep learning have revolutionized coronary artery segmentation, with U-Net-based
architectures becoming the dominant approach. An overview of promising state-of-the-art methods is
shown in Table 1.1. Huang et al. (2018) [19] proposed a fully 3D U-Net-based framework for lumen
segmentation from CCTA volumes. Their model processes entire 3D patches of the coronary tree to
generate voxel-wise segmentation masks, effectively capturing vessel morphology across multiple scales.
The authors further reported reliable vessel delineation even in challenging regions affected by motion
or poor contrast.
Pan et al. (2021) [36] examined coronary artery segmentation under class imbalance conditions using a
U-Net based architecture on CCTA images. The paper highlighted the inherent challenge of coronary
segmentation tasks, where vessel pixels represent a small proportion relative to background tissue. The
approach demonstrated that specialized loss functions and architectural modifications were necessary
to effectively handle this class imbalance and achieve robust segmentation of thin coronary vessels.
Building upon the foundation of convolutional architectures, researchers have developed increasingly
sophisticated multi-scale approaches to address the varying sizes of coronary vessels. Dong et al. (2023)
[13] details a novel multi-attention, multi-scale 3D deep network for coronary artery segmentation pro-
posed to capture vessel features across different spatial scales.
Dong et al.
incorporated attention
mechanisms that selectively emphasized vessel regions during training, allowing the network to focus on
relevant anatomical structures while suppressing background noise. This multi-scale approach proved
particularly effective for segmenting both large proximal vessels and smaller distal branches within a
unified framework.
The challenge of maintaining vessel connectivity and anatomical consistency has led to the develop-
ment of orientation-aware segmentation methods. Ori-Net, proposed by Jiang et al. (2024) [23], is
an orientation-guided neural network for automated coronary arteries segmentation.
The approach
integrated geometric constraints and orientation information into the learning process, ensuring that
segmented vessels maintained anatomically plausible connectivity patterns.
This orientation-guided
strategy demonstrated significant improvements in preserving vessel continuity across complex branch-
ing structures.
However, the development of task-specific architectures often requires extensive hyperparameter tuning
and domain expertise. The emergence of nnU-Net as described in Isensee et al. (2021) [20] revolution-
ized medical image segmentation by providing a self-configuring framework that automatically adapts
to different segmentation tasks without manual intervention. Isensee et al. demonstrated that nnU-
Net’s automatic preprocessing, network architecture selection, and training configuration could achieve
state-of-the-art performance across diverse medical segmentation challenges [22]. The framework auto-
matically determines optimal patch sizes, network depth, and augmentation strategies based on dataset
characteristics. This allows researchers to focus on data quality and clinical validation rather than
network design optimization, representing a significant advancement in making deep learning more ac-

1.2
Aim of Thesis
This thesis explores a series of experimental methods aimed at improving the automatic analysis of steno-
sis in the left coronary arteries. The work builds upon the ImageCAS dataset, a large-scale, clinically
annotated repository of 3D coronary computed tomography angiography scans. Through a combina-
tion of neural networks, geometric modeling, and image-derived priors, this work aims to strengthen the
robustness, interpretability, and reliable detection of stenotic regions. The ultimate goal is to enhance
risk assessment to support better patient diagnostics and take a step forward in advancing robust steno-
sis recognition. The proposed pipeline focuses on four key components: detection of calcified plaque,
segmentation of the coronary lumen, estimation of arterial diameter, and correction of artery centerlines.
An overview of the steps taken in this thesis is shown in Figure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: Illustration of proposed pipeline. From CT scans and artery segmentations, the amount and placement
of calcium is estimated. The segmentations are refined and passed through a 3D full resolution nnUnet, which
produces accurate segmentation of the images into lumen and background. The probability maps from nnUnet
are used to analyze and explore methods for stenosis detection involving estimating the diameter of the coronary
arteries and refining the centerlines.

CHAPTER 2
Clinical background
2.1
The Heart
The heart is a fist-sized, muscular organ located in the chest cavity, nestled between the lungs and po-
sitioned slightly to the left behind the breastbone. As the central component of the circulatory system,
the heart plays a vital role in sustaining life by continuously pumping oxygenated and deoxygenated
blood throughout the body. This process ensures that essential nutrients and oxygen reach the body’s
cells while simultaneously removing metabolic waste products such as carbon dioxide [2].
Figure 2.1: Depiction of the anatomy of the heart. The various chambers and valves of the heart are illustrated,
with arrows showing the direction of blood flow [37].
The heart itself, illustrated in Figure 2.1, is divided into four chambers: the right atrium (RA), right
ventricle (RV), left atrium (LA), and left ventricle (LV). The atria serve as receiving chambers that
collect blood returning to the heart, while the ventricles function as powerful pumps that push blood
to the lungs and the rest of the body [4]. Deoxygenated blood enters the RA through the superior and
inferior Vena Cava and is delivered to the RV. The RV pumps blood to the lungs through the pulmonary
artery (PA). The inhaled air in the lungs scatters into the bloodstream, oxygenating the blood, while
carbon dioxide is exhaled. The LA receives the oxygenated blood and transfers it to the LV, where it
is pumped into the body through the aorta. Separating the heart chambers are the heart valves, which
ensure the unidirectional flow of blood. These include the tricuspid valve, pulmonary valve, mitral valve,
and aortic valve, each of which prevents backflow and maintains efficient circulation. The myocardium
is a thick layer of muscle surrounding the heart chambers (shown in bright pink). The myocardium is
responsible for the heart’s contraction and relaxation, ensuring that the blood keeps moving [34].
While the heart pumps blood to the entire body, it also requires a dedicated supply of oxygen and
nutrients to sustain its function. This vital role is carried out by the coronary arteries, a specialized

network of blood vessels that encircle the heart and ensure its continuous oxygenation. Without an
adequate blood supply from these arteries, the heart muscle itself would struggle to function, potentially
leading to serious conditions such as ischemia or infarction. Understanding the coronary arteries is
crucial in recognizing the mechanisms behind cardiovascular diseases [34].
2.2
Coronary Arteries
The coronary arteries are the main blood vessels that supply blood to the heart. They allow the heart to
beat and pump blood through the body. The coronary arteries originate from the aorta, just above the
aortic valve, and branch out across the surface of the heart, forming an intricate system of circulation
known as the coronary artery tree [9]. The coronary arteries are divided into two main branches, which
are depicted in Figure 2.2.
1. The left coronary artery (LCA) carries blood to the left part of the heart and originates from
the left side of the aorta. The very initial part connected to the aorta is known as the left main
coronary artery (LMCA). It quickly branches into two main components.
• The left anterior descending artery (LAD) runs along the front of the heart and supplies
oxygenated blood to the front and lower part of the LV. This part of the coronary arteries
is sometimes referred to as the widow-maker because a blockage in this artery often causes
a fatal heart attack.
The larger branches extending from the LAD towards the margin
(essentially the edge of the heart) are called diagonals. The number of diagonals varies from
person to person.
• The left circumflex artery (LCX) curves around the left side of the heart and provides blood
to the LA and the side and back portions of the LV. The LCX further branches into the left
marginal artery (LMA).
2. The right coronary artery (RCA) arises from the right side of the aorta and mainly supplies blood
to the RA and the RV. It also has two main components.
• The posterior descending artery (PDA) runs along the back of the heart and is responsible
for supplying the bottom portion of the LV and parts of the RV.
• The right marginal artery (RMA) travels along the right side of the heart, providing blood
to the front and outer surfaces of the RV [24].

While the coronary arteries have a general pattern, there are several anatomical variations that can
occur. The coronary artery system can be right dominant, left dominant, or co-dominant. Right dom-
inant is the most frequently occurring, about 80% to 85% of the time. Right dominance occurs when
the PDA branches from the RCA. In cases of left dominance, occurring about 15% to 20% of the time,
the PDA extends from the LCX. Finally, co-dominant hearts occur in roughly 5% of cases. This oc-
curs when a single or duplicated PDA is supplied by branches of both the RCA and the LAD or LCX. [27]
The coronary arteries vary in thickness, with the thickest part being near their root and narrowing to-
wards the end. Dodge et. al [12] and Muneeb et al. [30] studied the diameters of the coronary arteries.
They report the circumference in three segments, proximal, mid, and distal of the LAD, as shown in

2.2.1
Coronary Artery Disease
Coronary artery disease (CAD) is the most prevalent cardiovascular condition affecting the coronary
arteries and is a leading cause of morbidity and mortality worldwide. It develops due to atherosclerosis,
a progressive condition characterized by the accumulation of plaque within the lumen of the coronary
arteries. The lumen refers to the space inside the arteries where the blood flows. This plaque consists
of fatty deposits, cholesterol, calcium, and other substances, leading to the gradual narrowing (stenosis)

or complete blockage of the coronary arteries [44], as illustrated in Figure 2.4(a).
CAD begins when the inner lining of the arteries becomes damaged due to risk factors such as high blood
pressure, smoking, diabetes, high cholesterol, and inflammation. This damage promotes the deposition
of lipids and inflammatory cells, leading to the formation of atherosclerotic plaques [16]. Over time,
these plaques can:
• Narrow the arteries and reduce blood flow to the heart muscle, leading to ischemia (insufficient
oxygen supply).
• Rupture, causing blood clots to form, which can rapidly block the artery and result in a myocardial
infarction (heart attack).
• Harden and stiffen the arteries (arteriosclerosis), reducing their ability to expand and contract,
which impairs circulation [44].

Atherosclerotic plaque can be classified according to its composition into calcified plaque, non-calcified
plaque, and mixed plaque, i.e., plaque containing calcified and non-calcified components.
Calcified

considered unstable and more prone to rupture. Such rupture may lead to acute coronary syndrome

assess the blockages, a coronary computed tomography angiography is a valuable tool [7].

2.3
Computed Tomography
Computed tomography (CT) is a non-invasive imaging technique that utilizes X-rays and computer
processing to produce cross-sectional images (slices) of the human body. In this process, an X-ray beam
is directed at a specific region of interest, such as the abdomen, heart, or brain. A detector system,
which may be stationary or rotating around the patient, captures the variations in X-ray absorption as
the beams pass through different tissues in the body [32].
During a CT scan, the gantry rotates around the body, capturing multiple perspectives of the same
anatomical structures. This design explains why CT scanners have a distinctive doughnut-shaped struc-
ture, as illustrated in Figure 2.5. The acquired data is then processed using advanced reconstruction
algorithms, which combine multiple projections and image slices sequentially. This results in a compre-
hensive 3D multiplanar representation of the body, allowing for highly detailed medical imaging and
diagnostic assessment [15]. The final CT scan is visualised in three different anatomical planes: axial,
saggital, and coronal. The planes are shown in Figure 2.6.
Figure 2.5: CT scanning system at Rigshospitalet.
The differentiation in CT images is a reflection of different attenuations of the X-ray observed by the
detector. Different kinds of tissue attenuate differently. Bones have a very high attenuation because
they absorb more of the X-rays, and therefore appear brighter in the image. While lungs and air have
a much lower attenuation and appears as darker areas. The different attenuation levels are represented
by Hounsfield Units (HU). HU is defined as a linear transformation of the measured X-ray attenuation
coefficient, expressed as
HU = 1000 · µt −µw
µw
here µt refers to the captured attenuation from the CT scanner and µw refers to the attenuation of water.
The HU scale is scaled such that water has an attenuation of 0 HU and air a density of -1000 HU.
One rotation of the CT scanner can typically cover anywhere from 4-16 cm of the body, depending on
the specific model. In many cases, this means that multiple rotations are needed to get a complete
view of the region of interest. Due to this fact, a common occurrence in CT scans is step artifacts.
Step artifacts happen as a result of misalignment between slices from different rotations, which creates
visible gaps or inconsistencies in the final reconstruction [42].

2.3.1
Coronary CT Angiography
Invasive coronary angiography (ICA) remains the gold standard for diagnosing coronary artery disease,
which can be complicated by both patient-specific anatomy and image quality. As an alternative, coro-
nary CT angiography (CCTA) has gained popularity due to its non-invasive nature, high-resolution
imaging capabilities, and the ability to provide a more comprehensive evaluation of coronary artery
stenosis [14].
Different from a regular CT scan, during a CCTA a contrast dye is injected through an intravenous
(IV) line in the arm [39]. Contrast has the effect that it makes the arteries ”light up” and be more
visible. The hollow central space where the contrast-filled blood flows within the coronary arteries is
called the lumen. The lumen appears brighter than normal tissue; however, calcification and metal (if
present) exhibit even greater brightness. A CCTA scan is performed during the arterial phase, when
the contrast is at its peak concentration. The intensity of the lumen’s brightness gradually diminishes
along the course of the arteries. Since the heart beats continuously, an ECG is used to synchronize
image acquisition with different phases of the cardiac cycle, reducing motion artifacts and enhancing
image clarity. However, when trying to identify plaque or calcifications along the arteries, the contrast
may make it difficult to separate the calcifications from the lumen since they both appear very bright
in the final image [49].
Since contrast enhancement dissipates rapidly from the heart, cases where the entire heart cannot be
captured in a single CT rotation are at risk of step artifacts. Additionally, attenuation differences may
arise between rotations, as the effect of the contrast agent diminishes during the second pass [39].
2.3.2

of calcified plaque in the coronary arteries [35]. The CAC score is calculated by assigning a weighted
density score to the location of calcium with the highest attenuation (HU) and then multiplying by
the area of calcification (in mm2). The test, however, is only performed on a regular CT scan, that

2.3.3
Stenosis classification
The coronary artery disease reporting and data systems (CAD-RADS) is a method developed to stan-

• Category 0: 0% narrowing - no coronary luminal stenosis and no plaque
• Category 1: 1-24% narrowing - minimal stenosis or plaque with no stenosis
• Category 2: 25-49% narrowing - mild stenosis
• Category 3: 50-69% narrowing - moderate stenosis
• Category 4: 70-99% narrowing - severe coronary stenosis
• Category 5: 100% narrowing - coronary occlusion

4.1.1
Spline Smoothing
Essentially s determines the number of internal knots of the spline. A small s means that the spline
follows the data very closely. To achieve this, the spline needs more flexibility, which is accomplished
by adding more internal knots, allowing it to bend and wiggle to fit each local variation in the data. A
large s penalizes curvature more strongly, encouraging a smoother (less wiggly) spline. To do this, the
algorithm reduces the number of internal knots, making the spline closer to a low-degree polynomial.
The strategy for finding the optimal number of knots goes as follows. First of all, the least squares
spline with no interior knots is found, denote it S 0, if ∑n
i=1 ||Pi −S 0(ui)||2 ≤s then S 0 is a solution and we
can stop. This is generally not the case, as it will usually be larger. To continue, successively increase
the number of knots nj and determine the least squares splines S nj until the condition in Equation 4.3
is satisfied. The concept is shown in Figure 4.2.
The number of knots added at each iteration depends on the number of knots added in the previous
iteration and the decrease in the sum of least squares residuals, i.e., left-hand side of Equation 4.3. The
new knots will then be placed in those knot intervals where the weighted sum of squared residuals is
large as compared to other ones. Therefore we can say that the strategy for adding knots is adaptive
in that there will be more knots in regions where the data is difficult to approximate and less knots
in areas that are already exhibiting smooth behavior, this can be seen in Figure 4.3 where a spline is
fitted to a centerline with different smoothing factors.

4.2
Curved Planar Reformatting
Curved planar reformatting (CPR) is an image processing technique used primarily in medical imaging
to visualize tubular structures. The goal of CPR is turning a curved structure into a straight volume,
which allows the entire tubular structure to be visible in a single image. The CPR process uses the cen-
terline of the target structure. In this work, we assume that we are given an estimate of the centerlines
of the coronary arteries. There are several methods for this, including maximal inscribed balls (MIB),
which is an approach that computes the largest possible sphere that fits within the segmentation of the
coronary arteries; however, recent studies have shown great development in fully automatic coronary
artery tracking algorithms [38].

4.3.1
Ground Truth Refinement
After transforming the data using CPR, it is necessary to refine the ground truth segmentation to
exclude plaque regions from the labeled coronary arteries. Since the goal is to segment only the lumen,
the open channel through which blood flows, any parts of the annotation that include plaque must
be removed. In some cases, the original ground truth segmentation wraps around plaque deposits, as
illustrated in the left image of Figure 4.8. However, since these regions do not contribute to actual
blood flow, they should not be included in the lumen label. Therefore, we modify the segmentation
masks to exclude these areas, resulting in a cleaner and more accurate representation of the true lumen,
as shown in the center image of the same figure.
During the CPR process, the coronary artery is centered in each cross-sectional slice, placing the artery
lumen at the center of the image.
Since all slices are of size 160 × 160, the geometric center is at
pixel coordinates (80, 80). To remove plaque regions from the lumen segmentation, we examine radial
lines extending from the center toward the edge of the annotation. Along each line, we evaluate the
intensity values of the pixels. Examples of this are shown in Figure 4.9. If a pixel exceeds the threshold
HUmean + 2SD (see Subsection 4.4.1) it is considered plaque. From that point onward along the radial
line, all remaining pixels are set to zero in the segmentation mask, effectively excluding the plaque-
covered area. This process ensures that only the true lumen, free of calcified plaque, is retained in the
segmentation. An example of this procedure is shown in Figure 4.10.

To account for cases where plaque regions overlap the center of the image or when multiple plaque

based on plaque location. Each cross-sectional slice is divided into four regions: top, bottom, left, and

direction, toward the opposite side. This adjustment is repeated iteratively if multiple plaque regions
are present, continuing until the new center no longer overlaps with any plaque.

4.4.2
Convolutional Autoencoder
Convolutional autoencoders are a specialized type of neural network designed for efficient representa-
tion learning, particularly in image data. They combine the power of convolutional layers, which excel
at extracting spatial features from images, with the principles of autoencoders, which are designed to
compress data into lower-dimensional representations and then reconstruct it.
The CAE architecture applied is shown in Figure 4.13. The CAE consists of two main components:
• Encoder: The encoder starts from the left-hand side of the figure. The goal of the encoder is to
compress the input image into a latent space representation by applying convolutional layers, which
are indicated by the orange arrows. One convolutional layer consists of a 3x3 kernel convolution
with a stride of 2 to downsample the features, followed by a ReLU activation function. These
layers help extract hierarchical features from the image, capturing patterns such as edges, textures,
and shapes.
• Decoder: The decoder reconstructs the input image from its compressed latent representation. The
decoder reconstructs the image by applying deconvolution operations as indicated by the brown
arrows. Similar to the encoder convolutional layer, the decoder layers consist of a transpose 3x3
kernel convolution with a stride of 2 to upsample the features. The first two deconvolutions are
again followed by a ReLU operation while the final output is transformed with a sigmoid operation.
The deconvolution layers gradually build the image back to its original dimensions, striving to
preserve its features as accurately as possible.
Autoencoders are particularly valuable when ground truth data is unavailable for training. In this case,
the goal is to identify areas of calcium for which there are no labeled annotations. To address this, the
CAE is trained using scans from healthy patients. After visual inspection combined with results from
calcium volume scoring, 5 LADs of patients from the training set are chosen as representing a healthy
class of patients showing no signs of calcification anywhere in LCA. Bear in mind, this is from the view
of a non-medical professional. The CAE is trained to reconstruct these images. The model is trained on
2D axial slices. The final number of slices is 13809. As a preprocessing step, each slice is first clipped to

4.5
nnU-Net for CPR Lumen Segmentation
This project utilizes a deep learning framework called nnU-Net to accomplish robust lumen segmenta-
tion. The goal regarding lumen segmentation is to have a precise model that can aid in the clinical
assessment of coronary artery stenosis. If we have an accurate model determining the lumen shape, we
can use it to infer a great deal about narrowing along the arteries. nnU-Net, or the ”no-new-Net”, is an
automated deep learning framework designed specifically for medical image segmentation. At its core,
nnU-Net is built upon the well-established U-Net architecture, which has proven effective in biomedical
image segmentation [40]. The following sections will describe some general details of the nnU-Net setup,
while focusing on specifications that are relevant to the CPR lumen segmentation task.
nnU-Net is a self-configuring setup that dynamically configures itself based on the properties of the
input data, making it a highly versatile tool in the field of medical imaging. This eliminates the need
for manual hyperparameter and configuration tuning. nnU-Net relies on the idea that a task-specific
design and configuration are more important than architectural variations and extensions [22].
A complete overview of the nnU-Net setup is visualized in Figure 4.14. Based on the input data, nnU-
Net creates a data fingerprint. The data fingerprint contains key properties of the input data, such as

image sizes, intensity statistics, class balance, voxel spacing, etc. (indicated in pink). From the data
fingerprint, a pipeline fingerprint is created. The pipeline fingerprint contains the design choices.

The parameters of the pipeline fingerprint are divided into three types.
• Blueprint (indicated in blue): Blueprint parameters are static across all datasets. These are the
fundamental design choices such as using the U-Net architecture, optimizer, loss function, and so
on. These are already determined in the nnU-net framework.
• Inferred (indicated in green): These are adapted based on the dataset and set of heuristic rules.
These are parameters that modify things like patch size, batch size, and image normalization.
• Empirical (indicated in yellow): These are determined autonomously during training and valida-
tion. This includes the choice of ensemble model (if applicable) and postprocessing steps such as
connected component labeling [20].
The heuristic rules used to find the inferred parameters are applied in an iterative process, where the
architecture configuration is continuously updated until all restrictions are met and the final optimal
U-Net structure is found. The relevant inferred parameters are described as
• Intensity Normalization: For CT scans nnU-Net employs intensity normalization using the 0.5
and 99.5 percentiles of the foreground voxels for clipping, as well as the global foreground mean
and SD for the normalization of all images.
• Patch Size: Patch size is initialized to the median image shape and is iteratively reduced, while
adapting the network topology accordingly, until the network can be trained with a batch size of at
least 2. A larger patch size will allow for more information to be retained and will typically increase
segmentation performance. However, it comes at a cost of reducing the batch size, which leads
to noisier gradients, because nnU-Net also prioritizes staying within a predefined GPU memory
budget. nnU-Net predicts memory usage for a specific network setup by analyzing the dimensions
of its feature maps and comparing them to reference memory benchmarks.
It then gradually
reduces the patch size, adjusting the network configuration at each step, until the estimated
memory fits within the available budget.

Structure of 3D Full-Resolution U-Net

spatial information is recovered in the decoder part of the network. The decoder gathers the high-level
semantic information at the lowest spatial dimension, i.e., the bottom of the U-Net, and combines it
with the higher resolution information from the encoder through skip connections. This ensures that
the network retains both semantic and spatial information needed for successful segmentation.

4.5.2
Training
By default, all networks in nnU-Net are trained for 1000 epochs to ensure thorough convergence across
diverse tasks and datasets. However, for the specific task at hand, it was empirically observed that
training for 250 epochs was sufficient to achieve stable performance. This adjustment provides a prac-
tical trade-off between computational efficiency and model accuracy, as the validation loss typically
stagnates around this point, indicating diminishing returns from further training.
In nnU-Net, a single epoch is defined as an iteration over 250 randomly sampled mini-batches, rather
than a full pass over the training dataset. This stochastic sampling contributes to better generalization
and enables efficient utilization of GPU memory.
Training is performed using stochastic gradient descent (SGD) with Nesterov momentum, where the
momentum parameter is set to 0.99 to accelerate convergence while dampening oscillations. The initial
learning rate is set to 0.01, and it is automatically adapted during training via a polynomial decay
schedule to facilitate stable optimization.

4.5.3
Inference
All models are trained from scratch and are evaluated using five-fold cross-validation on the training
set. This means that 5 different subsets of data are used to train five separate models. To enhance
overall performance, nnU-Net combines predictions into an ensemble model. This can be done either
within one configuration, i.e., combining 5 models within 3DfullRes or across multiple configurations,
such as 2 × 5 = 10 models from 3DfullRes and 3DlowRes. To create the ensemble model, we must first
perform inference.
Since the network employs patch-based learning, all inference is similarly done patch-wise. During in-
ference, a sliding window approach is used. The window size is chosen to be the same as the patch size
and moves across or ’slides’ along the image, while calculating the softmax probabilities of each patch.
The window moves with a stride of half the size of the patch in all directions. The concept is shown in
Figure 4.16.
Towards the border of the window, the predictions are less accurate, and to combat this, nnU-Net ap-
plies Gaussian importance weighting, where the center pixel in the window serves as the central point,
i.e., it is given the highest weight. This will suppress stitching artifacts and reduce the influence of
positions close to the borders. This sliding window scheme results in multiple predictions per pixel
from different patches. The final class assignment is then done by calculating a weighted average of the
collected softmax probabilities, using the weights from the Gaussian importance weighting scheme.
To generate the final segmentation, the ensemble method averages the softmax outputs (i.e., class
probability distributions) from all models. For each voxel, the final predicted class is determined by
selecting the class with the highest averaged probability across all models (argmax). If multiple ensemble
configurations are evaluated, the one achieving the highest dice score on the validation data is selected
as the final model.
4.5.4
Postprocessing
The only postprocessing step included in nnU-Net is based on connected component labeling (CCL), a
widely used technique in medical image segmentation. It works by identifying and retaining only the
largest connected region in the predicted segmentation, effectively removing small isolated false posi-
tives. nnU-Net automatically evaluates whether applying CCL improves the dice score on the validation
set. If it does, the postprocessing step is included in the final pipeline; otherwise, it is omitted.

4.6
Graph Cut Segmentation and Inverse Mapping
Accurate segmentation of coronary arteries is a prerequisite for reliable morphometric analysis, such as
diameter estimation. In this project, we adopt and extend the pipeline proposed by Rasmussen [25],
which integrates anatomical priors with advanced graph-based segmentation in CPR space.
Similar to this project, the pipeline begins by extracting the centerlines of the coronary artery tree,
which are then used to generate CPR volumes. The CPR images are then resampled in polar coordi-
nates to unfold the circular lumen structure into a 2D image, where segmentation becomes a matter of
identifying a horizontal boundary between the lumen and surrounding tissue.
To segment the lumen, a Sparse Layered Graph (SLG) is constructed across the stacked polar-resampled
slices. The algorithm performs a global graph cut based on the pixel-wise gradient magnitudes, aiming
to detect the optimal boundary across all slices. Importantly, the quality of this segmentation is heav-
ily influenced by the quality of the probability maps used to derive the gradients. Among the tested
preprocessing strategies—baseline (raw intensities), heuristic maps, and nnU-Net-derived maps—the
latter yielded the most accurate and anatomically coherent segmentations, as quantified by dice score
and Hausdorff distance.
Once the segmentation is complete in polar space, the resulting mesh is mapped back to the original
3D volume through a two-stage inverse transformation. First, the mesh is reprojected from polar to
straight space, and then from straight space back to the original curve space using the inverse CPR
transformation. This process reconstructs an anatomically correct lumen mesh in its original orienta-
tion. Additionally, the method supports the integration of multiple segmented branches into a single

4.7
Diameter Estimation
With the coronary lumen segmented using the SLG algorithm, guided by probability maps generated
by the trained nnU-Net model, we proceed to estimate the artery diameter along the vessel length. The
reconstructed mesh is structured such that its length corresponds to the arc length of the original artery
spline. Therefore, each cross-section of the mesh can be interpreted as representing a CPR slice perpen-
dicular to the artery’s centerline. Each of these slices contains a set of points outlining the boundary
of the lumen.
A naive method would compute the circumference by summing pairwise Euclidean distances between
neighboring points along the contour and then divide π to get the diameter. However, this can ac-
cumulate unnecessary length and significantly overestimate the true diameter. Instead, we employ a
more robust geometric approach: each point on the contour is reflected across the center of mass of
the slice, and the closest point on the opposite side of the contour is identified. This concept is shown
in Figure 4.18. Denote the center of mass COM, the contour point x and the reflected point x∗, the
reflection is given by x∗= 2 · COM −x.

To accomplish the complete re-centering of the centerline, first map the new center (i.e., the center of
mass) from image coordinates to physical coordinates. This is achieved using the affine transformation
matrix, which defines how voxel indices map to physical space in millimeters. Specifically, the affine
matrix encodes scaling, rotation, translation, and shearing components. Then apply the inverse map-
ping function to convert the coordinates back to the original curved space and use these new centerline
points to determine a spline and recompute CPR. The steps are shown in Figure 4.21.
Once the centerline has been re-centered, it is necessary to smooth it. Arteries typically follow a smooth,
continuous path without abrupt changes. Thus, smoothing the centerline ensures anatomical plausi-
bility and better quality in CPR visualization. As an example in Figure 4.22, we see the centerlines
visualized in 3D space. The blue curve represents the original centerline. The pink curve is the recen-

smoothed reestimated centerline. While the reestimated centerline (pink) is close to the original, it

4.8.1
Centering Score
Since we are only provided with estimated coronary artery centerlines, there is currently no definitive
way to verify whether the recentering process yields a more accurate result. To address this, we propose
a centering score, a metric that quantifies how well the new centerline appears to align with the true
artery center. Through optimization, this can be applied to determine how much each centerline should
be smoothed to achieve an optimal result.
For each slice in the CPR volume, extract intensity profiles along lines of different angles as shown on
the left-hand side of Figure 4.23. Define the centering score, combining both skewness and kurtosis to
assess the symmetry and shape of the radial intensity profiles. Skewness quantifies the asymmetry of
a distribution, while kurtosis describes the tailedness, or how peaked and heavy-tailed the distribution
is. A perfectly symmetric, bell-shaped (normal) distribution has a skewness of 0 and a kurtosis of 3. In
the context of lumen centering, we expect a well-centered intensity profile to resemble a Gaussian curve,
symmetric and moderately peaked. Therefore, deviations from 0 in skewness or 3 in kurtosis indicate
poor centering. We define the bell-curve deviation (BCD) score as:
BCD = |skewness| + |kurtosis −3|
(4.10)
A lower score (close to 0) suggests the profile is more Gaussian-like and, by extension, better centered.

Discussion
6.1
Calcium Detection Approaches
Due to the difficulty in collecting reliable labels for medical data, two unsupervised approaches to detect
calcium in the coronary arteries are studied. Both the threshold-based method and the CAE showed
the ability to outline calcium areas. While the threshold-based method is simpler, it relies heavily on a
correct threshold. This was evident in Figure 5.2 with the most extreme calcium volume score. Likely,
this is caused by the threshold being too low, perhaps attributable to the image being stitched together
at times where the contrast is more washed out in the aorta, but is still highly present in the artery.
Since going through every image in the dataset would be too time-consuming, it is unknown how many
images are victims of this effect and therefore overestimate the calcium burden. In contrast, the CAE
appears effective regardless of calcium size, shape, or intensity characteristics. Furthermore, the t-SNE
plot (Figure 5.4) showed that the CAE learned to discern between calcified and non-calcified slices, only
showing some minor overlap. It should be noted that the calcified slices are classified as such based on
the threshold-based method.
Comparing the two methods showed that they are mostly in agreement (see confusion matrix in Fig-
ure 5.5), with the most disagreement occurring in cases labeled as non-calcified by the threshold method
but classified as calcified by CAE. This suggests that the threshold method is more conservative. How-
ever, this could also suggest that the CAE may be more sensitive to subtle features not captured by
the HU thresholding approach. Potentially it might be able to identify fibrous or mixed plaque or
early signs of plaque not yet dense enough to exceed the intensity threshold. However, without proper
labels, it is not clear which method achieves the best performance. It is feasible that a combination
of the two methods might yield the best results, e.g., a calcified area only being classified as such if
both the threshold-based method and CAE agree. For further work in this project, it was determined
to use the threshold-based method e.g., in the data preprocessing step. This choice was taken because
thresholding is well-understood and interpretable in a clinical setting, and it aligns well with the already
clinically used CAC-score. In contrast, the CAE is a black-box model whose learned features are hard
to interpret and explain to clinicians.
Finally, the dataset distribution demonstrates reasonable consistency between training and test splits,
which is important for ensuring generalizability. Although the test set shows slightly elevated calcium
prevalence, the difference remains within acceptable bounds. The higher proportion of calcium-positive
cases in the test set may even serve as a useful stress test to evaluate how well the model handles more
challenging, clinically relevant cases.
6.2
nnU-Net Performance
The work done in this project relies heavily on well-segmented coronary arteries, and accurate segmen-
tation masks are essential for training the nnU-Net models. If the training data is flawed, the model’s
output will be affected. It was evident when evaluating the data that the segmentation masks did not
always accurately present the lumen, especially in cases where calcium plaques were visible, hence the
need for further refinement before training.
==PAGE:64==
52
6 Discussion
The performance on calcified arteries showed that the model is able to accurately avoid these areas
and only include the lumen in the segmentation, which was the main goal. Based on the probability
map in Figure 5.10, a probability of essentially 0 is assigned to calcium regions, with the only slight
discrepancies being near the boundary between lumen and calcium. The overall performance of the
models was good, with the majority of segmented arteries having a high dice score as indicated by the
violin plots (Figure 5.6 and Figure 5.7). Being able to assess the arteries in isolation can be useful
in revealing localized segmentation errors that might otherwise be diluted when evaluating the whole
tree, especially as we saw a significantly lower minimum dice score (see Figure 5.6). However, whether
evaluating the model on individual branches or combined trees, both indicated that 75% of test set
scores a dice higher than 0.85.
One of the pitfalls throughout the performance of the U-net model was around the distal end of the
arteries. As displayed in Figure 5.8 and Figure 5.9, there are both cases where the ground truth is
inadequate and cases where the prediction oversegments. The prominent reasons are the diminished
efficacy of the lumen, which is attributable to the arteries being extremely small and, concurrently, the
very low brightness of the contrast agent. The HU intensity variation poses a problem, but may also
be seen as a way to help the model be more robust and generalize better to unseen data. We also saw
that the model was sensitive to abnormal tree structures, which created awkward segmentations.
6.2.1
Model Selection
Choosing to work either with the LCA model or the LAD model required several considerations. Based
on the performance as summarized in Table 5.2, both models showed great performance across branches
and combined trees. However, there were slightly better dice scores for the LCA model, but it is nec-
essary to take into account whether the increased training time by going from 736 3D images to over
4000 3D images is worth it.
Another important thing to take into consideration is the prevalence of calcium. The preliminary exper-
iments around calcium volume score suggest that calcium deposits are more prevalent in branches such
as the LCX, diagonals, or marginal arteries, rather than being limited to the LAD. Therefore, focus-
ing solely on the LAD may overlook significant portions of the disease burden in the coronary artery tree.
Based on these considerations, it was decided that even with the added time burden, the most appro-
priate way to continue was to go with the LCA model to ensure that we capture as many different
structures as possible and stay as accurate as possible.
6.2.2
Comparison with State-of-the-Art Methods
Table 6.1 compares the performance of this project’s segmentation pipeline against several recent state-
of-the-art methods. While some of these methods report higher dice similarity coefficients, a closer
examination reveals important limitations in terms of dataset size, annotation completeness, and ar-
chitectural complexity. Furthermore, it is worth noting that all of these studies are performed using
small private datasets, because there are very few publicly available datasets for coronary artery seg-
mentation. This impacts reproducibility and cross-comparison, while this project uses ImageCAS, a
large-scale publicly available dataset, to increase robustness and real-world application.
Pan et al. [36] trained and evaluated their U-Net-based segmentation model exclusively on non-stenotic
CT angiography scans. Although their reported dice score is high (0.9691), the dataset lacks cases
with pathology, limiting its generalizability to real-world clinical scenarios. Furthermore, their test set
included only 42 images, reducing statistical robustness.

Dong et al. [13] proposed a multi-scale attention network for coronary artery segmentation and reported

ImageCAS dataset, evaluated over a test set of 99 scans, significantly larger than those used in the
compared studies.
This suggests that with appropriate training data and preprocessing, standardized architectures like
nnU-Net can match or exceed the performance of more complex custom models, while offering improved

the percentage narrowing to compare to the CAD-RADS classifications. This could perhaps be done
by combining the threshold methods with the CAE method to extract the calcifications, then estimate
the diameter of these regions, and find how big an area this takes away from the diameter of the lumen.
6.4
Centerline Re-centering
Centerline re-centering is an important step in coronary artery analysis. Since this thesis revolves around
CPR images, it is extremely valuable to try to increase the accuracy of these images. It was clear when
estimating the diameter that the results are greatly affected by how the centerline is estimated and how
accurately it follows the actual center of the lumen.
The Cscore developed for this thesis is not a perfect measurement, and it is important to note its limita-
tions. It was developed and defined based on slices of healthy arteries, by which is meant slices where
the artery is clearly visible and the background is uniformly darker than the lumen, and without any
signs of calcifications and branches. This is not always the case, as was evident, especially when looking
at slices from the distal end of the arteries or calcium areas. In these cases the Cscore cannot be expected
to accurately reflect a well-centered artery. However, this should be somewhat mitigated by taking the
average across slices. Furthermore, the score is sensitive to circularity, and although the lumen appears
circular in many cases, there are times when that is not the case. These limitations add up to a lack of
robustness and generalization.
With that in mind, the results reflected that determining the optimal smoothing parameter decreased
the Cscore significantly. This improvement is also reflected in the reduced interquartile range, suggesting
a more consistent centering performance across different subjects. Even though one of the goals was
to try and mitigate issues around the distal ends of the arteries, these persist, as there are several
spots where the BCD score remains high (Figure 5.15). However, it was revealed that in some cases
(Figure 5.16 and Figure 5.17) smoothed centerlines corrected large deviations in anatomical alignment.
This indicates that the smoothing process can help regularize these fluctuations, improving both geo-
metric and visual coherence in CPR reconstructions. However, the results also highlight that centerline

All together, these findings highlight that while the smoothing process generally improves visual quality

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
This thesis presented several experimental approaches for the analysis of coronary artery stenosis in
CCTA images. The pipeline focused on calcium detection, lumen segmentation, diameter estimation,
and re-centering of vessel centerlines—all aimed at enhancing the reliability of stenosis assessment.
Calcium detection was explored with two experimental approaches. Both methods successfully delin-
eated calcified regions, with the threshold-based approach estimating a total of 30.7% of the data being
affected by calcium. However, the absence of annotated ground truth limited objective performance
evaluation. The CAE demonstrated potential for detecting subtle features beyond the reach of intensity-
based thresholding, though its interpretability remains a challenge.
Accurate lumen segmentation was achieved with a 3D full-resolution nnU-Net model on CPR images.
Although the increase in performance was slim, training on all branches of the coronary artery tree was
preferable to training on data only containing the LAD. Furthermore, the model generated plausible
probability maps even in the presence of calcifications. The final model achieved a dice score of 0.882,
which was comparable to other state-of-the-art approaches. However, it stood out as being evaluated
on a large, fully publicly available dataset and without the need for specialized optimization due to the
self-configuring nature of the nnU-Net setup.
Diameter estimation guided by U-Net-derived probability maps resulted in diameter profiles consistent
with expected anatomical ranges. Furthermore, the method proved successful in detecting widening
areas consistent with calcification in the arteries. However, its accuracy was sensitive to the visual
consistency of the images. To combat this issue, a method for re-centering centerlines was developed
using probability maps to compute the anatomical center of mass. This led to an average improvement
of 0.27 in the proposed Cscore centering metric. Furthermore, the spline smoothing process was seen to
improve artery continuity but was also seen to introduce some unwanted artifacts in some cases.
Despite promising results, the methods were challenged by inconsistencies in the dataset, which affected
both segmentation and diameter estimation accuracy. Especially, artifacts at the distal ends of arteries
proved extra troublesome. These limitations highlight the importance of standardized, high-quality
imaging and preprocessing.
In conclusion, this thesis demonstrates that deep learning and image processing techniques offer a
feasible and promising approach to aiding stenosis detection in CCTA images. The work presented
marks a step toward improved clinical recognition and individualized risk assessment of coronary artery
stenosis, highlighting its potential to support more accurate and efficient diagnostics.
