Abstract
In this thesis the purpose is to to investigate the discrepancies in the literature about the
synergy between Cu and ZnO in industrial type Cu/ZnO/Al2O3 catalysts. The investigation
is performed by means of activity measurements on Cu/ZnO/Al2O3, Cu/ZnO, Cu/Al2O3,
ZnO/Al2O3, Cu/ZrO2, Cu/SiO2 and Cu/CeO2 catalysts prepared by co-precipitation, in-
cipient wetness impregnation, or sol-gel synthesis. The investigation were conducted under
varying space velocities from 1.5 · 103-4.3 · 107 L/kg/h at 250°C and 50 bars in a ﬂow reactor
setup, correlating the activities against the measured copper surface areas determined by
N2O-RFC at 60°C. Co-feeding of methanol and ethanol into the reactor with syngas was
conducted to investigate the possibility of auto catalytic behavior of methanol through an
esteriﬁcation.
At diﬀerential conditions a strong support eﬀect was found, but with limited relative diﬀer-
ence among some supports, ZnO∼= ZrO2>Al2O3≫SiO2. TOF of Cu/ZnO and Cu/ZnO/Al2O3
(5.06·10−2 s−1) was found within a factor of 2 of Cu/Al2O3(2.22·10−2 s−1). At low space ve-
locities, an acceleration in TOF (1.53·10−1 s−1) speciﬁcally for Cu/ZnO and Cu/ZnO/Al2O3
was observed, thus creating a strong synergy between Cu and ZnO at ﬁnite conditions.
Co-feeding of methanol shows a distinct increase in methanol activity supporting an auto
catalytic eﬀect.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Methanol is one of the most used petrochemicals in the modern world, and can be used in a
wide variety of products. The methanol synthesis is a mature process, which has been used
for about a century, producing methanol from synthesis gas consisting of CO2, CO and H2 at
pressures of now 20-100 bar and 200-300°C. It was discovered in the early ﬁrst part of the
20th century, that Cu based catalysts are very active for production, and since the 1960’s
Cu/ZnO based catalysts has been used in the low temperature process which now employs
the Cu/ZnO/Al2O3 catalyst[1, 2].
The methanol synthesis functions by the conversion of synthesis gas to methanol. The for-
mation of methanol can be described by the synthesis reactions (1.0.1) and (1.0.2) and the
water gas shift reaction (1.0.3):
CO2 + 3 H2 ⇆CH3OH + H2O

(1.0.2)

(1.0.3)

6th largest production, the 4th largest energy consumption and 3rd largest emission of green
house gasses in CO2 equivalents (denoted by the radius of the sphere)[3].

2.1.1
CO or CO2?
The mechanism of the methanol synthesis is a topic, which has been discussed and re-
searched for decades.
One of the major controversies about the mechanism used to be
whether methanol was primarily formed from CO or CO2 in the syngas over a copper cat-
alyst. However, this was solved in the 70’s and 80’s, and it was found that methanol was
primarily formed from CO2 under industrial applications. This was found out by the use
of 14C-labeling [8]. By labeling CO2 with radioactive 14C, it was found that the speciﬁc
radioactivity of formed methanol at the exit of the catalyst bed became equal to that of
incoming CO2 at high space velocity thereby verifying CO2 as the primary reactant as shown
in Figure 2.1. The quantiﬁcation of the amount of methanol made from either CO or CO2
was further investigated and it was found that even down to very low ratios of CO and CO2
the methanol was predominantly produced from CO2 [8], as shown in Figure 2.2. The results
were supported by other groups and experiments with other tracer atoms such as 18O and D
[9]. It was thus concluded that CO2 will be responsible for the majority of turnovers. Thus
in the following the focus will only be on the mechanisms, which converts CO2 to CH3OH.
2.1.2
Direct Hydrogenation
Mechanistic work has focused on elucidating a direct path from CO2 to methanol along a
series of HxCOy species such as formate, formyl and methoxide. The surface species from
adsorption of methanol, formic acid, paraformaldehyde, CO2 and CO on Cu/ZnO/Al2O3,
Cu/Al2O3, ZnO and Al2O3 were investigated [10] by use of 13C solid state NMR. It was found
that methanol adsorbed to become methoxy groups, which in presence of Cu became sym-
metrical formate, which could be converted to CO2. Paraformaldehyde disproportionates
into equal amounts of formate and methoxy on all investigated samples. Formaldehyde was
not detected under oxidation of methanol, but was deemed to reactive to be detected with
the applied protocol[10]. Thus, methanol adsorption had been observed to give CH3O and

There exists a low temperature, indirect methanol synthesis process, which makes methanol
from H2 and CO through methyl formate and was patented by Jens Anton Christiansen a
few years before the current direct process[39]. A possible reaction mechanism is as written
in (2.1.5) to (2.1.7).
OH∗+ CO ⇆HCOO∗
(2.1.5)
HCOO∗+ CH3OH ⇆HCOOCH3 + OH∗
(2.1.6)
HCOOCH3 + 4 H∗→2 CH3OH
(2.1.7)
In this alternative indirect method adsorbed formate reacts with methanol product to give
methyl formate, which is then hydrogenated to methanol.
In work by Fakley et al.[40], possible mechanisms for methanol synthesis from CO and H2 on
copper catalysts were discussed. It was suggested that the route patented by Christiansen
and not just the direct hydrogenation though formate could take place. Reaction (2.1.7) is
known to be a fast reaction compared to the methanol synthesis[40], and could thus account
for the rates observed on conventional catalysts. An auto catalytic mechanism cannot alone
describe the methanol synthesis since it needs a direct mechanism to produce the methanol
to initiate the mechanism (2.1.5) to (2.1.7) in the ﬁrst place[40]. On this basis, one can
hypothesize that a mechanism anologous to (2.1.5)-(2.1.7) but with HCOO from CO2 being
responsible for the auto catalytic behavior.

2.2
Active Sites
The nature of the active sites where the mechanism occurs is also unclear. Theories can
mainly be divided into two large groups with ample results to support each of them. The two
theories are that either only copper surface area is important or at least that all reduced sites
contribute similarly, and the other that there is a major synergy between copper and zinc
oxide that can raise the turnover frequency by an order of magnitude. This will be further
described in the following sections. However, before immersing into the diﬀerent theories the
knowledge of the working catalyst is summarized.

diﬀerent active areas for the catalyst. It was concluded, that metallic Cu is the active catalyst
for methanol synthesis and that the role of ZnO is one of degree of dispersion and possibly
distribution of surface planes exposed [23].
There is thus considerable data from both supported Cu and Cu single crystals to suggest,
that the reaction rate is only determined by the number of reduced sites, primarily the
metallic Cu surface.

2.2.3
Cu-ZnO Synergy
However, there are also numerous results with clear signs on a strong synergy between Cu
and Zn. They can be divided into experiments on single crystals with ZnO depositions and
reactor experiments.
For the reactor experiments the synergy is mostly reported in batch reactor studies. It was
for example found by IFP in France, that an order of magnitude diﬀerence in activity was
obtained when doping Cu/Al2O3 with ZnO[64, 65], and were later supported by Senanayake
et al.[33] for ZnO/Cu(111), (more details given later in this section).
Also experiments
by an Australian group comparing Raney-Cu catalysts with commercial catalysts in a ﬂow
reactor has found diﬀerent degrees of synergy, depending on the conditions under which the
experiments are performed. Where the Zn containing catalyst had an activity 2 times the
activity of the Zn-free Raney-Cu at diﬀerential conditions (GHSV = 750000 h−1)[60]. The
situation at ﬁnite conditions (GHSV = 10000 h−1) was that the activity of the Zn containing
catalyst was instead 9 times the activity of the catalyst with no zinc[66]. It could thus seem
that there may be a coherence between the exposure to the product and the measured synergy
between copper and zinc oxide.
The activity of various Zn-free and Zn-deposited Cu single crystals has been investigated by
Nakamura et al.[13, 18] with use of x-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) in combination
with a high-pressure ﬂow reactor. It was found that the catalytic activity of the Zn-free
copper planes, was (110)>(311)>(100)>(111), however polycrystalline Cu was very close to
the behavior of the most stable (111) plane. It was found from the kinetic measurements,
that though the activity of the Cu(110) plane was fourfold the activity of Cu(111), the
Zn deposited Cu(111) and Zn-deposited poly-crystalline copper had even better activity.
Thus the Zn-deposited Cu(111) and Zn-deposited polycrystalline Cu had activities 13- and
6-fold the activities of the Zn free surfaces, this even though the surface area of metallic
copper decreased due to the Zn deposition. The activity as a function of the Zn coverage
was investigated and Figure 2.20 shows the methanol TOF as a function of Zn coverage on
various Cu single crystals.
It can be seen that for the Zn/Cu(111) surface there is a maximum at ΘZn = 0.2 until which
the activity increases. The activity then decreases beyond Θ = 0.2. For the Zn/Cu(110) and
Zn/Cu(100) there is oddly enough no promotional eﬀect of Zn on the methanol formation.
It should also be noted, that the Zn-deposition did not in any case have promotional eﬀects
on the WGS reaction, which rather decreased to zero at ΘZn = 0.5, giving evidence that
the metallic copper surface decreases since WGS is well known to be catalyzed by Cu0.
This all suggest the formation of special sites, when adding Zn to the catalyst[13, 18]. In
the post-reaction studies, shifts in the Zn XPS peaks after reaction indicated, that Zn was
oxidized during reaction. It was also found, that the formate found on the catalysts after
reaction were more stable than formate on clean copper surfaces. Furthermore, there was
found oxygen in the form of ZnO or adsorbed O∗on Cu, which indicated, that the formate
on Zn/Cu(111) was formed on Zn. Metallic copper is however still necessary to facilitate

2.2.3.1
Structure Sensitivity
The previous two sections have illustrated obvious contradictions within the nature of the
active site that have not been explained. The sensitivity of the activity on the structure of the
catalyst is also still unclear, and some current ideas about resolving the varied observations
in terms of the synergy is that variations in the number of some especially active sites plays
a role. Work to identify the optimal structure for the industrial Cu/ZnO/Al2O3 catalyst
has been performed by Behrens et al.[28], applying a combination of DFT calculations with
experimental results from bulk, surface-sensitive, and imaging methods on high-performance
industrial catalysts. The experiments were executed on ﬁve diﬀerent working catalysts, and
one pure Cu metal sample as reference. All the samples had high Cu loading and particle
sizes of 5 to 15 nm, with at least 10 m2g−1 of Cu surface exposed, thus having similar prop-
erties as industrial catalysts. Activity measurements were conducted at 523 K and 483 K.

2.3
Hypothesis
In the above suggestions of at least two diﬀerent mechanisms on the methanol synthesis
catalysts have been described, namely, the direct hydrogenation of CO2 through formate
to methanol and an auto catalytic pathway possibly via a formate/methanol reaction. The
two main groups of theories about the active site of the methanol synthesis have also been
described, as well as the experimental results, which lay the ground for the theories.
In this work, the scope is to investigate whether the discrepancies about the nature of the
active sites could arise from an auto catalytic pathway made possible by the ZnO support

ZrO2 areas of 0.58-1.44 m2/g were achieved by impregnation and deposition precipitation
methods[91], which is relatively high compared to those used here, when taking the small
amount of Cu into account.
In the work by Chinchen et al. Cu areas of Cu/Al2O3 between 10 and 20 m2/g has been
reported, but given their assumption of the site density this corresponds to the present work.
The apparent copper surface area in fresh Cu/CeO2 is the highest measured by the N2O-
RFC in this thesis. This may in part arise from CuO/CeO2 being used as catalyst for N2O
decomposition in tail-gas treatment[92], which may naturally distort the accuracy of the
measurement. Thus is was decided to make a measurement on a spent catalyst sample. In
this case only a copper area of 2.8 m2/g was obtained, which is around the same as for the
Cu/SiO2, Cu/ZrO2 and 20 wt% Cu/Al2O3 samples, which seems reasonable since the copper
loading is the same. The measurement for the spent catalyst is thus used for later calculations
and comparisons. The reason for the major change in the spent Cu/CeO2 catalyst will be
discussed subsequently.
Overall it can be said, that the surface areas of copper in the catalysts prepared in this thesis
are within the range expected from the literature.

Chapter 4
Conclusion
In this thesis a number of diﬀerent catalyst samples prepared by diﬀerent routes have been
investigated w.r.t. methanol synthesis from a syngas mixture of 67.59 % H2, 29.63 % CO and
2.77 % CO2 at 250°C and 50 bar. With the catalysts the majority of the literature results,
such as the correlation between activity and Cu area, as well as the synergetic eﬀect between
Cu and ZnO, have been reproduced. At diﬀerential conditions a strong support eﬀect was
found, but with limited relative diﬀerence among some supports, ZnO∼= ZrO2>Al2O3≫SiO2,
in good agreement with the literature. TOF of Cu/ZnO and Cu/ZnO/Al2O3 (5.06 · 10−2
s−1) was found within a factor of around 2 of Cu/Al2O3(2.22 · 10−2 s−1) and a factor of
2-4 of single crystal data (depending on the extrapolation). Thus the limited synergy was
reproduced and has understandably been the conclusion in some earlier work. A basic oxide
such as Cu/CeO2 shows very low activity attributed to deactivation by structural changes,
possibly formation of hydroxy carbonates.
At ﬁnite conditions a strong acceleration of activity for speciﬁcally ZnO containing catalysts
increased the activity severalfold (to TOF = 1.53 · 10−1 s−1) compared to diﬀerential TOFs
and brings out the diﬀerence between ZnO and Al2O3 to around an order of magnitude. The
acceleration is attributed to an auto catalytic eﬀect of methanol and corresponded to an
apparent reaction order of 0.5. Thus both strong and limited synergy between Cu and ZnO
has been reproduced.
Experiments on co-feeding methanol into the reactor along with the syngas were performed.
It was found that it in all cases improved the activity of the catalyst, especially at a space
velocity of 1.6 · 106 L/kg/h where a factor of 2.5 in the activity of the catalyst was achieved
when feeding 0.8 mole% methanol, compared to a factor of 1.3 at a space velocity of 8.0 · 105
L/kg/h.This supports an auto catalytic eﬀect and is in accordance with the relative eﬀect
being stronger when starting closer to diﬀerential conditions.
The results in this thesis indicate an auto catalytic mechanism, which potentially makes it
possible to explain the discrepancies in the literature about the synergy between Cu and
ZnO since there, depending on the conditions, may be a strong or a weaker synergetic eﬀect
compared to other catalysts such as Cu/ZrO2 and Cu/Al2O3.

Chapter 5
Future Work
In this work results pointing towards an auto catalytic mechanism have been shown. This
makes it of interest to look into how the auto catalysis may be used in industrial processes.
Such an investigation may look at how big a reﬂux of methanol would give the optimum
reaction conditions, and economics.
It has been found in this thesis that only the catalysts containing Zn showed any auto cat-
alytic behavior and that the eﬀect requires an atomic mixing of Cu/ZnO to work. However,
it has not been found why, whether it is because Zn can be reduced, and if that is the case,
would SnO2 also work since both ZnO and SnO2 is known to form reasonably accessible alloys
with Cu?
The mechanism shown in Figure 3.17 should be investigated more in depth, than it has been
done in this thesis. It would be desirable with veriﬁcation of the facile nature of ester forma-
tion when treating Cu/ZnO with CO2/H2 to generate formate, followed by H2 or CH3OH/He
with TPR to compare how facile ester formation is compared to direct hydrogenation. If the
mechanism is according to presently proposed sequence then it should be possible to sepa-
rate the mechanism in to steps. It is proposed to investigate the mechanism in such steps
with use of in situ measurements such as EXAFS, TEM-EELS or surface studies such as
XPS. For the investigation a Cu/ZnO catalyst with a low ZnO content should be prepared to
have high dispersion of ZnO thus not diluting the results with bulk properties of ZnO. The
mechanism could be investigated in steps, ﬁrst by treating the catalyst with a CO2/H2 gas
mixture, measuring for formate. Then treating with methanol, measuring for zinc hydroxide
on the surface and methyl formate in gas phase by MS. Then treatment of the catalyst with
hydrogen and detection for metallic zinc. Lastly, the reduced form of the catalyst can be
treated with CO2/H2 gas mixture and detect for formate species on the surface to have a
complete catalytic cycle.
Regarding the mechanism not only experiments, but also DFT calculations could be desirable
to investigate the phenomena of auto catalysis. Not only to investigate the mechanism but

6.2.1
Setup
The activity measurements performed during this experimental work, were performed on a
high-pressure ﬂow reactor setup. The setup is built to accommodate measurements at up
to 100 bars of pressure as well as high temperatures. The reaction takes place in a tubular
quartz reactor (inner diameter: 8 mm, outer diameter: 10 mm, length: 1545 mm) to avoid
catalytic eﬀects from the reactor walls, though this should be of small inﬂuence on catalytic
measurements such as in present work. The quarts reactor is inserted into a pressure shell
made of a TP347 stainless steel tube (inner diameter: 22 mm, outer diameter: 38 mm). The
shell side of the reactor has a pressure control system consisting of two thermal mass ﬂow
pressure controllers (MFPC) from Brooks Instruments, model 5866, which delivers N2, to
the shell side of the reactor, as to not have a to large pressure gradient across the quartz
tube. The quartz reactor is placed horizontally in an Entech tube oven with three separate
controlled electrical heating elements, which produce an isothermal zone (±5 K) of app. 50
cm. The reactor temperature is monitored by K-type thermocouples (±2.2) placed inside
two thermopockets, which is inside the void between the quartz tube and the pressure shell
touching the external wall of the glass tube. It is possible to use up to four diﬀerent feed
gasses, which are pre-mixed before entering the reactor. The ﬂow-rates of the feed gases are
controlled by high-pressure digital mass ﬂow controllers (MFC) from Brooks Instruments,
model 5850S. Additionally, it is possible to feed liquid reactant, by use of a GILSON 305
HPLC pump, pumping the liquid through an evaporator before mixing with the gas feeds.
The gas system is pressurized, so the pressure in the setup is controlled by pneumatic pressure
valve (Baumann 5100) placed after the reactor and monitored by a diﬀerential pressure
transducer from Yokogawa, model DPharp EJX. The setup is shown in Figure 6.2.
It can be seen from the schematic, that the product is sent on further to the product analysis,
after passing the reduction valve, which reduce to atmospheric pressure. To make sure no
product is condensed on the way to the product analysis, the tubing, which is 1/8 inch diam-

Co-feeding Ethanol
For the initial measurements a concentration of app. 2 % ethanol was desired, since this con-

investigations. This was achieved by pumping 0.0320 mL/min liquid ethanol to the evap-

investigation of activity vs. ethanol concentration, the ethanol concentration was boosted by
an initial inﬂow of ethanol to the evaporator, after which the feed of ethanol to the evapo-

ethanol in the evaporator decreased. All experiments were performed at 600 NmL/min with
a catalyst loading of 0.0452-0.0453 g of catalyst, except for the very ﬁrst measurement, which
had 0.0220 g of catalyst. All the space velocities were in the region, where a promoting eﬀect
had been observed.

N2O-RFC
For the measurement of the number of reduced sites, N2O Reactive Frontal Chromatography
(RFC) is used. The measurements are performed in an autosorb iQ2 automated gas sorption
analyzer from Quantachrome Instruments. The ﬂow of the gasses is controlled by a single
needle valve, thus having the same setting for all the gasses. The chemstation is controlled
by the software ASiQwin, which makes it possible to make a program, choosing temperature,
heating rate, pressure and gas type. At the outlet of the chemstation a mass spectrometer is
mounted, which is a HIDEN ANALYTICAL QGA gas analysis system, and is controlled by
the software MASsoft 7, which is used to trace the outlet gas concentrations. The program
used for the N2O-RFC consisted generally of the steps:
• Flow 5% H2 in N2 for 15 min.
• Ramp temperature to 250 °C with 2 °C/min under ﬂow of 5% H2 in N2.
• Hold temperature and ﬂow 120 min.
• Change gas to helium, ﬂow for 20 min.
• Force cool to 60 °C.
• Flow helium for 30 min.
• Change gas to 1% N2O in He, and ﬂow for 40 min.
Between the gas changes, the setup purged the system. The ﬂowrate of the reduction gas
(5% H2 in N2) was measured to be 19 NmL/min and the ﬂow of 1% N2O in He was 19.3
NmL/min at the setting of the needle valve measured with an Agilent Technologies ADM2000
Universal Gas Flowmeter. In this preparation the reduction time was 2 h instead of 1 h as
in the activity measurements to better ensure full reduction. This is because the ﬂow during
reduction is below 1/5th of that in the activity measurements, and the catalyst will not
subsequently be exposed to high pressure syngas. Thus, the reduction time was prolonged
so that full reduction was achieved, which was monitored by looking on the H2 pressure

Project Description
Project Background:
The methanol synthesis is one of the largest processes within the chemical industry in modern
time. In the process methanol is produced from synthesis gas (CO2 /CO/H2) at pressures of
50-100 bar and temperatures of 200-300°C. Because of the high energy density of methanol
the process is seen as a possible way of converting emitted CO2 into methanol utilizing excess
sustCu/ZnO/Al2O3 catalysts are utilized. The main path for methanol formation is reaction
from CO2 (9.1.1). Furthermore, the water gas shift reaction (9.1.2) also takes place in the
reactor.
CO2 + 3 H2 ⇆CH3OH + H2O
(9.1.1)
CO + H2O ⇆CO2 + H2
(9.1.2)
Even though the methanol synthesis has been made on copper catalysts for over half a
century and despite the large scale of the industrial production the process is still a major
research ﬁeld with major questions still imperfectly understood. A major open question is

Calibration of GC w.r.t. methanol. For the plastic bag calibration of methanol was
used three small plastic bags (app. 10 L) and four large bags (>20 L). The small bags were
washed three times by pumping in air, until it looked "ﬂuﬀy", after which the air was pumped
out again. Similarly were done for the large bags, but the procedure were done twice instead
of thrice. The nitrogen ﬂow was then measured by use of a bubble ﬂowmeter, and a timer.
The volumetric ﬂow was found to be 1075.79 ccm/min, at 24.13°C as an average of multiple
measurements with small variation. The small bags were ﬁlled with nitrogen for 9 min, while
the large bags were ﬁlled with nitrogen for 18 min. This gave the calibration concentrations
given in Table 9.1.
