Abstract 
 
 
The performance of alternative configurations for large-scale ammonia heat pumps in district 
heating applications is investigated in this thesis. The focus is on comparing different 
configurations using the coefficient of performance (COP) Lorenz, which considers the 
temperature gradient in the heat pump system. The objective is to determine the most 
efficient and effective design for optimizing the performance of ammonia heat pumps in 
district heating scenarios. Configurations No. 1 and 4 are excluded from the research due to 
their condensation and evaporation temperatures falling outside the compressors' operating 
envelopes. Configuration No. 1, with a single compression stage, is unsuitable for efficient 
operation under the given temperature conditions (low temperatures conditions). 
Configuration No. 4, comprising two heat pumps connected in series with one compression 
stage each, has limitations in capacity and performance characteristics, making it 
inappropriate for the specific operating conditions. These exclusions highlight the importance 
of proper compressor selection and system design to ensure compatibility and optimal 
performance. Among the remaining configurations, Configuration No. 6 consistently exhibits 
the highest COP values, indicating superior performance in low-temperature operating 
conditions. The study analyses the impact of temperature differentials, load sharing ratios, 
and pinch temperature differences on the performance and cost-effectiveness of the heat 
pump configurations. It is observed that a lower return temperature and a larger temperature 
difference between the supply and return temperatures lead to improved performance and 
reduced heat costs. 
Furthermore, Configuration No. 3, incorporating a "low stage desuperheater" after the low-
pressure stage, shows promising results in enhancing system performance. The 
desuperheater utilizes waste heat after the low-pressure stage to support the main heat 
exchangers, reducing workload and improving efficiency. The comparison between using air 
and water as heat sources reveals that water as a heat source offers better thermal 
properties, resulting in higher COP values. Configuration No. 8 is identified as a cost-effective 
option for high external temperatures when operated with a high-temperature source and 
low return as the heat source. 
 
In conclusion, this research emphasizes the significance of appropriate compressor selection, 
system design optimization, and consideration of operating conditions in maximizing the 
performance and cost-effectiveness of large-scale ammonia heat pumps in district heating 
applications. The findings provide valuable insights for engineers and researchers working in 
the field of heat pump technology, contributing to the development of sustainable and 
efficient district heating systems.

2.1. Embracing Natural Refrigerants: ASHRAE's Path to Sustainable HVAC Solutions: The 
potential of ammonia as a refrigerant  
 
American Society of Heating, Refrigerating and Air-Conditioning Engineers, ‘’ASHRAE’’ tries to 
keep the environment safe by using refrigerants like ammonia, air, carbon dioxide, water, and 
hydrocarbons in refrigerators. They are learning and sharing better ways to use these things. 
Some ASHRAE teams are trying really hard to make refrigeration and air conditioning 
technology grow even more through research, sharing information, making rules, and giving 
suggestions. They are also aiming to use natural refrigerants in the safest way possible. 
ASHRAE is encouraging the use of natural refrigerants in systems to protect the environment 
instead of using harmful ones. They want the new systems to be secure, affordable, and work 
well. Refrigerants serve a crucial function in society through their deployment in a range of 
applications, including but not limited to food preservation, ice production, creation of 
optimal conditions for human comfort and productivity, and industrial operations support. As 
global economies continue to expand, the usage of refrigeration and air conditioning systems 
has correspondingly increased, thus resulting in the proliferation of refrigerant production 
and a heightened possibility of refrigerant emissions into the environment. There is mounting 
interest in natural refrigerants as a means of curbing greenhouse gas emissions. The category 
of natural refrigerants comprises ammonia, carbon dioxide, hydrocarbons, water, and air. The 
prudent selection of a refrigerant for a specific application necessitates a meticulous 
evaluation of various aspects, including but not limited to the initial investment, operational 
expenses, equipment dimensions and placement, operational temperatures and pressures, 
personnel competence, as well as local, national, and global regulations. The ASHRAE, an 
authoritative body in the realm of refrigeration and air conditioning, boasts of a global 
presence and proficiency. Its renowned members possess exemplary credentials that position 
them aptly to aid in the advancement of natural refrigerants. Natural refrigerants refer to a 
diverse group of organic and inorganic substances that possess the potential to be employed 
in diverse refrigeration and air conditioning system deployments. In recent times, the 
utilization of natural refrigerants has experienced an upsurge in various application domains 
previously catered to by fluorocarbon refrigerants, attributable to the benefits that the 
former offer. Reducing the quantity of refrigerant in refrigeration systems is often attained 
through the implementation of design methodologies involving plate heat exchangers and 
dry expansion evaporators. Utilizing secondary coolants in indirect refrigeration systems 
presents a viable option for reducing the refrigerant charge. However, such alternatives 
==PAGE:13==
warrant a consideration of the environmental costs and potential decrease in system 
operating efficiency owing to the introduction of a secondary working fluid. Moreover, there 
exists a pressing requirement to progress the enhancement of constituent elements of 
systems that possess the capability to realize energy and performance efficiencies at a 
reasonable cost. Ammonia is a pivotal natural refrigerant owing to its extensive and enduring 
utilization in the preservation and processing of food and beverages, as well as its 
progressively increasing uptake in various domains such as HVAC chillers, process cooling and 
air conditioning, thermal storage systems, district cooling systems, supermarkets, and 
convenience stores. The substance under consideration exhibits an Ozone Depletion Potential 
(ODP) and Global Warming Potential (GWP) of zero. Furthermore, it displays remarkable 
energy performance in refrigeration systems owing to its inherent attributes, superior 
thermodynamic properties, and elevated heat transfer coefficients. This substance can be 
detected by olfaction, as well as through the utilization of diverse electrochemical and 
electronic sensors. Its accessibility is characterized by a relatively economical cost. The 
paramount drawback associated with the use of ammonia is its potential toxicity upon 
exposure to higher concentrations. However, this perilous outcome is mitigated by the 
intense odor of the compound, which serves as a warning mechanism, safeguarding human 
health and safety through its timely detection. According to the ASHRAE Standard 34 
classification, ammonia is deemed to possess "lower flammability" in air provided its 
concentration remains within the range of 16% to 28% (by weight) and is known to be 
incompatible with copper and its alloys. (ASHRAE January 28, 2009). 
Ammonia refrigerating systems are subjected to legal regulatory frameworks and standards

2.2. Advantages of Ammonia as a refrigerant for High-Performance Heat Pump Systems 
Ammonia heat pumps present a comparatively more efficient and space-saving alternative to 
alternative refrigerants, namely R407C, R134a, and commonly employed HFCs, with regards 
to heating and cooling applications. The operational efficacy of ammonia, coupled with its 
environmentally friendly attributes as a working medium with nil ozone depletion potential 
(ODP) and global warming potential (GWP), renders it an alluring option for a vast gamut of 
practical implementations. Utilizing natural working fluids, such as ammonia, hydrocarbons, 
and carbon dioxide, within heat pump systems presents an ecologically sound and enduring 
resolution to the utilization of HFCs. Ammonia has been validated as a proficient and energy-
efficient substitute for the purposes of heating and cooling of non-residential buildings along 
with district heating and cooling systems. (Stene, Jørn, 2008)

Notwithstanding, the toxicity of the substance necessitates the implementation of stringent 
safety protocols and standards. The optimal performance of ammonia heat pump systems is 
highly dependent on the appropriate design and dimensioning of the system, as well as the 
careful selection of system components. The utilization of natural working fluids, such as 
ammonia, within heat pump systems presents a viable and auspicious route towards 
accomplishing sustainable heating and cooling alternatives. The utilization of ammonia as a 
working fluid is characterized by a lower mass flow rate, which subsequently decreases the 
requisite dimensions of pipelines and valves. This consequential effect culminates in attaining 
a relatively small compressor volume when compared to other frequently employed media. 
The utilization of ammonia refrigerating and heat pump systems has been impeded by 
stringent standards and regulations regarding their construction and operation in numerous 
countries. Nonetheless, in the context of Norway, ammonia has emerged as a widely 
employed working fluid in medium- and large-capacity heat pumps. The ongoing transition 
towards sustainable and eco-friendly practices worldwide has presented an appealing 
prospect for utilizing ammonia heat pump systems as a viable solution for regulating 
temperatures in non-residential structures as well as district heating and cooling systems. The 
use of ammonia heat pumps has been shown to exhibit superior performance when 
compared to R407C and R134a systems, especially when administered under identical 
operating conditions. This can be attributed to their advantageous thermophysical 
properties, resulting in a higher coefficient of performance (COP). The aforementioned 
characteristics encompass an amplified saturation temperature and pressure curve, 
enhanced heat transfer attributes, as well as elevated compressor efficiencies. In light of the 
evidence, it has been observed that ammonia compressors exhibit superior performance 
when compared to HFC compressors at relatively low-pressure ratios. The elevated specific 
enthalpy of vaporization of ammonia yields a diminished mass flow rate, leading to a

high specific enthalpy of evaporation demonstrated by ammonia leads to a reduction in mass

  PURPOSE AND AIMS OF THE PROJECT 
 
3.1. Background and motivation 
The heating sector plays a considerable role in contributing to the emission of greenhouse 
gases on a global scale. In order to attain the goals pertaining to climate change, it is 
imperative to explore approaches that facilitate the reduction of carbon emissions within the 
industry. An alternative solution to address this issue involves the utilization of large-scale 
heat pumps. In such systems, heat is transferred from a low-temperature source to a high-
temperature load, and the entire process is driven by electricity. This approach can be 
considered a low-carbon option that can replace conventional heating systems heavily reliant 
on fossil fuels. Such large-scale heat pumps fall under the category of HVAC equipment. 
Conventional ammonia heat pump configurations, which are widely employed in industrial 
refrigeration facilities, may not be appropriate for deployment in district heating heat pump 
systems. Consequently, a necessity arises to conduct a thorough investigation regarding 
alternative configurations, which exhibit enhanced efficiency and effectiveness in the context 
of district heating implementations. The goal of this project is to investigate the feasibility of 
various configurations and heat sources for large scale ammonia heat pumps in district 
heating systems.  
By studying and evaluating alternative configurations and heat sources, this project aims to: 
• Improve the effectiveness and efficiency of massive ammonia heat pumps for district 
heating. 
• Reduce the carbon emissions associated with the heating industry. 
• Consider the possibilities of different heat sources, such as ambient air, water or brine 
to enhance the effectiveness and functionality of heat pumps. 
This research is essential because it might contribute to the creation of district heating heat 
pumps that are more effective and efficient, helping to fulfil climate change goals and 
decarbonize the heating industry. Additionally, it may provide light on how various heat 
sources are used, which may have a big influence on how well heat pumps work. 
 
3.2. Purpose of the project 
In this study, large-scale heat pumps are used to provide district heating demand for 
Copenhagen city. Refrigerant used is ammonia due to its environmentally friendly features 
and high efficiency compared to its counterparts of Freon. The purpose of this project is to 
investigate and analyse different plant configurations and heat sources for large scale 
ammonia heat pumps for district heating. Specifically, the project aims to: 
1. Identify potential substitute system designs for large-scale ammonia heat pumps that 
are better suited for district heating applications. 
==PAGE:18==
2. Employ numerical modelling tools to examine the effectiveness and performance of 
various setups. 
3. Examine how various heat sources affect the functionality and effectiveness of the 
heat pumps. 
4. To find the best design for district heating applications, compare the effectiveness and 
efficiency of various configurations and heat sources. 
5. Based on the study's findings, offer suggestions for the development and use of large-
scale ammonia heat pumps for district heating. 
6. Comparison of different configurations in terms of economic cost and selection of the 
optimal. 
 
3.4. Problem statement  
District heating systems are considered to have a pivotal role in furnishing sustainable and 
efficient heating options for large applications. The utilization of ammonia heat pumps 
presents compelling prospects for district heating due to their capacity to offer elevated levels 
of energy efficiency while concurrently reducing adverse environmental effects. There exists 
a necessity to investigate alternate arrangements that may augment the efficacy and output

METHODOLOGY AND MATERIALS 
The present research endeavors to undertake a quantitative methodology to examine the 
performance and efficacy of diverse setups for extensive ammonia heat pumps in district 
heating contexts. The study will encompass a synthesis of theoretical scrutiny, computational 
simulation, and comparative appraisal. 
• Identification of possible alternative system configurations for large-scale ammonia 
heat pumps in district heating applications, such as configurations with flooded 
evaporators, cycles with parallel evaporators, or serial evaporators. 
• Comparison of possible alternative configurations among themselves to determine 
the best from a technical, thermodynamic, and economic point of view. 
• Analysis of different configurations based on numerical modeling using simulation 
software such as EES (HP configurations) to predict the performance and efficiency of 
the different configurations. 
• Analysis of different heat sources used in different system configurations, including 
ambient air, water, brine, and low-grade heat sources. 
• Comparison of the performance and efficiency of different configurations and heat

Method for economic analysis 
Total capital investment (TCI) is calculated based on the approach from the book: Tsatsaronis, 
George, Michael J. Moran, Adrian Bejan, and Michael Moran. 1996. Thermal Design and 
Optimization John Wiley. The TCI is the sum of the direct costs, indirect costs, and outlays. 
TCI = DC + IC + outlays 
The direct cost is the sum of "onsite" and "offsite" costs. The direct onsite cost is the sum of 
the purchased equipment cost (PEC) of the main components (the compressor, motor, and 
heat exchangers) and the subcomponents (the pumps, valves, and vessels). The cost of the 
main components depends on the calculated size (volume flow rate, power rate, area) and is 
determined by cost correlations. The cost of sub-components is given as a percentage of the 
main component's cost. Furthermore, the direct costs cover the installation cost, piping, 
instrumentation, controls, electrical equipment, and materials. The cost of these 
contributions is given as a percentage of the PEC. The direct offsite cost is the cost associated 
with the connection of district heating, the connection of a heat source, the power 
connection, and the building. The user can give a value for each contribution in absolute cost. 
The indirect cost covers engineering, consulting, the contractor's profit, and costs related to 
the construction site. The cost is a user input given as a percentage of the direct cost. The 
outlays are the cost of startup costs, working capital, licensing, research and development, 
and an allowance for founds during construction. The outlays are given as a percentage of 
direct costs plus indirect costs. The cost correlations used to calculate the cost of the plate 
heat exchangers, compressor units, and electrical drive are based on catalog prices. If a 
discount on these prices is excepted, a percentage can be provided here. The cost of air 
evaporators is based on user input. The area of one air evaporator and the cost of this module 
must be given. In the detailed results of each configuration, the required number of modules 
is shown. The excess area in percentages is also shown.

4.4.1. Coefficient of Performance (COP): 
COP (Coefficient of Performance) is a measure of efficiency. The efficiency of any machine or 
system can be calculated as the ratio of the amount of work done by the machine to the 
amount of work given to the machine. In the case of a heat pump, its efficiency is the ratio of 
useful heat energy produced to electrical energy consumption. For example, COP of 2.5 
means that the heat pump supplies 2.5 times as much heat energy to the system as it 
consumes in electrical energy. COP measures how efficient a heat pump is at turning electrical 
energy into usable heat. It doesn’t do this by creating energy or being more than 100% 
efficient. It does it by using heat energy already present in the outside air, ground or water.

4.4.2. Lorenz efficiency 𝜼_lorenz and Lorenz COP: 
The reverse Carnot cycle is one of the most famous ideal thermal cycles applied in the field of 
refrigeration and achieves the best performance coefficient. It consists of two isothermal 
processes and two adiabatic processes in which the heat source as well as the heat sink have 
constant temperatures. Carnot process constitutes a theoretical cycle that necessitated 
engineers and chemists to conceptualize its practical realization. In order to achieve diverse 
levels of saturation temperature, it is necessary to alter the pressure of the fluid, which can 
be accomplished through either vapor compression or by modifying the chemical 
composition. The effective temperature glide of the external flows as they traverse the heat 
exchangers renders the Carnot cycle a suboptimal choice in terms of efficiency. Accordingly, 
the Lorenz cycle, which incorporates adapted temperature glides in both heat exchangers, 
emerges as a more efficient alternative. This is attributed to the reduced temperature 
difference between the heat exchangers, hence minimizing the entropy generation during 
heat transfer. The Carnot cycle COP can be used conveniently, if no information about the 
external temperature glides is available. (Zehnder, 2004). 
The primary reason behind using the Lorenz cycle in this context is because it is theoretically 
the most efficient cycle for managing heat transfer under variable temperature heat sources, 
which are common in real-world applications like air conditioning, refrigeration, and heat 
pump systems. It's particularly useful when aiming to improve the system's energy efficiency, 
reduce irreversible losses, and increase the system's COP. It consists of four reversible 
processes: adiabatic compression, polytropic heat rejection, isentropic expansion, and 
polytropic heat absorption. Essentially, the Lorenz cycle can be thought of as the integration 
==PAGE:27==
of many Carnot cycles, which are the most efficient cycles under constant temperature 
conditions. ( Xiang Cao, et al.2017). 
The reasons and advantages of utilizing the Lorenz cycle, based on ( Xiang Cao, et al.2017): 
Efficiency: The Lorenz cycle is considered the most efficient under variable temperature heat 
sources. In such scenarios, the Lorenz cycle can match the coefficient of performance (COP) 
of an equivalent Carnot cycle at the mean temperatures of heat sources. The goal is to 
maximize efficiency and minimize energy losses during heat transfer. Reduce irreversible 
losses: The paper suggests using a stepped pressure cycle approach with pure refrigerants to 
approximate the Lorenz cycle. The stepped pressure cycle design involves the refrigerant 
flowing through a series of heat transfer units at varying pressures, which can help reduce the 
irreversible losses incurred during the heat exchange process. Performance improvement: 
The numerical analysis in the study showed that by approximating the Lorenz cycle with a 
stepped pressure approach, significant improvements in performance could be achieved. 
Specifically, for a dual-step suction and discharge pressure cycle using R32, an 11.5% cooling 
COP improvement was achieved for residential air conditioners. A dual-step discharge 
pressure cycle using R134a led to a 9.8% heating COP increase for heat pump water 
heaters.Pilot system testing: The paper also conducted preliminary testing of a pilot system. 
Compared to a single cycle system, this system achieved a 12.3% and 18.7% COP increase by 
applying dual and triple subcycles, respectively. ( Xiang Cao, et al.2017).

5.5. Economy analysis and results  
  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:47==
This section aims to outline and compare the operating and heat costs related to the top-
performing setups. ‘’ Cost of heat including investment: The production cost pr MWh heat 
given by the yearly heat expenses divided by the yearly heat production’’. The various 
operating circumstances will be taken into consideration as part of a thorough economic 
study. Finding out how variations in supply and return temperatures affect the related 
expenses is the goal. Additionally, the effect of changes in the ambient temperature on 
various heating costs and operating costs will be carefully examined. This analysis seeks to 
determine the best configuration from a performance and financial point of view, especially 
in low-temperature situations. This critical evaluation will make it easier to comprehend how 
operational circumstances and economic repercussions interact, offering helpful insights for 
heat pump system optimization. The input parameters in this analysis will be approved 
according to the parameters specified in the program, the details of which are explained in 
the "Methods for economic analysis" section. 
This analysis will be structured around three scenarios, each exploring the impacts of altering

CHAPTER 7 
 
Discussion 
The study found that configurations No. 1 and 4 were excluded from comparisons due to their 
condensation and evaporation temperatures falling outside the compressors' operating 
envelopes. The compressors in these configurations were designed with one compression 
stage, while configuration No. 4 had two heat pumps connected with one compression stage. 
These configurations were deemed unsuitable for handling specific temperature ranges due 
to their design specifications. Configuration No. 1 has only one compression stage, making it 
unsuitable for efficient or effective operation at the given condensation and evaporation 
temperatures. Additionally, configuration No. 4, with two heat pumps connected in series and 
one compression stage for each, has limitations in capacity, temperature range, and 
performance characteristics, making it unsuitable for those specific operating conditions. The 
exclusion of these configurations emphasizes the importance of selecting appropriate 
compressors or heat pump components that can operate within the desired temperature 
ranges. It also emphasizes the need for careful system design and consideration of operating 
conditions to ensure compatibility and optimal performance of heat pump configurations. 
Additionally, the limitations observed in these configurations may necessitate exploring 
alternative compressor options or system designs to ensure compatibility with specific 
operating conditions. 
Heat pump designs are developed to function under various operating conditions, including 
low temperatures as low as -6°C or -10°C. It is crucial for the compressors to operate within 
their specified operating conditions. In this study, configuration No. 6, which involves two heat 
pumps connected in series, each operating with two compression stages and a "low stage 
desuperheater" after the first compression stage, was found to have the highest performance 
coefficient (COP) compared to other configurations in low-temperature operating conditions. 
Looking at Table No. 3, we can observe that the forward temperatures range from 70 to 80°C, 
while the return temperatures vary between 30 and 50°C. The inlet temperature is -6°C, which 
means the heat pump needs to do extra work to raise the temperature of the refrigerant 
(ammonia) to the desired level. As the ambient temperature decreases, the temperature 
difference between the heat source and the working fluid in the evaporator increases, making 
it more challenging for the heat pump to absorb heat from the surroundings. Consequently, 
this can reduce the overall heating capacity of the system. 
The load share in all cases is set at 50%, meaning the workload is equally distributed between 
the two connected heat pumps. Importantly, configuration No. 6 consistently achieves the 
highest COP values across all operating conditions, indicating its superior performance in low-
temperature scenarios. 
==PAGE:56==
Arranging the configurations based on their performance, we can see that configuration No. 
6 consistently ranks as the top performer, followed by configurations No. 5, 3, 2, 8, and 7, 
respectively. Design No. 6 is composed of two heat pumps connected in series so that each of 
them is composed of a heat pump corresponding to Design No. 3 configuration. In view of the 
design and the number 3, it is a developed and improved model of configuration number 2, 
so we notice that a “low stage desuperheater” was added after the low-pressure stage. 
The idea behind adding a "low stage desuperheater" in Configuration No. 3 '' one heat pump 
cycle ''was motivated by the observation that the temperature rises significantly after the low-
pressure stage. This excess heat can be effectively utilized to support the main heat 
exchangers following the high-pressure stage, which are responsible for fulfilling the heating 
requirements. By harnessing the waste heat generated after the low-pressure stage, the 
workload on the main heat exchangers can be reduced, enabling them to meet the required 
heating load more efficiently. 
By integrating the "low stage desuperheater" into Configuration No. 3, the system takes 
advantage of the wasted heat after the low-pressure stage, which would otherwise be lost. 
This design approach optimizes the utilization of heat exchangers, including the 
desuperheater, condenser, and subcooler, to effectively distribute the heating load and 
improve overall system performance. 
By cooling down the superheated vapor from the first compression stage, the "low stage 
desuperheater" in Configuration No. 3 provides several thermodynamic advantages: 
Reducing the work done by the compressor, so the compressor will work more efficiently, 
which leads to saving energy and extending the life span of the compressor. Therefore, this 
will lead to saving costs through maintenance and replacement expenses. In addition, 
improving energy efficiency leads to reducing energy consumption, which leads to lower 
operating costs. It will reduce operating costs due to reducing energy losses due to improving 
the performance of heat exchangers by better distributing the heating load across multiple 
heat exchangers, which ensures efficient heat transfer processes.as shown in Tables 2,3 and 4 
According to table 8 As the return temperature increases, the performance of all 
configurations tends to decrease, and the COP values for each configuration decrease 
gradually as the return temperature rises. The decrease in the return temperature of the 
district heating network has a positive impact on the heat pump system's performance. As the 
return temperature decreases, the difference between the supply and return temperatures 
will increase. This larger difference provides a higher temperature gradient for the heat 
transfer process and thus will allow more heat to be extracted from the external medium, 
which is the air in our case, which leads to an increase in the coefficient of performance, and 
on the other hand, with a decrease in the return temperature, it will increase the sub-cooling, 
that is, it will take place. Ammonia, "which is the refrigerant medium, is cooled below the 
condensing temperature before it enters the expansion valve. Thus, the temperature at which 
the refrigerant is condensed will decrease, and the temperature difference between the heat 
source and the refrigerant in the evaporator will increase, promoting more effective heat 
absorption. These effects contribute to an overall improvement in performance and a higher

Based on the (Jørgensen, P. H, et al. 2019)., Configuration No. 6 demonstrates the highest 
performance coefficient among the different heat pump configurations considered. In order 
to ensure optimal performance, all heat exchangers are designed with a pinch temperature 
difference of 2.5 K, and a load sharing ratio of 50% has been determined. However, it is

The optimization process, explained in the "optimization" section, involves precise calibration 
of the pinch temperature difference and finding the optimal load sharing ratio. It was 
discovered that Configuration No. 6 can achieve higher efficiency and lower cost when the 
load is shared by 30%. Additionally, Configuration No. 3 exhibits comparable or sometimes 
superior performance to Configuration No. 6 under specific circumstances, particularly in cold 
weather when the pinch temperature difference is adjusted solely for Configuration No. 3. 
However, it is important to note that if the design of both configurations were to have a pinch 
temperature difference as low as 0.1 K, Configuration No. 6 would remain unmatched in terms 
of performance, albeit at an increased cost. Such a design would result in larger heat 
exchangers, making it impractical for other configurations to compete, as it is not feasible to 
achieve a temperature difference below 0.1 K in practice to improve the performance of 
others alternative configurations. 
Nevertheless, it is worth exploring the possibility of achieving comparable performance to 
Configuration No. 6 at a lower cost in applications that do not require extremely small 
temperature differences. This research has already achieved such a feat by attaining 
performance levels equivalent to Configuration No. 6, but at a reduced cost. 
The results of this study showed that Configuration No. 8 exhibited superior performance and 
cost-effectiveness when operating under high external temperatures, specifically at 30°C, and 
with a low return temperature. Moreover, a comparison was made between using air and 
water as heat sources for the heat pump. The comparison revealed that when the heat pump 
operated with water as the heat source, it achieved a higher coefficient of performance (COP) 
compared to when it operated with air under the same input parameters. This disparity in 
performance can be attributed to several factors. Firstly, water as a heat source generally 
offers better thermal properties than air. Water has a higher heat capacity and thermal 
conductivity, allowing for more efficient heat transfer between the heat source and the 
working fluid (ammonia) in the evaporator. This enhanced heat transfer results in improved 
performance and higher COP values. Secondly, water as a heat source often provides more 
stable and consistent temperatures compared to air. This stability reduces the variability in 
the temperature difference between the heat source and the working fluid, contributing to 
more reliable and efficient heat transfer processes. In addition, the performance of heat 
pumps that use brine is superior to that of the air source at the same operating conditions, 
especially at low temperatures. 
In general, series-connected heat pump designs are superior to individual heat pumps, and in 
some cases the cost is lower, as is the case when operating at high outside temperatures.

Configuration No. 8, which has the highest performance and consists of two connected heat 
pumps, is at the same time cheaper than Configuration No. 1. The component consists of one 
heat pump and this is consistent with the research (Ommen, et al, (2015) who confirmed 
Connecting industrial heat pumps in series can result in an increase in coefficient of 
performance (COP) and a decrease in net present value (NPV) due to the effects of economy 
of scale. It can also expand the working domains of vapour compression heat pumps at high 
sink outlet temperatures. The technical and economical working domains were calculated for 
two serial connections, and both configurations resulted in an increased working domain for 
the investigated dual unit serial connections. However, it should be noted that connecting 
heat pumps in series may reduce the discharge temperature, which is of particular interest 
when assessing R717 heat pumps. 
Looking at the results, it can be seen that with an increase in the return temperature, the heat 
cost increases, that is, with an increase in the difference between the return temperature and 
the supply, the cost decreases. The larger the temperature difference between the supply and 
return temperatures, the more work the heat pump needs to do, resulting in higher energy 
consumption and increased heat cost. On the other hand, the heat costs decrease when the 
external temperature “ambient” increases. In addition, the costs increase with the increase in 
the difference between the entry temperature of the evaporator and the exit temperature of 
the evaporator, i.e., ∆ T source. With a larger temperature difference, the heat pump can 
absorb more heat from the external medium, resulting in higher efficiency and reduced 
energy consumption. As a result, the heat costs decrease.  
When ∆T source is larger, it means that the working fluid entering the evaporator has a higher 
temperature compared to the desired exit temperature. In order to achieve the desired exit 
temperature, the heat pump needs to transfer more heat from the heat source to the working 
fluid. This requires the heat pump to work harder and consume more energy. 
In other words, a larger ∆T source indicates a greater thermal load that the heat pump needs 
to handle. The heat pump must extract a larger amount of heat from the heat source and 
transfer it to the working fluid in order to reach the desired exit temperature. This increased 
workload results in higher energy consumption by the heat pump, leading to higher heat 
costs.

CHAPTER 8 
 
Conclusion 
In this study, different configurations of ammonia-fired heat pumps for large-scale area 
heating were compared, where appropriate heat pump configurations were taken into 
account for the desired temperature ranges, and the importance of selecting compressors 
and heat pump components that could operate in specific operating conditions was 
concluded to ensure the best performance. add to that it has been explored the use of series-
connected heat pump configurations, as they have demonstrated superior performance and 
cost-effectiveness, particularly at high external temperatures. Such configurations can 
leverage the benefits of economy of scale and expand the working domains of heat pumps. 
It has been improved and optimized system design by integrating features such as a "low 
stage desuperheater" to effectively utilize waste heat and improve overall energy efficiency. 
This can lead to reduced compressor workload, lower energy consumption, and cost savings. 
The role of the return temperature and its features have been concluded because of its impact 
on the performance coefficient. Therefore, it is recommended to implement strategies to 
reduce the return temperature, such as improving heat exchangers and designing optimal 
configurations. This study developed an encouraging methodology to compare between 
performance and cost when designing heat pump configurations. While some configurations 
may have higher performance, they come with increased costs. Therefore, it is recommended 
to conduct a cost analysis to determine the most cost-effective configuration that meets the 
required performance criteria by continuing to investigate the pinch temperature difference 
and load-sharing distribution of two-pump heat pumps. Configurations were compared based 
on the coefficient of performance (COP) Lorenz, as this type of heat pump operates with a 
significant temperature gradient. The COP Lorenz is a performance metric that takes into 
account the temperature difference between the heat source and the heat sink, allowing for 
a more accurate evaluation of the heat pump's efficiency. By using COP Lorenz as the 
comparison metric, the study aimed to assess the performance of different configurations 
under varying temperature conditions and determine the most efficient and effective design 
for large-scale ammonia heat pumps in district heating applications. It was also found, in some 
cases, that the heat source has an important role in evaluating the performance of heat 
pumps, as it was concluded that in some cases, heat pumps that operate with water as a heat 
source perform better and cost less than those that use air as a heat source. It was also 
concluded that the heat source The saline solution is very effective at temperatures below 
zero degrees Celsius.
