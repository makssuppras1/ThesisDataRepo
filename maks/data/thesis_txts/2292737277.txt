Abstract
In this MSc. thesis colloidal inks containing YSZ particles are processed and tested in a PixDro
LP 50 3D inkjet printer with an installed Spectra SL-128 dop-on-demand printhead. Diﬀerent
inks are tested, all containing 20 wt.% YSZ and a PVP K15 dispersant. The solvents are water
and EtOH in all inks but in order to reach the right viscosity of the inks either a PVP binder
polymer or PPG-400/PEG-400 solvents have been added.
None of the inks tested are printed satisfactorily. In particular a non-permanent clogging of
the printhead nozzles is observed, which hinders the ejection of droplets but it does not make
the printhead unsuable.
The reason why the inks are not printed is not clear.
It could be
related to vapour bubbles present in the nozzle chambers, which blocks the pumping mechanism
of the prinhead. It is also possible that altering the voltage pulse, used to generate the pressure
wave ejecting the droplets, could improve the print-ability. The results also suggests that using
high molecular weight PVP binders (grade K30 or K90) to increase the ink viscosity is a path
which should not be pursued; instead high viscosity solvents should be part of the solvent system.
Characterisation of the inks indicate that they, according to the printhead speciﬁcations,
ought to be print-able. This is based on measurements of the viscosity, surface tension and
PSD in the ink. In addition the sedimentation and agglomeration stability of some of the inks
have been tested and it is concluded to be suﬃciently high. The main issue in terms of the
characterisation of the inks is, that it is done during vastly diﬀerent conditions than those
taking place in a inkjet nozzle during droplet formation and ejection. Especially the shear rates
and the time scales during printing cannot be ”simulated” when characterising the inks.

Chapter 1
Introduction and Motivation
1.1
Energy conversion technologies
The conversion of energy from one form to another with the option to store the energy sub-
sequently is an attractive way of handling excess electricity from intermittent energy sources,
and a possible scenario in future energy systems [1]. The Danish energy system, where the goal
is to be fossil free by 2050 [2], is most likely going to rely on some kind of energy storage in
the future as the share of renewables continue to increase. The driver in the transition of the
Danish energy system has so far been wind energy [3], and the large ﬂuctuations in electricity
production is already an issue with approx. 40 % of the annual electricity consumption produced
by wind turbines. How to handle the electricity, produced during hours of excess production,
is an essential factor to consider as the ﬂuctuations in the electricity is going to increase with
more renewable energy in the energy system. Energy conversion technologies oﬀer a possible
way of handling the excess electricity so it can be stored temporarily and possibly used during
hours of shortage in the electricity production - thus reducing the dependence on forced export
or import of electricity.
Fuels for transportation is currently very dependent on fossil oil. This is not sustainable and
alternative fuel sources are therefore likely to gradually replace oil within the current century.
Production of synthesised fuels such as methanol or DME is a possible scenario for the future
transportation system. The precursors for the synthesis of these fuels can be produced from re-
newable energy sources by means of energy conversion technologies such as electrolysis cells [4].
Batteries are another option, but they have some drawbacks such as the volumetric storage ca-
pacity which is, even though large improvements have been made, at least an order of magnitude
lower than gasoline [5]. In comparison liquid fuels such as methanol oﬀer a volumetric energy
density, which is approx. half the volumetric energy density of gasoline [6], and additionally
there is already an infrastructure based on liquid fuels.
Solid oxide cells (SOCs) have the potential to be used as energy conversion technologies
in an energy system, which is based increasingly on intermittent, renewable energy sources
such as solar and wind energy that produce electricity.
The solid oxide fuel cell (SOFC) is
an electrochemical cell, which produces electricity with heat and water (and possibly CO2) as
by-products.
A schematic diagram of a SOC operating in fuel cell mode is shown in ﬁgure
1.1a [7]. The input in a SOFC is H2 (and possibly CO) in combination with O2 from the air.
The solid oxide electrolysis cell (SOEC) operates in reverse compared to the SOFC and can
convert electricity and water (in combination with CO2) into H2 and O2 (or if CO2 is used,

1.2
Inkjet printing
The SOCs have so far been suﬀering from degradation and lifetime issues [11] and inkjet printing
is a processing technology, which it is envisioned can improve the SOC lifetime. Inkjet printing
technology allows for digitally controlled deposition of small droplets onto a substrate. The
droplet size is typically on the order of 10-100 µm [12], which allows for very selective deposition
and formation of complex structures. Today the inkjet technology is mainly known for its abil-
ity to print graphical outputs from computers and hence structures in 2D but the technology
was originally developed for commercial use in the 1970-1980s as a marking and coding tool
for products. The inkjet technology used for this purpose was continous inkjet (CIJ) printing,
but from the mid 1980s and onwards drop-on-Demand (DOD) inkjet printing was developed
and commercialised for individual consumers into what is known as inkjet printers for graphics
outputs [13]. The print head of a DOD inkjet printer consists of hundreds of nozzles often placed
in series. Each nozzle contains a small opening, approximately the size of a droplet, from where
the ink droplets are formed and ejected, which schematically is shown in ﬁgure 1.2 [14]. In the
jetting chamber a transducer is used to convert an electrical signal into a pressure wave, which

When one speaks of an ink for printing it is implicitly understood by most people that an
ink is a colored liquid or slightly more elaborated a solvent containing dyes or pigments, which
main function is to print speciﬁc structures in 2D with a given color.
However, during the
last approx. 15 years a growing interest for a number of unconventional applications of inkjet
printing have emerged. Using colloidal suspensions thin layers of inorganic material intended
for SOCs have been printed [15–17]. Other applications include printing of conducting polymers
for electronics [18], printing of biological material and electronic conducting layers for biosen-
sors [19], printing of suspended living cells with micrometer resolution [20] and selective printing
of thin-ﬁlm metal layers for capacitive humidity sensors and resistive temperature sensors [21].
It has thus become apparent that inkjet technology can be used for much more than graphics
outputs. It is in fact possible to alter the composition of the ink and use inks containing sub-
micron particles to print advanced materials in complex structures with a very high resolution,
where the drop to drop spacing is on the order of 100 µm.
1.3
Inkjet printing and Solid Oxide Cells
The three main parts constituting the SOCs, the cathode, the anode and the electrolyte, are
all composed of ceramic and metal materials (or solid oxide materials). Inkjet printing of the
diﬀerent layers has become an interesting way of processing these layers sequentially. The main
interest has been on processing very thin and dense layers that can be produced with a thickness
approaching 1 µm. These are attractive as electrolytes because a thinner electrolyte can enhance
the performance of SOCs by decreasing the ohmic resistance of the electrolyte and hence the
losses in the cell. When using traditional ceramic processing methods, such as tape casting,
it has proven diﬃcult to make the electrolyte thinner than approx. 10 µm. Other attracting
features are the high resolution that can be obtained and the fact that the inkjet technology
3

1.4 Goal and scope of this project
already has a high level of maturity. It is therefore the speciﬁc inks that need to be developed,
and not so much the deposition technology itself.
In this project a 3D printer will be used to print thin, dense ceramic layers. The fact that
it is a 3D printer means that the print head can move vertically as well as in the horizontal
plane, which allows for the deposition of 3D structures such as monoliths. The reason why
this is attractive is that with the current fabrication technology for SOCs, stacks are produced
by pressing individual layers of cells and interconnects together under operating temperatures,
ranging from approx. 600-900 ◦C, in order to produce electrical contact. The problem with this
method is that the operating temperature can vary considerably within the cells during this pro-
cess, which will induce thermal stresses in the formed stack. The result can be loss of electrical
contact and fractures within the cells, which will aﬀect performance and the life time of the cells.
A clear motivation for introducing 3D inkjet printing in the ﬁeld of ceramic processing is
therefore the envision, that all layers of the cells can be produced by a 3D printer by formu-
lating diﬀerent printable inks. The interconnects, which contain complex 3D structures, can
also be printed with a 3D inkjet printer. Now proposing the possibility of printing of an entire
SOC stack, it becomes apparent that the thick mechanical anode support layer in each cell,
which is necessary to avoid breakage of the individual cells during handling, can be removed. In
addition a co-sintering of the entire SOC stack could also reduce the problem related to the loss
of electrical contact, which was described. Moreover, inkjet printing can simplify and automate
the processing signiﬁcantly as several diﬀerent processing technologies such as screen printing,
tape casting and slip casting, have been used traditionally for the diﬀerent cell layers.
Ultimately an improved processing method using inkjet printing can reduce the cost per kWh
of electricity produced from a SOFC (or the cost per kWh to convert electricity into storable gases

2.1
Printing and print-ability of inks
Inkjet printing is a deposition method, which can be used to deposit droplets of pL volume se-
lectively onto a substrate. Conventionally, the inks are composed of pigments or dyes dispersed
in a solvent. In regards to ceramics such as SOCs the ink is a colloidal suspension 1 of ceramic
particles dispersed in a solvent along with some additives.
2.1.1
The diﬀerent inkjet printing technologies
Two inkjet printing technologies exist and these are, as stated in section 1.2, continous inkjet
(CIJ) printing and drop-on-demand (DOD) inkjet printing. The main diﬀerence is, as the names
indicate, that CIJ printing produces a continuous stream of droplets, which is steered by deﬂec-
tor plates whereas DOD inkjet printing produces one droplet at a time [23]. The technologies
produce droplet sizes of 10 µm to 150 µm in diameter and DOD droplets are usually in the
lower part of the interval where CIJ droplets are in the upper part. In CIJ printing the ink
is deposited selectively, and when no ink is required the ink is deﬂected into a collector to be
recycled. The main problem with this is that recycled ink can be contaminated after exposure
to the environment and it is thus not a very economical process if the ink cannot be recycled.
Additionally, the spatial resolution is typically smaller due to the larger droplet sizes and the
range of ﬂuids that can be deposited is more limited.
DOD printing has therefore been the preferred technology for ceramic processing where

2.1.2
The theory of droplet ejection from a printhead
For a single droplet to be formed upon ejection from a nozzle several factors come into play.
The physical properties of the ink are important with the density, viscosity and surface tension
being the main parameters of importance [24]. In practice the two latter are the easiest to
make signiﬁcant adjustments to. Moreover, when dealing with colloidal inks the particle size
distribution (PSD) becomes a crucial parameter to avoid clogging of the nozzles during printing.
The general rule is that the maximum particle size (PS) should be less than 10 % of the nozzle
oriﬁce to avoid clogging and the optimal PS is less than 1-2 % of the nozzle oriﬁce.
The other important factor is the waveform and amplitude of the driving pulse signal, which
leads to generation of droplets. Generally, this signal is trapezoid shaped and can be adjusted by
changing the amplitude, ramp up time, ramp down time or the length of the signal at maximum

2.3
Dispersion of colloidal inks
A colloidal system with ceramic particles dispersed in a solvent along with some additives is a
common way of stabilising ceramic particles, which is used for many ceramic processing meth-

negligible within a time frame of days, is only one of the steps in the processing of the ceramic
structure, but it is crucial for the succeeding processing steps. A colloidal suspension (read ink)
where agglomeration is observed can cause several problems. Firstly, the agglomerates can cause
blocking of the printing nozzles and secondly the sintered layer will most likely exhibit inho-
mogeneity in terms of thickness and density of the printed ﬁlm [37]. Creating a well dispersed
colloidal suspension is therefore essential.

agglomerating, to obtain a stable colloidal dispersion. A very general way of examining this
problem is to look at a situation with two particles approaching each other. The total potential

2.3.1
Electrostatic stabilisation
Electrostatic stabilisation relies on electrostatic forces arising from charges of equal sign residing
on the surfaces of particles in a suspension. This surface charge can be altered by several means
and for advanced ceramics (read not clay) the means are preferential absorption of ions from the
solution onto the particle surface or dissociation of amphoteric hydroxyl groups present on the
oxide surfaces [32, 38]. A common way to change the electrostatic repulsion of oxide particles
is therefore to add an electrolyte such as an acid or a base to the solution. If an acid is added
the surface becomes more positively charged and if a base is added the surface becomes more
negatively charged. This essentially means that at low pH the surface of the particles will be
positively charged and at high pH the surface will be negatively charged. The pH where the net
surface charge is zero is termed the point of zero charge (PZC). The strength of the stabilisation
depends on the induced surface potential of the particles, the dielectric properties of the solvent
and the ionic strength of the solution.

2.3.2
Steric stabilisation
The other main mechanism used to form a stable suspension is by steric stabilisation, which
relies on mechanical blocking of the particles from each other. This is done by the addition of
organic molecules, which are usually polymeric, to the solution. When the particles are added
the polymers adsorbs to the particle surfaces by means of chemical or physical adsorption. For
the steric stabilisation to be eﬀective the adsorption of the polymers should be suﬃciently strong
to prevent desorption upon collisions between particles during commuinution. The steric repul-
sive energy depends on the adsorbed layer thickness (δ), the adsorbed layer density, the solvent
quality and the separation distance between interacting particles.
A dense layer is also important to prevent entanglement of polymers residing on diﬀerent
particles as this can lead to bridging ﬂocculation, where the particles ﬂocculate due to the inter-
actions of steric polymers residing on their surfaces. Examples of diﬀerent polymer structures
are homo polymers, diblock polymers, comb-like polymers and functional short chain surfactants
with an anchoring head group and an extended tail. These are illustrated in ﬁgure 2.7 [38].
In order to obtain an optimal dispersion it is important that the steric dispersant is added
in correct quantities. If too small amounts are added there will be empty sites on the particles
where polymers from neighbouring particles can adsorb. This is a form of bridging ﬂoccula-
tion, as explained above, and leads to an increase of the viscosity. On the contrary, a too large
amount of dispersant can also be problematic as the the excess polymers can promote interac-
tion between particles and lead to depletion ﬂocculation, which is caused by an osmotic pressure
diﬀerence if a molecule/polymer suddenly is excluded from a small gab between two particles.
The net eﬀect of too high amounts of dispersant can, however, also promote stabilisation if there
is no interaction between the dispersant polymers [38].
2.3.3

Electrosteric stabilisation is a combination of the electrostatic and steric stabilising eﬀects. It
is commonly obtained by using polyelectrolyte polymers that carry at least one ionic group.
The polymers can have either cationic or anionic groups and the best electrosteric eﬀect is
obtained if the particle surface and the ionised group carry opposite charges. The non-ionic

Solvents and other additives
The solvent, which is used to disperse the ceramic particles, is important due to several rea-
sons. First of all, it should provide suﬃcient wetting of the particles for them to ﬂow easily and
secondly it should dissolve dispersants and other additives and provide the means for creating
a uniform solution. There are generally two types of solvents, which are water and organic
solvents. The organic solvents can further be classiﬁed into non-polar (or weakly polar) and po-
lar liquids. Important physical characteristics of solvents are the dielectric constant, functional
groups, surface tension, viscosity and latent heat of evaporation or the vapour pressure. Water
has a very high dielectric constant, which is at least twice that of most polar liquids. Water also
has a very high surface tension and it is non-toxic, which is the most important reason for using
water. On the contrary most organic solvents are toxic and they have low ﬂash points. Organic
solvents are important for several reasons though. They oﬀer a wide spectrum of polarity and
the surface tension is, as mentioned above, lower. In addition, they have varying vapour pres-
sure, which means that their evaporation rate spans a wide interval from very low to very high.
A high evaporation rate is desirable for a processing method such as 3D inkjet printing, where
the solvent has to evaporate quickly to allow for the deposition of several successive layers. The
solvent, used in a system, is often composed of several solvents as this allows to tune the solvent
system to the required properties.
Other additives include a wide variety and in the following only the relevant ones for inkjet
printing will be presented brieﬂy. A binder is generally an additive, which provides strength
to the green body, which is not important to thin inkjet printed layers. Binders are typically
long chain polymers, which means that they can also be used to change the rheology of the ink,
especially the apparent viscosity can be increased.
Anti-foaming agents are used to improve wetting and decrease foaming, which is of relevance
in pure water based inks, where the surface tension generally is too high for successful printing
to take place.
The combination of solvents and additives is not trivial as speciﬁc additives are only solvable
in certain solvents. Using several solvents will therefore typically also make it harder to ﬁnd
compatible additives.

2.3.6
Rheology of colloidal inks
Rheology is the science of deformation and ﬂow of matter. Rheological experiments can be used
to identify the ﬂow behaviour of liquids as well as the deformation behaviour of solids. When

often a combination of that of an ideal viscous liquid and an ideal elastic solid, which is termed
viscoelastic behaviour. The rheological behaviour is important as a given ceramic processing

2.3.7
Rheometry
Rheometry is the measuring technology that can be used to determine rheological properties of
a sample. Rotational tests can be used to determine the ﬂow behaviour of liquid solutions and
dispersions. A rotational test is performed by rotating either the top part or the bottom part
of the measurement system (MS) while keeping the other a rest (see ﬁgure 2.11 for examples of
MSs). The test mode can be either controlled shear rate (CSR) or controlled shear stress (CSS).
In CSR the rotational speed is controlled and the torque is determined by the instruments and
in CSS it is vice versa. From these raw data the shear rate can be determined according to eq.
2.16 and 2.15 and the viscosity calculated.
Oscillating rheology tests are another type of test, where either the top or the bottom part of
the MS is moved back and forth. Oscillatory test can be used to identify the loss modulus (G′′)
and the storage modulus (G′), which are related to the viscous and elastic behaviour respectively.
Initially, an amplitude sweep test is performed to identify the linear viscoelastic (LVE) range,
where G′ and G′′ are constant. This ensures the validity of Hooks law for the G′, and that of a
Newtonian ﬂuid for the G′′, which are basic assumptions in the test. Based on the LVE range
a value of the shear stress within this range is identiﬁed and used for the subsequent frequency
sweep oscillation (FSO) test. A FSO test is performed at constant shear stress and varying
frequency and determines the G′ and G′′ as a function of frequency. The ratio of G′′
G′ is called

2.3.8
Sedimentation analysis
The stability of a colloidal dispersed system can be characterised in several ways. The measure-
ment of particle sizes is one way to do this and another approach is ﬂow curve measurements .
A diﬀerent dimension to this is the stability over time, which is a good measure of how eﬀective
the dispersant is. A sedimentation analysis is one way to characterise the temporal stability
of a colloidal suspension. Very small particles falling in a uniform liquid will reach a constant
velocity, when the gravitational force equals the sum of the buoyant force and drag force on
the particle. From Stoke’s law a terminal velocity can be calculated when making a number of
assumptions: the particles are spherical and have smooth surfaces, they do not interact and the
ﬂow is laminar [22]. The ﬂow can be assumed laminar for a maximum particle size of approx.
50 µm and the terminal velocity is then given by:
v = r2(ρp −ρl)g
18η
(2.19)
where r is the radius of the particle, ρp the density of the particle and ρl the density of the liquid,
g the acceleration due to gravity and η the viscosity of the ﬂuid. This velocity is seen to be
proportional to the square of the radius of the particle. The size of the particle is therefore very
important in relation to how fast sedimentation happens and therefore agglomeration of particles
will speed up the rate of sedimentation. The viscosity is seen to be inversely proportional to
the velocity, which is important to keep in mind when comparing sedimentation of inks with
diﬀerent viscosity.

Maintenance of the printhead
The test printing of the ceramic inks demanded thorough cleaning of the print heads and other
equipment, which the inks were in contact with during printing such as tubes, reservoir etc. This
was due to the fact that the solvent gradually evaporates, which leaves behind the particles. This
is particularly problematic around the nozzle openings as this is where the evaporation takes
place and these can become blocked. The inks also caused problems during the printing in terms
of partial blocking of the printhead nozzles, so directly after a print session with ceramic inks
the printhead was detached and put in a ultrasound bath containing milipore water to avoid
permanent blocking of the nozzles. The other ink containing or carrying parts were also ﬂushed,
disassembled and cleaned with water and EtOH. After mounting the diﬀerent parts again a
maintenance run was carried out with a ﬂushing solvent. This procedure consisted of suction of
the ink through the nozzles, purging with a large overpressure (around 150-200 m Bar), jetting
and wiping the printhead surface. In this way it was possible to recover a printhead, which had
become blocked during printing.

3.4
Characterisation of the inks
3.4.1
Particle size measurements
The PSD measurements were done at two stages. First, they were performed during milling of
the ink to make sure the right PSD was obtained prior to binder addition. Second, they were
performed after the PVP binder had been added to some inks and dissolved to make sure that
the PS did not increase. The PS measurement procedure was to take 5 droplets of the ink and
add it to a small 3 ml container with EtOH. In this process the ink was heavily diluted. Next,
the diluted ink was added to the measurement chamber. Around 5-7 droplets of the diluted ink
was suﬃcient. The measurement standard operating procedure (SOP) used (a setting for the
measurement) was speciﬁc for YSZ powder in EtOH.
The PS measurements were, unless otherwise stated, performed in EtOH because that pre-
viously had been used successfully as an indicator of the PSD of similar inks. As the main
consituent of the solvent was water varying in content from 40-80 wt% the EtOH was not rep-
resentative of the solvent. Therefore it was also investigated what eﬀect it would have on the
PSD to perform the measurements in water as this represents the main part of the solvent.
3.4.2
Rheological behaviour
The rheological behaviour was investigated by a ﬂow curve measurement test. For the mea-
surements a 50 mm in diameter PP MS was used. The gap distance was set to 0.6 mm and a
solvent trap was used to limit the evaporation of solvent during measurements. The solvent
trap consisted of a small rolled up tissue, which was soaked with EtOH and placed in the small
measurement ”chamber”. Approx 2 ml of ink was distributed on the bottom plate of the MS
system, and subsequently the top plate was lowered to the gab distance. When this distance
had been reached the sample was ”trimmed”, if necessary, around the edges with a small cotton
swab. A lid was put on top and the measurement was started. The shear rate was varied from
1 s−1 to 1000 s−1 both ways in a step mode with 50 measurement points per session and 10 s
waiting time between successive measurements to allow equilibrium conditions to be reached
between the measurement points. The viscosity was determined in the shear rate range from
10 s−1 to 100 s−1 as this is an area of stable measurements.
An oscillatory rheological investigation was performed on ink 2.1 with a 50 mm PP MS and
a gap distance of 0.6 mm. In order to determine the linear viscoelastic (LVE) range an ASO
test was performed at frequencies of 0.1 s−1 1 s−1, 10 s−1 and 100 s−1 in the shear stress range
of 0.1-1000 Pa.

sured dynamic surface tension. Initially, the equipment was calibrated using milipore water and

The sedimentation analysis was carried out by ﬁlling approx. 30 ml of ink in a glass cylinder

diﬀerent cells were all around 42 mm. It was checked that no air bubbles were present on the
surface of the ink as this could cause artefacts and a lid with a stopper was screwed onto the
cylinder before inserting it into the Turbiscan. To begin with the samples were scanned hourly
for approx. two days and as it was quickly realised that the sedimentation was rather slow the

a fortnight. During the initial measurements, which were done every hour, the sample stayed in
the measurement chamber but subsequently the sample had to be inserted before and removed

sample, which would disturb the results. Three samples were prepared with ink 1, ink 1.2 and a
version of ink 1.1 with 1.5 wt % PVP (K90) and only the backscattered light detector was used

Filtering of the inks

blocked very quickly, and it is estimated that the particle fraction in the part of the ink that
was successfully ﬁltered was decreased with min. 50 % making it a very wasteful process. None
of the inks could be ﬁltered easily but ink 2.1 with the long PVP polymers allowed signiﬁcantly
less ink to pass through compared to ink 2 and 3 before becoming blocked, which is most likely
related to the presence of the long PVP polymers.

Chapter 4
Results
In this chapter the results from the ink processing as well as from the printing of the ink will
be presented and evaluated. The aim of the ink development and processing was to make an
ink, which had the right speciﬁcations, deﬁned by the printhead, in terms of viscosity, surface
tension and PSD. In addition the ink should be well stabilised to avoid the formation of ag-
glomerates prior to/during printing. The interval of the ink viscosity was 8-20 mPa s but the
optimal interval was from 10-14 mPa s and this was therefore the aim of the ink development.
Similarly, the aim for the ink’s surface tension was 28-32 mN m−1 and the whole interval was
from 24-36 mN m−1. In terms of the PSD it was, from previous experience [17] known that a
narrow PSD, with a median of approx. 150 µm and the 10th and 90th quantiles of around 100 µm
and 200 µm respectively, was possible to obtain. The upper limit of the PS was according to
the printhead manual 2 µm and this was therefore taken as an upper maximum, though it was
lower than the maximum of 5 µm described in section 2.1. This chapter will start presenting
the results of the printing of the inks, and following this the ink characterisation results will be
presented to back up that the inks in fact were suitable for printing. The Z number of the inks
was not a key parameter in the ink formulation and characterisation but it has been used to
benchmark the inks in relation to the theory on printability.

next step was the printing of the ink. The printing of the inks on a substrate was not done to
begin with either though. Instead it was investigated whether the ink was at all printable and
using ”dropview” how the formed droplets looked upon ejection just after leaving the nozzle.

4.1.2
Altering the solvent system in the inks
In a previous project [34] the possibility of increasing the solid loading of YSZ had been investi-
gated as a mean to increase the viscosity but it was clear that it had to be increased signiﬁcantly
to reach the required viscosity, which was problematic as clogging of the printheads was also
observed. It was therefore termed a too risky approach. Since it now had been tested that
adding long chain polymers to increase the viscosity was not the right approach neither was
increasing the solid loading of YSZ, the only other option seemed to be to substitute some of
the solvent with a highly viscous solvent, which was compatible with water and EtOH. In this
way the ink was altered as little as possible, which it was assumed would be the best approach
to avoid causing problems with the dispersant.
In the printhead manual [46] it was found that possible solvents, which were compatible with
the print head were lactate and acetate esters or aromatic solvents such as toluene, xylene or
anisole. These are, however, all low viscosity solvents with viscosities below 4 mPa s (see ap-
pendix B). Other possible highly viscous solvents were therefore investigated and in appendix
B most of the suggested ﬂuids have been listed along with the their physical characteristics.
Additionally some of the other considered solvents, based mainly on literature surveys on col-
loidal ceramic inks and on the printing inks used with either the PixDro LP50 printer or Spectra
SL-128 printhead, have been listed. This research suggests glycols to be the best candidates in
a partly water based system. The main disadvantage seemed to be that the vapour pressure at
room temperature was very low compared to that of water and EtOH. This could be a challenge
as fast evaporation of the solvent is desirable to allow for the deposition of several layers shortly
after each other. It was, however, a general trend that increasing viscosity of a solvent resulted
in a decreasing vapour pressure.
Three candidates, which were readily available in the laboratory, were therefore identiﬁed.
These were dipropylene glycol (DPG), polyethylene glycol (PEG) and polypropylene glycol
(PPG). PEG and PPG are short chain polymers, which are liquid at room temperature when
the molecular weight is below approx. 1000 g mol−1 and their solubility in water also decreases
with increasing molecular weight. The ones tested initially were therefore DPG, PEG-600 and
PPG-400 (where 400 and 600 refer to the average molecular weight of the polymers). The glycols
were mixed with the water/EtOH solvent (kept at a constant ratio) with diﬀerent amounts of
glycols to get an idea of how much was needed to obtain the right viscosity. Solutions were
prepared with 10, 20, 30 and 50 wt% of the glycols. The resultant viscosity of these solutions
are shown in ﬁgure 4.2.

recommended range. They exhibited a qualitatively similar shear thinning behaviour to that of

showed less shear thinning than the former inks. The viscosities were 8.8 and 9.4 of ink 4 and

Chapter 5
Discussion
In this chapter the problems of printing the colloidal inks will be discussed and assessed from
diﬀerent perspectives to get a broader perspective of the problems experienced during printing,
ink processing and ink characterisation. To have a broad perspective, on these issues, is im-
portant as the ink characterisation results did not reveal the cause of the printing problems.
In addition, the behaviour of the ink in the printhead is a black box, and printing of colloidal
inks is a relatively new research area, which is not that well understood; it is therefore unlikely
that the exact cause of the problems can be identiﬁed. Instead several solutions should be kept
open, so based on the discussion the most likely causes related to the printing diﬃculties can be
identiﬁed and the possible solutions to these causes can be proposed and tested in future work.
5.1
Printability in theory and practice
The original objective of this project, which was printing of ceramic inks, has not been success-
fully achieved. For all inks prepared there have been problems with the inks not being jetted at
all or a rapidly decreasing jetting performance. These problems are diﬃcult to explain directly
from the characterisation of the inks as the PSD, viscosity and surface tension of the inks were
in the prescribed ranges. Moreover, the Z number of the inks has been calculated, and though it
is a bit low(from 4-5) compared to the high 4-14 interval suggested in literature, this is close to
the optimal Z number when assessing it from the perspective of the physical parameters deﬁned
in the print head manual.
5.1.1
Ink behaviour during printing
The characterisation of an ink for printing in terms of rheological behaviour and surface tension
does not simulate the behaviour of the ink during printing. The reason this is not the case, is
that it is not possible to reach the high shear rates and short measuring time frames, which
occur during printing, with the available measuring equipment. In a PhD project [50] on this
matter, it has been determined that the maximal shear rates observed during inkjet printing can
approach 106 s−1, which is well above what commercial rheometers can go to. In [50] a capillary
viscometer was built and this was able to measure viscosities at shear rates up to 2 × 105 s−1.
While the maximum shear rate obtained during inkjet printing is very high at the maximum,
it also drops very fast. After just 15 µs it has dropped more than an order of magnitude so
the whole process of forming and ejecting a droplet takes place very rapidly in less that 100 µs.

actual surface formation process and thereby the actual surface tension during the formation

inks was measured before and after the ﬁltering and it did not change even though the solid
loading decreased, so the blocking was most likely a matter of capacity of the ﬁlters and the
presence of polymers in the inks. A high capacity ﬁlter used prior to jetting would still be a way
of controlling the PS better and avoid clogging if an ink has agglomerated.
The solvents, which were used were not any of the recommended stated solvents. The im-
portance of this is not clear though. It could be that the speciﬁcally stated solvents have been
tested and are known to be compatible with the materials which the ink is in contact with
during printing, but that these solvents should posses some special characteristic, which cannot
be formulated as a general physical parameter is very unlikely. For example it is known that
the test ink provided by PixDro was based on PPG and propylene carbonate, which works even
though it does not contain any of the recommended solvents . Moreover, the above stated sol-
vents all have low viscosities below 4 mPas. It is therefore necessary to include a higher viscosity
solvent such as a glycol, which can be used to increase the resultant ink viscosity. Alternatively
the solid loading could be increased but as mentioned earlier too rapid solvent evaporation and
stabilisation issues of the ink could very well be the outcome.
Another parameter related to the choice of solvent is the vapour pressure. It should not be too
high according to [46] as it can form cavities in the printhead and thereby block the piezoelectric
pumping mechanism that draws ink into the jetting chamber. As an example of a solvent with
too high vapour pressure IPA is mentioned. It is conjectured that vapour bubbles present in
the ink before printing therefore also can be a problem and it could be a possibility to de-aerate
the ink prior to printing it to avoid this possible source of error. Adding a de-foaming agent
could perhaps also serve that purpose as its main purpose is to avoid foaming of the surface,
and thereby it causes rupture of gas bubbles. A last thing related to the formation of cavities
would be to avoid applying excessive vacuum to the printhead as this could also let in air during

5.1.3
The eﬀect of having long chain PVP polymers in an ink
It has already been speculated that the long chain PVP polymers could be causing the printing
issues in some of the inks due to the very long and ﬂexible nature of these. These polymers
served the function of increasing the ink viscosity but it seems likely that the long PVP polymers
could become entangled with each other and with the PVP dispersants that were encapsulating
the YSZ particles. An estimate of their length was therefore made to test this hypothesis from
another perspective than the ﬂow curve measurements described in section 4.3.2. The monomer
constituting the PVP polymers is shown in ﬁgure 5.2a [51], and was used to determine an ap-
proximate extended length of the polymers. The monomer length was determined from the
atoms constituting the monomer and its extension assumed to be 4 times the covalent radius of
a carbon atom. For a PVP K90 polymer with a molecular weight of 360,000 g mol−1 a total of
3243 monomers per polymer are expected, which gives a polymer length of 10 µm. This is the
same order of magnitude as the nozzle opening and spans over several particles. In compari-
son the length of an extented PVP K30 or K15 polymer is around 1 µm and 0.25 µm respectively.
The elongated state is not a common thermodynamically stable conﬁguration of a polymer
as they tend to coil up in most solvents [42]. The term radius of gyration, Rg is used to ex-
press the geometrical extension of a polymer in a solution and it is a function of the molecular
weight. It can be visualised as the radius of a coiled-up polymer in a solution. For PVP K90 the
Rg has been determined to be 38 nm in water [53] which, though the solvent is diﬀerent from
the solvent system used in this project, provides an approx. length scale. In [53] the polymer
overlap concentration, c∗, of PVP was also determined, which is the concentration at which the
polymers in a solution begin to overlap when at rest. For the PVP K90 it was determined to be
0.68 wt%, which is very close to the concentration of 0.7 wt% in ink 1.1 and ink 2.1. For a PVP
of 55,000 g mol−1 c∗= 3.8 wt%. The PVP used in ink 3 had an average molecular weight of
40,000 gmol−1 and was added in an amount corresponding to 3 wt%, which is therefore below c∗.
In general the viscoelasticity of an inkjet printed solution containing polymers increases with
increasing polymer concentration or molecular weight of the polymers [54]. Part of the expla-
nation for this eﬀect has been described by a coil to stretch transition of the polymers and an
accompanying strain hardening eﬀect, which leads to a strong increase in the hydrodynamic drag

regime is highly dependent on the polymer molecular weight, its architecture and the solvent
quality and can therefore not be directly related to c∗. In [54] for example they jetted cellulose
ester (CE) polymers in γ-butyrolactanone and for a 30,000 g mol−1 CE polymer at a normalised
concentration c/c∗= 3.8. Another issue is of course what eﬀect it has on the drop formation
and ejection process that the solution contains dispersed particles as well as polymers. The
most likely answer is that it will increase the hydrodynamic drag force additionally and thereby
possibly the print-ability as well.
The oscillatory tests of the rheological behaviour of ink 2.1 were performed to see whether any
signs of viscoelastic eﬀects could be observed. The initial ASO test did not provide much useful
information on this matter as the useable results indicated that the storage modulus G′ was in
fact zero and hence no elastic response of the inks could be observed. The problem with this
test was again that the rheometer was not capable of simulating actual conditions during inkjet
printing and hence the results do not exclude that some inks, for example ink 2.1, have a highly
viscoelastic response during jetting, which can prevent droplet ejection. Assuming this is in fact
the case, the fact that ink 3 jets initially but stops after a short while could be explained as a
phenomena of gradual cross-linking of polymers in the jetting chamber and thereby increasing
viscoelasticity and associated drag forces.
5.1.4
The inﬂuence of the printhead on the ink print-ability
Even if the jetting of ink 1.1, ink 2.1 and ink 3 in fact were halted by a strong viscoelastic
response when generating ﬁring pulses there is still a question of why ink 4 and ink 5 do not
jet. These inks contain PEG-400 and PPG-400 polymers respectively, but an approximation of
the extended length of a PEG-400 polymer gives a length of 4 mm assuming nine monomers per
polymer (see ﬁgure 5.2b [52] for a picture of the monomer). The length of the PPG-400 polymer
is on a similar length scale. The nanoscale extension of the PEG-400 and PPG-400 polymers
means that they do not have the properties of a binder and they will therefore not exhibit any
entanglement. Moreover, they have been used for commercial inks such as the blue test ink
provided by PixDro. The reason why ink 4 and 5 are not jetted when added to the printer is
therefore most likely related to the dispersed particles. In fact it was found when formulating
the ink, solv. 5, that the ink was jet-able. Based on this it can be conjectured that the problems
are related either to the particles in the inks and thereby the dispersion or the ﬁring pulse shape
as this might not work properly for this type of colloidal ink.
The ink recipes were all based on an ink, which had proven successful for printing on a ther-
mal inkjet printer with nozzle openings of only 20 µm and even though the PSD could not be
replicated the max. PS was below the 2 µm. The other main diﬀerence between the thermal
inkjet printer and the piezoelectric inkjet printer was the mechanics behind the droplet forma-
tion process, which in the case of the thermal inkjet printer was based on evaporation of a small
portion of ink and subsequent formation of a vapour bubble, which upon collapse transmitted a
pressure wave through the chamber. In contrast to this the drop formation in the piezoelectric
printhead took place by an inward and outward deﬂection of a piezoelectric material, which gen-
erated a pressure wave. This could perhaps have some inﬂuence on the jet-ability but another
factor could also be the geometry of nozzle channels.

The printhead used was dimensioned with two 90 ◦kinks from the piezoelectric material to the
nozzle opening as shown in ﬁgure 5.3 [46]. The drop formation process in the printhead manual
is described as an initial suction mechanism that pulls ink into the nozzle chamber when the
piezoelectric material deﬂects outward. Upon relaxation of the piezoelectric material a pressure
wave propagates through the channel to the nozzle chamber and a droplet is ejected. It is quite
a complicated geometric structure leading to the nozzle opening and it is speculated that this is
part of the reason why the ink does not work in this printhead but a very similar ink works in
the thermal printhead. In fact the ﬂow in such a geometry will become turbulent at much lower
Re numbers compared to a straight tube channel causing the appearance of very non-linear ﬂow
phenomena. It is therefore realistic that the jetting issues can be solved by altering the voltage
signal used to produce the ﬁring pulse as this clearly is very important in regards to the droplet
formation process. In [55] it is argued that a double pulse waveform, with a larger ﬁrst signal
,comparable in shape to the one already employed, and a smaller secondary signal, similar in
shape but with a smaller amplitude, can promote the separation of the droplet from the nozzle.
That is a path which has not been exploited, and since it was observed during the project that
small changes of the ﬁring signal could have a large impact on whether droplets were jetted or
not it should be investigating.
5.1.5
Summary of the print-ability issues
The reason for the jetting diﬃculties of the processed inks cannot be uniquely identiﬁed. There
are several issues, which could be responsible. In regard to inks 1.1 and 2.1 and ink 3 it seems
likely that the presence of long PVP polymers in the ink relates to the jetting issues. Possibly
a viscoelastic response of the inks causes the droplets not to be detached from the nozzle. The
other inks, especially ink 4 and ink 5, do not contain long polymers and the most likely cause of
the missing jetting is related to the ﬁring pulse generated in the nozzle chamber. Eliminating
issues of agglomerates in the inks by passing the inks through a high capacity ﬁlter prior to
jetting is also a good option.
Lastly, the formation of gas bubble cavities in the printhead
during jetting should also be eliminated by de-aerating the inks prior to jetting, as this can also
explain why the inks are not jetted.

5.2
Ink characterisation
5.2.1
Particle size measurements
The PSD measurements were found to depend on what solvent the measurements were carried
out in. As the standard is EtOH and that has worked previously for similar inks this was also
used as the default during these experiments. The PSD was found to decrease when measured in
water compared to EtOH. In particular the peak around 1 µm disappeared (refer to ﬁgure 4.6)
when the measurements were performed in water compared to in EtOH so the measurements in
EtOH could be altering the PSD. All measurements of the PS were below the maximum 2 µm
and the printing did in fact not show signs of permanent clogging of the nozzles during printing,
which is thus as expected.
It should be noted that PSD measurements of ink 1 performed previously, approx. one year
earlier, showed only a single peak centered around 150 nm. The most likely explanation for
this discrepancy is that the PVP K15 dispersant used now is not the same as back then. The
PVP K15 dispersant used now has a diﬀerent molecular weight and it is therefore possible that
optimising the PVP loading again would change the PSD into a single peak and improve the
stabilisation of the ink. The other constituents in the ink seem unlikely to be the cause of the
discrepancy in the PSD. The YSZ powder used is from the same batch as one year earlier. The
EtOH, water and PE bottles are standard products available in the lab, and there is no reason to
suspect that they suddenly should be causing the problems. An option is also the measurement
equipment. Even though it is used for many diﬀerent types of slurries and residues accumulate
within the measuring chamber, (for example when performing a measurement with ultrasound in
the measuring chamber this was observed) it is unlikely that the equipment should be the cause
as similar problems have not been reported by anyone else. A last option is the experimental
procedures but these have been crosschecked by the persons making the original ink - and with
the same outcome.
5.2.2
Flow curve measurements
The rotational rheology measurements were used to measure ﬂow curves with shear stress and
viscosity data as a function of the shear rate, and thereby also any non-Newtonian ﬂow be-
haviour could be observed. The ﬁtting of the ﬂow curve data to the ”power law” worked well
as a mean to quantify the shear thinning behaviour. The shear thinning was found to be pre-
dominant in the inks containing PVP binder polymers (ink 1.1, ink 1.2, ink 2.1 and ink 3).
It was, however, not possible to obtain much useful information on the stability of the inks
from the ﬁts as all the ﬁts were in excellent agreement with the experimental results. This
is very likely due to the very low solid loading of the inks that makes it infeasible as a mean
to detect signs of a poor suspension stability simply because the particles are too spread out [47].
The ﬂow curve measurements provide a reasonable accuracy of the viscosity, but due to the
very liquid-like nature of the inks it was generally diﬃcult to reproduce the experimental results
with high accuracy. The main challenge seemed to be that the PP MS requires a certain amount
of sample to produce accurate measurements. That means that the gap between the two plates
should be ﬁlled with sample and there should be enough sample to form a small rim around the
plates during measurement (cf. ﬁgure 2.11). Any excess sample should be trimmed away before
starting the test. With a very low viscosity sample it becomes diﬃcult to control the sample

test. Due to the presence of particles in the ink it can be problematic to use the CP MS, and
the challenge, related to controlling the amount of sample would be present in this case also.
Other MS designed for low viscosity samples, such as the DG MS or narrow gab CC MS, exists

5.2.3
The dispersant
The PVP dispersant used to stabilise the inks has been shown to work for inkjet printing of
inks [17] using a printhead with nozzle openings of 20 µm. It is therefore reasonable to assume
that PVP works adequately well for stabilising YSZ particles in a water/EtOH solvent, which is
also what the PSD measurements suggest. As already mentioned several times, the PVP loading
needs to be re-optimised though due to the diﬀerence of molecular weight of the PVP polymers.
Electrostatic stabilisation would be an attractive way of increasing the stabilisation of the ink
- perhaps as a supplement to the the steric stabilisation. The IEP of aqueous suspensions with
8YSZ is not well agreed upon in literature as work in [58] suggests the IEP to be around 10 and
work in [59] suggests it to be 8.4. In both cases HCl and NaOH were used to adjust the pH
and the speciﬁc surface area of the 8YSZ powder used was also similar. Even though they do
not agree upon the IEP their work suggests that ζ-potentials on the order of 40-60 mV can be
obtained in water, which can be considered a good stabilisation. It would therefore be relevant

to investigate a broad pH range to see if an acidic or basic environment works best for an ink
with water(/EtOH) and PPG-400 for example.
Alternative dispersant options would be to look into other steric or electrosteric dispersants
- preferably short chain dispersants of the type block copolymers or comblike polymers (cf.
ﬁgure 2.7), which contain an insoluble part that anchors on the particle and a soluble part that
provides the mechanical barrier. These are the most eﬀective dispersants and the ones least
likely to cause bridging ﬂocculation compared to a simple homopolymer.
5.2.4
Ink suspension stability summarised
The suspension stability of the inks, based on the ink characterisation, was not found to pose
any problems in relation to the subsequent printing of the inks. The PSD showed signs of some
agglomerates with a particle size around 1 µm. These were, however, not necessarily present
in the ink, but could be a sign of some minor stability issues. The dispersant loading should
therefore be re-optimised as this could possibly eliminate this peak.
The sedimentation analysis provided a good measure of the ink stability over time and as
the rheology data did not seem suitable as a measure of the suspension stability it was also an
important tool. It was possible to track the sedimentation rate and agglomeration of particles
should also be observable from the BS data, but no signs of agglomeration of particles in the
inks was observed over time. The sedimentation analysis was not performed on ink 4 and ink
5 and it would therefore be relevant to do so. It could provide some more knowledge on the
temporal stability of these inks and it would also be possible to compare the results to those of
ink 1.1 (modf.) and ink 1.2.
5.3
Ink preparation using planetary ball milling
The ink preparation using a planatary ball mill is an attractive way of decreasing the milling
time from around 14 days down to approx 5 hours. The results show that it is possible to obtain
a PSD, which is very similar to the present PSD obtained with the traditional milling procedure.
There is no general rule for the optimal conditions during planetary milling [33] though, and
the optimization will therefore have to rely on further experimental work.
The three experiments are not directly comparable due to the simultaneous change of sev-
eral operational parameters, but they do give an indication of what is likely the most eﬀective
approach. The results suggest that using high beads:powder ratios are eﬀective and using a
combination of bead sizes is also a good approach, which has also been shown to be true theo-
retically [35]. Concerning the 1 mm beads, they make the ink processing more complicated as
they cannot easily be separated from the ink and they lead to a larger fraction of ink being
wasted. Using 3 mm beads instead could probably improve this issue. Regarding the rotational
speed, the results indicate that very high rotational speeds are not necessary to decrease milling
times dramatically and there is therefore no need to go to very high speeds, as this also has the
disadvantage of excessive wear of the mill and the milling equipment.
Using low rotational speeds with a mix of small 1 mm spherical beads and larger 10 mm cylin-
drical beads and a beads:powder ratio of 20:1 gave good results and even though the rotational
speed was only 50 RPM it was possible to mill the ink down in 4 hrs. In contrast milling at

150 RPM with 10 mm spherical beads at a 3:1 ratio of beads:powder took 7 hours to obtain a
similar result in terms of PSD. The last approach, which involved pre-milling of the dry powder
and millling at very high rotational speed of 300-400 RPM for the ﬁrst part produced a similar
PSD to the others in approx. 3 hours (excluding the pre-milling). A 20:1 beads:powder ratio
was used in this case with 1 mm spherical beads.
The experimental work so far has shown that the milling at very high rotational speeds, of
several hundred RPM, makes it very diﬃcult to control the process and excessive milling is in
fact observed, which makes the particles very prone to agglomeration and leads to an increased
PSD in the ink. Non-reversible agglomeration of particles caused by excessive milling has been
explained in [35]: As the surface area of the particles continues to increase the energy per surface
area eventually drops and since the importance of Van der Walls forces increases with increas-
ing surface area of the particles, they will start forming weakly physical bonded agglomerates.
These can eventually be transformed into chemically bonded agglomerates if milling continues.
This can also lead to amortization of the powder, which is also undesirable. This eﬀect has been
observed in milled powder but it is not directly comparable to the work in this project as the
whole ink is milled. In fact the dispersant is present in the ink during milling, which serves the
function of preventing re-agglomeration so the validity of the above argument can be questioned.
In fact it will require that the dispersant loading becomes insuﬃcient at a critical surface area
of the particles, or that the dispersant polymers in fact become damaged due to the excessive
milling.

Chapter 6
Conclusion and Outlook
6.1
Conclusion
In this thesis the formulation, processing and characterisation of colloidal ceramic inks intended
to be printed with a Spectra SL-128 printhead inbedded in a PixDro LP50 inkjet printer has
been carried out. The initial aim of the project, which was to print thin ceramic layers of YSZ
onto a substrate has not been reached as jetting the processed inks appeared to be a major
obstacle, which was not resolved.
The reason for the printing issues, is not clear and the characterisation of the inks in terms of
rheological behaviour, surface tension, sedimentation stability and PSD did not provide the an-
swer as these results indicated that the inks were suitable for printing. The main problems with
the characterisation was found to be that the conditions during inkjet printing diﬀer vastly from
those, which can be measured, and the processes that take place in the print head are a black box.
Several reasons have been identiﬁed as potentially causing the problems. Firstly, the jetting
signal, which is used to generate the droplets could be causing the problems as it might not be
suitable for colloidal inks. Diﬀerent shapes of the voltage signal used to generate the jetting
signal should therefore be investigated. Secondly, the problems could also be related the pres-
ence of gas bubbles in the ink during printing, as this can hinder jetting. De-aerating the ink
prior to jetting should therefore be tried as well as avoiding too high applied vacuums to the
ink as this can suck air bubbles into the jetting chamber through the nozzles. Though the ink
characterisation showed no sign of particles with a size that could cause nozzle clogging, ﬁltering
the ink, prior to jetting, would be a way to avoided problems with agglomerated inks.
The addition of PVP polymers as a mean to increase the viscosity of an ink showed not to be
a plausible solution. The long PVP binders did not jet at all, and a non-permanent clogging of
the printhead nozzles was observed. The problems were ascribed to the very long PVP binder
polymers in the ink, and it could be due to a strongly visocelastic response of the ink when
producing the acoustic jetting signals. Decreasing the length of the PVP binder polymers used
in the ink produced an ink, which was at ﬁrst jettable but a non-permanent clogging was also
observed in this case after a short while and hence using PVP binder molecules to increase the
viscosity of the ink was termed non-suitable
The only other viable approach to the viscosity issue was to include a high viscosity solvent.
60
==PAGE:70==
6.2 Outlook
Inks with 25 wt.% of the solvent substituted with PEG-400/PPG-400 were made. This was
eﬀective in terms of increasing the ink viscosity, though the viscosity was slightly below the
optimal range and the surface tension slightly above the optimal range, but still suitable for
printing. It is a possibility though that the optimal physical parameters of colloidal inks diﬀer
from those of traditional inks so these should not be ruled out yet. The PSD was similar to
the previous inks and the ink with PPG-400 in fact showed an improved PSD compared to the
starting ink.
6.2
Outlook
The main aim of any future work should be to formulate and process an ink, which can be suc-
cessfully printed with the PixDro LP50 printer. There are several paths, which can be followed.
The ﬁrst would be to continue the work on the inks containing a high viscosity solvent along
with water/EtOH. Mixing water/EtOH and PEG-400 or PPG-400 showed the most promising
results. The ink containing PPG-400 would be the best choice based on the characterisation
and the test printing of a similar ink without YSZ, and this should hence be experimented with
in terms of viscosity and surface tension. The viscosity could be increased slightly by increasing
the share of PPG-400 to 30 wt.% of the solvent. In order to change the surface tension the
EtOH:water ratio could be altered. A sedimentation analysis would also be relevant to perform
and compare to the previous tested inks.
The printing should be tested, while de-aerating the ink prior to printing and the optimal
applied vacuum should be identiﬁed and kept constant during the printing session. A diﬀerent
voltage signal applied to the printhead, such as a double peak, described in section 5.1.4, would
be a good experiment as well. If the above does not work the ﬁltering of the ink should be
considered, as well as decreasing the solid loading of the ink.
Another path could involve a diﬀerent dispersant and solvent system. A solvent system of
water and PPG-400 would be an option, and a suitable de-foaming agent as a mean to adjust
the surface tension would most likely have to be added. Alternatively a combination of EtOH
and PPG-400 could also be tested. In terms of the dispersant it would be relevant to test the
eﬀect of electrostatic stabilisation at varying pH in the water/PPG-400 solvent system. Relevant
steric or electrosteric dispersants could also be identiﬁed but generally they should be relatively
low molecular weight, similar to or lower than PVP K15.
In fact some preliminary tests were made using some steric dispersants, which were available
in the lab. The tested dispersants were ”Disperbyk 180, 194 N, 2050 and 2155” and they were
tested in a 7:3 ratio of water:PPG-400 . Initially the ”Disperbyk 2050” was discarded as it did
not dissolve in the solvent. Next a solid loading around 35-40 wt.% YSZ was added along with
some grinding beads. The beads:powder ratio was around 4:1 and the bottles were put on rollers
for approx. 10 days after which the PSD was measured. From the PSD results the ”Disperbyk
2155” dispersant was identiﬁed as the most promising. It produced a PSD very similar to the
already processed inks with a Q50 = 152 nm and a Q90 = 844 nm. Optimisation of the dispersant
loading and beads:powder ratio could possibly improve the PSD of an ink stabilised with ”Dis-
perbyk 2155”. The ”Disperbyk 2155” is an acrylate co-polymer. Unfortunately the molecular
weight or composition is not available, which is often the case with commercial dispersants, but
it is formulated for inks containing pigments, and therefore it can be assumed to be suitable for
61

ceramic printing inks as well.
A last consideration, which should not be ruled out yet, is whether the printhead is suitable for
colloidal ceramic inks. Even though it is stated that the printhead is suitable for inks containing
pigments, these are generally of lower density than YSZ and the PSD also smaller. Another
printhead would be the only way of determining if the printhead is the problem.
