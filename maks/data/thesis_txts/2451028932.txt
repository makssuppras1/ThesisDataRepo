Abstract
Wind farm owners are heavily investing in initiatives that reduces costs and im-
proves eﬃciency in both design phase of foundations and in during operation of the
farms. Increasingly detailed knowledge about the structures are desired to explore
the potential for optimization. This raises the need for reﬁned data analysis meth-
ods. Dynamic properties are of a signiﬁcant importance in calculating fatigue loads
during the design phase of wind turbines and in monitoring the structural integrity
of turbine foundations.
This thesis aims to use time domain operational modal
analysis to estimate modal parameters from measurements undertaken on already
installed o↵shore wind turbines.
The thesis provides a walk-through of the theory behind operational modal analy-
sis as well as descriptions of 5 di↵erent modal parameter identiﬁcation algorithms
within the framework. Identiﬁcations throughout the thesis are carried out using the
Eigensystem Realization Algorithm, which is found to be identical to a variant of
the Covariance driven Stochastic Subspace algorithm. The implemented algorithm
is veriﬁed ﬁrst on two linear mechanical dynamic models and then on an aeroelastic
model of an o↵shore wind turbine before being used on SCADA accelerometer data
and data from a measurement campaign. An analytic expression for the uncertainty
of the estimated modal parameter is found through Monte Carlo simulation. Stabi-
lization diagrams are used as the basis of an automated operational modal analysis
scheme utilizing both k-means clustering and hierarchical clustering. The identiﬁca-
tion algorithm along with the autonomous scheme identiﬁed modal parameters using
responses from an aeroelastic simulation. The algorithm is used on a large amount
of accelerometer SCADA data from a modern wind farm allowing for statistical
analysis of the estimates. Finally The algorithm is used to determine mode shapes
of a multi megawatt wind turbine tower in both fore/aft and side/side directions.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Supplying wind energy is becoming an increasingly competitive activity and requires
companies to constantly be on the forefront of technological development in order
to reduce both design and maintenance costs of wind farms. O↵shore wind farms
are gaining traction because of a more reliable wind resource, less space constrains
and a lesser impact on local society. The drawbacks of building an o↵shore wind
farm is that the costs associated with constructing and operating the farm is sig-
niﬁcantly higher compared to an onshore farm as access is limited and foundations
are complex and expensive. This emphasizes the need for optimizing the materi-
als in both turbine tower and foundations in order to reduce costs. A key design
parameter of an o↵shore wind turbine is the fatigue loading over a lifetime. The
fatigue loading is determined by the magnitude and frequencies of the stress cycles
experienced by the materials in the turbine from installation to decommissioning.
This makes it an important task to to accurately be able to predict the stress his-
tory in selected hotspots experienced by a turbine over its lifetime when placed in
an o↵shore environment. The stress history of turbines can be described as an in-
teraction of external loads induced by wind and waves and the dynamic properties
of the structure namely ﬂexibility, weight and energy dissipation, which in technical
terms are called sti↵ness, mass and damping. As a supplement to knowing these
properties in the design phase it is also important for the the wind farm owners
to monitor these properties over the lifetime of a turbine as a change in dynamic
properties can elongate or shorten the turbines lifetime.
The external wind and wave loads are chaotic at ﬁrst glance.
Wind loading is
dominated by turbulent eddies which makes the velocities in the wind ﬂow ﬂuctuate
heavily.
The wave pattern at a given spot is consisting of waves from multiple
directions caused by geographical and weather phenomena potentially happening
far away from the actual location. When observed over a time period, however,
these external loads can be modelled relatively precise as random variables described
by statistical measures reﬁned by the huge amount of weather and sea state data
available in the present day.

uncontrolled environment. The dynamics properties of the turbine depends of course

CHAPTER 1
1.2. ON OPERATIONAL MODAL ANALYSIS
The design of wind turbines are based on simulations in aeroelastic software where
the dynamic properties are used as inputs. In order to give designers of turbines
and foundations the best possible conditions to optimize future designs in terms of
complexity and material usage it is important to measure the actual dynamic prop-
erties of already build turbines so that these can act as references for the aeroelastic
models on which the designs are based.
This thesis covers the work of developing and assessing an operational modal analysis
tool based on the methodical framework of operational modal analysis. The tool
will be implemented in Matlab. The tool is then used to extract modal parameters
from wind turbine measurements provided by Ørsted.
By assessing the use of operational modal analysis on wind turbine dynamic data
this thesis aims to bring us a step closer to accurately and continuously measure
the dynamic properties of o↵shore wind turbine and hereby making use of the ever
growing volume of data that are logged every day in the o↵shore wind farms around
the world to gain further insight on their life cycle. Work has been done on the
subject in recent years by Bajric[11], Juul et al.[7] and Requeson[8] among others.
This work adds statistical analysis of large datasets from wind farms to the existing
research.
1.2
On Operational Modal Analysis
The term ”modal analysis” refers to the act of describing the vibrating motion
of a structure as a set of superpositioned modes which each is described through
a set of modal parameters, namely natural frequency, damping and mode shape.
Operational modal analysis is a branch of modal analysis where measurements are
obtained from a structure excited by unknown random forcing via its operating
environment as opposed to controlled excitation in a test facility. The OMA frame-
work is practical when the structure under investigation is too large to excite in
a controlled way. Examples are bridges and large buildings. Historically most of
the modal analysis methods matured during the start of the 1990’s. James III et
al.[15] introduced the NExT framework where existing experimental modal analysis
methods were incorperated into an OMA framework and Overschee and De Moor[3]
introduced the stochastic subspace methods which were further reﬁned by Peeters
and De Roeck[5][6]. With the powerful computers of modern day and well developed
data collection capabilities the OMA framework is getting increasingly relevant in
industry 4.0 to maintain digital twins of operational machines without having to
take them out of operation to undergo vibration tests. In 2015 Brincker and ven-
tura published a textbook collecting various methods in one text[2]. In regards to
wind turbines OMA is a perfect match as wind turbines are massive moving struc-
tures which performance and lifetime are very much dependant on their dynamic
properties.
When doing analyses of a response time series, one will ﬁnd that the response is a
mixture of information about the dynamic properties of the structure and the forcing
that excites it. This means that if an analyst uncritically investigated a response
from a structure based on output only measurements, he or she would retrieve

information distorted by the excitation signature. In traditional modal analysis the
forcing is know and can therefore be subtracted from the response. This is not the
case in OMA. The fundamental idea of operational modal analysis is that if the
excitation forcing is random enough, meaning that it’s energy is spread out on a
signiﬁcantly wider frequency range than the dynamic range of the structure, then it
contains no information that distorts the modal information related to the structure
embedded in the response.
Two di↵erent overall approaches exists in general modal parameter extraction. One
is where output data is analysed in frequency domain through analysis of the energy
per frequency band in the analysed signal. An alternative approach is analysing the
data in time domain where a decaying oscillation is used as basis for the analysis.
Several methods exists within both frequency domain and time domain analysis
ranging in complexity from visual inspection of plots to advanced ﬁtting algorithms.
This thesis works exclusively with the time domain methods because of their ca-
pability to distinguish between modes with closely spaced natural frequencies and
their robustness to noisy signals.
1.3
Scope of This Project

• To document and assess the currently available time domain OMA methods

2.1
Random Processes
A random signal is described as a stochastic process which again is deﬁned as a
collection of random variables. The random process is said to be stationary if the
statistical moments describing the process does not vary over time meaning that
it’s density distribution is the same no matter at which point in time the process
is observed. The process is said to be ergodic if the mean of a large ensemble of
process realizations is equal to the mean of a large sample from a single process
realization. In other words, the random process is ergodic if its ensemble average
equals its time average. The random process is said to be a Gaussian process if
its distribution follows a Gaussian distribution. If a process is Gaussian it can be
completely described through second order statistics i.e mean and variance. In this
thesis only stationary ergodic Gaussain processes are considered.
One of the important assumptions in OMA is that the inputs to the system are zero
mean Gaussian distributed random processes. Relevant statistical measures are the
ﬁrst statistical moment which describes which values the signal is most likely to
take (mean or expected value). The second statistical moment describes how much
variance there is in the signal (variance and standard deviation). A measure to
reveal harmonic content in a random stationary signal is the autocovariance of the
signal which is a measure of how correlated the signal is with itself at later or earlier
points in time. Another measure describing harmonic content in random signals is
the Spectral density which is a measure for the energy per frequency band.
A random stationary signal is said to be white noise if its energy is spread evenly over
an inﬁnite frequencies range and if its autocovariance is inﬁnitely high at a times
inﬁnitely close to each other before dropping to 0 after an inﬁnitesimal time lag,
These deﬁnitions are very important in OMA as if we can make these assumptions
about excitation forces it allow us to omit the inputs contribution to the output of
a system.

Matrix Decompositions
When working with large matrices it is often useful to decompose these into multi-
plications of smaller matrices which can lead to data reduction based on the rank
of the matrix, decomposition is also used to invert matrices when solving regression
problems.
Eigenvalue Decomposition
The eigenvalue decomposition is deﬁned from the case where the linear transforma-
tion of the vector v deﬁned by the square n ⇥n matrix A is equal to a scalar λ
multiplied by the same vector v:
Av = λv
(2.68)
Then v is said to be a right eigenvector of A and λ is said to be an eigenvalue.
Extending this line of thought, there might be up to a number of n eigenvectors and
eigenvalues fulﬁlling (2.68), in that case the equation is extended to:
A[v] = [v][λ]
(2.69)
Where [λ] is a diagonal matrix holding the eigenvalues and [v] holds the eigenvectors
in the columns as [v1, v2, . . . , vn]. If A is symmetric and has real and di↵erent non-
zero eigenvalues, the eigenvectors will be orthogonal and thereby constitute a natural
basis for their own vector space. Following Equation (2.69), a real symmetric matrix
A can be decomposed as
A = [v][λ][v]T
(2.70)
And in the general case where A is not symmetric and eigenvectors are non-orthogonal:
A = [v][λ][v]−1
(2.71)
The act of breaking down a matrix into orthogonal vectors is basicly what we do in
modal analysis when we describe the motion of a dynamic system through indepen-
dent modes.

2.4
OMA Assumptions
As the input forces are unknown in OMA some assumptions will have to to be made.
The main assumption is that the input forcing a stationary ergodic random process
consisting of zero mean Gaussian white noise. This will result in the contribution
from the input to disappear in covariance functions and spectral density functions
as earlier shown. In all the methods that the system under investigation should be
linear and time invariant. The last assumption is that all structural modes in the
frequency range of interest are well excited by the input forcing so that they will
show up in the measured signals.
2.5
Identiﬁcation Algorithms
This Section will be describing the investigated identiﬁcation algorithms. Identiﬁ-
cation algorithms can be divided into time domain methods and frequency domain
methods. As this thesis deals mainly with time domain algorithms, frequency do-
main methods will not be described further.
The time domain methods usually works with time decaying oscillating signals as
input. Many of the methods were originally developed to work on a set of impulse
responses. These counts the Eigensystem Realization Algorithm[12], the Ibrahim
time domain method and the Auto Regressive method. All of these methods can
be extended to work on stochastic systems where the input is unknown as the co-
variance function of the response of such a system can be used interchangeably with
a free decay as long as the fundamental OMA assumptions are not violated. This
procedure goes under the name of NExT[15], which stands for Natural Excitation
Technique and consists of the following 4 steps:
• Acquisition of response data
26
==PAGE:41==
CHAPTER 2
2.5. IDENTIFICATION ALGORITHMS
• Calculation of auto- and crosscovariance functions
• Model identiﬁcation (using any of the deterministic methods)
• Mode shape information extraction from identiﬁed modal parameters
Methods for identifying stochastic systems was developed in the 1990’s. A milestone
in the development was when Overschee and De moor published a general frame-
work for using Stochastic subspace identiﬁcation methods[3]. The book describes
algorithms using oblige and orthogonal projections to determine system parameters
from time series. The framework includes both deterministic and stochastic systems,
where the formulation for stochastic systems where only outputs are known are by
far the simplest one. It will be denoted as the SSI-data algorithm The general work
also showed that several already existing algorithms could be seen as special cases
under the subspace identiﬁcation frame work. These special cases are called Prin-
cipal component, Unweighted principal component and canonical variate analysis.
Only unweighted principal component analysis will be explained in this work. In
1999 Hermans and Van Der Auweraer[14] describes two covariance driven subspace
methods, namely the Balanced realisation and the Canonical variate analysis. The
unweighed principal component analysis can actually be seen as a reformulation of
Juangs ERA method only formulated for stochastic systems.
Common for the time domain methods is that they are all based on arranging
time series of various forms as Hankel structured block matrices in order to use
matrix decompositions to reveal the system information contained in the data. The
methods always requires a choice by the analyst on to how many rows should be
used in these block Hankel structures and how many modes the algorithm should
output. The number of rows used will be denoted as the identiﬁcation order and
the number of output will be referred to as number of identiﬁed modes. The nature
of the algorithms means that they will both output the physical system modes, but
also modes related to for example the noise in the signal and in case of the subspace
methods, the ﬁnite approximation of methods formulated for inﬁnite signals. One
of the more tedious tasks is to distinguish between physical modes and noise modes.
A visual approach to dealing with this to use a stabilization diagram which is a plot
of the identiﬁed modes as a function of model order. This will be introduced in
Chapter 3.

Stabilization Diagrams
When performing modal analysis the true number of modes in the investigated
frequency range is not necessarily known.
This fact puts a task on the analyst
to make a decision based on the captured data on to which model order is best
describing the system under investigation. To make sure all physical modes of the
system is captured, one strategy is to over-ﬁt the system with a model known to
contain more modes than the physical system. This model will then contain a mix
of physical modes describing the actual system under investigation and noise modes
or spurious modes, which presence is dictated by the over-ﬁtting. The job of the
analyst is now to pick out modes from the ﬁtted model deemed to be physical.
A generally accepted tool for this task is the stabilization diagram.
Which is a
visualization of multiple identiﬁcations undertaken on the same dataset but with
di↵erent model orders. This thesis aims at taking a step towards automatizing op-
erational modal analysis of wind turbine towers. The chosen approach is to make an
algorithm that interprets stabilization diagrams as a human analyst would. There-
fore this whole chapter is dedicated to stabilization diagrams and the algorithm
interpreting these.
3.1
Stabilization Diagrams Description
The stabilization diagram is a plot with either natural frequency on the x-axis and
model order on the y-axis. Natural frequencies identiﬁed at a given model order is
plotted as points. If a natural frequency is reoccurring at multiple orders of identi-
ﬁcation, it will form a vertical line of dots in the plot. This is the fundamental idea
behind stabilization diagrams as it can be assumed that a physical mode identiﬁed
using an over-ﬁtted model will be unchanged no matter how over-ﬁtted the model
is. Modes not repeated at di↵erent model orders can be deemed as noise modes. A
practical way of visualizing stabilization diagrams is to also include the PSD of the
signal used for identiﬁcation so that the analyst can see clearly whether the natural
frequencies of the identiﬁed modes corresponds to visual peaks in energy. Figure
3.1 shows an example of a stabilization diagram generated by the ERA algorithm.
The input for the identiﬁcation is a time series generated by the aeroelastic code
DeFlex owned by Ørsted. In the ﬁgure one can see a strong peak in the left side of
the plot, where also at least one physical mode can be identiﬁed as a vertical line
in the plot. It is also seen how noise modes are situated close to the physical one.
Further to the right, two more relatively closely spaced modes can be seen and at
higher frequencies two more modes can be seen that does not occur until a model
order of 26 and 21 respectively.

One of the motives for writing this thesis is to take a step closer towards a fully auto-

interprets a stabilization diagram as a human analyst would. The aim is that the
algorithm should work without any predeﬁned system speciﬁc threshold values. In
2012 Reynders, Houbrechts and De Roeck[21] proposed a method for an automated
interpretation of stabilization diagrams using validation criteira based on stabiliza-
tion diagrams. Their approach is used and adjusted in this work. The process can
be divided into three overall steps, which is

function.
B to clean out the stabilization diagram from noise modes by subjecting each
identiﬁed mode to a set of validation criteria which categorizes the mode as
either spurious or possibly physical
C to cluster identiﬁed possibly physical modes together into mode-sets each con-
taining identiﬁed modes deemed to belong to the same physical mode.

Hard Criteria
The hard ﬁltering criterias are the ones which has no middle ground and can take
only two values. either ”true” or ”false”. In this work 3 hard criterias are applied.
First, the natural frequency of the identiﬁed mode has to be below the Nyquist
frequency FN of the measurement. Second the mode has to be stable to be physical,
meaning that it’s real part is negative. The third criteria is due to the fact that the
structure under investigation is lightly damped. It is therefore deemed reasonable
that the modal damping ratio should be below 20%. summed up the criteria are:
fj < FN,
< (λj) < 0,

Reversing Covariance Function
A trick to further clean out the stabilization diagram when using covariance driven
methods is to reverse the covariance function before feeding it to the identiﬁcation
algorithm, meaning that instead of an oscillating logarithmic decaying sequence, you
will feed it a logarithmic growth sequence. To explain the reasoning behind this a
recap of the nature of an experimentally obtained covariance function estimate is
needed. The covariance function estimates will be a logarithmic decay not decreasing
completely to zero but instead into a noise tail which amplitude will wobble but stay
within a distinct band. When running the identiﬁcation algorithm on this sequence
the identiﬁcation algorithm of order s will attempt to describe this sequence using
a state space system with n di↵erent poles. As the signal consists of decreasing and
constant oscillations some poles will be stable and some will be marginally stable,
but stable none the less. It is a good assumption that all the physical modes will be
stable and the majority of the noise modes will be marginally stable only. The hard
criteria listed above only di↵ers between stable and unstable poles, and therefore it
does not di↵erentiate e↵ectively between noise modes and physical modes. If the
covariance function is ﬂipped however, so that it consists of a noise ﬂoor and a
logarithmic growth. In this case the poles that were previously responsible for the
logarithmic decay are now responsible for the logarithmic increase and are thereby
identiﬁed as unstable whereas the poles responsible for the noise ﬂoor will still be
marginally stable. The hard validation criteria can then be reversed to then validate
unstable poles instead and thereby discard all the marginally stable poles resulting
in a much cleaner stabilization diagram. This is visualized in Figures 3.3 and 3.4.
An example of a regular and reversed covariance function is shown in Figure 3.2.
The hard criterias that can be applied on a reversed covariance function are:
fj < FN,
< (λj) > 0,
⇣j > −0.20
It is important to note that the soft criteria ﬁltering should be performed before the
hard criteria are applied in order for the k-means clustering to work as intended.

Clustering into Mode-sets and Final Selection
The next operation in the automated stabilization diagram interpretation is the
clustering of similar modes into mode-sets containing modes identiﬁed at di↵erent
model order who is likely to be representing the same physical mode and then the
ﬁnal selection of mode-sets likely to be representing physical modes. The tool used
for this is hierarchical clustering which is a clustering method where the number of
clusters are not speciﬁed on beforehand but are decided based on the data.Initially in
a hierarchical clustering process, all data points are assigned to a separate cluster.
In the each of the following iterations the two nearest clusters are merged and a
cluster mean is calculated. This process continues until all points are merged into a
single cluster or until a threshold is reached on the euclidean distance between the
merged cluster centers. In this work the only parameter used to represent a mode is
the natural frequency fj of the identiﬁed mode. The stopping criteria dstop for the
clustering algorithm will be based on the the mean µdf and standard deviation σdf
of the natural frequency distance criteria (3.1):
dstop = µd(fj,fl) + 2σd(fj,fl)

Numerical Studies on Linear Systems
In this chapter the former described identiﬁcation algorithms are used to perform
modal parameter extraction on simulated mechanical systems excited by white noise.
The main energy dissipation of an o↵shore wind turbine at standstill is due to struc-
tural damping in the construction material. This means that it is lightly damped
system which impulse response functions will be logarithmic decaying oscillations.
The systems modelled in this chapter will then also be lightly damped with modal
damping ratios comparable to those of a wind turbine at standstill condition.
All the investigated systems will be fully linear with proportional damping and the
response time series generated by these systems will be noise free, the only variation
being the randomness associated with each excitation time series being ﬁnite. The
Automated procedure described in 3.2 is not employed in this chapter.

4.2
Identiﬁcation Algorithm Comparison
Up to this point 5 di↵erent time domain identiﬁcation algorithms has been presented
along with two di↵erent mechanical systems. All of the identiﬁcation algorithms are
working through the principle of arranging a time sequence, either a covariance func-
tion or time series, into a Hankel matrix and from there deﬁne a regression problem.
In the further work presented in this thesis the nature of the investigations will be
aimed at demonstrating the time domain methodology on wind turbine measure-
ment data. To limit the amount of experiments to carry out a single identiﬁcation
algorithm will be chosen for further work. This section discusses the choice of this
algorithm. The criteria for the choice of methods will be:
• Precision and accuracy
• Usability and ﬂexibility
• Documentation in literature
Precision and Accuracy
First it is tested if there is a systematic bias or error in any of the methods of
the covariance driven methods. The study is carried out by simply inputting the
Lyapunov solutions from the SDOF model and the 4 DOF model respectively into

Usability and Flexibility
The usability and ﬂexibility of the methods will be evaluated based on how well
the algorithms perform as universal tools applicable to any dynamic system the
user might throw at it. The main concern is how the algorithm can be adapted
in regards to the ratios between number of available response time series and the

and number of physical modes in the frequency range of interest in the investigated
system.
The number of identiﬁed modes by AR, ERA, SSI-Cov and SSI-Dat can be controlled
by adjusting the size of the initial Hankel matrix i.e. the order of the identiﬁcation
algorithm. The ITD method on the other hand is only able to identify a number of
modes equivalent to the number of simultaneously measured response time series. If
the number of available response time series is lower than the expected modes in the
system the user will be forced to make pseudo-Responses from cutting the measured
responses in fractions and using the resulting time series as separate inputs for the
algorithm.
One of the main goals with the tool developed in this thesis is that
it should be able to use SCADA accelerometer data as input, where only a single
sensor might be available from the nacelle of the turbines. For this purpose ITD is
highly impractical, and the other methods thus has a great advantage over ITD.
Documentation in Litterature
Lastly the methods are evaluated on knowledge available from litterature.
The
NExT methods AR and ERA originated in the 1980’s in a deterministic context[18][19]
and are well described in litterature. ERA is thoroughly described by Juang in [12]
and [13], which deems it a very well documented method. The theory behind SSI-
Data method is thoroughly described by Overschee and De Moor in [3]. SSI-Cov
is not as well documented as the latter two. An outline of the algorithm is given
By Hermans and Van Der Auweraer in [14]. From this work it is clear that SSI-
Cov can be seen as a combination of the theory describing ERA and SSI-Data and
also that the steps performed in the Balanced realization version of the algorithm is
practically identical to the steps of ERA. Small di↵erences do, however, occur and
these are described in more detail in Section B. in 2012 Reynders[20] carried out an
extensive comparative study of existing OMA methods, coming to the conclusion
that Subspace methods (this, technically also includes ERA) provides robust iden-
tiﬁcation of modal parameters when compared to other methods. Based on this as
well as the extensive documentation of theory it is decide to base further chapters
in this study on identiﬁcations done through ERA and SSI-Data.

Application on Measurement Data
In this section the developped identiﬁcation algorithm is employed on data obtained
from real operating wind farms. The names and locations of the windfarm as well
as the turbine types are conﬁdential. The aim of this section is to quantify the
uncertainty associated with the identiﬁcation and also display the use and limitations
of the identiﬁcation algorithm.
6.1
Single Channel Identiﬁcation - Standstill
This identiﬁcation is based upon data from a nacelle accelerometer placed in a
turbine positioned o↵shore in an anonymous wind farm. The accelerometer records
data in fore/aft and side/side directions sampling at 10 Hz. This identiﬁcation is
hereby very similar to the one undertaken in section 5.1.
The data is spread out on a seven month period from July to April. The data is
ﬁltered so that only time periods where the turbine is in standstill condition are
considered. The ﬁltering criterion is that rotation speed should be below 1 RPM.
This results in 903 standstill periods of each 2 hours duration. The brake is usually
not engaged resulting in the wind turbine being slowly idling at max 0.3 RPM.

is not excited by a strictly stationary random process. It is judged that modes 5, 7

little to the right compared to the spectral peak. It is then questionable whether
it is actually representing mode 4 from Table 6.1 or another mode which was not
excited in the initial identiﬁcation. A mode is identiﬁed at the same frequency in
the fore/aft direction, so it is highly likely that the same mode is causing movement
in both fore/aft and side/side directions, and hereby identiﬁed from both signals.
The densest cluster is by far the one associated with the lowest frequency in both
fore/aft and side/side directions. It is also clear from the spectral densities that the
energy density is very high around this mode.

7
Conclusion and Further Work
The goals of this project was split into two parts. The ﬁrst was to establish an
overview of existing time domain OMA methods and based on this develop a software
tool for time domain OMA analysis. The second was to demonstrate the capabilities
of the implemented method.
• 5 di↵erent OMA methods were considered, namely AR, ITD and ERA which
all ﬁts under the NExT framework, SSI-Cov which in a practical sense is
identical to ERA and lastly SSI-Data. From the bunch ERA was chosen as
the optimal method when usability, computation time and documentation in
literature was considered. SSI-Data delivers similar results but performs worse
in computation time.
An automated stabilization diagram interpretor was
implemented to make the method fully autonomous. The interpretor utilized
various data clustering algorithms to group the identiﬁed data. Some tweaking
of parameters like covariance function length and model order is necessary
prior to deployment to a speciﬁc system.
These choices are based on the
available sensors and number of expected modes. With the right parameter
choices the algorithm is found to identify modal parameters reliably with a
short computation time.
• Monte Carlo simulations were run for simple SDOF and 4 DOF linear me-
chanical systems showing Gaussian distributed modal parameters as long as
input response time series was of adequate length. Uncertainty was linked to
response time series length and damping level in the structure and a recom-
mendation was given for how many time periods of an oscillation is needed in
a response to do satisfying modal parameter identiﬁcation. When employed
on a large dataset generated by aeroelastic software the identiﬁcation algo-
rithm with the autonomous stabilization diagram interpretor proved able to
reliably identify a selection of well exited modes. The lowest modes where
most reliably identiﬁed.
• It was also possible to identify several modes from a large dataset containing
measurements from a single nacelle accelerometer. This resulted in a statistical
analysis of 903 2 hour periods which happened over the course of a year.
The uncertainty was higher for these identiﬁcations compared to the ones
from the aeroelastic simulations and the distributions for the damping ratios
where slightly skewed, possibly due to the high standard deviation of the
estimates. Finally the two lowest bending mode shapes in both fore/aft and
side/side directions of an o↵shore turbine was identiﬁed using the identiﬁcation
algorithm without the autonomous stabilization diagram interpretor activated.
The identiﬁcation was done based on a 20 minutes time series to ensure as
stationary loading as possible. The input data included very closely spaced
modes which the algorithm identiﬁed without problems.

7.1. FURTHER WORK
7.1
Further Work
1. Inﬂuence of loading conditions: The theory behind OMA applies when the
input loading is stationary Gaussian white noise. An interesting investigation
is what inﬂuence it has on the identiﬁed modal parameters if input is non-
stationary and coloured. A study could be carried out on the simple linear
mechanical models used in this thesis.
2. Model updating: The OMA results could be used to update the structural
model of the wind turbine in real time, giving the operators a tool to more
precisely update the lifetime estimates of the turbines.
3. Sensor Placement: To measure as many mode shapes as possible with as
little sensors as possible the placement of these sensors are of high importance.
A study could be carried out on the optimal placement to capture interesting
modes in the structures.
4. Identiﬁcation of damping devices in wind turbines: Damping devices
are widely used in wind turbines and it is relevant to document their e↵ect on
the structures. A study can be carried out where identiﬁcation is performed
before and after activation of a damping device followed by a comparison of
the results.
5. Full system identiﬁcation of Wind turbine: This project was limited to
analyzing data from sensors mounted in the turbine towers and nacelles. An
interesting scope is to perform OMA on a full wind turbine structure where
sensors are mounted in blades, shaft and tower, to map the complete system
and catch a full picture of the turbines modes.
6. Identiﬁcation of turbines in operation: a study can be carried out where
it is investigated whether the modal parameters of the structure itself can be
extracted from a wind turbine in operation.
This would be very useful in
monitoring properties of for example the foundations over time.
7. Identiﬁcation of wave time periods: during the work with this project
it was found that the peaks in wave spectra was often identiﬁed as spurious
modes in the identiﬁcation. There is a potential that this could be utilized
to gather information on wave time periods at wind farms without having to
deploy measurement bouys.

Comparison of ERA and SSI-Cov
Both ERA and SSI-Cov are based on using controllability- and observability matrices
obtained from a Hankel structure to estimate the state space system matrices A
and C The di↵erences between the Identiﬁcations obtained using these methods
will therefore be subtle. As it is the aim of this thesis to ﬁnd the optimal method
from a robustness and automation point of view, some attention will in this Section
be given to asses theses subtle di↵erences between the two approaches. Actually the
only di↵erence algorithm-wise lies in the formulation that estimates Ad. In ERA
Equation (2.136) estimates Ad using the singular value decomposition of the initial
block Hankel matrix. SSI-Cov uses the singular value decomposition of the Block
Hankel matrix to deﬁne an observability matrix for the system which is then used
to formulate a least squares problem to estimate Ad. As an extra option this least
square problem can be solved by regular inversion (2.79) or by the Moore-Penrose
inverse (2.81) Where B is an arbitrary m ⇥n matrix with m > n, or the Moore-
Penrose inverse can be used by doing a singular value decomposition. These di↵erent
formulations will not change the identiﬁcation of the physical modes in the system
for this simulation case, but the poles of the additional noise modes identiﬁed by the
methods are signiﬁcantly di↵erent. This can be visualized by plotting the identiﬁed
discrete poles in the complex plane along with the unit circle. The discrete poles
describe a stable mode if they fall inside the unit circle and an unstable mode if they
fall outside. This visualization is made in ﬁgures B.1 and B.2 using the three di↵erent
methods where the number n of returned modes is 4 and 200 respectively. Since the
system has 4 natural frequencies, the 4 modes showed in Figure B.1 has physical
meaning and are identically distributed in the complex plane across the 3 di↵erent
methods. For the case showed in Figure B.2 where 200 modes are returned by the
methods, all poles with the exception of 4 will be associated with noise modes, which
placement in the complex plane is dependant only on the identiﬁcation method used
as the identiﬁcations is run on the same time series. It is seen how the identiﬁed
discrete poles are placed much closer to the edge of the unit circle when using the
SSI-Cov method.
