Abstract
Filamentous fungi have great impacts on society, and understanding them is of interest both to control
their risks and to utilise them for sustainable production of chemicals, enzymes and pharmaceuticals.
As pathogens they cause huge damage to food supplies worldwide and cause illness due to their
mycotoxins or through infections. Industrially fungi are widely utilised for production of chemicals
and they are especially well suited for protein/enzyme production, because of their high capacity for
secretion due to their unique morphology.
Developing genetic toolboxes is essential to study and engineer fungi and the emerging CRISPR-
Mad7 technology hold potential to allow more companies to utilise the power of CRISPR genome
editing with a clear licensing agreement. The two industrially promising filamentous fungi Aspergillus
brunneoviolaceus and Penicillium brevicompactum were chosen as representative of industrially
promising fungi to have the genes ku70 and pyrG deleted, creating strains optimised for future efficient
genetic engineering. Because of issues with the sequence of the P. brevicompactum strain available it
was not possible to design CRISPR-Mad7 vectors for this strain and attempts at sequencing the the
genes of interest were not successful. A. brunneoviolaceus ΔpyrG strains were created with CRISPR-
Mad7 mediated knock-out, although the deletion led to the loss of ~350 base pairs downstream of
pyrG. Using the strain BRU4 it was demonstrated that the pyrG selection marker functions in A.
brunneoviolaceus . The biosynthetic potential of A. brunneoviolaceus was assayed with antiSMASH,
which showed it possess nearly 100 putative biosynthetic gene clusters with many seemingly novel
clusters.

Introduction
Filamentous fungi are a diverse group of organisms that can be found all throughout the world.
They have long been utilised by humans for the production of a wide range of products including
food, pharmaceuticals, enzymes, and chemicals [1, 2]. With enzymes and chemical part of the
biotechnology industry alone being estimated to be worth more than C400 billion dollars [2]. Large
economic gains can be made through more efficient engineering of fungi [2]. While fungi have
proved useful for industrial production, they also pose risk to human health and agricultural crops,
infecting both plants and animals [3].
With potential to be utilised and risks to be managed, developing genetic tools to efficiently research
and characterise fungi is of great importance to researches and industries alike.
1.1
The fungal kingdom
Within the eukaryota domain all fungi belong to the unique fungal kingdom separate from the plant
kingdom and animal kingdom [4]. Fungi are a very diverse group of organisms, with lifestyles
ranging from unicellular yeast to complex organisms forming fruiting bodies like mushrooms, leading
to huge variance in the morphology [5]. Fungi have been observed to reproduce through both sexual
and asexual means [5]. When sexual state of fungi is called teleomorph and the asexual state called
anamorph. Some fungi species both states of reproduction have been observed, while in other species
only one of the reproduction sates have been observed [5]. Many fungi produce spores as part of their
reproduction. Airborne spores allow fungi to spread and find new nutrients rich areas, that they can
colonise and utilise to produce new spores once again. A characteristic growth pattern found in many
fungi is hyphal structures where cells create long filaments.
1.1.1
Filamentous fungi
Filamentous fungi are not a clear taxonomic division as fungi from different phyla are referred to
as filamentous fungi, this group of organisms are characterised by their branching hyphal growth
forming the filamentous structure [5]. The morphology of filamentous fungi vary greatly from one
genus to another genus [6]. The hyphal growth plays an important role in making filamentous
fungi such efficient producers of enzymes. At the tip of the hyphae a structure known as the
spitzenkörper forms as the fungi grows [7]. To release enzymes that can breakdown and absorb

1.2
Use of fungi
One of the key uses of filamentous fungi in the biotechnology industry is the production of enzymes
[1]. Enzymes are used in a variety of applications, including the production of biofuels, detergents,
and textiles [1, 2]. Filamentous fungi are particularly well-suited for enzyme production due to their
naturally high secretory capacity of proteins, making downstream processing of the product easier

and cheaper [2]. They are also capable of producing a wide range of enzymes, making them possible
host for a myriad types of enzymes, like lipases, proteases and cellulases [1, 2].
Filamentous fungi are also used in the pharmaceutical industry for the production of a wide range of
drugs and other products. Filamentous fungi are used to produce antibiotics, such as penicillin, as
well as a variety of other drugs, such as pharmaceutical proteins [2].
Overall, filamentous fungi are an important resource for the biotechnology, pharmaceutical, and
food industries. These fungi have a wide range of applications and are used to produce a variety
of products that have a significant impact on our daily lives. As research continues to advance in
this area, it is likely that the use of filamentous fungi will continue to expand, leading to even more
innovative and valuable products. However only a few species are established as production hosts,
but the few hosts available might not be optimal for production. It is therefore of value to explore
more filamentous fungi species to find both new enzymes and metabolites, but also to find new and
potentially better production hosts.
1.2.1
Unexplored fungi
It is still uncertain how many fungal species there are in world where one study estimates the number
to be between 2.2 to 3.8 million species [9], with another study raising the estimate to as high as 6
million species [10]. Still with the estimates in the range of millions, only around 148,000 species
of fungi have been described [11]. Of the described species only few species within the Aspergillus
genus are well characterised and used for industrial production, with Aspergillus niger being the
main industrial work horse [1, 2, 12]. The few established cell factories are not necessarily the
most efficient at producing a specific product, but are simply used by companies as they only have
the genetic tools and facilities to work with them. New potential cell factories are therefore often
overlooked due to lack of tools to work with them, and unwillingness to risk moving away from
established platforms. Novel fungi species can potentially prove to be more efficient at producing
specific products, for example offering better substrate utilisation or better secretion capacity.
In this project the filamentous fungus Aspergillus brunneoviolaceus is used as an example of a
potential future production host or a species producing novel compounds. A. brunneoviolaceus is part
of the Nigri section within the Aspergillus genus [9, 13]. It is within this section of Aspergillus some
of most industrial important fungi, like A. niger, are found [13]. Fungi within the Nigri section are
generally known for producing a wide range of secondary metabolites and enzymes, making them

only genetic engineering that had been carried out was CRISPR-Cas9 mediated deletion of the genes
ku70 and pyrG. The strain generated from the preliminary study will also be part of this master thesis,
where it is validated whether the modifications to the strain can be useful for the further study and
genetic engineering.
1.2.2
Industrial relevant fungi Penicillium brevicompactum
Since the antibiotic penicillin was first isolated from Penicillium rubens, fungi from the Penicillium
genus have played a huge role in the discovery and production of pharmaceuticals [1, 16]. Another ex-
ample of such fungal derived pharmaceutical is mycophenolic acid (MPA). The secondary metabolite
was first isolated from P. brevicompactum by Bartolomeo Gosio in 1893 [17]. It has over the years
been found to have antibacterial [18, 19], antifungal [20] and antiviral [21, 22] properties. However
its main use now is as an immunosuppressant drug used in organ transplantation patients [23].
The extensive use of it therefore makes it of great interest to study its biosynthesis, and how to
potentially optimise its production in P. brevicompactum . The polyketide synthase responsible for
the synthesis of MPA in P. brevicompactum was first described by Regueira et al. [24]. Here they

filamentous fungi.
1.3
CRISPR technology in molecular biology and fungal systems
The role of Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats (CRISPR) found in bacteria,
was first proposed as a phage immune response system in 2005 by Mojica et al [27]. In 2007 a study by
Barrangou et al. published the first evidence of the mechanism by which CRISPR functioned as phage
immune response system [28]. In 2012 Martin Jinek, Krzysztof Chylinski, Ines Fonfara, Michael
Hauer, Jennifer A. Doudna, and Emmanuelle Charpentier first reported the use of CRISPR-Cas9
programmed with RNA to introduce site specific double stranded breaks (DSB) to edit the genome
of organisms [29]. Its ability to be quickly programmed and versatility lead to its use in all kinds
of organisms including yeast, animals, and importantly filamentous fungi [30]. The CRISPR-Cas9
technology has however been caught in a legal battle since 2014 about who owns the right and patent
for the system, making it unattractive for commercial use [31]. Therefore alternative CRISPR systems
utilising different endonucleases other than Cas9 have started to be developed. An example of such
is the CRISPR-Mad7 system [32]. In this section the mechanism of both Cas9 and Mad7 will be
explored along this the potential upsides and downsides of each system is looked at.

1.3.1
The CRISPR-Cas9 system
Having been used in mammalian cells, plants, yeast and filamentous fungi, the Cas9 endonuclease
is the most widely used CRISPR system [29, 30, 33, 34]. Thereby its ubiquity for use in genome
engineering have been proven across many cell systems.
Cas9 being an endonuclease means that it is able to make double stranded breaks in DNA. The
major components of the CRISPR-Cas9 system is the Cas9 enzyme itself and the single-guide RNA
(sgRNA). The sgRNA itself is made of a hybrid RNA molecule consisting of crRNA and tracrRNA,
see Figure 2 [29]. crRNA contains the guide sequence complementary the target sequence on the
genome, known as the protospacer. tracrRNA binds to the crRNA and matures it forming the sgRNA
that can bind to Cas9 and work as a guide for where to make the cut in the genome [29]. The length
of the protospacer for Cas9 is 20 base pair (bp) followed by a 3 bp PAM sequence [29]. The PAM
sequence is not part of the sgRNA, but is recognised by the Cas9 enzyme. For Cas9 the consensus
sequence for PAM is 5’-NGG-3’ [29]. How the Cas9 complex binds and interacts with the protospacer
is illustrated in Figure 2. This means the sgRNA must be complementary to the 20 bp protospacer
just upstream of the PAM sequence. The Cas9 enzymes makes blunt end cuts in DNA, 3 bp upstream
of the PAM sequence, an illustration of the cut pattern can be seen in Figure 2 [29].

While CRISPR-Cas9 have proved revolutionary for academic research, it is not in wide use in
industrial settings for the development of new productions strains, despite its potential to quickly
create new strains. This is due to the licence and patent legal battle that have been ongoing since 2014
[31]. The unclear ownership of CRISPR-Cas9 have made it near impossible and too risky for large
companies to licence the technology and use it. As a Cas9 engineered production strain, made under
the license from one group, could potentially have to be re-licensed if the patent changed hands once
again. This could prove to be very expensive for companies that might have to enter new agreements
where the terms of use to change, to keep their production strains. For this reason alternatives the
CRISPR-Cas9 are being explored, to enable companies to utilise the versatility and effectiveness of
CRISPR mediated genome editing.
1.3.2
The industrially catered CRISPR-Mad7 system
An emerging alternative to Cas9 has been the Mad7 endonuclease, also known as ErCas12a [35]. The
company Inscripta own the exclusive rights to the CRISPR-Mad7 system, and provide clear terms
of use and a agreeable licensing agreement compared to Cas9 [29]. The use of Cas9 in filamentous
fungi have been well established [30, 36–38]; whereas few studies have described the use of Mad7 in
fungi [39, 40]. The use of Mad7 has however been established in mammalian cells and the bacteria
Bacillus subtilis, showing it does have potential to work in a wide range of organisms [32, 41].
As Mad7 is related the the Cas12a/Cpf1 type of CRISPR systems, it has some differences in its
mechanisms compared to Cas9 [35]. The PAM sequence recognised by Mad7 has the sequence
5’-TTTN-3’ with N not being a T [35]. The longer PAM sequence does mean that it appears less
often in the genome of organisms, giving fewer options for protospacers compared to Cas9. The PAM
sequence is also found upstream of the protospacer recognised by the gRNA, see Figure 3 for how
Mad7 binds to DNA [35]. The DSB made by Mad7 produces sticky ends unlike the blunt end break
made by Cas9. One of the major differences between Mad7 is how the guide RNA (gRNA) is made,
as no tracrRNA is employed by Mad7, it can itself mature the crRNA into functional gRNA [35]. The
non-recognising part of the gRNA is also shorter than Cas9, with Cas9 needing 80 bp to make up the
crRNA, whereas Mad7 uses a 35 bp crRNA. The length of the protospacer needed for Mad7 also
differs a bit between Cas9 and Mad7, with Cas9 sgRNA using 20 bp to recognise the target, Mad7
uses 21 bp target sequence, although this makes little to no difference.

As an alternative to CRISPR-Cas9 the CRISPR-Mad7 system has shown promise to be a potential
candidate, especially for industrial use due to its clear licensing agreement. An important part of
exploring new fungi, or establishing new cell factories is having strong genetic toolbox that allows
for genetic engineering and easy to work with selection markers. Here CRISPR technologies can be a
powerful tool for establishing the desired strains.
1.4
Engineering laboratory strains
To prepare fungi for research it is desirable to knock out specific genes, where these knockouts make
them more convenient and efficient for research and potentially as production hosts. The two genes
that are of interest are ku70 and pyrG. By knocking out ku70 the non-homologous end joining (NHEJ)
repair pathway is disrupted, allowing for more efficient genetic engineering utilising homologous
recombination. Knock out of pyrG is done to create an auxotrophy marker, removing the need for
toxic antimicrobials for transformation selection.
1.4.1
Selection markers in filamentous fungi
When making mutants it is essential to be able to select for the desired genotype/phenotype, that is
being pursued. Selection markers are usually genes that encode some phenotype that only allows
successful transformants to survive some kind of selection pressure. The most common methods
for this is either selection for resistance to a antimicrobial compound or auxotrophic markers. The
antibiotic/antimicrobial compound hygromycin B functions by inhibiting protein synthesis in bacteria,
fungi and higher eukaryotes and has been used as a selection marker for fungi [42–44]. The inhibition
functions by stabilising tRNA binding sites on the ribosome [45]. The tRNA substrate now bound to
the acceptor site prevents the ribosome performing translation. However the problem with using the
hygromycin B is its toxicity, making it unpractical to work with requiring extra safety measures and
transformation steps.
It is therefore desirable to establish a different way to select for transformants when doing genetic en-
gineering in fungi. A possible approach to this is creating laboratory strains that have an auxothrophy
for a certain nutrient, so selection can be based on supplementation of a simple nutrient. In both yeast
and filamentous fungi a common gene to target is been the pyrG gene. This gene encodes for the

enzyme orotidine 5’-phosphate decarboxylase (OMP decarboxylase), that catalyses the reaction of
orotidine monophosphate to uridine monophosphate. This is an essential part of the biosynthesis of the
pyrimidine nucleotides uridine triphosphate, cytidine triphosphate and thymidine triphosphate, which
are all key in biosynthesis of RNA and DNA. Without the ability to synthesis these compounds the
organism will die, as it can not synthesis RNA or DNA anymore. By deleting this gene an auxotrophy
for uracil, or just uridine is created as these nutrients can make up for the missing synthesis step.
Thereby transformants can not grow without being supplemented with uridine/uracil in the media,
making it possible to screen for them by growing transformants on selective media. This can then be
utilised to select for successful transformants by restoring pyrG gene function using a pyrG that is
transformed together with desired DNA. The selection can then be carried out simply using media
with and without uridine and uracil being supplemented, allowing for simpler and safer workflow,
compared to using antimicrobials like hygromycin B.
An added benefit of deleting pyrG is that 5-fluoroorotic acid (5-FOA) can be used as an additional
level of selection [46, 47]. If a organism is producing OMP decarboxylase it will metabolise 5-FOA
into the compound fluorouracil (5-FU), which is toxic and will kill the organism [47]. This can be
used to confirm successful deletion of pyrG when establishing the uracil/uridine auxotrophy in the
first place, as mutants with the ΔpyrG genotype can grow in media containing 5-FOA. When then
using ΔpyrG strains for further genetic engineering, 5-FOA counterselection can then be utilised
as successful transformants can now not grow on media supplemented with 5-FOA. Deletion of the
pyrG gene therefore opens for creating strains that allow for efficient genetic engineering.
To further increase the efficiency of genome editing, the DNA repair systems in fungi can be
manipulated.
1.4.2
Knocking out DNA repair pathways in filamentous fungi
The DNA repair pathway non-homologous end joining is utilised by eukaryotes to repair DSB in DNA.
The pathway involves several proteins that mediate the repair, those proteins being: DNA-dependent
protein kinase catalytic subunit (DNA-PKcs), the Ku70–Ku80 heterodimer, and DNA ligase [48].
The repair is initiated by the Ku70-Ku80 hetreodimer binding to the ends of the DNA at the DSB, see
Figure 4 for an overview of the steps involved. The heterodimer helps protect the ends from further
degradation and recruits the DNA-PK, which then recruits the DNA ligase to ligate the ends back
together again [49]. The NHEJ pathway does not utilise any template, and is therefore by nature more

A widely used way to suppress the NHEJ pathway have been to delete the gene encoding the Ku70
protein, thereby not allowing the Ku70-Ku80 heterodimer to form and bind to the DSB and initiate
the repair pathway [40, 49, 50]. Making strains that are deficient in NHEJ has therefore become
an important tool to make more efficient genome editing using CRISPR systems, as it utilises the
homology-directed repair (HDR) pathway to make modification. By knocking-out the NHEJ pathway
cells are forced to use HDR to repair CRISPR induced DSB and by providing a custom repair template
any number of mutations can be made, from single point mutations too whole gene deletions or
insertion of genes from the provided repair template [49].
The HDR pathway utilise homologous recombination (HR) to repair DNA damage, by using a
template homologous to the area where the break has happened. It is this use of a homologous
template that is exploited when creating a DSB with CRISPR, where the addition of repair template
in the transformation allows for the HDR pathway to do the repair.

1.4.3
CRISPR mediated deletion of ku70 and pyrG
To achieve the knock out of pyrG and ku70 in filamentous fungi a plasmid based approach is used to
express a CRISPR enzyme and the gRNA along side resistance genes for both fungal and bacterial
selection [30]. The gene encoding a CRISPR enzyme, either Mad or Cas9 in this case, have been
codon optimised for expression A. niger, and is put under expression control of the constitutive A.
nidulans tef1 promoter and terminator, to ensure high expression of CRISPR enzyme [30, 40]. The
expression of the gRNA and tRNA cassette is put under expression of U3 polymerase III promoter
and terminator from A. fumigatus. For the assembly of the CRISPR vectors a USER cloning based
approach can be used. Fragments consisting of the promoter, terminator and the tRNA/gRNA cassette
can be made with PCR such that they form tails USER compatible overhangs consisting of the
protospacer sequence. Using the USER technique the whole cassette is assembled and inserted
into the backbone plasmid also through USER cloning. In Figure 5 an overview of the process is
illustrated using the structure of a CRISPR-Mad7 vector as an example. The cloned plasmids can
then be transformed into E. coli and selected for with ampicillin.
Figure 5: Assembly of a CRISPR-Mad7 vector, with four protospacers for different target sites. Five
PCR fragments are cloned into a CRISPR-Mad7 backbone through USER cloning. Figure modified
from Nødvig et al. to represent the Mad7 vector assembly [36].
The gRNA is released from its expression cassette using a glycine tRNA based splicing approach
[36, 40]. A hybrid molecule of tRNA and the gRNA with the protospacer is formed. Using native
RNase Z and RNase P activity that recognise sites in the tRNA, cuts are made what can release the
multiple gRNA molecules, a schematic of how the hybrid molecule is spliced can be seen in Figure 6.
This allows for multiple different gRNAs to expressed together and then be released to bind with a
CRISPR enzyme. By having multiple gRNA expressed together multiplexing where one plasmid can

As the purpose in this study is deletion of two genes the target for the gRNA is up-and downstream
of the target genes, ku70 and pyrG, to allow of more efficient full gene deletion. To facilitate the
deletion by HDR a repair template of 60 bp ssDNA is provided, here called a gene editing oligo
(GE-oligo) [36]. This has homology upstream of cut that occurs upstream of the gene and homology
downstream of the downstream cut. The repair template means that the fungi can use HDR pathway
and thereby completely delete the gene by following the template. The deletion can also occur as
a result of NHEJ, where the DSBs are resolved by ligating an end from each of the DSB together,
see Figure 7. A last possibility is that the gene might not be deleted, but the repairs of cut site DSB
through NHEJ create an erroneous repair resulting in disruption of the gene.

Materials and methods
In this section methods used in the project are described.
2.1
Growth media and miscellaneous solutions
For all general growth of A. brunneoviolaceus and P. brevicompactum minimal media (MM) was
used. The composition of the MM used can be seen in Table 9 in the Appendix 8.1. For mutants
which were pyrG deficient uridine was added to the media to a final concentration of 10 mM in the
media.
The antimicrobial selection marker hygromycin B was added to a final concentration of 80 μg/ml
when used for selecting transformants. Other concentration ranging from 25 μg/ml to 200 μg/ml were
used for the hygromycin B sensitivity assay. MM plates containing 5-FOA was used as a selective
media for mutants with the pyrG gene deleted. The concentration of 5-FOA in the plates used were
1.3 mg/ml.
For fungal transformations the transformation media (TM) was used for growth, its composition can
be found in Table 9. Like MM uridine was added to a concentration of 10 mM for cultivation pyrG
deficient strains. Hygromycin B was added to a final concentration of 80 μg/ml.
For over night (O/N) liquid cultures of fungi YPD media was used. Composition of the media used
can be found in Table 9. Plates of YPD was used for spore dissection. When making liquid YPD no
bacto agar was added.
For cultivation of E. coli LB media with ampicillin was used. The concentration of ampicillin in the
final media was 0.1 mg/ml. Bacto agar was not added to the media when it was needed for liquid
cultures. The composition if the LB can be found in Table 9.
All media was autoclaved to sterilise it before use.
For protoplastation, fungal transformations and purification of genomic DNA, various stock solutions
were used. Table 10 in Appendix 8.2 contains the composition of theses solutions.

2.3
Design of primers for sequencing ku70 and pyrG genes
Primers used for PCR amplification of fragments to be sequenced and for diagnostic PCR to verify
deletions were designed using the primer design tool within CLC Workbench. The primers were
ordered from Integrated DNA Technologies.
Primer pairs used for diagnostic PCR were designed to bind upstream and downstream of the target
gene, so gene deletions could be detected.
Primers to be used for sequencing were designed to bind upstream, downstream and within the gene
to allow for full gene sequencing. The spacing of the primers the primers were between ~600 bp to
~1000 bp.
2.4
Sequencing of ku70 and pyrG
Sequencing was handled by Eurofins Genomics using their Mix2Seq kit. PCR fragments purified
using gel purification were used as templates for the Sanger sequencing. As specified by the Mix2Seq
kit the concentrations of the DNA send for sequencing was 10 ng/μl together with a sequencing
primer with sterile Milli-Q (MQ) water added to a total volume of 10 μl.

2.4.1
Analysis of sequencing results using CLC Workbench
Sequencing result files were used for alignment with sequences file obtained of the genes from JGI
MycoCosm [51]. To compare the results to the reference gene the CLC Workbench BLAST tool was
utilised. The BLAST alignment could then be inspected for differences in the sequence, and provide
stats of the similarity and gaps between sequences.
2.5
Cultivation temperature assay of P. brevicompactum
Six MM plates with three-point inoculations of P. brevicompactum were made. Each plate was
incubated at a different temperature: 15 °C, 20 °C, 25 °C, 28 °C, 30 °C and 37 °C. The plates were
incubated for seven days at the respective temperatures. After seven day the diameters of the colonies
was measured.
2.6
Hygromycin sensitivity assay
Five MM plates with different concentrations of hygromycin B of 0 μg/ml, 25 μg/ml, 50 μg/ml, 100
μg/ml and 200 μg/ml were prepared for inoculation. Using a metal plate with a grid of pegs dipped in
96 % ethanol and then flame sterilised, four indents were made in the plates by gently pressing it into
the plates. 5 μl of spore suspension of varying concentrations of spores were added to each indent.
The spore concentrations used were 1 · 107, 1 · 106, 1 · 105 and 1 · 104. The plates were incubated
at the optimum temperature for the fungi for four to seven days. After the incubation inhibition of
growth could be observed and evaluated.
2.7
Constructing CRISPR vectors for A. brunneoviolaceus
Strains from cryo-stock of E. coli DH5α carrying the desired template and backbone plasmids were
used to inoculate 5 mL of liquid LB+amp media. The cultures were grown O/N at 37 °C with 150
rpm of shaking, before being used for plasmids purification. Using a GenElute Plasmid Miniprep kit
from Sigma Aldrich, the plasmids were purified from 4 mL of the LB+amp culture.

2.7.1
Verifying structure of plasmids with restriction digestion
To verify the structure of the plasmids were correct, before starting the PCR, cloning and transfor-
mations, restriction digests were performed on purified plasmids. Suitable restriction enzymes were
chosen based on their digest pattern and availability. The enzymes used for restriction digests were
PstI, XhoI and BspEI depending on the which plasmid was analysed. The reaction mixture volume
was 11 μl, consisting of 1 μl buffer, 7 μl sterile MQ water, 1 μl enzyme and 2 μl of plasmid solution.
For PstI and BspEI the buffer NEBuffer™r3.1 were used and the buffer for XhoI digestion was NEB
rCutSmart™. The reactions were incubated for 1 hour and 30 minutes at 37 °C. The digestion was
checked by running the product on 1% agarose gel with SYBR Safe at 100 volts for 20 minuets. The
agarose gel was pictured using a Bio Rad Gel Doc XR.
2.7.2
Obtaining protospacer+gRNA+tRNA fragments with PCR

made using the plasmid pDIV309 as a template for Mad7 vectors, and pFC902 for Cas9 vectors.

of the protospacer sequence thereby creating the gRNA in the plasmid. The tails with the protospacer
sequence are designed to be compatible with USER cloning. The reaction mixture consisted of 1
μl of purified plasmid, 10 μl CxL buffer, 5 μl dNTP, 2 μl of each primer, 0.5 μl Pfu x7 polymerase
and 29.5 μl sterile MQ water. The reaction was run using a Bio-rad T100 Thermal Cycler.. The
touchdown-PCR program used for the mixture was:

2. 98 °C - 15 seconds

5. 72 °C - 5 minutes

2.7.3
Linearising plasmids for USER cloning
To prepare plasmids to be used for USER cloning they needed to be linearised and cut to create
USER compatible overhangs. To linearise the restriction enzyme PacI was used. Two restriction
digest reactions were ran in parallel with respectively 1 μg and 2 μg of plasmids in the reaction. The
digest mixtures were 1 μg or 2 μg plasmids, 10 μl 10x rCutsmart buffer, 2 μl PacI and sterile MQ
water to a total volume of 100 μl. The digestions were ran over night at 37 °C. Afterwards the whole
mixture was loaded on 1 % agarose gels and run at 100 V for 20 minutes in 1 % TAE buffer. From
the gels a band of >10000 bp linearised plasmid was purified using the same Cystiva gel purification
kit mentioned earlier. After the purification step, the USER overhangs was generated in the plasmid.
Again two digest reaction are run using the different concentrations of plasmids. Rest of the digest
mixture consisted of 5 μl rCutsmart buffer, 2 μl Nt.BbvcI, 1 μl PacI and sterile MQ water to a total
volume of 50 μl. The digestions were done at 37 °C for 2 hours. After the 2 hours an additional 1 μl
of Nt.BbvcI was added and the digest was run for an additional 2 hours at 37 °C, and then 30 minutes
at 80 °C to inactivate the enzymes. Linearised plasmids with USER cloning compatible overhangs
could now be used for USER cloning.

2.8
Preparing protoplasts
Liquid over night cultures of A. brunneoviolaceus and P. brevicompactum were prepared. 100 mL of
YPD was added to a sterile shake flask. Around 2 mL of YPD was poured from the flask onto a plate
with a fully grown three-point inoculation of the desired fungus. Using a Drigalski spatula dipped in
96% ethanol and then flame sterilised, spores and biomass was harvested by rubbing the colonies with
the YPD. The YPD was then transferred from the plate back to the flask using a pipette with filter
tips. The flask was incubated at 30 °C for A. brunneoviolaceus and at 25 °C for P. brevicompactum
with 150 rpm shaking over night.
Next day the biomass was separated from the YPD using an autoclaved funnel with miracloth. The
liquid was discarded and the biomass in the filter was washed with APB. With a sterile spoon around
5 mL of biomass was transferred to a 50 mL Falcon tube. 15 mL of APB with 40 mg/ml Glucanex
dissolved in it, was added through a sterile filter to the biomass. The tubes with the biomass and
APB/GLucanex mixture was incubated at 30 °C for 2.5 hours with shaking.
The biomass was removed using miracloth with liquid going through the filter being collected in
a new 50 mL tube. ABP was added to the collected liquid to a total volume of 40 mL. 5 mL of a
50% mix of ATP and MQ water was carefully added on top of the 40 mL APB. The tube was then
centrifuged for 11 minutes at 3000 g. The acceleration was set to 9 with the deceleration set to 5. A
white halo had formed around were the boundary the APB and ATB had been. The halo was carefully
collected into a new 50 mL tube. ATB was then added to the collected halo for total volume of 40
mL. The tube was then centrifuged for 11 minutes at 3000 g. The acceleration was set to 9 with the

2.9
Transformation of fungi
10 μl of the plasmid and the GE-oligos, to be transformed into the fungal protoplast was added to a
tube. 200 μl of protoplast was added to the tube and the protoplast and DNA is left to incubate on ice
for 15 minutes. After the 15 minutes 150 μl of PCT was added to the tube. The PCT was mixed in
with the protoplast by inverting the tube gently. The mix was incubated at room temperature for 15
minutes. The mixture was diluted with 250 μl of ATB. Depending on which selecting marker was
utilised, different approaches was used for the plating of the protoplast.
2.9.1
Hygromycin B marker based transformation
15 mL of 60 °C TM agar was poured into a 50 mL tube. When the media had cooled down to around
40 °C hygromycin B was added to a concentration of 80 μg/ml. If the transformation would target
deletion of pyrG then the media was also supplemented with uridine using sterile filtered uridine
solution. The whole protoplast and DNA mixture then added to the still liquid TM agar, and mixed by
inverting the tube. The mix of protoplast and TM agar was poured on top of a TM plate as an overlay.
The plates were set to incubate at 30 °C. The next day another overlay of TM agar with supplements
was added on top. After 2 days another overlay of TM agar with supplements was added on top again.
Colonies of transformants were allowed to grow up trough the overlays and develop spores, which
were used for isolation of the transformants.
2.9.2
pyrG marker based transformation
The whole protoplast and DNA mix was transferred to a TM plate, and spread using a Drigalski
spatula dipped in 96 % ethanol and then flame sterilised. The plates were incubated at 30 °C for 4
days and checked for growth.

2.10.4
Spore dissection to isolate strains from single spores
Spore were collected by pipetting 30 μl sterile MQ water up and down on the re-streaked colonies.
The water now containing spores was transferred to top of a thick and level YPD held at a step angel,
so the water ran down making a streak down along the plate. The plate was placed under a microscope
equipped with a capillary needle for manipulating and moving single spores. Five spores from each
isolate was moved from the streak to the other side of the plate. The YPD plates were incubated at 30
°C to allow the single spores to germinate and form a single colony.
Single colonies from the YPD were once again re-streaked, with each mutant being re-streaked on
the same types of media as the initial re-streak.

Afterwards the tube was spun down using a table top centrifuge. 150 μl of the supernatant was
transferred to a new 1.5 mL tube. 15 μl of 5 M NaCl and 400 μl ice cold 96% ethanol was added

Not all experimental goals were met during this study, however the knowledge gained from the results
can still be of use to further genetic engineering in filamentous fungi. In the following sections the
results are discussed further and how they can be used for optimising future work.
4.1
The impact of growth characteristics on genetic engineering
Understanding the fundamental growth characteristics of the fungal strains that were to be used for
the project, was essential to perform further assays and experiments with the fungi.
Growing A. brunneoviolaceus at five different temperatures in the range 20 °C to 37 °C revealed its
optimal growth temperature to be around 30°C, with its growth completely inhibited at 37 °C. It was
able to completely fill the MM plate incubated at 30 °C after four days of growth. Compared to other
Aspergillus spp. such as A. niger and A. nidulans this is a comparably low optimum temperature
with the two other Aspergilli having their optimum at 35 °C - 37 °C [53]. A. brunneoviolaceus close
relative A. fijiensis does not have its growth completely inhibited at 37 °C [15]. So despite the close
relation between the two species, different conditions are a necessity for optimal cultivation. Being
incapable of growing at 37 °C indicate that A. brunneoviolaceus does not pose as a threat for being
a invasive pathogen to humans. It might however still produce mycotoxins, that can be harmful to
humans.
The same assay of P. brevicompactum IBT 23078 revealed it had a lower optimum temperature in
the range of 20 °C - 25 °C and only reached colony sizes of ~20 mm after seven days when growing
on MM plates. The optimum temperature and growth speed does match the growth described for
P. brevicompactum in the literature [54, 55]. The slow growth might impede the workflow of later
experiments as extra planning has to be considered when designing the experiments to take into
account its growth characteristics. Another consequence of having to cultivate P. brevicompactum
at lower temperatures is the efficiency of CRISPR enzymes are lowered, as both CRISPR-Cas9 and
CRISPR-Mad7 work most efficiently at 37 °C.
To combat the lowered efficiency an approach that could be utilised when working with P. brevicom-
pactum would be to use a two-phase transformation protocol for filamentous fungi as described in
Vanegas et al. [40]. Here the transformation plates are first incubated at 37°C over night to favour the
optimal cutting efficiency of CRISPR-Mad7 . Then the incubation temperature is switched to the

the same approach for P. brevicompactum potential problems of lower cutting efficiency at 25 °C

so it is unknown if the fungus can recover after being incubated at 37 °C, or if such high temperatures

Both A. brunneoviolaceus and P. brevicompactum were shown to be sensitive to the antimicrobial

4.2
Sequencing of ku70 and pyrG P. brevicompactum
As the genome available online for the P. brevicompactum strain CBS 1011305 had been shown to be
too unreliable to find protospacers in the P. brevicompactum IBT 23078 strain that were available
from the strain library, the area of the genome around ku70 and pyrG was attempted to be sequenced
in P. brevicompactum IBT 23078, so protospacers could be identified in the strain.
However, as presented in the results it was not possible to amplify most of the areas that were of
interest in the genome. The issue of amplification could possibly stem from the challenge that the
unreliable sequence of P. brevicompactum had to be used to design the amplification primers, and then
assume that the primer binding sites had been conserved between the strains. With only two reactions
producing a fragment of the expected size, the primer design to amplify the genome fragments must
have been flawed, as it was based on the unreliable reference sequence. Small single nucleotide
variations between the two strain can be difference between a functioning PCR, and a no desired
product being produced as primers can not bind.

4.3
Troubleshooting the construction of CRISPR vectors for A. brunneovio-
laceus
While constructing CRISPR vectors for A. brunneoviolaceus issues were encountered with the USER
cloning technique. Six rounds of USER cloning attempting to construct the three plasmids with the
same plasmid backbone lead to either nearly identical numbers of colonies or more on the negative
control plates compared to the actual transformation plates, when transforming the plasmids into E.
coli DH5α, see Table 6 for the exact results. The backbone used for these six transformations had been
linearised following a protocol where the purification step between the O/N linearisation and addition
of nicking enzyme had been excluded. As including the purification step greatly reduced the number
of colonies on the negative plate, the explanation for the high number of colonies in the previous
attempts can be that there were still circular backbone plasmids present that were not linearised during
the PacI digest. Thereby including the gel purification step the small amount of circular plasmids
were removed from the linear plasmids, not contaminating the USER cloning mixture.
Verifying both template, backbone and the constructed plasmids the main method used for validation
was restriction enzyme digestion, followed by checking the digest pattern with gel electrophoresis.
While this was useful confirming the overall structure and presences of inserts. The precise sequence
of inserts were still unknown. Even small mutations in the gRNA would lead to the CRISPR enzyme
not recognising the protospacer, making the vector plasmid ineffective at mediating the desired
deletions. Therefore a more reliable method that could have been utilised to ensure the integrity of
the plasmids would have been through sequencing. By getting the plasmids sequenced, or just the
insert part, full confirmation would be achieved that the USER cloning had been successful. However
sequencing every plasmid would also have been more costly and time consuming compared to more
cost effective digest approach that ended up being used for this project. The digest performed were
also useful, confirmed the structure of constructed vector plasmids.

Sequencing did however reveal that the ku70 mutants still had full homology to the reference sequence
of ku70 in A. brunneoviolaceus meaning that no disruption of the gene had taken place either.

4.5
Analysis of the A. brunneoviolaceus genome with antiSMASH display vast
biosynthetic potential to be explored
A. brunneoviolaceus has previously been identified as a candidate for bioindustrial applications, but no
extensive characterisation of its secondary metabolite production has been conducted [13]. Through
analysis of the A. brunneoviolaceus genome using the latest version of antiSMASH, 98 putative
biosynthetic gene clusters were identified in A. brunneoviolaceus . 63% of the BGCs had no similarity
to any known clusters, showing that it is very likely that novel compounds can be discovered in A.
brunneoviolaceus. The biological function or potential industrial value of the putative compounds
would have to be elucidated with chemical characterisation. To elucidate the biosynthesis of a
metabolite with promising properties, knockout experiments of its predicted BGC would have to be
performed to confirm the pathway of the compound. Here Δku70 and ΔpyrG mutants are a valuable
tool to create a more efficient workflow, with increased deletion efficiency when using CRISPR and
easy to work with selection markers.
Of the 36 BGCs that showed some similarity to known clusters, 14 of the BGCs had a 100% similarity
and one with 93%, with the rest having very low similarities. Although the clusters have high
similarity to clusters known to be involved in the biosynthesis of specific compounds, none of the
BGCs are known to be the complete cluster needed for the biosynthesis of the compound that it is
associated with. Of note was also that 24 of the BGCs were identified at contig edges in the genome
sequence. Potentially inflating the number of BGCs, compared to the actual number in genome as a
cluster on a contig edge might be counted twice with it being spilt up across two contigs. Nonetheless
the antiSMASH analysis of A. brunneoviolaceus still prove that there is large biosynthetic potential
to be explored. Using the engineering optimised strains that have been developed now along with the
CRISPR-Mad7 tools exploring the biosynthetic potential of promising filamentous fungi should be
more efficient and more accessible for industrial utilisation.

Future perspectives
Technical University of Denmark
5
Future perspectives
The use of CRISPR technology in filamentous fungi is still being developed and holds great potential
for more efficient genetic engineering of them. Challenges with CRISPR technology and potential
future research areas therefore needs to be explored further to make better use of filamentous fungi.
5.1
Maturing CRISPR-Mad7 technology in filamentous fungi
The CRISPR-Mad7 technology has just started to be implemented and tested in filamentous fungi,
with only one published article demonstrating the use of CRISPR-Mad7 in filamentous fungi at the
start of this project, with another article being published while this study was ongoing. More studies
are therefore still needed to better understand how CRISPR-Mad7 interacts with fungal systems. As
presented in this study, a Mad7 mediated deletion led to a ~350 bp unintended deletion in the genome.
As the CRISPR-Mad7 technology is oriented towards industrial use, a reliable system that does not
lead to unexpected genome disruption is necessary to make reliable and efficient cell factories. More
fungi needs to be tested to assay if the deletions observed in this study are a common occurrence.
The two-phase transformation protocol that was presented in Vanegas et al. to increase the efficiency
of Mad7, may be used for future work in filamentous fungi. For A. brunneoviolaceus a strain with
Δku70 mutation mediated by CRISPR-Mad7 is still needed for efficient knock-out experiments.
5.2
Exploring the chemical potential of A. brunneoviolaceus
With the mutant strains created of A. brunneoviolaceus explorations of its secondary metabolite holds
promise for discovery of novel compounds. With antiSMASH identifying near to 100 putative BGCs,
there are plenty of targets to investigate in A. brunneoviolaceus . The knock-out of BGC genes was
demonstrated in A. brunneoviolaceus with the knock out of albA, so the optimised workflow using
pyrG marker can be utilised for future pathway elucidation in A. brunneoviolaceus .
5.3
Optimising P. brevicompactum as a production host with genetic engineer-
ing
Problems with the sequence of P. brevicompactum prevented the development of CRISPR-Mad7
mediated knock out experiments of ku70 and pyrG. For further genetic engineering having a reliable

full genome sequence of the P. brevicompactum IBT 23078 is essential for identifying targets sites,
and designing genome engineering vectors. Any future studies of P. brevicompactum using IBT 23078
therefore needs to include a new full genome sequencing of the strain, before the genetic engineering
work can start. With the pathway of the pharmaceutical compound mycophenolic acid already
elucidated in P. brevicompactum it is an obvious target for genetic engineering. If CRISPR-Mad7 can
successfully be implemented in P. brevicompactum it would make it possible for companies to have
production hosts that could be efficiently engineered to increase the production of MPA. Different
approaches can be used for optimising P. brevicompactum for MPA production by means of genetic
engineering. One possible approach could be to increase the availability of the molecular precursors
used in the biosynthesis. This can be achieved by removing competing pathways using the same
precursors, which are acetyl-CoA and malonyl-CoA [24]. As MPA has a polyketide backbone with a
terpene sidechain, deleting unnecessary PKSs using the same polyketide building blocks, from the
genome could free up precursors from those pathways to be used for MPA production. The genomic
modifications should of course not have a negative impact of the overall fitness of the organism,
so not all PKS can just be removed from an organism without negative consequences. Introducing
multiple copies of the MPA pathway genes to genome could also help increase the production. The
extra pathways could then also be put under control of a constitutive or inducible promoter to get
high expression or allow for controlling the expression during production.

Conclusion
Technical University of Denmark
6
Conclusion
Throughout this project the implementation of the industry catered CRISPR-Mad7 technology in
the two promising filamentous fungi A. brunneoviolaceus and P. brevicompactum was the main
goal. The objective for both species was to create Δku70 and ΔpyrG strains using CRISPR-Mad7
mediated knock-out of the genes. With P. brevicompactum it turned out not to be possible to find the
protospacers for the targeting as the sequence that was available had too many discrepancies to the
sequence of the P. brevicompactum strain IBT 23078 that was used for the experiments. Attempts at
sequencing the areas around ku70 and pyrG in IBT 23078 yielded no useful reads. Hygromycin B
sensitivity assay of P. brevicompactum did reveal that the species is sensitive to the antimicrobial,
so future studies of P. brevicompactum can use it a selection marker utilising the exiting library of
CRISPR vector plasmids with hygromycin B markers.
Using CRISPR-Mad7 it was possible to knock-out pyrG in A. brunneoviolaceus creating a ΔpyrG
strain, but no successful knock outs of ku70 were achieved in A. brunneoviolaceus. The knock out of
pyrG led to a ~350 bp unintended deletion downstream of pyrG. While the mechanism behind the
deletion is not known, it is important to further test CRISPR-Mad7 in filamentous fungi to investigate
if Mad7 mediated knock-outs are prone to cause unintended deletions. The deletions could have
undesirable desirable effects in a production host, by potentially causing disruptions in genome
reducing the fitness of the organism. The knock-out experiments of albA in BRU4 demonstrated that
pyrG is a valid selection marker for A. brunneoviolaceus with it being possible to re-introduce its
functionality with a pyrG gene from A. fumigatus. By using the pyrG marker and working with a
NHEJ deficient A. brunneoviolaceus strain the bioindustrial potential of A. brunneoviolaceus can
more efficiently be explored and mapped targeting the putative BGCs found in its genome.
