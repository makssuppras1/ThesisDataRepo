The purpose of this study is to investigate the phytate degradation capability of strain 60,1, a soil bacterium found in 
an agricultural soil sample, by testing its capacity to grow with phytate as the only P-source. The expression of a 
putative phytase enzyme will also be assessed during this experiment. This can lead to the development of enzymes, 
and possibly biofertilizer, that can assist in lowering the amounts of phosphate added to agricultural soils, by 
releasing phosphates from phytate already present in the soil for plant uptake. In this project, due to the time frame, I 
will focus on one bacterial strain obtained from a microcosm screening for phytate-degrading bacteria in an 
agricultural soil system. The identity will be investigated by 16S rRNA gene sequencing and subsequent search in the 
NCBI database. The genome of the strain will also be assembled from a whole genome sequencing done previously 
by Sabrina Pittroff and annotated in the search for a possible phytase gene. Primers will be designed for the possible 
phytase gene and RNA samples from the growth experiments will be extracted and converted to cDNA to see the 
response to phytate in the medium with a quantitative polymerase chain reaction (qPCR). Samples will be taken for 
HPLC analysis, during the growth experiment as this will show the change in the chemical profile of the medium. If 
this strain can degrade phytate enzymatically, then there could be possible uses for it as biofertilizer or for enzyme 
production on an industrial scale, though this will require further investigation.

Abstract 
The human population is increasing rapidly and so does the need for fertilizer use in crop production to sustain 
it. This is a big issue because phosphorous (P) (an essential nutrient for growth) is obtained through mining 
and this fossil source is projected to become depleted within the next 50-100 years. Because of this, new P-
sources needs to be found. In the soil there is a large pool of organic P which cannot be fully utilized by the 
crops, but through the activity of microorganisms this can become available for plant growth. Phytate is the 
most abundant organic P compound in the soil and this can be degraded by enzymes called phytases. These 
are excreted by different microorganisms, making them, as well as the enzymes, interesting for the utilization 
of organic P.  
In this study a strain of Arthrobacter “60,1”, which had been isolated from a microcosm screening for phytate-
degrading bacteria in agricultural soil, was investigated for its ability to degrade phytate. It was grown in a 
medium in which the only P-source was a Ca-phytate complex and the genome was investigated in order to 
find a possible phytase-coding gene. That this strain belonged to the genus of Arthrobacter, was found 
through comparison of the 16S rRNA gene. A possible phytase gene was found that showed similarity to a 
phytase gene in A. nitrophenolicus. Primers were made to analyze the expression of this gene while the strain 
was grown under different P-conditions, through qPCR.  
60,1 was able to grow with the only P-source being Ca-phytate, and the qPCR showed that the possible 
phytase gene was expressed after two days of growth in the medium. This is consistent with other findings 
of phytase expression being induced by the presence of phytate. 
The expression of the possible phytase gene was also tested in media with free phosphate and without P, and 
here stochastic expression was seen when grown with free phosphate, and nothing was seen when the strain 
was grown without P in the medium. These results do not comply with other findings of phytase genes being 
regulated by the Pho regulon, with increased expression under P-starvation.  
The growth of 60,1 with Ca-phytate as the only P-source indicates that it can degrade phytate, Possibly 
through the expression of the proposed phytase gene. Based on these results it is proposed that Arthrobacter 
sp. 60,1 could be a potential candidate for future uses as biofertilizer and/or in phytase production.

1   Introduction  
  
Phosphorus (P) is an essential component for growth in all living organisms as it is required for 
several vital processes such as DNA and RNA synthesis, metabolism and membrane stability (Ochs, 
Raymond S. 2014). P is often a limited nutrient in soil and P-containing fertilizer addition, often with 
nitrogen and potassium as well, to agricultural fields is required to obtain optimal yields. As the 
human population continues to increase with a projected 50% by 2050 the global food demand will 
double (Tilman, et al. 2002). This means the demand for P-containing fertilizer to be used in 
agriculture will also increase. The world supply of phosphate in the form of mined rock-phosphate is 
a limited and dwindling resource, and though there are different estimates on when, it seems as though 
it will be depleted in the near future, with estimates of the peak production occurring in around 2030, 
and current global reserves being depleted in 50-100 years (Cordell et al., 2009; Van Kauwenbergh, 
2010). P is only available to plants as phosphate, but this is a highly reactive compound and therefore 
phosphate added through fertilizers quickly becomes unavailable because of precipitation, adsorption 
and the formation of organic P-compounds. Because of this, only around 10-30% of the added P can 
be taken up by the plants within the year of addition (Harvey et al., 2009). The added P that is not 
utilized by the plants or bound to the soil, can cause eutrophication of nearby water systems through 
runoff with the rain, causing a great environmental problem as well (H.C. Zhang et al., 2003). The 
microorganisms living in the rhizosphere of plants (symbiotic and free-living) help the plant to 
acquire nutrients on a larger scale than it would be able to on its own, and especially with P the 
microorganisms help by making it accessible from different sources to the plant as phosphate 
(Berendsen et al., 2012). The ways in which these microorganisms contribute to the availability of 
phosphate can be physical, as they increase the surface area in which the plant roots are in contact 
with soil and its nutrients, increasing the accessibility to these, or chemical and enzymatic, as 
excretion of acids and enzymes that release nutrients, including N and P, from otherwise unavailable 
sources (Alan E. Richardson et al., 2009). The chemical and enzymatic ways are of most interest 
regarding possible biotechnological applications, especially as these are the ways in which 
phosphates can be released from phytate. The purpose of this study is to investigate the ability of a 
bacterium, isolated from agricultural soil screening for phytate-degrading bacteria, to degrade 
phytate, whether this possible degradation is enzymatical or not, and possibly identifying an enzyme 
active during this. This could be used to design and/or produce enzymes or biofertilizer capable of 
increasing the P uptake by crops, leading to lower amounts of added phosphate and thereby more 
sustainable agriculture with less negative impact on the environment.

1.1 The P-cycle 
As mentioned above, P is an essential component of life because of its many important functions in 
cells, such as forming the backbone of DNA and RNA, the formation of phospholipids with their 
importance in membrane stability, its role in metabolism and in signaling (Ochs, Raymond S. 2014). 
The cycle of P in the soil is different from that of Nitrogen, as most P is found in solid form at the 
temperature and pressure found in most terrestrial systems, and very little in gas form meaning that 
there is no significant input of P from the atmosphere as there is with both N and C. Most P is found 
tightly bound in rocks and minerals and mostly in the mineral called apatite, which is unavailable for 
biological use (Tiessen, H. 2011). It is is introduced to soil systems through erosion and weathering 
through rain, as phosphate which, as mentioned above, is highly reactive and quickly binds minerals 
and other soil particles, or is washed away with the rain into nearby water systems. Much of this 
phosphate will therefore not be immediately available for biological use but become a part of a 
reservoir of P in the soil or washed away to potentially become an environmental problem with its 
role in eutrophication (Carpenter, 2008). Plants are only able to absorb P in the form of 
orthophosphate (in soil solution found as HPO42- and H2PO4-), and as these ions quickly become 
unavailable, it becomes a limiting factor for plant growth. Microbes in the soil play a big part in the 
P cycle in the soil, as they are very efficient in obtaining P from otherwise unavailable sources, using 
acids and enzymes. Microbes in the rhizosphere also increase the P uptake by plants through 
increasing the effective root area of the plant, adding their hyphae to the effective area, and by 
production of root-growth stimulating hormones (Arshad & Frankenberger, 1991; Harvey et al., 
2009). Soil microbes thus increase the availability of P in the soil, and though P is immobilized by 
the growing microbial cells, P is also mineralized to become available for the plants in the system. 
The P that is immobilized becomes a part of a very dynamic P-pool from which P can become 
mineralized based on different environmental factors, as turnover of the microbial biomass. (A. E. 
Richardson & Simpson, 2011).

Figure 1: Figure showing the role of microorganisms in the P availability in soil. From: A. E. Richardson & Simpson (2011) 
 
In a normal ecosystem, the P removed from the soil by the plant would be, in some part removed 
from the system by grazing animals, and in some part returned to the soil through decaying plant 
residue and animal waste. P lost through erosion and leaching with the rain, with the P removed 
ultimately winding up at the ocean floor for sedimentation and with time becoming embedded in rock 
again, would be the completion of the cycle. In agriculture, however, large amounts of P are removed 
entirely from the system with the harvesting of the crops, leading to the need to add additional P 
through use of fertilizer, which originates from mined rock phosphate and manure (Filippelli, 2008). 
Another problem with the current agricultural fertilizer practice, is that the phosphate fertilizer 
originates from mining, and this extraction of P is much faster than the sedimentation and eventual 
mineralization of P that forms the rock phosphate that is being mined. This means that the P-source 
for use in fertilizer is a limited resource and in the near future it will be depleted, meaning that it will 
be necessary to find other sources (Cordell et al., 2009). However, as the form in which P is found 
will be more diverse, this will become a problem. As much of the P will be found in organic form, 
the role of microbes, either as biofertilizer or as enzyme producers will become more important in 
the pursuit of a sustainable solution (Tilman et al., 2002).

1.2   Phytate – A potentially huge P-source present in soil 
There are several forms of P naturally present in the soil in both inorganic and organic form, the ratio 
between them differing largely between soil types and pH. The different forms in which P is found 
show differences in solubility and thereby availability for the organisms living in the soil according 
to the soil type (D. L. Sharma et al., 1963). The phosphate that is added through fertilizer is of fossil 
origin, and therefore a limited resource obtained through mining (Reijnders, 2014). The unsustainable 
nature of this practice means that alternative methods are required for a sustainable agricultural sector 
in the future. As plants can only take up inorganic P, there is a large pool of P in the organic form that 
is not being used sufficiently, but which in the future could become very important. Especially one 
P-compound is important to mention here, and that is Phytic acid. Phytic acid is the most abundant 
organic P-compound in soil as it is estimated to make up 10-50% of the total organic P (depending 
on the type of soil) while organic P makes up 30-50% of total P in the soil (Jorquera et al, 2008). 
==PAGE:10==
  
 
10  
  
Phytate is a salt of phytic acid (myo-inositol hexakisphosphoric acid) and is used as a storage 
compound for P, inositol and minerals in plant seeds, making it vital for seed development and 
growth. The molecule consists of an inositol ring with six phosphate groups bound to it by ester bonds 
(figure 3). Phytate accounts for up to several percent of the dry weight of seeds, and often between 
50-80% of the total P content. It is not only found in the seeds but also in pollen, and vegetative 
tissues. It is formed within the cell where it is found, and are stored in clusters called globoids, in 
association with the Endoplasmatic reticulum. It is also believed to play a part in controlling the levels 
of inorganic P in seeds and seedlings and it is found in complex with mono- or divalent cations (as 
Ca, Fe, Cu, K etc.) (Lott et al., 2000). As phytate binds strongly to mineral surfaces in the soil due to 
the large charge density from the six phosphate groups, it is often found resistant to mineralization 
by microbial degradation. Most phosphatases cannot degrade this molecule, only the ones termed 
phytases, which are not produced by all microbial species, and are only found in low amounts in the 
root exudate (Hayes et al., 1999). It is therefore believed that soil microorganisms with the ability to 
degrade phytate are very important in the cycle of P in the soil. Phytate functions as a P-storage 
molecule, and therefore seeds often have high concentrations thereof, and as we often use seeds in 
foods for both human and animal consumption, the phytate content in this can be very high. Phytates 
are only digested in very low amounts in mono-gastric animals and therefore manure addition to the 
agricultural fields from these animals add high amounts of phytate (Pallauf & Rimbach, 1997). 
Because these phytates bind strongly to soil mineral surfaces, together with the addition of phytate 
from plant residue and a low phytate-degradation in the soil, the phytate accumulates. This can also 
be an environmental problem if the field is close to water, as the phytate can leach into the water 
where it can be degraded by microorganisms present therein, and thereby cause eutrophication 
(Cheng & Lim, 2006). Because there is a large pool of phytate in the soil, and because of potential 
environmental risks, there is a large interest in applications of either phytases in the feed or soil, or 
inoculation of the rhizospheres with phytate-degrading microorganisms (Doolette et al., 2010). 
Because of its high affinity to minerals, and because it is not easily degraded, phytate is also believed 
to be an “anti-nutrient” meaning that it can cause mineral deficiencies by binding these and making 
them unavailable for absorption, and it is therefore an interesting compound regarding human and 
animal health. Phytases in animal feed are being used in an increasingly large scale and have been 
shown to increase P-uptake of the animals and decrease the P-content in the waste (Troesch et al., 
2013). Phytases in the soil however seems to rapidly lose effectiveness due to sorption. This 
immobilization has been observed in fungal-derived phytases released from roots of transgenic plants 
(M. Jorquera et al., 2008). More research in the degradation of phytate in the soil is needed to be able 
==PAGE:11==

to use this large P-source in the soil, to increase human and animal health, and to reduce the effect 
from phytate on eutrophication. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
1.3 Plant growth promoting rhizobacteria (PGPR) 
Plants can utilize the nutrients in the soil to some extent through the activity of their roots, but plants 
are also found in symbiosis with several microorganisms, as these relationships increase the 
availability of complex nutrients and water in the soil (Morgan et al., 2005). These symbiotic 
relationships are found both within the roots of the plant, and in the surrounding soil, and here the 
symbiosis with mycorrhizal fungi is the most well-known, as it is believed to be found in the majority 
of land plants (Kiers et al., 2011). Soil microbial communities are vast and very diverse, and in the 
rhizosphere alone it is estimated that there are more than 30.000 prokaryotic species. The importance 
of this microbiota has become more apparent in recent years as increasingly more evidence shows 
that it plays a vital role in plant health (Berendsen et al., 2012). Plants and microorganisms show an 
intimate evolutionary history together, something that is supported by molecular data and fossils, and 
the interactions between them are therefore very complicated and species specific (Selosse & Tacon, 
1998). Plants and microbes communicate through chemical signals with different secondary 
metabolites, plant hormones and other small molecules, which, depending on the nature of the 
compound, and the species, can lead to symbiosis, either positive or negative, by mutualism, 
commensalism or parasitism (Martin & Schwab, 2012). Plants use compounds that can both deter 
pathogens from colonizing the plant or the rhizosphere, but also attract beneficial microbes, and this 
entire chemical cross-talk in the rhizosphere is very important for the health of the plant. The innate

immune system of plants is designed to recognize molecular patterns from these microbes in the form 
of either microbial, damage and pathogen associated molecular patterns (MAMPs, DAMPs and 
PAMPs respectively). The metabolites produced by the microorganisms are not only used to interact 
with the plant, but very importantly also to interact with other microorganisms, either cooperatively 
or competitively (Chagas et al., 2018).

PGPR are bacteria that are indigenous in the rhizosphere and that, when introduced to a soil system 
will colonize the rhizosphere and increase the availability of primary nutrients for the plant, causing 
increased plant growth (Vessey, 2003). Many different bacteria are in this classification, and they can 
both be endophytic (inside the plant tissue) or in the soil. The ways in which these bacteria promote 
plant growth are a mix of increasing the availability of essential nutrients as P and N, keeping 
pathogen levels low and their production of phytohormones (Mendes, R. et al, 2012). The rhizosphere 
differs from the bulk soil because of activity of the plant roots, which significantly changes the 
==PAGE:13==
  
 
13  
  
physical and chemical conditions, as pH and water potential, in the area surrounding the roots. This 
means that not all bacteria can colonize the rhizosphere, only the ones that can grow in the specific 
conditions that are found in the rhizosphere system. As the plant also exudates sugars and other easily 
digestible Carbon-sources, many bacteria are specialized to live in these systems, though the specific 
conditions are plant specific, and therefore the community is different between each plant species 
(Vessey, 2003). The best-known example of PGPR is the N2-fixing bacteria rhizobia which is found 
in symbiosis with legumes. This symbiosis is of great ecological importance as the rhizobia creates 
nodules in the legume roots in which they can fixate atmospheric nitrogen and convert it to 
biologically available compounds such as NH3, creating an N input to the system.  As the knowledge 
of these plant-microbe systems increases, together with the growing public concern for the 
environment, more focus has been directed to possible applications of them in agriculture to improve 
crop yield in a more sustainable way. A term used regarding this is “Biofertilizer” which is defined 
as “a substance which contains living microorganisms which, when applied to seed, plant surfaces, 
or soil, colonizes the rhizosphere or the interior of the plant and promotes growth by increasing the 
supply or availability of primary nutrients to the host plant.” By Vessey (2003). Most higher plants, 
both in agriculture and nature, are found in symbiosis with mycorrhiza fungi in the roots, and it has 
been known for many years that this symbiosis, together with other beneficial microorganisms in the 
rhizosphere, increases the nutrient availability of the soil leading to higher growth rates and better 
protection against pathogens (Mosse, 1973). The exact mechanisms behind this are hard to determine 
as it is a complex system of different microorganisms, but it has been shown that adding known 
beneficial microorganisms to the roots of plants increases the growth of the plant through higher 
nutrient availability (Wu et al., 2005). This was shown to happen in different ways: it was shown that 
known PGPR increased the inoculation of the mycorrhizal fungi, and that different combinations of 
microorganisms showed different results, but mostly an increase in nutrient levels in the soil and in 
plant growth  
 
1.4   Known rhizobacteria: Arthrobacter and Bacillus  
Bacillus is a genus of Gram-positive rod-shaped bacteria, found in high abundance in soil of many different 
types, often as a plant growth promoting rhizobacteria. This is one of the most well-known soil phytate 
degraders, and especially Bacillus subtilis is well-studied, with the genome being one of the most studied 
along with that of E.coli (Kumar et al., 2012). The soil, however, contains many different microorganisms 
and not all are known in detail as is the case with the Bacillus genus. As it is known that the rhizosphere is a 
==PAGE:14==
  
 
14  
  
complex community of microorganisms, knowledge of more of the organisms is required for the design of 
optimal biofertilizer, or to make use of the enzymatic potential within these systems (Zhao et al., 2016) 
Members of Bacillus and Pseudomonas make up some well documented phytate-degrading soil bacteria, but 
little is known about phytate degradation by Arthrobacter though it has been reported (Hill et al., 2007). They 
are found in several different environments, and very commonly in soil where they are found in many 
different soil types. Arthrobacter is a genus of obligate aerobic bacteria that includes most of the bacteria that 
exhibit a morphological cycle in which they show a rod shape in young cultures and a coccus in older cultures, 
with a difference in Gram-staining, so that the young cultures appear as Gram-negative while the older 
appears as Gram-positive, though they are a part of the Gram-positive phylum Actinobacteria. All species 
are catalase-positive and show a very high metabolic diversity (Moreno & Moriyón, 2006). One of the reasons 
why Arthrobacter is so common in soil could be that they are very resistant to desiccation and starvation by 
being able to rapidly decrease their metabolism (Cacciari & Lippi, 2009). Arthrobacter species often 
constitute an important fraction of the rhizosphere microbiota, and can promote plant growth by production 
of plant growth hormones (phytohormones) such as Gibberellic acids and auxins (Brown, 1972). Some 
species have been proved, not only able to live in highly contaminated soil, but in some cases also capable of 
degrading contaminants into less harmful compounds, and therefore show a possible use in soil remediation 
(Ding et al., 2009). In fact, some Arthrobacter species are known to be able to grow with atrazine, a widely 
used herbicide that is causing environmental problems due to it being an endocrine disrupting chemical, as 
the only source of N, C and energy (Li et al., 2008). Other Arthrobacter species have been investigated in 
regard to their biosorption capabilities, and showed relatively high uptakes of several toxic metals (Veglió et 
al., 1997). This, together with the fact that some Arthrobacter species are also used for industrial production 
of several compounds (Moreno & Moriyón, 2006), make it an interesting genus to investigate further for 
possible uses as biofertilizer and for production of enzymes as for example phytases, to be used in agriculture. 
 
 
 
1.5 Phytases  
Phytases (myo-inositol hexakisphosphate phosphohydrolases) are a group of phosphatases that have 
a high specificity for Phytate, and that initiates a stepwise degradation thereof through hydrolysis of 
the bound phosphate groups (Mullaney & Ullah, 2003). There are different ways of classifying 
phytases; by their pH optima (acid or alkaline phytases), by the stereospecificity of the initial cleavage 
site on the phytate (3-, 4-, 5- and 6-phytase) by their source (from which organism) and by their 
catalytic mechanisms (Histidine acid phytases (HAPs), Purple acid phytase (PAPs), Cysteine 
phosphatases (CPs) and Beta-propeller phytases (BPPs)) (Menezes-Blackburn, et al. 2013). These 
==PAGE:15==
  
 
15  
  
enzymes can hydrolyze several phytate salts, with Ca-phytate as a very common and important 
example, but in the soil phytate will also be found bound to metals as Fe, Al, Cu and Zn. These salts 
are not easily hydrolyzed and requires the presence of organic acids for the hydrolysis to occur, which 
is a problem as organic acids also decrease the activity of some of the enzymes, though other phytases 
can work synergistically with organic acids (Jongbloed et al., 2000). As the root exudate of plants 
naturally contains several organic acids, it is important to understand the interactions of these with 
the enzymes present in the soil, from both the root exudate and from microorganisms (Tang et al., 
2006). Phytases are found in many different organisms, both plants, animals and microorganisms, but 
so far, the most focus has been on microorganisms as these are most promising for production of 
enzymes on a commercial level. Both fungi and bacteria are used for large-scale phytase production, 
with E. coli and A. niger as key producers (Rodriguez et al., 1999).  
PAPs are acidic phytases mainly found in plants and mammals where they have similar structure but 
very low sequence identity, but there have been indications that they are also present in fungi and 
bacteria (Schenk et al., 2000). They have been found in the root exudate of plants under low Pi 
conditions as a response by the plant, but they also play an important role in other physiological 
aspects such as general stress responses to both abiotic and biotic factors (Lung et al., 2008; W. Zhang 
et al., 2007). CPs are found to be the major phytate-degrading enzyme in the anaerobic environment 
in the rumen of ruminant animals and is therefore considered to be a very important enzyme for the 
P-cycle in terrestrial systems. CPs get their name from their active site which is near a Cysteine-
containing P-loop. They are acid phytases with 4,5 as the optimal pH, but CPs have been found in the 
rumen of goats and cows with an optimal pH of around 6, which is also more fitting with the rumen 
environment (Huang et al., 2011). The phytases that are used as feed additives, and therefore the ones 
we know the most about, are microbial HAPs from E. coli and Aspergillus niger. They are acidic 3- 
and 6-phytases with their optimum pH ranging from 4,5 to 5.5 and with high optimum temperatures 
ranging from around 40℃ to around 70℃. The active site of these enzymes is a conserved motif 
(RHGXRXP) with a Histidine which initiates the hydrolysis with a nucleophilic attack on the P on 
the phytate molecule (M. Z. Yao et al., 2012) Their effectiveness in animals, where they increase P, 
mineral and protein uptake, are well reported and they show high specificity to metal-free phytate at 
low pH and yields myo-inositol monophosphate as the final product (Dersjant-Li et al., 2015; Oh et 
al., 2004).

BPPs are found in soil bacteria like Bacillus species with B. subtilis and B. licheniformis as known 
examples, where it is well reported (M. A. Jorquera et al., 2013). Interestingly it is also the only type 
of phytase found in marine environments where it is found in Shewanella oneidensis (Cheng & Lim, 
2006). BPPs are the most abundant in soil, and a lot of work is being done in investigating this 
enzyme. For example: BPPs have been shown to be encoded by soil bacteria in Chilean andisols, by 
using primers for a known BPP in a Bacillus species, as this genus contains several known BPP 
producers. M. A. Jorquera and coworkers (2013) did this experiment, measuring the expression of 
BPP with qPCR to see how phytate addition would change the expression and abundance of the gene. 
They saw that indigenous bacteria, as well as their “Bacillus MQH-19” strain showed increased 
expression levels after addition of phytate, indicating that phytases are regulated by phytate 
availability. Here they also found that uninoculated soil samples showed high expression levels as 
well when phytate was added, indicating that the gene is cosmopolitan and that there probably are 
other bacteria with a similar phytase gene. The BPPs are alkaline phytases which show high 
thermostability, and their catalytic activity is highly dependent on the Ca2+ concentration as the 
binding of these cations is essential to the stability and binding specificity of the enzyme towards the 
phosphate groups on the phytate molecule as shown by (Shin et al., 2001). They found that six Ca2+ 
cations were bound to the enzyme when it was the most active, while the binding of only three of 
these cations yielded it inactive, this was supported by finding four Ca2+ cations in the active site 
when in complex with the substrate (plus one Ca2+ bound to the substrate when in complex with the 
enzyme) (see figure 6). Molecularly, the genes encoding phytases are, as the ones encoding other

phosphatases, found as a part of the Pho regulon in bacteria, which is important for the cells P 
metabolism among other things, as described in the following section.

1.6   The Pho regulon  
The Pho regulon is a global regulatory mechanism in bacteria that controls inorganic P levels, through 
activation and transcription of several genes encoding phosphatases and transporters, as well as 
enzymes responsible for P storage. Phytases, and other phosphatases have been found to be part of 
this regulon and therefore induced by P starvation (Santos-Beneit, 2015). The activation occurs 
through sensing a lack of inorganic P both in the media for bacteria as seen in E. coli, and 
intracellularly as it has been shown in S. cerevisiae (Shinagawa et al., 1983). It has also been shown 
that there is a cross-talk between inorganic P levels and that of other essential nutrients as N and C, 
meaning that N-, and C-levels also play a part in the regulation of the Pho regulon, and these 
interconnected molecular networks are found in different complexity with each species (Robichon et 
al., 2000; Site et al., 2006). The Pho regulon is controlled by a two-component regulatory system 
consisting of an inner-membrane Histidine kinase sensor protein, and a cytoplasmic transcriptional 
response regulator. The system was first characterized in E. coli where these proteins were named 
PhoR-PhoB, but it is also well studied in Bacillus subtilis where it is named PhoR-PhoP, and it has 
==PAGE:18==
  
 
18  
  
been given other names in other species. It works by phosphorylation of the response regulator by the 
receptor kinase when there are low levels of inorganic P, which enables it to bind specific regions on 
the DNA, the PHO boxes, and either activate or repress transcription of genes. The exact regulation 
is species specific and shows different levels of complexity (Lamarche et al., 2008). The PHO boxes 
have been defined as the sum of two 11-nucleotide direct repeats with seven conserved and four less 
conserved nucleotides in each. This has been shown in many bacteria, but the consensus region is 
different between the species (Blanco et al., 2002). Interaction of the Pho regulon with other stresses 
as osmotic, oxidative etc. have also been documented as it can assist in the regulation of stress-
response genes, for example an up-regulation of genes coding for catalases to help protect bacteria 
from oxidative stress (Yuan et al., 2005). The Pho regulon is also found to play a part in possible 
pathogenesis and secondary metabolite production, which makes it especially interesting in the 
biotech industry. Here a deep understanding of the Pho regulon is required to optimize the yield of 
compounds in, for example, medicine production, where knowing more about the pathogenesis of the 
target bacteria could also increase the efficiency of the medicine (Santos-Beneit, 2015). Little is 
known about the Pho regulon in Arthrobacter but recently it was shown that the species of focus for 
this study: sp. “60,1”, contain genes associated with the Pho regulon (Rasmussen, 2018).

1.7   This project   
In this project I will be adding to the work of Sabrina Pittroff, PhD student at the University of 
Copenhagen, done in her PhD project. She isolated several bacterial strains, from several genera 
including Bacillus, Pseudomonas and Arthrobacter, from agricultural soil using microcosms 
screening for phytate-degrading bacteria. I will be focusing on one of these: an Arthrobacter sp. 
(60,1). The reason why this strain is interesting is that it showed indications of enzymatic breakdown 
of phytate with a clearance zone on Ca-phytate agar without acid production. Primers for a very 
common phytase in soil, a BPP from Bacillus licheniformis, were used to screen for the enzyme with 
PCR, but this showed no significant results, indicating that 60,1 possesses another type of phytase. 
My hypothesis is that this species, 60,1, can grow in a medium where the only P-source is Ca-phytate, 
as it is able to degrade the phytate into lower-order inositol phosphates, and that it does this through 
the activity of a phytase. The aim of this project will therefore be to 1) grow 60,1 in minimal media 
with three different P-sources (÷P, PO4 and Ca-phytate) and measure the growth to see how it changes 
with the P-source, 2) Assemble and annotate the genome, and find a possible phytase, 3) test the 
==PAGE:20==
  
 
20  
  
expression of a putative phytase gene in samples taken during the growth experiment with primers 
designed with the assembled genome of 60,1, and 4) examine the change in inositol phosphate 
composition in samples taken during the growth experiment with HPLC. Understanding more about 
phytate degradation and the organisms involved, could lead to enhanced sustainability in the 
agricultural sector as it could eventually lead to production of enzymes with high stability and 
effectivity in the soil, or to the design of more effective biofertilizer. Both scenarios could 
individually, or perhaps in combination add to higher amounts of available phosphate in the soil, 
which could in turn lead to decreased amounts of added phosphate in fertilizers, and decreased 
amounts of P that will be lost by the soil system by runoff and aid in eutrophication of nearby water 
systems. Besides from this, a better understanding of phytases can also lead to the production of plant-
based foods with a higher nutritional value as phytate functions as an “anti-nutrient” binding minerals 
and keeping them from being absorbed. Knowing more about phytases, and phytate-degraders 
therefore shows a high potential for both decreasing the negative impact of agriculture on the 
environment, improving the sustainability of agricultural practices by adding less mined rock-
phosphate, which is a limited resource, and improving the nutritional quality of plant-based food.  
 
 
1.8 Experimental strategy 
The first step will be to assess the identity of 60,1 through a bioinformatic approach, comparing the 
16S rRNA gene, and the assembled genome of 60,1 to genome databases. As the hypothesis is that 
this species can grow in a medium in which Ca-phytate is the only P-source, and that it can do so 
because of enzymatic degradation of phytate to release phosphate, minimal media will be made with 
three P-conditions: no P, free phosphate and Ca-phytate. The assembled genome of 60,1 will be 
annotated using the RAST web-server to find a possible phytase gene. To investigate the activity of 
the possible phytase gene, primers will be designed based on a genome assembly, for use in an 
expression analysis with qPCR. 60,1 will be grown in the minimal media for three days, with samples 
taken for DNA/RNA extraction for later qPCR, growth measurement and HPLC (High Performance 
Liquid Chromatography). The HPLC samples are to observe possible changes in the chemical profile 
of the media during the experiment, as this will verify the enzymatic degradation of phytate over the 
course of the growth experiment.

2   Materials and methods  
 
2.1   Isolation of DNA  
The strain, 60,1, was grown in Lysogeny broth (LB) medium (per liter: 10g Tryptone, 5g yeast extract 
and 10 g NaCl in 950ml of MiliQ water), and on LB agar plates (adding 15g agar per liter). The strain 
was streaked on LB plate from frozen glycerol stock and incubated at 28℃ for 18 hours. A colony 
from the LB plate was used to inoculate 25ml of liquid LB medium, and this was incubated at 28℃ 
for 18 hours in a 50 ml conical centrifuge tube on a shaker at 200 rpm. DNA was extracted from the 
liquid culture using the DNeasy® UltraClean® microbial kit from Qiagen, following the quick-start 
protocol completely (see appendix A). The obtained DNA was stored at -20℃. 
 
2.2   16S rRNA gene amplification 
DNA concentration was measured using NanoDrop ND-1000. The protocol for the PCR was given 
by Sabrina Pittroff: in a 0,8 ml reaction tube a 49 µl master mix of the following was mixed with 1 
==PAGE:22==
  
 
22  
  
µl of DNA template: 5 µl of PCR buffer (containing 15 mM MgCl2) (Sigma), 0,5 mM MgCl2 (Sigma), 
0,5 mM dNTP’s (Sigma), 0,2 µM 27F forward, 0,2 µM 1492R reverse primer (TagC) (Bruce et al., 
2010), 0,25 µl (1,25 units) Taq polymerase (Sigma), and 35,75 µl H2O (Sigma). The thermal profile 
of the PCR was set to an initial denaturation step with 94℃ for 4 minutes, followed by 35 cycles of 
denaturation at 94℃ for 30 seconds, annealing at 62℃ for 60 seconds and elongation at 72℃ for 60 
seconds with a final elongation step of 72℃ for 7 minutes before cooling down to 14℃ at the end. 
The reaction was done in an AH diagnostics Mastercycler. 
The PCR product was run on a 1% agarose gel at 100V for 30 minutes on a RunOne electrophoresis 
cell (Embitec) and viewed with BIORAD gel doc 2000. The PCR product was sent to GATC biotech 
for purification and sequencing using both 27F and 1492R as primers. The obtained sequence was 
evaluated and trimmed using Bioedit, and a BLAST in the NCBI-database was performed 
(https://blast.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/Blast.cgi). The genome sequence of the best hit based on the 16S 
rRNA gene BLAST, was then compared with the genome of 60,1 through calculating the average 
nucleotide identity (ANI) with the following web-server: http://enve-omics.ce.gatech.edu/ani/, and 
by making an in silico DNA-DNA hybridization (DDH) with the following web-server: 
http://ggdc.dsmz.de/distcalc2.php.  
 
2.3   Preparation of the minimal media 
All minimal media (one without P, one with free PO4 and one with Ca-phytate as the only sources of 
P) were made with the following recipe: 10% root exudate, 1 mM CaCl2, 37 mM NH4NO3, 67 mM 
KCl, 2 mM MgSO4, 36 µM FeSO4 and 45 µM MnSO4. Beside from this the PO4 medium was made 
with 37 mM KH2PO4, and the Ca-phytate medium was made with 7,6 mM phytate and extra CaCl2 
in a 6:1 CaCl2:Phytate ratio for the formation of Ca-phytate.  
The root exudate was made with the following: 0,05 M D-fructose, 0,05 M D-glucose, 0,05 M 
sucrose, 0,025 M succinic acid, 0,015 M L-malic acid, 0,0125 M L-arginine, 0,0125 M L-serine, 
0,0125 M L-cysteine. This media recipe is based on the phytate screening medium by Howson & 
Davis, (1983), though with adjustments made by PhD student, Sabrina Pittroff. 
All stock solutions were autoclaved at 121℃ for 20 minutes, except for the root exudate, the NH4NO3, 
the K2HPO4 and the phytate which were filter-sterilized.  
For making the media, water was autoclaved, and all the sterile solutions were added in a sterile 
environment (LAF bench), and the pH adjusted at the end using NaOH and pH measuring strips. For 
the Ca-Phytate medium a sodium-salt phytic acid (Sigma) was used as the phytate source. CaCl2 was 
==PAGE:23==
  
 
23  
  
added first and then the phytate to make the Ca-phytate complex. The media was kept at room 
temperature until use.  
 
2.4   The growth experiment 
The strain was grown in liquid LB overnight and harvested at 10.000 g (Sigma 3-18K) The cells were 
washed twice with, and suspended in, 0,9% saline solution. 1 ml was used to inoculate 49 ml of the 
synthesized minimal media (÷P, PO4 and Ca-Phytate) in 100 ml flasks and in technical triplicates 
with a negative control. The bottles were incubated on a shaker on 200 rpm at 28℃ for three days

Growth of the strain was determined twice a day by OD measurements (600 nm) for the media:  ÷P 
and PO4, and using plate counts for the Ca-Phytate medium, as the Ca-Phytate made the medium 
white and cloudy which meant that the optical density could not be used to interpret the growth of 
the cells. 
For plate counts, ten-fold serial dilutions were made, and cell enumerated after drop plating (5 µL in 
triplicates) on LB plates divided in fields according to the dilutions (see picture 2)

The plates were incubated for 36 hours at 28℃ and counted at the field with the lowest dilution factor 
possible, and the average of the counts from the drops and the dilution field was then calculated per

1 ml was taken for HPLC analysis (twice the first day, at the end of day two and twice on day 3), and 
1 ml was taken for later DNA/RNA extraction (the end of day one, the end of day two and twice on

separated, and both were stored at -20℃. The averages of the growth measurements were plotted 
against time using Microsoft Excel to interpret the growth.

2.5.1 DNase treatment and reverse transcription (RT) 
The extracted RNA was DNase treated in 0,2 ml PCR tubes with 8 µl RNA, 2 µl DNase buffer 
(Promega RQ1 10x) and 1µl RNase free DNase (1U/µl, Promega RQ1). The tubes were incubated at 
37℃ for one hour, and then 2 µl stop solution (Promega RQ1) was added before the tubes were 
incubated at 65℃ for 10 minutes and transferred to ice. This was done to remove possible DNA-
contaminants from the samples before the RT reaction and conversion of RNA to complementary 
DNA (cDNA). The RT reaction was performed with 18 µl master mix consisting of 2 µl 10x buffer 
RT (Qiagen), 2 µl dNTP mix (5 mM of each), 0,8 µl random hexamer primers (500 µg/ml), 0,2 µl 
RNase inhibitor (Ambion, 20U/µl), 1 µl Reverse transcriptase (4U), and 12 µl DEPC-treated dH2O, 
and with 2 µl template for a final reaction size of 20 µl per sample. The samples were incubated at 
37℃ for 90 minutes followed by 95℃ for 10 minutes for enzyme deactivation, and then ending at 
4℃. This was done on an AH diagnostics Mastercycler. The synthesized cDNA was stored at -20℃ 
while the rest of the DNase treated RNA was stored at -80℃.

cutting off nucleotides with low quality scores from the 3’- and 5’ ends along with adaptor sequences, 
quality assessed based on a report generated after the trim in which the quality (coverage and GC 
content distribution of the sequences) was depicted and assembled according to the following 
workflow figure.

identity of 60,1 being unknown. The CLC de novo assembly is based on de Bruijn graphs as most

2.6.1   Primer design for the possible “4-phytase” gene  
A “4-phytase” was found from Arthrobacter nitrophenolicus through searching the NCBI database 
for phytases in Arthrobacter and the sequence downloaded for alignment in CLC. Here, with a BLAST 
search against the assembled genome of 60,1 a hit was found to a gene annotated as an “ABC 
transporter”. This gene was compared to other ABC transporters and showed low similarity, and 
both the 4-phytase gene and this were translated into amino acid sequences and aligned. The 
aligned amino acid sequences show high similarity which further supports that this gene could 
encode a phytase. The sequence of this gene was then uploaded to “Primer 3 plus” to create primers 
for further investigation (Untergasser et al., 2007). Primer 3 plus gave possible primer pairs based 
on the applied settings which were as follows: Primer size: minimum 18bp, optimum 20bp and 
maximum 27bp, primer melting temperature Tm: minimum 57℃, optimum 60℃ and maximum 
63℃, and primer GC%: minimum 20% and maximum 80%. The primers were made by Tag 
Copenhagen with the sequences designed by Primer 3 plus. The primers were tested with PCR using 
the protocol for 16S rRNA gene amplification described above, but with the newly designed primers, 
and with an annealing temperature of 55℃ instead of 62℃ and an expected band-size of between 
200-300bp. The obtained primers used for the expression analysis of the putative phytase gene are

2.7 qPCR expression analysis  
The qPCR was done with the cDNA samples as template, with the DNase treated RNA samples as 
controls and DNA extracted from an overnight LB culture to create a standard curve. The standard 
curve was made with ten-fold serial dilutions of DNA with a starting concentration of 97,5 ng/µl in 
triplicates.  DNA from the growth experiment was used to assess the relative expression in the cDNA 
samples by dividing the cDNA expression with the DNA expression from the same sample to see if 
the expression level increases or if a potentially higher number is because of more DNA from the 
growth itself.  
==PAGE:28==
  
 
28  
  
 
Figure 10: Workflow of the qPCR reaction 
The following recipe was used: for each reaction 18 µl of a master mix consisting of: 10 µl Brilliant 
III Ultra-fast qPCR master mix with low ROX (Agilent technologies), 1 µl BSA (20 mg/µl, New 
England biolabs), 0,8 µl (0,4 µM) forward primer, 0,8 µl (0,4 µM) reverse primer and 5,4 µl H2O 
(Sigma), was mixed with 2 µl of template (cDNA, DNA (from LB medium for the standard curve 
and from the growth experiment for the relative expression), and DNase treated RNA as a control), 
six reactions without template were made as negative controls. 
The following thermal profile was used: initial denaturation with 3 minutes at 95°C, followed by 35 
cycles of 15 seconds at 95°C and 20 seconds at 57°C, and finally a cycle of 30 seconds at 95°C, 30 
seconds at 55°C, 30 seconds at 95°C and then 30 seconds at 25°C. The reaction was performed on 
an AriaMx Real-time qPCR system (Agilent technologies). 
The increase in expression between samples were tested with a student’s t-test (2-tailed, paired).

3   Results  
3. 1   16S rRNA gene sequencing 
To identify the strain, DNA was extracted from an overnight culture grown in LB medium, and used 
as template in a 16S rRNA gene amplification through PCR, which yielded a band with the expected 
size of around 1500 bp. The PCR product was sent for sequencing at GATC Biotech. The obtained 
sequence was trimmed and blasted in the NCBI database and gave the following as the best hit: 
Arthrobacter sp. Score: 1323, Identity: 99%. The best suggestion for a species is A. aurescens. Further 
investigation was done by calculating the ANI which showed an identity of 83,66% to the genome of 
Arthrobacter aurescens TC1, which is below the threshold of 95% for species comparison, showed 
by Kim, M. and co-workes (2014). An in silico DDH showed 30,5% identity which is also below the 
species cutoff at 70%. This indicates that 60,1 is not an A. aurescens, but further supports that it is an 
Arthrobacter as the ANI threshold for genus cut-off has been found to be 62% (M. Kim et al., 2014).

inoculated with the strain for a three-day growth experiment. 60,1 grew well in both the Ca-phytate

relationship in the log transformed growth curves, the growth during this three-day period is 
considered exponential in these media. In the ÷P medium however, there is a very low increase in the 
OD of the samples, which is consistent with the lack of change in the colour of the medium. The cells 
in the PO4 and Ca-phytate media do not reach the stationary phase in the duration of this experiment.

4   Discussion  
4.1   Identification of 60,1 
In the soil there are thousands of species of microorganisms both in the rhizosphere of plants, but also 
free living, not all of which has been identified. This can be due to the fact that many soil 
microorganisms are adapted to the specific conditions that exist where they are found, making them 
very difficult to culture (Bruce et al., 2010). Huge advancements have been made however, in the 
study of soil microorganisms in the Earth Microbiome Project, in which metagenomic data from 
around the world is being combined and is freely accessible for researchers (Gilbert et al., 2014). The 
microbial communities in the soil affect plants in different ways, depending on the community 
composition. Therefore knowledge about the community in terms of the abundance and diversity of 
the different microorganisms is very important when trying to understand the processes that affect 
plant health (Berendsen et al., 2012). Metagenomics is a powerful tool for analyzing these 
communities based on the genetic information in the soil in question (Castañeda & Barbosa, 2017), 
however in this study focus has been on the cultivation and sequencing of one strain isolated from a 
microcosm screening for bacteria able to grow on phytate. Metagenomics would be able to show 
presently known phytase enzymes, and their abundance and to some extent, and give predictions of

study this was not done. To identify the strain 60,1 in this study, the 16S rRNA gene was amplified 
with PCR from extracted DNA. The product was sequenced and used to search the NCBI database 
for similarity. Identification by comparison of the 16S rRNA gene to that of other bacteria is often 
the preferred method of investigation, though some problems exist due to poor sequencing and lack 
of consensus in the definition of species based on the 16S rRNA gene data (Clarridge & Alerts, 2004). 
Whole genome sequencing was done prior to this study and the sequence files were assembled with 
CLC and annotated with RAST. Both the 16S rRNA gene sequence BLAST search and the annotation 
from RAST showed that the strain belongs to the genus Arthrobacter, and that the best suggestion of 
a species is aurescens. To see if 60,1 could be an aurescens, an in silico DDH was performed and the 
ANI between them was calculated. The ANI has been shown to be the most precise genome-based 
delineator, and this showed an identity of 83,66% which is below the threshold of 95%, indicating 
that 60,1 is not an aurescens, but still within the genus of Arthrobacter (M. Kim et al., 2014). The 
same conclusion was found with the DDH. To get a better indication of what species this is, it will 
be necessary to do more research on different species characteristics, such as enzyme activity assays, 
pH and temperature measurements for optimal growth (Bochner, 2009), but based on the genomic 
analysis it could be a previously unknown species of Arthrobacter. This is an interesting genus to 
investigate, as it has been shown to be a very common and abundant soil bacteria which can produce 
phytohormones and thereby stimulate plant growth, and as it has shown several possible uses already 
as in bioremediation and industrial production of various chemical compounds (Brown, 1972; Ding 
et al., 2009; Moreno & Moriyón, 2006). 
 
4.1.1   The growth experiment 
To investigate whether 60,1 was able to degrade phytate, a growth experiment under three different 
P-conditions, where Ca-phytate as the only P-source was one of them, was conducted. The growth 
experiment showed that 60,1 was able to grow well in the medium with free phosphate, and that it 
could not grow in the medium without a P-source. A growth experiment with Bacillus licheniformis 
in P-limited medium was performed by Hoi et al., (2006) in which the expression of P-starvation 
induced genes was studied. In their study however, they used a small concentration of available P for 
the cells to grow to a certain maximum density instead of using a medium completely lacking P, as 
in this study. Interestingly 60,1 also showed exponential growth in the Ca-phytate medium, and not 
only did it grow in the medium, but it did so without changing the pH, indicating that it could obtain 
P from the phytate through enzymatic breakdown (S. B. Sharma et al., 2013). The qPCR on the DNA 
==PAGE:38==
  
 
38  
  
samples taken throughout the experiment showed much higher amounts of DNA in the samples taken 
from the phosphate medium than those from the Ca-phytate (see appendix D), indicating that even 
though the growth seems exponential in both media, the rate is limited in the Ca-phytate medium. 
The limiting factor here could be the hydrolysis of phytate which is required for P uptake and growth. 
The regression analyses made on the log-transformed growth curves in both media however, show a 
higher growth rate in the Ca-phytate medium which is not consistent with the DNA amounts seen in 
the expression analysis. To my knowledge a growth experiment of this kind has not been performed 
on Arthrobacter before, and the knowledge of phytate degradation within this genus is also very 
limited, with the only reference I could find being Hill et al. (2007). However, a study was made by 
M. A. Jorquera et al., (2008) in which several strains of Pseudomonas from the rhizospheres of 
different crops were grown on Na-phytate plates, to assess their abilities to degrade phytate and how 
other factors, such as the glucose concentration, affected it. Hill et al. (2007) grew Pseudomonas, but 
also Bacillus strains isolated from a chicken industry site, as well as sites by a nearby river. This was 
also to detect phytate degradation and the strains were both grown on Ca-phytate plates, and in liquid 
medium with the only P-source being phytate as in this study. It was not possible to run the HPLC 
analysis of the samples taken, but this would have shown possible phosphate contamination in the 
Ca-phytate medium. The HPLC analysis, both regarding the detection of possible phosphate 
contamination, but also the change in inositol phosphate composition, would have been a very 
important step in verifying the phytate degradation by 60,1. The phosphate content of the samples 
could also have been indicated by a molybdate assay (Hill et al., 2007; M. A. Jorquera et al., 2008), 
but HPLC was preferred to as this could give a picture of the inositol phosphate composition, and 
thus the stepwise degradation of phytate (Hatzack et al., 2001) The growth observed in the phosphate 
and the Ca-phytate media did not reach the stationary phase, and because of this it would be 
interesting to make a longer growth experiment to see for how long the growth could be sustained in 
these media. 
 
4.1.2   Genome assembly and annotation 
As 60,1 was able to grow with Ca-phytate as the only P-source, the next step was to try and find a 
possible phytase gene in the genome. The genome of 60,1 was sequenced prior to this study, and the 
paired end sequences obtained, were given to me by Sabrina Pittroff for further analysis. The 
sequencing was performed with Illumina MiSeq sequencing. Illumina is the market leader in Next-
generation sequencing and MiSeq produces many (millions) reads of around 150bp which is a good 
size for most applications, though because of it being PCR-based then it is also prone to make errors. 
==PAGE:39==
  
 
39  
  
These errors are usually found in the ends of the reads and can therefore easily be corrected for with 
trimming software. It the cheapest sequencing type per base, making it very suitable for a small scale 
study as this (van Dijk et al., 2014). However, for a de novo assembly, PacBio sequencing would 
have been preferable as it produces much larger reads, but the price is also much higher and as good 
assembly software is also available for short reads, then Illumina sequencing can give good results, 
and it is therefore more feasible for a small study such as this (Wenyu Zhang et al., 2011). A de novo 
assembly was chosen because there was no apparent reference genome as the species identity could 
not be established. The de novo assembly gave a results very similar to those found in assemblies 
done on other Arthrobacter strains as shown by Y. Yao and co-workers (2015). The RAST annotation 
also showed several genes associated with the Pho regulon, which is very interesting as this has only 
rarely been shown in Arthrobacter (not in any published papers to my knowlegde), and because this 
regulon has been shown to have very important functions in the regulation of not only the phosphate 
metabolism, but also in regulation of the metabolism of other nutrients, in pathogenesis, protection 
against oxidative stress, and production of secondary metabolites (Lamarche et al., 2008). Only 7 Pho 
regulon genes were found with the annotation, though the it has been found to comprise 31 genes in 
E. coli (Bertrand & Dozois, 2011). Although few, the RAST annotation found important Pho regulon 
genes, as PhoB and PhoU which are essential for the Pi-dependent transcription regulation, but 
equally important is PhoR, the protein of which activates the phoB protein, initializing transcription 
activation (Shi & Hulett, 1999; Yamada et al., 1989). As PhoR is such an important part of the Pho 
regulon, it is not likely that 60,1 does not encode the gene, it is however more likely that the 
annotation from RAST did not find it due to a technical error either in the sequencing, the genome 
assembly or the annotation. The Pho regulon in 60,1 is something that warrants further investigation 
if this strain is to be used as biofertilizer or industrial production of enzymes. Phytases are often found 
to be controlled by the Pho regulon (Santos-Beneit, 2015) but the possible phytase gene found in the 
genome of 60,1 was not found to be activated by low (no) Pi content in this study, but this could be 
due to technical errors. The possible phytase gene investigated in this study was found through a 
BLAST search against the assembled genome for a “4-phytase” from Arthrobacter nitrophenolicus, 
a gene was found that was annotated with RAST as a “dipeptide-binding ABC transporter”. Primers 
were designed for this gene to investigate if it could be encoding a phytase through qPCR with the 
samples taken during the growth experiment. As for the type of phytase it could be, further 
investigation is needed to see how the pH would affect the degradation or perhaps see how well the 
phytate is degraded with another cation complex than Ca2+. This together with analyzing the amino 
acid composition and protein structure is needed to establish the type of phytase it is (Kim, Y. et al 
==PAGE:40==
  
 
40  
  
1998). The conditions in the media were made for a BBP, as it is very common in soil and therefore 
a good candidate for the type of enzyme that 60,1 could possess. No phytase hits were found with a 
BLAST search against the NCBI database however, and as mentioned in the introduction, Sabrina 
Pittroff had already tested this with BPP primers in a PCR but found nothing. 
 
 
4.2   Expression analysis  
To determine the expression of the possible phytase gene, a qPCR with the primers designed and the 
samples taken during the growth experiment, was performed. qPCR is a well-known tool for studying 
the expression of target genes based on RNA samples treated with reverse transcriptase to obtain 
cDNA (Dekkers et al., 2012). The qPCR showed no expression of the possible phytase gene in the 
samples from the ÷P medium, which is interesting as phytases have been found as a part of the Pho 
regulon, which is known to be upregulated during P-starvation, in other bacteria (Shinagawa et al., 
1983). A similar growth experiment was performed by Hoi et al., (2006) with a Bacillus licheniformis 
in which a phytase gene was expressed under P starvation. In this study it was attempted to use B. 
licheniformis as a positive control, but due to technical errors this produced no results. The lack of 
detection of the gene under P starvation in 60,1, could be because of the lack of growth and therefore 
continuously low number of cells as seen in the growth experiment, as it is also clear to see that the 
first two samples taken in the other media show very low and stochastic values. There was no 
expression in the DNA samples either, and therefore it could also be due to technical error. It does, 
however, show expression after two days in the Ca-phytate medium indicating that the transcription 
of this gene could be dependent on the presence of phytate (M. A. Jorquera et al., 2013). It would 
have been interesting to see how it would look if the experiment had run for a longer period, as it has 
been observed in Bacillus species that the expression increases over time (S. Lopez 2016). The 
samples taken from the phosphate medium showed some expression, but without any clear trend as 
it changed a lot between the samples and the duplicates. The amounts found in these expressions in 
the phosphate medium were however much higher than those found in the Ca-phytate medium, but 
the stochasticity in these samples make it impossible to extract any information regarding the 
regulation of the gene in this medium (see figure 16).

The random expression found in the phosphate medium, together with the lack of expression in the 
÷P medium indicates that this gene is not a part of a Pho regulon as it would have shown expression 
in the ÷P medium, but not in the phosphate medium in that case (Greiner, 2004). The clear expression 
in the Ca-phytate medium however, indicates that it could be a phytase-gene (M. A. Jorquera et al., 
2013), though perhaps not a part of the Pho regulon. Jorquera et al. (2013) showed that phytate 
addition increased phytase expression, indicating that it could be regulated by the presence of the 
substrate, perhaps instead of the lack of inorganic P. The results obtained in the expression analysis 
are not ideal as there was a mistake in the RNA-extraction of the first replicate in each medium, which 
is the reason why all the results of this, are from duplicates. This makes the statistics weak and it is 
therefore, not possible to make any real conclusions from these data regarding the regulation of this 
gene. In this expression study only a single reference gene, which was the target gene, was used to 
normalize the expression based on DNA samples taken at the same time points as the RNA samples. 
This is not the preferred method to analyze gene expression, as the normalization should be based on 
several reference genes that have been found to show the most stable expression during the 
experiment, and the reaction should be in cDNA samples (Rocha et al., 2015). This could not be done 
in this study however, because of time shortage but the method used should still give an indication of 
the gene expression. A positive control with a strain known to express phytate under these conditions 
was also attempted to be included in this study, but due to technical errors the results from this strain, 
Bacillus licheniformis 14580, could not be used. Because of this, a new experiment over a longer 
period as the growth in the phosphate and Ca-phytate media did not reach the stationary phase, which

5   Conclusion and Perspectives   
60,1, which is suggested to belong to the genus of Arthrobacter based on 16S rRNA gene sequencing 
compared to the NCBI database, followed by genome comparison to Arthrobacter aurescens TC1, 
was able to grow in a medium in which the only P-source is Ca-phytate. The exact inositol phosphate 
composition could however not be determined by HPLC. The genome of this strain was assembled 
with a de novo assembly based on a previous whole genome sequencing performed by Sabrina 
Pittroff, and through searching for phytase genes in Arthrobacter, a “4-phytase” was found in A. 
nitrophenolicus. This, when aligned with the assembled genome with BLAST, matched a gene coding 
for an “dipeptide-binding ABC transporter”. Primers were made for this gene to detect the possible 
expression when grown under the different P-conditions used in this study. Expression of a possible 
“4-phytase” gene was shown when the strain was grown in the Ca-phytate medium, which together 
with the fact that the pH remained the same throughout the experiment, indicates that the strain 
degrades the phytate enzymatically.  
For further study it would be interesting to perform the same growth experiment, but for a longer time 
to see how the expression would change over time as the growth observed during this study did not 
reach the stationary phase, which would be interesting to see the effect of, on the expression analysis. 
To further investigate whether this gene encodes a phytase or if it has a vital role in the utilization of 
phytate-bound P in general, knockout mutations could be made in which this gene was silenced. The 
mutant strain could then be tested for its ability to grow in a medium with the only P-source being 
phytate. As mentioned before, an HPLC analysis of the media would have given more insight into its 
chemical composition, as well as the activity in the form of an altered inositol phosphate profile over 
time. This was however not possible in this study but that is essential for further research in this area. 
If this gene is encoding a phytase, and not an ABC-transporter as suggested by RAST, then with more 
research the enzyme could potentially be used as a feed additive or directly added to soil to degrade 
phytate and increase the amount of available P, depending on the qualities of the enzyme regarding 
activity and stability. In this study it is proposed that Arthrobacter sp. strain 60,1 can degrade phytate 
through the activity of a phytase enzyme, and thereby release phosphate for uptake, making it possible 
for the strain to grow with phytate as the only source of P. This degradation of Phytate, whether it is 
enzymatical or not, could lead to an increase of available P for plant uptake, as Arthrobacter is a 
known PGPR. Further research on a potential use for 60,1 as a biofertilizer or cell factory could be 
very interesting as it could potentially add to the efforts of increasing the P availability in the soil and 
thereby decreasing the need for mined phosphate fertilizer.

Appendix C – RNA/DNA extraction protocol 
 
 Before starting:  
-Incubate pelleted and flash frozen cells for 20 min. with 100 µl lysozyme (1mg/ml in EB buffer pH8) 
-Prepare RLT Plus Buffer + mercapto ethanol, 10ul mercapto ethanol per ml buffer. Prepare fresh 
before each extraction 
Extraction protocol: 
1. Disrupt the cells by adding Buffer RLT + mercapto ethanol. Add 600 µl Buffer RLT Plus. Vortex or 
pipet to mix. 
2. Homogenize by passing cell-lysis buffer suspension through a 0.8 mm needle min. 5 times. 
3. Transfer the homogenized lysate to an AllPrep DNA spin column placed in a 2 ml collection tube 
(supplied). Close lid gently, and centrifuge for 30s at ≥8000 x g (≥10,000 rpm). 
4. Place the AllPrep DNA spin column in a new 2 ml collection tube (supplied). Store at room 
temperature (15–25°C) or at 4°C for later DNA purification. Use the flow-through for RNA 
purification. 
Total RNA purification: 
5. Add 1 volume of 70% ethanol to the flow-through from step 3. Mix well by pipetting. Do not 
centrifuge. Proceed immediately to step 6. 
6. Transfer up to 700 µl of the sample, including any precipitate, to an RNeasy spin column placed 
in a 2 ml collection tube (supplied). Centrifuge for 15 s at ≥8000 x g (≥10,000 rpm). Discard the 
flow-through. Reuse the collection tube in step 7. 
7. Add 700 µl Buffer RW1 to the RNeasy spin column. Close the lid, and centrifuge for 15 s at ≥8000 
x g (≥10,000 rpm). Discard the flow-through. Reuse the collection tube in step 8. 
8. Add 500 µl Buffer RPE to the RNeasy spin column. Close the lid, and centrifuge for 15 s at ≥8000 
x g (≥10,000 rpm). Discard the flow-through. Reuse the collection tube in step 9. 
9. Add 500 µl Buffer RPE to the RNeasy spin column. Close the lid, and centrifuge for 2 min at ≥8000 
x g (≥10,000 rpm). 
Optional: Place the RNeasy spin column in a new 2 ml collection tube (supplied). Discard the old 
collection tube with the flow-through. Centrifuge at full speed for 1 min to dry the spin column 
membrane. 
10. Place the RNeasy spin column in a new 1.5 ml collection tube (supplied). Add 30–50 µl RNase-
free water directly to the spin column membrane. Close the lid gently, and centrifuge for 1 min 
at ≥8000 x g (≥10,000 rpm) to elute the RNA.
