Abstract
In the upcoming years, the introduction of autonomous vehicles to the automobile market will
shape the driving environment. Simulation tools provide the means to evaluate their impacts
and their potential to collaborate in the improvement of the network overall performance. In
this context, the purpose of this study is to investigate the eﬀects of mixed traﬃc (autonomous
and regular vehicles) on congested motorways. Although many studies agree on the beneﬁts of a
fully automated traﬃc environment, diﬀerences arise when discussing what level of penetration
is required to observe signiﬁcant beneﬁts on the system. To address this question, we set up a
microsimulation of a dynamic congestion traﬃc situation in a single lane of a motorway, which
is studied in a Monte Carlo simulation scheme. The representation of autonomous vehicles is
based on the IIDM due to Treiber et al. (2000), while regular vehicles driving behaviour relies on
a stochastic version of IIDM. The experiment setup is applied to study the case of Copenhagen
motorways. Results show that at least a 50% of penetration of autonomous vehicles into the
automobile market is required in order to perceive signiﬁcant beneﬁts in travel time savings and
capacity. Furthermore, from a strategical planning perspective, the results show that at early
stages of implementation it would be more favourable to plan motorways with dedicated lanes
for autonomous vehicles than to plan lanes with mixed traﬃc. However, the ﬁndings should be
considered as a ﬁrst step in the investigation path as they rely on a simple experiment setup,
further research is required to assess the impacts on the network overall performance.
Keywords: Autonomous vehicles, microsimulation, heterogenous vehicle types, IDM, network
eﬀects.

Introduction
The introduction of autonomous vehicles to the automobile market could revolutionize the trans-
port networks. Over the past decades there has been several technological advances in sensing
technology, wireless communications and data processing which set an opportunity for vehicle-
based innovation. In particular, autonomous vehicles have been prototyped with substantial
advances in sensing technology and control intelligence, while wireless communication tech-
nologies provide the opportunity for individual vehicles to communicate with other vehicles
-Vehicle-to-Vehicle communications (V2V )- and with infrastructure -Vehicle-to-Infrastructure
communications (V2I)- [1].
Furthermore, congested traﬃc has become a common situation
which leads to great dissatisfaction among users and high congestion costs for cities. There-
fore, improving the infrastructure operations through intelligent traﬃc management systems
has become the challenging aim of transport planners. In this context, simulation tools provide
the means to investigate the impacts of introducing autonomous vehicles and to analyze their
potential to collaborate in the improvement of the network overall performance. Thus, making
possible to plan accordingly prior to the wide deployment of these vehicles.
For year 2050, trips of autonomous vehicles are expected to be between 7 and 61 percent of the
market share [2]. Hence, it is straightforward to think that for the next years we will live in a
mixed environment where regular cars will coexist with the new autonomous vehicles. Interac-
tions among regular cars and autonomous vehicles will delineate the new driving environment.
For this reason, it is even more important nowadays to predict the impacts of mixed traﬃc
conditions than to analyze the beneﬁts of a fully automated traﬃc which will only be reached
many years later.
Regular cars, which are human driven, and machine controlled autonomous vehicles have dif-
ferent driving logics. While human driving styles range from cautious to aggressive, machine
controlled vehicles would drive all in the exact same way. Moreover, humans have a longer
reaction time to a stimulus than machines but also humans can monitor the behaviour of some
vehicles ahead while autonomous vehicles can only detect with its sensors the behaviour of its
immediate leader. Adding V2V or V2I communications to autonomous vehicles would allow
them to have more or full information of the state of the network and therefore, connected and
autonomous vehicles could result in a more stable driving behaviour.
==PAGE:16==
2
From a modelling perspective it is a challenge to capture the eﬀects of these diﬀerent driving
behaviours.
Over the past sixty years, much of the research eﬀorts focused in ﬁnding the
models that could better describe human driving behaviour. Microscopic traﬃc ﬂow models
are the most suitable to describe the mixed environment of human driven cars and autonomous
machine controlled vehicles because they allow to characterize every vehicle individually and
also to investigate their interaction and collective behaviour.
In the literature, diﬀerent eﬀects of autonomous vehicles with autonomous cruise control (ACC),
and of connected and autonomous vehicles with cooperative autonomous cruise control (CACC)
systems have been reported. Although there is a general agreement on the beneﬁts of a fully
automated traﬃc environment, diﬀerences among researchers arise when discussing what level
of autonomous vehicles market penetration is needed to achieve important beneﬁts in the net-
work performance. Kesting et al.(2010) investigated the impacts of diﬀerent penetration rates
of autonomous and connected vehicles using an advanced version of the Intelligent Driver Model
(IDM) that adapts its default parameters according to the traﬃc situation in which the vehicle
is involved. Their results show that even for a 10 percent of autonomous and connected vehicles
the travel times and the level of the congestion could be signiﬁcantly reduced [3]. On the other
hand, the research investigation carried out by Atkins (2016) recognizes the great potential of in-
telligent vehicles to improve the network performance but suggests that the required proportion
of autonomous and connected vehicles should be between 50 and 75 percent for these vehicles to
take advantage of their enhanced connection capability. In this study they use the microsimula-
tion software package PTV VISSIM 8 which relies on the Wiedemann car-following model [4].
Talebpour et al.(2016) have also studied the eﬀects of introducing autonomous and connected ve-
hicles to a highway driving environment. They have implemented diﬀerent models to represent
accurately the diﬀerences between regular cars, connected vehicles and autonomous vehicles.
Their simulation results show that automation is likely to be more eﬀective than connectivity
alone in preventing congestion. They also reveal that the ﬂow increases as the penetration rate
of autonomous vehicles increases, although the scatter on fundamental diagrams increases from
0 to 50 percent and decreases after this point. They conclude that a substantial increase of
traﬃc ﬂow is only possible under certain market penetration scenarios [1].
The purpose of this study is to investigate the consequences of the mixed traﬃc environment,
of regular cars and autonomous vehicles, on the network performance. In particular, we address
our eﬀorts towards analyzing the eﬀects of introducing autonomous vehicles in congested traﬃc
situations on motorways. Congestion has many drawbacks related to capacity, travel time, traﬃc
safety and fuel consumption, therefore, many attempts have been made to reduce or prevent
it. In this case, we assess if the introduction of autonomous vehicles to the automobile market
is beneﬁcial to reduce the congestion in the network and if it increases the capacity and the
traﬃc ﬂow of the existent infrastructure. Furthermore, we investigate if autonomous vehicles
can increase infrastructure capacity even at low rates of penetration and if the beneﬁts of these

vehicles are proportional to its penetration rate.
This work focuses on the microscopic behaviour of traﬃc. Consequently, a microsimulation of a
bottleneck in a single lane with overtake which constitutes a dynamic congestion traﬃc situation
is studied in a Monte Carlo simulation scheme. The Improved Intelligent Driver Model (IIDM)
as presented by Treiber et al. (2000) is used to model autonomous vehicles [5]. An extension
of the IIDM with stochastic parameters is developed to represent human driving behaviour.
The simulations explore possible changes in the throughput, travel time and structure of the
fundamental diagram under diﬀerent penetration rates of autonomous vehicles. To accomplish
this we implement virtual detectors, at several positions of the single lane studied motorway
segment. They record passage times, speeds and number of vehicles to calculate performance
measures as average travel time, capacity drop and queue and also to determine the macroscopic
variables: ﬂow, speed and density.
The work is structured as follows: Section 2 presents the theoretical background to understand
vehicle to vehicle dynamics and an exhaustive review of the most relevant car-following models
presented in the literature. A special focus in the Intelligent Driver Model (IDM) car-following
model which is used to carry out this study, its acceleration function and the deﬁnition of the
parameters is given in Section 3. The simulation model framework implemented to investigate
the eﬀects of mixed traﬃc on a single lane congested traﬃc situation is presented in Section 4.
Section 5 introduces the inputs to simulate a standard motorway in Copenhagen during high
demand peaks and presents the ﬁndings for this case study. A discussion about the implications
on real-world planning, based on the previous results, is presented in Section 6. The work is
concluded with some summary remarks and future research needs in Section 7.

Conceptual background
2.1
Vehicle dynamics
In order to understand how congestion is formed and how acceleration and deceleration traf-
ﬁc waves propagate, it is relevant to study traﬃc ﬂow theory. It implies to understand the
fundamental characteristics of traﬃc ﬂow and the associated methods to describe the vehicle
dynamics. Examples of the characteristics of traﬃc are capacity and relations between ﬂow,
density and speed. Furthermore, the methods to delineate vehicle dynamics can be classiﬁed in
two groups: macroscopic models and microscopic models.
Macroscopic models describe traﬃc ﬂow in an analog way to liquids or gases in motion, therefore
also referred as continuum models. The dynamic variables, traﬃc density k(x, t), ﬂow q(x, t),
mean speed v(x, t) or the speed variance σ2
v(x, t), are locally aggregated quantities. Macroscopic
models are able to describe collective phenomena because the measures vary across space and
time. Hence, they are suitable to investigate the evolution of congestion and the propagation of
traﬃc waves.
Microscopic models describe the motion of individual vehicles which collectively form the traﬃc
ﬂow. These models describe the reaction of every vehicle depending on the surrounding traﬃc.
The dynamic variables are vehicle position xi(t), speed vi(t) and acceleration ai(t).
These
models are particularly well suited to asses how diﬀerent driving styles aﬀect traﬃc capacity and
stability. Since the evaluation of systems with heterogeneous traﬃc has become more and more
important, microscopic models appear to play an important role. Recent technological advances
have also beneﬁted this class of models that have heavy computational requirements.
2.1.1
Observation of traﬃc ﬂow characteristics
Traﬃc characteristics have commonly been measured using inductive loops which computed local
characteristics of traﬃc. With an inductive loop installed at a given cross section, it is possible
to measure local traﬃc ﬂow and local speed of vehicles at a given position over a period of time.
==PAGE:19==
2 Conceptual background
5
Oppositely, with inductive loops it is not possible to measure density which is an instantaneous
traﬃc characteristic and requires looking at a complete segment of road at a given time. Hence,
inductive loops traﬃc observations provide only local or aggregated information.
However, the situation is changing and GPS is now being used to gather traﬃc information. GPS
traces favor to observe the traﬃc ﬂow characteristics through the space-time domain. This kind
of data provides the trajectories of vehicles and therefore, the fundamental relations between
ﬂow, density and speed can be empirically revealed.
The following fundamental relations of traﬃc characteristics arise from the study "Deﬁnition
af trængsel" developed by DTU Transport [6]. The observed data is based on GPS traces and
corresponds to Helsingør motorway, north of Gl. Holte southbound, for the period Monday 24
to Friday 28 of September of 2012.
Figure 1 shows the observed fundamental relation between speed and ﬂow. The plot presents
two diﬀerent maximum ﬂow rates and fundamental curves, namely the pre-queue and queue
discharge. The pre-queue ﬂow corresponds to the observations of traﬃc just before the queue
begins to form in a bottleneck cross section. Hence, this ﬂow curve represents an unstable state
that leads to congestion onset within a short period. The queue discharge ﬂow curve is observed
while a queue exists upstream of the bottleneck [7]. A signiﬁcant diﬀerence among the observed
maximum ﬂows is pictured in Figure 1. While pre-queue maximum ﬂow reaches 300 vehicles in
5 minutes, during queue discharge the maximum ﬂow is limited to 200 vehicles.
Figure 1: Observed relation between travel speed and traﬃc ﬂow for Helsingør motorway [6].
Traﬃc ﬂow is not the best measure of congestion because when congestion arises, traﬃc ﬂow
falls. Instead, density continues to increase although congestion has emerged. Figure 2 presents

the fundamental relation between speed and density. In the ﬁgure, density has been plotted as
the number of vehicles in one kilometer segment of two lanes of motorway.
The maximum measured density is approximately 70 vehicles per kilometer per lane which
implies that the distance gap among vehicles in congestion is approximately 14 meters. From
the ﬁgure, it is observed that congestion starts to originate when vehicles drive at approximately

Congestion is an expression of vehicles reduced mobility, generated as a result of the imbalance
between traﬃc demand and infrastructure supply. In the previous plots, Figure 1 and Figure
2, three diﬀerent levels of congestion were identiﬁed: insigniﬁcant congestion, great congestion
and critical congestion. Travel speed, traﬃc density and traﬃc ﬂow relative to capacity are
indicators of congestion of a given road. However, the limits of speed, density and ﬂow that
deﬁne the congestion levels can vary depending on the road type and the country.
2.1.2

2.1.4
Strength and challenges of models to represent vehicle dynamics
Macroscopic models represent vehicle dynamics based on certain laws from physics which are
useful to understand traﬃc ﬂow theory, and are suitable to analyze general aspects of traﬃc
networks. Therefore, this class of models imposes a given structure on the system. Instead,
microscopic models mimic the motion of individual vehicles describing their speed and acceler-
ation behaviour. Macroscopic models can be classiﬁed as a top-down imposed structure while
==PAGE:24==
10
microscopic models emerge as a bottom-up analysis [10].
As observed in Section 2.1.3, the fundamental equation of traﬃc holds when all the vehicles
present a uniform speed and if not, it only holds considering the average space mean speed.
Moreover, independently of which formulation of speed is used, speed is deﬁned as a function of
density which is an overall system characteristic. Macroscopic models have only one equation
that applies to the entire driving process and do not consider diﬀerent driving styles or traﬃc
regimes. Hence, the macroscopic approach is only applicable to homogeneous settings. It is
not possible to represent heterogeneity among modelled vehicles neither an inhomogeneity of
the experiment layout (areas of reduced speed, intersections, stop lights). Furthermore, this
aggregated models are not able to capture the congested traﬃc regime in a realistic way. There-
fore, their descriptive power is frequently of concern. On the positive side, they are simple and
mathematically attractive.
Oppositely, the microscopic approach studies large systems of equations to represent the motion
of each individual vehicle and to get to the collective behaviour by integration. This computa-
tionally intensive cost that microscopic models have, adds a high level of ﬂexibility. Therefore,
in a microscopic model it is possible to simulate any type of heterogeneity. It is possible to
represent diversity among vehicles and also to investigate the eﬀect of an inhomogeneity in the
simulated network as for example, an area of reduced speed.
Microscopic models play out their advantages when representing heterogeneous drivers and vehi-
cles and this is a very important feature when simulating the eﬀects of new driving systems.

Car-following models
2.2.1
Car-following theory
Car-following models are the most important representatives of microscopic traﬃc ﬂow models.
In a strict sense, car-following models describe the drivers behavior only in the presence of
interactions with other vehicles while free traﬃc ﬂow is described by a separate model. In a more
general sense, car-following models include all traﬃc situations such as car-following situations,
free traﬃc and also stationary traﬃc. In this case, the microscopic model is complete [11].
Each driver-vehicle combination i is described by the state variables position xi(t) (position of
the front bumper along the road segment that increases in the driving direction) and speed
vi(t) as a function of the time t, and by the attribute vehicle length li. Some models include
additional state variables as the acceleration a = dv/dt.

For more than sixty years, researchers have investigated a diversity of car-following models. In

Forbes model, 1958
The Forbes model ensures a safe headway.
"To ensure safety, the time gap between a vehicle and the vehicle in front of it should be always
greater than or equal to reaction time".
Forbes and Pipes models are essentially equivalent, the speed coeﬃcient is just interpreted in

smini(t) = τi · vi(t)
Both Pipes and Forbes are simple models that present some problems when setting up a car-
following simulation. In ﬁrst place, the models are not able to represent free-ﬂow traﬃc regimes
because when the gap with the leading vehicle is very large, the speed and the acceleration tend
to an inﬁnite value. Hence, an external constraint has to be imposed in order to limit maximum
speed and maximum acceleration. In the same way, when a vehicle driving at a high speed has a
small gap with its leader, the deceleration might result unreasonably high. Therefore, maximum
deceleration should also be constrained. However, adding these constraints to the models could
introduce new problems as a vehicle colliding into its leading vehicle.

IDM: Intelligent Driver Model
The Intelligent Driver Model (IDM) by Treiber et al. (2000) belongs to the class of continuous-
time car-following models where the acceleration is of the general form [5]:
ai = dv
dt = f(si, vi, ∆vi)
The IDM acceleration depends on the vehicle own speed vi, the gap with its leader si and also
on the speed diﬀerence with the leading vehicle ∆vi, which plays an essential stabilizing role
when approaching congested traﬃc and avoiding rear-end collisions [12]. The acceleration only
depends on the immediate leader and it is instantaneous in time.
The IDM is a complete microscopic model that can describe all traﬃc regimes that may arise,
such as car-following situations, free traﬃc and also stationary traﬃc. The acceleration fulﬁlls
the following conditions [11]:
• It is a strictly decreasing function of speed vi. Moreover, the vehicle accelerates towards a
desired speed v0 if not constrained by other vehicles or obstacles.
• It is an increasing function of the distance si to the leading vehicle.
• It is an increasing function of the speed of the leading vehicle vi−1.
• A minimum gap s0 to the leading vehicle is maintained also during standstill. However,
there is no backwards movement if the gap has become smaller than s0 in the previous
step.
Transitions between the diﬀerent traﬃc regimes are smooth, i.e.
the time derivative of the
acceleration function is ﬁnite at all times.
The IDM is characterized by an equilibrium distance to the leading vehicle which is never less
than a "safe distance" s0 + v · T, where s0 is a minimum standstill gap and T the time gap to
the leading vehicle; and by an intelligent braking strategy that controls how slower vehicles or
obstacles are approached.

3.3.2
Maximum deceleration
The maximum deceleration Bmax does not appear explicitly in the IDM formulation. As previ-
ously said, the best well-known characteristic of the IDM is that the model is collision-free, this
is due to its dynamic term but also to the fact that the deceleration is not restricted. Hence,
in a critical situation the IDM can determine high values of deceleration which could be physi-
cally unrealistic. In order to avoid this unrealistic feature, the IDM can be implemented with a
constrained maximum deceleration Bmax.
Once more, the maximum deceleration parameter depends on several characteristics as the speed,
the driver, the vehicle and the weather conditions which have a high inﬂuence on the braking
friction coeﬃcient. Therefore, most publications report separately the maximum deceleration for

3.4
Calibration
The fundamental relations of traﬃc: ﬂow, density, speed and spacing are calibrated with v0 (at
low density), δ( in the transition region), T(at high density), and s0 (in congested traﬃc) [25].
Furthermore, in open systems the vehicle dynamics are controlled by the inﬂow, the strength of
the bottleneck and the history of the previous traﬃc situations.
The stability behavior of traﬃc in the IDM model is determined mainly by the model parameters
A, B and T [25]. Traﬃc becomes more unstable for decreasing A (which corresponds to an
increased required acceleration time), and for decreasing T corresponding to reduced safe time
headways. Furthermore, the instability increases with higher B because an increased desired
deceleration B corresponds to a less anticipative braking behavior.

Simulation model framework
This section presents a simulation based approach to investigate the eﬀects of mixed traﬃc
(regular and autonomous vehicles) on the network performance, speciﬁcally in motorways during
congested periods. To this end, we set up a microscopic analysis of a dynamic congested system
of heterogeneous traﬃc in a single lane with a permanent bottleneck. We simulate vehicles with
diﬀerent driving behaviours in a 13 kilometer segment of a motorway. The microsimulation
framework is structured in four modules: demand, car-following, overtaking and performance
indicators. Finally, the microscopic simulation is embedded in a Monte Carlo simulation scheme
in order to learn the eﬀects for a distribution of the input parameters.
On the one hand, the performance of dynamic congested systems depends on the type and
strength of the traﬃc breakdown that is implemented [5].
Section 4.1 includes a complete
description of the experiment layout and the bottleneck that induces the traﬃc breakdown. On
the other hand, open systems dynamics are controlled by the inﬂow, i.e. the ﬂow at the upstream
boundary, and the history of previous perturbations [5]. These concepts are developed in the
demand module in Section 4.3.
The characterization of the driving environment is addressed in Section 4.4 where the parametriza-
tion of the deterministic IIDM and a stochastic version of the IIDM is presented for autonomous
and regular vehicles, respectively. When simulating heterogeneous traﬃc, it is required to im-
plement vehicle overtaking in order to avoid the unrealistic formation of a long queue behind the
slowest vehicle [11]. This procedure is explained in the overtaking module in Section 4.5.
In order to obtain aggregated performance indicators of the simulated system, we implement
virtual detectors at several positions of the studied motorway segment that record passage times,
speeds and the number of vehicles. Hence, the simulations will allow to explore the changes in
the throughput, travel time, queue and in the structure of the fundamental diagrams under
diﬀerent hypothetical scenarios.
==PAGE:40==

4.1
Layout
The experiment is set up as an opened system of one link that represents a 13 kilometer segment
of a single lane in a motorway. The desired speed along the link is 110 km/h in accordance with
the maximum speed limit for motorways in Denmark.
An approach to simulate a congested system is to introduce a ﬂow conserving inhomogeneity to
the system. Meaning this, to make a model parameter vary as a function of the position in the
studied segment. For the IDM, the desired speed v0 and the time gap T showed to be suitable
parameters [25]. In this case, a 1 kilometer area of 40 km/h maximum speed is included in the
studied motorway segment. The desired speed of the vehicles driving through the segment will
therefore depend on the position xi of vehicle i in the link.
From the simulations, it can be observed that the local variation of the parameter v0 acts as a
bottleneck because the outﬂow qout at the downstream boundary of the segment is lower than the
inﬂow qin at the upstream boundary due to the capacity drop induced by the traﬃc breakdown.
Traﬃc breaks down with a ﬁxed downstream front at the bottleneck while the upstream front
propagates further upstream. The region with reduced speed can be interpreted as an uphill
gradient region, however, the type of bottleneck (on-ramps, lane closing or uphill gradient) seems
not to inﬂuence the congestion dynamics that are generated [5].
The 1 kilometer area of reduced speed which acts as a bottleneck starts 10 kilometers ahead of
the upstream boundary. Hence, the inﬂow qin at the upstream boundary is isolated of the direct
eﬀects of congestion propagating upstream of the bottleneck and presents free-ﬂow traﬃc during
the entire simulation period of all the evaluated scenarios. Moreover, the vehicles are launched
from standstill several kilometers before they reach the 13 kilometer analyzed segment so that
the studied segment is not aﬀected by vehicles speeding up to reach their desired speed.

Monte Carlo experiments follow the pattern:

• Generation of random inputs from a probability distribution over the domain.
• Deterministic computation of the inputs.
• Aggregation of results.

4.3
Demand module
The demand module indicates if a vehicle i at time step j is launched into the system or not.
A vehicle will enter the system only if the distance gap with its leader sji is greater than the
minimum required launching gap slji.
The demand is assumed to vary over time. The variation of demand is given by the variation
of the minimum required launching gap slji. The highest demand entails that the minimum
required launching gap slji is equal to the desired safe gap DesSji. In practise this would mean
that vehicles follow each other at the minimum safe distance.
When demand decreases the
spacing among vehicles is greater and therefore, the required launching gap is greater than the
desired safe gap. The variation of the demand that is launched into the system over time, implies
that the inﬂow qin at the upstream boundary of the 13 kilometer analyzed segment will variate
accordingly.
Furthermore, the congested traﬃc dynamics also depend on the history of previous perturba-
tions. Hence, a warm up period for the simulation is set up in order to analyze a system that
performs at a steady state and avoid the inﬂuence of the system loading. During the warm up
period the demand is assumed constant and equal to the demand at the ﬁrst time step of the
simulation.

4.4.1
Autonomous vehicles modelling
A deterministic framework to model the acceleration of autonomous vehicles is suitable because
these machine controlled vehicles can be assumed to perform in the exact same way every time
they face the same situation.
Moreover, it can be fairly assumed that they can react almost instantaneously to any stimulus
from the driving environment therefore, their reaction time ri is set equal to 0.
Due to this increased responsiveness it is reasonable to think that the time gap T between these
vehicles will be smaller than the safe time gap of regular human driven cars. Furthermore, they
are an homogeneous group which desired speed v0 will correspond to the speed limit of the
road or any regulatory or technological restriction. However, there is a great deal of uncertainty
about how these vehicles will perform as they are still not operating on the transport networks
to a large-scale. Therefore, a sensitivity analysis of these two parameters for the case study of
a motorway in Copenhagen is presented in Section 5.3.1.
4.4.2
Regular vehicles modelling
An important characteristic of regular vehicles which are human driven is the reaction time
which is generally considered to be of the order of 1 second but strongly varies between diﬀerent
drivers [12].
Hence, the population of regular vehicles is a heterogeneous group.
Moreover,
heterogeneity of human driven vehicles is also expressed with respect to desired speed. There are
drivers which are more aggressive and tend to drive faster than the motorway speed limits, while
others choose to drive slower. The group heterogeneity is modelled incorporating two stochastic
parameters to the IIDM, namely reaction time ri and desired speed v0i. The distribution of
both parameters follow normal distributions which are determined to ﬁt the available data as
presented in Section 5.1 for the case study.
Modelling wise adding the reaction time as a delayed adaption of the acceleration interferes with
the collision-free property of the IDM. It has been demonstrated that when the acceleration
parameter A is set equal to 3 m/s2, the IDM model presents an instable traﬃc ﬂow when
reaction time ri is higher than 0.7 seconds, due to the delayed response to the input stimulus [12].
Therefore, for this stochastic IIDM framework the maximum reaction time was limited to 0.5
seconds.
Another characteristic of human driving is the capacity to drive looking ahead, drivers con-
sider their leading vehicle behaviour but also can anticipate the behaviour of 2 or 3 vehicles
ahead. This is a stabilizing feature of human driving behaviour that is not modelled in this
stochastic version of the IIDM. Hence, the limitation of the destabilizing eﬀect of reaction time
==PAGE:47==

is expected to be compensated by the driving anticipation stabilizing feature of human driving
behaviour.
4.5
Vehicle overtake module
The procedure of overtaking is modeled as a discrete choice at a given time step j between two
alternatives: no overtake and overtake. A vehicle can choose to overtake, however, the required
space in the contiguous lane to perform the manoeuvre could not always be available. Hence,
when vehicles select the overtaking alternative as the ¨best¨ one, they also need to check for
availability to perform the manoeuvre. To this end, a shadow lane is modelled. The shadow
lane, considering equilibrium principles, is assumed to have the same traﬃc density than the
modelled lane. The probability of ﬁnding the required space to perform the overtake manoeuvre
is modelled as a function of the traﬃc density in the shadow lane. At a high level of traﬃc density,
i.e. congested regimes, there is a low probability of overtaking; while in free-ﬂow regimes there
is a high probability of overtaking.
The overtaking procedure is formulated based on the following assumptions:
• The ﬁrst vehicle in the system cannot be overtaken and therefore its desired speed is the
highest in the system.
• The speed of a vehicle before and after overtaking is the same (vi = vo).
• The ¨best¨ alternative is the one that allows a vehicle to maximize its speed, therefore,
it is the alternative in which the vehicle can perform a greater acceleration (incentive
criterion).

To investigate how the speed and the traﬃc ﬂow vary across the location on the motorway due
to the propagating eﬀects of traﬃc breakdown, we evaluate the information which is collected
in a series of intermediate detectors that are located over the analyzed segment.
Fundamental diagrams
The fundamental diagrams describe the statistical relation between the macroscopic traﬃc ﬂow
variables of ﬂow, density and speed. As it was previously explained and as Equation 3 and
Equation 4 show, ﬂow and density relate to the average time and distance gap, respectively.
Hence, the underlying assumption of fundamental diagrams is that vehicles will behave in a
similar way while traﬃc conditions are also assumed similar [7].
In order to determine the fundamental diagrams it is assumed a stationary period of one hour
of the simulation period. The data is collected with an interval time of 1 minute from virtual
detectors that are located at several ﬁxed locations on the studied motorway segment. With
the detector collected data we determine the ﬂow q, the space mean speed vs and the density
k.

Case study
The simulation framework previously presented is applied to model a standard motorway in
Copenhagen. The simulation experiment of a 13 kilometer segment of a congested motorway
where traﬃc breaks down due to an area with speed reduction is set up. The aim is to investigate
the overall eﬀects of increasing the penetration rate of autonomous vehicles on the motorway
performance.
General parameters of the model have already been presented in Section 3 and 4. However, the
deﬁnition of case speciﬁc parameters is addressed in this section. The model is then calibrated to
mimic the driving dynamics of a standard motorway in Copenhagen during high demand periods.
In order to investigate the impacts of autonomous vehicles on the motorway performance, in
ﬁrst place, a sensitivity analysis of some of parameters which characterize the autonomous
driving behaviour is developed. Finally, we produce an analysis of the performance indicators
for diﬀerent levels of penetration of autonomous vehicles.
5.1
Input parameters
The deﬁnition of case speciﬁc input parameters fosters the ability of the model to mimic the
speed, capacity and driving behaviour that are generally observed in Copenhagen motorways.
To this end, we analyze the available data to deﬁne the actual demand proﬁle and the driving
characteristics (desired speed and reaction time) of motorways in Copenhagen.
Demand proﬁle
Traﬃc demand depends on the time of the day. It is generally possible to identify two peaks
of high demand across a day, which correspond with the commuting time periods. Motorways
performance during high demand periods are generally characterized by congested regimes.
Hence, it is relevant for this study to analyze the demand proﬁle during a peak period.
We study three hours of a peak demand period in which the highest peak is reached after one and
a half hours. The demand variation during the simulated period is approximated to a normal
distribution.

Desired speed proﬁle
The desired speed of a driver-vehicle combination is deﬁned by the speed driven when other
vehicles do not inﬂuence the driver. There are drivers which are more aggressive and tend to
drive faster than the motorway speed limit, while others choose to drive slower. Therefore,
regular vehicles present diﬀerences in their desired speeds. We investigate the desired speed
available data to ﬁnd a distribution that reﬂects the behaviour of these vehicles.
Figure 13 presents the evaluated data for motorways with a maximum speed of 110 km/h in
UK [26], US [27] and Denmark [28]. The available information for Denmark belongs to the ISA
project that investigates road accident statistics and therefore, has targeted its data collection to
a group of young drivers as they represent the most aggressive driving style. In this study we seek
to represent the entire drivers population, i.e. aggressive and cautious styles, to this end we have
considered a distribution with a greater standard deviation than the one presented in the ISA
project. The desired speed proﬁle is assumed to follow a normal distribution DS ∼N(110, 132)
km/h truncated between 80 and 145 km/h. The deﬁned desired speed proﬁle is presented in the
ﬁgure together with the proﬁles of the US and UK that were considered as reference guides.
The diversity among regular vehicles is therefore modelled incorporating the desired speed as
a stochastic variable. Every regular vehicle is characterized with a desired speed v0i which is
randomly drawn from the previously deﬁned distribution.

Reaction time proﬁle
Drivers reaction time is a consequence of the physiological aspects of sensing, perceiving, deciding
and performing an action. It is of the order of 1 second but it strongly varies between the diﬀerent
drivers [12]. Hence, regular vehicles also present diﬀerences among each other in the reaction
time parameter.
Given that data for drivers reaction time in Copenhagen was not available, we base our parameter
deﬁnition on a publication of Xiao-Yun Lu et al. where they simulate the drivers reaction time
as a normal distribution R ∼N(0.66, 0.12) seconds [29]. However, due to the limitation of the
IDM to implement a delayed acceleration function and model a stable traﬃc regime respecting
its collision free property, the reaction time distribution had to be adjusted for this study. The
reaction time was limited to a maximum of 0.5 seconds. Hence, the reaction time drivers proﬁle
is assumed to follow a normal distribution R ∼N(0.5, 0.12) truncated between 0.3 and 0.5
seconds. Every regular vehicle is characterized with a reaction time ri which is randomly drawn
from this distribution.

5.4
Discussion and limitations
In the preceding sections, we investigate the consequences of mixed traﬃc of regular cars and
autonomous vehicles on the network performance by assessing motorway performance indica-
tors and their variation across scenarios. Average travel time and congestion is reduced when
increasing the share of autonomous vehicles, while capacity increases. However, signiﬁcant ben-
eﬁts only show when the rate of penetration of AVs is higher than 50%. This result is aligned
with what Talebpour et al.(2016) and the technical report by Atkins (2016) present [1] [4].
Furthermore, we evaluate diﬀerent values for the desired speed v0 and the time gap T parameter
that characterize the autonomous driving behaviour. The results of the sensitivity analysis of
both parameters indicate that in a mixed traﬃc environment, if AVs driving characteristics are
much more cautious than regular vehicles, the motorway performance could be worse than in
the base scenario, where AVs do not operate. It is found that, if the desired speed of AVs is
lower than 80 km/h in a motorway with 110 km/h speed limit, with AVs penetration rate being
50%; the overall performance could be worse than for the base scenario. Moreover, the time gap
parameter shows high dependence with the overall perfomance of the motorway in mixed traﬃc
conditions. Therefore, reducing the required time gap of AVs sets an opportunity to optimize
the overall performance.
The results presented should be considered in the context of the limitations that are given
by the simple modelling experiment and the high uncertainty about autonomous driving be-
haviour.
The limitations of the modelling framework are given by:
• An homogeneous segment of a motorway is modelled. There is no consideration of areas
with on-ramps and oﬀ-ramps.
• A single lane with overtake is modelled. Although overtake is modelled the friction that
the overtaking maneuver generates in the contiguous lane which is used to overtake is not
modelled. Then, expanding directly results that correspond to a single lane, to motorways
with more lanes implies an overestimation.
• Heterogeneity among regular vehicles is considered, however, trucks are not included in
the simulation.
• The eﬀects of congestion generated by a permanent bottleneck is investigated. Hence, the
simulations show how the queue builds upstream of the bottleneck cross section. Diﬀerent
vehicle dynamics could be observed if temporary bottlenecks were modelled.
==PAGE:66==
52
Furthermore, in order to model AVs driving behaviour a number of assumption were done.
• A strong assumption stating that all AVs will behave in the same way is formulated.
This assumption could have signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the simulation results, as beneﬁts
of AVs are maximized because of their homogeneity.
It is certain that all AVs could
behave in the exact same way, although it is also possible that they will behave diﬀerently.
Vehicle manufacturers will target their users requirements and this would output a range of
diﬀerent driving behaviours. However, regulations will deﬁne how diverse the AVs driving
behaviour can be.
• AVs are modelled to perform in an advantageous way from a system perspective. This is
assumed when modelling the safe deceleration rate when overtaking Bov equal to 0. AVs
only overtake if they do not aﬀect the performance of the rest of the vehicles in the system,
in this way, maximizing their eﬀect of traﬃc facilitators of the motorway performance.
Beyond the high uncertainty of autonomous driving behaviour and the limitations of the ex-
periment setup, the results are conclusive. There are high potential beneﬁts of an automated
environment, however, early stages of implementation could be challenging and signiﬁcant ben-
eﬁts may be delayed until a 50% of the automobile market share is autonomous.
Additionally, further research should study the impacts of autonomous vehicles on the local
network.
This work is also limited to autonomous vehicles, however, beneﬁts of connected
autonomous vehicles should also be investigated.

to prepare the regulatory framework.

7
Conclusion
Until recently, the concept of autonomous mobility seemed like science ﬁction. Today we are
spectators of one of the largest technological races that could shape future mobility. Several
automobile manufacturers are running to lead the development of autonomous vehicles (AVs)
and their wide deployment into the transport system. Although it is almost certain that AVs
will operate in the near future, it could take several decades to reach a fully automated driving
environment.
It is still uncertain how autonomous driving behaviour will diﬀerentiate from the actual be-
haviour of regular vehicles, however, this situation leads to analyze which enhanced driving
capabilities could create more eﬃcient networks. It is likely that in the early stages AVs will
operate in a cautious way and only when tested in larger scales, they will take full advantage of
their enhanced capabilities.
Therefore, the operation of transport infrastructure during the upcoming years will be delineated
by mixed traﬃc of autonomous and regular vehicles; while also a diversity of autonomous driving
behaviours could be observed. A huge challenge is set for transport planners and decision makers
who will have to deﬁne the regulatory framework and transport system operation looking after
the interests of future mobility.
In this context, it is relevant to investigate the eﬀects of autonomous vehicles on the performance
of the transport network, at diﬀerent stages of implementation. Consequently, we present a
microscopic simulation framework which recognizes diﬀerent vehicles types and uses the deter-
ministic IIDM to model autonomous vehicles and a stochastic version of the IIDM for regular
vehicles. By employing the presented framework on the experiment setup of a single lane of
a motorway segment, the potential travel time savings and capacity increase for the diﬀerent
implementation phases of AVs are assessed.
The sensitivity analysis performed on the desired speed v0 and the time gap T parameters
revealed that introducing AVs which drive in a more cautious way than regular vehicles could
result in a potential worsening of the indicators of traﬃc performance. Moreover, it was also
recognized the high inﬂuence that the time gap parameter T has on the network performance
in mixed traﬃc conditions. Leaning technological development towards reduced required safety
==PAGE:72==
58
gaps for AVs could set an opportunity to improve network performance even in mixed traﬃc
environments.
The results obtained of our experiment investigation show that there is high potential for sig-
niﬁcant time savings and capacity increase at high penetration rates of AVs. At low shares, AVs
enhanced capabilities are limited by the behaviour of the other regular vehicles. The penetra-
tion rate of AVs and network potential beneﬁts do not follow a linear relation. Furthermore,
the marginal beneﬁts at high penetration rates are much larger than at low rates. In order to
notice signiﬁcant improvements of the performance indicators, the market penetration of AVs
should exceed a 50%. Results indicate time savings, with respect to the actual travel time of:
12% for a 50% penetration rate, increasing to 20% at 75% of AVs and reaching almost 45% in
a completely automated driving environment.
Finally, we open a discussion about how it is more convenient to plan our existing motorway
infrastructure with the view that AVs will, in a short span of time, be integrated to traﬃc ﬂow.
Based on the results of our experiment setup, from a system optimum perspective and at early
stages of implementation, it is better to plan dedicated lanes for AVs than to plan lanes with
mixed traﬃc in the motorway system. However, there are several challenges that would need to
be considered as the entering and exit of AVs to dedicated lanes, the mixed traﬃc behaviour in
on-ramps and oﬀ-ramps and in the local network.
The ﬁndings should be considered in the context of the proposed assumptions about AVs capabil-
ities which were translated to the model parametrization. Furthermore, the results correspond
to a simpliﬁed experiment which was setup on homogeneous segment of a motorway during
a congested period. The impacts of AVs on the proximity of ramps locations on motorways,
on non-congested periods, and local network should be studied. Note that the simulation was
developed using the IIDM to represent autonomous and regular vehicles, however, other models
could have been used and it remains for future research to contrast ﬁndings across diﬀerent
models setup. Moreover, this work does not consider the potential induced demand due to the
capacity gain, being the purpose to analyze only the changes in the vehicle dynamics.

Appendix 3
In this Appendix, the methodology to estimate the total beneﬁts for each of the simulated scenarios is
presented.
The mean average time a vehicle requires to complete the 13 kilometer segment of motorway in each
scenario, is obtained from the previous simulations. Furthermore, the Ministry of Transport of Denmark
publishes that the Value of Time (VoT) for a commuting trip in free-traﬃc is 1.52 dkk/min while the
VoT in congested traﬃc is 2.28 dkk/min [32].
In order to identify the travel time that is driven in free-traﬃc and in congestion in each of the simulated
scenarios, a simpliﬁed estimation is implemented. The optimal travel time to complete the motorway
segment is deﬁned as the ratio of the 13 kilometers length and the maximum speed 110 km/h. Hence,
the optimal travel time in all scenarios is assumed 7.1 minutes.
The diﬀerence between the average
travel time and the optimal travel time is considered as the travel time in queue. Finally, the average
cost of travelling can be calculated as the product of travel time and VoT, taking into consideration the
diﬀerentiation of VoT according to the traﬃc regime.
The mean throughput is also an output of the previous simulations. It considers all the vehicles that pass
through the segment during the 3 hours of simulation period.
With the previous values it is already possible to plot the demand curve, as it is presented in Figure
21.
For calculations reasons, in the following table, the beneﬁts are computed divided into new beneﬁts and
direct beneﬁts and later summed to determine the total beneﬁts. Beneﬁts are calculated as the area
below the demand curve and above an horizontal line at the travel cost of a given scenario.
The deﬁned total beneﬁts, therefore, correspond to a 13 kilometer segment of a single lane, during 1
peak period of 3 hours. Then, the total beneﬁts are scaled to an year value. To this end, the calculated
beneﬁts are multiplied by 2, recognizing that all days have 2 peak periods, and by 285, which represent
the working days of an year. Finally, the total beneﬁts which are scaled to one year are presented in
millions of dkk (M dkk).
