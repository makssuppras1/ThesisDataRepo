Abstract
I analyze the latent representations of hierarchical VAEs and investigate the decoder vari­
ance, particularly in connection to model uncertainty. Through qualitative analysis, I find
indications that VAEs learn feature hierarchies along the latent depth. I observe that ELBO
and decoder variance correlate to input complexity, which could explain why VAEs tend to
estimate high density on specific images outside of the training domain. I propose a com­
plexity correction method that yields improvements in out­of­domain detection (OODD)
with ELBO and decoder variance. I show that, after correcting for complexity, the decoder
variance can be used for OODD, which indicates that it incorporates a notion of uncer­
tainty beyond the low­level statistics captured by an image compression algorithm. Fur­
thermore, I find that the magnitude of the decoder variance correlates with specific visual
attributes and can be used to classify them. My experiments provide support to specula­
tions about hierarchical VAEs made in recent research, inform the discussion about their
applicability to uncertainty modelling and anomaly detection, and give insight into the role
of the decoder variance.

1
Introduction
In this work, I focus on a group of probabilistic models called Variational Autoencoders
(VAEs) [1] in particular VAEs with hierarchical latent structure. I study the way the infor­
mation is encoded by specific internal representations of those models and investigate the
relation of the parameters of the latent distributions to low­level image statistics, high­level
semantics, domain shift and uncertainty.
The character of this project is mainly to improve the understanding of VAEs through an
in­depth analysis and to provide ground for further discussion and development of said
models, therefore proposing novel methods within this work is of secondary concern.
In the following sections, I first introduce some fundamental concepts, then refer to related
research to motivate and ground this project, and finally discuss the aims and contribu­
tions.
1.1
Theoretical background
1.1.1
Unsupervised learning and generative models
In unsupervised learning, contrary to supervised learning, there is no known target value
to predict [2, Ch. 1], which makes it possible to train the models on large datasets without
a need for target labels, which can be expensive to obtain. A particular type of (usually)
unsupervised models are generative models, which try to model the input distribution and
allow to sample new data points. Generative models that maximize the likelihood of the
data directly or through its approximation are called explicit likelihood models. Explicit
likelihood models allow for calculating the density (or its approximation) for a given sam­
ple, which can be used to evaluate and compare several models in a ”standard” way using
a test dataset. Popular explicit likelihood models include:
• Autoregressive models [3] – formulate the generative process in a sequential man­
ner, where the input distribution is factorized as:
p(x) =
n
∏
i=1
p (xi | x1, . . . , xi−1)

so each element of x (e.g. each pixel in an image) is conditioned on all ”previous”
elements. Autoregressive models (e.g. PixelCNN [3] and PixelRNN [4]) can gen­
erate compelling images, but due to the sequential nature are computationally very
expensive.
• Flow­based models [5] – in which the change of variable rule is used to map the
data distribution pX(x) to a chosen prior distribution pH(h) (e.g. Gaussian) through
an invertible function h = f(x). The need for the mapping function f to be invertible
makes it more difficult to scale the model up and requires some ”tricks” to achieve
a mapping function that is powerful enough to fit a complex dataset [6].
• Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) [1] – VAEs’ generative process p(x|z) (parametrized
by a decoder network) conditions the data x on some latent variable z. Distribution
of z is approximated through amortized variational inference [2, Sec. 10.1] by intro­
ducing an approximate posterior q(z|x) (parametrized by an encoder network). The
model is trained by maximizing the lower bound on the data likelihood (evidence

lower bound – ELBO). As VAEs are the main focus of this work I discuss them in

• Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPMs) [7][8] – diffusion models de­
fine the generative process as a Markov chain in which source noise (distributed
according to some prior e.g. white noise) is gradually transformed into a meaningful
image (or other data type). The reverse process (image to noise) is defined as a ran­
dom walk from the original image. The model is trained to remove the noise added
in the reverse process and reconstruct the original image. Similarly to VAEs diffu­
sion models also optimize the likelihood lower bound. DDPMs have been shown to
be able to generate images of very high fidelity while remaining higher diversity than
GANs [9]. Despite many similarities to VAEs (DDPMs are a special case of a VAE
with a very specific generative distribution and approximate posterior), DDPMs do
not produce meaningful latent space.
On the contrary, implicit likelihood models do not need to define the density function di­
rectly and are trained through optimizing some other objective, often through generating
samples from the model [10]. Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) [11] are very
popular implicit likelihood generative models. They are based on a minimax game be­
tween a generative model which aims to produce samples that will ”fool” the discriminative
model that in turn tries to classify between real data points from the generated samples.
GANs generate images of very high fidelity, however doubts about the diversity of images
they produce have been raised [12][9]. GANs also tend to be more difficult to train com­
pared to explicit likelihood models, as their optimization objective relies on a good balance
between the generative and discriminative model.
In this project, I focus exclusively on VAEs. As probabilistic explicit likelihood models
VAEs should, in principle, have some appealing properties. The high diversity of the im­
ages generated by VAEs (contrary to GANs that provide good fidelity, but low diversity)
[12] supports the hypothesis that VAEs cover the breadth of the data distribution better
than GANs. VAE’s ability to model complex data distributions can be used for anomaly
detection [13] [14][15]. Other possible applications of density modelling would be uncer­
tainty estimation and active learning.
VAEs can be easily evaluated and compared to other explicit likelihood models and have
recently been shown to yield competitive results in terms of likelihood [16]. Compared
to other high­performing explicit likelihood models such as autoregressive models and
DDPMs, VAEs are faster during inference and generation as they only require a single
pass through the model. VAEs learn latent representations that can be useful for semi­
supervised learning [17][18] or data compression [12].

1.1.3
Hierarchical VAEs
A hierarchical VAE can be seen as an extension of a simple VAE that introduces multiple
levels of latent variables [21]. The dependencies between latent variables should allow
the model to learn covariance (which is usually ignored within single latent distribution to
reduce the number of parameters to the diagonal instead of the full covariance matrix) and
more complex patterns in the data. In a basic form with L latent layers the joint probability
pθ(x, z) is now factorized as:
pθ(x, z) = pθ(x, z1, z2...zL) = pθ(x | z1)pθ(zL)ΠL−1
l=1 pθ(zl | zl+1)

As shown by [22], such an approach limits the utilization of higher latent variables as
both bottom­up and top­down paths go through each latent layer one by one. To improve
the information flow they introduce a deterministic bottom­up path, through the addition

we can see that now the information in the encoder can flow ”freely” from the data to each

each deterministic node, which should allow the higher latent variables to encode more
abstract information.
While [22] show that LVAE improves utilization of higher latent variables and can suc­
cessfully scale to 5 latent layers results of [18] suggest that this alone is not sufficient to
increase the depth even further. To aid this they propose two further improvements: skip­
connections in the decoder and a bidirectional inference that mixes elements of bottom­up
and top­down inference. This allows them to scale the model, which they call BIVA, to
20 latent layers and significantly improve the ELBO as well as the fidelity of generated
images compared to the previous latent­variable models.

1.2
Related work
1.2.1
Feature hierarchy
[23] have shown that deep neural networks (particularly convolutional networks for com­
puter vision) learn different levels of abstraction along the model depth forming a feature
hierarchy. Furthermore, their experiments imply that depth is vital for performance.
The importance of depth seems to be also the case for deep hierarchical VAEs. [24], how­
ever, question the ability of a simple top­down VAE (they used LVAE in the experiments)
to produce a meaningful hierarchy of features. By sampling from different levels of hier­
archy and comparing the results qualitatively they show that one of the layers captures
most of the variance, while sampling at other layers has little influence on the generated
images. They propose an alternative architecture with a flat latent distribution, where the
latent layers are independent of each other, which in terms of the probabilistic graphical
model corresponds to a simple one layer VAE. A hierarchy of features is imposed through
deterministic nodes in both the bottom­up inference path and the top­down generative
path. This way the latent z is split into parts that are inferred using an increasing num­
ber of hidden layers stacked on top of each other as in the case of the LVAE encoder,
but without the top­down stochastic path. They show that such an approach leads to the
model learning distinct and disentangled features at each level.
While the deterministic bottom­up and top­down paths bear some similarity to the skip­
connections used in VDVAE, the ”flat” latent structure is not what the state­of­the­art VAE
models use, which motivates the question of whether recent deep hierarchical VAEs learn
a meaningful feature hierarchy. While [18, appendix] and [16] do include some qualitative
analysis of generated images that aims at showcasing feature hierarchy, it is in both cases
very limited, particularly in terms of providing any intuition about the role of the encoder
and decoder variance.
Analyses of VDVAE [16] and VQ­VAE­2 [12] (recent state­of­the­art VAEs) both suggest
that the top latents encode more general information about the image, while bottom la­
tents contribute to generating finer details, textures in high­resolution images. Given that
the ”hourglass” architecture is very common in hierarchical VAEs (resolution of latent vari­
ables decreases from the bottom to the top) this can be related to the resolution of the

encoded representations to some extent. Similar relation of bottom stochastic layers with
fine detail and texture has been found in other generative models as well [25].
How deep hierarchical VEAs learn and how they utilize the parameters of the latent distri­

tion remains an interesting question. Providing a better understanding of the information
flow in hierarchical VAEs might lead to further improvements in those models and better
awareness of their limitations.
1.2.2
Relation to other generative models
[16] provides an interesting discussion about the relation of very deep hierarchical VAEs
to autoregressive models. The author proves that a VAE with as many latent layers as
the number of elements in the output would be able to operate like an autoregressive
model and thus in theory generalize it. He attributes the fact that autoregressive models
historically outperformed VAEs to their deeper statistical dependence, which could explain
why scaling the stochastic depth seems crucial for the performance of VAEs.
1.2.3
Controlled generation
[26] have shown that specific attributes of generated images can be changed through
manipulation of the latent variables, which involves extrapolating along precomputed at­
tribute vectors based on averaged representations of samples with the given attribute.
This suggests some regularity in the latent space w.r.t. the semantic information.
Semi­supervised approaches proposed in [17] and [27] introduce a class dependency in
the VAE structure which in turn allows for controlling the class and ”style” of the generated
images separately. The qualitative analysis of the images generated by both models
suggests that a certain level of disentanglement of the information encoded is possible,
as they successfully produce images of different digits written in similar style through
sharing the same latent representation.
Methods of controlled generation have been proposed for other generative models, some
of which yield very promising results [9][28].
Controlling the class or particular features during generation is an interesting task on its
own, but could also be used as a tool for analyzing the latent space of a specific generative
model.
1.2.4
The problem of out of domain data
One of the applications of generative models is anomaly detection. It is based on an
assumption that a density estimator (e.g. an explicit likelihood generative model) should
predict low density for samples away from the distribution of the training data. [29] show
that this assumption does not actually hold in practice for most of the popular generative
model types.
Since this problem was raised, several explanations and solutions have been proposed.
[29] and [18] hypothesise that this behavior is caused by low­level statistics influencing
the likelihood more than the high­level semantics, however, they provide limited support
to this claim.
To solve this issue, [18] propose an alternative formulation of ELBO in which instead of
using the approximate posterior qϕ to sample all of the latent variables, the approximate
posterior qϕ is used only for the top L −k layers and the remaining k layers are sampled
from the generative distribution pθ. The resulting alternative lower bound L>k is the fol­
lowing (note that what the distributions are conditioned on might depend on the specific

higher latents capture higher­level semantics that do not generalize between distributions.

addition to improved results using this method (again with few configurations) they also
provide qualitative analysis suggesting that reconstructing MNIST images with a model
trained on FashionMNIST is possible using lower latents, but not higher latents, which
would support the feature hierarchy argument.
[31] show a relation between input complexity (measured as the size of the image after
compression) and model likelihood in generative models. This supports the hypothesis
about low­level statistics influencing the OODD performance. The authors propose a
method in which they subtract the complexity estimation from the negative log­likelihood
to obtain an OOD score (both expressed in bits per dimension), which also can be seen
as a likelihood ratio approach. Their experiments however only include flow­based and
autoregressive models and it remains unknown if this behavior holds for VAEs.
Correctly identifying OOD data is an important goal in the development of robust and
reliable predictive systems. Analyzing a model’s ability to do so can be a useful tool in
learning about the model itself. This could be especially relevant for Bayesian methods,
that in principle should incorporate a notion of uncertainty (e.g. through the variance of
the predictive distribution).
1.2.5
Concerns about variance modelling
Recently doubts have been raised regarding the ability to appropriately model variance
in VAEs [32][33][34]. One of the problems raised by [34] is that neural networks tend to
extrapolate badly [35] and therefore we cannot rely on them to model variance correctly
outside the support of the training data. In [32] the authors argue for a variance function
that will yield high values outside the data support, however, it might be difficult to achieve
for high dimensional hierarchical latent distributions in modern VAEs.
Another issue is that to minimize reconstruction loss it is more beneficial for the VAE to
push the variances towards 0 [33]. This is regularized by the KL­divergence, which given
different decoder and encoder means will encourage the variances to be higher. For
hierarchical VAEs, in which during training it is the encoder representation that is used for
reconstructing the image, it could be argued that the incentive to shrink the variance is

1.3
Aims and contributions
A better understanding of how VAEs encode information could be very useful in the fur­
ther development of those models. The hypothesis about a feature hierarchy learned
along the latent structure of hierarchical VAEs is appealing, but has been challenged in
the past [24] and to the best of my knowledge, has not been studied in detail w.r.t. recent
high­performing VAEs. The role of the variance of the latent distributions has not been
sufficiently investigated and its robustness is debated [32][33][34]. The behavior of gen­
erative models under domain­shift [29] has also raised concerns. Further study is needed
to reevaluate VAEs’ ability to model uncertainty and to give empirical support to some of
the related speculations [18].
1.3.1
Aims
In this project, I aim to deepen the intuition of how hierarchical VAEs process information,
by addressing the following questions:
1. Do hierarchical VAEs encode different visual features in different latent layers?
• If so, do the encoded features form a feature hierarchy that is consistent with
the latent hierarchy?
2. Does the variance of the latent distributions encode a notion of uncertainty?
• Does input complexity influence the variance significantly?
• If so, does the variance encode any notion of uncertainty besides what can be
explained by complexity?
1.3.2
Contributions
The main contributions of this work are the following:
1.1 I show that the representations at different levels of the latent hierarchy correspond
to different visual features in the images.
1.2 I find that sampling at higher layers generates more diverse samples than when
sampling at lower layers. This is an indication that higher layers encapsulate more
global features.
1.3 I show that both mean and variance of latent distributions can be used to classify
facial attributes with varying accuracy depending on the latent layer, which suggests
selectivity of encoded information.
2.
I show that based just on decoder variance we are able to detect OOD data with
competitive performance if corrected by complexity. This indicates that decoder
variance does model uncertainty to some extent.
3.1 I show that both ELBO and decoder variance correlate with input complexity in VAEs.
3.2 I propose a complexity correction method that improves OODD performance when
using ELBO or decoder variance.

Models
In the experiments I use two model architectures: a simple bottom­up VAE with skip­

2.2.1
HVAE
I use the model from [15] and also refer to it as HVAE. It is a bottom­up VAE with L
latent layers with skip­connections in both inference path and generative path as shown
in Figure 2.1. In addition to the skip­connections, HVAE uses free bits scheme [47], which
constraints the influence of KL divergence term in ELBO for a number of iterations such
that the model is less regularized at the beginning of the training. Weight normalization
[48] is also applied.
The model itself is based on residual blocks. In the bottom­up path before each latent layer
there is a deterministic transformation composed of three residual blocks of the following
structure:
y = Conv (Act (Convs(Act(x)))) + x
with Act being the activation (here ReLU [49]) and Conv denotes a same­padded convo­
lution. Striding is only performed in the last of the three blocks and only for some of the
deterministic nodes (depending on the input resolution). This is followed by a stochas­
tic node which is a diagonal Gaussian distribution parametrized either by a convolutional

deterministic skip­connections. Source: [15]

Softplus activation for the standard deviation σ. Each deterministic block is fed the latent
representation sampled from the previous stochastic node and the output of the previous
deterministic block forming skip­connections.
The top­down path is very similar, it uses residual blocks of the same structure, but now
the stochastic nodes come before the deterministic nodes. The resolution change in the

2.2.2
VDVAE
VDVAE [16] has a much more complex architecture shown in Figure 2.2. Deterministic
bottom­up path is based on several residual blocks [51] built of convolutions with GELU
[52] activations. Resolution changes in the pool and unpool layers are done through aver­
age pooling and nearest­neighbours upsampling respectively. Tensors from the bottom­
up and top­down paths are concatenated and fed into additional convolutional layers to
obtain the approximate posterior distribution qϕ. To obtain the generative distribution pθ
only top­down information is used (similarly with additional convolutions, which in this
case are fed back to the top­down flow). Information from sampled latent z is added to
the top­down flow as well as info.
The architecture involves multiple residual connections as it not only uses res­blocks but
also the top­down segments resemble residual blocks, which should allow for a more
efficient information and gradient flow and therefore help scaling the network in depth.
The architecture corresponds to the following factorization (somewhat similar to HVAE,
but the encoder is now top­down):
pθ(x, z) = p(θx, z1, z2...zL) = pθ(x | z1,...,L)pθ(zL)ΠL−1
l=1 pθ(zl | z>l)
qϕ(z | x) = ΠL
l=1qϕ(zl | z>l, x)
(2.2)
Similarly to HVAE all latent variables are modelled as diagonal Gaussian distributions
(both p and q).

3.1
Hierarchical VAEs learn feature hierarchy on different
latent levels
I apply several sampling procedures to qualitatively assess whether deep hierarchical
VAEs encode different visual features at different latent layers and learn meaningful fea­
ture hierarchy. For that, I use VDVAE pre­trained on FFHQ­256 because human faces
are relatively regular and have a wide variety of distinctive features.
3.1.1
Forced attribute generation
In the first experiment, I leverage CelebA attributes to detect what kind of visual features
can be changed through each layer by trying to force specific attributes. This ”forceful”
approach should have high sensitivity to finding possible changes, as it is not limited by
model variance and the magnitude of changes can be sufficiently increased.
Similarly to [26] I try to obtain directions in the latent space that would correspond to spe­
cific visual attributes with simple arithmetic using binary attribute labels from CelebA­HQ.
Next, I qualitatively analyze how much a specific feature can be manipulated through a
simple linear extrapolation on different levels of hierarchy, by visually inspecting gener­
ated images. While prone to be influenced by correlations in the data this approach should
show what kinds of changes can be enforced at different levels of latent hierarchy.
Method
I use VDVAE trained on FFHQ­256 to obtain representations zl (l is the latent layer index)
of CelebA­HQ (using the encoder qϕ to sample those). For each CelebA attribute and
each latent variable zl I calculate the mean of the representations with and without the
attribute and then use the difference between the means as the “attribute direction” in the
latent space dl at the layer l. For some features sex correlates very strongly, therefore in
most cases I calculate a direction per sex and then average them or just use one of the
sexes if the feature is not available for the other (e.g. Heavy_Makeup will only be present with
sufficient frequency in female images), those I will refer to as grouped in the figures.

Discussion
The results suggest that higher latent levels correspond to more global changes across
multiple characteristics such as sex, race and age, while lower latents only encode more
specific features and localized attributes such as eye makeup and do not significantly af­
fect the general characteristic. The experiment shows that once some specific features
are determined at high latent levels, they cannot be changed by lower latents to a mean­
ingful degree.
Besides the number of layers connecting the input to a given latent (which increases as
we go higher in the hierarchy), the resolution of the representations at a given level might
also contribute to how the model encodes features. It seems reasonable that general
image characteristics and global features tend to be encoded in higher latents, which have
lower resolution and correspond to more depth. On the other hand, local features require
higher resolution but are often less abstract. To test this hypothesis, further experiments,
possibly involving modifications to the model architecture (e.g. changing the resolutions),
would be helpful.
Failure to generate specific attributes at some of the latent layers and the differences
in the results for layers that are relatively close to each other, implies some degree of
selectivity of encoded information. One could speculate that this might be related to the
skip­connections allowing the model to encode information selectively. Without the skip­
connections, the signal from the top latents would need to be passed through all layers
not to be lost. While this would make sense in terms of the efficiency of representations
it remains a hypothesis and could be an interesting direction for further research.
The ”soapy”, blurry effect when propagating the mode of the decoder distribution or sam­
pling with low temperature is typical for VAEs [16][18]. I do not pay particular attention to
this in the analysis, but the lack of texture observed when the decoder mean is used for
the lower latents could be another indication that features of this kind (fine­detail, texture)
are encoded lower in the hierarchy.

Sampling at a single level at a time
While the previous method gives some intuition about the link of the encoded informa­
tion to different latent levels, it entirely ignores the variance of the latent distributions. In
the next experiment, I test whether similar kinds of changes from layer to layer can be
observed while sampling from the model without forcing any specific direction. I use a
similar approach to the one used in [24], where the authors generate images by sampling
at a single layer at a time to assess how each of the layers contributes to the variability in
the output and whether their model learns a meaningful feature hierarchy.
Method
I use representations obtained from FFHQ­256. Similarly to the previous experiment for
each layer L I use fixed encoded representation for layers l > L (zl ∼qϕ), next I sample
the L’th layer from the decoder (zL ∼pθ) and for the remaining layers l < L I simply
use the mean of the decoder distribution µp,l instead of sampling. This way I obtain rows
of images for which only one layer was sampled (except of pre­computing the encoder
representations, which is done only once) and all the variation along those rows is caused
by the variance of this specific layer.
Results
The generated images are shown in Figure 3.2. It seems that layers between 62 and 64
(layer 64 in particular) account for the most significant changes in the images. The images
sampled at layer 64 have very little in common, suggesting that the top 2 latents contribute
very little to the final image (those are very small vectors after all). The layers 63 and 62
vary much less, but still introduce important changes (e.g. sex and hair). Further down
the latent hierarchy between 56­61 the variation is more subtle and local as features such
as glasses (presence, shape and color), mouth and hair detail change. For layers 39 and
below the images might seem identical at first glance, but on a closer inspection (espe­
cially skin, hair, reflections on the glasses) it is clear that the level of detail is increasing
and the images get less blurry as I include representations of the original image instead
of propagating the mode.
Most of those observations are consistent with the forced generation experiments, al­
though the previous experiment shows more drastic changes within each row, which is
somewhat expectable as we can assume that the magnitude of the changes in the la­

model variance. This difference however seems the most apparent in the lower latents
(e.g. 26th), where the variation between the sampled images is very small, barely no­

decoder variance of some of the lower latents is relatively smaller and does not contribute
as much to the variability of the generated images.

3.2
Is variance a good indicator of OOD data?
Qualitative analysis in Section 3.1 gives intuition about the relation of the latent space to
visual features. This, however, tells more about the meaning of the modes of the latent
distributions, while not necessarily explaining the role of the variances, which has been
debated (see Section 1.2.5).
Given both the generative distribution and the approximate posterior modelled with Gaus­
sian distributions, the variance could be interpreted as uncertainty about the value of the
latent variable. Therefore, in principle, it seems reasonable to expect the variance to
relate to model uncertainty about the input data.
A specific case under which one would expect a model to express uncertainty (although
deep learning models tend to fail to do so, which I discuss in Sections 1.2.4 and 1.2.5) is
when it is given a data point that falls out of the domain it was trained on. In the following
section, I use OODD experiments as a proxy to assess whether the variance of the latent
distributions incorporates a notion of uncertainty.
Method
I train HVAE and VDVAE on natural datasets (SVHN, CIFAR10 and FFHQ­32) and only
HVAE on grey­scale datasets (see Section 2.1), the latter in both binarized and contin­
uous versions. Next for each model (trained on different in­domain data), for each data

3.3
Input complexity influences ELBO and variance in VAEs
and can be leveraged to improve OODD
The problem of OOD data discussed in Section 1.2.4 appears to also affect the variance,
therefore in this section, I attempt to investigate this issue in further detail. My goal is
to test whether the relation of input complexity to likelihood shown for flow­based and
autoregressive models [31] holds for VAEs’ ELBO and if there is a similar link for the
decoder variance. If so, then I aim to check if this relationship can be accounted for in
OODD tasks to further study the decoder variance and uncertainty.
3.3.1
Image complexity correlates to ELBO and decoder variance
First, I analyze the complexity distributions of the used datasets and the correlation of
complexity with ELBO and variance.
Method
I calculate the complexity for each sample as the size of a JPEG compressed image.
Next, I use the VDVAE and HVAE models trained on different datasets to collect relevant
per­sample statistics.
Results
Figure 3.6 shows that the distributions of complexities differ between the datasets. Nat­
ural datasets (Figure 3.6b) have quite regular unimodal distributions with SVHN being
the easiest to compress and FFHQ32 the most complex. Binarized grey­scale datasets
(Figure 3.6a) have less regular complexities, but they still have significant differences
between simpler datasets like MNIST or Omniglot and more complex such as FashionM­
NIST. Some distributions appear multimodal e.g. FashionMNIST with a spike in density
around medium complexity and an even more irregular spike in notMNIST at very low
complexity.

decoder and encoder variances at layer 0 (p_var_0, q_var_0, see eq. (3.1)) as scatter­plots

Discussion
It is possible that it is the spread (not the mean) of the distribution of the training data that
reduces the correlation to complexity for FashionMNIST, which would also explain obser­
vations for CIFAR10 better. Given the small scale of the experiment, it is also possible
that the results are not significant. Regardless of the significance and the main factor,
the magnitude of the change was very small, therefore I would not expect this kind of
intervention to influence the ELBO­complexity relation strongly enough.
The experiment only included a simple HVAE which might have underfitted the data (at
least for CIFAR10), so it remains an open question whether this behaviour would be re­
produced with a more powerful model. I have considered some other functions for the
importance weight and have chosen the exponential function as it empirically yielded the
best results, but I have not explored this space exhaustively. It is possible that with a
larger model or a better­fitted weight function the results would be more (or less) signif­
icant, however, the relatively weak outcomes and limited importance to the main project
goals did not motivate further investment in this experiment. The approach could still be
interesting for a study focused primarily on the complexity relation.

3.3.3
Complexity correction improves OOD detection
To leverage the observed correlation to improve OODD results I propose a method of
complexity correction based on a linear trend estimated within the in­domain data that
can be applied to both ELBO and decoder variance.
Method
I use the experimental setup of OODD experiments from Section 3.2 to which I introduce
additional OOD scores. For reference, I use LLR>2 (see Section 1.2.4) which has been
shown to improve the results on HVAE [15], and complexity – to see how much of the
results obtained with ELBO and the decoder variance could be explained by the effect of
complexity alone.
I also use ELBO and the decoder variance scores modified to account for complexity. For
a given OOD score S I calculate a corrected version S(∗):
S(∗) = S −a · c
(3.4)
where c is the input complexity and a is the slope of a linear model fitted to predict the
score S given the complexity c on the in­domain data. To fit the slope I use the training
set, but in case of overfitting a separate data split could be a good approach (I did not
observe this to make a significant difference in my experiments). OOD data is not used
to fit a, so the method is still unsupervised.
This approach is similar to the one used by [31], but uses an additional slope parameter
making it a bit more flexible and allowing to use on other scores (e.g. based on variance)
or possibly with different complexity estimation approaches.
Results
The results of the OODD experiments are shown in Table 3.1 and the relevant pairwise
comparisons are visualized in Figure 3.9 for HVAE and VDVAE.
We can observe a clear pattern between the complexity and ELBO (Figures 3.9b and
3.9e), where while ELBO performs almost strictly better than the complexity, the results
for both scores correlate strongly and very often fail in the same configuration, which is

After correcting for complexity (Figures 3.9c and 3.9f) this relation is no longer so clear
for ELBO (now ELBO(∗)). As expected, the performance for the dataset configurations

Discussion
The proposed method of linear correction for image complexity improves the performance
in OODD tasks for both ELBO and decoder variance for both HVAE and VDVAE. In the
experiments, corrected ELBO yields results competitive to LLR.
The method was invented based on the analysis in Section 3.3.1, which means it is not en­
tirely uninformed of the model behaviour on OOD data, which could be considered a data
leak. However, this problem can very likely be present in most of the research within the
field, especially since the main discussion of generative models in application to OODD is
focused on the image domain with a very limited number of dataset configurations tested.
This might make the evaluation very susceptible to some kind of overfitting. In fact, signif­
icant number of state­of­the­art methods [54][55][15] evaluate mainly between CIFAR10
(in­domain) and SVHN (out­of­domain), and between FashinMNIST (in) and MNIST (out),
and only in one direction. In my experiments, the methods that perform better CIFAR10
(in) to SVHN (out) tend to have worse performance SVHN (in) to CIFAR10 (out). [56, Ap­
pendix] point out a similar problem with some of the recently proposed methods of OODD.
This makes a reliable comparison to state­of­the­art methods difficult.
3.4
Does decoder variance express uncertainty?
Having alleviated some of the issues connected to different input complexities among the
datasets, I continue to analyze the variance of the latent distributions and its relation to
uncertainty.
3.4.1
Back to out­of­domain detection
I follow up on the OODD experiments from Section 3.2 using the findings about the influ­
ence of input complexity from Section 3.3.3.
Method
I apply the complexity correction (Section 3.3.3) to the decoder and encoder variance, an­
alyze the resulting OODD performance for HVAE and VDVAE and compare it to corrected
ELBO and LLR.
Results
The results of the OODD experiments are shown in Table 3.1 and the pairwise compar­
isons relevant for this section are visualized in Figure 3.10.
For HVAE the corrected decoder variance achieves AUROC> 0.5 for almost all of the con­
figurations (except a few grey­scale) and performed comparably with corrected ELBO and
LLR (maybe even slightly better on natural datasets). For VDVAE the corrected decoder
variance and corrected ELBO yielded very similar results.
The encoder variance after correction performs comparably to the decoder variance on
natural datasets. However, for grey­scale it still fails to detect OOD samples in most
configurations.
Discussion
The average decoder variance shows competitive performance on OODD tasks, when
accounted for complexity, which indicates that it encodes a notion of uncertainty, that is
exhibited through a higher variance given a data point from a different domain and involves
more than just modelling the complexity of the input image. This could also be the case for
the encoder variance, however here the results are not as consistent, making it unclear
whether such conclusion can be drawn for the encoder variance as well.

3.4.2
Face attributes – analysis
To analyze how the variance behaves on unusual data points, but still within the domain,
I use some of the less frequent CelebA­HQ attributes. I expect that the variance will be
significantly different (higher) on the images with the rare attributes, as they could be (to
some extent) considered outliers.
Method
I analyze the per­layer encoder and decoder variances averaged over the data points with
a specific attribute and compare them against the data points without it. I use relative val­
ues normalized by the variance of the specific layer averaged over the whole CelebA­HQ
dataset, such that values close to 1 can be considered unaffected by the attribute. I use
bootstrapping to assess a range of values that can be expected in a random subsample
of similar size.
Results
The variance statistics are plotted in Figure 3.11. The variances within the rare attribute
groups on average deviate from the baseline more than would be expected by random
(at least for the decoder). The decoder variance varies relatively more strongly, more
regularly and more significantly than the encoder variance. The encoder variance seems
much noisier, which is consistent with the observations made in other experiments. The
average variances deviate both ways, higher and lower than the baseline, depending
on the attribute and the latent layer, contrary to the expectation that it would tend to be
higher. However, for the decoder, there is some regularity within the feature groups, at
least locally, as the variance around similar latent levels is consistently higher or lower
for a given attribute. For example, for Wearing_Hat it remains relatively high for almost all
layers it deviates at, while for Bald it is relatively low almost everywhere.
Interestingly, both for encoder and decoder, the variances deviate the most for higher
latents (above the 50th layer) and to a lesser extent for layers below the 20th, while the
20­50 region shows less to none relative variability.
Discussion
The variance, particularly the decoder variance, tends to deviate from the baseline for the
samples with rare attributes. It remains unclear whether the observed deviation is related
to uncertainty, particularly since some attributes tend to increase the average variance,
while other decrease it.
One could speculate that specific features will increase the decoder variance if they intro­
duce more sources of variability (e.g. adding a hat) or decrease the variance when they
simplify the image (e.g. baldness removes variability caused by hair). Following this hy­
pothesis, the differences in magnitude change between the layers within a feature group
(increased variance for one layer and decreased on another) could be explained by doing
both at the same time for different features and therefore affecting the latent hierarchy in
different ways.
A different hypothesis, not necessarily exclusive, would be that since those attributes
are less frequent, the model did not learn them as well and must increase the variance
in order to decrease the KL divergence between the encoder and decoder distributions.
Both hypotheses are speculative and should be treated with a grain of salt, but could be
an interesting direction for further experiments (however, they might be difficult to test).

Discussion and conclusions
The qualitative analysis of the images generated by latent extrapolation along ”attribute
dimensions” implies that hierarchical VAEs encode different features at different latent
layers. Sampling experiments further support this claim, as the visual features changed
through sampling or extrapolation depend on which latent layer is being manipulated.
While some entanglement of the features across latent layers can be observed, high­
level latent layers affect general features and low­level layers appear to correspond to
finer detail and local features. This supports the hypothesis that hierarchical VAEs learn
feature hierarchies across the latent structure.
Furthermore, I observe interactions between the features while sampling at two latent
layers, which can be a sign of the model learning covariance in the data through the de­
pendency between the latent layers. However, this remains a hypothesis, as alternative
explanations are also plausible. For example, one could speculate that the deterministic
path of the decoder might be responsible for composing a consistent image, resulting in
interactions between the features without the need for a hierarchical generative distribu­
tion.
The amount of variability in the visual features caused by changes in the latent representa­
tions differs from layer to layer and some latent levels appear to be more impactful on the
generated images. Trying to classify face attributes based on encoder representations,
yields very different results depending on which latent layer is used, with a pattern that is
somewhat consistent with the results obtained when trying to forcefully generate the same
attribute. These observations imply selectivity in where specific information is encoded
in the latent hierarchy. I hypothesise that this might be related to the skip connections
in both bottom­up and top­down paths allowing the information to flow more freely be­
tween higher latent layers and the input, without requiring the lower latents to redundantly
encode the same information.
I observe that, conditioned on the attributes of the image, the variances at specific latent
layers consistently deviate in magnitude more, compared to the variances at other levels
that on average barely change. This, combined with the previous observations suggesting
selectivity in encoding, leads me to speculate that maybe some of the latent variables are
not used to a significant extent and could be replaced with deterministic nodes. This
would, however, be in contrast to the findings about the stochastic depth being crucial
for the performance of hierarchical VAEs [16] and at the moment I do not have sufficient
evidence to support this hypothesis. Anyway, I believe that further study in this direction
could yield interesting results. A possible idea for investigating this would be to perform
ablation experiments where specific stochastic nodes are turned into deterministic ones,
and analyze how the performance changes.
The hypothesis that low­level image statistics are influencing OODD performance in spe­
cific dataset configurations [29][18] is supported by my analysis, in which I observe that
ELBO correlates with image complexity, measured as the size after compression. My at­
tempts to weaken this correlation by oversampling high­complexity images during training
do not yield satisfactory results. This implies that VAEs (and likely other generative models
[31]), as density estimators, are biased towards predicting high densities for data points
with low complexity, which might not necessarily be a desired property. This could be
Analyzing information flow in hierarchical Variational Autoencoders
45
==PAGE:54==
particularly relevant for applications such as anomaly detection or active learning, which
motivates further research and urges caution.
The correlation of ELBO to complexity could explain why dataset configurations in which
the OOD dataset is ”simpler” than the training dataset are particularly problematic in
OODD experiments. Based on those observations, I propose a method of correcting
for input complexity based on a linear trend that improves OODD performance when us­
ing ELBO and yields results that are competitive with the LLR approach [15]. The method
generalizes between the two VAE architectures I tried and can be applied to different OOD
scores (e.g. the average decoder variance), not only ELBO.
I observe the decoder variance to also correlate to input complexity. More complex images
result in higher total variance of the decoder distributions. This makes sense intuitively
as a more complex image should be more difficult to reconstruct and one could speculate
that it might cause the decoder to be more uncertain. After correcting for complexity, de­
coder variance can be used to detect OOD samples with performance that is comparable
with corrected ELBO and LLR. This implies that decoder variance captures some notion
of uncertainty, which goes beyond the low­level statistics that are used by image com­
pression algorithms. Furthermore, the magnitude of the decoder variance correlates with
some visual features and can be used to detect some of the less frequent facial attributes.
Here the findings are not as consistent, but might still suggest that the decoder variance
encodes uncertainty related to these attributes.
Some other, more general attributes can be classified with a simple KNN model based
just on the decoder variance, meaning that said variance must be reacting to the visual
attributes in a sufficiently regular way. The implications of this are also not clear, but one
could speculate that the decoder expresses uncertainty related to different features that
depend on the more abstract attribute, which allows to classify it.
I do note that some of those interpretations are speculative. However, taking into ac­
count competitive performance in detecting OOD data as well as experiments with facial
attributes within the domain (some of which could be seen as outliers), the results, collec­
tively, support the hypothesis that the decoder variance encodes a notion of uncertainty.
The discussion about VAEs’ ability to model uncertainty is highly relevant w.r.t. applica­
tions such as anomaly detection. While these findings are promising, I do believe, based
on the complexity analysis, that more research is needed to better understand what fac­
tors influence the behavior of VAEs, which is crucial in terms of reliability.
In my analysis, I focused much less on the encoder variance because it did not behave
as regularly and did not yield as good results as the decoder variance. I speculate that
this might be related to the encoder distribution being used to sample model representa­
tions, that are in turn used to reconstruct the image during training. While KL­divergence
will encourage the (encoder and decoder) variance to be higher in certain situations, the
reconstruction error might promote lower encoder variance. What is more, the encoder
variance depends on both the deterministic bottom­up and stochastic top­down paths,
while the decoder variance only on the top­down path. More research is needed to inves­
tigate the role of encoder variance, which is essential in understanding VAEs, but, to the
best of my knowledge, it has not been studied in sufficient detail.
