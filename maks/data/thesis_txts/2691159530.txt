Abstract 
This project aims to investigate and optimise a proposed new water recovery system 
for use in the Gateway, a lunar space station currently under development. The pro-
posed water recovery system consists of two membrane processes:  Forward osmo-
sis to extract water from the urine/condensate wastewater and membrane distilla-
tion to recover the extracted water as product water. 
 
As the most abundant compound in urine is urea which hydrolyses to ammo-
nium, the transfer mechanism of urea and ammonium through the two membranes 
was investigated. Acidifying the wastewater and the draw solution resulted in 
higher rejections for both compounds, but a closer investigation is needed to learn 
more about the exact transport mechanism. 
 
Full system operations revealed that it is possible to reach the required 90% wa-
ter recovery, but further optimisation of the draw is needed to ensure this perfor-
mance during long-term operation. The requirements of a 4 L/d water production 
and 90 days lifetime of consumables could not be fulfilled with the current system, 
as a five-day operation produced an average of 2.11 L/d and had a lifetime of 8 h 
before the ammonium concentration exceeded the allowed limit in the product wa-
ter. Suggested improvements of the system include a higher operating temperature 
of the membrane distillation loop which is believed to increase the water production 
to 4 L/d and an added zeolite ion exchange column to remove ammonium from the 
product water which is estimated to increase the lifetime to 47.6 days. In addition, 
the zeolite column shows potential as remineralisation process of the product water, 
but further investigations must be performed. 
 
Finally, a proof-of-concept experiment using a thermo-responsive draw as an 
alternative to the membrane distillation was conducted. A suggested process flow 
diagram for the forward osmosis system using thermo-responsive draw was com-
pared with a suggested process flow diagram for the system using membrane dis-
tillation. The compared parameters were weight, volume, and energy consumption, 
revealing the latter system being more beneficial and therefore being the suggested 
system for further investigations.

Chapter 1  
Introduction 
Humans are going to the Moon - again. And then to Mars. 
In 2017 the Artemis programme was launched with the intention of sending 
people to the Moon to explore and gain valuable knowledge to use when they take 
the next giant leap in Space history: Sending humans to Mars [1]. 
An important part of the Artemis programme is the lunar orbiting outpost, the 
Gateway, which will function as a temporary residence for astronauts on their route 
either to the lunar surface or destinations beyond the Moon [2]. As sending load into 
space is expensive (>10 000 USD/kg load) and time-consuming (travel time extend-
ing to several days) [3], The Gateway needs to provide reliable life support systems 
to make it as independent as possible of resupplies from Earth [2][4]. One of the 
important life support systems is a water recovery system (WRS) that can recover 
wastewater for reuse as drinking water. Such a system is currently in use at the In-
ternational Space Station (ISS) which recovers 85% of the produced wastewater con-
sisting of urine, condensate, and hygiene water [3][5]. 
This WRS consists of two units: 1) The Urine Processor Assembly (UPA) that 
treats the collected urine via two distillation assemblies and a gas separation unit, 
and 2) The Water Processor Assembly (WPA) that treats the distillate from the UPA 
and the crew latent and hygiene water via 7-8 steps including several filtration col-
umns and an ion exchange bed. All these steps make the WRS heavy, space-con-
suming, and prone to breakdowns, not to mention the requirement of occasional 
resupply of filtration and ion exchange medium. On average, since the implemen-
tation of the WRS in 2008, 0.21 kg disposable hardware was consumed per litre pro-
duced water [3][5]. For the Gateway which is a much smaller space orbiter located 
deeper into space where resupply is even more expensive and takes even longer, a 
new, smaller, simpler, and more reliable WRS is needed. As the function of the Gate-
way is to host astronauts only for a few days before being left empty for a longer 
period, the new WRS must also be easy to start up, shut down, and clean. Addition-
ally, it must meet the requirements of being able to treat 4-6 litres of wastewater a 
day, reach a recovery of 90%, and have a lifetime of 90 days of consumables. 
The Aquaporin Space Alliance - a joint venture between Aquaporin A/S and 
Danish Aerospace Company [6] - is working with ESA on developing this new WRS.

The suggested WRS consists of two membrane units: A Forward Osmosis (FO) mod-
ule to extract pure water and a Membrane Distillation (MD) module to recover the 
pure water as drinking water [7]. The project has been going on for more than two 
years and is currently at a point where the FO unit has been investigated as a urine 
concentrating unit. Results show that the nitrogen concentration in the final drink-
ing water is too high after only 2-3 days of operation. This is based on MD rejection 
assumptions as the MD unit has not yet been investigated [8]. 
Thus, this project takes a closer look at the mechanism of how nitrogen passes 
both the FO and the MD membrane and how too high product water concentrations 
can be mitigated by acidification and ion exchange. The MD unit was tested to see 
if it can fulfil the water production requirement of at least 4 litres per day. Addition-
ally, as an alternative to the MD unit, a thermo-responsive draw solution was inves-
tigated as this configuration also uses heat to recover the draw but eliminates the 
dependency on the MD membrane performance. Here, a few proof-of-concept ex-
periments were performed.

2.1 Osmotic processes 
Osmosis is the transport of water through a semipermeable membrane from a re-
gion with higher water chemical potential to a region with lower water chemical 
potential. The semipermeability of the membrane ensures that water can pass freely 
while solutes are being rejected. The water transport happens spontaneously to even 
out the difference in solute concentration thus the chemical potential and thereby 
reach equilibrium [9][10][11]. Osmotic pressure, π, is defined as the applied pres-
sure needed to prevent the diffusion of water through the membrane and is given 
by Van’t Hoff’s equation for an ideal solution (eq. (1)). Here i is the number of indi-
vidual particles of a dissolved compound, C is the molar concentration, R is the gas 
constant, and T is the temperature [10]. 
 
𝜋= 𝑖𝐶𝑅𝑇 
(1) 
There are three common osmotic processes: Forward Osmosis (FO), Pressure Re-
tarded Osmosis (PRO), and Reverse Osmosis (RO) (Figure 1).

osmosis with ΔP < Δπ, and reverse osmosis with ΔP > Δπ [9].

FO has no applied pressure (ΔP=0) and uses the difference in osmotic pressure be-
tween the feed and the draw as driving force. This leads to water permeation from 
feed to draw, concentrating the feed and diluting the draw. FO is therefore used in 
applications where it is wished to concentrate a feed such as food and pharmaceu-
tical processing, dilute industrial wastewater, or landfill leachate. In PRO the draw 
is pressurised with a pressure lower than the osmotic pressure difference (ΔP < Δπ). 
The water is then transported from the feed to the pressurised draw and the process 
can be used to produce electricity. In RO, the applied pressure exceeds the osmotic 
pressure difference (ΔP > Δπ), giving a water flux from the draw side to the feed. 
This is mainly used for desalination purposes.  
RO is the most investigated of the three processes and is currently being used 
in desalination plants all over the world. FO is not yet as established in the market 
but has received increased attention in the past decades due to advantages related 
to its pressureless operation, cost savings, ease of operation, less fouling issues, and 
higher rejection of many solutes [9][10]. In addition, operation safety increases, 
which also is an important factor for the new WRS. 
 
2.2 Forward osmosis 
2.2.1 Mass transfer mechanism 
FO membranes are commonly thin-film composite membranes consisting of two 
layers: A dense active layer that functions as the selective layer and a porous support 
layer that provides mechanical strength. The dense active layer does not contain 
pores where molecules travel through. Instead, the molecules permeate the mem-
brane via diffusion through free volume elements between polymers caused by 
thermal motion of their chains [12]. Mass transfer across a FO membrane is therefore 
commonly explained by the solution-diffusion model, where the difference in os-
motic pressure, Δπ, is the driving force for the water flux, Jw, (eq. (2)) and the differ-
ence in concentration, ΔC, is the driving force for the solute flux, Js, (eq. (3)). Aw and 
Bs are the membrane permeability coefficients of water and solute, respectively 
[12][13].

2.2.2 Concentration polarisation 
Concentration polarisation (CP) is the phenomenon of boundary layers created at 
the membrane surfaces where the solute concentration is different than the bulk so-
lute concentration. Thus, in FO, CP occurs at the draw side and at the feed side if 
this contains solutes (Figure 2). On the feed side, the CP is concentrative as the water 
permeates through the membrane while solutes are being retained at the membrane 
surface creating a boundary layer with increased solute concentration.  On the draw 
side, the CP is dilutive as the permeating water creates a boundary layer with de-
creased solute concentration [9]. 
 
Figure 2 - Concentration profile of two system configurations: Active-layer-facing-draw-solution 
with concentrative internal CP and dilutive external CP and active-layer-facing-feed-solution with 
concentrative external CP and dilutive internal CP. Despite lower effective Δπ, active-layer-facing-
draw-solution is used in FO due to non-severe and easier reversible fouling [9]. 
When CP happens at the active layer of the membrane, it is called external CP (ECP), 
and when it happens inside the support layer, it is called internal CP (ICP). As Fig-
ure 2 shows there are two system configurations: Active-layer-facing-draw-solution 
(AL-DS) with concentrative ICP and dilutive ECP, and active-layer-facing-feed-so-

greater effective osmotic difference, it also causes internal fouling in the support 
layer which is difficult to remove and causes significant flux decline. The AL-FS

2.2.3 Fouling 
Another cause for reduced water flux is fouling of the membrane. For non-porous 
membranes used in FO with AL-FS configuration, fouling usually happens by the 
formation of cake or surface layers on the active layer side of the membrane. This 
extra layer adds an extra resistance for the permeating water and can lead to an 
increased osmotic pressure at the feed side membrane surface resulting in lower 
water flux. External fouling like this is likely to be reversible [15]. 
 
2.3 Membrane distillation 
Membrane distillation is a thermally driven membrane process where the feed and 
permeate are separated by a hydrophobic porous membrane. The driving force is a 
difference in water vapor pressure induced by a higher feed temperature than per-
meate temperature. As the membrane is hydrophobic, water molecules can only 
pass the membrane in gas phase, giving the membrane a theoretical 100% rejection 
of non-volatile solutes such as salt ions [16][17]. 
MD has not yet been industrially implemented due to major barriers including 
MD module design, pore wetting, and low permeate flux. The number of publica-
tions in the area has however grown exponentially in the last decade, with a focus 
on MD for use in desalination, wastewater treatment, and liquid concentrate pro-
duction [18][19]. The four most investigated types of membrane distillation [16][19] 
are shown in Figure 3: Direct Contact Membrane Distillation (DCMD), Air Gap 
Membrane Distillation (AGMD), Sweeping Gas Membrane Distillation (SGMD), 
and Vacuum Membrane Distillation (VMD).

Ingrid A. Helgeland

Figure 3 - Principles of four membrane distillation (MD) configurations: Direct contact MD with con-
densation in a water stream, air gap MD with condensation on a cold plate, sweep gas MD and vac-

In DCMD and AGMD the driving force is purely the water vapor difference. In 
DCMD the permeate stream is pure water and the vaporisation and condensation 
happen directly at the membrane interfaces. It is the simplest MD method, but also 
has a problem with conductive heat loss. In AGMD the permeate side is a stagnant 
air gap which lowers the conductive heat loss but also lowers the mass transfer as it 
provides extra resistance. The permeate condensates at a plate, which is being kept 
cold by a cooling stream. In SGMD and VMD the driving force is both water vapor 
difference and removal of permeate, which is performed by a sweeping gas and by 
vacuum, respectively. These two methods therefore obtain higher flux than DCMD 
and AGMD but are also more expensive and complicated due to the needed external 
condenser unit. Also, SGMD requires large air flows and in VMD the vacuum must 
be regulated carefully to prevent wetting of the membrane [20][21]. For use in the 
new WRS, a SGMD configuration is used due to higher flux than DCMD and 
AGMD, and less risk of wetting compared to VMD.

2.4.3 Fouling and wetting 
The water flux can be further limited by fouling of the membrane which eventually 
can cause membrane wetting. 
Fouling in SGMD can happen by three mechanisms (Figure 5): 1) Pore closure 
where the pore size gets restricted, 2) Pore plugging where the pore gets blocked by 
compounds, and 3) Cake formation where a layer of foulants cover the pores. 
 
Figure 5 - Three types of fouling mechanism: Pore closure with reduction in pore size (left), Pore 
plugging which blocks the pore (middle), Cake formation which entails extra resistance (right) [15]. 
Which type of fouling occurs is dependent on many factors including pore size vs 
compound size and affinity between membrane and foulants. 
Pore closure or pore plugging is internal fouling which is often irreversible lead-
ing to the need for membrane replacement to maintain the desired flux. Due to the 
hydrophobicity of the MD membrane, fouling is more likely to happen if the feed 
stream contains hydrophobic compounds as they have a higher affinity towards the 
membrane than hydrophilic compounds [15]. 
Fouling can lead to wetting of the membrane, which reduces the rejection of 
non-volatile solutes. Wetting mainly happens due to three scenarios: The first sce-
nario is when foulants deposit on the membrane surface and reduces the hydropho-
bicity of the membrane. The second scenario is when the feed contains surfactants 
that attach to the membrane and reduce the hydrophobicity of the membrane. The 
third scenario is when an externally applied pressure (e.g. vacuum or underpres-
sure) is greater than the liquid entry pressure of the membrane [23].

2.5 The FO-MD system 
The proposed new WRS is a system consisting of two main units: A FO unit to ex-
tract water from the wastewater into a salt-based draw solution and a MD unit to 
recover the extracted water from the diluted draw solution as pure product water. 
The system is shown in Figure 6. 
The wastewater is pumped through the inside of the fibres (the lumen side) of 
a hollow fibre FO membrane coated with Aquaporin Inside® Technology in counter-
current flow direction with the shell side draw solution. The diluted draw solution 
is heated in two heat exchangers before entering the lumen side of a Memsift TS-30 
hollow fibre MD module. After exiting the membrane fibres, the concentrated draw 
enters the draw tank which is connected to the MD module. On its way back to the 
FO module the draw is cooled by a heat exchanger. An air suction pump creates an 
air flow functioning as sweep gas, through the shell side of the MD module to 
transport the permeate through a thermoelectric cooling module where the perme-
ate is condensed and collected in the permeate tank. The goal is to regulate the draw 
temperature by heating and cooling to reach a temperature of 25-30˚C in the FO 
membrane and a temperature of 70˚C in the MD membrane. 
Due to the theoretical complete rejection of non-volatile solutes in the MD unit, 
it is expected that the permeate water needs to be remineralised before being dis-
tributed for drinking. Using demineralised water as the only drinking water source 
is harmful to human health as it amongst other consequences causes increased uri-
nation, increased ion elimination (Na+, K+, Cl-, Ca2+, Mg2+) from the body resulting in 
overall negative ion balance, lower volumes of red blood cells, and inadequate body 
water redistribution which may compromise the function of vital organs [24]. These 
consequences would be even more critical onboard a space station thus reminerali-
sation of the drinking water is necessary. 
Another important factor in space is system operation safety, which is achieved 
by not using pressurised operations and operating at a maximum temperature of 
70°C.

2.5.1 Aquaporin Inside® Technology 
The Aquaporin Inside® Technology is an active layer coating with integrated aqua-
porin vesicles. Aquaporins are pore-forming proteins enhancing water transport 
through cell membranes in nature, giving them a perfect selectivity towards water 
[25]. The water transport through an aquaporin tetramer happens at a speed of 109 
water molecules per second corresponding to around 700 L water per second per g 
aquaporins. Therefore, by integrating the proteins into the polyamide-based active 
layer of a FO membrane, both the water selectivity and water flux increase [26][27]. 
Previous batch-batch experiments with the FO module using a 1 L 50:50 fresh 
urine/DI water feed and 2 L 1M NaCl draw solution have shown fluxes of 13, 10, 
and 8 LMH at 0%, 50%, and 70% recovery, respectively [28]. Additionally, an Aqua-
porin Inside® FO module has been tested successfully in microgravity onboard the 
ISS by astronaut Andreas Mogensen in 2015 [25].  
 
2.5.2 Memsift TS-30™ Improved Membrane Distillation 
The Memsift TS-30™ is a commercialised MD membrane by Memsift Innovations 
Pte Ltd. The membrane is made of the hydrophobic polyvinylidene fluoride 
(PVDF), which can withstand high temperatures and a variety of chemicals includ-
ing acids. PVDF also has high mechanical strength [29][30]. The hollow fibres are 
tri-bore fibres with three lumen channels inside one fibre allowing for 30% higher 
permeate flow than traditional fibres [31]. Memsift reports a water flux of 14 LMH 
for the module at draw tank temperatures of 70°C and a >99.9% rejection of NaCl 
[29].

2.6.2 Urea hydrolysis 
Several studies have investigated the urea hydrolysis of urine. As the hydrolysis (eq. 
(8)) turns the uncharged urea into charged compounds, an increase in conductivity 
is expected. Furthermore, an elevated pH is expected due to the formation of bicar-
bonate and hydroxide. This was confirmed by Ray et al. [36] who investigated both 
synthetic and fresh urine and confirmed correlations between the increase in pH 
and conductivity as a function of ammonium concentration (Figure 8). 
 
Figure 8 - Correlation between (a) pH and ammonia concentration and (b) conductivity and ammo-
nia concentration. Filled squares are fresh urine and empty squares are synthetic urine with added 
urease. Work by Ray et al. [36]. 
Based on conductivity measurements of fresh urine with addition of acids, Ray et 
al. also found that citric and acetic acid could inhibit the urease when added in con-
centrations of 32 meq/L and 81 meq/L, respectively, reaching a pH of ~4. Similar 
were the findings of Hellström et al. [37] as they also concluded that acid inhibits 
urea hydrolysis in urine. When adding 60 meq/L H2SO4 and 60 meq/L acetic acid 
they measured 2.2% and 4.4% NH4+-N/total-N, respectively, after 45 days of urine 
storage compared to 9.0% NH4+-N/total-N in the reference batch. 
Liu et al. [41] investigated the urea hydrolysis in fresh urine at room tempera-
ture at different initial pH adjusted by addition of 10 M NaOH. For raw urine with 
no addition of base, they found that pH and NH4+-N concentration stabilised after 
72 and 84 h, respectively, with only a slight increase in NH4+-N concentration for the 
first 40 hours of storage (Figure 9). Based on their experiments with different initial 
pH, they concluded that an initial pH of 8 delays the hydrolysis for more than 1 day.

2.8 Zeolite ion exchange 
Removing ammonium from drinking water is not a new concept and thus multiple 
techniques exist with one of the most common ones being selective ion exchange. 
Ion exchange is a widely used process with stable product quality that requires little 
maintenance [45] which are important factors for a WRS on a space station. Zeolites 
are naturally occurring aluminium silicate minerals with high porosity thus high 
sieving properties and high cation exchange capacities (CEC) and are therefore at-
tractive as ion exchange media. The most suggested zeolite variant for water treat-
ment applications is clinoptilolite which shows high ammonia selectivity. The 
chemical formula of clinoptilolite is (𝑁𝑎4𝐾4)(𝐴𝑙8𝑆𝑖40)𝑂96 ∙24𝐻2𝑂 which is struc-

rosity of approximately 34%. The substitution of Si4+ by Al3+ results in a mineral lat-
tice with high negative charge which is balanced by cations such as sodium, calcium, 
and potassium together with water molecules. These are the cations that are ex-
changeable with other cations [46][47].

Pre-treatment to homogenise the cations in the clinoptilolite lattice has shown to

ammonium CEC and that regeneration with a Na+ solution fully recovers the am-
monium CEC for the three first cycles.

Smaller grain size gives higher surface area and therefore increased CEC. Studies 
have shown that grain sizes should be < 1.0 mm for optimal ammonium CEC [46].

2.9 Thermo-responsive draw solution 
Alongside the increasing interest in FO in the past decades, the interest in draw so-
lutions and draw recovery methods have also increased. One method that combines 
draw solution and a pressureless draw recovery is the use of thermo-responsive 
polymers which researchers see potential in due to their desirable properties as 
draw solution: High osmotic pressure, low reverse solute flux, and easy regenera-
tion by temperature induced phase-separation [56]. Thermo-responsive polymers 
change from one-phase to two-phase systems and vice versa upon temperature 
changes. As the necessary temperature changes can be quite small, low-grade ther-
mal heat can be used for the recovery making it competitive to MD if it can reach a 
higher permeate flux. In addition, no latent heat of vaporisation is needed compared 
to MD where the water molecules go through a phase transition [57]. 
Thermo-responsive polymers mainly possess either lower critical solution tem-
perature (LCST) behaviour or upper critical solution temperature (UCST) behav-
iour, where the polymer in solution is a one-phase system at low or high tempera-
tures, respectively, as illustrated in the phase diagrams (Figure 12). The binodal 
marks the thermodynamic conditions where two phases may coexist and thus it 
separates the one-phase and two-phase region in the T-C-phase diagram. The criti-
cal temperature, Tc, is defined as the extrema of the binodal and the temperature at 
which the solution undergoes phase-transition at a given composition is called the 
cloud point temperature, Tcp. Tcp may vary depending on heating/cooling rates [58]. 
 
Figure 12 - Phase diagrams of thermo-responsive polymers possessing (a) LCST behaviour and (b) 
UCST behaviour. Tcp is the temperature at a given composition where the phase-transition happens 
and lies on the binodal. Tc is the extrema of the binodal [58].

Urea and ammonium concentrations were analysed using the Megazyme urea/am-

NADPH with ammonium ions (eq. (11)). The decrease in NADPH absorbance is

is added which catalyses the hydrolysation of urea to ammonium ions (eq. (12)). 
These ammonium ions then again react with NADPH leftovers (eq. (11)), absorb-
ance is measured once again, and the absorbance difference determines the urea 
concentration [60].

4.1.2 Mass balances 
To investigate if urea hydrolysis is inhibited and if there is a smaller ammonium 
loss, the mass balances are investigated. 
The mass balances for urea, ammonium, and the sum of those two, reported as 
nitrogen is shown in Figure 24 for the experiments using a u/c feed. The mass bal-
ances show loss of both urea and ammonium which may indicate that urea hydrol-
ysis does happen and that some of the produced ammonium reacts to ammonia and 
evaporates from the solution. If this is the case, it would however be expected to see 
a significantly smaller loss in the acidified solution which is not the case. As most of 
the cases have a loss of nitrogen and the mass balances are based on feed and draw 
concentrations, it could instead be due to adsorption of urea and ammonium in the 
membrane support and not an actual loss of nitrogen. This could be true for the six 
acidified experiments, but the three membranes used for the non-acidified experi-
ments had been used for previous experiments with urea and ammonium solutions, 
so they are expected to be saturated. Lastly, it could also be due to measurement

Water loss 
During the experiment, water loss from the draw loop was discovered. A water 
mass balance showed a total system water loss of ~870 mL corresponding to 35% of 
the water passing through the draw loop (calculations in appendix A3.3 Water bal-
ance). No leakages in the system were detected, however some of the water loss 
could be due to evaporation from the feed as the parafilm did not seal the feed blue-
cap bottle very well as it could not handle the heat. Also, some evaporation could 
have happened from the draw tank through the open inlet tube (picture in appendix 
A6 Pictures of open inlet tube into the draw tank). Lastly, some water could be lost 
through the suction pump if the condenser was not sufficient to condense all the 
product water. If this is the case, the real water production is higher than detected. 
However, more experiments, e.g. measuring air humidity in the outlet of the air 
suction pump, would be needed to investigate where the water loss happens, as it 
seems unrealistic that these suspected causes would result in a water loss of 35%. In 
the final system, water loss can rather easily be fixed, as it is a matter of creating a 
completely closed system with a sufficient condensing unit.

The wetting of the MD membrane is a major issue, which must be solved. Otherwise, 
the system cannot be used as the MD membrane will lose its rejection properties. 
Throughout all the experiments, it was observed that wetting happened when start-
ing the air suction pump at too low MD temperatures. The threshold was observed 
to be around ~50°C. This could be the reason why the wetting happened in the five-
day operation as the air suction pump was started at ~52°C, however it could also 
be that the fouling of the membrane made it more hydrophilic as described in sec-
tion 2.3 Membrane distillation. In addition, this could explain why letting the mem-
brane dry over a weekend does not regain the hydrophobic properties. It is also 
likely to be a combination of the two, where operating at too low temperatures 
caused the membrane to wet, enabling the precipitate to attach inside the pores and 
turning their surface hydrophilic. The fouling cause could be confirmed by a mem-
brane autopsy, and if any of these causes are true, then the wetting would not be a 
problem if the system is always operated at temperatures above 55°C and the pre-
cipitation is removed due to corrosion free system.

Chapter 5  
Conclusion and Outlook 
The proposed new water recovery system for use in the Gateway has been investi-
gated. The mechanism of urea and ammonium passing the FO and MD membranes 
was investigated and full system operations were executed to see if the system can 
fulfil the WRS requirements. Additionally, the potential of zeolite as a product water 
polisher and the effect of dormancy on the FO modules were tested. Proof-of-con-
cept experiments with a thermo-responsive draw were performed. 
 
Investigating urea and ammonium rejections of both the FO and MD membrane 
showed a considerable increase in rejection when acidifying the u/c feed and the 
draw with acetic acid to pH~4. FO rejections of urea went from 80.7% to 84.4%, while 
they for ammonium went from 80.5% and 97.7%. For MD, the urea rejection was 
100% while the ammonium rejection improved from ~25% to ~90% when acidifying 
the draw. Results also showed that the rejections were quite stable even at high 
wastewater recoveries (up to 90%). Acid clearly impacts the urea and ammonium 
transfer, but a closer investigation is needed to learn more about the exact transport 
mechanism. 
Full system operations showed that a water recovery of 90% was possible. How-
ever, the average water production was only 2.11 L/d, thus far from the requirement 
of 4 L/d. The calculated draw lifetime was 8 h, as the ammonium concentration ex-
ceeded the allowed limit of 1 mg/L after only 1.3 L produced water. The 1.3 L col-
lected product water did however show very low Na+, Ca2+, K+, and Cl- concentra-
tions, all well below their limits, contrary to TOC concentration of 42.8 mg/L which 
exceeds the allowed limit of 3 mg/L. Also, the product water pH of 4.15 was outside 
the allowed range of 4.5-8.5. 
Product water polishing via zeolite ion exchange was investigated both in batch 
and continuous operation. Analysis showed poor ion exchange and no ammonium 
removal in batch operation. The product water in continuous operation indicated 
ion exchange as the ammonium concentration decreased from 0.47 to 0.03 mg/L. In 
addition, the concentrations of Na+ and K+ increased while still being below the max-
imum allowed concentrations and the pH increased from 4.15 to 7.29 thus being 
within the allowed range. 
==PAGE:81==
Ingrid A. Helgeland 
 
4th of June 2021 
 
 
 
Page 67 
During the five-day full system operation three issues were discovered that re-
quire system improvement: 1) After day one, precipitate started forming in the 
draw, most likely consisting of iron from corroded heat exchangers. 2) On day five, 
the MD membrane wetted, which was either caused by operating at temperatures 
below ~50°C, by attachment of the precipitate making the membrane hydrophilic, 
or by a combination of those two. 3) Throughout the five days a water loss from the 
system was discovered. Since no leakage was detected, it is believed to be caused by 
evaporation from feed and draw, and by an insufficient condensing unit. 
 
 In general, the FO-MD system is easy to operate, start up, and shut down. No 
decline in FO module performance could be detected by a 10 weeks dormancy pe-
riod at 4°C after each module had been used to reach a 90% recovery of water from 
an acidified u/c feed. 
A FO-TR system for use in microgravity would require a centrifuge to separate 
the phases and a filtration step to remove the last percentages of polymer in the 
aqueous phase. In addition, a product water polishing step is needed as the aqueous 
phase after regenerating the thermo-responsive draw contained 2.20-3.61 mg/L am-
monium. The needed centrifuge results in a doubling of total system weight, vol-
ume, and energy requirement for the FO-TR compared to the FO-MD. Therefore, it 
is suggested to continue investigations with an improved FO-MD system instead of 
developing a FO-TR system.  
 
Suggested system 
The suggested FO-MD system for further investigations is shown in Figure 48. The 
process flow diagram is essentially the same as the one used in the five-day opera-
tion but with an added product water polishing step including a pump and a zeolite 
column. The following changes are suggested to meet the requirements for the new 
WRS. 
To continue being able to reach a water recovery of 90% when working with 
more concentrated urine than was used in this project, the current draw of 200 mL 
2M NaCl needs improvement. For comparison, the urine used in this project con-
tained 2-10 g urea/L compared to reported usual concentrations of 9-23 g urea/L. 
More concentrated urine brings about higher osmotic pressure of the wastewater 
thus lower FO driving force and it increases the ammonium concentration in the 
product water. Either a higher concentration or a bigger volume is needed to pro-
vide a sufficient osmotic pressure to reach 90% recovery in the FO unit. Experiments 
==PAGE:82==
Ingrid A. Helgeland 
 
4th of June 2021 
 
 
 
Page 68 
must also be carried out to investigate the magnitude of the reverse salt flux and its 
effect on the draw lifetime. It might be beneficial to investigate whether another salt 
enables higher ammonium rejections and/or lower reverse salt flux. 
 
Water production of 4 L/d using a 0.3 m2 MD membrane can be reached by 
operating at a stable draw temperature of 70°C compared to the current fluctuating 
draw temperature reaching a maximum of 60°C.  
A draw lifetime of 47.6 days can be reached by adding a 100 g zeolite column to 
polish the product water. This is purely based on the ammonium concentration 
however the TOC concentration must also be lowered to below 3.0 mg/L in the prod-
uct water. It must be investigated whether the TOC content is a result of evaporated 
acetic acid that has passed the MD membrane and, in that case, whether another 
acid (e.g. an inorganic acid) can be used instead. 
To overcome the three issues discovered during the five-day operation, the sys-
tem should consist of corrosion free material and must always be operated at tem-
peratures above 55°C. Additionally, to prevent water loss it must be ensured that 
the condensing unit is sufficient. Both the heating system and the condenser can be 
improved by better insulation, a larger heat transfer area, and/or using a material 
with a better heat transfer coefficient.

When operating the FO-MD system, every batch of wastewater plus the initial draw

showed an increase in pH from 3.64 to 5.11, either due to reverse acid flux from the 
draw to the wastewater or due to evaporation. 
The added product polishing step will function as an ammonium removal and 
remineralisation unit. Further investigations of the zeolite column on how to reach 
optimal ammonium removal capacity by adjusting e.g. residence time and type of 
zeolite must be carried out. In addition, zeolite pre-treatment could be investigated 
to tailor the column for remineralisation purposes. 
Before a possible overnight shutdown or longer dormancy period, it is sug-
gested that cleaning is performed by flushing the FO feed side with product water. 
How small the volume of the flush water can be is yet to be determined. The used 
product water can be collected and recovered as wastewater after the system is 
started up again. 
 
Further investigations 
The project of developing a new WRS for the lunar space station, the Gateway, has 
now taken a big step forward. The full system is tested, and challenges and possible 
solutions are identified. A summary of further investigations to confirm the usabil-
ity of the suggested system follows here: 
- 
Investigate whether the high TOC content in the product water is caused

-

o The impact of reverse salt flux and the needed initial salt concentra-

o The effect of more concentrated urine in the wastewater 
o Validate and optimise the effect of the added zeolite column

- 
Investigate the effect of dormancy on the MD module

pacity of clinoptilolite in continuous operation. They performed the experiment 
with a 5 mg NH4+/L solution through a 100 mL column containing 84.94 g clinop-
tilolite at different residence times. For a residence time of 15 min, they found an ion 
exchange capacity of 11.9 mg NH4+/g clinoptilolite before the column effluent 
reached a breakthrough concentration of 1 mg/L. 
The draw lifetime calculation with a 100 g zeolite column is therefore based on 
an ion exchange capacity of 11.9 mg NH4+/g clinoptilolite and an average ammo-
nium concentration of 5 mg/L in the product water. To account for an imperfect 
system an efficiency factor of 80% is included.

A8 Estimation of system comparison parameters 
The suggested FO-MD and FO-TR systems are compared on three parameters: 
Weight, volume, and energy requirement. 
The volume of the FO-MD system is reported by Memsift [29] and 2 L are added 
to account for the addition of the FO module and the water polishing unit making 
the total volume 24 L. The volume of the FO-TR system without the centrifuge is 
assumed to be around 2 L smaller than the FO-MD system due to replacing the MD 
module with a smaller filtration step. The volume of the centrifuge is assumed to be 
around 20 L based on pictures presented in [67] resulting in a total volume of the 
suggested FO-TR system of 42 L. 
The weight and power calculations are based on number of equipment units. 
Weight and power values for single equipment are the best-case scenarios reported 
by Memsift [29] except for the centrifuge values which is reported in [67]. The 
weight of the centrifuge is assumed to be at least 6 kg but is likely to be much heav-
ier. The weight of the tubing is assumed to around 300 g.
