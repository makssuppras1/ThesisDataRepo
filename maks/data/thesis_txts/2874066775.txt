Abstract
The urgent need to decarbonize European energy systems has positioned hybrid power plants
with integrated power-to-hydrogen (P2H) as critical infrastructure for industrial decarbonization.
This thesis investigates techno-economic optimization of hybrid plants combining wind, solar
PV, battery storage, and electrolyzers across diverse European electricity markets.
Using the HyDesign tool and 2024 datasets for weather, electricity prices, and grid carbon in-
tensity in 13 countries, the study evaluates standalone electrolyzers, renewable-only hybrids,
and integrated P2H under varying regulatory frameworks.
Results show that P2H integration substantially reduces renewable curtailment while adding
revenue streams, though economic performance involves trade-offs between higher profitability
and reduced capital efficiency. Regional analysis highlights strong variations: low-cost electric-
ity markets achieve high returns, while high-price markets make hydrogen production unviable.
Implementation of Renewable Fuels of Non-Biological Origin (RFNBO) requirements removes
viability in most markets, concentrating activity in countries with low-carbon grids. Environmen-
tal assessment confirms strong advantages, with green hydrogen far below the carbon intensity
of blue hydrogen alternatives.
Green hydrogen competitiveness requires coordinated policy support through CAPEX subsi-
dies, green premiums, and industrial demand mandates. Findings show that hybrid plants with
integrated hydrogen production can enhance renewable value and provide viable decarboniza-
tion pathways, provided regulatory frameworks align with local market conditions rather than
uniform European rules.
Resume
Det presserende behov for at afkarbonisere Europas energisystemer har positioneret hybride
kraftværker med integrerede power-to-hydrogen (P2H) systemer som central infrastruktur for
industriel afkarbonisering. Denne afhandling undersøger den teknisk-økonomiske optimering
af hybride kraftværker, der kombinerer vind, solceller, batterilagring og elektrolyse på tværs af
europæiske elmarkeder.
Ved brug af HyDesign og datasæt fra 2024 for vejr, elpriser og nettes CO2-intensitet i 13
lande evalueres fritstående elektrolyseanlæg, udelukkende VE-baserede anlæg samt integr-
erede P2H-systemer under forskellige regulatoriske rammer.
Resultaterne viser, at P2H-integration kraftigt reducerer spild af vedvarende energi og skaber
nye indtægtsstrømme, men med afvejninger mellem højere absolut profit og lavere kapitaleffek-
tivitet. Den regionale analyse afslører store forskelle: lave elpriser giver høje afkast, mens høje
priser gør brintproduktion økonomisk uholdbar.
Implementeringen af RFNBO-krav fjerner levedygtighed i de fleste markeder og koncentrerer
produktion i lande med lavkulstofbaserede elsystemer. Den miljømæssige vurdering bekræfter
klare fordele, idet grøn brint opnår markant lavere CO2-intensiteter end blå alternativer.
Grøn brints konkurrenceevne kræver koordineret politisk støtte gennem CAPEX-tilskud, grønne
præmier og industrielle efterspørgselskrav. Afhandlingen viser, at hybride kraftværker med in-
tegreret brintproduktion kan øge værdien af vedvarende energi og levere levedygtige afkar-
boniseringsveje, forudsat at reguleringen tilpasses lokale markedsforhold frem for ensartede
europæiske løsninger.

1
Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
The global energy sector is experiencing significant changes driven by the need for decarboniza-
tion. Climate commitments, including the COP28 pledge to triple global renewable capacity by
2030, are driving rapid deployment of variable renewable energy (VRE) sources, with wind and
solar photovoltaic (PV) becoming the dominant technologies [1]. This transition is transforming
electricity systems and requiring new approaches to power generation and grid management.
To address the variability of VRE sources, hybrid power plants (HPPs) combining wind, PV, and
battery energy storage systems (BESS) have gained attention as a technically and economically
viable solution. By using complementary resource profiles and storage capabilities, these sys-
tems can increase renewable integration, reduce short-term fluctuations, and improve system
reliability. Additionally, co-located HPPs offer benefits including optimized land use, shared
grid infrastructure, and enhanced operational flexibility [2]. These advantages can result in
lower costs and improved project economics.
However, rapid VRE scaling presents challenges. In many electricity markets, grid conges-
tion and curtailment events are becoming more frequent, reducing economic returns for asset
owners and affecting investor confidence. This highlights the need for innovative energy conver-
sion and storage solutions that can absorb surplus renewable generation and provide medium
to long-term storage capacity.
Power-to-hydrogen (P2H) represents a solution by converting excess renewable electricity into
storable chemical energy. Green Hydrogen, produced through water electrolysis powered by
VRE sources, can help decarbonize difficult sectors such as industrial processes, long-distance
transport, and seasonal heating [3]. In addition to sectoral decarbonization, integrating P2H into
HPPs offers system-level benefits, including better resource utilization, increased grid stability,
and diversified revenue streams. By supporting hydrogen economy development, P2H also
strengthens the long-term value of renewable assets.
1.2
Research Questions
This thesis investigates the co-optimization of hybrid renewable energy systems with integrated
hydrogen production, focusing on system sizing, operation, and competitiveness under diverse
market and grid conditions. It addresses the following research questions:
1. How can correlated datasets for weather, electricity prices, and grid CO2 intensity deter-
mine optimal sizing of wind, solar PV, and BESS in grid-connected HPPs across different
European locations?
2. To what extent does P2H integration reduce curtailment, improve VRE utilization, and
enhance economic performance under limited grid connection capacities?
3. How do the techno-economic and environmental performances of Green Hydrogen from
co-located HPPs compare to other types of Hydrogen?
4. Under which market, cost, and policy conditions can Green Hydrogen become cost-competitive
with blue Hydrogen?
1.3
Thesis Objective and Scope
The objective of this thesis is to develop an integrated techno-economic and environmental
optimization framework for sizing grid-connected HPPs combining wind, solar PV, BESS, and
1
==PAGE:16==
P2H systems.
The analysis uses correlated 2024 datasets for electricity prices, grid CO2 intensity, and me-
teorological inputs, allowing location-specific modeling across different European regions with
varying weather conditions, market structures, energy mix, and VRE penetration levels.
The research is conducted in three stages:
1. HPP-Only Optimization: Determine optimal sizing of wind, solar PV, and BESS under
current market conditions and grid constraints.
2. P2H Integration: Extend the model to include electrolyser systems for on-site Green Hy-
drogen production, evaluating their role in reducing curtailment, enhancing VRE utilization,
and improving project economics.
3. Hydrogen Competitiveness Assessment: Compare Green Hydrogen and other types
of Hydrogen, using cost and carbon emission metrics to evaluate competitiveness under
varying market, cost, and policy scenarios.
The outcomes provide location-specific optimal sizing strategies, quantify the economic and en-
vironmental benefits of P2H integration, and identify key market and policy factors to accelerate
competitive renewable-hydrogen system deployment in Europe.

This thesis is organized into the following chapters:

2.1
Hybrid Power Plants (Wind–Solar–Battery) Capacity Sizing
and Optimization
2.1.1
Technical Foundations and Research Evolution
Global deployment of wind and solar PV technologies has accelerated significantly in recent
years, with cumulative installed capacity projected to continue growing under both the Renew-
ables 2023 main case and the Net Zero Emissions (NZE) by 2050 Scenario [4]. As shown in
Figure 2.1, total wind and PV capacity is expected to nearly triple between 2022 and 2030, with
solar PV accounting for the majority of additions.
Figure 2.1: Historical and projected cumulative solar PV and wind capacity under RE 2023 and
NZE [4].
• Renewables 2023 main case: International Energy Agency (IEA)’s primary forecast for
VRE deployment up to 2028, based on current policies and market developments. This
forecast projects approximately 3,700 GW of new VRE capacity between 2023 and 2028,
with solar PV and wind accounting for 95% of global renewable expansion. [4]
• Net Zero Emissions (NZE) by 2050 Scenario: A pathway to limit global temperature rise
to 1.5°C above pre-industrial levels. The NZE Scenario aims to achieve global net zero
energy sector CO2 emissions by 2050, requiring global installed VRE capacity to reach at
least 11,000 GW by 2030.[5]
HPPs integrating multiple renewable technologies have emerged as a response to grid inte-
gration challenges and economic optimization requirements. [6, 2]. The concept has evolved
from co-located but independently operated assets toward fully integrated systems with shared
infrastructure, coordinated dispatch, and advanced control strategies [7, 8].
A key advantage of combining wind and solar lies in the complementary nature of their gener-
ation profiles. Solar PV output follows a diurnal cycle, peaking during midday, whereas wind
generation often exhibits higher capacity factors during evenings, nights, and winter months
in many European regions [4]. This temporal complementarity is also evident seasonally: in
Northern Europe, wind tends to peak in winter while solar peaks in summer, reducing overall
variability when resources are combined. Figure 2.2 shows monthly capacity factors for Den-
mark and Germany, illustrating how hybridization can smooth aggregate output.

The integration of BESS further enhances system flexibility by enabling time-shifting of energy,
mitigating short-term variability, and providing ancillary services such as frequency regulation
and reserve capacity [10]. In HPPs, BESS can also reduce curtailment during periods of high
VRE output and low demand or limited grid export capacity [11].
Recent research has focused on optimal sizing and coordinated operation of wind-solar-BESS
configurations, using meteorological, market, and grid datasets. Advanced optimization frame-
works seek to enhance techno-economic performance by maximizing NPV, minimizing LCOE,
and improving resource utilization while adhering to technical and regulatory constraints [6, 12].
As VRE share continues to grow across the EU, HPPs are expected to become crucial in achiev-
ing decarbonization targets and ensuring system reliability [1, 13].
This optimization challenge is compounded by grid congestion in high VRE penetration regions
and limited transmission capacity. Such bottlenecks lead to increased curtailment of renewable
generation, resulting in lost economic value and underutilized clean energy potential. In this
context, integrating BESS within HPPs not only supports system flexibility but also provides a
means to capture otherwise curtailed energy, shifting it to periods of higher demand or market
value. This capability is becoming critical for both improving project profitability and enabling
deeper renewable integration.
2.1.2
Grid congestion
Grid congestion has emerged as a critical challenge in integrating high shares of VRE into the
European power system. Congestion occurs when transmission or distribution networks reach
their operational limits, preventing electricity delivery from generation sites to demand centers.
This leads to increased operational complexity and curtailment of renewable generation, under-
mining decarbonization targets and investment returns [13, 14].
In recent years, renewable capacity expansion has significantly outpaced grid infrastructure
growth. According to ENTSO-E, several Member States including Denmark and Germany are
experiencing structural bottlenecks, particularly in regions with high onshore and offshore wind
capacity [15]. For example, north-to-south transmission corridors in Germany frequently reach
capacity limits during periods of high wind generation in the north, leading to redispatch mea-
sures and renewable curtailment. In Denmark, local congestion is increasingly observed in
West Jutland due to rapid wind and solar deployment coupled with limited interconnection and
internal transfer capacity.
Congestion has direct economic impact through increased redispatch and balancing costs,
while also reducing effective utilization of renewable assets. In 2023, curtailment volumes in
some European countries reached record levels, with a growing share attributable to structural
4
==PAGE:19==
rather than temporary constraints [13]. This trend is expected to intensify without timely in-
vestments in both physical infrastructure and operational measures, including advanced grid
management, demand-side flexibility, and hybrid asset optimization.
In the context of HPPs, grid congestion underscores the importance of co-locating BESS and
other flexibility options. These can absorb excess generation during constrained periods and
release it when capacity becomes available, thereby reducing curtailment, increasing revenues,
and supporting system stability [14].
2.2
Hydrogen in Decarbonization Strategies
Hydrogen is increasingly recognized as a versatile energy carrier with the potential to enable
deep decarbonization across multiple sectors. Unlike electricity, hydrogen can be stored for
long periods, transported over long distances, and used as a feedstock or fuel in applications
where direct electrification is technically challenging or uneconomical [16, 17].
2.2.1
Current Role, Carbon Intensity, and Decarbonization Potential
Global hydrogen production today is overwhelmingly dominated by fossil fuel-based pathways,
with natural gas and coal accounting for over 95% of supply. This results in annual CO2 emis-
sions of around 900 Mt, comparable to the combined emissions of the global shipping and
aviation sectors [16, 18]. Electrolytic hydrogen currently represents less than 1% of global pro-
duction, underscoring the scale of transition required to achieve decarbonization goals.

Implications for Hybrid Power Plants
From an HPP perspective, co-locating electrolyzers with wind and solar generation could:
• Install additional VRE capacity using limited grid connection
• Improve VRE utilization and reduce curtailment
• Provide new revenue streams from Green Hydrogen sales
• Mitigate grid congestion by absorbing excess generation

2.3
Integrating Power-to-Hydrogen (P2H) for Flexibility and
Curtailment Reduction
2.3.1
Flexibility Needs in High-VRE Systems
The increasing penetration of VRE sources, such as wind and solar PV, has transformed the
operational dynamics of modern power systems. While these resources are essential for de-
carbonization, their inherent variability and limited dispatchability create operational challenges.
Periods of high VRE generation coinciding with low demand can lead to significant surplus elec-
tricity, which, if not managed effectively, results in curtailment of otherwise zero-carbon energy.
P2H provides a promising pathway to address these challenges. Through electrolysis, surplus
electricity is converted into hydrogen, which can be stored, transported, or used in other sectors
such as industry, transport, and heating. By acting as a large, flexible, and controllable load,
electrolyzers can absorb excess generation and provide a demand-side balancing mechanism.
7
==PAGE:22==
Beyond curtailment reduction, P2H enables sector coupling, linking the electricity sector with
the broader energy system. Modeling studies for Europe suggest that electrolyzers operating
primarily on surplus electricity could supply up to 30% of current hydrogen demand, avoiding
approximately 20 Mt CO2 per year. However, relying solely on surplus electricity for hydrogen
production is rarely cost-optimal, reinforcing the need for integrated planning and complemen-
tary operational strategies. [4]
2.3.2
P2H in Hybrid Power Plants
Integrating electrolyzers directly with HPPs offers distinct technical and economic advantages.
During periods of low electricity prices or grid congestion, electrolyzers can convert low-cost
VRE electricity into hydrogen, effectively storing energy in chemical form for later use or sale.
This approach reduces VRE curtailment, improves asset utilization, and creates additional rev-
enue streams through Green Hydrogen sales.
Figure 2.5: Main configurations for integrating electrolyzers with VRE sources and the grid.
Adapted from [21].
As shown in Figure 2.5, P2H systems incorporated in HPPs can be classified into four main
configurations:
1. Inflexible, grid-connected: Electrolyzers run at high load factors using only grid electric-
ity.
2. Flexible, grid-connected: Electrolyzers source power only from the grid but adjust oper-
ating hours to minimize LCOH.
3. Co-located (island): Electrolyzers are connected directly to a VRE asset without a grid
connection, relying solely on on-site generation.
4. Co-located (grid-connected): Electrolyzers are directly linked to a VRE asset and also
connected to the grid, allowing both VRE and grid electricity supply.
Operational strategies vary greatly between these configurations: flexible grid-connected se-
tups can reduce LCOH by over 20% compared to inflexible ones, while co-located grid-connected
plants (Type 4) offer the best balance between high VRE penetration and economic perfor-
mance, particularly in markets with volatile electricity prices [21]. These systems can strate-
gically operate during low-price or negative-price hours, improving load factors without com-
promising their carbon intensity, especially when supported by Guarantees of Origin (GOs) or
RFNBO-compliant sourcing.

2.3.3
Challenges in P2H Integration
The deployment of P2H systems within HPPs involves technical, economic, and regulatory con-
siderations. From a design perspective, optimal electrolyzer sizing is important: oversizing
leads to low utilization factors and high capital costs, while undersizing limits the potential to
reduce curtailment and capture low-cost electricity. Hydrogen storage can separate produc-
tion from consumption, enabling more stable electrolyzer operation and load factors, but adds
significant cost, space, and safety requirements [13].
Electrolyzer efficiency, particularly at partial load, remains a key factor in project economics.
For example, alkaline units typically experience efficiency drops below 40–50% load, making
intermittent operation less attractive unless supplied with very low-cost or curtailed electricity.
Operating strategies that align electrolyzer dispatch with periods of low or negative electricity
prices can significantly improve competitiveness , but require advanced forecasting and market
access. [21]

2.4
Focus on Green vs Blue Hydrogen: Economic and
Environmental Perspectives
2.4.1
Life-Cycle Greenhouse Gas Emissions
While Blue Hydrogen is often presented as a low-carbon option, life-cycle assessments (LCAs)
show that it can still have significant greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions. These emissions come
from upstream methane leakage in the natural gas supply chain and from incomplete CO2 cap-
ture during production.
Studies show that methane leakage and the energy needed for carbon capture can make Blue

2.4.2
Economic Cost Competitiveness
Blue Hydrogen has been considered the more cost-competitive near-term option, benefiting
from the maturity of SMR technology and existing gas infrastructure. Between 2021 and 2023,
costs in Europe were typically estimated at €3–7/kg for Blue Hydrogen, while Green Hydrogen
remained more expensive due to electrolyzer CAPEX and VRE electricity costs [24, 25].
The 2021–2022 energy crisis significantly reduced this gap as rising natural gas prices drove up
Blue Hydrogen costs [16]. In contrast, Green Hydrogen is less affected by gas price changes
and is projected to reach cost parity by the late 2020s in many regions with abundant low-cost
wind and solar, such as the Middle East, North Africa, parts of China, and the EU [17].
Projections from IRENA indicate that Green Hydrogen could cost less than Blue Hydrogen in
many regions by 2030, with tighter methane leakage regulations and higher CO2 capture re-
quirements further reducing Blue Hydrogen’s competitiveness. Policy support plays an impor-
tant role: the U.S. Inflation Reduction Act, for instance, offers tax credits of up to $3/kg for clean
hydrogen, substantially improving Green Hydrogen economics [24].

2.5
European Context and Policy Drivers
The EU’s strategies for reducing carbon emissions, including the European Hydrogen Strat-
egy [27] and REPowerEU [28], play an important role in advancing HPPs and P2H systems.
10
==PAGE:25==
Green Hydrogen is recognized as an important low-carbon raw material for industry, helping
integrate large amounts of VRE into the electricity grid. Policy objectives aim to install 40 GW
of electrolyzer capacity within the EU by 2030, with significant investment in ”hydrogen valleys”
and large-scale infrastructure for renewable hydrogen.
Europe’s geographic resource differences guide technology preferences: Southern Europe’s
strong solar irradiance makes it suitable for PV-electrolyzer systems, whereas Northern Europe,
particularly the North Sea region, with its abundant wind resources, focuses on wind-based
hydrogen production.
Denmark leads in implementing offshore wind energy and integrating P2X technologies. The
Danish Power-to-X Strategy [29] aims to achieve an electrolysis capacity of 4-6 GW by 2030,
with a focus on transforming offshore wind energy into Green Hydrogen and e-fuels for both
local consumption and export. Several studies have investigated Denmark’s potential in this
area:
• Systems combining offshore wind with electrolyzers show that transforming surplus wind
power into hydrogen could help with low electricity prices. Research demonstrates that
selling hydrogen to sectors such as industry or transportation is more profitable than con-
verting it back to electricity, considering current fuel cell costs and efficiencies [30].
• Analysis of the potential market for Green Hydrogen in Denmark using historical power
market data found that in 2018, only around 14% of the time offered low-cost excess
wind power for running electrolyzers, suggesting a frequent need to use grid electricity.
As a result, hydrogen production becomes highly influenced by changes in wholesale
electricity prices. Forecasts indicate that with improvements and scaling, the LCOH could
drop below €2/kg, compared to a baseline of €2.5–5/kg [31].
2.5.1
Other Regional Studies in Europe
Denmark’s P2X roadmap can be compared by examining other European regions:
• Germany: Germany focuses on P2H integration to manage surplus VRE. The country
operates a large P2H plant with PEM electrolysis (Energiepark Mainz), which converts
surplus wind energy into hydrogen. Germany’s Hydrogen Strategy emphasizes convert-
ing existing natural gas storage facilities for hydrogen storage to support volatile VRE
integration.[3]
• Southern Europe : High-irradiance regions are promising for PV-electrolyzer systems.
Studies indicate that LCOH below €3/kg is achievable by 2030 under low-cost solar PPAs,
with projections suggesting costs could fall to €1.5/kg in optimal solar regions. However,
daily solar production profiles require grid integration or battery storage to increase elec-
trolyzer utilization due to the intermittent nature of solar generation. [32]
Renewable Fuels of Non-Biological Origin (RFNBOs) are essential to the EU’s decarbonization
strategy as outlined in the Renewable Energy Directive (RED II) and its amendments. These
fuels include renewable hydrogen and e-fuels derived from hydrogen, created using VRE, pro-
vided they follow strict sustainability and GHG reduction guidelines [33, 34]. The European
Commission’s Delegated Regulation (EU) 2023/1184 outlines specific electricity sourcing con-
ditions for RFNBOs, including:
• Additionality: Electrolyzers must be powered by newly constructed VRE assets (after
July 2023) or through PPAs with VRE facilities of equivalent capacity.
• Temporal correlation: By 2030, electricity used in producing RFNBOs should match with
generation on an hourly basis; until then, monthly matching is acceptable.
11
==PAGE:26==
• Geographical correlation: The VRE facility must be located in the same bidding zone
as the electrolyzer or in a connected zone with comparable or lower marginal emissions.
The GHG accounting requirements state that RFNBOs must achieve at least a 70% reduction in
life-cycle emissions compared to the 94 gCO2e/MJ fossil fuel benchmark. These calculations,
as defined in Delegated Regulation (EU) 2023/1185, take into account upstream electricity emis-
sions, the efficiency of the electrolyzer, and additional processing steps.
Countries like Germany, Poland, the Netherlands, Belgium, and France, being the leading am-
monia producers in Europe, face growing pressure to lower carbon emissions in their hydrogen
usage. The revised RED requires that by 2030, each Member State must ensure at least 42 per-
cent of their industrial hydrogen consumption comes from RFNBOs, excluding hydrogen used
in fuel refining.
Figure 2.7: Hydrogen demand per country and sector in Europe in 2023 [32].
In practice, meeting RFNBO criteria impacts the design and location choices for HPPs and P2H
facilities, especially where VRE resources, grid carbon intensity, and electricity market prices
vary by region. Projects in areas with high winds and low marginal emissions may find it easier
to meet hourly matching and GHG reduction conditions compared to those in grid-restricted or
carbon-heavy areas.
Under the Delegated Regulation (EU) 2023/1184, the use of grid electricity for RFNBO produc-
tion is allowed under strict conditions. Electricity drawn from the grid can be considered fully
renewable only if it meets the additionality, temporal correlation, and geographic correlation cri-
teria. In practice, this often requires the electrolyzer operator to secure PPAs or guarantees
of origin from VRE sources, ensuring that the electricity mix effectively matches VRE genera-
tion [33, 34].
During periods where the grid carbon intensity exceeds acceptable levels, the consumed elec-
tricity may not be eligible for RFNBO certification unless it can be shown that an equivalent
amount of VRE generation was simultaneously injected into the grid in the same bidding zone.
This approach aims to avoid indirect emissions from fossil-based generation while allowing flexi-
bility for electrolyzers to operate beyond direct physical coupling with VRE assets. There is clear

2.6
Key Factors Influencing Profitability and Performance
2.6.1
Renewable Resource Variability and Availability
The local resource profile of wind and solar availability strongly influences the use of HPPs and
electrolyzers. Year-to-year and seasonal weather changes can cause large swings in hydrogen
production and revenue. For example, in 2018 in Denmark, only 14% of the year had low-cost
surplus wind energy available for electrolysis, despite high wind penetration. [31]
Developers must balance between average and worst-case resource availability, potentially
oversizing VRE to ensure adequate electrolyzer operation. Poor resource matching increases
the need for storage. Sensitivity analyses show that hydrogen unit cost is highly sensitive to
VRE capacity factors: better siting and resource diversity significantly reduce production costs
[5].
Geographic spread of VRE assets can further reduce the impacts of local weather events, re-
ducing downtime and smoothing the power profile supplied to the electrolyzer. Variability also
affects electrolyzer performance: frequent cycling and partial-load operation can reduce effi-
ciency and accelerate stack degradation, increasing operational costs and replacement fre-
quency.
Integrating detailed forecasting into HPP dispatch strategies enables operators to
predict fluctuations, optimize hydrogen production schedules, and reduce curtailment losses
without significant overbuilding of VRE capacity.[17, 16]
2.6.2
Market Conditions (Electricity and Hydrogen Markets)
Electricity and hydrogen market dynamics are central to the viability of P2H. Electrolyzers are
most profitable when they can access low-cost or surplus electricity, such as off-peak or curtailed
power. High or volatile power prices reduce electrolyzer utilization, especially if operators must
buy electricity at market rates. In Denmark, historical market data show that long periods of high
wholesale prices can make hydrogen production uneconomic unless supplemented by curtailed
VRE generation or long-term fixed-price PPAs [31].
Hydrogen sale prices and market maturity are equally important. Strong demand in transport,
industry, or export markets is needed to make money from hydrogen production. Literature
commonly presents breakeven hydrogen price ranges from €2–6/kg, depending on electricity
costs, electrolyzer CAPEX, and financing conditions [16]. In developing markets, the absence of
13
==PAGE:28==
stable offtake agreements or hydrogen trading hubs creates revenue uncertainty, discouraging
investment. Policy instruments such as carbon pricing, guarantees of origin, and contracts for
difference for renewable hydrogen can significantly enhance project economics [16, 35].
2.6.3
Technology Cost Trajectories
The cost trends for electrolyzer technologies reflect a dynamic landscape marked by both re-
cent increases and strong long-term reduction potential. In 2022, hydrogen production costs
across all key electrolysis technologies increased, largely driven by high inflation, rising capital
investment requirements for both electrolyzers and new VRE sources, and higher interest rates.
[32].
Similarly, grid-connected electrolysis saw average costs increase, primarily due to high natural
gas prices that affected wholesale electricity markets. Despite these recent increases, primarily
due to supply chain disruptions and increased financing costs, the long-term outlook for elec-
trolyzer costs remains optimistic due to economies of scale and mass manufacturing [16].

Data Sources and Assumptions
3.1
Main Assumptions
This thesis is built upon several key assumptions that define the scope and limitations of the
analysis:
Permitting and Land Limitations. The optimization framework excludes regulatory permitting
procedures and environmental impact assessments from the analysis.
Electricity Market Data. The study assumes that 2024 day-ahead market prices provide an
accurate representation of electricity price profiles at each location, without considering future
price developments, hedging mechanisms, or long-term power purchase agreements. Imbal-
ance is also not considered.
Hydrogen Offtake and Transport. Complete hydrogen offtake is assumed at a specified sales
price, while hydrogen transportation and distribution infrastructure beyond the production facility
boundaries are not considered.
Technology Modeling. The analysis incorporates simplified models for renewable energy and
storage technologies based on the following characteristics:
• Wind turbines are modeled using rated power, hub height, power curves, and annual
degradation rates, while advanced control systems such as pitch or yaw control are ex-
cluded.
• Photovoltaic systems are represented as fixed-tilt installations with constant performance
ratios, annual degradation rates, and temperature-irradiance effects.
• Battery energy storage systems account for nominal capacity, round-trip efficiency, maxi-
mum depth of discharge, temporal degradation, and minimum state-of-health thresholds.
Hydrogen Production Technology. Electrolyzer performance models incorporate nominal effi-
ciency values and load-dependent efficiency curves, excluding stack degradation effects. Water
supply requirements include treatment cost considerations.
Grid Connection. A fixed maximum export capacity is assumed for each location, with grid con-
nection costs included in the economic assessment. Electricity consumption tariffs are excluded
due to the assumed thigh voltage level connection.
Financing Assumptions. The financial model incorporates corporate tax rates, weighted av-
erage cost of capital, and a fixed annual inflation rate, while excluding debt financing structures,
equity requirements, construction period interest, and loan repayment schedules.
Environmental Data. Carbon intensity values are sourced from Electricity Maps data base,
excluding site-specific environmental permitting requirements.[36]
Operational Considerations. The model does not explicitly account for forced outages, sched-
uled maintenance periods, or performance impacts from partial load operation.
Costs. All costs are primarily sourced from Copenhagen Energy, the Danish Energy Agency
database, and NREL (see Appendix A.1). The values reflect 2024 cost data.
Annual comparison. For the yearly comparison, data from 2021, 2023, and 2024 is considered.
The year 2022 is excluded, as it represents an exceptional case due to the war in Ukraine and
the resulting surge in gas prices. Including 2022 would therefore distort the analysis and not
provide a reliable basis for comparison.
15
==PAGE:30==
Design Constraints. The optimization design space reflects realistic European project develop-
ment conditions. Wind turbines are limited to 20 units due to typical permitting and visual impact
restrictions. Solar PV capacity is constrained to 120 MW, corresponding to approximately 100
hectares of available land at standard ground-mounted densities. Battery storage duration is
fixed at 4 hours, following current European market practice.

Optimization Methodology
This chapter outlines the modeling framework, data sources, energy management approach,
economic and environmental evaluation methods, and the optimization techniques employed
to assess and design hybrid power plants with power-to-hydrogen integration.
4.1
Overview of the HyDesign Optimization Tool
HyDesign is an open-source optimization tool developed at DTU Wind for designing grid-connected
utility-scale hybrid power plants [6]. The tool optimizes the sizing of integrated wind, solar PV,
BESS, and P2H systems to maximize net present value or minimize levelized cost of energy.
The methodology employs a nested optimization approach where the outer loop determines
optimal component sizing while the inner loop optimizes operational strategies through energy
management system control. Key features include detailed wind turbine selection algorithms,
wake loss modelling, component degradation models for wind, PV, and battery systems, and
economic evaluation of battery cycling during peak price periods.
HyDesign enables realistic hybrid power plant simulations by incorporating weather variabil-
ity, electricity price signals, and long-term component degradation effects. The tool provides
essential financial metrics for project feasibility assessment and includes publicly available doc-
umentation with sample weather and price data for various locations.
Figure 4.1: XDSM Diagram for HPP sizing in HyDesign
Figure 4.1 presents the HyDesign workflow for hybrid power plant sizing, illustrated as an Ex-
tended Design Structure Matrix (XDSM) diagram. The baseline model includes wind, solar PV,
and battery storage components. The nested optimization operates through three stages: wind
turbine selection and sizing, PV design, and energy management system (EMS) optimization.
18
==PAGE:33==
The EMS optimizes dispatch based on resource availability and curtailment minimization, while
the financial module evaluates NPV, IRR and LCOE metrics.
The XDSM structure visualizes data flow between optimization routines, surrogate models (wake
loss, battery degradation), and physical models through colour-coded blocks representing dif-
ferent model components.
This thesis extends the baseline HyDesign framework with:
• Bidirectional grid interaction for electricity import and export
• Power-to-Hydrogen subsystem integration via electrolysis
• Modelling of grid-only electrolyser operation
• Modelling of islanded HPP with electrolyser integration
• Modelling of Grid-connected HPP with electrolyser
• Hydrogen storage and offtake pathways
These extensions enable techno-economic analysis of integrated renewable generation, energy
storage, and hydrogen value chains within a unified optimization framework.

4.3
Energy Management Strategy (EMS)
The Energy Management Strategy (EMS) governs the optimal dispatch of the hybrid power
plant’s generation, storage, and hydrogen production subsystems. It is implemented as a time-
series optimization problem over a given scheduling horizon and solved using a MILP formula-
tion in DOcplex with the IBM CPLEX solver.
At each time step, the EMS determines the allocation of available renewable generation from
wind and solar among:
• Direct export to the grid,
• Charging or discharging the battery storage system,
• Supplying the electrolyzer for hydrogen production.
The objective of the formulation is to maximize the total system revenue, subject to a set of
operational and technical constraints described in the next section.
The EMS supports bidirectional grid interaction, allowing the plant to import electricity when
it is economically beneficial (e.g., during low or negative price periods) and to export surplus
generation when prices are favorable. It also incorporates the possibility of hydrogen storage
and delayed offtake, enabling temporal decoupling of production and market sales.

Carbon pricing
PCO2 denotes the carbon price in €/tCO2, reflecting the cost of emitting one
ton of CO2. It increases the cost of blue hydrogen, which has residual emissions, while green
hydrogen remains largely unaffected. Carbon pricing has not been implemented or analyzed in
this study.
The break-even condition LCOHgreen = LCOHblue provides critical thresholds for competitive-
ness as a function of electricity prices, gas prices, and carbon pricing policies.
Green hydrogen competitiveness can be achieved through two complementary pathways: re-
ducing green hydrogen costs (via lower electricity prices, cheaper electrolysers, or operational
improvements) or increasing the effective cost of blue hydrogen (through higher gas prices,
carbon pricing, or regulatory constraints on emissions).
Market penetration beyond cost parity depends on additional factors. Green hydrogen market
share Sgreen can be expressed as:
Sgreen = f(∆cost, R, I, D)
(7.6)
• ∆cost = LCOHblue −LCOHgreen: cost advantage,
• R: regulatory support (mandates, standards),
• I: infrastructure readiness (storage, transport, refueling),
• D: demand-side factors (industrial offtake agreements, clean hydrogen premiums).
Key levers for market penetration include: cost reduction (∆cost > 0), policy frameworks (R),
infrastructure development (I), and demand creation through industrial partnerships or carbon-
conscious procurement (D).
As established in the previous chapter, financing considerations including capital recovery fac-
tors, debt structures, and associated financial instruments are excluded from this analysis, with
costs presented on a simplified basis to focus on the fundamental economic drivers of hydrogen
production competitiveness.
Infrastructure requirements including transportation networks, and distribution systems are not
addressed in this study, as the analysis focuses exclusively on production-level cost competi-
tiveness between green and blue hydrogen pathways.

7.3
Examples of Regulatory Support
The European Union has introduced several policy mechanisms to support green hydrogen
development and improve its cost competitiveness against conventional hydrogen production :
RED III targets.
The Renewable Energy Directive III, effective since November 2023, estab-
lishes mandatory green hydrogen quotas. Industries must source at least 42% of their hydrogen
from renewable sources by 2030, increasing to 60% by 2035. The transport sector faces a 1%
minimum renewable hydrogen requirement by 2030 [34]. These quotas create guaranteed de-
mand for green hydrogen, reducing market risk for producers.
European Hydrogen Bank.
Launched in 2023, the European Hydrogen Bank provides direct
subsidies through competitive auctions. The first auction awarded €800 million in premiums to
seven projects. A second auction in 2024 allocated €992 million to 15 projects with premiums up
to €4/kg for 10 years [32]. These premiums directly reduce the effective cost of green hydrogen
production by supplementing market revenues.
Contracts for Difference.
Several countries have implemented long-term contracts that guar-
antee minimum prices or carbon savings, reducing investment risk and financing costs.
Germany’s carbon contracts for difference (Klimaschutzverträge) provide 15-year price guaran-
tees for industrial decarbonization projects. The first €4 billion program launched in 2024 covers
projects achieving 17 million tons of CO2 savings. The UK’s Low Carbon Hydrogen Agreements
offer 15-year revenue support, with the first round securing 125 MW of capacity. [18].
These regulatory instruments work by either creating mandatory demand (quotas), providing
direct cost support (subsidies), or reducing financing risk (long-term contracts). Together, they
aim to bridge the cost gap between green and conventional hydrogen until technological im-
provements achieve natural cost parity.[16]
Summary
This analysis suggests that green hydrogen could become cost-competitive through several
complementary pathways, including reducing electrolyzer costs, improving financing conditions,
introducing market-based mechanisms such as green premiums, and increasing capacity uti-
lization through industrial demand. A coordinated policy approach combining these measures
appears most promising.
It should be noted, however, that the LCOH values used here include the full HPP system, and
therefore cannot be directly compared with LCOH values from standalone electrolyzers or blue
hydrogen facilities. This discussion should be seen as a set of suggestions rather than a full
feasibility study, as the final economic viability has not been analyzed in detail.

8
Limitations
This study presents several methodological and analytical limitations that should be considered
when interpreting the results and applying findings to real-world project development. The analy-
sis relies on simplified cost models that may not fully capture the complexity of real-world market
dynamics, assumes consistent policy implementation across different regulatory environments,
and incorporates other methodological constraints that warrant careful consideration.
8.1
Modeling Assumptions and Simplifications
8.1.1
Technology Modeling
The optimization framework employs simplified models for renewable energy and storage tech-
nologies that may not capture all operational complexities. Production calculations were per-
formed using HyDesign, which employs voluntarily conservative modeling assumptions that
may underestimate actual project performance.
For real project development and financing, industry-standard software such as WindPRO for
wind energy and PVSyst for photovoltaic systems would be necessary to meet bankability re-
quirements and provide more accurate production estimates. Wind turbines are modeled using
basic power curves and annual degradation rates, excluding advanced control systems such
as pitch or yaw optimization that could improve performance. Similarly, photovoltaic systems
assume fixed-tilt installations with constant performance ratios.
The electrolyzer model incorporates nominal efficiency values and load-dependent curves but
excludes several critical operational factors:
• Degradation and maintenance: Stack degradation effects and periodic maintenance
shutdowns are not modeled
• Dynamic limitations: Ramping constraints, transient efficiency penalties, and opera-
tional time delays are omitted
• Parasitic loads: Standby power consumption during idle periods affects system eco-
nomics but is excluded
• Operational constraints: Minimum load requirements, cold start-up penalties, and ther-
mal management considerations are not represented
These limitations may result in optimistic performance projections compared to real-world elec-
trolyzer operations.
8.1.2
Financial and Economic Assumptions
The financial analysis relies on fixed discount rates and simplified cost structures that may not
reflect project financing complexity. A realistic financial model would incorporate:
• Capital structure: Mixed debt-equity financing with varying interest rates and repayment
schedules
• Construction phase: Interest during construction, construction loans, and phased capital
deployment
• Refinancing considerations: Potential refinancing opportunities and associated costs
throughout project lifecycle
• Market dynamics: Variable hydrogen demand and pricing rather than constant fixed-
price assumptions
77
==PAGE:92==
These simplifications may overstate project returns compared to actual financing conditions and
market realities.
8.2
Offtake Assumptions and Market Realities
8.2.1
Electricity Offtake Limitations
The electricity revenue model assumes pure merchant operations based on day-ahead market
prices, which presents significant financing challenges in practice:
• Merchant risk exposure: Reliance solely on spot market prices creates revenue volatility
that most project financiers find unacceptable for debt servicing
• Imbalance costs excluded: The model omits imbalance penalties and costs associated
with forecast deviations, which can significantly impact actual revenues
• Realistic financing requirements: Most renewable projects require partial revenue se-
curity through Power Purchase Agreements (PPAs) or Contracts for Difference (CfDs) to
achieve bankable financing terms
8.2.2
Hydrogen Offtake Assumptions
The hydrogen revenue model relies on several market assumptions:
• Infinite demand assumption: The model assumes unlimited hydrogen offtake capacity,
while actual markets have limited demand and require established supply chains
• Fixed price simplification: Continuous availability at fixed prices ignores market volatility,
seasonal demand variations, and supply-demand dynamics typical of emerging markets
• Storage and logistics omitted: The analysis excludes hydrogen transportation infras-
tructure, and delivery logistics that significantly impact project economics
• Price sensitivity: Hydrogen pricing, derived from literature review, has an extremely sig-
nificant impact on results. Negotiated prices or market conditions could yield completely
different economic outcomes
These offtake assumptions likely overstate project viability compared to realistic market condi-
tions and financing requirements.
8.3
Data and Temporal Limitations
8.3.1
Market Data Representativeness
The analysis uses 2024 electricity price data as representative of future market conditions, which
presents several limitations regarding market representation and risk assessment:
• Static market assumptions: 2024 data may not capture long-term structural changes in
European electricity markets, including evolving generation mixes and grid infrastructure
developments
• Geopolitical volatility example: The exclusion of 2022 data due to the Ukraine war
illustrates how external events can dramatically alter energy market dynamics, while this
conflict increased prices benefiting producers, it demonstrates potential for sudden market
reversals
• Contractual protection requirements: Without price protection mechanisms, projects
remain vulnerable to both positive and negative price shocks that could fundamentally
alter economic attractiveness and financing viability
• Future market evolution: Increasing renewable penetration, grid flexibility measures,
and energy storage deployment could substantially alter the price patterns and arbitrage
opportunities identified in this study
78
==PAGE:93==
These factors suggest that real project development should incorporate multiple price scenarios
and robust risk mitigation strategies rather than relying on single-year market data.
The carbon intensity data from Electricity Maps, while comprehensive, represents historical grid
conditions that may not reflect future decarbonization trajectories. As European grids become
cleaner, the RFNBO constraints and grid-based hydrogen production economics could change
significantly.
8.4
Geographic and Market Scope
8.4.1
Limited Regional Coverage
The detailed case study focuses primarily on Denmark and Germany, with broader European
analysis limited to 13 countries, which presents several geographic and cost modeling limita-
tions:
• Limited geographic representation: The scope may not capture the full diversity of Eu-
ropean energy markets, particularly in southeastern and newer EU member states where
different market structures and regulatory frameworks exist
• Uniform technology cost assumptions: The model assumes identical equipment costs
across all European locations, which may not reflect regional variations in labor costs,
permitting requirements, and supply chain access that could significantly impact project
economics
• Grid connection cost simplification: The analysis assumes uniform grid connection
costs across countries, while actual costs vary substantially based on local infrastructure,
grid capacity, and connection requirements
• Land cost standardization: Land acquisition costs are treated uniformly despite signifi-
cant regional variations in land prices, availability, and leasing structures across European
markets
• Freight and logistics variations: While cost data may differ slightly due to transportation
and logistics factors, these impacts are considered less significant compared to other cost
components and market structure differences
These standardized cost assumptions may mask important regional competitive advantages or
disadvantages that could influence actual project location decisions and economics.
8.5
Regulatory and Policy Limitations
8.5.1
RFNBO Implementation Uncertainty
The RFNBO constraint modeling assumes perfect enforcement and compliance monitoring,
while real implementation may face practical challenges and interpretation variations across
member states. The temporal correlation requirements, hourly matching by 2030, has been
implemented in this work but represent a significant operational constraint that may be difficult
to achieve in practice.
8.6
Technical and Operational Constraints
8.6.1
Grid Integration Simplifications
The fixed grid connection capacity assumption may not reflect dynamic grid constraints, conges-
tion management, or curtailment patterns that vary by location and season. Real grid integration
involves complex interactions with transmission system operators, balancing responsibilities,
and ancillary service provision that are not captured in the simplified modeling framework.
79
==PAGE:94==
8.7
Optimization and Methodological Limitations
8.7.1
Deterministic Optimization
The optimization framework uses deterministic weather and price scenarios based on single-
year historical data rather than stochastic approaches that could better capture the inherent
uncertainty in renewable resource availability and market conditions.
The deterministic methodology may lead to overoptimistic sizing decisions that perform well
under ”average” conditions but fail to account for:
• Resource variability: Low wind or solar years that could reduce production and revenues
significantly below modeled expectations
• Price volatility: Market conditions that deviate from historical patterns, potentially render-
ing optimized configurations economically unviable
• Worst-case scenarios: Equipment sizing that lacks sufficient margin for unfavorable con-
ditions, potentially threatening project viability and debt servicing
• Risk management requirements: Lender and investor expectations for robust perfor-
mance across multiple scenarios, not just optimal conditions (P50-P90...)
A stochastic optimization approach using Monte Carlo simulations or scenario-based optimiza-
tion would provide more robust sizing recommendations that account for uncertainty and risk
tolerance, though at the cost of increased computational complexity and data requirements.

Conclusion
This thesis has demonstrated that integrating power-to-hydrogen systems within hybrid power
plants brings value for renewable energy project development, transforming simple electricity
generators into industrial facilities capable of producing multiple energy carriers while dramati-
cally improving system efficiency.
9.1
Answer to Research Questions
Research Question 1: Optimal HPP Sizing Across European Locations
The optimization analysis revealed that correlated datasets for weather, electricity prices, and
grid CO2 intensity enable location-specific sizing strategies that differ significantly from conven-
tional statistical approaches. The increasing occurrence of negative price hours and growing
concerns about grid congestion further encourage the integration of multiple generation and
storage technologies, optimized jointly to maximize system value.
Regional analysis across 13 European countries confirmed that optimal sizing depends strongly
on local electricity price structures and grid carbon intensity. High-price markets favor renewable
energy sales configurations, whereas low-price markets with clean grids are more suited to
electrolyzer-oriented strategies, creating distinct opportunities for regional specialization.
This study provides a tool to evaluate the optimal installed capacities under a fully merchant
strategy based on historical data.
Research Question 2: P2H Impact on Curtailment and Economic Performance
Power-to-hydrogen integration achieves a strong elimination of renewable energy waste, reduc-
ing curtailment by up to 95%. This turns electricity that would otherwise be lost into valuable
hydrogen products.
The study also shows that adding an electrolyzer allows for higher renewable capacity to be
installed, which would otherwise be limited by grid connection constraints.
From an economic perspective, P2H can increase overall profitability despite the high elec-
trolyzer investment, with favorable hydrogen offtake conditions turning it into a strong business
case. Under supportive market conditions, it reduces curtailment, opens new revenue streams,
and supplies low-carbon hydrogen for industry, delivering both environmental and economic
benefits.
Research Question 3: Green vs. Other Hydrogen Types Comparison
The techno-economic analysis revealed that green hydrogen from grid-connected co-located
HPPs achieves carbon intensities of 1.28 kg CO2/kg H2 in Denmark, significantly below the EU
RFNBO threshold of 3.38 kg CO2/kg H2. Under RFNBO compliance, this improves further to
0.56 kg CO2/kg H2, representing very low emissions hydrogen production.
However, the economic comparison with blue hydrogen remains challenging. The LCOH of
green hydrogen from integrated HPP systems still exceeds typical blue hydrogen costs. This
cost gap reflects the early-stage nature of electrolytic hydrogen production and the need for
substantial cost reductions or policy support to achieve competitiveness.
This study has shown that the era of low-carbon hydrogen is emerging, but realizing its full po-
tential will require strong and decisive policies to accelerate the transition from carbon-intensive
fossil fuels to clean alternatives.

Cost competitiveness requires a coordinated approach combining multiple support mechanisms.
The analysis identified four suggested pathways:
1. Direct cost reduction: Electrolyzer CAPEX subsidies for low carbon Hydrogen produc-

2. Favorable financing: Reduced WACC through green financing mechanisms
3. Green premiums: Certificates or guarantees of origin enable
4. Demand optimization: Industrial mandates improving capacity factors of the installed

9.2
Suggestions for Future Work
The findings from this study reveal several opportunities for methodological advancement and
practical application that could significantly enhance the accuracy and applicability of hybrid
power plant optimization models.
Advanced Contract Design and Market Integration
Future research should investigate flexible hydrogen offtake contract structures that incorporate
adaptive pricing mechanisms for delivery shortages and surpluses, variable delivery horizons
aligned with renewable energy patterns, and differentiated pricing for RFNBO-compliant ver-
sus low-carbon hydrogen streams. Integration with electricity market participation and ancillary
services could provide additional revenue streams that enhance hybrid system viability under
regulatory constraints.
Stochastic Optimization and Risk Assessment
The deterministic optimization framework should be replaced with stochastic approaches using
Monte Carlo simulations to capture uncertainties in renewable resource availability, electric-
ity prices, and policy evolution. Multi-year risk assessment incorporating weather variability,
technology learning curves, and market development scenarios would provide more robust in-
vestment guidance for diverse European markets.
Enhanced Technical Modeling
Several technical modeling improvements could significantly enhance prediction accuracy. Fu-
ture work should incorporate detailed electrochemical models that capture current density ef-
fects, temperature dependencies, and catalyst degradation mechanisms. Advanced modeling
of thermal dynamics would provide more accurate efficiency predictions and enable better opti-
mization of electrolyzer operating strategies under variable renewable energy conditions.
Realistic Financial Structures
Future research should model realistic electricity revenue structures incorporating long-term
Power Purchase Agreements and Contracts for Difference rather than pure merchant market
exposure. Hybrid PPA structures combining fixed-price components with market participation
would provide more bankable revenue projections and reflect actual project financing require-
ments in European renewable energy markets.
Circular Economy Integration
Future studies should investigate the valorization of waste heat from electrolyzer operations
for district heating, industrial process heat, or greenhouse applications. Integration with circu-
lar economy principles could explore synergies with nearby industrial facilities, where excess
oxygen production could serve chemical processes while waste heat supports local energy de-
mands, creating additional revenue streams and improving overall system economics.
These research directions would collectively address the methodological limitations identified in
this study while advancing the practical deployment of economically viable hydrogen production
systems across diverse European market conditions.

9.3
Closing
In conclusion, the methodology developed in this study provides a systematic framework for
evaluating the economic viability of hybrid power plants with integrated hydrogen production
across diverse European electricity markets. While acknowledging the outlined methodological
limitations, the analysis offers valuable insights for both policymakers and industry actors seek-
ing to understand the complex interactions between renewable energy generation, hydrogen
production, and regulatory constraints such as RFNBO requirements.
The findings from the multi-country case study deliver directly applicable insights for strategic
planning of renewable energy and hydrogen projects under current and anticipated regulatory
frameworks. The comparative analysis reveals critical market-specific optimization strategies,
demonstrating that no universal hybrid solution exists across European markets.
While hybrid power plants with hydrogen production may become essential for achieving Eu-
ropean decarbonization goals and managing renewable energy integration, such investments
will only be realized through thorough understanding of location-specific business cases and
clear evaluation of optimal system configurations. The study’s findings suggest that successful
hydrogen economy development requires differentiated regional strategies rather than uniform
technological approaches, emphasizing the critical importance of matching technology choices
to local market conditions and regulatory environments.

P2H System
The power-to-hydrogen system encompasses electrolyzer technology and hydrogen storage.
Parameters are based on PEM electrolyzer technology and compressed hydrogen storage.
Rationale for Selecting PEM Electrolysis
Proton Exchange Membrane (PEM) electrolysis was selected for this study due to its superior
performance under variable renewable power supply conditions. Unlike Alkaline Electrolysis
(AEL), PEM systems can operate efficiently with intermittent wind and solar generation, offering
faster ramp rates and greater dynamic range to respond to electricity price variations [3, 2].
Electrolyzer and Hydrogen Storage Modeling
The electrolyzer model represents a grid-connected PEM system characterized by a piecewise
linear efficiency curve. Hydrogen is stored in a buffer tank with capacity constraints, enabling
intertemporal decoupling between production and offtake.

Cost Allocation Methodology
The shared costs are allocated based on system capacity and operational characteristics:
• Electrical Infrastructure: BOS and grid connection costs scale with total grid connection
capacity (GMW)
• Land Costs: Differentiated approach based on technology-specific land use require-
ments
• CAPEX vs OPEX: Electrical infrastructure treated as CAPEX; land costs as annual OPEX
Balance of System (BOS) Components
The BOS costs encompass multiple system components:
• Electrical Infrastructure: Switchgear, protection systems, and control equipment
• Communication Systems: SCADA, monitoring, and remote control capabilities
• Site Preparation: Access roads, foundations, and site development
• Interconnection Equipment: Transformers, metering, and grid interface equipment
Key Assumptions
• Integrated Approach: BOS costs represent an integrated hybrid power plant rather than
separate technology installations. Shared electrical infrastructure reduces overall system
costs compared to separate installations.
• Land Use Optimization: Solar PV land costs based on area requirements, then land is
rent for the project lifetime. Wind turbine costs based on individual turbine footprint impact
as a percentage of annual revenue paid to the landowner.

A.2
Use of Artificial Intelligence
Generative artificial intelligence (AI) tools were employed throughout this study as supportive
instruments to improve efficiency in both model development and documentation. Their use
was primarily limited to refining language, ensuring consistency, and enhancing the structure of
the academic text, thereby improving clarity and coherence.
All analytical content, model formulation, and interpretation of results were developed indepen-
dently by the author. AI contributed solely to accelerating the editing process and improving
overall readability.
In addition, AI was used as a coding assistant to debug Python scripts, optimize plotting routines,
and translate high-level modeling concepts into functional code. At no stage was AI applied to
generate data, perform numerical analysis, or replace the critical reasoning involved in inter-
preting results.
This use of generative AI is consistent with responsible academic practice, serving transparently
to enhance efficiency and writing quality while preserving the originality and integrity of the
research.
