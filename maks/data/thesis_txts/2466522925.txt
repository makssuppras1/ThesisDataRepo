Preamble
Mankind is greedy of nature. It always wants more, whether it is obtaining more wealth in the form of
resources or money, procuring a deeper understanding of our surroundings or seizing a larger extension
by going places no one has ever been before. To match the greed, technology is an ever transforming
matter, assisting us in obtaining more.
Within the last century, mankind has taken giant leaps in the process of seizing more in all the extends
mentioned, and technology has been our greatest help. Technology has made it possible to retrieve oil
from the North Sea, technology has enlightened humans about our origin, and technology has put the
ﬁrst men on the Moon.
But our greed is leading us to a point where we begin to need more. We need more to keep up with
the growing technologies, and to help us out of the hole we are digging. To keep obtaining more wealth,
we need more resources than what exist on Earth. To keep procuring a deeper understanding of our
surroundings we need to investigate things from the beginning. Not just the beginning of mankind, but
the beginning of our solar system and universe. And to keep going places no one has ever been before, we
need to go beyond the borders of the Earth, and even go further than New Horizons which is currently
cruising through the Kuiper Belt.
There is no solution that can solve it all, but by launching mining in space we are taken closer.
This thesis discusses some of the problems in the world, that mining in space would be able to solve, and
why the ﬁeld is needed in the modern world. The discussion is followed up by outlining the design of a
prospecting mission for mining in space. To outline the prospecting mission analysis of instruments for
the mission and possible targets is required. The thesis has a focus on the measurement techniques that
may be of use on such a mission, and presents two other products than the mission outline, being the
design of a simple reﬂection spectrometer for composition analysis, and a program to treat the measured
spectra from the reﬂection spectrometer with.

2.1
How does mining in space help solve a current problem on Earth?
The world of technology has been changing at a very rapid pace ever since the industrial revolution. But
with these changes new problems has arisen, both politically and technologically.
With the advancements in the electrical powered vehicles, better batteries are needed to hold and deliver
the required power of the engines. These batteries are made from lithium and cobalt to get as large a
capacity as possible, along with a light weight, compact size and fast charge and discharge capability.
More than half the amount of cobalt discovered in the crust resides in The Democratic Republic of Congo,
a country with doubtful human rights and child labor. If the cobalt could come from space instead, it
would be possible to stop the purchase from DRC and hence stop the support of child labor and their
controversial rule.[1]
The Rare Earth Elements (REE) is a group of elements composed of the Lanthanides along with the
elements scandium and yttrium, that, despite their name, are not rare on Earth. They are called as such
because they were considered rare back in the 18th century when they were discovered. They are however
diﬃcult and expensive to extract, as they are not found in concentrated ores, so even though most of
them fall within the 50th percentile of abundant elements in the crust, they are diﬃcult to access. They
have a large amount of uses, where some of the most common include the use as catalysts, magnets,
and polishers. They are essential in modern technology, and has no replacement. Retrieving these from
extraterrestrial objects could help prevent a otherwise possible shortage due to low accessibility of the
elements.[2]
The Platinum Group Metals (PGM) is also a group of elements that are of high value on Earth. The
elements include ruthenium, rhodium, palladium, osmium, iridium, and platinum, which are all among
the least abundant minerals on Earth. All known deposits of the elements are either found near meteor
impact sites, or volcanic eruption zones, meaning they are either brought to Earth from space, or come
from the core of Earth and has been brought to the surface through volcanic activity. Just like the REE
the PGM are used as catalysts, but also in electronic components, and medical supplies, just to mention
a few. As these elements are so scarce on Earth, it would be valuable retrieving them from space for
further technology advancements.[54]
Figure 1 shows the abundances of the elements in the crust of Earth. Many of the least abundant elements
may be found in extraterrestrial objects, which can help solve problems on Earth such as shortages of
important elements in technology, but also political problems. As mentioned, it would be possible to
boycott DRC and stop the support of their use of child labor if cobalt could be retrieved elsewhere. As
China and The United States of America are currently in a trade war, with many of the least abundant
and REE found in China, retrieving materials from space could prevent the trade war from blocking
technological evolution as there would be no material shortages.
When addressing this question, the possibility of keeping the materials for processing and usage in space
should not be left out, but this discussion will be attended to in section 2.3.

2.2
Where is the ﬁeld of mining in space today?
Was this question answered a year ago, two companies would have been working determinedly on mining
in space. Today, these two companies have been sold, and are no longer invested in mining. The compa-
nies being discussed are Planetary Resources and Deep Space Industries, two American companies that
had raised major funding and developed a plan for mining asteroids.[5] The companies will be brieﬂy
discussed nevertheless, as the general ideas they had about mining asteroids are still highly relevant.
Planetary Resources was founded in 2012 with the mission of expanding the natural resource base of the
Earth. This should be done by mining the near Earth asteroids for PGM which then should be retrieved
to the Earth, and water which could be used in space for fuel and life support. A set of telescopes was
planned to be deployed in orbit around Earth for studying and selecting targets for further inspection.
The further inspection should include a swarm of small spacecrafts send out to the selected targets to
further analyse them. Finally the targets would be selected based on the mineral maps constructed by
the small spacecrafts and larger, fully autonomous spacecrafts would be sent out to mine the selected
targets. The company managed to launch a small prospecting satellite into the orbit of Earth, but was
sold due to lack of funding.[6]
Deep Space Industries was founded in 2013, but with a broader mission than Planetary Resources. DSI
wanted to use the majority of the mined minerals in space, by capturing asteroids and moving them to
a station in orbit around Earth, to make the materials more accessible and not having to worry about
the asteroid’s period. They planned to extract everything from the asteroid, using the water-ice for fuel
for station keeping of satellites around Earth to prolong their lives, the minerals for utilization in space
through remoulding in a 3D printer, and only the metals not abundant on Earth, such as those in the
PGM would be transported to Earth for utilization. The company never managed to launch any space-
crafts, and struggled getting funding from the beginning, hence why it has now been sold.[7]
Despite the two main players are out of the game, the ﬁeld of mining in space is not a lost cause
yet. In June 2019, Trans Astronautica Corporation was granted funding from NASA to further develop
on their asteroid mining concept, where they plan to capture asteroids and use optical mining to extract
volatiles3, that can be converted into fuel. This fuel can be used to further propel the spacecraft and
be transferred to other spacecrafts to enhance their reach. The company is using some of the leftovers
from DSI to construct their ﬁrst spacecraft, which is set to launch in the beginning of 2020.[8] Also Moon
Express are further developing their idea of mining the Moon, with funding received from NASA. Their
plans have three steps; ﬁrst they will send up scouts to complete further analysis of the Moon, second
they will launch outposts for easier access and research of the poles and third they will begin harvesting
the Moon. The third step will be launched ﬁrst to prove the mission will work, and will launch in 2020.[9]
Neither ESA or JAXA have funded mining missions in space yet.

2.3
Where are the mined materials put to best use?
In section 2.2 where companies working on mining in space, were brieﬂy mentioned, it was also ad-
dressed that some planned to use the mined materials in space and others to bring them down to Earth.
If the materials are brought down to Earth, some of the problems mentioned in section 2.1 could be solved.
If the materials, however are kept in space it can help further develop the space technologies. Keep-
ing the materials in space requires the capability to process them there. This can be done either by each
of the mining spacecrafts, or on larger stations, where the aforementioned can be in orbit or placed on
the surface of an extraterrestrial object, being for example the Moon or Mars. Processing the materials
on each of the mining spacecrafts saves the costs of a station, but requires each of the mining spacecrafts
to be larger and more mobile so they can transport the processed materials to where they are needed. A
third option is designing the spacecrafts that require the use of the materials in a way so they can process
the materials themselves. This would although require that the spacecrafts would need to be even larger,
and it would cost less to have the required materials launched with the spacecraft from Earth, as the
mass of this probably would be smaller than the mass of a processing unit.
There is anyhow no need to keep the materials in space if they cannot be used there.
Water-ice extracted from the extraterrestrial objects can be split into hydrogen and oxygen, which can
be used as fuel for spacecrafts, and life support for manned missions. Water in its combined form can
also be used for life support, and as a radiation shield to both protect potential crew members on board
a spacecraft or sensitive electronic equipment where radiation may heavily aﬀect the performance.
Valuable materials, as those mentioned as REE or PGM in section 2.1 are also used in the space industry,
where they are used to enhance the strength and durability of components, make stronger magnets for
attitude determination and in batteries delivering power to the spacecraft, just to mention a few. Other
minerals, such as iron, can be used in construction materials. These kind of minerals need extensive
processing before they can be used in space. It is diﬃcult to imagine that it will be a possibility in the
near future to perform this processing, unless large stations are set up.
There are three possible scenarios where this could happen, as of today. If a base was set up on the
Moon, or set up on Mars, the materials could be brought to use in the construction process and if the
base was manned, in science aspects providing further material to build instruments. The third option
is the use of the materials in the construction of a Deep Space Gateway, or on board the station after
construction for the manufacturing of other instruments or spacecrafts to be launched from the station4.
It is possible to make a cost-beneﬁt of the situation, to establish where the materials are of best use.

Why do we need mining in space?
Mining in space is needed for three main reasons: science, business and a possible salvation. The reasons
will be addressed in the written order.
As will be brieﬂy touched upon in section 3.2, materials from extraterrestrial objects can help scien-
tists understand the formation of the Solar System and the Earth. This understanding can help resolve
how the Earth will evolve in the future, and how it became what it is today. Scientists hope to be able
to answer two of the most essential questions about our origin; how did water occur on Earth, and how
did life commence. By refueling spacecrafts in space, an even longer reach will also be brought to the
spacecrafts, and it is possible to achieve larger velocities when the majority of the fuel is not used to exit
the gravitational ﬁeld of the Earth.
Since the agricultural revolution brought along trade to the humankind, business has been a driver.
Most people will always want more, but as negative as it may sound, it is a good trait to posses as it
drives progression. Progression is required to enhance the lives of the inhabitants on Earth while preserv-
ing the nature. New technologies are invented and old technologies improved to provide progression in
these areas. Mining in space will help this by providing materials that are not very abundant on Earth,

The next measurement site is from the orbit of Earth. By placing telescopes in orbit around the Earth,
it is still possible to construct them at a decent size as they do not require a large propulsion system,
only a minor for orbital station keeping due to atmospheric drag. It is also possible to conduct minor
maintenance or reparations on them, as was done on the Hubble Space Telescope when it turned out
that the primary mirror of the telescope had the wrong shape.[10] By placing instruments in orbit, most
of the atmospheric disturbances are avoided, depending on the altitude.
To get a high resolution of small objects that are far away from the Earth, it is necessary to travel to
the object and conduct measurements at a close range. As many subsystems are required for the trans-
portation of the instruments to the targets, and for sending the results back to Earth for processing, the
measurement instruments can not be very large. It is also impossible to conduct maintenance or repairs
on instruments far away, so the mission lifetimes are short and the risk of failure are greater. But the
results are a great deal more precise compared to those obtainable from Earth, or the orbit around Earth.
For composition analysis, the most precise results are achieved when the minerals are measured directly,
and not remotely from a distance. This can be done, either by landing rovers on the surface or by collect-
ing surface material with a spacecraft in orbit. The material can either be analysed in situ, or transported
back to Earth for analysis. Landing on extraterrestrial objects is not easy, the risk of failure is large, and
it is also here not possible to perform maintenance or repair instruments. In situ measurements gives the
most precise results, and gives the opportunity to research more than only the surface of the object.
This section will describe some of the most common measurement methods for analysing extraterres-
trial objects. The main principles of the measurement methods are the same regardless of the location
of the instrument, but instrument will certainly have to be optimized for the location to ensure the best
possible results.
3.1.1
Reﬂection spectroscopy
Spectroscopy is when electromagnetic radiation, commonly known as light, is split up into the diﬀerent
wavelengths it is composed of, and the intensity of each wavelength is measured. As the elements each
absorb various wavelengths, it is possible to determine the composition by looking at the spectrum of an
object. Light sources also have diﬀerent emissions which can be determined from the spectrum.
The ﬁrst spectrometers were constructed using a prism to split the light, but in spectrometers today, a
diﬀraction grating is used instead as it gives a higher precision and lower losses. As a detector, most
commonly a charged coupled device is used, as it provides a high resolution and sensitivity, but other
detectors are used also. In order to focus the electromagnetic radiation, lenses and mirrors are used,
placed in a conﬁguration to minimize losses, and to get the optimal measurements, with consideration to
the mission design and goals.
The ﬁrst part met on a spectrometer is the telescope, which has the function of capturing as much
light as possible from the target. There exist many diﬀerent conﬁgurations, but only the Shafer conﬁgu-
ration will be discussed.
Shafer conﬁguration telescope
D. Shafer proposed a simple telescope design with a high level of stray-light rejection, consisting only of

three elements. The elements should be placed and designed such that no spherical aberration, coma,
astigmatism or distortion is present. The spherical aberration can be corrected for by aspherizing, which
is polishing the front or back surface of a lens, which when used also corrects for coma if it is bend in
a speciﬁc shape. By placing the lens on a stop, it is possible to get a design with no distortion. The
astigmatism along with the Petzval curvature or the lens can be cancelled out by using the right amount
of curvature on a small convex mirror in the system. The third element should be a parabolic mirror
which reﬂects the incoming light after it had passed through an aperture stop, ﬁrst onto a the small
mirror which should be placed at the ﬁeld stop and then through the lens, which should be placed at the
Lyot stop.[11]
After being focused by the telescope, the light enters the spectrometer. As there are many telescope
conﬁgurations, there are also many conﬁgurations of spectrometers, but only the Oﬀner conﬁguration
will be described.
Oﬀner spectrometer
The Oﬀner spectrometer design was proposed to achieve a high resolution of measurements covering a
large area, using a simple instrument design. The design contains two concave mirrors and a convex grat-
ing for diﬀracting the incoming photons, where all three elements are concentric, meaning that the center
of the concave and convex spheres are the same point. The advantage of this layout is that, due to the
rotational symmetry there is no axial aberration, no sagittal ﬁeld curvature and no coma. The ﬁrst, axial
aberration, occurs when diﬀerent wavelengths are focused at diﬀerent distances from the lens causing a
focus shift. The second, sagittal ﬁeld curvature, means a curvature of the ﬁeld in the longitudinal plane.
The third, coma, is the aberration of oﬀ-axis objects due to lens imperfections.[12]
Besides the importance of getting the correct focus from the mirrors and lenses in the spectrometer,
the diﬀraction grating is one of the most important components.
Gratings
A grating is used to split up the incoming electromagnetic radiation into diﬀerent wavelengths. It is made
by carving or placing parallel grooves on the surface of a substrate, and then coating it with a reﬂecting
material. When the light hits a reﬂective surface at an incident angle, it is reﬂected at another angle
depending on the wavelength. Depending on the angle, depth and spacing of the grooves, the grating can
be optimized for diﬀerent wavelengths. In spectrometers with a large wavelength range, it is sometimes
necessary to divide the grating into diﬀerent zones having diﬀerent grating properties, to support as large
a wavelength range as possible. The resolution of the grating will be best for the one wavelength it is
designed for, and then worsen as the wavelengths diverges from this. [13]
The last part of the spectrometer is the detector.

3.1.2
Optical measurements
Optical measurements of extraterrestrial objects are conducted using a camera with the same operating
principles as those used for taking pictures on Earth. The main diﬀerence between the cameras used on
Earth and in space, are the conditions which they have to work under. Cameras were designed to work
under humane temperatures, but in space, the temperature varies greatly due to the lack of atmosphere
keeping it consistent. When there is no atmosphere shielding the equipment, it is also exposed to a large
amount of radiation. The radiation can interfere with the electronics and cause failures, so no single
point of failures should be present, and a radiation shield should be applied. Another major diﬀerence is
the requirements regarding noise and sensitivity. Often the cameras in space take high resolution images
of faint objects very far away, so low noise and a high sensitivity in regards to light intake is crucial.
A camera consists of two main parts; the image sensor and front end electronics.[15]
The image sensor is placed after the front end electronics, and records the image.
Image sensor
In cameras, the image sensor takes the light received by the front end electronics and converts it into a
pixel value. This is most often done using a CCD, which was described in section 3.1.1. The wavelength
range used in a camera is usually from 300 to 1100 nm, and the semiconductor material used in the sensor
to register the photons is most often silicon.
Even though the sensor principle of the camera is the same as that of the reﬂection spectrometer, the
front end electronics diﬀer, meaning the input to the image sensor is also diﬀerent.
Front-end electronics
The front-end electronics has many functions, where the main include the controlling, clocking and sup-
ply for the image sensor, along with converting the output from the sensor to digital values that can
be transmitted through the communications system, and managing the communications from external
systems to the sensor.
Before the photons passes through the front-end electronics and into the image sensor, it is focused
using a lens.
Camera objective
When designing the objective for a camera to be used in space, it is of highest importance to have a low
distortion to avoid the aberrations of rays, and a high contrast so very faint objects can be recorded.
Where spectrometers uses a combination of refractive and reﬂective systems, cameras only use refractive
systems, meaning that they only contains lenses that refract the photons, and no mirrors that reﬂects it.

3.1.3
In situ measurements of samples
Instruments placed directly at the surface opens up for other measurement methods than those used for
remote sensing. The measurement methods in situ on an extraterrestrial object resembles those used for
composition analysis on Earth. The instruments will however need to be suited for landing on an object in
space. They will have to be more compact than those on Earth, as the mass is limited. They must be more
resistant as they have to land on the surface of an object in space, and not fail under the large temperature
diﬀerences present on an extraterrestrial object without an atmosphere. It is also required that they are
autonomous, as they are not operated directly by a human. Communication with the instrument will only
be possible through a telecommunications system, and it is not possible to repair or perform maintenance.
Of the instruments used for composition analysis on Earth, most widely used is mass spectrometry.
It is used to determine the composition of meteorites, which are fragments of rocks from space.
Mass spectrometry
Mass spectrometry separates ions depending on their mass to charge ratio. The ions are formed by a
bombardment of electrons and accelerated by an electric ﬁeld. They are then further deﬂected by another
electric ﬁeld, before being deﬂected by a magnetic ﬁeld, and then again by yet an electric ﬁeld. The ions
then enter a detector. By varying the diﬀerent magnetic and electric ﬁelds, ions of diﬀerent mass enter
the detector, and the ion composition can hence be determined. Samples that are not volatile will have
to be dissolved or heated in order to pass through the mass spectrometer.[17]
If a sample is available, it is also possible to use gas chromatography to determine the composition.
Just as mass spectrometry, the gas chromatography is a destructive measurement method, but for a
lander situated on an extraterrestrial object with a very large sample reservoir, this is of no concern.
Gas chromatography
The technique of gas chromatography is widely used in composition analysis on the Earth. By injecting
a volatile material into the chromatograph, the material will be split up into its diﬀerent compositions,
that then will arrive at diﬀerent time-steps at a detector depending on their boiling point and particle
size.[18]
The composition can also be determined, using images of the minerals. The images will however have to
be at a very high resolution, and of a small sample, so it is necessary to use microscopy.
Optical microscopy
When minerals are analysed using optical microscopy a small and ﬂat sample is inserted into the micro-
scope, thin enough for light to pass through it from below, where a artiﬁcial light source is used. The
mineral and its formation can then be determined by looking at the grain size and color.[19]

3.1.4
Other measurement methods
Besides the main measurement methods mentioned, there exist a few minor methods. Some of the meth-
ods mentioned has already been used on spacecrafts for composition analysis of extraterrestrial object,
while others are just conceptual ideas of instruments that might work.
Eddy current measurements
It might be possible to use the principle behind the metal detector to determine the composition of an
extraterrestrial object6. As the solar wind hits an object in space it can induce a secondary magnetic
ﬁeld, as the solar wind naturally alternates and hence works as a primary magnetic ﬁeld. The secondary
magnetic ﬁeld is created by eddy currents near the surface of the object, and will have a diﬀerent fre-
quency depending on the material, as some materials are easier magnetized than others. So by analysing
the frequencies of the measured secondary magnetic ﬁeld, the composition of an object can be determined.
Radiation spectroscopy
There are several types of radiation and for this reason, also several types of radiation spectroscopy.
Gamma ray spectroscopy measures the gamma rays emitted by minerals, when they are exposed to cos-
mic rays. The emission lines in the spectrum help determine the kind of elements that are responsible
for the emission, and hence the composition. The intensity of the lines shows the concentration of the
diﬀerent elements. Neutron spectroscopy measures the neutrons that are released when the cosmic rays
hit. The neutron detector can also tell whether water is present, as it can register the hydrogen, which
is present in the water. [20]
Radar measurements
A planetary radar can be used for composition determination of an extraterrestrial object, by sending out
a signal of radio frequency and analyse the returning signal. The waves will react diﬀerently depending
on the material and the shape that it hits, that is whether the object is solid or covered in regolith. [21]

3.2.2
Rosetta
The Rosetta mission, although delayed a year which required a change of target to the comet 67P/Churyumov-
Gerasimenko, was launched in 2004, with the objective of studying the early life of the solar system. As

what existed before it.[23]
It took the spacecraft 10 years to reach comet 67P, conducting two ﬂybys of asteroids on the way. Upon
arriving at the comet, the Rosetta spacecraft mapped the surface, and the search for a landing site for the
Philae lander began. About four months after arriving at the comet, Philae was deployed, but bounced
of the surface as it failed to get a hold of it. It ended at rest in the shade, unable to charge its solar
panels and acquire power, hence only a small amount of data was collected in the 3 hours of battery time
the lander possessed.

Hayabusa-2
Hayabusa-2 targets the asteroid 162173 Ryugu (also known as 1999 JU3), and was launched in December

also has the ability of capturing gases.

3.3
Selection of mission for in depth analysis
Within the ﬁve missions to extraterrestrial objects brieﬂy analysed in section 3.2 one is selected for a
more in depth analysis. To analyse a mission in depth, it is necessary to have access to data from the
missions. It has not been possible to acquire open source data ﬁles from the NASA missions OSIRIS-REx
or NEAR-Shoemaker, so these missions are not selected. The JAXA mission Hayabusa-2 is still ongoing,
and even though it is a highly interesting mission, many of the measurements will not be conducted in
due time to include them in the thesis.
Both Rosetta and Dawn provide the raw data ﬁles, along with reports following data processing. Rosetta
did however not accomplish all the mission goals, as the Philae lander failed to grab ahold of the surface
and instead bounced oﬀtwice. The surface samples are therefore incomplete. Dawn completed all of
its mission goals, and even had an extension of the mission, providing a large amount of data. It was
considered to travel to another object in the asteroid belt during the extension, but it was decided that
the spacecraft should remain around Ceres. The data acquired at Ceres from Dawn is hence extended.
Dawn was also launched later than Rosetta, allowing for technological advancements along with more
technological knowledge, with many of the instruments on-board being improved Rosetta instruments.
But Dawn still does not have any lander data, as a lander was not incorporated in the mission.
Looking at the target objects of the two missions, Rosetta targeted a comet, where Dawn targeted as-
teroids7. Comets mainly contain ice and dust, where asteroids, depending on the type, contain diﬀerent
types of metals, and also some water-ice. From a mining perspective it would make the most sense to go

3.4.1
The VIR spectrometer on board Dawn
The Visible and Infrared (VIR) spectrometer on board Dawn was brieﬂy described in section 3.2.3, which
mentioned that the spectrometer was based on the VIRTIS from the Rosetta mission, launched a few
years prior. This kind of visible and near infrared spectroscopy is the most common way to determine the
minerals situated at the surface of an asteroid, as the minerals most common to asteroids has absorption
bands in these regions. The spectrometer hence combines two data channels in one instrument, containing
the visible channel and the near infrared channel, covering 0.25 to 1.05 microns, and 1.0 to 5.0 microns
respectively. Especially minerals containing iron, but also water and hydroxyl produce distinct absorption
bands, so the method may reveal whether aqueous alteration has been taking place in the asteroid.

The VIR spectrometer consists of an optical head (shown on ﬁgure 6), and two electronics boxes housed

Target Analysis
This chapter is dedicated to the selection of a target for a prospecting mission. To select a target, all
possible targets must ﬁrst be brieﬂy analysed before it is possible to narrow down the possible targets to a

4.1
Possible target groups
The list of possible targets is endless, however some targets may be more convenient than others, either
due to their location, or physical factors such as size and composition. The most apparent targets for
a prospecting mining mission will be addressed in this section, along with a brief mentioning of other,
however not as convenient, targets in the solar system.
The objects most accessible from Earth are
analysed in depth. These include comets, moons and asteroids. They all have a decent size and are
believed to consist of valuable materials.
4.1.1
Comets
Comets are objects formed in the cold regions of the solar nebula, and are believed to be stored in two
reservoirs, namely the Kuiper belt and the Oort cloud, which surround our solar system and extends out
to 5 × 104 AU. Comets can be distinguished from asteroids as comets are active objects. This activity
is due to the composition of comets as they consist of minerals and volatiles, which are compounds that
has a low boiling point. When the comets exit the reservoirs and move into an orbit closer to the Sun,
the materials heat up and a coma is created consisting of gas, dust and rocks. The coma can reach a
size of up to 106 km. The gas that is escaping the comet becomes ionized due to the solar wind, and will
hence always be observed as facing away from the Sun.
Comets can be split up into two groups based on their orbit periods, where long period comets have periods
longer than 200 years, and short period comets have periods shorter than 200 years. It is suspected that
all short period comets origin from the Kuiper belt reservoir.
If a comet is orbiting close to the Sun and is active, it will be short lived. This may be due to its orbit
being unstable meaning that it can be altered by the gravity of planets so it passes even closer to the Sun
and lose even more mass during its active time close to the Sun. It may also be from fragmenting where
the comet splits up into smaller pieces. The fragmentation can happen because of tidal forces from nearby
planets, pulling the comet apart, but most incidents happen far away from the Sun where these forces are
small. Other reasons for the fragmentation include a change in the spin rate of the comet, internal gas
pressure, thermal stress and impact with other objects in space. Besides fragmentation, which can end
up as the comet only being dust particles, it can also sublime, where the compounds transition straight
from the solid to the gas phase. The last way a comet can end its life is by becoming dormant, after it
has lost all its volatiles, and becomes asteroid-like.
Besides having a coma when the comet is active, it also contains a crust showing little to no activity,

that covers most of the surface of the comet, only leaving small exposed areas where volatile compounds
may exit. From these regions, gas can be seen leaving the comet through jets. The outbursts may create
pits in the surface of various sizes, with steep walls and ﬂat ﬂoors. It is assumed that active comets lose
a quarter of their radius during their active time.
As mentioned previously, comets are composed of minerals and volatile compounds. One of the major
components is water ice, which dominates the activity of the comet. Besides water ice, the activity is
also due to the volatile compounds such as CO, which is super volatile and can cause activity far from
the Sun, and argon. These compounds can be trapped in the water ice if it occurs in an amorphous form,
meaning it does not have a crystal structure. The mineral composition of comets has been observed
to be fairly identical in all comets, unlike in asteroids where many diﬀerent minerals exists. A major
component is silicates, which shows as a feature in the spectrum at 10 µm, and present is also Olivine
as a feature at 11.2 µm. Chondrule-like fragments has been observed, containing either small or large
amounts of iron. To form, these minerals need very large temperature, meaning the comets containing
this must have formed close to the Sun, and then been transported outwards. Magnetite has been found
too, along with other similar minerals, which shows that aqueous alteration at low temperatures has been
present back in time. On some comets, organic material has been found, leading some people to believe
that life was brought to the Earth by the comets. Some also believe that the comets brought water to
Earth.[38]
4.1.2
Moons
A moon is deﬁned as a natural satellite of a planet, where the most well known is the natural satellite of
the Earth, which is obviously called the Moon.
The Moon orbits the Earth at an average distance of around 384,400 km, and has a radius of 1738 km.
Despite its large radius, compared to the Earth, it only has a mass 0.012 times that of Earth. Despite its
low pressure in its interior, the Moon still has igneous processes occurring. Samples returned from the
Moon by the Apollo missions among others, show that most of the craters on the surface is at least 3.9
billion years old. The Moon consists of a core, a mantle and a crust, just like the Earth. The upper crust is
a layer of loose and highly porous regolith which is believed to have a well deﬁned lower boundary 5-10 m
below the surface where the soil becomes larger rocks. As mentioned, the surface contains many craters,
where the impacting events has broken up these larger rocks, and caused melting due to the heat created
at the impact which has produced new rocks out of the formerly smaller ones. The Moon contains many
valuable elements that are rare on Earth but plays a key part in modern technology, including iridium,
samarium and platinum. It also contains radioactive isotopes of elements such as uranium and thorium
which can be used as energy sources. Signs of water has also been found, as there are clear implications
that the interior of the Moon contains water just as the mantle of the Earth.[39]
The most well known moons belonging to other planets than Earth are probably those of Jupiter. They
are however very far away, and hence mining them would be a costly aﬀair.
Luckily our closest neighbor Mars, contains two natural satellites, named Phobos and Deimos. Compared
to the Moon, the satellites of Mars are very small, having diameters of only 22.2 km and 12.6 km on
average, and also very light, with densities of approximately 1.85 g/cm2 and 1.48 g/cm2, all respectively.
From the low densities, it is clear that neither of the moons are dominated by metals or contain a core
of high density, like the Moon, and must hence be made of porous materials. From missions studying

4.1.3
Asteroids
Asteroids can be described as remaining planetesimals in the form of rocky or metallic bodies that are
scattered all over the inner solar system, and orbits the Sun. The most well known location of asteroids
are in the main asteroid belt located between Mars and Jupiter, where it is estimated that between 1.1
and 1.9 million asteroids above the size of 1 km in diameter, along with millions smaller asteroids are
located. Most of the asteroids in the main belt has a semimajor axis of between 2.1 and 3.3 AU. Another
well known group of asteroids is known as the near-Earth asteroids, which, as the name implies, are the
asteroids which have orbits that come close to that of the Earth. They range in size from a few meters
in diameters, to the largest being around 32 km in diameter. Two thirds of the near-Earth asteroids
observed so far has been classiﬁed as S- or Q-types, meaning objects containing silicates dominates the
population in this area. However almost all classes are represented in the near-Earth asteroids.
Because asteroids are such a diverse group of objects, it is hard to make a general description of them.
They can range in size from a few meters up to 530 km, being the diameter of 4 Vesta which is situated in
the main asteroid belt, and one of the targets of the Dawn mission (see section 3.2.3). Their composition
varies greatly as well, but it has been observed that the location of the asteroids in the solar system plays
a part in this, where for example, asteroids containing hydrated silicates decrease as the distance to the
Sun increases, and hence the amount of asteroids which has undergone aqueous altering also decreases
with distance. [52]

4.3.2
Asteroid mineralogy
The features mentioned for the diﬀerent types in table 2 corresponds to diﬀerent mineralogies and com-
positions of the asteroids. For measurements taken at Earth, there are however great insecurities linked
to the composition analysis. Furthermore, because of the asteroids being very far away, the analysis can
only be conducted over the asteroid as a whole, and it is not possible to map where on the asteroids the
diﬀerent minerals are located due to the low resolution. Other features such as the derived density and
spin rate is also considered in the analysis as the spectrum only shows what is located on the surface.
The albedo is also of great help in the determination.
The most common minerals are olivine and pyroxene, hence these will be discussed ﬁrst.[52]
Pyroxene
Pyroxene is a group of silicates having a variable composition. Common for them all is that they all
contain O6 and most commonly Si2, but the silicon can be replaced by aluminum. The mineral appear
on the spectrum as features at 1 and 2 µm.[49]
Asteroids of the O-type shows some features similar to those of pyroxenes of the diopside-hedenbergite
types, which contains a large amount of calcium, and has the compositions CaMgSi2O6 and CaFe2+Si2O6
respectively. R-type asteroids may contain partially melted pyroxene, along with olivine. S-types contain
a mix of pyroxene and olivine. Naturally, the Sr-type contains pyroxenes with a high or low calcium
amount. The Sv-type asteroids is believed to contain pyroxene also due to their strong 2µm feature.
Pyroxene is also common in V-type asteroids, containing calcium, iron or even chromium. E-types have
been shown to contain the pyroxene enstatite which has the composition Mg2Si2O6 and a whitish ap-
pearance. They have also showed weak bands around 0.9 and 1.8 µm which is believed to be due to
low-FeO pyroxene.
Olivine
Olivine is a group of silicates containing magnesium, iron or calcium, that is easily modiﬁed by weather-
ing and high temperatures. It shows on the spectrum as very strong UV features and moderately deep
absorption bands at 1 µm.[50]
The mineral is common in A-type asteroids, in the form of fayalite which has the composition Fe2SiO4.
It has also been detected in some K-type asteroids in the form of forsterite, having the composition
Mg2SiO4. Olivine may also be present in S-type asteroids along with pyroxene, where the dominating

Hydrated silicates
Hydrated silicates are recognized due to a feature at 3 µm. There is evidence of their presence on the
surface of some B-type asteroids. Also C-type asteroids have a feature at 3 µm meant to be due to the
structural OH in the hydrated silicates. In some igneous E-type asteroids hydrated silicates are surpris-
ingly present, as they are not expected to have undergone aqueous alteration. The 3 µ feature is present
in many M-type asteroids, which is odd as this is not consistent with the belief that they were mainly
consisting of metallic iron-dominated minerals.
Organic matter
Some asteroid types shows signs of containing organic matter. The P-type asteroids have spectral prop-
erties matching with organics, as they would usually darken the surface of an object. The same has been
observed in T-type asteroids and in D-type asteroids.
Some scientists argue that the organic matter on Earth may have been brought by asteroids, but the
evidence is scarce.
Water ice
Some types show signs of a feature at 3.1 µm which is due to water ice. This is known, as the same feature
has been measured on comets bearing water ice. Water ice has been found in P-type and C-type asteroids.
Other minerals
Weak absorptions around 0.7-0.65 µm and 0.8-0.9 µm are present in D-type asteroids, and are due to
iron oxides or sulfates. In B-types a broad band at 1 µm is believed to be magnetite. Spinel is a mineral
found only in L-type asteroids, in form of iron oxide bearing aluminous spinel which has the composition
FeAl2O4. The spectrum has steep UV features before it levels out after 0.75 µm. Small absorption
features can be present around 2 µm.[51]
4.4
Target selection parameters
When selecting the target for mining, ﬁve important parameters should be taken into consideration.
These include the composition, size, orbit, spin rate and change in velocity, also known as ∆v. Criteria
for the parameters can be set up to ensure that the most proﬁtable target is selected. [54]
Composition
The ﬁrst criterion in selecting a target, is choosing one with a relevant composition, as there is no reason
to go and further analyse a target which has already shown to be uninteresting. It will not be possible
to determine the exact composition of the asteroid, but by looking at the spectrum of the asteroid and
comparing the features to those presented in table 2, the asteroid type can be determined, and along
with this an approximate possible composition, using the information presented in section 4.3.2 about
the mineralogy of the diﬀerent types.

==PAGE:35==

Size
The second parameter to look into is the size of the asteroid. As it was mentioned in section 4.1.3 the
near-Earth asteroids range in sizes from a few meters in diameter up to the largest being 433 Eros with
a diameter of 32 km. The larger the asteroid, the more material it is possible to recover from it, but
with the larger size comes also a larger gravitational pull of the object resulting in the requirement of
more fuel and a greater launcher for take-oﬀ. A small asteroid contains too little material for it to be
proﬁtable, and will also be diﬃcult to land on if the mining mission requires that. A size in between the
two should therefore be searched for.
Orbit
The orbit of the asteroid is very important as this determines whether it is possible to reach it, and how
much fuel and time it will require to do so. The most crucial factor in the orbit is the inclination as this
should be very close to zero. Otherwise the window where the asteroid is reachable will be too narrow.
The orbit should also be shaped so that it comes by Earth often, and spends a long time close to Earth.
Looking at ﬁgure 9 the seemingly most approachable group of asteroids is the Apollo group, but the
other groups should not be left out. Factors such as where on the orbit the asteroid is compared to the
Earth, plays a large part since it does not matter if the asteroid is crossing, or is very close to the orbit
of Earth, if the Earth at that time is on the other side of the Sun compared to the asteroid.
Spin rate
If an asteroid is spinning too fast, it will be impossible to land on. An asteroid may spin if a collision
occurs. Due to the vacuum there is barely anything slowing down the spin of the asteroid so the spin rate
will decay very slowly. Most large asteroids does not spin very fast, as they usually collide with smaller
objects which does not aﬀect their spin rate as much. The spin rate of an asteroid can be determined
using light curve observations.
Change in velocity
The ∆v parameter describes the change in velocity required by a spacecraft to reach the asteroid. The
larger this parameter is, the more fuel and larger spacecraft is needed. The parameter can be found using
the Tsiolkovsky rocket equation shown as equation 4.2.
∆v = ve ln

162173 Ryugu lives up to the criteria set in section 4.4. First of all, it is a body in the solar system and

inclination of 5.88◦which is acceptable. The perihelion was mentioned earlier to be 0.963 AU and it has
an aphelion of 1.416 AU. It has an orbit that intersects with that of Earth, which places it in the Apollo
group. It has a very close passing with Earth of only 0.000638 AU, that moreover places it in the group
of potentially hazardous asteroids. 162173 Ryugu is classiﬁed as a C-type asteroid, from both remote
observations and from the observations made close by, by the orbiter. It takes the asteroid 7.63 hours to
complete a rotation, so the low spin rate makes it easy to land on. The required velocity change to get

5.1
Scan analysis
In order to ﬁgure out where the spacecraft scans on the surface of 1 Ceres, it is necessary to obtain the
spacecraft coordinates, and the orientation of the spacecraft.
The coordinates for the orbit is found in the database of The Navigation and Ancillary Information
Facility (NAIF), as SPICE kernel ﬁles provided by NASA.11 The coordinates are given in km, with the
orbit center, being the center of Ceres.
It is not possible to assume that the VIR instrument points towards the center of Ceres at all times, and
it has to be corrected for. The SPICE kernels for the orientation of the instrument, must be used to
determine where on Ceres the instrument is measuring.
5.1.1
Surface model of Ceres
A surface model of 1 Ceres is found from source [57], measured by the Framing Camera (FC2) during
the high altitude mapping orbit. It has its center in (x,y,z)=(0,0,0) and contains 98 306 points. The
coordinates are given in kilometers. This means that they are fully compatible with those obtained from
NAIF, as the units and center are the same. Figure 15 shows the surface, plotted in MATLAB.
To be able to determine the craters and hills on a 3D ﬁgure, greyscale colors must be assigned to each
point, as this is not included in the downloaded model. To make a rough model, 10 diﬀerent shades of
grey have been chosen, and a code generated so that the points closer to the center (0,0,0), are darker
than those further away. This means that the craters will occur darker than the hills.
As 1 Ceres is not a perfect sphere, but has been ﬂattened a bit, making the code this way, results in the
poles being darker than at equator, as they are simply closer to the center. The poles are hence not a
big crater, but the dark colors are merely a result of the simplicity in the code.

6.1
Design of a simple proof of concept Oﬀner spectrometer
An Oﬀner spectrometer consists of two concave mirrors and a convex grating.
The light enters the
spectrometer through a slit, gets reﬂected by the ﬁrst concave mirror onto the convex grating.
The
convex grating diﬀracts the light which is then reﬂected by the second concave mirror onto a sensor.
Figure 28 shows a sketch of the conﬁguration. The dot-dashed line shows the reference ray. The grooves
of the convex grating are parallel to the y-axis.
Figure 28: The conﬁguration of an Oﬀner spectrometer. The slit where the light enters is at the top left
side of the image. The detector is placed on the bottom left side of the image. [64]
By deﬁning a set of speciﬁcations for the spectrometer, it is possible to determine the physical properties
of the conﬁguration.14 The speciﬁcations are selected similar to those of the VIR spectrometer on Dawn.
The spectral range covers the wavelengths from 0.25 µm to 5.0 µm. This ranges over both visible and near
infrared wavelengths. On Dawn, the grating in the VIR spectrometer is divided into diﬀerent zones called
Rowland circles to optimize the instrument. This design will be very simple, and hence only contains one
grating, optimized to a design angle of 2.625 µm as this is the median of the spectral range.
The f-number of VIR depends on the range it covers and is 5.6 for the visible wavelengths and 3.2 for the
near infrared wavelengths. As this design is very simple, it will only use a single f-number with is chosen
as the median between the two of 4.4.
The diﬀraction order for the reference design is selected as -1, as this is the value used in the example
given in [64].
The density of the grooves in the grating ranges from 53.8 lines/mm in the infrared range and 267.9
lines/mm in the visible. A density of 200 lines/mm is selected, even though this lies closer to that of the
visible, because it seems more reasonable. This means that the grating will not diﬀract the light at the

6.2
Additional instrument requirements
The reﬂection spectrometers used on the missions today, listed in section 3.2 have large dimensions and
mass to be able to acquire measurements of a high resolution. In each of the previous missions, only a
single spacecraft is launched, so the instruments need a high performance. If several targets has to be
analysed (the mission outline will be discussed in section 7) mass and size restrictions are required to
keep launch costs down. This requires the development of reﬂection spectrometers that are even smaller
than those today. Making a compact spectrometer is diﬃcult, as numerous reﬂecting and refracting com-
ponents are required of speciﬁc sizes, placed at speciﬁc distances from each other to control the optical
path. The resolution will have to be compromised to accommodate the small physical requirements for
the instruments.
When the size of the spacecraft becomes smaller, less power becomes available. The reﬂection spec-
trometer will for this reason have to work using less power. The power consumption of the reﬂection
spectrometer is at present decently low as many of the elements are stationary and does not require
power, but the slit controlling the incoming light and the detector, has a very high power consumption.
The detector will have to either be designed as a CCD detector using less power, or a new type of detector
will have to be developed.
Since the objective of the reﬂection spectrometer is to look for certain materials, and not determine the
composition of the object it is measuring, not all wavelengths are interesting to measure. The taxonomic
group the asteroid belongs to is classiﬁed from the full spectrum, but if the asteroid is only searched for
water, the reﬂection spectrometer only need to look for the absorption features around 3 µm. It would be
more eﬃcient to develop a spectrometer only measuring around the wavelengths of interest, even if some
of these are far apart in the spectrum. Using diﬀerent zones in the grating dispersing the light already
partly does this, but it will have to be incorporated at a higher degree into the design of the optics and
detector, to meet the physical requirements of the small spacecrafts.

Mission outline
The design of the prospecting mission for mining in space, is divided into ﬁve phases, where the phases
should be executed in order, to ensure mission success. Figure 30 shows an overview with four diﬀerent
paths; red, green, blue and orange. The target is selected in the ﬁrst prospecting phases, mined and then
retrieved in the last. The scope of this Thesis is on the prospecting mission, so the last two phases will
only be brieﬂy addressed. Diﬀerent paths are available so they can be compared and the most proﬁtable
selected.
7.1
Mission elements
As mentioned, the mission is parted into ﬁve phases, as it gives a better mission overview. The phases
must be executed in the order indicated.
7.1.1
Phase 1 - remote sensing from Earth
The ﬁrst phase consists of measurements conducted from the ground and the orbit of the Earth. New
measurement instruments can be constructed, but it is also possible to use already existing instruments
made for discovering and analysing extraterrestrial objects. Using the parameters shown in the diagram
on ﬁgure 11, a group of potential targets can be selected, and phase two can begin. The group of selected
targets should not exceed 10, as the mission is prospecting, and a larger target group would make the
mission very costly.
7.1.2
Phase 2 - remote sensing in orbit around possible targets
In this phase a ﬂeet of small CubeSat16 sized spacecrafts are send to the targets selected in phase 1.
Two spacecrafts should be sent to each possible target for redundancy, faster collection of data, and
greater resolution, resulting in a ﬂeet size of 20 spacecrafts. The spacecrafts should contain a reﬂection
spectrometer, and an optical camera for conducting remote measurements of the surfaces of the possible
targets. From the remote measurements conducted by the spacecrafts, between 2 and 5 targets should
be selected for phase 3. The targets should be those that reveal the highest proﬁt, hence they should be
the easy to access and contain valuable materials. The target group will be diﬀerent depending on the
path chosen, but the diﬀerent requirements will be speciﬁed later.
7.1.3
Phase 3 - ﬁnal mining preparations
In this phase, the paths diverge. The red path should be followed if the mission is structured like that of
Deep Space Industries (see section 2.2).

The mission can be structured such that the targets are retrieved and brought closer to Earth, so that
the period of the target does not come in the way of mining. The targets should be smaller than those
of the other paths, as it would otherwise require to much power to bring them back to the Earth. Large

The targets are retrieved to Earth by the large retriever spacecrafts, and the mining of the targets has

target is close to Earth, it is possible to launch large mining spacecrafts, and as they do not require fuel
and propulsion systems to travel far, more mass can be used on the payload.
Other paths
The spacecrafts send out to mine the targets still in their original orbit can not be as large and contain
as great a payload, due to the longer distance they need to travel. They should also be fully autonomous
and not require maintenance, since this is not possible at their distance. The spacecrafts should be able
to land on the targets and mine the selected sites. Many small mining spacecrafts are hence required for
these mission paths, due to the amount of sites requiring mining at the diﬀerent targets.
7.1.5
Phase 5 - retrieving the mined materials
For the last phase the paths diverge, and converge, as there are three diﬀerent options in the retrieval
phase. The options were discussed in depth in section 2.3
Red and green paths
For the red path, the entire target is mined giving a diverse yield of valuable PGM, water-ice for fuel
and materials that can be used for construction. The PGM are of best use on Earth, where the metals in
this group are rare, but used in many electronics. The water-ice and common materials are of best use
in space for fuel production and construction of a station in space.
The same division of materials can be made of those retrieved from the targets that are still in orbit.
The share of common materials brought back from other orbits will however be small, as the retrieving
is just as expensive, as if the materials were launched into space from the Earth.

Blue path

7.2
Design drivers
For each measurement acquirer diﬀerent design drivers exist, as they operate in diﬀerent environments
and conditions, with separate goals. The measurement acquirers are described for each phase, where only
those included in the prospecting mission (the ﬁrst three phases) will be described. More design drivers
exist, but those mentioned below are the most important.
There are diﬀerent groups of failures, depending on their scope of aﬀection. Equal for all phases, is the
elimination of single point failures that can result in the entire mission being terminated.
7.2.1
Phase 1
In the ﬁrst phases, two diﬀerent instruments are used, one for acquiring measurements from the surface
of the Earth, and one for acquiring measurements from the orbit of Earth. They have diﬀerent design
drivers and will be discussed separately.
Ground based measurements
The design drivers for the measurements acquired from Earth are unique due to the easy access to the
instruments, making maintenance and reparations more straightforward, and the few system restrictions.
On Earth, the power supply is unlimited, and there are no mass restrictions, so the only physical con-
straints originate from the limits of technology. Single point failures are always a design driver, but
when maintenance and reparation is possible, it is not as great a concern. The main driver is hence the
resolution requirement. When measurements of space are acquired on Earth, the instrument has to mea-
sure through the atmosphere, which creates great distortion in the measurement results. The instrument
should be designed so this distortion is as little as possible, and it should be taking into consideration
during data processing so the remaining distortion can be corrected for.
Measurements from the orbit of Earth
When the instrument is put in the orbit of Earth, new design drivers emerge. The single point failures
become more critical, but it will still be possible to perform some maintenance if necessary, as was done
on the Hubble Space Telescope. As the instrument will have to be launched on a spacecraft, mass re-
strictions appear due to launch costs, and possibilities. The power supply is no longer unlimited, as the
spacecraft will have to generate power for the instrument, so the lower power supply drives the design of
Page 63 of 79
==PAGE:66==
Julie S. D. Petersen

the instruments and subsystems. Another design driver is the requirement of a minor propulsion system
for correcting the orientation and orbit station keeping. The propulsion system should be able to orient
the spacecraft so that, if it is designed with solar panels, produce enough power at all times, and the
instruments performing measurements of potential targets are directed towards them and not towards
Earth. Data becomes an additional design driver, since the subsystem handling it must be able to collect
and store a suﬃcient amount of data, and downlink it to Earth through the communications subsystem.
Since the instrument is no longer placed under the protection of the atmosphere in regards to radiation,
radiation shielding should be incorporated. The radiation can also aﬀect the electronics, making a driver,
where the electronics are designed for redundancy or a reset in case of failures. Keeping the spacecraft
in a low altitude orbit will still provide some protection from radiation due to the magnetic ﬁeld of the
Earth. The radiation belts should be avoided, but the thesis will not describe the orbit of the spacecrafts
around Earth further. Lastly, the temperatures in space are more extreme than on the surface of Earth,
so the spacecraft with its subsystems and instruments should be thermally shielded, and include a heating
and cooling systems where necessary.
7.2.2
Phase 2
In the second phase a ﬂeet of small CubeSat sized spacecrafts are launched and make their way to the
potential targets where they go into orbits around them to conduct remote sensing measurements at a
close distance. When the spacecrafts are far from Earth, single point failures, and the impossibility of
maintenance becomes more critical design drivers. As the ﬂeet has a size of 20 spacecrafts, the size of
a single spacecraft should be kept small, to reduce launch costs. This imposes a major design driver on
the subsystems and instruments on the spacecrafts. Each spacecraft should have a power supply able to
support the instruments and subsystems, such as propulsion, telecommunications and thermal control. If
the main power supply is solar panels, they should be oriented towards the Sun at all times to ensure power
generation, creating a driver for the orientation subsystem. The orientation subsystem should also be
able to point the instruments towards the target, and the communications system towards the Earth. The
data handling subsystem is driven by the same factors on the small ﬂeet spacecrafts as on the spacecraft
in orbit around Earth, with the addition of a more powerful and precise telecommunications subsystem
as the distance it has to downlink data, and receive uplink information is greater. The spacecrafts need
to travel a great distance which requires a propulsion system. The propulsion system should not take
up too much power and/or fuel, depending on the design selected, due to the small proportions of the
spacecrafts. The same design drivers exist in regards to thermal control, as for the satellite in orbit, but
as the spacecrafts are no longer shielded by the Earth from radiation, the design should be made more
redundant in regards to this. If many subsystems fail at once, the spacecraft should be able to enter a
safemode, which it should stay in, for as long as required, for the ground team to perform diagnostics
and communicate a solution to the spacecraft.
7.2.3
Phase 3
Phase 3 is not discussed for the red path, as it is not a part of the prospecting mission.
For the other paths, phase 3 includes sending large prospecting spacecrafts to the targets, and landing
rovers for in situ measurements of possible mining sites. As only a single spacecraft holding rovers are

==PAGE:67==
Julie S. D. Petersen

sent to each target, 2-5 spacecrafts are required. The design drivers for the spacecrafts transporting the
rovers, and the design drivers for the rovers are treated separately.
Transportation spacecrafts
Each spacecraft should hold 5 small rovers to conduct the measurements on the surface of the targets, so
they should be large, making size one of the greatest design drivers. The spacecrafts should not contain
instruments for taking measurements, so the only payload will be the rovers. The power distribution will
be diﬀerent from that of the other spacecrafts, as the payload does not require power, the telecommu-
nications subsystem only require little power, as it does not have to collect, store and downlink data to
the Earth, only diagnostics and uplink to control the spacecrafts are required and consume power. The
orientation system only has to be able to point solar panels towards the Sun to collect power, along with
pointing the telecommunications subsystem towards the Earth, as there are no instruments onboard.
The main power user is the propulsion system, which needs to be capable of transporting the rovers
to the target. The mass of the spacecraft is large, hence the propulsion system also needs to be large,
to keep down the transportation time. The requirement for radiation and thermal shielding is just as
great on this spacecraft as for the small ones in the ﬂeet, as it transports the rovers containing sensitive
electronics. The design should also be driven by avoiding single point failures, as only one transporta-
tion spacecraft travels to each target, and contains ﬁve rovers, making the consequences of failures severe.
Rovers
The rovers will be released from the transportation spacecrafts at some altitude, so a main design driver
of the rovers should be robust systems that does not break under the landing. A parachute or inﬂatable
landing padding should be considered in the design. The rovers will have to move around on a surface
of unknown roughness, so another design driver should be a driving system designed for the worst case
scenario of the surface roughness. The rovers should be able to acquire, store and downlink large amounts
of data, so the telecommunications subsystem should be able to retain this. The power subsystem should
be able to deliver power at all times of the orbit to the motion system, scientiﬁc instruments, and telecom-
munications subsystem, so a large power supply is needed. The power supply must be able to deliver
power no matter how far away from the Sun the target, and hence the rover is, to avoid time waste on
the mission. As for the spacecrafts, radiation and temperature diﬀerences should drive the design of the
shielding of the electronics. Single point failures should be avoided, and a safemode incorporated, so a
rover is not lost during operation, but can be diagnosed and mended.

In order to customize a prospecting mission for mining in space to 162173 Ryugu it is mandatory to look
into what observations are required of the target. To do this, the target selection parameters described
in section 4.4 are revisited, as these are the parameters that needs to be measured by the instruments.
In phase 1, the most promising targets are selected. An optical telescope, most often ground based,
is used to discover bodies in space. The optical telescope is also used to determine the distance to the
object, using the parallax, the orbit and the size of the object. From the orbit and distance to the object,

162173 Ryugu has an orbit that stays close to the Sun at all times, and it was simple to classify it as a near

from the orbit of Earth, as some wavelengths are attenuated by the atmosphere of Earth. The spectrom-

8.3
Will mining in space be a part of the future?
The short answer to this is yes. But it is highly unlikely that it will be within the near future.
The reason, is that mankind does not need it yet. Some of the most groundbreaking technologies have
been invented in times of war and crisis where they have been necessary for survival. Research is con-
ducted on the area, as was mentioned in the preliminary analysis, but not enough funding and eﬀort is
put into it since people would rather focus on current issues, instead of worrying about what may come
in the future.
As shown in the preliminary analysis, the cost of setting up asteroid mining outweighs the revenue greatly
at this point in time. Even though mining in space might help solve problems on Earth, the problems
are minor compared to the humanitarian crises, terrorist threats, trade war tensions and refugee crises
that are happening at the Earth, just to mention a few on a list that seems endless.
The fact that two of the most promising space mining companies has failed and been bought by other
companies might scare oﬀpeople from the ﬁeld, as they fear pouring money into an empty hole.
But there will come a time in the future, where it will be necessary to use the resources in space,
which is why the research on the ﬁeld needs to commence now so mankind will not be caught with its
pants down when it is already too late.
