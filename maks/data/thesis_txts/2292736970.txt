ABSTRACT 
 
Cold-formed thin-walled c-profiles are extensively used as load-bearing walls in building 
structures with relatively large building height. Researches have shown that perforation 
of the profile web provides a significant improvement in thermal performance of the 
profile compared to the solid profile, thus the use of slotted profiles is more interesting. 
However, slots in the web significantly change the transverse bending stiffness of web, 
thus influencing the local and distortional buckling behaviour of c-profiles, which leads 
to a decrease in bearing capacity. 
 
The objective of this thesis is to gain better and improved understanding of the 
distortional buckling behaviour of slotted thin-walled c-profiles subjected to axial 
loading. Moreover, the aim is to examine the significance that the slots in the web have 
on distortional buckling of the profiles.  
 
The investigation of the above mentioned objectives are carried out through analytical, 
experimental and numerical methods. The analytical calculations for solid profiles are 
made on basis of approaches presented in Eurocode 3 (EC3), whereas a new developed 
method in combination with EC3 are used for slotted c-profiles. In addition to the 
analytical predictions, short column tests are carried out to determine the bearing 
capacity and buckling modes of the profiles when subjected to axial loading. Moreover, 
bending tests are performed to determine spring stiffness of the c-profiles and to verify 
the results from the developed analytical method. Numerical linear and non-linear 
analysis is made to simulate the short column tests, which is used to investigate the 
buckling behaviour and failure shapes of the profiles.   
 
Results obtained from the three investigations show good agreement, whereas analytical 
and numerical results slightly overestimated the failure loads. The numerical and 
experimental determined failure loads are observed to be significantly dependent on 
initial geometrical imperfections. The numerical model, however, shows that the 
magnitude of geometrical imperfections is neglected.  
 
Based on results obtained from the experimental, analytical and numerical studies it is 
evident that the difference in bearing capacities of slotted and solid c-profiles is 
depending upon the buckling failure mode. In presence of local buckling, the failure loads 
of the profiles differ slightly, whereas in presence of distortional buckling, failure loads 
differ significantly. Furthermore, it is observed that the number of slots in the web has 
significant influence on the rotational spring stiffness which, leads to reduction of 
distortional buckling capacity.

SAMMENFATNING 
 
Kold-formede tyndvæggede c-profiler benyttes flittigt som bærende elementer i 
bygninger med forholdsvis stor bygningshøjde. Videnskabelige undersøgelser har vist, at 
perforering af profilkroppen giver en signifikant forbedring af den termisk ydeevne i 
forhold til den solide profil. Dette har medført, at anvendelsen af de slidsede profiler er 
blevet mere udbredt og interessant. Slidserne i kroppen har en betydelig indflydelse på 
den tværgående bøjningsstivhed, hvilket medfører en ændring af den lokale og 
distortional bulingsform af profilet og en reduktion i bæreevnen.  
 
Formålet med denne afhandling er, at opnå en general og forbedret forståelse af 
distortional buling for slidsede tyndvæggede c-profiler udsat for aksial belastning. 
Desuden ønskes indflydelsen af slidserne i profilkroppen på distortional buling 
undersøgt.  
 
De overnævnte problemstillinger bliver undersøg ved hjælp af analytiske, 
eksperimentelle og numeriske metoder. De analytiske beregning af de solide profiler er 
baseret på Eurocode 3 (EC3) anbefalinger, mens beregninger af de slidsede profiler er 
baseret på en kombination af en nyudviklet metode og EC3. Foruden de analytiske 
beregninger, udføres eksperimentelle trykforsøg af profilerne som anvendes til at 
bestemme bæreevne og bulings former. Desuden udføres bøjningstest af profilerne for at 
bestemme fjederstivheden, samt for at verificere resultaterne fra den udviklede 
analytiske metode. Trykforsøget bliver endvidere simuleret numerisk ved hjælp af en 
lineær samt ikke-lineær finite element analyse.           
    
Resultaterne fra de tre undersøgelser er i god overensstemmelse med hinanden, 
imidlertid viser de analytiske og numeriske resultater en anelse højere bæreevne. 
Bæreevnen af profiler bestemt numerisk og eksperimentelt er vist at være stærkt 
afhængige af de geometriske imperfektioner. Ud over dette, viser den numeriske model 
at størrelsesordningen af imperfektioner ikke har en betydning på bæreevnen.  
 
Baseret på resultaterne fra de eksperimentelle, analytiske og numeriske undersøgelser 
observeres at forskellen mellem bæreevnen af de slidsede og solide profiler er afhængig 
af bulingsbrudformen. Ved forekomst af lokal buling er difference mellem bæreevne af de 
solide og slidsede profilerne meget lille, hvorimod ved forekomst af distortional buling er 
forskellen signifikant. Der bliver endvidere vist, at antallet af slidser i kroppen har en 
betydelig indflydelse på rotations fjederstivheden, hvilket medfører en reduktion i 
distortional bulings kapacitet.

1. INTRODUCTION 
1.1 BACKGROUND OF RESEARCH 
 
Cold-formed thin-walled c-profiles of steel are extensively used as load-bearing 
walls in building structures with relatively large building height. Research [13] has 
shown that perforation of the stud web provides a significant improvement in 
thermal performance of the profile compared to the solid profile, thus the use of 
slotted profiles is more interesting. In the Nordic countries it is noted that the use of 
slotted profiles has increased over the previous years, which is why many steel 
production companies are now offering load-bearing systems with slotted thin-
walled profiles. However, the effect of the slotting has not been revealed fully yet. In 
Figure 1.1, the slotted profile wall system is illustrated excluding gypsum boards.   
 
 
Figure 1.1: Slotted profile wall system (THERMOnomic Afsnit 5 [7]) 
Perforation in the profile web will reduce the transverse bending stiffness of the 
web and unfortunately reduces the local buckling capacity. This leads to a decrease 
in distortional buckling capacity of the profile, which reduces the overall load 
bearing capacity. However, no codes are applicable considering the reduction of the 
web cross sectional area and the impact of it. 
 
This research thesis investigates whether it is possible to establish a simple 
analytical method to design and analyse the reduction in bending stiffness of slotted 
profiles and the impact it has on local and distortional buckling.  
 
 
==PAGE:20==
 
2 
 
1.2 OBJECTIVE OF RESEARCH 
 
The main objective of the present thesis is to gain better and improved knowledge 
of distortional and local buckling of slotted thin-walled c-profiles and to develop a 
simple calculation method that can describe distortional buckling capacity for 
slotted c-profiles.   
 
Furthermore, the aim of this thesis is to examine the bearing capacity difference 
between slotted and solid thin-walled c-profiles and to clarify the buckling failure 
mode of them.  
 
The investigations of the above mentioned objectives are carried out through 
analytical, experimental and numerical methods.  
 
The scope of the thesis is limited to compression members, thus bending behaviour 
of slotted profiles and the effect of gypsum sheathings are not investigated in this 
research.

2. LITERATURE REVIEW 
2.1 DESIGN AND RESEARCH OF SLOTTED STEEL 
PROFILE 
 
This section gives a brief overview of previous theories that have been developed to 
investigate the bearing capacity of slotted thin-walled c-profiles. The literature 
review is divided into sections related to the respective researcher. 
2.1.1 TORSTEN HÖGLUND 
 
Höglund [4] has developed a calculation method considering the slots in the slotted 
lightweight stud members. The method is based on a Swedish code for light gauge 
metal structures. The investigation includes several types of failure modes, which 
occur depending on the loading and support conditions as shown in Figure 2.1. 
 
 
Figure 2.1 Slotted stud. a) cross section, b) loading, c) slots, d) failure modes. 
Local and global buckling failures are taken into account by determining an effective 
cross section area of the members. The effective cross section area of the members 
is based on effective thickness, which is calculated in accordance to the Swedish 
codes.  
 
Experimental tests are performed and during these tests, it is noticeable that in most 
failure cases the resistance of the stud members is affected by shear deformation of 
the slotted part of the web, and the reduced transverse bending stiffness. Höglund 
[4] takes this into account by calculating an average shear modulus for the entire 
web.  
 
==PAGE:24==
 
6 
 
Furthermore, he develops an analytical method for shear strength. The analytical 
methods developed by Höglund are verified against the test results provided by the 
Norwegian Building Research Institute, The Danish Technological Institute and The 
Department of Structural Engineering, Steel Structures, Royal Institute of 
Technology in Stockholm. 
 
2.1.2 JYSTI KESTI 
 
Kesti [5] investigates the local and distortional buckling behaviour of slotted 
lightweight stud members under axial loading. He states that perforations in the 
web member reduce the local elastic buckling stress as well as the bending stiffness 
of the web, which in turn results in decreased distortional buckling strength of the 
member. A design method is developed by Kesti [5] to estimate the compression 
capacity of slotted stud members under centric loading. This method is compared to 
several analytical and numerical calculations. The method Kesti uses combines the 
stress found from the numerical method (General beam theory (GBT), finite element 
method (FEM) or finite strip method (FSM)) and uses them in the analytical method 
proposed by EC3 [1].  
 
Furthermore, he presents a calculation method which, includes the improvements 
gypsum sheathings have on distortional buckling strength. The method considered 
the lateral support by the gypsum sheathings as a spring with a given stiffness as 
shown in Figure 2.2. 
 
 
Figure 2.2: Rotational and lateral restraint given by the sheathing.   
Verification of the design method is based on short column test and gypsum board 
braced column test for web-stiffened c-section with slotted web and fixed end 
conditions as illustrated in Figure 2.3. 
 
==PAGE:25==
 
7 
 
 
Figure 2.3: Test setup for the short column test 
The short column tests are performed for web-perforated sections and for sections 
whose perforated area is cut away. The results show that the perforated web part 
gives restraint with respect to distortional buckling. Additionally, the tests show 
that failure loads of the entire sections are approximately 10% higher compared to 
the sections with flanges only (no web part) when the lips fail outwards, and 27% 
when the lips fail inwards. 
 
Gypsum board braced column tests show that the screw dense influences the 
compression capacity. The failure loads of the gypsum-sheathed studs with screw 
pitch of 200mm are at least 30% higher than if the section is without gypsum boards. 
However, Kesti concludes from the tests that the screw density does not influence 
the buckling mode or the number of buckling waves. 
 
Test results and analytically determined ultimate load predictions, according to EC3, 
are in good accordance. The best results obtained from test, compared to EC3, are 
achieved using effective widths when local buckling failure is present. For 
distortional buckling the optimal results are obtained using the effective thickness. 
 
 
2.1.3 TOM THÖYRÄ 
 
Thöyrä [6] investigates the shear- displacement and strength of slotted web through 
experimental tests and numerical calculations. He assumes that the former 
mentioned factors influence the determination of the shear capacity of the slotted 
stud. The purpose of the experimental investigation is to compare the test results 
with the results determined by the analytical method suggested by Höglund [4].  
 
The experimental test carries out the investigation of slotted studs with large and 
small slots. Thöyrä [6] observed that studs with large slots fail by flexural buckling 
of the small strips between the slots, even though global shear buckling occurs 
initially. The studs with small slots fail by global shear buckling without local 
flexural buckling of the strips.  
==PAGE:26==
 
8 
 
 
He also performed finite element analysis to assess whether or not it can be used in 
place of experimental tests to determine the ultimate shear stress and the shear 
modulus of the slotted web, which is needed in the analytical model suggested by 
Höglund.

3. ANALYTICAL CALCULATION METHOD 
FOR THIN-WALLED PROFILE 
3.1 GENERAL 
 
This section provides a theoretical background for the analytical calculation of three 
various buckling types: local, distortional and global.  
 
The following method is based on theoretical knowledge developed for solid thin-
walled cold-formed profiles. The theoretical basis for these calculations is 
represented in several international codes and guidelines. For this thesis, the basic 
knowledge is built on EC3 Part 1-3: General rules – Supplementary rules for cold-
formed members [1] and sheeting and EC3 Part 1-5: Plated structural elements [2].  
 
The individual components of cold-formed steel members are usually thin with 
respect to their widths and buckles at stress levels less than the yield point when 
subjected to compression. Common buckling modes of compressed thin-walled 
members are local, distortional, and global buckling. However, for cold-formed 
profiles, the main buckling mode is local buckling of the web, flanges or edge 
stiffeners. 
 
The three buckling modes are in the following thesis analyzed for two solid and two 
slotted thin-walled profiles with varying thickness and length. The cross sectional 
areas of the slotted and solid profiles are illustrated in Figure 3.1 and profile

3.2.1 LOCAL BUCKLING 
 
The main buckling mode of cold-formed thin-walled profiles is local buckling. The 
short buckling wavelength of the individual plate-element characterizes this mode 
and both edges remain straight in the longitudinal direction, different types of local 
buckling is shown in Figure 3.2.  
 
 
Figure 3.2: Plate-elements behaviour subjected to local buckling (Distortional Mechanics of Thin-
walled Structural Elements [3]) 
In most cases, local buckling mainly occurs in the central part of the web and flanges, 
where the deflection magnitudes of the parts decrease throughout the width due to 
the support conditions. However, local buckling does not normally result in failure 
of the section as global buckling does for a column. This is due to the fact that after 
reaching the buckling stress, the section enters a post-buckling state and continues 
to carry the load. The post-buckling stress is smaller than the yield stress and 
greater than buckling stress. Figure 3.3 illustrates how the stress distribution 
changes from being uniformly distributed in the pre-buckling state to being non-
uniform in the post-buckling state, and more concentrated near the supported edges 
until the yield strength is reached and failure occurs in the plate elements. 
 
 
Figure 3.3: Stress distribution in the plate-elements: a) Pre-buckling state; b) Intermediate post-
buckling state; c) Ultimate post-buckling state (Design of Cold-formed Steel Structures [8])

3.2.2 DISTORTIONAL BUCKLING 
 
The distortional buckling is generally governed by the rotational stiffness provided 
at the joint between web and flange. Distortional buckling of c-section is 
characterized by the rotation of each flange and lip around the junction, and 
translation of multiple elements, however, not the entire cross-section as shown in 
Figure 3.5.  
 
 
Figure 3.5: Plate-elements behaviour subjected distortional buckling (Distortional Mechanics of 
Thin-walled Structural Elements [3]) 
The geometry has a significant impact on distortional buckling stress. C-profiles 
with large web depth or wide flanges are more flexible, thus provide less rotational 
stiffness to the web-flange junction. Moreover, if the flange thickness is decreased, 
the local buckling of the web is obtained at wavelength close to the distortional 
buckling of the flange. However, edge stiffeners are beneficial against flange 
distortion and increasing the lip length leads to greater distortional buckling 
capacity. The distortional buckling may interact with local and global buckling. The 
wavelength of distortional buckling is intermediate between those of local and 
global buckling as illustrated in Figure 3.6. 
 
 
Figure 3.6: The elastic buckling mode various buckling modes as function of half-wavelengths for C-
profile column (Eurocode 3 - Design of steel structures - Part 1-3 [1])

compared to local and global buckling. Moreover, determining the exact distortion

4. BUCKLING OF SLOTTED THIN-
WALLED C-PROFILES  
4.1 GENERAL 
 
The following section describes the analytical method developed to determine the 
rotational spring stiffness of slotted c-profiles. The creation of the method is based 
on numerical analysis and virtual work principles.  The development of the method 
is made in collaboration with Jonas H. Jacobsen and Philip V. Madsen from the 
department of Civil Engineering.    
 
Studies have shown that the impact of slots in the web has significant influence on 
the transverse bending stiffness and axial stiffness of thin-walled c-sections. The 
calculation approach present in EC3 [1] do not consider perforated thin-walled 
profiles, thus investigation of new analytical method is needed to determine the 
critical buckling load of these profiles.  
 
As described in sections 3.2.1, 3.2.2 and 3.2.3 the profile stiffness influence the local, 
distortional and global buckling capacity, and the reduction of them will decrease 
the buckling capacity. In this chapter the reduction of local and global buckling 
capacity is not included. Even though, the local and global buckling of the perforated 
profiles are dependent on both the axial and transverse bending stiffness, it will not 
have much effect on buckling capacity. This is because the perforated part of the web 
is not included in the effective area of the web when determining the local and global 
buckling in accordance to the approach in EC3.  However, the reduction of the axial 
and bending stiffness has a huge impact on the distortional buckling capacity of the 
profiles. The distortional buckling capacity is significantly dependent on the 
rotational spring stiffness provided from the web element. Therefore, the method 
developed in this chapter predicts only the rotational spring stiffness as a result of 
the decrease in transverse bending stiffness of web due to the slots.    
 
The static system of the slotted web is considered in the analytical method as a 
simple supported beam loaded by a two-sided moment as illustrated in Figure 4.1. 
The beam supports represent the flanges of the c-section, and the bending moment 
corresponds to the unit load applied at the location of edge stiffener.        
 
==PAGE:42==
 
24 
 
 
Figure 4.1: Static system of the slotted web

analytical model for the profiles with the thickness of 0.7mm, it is observed that the 
difference in rotational spring stiffness for the three THR profiles are slightly, where 
highest value is found for TH145 and smallest for THR245. The same applies to the

==PAGE:65==

rotational spring stiffness between profiles are approximately within the range of 
2-3%; i.e. THR200 has 2% less spring stiffness than THR145 and so on. However, 
this is totally different for the rotational spring stiffness determined numerically. 
The rotational stiffness is decreased within the range of 8.8-21.6% for the profiles 
with 10 slots and 7.6-17.5% for the ones with 8 slots. This means that the analytical 
method predicted the rotational spring stiffness of different profiles to be much 
closer to one and other compared to numerical model, which is incorrect.  
 
Moreover, it is seen from Table 4.12 that the deviation between the two models does 
not follow any clear pattern. The negative deviation ratio indicates that the 
rotational spring stiffness determined from the analytical model is smaller than the 
one obtained from Abaqus.  
 
It is clear that the analytical method underestimates the rotational spring stiffness 
of the THR145 profiles when the thickness is smaller than 2.0mm. The rotational 
spring stiffness is approximately 9-10% underestimated compared to the numerical 
model. However, for the THR145 profile with the thickness of 2.0mm, the analytical 
method overestimates the rotational spring stiffness with 3.7%, meaning that 
analytical method is unsafe. For the remaining profiles it is observed that the 
analytical method overestimates the rotational spring stiffness of profiles with 8 and 
10 slots. In addition, it is observed that the thickness has significant impact on the 
rotational spring stiffness compared to the other parameters. The rotational spring 
stiffness of the web is approximately tripled when thickness is increased from 
0.7mm to 1mm, from 1.0mm to 1.5mm and so on.  
 
Table 4.12 also shows that the bending stiffness ratio between the slotted and solid 
web elements increases as the width of the web increases. As mentioned earlier the 
perforated part of the web remains constant even though width of the web is 
increased, meaning that the only area that varies in the web is the solid area (plate1) 
towards the edges. The increase of this area will result in increase of the entire 
bending stiffness of the web as shown in Table 4.12. However, the increase of the 
web width will lead to decrease of the rotational spring stiffness.  
 
The deviation between the analytical and numerical models is difficult to describe, 
as there are different parameters that can impact the determination of the rotational 
spring stiffness. It is difficult to tell whether the increase of the thickness or the solid 
areas towards the edges is the reason for the analytical method to become imprecise 
for the profiles. Several uncertainties in the development of analytical model can, 
somehow, explain some of the deviations between the two methods. Figure 4.22 
show that areas from plate 2, 3 and 4 overlap one and other repeatable amount of 
times throughout the model, thus the stiffness of them are counted twice. However, 
==PAGE:66==
 
48 
 
it is hard to avoid the overlapping of these areas as the plates are connected to one 
and other through the centrelines in the model. Additionally, one uncertainty can be 
related to the model geometry. The numerical model take into account entire length 
of the web element (1000mm), whereas the analytical model is based on a small 
section with the length of 49mm. Figure 4.22 shows how the analytical model is 
missing the adjacent plates (marked with red) of the web in the longitudinal 
direction compared to the FE-model, which will have an impact on the rotational 
spring stiffness.  
 
 
Figure 4.22: Missing adjacent parts in the analytical model in the longitudinal direction  
The stress distribution throughout the analytical model is assumed to be normal 
stress in plate 1, 2 and 4 and shear stress in plate 3, which is not entirely correct. 
The junction between plate 3 and 4 will have a combination of shear and normal 
stresses, which is not taken into account in this model. 
 
Even though the analytical model has the above-mentioned uncertainties, it can be 
concluded that the model gives a good estimate of the rotational spring stiffness. The 
model predicts good and conservative results for THR145 profiles with 10 slots, 
where the maximum deviation of these profiles are found to be approximately 11%. 
Moreover, this model is acceptable to be used for THR200 profiles with 10 slots even 
tough that the results obtained from the model is assumed to be unsafe. For the THR 
profiles with 8 slots the model showed relatively high deviation and it is concluded 
that a careful assessment needs to be made before using the results.

5.1 GENERAL 
 
In the following chapter the experimental tests performed for the solid and slotted 
thin-walled c-profiles is presented. The tests carried out in this thesis are: short

5.2.1 TEST ARRANGEMENT 
 
The test specimens are tested in a pinned support conditions, which is achieved by 
designing a special end support bracket for the test specimens to be placed in. In

machines. The test machines are programmed to run displacement as control mode 
with the deformation rate of 2.0mm/min, which corresponds to about 5.1 – 5.3kN

5.3 BENDING TEST 
 
This section briefly describes the test arrangement, specimens and the results 
obtained from the bending test. The bending test is carried out to determine the 
spring stiffness of the junction between the flanges and lips. The purpose of this 
experimental test is to verify the analytical calculation method determined in 
section 4.3. 
 
5.3.1 TEST ARRANGEMENT 
 
The test specimen of the bending experiment is tested in two support conditions. 
The flange is tested as a cantilever beam subjected to axial load, whereas the web 
element is tested as a simple supported beam subjected to bending moment as 
shown in Figure 5.14.

6. NUMERICAL ANALYSIS 
6.1 GENERAL 
 
The following chapter deals with the numerical investigation of thin-walled solid 
and slotted profiles buckling behaviour. The buckling mechanism of the profiles is 
investigated throughout a linear and non-linear finite element analysis. The results 
obtained from the analysis will be compared to the analytical and experimental 
result in chapter 7. 
 
The numerical analysis is performed in the commercial finite element program 
Abaqus. The analysis is carried out in two steps; the first step is a linear eigenvalues 
buckling analysis of the profiles, whereas the second is non-linear post-buckling 
analysis. In the non-linear analysis the implementation of initial geometrical 
imperfections are furthermore carried out.

in section 5.2. As it was mentioned, the eigenmodes generated from the buckling 
analysis are used to define the initial geometric imperfections in the non-linear

*Imperfection comment in Abaqus keywords editor:

imperfection are applied. The “mode number” corresponds to the eigenmode which

As previously mentioned, the method developed by Riks is used as step definition. 
The method is used to predict unstable, geometrically nonlinear collapse of the 
model. The load magnitude is used as an additional unknown to solve the system 
simultaneously for the load and displacement. The progress of Riks simulation is 
measured along the static equilibrium path in the load-displacement space, which is 
defined as the Arc-length in Abaqus.  
 
The load applied in this analysis is considered as a reference load and increases 
during the analysis. The load is considered to have a linear, proportionate growth 
and the load value in a specific time of the analysis is defined as:  
 
 
P = P0 + λ ∙(Pref −P0) 
6.5 
Where, the initial load, P0, corresponds to the dead load or initial load applied before 
the start of the analysis. The reference load, Pref, is the load applied within this step 
and the load proportionality factor, λ, is the factor that the load is multiplied with, in 
order to determine the actual load. The failure mode of the profile is obtained when 
the proportionality factor has reached maximum value. Given that the magnitude of 
the initial load in this model is zero, the failure load of the profile is defined as 
following: 
 
 
Pmax = λmax ∙Pref 
6.6

tests and analytical calculations, the shell edge load is determined as point load, 
using equation 6.7. 
 
 
F = Pmax ∙Ledge 
6.7

Table 7.1 shows that the analytical and numerical failure loads are overestimated 
compared to the ones determined experimentally. In general the analytical method, 
reveals significantly higher failure loads and the maximum deviation of 14% is 
observed for the 1mm solid profile. However, the deviation is slightly lower for 2mm 
profiles.  
 
The deviation between the analytical and experimental failure loads can be caused 
by several reasons. As mentioned in ”Trykforsøg af slidsede og solide tyndvæggede c-
profiler” [11], the axial load is transferred eccentric to the profile, due to this a 
bending moment is added to the profiles, which decreases the overall bearing 
capacity, thus leading to lower failure load. However, the contribution of the 
bending moment is not considered analytically, and might be one of the reasons to 
an overestimation of the failure loads.  
 
Furthermore, possible occurrence of warping in the machine head may have caused 
torque in the profile, which will reduce the experimental failure load. The 
appearance of torque in the profiles is not taken into account in the analytical 
calculation.   
 
In addition to the above-mentioned reasons, the initial geometrical imperfections in 
the profile might have had a significant impact on failure loads. The geometrical 
imperfections are difficult to measure and the magnitudes of them are not 
determined in this thesis. The analytical method presented in section 3.2 takes into 
account the geometrical imperfections in the profile through the reduction factor, ρ. 
==PAGE:105==
 
87 
 
However, the magnitude of imperfections (in experimental test elements) might 
have been larger, thus leading to the difference between the experimental and 
analytical failure loads presented in Table 7.1.  
 
The average dimensions of the test elements presented in Table 5.9 are used in the 
analytical calculations and any inaccuracies in the measurement could lead to 
inconsistency between failure loads.   
 
The numerical investigations show slightly better consistency with the 
experimental failure loads in relation to the analytical results. The failure loads 
simulated numerically overestimates the experimental results within the range of 
5-9%. The deviation might have been caused by several reasons. The two primary 
reasons with significant influence are the initial imperfections and boundary 
conditions. 
 
As mentioned in section 6.6 the initial geometrical imperfections are defined from 
the first buckling mode determined in the linear buckling analysis. The first buckling 
mode contains only local buckling of web, i.e. initial imperfections are only applied 
to web, which is not entirely correct. The initial imperfections in profiles have large 
impact on the failure loads, thus not implementing them in the flanges and lips will 
lead to higher numerical failure load.  
 
Additionally, the magnitude of the initial imperfections might have had an influence 
on failure loads. The magnitude of initial geometrical imperfections used in the 
numerical models is based on recommendation from EC3 [2]. To determine the 
influence of the initial imperfection magnitude on failure loads, three different 
imperfection magnitudes (b/20, b/200 and b/2000) are investigated for THR245T1 
model. 
 
==PAGE:106==
 
88 
 
 
Figure 7.1: Performance curve for THR245T1 using different magnitudes of initial imperfection   
Figure 7.1 shows insignificant difference between failure loads determined for the 
three magnitudes of initial imperfections. The negligible influence of imperfection 
magnitude is related to the small height of the investigated profiles. This is also 
observed in a study conducted by Kesti [5]. The author states that the magnitude of 
the initial imperfections has little importance on the failure loads for 1.2 m c-
profiles. However, a study made by Jacobsen [14] suggests that the failure loads of 
2.8 m c-profiles decrease significantly when the magnitude of the initial 
imperfections are increased. Based on these studies, it is evident that the initial 
geometrical imperfections mainly have an impact on profiles with a certain height. 
In conclusion, the impact of initial imperfection magnitude on failure loads is 
neglected for the investigated profiles. 
 
The second reason causing deviation between failure loads might be the boundary 
conditions. The boundary conditions applied to the non-linear numerical model are 
described in section 6.6, and contrary to the experimental investigation is fixed 
against warping. The warping in the support will, however, lead to torque in the 
profile, thus resulting in earlier failure load. This can explain the difference between 
the failure loads obtained numerically and experimentally.

8. CONCLUSION 
 
The main conclusion drawn from the current investigation is that the difference 
between failure loads of solid and slotted c-profiles are dependent upon the 
buckling failure mode. In presence of local buckling, the failure loads of the profiles 
differ approximately 0.5%, whereas failure due to distortional buckling result in a 
significant difference of around 9%. Furthermore it is concluded that the number of 
slots in the web element has a significant influence on the rotational spring stiffness 
which leads to a reduction in distortional buckling capacity. In addition to that, it is 
evident that the difference in the critical distortional buckling capacity is much 
higher between the 1mm profiles, compared to the 2mm profiles, which is 
concluded to be caused by the increase in thickness along with a decrease in slots.   
 
The analytical method developed to calculate rotational spring stiffness of the 
slotted profiles gives a good estimate with an average deviation of 12% from the 
numerical results. The method appeared to be suitable to estimate rotational spring 
stiffness of slotted c-profiles with the width of 200mm and 10 slots. However, for 
the 245mm width profiles, it can be concluded that the analytical method 
overestimates the rotational spring stiffness. In addition, this method is considered 
simple and time conserving compared to the numerical one.  
 
In the experimental investigations, it can be concluded that geometrical 
imperfections of the profiles have a significant impact on failure loads. Moreover, 
end supports are observed to influence failure loads of the profiles. The change of 
the end support improved the average failure loads with 13% and 18% for the solid 
and slotted c-profiles, respectively. Additionally, it can be concluded that 
uncertainties in the measured data and initial deflection of the c-profiles resulted in 
inaccurate spring stiffness values from the bending test. Moreover, it was observed 
that 1mm profiles failed due to buckling of the web element, whereas failure mode 
of 2mm c-profiles is caused by interaction between distortional and local buckling.       
 
Failure loads simulated numerically can be concluded to be in good accordance with 
the experimental and analytical values. Furthermore, it is observed that the initial 
geometrical imperfections have a significant impact on failure loads and failure 
shape. In addition to that, it is observed that the magnitude of imperfections have a 
neglectable influence on failure loads of the investigated 500 and 650mm profiles.

9. FUTURE RESEARCH 
 
A relevant future study should be carried out to measure the magnitude of 
imperfections in the test specimens and to investigate a way to implement them into 
the numerical model. Furthermore, the magnitude of imperfections implemented in 
the method described in EC3 [1] should be investigated and defined in order to be 
used in the numerical model.   
 
Another relevant consideration is that imperfections are only implemented to the 
web plate in the present thesis, therefore, a future study should apply the 
imperfections to the entire model (flange and lips) and investigate the resulting 
failure loads.   
 
In the experimental research, it is observed that the end supports influence the 
bearing capacity of profiles. A more efficient and improved way to transfer the load 
from the machine to the entire profile surface should be investigated. Moreover, to 
investigate the distortional buckling of 1mm thick profiles higher profiles should be 
tested. 
 
This thesis is limited to investigate c-profiles subjected to axial loading, a future 
research can be conducted to examine the overall behaviour and bearing capacity of 
c-profiles under the occurrence of combined lateral and axial loading.  
 
In addition, the impact of gypsum boards on failure load should be considered. 
Furthermore, the interaction between local and distortional buckling of the slotted 
c-profiles should be investigated more precisely.     
 
The uncertainties present in the developed analytical method to predict the 
rotational spring stiffness for the slotted c-profiles should be further investigated 
and improved to provide more accurate results. Additionally, the method should be 
investigated for different slotted c-profiles.
