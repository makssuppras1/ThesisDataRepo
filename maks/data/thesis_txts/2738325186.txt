Abstract 
This Master’s Thesis aims to 1) identify and understand the barriers and drivers of Absolute 
Environmental Sustainability Assessment in the Built Environment, and 2) develop a support 
for Danish Real Estate Developers to foster a future implementation of Absolute Environmental 
Sustainability Assessment in the BE.  These originate from the research question asking: “How 
to support a successful implementation of absolute environmental sustainability assessment 
for real estate developers in the built environment?” 
The thesis project takes its methodological offset in the Design Research Methodology (DRM) 
developed by Blessing & Chakrabarti, (2009) whereby an empirical study as well as a 
systematic literature review of both white and gray literature were carried out to identify 
barriers and drivers of Absolute Environmental Sustainability Assessment in the built 
environment. It was found that despite several benefits of applying Absolute Environmental 
Sustainability Assessment in the built environment, a variety of companies fail to implement 
the framework due to insufficient knowledge and collaboration across the industry to achieve 
Absolute Environmental Sustainability (AES). This is similarly hindered by a lack of resources 
in the Built Environment. The analysis resulted in a problem statement aiming to: “(…) increase 
real estate developers’ knowledge of absolute environmental sustainability through 
collaboration and by avoiding a resource-demanding implementation process.” Through 
collaborative workshops with real estate developers as well as the application of design 
approaches, the necessary support for the implementation of Absolute Environmental 
Sustainability Assessment in the built environment was developed, resulting in the AES 
Dialogue Tool.  A tool that assists real estate developers in facilitating an initial dialogue on 
absolute environmental sustainability during the early stages of a building project, enabling 
collaboration across the value chain to increase the collective knowledge of absolute 
environmental sustainability aspects and thereby provide the breeding ground for a future 
implementation of Absolute Environmental Sustainability Assessment in the Built 
Environment. 
While the developed tool proved to meet the requirements outlined in the performance 
specification, further testing with industry stakeholders is required to increase usability before 
it can be fully adopted by real estate developers, and thus foster a future implementation of 
absolute environmental sustainability in the built environment.

1. 
Introduction 
Over the past few decades, awareness of environmental issues at local, national, and 
international scales has steadily increased. Throughout this time, environmental issues have 
only grown within scale and urgency, increasing the need for action. The concentration of 
greenhouse gases affecting the Earth's climate has been increasing since before 
industrialization and can be explained mainly by two factors; economic growth and population 
growth (C. E. Andersen et al., 2020), indicating a strong correlation between human activities 
and increased environmental impact (L. Bindoff et al., 2013; M. Harris & Roach, 2017). Along 
with the constant increase in global population and economic growth, comes a growing 
demand for resources, which threatens to destroy the environmental stability of the current 
geological epoch, the Holocene, which is the only state that the Earth can safely fulfill the 
current human needs of society (Steffen et al., 2015).    
The construction sector plays a pivotal role in the increased environmental impacts and is a 
major consumer of natural resources. The sector which ranges from residential and 
commercial buildings to essential mobility infrastructure (Fraser et al., 2024) constitutes 37 % 
of the global operational energy and process-related CO2 emissions (UNEP, 2023), making the 
construction industry one of the largest contributors to greenhouse gas emissions. In addition, 
the conservative nature of the construction industry stands in sharp contrast to the progressive 
need for sustainability and environmental awareness. Thus, the built environment indicates a 
critical area with great potential for improvement and emission reduction, which is necessary 
to remain within the Holocene epoch. However, the value chain of the built environment is s 
characterized by its complexity and diversity of actors and processes. Thus, to drive the 
necessary change, key players with significant influence in the construction sector are 
essential. One such stakeholder is the real estate developers, whose central role in building 
projects connects multiple stakeholders across the value chain (Cramer, 2023).  
The increased awareness of the negative impact of human activities on the Earth's climate has 
led to several efforts including Planetary Boundaries defined by Rockström (2009) quantifying 
the Earth's environmental boundaries in an attempt to stay within the Holocene epoch. The 
concept of Planetary Boundaries has with its less than 20 years of existence gained ground in 
science by starting to operationalize the concept through absolute environmental 
sustainability assessment (AESA). AESA build upon life cycle assessment (LCA) and is a 
method of evaluating the environmental impacts of products or systems with an assigned 
share of the Earth's carrying capacity for various impact categories (Bjorn et al., 2020) in which 
the upper and lower limits of the planetary boundaries are often applied. However, the 
methodologies for assessing absolute environmental sustainability are relatively new and still 
==PAGE:10==
2 
 
evolving, resulting in a lack of uniformity, which is crucial when operationalizing the framework 
in the industry and utilizing the potential for reducing environmental impacts.  
 
Along with the ongoing research, and the great potential for the construction sector to reduce 
their environmental impacts, the concept of AESA has started to be adopted in building 
industry-driven initiatives. Initiatives such as the Reduction Roadmap, DGNB-Planet, 
Doughnut for Urban Development, and Science Based Targets Initiative (SBTi), each provide 
absolute environmental targets for the built environment to pursue. Despite the 
encouragement of the built environment to adopt AESA-based industry initiatives, it is 
uncertain whether companies apply the initiatives and whether AESA has been adopted within 
the industry. Understanding the barriers preventing the adoption of AESAs and AESA-based 
industry initiatives in the built environment is, therefore, crucial, to enable a more successful 
implementation and to ensure that the built environment utilizes its reduction potential to 
operate within the earth's boundaries and contributes to reducing global environmental 
impacts.  
 
1.1. 
Research goal and questions  
This master thesis aims to develop support for a successful implementation of Absolute 
Environmental Sustainability Assessment in the Built Environment (BE). The project will focus 
on real estate developers as a target audience due to their role within the built environment 
positioning them to engage other stakeholders across the value chain. Thus, this master thesis 
aims to answer the following research question (RQ0):   
 
How to support a successful implementation of absolute environmental sustainability 
assessment for real estate developers in the built environment? 
 
To support answering RQ0 as the main research question, the following sub-research questions 
(SRQ’s) are formulated: 
 
SRQ1: What does the current landscape of absolute environmental sustainability in the built 
environment look like? 
 
SRQ2: What barriers and drivers are the real estate developers facing in the application of 
absolute environmental sustainability? 
 
SRQ3: What support is required for the built environment to successfully implement absolute 
environmental sustainability assessment in their work?

2. 
Design Research Methodology 
The goal of this master’s thesis project is to develop support for the implementation and 
application of Absolute Environmental Sustainability Assessment in the Built Environment. The 
thesis is taking its methodological offset in the Design Research Methodology (DRM) developed 
by Blessing & Chakrabarti, (2009).  
The DRM was developed as a response to the lack of a rigorous approach for conducting design 
research in an effective and efficient way. The methodology is thus a systematic research 
framework and provides a stepwise approach to design research consisting of four main 
phases: Research Clariﬁcation (RC), Descriptive Study I (DS-I), Prescriptive Study (PS), and 
Descriptive Study II (DS-II). Through the completion of these phases, DRM aims to 
systematically guide the planning of research by providing a set of methods and guidelines, 
encouraging reﬂection on the applied method, and thus improving the chances of obtaining 
validated and more useful results (Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009b).  
The following section provides an overview of the methodological framework used for the 
design research alongside a detailed description of the four phases of the Design Research 
Methodology with relevance to this master thesis. 
 
2.1. 
The Methodological Framework  
A methodological framework structuring the four phases of the DRM model has been 
developed to support the design research process of this master thesis. The methodological 
framework shown in ﬁgure 1, divides the four phases of DRM into inputs and outputs 
emphasizing the requirements stated in each phase. The inputs and outputs have been 
developed in accordance with the overall research question (RQ0) and the corresponding sub-
research questions (SRQ1-SRQ3), as deﬁned in chapter 1.1. In addition, the framework 
provides a brief overview of the practices, tools and applied methods used to support the 
realization of the inputs and outputs. A detailed justiﬁcation and reasoning for the selection of 
the applied methods and tools will be presented in each phase of the methodological 
framework.

To answer the chosen research question (RQ0) and associated sub-research questions (SRQ1-
SRQ3) the type of design research approach was chosen. Thus, The Research Clariﬁcation will 
be performed as a review-based study, Descriptive Study I will be a comprehensive study,

aiming at closing the project (Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009b).  
2.1.1. 
Research Clariﬁcation (RC) 
The Research Clariﬁcation involves obtaining an initial understanding of the research objective. 
This entails identifying the areas of interest, deﬁning the research goal and focus of the 
research project as well as identifying research problems, questions, and hypothesis (Blessing 
& Chakrabarti, 2009b).  
To perform the review-based Research Clariﬁcation the following activities will be carried out:

•
A preliminary literature review exploring the current practices and theoretical
frameworks with relation to RQ0 to establish a scientiﬁc foundation for the remaining
thesis.
•
An ecosystem mapping providing an overview of the target audience’s position within

The main outcome from RC was an identiﬁcation of contributing areas of relevance to be 
included in the project, as well as an improved understanding of the current research

the rest of the project. 
2.1.2. 
Descriptive Study I (DS-I) 
The objective of the Descriptive Study I is to increase the understanding of the current situation 
gained from the Research Clariﬁcation stage. This entails clarifying in detail the factors that 
inﬂuence the existing situation while identifying the main problems and areas that are the most 
suitable to address in order to improve the current situation. The DS-I stage thus forms the 
foundation for the following PS stage, for the effective development of support (Blessing & 
Chakrabarti, 2009). 
To perform the comprehensive Descriptive Study I, the following activities will be carried out: 
•
A stakeholder survey exploring the barriers and drivers of AES in the built environment.
•
A systematic literature review, investigating drivers and barriers affecting the application
of AESA according to state-of-the-art literature as well as grey literature.
•
A feasibility study evaluating the relevance of problems as well as identifying the main
problem addressed in a problem formulation
•
A performance speciﬁcation stating the requirements and criteria for the intended
support based on the insights gained from the stakeholder survey and the systematic
literature review.
The main outcome from DS-I contributed to answering SRQ1 and SRQ2, by providing an 
increased understanding of the current landscape through an identiﬁcation of barriers and 
drivers of AESA in the built environment from an academic as well as stakeholder perspective.

2.1.3. 
Prescriptive Study (PS) 
The Prescriptive Study builds upon the results gained from DS-I and aims to deﬁne and develop 
the type of support necessary for creating a successful implementation of AESA in the built 
environment. The PS stage thus comprises a description of the actual support improving the 
existing situation, alongside, a ﬁrst evaluation of the support was conducted validating if the 
support was developed correctly (Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009).  
To perform the comprehensive Prescriptive Study, the following activities will be carried out:   
• A Task Clariﬁcation to clarify the problem to be solved and speciﬁes the requirements 
obtained from the previous DS-I stage.  
• A Conceptualisation to generate potential solutions for the intended support aiming at 
fulﬁlling the need deﬁned in the Task Clariﬁcation. 
• An Elaboration to describe the intended support and shape the concept. 
• A Realization to ﬁnalize the details of the concept, entailing a description of the core 
functionalities and contribution of the concept along with an evaluation plan for testing 
and evaluating the ﬁnal concept. 
• A Support Evaluation involving checking whether the intended support meets the 
requirements of the performance speciﬁcation to ensure that the Actual Support is 
developed to such an extent that it can be evaluated in DS-II (Blessing & Chakrabarti, 
2009b).  
The PS stage resulted in the development of the AES Dialogue Tool which contribute to answer 
the third sub-research question, SRQ3.  The support tool was developed to such an extent that 
it enabled an evaluation in DS-II. 
2.1.4. 
Descriptive Study II (DS-II) 
The Descriptive Study II is the ﬁnal stage of the Design Research Methodology and aims to 
investigate the impact of the AES Dialogue Tool and its ability to realize the desired situation 
(Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009). Thus, the DS-II focuses on the evaluation of the actual support 
developed.  
To perform the Initial Descriptive Study II, the following activities will be conducted:  
• Developing an Evaluation Plan involving selecting functionalities to be addressed and 
developing a setup and measurements to evaluate the applicability and usability of the 
actual support.  
• Undertaking Evaluation involving the facilitation of a workshop with the real estate 
developers to test and validate the actual support and collect feedback to identify 
necessary improvements to the concept.

• Drawing Conclusions based on the applicability, usability, and usefulness of the actual 
support’s ability to reach the desired situation, as well as reﬂections on the evaluation 
plan, workshop, and recommendations for future work.  
The main outcomes from DS-II were the evaluation of the actual support and its identiﬁcation 
of necessary improvements as well as recommendations for future work.

3. Research Clarification  
The Research Clarification (RC) stage aims at establishing an understanding of the contextual 
background to the current conditions for Absolute Environmental Sustainability Assessment 
(AESA) in the Built Environment (BE). By exploring the current conditions through relevant 
literature, it has been possible for the project team to clarify the scope of the study and to state 
a realistic Research Question (RQ0) supported by relevant Sub Research Questions (SRQs) 
(Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009a).  
 
Three main areas of relevance and contribution (figure 2) have been identified through a 
literature review applying desktop research and ecosystem mapping to identify the target 
audience.   
 
Figure 2: ARC-diagram (areas of relevance and contribution diagram)   
As evident in figure 2, the three overall relevant and contributing areas are the Built 
Environment (BE), Absolute Environmental Sustainability (AES) and the overlap between the 
two, with the aim of investigating how AESA can be implemented in the Built Environment. The 
following sections will elaborate on the three different areas.  
 
3.1. 
The Built Environment 
The BE represents the context of which AES is desired to be implemented and thereby 
constitutes the first half of this project. Special attention is given to scoping the project in terms 
of a target audience and investigating the external pressures and challenges that the BE is 
currently facing. This, to gain a better understanding of the current landscape that the BE is 
part of. 
==PAGE:19==
8 
 
The ‘built environment’ (BE) refers to human-made structures ranging in scale from buildings 
to cities encompassing the facilitation of living, working, and transportation activities (The Built 
Environment | UNESCO, 2024). 
3.1.1. 
The environmental pressure  
The BE is where human everyday life happens and with a continuously growing population, the 
future will inevitably have cities built at an unprecedented pace (Brooks, 2022). In fact, the BE 
constitutes for at least 37 % of the global operational energy and process-related CO2 
emissions, making the BE one of the largest contributors to greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions  
(UNEP, 2023). According to a 2019 OECD report, materials used in the construction of buildings 
represent an estimated 9% of overall energy-related CO2, and the traditional materials such as 
cement, steel, and aluminum represent the three largest sources of embodied carbon due to 
their resource-intensive and energy-demanding characteristics (UNEP, 2023). Construction 
materials and metals are even predicted to double by 2060 (UNEP, 2023) and given the 
magnitude of these impacts in combination with a traditional nature of construction activities 
and fragmented value chains, there is an urgent need for shared ambitions of change across 
the globe (Cramer, 2023). In fact, the building sector possesses the potential to globally reduce 
GHG emissions by 66% by 2050 if new ways of end use service provision are implemented 
(Calvin et al., 2023). 
Diving into the national context in which this project takes its offset, the BE in Denmark 
employed almost 200.000 people across various professions including entrepreneurs, 
manufacturers, tradesmen, developers, advisors, etc., positioning the BE as crucial to the 
country's development and economic growth (Byggeriets Handletank for Bæredygtighed, 
2024). However, looking at the territorial footprint referring to CO2-emissions directly from 
production activities such as building and machinery in Denmark, the BE constituted 23% of 
the total territorial emissions in Denmark in 2021 (Byggeriets Handletank for Bæredygtighed, 
2024) (figure 3).

From figure 4, it is evident that the use-phase of buildings represents the main contributor to 
climate change, followed by energy to manufacturing of construction materials. Thus, the use-
phase has a significant impact on the environmental performance of the BE which is a long-
known source to environmental impacts of buildings (Hasik et al., 2019). 
However, because of the nature of the BE’s approach to Life Cycle Assessments (LCA) being 
undertaken reactively to the process of constructing a building, the big energy consumption in 
the use phase overshadows energy-efficiency design approaches that could have been applied 
during the early stages of design (Hasik et al., 2019). According to Hasik et al. (2019), the 
challenge of overlooking early stages design potential lies in:  “(…) most design optimization 
efforts focus on minimizing the energy and water demand of buildings; however, the source 
type for those resources can be more influential from an environmental impact perspective” 
(Hasik et al., 2019). Thus, the BE is recommended to apply a proactive use of LCA throughout 
the design process to guide early-stage design decisions (Roberts et al., 2020), and to allocate 
more resources and time for these investigations and calculations to avoid traditional 
solutions (Byggeriets Handletank for Bæredygtighed, 2024).  
In summary, looking into the general environmental performance of the BE, it is clear how CO2 
is the primary indicator and how there is a great potential for reductions on a global as well as 
local scale. Given the conservative nature of the BE, it possesses a potential for reductions, 
which puts an environmental pressure on the BE to utilize its potential. Thus, the further 
investigation of the BE will look closer at the legislative conditions related to environmental 
responsibility. 
3.1.2. 
The legislative pressure  
With the aim of reducing global as well as local environmental impacts, legislation targeting 
the BE has become more comprehensive and prevalent. The regulation of buildings is defined 
by various authorities, which makes it challenging for the BE to navigate (Byggeriets Handletank 
for Bæredygtighed, 2024). Byggeriets Handletank for Bæredygtighed (2024), a cross-sectoral 
initiative established to provide the industry with a strategic direction for sustainable 
development, released a report stating that the regulation applied to the Danish BE even: 
”hinder sustainable initiatives rather than promoting sustainable construction (…)” (Byggeriets 
Handletank for Bæredygtighed, 2024), mainly referring to the BE as a highly regulated area 
within safety and health and environment (Executive Order on Building Regulations 2018 
(BR18), 2018). This emphasizes the challenges that the BE are facing in the navigation of a 
variety of legislations (Byggeriets Handletank for Bæredygtighed, 2024).  
Indeed, legislation in the BE is an ever-changing topic given the huge potential for greenhouse 
gas reductions which is becoming more significant as science advances. This was recently 
highlighted when the Danish government sharpened the requirements for the greenhouse gas 
emissions of new buildings (Ny Aftale Stiller Ambitiøse Klimakrav Til Nyt Byggeri - Social-, Bolig- 
==PAGE:22==
11 
 
Og Ældreministeriet, n.d.). The requirements are introduced as a part of Danmark’s National 
strategy for sustainable construction (National Strategi for Bæredygtigt Byggeri | Social- Og 
Boligstyrelsen, n.d.), referring to the implementation of EU directives, which happens in the 
wake of the Taxonomy Regulation entering into force in 2020 (EU Taxonomy for Sustainable 
Activities - European Commission, n.d.). The EU taxonomy defines criteria for economic 
activities that are aligned with the net zero trajectory by 2050, which sets out a narrow but 
feasible pathway for the global energy sector to contribute to the Paris Agreement’s goal of 
limiting the rise in global temperatures to 1.5 °C above pre-industrial levels (Net Zero Roadmap: 
IEA, n.d.). While the EU taxonomy requires companies to disclose which of their economic 
activities can be considered environmentally sustainable, a directive by Corporate 
Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD), requires companies to report on sustainability 
performances that are not related to financial activities (Relationships Between CSRD, EU 
Taxonomy, and SFDR, n.d.). From 2024 and onwards the first companies will gradually start to 
report on sustainability according to CSRD and will do so by using European Sustainability 
Reporting Standards (ESRS) (Corporate Sustainability Reporting - European Commission, n.d.). 
The ESRS provides disclosure requirements in three different areas: Social, Governance, and 
Environment. Within the environmental section, disclosure requirements are set for five 
different topics which are E1-Climate, E2-Pollution, E3-Water/Marine, E4-Biodiversity and 
Ecosystems and E5-Resource Use and Circular Economy (Europæiske Unions Tidende, 2023). 
This review provided a brief introduction and understanding of the legislation governing the BE 
from an environmental perspective and emphasizes the BE as a highly regulated area within 
safety and health and environment (Executive Order on Building Regulations 2018 (BR18), 
2018). However, the numerous legislations can even hinder innovative solutions meeting the 
necessity of urgent change (Byggeriets Handletank for Bæredygtighed, 2024). Nevertheless,

3.2. 
Absolute Environmental Sustainability  
Absolute Environmental Sustainability (AES) represents the academic area to which the 
developed support will contribute. Special attention is given to why it isn’t good enough to be 
‘just’ sustainable in a relative sense, and how the theoretical framework of Absolute 
Environmental Sustainability Assessment (AESA) can contribute to this context. 
3.2.1. 
Introduction to Absolute Environmental Sustainability 
AES draws upon Earth system science and refers to the planet's biophysical boundaries, 
known as the planetary boundaries (PBs), described by Johan Rockström in 2009. The 
planetary boundaries represent a total of nine interrelated biophysical flows that quantify the 
safe operating space (SOS) for humanity with respect to the Earth’s environmental systems 
(Rockström, 2009). The nine planetary boundaries are: climate change, ocean acidification, 
stratospheric ozone depletion, biogeochemical flows (phosphorus and nitrogen cycles), 
freshwater use, land-system change, change in biosphere integrity (biodiversity loss and 
species extinction), atmospheric aerosol loading, and introduction of novel entities 
(Richardson et al., 2023). Respecting these boundaries will allow Earth to remain in the current 
geological epoch, the Holocene, which is the only state that can safely meet the current human 
needs of society (Steffen et al., 2015). Thus, AES is a quantification of the earth's carrying 
capacity ensuring that human activities do not exceed the regenerative capacity of the planet 
(Bjorn et al., 2020). The term "absolute" in this context thereby means setting a specific 
standard or reference point. Today six of the nine planetary boundaries have been exceeded 
which are climate change, biosphere integrity, biogeochemical flows, land-system change, 
novel entities, freshwater use, and ocean acidification is approaching its limit, which is highly 
due to anthropogenic activities (figure 8). 
==PAGE:28==
17 
 
 
Figure 8: Illustrating the status of the nine planetary boundaries that make up the Earth's biophysical system. The green area 
indicates the safe operating area (below the threshold). The yellow to red zone indicates areas of increasing risk. The purple 
zone indicates the high-risk area where the Holocene state is very likely to be exceeded (Richardson et al., 2023) 
The transgressions of six PBs are critical for maintaining the stability and resilience of the Earth 
system. Nevertheless, they bring a scientific understanding of anthropogenic global 
environmental impacts into a framework that calls for considering the state of the Earth system 
as a whole (Richardson et al., 2023). To enhance the understanding of the PBs, they have been 
reviewed in terms of the importance of the earth system and the control variables indicating 
the current state of the individual PB.  
Biosphere integrity formerly known as "loss of biodiversity", refers to the global ecological 
system that includes all living organisms and their interactions (Steffen et al., 2015). Its 
thresholds are based on two control variables 1) the genetic distinction rate measured in 
E/MSY (extinctions per million species-years) and 2) the functional component of the 
biosphere integrity boundary as the human use of biosphere net primary production (NPP), 
measured in % HANPP. (Richardson et al., 2023) 
Freshwater Change reflects the change in freshwater flows that are crucial for maintaining 
biodiversity and the functions necessary to sustain ecosystem stability. Overexploitation of 
freshwater by humans can affect freshwater ecosystems (Ryberg et al., 2018). This PB 
considers the changes across the complete water cycle over land, and the streamflow is used 
as a proxy to represent blue water (surface and groundwater) and green water (plant-available 
water) (Richardson et al., 2023) as the two control variables for this PB.  
==PAGE:29==
18 
 
Climate change is one of the most established and well-defined PBs in environmental science 
and is even incorporated into the Paris Agreement. Climate change impacts the Earth’s climate 
system, resulting in changes both in the physical conditions on Earth (temperature, 
precipitation, storm activity, sea level, etc.) and in the biosphere (Ryberg et al., 2018). Its 
thresholds are based on two control variables: Atmospheric CO2 concentration (ppm CO2) and 
total anthropogenic radiative forcing (W m-2) (Richardson et al., 2023). 
Atmospheric Aerosol Loading represents airborne particles, which have multiple physical, 
biogeochemical, and biological effects on the Earth system (Richardson et al., 2023). The 
increase in aerosols affect monsoon systems (precipitation) in tropical regions and reduce the 
amount of sunlight reaching the Earth's surface (Richardson et al., 2023), which justifies its 
inclusion and importance as a planetary boundary.  A distinction is made between natural 
aerosols (soil dust, volcanic smoke, forest fires, etc.) and man-made aerosols (particles from 
air traffic, power plants, etc.), both of which contribute to polluting the atmosphere. However, 
the latter has increased drastically. The control variable for the PB is measured in 
Interhemispheric difference in aerosol optical depth (AOD), whereas AOD is an integrated 
measure of the total reduction of sunlight reaching the Earth's surface.  
Land System Change focuses on three primary forest biomes (tropical, temperate, and 
boreal) that globally have the largest impact on driving biogeophysical processes (Richardson 
et al., 2023). These act as important carbon sinks by absorbing and storing large amounts of 
atmospheric CO2 through photosynthesis as well as providers of habitat for a wide variety of 
plant and animal species (Steffen et al., 2015). The control variable of land system change is 
measured based on the remaining global forest cover as a percentage of the potential forest 
area in the Holocene, i.e. the possible forest area without human intervention (Richardson et 
al., 2023).  
Biogeochemical Flows represents anthropogenic perturbation to the global elemental cycle 
involving nitrogen (N) and phosphorus (P), as these two elements form the basic building 
blocks of life and also the control variables (Richardson et al., 2023). The global cycle has been 
greatly affected by excessive N and P emissions through agriculture and industry, causing 
eutrophication of lakes and freshwater systems (Richardson et al., 2023; Ryberg et al., 2018).  
This has profound effects on ecosystem composition and long-term effects on the Earth 
system (Richardson et al., 2023).  
Ocean Acidification is closely linked to the control variable, CO2, for the PB climate change 
(Steffen et al., 2015). The increasing concentration of CO2 in the atmosphere results in a greater 
uptake of CO2 into the oceans, forming carbonic acid and lowering the pH of the oceans. This 
acidification hinders the formation of carbonate ions in the oceans, affecting the growth and 
survival of calcifying marine animals. Over the past 200 years, the pH of the world's oceans has 
decreased by approximately 30% (Steffen et al., 2015), threatening global marine ecosystems 
==PAGE:30==
19 
 
and biodiversity. Thus, the control variable related to this PB is the carbonate ion 
concentrations in oceans (Richardson et al., 2023).  
Novel entities include anthropogenic-produced synthetic chemicals and substances such as 
microplastics, endocrine disruptors, and organic pollutants, which are released into the 
environment. This can affect the integrity and functionality of the biosphere, yet their full 
consequences are often unknown, and their use is poorly regulated. Around 80% of chemicals 
registered under the EU REACH regulation have been used for at least 10 years without 
undergoing a safety assessment. This lack of oversight and regulation means the potential for 
exposing the Earth to unforeseen and harmful effects (Richardson et al., 2023). The control 
variable for this PB is the Percentage of synthetic chemicals released to the environment 
without adequate safety testing (Richardson et al., 2023). 
Stratospheric Ozone Depletion is caused by anthropogenic halocarbon compounds. When 
released into the atmosphere, ozone molecules in the stratospheric ozone layer are depleted, 
resulting in a long-term reduction of the ozone concentration in the atmosphere (Richardson 
et al., 2023). The ozone layer is important as it protects the Earth from harmful ultraviolet-B 
(UV-B) radiation from the sun and a depletion of the ozone layer can therefore have serious 
consequences for the environment and human health (Ryberg et al., 2018). The control variable 
is measured in stratospheric O3 concentration (global average) in Dubson units (DU) 
(Richardson et al., 2023). 
3.2.2. 
Absolute sustainability in contrast to relative sustainability 
AES emphasizing the complexity of the Earth’s environmental systems is, however, not the 
primary perspective when discussing sustainability today, as the focus often is on relative 
sustainability involving a comparison of alternative products or systems to determine which 
has the best environmental performance. A well-known method to quantify the environmental 
performances of products and systems is the Life Cycle Assessment (LCA). This method 
estimates the environmental impacts of anthropogenic systems from a ‘cradle-to-grave’ 
perspective, considering all life cycle stages and impact categories (Bjorn et al., 2020). These 
LCAs are often used as decision support, aiming to support continuous sustainable 
improvements of alternative products with a particular focus on reducing environmental 
impacts. In contrast to relative sustainability assessing “what is best” from an environmental 
perspective, AES focuses on comparing whether products and systems perform "well enough" 
emphasized through a sustainability reference referring to the environmental carrying 
capacities of the Earth (Bjorn et al., 2020). Thus, a given product cannot be considered 
sustainable in an absolute sense, if it falls outside this reference as can be seen in figure 9.

Absolut Bæredygtighed | WE BUILD DENMARK, n.d.) 
3.2.3. 
Absolute Environmental Sustainability Assessment 
AESA is a subset of AES and evaluates whether a product or system is sustainable in an 
absolute sense. An AESA can be conducted by coupling LCAs with absolute measures using 
an Absolute Environmental Sustainability Reference (AESR) (C. E. Andersen et al., 2020). This 
is often referred to as an LCA-based AESA. Determining the AESR consists of multiple steps 
starting by identifying relevant environmental boundaries, often based on the planetary 
boundaries framework, quantifying the Earth’s safe operating space (SOS). The SOS is then 
allocated among all anthropogenic activities, which is usually done at three levels: country-
level, sector-level, and product-level, each expressing a share of the safe operating space 
(SoSOS). The principle for allocating these shares is defined as the ‘sharing principle’ which 
builds on various ethical norms, each of which has specific ways of dealing with fairness 
resulting in various outcomes depending on the sharing principle chosen (Bjorn et al., 2020). 
When interpreting the results of an LCA-based AESA for a product, exceeding the assigned 
share of the safe operation space (SoSOS) for one planetary boundary will result in the product 
not being defined as absolute environmentally sustainable (Ryberg et al., 2018).  
Today, various LCA-based AESA-methods have been developed as a result of the growing 
awareness of the planet's limited number of resources and finite nature (Bjorn et al., 2020). 
Two AESA-frameworks have been suggested by Bjorn et al. (2020); Planetary boundary-based 
life-cycle impact assessment (PB-LCIA) and carrying capacity-based normalization references 
(CC-NR). The first mentioned approach, PB-LCIA introduced by Ryberg et al., (2018), provides 
characterization factors (CFs) corresponding to the planetary boundaries. However, the 
planetary boundaries ‘biosphere integrity’ and ‘introduction of novel entities’ have not yet been 
quantified to an extent, where characterization models are considered mature enough for 
application and are thus not considered in the PB-LCIA. The second-mentioned approach, 
carrying capacity-based normalization references (CC-NR), introduced by Bjørn & Hauschild 
(2015) consists of a set of references calculated from science-based thresholds identified in 
the literature and expressed in metrics identical to midpoint categories (Bjørn & Hauschild, 
==PAGE:32==
21 
2015). This method differs from the PB-LCIA method in terms of impact category coverage.  
Due to the differences between the two methods, it is expected that the results on AES will vary 
depending on the choice of approach (C. E. Andersen et al., 2020). 
3.2.4. 
Inconsistencies in AESA terminology and methodology 
PB-LCIA and CC-NR are however not the only LCA-based AESA methods that have been 
developed. A study by Bjorn et al., (2020) reviews several different LCA-based AESA methods, 
highlighting the great diversity in the development and application of AESAs. The study 
identifies 34 different AESA methods intended for application across 45 reviewed studies. The 
study found that climate change was the most used impact category followed by water use, 
land-system change, and biogeochemical flows, and that most methods address only one or 
two impact categories and are therefore far from providing a comprehensive assessment in 
terms of covering all environmental impact categories (Bjorn et al., 2020). Additionally, the 
study highlights the confusion about the understanding and definition of sharing principles in 
general, as stated by Bjorn et al. (2020): “The 45 reviewed studies refer to individual sharing 
principles with a variety of names and sometimes use a similar name for different principles.” 
(Bjorn et al., 2020).  From the 45 reviewed studies a total of 10 sharing principles were 
identified, whereas the most applied principles were the Equal per capita, Grandfathering, and 
Economic value added.  
In parallel to this, the practical implementation and application of AESA across industries is 
not always aligned with definitions and terminology defined in academia. For instance, a study 
by S. C. Andersen et al. (2024) highlights the lack of a common understanding of the term 
‘carrying capacity’. This term is often translated into a reduction ‘target’ and used in the 
ongoing efforts of quantifying environmental impacts in e.g. companies and industries. 
According to S. C. Andersen et al. (2024), this perception is misunderstood since it is incorrect 
to strive for and maintain an environmental impact that is already at the critical point of the 
Earth's carrying capacity. Thus, carrying capacities do not indicate a ‘performance end goal’, 
but rather a ‘threshold’ at which the earth is at risk of being exposed to irreversible climate 
change (S. C. Andersen et al., 2024).  
In summary, contradictions within the terminology and methodology of AESA can still be found 
in the literature and highlights AESAs as a recent field of knowledge.

3.3.1. 
A review of industry initiatives 
The existing industry initiatives work towards bringing the built environment closer to or within 
the earth’s carrying capacity and thereby achieving absolute environmental sustainability. 
Four initiatives respectively Reduction Roadmap, DGNB-planet, Science Based Targets 
initiative (SBTi), and the Doughnut for Urban Development have been identified through 
desktop research as initiatives targeting the built environment. Thus, these are reviewed in the 
following sections.  
3.3.1.1. 
Reduction Roadmap 
Reduction Roadmap is a science-based tool rooted in climate science, that concretizes annual 
CO2 limits for new construction in Denmark (Baggrund for Reduction Roadmap Og Oplaeg Til 
Skaerpet Graense-Vaerdi for Bygningers Klimapåvirkning, n.d.). The tool proposes a stepwise 
approach to mitigate CO2e of new construction by mapping out the current position of the 
construction industry and the desired situation (figure 10), to fulfill the Paris Agreement’s goal 
of a maximum temperature increase of 1.5 °C, while staying within the safe operating space as 
defined by Rockström (2009).

3.3.1.2. 
DGNB-Planet 
DGNB-Planet is a label developed with the goal of bringing construction closer to the planetary 
boundaries. It builds on the existing framework of the DGNB system for new buildings and 
extensive renovations and consists of a series of knock-out requirements, all of which must be 
met to achieve the DGNB Planet label (Green Building Council Denmark, 2023). The first 
version of DGNB Planet was introduced in 2023, which sets requirements within four of the 
nine planetary boundaries: Biodiversity, Novel Entities (chemical pollution), Land-system 
Change and Climate Change (DGNB Planet | Rådet for Bæredygtigt Byggeri, n.d.). The second 
version of DGNB Planet is introduced in the 2025-pilot manual and sets requirements for three 
additional planetary boundaries: Freshwater Change, Biogeochemical Flows, and Ocean 
Acidification. With these additions, a total of seven of the nine planetary boundaries are 
introduced (Rådet for Bæredygtigt Byggeri, 2024). In addition, version 2 of DGNB Planet 
includes requirements for resources, since construction accounts for a large share of total 
resource consumption, and this consumption is expected to increase as the world population 
grows (DGNB Planet | Rådet for Bæredygtigt Byggeri, n.d.). 
3.3.1.3. 
Science Based Targets Initiative (SBTi) 
In line with the efforts to mitigate climate change, a similar tool has been developed to guide 
companies to reduce their CO2 emissions in alignment with the goal of the Paris Agreement, 
==PAGE:35==
24 
 
namely the Science Based Target Initiative (SBTi). The SBTi guidelines have been developed for 
various industry sectors, including the built environment, and are based on the latest scientific 
knowledge of what a given sector needs to mitigate to limit global warming to 1.5°C above pre-
industrial levels (Science Based Targets, 2023). Within the sector guidance for the built 
environment, the initiative has established two objectives related to climate change. These 
address the building's in-use operational emissions and up-front embodied emissions aligned 
with 1.5°C (Science Based Targets, 2023). The initiative has been developed in a collaboration 
between several organizations: CDP, The United Nations Global Compact (UNGC), World 
Resources Institute (WRI), and the World Wide Fund for Nature (WWF) (Buildings - Science 
Based Targets, n.d.).  
3.3.1.4. 
Doughnut for Urban Development  
The Doughnut for Urban Development is an industry initiative developed by Kate Raworth. It 
addresses the social challenges while respecting the ecological limits of our planet (The 
Doughnut for Urban Development - A Manual, n.d.). The framework consists of two concentric 
rings: a social foundation respecting basic human needs (food, housing, healthcare, and 
political voice) and an ecological ceiling respecting the planet’s life-supporting systems on 
which human life fundamentally depends. The social ring consists of twelve dimensions 
defined by UN’s Sustainable Development Goals in 2015, whereas the ecological ring consists 
of the nine planetary boundaries defined by Rockström (2009) (figure 11).  
 
Figure 11: “The inner ring, the social foundation represents the minimum social standards required for human well-being, while 
the outer ring, the ecological ceiling represents the ecological limits of the planet. Between these rings lies the doughnut-
shaped safe and just space for humanity that is regenerative and distributive by design.(The Doughnut for Urban Development 
- A Manual, n.d.)”. 
==PAGE:36==
25 
 
By ensuring no economic activities compromise social equality or exceed ecological limits, the 
model takes a holistic approach seeking to guide the built environment in decision-making 
processes toward a sustainable transition (The Doughnut for Urban Development - A Manual, 
n.d.). Although the Doughnut for Urban Development addresses all nine planetary boundaries, 
the main focus is on climate change and change in biosphere integrity, as these are defined as 
the central planetary boundaries. It means, that if they are significantly exceeded, they can 
independently drive the Earth system towards a new state away from the Holocene (The 
Doughnut for Urban Development - A Manual, n.d.). Based on the two key boundaries, the 
initiative provides 48 impact areas consisting of actionable indicators, tools, and benchmarks 
that can be used to align urban development to a safe and just space for humanity. (The 
Doughnut for Urban Development - A Manual, n.d.).   
3.3.2. 
Inconsistencies in industry initiatives targeting the BE 
The abovementioned industry initiatives all share a common goal, each aiming to establish a 
unified framework to integrate absolute targets or limits in the built environment. However, the 
initiatives build upon steps from the theoretical AESA-framework, and it is therefore important 
to acknowledge uncertainties in the understanding of natural systems (Bjorn et al., 2020) and 
be careful not to blindly apply the initiatives (S. C. Andersen et al., 2024). 
In fact, there are significant variations and many differences between the initiatives (Andersen 
et al., 2024). For instance, looking into the absolute reference, some initiatives use the carrying 
capacity that respects the 1.5 °C temperate goal from the Paris Agreement, e.g. Reduction 
Roadmap and SBTi, while others adhere to the Planetary Boundaries like The Doughnut for 
Urban Development and DGNB Planet (S. C. Andersen et al., 2024). This assumes that the first 
two initiatives only cover one impact category, climate change, whereas the latter two 
initiatives take a more holistic perspective on the Earth's carrying capacity covering multiple 
planetary boundaries. The tunnel vision on one impact category and the omission of the others 
can potentially lead to an unintentional burden shift (S. C. Andersen et al., 2024). Hence, there 
is a risk of the built environment’s adoption of AESA leading to inconsistencies with the 
academic definition of AESA caused by simplifications and conceptual misunderstandings (S. 
C. Andersen et al., 2024). Combined with the current national legislative initiatives’ focus on 
reducing GHG emissions from new buildings, ignoring how policies still allow the total 
emissions to increase, the industry initiatives are recommended to rather serve as a 
steppingstone for environmental sustainability transition (Andersen et al., 2024). Considering 
these discrepancies, S. C. Andersen et al. (2024) suggests recommendations for practical 
implementation of AESA through e.g. initiatives in the built environment: “It might therefore be 
worthwhile if academia and industry could collaborate more closely on balancing simplicity 
and validity and to align definitions and terminology.” Thus, the industry initiatives must be 
applied with caution.  
==PAGE:37==
26 
 
To conclude on AES(A)BE being the overlap between AES and BE, it must be noted how the 
contradictions in terminology and methodology found in literature also is inherited in the 
emerging industry initiatives aiming to operationalize AES within the BE. Nevertheless, the 
industry initiatives constitute a sound response to the environmental, legislative, and financial 
pressures on the BE even though it remains uncertain for the project team whether these 
initiatives and AESA in general are applied to the BE. Therefore, this uncertainty gave rise for 
the project team to form the following hypothesis (H0): "There is a lack of AESA-application in 
the built environment.”  
 
3.4. 
Conclusion on Research Clarification  
Through the Research Clarification the understanding of the current landscape related to RQ0 
was improved. Areas of relevance and contribution were identified as the built environment 
(BE), absolute environmental sustainability (AES), and the overlap of the two being absolute 
environmental sustainability assessment AES(A)BE.  
The literature review of the BE helped to understand environmental, legislative, and financial 
pressures in relation to environmental sustainability within the BE, and furthermore to form a 
target audience being Real Estate Developers (REDs). On the contrary, reviewing the literature 
on AES contributed to an understanding of the concepts in contrast to relative sustainability, 
to clarify planetary boundaries (PBs) included in Earth system science, and how to evaluate 
environmental performance in relation to these. However, the literature review also showed 
how the field and especially AESA is a relatively new research area.  
The overlap between the BE and AES has shown significant potential in terms of existing 
industry initiatives building upon AESA. However, these also inherit misunderstandings, and it 
is unknown to what extent they and AESA are applied. Thus, a hypothesis (H0) was formulated 
as: "There is a lack of AESA-application in the built environment.” Therefore, considering the 
pressures on the BE to improve the environmental performance and the relatively new concept 
AES reflecting the state of the Earth system influenced by anthropogenic global environmental 
impacts, the RQ0 has been stated and supported by SRQs, altogether aiming to support a 
successful implementation of AESA in the BE.

The Descriptive Study I (DS-I) builds upon the findings from the Research Clarification 
(RC) and aims to further increase the understanding of the current situation. This will

Question 1:  
From question 1 asking: “Are you familiar with the concept of absolute environmental

interest, and lastly, one respondent knows the term on a general level. The answers can

confirmed nor denied. However, only respondents 2 and 7 stated a clear yes with no 
further elaboration. The remaining respondents all add a disclaimer stating how they 
merely know AES on a general level, or as a term, etc. Thus, the hypothesis cannot be 
denied. However, as the responses are based on stakeholders actively engaging in 
sustainability issues, it can be argued that other REDs in the BE are not as familiar with

The affinity diagram consists of red and green post-its representing drivers (green) and

In contrast, several drivers experienced in working with AES have also been identified, 
which are demonstrated by several standalone drivers. These are "Internal Ambitions", 
reflecting companies' motivation to achieve their reduction targets. "Engagement", 
which emphasizes companies' motivation to work with AES and achieve success in 
moving closer to or staying within planetary boundaries. "Awareness”, which reveals 
companies' knowledge of the current development of AES, where biodiversity is in focus.

companies towards AES, which similarly reflects current awareness. 
In addition, the cluster “legislation and regulations as controlling elements” has a two-

ambition to reduce their emissions by complying with building regulations. However, 
regulations can also inhibit innovation and development opportunities, which is a 
perceived barrier. Another cluster with a similar mix of drivers and barriers is 
"responsibility". In this cluster, the majority of drivers appear, which highlights that the 
companies recognize their responsibility to protect the environment and their desire to 
participate in the change. On the contrary, they appear demotivated because achieving 
AES does not seem achievable and not everyone recognizes the impact and thus 
reduction potential of the BE.  
By identifying drivers and barriers, this study contributed to a deeper understanding of 
what factors either hinder or foster companies’ use of AESA, which will be relevant later 
in the project to determine where it is most appropriate to focus efforts on the 
development of a support.

challenges associated with the application of AESA. Given the implementation of AES 
being at an early stage in the BE, and how AESA is a relatively new field of research 
according to the RC stage, the following systematic literature review aims to identify 
academia’s presentation of drivers and barriers related to the application of AESA in the

4.1.2.1. 
Preparing the systematic literature review 
The systematic literature review was based on the approach defined by Biolchini et al, 
(2005) and aims to establish a controlled process for gathering scientific knowledge 
related to the study and to ensure that other researchers can obtain similar results by 
following the same procedure (Biolchini et al., 2005). The systematic literature review 
consists of three different stages: Planning, Execution, and Result Analysis. A 
comprehensive and detailed description of how the systematic literature review was 
conducted can be found in appendix 4.  
The Planning stage aims to identify the research objectives as well as define the process 
by which the sources and selection of studies were carried out. This included stating the 
problem and target of the systematic literature review as well as formulating relevant 
research questions and their effect, i.e. the types of results expected, to be answered by 
the systematic review (Table 1).  
By defining the scope of the Planning stage, the keywords related to RQ0 were defined 
and grouped into a search string which allowed a direction for the search towards the 
desired outcomes of the literature review. To ensure that the search string would 
produce the desired research, the search string has gone through several iterations due 
to AESA being a new research field of science, which affects the number of associated 
articles. This has implied that a too specific search string has resulted in no or very few 
results and thus not being broad enough to cover a meaningful number of articles for this 
research objective, which has impacted the way the research string is formulated. To 
enable a broader number of articles within the desired objectives of the literature review, 
the search string was relatively broad in its definition and only refers to AES. 
To systematize the selection of research during the Execution stage, three filters were 
made. The first filter focused on reading the title, abstract, and keywords, filter 2 focused 
on reading the introduction, conclusion, and figures, and finally filter 3 focused on 
reading the full paper. At each step of the process, the inclusion criteria were used to 
exclude studies that were not relevant to the purpose of the literature review. The 
inclusion criteria were: 1) Must include barriers to the implementation and application of 
absolute environmental sustainability in the built environment, and 2) Must include 
drivers for the implementation and application of absolute environmental sustainability 
in the built environment. The inclusion criteria were based on the desired output being 
the identification of drivers and barriers in the application of AESA in the BE. Thus, the 
inclusion criteria served as a qualitative guideline as well as a consideration for selecting 
relevant literature. The articles that did not meet the inclusion criteria were excluded. 
The outcomes from the Execution stage led to the Result Analysis of the systematic

35 
literature review, where the results and outcomes from the literature review were 
summarized and analyzed using criteria for classification (Biolchini et al., 2005).

Conclusion on Descriptive Study I 
The Descriptive study I provided an improved understanding of the current situation by 
conducting an empirical study exploring the barriers and drivers in the real estate 
developers work with absolute environmental sustainability and by investigating barriers 
and drivers in the application of AESA targeting the BE based on a systematic literature 
review on respectively white and grey literature. Thus, substantiated with comprehensive 
analysis, the hypothesis (H0); “There is a lack of AESA-application in the built 
environment” was confirmed and the analysis thus contributed to the exploration of 
which factors or elements that can contribute to or hinder the implementation of AESA.  
This resulted in the identification of five problems, whereby the problems “Problem 1 – A 
minimum of stakeholder awareness” and “Problem 3 – A Minimum of stakeholder

through a feasibility study and subsequently consolidated into a problem statement: 
“We will support a successful implementation of absolute environmental sustainability 
assessment in the built environment by increasing real estate developers’ knowledge of 
absolute environmental sustainability through collaboration and by avoiding a resource 
demanding implementation process.” This will together with the developed performance 
specification stating the requirements and criteria for the intended support form the 
basis for the subsequent PS stage, for effective development of support.

The PS consists of five stages: 1) a Task Clarification, to clarify the problem to be solved 
and the requirements obtained from the previous DS-I stage, 2) a Conceptualization, to 
generate potential solutions for the intended support aiming at fulfilling the need defined 
in the Task Clarification, 3) an Elaboration to describe the intended support and thus 
provide an overall shape to the concept, 4) a Realization, to finalize the details of the 
concept and how it should be tested, and 5) a Support Evaluation, involving checking 
whether the intended support meets the requirements of the performance specification 
to ensure that the Actual Support is developed to such an extent that it can be evaluated 
in DS-II (Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009). Due to the Prescriptive Study’s innovative nature, 
this stage will take an iterative development approach involving several workshops and 
design thinking activities.

5.2.1.3. 
Conducting workshop 1 
WS1 was planned as a physical workshop and the stakeholders participating in the 
workshop were four REDs with different backgrounds, as some are private companies 
and others are public organizations (appendix 2). The workshop was based on two 
purposes. The first purpose was to have the stakeholders discuss strengths and 
weaknesses to AESA through industry initiatives. This was done through a SWOT analysis 
adopted from (Osterwalder & Pigneur, 2010). The second purpose of the workshop focused 
on identifying the stakeholders desired type of knowledge about AES in a support tool. 
This was assessed through the ‘ranking through discussion’ as defined by the universal 
design guide (Ranking Through Group Discussion I Universal Design Guide, n.d.) based on the 
previously presented concepts. 
The workshop started with a brief introduction to AESA and the project’s focus based on 
findings from DS-I to ensure that stakeholders also consider this problem area to be the 
right one. The presentation was followed by two activities exploring each of the purposes 
mentioned earlier.  
Activity 1: SWOT Analysis 
The SWOT analysis was facilitated by having the four REDs select a random industry 
initiative and examine it through pre-selected literature to make sure the participants 
understand the initiatives. Meanwhile, they were asked to write their thoughts on 
strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, and threats on post-its focusing on the 
application of the industry initiative in the case of the company they represent. Finally, 
the participants presented their findings to the rest while placing post-its up on the board 
during a joint wrap-up. 
The SWOT analysis facilitated an insightful discussion among the stakeholders, which 
contributed to the project team identifying various relevant insights (appendix 9). The 
findings that were considered the most relevant and with the greatest contribution to the 
project are the following: 
•
Meeting the requirements of the DGNB Planet certification and following the
reduction ladder in the Reduction Roadmap can be financially beneficial.
•
There was a shared enthusiasm for the Reduction Roadmap because of its high
transparency in the calculation method, which increases usability and
understanding. According to REDs, it has realistic reduction targets based on
short-term periods and is less influenced by research language, making the
initiative more down-to-earth and user-friendly for the common user.
•
SBTi, DGNB Planet, and the Doughnut for Urban Development are considered too
comprehensive and thus too complex to understand, which reduces
engagement as well as the adoption of the initiatives. It may also mean that an
AESA expert may be required to allow for an integration.
==PAGE:73==
60 
•
Comprehensive solutions do not fit into existing processes, as the
construction industry is already a highly complex sector. The opportunity lies in
building upon existing workflows, where companies only need to make small
adjustments.
•
The reduction targets stated in Doughnut for Urban Development, SBTi, and the
new reduction staircase in Reduction Roadmap are considered unachievable
and thus demotivating to engage in.
Several of the findings support and elaborates the insights previously gained in RC and 
DS-I, i.e., the discussion indicated how the language should be less complex and not so 
influenced by research terminology for the future support to meet the requirement "must 
increase knowledge about industry initiatives" and “must be applicable without an AESA 
expert”. In addition, based on the key insights from the SWOT analysis a new requirement

5.2.2.1. 
Conceptualization of Readiness as input to workshop 2 
The conceptualization of ‘readiness’ consisted of three steps. The first step examines 
how 'ready' companies are to implement AES, using the ADKAR method (M. Hiatt & J. 
Creasey, 2012). The second step builds on the ADKAR analysis and unfolds the readiness 
concept by examining what type of concepts enable the change. The final step examines 
what skills, training, and education are required for the change.  
To facilitate the implementation of AESA, it has been necessary to understand where 
REDs are in their implementation process and how to get them ready for the 
implementation. To assess this, an adaption of the ADKAR method (M. Hiatt & J. Creasey, 
2012) has been applied. The ADKAR method was developed by M. Hiatt & J. Creasey 
(2012) and consists of a change management model guiding individual and 
organizational change. The five letters in ADKAR represent a stepwise approach that 
must be achieved by individuals to create change: 1) Awareness of the need to change, 
2) Desire to participate in and support the change, 3) Knowledge about how to change,
4) Ability to implement new skills and behaviors and 5) Reinforcement to keep the change
in place (ﬁgure 21). Aligned with business change, the ADKAR model states that
successful change is achieved when: “(…) the business change has been fully
implemented, and employees have the awareness, desire, knowledge, and ability to
implement those changes.”(M. Hiatt & J. Creasey, 2012). Thus, for a successful
implementation of AESA for REDs in the BE, the support tool must be tailored according
to where the organizations are in the change process and be continuous throughout the
transition phase (M. Hiatt & J. Creasey, 2012). For this reason, the readiness of the REDs
in the BE to implement AES has been assessed.  Note that the ADKAR method is adapted
to the activities in this project and will therefore not examine A, D, K, A, and R of individual
employees in implementing AESA, but rather assess the companies constituting the BE
in this project.
DS-I clarified that REDs are aware of the need to change towards AES, which fulfills the 
first step in the ADKAR model of 'awareness'. Moreover, the REDs have a clear desire to 
participate in and support the change towards AES, which fulfills the second step, 
namely 'desire’. However, through the DS-I and as evident in the problem statement, the 
REDs have limited knowledge about how to change (figure 21), which constitutes the 
third step, knowledge, in the ADKAR model.  
==PAGE:76==
63 
Figure 21: ADKAR model (M. Hiatt & J. Creasey, 2012) illustrating the current situation of where REDs are in their 
implementation process. 
According to M. Hiatt & J. Creasey, (2012) if ‘knowledge’ is critical in a change process, 
then “What is needed is education and training for the skills and behaviors that are 
required by the change.”(M. Hiatt & J. Creasey, 2012). Thus, to unfold the readiness 
concept another brainstorming session was conducted which led to five low-fidelity 
concepts each of which contributes to preparing the REDs for implementing AESA by 
providing education or training on the skills required, as stated by (M. Hiatt & J. Creasey, 
2012). The concepts were a onepager, course training, a protocol, a community, and 
conversation cards (appendix 10) each revisiting and examining some of the same ideas 
as inputs for WS1 due to the relatively low number of participants during WS1. Thus, the 
purpose of bringing these concepts to WS2 is to have a validation of the WS1 outputs as 
well as an identification of which collaborative features are desired by the participants in 
a support.

development process inspired by the RED processes defined in Olsson et al. (2015) and 
The Real Estate Development Process Map - Groundwork USA (n.d.). The sequence 
model served as a boundary object (Carlile, 2002) to identify where a support tool

was carried out by showing the participants the sequence model, asking if they could

This resulted in an insightful dialogue with the participants leading to an identification of

The reasoning behind AES knowledge creation fitting into phase 2 and 3 was based on 
the following findings based on the dialogues:    
•
The integration of AES knowledge into existing workflows must take place in the
earliest stage of the development process of a project, potentially in phases 1-
3, and form the basis for planning the project. This conclusion was based on the
REDs' previous experiences with buildings requiring, for instance, a DGNB
certification. In such cases, the certification was prior to the project development
and would typically be introduced in the initial stages of the project development.
•
AES knowledge creation should be used to align expectations during a project
planning process’s initial meeting i.e. project planning meetings, where
multiple professional competencies are represented. Such meetings have an
approximate duration of 1-2 hours.
•
The integration of AES knowledge will most likely impact downstream activities
and lead to an increase in price per m2. In contrast, a building with AES integrated
will increase the resale value of the property.

•
AES as an ambition will potentially be deselected in feasibility and project
planning phases due to financial constraints and the fact that AESA is not a
mandatory requirement in regulations and standards.
The findings also resulted in the addition of a new requirement and a new criterion to the 
performance specification: “Must increase knowledge about environmental legislation” 
and “Should demonstrate harmonization between environmental legislation and AESA” 
to prevent the potential deselection of AES as an ambition in the feasibility and project 
planning phases. This is also partly based on the knowledge gained through RC, which 
showed that the ESRS set requirements related to certain PBs. Thus, by providing the 
REDs with insights to how AES and current legislation interact, it might hinder AES being 
deselected as an ambition.

5.4.1. 
The AES Dialogue Tool 
The ‘AES Dialogue Tool’ described in the Elaboration step was developed based on the 
findings of the previous RC and DS-I stages and from the three cycles of 
conceptualization and elaboration during the PS stage. As explained in the 
conceptualization step, the tool is designed to support a successful implementation of 
AESA for REDs in the BE by increasing the REDs knowledge about AES through 
collaboration. Furthermore, as the tool’s intended use takes place during a project 
planning meeting, which is a part of the REDs existing workflows, the tool aims to avoid 
a resource demanding implementation process.  
==PAGE:85==
72 
The AES Dialogue Tool consists of three artifacts: a manual including an introduction, PB 
importance, and an explanation of relative versus absolute environmental sustainability 
(figure 26); three decks of 'knowledge cards' based on PBs, ESRS, and industry initiatives 
(figure 27); and a prioritization board activating the cards in terms of what AES knowledge 
should be included in a project (figure 28). The procedure of application of the artifacts 
is as follows:  
1) The manual will be read beforehand by all stakeholders participating in the
meeting to get an introductory description of how to apply the dialogue tool, an
explanation of AES and its importance, and a description of the difference
between AES and relative sustainability.
2) Hereafter, the stakeholders pull a card, read it out loud, and then pass it around
to all the participants attending the meeting.
3) Next, the participants discuss how this can be prioritized on the board in
relation to the case and place the card according to its prioritization.
4) Then a new card is drawn, etc. until all cards are discussed and placed on the
priority board.

Applying the tool’s three artifacts, the AES Dialogue Tool seeks to facilitate the REDs’

emphasized through PBs, ESRS, and industry initiatives. Thus, the tool serves as an initial 
support tool preliminary to the actual implementation of AESA and does therefore not 
include specific actions to reach AES.  
The application of the AES Dialogue Tool is intended to be used at the project planning 
meeting during the real estate development process, where multiple professional 
competencies are represented. Thus, the objective is to facilitate collaboration through 
discussions on PBs, ESRS, and industry initiatives, ultimately including AES in BE 
projects. Thus, the expected outcomes of applying the AES Dialogue Tool are: 
•
Alignment of expectations: Can facilitate the alignment of expectations for a
project in relation to absolute environmental sustainability in project planning
meetings in the real estate development process according to stakeholders
participating in WS2
•
Knowledge sharing: The collaborative nature of the tool can contribute to a
common understanding across professional competencies in a real estate
development process present at a project planning meeting.
•
Future proof: Since the tool builds on knowledge about environmental disclosure
requirements in ESRS gradually becoming effective from 2024 onwards, the
support can be used to prepare for the future according to upcoming
environmental legislation.

5.6. 
Conclusion on Prescriptive Study 
The Prescriptive Study (PS) resulted in the developed AES Dialogue Tool. A tool designed 
to support a successful implementation of AESA for REDs in the BE by increasing the 
REDs’ knowledge about AES through collaboration and by avoiding a resource 
demanding implementation process.  
As evident from the Conceptualisation step in this PS stage, the REDs’ desired 
knowledge is related to awareness of AES before an implementation of AESA 
emphasized by them choosing ‘Readiness’ as the desired knowledge creation. This 
readiness was by the project team unfolded through the ADKAR method (M. Hiatt & J. 
Creasey, 2012) revealing limited knowledge as a barrier to the change towards 
implementing AESA. In addition, the REDs emphasized how the opportunity for a solution 
increasing knowledge lies in the incorporation into existing workflows in the project 
planning process. Ultimately, conversation cards were chosen by stakeholders as 
desired means to collaboration due to their ability of facilitating the needed dialogue 
before an actual implementation of AESA takes place. 
Moreover, the Elaboration step in this PS reviewed existing tools including conversation 
cards to guide the development of three artifacts: a manual, three decks of knowledge 
cards, and a prioritization board. The three artifacts altogether constitute the developed 
AES Dialogue Tool expected to result alignment of expectations among during project 
planning meetings, knowledge sharing across professional competencies, prepare 
users for future environmental legislation, and lastly form a solid basis for a future 
implementation of AESA in the BE.  
Thus, the PS enabled the development of the type of support necessary for creating a 
successful implementation of AESA for REDs in the BE and thus aimed to answer the 
third sub-research question (SRQ3): “What support is required for real estate developers 
to successfully implement absolute environmental sustainability assessment in a 
corporate context?” 
In the following DS-II stage, the AES Dialogue Tool undergoes its final evaluation of 
applicability, usability, and usefulness based on the third and last workshop.

6. The Descriptive Study II  
The Descriptive Study II (DS-II) is the ﬁnal stage of the Design Research Methodology 
(DRM) and aims to evaluate the application and impacts of the developed design support 
(Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009). Based on work from the three previous DRM stages, this 
stage will develop an evaluation plan containing measurable functionalities of the 
developed support to test applicability, usability, and usefulness (Blessing & Chakrabarti, 
2009). The evaluation is also conducted during this stage, and its results are analyzed and 
discussed to conclude on the overall performance of the developed support. Ultimately 
the results and recommendations for further development of these are reﬂected on to 
evaluate whether the support lives up to the goal of the project.  
6.1.

outline the elements of the support that need to be tested (Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009)

during the Descriptive Study I (DS-I) and Prescriptive Study (PS). As evident in the Support

Evaluation.

successful implementation of absolute environmental sustainability assessment in the

point of departure from this problem statement and put it into the perspective of the RQ0:

6.3. 
Reflections 
As an extension of the results, the project team has interpreted the circumstances’ 
inﬂuence on these and considered multiple improvements for further development and 
testing. Taking a general perspective on WS3, the project team identiﬁed four overarching 
areas affecting the outcome of the workshop: 
1) Participants during WS3  
A signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the results of WS3 is inevitably the number of participants, 
since only two participants showed up on the day of the testing. This ﬁrst and foremost 
means less representativeness of the results, as the project team cannot, through the 
analysis, conclude whether these results are isolated incidents or actual improvements. 
Secondly, it has resulted in more involvement of the facilitators, the project team, to have 
a conversation ﬂowing, which furthermore has slurred the actual conclusions on whether 
the participants could have used the tool themselves.  
Nevertheless, the company respondent 6 and company respondent 9 were familiar with 
each other, since they have been working on real estate development projects together 
before and therefore were able to refer to a common real-life case and discuss the tool’s 
role in such context. However, a blind spot is then the applicability of the tool if the 
participants do not know each other which could impact the dynamics between the two 
participants. This also affected their role-taking during the WS3, since company 
respondent 6 quickly became ‘the expert’ and the company respondent 9 was more of 
‘the interrogator’ asking challenging questions. It aligns with the participants’ 
background and positions in respectively a private and a public company (appendix 2).  It 
therefore became clear that the participants had different levels of knowledge, resulting 
in one having a more prominent role than the other. Nevertheless, their different levels of 
expertise were contributing to the proof of how the tool is applicable without an AESA 
expert.  
2) Facilitation  
Another signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the results is the facilitation of the WS3. It has already 
been mentioned brieﬂy, but because of the few participants, the project team asked more 
questions to have the discussion ﬂowing. Looking aside from the few participants, a 
manual could have helped this facilitation on the way. As was explained during the 
Application Evaluation, the participants already discussed the different approaches for 
placing the cards, and the manual explaining these would have limited the questions to 
approaching the tool. However, the lack of the manual is not only a disadvantage, since 
this also provided the opportunity to explore what such manual must include without 
biasing the participants with what has already been put in a manual.  
Also, the terminology used by the project team during the presentation of the tool 
appeared to inﬂuence the participants’ understanding of the tool. The tool was presented 
to be applied during a project planning meeting in the early stages of the REDs’ workﬂows. 
However, it was evident by the participants that the tool could not be used in this context 
==PAGE:103==
89 
 
since this would be too late in the process to incorporate AES considerations and 
suggested that the tool should be used in proposal meetings instead. This also meant 
that the planned case study presented at the workshop could not be used to test the tool, 
as too many details of the case were already decided. Hence, company respondent 6 
addressed how the ﬂexibility of the tool was limited by the context it was expected to be 
applied. 
3) Knowledge creation  
The last overarching inﬂuence, with signiﬁcant impact on the analysis, was the questions 
asked in the self-assessment scheme. Once again, having only two respondents limits 
the results analysis and in the case of knowledge creation, it became clear that there 
were limitations to the conclusions, as the participants’ answers were very distinct. 
However, leaning towards the joint evaluation discussion and both participants’ positive 
attitude towards the tool, the project team decided to take a closer look at company 
respondent 6’s answers. Company respondent 6 regressed in knowledge in two areas. 
However, this was based on two PBs of which cards were not reviewed in the discussion. 
This also means that company respondent 9 improved on various cards, which were not 
reviewed. This might connect to the self-assessment scheme being extensive and 
including many new and difficult terms. Furthermore, these very speciﬁc questions were 
asked, when the participants did no longer have the cards in front of them. Thus, as 
already emphasized the results should be interpreted carefully and the conclusions 
likewise.  
Nevertheless, the collaboration among different professions was present and even 
discussed by the participants, as company respondent 6 believed that the tool could 
change some conceptual understandings among stakeholders with less knowledge 
within sustainability. 
4) The impact of the AES Dialogue Tool  
The AES Dialogue Tool can be argued to increase the REDs' knowledge of AES aspects, 
since it gives them a better basis and increased ability to translate the knowledge gained 
through the tool into practical action and thus move closer to a successful 
implementation of AESA, as stated in the ADKAR model. This allows them to potentially 
reach the "Ability" stage, where new skills, such as AESA, can be implemented based on 
the knowledge gained through the support.  
From testing the AES dialog tool in WS3, an additional beneﬁt of the tool emerged, 
besides increasing knowledge of AES aspects related to legislation, PBs and industry 
initiatives. It proved to be a tool that similarly could make trade-offs between AES aspects 
transparent and explicit through the content of the knowledge cards. This was considered 
a great value for stakeholders because it could facilitate informed decision-making 
between meeting participants to ﬁnd the most beneﬁcial prioritization of AES aspects as 
a basis for a building project. This was further conﬁrmed by one of the participants who 
wanted the project team to introduce the tool at a client meeting to align expectations of 
AES before the project started.

Furthermore, it was evident in WS3 how the stakeholders showed an understanding of 
that the planetary boundaries are interconnected and that deselecting one planetary 
boundary can potentially have a negative effect on another. The correlation between PBs 
was thus clearly reﬂected in the tool, which supported the narrative of the AES theory and 
underlined the importance of avoiding sub-optimization or tunnel vision when 
processing planetary boundaries.  
Considering the content introduced on each knowledge card related to environmental 
legislation, industry initiatives, and PBs, it is debatable whether the content provided on

AESA. This would require further testing with the stakeholders examining each element 
of the cards.  
  
6.3.1. 
Recommendations for further work 
Based on the reﬂections, the project team has furthermore identiﬁed areas of the 
developed support, the AES Dialogue Tool, to be improved, and recommendations for 
further work such as testing, to perform a comprehensive type of evaluation (Blessing & 
Chakrabarti, 2009). The improvements have been divided into themes based on previous 
reﬂections.   
More participants 
Based on the few participants during WS3 affecting the representativeness of the results, 
the project team has deemed it relevant for future testing to have a minimum of four 
participants during another test of the tool. This number is selected since it is 
emphasized by participants during WS3 how they would not prefer the tool to gather too 
many participants. This to maintain a successful dialog. As the project team still desires 
to have the tool tested with many stakeholders, it is recommended to divide the 
participants into smaller groups, and thereby have a test relying more on quantitative 
evaluation.  
A self-explanatory manual 
As mentioned, several times already during DS-II, it is highly relevant to test the tool 
without involvement from the project team. It was emphasized by the participants 
during WS3 how one from the company must bring the tool to a proposal meeting and 
thereby be the facilitator or at least the one to ensure the application of the tool during 
the meeting. Thus, the manual becomes an even more important part of the tool, both in 
terms of testing it, but also for the rest of the tool to be tested properly.  
Moreover, testing the tool with a low-ﬁdelity prototype without a manual was an efficient 
way to determine whether the tool was self-explanatory. However, as the criteria 
assessing this aspect of the tool did not reach its desired value, the project team could 
conclude how the tool is not self-explanatory. The manual is therefore relevant to develop 
==PAGE:105==
91 
 
accordingly, and it is recommended to further develop the manual including the 
following key take away from the WS3: 
• Include different application scenarios related to the order in which the different 
decks should be discussed. 
• Include explanations of PBs in the manual to keep the cards simple 
• Include procedures for the application of tool 
• Include an explanation of why the card decks are included in the tool 
• Include an introduction to the differences between Absolute Environmental 
Sustainability versus Relative Environmental Sustainability RES 
Context of application 
The tool should according to the participants be applied even earlier than the expected 
point of a RED process. It is therefore by the project team deemed relevant for future tests 
to take place with a case reﬂecting this point of the process, the proposal stage, where 
the initial meeting takes place in such a situation. To have a real-life case to test the tool 
on, would also allow for a more comprehensive testing of the ESRS, since many of these 
were skipped over, but also commented on by company respondent 6 that they might be 
easier to test if there were different participants’ strategies or policies to consider in 
the prioritization, which there most likely will be for a RED company.  
User-friendliness 
During the WS3, the participants read the cards out loud due to the small text on the 
prototype. Thus, the review of each card took longer than expected by the project team. 
These insights indicated how the low-ﬁdelity prototype as often is less user-friendly, and 
further development of the tool can focus on the user-friendliness to improve this.  
However, the project team did also ask the participants during WS3 if they had any 
recommendations for improvement for the further development of the AES Dialogue tool, 
and got the following answers: 
• The readability and explanations need improvement 
• The technical research terminology needs to become more understandable for 
REDs 
• Allowing participants to review the cards in advance would make it even easier 
and let them "form an opinion" ﬁrst. 
• Some training would be good for those who need to facilitate  
The cards are therefore relevant to improve in terms of bigger text and easy-to-read 
descriptions of the PBs, industry initiatives, and ESRS. Ultimately, as already 
discussed, the manual needs to be a part of the further testing to conclude whether the 
last two points highlighted by the participants are relevant.

6.4. 
Conclusion on Descriptive Study II 
The DS-II included the evaluation plan for the test of the AES Dialogue tool emphasizing 
the knowledge, resource, and collaboration related test methods for requirements and 
criteria, and the desired values for the results of these tests. Based on this, the third and 
last workshop with the stakeholders was planned, conducted, and lastly evaluated 
through participant self-assessment surveys, a joint evaluation discussion, and the 
project team’s observations. The results showed how all the requirements’ desired 
values were reached, but also, on the contrary, how none of the criteria’s desired values 
were reached. Thus, the developed support, the AES Dialogue Tool increases knowledge 
of PBs, industry initiatives, and ESRS legislation. It also facilitates collaboration and can 
be applied to existing RED workﬂows. However, even though the cards in the tool are easy 
to understand, the test did not conﬁrm the tool to be applied without the involvement of 
the project team and the linkages between the card decks are not tested enough to be 
concluded clear to the participants. The same is concluded for the duration of the tool 
application, since the participants did not ﬁnish within the two hours allocated for the 
test. Therefore, the developed support, the AES Dialogue Tool is applicable to the 
intended context and to some extent usable, but with signiﬁcant room left for 
improvements. This is mainly because of the low representativeness of the results, as 
only two stakeholders participated in the workshop. Thus, it must be clearly noted that 
further testing of the tool with more participants is a must and a good opportunity to test 
the manual for the tool, which has not been tested yet. Other areas for further 
improvement include the user-friendliness of the tool i.e. readability and the context for 
testing i.e. a better ﬁt of case study.  
Ultimately, because of the conditions for WS3, it can on a thin ground be concluded that 
the tool is applicable but further improvements are necessary to conclude the tool as 
usable. Moreover, it is not yet possible to conclude whether the AES Dialogue Tool 
answers the RQ0 and supports the REDs in implementing AES and eventually apply AESA. 
Further testing is a must.

7. 
Discussion 
This section involves a critical reflection on the limitations and challenges of this Master’s 
Thesis project pointing out several aspects that could have been improved or handled 
differently. 
Scoping of the project 
If the project team had been in possession of the knowledge they acquired during the project 
from the beginning, they would have narrowed their focus to a specific part of AES rather than 
directing their attention to AESA. This could have resulted in a more efficient and targeted 
effort, potentially leading to more detailed project results. In addition, an earlier scoping of the 
project prior to the DS-I stage, could potentially have led to more concrete results and perhaps 
led in a different direction than the identified problem areas found by gathering all insights in 
the merged affinity diagram.  A more precise definition of the project from the beginning could 
therefore have been beneficial to achieve a more elaborate solution. This could also have led 
to a greater focus on change management processes and transition strategies, which were 
only introduced late and to a limited extent in the project due to the lack of knowledge about 
what would lead to a successful implementation of AESA.  
Lack of case company affecting workshops 
This project was not carried out in collaboration with a specific case company, since the aim 
of the project focused on investigating the industry as a whole with the purpose of developing 
a support that could be used for the greater good. Retrospectively, this could have been 
beneficial to the project, as many challenges were experienced during the organization of the 
workshops with stakeholders. In particular, this was reflected in an incredible number of 
cancellations, which often could not be predicted by the project team, since the cancellations 
were received shortly before the workshops were conducted. This resulted in workshop 2 being 
held online and thus individually with each stakeholder, removing the collaborative and 
interactive aspects of the workshop. In addition, workshop 3 was originally planned to be 
attended by six industry representatives, but due to many cancellations, only two showed up 
for the workshop. This affected the validity of the testing of the final support, the AES Dialogue 
Tool, and therefore the conclusion on the testing cannot be said to be representative. A reliable 
collaboration with a case company could therefore have been advantageous for the project as 
it would have provided a better opportunity to test the product. On the contrary, the 
involvement of many different REDs helped to reinforce the common barriers and drivers 
identified through the stakeholder survey and workshops and can therefore be said to provide 
a stronger representation of the collective challenges experienced in the industry. 
 
 
==PAGE:109==
93 
 
Stakeholder characteristics  
Due to the involvement of a wide range of stakeholders with different professions and 
backgrounds within the real estate development industry, it would have been valuable to clarify 
where the different stakeholders should specifically contribute to the project. This would not 
only have made their contribution more targeted and efficient, but also ensured a more 
structured and coordinated effort from them. Furthermore, it was found that the level of 
knowledge of AES varied significantly between the participating REDs, which was reflected in 
workshop 3, where one participant showed extensive knowledge of AES and the other was 
therefore less prominent in the workshop. This was despite the fact that only stakeholders, 
considered to be ambitious in their work with environmental sustainability, were contacted. It 
could therefore have been relevant to further distinguish between the characteristics of the 
participating stakeholders and the companies they are part of. 
Identifying barriers and drivers using the affinity diagram method  
It is debatable whether the affinity diagram method was the most suitable method to 
systematize the insights obtained from the systematic literature review of white and grey 
literature. Applying the method led to an incredible number of insights that pointed in many 
different directions, making it challenging for the project team to group it into larger clusters. 
This also meant that the large clusters got a broader headline, in which some knowledge may 
have lost its effect. However, it was an effective method to identify continuous patterns that 
reflected common themes and issues, which could not have been achieved in the same way 
otherwise. In addition, the drivers found were not used as much as desired, as the project 
aimed to investigate and resolve problem areas hindering the implementation of AES whereby 
barriers automatically came more into consideration.  
Target audience  
The target audience for the developed solution was real estate developers due to their 
significant role in making key decisions in projects and bringing together stakeholders across 
the value chain. It is therefore debatable whether the target group should instead have been 
financiers, who in the ecosystem mapping seem to be the earliest participants in the building 
process. However, the financiers have no executive power on construction projects, nor do 
they have a role in bringing together actors across the value chain, and therefore it is still 
believed that the REDs are the best possible actors to implement and expand the knowledge 
of AES across the value chain.

8. 
Conclusion  
The built environment, which constitutes 37% of the global CO2 emissions, is proven to have 
significant potential to reduce its global environmental impact and thus operate within the 
Earth's carrying capacity. However, the industry is facing major challenges in the 
implementation and adoption of absolute environmental sustainability assessments, which 
operationalize the earth's carrying capacity through planetary boundaries. This is despite the 
fact that several AESA-based industry initiatives have been developed that target the built 
environment. Understanding the barriers preventing the adoption of AESAs and AESA-based 
industry initiatives in the built environment is, therefore, crucial, to enable a more successful 
implementation and to ensure that the built environment utilizes its reduction potential to 
operate within the earth's boundaries and contributes to reducing global environmental 
impacts. For this reason, the main research question of this master thesis has been:  
(RQ0): “How to support a successful implementation of absolute environmental sustainability 
assessment for real estate developers in the built environment?”. 
This Master’s Thesis took its methodological offset in the Design Research Methodology (DRM) 
which has served as the approach to four stages of developing the support. The first stage of 
the DRM, the RC stage, contributed to an improved understanding of the current landscape 
related to the RQ0. This stage included a literature review on areas of relevance and 
contribution to RQ0 being the BE, AES, and the overlap between the two, AES(A)BE. Thus, the 
literature review showed environmental, legislative, and financial pressures on the BE, while 
AES proved to elaborate on relative sustainability and possible to evaluate through the 
relatively new research area, AESA. The overlap identified four industry initiatives building upon 
AESA, however it is unknown to what extent they and AESA are applied. Thus, a hypothesis (H0) 
was formulated as: "There is a lack of AESA-application in the built environment.”  
The second stage of the DRM, the DS-I stage, contributed to answering SRQ1: “What does the 
current landscape of absolute environmental sustainability in the built environment look like?” 
and SRQ2: “What barriers and drivers are the real estate developers facing in the application 
of absolute environmental sustainability?” by providing an increased understanding of the 
current landscape through the identification of barriers and drivers in the application of AESA 
for REDs in the BE based on a systematic literature review as well as a stakeholder survey. This 
formed a comprehensive analysis confirming the hypothesis stated in RC and furthermore 
detected five problems, which through a feasibility study were narrowed down to two problems 
constituting the foundation for the problem formulation (PRS): “We will support a successful 
implementation of absolute environmental sustainability assessment in the built environment 
by increasing real estate developers’ knowledge of absolute environmental sustainability 
through collaboration and by avoiding a resource demanding implementation process.”  
==PAGE:112==
95 
 
Together with the developed performance specification stating the requirements and criteria 
for the intended support, this PRS formed the basis for the development of a support. 
The third stage of the DRM, the PS stage, contributed to answering SRQ3: “What support is 
required for the built environment to successfully implement absolute environmental 
sustainability assessment in their work?” through stakeholder workshops investigating the 
three knowledge, collaboration, and resource related aspects of the problem statement. Thus, 
the REDs’ desired knowledge is related to becoming ready for the implementation of AESA. The 
readiness was unfolded by applying the ADKAR method revealing limited knowledge as a 
barrier to the change towards implementing AESA. Moreover, to implement a support to such 
implementation, the REDs emphasized how it should be incorporated into existing workflows 
of their project planning process. Finally, stakeholders chose conversation cards as the 
desired means to collaborate forming the foundation for development of a solution initiated by 
a review of existing tools containing conversation cards.  
The developed support, the AES Dialogue Tool, consists of a manual, three decks of knowledge 
cards, and a prioritization board all tested and evaluated during the fourth and last stage of the 
DRM, the DS-II stage. Based on an evaluation plan, a stakeholder workshop and the 
comparison of these, the AES Dialogue Tool is concluded to hold significant room left for 
improvements mainly due to low level representativeness of the results. Ultimately, because 
of these conditions, it can on a thin ground be concluded that the tool is applicable but further 
improvements are necessary to conclude the tool as usable. Moreover, it is not yet possible to 
conclude whether the AES Dialogue Tool is useful and answers the RQ0 about supporting the 
REDs in implementing AES and eventually apply AESA. Further testing is a must.
