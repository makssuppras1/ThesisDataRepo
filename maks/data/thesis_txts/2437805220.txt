Abstract
To meet the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) presented by the United Nations,
the aquaculture industry needs to be given special attention to solve the hunger prob-
lem while maintaining ecological stability. In conjunction with the present concerns,
the aquaculture industry needs to be developed for supplying adequate fish. However,
the aquaculture industry faces a series of risks. Major concerns are fish escape issues
and fish health problems, especially caused by sea lice.
To cope with the challenge in aquaculture industry, closed fish cage is proposed
to use. However, there is limit knowledge to simulate the hydrodynamic loads on the
cage. More knowledge is expected for helping understand the cage response in the sea.
The aim of this thesis is to couple the interior sloshing with the exterior hydro-
dynamics for developing cage motion simulations in time domain. After the coupling
process is completed, the solution will focus on the swirling wave due to the swirling
wave may cause harm to the closed fish cage in reality.
The interior sloshing hydrodynamic loads and free surface elevation is simulated
by linear multimodal theory first. However, the linear sloshing theory has limitation
when the cage is in resonant state. The nonlinear multimodal theory will therefore
be developed for coupling which is more accurate to explain the sloshing problem in
resonant state. At the meanwhile, the swirling wave can only be simulated in nonlin-
ear sloshing solver. One can find the hydrodynamic loads induced by swirling wave
are several times bigger than the planar wave with same forced motions.
The main concern when establishing the coupling system for interior and exte-
rior wave effects, is that it requires the fully relationship between the hydrodynamic
loads and cage response. The exterior hydrodynamics obtained from WAMIT will
be transformed into time domain. It can be achieved by applying Impulse Response
Function [Cum62] and Inverse Fast Fourier Transformation in MATLAB script DTU-
MotionSimulator. The final result proves that the swirling wave induced by interior
sloshing will have big affect on the closed fish cage which need particular attention
for designing.

1.1
Background
The number of people inhabiting the earth is currently 7.5 billion and the United
Nations(UN) predicts that the world population will reach an astonishing 9.7 billion
by 2050. Urbanization and inhabiting areas closer to the sea are some important
trends within population growth. This growth translates directly into the need for
a tremendous increase in food production. The Food and Agriculture Organization
(FAO) of the UN predicts that the food production must increase by 70% to feed the
world population in 2050.
Figure 1.1: The Sustainable Development Goals.
In 2015 the UN presented the world with 17 global goals set to be achieved by
2030; the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) 1.1. The SDGs‚Äô ambition is to
change the world in terms of ending poverty, protecting the planet and ensuring pros-
perity. Each goal has a specific target which is set to be achieved by 2030 and cover

all three aspects of sustainable development: Economic, social and environmental.
To arrive at each target set by the SDGs, businesses and governments must work
together to create the building blocks for a greater and more innovative society. The
goals overlap and are highly interconnected, and an advancement in one will most
likely contribute to advancement in several others.
A sustainable increase in food production, especially proteins, is a necessity to
maintain a global development in line with United Nation‚Äôs Sustainable Development
Goals (SDGs). Land based agriculture is already pushing the limits of sustainabil-
ity and to feed new generations a more sustainable approach is needed, whereas the
oceans portray a great deal of unused potential.
Seafood is undoubtedly one of the most influential sources of protein. The growth
of wild fishing will be small in the coming years due to biological constraints and the
sustainability of wild fishing. Due to biological constraints and the sustainability of
wild catch, the growth within wild captures will be minimal in the years to come.
This implies that growth in the seafood sector must originate from the aquaculture
industry. This is echoed by the Norwegian Ministry of Trade, Industry and Fisheries
which recently released their vision to increase the marine value creation five-fold by
2050.
The UN‚Äôs Sustainable Development Goals are not single, unrelated goals, but they
are a set of 17 highly interconnected goals. Yet, three of the SDGs (No.2 Zero Hunger,
No.6 Clean Water and Sanitation, No.14 Life Below Water) apply more directly to
the aquaculture industry than others.
Goal 2 concerns the end of hunger, food security, improved nutrition and to pro-
mote sustainable agriculture. The global population is expected to reach 9.7 billion
by 2050, and a growing population will require more nutritious proteins, like fish.
Fish are considered very healthy because it contains all essential amino acids, essen-
tial fats (e.g. omega 3), vitamins and minerals. Including fish in the diet can provide
several health benefits. In developing countries, where animal protein consumption
is generally low, even small amounts of fish have shown positive nutritional impacts.
The consumption of fish has increased globally over the past years, and will require
more seafood in the future to come. As there is no possibility to increase the cap-
ture of the currently fished stocks, the increase of fish protein needs to come from
aquaculture production. The feed conversion ratio (FCR) is the amount of feed an
animal requires to gain a kilogram of body weight. A fish needs in average a little
more than 1.1 pound feed to produce 1 pound body weight (Figure 1.2). Figure 1.2
gives a general overview of the average ratios.

This makes proteins produced from aquaculture the most efficient way to produce
animal proteins, and thus saves the amount of feed needed for production.
Goal 6 concerns the access to safe water and sanitation and sustainable manage-
ment of freshwater ecosystems. Several people have limited access to water supply,
and taking care of fresh water resources is essential. Unlike sea water, fresh water is
very limited as it comprises only 3% of the total water on earth. Two-thirds of it are
preserved in glaciers or unavailable for use. The major global aquaculture production
is land based, and use of fresh water is extensive. With the growing population, there
is an increasing demand for freshwater and an increasing demand for aquatic food,
indicating that further growth in aquaculture cannot be based on more freshwater use.
Increasing temperatures due to climate change could increase the evaporation rate
of water and the melting of glaciers, making fresh water even more scarce. Marine
aquaculture needs to be developed and expanded to close the gap between the supply
and demand for aquatic products. With climate change and environmental concerns
in mind, the growth must be in a sustainable manner.

Goal 14 concerns the sustainable use of the oceans, seas and marine resources.
The capture of marine fish stocks has been kept stable at approximately 80 million
tonnes annually. Wild capture (of assessed stocks) are in many cases driven to their
limits. From the total numbers of stocks assessed in 2013, 58.1% were fully fished, and
31.4% were overfished. Growth in the current capture fisheries is therefore not likely
to occur in the future. The commercial fisheries have other major issues as well: by
catch, which is non-targeted and undersized organisms that are generally discarded
by the fishermen, damage to the seabed by trawling activities, and the loss of fishing
gear causing destructive ghost fishing. The aquaculture production can relieve some
of the pressure on the fished stocks and its environmental concerns. However, aqua-
culture production that interferes with the environment (e.g. net pens, floating ropes
etc.) must keep a practice that does not cause additional damage to the surrounding
environment.
The commercial fishery has played an important economic role for people living
in coastal environments, e.g. Africa with the second largest fishing fleet (Asia 75%
and Africa 15%). However, marine aquaculture in Africa is almost non-existent, and
could possibly be an additional source of income and an important source of nutrition
if established.
In order to provide a sufficient supply to the fish market, many cage designs and
models have been developed and are commercially available. Developing the open fish
nets was one of the efficient and economic methods for solving the problem. However,
the open fish nets are facing another challenge which are the external environmental
effects such as sea lice. The fish farming activity will cause high sea lice concentration.
And this will lead to higher mortality rates of the fish. In the meanwhile, the open
fish nets are vulnerable to damage and cause the escape of the fish. The escaped fish
may disrupt the wild fish gene-pool by breeding with nearby wild fish [LOS11]. As
response to the problem, recently the aquaculture industry has focused increasingly
on floating closed cages, and currently there are several working prototypes, and even
more in the planning phase [SIN].
Fish farming in floating closed cages has gained increased interest in the Nor-
wegian aquaculture industry, both due to reported promising results relative to key
production parameters as growth, mortality and fish health and due to access to new
licenses for production [SIN]. Problems such as fish escaping, organic waste and of
course salmon lice can be solved by using closed fish cage.

However, the closed fish cage has also met some challenges because of the unex-
pected wave induced effects and loads. Due to the limited knowledge on how the
closed fish cages behave, the cages may have some damage which will lead to the risk
of fish escapes. The closed fish cage will experience the phenomenon of sloshing. This
is a resonant problem where large wave motion can be generated inside the closed
fish cage at certain wave periods, which may greatly influence the behaviour of the
structure.
1.2
Scope and Methodology
In this thesis, the hydrodynamic behavior of an closed upright circular cylindrical fish
cage are investigated by considering the interior and exterior wave effects simulation.
The motion of the cage may excite the resonant sloshing of the liquid in the cage. In
return, the fluid dynamics in the cage will have an influence on the motion of the
cage. The objective of the thesis is to develop the solution for fluid sloshing effect
inside the cage and the linear wave effect outside the cage. These two effects will be
considered separately first, and then the effects are coupled together to simulate the
cage response in the real case.
1.2.1
Interior sloshing in the closed cage
The thesis uses the linear and nonlinear multimodal theories provided in the text
book Sloshing [FT09] for interior sloshing effect. Although the exterior wave will be
assumed relatively small, the interior fluid motion may be rather violent. Therefore,
the linear sloshing theory can not satisfy the case that the natural modes of the
interior fluid have been excited. To solve this, the nonlinear sloshing multimodal
theory will be presented for the resonance system. Sloshing [FT09] can provide an
analytical solution for developing both linear and nonlinear time domain solver.

Exterior hydrodynamics
Even though the closed fish cage may be designed to survive in some severe sea
conditions, the cage will be placed in a relatively calm sea. Therefore, the linear
potential theory can be applied for denoting the external hydrodynamic problems.
On the other hands, interior sloshing induced by the motion of the fish cage may also

‚ÄĘ Obtain the linear hydrodynamic coefficients in terms of added mass, damping
and excitation forces through a frequency-domain in WAMIT.
‚ÄĘ Use a time domain method based on Cummins equations [Cum62] to obtain the
motion of the cage. DTUMotionSimulator [Harry.B 2018] shall be used for this
purpose. The reason for a time domain solver should be used is that we want to
couple it with the nonlinear time-domain sloshing solver that will be developed
in this project.
1.2.3
Coupling of exterior and interior hydrodynamic problem
The last step in this thesis will couple the DTUMotionSimulator with the nonlinear
sloshing solver based on multimodal theory. The fourth order Runge-Kutta method
will be applied to simulate the motions and the velocities of the cage in time do-
main. DTUMotionSimulator is able to calculate the acceleration of each time step
which means the function derivative can be provided in Runge-Kutta method. And
the sloshing solver will be substituted into DTUMotionSimulator for calculating the
acceleration.

Structure of the thesis
Chapter 2 presents the coordinate system for analyzing the closed fish cage and the
formula of the Boundary Value Problem for interior sloshing problem.
Generally,

Therefore, the difference for the governing equations for these two solutions will be
presented. And we will linearize the governing equations for linear sloshing problem.
Chapter 3 develops linear sloshing solution by linear multimodal theory. First, the
linear natural sloshing modes ŌÜj(x, y, z) will be described for an upright circular cylin-
drical cage. Next, the generalized coordinates ő≤j(t) and Rj(t) will be presented with

and free surface elevation ő∂(x, y, t) can be explained with these functions. Other solu-

cage. To verify the result, another analytical method proposed by Liquid Sloshing
Dynamics [Ibr05] will be used.

Chapter 4 describes nonlinear multimodal theory. First, the reason of the lim-
itation of the linear multimodal theory has been presented. The limitation mainly
reveals when the assumed excitation frequency is close to the cage natural frequen-
cies. First, we start with redefining the Boundary Value Problem with taking into
account all the nonlinear terms. Next, the multimodal theory for nonlinear sloshing
will be developed. However, not all the terms in the general equation for multimodal
method will be used in the final case. Moiseev provides an ordering system which
we can use to reduce the infinite dimensional nonlinear modal equations to finite di-
mensional form. The surface elevation and hydrodynamic loads will also be presented
based on the nonlinear theory. To verify the accuracy of the solution between linear
and nonlinear solver, the prescribed cage motions will be assumed to avoid causing
resonance of the system. Therefore, the nonlinear terms effect will be reduced which
means a comparison between linear solution with nonlinear solution can be developed.
Chapter 5 includes the exterior wave effect on the cage based on linear wave theory.
The model data is obtained from Aquafarm Equipment. The exterior hydrodynamic
coefficients will be calculated in frequency domain in WAMIT. However, we will de-
velop the solution for coupling the exterior and interior wave effects in next chapter
in time domain. Therefore, a MATLAB script package DTUMotionSimulator will
be used to transform the solution into time domain with input regular waves. The
verification includes the comparison for response amplitude operators (RAO) of the
motion in frequency domain provided by WAMIT and DTUMotionSimulator. With
a series of input waves with different frequencies, the RAO results are supposed to
be the same for WAMIT and DTUMotionSimulator.
Chapter 6 contains the coupling process between the interior sloshing problems
and exterior hydrodynamics. The exterior causes the motions of the cage which will
lead to response of the interior fluid. At the meanwhile, the sloshing inside the cage
will react on the cage which will contribute to the cage motion. And the swirling
wave effects will be considered since the swirling wave may have a great influence to
the system.
Chapter 7 outlines the conclusive summary and recommendations for further work.

Solution
The linear sloshing solutions are developed by applying the governing equation and
boundary conditions in Section 2.3. The solution can be deduced by the velocity po-
tential Ōē(x, y, z, t). To visualize the sloshing behaviour, we will need the free surface
elevation ő∂(x, y, t). The theory in this section is based on textbook Sloshing [FT09].
For developing the liquid sloshing inside the circular cylinder cage, we will need
analytical expressions for the velocity potential Ōē(x, y, z, t) which are derived from
multimodal theory. In the multimodal theory, the velocity potential will be decom-
posed into two parts. The first part incorporates the Neumann boundary conditions
due to cage motions. The second part consists of a linear superposition of an infinite
number of natural modes and generalized coordinates. Generally speaking, the slosh-
ing natural modes decide the velocity potential distribution in space Ōē(x, y, z). And
the generalized coordinates denote the time component corresponding to each mode.
The first section in this Chapter will solve the sloshing natural modes. The second
section will explain the linear multimodal theory. In these two sections, there is not
damping effect inside the cage as we have assumed an ideal flow inside the cage. In
the third section, the hydrodynamic forces and moments will be calculated for the
circular cylinder. And in the forth section, we will add a small damping effect to the
system. In reality, boundary layers along the cage wall, internal structures in the cage
and wave breaking etc. may contribute to the damping effects. In the fifth section,
we will use a steady-state analytical solution of an upright circular cylindrical cage
under forced oscillations given in the text book Liquid Sloshing Dynamics [Ibr05] to
verify the accuracy of the linear multimodal solution.

5.1.1
Diffraction effect
The diffraction effect considers the forces and moments when the cage is restrained
from oscillating and there are incident waves. Because of the incident waves, the
hydrodynamic loads in diffraction effect are wave excitation loads. The loads are
composed of Froude-Kriloff and diffraction forces and moments
5.1.2
Radiation effect
The radiation effect is the forces and moments on the cage by assuming the cage is
forced to oscillate with the wave excitation frequency. Although there is no incident
wave, the forced motion of cage will generate radiating wave. Therefore, the fluid
pressure on the surface of cage will be constantly changed depends on time. And the
fluid pressure can be integrated on the surface of the cage to obtain the corresponding
added mass and damping force. Thus, the hydrodynamic loads that will be considered
in radiation effect are added mass, damping and restoring terms. The radiation force
is therefore given as:

like position or acceleration of the object.

7.1
Conclusion
In the present work, an early investigation of the forced moving closed circular cylin-
drical fish cage has been developed to simulate cage motions. The work carried out
an analytical solution for interior sloshing and a numerical results for exterior hydro-
dynamics. The effects have been coupled together for simulating cage response.
The interior liquid sloshing problems are studied in details. The liquid behaviour
was presented by assuming linear potential theory at first. The limitation of the lin-
ear theory will be reclaimed by nonlinear multimodal theory. Violent liquid sloshing
behaviour occurs when the excitation frequency is around 95 to 105 percent of the
cage natural frequency of the primary mode. In this excitation frequency range, the
closed cage is in resonant state, but swirling wave and chaos wave may occur when
the cage has a very small disturbance.
Hydrodynamic coefficients for exterior wave have been extracted in frequency
domain from WAMIT with specific models. DTUMotionSimulator substitutes the
coefficients into Cummins equation [Cum62] with prescribed incident wave in time
domain. The motion equation for the closed cage has been developed with coupling
the interior sloshing multimodal theory and exterior hydrodynamics.
The natural frequency for the specific closed cage is 0.9339 rad‚ąí1 for the primary
mode. When the wave frequency is close to the cage natural frequency, the hydrody-
namic loads induced by interior sloshing is relatively large because of the resonance.
And the hydrodynamic loads caused by the swirling wave are even bigger than the
loads caused by planar wave. Swirling wave has been proved that it will lead to a
violent closed cage response in Figure 4.5. This intense hydrodynamic loads may be
the reason that causes a large movement of the closed fish cage indicated in Figure 6.5.
Therefore, the designer should try to avoid the cage natural frequency approaching
to the wave frequencies. And the solution for strengthening the structural to counter
the occurrence of the swirling waves.

7.2
Future work
Suggestions to possible extensions of the present work are:
‚ÄĘ The damping coefficient inside the cage should be obtained as damping is an
important factor to the liquid sloshing problem. In this thesis, the damping
coefficient inside the cage is a hypothetical value. Without proper damping
coefficient, we can not predict when the transient effect of the liquid will be
damped out. One could carry out a result based on a viscous fluid inside the
cage and consider the wave breaking effect.
‚ÄĘ Developing the formal and complete nonlinear moment formulations for nonlin-
ear sloshing solver. The work proves that the system is very sensitive to the
hydrodynamic loads induced by interior liquid sloshing. One should be very
careful when dealing with the dimensional problems in coupling process.
‚ÄĘ The hydrodynamic loads on the closed fish cage shall be reported, since now
only the movement of the cage has been calculated. In order to ensure the
stability and reliability of the closed fish cage, we need to simulate the combined
hydrodynamic loads on the closed fish cage for different sea states. The future
work of the fish cage may be combined with structure assessment.
‚ÄĘ Some experiments about an upright circular cylindrical cage could be worked
out for verifying the accuracy of the simulation. The natural frequencies of the
cage could also be tested to compare with the analytical results.
‚ÄĘ The solution for eliminating the swirling wave can be developed. For exam-
ple, adding bulkhead inside the closed cage may reduce occurrence rate of the
swirling waves.

Linear sloshing multimodal theory:MATLAB code
Main code
The MATLAB scripts for solving linear liquid sloshing problem ares attached. The
code can provide solution for linear natural modes corresponding to the natural fre-
quencies with each mode number. The free surface elevation has also been carried
out by prescribing the excitation motions. There are two solutions for free surface
elevation: one is the animation of the liquid behaviour which has continuous playback
in the time history and another one is the free surface elevation measured by the set
probe. The hydrodynamic loads are also output data and it can be used to compare
the solution by Liquid Sloshing Dynamics [Ibr05].

Nonlinear sloshing multimodal theory:MATLAB code
Main code
This MATLAB script solves the nonlinear multimodal theory.
It is able to carry
out the solution for the wave free surface elevation in the upright circular cylindrical
tank and the natural frequencies. It should be mentioned that all the geometrical
terms are scaled by the radius of the tank. The solution for the ODE solver has been
worked out in Chapter 4. However, there are two ODE solvers have been investigated
in the code: ODE45 and ODE15s. Specify the mass matrix using the Mass option in
ODE15s solver. The disturbance which may induce the different flow regimes was set
as an initial value of the generalized coordinates. The hydrodynamic loads will also be
carried out. But it should be mentioned that as there is no effective formulations for
the moments, the moments calculation will still follow the linear general formulations.
