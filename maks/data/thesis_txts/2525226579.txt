Abstract 
This study has found that for attaining the SDGs by 2030 both the private and public sector must start 
work with the SDGs now. This study has been conducted by establishing the state of art for the 
research field and interviews with two different businesses has been conducted. The interviews were 
conducted with an SME and a large company in different sectors with differentiated knowledge of 
the SDGs. The SME have not worked with the SDGs prior to this study while the big enterprise is 
working with the SDGs and have been doing so since 2018.  
 
From the study it has been found that businesses may use the SDGs product specific and general for 
their whole business. Companies must be aware of the complexity of the SDGs when they are working 
with them. Corporations should be aware of the boundaries for their study, cherry-picking, and the 
interconnections of the SDGs when they work with them. Especially, when reporting their findings. 
 
Corporations can work with the SDGs in different ways depending on their size. Big enterprises have 
financial capabilities which SMEs normally does not have. They are therefore enabled to work more 
structured and thorough with the SDGs than the SMEs. Both the SMEs and the big companies will 
benefit from using a guide for structure and transparency in the work with the SDGs, but the existing 
guides are not usable for all companies. The usability of the guides differentiates as some of the 
guides provide an overview while others provide insight in which SDGs the business is working on 
and which they should prioritize.  
 
Based on the finding it is recommended that businesses work structured with the SDGs and report 
their activities in the attempt of attaining the Sustainable Development Goals.

Resume 
Formålet med dette studie har været at undersøge, hvordan firmaer kan implementere FN’s 
Verdensmål i deres forretninger. Det er blevet fundet, at firmaerne, er nødt til at begynde at arbejde 
med dem, hvis FN’s verdensmål, skal have en chance for at blive opnået inden 2030. Dette studie er 
delt i to dele, den første del er redegørende, hvor state of the art identificeres. Det ligger baggrund 
for hvad firmaer nu kan gøre. Den anden del er en interviewdel - Der er foretaget to interviews, et 
med et lille firma og et med et stort firma. De to firmaer blev valgt for at undersøge forskellene 
mellem store og små virksomheder og dertil var deres tidligere engagement til FN’s verdensmål også 
af interesse. Det lille firma har ikke arbejdet med målene, hvor det store firma har arbejdet med dem 
siden 2018.  
 
Igennem projektet er det blevet identificeret at firmaer kan benytte FN’s verdensmål på to måder. 
Den ene måde dækker de alle deres aktiviteter, hvor de i den anden metode dækker deres produkter 
specifikt. De to metoder kan benyttes til forskellige ting, den første giver et overblik over firmaet 
samlede indvirkninger. Den produkt specifikke analyse kan bruges til salgsmateriale, på samme måde 
som carbon-footprint kan benyttes. Uanset hvilken metode de benytter eller hvis de gør begge dele, 
så er firmaerne nødt til at være opmærksomme på afgrænsningerne i deres undersøgelse, potentialet 
for at de benytter cherry-picking samt de interne sammenhænge, der er i målene. Hvis disse aspekter 
ikke håndteres, kan firmaers analyse blive mangelfuld og uigennemsigtig.  
 
Firmaers arbejde med FN’s verdensmål er afhængig af deres størrelse. Store firmaer har generelt, til 
forskel med små firmaer, større økonomisk frihed til at arbejde med bæredygtighedsudfordringer. 
Uagtet firmaernes økonomiske muligheder vil firmaers arbejde med FN’s verdensmål generelt blive 
mere gennemskueligt og struktureret hvis de benytter en form for guide. Det eneste problem med de 
eksisterende guider er at de ikke nødvendigvis er dækkende og brugbare for firmaer. De tydeligste 
forskelle på de guiderne, der er identificeret i dette studie, er at nogle af dem er meget generelle og 
giver brugeren et overblik over hvilke mål de arbejder med. Den anden type giver brugeren 
retningslinjer, for hvad de kan arbejde videre med og eventuelt hvilke mål, der er af betydning for 
den sektor, som de arbejder i.  
 
Det er anbefalet af firmaer arbejder med FN’s verdensmål struktureret samt at de rapportere deres 
indvirkning, både positive og negative, af FN’s verdensmål.

1. Introduction 
This chapter will describe the background for the following project. The research question laying 
ground for this project will be outlined and scoping and boundaries for the study will thereafter be 
defined.  
 
1.1. The UN’s Sustainable Development Goals 
The Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) were defined by the UN in 2015. The SDGs aims are 
described as: “a universal call to action to end poverty, protect the planet and ensure that all people 
enjoy peace and prosperity by 2030.” (UNDP, 2020). The road towards the world described by the 
UNDP can be reached by obtaining the 17 SDGs, that covers both the economic, social, and 
environmental sustainability for all of mankind. The SDGs are a set of goals which require, that not 
only, the politicians of the UN, the EU, nor individual governments come together. Reaching the 
SDGs require contribution by the private sector. Businesses will require motivation to work with the 
SDGs and working with these should not compromise the business of business. The business of 
business is the economic sustainability. If the economic sustainability is not achieved, focus on the 
two other types of sustainability are not prioritized, as the status of the business is not stable. It is 
therefore of the essence that the business will benefit by spending time and money on the social and 
environmental sustainability. According to the Business and Sustainable Development Commission 
(BSDC) there are a potential of at least 12 trillion USD in market opportunities within four sectors: 
food and agriculture, cities, energy and materials, and health and well-being (Business & Sustainable 
Development Commission, 2017). Therefore, the vast estimated economic benefits are not to be 
neglected by companies. It is therefore of relevance for businesses to consider the SDGs in their 
strategies and implement them. 
 
1.2. Study aim 
The aim of this study is to identify in what way businesses can use the SDGs in their strategies. This 
will be done by investigating the state of the art within the literature. These findings will be evaluated 
and pitfalls and opportunities in the implementation will be discussed. The State of Art will be 
followed by interviews with two different businesses different in sectors and size. The understanding 
of how the SDGs can be implemented are described by both of them indicating both the problems 
and potentials by implementing the SDGs. The findings from the State of Art and the interviews will

be summarized, and the holes and potentials for implementation of the SDGs will be discussed. The 
findings from the discussion will be summarized and concluded on.  
  
1.3. Research Questions 
The main research question of this project is: 
1) How does SDG implementation influence businesses? 
 
To investigate the main research question a set of sub-questions will be studied. 
2) What may the SDGs be used for in businesses? 
3) What influence does the size of the business have for implementing the SDGs and does the 
decision level in the business affect the work with the SDGs? 
4) What are the effects of using the SDGs product-specific instead of covering the whole 
business?  
 
1.4. Boundaries 
This study is aimed towards the implementation of the SDGs in the private sector, excluding the 
public sector. This choice has been taken as the SDG, in its core, are directed towards the public 
sector where work already is conducted within that field.  
 
The two interviews, presented in chapter 3, provide insight in how business perceive the work with 
the SDGs and relevance for their business. Both businesses have their main offices in Denmark, but 
they have activities around in the world. The implementation of the SDGs is therefore not just limited 
to identification of the SDGs of relevance in Denmark.

2.1. The Sustainable Development Goals 
This section will provide insight in the history of the SDGs, what they are and how they are designed.  
 
  The Millennium and Sustainable Development Goals 
The United Nations defined in 2000 the Millennium Development Goal (MDG) which consisted of a 
total of 8 goals. All 8 goals relate to health and was set up such that improvements in health would 
be gained by improving one of the MDGs. In the same way improvements in health would also impact 
the MDGs. (WHO, 2019) The MDGs where planned to be reached by 2015. One of the differences 
between the MDGs and the SDGs is that the MDGs in general are reduction goals, for instance MDG 
1 target 1.A “Halve, between 1990 and 2015, the proportion of people whose income is less than 
$1.25 a day” while the goals in general for the SDGs are to be 100% obtained before the goal is 
==PAGE:11==
 
Page 4 of 59 
 
achieved. (UN, 2000) The SDGs are 17 goals, seen in figure 1, which covers both economic, social 
and environmental sustainability. The SDGs are defined in another way compared to the MDGs. The 
SDG does not have a “non-existing” goal which is influenced by the achievements in the individual 
goals as the MDGs had with health, although the SDGs have interconnections. These connections 
make working with the SDGs more complicated, as it is possible to work on one SDG which impacts 
on the SDGs positive or negative impacts on other SDGs. These interconnections should be taken 
into consideration when accounting performance on the SDGs. In case the interconnections are 
forgotten or neglected the results of the work can be deceptive. 
 
 
Figure 1: Overview of the 17 different Sustainable Development Goals. (United Nations, 2015) 
 
 The design of the SDG 
The SDGs are built up by goal, targets and indicators. There are 17 goals, each goal with a number 
of targets, resulting in 169 targets. The targets have 244 indicators, although a few of those indicators 
are used multiple places. This results in 232 unique indicators. (UNDS and DESA, 2019) Although 
the SDGs are relevant for the entire globe some of them are directed towards development countries 
while others are relevant for both development countries and industrial countries. This is of relevance 
==PAGE:12==
 
Page 5 of 59 
 
as some activities in the development countries are directed by businesses in industrial countries, 
meaning that the demand from the industrial countries affects the development countries. For an 
example the CO2-eq emission has the highest impact on the poorest while they are the ones who has 
little-too-zero saying in how the worlds countries currently are managing the managing of the 
emissions. (Collins and Zheng, 2015) It is therefore other’s decisions which impacts the poorest 
people while they must live with the consequences of the decision made by others. It is therefore of 
importance to recognize that the SDG are designed for all the world’s population, not just for the poor 
nor for the rich. Due to this it is of importance that everybody take part in achieving these 17 goals.  
  
2.2. The Relevance of the SDGs for Business 
This section will describe the need of action from the private sector for obtaining the SDGs. This will 
be described with a short overview of what the current state of sustainability is in Denmark followed 
by a portrayal of the sustainability for businesses and why it is meaningful for businesses to value 
sustainability in their businesses.  
 
 Sustainability action in Denmark 
The Danish Governments climate change goal is that by 2030 the CO2-eq emissions are reduced by 
70% compared to the level in 1990 (Finansministeriet, 2019). This can be obtained by work in SDG 
7, 9, 11, and 12 among others. Obtaining the goals cannot be done without the private sector. It is 
therefore of interest to investigate how corporations can implement the SDG in their businesses for 
obtaining SDGs while maintaining the business of business.  
 
As the SDGs are designed for the whole population of the earth, they are not taking the relevance of 
the individual countries into consideration. This means that some of the goals and targets are not 
relevant for all areas. Therefore, it may be difficult to work with some of the SDGs in industrial 
countries as the relevance are limited. According to the Legal Affairs Committee (Retsudvalget - 
REU) from the Danish Parliament the goals for SDG 1 No poverty are almost accomplished from a 
Danish point of view. Examples with target 1 and 2 in SDG 1 are portrayed in ways where Denmark 
could improve the work for SDG 1. Target 1 is of relevance even though that Denmark is one of the 
countries, which are living up to the commitment of donating 0.7 % of the GNI (Gross National 
Income) as assistance for development countries. REU finds that the money should be directed to 
help marginalized and poor people in the world. (Retsudvalget, 2015) The description of target 1.2 
==PAGE:13==
 
Page 6 of 59 
 
makes the target relevant as it is currently not possible to determine if the target is accomplished. The 
wording of the target is as following: 
 
“By 2030, reduce at least by half the proportion of men, women and children of all ages living in 
poverty in all its dimensions according to national definitions.” (IAEG-SDGs, 2016) 
 
Target 1.2 is of relevance in Denmark since the official poverty line was removed by the Danish 
government in 2015. It is therefore currently not possible to determine the amount of people in 
Denmark who are living in poverty. Statistics Denmark got the assignment to follow up on the 
accomplishments on the goals and targets in Denmark. They have therefore defined poverty for SDG 
1.2. Meaning that it is possible to follow up on the evolvement of poverty even though there are no 
official line of poverty. (Danmarks Statistik, 2018) 
 
The SDGs are designed to empower the poorest and most vulnerable in the world. It is therefore not 
all the targets and indicators which makes sense to prioritize from a Danish point of view. An example 
of targets which is found not to be of relevance in Denmark could be target 1.1. Due to the wording 
of the target: 
 
“By 2030, eradicate extreme poverty for all people everywhere, currently measured as people living 
on less than $1.25 a day.” (IAEG-SDGs, 2016) 
 
This target is not a problem for the Danish population, but it is a global issue which Denmark by its 
commitments works on, as described earlier.  
 
 Triple bottom line 
The triple bottom line (TBL) is a concept which is introduced in sustainability thinking of product 
development. TBL was defined by John Elkington in 1998. TBL describes how the economic, social 
and environmental sustainability relates to each other within accounting. The aim for businesses is to 
have a sustainable business (i.e. the business of business) and it is not sustainable to neglect the 
societal and environmental aspects of the business as those two factors impacts the business. TBL, 
seen in figure 2, is in other words were it is not just economic sustainable but also social and 
environmental sustainable for a business. (Elkington, 1998) The areas where only two of the three 
==PAGE:14==
 
Page 7 of 59 
 
sustainability factors overlap are; bearable, equitable, and viable. Bearable is the overlap between 
the environmental and social sustainability. The business of business is not meet and it is therefore 
not economic sustainable. Of those three overlaps two of them are good business following the 
concept of business of business. The bearable are not a good business model as it is only overlapping 
with environmental and social sustainability whereas the two other overlaps equitable, and viable 
both include the economic sustainability. Equitable have an overlap with economic and social and 
viable have overlap between environment and economic. The equitable and viable are therefore both 
reasonable approaches, while maintaining business of business to use from a business perspective as 
they have a focus on the economic sustainability.  
 
Figure 2: Depicting of the triple bottom line with its intersections and overlaps. (The Fun Entrepreneur, 2019) 
 
 SDGs for businesses  
The SDGs are in its core directed towards governments and states, however it is necessary that 
businesses also contributes if the goals are to be obtained. (GRI, UN Global Compact and WBCSD, 
2019) The fact that the SDGs are not described for businesses makes it problematic for businesses to 
be a driving factor in the work of attaining the SDGs. In addition to the lack of description, businesses

also need guidance in why they should invest time and money in implementing the SDGs into their 
business plans. Additionally, some businesses might need additional incitement for considering the 
SDGs. 
 
It should be mentioned that the size of businesses is of importance when working with initiatives like 
the SDGs. Some aspects will be easier for small/medium enterprises (SMEs) while other things are 
possible for the large enterprises. There are, in addition to the size of the business, different actors, 
who can impact the work on the SDGs. The different actors who may influence products and services

Motivators for the work with the SDGs: 
Some businesses need motivation before they will work with the SDGs. The SMEs has the possibility 
to make changes internally quite easy. One of the obstacles they face for making changes internally 
are the economic aspects. They might not have the economic capacity to execute major changes. 
According to the Business and Sustainable Development Commission (BSDC) businesses can earn 
money by implementing the SDGs in their business strategies, with a potential earning of 12 trillion 
USD. Those potential earnings can come from the savings the business can gain by optimizing their 
processes. (Business & Sustainable Development Commission, 2017) A study conducted in 2014 by 
Eccles, Ioannou and Serafeim found that an investment placed in 1993 would have generated more 
money when invested in businesses who prioritized sustainability high compared to businesses that 
placed little focus on sustainability. The sample size of their study was 90 businesses in each of the 
categories, high and low sustainability groups. The study found that an investment of 1 USD in 1993 
would over 17 years have grown to respectively 22.60 USD and 15.40 USD including inflation. 
(Eccles, Ioannou and Serafeim, 2014) The difference of 7.20 USD per invested USD should not be 
neglected, as it indicates that prioritizing sustainability is not only a good idea it is also good business. 
 
The way SMEs can work with the SDGs is by focusing on their products and make them as efficient 
as possible. While making sure the product is as effective as possible, they should be aware of the 
impact their product has from cradle to grave, depicted in figure 4. From the figure the raw material 
covers both extraction and processing of the raw materials that are to be used in the product, these 
materials are transported to the production facility where the product is produced, from there the 
product is transported and it goes into the use phase and after use it goes to end of life, which for 
instance can be recycling, incineration, or landfilling. Like a Life Cycle assessment (LCA) a product 
can affect some SDGs in one part of its life cycle and in other parts of its lifetime. It could have a 
negative impact, or impact other SDGs both in a positive and/or negative way. It is therefore of 
importance that businesses cover the whole life cycle of their products or services when considering 
the SDGs in relation to sustainability of the product. (Hauschild, Rosenbaum and Olsen, 2014)

SMEs who sell their services to other enterprises, can be influenced by client requirements. This may 
encourage SMEs to work with the SDGs. Given that the market is going to be the primary influencer 
the market will set the agenda. This could influence legislation and thereby set minimum requirements 
for the sustainability for different sectors. This require that the SMEs report their actions in such a 
way the clients can evaluate their performance. This will increase the awareness of the SDGs, which 
is of importance in the endeavor of obtaining the SDGs.  
 
In contrast to SMEs, big enterprises can work on the SDGs in various ways, which the SMEs are not 
able to, due to their size and impact. This can be done indirectly by influencing their competitors to 
meet a minimum requirement, and they can set requirements to their suppliers. The internal work can 
in some organizations be troublesome as decisions made on management level may be difficult to 
understand for the shop floor worker. The ideas might therefore not be executed as effective as 
intended. Thus, it is of important that the ideas are thought out and do not propose unnecessary 
inconvenience for the employees. In case the ideas are too inconvenient the implementation of the 
SDGs might not be as effective. Additionally, employees might not follow the ideas from the 
management. The staff on the floor might therefore not see the need of the goal(s) set by the 
management. Commitment towards the goals could be empowered by having the employees 
understand the reasoning for implementing the goals. This is one of the potential issues big enterprises 
can have as the distance between the different levels within the business are larger compared to SMEs. 
It is therefore important that management communicates with its employees when they implement 
changes in their work-ways.  
 
As described communication is of importance as it may motivate and educate the employees on the 
SDG work. A schematic approach to the communication within businesses and organizations can be 
seen in figure 5. Generally, two different ways can be considered: The top-down and the bottom-up 
communication.  
1) The top-down communication: The management takes decisions and the information travels 
down through the business. The top-down communication is a great way of stream-lining 
decisions, but issues may occur if the communication is only one-way. If top-down one-way 
communication is used there will be a lack of feed-back from the shop floor to the 
management. A consequence of this is that decisions made on the management level might 
==PAGE:18==
 
Page 11 of 59 
 
end up being based on thoughts and ideas instead of data and information. With the feedback 
of information, the management is enabled to make meaningful decisions and help their 
employees in their daily work.  
2) The bottom-up communication: Goes from the shop floor to the management level. If the 
bottom-up communication is one-way communication the information, the management will 
be provided with might be lacking and key information for their decision might not arrive to 
them. Thus, decisions might be taken without the necessary knowledge.  
 
Communication can motivate and educate the employees of the importance of the SDGs, but it is vital 
that businesses acknowledges that clear communication with their employees are required if the SDGs 
are to be implemented throughout the whole organization. Additionally, the best results are obtained, 
when the employees are motivated in the SDGs.

Annual reports

SMEs 
In general, no legal requirements for SMEs relates to the presentation of social responsibility they are 
doing. Even though it is not required, it can be a choice by the company to have a clear communication 
on their activities related to both social and environmental issues. By having a strong profile within 
one or both areas, the SME can differentiate themselves from other companies in the same market. 
By doing so the SMEs can enhance their business. This can be through their position in the market, 
differentiation in the product(s), reputation and legitimacy, make the company a place where new 
employees want to work and keep the employees who are already in the company. (Erhvervsstyrelsen, 
2019) In other words, businesses have multiple reasons for why focus on the social and environmental 
aspects are important. It can be for internal matters and awareness of the business or it can be used 
externally as they can take either a specific part of the market by differentiating their products from 
others in the market, which may be an important factor for some consumers. Corporations who are 
providing a service for other companies can use both the social and environmental aspects in their 
products as an encouragement for implementing their product in the client’s facility/ies. One of the 
issues SMEs can encounter when they approach potential customers. Is that, it is not the employees 
on the floor but, it is the management who will have to be convinced that both purchase and 
implementation of the product will be beneficial for them. As the social and environmental focus can 
be used as PR material, they can also be used to convince managements that the investment in the 
product will benefit not only on the social and environmental aspect, but also on the economic, which 
normally is a deal-breaker. Optimization of a process can accompany extra benefits, such as a 
reduction of the amount of emitted CO2-eq, which can originate from lower energy consumption, or 
more efficient use of the energy. For instance, documenting both a reduction of CO2-eq and a reduction 
of energy consumption can be the arguments which can motivate management to implement new 
systems in their production line, or adapt their current procedures. These aspects may normally not 
just be based on good intentions, they will also have to be supported by economical reasoning.

performance of companies. The requirements for the annual reports are defined and it is depending

2.3. Existing Frameworks, Roadmaps, and Tools 
This section will showcase a selection of roadmaps, frameworks, and tools developed or designed for

 Frameworks 
An SDG framework is a guide for working with the SDGs. The selection covers how investors can 
use the SDGs for investing, how businesses could attain the SDGs and use them for their business, 
and an example of how municipalities in Denmark should work with the SDGs.

Toniic SDG Impact Theme Framework is made for investors, to give an overview of how investments

the SDGs in general. An impact investor might think of investing in “Mental health” as seen in figure

 Roadmaps 
The SDGs are designed for governments and countries, and it is therefore problematic for 
corporations to incorporate them directly into their business strategy. Roadmaps for different sectors 
has slowly been created. The World Business Council for Sustainable Development (WBCSD) has 
currently conducted roadmaps for three sectors; the forest sectors, Indian cement production, and the 
chemical sector. WBCSD’s work on the roadmaps is important as the roadmaps makes it easier for 
businesses to use the SDGs in their strategy. Although, there are a lot of sectors, that are not covered 
by the work of the WBCSD, there is also an interest in the academical world on developing roadmaps. 
Sturman et al. shows a map for how the African mining industry impact and are impacted by the 
SDGs. Sturman et al. states that it is complicated to implement the SDGs in this sector as there are 
no national awareness on the SDGs. This is also seen for the knowledge of the local stakeholders, 
business models, and operations related to the SDGs (Sturman et al., 2020). In 2019 a study was 
conducted by Lock-Larsen focusing on the Information & Communication Technology (ICT) sector. 
(Lock-Larsen, 2019) This paper mapped out the obstacles this sector faces while also describing the 
way the sector may approach the SDGs. The roadmaps described here, are based on lifecycle thinking 
and therefore based on the stages; raw material, production, use, and end of life. (World Business 
Council for Sustainable Development, 2018, 2019) The same holistic approach is used by others in 
designing sector roadmaps with SDGs.  
 
The structure of a roadmaps should according to WBCSD inspire and provide insight for those in the 
sector and its value chain. From figure 11 it can be seen that the key components for designing a 
roadmap is divided in six key components. Define the vision of the sector followed by a 
demonstration of which SDGs the sector affects, thereafter an identification of the opportunities the 
sector has on the SDGs is needed. The identification enables that the sector can explore how they can 
achieve the opportunities. From this exploration the identification of both barriers and accelerators 
for the work on the SDGs in the sector is needed. The final component is engaging with stakeholders 
in the value chain for the sector. These six components can be performed by using the three enablers; 
Partnerships & collaboration, Data aggregation, and Innovation, also presented in figure 11. 
(WBCSD, 2018)

2.4. Problems with Implementation of the SDGs 
The following section describes some of the aspects, businesses may encounter while working with 
the SDGs. Being aware of these aspects are critical as decisions otherwise may be based on partial 
understanding of the SDGs.   
 
 Cherry-picking 
 
With the broad selection of frameworks and roadmaps presented in section 2.3.2 a lot of information 
about how businesses can implement the SDGs in their business are available. This large amount of 
possibilities may cause companies to react with confusion to the possibilities and may have 
difficulties in figuring out how to work with the SDGs using the existing guidelines (frameworks, 
roadmaps, and tools). One of the issues with the existing guides are found in the naming of the guides, 
which are not necessarily logical. This require that businesses have a list of the existing guides. With 
the lack of such a list, businesses might need to design their own approach for how they are using and 
eventually reporting on the SDGs.  
 
One-way businesses can report work on the SDGs is by looking at their business and then pick the 
SDGs which they find relevant. A pitfall of this approach is that some businesses might pick SDGs 
on the goal level, which is a problem as the goals are not fully explained on the goal level. It is 
therefore necessary that businesses consider at least the SDGs on the target level. In case businesses 
pick SDGs on the goal level they might pick the lowest hanging fruits when they decide on which 
SDGs they work with and they might therefore unknowingly neglect SDGs which they are impacting. 
The method of picking something intuitively is called cherry picking. Some of the consequences of 
using cherry picking is that businesses might be missing out on the SDGs which they are primarily 
working on. The business could therefore get a wrong impression of which SDGs they affect. This 
information is critical in case the business publishes the work they are doing on the SDGs as they in 
some way miss-informs the readers of those reports of what they are doing. Their readers could be 
stakeholders such as clients, investors, and the general public, who are interested in sustainability and 
the SDGs. Reports where cherry picking is used to identify SDGs may end up being un-intentional 
biased towards specific goals and/or targets depending on the analyzer’s interpretation of the 
business. With cherry picking businesses might forget to consider interconnection of the SDGs. They 
might therefore disclose partial impacts on the SDGs as they have not considered how their impacts 
impact elsewhere in the system. (GRI and UNGC, 2018) 
==PAGE:36==
 
Page 29 of 59 
 
 Interconnections of the SDGs 
The design of the SDGs enables interconnections as described in section 2.1.2 these interconnections 
mean that when a business is working on one target, they might have an impact elsewhere. These 
interconnections can be quite clear while others are a little obscure. Example of this could be with 
SDG 1“No poverty” target 1 will also impact SDG 2 “Zero hunger”, as the description of the target 
is that no one are living in extreme poverty - <1.25 USD a day. By working towards this goal, it is 
expected that people should be able to get food for them self and their family. As mentioned in section 
2.1.2 some of the SDG indicators are used for multiple targets. This indicate that there are some 
direct, easy to observe, correlation between the SDGs. A study performed Weitz using a scale, defined 
by the International Council for Science (ICS), explains the interconnections of the SDGs. The scale 
ranges from -3 to 3, which can be seen in figure 15a. It describes in which way the work performed 
on one SDG affects other SDGs. The use of the scale on the target level can be seen in figure 15b. It 
can be seen that influencing SDG 7.2 one counteracts SDG 6.6. This interaction is counteracting as 
SDG 7.2 states; “By 2030, increase substantially the share of renewable energy in the global energy 
mix“ while SDG 6.6 state; “By 2020, protect and restore water-related ecosystems, including 
mountains, forests, wetlands, rivers, aquifers and lakes”. (IAEG-SDGs, 2016) Both of these targets 
can be obtained but improvements in the increase of renewable energy will take up areas described 
in SDG 6.6 it is therefore counteracting work.

 Greenwashing 
Greenwashing is when a corporation claims that they, or their product performs in a way which is 
greener, than it actually is (Delmas and Burbano, 2011). This thought process is applicable when 
dealing with the SDG as the SDG can be “washed” in the same way. This is called SDG washing 
which describes how businesses may neglect the SDGs they impact negatively. The business will 
thereby seem like they are performing better than they are. (GRI and UNGC, 2018) 
 
Businesses must be aware of the potential of greenwashing when they work with sustainability and 
performance of either a product or a process. Terra Choice, a Canadian environmental marketing 
agency, performed in 2007 a study where they investigated the credibility of products and their 
==PAGE:39==
 
Page 32 of 59 
 
environmental claims. Based on that study and subsequently studies seven sins of greenwashing was 
defined to bring awareness of this. (TerraChoice, 2008) The seven sins are; 
1. Sin of the hidden trade-off 
2. Sin of no proof 
3. Sin of vagueness 
4. Sin of worshiping false labels 
5. Sin of irrelevance  
6. Sin of lesser of two evils 
7. Sin of fibbing  
These sins might be of relevance to investigate for businesses who are trying to cherry-pick the SDGs 
without investigating what the individual SDG stands for and what is included in it. In other word’s 
companies might receive critics if they end up arguing that they are doing something specific which 
they cannot defend or if it ends up being proven wrong when investigated.  
 
The SDGs is a relatively new concept, it is therefore likely that some corporations unknowingly 
greenwash their company or their product. According to Piche there is a general understanding that 
negative contributes to the SDGs are un-important for some companies. Businesses does in general 
prefer to describe their achievements on the SDGs which they are affecting in a positive way. (Piche, 
2018) This falls under the first of the seven sins of greenwashing. This indicates that some 
corporations are washing their profile. When they use the SDGs for this it is called SDG washing 
instead of greenwashing. Besides this there is a lack of proof as there is a lack of indicators for the 
targets. This means that some of the claim’s companies may come with while arguing they are 
working with the SDGs is not measurable, and it is therefore a grey area as the improvements is not 
measurable. This could be prevented by making a standard for when a corporation can claim that they 
are doing something and for comparability of two companies the standard should be measurable in 
some way. This may not be possible for all indicators, but it might be applicable for some. Due to the 
fact that some of the SDG are not backed up by data it is possible that a corporation could be arguing 
that they are doing something and meanwhile they are not supporting their statement (sin 3), they 
might be arguing which is irrelevant (sin 5), they might be portraying them better than their 
competitors. Although this is not a big issue in Denmark, compared to other markets, as commercials 
are not allowed to compare products in a way where they discredit or devalue a competitor’s product, 
brand, characteristic etc. (Erhvervsministeriet, 2019).  As sin 3 and 5 can happen relatively easy it is 
==PAGE:40==
 
Page 33 of 59 
 
realistic that costumers could get confused and it is therefore easy for products to hide within sin 6 as 
it is unclear for the consumers which product are the better one. Lastly sin 7 can be assumed not to 
be relevant, but it could happen if companies state “we are working with …“. This statement enables 
that a corporation can state that they are working with something in a given way which is in fact a 
lie. This can only be discovered if somebody digs into their statements and unravels the truth. In other 
words, the consumers are in the dark until this is standardized, in a way where it is transparent for 
both other companies and the common consumer what they are buying.

2.5. Summary of State of the Art 
It is important to implement the SDGs if they are to be meet by 2030. As described the SDGs are 
directed towards public sector. This makes it somewhat difficult for the private sector to adopt the 
SDGs and implement them in the current form. Each of the SDGs consist of a goal, each goal is 
assigned a number of targets and indicators. Currently not all of the targets have indicators. Providing 
indicators for all of the targets are an ongoing activity which is essential to track the progress on the 
SDGs. 
 
Businesses should invest time and resources in the work on the SDGs as there is a huge potential in 
investing in sustainability. Sustainability is obtained when the triple bottom line is meet. The business 
of business is to be attained for having a healthy business which is the economic sustainability in the 
triple bottom line. Therefore, if business of business is not attained both the environmental and social 
aspects of sustainability is difficult to obtain in a sustainable way while maintaining a business. 
Literature establishes a clear economic incentive for businesses to invest in the SDG’s and general 
sustainability. Studies have, prior to the definition of the SDG’s, found that 1 USD invested in 1993, 
over 17 years increased its value to 22.60 dollars, when invested in businesses who prioritized 
sustainability. The same one-dollar investment for a non-sustainability focused company only 
increased its value to 15.40 dollars. The financial crisis, experienced in the period 2007-09 did not 
have a significant negative impact on the financial increase, thus making it a long-term good business-
strategy to prioritize sustainability in general.     
 
There are a lot of different guidelines on how businesses can implement the SDGs, this makes it 
difficult for businesses to identify which SDG direction it is to follow. It is a significant challenge to 
businesses to identify what guidelines to follow as the individual guideline names often are non-
explanatory or needs to be known by name prior to start searching. Additionally, it is required for 
businesses to invest time in evaluating what the costs, deficiencies and usability’s, are for the 
individual guides. Lack of awareness can make business design their own way of implementing the 
SDGs. When businesses design their own approach there is a risk of cherry-picking and the 
interconnections of the SDGs may knowingly or un-knowingly be neglected. This can eventually lead 
to SDG washing which is not the intention of how business should use the SDGs.

3.  Communication with businesses  
This section will provide a brief overview of interviews conducted though the project period. Two 
interviews have been carried out in this project. The first interview presented is with a Sustainability 
Project Leader from Haldor Topsøe A/S and the other interview was conducted with the CFO and VP 
of Specshell ApS. Both interviews provided insight in how the two companies work or could work 
with the SDGs.  
 
Interview Methodology  
The methodology of interviews can be divided into three different types, there are the structured, 
unstructured, and semi-structured interviews. A structured interview is characterized by a firm 
structure with predetermined questions, where all the interviewed are to be asked in the same 
questions in the same order, with no additional follow up questions. Results obtained through the 
structured interview is therefore based on the questions. The unstructured interview is characterized 
by being a dialogue with no predetermined questions. This kind of interviews is considered to be the 
most biased and therefore the most unreliable with regards to the results. Lastly the semi-structured 
interview is a mix between the structured and unstructured. There will be some predetermined 
questions and additional questions can be added during the interview. (Research Methodology, 2019) 
 
The semi-structured interview methodology is used for the performed interviews in this project. The 
structure of the interviews started out with a presentation of the business by the person representing 
the firm who provided information about the number of employees, starting year, and what the 
business is producing. This was followed by the fixed questions;  
- 
Did the business work with the MDGs? 
- 
Has the business been working with other kinds of sustainability reports, for instance CSR? 
- 
Has the business knowledge of the SDGs? 
o If yes; why did the business start looking into it? 
o If no; what are the reasoning for not considering or working with the SDGs? 
- 
What are the potentials for the business from their point of view? 
- 
Which goals do the business or would the business work with? 
o If the business is working with the goals which goal are they working with and why 
those goals? 
==PAGE:47==
 
Page 40 of 59 
 
o What level of detail, goal, target, and/or indicator, will the business work on the 
SDGs? 
 
The interviews continued as a dialogue after the defined questions with the potential for additional 
information that might be of relevance for the study were discussed. Some of the fixed questions were 
investigated further during the dialogue if the answer to the questions needed clarification or the 
answer should be extended. The full extent of the interviews with questions and answers can be found 
in Appendix 1 and 2. 
 
3.1. Specshell ApS 
Specshell ApS started in 2012 with a granted patent in 2014. Specshell is an SME with 18 employees 
as of 2019. With one product, a measurement device used in a black box process in the mashing 
process in beer production, Specshell is a niche business. Specshell is currently working on expanding 
their business into the bio-ethanol industry.  
 
 Summary of the interview with Specshell ApS 
Specshell is producing InfraRed-measurement equipment used for optimization in the mashing 
process in beer production. They are primarily working with big enterprises within the market of beer 
production, as the product is too expensive for micro-breweries. Currently they have not been 
focusing on sustainability, but their clients have requested data on how their product affects the 
carbon footprint of the system. Specshell was not aware of the SDGs prior to the interview. They 
found that they might be able to use the SDGs as communication and promoting tool for their product. 
As their clients are requesting data on the carbon footprinting performance they expect that in time 
there could be a demand for knowledge of how the product affects the SDGs. The businesses 
Specshell are working with have a lot of stakeholders, and there might therefore be some of those 
stakeholders. That would be interested in the information of how Specshell’s product performs based 
on the SDGs. Through the meeting and discussion, it was found that they would be working with 
SDG 12 with process optimization. Specshell were interested in continuing the work with the SDGs 
as they saw some potential for them self in working with them. When asked how they would work 
with the SDGs on either a target level or goal level, Specshell found that they would do both. This 
could be done by making material on the target level explaining what they are doing on the target 
level. This material should be used for communication with people who aware of the SDGs and how 
==PAGE:48==
 
Page 41 of 59 
 
they are designed. Using the goal level would be for general materials for customers. The benefits of 
portraying the work at the goal level is that people with little to no knowledge of the SDGs can get 
an idea of how the product performs related to the SDGs on a general level.  
 
The complexity of the SDGs where discussed at the meeting as the interview indicated that for using 
the SDGs it is necessary that to be introduced to the SDGs. There should be an explanation of how 
the SDGs are designed and what the difference is between goal, target and indicator. In the beginning 
of the meeting Specshell thought that the SDGs where the 17 goals, which can be seen when one 
visits the UN webpage for the SDGs. During the meeting the explanation of how the SDGs are 
designed with the goal, targets and indicators made Specshell aware that the SDGs are a little more 
complex and that the SDGs may be used in multiple ways. 
 
 Reflections on the interview with Specshell ApS 
Specshell has recently discovered a new business opportunity in the bio-ethanol industry, as the 
mashing process is similar to the mashing process in beer production. Their current activities are 
focused towards big brewing companies as the equipment is not affordable for micro-breweries. The 
only sustainability accounting Specshell has done relates to client requests for carbon-footprint 
calculations. These requests motivated Specshell into investigate the affect their product have on the 
carbon-footprint.  
 
Specshell were not aware of the existence and usability of the SDGs for their business. They would 
have been willing to investigate the SDGs if either their clients had requested information on the 
SDGs or if the legislation were to require information about it. A pressure from external actors would 
therefore encourage Specshell to investigate the SDGs. Specshell identified the SDGs to be of 
relevance for their business as additional sales material. Their sales material currently has generic 
values for the carbon-footprint of the product. Specshell expect that they might use the SDGs as 
encouragement for the client’s stakeholders to invest in the product. In the sales material, Specshell 
might use the SDGs in two different ways. One very general dealing with the SDGs on the goal level 
and one going into the target and indicator level. Thus, the material would depend on the client’s 
interest and awareness of the SDGs, due to the complexity of the SDGs. 
  
==PAGE:49==
 
Page 42 of 59 
 
During the interview the structure of the SDGs were explained, as the three levels - goal, target, and 
indicator – of the SDGs was not understood. For example, it was found that SDG 13 was not what 
Specshell initially had expected. It was expected that SDG 13 were about optimization of processes, 
where in fact it is more related to mitigation of climate change. The relevant SDGs for Specshell was 
thereafter briefly discussed and SDG 9 and 12 was identified to be of interest with their activities. 
Specshell specified that an intuitive guide for SDG implementation would have been beneficial for 
them. It is proposed that such a guide should be easy to understand and use in order to reduce the time 
invested in understanding the basics with SDG work.  
 
 
3.2. Haldor Topsøe A/S 
Haldor Topsøe A/S is a company which was founded in 1940 and are currently employing 2,300. 
Haldor Topsøe are mostly known for catalyst production. They are in the chemical sector and they 
work with industries such as the battery -, oil and gas -, and renewable energy industries.  
 
 Summary of the interview with Haldor Topsøe A/S 
The statements presented in this interview is the personal opinion of the interviewed person. Haldor 
Topsøe began their work on the SDGs in 2018 in collaboration with DI and other corporations 
following DI’s program. The network from DI provided Haldor Topsøe with frameworks and ideas 
generated and designed by other companies. They have been working with sustainability prior to the 
SDGs through the annual reporting they are required to perform. Haldor Topsøe expect that the SDGs 
can be used to accommodate the expectance from the world. Haldor Topsøe would like to define who 
they are when sustainability is discussed. This lack of knowledge comes from their products as they 
for instance work with oil products which is not normally seen as sustainable. Although, if they do 
not optimize those processes the impact of their work will be less sustainable. Therefore, they state 
the following questions; 
- 
What is a sustainable solution? 
- 
When is sustainability obtained? 
Haldor Topsøe thinks that the SDGs enables a common language which the business can use 
internally. This language could eventually also be used with partners, suppliers, and perhaps 
competitors on the market. The SDGs can therefore be used as sales material as it can be used as a 
common language. Haldor Topsøe experienced that their clients began requesting carbon footprints 
==PAGE:50==
 
Page 43 of 59 
 
on their products, those requests have transitioned into requests for LCAs. It is therefore expected 
that the same could happen with the SDGs simply from market pressure. Haldor Topsøe are primarily 
working with SDG 2, 3, and 7 and they work with the SDGs on the target level. One of the issues 
which Haldor Topsøe identify with the SDGs on the target levels is that some of the targets lack 
indicators. It is therefore not possible for them to track improvements on those targets. Even though 
Haldor Topsøe are working with the SDGs on the target levels there are some of the targets which 
they have changed a little at least in their mindset. For instance, with SDG 7.3 “By 2030, double the 
global rate of improvement in energy efficiency”, which Haldor Topsøe is working towards, but they 
seek improvements, not necessarily a doubling. Haldor Topsøe think that for promoting the work 
with the SDGs there is a need for push. This push can come from regulators or the market, but none 
the less the market must be mature if changes are to be put into effect.  
 
 Reflections on the interview with Haldor Topsøe A/S 
In the interview it was indicated that Haldor Topsøe always has been a business who considered 
sustainability and issues in the world. Mr. Haldor Topsøe wrote in 1992 the book “Horisonten rundt: 
om globale udfordringer” (Danish title). This book describes the issues Mr. Topsøe saw as emerging 
global problems. Among the issues Mr. Topsøe portrayed were climate, population, and poverty 
among other global issues. After the publishing of the book Mr. Topsøe managed his company in a 
way where the issues identified in the book were taken on. It was indicated from the interview that 
the work was not structured nor implemented in a way where the employees were aware that they 
were working in that direction on those goals Mr. Topsøe had set. As described in the interview 
Haldor Topsøe began working with the SDGs in collaboration with DI in 2018. Haldor Topsøe was 
introduced to different types of guides and ended up designing their own with inspiration from others. 
Haldor Topsøe is working with the SDGs where they are assessing each of the products in the 
portfolio individually, this is not normally seen. Businesses working with the SDGs do normally 
describe the whole business, at least in the material they disclose. Haldor Topsøe has done this in 
anticipation that their clients might request documentation on the SDGs in the nearest future. In the 
same way clients request LCA data on products. It is therefore expected that the clients in time will 
begin to request information on the product’s performance related to the SDGs.  
 
It has been noticed in the business world, also in Haldor Topsøe, that employees value businesses 
who are prioritizing sustainable responsibility, this responsibility is clearer for newly educated 
==PAGE:51==
 
Page 44 of 59 
 
employees. Working with the SDGs and promoting these information’s to potential new employees 
can encourage them to apply for jobs at the firm. Mentioning this it is important that the work is not 
just “hot air”. As the employee will value the commitment of the work which their employer is doing 
on sustainable issues. It is therefore of interest for the business to promote the work on the SDGs not 
only for the customers and competitors but also for hiring new employees, as it can promote the 
company for new applicants. Businesses use the SDGs in various ways it is therefore difficult define 
how businesses may use the SDGs as the use is different for each business. It is therefore currently 
not possible to use the SDGs to compare a product with other products in the same market as the 
other producer might have taken other decisions in the analysis. The incorporation of the SDGs is 
therefore primarily thought of as a tool for communication and it is focused on internal 
communication. Due to the lack of coherence in the reporting methodology. Additionally, the SDGs 
can be used as a common language for both the suppliers and business partners. It can be used for 
streamlining the communication and therefore the actions on specific topics can be effectuated.   
 
There is sometimes a gap between what is thought and what is meant when working with 
sustainability. This gap is important to clear-up as an idea might, on the paper, be sustainable but 
once the whole operation and all the processes are considered and evaluated, a product might not be 
sustainable regarding one or more of the economic, social and environmental aspects. This leads to 
the discussion of when something is sustainable as it was discussed in the interview; “What is absolute 
sustainability?” It might be when the TBL is obtained. One of the products discussed in the interview 
was the catalysts Haldor Topsøe produces used in production of ammonia. The production of the 
catalyst makes it possible to deliver the demanded amount of ammonia as the natural production of 
ammonia without the catalyst is too slow to meet the demand. However, there are a lot of variables 
when discussing the sustainability of a product. The boundaries for the investigation are of 
importance. For example, with the catalyst the social and economic benefits of are almost indisputable 
as the lack of the catalyst will bring hunger. The production and use of the catalyst impact the 
environment, but the alternative is starvation in areas where SDG 2 is prioritized.  
 
It was found in the interview that Haldor Topsøe are striving to improve their processes and they are 
therefore also working on SDG 12 on a concept level at least. In addition to the work Haldor Topsøe 
currently are doing with SDG 2, 3, and 7. Haldor Topsøe are working with the SDGs on the target 
level and found that some of the targets are lacking indicators which they would like, as it is difficult 
==PAGE:52==
 
Page 45 of 59 
 
for the business to assess something without a standard for the measurement. The lack of indicators 
makes it difficult to monitor how actions impact the SDGs. Additionally, during the interview there 
was requested that the work on the SDGs might be politically determined and/or be necessitated 
through pressure from stakeholders.  
 
3.3. Discussion of how Specshell and Haldor Topsøe may use the SDGs 
The use of the SDGs seems to be beneficial according to both businesses even though they are in 
different business sectors. Both companies identify that they can use the SDG work internally as a 
communication tool and they can use it externally for added value as a sales differentiator. The 
internal use enables the businesses to define goals and track improvements on the goals. The 
businesses can thereby improve their own bottom line while taking their part in the work for attaining 
the SDGs.  
 
From the interviews it was identified that Specshell will work with the SDGs on the product level and 
Haldor Topsøe will be working both on the product level and meanwhile considering their whole 
business. One difference of the two companies is that Haldor Topsøe have a larger product portfolio, 
while Specshell currently have one highly specialized product. Based on this, it is possible to conduct 
an SDG analysis on the Specshells product, providing insight into most impacts of the SDGs. There 
might be some impacts which is outside the value-chain of the Specshells product and these impacts 
will therefore not be accounted for in the analysis. Specshell should consider those impacts and 
eventually also conduct an analysis covering the whole business. A full Specshells analysis will 
disclose the activities of the company and prevent misinterpretation of the product specific items from 
the impact caused by the whole Specshells operation.  
 
The product portfolio of Haldor Topsøe is divided into three categories; Catalyst, Equipment, and 
Process Licensing. Haldor Topsøe is primarily known for their work with catalysts. Haldor Topsøe 
have more than 150 different catalyst and they can design and manufacture catalysts to help with 
specific tasks. The sole amount of catalysts in the product portfolio makes it difficult for clients to 
use Haldor Topsøe’s evaluation of which SDGs the business is working with. The clients, buying one 
or a couple of Haldor Topsøe’s products, are not able to use the analysis covering the whole business. 
It is meaningful to provide product specific information to the clients, like it is done with LCA data. 
If the clients are interested in the SDGs, they may focus on the impacts the SDGs have on their 
==PAGE:53==

activities. Therefore, the overall information of Haldor Topsøe is not of relevance for the clients. This 
way of using the SDGs are for sales purposes. The SDG’s are important information for the clients, 
as other product evaluation tools do not consider the SDGs, tools such as LCAs. LCAs do not provide 
insight of how the SDGs are impacted but can be used to measure some of the targets of the SDGs.  
 
The primary difference between the two businesses are size. The size of the companies makes some 
aspects of the SDG work easier and other aspects more difficult. In Haldor Topsøe the SDG work is 
conducted by a few employees. The team is obtaining information from the different departments of 
the corporation. Based on the information the team is capable of evaluation and reporting Haldor 
Topsøe’s work with the SDGs. Specshell does currently not have the economic capability to have a 
full-time employee working on the SDGs. The employee in Specshell, working on SDGs and the 
reporting will be limited on time as SDGs work will only be one of many daily responsibilities. 
 
The work with the SDGs would for both businesses benefits from being provided with a guide that 
account for sector, size, and location of activities. By using a guide, the results become comparable 
with other companies using the same guide. If multiple companies use the same guidelines the results 
will become more transparent and individual company bias will be reduced.

3.4. Business Interview learnings  
Interviews outline that the SDGs are common knowledge for some businesses while unknown for 
others. Potential use of the SDGs was discussed in the interview with both Specshell and Haldor 
Topsøe. Specshell was not aware of SDGs prior to the interview and found that the they might be 
able to use the SDGs in internal processes and sales material. Specshell intent to conduct analysis of 
which SDGs their product impacts when it is in use. This analysis to encourage the client’s 
stakeholders when considering investing in Specshell’s product.  
 
The interview with Haldor Topsøe discovered an interest in the SDGs for communication used both 
internally and externally. Internal communication to bring awareness on how the company are 
performing on sustainability of products and solutions. The external communication is going to 
analyze how Haldor Topsøe’s products impact the SDGs, along with use in sales material. This 
awareness may, if it is considered in the production, improve the performance of the products related 
to the SDGs. The reasoning for this is that clients are expected to request information of the products 
impact on the SDGs, as seen with carbon-footprinting and LCAs. 
 
Both companies indicated challenges which they identified in implementation of the SDGs. From the 
interview with Specshell it was clear that the SDGs are not commonly known, a major problem with 
implementation of the SDGs. Additionally, Specshell needed an introduction of the structure of the 
SDGs which was not found intuitive. Haldor Topsøe indicated that there is no consensus on how the 
SDGs are to be evaluated. A business can use the SDGs for internal communication, but if different 
producers are to be compared the evaluation of the SDGs must be based on identical parameters. 
Specshell and Haldor Topsøe both indicated that implementation of the SDGs into their operations 
require outside motivation. This to come from either the market (The public, customers and 
competitors) or legislation.

4. How Businesses may work with the SDGs 
The SDGs are to be fully attained by 2030. This require that both the public and private sectors 
prioritize to work with the SDGs in a structured way. Due to the SDGs design it is not obvious how 
the private sector is to work with it. This was found with Specshell, where the structure of the SDGs 
where not intuitive and easily adaptable. It has been identified through the project interviews that 
both Specshell and Haldor Topsøe seek guidelines on how to use the SDGs. A number of different 
guides are currently available, but the identification (names), usability, and limitations are in general 
challenging and makes for a difficult acceptance and subsequent engagement. It can be concluded 
that businesses would benefit from being provided with both a collected overview and standardization 
of the guides. Additionally, Specshell and Haldor Topsøe are different both in size and knowledge 
regarding the SDGs. This makes it difficult to have guides that can cover both experienced and un-
experienced businesses. A clear gap in the existing guides have become visible throughout the project 
phase showing that the currently available guides are either very general or very specific.  
 
4.1. What businesses should be aware off when working with the SDGs 
Currently, the boundaries for SDG work is not specified. It is recommended that the boundaries must 
be defined if the work with the SDGs are to be standardized. The fact that businesses are not 
disclosing the SDGs boundaries impacts the evaluation of the work they are doing with the SDGs, 
which then over all reduces the transparency.  
 
One of the motivators for sustainability implementation is the financial bottom line. If financial results 
are affected negatively sustainability initiatives often becomes limited, as the economic sustainability 
is the business of business. The triple bottom line must be taken into consideration when businesses 
are working with sustainability. It is indicated in the study by Eccles, Ioannou, and Serafeim that 
show sustainable decisions in businesses generates a return of investment for investors which is 
bigger if the business prioritize sustainability. It has therefore been identified during the study that 
doing well while being good seems to be possible. 
 
It is of importance that businesses are clear about their decisions when working both with 
sustainability and the SDGs. The boundaries must be presented like the results they intend to present. 
The result is determined by the boundaries, i.e. if the boundaries are from Cradle-to-Gate the 
decisions that may be based on the result are only valid from Cradle-to-Gate. It is therefore crucial 
==PAGE:58==
 
Page 51 of 59 
 
that the boundaries are taken into consideration and presented when businesses present their financial 
results. Working with the SDGs on the product level is not normally seen, but it was identified as a 
strong possibility in the interview with Haldor Topsøe. Haldor Topsøe anticipate that clients in time 
will request data on the SDGs like they do with LCAs. If businesses start performing SDG analysis 
on their product portfolios a higher need for “ingredients SDGs” will be required. It is encouraged 
that standardization on the analysis methodology is done. The standardization is recommended for 
improving the transparency both for stakeholders, clients, and competitors (the market). The 
businesses can perform an SDG analysis as a part of their sales material in the same way, they have 
datasheets on products performance and other parameters like carbon-footprinting and sometimes full 
LCAs.    
 
4.2. How Businesses may use Existing Guidelines 
Both Specshell and Haldor Topsøe indicated that with outside motivation the companies would work 
or would already have begun working with the SDGs. The SDG driver could originate from either 
the public or private sector. Currently, the public sector does not seem to be requiring businesses to 
invest time and money in working with the SDGs. Therefore, it seems to be the private market that 
will have to drive the implementation. They may do this as described in chapter 3.3 as businesses 
may affect the engagement of the SDGs in the market. Businesses may impact other actors in the 
market to encourage them to work with the SDGs and alternatively the companies may influence the 
suppliers in their value-chain. 
 
The existing guides is divided into the three categories; frameworks, tools, and roadmaps. It has been 
identified in this study that the frameworks are too general as they are designed to be applicable for 
all types and sizes of businesses. Meaning that all businesses may use them. The tools can be divided 
into two categories; overview tools and sector tools. The overview tools provide the user an overview 
of the SDGs. The sector tool is based on input-output models providing the user with insight of the 
SDGs impact, based on a questionnaire. The roadmaps are divided into two parts; sector specific 
roadmap and sector and regional roadmaps. The sector specific roadmap provides direction and an 
overview of the most important SDGs the businesses should consider. The sector and regional specific 
roadmaps work in the same way as the sector specific roadmap, but it is also restricted to a region.  
 
==PAGE:59==
 
Page 52 of 59 
 
Businesses must be aware of the limitations the guide they use have. Using the frameworks can be 
fine, but the business must be aware that they are general and unspecific, in the same way as the 
overview tools. However, the fact that these two types of guides are working on a general and 
unspecific level makes them usable for businesses who are interested in the SDGs and have no prior 
knowledge. The first step in the guides are to establish understanding of the SDGs. The guides can 
also be used by businesses who have previously worked and are still working with the SDGs. They 
may use the guide as an iteration-tool, in order to track developments. Businesses that have gained 
knowledge and are familiarized with the SDGs may use either the sector tools, sector roadmaps, or 
the sector and regional roadmaps when relevant. The guides provide both directions and 
recommendations for which SDGs the business should consider prioritizing based on tendencies for 
the sector. It has been identified that businesses have designed their own approaches for working with 
the SDGs, some have done it as they find the existing guides lacking and insufficient. If businesses 
decide to design their own way to evaluate the SDGs, they may be subject to cherry-picking. Cherry-
picking is not a challenge as such, but business may unknowingly neglect or set aside important SDGs 
if they do not conduct a proper analysis of their activities. Businesses may also affect other SDGs 
that they are not aware of due to the SDGs interconnections.  
 
If a company miss potential interconnections or neglect SDGs, they might end up SDG washing their 
impacts, this should be prevented. SDG washing can be prevented by either prior obtaining a general 
understanding of the SDGs or alternatively using input-output tools. It is recommended that 
businesses interested in working with the SDGs invest both time and education in understanding the 
structure of the SDGs and their capabilities regardless of how they are going to assess them.

5. Conclusion 
It is currently voluntary for companies to start rolling out the usage of SDGs. This as governments 
and legislators currently do not provide requirements and drivers for individual companies to start 
implementing the SDGs in their business strategies. But it can be concluded that businesses must now 
start working with the SDGs if they are to be attained by 2030 in order to meet the UN goals. 
Businesses can work with the SDGs internally or externally. Working with the SDGs internally 
require that company management direction is clear, and that communication is effective. This can 
be executed in two ways by usage of top-down or bottom-up communications. The top-down 
communication require that management encourage employees to work towards the relevant SDGs. 
In the bottom-up method the motivation originates from the employees and it is the management who 
must support finance and adapt to the wishes of the employees.  
 
This project has shown that Haldor Topsøe is working with the SDGs product specific. This is 
currently not normally seen for businesses, but it may be very beneficial for the company as they 
anticipate clients to be requesting information of the products SDG performance. The SDGs can be 
used as a sales tool. It is recommended that Haldor Topsøe continue to analyze the full extent of the 
SDGs. By conducting both a product specific and an overall SDG analysis Haldor Topsøe increases 
the transparency of their operation. With the product specific SDG analysis, Haldor Topsøe may 
identify new potential areas where they can improve their products. 
 
It can be concluded that one major issue for SDG work is that smaller businesses, like Specshell, are 
not necessarily aware of the SDGs, its structure and design. This prevents businesses to utilize the 
SDGs, and for Specshell who are working with many stakeholders, the SDGs could be used as 
incentive when obtaining investments in their products. Using the SDGs may provide insight for new 
business opportunities and new ways of utilizing their product. 
 
In general, the size of the companies has an impact on how the businesses use or know about SDGs. 
Larger companies are more likely to have the economic capability to prioritize a thorough analysis of 
their activities, when compared to the same situation for SMEs. The time SMEs may invest in the 
SDGs is quite limited as there currently is no legislated requirements for execution and 
implementation. Large scale companies may utilize their position in the value-chain and may thereby 
motivate suppliers, clients, and competitors to work with the SDGs.

6. Working with the SDGs in the future 
The status of the work with the SDGs is that there is a need for a push in the market. The push may 
come from the public sector but given the current activities it is not expected that the public sector 
will be the driving factor. It does therefore seem to be the private sector who will have to lift that 
burden, at least for the next step.  
 
If businesses are expected to contribute substantially to the SDGs, they will require help for doing so. 
An idea could be to help businesses who work or would like to work with the SDGs and have activities 
in multiple countries or consider expanding their activities to other areas could benefit from an 
overview of which SDGs that are of the primary concern for that area. Such an overview could be 
inspired by the framework designed by KL, but instead of covering municipalities, it could cover 
national priorities on the SDGs. If there are big differences across a nation it may be beneficial to 
address the SDGs on parts of the nation. This overview could provide businesses with information of 
the SDGs that are prioritized in different areas where they might have activities in. Businesses could 
thereby work structured with the SDGs of priority for those regions. This could be beneficial for both 
the areas and the businesses, who do not have the capacity to evaluate the SDGs for every activity 
they have.  
 
It could be beneficial for businesses to have a repository where they can get access to the existing 
frameworks, roadmaps, and tools in one location where both the advantages and limitations are 
described for the individual guides. Compiling all these guides in one place may help businesses in 
working with the SDGs in a structured way. This may provide for a faster implementation and at the 
same time prevent cherry-picking, possibly also SDG washing, and make businesses aware of 
potential interconnections of the SDGs they would otherwise not have been aware of.
