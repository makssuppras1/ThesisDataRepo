Abstract
Incidents of treatment­resistant fungal infections are growing worldwide. One common airborne
fungal pathogen is Aspergillus fumigatus. The emergence of Azole­resistant Aspergillus fumi­
gatus (ARAF) is a growing problem. Numerous treatments of other diseases create a growing
pool of immunocompromised individuals who are susceptible to ARAF infections. The purpose
of this thesis was to identify possible azole resistance mechanisms within A. fumigatus and ver­
ify the virulence effects of the putative gene AFNHMT for the fungus. Throughout this, both a
molecular biology and bioinformatic approach were applied. With the usage of tools such as AU­
GUSTUS and CLC Genomic Workbench a genomic analysis of 11 clinical A. fumigatus isolates
was accomplished. Within this thesis, it was determined that using a Map to Reference ap­
proach was more ideal compared to De Novo Assembly. Additionally, using SSI­P3, a genome
that was isolated from the same country, was found to be ideal for the downstream analysis.
Firstly, a targeted analysis was performed which resulted in three genes of interest, cyp51A,
hmg1, and hmg2. Hereafter, a untargeted analysis was performed. To do so all nonsynony­
mous mutations were taken from CLC Genomics Workbench and grouped. It was determined
that grouping mutations based on their BLOSUM50 score was ideal. For those mutations that
did not receive a BLOSUM score, grouping was done based on their mutation type. This created
the following groups: ­5, ­4, ­3, ­2, ­1, Del, fs, Dup, Stop, SR, and STR. Code was written that
isolated 72 mutations of interest from the 32.683 mutations spanning these 11 groups. These
72 mutations were further narrowed down to three suggested genes, g8456, g3218, and g5306.
These six suggested genes are yet to be verified in the laboratory but provide a stepping stone
for further research. As well as this, within this thesis transformants for further testing of the
gene AFNHMT were created.

Motivation
Recent news from the United States of America shows growing incidents of treatment­resistant
fungal infections. It is believed that climate change, globalization, and the widespread use of
antifungal agents all contribute to the higher incidents of fungal infections [1]. Whilst these
infections are caused by various fungi, it shines a light on a bigger concern. Pathogens are
evolving and what treatment is accessible is no longer guaranteed to work.
One such fungus that causes infections is Aspergillus fumigatus. This fungus is a ubiquitous
mould that can be found throughout various environments, including soil and decaying plant
matter. A. fumigatus is responsible for a range of diseases termed Aspergillosis. These dis­
eases can be life­threatening in immunocompromised individuals who are unable to fight off in­
fection. A. fumigatus, amongst other fungal pathogens, is a growing problem due to an increase
in treatment methods for other diseases relying on weakening the immune system, thereby mak­
ing more patients viable candidates for infection. Additionally, treatment of these diseases is
becoming increasingly difficult due to the emergence of ARAF. Various resistance mechanisms
in ARAF render the antifungal drugs inert. These resistance mechanisms are not all known
and therefore the goal of this thesis is to find novel mechanisms. Along with resistance mecha­
nisms, virulence factors are an important area to study when interested in the pathogenesis of
A. fumigatus. Therefore, this thesis also wished to elucidate the effects of removal of a putative
gene AFNHMT.
It is important to study these aspects of A. fumigatus for several reasons. Firstly, more knowl­
edge helps us understand the underlying genetic mechanisms at play. This can be benefi­
cial for the creation of future therapies that are effective against resistant strains. Secondly,
understanding the resistance mechanisms can lead to identifying any specific factors that con­
tribute to the emergence of resistance. This can help with preventing future emergence. Finally,
studying the resistance mechanisms can be helpful when creating strategies for preventing and
controlling infections. Surveillance is one of the leading ways to prevent massive outbreaks of
disease and gathering knowledge helps aid surveillance. In summary, studying the virulence of
A. fumigatus and its azole resistance is essential for improving our understanding of this fungal
pathogen and how to combat its negative effect on humans.

Outline
The goals throughout this thesis were to learn more about emerging azole resistance mech­
anisms seen in A. fumigatus and the effects of a specific gene, AFNHMT, on the virulence of
the fungus. The former goal was the main focus of this thesis and the latter was included to
gain more experience in the laboratory. These two goals were split into two separate stud­
ies. The first one is named ’Elucidation of Azole Resistance Mechanisms in A. fumigatus’, an
overview of this study can be seen in Figure 1. This study was based on harvesting Genomic
DNA (gDNA) from clinical isolates and sequencing it. This was followed by a thorough bioinfor­
matical analysis of said sequences. The second study, named ’Effect of AFNHMT on Virulence
for A. fumigatus’, was focused on the effects of the removal of a gene referred to as AFNHMT.
An overview of what this study entails can be seen in Figure 2. This study focused on using
molecular biology to create transformants lacking said gene.
Figure 1: Elucidation of Azole Resistance Mechanisms in A. fumigatus. This figure shows an overview

and gDNA was isolated. This was sent for sequencing at SSI which resulted in files with reads for each
isolate. These reads were uploaded to CLC Genomics Workbench and run through QC. Hereafter the
reads were assembled and a consensus sequence for each isolate was prepared for the final step:
mutation analysis.

Introduction
1.1
The Kingdom Fungi
The tree of life is divided into three separate domains: Eubacteria, Archaea, and Eukaryota [2].
The Kingdom Fungi resides within the domain of Eukaryota. Otherwise referred to as Eumycota,
this kingdom is recognised as one of the oldest and largest clades of living organisms on Earth.
Eumycota encompasses ten phyla: Cryptomycota, Microsporidia, Chytridiomycota, Monoble­
pharidomycota, Neocallimastigomycota, Blastocladiomycota, Zoopagomycota, Mucoromycota,
Ascomycota, and Basidiomycota [3, 4]. These ten phyla have an enormous diversity, with varied
ecologies, life cycle strategies, and morphologies [5].
The focus of this thesis can be found within the phylum Ascomycota. This phylum, along with
the phylum Basidiomycota, forms the subkingdom Dikarya. Fungi of the Dikarya subkingdom
are generally known to cause lignin degradation [6]. Ascomycota has the largest number of cur­
rently known species with approximately 57.000 identified species. These species encompass
both non­mobile cellular organisms that grow as hyphae, as well as yeasts which are round uni­
cellular fungi that reproduce by budding. A main distinctive trait of this phylum is the possession
of an ascus. This organ is home to sexual spore production. Not all members of this phylum
have been seen to produce the ascus and have yet to have their sexual reproduction verified
[7, 8]. Asexual fungi are known as anamorphs and fungi with sexual phases are known as
teleomorphs. This phylum is utilised in various industrial applications such as food production
and flavouring [9].
1.1.1
Life Cycle
The life cycle of a fungus from the phylum Ascomycota starts and ends with the spores. Most
fungal spores can enter into a latent phase where the metabolism is reduced. This phenomenon
is called dormancy, which can be dealt into two types: exogenous and endogenous. The prior
type occurs when the spore is faced with unfavourable conditions. This could be a lack of
nutrients, low temperature, unfavourable pH, or the presence of an inhibitor. Here, the spore
will remain dormant and delay germination until environmental conditions become favourable.
The latter type occurs when spores fail to germinate immediately due to factors within the spore

itself. These factors could be nutrient impermeability or the presence of endogenous inhibitors.
Here, dormancy is terminated by ageing, physiological shock, or removal of said endogenous
inhibitors [10].
Once dormancy is terminated, and germination occurs, an average growth progression could
be as follows:
­ 0h: Conidium. Germination begins.
­ 0­5h: Isotropic growth. The fungal spores swell due to water intake.
­ 1­5h: Germling. The fungus creates a germ tube.
­ 2­6h: Postdivisional hyphae. Septa are formed, dividing the hyphae into smaller cellular
compartments. Growth extends from the apex.
­ 2­8h: Branching hyphae. Growth occurs from different ends, leading to branching.
A visual representation of the above can be seen in Figure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: Growth of Filamentous Fungi. The growth of a fungus starts from the conidium and pro­
gresses to branching hyphae. Figure adapted from Course Material from 27419 Filamentous Fungi:
Biology and applications [11].
This form of growth is termed filamentous extension, named after the fact that fungi extend
in filaments. After germination, the extension rate of the germling increases until a maximum
linear rate is reached. This value varies depending on the species and the environmental con­
ditions. As growth occurs, septa are formed to divide the hyphae into smaller compartments.
Septa have a single, central pore for transportation between compartments. When hyphal com­
partments are damaged or need isolating this pore is blocked by Woronin bodies, which are
mobile organelles with dense protein cores [9]. The fungus explores its habitat with the rapidly
growing, sparsely­branched hyphae. Once a nutrient source is located, the extension rate de­
clines and the branching rate increases. These branches can either form from the main hypha
(named a primary branch), a primary branch (secondary branch), or a secondary branch (ter­

tiary branch). Typically, these branches avoid each other, known as hyphal avoidance or nega­
tive autotropism. Negative autotropism is mostly observed at low hyphal densities, as a way to
maximise nutrient usage. As the mycelium matures and the hyphae densities grow, positive au­
totropism can occur. Young hyphae can be attracted to older hyphae leading to hyphal fusions.
Here, the central regions of the colony become more interconnected to support the growth of
the outer regions. After some time, unfavourable conditions can develop under the centre of the
colony, this could be nutrient depletion, pH changes, or the production of secondary metabo­
lites that inhibit growth. These conditions cause growth to become restricted which initiates the
production of spores [10]. Thus, leading to a reset of the cycle in a new environment.
1.2
Aspergillus
Aspergillus is a diverse genus of over 800 filamentous fungi species that can be found in various
habitats worldwide [12]. These fungi can flourish in many diverse conditions and environments,
including but not limited to soil, decaying vegetation, seeds and grains, and even the human
body [13]. Several fungi within this genus are capable of spoiling food, producing mycotox­
ins, or being pathogens to humans and animals [14]. Aspergilli are major players in various
ecosystems due to their saprophytic nature. Additionally, these fungi have vast nutritional flex­
ibility and can therefore grow on multiple carbon sources [13, 15]. These moulds have been
around for centuries and humans have learnt to exploit Aspergilli in various processes. The first
documented usage was for the transformation of rice, soybeans, and other plant foods in an
attempt to improve their palatability [15]. Since then Aspergilli have been exploited to produce
metabolites, such as antibiotics, organic acids, medicines, or enzymes, as well as food and food
ingredients [14, 16].
The name for this genus comes from the distinct morphology that Aspergillus species have. The
spore­bearing structure, known as the conidiophore, bares a striking similarity to an aspergillum,
which is the instrument used for sprinkling holy water in the Roman Catholic Church [15]. The
conidiophore consists of a long stalk called the stipe, the apex of which is swollen. The surface of
this swollen apex consists of spore­bearing cells called phialides. When mitotic division occurs
in the phialide nucleus, a chain of asexual spores is created. These spores are referred to as
either conidiospores or conidia. The shape of conidia varies from species to species, where
some are more spherical and others more elongated. The conidia are extremely hydrophobic

Aspergillus species can vary in colour, size, and production of sclerotia [15]. The classification
and identification of this genus have mainly been based on phenotypic characters, though in the
last decades, a change has been seen with molecular and chemotaxonomic characterisation
being utilised alongside older methods [14]. The genus Aspergillus is divided into six subgenera:
Circumdati, Nidulantes, Fumigati, Aspergillus, Cremei, and Polypaecilum [17]. Subgenera are
further split into sections. Pathogenic fungi can be found within a couple of these sections. The
most common pathogenic species are: Aspergillus flavus from the section Flavi, Aspergillus
niger from the section Nigri, Aspergillus terreus from the section Terrei, and Aspergillus fumi­
gatus from the section Fumigati [18]. Of these, the latter is one of the most commonly found
pathogens and is therefore of special interest.
1.3
Aspergillus fumigatus
Aspergillus fumigatus is a ubiquitous fungus. The main features that give rise to this are its
ability to grow in a wide range of environmental conditions, effective dispersal of spores, and
swift adaptability to changes within the environment [19]. The ecological niche of A. fumigatus

==PAGE:19==
is soil. Here, it leads a saprophytic lifestyle, degrading both organic and plant materials. It is the
most dominant fungus in garden and greenhouse soil, where it can account for 35 to 70 percent
of total colony­forming fungi [19]. Glycosylhydrolases and extracellular proteinases have been
found in the A. fumigatus genome. This corroborates with the ability of the fungus to survive by
degrading polysaccharides from plant cell walls and acquire nitrogen sources [19]. A. fumigatus
is a thermophilic fungus, meaning it is capable of growth at temperatures as high as 55°C. This
fungus has been isolated from decaying vegetation and soil at temperatures ranging from 12°C
to 65°C and with pH values of 2.1 to 8.8. Additionally, the propagules produced in the sexual
cycle, ascospores, can survive temperatures higher than 70°C. This is due to the thick wall that
protects them. Conidia can likewise tolerate harsher conditions, like stresses from freezing or
dehydration for prolonged periods [20, 19]. Nonetheless, the optimal growth conditions for this
fungus are 37°C with a pH between 3.7 and 7.6 [19].
A. fumigatus has additional features that equip the fungus to survive in these harsher conditions
that would be detrimental to other fungi. A principal defence system is the efflux pumps, which
remove toxic compounds from within the fungus. Furthermore, A. fumigatus produces potent
secondary metabolites, such as gliotoxin. Gliotoxin is a broad­range antimicrobial that can give
A. fumigatus an edge in a competitive environment. Lastly, the A. fumigatus genome is rich in
specific enzymes such as catalases, superoxide dismutases, and glutathione transferases for
the detoxification of Reactive Oxygen Species (ROS). Which helps prevent damage to cellular
components [19].
The conidia of A. fumigatus are an important part of their survival. They are globose to subglo­
bose and around 2.0­3.0 µm in diameter. Due to the hydrophobicity of the conidia, the slightest
air current can cause them to disperse. A. fumigatus has the most hydrophobic conidia in the
genus Aspergillus. A study by Kwon et al (2013) once estimated the emission rate of A. fumiga­
tus conidia from a compost pile to be 8–11×103 cfu/m2/s at the mean wind speed of 1m/s [19].
Additionally, transportation of the conidia can happen through other means. A swarming soil
bacteria, Paenibacillus vortex can facilitate the dispersal of A. fumigatus conidia [19]. A study
by Ingham et al. (2011) showed that this happens with the help of conidial surface proteins, as
treatment of the conidia with proteinase K removed the conidia­bacterial interaction [21]. On
top of this, it is suspected that conidia are passively dispersed via rodents, insects, and worms,
but this is yet to be studied further [19].

Life Cycle of A. fumigatus
A. fumigatus has the ability to switch between an asexual, sexual, and parasexual life cycle.
Within these cycles, the fungus can switch between being a homokaryon and a heterokaryon.
A homokaryon only has one nucleus type genetically speaking, whereas a heterokaryon has
at least two genetically different nuclei. The heterokaryonic state can be hard to maintain due
to being unstable, but some fungi are better equipped to stay in this form [10]. An overview of
these three varying life cycles and how they fit together can be seen in Figure 1.3.

Asexual
The most commonly seen life cycle for A. fumigatus is the asexual one. Here, an alternation
between multicellular and unicellular states happens from the generation of uninucleate asexual

Sexual
For a long while, it was believed that A. fumigatus was incapable of sexual reproduction. This

fully functional sexual reproductive cycle [22]. The reason for the delay in this discovery is that

found when growing the fungus at 30°C on oatmeal agar for 3–6 months in the dark. As of this
report, the natural niche needed for the formation of ascospores has not been identified [23].
For the sexual cycle to occur, two haploid nuclei of opposing mating types must be available.
These types are known as MAT1­1 and MAT1­2 and are what regulate sexual compatibility [24].
The process starts with the development of special hyphae from the mating strains. The ’male’
strain produces an antheridium and the ’female’ strain an ascogonium. At fertilisation these
two combine in plasmogamy, forming a network of dikaryotic cells [23]. From this ascogenous
hyphae arise, with each cell having a nucleus from the ’male’ strain and the ’female’ strain.
These cells undergo nuclear fusion and thus become diploid zygotes. The two zygotes go
through two meiotic cell divisions, followed by a post­meiotic mitotic division. This process
yields eight haploid ascospores which are formed within the ascus. The fruiting body, referred
to as the cleistothecium, can have 100 to 1000 asci and up to 105 ascospores. Each fertilisation
process can form its own cleistothecium [23, 24].
Parasexual
A parasexual cycle is normally seen in fungi that are predominantly or completely asexual.
Parasex occurs when hyphae from two haploid homokaryons with varying genetics meet. This
can happen via anastomosis, where the two hyphae fuse and exchange nuclei and other or­
ganelles. The mycelium now has two genetically unlike nuclei and is therefore a heterokaryon.
Hereafter, nuclear fusion occurs. This can either be between the like nuclei or between unlike

1.4
A. fumigatus as a Pathogen
As mentioned, one environment A. fumigatus can colonise is the human body. In fact, A. fu­
migatus is one of the most common airborne opportunistic fungal pathogens [13]. Worldwide,
3.000.000 chronic cases and 300.000 invasive diseases are caused by A. fumigatus annually
[26]. The average adult inhales more than 100 conidia daily [19]. Conidia are able to reach the
small airways and alveoli of the lungs due to their small size [26]. Genomic data gathered shows
that the virulence of A. fumigatus mainly results from the immunosuppression or genetic defi­
ciency of the host rather than from specific and unique fungal determinants. When infecting an
immunocompetent host various elements of the innate immune system recognise and eliminate
the spores, preventing the colonisation of the respiratory system [20, 27]. More specifically, the
main defences of the host are mucociliary clearance, the phagocytic and antimicrobial activities
of epithelial cells, and the innate immune cells [26]. Although, these defences can be the cause
of problems. A. fumigatus is known to cause farmer’s lung disease which is a form of hypersen­
sitivity pneumonitis. Symptoms are often seen as inspiratory crackles, tightness of the chest,
cough, and dyspnea. This disease comes from repeated exposure to high concentrations, or
prolonged exposure to low concentrations, of inhaled antigens from mouldy hay or straw. This
leads to sensitisation and consequently, an allergic reaction causing the disease [28]. Allergic
reactions are often exaggerated or even inappropriate immunological responses to an antigen
where the immune system ends up harming the body [29].
For immunocompromised individuals, the story is completely different. For them inhaling the
conidia from A. fumigatus can lead to multiple diseases [13]. When infecting an immunocom­
promised individual, A. fumigatus has mechanisms that help ensure colonisation. These mech­
anisms are a part of the fungal virulence factors. Virulence factors are defined as cellular struc­
tures, molecules, or regulatory systems that enable pathogens to colonise a host, evade or

==PAGE:23==
suppress the immune response, and/or obtain nutrition from the host [30]. Virulence of A. fumi­
gatus is known to be multifactorial, as it is associated with its structure, capacity for growth and
adaptation to stress conditions, and its ability to cause damage to the host [31]. This being said,
it is believed that the host’s immune response has a big effect on the virulence of A. fumigatus.
Therefore, it could be beneficial to study supposed virulence factors and see their effects on
both immunocompromised and immunocompetent hosts.
A paper has identified a gene in A. nidulans which encodes a specific enzyme: N­terminal his­

inactivation. This is done by post­translationally methylating the N­terminal histidine found in
LPMOs [32]. The same function is expected in A. fumigatus but has not been verified. LPMOs
are copper­dependent enzymes that can cleave polysaccharides through an oxidative mecha­
nism [33]. They are capable of breaking down lignocellulose which is built up of harder materials
such as cellulose, hemicellulose, and lignin [32, 34]. These enzymes are important for recy­

earth [34]. The mechanism of action is believed to be the cleavage of cellulose by the insertion
of oxygen at C1 and/or C4. This forms lactone which is hydrolysed spontaneously to an aldonic
acid or a ketoaldose [33]. The genome of A. fumigatus encodes for seven LPMO genes that all
belong to the auxiliary activity family 9 [35]. LPMOs have been documented as virulence fac­
tors in other species, such as in Aphanomyces astaci where LPMOs are believed to facilitate
infection through oxidative degradation of crystalline chitin in the crustacean exoskeleton [36].
Another example can be seen in the bacteria Vibrio cholerae. The virulence factor that helps
colonisation is an active LPMO [37]. This gene is of interest to see if it is a virulence factor for
A. fumigatus and whether the removal affects the pathogenesis.

1.4.2
Aspergillosis
Aspergillosis refers to the wide range of diseases caused by fungi from the Aspergillus genus.
These diseases vary from localised conditions to fatal disseminated infections [40]. Amongst
known Aspergilli, A. fumigatus is the most life­threatening species [13]. Aspergillosis has three
main clinical presentations: Allergic Aspergillosis, Aspergilloma, and Invasive Aspergillosis (IA)
[41].
Allergic Aspergillosis manifests in the lungs and facial sinuses and is a form of hypersensitivity
to the inhalation of spores. It is often seen in people who are predisposed to allergic diseases.
When in the lungs it most often presents as bronchospasm but can develop into bronchiectasis
or pulmonary fibrosis. Normal symptoms are nasal blockage, headache, facial pain, and in
some cases nasal polyps can be formed [13]. The aforementioned farmer’s lung disease is an
example of this.
Aspergilloma is often the result of colonisation of a preexisting pulmonary cavity, e.g. caused
by tuberculosis. Within the cavity, a fungal ball is created in which blood, hyphae, and cellular
debris can be found [41, 42]. Symptoms of Aspergilloma are weight loss, haemoptysis, and
chronic coughing [41].
IA can present as various syndromes depending on the individual and their compromised im­
mune system: Primary cutaneous Aspergillosis, acute invasive pulmonary Aspergillosis, chronic
necrotising pulmonary Aspergillosis, obstructive bronchial Aspergillosis, tracheal bronchitis, rhi­
nosinusitis, and disseminated Aspergillosis. The fungus invades the lungs causing cavities and
abscesses, which can restrict the blood flow in the area. Dissemination can occur to various
organs including the nervous system, liver, eyes, paranasal sinuses, gastrointestinal tract, skin,
heart, and bones [41].
Most of these diseases are associated with high morbidity and mortality. Cases of Aspergillosis

1.4.3
Treatment
Due to Aspergillosis being a range of various diseases, there are multiple treatments available.
Surgery is important in rarer disease manifestations, e.g. mycetoma requiring surgical interven­
tion to remove the fungal mass [44, 45]. Apart from surgery, the go­to treatment is the usage of
antifungals. Here, it is often key to diagnose a patient quickly so that early systemic antifungal
treatment can begin [44]. There are various antifungal classes used as treatment against A.
fumigatus.
Amphotericin B
Amphotericin B (AmB) is a polyene that binds to ergosterol in the fungal membranes. It forms
pores within the membranes causing cell death. AmB has a broad spectrum of antifungal activity
and is therefore used to treat a number of fungal infections including IA [43]. Unfortunately, this
treatment is coupled with significant infusional toxicity, nephrotoxicity, and electrolyte wasting.
Lipid formulations of AmB have been shown to have the same efficacy but have the benefit of
less toxicity [44]. Although, usage of these formulations is normally only for patients in which
the parent drug fails or with renal impairment [46]. A benefit to AmB is the lack of emergence
of resistance. Research shows that the trade­off between AmB­resistance and fungal virulence
would be dire for the fungus. Certain strains of Candida albicans have mutated to resist AmB
and as a consequence have become highly susceptible to the stresses of the host environment
and immune system [47].
Echinocandins
Echinocandins are peptides that inhibit the synthesis of glucan. They specifically inhibit β (1,3)­
D­glucan synthase, an enzyme that is necessary for the integrity of the fungal cell wall. This
antifungal works on various diseases, including invasive candidiasis, candidemia, and IA [48].
Known drugs of this class are caspofungin, micafungin, and anidulafungin. Caspofungin has
been approved by the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for treatment of IA, whereas mica­
fungin is better used as a prophylaxis for patients undergoing haematopoietic stem cell trans­
plant [48, 44]. Echinocandins are less nephrotoxic and have a lower incidence of infusion ad­

adverse effects are hepatotoxicity and derangement of serum transaminases. On top of this,
echinocandins have a higher cost compared to azoles and therefore are not a first­line drug
[48].
5­flucytosine
5­Flucytosine (5­FC) is a synthetic pyrimidine analogue [44]. The fungal toxicity of this drug
comes after the fungal cell has taken it up and it has been metabolised. The metabolites of
5­FC inhibit fungal RNA and DNA synthesis [49]. This treatment comes with severe side effects,
including hepatotoxicity, myelotoxicity, and gastrointestinal toxicity. The side effects are con­
centration dependant and therefore can be avoided with close monitoring [49, 44]. Monotherapy
with 5­FC often leads to the development of resistance, which is why it is normally used in com­
bination with AmB [49]. There is limited data on usage of 5­FC combination therapy for IA, but
it should be a viable option for the treatment of Aspergillosis [44, 49].
Azoles
Azoles were introduced as orally active compounds during the 1980s. Their principle mecha­
nism of action is to inhibit Cytochrome P450 (CYP450) enzymes. The major enzyme inhibited
is 14α­demethylase which is responsible for the conversion of lanosterol to ergosterol. Inhi­
bition leads to the accumulation of 14α methyl sterols within the cell. Ergosterol is a major
component of the fungal cell membrane, where it ensures permeability and fluidity of the cell
membrane [50]. This interference produces a defective cell membrane with altered permeabil­
ity. Azole compounds have a broad spectrum of activity, though some drugs work for certain
species and not for others [51]. Azoles are the frontline therapeutics used to treat Aspergillosis
[16]. There are a number of agents that are utilised against Aspergilli: itraconazole, voricona­
zole, posaconazole, isavuconazole. All of these agents are used for the treatment of acute
invasive and chronic pulmonary Aspergillosis and for prevention of IA. Azole drugs are the only
antifungal drugs against Aspergillus that are orally available, which is beneficial for long­term
usage of the drug. Unfortunately, azole resistance is rising amongst A. fumigatus which could
have critical outcomes [52].

Resistance Mechanisms
Due to ARAF not being a new development, some resistance mechanisms have been uncov­
ered. The most common resistance mechanism for ARAF is modifications occurring in the
cyp51A gene.
Azoles are competitive inhibitors of the Cyp51A enzyme, 14α­demethylase,
which as mentioned is important for the cell wall. Steric mutations of 14α­demethylase lead
to changes in the binding site which reduces azole binding affinity. This leads to decreasing
susceptibility to azoles [52, 55].
The most frequent mutation found in cyp51A is the TR34/L98H mutation. As the name states,
the mutations are a tandem repeat, 34 base pairs long, in the promoter region as well as a
single amino acid exchange of leucine98 to a histidine. TR34/L98H is found worldwide and
based on sequencing believed to stem from a common ancestor. Additionally, it is believed
that the mutation came from the usage of azoles in agriculture [55]. Another cyp51A mutation
with tandem repeats in the promoter region is TR46/Y121F/T298A. In vitro studies show that
the tandem repeats in both mutations elevate gene expression and therefore increase azole
resistance. Various tandem repeat mutations in the promoter region are known, but TR34 is
found in nearly all these mutations and therefore seems to be a key element to azole resistance
[56]. Another well­documented mutation is M220R, which has been proven to confer resistance
[57]. There are various cyp51A gene mutations that have been identified, but their role in azole
resistance has yet to be proven for all [50]. These mutations can confer resistance to one or
more azoles, making some A. fumigatus strains multi­azole resistant [50].
Other genes, hmg1 and hmg2, have been proposed as responsible for azole resistance in ARAF
strains. These genes produce enzymes that function in the ergosterol biosynthesis pathway
[58]. Already described mutations, that seem to be related to azole resistance, are found within

1.5
Genetic Engineering in Fungi
As mentioned, filamentous fungi have been exploited by humans for decades. As the knowl­
edge of fungal genomes grows, the application pool follows suit. Fungal metabolites can include
medically relevant compounds and industrially relevant enzymes, all of which could benefit from
optimised fungal cell factories. The pool of fungal secondary metabolites is considered a large
and under­exploited reservoir [59, 60]. Tools for genetic engineering in fungi are scarce for the
vast majority of fungi but are being rapidly developed [60]. Some known genetic engineering
techniques have yet to be proved usable in filamentous fungi, an example is RNA interfer­

engineered nucleases, which induce a targeted Double­strand Break (DSB) into the genome.
DSBs stimulate DNA repair mechanisms, Homologous Recombination (HR) or Nonhomologous
End Joining (NHEJ) [62]. Once a cut occurs NHEJ modifies the loose ends and ligates them
together. This is done with no regard for homology meaning that deletions or insertions can
occur. On the other hand, HR requires an undamaged DNA template to repair the break, which
leads to the Reconstruction of the original sequence [63]. This can be exploited with the addi­
tion of a repair template. A reason for hardships when deleting genes in A. fumigatus and other

1.5.1
Zinc­Finger Nucleases
The principle of ZFNs is based on the natural type IIS restriction enzyme, FokI. This enzyme
has physically separate binding and cleaving activities. Due to the cleaving domain having no
sequence specificity, it is possible to redirect the cutting by substituting the natural recogni­
tion domains with alternatives. The most common substitute is the set of Cys2His2zinc fingers
where each unit is bound to a single atom of zinc [65]. This finger functions as the DNA­binding
domain and is fused to the catalytic domain of FokI. The finger domain consists of 30 amino
acids, which create two antiparallel β­sheets and one α­helix [66]. Research shows that when
the fingers bind, several amino acids bind to three base pairs in the major groove of the DNA.

appropriate zinc finger modules together. For a DSB to occur two FokI domains must dimer­

recognise nearly all of the 64 possible nucleotide triplets, one can link the pre­selected modules
together to target virtually any DNA sequence. Another method is based on selection, here
context­dependant interactions between neighbouring fingers are taken into account. These
methods can be combined which gives the best results. Engineered ZFNs are available com­

of ZFNs fail, meaning that extensive testing can be required to find the perfect match. Another

not each finger makes equal contributions. This can become a puzzle to find the best combi­

1.5.2
TALENs
An alternative genome editing tool that likewise utilises FokI is TALENs [66]. Recently, it was
discovered that TALENs have a simple modular DNA recognition code, thus leading to the ex­
ploitation of this. TALENs are naturally found in the phytopathogenic genus Xanthomonas. They
have DNA­binding domains that consist of 33­35 amino acid repeat domains that each recognise
a single base pair. Specificity of TALENs are determined by Repeat­Variable Diresidues (RVDs)
which are two hypervariable amino acids [65]. Designing TALENs is more straightforward than
ZFNs. The repeats are linked together to recognise a contiguous specific DNA sequence. It
has been indicated that TALENs can be combined to recognise virtually any user­defined se­
quence. Due to TALENs recognising single base pairs, there is greater design flexibility than
seen with ZFNs. The downside to this is extensive identical repeat sequences. Strategies have
arisen to combat this: “Golden Gate” molecular cloning, high­throughput solid­phase assembly,
and ligation­independent cloning techniques. Due to all the leg work performed for ZFNs, nu­
merous effector domains are available to use with TALENs from the get­go. Limitations of this
method are the binding site having to start with a T base, the expense of the method, and how
time­consuming it is to engineer [65].
1.5.3
CRISPR
CRISPR/Cas9 is a novel gene­editing technology. It can perform precise modification of specific
sites within a gene of interest which has various purposes, such as finding functions of genes,
improving desired characteristics, and correcting mutations to see their effects [64, 67]. This
system is naturally used as an adaptive immune system by bacteria and archaeons, where it is
classified into three types based on the sequence and structure of the Cas proteins. Of these
three classes, only type II is of interest for genetic engineering, due to it being the simplest with
it only requiring CRISPR­associated protein 9 (Cas9) [68]. CRISPR/Cas9 has two components:
a single guide RNA (sgRNA) which matches a desired target gene and Cas9 an endonuclease
which cleaves double­stranded DNA to allow modifications to the genome. Precise targeting
is mediated by the protospacer sequence of the sgRNA, which guides the Cas9 nuclease to
the target. This protospacer is an important part of what makes CRISPR so simple. It only
Investigation of Azole­resistance and Virulence Factors in Aspergillus fumigatus
18
==PAGE:33==
consists of 20 nucleotides and recognises the target via base pairing [59]. Programming of
CRISPR to recognise new targets is much easier compared to the aforementioned technologies.
One restriction of this system is the need for a Protospacer Adjacent Motif (PAM) sequence.
This sequence varies between Cas9 proteins, but the most commonly used one is NGG. This
stems from the Cas9 of Streptococcus pyogenes. A PAM sequence must be located close to
the binding site of the protospacer. Additionally, off­target effects can occur, but this can be
circumvented by adding a few mismatches scattered throughout the pairing region, especially
in the 12 PAM proximal nucleotides of the protospacer [59].
CRISPR has been utilised in filamentous fungi and even in A. fumigatus. A loss­of­function
study targeted the polyketide synthase gene with the CRISPR system. Their results showed a
high editing efficiency of 25­53% and proved that expression of Cas9 had no detrimental effects
on A. fumigatus. Components of CRISPR­Cas9 have been adapted for usage in fungi. Specif­
ically, the Cas9 gene has been codon optimised for expression in A. niger and the gene has
been extended resulting in the addition of an SV40 nuclear localisation signal to the C­terminus.
This gene was fused to A. nidulans tef1 promoter and tef1 terminator. On top of this, a different
variant of the CRISPR­Cas9 system was used, in which the sgRNA is synthesised by RNA poly­
merase II which is better defined in filamentous fungi. The sgRNA is flanked by two ribozyme
sequences, Hammerhead (HH) and Hepatitis Delta Virus (HDV), which free the sgRNA [59].
Multiple genes can be targeted in a single transformation which is a big benefit of CRISPR [61].
For multiplexing with CRISPR, tRNA­spacer based systems can be used. This method exploits
the pre­tRNAs internal splice sites which when processed by splicing machinery, RNase P and
RNase Z, releases mature tRNAs. Multiplexing can be achieved by spacing different sgRNAs
with tRNAs which frees the sgRNA when spliced. Single­stranded oligonucleotides can be used
as templates for gene editing with high efficiencies in this system. [69].

1.5.4
Transformation Methods
Genetic modification of fungi has complications due to the cell wall structure and the vast dif­
ferences between strains. There is no universal transformation method for fungi due to this.
However, there are a few known transformation methods that are applicable for most fungi:
Agrobacterium­mediated Transformation (AMT), Electroporation transformation, Biolistic trans­
formation, Shock­wave­mediated Transformation (SWMT), and Protoplast­mediated Transfor­
mation (PMT) [60].
Agrobacterium­mediated Transformation
AMT is dependant on the gram­negative bacterium Agrobacterium tumefaciens. This bacterium
is capable of infecting injured plants where it integrates part of its tumour­inducing plasmid into
Investigation of Azole­resistance and Virulence Factors in Aspergillus fumigatus
20
==PAGE:35==
the genome. The integrated DNA fragment is referred to as Transfer­DNA (T­DNA). T­DNA is
flanked by two directional repeats and contains genes that cause tumour growth by forming plant
hormones. This system is exploited in AMT. A binary vector is designed to have a target gene
inserted between the directional repeats, which is then transformed into A. tumefaciens. This
bacterium is then used as a vehicle, delivering the target gene into the fungal genome. There are
multiple factors that influence the efficiency of AMT: The type of starting fungal material which
can be dependant on the strain, the concentration of acetosyringone which induces expression
of a domain in T­DNA, the ratio of fungi to bacteria, and the condition for co­culturing. Due
to the basis of this method, it is greatly suitable for knock­in mutations. Advantages of AMT
are various types of transformation recipients, the ability to integrate exogenous genes into the
genome to form stable transformants, and high transformation efficiency. The limitations are
the binary vectors which are hard to prepare and the various factors that have to be controlled
for better efficiency.
Electroporation Transformation
The principle of this method is based on electric charges. A capacitor is filled with electric
charges, building a high voltage. The sample is struck by the impulse voltage, which allows for
exogenous nucleic acid to be transferred instantly into the cells. Cells that are exposed to the
electric field have a change in the structure of the cytomembrane which creates micropores after
electric shock. This change in the permeability of the cell wall is reversible within the thresholds
of the voltage and the duration. Therefore, there are two outcomes for the micropores: they are
reversible where lipid and protein molecules restore the original structure when an appropriate
field intensity is applied or they are irreversible and the cells will recover extremely slowly leading
to cell death [60]. This is the major limitation of this method, a greater quantity of cells is needed
to overcome the substantial cell death caused. Another limitation is the nonspecific transport of
molecules in and out of the cell [70].
Electroporation has been successfully applied to multiple fungal materials and it has been dis­
covered that germinal spores are more amenable to this method. For the transformation of
fungi, it is often square waves or exponential decay waves that are utilised. The former is a
non­sinusoidal periodic waveform, where the amplitude alternates at a steady frequency be­
tween predetermined minimum and maximum values. The latter is generated by charging and
discharging a capacitor so that the electric field declines exponentially from the peak value. The
Investigation of Azole­resistance and Virulence Factors in Aspergillus fumigatus
21
==PAGE:36==
species is what determines the waveform used. Electroporation is simple, rapid, and efficient.
There are various factors that influence electroporation transformation, namely electric field in­
tensity, capacitance, and pulse duration and frequency. This method is influenced by external
factors, meaning that a buffer solution is needed [60].
Biolistic Transformation
Another name for this method is particle bombardment. Tungsten or gold particles are coated
with DNA which is then shot at the host cell at high velocity. Once inside the cell the DNA
elutes off the particles and can either be transiently expressed or stably integrated into the host
genome [71, 60]. Biolistic transformation efficiency is affected by biological parameters, such
as cell type, growth condition, and cell density. The efficiency can additionally be affected by
the instrumental setting, where particle type and size, vacuum and pressure level, and target
distance all are important. This method is one of the most powerful genetic transformation
methods, due to it not being dependent on host cell types or species. Biolistic transformation
is easy and convenient. The major downfall of this method is the expense of the instruments
and consumables. Therefore, it is generally only considered when other methods fail, such as
in fungi where protoplasts are hard to prepare [60].
Shock­wave­mediated Transformation
This method is based on changing the permeability of the cell membrane through acoustic
cavitation. Cavitation occurs when bubbles or voids form within a fluid due to a drop in pres­
sure, when these voids experience higher pressures they collapse, creating small shock waves.
These shock waves can mediate the uptake of exogenous nucleic acid into the cells, due to gen­
erating pressure disturbance and twisting forces [72, 60]. The advantages of SWMT are high
efficiency, it can act directly on spores, and the physical parameters are easily controllable.
The downsides are that the number of spores, energy, and speed of the shock wave need to
be controlled precisely. A quite detrimental limitation is the damage to the DNA that can occur
from the shock waves. On top of this, the instruments are expensive.
Protoplast­mediated Transformation
PMT is the most common fungal transformation method. As the name states, this requires pro­
toplasts of the fungus which entails the removal of the cell wall via enzyme treatment. Other
methods can be used for protoplastation, such as physical methods like grinding and supersonic
wave shock. However, enzymatic protoplastation yields the best results. Once protoplasts have
Investigation of Azole­resistance and Virulence Factors in Aspergillus fumigatus
22
==PAGE:37==
been acquired, transformation can occur. For the protoplast to uptake exogenous DNA they
are placed in a solution with calcium ions and osmotic stabilisers. Here, the calcium helps open
channels in the cytomembrane, facilitating entry into the cell. Whereas, the osmotic stabilisers
maintain the morphology of the protoplasts as they are sensitive to osmotic pressure. In this
solution Polyethylene Glycol (PEG) is added alongside the purified DNA. PEG is a cell fusion
promoter, which forms the molecular bridge between cells or between the cytomembrane and
DNA. Additionally, PEG can alter membrane permeability and facilitate entry of the exogenous
DNA. PEG is crucial for the efficiency of transformation. One main advantage of this method
is that due to its higher usage rate, there are many protocols for various fungal species to be
found. On top of this, it is simple, effective, and does not need expensive equipment. The dis­
advantages of this method are the many steps and critical reagents. PMT needs to be optimised
for the strain that is being transformed. The fungal cell wall is variable among different strains
meaning that there is no universal transformation method that can be used.
1.6
USER Fusion
Uracil­specific Excision Reagent (USER) fusion is a rapid and efficient technology used to fuse
and clone PCR fragments into a vector of interest. The method relies on generating overlapping
PCR products that are beneficial when assembling a customised DNA molecule. The primers
are designed to have 5’­overlapping sequences with the addition of a single uracil residue re­
placing a thymine residue. This uracil should be spaced 6­15 base pairs from the 5’­terminus.
After PCR amplification, the PCR fragments are flanked with single­stranded extensions of >6
base pairs that allow for seamless assembly of the fragments. These fragments are treated
with USER/Thermolabile USER II enzyme. This enzyme nicks the strands containing uracil
and generates 9 base pairs long 3’ single­stranded extensions. Complementary 3’ overhangs
are achieved in the vector with the use of the restriction enzyme, PacI, and nicking enzyme,
Nt.BbvCI. Usage of this method allows for multi­fragment assemblies in a single­format experi­
ment [73]. The advantages of this method are its simpleness, efficacy, as well as overall flexible
primer design with the only requirement being a thymine located 6­15 base pairs from an ade­
nine. Longer overhangs can be created but it is recommended that extra uracil residues are
added which increases the cost of the primers [74].

Discussion
Throughout this thesis, the goal was to learn more about the pathogen A. fumigatus. In order
to do so two overall studies were performed. The focus of one of these studies was to eluci­
date new resistance mechanisms within A. fumigatus with regards to the emergence of ARAF.
Whereas, the other study focused on the effects of the removal of the gene AFNHMT.
The latter study was unable to be finished due to time limits. The furthest gotten is creating the
transformants. Before being able to move on, these transformants would need to be verified,
which would have been done with tissue­PCR. The benefits to this method are: its high sen­
sitivity, specificity, speed, and the ability to perform it on multiple samples simultaneously. If
results confirmed the transformants to have the gene removed then the next course of action
would be virulence assays. These assays would test the virulence of FUM1 with and without the
gene. A possible way to do this would be with an animal model assay, where an animal would
be infected and monitored to see the progression of disease symptoms. Here, the comparison
between the severity and duration of the symptoms would be used to evaluate virulence. When
considering animal studies it is important to remember the 3Rs: Replacement, Reduction, and
Refinement. Therefore, it could be beneficial to start out with more ethical solutions such as cell
models. This could be the aforementioned A549 cells, though it has been shown that these cells
cannot fully reflect the complicity of the pathogenesis of a fungal pathogen. Another solution
could be to use insects such as Drosophila melanogaster or Galleria mellonella. This would be
more ethical, cost­effective, and give faster results than murine models [89]. Likewise, it could
be beneficial to test the effect of gene removal on fitness for A. fumigatus. This could be done
by measuring mycelium growth rates and comparing results. The goal of the former study was
to screen 12 SSI clinical isolates for mutations which could explain the azole resistance the
strains possessed. This process depended on achieving good quality gDNA for successful se­
quencing. Only 11 of the 12 strains gave results that could be moved forward with, as SSI2713
had a small number of low­quality reads. Unusually, this was not reflected in the gDNA sam­
ples’ measured OD and concentration. The low quality could therefore be the result of DNA
degradation due to transport from DTU to SSI. Although, it is unusual that this only occurred in
Investigation of Azole­resistance and Virulence Factors in Aspergillus fumigatus
42
==PAGE:57==
one sample. Another option is that the sample contained high concentrations of RNA, though
if this was the case a higher OD would be expected. Due to performing multiple trials of gDNA
harvesting, it was found that a higher biomass from the start resulted in the best gDNA results.
The next step was finding the optimal way to assemble the reads from the sequencing. Two
different assembly methods were tested: MapRef and DeNovo. Based on multiple criteria such
as genome coverage, AP, and time efficiency of the assembly, MapRef was found to be the
better assembly method. Utilising reference genomes often results in better accuracy which
can be seen in the genome coverage. Here, each strain had a higher number of high­coverage
sequences than what was seen for DeNovo. Though, this is contingent on having a reference
genome that is not highly divergent from the genomes being worked with. Using DeNovo would
capture the unique elements of a genome whereas MapRef would try to force likeness. On top
of assembly methods, two reference genomes were tested. Based on the number of nonsyn­
onymous mutations found when mapping the SSI strains to the two reference genomes, SSI­P3
and Af293, SSI­P3 was chosen as the sole reference genome. Using this reference led to fewer
nonsynonymous mutations and therefore easier downstream analysis. The downside of using
SSI­P3 as the reference genome was the lack of an annotation file as there is for Af293. This
was combated by running SSI­P3 through AUGUSTUS but means that the genes seen there
are predicted by an algorithm. This should not pose any issues in this analysis as genes of
interest were checked with BLAST to verify functions.
From the targeted analysis a few mutations of interest were found. SSI9652 was found to have
a mutation, M225R, in the gene cyp51A. As explained, this mutation could likely be the cause
of azole resistance in this strain, therefore it would be beneficial to confirm this suspicion. The
same can be said for SSI14680 and SSI14683 which both have the mutation F262del in their
hmg1 gene. In addition to this, it could be beneficial to examine the I226S mutation seen in
hmg2 for SSI12969 and SSI14863 due to it being within the SSD of this gene. The mutation
located within the cdr2 gene is less likely to be the cause of azole resistance due to the nature of
this gene. It is most common that mutations that affect transporters cause overexpression of the
gene which leads to resistance. A mutation of this kind would most likely be found in regulatory
regions of a gene which is not located within coding areas. The mutation C333F is located
within the transporter domain according to UniProt. Therefore, from the targeted analysis, the
genes that are suggested to be analysed further would be cyp51A, hmg1, and hmg2.

To be able to group the mutations a BLOSUM50 scoring matrix was used. The reason for using

locate mutations. BLOSUM50 was designed to be used for aligning more divergent sequences.
Due to this, it could be beneficial to see the effect of using a different matrix, such as BLOSUM62.
This matrix is more commonly used for aligning protein sequences that are moderately divergent
which fits the situation this thesis is in [90]. Using this matrix could change up the groupings
and make them smaller and therefore more manageable for downstream analysis.

type of mutation was of interest, such as a stop codon removal, then it was possible to isolate
all these mutation types and work with only them. In an attempt to not remove any mutation

4.1
Future Perspectives
As mentioned the overall goal of this thesis was to try to understand A. fumigatus and its re­
sistance mechanisms better. This knowledge could be beneficial as it can help with identifying
resistant strains, be a stepping stone to understanding resistance, and can be helpful with cre­
ating new therapies for ARAF.
Due to time limits, none of the suggested genes were tested to see if they had azole­resistant
giving mutations. Therefore, to move forward with this thesis the first point of action would be to
test the suggested genes. This would be done by reverting the mutations back to the sequence
seen within the reference genome. To do so the same methods used to genetically modify
FUM1 would be used. Protospacers with the reference genomes version of the genes would be
designed and CRISPR would be used to insert them into protoplasts. From here the genetically
modified strains would be subjected to antifungal susceptibility testing, where the MIC would
be found and compared to the not modified strains. If a resistant strain becomes susceptible
after modification one could conclude that that mutation gave some form of resistance. Fur­
ther validation could be beneficial, where a susceptible strain could be given the mutation. Of
course, it could be a bit more complicated than that, with maybe a group of mutations together
being the reason for resistance. Due to the usage of the CRISPR­tRNA system, it would be
possible to test multiple mutations in different genes in one strain, with various combinations
being easily created. Here, the results from one experiment would most likely guide you to the
next experiment. Additionally, even after testing the suggested genes, there would still be many
more options due to the sheer amount of mutations found within the SSI strains. Therefore, it
could be beneficial to see if this process could be streamlined with the help of a more suitable
reference genome. A perfect reference genome would of course be the same strain but before
azole resistance occurred within that strain. If it was possible to isolate a susceptible strain
from a patient before they were treated and then isolate a resistant strain afterwards, these
two could be compared with likely few nonsynonymous mutations which in turn would make the
process easier. This of course creates other problems such as having to follow patients, waiting

Conclusion
A. fumigatus is a common airborne pathogen that can have detrimental effects on the quality
of life for immunocompromised individuals. With emerging azole­resistant strains being found
worldwide, it is important to understand the resistance mechanisms at work within the fungus.
Therefore, the main goals of this thesis were to identify possible azole resistance mechanisms
and verify the virulence effects of the putative gene AFNHMT.
To achieve the latter goal, transformants lacking the gene were created using USER fusion to
create specialised CRISPR­tRNA vectors, which were then transformed into the strain FUM1
via protoplastation. The strain FUM1 was used for this study since it had the NHEJ pathway
removed prior to this thesis. Although these transformants were not verified within the timeframe
of this study, their creation is an important step towards future research.
In regard to the study of resistance mechanisms, a CLC workflow for the downstream analysis
of data was created. Due to working within CLC Genomics Workbench, it was possible to
create a workflow that was specific to the goal of this thesis. All tools used were added to
the said workflow, in an attempt to aid further similar research. The usage of software such
as CLC Genomics Workbench makes a bioinformatical analysis such as this more accessible
for individuals with a little bioinformatic background. The software has a plethora of tools and
manuals, which in turn can aid persons with their research. A bioinformatical approach to studies
such as the one done within this thesis is beneficial. It can act as an informative step before
entering the laboratory, helping define which targets to work with within the lab. This saves time,
money, and creates a wider perspective which can aid in understanding results.
Within CLC Genomics Workbench sequenced reads were assembled using two different meth­
ods, where the MapRef assembly was found to be the most effective. This was based on
the availability of reference genomes, the time frame of performing this assembly, and overall
genome coverage. Additionally, the reference genome SSI­P3 was chosen as the best fit for
the 11 SSI clinical isolates due to the lower number of nonsynonymous mutations seen.
The targeted analysis identified three genes, cyp51A, hmg1, and hmg2, that should be veri­
fied for conferring azole resistance. Whereas, the untargeted analysis resulted in a list of 72

48
==PAGE:63==
genes. This was narrowed down to three suggested genes, g8456, g3218 and g5305, based
on their possible function, the severity of mutation, and which strains had that mutation. These
suggestions of course need to be tested in the lab. If they turn out to not be the cause of azole
resistance then it could be beneficial to return to the list of 72 genes and locate other genes
of interest. There are, of course, many options to move further with here. As discussed, other
grouping methods could be tried in an attempt to narrow down the list. Additionally, there are
other areas of the genome that have not been taken into account within this thesis.
In conclusion, this thesis located six possible genes conferring azole resistance within the 11 A.
fumigatus strains worked with. In doing so a CLC workflow was created as to streamline future
research of similar capacity. As well as this, within this thesis transformants for further testing
of AFNHMT were created. Any knowledge gained from this thesis is a step in the right direction
to acquire knowledge on the pathogenesis of A. fumigatus.
