Chapter 1
Abstract
This project presents a sensitivity analysis of using an alkaline solution derived form construction waste for
alkaline scrubbing of CO2 (carbon dioxide) in a spray tower-based carbon capture and storage (CCS) system.
The project hypothesises that a cation-rich, high-pH aqueous solution derived waste crushed concrete can
become a low-cost, high-efficiency, clean sorbent for CO2 on-site capture and mineralisation for permanent
storage or utilisation. There are two key findings made in the the project. Firstly, the optimal mixing ratio of
fresh to CO2-rich sorbent in the continuous operation process is 3:7 for absorbing CO2 from a 10% CO2 gas
mixture was found under the assumption that the involved chemical compounds are able to come close to the
equilibria in the relevant reactions. Secondly, the average CO2 flux into the partially CO2-saturated crushed-
concrete sorbent droplets was estimated to be 4.9e-3 mol/m2s. It was, however, found to be insufficient for
a practical application in a spray tower-based CCS system. Analytical and numerical models of droplet
velocity, internal circulation and the CO2 transport in the sorbent droplets were developed; they showed
consistency with classical solutions and published results for velocities, but highlighted limitations such as
droplet deformation and early sorbent saturation when using a steady flow advective model. Experimental
results mainly supported numerical velocity predictions, but the experimental results were challenged by
variability in droplet generation and precise pH measurement, suggesting that improved pH measurement
and less invasive sealing between the sorbents and the CO2 gas mixtures were necessary.
Future work
should focus on refining numerical models to incorporate droplet deformation and a co-moving reference
frame as well as enhancing the experimental setup with automated droplet generation and less invasive pH
measurement techniques. These advancements would enable more accurate predictions of CO2 absorption
rates and sorbent capacities.

Chapter 2
Introduction
2.1
Project motivation
Carbon dioxide (CO2) is an inorganic compound present in the Earth’s atmosphere and oceans which plays a
crucial role in the heat trapping in the Earth’s atmosphere and the carbon cycle. Alongside other greenhouse
gases, it keeps the Earth warm by absorbing and re-emitting the infrared radiation from the Earth and the
Sun.
It is also absorbed by plants to be converted into oxygen as well as organic matter.
Before the
industrialization, atmospheric CO2 levels were relatively stable for hundreds of millennia, fluctuating in the
180-280 ppm range during ice ages and warmer interglacial periods. The widespread use of fossil fuels for
energy and industry as the consequence of the Industrial Revolution led to a documented anthropogenic
increase in the CO2 levels [35]. The current average concentration of CO2 in the atmosphere is around 410
ppm - much higher than the estimated 280 ppm in the pre-industrial time of the 19th century [8][35]. As
much as 25% of the CO2 emissions are attributed to the global industrial sector activity [6]. The excess
of the the CO2 in the atmosphere leads to more CO2 in the oceans and increased greenhouse effect in the
atmosphere. The amplification of the greenhouse effect has been linked to a rapid increase of the atmospheric
temperatures [8]. While CO2-species in water are indispensable for plants and animals, too much CO2 in the
ocean is causing acidification, which has an adverse effect on the marine wildlife [8][33]. Mitigation of the
climate change and acidification of the oceans requires first and foremost curbing the CO2 emission levels
from the existing sources as well as forcing and facilitation of the green infrastructure and economy [8][35].
The necessity to reduce the CO2 concentration in the atmosphere has motivated research in carbon
capture and storage (CCS). The current leading CCS methods are, unfortunately, expensive and limited
in terms of utilization as well as efficient and safe storage [6][17]. Therefore, CCS as a whole is currently
evaluated to be a costly and limited way of reduction of the CO2 levels on the global scale [8]. At the same
time, there are vital industrial sectors which cannot be easily decarbonised, such as cement production,
steel production and chemical industry. It is therefore paramount to seek improvement of the existing CCS
methods alongside with development of the alternative CCS techniques based on the principles of high
capture efficiency, flexibility and reliability, scalability and availability, cost-efficiency and sustainability to
meet the Sustainable Development Goals.
Alkaline scrubbing is one of the developed carbon capture methods which uses high pH liquid sorbents -
typically NaOH and KOH solutions - to absorb CO2 and capture it chemically by facilitation of formation
carbonate and bicarbonate salts. The absorption happens in reactors where the sorbent is allowed to come
into contact with a CO2-rich gas, typically a spray-tower, or a bubble tank. The method is well-established
for small-to-medium scale CCS, but the costs of producing and management of large amounts of the sorbent
make it hard to scale. Moreover, it needs to be combined with a storage method, as the CO2 is extracted
from the salts and isolated when the sorbent is regenerated after the capture. An alternative sorbent type
for alkaline scrubbing is being explored at DTU Physics by Susan Louise Svane Stipp and her team [38].
The solvents are derived from construction waste such as crushed concrete and waste stone-wool fibres, when

2.2
Project description
This project produces the sensitivity analysis of a post-combustion CCS system which utilizes alkaline
scrubbing in a spray tower reactor configuration with a sorbent derived from construction waste.
The
hypothesis under test is therefore:
An alkaline solution derived from cation-rich construction waste can be used as a sorbent for
alkaline scrubbing of carbon dioxide in a spray tower.
Testing of the hypothesis requires a multidisciplinary approach which integrates principles of chemical engi-
neering and fluid dynamics. The following aims are pursued during the project:
 Assessment of the currently available CCS methods and understanding the properties of a good
CCS system and proposal of a conceptual design for a spray tower-based CCS system suitable for the
alkaline solution under consideration;
 Understanding the chemistry of the the relevant chemical reactions, their equilibria and kinetics
and a numerical investigation of the proposed system through solution speciation of three sorbents
derived at DTU Physics during different phases of the capture. Different mixing ratios of the sorbents
with water are tested to address the optimisation of use of the solutions from the construction waste;
 Understanding droplet motion and internal circulation in two steps. The first step is construc-
tion of an analytical velocity profile with consideration of the input parameters from the sorbent and
gas properties and the initial injection velocity and the countercurrent gas flow velocity. The second
step is the theoretical and numerical exploration of the Navier-Stokes and advection-diffusion equations
with the aim to predict the dominant CO2 transport mechanism within a single sorbent droplet. The
investigation is followed by numerical modelling of the convective transport with chemical reaction
modelling using the Finite Element Method;
 Experimental campaign for testing the numerical and theoretical findings. The campaign
involves design and construction of a laboratory-scale spray tower. The experiments aim at assessment
of the derived droplet velocity profile and evaluation of the CO2 capturing capabilities of a crushed-
concrete sorbent alongside sodium hydroxide sorbents used for comparison.
This is done through
letting the droplets fall through a gas mixture and measuring the initial and final pH to derive the
sorbents’ speciations.
 Presenting a model for estimation of the spray tower dimensions which incorporates the
findings of the project and shows the relation between the different aspects and parameters of the
problem.

This chapter addresses the existing CCS technologies and motivates the exploration of alkaline scrubbing

3.1
Assessment of carbon capture and storage technologies
The pressing need for mitigation of the climate change calls for rapid progress in the carbon capture and
storage (CCS) field. Development of the CCS techniques must be performed with the following priorities:
 Consistent high capture efficiency;
 Flexibility and reliability for retrofitting at the existing industrial objects as well as upcoming industrial
facilities;
 Scalability and availability;
 Cost-efficiency;
 Sustainability.
A group of scientists lead by Susan Louise Svane Stipp at DTU Physics works on developing an on-site post-
combustion CCS system based on the principle of CO2 absorption into an alkaline solution and subsequent
mineralisation of the CO2 for permanent storage or recycling. To understand how this concept fits with the
existing CCS technologies, a brief overview of those technologies is in order.
A number of ways to perform carbon capture and storage (CCS) has been developed in the recent
decades. Capture and storage are the main topics of research, while utilisation and transportation rely on
the existing infrastructure and commercially established methods [6]. A graphic overview of the capture and
storage technologies has been taken from Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS): The Way Forward review
[6] and is shown in Figure 3.1. The most used capturing techniques are the post-combustion capture and
the pre-combustion capture, while the most common storage method is the geological storage (Fig. 3.1).
The post-combustion capture involves the CO2 capture by a chemical or a physical solvent from the flue gas
mostly at existing industrial facilities, because it is relatively easy to retrofit The pre-combustion capture is

a popular method in production of blue hydrogen in steam methane reforming and coal gasification [6]. Both
techniques are often complemented by the geological storage, which involves transporting liquefied CO2 to
a deep-underground storage site, pumping it into the site and monitoring it to prevent leakages.
In post-combustion capture, amine compounds are commonly opted for [6]. Amines are chemicals derived
from ammonia, characterized by one or more hydrogen atoms being replaced by alkyl or aryl groups, and
are commonly used in chemical processes, including carbon capture thanks to their ability to reversibly
react with CO2 [15]. During the capture the amine-rich solvent comes into contact with the flue gas, and
the amine properties promote absorption and subsequent chemical binding of the CO2. Extraction of the
CO2 and regeneration of the absorbing liquid is done through heating. The process requires high energy for
regenerating the amine solution after CO2 absorption, thereby reducing the energy efficiency of power plants
which use it. Amines are hazardous to begin with and also degrade over time causing efficiency reduction
and corrosion in the system. The degradation also leads to the formation of potentially harmful emissions,
such as aerosols and carcinogenic compounds.
Amine-based systems are complex and require constant
monitoring and maintenance. The infrastructure for amine-based CCS is expensive to install and operate,
with additional costs from energy use, amine replacement and environmental controls. Amongst promising
alternatives for amines in the post-combustion capture are the CO2 removal with polymeric membranes and
calcium carbonate looping (CaL). The membranes allow the selective passage of CO2 pass through them.
The membrane are easy to produce commercially, and they can be combined with absorption by a sorbent
of choice. However, the membrane fouling and clogging issue as well as the ability to handle the industrial
flue gas amounts is still a challenge for the membrane technology [21]. CaL is a two-step capture technique,
9
==PAGE:10==
where the CO2 first reacts with CaO to produce CaCO3, and this product then heated to release CO2 from
the compound and regenerate CaO for repeated use. The technique offers high capturing efficiency at a
relatively low cost compared to amine absorption, however, it is plagued by high temperatures required as
well as material and sorbent degradation [4].
The carbon capture methods above require a storage technique, and geological storage is the most used
commercialised storage method.
It involves pumping liquefied CO2 into geological formations, such as
exhausted oil and gas mining fields or saline formations. While using the exhausted or operational mining
fields is claimed to be beneficial for enhanced oil recovery and easily deployable thanks to the pre-existing
infrastructure, geological storage poses several challenges and risks. The primary concern is the risk of CO2
leakages from storage sites, which undermines the use of the method altogether. This can happen as a result
of fractures in the rock formations or poorly sealed extraction wells, and therefore careful site selection
and continuous monitoring is crucial. There is also the risk of induced seismic activity, where increased
pressure from CO2 injection can trigger small earthquakes in otherwise seismically stable regions, which has
been studied and documented [17]. Another concern is the groundwater contamination, where the escaping
CO2 could acidify water and damage ecosystem as the consequence. This storage method therefore does not
always ensure safe and permanent storage of CO2 and does not offer any sustainable CO2 utilization instead.
An alternative to the discussed carbon-capture processes is the alkaline scrubbing method. It involves
contacting the flue gas with a high pH-solution for absorption and temporary capture in the form of bicar-
bonates and carbonates. The salts remain dissolved in the sorbent until the CO2 is separated out through
thermal regeneration and captured for storage. The method is easily retrofittable and relies on a simple
concept. Moreover, the the end-products are not chemically toxic and are safer if the sorbent is handled in
accordance with the guidelines for basic solutions. This method works for small-to-medium scale facilities
because it requires continuous input or regeneration of KOH and NaOH. The reactor types used to absorb
CO2 with the alkaline solutions can be packed bed absorbers and spray towers. Packed bed absorbers use
a packed column filled with materials that facilitate gas-liquid contact by increasing the surface area where
absorption can occur. Spray towers are chambers where flue gas is sprayed with a liquid sorbent, enhancing
gas-liquid interaction and CO2 capture efficiency. For larger-scale facilities, it becomes a bulky and expensive
because large amounts of solution are required to manage the flue gas flow.
The novel construction-waste derived alkaline solutions are explored at DTU Physics [38]. The solutions
are rich with divalent cations and have a high pH, which makes them potentially suitable for alkaline
scrubbing.
Moreover, there is minimal utilization of construction waste in the current global economy,
which provides abundance of low-cost raw materials for the novel sorbent production [32]. The team at
DTU Physics focuses on the waste material surface dynamics when it is placed in water to dissolve and the
speciation of the solution which is produced in this leeching process. One of the most abundant divalent ions
in the DTU Physics solutions is Ca2+. The ion bonds readily with carbonate ions to produce calcite. Calcite
is a stable compound which occurs naturally in geological formations. Creating calcite on-site is a less costly
and safe way of storing CO2, because the CO2 stripping process is not necessary, and CO2 does not need
to be liquefied for further transport. The stability and non-toxicity of calcite has made it the key player
in the mineral storage technique used by a resent commercial success of CarbFix in Iceland [10] [20]. The
firm injects CO2-saturated water underground into porous basaltic rock formations abundant in Iceland’s
underground. The captured CO2 is chemically converted into solid carbonates, which which ensures long-
term safe storage. Provided that the flue gas is stripped of other impurities, such as sulphur compounds or
heavy metals, and the sorbent is carefully prepared, using construction waste-derived solutions for CCS and
utilization can become a cost-effective and sustainable alternative to KOH- and NaOH-scrubbing as well as
geological storage in exhausted mining fields or saline formations. It is therefore attractive to explore the
properties of the solutions derived from the construction waste as a candidate for CCS.

3.2
Conceptual design of a CCS system
The choice of a spray tower configuration for investigation is based on the conceptual simplicity, low pro-
duction cost and the relative ease of retrofitting for continuous operation at existing facilities. Spray towers
provide a large surface area of contact between the sorbent and the gas, and it is an established design type.
Since the alkaline solution under investigation is novel, many aspects must be identified and investigated. A
brief account and motivation for investigation of the defined parameters of importance are given below.
The primary objective of a reactor is to maximize the absorption of the target gas into the solvent, and
one of the parameters of importance is the sorbent residence time in the tower. Depending on how fast the
sorbent saturates, it needs a certain amount of time in contact with the flue gas. The residence time directly
affects the dimensions of the tower (Ht, dt in Fig. 3.2) and it depends on many factors. Firstly, it is directly
related to the flux across the gas-fluid interface, and it is strongly influenced by the circulation and diffusion
in the solvent as well as the reaction sinks if such are present. The reason for that is the direct proportionality
between the flux and the concentration gradient across the interface [28][26]. The gas diffused through the
surface is taken into the sorbent bulk by the convective flow, keeping the gradient across the interface, and
therefore the flux, high, promoting the absorption.
Another parameter is the sorbent surface area. In spray towers, the surface area is the summation over
the surface areas of the droplets in it if the wetting of the tower walls is neglected (Fig.3.2).
Maximal
achievable surface area is limited by the velocity of the countercurrent gas, because very small droplets
risk being carried upwards with the flue gas flow profile vgas(t). Moreover, an optimal droplet size must
be selected in relation to the CO2 species transport inside the droplets. The surface area must therefore
be chosen appropriately based on the amount of the gas which needs to be processed and the absorption
properties of the solvent. This and the circulation dynamics in the sorbent are explored in Chapter 5.

The chemical component must be considered carefully when investigating both the absorption and the
CCS system design. CO2 is classified as slightly soluble in water with the solubility at around 33 mol/m3
[34]. In alkaline solutions, however, the apparent solubility is a lot higher because reactions with hydroxide
ions give these solutions a high capacity for converting CO2 and cations in the solutions into carbonate salts
11
==PAGE:12==
[7]. This provides a reaction sink which promotes the CO2 flux into the solvent. It is, however, crucial to
design the CCS with the chemical reactions on which the system relies in mind. For instance, if the sorbent
becomes oversaturated with calcite, the calcite begins to precipitate. The precipitation can become an issue
if it happens in undesirable places of the CCS system such as the spray tower nozzles or the pipes which
carry the sorbent around. Moreover, if the concentration of CO2 in the sorbent is to high compared to the
equilibrium with the atmospheric air, then the CO2-rich sorbent can begin to de-gas once out of the spray
tower and create over-pressure in the system piping. This is explored further in Chapter 4.
All in all, the system design must be derived from the chemical properties of the sorbent as well as its
amounts available, the volume of the of the gas which needs to be processed. One such design has been
conceptualised in collaboration with DTU Physics, and it is presented graphically in Figure 3.2.
The CO2 absorption and the CaCO3 precipitation are separated by the means of introduction of a
precipitation tank before the spray tower. Another design choice is the dilution of the sorbent with water to
address the limited amount of sorbent alongside untimely de-gassing and precipitation (see Ch. 4 for more
details). At the initiation, the diluted high pH sorbent is directed to come into contact with the flue gas
flowing at the volumetric flow rate of Qgas in the spray tower. The sorbent on the outflow is CO2-rich and
has lower pH than upon the inflow. Then, the the CO2-saturated sorbent is mixed with the a new portion of
the non-depleted sorbent in the optimal proportion such that the calcium from the sorbent and the carbonate
ions from the CO2-saturated sorbent react to produce CaCO3 such that it begins to precipitate. The mixture
is then allowed to settle in the tank where it is to precipitate all the formed calcite. To avoid the exponential
liquid accumulation in the system, w% of the solution is taken out of the loop for processing, which could be
the leeching process for creation of more sorbent or analysis. Once the CaCO3 is precipitated, the sorbent
it is directed into the tower to absorb more CO2. Depending on Qgas and the target CO2 percentage in the
flue gas on the exit from the tower the Qsol can be adjusted.
This conceptual design requires a multidisciplinary analysis and optimisation to function as conceived.
Firstly, the mixing ratio of the CO2-rich sorbent and the fresh sorbent needs to be determined (Ch. 4).
Secondly, a relationship between the system parameters needs to be established, and the parameters need to
be combined to constitute a physical model for the capture and mineralisation process. Since the flux and
the surface area of the sorbent required for absorption of some given CO2 in the flue gas at the volumetric
flow Qgas is not known, an investigation of the interplay between the macroscopic flow and the droplet
internal circulation needs to be performed as well (Ch. 5). Finally, the flow dynamics and the chemistry of
the process need to be combined to estimate the flux across the sorbent interface and the required residence
time for the optimal saturation (Chapters 6 - 7).

Chapter 4
Exploring the chemistry
The chapter is dedicated to understanding the forces behind chemical reactions and the factors which influ-
ence the reaction progression. The CO2 equilibrium in aqueous solutions is introduced and visualised via
the Bjerrum plot, and the reaction rates relevant for this equilibrium are presented from literature. More-
over, the influences on the formation and precipitation of CaCO3 are outlined, and the saturation index at
equilibrium is introduced as a tool for understanding the speciation of the solution where CaCO3 is present.
An investigation of the chemical equilibria for three novel alkaline solutions is performed using the software
PHREEQc, where the optimal mixing ratio for the proposed CCS system design is determined.

The dynamics of calcite formation and precipitation are dependent on pH because H+ ions ”compete”
with Ca2+ for the carbonate. The higher [H+] favours the formation of hydrogen carbonate over calcite, as
there is then excess of H+. Conversely, the calcite formation is more favourable at higher pH levels, as the
CO2 species is mostly present in the form for carbonate. The optimal pH for calcite formation is around
10.3, while a pH from approximately 5 and lower hinders the process [34]. As calcite forms, bicarbonate
gives off H+ and turns into the carbonate, leading to a decrease in pH.

4.2
Kinetics
The equilibrium condition describes chemical systems in their final state and to what extent a chemical
reaction is possible from the thermodynamic standpoint. Understanding the time frame of the equilibrium
state achievement requires definition of reaction rates. A reaction rate is a quantity that represents the
reduction of reactant concentration - or increase in product concentration - over time. The rate of reaction
depends on a multitude of factors, such as the the concentration of reactants, the medium in which reaction
takes place, temperature, pressure, the reactant surface area and physical state as well as the presence
of catalysts and inhibitors. Temperature, pressure and concentrations relate to the number of collisions
necessary for the reaction occur. Surface area and the state of reactants also contribute to the reaction
speed, as the reactants need to interact to create products. Catalysts and inhibitors can affect the activation
energy for the reaction and alter the availability of the the reacting species in the medium. These factors
being addressed in the formal definition of the reaction rate give a basis for estimation of the reaction
progression in time.
The starting point for the determining the reaction dynamics is determination of the reaction order. The
reaction order is the relationship between the concentrations of reacting species and the rate of a reaction
through the rate constant k. The order and the rate constant are typically derived from the the rate reaction
expression that is obtained from experimental data.
For reversible reactions, both forward and reverse
reactions are considered; the system is in equilibrium when the forward and reverse rates are equal and
opposite: rf = rr. Applied to the CO2 equilibrium in water, the reaction orders of the relevant reversible

PHREEQc simulations
One of the objectives of the project is finding out the mixing ratio between the fresh sorbent and the CO2-
rich sorbent for optimal functionality of the proposed system.
PHREEQc software was used to achieve
that. PHREEQc is a geochemical modelling software used for simulating chemical reactions and transport
processes in water and soils. It allows users to model a wide range of geochemical processes such as aqueous
speciation, mineral precipitation/dissolution and ion exchange.
It is worth mentioning that PHREEQc
algorithms operate under a set of assumptions, those being:
 Infinite reaction time - all numbers are from equilibria that may take much longer than a cycle time
in the CCS capture system;
 No kinetic considerations are involved - the algorithm calculates thermodynamic equilibria;
 Standard temperature and pressure conditions apply.

solution types or gas mixture compositions to find a custom mixing ratio that is suitable for the process in

Model development
Nearly all system parameters are intertwined and are affecting each other. The spray tower concept suggests
that the sorbent is to be sprayed into the carbonation tower at a volumetric flow rate, Qsol [m3/s], that
corresponds with the optimal sorbent capacity in the relation to the gas flow. This implies that the solution
is separated into many droplets the size of which is often described by a droplet-size distribution. The droplet
formation with nozzles is generally driven by the liquid sheet instability - otherwise known as Rayleigh-Taylor
instability - followed by liquid jet instability commonly referred to as Rayleigh-Plateau instability [30]. The
final droplet-size distribution depends on the thickness of the liquid sheet a nozzle produces, which in turn
depend on the Q and the nozzle geometry. Determination of the droplet-size distribution is beyond the
scope of this thesis, and it is henceforth assumed that the droplet radius range is [0.1,5] mm, whereas the
initial velocity v0 does not exceed 50 m s−1 to keep the model simple. The number and the shape of the
particles allow to attribute a surface area to the solution in the tower and explore the absorption dynamics.,
and this is why it is important to find out how these droplets move. For establishment of basic relations
between the initial fall conditions and the droplet dimensions it would suffice to begin with considering just
one spherical droplet of a fixed radius that finds itself in a free fall through a countercurrent homogeneous
gas flow. The assumption of the constant spherical shape is based on examining the investigations by [2]
and [16], among others, where the deformation of the droplets is shown to be low to moderate. Knowing
the velocity profile of the droplet gives a tool for estimation of the residence time and the speed range of
the flow around the droplet that can be used later for studying the CO2 absorption from the gas phase via
solving the advection-diffusion equation. The purpose of this section is, therefore, construction of a simple

The numerical study of the flow and the convective transport of CO2 has been attempted in a variety of

6.3
Geometry, boundary conditions and the initial values
The chosen geometry for a spherical liquid droplet in a gas mixture is shown in Fig. 6.2. It is beneficial
to reduce the dimensions of the problem, as it reduces the required computing resources and time. In this
project the geometry utilises the cylindrical symmetry of the system, thus reducing the problem to two
dimensions. This dimensional reduction results in the reduced computing time, however, it also limits the
information acquired on the symmetry type in the droplet. For instance, there may be more than one vortex
in the droplet, which has been investigated in [29]. If there are multiple vortices in the droplet, it may lead
to less effective advective transport and increased role of the diffusive transport. The extreme case would
be where the number of vortices is so large that the advective transport coefficient is much smaller than
the diffusion coefficient. This would mean that the Peclet number is small, and that the flow in the droplet
is effectively stagnant. Conversely, if there is one big vortex, as is the case with the Hill’s spherical vortex
solution, the Peclet number is large, meaning that the advection contributes significantly to the transport
inside the droplet. The following numerical studies thus quantify the situation where the advective transport
is the dominant.

would have the terminal velocity at around 5 m/s. A number of values were tested in multiple simulation
runs, and the best flow simulation where the droplet stayed in the simulation domain was achieved at the
value of U = 6 m/s. The simulation is therefore closer to a real-life scenario where the droplet is injected
from a nozzle into the gas flow at around its terminal velocity. This choice directly affects the comparability
of the numerical results with the experimental work in Chapter 7 because the expected flow in the numerical
solution is faster.

A classic way of validating the mesh is conducting a mesh convergence study, where the difference between
the coarser-mesh solutions and the best solution achieved is investigated. The sign of good convergence is an
exponential decrease of the difference as a function of a mesh fineness parameter. It was, however, impossible
to obtain converging solutions for the laminar flow solution because any coarser mesh would result in growing
instabilities in the solution. The way to tell that a solution is diverging is inspection of the convergence plot,
where the reciprocal of the step size taken by the solver is plotted against the study step. An example of a
diverging solution convergence plot from one of the attempted mesh convergence studies is shown in Figure

possible to obtain the desired results for the mesh validation. It may be because the best obtained results

meaning that any coarser mesh could not resolve the flow and/or absorption dynamics, thereby destabilising
the solution.
The mesh convergence issue led to finding an alternative way of validating the numerical results. The
alternative way of the solution validation was the qualitative examination of the solutions on the subject of
mesh effects (Section 6.5) and comparison with the results in literature. Under the given circumstances, this
mesh size appeared to be sufficient to resolve the fluid element behaviour and make the model converge in
within the available time and resources. Next to no mesh effects were detected in the velocity field, and the
solutions appeared to yield reasonable results when viewed from the standpoint of the theoretically expected
results and the published results. This is explored further in the results section (Section 6.5) of this chapter
and in Chapter 9.

Chapter 7
Experimental work
This chapter is about the experimental work that has been conducted during the project with the purpose of
testing the theoretical and numerical studies performed in a laboratory setting. The experimental campaign
had three objectives:
 Acquiring the velocity profile and dimensions of a falling droplet by recording liquid droplets in free
fall;
 Benchmarking the tower concept against other CO2 absorption configurations using three NaOH solu-
tions by the means of measuring the pH of the solutions before and after having them in contact with
a 10% CO2 gas mixture and comparing the results to the results found in literature [9][37];
 Testing the CO2 absorption performance of one alkaline solution derived from crushed concrete to
compare the results with the numerical simulations in COMSOL Multiphysics and
PHREEQ-c using the pH values before and after having the solution samples in contact with a 10%
CO2 gas mixture.
The experiments required design of a tower-like airtight vessel (from now on referred to as a tower), so
the chapter opens with a brief description of the tower design and the final model. That is followed by
the experiment overview based on the objectives outlined above. Then, the method and the data analysis
for each phase are presented. Each phase is concluded by a comparison of the experimental results to the
developed models and discussion.
7.1
Tower design
The final tower design is shown in Figure 7.1. It consists of three main components: a lid with openings for
pumping in CO2 mixtures, one solution inlet and one outlet to attach a CO2 sensor, the transparent tower
body with d1 = 15 cm and h = 100 cm, and a funnel with a valve for sample collection which has diameter
d1 = 0.5 cm. The annotated sketch of the tower design is shown in Figure 7.1. The transparency of the
main body allows for droplet tracing and subsequent video analysis to compare the tanh-solution derived
in Chapter 5 to the profiles derived from the experiments. The tower materials are chosen such that any
chemical interactions with the tested solutions, the gas mixtures and the auxiliary compounds is minimised.
The tower construction has been performed by the DTU Physics Workshop.

The oil which has been selected for the Phases 2 and 3 is the IQX 2-Stroke mineral oil by Kroon. It
has a red colourant in it and incidentally looks similar to the dyed water from Phase 1. They are, however,
nothing alike - the density of the oil is 879 kg/m3 at 15 ◦C, while the dynamic viscosity is roughly equal to
that of water first at 40 ◦C[1]. The relatively low density ensures that the oil would float above the water,
and the colour difference makes for a clear separation between the water and the oil. The oil is claimed to
not mix with clear water [1], however, it may create an emulsion with aqueous solutions at high pH. Prior
to the main part of the measurements the interaction between oil and the three solutions has been tested
on the subject of the pH measurement alteration as the consequence of physical interaction. First, a tube
was filled with the oil and a pH measurement with a clean and calibrated pH-meter was measured to be
exactly 7.447. Then, a few millilitres of the oil were placed into three tubes, and a 5-6 ml of NaOH solution
was dropped into its designated tube using a pipette; it was done as quickly as possible to minimise the
interaction with the laboratory atmosphere. The tubes were shaken for a few seconds to let the solutions
be in contact with the oil. The measured pH for NaOH initial pH 12.1 was approximately 10.3, for NaOH
initial pH 10.0 - approximately 8.9, and for NaOH initial pH 7.9 - approximately 7.7. It appeared that the
presence of the oil in the tube interfered with the measurement of the pH, and it was therefore decided to
extract the solution from beneath the layer of oil in the sample tube into a separate tube before performing
the pH measurements. This would imply that the solutions would be briefly exposed to the laboratory air
and absorb additional CO2. It is therefore expected that the measured pH is lower that the actual pH
immediately after the fall is complete.
Another remark worth making is that it was crucial to minimise the solutions’ air exposure time to prevent
the CO2 from being absorbed from the laboratory air. Moreover, it was important for the experiment to
have the solution in droplets for the accurate measurement of the flux through the droplet surface.

Chapter 9
Discussion of the project results
The key results of this project are the estimates for the optimal mixing ratio for the proposed spray tower-
based CCS system and the numerical - as well as experimental - estimates for the CO2 flux into the the
crushed-concrete sorbent in the form of droplets of radius 1 mm.
Firstly, the optimal mixing ratio for
absorption from a gas mixture with 10% CO2 in it was found to be 3 parts fresh crushed-concrete sorbent
to 7 parts CO2-rich decalcified sorbent form the spray tower. This mixing ratio should be able to bring
down the amount of the sorbent being used and address the potential de-gassing or calcite precipitation
issues which could occur in the system. Secondly, the average CO2 flux into an undiluted crushed-concrete
(cc) sorbent 2 mm droplet was found to be 4.9e-3 mol/m2s when the CO2 concentration in the gas mixture
was 10% and the droplet velocity was 6 m/s. The flux was found to be insufficient for a spray tower-based
system, as the liquid-to-gas ratio exceeded the typical spray tower ratios [14] by two orders of magnitude. A
long list of factors and assumptions have influenced the final results on the flux especially. A breakdown of
the factors with benchmarking against the earlier published results is given in the following sections.
9.1
Proposed CCS design and chemistry
The proposed conceptual CCS design includes the spray tower, alkaline storage vessel, a mixing unit and pre-
cipitation tank. The design requires high-quality mixing, well-operated precipitation and balanced sorbent-
gas flow throughout the system. As seen in the chapter on the spray tower estimated, the a number of
parameters which were not considered in the calculation, such as the pressure drop and collective droplet
behaviour as well as the wall wetting were not considered. The droplets were assumed to be identical, which
is often not the case for spray nozzles used in spray towers. This affects the estimated surface area and
absorption efficiency. Improvement of the design can be done consulting a variety of sources which allow
to estimate the actual gas volumetric flow [28], the sorbent distribution in the reactor and droplet size
distribution [3] the required energy to operate the system [14] [30].
The data acquired with PHREEQc can be used to get a lower-boundary estimate for the volumetric flow
rates of the alkaline solution, flue gas and the CO2-rich solution which are required to achieve mineralisation
volumes necessary for the industrial-scale process. It is important to mention that the PHREEQc data only
reflects the equilibrium speciations and assumes that the chemical compounds have achieved equilibrium
at each stage of the simulated process. The data on the mixing ratios were not used in the numerical or
experimental studies, and the calculated fluxes as well the diluted cc sorbent capacity are likely to be higher
than they would be in reality if the diluted sorbent was to be used in alkaline scrubbing process. Dilution of
the sorbent means that it would saturate faster and be able to absorb less CO2 than the undiluted sorbent.
The main output of this investigation is the method for assessing chemical equilibria in the proposed CCS
system which can be used with different sorbents and CO2 concentrations.

9.2
Physics
Droplet velocity profile
The analysis performed in the analytical velocity profile section has yielded the analytical solution (5.11) for
the droplet fall where a spherical droplet of constant density and viscosity falls freely through a homogeneous
countercurrent gas affected only by the gravity and the drag force. The form of the solution agrees exactly
with the classical solution [40] (eq-n 2.57) for a sphere falling from rest, although it is fit for a solid sphere
rather than for a liquid droplet. The main difference between the classical solution and the solution obtained
in this project is the presence of the counter-current term, which makes sense, as the classical deals with
a zero-countercurrent-flow system. As for the solution drawbacks, there is no evaporation or deformation
considerations made in the model, has been shown to be important in the numerical studies chapter and
previously published studies [2] [3]. This is because the derivation procedure results in losing a range of
velocity derivatives - the differential equation was squared, meaning that the acceleration was no longer
allowed to change the sign. To predict the change of the direction of motion, a droplet that started with
some initial velocity and come to a full stop, implying the presence of a global minimum in the velocity
profile and a zero in derivative at finite time. This is not observed in Figure 5.2, and the mathematical
form of the solution does not allow it either. The constraints on the flow parameters which follow from the
analytical derivation give a limitation for the use of the solution to avoid unphysical predictions, so as long as
the droplets are not too small and the gas flow is not to high, the model holds. The expansion of the model
to three dimensions and a detailed investigation of the differential equation with, for instance, variational
calculus can provide a more realistic model of the droplet motion in the tower.
The analytical solution, however, has shown to fit the experimental data well and predict the droplet radii
sufficiently well (See Chapter 7, phase 1). The main uncertainty was concluded to rest with the experimental
methods and not the model.
Internal circulation
The properties of the numerical solutions found in Chapter 6 appear to agree with the expected values for
similar systems investigated in the assessed literature [22] [29] [43]. It appears that the solution tends to
the steady state derived by Hill [22] if one is to compare the streamline patterns (Figures 5.3, 6.12). Two
vortices were observed in the transient state similar to [29], but those ultimately subsided in the steady state.
The work by Wylock [43] on which the theoretical predictions were based assumes the Reynolds number
between 0.1 and 250, and the the numerical study in the current work largely agrees with this range when
the average Re is considered. However, Re is shown to be larger than 250 in the regions with high velocities
in the centre of the droplet and on the side (Fig. 6.14a). One of the key results in that paper [43] is the
evolution of the average Sherwood number, ¯

work is assumed to have reached the steady circulation at 50 ms, and the liquid dynamic viscosity, density
and CO2 thermal diffusivity the Fourier number corresponds to 0.001.

Also, the model ultimately simulated the circulation in water at 25 ◦C, and not the cc sorbent, which

The created model is ultimately quite limited because of the missing moving reference frame.
The

different transport properties than the steady state. It is, therefore, an obvious extension of the model to

Absorption and transport dynamics in the numerical model
The evolution of the CO2 concentration profile qualitatively agrees with what would be expected from such a
system [43]. The advective flow largely dominates the transport while the CO2 concentration is low, and then
the role of diffusion becomes more important as the concentration profile stabilises, and the only unsaturated
region of the droplet remains in the vicinity of the vortex. The drawback with using the steady flow model
without the droplet deformation is the early saturation of the high-pH sorbents which was obtained as the
result of the study (Figures 6.27a - 6.27b). This likely occurred because the boundary layer saturated, and
therefore no new CO2 was diffusing into the droplet. This was also the cause of the cc sorbent numerical
study termination at the simulation time of 0.45 s rather than 0.05 s - the chemical speciation of the droplet
stopped changing, and the droplet appeared to have started de-gassing as the average CO2 concentration
began to drop a little - about 0.01 mol/m3 out of 6.7 mol/m3 that the droplet achieved before saturating at
around 0.3 s. The NaOH pH 12 sorbent also showed the sign of saturation before it achieved the equilibrium
just towards the end of the numerical study. As shown in Figures 6.16c - 6.17a, the deformation facilitated
the mixing in the droplet during the transient phase. It is unclear how the early saturation affected the
numerically derived fluxes ⟨FCO2⟩, which appear to be on the same order of magnitude as two results found
in the literature [37], [9]. However, the fluxes in the mentioned papers were estimated for NaOH solutions
with a significantly higher pH - 13.74 [9] and in the range above 13 [37]. One would assumed that the
performance of less concentrated NaOH sorbent would be subpar to that of a more concentrated one, but
this can be caused by a different contacting scheme. For instance, if the sorbent was to be more stagnant
than the droplet in the project’s work, then the flux would drop sooner. Recent work on development of
spray towers show-cased better performance that in packed-bed reactors [11].
Another unsettling observation in the numerical solution results is the not negligible presence of the
negative concentration regions in the initialisation phase for all sorbents.
This is a clear sign that the
numerical solution is suboptimal, and that it requires revisions. Coupled with the manual management of
the reactions, it provided results which are not reliable. In reality, reactions do not happen at discrete times,
but continuously. The absence of reactions in the initialisation made the CO2 concentrations the same at
0.05 s for all sorbents, which is unphysical - the properties of the sorbents were too different for it to be true.
It is worth mentioning the choice made for the cc-sorbent in relation to the initial speciation.
The
experimental measurements showed that the sorbent had the pH 11.463 at the beginning of the measurements,
while the PHREEQc speciation predicted the pH of 11.7 according to the latest data from the discrete flow
analyser provided by DTU Physics. While it is possible to compare the experiment by scaling the numerical
solution so that it resembles the internal circulation developed in the droplet falling from rest, it was not
done, and therefore initialising the numerical study with cc-sorbent having been exposed to CO2 before the
initialisation was not a reasonable choice. This has likely resulted in the numerical simulation results not
doing the justice for the cc-sorbent relative to the other sorbents.
All in all the numerical study has given an insight into the circulation patterns in droplets falling through
air which are consistent with the literature, but the advection-diffusion equation simulation with the manual
management of the reaction term for CO2 has been challenging and provided limited insight into the CO2
dissolution and speciation of the final solution, which is difficult to verify. An incorporated reaction term
simulation with the dynamic flow throughout would be a better option if it was available.

Experimental work
The experimental results from Phase 1 are generally in good agreement with the analytical velocity profile
solution. The systematic overestimation of the the droplet radii came from the limited resolution of Camera

Better camera resolution would greatly improve the quality of the fits and give a better understanding of the

droplet generator, would reduce the human error and allow to test droplets of smaller sizes. Smaller droplet

The experimental results from Phases 2 and 3 yielded the results which are partially consistent with
the numerical study results and the theory known beforehand. The final amount of the absorbed CO2 was
lower than in the numerical studies, and so was the average flux. This is a reasonable result because the
circulation was hypothesised to be less developed in the fall from rest compared to the fall at around 6 m/s.
The advective transport was therefore slower at depleting the boundary layer to maintain the concentration
gradient. The experimental procedure also helped to reveal the likely inconsistent result from the numerical
study, which was the abnormal flux and total CO2 absorbed by NaOH pH 8. Although NaOH pH 8 and
NaOH pH 10 behave similarly in comparison to the higher pH solutions, it should still not be possible for
the NaOH pH 8 to perform better than the NaOH pH 10.
The experimental setup was simple and had its limitations. Firstly, it was difficult to produce droplets
of consistent size manually. The total of around 1200 droplets were made by hand for Phases 2 and 3, which
became progressively more difficult as the measurements went on.
Another consequence of the manual
droplet generation was the lingering time at the tip of the pipette, which meant that the droplets were
absorbing the CO2 prior to the fall. Secondly, the droplets were observed to linger on top of the oil seal
before falling through, which increased the absorption time. This implies that the experimentally derived
fluxes may be higher than they were in reality. Once again, automated droplet generation would improve
the quality of the experimental method. Moreover, if the oil seal was to be used again, a less viscous oil
should be selected to minimise the droplet lingering.
9.4
Spray tower dimensions estimates
The final tower dimensions estimates have yielded that the cc sorbent is unfit for spray towers based on the
data gathered in this project. The pH values of the alkaline sorbents tested in the literature appear to be
above pH 12 [9][13][24], which means that a higher concentration of hydroxide is required for a sufficient flux
and an optimal amount of the sorbent for a spray tower configuration to be viable. Enhancement of the cc
sorbent with addition of the fly-ash solution could be a possibility. A number of unexplored physics such
as the two-phase flow of a droplet distribution through countercurrent gas flow and pressure balance in the
spray tower are crucial for a realistic estimate.
Although the results of the projects in terms of the numbers are not particularly impressive or specific,
the methods and programming tools developed and used in the process can be reused for other solution types
and CO2 gas mixtures and droplet sizes to establish understanding of the interplay between the chemical
and physical properties of a spray tower-based CCS system.

Chapter 10
Future work
To further enhance the efficiency and applicability of the proposed carbon capture system, three main
paths are identified: sorbent performance, numerical study improvement and expansion, and the sorbent-gas
contacting scheme.
Improving sorbent efficacy by experimenting with the addition of higher pH solutions to the crushed-
concrete sorbent. This could potentially increase the CO2 absorption capacity and rate, which would make
the sorbent more fit for spray towers.
Future work in numerical simulations should address several key areas.
To improve the accuracy of
simulations, the effects of mesh anomalies, particularly in the corners of the liquid domain, can be mitigated
by avoiding the boundary layer function along the droplet symmetry axis. Additionally, implementing a
suppression method for negative concentrations will ensure more realistic results. Enhancing the reaction
term by making it more continuous will improve the depiction of CO2 absorption dynamics.
Applying
non-dimensionalisation and using a weak-PDE interface would provide greater control over the simulation
parameters, allowing for a more precise and customizable model. Moreover, simulating multiple droplets
rather than a single droplet will offer better insights into the overall performance and behaviour of the
sorbent in a spray tower environment. Finally, utilizing the mixing ratios determined through PHREEQc
modelling could further refine the effectiveness of the CO2 capture process.
A better evaluation of the sorbent performance in a spray tower-based CCS system requires assessment
of the flow balance, temperature and pressure changes as well as the energy cost of the capture process.
Exploring alternative configurations for the spray tower, such as using a packed-bed or bubble tank design,
could provide insights into optimizing the alkaline scrubbing process with the crushed-concrete sorbent,
potentially leading to improved CO2 capture efficiency.

Chapter 11
Conclusion
This thesis investigated the optimal conditions for a proposed spray tower-based carbon capture and storage
system using alkaline scrubbing with crushed-concrete derived sorbent. The key findings indicate that a
mixing ratio of 3 parts fresh sorbent to 7 parts CO2-rich sorbent optimizes CO2 absorption from a 10%
CO2 gas mixture. However, the observed CO2flux into undiluted crushed concrete sorbent droplets (4.9e-3
mol/m2s) was insufficient for a spray tower configuration, primarily because of the elevated liquid-to-gas
ratio which exceeded typical spray tower values by two orders of magnitude. Increase in pH of the sorbent
is suggested as the possible way of increasing the flux and the sorbent capacity.
The experimental and numerical studies highlighted several limitations in the current model and experi-
mental setup. The analytical solution for droplet velocity was consistent with classical models but did not
account for droplet deformation, evaporation or drift, limiting its applicability. The numerical simulations,
while generally aligned with the results found in literature, also demonstrated significant challenges, par-
ticularly with the manual management of reaction terms and use of the steady circulation pattern for CO2
convection in the sorbent droplet. These issues are hypothesised to have led to early saturation of the sorbent
in the steady phase and unphysical concentration profiles in the initialisation phase of the numerical studies.
Experimental results largely supported the analytical velocity predictions but revealed challenges in
droplet size consistency and CO2 flux estimation. The manual droplet generation method introduced vari-
ability, and the experimental conditions potentially led to overestimated flux values. Moreover, exposure of
the crushed-concrete sorbent to the air primary to the tapping and during the experimental procedure led
to the decrease in pH and a likely deteriorating performance throughout.
Overall, while the study provides valuable insights into the behaviour of crushed-concrete sorbent droplets
in a CCS system, it also identifies significant areas for improvement. Future work should focus on refining the
numerical model to incorporate droplet deformation and a moving reference frame, as well as enhancing the
experimental setup with automated droplet generation, improved sealing and a less invasive pH measurement
techniques. These advancements would enable more accurate predictions of CO2 absorption rates and sorbent
capacities.
