Abstract
Air pollution constitute a worldwide risk to both human health and the environment. Over the
past years, the air quality in Copenhagen has gained increasing awareness among citizens and
politicians within the municipality. This project aimed to quantify the contributions and influence
from local street­level emissions to the monitored air quality in Copenhagen.
This project is based on air quality data monitored by FORCE Technology on behalf of the mu­
nicipality of Copenhagen spanning from the fourth quarter of 2020 up to and including 2022 at
five different locations around the city. Particulate matter (PM) at two different sizes, PM2.5 and
PM10, along with particle number concentration (PN) and nitrogen oxides (NOx) are monitored at
the five air quality monitoring stations, while black carbon (BC) is monitored at two. The moni­
tored concentration of BC is subsequently differentiated into a fraction originating from fossil fuels,
BCff, and a fraction originating from wood burning, BCwb. This project focuses on PM2.5, PN,
NO2, NO and BC including the differentiation over a 1­year period from 01/01/2022 to 31/12/2022,
while the air quality data from 2020 and 2021 have been a part of the process of understanding
the characteristics of the air quality in Copenhagen. Air quality data is currently presented in low
time resolutions as 1­hour values, daily averages or annual averages, and this project has further
tested the importance of the time resolution of data.
The air quality data showed limited variations between air quality monitoring stations for PM2.5
and PN, while NO2, NO and BC showed greater level of variation across the monitoring network.
This project demonstrates how the moving median can be used to quantify the contribution from
local street­level emissions in data sets in high time resolution (1 second), while spectral analysis
is applied to several data sets in different time resolution. Using spectral analysis, this project
quantifies that local street­level emissions account for 2.4 % ± 0.4 % when considering PM2.5. For
PN, NO2 and NO, local street­level emissions respectively contribute 14.2 % ± 3.5 %, 7.9 % ±
0.8 % and 8.7 % ± 2.0 %, and when considering BC, BCff and BCwb, local street­level emissions
contribute 9.0% ± 0.6%, 17.5% ± 0.4% and 6.4% ± 1.3%.
Considering the cases with data in high time resolution for the specified air quality monitoring
stations, the two methods obtained results within the same order regarding the contribution from
local street­level emissions. Additionally, the analyzes conducted show how contribution from local
street­level emissions tend to be underestimated, while urban background and long­range trans­
port will be overestimated when time resolution of data is lowered. To gain further knowledge,
this project suggests additional measurements of air quality data in high time resolution along with
an improvement of the ranges considered for source apportionment conducted using the spectral
analysis.

Introduction
Over the past years, air pollution and its potential to both climate and human health has gained
increasing awareness among politicians and citizens. Air pollution is known to impact the radiative
balance of the Earth, when black carbon is deposited in the Arctic along with particles affecting
the properties and lifetime of clouds (IPCC 2021). Over the past decade, the additionally negative
impacts on human health have obtained an increasing focus as well. The World Health Organiza­
tion (WHO) estimates that poor air quality cause 7 million premature deaths worldwide every year,
and air pollution have been declared an environmental threat to human health at a level alongside
climate change (Health and Energy Platform of Action 2021). In September 2021, WHO presented
a set of stricter standards, the air quality guidelines (AQGs), which are based on a thorough review
of studies and evidence describing the adverse effects caused by the increasing exposure to air
pollution (G. Sun et al. 2016; Abdo et al. 2016; G. Chen et al. 2018; Ai et al. 2019; Wang et al.
2019; Wu et al. 2019; Zhang et al. 2020). The AQGs concern a set of substantial air pollutants,
including particulate matter ≤2.5 µm (PM2.5) and nitrogen dioxide (NO2) (World Health Organiza­
tion 2021). Compared to the legally binding EU standards, the AQGs recommended by WHO are
indicative. In December 2019, the European Commission engaged to continue to improve the air
quality within the EU, and additionally revise the current ambient air quality directive (2008/50/EC)
with the aim of adapting the AQGs by WHO to reduce the number of premature deaths along with
the impact on ecosystems (European Commission 2020).
The national monitoring program for air quality in Denmark carried out by the Danish Center for
Environment and Energy at Aarhus University (DCE) shows that all monitoring stations are within
the EU standards, nevertheless, Ellermann et al. (2024) estimate that during 2022 the current air
quality caused about 3660 premature deaths in Denmark along with a range of additional negative
health impacts, including respiratory diseases. All of Denmark is covered within the national moni­
toring program, but the monitoring program shows a special focus on cities due to the combination
of high population density along with increased air pollution. This combination causes more people
to be exposed to the increasing level of air pollution resulting from traffic etc. (Lazić et al. 2016).
In 2019, it was estimated that 440 premature deaths in Copenhagen was a result of the current
air quality (Sundheds­ og Omsorgsforvaltningen 2021). Consequently, the municipality of Copen­
hagen initiated a process to improve the overall health of the citizens in Copenhagen. As a part of
that process, the municipality approved the C40­declaration on clean air, which requires a plan for
meeting the AQGs by WHO along with efforts to reduce the levels of air pollution parameters. For
this purpose, the municipality initiated a monitoring program, starting in 2020, composed of five air
quality monitoring stations (AQMSs) placed around the city monitoring five different air pollutants:
PM2.5, PM10, PN, NO2 and BC (Bræstrup and Fuglsang 2021; Bræstrup, Fuglsang, and Kristensen
2022; Kristensen, Bræstrup, and Fuglsang 2023). The municipal monitoring program, along with
the national monitoring program, monitor and present air quality levels at low time resolution, e.g.,
hourly values, diurnal averages and annual averages. Considering the case of Copenhagen and
the lead in reducing the air pollution level managed by the municipality, it is substantial to be able
to analyze and quantify the contribution and influence from street­level emissions (SLEs). Con­
tribution from these sources are directly affecting the local air quality, and the municipality is the
responsible authority for implementing actions to reduce the air pollution level. The contribution
from local SLEs comprise a part of the average values along with the contributions from urban
background and long­range transport, but the low time resolution of data might be related to limi­
tations in the ability to quantify the contribution from local SLEs.
Several studies have showed the ability to apply spectral analysis to air quality data to outline the

the contribution from local SLEs will be quantified using the two methods along with analyzing the
importance of the time resolution of data. The importance of the time resolution of data will be
analyzed as a case study due to the limited availability of data in high time resolution.

Theory
2.1
Atmospheric Physics
Composition of the atmosphere and conditions within the atmosphere affecting stability, movement
of air as well as weather systems affect how air pollution disperse, deposit, and otherwise behave.
Therefore, this section aims to outline these principles to understand what controls the behavior of
air pollution within the atmosphere.
The general atmospheric circulation is driven by the difference in the distribution of energy received
by the Earth, as the motions within the atmosphere partly compensate the unevenly distribution
of energy (Goose 2015). The atmospheric circulation systems cause the two hemispheres to
be separated and related to limited exchange, causing the air pollution to largely stay within the
hemisphere in which it is emitted.
Processes and motions occurring within the atmosphere cause neither the atmosphere, tempera­
ture nor the pollution to be stagnant. The processes occurring within the lower part of the tropo­
sphere, the lowest 20 km of the atmosphere, is dominating when considering the movement and
dispersion of air pollution (European Environment Agency 2008; Andersen et al. 2009).
2.1.1
Atmospheric Stability
The atmospheric stability describes the conditions determining dispersion and the vertical move­
ment of air within the atmosphere, and considers how an air parcel behave in relation to the
surrounding air. The principle of atmospheric stability is based on the relation between the en­
vironmental lapse rate and the dry adiabatic lapse rate. The dry adiabatic lapse rate is the rate
describing the temperature decrease of an air parcel, as it is lifted in the atmosphere. The dry
adiabatic lapse rate is 9.8°C per km in an unsaturated atmosphere. The environmental lapse rate
describes the actual temperature profile of an air parcel moving in the air. The three terms de­
scribing atmospheric stability is briefly described in the following section, and the principles are
displayed in Figure 2.1.
Neutral conditions are present when the environmental lapse rate follows the dry adiabatic lapse
rate, meaning that dispersion is unaffected (Figure 2.1a).
Unstable conditions occur when the temperature decrease of environmental lapse rate is greater
than the dry adiabatic lapse rate, causing dispersion to be enhanced (Figure 2.1b). The unstable
conditions promote a fast vertical mixing of pollutants. The unstable atmospheric conditions are
common during the summer at daytime, as a result of the heating of the surface and air masses
just above. The unstable atmospheric conditions are created as the heated air is lifted and causes
convective turbulence. Furthermore, the buoyant lifting, lifting of an air mass caused by the tem­
perature difference, cause the mixing height to increase.
Stable conditions occur when the temperature decrease of the environmental lapse rate is less
than the dry adiabatic lapse rate, causing dispersion to be suppressed (Figure 2.1c). Stable at­
mospheric conditions appear in cases of ground­based inversion, where surface temperature de­
creases and causes the air above the surface to increase with height. The ground­based inversion
suppresses, and potentially prevents, vertical mixing of air and reduce dispersion of air pollution.
Stable atmospheric conditions constitute one of the main causes of air pollution episodes in cities,
as cases of surface cooling encourage domestic heating and usage of wood stoves which poten­
tially exacerbate the situation (Andersen et al. 2009).

Dispersion and Transport of Air Pollution
Despite the impact of the atmospheric stability, the air pollution can be part of physical and chemical
transformations taking place in the atmosphere. Gaseous pollutants undergo chemical transforma­
tion depending on the availability of reactants, and particles can be transported by the air flow and
disperse or deposit depending on the conditions within the atmosphere (European Environment
Agency 2008; Andersen et al. 2009). Small scale eddies, termed turbulence, in the mixing layer
drives the dispersion of air pollutants in the mixing layer of the atmosphere. The mixing layer de­
scribes the lowest 1 to 1.5 km of the atmosphere, where the wind is affected by the friction against
the surface. The mixing layer reaches its maximum during a summer day, and the turbulence
within the mixing layer forms due to wind breaking against obstacles and surfaces. Furthermore
traffic cause formation of additional turbulence (Andersen et al. 2009).
Additionally, air pollution can be removed from the atmosphere by deposition, either dry or wet
depending on the conditions at the time of emission. The presence of water within the atmosphere
enhances the formation of clouds and fog along with wet deposition. Particles serve as cloud
condensation nuclei and depending on the relative humidity cause water vapor to condensate on
the surface. Wet deposition of particles occurs due to presence of rain and precipitation, as air
pollutants is “washed out” of the atmosphere. Dry deposition describes the processes responsible
for removal of air pollutants not being affected by the presence of water vapor. Dry deposition
comprises among others the processes of sedimentation, interception and settling (Petroff et al.
2008; Andersen et al. 2009; Fowler et al. 2009; Hertel and Goodsite 2009; Burkhardt and Grantz
2017).
Street Canyons
Street canyon as a term refers to a relatively narrow street with buildings on both sides constructing
a canyon. Street canyons are characterized as hot spots for air pollution within urban areas, as the
air pollution are not fully diluted within street canyons due to the geometry (Lazić et al. 2016). The

concentration of air pollution within a street canyon consists of the background concentration along
with the concentration from the local traffic (Andersen et al. 2009). The wind plays an important role
in the air pollution level, as wind vortices forms within the street canyon under certain conditions
and contribute by adding air pollution from the urban background to the street canyon. The flow
inside the street canyon is controlled by the traffic, and differences in concentration of air pollution
between leeward and windward section of the street can develop due to the formation of wind
vortices (Berkowicz et al. 1997). Street canyons are often related to high levels of air pollution due
to the increased level of traffic along with the reduced natural ventilation within the street (Lazić
et al. 2016).
2.2
Air Quality Parameters
Air pollution is comprised by several various components with specific sources, behavior, and
impact. The air pollution parameters (APPs) in focus within this project are outlined and described
in the following section.
2.2.1
Particulate Matter
Particulate matter (PM) denotes airborne particles originating from various sources and of various
composition, including ammonia, black carbon, mineral dust, nitrates, sodium chloride, sulphate
or water (Andersen et al. 2009; Health and Energy Platform of Action 2021). PM relates to a
high level of chemical and physical complexity, and therefore, particles are classified according to
the aerodynamic diameter (Andersen et al. 2009). PM2.5 constitute the mass of particles with an
aerodynamic diameter less than 2.5 µm. Additionally, PM10 constitute the mass of particles with an
aerodynamic diameter less than 10 µm, including PM2.5. The aerodynamic diameter is defined as
the diameter of a spherical particle with a density of 1 g/cm3, which settles at the same velocity as
the particle studied (Blomqvist and Simonson­McNamee 2010). To distinguish between PM10 and
PM2.5 as sources and behavior of the pollutant differ, the difference between the two parameters
is denoted PMCoarse. PMCoarse will be considered according to sources and impacts.
PM is emitted both as primary pollutants and formed secondarily during chemical reactions within
the atmosphere, resulting in the chemical and physical complexity of the particles (Andersen et
al. 2009; Health and Energy Platform of Action 2021). Sources of PM are related to the aerody­
namic diameter, as PMCoarse mainly originates from mechanical and industrial processes, while
PM2.5 mainly originates from combustion processes, including non­industrial combustion and road
transport (Ellermann et al. 2022b; Grigoratos et al. 2015).
Health effects of PM are highly related to the chemical properties of the particle along with its
aerodynamic size, as the aerodynamic size determines the particle’s ability to enter pulmonary
vascular space. Both PMCoarse and PM2.5 can enter the upper airways. While PMCoarse mainly will
deposit in the upper airways, PM2.5 can reach the pulmonary space. Additionally, the particles carry
chemical substances, which can react with molecules and be taken up by the blood (Andersen et
al. 2009). PM is further affecting the climate as cloud condensation nuclei and climate­warming
agents (IPCC 2021), which is elaborated in Section 2.2.4.
The European Union has set limiting values for both PM10 and PM2.5, which are regulated by the EU
Directive (2008) (Table 2.1) and in September 2021, WHO presented updated air quality guidelines
(AQGs) with lower limiting values for both PM10 and PM2.5 (Table 2.2) based on the principle of
NOAEL (No Observed Adverse Effect Level) (World Health Organization 2021).
2.2.2
Particle Number
Particle number (PN) is often measured along with particulate matter, and PN is used as an indi­
cator for ultrafine particles (UFPs). UFPs constitute particles with an aerodynamic diameter less
than 0.1 µm and are therefore included when monitoring PM2.5, but considering the mass, UFPs

compose an often negligible part of PM2.5, while they compose a significant part when considering
the number concentration (particles/cm3) (Andersen et al. 2009). Within the urban environment,
UFPs are mainly emitted from different combustion processes including road transport, shipping
and residential heating (e.g., wood stoves) when combusting both fossil and bio based fuel (IARC
2015). The concentration of UFPs is high close to the source and decreases as distance to the
source increases due to formation of bigger particles and coagulation (Møller 2019).
UFPs are expected to potentially be related to great health effects and issues, due to both size and
chemical composition. The small size of UFPs enable them to enter pulmonary space (Møller 2019)
and potentially be transferred to the blood along with the ability to be taken up by the cells. The
chemical composition of the particles and the high ratio between surface area and volume cause
the particles to be highly reactive and react with molecules outside and inside cells (Andersen et al.
2009).
PN or UFPs are neither included in the legally binding limit values resolved within EU nor the
AQGs presented by WHO. The lack of PN in the AQGs is caused by currently available insufficient
data on health risks caused by PN, but WHO has developed some guidelines for good practice to
ensure systematic measurements along with actions to reduce emissions and exposure. WHO has
set estimates for high levels in urban areas at 20,000 particles/cm3 for hourly mean and 10,000
particles/cm3 for diurnal mean (World Health Organization 2021).
2.2.3
Nitrogen Oxides
NOx, nitrogen oxides, describes the sum of the gasses, nitrogen mono oxide (NO) and nitrogen
dioxide (NO2), which both are emitted from combustion processes. Combustion processes primar­
ily emit NO and to a lesser extent NO2, but emitted NO will undergo oxidation in the atmosphere to
NO2. The oxidation of NO to NO2 depends on the presence of ozone (O3) within the atmosphere
and the chemical transformation takes place over the time span of a couple of hours (Andersen
et al. 2009). Combustion processes occurring at high temperatures, such as power generation,
heating, road transport, and industry (Health and Energy Platform of Action 2021) are dominating
sources for NOx emissions.
NO is not causing impacts on health in relation to NO2, and therefore, only NO2 is regulated. Gen­
erally, the regulation of NOx in outdoor air is occurring in relation to the limit values determined for
NO2. Exposure to NO2 can cause and exacerbate respiratory diseases (Health and Energy Plat­
form of Action 2021). Presence of NO2 in the atmosphere cause a range of secondary pollutants
to form, pollutants which are measured as PM2.5 and PM10 and therefore contributes to further
impacts on human health. Additionally, NO2 absorbs visible radiation and potentially cause direct
impact on global climate (IPCC 2021).
The limiting values regarding exposure to NO2 are presented in Table 2.1 and Table 2.2. It should
be noted that EU limit values present an hourly mean and annual mean, while the AQGs by WHO
present a diurnal mean and annual mean.
2.2.4
Black Carbon
Black carbon (BC) is soot particles that originates from incomplete combustion of biomass, fossil
fuels and biofuels. BC can be emitted from both natural and anthropogenic sources (Helin et
al. 2018), and BC comprises a part of PM2.5. BC consists of particles with a core of elemental
carbon (EC) with a covering layer consisting of unburnt, condensed organic compounds e.g., tar
(IPCC 2021). Along with PN, BC can be used to indicate the emission of particles from road
transport, industry, and residential heating (e.g., wood stoves) (Rådet for Grøn Omstilling 2018),
and in the case of Copenhagen, the contribution to BC is expected to originate from road traffic,
wood stoves and potentially other combustion processes (Kristensen, Bræstrup, and Fuglsang
2023). To differentiate between BC emitted from biomass and wood burning (BCwb) and BC emitted

from fossil fuel (BCff), the optical differences are studied, as BCff appear optical black, while
BCwb appear more brownish. Part of BC originating from BCff and BCwb is based on the optical
differences, and the empirically determined parameters from literature (J. Sandradewi et al. 2008a;

Health effects related to emissions of BC can be compared to health effects related to PM2.5,

reducing the surface­albedo when deposited on snow and ice, thereby intensifying the warming of
the atmosphere by absorbing light (IPCC 2021).
BC is not included in the AQGs presented in 2021 by WHO, as the current available information

exposure levels in outdoor air are lacking for diesel particles including EC and BC, as well as legally
binding limit values regulated by EU are lacking.

3.2.1
PM2.5 & PM10
PM2.5 and PM10 describe the mass of collected particles in the size ranges outlined in Section 2.2.1.
The key principle of monitoring PM2.5 and PM10 is light scattering.
Air masses enter the measuring gauge and chamber, where particles are exposed to laser light,
making optical measurement possible. Light scattering occurs when particles are hit by the light,
and the intensity of the light scattering is measured by a set of detectors within the chamber. The
size of the particles is proportional to the intensity of the light reflected, and the number of light
pulses are a function of the flow of gas through the chamber and the concentration of particles.
This principle implies that the scattering of light can be converted to the concentration of parti­
cles as a function of both size and number. This method and calculation encompass assumptions
regarding density and optical properties of the particles measured. The assumptions represent av­
erage properties of particles, which can be expected to differ between different environments where
different types of particles dominate (Andersen et al. 2009; Bræstrup, Fuglsang, and Kristensen
2022). Therefore, it is standard procedure to compare optical monitoring with a reference method.
The optical gauge used by FORCE Technology has been compared to gravimetric measurements
following EN12341 at separate occasions. Comparison with the reference method have resulted
in correction factors, which are applied to data (Bræstrup, Fuglsang, and Kristensen 2022).
When considering measurements of PM10 the monitoring method has been related to continu­
ous challenges, when comparing measurements with the reference method. This has resulted
in updated correction factors, and missing measurements of PM10 in Bræstrup, Fuglsang, and
Kristensen (2022), due to the need of a more comprehensive comparison between the optical
measurements and the reference method.

3.2.2
Particle Number
The total number of particles in a sampled air mass is determined by using a Condensation Particle
Counter (CPC). The principle of the measurement is based on condensation of liquids onto the
particles to increase the size of the particles and enable them to be detected optically (Andersen
et al. 2009).
In the current measurements, sampled air masses enter a chamber containing saturated vapor of
butanol. Afterwards, the air masses enter a condenser that cools the air, causing the air containing
particles to become oversaturated. Oversaturation causes the butanol to condensate on the par­
ticles, which grow and enable them to be determined optically. A CPC has a cut­off size defining
the lower level of particle sizes, which can be detected. The cut­off size is a function based on the
oversaturation taking place in the condenser, and is related to the given CPC (Bræstrup, Fuglsang,
and Kristensen 2022).

3.2.3
NOx
The method used for the monitoring of NOx and NO, along with determination of NO2, is the refer­

and Kristensen 2022). The concentration of NO2 is determined by chemiluminescence with an in­
tegrated converter.
Air masses enter the chemiluminescence analyzer at a constant flow rate and sample through
a particle filter before entering the reaction chamber. Inside the reaction chamber, the air mass
is mixed with an excess of O3 to determine the concentration of NO. During the reaction with
O3, radiation is emitted, which is proportional to the number of NO molecules, and therefore, the
concentration of NO within the detection volume. The emitted radiation is converted to an electric
signal by a photodiode or photomultiplier tube after being filtered by a selective optical filter.
When considering the determination of NO2, the air masses enter a converter that reduces NO2 to
NO before entering the reaction chamber to undergo the process described above. The electrical
signal obtained is proportional to the concentration of nitrogen oxides, NOx, which is the sum of NO
and NO2. Therefore, concentration of NO2 is determined as the difference between NOx and NO,
when the sampled air was not passed through the converter. The method of chemiluminescence
is based on the following reaction:

4.4
Moving Median
The application of the moving median method on air quality data is a result of the process to
understand the importance of the time resolution of data.
The local SLEs will, depending on the time resolution of data monitoring, be present as separate
peaks in the data representing the specific vehicles emitting the specific APP in focus, or be repre­
sented in the hourly means, and it is interesting to test if the contribution from local SLEs is erased
from data in low time resolution. To determine the contribution from local SLEs, the SLEs must
be separated from the monitored level of APPs comprising contribution from local SLEs, urban
background and long­range transport, which can be completed using the moving median. The
method of moving median filtering is widely used to pre­process data prior to further processing,
and to improve results in solution processes and preservation of images (Richir and Dzbikowicz
2012; Lin 2007; T. Sun, Gabbouj, and Neuvo 1994). The method is primarily used for eliminating
outliers in data, spikes and noise while keeping the characteristics of the analytical signal (Wen,
Fan, and Z.­G. Chen 2007; Lago, Juliano, and Kascheres 1995).
The moving median filtering method is applied to air quality data at high time resolution to obtain
the most information about local SLEs as possible. In the case of air quality data, the peaks are the
result of the direct contribution from the local SLEs, and the peaks are mainly present in data series
at high time resolution, while the contribution from urban background and long­range transport will
be outlined in the underlying analytical signal. This project studies data series in a time resolution
of 1 second for selected APPs at selected AQMSs. By applying the method and a smoothing
interval, at which the underlying analytical signal is obtained, the influence and contribution from
local SLEs can be quantified by subtracting the moving median from the raw signal.
The smoothing interval used within the current analysis is the same between the different AQMSs,
but in other cases the smoothing interval might differ, because of the varying conditions for local
sources. The data processing and use of the moving median method have been conducted using
the statistical open­source program R. The moving median of odd data spans has been computed
using the following function (R Core Team 2023):
stats::runmed()
Computing the moving median results in a data set of same length as the input, and the output of
the moving median is compared to the raw data to quantify the contribution from local SLEs. The
quantified contribution from local SLEs is determined on hourly basis, comparing the hourly mean
of raw data with hourly mean of the moving median. The results obtained using on the moving
median will be compared to the results obtained using the spectral analysis to test if the results are
within the same order.

PM2.5 originates from non­industrial combustion and road transport, causing resuspension of al­
ready emitted particles, and their size enable them to be present in the atmosphere for a longer
period of time, and therefore also enable them to be transported over longer distances (Stohl et al.
2002). As BC comprises a part of PM2.5, the ranges used to define the contribution from the differ­
ent sources for the two APPs are identical for all ranges except the lower limit for contribution from
SLEs, as it is set to 20 seconds (0.05 s−1) for BC, as data in high time resolution at Folehaven has
shown peaks in concentration to last for 20 seconds.
The behavior of PN within the atmosphere is greatly related to their small size, which cause the
concentration to be high close to the source. However, due to coagulation and formation of bigger
particles, the concentration of PN decreases with increased distance to the source. Therefore,
sources of monitored PN are only considered to be SLEs and urban background for this case. The
lower limit for contribution from SLEs to the measured concentration of PN are set to 20 seconds
(0.05 s−1), as data in high time resolution has shown peaks in concentration to last for 20 seconds.
NO and NO2 are both reactive oxides of nitrogen, which undergo chemical transformation, causing
their presence in the atmosphere to be determined by the chemical reaction occurring. Conversion
of NO to NO2 is most commonly a result of oxidation by O3, a reaction which is assumed to be
instantaneous as it occurs relatively quickly. The exact time for the reaction to occur depends
on the availability of available reactants, including solar energy and O3. Kimbrough et al. (2017)
suggest that under high O3 conditions (>30 ppb) the conversion occurs at a fairly constant rate,
while under low O3 conditions (<30 ppb), the conversion rate will be limited by the availability
of O3. DCE reports the annual average concentration of O3 for two AQMS within Copenhagen,
which are part of the national monitoring program. The annual average concentrations for 2022
are reported to be 26 and 31.5 ppb (Ellermann et al. 2024), indicating that the conversion of NO

Kimbrough et al. (2017) further showed presence of a diurnal cycle in O3 measurements, which
is used as the basis for setting the upper limit of contribution from local SLEs to the monitored
NO2 concentration. As the concentration of NO and NO2 follow one another through the chemical

minutes before concentration drops again as a result of a polluting vehicle passing by the AQMS.
On the other hand, considering the contribution to NO2 concentration, the choice of the lower limit

Results & Discussion
The data analysis started out with a broad perspective including all the monitored APPs (excluding
PM10) and during the process several choices have been made to obtain the results, which will
be presented and discussed in the following section. The process with a broad perspective aimed
to obtain knowledge regarding the monitored air quality levels throughout the monitoring network
and how local conditions impact these concentrations. On behalf of the general knowledge, the
scope of the project has been narrowed down to focus on the contribution from local SLEs and
understanding how these are represented in data, and how the time resolution of data impact
the representation of these sources. In the following section, the output of data processing and
analysis is presented and discussed.
5.1
Delimitation of Data
Besides the delimitation according the choice of parameters, the results presented in the following
section only concern air quality data monitored in 2022, while data monitored in 2020 and 2021
have been a part of the preceding process. Data from 2020 and 2021 have been omitted in the
further analysis of data due to the great impact of the Covid­19 pandemic during these years, as
the years are greatly affected by lockdowns of society and activities which impact the air quality.
Restrictions and lockdowns are still a part of the society during the first two months of 2022, but
the activity in the society becomes as close to normal as possible for the monitoring period and air
quality data available (Statens Serum Institut 2022). The potential impact of the Covid­19 pandemic
on the monitored air quality will be outlined and discussed in Section 5.6. Results on data from
2021 concerning bivariate polar plots, time variation, moving median and spectral analysis will be
available in Appendices D to G. The results are presented and organized according to the data
processing and analysis conducted.

5.3
Moving Median
The use of the moving median as a method is a result of the process of understanding the impor­
tance of the time resolution of data. The method is currently used to remove spikes, outliers, and
noise from data prior to further data processing. When considering air pollution data, the method
can be used to determine the contribution from local SLEs, as they are present in data as peaks.
The purpose of applying the method to the current data is to test an alternative approach to air
quality data, and to use the approach to estimate the contribution from local SLEs for specific
cases and compare the results with the findings obtained by the spectral analysis.
The moving median method is applied to data in high time resolution, meaning data measured
every second, and data in high time resolution is only available for data concerning PN and BC for
selected AQMSs. NO data at a time resolution of 1 minute is available for the AQMS at Backersvej,
but will not be considered in the analysis. Previous analyses have shown that both local SLEs and
non­local sources contributes to the monitored concentrations at the AQMSs within Copenhagen.
This analysis aims to estimate the contribution from local SLEs for the selected cases.

5.4
Spectral Analysis
The method of spectral analysis is applied to air pollution data, as previous studies have shown
the ability to investigate the hidden periodicities of data (Lazić et al. 2016), along with the ability
to quantify the contribution from sources (Frederickson et al. 2022). Previous usage of spectral
analysis for source apportionment has based the analysis on data in low time resolution, either 1
hour or 30 minutes. As the aim of this project is to quantify the contribution from local SLEs to the
monitored air quality, data in a higher time resolution, 5 minutes and 1 second, has been used for
the spectral analysis and the following source apportionment.

5.6
Limitations
This section aims to describe and discuss a number of limitations related to the work presented
above.
5.6.1
Locations of Air Quality Monitoring Stations
The choice of locations and placement of the AQMSs are not considered in relation to any standard
procedure according to distance to nearest source, but is based on the requirements within the
tender documents and what is possible at the chosen locations. The five AQMSs are therefore
not equally and optimally placed in relation to local sources, as the distance to the nearest road
lane differs as well as other factors affecting dispersion and behavior of pollution differs, as further
described in Appendix A. Height of inlet is measured according to the ground level, and are not
necessarily considered according to the level of the road lane. The focus on the contribution
from local SLEs are not necessarily optimal with the current monitoring program and placement of
AQMSs, as the placement comprise an additional factor differing between the AQMSs besides the
local environment and sources in the urban background, such as potential street canyons.

5.6.2
Factors Affecting Data Representation
The period for data collection spans from the start of October 2020 until the end of December 2022.
Within this period, several events that potentially could affect the measured concentrations of APPs
occur. The impact of these events will not be estimated, as a baseline of measured concentration
of APPs prior to 2020 is lacking, but they have been taken into account and caused the delimitation
of data presented earlier. Factors affecting data representation are:
­ Covid­19 virus
­ Russia invasion of Ukraine resulting in energy scarcity
­ Fire in apartment building at Godthåbsvej
The events, considerations and potentially impacts on data representation is outlined in the follow­
ing.
Covid­19 virus
The outbreak of the Covid­19 virus in Denmark occurred before the starting point of data collection
(March 2020), and the following years were characterized by different levels of lockdowns. A
timeline describing restrictions and lockdowns of the society within the data collection period is
available in Appendix H. Generally, the period is characterized by lower load of traffic caused by
the increased share of people working from home.
The development in traffic load, when considering passenger cars, decreased to nearly 50% of the
2019 level during the first lockdown in March 2020. The continuous lockdowns and reopenings of
the society during 2020, 2021 and start of 2022 was visualized in the traffic load, which decreased
as a result of lockdowns and by 2022 reached the level of 2019 (Vejdirektoratet 2023a). The total
monthly traffic index in Denmark is presented in Figure H.1 in Appendix H.1.
The lockdowns of society caused an increase in teleworking, which was further expected to in­
crease the use of residential heating, e.g., wood stoves, as people are spending more time at
home, along with the lockdowns and request to work from home being more pronounced during
the winter months.
The reductions in traffic load which occurred, does not result in one­to­one reductions in emissions,
as different type of vehicles are related to different emission profiles (Brandt et al. 2020; Ellermann
and Hertel 2020). The vehicle type responsible for the greatest reduction is passenger vehicles
(Vejdirektoratet 2023a), and passenger cars are related to the lowest levels of emissions given per
km. Besides this, it is expected that the reduction in traffic load will be related to a reduction in
emission of NO2 and PM2.5. Brandt et al. (2020) and Ellermann and Hertel (2020) estimate that a
reduction in total traffic load of 40% will cause a reduction in emissions of 35%.
The outbreak of Covid­19 further affected the traffic and number of passengers travelling through
Copenhagen Airport. The traffic intensity at the airport can be of interest, as the AQMS at Back­
ersvej is within the area being greatly affected by emissions originating from the airport, when the
wind direction is from south­east or south­southeast. The traffic intensity and number of passen­
gers decreases as a result of the lockdown and by October 2020 the number of passengers is
at 9% of the level in 2019. This increases during the data collection period and reaches 83% in
December 2022 compared to 2019 level. It is assumed that the lower level of activity at the airport
affects the measured concentration of APPs, especially PN, at Backersvej, but these impacts will
not be estimated (CPH 2023).
Energy scarcity
Russia’s invasion of Ukraine showed several impacts on the global society such as energy scarcity.
The scarcity of energy was expected to potentially impact the measured concentrations of APPs,
as an increase in use of lower quality of fuel was expected, along with an increase in use of wood
stoves for residential heating. The presentation of data provided by FORCE Technology showed

5.6.3
Meteorological Data
The assumption that one data set assembled from four DMI MSs is valid for conditions at the five
AQMSs located across Copenhagen is related to some limitations. Ideally, meteorological data
should be measured at each AQMSs to obtain the best possible information about local conditions
affecting dispersion and behavior of the APPs. When ideal conditions cannot be met within this
project, the impact of the current method and data source can be discussed.
To consider the representativeness of the meteorological data, measurements from the four dif­
ferent MSs have been compared at randomly picked dates and time to consider the differences
between the MSs.
The aim of the comparison of wind data is to consider if the compiled wind data set can be used to
describe the general conditions regarding wind within Copenhagen. When considering wind data
and wind direction, the measurements conducted at the four DMI MSs are compared to determine
whether the measurements are within the same range or if they are biased. Wind direction is
rounded to nearest 10◦when used as input to the plots presented (bivariate polar plots), therefore,
it is the rounded numbers of the wind direction that are being compared.
The selected data reference points show an acceptable agreement between the MSs at CPH Air­
port and Jægersborg, while the MS at Landbohøjskolen differs according to wind direction. Mea­
surements from DMI is not obtained for the full period, but for the obtained data points, the reference
points show good agreement with the data obtained at CPH Airport and Jægersborg. The differ­
ence in local conditions at the four MSs can describe the disagreement between measurements of
both wind direction and speed. Landbohøjskolen are situated in between high­rise buildings which
impacts the exchange of air and at low wind speeds the measurement of wind direction can be sen­
sitive. When considering wind speed, the MS at CPH Airport generally measures higher values of
wind speed than Jægersborg, which can be caused by the differences according to location, such
as distance to the sea and surrounding environment, as the airport is placed within a more open
environment. Comparison of the wind speed measured at Landbohøjskolen with Jægersborg and
CPH Airport shows that measurements from Landbohøjskolen to a greater extent correlate with
measurements from CPH Airport, than it does with Jægersborg.
The analyses conducted within this project is not highly sensitive to wind direction, and therefore
does not require highly accurate measurements of wind direction and speed at locations of the
AQMSs. The data obtained from the four MSs can be considered as representative of the overall
conditions within Copenhagen, when taking local factors affecting wind conditions at the AQMSs
into account.

5.7
Future Studies
Air quality is currently monitored at low time resolutions and presented as daily or annual aver­
ages according to the legal requirements (EU), and the monitoring programs are generally based
on few sparsely located AQMSs. Even though, the municipal monitoring program consists of five
AQMSs, the AQMSs are still sparsely located within the municipality of Copenhagen, especially
when considering the hyper­local variation of APPs, particularly close to emission sources. Fred­
erickson et al. 2022 outlined how a monitoring network consisting of higher density of monitoring
nodes can provide useful information on local variations in air quality, along with the ability obtain
source apportionment by the use of spectral analysis.
This project seeks to provide information on local variations, the local SLEs, in air quality by con­
sidering the time resolution of data instead of the spatial distribution of data. The ideal conditions
to obtain a more thorough understanding of both the spatial and temporal variations in air qual­
ity could be obtained by the combination of more densely packed monitoring networks along with
data in high time resolution. Such a study is cost­intensive and might not be realistic. But further
analyses of longer time spans of data in high time resolution, simultaneously at several AQMSs,
could provide additional information on local variations in air quality caused by the site­specific
local SLEs. An improvement of the current project could be an optimization of the ranges used for
integrating the periodograms by, e.g., experimental determination of more accurate ranges.
Future studies could further investigate the impact of the expansion of the low­emission zone in
Copenhagen, now concerning passenger cars as well, and quantify whether the contribution from
local SLEs of PN decreases.

6
Conclusion
In this thesis, the contribution and influence from local street­level emissions to the monitored air
quality level in Copenhagen was quantified, and the potential impact of the time resolution of air
quality data tested.
To quantify the contribution from local street­level emissions, the method of spectral analysis has
been applied to the air quality data from Copenhagen available in different time resolutions. The
use of spectral analysis enabled the pollution signal to be apportioned to the defined pollution
sources, being local street­level emissions, urban background, or long­range transport. By in­
tegrating the periodograms obtained by the spectral analysis, the contribution from the defined
pollution sources could be quantified. The results outlined limited variation between the five air
quality monitoring stations for PM2.5 and PN, while NO2, NO and BC (including BCff and BCwb)
showed slightly greater variation in monitored concentrations. The quantified contributions from
the defined pollution sources also showed limited variation between the air quality monitoring sta­
tions, even for the air pollution parameters strongly linked to the local traffic. From the analyses
conducted, it can be concluded that contribution from local street­level emissions comprise the
smallest fraction of the monitored air quality in Copenhagen, with either the urban background or
long­range transport being the dominant source depending on the air pollution parameter. The
moving median was applied as an analytical method to air quality data in high time resolution (1
second) to quantify the contribution from local street­level emissions, and the method obtained
results within the same order, as obtained using spectral analysis.
To test the potential impact of the time resolution of air quality data, the method of spectral analysis
was applied to several data sets in different time resolutions from the same air quality monitoring
station. These results show that the time resolution of air quality data plays a significant role, espe­
cially when considering the contribution from local sources and local street­level emissions. The
results show, that lowering the time resolution of data results in contributions from local street­
level emissions to decrease, while long­range transport and urban background increases. This
shift, causing the pollution signal to move towards the low­frequency region, might result in emis­
sions from local street­level emissions to be underestimated, and causing urban background and
long­range transport to be overestimated.
The findings obtained within this thesis indicate that if the municipality of Copenhagen aims to follow
the air quality guidelines proposed by WHO, fundamental reductions is required, and contributions
from other than local street­level emissions should be considered. To gain more knowledge and
certain estimates of the contribution from local street­level emissions, a more standardized set­up
of air quality monitoring stations is proposed along with additional data in high time resolution, as
it can be used to study the local sources of emissions more thoroughly.

C.1
Monitoring Methods
Monitoring conducted by DMI adhere to the recommendations outlined by World Meteorologi­
cal Organization (WMO) and Intergovernmental Oceanographic Commission – UNESCO (IOC­
UNESCO). Temperature is monitored using an electrical thermometer, and temperature is moni­
tored within 1.25­2.0 meters height. Construction of monitoring setup is standardized. The tem­
perature is presented in tenth degree Celsius, °C.
Wind direction and wind speed are monitored using an automatic wind sensor. Mean wind speed
describes the speed of the wind averaged over the past hour. Mean wind direction describes the
average direction from which the wind originates from, averaged over the past hour. Wind speed
and wind direction are monitored at 10 meters height. Wind direction is presented in whole degrees
(1°­360°). Wind speed is presented in m/s (DMI 2023).

H
Corona Timeline
The following timeline describes some key points in the development of the Covid­19 pandemic
in Denmark and associated restrictions on society for the period of data collection of this thesis.
Information within the timeline is fully based on Statens Serum Institut (2022).
2020
October: Limitations on activities within the society. Expected increase in teleworking compared
to earlier.
November: Outbreak of new variant of Covid­19.
December: Increased lockdown. Teleworking is expected for all who has the ability. Lockdown of
liberal professions. Schools are being closed and online­teaching is established.
2021
January: Continuous lockdown. Advised against travelling abroad. Request to continuous tele­
working in professions where it is possible.
February: Lockdown of society is extended to the end of the month.
March: Partial reopening of the society.
April: Additional reopening of the society.
May: More or less complete reopening of the society at the end of the month. Nightclubs are the
only section being affected by limited activity.
June, July, August: Normal level of activity within the society.
September: Covid­19 is no longer considered a disease that is a critical threat to society.
October: Normal level of activity within the society.
November: Covid­19 is again considered a disease that is a critical threat to society.
December: Restrictions on activities are implemented.
2022
Jan: Continuous restrictions and lockdown of society.
February: Restrictions are removed. Covid­19 is no longer considered a disease that is a critical
threat to society.
March: Restrictions on travels abroad are removed.
