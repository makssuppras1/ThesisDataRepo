Abstract
The market of Internet of Things devices is booming. Many devices are designed
to only measure data. To utilise these devices fully the data that is measure needs
to be understood. This report revolves around a insole designed to measure plantar
pressure. It is part of a network consisting of the insole, a smartphone, database and
data visualisation platform. Initially it was intended for Charcot patients. Where if
pressure exceeded safe levels the device would warn the user. By analysing plantar
pressure a correlation was made between the force applied during heel rise stage in the
gait cycle and the total mass of the user. This enabled the device to be used to track
the load carried by workers. Allowing a detailed analysis of the work environment to
help both employer and employee. Further investigation was made to determine the
position of the load carried. This did not result in any substantial ﬁndings. Based
on the analysis design improvement were suggested to improve the functionality in
either of the two use cases.

Introduction
With the spread of wearable technology in the healthcare sector, more and more
devices are made to detect and prevent a wide variety of ailments. Some give access
to entirely new data sets that help medical professionals in diagnosing issues. Others
help people by making their daily lives easier. Behind many of these devices there is
a back-end that manages and analyses the data collected by the device.
A well designed analysis can give a warning of dangerous activity earlier leading to
quicker treatment and fewer complications. The beneﬁts of this technology could be
staggering saving doctors up to 15 hours a week by patients wearing wearables[26].
1.1
Previous work
This project will be based on a previously developed smart insole and its iteration.
This insole include a range of force sensors strategically placed to get readings from
the areas of the foot where pressure is applied. Temperature sensors are also present
to identify temperature changes that can be symptoms of ulcers and Charcot foot.
The design was made to be as unobtrusive as possible with criteria of being ﬂexible
and comfortable as well as having low power consumption.
1.2
Use Case 1: Diabetic Neuropathy
The design parameters stated above were dictated by the original problem: Moni-
toring diabetic patients to detect early onset of Charcot foot. Diabetics are more
susceptible to Charcot foot and ulcers in general because of diabetic neuropathy.
This condition damages the nervous system and reduces the sensory function with
a tendency to be most severe in the legs and feet. Diabetic neuropathy may aﬀect
as many as 50% of all people with diabetes[23] (Diabetes aﬀecting an estimated 211
million people as of 2014[32]).
The lack of sensory function limits the patients ability to feel if there are any harmful
amount of pressure put on certain points of the foot. The nerve damage is a result
of damage to arteries caused by the high blood sugar levels. The damaged arteries
also limit the healing ability making the problem even more severe. If not dealt with
complications may lead to foot ulcers, Charcot foot, or in worst case amputation.
Currently prevention of ulcers and Charcot requires the patient to check their feet
everyday and sometimes requiring a visit to the hospital. Here doctors and foot ther-
apists analyse the feet condition of the patients as well as the pressure proﬁle they
have underfoot when walking. They use this to customise support-shoes so the pres-
sure can be relieved from a given area enabling healing and preventing development
of ulcers or deformation. A smart insole that can send collected data to a phone or
to a cloud solution could give people a warning when harmful events might occur. It
will ease the burden of people with this chronic condition.

1.3
Use Case 2: Lift load analysis
Every year around 880.000 Danes experience back pain which leads to 84.000 hospital
admissions [28]. This is an issue not only Denmark is facing. It is estimated that
5.6% of the American population suﬀer from back pains[29]. All of this has a big
ﬁnancial impact. In Denmark 1,820,000,000 DKK is spent on treatment every year.
Due to sick days and lack of eﬃciency back injuries costs Danish companies around
4,840,000,000 DKK from lost production every year[28].
Common reasons for back pains are improper lifting or lifting too much. This is why
The National Research Center for Work Environment in Denmark (NFA) wants to
research how to reduce the amount of back injuries in the workplace. Currently they
use a device similar to the smart insole when measuring lifts. The main issue is their
device requires proximity of a researcher throughout the data collection session. They
seek a device that can be sent workplace and be placed in a worker’s shoe without
much interference. This enables them to gather much more data far quicker leading
to a greater understanding of the issues that lead to back pains.
Further development would lead to a commercial product. A personal device that
will detect when harmful lifts are done and a notiﬁcation will be delivered to the
worker or their boss. This will help the workers that do not identify their harmful
behaviour.

2.1
Diabetic Neuropathy
The condition Charcot neuropathic osteoarthropathy also called Charcot foot is a
development of neuropathy.
It is characterised by joint and bone fragmentation
along with other conditions like diabetes results in an acute localised inﬂammatory
condition. The inﬂammatory response can lead to permanent deformation the foot.
The deformation may lead to even further risk of ulceration and amputation. Any
increase in the plantar (underfoot) pressure even if minor can be the trigger of the
inﬂammatory event that leads to acute Charcot[14].
Clinical symptoms of Charcot are spread over its 3 stages according to the Eichenholtz
classiﬁcations[14].
1. Development
• Erythema (rash)
• Foot edema (swelling)
• Elevated temperature
2. Coalescence
• Decreased signs of inﬂammation
3. Consolidation
• Resolution of inﬂammation (healing of tissue)
• Changes in overall foot architecture (deformation as seen in ﬁgure 1)
(a) Normal Foot
(b) Charcot foot

2.3
Gait
In this section theory of gait and foot anatomy will be presented. The purpose of
which is to familiarise the reader with certain terms used in the paper. As well as
the anatomy and biomechanical principles propose solutions have been based upon.
The directions of the foot is split in 3 dimensions (see ﬁgure 3). Vertical which is
perpendicular to the bottom of the foot. Fore-aft that goes from the toe to the heel,
and lateral that is from side to side of the foot. The plane underneath the foot is
called the plantar plane.
Figure 3: The 3 directions of the foot[31].
The gait cycle describes the movement while walking. Its start point is when one
foot has initial contact with the ground and lasts until that same foot hits the ground
again (see ﬁgure 4). The stance phase is the duration the foot are in contact with
the ground (from initial heel contact to toe lift oﬀ).

2.3.1
Forces throughout a cycle
There are many diﬀerent ways of walking and pressure distribution over a gait cycle
is going to be diﬀerent for each. Even if a person walks in the same way there will
be variations between each step. There are however tendencies that describe normal
gait. Normal in this case covers a wide range of ages and body types. So it needs to
be noted that there can be a big diﬀerence in forces, speed etc. dependant on these
factors. These tendencies can however still be used to predict and understand the
ground force that is applied when a person walks.

The ground reaction force vector for a step in normal gait follows ﬁgure 5. It shows

Splitting the force vectors in 3 dimensions (from ﬁgure 3) gives insight to the action
the force is part of. Figure 6 shows how the force components are expected to act
throughout a gait cycle. The vertical direction accelerates the person upwards and
brakes the person coming down something that continuously happens when walking.
It is also what counteracts the gravitational pull of the body weight and if determined
can be used to estimate the load of the user. The complication explained earlier with
determining the weight of a object that is being accelerated is still an issue here.
Fore-aft forces act as propulsion and brakes in the walking direction. The negative
force of this section is braking while the positive pushes the person forward. So if the
goal is to increase energy eﬃciency while walking this should be reduced.
Lastly we touch upon the lateral direction. This value is generally very small and
moves the body slightly towards the opposite side of the leg with ground contact.
This is to ensure balance during gait.
It requires more advanced hardware to measure the 3 dimensional force vector. To
circumvent this issue a weight estimation will be based on a speciﬁc point in the gait
cycle where the lateral and fore-aft forces will be minimal will be pursued.

2.3.2
Center of force
Center of mass (COM) and center of force (COF) are similar with some key diﬀer-
ences. COM is positioned in 3 dimensional space dependent on the weight distribution
of a body. COF is in this case 2 dimensional (fore-aft and lateral) and is determined
by the force plantar distribution. COF is the point in a pressure plane where the
moment is zero. Calculating COF is usually done in one dimension at the time and

The plantar pressure distribution is mainly based on the user’s weight, foot proﬁle
(like ﬂatfoot) and walking style. Comparing a baseline measurement to any given
other measurement will indicate a change in any of these 3 factors. The nature of
the change having diﬀerent key indicators.
Overall increased pressure with the same distribution as baseline indicate increased
weight. This can be external load like clothes or a bag. Or simply that the user
has gained or lost weight. With the external load being added and subtracted very
quickly and weight gain or loss will be more gradual it should be possible to identify
which is which.
A spike in pressure at a certain point indicates change in foot proﬁle. For most people
the actual foot structure will not change and if there is a spike it is likely a foreign
object. For patients with Charcot one of the symptoms is foot deformation and this
type of pressure will indicate that symptom.
Walking style can have a range of diﬀerent aﬀects on the pressure distribution. But
a common change is how aggressively a person walks. Especially the 3 contact areas
(ﬁgure 18) will have an increase in pressure. The two front contact areas and the
space in between is where force is applied to accelerate forward.
The higher the
acceleration the higher the pressure in this area. The heel contact point is where the
braking force is applied which also roughly increases with the walking speed.
Dependent on the use case diﬀerent factors are relevant. A upper limit threshold can
be setup to warn the user if a certain peak pressure level is reached. identifying slight
pressure increases but over a longer period of time or systematic (e.g. to indicating
chaﬁng) are also relevant markers. These are especially relevant when examining
patients with diabetic neuropathy. These identiﬁers warn when an are needs to be
relieved and in turn reduce the risk of ulcers and other complications. The diagnosis
of possible Charcot foot development include a swollen and warm foot. Swelling can
be detected if average pressure increases compared to a baseline or the other foot.
Identifying the diﬀerence between swelling and an increased load, like a lift, might
be a challenge since the pressure proﬁle can be expected to be similar. This might
be solved by sampling over a longer duration (the swelling is likely to persist longer
than the lift). If the goal is to improve or analyse work environment in terms of
load carried. Weight estimation will be more applicable. This should be done over
a workday to discover if a worker is carrying too much and risking injuries in their
back. This enables change in work processes to help the workers prevent injuries and
keep eﬃciency at a peak.

Communication system
With the nature of the solution requiring to transfer information from a device at
the users feet, a communication technology will need to be applied. This section will
describe diﬀerent usable technologies and what their strengths and weaknesses are.
An evaluation on possible good options for diﬀerent use cases will be presented.
3.1
Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE)
Bluetooth Low Energy is a short range but low power consuming communications
technology. BLE is classiﬁed as a Wireless Personal Area Network (WPAN) and is
used in a wide range of wearable applications from diabetes monitoring systems[6] to
smart watches[20]. The original insole design utilised BLE to communicate between
the insole and the paired phone application.
The main design goals of BLE is to provide low power consuming communication
especially for peripheral devices. It is optimised for low cost production and low
complexity. These goals are well aligned with many developer needs and has seen a
rapid adoption rate.
3.1.1
BLE Protocol Stack Overview
The BLE protocol stack has three main parts: application, Controller and Host. Each
of these are comprised of layers that are essential for operation to be successful.
Application
The application is the highest layer and responsible for containing the logic and data
handling of the device that wants to communicate. Here this would be the collection
and parsing of measurement data from the insole.
Host
The host contains the following layers.
• Generic Access Proﬁle (GAP)
• Generic Attribute Proﬁle (GATT)
• Logical Link Control and Adaptation Protocol (L2CAP)
• Attribute Protocol (ATT)
• Security Manager (SM)
Controller
The controller contains the following layers.
• Link layer (LL)

The Physical layer is the part of the device that contains the radio hardware.
BLE operates in the 2.4 GHz band the same as the classic Bluetooth technology,

BLE utilises frequency hopping spread spectrum to hop between data channels. The
next channel that is switched to is determined by a formula with a frequency hop
variable that that is shared when a connection event has occurred. This means that
two connected devices will always changes channels parallel to each other. The pur-
pose is to reduce risk of interference. If there is interference on one channel and data
is lost it can be restored when the channel is swapped. It is especially useful because
the 2.4 GHz band is widely used by technologies with stronger transmitting power.
The modulation used by BLE is Gaussian Frequency Shift Keying (GFSK). A method
is widely used among other low power wireless protocols including classic bluetooth.
For BLE the modulation is ﬁxed at 1 Mbit/s and that is the highest possible through-
put for this protocol.
The Link layer communicates directly with the physical layer and manages how
the device connects to others. When two device establish a BLE connection one will
be the master and one the slave. The master is the one that initiates the connection.
The slave advertises its availability and accepts the connection. One device can have
multiple connections and can be both slave and master just not in the same connec-
tion.
The master and slave connection is asymmetrical in nature. The master will have
higher power consumption and require more computational power, while the slave
will need less. This is ideal for many IoT connections where one of the devices often
have a greater need for reduced power consumption and includes this projects use
cases.
Before a connection is made the two devices will be either an advertiser or scanner.
The advertiser (slave) advertises itself after which awaiting a scanner (master) to
connect to it. The advertiser is usually the sensor device in this case it would be the
insole. The advertising channels are used to broadcast advertising packets. A packet
is sent on one of the channels and after which the advertiser listens for a response for
a ﬁxed duration before switch channels and repeating the process. The 3 messages
on each of the 3 channels are called a advertising event the time between which is

A scanner is the device listening for incoming advertising messages. It listen on each
channel index in sequence for a duration called the scan window. After a ﬁxed time
called the scan interval, the channel index is changed and the process is repeated.
Advertising interval, scan interval and scan window all have a direct correlation to
power consumption of the device since it aﬀects how long the radio is active. It is
however also correlated with the probability of the advertising packet getting received
and a connection being made[24].
When a scanner receives an advertising packet it is ﬁrst chosen whether or not the
connection is wanted. If it is wanted the scanner becomes master and sends a connec-
tion request packet to the slave. If the slave responds a connection between the two
is made. Part of the request packet includes the frequency hop variable mentioned
earlier.
A connection is a series of data transmissions between the two devices each called
a connection event. Three important parameters are set by the master during the
connection.
• Connection interval
• Slave latency
• Connection supervision timeout
Connection interval is the time between two connection events. This controls how
often data is transmitted and by extension how often the radio is activated. This
also means that the longer the connection interval is the less power is consumed.
Slave latency is the number of events that a slave can chose to skip before a discon-
nection might happen. Connection supervision timeout is the maximum time interval
between two data packets before the connection is timed out.
An alternative way of transmitting data is broadcasting. This done without estab-
lishing a connection. A broadcaster sends advertising packets along with data at
regular intervals. This means that no handshake is made with the receiving device.
Lower power consumption is therefore achieved for the transmitter. One downside
here is that the broadcaster have no ability to conﬁrm whether or not the message
has been received.
3.1.3
Host Controller Interface (HCI)
The HCI enables communication between the host and the controller. These are sep-
arated due to the hard real-time constrained of the controller and the host consisting
of more complex layers better suited for more advanced CPUs[24]. As an example
most smartphones have the host run on the main CPU while the controller runs on
a separate chip. It has however become more common to have host, controller and
application run on the same chip. This is especially the case when looking at low
power sensor devices[12].

Host
Logical Link Control and Adaptation Protocol (L2CAP) serves a protocol
multiplexer that encapsulates multiple protocols from the upper layers in the BLE
packet format. If fragmentation is needed L2CAP will split payloads into parseable
packets (max. 27-byte)[12].
The Attribute Protocol (ATT) deﬁnes the communication between server and
client. Server and client are roles controlled by the GATT. The server have data in
the form of attributes. A client can request attributes which triggers the server to
send a packet with the data. The server can also send attributes without a request.
Notiﬁcations are such and do not require handshake from the client when received
while indications do. The server and client role are not connected to the master and
slave roles and one device can be both server and client simultaneous[12].
Generic Attribute Proﬁle (GATT) is layered on top of the ATT and is responsible
for the communication of characteristics between devices. Characteristics builds up
services with characteristics being a fragmentation of data. These are then bundle in
attributes and send to a client[12].
Generic Access Proﬁle (GAP) is the highest layer of the host. It controls the
interaction and roles between devices. Four roles are deﬁned for the underlying layers:
Central, Peripheral, Broadcaster and Observer. Central is designated to establish and
manage multiple connections. Since the BLE protocol is asymmetrical as mentioned
earlier the central often have more computing power and higher power consumption.
The Peripheral is often connected to a central and enables lower power and CPU
requirement. Broadcasters like mentioned earlier transmits advertisement packets
with data without any conﬁrmation if the message is delivered. As an opposite the
observer listens for messages continuously without knowing if a message will ever
come[12].

Evaluation of communication technologies
WiFi and BLE was originally selected as the communication technologies for the
solution. With both technologies integrated in most smartphones that served as an
ideal bridging device. Acting as a BLE receiver and a WiFi transmitter to combat
the range shortcomings of BLE while keeping the low power requirements for the

This depending on the use case could be when a too heavy object is lifted or a foreign
object was detected in the shoe. These are however not the only viable communication
technologies.
Based on the data collection from section 5.1 the 1260 datapoints produced 3.13
kb worth of data for a single measurement[15]. With a sampling rate of 50 Hz the
data rate results in 156.5 kb/s. The needed data rate is likely to be lower. Only
transmitting values for sensor that have changed compared to previous transmission
is an eﬃcient way of reducing data. This method will more than half the average
data rate needed.
In the diabetic neuropathy use case this is about the same amount of datapoints and
sample rate that can be expect for a successful solution. Sleep time for this application
can be a lot longer due to the nature that the severity of harmful pressure is increased
by the duration it is applied.
For the work environment use case not nearly as many data points are needed and
the 16 datapoints present in the original solution might be enough to estimate weight
to a satisfactory degree. For the solution to provide a complete picture of the loads
carried throughout a day every step needs to be measured. This a lower required
data rate but power consumption becomes more important.
If a smartphone is used as a bridging device the already integrated technologies BLE,
WiFi and cellular are very well suited. Cheap dedicated bridging device could be
made to use with other technologies. It does however not seem viable to go in this
direction when smartphones are so widely used. The cheaper installation of an app
and the reduced hassle of not carrying an additional device is the better option.
Disregarding the bridging device and placing nodes around users that the insole can
communicate directly with is also an option. Technologies not covered such as z-
wave and Zigbee are possibilities in this case[30]. It is required that the environment
operated in is fairly open for the range to be suﬃcient. This does not seem favourable
compared to the use of a smartphone. More infrastructure is require as well as the
limitation of the work environment with the only upside being that a phone is not
required among the users.

4.3
Database
The purpose of the database was to continuously store measurements from the device.
A database suited for a stream of large amount of data was required. To serve this
goal the time series database InﬂuxDB was chosen.
Scalability is very important for the solution. The amount of data required to be
stored is dependent on the use case. The two proposed use cases both produce quite
large data amounts. The diabetic neuropathy use case provides a detailed plantar
pressure map with many data points per foot. With a high sample rate for as long
as power consumption allows. The work environment use case also has a high sample
rate. The pressure map is how ever not required to be as detailed but in turn needs
to run for an entire workday at the time.
One of the downsides of InﬂuxDB and time series databases in general compared to
an ACID (Atomicity, Consistency, Isolation, Durability) database is its reliability.
This is less of a problem for the use cases presented. The large amount of datapoints
in combination with the cyclical nature of gait means that any trends should be
identiﬁable even with some loss of data. The analysis tools required to handle the
data would already need to handle contaminated data points due to many variables
that can impact the plantar pressure map. If the percentage of lost datapoints is too
high this will obviously be an issue but this is not expected.
Since the data will be a continuous stream it will be added to the database ordered
by time or at least close to it if sent in batches. InﬂuxDB is designed for this scenario
meaning performance is already optimised. If data is however in random order it
will be signiﬁcantly more cumbersome. Something that needs to be kept in mind
when designing the data management. Once data has been written, editing will not
be required. This means that by restricting editing functionality, write and query
functionality can be made more performant.

through a .txt ﬁle. A snippet of the ﬁle can be seen in ﬁgure 15. The database was

6.1
Design goals
The ideal speciﬁcations of the design is highly dependent upon its use with goals
that often are contradictory. This section will investigate and analyse the design
speciﬁcation of the original design and compare its alignment with the diﬀerent use
cases presented. New speciﬁcations will be presented if more suitable are found.
6.2
Diabetic Neuropathy
The original design is inherited ﬂawed with goal of identifying issues for patients
with Charcot foot. The sensors were placed strategically along points where pressure
usually is applied (see ﬁgure 18). This assumes the patients have the same main
pressure points as an standard foot. The problem with this is that a symptom of
Charcot is foot deformation. When the foot is deformed it will not have the same
pressure points anymore (see ﬁgure 33). And the principles that the sensors have
been placed according to, are no longer valid.
One of the original hypothesises were that by measuring the areas where the pressure
is supposed to be it, could be deducted if another resting point was relieving it. For
this to be true it is required to know both how pressure would be distributed and
its size. The size is however highly dependent on the weight of the user which is
something that gradually changes.Identifying the diﬀerence between a deformation
that relieves pressure evenly and the user losing weight will be incredibly diﬃcult.

Another downside is that the device can not measure pressure on the entire surface
of the foot. Meaning that it can only be used to detect an area of high pressure and
not conﬁrm the absence of it. This will likely lead to the patient still needing to visit
the hospital to ensure that the areas where no sensors are present are not developing
ulcers.
A foot therapists at Odense university hospital presented an example where a more
complete pressure map would save the patient a lot of discomfort along with saving
time for the health professionals. Here a patient came in with ulcers and the regular
process was done. Customising support soles until the pressure readings presented
no problem areas. When the patient returned the ulcers had grown and the process
was repeated but resulted in no detected issues. It turned out later that the patient
often sat at the computer with his feet on a footstool. The corner of the footstool
where the patients feet were resting caused the high pressure area that in turn lead
to the growth of the ulcer.
More sensors with less spacing covering the entire area of the foot would have de-
tected the issue. Adding sensor the sides of the foot would also be preferable due
to foot deformation. Deformed feet often lead to irregular walking patterns that can
cause load being applied along sides of the foot. The downside of these additional
features would be the increased power consumption. This can be combated with
lower sampling rate, larger battery, making it more easily rechargeable, or a device
sleep schedule.
The dangers of high pressure is increased with the duration of which it is applied.

6.3
Work environment safety
The original design ﬁts a lot better when its use is to measure standard feet. The
design premise is more aligned with the use of tracking work place behaviour. If the
goal would be to estimated to load that was carried by an employee, then design
parameters like power consumption and battery life become even more important
for a successful solution. With this use every step would need to be recorded and
at a high sampling rate. As covered in section 2.3.1 and 5.5 the heel rise needs to
be identiﬁed for a weight estimation to be done. The total force grows rapidly in
the part of the gait cycle where the heel rise occurs.
This means a high sample
rate would be required to measure accurate data. The device would require a long
enough battery life to continuously run for least 8 hours between charges, to ensure
the whole workday got monitored. This could be solved like before by adding a bigger
battery but again this would not be ideal due to the increase in price, weight and the
limitation of space.
Luckily the important data to measure is cyclical meaning it can be predicted. With
the goal to estimated load carried the only required measurements would be at the
heel rise. The heel rise is at the same point in the gait cycle. The length of the
gait cycle is however determined by the cadence of the user which will vary based on
factors like load carried and energy levels. A ﬁxed time based sleep schedule is not
viable. The pressure follows a pattern and this can be used to predict when the heel
rise is about to occur. The heel pressure would increase before the weight is shifted
forward and the heel rise happens.

Only the heel sensor would be required to identify when this increase happens. From
here on there are two solutions. Solution one is using the pressure as on/oﬀtrigger
for the rest of the sensor. If pressure was measured at sensor A (see ﬁgure 34) the
rest of the sensors would turn on. Based on the data collected, 0.4s of the gait cycle
(1.1s) had pressure on the heel which means power consumption would be reduced
to ﬁve sensors for 64% of the time.
Solution two uses the knowledge that pressure on the heel is slightly reduced before
it is entirely released at the heel rise. The heel sensors would be able to detect this
and trigger the rest of the sensors to switch on. This would mean all sensors would
only be active for 3-5 frames (0.06-0.1s) depending on how accurate the heel rise
can be predicted. This method requires some more calculations made by the micro
controller to determine when all the sensors should be turned on.
The correct of these two solutions can only deﬁnitely chosen when the entire device
is ready and power consumption measurements can be done.

7
Conclusion
Internet of Things is booming within the medical industry. Many avenues are being
examined to ﬁnd if such devices will provide beneﬁt and relief for both the patient
and health professional. This project investigates a previous designed IoT device
designed to assist patients with Charcot foot. The ailment was analysed and key
markers for complications was identiﬁed. It was determined that a detailed plantar
pressure map would be ideal to detect harmful levels of pressure. Another suitable
application was found in the form of work related back injuries. Many people suﬀer
from back injuries that both cause pain and costs employers a lot of money. There
was found a strong indication that at a certain point in the gait cycle it was possible
to measure the weight of the user. This can help assess if a worker is being worn down
by carrying too much weight for too long amounts of time. Additional investigation
was made to see if the position of the load could be determined. This lead to no
substantial ﬁndings concluding that more sensors was required. Based on the analysis
and its ﬁndings, improvements for the design were suggested. This resulted in two
design directions.
One would add more sensor cells resulting in a more detailed
picture of pressure, intended for Charcot patients.
To counteract the additional
power consumption sleep schedules would be used. The second direction was to aid
workers. The hardware is more aligned with the original solution with few sensor but
a high sampling rate. Power eﬃciency would be gained by utilising the knowledge
of the pressure distribution throughout a gait cycle to activate sensors only when
needed.
