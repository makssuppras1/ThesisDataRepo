Abstract
We present a study of both in-plane and out-of-plane lattice vibrations and phonons for 2D square and hexagonal
lattices. The in-plane phonons are based on a spring model, while the out-of-plane phonons are based on a Fermi-
Pasta-Ulam-Tsingou problem (FPUT problem). For the in-plane phonons, we have calculated dispersion relations
with both longitudinal and transverse branches, and for the case of different atoms in the same lattice, the dispersion
relation additionally has both acoustic and optical branches. Our studies show that a gap can exist in the dispersion
relation for a square lattice with two different kinds of atoms. Furthermore, we have calculated both the phonon
density of states and the density of occupied states. For the out-of-plane phonons, we have also calculated the
dispersion relations. Additionally, for these phonons we have calculated critical amplitudes for the π-modes, above
which the phonon solutions break down and are replaced by a new, erratic kind of motion in which the energy of
the system can get localised and form chaotic breathers. All analytical predictions, including the frequencies from
the dispersion relations and the critical amplitudes, are confirmed using numerical simulations and animations.

of motion using different models. We will use a coupled spring model for the in-plane motion and a Fermi-Pasta-

Chapter 1
Introduction
Since the isolation of graphene in 2004, there has been a growing interest in 2D materials, i.e. materials with a
thickness of a single atom, due to their exceptional characteristics including “high strength, extreme lightweight
nature, good mobility of carriers, great modulus of elasticity, (...) high UV adsorption, and superconductivity”
[1]. Because of these characteristics, 2D materials have many potential applications including “use in solar cells,
in micro- and optoelectronics, in composite materials, catalysis, in various types of sensors and light detectors, in
biomedical imaging or in the transport of drugs in the organism” [2] and many more. Like many other materials,
2D materials can vibrate, e.g. if excited by light [2]. The quanta of lattice vibrations are called phonons, and they
affect all physical processes in solids [3].
In this project, we will study the lattice vibrations and phonons of idealised 2D materials with different geometries.
We will study both phonons for vibrations in the plane of the materials as well as phonons for vibrations out of the
plane of the materials. As we will see, many different phonons exist, and they can have very different frequencies
and energies. For the in-plane phonons, some of the questions we will answer are the following: How can we build a
model that can describe phonons in 2D materials? Can we simulate these phonons? In what ways can 2D materials
vibrate? How does the frequency of the phonons depend on their wavenumbers? How does the geometry of the
lattice impact the phonons of the material?
The case of out-of-plane phonons is somewhat simpler than the case of in-plane phonons, as there is only one
direction in which the vibrations can take place. This makes out-of-plane phonons easier to handle, and therefore
we will dare to venture further for these phonons. Inspired by Dauxois, Khomeriki and Ruffo [4], who are themselves
inspired by the famous FPUT problem [5], we will study and simulate what happens when nonlinearities are added
to the equations of motion. This is interesting in two ways. Firstly, real interaction potentials between atoms
give rise to nonlinear equations of motion, and therefore we expect to get a better approximation to the real world
when we add nonlinearities to the equations of motion. Secondly, nonlinear dynamics is an interesting mathematical
subject in its own right since nonlinear equations can exhibit many interesting phenomena that are impossible in the
linear theory. For the case of out-of-plane phonons with added nonlinearities, some of the questions we will answer
are the following: What is the effect of the nonlinearities in the equations of motion? How does the distribution of
energy evolve in time? Can we predict conditions under which the energy will get localised and form breathers?

Chapter 2
In-Plane Vibrations
In this chapter, we will introduce the notation for talking about 2D materials, and we will use this to develop
models for different types of 2D lattices, namely the square and the hexagonal lattice. Using these models, we will
study in-plane oscillations in this chapter, while we will delay the study of out-of-plane oscillations chapter 3.

Square Lattice
2.1.1
Spring Model
We will start by constructing a model that can explain the concept of phonons in a 2D material. Furthermore, we
will also start by considering a square lattice of atoms, as this seems like the simplest possible 2D lattice geometry.
The square lattice spans a plane with R rows and S columns of atoms.
An example of a square lattice with
R = S = 5 is shown in figure 2.1. To describe the motions of the atoms in the lattice, we will use a local coordinate
system with the origin placed at each lattice position, indexed by (r, s), where r is the row number of the given
atom, and s is the column number of the given atom. Thus, the atom in the top left corner has indices (1, 1), and
the atom in the bottom right corner has indices (R, S). We denote the mass of atom (r, s) by mr,s. We let xr,s
denote the displacement in the x-direction from the origin of atom (r, s), and we let pxr,s denote the momentum in

choose the direction of each local y-axis to be downwards. This might seem counter-intuitive at first, but it turns
out to be very practical. When using this convention, yr+1,s will be at a larger global y-value than yr,s, and this
is (at least for us) easier to remember than the opposite convention. This convention is also useful when coding in
MATLAB or other programming languages with similar storage rules, as all of the x-values etc. can be stored in a
matrix in which xr,s is stored at position (r, s). In the following, we will consider movement only in the plane of
the lattice, i.e. in x- and y-directions.

In most of this project, we will use periodic boundary conditions (Born-van Karman boundary conditions) for the
lattices to avoid finite size effects. Periodic boundary conditions means that the lattice “wraps around” itself at
the edges. For the square lattice, this means that the atoms at the right edge (with index r = R) of the lattice
will be considered neighbours to the atoms at the left edge (with index r = 1), and similarly for the other edges
and for diagonal couplings. In general, periodic boundary conditions means that we will replace indices R + 1 and
S + 1 with index 1, and we will replace index 0 by either R or S depending on the position of the index. Using

Since we have a 2 × 2 dynamical matrix in equation (2.34), we have 2 eigenvalues for each combination of qr and

the displacement is in the same direction as the wave vector, (qr, qs), and the other branch consists of transverse
phonons where the displacement is perpendicular to the wave vector. However, this description of the phonons is
only accurate close to the centre of the Brillouin zone, i.e. close to qr = qs = 0. Away from this point, the phonons
are in general not entirely longitudinal or transverse, but they are still close to one of the two types. This fine
distinction is not taken into account in the paper by Tian et al. [6], and therefore they do not find all the possible
phonons of the system, but only the subset consisting of the phonons that are perfectly longitudinal or transverse.

combine this with an L for longitudinal phonons and a T for transverse phonons, and thus we can write LO, TO,

framework). The density of states is the number of phonon modes per unit frequency, ω, and this function is
used in e.g. statistical mechanics to calculate many thermodynamic properties [8]. We will denote the density of

From the simulations above, we quite clearly see that the square lattice is not stable when one is using the Lennard-
Jones potential. It very quickly deforms, and we see that the atoms reach some kind of quasi-hexagonal state.
On the other hand, the hexagonal lattice is very stable, and we see only very small displacements from the initial
configuration.
This suggests that we also need to study other types of lattices than the simple square lattice.
However, a Lennard-Jones model is certainly not the only way to model crystals or other compounds, and flat and
buckled square lattices have been observed and predicted for certain elements in certain environments, e.g. Fe in
graphene [14]. However, other sources suggest that the stable structure is actually a square lattice with a 2-atom
basis with both Fe and C atoms [15]. Finally, hexagonal lattices are common for many compounds, e.g. for Pb
[16]. We will now investigate these different lattices and their phonons in more detail. We will start with the square
lattice with a 2-atom basis (section 2.3), and afterwards we will investigate the hexagonal lattice (section 2.4).

First of all, this might destroy some structure from of problem that is present in D. In our case, D is Hermitian

Hermitian. Secondly, our numerical experiments in MATLAB show that solving the generalised eigenvalue problem
numerically is much more robust with respect to numerical errors than solving the standard eigenvalue problem for
M−1D. Luckily, generalised eigenvalue problems are easily solved in MATLAB, and thus the extra complexity is
not a problem for our calculations, though it may take a longer time to perform them. Using the matrix equation

If we look at the hexagonal lattice in figure 2.30, we see that the number of nearest neighbours is 6 for most atoms,
but it changes at the edges. If we use periodic boundary conditions, there is always 6 nearest neighbours, but if we
use free boundary conditions, we need to use a different number of nearest neighbours at the edges. For example,
the atom in the top left corner has 2 nearest neighbours and the atom at the bottom left corner has 3 nearest
neighbours when using free boundary conditions. Unlike the square lattice, the number of nearest neighbours is
not the same on each edge and in each corner. In the hexagonal case, the number of neighbours and their indices
relative to the considered atom instead also depend on the specific configuration of the lattice. This is why we
introduce the axial indices. To avoid confusion, we will be using (r, s) for the “standard” square indices and (n, m)
for the axial indices. Notice that this m is different for the m used for the masses, but we believe that it is always
clear from the context what m we are referring to. Looking at an atom in the middle of the lattice with axial
indices (n, m), its 6 nearest neighbours have indices (n −1, m + 1), (n, m + 1), (n + 1, m), (n + 1, m −1), (n, m −1),
(n −1, m), no matter its position [17]. If we had chosen to use square indices to find the nearest neighbours,
the indices of the nearest neighbours would depend of both the configuration of the lattice and if the row/column
was odd/even. Using axial indices, this problem is avoided entirely. Additionally, we can relatively simply find
the nearest neighbours at the edges and corners as well in the case of free boundary conditions. We will unless
otherwise noted use periodic boundary conditions for the hexagonal lattice as well. Furthermore, we will use the
standard square indices for writing up the equations, storage, plotting etc., but we will use the axial indices for
finding neighbours, unless otherwise noted.

Out-of-Plane Vibrations
3.1
Out-of-Plane Potentials
Until now, we have only considered movement of the atoms in the plane of the lattice. However, one can easily
imagine that the atoms can move out of this plane as well, e.g. perpendicular to the plane, like shaking a blanket.
We need some way to describe this motion as well. A first idea is to simply expand the model for the in-plane
oscillations with a z-variable, i.e. using the dimensionless distance measure
d(r,s),(r′,s′) =
q
(s −s′ + xr,s −xr′,s′)2) + (r −r′ + yr,s −yr′,s′)2) + (zr,s −zr′,s′)2).
(3.1)
Notice that there is no r −r′ or s −s′ in the z-term in equation (3.1) since all atoms are located in the same plane.
We could now proceed in the same way as in chapter 2 by writing up the Hamiltonian of the lattice, deriving the
equations of motion, linearising these equations etc. We have actually tried this for the square lattice, but it turns
out not to be a useful approach. Firstly, the equations of motion for the x- and y-directions are exactly the same
as equations (2.12)-(2.15) in the 2D case, except that the z-positions enter into the d-terms of the equations. The
equations for the z-direction are very similar, the only difference being that we do not have the ±1-terms on the
right-hand sides, since there are no differences in the z-positions of the atoms in the lattice. Secondly, linearising the
equations of motions both for the x- and y-directions yields exactly the same equations as in equations (2.26)-(2.29)!
Finally, linearising the resulting equations of motion for the z-direction simply yields 0. Thus, any movement in the
z-direction must be at least a second order effect. Since the entire framework of phonons is based on linear equations
of motion, we must adopt a different approach if we want to stay within the phonon framework. This leads the way
to a philosophical question: Should we throw away models that do not fit within a given framework, or should we
embrace these models? The answer depends on the circumstances. In this case, it is well-known that out-of-plane
phonons do exist [3], and therefore we need some way to describe them. This has been done successfully using
(nonzero) linear equations, and thus it seems to be the right way to proceed. Therefore, we will seek equations of
motion that have either have nonzero linear terms or yield such terms when linearising the equations of motion.
However, we can still consider nonlinearities added to these linear terms. This is interesting for two reasons. Firstly,
the real world is seldom linear, and nonlinear equations and analysis are thus needed in many applications. In the
case of vibrations of atoms, it is well-known that the linear equations of motion are an approximation, and we can
possibly make a better approximation by including nonlinear terms while still not considering the entirety of a real
potential. Secondly, nonlinear equations can show much more interesting and complicated behaviour than linear
equations, and thus the study of nonlinear equations is mathematically interesting in its own right.

Let us now consider what such a nonlinear model could look like. Consider a plane lattice of atoms. It is well
known that e.g. C atoms in graphene have a delocalised electron cloud. The atoms “share” electrons, and this
contributes to the stability of structure. Thus, one could imagine that any displacement in the z-direction of one or
more of the atoms would result in some resistance from the other atoms as the stable equilibrium is disturbed. This
would result in a force keeping the atoms in the planar structure, or at least trying to do so. Inspired by Dauxois,
Khomeriki and Ruffo [4] and our previous discussions, we choose to model this with a potential containing terms
of second and fourth order, V (z) = 1

The potential and the resulting equations of motion will be explained in more detail in the following section. For
now, we just motivate the overall idea. Equation (3.2) seems like a reasonable first approximation to many “real”
potentials.
Additionally, the potential is well-known and mathematically very interesting, as it gives rise to a
relatively simple dynamical system that can exhibit many interesting phenomena such as hysteresis and chaos [19].
Turning back to the in-plane spring model, we found that this kind of model cannot possibly give rise to equations
such as equation (3.2) due to the linear term. However, the third order term does actually appear in the Taylor-
expansion of the equations of motion for the z-direction together with many other third order terms. Thus, one
could imagine that the first order term in equation (3.2) is due to the electron interactions between the atoms or
some other effect, while the third order terms are due to the “in-plane springs” between the atoms.
When using the equation of motion in equation (3.2), we effectively decouple the out-of-plane motion from the
in-plane motion of the atoms. This decoupling is know from other models as well, e.g. for graphene [20]. Thus,
chapters 2 and 3 can effectively be regarded completely independently of each other. They do, however, describe
different phenomena in the same kind of system. Thus, the two chapters treat two different kinds of motion of the
considered systems, and to get the full picture we need to consider both of them.

to the lattice, and thus the only thing changing from 1D to 2D is the number of neighbours for a given atom and
thereby also the number of terms in the equations of motion. The form of the equations of motion, the number of
variables for each atom etc. stay the same. Additionally, the 1D problem is mathematically interesting in itself,
and we will see some interesting results shortly.

Looking at all the plots and videos above, we can see multiple interesting points. Firstly, our approximate expression
for the critical amplitude seems to work quite well. For a = 0.030, we see that the energy is completely evenly
distributed at all points in time in the simulation. However, for larger values of a, e.g. a = 0.050, we see that
energy gets localised at around t = 4200. For larger amplitudes such as a = 0.2255 and a = 0.425, this localisation
takes place much sooner at around t = 200 and t = 70 respectively. This all makes sense intuitively. When the
amplitude is smaller than the critical amplitude, ac ≈0.0456 for R = S = 20, the solution given by equation (3.29)
is completely conserved. However, when the amplitude is larger than the critical amplitude, the solution given by
equation (3.29) breaks down. The larger the amplitude is, the worse the linear approximation is, and thus the faster
the solution in equation (3.29) breaks down, and the energy gets localised. Looking at the animations, we can see
that the localisation, the chaotic breathers, move around in the lattice. Furthermore, we see that for a = 0.425 (see
figure 3.25), the participation ratio seems to decrease to a level around C0 = 2 after the initial increase. However,
no such thing happens for a = 0.2255 in our simulation (see figure 3.22), where the participation ratio instead
swings wildly between 15 and 45 after the initial increase from 0. Thus, it seems that a larger amplitude results
in a faster breakdown of the approximate solution, but our small investigation also shows that a larger amplitude
might also yield a faster equilibration of the energy after the initial localisation. All in all, we conclude that the
approximate expression for the critical amplitude again seems to match the numerical simulations well.

Looking at all the plots and videos above, we can see multiple interesting points. Firstly, our approximate expression

not seem to happen in our simulation in the case of the hexagonal lattice. The evolution of C0 for a = 0.05 also

is very short-lived. This is also very clear in the corresponding animation. An additional simulation until time

first peak. All in all, we conclude that our prediction of the critical amplitude for the (π, π)-mode works well for
both the square lattice and the hexagonal lattice.

Chapter 4
Conclusion and Future Work
In this thesis, we have investigated the lattice vibrations and phonons in idealised 2D materials with different
geometries, both in the plane of the material and out of this plane.
For the in-plane motion, we have build a spring model that approximately describes the motions of the atoms in
the plane of the material for both a square and a hexagonal lattice. Using this model, we have simulated different
kinds of motion of the atoms, and inspired by this we have linearised the equations of motion and used the resulting
linear equations to make calculations about the phonons of the system. We have calculated the dispersion relation
of the phonons, i.e. the frequency of the phonons as a function of the wavenumbers, and we have shown that both
longitudinal and transverse phonons exist in the systems with the longitudinal phonons having a higher frequency
than their corresponding transverse counterparts. When the lattice contains only one kind of atoms, we have shown
that only acoustic phonons exist, i.e. phonons with a frequency of 0 when the wavenumber is 0. However, when the
(square) lattice contains two different kinds of atoms, there also exist optical phonons, i.e. phonons with a frequency
different from 0 when the wavenumber is 0. We have shown that for certain parameters, a gap in the dispersion
relation exists. Thus, there is a range of frequencies that the collective motion of the phonons cannot exhibit. We
have also calculated the phonon density of states and density of occupied states, which are relevant in different
physical and chemical applications and measurements. In these densities of states, we see logarithmic peaks at the
stationary points of the dispersion relations, and we also clearly see the gaps from the dispersion relations when
these gaps exist. All of these results have been verified using numerical simulations.
For the out-of-plane motion, we have build a different model inspired by Dauxois, Khomeriki, and Ruffo [4] and
the famous FPUT problem [5].
In this model, we have studied nonlinearities and their effects on the out-of-
plane phonons. We have shown that the nonlinearities give an amplitude dependence in the dispersion relation
that increases the predicted amplitudes compared to the linear case. Furthermore, we have shown that critical
amplitudes exist for the π-modes of the phonons, and if the phonons have amplitudes above the critical amplitudes,
the phonon motion breaks down and is replaced by a new, erratic kind of motion in which the energy of the system
can be localised and form chaotic breathers. These critical amplitudes depend only on the size of the lattice. We
have shown that the the critical amplitudes appear in both the square and the hexagonal lattice, and the critical
amplitudes are in fact exactly equal in the two lattices. Finally, we have verified our prediction of the frequencies
and critical amplitudes using numerical simulations.
Though we have studied many systems and phenomena, there are still many things we have not yet considered. One
obvious extension of this thesis would be to add nonlinearities to the equations of motion for the in-plane motion
and to study what effect these have on the phonons. Could we see something similar to the out-of-plane phonons?
106
==PAGE:111==
Furthermore, it would be interesting to look at more different geometries of the lattices, e.g. the honeycomb lattice
of graphene. One could also study out-of-plane phonons in a lattice containing two or more different kinds of atoms
and/or calculate the density of states and density of occupied states for the out-of-plane phonons. Finally, one could
also try to connect the studies in this thesis to real materials by finding or calculating parameter values for a number
of real 2D materials. This could be useful in many technological applications as mentioned in the introduction.

A.3
Fourier Transform of the Phonon Simulations
In this section, we show the Fourier spectrum of one of the phonon simulations from section 2.1.2. To calculate
the Fourier spectrum, we use MATLAB’s fft that uses a fast Fourier transform algorithm. In fact, since our data
is dependent on both the position of the atoms in the lattice, e.g. (r, s), and time, t, we have a 2D signal, and
therefore we use fft2 in MATLAB. Furthermore, we consider the signal to be all x- and y-positions of all atoms.
Thus, we store the signal in a matrix with all positions at a given time in one row of the matrix. Our time step
is dt = 0.1, and our “index step” is dn = 1. The number of variables is 2RS = 2 · 202 = 800, and the number
of time steps is 1001 (from time t = 0 to time t = 100). It is known that to get the right result from fft, one
needs to scale the result by the discretisation size for each dimension1. In our case, that means we scale the result
by dtdn = 0.1 · 1 = 0.1. This is important when one wants to compare the result with analytical expressions, but
overall this is just a scaling of the intensity, and it does not impact the shape of the spectrum in any way. Using
the procedure and functions described above, the result of the simulation from section 2.1.2 with jr = 0 and js = 7
(figure 2.10) are shown below. Using equation (2.34), the predicted the frequency is ω = 2.1825.

inspection shows that the peak has split up in two peaks, one at ω ≈0.93 and one at ω = 1. At this point it is
unclear to us why this is the case, but one idea is that it could be due to numerical errors when calculating the
generalised eigenvalues that give us the squared frequencies, ω2. Small errors here can make a significant difference
in the density of states if the errors make a given state switch bin in the histogram, and it could be that this is
what we see in figure A.10. The rest of the densities of states look to be very similar, including both the peaks and
the gap. Thus, we conclude that both choices of indices give (approximately) the same results. Finally, we have
also calculated the density of occupied states for the density of states in figure A.2 in the same way as described in
section 2.1.3. The result is shown in figure A.11 together with the corresponding result from section 2.3.
