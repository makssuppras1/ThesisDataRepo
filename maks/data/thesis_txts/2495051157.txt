Abstract
Private spending has long been a fantasy for privacy-idealists, cyberpunks and criminals
alike. Still, the ordinary citizen is raised with the idea, that ”if you have nothing to hide, you
have nothing to fear”, and seldom see a particular beneﬁt from private value transfers. Never-
theless, knowledge is power, and with the spending history, the bank (and thereby government)
can infer whom you associate with, your location and more, limiting liberty. Money talks, but
talking is leaking, and we need private spending to protect against those who wish to exert
control and censor. In this work, we present three contributions, with the zero-knowledge
scheme being our main contribution.
First, we perform a systematic literature review on the realm of privacy for electronic cur-
rencies. We present the development of electronic currencies to cryptocurrencies and focus on
the techniques that are employed to enhance user-privacy. Furthermore, we present ﬂaws of
the current cryptocurrency systems, allowing an attacker to reduce the privacy of the users of
a system. Based on this literature review, we describe three research directions to enhance pri-
vacy for cryptocurrencies: transaction propagation mechanisms, ZK-SNArKs without a trusted setup,
and specialised trustless zero-knowledge proofs. We investigate two of these research directions
and the second and third contribution in this work is the results of these investigations.
Second, we propose an addition to the transaction propagation mechanism of MimbleWim-
ble-like systems that slightly improve the anonymity by incentivising fast transaction aggre-
gation through fees. We ﬁnd that current MimbleWimble projects seldom hide the individ-
ual transactions suﬃciently, an attacker who observes the network can partially recreate the
transaction graph, and thereby learn the spending history of users.
Third, we propose a trustless scheme, that limits the transaction graph and can be deployed
on top of currency managing outputs with Pedersen commitments. The scheme allows a user
to move coins from the base-currency to a shielded pool from which the user may spend her
coins without disclosing them. For the case of MimbleWimble, our scheme oﬀsets privacy with
storage, as it is not possible to prune our shielded outputs.

Chapter 1
Introduction
”Neither my government nor my neighbour can punish my thoughts because neither can
read my mind. That is why thoughts are free”, David D. Friedman1
Privacy has been highly debated in the media in the years after 2000. Each year is showcas-
ing new scandals of governments and companies performing mass-surveillance or failing to
address substantial security ﬂaws in their systems2,3.
With the move from cash to credit cards and e-wallets, spending has never been as conve-
nient as now. However, convenience comes at a price, never before have our spending habits
been monitored as much as they are now [1]. With the move from cash, we volunteered for
a cell in the panopticon4, allowing ﬁnancial institutions and governments to watch our every
purchase.
By claiming that the collected data is used to catch wrongdoers, governments have gained
a free-pass for additional surveillance at every occasion possible, most prominent by 9/11 and
the Patriot Act [1]. However, as showcased from the money-laundering through Danske Bank,
the collected information may not lead to catching the responsible5.
Following the belief that governments with complete knowledge will be able to pro-
tect against terror, lawmakers are pushing towards backdoor-by-law; demanding that tech-
companies provide a back-door to be used only by the government6. While creating a backdoor
is easy, guaranteeing that only a speciﬁc entity can use it is deemed impossible. However, say
that this is possible; then, the entity (or its employees) will decide who does right, who does
wrong, and when to utilise the backdoor. Providing such power to authorities may not be in
the best interest of the citizens.
With the ﬁnancial crisis of 2008, a pseudonymous actor, Satoshi Nakamoto, published a
proposal for a new monetary system, Bitcoin. The system provides a purely digital currency

Chapter 2
Literature Review Methodology
In this section, we present our methodology for discovering relevant literature; we base our
approach on the work of Petersen and Wohlin [2, 3]. We initially present the research questions
and then move into the search strategy and selection of papers. Beyond papers found through
this method, the author has included 2 relevant papers based on the presentations at the con-
ference Theory and Practice of Blockchains 2019 at Aarhus University that was not found by the
review.
2.1
Research Questions
We deﬁne privacy in cryptocurrencies, as the ability to perform private transactions. A pri-
vate transaction has two distinct properties: i) conﬁdentiality, i.e., hiding the amounts, and
ii) anonymity, i.e., hiding the sender and receiver [4]. To distil research questions and search
queries, we use the PICOC criteria as described by Kichenham et al. [5]:
• Population: we consider the set of digital currencies / electronic cash / cryptocurren-
cies.
• Intervention: the techniques utilised to perform private transactions.
• Comparison: we compare the techniques by their compatibility with existing curren-
cies, their objective and how well they protect the privacy of the users.
• Outcomes: we present privacy-techniques, their limitations and a direction for future
research within this area.
• Context: the comparison will be performed within academics, i.e., by scientiﬁc papers
analysing the protocols.
Based on this method, we deﬁne the following research questions:
1. How is privacy enforced in current cryptocurrencies?
2. How well do these protocols and techniques enforce privacy, and how can they be at-
tacked?
3. How can we improve privacy techniques?

2.1.1
Search Queries
From these research questions, we construct a set of keywords that we believe provides a broad
view of the current implementations and their weaknesses. To catch the area, we use the
synonyms for electronic cash, i.e., digital cash and cryptocurrency, to construct a set of search-
queries: anonymous electronic cash, anonymity attack electronic cash, anonymous digital cash,
anonymity attack digital cash, anonymous cryptocurrency, anonymity attack cryptocurrency.
2.2
Study selection
To ﬁnd appropriate studies, we initially perform an automated database search using the DTU
Find-it service; a service that taps into ACM, IEEE, Scopus, CiteSeer, arXiv and other widely used
journals and databases, as well as books and pre-prints1.
2.2.1
Structure
In this section, we outline our search structure based on the work of Petersen [2]. Details about
the individual parts are taken in the upcoming paragraphs.
1. Initial retrieval of the result for the search-queries.
2. (Automated) Duplicate removal (only include newest version of a speciﬁc paper)
3. (Automated) Perform inclusion and exclusion based on title and abstract
4. Perform manual duplicate removal
5. Perform manuel inclusion and exclusion on titles and abstracts
6. Full-text readthrough
7. Snowball sampling
2.2.1.1
Exclusion and Inclusion Criteria
As described by Petersen [2], we wish to utilise a series of exclusion and inclusion criteria (EC and
IC, respectively) to retrieve the subset of publications that seem the best ﬁt for our purpose.
We deﬁne the set of criteria as:
• EC1: paper is not accessible in full-text
• EC2: paper is not presented in English
• EC3: the paper has no title or abstract
• EC4: the proposal of the paper requires additional hardware, i.e., a physical e-wallet
• EC5: the paper has less than min((2019 −year) ∗2, 5) citations
1A full list is available at: http://libguides.dtu.dk/c.php?g=654792&p=4600996
4
==PAGE:15==
• IC1: the work should use, analyse or propose an anonymity-enhancing protocol
• IC2: the work should use, analyse or propose an electronic cash system
• IC3: the work should use, analyse or propose a decentralised protocol
Following the guidelines of Petersen et al., it is proposed to reject studies if they have not passed
peer-reviewing [2]; however, we ﬁnd that this could exclude essential papers, as some papers
that are published outside academia are highly inﬂuential on the entire area, e.g. the Bitcoin
paper [6].
A paper is excluded if it satisﬁes any of the EC’s and accepted only if it satisﬁes IC1 together
with either IC2 or IC3 and no EC’s. We use this construction as relevant protocols may come
from outside the cryptocurrency space. An example is anonymous broadcasting, which does
not satisfy IC2 but is useful for privacy-preserving currencies.
To enforce these criteria we perform a partial automatic search using the Python Script
shown in Appendix A [Python Literature Review Script], performing actions on the
extracted BibTex. As EC1 cannot be applied automatically, due to inconsistent results in DTU
Find-it, we perform this manually at the end of 5, hence the number of papers to check is then
the smallest. Also, we discard entries in the BibTex with no title or abstract. Because it is not
always clear whether the proposed or analysed protocol utilises a physical device (EC4) from
key-words alone, we perform this criterion manually only.
To enforce the IC’s, we believe that a set of keywords allows us to ﬁnd papers that lie within
this realm. We believe that the set of keywords below represent the IC’s well and allow us to
include potentially relevant papers.
To mitigate that we ﬁnd a vast number of papers focusing on the ﬁnancial value of the
currency and not much else, we introduce a threshold of 2 keywords to be included in the title
or abstract, for the paper to be deemed relevant.
Keywords: peer to peer, p2p, peertopeer, peer-to-peer, privacy, private, ledger, weakness, vulnera-
bility, vulnerable, attack, challenge, de-anonymising, trace.
For the manual readthrough of titles and abstracts, the papers are excluded outright only
if they explicitly state the need for a physical device. The papers that are included satisfy the
abovementioned IC’s following the outlined rules. As a single author does this, it may be a
single point of failure possibly rejecting papers that are useful for the review. We include a
paper for full-text readthrough if there is doubt to whether it should be discarded or not.
2.2.1.2
Snowball sampling
We perform forward and backward snowball sampling as described by Wohlin [3], to account for
vital papers, that may not have been included in our initial search because they were not caught
by our queries or may have been published outside the reach of DTU Find-it.
We pick possible papers based on where they have been cited, their title, and the authors.
If a paper is written by an author we know is working within this space (e.g., Benedict B¨unz, Dan
Boneh, Sarah Meiklejohn, Andrew Poelstra etc.) we pick it as a possible relevant paper.

2.3
Screening process
In this section, we elaborate upon the actual process of ﬁnding relevant papers. Further, we
present the number of papers found and excluded at each stage throughout the process.
Initial retrieval of the result for the search-queries
The search on DTU Findit was
performed on the 15 of August 2019. Following our initial search using the 6 search-queries,
we ﬁnd a total of 1823 papers.
Duplicate removal
By automatic removal of the duplicate papers, e.g., papers that have
been found by multiple queries, updated or in some other manner been retrieved multiple
times, we discard 802 papers, going further with 1021 papers. As seen in Figure 2.1, most
of the papers that seem relevant for our search is published after the year 2000.
Perform inclusion and exclusion based on title and abstract
By applying the key-
words used for exclusion and inclusion, we further shrunk the set of relevant papers to 250. This

3.1
A primer on Electronic Cash
The idea of transferring electronic cash backed by a bank, without the bank learning the
speciﬁcs is often credited to David Chaum. In 1983 Chaum published a paper [7] that proposed
the idea of such an anonymous electronic cash system by utilising blinded signatures. As noted by
Chaum, a blinded signature scheme allows an entity to sign a message, without exposing the
message. This is done by concealing the message, and then signing the concealed message; the
concealed signature can then be unveiled, revealing a signature on the initial message. An ana-
logue example provided by Chaum is carbon paper envelopes [7], i.e., signing on the envelope
will leave a signature on the paper within.
Let c be a function that conceals a message x and c(x) be the concealed message. To reveal
x, we perform its inverse c′(x), i.e., c′(c(x)) = x. Also, let σ be a function that signs a message x
and σ(x) be the signature. A blindedsignature scheme will then support that a signature σ(c(x))
of a blinded message, can be revealed by applying the reveal function c′(σ(c(x)) = σ(x). This
is done in such a manner that the signer cannot link the signature σ(x) to any of its blind
signatures σ(c(x)).
Within a decade, Chaum was able to construct a practical implementation of his envisioned
electronic cash system [8]; the system allows a user to create electronic cash only if she cooperates
with the bank.
”Generatinganelectroniccashshouldbediﬃcultforanyone, unlessitisdoneincooperation
with a bank”, David Chaum [8].
The electronic cash of the scheme takes the form of digital notes, with a ﬁxed monetary value of
1 USD. The scheme then utilises blindly signed notes to ensure that the anonymity of the user
is protected unconditionally; but only as long as she is honest. To ensure that a user is unable
to spend a note multiple times, i.e., perform a double-spending, the scheme splits every note
(1 USD) into a set of ”subcomponents”, of which half is transferred during payment. By doing
so, double-spending will generate overlapping ”subcomponent”-sets with high probability. In a
simpliﬁed example; where the initial spending uses the ”subcomponents” 1..k
2, and the second
spending uses k
2..k −1, the overlap, k
2, can be used to identify the malicious user.
A limitation of the system is that coins are non-transferrable, i.e., coins can be passed only
9
==PAGE:20==
once, after a transfer from A to B the coins have to be redeemed at the bank. It is at this with-
drawal that a possible double-spending is spotted, i.e., online veriﬁcation with a bank at the
time of payment is necessary to prevent double-spending.
Shortly after the proposal of Chaum, Hayes [31] proposed a system that allowed a coin to
be transacted multiple times, i.e., let A →B denote a transfer from A to B, then A →B →C
is possible. This was enforced by having the current owner sign the chain of transactions, i.e.,
when C receives the coin it contains a chain of transactions partly signed by A and B. To mitigate
leakage of the owners identities in these transaction-chains, the scheme proposes the use of
pseudonyms, of which the user can generate arbitrarily many [31].
Following the ideas of Chaum [8] closely, an approach building on Schnorr signatures [9] was
proposed by Brands [32]. His proposal strives to support oﬀ-line payments; however, to prevent
double-spending the scheme requires the use of physical e-wallets, called observers. If these
observers are not present, double-spending cannot be prevented, but only detected after it has
occurred, similarly to the scheme of Chaum.
As showcased by Petersen in 1997 [33], a variety of Electronic Cash systems with varying revo-
cation capabilities is proposed. As noted by the authors, the ability to revoke anonymity only in
the case of a double-spending, support criminals to perform perfect crimes. An example of such
is money-laundering; as no double-spending occurs, the identity of the launderer is perfectly
hidden. From this, the term ”fair cash system” is developed. A fair cash system is a monetary
system where an honest user is to be protected, but a malicious user should be deanonymized.
However, as malicious behaviour is not as strictly deﬁned as double-spending, such solutions
require some trustee is to be introduced; a judge (who is assumed to obey the law) or similar.
Hau et al. [34, 35] propose to use group-signatures with a judge as the manager to support what
they note as auditable tracing, i.e., support detection of illegal tracing.
Similarly, Pﬁtzmann [36] proposes to let the user himself act as a trustee to allow him to
deanonymize an entity that is extorting him. However, the paper does not clearly state how
malicious use of these actions should be limited.
In the work of Sander [37], we see another variation to the earlier scheme, proposing that
unconditional privacy should be available only for transfers below a ﬁxed monthly limit. Their
proposal take inspiration from the world of cash, where bank tellers are obliged to note cash-
transaction beyond 10,000 USD. Another scheme by Peirce [38] building on Chaum [8] found
that storing every spent coin is necessary to prevent double-spending correctly, leading to an
ever-expanding set of spent coins.
Starting with Belenkiy [39] and Towsley [40], we see academia move towards decentralising
the use of electronic cash systems. They propose to utilise an anonymous electronic cash sys-
tem in order to incentivise participants in Peer-2-Peer (P2P) networks and limit freeloading.
In addition, Palaka [41], sought to use a P2P network to transact said coins while still having the
bank participating in the minting of funds. As the current proposals were still having issues in
regard to double-spend prevention, Osipkovetal. [42] propose to utilise a Distributed Hash Table
to keep track of spent coins. While this somewhat minimises the power and need of the bank
at transfer-time, their solution demands trusted witnesses and only supporting probabilistic
searching.
While proposing some new and interesting capabilities for the users, the genuine usage of
10
==PAGE:21==
electronic cash was minimal [40]. This could in part be because most schemes enforce escrowed
transfers, that make it possible for some judge to revoke the anonymity of the user. The user
does not believe that he is truly anonymous, limiting the beneﬁt in comparison to credit cards
[43].
Beyond just electronic cash system, the idea of anonymous electronic coupons [44] has also
been explored, however, as those are often used as coupons in stores, this is to little interest
for our purpose.
3.2
The birth of a cryptocurrency - Bitcoin
With the Bitcoin paper [6], Satoshi Nakamoto combines previously tested ideas [31, 42] into a
fully-ﬂedged currency that exists without the cooperation of a bank, or entity of authority.
Bitcoin utilises a ledger of blocks to record every transaction that has occurred in the network
and thereby provide a shared ”truth” between peers. Each block consists of a set of transac-
tions, a hash reference to the previous block and a nonce used in the veriﬁcation mechanism
(Proof-of-Work).
A transaction in Bitcoin is a transfer of some value between pseudonyms known as addresses
(hashes of public keys). By owning the matching private-key, one can transfer the Bitcoin that
the address owns. As Bitcoin is based on unspent transaction outputs (also called UTXO), and
not a balance, the address will in practice control a series of outputs which contains the funds.
For some output O, we write val(O) for its value and owner(O) for its owner.
A transaction consists of a series of sources, destinations and signatures to prove ownership
of the sources. A source is a previous transaction output (or coinbase/block reward), that has
not yet been spent and a destination is a freshly generated output1. For ease of notion and
distinguishability between sources and destinations, we denote si as source i and destination
j as dj. We then deﬁne a transaction message M with n sources and m destinations as the

By storing all transaction on the shared ledger, every peer can identify the valid transac-

To append transactions to the shared ledger, the Bitcoin network performs a collective run

any peer to use some scarce resource, here computation, to increase its probability of winning
the lottery [45]. Also, chaining the blocks ensures that manipulating a block requires as much
work as the network has done since the creation of that block, thereby providing ”computa-
tional” integrity.
The idea of incentivising participation through monetary rewards has been explored by
other work [39, 40]; however, the reward has previously been unrelated to the purpose of the
system, limiting the systems ability to stand by themselves. To the best of our knowledge,
Bitcoin is the ﬁrst network where the value of the reward is supplied by the participation of
peers, i.e., the currency has value because it is tamperproof and it is tamperproof because it
has value.

Hence, as noted by Fischer et al. ”real” consensus protocols have issues of non-termination
[46], i.e., availability, they may not be particularly useful for the case of monetary transac-
tions, where the availability of the system is of high importance. Because of this, the notion of
consensus used within Bitcoin is probabilistic. This means that there is a chance that history
is reverted; however, the nature of the blockchain ensures that the likelihood decreases expo-
nentially with the age of the block, i.e., by adding several blocks after a particular block, the
probability of reverting it becomes negligible.
3.3
Bitcoin is not private
In this section, we review literature that highlights signiﬁcant ﬂaws of the Bitcoin cryptocur-
rency system regarding the anonymity and privacy of the users and the transactions between
them. Following this section, it should be clear that the privacy provided by many cryptocur-
rencies, in particular, Bitcoin, is unsatisfying, and that the notion of privacy given by the media
and public is heavily skewed [23, 47–50].
We recall that by privacy in cryptocurrencies [4], we refer to the ability to perform private
transactions where a private transaction has two distinct properties: i) conﬁdentiality, i.e.,
hiding the amounts, and ii) anonymity, i.e., hiding the sender and receiver.
The section will be structured in three subsections, each related to an attack vector: Trans-
action Graph Analysis, P2P Network Analysis and other. Each subsection will introduce the context,

3.3.2.1.2
Zero-Knowledge proofs
Beyond the idea of mixing, the use of Zero-knowledge proofs has received increased interest
from the literature. In layman terms, a Zero-Knowledge proof is a proof that convinces a veriﬁer
of some statement, without revealing any other information than that the statement is true
[60]. Within cryptocurrencies, this is valuable for privacy, at it supports a user to prove that
he has suﬃcient funds for some transfer, without disclosing how much he transfer or which
output is used as a source.
Furthermore, these schemes often require a setup to establish sound creation of proofs.
As noted by the ZKProof community reference7, there exists a number of variations of these
setups. The most famous being the trustless and trusted setups. A trustless setup supports any-
body to verify if it was computed correctly, where a trusted setup requires the user to trust in at
least one of the setup-participants. The values used throughout the creation of a trusted setup
is sometimes denoted toxic waste as knowledge of the values could enable an attacker to break
soundness of the proofs and thereby generate fake proofs that are veriﬁed as valid8.
However, as most of these schemes have been implemented in distinct currencies due to
compatibility issues, only a few will be introduced here. We note that these works are not
implemented in Bitcoin but have been implemented as smart contracts in Ethereum.

posed by B¨unzetal. [26], could be used to support conﬁdential transaction between two ’known’

3.3.2.2.1
Monero
To enhance the anonymity of users, Monero [14] applies ring-signatures [64] and a set of possible
sources rather than conclusive sources and signatures as seen in Bitcoin. This means that there
is no explicit notation of who performed the transfer, only that it was one of the users in the
ring. Also, a stealth address is used as the receiving address, i.e., a one-time address related to
some private-key [23].
Because a decoy-source can be discarded if the source is known to be spent, the anonymity
is protected only if it is impossible to determine if the output is spent [29]. This leads to an
ever-growing set of possibly-spent outputs in extension to the expanding transaction ledger.
As the Monero scheme initially (pre-RingCT update) did not enforce conﬁdential transac-
tions, it could be diﬃcult for users to actually create a ring of transactions with the same value.
Meaning that rings often had a size of 1 (only the actual spender), making it trivially to follow
the ”private” transaction [65, 66]. As noted by [23], the use of a ring-signature provides limited
privacy, as the anonymity-set is often limited [66]. In updated versions, the transacted values
are hidden by conﬁdential transactions (CT), making it easier to create a useable ring as any CT
can be used [21]. However, as shown by Moeser et al. [66], the method of picking mixins9 poses
a signiﬁcant threat to the anonymity of the users, and the actual source can usually (90%) be
detected by using the age of sources only.
While the improvement of conﬁdential transactions and new mixing-choosing mechanism
massively increased the anonymity of Monero users [66], several attacks on the ring-signature
and Unencrypted Payment ID as proposed by Wijaya [67, 68] still pose a threat to the network.
In addition, a careless participant, could by mistake use more than one output from the same
transaction and thereby break Monero unlinkability10 [65].
3.3.2.2.2
Zerocoin
The Zerocoin project, proposed by Miers et al. [17], presents a scheme that allows a user, to
deposit a coin from the Bitcoin into a pool of ”Zerocoins”. There a Zero-knowledge protocol
utilises commitments to Bitcoins and serial numbers to perform a non-interactive proof and
withdraw funds from the pool. The proposal has signiﬁcant reservations with a trusted setup
and a proof size of 25 KB (basic Bitcoin transactions are approximately 250 bytes). While this
was improved to 10 KB by reducing soundness of proofs [69], the Zerocoin transaction size is
still substantial in comparison to a typical Bitcoin transaction.
3.3.2.2.3
Zerocash
In the wake of Zerocoin, a new scheme, named Zerocash, utilising a relatively new type of
cryptography, namely Zero-Knowledge Succinct Argument of Knowledge proofs (ZK-SNArKs), was
proposed. The scheme signiﬁcantly improves on some of the drawbacks of Zerocoin, by mas-
sively reducing the size of proofs from multiple KB to 288 Bytes [70, 71]. This is done by util-
ising a trusted setup to support succinct proof constructions as introduced by Groth [17, 71, 72].

In addition to the trusted setup, Zerocash requires the knowledge-of-exponent and Diﬃe-Hellman
assumptions in bilinear groups to hold [29, 70].
To explain the idea behind the shielded transactions in Zerocash, we use a simpliﬁed model.
Assume that Zerocash relies on two sets of coins, one denoting every coin ever existed, and one
denoting a commitment to spent coins. By utilising the ZK-SNArKs mentioned above, Zerocash
can prove that the sources of a new transaction are within the set of existing coins but have
not been spent before without disclosing the sources. In practice, this means that ZeroCash,
similarly to Monero, keep an ever-expanding set of ”coins” and ”spent coins” [29].
In practice, the application of Zerocash, Zcash, utilises multiple sets of coins, a transparent
set as in the Bitcoin system, and a shielded-pool where transactions employ ZK-SNArKs to prove
validity while perfectly hiding the speciﬁcs. To address some of the regulatory complications,
an addition to allow for the taxation of Zerocash anonymous transactions is proposed in [73].
In theory, the Zcash shielded transactions are fully anonymous with strong security guar-
antees [74]. However, the use of this shielded pool is minimal (1.3% of transactions in the last
month11) with users impeding anonymity through their leaking behaviour (deanonymization
of 69.1% of the shielded transactions) [74]. An example of such particular user behaviour is
noted by Kappos et al. [74]. Here users were depositing and withdrawing x coins from the
shielded pool within a short time, making them easily linkable by temporal analysis.
In addition, Zcash falls short of addressing the issues of Bitcoin transaction propagation (in-
troduced later), and large fractions of the network can be deanonymized (linking transactions
with IP-addresses with 50% precision and 82% recall) [75]. To address this, Kappos et al. [76]
propose the use of Mixnets to perform anonymous broadcast.
3.3.2.2.4
MimbleWimble
Another idea for a scheme was initially proposed at a Bitcoin IRC channel when a pseudony-
mous individual ”Tom Elvis Jedusor”12 uploaded the idea of MimbleWimble13. While Jedusor
proposed ideas, they were not suﬃciently detailed and contained ﬂaws. To address these, An-
drew Poelstra [22] reﬁned the paper and published a mathematically sound scheme following
the MimbleWimble idea. This reﬁned paper is what is referred to as MimbleWimble.
The MimbleWimble proposal is a scheme very diﬀerent from previously known schemes,
as it eliminates the idea of performing transactions between addresses. This is enforced by
utilising a binding and hiding commitment scheme [10], together with range proofs [26]. By
using the commitments directly as outputs in the scheme, the coins can be said to have their
own ”private key”, knowing this key and the value could enable one to spend the funds.

mitments (group elements) and iii) a kernel. For simplicity, let us assume that this kernel con-
sists of a signature, a public key (named excess) and range-proofs for the destinations. The
excess can be computed be everyone and is derived as the diﬀerence between the sum of the
source and the destination commitments. Due to the nature of the commitment scheme [10],
this excess will be a valid public key only if the diﬀerence between the source and destination

3.3.3
P2P Network Analysis
Moving beyond the transaction graphs, to the P2P network cryptocurrencies interact in, can
provide powerful deanonymization tools for an attacker. For the area of P2P network analysis,
the P2P network can be modelled as a directed graph G, where vertices are peers and edges
are the outgoing connections of the peers. It is on these edges that the messages will be trans-
ferred. The creator of a message is denoted as the source.
Findingthesourceofsomemessageinapeer-to-peernetworkisnotrestrictedonlytocryp-
tocurrencies, multiple analysis techniques are of interest. One of these, introduced by Pinto
et al. [13] showcases that techniques utilised for predicting the source of a disease outbreak
(cholera) can be modelled in a network graph. The speciﬁc type of the network (Apollonian,
Erd¨os-R´enyi or Barab´asi-Albert) greatly inﬂuences the required number of observer nodes neces-
sary in order to locate the true source with high probability (90%). Note that these observer-
nodes can be seen as honest-but-curious nodes in a network setting, i.e., a node that follows pro-
tocol but logs any messages and metadata that it sees. It is found [13] that an attacker capable
of placing observers in high-degree nodes signiﬁcantly reduces the necessary number of ob-
servers. Further, we should note that the authors assume knowledge of the network topology
in order to let the attack be eﬀective.
In 2014, Koshy et al. [81] showed that anonymity in the Bitcoin network is very limited, and
by applying observer-nodes within the network allowed them to eﬀectively link transactions
21
==PAGE:32==
directly to the Bitcoin full node from which they ”originated”, thereby linking addresses and
IP-addresses. The authors map 1162 addresses to distinct IP-address, by looking at the relayed
packets. Further, they ﬁnd that monitoring network packets allow them to ignore the use of
mixing services.
The work of Erdin et al. shows that it is possible to compromise the identity of users em-
ploying the extensively used anonymity networks Tor and I2P [20, 50]. The authors note that
while high-latency networks can provide better anonymity, the popularity of low-latency net-
works is providing a more practical anonymity improvement. As found in [20], an attacker
that can trick a user at the application layer to use Java applets or similar can bypass the proxy
settings. Moreover, they ﬁnd that servers accessed through such a network may employ tech-
niques that allow them to perform deanonymization. However, the implications of attacks
from non-interacting peers have limited eﬀect if not employing intersection attacks.
Biryukov [82] extends the work of Koshy and shows deanonymization of up to 60% of the
network if smaller DOS attacks are accepted. It is argued that an attacker with suﬃcient power
may partition the network and create a relativereality for parts of the network, thereby allowing
double-spends to occur [82]. Furthermore, Biryukov [83] highlights that Bitcoin users that look
towards Tor in the hope of enhancing privacy are in for a surprise: an attack sustaining a relative
reality of the participants using Tor on a network-scale level costs as low as 2500 USD a month.
Beyond the use of observer nodes [81, 82], Fanti et al. [24] note that an attacker with the
power of an ISP is able to deanonymize users by linking the source of transmission directly to a
speciﬁc IP-address [24]. Furthermore, the authors note that while Bitcoin core developers have
updated the mode of propagation from ﬂooding to diﬀusion, there is no clear evidence that this
has improved the resilience of the network to such attacks. In addition, Fanti [24] shows that
attackers who can reach a large number of concurrent corrupt connections to Bitcoin nodes
have a heightened probability of detecting the real source due to the diﬀusion mechanism.
As noted by Neudecker [84] and Pinto [13], it is important to hide the actual topology of the
network. Also, Neudecker discloses ﬂaws in the use of Simple Payment Veriﬁcation clients, as users
need to trust these to relay ”relevant” transactions correctly. In the case of Cryptonote inspired
currencies, the user must share a view-key. Beyond the disclosing of these ﬂaws, Neudecker
shows that there is little incentive for the participants in the network to forward transactions
and blocks, especially in a manner that hides the participants. The author notes that scheme
designers should consider the tradeoﬀ: increase DoS resistance and performance or improve
anonymity, topology hiding and cost of participation. While one could make it expensive to
make outgoing connections (as these where shown troublesome in [24]), this could make rela-
tive reality attacks easier to perform. A summary of these methods and their objectives is shown
in Table 3.4.

and ﬂuﬀ; a node in the stem phase relays stem-message to a single peer and otherwise broad-

For any node that receives a stem message from its peer, it is not clear whether the peer is the
source, or just relaying the message. However, as noted by the authors in their extension paper
[27], the protocol makes very optimistic assumptions about the other peers in the network; i)
every node knows the complete list of active IP-addresses, ii) every node runs Dandelion, iii) a
node creates exactly one transaction and iv) all nodes obey the protocol.

perform anonymous broadcasting; however, they ﬁnd the protocols to be ineﬃcient and brit-
tle. Brieﬂy, a Dining Cryptographers network [87] works by having a group of peers generate
pairwise keys to each other. Every peer will then calculate and publish the sum of their keys
modulo 2; the broadcaster inverts the summed key at every index i that is a 1-bit in his mes-
sage. Now sum all these summed keys modulo 2, if a message was transmitted, it will appear,
otherwise, a zero-string appears. It is immediately clear that such a protocol is vulnerable to
Denial of Service attacks, e.g., by multiple peers trying to publish at the same time.

In this section, we will introduce alternative attack approaches as well as their counter-
measures. Such approaches include methods that utilise ad-trackers and exchange API to
deanonymization.

3.3.5.1
Cookies
Goldfeder et al. [88] present an attack method, utilising the ad-trackers and cookies applied by
most web stores. They show that some trackers directly link users utilising Bitcoin as a means
of payment, i.e., their personally identiﬁable information (PII), and the speciﬁc transaction
on-chain. The authors ﬁnd that some of these trackers directly stores both a unique identiﬁer,
i.e., e-mail or name of users, together with the actual transaction-id, making linking trivial.
It is noted that diﬀerent websites using the same tracker, and thereby cookie, can be used to
link individual logs or purchases together and identify both the user and her transactions, even
when she interact in multiple coinjoins. This means that multiple purchases at such stores can
render mixing services useless as the oﬀ-chain information contained by the ad-trackers may
uniquely link transactions together (even without them leaking PII) [88].
While mitigation of this attack is possible by blocking web-tracking in general through ad-
blockers, the authors note that the possibilities available to the average user are quite limited
as these tools are seldom perfect.
3.3.5.2
Cross-chain exchange through services
With the realisation that Bitcoin provides minimal privacy, a user that wishes to hide is in-
clined to exchange her coins with more privacy-preserving ones. However, as found in the
work of Yousaf et al. [89] exchanging from one cryptocurrency to another through a service is
not as ”safe” as one would initially believe. The authors note that criminals trying to extract
cryptocurrencies to Fiat-currencies will likely get caught by the exchange, as most perform
KYC for all users. However, as the authors specify, one could try to utilise a non-KYC service,
as Shapeshift before end-2018. As the paper shows, up to 90.54% of exchanges may leak data,
which enables linking the user’s transaction from one ledger to another [89]. To mitigate this
leakage, the authors in [89] note that the shielded pool of Zcash can be used directly into the
Shapeshifter API, however, they add that the feature appears to be discontinued as of writing.
Also, the implementation of KYC means that the user must prove her identity to the service,
restricting her privacy.
3.4
Discussion
Throughout this literature review, we have presented a myriad of attacks on privacy in cryp-
tocurrencies (Table 3.6). While these attacks showcase the vulnerabilities in cryptocurren-
cies, they also showcase that utilising existing anonymity techniques, as Tor & I2P, does not
guarantee anonymity. This could be partly due to the linked nature of the cryptocurrency
transactions, i.e., coins have to exist before you can use them and you often link them directly
as sources, whereas messages can be unrelated. To improve the privacy of cryptocurrencies,
particularly Bitcoin, several protocols have been proposed (Table 3.7). These proposals vary
from new cryptographic schemes utilising zero-knowledge proofs and shielded addresses to
network propagation mechanisms striving to hide the IP-address of the source. However, it is
clear from Table 3.7 that most eﬀort has been put into the area of cryptographic protocols.

3.4.1
Possible research directions
3.4.1.1
Transactions propagation mechanisms
As the propagation of transactions poses a threat to both decoy-based and zero-knowledge
systems, we believe that it is vital to research this area further.
As the type of information that is retrievable from the diﬀerent systems varies greatly, we
believe a distinction can be made for the further research of these systems.
In particular, we believe that the development of non-interactive anonymous broadcast-
ing can improve the network privacy of the zero-knowledge systems. However, anonymous
broadcasting does not apply directly to decoy-based systems as they still allow an observer to
construct the transaction graph. For these systems to be suﬃciently protected, we believe the
area of non-interactive transaction-aggregation throughout the propagation mechanism has
to be explored. While proposals exists18, we ﬁnd proof of improvement lacking.
3.4.1.2
ZK-SNArKs without a trusted setup
As noted by Ben Fisch in his presentation at Scaling Bitcoin 201919, there exists a number of works
looking into the area of succinct zero-knowledge proof systems without a trusted setup. How-
ever, in their current state, these proofs are rather large [91] (multiple if not hundreds of KB),
and verifying them is linear or polylogarithmic in the size of the circuit. In comparison, a
trusted setup SNArK from the libSNARK implementation is constant in size (0.13KB) and veri-
ﬁcation time.
Research leading to reductions in proof size and veriﬁcation time of these families would
considerably improve the privacy of deployed solutions while mitigating the risks that follow
the trusted setup.

& Lelantus.
However, these protocols seldom guarantee as strong anonymity, measured in anonymity-
set size, as their ZK-SNArK counterpart, due to performance limitations [4]. We believe the

4.2
Zero-Knowledge Proofs
We note that a brief description of Zero-Knowledge proofs has already been provided in
chapter 3 [Literature Review], we will therefore brieﬂy provide the formal properties
that constitute a zero-knowledge proof here. Recall that a zero-knowledge proof informally
can be said to support a Prover in convincing a Veriﬁer that some relation R between a witness
w and a commitment X, without disclosing the witness w [60]. A well-known example is the
range proof [26], which is introduced shortly. For a proof to be zero-knowledge, it must satisfy
the following properties2 [90]:
• Completeness: If the Prover knows the witness w, for some statement X, the Veriﬁer must
be convinced with probability 1. I.e., a valid proof will never be rejected by an honest
Veriﬁer.
• Soundness: If (w, X) /∈R, a Veriﬁer that follows the protocol honestly will only be
convinced otherwise with negligible probability.
• Zero-Knowledge: If (w, X) ∈R, and the Prover follows the protocol, the Veriﬁer will
learn nothing more than the single bit that (w, X) ∈R.
For the remainder of this section, we present an overview of interactive and non-interactive
protocols and brieﬂy introduce the range proof, which is vastly utilised within cryptocurren-
cies with conﬁdential amounts like MimbleWimble.
Interactive and Non-Interactive proofs
A Σ-protocol, is a 3-move protocol [90] that
supports zero-knowledge proofs as speciﬁed above. These 3 moves are i) an initial message
with commitments by the Prover, ii) a challenge from the Veriﬁer and then iii) the Prover re-
spond, based on the initial message and challenge. The Veriﬁer can after this interaction ac-
cept or reject the ”proof” as convincing. Because the Prover does not know what challenge
the Veriﬁer will pick, and have already committed to the values within his proof, he will only
be able to construct a valid proof if he indeed knows the witness w, of by negligible change.
Nevertheless, the interactive protocol is not ﬁt for blockchain setting, hence the Prover would
need to interact with every miner to convince them. Therefore, the proof need to be made non-
interactive, meaning that a proof can be created without the need for the Veriﬁer. Luckily for
us, the Σ-protocol, can be modiﬁed to a non-interactive protocol, by utilising the Fiat-Shamir
heuristic [93], i.e., using a cryptographic hash function H in the random oracle model [92], to
compute the challenge.

Lelantus
In this section, we will introduce Lelantus [4], a scheme that utilises One-Out-Of-Many proofs,
to prove knowledge of sources without disclosing the openings nor the speciﬁc source that is
used. This is great for anonymity as it removes the transaction graph, similarly to Zcash. This
section will ﬁrst, provide an intuition of the system is a whole i.e., how One-Out-Of-Many proofs
are used, before looking at the intuition of the proofs and ﬁnally the proof system itself.
The system builds on a two-coin basis, i.e., a base-coin and a shielded pool, similarly to
Zerocoin and Zcash. For intuition, assume that we have a fully public base currency where each
coin is owned by some address and that there exists some pool of shielded checks, a shielded
check is a commitment com(s, v, r) hiding the value and a serial key of said check. To make
36
==PAGE:47==
transfers anonymous, we can mint a fresh check by burning owned base currency. With the
One-Out-Of-Many proofs, we can prove that we own a check with the serial number s, and spend
the value hidden by publishing s without directly pointing at the check as a source, but merely
proving that the check is within a large set of checks. Linking a source check to a destination
is then infeasible when we pick anonymity-sets that are large enough, Lelantus propose 216 as
a performant size6. By storing the used serial numbers at the Blockchain, we can reject any
transfer trying to spend a previously spent check, i.e., protecting against double-spending.
The exact One-Out-Of-Many proof that is used in Lelantus is inspired by the proof of Groth [90],
but have some diﬀerences. For one, the Lelantus One-Out-Of-Many proves that a zero-opening of
a double-blinded commitment, i.e., com(0, v, r), is known by the Prover, instead of an ordinary
zero-commitment com(0, r). This allows Lelantus to satisfy anonymity, without requiring the
anonymity-set to be of checks with equal denominator [4]. Before this double-blinding, there
had to exist multiple pools of varying denominators within the system, and the One-Out-Of-Many
proof simply proved that you were eligible to use one of the coins in the set. Without the sets
of the equal denominator, it would be trivial to track unique amounts that used the Groth proof.
The performance of Lelantus is great for anonymity-solutions without a trusted setup. As
seen from Table 2 of the Lelantus paper [4], the protocol supports proofs with an anonymity-set
size of 216 that requires only 1567 bytes, generates in 5253 ms and is veriﬁed in 947. While this
veriﬁcation may seem slow, it is for a sole proof; if multiple proofs are to be veriﬁed, these can
be computed in batches, using only 35.6 ms per proof if batching 1000 proofs.

5.2
Extension to aggregating Dandelion
Based on the Dandelion variations described in section 4.5 [Dandelion], we propose an ex-
tension that incentivises early aggregation of transactions.
Similar to the idea of dummy outputs of Beam, we propose to add a privacy fee output that
hides some fee, i.e., com(fee, r). When the sender is passing the transaction to a peer, it also
passes the opening, i.e., fee and r. This allows for the peer to ”take” the fee by using it as a
source in his own transaction, and aggregate the two transactions before passing it on further.
As an example, let us assume that some user A wish to make a transfer to the user B, he
will then generate the transaction (a) →(b, c, fee), where a is an output controlled by A, b is the
destination for b, c is the change for A, and the fee is for the ﬁrst peer in propagation. Assume
that C is the ﬁrst peer that A passes this message to. C will then generate a transaction, (fee, d) →
(e), where d is an output controlled by C, and e is a new output controlled by C. The peer C will
then aggregate the transactions and perform cut-through, letting the aggregated transaction
be (a, d) →(b, c, e). For any peer receiving the aggregated transaction, it will see this larger
transaction with multiple kernels, indistinguishable from a ”normal” aggregate transaction. If
not the fee is taken by the peer, the sender can use the output as a source in a later transaction.
Our hypothesis is that this fee incentivises peers in generating a transaction, ensuring that
aggregation is possible, even when the peer has not received multiple messages within the
interval. As the idea intuitively allows aggregation at any phase, we believe that the method
can easily be implemented in both Grin and Beam.
The proposal has some obvious drawbacks, hence the nature of it is decoy-based, and most
likely just one decoy, it is thought to improve only slightly if seen by a peer that is close by.
However, for the case of stem-aggregation, we believe it would make transactions grow rapidly
in size due to the larger initial size. Nevertheless, the extension demands that the user pays an
additional fee, hence no peer will take the value if it is 0. The extension is, however, very simple
to implement, and could be implemented without any hard-forks3, as it is solely a change to
how the MimbleWimble scheme is used, not the protocols themselves. If only part of the network
accepts the extension, some transaction will simply be relayed and aggregated as normal, while
others, from one accepter to another, would be combined to retrieve the fee.

Chapter 6
Validation
In this chapter, we investigate the validity of the two proposals of this thesis: i) an extension
to the message propagation mechanism to improve anonymity, and ii) a Zero-Knowledge trans-
action scheme that supports conﬁdential outputs to be spent anonymously.
6.1
Evaluation of the anon-output proposal
As noted from chapter 3 [Literature Review], we know that message propagation is a
problem towards true anonymity in Decoy-based systems. We believe that the best way to evalu-
ate if a propagation mechanism is improving anonymity is to evaluate it against other proposals,
in this case, through simulation.
The deployed systems, Grin and Beam, do currently implement variations of the Dandelion
scheme [27, 86], which they believe will improve anonymity. As described in section 4.5
[Dandelion], they propose to do aggregation of transactions during the ﬂuﬀand stem phases.
However, at the time of writing, we have been unable to ﬁnd a comparison of the systems and
we will, therefore, perform simulations, to answer three questions:
• What scheme minimize the number of conclusive transactions
• What anonymity-set sizes are transactions hidden within
• What inﬂuence will extending the scheme with our proposal have.
6.1.1
Simulation outline
The simulations are based on a multi-threaded Dandelion Java-implementation1 emulating a
network of nodes. Each node has a message-queue of incoming transactions, to which any con-
nected node can append messages. Our simulation model distinguishes between received and
delivered messages. A message can be received by a node, i.e., appended to the message-queue,
without being delivered, if the node has not yet dequeued the message. A message is delivered
when the node dequeues it from the message-queue. Further, a node is capable of establishing
up to 8 outgoing connections, similarly to the Bitcoin implementation [82]. We assume that
1https://github.com/LHerskind/MasterNetworkSimulation
52
==PAGE:63==
these connections are reliable, i.e., if a message is sent, we guarantee that the other party will
receive it. However, we do not guarantee when the message will be delivered to the system;
also, a node cannot fail during the simulation.
The nodes will be within epochs of 10 seconds, where an epoch is either stem or ﬂuﬀ. When-
ever an epoch has passed, the node draws a random, and with 90% probability2, it will be in stem
for the next epoch. The nodes will perform actions in discrete steps of 10 milliseconds, where
an action can be to generate and transfer a new transaction or receive a transaction from the
message queue. The simulation does not enforce dummy UTXO, as described by Beam.
The simulation will be performed on three transaction densities, with every node expected
to make a transaction every 1, 10 or 60 minutes. Further, we investigate the inﬂuence of having
well-connected observers, in comparison to randomly assigned or poorly connected observers.
We order the nodes on the ”connectedness” by the number of their incoming connections, i.e.,
many incoming connections mean that the node is well-connected. Note that we can derive the
anonymity-set sizes that transactions are hidden within by counting the number of ground-
truth transactions within each set-size of the observer knowledge. The simulation will be run
in a network of 512 nodes, that exist for 10 minutes. We will perform one run for the protocols
that are used in Grin (aggregation in ﬂuﬀ)and Beam(aggregation in stem), as well as Grin and
Beam with our extension.
6.1.2
Deriving conclusive transactions
From the set of observers, two sets of knowledge will be derived. One set of conclusive transac-
tions, i.e., transactions that have only one source, and one set of inconclusive transactions, i.e.,
transactions with multiple sources. Based on these sets, the group of observers can reduce the
set of inconclusive transactions by applying set operations at a transaction level.
Reduction with conclusive transactions
Given that observers obviously know of the
transaction they create themselves or receive directly from the source, they can reduce the
inconclusive set through this knowledge. Assume that the observers know two transactions (A)
→(B) and (C) →(D). When it received a message (A, C, G) →(B, D, H), they can reduce by remov-
ing the two transactions (A) →(B) and (C) →(D), leaving (G) →(H) as a conclusive transaction.
The knowledge of the observers has been extended by (G) →(H), which may allow further re-
duction, similarly to cascading in Monero [65, 67].
Reduction with inconclusive transactions
Given a set of multiple inconclusive trans-
actions, it may be possible to derive additional conclusive knowledge by disjoining the trans-
actions. Say that the observers know two inconclusive transactions: (A, C) →(B, D) and (A, C, E)
→(B, D, F). By disjoining the transactions, we derive the conclusive transaction (E) →(F). Sim-
ilarly, the observers can intersect transactions to ﬁnd a conclusive transaction. Assume that
the observers know two inconclusive transactions: (A, C, E) →(B, D, F) and (A, G, I) →(B, H, J).
The intersection will then derive the conclusive transaction (A) →(B). Note that while it may
not be possible to derive a conclusive directly, the observers may be able to reduce the size of

Our proposal
While our extension hides transactions better than the deployed solutions, it is now clear that
the Beam protocol provides the best basis; and that our proposal complements its structure
well. Note that our extension, as seen in Figure 6.3, allow an attacker to learn conclusively
only about a transaction that they either create themselves or receive directly from the trans-
mitter. Also, the attacker gains only little beneﬁt by controlling well-connected nodes. How-
ever, as noted, the size of the anonymity set is small, and may not provide signiﬁcant protec-

6.2
Zero-Knowledge Scheme
In this section, we assess the proposed Zero-Knowledge scheme threefold. First, we evaluate how
well the design ﬁts in with the ethos of the MimbleWimble proposal. Secondly, we compare the
anonymity properties of our scheme with well-known currencies, as evaluated in chapter 3
[Literature Review] and seen from Table 3.8. Finally, we compare our proposal with the
Lelantus MimbleWimble proposal by Beam4.
As described in chapter 5 [Design], the modiﬁcations to the Lelantus protocol, allowed
us to support conﬁdential outputs as a source for minting shielded coins which can be used for
anonymousspending. OurschemelocksintoMimbleWimbleandallowstransaction-aggregation
as for ”normal” transactions, however, with the limitation that it is not possible to perform
cut-through on outputs that are either a source or a destination in an anonymous transaction.
While the lack of cut-through does not impact the soundness of the MimbleWimble system, it does
impact the ability to aggressively prune the system. Without the ability to massively prune,
our new scheme does not follow the ethos of MimbleWimble and may not be adopted by the
community as a whole.
Because our scheme does not support direct joinsplits, we have no use of the extended
bulletproof rangeproof as needed for Lelantus [4]. Recall that our joinsplit is enforced though a
spending, followed by individual minting, requiring only rangeproofs on simple commitments
com(v, r). Furthermore, as destinations from an Oscausi spending are inherently grouped,
their rangeproofs can be batched as well.
As seen from chapter 3 [Literature Review], Bitcoin provided no anonymity beyond
simply pseudonyms, and could very easily be broken and analysed by an attacker. As noted, the
main issue is that the transaction graph is fully public and thereby support analysis by anyone.
Further, the system put an extra linking between transaction, by allowing users to utilise the
same addresses multiple times. Our proposal removes the notion of addresses entirely, obfus-
cate the transaction graph and support zero-knowledge transactions that leak no information
to the transaction graph. However, the transactions require more storage and computation,
and may not be as accessible as Bitcoin due to the interactive nature of the scheme.
Furthermore, our proposal provide signiﬁcantly larger anonymity-set sizes than the Mon-
ero protocol (216 instead of 11), with similar proof generation and faster veriﬁcation time.
While being a signiﬁcant improvement for anonymity over Monero, our solution cannot
hold its own against the king of the hill Zcash, which supports an anonymity set of size 232,
with unbeatable size and veriﬁcation time. However, our solution does not rely on the trusted
setup nor ”complicated and un-auditable” cryptography, enabling it to be broader and more
easily adopted than ZK-SNArKs until a transparent setup is as more performant.

Chapter 7
Conclusion & Future Work
Throughout this work, we have presented three contributions. First, we display a systematic
literature review on private e-cash systems, in particular, the decentralised cryptocurrencies.
In the review, we introduce privacy-improving techniques and outlines how ﬂaws and struc-
ture can be exploited to limit the privacy of the users. Based on the literature, we propose
three research directions that we believe to be of general interest to the community: transaction
propagation mechanisms, ZK-SNArKs without a trusted setup, and specialised trustless zero-knowledge
proofs. Of these directions, we investigate two, which lead to our second and third contribution.
We present a modiﬁcation to the Dandelion aggregation that improves privacy and can be
introduced into existing blockchains without major changes. Through simulation, we ﬁnd
that rebuilding the transaction graph in current systems, is easier than expected and that our
proposal only improves this slightly. We, therefore, ﬁnd our modiﬁcation too costly for the
privacy-gain.
Lastly, we present a trustless second-layer zero-knowledge scheme, that supports good
anonymity through signiﬁcant anonymity-sets (216), while still being practical in a Blockchain
setting, due to batch-veriﬁcation and small memory footprint. Because our scheme can ex-
ist on top of currencies supporting conﬁdential transactions through homomorphic commit-
ment schemes, it is not conﬁned to MimbleWimble, but can be used on top of Monero or other
Cryptonote protocols with minor changes.
7.1
Future Work
For future endeavours within the space, we ﬁnd multiple subjects of interest. First, we be-
lieve that exploring batching of the proof variables may be useful, as multiple One-Out-Of-Many
proofs will often occur within the same spending, and combining variables across proofs may
lead to both performance and storage gains. Secondly, we believe that it is of great interest
for the MimbleWimble community, to investigate the possibility of performing cut-through on
shielded outputs in such a manner that it will not impact anonymity. Lastly, we believe the pos-
sibility of using the BLS signature scheme [78] should be explored more deeply, as the scheme
could support aggregation of signatures for the MimbleWimble transactions, as well as the sig-
natures proving ownership of the shielded sources in Oscausi.
