Abstract
Reducing the emission of greenhouse gases, like CO2, is of great interest, especially in the trans-
portation sector. Ammonia is considered a substitute fuel in the maritime industry. Ammonia as
fuel has challenges: it is hard to ignite, and other pollutants must be considered. Nitrous oxide
may be formed from ammonia combustion and is a potent greenhouse gas, much worse than
CO2. Therefore, it is of great interest to investigate the chemical kinetics of nitrous oxide under
conditions present in an engine.
This thesis investigates fuel mixtures of N2O/H2 because they may be found during ammonia
combustion and because it is the simplest fuel/nitrous oxide reaction system. This allows,
investigation of the interaction of N2O with the radical pool. Experiments are carried out in a
plug ﬂow reactor at intermediate temperatures (723 - 923 K) and a pressure of 100 bar. Data is
compared with simulations in Chemkin, using a mechanism developed by Glarborg et al. with
and without a subset, including HNNO species. Literature has previously suggested that an
HNNO intermediate may be stable at high-pressure conditions, and this is examined.
Based on the tests and simulations, it is strongly indicated that an HNNO species is formed at
these conditions as c-HNNO and t-HNNO. The mechanism, including the new species, can more
accurately predict the system for all tests conducted in this thesis. Including the HNNO species
causes less reactivity in the system because H radicals are trapped in less reactive species such
as HNNO. The HNNO species also adds an extra pathway for NH3 production, where reactive H
radicals are used in the formation of NH3, making the mechanism capable of predicting NH3 in
the system better than previously. Simulation of literature data showed that the formation and
importance of the new species highly depends on the experimental conditions.

Background: 
The use of carbon-free fuels is of great interest in the fight against greenhouse gas emission. One of the most 
effective ways is to substitute fossil fuels in vehicles for greener fuels. Challenges of using ammonia as a 
carbon-free fuel in engines and gas turbines are investigated extensively, and is especially relevant to the 
maritime industry. Concerns include poor ignition and combustion properties, as well as emissions of oxides 
of nitrogen. A particular concern is the formation of the strong greenhouse gas nitrous oxide (N2O). Levels of 
just a few hundred ppm of N2O is sufficient to offset the benefit of using a carbon-free fuel with respect to the 
greenhouse effect. To aid in the design of engines and operating conditions that minimize emissions of nitrous 
oxide, it is important to establish kinetic models that describe consumption of N2O accurately. The simplest 
fuel/nitrous oxide reaction system is that of N2O and H2. Investigation of this system avoids the complexity of 
more complex fuels and facilitates analysis of the interaction of nitrous oxide with the radical pool. While this 
reaction system has been studied under a wide range of temperatures only a few experiments have been 
conducted in the high pressure region. There is a need to characterize experimentally the reaction for pressures 
characteristic for maritime diesel, i.e., 100 bar, and develop models for the chemistry at high pressure.  
 
Project Objectives: 
The objective of this project includes establishing an overview of the current literature on the topic. 
Experiments on N2O/H2 mixtures, with and without O2, in a flow reactor setup that allows pressures of up to 
100 bar and temperatures of 500-925 K will be carried out. Experiments will be conducted as a function of 
temperature, reactant mixture, and stoichiometry at high pressure. The results will be interpreted in terms of a 
detailed chemical kinetic model, based on earlier work at DTU Chemical Engineering.

Introduction

Introduction
The desire to decrease the emission of CO2 is more signiﬁcant than ever. The transportation
industry is a big contributor to CO2 emission [1]. Thus, the interest in using fuels other than
fossil fuels is constantly increasing. Lately, ammonia has been in the spotlight as a potential
chemical to use as an energy carrier in, i.e., the shipping industry [1] [2]. An advantage of
implementing ammonia is that it is a well-known molecule, as it has been used as a fertilizer for a
century. This means production facilities and knowledge of handling and distribution are already
in place. Ammonia does, however, pose several challenges: The production has to be carbon-free,
it has to be economically viable, and it also has to be a reliable fuel [3]. Ammonia has a low
ﬂame speed, which makes for slower combustion and may be more ineﬃcient [4]. Ammonia has a
high auto-ignition temperature and a high heat of vaporization, which will cool the cylinder and
make compression ignition challenging [4].
Another critical factor in combustion is that harmful species’ pollution must be kept at a mini-
mum. A point of interest in ammonia combustion is that nitrous oxide (N2O) can be formed.
Nitrous oxide is a potent greenhouse gas and may easily oﬀset the potential gain of using a
carbon-free fuel [5].
In this thesis, the N2O/H2 system is investigated, which is the simplest N2O system that may
be present in an ammonia-driven engine. From this system, N2O’s reaction with the radical pool
can be examined. The purpose is to understand the chemical mechanism at high pressure and
medium temperatures. These conditions have not previously been experimentally studied in the
literature. The conditions can be present in an engine, and it is relevant to understand how N2O
reacts at these conditions, in the case it may be formed in large quantities.
To examine the N2O/H2 system, experiments in a ﬂow reactor are carried out at diﬀerent
equivalence ratios, at 100 bar, and temperatures between 725 K and 925 K. The N2O, H2, NH3,
and H2O species are measured.
The experimental data is analyzed and modeled with the software Chemkin. Two mechanisms
are utilized: a mechanism developed by Glarborg et al. (2023) [6] and the same mechanism,
with the addition of a subset including the HNNO specie by Burke et al. (2023) [7]. The HNNO
subset may be substantial in describing a nitrous oxide system at high pressure and medium
temperatures. With the additional HNNO subset, it will be possible to investigate if there is an
indication of HNNO being formed at high pressure and medium temperatures.

2.1
Ammonia as an energy carrier
Ammonia is a colorless gas with the molecular formula NH3. It can be seen as a competitor
to hydrogen in terms of being used as a green energy carrier for fuel and storage. Ammonia
can be produced renewably through the decarbonized Haber-Bosch process, which makes use of
hydrogen from the electrolysis of water [2][1]. Due to the strong hydrogen bonds, ammonia is
easily liquiﬁed. This makes it suitable for handling, as it can be stored and transported at far
smaller pressures than hydrogen (≥0.8 MPa against 17≥69 MPa). For a hydrogen-powered
vehicle to have a similar range, when compared to a vehicle using gasoline, a pressure of 700 atm
must be used. Using ammonia, room temperature may be used for a pressure of around 10 atm.
Furthermore, ammonia has a volumetric energy density of up to three times that of hydrogen
[2][1].
Due to the extensive use of ammonia in other industries, such as for fertilizer, infrastructure is
already in place, meaning it would be easier to implement ammonia as a fuel or for chemical
storage when compared to hydrogen [2].
Ammonia also has a strong smell, making it easy to notice leaks. It is of a lower reactivity (and
energy content) than hydrogen, thus making it safer regarding explosion safety and accidental
ﬁres. This low reactivity is, however, also a reason why combustion of ammonia is a challenge,
as it is challenging to ignite [2].
Other challenges when using ammonia are the emission of NOx (nitrogen oxide)-gasses, which are
NO and NO2 as well as N2O. NO, and NO2 are health hazards and contribute to the formation
of acid rain, while N2O is a very potent greenhouse gas [8][5].
2.1.1
Green ammonia
Ammonia may not release carbon by combustion, but during production, the Haber-Bosch process
contributes 1.4% of the world CO2 [2] emission because production facilities have not transferred
to the decarbonized process. The Haber-Bosch process is a century-old industrial process making
use of high temperature, high pressure, and catalysts to produce ammonia from H2 and N2,
through the reaction N2 + 3 H2 →2 NH3. The hydrogen has mostly been produced by steam
reforming from natural gas, and combining this with only fossil fuels during the Haber-Bosch
process produces brown ammonia [2]. Blue ammonia is produced using fossil fuels but utilizes
carbon capture and storage technology. Lastly, green ammonia is produced using sustainable
energy, water, and air, using the Haber-Bosch process [2]. An example of a carbon-free ammonia

Ammonia is readily available from raw materials (water and air) and power (electricity) and thus

chemicals does not have the hurdle of needing a geologically suited place, as hydro or compressed
air storage does [3].
Ammonia is also more convenient as a green power storage chemical when compared to hydrogen
due to the need for high pressure for hydrogen storage, which is not the case for ammonia [3].

2.1.2
Ammonia as fuel
The energy of ammonia can be recovered in diﬀerent ways, for example, by the use of fuel cells.
However, this work will explore the energy release from ammonia through combustion.
Any combustion process depends highly on pressure, temperature, and fuel mixture. At diﬀerent
conditions, the chemical mechanisms in the combustion can change rapidly due to diﬀerent
reaction pathways being more favorable (kinetically and thermodynamically) [8]. Combustion
chemistry is radical chemistry; for example, the radical pool will change considerably with the
fuel mixture. Diﬀerent radicals are formed at diﬀerent temperatures and pressures, and some
are found to have longer lifetimes than others (for example, OH2 and OH), quickly making the
reaction pathways complex [8].
Ammonia has been interesting for combustion processes for decades since fossil fuels contain
nitrogen compounds. These nitrogen compounds will produce ammonia as an intermediate,
which can be converted to NO [8].
If ammonia is present at the proper conditions, it will act as a reducing agent and, through
a selective non-catalytic reaction with NO, produce N2, making it possible to eliminate NO
(thermal deNOx) [9][10]. Thus, the oxidation of ammonia is of great importance, and the possible
pathways are shown in Figure 2.2 [11].
Figure 2.2: An overview of reaction paths for the oxidation of ammonia [11].
The pathways of combustion chemistry often have several product channels; thus, the same two
species reacting may form diﬀerent products depending on the channel. For example, in the
ﬁgure, two product channels exist for the reaction of NH2 with NO [1]. However, one product
channel may be preferred depending on what is of interest. One of the product channels is a
chain terminating reaction, producing no radicals (NH2 + NO →N2 + H2O) [1], while one
Page 4 of 104

is chain branching (producing the NNH and OH radicals). The NNH pathway promotes the
production of OH and O through the reactions (NNH →N2 + H), (H + O2 →O + OH) and
(O + H2O →OH + OH) [1]. This is the exact reaction utilized in the thermal deNOx reaction
[9][10]. In Figure 2.2, a possible pathway is also that of (NH2 →HNO →NO), which would
be promoted if too much O is present in the radical pool, which is precisely what happens at
high temperatures [9][10][1]. Conversely, the chain-terminating reaction consuming O-radicals
will dominate at low temperatures, inhibiting DeNOx. Thus, it is essential to ﬁnd the optimal
conditions for the desired reactions to take place [1].
Looking at Figure 2.2 it is seen that N2 can also be produced from NH3 by the pathway NH3 →
NH2 →NH →N2O →N2, which also consumes NO [1]. As mentioned, N2O is a greenhouse
gas. Thus, it is of great importance to minimize this pathway, or at least make all N2O react
and form N2 [1][11].
An overview of the pathway during ammonia combustion has now been presented; in the following,
the working principles of a combustion engine will be explained.
2.1.3
Combustion engines
This section investigates how to run a diesel engine with ammonia since it is the engine used for
ships, and thus where ammonia as a fuel is most relevant [12]. A diesel engine runs periodically
by injecting fuel, then igniting, and repeating the cycle [12].
Two phenomena regarding the mixedness of the fuel and oxidizer are related to a ﬂame: premixed
or non-premixed. In a premixed ﬂame, the reactants are well mixed before the combustion. In a
non-premixed ﬂame, the reactants are initially separated, and the reaction occurs at the interface
between the fuel and the oxidizer. At the interface, both mixing and reaction take place. It is
generally accepted that both phenomena occur inside a diesel engine, but the ﬂame is mostly
non-premixed [8].
Figure 2.3 shows a periodic combustion engine (diesel engine) [12].

The general cycle of the engine consists of the following steps [12]:
• Air-fuel mixture being let into the engine
• Compression of air-fuel mixture
• Combustion of the mixture
• Piston being pushed, resulting in work
• Combustion products being exhausted
For the engine to run eﬀectively, where the work is optimally extracted from the combustion, all
of the above has to be done optimally. Thus, a combustion engine quickly becomes complex. In
a diesel engine, the compression must cause the ignition. Ammonia is notoriously hard to ignite.
Thus, an optimal mixture of fuel and oxidizer is necessary for the engine to run optimally [12].
The engine’s entire combustion process must be investigated chemically and physically. Chemical
kinetics are helpful in terms of examining the exhaust products and producing the maximum
amount of energy from the process. At the same time, physically, it is essential that the engine
can handle the propagation of the ﬂame [12]. Maximizing ﬂame speed may also be vital since it
reduces the time the ﬂame travels in the cylinder [12]. By running the combustion process at
optimal conditions, the formation of extreme greenhouse gases may be avoided and replaced by
less potent greenhouse gases. An example is converting N2O to NO [12].

2.2
Nitrous oxide
Nitrous oxide is one of the NOx gasses formed during ammonia combustion and is a very potent
greenhouse gas. Therefore, knowing the reaction pathways to properly control the engine’s
nitrous oxide emission is crucial [14] [15]. In this section, nitrous oxide chemistry at combustion
conditions will be introduced.
2.2.1
Formation of nitrous oxide
In combustion engines, when using ammonia as fuel, two reactions are mainly responsible for the
formation of N2O [14]; one is shown in Figure 2.2:
NH + NO ≠≠æ N2O + H
(R 2)
NH2 + NO2 ≠≠æ N2O + H2O
(R 3)
Reaction (R 2) is dominant at high-temperature conditions and lower O2 concentrations, while
reaction (R 3) is dominating in the lower temperature environment and for larger O2 concentrations
[11]. As diesel engines operate at medium to high temperature and high pressure, Reaction (R 2)
is important during the initial state of the combustion [14]. During the combustion stage, most
of the formation will be due to reaction (R 2), which occurs at conditions favoring this reaction.
But at these conditions, N2O will be consumed rapidly because it thermally decomposes at
high temperatures. In the later stage of the cycle, reaction (R 3) becomes dominant. At these
conditions, overall higher production of N2O is expected than in the earlier stage of the cycle
because the decomposition reaction is not taking place as much as at the higher temperature
and pressure conditions [14]. Therefore, understanding the reaction pathways of N2O is crucial
to reducing emissions.

The forward reaction of nitrous oxide and atomic hydrogen producing (NH + OH) is poorly

N2O + H ( + M) ≠≠≠æ HNNO ( + M) hj (R7)
A third product channel of N2O + H has recently been proposed, most prominent at high
pressure and lower temperatures (<1300 K) [7]. The formation of HNNO species is pressure
dependent and stabilizes at higher pressures [20]. HNNO can be formed either as cis-HNNO,
trans-HNNO, or ONHN. The main species are cis-HNNO and trans-HNNO. At 1000 K, 70% of
the HNNO stabilizes as trans-HNNO, and the rest as cis-HNNO except 0.1%, which is stabilized
as ONHN [7]. Jet-stirred reactor experiments by Burke et al. have been done for a complex
H2, O2, N2O, NO, N2, Ar system to study the HNNO pathways [20]. NO, and NH3 formation was
observed at lower temperatures, which is not predicted by previous models, not including the
HNNO sub-model. These results indicate the importance of including HNNO species in modeling.
The investigation of HNNO stabilization is limited in literature [20] [7]. The rate constants for
the formation and decomposition of HNNO species are calculated by Burke et al. and will be
used as the rates in this study.

2.3
Experimental methods for studying combustion chemistry
Various experimental methods are used to study diﬀerent phenomena during combustion in
engines. This section presents the diﬀerent experimental methods and their purpose in studying
combustion chemistry.
2.3.1
Batch reactor
A batch reactor is a closed system with a ﬁxed amount of reactant in each experiment. In some
setups, the ignition of the combustion is initialized via an electric spark [27]. Having setups
carried out without an ignition point should be preferred as it is better to have clear boundary
conditions [28]. Batch reactors are used both to determine kinetics and explosion characteristics.
2.3.2
Flow reactor
In a ﬂow reactor, the aim is to obtain plug ﬂow, which can be used to study the kinetics of a gas
mixture. Even though plug ﬂow is not the dominant ﬂow pattern in engines, it makes for more
straightforward modeling and can help scientists understand phenomena during combustion.
No mixing can occur in the axial direction to obtain plug ﬂow, and the ﬂow needs to be one-
dimensional, which means that at any cross-section, the velocity, composition, and temperature
are constant [8]. Flow reactors are continuous devices and can be used in diﬀerent setups [29][30].

2.3.3
Shock tube
Shock tube experiments are used to study chemical reactions in high-temperature processes. A
shock tube is illustrated in Figure 2.8. It consists of a long pipe divided into two sections: the
high-pressure driver section and the driven part with the test gas. A diaphragm splits the two
sections. A shock wave is initiated when the diaphragm ruptures by the pressure from the driver
section [30]. The shock wave travels through the driven section, rapidly increasing pressure and
temperature to initialize the reaction of the gas mixture. The temperature of the reaction zone
is controlled by the velocity of the traveling shock wave [30]. The temperature and pressure
of the reaction can be adjusted by changing the pressure in the driver or driven section before
initializing the experiments. Both incident- and reﬂected shock waves can be used to study the
chemical reactions. An incident shock wave is the initial shock wave traveling through the pipe.
It can hit the end wall and travel backward, becoming a reﬂected shock wave [30]. The gas gets
stagnant after the arrival of the reﬂected shock wave, which is why this method is more popular,
as measurements are more accessible to obtain.

2.3.4
Flame
Flame experiments are usually carried out in closed vessels to keep a low pressure. A low pressure
prolongs the reaction zone of the ﬂame, making it easier to measure diﬀerent components [32]. An
important characteristic of the ﬂame is the laminar ﬂame speed since it can be used to validate a
kinetic model. It is deﬁned as the propagation rate (how fast the ﬂame expands) of the ﬂame
front relative to the velocity of the unburned gas [30] [32]. Laminar ﬂame speed can also be used
for information about reactivity. Generally, the highest ﬂame speeds are found at the highest
temperature in the system, which is when most exothermic reactions occur. The laminar ﬂame
speed can be used to evaluate the burning rate directly [32]. The diﬀerent species in the system
can also be measured, and the data can then be used to evaluate a chemical reaction model [33].
Another factor that may be used to evaluate chemistry is the Markstein length, which relates
ﬂame speed and ﬂame stretch and is used to evaluate ﬂame stability directly [34].
A setup that has been used to evaluate the chemistry of H2 /N2O ﬂames is shown in Figure 2.9.
In this setup, quartz windows make it possible to observe the inside of the steel shell, which is
how the laminar ﬂame speed is determined using Schlieren photography.
Figure 2.9: A spherical bomb setup which has been used by M´evel et al. to measure laminar ﬂame speeds.
Probes are used to measure voltage and current, electrodes are used for ignition, and the oscilloscope is
used to display voltage in the system [35].

2.4
Previous studies of N2O/H2 systems with or without oxygen
The combustion of mixtures of ammonia, NOx-gases, hydrocarbons, etc., has been studied im-
mensely in the last hundred years. As this study focuses primarily on the oxidation of hydrogen
by nitrous oxide with and without the presence of oxygen, only literature with N2O/H2- and
N2O/H2/O2 mixtures is explored.
The following sections review the numerous studies conducted for the two mixtures in multiple
reactor settings, including results from batch reactors, ﬂow reactors, shock tubes, and ﬂame
tests. Detonation studies have also been explored in literature, but these are used to determine
explosion characteristics [36] [37] [38] [39]. Since this study focuses on nitrous oxide as an emission
from combustion, the physical system has no risk of exploding. Thus, detonation characteristics
will not be of great interest.

2.5
Sub-conclusion: Reactions of the H2 /N2O-system
Experiments of the H2/N2O/(O2)-system have been carried out at several equivalence ratios,
multiple pressures, and at various levels of dilution using diﬀerent methods.
From the literature, it is widely agreed that the following reactions ((R 4) and (R 5)) are the
most important in terms of N2O consumption and global reaction rate:
N2O ( + M) ≠≠æ N2 + O ( + M)
N2O + H ≠≠æ N2 + OH

==PAGE:45==
Literature Research
Where the atomic oxygen and OH-species are mostly consumed by:
O + H2 ≠≠æ OH + H
(R 11)
OH + H2 ≠≠æ H2O + H
(R 12)
Reactions (R 5) and (R 12) are highly exothermic, thus adding heat to the system.
Depending on the equivalence ratio and temperature, the importance of each reaction changes.
The activation energy of reaction (R 4) is higher than that of reaction (R 5), meaning it is more
dominant at higher temperatures.
The literature agrees that in the presence of hydrogen, reactions (R 5) and (R 6) take place since
the activation energy is generally lower, but (R 5) is dominating. Reactions involving hydrogen
play a bigger role at fuel-rich conditions due to the higher hydrogen content.
A limited amount of literature looks into the importance of the HNNO formation from H + N2O.
Experimental data is only available for more complex systems than H2 / N2O. Literature suggests
that HNNO stabilization is most important for high-pressure, intermediate-temperature systems.
As no experimental data is available at these conditions, it is currently diﬃcult to explore the
importance of this species.
Several experiments were conducted to measure the NO concentration. According to the literature,
the reaction (R 6) is generally the most important in terms of NO-production:
N2O + H ≠≠æ NH + NO
The reactions not including hydrogen become more relevant at leaner conditions:
N2O + O ≠≠æ N2 + O2
N2O + O ≠≠æ 2 NO
But overall (R 8) and (R 9) do not play a signiﬁcant role in the N2O consumption.
It is important to note that most reactions in literature mainly consider N2O/H2-systems and
thus lack the addition of oxygen. It is also important to note that most tests are carried out
at lower pressures (a maximum of 30 bar has been found, i.e., most ﬂame tests are carried out
below 1 atm). Thus, the chemistry at higher pressures may diﬀer, where some of the proposed
Page 35 of 104

mechanisms from the literature fall short. Especially the formation of the intermediate HNNO
might have a signiﬁcant eﬀect, but this requires further investigation.

Experimental setup
The setup uses a high-pressure ﬂow reactor, which is assumed to approximate plug ﬂow at the
present conditions [55]. The setup can operate from 10 to 100 bar and up to 925 K. Figure 3.1
presents a schematic overview of the system.
Figure 3.1: Schematic overview of the system. Selfmade illustration.
The reactions occur inside the 155 cm long quartz reactor with a diameter of 0.8 cm. The
reactor is placed in a steel tube, which works as a pressure shell. This is required to ensure
that pressure gradients across the quartz reactor will not fracture the quartz. Two mass ﬂow
pressure controllers (model 5866 from Brooks Instruments) are manually controlled, allowing
the operator to make a setpoint for the pressure on the shell side of the quartz reactor. The
controllers then allow nitrogen gas to enter to maintain the desired pressure on the shell side.
The pressure inside the reactor is regulated by a pressure control loop and is illustrated by the
dashed lines in Figure 3.1. A pressure transmitter is located before the reactor, which controls
the Baumann valve (model 51115S) after the reactor.
A purge is located before the reactor, which can depressurize the system in case of a system
shutdown.
Three heating elements are attached to heat the steel tube. The purpose is to achieve an
Page 37 of 104
==PAGE:48==
Experimental work
isothermal zone, as it is of great interest to keep constant reaction- and ﬂow conditions. The
reactor’s temperature is measured by a thermocouple within a steel thermopocket placed inside
the steel shell. Using this thermocouple, the length of the isothermal zone in the reactor is
determined to 38 cm for the conditions of the tests carried out.
Four gases can enter the system and are premixed before entering the reactor. The pipes used
for gas ﬂow in the system are 1/8 in. and made of stainless steel. A high-pressure ﬂow regulator
monitors each gas, called a mass ﬂow controller (Brooks Instrument, model SLA5800). The
Mass Flow controllers (MFCs) are not calibrated to the reactants used, which means the input
setpoint does not correspond to the output ﬂow. The MFCs used for N2, N2O and O2 use mass
ﬂow setpoints. The MFC used for H2 uses an oﬄine Brooks controller, where the setpoint is %.
The ﬂow controllers can operate from 0 - 5 NL/min or 0 - 0.5 NL/min. Due to the uncertainties
of the MFCs, the total ﬂow is measured by a glass pipe with soap.
Air Liquid supplies the high-pressure gases. Six cylinders of N2 (Æ 99.999%), one cylinder of
N2O diluted in N2 (0.2483%:99.7517%), one cylinder of O2 diluted in N2 (2.4988%:97.5012%),
and one cylinder H2 diluted in N2 (1.5016%:98.4984%) are utilized.
The system gets pressurized by the inlet gas containers. Attached to each gas cylinder is a
pressure reduction valve. The valve aims to ensure that the outlet pressure is above the system
pressure, which is approximately 105 bar. All containers need to have an internal pressure of at
least 110 bar to keep up with the system’s pressure.
After the Baumann valve, the gas is depressurized to atmospheric pressure before product analysis.
H2 and O2 are measured by Gas Chromatography using a Thermo Scientiﬁc Trace 1300 from
Thermo Fischer Scientiﬁc and NH3, H2O and N2O by Fourier Transform Infrared Spectroscopy
using a multigas 2030 FTIR from MKS instruments. The downstream tubes are heated by
heating cables to ensure no condensation of gases before product analysis.
Gas cylinders
There are minor uncertainties related to the gas cylinders provided by Air Liquid. This means
that the hydrogen and nitrous oxide gas bottles may vary with 1.5±0.03% and 0.25±0.005%,
respectively. This has a minor inﬂuence on the system.
3.2
Experimental procedure
The experimental work aimed to investigate the behavior of the H2/N2O-system at high pressure
and medium temperatures. This investigation has not been carried out at high pressures before,
Page 38 of 104
==PAGE:49==
Experimental work
which is why it is highly relevant, as this condition can be present in an engine.
Two systems were investigated: a more diluted one, where the reactants are diluted by nitrogen,
and a less diluted one, where they are not diluted by nitrogen. In total, eight experiments were
carried out for the two systems.
All experiments were carried out at low ﬂow rates, making obtaining high pressures challenging.
This means that before each test, the ﬁrst thing that was done was to pressure the system
with pure nitrogen to save reactants. At the same time, the system was heated since this helps
regulate the Baumann valve. It was discovered that increasing the temperature of the heater
responsible for heating the outlet pipe of the reactor, improved the capability of the Baumann
valve signiﬁcantly.
After reaching the desired pressure and temperature, hydrogen and nitrous oxide were introduced
to the system. The ﬂow was measured manually using a glass pipe, and the inlet concentration
of the reactants had to be determined. The initial hydrogen concentration was measured after
the reactor using Gas Chromatography (GC) at a temperature where no reaction would occur.
This temperature was approximated using simulations. Concentrations of N2O, NH3, and H2O
were measured using the Fourier Transform Infrared Spectrometer (FTIR).
For conditions where no reaction was observed from either reactant, only two H2 measurements
were taken. However, when reaction was observed, three measurements were taken. It was
noticed that a steady state was quickly obtained for N2O and H2, which is why it was chosen
that just after the injection of the third H2 measurement in the GC, the condition was changed
if the ﬁrst two measurements were of similar values.
For the less diluted system, measuring N2O with the FTIR was not viable, due to detection
limits. Instead, it was calculated from a mass balance calculation using the ﬂow. Thus, for the
less diluted system, where NH3 was of great importance, NH3 steady state dictated condition
change, and for high temperatures, this could require more than two hours.

Temperature uncertainties
Several uncertainties are coupled with temperature measurements by thermocouples. The tem-
perature of most interest is inside the reactor, but the thermocouple touches the thermo pocket
Page 41 of 104
==PAGE:52==
Experimental work
outside the reactor. Thus, the temperature measured by the thermocouple and the temperature
inside the reactor may vary. This thermocouple is a Class 2, Type K, produced by RS components,
and it is known to have a relative uncertainty of 0.75% and an absolute uncertainty of 2.5 K [57].
It should be noted that aging may also play a role, meaning uncertainty may be bigger.
A thermocouple and a ruler are applied when measuring the temperature proﬁle. The temperature
is measured every 2 cm. There might be some uncertainty related to the measured length as
a person judges the 2 cm increase, but this is of minor importance regarding the isothermal length.
It is assumed that the temperature inside the reactor is equivalent to the one measured by the
thermocouple. It can vary by ±5K from the temperature proﬁle (Figure 3.3). The variations
have a more signiﬁcant eﬀect at higher temperatures, as the reactions are highly sensitive to
temperature. The isothermal length may eﬀectively vary from 36 cm to 40 cm, which aﬀects the
residence time. This may inﬂuence the comparison of model kinetics to the data obtained during
experiments. The uncertainty of the temperature measurements is calculated as a square average
of the thermocouple uncertainty of 2.5K and the isothermal zone uncertainty of 5K, which ends
up at an uncertainty of 6K.
It is known that some of the reactions taking place are exothermic. The heat release may increase
the temperature in the reactor without it being detectable.

Flow uncertainties
The ﬂow was measured using a glass pipe with soap in the bottom and a stopwatch. This
will inherently yield some uncertainties. The glass pipe was not made for very accurate ﬂow
measurements, so the volumes written on the pipe may not be perfect. To yield more accurate
ﬂow measurements, two data points were taken for each volume interval, and several volume
intervals were used (i.e., both measuring from volume 0 - 500 ml and from 350 - 450 ml).
The measurements were carried out by a human looking at the glass pipe and deciding when the
soap bubble reached a certain volume. This also carried some uncertainty as a human cannot
accurately tell when the soap bubble is at a particular level in the glass pipe. If a small pressure
diﬀerence is present in the piping around that glass pipe, this will also have an inﬂuence. Pressure
in the ventilation system, where the ﬂow ends, is slightly below atmospheric and does ﬂuctuate.
This ﬂuctuation is, however, so small that it can be assumed not to inﬂuence the ﬂow.
Lastly, one must also react using a stopwatch, which may not be carried out perfectly and may
yield some error.
However, the ﬂow measurements were taken at seven-volume intervals and were repeated twice.
Thus, the the ﬂow rate was determined as accurately as possible. One can get an idea of the
numerical deviation by quantifying it using standard deviation. This is found to be at a maximum
of 12% but does not consider that there is a higher error margin when taking measurements in
smaller volumes, as reacting time plays a bigger role.

3.3.3
Pressure
A Baumann 51000, High Pressure, Low Flow control valve sits between the setup’s high-pressure
and low-pressure side, thus controlling the pressure in the reactor. It is a globe valve utilizing a
pin traveling into and out of a seating surface using pressurized air. Rubber ﬁttings ensure no
leaks, but these may degrade with time [58]. The Baumann valve could keep the pressure at 100
bar ±0.1 bar in most cases but ﬂuctuated at times, speciﬁcally at the beginning of a test when it
was not heated thoroughly. In this case, the uncertainty was ±0.5 bar.
3.3.4
Gas Chromatography (GC)
Hydrogen and oxygen measurements are carried out using Gas Chromatography with a Thermal
Conductivity Detector and a Flame Ionization Detector (GC-TCD/FID for short).
The working principle of GC is the separation of components in a sample based on volatility.
This means only volatile or semi-volatile components can be measured using GC; see the working
principle of GC in Figure 3.6 [59].
Page 46 of 104
==PAGE:57==
Experimental work
Figure 3.6: Gas chromatography working principle [59].
The sample is injected and heated, and the components travel through the capillary column at
diﬀerent speeds. The capillary column has a stationary phase coated inside, which holds back
components based on intermolecular forces. A carrier gas is also needed to transport the sample.
In this case, argon is utilized since H2 is measured and therefore cannot be used [59].
For this speciﬁc GC, the detectors TCD and FID are present [60]. Flame Ionization Detectors
measure the small ion currents produced when burning a compound. This takes place in a
small H2-O2 diﬀusion ﬂame, and the lower detection limit can be around 10≠12g/s. Thermal
Conductivity Detectors detect a diﬀerence in the carrier gas thermal conductivity due to the
present sample.
The detection occurs as the gas ﬂows through a cavity, and the thermal
conductivity changes, resulting in a temperature change in the sensor. The detection limits range
from 10≠6 - 10≠8 g per peak [60]. H2 and O2 cannot be measured by FID since they are already
present in the ﬂame thus, for these tests, only TCD was applied [60].
The result of a measurement by the GC is a chromatogram, and calibration is needed to know
which components are placed at which times of the chromatogram. Calibration is carried out
using pure compounds of what is desired to be measured during the test. It is then possible to
know the sample’s content if the proper calibration has been carried out [59].
Each GC measurement takes 20 minutes, most of which is ﬂushing of the capillary column to
ensure no residue of the previous sample remains.

GC uncertainties
Diﬀerent uncertainties are connected to the GC measurements.
Firstly, in the GC measurements, H2 is generally a hard molecule to measure very accurately due
to its small size, which is why these measurements may ﬂuctuate, even when N2O is at steady
state. Cross sensitivity may also take place, as molecules such as O2 have a peak close to that of
H2 at these GC conditions. Furthermore, manual integration has to occur since the peak is not
completely horizontal, which also yields some uncertainty.
It is also important to ensure proper ﬂow to the GC. In this case, a pressure of 0.5 bar was held
constant going to the GC; however, due to an unstable Baumann valve, the pressure sometimes
ﬂuctuated between 0.5 and 0.7 bar. The pressure is important to ensure the correct ﬂow rate to
the GC [61].
Generally, it is observed that for H2 concentrations of 50 ppm and below, a higher uncertainty
is noticed. This may be because pure H2 has been used to calibrate the equipment or that
small concentrations of hydrogen are challenging to measure accurately. The overall uncertainty
is quantiﬁed using the standard deviation found from the measurements. The uncertainty is
generally below ±5%. At concentrations below 70 ppm, hydrogen measurements are more
inaccurate (upwards of 25% uncertainty from STDV). However, this may also be expected, as
this machine is calibrated using pure H2 and may have trouble detecting small concentrations.
Thus, when minimal concentrations are present, it is more accurate to use absolute uncertainty,

3.3.5
Fourier Transform Infrared Spectrometers (FTIR)
Fourier Transform Infrared Spectrometers (FTIRs) measure molecules with covalent bonds. This
is very useful, as many compounds have covalent bonds [62]. The principle is that depending on
what atoms are at the diﬀerent ends of the covalent bonds, the bonds vibrate diﬀerently and
are quantized. Many diﬀerent types of vibrations are possible [62]. Thus, depending on the
speciﬁc covalent bond and the speciﬁc atoms, a diﬀerent adsorption wavelength is observed [62].
A changing dipole is needed for energy to be transferred, and thus, only molecules having dipole
moments are detectable [62].
The FTIR is used to measure N2O, NH3, and H2O. The working principle of an FTIR is seen in
Figure 3.8.
Figure 3.8: FTIR working principle [62], the ﬁgure is slightly modiﬁed.
Page 49 of 104
==PAGE:60==

An infrared light source is continuously beamed inside the spectrometer, where it is split, one
beam going to a ﬁxed mirror and another to a moving mirror. The two beams then combine
again, causing constructive and destructive interference due to the moving mirror [62]. The
combined beam then contains all the radiative energy and produces an interferogram with many
diﬀerent wavelengths. The interferogram is then pointed at the sample, which simultaneously
adsorbs all wavelengths. The sample modiﬁes the interferogram and reaches the detector, where
it is compared to a reference laser beam [62]. The Fourier Transform can extract all the individual
frequencies’ information about the sample. By knowing where diﬀerent compounds adsorb in the
IR spectrum, the diﬀerent compounds can be measured [62].

FTIR uncertainties
Generally, FTIR is a very sensitive method, meaning it can detect very small quantities of
compounds. However, it will be inﬂuenced by changes in the reactor, such as ﬂuctuation in ﬂow
or temperature, making it sensitive. Before experiments, the FTIR was ﬂushed using nitrogen,
and the data from the ﬂush was used as a baseline for H2O and NH3. This baseline may diﬀer
from test to test.
The water measured by the FTIR is calibrated to measure a maximum of 20%, which means it will
not be very accurate on a ppm basis. Because of this, the water measurements are excluded for
the more diluted conditions due to the high uncertainty. The relative standard deviation is used
for quantifying the uncertainty. The uncertainty of H2O is generally 2%, while an uncertainty of
5.5% is observed for one measurement, where the FTIR ran out of liquid nitrogen. The absolute
deviation from average at low concentrations is very relevant for H2O due to the measurements
being conducted for concentrations at the calibration’s very low end. This is found to be 120
ppm for H2O, found at low concentrations, which is substantial but expected.
The FTIR has a maximum (and minimum) detection ability for each species. Closer to the max
detection capacity, it will be more uncertain, which also holds at lower values. This is due to how
the machine is calibrated, i.e., calibrating for 300 ppm, it will struggle to detect on a ppb basis.
The FTIR displays a maximum detection capacity of 300 ppm for N2O but is known to go higher
(measured 410 ppm) and still uphold the mass balance. The uncertainty of N2O is generally low
since the amount observed is mostly further away from the maximum detection rate. Thus, the
uncertainty of the N2O measurements is 1%, with an absolute uncertainty of 1 ppm.
The uncertainty of the measurements of the NH3 highly depends on the amount of NH3 since a
very small amount is sometimes formed. Thus, a maximum uncertainty of 6% is observed, but
for larger amounts of NH3, the uncertainty may only be 0.5%. The absolute uncertainty is 0.1
ppm, found for low concentrations of NH3. According to the manufacturer, the detection limit is
0.5 ppm. This means values below this value will be uncertain, but this is hard to quantify [63].
Cross sensitivity is also a concern when trying to detect small concentrations, as the formed
water may inﬂuence all other measurements due to the broad absorption band. This makes
trace measurements of, for example, ammonia, uncertain as the small concentration may be
due to water. The FTIR user interface, displaying detection capability, as well as the FTIR
measurements and the uncertainties of each measurement, is shown in Appendix A.3.

4.1
Modeling
All experiments are modeled with the software Chemkin. The mechanism used in Chemkin
includes chemical kinetics and thermodynamic parameters from Glarborg et al. [6], which will
be referred to as GLA23. An OH* subset has been included in GLA23 from Mevel et al. [26] to
later on compare the models’ predictions with shock tube experiments from the literature.
The eﬀect of the intermediate HNNO is investigated by including an HNNO subset to GLA23 pro-
posed by Burke et al. [7]. This mechanism will be referred to as GLA23HNNO. The GLA23HNNO
subset includes 49 new reactions, and six new species are introduced: t-HNNO, c-HNNO, ONHN,
H2NNO2, t-HNN(O)OH, and c-HNN(O)OH. The experimental data is compared with these two
models.
In Chemkin, the system is modeled as a Closed Homogeneous reactor, and the problem type is set
to ’Constrain pressure and temperature,’ which assumes that the system behaves as an isothermal
plug ﬂow. A batch reactor can simulate a plug ﬂow reactor in Chemkin if the independent
variable is converted from length to time. This is done by using the isothermal length of the
reactor to calculate the residence time in the reactor, making it possible to correspond the
residence time of the closed reactor to the residence time of the plug ﬂow reactor. The next
subsection (4.1.1) discusses how well the assumption of plug ﬂow holds.
The input in Chemkin is the inlet concentration of H2, N2O, and N2, along with pressure,
temperature, and residence time. Each temperature has a corresponding residence time (·). The
residence time calculated from each test is found from the reactor volume and the volumetric

4.1.1
Plug ﬂow assumption
It is assumed that the setup runs at plug ﬂow conditions. Whether this assumption holds has
been investigated for 500 - 925 K and 100 bar for ﬂows at 850 NmL/min and 1500 NmL/min, as
these conditions are used in the experiments. Two criteria have to be met to ensure that the
assumption is appropriate [55]:
• The gases have to be premixed before the reaction
• The radial velocity gradient has to be small
The ﬁrst criterion will be achieved, as the gases are mixed early in the setup and have time
to be thoroughly mixed before entering the reaction zone [55]. The second criterion is not as
straightforward to evaluate. The radial velocity gradient will be zero if there is no friction
on the inside walls of the pipe [64]. In reality, this is not possible, and it will be evaluated
if the gradient is suﬃciently small to approximate plug ﬂow. Initially, it has to be checked
whether the ﬂow is laminar, achieved if the Reynolds number is below 2000 [65]. The Reynolds
number has been determined to be 52 < Re < 176, meaning there is laminar ﬂow. The density
and viscosity used to calculate Reynolds number is that of nitrogen and has been correlated to
account for pressure and temperature dependency [66]. Calculations can be seen in Appendix B.2.
The laminar ﬂow may behave as a plug ﬂow, which must be examined. The dispersion model can
be used to evaluate the deviation from plug ﬂow [64]. The model describes the radial dispersion
and is characterized by the dispersion coeﬃcient (Ddisp) [55]. Ddisp describes how much the ﬂow

and atomic H is removed. This also explains the positive sensitivity for N2O + H →NH + NO,
where atomic H is also removed. The sensitivity of this reaction is small since only a minor part
of N2O is consumed through this reaction.
The system is also quite sensitive to the reaction: H + t-HNNO →NH2 + NO. The reasoning is
that this reaction consumes atomic H to form less reactive species, and this general reaction path
ends with more atomic H to form NH3. Generally, the t-HNNO pathway reduces the consumption
of N2O due to the consumption of more reactive species to form less reactive species, such as the
consumption of H to form t-HNNO. This is one of the main reasons why GLA23HNNO is better
at reproducing the experimental data in Figure 4.2.
Lastly N2O shows negative sensitivity towards HNO + H ( + M) →HNOH ( + M) and HNO +
H ( + M) →H2NO ( + M). This is not obvious as both reactions consume H radicals, which
would be expected to lower the system’s sensitivity and slow down the consumption of N2O. It
is not expected that HNO + H ( + M), giving either HNOH or H2NO, has a positive eﬀect on
N2O. HNO + H has three product channels, which N2O shows sensitivity towards. If NO + H2
is produced, it will inhibit consumption, meaning if any of the two other products are made, it
will be less inhibiting. This could explain the negative sensitivity towards these two reactions.

Discussion
5
Discussion
This section compares and discusses the diﬀerences between lean and rich experiments. Finally,
it discusses why GLA23HNNO outperforms GLA23 at intermediate temperatures and high
pressures.
5.1
Diﬀerences between a lean and a rich system
The major reaction pathways of the system do not change with equivalence ratio or level of
dilution. Since it is such a simple system, one reaction dominates: N2O + H →OH + N2,
where OH + H2 →H + H2O follows. However, the minor reactions are highly dependent on the
equivalence ratio.
The diﬀerent tests show that a higher reactivity is observed in lean systems, compared to rich
systems, since reactants are consumed at lower temperatures. The main products are found to
be H2O and N2. It is found that most of the H2 is consumed through OH + H2 →H + H2O.
Where OH is produced through the reaction N2O + H →OH + N2. It is also found that the
general initiating reaction for all tests is the decomposition reaction of N2O: N2O ( + M) →N2 +
O, where O reacts with H2 to form OH and H. Thus, the reaction of the system is not initiated
by H2, but by N2O.
The N2O specie is therefore responsible for higher radical concentration in the system since it
both initiates the reaction and promotes it due to the need for O-containing radical species. If
there is an excess of N2O, relative to H2, more OH is available to react with H2. More oxidizing
molecules mean more fuel molecules can react, leading to faster combustion. This explains why
the lean system obtains full conversion before the rich one.
It is observed from the obtained data that NH3 production is higher for a rich system. This
is due to the higher availability of H2 promoting production pathways of NH3. A diﬀerence
between the lean and rich systems, in terms of NH3 production, is that the lean system reaches
an optimal temperature in terms of NH3 production in the temperature range investigated in
this thesis. Ammonia is consumed through a reaction with OH, forming H2O and NH2, so an
optimal radical concentration is expected as the OH production increases with temperature.
For an equivalence ratio of 0.8, the optimum is found at 885 K. None of the rich systems reach
the optimum in the investigated temperature range, which indicates that this optimal radical
concentration is at a higher temperature for the rich systems. This further supports the claim
that higher N2O concentration leads to higher radical concentration.
Page 94 of 104
==PAGE:105==
Discussion
The rate-limiting reactions of the rich and lean systems are generally the same for the main
species of N2O and H2. For a minor species, such as NH3, diﬀerences in sensitivity and rate of
production may be observed. Generally, for NH3, the production is limited by a lack of radicals,
which is why the decomposition reaction is of high sensitivity: N2O ( + M) →N2 + O. For a
leaner system, the relative sensitivity of N2O + H →OH + N2 may be higher than that of HNO +
H ( + M) →HNOH ( + M), where for a richer system, the relative importance is reversed. The
richer system is more impacted by chain terminating reactions, such as HNO + H ( + M) →
HNOH ( + M), since radicals are taken out of the system, and these are not produced as much
for the richer system. This is also apparent from the fact that the relative importance of the
reaction: H + t-HNNO →NH2 + NO increases for the rich system because H is consumed. Thus,
the rich system is generally more sensitive to chain-terminating reactions since fewer reactions
occur than in the lean system.
5.2
Indication of formation of HNNO species
Three lean and ﬁve rich tests of the N2O/H2 system have been carried out in the experimental
work. All experiments have been modeled with GLA23 and GLA23HNNO.
Under experimental conditions, GLA23HNNO enhances the ability to reproduce the species in
the system for all experiments compared to GLA23. GLA23HNNO predict lower reactivity of
H2, H2O and N2O compared to GLA23.
The reason why GLA23HNNO is better at reproducing the data can be found by looking at the
pathway of t-HNNO and c-HNNO. From this pathway, very reactive radicals, such as H and
O, are trapped in a less reactive compound, such as t-HNNO. The main pathway of t-HNNO
and c-HNNO is to break into N2 and OH. However, it can also react further with H-radicals to
produce species that end up as ammonia. This entire pathway decreases the reactivity of the
system.
GLA23HNNO is also better in predicting the NH3 formation than GLA23 because it has an
additional pathway to produce NH3 through t-HNNO and c-HNNO. With this being said,
GLA23HNNO still falls short in many instances for the NH3 production since it, for some
experiments, only predicts 50% of the ammonia concentration compared to data.
Overall, from a comparison of GLA23 and GLA23HNNO, it can be concluded that there is
a strong indication that HNNO species form at high pressure and intermediate temperatures.
But further improvements are still required to improve the modeling of especially NH3 at the
experimental conditions.

Conclusion
6
Conclusion
Ammonia is considered a carbon-free alternative fuel in the maritime industry. Some challenges
are present if ammonia is used as fuel: it is hard to ignite, and other harmful species may be
released during combustion. The motivation behind this thesis has been to investigate one potent
greenhouse gas that may be formed during combustion: N2O.
Experiments have been conducted on the simple mixture of N2O/H2, which can be present in an
engine. This simple mixture, allows investigation of the interaction of N2O with the radical pool.
According to previous studies of this mixture, the most important reactions are those of N2O ( +
M) →N2 + O ( + M) and N2O + H →N2 + OH. These studies have been carried out in several
diﬀerent reactor types, using several methods but at lower pressures. Generally, the dissociation
reaction of N2O is highly relevant at higher temperatures, but other reactions become important
at temperatures below 1000 K.
A plug ﬂow reactor setup with an isothermal zone has been utilized for studying the system
at temperatures from 723 K to 923 K and a pressure of 100 bar. Diﬀerent equivalence ratios
have been investigated at two diﬀerent dilution levels. The compounds H2, N2O, NH3 and H2O
have been measured. These tests resulted in data of low uncertainty, which generally upheld the
mass balance. The tests showed that the reaction initiation occurs around 800 K, where the lean
system is more reactive than the rich system. Larger amounts of NH3 production is observed
for richer systems. The test conditions were simulated in Chemkin using two diﬀerent models.
One model, GLA23, is developed by Glarborg et al., and the second model, GLA23HNNO, also
includes the model by Glarborg et al. but also includes a new subset by Burke et al., including
six new species, the most important being t-HNNO.
GLA23HNNO improved the model’s capability to predict the system behavior for all tests
compared to GLA23. The improvement in prediction capability is due to the system’s reactivity
decreasing by producing t-HNNO and c-HNNO. The new reaction path uses atomic H, which is
a very reactive species, to produce less reactive species. Furthermore, this pathway enhances
the production of NH3 since t-HNNO reacts by the reaction: t-HNNO + H →NH2 + NO. Thus,
the mechanism with the new species was better at predicting the system behavior due to the
reduction in reactivity and the increased production of NH3. The model can still be improved to
model NH3 even better.
Previous studies were simulated with both models to investigate the eﬀect at other conditions.
The studies were carried out at too high temperatures or too low pressures for the HNNO-specie

to be stable, except for one study, where it improved the predictions. This indicates that the
HNNO species is stable at intermediate temperatures and a large range of pressures (3-100 bar).
This thesis’s results strongly indicate the formation of an HNNO species for the N2O/H2 system
at intermediate temperatures and high pressures and proposes a pathway for nitrogen that
supports the increased production of ammonia at high pressure.

Conclusion
Future work
In section 4.5 optimization of GLA23HNNO was investigated. It would be interesting to look
further into the reaction rate of HNO + H →NO + H2. This is a rate-limiting reaction for NH3
for all experiments, and by lowering the reaction rate, the ammonia formation would increase.
On the other hand, this would also increase the system’s reactivity because more H radicals
would be present. This emphasizes that it is not straightforward to optimize the modeling of the
system. The rate constant of reaction (13) and HNO + H →NO + H2 was tuned together, and
promising results were achieved. The physical limits of the reactions were not included in the
tuning, which is why this analysis is not included in the thesis.
A possible next step after this thesis would be to optimize GLA23HNNO further to better
reproduce the data of NH3.
