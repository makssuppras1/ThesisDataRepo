Abstract
Cold climate areas are becoming increasingly relevant for the production of wind energy. Never-
theless, planning wind farms in cold climates can be troublesome considering the negative tem-
peratures the rotors must endure for longer periods of time. In fact, icing phenomena not only
critically affects the annual energy production but also poses a threat to the nearby structures and
people. It is then essential to investigate and understand the icing accretion process.
Historically wind tunnel facilities have been the most common set-up to research how curved sur-
faces behave when approached by wind from various directions. Although these experiments
provide valuable insights into the aerodynamics of airfoils, the results obtained provide only an
approximation of actual scenarios. In the recent years, the advances in computational power have
offered new opportunities to simulate conditions which had been difficult and expensive to test in
real-life. The previously developed solutions for wind turbines’ airfoils gave precise results only
for limited ranges of conditions. Given this context, there has been a need to present a numerical
tool to help estimate the ice accretion process on rotating turbines precisely.
This study focuses on the validation of a numerical solver earlier developed at the DTU Depart-
ment of Wind Energy [1][2] and its application on some identified sample cases. The solver has
been tested on 2D cases over a range of different airfoil geometries and weather conditions. The
two airfoil geometries tested are NACA0012 and SD7032, the latter has been solved for different
chord lengths and angles of attack. The solver calculated ice accretions for rime ice and high wind
speeds. Furthermore, the weather conditions have been simulated for different values of liquid
water content, water droplet diameter and exposure time.

Introduction
The increasing interest in sustainable sources of energy creates new challenges in the field of engi-
neering. In the renewable energy development, wind energy plays an important role, since it is one
of the most prolific ways to produce clean power. The Global Wind Energy Council confirms that
one of the most prolific years for wind energy was 2021, with a rise of 93.6 GW of new capacity
installed. This value is only 1.8% lower than 2020’s record year [3]. This growth builds up to
make the 837 GW of wind power capacity worldwide in 2022 which helps avoid 1.2 billion tonnes
of CO2 emissions [3]. Albeit the sustained growth in the past few years, more capacity needs to
be installed to meet the net-zero targets of 2050. It is then crucial to find new areas to harness the
wind resource for the production of energy.
Figure 1.1: 2020-2030 wind energy growth [3]
The exponential increment needed in this energy branch requires the exploitation of the wind re-
source in extreme environmental conditions too. Remote areas are considered ideal to install wind
farms in order to not compromise the landscape and avoid disturbance to the population. For these
reasons, cold climate sites are some of the most attractive locations. It is also important to con-
sider that winter is the season that typically yields most wind energy thanks to average higher wind
speeds and increased air density. The same applies during the night when the temperature level
drops more easily below 0 °C [4]. Besides, such conditions are not restricted to the Nordics or
mountainous regions anymore. Newly designed turbines can be high enough to experience cold

1.1
Project Scope
Considering the growing potential for wind energy in cold climates [8], it is relevant to study tools
that can predict the impact of ice on wind turbine blades’ performance. Besides, the understanding
of the icing accretion process is necessary so that targeted safety countermeasures can be imple-
mented. This master of science project has been carried out between the 1st of February 2022 and
the 1st of July 2022. The detailed timeline of the project is pictured in figure 1.3. The objective
of the project is to perform CFD-based simulations for rotating wind turbine systems and under-
stand the icing phenomenon. The numerical investigation starts with the generation of a 2D mesh
in commercially available software. Next, the mesh is exported to OpenFoam where the WISE
solver has previously been developed and validated [1] [2]. The code is then solved for an SD7032
airfoil which has been tested for similar conditions at the FORCE Technology wind tunnel. Dif-
ferent combinations of liquid water content, exposure time, chord length and angle of attack are
then simulated to compare different potential scenarios.

1.3
Background
Cold climate is defined, in a wind energy context, both as the condition which allows the formation
of atmospheric icing; and as the state where the temperatures drop below the operational limit of
a standard turbine (usually -10 °C for operation, -20 °C for survival). These conditions are strictly
related to the location of the site and the season. Even if the annual average temperature might
be mild, during the winter some serious ice accretion can compromise the wind turbine. A more
detailed definition of this concept has been given by the IEA Wind (Task 19 Wind Energy in Cold
Climates):
”Cold climate locations are sites where the temperature can go lower than the operational temper-
ature of the wind turbine (Low Temperature Climate - LTC), or icing events occur (Icing Climate
- IC).”
Figure 1.4: Cold Climate definition with Low Temperature Climate and Icing Climate
IC is correlated to atmospheric icing. This can have both effects on the wind turbine and on the
meteorological instruments. LTC instead is when extremely low temperatures occur, this instead
can affect the maintenance of the turbine. Furthermore, it is important to specify there are two cate-
gories of atmospheric icing: in-cloud icing (glaze, rime and mixed) and precipitation icing (caused
by freezing rain or wet snow) [9]. The mechanical characteristics of the ice depend on different
parameters related to meteorological and atmospheric conditions. As instance, the liquid water
content (LWC), median volume droplet diameter (MVD), wind speed, atmospheric pressure, air
temperature and humidity can all affect the ice formation process. These parameters also influence
the ice accretion process which is further categorized as follows:
• In-cloud icing: related to temperatures below 0 °C and clouds at low altitude.
• Precipitation icing: dependent on precipitation. It can also happen at positive temperatures
in form of wet snow.
In order to better understand the concept, a short explanation of the different types of ice and the
accretion process is given in the following sections.
1.4
Icing types
Icing is a widespread issue that can critically affect many structures such as aircraft, bridges, power
lines, vessels, offshore oil platforms and wind turbines [10]. Depending on the meteorological
conditions, ice can feature different shapes, content and appearance. The main in-cloud icing cate-
gories are glaze ice, rime ice and mixed ice. All these types can affect the blade surface roughness

within seconds and deform the blade shape within several minutes to hours. Figure 1.5 reports the
relationship between different types of ice accretion, temperature and wind velocity.
Figure 1.5: In-cloud icing classification [9]
Glaze ice
Glaze ice is an ice coating characterized by a high density (900 kg/m3), transparency, smoothness
and homogeneous appearance similar to clear ice. Glaze ice is caused by large droplets of freezing
rain or wet in-cloud icing between 0 °C and -4 °C. The accretion rate of glaze ice depends on the rate
of precipitation, wind speed and temperature. Glaze ice is also typically hard to remove because of
its strong adhesion and cohesion forces [11]. From previous studies, it was found that its presence
on airfoil results in the formation of double or multiple horns ices which cause a decrease in lift,
an increase in drag and an increase in pitching moment. Furthermore, the formation of horn ice
on the surface leads to increased projected height of the airfoil causing higher turbulence intensity
[12]. Based on flight test data comparison with wind tunnel experiments, it has been concluded
that the extent of drag increase is larger (up to 230 %)than that of lift increase (35 %) [13].
Rime ice
Rime ice is white ice with in-trapped air which is formed when supercooled water droplets (liquid
water below 0 °C) freeze on surfaces causing windward icing structures. Because of its crystalline
structure rime ice is brittle; moreover, depending on the temperature, rime ice can be further clas-
sified as soft rime or hard rime. Soft rime is similar to hoar frost, it forms below -8 °C, is less dense
(200-600 kg/m3) and is more crystalline than hard rime. Conversely, hard rime forms between -2
°C/ -8 °C, is denser (600-900 kg/m3) and has a more opaque appearance. The accretion rate of
rime ice depends on many parameters: the dimension of the object exposed, wind speed, the water
content in the air and temperature [11]. The aerodynamic effects of rime ice accretion on an airfoil
consist of a reduction in lift and an increase in drag [14]. Because of its streamlined shape, the
impact of rime ice on the airfoil aerodynamics is more limited compared to glaze ice and mixed
ice.
Mixed ice
Mixed ice is a mixture of glaze ice and rime ice and is one of the most common icing forms in
real-life scenarios. Its appearance consists of layers of clear and opaque ice as a result of variation
in liquid water content, air temperature and droplet size. This ice type appears clear near the
stagnation line and turns to white rime near the edges [15]. Similar to glaze ice, mixed ice can form
horn ice and is more difficult to remove than rime ice. Previous papers determined that similarly
to glaze ice, mixed ice formations cause high degradation in lift and increase in drag [16].

1.5
Icing accretion process
When supercooled water droplets contained in the air hit the structures of a wind turbine, the ice
accretion process starts. The process is a time-dependent mechanism described in equation 1.1
formulated by Makkonen [18]. In particular, the equation calculates the icing rate dependency
with the parameters presented below.
dM
t
= α1 ∗α2 ∗α3 ∗w ∗v ∗A
(1.1)
Where:
• α1: collision efficiency, refers to the flux density of the particles.
• α2: collection efficiency, describes the rate of collided particles that stick to the surface.
• α3: accretion efficiency, refers to the ratio of icing particles on the body and the flux density
of the particles.
• v: particles’ velocity relative to the airfoil,
• w: mass concentration of the water droplets in the air,
• A: cross-section area of the airfoil.
The three efficiencies, with a value from 0 to 1, are correction factors that may reduce the ice mass
rate from its maximum value (w ∗v ∗A). True values of collision efficiency α1 will always be
lower than 1, however, α1 increases proportionally with the size of the droplets, or the air velocity.
1.6
Icing terminology and parameters
In this paragraph, the atmospheric parameters used in the report are explained in detail. Neverthe-
less, it is important to specify that ice rate and type are also dependent on other variables related
to the blade geometry (chord, airfoil and angle of attack).
LWC - Liquid Water Content
The liquid water content is the measure of the mass of water in a cloud at a specific amount of dry
air (g/m3). High values of LWC suggest the formation of glaze ice; on the other hand, low LWC
values are an indicator of rime ice accretion.

MVD - Median Volumetric Diameter
The median volumetric diameter defines the median size of water droplets in the cloud (µm). Usu-
ally, this value decreases when the LWC value increases. α1 and α3 efficiencies depend propor-
tionally on median volumetric diameter. High MVD increases the droplet rate hitting the surface.
Wind Speed
The velocity of the air affects both the rate of ice accretion and the ice type formation. Faster
droplets also rise the collision efficiency.
Air Temperature
One of the most determinant parameters in the icing process is the air temperature. By changing
the convective heat, the air temperature affects the liquid water content and the accretion efficiency
(α3). Figure 1.7 reports the ice accretion probability depending on the air temperature.

1.7
Effects of ice on wind turbines
1.7.1
Power losses
Based on the blade element momentum method, the aerodynamic loads for the affected chord
lengths can be solved to determine the torque and power loss [20]. Depending on the frequency,
duration and intensity of the icing phenomenon the wind turbines may be forced to curtail their
power production. The effects of icing on wind turbine blades can lead up to a 17% loss in AEP
and reduce the power coefficient from 20% to 50% [21].
1.7.2
Mechanical problems
Icing phenomenon is characterised by uneven distribution along the blade which can lead to im-
balances and quicker wear of the wind turbine’s components. Moreover, ice accretions are not
limited only to the blades of the turbine but can also form on the nacelle and tower adding mass to
the overall structure and cause vibration phenomena.

1.7.3
Measurement errors
Ice accretion can compromise the performance of meteorological instruments such as anemometers
and wind vanes. For example, the additional mass on the anemometers makes the instrument act
like a flywheel which responds more slowly to changes in wind speed. Wind speed prediction errors
caused by ice accretion can then be as large as 10% to 30 % [22]. This can generate overloads in
the turbines which rely on the wind speed parameter read by the meteorological instruments.
1.7.4
Safety problems
The most common safety issue of iced wind turbine blades is the ice shedding phenomenon which
typically takes place when the air temperature is just above the freezing point. Usually, the risk
is circumscribed to a small radius around the tower of the wind turbine, however, there is still a
significant risk for the public, surrounding structures and employees responsible for constructing
and maintaining the wind farm. Ice chunks shed from the turbine can weigh up to several kilograms
and can potentially impact and critically damage other parts of the turbine during the impact.
1.8
Anti-icing and de-icing techniques
Based on the type of ice accretion, there are potential solutions such as de-icing and anti-icing
to prevent critical failures. De-icing systems (mechanical or thermal removal) aim at removing
the ice once it is formed; whereas anti-icing systems (coating or re-design of the surface’s shape)
aim at preventing icing phenomenons. These systems can be further categorized into active or
passive. On the one hand, active systems require an external energy supply; on the other hand,
passive systems can operate without an external energy supply. Passive anti-icing systems repre-
sent the best solutions in terms of energy efficiency however, they cannot completely prevent icing
phenomenons therefore active solutions must be integrated too [23]. Current solutions offered on
the market vary depending on the blade manufacturer. Some valid options include black paint,
pitching of the blade, chemical coatings, microwaves [24] or shaking the blades [25]. Even so,
all these solutions have some drawbacks and require major investments; it is, therefore, crucial to
implement reliable icing estimation strategies.

2.1
Numerical tools for icing of airfoils
The validation of experimental results with numerical simulations is increasingly becoming an
integrated part of engineering projects. Icing on aircrafts and bridges is a long-known issue [26]
that bolstered the research and the development of new analytical tools. Some examples of CFD
ice accretion tools are LEWICE [27], TURBICE [28] and Ansys FENSAP-ICE [29]. All these
toolboxes are characterized by small differences with the results from wind tunnel experiments
but overall they align well with the scenarios tested. These CFD packages all rely on turbulence
models to calculate the vector fields. The most common turbulence models used are the k-ϵ, the
k-ω and the Spalart-Allmaras model:
• The k-ϵ is the two-equation model firstly proposed by Launder and Sharma [30] most widely
used and tested. It is characterized by the turbulence kinetic energy (k) equation and turbu-
lence dissipation rate (ϵ) equation. The standard k-ϵ model behaves well in estimating tur-
bulent shear flows far from the boundary walls; however, it yields poor results for separated
flows [31].
• The k-ω is another popular two-equation turbulence model proposed by Kolmogorov in 1942
[32]. It consists of the turbulence kinetic energy (k) equation and the specific dissipation rate
(ω) equation. The k-ω model behaves well in estimating turbulent shear flows and separated
flows near the boundary walls; however, it is weak when applied to flows with free-stream
boundaries.
• The Spalart-Allmaras model [33] is a one-equation model developed and used for the aerospace
industry and more specifically for flows past wings and airfoils. It consists of a transport
equation for the turbulent viscosity. The model is also applicable for any type of mesh for
example structured or unstructured, single-block or multi-block. The model produces good
results however it creates high diffusion [31].
Regardless of the turbulence model, some further validation is needed for each of the CFD solu-
tions mentioned above, since not all the possible scenarios have been verified yet. First, in the
case of TURBICE, the weather parameters only for rime ice cases were calculated whereas glazed
ice was not simulated due to its complexity [28]. Next, LEWICE also fairly predicts lift and drag
coefficients for iced airfoils however it under-estimates the splashing effect at high wind speed
values [27]. Last, Ansys FENSAP-ICE agrees well with the wind tunnel tests however it cannot
accurately predict small changes in the airfoil surface roughness [34]. Most of the research and
commercially available solutions have been designed for in-flight aircraft simulations and are op-
timized for fixed-wing airfoils only. It is therefore important to investigate the application of CFD
icing numerical tools on wind turbines specifically. The use of existing tools requires a deep un-
derstanding of the software architecture and governing equations since the input parameters need
to be changed to calculate specific weather conditions and geometries of wind turbines. Based on

these considerations, it emerged the need to propose a new icing code for the wind energy sector

2.2
OpenFoam toolbox
OpenFoam is a C++ toolbox with extensive CFD and multi-physics capabilities. The toolbox is
open source which means its code is publicly available, anybody can see it and modify it. The
solvers developed on this platform can then be shared with other research groups or industrial
partners. The software is constantly under development which means its capabilities mirror those
of commercial CFD packages. Throughout the project, the OpenFoam version used is v1712 which
was released in 2017. The main difference between Openfoam and other commercial CFD pack-
ages (like Ansys Fluent) is that rather than having a big executable, OpenFoam is characterized by
many smaller solvers that are written specifically for the type of project executed. For example, for
a steady-state, incompressible, turbulent, single-phase flow; the solver to be used is simpleFoam.
Even though each solver is designed for specific applications, they are all characterized by the
following features:
• Discretisation: polyhedral finite volume method which is second order in space and time.
• Parallelism in domain decomposition mode: the whole mesh can be split into bits and then
each bit is assigned to each core to save computation time.
• Components implementation in library form for easy re-use.
• Equation mimicking: implementation of the physics model necessary to solve the problem
(e.g. turbulence kinetic energy equation, conservation of mass, momentum and energy equa-
tions, etc..).
Thanks to the above-described components, OpenFoam can be applied to a wide range of prob-
lems. The package can solve some basic problems such as the Laplace equation, potential flow
equation and passive scalar/vector/tensor transport. Incompressible and compressible flow prob-
lems are also possible thanks to segregated pressure-based algorithms. RANS for turbulent flows
can also be calculated including 2-equation models, Reynolds turbulence stress model and large
eddy simulation.
2.2.1
Running OpenFoam
One of the advantages of OpenFoam is that it is possible to pick the single components necessary
to solve the governing equations of the problem. The basic components of OpenFoam can be
summarised as follow:
• Foundation libraries: discretization, mesh handling and other features in a re-usable form.
• Physical modeling libraries: thermo-physical models, viscosity models and turbulence mod-
els.
• Utilities: mesh import and manipulation, parallel processing, post-processor hook-up and
data manipulation.
• Customised, purpose-written and optimized executables for each physics segment.

Once geometry and physics are known, they need to be arranged in a specific system which is
described below.
Each case in OpenFoam must be organized and managed in a UNIX light-weight directory system
unlike in standard CFD packages where the case is stored in a file. The case directory can be
changed and re-read during a run which means the solution can be dynamically adjusted without
restarting the calculations. The case directory in OpenFoam is arranged as illustrated in figure 2.1.
Inside each case directory, there are three main components: the system, the constant and the time
directories.
Figure 2.1: Case directory structure
System directory
The system directory contains the items to control the simulation. For example, in the control
dictionary, the user can specify, among the rest, the start time, end time, time step, when to input
and when to output. In the fvSchemes directory, the discretization parameters for various equations
that the problem is solving can be specified. The fvSolution directory contains the solver-related
parameters.
Constant directory
In the constant directory, there are the bits that remain constant during the simulation, for example,
material properties, choice of turbulence model and computational mesh. Even the computational
mesh (polyMesh) is organized by components. The mesh’s points, cells, faces and boundaries are
defined in separate files. Nevertheless, the mesh components must agree with each other to compile
without errors. To verify if the mesh is wrong, the user can launch the checkMesh command in the
terminal window.
Time directories
The time directories typically start with the directory called 0 which is the initial field. In this
directory, the user can set the initial parameters including the pressure field, the velocity field
and the turbulent representation (k, omega, kinematic turbulence viscosity). Once the code starts,

further time directories which contain the field values at that specific iteration will be created. Each
new time folder is used to start the simulation for that point in time. The number of time folders
created can be specified in the system directory (control dictionary).
2.2.2
Extracting the data
Sampling, graphing and integral properties tools are present as part of foundation libraries. The
post-processing uses the same algorithms as the solver which means it is consistent with discretiza-
tion. There are different possible graphical post-processing tools, the most common one is Par-
aView. ParaView is a free-to-use data analysis and visualization application that is commonly
integrated with OpenFoam simulations. Each case can be opened in ParaView by launching the
command paraFoam in the terminal window. Once the case is correctly opened, the user can de-
cide to display the full mesh or individual parts of the mesh only. The final ice-covered surface
and the other calculated properties are visualized by selecting the last iteration in ParaView. The
mesh and the surface field for every iteration can also be exported as a comma-separated value file
(.csv) and plotted in external programs such as Matlab or Excel.
2.3
WISE code
WISE is a three-dimensional icing simulation code integrated into OpenFOAM. Previously devel-
oped icing simulation tools could not accurately calculate ice accretion of rotating turbines, since
for rotating surfaces the icing limits are not properly estimated. WISE has been spinned-off from
the fixed-wing aircraft icing tools ISEPAC (Ice Shape Evaluation and Performance Analysis Code)
[35] by considering rotating motion and flow transition instead. The rotational motion is explained
by a moving reference frame, meanwhile, the flow transition is simulated by applying the critical
Reynolds number instead of the high Reynolds numbers which are used for fully turbulent cases.
From the comparison against other numerical simulations, it was determined that for the rime ice
condition, icing limits, maximum thickness and location are well predicted by WISE as it can be
seen in figure 2.2. Despite this, for the glaze ice condition, the WISE code tends to underestimate
the thickness of ice on the pressure side [1].
Figure 2.2: WISE code validation [1]

2.3.1
Structure of WISE
WISE consists of four modules constructed on the OpenFOAM platform as listed in figure 2.3:
aerodynamic, droplet field, thermodynamic and grid generation modules. The moving reference
frame (MRF) is applied in the flow analysis and the droplet trajectory calculation. First, the aero-
dynamic module calculates the compressible and turbulent flow considering the rotation effect.
Second, the droplet field module simulates the behavior of droplets in the flow field by solving the
drag, gravity buoyancy, Coriolis and centrifugal forces. Third, the thermodynamic module obtains
the motion of thin water film by applying the MRF. Last, the grid generation module re-meshes
the 3D surface [1]. All the modules are run for every iteration until the final one is solved. The
total icing exposure time is split into several intervals. Each interval computes the four modules
under the steady-state assumption. Overall WISE can predict well ice accretion shapes under rime
icing conditions for 3D rotating wind turbines. The effects of structural vibrations and bending of
the blades due to gravity are neglected.

3
Methodology
The increased computational power availability translates into lower costs, which presents valid
alternatives to traditional physical tests. Another key trend is the increased size of wind turbines
which is making it more difficult to test a real-size airfoil in closed-environment conditions. Some
potential solutions are gathering data from real-life turbines or developing accurate icing simulation
tools. The former has the advantage of collecting reliable data which are nevertheless subjected to
the stochastic nature of weather conditions; in other words, one cannot easily set the weather and
design parameters. The latter can simulate virtually any weather and design parameters however it
requires to be confirmed by benchmark results. This chapter introduces the methodology followed
to investigate the icing of wind turbine blades using the WISE code. The goal is to compare the
2D results obtained from tests on small-scale wind turbine models at the wind tunnel of FORCE
technology. The process starts with the mesh generation which needs to be validated, testing the
WISE solver against some literature cases and eventually the simulation of new airfoil geometries
and meteorological scenarios.

3.1
Mesh Validation
To successfully run the icing simulation on OpenFOAM, it is necessary to generate a grid in a
CAD software. Many software like ANSYS, Star-CCM+, PointWise or Beta’s ANSA can serve
the purpose; in this project, it has been decided to generate the mesh in ANSYS since free academic
licenses are available to students of Denmark Technical University. The reliability of the results is
built on the quality of the mesh designed in the CAD software. To make sure the mesh refinement
is appropriate, the aerodynamic results must be compared to benchmark results. To achieve good
results, airfoil geometry, domain and mesh sizing must be aligned with the validation case. The
mesh in Fluent has then been designed with the aim of reproducing the grid used in the NASA
validation case [36]. The validation of the mesh accuracy is further described in the sections below.

3.1.1
Geometry and domain
The mesh accuracy is estimated by running an aerodynamic simulation and comparing the lift and
the drag coefficients from the NASA validation case [36]. The selected airfoil for the validation
is NACA0012 at a chord length of 1 m. The cross-section of the airfoil is placed at the center of
the domain which is located 100 chords away from the airfoil. The domain geometry is split into
6 different 2D faces to allow further refinement, the face split can be seen in figure 3.2. Resizing
is then applied along the edges of the six face splits. The resizing is defined according to a number
of divisions, moreover, a bias type is selected so that further divisions are applied around the rotor.
In other words, the mesh will be finer around the airfoil and coarser in the proximity of the domain
edges. These refinements are necessary to capture the viscous boundary layer accurately [36].
Similar to the validation case, the meshing method used is quadrilaterals. Compared to triangular
mesh, the quadrilateral meshes provide better results and retain better numerical stability. On the
other hand, a triangular mesh is easier to generate thanks to the automated mesh generator. The
resulting mesh is illustrated in figure 3.3 and 3.4. As it can be seen, NASA’s grid features a finer
cell base size which translates into higher accuracy and higher computational power required. In
the Fluent grid, a similar but slightly coarser grid is obtained, however, as it is presented later, this
did not critically affect the results.

5
Discussion
5.1
Mesh Validation
The first simulations were run for 500 iterations. More iterations yield a more accurate solution but
also require more computational time. This simulation produced good results however, for higher
angles the solution did not converge to the expected values. Increasing the number of iterations
to 1000 reduced the issue and showed more stable results also for higher AoA as can be seen in
picture 4.1. Overall, the lift coefficient is nicely predicted whereas the drag coefficient does not
seem to be predicted as nicely, however, it must be considered that drag results are plotted on a
smaller scale therefore small differences are magnified. Considering the small deviation from the
validation case, the generated mesh has been deemed valid to run the next simulations.
5.2
WISE Validation
As illustrated in figure 4.3, only case 4 yields accurate results from a single run. On the other hand,
cases 1, 2 and 3 are overestimating the ice accretions. Nevertheless, the ice is generated in the same
location, this suggests that the angle of attack and wind speed vector are well aligned in the code.
Overall, the simulations are closer to the validation cases for the rime ice cases. To attain better
results, the simulation is split into four different time steps to adjust the airfoil’s coordinates with
the updated ice contour. While case 4 was validated already with a single run simulation, cases
1 and 3 were re-run and yielded results closer to the reference even though they still do not fully
converge to the LEWICE contours. Case 3 compiled more precise ice contours whereas case 1 still
did not predict the ice horn shapes typical of glaze ice. It can be concluded that the code yields
better results for rime ice while further investigation is needed for glaze ice scenarios. Last, the
solver has been run for relatively high wind speeds (40 m/s) since for lower wind speeds (6 m/s)
no ice shapes could be generated even after 600 seconds of exposure time at low temperatures (-
20 °) and high liquid after content (0,6 g/m3). Considering each run takes a while to run it has
been decided to focus on rime ice only since for this type of ice more exact ice contours had been
computed.

6
Conclusion
Wind energy in cold climates is characterized by valuable wind resources which are usually unex-
ploited. To allow further accessibility in these areas it is important to investigate how to develop
designs that can sustain the typical icing phenomenon. This project investigated the application of
a numerical tool to predict ice accretions for different sets of meteorological conditions. For rime
ice conditions, the ice accretions have been approximated well. The scenarios simulated proved
the critical contributions of airfoil geometry and weather conditions. The angle of attack influ-
ences the aerodynamics of the blade by shifting the ice accretion along the leading edge which
in turn becomes thicker and longer. Next, the investigation of different chord lengths confirmed
the critical role ice can play especially for smaller turbines where the blades are characterized by
shorter airfoil chords. Last, meteorological parameters such as LWC and MVD affect the shape of
the ice which, depending on the case, increases the chord length or chord thickness. The project
also prompted potential future studies about:
• The solver proved its efficiency for rime ice application only whereas glaze ice conditions
need further investigation to be validated.
• Estimate how the performances of turbines are affected by specific ice shapes. Depending
on the icing pattern lift and drag coefficient vary in a different way.
• This study focused on LWC and MVD only since they are the most critical parameters how-
ever, wind speed, air temperature and air pressure also affect the ice accretions. The impact
of these parameters should also be understood to develop more reliable estimation mod-
els. Moreover, include more wind speed directions, as of now WISE solves for wind speed
approaching the leading edge which corresponds to parking mode for wind turbines. In con-
trast, wind turbines operate by shifting the airfoil to 90 ° which causes the wind speed to
approach from the pressure side instead.
• Extend the computing time of each simulation, this would result in more realistic ice contours
but such change will further extend the real-life time to solve the cases.
• Simulate 3D blade geometries to obtain results closer to real-life scenarios. Nevertheless,
to do so it is necessary to have more computing power available in order to complete the
simulations within a reasonable time.
