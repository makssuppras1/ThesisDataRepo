Abstract
The dextran oligomers between 5-8 kDa are employed for iron deficiency anaemia ther-
apy. The industrial production process is acid hydrolysis followed by ethanol precipitation.
This process presents several challenges: low product yield, and presence of contami-
nates downstream. A enzymatic membrane reactor (EMR) can address these issues.
The project investigated immobilisation of dextranase onto polymeric membranes; with
the ultimate goal of producing a EMR unit for oligodextran production. The main immo-
bilisation method employed is covalently bound enzymes via addition of glutaraldehyde
(GA) as cross-linker, upon a polydopamine layer (PDA).
Free enzyme kinetics were investigated, observing the Michaelis-Menten parameters un-
der increased temperature and the GA cross-linked enzyme. At T = 50 ◦C, the enzyme
maximum rate (Vmax) displayed a near 4-fold increase compared to the room temperature
counter part (from 0.22 to 0.8 μmol/min/mg), but also showed an increase in Km, being
0.25 ± 0.06 mmol/L from 0.05 ± 0.03 mmol/L, representing a decreased in perceived
affinity between substrate and enzyme. Upon GA cross-linking, the Michaelis-Menten
parameters appeared to be unaffected, but the overall activity greatly decreased.
A correlation analysis was done on the effects of dopamine concentration, and dopamine
incubation time in relation to the enzyme loading. Neither of the two parameters ap-
pears to greatly affect the enzyme loading, as all the results reported a loading between
15.0 ± 2.8 % (wt) and 17 ± 0.1 % (wt). The membranes tested were regenerated cel-
lulose (RC) and polyethersulfone (PES) with a molecular weight cutoff (MWCO) of 30
kDa, and it was found that the PES displayed an increased loading (17.0 ± 0.6 %(wt) to
7.5 ± 0.5 %(Wt) in loading efficiency).
Two interesting findings were observed with the covalently bound dextranases:
• A perceived modification in selection of the hydrolysed bond. The immobilised en-
zyme appeared to selectively produce isomaltose, contrary to the random behaviour
of the free enzyme.
• A lag in detection of enzymatic activity upon immobilisation. The products of hy-
drolysis become apparent between 60 to 120 minutes from the exposure to the
substrate.
It is hypothesised that these effects are as a result of substrate and product mass transfer
limitation.
Filtration-induced immobilisation showed improved behaviour compared to the covalently
bound enzymes: activity was detected after 15 minutes, and the overall activity was
higher. More interestingly, the perceived activity was comparable to the non-selective
behaviour of the free enzyme. Filtration-induced immobilisation, however displayed sta-
bility issues: after 24 hours, 26 % of the immobilised enzyme detached from the support.
Large water permeability losses post-immobilisation have been observed: the benchmark
and modified covalent protocols recorded water permeability losses of 82.6 ± 3.2 %, and
85.4 ± 0.5 %, respectively. The fouling-induce immobilisation appeared to be the most
severely affected, with a loss of 94.1 ± 1.4 %.
The results of this project would invite for further exploration in the modification of the
perceived enzyme behaviour. For future work, to decoupling the membrane from support
and separating unit would be recommended.

Project description and scope
Background
Dextrans are large homopolymers of D-glucose, predominately linked with α −1, 6 glyco-
sidic bonds. Dextrans and its derivatives have a wide array of employment in several no-
ticeable industrial sectors, such as pharmaceutical, medicinal, alimentary and cosmetic.
Low molecular weight dextrans (LMWDs) have shown potential in the medical field, specif-
ically in iron-deficiency anaemia treatments, however the larger dextran molecules induce
several undesirable side effects. The traditional mode of production of the LMWD is via
acid hydrolysis of large dextran molecules, with an order of magnitude of millions Dalton;
from hereon these large substrate molecules will be referred as macromolecular dextrans.
The acid hydrolysis poses several challenges to be addressed: a poor product yield, a
highly polydispersed product, high purification requirements, and additional chemical re-
quirements. These issues can be addressed via employing a hydrolysing enzyme, known
as dextranase.
This thesis will explore the opportunity of producing and employing an enzymatic mem-
brane reactor, specifically a biocatalytic membrane, to produce oligodextrans. This would
act as an in-situ product removal (ISPR) system, where the oligodextrans with the desired
molecular weight would permeate away from the reactive side of the membrane, prevent-
ing any further hydrolysis. Specifically, this project will explore the immobilisation of the
dextranase onto polymeric ultrafiltration (UF) membrane. The immobilisation of the en-
zyme is carried on a polydopamine (PDA) coated polyethersulfone (PES) UF membrane.
The enzyme is mixed with a cross-linker, being glutaraldehyde (GA), to increase retention
onto the support.
Goal
This project will therefore focus its scope on the following:
• Investigation of the free enzyme activity in a batch reaction system.
• Parameter optimization of enzyme immobilization onto the membrane. The project
will start with the optimisation of the polydopamine layer to maximise the enzyme
loading onto the membrane. The following steps will include analysis of the activity
of the immobilised dextranase, as well as the effects on the water permeability of
the membranes used as supports.
Saccharides and protein concentration will be carried out via UV-vis spectrophotometry,
with the 3,5-dinitrosalicylic acid (DNS) and Bradford reagent methods respectively. The
molecular weight distribution of the oligodextrans will be done via gel permeation chro-
matography (GPC).

Literature Study
1.1
Introduction
Dextrans are large homopolymers of D-glucose, predominately linked with α −1, 6 gluco-
sidic bonds. Dextrans have a wide array of employment in several noticeable industrial
sectors, such as pharmaceutical, medicinal, alimentary and cosmetic. Low molecular
weight dextrans (LMWDs) have shown potential in the medical field, specifically in iron
deficiency and anemia treatments. For the scope of this project, the target LMWD are cho-
sen to be in the molecular weight range between 5-8 kDa, or a degree of polymerisation
between 30 and 50.
The current production of LMWD is predominately done via acid hydrolysis of large native
dextrans (from Leuconostoc mesenteroides NRRL B-512F), and ethanol precipitation to
separate and re-hydrolyse the largest dextran molecules. This method requires additional
chemical compounds, provides a low yield, and it requires an extremely thorough separa-
tion process to provide a clinical-grade product. Additionally, the molecules are provided
over a broad molecular weight distribution, increasing the requirements on the fractional
alcohol precipitation purification. As an alternative to acid to hydrolyse the large dextrans
is the use of enzymes, namely dextranases (E.C. number 3.2.1.11). The employment of
these enzymes would potentially lead to a more environmentally sustainable process and
milder operation conditions, however would still leave several issues: the random hydrol-
ysis of the glycosidic bonds, poor enzyme stability outside narrow operation conditions
and complicated separations further downstream if a free-enzyme system is employed.
The design of an enzymatic membrane rector, where enzymes would be attached to the
feed would address these two issues; potentially act as ISPR (in situ product removal), re-
moving the oligodextrans with the desired molecular weight, hence preventing any further
hydrolysis, then increasing the overall yield of the process. This would potentially lead
to narrow the molecular weight distribution of the oligodextrans, while removing poten-
tially potentially harmful compounds from the process, and streamlining the downstream
process to follow. Additionally immobilisation could improve the enzyme stability and re-
cyclability within the process. The enzyme immobilization, however, comes with its own
set of challenges: predominately fouling of the membrane, loss of enzyme activity upon
immobilisation and rate matching between the hydrolysis and the permeate flux.

1.2
Dextran
The industrial and academic interest in glucose-based homopolymers is well established
(De Belder 1993; Khalikova, Susi, and Korpela 2005; Naessens et al. 2005; Heinze et al.
2006).
Among polyglucans, polymers composed of glucose monomers, one of the most well
known is cellulose: where the D-glucose monomers are linked by β −1, 4 glycosidic bond.
A second well known polyglucan is starch: a homopolymer with a backbone of α −1, 4
glycosidic bonds, and branchings formed with α−1, 6 bonds (Heinze et al. 2006). Among
the industrially significant glucans is there is another one: the dextran.
Dextrans (DXT) are a class of homopolymers of D-glucose, predominately linked with
α −1, 6 glucosidic bonds. Dextans have a wide array of employment in several notice-
able industrial sectors, such as pharmaceutical, medicinal, alimentary and cosmetic. Low
molecular weight dextrans (LMWDs) have shown potential in the medical field, specifically
in iron deficiency and anemia treatments. Dextran 70 kDa is on the ”WHO Model List of
Essential Medicines” in 2019, as the current best pharmaceutical candidate for plasma
substitutes (WHO 2019).
The investigation on dextrans started in 1861, when Pasteur identified a slime-producing
bacteria, later on named by van Tieghem Leuconostoc mesenteroides in 1878. The iso-
lated polyglucan was then named ”dextran” by Scheiber, due to compound being dex-
trorotatory under optical rotatory polarisation (Naessens et al. 2005; Dhaneshwar et al.
2006). More dextran-producing microbial hosts were later on discovered, as in 1910 the
Bacillus mesentericus was observed by Beijerinck. By 1941, Hehre provided more rig-
orous evidence for the dextran synthesis in the Leuconostoc genus. Extraction studies
of the enzyme reasonable for dextran synthesis, the dextrasucrase, were performed by
Erbert in 1962, and later on followed up by Robyt and his colleagues (De Belder 1993).
Since then, dextran synthesis on industrial scale is only carried through dextransucrase
(Parlak, Ustek, and Tanriseven 2013; Ramawat and Mérillon 2015).
Dextrans are widely solubile in aqueous envirmoment, additionally to several solvents
as formamide and DMSO. Dextrans are physiologically harmless, as it is biocompatible,
biodegradable, and are they present no immunogenic or antigenic properties (Heinze et
al. 2006; Ramawat and Mérillon 2015).
Another key set of parameters is that dextrans are falling under the definition of a ”green”
compound: dextrans are produced in a sustainable process from renewable resources,
and they can be simply disposed, as readily degradable in the ecological system (Naessens
et al. 2005; Leemhuis et al. 2013).

1.2.1
Properties
A challenge in defining universally the properties of this class of polyglucans is that the
term ”dextran” is an umbrella concept for any saccharide with a backbone composed of
α −1, 6 glycosidic bond. An image of what a generic dextran is offered in fig. 1.1. It is
important to make some differentiation among some the key parameters, that drastically
influence the bulk of the physico-chemical properties of the molecules.
The key parameters to observe are the following:
• Glycosidic bond quality
• Molecular weight and structure
• Dispersity
Many properties are dependant on the structure and molecular weight (or degree of poly-
merisation).

Glycosidic bond quality
The physico-chemical properties are greatly dependant on the quality of the bonds. For
instance, solubility in water is greatly affected by the degree of branching; as branch-
ing increases solubility decreases too (Khalikova, Susi, and Korpela 2005; Heinze et al.
2006). Certain bonds are water soluble, such as α −1, 6, and others as α −1, 3 are wa-
ter insoluble (Ramawat and Mérillon 2015). Dextrans are characterised by a main-chain
α−1, 6 linkage, and additional short side-branching. The percentile fraction of α−1, 6 can
vary between 50 to 97 % of the total glycosidic bonds. Side-branching bonds can vary in
nature, however, the more common branches are linked via α −1, 2, α −1, 3, and α −1, 4
bonds. A simple representation of these bonds can be seen in fig. 1.1.
The branching frequency and bond type is heavily dependant on the microbial strand
generating the dextransucrase. Dextran molecules have been found to be produced by
at least 96 bacterial strains, as reported by the first large classification work by Jeanes
et al. 1954; and more microbes have been found to be able to produce dextransucrase
since. There is a large discrepancy in frequencies of the different glycosidic bonds, and
subsequently the micro and macro structures of the dextrans (Heinze et al. 2006).
It is a key factor to determine the type of dextran that is being employed: for the extent
of this project, the dextran originates from the microbial host Leuconostoc mesenteroides
NRRL B-512F. There are several reasons behind this selection: predominantly the fact
that L.m. NRRL B-512F is the current industry standard for dextran production (Leemhuis
et al. 2013), additionally a large body of academic research is established specifically in
relation to this strand (Jeanes et al. 1954; Khalikova, Susi, and Korpela 2005; Heinze
et al. 2006; Leemhuis et al. 2013).
The dextran from (Leuconostoc mesenteroides NRRL B-512F) has 95 % of its bonds
being α−1, 6, and the remaining side-branches are limited to one or two monomeric units
(Vollmer et al. 2009; Heinze et al. 2006), and are predominately α −1, 3 bonds.
Molecular weight and structure
Molecular weight and structure are two key parameters for the application of the dextrans.
The molecular structure is dependant on the size of the dextran (Granath 1958; Antonini
et al. 1964; De Belder 1993; Rolland-Sabaté et al. 2008).
An alternative mode to define molecular weight is via degree of polymerisation (DP). The
simplest definition for DP is the number of repeating monomeric units in a linear homopoly-
mer. This assumption is justified as the linear backbone represents 95 % of the molecule.
Hence, DP can be approximated as follows:
DP =

MWRepeating Unit
(1.1)
For the specific case of glucans, such as dextrans, the repeating unit would be a glucose
unit minus a water molecule, leading to the brute formula of C6O5H10, and an approximate
molecular weight of 162 g/mol, hence this project’s target oligodextrans are in the DP
range of approximately 30 to 50. This approximation is justified under the assumption that
the studied dextrans are predominantly linear molecule in the MW of this work, and that
for the specific dextran employed, branching is very weak. When using native dextran, it
would be recommended to correct the degree of polymerisation to a more accurate model,
as for an hyper-branching structure.
As previously established, the monomer is D-glucose, and the dimer is isomaltose. In-
creasing the DP, the dextrans are arranged in a rod-like (or ellipsoidal) configuration up

to a DP of 12 monomeric units, or 2000 Da (Gekko 1981; Heinze et al. 2006). A rod-like
configuration is defined by a high degree of stiffness (Kontogeorgis and Kiil 2016). Above
the molecular weight of 2000 Da, the dextrans starts to show increasing flexibility, and
undergo a geometric reconfiguration and start to fold into a random coil structure (Gekko
1981; De Belder 1993). With the increasing molecular weight, the dextrans will behave
as in a coil structure, more extended at lower molecular weights, and more compact at
higher molecular weights (Granath 1958). It is understood that below a degree of poly-
merisation of 50, the dextrans are behaving predominately linearly, with no discernible
interference from the branching (Rolland-Sabaté et al. 2008). Hence at the molecular
weight of 8 kDa branching starts to be observed, and the short side branching lead the
molecule to assume a comb-like structure. The statement is corroborated by the experi-
ment performed by Granath 1958, where it was found that the degree of branching was
rapidly decreasing when observing the MW range from 20 kDa to 10 kDa. As the molec-

1.2.2
Applications
The range of application of dextran is much varied, predominantly depending on the
molecular size.
High molecular weight dextran
High molecular weight dextrans (HMWD), with a molar weight above 100 kDa, are outside
the clinical range. A common side-effect in introducing colloids in the blood stream is the
issue of hypersensitivity reactions, which could potentially lead to the death of the patients
(Iqbal et al. 2017). Dextran induced anaphylactic reactions (DIAR) were a common oc-
currence in the early employment of dextrans: this was correlated to the larger degree of
polymerisation having a larger amount of non α −1, 6 bonds, which in return would lead
to a higher incidence of adverse reactions (Hussain et al. 2013; Naessens et al. 2005).
Native macromolecular dextrans, with MW in the millions of Daltons, are employed as
ingredients for cosmetic and bakery, due to moisturising properties. In frozen dairy prod-
ucts, native dextrans have been shown to improve heat shock capacity, increasing the
melting point of products (McCurdy et al. 1994).
Low molecular weight dextran
LMWD find application in a clinical setting, with the molecular weights of 40, 60 and 70
kDa being the most employed members of the class. These act as substitutes for blood
proteins, such as human serum albumin (HSA), to increase the colloid osmotic pressure
and displacing water from the interstitial space in the plasma fraction of the blood. The
advantage of dextrans as a volume expander is that 1 g of the polysaccharide will contain
about 20 mL of water, hence increasing the plasma volume (Naessens et al. 2005). Dex-
tran 70 is a WHO 2019 essential medicine, reported as the sole compound in its category
(WHO 2019). Additionally, high dextrans with a high percentage of α−1, 6 glycosidic bond,
such as the dextrans produced from Lm NRRL B-512(F), shows high biological stability
into the blood, due to the low antigenicity and high water solubility (Heinze et al. 2006).
Another factor to account is that the dextrans above 55 kDa are too large to be expelled
via ultrafiltration in the kidneys, and require to be hydrolysed in the liver or spleen (Larsen
1989). Antithrombotic effects are also employed on a prophylactic basis, for deep venous
thrombosis and post operative pulmonary emboli. Dextran 40 kDa increases blood flow;
it is thought that it is due to the reduction of blood viscosity and inhibition of erythrocyte
aggregation (Heinze et al. 2006).
Another advantage on dextrans with a high percentile of α−1, 6 glycosidic linkages is that
the native enzymes in humans are able to hydrolyse the bond slowly, contrary to α −1, 4
bonds (in starch and glycogen), which are broken at a much faster rate; his increases the
stability of the dextrans in blood (Naessens et al. 2005).
LMWD can also be employed as precursors for other compounds, such as ethers, sulfates
(5 to 500 kDa), phosphates (MW 40 kDa) or esters (about 5-50 kDa). A common dextran
derivative is obtained from reacting the homopolymer (MW 6 kDa) with epichlorohydrin
in an alkaline solution to produce a cross-link chains between the dextrans (Heinze et al.
2006). The product is a gel employed as a molecular sieve, and the original commercial
name was Sephadex. This acts as a medium in size exclusion chromatography columns
(Naessens et al. 2005). In the medical analytic field, specified LMWD magnetites complex
have been studied and shown promise as contrast agents to improve magnetic resonance
imaging (Magin et al. 1991; Naessens et al. 2005).

Oligosaccharides
Oligosaccharides, and isomalto-oligosaccharides among them, containing between 2 to
20 monomeric glucose units, can act as prebiotics. A prebiotic is a compound that is not
digested in the small intestine but it is metabolised by specific members of the bacterial
flora (Pinelo, Jonsson, and Meyer 2009). Oligodextrans are defined by both P. Monsan,
Paul, et al. 1987; Goulas et al. 2004 as dextrans with molecular weight below 20 kDa.
Oligosaccharides with too low molecular weight is removed quickly from the primary cir-
culation system by the kidneys, making them poor blood stabilisers (R. Alsop and G.
Vlachogiannis 1982). Dextrans of MW around 1000 Da find use in the clinical setting as
haptens; being a pre-injection to limit hypersensitivity reactions before the larger clinical
dextrans are administered (Heinze et al. 2006).
This project is focused on the key medical application of dextrans is in relation to intra-
venously administered iron-dextran complex, for the treatment of aneamia. The intra-
venous mode of administration bypasses the challenge in the oral administration, where
the gastrointestinal tract struggles to effectively uptake the iron, due to limited iron ad-
sorption capacity (Naessens et al. 2005; Ramawat and Mérillon 2015). Iron-dextran com-
plexes have an established role as an intravenous treatment for iron-deficiency anemia,
with a particular interest in low molecular weight between 6 to 8 kDa (Hussain et al. 2013;
Gan et al. 2014; Y. Q. Zhang et al. 2017; Huang et al. 2019).

Native dextran synthesis
As previously reported, LMWDs have found many applications and are a necessity in
several industries, particularly in the clinical setting. The production of these LMWDs
is not a simple one: currently it is not possible to produce LMWDs via direct synthesis
at the desired molecular weight. The current mode of production is to produce the na-
tive macromolecules of dextran and then hydrolyse them (Khalikova, Susi, and Korpela
2005; Kothari and Goyal 2013). The choice of producing large molecules of dextran and
then hydrolyse them is due to the improved control over the reaction parameters over
the ”bottom-up” fermentation approach (Falconer, Mukerjea, and Robyt 2011; Ramawat
and Mérillon 2015). The native dextran production is carried out via enzymatic catalysis,
where the enzyme is referred as dextransucrase.
Dextransucrase (E.C. 2.4.1.5) sole industrial enzyme employed for commercial produc-
tion of dextrans. Commercially, Leuconostoc mesenteroides NRRL B-512(F) or B-512
are the two strain cultivated for the production of the dextransucrase on a commercial
scale (De Belder 1993; Monchois and Pierre Monsan 1999; Leemhuis et al. 2013; Parlak,
Ustek, and Tanriseven 2013; Ramawat and Mérillon 2015). Leuconostoc mesenteroides
is recognised as a GRAS-status organism (Naessens et al. 2005). One of the early sur-
veys on the α −1, 6 bonds producing bacterial organisms, has found that 96 strands are
able to function as host, with different quality when looking at the percentage of the α−1, 6
bonds (Jeanes et al. 1954). The largest fraction of the bacterial strands fell under the label
of lactic acid bacteria (LAB). Each bacterial strand produces a unique dextransucrase en-
zyme, leading to different dextrans, when observing degree of polymerisation and quality
of linkages (Jeanes et al. 1954; Vettori, Franchetti, and Contiero 2012; Kothari and Goyal
2013; Ramawat and Mérillon 2015).
Dextrans fall under the category of extracellular polysaccharides, or exopolysaccharides
(EPS) (Leemhuis et al. 2013). The glucansucrases, as the dextransucrase would be, are
usually excreted by the cell and allowed to operate on the sucrose molecule extracellularly
(Badel, Bernardi, and Michaud 2011), this is a key factor in the production of the dextran
macromolecules, as it streamlines the upstream production.
The current industrial mode of production is to cultivate L. mesenteroides, and separate
the extracellularly secreted dextransucrase. In both the cellular growth phase and dextran
production phase, sucrose is employed as the substrate (Ramawat and Mérillon 2015;
Leemhuis et al. 2013; Majumder et al. 2009). The current mode is to employ fermenta-
tion, and much work has been done to optimise the operational parameters (Majumder
et al. 2009). Efforts have been done to attempt to produce oligosaccharides from immo-
bilised dextransucrase, however substrate mass transfer appeared to be a large factor in
hindering the process, and enzyme activity (P. Monsan and Lopez 1981).
The dextransucrase acts a catalyst for two separate reactions: the hydrolysis of su-
crose, and the polymerization of the glucan chain via α −1, 6 glycosidic bonds. The
dextransucrase reaction has been identified to follow the double-displacement mecha-
nism (Barker, Ganetsos, and Ajongwen 1993; Monchois and Pierre Monsan 1999; Ito
et al. 2011; Leemhuis et al. 2013; Ramawat and Mérillon 2015). The first stage of this
reaction is the cleaving of the α −1, 2 link in the sucrose, leading to a release of fructose
in a 1:1 molar ratio. Then a glucosylenzyme intermediate is formed, followed by poly-
merization reaction to the non-reducing end of the growing glucan, linked via the dextran
bond. A schematic diagram of the dextransucrase activity is offered in fig. 1.3.

media, salts, and additional organic matter. Then, the purified enzyme is allowed to op-
erate directly on the sucrose feedstock, with a higher productivity, yield and operational
compared to the 1-stage fermentation.

1.3
LMWD production
As previously reported, LMWD show the properties required to be employed in the clinical
setting, and the current most feasible option is to hydrolyse the native dextran to the
desired MW. This process presents several technical challenges, predominately linked to
the two following parameters:
• Degree of polymerisation
• Dispersity
The degree of polymerisation of the product is dependant on the time of reaction. This
implies that there is the possibility to over-hydrolyse the native dextran outside the de-
sired DP range (30-50 monomeric units for this project). Over-hydrolysed product is to be
accounted as waste, and resulting in a yield loss.
Additionally, the hydrolysis reaction is non-selective: all the α −1, 6 glycosidic bonds can
be subject to hydrolysis. This leads for the product to be under a wide range of MW. This
furthers complexifies the reaction system, as random reactions will increase dispersity,
while the target product requires an extremely narrow range in molar weight.
Compared to peptide/oligopeptide hydrolysis, oligosaccharides are more complicated to
produce, due to the lack of stereospecificity of the enzymatic hydrolysis on the saccha-
rides, hence a much larger number of combinations available based on the location of the
hydrolysed bond (Delattre and Vijayalakshmi 2009).
Currently, there are two modes to obtain LMWD: via acid or enzymatic hydrolysis.
1.3.1
Acid hydrolysis
Acid hydrolysis is the commercially predominant process to break down the α −1, 6 gly-
cosidic bond in native dextran (Gan et al. 2014). Industrially, the native dextran’s α −1, 6
bond can be hydrolysed with any mineral acid (Dhaneshwar et al. 2006), with the preferred
industrial compounds being hydrochloric and sulfuric acid (Heinze et al. 2006).
The traditional acid hydrolysis process for the production of LMWD was established and
widely employed since the 1950’s, with little to no modifications (Kim, Seo, and Day 1996).
Acid hydrolysis is performed to obtain the LMWD fractions, and the size control is carried
via partial ethanol precipitation. Subsequently, further purification is employed with dis-
tillation and ion-exchange resin separation and spray-drying unit (Barker, Ganetsos, and
Ajongwen 1993).
Earlier studies accounted the acid hydrolysis to be fully random, meaning that all the
α −1, 6 bonds had the same chance of being hydrolysed (Antonini et al. 1964). Later
on, Basedow, Ebert, and Ederer 1978 reported that acid hydrolysis behaves differently
from random. In the specific case, sulfuric acid was used, and the dextran was originated
from L.m. 512F. They found that the reaction rate in the acidic hydrolysis is related to
the power of 2/3 of the MW of the molecule, and that the rate constant for the cleavage
are dependant on the location on the polymeric chain. This implies that the acid has a
higher rate of hydrolysis at the terminal bonds of the molecule over the central bonds; this
leads to greater losses of product, as oligodextrans smaller than the target MW are more
likely to occur. A partial justification is suggested by Basedow and colleagues, where
disentanglement diffusion is partially responsible for the variation from random behaviour
(Basedow, Ebert, and Ederer 1978; Basedow 1980).
There are several issues with acid hydrolysis: low product yield (10-12 %) due to the
quasi-random behaviour (Basedow, Ebert, and Ederer 1978; Khalikova, Susi, and Kor-

1.3.2
Enzymatic hydrolysis
As an alternative to acid to hydrolyse the native dextrans is the use of enzymes, namely
dextranases (DN). These enzymes have shown some potential for application; so much
so that the academic community since the first reports about dextranases (being from
the Cellvibrio fulva host), an excess of 1’500 scientific papers have been published on
the subject, and more than 100 of patents have been filed (Khalikova, Susi, and Korpela
2005).
The selection of the enzyme is based on endodextranases, which selectively attack the
inner bonds of the molecule (formally the name of the family of enzymes is 1, 6 −α-D-
glucan-6-glucanohydrolase, with the E.C. number 3.2.1.11) (Virgen-Ortíz et al. 2015). The
selection of the hydrolysed bond appears to be random (Basedow 1980; Khalikova, Susi,
and Korpela 2005; Virgen-Ortíz et al. 2015). The Penicillium sp. endodextranases’ ac-
tion pattern can be summarised with the following: α,1-6 is the optimal cleavage point,
isomaltose is the minimum digestion domain on the back bone, and the final product will
be the isomaltose dimer (Pleszczyńska et al. 1997; Yi et al. 2015).
From a commercial prospective, endodextranses are predominately looked from fungal
hosts; historically, no interest has been shown from higher organisms, due to the in-
creased complications in growing mammalian or plant cells when comparing to fungi (Kha-
likova, Susi, and Korpela 2005). Additionally, for the specific role of producing LMWDs,
dextranases hydrolysing the endo-bonds represent a potential for higher yield compered
to the exo-bond hydrolysis.
The specific enzyme properties are dependant on the microbial host producing the dextran-
hydrolysing enzyme. The preferred microbial host is mold fungi:they show improved ac-
tivities compared to bacteria and yeasts, and molds usually secrete the enzyme extracel-
lularly, simplifying the downstream operations (Garcia et al. 1996; Khalikova, Susi, and
Korpela 2005). In order to allow dextranases to become an alternative to the acid hydroly-
sis on the industrial scale, it is paramount that the enzyme selected display a high activity
and a strong stability in operational conditions. Enzymatic hydrolysis has potential for sim-
pler equipment, a decrease in energy consumption for operation and a more uniform final
product (Khalikova, Susi, and Korpela 2005). The employment of enzymatic hydrolysis
has the potential to increase the overall productivity of the hydrolysis step (Kim and Day
1994). The general understanding of enzymatic processes is that they are able to be con-
ducted under mild operation conditions (being around ambient temperature, atmospheric
pressure and physiological pH) , with high selectivity and competitive rates (Sheldon and
Pelt 2013). Additionally, high pressure appears not to affect most enzymes (Vennestrøm
et al. 2010).
Endodextranases are found into 2 families of GH families: 49 and 66. Dextran from Peni-
cillium sp. and Arthrobacter sp. and additional bacterial strands are part of the 49 family
(Khalikova, Susi, and Korpela 2005). Suzuki et al. 2012 found that the family of GH 66
employs a double displacement mechanisms (classical Koshland retaining mechanism),
hence exhibiting a different mode if action from the GH 49. For the extent of this project,
the enzyme employed is a member of the GH family 49, specifically from the Penicillium
species.

Comparison of acid and enzyme hydrolysis
In several publications it was found that the enzyme hydrolysis is less polydisperse com-
pared to the acid counterpart.
Charles and Farrel 1957 did a comparative study of an enzyme from Penicillium lilag-
inum against acid hydrolysis. The overall product yield of the enzyme was found to be
between 55 to 60 %, larger than hydrolysis via hydrochloric acid, as the first precipitated
fraction showed a partial recovery (about 20% for the enzyme and negligible for the acid).
Basedow 1980 found that the dextran hydrolysis via dextranase from Penicillium funiculo-
sum produced less over-hydrolysed molecules compared to acid hydrolysis. This implies
a more monodisperse value for the enzyme; Basedow accounted this phenomena to the
configurational mobility of the enzyme, which minimised the diffusion limitation in disentan-
glement from the catalyst to the product. Iqbal et al. 2017 performed a direct comparison
between acid and enzymatic hydrolysis. It is important to point out that the dextranase
employed was from the GH 66 (Bacillus megatertum), hence using a retaining mode of
action, different from the one of the enzyme studied in this project. Usually, it is found
that GH 49 enzymes outperform member of the GH 66 family (Pleszczyńska et al. 1997).
The main findings in Iqbal et al. 2017 were that the rate of reaction was faster for the acid,
however showed a broader fractionation range. The enzyme hydrolysis was found to be
less polydisperse, hence incurring in reduced downstream requirements.
Another investigated advantage of enzymatic hydrolysis is the apparent discrimination in
the rate of reaction, based on the molecular weight of the substrate.
Basedow 1980 observed that below 5 kDa, the enzymatic (Dextranase from Penicillium
funiculosum) reaction ceases to be linear with MW, and considerably slows down, sug-
gesting that the molecules below 5 kDa are negatively discriminated in the enzyme selec-
tion. This statement is also corroborated by Gan et al. 2014; it was found that dextranase
(from Penicillium aculeatum) preferentially degraded high molecular weight dextran. The
experiment carried introduced two dextran solutions of different average molecular weight
(DXT A approximately 50 kDa, DXT B approximately 6 kDa). The dextrans were mixed
and the dextranase was added, resulting into a rapid decrease of the molecular weight of
A and a minor shift of DXT B. This molecular weight discrimination by the enzymes has
been confirmed by Yang, Zhou, and Tian 2018, with the team’s investigation of dextranase
from Chaetomium globosum. Even more relevantly to the Penicillium sp dextranases, an
increased affinity (lower Km values) of the enzymes for larger dextran molecules was ob-
served by Huang et al. 2019.
Historically, the largest commercial field of dextranase employment was in the sugar in-
dustry (Wynter et al. 1997; Eggleston and Monge 2005; Jiménez 2009). The quality of
sugar product is related to the sucrose content (Bhatia et al. 2016). In this sector, dextran
is a contaminating product, resulting in an increase of viscosity of the sugar solutions,
increase in insoluble particle aggregation, and a decrease of overall yield of the sucrose
production. Viscosity and solubility issues represent a challenge for the following filtration
and crystallisation units, leading to a significant impoverishment of the process (Jiménez
2009). These molecules appear as a result of microbial contamination, such as members
of the Leuconostoc genus.

dextranases’ activity (Wynter et al. 1997; Jiménez 2009; El-Tanash, El-Baz, and Sherief
2011; Virgen-Ortíz et al. 2015; Y. Q. Zhang et al. 2017). For the high stability at high
temperature and over a large pH range, Chaetomium erraticum dextranase became the

Limitations in enzyme hydrolysis
Two main factors that prevented enzymes to be widely employed in industry: long-term
stability of the protein, and issues in recovery and reuse of the enzyme (Sheldon and Pelt
2013).
Dextranases still suffer several limitations for employment (even if extensive applications
of dextranases are present) are: poor enzymatic stability in harsh conditions leading to
a loss in enzymatic activity, and difficulty of dextranase separation and re-employment
from the solution (increasing separation costs and decreasing product quality). Hence
increasing the dextranase stability and reusability are paramount for industrial applications
(Y. Wang et al. 2018).
Enzyme membrane reactors have the potential to address both of these issues (Tufvesson
et al. 2010; Di Cosimo et al. 2013; Sheldon and Pelt 2013; Y. Wang et al. 2018).

1.4
Membrane Technology
Membrane technology is not an uncommon presence in current industrial scenario, and in
the specific instance of oligosaccharides production, there are many opportunities. Mem-
brane technology holds several advantages compared to conventional separation oper-
ation units (e.g. chromatographic separation techniques), and it is prospected as the
most likely separation technology for oligosaccharides in the future. The advantages of
membrane technology are: a low energy operational requirements, simple operational pa-
rameters handling (e.g. pressure, temperature, feed flow, agitation), and relatively easy
scale-up and scale-out (Pinelo, Jonsson, and Meyer 2009).
Specifically for LMWD production, membranes provide an excellent opportunity to frac-
tionate the oligosaccharides via the size-exclusion principle.
For the extent of this project, a micro-porous ultrafiltration membrane is employed as
support for the enzymatic immobilisation.
Porous membranes can be widely categorised in several aspects: material, pore size,
modular configuration, and operational configuration (Baker 2004).
The selection of the membrane material is fundamental. High water permeability (LP)
are required within the industrial setting, and this parameter is highly dependant on the
hydrophobicity/hydrophilicity of the membrane material. Additionally, the material will in-
teract with the solute and a favourable relationship is required for commercial use.
The pore size, or molecular weight cut off (MWCO), represents the average size of the
pore, determining whether solute will be able to pass through or not. It is important to
recognise that this value will represent a Gaussian representation of the pore size, larger
and smaller pores will be present.
Traditionally there are four modular configurations; these being the following: hollow fibre,
spiral wound, tubular and frame and plate. Depending on the requirements of the specific
operation, the module will change, however the more common ones are hollow fibre and
the spiral wound, due to their high surface area to packing volume ratio.
The two operations are dead-end or cross-flow. The dead-end (or head-on) is simply a
flow going perpendicular to the membrane and the solute maintaining the same direc-
tionality. This leads to the most significant issues in membrane technology to become
rapidly apparent: fouling and concentration polarisation. Cross flow is able to mitigate the
issue to some extent, via employing a tangential flow to minimise cake layering formation
(Mulder 1991; Baker 2004).
In this project dead-end filtration is the configuration employed, as it provides the easier
set up. Due to the ease of the experimental set up, it is also the favoured conformation for
enzyme reactions and membrane filtration are empirically researched (Pinelo, Jonsson,
and Meyer 2009).

1.4.1
Ultrafiltration
Ultrafiltration has pores in the range of 10-1000 Å(Baker 2004). The current landscape
sees the predominat size of the UF membranes being of polymeric stamp (cellulose based
or PES), but ceramic-based membranes are founding employment, specifically in areas
where polymeric membranes struggle (e.g. at high temperature, or high-cleaning require-
ments with aggressive cleaning compounds).
Ultrafiltration membranes are predominantly anisotropic structures made by the Loeb-
Sourirajan process. The structure of a UF membrane would be a finely porous skin layer
(giving the selectivity properties of the membrane) and a much less restrictive structural
layer (giving structural support to the membrane). The separation principle is based on a
size discrimination (molecular weight of the solute) but several additional factors must be
accounted; such as the molecular shape of the solute (Mulder 1991).
Research of UF for dextrans as a separation and concentration mode has been reported
in academic studies. Barker, R. M. Alsop, and G. J. Vlachogiannis 1984 successfully man-
aged to concentrate dextran 40 kDa solutions from 20 to 350 g/L, via using ultrafiltration
membranes (in both flat disks configuration and hollow-fibre). Additionally, separation
of the lower molecular weight fraction dextrans was achieved. Balmann and Nobrega
1989 studied the use of UF for dextran fractionation and concentration. Without applying
a trans-membrane pressure(∆P), hence allowing diffusion alone to be the mass trans-
fer mechanism, De Bruijn, Salazar, and Bórquez 2005 observed the fouling behaviour of
dextrans of different sizes. They studied the permeation model of two separate dextran
solution, one with 70 kDa and the second with 2000 kDa; the mode of operation was
cross-flow. They observed that cake formation to be the dominant type of fouling. Addi-
tionally, it was observed that increasing the ∆P, for the 70 kDa molecules, in addition to
the cake layer formation, internal pore blocking became a significant issue. This was in
accordance with the general consensus that the highly flexible smaller dextran molecules
are able to permeate through the membrane even if above the MWCO of the membrane.
For this experiment, this value was 30 kDa.

1.5
Enzymatic membrane reactor
The concept of enzymatic membrane reactor (EMR) is based on in-situ product removal
(ISPR) design. This allows the theoretical coupling of a reaction unit and a separation
operation in one single operational unit.
There are two main types of EMR (Jochems et al. 2011):
• Free enzyme membrane reactor: this mode employs the membrane solely as a
barrier. The membrane contains in the feed-side the large enzymes, and in the
specific case of oligodextran production, the dextran molecules with a molecular
weight above the MWCO. Only the oligodextrans with the desired molecular weight
would be able to leave the reactive unit via the membrane.
• Immobilised enzyme membrane reactor: the role of the membrane in this mode
is dual, as it acts both as a selective barrier and as a support for the enzymes. In
this system, the enzymes are anchored onto the membrane an, where they catalyse
the desired reaction. The separation mode would still occur as in the free system.
In literature this type of membrane is also reported as a biocatalytic membrane.

1.5.2
Immobilised enzyme membrane reactor
One of the more pressing issues with free enzyme membrane reactors is the struggle to
keep a stable enzyme-substrate ratio, which in return allow for reduced and decreasing
reaction rates (Pinelo, Jonsson, and Meyer 2009). Others issues with free enzymes are:
denaturation under thermal and pH ranges, catalyst leakage and denaturation due to
adsorbtion/deposit on the membrane (Paolucci-Jeanjean, Belleville, and Gilbert M. Rios

separation of the product. Immobilisation would render the enzymatic handling simpler,
from an operational stand-point, having the catalyst in a different phase (solid from liquid)

minimal or null presence of protein. A key advantage that immobilisation presents is the
possibility to allow for continuous operation designs, as the catalyst would be immobilised
on a support. Another advantage is the increased stability of the enzymes, in both oper-
ational and storage scenarios (less prone to heat degradation or organic solvent induced
denaturation or autolysis) (Sheldon and Pelt 2013).
Addressing the issues of enzymatic stability and recyclability, enzymatic immobilisation
onto a solid carrier is considered a viable solution (Sheldon and Pelt 2013; Di Cosimo

Enzyme Immobilisation
There have been many attempts to immobilise dextranases throughout the years (Cheetham
and Richards 1973; Madhu and Prabhu 1985; Rogalski et al. 1997; Abdel-Naby et al.
1999; Yakup and Aziz 2007; Erhardt and Jördening 2007; El-Tanash, El-Baz, and Sherief
2011; Luo et al. 2013; Bertrand et al. 2014; Gibriel et al. 2014; H. Zhang et al. 2018; Y.
Wang et al. 2018; Su et al. 2018). One of the main issues in enzyme immobilisation is
that no universal standard guideline structure is employed, therefore progress is achieved
via trial and error (Sheldon and Pelt 2013). Prior to explain the selection choices for this
project, a brief introduction in the enzymatic immobilisation is given.
1.6.1
Enzymatic immobilisation techniques
There are many different modes of immobilisation, they can broadly classified in 3 cate-
gories: physico-chemical bonding, enzymatic entrapment and cross-linking.
The physico-chemical bonding includes a quite wide array of options. Predominately any
at all form of direct attachment of the enzyme onto the carry. The forces employed can be
physical (Van der Waals forces or hydrophobic interaction), or of chemical nature, such
as ionic or covalent bonds (Sheldon and Pelt 2013). Examples of physical bonding could
be fouling induced immobilisation (Luo et al. 2013; Luo et al. 2014; Su et al. 2018).
Enzymatic entrapment requires the enzyme to be entrapped via being included into a
polymeric matrix (can be either organic or inorganic, e.g. polyacrylamide or silica sol-
gel). This method alone is usually too weak to prevent enzymatic leaking, and usually is
coupled with chemical attachment, mostly via covalent bonding (Norouzian 2003; Sheldon
and Pelt 2013).
Cross-linking employs a bifunctional reagent, to covalently bond the enzyme onto/into the
membrane, while being binding the enzymes to each other (Sheldon and Pelt 2013).
1.6.2
Membrane modification
In the case of enzyme immobilisation it is required for the membrane to have selective
binding sites for the enzymes to attach onto; and these binding sites might not be always
present on native membranes, but they can be introduced with specific modification tech-
niques. The main goal of modification is to add the desired binding sites, without affecting
the bulk membrane properties. There are 3 main membrane modification techniques:
grafting, hetching and coating (X. J. Lee et al. 2018).
Grafting can be described as direct introduction of functional groups via several modes
(being wet chemical methods, photo induced or plasma techniques). For a better descrip-
tion, the reader is invited to access the review from X. J. Lee et al. 2018.
Hetching is the introduction of defects on the membrane surface, which leads to the gen-
eration of new functional groups.
The last technique is coating, which is the addition of a chemical layer onto the membrane
to allow for binding site formation (Jochems et al. 2011).

Glutaraldehyde cross-linking
Covalent binding is extremely efficient for enzymatic immobilisation; leading to minimal
enzyme leaching into the porous media and no protein contamination of the product. The
formation of several covalent bonds reduces the conformational flexibility and thermal
vibration, preventing the potein to unfold and denaturate (Hanefeld, Gardossi, and Magner
2009). The amino groups are traditionally employed for the immobilisation: the amino
group acts as a nucleophile and can attack an aldehyde functional group. The length
of the spacer (cross-linker) can influence the activity of the enzyme: longer spacers are
expected to allow for more flexibility for the enzyme. In the case of shorter cross-linkers
(such as GA), the main advantage that can be obtained is the increase in stability, both
thermal and temporal. In the case of lipases (they require a flexible tertiary structure to
activate) a longer chain is required to allow for flexibility (Hanefeld, Gardossi, and Magner
2009).
Glutaraldehyde (GA) is the more prominent among the cross-linking agents (Walt and
Agayn 1994; Norouzian 2003). Traditionally, GA is a greatly dependable cross-linker,
associated with high degrees of enzyme immobilisation.
High degree of stability is found when the GA interacts with the lysine residues located on
the surface of the enzyme (Sheldon and Pelt 2013). It was assumed that the mode of re-
action was as a Schiff’s base, however this appears not to be the case. Schiff’s bases are
unstable under acid conditions, and they would breakdown to regenerate the aldehyde
and amine (Walt and Agayn 1994). A comparative experiment was undertaken, which
showed that GA treated proteins did not regenerate the amine bonds in lysine upon pH
modification (Quiocho 1976), suggesting that the reversible Schiff’s base reaction does
not provide a full depiction of the reaction mechanism. Even today the interaction mech-
anism is not fully understood (Kildeeva et al. 2009; Sheldon and Pelt 2013).
The configuration of GA varies greatly based on the conditions, and generally the monomeric
form can be observed at the free aldehyde state or hydrate state; also polymeric isomeric
structures occur (Walt and Agayn 1994; Kildeeva et al. 2009). Each of the different forms
will affect the cross-linking capacities of the GA. These reactions are reversible and when
diluted or the temperature is increased, the equilibrium is shifted back to the monomeric
version of GA (Walt and Agayn 1994).
Industrial application in enzyme-related fields: lactase immobilisation via adsorption fol-
lowed by GA cross-linking; penicillin acrylase immobilisation via GA for 6-aminopenicillanic
acid. GA can work with many supports: nylon, fumed silica, controlled-pore glass, pro-
teins as gelatin and BSA, and polymers with pendant amino groups (such as dopamine)
(Walt and Agayn 1994).
Fernandez-Lafuente et al. 1995 found that concentration of GA can have massive effects
on the activity (0.11-0.55 % v/v of GA retained 85-90 % of the initial activity; while 10 %
v/v of GA cut the activity by 50 %; the enzyme was penicillin G amylase). The paper
theorises that the GA started interacting with other functional groups besides the amines.
The same paper formaldehyde was used, but found GA to be superior in performance
(lead to lowed activity decrease). It was found that the cross-linking lead to a decrease
in activity compared to the free system counterparts; the decrease in activity was cor-
related with and increase in stability of the modified enzyme. Fernandez-Lafuente et al.
1995 theorises that the cross-linking is responsible for such modification in behaviour: the
team believes that the increase of rigidity due to the cross-linkers distort the tertiary struc-
ture, hence decreasing the amylase activity. The inactivation was confirmed in several
operating condition, as different pH, temperature and presence of organic cosolvents.

or enzyme (Abdel-Naby et al. 1999), or conformation change in the enzyme, leading
to increased steric hindrance (Rogalski et al. 1997).
1.7.3
Enzyme action mode
Another possible effect on the activity upon immobilisation is the change in the selection
of the reactive bond. In the case of dextranases would be the hydrolysis of the α −1, 6
glycosidic bond.
This change in behaviour was reported by Siso et al. 1990 for the immobilisation of α-
amylase. In this experiment they observe that the endo-hydrolysing enzyme changed
its behaviour, to favour the hydrolysis of terminal bonds. In this work, the authors claim
that the mass transfer resistances are in direct relation with the modification of the enzyme
action pattern post-immobilisation.The explanation proposed is that the diffusion limitation
would allow for a stagnant micro-environment at the enzyme level, leading to localised
substrate depletion and product accumulation.
This change in perceived action pattern is important to add as a separate feature, as it
does not have to linked to the reaction kinetics, and several testing methods would not be
able to discern the modification of the product molecular size. This is particularly important
for this project, as the reducing sugar test is used: this test provides kinetics values, but
it is ”blind” to the change of product molecular size. The nature and implications of this
test will be further discussed in the Materials and Methods section.

It is important to understand that the kinetics values are obtained from the DNS reducing
sugar test. Each linear dextran, being millions of monomers long or isomaltose, has just
one reducing end (aldahyde on the C1 carbon). This reducing end is what is reacting with
the DNS, and in return giving the distinctive yellow/orange colour to the solution. A key
assumption to obtain the Michaelis-Menten kinetics is that the rate of product generation
is identical to the substrate consumption:
d[P]
dt
= −d[S]
dt
(3.1)
This test allows us to observe the rate of hydrolysis, but it does not consider the location
of the hydrolysed bond, and in return the molecular size of the product. Based on this
understanding, the test does not differentiate between the original substrate, and the fi-
nal product; hence it was required to obtain the reducing sugar test values for both the
initial DXT 70 kDa substrate and the sample introduced to the enzyme. For the instance
of hydrolysis of polymers, it is important to understand the concentration of ”substrate”
in this instance is more closely related to the concentration of hydrolysing bonds; and
the ”product” detected is more representative of the presence of reducing ends, this in-
stance aldehyde bond on the C1 of the terminal glucose bond. Under these premises,
the assumption of equality between product generation and substrate consumption holds
true.

3.2.1
Effect of operating temperature
As reported in the previous section, the value of Vmax represents the maximum rate of
reaction. Upon increasing the temperature to 50 ◦C it can be seen that there is a 4-fold
increase for the maximum rate of reaction, from 0.22 to 0.8 μmol/min/mg . This increase
is to be expected, as it is well understood that Penicillium sp. endo-dextranases perform
sub-optimally at room temperature. The selection of this new temperature is based on
the work from Jiménez 2009, where most of the industrially employed strands from the
Penicillium species have an optimal operational temperature ranging from 50 to 60 ◦C.
Having increased the temperature, the Km increased compared to the room temperature
counterpart. This implies that the perceived affinity between the substrate and the enzyme
is decreased (Fullbrook 1996). It can be seen in eq. (1.13), here repeated:

et al. 2014), e.g. due to increase in Brownian motions of both enzyme and substrate. At
this stage, it is hard to determine which specific hypothesis, or combination of these, is
the more likely to represent the modifications in behaviour.

It is possible to observe, in fig. 3.11 and fig. 3.12, that there is a change in behaviour:
the enzyme upon immobilisation does not hydrolyse the substrate in a random fashion.
It appears to selectively hydrolyse the substrate at the tail-end of the polymer chains,

120 minutes, followed by the even sharper isomaltose peak at 240 minutes. The reaction
approximates completion after 21 hours.
When observing the modification of enzymatic hydrolysis behaviour, it can be seen that
the enzyme proceeds to hydrolyse the tail-end of the substrate; but it is important to un-
derstand that the final product is still isomaltose. As such the behaviour is not changed
from an endo to exo-bond hydrolysing enzyme, but it is still an endo-enzyme, that is per-
ceived to target the tail end of the dextran polymer. This is a sharp contrast compared to
the random behaviour seen in the free system, particularly evident in fig. 3.6.
This apparent change in enzyme hydrolysis action occurred as well in the works from
D. D. Lee et al. 1980 and Siso et al. 1990. D. D. Lee et al. 1980 worked with dextrin hy-
drolysis via covalent immobilisation of glucoamylase, on porous supports. The selective
production of smallest reducible unit, β−glucose, was noted. Siso et al. 1990 worked with
covalent immobilisation (GA mediated) of an α-amylase, on an array of different support,
porous ones among them. The findings were similar to our own, upon immobilisation the
enzyme, that displayed random behaviour in solution, selectively produced low polymeri-
sation degree products.
Both works suggest that mass diffusion limitations are chiefly responsible to this be-
haviour. It is hypothesised that the larger fraction of the substrate is limited in its access
to the enzymatic micro-environment, and that product is diffusion limited upon returning
to the bulk solution, hence increasing the residence time of the smaller polymeric frac-
tions within the micro-environment where the enzyme is present. In D. D. Lee et al. 1980,
the fraction of monomeric glucose was found to be increased within the pores, where the
enzyme was immobilised, when compared to the bulk of the solution. The decrease in
the overall hydrolysis rate was confirmed both on via a mathematical model of their sys-
tem and empirically. In Siso et al. 1990, the team quantified the effects of both internal
and external diffusion on their porous substrates, on the change of behaviour of the en-
zyme. The found that the limitation in mass diffusion was directly related to the increase
of modified behaviour.
The mass diffusion limitation of the substrate and product from and to the enzyme micro-
environment can explain the observed behaviour. A potential cause for this mass transfer
could be the geometry of the support: in this instance a flat membrane was employed. In
reference to future work, different geometries of the support could be tested (e.g. cylindri-
cal or spherical). The decrease of of overall activity is well explained by this hypothesis,
as the hydrolysis kinetics would be mass transfer limited. The nearly two hour delay in
detection reducing sugar ends, as observed in fig. 3.13, is aligned with the concept of the
large substrate struggling to access the enzyme’s active site, and with the product being
limited to re-access the bulk solution for detection. Lastly, the modification of perceived
modification in action pattern can be explained by the substrate being confined into the
enzyme micro-environment. The individual substrate molecule is subject to complete hy-
drolysis near the active site, and as such, the GPC would show a large fraction of the
substrate being unaffected by the enzyme, and an a growing peak of isomaltose as time
increases.

Conclusion
This project investigated the effects of temperature and GA mediated cross-linking on
the free dextranase. It was observed that the Michaelis-Menten parameters were al-
tered at 50 ◦C, displaying increased Vmax (0.8 ± 0.1 μmol/mg/min from 0.22 ± 0.03
μmol/mg/min) and increased Km ( 0.25 ± 0.06 mmol/L from 0.05 ± 0.03 mmol/L). These
findings represents an increase in maximum hydrolysis rate, however with a perceived de-
crease in affinity between the substrate and enzyme; this second factor is hypothesised
to be due to the increase of Brownian motion of both the substrate and enzyme alike. The
Michaelis-Menten parameters for the GA cross-linked enzyme appear to be alike to the
unaltered enzyme; however via the reducing sugar testing it is evident that the activity
of the enzyme is greatly decreased past the first 5 minutes. It is hypothesised that the
mass transfer limitations between the product/substrate and the enzyme could be an ex-
planation for this phenomenon. With the GA cross-linked enzyme, it was evident that the
enzyme hydrolysed in a non-selective mode.
In the selection of the immobilisation support, two different UF membranes were tested
for loading, being RC 30 kDa and PES 30 kDa. The PES 30 kDa membrane displayed
the better loading with 17.0 ± 0.6 % of the overall incubated enzyme, against 7.5 ± 0.5
% of the RC membrane under same experimental conditions.
An investigation of the effects of dopamine concentration and time of incubation in rela-
tion to the enzyme loading, showed that the neither parameter, in the ranges evaluated,
displayed correlation.
Three different immobilisation protocols, on PDA layer-modified PES 30 kDa membranes,
were tested: two enzyme incubations and a fouling-induced mode. The fouling-induced
protocol provided the best loading ( 36.2 ± 6.1 % of overall enzyme introduced), while
the modified (T = 50 ◦C and increased GA cross-linking time to 16 hours) incubation
protocol showed slightly better loading (23.9 ± 1.7 %) when compared to the benchmark
protocol (16.9 ± 0.1%).
The most interesting finding of this project are found in the enzyme activity upon immo-
bilisation:
• Modification of perceived enzyme activity upon covalent immobilisation. The en-
zyme appears to favour the production of isomaltose, deferring from the random
hydrolysis of the free dextranase.
• Presence of an initial activity lag when with GA mediated immobilisation. Within the
first 60 minutes, no enzyme activity could be detected.
• Decrease of activity when compared to the free system. The modification in the
reducing sugars cumulative curves (due to the lag in activity detection) renders dif-
ficult the activity comparisons. At 120 and 240 minutes, the free enzyme displayed
an overall activity of 2.8 ± 0.2 μmol/mg and 3.08 ± 0.02 μmol/mg, respectively.
The best performing covalent bound immobilisation reported 2.3 % and 9.1 % at the
two respective points, when compared to the free system.
All these findings are coherent with the mass transfer limitation hypothesis proposed by
D. D. Lee et al. 1980 and Siso et al. 1990. The substrate rate of access to the active site
can contribute to an initial perceived lag of activity, and the inability of the product to leave
Enzymatic membrane reactor for oligodextran production
63
==PAGE:76==
the enzymatic environment leads to over-hydrolysis, and subsequently the detection of
the enzyme modifying its hydrolysis selection.
Employing adsorption in combination with the covalent bonding, improves the immobilised
enzyme activity, and appears to nullify the modifications in behaviour and the activity lag.
This mode comes with the drawback of increased permeability loss (94 % of the virgin
membrane permeability) and a significant enzymatic detachment as a function of time
(26 % detachment after 24 hours).
The permeability losses post-immobilisation have been found to be large for all the proto-
cols. For the benchmark and modified covalent protocols, the water permeability losses
are found to be 82.6 ± 3.2 %, and 85.4 ± 0.5 %, respectively. The fouling-induce immo-
bilisation appeared to be the most severely affected, with a loss of 94.1 ± 1.4 %.
For future work, it is required to address the finding of perceived modification of hydrolysis.
This is issue would take precedent on all the other aspects of research.
• Firstly, it is required to investigate alternative geometrical set-up: on a cylindrical or
spherical supports. This would help to determine whether mass transfer hypothesis
is correct.
• Based on the great losses in permeability, it could be proposed to decouple the
immobilisation support from the membrane, in a cascade like system.
• In light of great activity loss, decoupling the hydrolysis and the separation in an
EMR, over an biocatalytic membrane. A possible avenue is to attempt CLEAs with
