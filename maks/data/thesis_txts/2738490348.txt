Investigating the VLM in Greenland
Abstract
Accurately correcting relative sea level measurements is crucial in a time of global warming where precise measure-
ments of sea level are important, especially for the population living on the coastlines. However, those measurements
are affected by different processes such as vertical land motion (VLM) for example, so they can’t be used directly.
In order to better understand vertical land motion and geoid fluctuations in Greenland, we focused our analysis in
the four tidal gauge stations available in Greenland: Qaqortoq, Ittoqqortoormiit, Nuuk, and Pituffik. Corrective
trends were determined for every station, taking into consideration the effects of both current melting (modeled
using GRACE data) and glacial isostatic adjustment (GIA). Gravitational data from GRACE mission were used
using a novel approach that consists in fitting the mass distribution into mass concentration blocs (mascon solution),
whereas we used existing models for the GIA signal. We then apply our corrective trends to the tidal gauge data
at the four stations after a preliminatory work of data quality checking.
Moreover, with our proposed technique, these trends can be extended to any point in Greenland or in the world, and
provide the vertical deformation and geoid variation at any point. Those trends can correct sea level measurements
both from satellite altimetry or tidal gauge data: data from satellite altimetry has to be corrected by subtracting
the geoid trend, whereas tidal gauge data has to be corrected by adding the vertical land motion and subtracting
the geoid.

Introduction
1
Introduction
In a context of climate change, precise measurements of climate indicators are necessary to predict the future climate.
Especially, indicators describing how climate change will directly impact population are essential. One example
is measuring sea level rise for those populations that live next to the coastlines. However, those measurements
sometimes need adjustments or correction. For example, to measure local sea level, tidal gauge stations are used.
However, those measurements correspond to a relative sea level, i.e. a sea level relative to a point in land, and
are therefore sensitive to land-motion (and as we’ll see later, to geoid variation as well). Those land motion can
have several origins, from past deglaciation to present day melting. And in regions where a strong mass variation
occurs (for example in polar regions where the ice is melting ), land motion and geoid variation occurs continuously.
The issue is that models that predict the global mean value of sea level rise might take into account the sea level
data from tidal gauges. Therefore those measurements need to be corrected with new models of geoid and crust
deformation. Those corrections are pointed out as a main factor to explain the non closure of the sea level budget
by IPCC in their report (Fox-Kemper and Yu [10]). This means that the measured sea level doesn’t fit the expected
value from the sum of all the contributions to sea level rise. Therefore, it is of particular interest to quantify vertical
land motion and apply those corrections to the tidal gauge data in order to have better estimates of the local sea
level, to then have a better estimate of the absolute global mean sea level. The focus in Greenland has several
interests. First, Greenland, as an island, is very impacted by sea level rise (Rose et al. [11]). Moreover, Greenland
is responsible of such a big loss in ice that it becomes in turn responsible for sea level rise in other regions which
makes it even more important to predict how much mass is lost (Landerer and Jentoft-Nilsen [12]). The ice loss
in this whole region has been the main focus of several studies, such as Otosaka et al. [13]. Finally, as stated
before, due to the geography and the composition of Greenland, this region is very sensitive to the so-called Glacial
Isostatic Adjustment (GIA) as well as ice melting, and is therefore a region where vertical land motion is especially
important.
Several studies and especially studies aiming at exploring those discrepancies in the local sea level budget have
been done using GNSS measurements. Generally, those studies are focusing on a particular region in the world, for
example French Polynesia (Li et al. [14]), the Northwest Atlantic coast (Frederikse et al. [15]), the Bohai sea (Zhou
et al. [16]) or Greenland (Berg et al. [17]). GNSS present the advantages to have a good resolution, but have a bad
coverage and do not measure the geoid (Frederikse et al. [18]).
This is why other studies have used GRACE satellite, a satellite that measures Earth’s gravity field anomalies.
For example, Garc´ıa et al. [19] uses GRACE data to compute the contribution of the mass change in the sea level
variation. However, most of them (as Garc´ıa et al. [19]) use the stokes coefficients derived from the expansion in
spherical harmonics of the gravity field, which, as we’ll see later, present some disadvantages. Our work is new in
the sense that we use a new representation of the mass distribution responsible for the measured gravity field, in
order to compute the vertical land motion and the geoid changes in Greenland.
Moreover, some studies also focused on using satellite altimetry instead or in addition to tidal gauges. his presents
the advantages of not being affected by land movement. However, those measurements are still affected by the
geoid variation, and have a poor coverage in the polar region.
We’ll start by introducing some scientific background, including the difference between the two sources of vertical
land motion we study, as well as the data used to measure it.
Then, we’ll present the different methods and
strategies used in this study, to, one hand hand, extract and analyze the trend of the tidal gauge time series
(without corrections), and then, how to compute the vertical land motion and the geoid variation from GRACE
data to correct those trends and predict sea level.

2
Theory - Scientific background
This section focuses on describing the different sources of land motion and how they can affect the measurements
done by tidal gauges.
2.1
Vertical Land Motion and tidal gauge stations
Tidal gauges measure relative sea level, i.e., the sea level above an arbitrary point located on land. Therefore, a
motion on land could move this reference, and introduce errors in the measurements. Before going through more
details into those measurements, let’s investigate what is vertical land motion and those origins.
Figure 2.1: Causes of relative sea-level change. Figure is from [2]
Vertical land motion (VLM) has different origins, as shown in Figure 2.1. It occurs due to the response of the solid
Earth to mass re-distributions. Earth acts as a viscoelastic body, so its response to mass distribution has a different
timescale (see Figure 2.2), depending on the response being either elastic or viscous.
An important process is the motion of ice and water, and especially, the melting of old glaciers. Places that used
to be covered by glaciers during the last glaciation maximum (16,000 years ago) are now rebounding and going
back to their original shape. This long term viscous response is called Glacial Isostatic Adjustment (GIA) or post-
glacial rebound. However, mass changes still occurs nowadays: recent ice melting, earthquakes, sedimentation etc.
Proportionally to the loading and depending on the rheological properties of the Earth, the elastic deformation can
be computed using Love numbers. This produces a higher frequency signal that will be referred here as vertical
land motion from present day melting (PDM), even if it encompasses broader origins than only ice melting. In
other words, Earth is subject to mass redistribution, that have mainly two origins :
• past deglaciation : the Earth is still rebounding from that time due to its viscous composition
• present day melting: In Greenland, ice sheets are melting due to global warming and seasonal variation. Those
mass variations induce a direct answer due to the elastic composition of Earth.
Moreover, an even more subtle process appears. Due to the redistribution of mass around Earth, the geoid is also
changing, and will affect the measurements of the relative sea level (see Figure 2.3).
Therefore, in order to infer correctly the absolute sea level change, two parameters are needed.
• The Vertical Crust Deformation
• The variation of the geoid

2.2
GRACE
2.2.1
Measurements mission instrument
We need to measure both vertical deformation and the new geoid. To do so, we’ll use data from GRACE and
GRACE-FO mission. Gravity Recovery And Climate Experiment (GRACE) and GRACE-FO (GRACE Follow-
On) are two missions launched by the NASA and DLR, respectively in 2002 and 2018. The aim of the mission
is to map the gravity field of Earth. Each mission consists of two identical satellites GRACE-A and GRACE-B

2.2.3
Mascon solution
However, several works brought out the apparition of so called ”stripes” in the data, that are usually removed via
empirical smoothing and/or “destriping” algorithms. However, the destriping also removes some real geophysical
signal along with the stripes(Watkins et al. [21]).

itational field into mass concentration blocks (rather than into the spherical harmonics). This solution presents the
advantage to implement geophysical constraints much easier, and helps filtering out noise from the GRACE/GRACE-
FO observations, without using empirical filtering ([22]). The data is described further in section 3.

Mass loss over Greenland
We now have methods to :
• Derive the VLM and the geoid from PDM using GRACE data
• Derive the VLM and the geoid from past deglaciation using GIA model
• Extract a trend for our four tidal gauge stations.
Before analyzing the results, we proceed to a validation by computing the mass loss over Greenland to check if
the mass distribution we inferred from GRACE data was correct.
Indeed, this values have been computed by
different authors. For example, Otosaka et al. [13] found a trend of -216 Gt/year in the period 2002-2020 by using
the combined results of 50 independent estimates of ice sheet mass balance derived from satellite observations of
temporal changes in ice sheet flow, in ice sheet volume, and in Earth’s gravity field.

-269 Gt/year. The difference in the result might be due to the difference in the interpolation to handle the missing

seasonal decomposition to the time series using from the package statsmodels from Seabold and Perktold [7] we

by pieces. The only time where the residuals have a value equal to the seasonal period is around 2012-2013 and

5.2) between 2013 and 2018. Wang et al. [8] explained this rate change from 269Gt/year to 217Gt/year by the

6.1
Trend from the tidal gauge station without correction
Table 4 shows the results of our data processing in each station. We can see that we have a high range of values,
going from -8.29 mm/year to 8.77 mm/year. But what would be a sensitive value ? We know that the global mean
sea level is around 3,4 mm/year at least. Being close to a region where the ice is melting, we would expect to have
a bigger/similar value. Both positive trend seem possible, and the trend around Pituffik and Ittoqqortoormiit can
seem counter intuitive. However, that is the whole point of this study: correcting the relative sea level measured
at the tidal gauges as those might be disturbed by vertical land motion and geoid variation due to the mass
redistribution oh this same melting ice. Therefore, maybe than a negative trend is not so abnormal. That being
said, the high variability among the 4 stations is a bit more alarming, and might indicate than the trend from the
tidal gauge stations we found have to be used with caution. This will be discussed later in section 8.

7.1
Results from GRACE data
We can finally look to our main results, i.e, the vertical land motion and geoid variation computed with GRACE
data (so coming from PDM). Figure 7.1 is a map of the trend of the vertical deformation and the trend of the geoid
variation computed at each point, using the calculation detailed in section 4. Table 6 focuses on the four studied
stations. To compute the trend, we simply apply a linear regression in our data.
In the maps of Figure 7.1, we can clearly identify the same pattern than in the mass variation maps, with areas
of high values in the zones 1-4 from Figure 5.2, and a field around 0mm/year (0Gt/y) in the center of Greenland.
This is consistent with the method we applied and equation 4.4, where the geoid ∆N and the vertical deformation
U are directly linked to the mass distribution.
We can still distinguish the original shape of the mascons (the squared pattern), but with a ’blurred’ effect. This
is due to the effect of the β-factor that we apply to smooth our results. Lets note than the higher the degree, the
higher the precision of the map. Here, with a degree of 256, we can get a spatial resolution of lN = 2πr
N , which is
the horizontal wavelength of a spherical harmonic Pn(θ) at the surface of a sphere of radius r (Finlay and Olsen
[36]). That gives us lN = 156km. Even at those high degrees, the resolution is still course, so we must use our
results with caution when comparing to the tidal gauge station trends that are points measurements.
Another interesting results is the similarity between the variation of the geoid and the vertical deformation. We
can see that we have the same patterns in both, and the only changing parameter is a negative multiplicative factor
between them. This comes directly from our formula 4.4, the only difference between the formula is the use of
either hn or 1 + kn.
Finally, the highest observed variation occurs in region 1, 2 and 4 (regions are defined in Figure 5.2), with a highest
value around 10mm/y next to the station Pituffik. From this first map, we can actually guess that the biggest mass
variation will occur next to Pituffik.
The time series at each station are presented in section 10.2 in Figure 10.1 to10.4.

Here, we’ll discuss the importance of our results as well as the possible sources of errors in our data and/or methods.
For a better separation, we decided to separate this section between the analysis from the tidal gauge station and
the one from the GRACE data.
8.1
Importance and utilisation of the results
In section 7, we derived a corrective trend that is the correcting factor one must apply to tidal gauge time series
to correct them from the influence of past and present day melting. Table 7 can also be used by satellite altimetry
users. Indeed, satellite altimetry presents the advantage to not be sensitive to land motion, but is however sensitive
to the change of the geoid.
It also shows that the present day melting is actually responsible for the biggest part of the signal, both in the
geoid and in the land motion. However, most of the studies only takes into account the GIA effects. For example,
in the dataset from CMS [9] (Copernicus Marine Service), they provide the sea level from satellite observations.

8.2
Discussion of the tidal gauge measurements
8.2.1
Data processing at the tidal gauge stations
After investigation of the tidal gauge time series, it is noticeable that the variance of the data is very high. It is
sometimes hard to find a trustworthy trend.
The main challenges we faced were:
• The gaps in the data, especially in Nuuk (See Figure 4.13).
• The comparison with Copernicus data could also be questioned, especially because we try to correct data
from tidal gauges with a model that uses both tidal gauges and satellite altimetry. Moreover, as detected by
the PQ GLO MFC Team [37] the error between observation and model is quite important around Greenland,
so this comparison with models might introduce new errors.
• The shifts in the data, very noticeable for example in Qaqortoq in Figure 4.8.
More precisely, talking about the shift detection in Qaqortoq, our strategy has been to select a period where no
shift had been detected. But how can we be sure of this, as the period last around 5 years, and than the sensors
are sent every 2 years to Norway to be calibrated ?
8.2.2
Future works
Moreover, the shift detection is an interesting step in the quality control process, but we couldn’t make the deshifting
works. Several things were tried in this matter:
• For each segment, compute the trend of the segment
• Put the segment end to end to remove the effect of the shifting.
However, the only result we got from this part is the following trend, in Figure 8.2 and then, we couldn’t make the
deshifting works.
To improve our results from the tidal gauge stations, as we saw, being able to make the deshifting is a first step.
Other leads could be to do a multivariate analysis from other variables provided by Copernicus, such as the wind,
the pressure, in a similar way than what Bellinghausen et al. [38] did with the multivariable analysis using different
stations (which we cannot do due to the poor coverage of our stations)
8.3
GRACE data results
Now that we saw how our results can be used, let’s dive into an deeper analysis of our results. An interesting
step is to compare what we found to other studies that tried to measure the same. In Table 9, we provide the
computed trend from vertical land motion (from PDM amd GIA), that we compare with the study from Berg et al.
[35], who mapped the vertical land motion measured by Greenland’s Global Navigation Satellite System (GNSS)
Network (GNET). First, let’s note that the time series do not necessarily have the same time range. Secondly, we
can see that our results are systematically higher than the GNSS ones, with similar results for Ittoqqortoormiit,
Nuuk and Pituffik, but a big difference in Qaqortoq. The small difference can be due to to difference in the method
used of course, and as well as in the difference of resolution.
Indeed, GNSS are point measurements, whereas
we saw that our results have a resolution of 156km. However, the results found by Berg et al. [35] also present
some discrepancies with other models (for example the GIA models), so the comparison is informative but is not
invalidating our results. That being said, it is of course necessary to investigate our possible sources of error.

Possible sources of errors
8.4.1
CRI filter
As explained in section 3, we use the solution after it has been filtered with the CRI. However, as we can see in
Figure 8.3, this can lead to mascon with a high value because the mascon is at a coastline, with a very small part
on land. It is clearly the case for the station Pituffik, that is located on a mascon with a small part on land, and
might explain the huge trend we find at this place.
8.4.2
Smoothing factors
As we saw in equation 4.3, the β-factors are supposed to be computed individually for each grid cell (as each grid
cell has a different area depending on its latitude). However, for computation time matters, we chose a fixed value
for the β-factors. To check if this assumption doesn’t imply too many changes, we computed the values of the beta
factors for the latitudes in Greenland, and at the precision machine, there were the same.

9
Conclusion
In conclusion, we have been focused on four stations in Greenland : Ittoqqortoormiit, Qaqortoq, Nuuk and Pituffik.
We derived a corrective trend available in Table 7, that provides at each station:
• the vertical deformation trend
• the geoid variation
Each trend encompass the effect from past deglacitation (GIA) and present day melting. The GIA model has been
derived from preexisting models, whereas our present day melting model has been derived from GRACE data. Let’s
note than our code allows to provide these trend at any point in Greenland, and not only in the specified station.
Those results can have a broad application and can be used to correct tidal gauge measurements using both VLM
and geoid variation, or to correct satellite altimetry by using only the geoid variation.
In our case, we focused on tidal gauges measurements, and after proceeding to a preliminary quality check of the
water level signal, we applied our corrective trends to the sea level trend,. However, work still need to be done in
the quality check to ensure the validity of this data due to the high variability between each station.
More generally, those results (or at least the methodology that we derive to compute the corrective trend) can be
applied to any measurements in the world and not only in Greenland. Those correction might be helpful to close
the so-called sea level budget, and fill the gaps between the measured sea level and the modeled one.
