ABSTRACT 
 
 
Therapeutic antibodies present a highly valuable class of biopharmaceutical products based on their 
exquisite specificity and low level of toxicity. Taken together with the unmet medical needs in cancer 
and inflammatory diseases, there is substantial interest in the development of novel technologies 
facilitating the isolation of high-affinity antibodies with clinical potential.  
 
Here we describe the development of a novel mammalian cell-based platform for the display of full-
length antibodies. Antibody expression on the cell surface was achieved by fusing the immunoglobulin 
heavy chain gene to a glycosylphosphatidylinositol (GPI) anchor. By including the UGAC 
tetranucleotide sequence between these two genetic elements, we were able to obtain simultaneous 
cell surface presentation and secretion of antibodies from transiently transfected mammalian cells. 
This alternative display method offers a highly attractive system, by permitting high-throughput 
screening of the displayed antibodies through flow cytometry and direct functional analysis of the 
soluble antibodies.  
 
To enable enrichment of rare functional antibody clones, we aimed to develop a stable mammalian cell 
line. Due to the unique ability of retroviruses to incorporate their nucleic acid into host cell genomes, 
we aimed to develop a retroviral expression system. Our results demonstrate that retrovirus particles 
can be produced in a packaging cell line and used for subsequent transduction of a target cell line. 
Using this strategy, successful expression of green fluorescent protein (GFP) was observed in a 
transduced murine cell line. Further investigation is required to extend the technique to antibody 
expression.  
 
Once the mammalian display platform is fully developed, it will be interesting to explore the 
technology’s applicability for antibody repertoire screening. The ultimate objective is that the platform 
will facilitate identification of promising lead candidates in a rapid, efficient and cost-effective manner.

RESUMÉ 
 
 
Farmaceutiske antistoffer er en værdifuld type af lægemidler grundet deres udsøgte specificitet og lave 
risiko for at påvirke kroppen toksisk. Da der samtidig er et stigende behov for ny medicin til behandling 
af kræft og andre inflammatoriske lidelser, skaber dette en voksende interesse inden for udviklingen 
af nye teknologier, som kan muliggøre karakteriseringen af antistoffer med høj affinitet og klinisk 
potentiale.  
 
Her beskriver vi udviklingen af en ny display-platform, der benytter mammale celler til at udtrykke 
komplette antistoffer. Ved at fusionere antistofgenet sammen med den kodende sekvens for et 
membrananker var vi i stand til at udtrykke antistoffer på overfladen af mammale celler. Da vi tilmed 
indsatte UGAC nukleotidsekvensen, observerede vi både ekspression af antistoffer på cellemembranen 
og produktion af frie antistoffer i vækstmediet. Disse resultater opnåede vi ved at transfektere vores 
celler transient. Denne alternative display-metode er yderst attraktiv, idet den tillader effektiv 
screening af antistoffer, mens de er koblet til celleoverfladen. Direkte bagefter kan de frie antistoffer 
benyttes til analyse af deres biologiske funktion. 
 
For at berige sjældne antistoffer er det ofte en fordel at benytte en stabil cellelinje, hvorfor vi ønskede 
at udvikle et sådan system. Vores metode er baseret på retrovirus grundet dens unikke evne til at 
indsætte sit genom i værtens DNA. Vores resultater viste, at det var muligt at producere viruspartikler 
i en passende cellelinje, efterfulgt af transduktion i en anden cellelinje, som agerede som vært. Denne 
strategi resulterede i høj expression af det fluorescerende molekyle GFP i de transducerede celler. Der 
er behov for yderligere forsøg for at udvide teknologien til ekspression af antistoffer.  
 
Det vil være interessant at validere anvendeligheden af den mammale display-platform i forhold til 
biblioteksscreening, så snart den er færdigudviklet. Det ultimative mål er selvfølgelig, at teknologien 
skal kunne bruges til at identificere antistoffer med særlige egenskaber på en hurtig og 
omkostningseffektiv måde.

1 INTRODUCTION 
 
 
Over the past three decades, recombinant antibodies have emerged as important therapeutic agents 
against a range of human illnesses, including inflammatory diseases and cancer1. Currently, more than 
30 antibody-based drugs are approved by the US Food and Drug Administration and many more are 
in clinical trials2. Although monoclonal antibodies (mAbs) are effective as single agents, they are often 
combined with conventional chemotherapy to treat human malignancies. Rituxan® (rituximab), 
manufactured by Genentech, is an example of one of the best-selling antibody drugs used for treatment 
in a variety of human lymphomas and B-cell chronic lymphocytic leukemia3. Another well-known mAb 
that has proven its efficiency in cancer therapy is Herceptin® (trastuzumab), also marketed by 
Genentech. This is a humanized antibody used to treat metastatic breast cancer in patients 
overexpressing the Her2 receptor4.  
 
Due to attractive features such as antigen specificity and mechanism of action, antibody-based drugs 
are able to deliver their therapeutic effects to target cells with minimal toxicity. Given this enormous 
therapeutic potential of mAbs, they continue to take up a large part of biopharmaceutical agents in 
development. However, creating high-quality antibodies for clinical use is a challenging task and 
aspects of affinity, avidity and immunogenicity must be taken into account. Thus, a lot of research 
focuses on the development of screening platforms that allow rapid and direct screening of human 
antibodies with predetermined selectivity.  
 
With the advent of display technologies, large antibody libraries can be constructed, creating a physical 
link between genotype and phenotype, thereby allowing fast isolation of antibody encoding sequences 
with attractive binding affinities5,6. Phage and yeast display are the most well established platforms to 
screen libraries of antibody fragments. Despite the power of these technologies, the selected 
antibodies are not subject to mammalian posttranslational quality control and modifications. As a 
consequence, they do not have predictable biological activity in mammalian systems. A mammalian 
cell-based platform represents an attractive approach to express fully human immunoglobulins on the 
cell surface by ensuring correct eukaryotic folding and assembly of antibody heavy and light chain 
domains. The compatibility of such display scheme with flow cytometry applications allows direct 
selection and identification of full-length and functional antibodies from immunoglobulin libraries 
expressed on the surface of mammalian cells.  
 
Although membrane-bound antibodies are desirable for screening purposes to identify candidates 
with high binding affinity, a soluble form of the antibody is needed to perform downstream tests on 
stability, solubility and functional activity. Thus, it would be optimal to have a mammalian system 
permitting simultaneous cell surface expression and secretion of antibody from the same cell. Such a 
platform would be able to facilitate high-throughput cell-based screening and direct functional 
evaluation without the need to subclone and re-express the individual antibody constructs. As a result, 
==PAGE:10==
2 
 
valuable time and costs could be saved. Aminoglycoside-mediated translational readthrough has 
previously been shown to direct a recombinant protein to either the cell membrane or the secretory 
pathway7. This was obtained by fusion of the recombinant protein, a nonsense (stop) codon and a 
membrane-anchoring domain. In the present project, we wish to test whether the same strategy can 
be used to produce both surface-bound and secreted antibody from the same mammalian cell. 
 
To ease the discovery of therapeutic antibodies with desired properties, Symphogen is currently 
putting a lot of effort into the development of a novel mammalian display technology. A number of 
mammalian display platforms, using various expression systems and different transmembrane 
domains to link the antibody to the cell surface, have already been developed. These include, for 
instance, transient transfection of mammalian cells using episomally replicating vectors8 and viral 
vectors based on Sindbis virus9 or Vaccinia virus10. Stable expression has also been established by Flp-
In™ recombinase-mediated integration11,12. We want our display platform to be capable of displaying 
large repertoires consisting of whole antibody molecules in a stable mammalian cell line. Furthermore, 
the system should enable single copy integration, such that each cell only expresses one antibody. It is 
believed that retrovirus can be used for this purpose. Due to its natural ability of genome integration, 
stable cell lines expressing the gene of interest (GOI) can be selected. Previously, it has been 
demonstrated that retroviral display is capable of generating and screening a library consisting of 
antibody fragments13. To extend the technique, we wish to engineer retroviral vectors enabling display 
and selection of whole antibody molecules directly on the surface of mammalian cells. Symphogen can 
greatly benefit from such a display platform in the sense that it will enable the company to screen very 
large antibody libraries in the search for rare antibodies clones targeting various cancer antigens.

2 BACKGROUND 
 
2.1 THE BASICS OF ANTIBODIES 
 
Immunoglobulins, also known as antibodies, are host proteins found in the blood stream and play a 
critical role in the adaptive immune response in the fight against pathogens and foreign particles. 
Whereas different antibody formats exist, the most predominant type for drug development is 
immunoglobulin G (IgG)14. This molecule is a heterotetramer consisting of two identical heavy and two 
identical light polypeptide chains, which are held together by disulfide bonds (Figure 1). Light chains 
are composed of one constant domain (CL) and one variable domain (VL), while the heavy chain 
consists of three constant domains (CH1, CH2 and CH3) and one variable domain (VH). Within the 
antibody, three functional domains exist: two identical Fab (fragment, antigen-binding) regions with 
antigen specificity and one Fc (fragment, crystallizable) region responsible for mediating effector 
functions. Because the IgG molecule is made up of multiple chains, correct assembly and glycosylation 
of the heavy and light domains are critical to render the antibody functional.  
 
 
Figure 1. Schematic representation of the IgG structure. The constant regions are shown in grey, while the variable 
domains are colored blue. 
 
By recognizing and binding specifically to target cells or proteins, antibodies activate other 
components of the immune system, which are capable of attacking and destroying such cells and 
antigens. The unique abilities of immunoglobulins to bind to an antigen with high affinity and 
specificity and to convey effector functions make them very attractive in therapeutic applications. 
Additional benefits of antibody-based drugs include stability, solubility and safety, but will not be 
further discussed in the present report.

2.2 THERAPEUTIC ANTIBODY DISCOVERY 
 
2.2.1 Hybridoma technology 
 
The discovery of hybridoma technology in the 1970’s kick-started the development of antibody-based 
medicine15. Köhler and Milstein were the first to produce mAbs of desired specificity by fusion of an 
antibody-producing lymphocyte from an immunized mouse with a myeloma cell line. Although 
antibodies derived from the technology quickly became applied in many research and diagnostic 
applications, mouse mAbs have major limitations. First of all, they are immunogenic in humans and 
elicit an anti-mouse response, limiting their use in a clinical setting. Secondly, it is very hard to generate 
antibodies with sufficient affinity against highly conserved or toxic targets16.  
 
Alternatively, non-rodent species, such as rabbit or chicken, may be used for immunization17,18. These 
species have an immune system that is more phylogenetically distant from humans compared to 
rodents. This enables rabbits and chickens to recognize antigens that do not normally provoke a strong 
immune response in mice or rats. However, B-cell fusion with chicken myeloma cell lines is very 
unreliable.  
 
2.2.2 Recombinant DNA technology 
 
Increased understanding of the mechanism of action of antibodies along with major advances within 
recombinant DNA technology in the early 1990’s, made it possible to manipulate immunoglobulin 
genes19. Briefly, recombinant antibody technology involves cloning of immunoglobulin genes into a 
vector followed by introduction into a suitable host for protein expression. 
 
In an effort to overcome obstacles associated with immunogenicity, scientists used cloning approaches 
to engineer mAbs from hybridomas. This has led to the production of chimeric20 and humanized21 
antibody constructs. By coupling the antigen-binding site of an antibody to a human constant domain, 
it is possible to develop antibodies with high binding affinity, low immunogenicity and Fc-mediated 
cellular effector functions. This enables antibodies derived from the hybridoma technology or non-
rodent species to be used as human therapeutics. 
 
Recombinant in vitro display systems offer a range of advantages for generation of antibody libraries5. 
These platforms are considered an attractive alternative to conventional in vivo immunization-based 
technologies due to the elimination of hybridomas and production of antibodies with enhanced 
specificity without prior immunization and decreased risk of immunogenicity6,13. Furthermore, 
screening procedures of very large recombinant protein libraries are simplified by creating a physical 
connection between phenotype and genotype, i.e. coupling the antibody nucleic acid sequence with 
binding affinity5,6. Additionally, screening can be performed in a high-throughput mode, characterizing 
antibodies with picomolar affinity levels22,23.

2.2.3 Phage display 
 
A number of different expression systems and display technologies are in wide use today for antibody 
discovery. Phage display is the most established and commonly used method to screen libraries of 
antibody antigen-binding fragments24,25. In this type of display, a foreign gene is fused in frame to a 
phage capsid protein sequence, such as pIII, and cloned into an appropriate vector to allow display on 
the surface of a filamentous bacteriophage. Although the technique is highly robust and versatile, this 
prokaryotic expression system has certain limitations26. Recombinant proteins produced in these 
systems are often prone to misfolding and incorrect glycosylation, which potentially can alter protein 
function. Improper folding may also result in the formation of insoluble aggregates. Furthermore, re-
engineering the antibody fragments into full-length antibodies can be a time-consuming process, and 
it can be difficult to predict the expression levels of these when produced in a mammalian system. 
 
2.2.4 Yeast display 
 
To avoid the issues associated with prokaryotic display systems, yeast display was developed. 
Saccharomyces cerevisiae is the most commonly used yeast cell line to display eukaryotic proteins with 
improved folding characteristics. In this approach, immunoglobulin genes are normally fused to one 
of the many cell wall proteins allowing display of the antibody on the cell surface. Traditionally, yeast 
display systems have been used to express small antibody fragments27,28, but full-length IgG display 
has also successfully been achieved29. One remaining limitation associated with yeast expression 
systems is the fact that they are not applicable to generate libraries as large as those obtained by phage 
display. This deficiency is related to the lower transformation efficiency of yeast.  
 
2.2.5 Mammalian cell display  
 
Although both phage and yeast display have achieved considerable success in many biological 
applications in recent years, they lack important features for ideal display. Whereas protein expression 
in yeast cells mimic the eukaryotic environment of mammalian cells, there are still differences in 
glycosylation patterns. Furthermore, soluble IgG manufacturing for therapeutic applications is not 
compatible with neither phage nor yeast systems. In light of this, mammalian cells have emerged as a 
promising display platform. 
 
Mammalian cells have an inherent ability to efficiently assemble and express complex eukaryotic 
proteins such as immunoglobulins. Furthermore, mammalian cell-based display systems offer the 
possibility of selecting antibody candidates with desirable characteristics from an early point in the 
discovery phase, by enabling expression of full-length and functional antibodies. Thus, minimum 
conversion is required to assess the biological activity of isolated hits.  
 
Mammalian cell display relies on transfection of the foreign gene to promote antibody expression. 
Broadly speaking, methods of transfection are categorized into three groups. These include physical, 
==PAGE:14==
6 
 
chemical and biological transfection. The physical methods generally rely on either microinjection or 
electroporation. By creating small holes in the plasma membrane, foreign DNA is introduced into host 
cells. On the other hand, the chemical methods are based on electrostatic forces. Positively charged 
transfection agents make complexes with the negatively charged plasmids, thereby encouraging them 
to enter the negatively charged cell membrane. Chemical methods utilize many different transfection 
agents, such as cationic lipids, cationic polymers and calcium phosphate. Finally, biological transfection 
refers to virus-mediated transduction. In this approach, virus particles are used as carriers of the 
foreign gene.  
 
Antibody genes introduced into mammalian cells may result in either transient or stable protein 
expression. Deciding which strategy to use generally depends on the goals of the particular 
experiment. Transiently transfected cells are capable of expressing foreign DNA at high levels but do 
not integrate it into their genome. Consequently, the foreign gene is not replicated during cell division. 
As the transiently transfected genes are only expressed for a limited period of time, the cells are 
typically harvested 24 to 72 hours post transfection. Additionally, the antibody genes need to be 
recovered from isolated cells if further studies are intended. Nevertheless, transient transfection offers 
a fast method for investigating properties of proteins expressed by mammalian cells.  
 
Stable cell lines are superior when several rounds of selection and enrichment are required. Stable 
cells sustain transgene expression by the use of genome integration and appropriate selection 
markers. Antibiotic resistance markers are commonly used for this purpose. Co-expression of the 
marker with the transgene enables scientists to identify cells that successfully have integrated the 
foreign DNA. It is generally considered to be a difficult task to establish a stable cell line expressing a 
large antibody library due to the risk of incorporating multiple genes per cell. Antibody screening is 
complicated if a single cell expresses several clones with different specificities. In an attempt to control 
gene copy number, several approaches have been investigated. One of these relies on genome 
integration into an active transcriptional site and is referred to as the Flp-In™ system11,12. 
 
A number of different cell types have been used for mammalian display. Among some of the most 
prominent laboratory cell lines for expression of recombinant antibodies are African green monkey 
kidney cells (commonly referred to as COS cells), Chinese hamster ovary (CHO) cells and human 
embryonic kidney 293 (HEK293) cells30–32. Additionally, antibody display has been studied on a 
selection of B-cell lines, using either naturally hypermutated immunoglobulin genes33 or via the 
introduction of an antibody repertoire34. 
 
For the display of antibodies on the cell surface, a heterologous transmembrane moiety is usually fused 
in frame to the C-terminus of the heavy chain gene. Some examples of these include human 
thrombomodulin35, PDGFR (platelet-derived growth factor receptor)12,36 and a number of CD (cluster 
of differentiation) proteins, such as CD437,38, CD839 and CD2839. Additionally, fusion to a 
glycosylphosphatidylinositol (GPI) anchor has been used to link IgG to the cell membrane40,41. 
 
==PAGE:15==
7 
 
A major advantage of antibody display on mammalian cells is that it enables high-throughput screening 
by flow cytometry or paramagnetic beads. This allows selection and enrichment of specific antibodies 
with fine resolution. Most flow cytometry equipment employed today is capable of processing 107 –
108 cells in a few hours. Furthermore, cells can be co-selected for multiple properties, such as antibody 
expression and antigen binding, by the use of different labeled fluorochromes.  
 
2.3 ALTERNATIVE DISPLAY METHODS 
 
While discovery applications require the display of IgG molecules on the cell surface to allow high-
throughput screening by flow cytometry, downstream applications involve soluble antibodies for 
functional testing. Thus, a robust technology that can facilitate high-level cell surface display and 
simultaneous secretion of the same antibody in significant quantities is highly desired (Figure 2). In 
this report, we tested two alternative display approaches to obtain both surface-bound and soluble 
antibody from a single cell. These include translational stop codon readthrough and furin cleavage. In 
the following sections, the basic features of the two strategies are summarized.

2.3.1 Translational stop codon readthrough 
 
Normally, eukaryotic ribosomes recognize stop codons with extremely high accuracy to ensure correct 
translation and termination of polypeptides. Although the three nonsense (stop) codons UAA, UAG and 
UGA typically result in translational termination, a mechanism known as “stop codon readthrough” is 
sometimes observed. Instead of terminating protein synthesis, the ribosome adds an amino acid to the 
growing polypeptide chain. It has been suggested that the base following a translational stop codon 
may influence the efficiency of termination of protein synthesis in bacteria and yeast43,44. 
Manuvakhova and co-workers were the first to study termination efficiency in higher eukaryotic cells. 
In 2000, they published a journal article demonstrating that sequences surrounding a stop codon play 
an important role in determining its susceptibility to suppression of aminoglycosides in mammalian 
cells45. The phenomenon was tested by using a reporter gene containing different nonsense codons 
and the results revealed that either a 25 kDa or a 35 kDa protein product was produced. Furthermore, 
the ratio of the two polypeptides was dependent on the susceptibility of aminoglycoside-mediated 
suppression.  
 
Based on these observations, a new technology referred to as regulated stop codon readthrough was 
developed7. By incorporating a nonsense codon in between a recombinant blood coagulation protein 
and a membrane-targeting peptide, the authors of the study demonstrated that readthrough was 
promoted in the presence of an aminoglycoside antibiotic, resulting in the display of the protein on the 
cell surface. The tetranucleotide UGAC was shown to have the highest level of translational 
readthrough in the presence of the aminoglycoside Geneticin® (manufactured by Invitrogen). In 
addition, the authors performed a flow cytometry analysis, which revealed a correlation between 
membrane-anchored and soluble recombinant protein levels. Finally, the study demonstrated 
compatibility of regulated readthrough with flow cytometry when enrichment is desired. By using 
multiple enrichment rounds, it was possible to select for cells expressing very high levels of 
recombinant protein on the cell membrane. 
 
2.3.2 Furin cleavage 
 
Furin is an inherent proteolytic enzyme, which is known for its ability to cut a number of precursor 
proteins at specific sites. This action turns the cleaved proteins into their biologically active form. The 
recognition site of furin is a specific amino acid sequence consisting of four residues: Arg-X-Arg-Arg, 
where Arg represents arginine and X can be any amino acid46. Furin cleavage of proteins occurs 
downstream of the last arginine residue. However, cellular furin cleavage does not happen in a highly 
effective manner. Consequently, a fraction of protein substrates is not successfully cleaved. The 
inefficient mechanism of furin has previously been utilized to target antibodies to either the cell 
surface or the secretory pathway47.

2.4 GENE DELIVERY BY RETROVIRUSES 
 
Because of the natural ability of retroviruses to integrate their nucleic acid into host cell genomes, they 
provide an important tool for establishing efficient and stable transgene expression in mammalian 
cells48. In contrast to conventional transfection, which only results in transient expression of the 
introduced nucleic acid, viral-based transduction allows stable genomic integration and long-term 
protein expression of the transgene over repeated cell divisions. Moreover, gene delivery by 
recombinant retroviruses is generally considered both safe and easy, owing to the simple nature of the 
retroviral genome. 
 
Today, a range of retroviral vector systems is available providing stable gene transfer to many different 
cell types and tissues. The following sections describe the basic principles of retroviruses and provide 
an overview of the steps involved in virus production and host cell infection. Moreover, the general 
characteristics of commonly used retroviral vectors are discussed.  
 
Retroviruses are members of the Retroviridae family and are known for the ability to reverse 
transcribe their RNA genome into a cDNA copy, followed by stable incorporation into the host cell 
genome. These viruses are broadly categorized as either simple or complex49. Simple retroviruses are 
also referred to as γ-retroviruses and include the Moloney murine leukemia virus (MMLV), which will 
be the focus of this report. Lentivirus, on the other hand, is an example of a complex retrovirus. One 
drawback associated with γ-retroviruses is that they are only able to infect actively dividing cells. For 
transduction in non-dividing cells, the more complex lentivirus is often recommended. 
 
The basic retroviral genome consists of single-stranded RNA, which is 7-12 kb in length49. It contains 
three critical genes important for transcription of retroviral RNA in the host nucleus and correct 
assembly of virions50,51. These are denoted gag, pol and env. The gag gene encodes structural proteins 
that make up the nucelocapsid shell of retroviral virions. The capsid is surrounded by a lipid envelope, 
into which virus-encoded envelope proteins are embedded. These surface-expressed glycoproteins 
play an important role in determining viral tropism and fusion-mediated entry into target cells. 
Enzymatic proteins are encoded by the pol gene and these include protease, reverse transcriptase and 
integrase. These enzymes are associated with the viral genome within the viral core.  
 
Production of retroviral particles is based on conventional transfection in a packaging cell line as 
illustrated in Figure 3. Retroviral vector packages exist in various formats, but tend to come with three 
plasmids: two packaging vectors that provide the necessary gag, pol and env genes and a retroviral 
expression vector carrying the GOI. All vectors are co-transfected into a packaging cell line and once 
inside the cells, efficient transcription and translation of the retroviral plasmids lead to production of 
viral proteins.

Upon recognition of the psi packaging signal (Ψ) on the recombinant viral RNA sequence, virus 
particles start to assemble close to the cell membrane. Newly produced and infectious virions leave 
the cell by budding, resulting in enveloped virus particles covered with envelope proteins. The 
supernatant of the transfected packaging cells is then collected and used to infect target cells. A virus 
particle enters a host cell by recognizing specific receptors on the cell surface. In a research setting, 
this process is often referred to as transduction as opposed to infection. Once inside the target cell, the 
recombinant RNA is reverse transcribed, and the cDNA is integrated into the host genome. Following 
successful integration, the viral genome is stably and continuously expressed and depending on the 
construct, the recombinant proteins may be displayed on the cell surface.  
 
Sequences responsible for virion assembly, viral integration and replication within host cells must be 
included in the retroviral vectors. Therefore, construction of these follows a common strategy. Foreign 
DNA is inserted into a pair of long terminal repeats (LTRs), which are important for transcription and 
integration of the viral genome (Figure 4). Each LTR can be divided into three distinct regions: U3, R 
and U5. The U3 region contains important enhancer and promoter signals enabling host RNA 
polymerase II to bind. This region is flanked by the R region, which is where transcription begins. The 
U5 region is together with R believed to be important for the reverse transcription process52.  
 
==PAGE:19==
11 
 
 
Figure 4. Example of a typical retroviral expression vector showing the critical cis-acting elements. In this 
illustration, transcription of the foreign gene is driven by an internal promoter. The promoter also drives 
expression of an antibiotic resistance markers via the inclusion of an internal ribosome entry site (IRES) element.  
 
All elements in the retroviral expression vector need to be cis-acting to allow efficient transcription of 
viral mRNA, packaging and integration. Transcription of RNA is by definition initiated at the beginning 
of the R region within the 5’ LTR and continues through the provirus terminating at the R sequence in 
the 3’ LTR by the addition of a polyadenylation (pA) tail. Flanking the 5’ LTR, a viral packaging signal 
is located. This sequence is responsible for directing vector RNA into newly formed virions.  
 
Retroviral vectors are designed to be either replication-competent or replication-defective. The latter 
type is frequently more convenient to handle for certain research applications. Often viral proteins 
only need to be synthesized in packaging cells and not in target cells, as the viral genome is integrated 
into the host cell genome. Modifying or deleting regions critical for virion replication in the LTRs can 
render retroviruses self-inactivating. These modified viruses are still capable of entering target cells 
and delivering the viral load, but are unable to replicate further within the host cells. Another common 
approach to minimize the risk of generating replication-competent virus is to use separate retroviral 
plasmids carrying the gag-pol and env genes53–55. Provision of the packaging and enzymatic genes in 
trans reduces the chance of homologous recombination events. 
 
If self-inactivating LTRs are included in the plasmid, the promoter in the 5’ LTR cannot be used to drive 
expression of the transgene in target cells. Frequently, an internal promoter is included as an 
alternative approach56. In many MMLV-based retroviral vectors, the eukaryotic cytomegalovirus 
(CMV) promoter is incorporated upstream of a multiple cloning site, into which the GOI can be 
inserted. This strong CMV promoter is known to produce constitutive protein expression in a variety 
of mammalian cells. However, several studies show that the use of strong internal promoters and/or 
enhancers may lead to a phenomenon called promoter interference57–59. If the included transcriptional 
elements, for example, suppress the 5’ LTR promoter, then reduced viral titer might be observed.  
 
Another common strategy used in retroviral vector design is the use of an internal ribosome entry site 
(IRES) allowing translation of more than one gene from a single promoter60. Often, the coding sequence 
of the GOI is located upstream the IRES element and a drug-resistance gene acts as the second open 
reading frame in the bicistronic transcript. Linking antibiotic resistance to the expression of a 
particular gene is often very useful for selection of stable cell lines as previously mentioned.  
 
==PAGE:20==
12 
 
Some scientists might be tempted to include sequences in the retroviral vector required for high-level 
protein expression in eukaryotic cells. However, not all genetic elements are compatible with currently 
available retroviral packaging systems. They can decrease virus titer if they, for example, interfere with 
transportation of mRNA from the nucleus to the cytoplasm or express truncated viral genomes that 
cannot be packaged efficiently. In a study, performed by Blø and co-workers, retroviral vectors 
incorporating either an optimized intron or a strong pA signal were engineered to determine whether 
such elements influence virus titer61. The results showed that introns embedded in the retroviral 
constructs were removed by splicing events prior to packaging. Loss of the transgene intron did not 
lead to a reduction in infection efficiency. On the other hand, a more dramatic effect was observed with 
retroviral plasmids incorporating a pA sequence flanking the transgene. Transcription of the vector 
inside the packaging cell line yielded an ineffective truncated product lacking the critical 3’ LTR. The 
presence of a strong pA site in the same direction as the 5’ LTR promoter lead to complete loss of virion 
production.  
 
As previously mentioned, this report focuses on MMLV-based retroviral vectors. This retrovirus 
subgroup has been extensively applied in gene delivery mainly because of the simplicity of its genome. 
MMLV can be further divided into three groups based on host specificity. The wild-type ecotropic virus 
is specific for the murine cationic amino acid transporter 1 (mCAT-1) found on mice and rat cells and 
can therefore, only infect cells of rodent origin62. Amphotropic virus has a broader host range and is 
capable of delivering genes to most mammalian cell types including human cells. The sodium 
phosphate transporter (PiT2) acts as specific receptor for amphotropic virus63. Finally, a pantropic 
version of MMLV is available. This type of virus is also termed “pseudotyped” and can infect any 
mammalian cell line. Pantropic virus is covered with vesicular stomatitis virus G protein (VSV-G), 
which recognizes phospholipid structures present in the cell membrane instead of a particular 
receptor64,65. Depending on which envelope protein is used, special safety precautions must be taken. 
As both the amphotropic and pantropic virus are capable of infecting primary human cells, all 
experiments involving these must be handled under Biosafety Level 2 conditions. 
 
2.5 FLOW CYTOMETRY 
 
Fluorescent-activated cell sorting (FACS) is a high-throughput analytical technology capable of 
separating single cells in a heterogeneous suspension based on fluorescence and light scattering 
properties of the cells. The isolation of single cells allows the particles to be analyzed for several 
physical characteristics individually. Antibody expression can, for example, rapidly and quantitatively 
be determined directly on the cell surface. Thus, mammalian display coupled with FACS provides a 
powerful tool for screening of large antibody repertoires and selection of rare antibody clones with 
desired binding affinity66.  
 
 
==PAGE:21==
13 
 
2.5.1 Principles of flow cytometry 
 
A typical flow cytometer is made up of four basic components: fluidic system, lasers, optical system 
and detection platform (Figure 5). The function of the fluidic system is to separate cells in suspension 
into single particles. This setup enables the analysis of single cells moving in a liquid stream. When the 
cells pass a focused laser beam, scattered light is measured. Light refracted in the forward direction is 
related to the size of the cells, whereas side-scattered light is proportional to cell granularity. Thus, a 
cell with a large volume and a complex internal structure will have high forward scatter (FSC) and side 
scatter (SSC) compared to a small and simple cell.  
 
 
Figure 5. Schematic diagram of a typical flow cytometer. In the nozzle, cells in suspension are single-sorted within 
a fluid stream, before they pass through a laser beam. Forward and side scattered light are detected from all cells, 
and fluorescent emission is detected from stained cells. Separate fluorescence (FL) channels and optical filters 
(referred to as dicroic mirrors in the figure) are used to obtain emission signals. (Adapted from 
http://utbiorad.casaccia.enea.it.) 
 
To enable detection of specific characteristics in addition to morphology, the cells need to be labeled 
with fluorescent probes. This allows emitted fluorescent light to be detected when the individual 
particles pass through the laser. The blue Argon-ion laser, which emits a 488 nm light, is most 
commonly used because of its ability to excite multiple fluorophores. Several fluorochromes exciting 
at the same wavelength may be used simultaneously if they generate peak emissions at distinct 
wavelengths. The two most popular fluorophores for labeling are fluorescein isothiocyanate (FITC) 
and phycoerythrin (PE), both excited at 488 nm. The emission peaks of these are distinctly different 
(530 nm and 580, respectively) to allow their signals to be detected by different detectors. The filters 
included in the optical system only allow a narrow range of wavelengths to pass. Thus, FITC is detected 
in the fluorescence channel 1 (FL1), whereas PE is detected in FL2.  
 
When using multiple fluorochromes at the same time, the emission spectrum of one fluorophore might 
spill over into the emission spectrum of another. This phenomenon is referred to as spectral overlap 
and is commonly seen with FITC and PE (Figure 6). In this case, some of the FITC emission is 
==PAGE:22==
14 
 
transmitted by the PE filter, meaning that FITC is detected by more than one detector. Fortunately, 
spectral spillover can be corrected by means of compensation. This is done by subtracting a portion of 
the FITC signal from the PE signal. Most modern analysis software is able to do this automatically.  
 
 
Figure 6. Spectral overlap. The emission spectra of FITC and PE are shown. A proportion of the FITC emission 
overlaps in the FL2 detection channel. (Figure generated using BD Fluorescence spectrum viewer, 
http://www.bdbiosciences.com.) 
 
The detection platform is generally an external computer, which converts light signals into electronic 
data, enabling the researcher to perform analysis using appropriate software. Data analysis usually 
involves plotting FSC and SSC to identify subpopulations of interest. These are then gated to allow 
further analysis of their fluorescent signals.

3 PURPOSE OF PROJECT 
 
3.1 HYPOTHESIS 
 
If large antibody repertoires can be stably expressed on the cell surface of mammalian cells by means of 
a retroviral-based display technology, it will be possible to screen the library for antigen binding by flow 
cytometry in a high-throughput manner. Furthermore, if the system is developed in a way that permits 
simultaneous secretion of antibodies, direct functional testing can be performed on the soluble antibodies 
without recovery of antibody genes and subcloning of the individual constructs.  
 
3.2 PROJECT AIM 
 
The main objective of the present master’s thesis project is to develop a robust mammalian cell display 
platform allowing simultaneous surface display and secretion of large and diverse antibody libraries.  
 
One of the limitations coupled with phage and yeast display is the inefficient identification of biological 
functional immunoglobulins for therapeutic use. Such systems often require conversion of antibody 
fragments to full-length antibodies, which is time-consuming and may result in loss of affinity. Thus, 
expression and selection of antibody repertoires in their natural eukaryotic environment is highly 
desired. In this project, we aim to develop a mammalian cell-based system allowing expression of 
whole IgG molecules anchored to the cell membrane for affinity screening and avidity enrichment. An 
additional attractive feature to the platform would be the possibility to convert the membrane-bound 
form to soluble antibody for functional screening and validation. Such alternative display method 
would enable high-throughput screening by circumventing subcloning and re-expression. To include 
this desirable feature in our platform, we will design our constructs in a way that allows regulation of 
membrane-bound IgG for initial affinity screening and soluble antibody for downstream applications 
– an approach referred to as regulated readthrough. 
 
In the longer term, the mammalian display platform will be used for antibody library screening in 
Symphogen. In addition to the ability to produce both surface-anchored and soluble antibodies, the 
technology should enable uniform antibody expression. If a single cell expresses multiple antibody 
clones with varying specificity, identification and isolating of antibodies with desired affinity is 
challenged. Furthermore, a stable expression system is desired to allow multiple rounds of enrichment 
of specific cell populations. FACS-based screening and enrichment would allow us to identify antibody 
clones exhibiting low reactivity towards a difficult target. Thus, to ensure that the antibody genes are 
stably integrated into the host cell genomes and each cell only expresses one antibody clone, the 
mammalian display technology will be based on retroviral transduction. By controlling the multiplicity 
of infection, we believe we can achieve single copy integration.

4 EXPERIMENTAL STRATEGY 
 
 
Depending on the desired screening system, antibodies can generally be expressed in two different 
manners: (i) mammalian display or (ii) soluble expression. We wish to test a third approach based on 
alternative display. The first alternative display method is based on translational stop codon 
readthrough. It is expected that this option will enable simultaneous display and secretion of the same 
antibody clone from a single cell. Regulated translational readthrough has previously been tested for 
a recombinant monomeric protein7, but not for IgG expression. Immunoglobulins are made up of 
several domains and therefore, we need to investigate whether this approach is able to generate 
correctly folded and functional antibodies. We also aim to include another alternative display method 
based on furin cleavage, as this approach successfully has been used to produce both surface-displayed 
and soluble antibodies previously47. Expression vectors with translational stop codon readthrough or 
a furin cleavage site (FCS) will be used to study the relationship between displayed and secreted 
antibody levels. Also, they will be compared to an expression vector containing only the GPI anchor. 
Ideally, we would prefer to use the regulated readthrough method in our mammalian display platform 
due to Symphogen’s in-house knowledge and experience about this. If neither regulated readthrough 
nor furin cleavage is capable of displaying sufficient antibody levels for cell-based selection, then 
option (i) will be used for the affinity screen. 
 
To facilitate translational readthrough, we will incorporate the UGAC tetranucleotide sequence 
between a known antibody gene and a GPI anchor. This specific sequence is chosen because it has been 
shown to result in optimal readthrough compared to other nonsense codons. Immediately upstream 
of the GPI-coding sequence, a V5 epitope tag will be included to monitor antibody expression. In the 
furin cleavage-based vector, we will replace the UGAC sequence with a furin cleavage site with the 
following amino acid sequence: Arg-Ile-Arg-Arg. 
 
One of the main problems associated with mammalian display is the risk of transfecting a cell with 
several plasmids. We aim to develop a novel mammalian cell-based display platform with the ability 
to integrate one antibody clone per cell, thereby facilitating easy comparison of cells expressing 
different antibodies and isolating those with desired affinity. To obtain this, we will take advantage of 
retroviruses. The project will include a number of proof of concept experiments to demonstrate 
retrovirus production and transduction for both green fluorescent protein (GFP) and IgG display.  
 
Special precautions must be taken when working with retrovirus in the laboratory. As we wish to work 
with a retrovirus that is both safe and simple, MMLV appears to be a suitable choice. Other viruses, 
such as lenti- and adenovirus, were also considered but will not be used due to their complexity.  
 
Appropriate cell lines must be selected for the retroviral setup. The adherent 293FT cell line derived 
from a human embryonic kidney line is commonly used for production of virus particles and will be 
used as producer cell line in this study. It is a fast-growing cell line that has been transfected with the 
==PAGE:25==
17 
 
SV40 large T antigen, allowing high levels of protein to be expressed when using plasmids containing 
the SV40 origin of replication. To produce retrovirus, the 293FT cells must be co-transfected with a 
retroviral expression vector and viral packaging vectors. Alternatively, we could have chosen the 
Phoenix™ helper-free retrovirus producer cell line, which is also based on the 293T cell line. This cell 
line only requires transfection with the retroviral expression plasmid, as it stably expresses the 
retroviral gag and pol genes along with either the ecotropic or amphotropic envelope protein. 
However, we would have to pay a license in order to use the Phoenix™ cell line.  
 
Ideally, we wish to use a cell line adapted to suspension culture for mammalian antibody display. 
Suspension cells are generally used for recombinant protein expression and production. Furthermore, 
cells in suspension are much easier to passage and handle in contrast to adherent cells, which require 
enzymatic dissociation. Since Symphogen has extensive experience with CHO-S and HEK293 cells, 
these two cell lines are selected as potential target cell lines for mammalian antibody display. However, 
before performing transduction experiments on these cell lines, we want to test our experimental 
setup using a suitable control cell line. The adherent mouse embryonic fibroblast cell line NIH3T3 
serves this purpose. Because the cell line is of murine origin, we will produce ecotropic virus particles 
and use these for infection. As neither the CHO-S nor HEK293 cells are of murine origin, we need to try 
different approaches to allow them to take up the virus.

located immediately upstream of the first MCS. Insertion of transgenes into pQCXIX was performed by

5.2.2 Antibody expression vector 
 
To display immunoglobulins on the cell surface of mammalian cells, the IgG molecule needs to be 
attached to a membrane-anchoring motif. Thus, before cloning a known antibody gene into the pQCXIX

peptide tag flanked by a GPI anchor was used for this purpose. GPI is a natural occurring glycolipid 
that attaches the C-terminal of the protein to the membrane. The V5 tag was used to monitor the 
expression level of the individual construct. Furthermore, we added a Woodchuck hepatitis

and virus titer67. The gene string including the V5, GPI and WPRE sequences is shown in Figure 7 and 
was ordered from GenArt. By including this sequence downstream of the antibody gene, our construct 
should be able to display antibody on the cell surface via the GPI anchor. An AgeI restriction site was 
included immediately downstream of the GPI anchor (not shown in figure) to remove the 
transmembrane domain for soluble antibody production.

5.3 CELL CULTURE 
 
The following cell lines were used in the project: FreeStyle™ CHO-S (Invitrogen, R800-07), a Chinese 
hamster ovary cell line adapted to suspension culture; FreeStyle™ 293-F (Invitrogen, R790-07), 
derived from the human embryonic kidney 293 cell line and adapted to suspension culture; 293FT 
(Invitrogen, R700-07), an adherent human embryonic kidney-derived cell line; and NIH3T3 (ATCC, 
CRL-1658), an adherent mouse embryonic fibroblast cell line. The 293-F cell line is hereafter referred 
to as HEK293. CHO-S cells were cultured in FreeStyle™ CHO Expression medium supplemented with 
100 units/ml penicillin, 100 µg/ml streptomycin and L-glutamine to a final concentration of 8 mM. 
HEK293 cells were grown in FreeStyle™ 293 Expression medium containing 500 µg/ml Geneticin. 
293FT and NIH3T3 cell cultures were maintained in Dulbecco’s modified Eagle medium (DMEM) 
supplemented with 10% heat-inactivated fetal bovine serum (FBS), 100 units/ml penicillin and 100 
µg/ml streptomycin. Additionally, the growth medium of the 293FT cell line contained 0.1 mM MEM 
non-essential amino acids (NEAA) and 500 µg/ml Geneticin.  
 
The adherent cell cultures were grown in T75 tissue culture flasks (Thermo Scientific) at 37°C in a 
humidified environment with 5% CO2. The cells were passaged at 70-80% confluency every 48-72 
hours. The split was done by washing the cells in 10 ml pre-warmed 1X Phosphate Buffer Saline (PBS) 
(Gibco), followed by five minutes incubation with 2 ml TrypLE Select. Trypsination was inactivated by 
adding 8 ml fresh growth medium. Finally, the desired amount of cells was transferred to a new T75 
flask and medium was added to a final volume of 25 ml.  
 
The suspension-adapted CHO-S and HEK293 cell lines were grown in shaker flasks (Thermo Scientific) 
in a 37°C incubator with 5% CO2 on a shaker platform rotating at 150 rpm. The cells were passaged 
every 48-72 hours by determining the cell density and calculating the split ratio required to seed a new 
flask with a final concentration of 0.3 x 106 viable cells/ml.

5.4 TRANSIENT TRANSFECTION 
 
Invitrogen’s FreeStyle™ CHO-S Expression system was employed for transient transfection of DNA 
plasmids using CHO-S cells growing in suspension.  
 
DNA constructs were transfected into CHO-S cells using FreeStyle™ MAX transfection reagent 
according to the enclosed protocol. On the day prior transfection, cells were passaged at 0.7 x 106 
cells/ml. Transfections were set up in 10 ml scales in 125 ml shaker flasks and cells were passaged to 
a density of 1.0 x 106 cells/ml, given that the cell viability was above 95%. For each 10 ml transfection 
volume, we used 12.5 µg of plasmid DNA and 12.5 µl MAX reagent – each mixed with OptiPRO™ to give 
a total volume of 200 µl. Next, the MAX mix was added to the DNA mix and incubated for 10 minutes 
at room temperature before slowly adding the whole mixture to the cells, while swirling the flask.  
 
Cell density and viability was assessed 24 hours post transfection. Expression levels were analyzed by 
flow cytometry as described in Section 5.7.

Eppendorf tube, where after we directly added 1 ml supernatant. The whole content was added to the

place by centrifuging the plate for 1 hour at 500 x g, followed by incubation at 37°C and 5% CO2. After 
4-5 hours incubation, the supernatant was removed and replaced with 3 ml fresh medium. The cells 
were put back in the incubator and left overnight.  
 
Analysis of the cells were performed either 24 or 48 hours post transduction.

6 RESULTS 
 
Here we describe the initial experiments conducted in the development of retroviral vectors for GFP 
and IgG expression in mammalian cells. Following this section, we describe the results obtained from 
transient transfection of mammalian cells to study various alternative display methods. We mainly 
focused on a strategy based on translational stop codon readthrough, but results based on furin 
cleavage were also included. In the last part of the Results Section, we describe how retroviral 
transduction could be used to establish protein expression in a mammalian cell line. A number of 
experimental factors were tested to optimize our protocol. These results all provided valuable 
knowledge on mammalian display, which can be used in the ongoing development of the discovery 
platform.  
 
6.1 RETROVIRAL VECTORS FOR GFP AND ANTIBODY DISPLAY 
 
The MMLV-derived retroviral vector pQCXIX was used as a backbone to construct new expression 
vectors as described in Section 5.2. Vector pQC-GFP was derived from pQCXIX by cloning the sequence 
coding for GFP into the plasmid downstream of the internal CMV promoter (Figure 12). The GFP 
display vector was used as a positive control for viral transduction.

6.2 ANALYSIS OF ANTIBODY EXPRESSION BY FLOW CYTOMETRY 
 
For the proposed mammalian display system to be successful, we aimed to show that the displayed 
antibody was properly assembled and expressed on the cell surface. In addition, it was critical to 
demonstrate that the displayed antibody retained binding to its soluble antigen. Thus, we transiently

Because the light chain did not contain an anchor sequence, it could only be detected if it had been 
correctly folded with its heavy chain domain on the cell surface. Following staining, the cells were 
analyzed by FACS.

6.3 MAMMALIAN DISPLAY USING ALTERNATIVE DISPLAY METHODS 
 
One of the limitations with mammalian display technology is that flow cytometry-based affinity 
screening requires attachment of the antibodies to the cell surface via a membrane anchor, whereas a 
significant level of soluble IgG is needed to assess functional properties. It has previously been shown 
that a recombinant monomeric protein can be targeted to either the cell membrane of mammalian cells 
or into the culture medium by aminoglycoside-mediated translational readthrough7. Based on these 
observations, we wanted to assess whether this strategy could be applied to immunoglobulins. Thus, 
we designed a vector (pQC-BI-mAb-UGAC-GPI) incorporating an inefficient stop codon between the 
heavy chain of a known antibody gene and a GPI anchor. We also investigated another strategy based 
on furin cleavage, as previously described47. Thus, a FCS-containing construct (pQC-BI-mAb-FCS-GPI) 
was designed. A plasmid that did neither include a nonsense stop codon nor a furin cleavage sequence 
between the antibody gene and the GPI anchor was included as a positive control for membrane 
display and negative control for soluble antibody production. Additionally, a vector missing the GPI-
coding sequence was included and used as negative FACS control and positive secretion control. To 
compare the ratio of membrane-anchored to secreted antibody, the four constructs were transiently 
transfected into CHO-S cells. The cells were stained with FITC-labeled antibody specific for V5 and PE-
labeled antigen, followed by FACS analysis. An appropriate gating strategy was applied to remove 
cellular debris and clumps from the analysis. Thus, the dot plots shown in this report all represent 
populations of single, live cells.  
 
The relative amounts of surface-bound and secreted antibodies obtained from cells transfected with 
the different constructs are shown in Figure 15. As expected, the vast majority of cells from the pQC-
BI-mAb-GPI transfection displayed antibodies on the cell surface. For this particular experiment, more 
than 97% of the live cells were considered positive for antibody display, indicating very high 
transfection efficiency. On the other hand, cells transfected with the vector lacking the GPI anchor were 
incapable of expressing detectable levels of displayed antibody. Instead, these cells expressed soluble 
==PAGE:39==
31 
 
antibodies, which could be detected 24 hours post transfection. No significant amount of soluble 
antibodies was detected for the pQC-BI-mAb-GPI transfection. 
 
 
Figure 15. Proof of concept for mammalian displaying using alternative display methods. The experiment was 
performed using transient transfection. Cells were analyzed for antibody display by FACS 24 hours post 
transfection. At the same time, the supernatant was used to determine the level of soluble antibody expression. MFI 
values for FITC and PE, respectively, are given for each method. 
 
Not surprisingly, we observe a lower percentage of cells displaying detectable antibody level when 
using the pQC-BI-mAb-UGAC-GPI construct. Focusing on the mean fluorescence intensity (MFI) values 
for FITC and PE, a 5-12 fold decrease regarding IgG display in the FACS-positive cell population was 
seen. Nevertheless, ~27% of the analyzed cells were FACS-positive, which is still a significant 
proportion – especially considering that these cells were not even treated with aminoglycoside to 
induce translational readthrough. These findings demonstrate that the basal level of stop codon 
readthrough is compatible with FACS-based applications.  
 
As with the 100% display and the translational readthrough constructs, the method based on furin 
cleavage also lead to detection of surface-bound antibody on the mammalian cells. In fact, we observe 
a much larger percentage of positive cells and higher MFI values as compared to the UGAC-containing 
construct. However, there was no linear correlation between FITC and PE, reflected by the smear 
towards the V5-FITC side. Moreover, scattered subpopulations were observed in the plot. This could 
be explained by the fact that furin cleavage was not identical in all cells. Thus, a cell with efficient furin 
cleavage would express high level of V5 but yield low antigen binding, because the majority of 
antibodies would be secreted without being linked to GPI. A cell exhibiting inefficient furin cleavage 
would express the same level of V5 assuming that protein expression levels are the similar in the two 
==PAGE:40==
32 
 
cells. However, significantly more antibodies would remain attached to the cell’s surface leading to a 
higher FITC signal.  
 
Even though it would be advantageous to have such a high IgG display as obtained by the furin cleavage 
approach, we would prefer to have a linear correlation between V5 display and antigen binding.  
 
Both alternative display methods studied here were able to produce soluble IgG levels comparable to 
the construct lacking the GPI anchor.  
 
6.4 AMINOGLYCOSIDE-INDUCED TRANSLATIONAL READTHROUGH 
 
To study the influence of aminoglycoside on translational readthrough we performed two identical but 
disparate experiments. Factors, such as transfection efficiency and staining procedure, can have a 
relatively big impact on the final outcome. Therefore, we wanted to be sure that the same tendency 
was observed upon repetition of the experiment. However, to truly verify reliability, the experiment 
should be repeated several more times.  
 
Once again, CHO-S cells were transfected with the UGAC-containing vector and different 
concentrations of Geneticin, ranging from 12 to 100 µg/ml, were added to the cells. By studying the 
level of basal readthrough of the two experiments, we can conclude that the efficiency of these 
transfections were lower than the previous one described in Section 6.3. Whereas we were able to 
obtain a background level as high as 27% previously, the two experiments described here yielded 17% 
and 22% FACS-positive cells, respectively. Thus, transfection efficiency clearly had an impact on our 
analysis and the degree of cells detected as positive for IgG display.  
 
When the transfected cells were subject to various Geneticin concentrations, a moderate increase in 
the percentage of living cells displaying detectable levels of membrane-associated antibody was 
observed. The graph shown in the top half of Figure 16 shows that the FACS-positive cell population 
on average increased from 20% at basal readthrough to almost 30% at the optimal Geneticin 
concentration of 75 µg/ml. Both experiments yielded a maximum at this concentration. Using a higher 
Geneticin concentration (i.e. 100 µg/ml) led to a small drop in the fraction of cells positive for antibody 
display. The bottom half of Figure 16 illustrates a representative example of the dot plots from one of 
the experiments created in the FACS analysis. In this particular case, one third of the cells was regarded 
as FACS-positive in the presence of Geneticin. 
 
We believe that the moderate change in percentage of positive cells upon drug treatment was due to 
relatively low transfection efficiency. Cells that had not been successfully transfected, would obviously 
not be regulated by the presence of aminoglycoside. Whether Geneticin was present in the culture 
medium or not, these cells did not carry any antibody-coding genes. However, we cannot conclude to 
what extent transfection efficiency influenced the results. Due to the fact that our results were obtained 
==PAGE:41==
33 
 
from transiently transfected cells, we will need to repeat the experiment using stable pools. The 
purpose of this is to evaluate the exact impact of Geneticin-induced translational readthrough that can 
be expected when the setup is extended to IgG libraries.

indicating that Geneticin was able to efficiently induce translational readthrough in the FACS-positive 
cell population.

6.5 VIRAL TRANSDUCTION 
 
Due to time constraints, several of the experiments described in this section were only performed once. 
Thus, the results should be considered preliminary until verified by future studies.  
 
To establish a platform for the selection of high-affinity human antibodies in their IgG format, a stable 
expression system is generally desired. A distinct advantage of using a stable cell line for mammalian 
display is that it allows flow cytometry-based enrichment of rare antibody clones by multiple rounds 
of selection. Another attractive feature of mammalian display systems is uniform antibody expression, 
entailing that each host cell only expresses one antibody clone with defined specificity. Antibody 
repertoire screening is dependent upon such characteristic to allow proper comparison and isolation 
of cells exhibiting high-affinity target binding.  
 
We believe that single copy integration and stable protein expression in mammalian cells can be 
obtained by exploiting the principles of retroviruses. Thus, retroviral expression vectors were 
constructed based on the genome of MMLV. To test the feasibility of our experimental setup, we 
initially performed transduction experiments with a retroviral GFP construct. Briefly, we co-
transfected the necessary retroviral plasmids into 293FT packaging cells to promote production of 
virus particles. Next, the supernatant from these cells was harvested and used for transduction of the 
adherent NIH3T3 cell line. Since this cell line is of murine origin, we were able to work with the 
ecotropic envelope protein under Biosafety Level I conditions. We found that ecotropic virus particles 
successfully could be produced in packaging cells and that subsequent transduction of target cells lead 
to significant GFP expression. 
 
In an attempt to improve efficiency of retroviral transduction using the NIH3T3 cell line, the effect of 
various parameters on GFP expression was tested. First of all, we plated out different amounts of 
293FT, as the condition of the cells at the time of infection is known to have a big impact on their ability 
to produce high virus titer. It is, for example, extremely important that the cells are not over confluent 
or form clumps. Secondly, we tested whether serum had an effect on the setup. Thus, when changing 
the medium of the virus-producing 293FT cells on Day 2, it was replaced with fresh culture medium 
containing either 2% or 10% FBS. Although transfection agent FuGENE does not require removal of 
serum, the presence of serum may still influence transduction efficiency of the target cell line. A third 
parameter under study was spin-fection, as many studies have shown that this process is able to 
enhance retroviral transduction.  
 
The most significant finding was that spin-fection appeared to be superior in mediating transduction 
of target cells as compared to traditional co-cultivation. In Figure 19, it is clearly seen that NIH3T3 cells 
==PAGE:45==

subjected to spin-fection expressed higher levels of GFP compared to cells that did not receive this 
treatment. We believe this observation was due to the fact that spin-fection allowed the virus particles 
to become deposited on the surface of target cells, thereby making fusion easier. Keeping focus on the 
centrifuged cell populations, we found that the optimal number of 293FT cells to plate was 1.0 x 106. 
As expected, the confluency of the packaging cells affected efficiency of transduction. Furthermore, the 
data demonstrated that the concentration of serum in the culture medium did not have a significant 
impact on transduction when target cells were subject to spin-fection. However, in the case of co-
cultivation, the lower concentration appeared to yield a slight improvement in efficiency.

7 DISCUSSION 
 
 
The majority of antibody-based drugs available on the market are derived from conventional 
hybridoma technology. However, as the demand for therapeutic antibodies for treatment of human 
diseases rapidly grows, so does the need for novel and sophisticated discovery platforms to identify 
viable candidates. In an attempt to improve efficacy and potency of antibody drugs, the 
biopharmaceutical industry has turned its attention towards platforms based on recombinant in vitro 
screening. As a result, an increasing number of human antibodies derived from these emerging 
technologies are entering clinical studies14,68. Particularly display technologies present a valuable and 
versatile tool by enabling the selection and isolation of antibodies with remarkable properties. Phage 
and yeast display technologies have been widely used to identify antibody fragments with desired 
specificity, but these often require reconstruction to allow compatibility with soluble IgG 
manufacturing. This process is both technically challenging and can lead to loss of antigen affinity. 
Thus, a mammalian expression system is the desired platform for isolation of human full-length 
antibody candidates for clinical use. The features rendering mammalian display optimal for human 
antibody discovery include native domain folding, eukaryotic glycosylation patterns, expression and 
secretion of full-length functional IgG molecules and cell-based affinity screening.  
 
Despite all the benefits associated with mammalian display technology, functional evaluation of the 
antibodies cannot be conducted, while they are still attached to the cell surface. Thus, extensive 
reformatting is required to allow soluble antibody production. This typically involves recovery of 
antibody-encoding genes by RT-PCR, subcloning of these into new constructs lacking a membrane-
anchoring domain and re-transfection. Multiple vectors and labor-intensive steps are needed to obtain 
this. As antibody-based drug development generally is associated with very large expenses, major 
efforts are constantly being made to reduce manufacturing costs69. A platform permitting 
simultaneous surface presentation and secretion of antibodies would provide a number of advantages 
over existing mammalian display technologies (Figure 21). It would allow initial high-throughput cell-
based affinity screening and direct functionality analysis of the soluble antibodies. By coupling these 
two features into a single cell, the challenging reformatting step can be bypassed, saving valuable time 
and costs.  
 
Furthermore, a much larger number of clones can be selected for functional characterization using 
alternative display methods. In the classic mammalian display setup, all clones must be kept separate 
to maintain correct pairing of the rescued variable heavy and light chain genes. This isolation of clones 
only allows up to 500 antibodies to be analyzed for biological activity. In contrast, alternative display 
methods do not require subcloning and are therefore, not limited by this capacity. Cells expressing 
antibodies with desired affinity can easily be single-sorted by flow cytometry allowing 10,000 of 
antibody clones to be tested for functionality. In turn, the probability of successfully identifying 
antibodies with optimal functional activity increases.  
 
==PAGE:49==
41 
 
 
Figure 21. Overview of the major steps involved in antibody discovery by classic mammalian display. By enabling 
expression of membrane-bound antibody and simultaneous production of the soluble version from the same cell, 
the reformatting process (marked in blue) can be bypassed. 
 
An additional benefit of using the same cell to express antibodies on the cell surface and produce 
soluble IgG is the possibility of selecting cells with high soluble production. Assuming that the 
alternative display method allows a correlation between the levels of displayed and secreted antibody, 
cells with high surface expression will naturally yield high soluble production as well. This has 
previously been shown to be the case for another recombinant protein7, and we predict the same to be 
true for antibodies. In the classic mammalian display setup, the situation is quite different. Although it 
is possible to select cells expressing high levels of antibody in the screening step, subcloning and re-
transfection do not ensure that the producer cells express antibody levels to the same extent. 
Recombinant protein expression is very dependent on how each cell responds to transfection. If a cell 
is more susceptible to transfection, more plasmids will be introduced into it, leading to relatively high 
expression. 
 
Recently, a mammalian expression system permitting simultaneous display and secretion of antibody 
has been described42. By including alternative splicing sites in a construct coding for an antibody linked 
to a transmembrane domain, different mature mRNA transcripts were generated from pre-mRNA as a 
result of splicing. One of these were translated into membrane-anchored antibody, whereas the other 
transcript lacked the transmembrane moiety and therefore, yielded a soluble form of the 
corresponding antibody. Another study has also successfully achieved simultaneous antibody display 
and secretion by taking advantage of the ineffective process of furin cleavage47. To our knowledge, 
these are the only two published strategies enabling antibody display and production of the soluble 
version from the same cell.  
 
We report here several proof of concept studies in the development of a novel and unique mammalian 
display technology for antibody discovery. Briefly, we successfully demonstrated that full-length and 
functional antibodies could be expressed on the surface of mammalian cells in the presence of a GPI 
membrane anchor. This finding is in agreement with observations made by Akamatsu and co-
workers40. Since GPI anchors are ubiquitous in most eukaryotic organisms, these glycolipids are 
==PAGE:50==
42 
 
generally considered a convenient alternative to transmembrane domains to attach recombinant 
proteins to the surface of mammalian cells. 
 
We designed our platform to exploit the mechanism of regulated translational readthrough, a process 
first described in 20067. This approach relies on the natural properties of the UGA nonsense codon. 
More importantly, the UGAC tetranucleotide sequence has proven to be relatively inefficient in 
terminating protein translation in eukaryotic cells. By fusing the 3’-end of a heavy chain to a GPI anchor 
in frame and separating these by such an inefficient stop codon, we showed that both membrane-
bound and secreted antibody could be generated from the same cell. Furthermore, translational 
readthrough could be promoted in the presence of Geneticin in the culture medium. The use of 
aminoglycoside-induced readthrough combined with a GPI anchor for simultaneous expression of 
surface-displayed and secreted antibody from the same cell has not previously been reported.  
 
An interesting observation made during the project was that the fraction of cells displaying detectable 
level of membrane-bound antibody was relatively high even in the absence of aminoglycoside. In our 
best transfection experiment, up to 27% of the cells were regarded as positive. This was a very 
satisfactory finding, as it allows compatibility with flow cytometry-based IgG library sorting. In a 
previous study, the authors established a stable cell line to study the effects of translational 
readthrough using blood coagulation factor protein C instead of an antibody7. Using a construct 
harboring a fusion between protein C, the UGAC nonsense codon and a GPI anchor, they were able to 
obtain a basal readthrough of 19%.  
 
A possible reason why we observe a higher basal IgG display compared to protein C, is related to the 
multimeric nature of antibodies. To detect antibody expression on the cell surface, only one GPI-
associated heavy chain is necessary. Therefore, the probability of having a multimeric protein 
containing at least one GPI is higher as compared to that of monomeric proteins. It has previously been 
demonstrated that readthrough of the UGAC tetranucleotide occurs at a frequency of 4%45. In the case 
of immunoglobulins, we calculate that approximately 8% of the full-length antibodies will contain at 
least one heavy chain domain fused to a GPI-anchoring moiety (Figure 22). Clearly, it would be very 
interesting to see how much the percentage of positive cells could be increased by repeating the 
experiment in a stable cell pool, which is the format for future IgG libraries.

Upon Geneticin treatment, we were able to increase the fraction of cells positive for surface-displayed 
antibody by approximately 10%. Because these results are based on expression of transiently 
transfected genes, we strongly believe a higher fold change can be obtained with a stable cell line. 
Although transient transfections offer an attractive approach for analysis of short-term protein 
expression, this approach has inherent limitations. The transfection efficiency varies from experiment 
to experiment, making statistical analysis difficult. Furthermore, the process of introducing foreign 
DNA into mammalian cells without comprising cell viability it often a difficult task. In addition, because 
transiently transfected cells do not incorporate the foreign gene into their cellular genome, cells with 
stable expression cannot be selected for. In contrast, stable cell lines are generally very useful for long-
term expression of exogenously introduced genes. However, establishment of stable cell pools is often 
challenging and may take several weeks. For this reason, stable transfection of cells is only used when 
stable pools are required for a particular purpose. Thus, in cases where a suitable stable cell line is not 
available, the ability to transiently transfect genes is highly beneficial.  
 
Part of this project was also to perform some preliminary studies in the development of a mammalian 
display platform based on stable transgene integration. Establishment of a stable cell line would allow 
us to perform several rounds of selection to enrich for cells displaying high-affinity antibody. An 
additional requirement of the platform should be uniform antibody expression to allow efficient 
comparison of antibodies with defined specificity. This is a particular important feature in the case of 
antibody repertoire screening. However, this is often a challenging task, as conventional transfection 
strategies tend to result in the introduction of multiple plasmids per host cell. Recombinant 
retroviruses offer an excellent approach for stable transgene insertion. Besides, the copy number can 
be controlled by varying the multiplicity of infection. Transduction of a target cell line with a retrovirus 
at an ideal multiplicity of infection should minimize the frequency of cells harboring more than one 
transgene.  
 
==PAGE:52==
44 
 
As MMLV-derived vectors generally are regarded as both safe and effective, we decided to use these to 
construct our retroviral expression plasmids. Using a GFP-expression construct and the ecotropic 
envelope protein, we demonstrated successful transduction of murine NIH3T3 cells. By doing a little 
optimizing of various parameters, we were able to significantly increase the number of cells expressing 
detectable levels of GFP. Most notably, spin-fection appeared to yield the highest transduction 
efficiency. However, when we moved on to antibody expression, disappointingly low levels of antibody 
expression were observed. The limited success we experienced with antibody expression in target 
cells based on retroviral transduction might be due to several factors as discussed below.  
 
First of all, we speculated whether the size of the antibody expression cassette put constraints on 
mRNA packaging. The upper limit on retroviral genome size appears to be approximately 8 kb70. As 
the total size of our mRNA was about 5 kb, we quickly excluded this possibility. If we wanted to actually 
test whether the size had an influence, we could have done this by adding extra genetic elements to 
the GFP construct, so that the sequence between the two LTR regions would be similar to the antibody 
expression vector. This new vector would then be tested for GFP expression in NIH3T3 cells by 
transduction.  
 
Promoter interference is another possible mechanism, which could explain the absence of antibody 
expression in target cells. In contrast to the GFP-expressing vector, expression of antibody heavy and 
light chain was driven by a bidirectional CMV promoter. The use of internal promoters offered an 
attractive strategy for us, as we preferred to work with replication-incompetent virus. Moreover, by 
incorporating a dual promoter that is active in both directions, we could limit the number of internal 
promoters in our construct. However, it has been suggested that the used bidirectional CMV promoter 
may compete with the viral promoters included in the retroviral plasmids71,72. In the packaging cell 
line, host transcription factors must be able to bind to viral enhancer and promoter sequences in the 
5’ LTR region. If these also bind to the internal promoter further downstream, then transcription of 
the full-length viral genome may be limited. As a result, the majority of transcripts will exist from the 
internal promoter and these cannot be assembled into virion particles because they lack a packaging 
signal. We could have run RT-PCR on mRNA from the packaging cell to troubleshoot this. However, 
since this procedure requires several steps such as RNA extraction and cDNA synthesis, we would 
prefer to avoid it.  
 
If promoter interference appears to be the issue in our experiments, we might try to an alternative 
promoter with less strong enhancer and promoter regions. In fact, we have already planned to perform 
experiments using a MSCV (murine stem cell virus) retroviral vector system. This vector utilizes a 
different promoter strategy compared to the MMLV-based plasmid. It contains LTR regions from the 
murine stem cell PCMV (porcine cytomegalovirus) virus. The LTR sequences are specially designed to 
enhance transcription activation. Furthermore, the PCMV LTR can be used to drive constitutive 
expression of transgenes in murine stem cells and most mammalian cell lines. This eliminates the need 
to include strong internal promoters in the retroviral vectors.  
 
==PAGE:53==
45 
 
We also considered whether the target cell lines were too hard to transduce. Maybe retroviral particles 
were efficiently produced in the packaging cell line, but were unable to infect the target cells. In future 
experiments, we want to be able to test for the presence of retroviral particles in the supernatant 
obtained from the packaging cell line. A number of kits allowing determination of the supernatant viral 
titer are available on the market. Although several studies have demonstrated that both CHO-S and 
HEK293 cells can be stably transduced with recombinant proteins using various viral expression 
systems73–76, limited data exist for these cell lines using a retrovirus-based strategy. In future 
experiments, a suitable cell line should always be included as a positive control to fully validate this. 
Furthermore, we want to try an alternative target cell line. We have already ordered a murine pre-B 
cell line termed BA/F3 from DSMZ. Similar to the CHO-S and HEK293 cells, the BA/F3 cells are grown 
in suspension culture. However, we need to be aware of the fact that this cell line might express 
surrogate heavy and light chain genes.

8 CONCLUSION 
 
 
The overall aim of this master’s thesis project was to perform a number of proof of concept 
experiments in the development of a novel mammalian display technology. We successfully 
demonstrated that full-length antibodies could be linked to the surface of mammalian cells via a GPI 
membrane anchor without compromising their binding affinities. Despite the many advantages 
associated with such a platform, functional evaluation of the antibodies cannot be undertaken while 
they are still attached to the cell membrane. To enable rapid discovery of antibodies with desired 
functional properties, we developed an alternative display method based on translational stop codon 
readthrough. By including the UGAC tetranucleotide sequence between the antibody and GPI anchor 
genes, it was possible to obtain simultaneous surface expression and secretion of the same antibody 
clone. Although translational readthrough could be induced in the presence of an aminoglycoside, our 
results indicate that the basal level was highly compatible with flow cytometry screening. The 
significance of this approach lies within its ability to allow high-throughput cell-based screening of the 
displayed antibodies and direct functional analysis of the soluble antibodies. 
 
Another important objective of the project was to establish a stable mammalian cell line through 
retroviral transduction. Retroviral expression vectors were constructed from a MMLV-derived 
plasmid. Our results demonstrate that ecotropic retrovirus particles could be produced in a packaging 
cell line and used for subsequent transduction of a suitable target cell line. Using this strategy, we were 
able to achieve significant GFP expression in a murine cell line. Due to time constraints, we were not 
able to extend the technique to antibody expression. Further investigation is needed to obtain this.  
 
In conclusion, our results provide a solid foundation to support the further development of the 
mammalian display platform.

9 FUTURE PERSPECTIVES 
 
 
Once proof of concept has been established for antibody expression using retroviral transduction, 
focus can be directed towards optimization of virus production and determination of the multiplicity 
of infection. Another important proof of concept experiment that should be performed in the near 
future involves enrichment of specific binders by FACS. This could, for example, be done by 
transducing cells with two known antibodies targeting each their antigen. This would result in 
mammalian cells expressing antibodies specific for either target I or target II. By only adding one of 
the target antigens to the mixture of cells and performing several selection rounds, it can be revealed 
whether flow cytometry can be used to isolate target-specific antibodies expression on the surface of 
mammalian cells.  
 
The experiments performed during this master’s thesis are part of a much larger development project 
in Symphogen. To efficiently compete with other biopharmaceutical companies specializing in the 
development of antibody therapeutics, Symphogen is constantly looking for new ways to discover safe, 
specific and high-affinity antibodies. In an attempt to achieve this, the company is currently developing 
new techniques for the generation of very large and diverse antibody repertoires, requiring high-
throughput screening platforms. This is where our project comes into play. Developing a mammalian 
display technology is a complex task, and this project was undertaken to perform some preliminary 
studies on the subject. Once the retroviral-based expression system is fully developed, it will become 
part of Symphogen’s integrated discovery platform.  
 
An overview of the screening phase is presented in Figure 23. The figure is included to show the reader 
how the mammalian display platform eventually will be used to isolate functional IgG molecules with 
desired properties in the early discovery phase. 
 
In Symphogen, immunoglobulins are generally obtained from either chickens or transgenic rats 
immunized with a validated target. After isolating the antibody-producing B-cells from these animals, 
variable genes will cloned into appropriate vectors to construct an antibody display repertoire. To 
produce retrovirus, a packaging cell line will be transfected with the necessary retroviral vectors. Next, 
the supernatant will be harvested and used for infection of a target cell line. This will generate a 
population of cells expressing antibody attached to the membrane, allowing high-throughput FACS-
based screening for antigen binding. By establishing a stable cell line, we will be able to perform several 
rounds of selection to enrich for specific binders.

Figure 23. The major steps involved in antibody library screening. The figure illustrates how our mammalian 
display technology eventually will become an integrated part of Symphogen’s antibody discovery platform.  
 
Once we have enriched for cells with high-level antibody expression, these will be single-sorted and 
used for manufacturing of soluble antibody. Because we exploit the principles of regulated 
readthrough, we do not need to subclone the candidate antibody genes into new vectors for soluble 
production. Finally, an assessment of the biological properties of each hit will be made to evaluate their 
therapeutic potential. At this stage, the outcome of a successful library screen will be the identification 
of a number of promising lead candidates that will progress into preclinical and possible clinical 
development. The ultimate goal is, of course, that one or more leads eventually will be marketed as a 
therapeutic product.
