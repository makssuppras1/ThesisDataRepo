Abstract
This master thesis studies the feasibility of a Change Detection approach in the con­
text of Surface Soil Moisture (SSM) Retrieval from Sentinel­1 Synthetic Aperture Radar
(SAR). The method implemented is based on the Short Term Change Detection (STCD)
algorithm previously proposed in several papers, which takes advantage of the alpha ap­
proximation that decouples the soil moisture contribution of the radar signal by taking the
VV co­polarized backscatter ratios between subsequent acquisitions. The reasoning be­
hind this is that the mentioned ratios will be only dependent on soil moisture changes if
and only if the other surface parameters that affect the reflected wave, namely surface
roughness and vegetation structure, hold constant during the N acquisitions processed.
Nevertheless, some upper and lower boundaries are needed for the SSM retrieval since
it relies on a Bounded Least Squares (BLS) method for the solution. In­situ data and al­
ready existing coarse resolution SSM products have been used in previous literature to
estimate them, with the drawback of needing some auxiliary data. Therefore, a new ap­
proach is explored in this study, which innovation lies on applying a simulated backscatter
forward model to estimate the boundaries in the retrieval, αmin and αmax. This is done by
means of a Look Up Table (LUT) containing simulated values of backscatter coefficient
σ0
V V and polarization amplitudes αV V . The Dubois model has been chosen for the simu­
lation of σ0
V V and this needs the surface roughness Root Mean Square (RMS) height as
an input. Hence, this one is roughly estimated directly from the backscatter data without
requiring any extra information. This has been tried out using the backscatter from the
different polarizations, σ0
V V and σ0
V H, and the depolarization ratio σ0
V H −σ0
V V . The algo­
rithm implementation developed has been tested over two different time series of SAR
acquisitions: first over 5 acquisitions in January 2018 and then over 10 acquisitions in
April and May 2018.
The SSM measurements provided by Hydrological OBsErvatory (HOBE) stations in Den­
mark, which is part of the International Soil Moisture Network (ISMN), have been used as
the main source for validation, allowing to compare them with the SSM estimated from the
backscatter measured by the satellite SAR instrument. Copernicus SSM product has also
been used for validation in the case of January, when two dates with frozen soil were en­
countered. It has been proven that the STCD algorithm is better suited for SSM retrieval
under frozen soil conditions than the Long Term Change Detection (LTCD) implemented
by Copernicus, obtaining better results when comparing with HOBE.
In the case of the January time series, SSM was estimated with a mean error of around
10% and a mean correlation coefficient R = 0.65 using all the 25 stations available for
validation. However, this gets as good as R = 0.78 excluding five outliers and a mean
error around 6% is obtained when using for validation stations with agriculture land cover
only. However in the case of the Spring time series more problems were encountered in
the validation in part due to the growing vegetation but also because of the low number of
stations without gaps in the time series (only 8 vs 25 in January). The best results for the
April and May time series were obtained using VH polarization to estimate hrms giving a
mean error around 9 % and a mean correlation coefficient R = 0.41, that was reduced to
6% and R = 0.59 when excluding one outlier.
Summarizing, the STCD algorithm implemented with a simulation approach to estimate
the boundaries of the retrieval, has been proved to be successful for SSM estimation,
although it is acknowledged that more work is needed in order to provide an operational
product for national or global level.

Introduction
Soil Moisture may be defined differently depending on the field, but roughly speak­
ing, it is the quantity of water within a soil volume, which is usually expressed as
water fraction by volume in m3 m−3 or multiplied by 100 as volumetric percentage
of water in soil in %. It is listed as one of the 50 Essential Climate Variables (ECV)
by the Global Climate Observation System (GCOS) in order to support the work of
both the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) and
the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC). These ECV are defined as
”any physical, chemical or biological variable or a group of linked variables that crit­
ically contributes to the characterization of Earth’ s climate” [1]. As such, soil mois­
ture is a key factor that plays a very important role in the exchange of heat, water
and biogeochemical fluxes between the atmosphere and the land surface through
evaporation, plant transpiration and other processes. Thus, it affects the weather
patterns and the occurrence of precipitation due to the fact that depending on the
spatial soil moisture pattern, cloud formation might be enhanced or suppressed [2].
Soil moisture can be retrieved by in­situ measurements, model simulations or re­
mote sensing. Due to its high temporal and spatial variability it is difficult to develop
and validate continuous datasets using only ground based measurements. More­
over, in­situ measuring and model simulations presents important spatial coverage
limitations. This is the reasoning behind remote sensing retrieval of soil moisture,
that can also eventually be used as independent data to validate simulations. Re­
mote sensing allows to map soil moisture in very isolated and inaccessible vast
extensions of land where otherwise would be impossible to measure.
Remote sensing of soil moisture include optical, thermal­infrared and microwaves
frequency bands. However, due to its lack of penetration through clouds and its
indirect physical link to soil moisture, optical and thermal­infrared are regarded as
less suitable for this purpose. Nevertheless, atmosphere is mostly transparent to
low frequency microwaves including Ku, X, C and L bands, which have already
demonstrated its potential for soil moisture retrieval with different algorithms. We
can differentiate between passive and active microwave sensors. Although passive
sensors may provide frequent observations, they do it with a poor spatial resolution.
Active sensors, such as SAR provide a finer resolution but at the expense of more
challenges regarding the retrieval of soil moisture. This is due to the combination
of vegetation structure, surface roughness and water content in the backscattering
coefficients.
Decoupling other effects than soil moisture in order to isolate its contribution in the
reflected radar signal is not an easy task. Despite of this, it has been the subject of
numerous studies, proving that remote sensing soil moisture estimates can be very
useful for a wide range of applications.
These include Numerical Weather Prediction (NWP), climate modelling and research,
hydrology, hydro­meteorological disasters (floods and droughts), agriculture, mon­
itoring of wetlands and riparian zones and other applications such as forestry or
ecossystem research. Depending on the application, the spatial and temporal res­
olution requirements may vary, for instance ranging from low spatial resolution and

high temporal resolution for NWP to high spatial resolution and low temporal res­
olution for forestry [2]. In this sense, accurate soil moisture retrievals from remote
sensing will have a positive impact on society, benefiting scholars in different fields
and helping to achieve the different United Nations (UN) Sustainable Development
Goals (SDG) addressing land and soil [3]. For example, they could be used for as­
similation of soil moisture or low­frequency microwave brightness temperature into

Literature Review
Any soil moisture retrieval from radar data relies on its relationship with the dielectric
constant ε. This can range from very low values in extremely dry areas to very high
values in areas saturated with water, thus extremely wet. The radar backscatter is
directly proportional to the dielectric constant, due to the fact that drier soils (lower ε)
will transmit easily the electromagnetic waves into the deeper layers of the medium,
thus attenuating the signal, while wet soils (ε) will reflect most of them in the surface,
almost not allowing penetration, so the reflected backscatter is higher.
There are several algorithms that have already been proved to be successful for
SSM retrieval. A first broad classification can be made distinguishing between co­
herent and incoherent methods, depending on the information they use from a SAR
acquisition, which is a Single Look Complex (SLC) image, that is to say, each image
pixel is represented by a complex (I and Q) magnitude value and therefore contains
both amplitude and phase information [4]. While the coherent methods use the
phase (coherent information) and apply interferometry techniques, the incoherent
algorithms use only the amplitude data (incoherent information). In the context of
Sentinel­1, that this master thesis focus in, these algorithms can also be divided
depending if they only use Sentinel­1 data (C­band) or if they combine it with either
some other microwave sensor data such as those from Soil Moisture Active Passive
(SMAP) and Soil Moisture and Ocean Salinity (SMOS) or with optical like Sentinel­2
or Landsat [5].
An example of an algorithm that uses some spectral information, namely Normal­
ized Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI) to constrain the retrieval is the MUltitem­
poral LEast Squares Moisture Estimator (MULESME) by Pulvirenti et al (Centro In­
ternazionale in Monitoraggio Ambientale of Liguria) [6]. It combines Sentinel­1 and
optical data and is based on a multi­temporal maximum likelihood approach used to
invert a forward model of backscattering from a bare soil, specifically the Oh model,
which uses only the surface height standard deviation s to represent soil roughness.
As any forward backscattering model it relates the state of the soil, in this case rep­
resented by SSM and s to the backscattering coefficient σ0. MULESME represents
this relationship through a LUT which combines 50 values of s, 100 values of SSM
and 13 values of incidence angle. The algorithm assumes that a time series of M+1
σ0 measurements are available at the current time and at M previous times. It imple­
ments a cost function that tries to minimize the squared difference between modeled
values of σ0 simulated using the LUT and the ones measured by the satellite ac­
quisition. Following the reasoning that the temporal variation in surface roughness
takes place at a slower pace than the soil moisture, the minimization assumes one
constant value of s and M+1 values of SSM in the M+1 acquisitions. Specifically,
M = 4 was chosen based on a trade­off between accuracy and computational time.
The backscatter used for the minimization has been previously rid of the vegetation
contribution, thus representing only the soil contribution. This is achieved by the re­
trieval of water plant content from optical data, exploiting its relationship with NDVI.
This relation is represented by means of a linear equation which has been devel­
oped for Italy, thus limitating to its territory the application of MULESME algorithm
[6].

Efforts on exploiting coherent and incoherent information have also been made by
Palmisano et al. [7] by combining two methods, one that relies on the closure phase,
namely the sum of the SAR phase over three acquisitions and other that does so
on the temporal changes in the backscatter, therefore using the ratios.
It has been found that some promising approaches rely on Change Detection tech­
niques [8], like the LTCD and STCD algorithms by Bauer­Marschallinger et al (Tech­
nical University of Wien) [9] and Balenzano et al (Consiglio Nazionale Delle Ricerche
of Rome) [10], respectively. These two algorithms take advantage of an incoherent
change detection approach, retrieving soil moisture at 1 km resolution [5].
The LTCD requires a very long data time series in order to estimate the driest and
wettest conditions possible. Thus, any SSM estimation is scaled between these two
values, meaning that is a relative measurement in terms of a percent between the
two extreme wetness conditions. LTCD is a snapshot approach, since it converts
single date S­1 acquisitions into single SSM estimates through the scaling equa­
tion. The need of a very long time series comes with the drawback of acquiring
and processing all this data, what entails requiring more computing power which
may not be available. Ideally the time­series should cover a time span long enough
(e.g. 10 years) to assure that the driest and wettest possible conditions of the soil
are encountered. This is often not possible due to the data temporal coverage be­
ing too short, to outliers like frozen conditions or because in some places one of
the extreme conditions will never be achieved (e.g. wettest condition over arid cli­
mates). In order to face this problem and to reduce the temporal length of the time
series needed, the extreme references, σ0
dry and σ0
wet are estimated statistically, us­
ing the 10% and 90% of the normalized backscatter time series respectively. The
backscatter is previously normalized taking into account the Local Incidence Angle
(LIA), choosing a reference of 40°, so every resampled backscatter value is normal­
ized to the LIA of 40°. For this purpose, model parameters are generated for each
grid point, estimating the slope through a regression, which describes The depen­
dency of the backscatter to the LIA. Due to the fact that the dry and wet backscatter
references are determined statistically, extreme wet or dry conditions can lead to
SSM values exceeding 100% or below 0%, respectively. This is corrected by setting
the values that are ±20% out of the limits to 0% or 100%. If any value surpasses
the 0­100% range by more than 20% is then masked out as no data. Summarizing,
the LTCD processing chain upscales the SAR images using a Dynamic Gaussian
Upscaling (DGU) method. This implements a dynamic masking of the backscatter
images, since extreme values are very unlikely to provide any soil moisture informa­
tion. For example, the backscatter of a building (very high) or from water (very low),
is not coming from the soil and thus disregarded. After this, pixel values are aver­
aged (pixel aggregation) to upscale to the 1km resolution. Then, Gaussian filtering
is applied to remove the aliasing effects induced in the previous pixel aggregation
step. The model parameters are generated for each grid point to normalize the
backscatter as explained and finally, SSM is estimate by the scaling equation which
uses the extreme references σ0
dry and σ0
wet.
In the other hand, STCD comes with the advantage that can be applied to shorter
time series and moreover, it allows to retrieve absolute SSM estimates through
the alpha approximation. This relates any change in backscatter to soil moisture
changes exclusively, considering other surface parameters affecting the radar sig­
nal like surface roughness and vegetation layer as approximately constant [5]. Con­

Theory
The method implemented takes advantage of the alpha approximation to obtain the
polarization amplitudes (αV V ) from the backscatter coefficients ratios. The theory
needed to understand this process is explained in section 3.1. Once that αV V is
derived by applying a Bounded Least Squares (section 3.2), the dielectric constant
ε can be inverted using equation Equation 3.3. Finally, the volumetric Surface Soil
Moisture can be estimated by using the Hallikainen model that relates it with ε. This
is detailed in section 3.6.
3.1
Alpha approximation and Short Term Change
Detection
The algorithm exploits the fact that surface soil moisture changes occur at short
temporal scales, for instance a few days or less, while other changes that may
affect the radar backscatter such as soil roughness, canopy structure and vegetation
biomass take place at longer temporal scales, namely weeks. Thus, if a dense
enough SAR time series is available, a change detection approach can be assumed
to monitor changes in SSM only. This approach is known as STCD and was first
propposed by Balenzano et al. in [11]. The assumptions of the algorithm will hold
better the shorter the SAR revisit time.
Another fundamental concept of the algorithm is awareness of the SAR signal be­
haviour at C­Band, which can not always penetrate the vegetation layer completely.
Therefore, without a backscatter from the ground, the sensitivity to SSM is lost.
This is the reason why the algorithm is not suitable over dense forests, where the
predominant mechanism is volume scattering which is mainly caused by the radar
signal interaction with the tree crown. In these cases, surface scattering or double
bounce is not existent or at least not strong enough to provide sensitivity to the water
content in the soil.
According to a first order approximation of the radiative transfer theory, the total
backscatter of a vegetated surface can be expressed as a superposition of the fol­
lowing terms: the attenuated soil backscatter, the volume contribution and the in­
teraction between the soil and the vegetation [12]. The STCD exploits two primary
approximations:

100% correct. Something to take into account is the sensors accuracy decay

sensor prongs and measuring the charge which is proportional to the dielectric

Hallikainen model
The Hallikainen model is an empirical model that evaluates the behaviour of soil­
water mixtures to microwaves as a function of water content and soil textural com­
position. The study was realised for five different soil types at frequencies ranging
between 1.4 and 18 GHz [25], empirically deriving a expression for the dielectric
constant ε depending on these parameters. The general polynomial expression is
given in Equation 3.13 where ai, bi and ci (i=0,1,2) are coefficients depending on

clay content respectively. Different coefficients are also given for both the real and
imaginary part of the dielectric constant ε, which is a complex number. In order to
investigate its relation with the volumetric soil moisture, only the real part is nec­
essary since the imaginary part is related to the attenuation of a wave into the soil
layers in the form of dielectric losses, including dispersive losses, free­water relax­

a higher electric flux density, which will help the microwave signal to reflect off the
media, bouncing back to the sensor. In the other hand, a lower dielectric constant,
equivalent to a lower electric flux density, may cause the electromagnetic waves to
travel directly through the medium, thus not returning to the sensor [27].

Data and study area
The study area was decided to be around Skjern River Catchment, in Jutland, Den­
mark, due to the availability of soil moisture ground data. This is provided by the
HOBE network, which is part of the ISMN. Soil moisture, namely water stored in the
upper soil layer, is a crucial variable for various processes and applications, from
NWP to flood forecasting, precision agriculture, water resources management and
so on, as explained in chapter 1. Therefore, ISMN database is a key tool for vali­
dating and improving global satellite products as well as land surface, climate and
hydrological models [29].
HOBE network consistently provides soil moisture measurements at different depths
in several stations spatially distributed in various soil types with a time resolution of
half an hour. It measures not only soil moisture but temperature and electrical con­
ductivity over 30 stations installed with Decagon 5TE sensors at depths of 5, 20 and
50 cm from 2009 to 2019. The stations are found in three main fieldsites: an agri­
cultural site close to Voulund, a forest site in Gludsted Plantation and a meadow site
in Skjern enge. It is worth to mention that are gaps in agricultural sites time­series
due to devices being removed for cultivation twice a year and then reinstalled. The
network was first established in 2009 as a SMOS validation site and designed for
that purpose [30] [31] [32]. Therefore, it accounts for a representative coverage of
land cover, soil type and precipitation conditions. The HOBE data will be used for
validation of the SSM estimates obtained from SAR.
The data used for this project consists of Sentinel­1 SAR images acquired over the
study area during the months of January, April and May of 2018. Therefore, the al­
gorithm has been intentionally tested over two different seasons: winter and spring.
Sentinel­1 carries a microwave SAR instrument that uses a central frequency of
5.405 GHz [33]. The data has been obtained from the Alaska Satellite Facility (ASF)
Distributed Active Archive Center (DAAC) Sentinel­1 SAR dataset using its data
search Vertex [34]. The SAR images are Single Look Complex, what means that
both the amplitude and phase are stored as a complex number. The swath mode

5
Methods Implementation
This chapter will explain the methodology applied in the algorithm developed based
in the theoretical framework explained in chapter 3.
5.1
Data Pre­Processing
The SAR images used, described in chapter 4, need to be pre­processed before
being fed as input into the algorithm, in order to correctly remove the thermal noise,
obtain the backscatter coefficient from the stored digital numbers and co­register
the products as a stack of images. Such a pre­processing workflow has been im­
plemented in SNAP following the general steps for SAR data, specially in the sense
of a Change Detection approach [42] [43]. For this purpose, a graph has been de­
signed in SNAP graph builder, which is shown in Figure 5.1. The pre­processing
has been done for N = 4 images each time, following the time series approach.
This graph implements the following pre­processing steps:
• TOPSAR­Split. First of all, the SAR product is splitted, since we are not inter­
ested in the whole image, only the part covering the study area where HOBE
is located. The subswath, polarization and bursts desired are selected, which
are IW1, VV or VH and all bursts 1 to 9.
• Thermal Noise Removal. Sentinel­1 images as any SAR image, are prone to
thermal noise in its instrument circuits, caused by microscopic motions of elec­
trons due to temperature [44]. Thermal noise in SAR images is the addition
of two noise sources, one varying in each dimension. The first one, related to
the antenna pattern varies in the range direction, thus causing discontinuities
in the inter­swath texture, or in other words, different intensities between sub­
swaths. The second one is the scalloping noise, which does so in the azimuth
direction causing the burstwise variation in intensity [45] [46].
• Apply orbit file.
The orbit state vector provided with SAR products meta­
data are often not accurate. The satellites precise orbits are usually deter­
mined after a few days and are not inmediately available when the product
is distributed. Therefore, it is neccessary to download and apply the precise
Sentinel­1 orbits, updating the orbit state vectors for each SAR scene in its
product metadata, thus providing accurate satellite position and velocity infor­
mation. [47].
• Radiometric Calibration. The pixel values in a SAR product are stored as a
Digital Number, which need to be radiometrically calibrated, what means con­
verted to a value that can be directly related to the backscatter of the scene,
for instance, the backscatter coefficient σ0. In other words, it consist on adjust­
ing the gray­scale values by calibrating them with some known value defined
from theory. The calibration factor is obtained by measuring the SAR system
against reference point targets with well­known radar cross section such as
transponders and corner reflectors, together with reference distributed targets
like the Amazon rainforest [48]. In other words, is like measuring something
known to weight 1 kg, obtaining 0.99 kg and then adjusting the result to be
Sentinel­1 SAR Soil Moisture Retrieval
21
==PAGE:34==
1kg, thus scaling every measurement by adding 0.01. This is a mandatory
step for comparability between different SAR images.
• TOPSAR­Deburst. This step is required in order to combine all the burst to
form one single seamless image. [43].
• Create Stack.
The four images are stacked and, therefore, co­registered
to perfectly align one with each other pixels. This is a crucial step for any
change detection approach that needs to be performed carefully to assure a
perfect co­registration, either way the pixels correspondence between the im­
ages would be compromised and thus any operation between the acquisitions
would result in mixing pixels from different points at ground, hence making im­
possible to detect any change.
• Write.
The output final product is then written as a stack of the four pre­
processed images.

5.2
Soil Moisture Retrieval Algorithm
The Soil Moisture Retrieval Algorithm developed has been implemented in Matlab,
following the workflow presented in Figure 5.2. The part in orange color corresponds
to the forward backscatter simulation which uses the Dubois model as explained in
section 3.4. The parts in blue correspond to data or variables straight calculated
from the data, as for instance the square root of the backscatter ratios ˆSij. The
parts in green correspond to the variables finally estimated by the algorithm, such
as the the polarization amplitudes αV V , the dielectric constants ε and the retrieved
SSM.
First of all, the SAR images that have been previously pre­processed in SNAP are
read into Matlab. The multilooking must be the first step in order to reduce the
speckle effect characteristic of radar [49]. Multilooking consists on averaging using
a sliding window and decimating after, thus with the drawback of loosing resolution.
Each pixel value is replaced by the average value over the window. Because of
the different resolution in range and azimuth direction (5m x 20m), as reported in

In order to solve it using a Bounded Least Squares approach, the boundaries αmin
and αmax need to be estimated first for every pixel. This is done by means of a
simulated forward backscatter model that implements the following steps:
• Create an artificial volumetric Soil Moisture dataset with 1000 data points from
0.04 to 1 m3 m−3 (4% to 100%).
• This artificial SSM dataset, together with the Sand and Clay information of
each pixel, is used to simulate the corresponding dielectric constants εsim
through the Hallikainen empirical model shown in Equation 3.13. Then, the
corresponding αV V sim simulated coefficients can be derived through Equa­
tion 3.3.
• Apply the Dubois model as shown in Equation 3.9 to simulate the correspond­
ing backscatter coefficients σ0
V V sim for every εsim simulated using the surface
roughness hrms information of that pixel.
The Dubois model needs the surface roughness RMS height as input, which has
been previously estimated, not simulated as in the case of the dielectric constant
εsim. For this purpose, the linear relationship between hrms and the backscatter has
been exploited based on an approach previously proposed by Srivastava et al. [24]
as mentioned in section 3.5. Since the specific model fit in the latter study has been
Sentinel­1 SAR Soil Moisture Retrieval

obtained for a dataset in India, which may most likely not represent the soil condi­
tions of Denmark, the exact same equations given in that paper could not be used,
since they led to inaccurate SSM estimations when compared to HOBE. In fact, it
could be reasoned that hrms was overestimated, since it led to underestimation in
SSM. Moreover, hrms values for water bodies were checked to be around 0.5 cm
when they should be close to zero [50]. Thus, the approach taken was to fix b in
hrms = a + b · σ0 and to try to find an appropriate coefficient a, namely lower, that
makes hrms for water bodies to be as closer as possible to zero but without being
negative. This is analogous to a calibration with a known target as performed for
instance with a corner reflector for imaging radars like SAR. The approach taken
was inspired by that well­known method. This was done independently for VV and
VH polarizations, and for the depolarization ratio (VH­VV) and the corresponding
equations obtained were shown in section 3.5.
This forward backscatter model simulates 1000 values of SSM and their corre­
sponding 1000 values of εsim, 1000 values of αV V sim and 1000 values of σ0
V V sim for
every pixel. Summarizing, a range of all possible SSM values is simulated and then
used to calculate their corresponding simulated values εsim, αV V sim and σ0
V V sim for
every pixel, considering its textural composition (Sand and Clay) and surface rough­
ness (hrms), which have been previously estimated. This allows us to create two
individual LUT for every pixel, one with pairs of simulated backscatter σ0
V V sim and
αV V sim and another one with pairs of αV V sim and εsim. The first one will permit to
estimate the boundaries for the BLS, αmin and αmax. The second one will be used
to invert the estimated ε from the estimated αV V obtained as output from the BLS.
So, the processes in the simulation and in the retrieval are analogous but inverted.
While the forward model simulates the backscatter coefficient from the artificial soil
moisture dataset, the retrieval algorithm does kind of the opposite, with the addition
of the alpha approximation applied.
The minimum and maximum values of σ0
V V in the time­series of N = 4 dates are
used to search in the LUT the closest simulated backscatter coefficients. Then,
the corresponding simulated αV V sim in each case, can be assigned as αmin and
αmax respectively. These boundaries for the αV V solution, together with the matrix
A shown in Equation 3.7 are fed into the BLS algorithm, which returns the estimated
⃗αV V = [α1, α2, α3, α4] that complies with Equation 3.7. It is worth to note once again
that this process is done for every pixel in the time­series approach.
Once that the specific αi for each date (and every pixel) has been obtained, the LUT
containing simulated εsim and αV V sim is used to invert Equation 3.3 and get the cor­
responding estimated ε. As explained before, both LUT are calculated individually
for each pixel taking into account its Sand and Clay content and surface roughness
hrms.

Sliding Window
In the same fashion as in the STCD algorithm implemented by Balenzano et al.
[10], the retrieval is applied to stacks of N = 4 SAR images, obtaining as result a
stack of N SSM maps. This is performed applying a sliding window that moves one
image forward in the time series at each time. As a result, multiple estimates will
be obtained for the same date, allowing to average them and thus, enhancing the
precision of the SSM product. Since the different SSM maps estimated for the same
date will be partially correlated, such an average will reduce their total variability.

Validation
7.1
Datasets
In order to validate the results, the HOBE network data has been used as the main
source for validation. The estimated SSM must be first interpolated to the corre­
sponding stations rows and columns in SAR geometry (lines and samples). Once
the SSM results are interpolated for every station corresponding pixel and every
date in the time series, they are stored in a matrix of size Nstations ·Ndates. The SSM
measured from HOBE is extracted for the time of the day of the satellite pass over
the study area (around 17:00) as the mean of the measurements in a range of 1 hour
apart. That is to say, the mean value between the HOBE measurements at 16:00,
17:00 and 18:00 for each day. Then, the corresponding HOBE SSM data, which
has been previously shaped in the same matrix form is subtracted and thus the er­
ror in the retrieval is obtained as the difference SSMest −SSMHOBE. Therefore, it
is straight forward that this difference will be positive in case of overestimation and
negative in the case of underestimation. This allows to an easy visual interpretation
of the scatter plots presented in the following pages. Some statistics about the es­

8.1.1
Winter: January
If we specifically observe the Copernicus SSM map for 08 January (Figure 7.1c),
some gaps in the data are spotted (white parts in the image). It has been found that
they correspond to a flag 241 = exceeding minimum (dry reference). Extreme dry
conditions, frozen soil, snow covered soil and flooding are mentioned in Copernicus
SSM manual as cases in which the SSM retrieval is ill­posed [51] and thus replaced
by some flag. Due to the very low values in the rest of the image, the time of the
year and geographical location (January in Denmark) we can almost assure that
this situation is due to frozen soil in the study area. In order to ascertain if this
hypothesis is true, Danmarks Meteorologiske Institut (DMI) weather archive can be
used to compare and try to make sense out of the results. The temperature and
rainfall in Herning (very close to HOBE study area) in January 2018 are shown in
Figure 8.1. It can be seen that the rainfall event on 02 January agrees with the wet
SSM estimates for that date. As it can be observed in Figure 8.1a, the temperatures
quickly drop below 0°C after 04 January, reaching a minimum of −8.8°C on 07
January. Therefore, it can be confirmed that the soil was frozen on 08 January
with a minimum temperature of −7°C and a mean of −2.1°C. The soil has already
started to defrost on 14 January but is still very dry. The absence of precipitation
from 05 to 14 January is confirmed in Figure 8.1b, what makes sense with SSM dry
estimates for 08 and 14 January. In the other hand, the soil moisture increase on
20 and 26 January is explained by the rainfall events from 15 to 19 and from 22 to
24 respectively. Also, the melting of the snowfall occurred from 17 to 23, which can
be seen in Figure 8.2, may have a positive impact on the soil moisture retrieved for
20 and 26 January.
The scatter plot shown in Figure 7.2 compares the Copernicus SSM converted to
absolute values though porosity and the SSM estimated in section 6.1 for the 02, 08,
14 and 20 of January. As it can be observed, Copernicus tends to underestimate the

The effect and performance of the different hrms estimated can be commented
based on the SSM results obtained for each case. It is clear that underestimat­
ing surface roughness RMS height leads to an overestimation of SSM and vice
versa. The backscatter is a combination of the surface roughness contribution and
the soil moisture contribution. Thus, for a given measured σ0, if we estimate a lower
hrms than it really is, the soil moisture contribution will be regarded as bigger than
it actually is. This reasoning applies to the LUT created in the forward backscatter
model in the same way. If a lower hrms is estimated, then a lower σ0
sim will be sim­
ulated for each corresponding αV V sim and Esim than it should with the true surface
roughness RMS height. Therefore, when comparing the real measured σ0 with the
simulated ones in the LUT, a higher αV V than corresponding will be assigned to
that pixel and thus, an overestimation of SSM will occur. In other words, this is due
to the fact that underestimating hrms implies that a lower σ0 will result into obtaining
certain boundaries αmin and αmax, that should correspond to a higher σ0 if hrms
was correctly estimated. Because of the BLS boundaries being estimated in such
a way, it is straight forward that an overestimation of them leads to a higher SSM
than it should and the same applies in the opposite way. In this regard, the hrms ob­
tained using the depolarization ratio underestimates the actual surface roughness

enough. Any measuring technique of soil moisture is characterised by its spacing,
extent and support. The spacing refers to the distance between samples (stations
in the case of HOBE), extent refers to the overall coverage (HOBE study area) and
support refers to the effective area that each measurement represents [54] [10].
It is then obvious that the support of in­situ measurements, which are point­scale
(≈0.1km) is much smaller than the one of the satellite estimations. Thus, to allow
for a meaningful comparison, a dense network would be needed to average differ­
ent stations measurements inside the satellite product pixel. The more stations the
better since the Spatial Representativeness Error (SRE) decreases with more in­
dependent point scale measurements (S) used to average. The SRE is simply the
margin of error in estimating the mean SSM of an area with S point scale observa­
tions at a specific level of confidence [10]. Although a dense enough network is not
available in the case of HOBE, specially for the Spring case when a lot of devices

with stations 1.01, 1.03 and 1.05, which are the only ones close enough to apply

8.2
Validity of the algorithm: Assumptions and
limitations
The validity of the soil moisture retrieval algorithm implemented is constrained by
the assumptions made along the way and thus it presents some limitations.
The most important is regarding the surface roughness and vegetation structure,
which are assumed to be constant during each stack of N = 4 acquisitions pro­
cessed. This is not always completely true, resulting in inaccurate estimates of
SSM. The alpha approximation shown in Equation 3.4 will not hold if these two pa­
rameters are not constant and thus, the backscatter ratios shown in Equation 3.2
will not be only dependent on soil moisture but also on the changes on surface
roughness and vegetation. This implies that the decoupling of soil moisture effect
from these two other surface parameters will not be achieved and thus, the main
advantage of STCD algorithm will be lost.
This effect may be specially significant in Spring due to cultivation. Spring barley,
for instance, which is present in the HOBE study area (Figure 4.6) is sown in spring.
This can cause the roughness to diminish as farmers prepare a smooth soil seedbed
[10]. Hence, increases in backscatter due to a smoother soil can lead to a misinter­
pretation as an increase in soil moisture. When the crops start growing and finally
emerge, different types of crops will present varying canopy structures and thus the
scattering mechanism will differ. This leads to a backscatter more heterogeneous
among different crops fields, what can confuse the algorithm and make the retrieval
more complicated. For example, the fast growth of cereals canopy, known as stem
elongation phase, will trigger a higher attenuation of the signal and thus, a lower
backscatter coefficient measured by the satellite. In fact, the minimum backscatter
for barley crops is measured to be around the first days of May [55]. Moreover,
the backscatter decrease with growing vegetation applies to any type of narrow leaf
crops, while the opposite applies for broad leaf crops [56]. Therefore, considering
that most of the study area is covered by either forest or crops, the growing vege­
tation can have a significant impact on the retrieval, causing an underestimation of
soil moisture as it occurs in April for the results obtained with hrms estimated from
VH and VV polarization (Figure 7.27).

ulation, which is explained in detail in section 5.2, must also be considered. The
model is optimized for bare soil with a factor k · hrms ≤2.5, where k = 2π

8.3
Room for improvement
Although the results obtained are promising, a longer time series implying several
years of data would be needed in order to better assess the validity of the STCD
algorithm implemented with a simulation approach to estimate the boundaries αmin
and αmax. Therefore, more work is required in order to implement such a method
in a large scale, for instance national or global. However, it has been demonstrated
that a forward backscatter model is a feasible approach to estimate the BLS bound­
aries. A very positive fact is the good results obtained even if the algorithm is not
completely optimized in some aspects and thus, there is a big room for improvement
or future work.
Any masking have been applied, in order to be able to compare the performance
of the algorithm in different conditions of land cover, soil texture and so on. Af­
ter the results obtained, it is clear that the STCD approach is not suited for some
areas where the backscatter is not dominated by the soil attenuated contribution,
for example, dense forest with a high contribution of the vegetation. Therefore, it
is acknowledged that masking should be applied to discard from the retrieval very
high and very low values of backscatter which are not likely to carry any soil mois­
ture information in the signal. In this regard, buildings (very high backscatter) or
water (very low backscatter) should be the first things to mask out. Then, vege­
tated areas not dominated by soil attenuated scattering should be also treated. The
simplest solution would be masking them out but another option is to correct for
the vegetation effects on the backscatter measured by Sentinel­1. This is possi­
ble incorporating optical data in the retrieval to calculate the NDVI [6]. Then, this
can be used to estimate the water plant content, W, which can later be used as a
threshold to discriminate whether correction is needed or not. For instance, if W <
0.25 kg m−2, the influence of vegetation on the radar signal can be considered as
negligible. Even if the vegetation contribution is the dominant one, the backscatter
can still be corrected to isolate the soil contribution carrying soil moisture informa­
tion, by means of some empirical models, such as the Water Cloud Model used for
small plant constituents (narrow leaf crops) and the Ratio Method applied for bigger
plant constituents [6].
Another aspect that could be improved is taking into account the LIA instead of using
a constant incidence angle. It is also worth to note that terrain correction has not
been applied to the SAR data. Although this may be fine for Denmark due to its flat
topography, that is not the case for other areas. Hence, these two things should be
considered in an operational product application.
A significant improvement in the algorithm performance could be achieved with a
shorter revisit time. This would help to keep true the assumptions about constant
surface roughness and vegetation. For instance, with N = 4 and a revisit time of 6
days as implemented, there are 18 days difference from the first to the last acqui­
sition in each stack. It is likely that in that time, almost three weeks, some places
will have changed its surface roughness and canopy structure, specially in Spring
when vegetation is growing. If the revisit time was, let’s say, 2 days, the difference
from the first to the last acquisition would be just 6 days. Therefore, it would be
more likely that the alpha approximation holds during less than a week. This would
also allow for a better comparison with HOBE with a higher temporal density of es­
timates. One way of achieving a shorter revisit time could be combining ascending

This procedure could provide a shorter revisit time, with the explained advantages,

The biggest improvement could be a better estimation of the RMS height. Although

with surface roughness hrms according to the previous study by Srivastava et al.

one presented in Equation 3.12 could be investigated. Another approach could
consist on simulating also hrms instead of estimating it. This have been previously
implemented in MULESME by Pulvirenti et al. [6]. In such a method, the LUT
created combines values of SSM, hrms and incidence angle. Therefore, instead
of estimating the hrms for each pixel, different values could be simulated and then,
their corresponding backscatter. By this way, the LUT used in the estimation of the
boundaries, αmin and αmax, would account for a range of possible hrms instead of
asserting a value that may not be true.
Last but not least, it must be considered that if the algorithm was applied to a longer
time series, eventually every SSM product would be the average of N estimates (N
= 4 in this case) as explained in section 5.3 and shown in Figure 5.3. Therefore, the
spatial variability and thus the accuracy would be improved for every day as it is for
the central days shown in Figure 7.13a, Figure 7.15a and Figure 7.17a.
The last­step of low filtering the SSM maps could be improved using advanced tech­
niques such as Statistically Homogeneous Pixels (SHP) identification. Therefore,
the spatial averaging would be performed with a dynamic filter size depending on
the target statistics. In other words, it would average with a bigger or smaller filter
size when needed and it would not mix pixels from statistically heterogeneous re­
gions, namely different features. This could help to highlight the relation with soil
moisture while preserving the shape and borders of the different features in the SAR
image, e.g. crop fields.

9
Conclusions
This Master Thesis presents a study of the STCD algorithm developed by Balenzano
et al. in several pappers [10] [11], which uses the Sentinel­1 SAR backscatter in VV
polarization, namely the ratios between subsequent acquisitions, to retrieve SSM.
The latter allows to decouple the effect of vegetation and surface roughness in the
SAR signal if these two keep constant in the temporal dimension of each stack
of N images processed. Only in that case, the alpha approximation that relates
changes in the backscatter ratios exclusively to soil moisture changes, will hold.
This is crucial for the algorithm, since the BLS method that calculates αV V for each
date, do so based on such an approximation.
The innovation in the approach implemented relies on using a forward backscatter
model to simulate a dataset of σ0
V V and αV V , instead of using in­situ data to create a
calibration curve or already operational low resolution SSM products, as previously
done. The simulated σ0
V V and αV V form a LUT which is used to estimate the bound­
aries for each pixel αmin and αmax, that drive the retrieval through the BLS method.
One of the reasons for satellite soil moisture estimation is its ability to map large
and remote areas, where in­situ data would not be available. Thus, the advantage
of using such a method is clear since there is no need for auxiliary data such as
in­situ data or other SSM products to drive the retrieval. The only auxiliary infor­
mation needed to implement the method developed is the soil textural information
in the region of interest, namely Sand and Clay content, which are obtained from
already available global products on OpenLandMap [40]. These two are used in
the inversion of the Hallikainen empirical model that relates the dielectric constant
to volumetric soil moisture.
Synthesising, the steps in the retrieval are to estimate αV V for each date from the
backscatter σ0
V V ratios, calculate the corresponding ε and invert the SSM that pro­
duced such an observation. In this sense, the SSM retrieval is analogous to an in­
verse problem, where we want to estimate the factors that produce some observed
data, in this case the soil moisture that produces a specific backscatter measured
by the satellite. The dependency of the retrieval on the boundaries αmin and αmax
is a characteristic of the STCD algorithm. However, because of the Dubois model
simulation approach implemented in this study to estimate them, a dependency on
the surface roughness RMS height hrms estimated is introduced. Instead of a cal­
ibration curve from in­situ data driving the retrieval, a simulated LUT with σ0
V V sim
and αV V sim is used. Due to the fact that σ0
V V sim is simulated through the Dubois
model which uses the hrms as an input, the latter emerges as a tuning parame­
ter of the retrieval since it calibrates the relation of backscatter and αV V , resulting
in rather overestimation or underestimation of the boundaries and thus, this effect
is propagated to everything else, until the final SSM estimated. For instance, for
a given simulated αV V , corresponding to certain dielectric constant and soil mois­
ture, the simulated backscatter for the same ε and SSM, will be lower than it should
if hrms is underestimated, due to its positive linear relationship. This will result in
a higher αV V being assigned to the measured σ0
V V and thus, an overestimation of
SSM. Summarizing, underestimation of hrms leads to overestimation of SSM and
vice versa.

The results obtained confirm the suitability of this simulation approach for STCD.
Two different time series have been processed, applying the algorithm implemented:
one in Winter with 5 Sentinel­1 acquisitions from 02 to 26 January and another in
Spring with 10 SAR images from 02 April to 26 May, both with a revisit time of 6
days. While 25 out of the 30 HOBE network stations have been used for validation
in the January time series, only 8 were found without containing any gaps for the
Spring time series, which has been a nuisance. The best results for the January
time series are achieved using only the agriculture stations, with a Mean Error =
6.16 % and a Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) = 7.86 %. The mean correlation
coefficient found in the January time series retrieved has been R = 0.65 with all the
25 stations and R = 0.78 without 5 outliers with R<0.6. The January SSM products
have been compared with the Copernicus SSM product that implements LTCD. It
has been found that Copernicus product is ill­posed for SSM retrieval under frozen
soil conditions, encountered on 8 and 14 January while the STCD based algorithm
implemented have been proved to agree better with HOBE network measured SSM.
In fact, the mean error comparing with HOBE was found to be around 10% and 8 %
lower for the SSM estimated in this study than for the SSM estimated by Copernicus,
for the two dates latter mentioned, respectively.
In the case of the Spring time series, the best results are obtained using VH polar­
ization to estimate hrms, what gives a Mean Error = 6.04 % and a RMSE = 7.30
% without outlier station 2.09. However, the results in Spring show a lower correla­
tion than in January, with the best being R = 0.41 with all the stations and R = 0.59
without outliers. Despite the fact of a low correlation when looking at each station
time series, the estimates are improved when the mean of a number of stations,
namely three, is compared with the mean SSM estimated for them. In that case,
the correlation coefficient is found to be R = 0.74 (with hrms from VV), R = 0.75 (with
hrms from VH) and R = 0.70 (with hrms from VH­VV). If the mean for all the stations
is considered, then R = 0.67 (with hrms from VV), R = 0.72 (with hrms from VH) and
R = 0.66 (with hrms from VH­VV).
Besides the good agreement found with HOBE stations, more work is needed in
order to implement this method in a national or global scale. It is difficult to assess
the true quality of the retrieval method with a short time series used for validation,
ideally should be years. But not also the temporal scale is problematic, also the spa­
tial. Single point measurements as the in situ data provided by HOBE may not be
representative enough to compare with single pixels satellite estimates. Therefore,
more dense networks are needed in order to provide a better source of validation.
The revisit time of the SAR acquisitions should be lower to meet user requirements,
ideally 1 or 2 days. In that case, the STCD algorithm is expected to improve, as the
assumption made about surface roughness and vegetation being constant during
the N acquisitions processed at each time, will be more likely to hold true. Some
promising steps for better C­band SAR SSM retrieval at high resolution rely on data
fusion with optical data or other SAR instruments operating at different frequen­
cies, e.g. L band, which enables more penetration in the vegetation layer and thus
highlights sensitivity to soil moisture.
