Abstract
Obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) is strongly associated with cardiac arrhythmias, es-
pecially atrial fibrillation (AF). However, it is still unclear whether individual apnea
events can acutely trigger changes in atrial electrophysiology or directly induce atrial
arrhythmias. Understanding these acute effects could provide valuable insights into
the mechanisms linking OSA and AF.
This thesis investigates the short-term effects of acute OSA events on atrial electro-
physiology and atrial arrhythmia occurrence in a cohort of Danish post-stroke patients.
Using polysomnographic ECG data, we analyzed RR, PR, and P-wave intervals, as
well as arrhythmic events, across defined temporal segments: before, during, and
after acute apnea events.
Our results show that in patients with normal sinus rhythm, apnea onset induced a
bradycardic response, followed by a transient tachycardic response upon apnea ter-
mination, suggesting a shift between parasympathetic and sympathetic systems. In
contrast, patients with continuous AF exhibited no significant changes in RR intervals.
Changes in PR interval and P-wave lacked physiological significance. No increase
in atrial arrhythmias was detected during or after isolated apnea events compared to
normal breathing.
Our findings suggest that single short apneas do not acutely alter atrial electrophys-
iology or trigger atrial arrhythmias in this population. Future research could explore
cumulative, longer, or sleep stage-dependent effects of OSA on atrial cardiac func-
tion.

Introduction
1.1
Obstructive sleep apnea
Obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) is a common sleep disorder, occurring in approxi-
mately 6% to 17% of the general population [1]. Many patients are asymptomatic and
it is estimated that 82% of men and 93% of women with OSA remain undiagnosed
[2]. Risk factors include obesity, male sex, older age, and craniofacial abnormalities
[3]. OSA has been shown to be associated with serious adverse outcomes such as
cardiovascular disease (hypertension, coronary heart disease, heart failure, atrial fib-
rillation), stroke, metabolic disorders (such as diabetes mellitus) and early mortality
[4]. Timely diagnosis of OSA is therefore a medical priority.
OSA is characterized by recurrent episodes of partial or complete obstruction of the
upper airways during sleep due to muscle relaxation and soft tissue collapse. An
apneic episode can lead to intermittent hypoxia, hypercapnia, and fluctuations in in-
trathoracic pressure [5]. These pathological processes influence the cardiovascular
system and are considered to play a role in OSA-related heart failure and atrial fibril-
lation (AF) [5].
Diagnosis of OSA is typically made through in-lab studies (polysomnography (PSG))
or home sleep apnea testing which records physiological signals overnight. OSA is
defined and its severity is classified based on the apnea-hypopnea index (AHI), which
measures the number of apnea (complete obstruction of the airways) and hypopnea
(partial obstruction) per hour of sleep.
The primary therapy for OSA is Continuous Positive Airway Pressure (CPAP), which
consists in wearing a mask connected to a machine that delivers pressure to the
airways to keep them open during sleep. CPAP was shown to reduce AHI to normal
levels in more than 90% of patients while using the device [6].
Among the various cardiovascular complications associated with OSA, AF has gained
significant attention due to its prevalence and potential consequences.
1.2
Atrial fibrillation and its association with
OSA
Atrial fibrillation is the most common cardiac arrhythmia, and it is estimated that 46.3
million individuals worldwide suffer from AF [7]. AF is associated with serious clinical
outcomes. It increases the risk of ischemic stroke, heart failure, myocardial infarction
and sudden cardiac death, and patients with AF are also predisposed to extracranial
systemic embolism, cognitive impairment and dementia [8].
1
==PAGE:9==
AF is characterized by an irregular and often rapid heart rate caused by disorganized
electrical activity in the atria. In a normal heart, the sinoatrial node acts as the primary
pacemaker, initiating all electrical impulses in the atria. However, in AF, fibrillatory
electrical impulses originate either from multiple ectopic sites or from reentrant acti-
vations, leading to loss of atrial contraction [8].
An electrocardiogram (ECG), which is a recording of the heart electrical activity, can
be used to detect AF. A typical ECG signal consists of a P-wave (atrial depolariza-
tion), followed by a QRS complex (ventricular depolarization) and finally a T-wave
(ventricular repolarization). In AF, the ECG shows an irregularly irregular ventricular
rhythm, typically 100-180 beats per minute, with QRS complexes appearing in an
unpredictable pattern. P-waves are not discernible and may be replaced by fibrilla-
tory waves (f-waves), which appear as rapid, irregular oscillations of the baseline [9].
Figure 1 illustrates the differences in the ECG signal between AF and normal sinus
rhythm.
Figure 1: Comparison of two ECG signals. a) ECG signal showing normal sinus rhythm,
with annotated P-wave, QRS complex, and T-wave. b) ECG signal showing atrial fibrillation,
characterized by an irregular heart rate and the presence of f-waves.
Research has established a strong association between OSA and AF, suggesting
that OSA contributes to the development and recurrence of arrhythmias [10]. Table
1 presents an overview of observational studies exploring the associations between
OSA and AF. Several cohort and prospective studies report a more than two-fold
increase in AF risk among OSA patients, particularly those under 65 years of age
[10, 11]. The severity of OSA also appears to correlate with greater AF recurrence
rates and higher hospitalization risk in patients with AF [12, 13]. Moreover, CPAP
has been associated with a reduced risk of AF progression and recurrence [13, 14],
further supporting the idea that OSA plays a causal role in the development of AF.
The unidirectional relationship where OSA triggers arrhythmic episodes is also sup-
ported by a study by Linz et al. [15]. By analyzing the dynamics between night-to-
night sleep disordered breathing (SDB) severity and daily risk of AF, they found that
severe SDB episodes (i.e. nights with higher AHI) led to a higher likelihood of experi-
encing AF the following day, but days with severe AF episodes did not lead to higher
2

SDB severity the following night.
Furthermore, a case-crossover analysis of PSG data by Monahan et al. [16] found
that respiratory disturbances were associated with a nearly 18-fold increased risk
of paroxysmal AF within 90 seconds of the event, supporting a direct temporal link
between SDB events and the onset of arrhythmias.

1.3
OSA-induced changes in pathophysiology
A combination of different pathophysiological mechanisms has been proposed to ex-
plain how OSA promotes AF [20].
1.3.1
Autonomic nervous system dysfunction
Apneic episodes trigger rapid shifts between parasympathetic and sympathetic activ-
ity. The parasympathetic system dominates in the early phase of an apnea, leading
3
==PAGE:11==
to transient bradycardia. This initial parasympathetic response corresponds to the
diving reflex, an oxygen-conserving mechanism that reduces myocardial oxygen de-
mand [21]. Sympathetic response later during the apnea gradually intensifies as
hypoxia progresses, eventually overtaking the parasympathetic dominance. Imme-
diately after apnea, when breathing resumes, there is a sympathetic surge arising
from hypoxia, arousal, and inhibition of pulmonary stretch, leading to tachycardia
and increases in blood pressure [22].
These shifts may trigger AF and, if repetitive, maintain it in the long term [23]. Indeed,
parasympathetic activation shortens the atrial refractory period, which favors reentry
circuits [23]. Sympathetic activation increases heart cell excitability, which can cause
afterdepolarizations (abnormal depolarizations of cardiac muscle cells) and trigger
ectopic beats that may initiate AF [23].
1.3.2
Intrathoracic pressure fluctuations
Respiratory effort against airway obstruction results in large negative intrathoracic
pressure swings. These pressure changes can reach magnitudes of up to 60–70
cmH2O [24], compared to -5 to -8 cmH2O in normal quiet breathing [25]. This causes
direct mechanical stress on the atrial walls, inducing atrial stretch. Acute atrial stretch
activates stretch-sensitive ion channels, increasing calcium entry into heart cells.
This calcium overload can trigger afterdepolarizations, which may cause ectopic
beats and initiate AF [26]. Moreover, long exposure to atrial stretch is linked to chronic
structural and inflammatory remodeling, creating a susceptible substrate for reentry
and thus AF [26].
Baroreceptors, which are specialized mechanoreceptors that detect changes in blood
pressure, activate during intrathoracic pressure fluctuations. They trigger a reflex re-
sponse with parasympathetic activity that causes a shortening of the atrial refractory
period, potentially creating a substrate for arrhythmias [27].
1.3.3
Intermittent hypoxia
Repeated hypoxia-reoxygenation cycles generate CO2 fluctuations, oxidative stress
and inflammation. Oxidative stress, resulting from the generation of reactive oxygen
species, can damage cardiac tissue, whereas fluctuations in CO2 levels disrupt atrial
conduction properties [28]. In the long term, intermittent hypoxia can lead to structural
changes in the heart, including atrial remodeling and fibrosis, which further promote
arrhythmogenesis [29].
1.4
OSA-associated ECG alterations
To better understand the pathophysiological mechanisms linking OSA to cardiac
arrhythmias, researchers have examined electrocardiographic changes related to
OSA.
4
==PAGE:12==
Tables 2 and 3 provide an overview of studies examining ECG changes associated
with OSA. Table 2 focuses on chronic changes, whereas Table 3 presents the acute
effects of OSA events on the ECG.
Patients with OSA exhibit alterations in ECG parameters, reflecting changes in car-
diac conduction and repolarization. The QT interval, which represents ventricular
repolarization, has been shown to vary during apnea. Gillis et al. [30] reported that
QT intervals were prolonged during apnea but shortened post-apnea, while QTc (cor-
rected QT interval, QT/√RR) intervals were prolonged at apnea onset, shortened
during apnea, and further reduced post-apnea. Similarly, the Tpe interval (interval
between the peak and the end of the T-wave), a marker of transmural dispersion of
repolarization, increases during apneic episodes compared to normal breathing, as
observed by Zeng et al. [31].
The RR interval, which reflects ventricular heart rate, is typically shorter in OSA pa-
tients [32]. However, Gillis et al. [30] observed that RR intervals were prolonged
during apnea but shortened post-apnea, while Zeng et al. [31] also reported a de-
crease in RR intervals during apneic episodes.
Atrioventricular conduction is also affected, as indicated by changes in the PR in-
terval. While patients with OSA generally exhibit longer PR intervals [33], transient
reductions occur during respiratory-induced arousals, as reported by Smith et al. [34].
Zeng et al. [31] similarly found a decrease in PR intervals during apneic episodes.
In addition to these variations, atrial depolarization is generally altered in OSA pa-
tients, as evidenced by prolonged and more dispersed P-wave durations [35]. Gaisl
et al. [36] further demonstrated that P-waves were longer and more dispersed during
simulated obstructive apneas and hypopneas compared to normal breathing.

1.5
Stroke and its association with OSA and
AF
A stroke is a loss of blood flow to part of the brain causing damage to brain tissue. It is
most commonly caused by an obstruction in a brain artery (ischemic stroke) due to a
blood clot, or a blood vessel rupture in the brain (hemorrhagic stroke) [43]. OSA, AF,
and stroke are closely linked. Indeed, OSA is an independent risk factor for stroke
[44], as it contributes to hypertension, inflammation, and increased blood coagulabil-
ity. At the same time, OSA promotes the development and recurrence of AF, which
is one of the most significant risk factors for ischemic stroke [45]. Irregular atrial con-
tractions can lead to blood stasis and thrombus formation, which can embolize to the
brain, causing ischemic stroke.
1.6
Knowledge gap and motivations
Despite the different mechanisms that have been proposed to explain how OSA cre-
ates a substrate for AF, it remains unclear which specific pathophysiological changes
trigger arrhythmia during and immediately following apneic episodes. Most of the
prior studies rely on intermittent or average measurements of physiological changes,
whereas real-time electrophysiological changes in the atria during and after acute
apnea events remain poorly explored.
Therefore, we investigated atrial electrocardiographic changes during acute obstruc-
tive sleep apneic episodes extracted from continuous overnight ECG recordings from
PSG data in a cohort of Danish patients with previous stroke. The goal was to identify
immediate temporal electrophysiological changes in the atria during and immediately
after OSA episodes compared to normal breathing. Additionally, the study aimed to
detect atrial arrhythmias (specifically AF and supraventricular premature complexes)
to investigate whether OSA events can directly trigger an atrial arrhythmic response.

2
Methods
2.1
Workflow
The scope of this project was to determine acute atrial electrophysiological changes
caused by obstructive sleep apnea events in humans. Therefore, we focused on
detecting P-waves and atrial arrhythmias in ECG, before, during and after apnea
events. A general overview of the methods is given in Figure 2.
PSG data was collected and annotated in a previously published article. The ECG
signals and apnea annotations were extracted from the PSG files. ECG signals cor-
responding to isolated apneas (defined later) were extracted and filtered to remove
noise and baseline wandering. R-peaks were first detected, followed by the identi-
fication of AF and other arrhythmias. Each heart beat was classified as normal or
arrhythmic. Subsequently, P-waves (onset, peak, and offset) were detected for each
non-arrhythmic beat. Finally, we analyzed different time intervals and arrhythmia oc-
currence in 3 phases: before, during and after apnea events to investigate the acute
effects of obstructive sleep apnea on atrial electrophysiological activity.
Figure 2: Overview of the different steps of the workflow
2.2
Data collection
As we aimed to determine acute electrocardiographic effects of acute obstructive ap-
neas in humans, we performed a retrospective ECG analysis on a Danish patient co-
hort. The patients were recruited, and the data was collected in a previous published
study investigating PSG indicators of mortality in stroke patients [46]. All patients in
9
==PAGE:17==
this clinical trial had recently experienced a stroke or transient ischemic attack. They
were recruited at the Danish Center for Sleep Medicine between December 2010
and June 2012 and assessed with PSG within 1 to 26 days after hospital admission
to evaluate SBD. 70 patients initially participated in this study. However, 7 had to
be excluded: 3 patients did not have stroke upon reexamination, 3 patients removed
the respiratory equipment during recording, and 1 patient’s recording failed due to a
defective battery in the PSG recording device.
PSGs were conducted according to the American Academy of Sleep Medicine (AASM)
2007 guidelines and included recordings of electroencephalography, eye electrodes,
chin electrodes, nasal cannula, pulse oximeter, respiratory belts, electrocardiogra-
phy electrodes, and limb electrodes. Nevertheless, only the ECG channel was of
importance for our study. It consisted of a single-lead ECG recorded at a sampling
rate of 256 Hz.
Sleep apneas were identified and annotated in the previous published study in accor-
dance with the AASM 2007 guidelines. They were defined as periods of completely
flat nasal airflow signal for ≥10 s. They were automatically detected using a software
(Nervus version 2.71, Nervus Ltd, Stockbridge, UK) and verified by a clinician (Medi-
cal Doctor and PhD student). Apnea events were classified as obstructive or central
based on the presence or absence of respiratory effort during the apneic period, re-
spectively.
2.3
Data preprocessing
The original PSG files were provided in MATLAB (.mat) format and contained all the
recorded physiological signals. As we aimed to study acute electrocardiographic
changes, only the ECG channel was relevant for our analysis. To ensure that the an-
notations corresponded to respiratory events, a few examples were manually checked
to confirm that the annotations aligned with the flat segments of the airflow signal.
Subsequently, only the ECG signals and sleep event annotations were extracted
and converted to compressed NumPy (.npz) format for further analysis. This extrac-
tion and format conversion enabled us to retain only the signal of interest while also
improving data loading speed. A small portion of the ECG signals were visually in-
spected for each patient. ECG signals with inverted polarity were detected manually
and corrected by flipping. All subsequent analyses were conducted using software
Python 3.10.5 (Python Software Foundation, Wilmington, DE, USA).
2.4
Apnea extraction
Our goal was to investigate the acute effects of OSA events on atrial electrophysiol-
ogy using the ECG, by comparing the signal before, during, and after OSA events. A
critical step was to ensure that baseline values (before the apnea) were not influenced
10
==PAGE:18==
by a previous apnea. Therefore, we defined and focused on isolated obstructive ap-
neic episodes. Isolated OSA events were defined as obstructive apneic episodes
with no other apnea events (obstructive or central) occurring within a 60-second win-
dow both before apnea onset and after apnea termination. This selection criterion
was implemented to prevent interference from adjacent apneic events and to ensure
that patients have sufficient time to physiologically recover between apneas.
Each isolated apnea was segmented into three distinct phases, as illustrated in Fig-
ure 3.
• Before phase: a 20-second period immediately preceding the onset of the
apnea event. This phase served as the baseline reference period capturing
the patients’ cardiovascular activity during stable breathing.
• During phase: the complete duration of the apnea event, with a minimum
duration of 10 seconds.
• After phase: a 60-second period immediately following the end of the apnea
event. This duration was selected to capture both immediate post-apneic car-
diovascular responses and the gradual return to baseline.
The arrhythmia detection methods described in the following sections required a cer-
tain amount of ECG data to work properly. These algorithms need to analyze the
signal and establish normal rhythm patterns before they can accurately identify ab-
normal heartbeats. Therefore, larger ECG segments were extracted for analysis,
spanning from 80s before apnea onset to 120s after apnea termination. This ex-
tended time window provided the algorithms with sufficient signal length to detect
arrhythmias in all phases.
Figure 3: Temporal phase definition and isolation criteria, showing the before phase in green
(20s), during phase in yellow (≥10s), and after phase in red (60s), with 60-second isolation
buffers in blue and overall analyzed ECG signal in black.
2.5
Signal cleaning
ECG signals were preprocessed to remove noise and baseline wandering. A fifth-
order Butterworth high-pass filter with a cutoff frequency of 0.5 Hz was applied to
11
==PAGE:19==
remove baseline wandering. Powerline interference at 50 Hz was attenuated using
a moving average filter.
2.6
R-Peak Detection
R-peak detection was the first processing phase. This step was essential to divide
the signals into individual beats so that we could later identify one P-wave per beat
and classify each beat as normal or arrhythmic.
R-peaks were detected using the neurokit algorithm from the NeuroKit2 Python pack-
age [47]. This method was selected for its ease of use and its demonstrated robust-
ness across different ECG databases [47, 48, 49].
To improve detection of R-peaks, particularly for arrhythmic beats, we implemented
a post-processing step. For each apnea event, R-peaks with amplitudes below 20%
of the mean R-peak amplitude were excluded to eliminate noise artifacts and large
T-waves that may have been falsely detected as R-peaks. Potentially missed beats
were then searched when no R-peak was detected within 1.5-second intervals. This
was performed using SciPy’s find_peaks function with a minimum amplitude thresh-
old of 20% of the mean amplitude and minimum separation of 0.4 seconds to prevent
duplicate detections. The process is summarized in Figure 4.

ECG segment (blue) with detected R-peaks marked in red.

2.7
Arrhythmia detection
Our primary interest was in detecting atrial arrhythmias, as they are directly associ-
ated with atrial electrophysiological activity. However, we also aimed to analyze P-
waves. Therefore, all types of arrhythmias had to be considered in order to identify
beats with absent or abnormal P-waves. Atrial fibrillation and ventricular premature
complexes were identified using automated methods. Other arrhythmias were de-
tected semi-automatically by identifying beats with abnormal durations and manually
reviewing them.
2.7.1
Atrial fibrillation detection
AF episodes were detected using Shannon entropy analysis of RR interval variability,
as described in [50]. Shannon entropy is a mathematical measure that quantifies the
randomness in a sequence of data. In this case, it was used to quantify how chaotic
the heart rhythm patterns are. The algorithm followed these steps:
• RR intervals were converted into symbols (0, 1 or 2) representing short, normal,
and long intervals relative to the median RR interval: short beats (0) for intervals
<90% of median, normal beats (1) for intervals between 90-110% of median,
and long beats (2) for intervals >110% of median.
• These symbols were then used to form “words”: groups of three consecutive
symbols. These words represent short-term heart rate variability.
• Shannon entropy was calculated for each heartbeat from the word distribution
of 59 consecutive words centered around the beat to classify. Shannon entropy
is defined by the formula:
H = −Σ(pi × log(pi))
where pi is the probability of each unique word. In normal sinus rhythm, words
tend to be repetitive, resulting in low entropy. However, during AF, they become
highly unpredictable resulting in high entropy.
• A beat was classified as AF if its entropy exceeded a threshold of 2.65. This
value was selected empirically based on different ECG segments with and with-
out AF to find a proper threshold.

2.7.2
Ventricular premature complexes detection
Ventricular premature complexes were identified using morphological analysis based
on the area under the curve (AUC) method described by [50]. For each detected R-
peak, a 300 ms window (150 ms pre- and post-peak) was extracted, and the AUC
of the absolute ECG signal was calculated using the trapezoidal rule. Beats with
AUC values exceeding 1.3 times the median AUC of the preceding normal beats
were classified as ventricular premature complexes. To prevent over-classification,
if more than 75% of the beats were labeled as ventricular premature complexes, all
labels were reset to normal sinus rhythm.

2.7.3
Other arrhythmias detection
Other arrhythmias were identified through RR interval analysis combined with man-
ual assessment. For each ECG segment, RR intervals deviating by more than two
standard deviations from the mean were flagged and plotted for visual inspection.
Flagged beats were manually classified as:
• Supraventricular premature complexes: beats with narrow QRS complexes
with absent or morphologically abnormal P-waves.
• Ventricular premature complexes: beats with wide QRS complexes not cap-
tured by the automated detection.
• Normal beats: beats with normal morphology that were flagged due to RR
interval variability but showed no pathological features.
Examples of beats that were manually classified are given in Figure 7.

2.8
P-wave detection
Our goal was to explore how obstructive sleep apnea events influence the electrical
activity in the atria. Detecting P-waves in ECG signal was thus primordial, since they
correspond to the part of the ECG representing atrial depolarization. P-waves were
detected in several steps to ensure accurate identification and measurement.
• Initial detection: P-waves were initially detected using NeuroKit2’s discrete
wavelet transform method [47], which has been validated across different ECG
databases [51]. This method identifies P-wave onsets, peaks, and offsets.
• Detection improvement based on asymmetrical amplitude: The initial de-
tection was post-processed to correct common errors, particularly for P-waves
with notched morphology where the notch could be mistakenly identified as
the onset or offset. The algorithm detected these errors by comparing the am-
plitude from onset-to-peak versus peak-to-offset. When one amplitude was
more than 40% smaller than the other, indicating asymmetry, correction was
attempted. To correct the onset, the algorithm searched within a 50ms window
16
==PAGE:24==
before the detected onset to find the steepest slope, then traced backward to
locate where the signal became relatively flat (defined as 50% of the steepest
slope). If such a point could be found, it was taken as the new onset. Otherwise,
the initial onset was kept. The process is illustrated in Figure 8.
For offset correction, a similar process was applied using a 25 ms window after
the detected offset to find the true P-wave ending.
• Exclusion of unphysiological P-waves: Detected P-waves were validated
to remove unphysiological values: P-waves were set as missing if they were
shorter than 20 ms or longer than 180ms, or smaller than 10% of the associ-
ated R-peak amplitude, or exhibited inconsistent temporal order (for example
P-onset after P-peak). P-waves excluded in this step were most likely false
detections caused by signal noise.
• Exclusion of P-wave values for arrhythmic beats: P-waves were set to miss-
ing values for all arrhythmic beats, as they are either missing or have abnormal
morphology during these beats.

2.9
Evaluation of detection performance
Since all subsequent analyses rely on the results of the detection algorithms, it was
essential to estimate the detection performance. To do so, one 20-second ECG
segment was randomly selected for each patient. The algorithms’ outputs along with
the ECG segment were visually inspected to manually categorize the results and
compute performance metrics.
R-peak
Each detected R-peak was categorized as:
17
==PAGE:25==
• Correct: a detection that matches a visible R-peak
• Wrong: a detection at a location where no R-peak is present
• Missing: a visible R-peak not detected
The following metrics were calculated:
• Precision = Correct / (Correct + Wrong)
• Sensitivity = Correct / (Correct + Missing)
• F1 Score = 2 × (Precision × Sensitivity) / (Precision + Sensitivity)
AF and ventricular premature complexes
For AF and ventricular premature complex automated detection respectively, each
detected beat was categorized as:
• True Positive (TP): AF/ventricular premature complex correctly identified
• False Positive (FP): normal beat wrongly labeled as AF/ventricular premature
complex
• False Negative (FN): AF/ventricular premature complex missed by the algo-
rithm
• True Negative (TN): normal beat correctly labeled as normal
The following metrics were calculated for each beat type:
• Accuracy = (TP + TN) / (TP + TN + FP + FN)
• Precision = TP / (TP + FP)
• Sensitivity = TP / (TP + FN)
• Specificity = TN / (TN + FP)
• F1 Score = 2 × (Precision × Sensitivity) / (Precision + Sensitivity)
18
==PAGE:26==
P-wave onset, peak and offset
P-wave onsets, peaks and offsets were categorized for each detected beat as:
• TP: detection matches a visible onset/peak/offset
• FP: detection where no onset/peak/offset is visible
• FN: visible onset/peak/offset not detected
• TN: absent onset/peak/offset correctly ignored
Accuracy, precision, sensitivity, specificity and F1 score were calculated for each P-
wave component (onset, peak and offset).
2.10
Time interval analysis
The goal was to determine how acute obstructive sleep apnea events influence dif-
ferent time intervals in the ECG. As we were mainly interested in atria, we focused
on P-wave related cardiac interval. We also considered the RR interval to assess
heart rate changes.
2.10.1
Time interval selection
The following intervals were included in the analysis:
• RR interval (R-peak to R-peak): represents the time between consecutive
heartbeats and serves as the measure of heart rate.
• PR interval (P-onset to R-peak): represents the time from the onset of atrial
depolarization to the onset of ventricular depolarization. It reflects the atrioven-
tricular conduction.
• P-wave duration (P-onset to P-offset): represents the total time required for
atrial depolarization.
• P-peak to P-offset interval: represents the latter part of atrial depolarization,
from the highest point of the P-wave to its end.
2.10.2
Inclusion criteria
Apneas with continuous AF throughout all their phases (before, during, and after)
have a persistently irregular heart rate and absent P-waves. Therefore, patients with
continuous AF were analyzed separately from the others, and only the RR intervals
were considered for them, as the other intervals could not be defined.
19
==PAGE:27==
2.10.3
Temporal interval analysis
These cardiac intervals were analyzed in 5-second time bins across the three phases:
before (20 seconds), during (variable duration based on apnea length), and after (60
seconds) the apnea event, as defined previously in Figure 3. The 5-second bin width
was chosen to ensure at least two data points per phase, even for short apneas
lasting only 10 seconds. For each apnea event, cardiac intervals within each bin
were averaged to generate a representative value for that bin. Patient-level averages
were then calculated by averaging across all apnea events for each individual patient
within each bin. A grand average was then calculated for each time bin as the mean
across all patient averages. The variability of the grand average was expressed as
the standard error of the mean (SEM).
2.10.4
Normalized interval analysis
These intervals were also analyzed in a normalized version. The before phase was
selected as the baseline period, as it aims to represent the patient’s normal pre-apnea
electrophysiological state. For each apnea event, the mean interval duration during
the baseline was calculated and used as the normalization factor for all three phases
of that specific apnea event. Normalized intervals were expressed as ratios relative
to the baseline value:
Normalized interval = Temporal interval / Baseline
Therefore, a normalized value > 1.0 represents a prolongation of the interval com-
pared to its baseline, whereas a normalized value <1.0 represents a shortening of
the interval compared to its baseline.
The goal of this normalization was to enable direct comparison of each apnea to its
baseline and to account for relative changes across patients with different baseline
characteristics.
The normalized intervals were analyzed with the same approach as the temporal
intervals (5s binning, patient-level average and grand average with SEM).
2.10.5
Statistical analysis
The goal was to identify whether the different intervals are significantly shorter or
longer during and after an OSA event compared to normal breathing before the ap-
nea. For each 5-second bin in these phases, one-sample t-tests were conducted to
compare the normalized interval values against the expected baseline value of 1.0.
One-sample t-tests were chosen because the normalization process establishes a
baseline value (1.0) against which deviations can be assessed. To account for multi-
ple testing across time bins, false discovery rate (FDR) correction was applied. Statis-
tical significance was defined as FDR-corrected p<0.05. A minimum sample size of
20
==PAGE:28==
5 patients per time bin was required for statistical testing. Time bins with insufficient
data points were thus excluded from the statistical analysis.
2.11
Arrhythmia occurrence analysis
The goal was to determine how OSA events influence the occurrence of atrial arrhyth-
mias during and after apnea compared to normal breathing before apnea. Our hy-
pothesis was that apneas would act as a trigger for arrhythmias, leading to a higher
frequency of arrhythmic events during and after the apnea compared to baseline.
We therefore analyzed the temporal dynamics of arrhythmia occurrence during the 3
phases (before, during and after) and we performed statistical comparison between
these phases to identify any significant change.
Patients with continuous AF throughout all phases of all their apneas were excluded
from this analysis, as the constant arrhythmic activity would mask other arrhythmic
changes.
2.11.1
Arrhythmia occurrence across time
To visualize the temporal dynamics of atrial arrhythmia occurrence with time, we fol-
lowed a similar approach as in the time interval analysis: the data was analyzed in
5-second bins. Two metrics were considered: the number of atrial arrhythmias per
bin and the percentage of atrial arrhythmic beats. The second metric was introduced
to account for heart rate changes. The metrics were computed for each bin of each
apnea. Patient-level averages were then calculated by averaging across all apnea
events for each individual patient within each bin. A grand average was then calcu-
lated for each time bin as the mean across all patients. The variability of the grand
average was expressed as the SEM.
While our primary focus was on atrial arrhythmias, we also detected ventricular pre-
mature complexes and therefore performed the same analysis for ventricular arrhyth-
mias separately and for all arrhythmia types combined.
2.11.2
Statistical comparison between phases
For the statistical analysis, we wanted to test if the occurrence of arrhythmia dur-
ing and after apnea significantly changes compared to before. For each patient, we
calculated the average number of arrhythmias per minute and the percentage of ar-
rhythmic beat across all their apnea for each phase. We then calculated the overall
mean with SEM.
Given the non-parametric nature of the data and the lack of assumption of normal
distribution, we used the Mann-Whitney U test for all comparisons. All statistical
tests were performed as two-tailed tests to detect any significant differences be-
tween phases without assuming a specific direction. Three pairwise comparisons
21
==PAGE:29==
were performed for each metric (before VS during, before VS after and during VS
after). Statistical significance was defined as p<0.05.
The same statistical comparison was also performed for ventricular arrhythmias sep-
arately.

4
Discussion
4.1
Arrhythmic response to OSA events
4.1.1
Atrial arrhythmias
Contrary to what we were expecting, we found no significant changes in atrial ar-
rhythmia occurrence during or after apnea events compared to baseline. AF was
continuous before, during and after apneas for all patients with AF, and there was
no statistical difference for supraventricular premature complexes between the three
phases. Overall, our results show no evidence that isolated OSA events directly trig-
ger an atrial arrhythmic response. Several factors may explain this negative finding.
First, single isolated apnea events may be insufficient to trigger an arrhythmic re-
sponse for supraventricular premature complexes. A cumulative effect throughout
the night may be required to see any differences. The relatively short apnea duration
of the apneas included in our analysis may also have limited arrhythmic responses.
Moreover, there was a low prevalence of supraventricular premature complexes.
Only 12 patients exhibited at least one supraventricular premature complex. Less
than 100 supraventricular premature complexes were detected combining all these
patients, which were distributed in 42 apneas. This rarity of arrhythmic events sug-
gests that a larger sample size would be needed to detect clinically meaningful dif-
ferences.
The specific population type (post-stroke) may have influenced results, as they may
have different underlying pathophysiology that interfere with the effect of isolated ap-
nea events. This could explain the continuous AF that we saw throughout all apneas
for the patients with AF.
Finally, supraventricular premature complexes were detected semi-automatically through
manual assessment of beats with abnormal duration by a biomedical engineering
student rather than a trained clinician. This approach may have resulted in missed
events or misclassifications, introducing measurement uncertainty that could prevent
detection of changes in arrhythmia occurrence.
4.1.2
Ventricular arrhythmias
Our primary focus was on atrial arrhythmias, but we also briefly explored ventricu-
lar arrhythmias. For ventricular premature complexes, we also saw no significant
changes. This could be explained by the same reasons as for atrial arrhythmias.
38
==PAGE:46==
4.2
Heart rate response to OSA events
Patients without AF
We found a statistically significant prolongation of RR intervals during sleep apnea
events compared to normal breathing preceding the events, especially during the
first 20 seconds of the apnea (+3 to 5%). This indicates a slower heart rate, so a
bradycardic response. It supports the presence of a diving reflex initiated by the
parasympathetic system: the heart rate slows down to reduce myocardial oxygen
demand during periods of reduced oxygen availability [21].
After the apnea termination, we found a transient shortening of RR intervals, espe-
cially significant in the 5-10s following apnea end. The RR intervals were then not
significantly different from baseline, suggesting a return to normal heart rate. This
indicates a transient faster heart rate immediately following apnea termination, so a
brief tachycardic response. The resumption of breathing indeed triggers sympathetic
nervous system activation in response to preceding hypoxic stress to restore oxygen
levels [22].
This heart rate response is well documented and is considered one of the mecha-
nisms that may promote arrhythmias, especially AF. The repetitions of these heart
rate fluctuations and autonomic shifts throughout the night may indeed contribute to
their development [23].
From a methodological perspective, our results concerning the heart rate response
to OSA events was highly dependent on the R-peak detection. The estimate of the
detection performance for the R-peak showed excellent results (precision of 0.996
and sensitivity of 0.989). This suggests that our heart rate analysis is based on
reasonably reliable beat detection.
Patients with AF
In contrast to patients with normal sinus rhythm, there was no statistically significant
difference in RR interval duration during and after OSA events in patients with AF.
This finding can be explained by the characteristically chaotic heart rhythm of AF. The
baseline values in these patients were calculated from highly irregular heartbeats,
which may have masked the more subtle changes typically observed in sinus rhythm.
As a result, it becomes difficult to detect significant heart rate changes in response
to apnea.
Furthermore, in normal sinus rhythm, the observed response is driven by autonomic
modulation of the sinoatrial node, which governs the heart rate by initiating electrical
impulses in the atria. However, during AF, electrical impulses originate from multi-
ple ectopic sites or reentrant circuits [9], and could therefore be less influenced by
autonomic fluctuations.
39
==PAGE:47==
4.3
Atrial response to OSA events
4.3.1
PR interval
We did not observe significant changes in PR interval during apnea. However, due to
insufficient data points, we could not statistically test for apnea duration after 45s. Our
results indicate a possible trend toward PR interval shortening with longer apneas,
particularly after 55 seconds, but this statement would require more analysis with
longer apneas.
After apnea events, we found a statistically significant shortening of PR intervals,
particularly between 20-55 seconds after apnea termination. However, the effect
was small (-1 to -2%) and not be physiologically significant.
The PR interval represents the time between the onset of atrial depolarization to the
peak of ventricular depolarization. Physiologically, this interval reflects the time it
takes for the electrical impulse to travel from the atria, through the atrioventricular
node, and the early ventricular depolarization.
A shortening of the PR interval may be explained by faster atrioventricular nodal con-
duction or the presence of an accessory pathway that bypasses the atrioventricular
node [52]. However, these mechanisms are very unlikely to be present in our study,
as the observed changes in PR interval had a very small magnitude. The changes
we observed are more likely to be caused by normal physiological fluctuations rather
than pathological conduction abnormalities.
Other studies found contradictory results concerning PR intervals. Zeng et al. [31]
found a decrease in PR intervals during apneas compared to normal breathing whereas
Smith et al.
[34] found that respiratory arousals induce PR interval lengthening,
greater than with spontaneous arousals.
Overall, there is no evidence regarding PR interval changes induced by acute OSA
events, and the small magnitude of changes observed in our study does not suggest
a mechanism that would explain the development of atrial arrhythmias.
4.3.2
P-wave and P-peak to P-offset interval
P-wave duration and P-peak to P-offset interval did not show any statistically signif-
icant changes both during and after apnea compared to baseline. For the P-wave,
there was no clear trend. The P-peak to P-offset interval showed a possible pro-
longation after 35 seconds of apnea, but we did not have enough data to test for
statistical significance after 45s of apnea. Further analysis with longer apnea would
be required to investigate the effect of prolonged apneas.
The P-wave duration reflects the total time of atrial depolarization, while the P-peak
to P-offset interval represents the latter phase of this depolarization.
A previous
40
==PAGE:48==
study by Gaisl et al. [36] investigating the acute effects of 20-second simulated OSA
events found a significant prolongation of P-wave duration, by approximately 12-13
ms, which may indicate atrial conduction delay. However, their study used controlled
simulated apneas in healthy subjects, whereas our analysis examined spontaneous
apneas in stroke patients, which may explain the difference in the results.
Overall, there is also no evidence regarding P-waves changes induced by acute OSA
events based on our results.
4.3.3
P-wave detection limitation
The analysis of PR interval, P-wave duration, and P-peak to P-offset interval was
dependent on the detection of P-wave components (onset, peak and offset). P-wave
detection showed reasonable performance estimates (precision 0.949-0.958, speci-
ficity 0.895-0.912) but remained inferior to R-peak detection. This detection uncer-
tainty may explain the absence of significant results in these intervals, as the changes
we see could be caused measurement errors.
Moreover, the changes we observed approached the temporal resolution limit of our
ECG signals. The ECG were recorded with a sampling frequency of 256 Hz, resulting
in a sampling period of 4 ms. For a PR interval of 225 ms, a 2% change corresponds
to a temporal change of 4.4 ms. Such small changes are at the threshold of what
can be reliably detected and measured with our current sampling rate, potentially
introducing measurement uncertainty. A higher resolution would thus be required to
study these changes with more precision.
4.4
Patient population
The analysis included 52 patients who had recently experienced a stroke or a tran-
sient ischemic attack a few days before the PSG recording. The clinical charac-
teristics of our cohort are consistent with a stroke population. The patients were
overall old, with a mean age of 68 years, and nearly two third were overweight or
obese (65%). This reflect a typical stroke clinical population, as both age and obe-
sity are risk factors for stroke. The gender distribution (63% male, 37% female) also
reflects typical stroke demographics, with men showing a slightly higher incidence
than women [53]. We found a high prevalence of AF (30%) in the cohort. This pro-
portion is higher than in the general Danish population, where AF prevalence was
approximately 3% in 2018 [54], but it is coherent with stroke populations where AF is
more common. Other studies have indeed found similar proportions of AF in stroke
populations [55, 56].
Analyzing a stroke population is clinically relevant because this group is both at risk of
OSA and cardiac arrhythmias, making it appropriate for investigating OSA-arrhythmia
interactions. Furthermore, the stroke population allowed us to study real clinical data
41
==PAGE:49==
in a group with frequent apneas. In the general population, we would have had less
patients with OSA and fewer apneic events available to explore.
However, our research goal was to investigate the acute effects of OSA events on
atrial electrophysiology, not to explore stroke mechanisms or outcomes. The pres-
ence of stroke can thus also be seen as a limitation. Stroke itself may affect cardiac
electrophysiology, independently of OSA events. This could have confounded our re-
sults, making it challenging to distinguish between OSA-related changes and stroke-
induced cardiac complications. Stroke patients are indeed at elevated risk for atrial
arrhythmias through mechanisms unrelated to OSA. The results we observed during
our analysis may therefore be caused by underlying stroke-related pathophysiology
rather than being directly induced by individual OSA events.
Another limitation is the reduced size of the cohort. Of the initial 70 patients assessed
with PSG, 18 had to be excluded for different reasons (absence of stroke, technical
problems during recording, empty data files, or complete absence of OSA events).
This reduced our analyzable population to 52 patients. Moreover, all patients with AF
presented continuous AF in all their apnea events, independently of apnea phases.
They could thus not be included in our main analyses, reducing the number of patients
to 36. All these exclusions reduced sample size for statistical comparison.
4.5
Apnea selection
A critical methodological consideration was the definition of isolated sleep apnea to
ensure that measurements in the baselines and recovery periods were not influenced
by other apneic episodes. The 60-second threshold used to define isolated apneas
was selected as a compromise between allowing sufficient physiological recovery
and preserving a sufficient number of apneas. This exclusion choice reduced our an-
alyzable events to 27% of all annotated obstructive apneas. Using a longer recovery
period might have given better cardiac stabilization of the baseline but it would have
reduced the sample size.
Moreover, this criterion might introduce a bias in the data. Patients with severe OSA
who experience more frequent events are more likely to have closely spaced apneas
and thus fewer isolated events. Although each patient contributed equally to the
overall analysis, the reduced number of available apneas for patients with severe
OSA might make the results less representative for them.
The isolated apnea events had a median duration of 15s, and 90% lasted less than
27s (compared to a median of 18s and a 90th percentile of 35s for the whole dataset).
These short durations may not provide sufficient time to trigger significant arrhythmic
responses or detectable changes in atrial electrophysiology. We could also not per-
form statistical analyses after 45 seconds of apnea due to insufficient data in this
range, preventing us from examining the effects of longer apneic events.
Another limitation of the selection criterion is that we considered isolated events re-
42
==PAGE:50==
gardless of when they occurred during the night, without accounting for potential
cumulative effects or circadian influences. Sleep stage, time of night, and the cumu-
lative burden of preceding apneic events might influence the responses to individual
events.
Finally, the isolated apnea selection depended entirely on apnea annotations from
a previous study. This creates potential sources of errors, as inaccurate onset or
termination could affect phase definitions and missed apneas could bias the baseline
and recovery phase.
4.6
Limitation of the detection performance
evaluation
The evaluation of algorithm detection performance was conducted through visual
inspection of one randomly selected 20-second ECG segment per patient, where
detected events were manually categorized as correct or incorrect. This very simple
approach introduces several important limitations.
The limited sample size of one single short segments per patient may miss challeng-
ing cases or specific arrhythmia patterns that could reveal algorithm weaknesses.
Moreover, visual assessment is subjective and was performed by a biomedical en-
gineering student rather than a clinical expert, potentially introducing classification
errors.
Most critically, the algorithm was validated by directly observing its results. This in-
troduces a bias, as judgment may be unconsciously influenced by the algorithm’s
outputs. A more rigorous approach would involve blind annotations independent of
algorithm results, followed by comparison to avoid such bias. These methodological
limitations suggest that the reported detection performance may be overestimated
and should be interpreted with caution.

==PAGE:51==
5
Conclusion
In this study of a post-stroke danish patient cohort, isolated OSA events did not
acutely increase atrial arrhythmia occurrence or significantly alter atrial conduction
markers. These findings suggest that single apneas may be insufficient to induce de-
tectable electrophysiological changes. Future research could investigate the effects
of longer apneas, repeated episodes, the influence of sleep stage and time of night
on cardiac responses to OSA, or study different populations.
