Abstract
This thesis studies the scattering effects of a DLA and how it changes the sound
field that is being recorded.
The application considered for this type of array is
near field sound reconstruction, especially near a porous material wall.
The pro-
posed method is looking at the difference between a recording with the array and
an ideal recording where the effect of the array presence in the sound field is null.
This difference was estimated through simulation and measurements where multiple
parameters were tested. Then, the results are analyzed and the multiple parameters
are compared.
keywords: system evaluation, double-layer-array, wave scattering, errors in record-
ing, acoustic holography, sound field reconstruction, porous material

Chapter 1
Introduction
Using a single device made out of multiple microphones for one recording is a
logical solution to improve the recording of acoustic signals. Such systems are called
microphone arrays. They are an essential component of many acoustical engineering
areas (speech recording, environment surveillance, room acoustics characterization,
sound system design, etc.). Thus a multitude of array types exist, varying in shape,
size, number of microphones, and microphone distribution.
The array type studied in this thesis is formed of two parallel grids of evenly
spaced microphones called a double layer array (DLA). The application considered
is the mapping of the near sound field. Indeed, grid DLA are one type of array that
is used in acoustics beamforming and near field holography. Beamforming uses the
multiple microphone inputs to focus on a single direction of the signal, while holog-
raphy reconstructs the acoustic pressure or velocity field. As the particle velocity is
a vector, using two layers recording the front and back directions allows for a better
assessment of the sound field.
Research has seen a surge of newer and better applications for sound field recon-
struction. The majority of applications deals with noise source identification. For ex-
ample Reference [3] looks into computing a real time display of noise sources through
simulation and an experiment inside a Boeing 757 aircraft in flight. Additionally,
Reference [4] investigates the design of an array for both near field holography and
beamforming on a set of monopoles, with simulation and two loudspeakers, with mea-
surements. Other applications use reconstruction in enclosed spaces to give insights
in room modes, reflection, reverberation, etc, as illustrated in Reference [5] which
reconstructs room impulse response with no prior assumptions on the characteristics
of the room. Using the grid DLA studied in this thesis, Reference [6] reconstructs
the sound field on the surface of a porous material to calculate its surface impedance
and absorption coefficient.
Rather than using an impedance tube, the array was
chosen because of its ability to perform the material measurement directly where the
material is mounted.
The goal of the previously referenced studies was to evaluate the accuracy of
sound field reconstruction using an array. However, very few evaluations of the accu-
racy of recording exists. Such evaluation exists only in Reference [7] which shows that
the sound pressure scattered by the array back toward the source does not change
the incident sound field in a significant manner. As reconstruction, and most other
DLA application, rely on the recording, it would make sense to at first consider the

recording accuracy.
The study of how a single microphone or other more complex objects influence
the sound field shows that scattering of the sound waves can change the sound field
in various degrees of importance.
Reference [8] shows that a sound field record-
ing method considering multiple scattering is more accurate than a single scattering
method. Some rigid arrays are even used in such a way that the scattering is pre-
dictable by using a solid sphere, and so, easily compensated for [9]. However, the
few existing scattering studies focus on spherical arrays while the grid DLA falls into
a more complex category. While numerous applications using the specific grid DLA
studied in this thesis already show appropriate results [2] [6] [10], a focus on its effect
on the surrounding sound field may bring further information. It may assure that
the lack of emphasis in research on its scattering effect is valid. Or it may bring up
the need for a calibration targeted at the errors introduced by scattering. As such,
the following thesis will report the evaluation and quantification of the scattering in-
troduced by evenly spaced, grid DLA with a later focus on one application of sound
field reconstruction.
This report starts by highlighting the theory guiding the project. It describes
the DLA devices before formulating the simulation, and then measurements methods.
Following this, the results are divided in three sections showing the simulation results,
the measurements results and comparing the different parameters of the studies.

2.1
Basics of sound waves[1]
• Sound field & Free field
A sound field is a region of an elastic medium containing sound waves (IEC
801-23-27 [11]). It is a result of the sound sources and the acoustic properties of the
medium which defines how the sound waves interact with the region.
A free field is a sound field in a homogeneous isotropic medium whose boundaries
exert a negligible effect on the sound waves (IEC 801-23-28 [11]). This means that
the sound waves are propagating in a region with no obstacles, so no reflections.
• Medium properties [12]
The primary medium used in acoustics is air which is characterized by its speed of
sound and density. Simplified relations for dry air (without the inclusion of moisture)

2.2
Sound waves interaction with objects
A sound wave that comes in contact with a surface can be reflected, absorbed
or transmitted.
• reflection
A plane wave that comes in contact with a perpendicular plane rigid surface
is reflected in the opposite direction. The terms generally used are incident wave
and reflected wave. The pressure is now composed of the sum of these waves, such
that ptot = pi + pr. More precisely, it is the sum of a wave traveling in the positive
x-direction (pi) and a wave traveling in the opposite direction (pr).
ˆp = p+ej(ωt−kx) + p−ej(ωt+kx)
(2.6)
However, the rigid surface (placed at x = 0) tells us that p+ = p−as the entirety of
the incident wave is being reflected (no absorption). From this relation follows
ˆp = 2p+cos(kx)ejωt,
(2.7)
and at x = 0 , ˆp = 2p+ejωt. This means that at the surface of a rigid plane, the
amplitude of a wave traveling in a perpendicular direction is doubled.
• interference
When two waves of the same phase meet each other, the amplitude of the result-
ing wave is the sum of each wave amplitude. This phenomenon is called constructive
interference. (The previous doubling at a rigid surface is a specific example of it).
When two waves of the opposite phase meet each other, the amplitude of the
resulting wave is the difference of each wave amplitude. This phenomenon is called
destructive interference. In the case of two waves having the same amplitude to begin
with, the resulting pressure is null, as the amplitude cancel each other.
• Surface size and wavelength
Reflection, interference, and in general scattering depends on the relation be-
tween the wavelength λ of the incoming waves and the dimension of the object D.
When D ≪λ, the reflection is weak to none.
When D ≈λ, the reflection
becomes noticeable, and has the wavelength increases so does the reflection.

3.2
Simulation Method
The goal of the simulation is to quantify the scattering effect of the array. Using
a simulation allows to recreate the sound field interacting with the array geometry at
any point in space and not just at the microphones position. It also allows to record
the pressure at each microphone position, even when the array is not present in the
space (later called reference). The difference in sound field between the situation
where the array is present and when it is not, shows its scattering effect.
Multiple situations regarding the DLA are tested. The primary situation uses
the condenser DLA from B&K placed 34 cm away from the source in a space free of
obstacles. The other situations are represented in Figure 3.6.
Figure 3.6: Outline of the different variables of the simulation
The condenser DLA will be switched out with the MEMS array to test a different
DLA design and the distance of 34 cm will be increased and decreased. A situation
where the array is placed 14 mm from a wall of porous material with a source posi-
tioned behind the array is tested. The source will always be aligned with the center
of the array with no angle of incidence.
COMSOL Multiphysics is the software chosen to perform the simulations because
of its Acoustics module allowing frequency studies to be made. The following sections
explains the steps for the main conditions.

Simulation
The following chapter presents the results of simulations that were carried out
in COMSOL. The focus is on the effect of the pressure waves scattering on the DLA

At this second layer, a new effect of scattering is observed at frequency 5 kHz: a row

to travel through the 1st layer gaps, in between the microphones, before reaching the
second layer. As such, only a part of the waves travel to the second layer without
coming into contact with the 1st layer; those create the high pressure rows on the
2nd layer. The rest is reflected and scattered in other directions by the 1st layer;
those create the low pressure rows on the 2nd layer. The reason it happens at this
frequency only has to do with the wavelength since scattering is ruled by the size
of the object compared to the wavelength of the incident sound. The wavelength of
a 5 kHz pure tone in this simulation is of 6.86 cm but the gaps between the rows
of microphones are only 2.5 cm in height and 3.5 cm considering the diagonal of a
4-microphone square.

4.3.1
Errors regarding Sound Reconstruction in references
This small section relates values of difference in pressure on the recording to
sound field reconstruction error, by citing different references. The goal is to give
meaning to the amplitude and phase difference values.

error while ±1 of recording error, up to -11 dB. Only in the range of 50–200 Hz, does
the phase error give acceptable intensity estimates for ±0.1.

recorded pressure and looked at the pressure predictions near a large panel driven
at 600 Hz, using simulation and the SONAH method. Overall, the predictions were
satisfying with around 1 dB SPL of error on the reconstruction.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
This thesis had the objective of evaluating the influence of a grid DLA on the
sound field due to scattering. It used simulation with two types of DLA and multiple
distances between DLA and source, as well as measurements but only with an 8x8
condenser DLA.
The results show clear signs of scattering by the arrays. Focusing on the recorded
pressure, all distances and arrays present significant recording errors which will cre-
ate errors when applying sound reconstruction methods.
An important source of
errors for the condenser DLA is shown, from both simulations and measurements, to
come from the front of the handle. It was highlighted that, as the frequency of the
incident sound waves increases, so do the scattering errors. Finally, the simulation
tested a situation with a porous material wall, representing the application of in-situ
characterization of a material with a DLA. For this situation, while the errors on the
pressure amplitude are minimized, the errors on the pressure phase becomes larger,
especially at higher frequencies. Having a proper study showing the scattering effect
of this type of DLA
The next step would be to use methods of sound field reconstruction to directly
relate errors in the recording with errors in the reconstruction.
