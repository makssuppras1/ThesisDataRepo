Abstract
Globally, mental­health concerns among young adults remain elevated while smartphones
have become nearly ubiquitous, making mobile platforms a natural venue for brief and
private emotion work. We developed a lightweight mobile application that enables non­
clinical users to record their moods with minimal effort and receive gentle, non­judgmental
feedback. Guided by principles of low interaction burden, clear single­screen flows, com­
passionate tone, and all data encrypted on­device, the system was designed through a
user­centered, iterative process from formative research to prototyping. The resulting
app allows users to log emotions via a one­gesture interface with optional text notes, vi­
sualize mood patterns through glanceable weekly or monthly charts, and receive context­
sensitive support such as brief affirmation messages or a 60­second guided breathing
exercise after negative entries. In a one­week field evaluation with 10 young adults, all
core features (mood check­ins, visual summaries, and gentle interventions) were used
successfully with high usability (mean System Usability Scale ≈78). Participants found
the app’s gentle tone and at­a­glance summaries easy to accept and interpret, and most
exhibited a slight improvement in short­term affect balance, suggesting that this approach
can foster everyday emotional reflection and support well­being without imposing burden.

1
Introduction
Mental health has become a critical concern in modern society, particularly for young
adults, university students, and early­career professionals who face elevated risks of anx­
iety, stress, and existential confusion. Although professional therapy remains indispens­
able, not everyone has reliable access or feels at ease with traditional clinical channels.
Contemporary surveillance underscores the scale of need among adolescents and young
adults. Globally, about one in seven adolescents (10–19 years) lives with a mental dis­
order; depressive and anxiety disorders contribute substantially to years lived with dis­
ability, and suicide is the fourth leading cause of death among those aged 15–29[1][2].
Within higher education, the 2023–2024 Healthy Minds Study reports that 38% of students
screened positive for moderate­to­severe depression (PHQ­9 ≥10), 34% for moderate­to­
severe anxiety (GAD­7 ≥10), and 13% reported past­year suicidal ideation.[3] Although
indicators have eased from pandemic peaks, depressive and anxiety symptoms remain
more prevalent than before the pandemic across Organisation for Economic Co­operation
and Development(OECD) countries[4]. In this context, digital self­care tools, especially
mobile applications, which have emerged as accessible and scalable means to support
mental well­being. Over the past decade, the near­ubiquity of smartphones has created
a practical conduit for capturing everyday affect and subjective well­being in situ, rather
than retrospectively. In the United States, for example, around nine in ten adults now own
a smartphone, making mobile platforms a plausible default context for self­tracking and
lightweight feedback in daily life.[5] Beyond basic logging, mobile devices enable real­time
sampling of experiences, ecological momentary assessment (EMA), and the delivery of
ecological momentary interventions (EMIs), i.e., brief, context­proximal supports delivered
in naturalistic settings.[6]In personal informatics, these capabilities align with stage­based
models in which collection should be simple, reflection legible, and action proximal to daily
routines.[7], the details regarding this system will be explained in subsequent chapters.
Despite this promise, day­to­day engagement with well­being and mood apps remains
fragile. Reviews of digital mental health tools repeatedly identify high user effort (e.g.,
frequent text entry, long forms) and cognitive load as barriers to sustained use; ease of
use, flexible flows, and concise interactions are recurring facilitators.[8][9]From a human–
computer interaction viewpoint, heavy collection demands in the “collection” stage tend to
undermine progression to “reflection” and “action.”[7] In parallel, intervention design liter­
atures suggest that small, timely supports, rather than didactic or prescriptive messages,
are better candidates for near­term engagement, especially when adapted to momentary
state, specifically, the just­in­time adaptive intervention(JITAI) approach often summa­
rized as delivering the right type and amount of support at the right time, which supplies
context­aware, timely micro­interventions via mobile devices.[10]
Another limitation concerns feedback style and tone. Many existing products privilege an­
alytic dashboards or corrective prompts. By contrast, work in positive computing and pos­
itive psychology interventions indicates that brief, strengths­oriented activities and com­
passionate wording can enhance subjective well­being and reduce negative affect, par­
ticularly when the burden is low and the interaction is perceived as supportive rather than
evaluative. Also, when negative emotions prevail, interactions and feedback should be
kept gentle to avoid imposing a negative burden on users. In short, the felt quality of
feedback matters, not only its informational content.[11][12]
A cross­cutting issue is privacy and trust. Analyses of popular mental health apps have

shown undisclosed data transmission to third­party services and inconsistencies between
privacy policies and actual practices, or non­encrypted information leakage, fueling user

1.1
Research Question
This thesis addresses the practical gap between what current well­being apps enable
and what non­clinical users are willing and able to do in everyday settings. The project
targets young adults (e.g., university students and early­career professionals) who seek a
low­effort, non­judgemental, and privacy­respecting way to record emotions and receive
gentle support. The goal is not diagnosis or treatment, but emotional first aid: a safe
space to record, reflect, and release.
The research question that this master’s thesis will resolve is:
How can a lightweight mobile system help users express and release emo­
tional distress while providing gentle encouragement and emotional feedback
that supports self­awareness and short­term well­being?
Specifically, a number of sub­questions were formulated: Firstly, regarding low­burden
input/usability: how can a mobile interface be designed to support emotion recording
with minimal interaction cost while preserving expressiveness? Secondly, which forms
of gentle support (e.g., affirmations, breathing prompts, wording tone) are perceived as
supportive and non­judgemental in everyday use, and under what contexts? Then, we
need to systematize the process of tracking →reflection: How can tracking and feedback
be combined to scaffold a meaningful, repeatable reflection process in daily life?
1.2
Research goals
To address these problems, this thesis will accomplish the following sub­goals:
1. Survey theory and prior systems relevant to low­burden input, gentle support, and
privacy­by­design.
2. Translate these evidence into design principles and interaction requirements under
this study.
3. Design a mobile application that operationalizes these principles. And enables low­
friction emotion recording and delivers gentle, non­judgmental support for nonclini­
cal users in everyday contexts.
4. Evaluate usability, perceived support, and short­term experience in naturalistic use
The work does not make clinical efficacy claims, does not provide diagnoses, and does
not evaluate long­term mental­health outcomes. Cloud synchronization, therapy portals,
and personalized recommendation systems are beyond the scope of this project.
1.3
Thesis overview
This thesis follows a design–build–evaluate arc that moves from evidence and context to
a working system and a small­scale field study. The work targets everyday, non­clinical
use and is guided by three priorities that appear throughout the chapters: low interaction
burden, legible and gentle feedback, and privacy­by­design with local encrypted storage.

Background and Related Work
2.1
Conceptual Foundations for Non­clinical Well­being
This subsection outlines the concepts and theoretical strands that commonly underpin
non­clinical mobile support for affect and subjective well­being. The aim is to provide a
neutral frame for interpreting prior systems and empirical findings.
2.1.1
Core constructs
In everyday contexts, affect refers to short­lived valenced feelings; emotion typically de­
notes patterned affective episodes with appraisals and action tendencies; and mood de­
notes more diffuse, longer­lasting affective tone. Subjective well­being (SWB) is often
described along a hedonic axis—positive and negative affect plus life satisfaction—and,
in some accounts, a eudaimonic axis concerning meaning and functioning. Non­clinical
technologies generally target self­awareness, reflection, and short­term regulation of af­
fect rather than diagnosis or treatment.
2.1.2
Personal informatics and the burden–reflection trade­off
Personal informatics research characterizes self­tracking as a stage­based process (e.g.,
collection →integration →reflection →action)[7]. A recurring observation is that inter­
action and cognitive burden accumulates at the collection stage, and that high effort can
suppress adherence and downstream reflection; conversely, simple, legible representa­
tions facilitate integration and reflection. Systematic reviews of health and well­being apps
converge on similar facilitators and barriers: concise flows, clear affordances and flexibil­
ity support engagement, whereas frequent text entry and long forms undermine it [15][16].
These findings do not prescribe one interface, but they motivate a general design goal of
low interaction cost while preserving expressiveness.
Figure 2.1: The Stage­Based Model of Personal Informatics Systems[7]
2.1.3
In­situ assessment and micro­support
Mobile devices afford ecological momentary assessment (EMA)—sampling experiences
in situ—and ecological momentary interventions (EMIs)—brief supports delivered in nat­
uralistic settings. Within this space, just­in­time adaptive interventions (JITAI) provide a

==PAGE:14==
conceptual language for matching amount and type of support to momentary state and
opportunity[6][10]. The literature notes well­known trade­offs: timeliness versus intrusive­
ness, and adaptivity versus transparency. These approaches are widely used in every­
day, non­clinical studies precisely because they align with short interactions embedded
in routine.
2.1.4
Feedback styles and supportive tone
Prior systems span at least two feedback families. Analytic feedback presents summaries,
trends and comparisons intended to prompt reflection or behavior planning. Supportive
feedback provides brief activities or messages aimed at emotional regulation (e.g., breath­
ing exercises, self­affirmation, strengths­oriented prompts). A recent systematic review
and meta­analysis on apps that promote emotion regulation and positive mental health
reports small­to­moderate benefits for brief, supportive activities, with substantial hetero­
geneity across implementations[17]. Usability­focused evaluations of mHealth interfaces
additionally emphasize the role of non­judgemental wording, clarity, and discoverability
in shaping perceived support and acceptance[16]. Together, these strands suggest that
tone and optionality are not merely stylistic choices but factors that interact with burden
to influence uptake and sustained use.
2.1.5
Measures commonly used in non­clinical evaluations
Non­clinical studies typically assess usability and short­term affective outcomes rather
than clinical endpoints. The System Usability Scale (SUS) is widely adopted as a brief,
technology­agnostic usability measure[18]; for affective experience, instruments such as
the Scale of Positive and Negative Experience (SPANE)[19] capture short­term positive
and negative feelings. The choice of measures depends on scope—usability for interac­
tion quality, affect measures for proximal emotional impact.
2.2
Interaction and Intervention Design in Prior Systems
This subsection surveys how non­clinical systems have approached everyday emotion
tracking and support delivery. The aim is to summarize design patterns, evidence, and
known trade­offs.
2.2.1
Recording and tracking
Prior work spans three broad capture approaches.
• Free­text diaries and open journaling: Mobile journaling maximizes expressive­
ness and can scaffold narrative reflection, yet it is time­demanding. Early mobile
self­monitoring studies report mixed adherence in naturalistic use, citing the effort
of repeated text entry and multi­step forms as a barrier[20].Reviews across health
and well­being apps converge on similar barriers : high entry effort, long forms, and
facilitators: concise flows, clear affordances, flexibility[15].
• Fixed­format self­reports: To reduce interaction cost and standardize data, many
systems employ categorical or scalar inputs, Likert items, emoji pickers, or valence–
arousal sliders. In personal informatics, self­tracking is modelled as a stage­based
process (collection →integration →reflection →action); burden accumulates at col­
lection, and ease at this stage enables downstream reflection[7]. For compact affect
self­report, instruments such as the Self­Assessment Manikin (SAM) and the digital
Affective Slider provide quick ratings of valence and arousal and are widely used as
low­friction inputs[21]. A survey of commercial mood apps similarly documents the
dominance of brief categorical and slider­based inputs in the field, consistent with
these patterns, an analysis of 32 commercially available mood­tracking apps found
6

that most supported collection and reflection but offered limited features for prepa­
ration and action; users valued visualizations and reminders, yet reported high input
burden (and, in some cases, stability or paywall issues) [22].
• Event­ or time­based prompts (EMA) and hybrid sensing: Experience­sampling
schedules—fixed, random, or context­triggered, which enable in­situ data capture
but face a timeliness–intrusiveness tension; compliance improves with short prompts
and predictable rhythms. Some systems pair brief self­report with passive context
(e.g., activity, location) to reduce interaction cost while retaining meaning.[6]
Overall, richer inputs can deepen reflection but may reduce adherence; lean inputs sup­
port regularity but may need complementary mechanisms to preserve subtle differences.
2.2.2
Emotion representation
Figure 2.2: 12­Point Affect Circumplex (12­PAC)[23]
Prior work conceptualizes core affect as a two­dimensional space spanned by valence
(pleasantness) and arousal (activation) and proposes a twelve­sector circumplex, which
often referred to as 12­PAC(Fig 2.2), that allows affect terms to be positioned with fine
granularity on a polar coordinate system. Design implications used in Chapter4: one­
hand, single­surface logging via drag on a discretized valence–arousal plane; intensity
encoded by distance.
This classification strategy also effectively addresses the shortcomings of the one­dimensional
categorization commonly adopted by apps in app stores.
2.2.3
Feedback and intervention: analytics, prompts, and supportive
activities
Feedback in prior systems falls into the following families.
• Analytic feedback: Dashboards that summarize patterns (trends, comparisons,
calendars) aim to trigger reflection and planning. Personal and lived informatics
studies emphasize that reflection depends on legible representations and discover­
Mobile Mental Health Application for Everyday Mood Tracking and Gentle Support
7
==PAGE:16==
able navigation; heavy text or unclear affordances depress uptake. Recent usability
evaluations of mood apps echo these issues.[7][16]
• Supportive micro­interventions: Many systems deliver brief activities to aid breath­
ing exercise, mindfulness prompts, gratitude or self­affirmation messages. A sys­
tematic review and meta­analysis reports small­to­moderate improvements in emo­
tion regulation and positive mental health from mobile interventions, with substantial
heterogeneity across content and delivery. Also, usability evaluations of mHealth
interfaces further highlight the role of non­judgemental wording, clarity, and concise
copy in perceived support and acceptance[17].
In social psychology, self­affirmation interventions (brief exercises that affirm per­
sonally important values or strengths) have been shown to buffer threat and reduce
stress responses across domains, with effects documented in health contexts as
well as education. Delivering such content in concise, non­judgemental phrasing
suits mobile micro­moments and avoids evaluative tones that may trigger informa­
tion avoidance[24].
Ecological Momentary Interventions (EMIs), involve delivering treatment or sup­
port to individuals in real time during their everyday lives via mobile technologies.
These interventions have demonstrated feasibility and general acceptability across
a range of domains, including smoking cessation, physical activity, and healthy eat­
ing. Among the most commonly employed components are brief motivational or
encouraging messages delivered in a lightweight design[25].
• Paced breathing: Heart­rate­variability(HRV)biofeedback and paced breathing have
meta­analytic support for reducing stress and anxiety, some evidence also indicates
benefits for depressive symptoms[26][27].
• Timing and optionality: Just­in­time adaptive interventions (JITAIs) formalize the
idea of providing the right type and amount of support at the right time by adapting
to momentary state and context; the framework highlights the risk of burden from
ill­timed prompts and the need for transparency and user control[10].
• Other engagement strategies: Gamification and social features (points, challenges,
sharing) have shown short­term engagement benefits in online and mHealth con­
texts, though sustainability and fit to affective goals vary; these approaches may in­
troduce comparison pressures in mood domains and are often used cautiously[28].
Therefore, outcomes are context­dependent and should avoid heavy or competitive
framings in well­being settings[29]. In addition, these cues are optional and can be
rejected by the user.
2.2.4
Synthesis
Across modalities and feedback types, several points recur:
1. Interaction cost is a first­order determinant of adherence. Short, clear flows and
visible affordances facilitate continued use; heavy text entry and multi­step forms
do not.
2. Reflection benefits from legibility and proximity to routine. Representations that are
simple and discoverable are more likely to elicit reflection and planning.
3. Timing and user control are central to acceptance.
4. Systems often emphasize “collection/representation” but under­support “prepara­
tion/action.”
8

5. Just­in­time prompts can help when sparse and predictable, but ill­timed or opaque
reminders erode goodwill; optionality and transparency are therefore central.
6. Supportive micro­activities show small­to­moderate short­term benefits, with hetero­
geneity by design and delivery. Meta­analytic evidence indicates that brief, support­
ive content. For example, breathing, mindfulness, positive activities can improve
emotion regulation and positive mental health, though effects vary across imple­
mentations.
Together, these strands provide a neutral backdrop for evaluating non­clinical emotion­
support systems in everyday settings.
2.3
Privacy and Data Protection in mHealth for Affective Data
This subsection reviews how prior work conceptualizes privacy in non­clinical emotion
and well­being apps, the risks most often observed, and the safeguards recommended in
research and standards. These will provide a good reference basis for the data encryption
and storage solution adopted in this paper to a certain extent.
2.3.1
Why affective data are sensitive, and what goes wrong in practice
Affective self­reports, notes, emotion records and context traces (e.g., time, location, de­
vice metadata) can reveal highly personal states and routines. Independent audits of
mental­health and health apps have repeatedly found third­party data transmissions and
gaps between practice and disclosure. A cross­sectional study of top depression and
smoking­cessation apps showed that most transmitted data to Facebook/Google services,
while only a minority disclosed this accurately in their privacy policies[13]. Scoping and
systematic reviews likewise document inconsistent privacy practices and large variation
in how app privacy is assessed and reported, cautioning that policy text alone is a poor
proxy for behavior. Some consumer investigations have echoed these concerns at scale,
though methods differ from academic audits[30].Recent work on patients’ perspectives
further shows that confidentiality, control and clarity remain central expectations shaping
willingness to use digital health tools[31].
2.3.2
Data classification and legal basis.
In many regions, general data­protection regimes apply to consumer well­being apps.
The EU General Data Protection Regulation(GDPR) establishes principles: lawfulness,
fairness, transparency; purpose limitation; data minimization; accuracy; storage limita­
tion; integrity and confidentiality; accountability, which set the baseline for processing
personal data. In this study, mood self­reports, optional notes, and time/location meta­
data are treated as personal data under GDPR Art. 4[32]. Where such data reveal or
are processed to infer a participant’s mental health status, they are treated as special­
category “data concerning health”, which guide us adhere to privacy design principles
during the design process.
2.3.3
Privacy­by­Design and data­minimization principles
Across jurisdictions and guidance, two ideas recur. First, Privacy­by­Design (PbD): em­
bedding privacy into system architecture “by default,” including proactive, preventative
controls; privacy as the default setting; end­to­end security; and transparency/user re­
spect[33].Second, data minimization and purpose limitation: collect only what is nec­
essary for clearly stated purposes; retain data only as long as needed; and give users
agency over access, export and deletion[32]. These principles are often invoked in re­
views of mHealth privacy assessments as criteria for evaluating apps and certification
schemes.

2.3.4
Open questions
In this study, these privacy principles on affective mHealth highlights several unresolved
problems:
(i) how to balance data minimization with the desire for richer context for reflection;
(ii) how to provide meaningful consent and usable controls on small screens;
(iii) how to empirically link privacy practices (e.g., local storage, clear deletion/export) to
user trust and sustained engagement in everyday, non­clinical use.
Current evidence maps risks and recommended safeguards, but comparative evaluations
of different privacy designs on engagement and outcomes remain limited.

4.2
Design approach and Brief
4.2.1
Design approach
Overall, the work follows an evidence­informed, human­centred design process with
ISO 9241­210[37], combining literature synthesis and a formative survey to derive re­
quirements, producing and refining design solutions, and evaluating them with users.
In practical terms, the work advances through four linked activities:
1. understand the context of use, formative survey and light desk research;
2. derive user needs, personas and usage scenarios;
3. propose solutions, like experience model, requirements, and low/high­fidelity pro­
totypes;
4. evaluate and iterate.
Beyond the process above,the design in this study is framed by two strands of prior
literature: First, personal informatics models describe the flow from preparation/col­
lection through integration to reflection and action; we therefore prioritize low­friction,
sub­minute capture with optional annotations. Second, for in­situ interactions we draw
JITAI framework to match the amount and type ( the content provided is appropriate for
the current user’s status.) of support to momentary state. In the subsequent subsec­
tions , we make this concrete by showing how these perspectives are operationalized in
requirements and user flows, and where we appropriately extend them for our study’s
design. From the outset, privacy is the constraint: we follow the data­minimization prin­
ciples.
4.2.2
Design Brief
• Audience and context Nonclinical young adults, e.g. university students and
early career professionals, who want to capture everyday affect and receive light,
non­judgmental support during short moments, such as commute, study breaks, or
evening wind down, etc. Typical constraints include single­hand use, intermittent
connectivity, and limited attention.
• Purpose Provide a frame/ structure of emotional aid application: a safe, private
space to record, reflect, and release, without diagnosis or treatment claims.
• Scope Individual self­care only; no clinician interface, crisis triage, or long­term
therapeutic outcomes. The product focuses on short, repeatable interactions that
can fit within daily routines.

4.3.1
Overview Process
Purpose
This subsection explains the page­level process that structures the app experience and
how it operationalizes the stage logic discussed in Chapter 2 (stage­based/lived per­
sonal informatics) and the user­evidence patterns in Chapter3. The process is designed
for short, private, and optional interactions in everyday settings, and serves as the bridge
between the normative baseline in chapter3 and the concrete screens shown in the fol­
lowing.
Process schema
We adopt a four­stage flow that users can enter and leave at any point (also links each
stage to the page that carries it and the key constraints already formalized in Chapter3):
Collect →Overview →Reflect/Insight →Act/Support
1. Collect: quick emotion capture (P1/P2, FR1)
2. Overview: a glanceable temporal map (calendar) ( NFR­7)
3. Act/Support: immediate, lightweight relief and affirmation (P3, FR­2/FR­3)
4. Reflect/Insight: periodic inspection of patterns and precise comparison (P3, NFR­
3, FR5)
The sequence protects adherence by keeping capture proximal and light, anchors rou­
tine with a single “home” overview, places relief next to collection without commitment
to long interventions, users can lapse and resume anytime.
Departures from standard models
Classical accounts separate integration, reflection, and action[7][38]. We merge “inte­
gration” into Overview (a calendar/weekly map that collates entries) and split action into
two tempos: (a) an immediate micro­support adjacent to collection (Act/Support), and
(b) planned adjustments that follow from Reflection/Insight. This adjustment accommo­
dates short attention windows and the need to lapse/resume with minimal friction, while
keeping longer­form analysis optional.

5.2.5
Privacy and Security
This implementation follows a local­first design with no network transmission and no
third­party SDKs. All storage is on­device and encrypted, and all access is mediated
through repository interfaces. These choices were built into the architecture from the
outset to align with privacy­by­design and data­minimization principles.
Core principles
Local­first, zero sharing. The app operates entirely offline; there are no analytics, crash
reporting, or external APIs. Dependency lists confirm only Hive, flutter_secure_storage
and state­management libraries.
User data ownership. Data remain on device and are included only in OS­level backups;
the app itself implements no cloud sync.
Data minimization. Only fields necessary for mood tracking are stored (direction/inten­

Encryption at rest and key management
All Hive boxes are encrypted with AES­256. On first run an Service generates a device­

adapters; application code works with typed models only.

• Repository boundary. UI and state never access storage directly. The abstract

access and maintains business logic separation.
• Type safety. Hive TypeAdapters for MoodRecord, AffirmationCard, and related

• No network surface. Features (recording, trends, breathing, affirmations) func­

Privacy­by­design features
• No PII required. Optional user fields are supported but not required; the app has
no accounts and maintains no authentication tokens.
• Least privilege and consent. The only gated capability is local notifications; users

6.1
Study Aim
This study evaluates a lightweight mobile application designed to help non­clinical users
record emotions and receive gentle support in everyday contexts. In line with the thesis
goals (Chapter1.2), the evaluation focuses on low­burden usability, perceived gentle­
ness and non­judgement, and short­term user experience during a one­week naturalis­
tic deployment. The study does not seek clinical efficacy; rather, it examines whether
the system enables reliable task performance, is acceptable to users, and appears to
support brief reflection and emotional relief.
The assessment operationalizes standard human–computer interaction constructs: ef­
fectiveness and efficiency through task performance; perceived usability through the
System Usability Scale (SUS)[18]; acceptance of key design choices through three
custom Likert items (Gentle mode, at­a­glance Maps, feeling understood/supported);
and short­term affective change through the Scale of Positive and Negative Experience
(SPANE)[19]. Given the small, formative sample, results are reported with robust sum­
maries (medians and IQRs) and binomial Wilson intervals for rates.
Research questions and planned indicators
• Q1: Low­burden usability. Can core tasks be completed with high reliability and
low friction?
Indicators: task completion rate and critical error rate (both with 95% Wilson CI),
and completion time for successful trials (median [Q1, Q3]).
Consistently high completion with few critical errors and stable times indicates
low­burden interaction.
• Q2: Perceived usability. Does the system achieve acceptable overall usability?
Indicators: SUS total score (Mean±SD and Median[IQR]); proportion of partici­
pants with SUS ≥68 (the “at/above average” benchmark).
• Q3: Acceptance of gentle support and maps. Are Gentle mode and the visual

Indicators: three custom 5­point items (Gentle acceptance, Maps at­a­glance com­
prehensibility, feeling understood/supported), reported as Mean±SD and Median[IQR],

Participants. Ten adults (N = 10, 22­26 years) were recruited for from the student /

Discussion
7.1
Key Findings
• Low­burden logging and task usability. Core tasks were completed reliably and
in short, repeatable episodes, indicating that interaction cost is low and navigation
is predictable. The near­absence of critical errors suggests that default choices
and page layouts prevent slips, while the only weaker spot (the note taking step)
points to the need for more guidance or lighter­weight text input/ interaction model.
The guided breathing task took longer by design, which confirms that the duration
was not governed by interface friction.
• Overall usability. System level usability met the commonly used benchmark for
this population, which implies that the screens, flows form a coherent whole suit­
able for everyday use. Variation among participants hints at improving opportu­
nities, primarily around copy clarity and some micro flow, rather than structural
changes to navigation.
• Perception of gentle support and Maps. Participants agreed above neutral that
the gentle tone is acceptable and that Maps communicate status at a glance. This
indicates that the wording, color encodings, and summaries are read quickly and
without judgement, and that the prompts nudge reflection without pressure. The
qualitative comments and task behavior align with this: people found the tone sup­
portive rather than discouraging, and used CTAs to move to breathing or recording
when appropriate.
• Short­term experience. Over seven days most participants showed a small up­
ward movement in affect balance. This pattern is consistent with the intended role
of the prototype: as a light, non­clinical scaffold for reflection and emotion regula­
tion, while remaining strictly formative and not evidential of therapeutic effect.
Across the study, routine interactions were reliable and quick, indicating that low­burden
logging was achieved. Perceived usability met the commonly accepted benchmark for
our target context. Participants reported that the gentle tone and maps mechanism
were easy to accept and to read, and task behavior was consistent with this impression.
Over the seven­day use period, affect balance tended to shift slightly upward for most
participants.
Taken together, the prototype supports everyday reflection with low effort and non­
judgmental prompts while keeping feedback glanceable. The observed change in affect
is formative and should not be interpreted as a clinical effect.

7.2
Research Question
Low­burden input and everyday usability. The evaluation shows that routine ac­
tions, recording a mood, adding a note, viewing Maps, starting a breathing session and
saving an affirmation. All of them were completed reliably and quickly (see Chapter6.5).
Critical errors were rare and localized to the optional note recording step. This pattern in­
dicates that gesture input(slide, select, etc), defaults, and progressive information keep
the interaction cost low. The note taking part would benefit from easier entry points or
lighter text capture/input model(e.g. direct selection from multiple scenarios). Overall,
the prototype meets the practical requirement that logging must fit into brief, everyday
moments.
What forms of “gentle support” are acceptable. Participants reported that the tone
of the system is non­judgmental and acceptable and that the Maps view communicates
the status at a glance (Chapter 6.5). SUS scores met the commonly used benchmark
(Chapter6.5), which supports the claim that the guidance, copy, and visual encodings
are not burdensome. Together, these results answer the design question about tone:
supportive wording, soft prompts, and assets such as affirmations can be used without
producing reactance.
How tracking leads to reflection and action.
The combination of quick logging,
glanceable summaries (Maps/Insights), and breathing guidance produced the intended
loop of notice/collect →reflect →regulate/action. Task behavior showed that people
could move from Insight to breathing or back to recording without confusion, and most
participants showed a small positive shift in affect balance over seven days (Chapter
6.5). This provides evidence for short­term reflection mechanism.
Answer to the overarching question. A local­first, lightweight app can support non­
clinical users by (i) enabling fast, reliable mood entry; (ii) presenting state in a glance­
able, non­judgmental way; (iii) offering gentle prompts that channel reflection into brief
regulation exercises. In this pilot, the approach was usable and acceptable for the tar­
get group and was associated with a small short­term improvement in affect balance.
Further work should refine the note flow, test personalization of prompts and Maps, and
examine effects over longer periods with larger and more diverse samples.
7.3
Design
The study suggests several concrete directions for designing low­burden, mental­health
apps that support everyday reflection.
Keep capture process single­surface and ≤3 actions.The drag­to­select quadrant
and intensity proved quick and reliable. This design belongs to the same category as
sliders and emoji scales. They all simplify reading and decision­making steps into a
single gesture operation. Retain the drag selector and make text input optional. Ensure
success notifications and note entry points are prominently visible, supporting auto­
save, quick phrase/voice input, and undo functionality.
56
Mobile Mental Health Application for Everyday Mood Tracking and Gentle Support
==PAGE:65==
Prioritize glanceable feedback; expose precision on demand. Participants could
“read” their situation at a glance on Maps, and still drill into Detail. Maintain the week­
ly/monthly map overview with minimal numerics, consistent color/streak display, and
expose bars/tables on demand. This pattern also seen in calendar heatmaps and step
rings. The design also matches everyday check­in: overview first, details on demand
without raising cognitive load.
Support a dual­mode insight layer The two­mode design was understandable and
accepted in the test. This indicates a common split in well­being tools between a sum­
mary view for routine checks and a detail view for inspection, and allow an immediate
switch.
Sustainable habit formation without pressure.High task completion and short times
suggest the flow fits into small daily windows. Keep user­scheduled single reminders,
tolerant streaks/milestones, and small goals (“log today”, “breathe for 60 s”). This design
uses streaks as gentle cues rather than penalties.
Strengthen reliability for everyday contexts. Keep large targets, one­hand reach,
and support dynamic type/high contrast. Auto­save partial entries so interruptions do
not cost work. Vibration/Haptic feedback can confirm success without extra interaction.
Use short interventions as CTAs. Place brief at the moment of reflection that suggests
a need (e.g., after viewing a trend). Default one­tap in/one­tap out, returning the user
to the prior screen. Offer a small, stable tools (breathing exercise, quick note, stretch
exercise, etc.) with lightweight personalization (favourite, default choice). Keep the tone
supportive. This pattern generalizes across well­being and habit tools, and helps users
move from noticing to acting without leaving the moment.
7.4
Limitations
Study design and sample. The evaluation used a convenience sample (N≈10) in a
narrow age range, and only over one week without a control condition. Effects due to
novelty, demand characteristics, or regression to the mean cannot be ruled out￿which
limits external validity.
Measures and analysis. All affect outcomes were self­reports (SPANE pre/post). The
customized three Likert items were purpose­built for this prototype, and were not psy­
chometrically validated, so inferences from them should be read as formative. SUS was
analyzed descriptively; the study was not powered for inferential testing. Task comple­
tion was summarized with small­sample intervals. Near­ceiling rates reduce sensitivity
to differences.
Procedure and instrumentation. Usability tasks between Day 1 and Day 7, adherence
was encouraged but not instrumented with continuous logging by design (local­first, no
analytics). As a result, we cannot precisely quantify in­the­wild frequency or context of
use.

Conclusion
This thesis set out to bridge a practical gap in everyday mental­health support, enabling
non­clinical users to record feelings with minimal effort, and receiving non­judgemental
prompts and glanceable feedback which make reflection feasible in short moments. To
the end, we designed and implemented a lightweight, local­first mobile application that
keeps all data on device with encrypted storage; and no third­party analytics, we orga­
nized the experience around a simple loop: one­gesture capture/collect →glanceable
overview →reflect →context­sensitive gentle prompt, this process combine with pre­
cise views available on demand. The system was realized as an offline Flutter app
with layered architecture (presentation →state →services/repositories →encrypted
persistence) and evaluated in naturalistic use over one week.
The study shows that core actions: logging a mood, adding a short note, reading Map­
s/Insights, starting a one­minute breathing exercise, and saving an affirmation, all of
them were completed reliably and quickly, with very few critical errors. Perceived us­
ability exceeded a common benchmark, and the custom acceptance items indicated that
the gentle tone and at­a­glance Maps were easy to accept and to read. Over seven days,
most participants exhibited a small, coherent improvement in affect balance, consistent
with the prototype’s aim as a non­clinical intervention rather than a treatment. Together,
these findings suggest that low­burden capture paired with quiet, optional support and
clear summaries can sustain everyday reflection without adding pressure.
The work contributes (i)A working Minimum Viable Product(MVP) mobile app for every­
day mood tracking and gentle support; (ii) an interaction pattern that can be reused:
single­surface capture, overview­first feedback, and short CTAs placed next to the ev­
idence that motivates them; (iii) an evaluation recipe that combines task performance,
SUS, attitude items, and a brief pre/post affect measure to assess usability, acceptance,
and short­term experience in situ. At the same time, limits remain: the sample was small
and convenience­based, the period short, there was no control condition. These bound­
aries call for longer deployments with comparison conditions, light on­device personal­
ization of prompts and thresholds, and robustness features such as backup/migration
and cross­platform parity, and maintain a stance of privacy that underpins user trust.
In sum, this application shows that it is possible to meet the everyday bar: fast, ex­
pressive logging; calm, glanceable feedback; and kind prompts that are easy to accept.
Within that frame, users can keep a gentle rhythm of noticing, reflecting, and taking a
brief step to regulate, on their own device, and on their own terms.

Using SUS[50]
The SU scale(Fig B.2) is generally used after the respondent has had an opportunity to
use the system being evaluated, but before any debriefing or discussion takes place.
Respondents should be asked to record their immediate response to each item, rather
than thinking about items for a long time.
All items should be checked. If a respondent feels that they cannot respond to a partic­
ular item, they should mark the centre point of the scale.
Scoring SUS
SUS yields a single number representing a composite measure of the overall usability
of the system being studied. Note that scores for individual items are not meaningful on
their own.

To calculate the SUS score, first sum the score contributions from each item. Each item’s

scale position. Multiply the sum of the scores by 2.5 to obtain the overall value of SU.
SUS scores have a range of 0 to 100.
