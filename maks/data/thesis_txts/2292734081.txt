Abstract 
Concrete is a widely used and essential building material. One of the major durability problems 
that concrete structures experience is the deterioration processes caused by chloride ions. The 
chloride ions break the passive layer around reinforcement allowing corrosion to occur. Chloride 
ions are found in de-icing salts, which together with water proves to be a big challenge for the 
service life of concrete structures. Normally in Denmark, an increased concrete cover would be 
used to extend the service life. It is known that structures constructed during autumn and winter 
have reduced service life compared to structures constructed in the spring and summer mainly 
due to their low maturity when exposed to de-icing salts, i.e. chloride ions. This Master’s Thesis 
seek to investigate this problem and seek a new durability strategy. This strategy consists in 
applying hydrophobic impregnation on low-maturity concrete to enhance the chloride- and water 
resistance on short-term basis. First, an experimental test setup was designed in order to simulate 
a splash-zone by utilising wetting-drying cyclic exposure. Five similar specimens with different 
pre-conditions was tested in the test setup. The conditions corresponds to four different maturities 
and being covered or not during initial curing. During the course of the exposure, certain tests 
was made to verify the progress. The four major tests conducted was measuring impregnation 
depth and consumption of the hydrophobic impregnation, determining the water absorption 
coefficient by capillary suction, measuring the chloride front every other week during exposure 
and determine the chloride profiles. The results showed that the covering of the concrete is 
essential to avoid a dry and poor surface. This affected the results more than the maturity. For 
the covered concrete samples, the results showed that there is a huge benefit by applying 
hydrophobic impregnation for mature concrete. The less mature concrete showed a distinct, but 
not significant benefit and much less than the mature concrete.

Resume 
Beton er et bredt anvendt og væsentlig byggemateriale. Et af de store holdbarhedsproblemer, 
betonkonstruktioner oplever er nedbrydelsesprocesser forårsaget af kloridioner. Kloridionerne det 
bryder passive lag omkring armeringen hvorved korrosion kan forekomme. Kloridioner findes i 
tøsalte, som sammen med vand er en stor udfordring for levetiden af betonkonstruktioner. Normalt 
i Danmark vil en øget dæklag anvendes til at forlænge levetiden. Det vides at konstruktioner 
opført i løbet af efteråret og vinteren har reduceret levetid i forhold til konstruktioner, der er 
bygget i foråret og sommeren hovedsageligt af den grund at de har lav modenhed, når de udsættes 
for tøsalte, dvs. kloridioner. Dette kandidatspeciale ønsker at undersøge dette problem og 
bestemme en ny holdbarhedsstrategi. Denne strategi består i at anvende hydrofob imprægnering 
på beton med lav modenhed for at forbedre klorid- og vandmodstanden på kort sigt. Først blev 
en eksperimentel forsøgsopstilling designet med henblik på at simulere en splash-zone ved at 
udnytte væde-tørre cyklisk eksponering. Fem ens prøver med forskellige forsøgsparametre blev 
testet i forsøgsopstillingen. Betingelserne svarer til fire forskellige modenheder og om hvorvidt 
prøven var afdækket eller ej under den indledende hærdning. De fire hovedforsøg var at måle 
imprægneringsdybden og -forbruget af den hydrofobe imprægnering, bestemme vandet 
absorptionskoefficienten ved kapillarsugning, måle kloridfronten hver anden uge under 
eksponering og bestemme klorid-profiler. Resultaterne viste, at afdækningen af betonen er vigtig 
for at undgå en tør og dårlig overfladekvalitet. Dette påvirkede resultatet mere end modenheden. 
For de afdækkede prøver, viste resultaterne, at der er en enorm fordel ved at anvende hydrofob 
imprægnering på en moden beton. De mindre modne betonprøver viste en tydelig, men ikke 
signifikant fordel og meget mindre end den modne betonprøve.

1 Introduction 
Concrete is an essential building material. It has long been considered efficient and cost-effective 
[1]. Despite being very economical durable, 40 % of the construction industry’s resources goes to 
repair and maintenance in industrially developed countries [2]. This makes the durability and 
maintenance perspective particular relevant. 
One of the main durability-problems is water. Water acts as mean of transportation for harmful 
ions and it is a source of chemical degradation. Acidic water is particular harmful since concrete 
is alkaline. Water can be found in many forms such as seawater, groundwater, in snow, ice, vapour 
and rain. It is a good solvent and the molecules are very small [2], [3].  
A particular problem is the chloride ingress in concrete. The main concern is that the chloride 
ions destroys the passive layer on reinforcement steel and acts as catalysts to the corrosion process 
of reinforcement [4]. This will reduce the service life of a structure. In Denmark, this is handled 
by using a relatively large concrete cover and high performance concrete. Even high performance 
concrete with a water-cement ratio of 0.30 has been found to absorb chlorides [5]. And the cover 
only provides an effect for a short period of time [6]. Concrete structures constructed in the 
autumn and winter in Denmark will have a shorter service life since they are exposed to de-icing 
salts and thereby chlorides earlier than structures constructed in the spring or summer. 
Rarely, only one deterioration problem occurs more often multiple problems occurs simultane-
ously or as a consequent of each other [2]. In laboratory circumstances, it is easier to isolate and 
study one phenomenon at a time. 
Since chloride ions are found dissolved water, one strategy to try to prevent the chloride ingress 
could be to make the concrete surface hydrophobic. This can be done by e.g. applying a hydro-
phobic impregnation on the concrete surface.  
Hydrophobic impregnation silanes have been used for several years. In 1986, the United King-
dom started using a hydrophobic agent on bridges to avoid chloride ingress. The use of hydropho-
bic impregnations is not limited to the United Kingdom alone as the Department of Transporta-
tion in Germany and different agencies in the United States also have been using these agents on 
bridges with the same goal [7]. 
Using silane-based, hydrophobicity-inducing surface protection systems, the damage done by 
harmful substances such as chlorides or water can be reduced [8], [9]. 
==PAGE:15==
| 4 
 
Longer-term efficiency of hydrophobic impregnation is unlikely on young concrete as the hy-
dration process has not concluded and the concrete must have an age of at least 28 days [7]. This 
begs the question whether short-term efficiency viable for young concrete and/or concrete struc-
tures constructed in the autumn or winter. The desired short-term efficiency would in this case 
be the upcoming winter-season. 
If this is the case, a new strategy can developed: by treating young concrete structures con-
structed in the autumn or winter with a hydrophobic agent in hopes of reducing the chloride 
ingress and thereby extending the service life of the structure.  
This strategy would be relevant for parts of the structure that is exposed to chloride ions 
typically in splash-zones such as edge beams on bridges, the bottom part of columns in a parking 
garage or even structures tidal zones or just close to the sea where the air contains a lot of 
vaporised salt-water [10].  
1.1 Objective 
The objective with this project is to investigate the effect of hydrophobic agent applied on concrete 
with low maturity in order to observe the effect on the water intake and the chloride ingress to 
determine a viable durability strategy and in which cases the strategy is feasible. 
Planning and execution of experiments is a large part of this project. A suitable test setup 
must be designed along with appropriate concrete specimen.  
Two main tests should carried out, one measuring the water absorption properties and one 
measuring the chloride ingress. The first allows for interpreting the effect of hydrophobic treat-
ment on the water absorption properties of the concrete. The second allows for interpreting 
whether the investigated strategy is viable and whether the water absorption properties affects 
the chloride ingress. The specimen should be exposed to cyclic expose since it depicts the occur-
rence in a splash-zone.  
Literature investigating concrete exposed to chlorides in general are plentiful but less so when 
the exposure is cyclic. The knowledge on cyclic exposure is still limited although some studies 
exists [1], [11]–[15]. When considering the pool of literature that concerns water absorption and 
hydrophobic impregnation the pool becomes significantly smaller when the focus is on young 
concrete although some exists e.g. [16]. While several articles exists studying the significance of 
the hydrophobic impregnation effect [9], [15], [17]–[19], most of them consider the water-cement 
ratio or different hydrophobic agents. The age or maturity often seem to be constant parameter 
and the samples are very mature. This leaves plenty of room for this project to explore. 
The nomenclature can be found as the last page in the report.

2 Theory 
2.1 Maintenance and Life Time of Structures 
A structure is considered durable as long as its properties remain conserved. Once deteriorated, 
the structure may be considered unsafe or may simply be unable to satisfy its use and is said to 
have reached the end of its lifetime. According to Tuutti [20], the degree of damage can be divided 
into two parts: initiation and propagation; cf. figure 1.  
 
Figure 1: Definition of service life. Courtesy of Marianne T. Hasholt from. 
Figure 1 shows the age of the structure on the abscissa and the degree of damage on the 
ordinate. The initiation period can occur over a long period of time and is relatively unknown 
since the damage is mostly clearly visible when the propagation stage takes over. When the 
amount of damage exceeds the acceptance limit, the structure is rendered useless or is no longer 
safe to use and that period can be described as the technical service life. While this is technical 
correct, it also leaves little safety margin so the service life should rather be defined as the initia-
tion period. That leaves time for repairs, maintenance and re-evaluation of the structure.  
==PAGE:17==
| 6 
 
According to Mehta and Monteiro [2], it is estimated that, in industrially developed countries, 
repair and maintenance takes up 40% of the resources in the construction industry. This only 
emphasises that making durable and sustainable structures is not only ecological but also of eco-
nomic interest. Making a durable structure may allow the structure to reach its expected lifetime 
and maintenance measures could potentially be avoided. A durable concrete can be defined as a 
concrete that maintain its properties in a desired period [21].  
There are three main considerations regarding the lifetime of concrete structures: water, poor 
concrete quality and deleterious chemicals. Not every durability aspect of concrete can be related 
to these three parameters, but the majority of problems involves water [2]. 
Water acts as conveyer of aggressive ions such as chlorides and as a source of chemical degra-
dation process. This makes water potentially dangerous to concrete structures on a longer-term 
basis. Water molecules are very small making them able to ingress into smaller pores. Water is 
also an excellent solvent, which means that it can contain a large variety and amount of dissolved 
ions. Furthermore, acidic or slightly acidic water proves more harmful since concrete is an alkaline 
material. In nature, water can be found many in different varieties, e.g. seawater, groundwater, 
snow, ice, vapour, rain, etc. [2]. Water is also relevant for carbonation, freeze-thaw durability and 
it is the key ingredient for oxidation reaction of steel [1]. 
Identifying the deterioration phenomenon on a real-life structure with a durability problem 
can be tricky since often multiple phenomena occur at the same time or follow each other [21]. 
For example, a concrete develops cracks during freezing and thawing. It is through these cracks 
chloride penetration occurs, and corrosion of the reinforcement could follow. The problem in this 
case is not only the freeze-thaw or the chloride penetration but both phenomena, although it 
started with freeze-thaw damage. This shows that one should take care when classifying durability 
problems and limiting them to one specific case. In laboratory conditions, different deterioration 
phenomena can be studied under more controlled conditions and it is therefore easier to create an 
isolated instance of one problem. 
Mehta and Monteiro [2] divides causes of deterioration into physical and chemical causes. This 
may prove excellent on a theoretical level, but when it comes to classification, the problem may 
not fit into one single box, as Mehta and Monteiro states: “It should be emphasized again that 
the distinction between the physical and chemical causes of deterioration is purely arbitrary; in 
practice, the two are frequently superimposed on each other.” 
DS/EN 1504-9 uses another classification system, which is shown in figure 2. Here, chlorides 
can be found as an aggressive agent under chemical causes of deterioration of concrete and as a 
corrosive contaminant with respect to reinforcement corrosion. 
==PAGE:18==

Figure 2: Common causes of deterioration from DS/EN 1504-9. 
2.1.1 Preventive Measures 
When deciding which maintenance strategies to use it is important to analyse the environment in 
which the structure is built, in order to apply the appropriate measure. Making preventive 
measures is preliminary maintenance to ensure further durability and to avoid the need for repair 
later in the structure’s lifetime. To address chloride penetration, many strategies exist to avoid 
ingress and corrosion, such as, choosing a dense or less permeable concrete, use epoxy-coated 
reinforcement bars, increase the depth of the concrete cover, use cathodic protection, apply pro-
tective sealers or membranes or use a hydrophobic impregnation, just to mention a few [14]. None 
of these strategies are exact science and the usefulness of epoxy-coated reinforcement bars towards 
corrosion has been doubted [22]. 
One should refer to DS/EN 1504-9 for a full table of methods and principles for protection and 
repair of concrete structures. The preventive measures take place in the first stages of the con-
crete’s lifetime. It should be noted that surface protection systems, such as hydrophobic impreg-
nation, must be reapplied later in the lifespan of the structure since it is prone to erode away or 
deteriorate due to UV-radiation or other harsh weather effects. Surface protection systems are 
discussed in more detail in section 2.6.

2.1.2  Repair and Maintenance 
It should be mentioned that repairs not only concern existing structures but also new structures, 
where failures occurred either in the design phase or during construction. The appropriate repair 
method for the relevant cause can be found in the European norm DS/EN 1504 and in order to 
use the norm properly the primary cause must be known, which, as mentioned earlier, may not 
be straightforward. One should never underestimate the execution time and cost of repair works, 
as the latter tend to increase exponentially with number of repairs needed; say the first repair 
costs one unit of money, then the second may cost five units, the third will cost fifteen units and 
so on [23].  
2.2 Chloride Ingress 
As previously stated, chloride ingress in concrete is unwanted and is a major durability problem. 
The primary sources for chlorides is de-icing salts and marine air for marine structures in partic-
ular [17]. De-icing salts also further affects the damage caused by freeze-thaw deterioration [21].  
Chloride ingress due to capillary suction or diffusion is one of the major causes of corrosion of 
reinforcement [17]. The porosity of the concrete is a critical factor [21]. That makes all parameters 
that affect the porosity relevant, such as water-cement ratio, maturity i.e. hydration, etc. Even 
high performance concrete with a water-cement ratio of 0.30 has been found to absorb chlorides 
[5]. 
Reinforcement in concrete stays in the protected area as long as it stays passive. This is partly 
ensured by the alkalinity in the concrete [21]. De-passivation is occurs in the presence of chloride 
ions, carbon dioxide and sulphur oxides [1]. The reason that chloride ions are able to break the 
passive layer is due their ability to become polarised [21]. Once corrosion has started it accelerates 
quickly and the residual life time of the structure is estimated to be short i.e. 2-5 years [5]. 
The transport mechanisms relevant for chloride ingress are listed in the section below. 
2.3 Transport Mechanisms 
There are many transport mechanisms in play when discussing fluid ingress in concrete for liquids 
as well as vapours, but with respect to chloride ingress there are six governing mechanisms: Ab-
sorption, diffusion, permeation, migration, chloride binding and wicking [5]. These are briefly 
explained below [5], [14], [24], [25]: 
1. Absorption/capillary suction: The absorption in concrete takes place through capillary 
suction as an unsaturated surface will draw and absorb the liquid into the pores 
through capillary surface tension.  
2. Diffusion: The movement of fluids driven by a difference in concentration in a satu-
rated concrete is called diffusion. Seeking equilibrium, chloride ions in a solution will 
move from higher concentrations towards lower concentrations, which usually is in the 
concrete.  
3. Permeation: A hydraulic pressure difference resulting in fluid transport is called per-
meation.  
4. Migration: Migration is the transport of ions due to difference in electrical potential.  
==PAGE:20==
9 | 
5. Chloride binding: Chloride binding is a binding of chloride ions during hydration in 
cement paste where the chloride ions interact with the pore solution forming Friedel’s 
salt. 
6. Wicking: Convection of chloride solution can occur due to a wicking action when a 
concrete surface is exposed to unsaturated air. The pore water near the surface will 
evaporate and pre-crystallisation of chlorides occurs. This will then lead to increased 
absorption. 
Every transport mechanism depends on the fluid/liquid considered and the concrete properties. 
Since the fluid transport in concrete takes place in the pores, every parameter affecting the pores 
in the concrete also affects the transport mechanism, such as water/cement-ratio, mix design, 
hydration, etc. Other factors such as exposure time, workmanship, chloride concentration, etc. are 
also relevant. Capillary action can be treated as a special case of permeation.  
For non-saturated concrete in general, absorption through capillary suction is often a very 
dominant mechanism due to the porous nature of concrete. When exposing concrete to cyclic 
wetting and drying, the relevant transport mechanisms is a mix of diffusion and capillary suction. 
First capillary suction and once the concrete is sufficient saturated, diffusion will take over [14], 
[25].  
2.3.1 Absorption 
Absorption, also referred to as sorptivity or surface absorption in literature such as [5], through 
capillary suction is, as stated in the beginning of section 2.3, the transport of a liquid into the 
pores and capillaries through capillary surface tension. The rate of absorption is therefore affected 
by the properties of the pores, their structure such as radius, tortuosity, continuity, distribution 
and the surface energy of the concrete [25]. The role of surface energy is discussed in section 2.6.2 
hydrophobic effect. 
When the surface of a dry, porous material, such as concrete, is brought into contact with 
liquid water, it will absorb the water through capillary suction into the pores and the capillaries 
[26]. The ingress can be measured to 5-15 mm in a few hours or days if the concrete is not 
saturated [5]. The surface of the fluid in the capillaries, called a meniscus, will always be concave. 
It is characterised by the radius of its curvature and the radius of the meniscus itself. The radius 
of the pore is often assumed to be equal – or approximately equal – to the radius of the meniscus. 
The surface tension in the water has an influence on the formation of the meniscus [26]. 
Generally speaking, the rate of absorption (AR) or sorptivity is a function of time, or square 
root of time, indicating the transport properties of the cover of the concrete related to the ability 
to absorb water in the capillaries [14]. The AR can be described as shown in equation (1):

2.5 Cyclic Wetting and Drying 
Cyclic wetting and drying consists of a continuous cycle of fixed periods of wetting and drying of 
a sample. The wetting and drying cycle causes moisture movement through the concrete and 
accelerates durability problems if simultaneous exposed to harmful substances e.g. chlorides [14].   
 
Cyclic wetting and drying is a well-known phenomenon in real life and for reinforced concrete 
structures exposed to chloride it can be a problem. The most common locations this phenomenon 
is observed are: 
1. Highway bridges or raised roadways 
2. Columns in parking garages 
3. Marine environments 
In marine and coastal environments, some parts of a structure may be partly covered in salt-
water due to tidal waves or just being located in the splash zone. Highway bridges and the lower 
part of columns in parking garages may be exposed to chloride ions through de-icing salt. When 
cars are driving over the bridge or through the parking garage the de-icing salt and possibly slush 
if present may splash onto the structure.  
When a dry or mostly dry concrete is exposed to salt-water it absorbs the water into the pores 
by capillary suction as explained in section 2.3.1. The concrete will suck up all the water or until 
it is fully saturated. If there is no more water around the concrete the pure water in the concrete 
starts to evaporate, but the salt mainly stays in the concrete pores. If the concrete then is exposed 
to more salt-water, it will absorb the salt-water as before and pushing the already existing salt in 
the concrete further into the concrete. This is the general concept of wetting and drying cycles 
[14].  
If the concrete remained exposed to water, meaning no dry period, the transportation would 
be through diffusion. Therefore, the dry period is key to this method and the combination of 
wetting and drying is an important parameter. With a short wet period, the salt is carried into 
the concrete and the concentration is increased during the dry period. The mechanism of this 
method can be said to be absorption of salt-water through capillary suction, diffusion and the 
increasing concentration with evaporation of water [14].  
As mentioned before the dry period is very important and the parameters concerning the dry 
period is also very important with regards to the chloride ingress such as the duration, degree of 
drying and sequence of wetting and drying. With a long drying period, the concrete dries to a 
greater depth allowing it absorb more water in the following wetting period resulting in a greater 
concentration of chloride ions. The rate of drying depends on the concrete itself and the moisture 
content, thus the relative humidity. The rate of drying is affected by the microstructure of the 
concrete such as pore size, pore size distribution, pore continuity and tortuosity [14].  
==PAGE:27==
| 16 
 
According to Hong and Hooton in [11] an increased drying period in respect to the wet period 
increases the sorption. This is partly confirmed by Zhang et al. in [13] that also showed that 
choosing longer drying period is not always equal to greater penetration depth.  Gang et al. [12] 
concluded that the chloride ion transport was accelerated if the drying period was increased in 
respect to the wetting period until reaching the ratio of 5:1, where it became stable.  
Basheer et. al. lists different assessment methods in [32] found in literature for testing chloride 
ingress of surface treated concrete. Whereas most methods are variants of immersion and diffusion 
tests the cyclic wetting and drying is used, c.f. [1], [11], [12], [15], since it “assesses the weather-
ability of the surface treatments” [1] and accelerates durability problems [14]. It can therefore also 
be used in investigate aging properties [33] 
2.6 Hydrophobic Surface Protection 
2.6.1 Surface Protection Systems 
When dealing with surface protection systems, DS/EN 1504-2 defines three types of protection 
within this category: Hydrophobic impregnation, impregnation and coating. These types are 
shown in figure 4 below.  
 
Figure 4: Three different classification of surface protection systems [34]. 
According to DS/EN 1504-2, the coating treatment produces a thick, continuous layer on the 
surface. This type of treatment is often the most effective of the three, but it also alters the 
concretes appearance [35], which may be unwanted due to architectural or other reasons.  
The standard impregnation is used to “reduce the surface porosity and strengthen the surface. 
The pores and capillaries are partially or totally filled.” [35]. This technique usually results in a 
thin film on the concrete cover although not as thick and noticeable as the coating. 
The last type is the hydrophobic impregnation. This impregnation type has the main purpose 
of making the concrete surface water-repellent though DS/EN 1504-9 states, that hydrophobic 
impregnation also can be used for moisture control and increasing resistivity besides just protect-
ing against ingress. The pores and capillaries are only coated internally, but they are not filled, 
so no film is formed [35]. Although not as effective as the other two [35], there is no visually 
alteration of the concrete surface  and this impregnation type is permeable allowing water vapour 
diffusion from the concrete surface to the surroundings [7]. The latter is important in order to 
avoid damage due to water pressure build-up [15] and to gain an efficient cyclic wetting and 
drying exposure. The active chemical compounds are often silane, siloxane and for non-concrete 
and –mortar substrates also siliconate [35].  
DS/EN 1504-2 also describes the main (physical) factors for the performance of the hydropho-
bic impregnation to be depth of penetration, drying rate, water absorption and resistance to 
alkaline environment [35]. Vries and Polder showed in [15] that the penetration depth is a very 
important parameter, especially when it comes to denser concretes. When discussing performance

2.6.2 Hydrophobic Effect 
When liquid water is spread out on a surface and droplets do not form, the surface is said to be 
hydrophilic. If droplets form, then the surface is said to be hydrophobic [36]. For a water-repellent 
surface, the term ’hydrophobic’ describes the repulsive interaction of the surface boundary layer 
with liquid water and/or water vapour [36]. If the water viscosity and the pore structure is con-
stant, then only the contact angle between water and substrate is changed [7]. The contact angle 
cab be determined from the free surface energies. A difference in surface energies between the 
substrate surface and the liquid water creates the hydrophobic effect. Since liquid water is a fluid, 
the surface free energy is a synonym for the surface tension [36], [37].  
In terms of water droplet, forming it is a question of whether it is more favourable to build up 
the droplet or make it spread out on the surface. For a hydrophobic surface, the surface energy is 
so large that it takes less energy to build up the droplet than to disperse it. The concept is shown 
on figure 5a, where the arrows indicate the movement of the droplet, and b. For concrete, this 
action is directly related to the surface chemistry and the pores [36]–[38].  
 
Figure 5: Water droplet on solid substrate surface. a) Low difference in surface energies makes the droplet 
disperse over the surface. b) High surface tension preserves the droplet. 
If the contact angle θ is larger than 90°, then the surface is classified as hydrophobic [39]. If 
the contact angle is smaller than 30°, the surface is hydrophilic. In the interval between 30º and 
90º, the surface is said to be non-hydrophobic [39]. The contact angle related to the droplet 
formation on the solid surface is shown on figure 6. Normally, the contact angle cannot be larger 
than 120°, although if dealing with a special substrate, it may be higher [36].  
 
Figure 6: Water droplet on solid substrate. a) Low contact angle and non-hydrophobic surface b) High con-
tact angle and hydrophobic surface.  
==PAGE:29==

A contact angle below 90° between water droplet and substrate surface indicates that the 
molecular forces – van der Waals forces, dipole interactions and hydrogen bonding – between 
substrate surface and the water droplet are stronger than the surface tension. As a result, the 
water droplet will be absorbed into the surface. A contact angle above 90° indicates smaller at-
traction between water droplet and substrate, and the droplet will remain on the surface due to 
the surface tension i.e. the hydrophobic effect [7], [36].  
Concrete is a hydrophilic material due to its porous structure. Concrete absorbs the water into 
the pores due to capillary forces that are determined by the surface tension, the contact angle and 
pore radius [15]. The surface energy is high if numerous pores are present [2]. Through use of 
silane-based products, one can control the hydrophobic behaviour of the substrate1. A wide range 
of literature shows that hydrophobic impregnations with a silane agent have a significant effect 
on the water absorption, e.g. [4], [7], [16], [40]–[45]. However, some use capillary tests (e.g. [4], [7], 
[9], [41], [46]) and some uses full immersion tests ([7], [41], [47], [48]); the results are in general 
conclusive.

3 Materials and Methods 
3.1 Overview of Experiments 
The practical part of this project revolves around a cyclic salt-water exposure of concrete speci-
mens inspired by NT Build 489 [50] and different tests both before, during and after exposure. 
First, the hydrophobic impregnation depth will be measured followed by a water absorption test. 
During the exposure, the chloride front will be measured and lastly, a chloride profile will be 
determined.  
Specimens, which have been hydrophobic impregnated with the hydrophobic agent, will be 
referred to as treated specimens.  
Beside the tests mentioned above the temperature at the test setup is also registered along 
with a check of the salt concentration in the exposure solution.  
3.1.1 Objectives 
The purpose with every major test performed is explained here. The procedure of each experiment 
is described later on.  
Impregnation depth: The objective with testing the hydrophobic impregnation depth is to 
verify the presence of the hydrophobic agent. On top of that, it is a measurement tool to compare 
the effect of the different concrete samples concerning their ability to absorb hydrophobic agent 
and if possible their water repelling performance.  
Water absorption: The objective with water absorption test is to investigate the capillary 
suction of the different samples and the hydrophobic agent’s effect on the capillary suction. This 
only seems fitting since the capillary suction is the primary transport mechanism.  
Chloride front: The objective with measuring the chloride front is to observe the chloride 
ingress at different moments making sure the exposure is effective. The observations also serve as 
a cornerstone for determining the step size of the chloride profile. 
Chloride profile: The objective with measuring the chloride profile is to get the full picture 
of the chloride ingress of treated and untreated specimens. The goal is to estimate an equivalent 
==PAGE:35==
| 24 
 
thickness of concrete cover and judge whether or not applying hydrophobic impregnation on young 
concrete has the desired effect.

3.2.2 Casting Procedure 
Due to the large amount of concrete to be cast, the casting is divided into two batches. Batch 1 
consists of specimens 1, 3 and 5 and batch 2 consists of specimen 2 and 4. The casting of batch 1 
and 2 is done one week apart.  
Before mixing and casting the concrete, the sand and the aggregates moisture content most be 
determined in order to get the correct w/c ratio for the mix. This is done by taking a known 
amount of aggregates and sand and microwaving them for 30 minutes. The change in mass corre-
sponds to the moisture content that has to be accounted for. 
After the moisture correction, the mixing can commence. The ingredients are added to the 
concrete mixer in the following order: half of the sand, coarse aggregates, cement and rest of the 
sand. The components are then mixed without water for two minutes. After two minutes, the 
water is added and the mixer then runs for five minutes. After five minutes the mixer is stopped 
and if the concrete mix is evenly distributed and approximately homogenous, a slump test is 
carried out. The slump goal for this concrete is 100 mm. If the slump test is passed, the concrete 
is poured into the moulds. If the slump test is not passed the concrete mix is adjusted using 
Popovic’s method [21] as shown equation (15) below.

3.3 Hydrophobic Impregnation with MasterProtect H 303 
The hydrophobic impregnation agent is applied 24 hours after removing the plastic. The point of 
waiting 24 hours is to ensure the outer pores in the concrete is not filled with water. If that were 
the case, the hydrophobic agent would only have a small, if any, effect. The appliance is carried 
out outside in an enclosure, which means that the temperature and the humidity deviates from 
the determined conditions. This is a known source of error and is taken into account when con-
sidering the maturity of the specimens. 
The appliance was carried out with a water vaporiser and by spraying three consequential 
times. After an appliance, the surface should be almost dry before applying again. The wait 
between the first and second application usually took around 5-10 minutes and the wait between 
the second and the third application usually lasted around 15 minutes. When the surface is dry, 
typically 20 minutes after the third application, the specimen was returned to storing.  
During application, a tray is placed underneath the sample in order to catch the spill that runs 
off the sample. This way, the consumption of hydrophobic agent can be determined also account-
ing for the spillage. The application setup is shown in figure 11.  
 
Figure 11: Hydrophobic impregnation application setup.

After application, the spill in the tray was poured back into the water vaporiser and the total 
remaining hydrophobic impregnation agent was weighed. The difference in weight makes up the 
estimated consumption. 
The specimens were allowed to rest for a week. This means that the age of the young specimens, 
specimen 2, 3 and 4, is actually closer to 14 days than 7 days. If the exposure was started imme-
diately or 24 hours after impregnation, the hydrophobic agent would have been washed off and 
no effect would have been measured. This is also the reason behind choosing a slow-reacting 
cement type and thereby retarding the maturity of the concrete a little bit. In no way does it 
compensate for the seven days of rest, but it is a way of slowing the hydration process down a 
while. Nevertheless, these specimens will still be mentioned as ‘the young specimens’ or as having 
an age of 7 days. 
Due to the forces of gravity, it is expected that the amount of hydrophobic impregnation is 
greater towards the bottom than the top, although the application done thrice should minimise 
this. It was also noted that hydrophobic agent was accumulated in pinholes in the surface.

BASF. The product will be denoted MPH303 for short. MPH303 is a water based alkyl alkoxy 
silane hydrophobic impregnation product containing 20 weight-% dry matter of the active sub-

3.5 Cyclic Exposure 
The exposure procedure is based on NT Build 489 [50] since this testing method is suited for 
testing paint systems’ effect on chloride ingress in concrete. In addition, standardised experimental 
procedures usually generates results that are easy to relate to or compare with other standards. 
To suit this project better, some alteration are made. The test setup along with the exposure for 
this project is discussed in the sections below.  
3.5.1 Test Setup Design 
A lot of time went into the discussing and designing the test setup. The inspiration for the setup 
was a setup similar to the example shown in the NT Build 489 Annex A [50] and the ideal setup 
would be a two-chamber system with electrical controls like the one shown on figure 2 in [13]. 
The basic idea is that the samples are placed in an empty tub. The tub is then filled with salt-
water and after a certain period of time the water is removed. After some time with no water, the 
tub is filled again and the cycle continues. In this case, it is convenient to store the salt-water in 
another nearby tub and the transportation of water happens through a pumping device. Evapo-
ration of water should be avoided as much as possible. In case evaporation happens the water 
should be easy accessible. When the tub with the specimen is empty of water, a certain humidity 
should be ensured. In practice, this means that the tub must be ventilated or dried somehow.  
The basic idea then forms the demands for a proposition to a test setup. These demands can 
be further specified and are listed below: 
- 
Ability to fill and empty tub of water in a certain timeframe 
- 
Ability to control when the filling and emptying takes place (cycle duration) 
- 
Ensure no – or minimal – evaporation of water 
- 
Ensure certain exposure conditions e.g. relative humidity 
- 
Ensure easy accessibility to the water in order to control liquid conditions 
- 
Ensure easy accessibility to specimen for sampling  
The design must then somehow satisfy these demands. The theoretical design chosen to satisfy 
these demands are sketched below in figure 13 and is based on two HS Euroboxes. 
==PAGE:41==
| 30 
 
 
Figure 13: Theoretical test setup. 
The upper tub should be filled and emptied in no more than 15 minutes according to NT Build 
489 [50]. By timing the inflow and outflow of salt-water in the upper tub it was found that the 
filling takes 11 minutes and emptying the tub takes 13 minutes. Even though the requirement 
stated above is accommodated, it is noted that the bottom half of the specimen is affected by the 
salt-water longer than the upper half. Consider a cycle of 3 hours of wetting and 9 hours of drying. 
During one cycle, which includes one filling and one outflow, the bottom of the specimen will 
experience a wet period that is approximately 24 minutes longer than top of the specimen. This 
corresponds to an increase of roughly 13 % of the wetting period and shortening of the drying 
period 4.5 %. This is taken into account when choosing the future location of the cores. All cores 
used to determine the chloride front is taken from the bottom part of a specimen. That way, all 
cores used to chloride front determination has been given the same wetting period length. This 
explains the assigned numbering of core location shown in figure 10. 
Choosing the size of the tubs, the size of one concrete specimen and not go over budget was a 
very iterative process. Once the concrete specimen size and the tub size was determined, the water 
demand and pump requirements were calculated, cf. appendix 3. The final design is shown in 
figure 14 direct from the side (left) and slightly from above (right). 
The test setup consists of two plastic tubs and a pump system. The upper tub is angled 90 
degrees in order to ensure access to the lower tub. A welded steel frame connecting the two tubs 
ensures that they do not slide or move relative to each other. A small drain is placed at the 
bottom of the lower tub for easier emptying. The tubs have a volume of 82.87 litres.  
The pump is placed on the bottom of the lower tub at the location which corresponds to 
directly beneath the centre of the upper tub. The upper tub is then connected to the pump 
through a hole in the bottom and a rubber tube. An overflow tube is installed in the top of the 
upper tub. That way, the salt-water stays in circulation and the pump can run continuously 
during the wetting period without the risk of running dry or overflowing the upper tub. The pump 
is connected to a time switch and tuned to the chosen exposure cycle.  
In order for the salt-concentration to be as constant as possible, the tubs must be closed when 
the wetting period is on to avoid evaporation. When the drying period is occurring, the upper tub

must be open to ensure the correct and constant humidity for the test specimen. To ensure this, 
the solution of the lid following the water level in the bottom tub was chosen. This should be 
understood, as when the water level in the bottom tub is low; the level of the lid should also be 
low, which would correspond to closing the upper tub. When the water level is high in the bottom 
top meaning that the drying period is present, the level of the lid should also be high.  
In practice, this is implemented by attaching the lid to two metal rods. These metal rods are 
fixed in a block of Roofmate™ floating in the bottom tub. Roofmate™ is a commercial Styrofoam 
material, which means that it is much lighter than water making it able to float and unlike 
polystyrene; it is more resistant to water. The metal rods is given thread so the height of the lid 
can be adjusted depending on the sample volume.

3.5.2 Exposure Conditions 
The exposure consists of 24 hours of pure water exposure and 6.5-7 weeks salt-water exposure. A 
24 hours exposure in pure water ensures that the concrete specimen is somewhat filled with water 
and when exposed to salt-water, will not experience a huge initial chloride ingress. The salt-water 
exposure was chosen as long as the project period allowed for.  
==PAGE:43==
| 32 
 
The salt-water concentration is chosen according to NT Build 489 to be 165 g ± 1 g per litre 
solution. The amount of salt for the solution is then determined by multiplying said concentration 
with the volume of the lower tub, cf. calculations in appendix 3.  
The lower tub has a free volume of approximately 71 litres to be filled with salt-water. The 
salt-water solution was mixed in three 25L plastic canisters. The solution was made by boiling 
roughly 1.5 litres of de-ionised water, pouring into the canister and adding a sufficient amount of 
salt. The canister is then shaken until the salt is dissolved. This is repeated until the canister is 
filled where it is placed outside (at around a temperature of 5°C) for cooling. When the solution 
is cooled down to 23°C ± 2°C it is poured into the exposure tanks and the exposure can begin. 
This is also the wanted temperature for the solution in the wetting period and the air in the 
drying period.  
The test setup is placed in a climate chamber where both the temperature and the relative 
humidity is more or less constant. The relative humidity in the air is chosen to be 65 % ± 5 % 
and the temperature is chosen to be 23°C ± 2°C due to the conditions in the climate chamber.  
Every week the salt-water concentration is checked for every test setup and adjusted properly. 
This check is described further in the following section. The temperature is registered several 
times a week.  
Since time is of the essence, the main factor when choosing the wetting-drying period (w/d-
period) is to get reliable results as fast as possible to minimise the length of the total experiment. 
NT Build 489 suggests a w/d-period of 2/4 hours. Based on the information in section 2.5 cyclic 
wetting and drying, the w/d-period was chosen to be semidiurnal cycle of 3 hours of wetting and 
9 hours drying.  
The following analogy could also be made to justify the chosen w/d-period: assume frost con-
ditions, where de-icing salts are spread in order to avoid ice on the road. In rush hour, a lot of 
people will be driving to work splashing the de-icing salt upon a nearby considered concrete 
structure. Approximately nine hours later, people will drive home for work and the salt splashing 
is repeated. This is no indication that the chosen exposure for this project resemble a life-like, 
modelled condition when interpreting results, but just to illustrate that a somewhat similar situ-
ation happens outside the laboratory.

3.7 Impregnation Depth 
DS/EN 1504-2:2005 [34] states that the testing shall be carried out according to the method 
presented in DS/EN 14630:2006 [53] using water instead of phenolphthalein. The method used is 
therefore based on DS/EN 14630:2006 although the cores were cut instead of split. Splitting the 
core requires no water, which is preferable in this case, but since water is required to drill the core 
in the first place, cutting was used to divide the core since it usually results in more evenly halves.

3.7.2 General method 
1) The core is cut along the longitudinal axis into two half cores and immediately dried with 
a cloth to absorb the water from the cutting process. 
2) The half-cores are stored in the climate chamber at 23°C and 65 % RH for 6 hours. 
3) The half-cores are then placed with the cut surface upwards and water is sprayed on 
surfaces with the water sprayer. 
4) The dry area on the surfaces are then measured with the ruler to register the penetration 
depth of the hydrophobic agent. 
5) Isolated pockets are avoided when measuring the effective depth and calculating the av-
erage penetration depth. Isolated pockets should also be measured and noted separately.  
Upon investigation, it was observed that simply spraying water on the surface just to make it 
wet, was not sufficient to clearly observe the penetration depth of the hydrophobic impregnation 
treatment. Instead, a pipette was used to wet the surface more carefully. The penetration depth 
was then clearly visible as a dry, thin ‘stripe’. Prospectively, the water sprayer in step 3) is 
substituted with a pipette. 
3.8 Water Absorption 
The method used was inspired by ISO 15148 [28] where the specimen is placed on brackets in a 
small tub with de-ionised water reaching approximately five millimetres up on the sides of the 
specimen. This method ensures one-dimensional transport since all of the relevant surface has 
water contact. At certain intervals, the specimen is weighted in order to register the mass change 
over time. The intervals are more frequent in the beginning of the experiment in order to register 
the initial linear mass increase and is less frequent towards the end of the experiment as the mass 
increase stagnates. 
The experiment is temperature dependent but does not consider a temperature gradient as 
long as the temperature stays in with the range of 18°C to 28°C and only variates with ±2°C 
during the experiment. It is also very moisture dependent so a well pre-defined moisture state for 
the test specimens is very important. It should be noted that drying in an oven pre-testing is not 
an option since it may advance the hydration of the young specimens.

5 Discussion 
5.1 Salt Concentration Check 
The ideal progress would be a constant salt concentration of 165 g/L. Since this is very hard to 
achieve an allowable interval has be given by NT Build 489 [50] suggesting a near constant salt 
concentration of 165 ± 9 g/L. The variation in salt concentration during exposure is caused by 
two main factors: evaporation and absorption of the salt-water. 
When the concrete specimen is exposed to the salt-water, it will absorb it through capillary 
suction and diffusion. Once the exposure ends and the concrete specimen enters the drying period, 
the water in the in pores will begin to evaporate but the salt in the pores will remain. This will 
cause the salt concentration of the system to increase since water is leaving the system, but as 
the water drain away, some salt residue may be found on the specimen or in the bottom of the 
upper exposure tank.  
Another take on the same event could be that the despite the evaporation of water the residue 
salt is causing the measured concentration to decrease since it remains in the upper tank. The 
water evaporation could also be significantly lower compared to salt imbibed by the specimen 
resulting in decrease in salt concentration.  
In other words, there is no exact way of knowing for sure, whether the salt concentration 
increase or decrease unless a more controlled test environment is utilised. The somewhat stable 
increase in salt concentration of 4 – 8 g/L on a weekly basis from 2 weeks of exposure to 5 weeks 
of exposure suggests that evaporation is dominating over residue salt formation.  
Furthermore, it was observed that when the upper tank was filled, it would bulge outwards 
preventing the lid from closing properly as the lower nut would be stuck on the edge of the tank 
and rest there. This created a small opening of 1 – 2 cm allowing evaporation and leaving residue 
salt and on the edge of the tank. This was prevented by gently pressing against the bulges allowing 
the lid to fall into place. Yet this could only be done in the daytime. The salt residue is shown in 
figure 34 where the lid has been pushed into place.  
==PAGE:63==
| 52 
 
 
Figure 34: Residue salt on the edge of the upper tank. 
The salinity of all test setups – with the exception of sample 4 – starts out below the allowed 
lower limit indicating that not all of salt had been fully dissolved. This entailed a correction of 
adding salt to the solution. 
After 1 week of exposure, the salt concentration for the test setup for specimen 2, 3 and 5 was 
still below the lower limit although higher than the previous week. This suggests that despite the 
added salt, the salt-water concentration was still decreasing. Most notably is the concentration 
for sample 3, which stays the same. This indicates that it ‘consumes’ more salt than both sample 
2 and 5.  
After 2 weeks of exposure sample 3 was still below the lower limit. This is a significant source 
of error, when considering the results since it could mean that sample 3 in theory could imbibe 
more salt or maybe the drying period did not have optimal moisture condition. Once again, there 
is no way of knowing for sure.  
After 3 weeks of exposure, every concentration measured was close to the optimal concentra-
tion and would stay this way until the experiment ended. A correction was only made if the 
concentration exceed the allowed limit.  
The lid height is adjusted so that the Roofmate™ follows the water level throughout the 
experiment. This means that every time a core is removed more water is required in the upper 
tank. The distance between the Roofmate™ and the lid can then be increased. This affects the 
specimen during the drying period since the level of the lid is effective increased with approx. 5 
mm every time a core is removed.  
Under more controlled circumstances, this would have been an interesting parameter to com-
pare to the chloride front or the chloride profile but it makes little to no sense to do in this case 
since one test setup holding one concrete sample contained both the treated and untreated side.

5.2 Evaluation of test setup 
The test setup, which allowed for cyclic wetting and drying tests, was successfully designed. Dur-
ing the course of the experiment, it was discovered that the setup could be improved or optimised 
in different ways. Three main points are discussed below: 
1) The concrete sample was placed in the middle of the upper tub. This means, that despite 
being placed on two shallow brackets, the pump was not efficient enough to push the salt-
water up into the upper tank within the given time-frame with the sample partly ob-
structing the flow. The sample was therefore placed offset from the centre. This is a source 
of error even though the sample was rotated every two weeks. This problem could be 
solved altering the position of the pump’s outflow in the upper tub or by using a more 
efficient pump. 
2) When the upper tub was filled, it bulge outwards to the mass of salt-water and concrete 
specimen combined. This prevented the lid from completely closing as discussed in section 
5.1. This source of error entails an unnecessary evaporation and thereby variation in salt-
concentration. This can prevented by choosing a stronger material for the exposure tub 
or installing a clamp to reduce the bulging. 
3) The lid system could also be optimised. For example, it could be controlled electronically 
instead of following the water level. Furthermore, being dependent on the water level in 
the lower tub also means that it would have to be adjusted every time a core is removed. 
The headroom between lid and upper tank does then not stay the throughout the course 
of the experiment. This is also a source of error.

5.4 Water Absorption 
The results are analysed according to ISO 15148 [28] supplemented by the notes from K. K. 
Hansen [27]. By isolating the linear part for each sample’s water absorption curve and inserting a 
linear trendline, the water absorption coefficient can be found directly as the curvature of the 
trendline as long as the correlation coefficient is greater than 0.98: r2 > 0.98 [14]. This is only the 
case if the curve pattern follows the expected curve pattern confer the one shown in figure 3. If 
the initial curve is not a straight line but a graph of some other sort, the water absorption coeffi-
cient is calculated using the amount of absorbed water after 24 hours according to ISO 15148 [28]. 
The capillary and resistance number are then determined according to notes from K. K. Hansen 
[27]. This is all examined below. The water absorption curve for sample 1 can be seen in figure 
39, the remaining are shown in appendix 7. 
 
Figure 39: Water absorption over time for sample 1. 
All curves for the untreated samples (cf. figure 27) yields a linear initial part except for sample 
3, which seems to have not only one bend after the linear part but also a bend just before the 
first hour of testing. The first 11 hours, corresponding to roughly 200 √s, of testing is shown in 
figure 40, where the initial linear part bends around 50 √s (roughly 42 minutes) and continues as 
a linear part but with a lower curvature. This could be explained by the lack of cover of the 
sample in the initial curing days. With no cover, the surface of the sample will not cure properly 
due to its dry state resulting in plastic shrinkage and a poor surface. The surface may then contain 
fine cracks. If this is the case, the water absorption will initially happen faster by the water flowing 
into the cracks and filling the surface pores quicker. 
Overall, the untreated sample 3 yields two values for the water absorption coefficient, since it 
makes no sense to make an average as it would depend on the amount of data points for each 
linear part. Upon inspecting the water absorption coefficient, the value from the first linear part 
yields a number almost 2.6 times as large as for the following linear part. With the above-men-
tioned consideration regarding the poor surface in mind, the results present from this point and 
forward is based on the second linear part, since the first linear part only lasted around 42 minutes 
and the initial linear part of the other samples lasted around 11-12 hours (approx. 200 √s).

Zhu et al. [41] showed that even with recycled aggregates the water intake can be significantly 
reduced with hydrophobic impregnation although the order of magnitude of the water absorption 
coefficients found may be caused by the fact that the aggregates are recycled.  
Aitken and Litvan [57] showed that the effect of the hydrophobic impregnation may vary a 
great deal depending on the product, which was also mentioned in section 5.3. 
Monosi and Ruello [46] tests four different hydrophobic impregnation products a concrete with 
w/c-ratio of 0.65 and one with 0.45. They find that capillary absorption coefficient for treated 
samples tested in accordance to UNI EN 13057 varies from 0.21 – 0.59 kg/(h½m2) and the reference

1) The curing temperature has no effect on the capillary number and the difference seen is 
caused by measuring inaccuracies in the water absorption experiment. 
2) The curing temperature affects concrete depending on the concrete’s age. A young concrete 
will experience a decrease in capillary number if the curing temperature is lowered to 5°C 
and a 28 days old concrete will experience an increase in capillary number. 
3) There may be an effect but the difference is caused by measuring inaccuracies in the water 
absorption experiment or an unknown difference between concrete batches.

batches may either enhance a small statistical variance or blur an actual difference in the capillary 
number.  
Point 1) seem unlikely since ISO 15148 [28] states that: “(…) if eight values of mt are measured 
each subject to a random error of ±5 % due to handling, the standard error of the slope will be 
about ±4 %.” Note that ‘mt’ is mentioned as absorbed amount of water or Q in this report. 
Assuming the measurements carried out with the random error of ±5 % and the slope therefore 
is subjected to ±4 % standard error, this does not explain the difference in capillary number. 
Additionally, the error related to determination of Qcap is still unknown although it was determined 
with great carefulness. Furthermore, around 12 measurements are used to create the trendline, 
which increases the accuracy of the random error and the correlation coefficient, R2, was 0.99 for 
specimen 1, 2 and 4, 0.98 for specimen 5 and 1.00 for specimen 3.  
No evidence supporting point 2) was found in the literature study carried out in the beginning 
of this project. For further discussion of the capillary number, more tests have to be made. Lastly, 
it should be mentioned that using the values derived before the first bend on sample 3’s water 
absorption curve enhances the capillary number with a factor of 2.2. 
This discussion above assumes no errors in prior process e.g. casting, which is a real possibility. 
The discussion can furthermore, almost directly be applied to the water absorption coefficient 
since the capillary number is a pseudo-water absorption coefficient.

5.6 Chloride Profiles 
When applying Fick’s second law of diffusion a number of assumptions must be made in order for 
it to be valid. One is that mechanism considered is diffusion hence the name. As stated earlier, in 
a cyclic exposure, both capillary absorption and diffusion is the driving mechanisms, but when 
the sample is saturated diffusion take over. 
A best-fit curve of Fick’s second law of diffusion can still be applied remembering that the 
values found are not the true values but pseudo-values. This is done accordingly to NT Build 443 
[29]. The non-linear regression is carried out in Microsoft Excel using the solver-function by min-
imising the summed squared difference between the measured values (meas.) and calculated values 
(calc.) and varying the surface concentration, the initial concentration and the diffusion coeffi-
cient. The curves can be found from figure 50 to 52. It should be noted that predictions made 
outside measurement points carry a lot of inaccuracy and the found values shall not be used for 
extrapolation [29]. When the said curves are extrapolated towards origin, it is simply to show a 
guess on a tendency that in reality may vary a great deal.  
The best-fit curve for untreated sample 1 can be seen in figure 50 to the left and the treated 
sample 1 to the right. The regression line for the untreated sample seem to follow the measure-
ments whereas it deviates a bit more for the treated. The measurement point around specimen 
depth 8 mm and the last one strike out from the tendency created by the rest of the series. Noting 
the scale of the ordinate deals in a very small order of magnitude indicates that only a very small 
error needs to be made to observe this deviation.

mised difference is quite small suggesting reliable results with respect to the measured values. The 
order of magnitude lies within 10-5 to 10-2. The greatest difference is found for the untreated sample 
5 and may be caused by the few number of measurements used in the regression. The variation 
according to NT Build 443 is 20 % on the surface concentration and 15 % on the diffusion coeffi-
cient [29]. 
The pseudo-diffusion coefficient will be interpreted as a resistance parameter against ingress 
in the given exposure conditions. Normally, the diffusion coefficient is around 0-28×10-12 m2/s [21]. 
The treated sample 1 having the lowest diffusion coefficient shows the greatest resistance in the 
order of magnitude of 10-14 m2/s whereas the untreated sample is almost a factor of 90 worse and 
10 times better than the untreated sample 4. Sample 4 and 5 is within the magnitude of 10-12 m2/s 
although the untreated samples are much closer than the treated sample. This again, suggests 
that the maturity greatly affects results, where at ‘high’ maturity (sample 1) there is much to 
gain by hydrophobic impregnation but at ‘low’ maturity (sample 5) the gain has been diminished. 
An improvement between treated and untreated in diffusion coefficient of almost 100 % for sample 
1 is reduced to roughly 60 % (sample 5) by reducing the equivalent maturity from 24 days to 15 
days. Additionally, at ‘low’ maturity the difference in diffusion coefficient is small, cf. untreated 
sample 4 and 5.

Yeih et al. [61] find that the diffusion coefficient changes rapidly with exposure time for young

further variation or inaccuracy to the values found for low-maturity samples. It also imposes that 
deviation in time of measuring could be a big source of error. 
Liu et al. [56] measure on real-life harbour structures after four years of exposure and find 
diffusion coefficients in the order of 10-13 to 10-12 m2/s for all tested hydrophobic products. They 
further conclude that despite having almost no detectable penetration depth a sufficient surface 
protection for early stages may still be present. This agrees with this project’s sample 1, which 
had the lowest penetration depth but still the best chloride inhibiting results. While this effect is 
notable for the sample 5 with lower maturity, it was not significant and the effect may only be 
viable in early stages, if at all.

impregnation penetration depth 8.2 mm.

seems plausible. There is no apparent relation between the water absorption coefficient and 
pseudo-diffusion coefficient.

6 Conclusion 
An experimental test setup that allowed for cyclic wetting-drying testing was successfully designed 
although several improvements was suggested.  
The salt concentration was measured and corrected if necessary. The general tendency was 
that the salt concentration was always increasing  
The salt concentration was within the allowed interval after 2 weeks of exposure for all samples 
except sample 3. After 3 weeks of exposure, every sample was within the allowed interval and 
would stay that way until the experiment ends.  
In the initial 2-3 weeks of exposure several samples experienced a lower salt concentration than 
the optimal. This is a significant source of error when discussing chloride front and chloride pro-
files. 
In further studies, the salt concentration in the test setup could provide an interesting sup-
porting parameter in a chloride-related analysis.  
A hydrophobic impregnation penetration depth was observed for every specimen. Some speci-
mens showed a more distinct penetration depth than others.  
Not covering the concrete accordingly to the rules laid out by the Danish National Road 
Directorate [51] greatly affects the hydrophobic impregnation penetration depth, as sample 3 
showed the largest penetration depth. The ideal sample, sample 1, showed the lowest penetration 
depth indicating a dense structure. Even though there is no distinct difference between sample 2, 
4 and 5 for this type of concrete, the difference in results suggests – that the maturing temperature, 
and thereby the maturity, affects the penetration depth a little bit more than the actual age of 
the concrete – should be ruled under a margin of error.  
A large variation in penetration depth between measurement points was observed. The greater 
the variation, the more measurement points are required to get a reliable mean value. The results 
are considered reliable but would still benefit from an increased amount of measuring points, e.g. 
like the method recommended in NT Build 492 [54]. 
The MPH303 consumption was measured but not found trustworthy to base conclusion on. 
Sample 3 consumed most agent and sample 1 consumed the least, which was also expected since 
sample 3 was in a drier state than the rest and sample 1 is the optimal, most dense concrete. 
==PAGE:85==
| 74 
 
When comparing consumption to average penetration depth not relation was found and choosing 
between the two, the consumption is the least reliable due to the several sources of error.  
Improving the impregnation depth aspect of this project could include better measuring of the 
consumption and actual penetration depth as long with using a sprayer with a pump.  
The water absorption coefficient was successfully determined for every specimen with the ex-
ception of the treated specimen 1.  
The conducted test showed that the lack of cover, which was the case for specimen 3, is a 
dominating parameter. This was also the case when considering the chloride front and impregna-
tion depth. It can be concluded, that covering the specimen to ensure optimal curing and moisture 
conditions is crucial to the quality of the outer part of concrete.  
The improvement achieved by hydrophobic impregnation varied from 71 % for sample 2 to 94 
% for sample 3 with sample 4 and 5 being in between. This does not correspond with sample 2 
having a lower maturity than sample 5 but greater than sample 4. This could be explained by 
statistical variance or a source of error.  
The amount of absorbed water was in the order of 0.74 – 0.90 kg/m2 except sample 3, which 
imbibed 2.25 kg/m2 once again showing the importance of initially coving the specimen. 
The test method was less useful for treated samples and did not provide any useful information 
after 24 hours of testing for the treated samples.  
Despite Hosoda et al. [16] recommending applying hydrophobic impregnation at early concrete 
age to achieve better water absorption properties this project shows that it is not beneficial with 
the regards to the chloride ingress. 
The chloride front was successfully determined for all specimens after 2, 4 and 6 weeks of 
exposure although the untreated specimens showed no tendencies as the difference between the 
values was small enough to be explained as a statistical deviation and/or a source of error.  
The treated samples varied a lot from specimen to specimen. Yielding the best result was 
specimen 3 explained by having the largest impregnation depth of all specimens. Despite having 
the worst impregnation depth,  the second best was sample 1, which was expected to be perform 
the best since it had be given optimal curing conditions, temperature and age ensuring a dense 
concrete. Performing worst was sample 4 closely followed by sample 2 explained by their low age. 
Sample 5 performs mediocre and 28 days of age provides a better result than for sample 2 and 4 
but the low curing temperature makes it inferior to specimen 1. 
Percentage-wise, specimen 3 and 1 improves the most compared to untreated specimens with 
an average of 84 % and 67 % respectively throughout the six weeks of exposure whereas specimen 
5 improves with roughly 43 %. Sample 2 seem to improve mediocre after 2 weeks of exposure but 
the improvement is decreased significantly to an almost negligible level over the following four 
weeks of exposure. The same pattern is observed for sample 4 only on a lower level.  
Only five of the ten desired chloride profiles was successfully determined. The found chloride 
profiles were successfully analysed with a non-linear regression constructing a best-fit curve mod-
elled after Fick’s second law of diffusion.  
The diffusion coefficients found ranged from 1.09×10-14 to 2.71×10-12 m2/s with sample 1 show-
ing the best performance. Even the untreated sample 1 beat the treated sample 5. It was found 
that maturity is an important parameter to take into account when considering chloride ingress. 
For low maturity concrete, the chloride inhibiting effect is notable on the chloride profile but 
it is not significant, particular not when compared to the sample with optimal conditions, cf. 
sample 1, which improved significantly with hydrophobic treatment. This could also explain why 
no water absorption coefficient could be found.  
==PAGE:86==
75 | 
It was also found that there is not necessary a correlation between the hydrophobic impregna-
tion depth and the effect is has on the chloride ingress. The same can be said for the water 
absorption properties, which does not seem to correlate directly chloride ingress.  
Another phenomenon not covered in this project is the role of interfacial transition zones, 
which would further drive transport. Another perspective for further study could be the role of 
cracks and frost damage. The finances and the economical aspect of the problem is also left 
untouched, which very much related to the equivalent thickness of a concrete cover compared to 
using hydrophobic agent.  
A completely different future perspective could be biological and sustainability. There is a 
growing trend to make everything more friendly towards nature and sustainable. This way of 
thinking often gives birth to ideas mixing previously non-biological solutions with biological solu-
tions. Examples could be gardens atop of high-rise buildings, atriums filled with plants, etc. Some 
biological growths like algae form humic acid, which reacts aggressively with the cement paste 
[21]. This is an opportunity to experiment with hydrophobic impregnation to avoid those situ-
aions.

MasterProtect H 303 er brugsklar; omrøres let.

Overflade-, luft- og materialetemperaturen skal være

H 303 må ikke anvendes når temperaturen forventes at 
falde til under 5°C indenfor 24 timer, eller der kan forven-

Overfladen kan være let fugtig, men for at få det bedste 
resultat anbefales det, at overfladen er tør, så der opnås 
maksimal indtrængning. 
Test et lille område af betonoverfladen inden påføring af 
enhver transparent indtrængende forsegler for at sikre 
det ønskede resultat og dækningsgrad. 
Materialet påføres med en kontrolleret nedløbning på 
max. 20 cm på lodrette flader, indtil overfladen er mættet. 
På vandrette flader anbefales det at materialet påføres 
med lavtrykssprøjte, ikke forstøver; eller det hældes ud og 
fordeles jævnt med en børste. 
 
Hærdning 
Det anbefales at overflader som er behandlet med 
MasterProtect H 303 skal hvile i mindst 4 timer for at sikre 
korrekt indtrængning. MasterProtect H 303 kan efterlade 
en forbigående fedtet overflade i flere timer efter påføring. 
Derfor må steder med kørende trafik ikke genåbnes, inden 
den behandlede overflade er tør.  
Maling af linier skal udføres efter påføring af MasterPro-
tect H 303, når MasterProtect H 303 er fuldt udhærdet.
