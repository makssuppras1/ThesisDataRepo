Abstract
By applying a speed gradient in the DTU475 coating chamber with a single layer W, the
thickness uniformity was improved across the mirror, with the dimensions of the parabolic
VERT-X mirror, 596 mm in length. Implementing a linear speed gradient produced a
thickness deviation from the center to edge of 20%, relative to the 56% from the con-
stant reference speed. The results of a 2nd order polynomium speed gradient showed a
thickness deviation from center to edge, similar to the linear of 20%, although the poly-
nomium showed more symmetry around the center. The ratio of the time difference be-
tween the speed gradient and the reference coating at each point along the mirror surface
can be used to estimate the expected mirror thickness relative to the speed gradient im-
plemented.

Introduction
Knowledge is a powerful tool capable, if used correctly, of predicting the outcome of a
certain situation and thereby be better prepared for the future. It can be used to make
sure that history does not repeat itself, whether or not it rains tomorrow, the motion of ce-
lestial bodies or the thickness across a mirror surface from a speed gradient. The better
equipped we are with knowledge, the better equipped we are at facing the morrow. To
gain knowledge in the high energy astrophysics field, the ATHENA (Advanced telescope
for high-energy astrophysics)[15] mission will be utilizing Silicon Pore Optic modules, en-
hanced by a thin film deposition[15]. ATHENA will be observing supermassive black holes
in the center of galaxies as well as galaxy clusters in the 0.2-12 keV energy range[18].
To ensure the performance of the telescope, it must be verified and calibrated on ground
before launch. Single mirror modules are calibrated and verified at the BEaTriX facility[18]
whereas the mirror aperture (all optical components assembled) is to be calibrated at the
VERTical X-ray (VERT-X) raster-scan facility[18]. Collimating mirrors, in a wolter type 1
configuration, consisting of a parabolic and hyperbolic mirror[20] are used to produce a
parallel beam by reflecting the diverging beam from an x-ray source[18]. To enhance the
reflectivity of the collimating mirrors, a thin film deposition is required.
The mirrors generally used at DTU Space has the dimensions 70 mm x 10 mm and are
coated in the DTU475 coating chamber which has a fixed radius of 475 mm. Non uni-
formity with those dimensions are not a problem and the coating runs can be performed
with a constant ring speed. If one of these variables are to change, the result cannot be
expected to be similar. At small angles of a circle, its edges resembles that of a line, as
with the horizon of the Earth. By increasing the dimensions of the mirror, the Target to
Substrate Distance (TSD) difference between its center and edges will vary much more
than for the smaller mirror. The DTU475 introduces many more parameters than purely
the geometrical ones from the mirror dimensions and will be investigated throughout this
thesis. Previous coatings have shown the effects of non uniformity in thickness across
the surface for longer mirrors (≥475 mm) with Pt/Cr[1] and NiV[3], whereas this thesis
will study the effects of single layer W. Furthermore it will be studied if and how a speed
gradient can adjust for the non uniformity problem caused by the variation in TSD as well
as gain insight into the parameters used for predicting the outcome thickness across the
mirror surface of an arbitrary speed gradient

2.1
DC Magnetron sputtering
The coating chamber used in this thesis is the DTU475 chamber which uses DC mag-
netron sputtering for thin film deposition. To remove contaminating particles the DTU475
coating chamber utilizes heating to evaporate any liquids as well as using turbopumps to
pump the pressure down to 10−6 mbar[15]. Having removed any contaminating particles
of influence, the chamber is left with the substrate, silicon, which needs the thin film de-
posited onto its surface and the target which contains the material that are sputtered onto
the surface of the substrate. The sputtering process starts by injecting a gas, argon for
this project, into the chamber. Close to the target, the argon particles will be subjected
to an electric field, free electrons as well as to a static magnetic field[15], that will excite
the argon particles and cause an ionization, thereby creating a plasma. The target is sit-
uated on a metal holder with another metal plate around, electrically floating. By applying
a negative potential between the metal holder and plate, they function as cathode and
anode and are the supplier of free electrons. The magnetic field is there to trap the free
electrons and guide the argon ions toward the target and the electric field is there to ac-
celerate them. The argon ions will collide with the surface of the target and particles from
the target will be released, thereby creating the sputtering effect. The sputtered particles
from the target travel towards the substrate where it collides with the surface and if the
kinetic energy of the target particle exceeds the binding energy of the substrate, it will be
deposited and over time become a thin film as demonstrated in figure 2.1.

2.2
X-ray reflectrometry
After coating the substrates with the aimed thin film, the mirrors are characterized with
the XRR setup situated at DTU Space. For the x-ray source, a copper cylinder is rotated
and acts as an anode with tungsten (W) as the cathode, releasing a flow of electrons,
accelerated by the rotating copper (anode), emitting bremsstrahlung[3] spectrum plus the
emission lines for the specific material. To decrease the divergence of the beamwidth, the
photons are subjected through a narrow collimator. The photons are at different energies
and to control the energy output, a germanium monochromator is used to reflect the pho-
tons twice, resulting in a single energy at 8.048 keV to pass. It’s reflected twice to keep
a parallel beam and to enhance the monochromator effect. By reiterating the reflecting
through monochromators improves the monochromatic energy due to Braggs equation
but parts of the original beam is removed as well. A final aluminium collimator is set to
determine the amount of photons passing through to the mirror and detector, as to make
sure that the detector does not get saturated. A mirror is placed on a vacuum chuck with
a vacuum pump connected so all the surface of the mirror is exposed to the x-rays. The
mirror is followed by a tube containing the detector. The system in its entirety is shown in
an illustration in figure 2.2.

2.3
VERT-X calibration method
The dimensions of mirrors used in flight for x-ray space missions are typically limited due
to the mass constraint of the mission, whereas smaller mirrors are stacked up in modules.
But before the launch of the x-ray space mission, they have to be validated and calibrated.
As the atmosphere of Earth acts as a shield or barrier for x-ray photons due to them being
absorbed in air, it is not possible to test the instrument by pointing it toward the sky and the
final testing therefore has to occur on ground in a testing facility. To test and calibrate the
optical assembly of the ATHENA X-ray telescope, VERT-X is in the process of creating this
test facility. Validating and calibrating x-ray space telescopes is nothing new in the space
industry and has been accomplished in test facilities such as PANTHER in Munich with a
125 meter long vacuum tube[20] was used for the XMM-Newton mission and the length
of the tube is only surpassed by the X-ray & Cryogenic Facility by NASA with a vacuum
tube of 500 meters[20] which was used for the Chandra mission. As the technology for
x-ray space missions evolve, so must the test facilities and with a focal length of 12 meters
and an optical assembly of 2.5 meters the source should be placed much larger than 500
meters[20]. Instead of increasing the beamline length to fully illuminate the entire Mirror
Assembly (MA), VERT-X has chosen to utilize a raster scan with a 1 arcsec[20] collimated
beam and move it around illuminating small parts of the MA at a time. This will shorten
the vacuum tube to be 20 meters with the raster scan mechanism, containing the x-ray
source and collimator, located 5 meters below the MA[18], all placed vertically as to allow
minimum gravitational deformations on the MA as shown in figure 2.3.
Figure 2.3: Illustration of the VERT-X test facility with focus on the ray tracing from
source to detector. Everything is encapsuled by a vacuum tube[18].

2.3.1
Wolter type 1 design
The wolter type 1 design is the most popular out of the 3 types proposed by Hans Wolter
in 1952 in regards to x-ray space telescopes such as NuStar which launched in June
2012[14]. The wolter type 1 design consist of a pair of grazing incidence mirrors, paraboloid
as the primary mirror and the hyperboloid as the secondary mirror. The wolter type 1 de-
sign allows nesting of mirror shells due to its hollow geometry[28] which increases the
collective area of the telescope. The wolter type mirrors fulfills the abbe sine condition
which is the relation between the angle of the ray from the object and the angle of the
same ray from the image is proportional to the magnification[11] and if that relation is
satisfied, there is no spherical aberrations[11].
2.4
X-ray physics
In 1895 in the city of Würzburg, Germany, a physics professor named W. C. Röntgen
discovered, when applying high voltage to electrodes, a ray of light that could penetrate
lighter materials such as paper, wood and human flesh but was stopped by bones and
heavier metals[19]. These type of photons he named x-rays[19]. 75 years later the first
x-ray space telescope, Uhuru was launched to discover cosmic x-ray sources in the 2-20
keV range and discovered quasars, active galaxies and galaxy clusters[19]. The future
of x-ray space missions is the ATHENA mission (Advanced Telescope for High Energy
Astrophysics)[9] which it set to launch in the 2030s[8] and is operating in the 0.1 to 10 keV
range[9] and will be utilizing silicon pore optics[8] to converge the x-rays to the detector.
High energy photons, such as x-rays, behave differently with matter or interfaces com-
pared with lower energy photons. They are either being absorbed, as is the case for
cosmic x-rays entering the atmosphere of Earth, or they will pass right through the ma-
terial. How the x-rays interact with the material they encounter depends upon numerous
properties such as the energy of the photon, the material density, thickness etc. and the
grazing incident angle is one of these influential properties. The interactions important for
this thesis is the reflection and refraction, how the path of the incident photon changes in
a different refractive material. The change in angle can be found by snell’s law and the
refractive index (eq. 2.1).

Installation
To be able to coat a long mirror inside the DTU475 coating chamber with minimized non
uniformity and verify it as a success, two key points have to be addressed. A special
holder for the long mirror has to be designed, approved and tested and that is described

3.1
Coating material
The objective of the collimator long mirrors for the VERT-X test facility is to achieve a
reflectivity efficiency of (0.82, 0.83, 0.62, 0.52) at the respective energies (0.5, 1.0, 5.0,
10) keV[18]. The first decision to be made to achieve these criteria is the material of
the coating and its thickness, both of which are found through simulations but more pa-
rameters concern the choice of coating material. The coating material should have good
reflective properties for x-rays at low grazing angles from which a high density material is
preferable (see equation 2.3). The purpose of the mirror is for it to be used as the first re-
flective surface of the x-ray source. This poses certain strains from radiation damage and
thermal gradients[17] because the emitted power from the source is one order of mag-
nitude smaller for the second optical element compared to the first[17]. Candidates for
top coating material is Tungsten (W), Gold (Au), Iridium (Ir) and Platinum (Pt) due to their
excellent reflective properties and have previous records of being used for x-ray space
missions as bilayers[1][6][21][7]. To compare these materials, their smallest thickness is
found to achieve the reflective efficiency criteria mentioned above. This is accomplished
by simulations via ”https://henke.lbl.gov/” for bilayers. How the target material is coated
onto the surface of the mirror depends (among other variables) upon the choice of bottom
or adhesion layer as materials such as Pt will have a smoother film if Cr is used as a
bottom layer instead of Si[1]. To better compare the performance of the top layer, all of
them share the same Cr adhesion bottom layer and roughness of 4 Å, shown in table 3.1.

increasing the thickness of the thin film, the reflectivity efficiency improves, until a certain

Design
Two parts are of concern that will cause a uniformity in the coating if changes are not
implemented on the coating protocol for the carausel speed. The first, and of most con-
cern, is the longer length of the mirror, the horizontal part. as the difference in target to
substrate distance varies with 184 mm (see fig 3.6). The second part is that the mirror is
hyperboloid/ paraboloid meaning that the mirror has a curve. The difference in target to
substrate distance in this case is 6 mm between the vertical sides[20] and is therefore a
secondary concern as the horizontal length of the mirror has a higher chance of causing
non uniformity than the curve of the mirror. To be fully prepared to coat and characterize
the VERT-X collimator mirrors, 3 designs for holders in different situations is required. To
replicate the target to substrate distance and test the optimal speed gradient before imple-
menting it to the VERT-X mirrors, it has to be tested on a number of smaller mirrors such
is shown in figure 3.8 for the BEaTriX reference mirrors. The second design of a holder is
for the actual VERT-X mirrors. The VERT-X mirrors pose a problem concerning its weight
and the design must be rigid and stable to hold the mirrors. The second design should
either be able to contain both the hyperboloid (544 mm) and the paraboloid (596 mm), not
simultaneously, or the design is split up in two holders, with different lengths. The third
design concerns the characterization of the long mirror. The XRR measurements at DTU

required for the characterization as well as the present one has to be modified for long
mirrors.

4.3
Coating chamber holder
As the VERT-X mirrors are substantially larger in dimensions compared to the mirrors
normally coated in the DTU475 coating chamber, the present holders will not be sufficient
to contain the mirror module shown in figure 4.1. The mirror module poses problems
concerning its weight, 8 kg for the paraboloid, and its large dimensions. The requirement
for the holder is to be rigid enough to carry the weight of the mirror module for the duration
of the coating process and to lock it in place so it will not move around during the speed
gradient. A holder for the coating chamber for a long mirror has been succesfully achieved
for the BEaTriX mirror[1] but as the dimensions vary, a new holder must be designed
and built although inspiration has been adopted for the VERT-X design. There are two
designs for the holder, both with the same base only varying in the locking of the module.
To withstand the weight of the mirror module, it is placed atop of three angle brackets with
a counterpart opposite of itself, forming a rectangle that the mirror module fits in as shown
in figure 4.4. By adding the counterparts to the angle brackets the mirror module cannot
tip over, but as they only limits the modules vertical motion, it can still move horizontally,
as for the second requirement of locking it down comes the variability in the designs. The
first design comes from utilizing that the length of angle bracket (125 mm) is longer than
the height of the mirror module (115 mm) and by inserting a screw through a piece of
teflon and the angle bracket, the mirror module is locked. This requires the teflon to fill
the gap between the screw and mirror module as shown in figure 4.4b. The material for
the block should have low outgassing properties, either a metal such as stainless steel
or plastics like PTFE or teflon. The advantage of the plastics is that they can be modified
more easily if some material has to be removed to make it fit better. The second design
utilizes a clamp to lock down the mirror module and remove any horizontal movement.
The clamp has to be made entirely out of metal, the handles are typically made from
either wood or outgassing plastics but those will have to be removed as to not increase
contamination during coating. To determine which locking mechanism is superior, they
need to be compared against certain criteria such as cost, complexity, adjustability and
how easy it is for other people to use the holder as shown in table 4.1. When the mirror
arrives the procedure should be plug and play, meaning the mirror module should fit in
the holder even if the dimensions are a bit off. That is the reasoning for including the
adjustability criteria in the tradeoff study.
4.3.1

The cost of the teflon blocks and clam depends upon which assumptions are made. Teflon
has to be bought in as a large cylinder but the amount used for the actual holder is small,
and the rest of the material can be used for other projects. A single clam is less expensive
than the long cylinder of teflon, but it depends upon how many clams are necessary,
using 4 would be close to the same price as the teflon. Both of them has been set as the
same, as both of them could be used for other projects and are in the same price class.
Complexity in table 4.1 is the difficulty of creating the holder due to the choice of locking
method. Teflon poses little complexity as it requires a nut and bolt to lock it in place.

The coating plan is made for a focused coating schedule. The reference coating is set to
two days, one for coating, another for the XRR measurements. The same amount of time
is set for the first speed gradient implementation as everything is prepared for the linear
speed gradient as shown in figure 3.9b. The second speed gradient is from a Friday to a
Tuesday, given it the weekend to think about how to optimize the first speed gradient. The
third and final speed gradient implementation is given a week to complete as it has to be

to be introduced. Two weeks are given for the coating of the VERT-X mirror. This is sub-
stantially more time than the other coatings and is because a special holder is required for
the 16 kg mirror module as shown in figure 4.1. If the VERT-X mirror cannot be delivered
in time for this thesis as it is an external company in charge of the delivering, more time is
available for the three speed gradient implementations, although the focused coating plan
should have the necessary wiggle room of time. The 36% reduction in thickness from the
center to the edge is based on a previous reference coating using NiV[3] with the same
dimensions as for the parabolic VERT-X mirror of 596 mm.

Linear speed gradient
The thickness of the mirrors with the honeycomb collimator as shown in figure 5.5 with
the center point on the mirror with an estimation of 6̃.6 nm is on the edge of being too
thin for reliable data. A thickness of 12 nm is warranted. Instead of performing another
reference coating, the data points with a collimator is scaled up with 181% corresponding
to a thickness of 12 nm for the centered mirror. All the points are scaled up assuming,
based off of figure 3.3b, that the reduction in thickness at the edges will have the same
reduction in percentage. The thickness increase is found in the ring speed reduction and
the ring speed for this thicker speed gradient is a factor of 0.55, reducing the center speed
from 1960 steps/s to 1086 steps/s. The safety angles was supposed to be from whenever
the gradient was not used. To reduce the coating time, the safety angles were reduced
from 130◦to 50◦, reducing the coating time with 10 minutes and still being out of the
sputtering range from the honeycomb collimator[25]. The speed gradient used is shown
in figure 5.9. The center of the mirror starts 180◦from the W magnetron. As the mirror
is 100◦across its surface and the sputtering angle of the honeycomb collimator is 50◦
relative to the target (31◦from center, see fig 6.1), the edge of the mirror can rotate 99◦
before affected by sputtering. The 80◦is rotated with 7000 steps/s as shown in figure
5.9a. When implementing the .csv file with the speed and time per degree (see figure A.3

5.4.2
Asymmetry
There was an asymmetry in the chamber. The linear fit is based only on 3 points, the
edges and the center. As the left-center point (at 145 mm in fig 5.10b) is not following
the model, with the model being too low, the speed for that point will be too slow and
the consequence will be an increase in thickness. The two edge points however are of
similar thickness and the model is created from those points, but the resulting thickness
varies. Two reasons may explain this dilemma. The first is the possibility of errors in
the data points. The data points used for the linear speed gradient was scaled up, with
the two edge points being below 3 nm, making them unreliable. The second reason
came from investigating the W target with the collimator attached. It was discovered that
the collimator and the target had an electrical connection, which should not be the case.
With this connection, the collimator would change the electrical field near the target and
affect the trajectory of the ions. The reason for the connection was a pin at the top right
corner, between the honeycomb mesh and the target. The connection was removed by
re-screwing the pin back in, so that the collimator was now floating (not connected to
anything). By removing the electrical connection, the characteristics of the coating is
changed and a model cannot be made on previous, error filled, data points. To have a
thickness of 12 nm at the center point a reference coating with a constant speed of 1088
steps/s was performed with the results shown in figure 5.11. As seen in figure 5.11, the
new data points show an improvement in the symmetry around the center. It is clear
however from the new data points that a linear function will not create a uniform surface,
as the two points closest to the center both are higher than a linear model. The next speed
gradient model has to include all 5 points.

5.5
Thickness simulation tool
The process from coating to data analysis is cumbersome and as there has been many
coatings due to various factors in this thesis, only one remains. A model could be fitted
to the 5 data points using equation 3.3, which showed that a 5th order polynomium would
be the best fit to the data points. The results of the different models on the thickness
outcome was not clear however. To acquire a better understanding of the coating results, a
simulation tool was created from the idea that the only difference between the two coating
runs would be the time difference during sputtering, as shown in equation 2.17. The
target does not sputter in a line to a point but across an angle as shown in figure 6.1. The
simulation tool takes the time difference between the speed gradient and the constant
speed, which was used to generate the first data points, across an acceptance angle.
The sputtering angle is the angle between the center of the target to the trajectory of the
sputtered particle as shown in figure 6.1, whereas the acceptance angle is the number of
data points to each side of the sample that is used to generate the thickness outcome as
shown in figure 5.12. Sputtering angle is a geometric parameter that depends upon the
choice of collimator. Acceptance angle is how much of the sputtering angle is accepted
for each point on the mirror surface. The sputtering angle is from the target, whereas
the acceptance angle is from the center of the chamber, as each data point in the speed
gradient corresponds to 1◦in the chamber relative to center. This procedure is done for
each point and shown in figure 5.12. The larger the time difference, the thicker each
point will be. The model is made from the data points of constant speed, to find the time
factor, △t, that are multiplied to the data points. The determining factor is the value for
the acceptance angle, as a larger acceptance angle will either decrease or increase the
thickness depending upon the specific point on the mirror surface. From the visualization
of the model, figure 5.12, it is clear as to why the center point is thicker than the reference
point, as the acceptance angle is not 0◦. If the acceptance angle was 0◦, a model fitting
all 5 points would create a flat line, uniform mirror thickness. As there were only a single
coating left and many questions still unanswered regarding which model is the superior, it
was decided to change focus from acquiring a flat, uniform thickness across the mirror to
instead optimizing the simulation tool as it would be useful to test other speed gradients
at a fast pace. The final coating would therefore be one which could be used to optimize

Discussion
6.1
Acceptance angle
One of the big hurdles to overcome in this thesis was regarding the acceptance angle and
how to work with it. For simplicity the sputtering is constrained to originate from a single
point at the target, although the sputtering will occur across the entirety of the target sur-
face in reality. The sputtering angle has great impact on the thin film deposition, figure
5.5 to 5.7 shows a decrease in thickness and an improvement in lowering the roughness
across the long mirror by mounting a mesh type 4 honeycomb collimator that narrows the
sputtering angle from the target to 49◦[25]. As discussed in section 5.5, the acceptance
angle greatly affects the process of estimating the thickness of the mirror based off an
arbitrary speed gradient. The acceptance angle is the number of data points to each side
of the sample that is used to generate the thickness outcome as shown in figure 5.12.
The acceptance angle is not the same for the 5 points. This has been deduced from
the thickness simulation tool by calculating, empirically, the acceptance angle that makes
each point fit with the measured data. This gives 5 different acceptance angles but also
reveals a trend. The process of finding these acceptance angles empirically through the
simulation tool was fast but leans heavily on trusting the thickness simulation tool. As the
goal of the thesis changed from creating a uniform thickness across a mirror surface to
estimating the thickness based off an arbitrary speed gradient, it was necessary to find
the acceptance angle that was to be used for the expectation of the last 2nd order poly-
nomium coating showed in figure 5.15 and was set to be 19◦for all 5 points, a simplified
view of the acceptance angle.
As to acquire a deeper understanding of the acceptance angle for the 5 points across
the mirror surface, the first step is to understand its orientation to the target. If the mirror
surface is parallel with the sputtered particles, then there can be no deposition. The re-
sults from each of the 5 points travelling 62◦(relative to center) during sputtering is shown
in figure 6.2b. The model is based of the relative sputtering between the normal vector
of the mirror surface and the angle from the target (see fig 6.2a). When the ring rotates
with 1◦, the normal vector of the mirror changes one degree but the angle from the target
(a2 in figure 6.1) changes more than 1◦. Figure 6.1 shows the relation between the angle
from the center and the the angle from the target (assuming a single point). Following
one point on the mirror, the distance is the same to the center at any given time, thereby
a triangle with two identical (in length) sides can be formed. From the equations shown
in figure 6.1, the angle from the target depends upon the target to substrate distance and
how much it rotates from the center and will therefore change for each point along the
mirror surface as shown in figure 6.2b. If the relative sputtering angle is 90◦, it means that
the sputtered atoms are parallel with the mirror surface and no deposition rate will occur
(assuming single point target). From figure 6.2b it is clear that the relative sputtering angle
varies greatly across the mirror surface with the maximum angle of the center at 38.3◦and
the edges at ±69◦. This further validates the grounds for the acceptance angle (in the
simulation tool) is not the same across the mirror surface due to the varying TSD. One
question remains however, how can we determine the acceptance angle of each point
along the mirror surface based on the knowledge presented in figure 6.2b?

6.2
Asymmetric tool
The result from the 2nd order polynomium coating shown in figure 5.18 revealed a differ-
ence between the measured values and the expected outcome from the thickness simu-
lation tool. As discussed in section 6.1, the acceptance angle has a great impact on the
simulation tool and should vary, contrary to what was used for the expected value in figure
5.18, which used 19◦for all the 5 data points. By using the same amount of acceptance
angle for the points (or adding the same weight) assumes symmetry, which is disproved
by the result of the 2nd order polynomium. The data points was changed to be symmet-
rical but if they weren’t, the left side would be a bit thinner as the data point was reduced
and the right side would be a bit thicker as that data point was increased, not resulting in
a better fit for the measurements of the 2nd order polynomium coating. Besides the thin
film deposition being asymmetric, it was discovered that the simulation tool itself proved
asymmetric as well. By using a data set of symmetric data points, to create the model
and for the expected outcome, the expected thickness should be symmetrical around the
center if the sputtering range is the same for the samples. Figure 6.4 shows the differ-
ence between a 2nd and 4th order polynomium fit and expected thickness from the speed
gradients. From the expected thickness (fig 6.4b), it can be seen that the two most right
data points are lower than the left side despite the data points being symmetrical. As the
2nd order polynomium has a larger residual in terms of fitting the data points than the 4th
order (see fig 3.11), that could have been the culprit, but by using a 4th order polynomium,
the same trend occurs with a thinner right side than the left. Splitting the thickness model
in two, right and left from the center and taking the average it was discovered that the
right side had a larger average. A larger thickness model results in a lower speed gradi-
ent which consequently leads to a thinner film. By checking the average between each

6.3
Modelling tool for uniform thickness
The goal of this thesis is to understand how a speed gradient can adjust for the non
uniformity caused by the varying TSD and utilize that knowledge to predict the outcome
of a speed gradient through simulation (see section 1). By using the acceptance angle
found through fitting the model with the data points, it is possible to not only simulate the
result of previous coatings but create a model, that would result in a flat thickness line
across the mirror surface. This process is heavily dependent upon the performance of
the simulation thickness tool, and to improve the reliability of the model, another coating
could be done to test the similarity with the new acceptance angles. In section 5.5 different
models were tested by extrapolation and forced symmetry. In the final model, shown in
figure 6.6, the data points can be manipulated as well, in both x- and y- direction. Data
manipulation is a dangerous path to take, but by manipulating the position of the data
points (from the reference coating), it is possible to create a flat thickness line from which
a model is produced that might provide more insights to increase the knowledge behind
the understanding. From the speed gradient model resulting in an expected thickness
uniformity, shown in figure 6.8, it can be seen that due to the asymmetry in the chamber,
the safety angle should not be the same for the left and right side but instead depend
upon the values of their respective edge. When fitting a model to data points there are
not a singular possibility but many, as is the case with this tool. It is clear however, that
uniform thickness cannot be achieved with a linear fit unless the center data points from
the reference coating lies on the line. By moving the position of the edge points, it could
be that the modified line fitted with the centered points however. When the desired speed
gradient has been found in the program, the user can press ”export to .csv” in order to
create .csv file containing the speeds and time as with the files shown in figure A.3 in
the appendix. Using the 4th polynomium, the model should be narrower than the original
model as the 4 modifiable data points are moved closer to towards the center and lowered.

Future works
7.0.1
Adding more data points
This could be done once at a constant speed. The polynomiums are fitted to data points,
and the more data points, the better the understanding of the thickness along the mirror
can be acquired. This could be achieved by mounting the samples vertically (±90◦of
figure 5.1).
7.0.2
Invert the rotation
From the 30 samples coated and measured throughout this thesis, there is a clear dif-
ference between the left edge sample and the right edge sample from each coating run
(see figures A.7 - A.13), with the right edge having more well defined kiessig fringes than
the left edge. Having two coatings, one clockwise, the other counter clockwise. This pro-
cedure could provide knowledge as to why the left edge sample behaves differently, if it
has to do with the starting sequence of the coating/ speed gradient or if it is purely due to
the asymmetry with the target. The asymmetry at the target/ magnetron could be tested
as well. This could be done by mounting the same material and collimator to another
magnetron. If the results remain the same, another honeycomb mesh type 4 collimator is
implemented to test if it is a collimation issue. Lastly the material plate can be changed
to a new one. All of these test coatings can be achieved with only one sample per run,
placed at the furthest left edge. Before any of the test coatings are run, check the electrical
connection between the collimator and the magnetron. A useful system implementation
would be a feedback loop that could read out the current position, thereby knowing the
starting position. If the program for the coating is too large in terms of angles, then the
starting position would increase for each coating run, thereby shifting and worsening the
results. There could also be a glitch in the system that counted more or less, by hav-
ing a feed back loop of the position, any doubt regarding the starting position could be
neglected.
7.0.3
Uncertainty in thickness tool
The current state of the thickness simulation tool described in section 5.5 is to aim at an
exact fit. This is not realistic as there are many parameters that may change in between
coatings. A study ranging from the mounting of the samples to the fitting of the samples
are necessary to conduct to implement an uncertainty to the model. From fitting alone it
is clear from figure 5.17b and the other data sets (fig A.12), that the left edge is harder to
fit and get a precise thickness than the other samples, resulting in a larger uncertainty in
the fitting section.
7.0.4
Other materials
From the results it was shown that the worst result from the speed gradient was ~20 %
from the center, and with constant speed it was ~56 %, all the coatings with a single layer
of W. From previous projects concerning thin film deposition, used materials such as Pt
with Cr as the adhesion layer[1], which had a maximum deviation of ~27 % from a constant
speed alone, although the mirror length was 456 mm. Another project used NiV as the
material[3], which had a deviation from the reference coating with constant speed of ~36
% with the same length of mirror as with this thesis, 596 mm. Implementing the same 2nd
order speed gradient as in figure 5.15 with NiV or Pt/Cr would be intriguing to compare the
results and see if changing of the materials would improve the results, relative difference
from each point to the center.

Conclusion
When performing the reference coating with a constant speed, by mounting a honeycomb
mesh type 4 collimator to the W magnetron, there was a reduction in the thickness with an
average of 67% relative to the reference coating without collimation. After mounting the
honeycomb collimator, increasing the power from 300 W to 700 W and doubling the ring
speed the roughness (see figure 5.7) became more uniform across the mirror surface.
The thickness reduction from center to edges are at 56% at the worst for the honeycomb
collimator reference coating (figure 5.5b) which is lower than the expected ≈90% from
the deposition rate ratio in figure 3.7 and equation 2.13.
Applying a speed gradient in the DTU475 coating chamber showed an improvement in the
thickness uniformity across the mirror surface. By implementing a linear speed gradient
(see figure 5.10) showed a maximum deviation in thickness of 20% from the center point.
The same amount of thickness deviation from the center was observed from the result of
implementing a 2nd order polynomium (see figure 5.18), although by using a polynomium
model based on all 5 data points (samples) gave more control of the thickness uniformity
than the linear model based on 3 of the data points.
The time difference during sputtering (equation 2.17) between the reference coating and
the speed gradient can be used to generate an expected thickness outcome. The ex-
pected result of the 2nd order polynomium, with an acceptance angle of 19◦for all 5
points, showed the same trend as the results from the coating, shown in figure 5.18 with
the right side lower than the left, tilting. The acceptance angles, used for the thickness
simulation tool, should not be the same for the 5 points across the mirror surface as their
relative sputtering angle varies greatly, with the edges up to ±69◦and the center ±38.3◦,
during sputtering, which is calculated by assuming single point target. By changing the
acceptance angles to make the expected result match the measured result, it is possible
to create (through simulation) which model could result in a straight line as shown in figure
6.8 if the parameters in the coating chamber are similar as for the reference coating.

 W coating plan 
For the 596 mm VERT-X mirror 
Purpose: Find carousel speed gradient that reduces non uniformity in long (596 mm) mirror 
Coating material: W/Si 
Structure: Single bilayer of thickness W = 10 nm. 
Common coating parameters: Power 350 W, TSD 90 - 212 mm at center to edge of mirror, Pressure 
3 mTorr. 
Important considerations: 
 
Presputtering: Sample location, should the target rotate between target presputtering to 
avoid deposition? 
 
At what position (in the chamber) should the speed gradient start? 
 
a. W reference coating (Monday 24th to Tuesday 25th April) 
Constant speed of 980 steps/s for a thickness of 10 nm. 
Expected thickness at the edges: 36 % reduction = 6.36 nm 
Characterization technique: 8.048 keV XRR 
Number of samples: 5 for 1 single run

Linear speed gradient

Number of samples: 5 for 1 single run

Linear speed gradient (optimized)

Number of samples: 5 for 1 single run

Optimized linear or polynomial speed gradient

Number of samples: 5 for 1 single run
