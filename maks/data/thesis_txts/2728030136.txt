English abstract   
The preparation of seaweed dishes has a long history and tradition. When seaweed is 
imported from native countries, typically the traditions do not follow. This may give rise to 
negative effects from excessive iodine intake. This report investigated if traditional practices 
of seaweed for the preparation of dishes in Chile and Japan influence the risk of a high 
intake of iodine. The investigation was based on the conduction of an experiment and 
reviewing existing literature. The experimental work of this study was inspired by Chilean 
and Japanese cooking traditions. Our findings indicate that the traditional Japanese method 
of wiping dried seaweed does not reduce the concentration of iodine. On the other hand, 
we discovered that the traditional soaking treatment of seaweed, both in Chile and Japan, 
influences the risk of high iodine intake. Soaking treatment of kombu (Laminaria digitata) 
for 2 hours in cold tap water (11°C) resulted in substantial removal of iodine, with 95-98% 
being extracted. These findings demonstrate the effect of traditional soaking treatment on 
the iodine concentration in dried kombu. Other experimental studies on the soaking 
treatment of fresh seaweed from existing literature revealed a lower loss of iodine 
compared to dried seaweed. The experiment showed that soaking dried seaweed was more 
effective in removing iodine than soaking fresh seaweed. However, further studies are 
needed to investigate this observation. The effect of traditional Chilean and Japanese 
soaking treatment of seaweed on the risk for consumers was also examined. It was found 
that soaking treatment in the household mitigates the risk of adverse effects resulting from 
excessive iodine intake. However, there are also examples that following these traditions 
may not reduce the risk. Based on the Japanese risk assessment, the traditional preparation 
of dashi stock in Japan may pose a risk to children due to the high iodine content leached 
from the seaweed during soaking. This risk can be reduced by replacing the stock water 
containing seaweed with fresh water. Further, the daily average consumption of kombu may 
pose a risk to Japanese children. Based on the Chilean risk assessment daily average 
consumption of cochayuyo chips may pose a risk to Chilean children. Findings suggest that 
soy may have a protective function in a diet with excessive seaweed consumption, and 
potentially serve as a source in the prevention of adverse effects, including thyroid 
disorders. However, a diet high in soy but lack of seaweed may potentially promote the 
incidence of thyroid disorders. According to the Danish risk assessment, it has been found 
that daily consumption of seaweed chips poses a high risk to the Danish population.

Following the traditional Japanese and Chilean guidelines for soaking treatment of seaweed 
species with low iodine contents for chips production in Denmark, may pose a low risk for 
adults, adolescents, and pregnant and lactating women. On the other hand, it may pose a 
high risk for children. In conclusion, given that soaking is not a common practice in 
Denmark, a variation between seaweed chips and regular chips is recommended for the 
Danish population. It is also not recommended to consume seaweed chips daily.

1. Introduction 
Seaweed is a common source of nutrients in several countries and there is a long history and 
tradition on how to prepare seaweed dishes. These traditions may influence the chemical 
composition of the seaweed and thereby potentially impact the risk of a high intake of some 
substances, for instance, iodine. In this report, it is investigated if there is a traditional practice of 
seaweed for the preparation of dishes in native countries that influences the risk of a high intake 
of iodine. Furthermore, an interaction between the intake of other dietary sources such as soy, 
and the intake of iodine on the function of the thyroid gland has been suggested.  
Soaking seaweed for at least a few hours is suggested in many recipes from traditional cooking 
books. Furthermore, traditional Japanese recipes also recommend cleaning the seaweed with a 
damp cloth before soaking. Several studies have been performed using different soaking processes 
to analyze iodine loss under certain conditions. Some have been using deionized water, ultrapure 
water, or tap water. However, current literature lacks information on the effect of cleaning 
seaweed with a damp cloth before soaking.  
In Denmark, there have been reported cases where the levels of iodine found in brown algae have 
been considered to pose a risk to the consumer. This has raised concerns about potential risks 
associated with these products.   
The purpose of this project will be:  
1) To suggest methods that can be applied in the Danish household and production to 
reduce the concentration of iodine in brown algae based on traditional Japanese and 
Chilean cooking traditions. This could be used by the Danish Food and Veterinary 
Administration to improve the guidance to the consumer and the production 
concerning the treatment of brown algae before consumption.  
2) To perform a literature search and chemical analysis on the impact of selected 
traditional preparations 
3) To investigate if soy and seaweed are used in the Japanese kitchen such that the intake 
of seaweed modulate the potential toxicity of soy.

3. Background  
3.1 Role of iodine in human  
In this section, the role of iodine in humans is introduced including iodine deficiency and excess, 
and imbalance in the level of thyroid hormones (TH).  
3.1.1 Role of iodine  
Iodine is an essential micronutrient. It has a short half-life and is almost completely absorbed in 
the body. Absorbed iodine is excreted primarily in the urine (>97%). (Nielsen Elsa et al., 2014). 
Iodine exists as an ion in the body. In nature iodine can be present in different forms; as a simple 
inorganic form of iodide (I-) and iodate (IO3-) or as iodinated complex organic compounds such as 
==PAGE:14==
 
 
 
 
 
3 
thyroid hormones (TH) namely T4 (3,5,3ʹ,5ʹ-tetraiodo-L-thyronine) and T3 (3,5,3ʹ-triiodo-L-
thyronine) and iodine lipids, iodine sugars, and other organic iodine compounds.  Iodine is a trace 
mineral that is essential to produce TH which is biosynthesized by the thyroid gland in the body 
(Sorrenti et al., 2021). TH has several significant roles in the human body including normal 
development and growth of tissues such as the brain and skeleton and controlling energy and 
metabolism (Segni, 2017; Shahid et al., 2022). 
3.1.2 Iodine deficiency and excess  
Both, iodine deficiency and excess can give rise to thyroid dysfunction and cause serious diseases 
such as thyroid disorders. Most healthy individuals can tolerate a high iodine intake. However, in 
some people, excess iodine intake over a long period may lead to thyroid disorders such as 
hypothyroidism, an underactive thyroid gland with too low hormone production, and 
hyperthyroidism, an overactive thyroid gland with too much hormone production. Iodine intake 
over a long period may also be linked to the development of goiter, thyroid enlargement and 
Hashimoto’s thyroiditis, Low thyroid production, due to a thyroid autoimmunity disease involving 
the body’s production of antibodies that attack the cells in the thyroid. Graves’ disease is also an 
autoimmune disease that can be caused by hyperthyroidism, overactive thyroid. Iodine deficiency 
disorders on the other hand may lead to hypothyroidism, goiter, and Hashimoto’s thyroiditis. 
(Farebrother et al., 2019).   
3.1.3 Imbalance in the level of thyroid hormones   
If the level of thyroid hormones is in imbalance it can, among other things, affect fertility and 
pregnancy therefore an adequate level is crucial for maintaining a normal thyroid function.  
Especially, the developing fetal brain depends on the maternal thyroid level. To ensure normal 
brain development adequate amounts of maternal T3 and T4 must be distributed across the 
placenta to the fetus otherwise negative effects such as irreversible brain damage in children can 
occur (Segni, 2017). 
3.2 Thyroid hormones  
TH has an essential role in the body's function and appears in almost all tissues and organs. TH are 
amino–acid–derived water-soluble compounds including thyroxine (T4) and triiodothyronine (T3). 
Iodine is a needed element for the synthesis of TH which is reflected in its chemical structure

which represents 3 and 4 iodine atoms respectively. (Gilbert et al., 2020). (See Fejl!

Around 20% of T3 comes from the thyroid gland but more than 80% of the T3 that enters the cell 
comes from the conversion of T4 by deiodinase 1 or 2 that remove one of the iodine atoms so T4 
enters the tissue and converts to T3. However, T4 has a strong binding to globulins in the serum 
and has a half-life between 5-7 days while the half-life of T3 is only 1 day. Thyroid function is 
clinically assessed by serum or plasma concentrations of TSH/T4/T3 (Gilbert et al., 2020; 
Pirahanchi et al., 2022). 
3.3 Thyroid hormone pathology 
TH are produced by the thyroid gland which is a butterfly-shaped organ in the front of the neck. 
The thyroid gland is a fundamental hormone gland that plays a major role in the metabolism, 
growth, and development of the human body. In some cases, the thyroid gland may grow in size 
as a consequence of an overactive thyroid (hyperthyroidism) where the thyroid gland makes too 
many hormones, or an underactive thyroid (hypothyroidism) where the thyroid gland makes too 
few hormones. Either the whole thyroid gland becomes enlarged (diffuse goiter), or individual 
lumps called nodules grow in the gland (nodular goiter). The number of hormones from nodular 
goiter can be examined by a method called thyroid scintigraphy. In cases where nodular goiter 
makes more hormones than the rest of the thyroid tissue, they are referred to as  “hot” nodules. 
In cases, where they make fewer hormones, they are referred to as “cold” nodules. By constantly 
releasing a balanced amount of TH into the bloodstream the thyroid gland helps to regulate many 
body functions. The thyroid gland produces more hormones in certain situations where the body 
needs more energy for instance for growth, or cold, or during pregnancy (How Does the Thyroid Gland 
Work?, 2018).

3.4 Thyroid hormone regulation  
The production of TH depends on being released by the hormonal stimulus of other hormones 
including (TRH) the hypothalamic-thyrotropin-releasing hormone and glycoprotein (TSH) the 
thyroid-stimulating hormone. In the hypothalamus-pituitary-thyroid (HPT) axis, the hypothalamus 
releases TRH which impacts the pituitary gland to produce TSH by TRH binding to receptors on 
thyrotropic cells of the anterior pituitary gland which causes them to release TSH in the systemic 
circulation. TSH finally stimulates the thyroid gland to synthesize and release thyroid hormones in 
the form of T3 and T4 in the bloodstream (See Fig. 2)(Moini et al., 2020). 
 
FIG. 2 - The Hypothalamus-Pituitary-Thyroid (HPT)-axis (Biorender.com) 
The negative feedback system controls the level of TH to maintain homeostasis by using the 
hormones in the HPT axis. The hormones in the HPT axis depend on the circulating plasma 
concentration of T3 and T4 that either reduce or increase the secretion of TRH and TSH dependent 
on whether the level of TH is high or low. When the level of TH is high, the production of TSH is 
decreased and when the level of TH is low, the production of TSH is increased. The high and low 
levels of TSH may imply either hypothyroidism or hyperthyroidism as a result of iodine deficiency 
or excess (Moini et al., 2020).

3.6 Wolff-Chaikoff effect  
3.6.1 The Wolff-Chaikoff effect  
In 1948 Wolff & Chaikoff showed that when plasma iodide (I−)  levels elevated and reached a 
critically high concentration in the plasma, the organic binding of iodide in the thyroid decreased 
as well as the TH biosynthesis (Eng et al., 1999). This is referred to as the acute Wolff-Chaikoff 
effect in normal individuals, where excess iodide inhibits thyroid hormone synthesis. (See section 
3.5.1, Fig. 4). TPO mRNA and protein synthesis (thyroglobulin iodinations) is temporally inhibited 
by formed iodopeptides. The effect lasts a few days (7-14 days). (Visser & Weber-Schöndorfer, 
2015). Normal synthesis of T4 and T3 returns by the resume of intrathyroidal iodide. This is 
referred to as an escape from the Wolff-Chaikoff effect. The intrathyroidal inorganic iodine 
concentration decreases by downregulation of sodium iodide symporter (NIS), responsible for the 
iodide transport into the cell. This results in the TPO-H2O2 system returning to its normal activity 
(Visser & Weber-Schöndorfer, 2015).  
3.6.2 The function of the Wolff-Chaikoff effect  
A healthy thyroid can adapt to iodine excess in a short period due to the escape from the acute 
Wolff-Chaikoff effect (De Leo & Braverman, 2019). However, individuals with thyroid problems 
may cause failure to escape from the acute Wolff-Chaikoff effect. For this reason, the Wolff-
Chaikoff effect is believed to be linked to adverse effects such as Iodine induced hypothyroidism. 
However, the Wolff-Chaikoff effect is a body function. Body functions are in their nature supposed 
to be protective. This means that the Wolff-Chaikoff effect may most likely have a protective 
function in the body over the short term. Therefore, there is no reason to believe that its function 
in the body is to cause adverse effects.

3.7.2 Seaweed composition  
The composition of brown seaweed (Laminaria sp.) is based on 18g water,  6.7 g raw proteins, 1.6 
g fat, 49.1 g carbohydrates, 5.4g fiber, and 19.2 g ash which consists of minerals or heavy metals 
that are accumulated in the biomass from the surrounding water (Arasaki & Arasaki, 1983). There is a 
huge variation in composition due to results obtained from many seaweed species (Holdt,2023a). 
Seaweeds contain anti-enzymatic activity, anti-microbial activity, and antioxidant activity. It is high 
in fibers, vitamins, and minerals such as Ca, Na, and Fe.  It is also high in carotenoids (fucoxanthin) 
and polyphenolic compounds which are good as antioxidants and UV protectors (Holdt, 2023a). 
The entry and exit of inorganic ions like iodine, are controlled by the specialized structure of the 
thick and soft cell walls of seaweeds containing polysaccharides including cellulose and chitin 
specially for the brown seaweed. This is crucial for the high concentrations of salt in the seawater 
environment. Furthermore, brown seaweed contains intercellular mucilage polysaccharides 
including alginic acid and fucoidan,  and storage polysaccharides including Laminaran (Arasaki & 
Arasaki, 1983). The laminaran, which is the storage sugar for brown seaweed, is placed inside the cell 
of the whole biomass of the seaweed, specifically in the vacuole (Holdt,2023a). 
3.7.3 Seaweed synthesis  
 Each seaweed cell is capable of doing photosynthesis, taking up nutrients, and being reproductive 
or growing vegetatively. The seaweed functions like a colony of macroalgae that have been glued

together which means they don’t have transport cells that transport the sugars from one end of 
the biomass to the others. All seaweed species take up nutrients over the whole biomass. This also 
means that seaweed doesn’t have a root because they will not be needing it for nutrient uptake. 
Instead, they have a holdfast that can look like a root or a small disc attached to e.g. a stone or

3.7.4 Cultivation methods  
Commercial seaweed production accounts for 20 % of the total aquaculture production with 
30,000,000 tones whereas cultivated seaweed accounts for 96% of total seaweed production. 
Cultivated seaweed production primarily happens in Asia while wild harvest in natural populations 
typically occurs in cold regions. Cultivation is a sustainable choice that does not exhaust resources 
e.g. fresh water supply or fertilizers and no need for land. With Cultivation it is easier to design the 
growth, and there is no mix of species. Some typical cultivation methods include closed 
bioreactors and open tank cultivation which are advantageous for growth control, and high 
maintenance but have a high cost. Furthermore, field cultivation is advantageous in having less 
costs, but is labor intensive and little controlled. These cultivation methods can be used for 
biomass growth (exponential growth) by taking the seaweed and getting the spores out. These are 
kept in lab nurseys (lag phase), then the spores are put on robes and start to germinate, and lastly, 
they are put out in the sea to grow. Regeneration can occur by partial harvest which includes 
skipping the lag phase and obtaining the same specific growth rate in a shorter time by cutting the 
seaweed from the sea right above the exponential growth zone. The lifecycle of seaweed is 
different depending on the type of species (Holdt,2023a). Sugar kelp has a macroscopic life phase, 
where it starts by releasing spores that come out as male and female that reproduce 
macroscopically, and then the fertilized egg will start to grow into seaweed that can be utilized 
(Arasaki & Arasaki, 1983). Cultivation methods can be used to manipulate the life cycle and change 
seasons to optimize the growth season and avoid biofouling (Holdt,2023a).

3.8.1 Role of iodine  
Iodine plays a key role in the environment where it is released in the form of aqueous sea 
elements and gases. Micro and macro algae play a key role in the transformation of iodine, mostly 
from seaweed.  Depending on algae species, commonly eatable seaweeds contain possibly 
between 9-99% of iodine which may be water soluble (Nitschke & Stengel, 2016). Three types of edible 
macroalgae seaweeds are rich in iodine including green algae (Chlorophyta), brown algae 
(Phaeophyta), and red algae (Rhodophyta). The iodine content depends on the different species 
and taxonomies. Brown algae which have the highest total biomass produced is also the richest 
source of iodine, followed by red algae and lastly green algae (Aakre et al., 2021). The brown algae 
kelp (Laminaria) contains the maximum level of water-soluble iodine by 99.2%, inorganic iodine (I-) 
up to 93%, organic iodine between 5.5 to 37.4%, and iodate (IO3) between 1.4-4.5%. Results 
indicate that various algae have different mechanisms for iodine enrichment and bioavailability, 
which relate to the ability of iodine to be absorbed and used by the body. (Hou et al., 1997). The 
iodine content in seaweed is strongly influenced by several factors such as harvesting area and 
geographical and seasonal variation. For instance, the amount is lower in spring and summer 
compared to autumn and winter (Stévant et al., 2018). Among living organisms, Kelps are the 
strongest iodine accumulators in the environment (See Fig. 5).

When laminaria is submerged in seawater and unstressed, it accumulates iodide (I-) with the help 
of vanadium haloperoxidase.  
3.8.1.2 Role of iodine in a stressed environment 
In the presence of oxidative stress, iodide is released to assist in detoxifying ROS which is present 
at the thallus surface outside the cell membrane. These ROS include aqueous oxidants like H2O2 
and gaseous oxidants like O3 which can oxidize iodide to iodate.  
3.8.2 Oxidative burst from seaweed caused by alginate–degrading pathogen  
In periods with oxidative stress, such as high tide when the seaweed is submerged in seawater, 
oxidative stress occurs as a result of an oxidative burst from the seaweed caused by an alginate-
degrading pathogen, specifically a bacteria in a biofilm that is initiated by oligosaccharides, namely 
oligoguluronates, that are the main trigger for the oxidative burst. The oligosaccharides have been 
identified, and their effect has been tested by placing oligosaccharides without pathogens in water 
with seaweed which resulted in an oxidative burst from the seaweed because it thought it was 
being attacked by pathogens. For this reason, there is significant evidence that oligosaccharides 
are the main cause of the oxidative burst of free radicals from the seaweed (e.g. hydrogen 
peroxide, hydroxyl radicals) which damages the cell membrane of the pathogen. (Küpper et al., 
2008). Molecular species of free radicals are capable to exist and containing an unpaired electron 
in an atomic orbital. They can act as oxidants or reductants due to their ability to either donate an 
electron or accept an electron from other molecules. ROS are oxygen-containing free radicals 
which are highly reactive species. ROS are capable in the nucleus and in the membranes of cells of 
damaging biologically important molecules such as DNA, proteins, carbohydrates, and lipids. Free 
radicals attack essential macromolecules resulting in cell damage (Lobo et al., 2010).   
3.8.3 Lower iodine content in seaweed in Spring and Summer  
Iodine act as an antioxidant to protect the cell membrane of the seaweed (from pathogen attack) 
which results in the oxidation of iodide to iodine and potentially other iodine oxides which are 
released into the seawater. Geographic locations or seasons with higher temperatures are optimal 
growth conditions for pathogens. Summer brings higher temperatures, and has therefore optimal 
==PAGE:24==
 
 
 
 
 
13 
growth conditions for pathogens which results in a higher incidence of oxidative burst from 
seaweed. This is the reason for the iodine content in the seaweed being lowest in spring and 
summer. The iodine concentration can be decreased by  50% or more during summer (Küpper et 
al., 2008).  
3.8.4 Aerosol particles released into the coastal atmosphere  
In contrast to Iodide from kelp being released in seawater, aerosol particles from molecular iodine 
are released directly into the coastal atmosphere due to the ozone-scavenging reactivity of iodide 
on kelp surfaces at low tide. (See Fig. 5).  Ozone is a gaseous oxidant from sunlight that can be 
stressed and oxidized iodine into iodate. Iodate is a prooxidant that at a certain time will meet a 
substance in the environment it can oxidize and deliver its oxygen atom to and thereby be 
reduced back to iodide or react on kelp surfaces and release molecular iodine into the coastal 
atmosphere (Küpper et al., 2008).  
3.9 Seaweed in ancient times  
Seaweeds have been harvested by many coastal inhabitants around the world (e.g., in Chile 
[14,000 BCE], Japan [13,000 BCE], China [2,700 BCE], Egypt [1,550 BCE], and India [300 BCE]). 
Seaweed is vital to traditional home remedies and folk medicines for the treatment of various 
predominant disorders and diseases. The earliest known evidence of the use of seaweeds in food 
and medicine was in South America, dated to approximately 14,000 -12,000 BCE from Monte 
Verde in Chile. Residues of several types of seaweeds were discovered in a midden which most 
likely originated from species of brown seaweed. The inhabitants of the Andean regions of South 
America sold the seaweed as stems in shops which were used as gum and chewed to treat goiter. 
The variety of seaweeds used in these countries as herbal medicine and home remedies was huge, 
compared to Western countries as well as Asian countries such as China and Japan (Peñalver et al., 
2020). The use of seaweeds as foods and traditional medicines by inhabitants of Asian coastlines 
was an ancient viewpoint and schooling that were far more believed. It placed disease prevention 
by way of a healthy diet before the treatment of diseases by way of drugs. Most of the species are 
a proper source of iodine and were mainly proposed to remedy certain thyroid matters such as 
goiter (Peñalver et al., 2020).

3.11 Traditional dietary lifestyle  
3.11.1 Practice of washoku – traditional Japanese cuisine  
According to Japanese food sciences, future Japanese cuisine looks to fresh twists for an age-old 
cuisine representative for washoku which was the ancient way of preparing Japanese food. 
washoku is a traditional Japanese cuisine that takes emphasis on community cohesion, natural and 
seasonal ingredients, and respect for nature through the sustainable use of natural resources. 
Practicing washoku in Japanese cuisine equals that you value the viewpoint that the future of food 
is about respecting the past while moving forward. The acknowledgment of washoku became 
more noticeable in 2013 when it was listed by United Nations Educational, Scientific, and Cultural 
Organization (UNESCO) on the Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity. 
Japan has since then used this profile in the country and across the world to gain interest in their 
nation's food concept and thereby allowed them to express how their cuisine can address some 
essential societal goals (Nature Research Custom Media, 2023).   
3.11.2 Traditional foods in Japan  
“Traditional foods in Japan are mostly of plant origin and high in health-promoting elements such 
as polyphenols and microbial enzymes involved in the fermentation of lactic acid, amino acids, 
oligopeptides, and oligosaccharide”  claims Keiko Abe, Professor of Applied Biological Chemistry at 
the University of Tokyo. Furthermore, Eriko Komiya, Director of the Food Cultures Office, of the 
Overseas Market and Food Cultures Division in the Ministry of Agriculture Forestry and Fisheries, 
explains that: “fermented foods are well known for their health benefits and are characteristic of 
washoku and other cuisines” (Nature Research Custom Media, 2023). A traditional Okinawan diet

foods, and medicinal plants. Moreover, marine foods, lean meats, fruit, medicinal garnishes and

Asian cultures, seaweeds are commonly cooked with foods containing goitrogens such as broccoli, 
cabbage, bok choi, and soy. Especially soy is a common and largely consumed staple source of the

3.11.3 Dietary health benefits of Japanese 
Eriko Komiya explains that the obesity rate and fat intake rate of Japanese people are low 
compared with other countries around the world and that these low rates relate to Japanese food 
culture and diet, for example, eating a nutritionally balanced meal using a variety of food materials 
and using soup stock, rather than oil, for cooking and flavoring. Moreover, according to a 2019 
World Health Organization report Japan also consistently maintains the longest life spans globally 
for both men and women (Nature Research Custom Media, 2023). According to a study of elderly 
Okinawans aged 99 and older, carried out by the Okinawa Centenarian Study (OCS) research group 
since 1977 for over four decades results indicate by several measures of health and longevity that 
the Okinawans can be claimed to be the world’s healthiest and longest-lived people (B. J. Willcox 
et al., 2017). Furthermore, Professor Abe emphasizes that traditional Japanese foods are being 
investigated for their health benefits as Japan has the potential to become a global leader in 
providing scientific evidence on specific foods' role in supporting a long and healthy life. However,  
Japanese eating practices need more research to gain knowledge about what strengthens their 
benefits and thereby increase the evidence base of the impact of the whole Japanese dietary 
lifestyle on health (Nature Research Custom Media, 2023).

lifestyle. Between 2016 and 2017 33.7% of women and 28.6% of men were reported by the 
National Survey of Health in Chile to have obesity (Gómez-Pérez et al., 2021). The poor diet in 
Chile is stated to be correlated to having the highest recorded sale of sugar-sweetened beverages 
worldwide in 2014 along with a high intake of sugar-sweetened beverages and consumption of 
ultra-processed and high-sugar foods, and sugary and salty snack foods before 2016 (Dillman 
Carpentier et al., 2020). Contrary to the Japanese diet, these eating patterns indicate that foods 
containing goitrogens such as soy which is a traditional staple food in Japanese cuisine, may most 
likely not be common in the Chilean diet as it is not a traditional staple food in the Chilean cuisine 
which primarily consists of meat, fish, and legumes. However, other traditional Chilean practices 
may promote some of the dietary health concerns contrary to the Japanese, such as the 
traditional use of large plates which may contribute to overeating and thereby obesity (Aguilera 
Miguel & Ferrada, 2021).  
3.12 The interaction between soy and seaweed for the T4 production 
3.12.1 – Inhibition of iodine uptake  
Soy and seaweed are consumed in large quantities in Asian countries. High iodine intake from 
seaweed consumption can cause health problems in individuals with for example pre-existing 
thyroid disorders (Zava & Zava, 2011). Iodine-induced goiter and hypothyroidism are common in 
Japan and can be reversed in most cases by limiting seaweed intake (Ishizuki et al., 1989). Soy, 
which is a commonly consumed goitrogenic food in Japan, contains phytochemicals that may 
==PAGE:30==
 
 
 
 
 
19 
competitively inhibit iodine uptake by the thyroid gland including inhibition of the incorporation of 
iodine into thyroid hormone including soy isoflavones (Zava & Zava, 2011).  
3.12.2 – Soy isoflavones  
Soy produces estrogenic isoflavones with Genistein as the major soy isoflavone synthesized by the 
soybean. Given the evidence from the literature, Genistein has both goitrogenic and estrogenic 
activity. Genistein has been shown to have an estrogenic effect in women assessed in a controlled 
trial of women given a dose of 30 g soy flour/day. Results showed estrogenic effects, including 
lengthening of the menstrual cycle. Soy effects on the thyroid involve the critical relationship 
between iodine status and thyroid function where iodine deficiency increases soy antithyroid 
effects and iodine supplementation becomes protective. However, for soy to cause overt thyroid 
toxicity additional factors may appear necessary besides iodine deficiency such as additional soy 
components, other defects of hormone synthesis, or additional goitrogenic dietary factors (Doerge 
& Sheehan, 2002).  
3.12.3 – Link between soy consumption and goiter  
Despite soy is believed to be safe for consumption, the possibility that widely consumed soy 
products may cause harm to the human population via estrogenic and goitrogenic activities is of 
concern. It is with evidence from animal and human studies, proposes a link between soy 
consumption and goiter. There is evidence for the binding of isoflavones to the estrogen receptor, 
in vivo estrogenicity and developmental toxicity, and estrogenic developmental carcinogenesis in 
rats. Moreover, there is evidence for rats consuming genistein-fortified diets, where genistein was 
measured in the thyroid at concentrations that produced dose-dependent and significant 
inactivation of rat and human thyroid peroxidase (TPO) in vitro. Furthermore, rat TPO activity was 
dose-dependently reduced by up to 80% (Doerge & Sheehan, 2002).  
3.12.4 Inactivation of thyroid peroxidase (TPO) by soy isoflavones 
As mentioned earlier, Thyroid peroxidase TPO is required for thyroid hormone synthesis. (See 
section 3.5.1, Fig. 3). An in vitro and in vivo study investigated the inactivation of thyroid 
peroxidase by soy isoflavones including Genistein and daidzein which were identified as the 
components in soy that inhibited TPO-catalyzed iodination and coupling for the synthesis of 
thyroid hormones. Besides TPO activity, these Soy isoflavones were investigated to inhibit 
==PAGE:31==
 
 
 
 
 
20 
Lactoperoxidase (LPO) activity which is a model enzyme that shares many structural and functional 
properties with TPO (Doerge & Chang, 2002).  
3.12.5 interaction between Genistein and Daidzein with TPO activity  
Genistein and Daidzein showed to have two influential ways of interacting with TPO activity 
including; I) the absence of iodine, and II) the presence of iodine. The theory is described by the 
thyroid hormone formation principle (See section 3.5.1, Fig. 3).  
I) 
Genistein and Daidzein were shown to be suicide substrates for TPO and LPO in the 
absence of iodide. This was demonstrated by the irreversible loss of both iodinating 
and coupling activities for thyroid hormone synthesis and shown by changes in the UV–
Vis spectrum of the enzyme.  
II) 
In the presence of iodide Genistein and Daidzein acted as substitute substrates that 
blocked tyrosine iodination through the formation of iodinated isoflavone derivatives, 
and thereby also severely decreased the rate and extent of genistein-mediated loss of 
peroxidase activity. However, genistein, the glucoside conjugate, was much less 
effective in inactivating TPO. (Doerge & Chang, 2002).  
3.13 Discussion on the reasons for Japanese healthy lifestyle   
3.13.1 Practice of washuko resulting in lower consumption of processed food  
Japanese can tolerate high amounts of seaweed. Part of the reason may be that they have been 
practicing washuko which is associated with a spirit of respect for nature. This practice may 
traditionally have led to a lower consumption of processed food, but naturally a higher 
consumption of plant-based food including seaweed which the Japanese people have gained a 
genetic selection toward over time. This also means that part of the reason for the Japanese 
healthy lifestyle may, according to this theory, be due to what they don’t eat rather than what 
they do eat. However, Japanese eating practices need more research to gain more knowledge 
about what strengthens their benefits and their dietary lifestyle on health. (Section 3.11.3).  
3.13.2 Interaction of soy in the Japanese diet with high consumption of seaweed and 
soy 
Other reasons for the Japanese healthy lifestyle may be due to other dietary sources. For example, 
besides seaweed, soy is a largely consumed staple source of the traditional Japanese diet. In cases 
when consuming both a high soy intake (containing isoflavones) and excessive seaweed 
==PAGE:32==
 
 
 
 
 
21 
consumption which is a high iodine source commonly in Asian countries like Japan, the high soy 
intake might even compensate for the negative effects of high iodine intake from excessive 
seaweed consumption. This can be explained by the mentioned mechanism of the blocked 
tyrosine iodination through the formation of iodinated isoflavone derivatives from soy, which may 
competitively inhibit the incorporation of iodine into thyroid hormones (See section 3.12.5). In 
conclusion, this means that soy, may compensate for a too-high seaweed consumption and 
potentially function as an essential source in the prevention of incidence of thyroid disorders 
caused by excessive iodine intake from seaweed consumption.  
3.14 Discussion on the interaction of soy and its toxicity on the Chilean and Danish diet    
3.14.1 Interaction of low consumption of soy and high consumption of seaweed in the 
Chilean diet  
Soy is not a largely consumed staple source of the traditional Chilean diet. This means that the 
Chilean diet does not consist of a high soy intake to compensate for the negative effects of high 
iodine intake from excessive seaweed consumption. The lack of soy in the Chilean diet prevents 
the occurrence of this compensatory mechanism (See section 3.12.5). Consequently, this may 
promote the incidence of thyroid disorders. However, this is possibly not the case for the original 
people who have consumed seaweed for many years, as their prolonged exposure may have led to 
genetic adaptations. Additionally, when considering the Wolff-Chaikoff effect, it is possible that 
these factors together compensate for the excessive iodine intake. 
3.14.2 Interaction of low consumption of soy and seaweed in the Danish diet  
Soy and seaweed are not largely consumed staple sources of the traditional Danish diet. Since 
there are no consumption data available on seaweed in Denmark, the Danish diet is assumed to be 
associated with a low consumption of seaweed and soy suggesting a low concern. However, 
consumption of soy in certain population groups in the absence of seaweed in the diet may be 
linked to a concern, and support the explained mechanism about Genistein and Daidzein to be 
suicide substrates for TPO and LPO in the absence of iodide that will block activities for thyroid 
hormone synthesis, which may promote the incidence of thyroid disorders. (See section 3.12.5). 
Certain healthy population groups who have a high seaweed consumption may be protected by 
the Wolff-Chaikoff effect due to the adaptation to excessive iodine intake.

3.16 Soaking experiments  
In this section, 3 types of soaking experiments will be introduced to define the experiment from 
the work of this thesis and to consider the validity between scientific methods and practice.  
The types of soaking experiments introduced are;  
1) Scientific experiments using standard laboratory methods 
2) Scientific experiments that aim to mimic the homemade cooking techniques for the 
preparation of seaweed dishes performed by consumers 
3) The experiment from the work of this thesis investigates the wiping and soaking of 
seaweed according to a traditional Japanese recipe.    
Existing experimental studies from the literature have shown that the processing of seaweed plays 
a major role in iodine reduction. However, some of these studies have not taken into 
consideration the recommended techniques outlined in cookbooks. Instead, they have mainly 
focused on iodine reduction using standard laboratory methods or homemade cooking techniques 
like soaking in tap water, blanching, and cooking, but without following the instructions in 
cookbooks.   
==PAGE:37==
 
 
 
 
 
26 
The experimental part of the work of this thesis specifically focuses on following the 
recommendations provided in cookbooks. The purpose of the experiment is to determine if 
traditional Japanese wiping of kombu with a damp cloth before soaking, as suggested in Japanese 
cookbooks, influences the iodine content of the kombu, and the final dish. I have chosen to 
investigate this area because, to my knowledge, it has never been performed. Additionally, the 
investigation aims to determine if wiping and subsequently soaking seaweed, according to the 
traditional Japanese recipe, results in a lower iodine content compared to only soaking the 
seaweed. Dried kombu has been used in this experiment, based on traditional recipes for dashi 
(See section 3.15.2).   
3.16.1 Scientific experiments using standard laboratory methods 
Some experimental studies aimed to investigate the effect of the treatment of sugar kelp by 
soaking it in water for the iodine content (Nitschke & Stengel, 2016; Sun et al., 2022).  
3.16.1.1 – (Nitschke & Stengel, 2016) 
A study from 2016 aimed to asses iodine in edible brown (Alaria esculenta), red (Palmaria 
palmata), and green (Ulva intestinalis) macroalgae, and quantify the iodine loss during processing 
(Nitschke & Stengel, 2016). The processing procedures involved washing, dehydration, rehydration 
(soaking), and cooking (boiling). The seaweed was collected from NE Atlantic on the west coast of 
Ireland in August 2014 during a spring low tide. The species were maintained in aerated natural 
seawater at 15 °C. Washing (10 min. in deionized water) and dehydration (air-, oven- and freeze-
drying) only marginally affected iodine levels. This suggests that gentle preservation techniques 
prevent iodine loss. However, iodine was reduced by 10% in A. esculenta.  On the other hand, 
rehydration (1–24 h soaking in deionized water) significantly reduced iodine by 62 % in A. 
esculenta, by 15 % in P. palmata, and by 10 % in U. intestinalis. The iodine levels of all three 
species decreased further upon boiling (20 min in deionized water). Results indicate a higher 
iodine loss from the rehydration of dried brown seaweed (A. esculenta) compared to the other 
species (Nitschke & Stengel, 2016).  
3.16.1.2 (Sun et al., 2022) 
A study from 2022 aimed to investigate the effects of different pretreatment methods and dietary 
factors on the form and bioavailability of iodine in Laminaria japonica (Sun et al., 2022). Fresh L. 
japonica was obtained from Xiangshan, Zhejiang, China. L. japonica samples were rinsed with 
==PAGE:38==
 
 
 
 
 
27 
ultrapure water three times to remove debris, then naturally dried and cut. The dried L. japonica 
was pre-treated by the traditional household methods, including soaking (30 min. in 500 ml 
ultrapure water), boiling (10 min. in 500 ml water), and steaming (10 min. by water vapor). The 
untreated L. japonica had an iodine content of (456.16 ± 5.12 µg/g). All of the treated samples had 
significantly lower iodine content than the untreated L. japonica (456.16 ± 5.12 µg/g). Boiled L. 
japonica had the lowest iodine content (280.71 ± 8.95 µg/g) which had an iodine loss of (38.46 ± 
1.96%). This was followed by the soaked L. japonica (339.38 ± 10.36 µg/g) which had an iodine loss 
of approximately 27%. Steamed L. japonica had the highest iodine content (379.98 ± 17.84 µg/g) 
which had an iodine loss of approximately 17%. Results indicate that boiling and soaking are most 
sufficient to remove iodine from dried L. japonica with ultrapure water compared to steaming 
(Sun et al., 2022).

3.16.2.3 (Correia et al., 2021) 
A study from 2021 aimed to investigate the seaweed rehydration and boiling for the impact of 
iodine, selenium, and total arsenic contents and health benefits for consumption (Correia et al., 
2021). The study focused on processes that intended to mimic the homemade cooking techniques 
performed by consumers. In this case, soaking treatment in cold tap water for 5 minutes at room 
temperature was investigated followed by boiling different seaweed species including S. latissima, 
L. digitata, U. pinnatifida, and C. crispus. Results showed that a fraction of 7,6% of iodine from 
dehydrated sugar kelp was removed by soaking for 5 minutes at room temperature reducing the 
iodine content from 2510 ug/g dw to 2319 ug/g dw. Further boiling in 20 minutes removed 55% of 
the iodine reducing the iodine content further to 1128 ug/g dw. For dehydrated L. digitata results 
showed that a fraction of 43,1% of iodine was removed by soaking for 5 minutes at room 
temperature reducing the iodine content from 4228 ug/g dw to 2406 ug/g dw. Boiling in 20 
minutes removed 77,6% of iodine reducing the iodine content further to 945 ug/g dw. (Correia et 
al., 2021). However, it is worth noticing that an iodine content of (1128 ug/g DW) in sugar kelp 
from the study (Correia et al., 2021) is surprisingly high after 20 minutes of boiling compared to an 
iodine content of 343 ± 41 mg/kg-1 dw-1  in sugar kelp by blanching at 80 °C achieved in the study 
(Nielsen et al., 2020). It could be an important factor to consider that the sugar kelp used in the 
(Correia et al., 2021) study was already dried compared to the sugar kelp used in the (Nielsen et 
al., 2020) study which was fresh. It is possible that the iodine content in the dried sugar kelp is 
bound harder and therefore may be more difficult to rinse out with water. This may explain the 
higher content of iodine in the dried sugar kelp after processing compared to the fresh.

3.16.3.2 Key findings from the study (Nielsen et al.,2020) 
The control has been developed based on the key findings from the study  (Nielsen et al., 2020).

3.17 Regulations in different countries 
The iodine content of algae or foods containing algae is currently not regulated in Europe (Blikra et 
al., 2022). France is the only European country contributing with defined limits of potentially 
harmful compounds in seaweed for human consumption. A threshold of a maximum of 2000 mg 
iodine/kg dry matter for all edible seaweed species is set by the French Agency for Food, 
Environmental, and Occupational Health & Safety (ANSES). ( AFSSA Request no.2007-SA-0007; 
April 2007) (AFSSA, 2009). However, these limits are not legally binding or health-based (Stévant 
et al., 2018). In the united states, the maximum threshold limit of iodine in a product is 5000 ug 
per gram of dry matter. (Holdt, 2023b). None of the limits are based on health considerations.

4.4. Chemical analysis of iodine  
4.4.1 Instrumental technique  
Iodine and other elements at trace levels in biological samples are measured by ICP-MS 
(inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry) analytical technique. ICP-MS analysis includes 6 
elements: the sample introduction system, inductively coupled plasma (ICP), interface, ion optics, 
a mass analyzer, and detector. Fig. 13 illustrates a diagram of the instrument (Wilschefski et al., 
2019).  
 
FIG. 13 - ICP-MS analysis (Wilschefski et al., 2019) 
In the sample introduction system before being introduced into the ICP, liquid samples are 
aerosolized by a nebulizer. Commonly an autosampler and a peristaltic pump are used to deliver 
the sample to the nebulizer. After being aerosolized by the nebulizer, the sample enters the spray 
chamber which has a few significant purposes that include selectively filtering out the larger 
aerosol droplets that are created by the nebulizer and smoothing out nebulization pulses 
produced by the peristaltic pump. This is a crucial act because the plasma itself is inefficient at 
dissociating large droplets (>10 μm diameter). Afterward, the nebulized samples in the sample 
introduction system with created fine aerosols are subsequently transferred to the argon plasma. 
The high-temperature plasma is an ionized gas of positively charged ions and free unbound 
electrons that atomizes and ionizes the sample, generating ions that are then extracted through 
the interface region and into a set of electrostatic lenses called ion optics. The ion optics focus and 
==PAGE:50==
 
 
 
 
 
39 
guides the ion beam into the quadrupole mass analyzer. The mass analyzer separates ions 
according to their mass-charge ratio (m/z), and these ions are measured at the detector.  
4.4.2 Advantages and limitations  
ICP-MS is a sensitive method that is precise with a large analytical range and low detection limit. 
Furthermore, it is coupled with simple sample preparation and short analysis time.  It is a method 
that allows very high sample throughput in the laboratory with a low sample volume. It has a 
multi-element capability which allows multiple elements to be measured simultaneously in a 
single analysis. Moreover, it has a high resolution and tandem mass spectrometry and its (triple-
quadrupole) instruments offer a very high level of interference control. However, ICP-MS 
instruments are quite expensive (Wilschefski et al., 2019)   
4.4.3 Sample preparation for ICP-MS 
Before analysis biological samples are usually diluted or thermally digested. Most Common 
diluents include dilute acids (e.g. nitric acid, hydrochloric acid) or alkali (e.g. ammonium hydroxide, 
tetramethylammonium hydroxide). Deionized water is used as a diluent. However, acidic or 
alkaline diluents are preferred in most cases because some elements are unstable in pure water 
(Wilschefski et al., 2019). Specifically, for this method, Iodine was analyzed in the aqueous 
solution, as most iodine compounds are readily soluble in water. Especially inorganic iodine is 
highly soluble in water. The most common inorganic form of iodine in brown seaweed is Iodide (I-). 
(See section 3.8). The measurement of total iodine content in kombu (Laminaria digitata) was 
based on the standard (DS/EN 17050:2017), method of sampling and analysis for determination of 
iodine in animal feed by ICP-MS.

4.6 Discussion 
4.6.1 The experiment from the work of this thesis investigates the wiping and soaking 
of seaweed according to a traditional Japanese recipe 
Based on the established hypotheses, it can be concluded that the traditional Japanese method of 
wiping dried kombu with a damp cloth before soaking treatment, does not reduce the iodine 
concentration. This also means that wiping and subsequent soaking of seaweed does not lead to a 
lower iodine content compared to only soaking the seaweed. Around 95-98% of the iodine from 
the dried seaweed was leached in the tap water, indicating that kombu (Laminaria digitata) was 
highly effective for the soaking process. Iodine was found to be relatively loosely attached to the 
seaweed but interestingly, even when the seaweed was wiped, no removal of iodine was 
observed. This strongly indicates that wiping the seaweed does not eliminate iodine.

5.1 Identification of health-based guidance value  
This section introduces the health-based- guidance value for iodine in Europe, Japan, and America, 
and discusses their uncertainties. The discussion is based on several studies, including 
dose/response assessment from intervention studies, epidemiological studies, and case reports.  
5.1.1 EFSA’s recommended daily tolerable upper intake level of iodine  
EFSSA arguments for setting up the upper limit of 600 ug/day for the iodine intake primarily based 
on dose/response assessment from two American intervention studies of short duration (two 
weeks) supported by a five-year epidemiological study (Gardner et al., 1988; Paul et al., 1988; 
Thomas et al., 1978).  
5.1.1.1 – intervention Study of (Gardner et al., 1988)  
The first study was an interventions study from 1987 performed on 30 healthy men between the 
ages of 22-40 years who were all chosen randomly to participate in the study (Gardner et al., 
1988). The study aimed to determine the effect of low-dose oral iodide supplementation on 
thyroid function in normal men. All subjects were treated with 500, 1500, and 4500 µg/iodide per 
day for 2 weeks. Blood samples were collected on day 1 and day 15 for measurements of serum 
T4, T3, T3-charcoal uptake, TSH, protein-bound iodide (PBI), and total iodide (Gardner et al., 1988). 
Samples were collected on these days for baseline urinary iodine (I) and creatinine (Cr) excretion. 
==PAGE:57==
 
 
 
 
 
46 
The purpose to measure urinary Cr was to ensure that the 24-hour urine collection was complete. 
Urinary iodide excretion was measured to estimate the dietary intake of iodine which was 
approximately 300 µg/person/day resulting in a total ingested dose of approximately 800, 1800, or 
4800 µg iodide/day (Nielsen Elsa et al., 2014). Meanwhile, TRH tests were obtained before and at 
the end of the period for iodide administration. Results showed a significant decrease in T4 
concentrations and free T4 index values at the high doses of 1500µg/day and 4500 µg/day. A 
significant increase in the serum TSH response to TRH occurred at the 500 µg iodide/day dose, 
while the two larger doses of iodide 1500 µg/day and 4500 µg/day resulted in both basal and TRH- 
stimulated serum TSH concentrations. No changes were seen for serum T3 concentrations and T3 
charcoal uptake at any doses. These findings suggest that iodide supplementation within a high 
daily intake of iodine has a significant inhibitory effect on thyroid hormone secretion in healthy 
men in the short term. However, the 14-day study period was insufficient to draw definitive 
conclusions about its impact on thyroid function.  
Moreover, limitations of the study include the fact that blood samples and urine samples were 
only collected at the start and end of the study,  making it impossible to assess any variations in 
serum concentration or iodine excretion during the 2 weeks. However, it is also uncertain when 
the Wolff-Chaikoff effect may have occurred, as blood samples were only obtained on day 1 and 
14. (See section 3.6.1).  Furthermore, the men participating in the study were a small group of 
relatively young volunteers limiting its representativeness across different age and population 
groups (Gardner et al., 1988).  
5.1.1.2 – intervention Study of (Paul et al., 1988)  
Another study from 1988 was performed on volunteers with normal thyroid function including 9 
euthyroid men between the age of 26 and 56 years and 23 euthyroid women between the age of 
23 and 44 with 5 age-matched controls (Paul et al., 1988). The study aimed to observe the effects 
of the thyroid gland function after small increases in iodine intake. All subjects were treated with 
doses of iodine every 12 hours for 14 days. Samples were collected for baseline urinary iodine (I) 
and creatinine (Cr) excretion. Furthermore, the basal and TRH-stimulated serum TSH 
concentrations, T4, and T3 were measured. The subjects received 250, 500, and 1.500, µg of 
supplemental iodine daily to estimate the effect of the doses on thyroid function during the 14 
days (Paul et al., 1988). Urinary samples were collected to estimate the dietary intake of iodine 
==PAGE:58==
 
 
 
 
 
47 
from urinary iodine excretion with approximately 200µg/person/day resulting in a total ingested 
dose of approximately 450,700 or 1700 µg iodide/day (Nielsen Elsa et al., 2014). Results showed a 
significantly lower level of T3 and T4 in the serum compared to normal values for the serum levels 
with an intake of 1500 µg iodine daily. It also showed an increase in serum TSH concentration and 
the serum TSH response to TRH.  However, no changes were observed in the pituitary-thyroid 
function for the administration of 500 µg or 250 µg iodine daily. There was simply no significant 
effect for those who were exposed to 500 or 250 µg of iodine daily. For those who were exposed 
to 1500 µg iodine an increased expression of iodine in the urine was observed from 211 ± 41 to 
1.360 ± 74 from day 1 to day 7, suggesting the occurrence of the acute Wolff-Chaikoff effect, 
which prevents the absorption of iodine in the thyroid gland. (See section 3.6.1). Afterward, it was 
observed that the excretion level was stable at 1.308 ± 58 until day 14.  The escape of the Wolff-
Chaikoff effect may have contributed to the effect stopping and the absorption of iodine in the 
thyroid gland starting again. On day 15, blood results showed a significant decrease in serum T4 
and T3 and an increase in TSH to regulate the decrease.  
There were no observed clinical effects during the study, but as it only lasted for 14 days, it is 
unclear what potential health effects may arise from long-term iodine intake at these levels. (Paul 
et al., 1988). Though, goiter may occur after a long period of high exposure to iodine (Mu et al., 
1987).  EFFSA found that observations of T3 and T4 were changed but still within the normal 
range, suggesting that the effects were not adverse.  and which information from this study they 
found sufficient to establish a recommended upper limit. However, this study does not provide 
information about iodine intake in older individuals, pregnant women, children, and other 
population groups with diagnosed health problems. The representation of the study is weakened 
by the fact that participants, including those in the control group, are generally healthy individuals 
of the same age. Moreover, another significant limitation of the study Is the limited number of 
participants (Paul et al., 1988).  
5.1.1.3 – Discussion of the two intervention studies  
In both intervention studies, the participants were healthy volunteers who were randomly 
assigned. As a result, These studies may not fully represent the impact of iodine intake on 
different age groups and populations with varied lifestyles. For instance, Children’s endocrine 
system and pituitary gland, are not fully developed making them more susceptible to harmful 
==PAGE:59==
 
 
 
 
 
48 
substances. This susceptibility can potentially interfere with their developing systems and affect 
thyroid function, leading to delays in mental development, growth, and a decrease in IQ, among 
other possible effects.  
5.1.1.4 – Epidemiological Study of (Thomas et al., 1978)  
A five-year study from 1978 by Stockton and Thomas was considered with exposure to similar 
iodine intakes based on iodinated drinking water (1 mg/L) supplied to 750 prison male and female 
inmates which received an average dose of 30 µg/kg bw (equivalent to approximately 1800 µg 
iodide/day) (Thomas et al., 1978). It turned out that 177 female inmates gave birth to 181 infants 
with no thyroid-related adverse effects. The group size and duration of this study were adequate 
compared to the other two studies. Though, the difficulty of this study was that it was an 
epidemiological observation study. There was a lack of information regarding the participants’ 
dietary and fluid intake as well as variations in exposure time. Moreover, this study did not 
provide information on the potential inhibitory effects of T3 and T4 hormones, which could have 
strengthened its reliability.(Thomas et al., 1978).  
5.1.1.5–EFSA’s recommended daily tolerable upper intake level of iodine 
EFSA decided to use the 600 ug/day Upper limit of iodine for adults in the European population 
based on the two intervention studies on adults from the USA. The results from the studies were 
based on the total iodine intake from the doses that gave the Lowest Observed Adverse Effect 
Level( LOAEL).  In both cases, LOAEL was observed at the dose of 1500 µg/day. Moreover, the 
additional iodine intake from the regular diet was also taken into account which in total resulted in 
1700 µg iodide/day and 1800 µg iodide/day respectively.  These numbers were dived with an 
assessment factor of 3, the default factor for nutrients, which resulted in the UL value of 600 
µg/day iodine for adults in the European population. The UL was also chosen with the argument 
that results were within normal standards and excluded the few studies with results far over the 
normal range. The way the Scientific Committee on Food (SCF) set up the upper limits for children 
was simply done by adjusting the UL for adults based on body surface area (body weight 0.75). The 
tolerable upper intake level (UL) of iodine in different age groups is summarized in Table 5 below.

important factor to consider because iodine excess may trigger thyroid diseases such as 
lymphocytic Hashimoto’s thyroiditis (LT).  
Serum samples were collected to test for anti-microsomal (MAb), anti-thyroglobulin (TgAb), 
second colloid antigen antibodies (CA2-Ab), and TSH binding inhibitory immunoglobulins (TBII). 
Furthermore, thyroid growth-stimulating activity (TGI) was tested by IgG-purified affinity 
chromatography. This was examined by two different methods including a sensitive cytochemical 
bioassay (CBA) using guinea-pig thyroid explants and a mitotic arrest assay (MAA) using a 
continuous rat thyroid cell line (FRTL-5). Results showed no increase of LT in children with endemic 
iodine goiter. Between the two groups of children levels of MAb, TgAb, and CA2-Ab did not differ 
significantly.  In children with endemic iodine goiter thyroid growth-stimulating immunoglobulins 
(TGI) were the major antibodies found. Results from goitrous children obtained from CBA showed 
12 of 20 (60%) were positive for TGI while results for healthy children showed 0 of 12 (0%) were 
positive for TGI. Similar results were obtained for MAA which indicated a good correlation 
between the results of CBA and MAA. A good relationship with clinical goiter was obtained from 
the results of maximal TGI activity in dose-response CBA indicating a possible role of these 
antibodies. The study concluded, therefore, that autoimmune growth factors such as TGI may play 
an essential role in the pathogenesis of thyroid growth in this condition and that no increase of LT 
==PAGE:63==
 
 
 
 
 
52 
in children with endemic iodine goiter occurred (Boyages et al., 1989). Certain aspects of the study 
need to be considered. Although the study compares cell data from humans, the results are based 
on the examination of cells and do not account for the impact of excessive iodine on children with 
goiter. The study mainly relies on information about changes in the mechanism. Furthermore, 
animal cells have been used as controls for chromatography measurements which may provide 
variability in data (Boyages et al., 1989). However, ATSDR used this study to highlight that the 
NOAEL was based on a sensitive endpoint in children who represent a sensitive subpopulation 
(Boyages et al., 1989). 
5.1.4.2 – Study of (Mu et al., 1987) - NOAEL for Children 
The argument was supported by another study from 1987 performed on school children from two 
villages in central China including the Gaojiabu village and Huanglou village with iodine 
concentration in drinking water being respectively 462,5 ug/l and 54 ug/l (Mu et al., 1987). 120 
children in the age group 7-15 years were examined from the Gaojiabu village while in the control 
group from the Huanglou village 51 schoolchildren in the age group 7-15 were examined.  
Measurements were recorded for height, sitting height, and weight. Furthermore, clinical thyroid 
status was assessed and goiter size was graded with modified WHO criteria. The thyroid was 
assessed by ultrasonography in 10 Gaojiabu children with the highest grade of goiters and 11 age 
and gender-matched controls. In 8 of the 10 children from Gaojiabu thyroid  131I uptake was 
measured. Furthermore, cognitive function was assessed with the Hiskey-Nebraska test of 
learning. All children in both groups were clinically euthyroid and neurologically normal.  
Results showed that goiters were present in 65% of the 120 children from the Gaojiabu village and 
15.4% of the 51 children from the Huanglou village, respectively. 
In children from the area with iodine excess, there was no evidence of neurological deficit or 
impairment in cognitive function. Growth measurements and intellectual performance were 
similar in the two groups. Urinary iodine concentrations were measured with a technicon auto-
analyzer. Blood was respectively collected from 30 and 28 children from Gaojiabu and Huanglou. 
T3, T4, and free thyroxine (FT4) concentrations were measured with radioimmunoassay (RIA).  
Moreover, serum thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH) concentrations were assayed by a sensitive 
immunoradiometric assay.

Results showed that children from the high-iodine Gaojiabu village had lower mean serum 
triiodothyronine and higher serum free thyroxine and serum thyroid-stimulating hormone 
concentrations than the children from the control Huanglou village. In the high-iodine village, two 
cases of overt hypothyroidism were detected. The signs of hypothyroidism are often difficult to 
detect in group surveys. However, two children with very high serum TSH concentrations and very 
low thyroid hormone values appeared (Mu et al., 1987).  
5.1.4.3 – NOAL applicable for children according to ATSDR  
According to ATSDR the findings from this study also supported that the NOAEL could be 
applicable for children who represent a sensitive subpopulation and therefore this explains why 
they did not find an assessment factor needed to adjust the NOAEL to account for human 
variability in sensitivity. These studies supported an MRL of 0.01 mg/kg bw/day derived from 
chronic-duration (>365 days) oral exposure to iodine-based on a NOAEL of 0.01 mg/kg bw/day and 
a LOAEL of 0.029 mg/kg bw/day for sub-clinical hypothyroidism in healthy human children 
(Boyages et al., 1989; Mu et al., 1987).  
5.1.4.4 – Study of (Chow et al., 1991) – NOAEL for elderly adults  
Moreover, it was also supported by another study from 1991 that the NOAEL could apply to 
elderly adults who may represent another sensitive subpopulation (Chow et al., 1991). The study 
was an intervention study performed on 30 healthy elderly adult females without evidence of 
thyroid peroxidase antibodies (TPA). The subjects received daily doses of 500 µg I/day (potassium 
iodide) for a period of 14 or 28 days. Urinary iodide measurements were taken for analysis of pre-
existing dietary iodine intake levels and the result showed a pre-existing dietary iodine intake of 
approximately 72-100 µg/day and the total iodide intake was therefore approximately 600 µg/day 
(0.0086 mg/kg/day). Moreover, blood samples were collected for measurements and the result 
showed a significant decrease in serum concentrations of FT4 and a significant increase in serum 
TSH concentrations of the subjects relative to a placebo control group. The changes did not cause 
clinically significant depression in thyroid hormone levels of the elderly adult females (Chow et al., 
1991).

5.1.5.4 Estimated UL for adults  
Taking the values from the studies as the NOAELs and using an assessment factor of 10, the ULs 
were estimated as 2.8, 2.7, and 3.5 mg/day, respectively. Based on the findings from these studies 
the values support the estimated UL of 3.0 mg/day for adults.  
5.1.5.5 Estimated UL for children based on an international epidemiological study (Zimmermann 
et al., 2005)   
For children aged 6-17 years the UL was set to 500 µg/day based on an international 
epidemiological study from 2005 examining children worldwide (Zimmermann et al., 2005). The 
study was based on collected samples of 3319 children between the age of 6-12 years from 5 
different continents with iodine intakes ranging from adequate to excessive. The study aimed to 
investigate whether high dietary intakes of iodine in children result in high thyroid volume (Tvol), a 
high risk of goiter, or both. Tvol was measured by ultrasound. Furthermore, measurement of the 
urinary iodine (UI) concentration was also taken. Statistical regressions were done on Tvol and 
goiter using age, body surface area, sex, and UI concentration as covariates.  
The median UI concentration ranged from 115 ug/L in central Switzerland to 728 µg/L in coastal 
Hokkaido, Japan. 31% of children had UI concentrations above 300 µg/L, and 11% had UI 
concentrations above 500 µg/L. Furthermore, 59% of children had UI concentrations above 500 
µg/L in coastal Hokkaido, and 39% had UI concentrations above 1000 µg/L. Results showed that 
chronic iodine intakes approximately twice those recommended in the range of 300 –500 µg/L, 
indicated by UI concentrations,  do not increase Tvol in children. However, UI concentrations 
above or equal to 500 µg/L are linked to increasing Tvol, which provides insight into the adverse 
effects of chronic iodine excess (Zimmermann et al., 2005).  
5.1.6 Discussion on approaches  and uncertainties from studies on adults    
The UL for the daily intake of iodine for different population groups has been approached in 
different ways, worldwide based on several studies which all come with uncertainties. (see 
section).  
5.1.6.1 EFSA  
EFSA chose an untraditional approach by using the LOAEL and an assessment factor of 3  per 
default. (See section). However, normally a traditional approach aims for using the NOAEL value 
and an assessment factor of 10 for substances capable of inducing systemic effects in the body, 
such as iodine. EFSA made practically more sense in using their approach compared to the 
==PAGE:68==
 
 
 
 
 
57 
traditional approach because the traditional approach would cause UL for iodine intake in adults 
to be significantly low, approximately 50 µg/day based on the studies from (Gardner et al., 1988; 
Paul et al., 1988).  
5.1.6.2 ATSDR  
ATSDR chose an approach by using the NOAEL value of 0,01 mg/kg bw/day in healthy adult 
humans, also based on the studies from (Gardner et al., 1988; Paul et al., 1988). Though, ATSDR 
chose not to use an assessment factor to adjust the NOAEL to account for human variability in 
sensitivity. They found their approach more suitable compared to the traditional approach which 
would otherwise also cause the UL for iodine in adults to be 50 µg/day. This would practically not 
align with WHO regulations, which recommend a daily mean intake of iodine of 150µg for adults. 
(See section 5.1.3).  
5.1.6.3 Health Canada  
Health Canada chose to use the LOAEL value based on the two studies but chose an even lower 
assessment factor of 1.5 per default which meant that the recommended upper limit for adults 
was even higher (See section 5.1.2). The UL was based on the observations that the studies did not 
reveal any clinical adverse health effects in the two weeks of exposure. There was a small but 
significant fall in T4 and T3 and a rise in TSH to upregulate within normal limits. These studies 
indicated therefore that iodine intakes equal to or above 500 µg/day over several weeks induce 
subtle, reversible changes in pituitary-thyroid function in adults, with values remaining within the 
normal range, probably by inhibiting thyroid hormone release. However, there are limitations of 
the studies that need to be considered. The studies were limited by being based on only a few 
participants and they were only based on healthy adults. Moreover, the daily iodine intake from 
the regular diet before the trial was not analyzed but estimated which may contribute as a source 
of uncertainty in the actual total amount of iodine each subject had. Furthermore, there is no 
information about whether the studies were double-blinded or single-blinded. A double-blinded 
study would be preferable to prevent bias.  As the studies were only based on two weeks it is not 
known what further health-based effects could rise from the intake of iodine at those levels and 
for instance how the Wolff-Chaikoff effect would change after two weeks. (See section 3.6.1). 
Based on the studies the escape phenomena of the Wolff-Chaikoff effect may kick in after 7 days 
when T4 levels start to stabilize again.  Many studies have examined the effects of excess iodine 
==PAGE:69==
 
 
 
 
 
58 
on thyroid hormone or the occurrence of thyroid diseases. Japanese people's high tolerance for 
iodine may rise from their ancient history of using seaweed and preparing iodine-rich seaweed 
dishes, which they may have built an adoption toward over time. (See section 3.10). Several 
Japanese intervention studies based on excessive iodine intakes support this statement and this 
also explains the upper limit for daily iodine intake of 3.0 mg for Japanese adults (Miyai et al., 
2008; Namba et al., 1993). An iodine intake from seaweed of 35-70 mg/day in 7-10 days increased 
the TSH levels and decreased the serum free thyroxine (FT4) and or free 3,5,3'-triiodothyronine 
(FT3) concentrations slightly within normal limits. Afterward, within a long period of daily intake of 
15 g kombu for 55-87 days, the TSH levels continued to increase but the T4 and T3 levels were 
unchanged (Miyai et al., 2008).  
This could indicate that along with the high tolerance of iodine, the Wolff-Chaikoff effect may have 
been triggered since the T4 and T3 concentration became unchanged afterward and such high 
levels were tolerated with reversible effects. (See section 3.6). However, there is no clear evidence 
of how long time the Wolf-Chaikoff effect would last in the body. Furthermore, the threshold for 
tolerating high levels of iodine may most likely also be different from country to country and from 
person to person. This discrepancy may be triggered by factors such as the varying incorporation 
of iodine-rich seaweed into regular diets, and differences in its occurrence, accessibility, and 
exposure. Consequently, some of these significant factors could explain why recommended UL for 
daily iodine intakes differ worldwide.  
5.1.6.4 Discussion on the approaches  
EFSA’s advice of 600 µg/day for adults is built on a conservative approach. In reality, the tolerance 
for iodine may be considered higher due to the Wolff-Chaikoff effect, therefore an iodine intake of 
1100 µg/day based on Health Canada regulations is also reasonable. It is especially reasonable 
because the UL values are obtained from the same studies.(Gardner et al., 1988; Paul et al., 1988). 
Furthermore,  ATSDR regulations with a NOAEL value of 0,01 mg/kg bw/day in healthy adult 
humans have also used arguments from the two studies (Gardner et al., 1988; Paul et al., 1988).  
(See section 5.1.1).   
5.1.6.5 Discussion on  the approaches – ATSDR – a traditional approach  
ATSDR uses a more traditional approach by using a NOAEL value compared to EFSA and Health 
Canada which use a LOAEL value. However, the justification for why ATSDR does not use an 
==PAGE:70==
 
 
 
 
 
59 
assessment factor is weak, if it is only based on arguments from other studies suggesting that the 
NOAEL they found could also apply to children and the elderly subpopulation (Boyages et al., 
1989; Chow et al., 1991; Mu et al., 1987). This is not a good reason to justify that they did not find 
it necessary to use an assessment factor to adjust the NOAEL to account for human variability in 
sensitivity. This approach does not account for sensitivity variability in the subgroups which is also 
an important factor to consider even if a NOAEL value is used. However, the approach by EFSA and 
Health Canada has not been considered a better option just because they use an assessment 
factor. Their approach is based on a LOAEL value which is a dose with effect within the normal 
limit. Even though it is not an adverse effect there was still observed a significant effect. 
Moreover, it should be noted that all individuals experiencing the same relative effect will not 
necessarily remain within normal limits. The studies were only based on healthy individuals. If the 
studies were based on other sensitive population groups results would maybe have looked 
different, and showed to be out of normal limits.   
 5.1.6.6 Discussion on the approaches – EFSA – a conservative approach  
The approach by EFSA was done by nutrient experts. this can be considered a problem because no 
matter what approach they use, the approaches will be considered inconvenient concerning a 
pollutant perception. Intake of a nutrient, in reality, will never equal an ideal intake with zero 
pollution. This is something nutrient people do not account for in the same way as toxicologists 
do. Moreover, ATSDR does also have an inconvenient approach. Their way of thinking about 
pollutants is no different, and their concept cannot be argued.  
5.1.6.7 Conclusion on the approaches  
For this reason, no approach is considered the best approach, because there is no right approach.  
5.1.7 Discussion on approaches  and uncertainties from studies on children and the 
elderly subpopulation  
There are data on the adverse effects of chronic exposure to high iodine intakes, particularly in 
children. The effect of high iodine intake in children can ideally be examined by intervention 
studies. An ideal design will require extreme methods such as high iodine exposure over a long 
period. Such studies are for good reason not allowed because they can be complex and give rise to 
harmful effects on children. The two short-duration intervention studies are examples of studies 
that lack information about the effect of iodine intake on children.  EFSA also uses the 5-year 
epidemiological study by (Thomas et al., 1978) to support their arguments. The study is good in 
==PAGE:71==

terms of examining a high amount of pregnant female inmates who shows to give birth to healthy 
children even though they have received a high amount of iodinated drinking water for a long 
time, but the study cannot be used because it cannot conclude anything when there are no results 
to show any effects.    
5.1.7.1 ATSDR 
ATSDR’s arguments from other studies that support that the NOAEL they found could also apply to 
children and the elderly subpopulation can be questionable (Boyages et al., 1989; Chow et al., 
1991; Mu et al., 1987). (See section 5.1.4). 
The study from (Chow et al., 1991) for the elderly subpopulation is based on an intervention study 
limited by being a short-duration study with few healthy elderly females. Furthermore, only one 
dose was studied which does not provide fulfilling information. 
(Boyages et al., 1989) is a study based on cells that alone cannot provide information about what 
iodine excess can do to goiter children in the body. However, serum samples were collected for 
comparing thyroid antibodies from 29 goitrous children from an iodine excess area and 26 healthy 
children. This study alone cannot base an argument. However, the study was supported by 
another study (Mu et al., 1987) which can be considered stronger because it was a group survey 
performed on school children from two different villages in central China with respectively high 
and low iodine concentrations in drinking water. The study was based on clinically euthyroid and 
neurologically normal children which made it easier to detect two cases of overt hypothyroidism 
in the high-iodine village. 
An international epidemiological study from 2005 performed on children aged 6-17 years showed 
variations in mean urinary iodine concentrations from different countries ranging from 115 µg/L in 
central Switzerland to 728 µg/L in coastal Hokkaido and (UI) concentrations above or equal to 500 
µg/L linked to increasing thyroid volume. Based on the international study, the UL was set to 500 
µg/day for children aged 6-17 years. These findings may indicate that people from Asia are more 
exposed to iodine, and can tolerate it more than people from Western countries. The 
recommended UL for iodine intake from EFSA for children aged 15-17 years is 500 µg/day. The 
Japanese UL for iodine intake of 500 µg/day is recommended for Japanese children aged 10-11, 
indicating a significant difference in tolerance of iodine between different populations.

5.1.8 pregnant and lactating women 
Based on the evidence about increased thyroid hormone production, increased renal iodine 
losses, and fetal iodine requirements in pregnancy that lead to dietary iodine requirements to be 
higher in pregnant adults than in nonpregnant adults, EFSA’s UL of 600 µg/day iodine for adults 
and Health Canada’s UL of 900 µg/day could respectively indicate to be acceptable for pregnant 
and lactating women (Leung et al., 2011). 
Based on findings indicating that iodine is critical to fetal health, WHO suggested that women get 
more of it than usual during pregnancy and when they are breastfeeding. Therefore, WHO 
recommends a higher daily iodine requirement for pregnant and lactating women including an 
adequate iodine status during pregnancy through a recommended dietary intake of 250 µg of 
iodine daily (Vitamin and Mineral Requirements in Human Nutrition Second Edition, 2004).

5.2.2.2 Consumption Data   
Data on dried kombu (Saccharina japonica) was selected because it is a brown seaweed frequently 
used in a variety of traditional dishes in the Japanese kitchen, especially in dishes like dashi, a 
commonly used stock in Japanese recipes. (See section 3.10.3). As kombu is readily available in local 
shops, it is made convenient for consumers to incorporate it into home-cooked meals. Japan has a 
very high consumption of seaweed. In Japan, seaweed is part of approximately 21% of Japanese 
meals. 20-38% of the Japanese male and female population aged 40-79 years consumes seaweed 
more than five times per week followed by 29-35% who consume seaweed three to four times per 
week, 25-35% consume seaweed one to two times per week, 6-13% consumes seaweed one to two 
times per month while 1-2% rarely consumes seaweed. According to these numbers, the majority 
of Japanese people consume seaweed regularly (Zava & Zava, 2011). In 2006 the average annual 
consumption of kombu per household in Japan was 450 g dry weight with older individuals 
consuming up to 4 times more than those under the age of 29.  Assuming a stable consumption 
pattern for kombu until 2022, and considering that the average number of members per private 
household in Japan is 2.22 in 2022, the daily average kombu consumption in Japan would be 0,56 
g/person/day. This roughly equals 1 g/person/day dry weight (Japan: Number of Members per Household 
2022 | Statista, 2022; Zava & Zava, 2011). Some sources report that average seaweed consumption is 
estimated between 4-7 g/day dried weight (Teas et al., 2004, 2007). Another source reports that 
the Japanese population consumes on average 10.4 g of seaweed per person per day (Murai et al., 
2021). However, an average consumption of 1 gram of kombu per day per dry weight is significantly 
lower than the recommendations in the traditional Japanese cooking books, which suggest a 
consumption per serving of around 10 grams of kombu. (See section 3.15.2). If seaweed 
consumption was limited to once a week such as observed in a significant part of the population 
according to (Zava & Zava, 2011), it is likely that the average seaweed consumption of 10g/person/day, 
as claimed by (Murai et al., 2021), is not being met. In a food frequency questionnaire on the 
Japanese kombu association website from 2010, survey respondents states they consume kelp 
(assuming kombu) at a rate of 27.5% once per week followed by 25.5% once per month, 18% three 
or four times per week, 15,9% once every few months, and only 6.1% nearly every day (Zava & Zava, 
2011). Unfortunately, data regarding the number of participants and population groups in the survey 
was inaccessible, likely due to a lack of available data, or because the information on the 
association’s website was not translated into English. In 2015 The Government of Japan, The 
Ministry of Environment provided recommendations about iodine intake partly based on the

5.3 The use of results from the effects of a traditional preparation of seaweed on 
the Japanese and Chilean exposure to optimize the seaweed chips production in 
Denmark.   
5.3.1 introduction  
Danish producents commonly use brown seaweeds such as kombu (Laminaria digitata) or 
(Saccharina latissimi) to produce seaweed products like seaweed chips. However, there have been 
cases with high iodine levels from these products that have been considered to pose a risk to the 
consumer (RASFF, 2023). In Denmark, regular chips are consumed in large quantities.  Therefore, it is 
essential to access whether seaweeds with low iodine levels, such as cochayuyo (Durvillaea 
antarctica)  and nori (Porphyra sp.), also pose a potential risk for the consumers in Denmark.  To 
address this concern, an exposure assessment was performed to evaluate the intake of iodine from 
seaweed with both high and low iodine contents in Denmark. The exposure assessment considered 
data on the occurrence and consumption of seaweed, taking into account the uncertainties 
associated with these sources.

5.4.1 Uncertainty in occurrence  
The concentration of iodine has the potential to vary significantly due to various factors. The 
geographic location and season are considered important parameters for the exposure assessment. 
The concentration of iodine in seaweed is greatly influenced by these factors. The reason for this 
variation in concentration from geographic location and season is explained in (Section 3.8.). 
Another huge contributor to variation in concentration is also observed with variation between 
different species, within the same species, and even within the same sheet/leaf. (See Table 9). 
Moreover, cultivation methods may also affect the variation in concentration. The number of 
samples analyzed is also considered significant for concentration variation. The small size of 
samples has been observed in publications. This is either due to no access to publications or no 
translated publications. Furthermore, It is unknown how much soaking treatment is used, and how 
much concentration is reduced by soaking treatment. As a result, concentration data are generally 
considered to be a major factor contributing to risk assessment uncertainty. 
5.4.1.1 Uncertainty in occurrence – Japan  
The most significant contributor to uncertainty in Japanese exposure is the variation in 
concentration data resulting from various samples of the same species, various samples of different 
species, and the number of samples. Furthermore, It is unknown how much concentration is 
reduced by traditional soaking treatment in Chile. As a result, concentration data are considered to 
be a major factor contributing to risk assessment uncertainty. 
5.4.1.2 Uncertainty in occurrence – Chile  
The most significant contributor to uncertainty in Chilean exposure is the variation in concentration 
data resulting from various samples of the same species, various samples of different species, and 
the number of samples. Furthermore, it is unknown how much concentration is reduced by 
traditional soaking treatment in Chile. As a result, concentration data are considered to be a major 
factor contributing to risk assessment uncertainty. 
5.4.1.3 Uncertainty in occurrence – Denmark   
The most significant contributor to uncertainty in the Danish exposure is the variation in 
concentration data resulting from various samples of the same species, various samples of different 
species, and the number of samples. As Denmark does not have a tradition for soaking treatment, It 
is unknown how much soaking treatment is used and how much concentration is reduced by 
==PAGE:88==
 
 
 
 
 
77 
soaking treatment. As a result, concentration data are considered to be a major factor contributing 
to risk assessment uncertainty. 
5.4.2 Uncertainty in consumption  
The consumption of iodine has the potential to vary significantly due to various factors. For 
instance, to avoid overestimation or underestimation of iodine intake from seaweed consumption, 
it is important to know whether the seaweed is dry weight or wet weight. Uncertainty in the 
estimation of iodine intake can arise If dietary records or surveys fail to specify the use of dry 
weight, wet weight, or a combination of the two when recording daily or yearly seaweed 
consumption. There can be huge uncertainty in the consumption of seaweed in countries where 
seaweed is not a staple food. The total iodine amount may not be known because of iodine from 
other dietary sources other than seaweed people consume. Furthermore, the iodine after ingestion 
in the body can also influence the estimation, knowing that normally almost all ingested iodine is 
absorbed, and excreted in the urine. (See section 3.1.1). The excretion of iodine in the urine may 
therefore also be a good measure of iodine intake. In conclusion, uncertainty from consumption 
can be both large and small.   
5.4.2.1 – Uncertainty in consumption – Japan  
As seaweed is a staple food in Japan it is known that most people consume it. For this reason, 
consumption data for seaweed are published in Japan e.g. data from a food survey for kelp 
consumption reported in 2010. In Japan consumption data of seaweed may therefore have a low 
contribution to uncertainty in risk assessment.  
5.4.2.2 – Uncertainty in consumption – Chile 
As seaweed is a staple food in Chile it is known that most people consume it. For this reason, 
consumption data for seaweed are most likely published in Chile, but there is no access to it. 
Consumption data of seaweed in Chile may have a low contribution with uncertainty in risk 
assessment. This can be stated even though there was no access to consumption data to support 
the assumption. 
5.4.2.3 – Uncertainty in consumption – Denmark  
As seaweed is not a staple food in Denmark, it is not known how many people consume seaweed. 
The frequency and quantity of eating different types of seaweed e.g. nori, cochayuyo, and kombu 
(Laminaria digitata) and (Saccharina latissima), can be assumed to vary a lot between people, and 
consumption data are not recorded for it. It is assumed that only a minority of the Danish 
==PAGE:89==
 
 
 
 
 
78 
population consumes seaweed. Consumption data of seaweed in Denmark may have a high 
contribution to uncertainty in risk assessment. Consumption data, such as the amount and 
frequency of the different seaweed species consumed, must be collected to improve the exposure 
assessment.  
5.4.3 Uncertainty in exposure 
5.4.3.1 – Uncertainty in exposure – Japan   
There is a large uncertainty in the exposure assessment for Japan due to the huge uncertainty from 
occurrence data.   
5.4.3.2 – Uncertainty in exposure – Chile  
There is a large uncertainty in the exposure assessment for Chile due to the huge uncertainty from 
occurrence data.   
5.4.3.3 – Uncertainty in exposure – Denmark  
There is a large uncertainty in the exposure assessment for Denmark due to the huge uncertainty 
from both occurrence and consumption data. Denmark is therefore considered to be the largest 
uncertainty contributor to the exposure assessment.

6. Discussion  
According to both my own experiment and the available literature, there is a traditional practice in 
Chile and Japan of soaking seaweed before cooking, which can affect its chemical composition and 
potentially influence the risk of a high intake of iodine. However, my findings indicate that the 
traditional practice of wiping seaweed for the preparation of dishes in Japan does not have an 
impact on the iodine content of the seaweed.  A key finding from the experiment is that when dried 
kombu (Laminaria digitata) is soaked in cold tap water (11°C) for 2 hours, approximately 95-98% of 
the extracted iodine is leached out. This indicates that soaking treatment has a significant effect on 
reducing the iodine concentration in dried kombu. (See section 4.5, Table 4).  
Other experimental studies on soaking treatment of fresh seaweed have shown a lower loss of 
iodine. For example, one study (Stévant et al., 2018) found that soaking fresh sugar kelp in tap 
water at 32°C for 1 hour resulted in an 88% reduction of iodine. (See section 3.16.2.1). Another 
==PAGE:102==
 
 
 
 
 
91 
study (Nielsen et al., 2020) demonstrated a 78% reduction of iodine in fresh sugar kelp soaked in 
fresh tap water at 30°C for 300s. Blanching fresh sugar kelp at optimized conditions (≥45 °C and ≥30 
s) led to an iodine reduction of up to 88%. (See section 3.16.2.2). These findings suggest that the 
soaking treatment of dried seaweed may be more effective in removing iodine compared to the 
soaking treatment of fresh seaweed. However, a study by (Correia et al., 2021) contradicts these 
observations. Their results showed that only 43% of the iodine in dried kombu (Laminaria digitata) 
was removed by soaking treatment for 5 minutes while boiling for 20 minutes resulted in a 78% 
iodine loss. (See section 3.16.2.3). These results indicate, that soaking treatment for 2 hours may 
have removed a fraction of iodine between 43 to 78% from dried kombu. These findings 
demonstrate a lower degree of iodine removal compared to my experiment, where 95-985 of the 
extracted iodine was leached out in the tap water, indicating a high efficacy in removing iodine 
from kombu. Furthermore,  the results from (Correia et al., 2021) also suggest that a lower fraction 
of iodine is removed from dried seaweed compared to the mentioned experimental studies on 
soaking treatment of fresh brown algae in the literature. In conclusion, while the results are not 
conclusive in indicating that iodine is more easily removed from dried seaweed compared to fresh 
seaweed, my experiment and several other studies support this observation.  
According to an analysis conducted by the Danish Veterinary and Food Administration, certain 
brown algae products like chips have been found to have high levels of iodine. This along with the 
rapid alert, has raised concerns about potential risks associated with these products. (RASFF, 2023). In 
Denmark, regular chips are consumed in large quantities. Therefore, it is essential to access 
whether seaweed chips with low iodine levels, such as cochayuyo (Durvillaea antarctica)  and nori 
(Porphyra sp.), also pose a potential risk to consumers in Denmark. It is therefore essential to 
examine and address these issues to ensure the safety and minimize potential risks associated with 
these products. Based on the findings from the risk assessment, daily consumption of kombu chips 
without soaking treatment has been considered to pose a high risk for the Danish population. 
Based on the findings from the risk assessment, adults, adolescents, pregnant and lactating women, 
and children from 9-13  years can safely consume a daily consumption of kombu chips with soaking 
treatment (See section 5.7.1).  However, there is uncertainty regarding whether it poses a low risk 
for the 9-13 age group due to various factors, such as the health-based guidance value, the 
variation in iodine concentration within and between species, geographic location and seasonal 
differences, limited sample size, and the effectiveness of soaking treatment in removing iodine. 
==PAGE:103==
 
 
 
 
 
92 
Additionally, since there is no consumption data available for Danish seaweed chips, concern may 
also arise for the 9-13 age group.  
Based on the findings from the risk assessment a daily consumption of seaweed chips with low 
iodine content without soaking treatment is considered to pose a high risk for the Danish 
population. According to the risk assessment, high daily consumption of nori chips by children, 
particularly those in the 9-13 age group, may pose a risk. (See section 5.7.10.4). A low daily 
consumption of nori chips by children, particularly those in the 4-6 age group, is unlikely to pose a 
risk. Low consumption of cochayuyo chips without soaking treatment may pose a high risk for 
children, adolescents, and pregnant and lactating women, but not for adults. (See section 5.7.7).  
However, it cannot be definitively determined that it poses a low risk for adults due to various 
uncertainties identified in the risk assessment as stated earlier. Therefore concerns may also arise 
for adults. In conclusion, at least one of the seaweed species with low iodine levels, specifically  
Cochayuyo has showed to potentially poses a risk for the Danish population. This is a valid reason to 
argue that low iodine level chips without soaking treatment may pose a risk for the Danish 
population.  
Based on the results of the risk assessment, it has been determined that consuming seaweed chips 
with low iodine content with soaking treatment daily does not pose a high risk for adults, 
adolescents, pregnant and lactating women, and children between the ages of 9 and 13.   
Nori chips, prepared with soaking treatment, may not pose a risk for the Danish population. (See 
section 5.7.9). High consumption of cochayuyo may not pose a risk for children 9-13 years, 
adolescents, pregnant and lactating women, and adults. (See section 5.7.6). However, it is uncertain 
whether it poses a low risk for children aged 9-13 years due to the mentioned factors from the risk 
assessment contributing to uncertainty. However, low consumption of cochayuyo may not pose a 
risk for children from 4 years of age. In conclusion, at least one seaweed species with low iodine 
levels, specifically Cochayuyo, has clearly shown a potential risk for children up to 8 years of age. 
The level of uncertainty in the Danish risk assessment is large to argue that the concerns of risk for 
children between age groups are different. Therefore it is reasonable to argue that daily 
consumption of low iodine-level chips with soaking treatment may pose a risk for children in all age 
groups.  
However, it is important to note that individuals who have a high daily consumption of seaweed 
over a short period, exceeding the health-based guidance value, may be protected by the Wolff-
==PAGE:104==
 
 
 
 
 
93 
Chaikoff effect. (See section 3.6.2). Furthermore, it should also be taken into account that absorbed 
iodine has a short half-life, and almost all absorbed iodine is completely excreted in the urine. (See 
section 3.1.1). Hence, occasional excessive iodine intake from seaweed is unlikely to cause adverse 
effects such as thyroid disorders. However, long-term high daily consumption of seaweed is not 
recommended, as the Wolff-Chaikoff effect may only protect individuals from adverse effects, for a 
short period of 7-14 days. (See section 3.6.1). Moreover, individuals who have a high seaweed 
consumption and high soy intake may be protected by isoflavones from soy, which interact with the 
iodine from seaweed and provide a protective function (See section 3.14.2). On the other hand, 
findings suggest that a large soy consumption with a lack of seaweed in the diet might lead to 
negative effects. (See section 3.13.2).   
6.1 Future perspectives and recommendations 
6.1.1 Future perspectives 
Further research should be conducted to explore the observation from our experiment, which 
discovered that soaking dried seaweed is more effective in removing iodine compared to soaking 
fresh seaweed, as previously reported in the literature. This investigation may have the potential to 
provide valuable processing recommendations for seaweed producers in Denmark.  
6.1.2 Recommendations  
Iodine can only vaporize in its natural state as (I2). When hydrogen iodide (HI) is dissolved in water, 
it becomes hydroiodic acid and completely dissociates into ionized I- + H+. (HI (Hydroiodic Acid), 2023). 
This means that the iodine leached from the kombu in the tap water cannot vaporize because it is 
not in its natural state. Consequently, there may be a concern regarding the high iodine content in 
the leached water. To mitigate this risk, producers and consumers should avoid using water with 
leached iodine when preparing dishes or products. They can do this by either straining the water or 
discarding it and using fresh water, depending on the recipe or procedure. Læsø Tang, a producer in 
Denmark, follows these procedures when preparing seaweed-based products like hummus. They 
soak the seaweed and chickpeas in tap water for 12-15 hours, allowing the iodine to leach into the 
water. Then, they strain the soaked ingredients to remove the iodine content before boiling them 
in fresh water. (See section 3.18.3.3).  
According to results from the Japanese risk assessment, traditional cold or hot method techniques 
can be used to prepare dashi for adults and adolescents. (See section 3.15). however, when 
==PAGE:105==
 
 
 
 
 
94 
preparing dashi for children the usual traditional technique is not sufficient to mitigate the risk of 
high iodine content from kombu, leached in the water. In these cases, the stock water containing 
kombu should be replaced with fresh water. Interestingly, this practice is not traditional in Japan. 
Furthermore, results from the Japanese risk assessment, showed that a concern may be observed 
of a daily average consumption of (6 g) kombu by children (See section 5.5.1.5). Japanese children 
are therefore advised to vary their consumption between regular chips and seaweed chips, ad to 
avoid daily consumption of seaweed chips. Similar concerns are observed in Chilean risk assessment 
for children who consume nearly half a pack (14g) of cochayuyo chips daily. (See section 5.6.1.6). 
Chilean children are advised to vary their consumption between regular chips and seaweed chips 
and to avoid daily consumption of seaweed chips.  
In Japan, the consumption of seaweed chips is not part of the country’s culture. This is likely due to 
the high levels of iodine found in kombu, which is commonly eaten. As per Japanese traditions, 
Danish seaweed producers are not advised to produce chips made from seaweed species with high 
iodine contents such as kombu (Laminaria digitata) and (Saccharina latissima). Instead, it is advised 
to use seaweed species with lower iodine content like Chilean cochayuyo or Japanese nori, which 
are readily available in the Danish market.  Imported seaweeds of these types can be used for chips 
production in Denmark by following traditional Japanese and Chilean guidelines for soaking 
treatment of seaweed (See sections 3.10.3 and 3.10.4). Adults, adolescents, and pregnant and 
lactating women can safely consume seaweed chips of low iodine content with soaking treatment. 
However, it is not recommended for children to consume seaweed chips daily even with soaking 
treatment. Given that soaking treatment is not a traditional practice in Denmark, it is 
recommended for the Danish population to vary their diet between seaweed chips and regular 
chips. It is also not recommended to consume seaweed chips daily.

8. Conclusions and recommendations  
1.1) 
Wiping of dried seaweed may reduce the iodine concentration.   
• The experimental work of this thesis showed that traditional wiping of dried 
seaweed does not reduce the iodine concentration. However, the total extracted 
iodine from the kombu of (95-98%) was leached in the tap water by traditional 
soaking treatment.  
1.2) 
Observation from experiment   
• An observation from the experimental work of this thesis indicated that soaking 
dried seaweed is more efficient in removing iodine compared to soaking fresh 
seaweed as observed in existing literature.  
2) Traditional preparation of seaweed in Chile and Japan in the household mitigates the risk 
of adverse effects resulting from excessive iodine intake.  
• Based on the Japanese risk assessment, preparing traditional dishes for children in Japan, 
such as dashi using the cold and hot method, may pose a risk to children due to the high 
concentration of leached iodine from seaweed in the stock water. The risk can be mitigated 
by replacing the stock water containing seaweed, with fresh water. However, it is not a 
traditional Japanese practice to replace the water.   
• Further, the daily average consumption of kombu may pose a risk to Japanese children.  
• Based on the Chilean risk assessment daily average consumption of cochayuyo chips may 
pose a risk to Chilean children. 
• Following the traditional Japanese and Chilean guidelines for soaking treatment of seaweed 
species with low iodine contents for chips production in Denmark, may pose a low risk for 
adults, adolescents, and pregnant and lactating women.

Interview with Rie Ladefoged from Læsø Tang:    
 
Cookbooks: 
Rie from Læsø Tang said: Kazumi grew up in Japan and moved to Denmark as an adult. She 
says they soak seaweed the day before. I think it is because their seaweed is much thicker. If 
they had to cook rice, they put a piece of seaweed in the water that they had to cook the 
rice in. Kazumi didn't know there were cookbooks for traditional Japanese old-time food, 
because it was something you learned from your mother, how to treat the seaweed. But 
then she still managed to find the cookbooks for me, which she forwarded to Susan.  
2. Danish Food and Veterinary Administration  
==PAGE:114==
 
 
 
 
 
X 
I said: “We don't have much of a tradition of soaking in Denmark in the household”.  
Rie said: "But that could be made a regular part of the cookbooks - Then the Danish Food 
Agency can say that it must be the recommendation”. 
Kombu (Laminaria digitata): 
Rie said: "I have moved away from using kombu (Laminaria digitata), because in 2018 we 
had a heat wave, as we also have this year”.  
“Before 2018, fishermen put out fishing nets in these areas where kombu (Laminaria 
digitata) and sugar kelp (Saccharina latissima) were found. 80% was sugar kelp and 20% was 
kombu (Laminaria digitata). after the heat wave In 2018, kombu (Laminaria digitata) was 
down to somewhere between 2 and 5%. Therefore there is not much up here where they 
have put out yarn. Now I am eco-certified and have specific harvesting areas, but it is also 
very limited what is to find there”.  
"I save on it. if I'm going to make a good soup, I use kombu (Laminaria digitata), because it 
has much more umami content. Ole G. Mouritsen has found out that only the Danish kombu 
(Laminaria digitata), sugar kelp, and palm seaweed have umami”. 
Sugar kelp and bladderwrack: 
Rie said: “I mainly use sugar kelp and bladderwrack. It is easiest to harvest, most of it. If you 
hang it on a clothesline, or a fishing line, it will be sun-dried in a greenhouse with clear 
windows. It is sensitive to hang outside like the Japanese. As soon as it starts to rain, the 
seaweed absorbs its moisture again. It becomes greener, and pigmentation starts when 
exposed to direct sunlight”. 
Products and process: 
Rie said: “Pickled seaweed, mustard, and Jam are made by boiling the fresh seaweed, along 
with other ingredients for 20 minutes. “When I make jam, I use 8% dried sugar kelp. I boil 
the ingredients. Moreover, I boil bladderwrack with sugar, vinegar, honey, and more. When 
I make Mustard products, I also use Bladder Wrack. The dried seaweed goes through a fuel 
processor before being homogenized in a stone mill. Finally, it is transformed into the 
products”.  
“Hummus, on the other hand, is made by soaking kombu (Laminaria digitata) and chickpeas 
in tap water for 12-15 hours. The soaked ingredients are then rinsed, and boiled in fresh 
water until the chickpeas are cooked. Then the water is strained off.  
==PAGE:115==
 
 
 
 
 
XI 
Læsø salt boils salt according to medieval techniques, and mix bladder wrack in it. I make 
the product. I also mix bladderwrack in flour for baking shortbread, for sale in-store. 
Seaweed chips are dried in a dehydrator at 60 °C (gentle drying) and sold as a snack”. 
Dried seaweed in packs: 
Rie said: “Dried bladderwrack, sugar kelp, and saw seaweed are sold In packs. Some order 
50 g, otherwise 20 g is fixed”.  
Collaboration with other companies: 
Rie said: “We put dried seaweed in schnapps and vodka for a week, pours on small bottles 
for sale. A gin company “Knaplund” put sugar kelp into the gin with a slightly salted sea 
taste. Løkken Brewery brews beer on Læsø sugar kelp”.  
Safety and quality: 
Rie said: “Seaweed loses color over time when exposed to sunlight. Keeping our seaweed 
fresh is difficult as I have many customers in the summer. I harvest bladderwrack and saw 
seaweed, so they are used throughout the year. They are stored in large plastic boxes with 
lids at 16 °C in the summer, probably a little colder in the winter”. 
Seaweed used as fertilizer in Denmark:  
Rie said: “My brother had picked up seaweed on the beach In the sand. He used it as 
fertilizer In the kitchen garden e.g. for potatoes. It gave tasty potatoes”. 
A study at DTU carried out by a student 
Rie said: “A study at DTU was carried out on the cultivation of potatoes with seaweed from 
the beach. The study showed a 2/3 higher protein content in the potatoes grown on 
seaweed. This suggests that people back then knew that seaweed was useful, in the time 
before it was possible to do analyses, but did not know what it was that made it useful. 
The DTU student back then, who carried out the study, also suggested that people had to be 
careful when using kombu (Laminaria digitata) because of the high iodine content. She 
further implied that they did not use kombu (Laminaria digitata) anymore in Norway 
because of that”.  
Rie Said: “So for me what you have found out is very useful”.
