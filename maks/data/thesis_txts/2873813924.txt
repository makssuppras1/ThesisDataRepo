Abstract
The transformation of the energy sector towards a carbon-neutral system requires a large-
scale expansion of renewable energy sources (RES) and supporting flexibility technologies.
Battery energy storage systems (BESS) are among the most promising options to integrate
high shares of RES by mitigating variability, providing balancing, and enhancing reliability.
This thesis assesses the capacity requirements and economic feasibility of RES and BESS
needed to achieve Germany’s 2030 Net Zero targets, focusing on the government’s ambition
for an 80% RES share.
A cost-minimizing capacity investment model with hourly resolution and stochastic weather
scenarios is applied to a German reference system. Scenario analysis varies demand, car-
bon prices, and policy constraints. Based on the modelled results, the financial viability
for RES and BESS build-out is evaluated.
The results indicate that an 80% RES share by 2030 is unlikely under current build-
out rates, especially for wind, and that capacity needs are highly sensitive to demand
growth. In most high-RES scenarios, both RES and BESS are not financially viable under
the current market design. Contracts for Difference (CfDs) and Capacity Remuneration
Mechanisms (CRMs) are discussed as key instruments to bridge the investment gap and
ensure that suﬀicient capacity is deployed to meet Net Zero targets.

Introduction
The consequences of climate change necessitate a transition from today’s predominantly
fossil fuel-driven energy systems to greener forms of energy consumption. This transfor-
mation poses significant technical, economic, and systemic challenges. Among the most
central elements of this transition is the large-scale integration of RES, particularly wind
and solar power, into the electricity system (IEA, 2024b).
RES offer a sustainable and scalable solution to decarbonizing power systems, yet their
inherent variability introduces substantial complexity.
The fluctuating nature of wind
and solar generation demands new levels of flexibility and resilience from power systems.
Ensuring the availability of electricity at all times under these circumstances becomes a
key challenge. Addressing this challenge requires a comprehensive understanding of both
the capacity build-out required for RES and the supporting infrastructure and market
design needed to enable their effective deployment.
BESS are among the most promising technologies to support the integration of high shares
of RES. They can store excess electricity during periods of high RES production and dis-
charge it when production is low, thereby contributing to system balancing and reliability.
In addition, BESS can enhance grid stability by participating in ancillary service markets.
The economic case for BESS has significantly improved in recent years. Battery costs
have fallen from almost 800 $/kWh in 2013 to below 140 $/kWh in 2023, contributing to
a rapid global increase in installed battery capacity, from 100 GWh in 2015 to 2,400 GWh
in 2023, mostly in the form of electric vehicles (EV) (IEA, 2024a). Despite this growth, a
large share of the battery fleet is not yet fully leveraged to support grid stability or RES
integration. Greater utilization of existing home-scale storage and significant deployment
of grid-scale BESS will be required to meet future RES targets (AhmadiAhangar et al.,
2024).
In 2023 alone, 85 GW of BESS power was installed globally, with utility-scale systems
comprising 65% of this capacity. Yet to align with a 2050 net-zero pathway, the Interna-
tional Energy Agency estimates that global BESS capacity must reach 1,200 GW by 2030
(IEA, 2024a).
Achieving this target will not only demand substantial investment in infrastructure and
supply chains but also require a conducive market environment that supports the economic
viability of both RES and BESS investments.
Without appropriate market signals and mechanisms, insuﬀicient returns could hinder the
deployment of necessary BESS capacity. In turn, this would limit the system’s ability to
absorb high shares of renewable energy, ultimately delaying or derailing the transition to
a carbon-neutral electricity system (Setiadi et al., 2018). Thus, the success of the energy
transition hinges not only on technical feasibility but also on the economic viability of
large-scale RES and BESS integration.
In light of these challenges, this thesis investigates the financial feasibility of deploying the
required RES and BESS capacities under various power system development scenarios.
The analysis is based on a reference system that mirrors key characteristics of the German
power market and its 2030 net-zero targets.
Germany serves as an insightful example for this analysis, as it is a highly industrialized
country undergoing an ambitious energy transition, facing challenges that are similar to
those in many other industrial nations.

While the model is not a complete replica of the German system, its design allows for

By modeling cost-optimal capacity build-outs for different future scenarios, this thesis
generates insights into potential RES shares, necessary BESS capacities, and associated

and BESS investments. Ultimately, the study aims to assess the plausibility of achieving
net-zero targets and provide actionable recommendations for market design that supports
a cost-effective and realistic path toward a 100% renewable power system.

Literature Review
The literature review is divided into two sections. The first section provides a brief intro-
duction to various perspectives on the future German power market and net-zero targets,
aiming to offer a comprehensive overview of the ongoing discourse and place the results
of this thesis into context. The second section focuses on reviewing literature related to
capacity planning and market modeling in power systems, as well as financial feasibility
analysis.
2.1
Situation Today and Potential Development Paths for the
German Power System
As mentioned in Chapter 1, this thesis will apply the developed methodology to a reference
system based on the German power system. Germany represents an insightful example
of managing the energy transition challenge, being an industrial economy that aims to
become net-zero by 2045 (BMWK, 2023).
For 2030, the German government aims for an 80% RES share in the electricity generation
mix. To achieve this, a RES capacity of 115 GW onshore wind, 30 GW offshore wind, and
215 GW of solar PV power is to be installed by 2030 (BMWK, 2023).
Compared to the installed capacity in 2024, this represents an increase of 55 GW for
onshore wind, 21 GW for offshore wind, and 138 GW for solar PV (Bundesnetzagentur,
2025). To fully utilize this capacity, estimates for required BESS capacity in 2030 range
from 50 GWh (FrontierEconomics, 2023) to 104 GWh (Wille-Haussmann et al., 2022),
reflecting differences in modelling approaches and sensitivities to key input assumptions,
yet consistently pointing to the need for a substantial scale-up. Given that installed BESS
capacity in 2023 was only 11 GWh, of which 8 GWh were residential units (BMWK, 2023),
the planned expansion of both RES and storage infrastructure is evidently ambitious.
However, these build-out targets are based on the assumption of a massive increase in
electricity demand. The total demand in 2024 was 464 TWh (Bundesnetzagentur, 2025),
while the 2030 net-zero targets project a demand of up to 750 TWh (BMWK, 2023).
This forecasted increase is based on the assumption of fast-progressing electrification of
the heating and transport sector. As this electrification has faced headwinds and at the
same time the German industrial electricity consumption has decreased, the total German
electricity consumption has actually decreased over the last years (Bundesnetzagentur,
2025). While this trend may shift in the upcoming years, the target of 750 TWh remains
subject to considerable doubt. Obviously, if the power demand was lower in 2030, less RES
and BESS capacity would be required to meet the 80% target, making the target more
realistic. McKinsey recently published an analysis of future German electricity demand,
estimating demand in 2030 to be 530 TWh (McKinsey, 2025).
Thus, this thesis will
analyze the optimal RES and BESS capacity mix to meet the net-zero targets for different
scenarios, aiming to capture the complete range of potential outcomes.
2.2
Methodology Literature Review
Power system modeling can be approached through various frameworks, depending on
the analysis goals, the level of system detail required, and the treatment of uncertainty.
Mancò et al., 2024 provide a comprehensive overview of modeling approaches for energy
systems. The paper distinguishes between deterministic and scenario-based frameworks,
3
==PAGE:17==
Master Thesis
Konstantin Dürr
each suited to different types of uncertainty and planning purposes. Deterministic models
assume perfect knowledge of all parameters and yield a single optimal solution. However,
in the context of complex problems, this is not suﬀicient, as they are subject to uncertain-
ties. Consequently, scenario-based models are employed that embed uncertainty directly
into the optimization process. These approaches may optimize expected outcomes across
multiple scenarios, minimize worst-case results, or explore the full distribution of possible
outcomes rather than seeking a single optimum, and make decisions associated with risk
certificates according to such a distribution.
This thesis focuses on optimizing expected outcomes across a set of scenarios.
Zerrahn and Schill, 2017 provide an overview of existing power system models used to an-
alyze the integration of variable RES and to model capacity investment problems. They
highlight key differences in model scope, temporal and spatial resolution, and treatment
of flexibility options. Some models focus on dispatch and short-term operation, while oth-
ers concentrate on long-term investment decisions. A major distinction is made between
models that allow for explicit flexibility mechanisms, such as demand response, storage,
and sector coupling, and those that simplify or omit them. They also point out incon-
sistencies in how curtailment is treated, as well as whether transmission and storage are
co-optimized or treated as fixed inputs. Some of the simplifications they outline do also
apply to this thesis and are discussed in Chapter 6.
Haller et al., 2012 emphasise the importance of storage and transmission infrastructure in
enabling a fully renewable energy system. Using a cost-minimizing optimization model,
the paper examines how different combinations of technologies can meet energy demand
without fossil fuels, considering both investment and operational costs. A key finding is
that large-scale energy storage and expanded transmission capacity are crucial for bal-
ancing the variability of renewable energy generation. Storage helps shift surplus electric-
ity to times of scarcity, while an appropriate transmission infrastructure enables spatial
balancing by connecting regions with complementary generation profiles. Although the
model demonstrates the technical and economic feasibility of a 100% renewable system,
it simplifies grid constraints by assuming unlimited electricity transfer across the system.
This assumption highlights the potential role of transmission but may underestimate the
operational challenges and infrastructure requirements in practice. The authors acknowl-
edge this limitation and emphasize the need for coordinated grid planning to support the
integration of renewable energy sources.
Hart and Jacobson, 2011 propose an alternative to traditional cost-minimizing models
mentioned above by formulating the capacity planning problem as a carbon minimiza-
tion task. Instead of identifying the least-cost energy system configuration, their model
searches for solutions that achieve the lowest possible carbon emissions under predefined
constraints, such as supply reliability and resource availability. This perspective is also
briefly introduced in this thesis. To explore the vast space of possible system configura-
tions, they apply a Monte Carlo simulation approach (Hart & Jacobson, 2011). In this
framework, input parameters such as technology costs, demand growth, and resource po-
tentials are randomly varied across many iterations. Unlike deterministic optimization,
this approach does not yield a single optimal solution; instead, it identifies a range of viable
low-carbon configurations, making it particularly relevant for long-term planning under
deep uncertainty. A similar approach was chosen in this thesis to account for weather
uncertainty in the modelling.

Safaei and Keith, 2015 examine the role of large-scale BESS in a decarbonized electricity
system by developing a greenfield optimization model that minimizes total system cost.
Their analysis focuses on how the economic value and deployment of storage change under
different carbon emission constraints. The model is used to simulate systems at varying
carbon intensity limits, with and without access to dispatchable zero-carbon technologies.
The results show that BESS plays a limited role unless an emission reduction of more
than 70% is required and alternative dispatchable low-carbon options are unavailable.
Even when storage is cost-effective, the economically optimal capacities are moderate,
and long-duration or seasonal storage is not justified in most cases.
In addition to storage capacity, both international and national transmission capacity are
crucial for fully utilizing RES. Fürsch et al., 2013 emphasize the critical role of transmission
capacity in enabling a cost-eﬀicient and reliable integration of high shares of renewables.
Transmission allows spatial smoothing of variable generation by linking regions with com-
plementary wind and solar profiles, thereby reducing the need for backup capacity and
storage. The study’s results show that expanding transmission substantially lowers system
costs and reduces the required installed capacity of both generation and storage technolo-
gies. If transmission is not considered in system modeling, the benefits of inter-regional
balancing are not captured, resulting in a need for more capacity.
While the literature mentioned above does not consider the location of storage systems
within the grid, Babrowski et al., 2016 model storage investment within a multi-node
transmission network, allowing for the optimal placement of storage across different grid
locations. The spatial allocation is determined endogenously based on its effect on system
cost, grid congestion, and renewable curtailment. By incorporating power flow constraints,
the model identifies where storage can provide the highest value, highlighting that the
effectiveness of storage depends not only on capacity but also on location.
Especially relevant to this thesis, Sinn, 2017 analyzes the storage capacity required in
Germany to achieve governmental carbon reduction targets. He models the integration
of variable renewable energy under the assumption that all renewable generation must be
used, and no curtailment is allowed. The result is an extremely high storage requirement
for Germany (over 13,000 GWh), leading to the conclusion that storage is the main bot-
tleneck in achieving high RES shares, and that reaching these targets is unrealistic and
costly.
Zerrahn et al., 2018 challenge this claim by introducing a cost-optimizing capacity planning
model that allows curtailment, which reduces the needed storage capacity by an order of
magnitude.
A central feature of their approach is the implementation of a renewable
energy share target as a model constraint, meaning that a specified percentage of demand
must be met by RES. This approach was adjusted and applied to this thesis.
A similar approach was taken by Gaete-Morales et al., 2019, the study presents a mod-
eling framework for optimizing future electricity systems with high shares of renewables.
The model co-optimizes generation, storage, and transmission infrastructure in a cost-
minimizing linear programming setup, using Chile as an example.
A central component of their approach is the use of scenario-based sensitivity analysis.
The results demonstrate how different policy assumptions lead to shifts in the optimal
mix of generation and storage technologies, reflecting reality as power system planning is
always subject to policy changes. Aligned with this approach, this thesis employs various
scenario frameworks to assess the sensitivity of the modeled results.

Coester et al., 2020 contribute relevant insights on the economic feasibility of BESS in the
future power system. Using Germany as a reference system, electricity prices are modeled
by matching a load duration curve with a merit order stack. This output is used to evaluate
the financial feasibility of BESS under free market conditions and under a subsidy scheme.
They conclude that BESSs are not profitable under free market conditions; thus, subsidies
are required to ensure suﬀicient capacity to meet renewable targets.
Xia and Elaiw, 2010 present an overview of different more sophisticated economic dispatch
modeling approaches. The paper identifies that Optimal Dynamic Dispatch problems are
primarily formulated in two ways: Optimal Control Dynamic Dispatch and Dynamic Eco-
nomic Dispatch. Optimal Control Dynamic Dispatch treats the dispatch problem as an
optimal control task, where ramp rates are control variables and generator outputs are
state variables. Dynamic Economic Dispatch, on the other hand, discretizes the dispatch
horizon and directly optimizes the power outputs of generators, enforcing ramp rate con-
straints between intervals. The decision variable is the generation volume of generators.
For this thesis, the economic dispatch is formulated as Dynamic Economic Dispatch.
There are not only different dispatch mechanisms but also different power market designs.
Ding and Fuller, 2005 provide a comprehensive overview and analyze different electricity
pricing mechanisms, nodal marginal pricing, uniform marginal pricing, and zonal marginal
pricing. Nodal pricing is theoretically the most eﬀicient, as it fully accounts for transmis-
sion constraints and losses by assigning a separate price to each node. However, it is often
criticized for its complexity and the large number of prices involved, which can be confus-
ing for market participants and burdensome for data handling and settlements. Uniform
pricing, in contrast, applies a single price across the entire system. While simple and
transparent, it overlooks network constraints, resulting in discrepancies between sched-
uled and actual dispatch. Zonal pricing serves as a compromise, assigning a single price
per zone while disregarding intra-zonal constraints. Although less granular than nodal
pricing, it can still better reflect transmission limits than uniform pricing. However, it
shares similar drawbacks with uniform pricing in terms of uplift payments and distorted
incentives, especially when zones are not well-defined in relation to physical constraints.
In the context of integrating RES in power systems, dealing with uncertainties is crucial. A
key conceptual contribution from Mancò et al., 2024 is the differentiation between dealing
with uncertainty through sensitivity analysis or by decision-making under uncertainty.
This distinction is important when choosing the appropriate modeling framework: while
sensitivity analysis aims to understand the impact of uncertainty on the model output,
the latter aims for making an optimal here and now decision under uncertainty. Both
perspectives will be applied in this thesis.
BESS can earn revenues in multiple markets simultaneously. While most research focuses
only on day-ahead market revenues, Andreotti et al., 2024 analyzes the economic feasibility
of BESS considering the day-ahead and intraday market. This study reveals that BESS
are more competitive in the intraday market than in the day-ahead market, highlighting
the importance of including the intraday market in the modeling.
Modelling intraday prices is more complex than modeling day-ahead prices.
Intraday
prices are not derived from economic dispatch; instead, power is continuously traded.
Narajewski and Ziel, 2019 develop a model to forecast very short-term electricity prices in
the German intraday market, focusing on the ID3 price, an average of trades from three

hours to 30 minutes before delivery.
The forecasting model is a high-dimensional linear regression that incorporates a large
number of past price measures, auction results, and balancing market data. To estimate
this model, they use Lasso and elastic net methods, which can handle many regressors by
shrinking unimportant ones to zero. A similar but simplified approach is applied in this
thesis.
Schaurecker et al., 2025 introduce an automated high-frequency trading strategy for BESS
on the intraday market. This paper demonstrates the impact of intraday high-frequency
trading compared to optimizing only once an hour. When the storage asset is allowed to
participate in high-frequency trading, revenues are 58% higher than compared to partic-
ipation in hourly intraday trading. This suggests that, due to common simplifications,
most models of BESS profitability tend to underestimate potential revenues. The same
limitation applies to this thesis and will be discussed further in Chapter 6.
Hornek et al., 2025 develops a similar model, operating in the continuous intraday market.
This study compares the performance of the model with perfect foresight to that of a
forecast-driven model. Based on real 2023 German intraday market data, the forecast-
driven model performs only 11% below the perfect foresight model.
However, it also
demonstrates the importance of distinguishing between forecast-driven models and perfect
foresight models when assessing the performance of BESS.
In addition to arbitrage revenues in day-ahead and intraday markets, BESS can also earn
revenues in ancillary service markets. Literature on this is relatively scarce.
Rancilio et al., 2024 provide an overview of the ancillary service markets in which BESS
can operate and how market design could be adjusted to make conditions more favorable
for BESS to participate.
Feng et al., 2022 introduce a model that optimizes price arbitrage as well as ancillary
service provision. Using China as a case study, the model’s outcomes demonstrate that
arbitrage revenues are five times higher than reserve ancillary service revenues.
When modeling the revenue of BESS, it is crucial to consider not only various revenue
streams but also the effects of degradation.
Menye et al., 2025 review key degradation mechanisms in BESS, noting that operational
and environmental stressors often outweigh battery chemistry in determining long-term
reliability. Temperature is critical: heat accelerates chemical breakdown, while cold can
cause lithium plating; uneven heating in large systems further shortens lifespan, making
active thermal management essential. High charge and discharge currents generate heat
and internal stress, with fast charging posing particular risks when batteries are warm or
near full. Cycling profiles also matter, pulsed charging or asymmetric charge or discharge
rates may reduce degradation. Maintaining moderate SOC ranges (e.g., 20–80%) helps
avoid damage from high SOC or deep discharges. Finally, calendar aging occurs even
when idle, worsening under high SOC or heat, underscoring the importance of controlled
storage conditions.
There is a wealth of literature on modeling battery degradation in various use cases.
Horstkötter and Bäker, 2023 propose an application-oriented degradation modelling frame-
work for lithium-ion batteries aimed at ageing prediction. The authors develop a proba-
bilistic degradation model based on laboratory ageing studies that account for both cyclic

and calendar ageing under different stress conditions (depth-of-discharge, average state-

Their modelling approach separates cyclic and calendar degradation effects. It represents
the degradation rate as a function of multiple parameters, including the state-of-health,
which captures the non-linear nature of ageing.
To predict ageing, the framework processes time-series load data into simplified cycle
profiles and simulates degradation iteratively using the developed models. The approach
achieves high accuracy, particularly when incorporating ageing variance, and is suitable
for simulating battery life under real-world operating conditions.
As highlighted above, degradation is significantly influenced by the operation of an asset;
therefore, sophisticated BESS optimization should account for degradation.
Reniers et al., 2018 demonstrate that including realistic battery degradation models in
the optimal control of batteries significantly improves profitability. By comparing three
battery models of increasing accuracy, the study finds that more accurate degradation
modelling enables better revenue-degradation trade-offs.
The results highlight that simplistic models can significantly underestimate the value
of batteries and lead to suboptimal investment and operational decisions.
Therefore,
incorporating realistic degradation into modeling is crucial for maximizing the economic
potential of grid-connected batteries.
Xu et al., 2018 propose a multi-objective, chance-constrained day-ahead scheduling model
for power systems that integrates BESS, explicitly considering battery degradation. Un-
like traditional models that simplify or ignore BESS degradation, this work models the
nonlinear relationship between the depth of discharge and BESS life loss. The degradation
is incorporated as an explicit optimization objective rather than a fixed cost, recognizing
that the same energy discharge at different depths of discharge can cause significantly
different levels of degradation.
2.3
Research Questions
As outlined above, there is a broad and sophisticated body of literature on modeling
capacity investment, power markets, and BESS optimization. However, most studies tend
to focus either on system-level optimization or on the profit maximization of individual
market participants. This thesis seeks to bridge these two perspectives by analyzing the
cost-optimal capacity build-out from a system perspective while also evaluating whether
market participants can operate profitably within the resulting market environment.
Although the methodology is applied to a German reference system, the insights derived
from this analysis are intended to be generalizable. The findings are aimed at decision-
makers involved in market design and energy policy, offering guidance on how to ensure
suﬀicient deployment of RES and storage capacity to support a successful transition to
Net Zero.
Thus, the following research questions are investigated in this thesis.
R1: What generation and storage capacities are required to achieve an 80%
share of renewable energy in Germany’s power generation by 2030 under
different scenario assumptions?

3
Methodology
To analyze power system scenarios for 2030 and the economic feasibility of required RES
and BESS capacity build-out, this thesis relies on an optimization-based methodology.
Initially, a capacity investment problem is modeled for different scenarios, resulting in the
need for RES and BESS capacity to be installed to fulfill demand and achieve a certain
RES share in a given year. In the same modeling, day-ahead and intraday prices are
extracted on an hourly basis. The resulting capacity will be challenged by a set of unseen
weather scenarios, testing whether the built capacity is suﬀicient to meet demand and
achieve the desired RES share for years with unseen weather conditions.
The obtained prices form the basis for a feasibility assessment of RES and BESS. Different
scenarios will be executed, analyzing whether the build-out of RES and BESS is financially
feasible or if another market design is needed.
A schematic overview of the developed methodology is displayed in Figure 1.

The capacity investment model is a system cost-minimizing optimization problem, result-
ing in an optimal investment decision that is subject to constraints and various input data
scenarios (it corresponds to block C in Figure 1). The goal of this model is to identify
the most cost-eﬀicient RES and BESS capacity build-out to meet demand and RES share

model uses different weather years as scenario inputs. To meet a predefined RES share for

European Union (EU) has outlined a flexibility framework in its taxonomy. According

achieved in a given year. (EU, 2023) This relaxation has been applied to this model, im-
plemented as a chance constraint formulation. Chance constraint formulations are often

3.3
RES Capacity Economic Feasibility
To assess the financial feasibility of RES capacity for different scenarios (see block K in
Figure 1), the concept of capture prices is utilized. Capture prices express the average price
a given technology can earn on wholesale markets. If a generation unit were constantly
generating at full capacity, its capture price would be equal to the average wholesale price.
As the generation of RES technologies depends on weather conditions, they are only
capturing market prices at the hours they produce. As RES technologies have relatively
low marginal costs, the price in these hours is low. Hence, RES technologies’ capture prices
are usually lower than the average price, which is also referred to as self-cannibalization
(Blume-Werry et al., 2021).
14
==PAGE:28==

Konstantin Dürr
A simplified approximation of a generation technology’s profitability is comparing the
LCOE to the capture price. If the LCOE is higher than the capture price, it indicates that
this technology is not profitable, and vice versa. Based on this metric, it will be evaluated
whether the capacity built out, as determined by the model, is financially feasible.
If a scenario is not feasible, the delta of LCOE and capture prices will be assumed as a
proxy of the needed subsidy to make this financially unfeasible technology feasible.
Based on the delta and the annual generation of a given technology, the annual cost of
subsidy is calculated.

Scenario Framework
As outlined above, the developed methodology is applied to a German reference power
system.
To perform sensitivity to the obtained outputs, various scenarios are created
where input parameters such as power demand and carbon cost are changed. For each
scenario the same set of randomly created weather scenarios is used, enabling the model
to take a here-and-now decision under uncertainty.
4.1
Creation of Weather Scenarios
Among other factors, the generation of RES technologies mainly depends on the weather
conditions. As these are very volatile and hard to plan for, a power system based on RES
technologies requires scenario analysis and planning under uncertainty. One approach to
incorporate weather uncertainty is the utilization of capacity factors (Jahns et al., 2023).
Capacity factors describe the availability of a renewable power plant in relation to its
nominal capacity (Bolson et al., 2022).
For four different years (2021, 2022, 2023, 2024), hourly capacity factors γg,h for offshore
wind, onshore wind and solar PV were calculated as described in (4.1), dividing the actual
generation in hour h by the nominal capacity available at that hour.

factors in curtailment. There are two kind of curtailments, economic curtailment and
system curtailment. Economic curtailment occurs when the operator of a power plant
decides to curtail generation due to low or negative prices. System curtailment occurs
when grid operators reduce the power output of power plants due to insuﬀicient grid
(Bird et al., 2016). Both types of curtailment are implicitly included in (4.1), hence the
obtained capacity factors are rather conservative.
Another simplification inherent to this approach is the assumption that new-built capacity
will have the same capacity factors as the existing capacity on which the calculation of
the capacity factors is based. Typically, sites with the best wind or solar conditions are
already taken, so decreasing capacity factors could be expected. However, due to a lack
of reliable data, this effect was neglected.

5.1
Model Comparison with 2024
The average day-ahead price in Germany in 2024 was 78.55 €/MWh, while the modelled
average using the framework described in Chapter 3 for the same period is 80.07 €/MWh.
This suggests that the model performs reasonably well in replicating average price levels.
However, a more detailed comparison on an hourly basis provides additional insights.
Figure 6 displays actual and modelled day-ahead prices for a representative summer day.
The figure shows that the model successfully reflects the general price dynamics observed
in the market, but it fails to capture the full extent of price volatility.
This shortcoming can be attributed to the fact that real-world electricity markets do not
operate as perfectly competitive markets. In practice, price-makers may submit bids that
do not reflect their OPEX, which can result in prices exceeding the marginal cost of the
last dispatched plant (Boeck et al., 2025). Additionally, fixed feed-in tariffs support many
RES in Germany, incentivizing them to generate electricity independently of actual market
signals.
As a result of this lower modelled volatility, BESS revenues are underestimated in this
thesis, since fewer arbitrage opportunities arise under smoother price curves.
The actual RES share in Germany in 2024 was 59.4%, whereas the modelled RES share
is 68.70%. This discrepancy is primarily due to modelling assumptions, hydro power is
assumed to have a maximum capacity factor of 1 while in reality it only had a capacity
factor of 0.39 in 2024. In the modelled results for 2030, the impact of hydro power is lower
as more RES capacity is available. However, to improve the quality of the results, Hydro
power should also be considered a variable generation technology.

6
Discussion
The results clearly indicate that the capacity build-out required to achieve an average
80% RES share by 2030 is highly sensitive to demand projections. In the Base scenario,
reaching this target would require additional investments of 42 GW in solar PV, 38 GW
in onshore wind, 11 GW in offshore wind, and 5 GWh in BESS capacity.
Since the
model assumes a starting point in 2024, this implies a deployment timeline of six years,
translating to annual additions of approximately 7 GW solar PV, 6.3 GW onshore wind,
1.8 GW offshore wind, and 0.8 GWh BESS.
When compared to the actual build-out rates in 2024, 13.5 GW solar PV, 2.3 GW onshore
wind, 0.3 GW offshore wind, and 1.4 GWh BESS, it becomes clear that current deployment
levels are only suﬀicient for solar PV and BESS. Significant acceleration in both onshore
and offshore wind deployment would be necessary to meet the 2030 target.
Encouragingly, recent reforms aimed at streamlining permitting procedures for wind power
in Germany may help increase build-out rates in the coming years (BMWK, 2024b).
Importantly, even under the High Carbon Price scenario, the results show that the EU
Emissions Trading Scheme alone does not generate suﬀicient investment signals to achieve
an 80% RES share. This highlights the need for additional policy measures to close the
investment gap.
The results suggest that achieving an 80% RES share by 2030 in the Net Zero Path scenario
is unlikely when comparing the required capacity build-out to realistic deployment rates.
However, the scenario assumes an annual electricity demand of 750 TWh by 2030, a
figure that appears unrealistic. As such, the resulting capacity estimates may reflect an
overbuild relative to likely demand. Rather than dismissing these targets, they can be
seen as forward-looking benchmarks, representing the capacity that would be needed if
such demand growth were to materialize in the future.
The capacity requirements identified in this thesis are relatively modest compared to
the government’s current expansion targets (BMWK, 2023). This highlights the impor-
tance of regularly updating build-out plans to account for new assumptions, technological
progress, and evolving system conditions, in order to prevent both overinvestment and un-
derplanning. The modeled BESS capacity aligns broadly with various Net Zero scenario
projections, falling at the lower end of the estimated range (FrontierEconomics, 2023).
Consistent with Safaei and Keith, 2015, the results suggest that RES shares of around
70% can be achieved without substantial BESS deployment, whereas higher RES shares
make BESS indispensable.
Notably, even in scenarios targeting an 80% RES share, the model still invests in 22 GW
of new gas-fired power capacity. This aligns closely with the German government’s current
goal of adding 20 GW of new gas plants by 2030, which is primarily motivated by concerns
over supply security during the energy transition. However, just like the RES targets, this
gas build-out may also prove unrealistic within the given timeframe. Moreover, the 20 GW
gas target has faced criticism for potentially leading to overcapacity and for conflicting
with long-term decarbonization goals.
Critics argue that the assumptions behind this
target underestimate the potential role of demand-side flexibility and expanded energy
storage.
In line with this criticism, it is important to note that the model in this thesis assumes
a static and inflexible demand profile, identical to that of 2024. This assumption likely
overstates the need for dispatchable gas capacity.
Future research should explore the
39
==PAGE:53==
Master Thesis
Konstantin Dürr
effects of demand-side flexibility and its potential to reduce the need for new thermal
capacity while improving system adequacy.
The results of the unseen scenario modelling show that, although the capacity build-outs
meet renewable energy targets under both modelled and unseen weather conditions, they
do not ensure supply security under all circumstances. To ensure demand is met at all
times, the model resorts to an artificial, high-cost backup technology, highlighting gaps
in the robustness of the capacity mix. This brings attention to the energy policy triangle,
which emphasizes the balance between supply security, sustainability, and cost eﬀiciency
(Weiss et al., 2021). In this thesis, the focus has been placed primarily on sustainability
and cost eﬀiciency, while supply security has been treated as a secondary concern. If the
capacity investment model had been executed with a broader range of weather scenarios,
the capacity planning would have been more robust in terms of supply security. Due
to computational limitations, this was not done suﬀiciently. In addition to considering
more scenarios, future work should incorporate supply security into the modelling frame-
work and investigate how demand-side flexibility can simultaneously reduce the need for
dispatchable capacity and enhance system reliability.
It is also noteworthy that the model does not invest in hydrogen-fueled power plants,
primarily due to their high operational costs. However, the investment in new gas power
plants should be interpreted as an investment in hydrogen-ready plants, capable of oper-
ating with green hydrogen in the future. According to the model results, the transition
from natural gas to green hydrogen as a fuel source begins at a renewable energy share
of 90%, indicating that hydrogen starts to play a significant role in replacing fossil gas at
high levels of renewable penetration.
The ambitious RES build-out targets face several challenges, not only from permitting
delays, grid limitations, and land availability, but also, as highlighted by the recent failure
of the German offshore wind tender (WindEurope, 2025), from developers withdrawing
due to unfavourable market conditions. The results of this thesis show that in scenarios
where no RES share is enforced, RES technologies achieve capture prices above their
LCOE, indicating economic viability. However, once a RES share of 80% is enforced, only
the High Carbon Base scenario yields capture prices above LCOE. In all other scenarios,
capture prices fall below the LCOE, implying that under current market conditions, even
with ideal permitting and grid infrastructure, the required RES capacity to meet Net Zero
targets would not be built as developers would face unprofitable investment cases.
When comparing the high carbon price scenarios with the regular carbon price scenarios,
it becomes evident that the Emission Trading System (ETS) is a relevant tool, bringing
RES capture prices closer to LCOE by increasing OPEX of fossil-fueled power plants.
However, in the Net Zero Path scenario, even with a higher carbon price, RES capture
prices are still below LCOE.
This phenomenon is commonly referred to in the literature as the missing money problem.
(McElroy et al., 2018) It describes the situation in energy-only markets where generation
capacity investments fail to generate suﬀicient revenue to cover their costs. To address
this issue, various subsidy schemes in addition to the ETS have been proposed, with the
most prominent in recent years being CfDs.
CfDs are financial instruments aimed at reducing market risk and encouraging investment
in renewable energy by stabilizing revenues. They operate by guaranteeing a fixed strike
price for electricity. If the actual market price falls below this strike price, the producer
40
==PAGE:54==
Master Thesis
Konstantin Dürr
receives a top-up payment from the government. Conversely, if the market price exceeds
the strike price, the producer returns the difference. In contrast to one-sided CfDs, where
producers do not need to return profit above the strike price, this CfD structure is referred
to as a two-sided CfD. This mechanism ensures a stable income over the lifetime of a
project and protects consumers from price peaks without interfering with electricity market
operations, as power continues to be sold on merchant markets (Kitzing et al., 2024).
In the context of this thesis, the top-up payment corresponds to the gap between capture
prices and LCOE. Beyond bridging the missing money gap, CfDs also lower the cost
of capital for RES developers by providing revenue certainty, which in turn reduces the
LCOE (Beiter et al., 2024). Research indicates that if two-sided CfDs were implemented
in Germany instead of the current subsidy schemes, the resulting reduction in the cost
of capital could generate annual savings of approximately 800 million euros (Duso et al.,
2022).
Therefore, this thesis recommends that policymakers in Germany adopt two-sided CfDs
as the primary subsidy mechanism for all RES technologies, not only to bridge the missing
money gap but also to mitigate investment risk. CfDs represent a central policy tool to
enable a suﬀicient, market-oriented, and cost-eﬀicient build-out of RES capacity required
to meet the Net Zero Path targets. In addition, an ambitious reduction of carbon certifi-
cates at the European level should be maintained to provide a strong baseline incentive
for decarbonization.
Similar to RES, BESS do not achieve positive NPVs in all modeled scenarios. But these
findings should be interpreted with caution. The model applied in this study does not in-
clude ancillary service markets, and the simulated electricity price signals lack the volatility
typically observed in real-world markets. Additionally, Schaurecker et al., 2025 demon-
strate the importance of high-frequency intraday trading for BESS revenues, which was
neglected in this thesis.
However, evidence from more mature BESS markets indicates that as storage penetration
increases, ancillary service markets tend to saturate, leading to a shift in revenue streams
from ancillary services to wholesale arbitrage (TimeraEnergy, 2022), (Enspired, 2024).
Positive NPVs are only observed in scenarios with high carbon pricing, particularly in
the High Carbon Price and Net Zero High Carbon Price scenarios without a binding
RES share. These outcomes underline again the importance of carbon pricing as a key
driver of BESS profitability. However, profitability is notably reduced in scenarios with an
80% RES share, as these scenarios feature fewer high-price hours, limiting the arbitrage
opportunities for BESS. Coester et al., 2020 also conclude that BESS are not financially
feasible in scenarios with high RES penetration, thus they suggest that BESS will need
subsidies.
One mechanism under discussion is the implementation CRMs. CRMs are policy tools
designed to ensure the long-term adequacy of electricity supply by compensating capacity
providers not just for the electricity they generate, but for their availability to deliver
power during times of system stress.
Energy-only markets rely on scarcity pricing to
incentivize investment, but as described above, this can lead to a missing money problem.
In response, many countries have introduced CRMs to stabilize investment conditions and
guarantee suﬀicient system reliability (Höschle, 2018).
However, most existing CRMs were designed for conventional thermal plants and are often
incompatible with energy-limited technologies like BESS (Fraunholz et al., 2021).
Given these challenges, this thesis argues that capacity markets must evolve beyond a one-
41
==PAGE:55==
Master Thesis
Konstantin Dürr
size-fits-all design. Modern CRMs should reflect the unique strengths of BESS, including
their rapid response times, high flexibility, and ability to shift renewable energy generation
across time. Further research into the optimal design of capacity markets could provide
valuable insights into how to effectively integrate BESS and other emerging technologies
while ensuring long-term system reliability.
In the context of Germany’s planned capacity auctions for gas power plants, it is essential
that these tenders are designed to be technology-agnostic. This would allow BESS to
contribute meaningfully to system security, while also accessing stable revenue streams
that help address the missing money problem. Such an inclusive approach can enhance
cost-eﬀiciency and support a lower-carbon path to supply security. (BMWK, 2024a)
Contrary to the argument that BESS would be a key bottleneck in the energy transition
(Sinn, 2017), the analysis of the 80% RES Net Zero Path scenario shows otherwise. The
annual subsidy cost for RES in this scenario amounts to 5,138 MEUR, while the corre-
sponding cost for BESS is only 256 MEUR, indicating that the required BESS deployment
is financially minor compared to the scale of required RES expansion.
The modeled total system costs indicate that transforming the power system to achieve an
80% RES share is more expensive than operating a system without a binding RES target.
Furthermore, reaching this RES share becomes relatively more costly under higher demand
scenarios, as additional capacity must be installed to meet both the increased load and
the renewable target. The results also suggest that pursuing Net Zero targets is more
cost-effective when a higher carbon price is applied. A robust carbon price reduces the
missing money gap and displaces ineﬀicient fossil-fueled power plants in the merit order,
leading to a more eﬀicient system configuration.
It is important to acknowledge that the total system costs analyzed in this thesis do not
account for the costs associated with necessary grid expansion. These costs are closely
linked to the share of renewable energy, as well as the deployment of BESS and other
flexibility technologies (Babrowski et al., 2016). Including grid infrastructure costs could
lead to alternative Net Zero pathways. Therefore, future research should aim to integrate
grid investment and transmission requirements into capacity planning models to provide
a more comprehensive assessment of system-wide costs and optimal technology mixes
(Fürsch et al., 2013).
Among the scenarios analyzed, the Political Turnaround scenario yields the lowest relative
cost, primarily due to the continued operation of depreciated nuclear power plants with
low operational expenditures.
However, this outcome should not be interpreted as a
justification to delay or reverse the phase-out of nuclear energy. The model simulates a
single year and does not account for the eventual decommissioning of aging nuclear plants.
Once these units reach the end of their operational lifetime, it will be essential to have
suﬀicient RES capacity in place to ensure system reliability and climate targets are met.
Therefore, this thesis advises against halting the nuclear phase-out, as doing so could
undermine the necessary investment signals for renewable energy deployment.

Conclusion
This thesis sets out to evaluate the RES and BESS capacities necessary to achieve Ger-
many’s Net Zero targets by 2030. The model results show that, in all scenarios, reaching
an 80% RES share by 2030 is unlikely, as the build-out rate required exceeds what has
been achieved in recent years. While the expansion targets for solar PV and BESS appear
attainable in the Base scenario, onshore and offshore wind remain the primary bottlenecks
in all scenarios.
Moreover, the profitability of RES and BESS in different market and policy environments
is assessed, while also evaluating the potential role of CRMs and CfDs in supporting the
transition toward a Net Zero electricity system.
The results show that under current market conditions, RES technologies do not achieve
positive business cases in all modeled scenarios, particularly when no additional policy
instruments are in place. Their profitability is highly sensitive to the level of carbon pricing
and the presence of market support mechanisms.
Only in scenarios with high carbon
prices and CfD support does the required RES build-out become financially feasible. This
demonstrates that despite declining technology costs, RES still require targeted policy
support to ensure suﬀicient investment in line with Net Zero targets. Thus, this thesis
recommends the implementation of two-sided CfDs for all RES technologies to bridge the
missing money gap and lower the cost of capital. Otherwise, the Net Zero targets will not
be achieved as developers are not suﬀiciently investing in new RES capacity.
BESS profitability shows a similar pattern. While BESS plays a critical role in integrating
high shares of RES, it does not reach positive NPVs in most modeled scenarios. Prof-
itability improves in scenarios with high carbon prices where market conditions are more
favorable and price volatility increases. This underlines the importance of coordinated
policy design to create investment signals for BESS.
CRMs are discussed as a promising tool to close the missing money gap for BESS. When
properly designed, taking into account the energy-limited nature of BESS and applying
fair derating factors, they can provide stable revenue streams that improve the investment
case for BESS and lower the cost of capital. However, the thesis stresses that capacity
markets must evolve beyond a one-size-fits-all approach. To be effective, they must be
technology-neutral in practice and reflect the diverse characteristics of modern power
system assets.
The analysis also explores the importance of the ETS as a base policy instrument. Carbon
pricing provides a significant incentive to decarbonize power generation; the modelled
results demonstrate that high carbon prices lead to higher RES shares without active
enforcement.
In conclusion, achieving a effective and cost-effecient Net Zero electricity system will re-
quire a combination of high carbon pricing and well-designed market instruments such as
CRMs and CFDs. These tools can help unlock investment in RES and BESS, close the
missing money gap, and ensure a reliable transition toward a decarbonized energy future.
