Abstract 
Plastic consumption on a global scale is increasing in quantities every year. As plastic 
consumptions raise so do the use of plastic additives. Plastic additives cover many kinds of chemicals, 
giving plastic specific properties and characteristics. Some of the chemicals used as additives in 
plastic are categorized as harmful to human health and to the environment.  
Since 1990, the global amount of plastic sent to recycling facilities has been steadily raising. With 
the quantities of plastic used today, large-scale recycling is a great option to lowering the amounts of 
virgin plastic produced. During recycling, plastic materials undergo a melting and degassing process, 
which are partly used to clean the polymers for unwanted chemicals, while recycling the polymers in 
the plastic. Some chemicals might not evaporate and disappear during this degassing process which, 
consequently, can lead to an accumulation of both undesirable or harmful inorganic and organic 
chemicals in plastic products.  
More research is necessary concerning what happens to the harmful additives during recycling 
process to avoid increasing exiting problems or the creation of new problems. The aim of this project 
is therefore to quantify the fate of critical chemicals during plastic recycling. To quantify the fate of 
chemicals in plastic, four different polymers were investigated: Polypropylene (PP), Acrylonitrile 
Butadiene Styrene (ABS), Polystyrene (PS), and Polyethylene Terephthalate (PET). In these 
polymers four inorganic chemicals were examined: Antimony (Sb), Titanium (Ti), Chlorine (Cl) and 
Bromine (Br). Two organic chemicals were examined: Tetrabromobisphenol A (TBBPA) and 
Hexabromocyclododecane (HBCD). The investigation was performed by using an extruder two 
times, to simulate a normal recycling cycle of plastic. The first run through the extruder illustrates the 
extruding process, and the second time illustrates an injection molding process. After the recycling, 
all samples were prepared and analyzed using three different analyzing method: Inductively Coupled 
Plasma Mass Spectrometry (ICP-MS) for Sb and Ti, Ion Chromatography (IC analysis) for Cl and Br 
and Liquid Chromatography Mass Spectrometry (LC-MS) for TBBPA and HBCD. 
 Results showed loss, accumulation and lack of effect on the chemicals in the samples that went 
through the recycling process. The concentration of Sb in PET containing plastic went from 288 µg/g 
PET to 208 µg/g PET as more recycling cycles took place. The concentration of Ti in PS containing 
plastic increased indicating an accumulation from 119 µg/g PS to 139 µg/g PS during the recycling 
cycle. The concentration of Sb in ABS had a lack of effect through the recycling cycle, meaning the 
concentration changes observed through all samples was neither a loss or increase in concentrations. 
The concentration started at 16.8 mg/g ABS and became 13.7 mg/g ABS and ended on 17.2 mg/g 
ABS. Both the accumulation and the lack of effect has a potential for further accumulation after 
recycling processes if extra material is added to the recycled plastic for a desired feature. It is by 
experimental data shown that losses happened in 4 of 11 data sets, accumulation happened to 5 out 
of 11 data sets and lack of effect happened to 2 out of 11 data sets for 9 inorganic data sets and 2 
organic data sets. These measurements accounts for all four polymers of which went through plastic 
recycling. Each polymer should be investigated further and in depth for detailed individual 
conclusions.

1. Introduction  
In 1950 mass production of plastic started with an annual production of 2 million metric ton 
(Mt)(Geyer, Jambeck and Law, 2017). Since 1950 the production of plastic has risen to an annual 
production of 335 Mt in 2016 (PlasticsEurope, 2016), with different applications and types of polymer 
used globally. Plastic is a product made of high percentages of synthetic organic polymers. Polymers 
are defined by a construction of identical and repetitive carbon-chain making each kind of polymer 
unique, in their properties and usages. In 1988 an identification code for different polymers like #1 
Polyethylene terephthalate (PET), #5 Polypropylene (PP) and #6 Polystyrene (PS) were introduced, 
helping the producer and consumer identify which polymers the specific product contains. This 
identification methods have been adopted today and are used in waste collection and sorting of plastic, 
to make the plastic waste as homogenous as possible. Besides the different kinds of polymers 
produced, most kinds of plastics contain a mixture of additives and one or more kinds of polymer. 
The mixture of additives in plastic makes waste handling by recycling a difficult process. Additives 
are chemical products applied to polymers to give the plastic a specific feature. These features make 
plastic a versatile product, with a wide range of applications. In 2015 a total of 25 Mt of additives 
were produced globally (Geyer, Jambeck and Law, 2017). In Europe (EU) a total of 60 Mt of plastic 
were produced in 2016 which were both used and exported globally. In EU a total of 49.9 Mt of 
plastic were used in 2016 with 27.1 Mt of plastic sent to waste handling (PlasticsEurope, 2016). The 
excessive amount of plastic is in stock in either buildings or equipment with a long lifetime. The 
biggest sector within plastic usage are the packaging sector accounting for 39.9% of the total plastic 
usage in EU. Packaging is a big part of the collected plastic waste due to the short lifetime that 
packaging product have. Of the 27.1 Mt of plastic collected in EU a total of 11.3 Mt were sent to 
energy recovery, 8.4 Mt sent to recycling facilities and 7.4 Mt sent to landfilling (PlasticsEurope, 
2016). The high amounts of energy recovery are caused by the high energy release when burning 
plastic, making incineration a preferred way of handling the plastic waste. The amount and 
percentages of plastic waste recycled have been increasing between 2006 and 2016, caused by the 
increasing focus on material usage. The combinations of polymers and different additives make the 
recycling of homogenous plastic challenging, which is why many recycling facilities have 
implemented all kinds of separation and cleaning procedure of their products, before the recycling 
process begins. The mainly used plastic recycling process is mechanical recycling. Mechanical 
recycling is a chain of processes in which different machines are used to prepare, process and produce 
plastic products. The first process are the cleaning and separations, which is used to make sure that 
the product, is of the highest purity possible with as little mixing of polymers and other kinds of 
materials. The second process is the extrusion, which is a melting and mixing process of the recycled 
plastic. Extrusion is done by heating the plastic to a temperature specific for the polymer that kind of 
plastic contains. The heating performed by the extruder is a combination of heating elements, friction 
and pressure, causing the plastic to melt and unwanted chemicals to being released from the plastic. 
The third process of recycling is an optional phase used by many to produces some kind of product. 
The process could include an injection molding which produces product by injecting molten plastic 
into a shape of a specific product. This is a common way of producing plastic products.

Most recycled plastic are sold as a pellet formed product, which can be used to produce all kinds 
of product. To produce a new product with new features new additives must be added. Some additives 
are made of inorganic material of which has high persistency, which is why they might still be present 
after the different recycling processes. when a new product is made with new additives, a possible 
accumulation of chemicals might occur causing the plastic product to have other impacts than 
intended. 
 
1.1 Problem definition 
With the missing knowledge of the fate of chemicals during recycling process, a mishandling of 
the plastic products can cause a pollution in the environment due to the unknown concentrations of 
chemicals in secondary plastic products. With unknown concentration, a risk assessment on the 
behavior of the chemicals in recycled plastic would not be representative.

2. Theory  
2.1 Plastic material 
Plastic material is mostly made of synthetic organic polymers (Geyer, Jambeck and Law, 2017). 
Polymers are made of a construction of specific molecules, connected in long identical and repeated 
chains. Plastic polymers are long carbon chains, but many different polymers are used for plastic 
production. The different polymers have each a specific construction, that defines that type of 
polymer, and gives each type some specific features and uses (Cantor and Watts, 2011). A finished 
produced plastic product contains a certain amount of polymers, and additives. The combination of 
polymers and additives depends on what usage the plastic product has. 
The word plastic will in this report only be used to describe the combination of polymer and 
additives, where polymer will describe the simple combination of carbon chains making up the basic 
element in plastic.  
Polymers can have two different categorizations, either it is a thermoplastic or a thermoset 
(Cantor and Watts, 2011). An illustration of polymers in thermoplastic without crosslink and 
polymers which are crosslinked as thermosets can be seen in Figure 1 
 
Figure 1: Illustration of the difference between thermoplastic (right) and thermoset (left) (Cantor and Watts, 2011) 
 
Thermoplastic is defined as a polymer which can be heated and molded to a desired form multiple 
time. Even after the polymer have been cooled and set in the desired form, the polymer can be 
reheated and remolded to new forms (Massy, 2017). Thermoset are polymers that have undergone a 
curing process making the polymer have high resilience and high heat resistance. A thermoset cannot 
be remolded after setting into the desired form, reheating will only deteriorate the polymers structure. 
The curing process includes curing or hardening agents which are added to the polymers. Combining 
the agents and heat, the polymers forms crosslinks between the single polymer chains, when the 
crosslinked polymers cools down the plastic starts to harden (Belcher, 2011).  
Because of the lacking crosslink, the polymers in thermoplastic can move around when reheated 
giving the plastic the opportunity to be remolded.  
Most of the polymers produced and sold today are thermoplastic because of the easy applications 
and molding of the materials compared to a thermoset (Cantor and Watts, 2011). 
 
2.2 Plastic production and consumption  
The first recording of plastic usage, dates back some 3600 years ago, when rubber where used to 
make toys and decorations (Hosler, Burkett and Tarkanian, 1999). The first synthetic organic polymer 
==PAGE:11==
10 
 
made, where in 1868. The polymer where a further development of the first man-made plastic, 
Parkesine. The plastic was produced of a combination of nitric acid and natural materials such as 
cotton (Science history institute, 2000). The first man-made plastic called Bakelite was first produced 
in 1907, thereby representing the beginning of the synthetic plastic we know today, without any 
natural found components.  
Plastic was not mass produced in the early 20th century, but during WW2 plastic started to be a 
desired material, because it was easy to use and form for the products used during the war (Science 
history institute, 2000). In 1950, mass production started. The use of plastic during the war had shown 
the many possibilities that plastic provided (Geyer, Jambeck and Law, 2017). In 1950 the global 
plastic production was 2 Mt (Geyer, Jambeck and Law, 2017) and 14 years later the production had 
risen 750% to 15 Mt (Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2017). The next 52 years the global production 
rose to a yearly production of 335 Mt in 2016 with many different types of polymers and plastic 
(PlasticsEurope, 2016). 
 
The main types of polymers sold in the last 12 years are Polyethylene (PE) (includes low density 
polyethylene (LDPE) Linear low-density polyethylene (LLDPE) and High-density polyethylene 
(HDPE)), PP, PS, Polyvinylchloride (PVC) and PET (EC, 2011; PlasticsEurope, 2016). In 1988, the 
US Society of plastic industry introduced a way of categorizing the different polymers in plastic. This 
system called Resin Identification Code (RIC) was designed for identifying what kind of polymers 
the used plastic contained. The system contains seven identification codes, and each one is assigned 
to one kind of polymer see Figure 2, an exception being the “number 7”, which is used to represent 
all other polymer-based plastics (Clauson, 2013). RIC was later adapted for recycling purposes and 
provides an easy way for consumers to separate their plastic waste in the right polymers for better 
and more homogeneous recycling. 
 
Figure 2: The seven categories of the Resin Identification Code (RIC) (Reclaim, 2004) 
 
Plastics are used globally and in all sectors of human life. The biggest sector is the packaging 
sector, accounting for ~40% of the global plastic use (Shen and Worrell, 2014; PlasticsEurope, 2016). 
Packaging includes single use plastics, such as plastic bags, cutlery and many other things, with 
mostly a short life span compared to many other sectors (EC, 2011). Building and construction are 
the second largest sector, accounting for 20% of total use. Unlike packaging, the building and 
construction sector has a very low discard rate, due to the large amounts of materials ending up in

anthropogenic stocks in the sector. In general, a significant amount of the plastic produced is stocked 
in the use phase because of the lifespan of the products (Shen and Worrell, 2014), and this effect is 
particularly pronounced for both the building and construction as well as the electrical and electronic 
sectors.  
 
2.3 Chemicals in plastic 
Plastics have a vast application usage, stretching from single-use packaging to reinforced glass. 
To be used in so different applications, additives are added to the different polymers to modify the 
properties of the plastic. Additives are the reason plastic are widely used today, as they allow to 
cheaply modify low-cost materials such as polymers into a multifunctional product such as plastic. 
Additives are used for different purposes, including stabilization or prolongation of the lifetime of 
the plastic, increase the fire- or environmental resistance, color change of the plastic, increase in the 
amount of plastic obtained by the polymers, and just to reinforce the plastic (Hansen et al., 2013).  
In thermoplastics, additives could help the molding processes or the stabilizing process of the 
plastic. To produce thermosets additives are needed. In thermosets curing agents are used to make 
the curing process resulting in the cross linking of the polymer chains as seen in Figure 1. Cross 
linking can also be done between plastic and other materials such as wood or metal with the use of 
additives (Coleman, 2011).  
The purpose of the additives is in many cases to improve the properties of the polymers, but in 
some cases, additives are used to lower the prices of the plastic product as some functional fillers are 
cheaper than clean polymers (Hansen et al., 2013). In Table 1, the distribution of additive types is 
shown for USA and Europe in 1999 and 2006 respectively; the most consumed additives in these

2.4 Waste management 
2.4.1 Overview of plastic waste management  
Plastic products have since the mass production started, seen an increase in demand. The lifespan 
of synthetic polymers and the way plastic have been handled and, in some countries, still are handled, 
has affected the environment negatively for many years. In 2010, an estimate of 5 to 13 Mt of plastic 
waste entered the aquatic environment, accounting for 2-5 % of the total plastic waste (Jambeck et 
al., 2015). These are some of the waste management problems seen around the world.  
The three main methods of plastic waste handling are landfill, recycling and energy recovery. In 
EU25+NO/CH, 24.5 Mt of plastic waste were collected accounting for 50% of used plastic in 2006, 
12.9 Mt of those were sent to landfill and only 4.7 and 7.0 Mt were sent to recycling and energy 
recovery respectively (PlasticsEurope, 2006). In 2016, EU28+NO/CH had a total of 27.1 Mt of 
collected plastic waste, accounting for 54% of total used plastic. 8.4 and 11.3 Mt of the collected 
waste were sent to recycling and energy recovery respectively, and 7.4 Mt were sent to landfill 
(PlasticsEurope, 2016). These increasing amount of recycling and energy recovering of plastic waste 
shows that actions, towards a better handling of plastic waste, are taken, but it takes time.  
In many countries around the world, the waste management systems in place are not equipped or 
advanced enough to treat all the generated plastic waste. Because of these big amounts of waste, some 
countries use landfills. Even in Europe, which has the lowest amounts of landfilled plastic waste, 
countries such as Malta or Greece still deposit ~80% of their plastic waste in landfill. Reason for 
landfilling could be lack of funds or other kinds of resources (PlasticsEurope, 2016). Lack of funds 
and resources and other reasons are also seen around the world, making landfill a chosen way of 
discarding plastic waste. Globally the rate to which plastic waste is landfilled has been declining since 
1980 but have since 2012 made a drastic decline in amounts landfilled (Geyer, Jambeck and Law, 
2017), showing some positively tendencies around the world. Some countries use the plastic waste 
for energy recovery because of the high energy, plastic contains. Plastic are usually petroleum based 
and depending on the polymer type can have a calorific value of 19 – 49 MJ/kg dw (Komilis et al., 
2012). These high energy values make plastic waste a good source of energy for countries with the 
necessary recovery technologies.  
The third options to treat the plastic waste is recycling. Recycling can be done in different ways, 
depending on the kind of plastic (Thermoset or Thermoplastic). Since thermosets cannot be remolded 
with heat after crosslinking, recycling can only be done by chemical recycling. Chemical recycling 
includes depolymerizing of the plastics polymers into monomers (Ragaert, Delva and Van Geem, 
2017), which can be repolymerized for the recycling process. This process is an option for the more 
difficult kinds of plastic such as PVC, which contains many additives and different kinds of 
chemicals, but this method is costly and are still in further development for improvement.

2.4.2 Mechanical recycling of plastic waste 
The most used recycling method is mechanical recycling. Mechanical recycling is a recycling 
method were mechanical processes as grinding and heating are used to make plastic into secondary 
plastic products. Mechanical recycling can have different optional processes. Most of the optional 
processes are cleaning which happens before heating/melting of the polymer to ensure a clean product 
after recycling. A mechanical recycling cycle can be seen in Figure 3. The different processes 
simulate some of the most commonly used processes for industrial recycling.  
 
Figure 3: Illustration of the three main processes of mechanical recycling. The preparation, extrusion and injection molding. 
 
The first process is the preparation process. Some plastic products contain different kinds of 
polymer. These different polymers are not necessarily melted together, but they are still mixed in 
some ways as labeling or bottle caps made from PP on a PET bottle. The polymers can also be 
contaminated with organic materials, depending on the use of the plastic before discarding took place. 
The impurity is washed of and the different polymers are separated with the help of the different 
optional cleaning processes. These processes include:  
• cleaning, where the plastic products are washed to remove unwanted materials; 
• size reduction, where the plastic products are shredded for an easier handling and to make the 
plastic the right size for the extruders used later in the recycling cycle;  
• material extraction, where mixed kinds of polymer or other materials such as metals are 
removed; 
• bailing, where all clean and homogeneous polymers are collected or bailed together. 
These processes are used in most cases to sort the polymers as pure as possible (Shen and Worrell, 
2014; Ragaert, Delva and Van Geem, 2017).  
The second process of mechanical recycling is the extrusion of the plastic. In the extruder, the 
cleaned, homogeneous and shredded plastic is heated to 210 – 280oC depending on the polymer type 
(e.g. 210oC for PP and 280oC PET). At this temperature, the plastic melts and the different additives 
evaporates through a degassing hole placed in the middle of the extruder, which only allows gas to 
pass through. These gasses can be different kinds of additives from organic flame retardants to 
functional fillers. The extrusion process is as shown in Figure 4.  
==PAGE:15==
14 
 
 
Figure 4: Cross-section view of an extruder, from the insertion of plastic granulate to the recycled melted plastic. 
 
An extruder consists of a hopper where the shredded plastic materials are inserted. From the 
hopper the plastic falls in a tube, where a screw pushes the plastic material forward through chambers 
with different temperatures settings. The area between the screw and the tube walls gets smaller as 
well, due to a size change on the screw. This change in size causes frictional heating to the plastic. 
The frictional heating in combination with the temperature changes through the chambers, causes the 
plastic material to reach the desired temperatures of 210-280oC. As the plastic is pushed, the melted 
plastic comes out in a cylinder form which can be cooled (e.g. in a water bath) and cut into smaller 
plastic pellets.  
The last process in the mechanical recycling phase is forming of a product, depending on what 
kind of final product the recycled plastic should be. One example could be injection molding as shown 
in Figure 5. Injection molding are identical to extrusion in the beginning. It has the hopper, the tube, 
the heaters and the screw. Instead of making pellets such as the extrusion, the melted plastic is under 
pressure pushed into a mold to form the desired form of the plastic product, as shown in Figure 5. 
Injection molding is a quick way of producing many identical plastic products such as single use 
cutlery or other mass production plastic products. Because injection molding melts the plastic pellets 
and use high pressure to form new products, mixed plastic products or products with lower quality 
requirements can be produced at the recycling plant with injection molding.  
 
Figure 5: Cross-section view of an injection molding process, from the insertion of plastic granulate to a finished molded product

The other forming processes includes (Shen and Worrell, 2014): 
• Blow molding used for plasticized polymers to make containers. This needs a clear 
combination of polymers and plasticizer;  
• Film blowing, used for producing plastic bags and other film based plastic products, which 
needs a high-quality polymer material;  
• Fiber extrusion used for fiber production with polyester (Shen and Worrell, 2014). 
 
 
2.5 Challenges with chemicals in plastic 
Additives in plastic are the main reason plastic is so widely used and has so many applications. 
As described earlier, the purest recycled plastics are the ones where the polymers can be properly 
sorted. But recycling of plastic is affected not only by the many different types of polymer, but also 
the many combinations of polymers with additives, making recycling of plastic a difficult job. In 
Europe, a legislation has been passed, securing that the plastic used for food packaging must be clean 
and free of harming chemicals due to consumer safety (European Union, 2008). This legislation 
lowers the amount of recycled plastic able to be sold to the food packaging sector, because many 
types of plastic send to recycling are filled with chemicals that have or show signs of harming 
properties towards humans. 
All kinds of plastic are a combination of additives and polymers, and additives can be all kinds 
of chemicals specifically designed for a task. The mixing of additives which contains harmful 
chemicals in plastic, are a consequence of missing unharmful chemicals with same features. The 
harmful chemicals are not the most desired chemicals for companies to use in their products, which 
is why the amount of harmful chemicals rarely exceeds the benefits of which the chemicals bring to 
the products.  With the many complex mixing of chemicals in additives and plastic and the increasing 
wish for more recycled plastic, the waste management sector is facing many continuously changing 
challenges, that this report will help shed some light on.  
Additives binds to polymers differently depending on what kind of additive are used. The additive 
functional filler, which is used to different things some of which is strengthening properties of the 
product, make a product nontransparent or to cut the prize of the plastic. Some of these additives is 
bound directly to the polymer chains as an extension to the chains, and some of the additives are a 
coating on the polymer chain. Depending on how the additives are bound with the polymer chain, the 
cleaning of additives from the polymer’s changes in difficulty. If the additives are not released from 
the polymer, and the secondary plastic product has been added some of the same additives, some 
accumulation might happen during the secondary production.

3. Materials and methods  
3.1 Plastic samples 
The chosen polymers for this project are PP, PS, Acrylonitrile Butadiene Styrene (ABS) and PET. 
The four polymers were chosen for different reasons to verify the aim of the project. This project 
aims to quantify the losses of organic and inorganic additive materials in plastic during mechanical 
recycling of plastic. To quantify the losses, industrial mechanical recycling was used. The mechanical 
recycling phase in this project were on a lab scale which could have had some impacts on the results. 
The different machines, temperatures, pressure and handling was an imitation of the industrial 
recycling. The four different polymers gave an overview of how different kind of polymer reacts to 
mechanical recycling and if the different composition of carbon chains, binds the organic and 
inorganic material differently. The four different polymers were also chosen because of the following 
reasons: 
• Polymer PP was chosen due to the use of flame retardants and inorganic material in the 
polymer. PP is the number one most sold polymer in the world. Due to the amount used and 
sold around the world, and the amounts sent to recycling, a better understanding of the effect 
of recycling on this polymer was investigated.  
• During literature review, it was found that PS and ABS are used in electrical and electronic 
equipment, and the polymers can contain high amounts of the organic flame retardant 
Tetrabromobisphenol A (TBBPA) (Pivnenko et al., 2017). The use of organic flame 
retardants makes the quantifying process of lost organic material possible, which is an 
important part of this project.  
• The fourth chosen polymer is PET, which is used worldwide as the main polymer in plastic 
bottles which undergo mechanical recycling many times over. The presence of inorganic 
materials such as Sb shows that a contribution of unwanted materials happens. To see whether 
the Sb and other unwanted metals accumulates in the PET during mechanical recycling, the 
chosen PET samples would undergo the recycling cycle three times to show eventual changes 
to the quantity of Sb and Ti per quantity of PET. The PET sample was not analyzed for flame 
retardants or Cl/Br, since flame retardants are not as commonly used in this type of polymer 
as the other polymer. PET would only be used to see the eventual accumulation of inorganic 
metals during more than one recycling cycle.  
 
All four polymer are sold and used in high amounts all over the world, and they all have recycling 
potential. All the picked samples have been analyzed for TBBPA/Hexabromocyclododecane 
(HBCD), Sb/Ti or both. The samples originate from different companies and had different purposes 
before the recycling process. Since this project are looking into the losses or accumulation happening 
during the recycling processes, the original purpose of the plastic is not important. The important part 
about the use of the four different plastics are whether not they contain Sb/Ti, Cl/Br and 
TBBPA/HBCD, due to the aim of this project.

3.3.1.2 ICP-MS analysis 
ICP-MS was used to quantify the specific amounts of Sb and Ti. ICP-MS is a well-known and 
used method for determining the specific quantities of metal in different materials. ICP uses a gas and 
high electro magnetism to create plasma, which heats an evaporated sample, to such high degrees that 
the different elements in the sample get ionized, which are identified in MS by differentiate between 
the mass of the ions. The MS also provides a specific concentration of each element measured. The 
plastics used in this report has as written been digested to a liquid phase with strong acids and high 
temperatures, to release all quantities of metal stored in the polymer chains and turning the solid 
samples into liquid form. This method was used to determine the quantitative fate of inorganic 
material during mechanical recycling of four polymers.  
The ICP-MS had Yttrium and Scandium as internal standards added directly in the samples. A 
Platinum tipped skimmer and sample cones were equipped. A double pass Scott spray chamber, 
which operates at 2 °C and a Micromist spray chamber. All elements were analysed with He collision 
mode. The He flows were 5 ml/min, the carrier gas flow were 0.34 ml/min, the dilution gas flow were 
0.59 ml/min and the depth of the sample cone were 8 mm. The platinum tip was necessary because 
of the use of strong acids and the organic content, which is not suitable for a normal Nickel tip.  
 
3.3.1.3 IC analysis 
IC analysis was used to measure the quantity of Cl and Br in 9 out of the 16 samples. IC analysis 
is used on chemicals which is soluble in water and shows conductivity when in a liquid solution. Cl 
and Br are often measured with the use of IC analysis due to the precision of the measurements, 
capable with this analytical method. IC analysis works by introducing the sample in question for a 
stationary phase which consists of either positively or negatively charged functional groups. For Cl 
and Br, anion exchange is the used method. The stationary phase is positively charged and interacts 
with the negatively charged ions Cl- and Br-, this interaction can be measured and quantified.  
The measurement of Cl and Br are done to verify the measured flame retardants results, by 
looking into the specific amount of Br molecules found in each polymer compared to the amount of 
Br found in the measured flame retardants.  
The IC analysis was performed on a suppressed conductivity detection. The anion-exchange 
column was equipped with a Thermo Stientific guard column of four IonPac AS22 250 mm and four 
Ionpac AS22 Guard column of 50 mm. the separation of ions was done under isocratic conditions, 
using a 4.5 mM bicarbonate and a 1.4 mM carbonate eluant. A constant flow of 1.2 ml/min was 
obtained. The temperature in the compartment was 20 oC. Each sample were analyzed for 13 min 
with a sample loop of 200 µl.

3.3.1.4 Cleaning of carrousel  
After Microwave digestion with H2SO4, the carrousel used in the microwave, had to be 
completely cleaned to avoid destruction of equipment. As described, two carrousels melted during 
this project. The reason for the melt down was due to the high boiling point of H2SO4, which reaches 
temperatures of 337 oC. If the carrousel is not properly cleaned after each digestion, the leftover 
H2SO4, can melt the plastic carrousel.  
The cleaning process started with all the ceramic tubes being placed in an oven for 2 hours at 570 
oC. All Teflon tubes got 10 ml millQ grade water and 10 ml Nitric acid (67 – 69 % grade). The Teflon 
tubes were placed in the ceramic tubes in the carrousel and a temperature and pressure sensor were 
placed on the first tube, the rest got pressure secured lids on. The carrousel was placed in the 
microwave and ran a cleaning program, using 1400 W with a 5 min ramp of 0.5 bar/s, max 
temperatures and pressure of 210 oC and 40 bar respectively. The program ran for 20 min and had a 
cooldown period of 25 min, with no stirring.

3.3.2.1 Microwave extraction  
Flame retardants extraction was used on the 9 samples analyzed with LC-MS. Each of the samples

For microwave extraction, 1 g (±0.01 g) of the samples is needed for the analytical process. All 
samples needed stirring during the extraction, so a rotary magnet was placed in each tube. When all 
15 samples were weight and the right information was written down as seen in Figure 34 and 35 in 
9.7 appendix. All 16 samples had 10 ml n-hexane and 10 ml isopropanol added. A temperature and

ceramic tubes. The carrousel was placed in the microwave (Multiwave 3000, Anton-Paar). The power 
was set to 1200 W with temperatures of 100 oC with a 0.2 bar/sec for 10min ramp time. The runtime 
was 50 min and had a stirring of level 3 and a 10 min cooling time. See Figure 36 in 9.8 appendix for

containing 5 µg/ml mixed with 800 µl methanol. The internal standard had a concentration of

reduced to 1.5 ml by boiling the n-hexane and isopropanol away. To do so all samples was placed

temperatures was chosen, because of n-hexane and isopropanol’s boiling points respectively.

3.3.2.2 LC-MS analysis 
LC-MS was used to quantify the amount of the two flame retardants TBBPA and HBCD in 9 out 
of the 16 samples made. The method used for this analysis was developed and optimized by Isabella 
Bech Meklenborg Joensen during this project. LC-MS is used to analyze complex organic 
compounds. LC-MS work by taking the sample and mixing it with a solvent, the solvent is referred 
to as a mobile phase as it flows through the machine. The mobile phase with the sample is pumped 
under high pressure into a column containing a stationary phase were the different compounds found 
in the sample mixes with either the stationary or mobile phase. With time the different compounds 
flow out of the column and into the MS were the specific quantities of the compounds can be 
measured. LC-MS is used in this project to identify the quantitative amounts of organic material, 
found in the samples before and after mechanical recycling of plastic.  
The LC-MS used a gradient of 10% of a 0.01% acetic acid and 90% of a mixture of MeOH + 
0.01% acetic acid. The gradient had a flow of 1 ml/min and the injection volume of each sample were 
5 ul. A ZORBAX Eclipse Plus C18 3x50 mm 1.8 micron column was used. The gas temperature was 
100 oC and the sheath gas temperature was 300 oC. The nebulizer gas pressure was set at 35 psi. All 
samples were filtrated through 0.2 µm in a 13 mm filter by (Agilent, Captiva Econo filter) 
  
3.4 Statistical analysis 
This report was investigating the differences seen in the results using statistical analysis. The 
analysis includes a visual presentation using boxplot, a difference analysis using t-test and SD on the 
average results to identify the uncertainties of the values.  
Boxplot was used as a visual description of the data, showing how the measured replicas 
distribution was while giving a visual comparison to the other samples. The used boxplot follows a 
95% distribution, with first and third quartile depictured as the edges of the box in each boxplot. 
Quartiles are the distribution of data divided onto three different boundaries. The first quartile divides 
the first 25% of the total numbers of data from the last 75% of the data. The second quartile is the 
median dividing the measured data points at the 50% of the measured data points. The third quartile 
divides the upper 25% of the data points from the lower 75% of the total data points. The used 
whiskers reach from the minimum and maximum measured point, to the first and third quartile, 
providing the visual depicturing of the distribution between all three replicas. The median, which in 
the case of 3 measurements, is the replica with a measured value in between the minimum and 
maximum measured value. This is depictured by a line in between the first and third quartile, showing 
how close that measurement was to the other replicas.  
T-test was performed in this report to compare the measured reference replicas, to the measured 
recycled samples replicas. A t-test is a comparison of data showing if a significant difference between 
data sets are seen or not. Before making the t-test a null hypothesis needs to be established. A null 
hypothesis is a statement tested to ensure whether not the experimental data has a significant 
difference. A null hypothesis states that the measured data are equal, meaning there is no difference 
in the measured data set, and an eventual difference is just background disturbances. The null 
==PAGE:30==
29 
 
hypothesis is always true until disproven. The null hypothesis for this project states as shown in 
equation one 
H0 = μ = μn  
 
 
(1) 
H0= Null hypothesis 
µ = Reference sample 0.0 
μn= Sample 1.1 – n.2  
The hypothesis states that the concentrations measured in the reference sample, is the same as the 
samples that went through the extruder. To disprove a null hypothesis the results given by executing 
a t-test is needed. The results from a t-test is called a p-value, which depending on the result, gives 
evidence against the null hypothesis. The evidence depends on the size of the value as seen in Figure 
14. A high p-values agrees with the null hypothesis, but a low p-value give more evidence against the 
null hypothesis. 
 
 
Figure 14: scale of which degree of evidence the p-value shows against a null hypothesis (Brockhoff, 2016) 
 
A calculation was performed on the average measured values of all samples. The SD is a 
calculated variation of measurements compared to the calculated average value of all samples. SD is 
used to see how much uncertainties each average value has. The higher the SD the higher uncertainties 
are found.  
The experiments performed during this project, was analyzed on machinery of which had a 
specific LOD. The LOD was often used as an indicator towards a sample not containing any 
concentration of the investigated chemical. But to perform the wanted statistical analysis, some 
replicas which was measured as below LOD, had to get an assigned value. Since below LOD means 
that the machine cannot be precise on the exact value measured, meaning the concentration could be 
0 but it could also be right below LOD. Based on the uncertainty found in the machines and to make 
the statistically analysis possible, an assumption was made for some of the samples below LOD. The 
assumption states that a sample measured to be below LOD has the same chance of being 0 as it is 
LOD meaning an average value between 0 and LOD is the value given to that measurement. If all 
replicas and all samples of a polymer were measured to be below LOD, the assumption was 
disregarded, and no average value were assigned to those measurements.

The specific degree of evidence for all samples, have been described in previously chapter for 
each measured polymer. A comparison between Table 3 and Table 4 gave an overview of total loss 
or accumulation for each chemical.  
For the Ti concentrations, the polymers PP and PS showed no changes between the reference 
samples and recycled samples. Ti concentrations in ABS and PET showed evidence towards losses 
happening during the recycling process. For ABS strong evidence happened in the first recycling 
cycle, but for PET the evidence was only weak and in the third recycling cycle. 
For the Sb concentrations, the polymers PP and ABS showed no changes between the reference 
samples and any replicas. For PS the Sb concentration rose between reference sample and both runs 
through the extruder. For PET the concentration dropped during the recycling cycles. During the first 
cycle some weak evidence showed losses, but during the second and third cycle, the evidence became 
stronger and the third run through the extruder had strong evidence towards a drop in the Sb 
concentrations in PET.  
For the Cl concentrations, only ABS showed any concentrations of the three polymers analyzed. 
ABS showed a loss of Cl between reference sample and sample 1.1 but no evidence between samples 
0.0 and 1.2.  
No Br concentrations were measured in any polymer during this project. 
For TBBPA concentrations, only the polymer PP showed no concentrations. The total 
concentration measured in the ABS samples were in the size of percentages with a maximum average 
content of 5.46% of the flame retardant TBBPA in sample 1.1. ABS showed no evidence towards 
changes in concentration happening during the recycling. For PS only weak evidence was found 
towards a raise in concentration between samples 0.0 and 1.2.  
For all the plastic samples analyzed in this work, HBCD concentrations measured were below 
the LOD (<1 ng/g).

5. Discussion  
The results shown in chapter 4, are the produced results made during this project. The focus of 
the project is to quantify the fate of critical chemicals during recycling processes, and to do so, a 
recycling process was made, and analytical work was done on that process. Both the recycling process 
and the analytical process was done with some challenges. These challenges are the reason for some 
of the missing parts of the results chapter and will partly be commented in chapter 5.4.  
For the obtained results, chapter 5.1 – 5.3 will be the interpretation and discussion on these results. 
The interpretation is divided into an inorganic chapter 5.1, an organic chapter 5.2 and an overview 
5.3. 
 
5.1 Inorganic additives  
For the inorganic compounds investigated in this project, three out of four compounds were 
quantified. The inorganic compounds are Ti, Sb, Cl and Br. When looking at the fate of Ti and Sb the 
melting point and boiling point are important. The high melting point of Ti and Sb indicates, that the 
heat of the recycling process will not evaporate these compounds. The highest temperatures during 
the recycling process was 280 oC, which is not near the melting temperature of either Ti or Sb. Both 
compounds show loss during recycling, showing that some amounts of chemicals disappear during 
the recycling.  
Cl and Br have a low boiling point, meaning that during recycling, the free ions of the chemicals 
could evaporate from the melting temperature of plastic. The origin of Cl in the samples are not 
known in this project making it hard to explain why or why not some of it is found in the plastics. Br 
is present in the samples because of flame retardants increased effectiveness when Br is part of the 
complex composition.  
 
5.1.1 Titanium (Ti) 
For Ti most samples have the same amount of Ti in the reference sample and the following 
samples going through the extruder. Ti has a melting point of 1668 oC and a boiling point of 3287 oC, 
making the melting temperatures of plastic (between 210 – 280 oC) having no direct effect on the Ti. 
Ti is used as a functional filler in plastic, mostly as a pigment to change the transparency of the plastic 
(DeArmitt, 2011). The concentrations of Ti found in this study ranged between 3.15 µg/g PP to 139 
µg/g PS which is considerably lower than the concentrations found in the same PS sample by Eriksen 
et al., (2018). The amount of Ti found in the four polymers still shows that Ti is present in these 
samples.  
The concentrations of Ti in the recycled PP and PS only raise marginally as seen visually 
compared to the reference samples. This could be an indicator of the amount of Ti lost during one 
recycling cycle compared to degraded polymers are not completely equal, but this difference cannot 
be statistically proven. The loss of Ti can be caused by the added pigment molecules sticking to the 
polymers which are evaporating during the recycling. As written in chapter 3.2.5, some polymers are 
lost during recycling due to mechanical degradation. For that chapter it is the amount of PET that are 
==PAGE:43==
42 
 
degraded and lost. The degradation and loss of polymers was observed with all polymers. The amount 
of samples coming out of the extruder were lower than the amount of polymer going in. This is 
supported by studies of mechanical degradation during recycling (Ragaert, Delva and Van Geem, 
2017). For PET three recycling cycles must be performed before the Ti concentration per gram 
polymer statistically changes. The same could happen to PP and PS if more recycling cycles were 
performed, meaning that some accumulation could be happening in these polymers, but more 
recycling cycles must be performed to specify this statement.  
For ABS, the statistically calculated difference is verified by the visual presentation. A clear 
visual drop between sample 0.0 and sample 1.2 happens. The ABS sample had some color additives 
of unknown origin, mixed with some pigment making the pellets transparent. The pigment could be 
TiO2, and since some of the color seemed to disappear as shown in Figure 11 the pigment might have 
evaporated with the color additive. In addition, some loss of Ti could be because of the plastic sticking 
to the extruder, which was observed happening to the different polymers. Since the next sample 
through the extruder was the PS, which had higher initial Ti concentrations (see Table 3 for details), 
a contamination could not be seen in this sample, and the contamination of Ti in the extruder from 
ABS to PS cannot be specified or verified.  
For PET the average Ti concentration in all recycled samples is relative higher than the reference 
sample. The total concentration of Ti in PET is low compared to the PS sample which was recycled 
in the extruder before the PET. A small contamination from PS to PET could have happened. PS had 
a maximum measured concentration of 139 µg/g PS of Ti compared to the start concentration of 3.97 
µg/g PET. A small percentage of Ti from PS could change the concentration of Ti for PET. If the 
assumption of contamination from PS is true, and the PET samples are inspected without the reference 
concentration, the average values of Ti is falling from sample 1.1 to sample 3.2 (except for the sample 
3.1 which contains an outlying measurement). The losses are marginal but still present and can 
indicate that Ti do get lost during recycling of PET, but all values are low, and a contamination might 
have taken place, making the statement of losses of Ti in PET hard to verify without more experiments 
and samples.  
 
5.1.2 Antimony (Sb) 
Sb is a Metalloid meaning it has some properties in between a metal and a nonmetal. Sb do have 
some of the metallic properties that makes the loss in concentration from plastic recycling seem 
unrealistic. Sb has a melting point of 631 oC and a boiling point of 1635 oC, making the temperatures 
of plastic recycling have small to no effect on Sb. The origin of Sb in PP and PS is unknown, but in 
ABS it is expected to originate from an unknown flame retardant and in PET it is expected to originate 
from the production. Sb as antimony trioxide (Sb2O3) is used in some flame retardants in combination 
with bromine to increase the effectiveness of the flame retardant.  
The content of Sb in the PP polymer shows that it is present in the samples, but whether the 
concentration is going up or down is not possible to verify. The reference sample had 2 replicas below 
LOD while sample 1.1 had no link between measurements as all three replicas were considerably 
different. Sample 1.2 had two measurements with a high spread and one replica below LOD. All these 
==PAGE:44==
43 
 
factors show that the specific concentration of Sb in the PP samples cannot be specified, making the 
interpretation of whether or not a loss or accumulation happens, impossible with the data obtained.  
The concentration of Sb in the ABS samples are by far the highest measured Sb concentrations. 
As stated, the origin of the Sb is unknown, but since ABS uses high amounts of flame retardants due 
to the usage of ABS in high temperature environments such as electronics, the used ABS sample 
could contain flame retardants other than TBBPA which could be containing Sb2O3. The measured 
concentration of Sb in ABS gives no indication of it being lost or accumulated. The visual 
presentation shows both a considerable drop and a considerable raise in the concentrations between 
sample 0.0 and samples 1.1 and 1.2. The unclear answer to Sb in ABS could originates from the 
microwave digestion of the ABS samples shutting down due to an error in the machinery. The 
additives added to ABS polymers are to strengthen the durability and properties of the plastic, making 
it extra important that the plastic samples go through the entire microwave digestion. A complete run 
through the process gives the best assurances to the end results and to be sure that all chemicals are 
released from both the polymers but also from the complex compound from which they are bound. 
Precipitation was observed in the ABS samples after microwave digestion, making the measured 
concentration have a high uncertainty and further investigation of ABS samples is recommended.  
For the Sb concentration in PS, the measured and calculated difference between the reference 
sample and samples 1.1 and 1.2 seems to be right. The problem with the difference is that the amounts 
of Sb found in 1.1 and 1.2 makes no sense compared to the below LOD that the reference sample had. 
In the report by Eriksen et al., (2018) the amount of Sb found for the same PS sample were just around 
the LOD just as found in this project. The high difference in the PS samples could indicate a 
contamination from the ABS sample which had high quantities of Sb measured. Since the PS sample 
was recycled after the ABS sample, a contamination is a possible explanation of why the measured 
Sb in PS are so high. Another contamination of PS sample from the ABS sample will be further 
described in chapter 5.2.1. Because of the contamination the accumulation seen in the samples are 
hard to verify, since they can originate from more ABS contamination. The PS polymer did not lose 
extra quantities of polymers during the recycling process compared to the other polymers, so a 
massive accumulation of low concentration cannot be the reason for this accumulation of Sb in PS. 
In the PET samples some outlying measurements are seen affecting the statistical analysis of 
difference for some samples in comparison to the reference sample. For the Sb concentrations found 
in PET a slow but steady loss of Sb happens during the recycling process seen in the visual presented 
data. Sb2O3 is used as a catalyst in PET production, but some residues of Sb are still found in PET. 
Some of the Sb residues are on the surface and can be cleaned of easily, but the residues mixed in to 
the polymer chains are the once measured in this project. Sb is just part of the catalyst meaning it 
should not be found in PET products, and since PET products are used in the food industry the present 
of a toxic chemical is not wanted. Sb in PET have been investigated by both governments and World 
Health Organization (WHO) and the leaching of Sb from PET bottles have been found to be 
happening (Cheng et al., 2010).

5.1.3 Chlorine (Cl) 
Cl is a reactive nonmetal gas. Cl is found in many compounds as its negatively charged ion state 
chloride Cl-, which is the state of which IC analysis measures. Since Cl reacts well with other 
compounds, the quantities expected to be found after the extrusion processes were low. The chosen 
samples have not been investigated for any Cl containing substances, so no comparison with 
previously known concentrations could be made with the findings of this report.  
Cl was not found in PP or PS during the analytical work of this project.  
ABS showed high concentrations of Cl in all samples. The amount of Cl in the ABS samples 
showed both from the average values but also from visual presentation a loss of Cl concentration 
happening from reference sample to the recycled samples 1.1 and 1.2. The loss of Cl could be 
connected to the reactivity, meaning a low amount of energy is needed for Cl to evaporate. The reason 
for the losses of Cl concentrations could also be because the samples were cooled down in water, in 
which Cl dissolves easily. 
Since the origin of Cl in the ABS sample are unknown, further explanation for whether not the 
Cl concentration could have been different, is unavailable. 
 
5.1.4 Bromine (Br) 
Br is Reactive nonmetal liquid. Br is found in both flame retardants in focus in this project, 
TBBPA and HBCD. Br is used in this project as an indicator of the amount of TBBPA and HBCD 
each polymer is mixed with. Br is measured as its negatively charged form Br- in an IC analysis. The 
boiling point of Br is 59 oC meaning the temperatures in an extruder could evaporate the liquid form 
of Br. The mixing of Br in flame retardants are a well investigated enhancer of the effect flame 
retardants have on polymers. In the chapter 4.4 an overview of the amount of Br measured is seen, 
and no amount of Br was measured in any of the polymers investigated in this project. Previously 
report by Pivnenko et al., (2017) found both TBBPA and HBCD in the chosen polymers, and when 
Br has the percentages weights of 59 % and 75% in TBBPA and HBCD respectively, the findings of 
below LOD gives reason of doubt. Based on due diligence on the chosen samples some Br should be 
measured in the ABS reference sample, and if not, all flame retardants were evaporated during the 
recycling process, some in the recycled samples. For PP and PS, the amount of Br from the flame 
retardants would be below LOD. As seen in the results, high concentrations of flame retardants were 
measured in all ABS samples.  
For PP, the Br concentration in the reference sample should have been 1.78 µg/g PP based on the 
previously found TBBPA and HBCD concentrations, this amount is found by calculating the total Br 
content of the flame retardants. The concentration is below LOD for the IC analysis, which means 
that the amount of Br in the recycled samples cannot be quantified.  
For ABS, the Br concentration in the reference sample should have been 15.3 mg/g ABS based 
on the previously found TBBPA and HBCD concentrations, this amount is found by calculating the 
total Br content of the flame retardants. Based on the measured flame retardant TBBPAs 
concentrations in this report, samples 0.0, 1.1 and 1.2 in average should contain at least 28.2 mg/g 
==PAGE:46==
45 
 
ABS, 32.2 mg/g ABS and 29.8 mg/g ABS respectively. These concentrations are above the LOD of 
IC analysis, which indicates somethings not right with some of the measured results.  
For PS the Br concentration in the reference sample should have been 0.6 µg/g PS based on the 
previously found TBBPA and HBCD concentrations, this amount is found by calculating the total Br 
content of the flame retardants. Based on the measured flame retardant TBBPAs concentrations from 
this project samples 1.1 should contain 0.00 µg/g PS, but 1.2 should contain at least 0.02 µg/g PS. 
All the concentrations of PS are below LOD for IC analysis. 
For PP and PS, the results from the IC analysis matches what could be expected. The measured 
concentrations from ABS do not add up to what it should. Because of the missing Br concentrations 
from the ABS samples the entire IC analysis were done two times to ensure the measured 
concentrations. Because both IC analysis showed all concentrations below LOD, and the LC-MS 
done on the same samples showed high concentrations of TBBPA in ABS samples, method in 
question is the microwave digestion. An understanding of the results could be partly based on a 
project by Simons et al., (no date) saying some Br evaporates during microwave digestion. The 
findings of the project only show 16% of total Br content evaporating. The last amount of Br missing 
from the ABS samples could come from the fact that all ABS samples were in the microwave 
digestion runs that did not run for the entire duration of the digestion method, because of the 
destruction of equipment seen in Figure 28 in 9.4 appendix. The effect of missing time could mean 
that not all flame retardants were digested, and all Br would still be in the form of flame retardants in 
the precipitation that were in the samples. To ensure more precise Br concentrations, the microwave 
digestion should have been done again to ensure whether not the method or the accident happening 
in this project are the reason for the missing data.  
 
5.2 Organic additives 
Of the two organic substances investigated in this project, only one was found in the LC-MS 
analysis made to quantify those flame retardants. This project did not measure any HBCD in any 
samples even though the background analysis indicated that all three polymers investigated should 
contain some HBCD concentrations above LOD. For TBBPA the background analysis showed two 
of the three polymers contained TBBPA, but only one of the two showed any quantities during 
analysis. The third polymer which was not found to contain any TBBPA showed concentrations in 
the recycled samples.  
Both compounds have a melting point below the highest used recycling temperature of 220 oC 
for the three polymers investigated for flame retardants. Because of the melting points of the flame 
retardants some of the compounds should be disappearing during recycling of plastic. 
The lack of TBBPA and HBCD measured in this project was discussed with the lab technician 
Mikael Emil Olsson to be caused by some accident happening during the microwave extraction. The 
LC-MS method used have data supporting the measured results of that method. The known content 
of TBBPA in PP and the known content of HBCD in PP, ABS and PS was found using the same LC-
MS method with small adjustments. The adjustments made in this project were made to improve the 
method which is also documented. To locate any problems with the microwave extraction, a standard 
==PAGE:47==
46 
 
solution was added to one of the tubes of microwave extraction. The standard solution was analyzed 
right after the ABS samples, which have contaminated the standard sample to a degree of which it is 
unusable. 
 
5.2.1 Tetrabromobisphenol A (TBBPA ) 
TBBPA is an organic reactive flame retardant. The reactivity means that TBBPA binds to the 
polymer chains instead of coating the polymer. TBBPA has a melting point of 178 oC making the 
flame retardant melt with the plastic during recycling process for all polymers chosen. The boiling 
point of TBBPA is 250 oC which means that the compound does not boil during recycling processes 
for PP, PS and ABS. PP and ABS both had a measurable content of TBBPA with the use of LC-MS. 
PS did not contain any TBBPA before this project.  
The PP sample chosen had been measured to contain 3 µg/g PP of TBBPA which is 2000 times 
the size of LOD. The lack of measured PP in both the reference sample and the recycled samples, 
shows that something happened during the microwave extraction. But the lack of standard samples 
makes it hard to locate what the problem is.  
For ABS the measured TBBPA showed no visual signs of accumulation or loss. The average 
values showed a raise between the reference sample and the recycled samples showing that the 
amount of TBBPA do accumulate during recycling. The statistical evidence shows no changes which 
means that the difference in concentration is hard to categorize as an accumulation or a loss. The 
content of TBBPA in ABS are in the size of percentages which is normal for plastic containing ABS 
polymers. Because of the high concentration of TBBPA in the ABS samples, all ABS samples were 
diluted 100 times to see the exact TBBPA concentrations. The high concentration caused the analysis 
to be performed three times before a precise concentration for the ABS samples could be found. 
PS had no TBBPA in its reference sample, which matches the previously found measurements. 
The recycled samples showed a small content of TBBPA matching to the expected contamination 
from the ABS to the PS samples. The content of TBBPA a raising in the PS samples for each run 
through the extruder. The raise could be an accumulation happening because of pure polymers 
evaporating during the recycling. The raise could also be a sign of further release of TBBPA 
happening inside the extruder. Because of TBBPAs melting point is lower than the boiling point, and 
since the melting temperatures of PS is lower than the boiling point for TBBPA, a mixing with PS 
polymers might happen during the entire recycling process.  
 
5.2.2 Hexabromocyclododecane (HBCD) 
HBCD is a persistent organic flame retardant. HBCD are on the Annex A list of the Stockholm 
convention meaning that uses of this compound should be kept at a minimum and alternative options 
should be used. The melting point of HBCD is 186 oC which mean that the flame retardant should 
melt during recycling of plastic containing HBCD. The polymer chosen because of flame retardants 
all contained small amounts of HBCD.  
The PP sample were found to contain 0.01 µg/g PP in previous report, the content was close to 
the LOD which could be the reason for this report lack of data on the HBCD content of PP. The same 
==PAGE:48==
47 
 
problems described about the TBBPA content of PP applies to the HBCD content. The LC-MS 
method has supporting data and the samples did contain HBCD before microwave extraction making 
the extraction method the unreliable part of this analytical process.  
For ABS, the previously found HBCD content was 0.14 µg/g ABS, which is above the LOD. All 
ABS samples were diluted 100 times because of the high TBBPA content making the HBCD 
concentrations become close to the LOD, which could be the reason for the lack of content found. 
Further investigation on the fate of HBCD in ABS is needed for any conclusion to be drawn.  
The PS sample were chosen because of the high content of HBCD. The missing data on HBCD 
is suspicious due to the previously found results being 500 times higher than the LOD. The PS 
samples were investigated by the lab technicians more than once because of the missing data, but 
both times the concentrations were below LOD. The microwave extraction done by the author of this 
project are the questionable factor of the missing data.  
 
5.3 Implications for plastic recycling 
This chapter will include overall discussion about results and some of the problems from the 
experimental and analytical part of this project.  
This project aimed to quantify the fate of chemicals during plastic recycling, but the effect of 
accumulation, loss or lack of effect of chemicals during recycling can be very different. When plastic 
have been recycled and is sold to a producer, the material will undergo some changes depending on 
the wanted product. These changes normally depend on the quality of the polymer sold. PP is mostly 
used for packaging but is also used for consumer products. ABS is mostly used in electronics but can 
also be used for toys such as LEGO. PS is mostly used in packaging but also in buildings and 
constructions. PET is used for packaging in the form of bottles or other products alike (Geyer, 
Jambeck and Law, 2017). The applications the different polymers had, has different kinds of content 
requirements. Most packaging products needs to be clean polymers due to the handling of food 
products, which have strict concentration rules. Building and construction need flame retardants if 
placed as isolating foam or functional fillers if the polymers need specific properties. For the 
electronics products they need to be resistance to high temperatures and electrical charges to prevent 
damage to electrical components. All these properties come from added additives, and when the 
assumed disappeared additives are still present after recycling, further accumulation could happen. 
All samples showing no change or an accumulation happening during recycling of the polymer can 
potentially accumulate further, because of additives added to the plastic after recycling. For chemicals 
such as TiO2 an accumulation will not have many negatively effects. But for chemicals such as Sb 
which is toxic an accumulation could have health effects on the user of such a product.  
Packaging is the sector in which plastic is used in highest quantities. Packaging is as mentioned 
often used as single-use packaging in food industries which have high demands to the quality of the 
plastic. Because the packaging sector uses the highest amounts of plastic, compared to all other 
sectors, a total conversion from virgin plastic to recycled plastics in this sector could have high 
environmental impacts. But for a sector such as food packaging to change to recycled plastic, the 
recycling process needs to produce higher quality products. To get higher quality products, additives 
==PAGE:49==
48 
 
need to completely evaporate or disappear during recycling. The results found in this project shows 
accumulation or lack of effect on some of the chemicals on at least three of the four polymers 
investigated. This result is concerning if the future use of the recycled plastic requires more additives. 
For the polymer PET which had the highest recycling temperature and the most recycling cycles, no 
concrete evidence towards accumulation was seen. The lack of effect seen in Ti could as argued also 
be a drop if the recycled samples were contaminated. The lack of accumulation happening in PET 
could be caused by the high temperatures needed for recycling, but the effect could also be an 
observation made possible because of the extra recycling cycles. Further investigation into the effect 
of recycling cycles and the temperatures should be made for better understanding of these results.  
 
5.4 Source of error 
To achieve the aim of this project, experimental and analytical work was as described performed. 
During the experimental and analytical work shortcomings, were observed, which could have an 
impact on the results shown. This chapter will explain some of the observations done, during the 
different processes.  
 
5.4.1 Experimental work (extruder) 
During the Experimental work errors and misunderstandings of plastic recycling were discussed 
with Michael Guldager and Frederik Grønborg Madsen who were the expert on plastic recycling. The 
discussion resulted in the used method described in chapter 3.2. 
During the runs of all plastic types, all observations were noted. The first noted observation which 
could have affected the results is the use of to much of the PP sample. This resulted in the amount of 
the reference PP sample having to be cut in half to have enough polymer for 3 replicas during 
microwave extraction.  
The clean polymers which ran through the extruder before the samples, could have caused a 
dilution of the samples which could be seen with the ABS sample. The ABS sample were colored 
black, but after the first run through the extruder, the pellets showed a white ring on the edge of the 
sample which might have been a dilution. Reasons for the effect being a dilution and not a 
disappearing of color additive, are that after the second run through the extruder, the sample did not 
have a white ring on the edge because no clean polymers were used between the two runs of the same 
polymer. The contamination kept happening, based on the results seen in the PS samples. The 
cleaning polymers after the ABS samples should have been white, but because of ABS residues the 
color of the cleaning PS polymer was grey, this is seen in Figure 37 in 9.9 appendix, which is why a 
total of 1 kg cleaning polymer was used after ABS. The residues are expected to have mixed with the 
chosen PS sample even though no color from the ABS was observed after the recycling process. 
As explained the containers that the PET sample were dried in was misshapen as seen in Figure 
26 in 9.2 appendix because of the drying temperatures of PET. The PET sample that were stuck to 
the drying containers were still used because of the amount of PET sample which got lost during the 
first run of the PET through the extruder. The used material might have been contaminated effecting 
the results. The PET samples were all shredded after running through the extruder. The losses of 
==PAGE:50==
49 
 
material seen in the shredder was never tested but it could influence the results and cause a loss of 
material due to mechanical degradation of polymers.  
 
5.4.2 Analytical work (preparation and exeacution)  
The analytical work leading to the results presented in chapter 4. were subject to some 
shortcomings. The problems happened mostly during the preparation of the samples.  
The preparation for the microwave digestion went as it should for all samples. The microwave 
digestion did not go as planned. Because of missing knowledge of microwave digestion with H2SO4, 
the cleaning of the carrousels was not performed as it should have been. The result of the missing 
cleaning process was that the first and third run in the microwave, destroyed the carrousel as see in 
Figure 28 in 9.4 appendix. An assumption was made that all samples were properly digested even 
though the full run was not completed. Samples such as ABS could have given more consistence 
results if the entire run was completed. Before analyzing all samples, the PS samples were 
centrifuged, this might have been needed on all ABS samples as well.   
During the first run of microwave extraction the microwave had a turntable error happening 17 
min into the extraction. All samples from that run were placed in the microwave a second time and 
ran as it should have. After the second microwave extraction all samples needed a volume reduction. 
Before volume reduction, an internal standard was supposed to be added to all samples, but this part 
was misinterpreted, and the internal standard was only added to all samples after volume reduction 
had happened. The volume reduction did not go as the guidelines suggested, instead of using water-
bath rotary evaporator, a simple heater was used. The temperatures used were higher than the 
guidelines but was accepted by lab technicians. No samples were taken before volume reduction, so 
the method could not be tested to see if the change of method had any impact on the data. All samples 
sent to LC-MS were filtrated before analyzed because of precipitation after volume reduction.

6. Conclusion  
During this research four different kinds of polymers (i.e. PP, ABS, PS and PET) were 
investigated for the fate of up to six different kinds of chemicals and compounds (i.e. Ti, Sb, Cl, Br, 
TBBPA and HBCD). All samples were chosen because they had been analyzed before and showed 
concentrations of some of the chemicals in question. The experimental phase was performed using 
an extruder twice to simulate the extrusion used in mechanical recycling and the injection molding, 
which have many similarities to extrusion. The injection molding was not possible due to the amount 
of sample obtained. The fate of the chosen chemicals was examined in different analytical machines 
and quantitative values were obtained. No comparable outcome of the results between the four 
polymers and the measured chemicals was observed. A general trend of the fate of critical chemicals 
in plastic during mechanical recycling was not found. 
In conclusion both losses, accumulation and lack of effect, was observed for inorganic and 
organic chemicals, in all four polymers during plastic recycling. Each polymer should be investigated 
further and in more dept for detailed individual conclusions. 
 
6.1 PP 
The chosen PP sample showed accumulating tendencies happening of inorganic Ti and Sb during 
plastic recycling. No quantities of Cl, Br, TBBPA and HBCD were measured. The PP sample should 
have been containing TBBPA and HBCD which were above LOD, further investigation of PPs fate 
during plastic recycling is needed. 
6.2 ABS 
The chosen ABS sample showed no losses or accumulation of inorganic Sb and organic TBBPA. 
The lack of reaction will result in an accumulation if further additives are added to the polymer, on 
the assumption that Sb and TBBPA would disappear during recycling.  
The sample lost inorganic Ti and Cl during plastic recycling. For Br and HBCD no quantities 
were measured. Both Br and HBCD should be present in measurable amounts, further investigation 
is recommended. The ABS sample had the highest content of Sb, Cl and TBBPA of all the chosen 
polymers 
6.3 PS 
The chosen PS sample showed accumulating tendencies of inorganic Ti. An accumulation was 
observed for inorganic Sb and organic TBBPA. The observed accumulation is expected to be caused 
by contamination from the ABS sample happening during the experimental phase. No quantities of 
Cl, Br and HBCD were measured. The PS sample should contain measurable amounts of HBCD, 
further investigation of the fate of PS during plastic recycling is needed.  
6.4 PET 
The chosen PET sample showed losing tendencies of inorganic Ti. PET showed losses of 
inorganic Sb. PET were subject to three recycling cycles in comparison to the rest of the polymers 
which were only subject to one cycle. The concentration of Ti in PET might have been contaminated 
by the PS sample.

7. Reflection and future work 
The aim of this project was not answered due to too many challenges happening through the 
entire project. If further information of the fate of chemicals during recycling were to be investigated, 
this project should be used as a guideline. The experiences developed during this project can save 
future researchers time, as different methods have been investigated and tried.  
 
7.1 Guidelines to future recycling of plastic investigations 
For future work with recycling of plastic the following chapters will provide some 
recommendations to things important to include in the planning process during development of the 
aim of the project. The advices made in the following chapters are written with the intention of getting 
results from the use of mechanical recycling and investigation of the fate of chemicals.  
 
7.1.1 The used polymers 
In this project four different polymers were used (i.e. PP, ABS, PS and PET). The used polymers 
had different advantages and challenges, of which is good to know before choosing which to work 
with. All four polymers are recycled using mechanical recycling processes as the one described in 
this project. In 9.11 Appendix four detailed and informative chapters about knowledge and advises 
about future work with the four polymers are written.  
 
7.1.2 Methodology  
The methodology used in this project is a result of missing knowledge of amounts and specific 
information about recycling and its processes. Only the Extrusion were used in this project, but for 
further development of knowledge about the fate of chemicals in plastic during mechanical recycling 
both the preparation process (cleaning and washing and size reduction) and the injection molding 
should be tested. To do all these processes specific amounts and samples are needed. A total of 3 – 5 
kg of each material or polymer is recommended. The samples should be in the shape of a product, 
not in pellet form. When all samples are prepared, it is recommended that the extruder is disassembled 
for a complete cleaning procedure of the screw, to ensure no contamination of previously used 
materials. When the machine has been cleaned pour the sample in the machine and discard the first 
0.5 kg to 1 kg of sample (cleaning part of the sample). For detailed description on the use of the 
specific twin screw extruder available at DTU MEK see 9.10 appendix. After extrusion clean the 
injection molding machine and insert the last sample in the injection molding discard the first 1 – 2 
kg of sample (cleaning part of the sample). 
The explained method should represent one recycling cycle. Extract sample enough for a 
minimum of three replicas, it is recommended to take enough for five replicas for more precise 
analysis. 
 
==PAGE:53==
52 
 
7.1.3 Analytical compounds  
The used analytical methods in this project, are the recommended methods for the measurements 
of Ti, Sb, Cl, TBBPA and HBCD. Make sure the cleaning processes between each microwave 
digestion is performed. The preparation of samples for the analysis with cleaning in between are time 
consuming but necessary for reliable data. If problems with the microwave during digestion of 
material occurs, it is recommended that the samples are prepared and digested again. For Br analysis, 
it is recommended that instead of microwave digestion, a chemical digestion is used, to ensure the 
present of Br.

9.10 Appendix 
Detailed description of the extruder startup. 
The extruder was turned on. Use the connected computer and start the program HAAKE polylab 
system. Use the David method, then press the smiley and an information box will open. In this 
information box the desired temperature and other factors for the specific polymer can be written. In 
the first sector, write a temperature 20 – 40 C lower than the optimal melting temperature of the 
specific polymer, so that the sectors can gradually raise in temperature. Raise the temperature with 5 
– 10 C for each sector until the optimal melting temperature for the specific polymer is reached in the 
fourth chamber. When the specific information is written click the button instrument and then the 
button heating, and the computer and extruder will start connecting to each other and the extruder 
will start heating its sectors to the desired temperatures. To ensure a clean run-through of the sample 
the extruder needs to be cleaned. Cleaning the extruder is in this project done by inserting a minimum 
of 400 g (optimal 1 kg) clean virgin polymer, preferably of the same polymer as the sample that needs 
to be melted. By adding the clean polymer previous melted plastic fragments get cleaned out of the 
tube. When all the cleaning polymers has gone through, add the sample. When the sample start getting 
out of the extruder, take the string and drag it through the water bath and insert the string in the 
pelletizer.  
When all the sample has gone through the extruder and pelletizer, remove 10 – 20g of sample for 
analyzing purposes. Pour the sample into the hopper again and repeat the extrusion process. When 
the samples have been pelletized a second time, the mechanical recycling phase is over for the three 
polymers PP, ABS and PP. 
Repeat the entire cycle for the PET polymer 3 times in total and extract 10g of sample every time 
the polymer has gone through the extruder and pelletizer.

9.11 Appendix 
PP 
PP is a good polymer for people who have not been working with melting of polymers before. 
The temperatures, speed of the extruder and preparation and handling of PP are not too complicated. 
Literature of PP is available, and as the most used kind of polymers different kinds of studies have 
been made on PP. PP does not need drying and the temperatures that the polymer melts at are 210 oC 
(low in comparison to the other polymers). The polymer flows easy through an extruder which makes 
the cleaning process easy as small amounts of residues are left inside the extruder. At DTU MEK, 
clean PP are obtained in large amounts material which means the available amounts are high. PP did 
not show specific difficulties during analytical preparation. PP are mostly used in packaging and 
consumer products, for data showing any traces of chemicals, a relevant sample needs to be picked, 
which can prove to be difficult to find.  
 
ABS 
ABS is a good polymer to choose if many chemicals are of relevance. The melting processes of 
ABS is possible, but cleaning of residues proved to be difficult. The amount of literature on ABS is 
limited as the polymer is not one of the six most used in the world. The temperatures of which ABS 
melt at is low, but preparation takes time. ABS need to be dried at 80 oC for 4 hours to ensure no 
water is present in the samples. If drying is not used, extrusion could prove difficult. During the 
extrusion ventilation is recommended as a lot of gas is released, because of chemicals but also the 
rubber content of ABS. If other samples are to be investigated with ABS, make sure ABS is the last 
sample to be recycled and analyzed because of the high uses of flame retardants possible in ABS. All 
ABS samples ended up being difficult to analyze because of the properties of the sample. The 
preparation of ABS needs to be completed fully for correct results to be obtained.  
 
PS 
The PS polymer have some of the same features as PP. Literature concerning PS is available and 
different kinds of study have been made concerning recycling and analytical work. The polymer is 
easy to work with and flows through the extruder with ease. PS needed no drying and the temperatures 
of which PS melts are 220 oC making it a bit higher than PP. For this project, the used PS samples 
was a recycled material, making the used temperature a challenge. The polymer started partly to melt 
in the hopper instead of in the melting chamber, which can be caused by it already being a recycled 
material (Ragaert, Delva and Van Geem, 2017). The prematurely melting also happened to the 
cleaning material, indicating that PS is harder to extrude. The preparation for analysis of PS has some 
difficulties as PS cannot be totally digested (Vollrath et al., 1992). The non-digestible material is 
found to be organic compounds, further investigation of accumulation of chemicals in these 
compounds is recommended.  
==PAGE:71==
70 
 
 
PET 
PET is hard to recycle. The literature available is large concerning recycling. The polymer needs 
high temperatures and the speed of which the plastic exits the extruder makes the pelletizing difficult. 
The amount of sample lost during PET recycling is high because of the recycling temperatures of 280 
oC. PET sample needs to be dried at 120 oC which should be done in a clean metal container. Clean 
PET material is not something that is usually obtained so this is something that needs to be ordered 
before experiment start. Supervision from someone knowledgeable of recycling is recommended if 
this is the first polymer that is recycled. The analytical work was not difficult with PET, and the 
results are consistent.
