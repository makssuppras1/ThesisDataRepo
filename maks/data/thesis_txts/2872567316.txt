Abstract 
The integration of Generative AI (GenAI) platforms in higher education presents new op-
portunities for personalized learning and enhanced administrative efficiency. However, ex-
isting user experience (UX) evaluation instruments often lack theoretical grounding and fail 
to address the diverse needs of educational stakeholders. This thesis addresses these gaps 
by developing two deliverables: a role-specific UX evaluation instrument and a set of design 
requirements tailored for GenAI educational platforms. Drawing on Cognitive Load Theory 
(CLT), Expectancy-Value Theory (EVT), Self-Determination Theory (SDT), and Construc-
tivist Learning Theory, the instrument evaluates cognitive, motivational, and ethical dimen-
sions of user experience across students, teachers, and teaching assistants (TAs). A mixed-
methods approach, including workshops, interviews, and quantitative analysis, informed the 
development of both the instrument and the requirement list for platform design. The find-
ings reveal significant differences in role-specific needs, highlighting the importance of eth-
ical transparency, personalization, and system usability. Although ethical dimensions (fair-
ness, bias, privacy, AI responsibility) did not show a statistically significant influence on 
user satisfaction, their inclusion remains essential for fostering trust and long-term engage-
ment. The study concludes with a click-through prototype of a redesigned GenAI platform 
interface, based on our case study, ChatTutor, demonstrating the practical application of the 
developed requirements. This work contributes to the creation of more effective, useful, and 
ethically responsible GenAI learning environments in higher education.

Chapter 1 
Introduction 
1.1 Background  
The 21st century has seen rapid advancements in artificial intelligence, with Generative AI 
(GenAI) chatbots emerging as transformative tools across various sectors, including the ed-
ucational one (Møller et al., 2024; Saihi et al., 2024). Unlike traditional conversational AI, 
which primarily responds to input using pre-defined rules or datasets, GenAI chatbots lev-
erage large language models to generate new content such as text, images, and audio, ena-
bling more personalized, interactive, and independent user experiences (Finn, 2025). These 
capabilities have positioned GenAI as a promising tool in higher education, where educa-
tional chatbots support students, teaching assistants (TAs), and teachers with personalized 
guidance and help, and assist teachers with administrative tasks (Hobert, 2019; Okonkwo 
and Ade-Ibijola, 2021; Saihi et al., 2024). 
1.2 Problem 
Despite the growing adoption of GenAI platforms in higher education, there is a notable 
lack of role-specific and theoretically grounded instruments for evaluating user experience 
(UX) in GenAI educational platforms. Current UX evaluation instruments, such as the Tech-
nology Acceptance Model (TAM), System Usability Scale (SUS), and User Experience 
Questionnaire (UEQ), primarily focus on usability factors like perceived usefulness, user 
satisfaction, and ease of use (Ghimire and Edwards, 2024; Schrepp, 2023). These tools are 
not designed with the nuanced cognitive, motivational, and pedagogical demands needed in 
an educational context. Furthermore, they fail to account for the diverse roles and expecta-
tions of students, teachers, and TAs. As highlighted by Saihi et al. (2024), the expectations 
and requirements for AI adoption differ significantly across educational stakeholders, high-
lighting the need for a role-specific evaluation approach. 
More recently developed instruments tailored for GenAI chatbots (e.g., Bubaš et al., 2023; 
Møller et al., 2024) show progress in addressing GenAI-specific capabilities, however, they 
still fall short when applied to educational settings. These instruments generally lack inte-
gration of key learning theories, such as Cognitive Load Theory (CLT), Expectancy-Value 
Theory (EVT), Self-Determination Theory (SDT), and Constructivist Learning Theory, 
which are fundamental for assessing how GenAI supports learning and motivation. Addi-
tionally, they also assume a homogenous user group, ignoring how user needs differ based 
on role and interaction with the platform. Finally, ethical considerations such as fairness, 
bias, privacy, and AI responsibility, which are critical for sustained engagement and trust in 
educational environments (Fu and Weng, 2024), are underrepresented in existing UX eval-
uation instruments. 
==PAGE:12==
 
 
 
12
 
Alongside the absence of suitable evaluation instruments there is a need for a role-sensitive 
requirements list to guide the design of GenAI educational platforms. Most existing plat-
forms are developed by computer scientists and software engineers who prioritize technical 
feasibility and system functionalities, often at the expense of addressing the real, context-
specific needs of students, teachers, and TAs. As a result, platforms tend to offer generic 
user experiences that fail to align with the actual needs of different educational roles. Ac-
cording to Hartson and Pyla (2018), effective UX design requires the practical application 
of Human-Computer Interaction (HCI) principles that reflect users’ goals, tasks, and con-
texts. While general design guidelines such as Shneiderman et al.’s (2016) Eight Golden 
Rules and the GenAI-specific principles by Weisz et al. (2024) provide useful starting 
points, they are most effective when grounded in an informed understanding of user needs. 
For that reason, there is a need for requirements specifically tailored to a GenAI platform 
used in an educational context, where diverse user roles and pedagogical goals are taken 
into consideration. 
1.3 Research Aim and Objectives 
To address the identified gaps in the design and evaluation of GenAI educational platforms, 
this thesis examines the following three research questions: 
RQ1: What learning and motivation theories should be integrated into a role-specific UX 
evaluation instrument for GenAI educational platforms, and how can it be developed? 
RQ2: What are the different needs of users (students, teachers, teaching assistants), and 
how do these needs influence UX requirements for GenAI educational platforms in higher 
education? 
RQ3: How do ethical dimensions such as fairness, bias, privacy, and AI responsibility in-
fluence user satisfaction in GenAI educational platforms? 
The research questions will be answered through two main deliverables:  
1. A role-specific UX evaluation instrument for GenAI educational platforms that integrates 
learning and motivational theories, as well as ethical considerations relevant to the educa-
tional context. 
2. A requirement list for UX and interface design, derived from the specific needs and pain 
points of students, teachers, and TAs. These requirements are applied in a case study, where 
a new UI and user stories for an existing GenAI educational platform is developed. 
1.4 Significance of the Study 
By addressing these research questions, this thesis contributes to the development and eval-
uation of GenAI educational platforms for higher education. The creation of a role-specific 
UX evaluation instrument based on established learning and motivational theories offers a 
more nuanced approach to evaluation that effectively captures the unique needs of students, 
teachers, and TAs.   
==PAGE:13==
 
 
 
13
 
Furthermore, by including ethical factors like fairness, bias, privacy, and AI responsibility 
into the UX evaluation tool, the study emphasizes the significance of trust. This trust fosters 
consistent engagement, which is crucial for the long-term adoption of GenAI technologies 
in education (Fu and Weng, 2024). 
The developed requirements list and its application in a case study provide actionable in-
sights for platform designers, promoting the development of user-centered and context-sen-
sitive GenAI educational tools. Ultimately, this work supports the creation of more effec-
tive, ethical, and useful GenAI learning environments. 
1.5 Delimitations 
The research is limited to GenAI educational platforms that support higher education con-
texts, thereby excluding other sectors and general-purpose GenAI applications. Addition-
ally, the study emphasizes qualitative and theoretical analysis rather than extensive quanti-
tative validation of the developed instruments. Ethical considerations, such as fairness, bias, 
privacy, and AI responsibility, are included in the scope, although other relevant ethical 
dimensions are not explored.  
The case study applies the developed requirements to one existing GenAI educational plat-
form for higher education, which may limit the generalizability of the design recommenda-
tions to other types of platforms or educational levels. Furthermore, this research does not 
cover the technical development of GenAI models but focuses instead on UX evaluation 
and design grounded in CLT, EVT, SDT, and Constructivist Learning Theory. 
1.6 Case Study 
The study will include a case study using the Danish startup ChatTutor, a GenAI educational 
platform designed to enhance learning and engagement in higher education. The startup was 
established in October 2022, designed to enhance student learning outcomes, reduce drop-
out rates, and provide on-demand support through GenAI-driven tutoring specifically con-
nected to individual courses (“ChatTutor - The future of AI-assisted learning,” n.d.). 
The platform allows teachers to invite students to courses via email and upload relevant 
course materials. The GenAI tutor uses this material to generate tailored responses to student 
inquiries. By focusing on the course content provided by teachers, ChatTutor ensures that 
the responses are contextually appropriate and aligned with the curriculum. In addition to 
the GenAI-driven interactions, the platform includes a social component that facilitates 
peer-to-peer engagement. Students can ask questions, share answers, and access peer-re-
viewed insights from teaching assistants and course materials. This collaborative approach 
aims to enhance student engagement while reducing the workload on teachers in large 
courses, allowing them to concentrate on other essential tasks. In Figure 1, the user interface 
of the main page on ChatTutor a teacher is shown.

cludes by identifying the three key research gaps.  
Chapter Three outlines the methodology, starting with the overall project structure and a 
summary of the methods used. It then provides detailed descriptions of the approach for

Chapter Two 
Literature Review 
 
This literature review begins by examining the definitions of User Experience (UX) design 
and User Interface (UI) design and establishing a foundational understanding of how these 
concepts are relevant to educational chatbots. It then explores existing UX assessment in-
struments for GenAI used in educational contexts, highlighting their strengths and limita-
tions. Following this, the review examines how theories of learning and motivation can im-
pact the design and evaluation of these tools. Finally, it addresses principles of designing 
responsible GenAI for educational use, including ethical considerations and challenges to 
adoption. 
2.1 Exploring User Experience in GenAI and Its Evaluation for As-
sessment in Higher Education 
The integration of GenAI has significantly transformed the learning experience over the 
years, with various chatbots becoming increasingly central in supporting both students and 
teachers. Consequently, the field of human-computer interaction (HCI) has become more 
important, as the design, interface, and functionality of technology significantly impact the 
interaction between humans and computers, ensuring that user needs are effectively met 
(Yang et al., 2024).  
According to Hartson and Pyla (2018), HCI refers to a broader field of study, and UX design 
can be understood as the practical application of HCI within that domain. Although the 
terms are often used interchangeably, UX design encompasses the design of interactions, 
the conceptual design, the ecology, and much more. User Interface (UI) design is also often 
used to explain UX design, however, UI design is a subset of UX design (Hartson and Pyla, 
2018). Nevertheless, UI exists in all systems, serving as an intermediary component between 
the user and the system, enabling users to interact with the system effectively, intuitively, 
and reliably (Costa et al., 2024). Several principles and guidelines have been developed for 
UX and UI design to ensure good usability of systems. An example of some very well-
known guidelines for UI is the Eight Golden Rules by Shneiderman et al. (2016). These 
rules can be applied to nearly all interactive systems and help designers create user-friendly 
interfaces. The Eight Golden Rules are: 
 
 
• Strive for consistency 
• Prevent errors 
• Enable frequent users to use shortcuts 
• Permit easy reversal of actions 
• Offer informative feedback 
• Support internal locus of control 
• Design dialogs to yield closure 
• Reduce short term memory load 
==PAGE:17==
 
 
 
17
 
Moreover, principles and guidelines have also been developed specifically for the design of 
UI for AI-driven web applications. Costa et al. (2024) have developed seven practical guide-
lines that designers can use to help users complete desired tasks in a pleasant, efficient, and 
safe manner. The seven guidelines are: 
 
 
• Affordance 
• Internal and External Consistency 
• Discoverability 
• Ease of forgetting 
• Reliability & Predictability 
• Help and Documentation 
• Feedback 
 
Moreover, principles have also been developed for the UX of GenAI. Weisz et al. (2024) 
have developed a set of six design principles that can help designers create UX that promotes 
effective and safe usage. Since the principles focus on the UX design rather than only UI 
design, they also include strategies to incorporate into the design process. The principles 
are: 
 
 
• Design Responsibly  
• Design for Generative Variability 
• Design for Mental Models 
• Design for Co-creation 
• Design for Appropriate Trust and Reli-
ance 
• Design for Imperfection 
The purpose of guidelines and principles like those mentioned above is to ensure that the 
designed system effectively meets the user's needs. However, as UI and UX differ greatly 
across various contexts, it is essential to choose guidelines that are relevant to the specific 
context in which you are developing a system. 
From the perspective of educational chatbots, effective UI design must align with human-
computer AI (HCAI) principles, which will be presented in section 2.3. This alignment helps 
ensure adaptability, trustworthiness, and user satisfaction (Fu and Weng, 2024). Addition-
ally, it is important to acknowledge the challenges of creating UIs for diverse user roles, as 
these roles have varying needs. Ethical considerations such as fairness, bias, privacy, and 
responsibility must also be addressed to cultivate trust and encourage reliable adoption of 
GenAI technologies (Fu and Weng, 2024). While the UI design of a platform is significant, 
other aspects of the overall UX design should also be taken into account. 
Generally speaking, UX design embodies the “totality of the effects experienced by the user 
before, during, and after their interaction with a product or system within a given context” 
(Hartson and Pyla, 2018, p. 6). This definition highlights that UX is a subjective experience 
that includes both the direct and indirect outcomes of interaction, the internal feelings of the 
user, the meaningfulness of the experience, and encompasses the overall effects, including 
usability, usefulness, emotional resonance, and more (Hartson and Pyla, 2018). The increas-
ing complexity of systems, particularly with advancements in GenAI, has highlighted the 
need for effective and safe UX design (Weisz et al., 2024). Poor UX design can lead to 
significant costs and, in extreme cases, even fatalities in certain contexts (Hartson and Pyla, 
==PAGE:18==
 
 
 
18
 
2018). This emphasizes the critical need to prioritize and assess UX design in any product 
or system. 
2.1.1 Existing UX Evaluation Instruments for GenAI Used in Higher Educa-
tion 
As mentioned, good UX design is critical for educational technology, particularly in GenAI 
chat-based learning environments, making its evaluation equally important. Many methods 
have been developed to assess the quality of UX design, each focusing on different param-
eters of the user experience and tailored to specific contexts (Peras, 2025). The diversity in 
instruments emphasizes that no single evaluation method fully captures all aspects of a sys-
tem’s user experience. 
In this literature review, eight evaluation instruments, selected for their distinct focus and 
relevance to UX design, are explored. Each instrument has either been directly applied in 
the reviewed studies or has served as the foundation for developing evaluation instruments 
in the respective research. These instruments vary in their applications, with some tailored 
for broader usage than others. Well-established instruments, including the Technology Ac-
ceptance Model, the User Experience Questionnaire, Unified Theory of Acceptance and Use 
of Technology, and the System Usability Scale, are discussed in this section. These instru-
ments are widely adopted in the field of UX design and serve as foundational instruments 
for assessing the user experience of GenAI platforms. Other assessment instruments, such 
as the Positive and Negative Affect Schedule (PANAS), and the EVT – Measurement 
Model, which provides insights into emotional and motivational responses of users, are also 
examined. Finally, we consider the Instrument for Conversational Artificial Intelligence 
(CAI) and a comprehensive 40-item scale developed by Møller et al. (2024), further enrich-
ing our understanding of UX evaluation of GenAI/AI chatbots. This review aims to provide 
a thorough overview of these diverse instruments, highlighting their unique contributions to 
the field of UX evaluation and their limitations. 
The selected instruments are divided into three categories: usability-focused, emotion-
driven, and GenAI/AI chatbot instruments. While each of the instruments provides valuable 
insights, they would need to be combined and changed to get a more comprehensive and 
useful evaluation of the UX design in role-specific GenAI educational platforms.  
Usability-focused Metrics 
These instruments focus on measuring efficiency, effectiveness, and ease of use.  
Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) 
The Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) (appendix DIS03), developed by Davis (1987), 
uses structured measurement instruments and explores the interrelationship between system 
design features, perceived usefulness, perceived ease of use, attitudes toward usage, and 
actual usage behaviour in computer systems in an organizational context. This well-estab-
lished theory has significantly influenced many subsequent metrics (Ghimire and Edwards, 
==PAGE:19==
 
 
 
19
 
2024; Saihi et al., 2024). While TAM has been extensively validated, including in the con-
text of GenAI acceptance in education, it does have its limitations, as noted by Ghimire and 
Edwards (2024). The model is relatively simplistic, addressing only a limited number of 
factors that influence a user’s intention to adopt technology. Notably, it overlooks cultural 
and social aspects, as well as the potential benefits and risks associated with using GenAI 
(Ghimire and Edwards, 2024) 
User Experience Questionnaire (UEQ) 
The User Experience Questionnaire (UEQ), developed by Laugwitz et al. (2008), is a 26-
item assessment that includes six scales: attractiveness, clarity, efficiency, reliability, stim-
ulation, and novelty (appendix DIS04). The UEQ was designed as a quick assessment in-
strument for users to express their immediate impressions of software products. Since its 
introduction, the UEQ has become a standardized instrument for evaluating usability, in-
cluding in the context of GenAI (Hobert, 2019). While usability is a critical aspect, focusing 
on it alone overlooks other important factors, such as adaptability and learnability, which 
are also relevant to the user experience in educational chatbots.  
System Usability Scale (SUS) 
The System Usability Scale (SUS) (appendix DIS05) is also considered one of the golden 
standard instruments for evaluating usability (Bubaš et al., 2023; Tubin et al., 2022). It was 
developed by Brooke (1996) as a quick post-test instrument for evaluating the usability of 
computer systems and software in an industrial context. It consists of 10 items that assess 
classical usability criteria such as ease of use, usefulness, perceived complexity, con-
sistency, and ease of learning. The SUS provides valuable insights into the user’s overall 
experience with a system. However, its simplistic format does not directly address the rea-
sons users may struggle with the product, nor does it consider important factors like engage-
ment and emotional response (Laubheimer, 2018). When evaluating GenAI educational 
platforms, it also overlooks key aspects such as personalized learning and the ethical impli-
cations of GenAI use. 
Unified Theory of Acceptance and Use of Technology (UTAUT) 
The Unified Theory of Acceptance and Use of Technology (UTAUT) version 1, developed 
by Venkatesh et al. (2003), builds on the TAM and the Innovation Diffusion Theory and 
can be used to evaluate the potential success of new technology implementations (appendix 
DIS06). Originally, it was designed to explain and predict employees' adoption of new 
workplace technologies, particularly in business and enterprise environments. By having 
employees or users respond to a set of 31 questions, it is possible to identify the critical 
factors that influence their acceptance of technology. Understanding these factors allows for 
the creation of targeted interventions, such as training and marketing efforts, aimed at en-
couraging adoption, especially among hesitant users (Saihi et al., 2024). While UTAUT 
provides valuable and more comprehensive insights into technology adoption compared to 
TAM, it also overlooks important aspects like ethics, navigation, and engagement, which 
are important when assessing the UX in role-specific GenAI educational platforms. 
==PAGE:20==
 
 
 
20
 
Emotion-driven Metrics 
These instruments focus on measuring users’ emotional and motivational responses. 
Positive and Negative Affect Schedule (PANAS) 
Developed by Watson et al. (1988), the Positive and Negative Affect Schedule (PANAS) 
(appendix DIS07) is a self-report mood scale used to assess users' feelings and emotions in 
the moment and over time. This 10-item questionnaire was originally designed for psychol-
ogy, but it also offers valuable insights into the emotional aspects of UX when used in that 
context (Riopel, 2019). Since the questionnaire mainly focuses on emotions, it is often used 
in combination with other evaluation tools when assessing user experience, as it does not 
address factors related to usability, engagement, or ethical considerations. Therefore, rely-
ing solely on this scale to measure user experience will overlook several critical factors 
related to UX design, and in this case, to learning and motivational theories that will be 
examined in section 2.2. 
EVT – Measurement Model 
In a study by Jose et al. (2024), which will be elaborated further in section 2.2.2, a question-
naire was developed to explore students' perceptions and usage of GenAI in education (ap-
pendix DIS08). The questionnaire was based on the motivational theory Expectancy-Value 
Theory (EVT) and the learning theory Cognitive Load Theory (CLT). It consists of 25 close-
ended questions, each aligned with one of five factors: attainment value, intrinsic value, 
utility value, student motivation for GenAI, and information literacy. While this evaluation 
tool considers motivational and learning theories, it does not include the nuances needed for 
role-specific GenAI educational platforms, such as ethics and personalized learning. 
GenAI/AI Chatbot Metrics 
These instruments are designed to measure UX in specific GenAI/AI chatbots. 
Assessment Instrument for the Evaluation of Conversational AI (CAI) 
Bubaš et al. (2023) have developed an assessment scale to evaluate the usability and user 
experience of CAI tools utilized by university students, specifically focusing on the CAI 
Bing Chat tool (i.e., Microsoft Copilot) (appendix DIS09). This instrument addresses vari-
ous factors, including perceived usefulness, general usability, learnability, system reliabil-
ity, visual design and navigation, information quality, information display, cognitive in-
volvement, design appeal, trust, personification, risk perception, and intention to use. These 
factors are inspired by the ones in TAM, UTAUT, and SUS. The primary aim of this assess-
ment instrument is to evaluate Bing Chat in relation to its application in teaching and learn-
ing activities within higher education, emphasizing usability and user experience (Bubaš et 
al., 2023). Although this evaluation instrument shares features with a potential UX instru-
ment for GenAI educational platforms, it is designed for CAI chatbots and a general user 
base. It lacks alignment with GenAI-specific features like personalized learning and does 
not account for role-specific needs.

21
 
40-Item Scale for Evaluating AI Chatbots  
The 40-item scale developed by Møller et al. (2024) focuses on evaluating user satisfaction 
specifically for customer support chatbots (appendix DIS10). It measures humanness/ an-
thropomorphic design cues, dialogic communication, information quality, perceived pri-
vacy risk, perceived usefulness, human-AI collaboration, satisfaction, and continuance in-
tention. While this instrument brings valuable insights into the user experience in customer 
support chatbots, it fails to address specific features in GenAI educational platforms that are 
related to learning theories.  
While the instruments in the three categories each provide valuable insights into user expe-
rience, none are based on both motivational and learning theories while also covering all 
relevant UX nuances of role-specific GenAI educational platforms. Usability-focused in-
struments evaluate efficiency and ease of use but tend to overlook engagement and ethical 
considerations. On the other hand, emotion-driven instruments capture users’ feelings yet 
lack insights into usability. GenAI/AI-specific instruments assess the quality of interactions, 
but they are not tailored for GenAI educational platforms that are role-specific. This high-
lights the need for a new tool specifically designed to evaluate the user experience of role-
specific GenAI educational platforms. 
2.2 Learning and Motivation Theories for Design and Evaluation of 
GenAI Chatbots for Higher Education 
Research indicates that the integration of GenAI chatbots can significantly enhance and 
transform educational experiences. However, to realize these positive learning outcomes, it 
is essential to apply different theories related to learning, motivation, and engagement. Dif-
ferent studies have examined GenAI chatbots’ influence on learning experiences and en-
gagement through various theories, which are discussed in the following.  
2.2.1 Cognitive Load Theory (CLT) 
Patac and Patac (2025) conducted a study focusing on ChatGPT's impact on cognitive load 
management and learning outcomes in mathematics education. Specifically, the study tested 
how functionalities and specific features affected learners' management of cognitive load 
and how ChatGPT’s interventions impacted their intrinsic, extraneous, and germane cogni-
tive load.  
Cognitive Load Theory 
Cognitive Load (CL) refers to the mental activity that humans use in working memory when 
performing a given task. Sweller (1988) introduced the Cognitive Load Theory (CLT), sug-
gesting that memory can hold only a specific amount of information and emphasizing that 
overloading should be avoided to maximize learning. In other words, optimal learning oc-
curs when instructional design acknowledges the limitations of the user’s cognitive capacity 
(Jose et al., 2024). CLT was first proposed in the context of problem-solving and encom-
passes three different kinds of load. Intrinsic Load (IL) describes the complexity of the task 
and the learner’s prior knowledge. Extraneous Load (EL) refers to how difficult the activity 
==PAGE:22==
 
 
 
22
 
is and elements that do not directly contribute to learning. Germane Load (GL) is the load 
used for learning, indicating the extent to which the learning can be applied, the meaningful 
learning (Patac and Patac, 2025). By considering the different types of CL, designers can 
understand why learning outcomes may differ and how to create more effective and efficient 
learning environments (Saihi et al., 2024).  
The study by Patac and Patac (2025) involved personal interviews with students (n = 38) 
familiar with ChatGPT. It concluded that using ChatGPT in educational practices reduced 
both intrinsic and extraneous cognitive loads, resulting in a more efficient learning environ-
ment. In other words, the germane cognitive load was enhanced, indicating that GenAI sup-
ports a deeper understanding of learning, which can lead to improved academic performance 
(Patac and Patac, 2025). Furthermore, several studies, such as those by Jose et al. (2024) 
and Saihi et al. (2024), have incorporated this theory in the development of their evaluation 
instruments. Building on these findings, it is essential to incorporate Cognitive Load Theory 
(CLT) along with metrics to measure cognitive load, as the theory emphasizes that cognitive 
load significantly influences effective learning when utilizing GenAI. In the context of UI 
design, CLT also suggests that reducing unnecessary complexity can enhance learning 
(Sweller, 1988). 
2.2.2 Expectancy-Value Theory (EVT) 
A similar study by Jose et al. (2024) examined the factors influencing students’ motivation 
when using ChatGPT. A survey was conducted with students (n = 478) from different insti-
tutions in southern India. They collected data through a structured questionnaire (instru-
ment), which was presented in section 2.1.1, exploring students’ perceptions and usage of 
GenAI in education. It consisted of closed-ended questions based on the Expectancy-Value 
Theory (EVT) and CLT. Jose et al. (2024) pointed out that incorporating EVT into the in-
strument provides a better understanding of students' motivation and attitudes when using 
GenAI tools, as their beliefs about success and perceived values of outcomes significantly 
influence their motivation and behavior. 
Expectancy-Value Theory 
The EVT by Atkinson (1957) explains the underlying factors of a person’s motivation to 
engage in a specific task and is widely used in educational and psychological settings. The 
two main factors are expectancy and value. Expectancy refers to the individual's confidence 
in their ability to successfully complete the task, while value represents the importance that 
the individual places on the task (Jose et al., 2024). According to EVT, there are three types 
of value: attainment value, intrinsic value, and utility value. In the context of GenAI, attain-
ment value describes how important students perceive mastering GenAI tools to be for their 
success. A high attainment value motivates students to complete tasks more effectively. 
Intrinsic value refers to the enjoyment students experience when performing tasks using 
GenAI. Utility value focuses on how GenAI is useful for learning and completing tasks.  
The investigation by Jose et al. (2024) revealed that GenAI reduces extraneous load by 
providing efficient ways to locate, evaluate, and use information. This allows students to 
==PAGE:23==
 
 
 
23
 
focus on learning rather than searching for resources, which contributes to increasing moti-
vation. Moreover, when students perceive GenAI as valuable, they are more motivated and 
persistent when learning, which leads to higher achievement and satisfaction (Jose et al., 
2024). The theory also suggests that an interactive and appealing design can foster motiva-
tion (Jose et al., 2024).  
2.2.3 Constructivist Learning Theory and Self-Determination Theory (SDT) 
A study by Saihi et al. (2024) examined the adoption of GenAI chatbots among students 
and educators in higher education settings. The research aimed to develop an instrument 
evaluating the various factors affecting these chatbots’ acceptance, concerns, expectations, 
and usage. To achieve this, a survey was distributed to teachers (n = 85) and students (n = 
107), who all came from a variety of educational backgrounds, including engineering, med-
icine, and business studies. The survey was developed using variables established from the 
TAM and UTAUT, which were described in section 2.1.1, and insights from educational 
theories such as Constructivist Learning Theory, Self-Determination Theory (SDT), and 
CLT (Saihi et al. 2024). However, the study only presents a detailed overview of the frame-
work’s factors, variables, and indicators, without providing the final instrument (appendix 
DIS02).  
Constructivist Learning Theory 
The Constructivist Learning Theory, developed by Piaget (1952), emphasizes the im-
portance of each individual learner creating their own understanding of their journey 
through reflection and experiences. According to Saihi et al. (2024), the Constructivist 
Learning Theory supports the factors of perceived ease of use, perceived interactivity, ac-
cessibility, and learning outcomes in their developed framework. All these factors contrib-
ute to fostering a more constructivist learning environment. By utilizing GenAI chatbots, 
learners can benefit from personalized and interactive learning experiences that offer instant 
feedback and adapt to individual needs.  
Self-Determination Theory (SDT) 
The Self-Determination Theory (SDT) explains human motivation and personality. The the-
ory was first introduced by Deci and Ryan (1985) and identifies three basic human needs: 
autonomy, competence, and relatedness. Autonomy refers to control over one’s actions, 
competence involves the need to feel effective and capable in activities, and relatedness 
refers to connecting with others, which are all essential in enhancing intrinsic motivation 
and engagement (Deci and Ryan, 1985). People are motivated by various goals and ideas in 
order to fulfill their wants and needs. According to Saihi et al. (2024), this theory supports 
using GenAI chatbots as they provide personalized assistance tailored to each learner's 
needs. They offer a sense of competence through constructive feedback and foster related-
ness by providing continuous support and interactive engagement. The factors related to 
SDT in Saihi et al. (2024) instrument include perceived usefulness, trust in GenAI chatbots, 
facilitating conditions, user satisfaction, and personalized learning experience.  
==PAGE:24==
 
 
 
24
 
Additionally, Saihi et al. (2024) also include CLT, which has already been discussed earlier 
in section 2.2.1 in relation to the study conducted by Patac and Patac (2025). However, Saihi 
et al. (2024) further link this theory to the factors of perceived efficiency, information qual-
ity, and learning outcomes. 
Saihi et al. (2024) build a strong theoretical foundation for evaluating adoption of GenAI 
chatbots used in higher education by referencing Constructivist Learning Theory, SDT, and 
CLT. The study incorporates principles from these three theories into the selection of factors 
used to measure the adoption of GenAI chatbots. These theories play an effective role in 
explaining how to enhance learning experiences. The study found that GenAI chatbots sup-
port active, student-centered learning. Students and teachers who perceived chatbots as easy 
to use, efficient, and convenient were more likely to engage in the learning, leading to pos-
itive learning experiences that align with constructivist principles. Furthermore, participants 
experienced easy and instant access to knowledge and resources when using GenAI that 
supports CLT, as reducing EL improves learning outcomes. However, the study also re-
vealed that the personalized learning experience did not significantly impact technology 
adoption. Users prioritized the efficiency and usefulness of GenAI chatbots over the per-
ceived value of personalization. Nevertheless, Saihi et al. (2024) highlight that personalized 
learning experiences may still hold significant long-term value as GenAI technology, and 
the role of personalization continues to evolve. This aligns with SDT, which suggests that 
personalized support enhances learners’ sense of autonomy and competence, potentially in-
creasing their satisfaction and learning engagement over time. Although this study focuses 
on adoption, it still provides valuable insights for understanding how GenAI chatbots can 
contribute to positive learning experiences.  
All the theories discussed in Section 2.2 emphasize the importance of using theoretical 
frameworks to evaluate GenAI platforms in higher education. Applying these frameworks 
offers valuable insights into users’ motivations, engagement, and learning efficiency. This 
understanding is essential for customizing GenAI tools and their evaluation instruments to 
effectively address diverse learners' needs, enhance user satisfaction, and ultimately im-
prove learning outcomes. While theoretical frameworks help us understand how users in-
teract with and benefit from GenAI platforms, it is equally important to consider how these 
technologies are designed and implemented, which will be investigated further in the fol-
lowing section. 
2.3 Designing Responsible GenAI for Educational Use 
In a systematic mixed study review, Fu and Weng (2024) synthesized findings from 40 
primary research studies to examine the ethical and responsible use of Artificial Intelligence 
in Education (AEID). The papers were selected based on four inclusion and four exclusion 
criteria and were then read and coded, which resulted in five key themes: Fairness and Eq-
uity, Privacy and Security, Non-maleficence and Beneficence, Agency and Autonomy, and 
Transparency and Intelligibility. Fairness and Equity refer to the quality of education, in-
cluding fair learning opportunities, personalized instruction, and social justice, as well as 
==PAGE:25==
 
 
 
25
 
addressing bias and discrimination. Privacy and Security primarily include the governance 
of data, its collection, management, storage, access, and usage, especially concerning the 
privacy of learners and teachers. The theme of Non-maleficence and Beneficence implies 
that designers should ensure the benefits of AI outweigh its risks. Human Agency and Au-
tonomy relate to user control and awareness of technology's functions, closely related to AI 
transparency and intelligibility. Lastly, Transparency and Intelligibility refer to users being 
able to access the technology’s decision-making process, maintain algorithmic transpar-
ency, and require developers to disclose data collection and dissemination practices when 
designing AI applications. Using the identified themes from the 40 papers, Fu and Weng 
(2024) developed a vision, which includes stakeholders’ responsibility in relation to HCAI 
characteristics, for responsible human-centered AIED. This vision is shown below in both 
Figure 2 and Figure 3 and could, according to Fu and Weng (2024), lay the foundation for 
the creation of a theoretical framework for responsible AIED.

Figure 3: Vision of Responsible HCAI in Education (Fu and Weng, 2024) 
Citing Shneiderman (2020), Fu and Weng (2024) emphasized that adopting an HCAI ap-
proach focused on human experiences, satisfaction, and needs, as illustrated in their vision, 
can improve user performance by ensuring AI systems are reliable, safe, and trustworthy. 
In other words, researchers and developers should adopt an HCAI approach that prioritizes 
fairness and equity, privacy and security, agency and autonomy, and transparency and in-
telligibility when designing AI. By doing so, they can create engaging and user-friendly 
experiences in educational environments, ultimately leading to the development of effective 
learning chatbots that prioritize usability and adaptability (Fu and Weng, 2024). 
2.3.1 Ethical Considerations in Educational GenAI Integration 
As mentioned, AI has the potential to be a transformative tool in education, providing op-
portunities for personalization, efficiency, and inclusivity (N. et al., 2024). However, as 
pointed out by Fu and Weng (2024), when integrating such technologies, it is crucial to 
consider ethical aspects to promote fairness and equity and mitigate privacy and security 
risks. Failing to address these concerns can lead to misuse of data or lack of trust, which 
ultimately can hinder the adoption of the technology. While Fu and Weng (2024) highlight 
that ethical implications in AIED are getting more attention in both research and society, it 
became evident when researching that most UX design evaluation instruments used for 
GenAI chatbots do not include ethical considerations in their evaluation instruments.  
2.3.2 Educator Perspectives on GenAI Integration and Adoption Challenges 
Similar findings to those of Fu and Weng (2024) were found in a study aimed at identifying 
and evaluating factors influencing AI usage in education and research by N. et al. (2024). 
In their study, they used a mixed approach of qualitative and quantitative methods. This 
included an online survey answered by teachers (n = 177). The survey consisted of seven 
==PAGE:27==

dimensions: Awareness of AI-based technology usage in education, Benefits of AI tools 
(ChatGPT) usage in education, Opportunities of AI tools (ChatGPT) usage in education, 
Challenges of AI tools (ChatGPT) usage in education, Risks AI tools (ChatGPT) usage in 
education, Sustainability of AI tools (ChatGPT) usage in education (this refers to duplica-
tion of work, unethical paraphrasing, etc.) and Ethical aspects of AI tools (ChatGPT) usage 
in education (appendix DIS01).  
In their study, they found that dimensions such as awareness, opportunities, and sustaina-
bility positively influenced higher education teachers’ perceived usage of AI tools. How-
ever, risks and ethical considerations related to the usage of these tools have a negative 
influence on perceived usage. Highlighting the importance of addressing risks and ethical 
considerations, as well as enhancing awareness and opportunities, when integrating such 
technologies into academia and research. 
2.4 Defining the Scope of Research 
Based on the literature review, several gaps have been identified, which have laid the foun-
dation for three research questions. The first gap identified is that current UX evaluation 
instruments do not adequately measure how GenAI educational platforms support cognitive 
processes and learning outcomes. Instruments such as TAM, UEQ, and SUS primarily focus 
on assessing usability, perceived usefulness, and ease of use, but they do not address im-
portant factors related to learning and motivation. Instruments developed by Bubaš et al. 
(2023) and Møller et al. (2024) evaluate GenAI/AI chatbots, but they are also not suitable 
for GenAI educational platforms due to their lack of nuanced detail and consideration of 
factors relevant to an educational context. 
Another gap identified in the literature review is that existing UX evaluation tools for GenAI 
chatbots often assume a homogeneous user base, failing to differentiate between user groups 
such as students, teachers, and TAs. Instruments like PANAS, the Assessment Instrument 
for the Evaluation of CAI, and UEQ do not sufficiently account for the differing expecta-
tions, tasks, and engagement patterns of these roles. As highlighted by Saihi et al. (2024) 
technology adoption is influenced by different expectations and needs that differ among 
user groups. This highlights the need for a UX evaluation instrument that can capture these 
differences and assess how role-specific GenAI educational platforms support learning, as-
sessment, and administrative functions across user groups. 
While studies by Patac and Patac (2025) and Jose et al. (2024) demonstrate the importance 
of integrating motivational and learning theories when evaluating educational instruments, 
there is currently no UX evaluation instrument for role-specific GenAI educational plat-
forms that incorporates key theories such as CLT, EVT, SDT, and Constructivist Learning 
Theory. Although Saihi et al. (2024) connect CLT, SDT, and Constructivist Learning The-
ory to specific factors in their study, their evaluation instrument is not designed for role-
specific GenAI educational platforms tailored to diverse user needs in educational settings. 
==PAGE:28==
 
 
 
28
 
Together, these gaps highlight the need for a role-specific UX assessment instrument that 
integrates key learning and motivational theories and differentiates between various educa-
tional stakeholder groups. This need has led to the formulation of the first research question: 
RQ1: What learning and motivation theories should be integrated into a role-specific UX 
evaluation instrument for GenAI educational platforms, and how can it be developed? 
Another critical gap in research is the lack of understanding of the distinct needs of different 
user roles, such as students, teachers, and TAs. Most GenAI educational platforms are not 
designed with differentiated user experiences in mind, and as a result, existing evaluation 
instruments also fail to capture role-specific UX dimensions. GenAI educational platforms 
are often developed by computer scientists who prioritize system functionalities over ad-
dressing the real pain points experienced by students, teachers, and TAs. 
According to Hartson and Pyla (2018), UX design involves the practical application of HCI 
principles and must align with user goals, tasks, and contexts. While various UI and UX 
guidelines and principles exist, including Shneiderman et al. (2016)’s Eight Golden Rules 
and Weisz et al. (2024)’s GenAI-specific principles, these guidelines/principles are only 
effective when grounded in a thorough understanding of user needs. In the context of higher 
education, these needs are influenced by role-specific interactions with the platform, rang-
ing from pedagogical support to content delivery and course management. Furthermore, 
theories such as CLT and EVT emphasize that motivation and learning are enhanced when 
cognitive load is reduced and user expectations are met. Expectations that necessitate tai-
lored UX design for different roles. This has led to the second research question: 
RQ2: What are the different needs of users (students, teachers, teaching assistants), and 
how do these needs influence UX requirements for GenAI educational platforms in higher 
education? 
Lastly, the literature reveals a gap concerning the integration of ethical considerations in 
UX evaluation instruments for GenAI educational platforms, despite the increasing focus 
on responsible use of GenAI in education. While Section 2.1 emphasized the importance of 
HCAI principles, including fairness, bias mitigation, privacy protection, and responsible AI 
use, existing evaluation instruments largely overlook these dimensions. Ethical factors are 
critical because they directly affect user trust, satisfaction, and ultimately the adoption and 
sustained engagement with GenAI educational tools (Yang et al., 2024). Understanding how 
these ethical dimensions influence user satisfaction is essential, as satisfaction strongly im-
pacts users’ willingness to rely on and continue using the platform. 
Although Fu and Weng (2024) note the increasing focus on ethical AI in education, widely 
used UX evaluation instruments such as SUS and UEQ do not incorporate factors address-
ing how ethical concerns influence the user experience. Instruments that partially address 
ethics, like Bubaš et al. (2023) Assessment Instrument for the Evaluation of CAI and Møller 
et al. (2024) 40-item scale, are not designed specifically for GenAI educational platforms 
and emphasize trust and risk, but insufficiently cover privacy, bias, fairness, and AI relia-
bility. 
==PAGE:29==
 
 
 
29
 
This gap underscores the need for a UX evaluation instrument that explicitly integrates eth-
ical dimensions aligned with the vision advocated by Fu and Weng (2024). Several studies 
have investigated the impact of ethics on technology adoption, however, the correlation be-
tween ethical considerations and user satisfaction in GenAI educational platforms remains 
unexamined. Investigating this correlation can inform the development of more trustworthy, 
inclusive, and effective GenAI platforms for education. This has led to the development of 
the third research question: 
RQ3: How do ethical dimensions such as fairness, bias, privacy, and AI responsibility in-
fluence user satisfaction in GenAI educational platforms?

3.1 Revamped Double Diamond 
This project followed a structured framework that balances exploration and refinement 
through an iterative approach to problem-solving, using a Revamped Double Diamond 
framework combined with agile management practices, illustrated in Figure 4. This process 
emphasizes human-centered design and design thinking (Nessler, 2018). 
 
Figure 4: Revamped Double Diamond 
The two diamonds represent different phases, each with its own focus. In the first diamond, 
we explored the problem space during the Discover phase. This involved reviewing various 
studies focusing on UX evaluation as well as theories related to learning, motivation, and 
engagement, as presented in the literature review. The identified gaps in our research laid 
the foundation for the development of our research questions. Additionally, we conducted 
several interviews and workshops to identify the needs of different user roles before entering 
the next phase. 
In the following phase, Define, which is also part of the first diamond, we synthesized our 
findings and insights. This included creating personas, developing a mind map to identify 
themes from existing evaluation instruments, and compiling a list of requirements and cri-
teria. This groundwork established the foundation for the creation of our UX evaluation 
instrument, specifically designed for GenAI educational platforms, which is one of our main 
deliverables.  
==PAGE:31==
 
 
 
31
 
In the next phase, Develop, which is the first phase of the second diamond, we gathered data 
from the UX evaluation instrument and performed a statistical analysis using various calcu-
lations in RStudio. Moreover, we focused on creating a requirements list for GenAI educa-
tional platforms, serving as our second deliverable. This list was developed based on the 
identified role-specific needs, existing UI guidelines and principles, as well as theories from 
the earlier phases. The requirements list acted as a guideline for developing various 
wireframes for the GenAI educational platform, ensuring we addressed the identified user 
needs and aligned with multiple UI guidelines. During this phase, numerous usability tests 
were conducted to ensure the platform was user-friendly and intuitive.  
The final phase, Deliver, involved connecting all the iterations of the developed wireframes 
to create a final clickable prototype of the GenAI educational platform. This phase also 
incorporated usability tests and refinements. 
As previously mentioned, each diamond comprises distinct phases that utilize various meth-

the structure of the double diamond is iterative, we navigated back and forth between meth-

3.3 Discover 
3.3.1 Desk Research  
While conducting desk research for the literature review, we wrote down the factors from 
the existing evaluation instruments on post-it notes. Once our research was complete, we 
utilized a clustering technique to organize all the notes. Similar post-it notes were grouped 
into clusters, and each cluster was given a headline that encompassed the grouped factors. 
This method allowed us to identify commonalities among the existing evaluation instru-
ments, which served as the basis for potential factors to incorporate into our evaluation in-
strument. 
3.3.2 Brainstorming Workshop 
To identify the needs of students, TAs, and teachers, 7 brainstorming sessions were held. 
Some of the workshops had only one participant, while others had 2-3. The workshops al-
lowed us to observe the needs of the different roles in a learning and teaching setting. The 
focus was not only on the functionalities of GenAI but also on all tools and resources that 
could help enhance the learning and teaching experiences for each role, including ideal or 
wishful ideas. Participants wrote down one functionality from any tool they used or one 
wishful thought on each post-it note. We noticed that some participants faced more chal-
lenges than others, and we provided guidance to those who needed it. Overall, these sessions 
gave us valuable insights into how each role interacts with educational tools and the func-
tionalities they find useful. The outcome of the brainstorming workshop was used for the 
following ranking workshop. 
3.3.3 Ranking Workshop 
As a second part of the workshop, we asked participants to rank all the written tools, func-
tionalities, and wishful thoughts based on what they found most useful. In other words, we 
wanted to know which elements they found most helpful and likely to improve their learning 
or teaching experience. Participants did this individually if they attended the workshop 
alone, or in close collaboration, discussing each element if they were in groups. This ap-
proach allowed us to gain insight into the needs that an educational GenAI platform should 
fulfill and laid the foundation for the personas. 
3.3.4 Semi-Structured Interview for Identifying User Needs 
To become familiar with ChatTutor, two initial conversations were held at the start of the 
project - one with a teacher and one with a student. Both had been introduced to ChatTutor 
through a course, and the student had worked on a project aimed at improving the platform. 
The goal was to gain insights into how ChatTutor is currently being used and to understand 
their initial impressions of the platform. This was used for the first iteration of user story 
maps. 
As mentioned, to gain a deeper understanding of users' needs, we conducted workshops. 
However, due to the discomfort expressed by two of the teachers with the workshop format, 
it was decided to gather their insights through semi-structured interviews. Each interview 
==PAGE:36==
 
 
 
36
 
lasted approximately 30 minutes and was guided by a combination of prepared and sponta-
neous questions regarding their needs. The interviews were recorded and subsequently tran-
scribed using AI technology. Following this, we documented their key needs on post-it 
notes, allowing us to compare them with the needs highlighted during the workshops. The 
needs from both the workshops and the interviews were used for the development of the 
personas. 
3.3.5 User Story Map  
To thoroughly understand the current version of ChatTutor’s functionalities and identify 
both opportunities and challenges, we created three separate user story maps, one for each 
user role. Each map specified the particular goals, activities, and tasks related to interacting 
with the system for that specific role. After gathering insights into the needs of each role 
through workshops and interviews, we developed a second iteration that featured updated 
user story maps, incorporating functionalities designed to meet those needs. We then created 
a third iteration to visualize the functionalities included in the final prototype, guided by the 
requirement lists that highlighted the identified user needs, UI principles, and theories from 
our literature review.  
3.4 Define 
3.4.1 Personas 
To gain a better understanding of the three user groups, we created three personas based on 
the insights gathered from the workshops and interviews. Each persona included a brief 
description of their personality, as well as their motivations, needs, and frustrations. This 
approach ensured that our evaluation instrument was specifically tailored to address the 
unique needs of each user group. Additionally, these personas played a crucial role in guid-
ing the user-centered design process for the development of the requirement list, which was 
essential when developing the UI for our prototype.  
3.4.2 Requirement List for Development of Instrument – Deliverable 1 
Based on the insights gained when reviewing literature on existing evaluation instruments 
and when conducting workshops and interviews, we developed a comprehensive list of re-
quirements and criteria for the evaluation instrument. The seven requirements and six crite-
ria served as a guide in selecting the most relevant questions and defining their format. It 
was an iterative process, as we continuously added new requirements and criteria through-
out the project. 
3.4.3 Brainstorming: 7 Steps for Development of Instrument - Deliverable 1 
The design of the questionnaire was based on a comprehensive 7-step framework that we 
developed during its creation. Steps 1 to 3 focused on gathering essential information and 
getting an overview of relevant theories and existing evaluation instruments, as described 
in 3.3.1 Desk Research. In steps 4 to 7, we reviewed and organized questions from existing 
==PAGE:37==
 
 
 
37
 
instruments and linked them to the appropriate theories. An overview of the 7 steps is pro-
vided below:  
1. Researched and explored existing evaluation instruments.  
2. Got an overview of the elements, factors, and questions identified in selected evaluation 
instruments.  
3. Clustered similar factors and questions to create a comprehensive map overview.  
4. Established subcategories for the larger clusters.  
5. Linked questions to relevant theories and removed those that did not have theoretical 
support.  
6. Developed questions that could be measured on a 5-point Likert scale, formulating 4-5 
questions for each factor based on existing questions from the mind map and theories.  
7. Reviewed all questions, tailoring some to be role-specific.  
3.4.4 Cognitive Interviewing  
To validate our instrument before distribution, we conducted six cognitive interviews. This 
technique helped ensure that our questionnaire would be more clear and easy to understand. 
In total, we conducted six short qualitative interviews, with two participants for each role, 
during which they went through all the questions one by one. The focus of these interviews 
included formulation, wording, spelling, relevance for each role, and identifying any dou-
ble-barreled questions. While the interviews featured some think-aloud elements, we also 
noted non-verbal cues, particularly when participants hesitated while reading a question. 
The feedback and observations we gathered allowed us to refine several questions and pro-
vided valuable insights into areas where the instrument needed improvement. As a result, 
we made adjustments that included changing wording, rephrasing questions, and deleting 
some items. Moreover, the instrument was reduced to a maximum of 40 questions for each 
role and organized so that the most important questions were placed in the beginning. Lastly, 
a new student was asked to fill out the instrument to test how long it would take to complete. 
3.5 Develop 
3.5.1 Quantitative Analysis of the Data from the Instrument 
The quantitative analysis of the data involves descriptive statistics, including the mean, 
standard deviation, maximum, and minimum, which were calculated for each factor. We 
did this using RStudio, which provided an overview of the responses gathered from our 
instrument. The data was extracted from SurveyXact into an Excel file, where it was orga-
nized to include only those respondents who completed the entire questionnaire. As nearly 
all participants indicated ChatGPT as their primary platform, responses from other plat-
forms were excluded from the analysis. Furthermore, given that the instrument is role-spe-
cific, calculations were conducted for each role. 
Cronbach’s Alpha 
To assess the internal consistency of our instrument, we calculated Cronbach’s alpha for 
each factor. Utilizing the same data file as for the descriptive analysis, we attempted to have 
==PAGE:38==
 
 
 
38
 
RStudio calculate the alpha values for each of the roles. Since the instrument is role-specific, 
not all participants answered the same questions, which led to some empty columns in the 
dataset. This initially resulted in inaccurate results. However, after excluding the empty col-
umns, we obtained correct results. 
One-Way ANOVA – Ethical Dimensions Influence on User Satisfaction 
To address our third research question, we conducted a one-way ANOVA test to determine 
if there was a statistically significant relationship between the four ethical dimensions (fair-
ness, bias, privacy, and AI responsibility) and user satisfaction. We extracted responses 
from the questions related to these dimensions from the UX evaluation instrument for each 
role. The correlation between each response and the User Satisfaction was calculated. The 
analysis was conducted in RStudio. 
3.5.2 Content Validity - Assessment of Instrument 
To validate the development of our UX evaluation instrument and ensure it included ques-
tions supported by the four selected theories (CLT, EVT, SDT, and Constructivist Learning 
Theory), we contacted two experts. During the meetings, we asked them to review our de-
veloped instrument and address our first research question. They provided valuable insights 
that helped us improve our instrument and asked questions regarding some of the decisions 
we made while developing the instrument. Since the content validity assessment meetings 
were held at the end of the process, we did not have time to refine our questionnaire based 
on the expert's feedback. Nonetheless, their insights offered us significant points for reflec-
tion and provided excellent ideas for future work. Both interviews were transcribed using 
AI technology. 
3.5.3 Requirement List for GenAI Educational Platforms – Deliverable 2 
A new list of requirements was created for the development of GenAI educational platforms. 
This list was based on the foundational information from our literature review, workshops, 
and interviews. The primary focus was on the identified user needs and existing UI princi-
ples when developing the requirements list. We established nine distinct requirements that 
include specific UI principles and guidelines, their theoretical foundations, and their link to 
relevant user needs. The requirements list aided us in developing a functional click-through 
prototype, ensuring that it adheres to the identified user needs alongside existing UI princi-
ples, guidelines, and theories. 
3.5.4 Rapid Prototyping - Interface Sketching  
To initiate the brainstorming process for a new version of the ChatTutor UI, we utilized the 
developed requirements and the second iteration of user story maps. To communicate our 
ideas and layouts effectively, we created quick sketches. These sketches focused on the 
header and sidebar of the page, the layout of the community page, and the chat interface. 
We chose these three elements as initial points of focus due to their significant impact on 
the user stories. The sketches of the interface provided the foundation for developing more 
advanced prototypes. 
==PAGE:39==
 
 
 
39
 
3.5.5 Medium-Fidelity Prototyping – Interface Wireframes 
The sketches from the rapid prototyping phase were transformed into elements on 
wireframes using Figma. Multiple iterations and variations of the main elements were cre-
ated, and a selection of these was chosen for user testing. Throughout the development of 
the wireframes, the requirements list was reviewed to ensure that the design met the criteria 
for a GenAI educational platform. 
3.5.6 Think-Aloud Test 
The medium-fidelity prototypes for the side and top bar and the layout of the community 
page were tested with a total of nine potential users. For the Think-Aloud test of the side 
and top bar, four versions were evaluated by four participants, one of which was the original 
ChatTutor layout. Participants were asked nine questions regarding their initial user satis-
faction and were required to score each prototype from 0 to 10. 
For the Think-Aloud testing of the community page, five participants assessed four different 
versions, including the original ChatTutor user interface. In this part of the test, participants 
answered six different questions. After conducting the tests, refinements were made based 
on the feedback received before integrating the improvements into the final high-fidelity 
click-through prototype.  
3.5.7 Think-Aloud Protocol - A/B testing 
To test the chat view, we conducted three separate tests using a Think-Aloud Protocol com-
bined with A/B testing. In the first test, three users were shown three different versions of 
the same wireframe, each with a different placement of the language model. Participants 
were asked to verbally express their initial thoughts and select the version they liked the 
most, while we took notes. The second test followed a similar format, but this time the 
participants viewed three variations of "inspirational" prompts. In the third test, two users 
participated, focusing on how references should be presented in the chat. By structuring the 
tests this way, we were able to gather more nuanced feedback, which subsequently was 
incorporated into the final prototype. 
3.6 Deliver 
3.6.1 High-Fidelity Prototyping – Click-Through Figma Prototype 
To closely replicate the final user experience, we developed a high-fidelity prototype in 
Figma. Building on insights from earlier tests and iterations, we refined the existing 
wireframes and designed additional elements to make more features functional within the 
clickable prototype.  
3.6.2 Usability Testing Using Developed Instrument 
The final prototype of the new user interface for ChatTutor was tested with a set of 20 
questions taken from the evaluation instrument we developed. These questions were trans-
formed into a survey using SurveyXact. A total of six potential users were given access to 
==PAGE:40==
 
 
 
40
 
the functional prototype to explore, and they were subsequently asked to complete the sur-
vey. This approach enabled us to evaluate how our interface performed across key parame-
ters relevant to a GenAI educational platform. It also provided a basis for comparing the 
user experience of our prototype with that of ChatGPT.

4.2.5 Discussion of  Selected Theories and Their Limitations  
This section will address the first research question: What learning and motivation theories 
should be integrated into a role-specific UX evaluation instrument for GenAI educational 
platforms, and how can it be developed? During the literature review, we gained insights 
into CLT, EVT, SDT, and Constructivist Learning Theory, which is why we consciously 
chose them as the theoretical foundation for developing the evaluation instrument. Each 
theory offers a distinct perspective on user engagement, learning, and motivation, and to-
gether they provide a holistic understanding of these concepts. CLT focuses on the cognitive 
load and mental effort that is required to learn in the best way possible, EVT emphasizes 
the learners’ belief about success and gives insight into their motivation, SDT provides an 
understanding of the human needs in order to enhance motivation and engagement, and 
Constructivist Learning Theory emphasizes the construction of the individual students’ 
learning path through reflections and experiences. These differences make them valuable 
when combined, as no single theory addresses both motivation, cognitive load, and person-
alized learning. 
Another reason for selecting CLT, EVT, SDT, and Constructivist Learning Theory is that 
these theories have been widely applied in the development of evaluation instruments, as 
described in the literature review in Chapter Two. Jose et al. (2024), Patac and Patac (2025), 
and Saihi et al. (2024) all apply CLT to evaluate the mental load associated with using 
ChatGPT and GenAI-based chatbots among students and teachers. Moreover, Jose et al. 
(2024) apply EVT to examine how students perceive the value of GenAI and its influence 
on their intrinsic motivation. Lastly, Saihi et al. (2024) argue for SDT and Constructivist 
Learning Theory, emphasizing the benefits of personalized and interactive learning, as 
GenAI platforms offer constructive and instant feedback. 
A theory closely related to Constructivist Learning Theory is Social Constructivist Learning 
Theory. Although the two theories share similar aspects, they differ from each other. While 
Constructivist Learning Theory emphasizes the individual learner's role in constructing their 
==PAGE:58==
 
 
 
58
 
own knowledge, Social Constructivist Learning Theory focuses more on the social pro-
cesses involved in the co-construction of knowledge (Palincsar, 1998). Psychologist Lev 
Vygotsky developed this theory in 1968 and argues that knowledge is developed through 
how people interact with one another in a social and cultural context (Fosnot and Perry, 
2005). While using this theory could have provided another perspective for the instrument, 
we chose to only focus on Constructivist Learning Theory since our instrument emphasizes 
the individual user’s journey toward learning and motivation rather than group-based learn-
ing. 
However, focusing solely on one of the theories might have been beneficial. This approach 
could have simplified the survey, creating a clearer alignment between the factors and re-
sulting in a shorter instrument, which could have encouraged more respondents to partici-
pate. Our current instrument experienced quite a few dropouts, meaning that some respond-
ents did not complete it in full, which will be elaborated on in section 4.3.3. However, con-
centrating on only one theory could have limited our insights and caused us to overlook 
crucial elements influencing learning experiences. 
When developing the instrument, we started by identifying potential factors and linking 
them to the theories. We compared these potential factors with those from the existing eval-
uation tools. Through an iterative process, we aligned the factors from the mind map with 
our potential factors, ultimately removing factors that lacked theoretical support, and 
merged similar ones for clarity. This structured approach ensured that the instrument had 
theoretical relevance by applying all four theories. However, it also introduced complexity, 
meaning the instrument needed to address numerous factors. This highlights the trade-off 
between theoretical depth and practical feasibility, which may have contributed to the length 
of the questionnaire and the number of participants who dropped out.  
The discussion above addresses RQ1 and provides the rationale for theoretical integration, 
explaining how these theories guided the iterative development of the UX evaluation instru-
ment for GenAI educational platforms.

4.3.1 Distribution of Instrument 
When developing the questionnaire, our initial intention was to distribute it only to users of 
ChatTutor. However, due to the limited user base of ChatTutor, we had to reevaluate our 
approach to increase the number of responses. As a result, we decided that the questionnaire 
should accommodate users from multiple platforms. This led us to include an initial question 
asking respondents to select their preferred GenAI platform. Besides selecting their pre-
ferred GenAI platform, respondents should also identify their role (student, TA, or teacher), 
their associated Danish university, and consent to participate. All elements can be found in 
appendix DEF01. 
As mentioned, we created the questionnaire using SurveyXact and distributed it to all the 
students, teachers, and TAs we contacted when conducting interviews/workshops. Addi-
tionally, we shared it with our own network on LinkedIn and Facebook. Initially, we planned 
to keep the survey open for two weeks, however, during that time, the number of respond-
ents was quite limited, particularly among teachers (n = 6) and TAs (n = 3). At that point, 
we focused only on respondents from Denmark, but to increase participation, we extended 
the survey's availability by an additional 1.5 weeks and expanded it to universities world-
wide. Additionally, we added a new question that asked participants to select their home 
country, including an "other" option for those whose universities were not listed (see appen-
dix DEF01). This approach allowed us to gather a broader range of respondents from a 
global audience, while still collecting relevant demographic information for our data analy-
sis. 
4.3.2 Quantitative Analysis of the Data from the Instrument 
After the survey had been open for 3.5 weeks, we analysed the data. We received a total of 
148 responses, however, only 103 participants completed the questionnaire. Among the 103 
respondents, there were 11 TAs, 23 teachers, and 69 students. Most of the responses came 
from Denmark and Germany, followed closely by France and Palestine. Additionally, we 
received responses from various other countries around the world, as detailed in appendix 
DEV02. Out of the respondents, 92 chose ChatGPT as their preferred chatbot or platform, 
which represents 89% of those who completed the survey. The remaining options had very 
few selections: three users selected ChatTutor, four users chose Microsoft Copilot, three 
users preferred Google Gemini, and only one selected DeepSeek. Therefore, we decided to 
only look at the respondents using ChatGPT. Analysing the limited responses for the other 
platforms would make the data analysis unreliable. 
The analysis aimed to calculate the mean, standard deviation, minimum, and maximum val-
ues, as well as Cronbach’s alpha for each factor within the dataset for each user role. In 
RStudio, we initially defined each factor by grouping all the relevant questions, regardless 
of their relevance to the specific role being analysed. This resulted in some factors being 
either negative or returned as “N/A” when calculating Cronbach’s alpha. Further investiga-
tion revealed that this was due to the role-specific questions. Some questions were only 
designed for a particular user group, meaning that the corresponding columns contained 
empty values for the other roles. This caused inconsistencies in the reliability calculations.

4.3.3 Discussion of Reliability 
This discussion focuses on addressing RQ1: What learning and motivation theories should 
be integrated into a role-specific UX evaluation instrument for GenAI educational plat-
forms, and how can it be developed? Establishing the reliability of the developed instrument 
is an important step in its development.  
To evaluate the internal consistency of the factors, we calculated Cronbach’s alpha values 
for each factor across the three respondent groups: teachers (n = 23), TAs (n = 11), and 
students (n = 69). Cronbach’s alpha is a measure of scale reliability, with values above 0.7 
generally considered acceptable and values below 0.6 indicating potential issues with inter-
nal consistency. It is important to keep in mind the differing sample sizes for each group, as 
these can significantly influence the reliability scores. In particular, the estimates for TAs 
and teachers may be more unstable and less representative of population-level consistency. 
Several factors demonstrated low internal consistency among teachers. The factors Trust (α 
= 0.50), Usefulness (α = 0.60), and Engagement (α = 0.55) fell below the acceptable level 
of internal consistency. Additionally, System Quality (α = 0.34), Human vs. Chatbot (α = 
0.33), and Cognitive Load (α = 0.39) exhibited very poor reliability. This indicates possible 
measurement inconsistencies, suggesting that these factors may require revision in future 
iterations of the evaluation instrument. In contrast, factors like Ethics (α = 0.87) and As-
sessment Efficiency (α = 0.98) showed strong internal consistency, indicating that they do 
not need modification in the upcoming iterations. 
For TAs, strong alpha values were observed in Assessment Efficiency (α = 0.98), User Sat-
isfaction (α = 0.96), and Usefulness (α = 0.92). These high values indicate a strong level of 
consistency in these factors. However, other areas, such as Human vs. Chatbot (α = 0.24), 
Trust (α = 0.45), and Personalized Learning (α = 0.37), showed low reliability. These in-
consistencies may be due to the small sample size (n = 11) or issues related to the alignment 
or clarity of the questions. 
Among the students, who formed the largest group (n = 69), the reliability scores were gen-
erally more stable. Strong reliability was observed in the categories of Ease of Use (α = 
0.83), User Satisfaction (α = 0.82), Usefulness (α = 0.82), and Engagement (α = 0.76). Other 
==PAGE:63==
 
 
 
63
 
factors, such as Human vs. Chatbot (α = 0.66) and Ethics (α = 0.68), fell within an acceptable 
threshold. However, System Quality (α = 0.31), Cognitive Load (α = 0.37), and Personalized 
Learning (α = 0.41) exhibited poor internal consistency, indicating potential issues with the 
questions used in these factors. 
Overall, the reliability analysis reveals that several key factors in the UX evaluation instru-
ment demonstrate strong internal consistency, particularly within the student group, where 
the larger sample size enables more stable and reliable estimates. In contrast, lower reliabil-
ity scores were observed among teachers and TAs, especially for factors such as Trust, Hu-
man vs Chatbot, and System Quality. This may be due to both poor consistency within these 
factors and the constraints of the small sample sizes. To enhance the instrument’s validity 
and applicability, future iterations should focus on revising the low-performing factors and 
validating the tool with larger, more balanced participant groups to improve generalizabil-
ity. Additionally, the fact that a significant number of participants (n = 66) did not complete 
the questionnaire suggests that it may have been too long. Therefore, it would be relevant 
to consider removing some questions associated with the lower-performing factors.

4.3.5 Discussion of Ethical Dimensions Influence on User Satisfaction  
This discussion will address the third and last research question: How do ethical dimensions 
such as fairness, bias, privacy, and AI responsibility influence user satisfaction in GenAI 
educational platforms? 
The four ethical dimensions of fairness, bias, privacy, and AI responsibility were con-
sciously chosen based on the examination conducted by Fu and Weng (2024) regarding the 
ethical and responsible use of AIED. However, Fu and Weng (2024) also highlight other 
important ethical dimensions within each theme, which should be prioritized when design-
ing generative AI systems. The reason for focusing on the four mentioned ethical dimen-
sions is due to our role as researchers/developers and their ease of measurement.  
Fairness ensures equal learning opportunities for all user roles while providing personalized 
support and promoting social justice, which are crucial elements for any GenAI educational 
platform. Bias refers to the prejudiced outputs from the GenAI platform, affecting how users 
perceive the reliability of the responses. The third ethical dimension is privacy, and includes 
the storage and usage of users’ data, ensuring it is protected and that users' privacy is main-
tained throughout their learning experience with the GenAI educational platform. The final 
dimension is AI responsibility, which relates to how trustworthy users view the GenAI ed-
ucational platform, focusing on how it operates transparently and ethically.  
Incorporating these four dimensions from Fu and Weng (2024)’s vision into the UX evalu-
ation instrument for GenAI educational platforms ensures a safe, reliable, and trustworthy 
learning platform. 
However, the statistical analysis in section 4.3.4 showed that none of the four ethical di-
mensions had a statistically significant influence on User Satisfaction across any of the three 
==PAGE:66==
 
 
 
66
 
user roles (P-values > 0.05). This indicates that while ethical dimensions are important in 
theory, they may not significantly influence users' evaluation of their experience with 
GenAI platforms. An explanation could be that users tend to prioritize learning-related func-
tionalities and features over ethical transparency when evaluating satisfaction. Nevertheless, 
it remains crucial to include the ethical dimensions of fairness, bias, privacy, and AI respon-
sibility into the development of UX evaluation instruments, as mentioned by Fu and Weng 
(2024). 
4.3.6 Cognitive Validation of Evaluation Instrument 
To properly validate our UX evaluation instrument, we reached out to two experts. This 
validation should have occurred earlier in the process to allow us to implement their feed-
back and refine the instrument before collecting data. However, at the time, we were not 
aware of this type of validation, which is why it was conducted at this point in the process.   
The experts we consulted were Lasse Nielsen Langendorf (appendix DEV03) and Troels 
Peter Mourits Jensen (appendix DEV04), both Ph.D. students in the Department of Mathe-
matics and Computer Science at the Technical University of Denmark. In the two separate 
meetings, we asked them to review our evaluation instrument and our first research ques-
tion, specifically whether the instrument was aligned with CLT, EVT, SDT, and Construc-
tivist Learning Theory. Both Lasse and Troels acknowledged that the questions and factors 
had a clear connection to the theories. However, they both commented on the length of the 
questionnaire, noting that it was too long. What they did not agree upon was our decision to 
use a 5-point Likert scale. Troels argued that we should use a 6-point Likert scale rather 
than a 5-point Likert scale to encourage respondents to express an opinion, avoiding neutral 
answers. In contrast, Lasse supported the use of a 5-point Likert scale, as forcing respond-
ents to select an option when they have no opinion can lead to unreliable data.  
Additionally, they both found the wording of the questions to be clear and easy to under-
stand. However, Troels pointed out a few double-barreled questions that we should consider 
rephrasing, such as: I can quickly access and use key features like menus, copying, and 
pasting. He also suggested that it could be interesting to gather more demographic details, 
such as gender, age, and the frequency of respondents' use of GenAI chatbots. 
These insights would have been useful to address before sending the UX evaluation instru-
ment out. However, they should be included in a future version of the instrument.  
4.3.7 Development of Requirement List – Deliverable 2 
As mentioned, most existing platforms are developed by computer scientists who focus on 
technical feasibility and system functionalities, often overlooking the specific needs of stu-
dents, teachers, and TAs. Therefore, there is a need for a role-sensitive requirements list to 
guide the design of GenAI educational platforms. 
To create the requirement list tailored for GenAI educational chatbots, it was essential to 
include the theories outlined in Table 4, the factors presented in Table 6, and the specific 
needs of each persona involved in the development process. Furthermore, given that this 
==PAGE:67==
 
 
 
67
 
requirement list also emphasizes the user interface of the platform, incorporating fundamen-
tal UI principles into this mapping process was necessary.  
The principles that were selected to include in this requirement list were taken from the 
well-known Eight Golden Rules developed by Shneiderman et al. (2016), the seven practical 
guidelines for AI-driven UI design for web applications by Costa et al. (2024), and the six 
design principles for GenAI applications by Weisz et al. (2024). Where the Eight Golden 
Rules by Shneiderman et al. (2016) are developed as guidelines for nearly all interactive 
systems, the seven practical guidelines by Costa et al. (2024) are specifically developed for 
AI applications to help designers create interfaces that successfully utilize the potential of 
AI while prioritizing user needs, accessibility, and ethical considerations. The goal of these 
seven guidelines is to facilitate users in completing desired tasks in a pleasant, efficient, and 
safe manner. The last set of principles included in the development of the requirements list 
was the six principles for GenAI applications proposed by Weisz et al. (2024). These prin-
ciples aim to address the unique characteristics of GenAI user experiences and assist de-
signers in creating user experiences that promote effective and safe usage. 
While Costa et al. (2024) and Shneiderman et al. (2016) both concentrate solely on UI, 
Weisz et al. (2024) present six principles that emphasize the entire UX design process and 
incorporate strategies to consider throughout the design process. By incorporating all of 
these principles and guidelines into the requirements list, we were able to integrate UI prin-
ciples into each requirement while still maintaining a focus on the overall UX design of a 
GenAI educational platform. Each requirement is accompanied by a brief explanation, fol-
lowed by several key UI principles and guidelines. Afterwards, the connection to the rele-
vant theories (CLT, EVT, Constructivist Learning Theory, and SDT) is explained. Finally, 
we highlight their connections to users' needs. Below is our list of requirements

ing process (Saihi et al., 2024). 
  
User need:    
“I need real-time system feedback to ensure that I’m using the platform as intended.” - General insight  
  
7. Foster Engagement and Motivation  
The platform should include features that stimulate curiosity, enjoyment, and a sense of accomplishment to 
promote intrinsic motivation.  
  
Key UI principles/guidelines:  
Keep users in control: Give the users a feeling of being in charge of the interface (Shneiderman et al., 2016). 
Affordance: The platform should clearly indicate how to use it interactively (Costa et al., 2024). 
Ease of forgetting: Allow users to pause/resume and replay learning components (Costa et al., 2024). 
  
Theoretical foundation:   
EVT: Intrinsic motivation increases when learners perceive tasks as valuable and feel confident in their abil-
ity to succeed (Jose et al., 2024).   
SDT: When users find the GenAI chatbot enjoyable, it enhances their intrinsic motivation, which is crucial 
for engagement (Saihi et al., 2024). 
  
User need:  
“Creating examples and stories with AI helps me make lectures more engaging without starting from 
scratch, which is time-consuming.” - Teacher  
==PAGE:70==
 
 
 
70
 
“Seeing examples gives me a clear starting point when I don’t know how to approach an exercise.”  - Stu-
dent  
“I create examples to break down complex topics and make them easier for students to understand.” - TA  
  
8. Integrate Human-AI Collaboration  
The platform should clearly indicate when to use generative AI and when to seek human support (from TAs 
or teachers), ensuring seamless transitions between the two. These transitions should be possible directly 
within the platform to optimize efficiency and support effective learning.  
  
Key UI principles/guidelines:  
Offer informative feedback: Clearly indicate to the user when switching between modes (Costa et al., 2024; 
Shneiderman et al., 2016). 
Reliability & Predictability: Clarify who (GenAI/human) is responding (Costa et al., 2024). 
Help and Documentation: Explain when to use GenAI vs. human support (Costa et al., 2024). 
  
Theoretical foundation:  
EVT: Supports the idea that when users clearly understand how to navigate between the GenAI chatbot and 
human help, it increases their expectancy of success (Jose et al., 2024). 
  
User need:  
“AI can help answer frequently asked questions from students and TAs, making it easier to manage re-
sponses when time is tight.”  - Teacher  
“Answering common questions on a visible platform for students helps me save time.” - TA  
  
9. Maintain High System Quality and Performance  
The system must be responsive, stable, and fast, ensuring users stay engaged in the learning process without 
technical interruptions.  
  
Key UI principles/guidelines:  
Prevent errors: Make sure that the users cannot make serious errors. If an error occurs, the interface should 
offer simple, constructive, and specific instructions for recovery (Shneiderman et al., 2016). 
Feedback: The platform should provide the users with loading indicators and status (Costa et al., 2024; 
Shneiderman et al., 2016). 
Help and Documentation: The system should assist users when in doubt (Costa et al., 2024). 
  
Theoretical foundation:  
CLT: A stable and reliable system minimizes extraneous cognitive load by preventing distractions and inter-
ruptions, allowing users to focus fully on the learning tasks (Patac and Patac, 2025). 
  
User need:   
“A stable system keeps distractions away so I can stay focused on the task.” – General insight  
 
The presented requirement list is to be used in the development of GenAI educational plat-
forms to ensure that it effectively addresses user-specific needs and encourages ethical and 
safe use.  
4.3.8 Discussion on User Needs and UX Requirements 
This section addresses the second research question: What are the different needs of users 
(students, teachers, teaching assistants), and how do these needs influence UX requirements 
for GenAI educational platforms in higher education? Understanding these distinct roles 
and their unique needs is essential for designing a platform that effectively supports all us-
ers. Each group’s needs directly shape key UX requirements such as interface simplicity, 
personalization, role-specific tools, feedback mechanisms, and transparency. 
 
==PAGE:71==
 
 
 
71
 
Students’ influence on UX requirements 
Students, like our persona Anna, primarily use the platform as a self-directed learning tool 
to explore concepts, ask questions, and practice skills. Their expressed need to avoid feeling 
overwhelmed strongly shapes the requirement to minimize cognitive load and ease of use. 
This calls for a clean, intuitive interface with structured, step-by-step guidance and imme-
diate, informative feedback that keeps students motivated. 
Additionally, students also seek personalized learning paths that adapt to their prior 
knowledge and learning level. The platform must enable easy discovery of personalization 
settings and maintain users’ sense of control, supporting autonomy and competence in line 
with Constructivist Learning Theory and SDT. Therefore, real-time feedback mechanisms 
are essential for helping students clearly understand their progress and the steps they need 
to take next. 
Moreover, students’ desire for enjoyable learning experiences influences the need to foster 
engagement and motivation. Features that promote curiosity and a sense of accomplishment, 
such as interactive examples and the ability to pause, replay, or explore learning compo-
nents, enhance intrinsic motivation, making the platform more effective and enjoyable. 
Lastly, although the platform serves multiple roles, students also benefit from role-specific 
functionalities. This could include hints or scaffolds designed specifically for learners, help-
ing them navigate exercises confidently without unnecessary complexity. 
In summary, students influence UX requirements related to minimizing cognitive load, ease 
of use and accessibility, personalized learning paths, immediate and informative feed-
back, fostering engagement and motivation, and offering role-specific support and function-
alities that cater to their unique learning needs. 
Teachers’ influence on UX requirements 
Teachers’ needs, like our persona Peter’s, focus on efficiency, clarity, and maintaining high-
quality content creation. They utilize the platform to generate quizzes, exams, and instruc-
tional materials, which require features tailored specifically to their role. This includes tools 
for content organization and exam question generation, allowing teachers to delegate routine 
tasks to AI. As a result, they can concentrate on pedagogical decisions, thereby enhancing 
their autonomy and competence. These features should be intuitive and easily discoverable, 
considering teachers’ varying levels of technological familiarity, while also minimizing 
their workload.  
Additionally, teachers prioritize ethical transparency and data privacy, necessitating clear 
and accessible explanations of how their data is managed and how GenAI responses are 
generated. This transparency is crucial for building trust and ensuring compliance with pri-
vacy standards. Moreover, to prevent disruptions during teaching or preparation, teachers 
emphasize the importance of system stability and performance. They require a responsive 
and reliable user experience. 
==PAGE:72==
 
 
 
72
 
To summarize, teachers influence UX requirements related to role-specific support and 
functionalities, ease of use and accessibility, engagement and motivation, ethical transpar-
ency and data privacy, as well as high system quality and performance. 
TAs’ influence on UX requirements 
TAs bridge the roles of students and teachers, supporting learning through example creation 
and clarifying complex topics. Their responsibilities highlight the need for role-specific sup-
port and functionalities that allow them to create tailored examples, scaffold explanations, 
and efficiently manage frequently asked questions.  
Since TAs often handle common queries and scaffold learning, the platform must support 
human-AI collaboration, with clear UI elements indicating when AI assistance is used ver-
sus when human intervention is required. This ensures seamless transitions and efficient 
handling of complex or sensitive questions. The platform also needs to foster engagement 
and motivation by enabling TAs to participate actively in content creation and student in-
teraction. 
This means that TAs influence UX requirements related to role-specific support and func-
tionalities, human-AI collaboration, ease of use and accessibility, as well as engagement 
and motivation. 
To summarize, the needs of students, teachers, and TAs significantly shape the UX require-
ments of GenAI educational platforms. While students require simplicity, personalization, 
and emotional safety, teachers need tools for efficient content creation, control, and data 
transparency. TAs lie in between, supporting personalized learning and bridging the human-
AI interaction. Designing for these roles demands differentiated UX requirements that bal-
ance autonomy, support, and engagement to meet each group's needs and goals effectively.

example, when designing a new UI and user story of a GenAI educational platform.

As previously mentioned, the selected wireframes from the Develop phase were combined

functionalities. When we compared the average scores of this assessment to the responses

security. This observation stands despite our prototype outperforming students' responses 
to the same questions about ChatGPT. Additionally, features of the platform should be com-
municated more clearly to prevent users from needing to figure them out independently. It 
was also noted that a copying feature for answers on the community page would be benefi-
cial.

was added to all answers to questions in the community. Moreover, a pop-up was introduced

privacy policy.

5.1 Conclusion 
This thesis investigated how user experience for GenAI educational platforms in higher ed-
ucation can be more effectively evaluated and designed. Through the development of a role-
specific UX evaluation instrument and a requirement list for UX and UI design, this study 
addressed three research questions concerning the integration of learning theories, user role 
needs, and ethical dimensions. 
The first research question examined how a role-specific UX evaluation instrument can be 
developed by integrating learning theories. By drawing on Cognitive Load Theory, Expec-
tancy-Value Theory, Self-Determination Theory, and Constructivist Learning Theory, the 
instrument was designed to assess not only usability, but also deeper educational and moti-
vational aspects of the user experience. Cognitive Load Theory guided the inclusion of fac-
tors related to simplicity and clarity in design. Meanwhile, Expectancy-Value Theory and 
Self-Determination Theory shaped the evaluation criteria for autonomy, feedback, and per-
ceived usefulness. Constructivist Learning Theory played a key role in the incorporation of 
personalization and interactivity. The outcome was a multidimensional instrument capable 
of evaluating GenAI educational platforms across diverse educational roles rooted in theo-
retical foundations and pedagogical relevance. 
The second research question explored how the diverse needs of students, teachers, and TAs 
shape UX requirements. Findings from user research revealed that students prioritize ease 
of use, minimal cognitive load, personalized guidance, and engagement. In contrast, teach-
ers focus on efficiency, high-quality content generation, system reliability, and ethical trans-
parency, while TAs seek functionalities that facilitate scaffolding, content adaptation, and 
seamless collaboration with students and GenAI. These varying needs directly influenced 
the design of both deliverables. The first deliverable, the UX evaluation instrument, incor-
porates role-specific items to assess satisfaction and effectiveness in relation to the unique 
requirements of each role. The second deliverable, the requirements list, translates these 
identified needs into actionable UX specifications. For instance, requirements were formu-
lated to ensure a simplified and clean UI for students to minimize cognitive overload, while 
teachers’ needs informed requirements for GenAI features that support curriculum-aligned 
content creation, such as exam questions and examples. TAs required easy navigation be-
tween supporting students and using GenAI, which influenced the requirement: Integrate 
Human-AI Collaboration.   
The third research question explored the role of ethical dimensions (fairness, bias, privacy, 
and AI responsibility) in UX evaluation and their impact on user satisfaction. Although 
these dimensions were explicitly incorporated into the UX evaluation instrument, following 
Fu and Weng (2024)’s ethical AI vision, statistical analysis indicated no significant corre-
==PAGE:84==
 
 
 
84
 
lation between the ethical dimensions and user satisfaction across any user group. This find-
ing suggests that, while ethical principles hold conceptual importance, they may not cur-
rently be regarded by users as central to their satisfaction with GenAI platforms. For teach-
ers, ethical concerns might be perceived as institutional responsibilities. Whereas for stu-
dents, trust may be assumed as a default. For TAs, the focus may remain primarily on per-
formance and support. Nevertheless, the inclusion of these ethical dimensions in our UX 
evaluation instrument is essential for fostering transparency and ensuring long-term trust-
worthiness in the evaluated platform. 
In summary, this thesis presents a UX evaluation instrument and a requirement list for de-
signing and evaluating GenAI educational platforms that align learning theory, role-specific 
requirements, and ethical considerations. The two deliverables offer practical tools for re-
searchers, designers, and educational institutions aiming to develop inclusive, effective, and 
ethically responsible GenAI learning environments. As GenAI technologies continue to 
evolve, this research highlights the importance of grounding their development in actual 
user needs, learning and motivational theories, and ethical expectations of diverse educa-
tional stakeholders. 
5.2 Limitations 
During the project, we acknowledged that several dimensions and aspects were not covered 
in the literature review. The focus of the literature review has primarily been on existing 
instruments rather than on requirements. In the literature review, we identified four key 
theories: Cognitive Load Theory (CLT), Expectancy-Value Theory (EVT), Self-Determi-
nation Theory (SDT), and Constructivist Learning Theory. However, other motivational and 
learning theories may also be relevant when examining the educational context. Neverthe-
less, we were able to identify 12 relevant factors (Ethical Considerations, Trust, Usefulness, 
Human vs. Chatbot, Engagement, System Quality, Cognitive Load, Personalized Learning, 
Ease of Use, Efficiency, Assessment Efficiency, and User Satisfaction) that provides a ho-
listic view of the user experience in a GenAI educational platform. Regarding the instru-
ment, it has not been altered since its validation by the two experts, which presents another 
limitation, as we collected data before its validation. This necessitates a new iteration that 
requires further testing and validation. Additionally, our response rate was low, which limits 
the reliability of our data. When analyzing the data, we only investigated the influence of 
the four ethical dimensions (fairness, bias, privacy, and AI responsibility) on User Satisfac-
tion, revealing non-significant results. This limited focus may suggest that other important 
relationships between the ethical dimensions and additional relevant factors were not ex-
plored. Lastly, we did not get any validation of our requirements list, which could have been 
beneficial for formulation and clarity. 
5.3 Future Work 
Based on the mentioned limitations, further research could be done in several areas. 
As mentioned, the UX evaluation instrument was validated after data collection, which ne-
cessitates the need for a new iteration that incorporates refined insights. To establish the 
==PAGE:85==
 
 
 
85
 
reliability and construct validity of the evaluation instrument, it could be beneficial to ex-
amine both convergent and discriminant validity, as well as conduct Confirmatory Factor 
Analysis (CFA). When assessing convergent validity, we ensure that the items measure the 
same constructs and are related to one another within each factor. This should be considered 
alongside discriminant validity, which distinguishes between items that represent different 
theoretical constructs (Brown, 2015). This evaluation is important as it highlights which 
items are not correlated, leading to a more consistent evaluation instrument. In this study, 
we have already calculated Cronbach’s Alpha to assess internal consistency. The results 
showed lower reliability among teachers and TAs for factors such as System Quality, Hu-
man vs. Chatbot, and Trust. By applying CFA in future research, we can statistically evalu-
ate whether the items within these factors align with established theories and prior research 
(Brown, 2015). Essentially, CFA helps identify any misalignment of factors or poorly per-
forming items, which can be compared to the values of Cronbach’s Alpha. Applying CFA, 
along with assessments of convergent and discriminant validity, will strengthen the reliabil-
ity of the evaluation instrument. Furthermore, these analyses will identify items and factors 
that are poorly related, allowing for their removal and resulting in a shorter instrument for 
future iterations. However, this iteration will require more time compared to the iteration of 
implementing the insights and feedback we got from the two experts.  
Although the ethical dimension did not show any statistically significant influence on User 
Satisfaction, previous studies, such as the one by Fu and Weng (2024), highlight that ethics 
have an impact on adoption, which is influenced by user satisfaction. Therefore, it would be 
interesting to investigate why no correlation was observed between the four ethical dimen-
sions (fairness, bias, privacy, and AI responsibility) and User Satisfaction across the differ-
ent user roles. One approach to test this could involve scenario-based assessments, where 
the GenAI educational platform generates unethical responses, such as biased content or 
data misuse. This would allow us to see whether students are unconcerned about ethical 
dimensions. Another perspective to consider is that users may not fully understand the im-
plications of the four ethical dimensions. Therefore, providing a brief explanation of each 
dimension in future work could be beneficial, ensuring that users understand its meaning 
within the context of a GenAI educational platform. 
Ultimately, if we had more time, we would have had experts validate our requirements list. 
Unfortunately, that was not the case, so this task is set for future work. Validating the re-
quirements could improve their quality, especially regarding clarity, formulation, theoreti-
cal foundations, and connection to UI principles and guidelines.
