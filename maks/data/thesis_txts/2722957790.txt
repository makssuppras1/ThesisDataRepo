Abstract
A numerical method to simulate the full trajectory of single ions in a plasma under the influence
of the Lorentz force was created and coded in Python. First, it was studied whether an often-used
approximation for the speed of single ions was valid. We found that the approximation was valid
for the lighter ions in static electric and magnetic fields but not for the heavier ions and that it
was not valid for any of the ions in dynamic electric and magnetic fields. The dynamics of ions in
both static and dynamic fields were studied. The dynamics of the ions were studied by looking
at the position and velocity of the ions and finding their mean position and mean kinetic energy.
We found that the ratio between the charge and the mass of the ions is important for how the
dynamics of ions in the plasma. Heavier ions are more difficult to set in motion but can move
more freely around the domain because it is not locked in and the potential lines in the plasma.
We thus found that the distribution of the heavier ions was greater than that of the lighter ions.
The energy in the ions increased throughout the simulations and the heavier ions gained more
energy than the lighter ones. When changing from static fields to dynamic fields, the scattering
of the ions increased and the ions were supplied with more energy.

Introduction and motivation
Transitioning to more green and sustainable energy is a very important agenda in the world. To
make the transition from fossil fuels to carbon-free energy we need not just one but multiple
solutions.
There are already a couple of renewable energy sources such as wind and solar. These energy
sources have their downsides which are they need a lot of space to produce the required energy
and are dependent on the weather which is unpredictable.
Another way to produce energy is by a fission power plant which is not reliable for the weather
but has its own drawbacks. The drawback is that the fuel used is rare and it has radioactive
waste which can be very dangerous if not treated correctly.
A promising energy source is thermal fusion. It is not dependent on the weather and has no
radioactive waste like a fission powerplant other than the chamber itself. Fusion still has some
drawbacks but they har mostly technological.
The efficiency of a fusion power plant is measured with a Q-factor
Q = Pfus
Pheat
where Pfus is the power being released by the fusion reactions in a reactor and Pheat is the heating
power being supplied in normal operating conditions. For a fusion powerplant to have a surplus
of energy the Q-factor should be above 1 (Q > 1). It is planned that the tokamak experiment
ITER will achieve a Q ≈10 when it is finished being built and will be the first of magnetically
confined fusion experiments to break even.[6]
1.1
General concepts
Nuclear fusion is when two smaller atoms merge into a larger atom which is the concept of the
sun. In the sun lighter atoms as H will merge together to create He and send out energy.[6]
For fusion, there are 3 important variables that are collected in the Lawson criterion nTτ. n is
the density, T is the temperature and τ is the energy confinement time. The Lawson criterion
compares the rate of energy being generated by fusion reactions within the fusion fuel to the rate
of energy losses to the environment. There are two main ways the world is making fusion. That
is by magnetical confinement or Inertial confinement. Magnetic confinement fusion tries to have
high energy confinement time whereas inertial confinement fusion tries to have a high density and
both of them have a high temperature. This project belongs under magnetic confinement fusion
which uses high temperatures.
The kind of magnetic confinement in this project is an experiment called a tokamak. A tokamak
uses a gas that is heated up to very high temperatures at 150−200 million °C so the gas becomes
ionized and is classified as plasma. Plasma is the 4’th state of matter and consists of ions and
electrons.
Of the thermonuclear reactions, DT stands out as being the most feasible because it has the
largest reaction cross-section at accessible temperatures. The DT reaction looks like

of energy and momentum conservation.

temperatures are needed. In order for a controlled thermal fusion reaction to be economically
useful, more energy must be made than put into the system. The variable Q is used to determine
when an experiment break-even. The lifetime of the plasma with the correct conditions is essential
to break even and is characterized by energy confinement time τ. One of the problems for the
energy confinement time is the anomalous radial transport of energy and particles in the Edge

1.2
Motivation of project
From observation of the plasma in experiments impurities are found. Impurities are ions that are
different then the main gas put into the experiment. These impurities come from the equipment
inside such as the chamber walls and the diverter. The impurities found in some experiments are
B, Be, C, and W. The experiments have reported both negative and positive things from having
impurities in the plasma. One of the negative consequences is if the impurities get into the core
plasma because it can then radiate and cool of the plasma and make it disrupt. Especially W is
bad to have in the core plasma because it is not fully ionized in the core and can therefore radiate
strongly[3]. One of the positive consequences reported is an increase in the energy confinement
time if the impurities are injected into the Edge layer.[3][4]
Often numerical codes do not take impurities into account and if they do it is not often the
full trajectory that is simulated. The first step to a better understanding of the effects of the
interaction between impurities and plasma is by simulating the dynamics and interaction between
impurities and plasma.

3.3
Different simulations
There are 8 different simulations that are done for different atoms. The different simulations will
be explained in the following
- Static fields, position at LCFS and zero velocity (SEZ): This simulation has static
inhomogeneous electric and magnetic fields, the initial position of the particles is at the
LCFS around x = 0m and spread over all y, and the initial velocity of the particles is 0.
This simulation is done for the atoms H, He, Li, Be, B, C, N, O, Ne, Na, Mg, and W.
- Static fields, position in SOL and zero velocity (SSZ): This simulation has static
inhomogeneous electric and magnetic fields, the initial position of the particles is at the SOL
around x = 0.015m and spread over all y, and the initial velocity of the particles is 0. This
simulation is done for the atoms H, He, Li, Be, B, C, Mg, and W.
- Static fields, position at LCFS and not zero velocity (SEM): This simulation has
static inhomogeneous electric and magnetic fields, the initial position of the particles is at
the LCFS around x = 0m and y = 0m, and the initial velocity of the particles is taken from
a Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution of speed for T = 1eV and in a random direction. This
simulation is done for the atoms H, He, Li, Be, B, C, N, O, Ne, Na, Mg, and W.
- Static fields, position in SOL and not zero velocity (SSM): This simulation has
static inhomogeneous electric and magnetic fields, the initial position of the particles is at
the SOL around x = 0.0.15m and y = 0m, and the initial velocity of the particles is taken
from a Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution of speed for T = 1eV and in a random direction.
This simulation is done for the atoms H, He, Li, Be, B, C, N, O, Ne, Na, Mg, and W.
- Dynamic fields, position at LCFS and zero velocity (DEZ): This simulation has
dynamic inhomogeneous electric and a static magnetic field, the initial position of the par-
ticles is at the LCFS around x = 0m and spread over all y, and the initial velocity of the
particles is 0. This simulation is done for the atoms H, He, Li, Be, B, C, N, O, Ne, Na, Mg,
and W.
- Dynamic fields, position in SOL and zero velocity (DSZ): This simulation has dy-
namic inhomogeneous electric and a static magnetic field, the initial position of the particles
is at the SOL around x = 0.015m and spread over all y, and the initial velocity of the par-
ticles is 0. This simulation is done for the atoms H, He, Li, Be, B, C, Mg, and W.
- Dynamic fields, position at LCFS and not zero velocity (DEM): This simulation
has dynamic inhomogeneous electric and a static magnetic field, the initial position of the
particles is at the LCFS around x = 0m and y = 0m, and the initial velocity of the particles
is taken from a Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution of speed for T = 1eV and in a random
direction. This simulation is done for the atoms H, He, Li, Be, B, C, N, O, Ne, Na, Mg,
and W.
- Dynamic fields, position in SOL and not zero velocity(DSM): This simulation

5.6
Discussion
This discussion is for the simulations with static electric and magnetic fields.
Section 5.1 showed the velocity drift approximation is good for the lighter ions but for ions, as
heavy as W the approximation does not work. This must be because the gyroradius is so big that
the restriction that the fields should be nearly uniform over the spatial extent of a gyroradius is
not upheld for W ions in our system.
Section 5.2 showed that the heavier ions were harder to move but because they have a bigger
gyroradius and therefore do not follow the potential lines as well and will spread more over time
than the lighter ions. Section 5.3 and 5.5 showed that if the initial position of the ions is in the
SOL where the potential is lower than at the LCFS the ions will not move across the LCFS and
towards the core plasma. This is because it is harder for the ions to go against the potential.
Section 5.2 and 5.4 showed that if the initial position of the ions is close to the LCFS the ions
will be spread on both sides of the LCFS and that the lighter ions will on average move further
into the plasma than the heavier ions but because the spread of the heavier ions is larger the ions
that will move the furthest into the plasma is W.
For the average kinetic energy section 5.2, 5.3, 5.4 and 5.5 showed that all the ions, except for H
in section 5.5, gain energy throughout the simulation. This means all the ions will take energy
from the plasma. The amount of energy the ions gained is depended on the mass of the ions. The
heavier the ions the more energy they gained in average.

6.6
Discussion
This discussion is for the simulations with dynamic fields and comparison with the simulations of
the static fields.
Section 6.1 showed the velocity drift approximation is not valid for any of these simulations of
the different ions. For the heavier ions, this is as expected because of the large gyroradius. For
the lighter ions e.g. H with a small gyroradius the simulation is neither valid, which means the
fields vary too much over a gyroradius.
For the trajectory of the ions for the simulations with dynamic fields, it is hard to see if they
follow the potential line because the potential lines also change in time. It seems they are more
chaotic than the trajectory of the ions for the simulations with static fields.
For the ions with the initial position at SOL as in section 6.3 and 6.5 the heavier ions mean
position in x is further from the plasma core than the lighter ions. The opposite is happening
when the initial position is at the LCFS as in section 6.2 and 6.4. The heavier ions have a mean
position in x closer to the plasma core than the lighter ions except for W which is the ion with the
mean position furthest from the plasma core. The mean position is further out in the plasma but
the spread of W is big and some of the ions have disappeared from the simulation because of the
boundary conditions. This could mean that W ions have hit the x-boundary towards the plasma
core and disappeared henceforth moving the mean position in x further out in the plasma.
The ions spread σx has a tendency of being larger for the heavier ions for all the simulations. The
spread for the ions in dynamic fields is larger than for the ions in static fields. This can mean that
more ions can get closer to the plasma core and can take energy out of the plasma and disrupt
it. Even if the initial position of the ions is in the SOL they get spread out in the whole domain
which did not happen with static fields.
The average kinetic energy in section 6.2, 6.3, 6.4 and 6.5 showed that all ions, except H, gain
energy throughout the simulations. This means all the ions will take energy from the plasma.
The amount of energy the ions gained is depended on the mass of the ions. The heavier the ions
the more energy they gained on average. The ions with the initial position at the LCFS gain more
energy than the ions at the SOL and the ions with initial velocity ⃗v ̸= 0 gain more energy than
ions with initial energy ⃗v = 0. This is the same tendency as for the static fields. The difference
between the simulations with dynamic and static fields is that the average kinetic energy still has
a slope at the end of the simulations and that the average kinetic energy is larger for the ions in
the dynamic fields.

7
Conclusion
In this project, simulations of the full trajectory of charged impurities influenced by the Lorentz
force have been made. The electric and magnetic fields used are from a nHESEL simulation.
Numerical methods have been tested and then used to simulate the equation. The two numerical
methods tested are Forward Euler and Runge-Kutta 4, and from the tests, the conclusion was
that the numerical method Runge-Kutta 4 was the best.
The ions simulated are first ionized H, He, Li, Be, B, C, N, O, Ne, Na, Mg, and W particles. The
Lorentz force influencing the ions is dependent on the ratio of the charge and mass of the ions.
Often the drift equation is used instead of the entire trajectory, so the drift equation was compared
to the whole trajectory to see if it would be good enough. For simulations with static electric
and magnetic fields, the drift equation was good enough for the lighter ions, e.g., H and B but
not for heavier ions, e.g., W. For simulations with dynamic electric and magnetic fields, the drift
equations did not match the trajectory, not even for H, and would therefore be wrong to use.
First, simulations with static fields were made with different initial conditions. From these, we
can conclude that the lighter ions follow the electrostatic potential line better than the heavier
ions. The lighter ions have a mean position in x closer to the plasma core than the heavier ions,
but the heavier ions have a larger spread than the lighter ions. This results in a fraction of the
heavier ions being closer to the plasma core than the lighter ions.
For the simulations where the ions were placed in the SOL, the ions spread only out on the outer
side of the LCFS in the SOL. For the simulations where the ions were placed at the LCFS, the ions
spread out in the whole domain but had a lower concentration where the electrostatic potential
was highest.
Second, simulations with dynamic fields were made with different initial conditions. The spread
of the ions is larger for the heavier ions than the lighter ions. The spread of the ions in x is larger
with dynamic fields than for static fields.
The ions gained kinetic energy from all the simulations except for the H ion in some of the
simulations. The heavier ions gain more energy than the lighter ions. The ions with an initial
position at the LCFS gained more energy than the ions with the initial position in the SOL. The
ions in the dynamic fields gained more energy than the corresponding simulations with ions in
the static fields.
