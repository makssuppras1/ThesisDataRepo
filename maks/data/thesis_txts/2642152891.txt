Abstract
The development of phase transfer catalysts is a process that often relies on screening a wide
variety of compounds for catalytic activity which requires resources and most importantly
time. To potentially reduce the resources and time spent in such development, a theoretical
study of phase transfer catalysis was conducted, and the molecular design methodology
presented by Wengong Jin, Regina Barzilay, and Tommi Jaakkola was investigated with the
aim of evaluating its potential use in the ﬁeld of phase transfer catalysis. As the cornerstone
of their approach, the extraction of substructures indicative of a particular property from full
molecules, relies on predictors based on the use of random forest classiﬁers, it is believed that
these predictors can easily be modiﬁed to predict for properties such as thermal stability,
solubility, octanol-water partition coeﬃcient etc. The main challenge appears to be the fact
that no public database of phase transfer catalysts is currently available. A random forest
classiﬁer could still be trained on a small dataset of catalysts, but one would have to take
the resulting dependency into account. However, sites that bind the anions in phase transfer
catalysis are often very characteristic. A library of a wide variety of these substructures
could be constructed manually, and from these as well as other substructures, new catalysts
could be designed, though this approach means the proposed catalysts could not actually be
validated for catalytic activity. Furthermore, it is suggested that to increase the chance of
substructure properties being kept intact through exterior generation, implementation of an
attention mechanism in the variational autoencoder should be investigated. The Node and
Edge features in graph Neural Networks model is an interesting option in this regard, as it
has achieved state-of-the-art results on a number of benchmark datasets due to its dual-level
attention mechanism.

Introduction
Phase transfer catalysis (PTC) is the use of a catalyst to bring reactants of diﬀerent phases
into proximity, such that reaction can transpire. The use of PTC has gained widespread
attention today. Especially in industrial processes for pharmaceuticals and agrochemicals,
the ﬁeld of laboratory organic synthesis, monomer synthesis, and polymer modiﬁcation.
The number of commercial uses of PTC grew dramatically from 1975 to the early 1990s
from less than 10 to more than 600. Since then, interest has only grown. However, the
development of new catalysts is one that relies heavily on screening. When investigating
α-methylnaphthylamine derived catalysts, 40 compounds were screened, and when design-
ing the Denmark catalyst, more than 160 catalysts were tested[1][2]. Thus, there is great
potential for improvement in the resource and time expenditures involved in this process.
In machine learning, vast progress has been witnessed which is especially true for its use
in quantitative structure-activity relationships (QSAR). In their article Multi-Objective
Molecule Generation using Interpretable Substructures, Wengong Jin, Regina Barzilay, and
Tommi Jaakkola present a design methodology (RationaleRL) based on molecular substruc-
tures (rationales), that achieved state-of-the-art results on the task of designing dual in-
hibitors of the biological targets GSK3-β and JNK3 with additional constraints on the
quantitative estimation of drug-likeness (QED) and the synthetic accessibility score (SAS)[3].
In this thesis, with the aim of reducing the resource and time expenditures of phase transfer
catalyst development, the potential of using the RationaleRL method is investigated, as it
is believed that this could be beneﬁcial to the aforementioned industrial ﬁelds. To this end,
a litterature study of PTC and the use of machine learning in related ﬁelds is conducted in
chapter 2 and 3. It should be noted that only liquid-liquid PTC (LL-PTC) is considered
in this regard. Furthermore, to illuminate the fundamentals required of this investigation,
study of the machine learning basics is also performed, before RationaleRL can be examined.

Phase transfer catalysis
For reaction to occur between molecules, ions, etc. one of the primary conditions involved,
is that they are able to get into proximity of each other. If no interaction can be established,
then nor will any reaction be able to transpire. With reactants that are not readily brought
in contact with one another, this poses a natural limitation on the transpiring of the reaction,
as is the case with polar and non-polar reactants that will favor diﬀerent solvents. It was not
until 1951, when it was reported by Jarousse that eﬃcient alkylation of phenylacetonitrile
with ethyl chloride could be achieved in the presence of 50% NaOH and approximately
20% benzyltriethylammonium chloride that other options than using solvents in which both
reactants were at least semi soluble were considered[4]. Good results were achieved with
dipolar aprotic solvents such as dimethylformamide (DMF) and dimethylsulfoxide (DMSO).
However, the diﬃculty of solvent recovery post reaction along with the cost meant other
means were desired. This desire led to the foundations of phase transfer catalysis being laid
in the 1960s and 1970s, two front ﬁgures in this development being Stark and Brandstrom,
based on the discovery of Jarousse as a cheap and environmentally friendly alternative to
the solvents such as DMF and DMSO. Cheaper due to an increased ﬂexibility when it
comes to the choice of solvents and a diminishing of the post processing required. Increased
environmental friendliness due to the same reasons, as well as a more eﬀective recovery of
process valuables for the small cost of only catalytic amounts of PTC catalyst[5].

Chapter 2. Phase transfer catalysis
2.1
LL-PTC principles
As stated in the previous section, PTC is used to bring reactants located in diﬀerent phases
together. In LL-PTC, the two phases are usually an aqueous phase acting as a reservoir
for reactant anions, and an organic phase that acts as a source for catalyst and organic
reactants[6]. The PT catalyst ensures contact between reactants of the two phases, and
is then regenerated upon the ensuing organic phase reaction (the intrinsic reaction). The
method of transfer is dependent on the system in question, but generally anion exchange is
believed to happen in the aqueous phase. The anion is then transferred to the organic one in
an active state, where reaction can transpire (Stark transfer method)[5]. Without activation
of the anion, no reaction will occur.
2.2
Mechanism breakdown
When attempting to grasp the concepts and gain insights into the kinetics of LL-PTC,
breaking it down in two major steps is beneﬁcial. The ﬁrst being the intrinsic reaction,
and the second being the reactant anion transfer. As an illustrative example, the case of
quarternary ammonium chloride salt catalyzed cyanide displacement on 1-chlorooctane is
used (see ﬁgure 2.1)[7].
Figure 2.1: Cyanide displacement on 1-chlorooctane catalyzed by a quarternary ammonium
chloride salt[7]
In this case, the chloride displacement by cyanide yielding 1-cyanooctane is the intrinsic
reaction (organic phase reactions of other systems are not necessarily this simple).
For
deeper understanding of the transfer step, it can be broken into three additional steps:
• Catalyst transport from organic phase to aqueous phase

• Ion exchange in aqueous phase
• Transfer of reactant anion to organic phase by catalyst cation
For now, only understanding of what the transfer step is, is necessary. The subset of steps
of the anion transfer will be considered in more depth shortly along with factors aﬀecting
each of them. The system’s maximum total rate is directly dependent on the rate of both
of the major steps, as they will necessarily be of equal size when a steady state is reached.
Thus, to be able to maximize the total rate of the system, one must know which of the steps
is the rate limiting one. The principle is illustrated in ﬁgure 2.2[7].
Figure 2.2: Rate matrix of a PTC system[7]
When both steps of the PTC cycle are slow, the total rate will naturally be slow, and vice
versa when both steps are fast. If the intrinsic reaction rate is fast but the anion transfer
rate slow, the system will be in the transfer rate limited region and the transfer rate should
be optimized. When the anion transfer rate is fast and the intrinsic reaction rate slow, the
system will be in the organic phase rate limited region, and the intrinsic rate should be
optimized for better performance. There are various factors that can aﬀect the PTC cycle.
Not all steps are aﬀected equally by each factor. This makes it possible to achieve the desired
performance of the system to an extent. The factors and their eﬀects are summarized in
table 2.1[7].

2.3
Factors aﬀecting the mechanism
As mentioned earlier, to aﬀect the PTC cycle there are various ways including the choice of
catalyst, choice of solvent, and choice of anions. With a focus on the simple cases capable of
illuminating the needed theoretical concepts, the most important methods will be examined,
meaning an exhaustive explanation is not the purpose of this section.
2.3.1
Agitation
Using the reaction matrix, it was shown that in the total rate of reaction is dependent on not
only the intrinsic reaction rate but also the rate of the anion transfer step across the phase
boundary. When a system is in the transfer rate limited region, an easy ﬁx is to agitate
the mixture by stirring. This increases the area of the phase boundary, facilitating the total
cross boundary movement. The transfer rate can be increased by increase of the stirring rate
until the rate of transfer substantially exceeds the organic phase reaction rate. When this
point is reached the transfer will no longer increase with stirring rate. Typically, stirring
rates of approximately 200-300 rpm are used. With rates of this size adequate phase contact
is achieved[7][6][5].
2.3.2
Cation
When choosing a catalyst for PTC, there are several catalyst classes to investigate. The most
widely utilized ones are the quarternary ammonium and phosphonium salts (also called
quarternary onium salts).
While similar, there are diﬀerent beneﬁts of the two classes.
Quarternary ammonium salts are moderately stable to basic conditions and temperatures
up to 100° celsius. The phosphonium salts are thermodynamically more stable but come at
a greater cost. Another drawback is that they are less stable under basic conditions[7][5].
2.3.3
Anion
For the transport of reactant anions to the phase of reaction, there are a number of important
considerations to take into account. These include hydration sphere, electronegativity, and
charge-to-volume ratio. Anions with high charge densities can pose problems for transport
due to their high levels of hydration. The trend of relative extractability for similar ions

2.3.4
Ion pair
As just mentioned, the hydration sphere is a great factor in the level of activation. An addi-
tional factor is that of the bond length. The longer the bond, the weaker the ionic interaction,
meaning the level of activation is high. The cases of sodium bromide and tetrabutylammo-
nium bromide are considered. In the ﬁrst case, the ionic distance is 2.85Å, while it is 6.28Å
for the second. The diﬀerence in bond length is approximately 3.4Å, which in praxis results
in a increased reaction rate of up to 4 orders of magnitude[7]. The diﬀerence in ionic distance
is mainly caused by the bulkyness of the catalyst cations, which can cause steric limitations.
Thus, the catalyst structure does not only aﬀect the organophilicity/hydrophilicity, but also
to a great extent the activation of the ion pair[6].
2.3.5
Solvent
When utilizing the principles of LL-PTC, an organic solvent is not always a necessity. The
neat organic reactant can sometimes act as the organic phase, which can lead to a variety of
beneﬁts including increased yield, purity of product, and reaction rate, while at the same time
avoiding solvent related environment and recovery problems. However, sometimes the solvent
is still needed to achieve a greater rate of anion transfer, intrinsic reaction, or dissolving
an otherwise nonreactive solid reactant[7]. Choosing a solvent with greater polarity than
otherwise can also aﬀect the transfer equilibrium by increasing the ease of transfer for the
reactant ion pair. In cases with small high-charge to volume ratio, the eﬀect will be greatest
compared to low-charge density ions or ions of organic structure[7].
When the reactant of the organic phase is used as the neat organic phase, the polarity can
change signiﬁcantly during the course of the reaction as more and more reactant is converted
to product. This can aﬀect the intrinsic reaction rate to a potentially great extent, though

2.3.6
Aqueous phase
Through the aqueous phase it is possible to manipulate the equilibrium of transfer. By
adding more of the inorganic salt to the solution, the law of mass action states that the system
should be driven towards transfer of the catalyst-anion pair. As previously explained, this
will have the added beneﬁt of dehydrating the anions resulting in a higher level of activation.
This is exempliﬁed by the n-octyl methanesulfonate substitution reaction with bromide in a
chlorobenzene-water system, where catalytic activity of Bu4N +Br−increases up to 20 times
when going from 1 M to 6 M of the inorganic salt KBr[6].
2.3.7
Temperature
The organic phase reactions are often times to some degree dependent on the system tem-
perature. Thus, when a system is in the intrinsic reaction rate limited region, a temperature
change will tend to be the ﬁrst attempt to reach more favorable conditions. However, this
change can have unintended consequences, making it important to consider all aspects of

Phase transfer catalysis and its current
landscape
Since the foundation of PTC was laid approximately 60 years ago, more and more attention
has been given to the ﬁeld. Today, many classes exist, all with diﬀerent advantages and
disadvantages, and even while these widely applicable and modiﬁable classes exist, research
is continuously being conducted to discover new useful ones, and optimize the existing ones.
In this chapter, the landscape of phase transfer catalysis will be laid out as it is today with
the aim of understanding how the development might potentially be improved upon.
3.1
PT catalyst classes and their diﬀerences
Among the most widely used PT catalyst classes are the quarternary onium salts, crown
ethers, cryptands, and polyethylene glycols (PEGs). There are advantages and disadvantages
to all of them, but a common factor is the modiﬁcation potential of all of them, which is
what for most cases makes them the ﬁrst catalysts considered.
3.1.1
Quarternary onium ions
In ﬁgure 3.1, the quarternary onium ions are illustrated. These are the most widely used cata-
lysts. Tetrabutylammonium, benzyltriethylammonium, and hexadecyltributylphosphonium
in particular have found much use[7] in the industry. The alkyl groups of the ammonium
and phosphonium catalysts can freely be chosen according to the desired properties, espe-
cially w.r.t. organophilicity that will naturally grow with growing alkyl group size, but also
enantioselectivity can be induced into catalyst as with the so called Maruoka catalysts[8] or
certain cinchona alkoloid derived tetraalkylammonium ions[9].

conﬁgurations and the ligand topology. Open chain PEGs exhibit the same kind of behavior,
but the stability of the formed complexes is lacking compared to the other two classes[6].

Choice of catalyst
The diﬀerent classes naturally have diﬀerent areas of application, though there is still overlap
of these areas. To navigate through the most important considerations, the main points of

In neutral environments, and also to
some degree basic ones, the quarternary ammonium ions are viable when the temperature
is below 100°C. The phosphonium catalysts exhibit greater thermal stability and work in
temperatures up to approximately 150°C above which dequarternization will happen by the
reverse Menshutkin reaction, but the stability towards basic conditions is less than that of
their ammonium counterpart. The PEGs, crown ethers, and cryptands can be used at even

and crown ethers are both toxic and expensive, making PEGs the go to for basic conditions,
high temperature conditions, or a mix thereof. The main disadvantage of PEGs is that
they oﬀer little anion activation.
In fact, the activation PEGs oﬀer is the lowest of all
the considered classes. Thus, comparatively more catalyst will have to be used when using
PEGs. Cryptands and crown ethers on the other hand, make highly activated anions[5].

Prospect of using computational chem-
istry
To reduce the resource expenditures, computational chemistry seems a promising path for-
ward. By making use of this tool, the process of testing and screening can hopefully be
heavily reduced by determining the optimal catalyst in silico. Computational chemistry is
already a growing part of the phase transfer ﬁeld, and direct catalyst design could potentially
be the next step pushing the development. So far, as an example, computational chemistry
has been used in the case of the enantioselective alkylation by Maruoka and cinchona alkoloid
catalysts. By using semiempirical quantum mechanical (QM) calculations, it was possible to
accurately describe the non-covalent interactions and determine the most stable intermediate
conformation, resulting in a previously unknown and more stable structure[8]. Additionally,
the ONIOM method of electronic structure theory, which is a combination of the MP2 and
MP4 methods (see reference [10] for explanation), has been used to evaluate the solvation
potential and correctly predict the catalytic activity of a newly designed compound[11].
In the related ﬁelds of drug and non-PTC catalyst design, machine learning has proven
to be a powerful tool, and is therefore an interesting option in the furtherment of phase
transfer catalysis. Particularly the quantitative structure-activity relationships have been
investigated in drug design, where they have been used to create virtual representations of
molecules and map them to desired activities like the viability of use in a human body,
and thus creating an eﬃcient screening method[12]. Likewise, in non-PTC catalyst design
there has been great progress. In applications such as fuel cells, biomass conversion, and
natural gas conversion, alloy nanoparticles are greatly utilized as catalysts. However, it can
be a large challenge to determine the active sites and optimal composition due to the high
tunability. It is common practice to only model a few stable facets, as the required quantum
mechanical calculations are a computational bottleneck, but it has been shown that by inte-
grating the QM calculations with ML, it is possible to overcome this bottleneck, and enable
accurate virtual screening of large catalyst spaces[13].

The hope is that these eﬀorts can now be brought into the ﬁeld of PTC. One of the major
challenges is that no databases exists speciﬁcally for LL-PTC, as they do for drug design,
and for an ML algorithm to be able to learn enough to create meaningful relationships
between molecules and activities, there must be many examples to learn from. Preferably
in the order of thousands. It is possible that pre-existing databases can be used (maybe
mixing databases of diﬀerent types to introduce more diversity), despite not containing
any known PT catalysts, by using the methodology of Wengong Jin, Regina Barzilay, and
Tommi Jaakkola in their article Multi-Objective Molecule Generation using Interpretable
Substructures. Their methodology named RationaleRL, is based on the use of substructures
called rationales.
A rationale is a substructure of a molecule that is determined to be
indicative of a property of the molecule. In their work, the properties considered were activity
as an inhibitor of the biological targets GSK3-β and JNK3, the quantitative estimation of
drug-likeness, and the synthetic accessibility score (SAS). Rationales for these properties are
extracted from the ChEMBL database, which is a database of bioreactive molecules with
drug-like properties[14], by iteratively removing exterior atoms and determining whether
or not the substructures are still active using a property predictor. Assuming there is a
suﬃcient overlap in structure, rationales of diﬀerent properties can then be superimposed
on one another to create a multi-property rationale. The multi-property rationales then
only need an exterior estimated, which is handled by an ML model trained as a variational
autoencoder on the molecules from which the rationales were extracted. RationaleRL was
evaluated against the molecular generation methods JT-VAE, GCPN, GVAE-RL, and the
state-of-the-art REINVENT method, and managed to outperform these in terms of accuracy,
novelty, and diversity on the multi-property molecular design task[3]. The reason of this
appearing to be substructure based generation as opposed to the atom-by-atom generative
process of the other tested methods. Therefore, the RationaleRL method was chosen for
further investigation of potential implementation in PT catalyst development.

The loss of a model is plotted against its weight, w1, yielding a convex curve. When at the
starting point, it is not necessarily immediately obvious how to update weights to reach the
point of minimum loss, but by computing the negative gradient, the direction in which the
loss decreases the fastest is obtained. The gradient is used as a guide, and the weight is
updated. The size of the step taken in the determined direction is of importance to how
eﬀectively the model is trained and whether or not it will even converge. The step size is
determined by the length of the gradient vector and the learning rate. The learning rate is
an adjustable parameter that is multiplied onto the length of the gradient, meaning the step

to compromise between eﬃcient computing and correct gradient direction[20].

5.4.1
Balancing under- and overﬁtting
A common method to make sure there is a proper balance between under- and overﬁtting, is
to divide the data into a training and a test set. The split will usually be approximately 90%
for training and 10% for testing. When drawing these separate sets of data, an important
criteria is that examples must be drawn independently and identically to ensure the best
conditions for the model to learn the true distribution of the data. Evaluation of the model
is assessed by ﬁrstly looking at the value of the test loss. Is the loss acceptable or is it
too great? Secondly, it is assessed by comparing the loss of both data sets. If they are
comparable, the model has most likely approached the true distribution of the data. If not,
the balance of ﬁtting can be assumed to be oﬀ. Often the data is also split into a third
part called the validation set. This set is used while testing diﬀerent conﬁgurations, and the
test set is then the ﬁnal test. The split in this case will tend to be approximately 80% for
training and 10% for the remaining sets[22][23][24]. A good indicator of whether the model
is under- or overﬁtted is the plot of loss vs the number of iterations as shown in ﬁgure 5.4.
As the training progresses, the loss based on the training data as well as the validation data
drops due to the improvement of the model. At a certain point, the loss of the validation
data starts to increase. This is because the model is now starting to ﬁt to the peculiarities
of the training data, which limits the model’s extrapolability. A common way to avoid this
problem is to identify when the model starts to overﬁt, and simply stop further training at
this point[25].

6.1.1
SMILES
The simpliﬁed molecular-input line-entry system (SMILES) is used for molecular represen-
tation. It is relatively compact and can be read as well as edited by computers and humans
31
==PAGE:38==
Chapter 6. Rationale based molecular design
alike making it easy to work with[30]. The line notation of SMILES was developed by David
Weininger, Gilman Veith, and Eric Anderson in 1987[31]. SMILES are not without draw-
backs. They are based on valence bond theory (VBT) which is widely applicable, but certain
types of chemical species such as purely ionic or metallic compounds are not well described
using this theory[30]. However, as mainly organic compounds are considered with regards to
phase transfer catalysts, this limitation is acceptable. The aspects of SMILES and the logic
behind them are superﬁcially explained in the following. With each new aspect presented,
see ﬁgure for a visualization.
SMILES strings consist of letters where atoms are represented by their standard abbreviation,
although any hydrogen atoms are implicit. The bonds between atoms are speciﬁed by using:
• ’.’ : No bond. Can be used to specify the dissociation of sodium chloride.
• ’-’ : Single bond. A single bond is assumed if nothing is speciﬁed.
• ’=’ : Double bond.
• ’#’ : Triple bond.
• ’$’ : Quadruple bond.
• ’:’ : An aromatic or "one and a half" bond.
• ’/’ and ’\’ : Single bonds next to a double bond can be represented by slashes to
specify the stereochemical conﬁguration.
Furthermore, to specify stereochemistry at tetrahedral carbon atoms, ’@’ and ’@@’ can be
used.
When looking at the central carbon atom from the perspective of the ﬁrst bond,
’@’ will indicate that the remaining bonds are counter-clockwise and ’@@’ that they are
clockwise.
To specify the closure of ring structures, numeric labels are used to indicate the connectivity
of atoms that are not adjacent in the SMILES notation. This is also used with aromatic
rings. As already stated, an aromatic bond can be speciﬁed by a colon. However, this is not
the only way to represent aromaticity using the SMILES notation. The Kekulé form in which
single bonds and double bonds are used alternatingly is one option, but more commonly,
lower-case letters are the choice for aromaticity representation. Branching is a concept that
cannot be described by the above. Parentheses are used to enclose separate branches, at
speciﬁc locations in the SMILES string.

Graph theory
The concept of graphs originates from Leonhard Euler back in 1736, when he was faced with
the problem of the seven bridges of Königsberg. As in ﬁgure 6.2, there is an island, A, and
seven bridges, a, b, c, d, e, f, and g. The problem is whether or not it is possible to walk in
such a way that you cross each bridge, but no bridge more than once.
Figure 6.2: The problem of the seven bridges of Königsberg[33].
Euler proved that that in fact was impossible, but the essential part in this was not the actual
proof, but instead the way of thinking that he introduced. Euler stripped the problem of
every piece of information that was not vital, thus reducing it to the illustration in ﬁgure
6.3 which gave rise to the theory of graphs[33].
Figure 6.3: The problem of the seven bridges of Königsberg reduced.[33].
In this theory, the points marked by capital letters are called vertices or nodes, and the lines
marked by small letters are called edges. There are diﬀerent types of graphs. The most

otherwise speciﬁed, graph usually refers to the undirected kind. The diﬀerence in the two
types is that in a simple graph, such as the one illustrating the seven bridges, one can walk
over the bridge in both directions. If only one direction was allowed, the graph would be
directed. A graph is usually represented by G = (V, E), where V is the set of nodes, and E
is the set of edges[34].

6.2.1
Random forest classiﬁer
For classiﬁcation problems, the random forest classiﬁer (RFC) is a popular choice.
The
RFC is a method based on decision trees and the idea of bootstrap aggregation also called
bagging. This combination is to overcome the problem with the risk of decision trees over
ﬁtting to data in some cases.
Decision trees
A decision tree is an algorithmic approach to classifying an event or predicting output values
of a variable.
In essence, the approach is about splitting data according which feature
creates the greatest diﬀerence between the resulting groups, and the greatest similarity of
observations within each of those subgroups[35]. Figure 6.5 illustrates this approach with a
simple example. The data set is the list of 1s and 0s in the top, and features of the data set
are red vs blue and underlined vs not underlined. It is desired to split the 1s from the 0s,
and the most eﬀective feature for the ﬁrst split is red vs blue. After the split there are only
1s in one group though it still contains a 0. The next split is based on underlined vs not
underlined and the objective is then accomplished.

Randomness
As mentioned, decision trees are at risk of over ﬁtting to data.
To avoid this issue, an
ensemble of decision trees is used (a forest), and the ﬁnal decision is made based on the
decisions of all trees.
This is the aggregation aspect of bagging.
However, to gain the
full potential of the forest’s predictive capabilities, the trees must be uncorrelated to each
other. This means some trees may make incorrect classiﬁcations, but their mistakes are
outweighed by the correct classiﬁcations from other trees resulting in a ﬁnal and correct
classiﬁcation[35]. That trees are uncorrelated is partly ensured by bootstrapping the data.
This means to randomly extract data examples from the dataset and replacing them (so
they can be extracted more than once), thus producing multiple training sets. If a data set
is [1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6] a bootstrapped training set could be [1, 2, 4, 4, 5, 5] and another could be
[1, 1, 2, 3, 3, 4][37].
Another method of ensuring diverse trees, is to introduce feature randomness. In a decision
tree, the resulting splits of all features are considered before choosing the most eﬀective one.
However, trees in a random forest only have a random subset of the features available.

6.3.1
Monte Carlo tree search
MCTS enables selectively expanding a tree like graph structure as is illustrated in ﬁgure
6.10. With such structures, the top node is usually termed the root node, and two nodes
directly above and below each other are parent and child node. The bottom most nodes
are leaf nodes, and all the paths from root to leaf are considered branches. This method
has many uses such as in optimization problems, games where it can determine the best
actions to take, and this case where the root node is a molecular structure that is to be
reduced to informative substructures. Building selectively means to impose a balance on the
expansion (instead of exhaustively searching through the entire potential tree), such that
there is a tunable tradeoﬀbetween exploitation and exploration. Exploitation meaning to
further search in areas that appear to yield promising results, and exploration meaning to
test areas that have only received limited attention if any at all. The balance can be imposed
by seeking to maximize an equation such as the upper conﬁdence bound (UCB) equation by
choice of child node:
UCB = ¯Xj + 2Cp

Here n is the number of times the parent node has been visited and nj the number of times
the child node has been visited. Cp is a tunable constant and ¯Xj is the reward average for the
action of child node j). How to deﬁne reward is dependent on the problem, but the result is
based on simulation. The left term corresponds to the exploitation term, and the right term
corresponds to the exploration term. It is worth noting that due the denominator nj of the
square root, the second term will be inﬁnite if a node a has never been visited before. Thus,
all possible child nodes must have been visited at least once, before further exploitation can
be carried out[40].
The MCTS algorithm is performed iteratively until a chosen constraint is reached. Typically
this will be a time, memory, or iteration constraint. There are diﬀerent MCTS algorithms,
but the general case has four steps[41]:
1. Selection: Starting from the root node, UCB is applied to descend through the tree
until the ﬁrst expandable node is found (a node is expandable if it is non-terminal and
has unvisited child node).
2. Expansion: One or more child nodes are created.
3. Simulation: A simulation is performed from the new node/nodes.

In the MPNEncoder, the base unit of the ML architecture is chosen according to the provided

can be represented. However, a common problem of basic RNN’s is that they suﬀer from
short term memory. This means they struggle with long sequences, as the information from
earlier steps gets conﬂated. The RNN types available in the script are LSTM (Long short-
term memory) and GRU (Gated recurrent units), which both manage to deal with the issue
of short term memory (only LSTM will be presented here, as this is the default).
The
structure of the LSTM cell is illustrated in ﬁgure 6.20.
The main improvement over basic RNN’s is the addition of a cell state, which acts as a
sort of information highway. The ﬁrst step in the LSTM unit is passing the input concate-
nated with the output of the previous cell (the hidden state) through a sigmoid activated
neural layer. This allows the model to learn which pieces of information to be "forgot" or

"remembered" by compressing data to values between 0 and 1. 0 meaning to forget and 1
to remember. To update the cell state, ﬁrst the input and previous hidden state is passed
through a sigmoid activation function again. In a parallel operation, tanh is also applied.
Tanh allows the model to learn from the input and hidden state, and the sigmoid learns the
relative importance of each element. The results are then added to the cell state. Lastly,
the hidden state of the current cell is determined. Once again, the sigmoid function is used
as activation function. The result is multiplied with tanh of the cell state. Thus, both the
cell state and hidden state can be passed on in the model[42].

Fine-tuning
Whereas gnn_train aims to train the model in completing rationales to realistic molecules
(based on training using the ChEMBL database), ﬁnetune aims to further train the model
in generating molecules that satisfy all property constraints. This is a three-step training
process. First the script takes multi-property rationales and generates complete molecules
by decoding based on the current model, then the molecules are passed through a property
ﬁlter which removes molecules that don’t fulﬁll all the constraints, and lastly the molecules
are used to further train the model using the same methodology as for gnn_train.
The script, ﬁnetune, has many inputs that allow for customization of the ﬁne-tuning process.
These can be viewed in appendix B.6 line 62-86.
Required are the properties on which
molecules are to be evaluated, the directory of multi-property rationales, and a save directory.
Using the ﬁrst two inputs, scoring functions are initialized by the previously mentioned
function get_scoring_function, and rationales to be decoded are loaded. The rationales are
passed to the class SubgraphDataset (appendix B.8.8 line 54). This class takes the root
atoms, which are marked by atom map numbers, and generates X diﬀerent combinations of
these atom map numbers speciﬁed the the input num_decode. If X diﬀerent combinations
are not possible, some combinations will be sampled more than once. Furthermore, the
directory of the desired model for decoding should be speciﬁed.

Adaptation to the ﬁeld of PTC
With the aim of bringing the ﬁeld of molecular generation to PTC, the rationale based
molecular design methodology of the previous chapter was chosen for three reasons:
1. Great insights in the underlying design reasoning
2. Ability to design molecules based on multiple properties
3. The generalizability of the methodology
The concept at the core, the rationales, is the explaining factor of reasons 1, 2 and partly
of reason 3 as well. From larger molecules, these substructures, that are predicted to be
indicative of the molecules properties of interest, are extracted, and using these as building
blocks, new molecules are generated. Thus, the display of a certain property of a generated
molecule can be attributed to a certain recognizable substructure from which it is easy to
derive meaning. The nature of this structure-activity relationship also means that molecules
active in multiple properties are easily designed and understood by taking rationales of dif-
ferent properties, investigating them for structural similarities, and superimposing them on
one another if possible.
The reasoning of the generalizability of the of the methodology is two-part. Firsty, the con-
cept of structure-activity relationships with regards to rationales is widely applicable, and
it is witnessed throughout chemistry, be it with solubility, acidity, or stability of chemical
species such as carbocations. Secondly, the use and implementation of the random forest
classiﬁer makes extending the code to include prediction of new properties a relatively simple
matter. The main concern in this undertaking is the availability of data for the RFC to learn
from. When designing GSK3-β and JNK3 inhibitors, approximately 2 million bioreactive
molecules with drug-like properties were available through the ChEMBL database[14], and
substructures indicative of inhibitor activity could then be learned eﬃciently by training on
a data set with examples classiﬁed as either being active as inhibitors (symbolized by a 1)
or being inactive as inhibitors (symbolized by a 0). Currently, no public database dedicated

that it is decided to train the variational autoencoder on a dataset containing a subset of PT
catalysts, as an increased learning ability would be useful considering the limited PT data,
and the attention mechanism has proven highly successful in sequence to sequence modeling
tasks. This success is largely due to the fundamental principle of attention, which is allowing
to attend to speciﬁc parts of an input deemed particularly relevant[46]. The mechanism was
ﬁrst employed as an addition to recurrent neural networks but has since been investigated
for its stand-alone potential given the results of the Transformer presented in the 2017 article
Attention is All you Need by Vaswani et al.[47]. The transformer is solely based on atten-
tion mechanisms and achieved 28.4 BLEU (BiLingual Evaluation Understudy - an evaluation
metric for the machine translation task) on the WMT 2014 English-to-German translation
task. This was the highest scoring result recorded. On the 2014 WMT English-French,
the Transformer achieved a BLEU score of 41.0, thus again outperforming state of the art
models and doing so only with a fraction of the training time due to the parallizability of
the attention mechanism.
The Transformer architecture is similar to that presented in the previous chapter, as it also
follows the encoder-decoder structure. First, an embedding is created for each word, and
due to the non-sequential computation, a positional encoding is added to account for ef-
fects of sentence structure. It is on the resulting vectors from these steps that the attention
mechanism is applied to determine the importance of vectors relative to each other. The
same principle is used the work presented by Veličković et al. in the article Graph Atten-
tion Networks[48]. Here nodes represented by feature vectors are encoded instead of words,
and the attention mechanism is applied to the local neighborhood of each of those nodes.
Thus, the graph attention networks (GATs) enable implicitly assigning diﬀerent weights to
diﬀerent nodes of a neighborhood without knowing the structure beforehand or requiring
costly matrix operations. This method proved eﬀective too, as it achieved state of the art
results on the benchmark datasets Cora, Citeseer, and Pubmed, as well as a protein-protein
interaction dataset.
While the GATs manage to successfully apply the attention mechanism on graph related
problems, in relation to the molecular generation approach of this study there is one inherent
obstacle which is the node based nature of the GAT. The ability to predict bond types
between a substructure and a new atom relies on learning from the edge feature vectors
in which those bond types are speciﬁed. Thus, GATs are not immediately implementable.
However, the recently proposed NENN model (Node and Edge features in graph Neural

Networks) is a model that alternately stacks node-level attention layers aiming to learn
the importance of node and edge based neighbors of each node and edge-level attention
layers aiming to learn the importance of node and edge based neighbors of each edge. This
causes a mutual reinforcement of learned embeddings, and as a result the model has shown
to generally outperform GAT and other state-of-the-art methods on the Cora, Citeseer,
and Pubmed datasets[49]. This presents an interesting option of implementing attention
to enhance the generative capabilities of the variational autoencoder of the rationale based
design approach while maintaining the ability to specify bond types.

Conclusion
Due to its ability to generate molecules with multiple properties, the molecular design
methodology presented in the article Multi-Objective Molecule Generation using Interpretable
Substructures has been investigated with the purpose of evaluating its potential of use in de-
signing phase transfer catalysts. As the substructure/rationale extraction from full molecules
is facilitated by a random forest classiﬁer, the act of altering the property on which sub-
structures are judged is a relatively easy task. The only substantial change to the predictor
classes needed is the RFC used which naturally will require training a new classiﬁer. With
regards to PT catalysts, training RFCs to recognize their characteristic substructures, such
as quarternary ammonium ions, cryptands, and crown ethers, poses a challenge as no pub-
lic databases of phase transfer catalysts currently exist. However, an RFC could still be
trained on a dataset containing only a few hundred training examples, but the dependency
should then be taken into account. A potential way of circumventing the issue, is to use
those known characteristic catalyst substructures as rationales, the same way rationales of
any other property would be used.
However, without the classiﬁer, property ﬁltering is
not possible with regards to activity as a PT catalyst, though it is still available for any
other property of interest for catalyst design, such as solubility, thermal stability, and the
octanol-water partition coeﬃcient.
Furthermore, to enhance the ability of the variational autoencoder to generate full molecules
from rationales while preserving their activity, replacing the use of RNNs with attention
based mechanisms could be advantegous, as these have proven highly successful in recent
works.
To this end, the NENN model shows great promise, as it employs a dual level
attention mechanism that has achieved state-of-the-art results on multiple datasets.
