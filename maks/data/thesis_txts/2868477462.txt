Abstract
The increasing demand for sustainable alternatives to fossil-based products has brought
attention to the need for efficient microbial cell factories, capable of vaolrizing lignocellu-
losic hydrolysate (LCH). A fraction of LCH, known as Hemicellulosic hydrolysate (HCH),
present a promising renewable feedstock for bioconversion, into a broad range of value
added compounds.
However, microbial growth and valorization of HCH, is hindered by toxic/inhibitory com-
pounds, and substrate utilization of carbon sources, present in HCH. This study aims
to develop Escherichia coli strains with enhanced utilization of, and tolerance to HCH
through adaptive laboratory evolution (ALE). Engineered strains with different primary
carbon source preferences, namely glucose, xylose, and arabinose, plus a strain ca-
pable of co-utilization of glucose and xylose, were subjected to ALE to improve growth
and metabolic performance in HCH. All four strains were constructed to be acetate aux-
otrophic. The evolved strains were analyzed for growth rate and substrate utilization, of
HCH.
Several variants from each strain were selected for further analysis, as they exhibited
superior growth in HCH. During this analysis an error occurred, and growth was only
observed in the CRP* strains. This led to the exclusion of the three remaining strains,
and four variants from CRP*, were selected for production experiments.
The four selected CRP* variants demonstrated significant production of isobutyric acid,
validating their potential as efficient production hosts. Their ability to valorize HCH, thereby
supports a more sustainable bioprocess for isobutyric acid production.
Future work concerning this project will include getting the three remaining strains to
work, and run them through the same selection and verification, as done with the CRP*
strain. Furthermore, DNA extraction and sequencing could be done, to determine poten-
tial causal mutations, to improve future cell factory organisms.

Introduction
The common practice for producing fermentation-based products is currently to use first-
generation biomass, i.e. sugars from edible food sources. However, when using these
sugars to produce bulk chemical replacements, long distance transportation fuels, or bio-
ethanol, the cost of feedstocks can be affected, resulting in price fluctuations that can
impact food markets. Furthermore, to keep up with demand for these high sugar con-
tent crops, such as sugarcane, deforestation of threatened and fragile ecosystems has
increased, along with other environmental issues stemming from sugarcane farming. It is
therefore important to reduce the dependency on first-generation biomass, and look for
more sustainable substrates, such as lignocellulosic biomass.
Lignocellulosic biomass is a waste product of many industries, and is currently primarily
being burned or used as feedstock for livestock. However, if we can engineer a bacte-
rial strain to efficiently valorize lignocellulosic biomass, we can minimize the use of first-
generation biomass, and work our way towards a more sustainable future.
Prior to this project engineered strains of Escherichia coli was subjected to adaptive labo-
ratory evolution (ALE), in order to improve its tolerance to toxic and inhibitory compounds
found in hemicellulosic hydrolysate.
1.1
Project aim
In this project, the fitness of individual ALE strains will be analyzed, after which their
capability of still being able to produce a product of interest will be verified.
The overall objective is to contribute to the development of an efficient E. coli based
cell-factory tolerant to hemicellulosic hydrolysates, thereby facilitating a more sustainable
production of bulk chemical replacements, reducing environmental impact and improving
resource utilization.
1.2
Future work
The most promising strains will be re-sequenced in order to identify mutations of interest.
Potential causal mutations, most likely to influence fitness during growth on hemicellulosic
hydrolysate, will be reintroduced into the starting strains through reverse engineering.

Background
2.1
Lignocellulosic Biomass
Lignocellulosic biomass (LCB) is classified as a second-generation biomass, meaning a
non-food biomass obtained as a byproduct from various industries [1]. LCB is considered
an attractive feedstock due to its high abundance, low cost, and widespread availability [2],
making it a promising alternative to conventional feedstocks. While a significant amount
of commercially viable products derived from LCB today are biofuels, the material has the
potential to support a diverse portfolio of bioproducts. Given the substantial quantities of
LCB waste generated by sectors such as agriculture, there is high potential to sustainably
produce biomass-derived materials at reduced costs [3].
These biomass-derived materials have also been suggested to be one of the most promis-
ing alternatives to fossil fuels, in regards to minimizing our carbon footprint, as they are
generated from atmospheric CO2, water, and sunlight [4]. Thereby, replacing chemical
synthesis, from oil derived compounds.
2.1.1
Composition
The main components of LCB are cellulose, hemicellulose, and lignin. Lignin is resistant
to biodegradation of most microorganisms, and its role in plants is to protect the carbo-
hydrates from enzymatic and microbial attack. Cellulose and hemicellulose on the other
hand, can be hydrolyzed into monomeric sugars like glucose, xylose, and arabinose [5].

ponent constituting 35-40%. B) Yellow part - is the hemicellulose component constituting

these numbers varies a lot between different sources). Figure has been slightly modified

Cellulose
Cellulose (Figure 2.1 A) is the most abundant and the main load bearing component
of LCB. It is also thought to be the most abundant biopolymer on earth, with several
billion tons grown annually [7], further showcasing the impact of conversion into biofuels
and value added compounds. Cellulose microfibrils are crystalline structures composed
of around 24 hydrogen-bonded chains, of β(1,4)-linked glucose molecules. The chains

are parallel, and consecutive glucose molecules are rotated 180◦, forming the repeating
disaccharide cellobiose.
The depolymerization of cellulose into cellobiose and glucose, is carried out by a handful
of cellulases. However, these enzymes only act on one face of the cellulose at a time,
meaning only the outermost chains are depolymerized, before underlying chains are ex-
posed to the cellulases. This causes the process to be relatively slow, compared to similar
quantities of the α linked starch, where a faster mode of action is employed [8].
Hemicellulose
Hemicellulose (Figure 2.1 B) is the second most abundant component in LCB. Most hemi-
cellulose have a backbone consisting of chains of glucans, xylans, galactans, mannans,
or glucomannans, bound together with β(1-4) linkages, with some species having mixed
β(1-3) and β(1-4) linked glucans. These chains are then branched, with either single
or longer glycosyl residues. Hemicellulose functions as load-bearing components in the
cell wall, by cross linking cellulose bundles, lignin, pectins, cell wall proteins, and non-
structural polysaccharides, through various covalent and non-covalent interactions.
Although hemicellulose consists of a potentially large source of fermentable sugars, it also
presents some minor challenges, in regards to fermentation. This is due to the diverse
nature of hemicellulose branching and composition. These aspects differ widely among
different plant species, and changes throughout the different stages of plant growth. The
variability in the monosaccharide components of hemicellulose, makes the suitability for
microbial fermentations very specific, and the linkages constituting the diverse structure
of hemicellulose, can affect the hydrolysis efficiency [8].
The major components of hemicellulose derived from wheat straw are xylans, composed
of β(1-4)-D-xylose linked subunits [9].
Lignin
Lignin (Figure 2.1 C) is an irregular polymer of phenylpropanoid monomers, without a
clearly defined shape or form. It is located in the cell walls of vascular plants, where
it during cell growth, is secreted into the cell wall, infiltrating the polysaccharide matrix,
whereby the polymerization process leads to a randomized structure. This results in the
formation of a rigid protective barrier surrounding the polysaccharides, which facilitates
the development of specialized tissues for water transport, supports vertical growth, and
prevents pathogens from invading [8].
For LCB use in bioproduction, lignin prevents depolymerization of cellulose, binds and
inactivates cellulolytic enzymes, and can produce compounds, inhibitory and toxic to-
wards the production organism [8]. Therefore, pretreatment of the biomass, to reduce the
amount and effect of lignin, is required.
2.1.2
Pretreatment
The pretreatment of LCB is a key aspect, for its use in bioconversion into biomass derived
materials. Pretreatment degrades the three aforementioned components, ensuring higher
fermentation/bioconversion efficiency [10]. The goal of the pretreatment is to delignify,
and break down the polymeric structure of hemicellulose. These two measures degrades
the crystalline structure of cellulose, increasing availability for enzymatic and microbial
digestion [11]. Furthermore, depolymerization of hemicellulose increases the amount of
digestible monomeric sugars, available in the resulting hydrolysate.
There are a plethora of pretreatment methods, for the production of lignocellulosic hy-
drolysate (LCH). These pretreatment methods can be divided into two major groups;
physical and chemical. The physical methods are used to decrease particle size of the

biomass, and include methods such as milling, steam explosion, grinding, and many
more. The goal with the chemical methods is to break down the biomass on a molec-
ular scale, and include methods using, acidic or alkaline liquids, enzymes or solvents
[10].
Hemicellulosic Hydrolysate
LCH can be further fractioned into cellulosic hydrolysate, lignin, and the most relevant
for this report, hemicellulosic hydrolysate (HCH) [12]. The pretreatment employed for the
hydrolysate used in this project, is an organosolv method called Fabiola. In this method,
the organosolv fractionation of sugars and lignin, uses an acetone-based process, in place
of the more commonly used organosolv ethanol. The use of acetone prevents undesired
ethylation of sugars and lignin, directly improving sustainability performance, via reduced
energy demand, and investment thereof. The Fabiola process is paired with a method
called LigniSep, which is a continuous lignin precipitation process, using a pulping liquor
that aggregates the lignin [13].
During the Fabiola process, a liquid fraction is generated, which is HCH. The hemicel-
lulosic fraction is separated, as other fractions are less efficiently liquefied, and thereby
remains in a solid residue [9]. The result is a hydrolysate, where the main sugar compo-
nent is xylose, followed by arabinose and glucose. In addition to these sugars, the HCH
contains a significant amount of acetic acid, acetate being the ester form thereof.
2.2
Value Added Products
As discussed, there are a multitude of high value products/biomass derived materials,
from the bioconversion of HCH. However, for the intents of this project, the focus will be
on isobutyric acid.
Isobutyric acid is a colorless liquid with a distinct odor. It has a wide range of applications,
including use as a solvent, flavoring agent, and in perfumes [14]. However, its primary
application is as a precursor in the production of methacrylic acid, which has an annual
market size of 2.7 million tonnes [15]. Methacrylic acid is used in various products, in-
cluding adhesives, paints, and textile finishes, and is also the main component in the
production of plexiglass [16].
The bioconversion pathway implemented in this project, starts with pyruvate or pyruvic
acid, as shown in Figure 2.2. The pathway requires 5 enzymes for the conversion, namely
AlsS (from Bacillus subtilis), KIVD (from Lactococcus lactis), and IlvD, IlvC, and PadA
(from E. coli).

The alsS gene from B. subtilis is responsible for the production of AlsS, which is an en-
zyme in the acetoin synthesis pathway. It catalyzes the aldo condensation of two pyruvate
molecules, to form acetolactic acid [17]. The next enzyme, IlvC, is produced from the na-
tive E. coli gene ilvC. It catalyzes an alkyl migration, and a subsequent ketol-acid reduction
of acetolactic acid, to produce (2R)-2,3-dihydroxy-3-methylbutanoate [18]. IlvD is again
produced by a native E. coli gene, ilvD. It catalyzes the dehydration of (2R)-2,3-dihydroxy-
3-methylbutanoate, to produce 3-methyl-2-oxobutanoate [19]. The following reaction is
carried out by the KIVD enzyme, produced by the heterologous gene kivd from L. lac-
tis. It catalyzes the decarboxylation of 3-methyl-2-oxobutanoate to isobutanal [20]. The
final reaction is carried out by the enzyme PadA, which is produced by the E. coli native
gene feaB. It catalyzes the oxidation of isobutanal, giving the end result of the pathway,
isobutyric acid [21].
To convert isobutyric acid to methacrylic acid, the isobutyric acid is subjected to oxidative
dehydrogenation. Subsequently the methacrylic acid can be subjected to esterification,
to yield methyl methacrylate (visualized in Figure 2.3) [22], the monomer of the polymeric
structure of plexiglass [16].

2.3
Bacterial Strain
The production host used in this project is E. coli K-12 MG1655. E. coli is a rod-shaped
gram-negative bacterium, commonly found in the digestive tract of humans, and other
warmblooded animals, with numerous non-pathogenic strains [23]. E. coli is a widely used
model organism, for cell factories, due to several key advantages. Firstly, the doubling

time commonly ranges between 15-30 minutes, when grown at 37◦C, and the culturing
is very simple. Furthermore, a large ”genetic toolbox” has been developed for E. coli,
facilitating a wide range of genetic modifications [24].
E. coli can metabolize most common sugars, including glucose, xylose, and arabinose.
However, like most microorganisms, E. coli exhibits a preference for glucose. The hierar-
chy for the utilization of the three main sugars in HCH is glucose first, arabinose second,
and xylose third [25], due to carbon catabolite repression (CCR).
As the sugar contents of HCH is heterogeneous to a high extent, and simultaneous
metabolism of all three sugars plus acetic acid has not been achieved yet, a consortium
based approach could be the solution to this challenge [26]. To face this challenge, four
different strains were constructed.
2.3.1
Genetic Engineering
The four strains constructed prior to this project are, a strain that metabolizes xylose as
primary carbon source, one that metabolizes glucose as primary carbon source (same
primary carbon source as the wild type), one that metabolizes arabinose as primary car-
bon source, and one that more efficiently metabolizes glucose and xylose simultaneously,
as primary carbon source. Common for all four strains is that they have been engineered
to be acetate auxotrophic, and they accumulate pyruvate (used for production of isobu-
tyric acid). Furthermore, each strain expresses a fluorescent protein, which can be used
to distinguish them from one another, and the starting strain. The genotype of the four
strains is shown in section 3.2.
Acetate Auxotrophy
The strains were constructed to be acetate auxotrophic, to facilitate uptake of acetic acid
and thereby prevent accumulation of acetic acid, as that could pose a challenge in po-
tential fed-batch and continuous fermentations. Acetate is a strong inhibitor of growth,
even at low concentrations, and is typically not utilized, when sugar is available. The
co-consumption of acetate and sugar, is therefore key to a good production host.
Seven knockouts were done, in order to construct the acetate auxotrophy [26]. The first
three was: 1) The aceEF operon, which catalyzes the overall conversion of pyruvate
to acetyl-CoA and CO2 [27][28]. 2) The focA-pflB complex, where focA is involved in
the transport of formate [29], and pflB catalyzes the conversion of pyruvate to formate
and acetyl-CoA [30]. 3) The poxB gene, which synthesizes a peripheral cell membrane
enzyme, that catalyzes the oxidative decarboxylation, to form acetate and CO2 [31].
In addition, four genes were identified as possible sources of acetate and acetyl-CoA,
through constraint-based metabolic modelling. Namely, tdcE (oxygen sensitive pyruvate
formate lyase), pflDC (oxygen sensitive pyruvate formate lyase II), ybdK (oxygen sen-
sitive pyruvate-flavodoxin oxidoreductase), and deoC (deoxyribose-phosphate aldolase,
involved in purine and pyrimidine degradation) [26].
As is evident from the function of these seven enzymes, the knockouts are also respon-
sible for the accumulation of pyruvate.
C5 and C6 sugar utilization
The constructed acetate auxotroph was used as the background strain for developing cell
lines, capable of utilizing the different sugars, present in HCH. To engineer an acetate-
auxotrophic strain that metabolizes glucose (C6) and is incapable of utilizing xylose and
arabinose (both C5), two gene complexes were deleted [26].
The xylAB operon, which is an essential part of xylose metabolism in E. coli was deleted.
It works in unison with the xylFGHR operon, which is responsible for the binding of xylose

and ATP, and transport over the cell membrane [32]. Additionally, the araBA operon was
deleted. Similar to xylAB, araBA works in conjunction with the araFGH system, which
facilitates substrate binding and transport. As a result, araBA is essential for arabinose
metabolism, and its deletion prevents E. coli from utilizing arabinose [33]. This strain will
be referred to as the glucose strain.
For the construction of an acetate auxotroph, incapable of utilizing glucose, four genes
were deleted [26]: 1) The manZ gene is a phosphoenolpyruvate-dependent sugar phos-
photransferase system (PTS), a major active transport system of carbohydrates. It cat-
alyzes the simultaneous translocation over the cell membrane, and phosphorylation of
incoming sugar compounds, and is involved in the metabolism of mannose and glucose
[34]. 2) The ptsG gene is also a PTS system, involved in the transport and simultaneous
phosphorylation of glucose [35]. 3) The gene gcd encodes a membrane bound enzyme,
glucose dehydrogenase (Gcd). Its role in the utilization of glucose is not fully elucidated,
as Gcd under normal conditions exists as an apoprotein [36], a protein missing a pros-
thetic group essential for enzymatic function. 4) The glk gene, encodes a cytoplasmic
glucokinase (Glk), which under normal conditions is not necessary for growth on glucose,
as glucose is phosphorylated by the PTS system [37]. However, in this strain the PTS
systems responsible for the uptake of glucose, have been deleted, and the Glk, can act
as a backup for the metabolism of glucose.
This strain incapable of utilizing glucose, was used as basis for constructing two strains,
one incapable of utilizing arabinose (referred to as xylose strain), and one incapable of
utilizing xylose (referred to as arabinose strain). To prevent the strains from utilizing ara-
binose or xylose, the genes araBA or xylAB was deleted, respectively [26].
CRP* strain
The fourth strain was engineered to better co-utilize all sugars and acetate simultaneously.
This was achieved through modifying the cyclic AMP receptor protein (CRP) operon.
CRP is known for its global regulatory role in E. coli. When readily metabolized car-
bon sources are absent, adenylate cyclase is activated, to produce cyclic AMP (cAMP).
cAMP binds and activates CRP, increasing the affinity for DNA, which for the purposes of
this project, activates operons associated with metabolism of alternative carbon sources.
Evolved mutations in crp has also been studied intensively, and mutagenesis have been
induced using e.g. UV-radiation, chemical reagents, or targeting the crp gene with error
prone PCR, though several studies also have relied on spontaneously arising mutants.
The CRP mutations has generally been generated in cAMP deficient production strains,
and screened for mutations enabling utilization of alternative carbon sources.
These
cAMP independent CRP variants are known as CRP* [38].
The structure of CRP*, is conformationally similar to cAMP-bound CRP, and intervenes
in the CCR, to allow for co-utilization of non-PTS sugars in the presence of glucose [39].
Furthermore, a study (by Yuan, X et al.) has shown, that a strategy utilizing the deletion
of ptsG in combination with CRP*, increases the simultaneous consumption of xylose and
glucose, improving sugar utilization efficiency [40]. This might also be worth investigating
in this CRP* strain, if future HPLC results show a low consumption rate of xylose.
Production of isobutyric acid
In addition to being transformed, with the plasmids referenced in section 3.3, for the pro-
duction of isobutyric acid - prior to this project the yqhD gene was deleted. yqhD encodes
a broad-substrate range NADPH-dependent aldehyde reductase [41], which has been
shown to be essential for prevention of isobutanol formation, during isobutyric acid pro-
duction [26].

2.4
Adaptive Laboratory Evolution
Also prior to this project, the four strains were subjected to ALE, to increase their toler-
ance towards HCH, and carbon source utilization of the strains corresponding sugar(s).
ALE can be conducted by progressively exposing cells to increasing concentrations of an
inhibitory or toxic compound, thereby selecting for enhanced tolerance over time. ALE
can also be used to increase a cell populations carbon source utilization, when growing
on a substrate, that has sub-optimal growth conditions [42].
For the purpose of this report, the cells were grown in a mock hydrolysate, described
in section 3.1.4. To increase the tolerance of the inhibitory/toxic compounds found in
HCH, between passes in the evolution the mock hydrolysate was supplied with, increas-
ing amounts of the actual HCH. Between every increase of HCH, the fraction of actual
hydrolysate was increased by 2.5 - 5 g/L. All cell-line populations had been adapted for
growth in HCH with a final concentration of 40 g/L xylose.

3.1
Media and Solutions
3.1.1
LB Media
10 g/L tryptone, 10 g/L NaCl, and 5 g/L Yeast extract dissolved in MilliQ water (MQ) and
autoclaved.
3.1.2
M9 ”extra” - Minimal Media
400 mL MQ is added to a beaker, followed by 100 mL 10x M9 salts, and mix by shaking.
Add 1 mL 2 M MgSO4, and mix by shaking. Dissolve 100 µL 1 M CaCl2 in 50 mL MQ, mix
by shaking, and add it to the beaker, mix by shaking. Dissolve 1 mL FeCl3 solution in 50
mL MQ, mix by shaking, and add it to the beaker, mix by shaking. Dissolve 500 µL ”extra”
trace elements in 50 mL MQ, mix by shaking, and add it to the beaker, mix by shaking.
Add MQ to 800 mL in the beaker, adjust pH to 6.8, and filter sterilize into autoclaved blue
cap bottle.
Stock solutions
10x M9 salts: 128 g/L Na2HPO4 · 7H2O, 30 g/L KH2PO4, 5 g/L NaCl, 10 g/L NH4Cl. Au-
toclave.

3.5.1
Growth Curves
For doing multiple analysis of mutants, the 94 colonies were transferred into a clear
polystyrene 96 well plate (in addition to the starting strain and a well only containing me-
dia), containing 200 µL liquid LB media + 1 g/L NaOAc. The cells were then grown for 12
hours in an incubator, at 37◦C and 250 rpm. From this point, two separate methods were
applied.
Tolerance Test to 20 g/L Hydrolysate
10 µl LB culture was transferred to 190 µL M9extra + 3 g/L NaOAc + 5 g/L of the strains
corresponding sugar (mock hydrolysate for CRP*), and grown for 14 hours in an incu-
bator, at 37◦C and 250 rpm. The cultures were then transferred to 20 g/L hydrolysate
(meaning the hydrolysate is adjusted to 20 g/L xylose), and placed in a microtiter plate
reader (ELx808, BioTek, USA), at 37◦C and 250 rpm linear shake, and the growth is
followed, by measuring OD600, every 10 minutes, for up to 48 hours.
Tolerance Test to 40 g/L hydrolysate
10 µl LB culture was transferred to 190 µL 20 g/L hydrolysate, and grown for 14 hours in an
incubator, at 37◦C and 250 rpm. The cultures were then transferred to 40 g/L hydrolysate
(meaning the hydrolysate is adjusted to 40 g/L xylose), and placed in a plate reader, at
37◦C and 250 rpm linear shake, and the growth is followed for up to 48 hours (same as
above).
3.5.2
Colony PCR
Colony PCR, also known as whole cell PCR, is the amplification of target DNA, without
prior purification of the DNA. The method involves picking a single colony of cells, and
transferring it to a PCR mix, containing all required components for amplification. For
colony PCR to be effective, a sufficient amount of cells must be lysed prior to the reaction,
which can either be done chemically, or more commonly by exposing the cells to high
temperatures in the initial step of the PCR. Additionally, the amount of cell debris must
not be high enough to present PCR inhibition, thereby preventing PCR amplification.
The main advantages of colony PCR, is that the DNA purification step is averted, cutting
down on time and cost, while sample handling is kept at a minimum, lowering the risk of
contamination [45].

3.5.4
NanoDropTM
Purity of the extracted DNA was assessed using NanoDropTM. NanoDropTM uses spec-
trophotometry, for a quick estimation of the concentration of DNA, RNA, or protein in a
given sample. The main disadvantages, when using spectrophotometry is that it is not
selective for DNA, RNA, or protein, meaning it cannot distinguish between the three, and
some noise is to be expected in the measurements [47].
In addition to concentrations estimation, the NanoDropTM does two additional reads, to
assess the purity of the sample. Both measurements are given as a ratio, 260/280 and
260/230. DNA and RNA both absorb light at a wavelength of 260 nm, and the second
measurements in both ratios, then reads at 280 nm, and 230 nm respectively, giving an
indication of the purity of the sample [48].
3.5.5
QubitTM
Quantification of the extracted DNA was measured using QubitTM. QubitTM is a method
that relies on fluorimetry, to measure the concentration of DNA, RNA, or protein in a
given sample. It works by using sample specific dyes, that when not bound to the target
molecule, have extremely low fluorescence. Once the dye is bound to the target molecule,
the fluorescence increases, and can be read as a measurement of DNA/RNA/protein con-
centration, depending on target molecule [49].
In this project, the InvitrogenTM, QubitTM dsDNA BR Assay Kit was used for quantification,
following manufacturers instructions.
3.5.6
Transformation by Electroporation
Electroporation is a widely used transformation method, which induces pores in the cell
membrane with a brief high-voltage electrical impulse. The pores makes the membrane
permeable, allowing the uptake of DNA into the cell [50].
For this method, a liquid culture of competent cells is needed, to which the wanted DNA is
added. After the electrical impulse, the cells are extremely fragile, and must be transferred
to a low stress media, followed by incubation at optimal temperature (for E. coli that is 37◦

C). When the cells have restituted, they can be plated onto a selective media, only allowing
successfully transformed cells to grow.

3.5.7
Preliminary Production Experiment
The preliminary production experiment was started by growing an O/N culture (approx 16
hours) in 5 mL media, described in section 3.1.5, in a 50 mL Falcon tube. The cells were
grown in an incubator at 37◦C, 250 rpm. A sample was then taken from each, and the
OD600 was measured. An amount of the culture was then added to a new Falcon tube,
containing 4 mL of the same media, for a start OD600 of 0.1. The cells were induced with
4 µl 0.1 M IPTG (for a final concentration of 0.1 mM) 3 hours after inoculation.
The first sample was taken at 7 hours after inoculation (4 hours after induction), and
a second sample was taken 24 hours after inoculation (21 hours after induction). The
OD600 was measured, the samples were spun down, and the supernatant was saved and
stored at -20◦C, for later HPLC analysis.
3.5.8
Production Experiment
The actual production experiment was conducted similarly to the preliminary production
experiment, with some key differences:
• Container: A 250 mL shake flask, allowing for more aeration.
• Media: 20 g/L hydrolysate, instead of the mock hydrolysate.
– Still with the addition of kanamycin and ampicillin for plasmid maintenance.
• Working volume: Increased to 50 mL.
• Temperature after induction: Was lowered to 30◦C.
– This adjustment could have been implemented in the preliminary production
experiment but was inadvertently omitted.
Additionally, samples were collected more frequently (0 h, 3 h, 5 h, 7 h, 9 h, 12 h, 24 h,
and 29 h). However, only the initial media sample, and endpoint samples (29 hours) were
thus far analyzed via HPLC. The remaining samples are intended for further analysis in
future work.
3.5.9
HPLC
HPLC or high-performance liquid chromatography works, by separating compounds in a
given sample, based on size, charge, and polarity. The system is pressure driven, with
a mobile phase, containing the analytes. The mobile phase then flows through a col-
umn containing the stationary phase. Depending on the analytes affinity to the stationary
phase, they will elude from the column at different speeds [51].
When the mobile phase has eluded from the column, two detectors (for the method used
in this project) are in place to measure the amount of compound present in the sample.
One detector is a UV absorbance detector, which has the advantage of being very pre-
cise, even at low concentrations of analyte. The downside for this detector is that it only
detects UV-absorbing compounds [52]. The other detector is a Refractive Index Detec-
tor (RID), which has the advantage of being able to detect any compound, based on how
light is refracted, when it hits a certain compound. The downside here being, that a higher
concentration of the compound is needed, in order to measure the concentration reliably
[53].

Ultimately variants of the CRP* strain were assessed individually, as with the other strains,

4.1.6
Heterogeneity
An overall point to be made, that could explain some of the inconsistency observed in all
four strains, is that the toxicity of the hydrolysate lowers over time, as exposure to sunlight
can cause reactions, and some of the toxic compounds precipitating, thereby not making
it into the cultures. In extension of that, there is a possibility of some of the precipitate
accidentally making it into the cultures, when they were prepared. This could lead to
an increased toxicity, randomly divided into different variants and replicates, without the
possibility of finding out which, and take it into account when assessing the variants.
Aside from the heterogeneity stemming purely from the hydrolysate, it could be due to
an amplification of heterogeneity, when transferring the precultures to their respective
hydrolysate. As the precultures were grown in an incubator, and no measurements were
taken, there was no way of knowing the start OD600.
One way to circumvent the heterogeneity, would have been to further optimize the pro-
tocols, to ensure everything is done the exact same way between replicates. This could
be done by making the replicates in batches - meaning e.g. for the xylose strain in 40
g/L hydrolysate, the replicates could have been made at the same time, using the same
batch of media/hydrolysate. Another approach could have been ensuring all variants start
at the same OD600, by diluting the samples to a specific OD600 (as done in the verification
of growth, and production experiments). However, working with such a big sample size as
done in this project, such a task would be nearly impossible, without the use of robotics.

4.2.3
Comparing results
When comparing Figure 4.10 and Figure 4.13, panel A in both figures reveals that higher
levels of isobutyric acid were produced during the preliminary production experiment, with
the exception of variant A11 replicate 3. A potential explanation for this observation is that
the cells in the preliminary experiment were cultured in 50 mL Falcon tubes, creating a
more microoxic environment. This could have redirected more NADP+ towards isobutyric
acid production rather than respiration [54].
However, when comparing the results of this project with those from a previously published
study [55], the titer achieved in the preliminary production experiment was nearly twice
as high as that reported in the prior study. In contrast, the titer obtained in the actual
production experiment was more comparable to the previously published results. This
significant difference is cause for further validation to confirm the high titer, and if the
results are reproducible.
This finding is however, still relevant to consider for potential scale-up in a bioreactor.
While sufficient oxygenation would be required initially to achieve an adequate cell density,
reducing oxygenation after the growth phase could enhance isobutyric acid production.

6.2
Perspectives
Reflecting on this project, one aspect that could have been improved was conducting all
three replicates for each strain simultaneously, using the same batch of media and hy-
drolysate, as discussed in section 4.1.6. This approach would most likely have minimized
the heterogeneity observed during the initial growth experiments, thereby quickening the
following analysis.
Additionally, the overall timeline could have been shortened by scheduling growth exper-
iments consecutively, which was implemented later in the project. Early on, however,
significant downtime between experiments contributed to delays in completing the initial
analysis.

Hemicellulosic Biomass: A Sustainable Carbon Source
The common practice for producing fermentation-based products is 
currently to use 1st generation biomass, i.e. sugars from edible food 
sources. In this project we:
X
• Use hemicellulosic biomass hydrolysates
• Engineer bacterial strains to efficiently valorize hemicellulosic biomass
• Minimize use of 1st generation biomass
• Work our way towards a more sustainable production
Adaptive Laboratory Evolution
Prior to this project engineered strains of 
Escherichia coli was subjected to adaptive 
laboratory evolution (ALE), in order to 
improve its tolerance to hemicellulosic 
hydrolysate.
Analysis of 384 Colonies
384 individual ALE strains will be 
investigated.
Robustness
The cells’ capability of still being able to 
produce a product of interest will be verified.
Mutants with Highest Fitness
The mutants with the best ability to grow on 
the hemicellulosic hydrolysate while still 
being capable of production will be picked for 
further investigation.

Objective
The overall objective is to contribute to the 
development of an efficient E. coli based cell-factory 
tolerant to hemicellulosic hydrolysates, thereby
