Abstract
In this thesis adaptive sliding mode controllers for a motion system are designed
and evaluated. These controllers are capable of adjusting itself in order to reject
disturbances and unknown dynamics of the system while maintaining the desired
accuracy. A mathematical model of a single-axis positioning system is used as the
reference motion system. The adaptive controllers are designed using the adaptive
gain technique as well as the time-varying sliding surface one. The controllers are
simulated in different test scenarios with changing disturbances and reference signals.
Its performance is evaluated and compared with conventional controllers used in
these kind of systems.
The results show great adaptation capabilities for both
techniques and outperform the conventional controller.

Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
Nowadays, the use of computerized manufacturing is very widespread. These kind
of processes combine many devices which are operated by their respective control
loops. The combination of these control loops determine the overall accuracy of the
relative positioning between the tool and the workpiece.
The lowest level of control, the axis control, is responsible for positioning each axis
of the machine to the desired point of the trajectory. This control loop is the most
important one when it comes to ensuring a positioning precision. This is due to
the degrading phenomena that appear and are handled by this controller such as
varying friction or load inertia due to wear.
In order to achieve suﬀicient manufacturing tolerances it is vital to ensure a high
accuracy in the positioning system. This means that any kind of disturbances or
degradation need to be taken into account by the controller. Conventional solu-
tions satisfy these requirements but only whenever these disturbances are correctly
modelled. However, if the parameters of the system change because of wear the con-
troller performance worsens unless it is calibrated again. Thus, it can be a highly
costly process in terms of time and money.
In order to to mitigate this problem, the introduction of adaptive controllers that
can adjust to these variations poses significant interest. These should be able take
into account any kind of degrading phenomena as well as being robust to additional
disturbances. Therefore, the use of sliding mode controller presents itself as a proper
candidate.
Sliding-mode controllers have been shown to facilitate robust high-accuracy tracking
in motion control systems. This makes sliding-mode algorithms excellent candidates
for servo and positioning solutions in electromechanical drive-train systems such as
machine tools.
Thus, the aim of this project will be to design an adaptive sliding mode controller
for an axis positioning system that is able to adapt to varying disturbances and
degrading phenomena while maintaining a desired accuracy.

1.3
Literature Review
In this section a review of the current methodologies and techniques used in this
thesis is given. Firstly, sliding mode controllers and its properties are introduced.
Then the adaptive sliding mode controllers are explained and in the end the uses of
the barrier function are also mentioned.
1.3.1
Sliding Mode Controllers
Sliding Mode Controllers (SMC) have been widely used in electromechanical systems
[1]. These controllers are based on a very high frequency switching control signal
and were first presented in [2].
This allows them to be robust against matched
perturbations or disturbances and ensure finite-time convergence.
However, the
high frequency signal introduces what is called control chattering, which can cause
damage to the actuators of the system. In order to reduce chattering, the use of
continuous control functions such as saturation was introduced in [3]. Unfortunately,
these types of control functions limit the accuracy of the controller.
Therefore, the use of High Order SMC (HOSMC) was introduced in [4], with a
notable mention to a kind of second order controller named Super-Twisting Sliding
Mode Controller (STSMC). The introduction of HOSMC also allowed for robust

exact differentiators [5]. Later, the STSMC was also modified to obtain arbitrary
order STSMC as proposed in [6].
1.3.2
Adaptive Sliding Mode Controllers
The Adaptive Sliding Mode Controllers (ASMC) are a combination of Adaptive
Controllers and SMC. This means that they can change their parameters to take
into account the varying conditions of the system.
One of the first adaptation of SMC was in [7], where a strong Lyapunov function
allowed the STSMC to reject linearly growing disturbances. Then in [8] the use of
adaptive gains with the STSMC was introduced and further developed in [9]. Thus,
this meant the appearence of the Adaptive Gain STSMC (AGSTSMC) which can
reject bounded disturbances with an unknown bound. This adaptive gain technique
was also applied to arbitrary order SMC in [10] by using an adaptive continuous
SMC. To ensure a desired performance of the system [11] proposed a prescribed
performance ASMC that guarantees a certain error within a given time.
Another ASMC approach to prevent overestimation of gains that has been gaining
a lot of attention recently is the use of Barrier Functions (BF). These were first used
in [12] in combination with Lyapunov functions for nonlinear systems. Then [13]
used it to adapt the Super-Twisting (ST) differentiator. The first use in SMC was
shown in [14] where a BFASMC was introduced for first order SMC. Afterwards, in
[15, 16] the BF method was adapted for the ASTSMC. As with the standard SMC,
HOSMC were also modified in [17] to make use of this technique for an arbitrary
order of the controller. A further study of these type of HOSMC was done in [18] for
nonlinear systems specifically. Lastly, [19] also proposed the use of BF for prescribed
performance ASMC.
The other main type of ASMC are those that make use of time-varying sliding
surfaces. This was proposed in [20] for second-order uncertain systems. Later, the
optimal controller parameters according to performance specifications were shown
in [21] for second and third-order systems when the bound of the disturbances is
known. In [22] a moving surface with variable pole placement was presented. This
allowed the poles to increase or decrease its speed to reject the disturbances. Then
in [23, 24] alternative adaptation laws for the surface parameters were proposed.

Adaptive Sliding Mode Control
In this section a brief overview of SMC and ASMC theory will be explained. The
main goal is to introduce the concepts that will be further used in Section 4. First,
the standard SMC will be explained and then ASMC.
3.1
Sliding Mode Control
SMC makes use of a term called the sliding variable which is a function to be
designed by the user. Whenever this sliding variable is equal to zero the sliding
surface (or sliding manifold) is defined. Choosing an adequate sliding variable is key
to the desired performance of the SMC, since the closed loop performance of the
system while on the manifold depends on it.
Therefore, the goal of SMC is to drive the system to the defined sliding surface and
maintain it once it reaches it. While on the manifold, the system exhibits the traits
of SMC such as great accuracy, robustness to matched disturbances and finite-time
convergence of the sliding variable to zero.
From the previous explanation it can be seen that SMC requires two main steps.
The first one is choosing a function for the system on the sliding surface to satisfy
the desired performance. The second one is the design of the control law that will
drive the system to the sliding manifold.
Let us show an example of a basic SMC. Consider the following uncertain system
˙x = x + u + ϕ(t)
(3.1)
with ϕ(t) being the bounded disturbance. A simple SMC law for (3.1) is
u = −x −k · sign(σ)
(3.2)
where σ is the sliding variable. The first term is a conventional state-feedback term
and the second one is the SMC term. This last one is in charge of rejecting the
disturbance ϕ(t) by using an infinitely fast switching term. The use of such a term
is one of the main traits of all SMC. However, this also introduces chatter in the

3.2
Adaptive Sliding Mode Control
In this thesis the control technique that will be used is ASMC. As the name implies,
this is a combination of adaptive control and SMC. Adaptive control is defined as
a type of control that changes its parameters according to the varying situation of
the system. Its use with SMC is of great interest due to some limitations of SMC.
As explained in Section 3.1 the SMC is widely used due to being able to reject
disturbances and perturbations. However, in order to do so, the bound of the dis-
turbance needs to be known or else it will lead to an overestimation of the controller
gain. This is a major concern since in most real-life applications it is practically
impossible to know the bound. Also, overestimating the gain leads to an increase in
magnitude to the chatter.
This is where the adaptive techniques become extremely useful. Most of these ASMC
techniques focus on adapting the gains of the controller in order not to overestimate
them. One of these applied to the STSMC is

The parameters kα, γα, µ, αm, η and ε are all positive constant values. The role of

• kα and γα both determine how fast α increases or decreases when α is greater

value can not be known beforehand and the addition of disturbances will likely
increase this reachable threshold.

• η is the slope of the increasing rate of α when it gets below the set value of

• ε is just a constant of proportionality between α and β.

5.3
Discussion
Analyzing the results from Section 5 yields some interesting remarks. The first one
is that only three of the proposed controllers satisfy the desired accuracy criterion,
those being the AGBF, MS and MSBF. From these ones, AGBF performs the best in
nearly all the test scenarios except the ones with high frequency and friction, where
the MSBF performs slightly better than these other two. Therefore, it seems that
for extreme cases of disturbances both methods of adapting the gains and variable
sliding surfaces provide similar results.
However, the implementation of the controllers is also an important aspect. The
AGBF requires fewer parameters than the MSBF and even less than the AG while
obtaining better results. The simplest controller is the P-PI which is also the worst
performer overall. Its simplicity shows why it is such a widespeard option.
Also worth mentioning is the presence of chatter in the control signal. The three
compliant controllers manifest the same amplitude of chatter although the signals
are not the same. Whereas AGBF and MS signals are very similar, MSBF presents
a completely different shape which seems to be smoother than the others.
The
amplitude of the chattering seems to be unaffected by neither the increase in friction
or frequency.
Lastly, it is also very evident that the methods that use the BF obtain better results
than their counterparts. Nevertheless, in the case of the MS controller this difference
is not that large since it is still able to perform very closely to the MSBF controller.
MSBF controller however shows a substantially faster adaptation speed which is a
lot more desirable for sudden and unexpected disturbances.
==PAGE:56==
6
Conclusions
The main objective of this thesis was to develop an ASMC capable of adjusting itself
to different degrading phenomena while maintaining a desired positioning accuracy.
Different approaches to ASMC have been used to compare their performance.
The adaptation techniques include the use of adaptive controller gains as well as a
time-varying sliding surface. The use of the moving surface shows that this adap-
tation method can also be a reliable technique for ASMC. Both methods have also
been modified to introduce the use of barrier function, which has led to a more
robust controller.
In total five different ASMC have been designed, evaluated and compared with con-
ventional axis positioning controllers. Simulations with different friction values and
reference signal speeds have been carried out to determine the adaptation capabili-
ties. Performance indexes have also been defined in order to quantitatively compare
them. The controllers design and implementation complexity has also been analyzed
and quantified.
Three of these controllers (AGBF, MS and MSBF) satisfy the desired accuracy in
all the simulations. The AGBF controller offers the best performance overall while
being less complex than its two competitors. Nevertheless, both MS and MSBF
manage to outperform it in the highest disturbance scenario.
6.1
Future work
The work shown in this thesis is open to further expansion. Some of the aspects
that could lead to wider study are the following:
• Real system testing: this thesis compares the algorithms only through sim-
ulations. By implementing the controllers on real axis-positioning systems,
the comparison between the controllers would lead to even more interesting
results. It would also allow to determine unforeseen limitations of each con-
troller.
• Controller tuning algorithms: one of the main drawbacks of the ASMC
is the need of manually tuning the controller parameters. This is very time-
consuming and does not ensure an optimal choice of the parameters. There-
