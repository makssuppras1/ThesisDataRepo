Abstract
Global warming has made the shipping lanes through the Arctic Ocean more possible and ac-
cessible than ever imagined by melting an unprecedented amount of sea ice during the summer.
This development may provide great cost savings for the shipping industry, but also presents
new challenges in terms of safety both for Arctic environment and the ships. The energy explo-
ration in Arctic has already began transporting LNG using ships that also act as ice breakers.
The present project focusses on the numerical modelling of ship-ice interaction together with
considering liquid sloshing in the tanks. The ice breaking process is simulated by a numerical
model developed by Su(2009)[1] and liquid sloshing in LNG tanks are modelled by linear and
non linear multimodal model Faltinsen(2009)[2]. Two diﬀerent cases of ship motion in ice were
analyzed. One with surge only and other with the surge, sway and yaw motion combined. It
was found sloshing that cannot be ignored from hydrodynamic calculations and depending on
ship motion there are diﬀerent sloshing observation. Linear multimodal theory alone cannot
explain the sloshing inside LNG tanks as in some cases and have to use non linear theory. In
some ﬁndings an adaptive non linear modal approach is required. Regarding the force on the
ship structure, it was found that sloshing reduces the inertial eﬀect of ship. The observation
invites future work investigation using a coupled approach between ice-ship interaction and
sloshing multimodal solver using an actual ice breaker LNG ship dimensions and inclusion of
all 6 degrees of freedom.

1.1. Background and motivation
With rising temperature and continuous thinning of Arctic sea ice, the northern sea route
(NSR) has been become an attractive sailing route providing much shorter route from Asia to
Europe compared to the existing route of Indian ocean via Suez canal or Panama. In recent
times there has been an increase in the number of ships crossing through the NSR without
the assistance of ice breakers. With the retreat of ice, the shipping distance between Asia and
Europe becomes shorter using NSR and is highly likely with the climate trend that by 2050
NSR will be available for year-round passage of conventional vessels with no ice reinforcement

1.4. Sloshing and its impact on LNG ships operating in
ice
There is a thin line between exploration and exploitation which has been witnessed in many
human endeavors.
The drive for maximizing proﬁts can sideline the need to look into the
environmental impact. LNG shipping in the Arctic indeed fulﬁlls the need for a cleaner fuel
and serves energy hungry nations. But at the same time a failure or an accident has a high risk
of disturbing the ecology of the pristine Arctic. The lessons in Arctic shipping involving LNG
should not be learned the way tanker shipping did. With shipping in Arctic using dedicated
LNGC still in its nascent stage, it is imperative that the risks are addressed based on scientiﬁc
ﬁndings without relying solely on existing rules.
The thesis is an attempt to analyze the
impact of sloshing inside an LNG tank during ice breaking process. The motion of hull during
ice breaking can result in excitation of free surface of liquid inside LNG tanks and depending
on the motion of the ship, it might result in violent sloshing. The author believes that the
topic has not yet been investigated scientiﬁcally and is the ﬁrst approach to looking at sloshing
during ice breaking process.

1.5. Problem deﬁnition
Presently the design cycle of an LNG vessel for operation in Arctic starts with treating the
liquid cargo in tank as a solid mass. The main purpose behind ﬁxing the tank dimension is
maximizing the cargo carrying capacity. Be it the spherical moss tanks or the membrane tanks
currently used in the industry. However, the tank dimension has a direct relation with natural
sloshing modes and some of the modes may be excited on the excitation frequency. In the scope
of the work following questions have been asked -
? Does ice breaking mechanism acts as an excitation for the liquid free surface resulting in
sloshing in the tanks.
? To what complexity should the sloshing be analyzed.
? Which is a better solvers for sloshing.
– treating liquid as a solid mass.
– using linear sloshing method to analyze sloshing.
– using non linear sloshing method to analyze sloshing.
? Can sloshing be ignored or not.
1.6. Objectives
Present work is constituted by the following sub-tasks:
• Develop a Linear Multimodal solver for sloshing in a typical 3D rectangular tank using
potential theory.
• Develop a Non linear multimodal sloshing solver to analyze sloshing in tank at frequencies
close to resonance and large forcing amplitude.
• Veriﬁcation of multimodal solvers.
• Importing numerical method of ice-ship interaction during ice breaking process and im-
plementing in the sloshing solver for a typical LNG tank size.

2. Deﬁnition of sloshing problem
Sloshing is the motion of free surface of liquid. For sloshing to happen there needs to be a free
surface meaning the tank has to be partially ﬁlled. The motion of free surface can be initiated
by either external force or by a free vibration. The later kind of sloshing is called as natural
sloshing and will be discussed in this chapter. As the word ’Natural’ says, this kind of sloshing is
something that is not due to an external force. The word natural oscillation/vibration, natural
frequency, natural modes are used extensively in the science of sloshing.
Potential ﬂow theory is used to explain the liquid motion inside a tank. In potential theory the
liquid is assumed to be incompressible, inviscid, irrotational. And ﬂuid velocity at any time
’t’ can be expressed in terms of spatial derivative of velocity potential Φ, which satisﬁes the
Laplace equation, 52Φ = 0 in the ﬂuid domain. Surface tension is ignored considering uniform
gravitational ﬁeld.
Linear theory will be used to estimate the natural sloshing frequencies by using analytical
expressions. These expressions exists only for a few tank shapes including a 2D and 3D rect-
angular tank. The shape of free surface can be understood as modes which depends on the
distribution of number of nodes and anti-node in space. Modal Theory is the understanding of
sloshing as modes. The main aim of modal theory is to develop equations using potential theory
and solving boundary value problem to calculate the natural frequencies. The free surface nat-
ural frequencies depends solely on the geometry of tank. Hence in the design phase it becomes
critical to shape the tank such that the natural frequencies are kept away from frequencies it
will be subjected to.

3.4. Conclusion
• From results in Eq.3.10 (3D case), the natural frequency of free surface oscillation in a
rectangular tank depend on tank characteristic dimensions ’L1’,’L2’ and the water depth
’h’ but not on tank’s height. An important mathematical conclusion is the natural fre-
quency increases with increasing modes. Meaning the lower modes have smaller natural
frequency and vice-versa. When a tank is subjected to a range of excitation frequency, the
lower modes can be easily excited. Thus a prior knowledge of natural sloshing frequency
is highly important. In the design of tank it is important to keep the natural sloshing
frequencies away from the frequencies it will be subjected to.
• The oscillation of free surface, has the same property as a pure standing wave. If there
is no damping in a rectangular tank there is no escape of energy and it keeps building
up inside the tank if forced to frequency close to the natural oscillation. According to
the linear sloshing theory, resonance condition will results in violent oscillations of free
surface, which in turn will result in loss of stability of the ship and build up of high
pressure within tank.
• The derived potential is for natural oscillation of liquid free surface.
However in the
following chapters when sloshing is analyzed on tanks with forced excitation, the same
concept to satisfy the spectral boundary condition together with conditions due to tank’s
motion will be employed.

4. Linear Modal Theory
4.1. Introduction
With the concept of natural frequencies which involved no forcing, the next step is to develop
equations for a tank subjected to an external force. The boundary value problem remain the
same with an additional requirement that it has to account for rigid body motion of tank.
However in the boundary value problem, the non linear terms are ignored. In order for Linear
modal theory to be applicable, both the oscillatory motion of tank and the liquid motion are
assumed to be small compared to tank size. And the frequency of motion should not be close to
natural frequency of sloshing. Damping and non-linear eﬀect are ignored but will be introduced
in the later part.
Modal theory is explained in this chapter which explains the transformation of boundary value
problem to a set of multidimensional ordinary diﬀerential equations. It is called modal theory
because the free surface or the velocity potential is expressed as a summation series of product
of each modal function with a coeﬃcient that is mode and time dependent. In the series of free
surface, this coeﬃcient is called as generalized coordinates for free surface, β(t) and for velocity
potential series is called as generalized coordinates for velocity potential, R(t).
Indexing of modal equations and hydrodynamic coeﬃcient
In the chapter, the modal equations and hydrodynamic coeﬃcients are indexed with either a
single integer (m) or a pair (m,n). The single index represents the sloshing mode of liquid in
2D rectangular tank and the pair for 3D rectangular tank.
4.2. Assumptions of Linear modal theory
• Ship motions are small relative to the breadth of the ship. The motion are assumed to
be linear meaning the oscillatory ship motion at any point on ship is small compared to
the breadth and draft of ship. Linearization is done by introduction of a factor ", which
expresses the order of magnitudes as below

4.5. Damping Coeﬃcient
Damping coeﬃcient has a direct relation on time duration for sloshing to reach steady state
condition. Due to the complexity of factors aﬀecting damping, it is not possible to ﬁnd reliable
data unless speciﬁc model test are performed. Model tests provide a means to calibrate the
empirical/assumed formulae. The damping coeﬃcient in the code is an assumed number, which
is not veriﬁed for its magnitude.
A conservative small value of 0.005 or a higher value of 0.01 has been used in the calculation.
One can argue whether 0.005 is a small value. However, it is noted in the solution to Modal
equation, a higher value of damping coeﬃcient although make the transients die out but it
also aﬀects the particular solution. To summarize, the damping should be small enough for
transients to die out gradually, but not too big enough to aﬀect the results for the particular
solution. Further discussion on how damping inﬂuences the outcome of steady state solution
has been made in Sec.5.7

4.7. Steps in calculation - Linear modal theory
The derivation and use of the linear modal theory for a deﬁned motion involves the following
steps
1. Solve the spectral boundary problem for few initial modes and frequency(between seven
and twelve).
2. Solve Neumann boundary-value problem to obtain Stokes-Joukowski potential.
3. Calculate hydrodynamic coeﬃcients and inertia tensor.
4. For the deﬁned motion, at each time step get Km(t) in the linear modal equation and
solve for generalized coordinates for liquid motion (β(t)) at the same time step. In the
multimodal solver couple the excitation and Modal equation (ODE) to get the solution
at each time step.

4.11. Conclusion
• The forced sloshing problem can be expressed as an ordinary diﬀerential equation in term
of generalized coordinate of free surface. The governing equations used are linear, and
the approach is called as linear modal theory.
• The advantage of using modal theory over other direct numerical time-domain solution is
computational time saving for long time simulation. The solution of linear modal theory
consists of solving for inﬁnite set of sloshing modes.
However, it is seen that higher
modes have relatively small contribution in the ﬁnal result. Instead of solving for an
inﬁnite number of modes the solution can be truncated to a limited number of initial
sloshing modes.
• A direct solution in the time domain require solving boundary value problem at each time
step whereas the modal formulation requires numerical solutions for a limited number of
modes and Stokes-Joukowski potential before time-domain solution.

Diﬀerences in linear multimodal and Ibrahim’s approach
Linear multimodal approach to solving the ODE for forced harmonic oscillation consists of
sum of homogeneous solution and a particular solution. In simple words the solution consists of
an initial transients dominated phase and a steady state after transients die out. It is because
of the way the diﬀerential equation is solved (Refer Appendix.A) the results of sloshing are
plotted in time domain and the simulation are run for suﬃciently long time depending upon
the excitation frequency and damping in the system.
Ibrahim’s approach, does not take damping in to account and the solution oscillates with
steady state from the start of simulation. There is no need to run the simulation as there is
not any change either amplitude or phase.

6. Non linear Sloshing Theory
6.1. Introduction and Historical development of non
linear modal analysis
Non linearity plays a major role for the free surface dynamics when frequencies are close to
resonant frequencies of the lowest mode and for large forcing amplitudes. The derivation of
non linear modal equation is a complex mathematical task and requires a great deal of patience
both in understanding and time. The timelapse of development in the ﬁeld of non linear modal
analysis is given below-
• In the beginning of non linear multimodal thoery, only a few modes were considered.
Second and higher order contribution from non-leading terms were neglected.
• Later on, the non linear modal analysis adopted asymptotic1 intermodal ordering between
leading modes and some of the second order modes. Narimov(1957) assuming the lowest
natural modes dominate reduced the mathematical derivation of modal system in to a
series of Neumann boundary problem based on Taylor expansion of boundary condition.
• In 1970’s a variational approach was adopted which made it possible to obtain multidi-
mensional nonlinear modal equations without assuming the asymptotic ordering. However
using the asymptotic ordering helps to reduce the inﬁnite dimensional non linear modal
equations to ﬁnite dimensional form.
• The nonlinear multimodal theory applied in the present work follows the work of Faltinsen
& Timokha (2009). This means Moissev type of ordering is used and single-dominant
modal equations will be applied for 3D rectangular tanks.
Moissev type of ordering,
which assumes that the dominant response is of order of O("1/3), has been proven to be
a very good assumption in the development of nonlinear multimodal theory. It means
that the sloshing response is of lower order at the excitation. Here " is a small parameter
deﬁned by ratio of tank motion and characteristic tank length. If the dominant sloshing
response is assumed as O(epsilon) as it is normally done in marine hydrodynamics when
dealing with external wave-structure interaction, unphysical results will be expected.
Only linear terms with respect to the unknowns ⇣(free surface coordinate) and Φ(velocity
potential) are considered in the formulation of linear sloshing model.

6.2. Limitations of using linear sloshing model
• The assumption for linear case which limits the ship’s motion amplitude to be small
compared to ship’s breadth and draft.
• The linear model can not explain sloshing at resonance. It gives inﬁnite response at the
natural frequency. Refer Appendix.B.1.
• The linear model is a linear summation of each sloshing mode, assuming each individual
mode contribute in the same order. Whereas it has been found that the contribution from
lower modes is more than the higher modes. The linear sloshing model does not take this
into account. The solutions from each modal equations are decoupled.

6.8. Swirling waves in a square based tank
Another phenomenon that is explained by non linear multimodal equation is the observation of
swirling waves in a square based tank. It is very unlikely that the sloshing in a tank can purely
start by a planar excitation. In the real system, there might be a small disturbance present in
lateral direction before a pure planar excitation is given. Based on the operating frequencies
and the natural frequency, the initial disturbance may die out or might even build up cases of
swirling. Swirling as the name suggests is not a planar sloshing phenomenon even if the given
excitation is purely planar.

6.9. Conclusions
• Coupled equations of modal functions - The non linear multimodal analysis at frequencies
close to resonance uses the concept of interaction between the lower modes. This inter-
action between modes is formulated in the multimodal equation in the form of coupled
generalized coordinates β(i,j).
• In case of resonance condition for one mode, there is intermodal transfer of energy which
is possible to explain by coupled ODE formulation. The linear multimodal ODE fails to
give a solution as the solver has no option of transferring the energy to anywhere other
than damping.
• In order to analyse the oscillation of a system, if known before hand that the excitation is
far away from the natural frequency and amplitude of motion also small compared to the
system dimension, linear multimodal solver is a better option without the need to look
into the non linearity
• In a typical ship and ice breaking process it is diﬃcult to estimate if the excitations are
suﬃciently oﬀset from resonance. As in such cases analyzing sloshing in ship’s tank, it is
not only the cross dimensions but the varying ﬁlling ratio of liquid that presents a wide
range of natural frequencies. In such cases, a comparison between the two(linear and non
linear) must be made establishing a clear reason on the selection of the method.
• Even in cases of application of non linear solver, it might be possible that single dominant
theory may fail as seen in Sec.6.7 (Case 2), in such cases an adaptive modal theory must
be used restructuring the order of generalized coordinates and rewriting the set of coupled
modal equation (Eq.6.9-6.18) from the general inﬁnite modal system Eq.6.7. Therefore,
one may not assume that if linear multimodal analysis does not give accurate result,
non linear multimodal analysis should be used without looking into the ordering of the
generalized coordinates. Only if the validity of single dominant modal theory has been
established, the calculation can proceed with the equations mentioned in the chapter.
• Another phenomenon that is explained by non linear multimodal equation is the observa-
tion of swirling waves in a tank with square base. It is very unlikely that the sloshing in
a tank can purely start by a pure planar excitation. In the real system, there might be a
small disturbance present in lateral direction. Based on the operating frequencies and the
natural frequency of tank, the initial disturbance may or may not die out. In such case
where the eﬀect of disturbance exists long after initial disturbance, cases of swirling and
chaos can be observed. Swirling and chaos as the name suggests is not a pure 2D planar
phenomenon even if the given excitation is purely planar. These initial disturbance based
on excitation frequency and ﬁlling ratio might die out as seen in planar wave or build up
a sloshing pattern which is not expected but is very much possible especially in real world
systems where the initial disturbances do exist.

7.1. Introduction
With the sloshing module developed for a 3D rectangular tank capable of handling both lin-
ear and non linear case, it is now used to analyze sloshing in LNG carriers during ice-ship
interaction.
Ice breaking can happen either by crushing the ice at the point of contact or by bending failure
of ice. An ice breaker LNG ship while manoeuvring through ice-sheet experiences diﬀerent
kind of forces on the hull for example reaction force, friction force, buoyancy force from ice
ﬂoes under the hull etc. For a given ice and ship characteristic, the ice breaking process can
be represented as an instantaneous ice force and ship motions using a numerical simulation
model(eg.Su [1]). The instantaneous ship motion during ice breaking acts as an excitation for
sloshing inside the LNG tanks.
The motion proﬁle of ship moving through ice is not similar to that in the open sea. Before
implementing ice-ship interaction in to sloshing model, a brief description is made for the readers
to get an understanding of sea ice, factors aﬀecting ship design, ice breaking mechanics. The
performance of a ship in ice is mutually agreed upon by the owners considering his business and
the shipyard’s capability including its past building experience. Ship’s conditions of operation
and design are documented in the speciﬁcation of the ship.
An example from the Main speciﬁcations of the LNG icebreakers for Yamal LNG in Arctic
includes to support year-round navigation without any icebreaker assistance along westbound
navigation routes, and during summer navigation season - eastbound via the Northern Sea
Route. Source: [5]. Some of the other design features from speciﬁcation for performance in ice
include
• Speed in 1.5 m thick ice - 5.5 knots
• Primary fuel - LNG, with a dual fuel diesel-electric propulsion system equipped with three
"Azipod" units
• Vessel design is based on the double action concept: the bow has been adjusted for
navigation in open water and thin ice, while the stern has been optimized to enable
navigation in severe ice conditions.
The way a ship is operated in the encountered ice determines the ice-ship interaction. The
most severe ice condition might not apply in all cases and for all ships. The ice-ship interaction
works as the base for analyzing the sloshing eﬀect inside tanks of LNG ship. The force on ship
when she hits or navigates through the ice acts as an excitation force for the liquid in tank.

7.3. Numerical method for the prediction of ship
performance in level ice
7.3.1. A brief description
Ship’s performance in level ice is measured by the ice thickness - velocity curve(h-v) which
denotes the velocity a ship can maintain while sailing through diﬀerent ice thickness and running
at full engine power. This h-v curve is usually determined using empirical formula or full-scale
trials and model tests.
Su [1] presented a numerical method to simulate ship manoeuvres
in level ice in the three degree of freedom - surge, sway and yaw considering coupling eﬀect
between ice breaking and ship motions. The numerical simulation model investigates the global
ice load and performance in level ice. The results from numerical methods were veriﬁed against
data collected from ice trials of ice breaking and anchor handling tug Tor Viking II conﬁrming
good agreement. This numerical simulation model is partly based on the empirical data and it
reproduces the continuous ice breaking process. Later the model was extended to more complex
condition of random ice thickness and strength properties.

7.3.2. Working principle behind the numerical method and its
development
The ﬁrst attempt to simulating ice breaking mechanism was done by analyzing the force of an
advancing level ice on a ﬁxed level cone structure. Later this was simulated computationally by
geometric grid method. Using the same approach Su [1] continued the work of continuous ice
breaking mechanics using ice failure method by additionally including coupling of ice breaking
force and ship motion. The inputs include ship’s characteristics and the ice characteristics. In
this method, the waterplane of the hull and ice edge is modelled in to a number of nodes. The
discretized ship hull is modelled as a closed polygon. To ﬁnd the contact between ice and hull,
the program calculates ice nodes which are inside the closed polygon at each time step. Using
discretized water plane the program can calculate the point of contact with ice at each time
step. Also important to mention here is the numerical method does not consider the vertical
motion of the ship during ice breaking as a result heave, pitch and roll’s degree of freedom are
neglected.

7.4. Failure types of ice during ice-ship interaction
After discretization of waterplane, the program next conﬁrms the process by which ice breaking
happens i.e. either by crushing force or bending failure. The landing bow plays a signiﬁcant
role in ice breaking process. Before bending failure occurs in ice, it is assumed that the ice is
crushed locally at the contact surface. The crushing force can be determined by the ice crushing
strength and the contact area.
As the bow moves over the ice, initially ice is crushed locally and when the contact area has
increased enough to generate a suﬃciently large vertical force component to cause a bending
moment in the ice cover that exceeds the bending strength of the ice cover, an ice ﬂoe is created
by formation of a bending crack. The forces acting on ice are the hydrodynamic support force
from water, contact force and frictional force. A typical representation is made in the Fig.7.5.

Based on empirical formulas, the values of force required for ice breaking by crushing or bending
can be calculated and the failure cause can be conﬁrmed. If the vertical component of crushing
force (Fv) is greater than bending failure load (Pf), then bending failure happens, and the ice
wedge is crushed and cleared from ice edge. If the Pf is greater than Fv, no bending failure

time step, continuous ice breaking pattern can be simulated and the time history of ice breaking
force is plotted.

breaking mechanism leads to a substantial increase in resistance as the ship’s hull is constantly
in contact with the ice.

But it did not bring any signiﬁcant eﬀect of non linearity in sloshing.

structure is smaller than inertial force. Sloshing reduces the inertial eﬀect of ship. This
can be loosely understood as a loss of added mass, which may not have a direct impact
on structure but aﬀects the seakeeping capability during ice breaking.

8.1. Conclusion
The present thesis focuses on using multimodal approach to analyze the eﬀect of sloshing in
rectangular 3D tanks. A multimodal solver was develped to handle cases for linear and non-
linear analysis. Veriﬁcation of the multimodal solver has been made for both the cases. To
analyze nonlinear sloshing the solver is able to handle any dimension of tank not restricted to a
rectangular base but also square base which helps explaining the phenomenon of swirling waves.
The solver is capable of handling any kind of motion along any of 6 DOF or a combination of
motion assuming it can be explained using linear or nonlinear multimodal theory. Due to the
structure of code, multiple holds at the same time can be modelled thereby making it resemble
a real LNGC.
The solver as well as non linear multimodal theory assumes inﬁnite tank height and ignores the
eﬀects of wave breaking or wave-roof interaction. It assumes the ﬂoor is always wet.
To this solver the output from ice-ship interaction module is imported to analyze the eﬀect of
sloshing inside tank during continuous ice breaking. The forces on ship hull sets oﬀexcitation
which initiates considerable sloshing inside tank. The sloshing pattern during an ice breaking
is completely non harmonic however as an initial guess on the expected pattern can be guessed
by observing motion proﬁle of ship. For example during the surge only motion, it is obvious to
generate a planar sloshing pattern. However during a turn it was observed that at certain time
instances the sway direction sloshing pattern were more dominant than surge.
The excitation frequency during ice breaking as seen in the numerical simulation model has
been in the range of lower natural modes of the tank. In certain cases involving multiple DOF
a considerable diﬀerence between the two methods (linear & nonlinear) was observed. This

8.2. Future recommendation
• Coupling the eﬀect of motion from the ice-ship interaction module and the multimodal
solver.
The present work has been undertaken ignoring any interaction between ship
motion during ice breaking and ship motion inﬂuenced due to sloshing. However it can
be argued based on the results that a coupled relation between the two modules would give
a much clearer picture to understanding sloshing. Comparison of coupled and decoupled
approach will also tell us if a simpliﬁed decoupled approach could be used in the design
process.
• Coupling the multimodal solver with the latest numerical simulation model that handles
all 6 DOF during ice-ship interaction. The current used numerical simulation model for
ice-ship interaction is based on 3 horizontal DOF - surge, sway and yaw. There has already
been developed a numerical model for handling 6 DOF during ice-breaking. Although
the multimodal sloshing solver has no eﬀect due to heave motion, however the eﬀects due
to pitch and roll are highly likely to inﬂuence sloshing. As it was observed that sway and
yaw greatly aﬀect the surface elevation and also marked signiﬁcant diﬀerence between
linear and non linear approach.
• As it has been revealed that sloshing in tank will reduce the inertial eﬀect of ship. It
will be interesting to know if the resulting ice breaking force will be inﬂuenced which can
aﬀect the local structural design.
• Developing non linear multimodal equations for cases which can not be explained using
the single dominant asymptotic non linear modal theory. As has been observed during
turning of ship, the non linear multimodal solver does not follow the assumptions on
which it was developed. This requires a deeper investigation and proposal of developing
non linear equations that account for higher modes to be of same order and dominant as
the lower ones.
• In the current work a constant ice thickness and properties have been assumed. In reality
they should be considered as random variables considering spatial distribution of strength
properties and thickness.
• Comparing the forces due to sloshing with open water propeller force which can be cal-
culated using empirical methods will give a relative magnitude of sloshing force with ice
breaking and propeller thrust force.
• Analysis of existing icebreaker LNGC and conﬁrm sloshing for varying ﬁlling ratio. In the
thesis a rough dimension of tank was selected to match the numerical simulation model
for ice-ship interaction. Assuming that the hull lines for an ice breaker can be scaled
up to match a real size LNGC and tank size, this would shed more accurate light on the
impact of sloshing during ice breaking. Also to conﬁrm the tank’s eﬀectiveness in sloshing
by conﬁrming the range of natural frequencies due to diﬀerent ﬁlling height. To add up
on this it would be interesting to ﬁnd if the current standards to operation of LNGC
in Arctic restricts hold as partially ﬁlled or not. If no, are the eﬀects due to sloshing
accounted for.
• A study of the mentioned points above will lead to a more realistic LNGC design and
this could be used in the future to operate in the Arctic region.

The wave induced motion of a ship can be related to a mass-spring system with damping. In
a ship, the diﬀerent modes of motion are coupled, for example, heave and pitch, sway, roll and
yaw. However, to represent this in a simple way by considering only one degree of freedom, the
second order diﬀerential equation is represented as
m¨y(t) + b˙y(t) + cy(t) = f(t)
(A.1)
where, y(t) is the response variable here denoting the heave motion of the ship.
m - mass term including ship mass and added mass
b - damping coeﬃcient
c - restoring coeﬃcient
The ﬁnal solution to the Eq.A.1 depends on the value of right hand side of Eq.A.1. If f(t)
is zero, meaning the absence of forcing term, the system of diﬀerential equation is called a
homogeneous equation and represents a free vibrations.
And if f(t) is non-transient 1, steady state solution can be calculated after the decay of free
oscillations. This results in a particular solution which is the sum of both free oscillation and
the forcing term f(t).

λ where the sign of discriminant decides the how the solution decays with time. This introduces
the concept of damping. If there is no damping, the system oscillates indeﬁnitely with no decay
in amplitude. However, in physical systems, damping is inherently present which eventually
results in response to die out.

import numpy as np
from matplotlib import pyplot as plt
from mpl_toolkits.mplot3d import Axes3D

class Tank3D(object):
def __init__(self, length, width, wdepth, g=9.81, rho=1025):
’’’
Constructor
’’’
self.L1 = length
self.L2 = width
self.h = wdepth
self.g = g
self.rho = rho
@property
def mass(self):
return self.rho * self.L1 * self.L2 * self.h
@property
def xyz_lc0(self):
return[0.0 * self.L1, 0.0 * self.L2, -0.5 * self.h]
def discretize(self, nx=10, ny=10, nz=10):
self.xs = np.linspace(-self.L1 / 2, self.L1 / 2, nx)
self.ys = np.linspace(-self.L2 / 2, self.L2 / 2, ny)
self.zs = np.linspace(-self.h, 0., nz)
self.xm, self.ym = np.meshgrid(self.xs, self.ys)
self.zm = np.zeros_like(self.xm, dtype=’float’)
return self.xs, self.ys, self.zs, self.xm, self.ym
def spectral_para(self, imod_L1=1, imod_L2=1):
’’’
imod_L1+imod_L2 != 0
’’’
k_ij = np.pi * np.sqrt((imod_L1 / self.L1) ** 2 + (imod_L2 / self.L2) ** 2)
self.kappa_ij = k_ij * np.tanh(k_ij * self.h)
return self.kappa_ij
def natural_freq(self, imod_L1=1, imod_L2=1):
sigm_ij = np.sqrt(self.g * self.spectral_para(imod_L1=imod_L1, \
imod_L2=imod_L2))
return sigm_ij

- sum_lam1mn_beta_tt_mn
self.forces[istep, 1] = Ml * (-g * d[3] - acc[1] - acc[5] * x0 + acc[3] * z0)
- sum_lam2mn_beta_tt_mn
self.forces[istep, 2] = Ml * (-acc[2] - acc[3] * y0 + acc[4] * x0)
self.forces[istep, 3] = Ml * (g * z0 * d[3] + z0 * acc[1] - y0 * acc[2]) -
self.J011 * acc[3] - Sum_mom_xx
self.forces[istep, 4] = Ml * (g * z0 * d[4] + x0 * acc[2] - z0 * acc[0]) -
self.J022 * acc[4] - Sum_mom_yy #
self.forces[istep, 5] = Ml * (-g * (x0 * d[3] + y0 * d[4]) + y0 * acc[0] - x0
* acc[1]) - self.J033 * acc[5] - sum_lam03mn_beta_tt_mn
return self.forces
