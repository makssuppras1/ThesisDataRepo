ABSTRACT 
 
Concerns about human health and nutrition are leading food companies to produce more 
sustainable and natural ingredients. To achieve this, manufacturers try to substitute animal-
based ingredients with plant-based and synthetic substances with natural ones. 
The main aim of this thesis project was to investigate if emulsifiers’ production from Brewers’ 
Spent Grain (BSG) was possible, using enzymatic hydrolysis to create emulsifying functional 
peptides from proteins present in BSG.  
Six different food-grade barley malts were used in this study, in order to produce BSG fractions 
from each of these. Pilsner, Carafa Typ 3, Caramunich Typ 2, Malt flour, Planet and Laureate malts 
were used as raw materials for the mashing. Once BSG were obtained, the protein content was 
measured and they underwent a Supercritical CO2 Extraction, under different conditions. It was 
attempted to identify if Supercritical CO2 Extraction conditions affect the extraction yield and if 
it is a preferable choice to isolate proteins. It was also investigated if it had any effect in the 
enzymatic hydrolysis of the residual biomass compared to samples without extraction treatment. 
Enzymatic hydrolysis was performed using Alcalase as the preferred proteolytic enzyme. 
5 wt% rapeseed oil in water emulsions were prepared from 0.2 wt% BSG hydrolysates, and the 
physical stability of emulsions was evaluated based on Zeta potential, droplet size distribution 
and visual observation. Zeta potential was measured on Day 1, while droplet size distribution was 
measured on Days 1, 3 and 7.  
Protein content of BSG samples was found to be between 13.2-17%, while Malt flour BSG had 
the highest protein content with a percentage of 17%. The yield of Supercritical CO2 Extraction 
was between 4.2-7.2%, with higher yield observed in samples extracted with co-solvent, 
increased extraction time and co-solvent flow rate. The increase in protein content of the 
samples after Supercritical CO2 Extraction, was not exceeding 1.2%, which indicates that this 
extraction method was not suitable to isolate proteins. 
The yield of hydrolysis was between 69.7% and 71.4% and the degree of hydrolysis was found to 
be 2.01-2.22% for all tested samples. Moreover, it was found that Supercritical CO2 Extraction of 
the samples prior to hydrolysis does not affect the hydrolysis process. Overall, it was possible to 
produce peptides from BSG through enzymatic hydrolysis. 
Finally, Zeta potential showed promising stability (lower than -50 mV). The smallest droplets on 
Day 1 of storage were formed by the Malt flour BSG sample treated with SFE and without use of 
co-solvent (D[3,2]=1.53 μm), while on Day 7 all samples appeared droplet size between 20.5-31.7 
μm. According to the findings, emulsions produced were not stable, based on the droplet size 
and the visual stability observation.

1. INTRODUCTION 
 
Nowadays, there is a growing political and social pressure to reduce industrial pollution. Most of 
the countries are trying to adapt this theory and convert their processes to more sustainable 
solutions in order to recycle their waste. 
Brewing industries produce large amounts of by products and waste such as spent grain. Most of 
these products are agricultural products that can be reused or recycled. Thus, brewing industries 
are characterized as environmentally friendly compared to others. Spent grain is the most 
abundant brewing by-product, around 85% of total by-products that are produced. BSG is 
available at low or no cost throughout the year and is produced in massive quantities not only by 
large, but also by small breweries [1]. 
Barley is the main raw material used to produce beer. The barley grain is divided into three parts: 
the germ (embryo), endosperm (aleurone and starchy endosperm) and grain coverings.  During 
the brewing process the starchy endosperm is under enzymatic degradation and fermentable 
and non-fermentable carbohydrates, soluble proteins, polypeptides and amino acids are created.  
BSG consists of important compounds with respect to health benefits such as fiber and phenolic 
components. The protein fraction of BSG is also important with high content of amino acids and 
lysine. The main current application of BSG is as animal feed, where it can be utilized directly in 
wet form or as dry material [2]. 
Despite the need to reduce industrial pollution, there is also the arising need for commercial 
products produced naturally and in respect of sustainability such as food ingredients and 
cosmetics. Oil in water emulsions is a significant part of producing food ingredients and other 
personal care products. In order to produce these products with a long self-life it is mandatory 
to use stabilizers such as emulsifiers which are important to form stable emulsions. Most of the 
emulsifiers are synthetic or animal based such as egg protein and whey protein. However, 
because of the need for more natural and environmentally friendly products, many industries try 
to convert synthetic surfactants with natural alternatives or replace the animal proteins with 
plant proteins [3]. 
Previous studies about the isolation of proteins of BSG and the production of emulsifiers varies. 
Different methods have been used in order to isolate proteins from BSG. The main extraction 
methods are alkali-soluble acid precipitation, organic solvent extraction, and ultrasonic-assisted 
extraction.   
Different studies showed that BSG from beer extracted by alkali-soluble acid precipitation 
method are rich in glutamine/glutamic acid, valine and leucine. Also, proteins from BSG 
compared to those from barley have a similar amino acid profile and common utilities. Qin, 
Johansen, and Mussatto (2018) concluded that from all pretreatment methods of extraction, the 
highest protein extraction rate (95%) was acquired by continuous alkaline and dilute acid 
==PAGE:6==
2 
 
pretreatment, followed by dilute acid pretreatment (90%) and hydrothermal pretreatment (64-
66%). Some other studies have shown that ultrasound extraction methods improved the yield 
and the properties of proteins. All the extraction methods have advantages and disadvantages, 
for instance the most common extraction method alkali dissolution acid precipitation has high 
extraction rate, simple operation, and low cost. However, high alkali concentration also affects 
the nutritional properties of the protein causing the Maillard reaction.  
Some researchers reported that the solubility of BSG proteins is low and there are not enough 
studies around the functional properties of BSG proteins. A few research focused on the 
enzymatic treatment of BSG proteins to produce hydrolysates, in order to investigate their 
emulsifying and foaming properties, solubility, turbidity, oil, and water absorption capacity [4]. 
Application of enzymatic hydrolysis is considered an environmentally friendly treatment to 
produce food ingredients.[5] Other studies aimed at fractionation of non-prolamin and prolamin 
proteins from wheat beer BSG, and explored their emulsification potential [6] and the 
combination of quantitative proteomics, bioinformatics prediction, and functional validation to 
identify novel emulsifier peptides from seaweed, methanotrophic bacteria, and potatoes [7]. 
Based on previous studies, this project aims to develop functional emulsifying peptides from BSG 
proteins, by involving combination of processes that have not been applied before and evaluate 
their properties. 
 
1.2 Project aims 
 
The present study mainly deals with the production of valuable substances from BSG, the beer 
brewing process wastes. These processed malted barley grains will be produced and will undergo 
multiple process steps to conclude whether the aims of the project were achievable.  
The main aim of the project is to investigate if emulsifiers’ production from BSG is possible, using 
enzymatic hydrolysis of the waste proteins to create emulsifying functional peptides. Emulsions 
will be prepared, and the properties of the emulsions will be evaluated.  
The sub aims are: 
a) To observe and evaluate the mashing performance of the different malts used and 
analyze the created fractions. 
b) To compare different Supercritical CO2 Extraction conditions for lipid extraction to each 
other in terms of efficiency. 
c) To investigate if Supercritical CO2 Extraction is considered as a justified treatment to 
concentrate the protein content of the samples, prior to enzymatic hydrolysis and if the 
specific extraction method affects the hydrolysis behavior.

1.3 Barley 
 
The quality of raw materials is of major importance to the quality of beer. Barley is the main 
ingredient used for beer production because of the high starch content but first has to undergo 
a malting process.  Europe is the greatest region in cultivation of barley [8]. 
Barley is one of the most important cereals. It is fifth in the harvest rank behind maize, wheat, 
rice and soybean. Barley was one of the first agriculture from 10000 years ago in the Middle East. 
It was first used as a human feed as well as for brewing and malting. One of the greatest 
advantages of barley is its health benefits, as barley β-glucans lower the blood cholesterol and 
glycemic index. Barley is also rich in tocopherols and tocotrienols which are well known for 
decreasing LDL cholesterol through its antioxidant activity. Barley can grow in different climates 
and is categorized as spring or winter type agriculture. Unpeeled barley is preferred to peeled for 
malting and brewing because peels work as a filter in brewing, maintaining the flavor of beer.

1.4 Beer Brewing 
 
Beer production is divided into the following biochemical processes: The development of 
enzymes in germination process, the breakdown of starch to sugar by those enzymes and the 
fermentation process from sugar to alcohol and CO2. These processes have been known for 
thousands of years. In Europe, beer was a drink produced in households for personal usage. It 
turned to the brewing industry in monasteries and nunneries that brewed not only for personal 
consumption, but as a method to earn profit. The main ingredients for beer brewing according 
to the law are water, malt, hops and yeast.  
One of the most important parts for beer production is to convert the insoluble components of 
malt to soluble. The development of these components is the goal of wort production. In the 
beginning of the process, malt is transferred from storage room to the mill in order to be grinded 
in suitable size. This is called milling step. In the next stage, the grounded malt is transferred to 
mashing tanks in order to receive all the soluble components. To separate the soluble extract 
from the insoluble the mash is transferred to a lauter tank. This stage is called lautering. Then the 
wort is boiled with hops to develop the flavor. Finally, the last stage is the fermentation of sugars 
which is considered the most important stage of beer production because of the creation of 
alcohol and CO2 [8]. 
Malting 
The first step of the production is to convert the barley to malt. Barley produces the most proper 
malt for beer production. The malting step involves enzymes production that break down starch 
to sugar. The malting process is divided into three stages: steeping, germination and kilning. 
 
 
==PAGE:9==
5 
 
Steeping 
 The steeping process must be done in order to increase the moisture content of barley from 
~12% to ~40% to initiate germination, as well as to clean the grains from germination inhibitors 
and remove other floating substances by skimming. Enzyme development is faster at higher 
moisture levels. The germination process starts after the moisture content reaches 30%. Water 
uptake happens under proper temperature conditions (12-18°C). 
Germination 
After the steeping process the grains are transferred in vessels and exposed in air temperature 
called falling temperature program (18°C at the beginning to 13°C at the end) that improves 
quality. The aim of the germination process is to control the breakdown of cell walls and matrix 
proteins, to produce hydrolytic enzymes as well as hydrolyze protein in order to create free 
amino nitrogen and the production of malt for kilning. All these aims can be achieved in proper 
conditions of time, temperature, ideal steeping degree and O2: CO2 ratio. The breakdown of the 
cell walls and the matrix proteins, as well as the production of enzymes are essential for the 
mashing process. 
Kilning  
The malt is transferred to the kiln. The aim of the kilning is to decrease the moisture content of 
grains in order to store them properly, maintain the enzymes that developed in the malting 
process, develop color and flavor characteristics and finally, to stabilize the modification of the 
structure of the endosperm. The temperature for low initial drying temperatures is about 50°C 
and ends at around 65°C. For the kilning process the usual time and temperature is 5 hours at 
80°C, but the same quality can be obtained by applying shorter time of 2 to 3 hours at 85°C. The 
color and the flavor are outcomes of Maillard reaction [11]. Enzyme activity and free amino 
nitrogen levels decrease at higher kilning temperatures. Higher final kilning temperatures are 
used in dark malts, and this leads to less functional peptides ending up in the BSG compared to 
lighter malts [12]. 
Milling 
The aim of the milling step is to give the chance to malt enzymes at the mashing stage to react 
with the malt components and break them down. To make this happen the malt has to be 
grounded in small particles. In this mechanical process the husks should be treated properly 
because husks act like a filter later in the lautering stage. Before the process, the malt should be 
cleaned from dust, stones, iron and other particles to avoid damaging the mill [8]. 
Mashing 
Mashing stage is the most important stage of wort production. In this stage water is mixed with 
malt and with the help of enzymes the extract is obtained. As already mentioned, the aim of the 
mashing is to convert all the insoluble components (starch, cellulose) to soluble (sugars, dextrins, 
==PAGE:10==
6 
 
proteins) because only these components can be transferred to the beer. The majority of water-
soluble proteins and peptides are extracted into the wort, while BSG holds only small amounts 
of these substances. In other words, the goal of mashing is the degradation of starch to sugars 
and soluble dextrins. As mentioned, to obtain the extract the enzymatic activity is really 
important and depends on various conditions such as temperature, which is the most significant 
and pH. Enzymatic activity rises at higher temperatures and gives its higher value when the pH is 
optimal. Another important factor for the brewing is the degradation processes like starch 
degradation, β-glucan degradation and protein degradation. In starch degradation starch is 
degraded to maltose in order to obtain the alcohol in the beer. Starch degradation consists of 3 
stages, gelatinization where flatulence and bursting of starch granules are taken place in hot 
water. The temperature of gelatinization differs for each cereal type. Malt and barley starch 
gelatinized at 60°C, on the other hand, rice starch gelatinized at 80 to 85°C. Another stage of 
starch degradation is liquefaction where the starch absorbs water, and the process is 
enzymatically driven by alpha-amylase. It is a slow process in ungelatinized starch but occurs 
rapidly in gelatinized starch; therefore, liquefaction cannot occur effectively in mashing until the 
starch is gelatinized. The last stage of starch degradation is saccharification which is the 
conversion of starches by enzymes into sugars and dextrins during the massing process. The 
greater enzyme activity is achieved after 10 to 20 minutes at 62-63°C where the maltose content 
increases. Also, the optimum pH during mashing must be 5.6 to 5.8. 
Optimum temperatures for enzyme activity: 
 45 to 50°C protein and β-glucanase rest 
 62 to 65°C maltose production rest 
 70 to 75°C saccharification rest 
 75 to 78°C final mash temperature [8] 
Lautering 
Lautering is a filtration step and the malt residues (spent grains) act as a filter in order to obtain 
the first and the second wort (sparging). The wort that passes through the spent grain is called 
first wort. After receiving the first wort, the sparging stage follows. The extract that preserved in 
spent grain washed by hot water in order to take the second wort. The most important condition 
in this process is the temperature of the water, the higher the temperature, the viscosity of the 
liquid decreases. The optimum temperature for lautering is 100°C. The separation of wort from 
spent grains is achieved by mashing filters [8]. 
Boiling 
The wort is boiled for about 60 minutes. In this process hops are added, so the aroma and flavor 
substances are transferred into the wort and at the same time proteins are precipitated. 
In the boiling process different reactions are taking place such as: Formation and precipitation of 
protein-polyphenol compounds, color and aroma development, evaporation of water, wort 
==PAGE:11==
7 
 
sterilization, the inactivation of enzymes which is important (otherwise the result would be over 
fermented beer), evaporation of undesirable aroma substances. In the boiling stage due to 
Maillard reaction at 80°C wort develops new aroma substances [8, 11]. 
Fermentation 
The most important process in beer production is the fermentation of the sugars in the wort to 
ethanol and CO2 by the yeast. The process starts by adding yeast to the wort. In the fermentation 
process fermentation by-products are also produced (esters, organic acids etc.) which are 
responsible for aroma and taste substances. The aim of the fermentation is to create a stable 
beer quality with a long shelf-life. Stable fermentation times and desired degree of fermentation 
lead to strong extract degradation, pH drop and maintenance of analysis parameters.  
After the fermentation process, maturation and filtration processes follow. Regularly, maturation 
and fermentation take place in the same vessel. The filtration of beer makes it biologically stable. 
After these processes the beer is ready for bottling [8, 11]. 
  
1.5 Brewer’s Spent Grain (BSG)   
 
BSG is the major by-product which is produced from beer production. The BSG is produced after 
mashing procedure where the wort is filtered through BSG and goes for fermentation. The yearly 
amount of BSG is estimated at 39 million tons globally. Nowadays, BSG is used for animal feed 
with low market value. BSG consists of a high moisture level and that can cause the spoilage of 
the by-product [2]. BSG includes seed coat-pericarp and husk layers, the starch content is low 
and BSG can be characterized as a heterogeneous substance. BSG is rich in sugars, proteins and 
minerals. The composition may vary due to the variety of factors depending on malt, water and 
the harvest period. As it can be seen in Table 2, carbohydrates are the dominant components of 
BSG, followed by proteins (14.2-24.7%). Another important component of BSG is lignin holding 
rates of 11-28% of total dry weight of BSG. Lignin is significant in conserving the plant cell walls, 
while there are also considerable amounts of lipids (6-13%) [2]. Proteins and lipids content are 
the most relevant values for the present study. Protein range varies based mainly on the malt 
used for brewing, making it impossible to average the content, since the range differences are 
wide [1]. 
As mentioned above, BSG is used as animal feed. However, because of its composition, 
availability and low market value it has been investigated for further applications in human feed 
with the possibility for health benefits and other applications in the food industry. Also because 
of the current energy crisis, BSG is proposed to be applied as an alternative ingredient to produce 
energy including thermochemical conversion, biogas and ethanol production [1].

Proteins 
The most significant proteins in BSG are hordeins, glutelins, globulins and albumins. Essential 
amino acids hold almost 30% of the total protein content. Proteins are the second largest 
component in BSG after carbohydrates, depending on mashing or malting process. Despite the 
degradation of proteins and after solubilization of starch, the protein content of BSG increases 
compared to barley grain. Albumins and globulins are extracted to the wort, and they make up 
7.5% of total protein content. Glutelins represent 21.5% and hordeins the 43% of total protein 
content. BSG is also a source of essential amino acids methionine, phenylalanine, tryptophan, 
histidine and lysine and nonessential amino acids like serine, alanine and glycine [14]. 
Minerals and Vitamins  
 
The most abundant minerals in BSG are calcium, magnesium, phosphorus and sodium. The most 
abundant Vitamins in BSG are Folic acid, Biotin, Niacin. Thiamine and Choline. Most of the studies 
mentioned  tocochromanol vitamins that include tocotrienols and tocopherols, most known as 
vitamin E. Tococromanols are responsible for the decrease of LDL Cholesterol and act as 
anticarcinogenic factor [14].

Phenolic profile  
Phenolic acids, like hydroxybenzoic acids, and hydroxycinnamic acids, are plant secondary 
metabolites present largely in plant foods. Phenolic acids have anticarcinogenic, anti-
inflammatory, and antioxidant properties [1]. 
BSG is a significant source of phenolic acids since husks contain abundant amounts of these 
compounds. There is a high concentration in p-coumaric and ferulic acids around 565-794 μg/g 
and 1860-1948 μg/g respectively. It is also proved from different studies that p-coumaric and 
ferulic acids are included in antioxidants. Various acids are contained in spent grains like catechin, 
quinic, gallic and syringic acids but the phenolic content in BSG depends on the different species 
of malt [14].

2. BSG PRODUCTION 
 
Six mashing processes were performed in this stage, using six different malts. Two main fractions 
were obtained after the mashing. A BSG and a wort fraction. Both fractions were tested and the 
whole mashing process was monitored and evaluated. BSG production and their properties with 
a focus on the protein content was the target.  
Production of BSG is the main target of the mashing process. With the use of specific methods, it 
was attempted to create a composition profile of the produced BSG focused on the proteins. 
Moisture and ash content were also measured. 
Multiple properties of the first and second wort were investigated, with focus on the second 
wort. The second wort is the material feeding the rest of the process of beer brewing, after the 
sparging stage that washes out remaining soluble substances from the BSG. The purpose of 
testing the worts is to create a profile of the mashing performance in connection with the BSG 
analysis results. The properties selected to be analyzed will provide information about physical 
properties like sugars concentration, color and specific gravity that can help evaluate the mashing 
process and compare the expected results with the actual wherever was possible.   
Below a brief introduction to the methods used is available. 
 
2.1 Materials and methods 
  
Mashing 
Six different food-grade barley malts were used in this study, in order to produce wort and BSG 
fractions from each of these. Malt flour, Planet and Laureate malts were obtained by Viking Malt 
A/S, Denmark. Malt flour was barley malt, germinated for 10-12 days, dried, roasted and then 
ground into flour. Pilsner, Carafa Typ 3 and Caramunich Typ 2 malts were obtained by 
Weyermann Specialty Malts, Germany. All malt samples were produced in 2021.  
The target of this step is to produce BSG fractions from all six malts. For this reason, the beer 
brewing process was terminated after the sparging stage. In Figure 2 the BSG and wort 
production flow diagram is presented. Red blocks represent analysis in the referred fractions. 
  
 
==PAGE:15==
11 
 
 
 
Figure 2. BSG and wort production flow diagram. 
 
At first, malt samples were milled using a Buhler Universal Laboratory Disk Mill DLFU, 230 V, set 
to 2 mm disks distance. Mashing stage followed, using a laboratory scale Lochner Labor + Technik 
GmbH mashing apparatus (Figure 3). For each malt, four mashing procedures were run 
simultaneously under the same conditions to ensure higher accuracy and repeatability of the 
produced samples. 100 g of milled malt and 400 mL of water were added in each of the four 
vessels. The pH of the added water was adjusted to 5.6 with the use of citric acid to avoid poor 
enzymatic conversion of the mash. The temperature-time mashing profile followed in this stage 
was: 10 minutes at 50oC (optimal protein breakdown at 45-50oC and β-glucanase at 45oC), 60 
minutes at 65oC to break down β-amylase, 5 minutes at 72oC to break down α-amylase and finally 
5 minutes at 78oC to deactivate α-amylase before the mash is cooled down to 20oC. As 
mentioned, six different mashings took place. Four of them were performed with single malt 
==PAGE:16==
12 
 
samples (Pilsner, Planet, Laureate and Malt flour) and two more were combinations of malts 
(95% Pilsner + 5% Carafa Typ 3 and 90% Pilsner + 10% Caramunich Typ 2). Malt mixing ratios for 
the combination malts were based upon the producer recommendations.  
  
 
Figure 3. Lochner Labor + Technik GmbH mashing apparatus. [15] 
  
When the mashing was over and the mash was cooled down to 20oC, the filtering stage followed. 
Mashes were filtered using Whatman folded filter papers with a diameter of 320 mm [16]. At this 
point the first wort was collected. The filtration time in order to receive the first wort was over 2 
hours for all samples, which according to EBC is characterized as “slow filtration”. BSG samples 
were then sparged three times with 100 mL of water at 78oC, to wash out any remains of soluble 
substances from the BSG. The filtrate after the sparging stage was the collected second wort.  
The mash obtained after the Malt flour mashing was centrifuged instead of filtered, because of 
its state. A Heraeus Multifuge X3R was used, set at 3500 RCF, 4oC for 15 minutes. After 
centrifugation, two fractions were collected. The solid fraction was the BSG and the liquid one 
was the wort. Given the fact that sparging in this sample was not feasible, the only wort collected 
was considered as second wort, since this centrifugal force is considered powerful enough to 
force migration of soluble substances to the liquid phase replacing sparging as much as possible. 
First and second wort samples from all mashings were collected in 50 mL sample vessels and 
stored in freezer at -20oC for further analysis. BSG samples were freeze dried to 8-10% moisture 
content using a Heto PowerDry DW8 freeze dryer, before been pulverized and sieved to 0.5 mm 
==PAGE:17==
13 
 
particle size using a Buhler Universal Laboratory Sifter. Finally, the samples were collected in 50 
mL sample vessels and stored in freezer at -20oC for further analysis, too.  
pH 
Measuring pH in beer wort was performed using a Hanna Checker - pH tester (HI98103). PH 
values affect not only organoleptic properties like foaming and flavor but is also a quality 
indicator providing information about amylolytic, cytolytic and proteolytic enzymes activity. PH 
also affects wort composition and microbiological stability of the final product. Milling, mashing 
temperatures and water properties used for this stage can provoke changes to pH [17]. 
Measurements were performed in biological replicates (n=4).  
EBC  
Color of beer defined by the European Brewery Convention (EBC). This is a quantitative method 
that involves measuring the absorbance of the samples at a wavelength of 430. Color is calculated 
using the following formula:  
EBC = 25 · D · A 
Where D = dilution factor of the sample and A= the light absorbance at 430 nm in 10mm cell 
tube.  
A Thermo Scientific GENESYS 10S UV-Vis spectrophotometer was used [16]. Measurements were 
performed in biological replicates (n=4). 
Specific gravity 
Specific gravity (SG) is a measurement of the density of the wort. In fact, it is a corrected density 
divided with the water density at 20oC. This density can also be expressed as Plato degrees (°P), 
but the relation between them is not linear. One of the equations describing their relation is the 
following [18]: 
o Plato = -463.37 + 668.72 · SG - 205.35 · SG2 
Specific gravity was measured as a mashing stability indicator. An Anton Paar Density meter DMA 
35 was used [19]. Measurements were performed in biological replicates (n=4). 
Plato  
Plato is an indicator of the concentration of dissolved solids in brewing wort. It is a measure of 
extract concentration (mainly sucrose from malt but also other soluble materials in wort) 
measured in degrees Plato (°P). It was measured by an Anton Paar Density meter DMA 35 [20]. 
Measurements were performed in biological replicates (n=4). 
Sugars (Maltose and Glucose)  
By using HPLC, it is possible to directly determine oligosaccharides. Therefore, HPLC is a bright 
method for sugar analysis due to its accuracy for determining carbohydrates. Researchers 
==PAGE:18==
14 
 
mentioned that HPLC is an established method to measure sugars in a carbohydrate mixture 
because it is accurate and simple [21].  
Separating and identifying components in a mixture can be accomplished with high-performance 
liquid chromatography. A liquid solvent is passed through a column containing sorbent material. 
When the solvent passes through the sorbent material, components of the samples react with it 
and as a result give a variety of flow rates. Consequently, the components separate. This method 
has been widely used for the analysis of sugars.  
For this study, all tested samples were diluted 10 times and a standard curve was created, using 
a 20 g/L maltose and glucose standard solution. Analysis was conducted with the use of a Thermo 
Scientific DIONEX UltiMATE 3000 system [22, 23]. Measurements were performed in biological 
duplicates. 
Protein content - DUMAS 
DUMAS analytical method was used for the estimation of protein content of BSG by the 
determination of total nitrogen content. The use of DUMAS analysis was performed with an 
Elementar rapid MAX N Exceed and the protocol can be seen in Appendix A2.  
By using this method, the protein content of the sample is calculated by multiplying the detected 
nitrogen content with a conversion factor in order to convert the nitrogen into protein. This 
factor varies depending on the raw material tested. The protein content expressed as percentage 
of total nitrogen. During the analysis, an auto sampler transports the sample into the combustion 
tube, which burns the sample completely in an oxygen-rich atmosphere. A typical combustion 
temperature is 800°C. For the calculation 0.25 g of each dried sample was put in the crucible and 
“cereals” method with a protein conversion factor of 5.83 was used. Measurements were 
performed in biological duplicates. 
Moisture content 
Moisture content was measured with the use of an oven. Samples were heated to 100oC for 24 
hours and were weighted before and after drying. The loss of weight was used to calculate the 
moisture content of the samples. Measurements were performed in biological duplicates. 
Ash content 
Ash refers to the inorganic residue remaining after oxidation of organic matter in a foodstuff. The 
ash content in foods represents the total mineral content. Ash content is important to determine 
for several reasons. In order to prepare a food sample for specific elemental analysis, ash analysis 
is the first step. The ash content of certain foods is significant because it indicates levels of 
particular minerals. The sample was combusted at 500°C for 8 hours in an oven to determine the 
amount of ash in the sample. After cooling to 35°C, the sample was placed in a desiccator until it 
was weighed [24]. Measurements were performed in biological duplicates.

3. SUPERCRITICAL CO2 EXTRACTION 
  
In this experimental phase, samples underwent a lipid extraction. It was attempted to identify if 
SFE conditions affect the extraction yield and if it is a preferable choice to isolate proteins. Later, 
it will also be investigated if the pretreatment of the BSGs with Supercritical CO2 Extraction will 
have any influence on the protein functionality when followed by enzymatic hydrolysis, 
compared to samples without extraction treatment.  
More efficient and environmentally friendly technologies have been utilized in order to extract 
valuable compounds from various materials intended to be used in food, cosmetic and 
pharmaceutical products. Among other methods, Supercritical Fluid Extraction (SFE) is under 
research for extracting such components from BSG. One of the main advantages of SFE is that 
small pressure and temperature changes, within the critical range, can provoke remarkable 
changes in solubility and density of the solvent. This makes SFE flexible while holding high 
selectivity when compared to other widely used extraction methods [25]. Supercritical CO2 
Extraction utilizes carbon dioxide (CO2) as a solvent. With a critical temperature of 31°C and a 
critical pressure of 74 bars, CO2 is an exceptional solvent for SFE. These low critical conditions 
allow extraction at relatively high pressures, increasing efficiency [26]. CO2 is a cheap, abundant 
and environmentally safe gas according to FDA and EFSA. Moreover, it does not affect either the 
extracts or the residual biomass, can be easily separated from the extract and can be potentially 
recycled. The main characteristic of CO2 as a solvent is its low polarity that makes it suitable for 
extraction of non-polar substances like lipids, but not for polar ones. For that reason, co-solvent 
is used in order to enhance the efficiency of the extraction. The best co-solvent due to reaction 
with CO2 is ethanol. It is also well known that ethanol is non-toxic for food products [27]. 
In Figure 6 a typical SFE set-up is presented. The main parts are a CO2 source, a pump, an oven 
containing the extraction vessels and an analyte-collection unit in which the extracts are 
collected in vessels as well. First, liquid CO2 flows into the system with the help of a pump. A heat 
exchanger is utilized to raise the temperature of the liquid above its critical temperature and at 
the same time, the pressure increases above its critical pressure stated above. Then, the 
supercritical fluid passes through the extraction vessels that are loaded with sample and the 
mixture of CO2 and extract is collected as an output in the collection vessels. Extraction vessels 
are equipped with filters on both ends of the vessel to prevent sample biomass migration from 
the extraction to the collection vessels, spoiling the extract. After extraction is complete, the 
supercritical liquid changes phase to gas because of depressurization. This drop of pressure leads 
the gas to be released in the environment, while the extract is purified from the solvent. In case 
of co-solvent use, another pump helps the flow of the co-solvent through the extraction vessel, 
and it ends up in the collection vessel. Solvent and co-solvent flow rates are set from the user 
and so is the time. SFE can be performed in different modes. Dynamic, static or a combination of 
both. In the dynamic mode, supercritical fluid flow remains stable in the extraction oven, while 
in static mode, flow, pressure and temperature are dropping. A combination of the two modes is 
reported to increase efficiency [28]. 
==PAGE:25==

Figure 6. Supercritical CO2 Extraction set-up. 
  
A thorough literature search was performed in order to conclude on what conditions should be 
tried to extract lipids from BSG [27, 29, 30, 31, 32]. The important condition in this stage is the 
combination of pressure and temperature. When it comes to the co-solvent, ethanol (70%) was 
used to boost the extraction. Theoretically, the higher the flowrates, the concentration of the co-
solvent and the extraction time, the more efficient the extraction is. The goal is to purify the 
residue after SFE, by removing lipids and potentially increase the protein content. Most of the 
studies have shown that SFE for BSG lipids extraction, performed on different pressure conditions 
of 150, 200 and 350 bars and temperatures from 30 to 60oC, as well the use or not of co-solvent. 
The extractor used for this stage was a laboratory scale that can handle conditions up to 300 bars. 
For this reason, the sets of conditions used for the extraction were chosen to involve pressures 
lower than 300 bars. 
 
3.1 Materials and methods 
 
Supercritical CO2 Extraction 
To perform the extraction, the BSG samples (Malt flour and Planet malt BSG) were first grinded 
to particle size <0.5 mm and 7.5 g of sample were weighted in the extraction vessel. The 
experiment was divided into 2 different stages. In the first one Planet BSG samples were used in 
two different sets of conditions to monitor the extraction and use the results as an indicator for 
adjusted settings for the next stage. In the next stage, Malt flour BSG samples were put under 
extraction with adjusted conditions and a comparison between the findings was conducted. 
==PAGE:26==
22 
 
For Planet malt BSG, the extraction was performed under two sets of conditions in order to 
extract lipids. Both were set to the same temperature (33oC) and pressure (177 bar) [33], but in 
different extraction time and either CO2 or CO2 with ethanol (70%) as a co-solvent. CO2 flow of 
10 mL/min and ethanol flow rate of 2mL/min were applied. For the sample without co-solvent, a 
make-up flow of 0.5 mL/min ethanol 70% was used. The extraction time was set to 90 minutes 
(80 min of dynamic phase + 10 min of static phase) for the sample extracted with co-solvent and 
50 minutes (40 min of dynamic phase + 10 min of static phase) for the sample extracted without 
co-solvent. The extractor used was a Waters Supercritical Fluid Extraction chromatography 
system MV-10 ASFE®. After the extraction, the samples were collected, weighed and stored for 
further analysis. The samples that were extracted with co-solvent were dried using Nitrogen at 
25oC to get rid of the residual ethanol. 
Protein content - DUMAS 
After extraction all samples’ residues were tested for protein content (DUMAS). The method was 
performed as described in Chapter 3.1. Measurements were performed in biological duplicates.

4. ENZYMATIC HYDROLYSIS 
 
The aim of this chapter was to hydrolyze selected samples from the previous experimental phase 
(untreated and SFE treated Malt flour BSG) in order to produce functional peptides. Enzymatic 
hydrolysis was performed with the use of Alcalase. 
Enzymatic hydrolysis is a valuable process in order to improve the properties of the protein 
without damaging the nutritional value. Protein hydrolysates have a great range of applications 
and are widely used as food ingredients. The functionalities of peptides differ depending on 
molecular size, structure and amino acid sequence. The most important parameter to describe 
the range of hydrolysis as well as the properties of hydrolysates is the degree of hydrolysis (DH) 
which presents the percentage of broken peptide bonds. Plant proteins have been used 
increasingly to perform new functional sources in the food industry, as the amino acids 
composition of plant proteins is suitable for human consumption [36]. By choosing the right 
proteolytic enzyme, the environmental conditions and the degree of hydrolysis, the 
improvement of functionality of protein properties can be achieved. Factors that can influence 
the functional properties of proteins can be characterized as internal or external. Factors that 
can affect enzymatic hydrolysis can be classified as enzyme specificity, extent of protein 
denaturation, substrate and enzyme concentrations, pH, ionic strength, temperature and 
absence or presence of inhibitory substances. The enzyme specificity is a significant factor 
affecting the number and location of peptide bonds that are hydrolyzed.  As proteolysis proceeds, 
either one peptide is released sequentially, or intermediate compounds are formed that are 
further hydrolyzed to smaller peptides. The degree of proteolysis is captured by the degree of 
hydrolysis (DH) [34]. 
In this project Alcalase is the preferred proteolytic enzyme. Alcalase is a food grade enzyme which 
is often used because of its price. It is a serine alkaline protease produced by a selected strain of 
B. licheniformis. Its main enzyme component, subtilisin was prepared and crystallized for the first 
time by Guntelberg and Ottesen. Subtilisin consists of a single peptide chain of 274 amino acid 
residues with no disulfide bonds, and it has a molecular mass of 27277 Da with an isoelectric 
point of 9.4. The enzyme has broad specificity, hydrolyzing most peptide bonds, preferentially 
those containing aromatic amino acid residues. The enzyme has a pH optimum for activity 
between pH 8 and 9 and broad pH stability, being inactivated quickly below pH 5.0 and above pH 
11.0 [37]. Sukan and Andrews have observed that a greater degree of hydrolysis could be 
achieved with Alcalase as compared to other enzymes such as pepsin and chymotrypsin [38]. 
Also, in other reports it has been observed that hydrolysates occurred with the use of  Alcalase 
as an enzyme have better water and oil adsorption capacity as well as foaming and emulsifying 
properties [36].

solution was used instead of 150 mL. The added enzyme (Alcalase 2.4 L FG) was calculated

solution. Samples analyzed were untreated Malt flour BSG, Malt flour extracted with co-solvent 
and Malt flour BSG extracted without co-solvent. Whey protein samples were hydrolyzed in 
duplicates as reference samples to control and verify the method. Calculations can be seen in 
Appendix A5. 
1% protein solutions for all samples were prepared and heated to 50oC in a Julabo SW22, water 
bath, set at 150 rpm. The solutions’ pH was adjusted to 8, using 0.1 M NaOH, for the optimum 
activity of Alcalase. Then, the enzyme was added to volume equivalent to 1% E/S of Alcalase (8.55 
μL) and hydrolysis process began. After 2 hours, the solutions were heated to 90oC for 15 minutes 
in another similar water bath, set at 150 rpm as well, before cooled down to room temperature. 
Finally, the samples were centrifuged at 6700 g and 4oC for 20 minutes, using a Thermo Scientific 
Sorval RC 6 Plus centrifuge. Supernatants were collected in 14 mL sampling tubes and analyzed 
for protein content. Hydrolysis was performed in duplicates for all samples, including the 
reference samples. 
Protein content-DUMAS 
After hydrolysis all samples’ supernatants were tested for protein content (DUMAS). The method 
was performed as described in Chapter 3.1, except for the “beer” method was used this time and 
the sample weight was 2 g. Measurements were done in biological duplicates.

5. EMULSION PRODUCTION 
 
Emulsion production was the final step of the experimental flow for this project. Hydrolysates 
obtained from the previous enzymatic hydrolysis stage were used to stabilize 5% oil in water 
(O/W) emulsions. The stability of these emulsions will be evaluated through Zeta potential, 
droplet size and distribution measurements and visual observation. 
Emulsions are examples of metastable colloids. They consist of two immiscible fluids, one being 
dispersed in the other, with surface-active agents present. Fragmenting one phase into another 
is a result of shearing two immiscible fluids. A simple direct emulsion is composed of oil droplets 
dispersed in water (O/W), whereas an inverse emulsion is composed of water droplets dispersed 
in a continuous oil phase (W/O) [41]. Emulsions are present in many food products such as 
mayonnaise, milk, butter, salad dressing and margarine [42]. Emulsions are characterized as 
thermodynamically unstable, and they tend to breakdown due to different physicochemical 
mechanisms. In order to improve their long-term stability stabilizers and emulsifiers are included 
in emulsion formulation [43]. 
Depending on the droplet size of emulsions they are divided into 3 categories: micro-emulsions 
(10–100 nm), mini (nano)-emulsions (100–1000 nm), and macro-emulsions (0.5–100 μm). The 
properties of different emulsions are presented in Table 11. Emulsion droplet size plays a key 
role in many emulsion properties such as stability, color, appearance, texture, and rheology [44].

Among the most important stabilizers in any product is the emulsifier used in the emulsion 
formulation. Apart from stabilizing emulsions, they offer several additional advantages. The type 
of emulsifier used also has an impact on the ease of emulsion, the final product's formation and 
==PAGE:37==
33 
 
functional attributes. Emulsifiers are typically amphiphilic molecules with both hydrophilic and 
hydrophobic groups.  
There are different types of emulsifiers depending on their molecular and physicochemical 
properties. Emulsifiers divided into four types (Figure 12):  
1. Low-molecular-weight emulsifiers or surfactants: This type of emulsifier includes small 
molecules from 250g/mol to 1,200 g/mol. They can be synthetic or natural. They are 
characterized by a non-polar tail and a polar head, while some surfactants have different 
structures of hydrophobic and hydrophilic sides. 
2. Phospholipids: They are small molecule surfactants coming from the cell membranes of 
plants and animals. Phospholipids are made of a glycerol moiety with a phosphate group 
usually located at the sn-3 position, and two fatty acid chains located at the sn-1 and sn-
2 positions. They are usually used in the food and beverage industry. The functionality of 
phospholipids as an emulsifier depends on the nature of the head and tail.  
3. Proteins: Most proteins are amphiphilic with polar and non-polar heads in their surface 
such as caseins, egg proteins, soy proteins, gelatin and meat proteins. Proteins as an ideal 
emulsifier should have a good balance of polar and non-polar groups in order to develop 
ideal water solubility and surface activity.  
4.  Polysaccharides: Used in the food industry, they are hydrophilic molecules with low 
surface activity that makes them not appropriate as emulsifiers [43]. Polysaccharide 
emulsifiers can be either natural or synthetic.

The process that is conducted in order to convert two immiscible liquids to an emulsion or to 
reduce the size of the droplets in a former emulsion is called homogenization. Such processes in 
the food industry are performed by mechanical devices called homogenizers including high speed 
blenders, high pressure valve homogenizers and colloid mills. Depending on the starting material, 
it may be convenient to divide the homogenization in two steps, forming an emulsion directly 
from a mixture of two liquids which is characterized as primary homogenization and the 
reduction of the size of the droplet of an already existing emulsion which is called secondary 
homogenization (Figure 13) [45]. 
 
 
Figure 13: Homogenization procedure. 
 
Most experimental emulsion preparations take place in two steps because it is more efficient. 
First the oil and water phases are mixed to form an emulsion with a rotor-stator device and then 
the reduction of emulsion droplet size by high pressure system or ultrasonic system takes place. 
As already mentioned, there are a variety of emulsification systems with different functional 
capabilities. The choice of a specific system depends on various factors such as: emulsion volume, 
viscosity of emulsion and its phases, surfactant type and concentration, temperature and final 
emulsion droplet size and size distribution. The systems that are used for emulsification are 
divided in rotor-stator systems in which the droplet distribution is less efficient because the 
dispersing zones of rotor–stator systems usually have larger volumes and high pressure systems 
that can be more efficient because they can achieve an emulsion droplet size as small as 0.1 μm 
with a wide droplet size distribution. Finally, ultrasound emulsification can produce smaller 
droplets and is more efficient than rotor–stator systems. The main drawback is that ultrasound 
is proved efficient only on a lab scale and research [44].

5.1 Materials and methods 
 
Emulsion production 
Low-fat (5%) rapeseed oil-in-water (O/W) emulsions stabilized with BSG protein 0.2 wt% were 
produced. Numerous past studies have been performed under similar homogenization technics, 
producing 5% O/W emulsions from 0.2 wt% peptide solutions [7, 46, 47]. The raw materials for 
the peptide solutions were mainly seaweed, potato and methanotrophic bacteria. The emulsions 
production procedure was carried out following the same formulation, so the results can be 
comparable. This will provide vital information about the differences occurring and help readjust 
parts of the formulation for future studies involving BSG proteins as emulsifiers. 
The used hydrolysates were the ones obtained from the previous step of enzymatic hydrolysis. 
Emulsions were produced in triplicates, using solutions of 3 differently treated BSG samples (Malt 
flour BSG, Malt flour SFE treated with co-solvent and Malt flour SFE treated without co-solvent), 
and the total amount of each emulsion was 2 g.  Calculations regarding the 0.2% BSG protein 
solutions preparation are available in Appendix A8. One emulsion of each sample was stored at 
room temperature to observe the stability and the phase separation over the storage time of 7 
days. The rest 2 emulsions of each sample were tested for stability. 
A two steps homogenization was performed for the small-scale emulsion production. Primary 
homogenization was conducted by mixing for 30 seconds, at 18,000 rpm by using a POLYTRON® 
PT1200E (Kinematic Inc., New York, USA). Secondary homogenization was performed using a 
sonicator Microson XL2000 equipped with a P1 probe (Misonix, Inc., New York, USA). Emulsions 
were homogenized at a 75% amplitude (maximum amplitude of 180 mm), running 2 phases of 
30 seconds with a break of 1 minute between phases. During sonication the emulsions were 
surrounded by iced water to minimize the increase in temperature.  
Zeta potential 
Zeta potential is a physical property that is exhibited by any particle in suspension. It is used in 
order to optimize the formulation of emulsions. Zeta potential can also minimize the time of the 
production of the emulsion and predict the stability of them. The liquid phase that surrounds the 
particle is divided into two parts: the inner region where the ions are strongly bound and the 
outer region where the ions are less bound. Within the outer region there is a theoretical border 
where the ions and particles form a stable substance. When a particle moves because of gravity, 
ions beyond the borders move it and those ions stay with the bulk dispersant. The potential at 
this border is the zeta potential. The extent of zeta potential gives an indicator of possible 
stability. If all the particles in suspension have large negative or positive zeta potential, they will 
repulse each other and there will be no possibility for particles to come together. Otherwise, if 
the particles have low zeta potential, then they can come together. The limits between stable 
and unstable suspensions are taken at +30 or-30 mV. Particles with zeta potential more positive 
than +30 or more negative than -30 considered stable. However, if the particles have a different 
==PAGE:40==
36 
 
density from the dispersant, they will form a sediment. Factors that can affect the zeta potential 
are the pH, the conductivity and the concentration of a formulation component [48].  
The zeta potential of the emulsions was measured on Day 1 in a Zetasizer Nano ZS (Malvern 
instruments Ltd., Worcestershire, UK) with a DTS1070 cell at 20oC. Before analysis, the emulsions 
were diluted in buffer (20 μL emulsion in 10 mL buffer). The zeta potential range was set to -100 
to +50 mV and the samples were analyzed with 100 runs. The SOP used was “zeta oil in water 
emulsions” that was created by previous researchers based on 5% oil-in-water emulsions. The 
complete protocol can be seen in Appendix A9. Measurements were done in duplicates (triplicate 
measurements were performed when differences between the duplicates averages were 
observed). 
Droplet size 
Droplet size was measured on Day 1, 3 and 7 using laser diffraction in a Mastersizer 2000 
(Malvern instruments, Ltd., Worcestershire, UK).  SOP used was created by previous researchers 
based on 5% oil-in-water emulsions. Results were expressed as surface-mean diameter D[3,2] 
and volume-mean diameter D[4,3]. Emulsions were diluted in circulation water at 3000 rpm until 
an obscuration just above 12%. The refractive indices of sunflower oil and water were used as 
particle and dispersant. Measurements were done in duplicates or triplicates.  
The size distribution of droplets was measured and used to calculate the surface weighted mean 
diameter D[3,2] and the volume weighted mean diameter D[4,3]. 
 
5.2 Results and discussion 
 
After the formation of an emulsion, it is important to remain stable. Emulsions can be collapsed 
for several reasons of destabilization mechanisms such as creaming, flocculation, sedimentation, 
coalescence and Ostwald ripening. The nature of the emulsifier that includes the droplets may 
affect the instability mechanisms. The size of the droplet after homogenization which can drive 
to creaming situation is a factor that can affect the emulsion stability. Emulsion with smaller 
droplet size can give better stability. Another factor that affects the stability of an emulsion is 
when the emulsifier layer affects the relative strength of the attractive and repulsive interactions 
between the droplets. Emulsifiers that produce strong repulsive interactions act better as 
inhibitors for droplet aggregation. The two major stabilization mechanisms produced by 
emulsifiers are steric and electrostatic stabilization [49]. 
As mentioned, emulsions were created from each sample and stored at room temperature for 
observation of their physical stability. The samples were stored for 7 days in total and observation 
was monitored on Day 0, 1, 3 and 7. In Figure 14 an overview of the visual observation is 
presented. As is shown, emulsions were destabilized following the same trend, gradually from 
Day 0 to Day 7. The phase separation is more intense moving from Day 1 to Day 7. Droplet 
==PAGE:41==

aggregation by accumulation was observed, leading less dense particles to move to the top of 
the tube. Nonetheless, some droplets are still present in the aqueous phase. These observations 
indicate that attractive interactions between the droplets are the dominant forces in the matrix 
[3]. 
In general, the emulsions were not stable. This is an indicator that droplets size could be big 
rather than small, but this will be verified using the droplet size measurement. This unstable 
behavior may also mean that the specific produced peptides lack efficient emulsifying properties.

6. CONCLUSION  
 
A stable mashing process was performed for six different barley malts samples (Malt flour, Planet, 
Laureate, Pilsner, Pilsner + Carafa Typ 3 and Pilsner + Caramunich Typ 2). There were some 
properties differences between the produced BSG samples, but they were expected due to the 
default malt characteristics. Protein content of BSG samples was found to be between 13.2-17%. 
Among these samples, Malt flour BSG had the highest protein content with a percentage of 17%. 
Planet and Laureate BSG showed higher protein content compared to all Pilsner-based BSG 
samples. Moisture content was found to be between 8-10%, while ash content was between 1-
2% for all the samples. Malt flour and Planet malt BSG were selected for the next experimental 
phase of Supercritical CO2 Extraction, based upon their higher protein content. 
Supercritical CO2 Extraction was performed under different sets of conditions, in order to extract 
lipids. The yield of the extractions was between 4.2-7.2%, with higher yield observed in samples 
extracted with co-solvent indicating a higher extraction efficiency when co-solvent is used. 
Increased extraction time and co-solvent flow rate also appeared higher yield. The increase in 
protein content of the samples after Supercritical CO2 Extraction, was not exceeding 1.2%, which 
clearly indicates that this extraction method is not suitable to isolate proteins. Other extraction 
methods should be considered based on the molecular weight of the proteins. Extracted Malt 
flour BSG samples had the highest protein content after extraction and were selected to undergo 
the next stage of enzymatic hydrolysis. 
Enzymatic hydrolysis was performed using Alcalase. The samples put in testing were Malt flour 
BSG, Malt flour BSG extracted with co-solvent and Malt flour BSG extracted without co-solvent. 
The yield of hydrolysis was between 69.7% and 71.4% and the degree of hydrolysis was found to 
be 2.01-2.22% for all tested samples. The degree of hydrolysis was low for all samples, but 
increased hydrolysis time and/or enzyme volume could potentially increase the degree of 
hydrolysis. Moreover, it was proved that Supercritical CO2 Extraction of the samples prior to 
hydrolysis does not have any effect on the hydrolysis process. Overall, it was possible to produce 
peptides from BSG through enzymatic hydrolysis. 
Finally, 0.2 wt% BSG hydrolysates of each selected sample were used to produce 5% rapeseed oil 
in water emulsions that were tested for stability over 7 days of storage. Zeta potential showed 
promising stability (lower than -50 mV). The smallest droplets on Day 1 of storage were formed 
by the Malt flour BSG sample treated with SFE and no use of co-solvent (D[3,2]=1.53 μm), while 
on Day 7 all samples appeared droplet size between 20.5-31.7 μm. This increase was significant. 
Droplet distribution showed that smaller droplets attached to each other creating larger droplets 
and at the same time, larger droplets showed a dimeter increase as well. This kind of instability 
can be explained by the Ostwald ripening mechanism. 
==PAGE:48==
44 
 
To sum up, emulsions produced were not stable, based on the droplet size and the visual stability 
observation, where droplet aggregation by accumulation was observed. Different treated 
samples also showed no remarkable differences according to their emulsifying properties.

7. FUTURE RESEARCH 
 
In the present study, the beer brewing process was terminated after the mashing stage. Since 
the findings from the Malt flour BSG were promising in terms of protein content, monitoring the 
whole brewing process of the specific malt would be interesting. This will reveal both the 
fermentation and the overall brewing performance of the Malt flour. If a quality beer can be 
produced, giving more valuable by-products by altering the state of malt, then further research 
can be conducted around this subject. 
Extraction of proteins from BSG can be more efficient with other methods than Carbon Dioxide 
Supercritical Extraction. Alkaline extraction, acid extraction and filtration are the most widely 
used methods right now. Protein recovery from BSG using ultrafiltration is reported to be up to 
92% [52]. Ultrafiltration can be tested prior to enzymatic hydrolysis. 
An alternative approach to what was performed in this study is to use the lipids extracted from 
SFE as emulsifiers instead of the protein fraction, assuming that there is a considerable number 
of phospholipids in the extract.  Moreover, carbohydrate extraction to purify the extraction 
residue and enhance the protein content could be tried. 
Protein identification prior and after enzymatic hydrolysis is recommended so enzyme selection 
and applied hydrolysis conditions can be set accordingly. Mass spectroscopy is reported to be the 
most convenient method among the commonly used ones for protein identification [53]. 
Experimentation with different Alcalase ratios to increase the degree of hydrolysis and 
comparative processes using other enzymes can also be applied. Although Alcalase is reported 
to give a better degree of hydrolysis in similar studies hydrolyzing plant proteins, Protamex and 
Flavorzyme enzymes is suggested to be tried.  
Homogenization in a higher pressure could create smaller droplets, adding stability to the 
emulsions [54]. Increased peptide concentration in the initial solutions could also increase 
stability. It is known that high concentration of small peptides tends to show larger droplet size 
and hence more unstable emulsions in general [55], but the activity profile of the produced 
peptides in this project is not defined. Finally, stabilizing agents' addition to the emulsions could 
lead to better stability over time.
