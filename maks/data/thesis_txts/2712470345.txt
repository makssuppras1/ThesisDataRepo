Abstract
This master’s project is aimed to evaluate the thermal behavior of a 60000 m3 water
pit heat storage by performing numerical and experimental analysis of the water pit. The
experimental data obtained from Dronninglund solar district heating plant in 2017 are ana-
lyzed, including diffusers outlet temperature, the temperature from sensors in the storage,
flow rates, etc. Numerical simulations based on Type 1535 and Type 1301 are carried out
to compare with the data obtained from measurement.
Before carrying out parametric analysis, the Trnsys model with 32 layers is selected to
compare with the real PTES for validation. The coefficient of determination is used to show
the prediction accuracy of the Trnsys model. According to the result, the model has high
accuracy in simulating diffusers’ temperatures, average temperatures of the storage, and
charged/discharged energy. The overall coefficient of determination is 0.982. Therefore,
the Trnsys model is considered validated.
Afterward, parametric studies are conducted regarding the number of nodes in storage,
soil properties, the slope of water pit and diffusers’ locations. These parametric analyses
aim to find the best parameters for the storage to have the optimal thermal behaviors.
First, the number of nodes that the storage is divided into is changed. The model with
the maximum number of nodes (100 layers) is found to have the maximum charged and
discharged energy, which is 12299 [MWh] and 11953 [MWh], respectively. It can also be
found that generally, the more layers the storage is divided into, the better the thermal
stratification condition as well as prediction accuracy.
Second, different types of soils are selected to see the influence of soil properties on the
thermal behavior of the storage. This project introduces a relative Fourier number to unify
the coupling relationship of soil density, specific heat capacity, and thermal conductivity. At
the same time, the influence of time and heat exchange area is also eliminated. According
to the simulation, a higher relative Fourier number of soil leads to more considerable heat
losses, lower storage efficiency, higher charged energy, and lower discharged energy.
The effect of heat transfer area on water pit stratification and heat losses is studied by
changing the slope angle. Based on the results, a larger slope angle of the water pit results
in larger discharged energy, smaller heat losses and higher storage efficiency. Regarding
thermal stratification conditions, the storage with the largest slope angle (86.51 ◦) have
the smallest MIX number, which implies better stratification conditions inside the storage.
Finally, the locations of the top, middle and bottom diffusers are changed for investiga-
tions. The charged and discharged energy increase with middle and top diffuser positions.
Conversely, the bottom diffuser shows an opposite pattern in energy. When moving to
higher positions (node 50 to node 42 ), a drop from 12141 MWh to 11578 MWh is found in
charged energy and a decline from 11793 MWh to 11391 MWh is observed in discharged
energy. More discussion about energy change and thermal stratification can be found in
this thesis.
This thesis serves as a reference for future researchers and designers to better analyze
or design the large-scale water pit.

Introduction
In response to continuously increasing fossil fuel energy consumption and global warm-
ing, renewable energy is considered the optimal solution to replace fossil fuels and help
navigate the energy transition. Renewable energy has the potential to provide abundant
and clean energy derived from self-renewing resources such as the sun, wind, earth, and
plants. However, renewable energy sources make up only a tiny portion of overall en-
ergy demand due to various reasons such as high upfront costs, intermittent supply, and
geographic limitations, which impede the shift towards sustainable development.
Recently solar energy has drawn much attention because it has the advantages of wide
distribution, inexhaustible supply, and high energy conversion efficiency. Thus, solar en-
ergy is regarded as one of the most promising alternative energy resource options. [1]
However, one barrier retarding the extensive solar energy utilization is the temporal mis-
match between the energy consumption and supply. Energy storage is therefore essential
to solving the problem of temporal mismatch. Among different storage techniques, ther-
mal energy storage (TES) is considered one of the most promising techniques.
There are currently three thermal energy storage technologies: sensible heat storage, la-
tent heat storage, and chemical storage.[2] Among these three technologies, latent heat
storage and chemical storage are still in the experimental stage, while sensible heat stor-
age is already a mature technology.
The general thermal energy storage (TES) can also be categorized into two types based
on the storage duration. The first type of TES is called short-term systems or diurnal heat
storage, and the second type is called long-term storage systems, also known as seasonal
thermal energy storage (STES). The STES are usually either fully or partially buried un-
der the ground. Therefore these STES systems are also known as Underground thermal
energy storage (UTES) systems. The most common UTES systems determined by their
construction methods are aquifer thermal energy storage (ATES) system, borehole ther-
mal energy storage (BTES) system, tank thermal energy storage (TTES) system, cavern
thermal energy storage (CTES) system, and pit thermal energy storage (PTES) system.[3]
Figure 1.1 summarizes the advantages and disadvantages of the four most common types
of UTES systems.

TRNSYS performs well as software for system simulation in modeling thermal storage of
different types. However, the existing literature studies focus more on the system simula-
tion, including the selection and operation strategies of thermal storage. Few studies on
thermal storage and especially PTES models have been validated by measurements.
Performing coupled simulations over a long period is not easy, considering the details of
PTES and the whole solar district heating system. In this paper, a model of PTES (Type
1535-1301) from TRNSYS containing a 1D water pit and a 2D soil is used as a research
object. The measured data of 60,000 m3 of PTES in Dronninglund, Denmark, in 2017 are
utilized as input parameters and validation data.
The different numbers of simulation nodes are selected to observe the differences be-
tween the simulation results and the experimental results, such as temperature, energy,
and stratification coefficient. Then, the influence of the node number of storage on the
simulation accuracy and numerical diffusion is judged. In addition, the effect of critical
parameters such as geometry, soil properties, and diffuser location on temperature distri-
bution and energy yield is investigated.

2.1.1
Dronninglund solar district heating plant
Dronninglund solar district heating plant is one of the world’s largest solar district heating
plants. The operation of this plant began in 2014, and it has a capacity of 26300 in kWth,
which currently ranks the 4th among large-scale solar heating plants in Europe. [4] The
solar thermal plant is comprised of 2982 Arcon Solar panels with a total area of 37573 m2.
Each field of panels is linked to a heat exchanger in the technical building. The collector
fields can generate a maximum power of 26MW, while the minimum power produced by
collector fields is 12MW.[5] The owner of the Dronninglund Fjemvarme plant is a customer-
owned cooperative, serving around 1350 consumers over a 46-kilometer district heating
network.

2.1.2
The water pit heat storage
The main focus of this thesis will be on water pit heat storage. The water pit heat storage
is constructed by digging a hole formed as an inverted truncated pyramid, and the soil is
used as banks. The optimal position of groundwater level is below the storage. According
to geotechnical investigations [5], the abandoned gravel pit was the only place where the
ground water level was below the storage bottom and the soil could be used as banks.
Also, the use of abandoned gravel pit to build PTES can effectively reduce the construction
cost. As a result, the water storage in Dronninglund was selected to be placed in an
abandoned gravel pit.
The PTES has a volume of 60000m3 and is built as an inverted truncated pyramid as
shown in Figure 2.1(right). The depth of the PTES is 16m, and the tilt angle of the stor-
age is 26.6◦. The top and bottom lengths of the PTES are 90m and 26m, respectively.[6]
Various insulation materials were used to cover different surfaces of PTES. Three layers

of the material Nomalen is used at the top surface with a total thickness of 240mm. While
the rest of the surfaces were covered by a 2.5 mm HDPE liner and a geotextile layer.[7]
Inside the PTES, three diffusers were installed from bottom to top at the height of 0.5m,
11.2m and 15.5m, respectively. These diffusers, each connected with a pipe, can charge
and discharge water to ensure good operation of the PTES. When the diffusers operates
at the same time, they share the same hot or cold water pipe. It is also worth noting that
the middle diffuser was placed at the volumetric center of the PTES. [7]
2.1.3
Measurement
The measurement data of the PTES in Dronninglund SDH plant mainly includes water
temperatures of each layer inside the storage and each inlet and outlet, volumetric flow
rate inside the PTES and ambient temperature. In order to monitor and record the tem-
perature and flow rate inside the storage, 32 temperature sensors were installed inside
the PTES, evenly distributed from top to bottom (from 0.5m to 15.5m), except that the top
sensor was mounted 0.1m below the cover. [7] Besides that, three temperature sensors
and three flow meters were placed to monitor the temperature and flow rate of each inlet
and outlet of PTES. Outside the PTES, the surrounding soil temperature was recorded
by four temperature sensors at the depth of 10m, 15m, 20m and 25m, respectively. The
placement of different sensors is illustrated in Figure 2.2. All of the data is recorded with
a time step of 10 minutes for one year.

2.2.1
TRNSYS model
Figure 2.3: TRNSYS model of Type 1535-1301
For this project, the simulation part of the PTES is carried out in TRNSYS. Two critical
components are used in the simulation: Type 1301 and Type 1535. Type 1301 is coded to
model the heat transfer to the soil for a buried truncated conical-shaped storage tank, while
Type 1535 is used to model a fluid-filled, constant volume storage tank. It is worth noting
that Type 1301 simulates the thermal behavior of surrounding soil in two dimensions, while
Type 1535 simulates the storage behavior only in one dimension.
More details about the differences in dimensions between the storage and surrounding soil
can be found in Figure 2.4. Other components include data readers, equation calculators
and online graphics components. The following sections will discuss some of the essential
formulas used in the equation calculator and the source code.

each layer is neglected in this equation.

The mesh scheme of PTES and surrounding soil generated by Type 1301 is quite compli-

walls of storage in the vertical and radial directions. A simplified plot (Figure 2.4) is pre-

According to the source code in Type 1301, the nodes are generated in two directions,
radial r-direction and vertical z-direction. The mesh is divided into three areas in the
vertical direction, each having a different meshing method. Denser meshes are generated
at two ends from the surface to the top of the tank and coarser meshes are generated in
the middle. Then moving down to the storage height, similar noding patterns are applied
for adjacent soil nodes corresponding to each node in the storage. The meshes of soil
at the height corresponding to the boundary of each water node are denser than the soil
meshes generated at the height corresponding to the middle of each water node. In order
to calculate the heat transfer from the fluid-solid boundary to the deep earth boundary,
the meshes near the walls are refined while the deep meshes are sparse.
When it comes to the mesh generation in the radial direction, there are mainly three parts.
For the bottom tank radius, the soil meshes become denser from the symmetry axis to
the right corner of the bottom. Regarding the grid scheme of soil adjacent to each water
node for the length of the slanted tank wall section, denser meshes of soil are generated
at two boundaries of each water node while coarser meshes are generated in the middle.
From the edge of the insulated top section to the far-field boundary, the meshes close to
the edge of the top surface are refined while the far-field boundary is sparse.

temperature has to be interpolated first according to the volume of each layer before it can
be used as the initial temperature in TRNSYS models. This is because the experimental
water storage is divided into 32 iso-height layers with different volumes, while in simula-
tion, each node of storage is assumed to have the same volume. With this interpolation,
it is easier to study the effect of node number on the thermal performance of PTES.

validation of the TRNSYS model. These key parameters include charged and discharged
energy, internal temperatures of top, middle and bottom locations, heat losses, etc. The
top, middle and bottom can represent the temperature distribution inside the water pit and
are positioned at the same height as the diffusers. Figure 3.1 is given below depicting the
evolution of temperatures of three positions throughout the whole year.
The prediction of top temperature is generally accurate, with the most significant deviation
of 18.13 ◦C occurring at the end of March. This deviation may be due to numerical diffusion
caused by long time steps or low simulated node number, so the simulation lags in the
calculation of the temperature change rate. As for the middle, apparent mismatches can
be seen from early May to mid-August, with the largest deviation of 7.19 ◦C witnessed

Thermal stratification

MIX number describes the degree of mixing inside the storage. A larger MIX number
indicates a higher degree of mixing, while a smaller MIX number represents better stratifi-
cation conditions in the tank. From figure 4.4, it can be seen that the accuracy of 50 nodes
is higher when charging and the deviation of 20 nodes is smaller when discharging.
Possible explanations are given here. According to the actual operation of the storage,
the mixing caused by the water inflow during charging is relatively small but the temper-
ature difference is large. Therefore, when the number of nodes is small, it will cause a
numerical diffusion of computational values. However, the effect of blending gradually
exceeds the numerical correction part when the number of nodes is too large, which lead
to the accuracy will be reduced. Contrary to this, the temperature fluctuation of the mid-
dle and bottom inlet water flow over time will cause a large amount of admixture when
discharging. This causes a vortex near the inlet and creates temperature mixing. A 1D
simulation cannot account for this phenomenon and therefore only the node where the
inlet is located is affected by the flow. Therefore, the precision will be low if the node is
large, but there will also be numerical diffusion since the node is too small. It should be
noted that the optimal conclusion of the number of 20 and 50 nodes given by this project is
only applicable to this Trnsys model. To better interpret this phenomenon, future analysis
can be done with the help of CFD simulation to precisely analyze the flow state of water
during the charging period and discharging period.

5
Future study
Some potential analysis can be carried out in the future to improve the simulation accu-
racy and determine better parameters for the PTES.First, when performing parametric
investigations of the storage. The models with fewer nodes have higher prediction ac-
curacy of thermal stratification indicators during charging period, while models with more
nodes have better accuracy in prediction during discharging period. In order to increase
the overall prediction accuracy throughout the whole year. Future study is suggested to
utilize a model composed of various number of nodes based on whether it is charging or
discharging period of the year. For example, during discharging period, a smaller number
of nodes can be used to simulate the temperatures and thermal stratification indicators,
and a larger number of nodes can be selected for simulation during charging period of the
year. In this way, the accuracy of the simulation can be maximized to achieve the best
results.
To better interpret some phenomena in parametric studies, future analysis can be done
with the help of CFD simulation, which can precisely depict the flow state of water during
the charging and discharging periods. Combining the result of both TRNSYS simulation
and CFD simulation, the storage’s thermal behavior and flow condition can be compre-
hensively evaluated.
Furthermore, research can be implemented on the relative Fourier number of the soil
regarding soil properties. In this master’s project, only nine types of soil are considered.
However, a broader range of soils is needed for further investigations. Validations should
also be done to prove that the relative Fourier number itself is enough to represent the
soil properties when it comes to simulating the different thermal behaviors of the PTES.
In this project, the properties of water such as density, specific heat, and dynamic vis-
cosity are assumed to be constant. However, these physical properties of water depend
on multiple factors like temperature, pressure, and even fluid composition. Therefore,
new water properties should be calculated based on time-varying factors in future analy-
sis. That way, TRNSYS models can simulate more accurately, and the results are more
reliable and close to the actual experiment.
Last but not least, the method of orthogonal experimental design can be used to consider
the location changes of three diffusers simultaneously. This method is to study multi-
factor and multi-level. It selects some representative points from the comprehensive test
according to the orthogonality. In this project, only one single factor is considered. The
location of each diffuser is changed only if the other two diffusers’ locations remain un-
changed. Therefore, the method of orthogonal experimental design is able to study the
holistic impact of three diffusers on the thermal behavior of PTES.

Conclusion
Numerical and experimental investigations have been performed on the thermal perfor-
mance of the water pit heat storage in the Dronninglund SDH plant. Type 1535 and Type
1301 are used in this project as the key components. By carrying out parametric analyses
of the PTES, the impact of the node number of storage, soil properties, the geometry of
water pit and diffusers’ locations on the long-term thermal performance of PTES is studied
comprehensively. Some conclusions drawn from the results are listed below:
1. Among the models with different nodes, the model with 75 nodes of storage has the
best total prediction accuracy, and the total coefficient of determination is 0.985. The
model with 15 nodes is the smallest, with a value of 0.975, and the model with 100
nodes is 0.984. Overall, the more nodes of the storage, the smaller the numerical
diffusion. However, the model with larger node numbers ignores more turbulent
mixing in the water pit. Thus, the model with 100 nodes has slightly lower accuracy
than models with 75 nodes.
2. The relative Fourier number Fom couples the soil thermophysical properties and is
therefore considered to be a better reflection of the rate of thermal diffusion. For the
largest (Fom = 5.0), the smallest discharging energy and largest charging energy
are observed, which are 11257 MWh and 12392 MWh, respectively. Both of the
largest discharging energy and smallest charging energy is found when Fom is equal
to 0.26, which are 12111 MWh and 12025 MWh, respectively. The larger the Fom,
the greater the soil temperature threshold, the greater the heat loss and charge, and
the smaller the discharge and storage temperature.
3. In this project, changes in slope angles of the storage results in changes in the
contact area of the storage and the surrounding environment. When the water pit
is shifted towards the cylinder shape, the general thermal performances, including
storage efficiency, heat losses, energy delivered, and thermal stratification, are sig-
nificantly improved. The biggest problem that this geometry change will cause is the
increase in the excavation costs due to the increasing challenge for the excavators
to carry out the excavation work. So economic analysis will be required to calculate
the detail.
4. The locations of each diffuser highly impact the thermal behavior of PTES according
to the results of simulations. The highest location of the middle diffuser, which is
node 15 in this project, has the maximum storage cycle times (1.96), the best thermal
stratification and the maximum discharged energy (12210 MWh). Thus, placing the
middle diffuser at higher positions inside the storage can significantly improve the
overall thermal behavior of the PTES.
Simulations show that lower positions of the top diffuser and higher positions of
the bottom diffuser lead to better thermal stratification of the storage. However,
Numerical and experimental analysis of a large-scale water pit heat storage
45
==PAGE:50==
other energy indicators such as discharged energy, storage cycle and heat losses
are getting worst in this condition. When the top diffuser moves to node 13 and the
bottom diffuser is elevated to node 42. The discharged energies drop to 11002 MWh
and 11391 MWh, while The storage cycles decrease to 1.83 and 1.77, respectively.
It is advised to put the top diffuser at the top and place the bottom diffuser at the
bottom to make full use of the heat of the water. Regarding the middle diffuser, it
is suggested to be placed at higher positions to achieve better thermal stratification
and more energy discharge.
In summary, this thesis can serve as an inspiration for future analysis of thermal
behavior of PTES. The parametric analyses carried out in this project can be used
as a basis for extensive studies in the future.
