Abstract
This thesis applies Brinkman penalised topology optimisation to Navier-Stokes pipe ﬂow
problems for the design of axial ﬂow machinery. The goal of the thesis is to apply topology
optimisation to maximise the moment of force for an axis of rotation, and investigate the
dynamic eﬀects of an turbine impeller in rotation. The Coriolis and centrifugal forces
are added to the Navier-Stokes equations to view the ﬂow within a rotational frame of
reference.
The ﬂow characteristics and design domain parameters are seen to have a
fundamental impact on the topology optimised solution. The formulation used to specify
the problem to axial ﬂow machines is easily changed to alternative rotating ﬂow machinery.
The range of ﬂow problems is conﬁned to steady state incompressible laminar ﬂow at low
to moderate Reynolds numbers.
The optimisation is performed large scale for three dimensions and is implemented by code
written in C++ that uses a parallel ﬁnite element framework developed by the TopOpt
research group of the Department of Mechanical Engineering at the Technical University
of Denmark.
This thesis contributes to the ﬁeld of topology optimisation in ﬂow machinery, which is a
ﬁeld that has only recently begun to see documented research. To the authors knowledge,
no other literature currently exists that applies topology optimisation in three dimensions
to ﬂow machinery with impellers in rotation.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The aim of this thesis is to apply the method of topology optimisation to generate designs
for axial ﬂow machine impellers. More speciﬁcally, the designs seek to be optimised for
the conversion of kinetic energy in a ﬂuid ﬂow into rotational energy for an axial turbine
impeller.
Some familiarity with ﬂuid mechanics and how to use ﬁnite element formulation to discre-
tise a ﬂow problem is recommended for readers of this thesis as this will not be extensively
covered.
This thesis will present designs that are solved in three dimensions and in a rotating frame
of reference in order to apply the eﬀects of a rotating ﬂow. Both of these aspects are
cornerstones for the thesis.
The dynamic eﬀects of a rotating impeller on the ﬂuid are expected to be quite signiﬁcant
which implies that a solution where no rotation has been considered, might perform poorly
in a rotating ﬂow ﬁeld.
Solving in three dimensions is necessary in order to properly generate a design that could
have real application. A two dimensional solution would require heavy human interpreta-
tion in the neglected dimension in order to result in a realistic design for axial ﬂow. This
could also be viewed as a missed opportunity for the optimiser to create complex and inter-
esting designs. A three dimensional optimisation is considered necessary for generating a
design that is operable and can be produced with little or no human design interpretation,
ideally. However, solving in three dimensions is not feasible without the use of large scale
parallel computation as these problems are costly to solve for a proper amount of degrees
of freedom. The problems are solved with code based on that developed by Joe Alexan-
dersen for solving natural convection problems in three dimensions. This code is written
in C++ and uses the Portable and Extendable Toolkit for Scientiﬁc Computing (PETSc)
framework for functionalities such as parallel (MPI) computing, matrix and vector objects
and manipulations, and solvers for the optimiser.
This problem has only been partly solved in other works at this point. These works include
the thesis by Madsen [12], where similar problems are solved in three dimensions, but only
1
==PAGE:8==
for static impellers, and the article by Romero and Silva [14], where the Navier-Stokes
equations are solved in a rotational frame of reference, but only for two-dimensional radial
impellers. Large scale topology optimization for ﬂuid problems were introduced by Aage
et al. [2] for more general problems, such as minimising power dissipation, in two and
three dimensions.
Topology optimisation is a well documented method for optimising material distribution
and was initially developed to minimise compliance for structures by Bendsøe and Kikuchi
[4] in 1988. It was extended to ﬂuid mechanics by Borrvall and Petersson [5] when they
introduced topology optimisation for Stokes ﬂow in 2003. They proposed to use Brinkman
lubrication theory to apply penalising friction to the ﬂow in order to interpolate between
solid and ﬂuid elements, which is generally known as Brinkman penalisation. Guest and
Pr´evost [9] proposed a similar but alternate approach in which a Darcy-Stokes system is
formulated. Olesen et al. [13] modiﬁed the approach from Borrvall and Petersson [5] where
the friction term instead arises due to ﬂow through an idealised porous medium.
Topology optimisation in ﬂuid mechanics was expanded to moderate Reynolds numbers in
Navier-Stokes ﬂow as can be seen in [13] and Gersborg-Hansen et al. [8].

3.2.1
Constraints
A volume constraint is very common for structural optimisation problems as the compliance
of a structure is often minimised by adding material to the solution, meaning a constraint
to the maximum allowable material is imposed.
Optimisation problems in ﬂuid mechanics that minimise the energy dissipation often see
the opposite problem; that minimal energy dissipation in the ﬂuid is achieved with little
or no added material in the solution. This motivates the use of a volume constraint that
enforces a minimum amount of material that must remain in the solution.
For this particular problem, those situations are not expected as some material must be

added to produce a moment of rotation, but too much material would severely constrict
the velocity ﬁeld. That makes the objective function for this problem very well suited for
topology optimisation, as it contains conﬂicting components when adding material. This
means that no constraints should ideally be needed, but having a volume constraint to
enforce an upper limit of allowed material is seen to be beneﬁcial for some cases, as will
be shown in the results section.

3.2.2
Method of moving asymptotes
The method of moving asymptotes (MMA) is the optimisation algorithm that is used to
determine how the design ﬁeld should be changed on each iteration, after the current state
ﬁeld has been solved. It is developed by Svanberg [16] and a very robust optimisation
algorithm, initially developed for structural optimisation. However, as it is developed to
be ﬂexible and can optimise general non-linear problems, making it also applicable to ﬂuid
mechanics problems. It also allows the enforcement of constraints that are only limited by
the fact that their derivative with respect to the design variable must also given as an input.
MMA is very suitable for problems with a large design ﬁeld and a few constraints which
accommodates most topology optimisation problems, the one for this thesis included. It
is implemented for parallel computing using the code written by Aage and described by
Aage et al. in [1].
3.2.3
PDE ﬁlter
A Helmholtz type PDE ﬁlter is applied to the design ﬁeld after it has been updated by the
MMA step on a optimisation iteration. This ﬁlter is presented by Lazarov and Sigmund
[11] acts like a density ﬁlter that eﬀective smooths out the densities in the design ﬁeld. This
mainly serves to prevent false penalisation of ﬂuid elements that might appear in between
any solid elements. This will also create an interpolated transition layer of densities in the
ﬁltered design ﬁeld, but this can be ignored when post-processing the designs in Paraview.

3.2.4
Brinkman interpolation
The last line in (3.1) states that the design elements in x will take a value between 0 and
1, where 0 is solid material and 1 is pure ﬂuid (ndel number of design elements). This is
interpreted physically by the Brinkman interpolation to model frictional resistance of ﬂow
through porous media, and the evaluated value describes the inverse permeability of an
element i
αi = Amin + (Amax −Amin) 1 −xi
1 + xiq
(3.4)
where Amin and Amax are the lower and upper boundary values and q is a penalty value
that is selected to make the interpolation follow a concave curve. The interpolation used
in this thesis follows the formulation laid out by Stolpe and Svanberg [15].
As a default for this thesis, Amin and Amax are set to 0 and 104 and q is set to 100.
In most cases q > 0 is recommended since a linear interpolation would be too heavily
penalising. This could mean that the optimiser is excessively prevented from increasing
design element densities and would likely cause it to converge toward a lesser solution.
However, ramping the penalty factor toward 0 during optimisation could be beneﬁcial
if the optimiser has diﬃculties converging toward a solution of exclusively solid or ﬂuid
elements, commonly referred to as a 0-1 solution. A 0-1 solution is a preferable outcome
as elements of interpolated density are non-physical, and a decision of whether to consider
them solids or ﬂuids in the ﬁnal design solution will have to be made.

3.4.1
System stabilisation
It has been chosen to use u1p1 elements to discretise the domains. This means the cubic
elements will only have nodal values for the velocities and pressure in the 8 corners, which
gives each element 32 degrees of freedom for this problem.
This choice is motivated by the fact that any higher order elements would cause the system
to have so many more degrees of freedom that the number of elements would have to be
severely compromised to maintain realistic computational costs, in addition to be more
complex to be implemented. A high number of elements is prioritised in order to have the
most reﬁned mesh of the design solution as possible. However, using this ﬁnite element
discretisation opens up the ﬂow to be unstable for two distinct reasons as also discussed by
Tezduyar et al. [17]. These introduced numerical instabilities are expected to be signiﬁcant
for this problem and motivates the use of two stabilisation methods
PSPG stabilisation
Choosing u1p1 elements means the interpolations of the velocity ﬁeld and the pressure ﬁeld
are both of the ﬁrst order. Having equal-order elements does not satisfy the Ladyzhenskaya-
Babuska-Brezzi (LBB) stability condition for ﬁnite element spaces, and numerical errors
will cause oscillations in the pressure ﬁeld for an unstabilised ﬂow. However, the PSPG
stabilisation method generalised in [17] for incompressible Navier-Stokes ﬂow, adds to the
ﬁnite element formulation and allows for the LBB condition to be circumvented. The need
for stabilisation becomes less as the mesh is reﬁned, or elements that are appropriate for
the LBB condition could be implemented.
SUPG stabilisation
The streamline-upwind/Petrov-Galerkin (SUPG) method was introduced by Brooks and
Hughes [6] for Navier-Stokes ﬂow. The ﬁnite element formulation for an unstabilised and
13
==PAGE:20==
convection-dominated ﬂow would introduce signiﬁcant numerical errors that mainly cause
oscillations in the velocity ﬁeld. The errors are due to too-large velocity gradients in the
streamline direction, but the oscillatory eﬀects of these errors can eﬀectively be suppressed
by the SUPG method. This problem can also be alleviated by reﬁning the mesh further
or having higher-order velocity interpolating elements.

Chapter 5
Results
This chapter is divided in two main sections for the purpose of presenting the results of
two diﬀerently size design domains. This refers to the size of the design domains, where
the ”Channel domain” has a long design domain (ﬁgure 5.2) and ”Slim domain” has a
short design domain (ﬁgure 5.15).
All the design results are showed with a bright color scheme in an eﬀort to highlight the
design features. The designs will be shown with non-consistent orientation angles, but
blue color always indicates the front of the design and red always indicates the back of the
design, where relation to the ﬂow direction. A small indicator for the coordinate system is
also shown in bottom left corner of each ﬁgure, and the positive x-direction always points
in the ﬂow direction.
Tables of numerical results are also shown for all solved cases. These results are shown in
table 5.1 and 5.2. Here the evaluated unscaled objective function, the volume constraint
value and the volume of the design is shown for the speciﬁed iteration. In addition to the
unscaled objective function, which represents the produced moment of rotation Mx, the
two other moments My and Mz are also shown as an indication of how rotationally stable
the design might be.
A reference case will be deﬁned for each type of design domain. Variational cases will
be variations in a single aspect of these reference cases, and will either be a variation
in the characteristic of the ﬂow or a design domain parameter. While this method will
probably not be exhaustive of all possible and viable cases, it will help to determine how
these diﬀerent aspects aﬀect the design solution. The same types of case variations will be
applied to the reference cases of both design domains.
5.1
Case parameters
This section will describe the parameters that are considered default, which means the
cases with all the default parameters will be the reference cases. The set of variations to
26
==PAGE:33==
the reference cases will also be presented along with a description of why these variations
are included.
5.1.1
Rotational velocity magnitude and Reynolds number
The default prescribed rotational velocity magnitude is ω = 10. This, together with a
default Reynolds number Re = 10, will create a ﬂow ﬁeld that is signiﬁcantly aﬀected by
the rotational forces in the design domains.
Case variations of these parameters will be: no rotation in the ﬂow ω = 0, or a low
Reynolds number Re = 0.01.
A ﬂow without rotation is meant to be a comparison between optimising a solution for a
static impeller and an impeller at a prescribed operational rotation speed. This will show
if the rotational forces will aﬀect the design solution, which is a corner stone of this thesis.
The low Reynolds number will make the ﬂow behave more like a creeping ﬂow, and the
rotational forces at ω = 10 coincidentally no longer has a observable eﬀect. This ﬂow is of
a very diﬀerent character and the optimiser is expected to handle this very diﬀerently.
5.1.2
Varying volume fraction
The default volume fraction for the volume constraint (3.3) for the ”Channel” design
domain is set to vf = 0.2 and for ”Slim” design domain to vf = 0.4.
Variational cases that have lower or higher volume fractions will be presented. As the
design ﬁeld will start as practically empty and the optimisation process is expected to
mainly be increasing the total design ﬁeld density at most iterations.
Decreasing the volume fraction can help to determine if certain design features are priori-
tised as less total material is available.
Increasing the volume fraction could also help indicate if the optimisation can ﬁnd a solu-
tion that converges toward a design where adding material becomes obsolete.
5.1.3
Solid shaft
The reference cases will start with a empty ﬂow ﬁeld (apart from the design seed), but
solutions for variational cases with an imposed center shaft will be shown. The elements
of the shaft will be solid xi = 0 and will be coincidental with the axis of rotation and
extend throughout the design domain and a bit on either side into the ﬂow domains. The
elements of the shaft that previously belonged to the design domain will still be counted
for the volume constraint, but they will not be subject to change.
The radius of the shaft will be rshaft = 0.07 for all associated cases. Impellers for axial
27
==PAGE:34==
ﬂow machines are usually mounted on a center axis, which should take this case a step
toward a realistic design problem.
5.1.4
Design seed
Because the axis of rotation is centered in the pipe, a small disturbance must be introduced
to the ﬂow as a measure to get the topology optimisation started properly. In ﬁgure 5.1 a
small tetris-like design can be seen for the large channel design domain. This design seed
is implemented as shown, a small piece in one side only and with some distance to the
center axis of rotation.
Figure 5.1: Design seed in ”Channel” design domain
The design seed is supposed to make sure a low and positive objective function is evaluated
for the ﬁrst design iteration. Without this initial design, the objective function will be
practically zero in ﬁrst iteration, which causes a too-drastic improvement in the second
iteration for optimiser to handle. This problem is only seen for ﬂows without rotation, but
having an initial design is expected to have some inﬂuence on the ﬁnal solution, so it is
applied as a default for consistency.
Two solutions will also be presented for cases that are solved without the design seed: The
reference case and the shaft case.
5.1.5
Mesh resolution
The dimensions for the full domain is 2 × 1 × 1 and the mesh resolution for all cases is
256 × 128 × 128 elements which results in just over 4.2 million elements. The mesh is
28
==PAGE:35==
structured, meaning all elements are identically sized cubic elements, and this resolution
is the most reﬁned mesh that was possible to use to solve all the cases presented within
the time frame of the project.
5.1.6
Filtering and default threshold
The currently implemented PDE ﬁltering adds an artiﬁcial transition layer of densities, so
the presented designs will be shown within a ﬁltered design variable interval 0 ≤xPhys
i
≤
0.1. The upper limit of 0.1 is used to represent the ﬁnal designs as a pure 0-1 solutions are
generally not obtained, and this upper limit results in somewhat connected and smooth
designs for most cases.
The ﬁlter radius rmin = 0.012 is default for all cases and should take all neighbouring
elements into account, as this corresponds to just approximately 1.6 element edge length.

Chapter 6
Discussion
This chapter will present a discussion of the results of the thesis and possible sources of
error.
6.1
Modelling
As fundamental parts of the ﬂow modelling in the code has not been changed for the
purposes of this thesis, the detailed discussion of the problems associated to the ﬂow
modelling in the thesis of Alexandersen [3] is thorough and believed to be relevant for this
thesis.
6.2
Topology optimisation
Topology has been applied to optimise for an axial ﬂow machine. Other types of ﬂow
machines should relatively easily be solvable by various changes, such as the formulation
of the boundary conditions and the objective function.
The topology optimisation was generally seen to function well in practice, but this would
not be veriﬁed by a ﬁnite diﬀerence check. While the element sensitivities were shown to
have the right signs, a diﬀerence in the value magnitudes were often seen to be more larger
than acceptable. A bug in the code has not been deﬁnitively ruled out, but some error in
the modelling of the ﬂow is also possible. Speciﬁcally, it has previously been mentioned
that stabilisation of the ﬂow has not been extended to include the rotational forces and
this is considered a possible source of error. Further global mesh reﬁnement should also
help ﬂow stabilisation, but the number of elements needed for further reﬁnement means
computations start to become inconveniently long.
48
==PAGE:55==
6.3
Results
The topology optimisation problem has been solved for two distinctly sized design domains,
each with the same set of general parameter variations that has yielded a large array of
solutions.
In most cases, the solutions would converge toward a design that would not be at the limit
of the volume constraint, which could indicate that the solutions had a local optimisation
point within the given design limit.
Even though no designs are tested to verify their performance, it is expected that the
designs would perform best in type of ﬂow they were optimised for. The most variations
in the designs were seen when variations in the ﬂow was applied. This also means that the
assumptions used for the Naver-Stokes equations must be carefully chosen and possibly
extended from what has been used in this thesis.
6.3.1
Channel design domain
The Channel design domain was considerably large, meaning the optimiser had a lot of
design elements to take into account for the solutions.
This might be reﬂected in the
numerical results in table 5.1, that show a signiﬁcant range of values for all results.
The design solutions seen for this design domain were often seen to have many holes and
unconnected design features. These solutions are not considered erroneous, but rather
somewhat unpreferable. While the goal of this thesis is not to produce designs that are
ready for real-world application, the designs that seem at least close to being physically
operational are considered most successful.
For the large design domain, the best results are those of the reference case, the high
volume fraction case and the shaft case. While these would require some degree of human
design interpretation to be considered operational designs, the long screw-like impeller
blades seen in these solutions might have some merit.
However, it is important to note that this set of cases had multiple unstable optimisation
processes. This was especially true for the case with a decreased volume fraction and the
two that were solved without a design seed. The optimisation process was progressing
badly for these cases, but whether to assign this error to a faulty implementation in the
code, an instability in the ﬂow or something else is not known at this point. It should be
worth noting that the error was not seen in the other solutions in the large design domain,
and not at all for the solutions in the small design domain. If the rotational forces were
a large source of instability for the ﬂow, which would then be much more prominent for a
large design domain, a more consistent eﬀect might have been expected to be observable
on the results.
49
==PAGE:56==
6.3.2
Slim design domain
The volume of the small design domain was approximately 4 times smaller than the large
design domain. It could be that since the range of possible solutions is also smaller with
this design domain, it has enabled the forming of more tight solutions, as the performance
of these seem to be much less widespread when observing the results in table 5.2.
At any rate, it was seen that the results for these cases produced arguably better designs
in general. This is true if comparing the objective functions for these solutions in table
5.2 with the ones for the large design domain in table 5.1. The objective function results
for both design domains are seen to be at very comparable levels. This means that if also
taking the design volumes into account, the designs with the small design domain are indi-
cated to perform much more eﬃciently as they are signiﬁcantly smaller (when disregarding
the bad solutions for the large design domain).
The designs for the two cases solved without design seeds are considered the most success-
ful. It is remarkable that their objective functions are the highest for the reference ﬂow,
while their alternate moments are the lowest. The seemingly most operable design of the
two is the one with an imposed shaft. It should be interesting to verify its performance,
especially as it is indicated to be the most rotationally stable design.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
The fundamental goals of the thesis has been achieved. The method of topology optimi-
sation has been applied to Navier-Stokes ﬂow to produce designs solutions for axial ﬂow
machine impellers. Adding the rotational forces to the Navier-Stokes equations allowed
modelling rotation in the ﬂow within the design domain. A large number of cases has been
solved, and it has also been shown that prominent dynamic eﬀects of a rotating impeller,
will produce vastly diﬀerent optimised solutions than for a static impeller.
The presented results are simple and academic, but some solutions already show promise as
operable designs. It is believed that further development of the applied methods would be
able to improve the solutions and be a step toward using topology optimisation to design
ﬂow machines for industrial use. This thesis has chosen to focus on axial ﬂow machines,
but the described methods can be formulated to also be applicable for other types.
The presented work is seen as contributing to the current applications of topology optimi-
sation, speciﬁcally to the area of ﬂow machinery.
7.1
Further work
The actual performance of the designs need to be assessed, if used in a ﬂow the design was
not speciﬁcally optimised for, as the performance of the designs are only evaluated by the
topology optimisation code in this thesis.
It is also recommended to investigate the beneﬁts of additional constraints that are designed
to reduce rotational vibrations of an operating impeller, and methods that ensure the
solutions to have connected design features.
If the designs are to be designed for operational use, the structural tensions in the impeller
or something related will need to be accounted for to ensure the structural strength of the
design.
