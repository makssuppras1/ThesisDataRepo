Abstract
This master thesis investigates the reliability of the Peristeen Plus product by combinВӯ
ing experimental material characterization with Finite Element Analysis (FEA) robustness
evaluation. The study focuses on addressing customer feedback regarding leaks in the
product, specifically the deflation or loss of air in the balloon component. The research
involves a detailed examination of the materials used in the product, primarily silicone
rubber and polypropylene plastic, and their behavior under various conditions. Tensile
tests and simulations are conducted to develop and validate material models, which are
then applied to simulate the interface between the silicone tubes and plastic stubs in the
control unit. The findings reveal that while the nominal design of the interface is airtight,
tolerances in the silicone tubes can lead to significant deformation of the silicone tubes
and potential leakage. Recommendations for redesigning the interface to ensure airВӯ
tightness are provided, along with suggestions for future work involving virtual design of
experiments to optimize the product further.

Introduction
This MasterвҖҷs thesis investigates the reliability of the Coloplast Peristeen Plus device, foВӯ
cusing on customer complaints of air leakage from the balloon. Chapter 1 provides an
overview of the company, the patients, and the device, establishing the context for the
study. Chapter 2 details the initial product investigation, which includes a water immerВӯ
sion leak test. Based on the findings, a hypothesis is formulated, and the scope and
procedures for the research are defined.
The relevant theory on hyperВӯelastic and viscoВӯelastic material behavior, as well as Finite
Element Analysis (FEA), is reviewed in Chapter 3 to provide the foundation for the study.
Chapter 4 focuses on the development and evaluation of a material model, including tenВӯ
sile testing, data processing, and model calibration. In Chapter 5, the material model is
applied to simulate the interface, evaluating potential leakage scenarios under various
conditions.
Finally, Chapter 6 provides an overall discussion of the findings, Chapter 7 concludes
the study with recommendations for Coloplast, and Chapter 8 suggests future work and
improvements to enhance product reliability further.
1.1
Company
Coloplast is a multinational company headquartered in HumlebГҰk, Denmark. Founded in
1957, it specializes in products and services designed to improve the lives of people with
private medical conditions. Coloplast focuses on areas such as Ostomy Care, Continence
Care, Wound and Skin Care, Interventional Urology, and Voice and Respiratory Care,

1.2
Patients
The Peristeen Plus in focus relates to patients requiring continence care, specifically indiВӯ
viduals with neurogenic bowel dysfunction (NBD), which includes constipation and fecal
incontinence. This dysfunction occurs in the majority of patients with chronic neurological
diseases such as multiple sclerosis, spinal cord injury, and ParkinsonвҖҷs disease [1].
The symptoms of NBD have a significant negative impact on quality of life, with only 6%
of patients with spinal cord injuries able to achieve a functional bowel movement without
intervention or support. Up to 65% need to resort to intrusive methods such as digital
stimulation or evacuation of the anorectum, and oneВӯthird require assistance with bowel
care. The consequences of this include loss of independence and dignity, embarrassВӯ
ment, anxiety, depression, social isolation, and impaired sexual relationships[1]. FurВӯ
thermore, spinal cord injury (SCI) patients report bowel dysfunction as more problematic
than any issues related to bladder dysfunction, sexual dysfunction, pain, fatigue, or body
image perception.
When dietary changes and laxatives are ineffective in addressing bowel dysfunction, a
Trans Anal Irrigation (TAI) system can be utilized, as described in Section 1.3. Using a
TAI system, which is effective at emptying the distal colon and rectum, ensures that new
feces do not reach the rectum for an average of two days, preventing leakage between
uses [2]. This provides considerable freedom to the user by allowing them control over
their bowel movements and enabling a routine for their toilet visits during the day.

2.1
Water Immersion Leak Test
To investigate the customer complaints concerning the balloon deflating or losing air, an
investigation was initiated. When leaks of air are suspected, a water immersion leak
test is the obvious choice. A colleague from Coloplast provided several devices that had
previously been used for testing. Although the history of these devices was unknown,
they had not been used for anything outside the ordinary use scenario. Furthermore, the
devices had been assembled in 2022, which is 2Вӯ3 years prior to the use in this project.
The test aimed to determine the source of the leakage by pressurized air. To simulate
the deviceвҖҷs operating condition, a pressurized air tube was used in place of the catheter.
This modification allowed for the control of the internal pressure within the system. AlВӯ
though the catheter could potentially be a source of leakage, it was not tested as part of
this system evaluation. This approach was deemed reasonable, as a separate testing
method applied to the catheter itself would effectively identify any leakage. Furthermore,
the connector between the catheter tube and the control panel (B in Figure 1) is identical
to the connection between the catheter tube and the catheter (A in Figure 1), suggesting
that any leaks would likely be detected during the examination of the control panel.
The test submerged the control panel in water, resulting in the emergence of bubbles.
This bubbling was mainly attributed to the hollow volume of the control panel. The deВӯ
vice remained underwater until the bubbling ceased. Following this, the air pressure was
initially increased to 0.225 bar, which is the functional internal pressure limit set by the
Peristeen Plus System Requirements and then to 1 bar, leading to additional bubbles
emerging from the system for some of the devices. The onset and cessation of bubbles
correlated directly with changes in internal pressure, indicating a leakage within the sysВӯ
tem, which is not expected to occur under normal conditions. The pressure in the system
was intentionally turned on and off enough times to verify that it was not merely remaining
air from the control panel cavity.
Subsequent tests were conducted using modified devices designed to pinpoint the source
of the leakage by gradually drilling holes, cutting away material and ultimately removing
the entire top half of the control panel. Findings from the test indicated three potenВӯ
tial sources of leakage: OneВӯway valve leakage at low pressure, leakage from the siliВӯ
cone tubes within the control unit, and leakage from the twisting connection between the
catheter and the control unit.
2.1.1
OneВӯway valve leakage at low pressure
The issue of oneВӯway valve leakage at low pressure had previously been identified by a
consultant hired by Coloplast, with investigations referenced in [7], which demonstrated
leakage at low pressures. During the water immersion leak tests, the same results were
observed, with a significant amount of air emerging from the control unit at a pressure
of 0.01 bar. However, when the pressure was increased beyond that point, the system
ceased to leak.
In the previous investigation, the consultant had contacted the supplier, who stated that
the valve does not close completely until 2 psi (вүҲ0.14 bar), which is considerably higher
than what was observed but aligned with this statement. The supplier stated that the
valve would seal once the airflow was large enough to overcome the inertia of the valve
and move it to block the hole, as only the inertia of the valve needed to be overcome for

2.1.2
Leakage from the silicone tubes inside the control unit
During the leak test, a location that leaked bubbles was a silicone tube that connected the
airway from the pump to the catheter through the control unit, as depicted in Figure 4a.
When separating the parts, it was observed that a press fit was used to keep the interface
tight, with no adhesive used, which could have been expected as it was utilized in similar
cases elsewhere in the device.
Analyzing the CAD drawing, it was noted that the nominal measurements of the two siliВӯ
cone tubes, situated beside one another, were designed to have an overlap of 0.07 mm,
visualized by the dotted line in Figure 4b. This suggests that tolerances might produce a
larger overlap, as the variations in rubber tolerances can be significantly higher than those
typically associated with metals. For instance, a cylinder wall with a thickness between 0
and 4 mm may vary by Вұ0.2 mm.
In the worstВӯcase scenario, this variation could result in an added thickness that increases
the overlap to 0.47 mm, as specified by the ISO standard for the supplier of the silicone
rubber tubes [9]. The nominal distance between the two plastic stubs is 1.650 mm, which
means that according to the nominal measurements, the silicone rubber needs to deform
by 4.2% at the overlap and 28.5% if both silicone tubes are the widest within specifications.

2.1.4
Interface Focus
With three possibly interface leaks identified, a pros and cons analysis of investigating
them further was conducted to determine what would be most beneficial for the user exВӯ
perience and valuable for Coloplast:
вҖў The leakage from the oneВӯway valve occurred frequently, but it only happened at low
pressures, which would not occur during balloon use, as the pressure exerted by the
user is presumably higher than 0.01 bar, as evidenced by this handsВӯon experience.
вҖў The leakage from the silicone tubes was difficult to replicate and confirmed in only a
few devices. However, the interface design had obvious flaws due to its overlapping
tolerance of the two tubes and the potentially inadequate method of keeping the
system airtight, relying solely on the press fit of the silicone tube onto the smooth
spigots. Furthermore, as discussed further down in the text, the relaxation of the
silicone tubing should also be considered.
вҖў The twisting interface occurred in two places, so any potential error could happen
twiceНҫ however, the interface appeared to function more consistently as intended
than the silicone tube interface.
Ultimately, due to the potential for an interesting project and useful information for the
company, the leakage from the silicone tubes was chosen as the focus for the project1.
2.2
Hypothesis
For worstВӯcase tolerances and a threeВӯyear operational lifespan, the silicone tube interface
does not provide enough contact pressure to stay airtight when being exposed to the
required internal pressure of the system of 0.225 bar.
1While the initial tests could have been more thorough, and the certainty of the origin of the leakage could
be investigated with greater precision. A decision was made to move ahead, both to supply Coloplast with
useful knowledge about the silicone tube and its interface, and to allow more time for the technical aspects,
which would provide valuable insights even if the leakage turns out to be in a different location also.

2.3
Scope and Procedure
One approach to addressing the hypothesis is to bring numerous products home and test
their ability to retain air over a threeВӯyear period. However, this method is both resourceВӯ
intensive and provides limited information regarding specific improvements. An alternaВӯ
tive solution is to utilize simulations and virtual design of experiments (vDoE). This apВӯ
proach optimizes the analysis by focusing on critical variables and using statistical techВӯ
niques to reduce the number of simulations required. By combining simulations and virВӯ
tual design of experiments, one can efficiently determine whether the contact pressure is
adequate to maintain an airtight connection.
Figure 7: The process of the project.
This project spans multiple disciplines, each of which could be explored in greater detail as
an independent study. The primary objective, however, is to evaluate the entire process
to determine whether it serves as an effective method for reaching conclusions beneficial
to the company. While this thesis does not delve deeply into each individual aspect, every
step has been carefully examined and sufficiently developed to support a confident and
reliable conclusion. This approach establishes a foundation for future work, which could
refine specific aspects for more precise results.
Although much of the process was completed, the vDoE simulations (indicated by the
dashed lines in Figure 7) were scaled back due to the steep learning curve associated
with the Optistruct simulation program. To ensure relevant conclusions within the time
frame, the focus shifted to prioritizing worstВӯcase scenarios and evaluating their outcomes.
Nonetheless, the conceptual framework for the vDoE has been developed and is detailed
in the future works section (8), offering a foundation for further exploration.

3.1
Material Properties
To accurately simulate the design of the interface shown in Figure 4a, understanding the
behavior of the materials involved is essential. As highlighted in Section 1.3, the materials
of interest are silicone rubber and polypropylene, both of which are polymers.
3.1.1
Material Science of Polymer
Polymers are substances with a molecular structure composed of long chains of repeatВӯ
ing units held together by covalent bonds. These chains may interact with other chains
through weak van der Waals forces, as illustrated in Figure 8. However, the extent and
nature of these interactions depend on the specific type of polymer.

Although both polypropylene (PP) and silicone rubber are polymers, their distinct molecВӯ

such as enhanced elasticity, thermal stability, and resistance to chemicals and wear [12].
PP is a carbon chain polymer with a molecular weight ranging from 10k to 40k g/mol
[13], whereas silicone rubber consists of alternating silicon and oxygen atoms, with chain
lengths and molecular weight significantly larger than PP, at a molecular weight ranging
from 450k to 700k g/mol [14]. The chain length affects the material properties in various
ways, such as material strength, durability, flexibility, melting point, and more.
In the project, the effects of longВӯterm storage and inВӯuse conditions are investigated.
Compared to metals, polymers exhibit a unique behavior that must be considered. When
a polymer is subjected to constant strain, the stress within the material decreases over
time. This process, known as stress relaxation, occurs because the long polymer chains
can move and rearrange themselves, gradually dissipating the applied stress.
Additionally, silicone rubber exhibits partial crystallization due to its unique polymer strucВӯ
ture. This crystallization occurs as parts of the polymer chains align and pack into an
ordered arrangement under certain conditions. The crystallized regions act as physiВӯ
cal crossВӯlinks that restrict the motion of the polymer chains, giving silicone rubber its

viscoplastic behavior. Unlike viscoelastic materials, which return entirely to their origiВӯ
nal shape after the removal of stress, viscoplastic materials like silicone rubber exhibit a
combination of reversible deformation (elasticity) and irreversible deformation (plasticity).
The crystallization is also influenced by factors such as temperature, strain rate, and the
specific formulation of the silicone rubber, further contributing to its unique mechanical
properties.
This behavior is closely tied to the concept of entropy, which measures the disorder or
randomness within a system. Over time, the molecular chains in a polymer rearrange
themselves into configurations with higher entropy, allowing the material to minimize its
internal energy. This entropyВӯdriven process is especially pronounced in rubber materiВӯ
als, where flexible polymer chains readily adjust under strain, contributing significantly to
stress relaxation. The concept of entropy in polymers is central to understanding material
behavior and creating material models, which will be explored in greater depth in Section
3.3.
3.2
Continuum Mechanics
Understanding how the material behaves at the atomic level is important in explaining
its overall behavior. However, determining the materialвҖҷs specific and other properties
is challenging, primarily because studying the strength of a single chain is irrelevant for
the entire material, as the lengths of the chains in a polymer vary from one to another,
resulting in differing strengths. Additionally, the crystallization of polymers varies at the
atomic level.
Therefore, continuum mechanics is essential to model materials at the relevant length
scale. Continuum mechanics assumes that materials are continuous and homogeneous,
meaning they completely fill the space they occupy without any gaps or voids and the
material properties are constant throughout the material. This approach simplifies the
analysis by disregarding the discrete nature of matter at the molecular scale [15].
3.2.1
Connecting Stress, Strain, and Deformation via Tensor Analysis
Using tensors in continuum mechanics to focus on strain and then determining stress
through equations offers several benefits, especially for hyperelastic material described
in Section 3.3. Tensors enable a detailed and precise representation of strain in a mateВӯ
rial, capturing its deformation characteristics. Once the strain is accurately represented,
constitutive equations can be used to determine the stress, predicting how the material
will respond under various loading conditions.
Because tensors are invariant under coordinate transformations, the strain tensor remains
consistent regardless of the coordinate system, making calculations robust and applicaВӯ
ble in different orientations and scenarios. This mathematical framework is particularly
beneficial for modeling the nonВӯlinear and complex behavior of hyperelastic materials,
capturing the full range of material responses. By focusing on strain tensors first and
then using equations to determine stress, the analysis process is streamlined, ensuring
accurate and reliable predictions of material behavior, which is crucial for engineering
applications where precision and consistency are essential[15][16].

3.4.2
Linear viscoelasticity in Nonlinear Frameworks
Linear viscoelastic materials exhibit stress relaxation that is independent of strain magВӯ
nitude, adhering to timeВӯdependent deformation mechanics without considering the efВӯ
fects of large strains. In contrast, nonlinear viscoelastic materials demonstrate strainВӯ
dependent relaxation behavior, where the relaxation response evolves with changes in
the applied strain level.
To incorporate the nonlinear characteristics of elastomerВӯlike materials, the linear visВӯ
coelastic Prony series (Section 3.4.1) can be combined with the ArrudaВӯBoyce EightВӯ
Chain model (Section 3.3.2). This approach integrates the nonlinear elastic behavior
of the EightВӯChain model with the timeВӯdependent relaxation effects described by the
Prony series. While the Prony series itself is rooted in linear viscoelasticity, embedding
it within the nonlinear ArrudaВӯBoyce framework introduces strainВӯdependent relaxation.
Over time, this results in stress reduction and relaxation behavior influenced by the apВӯ
plied strain, which can resemble nonlinear behavior during tensile tests.
Though this combination effectively represents the materialвҖҷs timeВӯdependent and elastic
properties, it does not capture strain rate effects. Additionally, while the superposition
principle inherent to the Prony series aids in fitting experimental data, the true relaxation
behavior of rubber is often better described using a fully nonlinear viscoplastic model.
One such example is the BergstrГ¶mВӯBoyce (BB) model, which extends the ArrudaВӯBoyce
framework by explicitly incorporating nonlinear viscoelastic effects.

3.5.2
Implicit Solver
The implicit solver also uses direct integration methods, although in the implicit solver, the
solution at the next time step is calculated by solving an equation that involves both the
current and next time step values [25], by solving the equilibrium equations at each time
step [15]. A common implicit solver is the backward Euler method:
un+1 = un + вҲҶt В· f(un+1)
(37)
Where un is the solution at the current time step, вҲҶt is the time step size, and f(un+1) is the
function representing the systemвҖҷs dynamics evaluated at the next time step. Solving this
equation usually requires iterative methods, since un+1 is implicitly involved in f(un+1).
The system dynamics can be determined based on the external forces described in EquaВӯ
tion (32).
In an implicit solver, the known values are the solution of the current step and the external
forces at the next time step. Therefore, a solver is needed to handle the K, C, and M
matrices in Equation (32). Due to the assumption that the mass of the elements remains
constant, M is treated as a constant matrix. Since the simulation in this case is assumed
to be quasiВӯstatic, meaning the dampening effects are negligible compared to the stiffness
forces, the damping matrix C can be set to 0, as velocity and damping effects are negligible
in such conditions. This simplifies the problem, leaving the stiffness matrix K as the
primary component that needs to be solved.
The stiffness matrix represents interactions across all degrees of freedom (DOF) in the
system. Unlike an explicit solver, which can analyze each elementвҖҷs stiffness matrix indiВӯ
vidually based on its neighboring elements, the implicit solver requires the global stiffness
matrix to solve Equation (32). Each node in the system has m degrees of freedom (e.g.,
3 DOF for solid elements: translation in x, y, and z), making the total number of DOF
proportional to the number of nodes, m В· n. Since the stiffness matrix describes how each
(DOF) influences every other DOF in the system, it is a square matrix with dimensions
mВ·nГ—mВ·n [25]. Solving the global stiffness matrix is computationally intensive, and its size
grows quadratically as the number of nodes increases, making the process exponentially
more challenging with finer meshes or larger models.
Key Characteristics
вҖў Stability: Implicit solvers are unconditionally stable, meaning they can handle larger
time steps without the risk of numerical instability (although accuracy will suffer) [25].
вҖў Computational Cost: Implicit solvers involve solving a system of equations at each
time step, which can be computationally intensive, but since the solver is uncondiВӯ
tionally stable, the individual time steps may be large
вҖў Accuracy: Implicit methods offer improved accuracy for longВӯterm simulations, as
they are less inclined to accumulate errors over many time steps. However, the
accuracy can depend on the time step size and the specific problem being solved.
Implicit solvers do not have a critical timestep constraint like explicit solvers. Their stability
allows for time steps to be determined based on possible convergence, making them

3.5.3
Best solver in regard to the project
With the characteristics of implicit and explicit solvers outlined, the task is to determine the
best solver for this projectвҖҷs simulations, which are detailed in Section 5. To summarize:
Two tubes are pulled over two stubs within one second, resulting in deformation and
overlap with the stubs. The movement then ceases, and the system remains at a standstill
for the equivalent of three years in real time to analyze the stresses.
The pulling phase, characterized by sliding contact and large geometrical deformations, is
better suited to explicit solvers due to their efficiency with small time steps. Implicit solvers,
although accurate, are less ideal during this phase as they require frequent updates to
the stiffness matrix, resulting in higher computational complexity and reduced efficiency.
In contrast, the standstill phase involves prolonged silicone relaxation, making implicit
solvers the optimal choice. Their ability to handle large time steps ensures stability and
efficiency over extended periods. Explicit solvers, on the other hand, would necessitate
an impractically high number of small time steps to maintain stability.

Development and Evaluation of Material Model
To develop the material model, experimental data are first extracted from a tensile test,
then used to derive material parameters. Then, simulations using those parameters are
compared to the experimental data to verify the quality of the material model.
The interface under consideration in this section consists of a PP stub and a silicone rubВӯ
ber tube. Given that the stiffness of the PP is significantly higher than that of the silicone
rubber, and that the silicone rubber can undergo large deformations, the properties of the
silicone rubber will influence the contact pressure between the two interfaces, making it
the primary focus of this study. The most critical property of the silicone rubber is its tanВӯ
gential modulus during hyperelastic extension and the extent of relaxation it experiences
over prolonged periods of elongation.
The data is fed into MCalibrate, which uses the acquired data to fit a model, such as the
BergstrГ¶mВӯBoyce model or the Prony series model, as described in section 3.4.2. These
models are selected for their ability to depict the hyperelastic and viscoelastic behavior of
silicone rubber accurately. The models are subsequently imported into Altair Hyperworks
to serve as the material characteristics for the silicone rubber in the simulation.
To validate the accuracy of the material model, a simulation representing the original tenВӯ
sile test is conducted in Altair Optistruct, to replicate the test and compare the results,
ensuring that no errors occur in interpreting the test data or incorporating that interpretaВӯ
tion into the FEA program.

5.1
Modeling the assembly and relaxation of the interface.
To model the assembly, two methods were considered. One was to expand the silicone
tubes in the radial direction using boundary conditions to increase the diameter of the
stubs and start the simulation with the stub being located at the correct axial position after
the displacement by the boundary conditions, the contact condition between the tubes and
the stubs are introduced in the simulation. This approach offers the advantage of minimal
movement, making the simulation more compatible with an implicit solver. However, imВӯ
plementation proved challenging due to the transition from Cartesian to polar coordinates
and the complexity of setting up initial deformation conditions6.
Therefore, the second method was employed. In the second method, the tubes began
from a position with no contact with the stub at the outset. They were pulled down at a
constant velocity over the stubs, which facilitated an easy start to the simulation since
there would be no contacts. A downside to this method is that it does not suit an implicit
solver with contacting parts, as discussed in Section 3.5.2, as the stiffness matrix needs
to be recalculated frequently. However, the benefit of this simulation is that it reflects the
real scenario of mounting the tubes on the stubs.

5.2
Mesh Convergence
When conducting simulations, it is crucial not only to select the correct equations, such
as the appropriate material models and solver, but also to implement them correctly.
The choice of parameters plays a significant role in ensuring valid results: for instance,
a coarse element size can undermine the accuracy of even the most suitable material
model, while an overly relaxed convergence criterion may prevent the stiffness matrix from
updating adequately to reflect the materialвҖҷs true stressВӯstrain behavior. In both cases, the
results can be flawed, even if convergence is achieved. To ensure the mesh size is apВӯ
propriate, a mesh convergence study was conducted.
Four identical simulations were carried out with the only change being the element size:
1 mm, 0.5 mm, 0.25 mm, and 0.125 mm7. Thus, each 1x1x1mm3 cube of the model
contained one element in the coarsest simulation and 64 elements in the finest simulation.
The reduction in elements also minimize the number of DOF, which has a notable impact
on computational requirements. With finer meshes, the increase in DOF would otherwise
result in a substantial increase in computational power needed to process the simulation,
as each halving of the element length would result in an eightfold increase in the total
number of elements.
Using a supercomputer provided by Coloplast, the simulation time ranged from a few
minutes for the coarsest mesh to around five hours for the finest mesh. The last simulation,
which used an element length of 0.125 mm, included around 200,000 elements, making
it the finest simulation possible.

6
Discussion
With the discussion and evaluation of the material model presented in Section 4.6 and the
simulation addressed in Section 5.4, a discussion of the overall process and the interplay
between the material model and simulation is provided here.
A material such as silicone rubber is produced with wide tolerances due to the varying
shrinkage range depending on the material being extruded. Therefore, designs incorpoВӯ
rating rubber should account for large tolerances, which seems to not be the case for the
control unit. The nominal design introduces an overlap of the two silicone tubes, which
necessitates very small tolerances to function. There is no apparent reason for the small
distance between the two stubs which, in turn, causes the overlap. Furthermore, as shown
in Table 1, the airВӯtransferring silicone tubes are, on average, 5% larger than the nominal
dimensions (based on a small sample size of 10).
The dimensions for Case 3, which is clearly not airВӯtight, according to the simulations,
show a crossВӯsectional area that is 13% larger than the nominal values9. It is also imporВӯ
tant to note that Case 3 is not the threshold for when it stops being airtightНҫ the limit lies
somewhere between the nominal dimensions and the worst case.
The material model for the viscoelastic relaxation has flaws. However, the 10% difference
in the material model compared to the physical tests is still small when compared to the
outcome of the simulation of Case 3, which required an increase of contact pressure by
a factor of 5 to become airtight. Furthermore, assuming the stubs are rigid in compariВӯ
son to the silicone tube, increasing the stress of the material model has minimal impact,
since the two overlapping silicone tubes have the same material properties. Therefore,
strengthening the material has little effect on making the interface airtightНҫ only a reduced
overlap will significantly lower the risk of leakage.
Neglected friction in the simulation: In this simulation, the primary focus was analyzing
the contact pressure exerted on the stub in its stationary state. Therefore, frictional forces
have been deliberately neglected. This decision was based on the premise that once the
tubes are static, friction plays a minimal role in influencing contact pressure, which is the
primary parameter of interest. This assumption enables a more straightforward investiВӯ
gation of the contact pressure mechanics without the need to establish friction coefficient
between the PP and silicone rubber.
Neglected material properties in regards to temperature change. The viscosity of SilВӯ
icone Rubber, like other polymers, is affected by changes in temperature. The advantage
of silicone rubber is its long molecular chains, which provide significant thermal stability,
as touched upon in Section 3.1.1. The Silicone rubber investigated in this project has a
temperature resistance from Вӯ60 to +200 В°C (No steam, dry air) [33]. This means that the
material functions over a wide range of temperatures.
To investigate whether temperature could influence the material, an article was reviewed
that examined a silicone rubber with a different additive but similar overall properties,
featuring a thermal resistance ranging from Вӯ62 to 260В°C. [24]. In the article, the same
silicone rubber is used in a tensile test. A strain of 100% was measured at temperatures
of Вӯ40В°C, Вӯ20В°C, 0В°C, and 20В°C, as shown in Figure 44. In the figure, it can be observed
that the normalized relaxation modulus changes depending on temperatureНҫ however, the
closer it is to room temperature, the less relaxation there is. Furthermore, it can be noted
that a decrease in temperature from 20В°C to 0В°C causes the relaxation to decrease from

7
Conclusion
The initial investigation revealed three areas of leakage: the oneВӯway valve, the siliВӯ
cone tubes in the control unit, and the twisting interface connecting the control unit to
the catheter.
Further investigation of the silicone tubes indicated that the nominal design of the interface
between the silicone tubes and the rubber stubs is airtight. However, when the tolerances
of the rubber tubes are taken into consideration, the design becomes flawed, especially
when the dimensions of the tubesвҖҷ inner diameter and wall thickness exceed the nominal
standards. Given these tolerances, the airВӯtransferring silicone tube becomes deformed
to an extent where it nearly loses contact with the stub at the midpoint due to the overlap of
the two silicone tubes. This deformation creates a pathway for air to escape, with the conВӯ
tact pressure between the tube and the stub as low as 20% of the pressure requirement
for the system (visualized clearly in Figure 43).
The methodology used to reach this conclusion involved tensile tests and subsequent
calibration of models for the material properties, notably for longВӯterm relaxation. UnforВӯ
tunately, the most suitable model was not yet implemented in the available FEA software.
Nevertheless, the final model was able to evaluate longВӯterm relaxation relatively well.
Furthermore, the simulation of the interface faced limitations due to the scenario enВӯ
compassing two stages, with neither an explicit nor implicit solver adequately fitting both
stages. With focus on longВӯterm relaxation, the implicit solver was selected. However,
the final simulation results are believed to be accurate enough to confirm that a redesign
may be justified.
The process of specifying materials and analyzing the interface using simulations yielded
valuable insights into the consequences of the tolerances and uncovered an unexpected
failure mode. Specifically, while one would typically expect increased wall thickness to
raise contact pressure when a tube is pressed onto a stub, the region of overlapping
tubes actually decreases contact pressure in other regions, which can lead to leakage.
Concluding that the overlap of the two tubes is likely at least one of the causes of leakage
in the connector.

7.1
Recommendations and observations for Coloplast
From the initial investigations and the outcome of these simulations, it appears that the
nominal design did not account for the significant tolerances of rubber tubes, as even
with the nominal design an overlap is formed. The two plastic stubs are positioned close
enough for the nominal silicone tubes to overlap, with no apparent reason, such as space
constraints from other functions in the control unit. Introducing tolerances into the interface
complicates the design due to the overlap, which can result in a potential leak. Various
solutions to rectify this issue are available:
вҖў Adjust the position of the plastic stubs. Presently, the plastic stubs are nominally
spaced 1.65 mm apart. When the silicone tubes with the most significant acceptable
tolerances (Case 3 in Figure 39) are pulled down on the stubs, the deformed thickВӯ
ness of the two silicone tube walls without overlap totals 2.29 mm. By increasing
the distance between the plastic stubs to 2.35 mm, adding 0.7 mm to the current
1.65 mm, the potential for overlap in the design is eliminated, even for worstВӯcase
dimensions of stubs and silicone tubes.
вҖў If a redesign of the plastic stub is not preferred, specifying more precise tolerances
for the silicone tubes or adjusting the nominal design to have a wall thickness of
0.8 mm instead of 1 mm will reduce the risk of overlap, while still ensuring sufficient
contact pressure for an airtight system. Furthermore, a smaller inner diameter can
enhance contact pressure. However, the lower limit for the inner diameter will impact
the ease of assembly, which becomes increasingly challenging with a reduced inner
diameter.
вҖў Applying adhesive to the surface interface may also lower the risk of leakage.
вҖў If a redesign of the interface is considered, a geometrical seal or a type of clamp
would be advisable. Due to the tapered stub end, there is an increase in contact
pressure at the edge. However, intentionally incorporating multiple geometric exВӯ
pansions in the manner of a barbed fitting could improve the reliability of the conВӯ
nections. An alternative option could involve a pushВӯtoВӯconnect fitting or the use of
clamps, which might significantly enhance airВӯtightness although this may increase
production costs[39].
Furthermore, using an implicit solver presents a broader range of opportunities for simuВӯ
lating relaxation compared to the explicit solver currently employed within the company.
This will be especially beneficial if a request is made to Altair for the implementation of
the BergstrГ¶mВӯBoyce model in Optistruct.
An observation made while analyzing the simulations was that the CAD drawing had a
nominal overlap of 0.07 mm, as discussed in Section 2.1.2. Creating a simulation of the
nominal geometries (not shown in the report) reveals that the overlap in CAD seems too
low, with simulations indicating a larger overlap of around 0.195 mm.

Virtual design of experiments
To reduce costs and time while ensuring that a product functions as intended, design
of experiments (DoE) can be utilized. DoE focuses on varying design parameters in a
way that minimizes the number of tests required while maximizing the insights gained.
In traditional experiments, noise must be accounted for by repeating tests multiple times.
However, in simulationВӯbased experiments, as conducted in this project, Experiments with
identical input will result in identical output results.
A definitive screening can be established, as shown in Figure 46, to identify which tolВӯ
erances should be assigned to each simulation. Referring to Figure 33, tolerances are
represented with Вӯ1 indicating the smallest allowable tolerance, 0 the nominal value, and
1 the largest allowable tolerance.
Figure 46: Design of experiments table (Definitive screening design by SAS JMP), showВӯ
ing which dimensions need to deviate from nominal values and whether they should inВӯ
crease or decrease.
With five factors, a total of 17 simulations are required to generate contact pressures for
17 different configurations. While these pressures can be manually assessed, a single
representative value is essential for the vDoE to effectively evaluate the modelвҖҷs tolerВӯ
ances.
Simulation Results: To evaluate the quality of the different configurations, a quantifiable
number must be derived. One such number could be based on the contact pressure
within sections along the length of the stub, as shown in Figure 47. In each section, the
contact pressure is evaluated, and the section with the highest average contact pressure
is chosen. Within this section, the lowest contact pressure point will be used in the vDoE
table as the result. The rationale is that for the interface to be airtight, it is sufficient that
a single section has a contact pressure that exceeds requirements. Without sections, the
lowest contact pressure would be too pessimistic in the evaluation of leakage, since other
parts of the stub may prevent leakage. Using the mean pressure of the entire surface
could overlook a potential lowВӯpressure area running from one end to the other, as was
the situation for the overlapping cases considered here.

a matching simulation setup would provide extra verification of the ability of the simulation
model to, accurately, model the considered interface.
