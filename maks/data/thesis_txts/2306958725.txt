Abstract
This work presents an analysis of the orthogonal cutting process, based on simple
theoretical models and Finite Element Modeling (FEM) compared with results of
experiments on brass.
The validity of shear angle and chip compression theoretical models was exam-
ined showing that chip compression relationships are able to better describe the
mechanics of chip formation.
An investigation was carried out about the feasibility of using extrusion cutting
as a means to generate metals constitutive equations for use with FEM. It was
shown that simulation results achieve a superior closeness of agreement with ex-
periments than commonly used material models. The extrusion cutting approach
has shown robustness under different cutting conditions.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Metal cutting is a field of paramount importance [1]. Components manufac-
tured by casting, forming and shaping processes usually require further opera-
tion before being ready for use, including many parts made by near net-shape
or net-shape methods. Machining is a general term describing a group of pro-
cesses that consist of the removal of material and modification of the surfaces of
a workpiece [7].
Turning is a process characterised by a rotary movement of a workpiece com-
bined with a linear motion of the tool. Here, a harder tool removes a layer from
the softer workpiece, producing a chip.
It is important to notice that understanding the mechanics of chip formation
is a crucial step to good chip control, a necessity for automated machining. A
lack of chip control, in fact, often leads to rough surface finish, poor machining
accuracy and issues with chip removal from the machining zone [17]. Even if
the process is practically well known and widely used, its description has not yet
reached the point of full explanation[9].
As for the other manufacturing processes, metal cutting has been studied
analytically since the first part of last century. Its analysis contains a number of
aspects which are not common in bulk deformation operations [9].
An important aspect that should be taken into account is the fact that the
object of our observation is not part of the final product but belongs to the chip.
In addition, the cutting process is characterised by particular conditions such
as[9]:
• The process geometry cannot be determined beforehand by the tool. In-
deed, only one surface of the chip is in contact with the cutting tool whereas
the outer surface is not constrained. As a consequence, depending on
the material being cut and cutting parameters, different chip morpholo-
gies and thicknesses can be achieved;
==PAGE:26==
2
1 Introduction
• The process is extremely sensitive to frictional conditions on the tool rake
face. In particular, frictional condition are characterised by adverse con-
ditions for lubricant access;
• The material is deformed under severe conditions of shear strain, strain rates
and temperatures. As a consequence, the conditions are different from
the ones determined by conventional testing methods;
• After the removal of material, the workpiece exposes virgin metal surfaces.
These are chemically very active and give rise to particular tribological
conditions regarding adhesion, diffusion and other phenomena.
Furthermore, the experimental analysis is limited by different practical difficul-
ties because the cutting area is very small, most often hidden, the chip is formed
at high speed and the process is characterised by a large number of parameters.
Therefore, experimental approaches to study machining process are extremely
expensive and time consuming.
For the above reasons, the theoretical analysis of the mechanics of chip for-
mation in metal cutting can be developed on the basis of several assumptions
and idealizations which are still in process of development. In particular, several
simple models from the theory of plasticity, describing the process geometry in
terms of shear angle or chip compression while the friction conditions along the
tool rake face with friction angle or contact length, have been developed but,
generally speaking, the simplifications are expected to be valid in some cases
while they may be totally misleading under other conditions [21].
Furthermore, due to an increasing demand of full understanding and control
of chip formation and flow in modern automated manufacture[9], alternative
approaches have been developed as mathematical simulations where numeri-
cal methods are implemented. Among them, finite element method has proved
to be useful and widely used [28].
Finite Element Method (FEM) is characterised by the discretization of a contin-
uum into small elements. The simple equations that model these finite elements
are then assembled to reach a system of equations whose solutions give the be-
haviour of the overall system.
In the early 1970s, first attempts of machining modeling were presented in
different publications[29]. With the developments of hardware and commercial
==PAGE:27==
1 Introduction

FEM software, modeling limitations and computational difficulties have been par-
tially overcome[28]. Nowadays, FEM is used for gaining knowledge on fundamen-
tal aspect of material removal mechanisms and for their ability to predict cutting
force, stress, temperature, chip geometry, etc., essential for the prediction of the
process outcomes and to guarantee the quality of the final components, without
performing any practical experiment[29].
It is important to notice, however, that material testing for machining model-
ing is particular tricky and, so far, no one has been able to develop a techniques
for studying materials behaviour at high strains, strain rates and temperatures. In-
deed, in literature, it is possible to find several approaches for obtaining mate-
rial data at high strain rates and temperatures (e.g. Split Hopkinson Pressure Bar
(SHPB) complemented with an inductive heat source) but they usually cover only
strains from 0 to 0.3-0.4 while, during cutting, deformations reach values up to 5.
Values beyond test results are usually interpolated.
In this study, a theoretical analysis as well as modeling and simulation using
finite element method of orthogonal cutting are performed.

Chapter four gives a detailed description of extrusion cutting experimental

eling of metal cutting operations. Indeed, using this technique, it is possible to

In order to analyse metal cutting mechanics, two different approaches can be
used, namely Orthogonal and Oblique Cutting. In orthogonal cutting, the cut-
ting edge is perpendicular to the direction of relative motion between tool-workpiece
[1][28], the chip flow velocity is normal to the cutting edge of the tool and just
two forces components, cutting force and thrust force, are acting. Hence, metal
cutting cutting is considered as two dimensional cutting [2]. In oblique cutting,
the major cutting edge is inclined to the vector velocity and, here, three forces
components are acting, cutting, radial and thrust Force. Therefore, metal cutting
is considered as three dimensional cutting [2].
Even dough most metal cutting operations are oblique, orthogonal cutting
has been widely used due to its simplicity and its closeness of agreement with
experiments.

Chip formation [1][9]. Under two dimensional material deformations conditions,
four different types of chip can be identified namely flow or continuous, built up
edge , serrated or segmented and discontinuous.
Flow chips usually assure good surface finish but, as that tend to become
entangled and disturb the machining operation, they can be unwanted. Chip
breakers on the tool rake face are commonly implemented for overcoming this
issue.
Built up edge consists of layers of material that are gradually deposited on
the tool. When the BUE becomes unstable it breaks up an part of it is carried
away by the chip while the rest is deposited randomly on the WP surface. As a
consequence, this type of chip provokes a poor surface finish.
Serrated chips are continuous chip with a non uniform thickness and defor-
mation. It is typical when cutting metal with low thermal conductivity and high
strength.
Discontinuous chips are formed by separate elements that may reweld to-
gether. These morphologies occur when cutting hard brittle materials. Due to
the discontinuous nature of the chips, forces vary continually causing vibrations
and chatter in the machine tool with the end results of poor surface finish.
Figure 2.5: (a) Flow chip; (b) BUE chip; (c) Serrated chip; (d) Discontinuous chip
[7].
Friction in Orthogonal Cutting. Friction plays an important role along the tool
rake face of a sharp tool and on the clearance face of a worn tool [1] because
it influences process variables as temperature and tool wear. First friction law was
introduced by Leonardo Da Vinci in 1490s and, later, it was improved by Amonton
and Coulomb[15]. In 1699 Amonton presented two friction laws [15]:
==PAGE:38==
14
2 Literature review on orthogonal metal cutting
• Friction force and normal force are proportional;
• Friction force is independent of the size of the contact area.
Later, in 1875, Coulomb verified Amonton’s friction law and made a distinction be-
tween static and kinetic friction. In particular, he observed that the kinetic friction
is lower than the static one and nearly independent of the sliding speed.Then he
concluded that the friction is due to interlocking of surface asperities and repre-
sented in the main work of lifting the load over the summits of these asperities[15].
As a result, Coulomb’s friction model is described by:
τf = µσn
(2.21)
where µ is the coefficient of friction, σn is the normal pressure and τf is the fric-

relationship between the friction and the normal stress may not be valid because
the shear stress, τf, cannot exceed the material shear strength, k[3].

shear friction model, expressed as follow[15]:
τf = mk

Temperature in Orthogonal cutting[28]. During machining operations, high tem-
peratures are generated mainly due to the plastic deformation of the workpiece
material and friction at the contact interface tool-chip. Temperature influences
greatly the process efficiency in terms of rate of tool wear, strength of the cut
material, surface integrity and mechanics of chip formation[28]. Three different
main zones can be identified:
• Heat source Q1 occurs on the shear plane and it is caused by material

• Heat source Q2 is localised at the tool rake face-chip interface and is

• Lack of experimental validity. It is important to notice that the relationships

• Lack of theoretical validity. Two strong assumptions have to be made in or-

factor or the friction angle and the minimum energy principle;

• Lack of explicitness. The expression do not relate process geometry directly
to independent variables but through a parameter describing friction con-

Comparing the two groups of relationships, chip compression models are
preferable to the shear angle relationship for several reasons, among which:
• As consequence of the high normal pressure in cutting, assumption of con-
stant shear stress on the tool rake face gives a more accurate description
of the friction conditions than the constancy of the friction angle;
• Chip compression expressions allow an easy description regarding the op-
erating mode of cutting tools with restricted contact length (shear angle
relationships do no take into account RC tool operations);
• Through the description of the friction condition on the tool rake face with
the contact length factor, it is possible to explain the mechanism of lubrica-
tion in cutting without requiring any fluid access at the tool-chip interface;
• With RC tools it is possible to realise practical conditions where the assump-
tion of η constancy (friction constant) is valid;
• When RC tools are used, these relationship describe the process geometry
directly as function of independent variables.
2.3
Restricted contact length tools[21]
In 1925 the German researcher Klopstock introduced a cutting tool for turning
with restricted contact on the rake face (RC tools)[20] which had several advan-
tages compared to normal tools such as lower main cutting force (approx. 15-
25%), lower feed force (approx. 50-60%), lower chip temperature (up to 100◦C)
and longer tool life.
In the beginning, the lack of interest in RC tools was probably due to the dis-
covery of carbides, which came shortly after, that led to a great improvement of
metal cutting processes[13]. Additionally, the classical theories for the mechan-
ics of cutting, such as Merchant, Lee and Shaffer and others,do not take into
account the contact length.

Contrariwise, the contact length approach presented in the simple upper
bound model allows a quantitative explanation of the RC tools. Indeed, when
the reduction of the contact length is realised by using an RC tool, the following
conditions are realised:
• The chip compression factor, λ, decreases. Using Eq. 2.47, the chip com-

are produced;
• The shear angle, ϕ, is higher. Using Eq. 2.29, a reduction of the chip com-

• The chip speed is higher. With Eq. 2.30, it can be notice that a decrease
in chip compression factor causes an increase in chip speed;
• The degree of deformation inside the chip is lower. Accordingly to Eq.2.31,
a reduction of the chip compression provokes a reduction of shear strain
in the chip;
• The cutting forces are reduced. Using Eq. 2.35, the lower the chip com-
pression factor, the lower the specific cutting force;
• Vibrations are lowered, thanks to the forces reduction;
• Stresses are increased. In particular, restriction of the contact length in-
creases the average stress giving flatter stress distribution;
• Chip curl is decreased because of the flatter stress distribution;
• BUE formation is stabilised, due to the higher average stress;
• Cutting power requirements are reduced (Eq. 2.35);
• The heat produced is lowered. As a consequence, lower chip (Eq. 2.37)
and cutting temperature are expected;
• Surface integrity is improved due to the BUE stabilization; Tool life is increased
thanks to the lower process temperature.

2.4
Extrusion Cutting
Extrusion-cutting is a process which can be described as geometrically restrained
orthogonal cutting, where the deformation is fully determined by the tool and
not, as occurred in the conventional cutting operations, also by the property of
the material being cut (Fig.2.15)[22]. In other words, during this process the chip
back surface is constrained by a tool, called shoe, in such way that the material
is extruded through the gap between shoe and cutting tool. In contrast to what
happens in conventional cutting, chips produced are of very good quality, be-
ing shiny on both side and ductile [23]. As a consequence, several investigations
were made in order to evaluate if the process could be used as an alternative
to manufactures strips and wires. In [23], it was observed that if (a) the lathe and
set-up is stiff enough and free free from vibration, (b) the cutting tool as well as

28
2 Literature review on orthogonal metal cutting

the shoe are sharp, (c) the shoe is mounted with an entrance angle of approx-
imately 2◦, (d) at the start of cut, a very small feed is implemented and (e) the
work piece material is free from porosity, the extrusion cutting process can be
carried out as a stable process. It is important to notice that the quality of the
chip produced rely strongly on the tool-shoe gap, that commonly should be set
equal to 1.5. In this case, it was found that brass chips manufactured by extrusion
cutting satisfy the requirements set by standards to strips which are manufactured
conventionally[24]. On the contrary, when the gap is larger, no restriction of the
chip back surface is realised (common orthogonal cutting) while, as the gap is
decreased, partial chip extrusion takes place and fully extruded chip is obtained
for gaps less than twice the feed. If the gap is decreased further, the resistance
of the material to being extruded increases with vibration and foldings. It is im-
portant to notice that, when the tool-shoe gap is lower than 1, side cracks start

2.5
FEM simulation of metal cutting
Metal cutting researches focus mainly on determining the best cutting conditions
and tool geometries in order to increase the process efficiency [28]. Experimen-
tal investigations are necessary to obtain results but they are very expensive and
time consuming, especially when a wide range of different parameters have to
be investigated. As a consequence, over years, an important aim has been to
delete this type of approaches developing several simplified analytical methods.
However, they cannot be used for complex cutting operation and they have
a limited application (their validity depends strongly on the cutting parameters
used). At this point, numerical models acquire importance and, among them,
finite element methods is proved to be useful and widely used[28]. Generally
speaking, Finite Element Method (FEM) is characterised by the discretization of a
continuum into small elements. The simple equations that model these finite ele-
ments are then assembled to reach a system of equations whose solutions give
the behaviour of the overall system. Using FEM simulation it is possible to predict
several outputs that characterised cutting processes such as forces, temperature,
strains, chip morphology, etc. without performing any experimental campaign
[26].
In the following, some basic features of FEM simulation are presented with an
evaluation and comparison of the most commonly used software.

2.5.1
Model Formulation
Three main model formulation are used in metal cutting simulations: Lagrangian,
Eulerian and Lagrangian-Eulerian (ALE).
Lagrangian [29]. With this model formulation, the cells are attached to the mate-
rial and when it is deformed, due to the cutting tool penetration, so is the mesh.
Unconstrained material flow in Lagrangian formulation allows for simulation from
incipient chip formation to steady-state conditions and modeling of segmented
chip besides the continuous one. In particular, in an explicit approach the dis-
placement of the WP and the attached mesh is time step dependant and can
be related to the material removal rate while in an implicit formulation the size
of the time step has no influence on the stability of the solution. An important
disadvantage of this formulation is the high distortion that occurs during the simu-
lation because of the plastic deformation in the main cutting zone. Pre-distorted
mesh as well as re-meshing techniques are implemented in order to overcome
this problem. Furthermore, for the formation of the chip is necessary to implement
a chip separation criterion at the tool cutting edge; due to the fact that the crite-
rion definition is tricky, an updated Lagrangian formulation has been developed
in which the chip separation criterion is replaced by a continuous re-meshing and
adaptive meshing.
Eulerian [26]. In the Eulerian formulation, the mesh is fixed in the space and covers
a defined control volume. The variables are calculated at fixed spatial locations
as the material flows through the mesh. In order to use this approach, it is nec-
essary to know beforehand the geometry of the structure under investigation.
Hence, a disadvantage in cutting is the necessity of knowing the chip morphol-
ogy prior to the simulation. In addition, the chip thickness, the tool-chip contact
length and the friction conditions have to be maintained constant and this not
correspond to reality. Thus, this type of simulation is suitable for steady-state cut-
ting with continuous chip.
Arbitrary Lagrangian-Eulerian [29]. This formulation has been developed with the
purpose of combining the advantages of the two after mentioned formulations.
In particular, the mesh is neither fixed nor attached to the material but it is al-
==PAGE:57==
2.5 FEM simulation of metal cutting
33
lowed to arbitrarily move relatively to the material with an overall displacement
being the sum of a Lagrangian displacement and Eulerian displacement. In cut-
ting simulation this formulation is implemented so that Eulerian approach is used
for modeling the area around the tool tip (severe element distortion is avoided
and remeshing is not needed) while Lagrangian approach is employed for the
unconstrained flow of material at free boundaries.
2.5.2
Meshing
In FEM simulations, the continuum is discretised by finite elements forming a mesh;
each element is simpler in geometry and therefore easier to analyse compared
to the overall structure. The initial mesh of the WP is very significant due to the fact
that the convergence of the numerical procedure as well as the accuracy of the
predicted variables rely on it. Generally speaking, the mesh must be capable to
represent accurately the geometry of the main structure and be able to han-
dle the analysis to be performed. It should be take into account the fact that a
large number of small sized elements enhances accuracy but, at the same time,
increases the computational time. There is a threshold beyond which further de-
crease in the dimension of the elements geometry will increase significantly the
analysis time with a marginal gain in accuracy. As a rule of thumb, a coarser
mesh should be implemented for testing a model and then a finer meshes has to
be applied when the model works. In addition, finer meshes must be used in the
most relevant regions of the analysis (primary and secondary deformation zone)
[29].
In metal cutting, an issue related to meshing is the huge elements distortion
during the process and, because of this, the simulation could present problems
in terms of convergence, numerical errors, etc. This can be controlled by the
redefinition of the mesh after some stages of simulation, implementing specific
adaptive mesh procedure, namely re-meshing, smoothing and refinement[26].
2.5.3
Material Modeling [28]
In FEM simulations, material modeling is of paramount importance. In particular,
the flow properties of the material being cut and the corresponding equations
that have to be included into FEM have been extensively studied. These con-

which the work material starts to plastically deform or flow. The constitutive mod-
els most commonly used are elastic-plastic, elastic-viscoplastic, rigid-plastic and
rigid-viscoplastic. If σ is the stress, ϵ is the plastic strain, ˙ϵ is the plastic strain rate

The main issue in metal cutting simulations is the lack of data from standard
tests, e.g. tension test, for a correct material model definition because, in ma-

which greatly influence flow stress properties. For overcoming this problem, dy-
namic experimental material tests, such as Split Hopkinson Pressure Bar (SHPB)
impact and Taylor’s testing, are employed nowadays. Specimens are deformed
under very high speed, with strain rate up to 105s−1, and temperatures up to
700◦C. However, the results are not sufficient for the deformation behaviour, es-
pecially in high speed machining: values above test results are calculated by
interpolation.
2.5.4
Chip separation criterion
Lagrangian metal cutting simulations require a material separation criterion to
provide real chip formation; the criteria can be grouped as geometrical, such as
the “distance tolerance” criterion, and physical [28], e.g. the “strain energy den-
sity” criterion, a stress-based criterion and a fracture-mechanics based criterion
[31].
According to geometrical criteria, chip separation start when the tool tip ap-
proaches a node within a critical distance. Then, the node is separated from the
workpiece and it becomes part of the chip [28]. Because of the fact that the crit-
ical distance has to be chosen arbitrarily, it does not show real physic mechanism
of chip formation.
According to the physical criteria, separation of nodes occurs when the value
of predefined critical physic parameters is reached at a node or element. This
critic parameter can be selected as strain, stress or strain energy density [28].
Due to the fact that the definition of an appropriate chip separation crite-
rion in front of the tool edge is quite thorny, a latest Lagrangian formulation, an

updated Lagrangian analysis, has overcome this disadvantage by applying con-
tinuous re-meshing and adaptive meshes dealing at the same time with the mesh

2.5.5
Friction modeling
In FEM analysis, friction modeling at the tool-chip interface plays an important
role for a correct evaluation of cutting force, tool wear and surface quality. As
already presented in the introduction, an accurate analysis of the friction condi-
tions at the tool-chip interface is rather complicated. Commonly, three different
types of material models are implemented:
• Coulomb friction model (τf = µσn);
• Tresca friction model (τf = mk);
• An hybrid model, which uses the Tresca’s model in the sticking zone and
Coulomb’s model in the sliding region.
Different coefficients are tested in order to find the values that lead to a higher
closeness of agreement with experimental results.
2.5.6
Model validation
Model validation is a vital step for defining a reliable model. As soon as the solver
has completed the calculations, an analysis of the results has to be done. In par-
ticular, comparison between real and simulated chip geometry, temperatures,
forces, etc. is usually made. At this point, the simulation inputs are adjusted in
order to increase the closeness of agreement between simulations and experi-
ments.

Test procedure
Schematically, test procedure followed for the experimental campaign was:
• Workpiece clamped on the mandrel;
• Charge amplifier turned on from reset to measuring mode;

4.1
Design and manufacture of setup for
extrusion cutting
In order to realise the extrusion-cutting experimental campaign, a second tool
post was designed. Indeed, during the process, the chip back surface is con-
strained by second tool, called shoe, in such a way that the material is extruded
through the gap between shoe and cutting tool. The design constraints of the
new setup were:
• The shoe had to be placed close to the cutting tool with the possibility of
movements up and down for changing offset with respect to the cutting
tool;
• The shoe had to remain fixed during the process;
• The shoe edge had to be parallel to the tool cutting edge;
==PAGE:90==
66
4 Extrusion cutting experimental campaign
• The shoe-dynamometer plate distance had to be minimised since during
the process the forces acting on the cutting tool were evaluated (the lower
the tool-dynamometer arm, the higher the measuring accuracy);
• The system holding the shoe had to be mounted on the lathe and no con-
tact with the mandrel clamping system as well as the dynamometer was
allowed;
• The overall system had to be stiff enough;
Bearing them in mind, several preliminary setup were designed. Among them,
the final decision was defined trying to minimise the quantity of material needed
as well as taking into account the manufacturing process for the different com-
ponents.

Hardness
Since in cutting shear strain of the order of 3 is realised [23], conventional brass
chips are usually much harder than the work piece material, because of strain
hardening behaviour.
On the contrary, extruded chips undergo lower shear
strains, around 1.6, and, simultaneously, a thermal softening because of the heat
confining effect by the shoe [23]. In order to examine this phenomenon, micro-
hardness tests were realised on the extruded chips. In the following, a description
of specimens preparation and methodology used in the analysis is given followed
by the presentation of results.
After having cut chips in smaller pieces, the specimens were embedded in
a thermoset resin, Polyfast, using Struers ProntoPress-20. The heating temperature
as well as the force necessary for the operation were defined by the program
already present in the machine. Later, the embedded specimens were grinded
and polished for a proper microhardness analysis. In order to improve the process
efficiency and quality, a combination of Struers RotoPol-22 and Roto Force-4 was
used. Once the specimens surface was shiny, an analysis using microhardness
tester (Future-Tech FM700) and a Vickers diamond indenter, under a static load
of 25 g applied for 10 seconds, was performed along the chip cross section in
eight different points equally spaced.

Figure 4.12 gives hardness measurements of chip extruded at different tool-
shoe gap. As expected, the conventionally cut chips (Λ=2.7) presented an higher
hardness than the material being cut. Adjusting differently the tool-shoe gap, the

that for a value of Λ=1.2 the chip hardness was similar to the work material one.

should be notice that, while commonly cut chip, because of heavily work hard-

yield stress, i.e. higher resistance to plastic deformation, that is reflected in higher
hardness but less ductility), extruded chips had a lot of ductility left and, generally,
they could not be fractured by repeated bending.

terpolation) while simulations revealed strains around 1.5-2 in magnitude in the
shear zone, exactly where the third material model was properly defined.

closeness of agreement can be obtained modifying properly other simulations
data, such as friction model and mesh discretisation. Influence of the two after

shear angle and chip thickness, with this model the discrepancy passed from an
overestimation of length of contact of 72% to an underestimation of 44% for thrust
force. On the contrary to what occurred in thrust force prediction, the cutting
force was highly overestimated (34%). Tresca friction model with a friction factor
of 0.6 was then tested. Generally, it can be notice that the gap with experiments
was lower than the previous model excluding chip thickness. An increase of the
friction factor to 1 on the whole tool, led to an extremely high overestimation
especially in terms of forces with a thrust force estimated to be more than four
times the real one. Unexpectedly, this friction model was the best in chip thickness
prediction. Finally, the two different hybrid models were analysed. It can be
notice that process geometry prediction was similar for both with a high closeness
of agreement with experiments in terms of length of contact. Looking at forces,
while the first models led to very good prediction, especially for thrust force, the
second one presented a bigger deviation (30% for cutting force, 47% for thrust
force).
Generally speaking, looking at the results obtained, it can be inferred that
friction model selection, with relative constants, affects more forces prediction
rather than process geometry.

5.4.5
Discussion on FEM
From results obtained, it is possible to conclude that no perfect simulations can be
obtained. However, it has to be noticed that, with preliminary simulations aimed
to the definition of appropriate FEM modeling precautions, very good closeness
of agreement between real and predicted data can be reached.
It is seen that flow stress material modeling has a heavy influence on simulated
outcomes, both in terms of forces and process geometry.
Modified Johnson-
Cook material model presents lowest discrepancy from reality while Extrusion-
Cutting model highly overestimates forces, meanwhile process geometry is simi-
lar to the other material formulations. The explanation of why EC modeling leads
to higher forces can be address to the higher flow stress values obtained when
testing material with extrusion cutting [22]. Indeed, Modified Johnson-Cook and
Thermoviscoplastic models constants are based on torsion and dynamic Hopkin-
son bar test (SHPB) over wide ranges of strain rate and temperature but at low
strains (ϵ ∼0.3). It is important to consider that the friction model used in this
analysis was optimised for the first two material formulations (M1 and M2).
As forces prediction is found to be mostly influenced by friction conditions at
tool rake face-chip interface, adjusting properly friction parameters (friction mod-
eling) allows the achievement of higher closeness of agreement between forces,
without altering process geometry prediction. Implementing Extrusion Cutting
flow stress curves in combination with a hybrid friction model, characterised by
Shear friction (m=0.6) around tool tip and Coulomb friction (µ=0.5) on the rest
of the tool, very good results are achieved. Indeed, apart for chip thickness un-
derestimation (16%), the other dependent variables are well predicted (from 0%
to 10%). Therefore, implementing M3 with an optimized friction model, it is possi-
ble to achieve superior closeness of agreement with experiments than previous
material formulations (Table 5.14, Figure 5.23).
Workpiece mesh design is crucial for a suitable process geometry modeling
as well an acceptable computational time. In a process simulation of only 0.003
seconds, discretising the WP with 3000 elements leads to a reduction of approx-
imately 5 hours for simulation convergence, forces prediction similar but an evi-

The assessment of model robustness is of paramount importance as the FEM

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
Metal cutting is a field of paramount industrial and scientific importance. Its anal-
ysis is, however, very complex as the process is based on a group of particular
conditions, e.g. high strain, strain rate and temperature. Theoretical and Finite
Element Modeling have not been able of properly model the process yet. The
present thesis has encompassed an analysis of simple thin zone models as well as
FEM based on comparison with experimental data obtained in orthogonal cut-
ting of brass with HSS tool at three cutting speeds and fourteen different feeds
under dry condition. A step forward in this field has been taken by implementing
extrusion cutting as a way to generate metals flow stress data.
Simple thin zone models, based on shear angle and chip compression, have
been compared with experimental results. Both approaches have proven not to
be able of properly describe the mechanics of chip formation. It has been shown
that chip compression relationships present higher closeness of agreement than
shear angle approaches. For both, the validity of the relative general equation
is limited, as coefficient of determination ranges between 0.82-0.91 for chip com-
pression relationships and 0.67-0.76 for shear angle formulations. Moreover, as
general equation parameters change under different cutting conditions, their
applicability is restricted to specific conditions.
The operating mode of RC tools has been analysed. Their implementation for as-
sessing tool rake face-chip length of contact has been investigated carrying out
friction force measurements at increasing feeds. It was found that the contact
length factor decreases increasing the cutting speeds.
A setup has been designed and manufactured to realise extrusion cutting experi-
ments. A stable extrusion cutting was achieved when using a small feed (0.03mm)
at very start of the cut.
A way of using extrusion cutting as technique for testing material under severe
conditions of stains, strain rates and temperatures has been implemented. The
==PAGE:148==
124
6 Conclusion
analysis led to the definition of a brass constitutive equation by means of which
stress-strain curves were defined at constant strain rates and temperatures. These
were implemented to generate a FEM material model.
It has been shown that Extrusion Cutting material model (M3) gives a superior
closeness of agreement with experimental results than two others material mod-
els already present in literature.
Optimized in terms of mesh design and fric-
tion conditions, the FEM model presents an average discrepancy from reality
extremely low, around 6%, while Modified Johnson Cook (M1) and Thermovis-
coplastic (M2) models present a gap from reality around 14% and 13% respec-
tively (more than double the discrepancy obtained with M3).
In conclusion, it has been shown that metal cutting is characterised by a series
of particular conditions which make its description very difficult. It is suggested
that better understanding of the mechanics of chip formation can be reached
using FEM analysis in combination with the implementation of materials constitu-
tive equations obtained by extrusion cutting process.
In this direction, further steps of this work can be:
• Trying to increase the region in which the constitutive equation is defined,
i.e. cover bigger ranges of temperature, strain rate and strain during extru-
sion cutting material testing;
• Test extrusion cutting process as a means for material data generation with
other metals;
• Try to combine results obtained at low strains (e.g. SHPB) with the ones
evaluated with extrusion cutting.
