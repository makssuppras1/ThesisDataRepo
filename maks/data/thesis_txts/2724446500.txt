Abstract 
 
This study considers a new commercial chlorine dioxide based disinfectant, product 
X, as a potential way to inactivate bacteria in water from Recirculating Aquaculture Systems 
(RAS). The disinfectant was first tested in degradation trials with four different 
concentrations: 0.5, 1, 2 and 4 ppm. Across all concentrations, there was little degradation of 
the disinfectant in ultrapure water. In RAS water, the chlorine dioxide concentrations resulted 
in half-lives from 2-17 hours with the 4 ppm concentration having the longest half-life. The 
addition of 1 ppm product X in RAS water with three different levels of organic matter 
affected the degradation rates and half-lives of chlorine dioxide. In RAS water with the 
lowest level of organic matter, 7.1 mg O2/L, chlorine dioxide had the highest half-life of 9 
hours while the half-lives of product X in the medium and high levels of organic matter, 14.2 
mg O2/L and 28.4 mg O2/L, were 6 and 4 hours, respectively. The chlorine dioxide 
concentrations and levels of organic matter all were affected by the instantaneous 
consumption of chlorine dioxide, which caused their initial concentrations to be lower than 
the expected nominal concentrations. Product X’s ability to inactivate bacteria was also tested 
at realistic chlorine dioxide concentrations from 0.5 to 4 ppm. The four concentrations were 
all capable of killing bacteria and the 4 ppm concentration had killed approximately 50% of 
the bacteria after 24 hours. The organic matter level strongly affected the proportion of 
bacteria that was killed. A concentration of 1 ppm of product X in RAS water with the lowest 
level of organic matter killed approximately 50% of bacteria after 24 hours as well. The 
lower concentrations and highest level of organic matter experienced some bacterial regrowth 
after 24 hours. While the chlorine dioxide concentrations tested in this study did not achieve 
high bacterial inactivation levels of approximately 90%, which low concentrations of ozone 
and PAA have achieved, it may be possible in future trials. It was calculated that a product X 
dosage of 8.74 ppm in RAS water with the highest amount of organic matter or a dosage of 
2.2 ppm in RAS water with the lowest amount of organic matter may kill 90% of the bacteria. 
The addition of membrane filtration or foam fractionation in the RAS facility could help filter 
out the organic matter so less disinfectant is required.

Introduction 
Aquaculture and Fish Diseases 
 
Aquaculture is being viewed as a potential solution to a rapidly growing world 
population. Although, the concept of aquaculture is not new. Aquaculture has been providing 
food for communities since 3000 BC. The difference is that previously, aquaculture provided 
a small contribution while fisheries brought in the majority of the aquatic resources. In 
current times, aquaculture provides more than half of the world’s fish for human consumption 
and production of fish from aquaculture is expected to rise further in the coming years (FAO, 
2022) (Figure 1). In 2030, it is predicted that aquaculture production will have a 22% growth 
increase or about 19 million tons more of produced fish than in 2020 (FAO, 2022). This 
estimated growth in aquaculture follows the predicted growth of the world population.  There 
is pressure on the food production sector to grow along with the world population in order to 
feed everyone, but the fisheries sector has been struggling with illegal overfishing, so the 
limited fish stocks need time to recover before quotas can increase again (FAO, 2022). Thus, 
in the future, the aquatic food available for human consumption will continue to shift towards 
being more from aquaculture.  
 
Figure 1- The amount of million tons of fish that is estimated to be produced in aquaculture and captured at sea in 2030 
(FAO, 2022).  
 
Aquaculture can be practiced both inland and in net pens in aquatic environments. In 
Denmark, there is less of an emphasis on net pen aquaculture compared to inland aquaculture. 
This was due to reports of the natural aquatic systems being polluted with an excess of 
nutrients and disease spreading to natural fish stocks (Skov et al., 2020). After criticisms of 
the environmental impacts of net pen aquaculture, the Danish government implemented strict 
==PAGE:6==
2 
 
rules on the placement of nets and the nutrient release of the fish (Skov et al., 2020). In 2021, 
only 9% of Danish aquaculture farms were sea cages (Danmarks Statistik, 2023). 
Aquaculture, in Denmark, has mostly moved to inland possibilities. 
  
Enclosed aquaculture allows for the possibility of a variety of species to be reared 
because variables like temperature, oxygen availability and light can be controlled. Rearing 
fish on land offers control of the environment and flexibility in location so it can be placed 
close to market, making it more cost-efficient (Liu et al., 2016). Recirculating Aquaculture 
Systems (RAS) are one of the technologies used to grow fish on land. RAS involves 
technological strategies to filter out waste and reuse the water in the system. Not only is it a 
more efficient use of water but it also reduces the number of parasites being released into the 
natural environment (Bouwmeester et al., 2021). Approximately 38% of Danish aquaculture 
farms recirculate a portion of their water (Danmarks Statistik, 2023). 
 
As the degree of recirculation increases in RAS setups, the amount of make-up water 
that is added decreases. A low level RAS usually replaces water at 171 m3/h, while a high 
intensity RAS only replaces water at 17 m3/h (Bregnballe, 2015). Growing a high density of 
fish with little water replacement and a frequent feeding regime can accumulate a large 
amount of organic matter and micro-particles (Pedersen et al., 2017).The organic matter, 
mostly uneaten food and feces, over the size of 40-60 µm are mechanically filtered out 
(Chiam & Sarbatly, 2011). Some of the smaller organic matter gets captured in the biofilter, 
which can reduce the efficiency of the nitrifying bacteria (Guerdat et al., 2011). The rest of 
the smaller particles can become mineralized and dissolved in the RAS water. The dissolved 
organic matter is the perfect substrate for bacteria to grow and thrive on (Fossmark et al., 
2020). The bacteria colonize all surfaces in RAS, including organic matter, fish and the walls 
of the tanks and pipes (Sunyer, 2013; Teitge et al., 2020).  
 
When the bacteria colonize on the fish, the fish’s immune system in the mucus layers 
can identify between the dangerous pathogenic bacteria and the benign commensal bacteria 
(Liu et al., 2018). The pathogenic bacteria are attacked by immune responses from the fish. 
Under the ideal situations, the immune response would balance out the bacterial advance and 
the fish would remain unaffected (Gomez et al., 2013). In less ideal situations, especially 
when the fish is experiencing stress, the colonized bacteria may be the beginning of a 
bacterial infection (Teitge et al., 2020). 
 
Bacterial infections and other diseases are one of the threats that face recirculated 
systems. The high stocking densities of RAS make it easy for bacteria to spread horizontally, 
from fish to fish (Salama & Murray, 2011). This is especially true if fish have body injuries, 
which occur more frequently at high densities. The fish in RAS often come from the same 
hatchery and thus there is a low genetic diversity of the fish stocks, which can lower the 
immune system of the fish (Kennedy et al., 2015). Diseases are often treated with antibiotics 
and recently, there has been a heavy reliance on antibiotics (Liao et al., 2021). This overuse 
has caused an increase in antibiotic-resistant bacteria which could spread to human 
pathogens. The World Health Organization (WHO) has been working to reduce the use of 
antibiotics in the veterinary field and track the disease-resistant bacteria (Liao et al., 2021; 
WHO, 2021).

Disinfection in RAS  
A disinfectant is defined as an agent that destroys infection-producing organisms 
(Yanong & Erlacher-Reid, 2017). Disinfection should be implemented into an aquaculture 
farm’s typical biosecurity routine. Disinfectants can be used to clean an affected area and 
reduce the abundance of bacteria and other microbes, even organic matter. Many different 
types of disinfectants are used in aquaculture. There can be physical disinfectants such as 
heat which denatures the proteins of pathogens, but different microorganisms require 
different temperatures and different lengths of time (Mori & Smith, 2022; Yanong & 
Erlacher-Reid, 2017). Disinfectants can also be chemical disinfectants and many of these are 
used in aquaculture. Some of the most common ones are formaldehyde and oxidizing agents, 
such as chlorine dioxide and hydrogen peroxide (Burridge et al., 2010). Iodophors are another 
type of chemical disinfectant used against specific pathogens (Burridge et al., 2010). 
 
Within an aquaculture facility, disinfection is used in a variety of areas. The 
equipment in the farm including tanks, transport vehicles and all hard surfaces involved in the 
production needs to be disinfected after use (Bera et al., 2018). These surfaces are often 
cleaned by a method of foaming, spray or immersion and usually with detergents or 
iodophors (Sadler & Goodwin, 2007; Wennberg et al., 2022). Chlorine has also been used to 
disinfect the rearing tanks (Boyd, 2008). Furthermore, eggs, when they arrive at the facility, 
need to be disinfected before being integrated into the aquaculture facility. The disinfection 
needs to be able to remove vertically transmitted diseases on the outside of the eggs without 
causing harm to the fragile inside (De Swaef et al., 2015). While many different types of 
disinfectants have been used on eggs, hydrogen peroxide and iodophors are the most common 
(De Swaef et al., 2015; Yanong & Erlacher-Reid, 2017). Finally, the water in the aquaculture 
facility needs to be cleaned. This is especially true for RAS setups, where the water is 
recirculated and there is little make-up water added to dilute the buildup of organic matter 
and bacteria. There are many different techniques to disinfect RAS water and they will be 
discussed in further sections. 
 
The ideal disinfectant for RAS must fulfill the following criteria. Firstly, it must be 
relatively cheap and simple to use by the employees. The disinfectant should function 
effectively on surfaces, water or any area it is applied to (Danner & Merrill, 2008). It is also 
imperative that the disinfectant is safe for the employees working at the facility and does not 
harm the fish in any life stage (De Swaef et al., 2015). The environment should be considered 
to ensure that the disinfectant is not harmful to the surrounding area if it is present in any 
sludge or water being expelled. Finally, the disinfectant should not harm the bacteria in the 
biofilter, because by destroying the nitrifying bacteria, the fish can be exposed to toxic levels 
of ammonia and nitrite (Pedersen et al., 2009).  
 
Current Disinfectants used in RAS 
 
Formalin is a common disinfectant in aquaculture that inactivates bacteria by 
denaturing and alkylating amino acids and nucleic acids (Leal et al., 2018). Commercial 
formalin disinfectant products (e.g. Paracide-F) have been used within the fish production 
==PAGE:8==
4 
 
sector in the United States since 1909 (Danner & Merrill, 2008). In recent years, companies 
have been reducing their use of formalin because of its adverse side effects. Even at quite low 
concentrations, less than 1 ppm, the gas produces a very pungent odor and vapors that can be 
irritating for humans and some individuals can even develop allergies to the gas (Wagner et 
al., 2012). Formalin has often been reported to cause permanent damage in the gills of fish 
and alteration of their mucus cells (Buchmann et al., 2004; Leal et al., 2018). The median 
lethal concentration (LC50) determines the concentration of a chemical in air that kills 50% of 
the test animals in the experiment length so a lower LC50 alludes to a higher toxicity. LC50 
values for formalin are often below 100 ppm for young fish across many fish species (Leal et 
al., 2018). Concentrations of formalin approximately 110-160 ppm have been said to 
temporarily impair the nitrifying bacteria in the biofilter (Keck & Blanc, 2002).  
 
Since the 1970s, ozone usage has increased in the aquaculture industry as a 
disinfectant (Cheremisinoff, 2002). It is a powerful oxidizing agent and reacts with dissolved 
organic matter by electrophilic interactions and generating very reactive hydroxyl radicals to 
oxidize the organic molecules (Sonntag & Gunten, 2012). Ozone can also destroy microbes 
by damaging their cell walls (Ramseier et al., 2011). It is an allotrope, another form of 
oxygen that often rapidly reduces into oxygen. In addition to oxygen, ozone degradation in 
saltwater produces by-products such as oxidized halide species like bromates. These 
compounds are toxic and can be carcinogenic (Gonçalves & Gagnon, 2011). Ozone dosages 
above 4 ppm can cause behavioral and physiological effects in the fish (A. Powell & 
Scolding, 2018). In severe cases, mortalities can occur. Ozone often has an LC50 value of 
below 1 ppm for young fish across many fish species (A. Powell & Scolding, 2018). While 
being efficient against the bacteria in the RAS water, experiments had indicated that bacteria 
can grow resistant to ozone after a few weeks of constant application (de Jesus Gregersen et 
al., 2021). 
Hydrogen peroxide is also commonly used in the aquaculture industry. Hydrogen 
peroxide is a strong oxidizer that attacks the cell machinery of bacteria cells (Danner & 
Merrill, 2008). It degrades into oxygen, water and a hydroxyl radical which makes it such a 
powerful oxidizer (Pardieck et al., 1991). It is not very toxic to the environment or to 
employees using the disinfectant. Nonetheless, transportation, storage and handling of 
concentrated hydrogen peroxide solutions can be dangerous as they can irritate exposed skin 
and eyes (Yao et al., 2017). Low concentrations of hydrogen peroxide solutions of 1% 
hydrogen peroxide or 1 g/L can be toxic to the eggs of yellowtail snapper and rainbow trout 
(Douillet, 1994; Gaikowski et al., 2011). Furthermore, increases in water temperature may 
increase the toxicity of hydrogen peroxide to channel catfish eggs (Small, 2011).  
Peracetic acid (PAA) is another disinfectant that is being used in RAS systems. It is 
very effective against a range of pathogens and even at low temperatures (Carletto et al., 
2022). PAA is also relatively safe for fish and people. Another benefit of PAA is that it 
degrades into harmless compounds such as acetic acid, hydrogen peroxide and eventually 
water (Pedersen et al., 2009). The issue is that acetic acid, an organic molecule, is easily 
degraded by heterotrophic bacteria (Qi et al., 2022). As PAA kills some of the heterotrophic 
bacteria in the RAS water, the compound degrades into an energy source for the rest of the 
==PAGE:9==
5 
 
surviving bacteria to feed from (Teitge et al., 2020). The PAA concentrations above 2 ppm 
can negatively affect the biofilter function, which can cause sudden increases in the levels of 
nitrite (Pedersen et al., 2009). The LC50 values for a few species of young fish including 
rainbow trout are around 1-2 ppm (Straus et al., 2012). 
Ultraviolet (UV) light is another way to control bacterial growth in RAS systems. UV 
light degrades microbial DNA and stops the bacteria from being able to multiply but does not 
have an impact on any non-living particles or organic matter (Ebeling & Timmons, 2010; 
Ultraqua, 2023). UV light can have very high removal rates of bacteria, approximately 90%, 
but only when the turbidity is below 9.9 nephelometric turbidity units (NTU) (Gullian et al., 
2012). As the concentration of organic matter increases in a system, the particles scatter the 
UV light which results in the bacteria at the bottom of the water column not being hit by the 
UV light (Caron et al., 2007). Gullian et al, (2012) found that if the turbidity is very high, 
above 31.3 NTU in their case, UV light may have none to minimal effect on the bacteria at a 
common application level. However, when the UV light was run constantly at 100 mW-s cm-
2, which is approximately three times the value used in commercial RAS farms, it can lead to 
a 90% reduction of bacteria despite a high amount of organic matter being present in the 
water (de Jesus Gregersen et al., 2020). The disadvantage of this is the expensive cost of 
electricity to run the UV light constantly on a high level (de Jesus Gregersen et al., 2020; 
Huyben et al., 2018).    
The process of membrane filtration indirectly removes bacteria by removing their 
food source, organic matter. Many RAS facilities include mechanical filtration to some 
degree, usually a filter size of 40-60 µm, but the system of membrane filtration uses 
microfilters (0.1-10 µm) and ultrafilters (0.001-0.1 µm) (Chiam & Sarbatly, 2011; Guerdat et 
al., 2011). It is proposed that as the filters reduce the abundance of fine particles, they will 
also reduce the bacteria as they have a lower nutrient supply per bacterium (Attramadal et al., 
2014). In the previous experiments, it has been shown that membrane filtration efficiently 
removes particulate organic matter but is less efficient in removing dissolved organic matter. 
Studies found only 2-6% less dissolved organic matter in membrane filtrated systems than in 
control systems and bacteria growth was still visible, albeit in smaller amounts than in the 
control (Bugten et al., 2022; Fossmark et al., 2020). Furthermore, membrane filtration 
requires frequent backwashing to remove the fouling from the organic matter over time (Le 
Clech et al., 2006). Overwise, the membrane efficiency can decrease and damage the 
membranes (Huyben et al., 2018). This leads to the operating costs and cleaning of the 
membrane filtration unit to being quite high (Huyben et al., 2018). 
 
Chlorine Dioxide as a Potential Disinfectant in RAS 
 
Chlorine dioxide has been used in many forms and across many sectors. The gas is 
yellowish in color and smells like chlorine. It does not react with water and diffuses easily 
into water, especially at low temperatures (Gordon & Rosenblatt, 2005).  Chlorine dioxide 
has previously been used in the pulp bleaching industry and combatting biological warfare 
(Bajpai, 2012; Lowe et al., 2013). Chlorine dioxide has also been used extensively as a 
==PAGE:10==
6 
 
disinfectant for drinking water. It is effective against many water-borne parasites such as 
Giardia and the highly resistant parasite Cryptosporidium (Gordon & Rosenblatt, 2005). This 
is what makes it such an appealing disinfectant for fish farms.  
Chlorine dioxide is efficient across a wide range of pH values, from 4-10 (Lenntech, 
2017). This is because it forms a true solution of gas in water (Donohue, 2003). The usability 
of chlorine dioxide over a wide pH range is important in RAS, where the pH values within a 
facility can fluctuate around 7 ± 2 based on the efficiency of the biofilter and the production 
of nitrate (Wambua et al., 2021). Chlorine dioxide also does not react with ammonia to form 
potentially toxic chloramine as chlorine does (Chen & Czarneski, 2021). This is beneficial 
because the fish release ammonia into the RAS water from their gills, the typical total 
ammonia nitrogen (TAN) levels vary between 0-2 mg/L in commercial aquaculture facilities 
(Losordo et al., 2015). 
Furthermore, chlorine dioxide oxidizes compounds and does not produce any of the 
dangerous chlorinated byproducts such as trihalomethanes (THM) or haloacetic acids (HAA) 
(Gordon & Rosenblatt, 2005). Additionally, it does not produce any of the typical byproducts 
of oxidation of organic matter such as aldehydes, ketones or ketoacids (Gordon & Rosenblatt, 
2005). Chlorine dioxide is also able to oxidize iron and magnesium and destroy sulfides and 
cyanides. Hydrogen sulfide is particularly toxic for fish as it prevents oxygen release, thereby 
causing cellular anoxia (Kiemer et al., 1995). Outbreaks of hydrogen sulfide in marine RAS 
facilities have caused mass mortalities and odor problems (Dalsgaard, 2019). Its’ ability to 
oxidize also allows it to control the taste, color and odor of water by removing phenolic 
compounds (Chen & Czarneski, 2021).  
Chlorine dioxide can quickly inactivate many types of bacteria, viruses and larger 
microbes including pathogens in aquaculture water. It has been further shown in experiments 
that chlorine dioxide has a virucidal effect against Infectious Salmon Anemia (ISA) virus 
(Smail et al., 2004). ISA virus have caused huge outbreaks in the past and can cause salmon 
mortalities of over 90% (Rimstad & Markussen, 2020). Chlorine dioxide, at 25 ppm, has also 
been shown to reduce the abundance of the parasite called Ichthyophthirius multifiliis, which 
causes white spot disease in tropical fish by more than 50% (Nunes de MELO et al., 2015). It 
has been reported that 25 ppm of chlorine dioxide can remove even 50% of the amoeba 
Neoparamoeba pemaquidensis, which causes Amoebic gill disease (AGD) in Atlantic salmon 
(M. D. Powell & Clark, 2004). The amoeba causes changes to the gill morphology and can 
lead to gill failure. There have been many AGD outbreaks all over the world (Nowak et al., 
2002). Chlorine dioxide, when tested on agar plates, may effectively inhibit the growth of the 
bacterium Aeromonas salmonicida, which causes Fin and Tail Disease (Jung, 2019). It causes 
erosions and disintegration of the fins and tail and is very common in young fish (Satish et 
al., 2013).

Chemistry of Chlorine Dioxide 
 
Chlorine dioxide is a small, volatile molecule with high energy. It is a chlorine 
molecule bonded with two oxygen molecules (Figure 2). It has one unpaired electron, making 
it a free radical (Rak-Banville, 2009).

Applications for chlorine dioxide are extensive due to its’ one-electron exchange 
possibilities. The equation below demonstrates a reaction of a chlorine dioxide gas molecule 
attacking an energy-rich organic molecule and oxidizing it (Equation 1). The chlorine dioxide 
molecule first accepts an electron and then reduces to a chlorite molecule (Gordon & 
Rosenblatt, 2005).

The reaction has accepted five electrons in total which gives it a very high oxidizing 
power. In fact, chlorine dioxide has two and a half times the oxidizing power that chlorine 
has (Donohue, 2003). The chloride stays in the water and explains why no chlorinated by-
products are produced (Gordon & Rosenblatt, 2005). However, due to chlorine dioxide’s high 
energy, the gas is very unstable. This makes it very difficult to centrally manufacture and 
transport so instead, chlorine dioxide is typically produced on-site (Gordon & Rosenblatt, 
2005). There are alternative ways to produce chlorine dioxide.

The most prominent way of generating chlorine dioxide on site is by a reaction of 
sodium chlorite with an acid and chlorine (Donohue, 2003). Another technique is the 
electrolysis of a sodium chlorite solution, but this technique is very expensive (Gordon & 
Rosenblatt, 2005). A third technique is reducing sodium chlorate with a mixture of hydrogen 
peroxide and a concentration of sulfuric acid (Gordon & Rosenblatt, 2005). Unfortunately, 
this technique might produce a human carcinogen called perchlorate ion (Gordon & 
Rosenblatt, 2005).   
 
Research Question  
 
The goal of this thesis was to use a developed protocol to investigate the degradation 
kinetics of a commercial aqueous chlorine dioxide product (product X) in MQ (ultrapure) 
water, and in RAS water under different organic matter concentrations and examine the 
ability of chlorine dioxide to inactivate bacteria in RAS water with different concentrations of 
organic matter.

Experimental Setup 
Product X 
A company has offered its product, product X, to have it investigated as a potential 
disinfectant for aquaculture facilities. Previously, product X has been used to successfully 
disinfect hard surfaces. The main compound in product X is chlorine dioxide. Unlike other 
products containing chlorine dioxide, product X contains chlorine dioxide in a liquid form, 
not as a dissolved gas. It also remains in its liquid form when used in water. Product X is very 
stable so it can be safely stored for long periods of time.  
The company has a specific way to produce product X (Equation 3). The equation 
was provided in a BSI Standards Publication (BSI, 2016). The production of a chlorine 
dioxide solution in this way results in over 90% yield (BSI, 2016).

Water sampling 
The RAS water was taken from a 20 m3 pilot-scale RAS of rainbow trout at DTU 
Aqua in Hirtshals, Denmark. The system had constant operating conditions of 2 kg/day feed, 
120 L water renewal/day, temperature of 16℃ and oxygen saturation of 95 ± 10% for several 
months before the trial began. The trial has currently been running for over a year with 
similar operating conditions. Water samples of 50 mL of RAS water were taken at the start of 
every trial. To preserve the water samples, 500 µl of 4M H2SO4 acid was added to each 
sample and then they were immediately placed in the fridge for later testing. To assess the 
content of organic matter, chemical oxygen demand (COD) was applied. The water sample’s 
TCOD and dissolved COD, filtered with a 0.2 µm syringe filter, were measured with a Hach 
Lange kit (LCK914). The oxidizable substances react with sulfuric acid and potassium 
dichromatic solution in the presence of silver sulfate as a catalyst and the green color was 
evaluated with a spectrophotometer (Hach Lange, 2023). 
In addition to the four different chlorine dioxide concentrations, three different levels 
of organic matter were tested in these trials. Across the trial days, there was an average 
TCOD of 28.4 ± 5 mg O2/L. This was considered the highest amount of organic matter. The 
second level of organic matter tested, the medium level, had a TCOD of 14.2 mg O2/L. The 
lowest level of organic matter had a TCOD of 7.1 mg O2/L. 
 
Development of Chlorine Dioxide Measurement Protocol 
 
The following protocol was adapted from two colorimetric protocols for determining 
chlorine dioxide (Eaton et al., 1995) (Appendix 1.1). The protocols were similar except that 
one included a glycine solution which was supposed to suppress the amount of free chlorine 
so that chlorine dioxide could be measured. Both methods included using the compound 4,5-
Dihydroxy-2,3-pentanedioneto (DPD) to show the oxidation. DPD is a colorless compound 
but will turn pink when exposed to chlorine or other oxidizing agents because the chlorine 
compounds oxidize the DPD. The amount of pink color, its’ absorbance measured on a 
spectrophotometer, is proportional to the amount of free chlorine present. It is a fast-acting 
technique that is commercially used to measure chlorine in spectrophotometers (Watts, 
2022). 
 The protocols were tested with product X in ultrapure water at a concentration of 0.5 
ppm, 1 ppm and 2 ppm. For the glycine protocol, 0.04 mL of the glycine solution was added 
to a 2 mL water sample and then mixed. The mixture was then added to a separate cuvette 
with 10 mg ± 0.5 mg of DPD powder and 0.1 mL of a phosphate buffer solution and mixed 
again. When following the non-glycine protocol, the same amount of DPD powder, water 
sample and buffer were mixed in a cuvette. The cuvettes were gently turned for a minute and 
then the outsides of the cuvettes were wiped with a fresh cuvette wipe. The cuvettes were 
placed in the spectrophotometer and read at 552 nm. The absorbances were recorded in a 
table (Table 2).

Discussion 
Product X is one of the first products to implement stabilized chlorine dioxide as a 
liquid (CoviPure, 2023). However, the product has mostly only been tested in agriculture and 
drinking water treatment. The aquaculture conditions in these experiments were the first of 
their kind to be performed with this product. The four concentrations of product X were 
tested to understand how the compound degrades over time by considering the degradation 
kinetics of the product and to define what would be a realistic concentration to reach a high 
enough bacterial inactivation in RAS water. The concentrations of 0, 1, 2 and 4 ppm were 
tested because they have been previously used in other experiments, although with chlorine 
dioxide as a dissolved gas (Lenntech, 2017; Olivieri et al., 1986). 
The degradation kinetics and bacterial inactivation were tested in ultrapure water and 
RAS water with different dilutions of organic matter. The dilutions of RAS water were used 
to represent different RAS conditions and had different levels of organic matter with the 
highest level of organic matter being 28.4 mg O2/L. This amount of organic matter is 
consistent with previous studies reporting high levels of organic matter of around 24 mg O2/L 
and 35 mg O2/L. (Fossmark et al., 2020; Henao, Compagni, et al., 2018). The medium level 
of organic matter was 14.2 mg O2/L and the lowest level was 7.1 mg O2/L.  
 
Instantaneous Consumption of Chlorine Dioxide 
The initial chlorine dioxide concentration, measured after 2 minutes of the trial, varied 
depending on the type of water it was in. The ultrapure water resulted in concentrations of 
approximately 0.5, 1, 2 and 4 ppm while the initial concentrations in RAS water resulted as 
0.21, 0.42, 0.9 and 1.77 ppm. Furthermore, in RAS water with the three levels of organic 
matter, there was an initial chlorine dioxide concentration of approximately 0.41 ppm when 1 
ppm was expected.  
This significant instantaneous drop in concentration has been previously observed in 
studies with peracetic acid in RAS water. In water with no organic matter, similar to the 
ultrapure water in this study, the concentration of 1 ppm of PAA has been found to remain 
stable throughout the experiment of 60 minutes (Pedersen et al., 2013). In RAS water, the 
applied PAA concentration of 1 ppm has been measured to vary from 0.75 ppm with 35.4 mg 
O2/L organic matter and 0.4 ppm with 70.8 mg O2/L of organic matter (Pedersen et al., 2013). 
The initial drop in concentration was found to be larger in the presence of higher levels of 
organic matter, at 70.8 mg O2/L (Pedersen et al., 2013). In this study, when using the same 
concentration of chlorine dioxide, there was no difference in the initial concentrations 
between different levels of organic matter. This could be explained by the mechanisms of 
how chlorine dioxide degrades as compared to PAA or differences in how these two 
compounds interact with organic matter. Further experiments should be done with product X 
to chemically understand how it degrades in RAS water with high levels of organic matter 
and what byproducts are formed. 
==PAGE:28==
24 
 
The observed drop from the applied to the initial measurement of chlorine dioxide is 
referred to as instantaneous or spontaneous consumption. It has been described in 
experiments with PAA (Henao, Cascio, et al., 2018; Henao, Compagni, et al., 2018; L.  
Pedersen et al., 2013). The organic matter in a liquid is immediately oxidized when PAA, or 
chlorine dioxide in this case, is added and this occurs across all concentrations (Henao, 
Compagni, et al., 2018). Chlorine also experiences this instantaneous phase within 2.5 
minutes (Du et al., 2020). Following the initial instantaneous consumption, the chlorine 
residuals continue to degrade as an exponential decay function (Henao, Compagni, et al., 
2018; Pedersen et al., 2013). Similarly in our study, the initial chlorine dioxide 
concentrations, measured only after 2 minutes of adding product X, were already decreased 
by approximately 55% of the added concentration, and after that, degradation continued 
following the exponential curve.  
 
For the most accurate results, future degradation trials with chlorine dioxide should 
adjust the initial dosage of product X to counteract the effect of instantaneous consumption. 
An equation may be used to determine the concentrations of product X needed to overcome 
the instantaneous consumption and reach the desired initial concentrations (Appendix 2.2). 
To achieve a concentration of 1 ppm after spontaneous consumption, 420 µl of the 10-times 
diluted product X stock solution is needed, instead of the 187 µl used. The unexpected effect 
of spontaneous consumption emphasizes the importance of completing pretrials with product 
X in both RAS water and ultrapure water so that the initial concentrations in the trials are 
actually what were expected. 
 
Chlorine Dioxide Degradation Kinetics 
 
The half-lives for the 0.5, 1, 2 and 4 ppm concentrations were approximately 2, 4, 9 
and 17 hours, respectively. The half-lives are relatively proportional to each other and that is 
because there is a linear relationship between the length of the degradation and concentration 
of chlorine compounds (Du et al., 2020). In the previous studies with PAA, these proportional 
relationships between the half-lives and added disinfectant concentrations were also found 
(Pedersen et al., 2009). The half-lives for product X are much higher than the ones found in 
literature, which are typically between 20-180 minutes for chlorine dioxide concentrations of 
1-10 ppm (Olivieri et al., 1986; Taylor, 2004). This is due to a few factors. Firstly, some of 
the previous experiments tested the degradation of chlorine dioxide in water with a pH of 12 
(Taylor, 2004). A higher pH and temperature cause the degradation to occur at a much faster 
rate and lead to shorter half-lives (Li et al., 2012). Secondly, chlorine dioxide as a dissolved 
gas in water is much more unstable than product X (Olivieri et al., 1986). In the unstable 
gaseous form, chlorine dioxide degrades much faster than it would in its liquid form (Olivieri 
et al., 1986).  
As compared to PAA, the half-lives of chlorine dioxide were also much longer. PAA 
concentrations between 1-4 ppm all have half-lives below 5 hours (Pedersen et al., 2009). On 
one side, this means that chlorine dioxide, compared to PAA, has the potential to keep 
inactivating bacteria for a longer time but on the other side, it means that the fish and biofilter 
==PAGE:29==
25 
 
are exposed to the disinfectant for an extended period of time. In a previous RAS experiment 
with an addition of 1 ppm chlorine dioxide added to the tanks twice a month, it was shown 
that the disinfectant had no effect on fish behavior and feeding pattern (Han et al., 2016). In 
another aquaculture study, where the fish were bathed in 5 ppm of chlorine dioxide three 
times a day for 3 days, it was found that chlorine dioxide can alter antioxidant defenses in the 
heart tissue of rainbow trout (Tkachenko et al., 2015). Unlike the fish, the biofilter might be 
more seriously affected by chlorine dioxide. Already in the previous study, it has been found 
that with an addition of 1 ppm of chlorine dioxide, the biofilter might experience a decrease 
in nitrification efficiency as the concentrations of TAN and nitrite have been seen to increase 
(Han et al., 2016). Additions of formalin, PAA and hydrogen peroxide to the rearing tanks 
have been found to lead to similar spikes in nitrite concentration around 2-5 mg/L and almost 
complete impairment of nitrification of TAN, in the case of hydrogen peroxide, but the spikes 
are short-term, as in all cases, TAN and nitrite levels return to normal levels usually between 
10 days and a month (Keck & Blanc, 2002; Pedersen et al., 2009; Schwartz et al., 2000). It is 
essential that further experiments are run to test the effect of product X on the health of the 
fish and the biofilter, as the previous studies have used chlorine dioxide dissolved as a gas in 
water and not as a liquid as in this study. 
The organic matter concentration in RAS water was found to significantly affect the 
degradation kinetics of chlorine dioxide. In water with a high organic matter concentration, 
the half-life of 1 ppm of product X was 4 hours. The half-lives of chlorine dioxide in RAS 
water with medium and low organic matter content were 6 and 9 hours, respectively. This 
trend is also seen in trials with PAA but, again, concentrations of product X have much 
longer half-lives than those of PAA, as the half-life for 1 ppm of PAA was 1.2 hours for low 
organic matter, 28.6 mg O2/L and 0.6 hours for high organic matter, 48.8 mg O2/L (Pedersen 
et al., 2009). It is seen in the previous degradation studies with PAA, that the degradation rate 
increases with increasing organic matter concentration in water, the disinfectant staying 
longer in the RAS water or wastewater with lower organic matter concentrations (Henao, 
Cascio, et al., 2018; Pedersen et al., 2009). The longer half-life allows the disinfectant to be 
active in the water for a longer period. This would suggest that low organic matter RAS water 
would require less frequent dosing of disinfectant or even that a lower dosage of disinfectant 
would be sufficient. 
 
Bacterial Inactivation 
There was a very distinct and proportional difference between the different 
concentrations of chlorine dioxide in the degradation trials, which was also expected in the 
disinfection trials, but the results were not as such. The addition of 4 ppm chlorine dioxide 
led to a significant increase in the proportion of dead cells, which was much more than two 
times larger than the one reached with 2 ppm of chlorine dioxide. However, 0.5, 1 and 2 ppm, 
killed bacteria in numbers proportional to each other. The same phenomenon was seen in the 
tests of different amounts of organic matter, where the proportion of dead cells in RAS water 
with low organic matter concentration was more than 2 times higher than in water with 
medium organic matter loading. Meanwhile, the proportion of dead cells in the water with the 
==PAGE:30==
26 
 
low organic matter was indeed half of the number of dead bacteria cells in the water with a 
medium amount of organic matter. It is currently unclear why this disproportionate pattern 
between the concentration/organic matter levels has emerged, perhaps it has to do with how 
product X destroys the bacteria. That being said, all the disinfectant concentrations have been 
shown to kill, in different amounts, the bacteria in the RAS water. Furthermore, there seems 
to be a positive correlation between the length of the half-lives and the number of bacteria 
killed, whereas in RAS water with higher disinfectant concentrations or with lower organic 
matter, which takes longer to degrade, the disinfectant can deactivate more bacteria. This is 
confirmed when other experiments with chlorine dioxide have also established that the 
disinfectant can decrease the number of heterotrophic bacteria in the water (Han et al., 2016). 
Nonetheless, the efficiency of disinfection is dependent on which species of bacteria being 
targeted. Experiments in wastewater have shown that PAA and chlorine-based disinfectants 
do not perform equally against all bacterium types (Hassaballah et al., 2019; Zanetti et al., 
2010). 
 
Other disinfectants, such as ozone and PAA, have been shown to lead to much higher 
proportions of dead bacteria in their trials in RAS water. They both have proven to eradicate 
90% of the bacteria within a week at low dosages, 7 g O3 kg -1 feed and 1 ppm of PAA (Aalto 
et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2018). To reach similar bacterial inactivation levels as those studies, it 
is calculated that a concentration of 8.74 ppm of product X, before instantaneous 
consumption, would be needed for 1 L of RAS water (Figure 10). After instantaneous 
consumption, the initial concentration in RAS water would be 3.9 ppm (Appendix 2.21). 
However, to reach a concentration of 8.74 ppm, it would require a larger addition of product 
X (approximately 1.6 mL/L of 10-times diluted product X). This would, in turn, increase the 
half-life life and would result in the disinfectant staying and killing bacteria in the RAS water 
for a longer time (Pedersen et al., 2009). In terms of a commercial RAS facility with high 
levels of organic matter, using a disinfectant with a longer half-life means less frequent 
applications of disinfectant would be needed (Pedersen et al., 2013). Furthermore, this high 
of a concentration is most likely only needed if there is a sudden peak of bacterial biomass 
(Lazado & Good, 2021). To keep an efficient biosecurity routine, aquaculture facilities add 
disinfectants regularly at a low concentration (Yanong & Erlacher-Reid, 2017). An addition 
of 0.5 ppm or 1 ppm of product X once a week could be enough to inhibit the growth of 
heterotrophic bacteria. However, further tests with a continuous dosing of product X should 
be completed to answer this question. 
 
Chlorine dioxide inactivated bacteria over the length of the trials with all three levels 
of organic matter, but it was most efficient in RAS water with the lowest level of organic 
matter, where it led to the highest proportion of inactivated bacteria and continued to 
inactivate them for 24 hours. If there is a lower amount of organic matter in the water then 
there should also be a lower number of bacteria in the water, as the bacteria feed on the 
organic matter (de Jesus Gregersen et al., 2019; Pedersen et al., 2009). This means that the 
disinfectant is capable of inactivating a larger share of bacteria in RAS water with the low 
organic matter water because there is a lower number of bacteria thus more disinfectant per 
bacteria cell. Furthermore, in RAS water with higher levels of organic matter, product X 
might be less efficient in inactivating bacteria because the organic matter can act as a 
==PAGE:31==
27 
 
condensation nucleus, providing the bacteria with a material to attach to and create small 
clusters around, giving protection from the disinfectant (de Jesus Gregersen et al., 2019; 
Henao, Cascio, et al., 2018; Turolla et al., 2017). When the RAS water has less organic 
matter in it, the bacteria cannot form aggregates as easily.  
 
As discussed previously, a higher concentration of disinfectant leads to a higher 
proportion of dead bacteria. However, organic matter significantly decreases the efficiency of 
the disinfectant (Henao, Cascio, et al., 2018). Indeed, after 5 hours in this study, an added 
concentration of 1 ppm, with a measured initial concentration of only 0.42 ppm after the 
instantaneous consumption, led to an increase of 8% of killed bacteria under high organic 
matter conditions and approximately 41% under low organic matter conditions. The latter 
value is the same as the proportion of dead bacteria that was reached after 5 hours with the 4 
ppm chlorine dioxide in the highest level of organic matter. From the perspective of an 
aquaculture facility, it would be much cheaper and most likely much safer for the fish and 
biofilter to reduce the amount of organic matter in the system and add less disinfectant to 
achieve the same inactivation level of bacteria as with a high concentration of disinfectant in 
a high organic matter system. Membrane filtration and foam fractionation are effective ways 
to remove excess organic matter in a system (de Jesus Gregersen et al., 2021; Fossmark et al., 
2020). In combination with product X, either of these could be an extremely successful 
solution to keep the amount of organic matter and bacteria low in the RAS setup. 
 
The results show that it is important to choose the correct dosage of chlorine dioxide 
before adding it to a RAS facility. Adding too little disinfectant will allow the bacteria to 
regrow after hours of application. A significant decrease of dead bacteria was observed after 
24 hours when using the 1 ppm concentration and in RAS water with the highest level of 
organic matter. The population of surviving bacteria, after the application of disinfectant, 
grows quickly because there is less competition for food (Wang et al., 2021). One of the 
concerns of bacterial regrowth is that the bacteria that survive disinfection and multiply will 
be resistant against stress factors, meaning that it will become much harder to remove them in 
future attempts of disinfection (Michael et al., 2020). Another concern is antibiotic-resistant 
bacteria. If a pathogenic bacteria community, carrying antibiotic-resistance genes, is not 
completely eradicated with a disinfectant then they have the potential of infecting the fish and 
making it exceedingly difficult for farmers to remove the infection with antibiotics (Wang et 
al., 2021). It is very important that a sufficient amount of disinfectant is added so that the 
heterotrophic bacteria are completely inactivated. Further tests are needed to determine 
product X’s ability to accomplish this without harming the fish or the nitrifying bacteria in 
the biofilter.

Conclusions 
 
A novel chlorine dioxide based product, product X, was studied in degradation and 
disinfection trials. It was found that higher concentrations of chlorine dioxide took a longer 
time to degrade and the degradation rate increased with increasing organic matter content in 
RAS water. Conversely, all the chlorine dioxide concentrations remained stable in ultrapure 
water and there was little degradation. More than half of the added chlorine dioxide was 
consumed within the first minutes after application, indicating that this instantaneous 
consumption needs to be taken into account when applying the product in RAS facilities. The 
inactivation efficiency, the increase in the proportion of dead bacteria cells in water, 
increased with increasing chlorine dioxide concentrations and was reduced with increasing 
organic matter concentrations in RAS water. There was a positive correlation between the 
length of the degradation and the proportion of bacteria the disinfectant inactivated. There 
was some regrowth seen in the smaller concentrations of product X and in RAS water with 
the highest level of organic matter. 
 
In practice at aquaculture facilities, approximately 55% more product X should be 
added to compensate for the loss of instantaneous consumption. The increased addition of 
product X will lengthen the degradation time in RAS water which will allow the disinfectant 
to deactivate a larger proportion of bacteria. To kill 90% of the bacteria in RAS water with 
the highest amount of organic matter, 8.74 ppm of product X is needed, with the 
consideration of instantaneous consumption. Without spontaneous consumption, the 
concentration would only be approximately 4 ppm, which was one of the concentrations this 
study aimed to test. As organic matter prevents the disinfectant from efficiently inactivating 
bacteria, only a concentration of 2.2 ppm product X, before spontaneous consumption and 
0.88 ppm after, is required to kill 90% bacteria in RAS water with the lowest amount of 
organic matter, 7.1 mg O2/L. To use product X most efficiently, it is recommended to use 
membrane filtration or foam fractionation to remove most of the organic matter and then 
consistently add a small amount of product X every week. 
It is imperative that further research is done on product X before it is applied to RAS 
facilities. The effects the disinfectant may have on the health of the fish are necessary to 
know. Furthermore, the effects on the biofilter should also be investigated as a drop in the 
biofilter efficiency can harm the fish. An investigation should be completed to understand the 
instantaneous consumption and confirm the amount of product X that should be added to 
RAS water to give the initial concentrations of 0.5, 1, 2 and 4 ppm. Then degradation and 
disinfection trials should be repeated and the disinfection trials should be run for the length of 
the degradation trials to see what proportion of bacteria product X kills over the full length of 
its degradation.

Appendix 1: Protocols 
 
1.1 Glycine vs. Non-glycine in Ultrapure Water 
 
Preparation of Product X 
Pour 1 L of ultrapure water into 3 beakers. Prepare a stock solution of 10-times diluted 
product X. Add 94 µl of the diluted product X into one beaker (0.5 ppm). Add 187 µl into a 
second beaker and 371 µl into a third, making 1 ppm and 2 ppm concentrations. 
 
Glycine Method 
Glycine is added because it suppresses the amount of free chlorine without affecting the 
amount of chlorine dioxide. This method is also only used for less than 5 ppm free chlorine. 
This should be fine for our concentrations of Product X but otherwise we will dilute the 
samples before. 
Reagents: 
Glycine solution: Dissolve 10 g NH2CH2COOH in 100 mL MQ water. 
Phosphate buffer solution: Dissolve 24 g Na2HPO4 and 46 g KH2PO4 in MQ water. Combine 
with 100 mL MQ water with 800 mg disodium EDTA dissolved. Dilute to 1 L.  
DPD: Measure 10 mg ± 0.5 mg DPD powder in every cuvette 
Sample Procedure: 
Take 2 ml of water from the prepared concentrations. Add 0.04 mL glycine solution to the 2 
mL sample and mix. Pipette 0.1 mL of buffer reagent and 10 mg ± 0.5 mg DPD powder in 
separate cuvette and mix. Add the glycine-treated solution and mix again. Gently turn the 
cuvette for a minute and then measure the absorbance at 552 nm on a spectrophotometer. 
Prepare a blank sample without DPD and measure the absorbance as well. 
 
Without Glycine Method 
This is also only used for less than 5 ppm free chlorine. 
Reagents: 
Phosphate buffer solution: Add 24 g Na2HPO4, 46 g KH2PO4, and 0.8 g EDTA (no salt) (E1) 
to MQ water. Dilute it to 1 L of MQ water. Store above 4℃ in a dark room. 
DPD: Measure 10 mg ± 0.5 mg DPD powder in every cuvette 
Sample Procedure:

Add 10 mg ± 0.5 mg DPD powder and 0.1 mL of buffer to the cuvette. Add 2 mL from the 
prepared concentration. Gently turn the cuvette for a minute and read the absorbance at 552 
nm with spectrophotometer. Prepare a blank sample in the same way but without DPD and 
read absorbance.  
 
1.2 Degradation Kinetics Protocol 
 
First Experiment: Degradation Kinetics 
 
Step 1: Gather 4 glass beakers. Place the beakers on magnetic stirrers. Label all the cuvettes 
with the sampling times and add 10 mg ± 0.5 mg into each cuvette. Add 1 L of water to the 
beakers. Take pH and temperature of the water. The different water matrixes that will be used 
in the experiment are listed below. Take 50 ml of RAS water aside for TCOD testing later. 
Types of Water- 
• Ultrapure water 
• RAS water (high organic matter) 
• 500 ml RAS water and 500 ml Ultrapure (medium organic matter) 
• 250 ml RAS water and 750 ml Ultrapure water (low organic matter) 
 
Step 2: Add the appropriate amount of product X to each of the beakers to reach the desired 
concentration. At 2 minutes, take a 2 mL sample from the beaker and pipette it into the 
cuvette. Gently shake the cuvette for a minute. Make sure there are no air bubbles. Wipe the 
sides of the cuvette with a cuvette wipe and put it in the spectrophotometer. Read it at 552 nm 
and record the absorbance. Prepare for the next time sample and continue measuring.  
Step 3: Repeat the trials so you have 3 replicates for the ultrapure water, RAS water and the 
different levels of organic matter. Clean up the workspace. 
 
1.3 Disinfection Protocol 
 
Second Experiment: Disinfection  
Step 1: Gather 4 glass beakers. Place the beakers on magnetic stirrers. Label all the 20 mL 
tubes and Eppendorf tubes with the sampling times. Place cell filters on top of the 20 mL 
tubes. Add 400 µl of 0.2 µm filtered tap water to the Eppendorf tubes. Add 1 L of water to 
the beakers. Take pH and temperature of the water. The different water matrixes that will be 
used in the experiment are listed below. Take 50 ml of RAS water aside for TCOD testing 
later.

Types of Water-

• 500 ml RAS water and 500 ml Ultrapure (medium organic matter)

Step 2: Add the appropriate amount of product X to each of the beakers to reach the desired 
concentration. At 2 minutes, take a 5 mL sample from the beaker and pipette it into the 20 
mL tube. Remove the cell filter and add the appropriate amount and concentration of sodium 
thiosulfate. Gently shake the tube for 15 seconds. Pipette 100 µl into two separate Eppendorf 
tubes.
