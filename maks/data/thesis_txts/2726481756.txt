Master thesis
Abstract
This report focuses on the transient stability analysis of converter-interfaced offshore
wind power plants (WPPs) connected to power systems during large disturbances. It aims
at developing a reduced-order model (ROM) for transient stability purposes. In the litera-
ture, such models exist, but they are only valid for a very simple representation of the grid
connection. The main objective of this project is to build a reduced-order model of a wind
turbine converter with a detailed interconnected grid. The proposed model has an applica-
tion in identifying the synchronization instability between the grid and the converter, which
is governed by the phase-locked loop (PLL) control.
In the first part of the report, an existing reduced order model is investigated and there-
after modified, in order to overcome its limitations. The proposed methodology is to model
the converter and the grid subsystems separately. The converter model remains largely un-
changed, while the grid model is developed as a generalized state-space model in the dq
domain.
In the second part, the proposed model is evaluated with two different grid-lumped sys-
tems. For each grid model, several case studies, corresponding to various grid disturbances
(voltage dip, phase jump, line impedance jump, frequency variation) are carried out. The
model demonstrates a very good match with the Electromagnetic Transient (EMT) simu-
lations, in terms of angle misalignment and frequency, during and after large disturbances.
The model is also tested with a detailed grid model with various voltage levels, that is rep-
resentative of real-world systems. The applicability to various grid models and various types
of disturbances is presented and discussed.
Overall, this report contributes to a better understanding of the behavior of offshore
grid-following wind turbines during large grid disturbances. The reduced order model of
the converter together with the systematic methodology to study the transient stability
analysis of the system are the main outcomes of the present thesis. Future work may in-
clude: finding a more suitable solver that can handle large DAEs systems, defining a more
generalized methodology to study disturbances that happen in the middle of the grid, and
adding more converters in order to represent a wind power plant and not only a wind turbine.
Keywords: transient stability analysis, converter-interfaced offshore wind power plants,
wind turbine, phase-locked loop control, reduced-order models, state space modeling, and
time domain simulations.

Introduction
1.1
Background
The use of renewable energy technologies, such as wind power, is expected to grow
in the near future. For example, it is projected that by 2030, the European Union will
have installed up to 320 GW of wind power, with 66 GW of that capacity being offshore
[4]. However, the integration of converter-interfaced offshore wind power plants in power
systems introduces significant stability issues. To ensure the reliability and safety of the sys-
tem, stricter requirements have been imposed on wind power plants through modifications
to grid codes, similar to those imposed on conventional power plants [2]. It means that wind
farms must be able to restore stability and remain connected during grid faults.
Small signal stability analysis is a commonly used method for determining the stability
of a power system. However, when assessing the stability of a system that includes wind
turbines, it is important to take into account the nonlinearities of the converter systems
of the turbines. Specifically, recent research has shown that the phase-locked loop control,
which is used to synchronize the phase and frequency of the current flowing in the converter
to the phase and frequency of the voltage at the point of common coupling (PCC) with the
power grid, can introduce instabilities when the system is subjected to large disturbances.
Therefore, a more comprehensive approach known as transient stability analysis must be used
to evaluate the system’s ability to recover from such large disturbances. Transient stability
is a method that focuses on analyzing how the system behaves during and after a large
disturbance and enables to keep the non-linearities of the system’s model. Traditionally, the
transient stability of wind farm connections has been analyzed by running batches of time-
domain simulations, using representative models of the wind turbine generators, with their
associated controls and also with the connected power systems. Usually, industries use EMT
(Electromagnetic Transients) models that represent the complex behavior, electromagnetic
interactions, and non-linear effects of the system. However, due to unavailability, black-
boxed, or computationally heavy EMT models, simplified reduced-order models (ROMs)
aiming to resemble the original system behavior must be developed. Some reduced-order
models already exist in the literature. For example, in [1], the author build a ROM for the
WT converter that focuses on the phase-locked loop instabilities due to large disturbances
[5, 6].
1.2
Scope and methodology
The objective of this project is to develop a reduced-order model of a wind turbine con-
verter connected with a generic grid and to develop a systematic methodology to study the
transient stability of the combined system under different large disturbances. The proposed
model will be a set of non-linear differential algebraic equations (DAEs) and will be appli-
cable to many different cases. The starting point is the existing reduced order model of a
wind turbine developed in [1]. The model focuses on the instability of the phase-locked loop
control - responsible for the synchronization of the turbine’s angle with the grid’s angle. In

this model, the connected power system is lumped as an inductor and the Thevenin equiva-
lent is a simple R-L system, which is not representative of a real-world grid. Moreover, the
model is not scalable as the grid can not be replaced by a more complex and more realistic
grid. The proposed model will build upon the existing reduced order model of the converter
but will be able to connect any grid that would be defined as a detailed state space model.
To achieve this, the project will model separately the two subsystems of the large system,
the converter on one hand and the network/grid on the other hand. Then, the two models
will be interconnected, using the outputs of one model as input to the other model and vice
versa. For the converter side, the existing reduced order model will be used as a starting
point: the main assumptions and equations will be the same, but the converter’s equations
won’t be merged with the network equations. For the network side, the grid will be modeled
as a state space model in the dq domain. The two models, both written in the dq domain,
don’t have the same reference frame. As a consequence, the interconnection will require
a transformation of the inputs and outputs to the relevant reference frame, system frame
or converter frame, using the reference frame transformation, which is a function of the
misalignment angle. This modeling strategy will be detailed in the paper. As a result of this
approach, whenever the grid changes, the equations will be the same, only the state space
matrices of the grid model must be updated to reflect the new connected grid.

2.1
Fundamentals of wind turbine converter
With the increasing demand for cleaner energy sources, wind energy has emerged as one
of the most promising options over the past few decades. To convert the energy generated
by the wind into usable electricity, wind turbines use a converter system. The converter sys-
tem is responsible for transforming the variable frequency and voltage of the wind turbine
generator into a constant frequency and voltage that can be used by the grid. Usually, wind
turbines are integrated with the electrical grid using grid-following control systems, allowing
the wind turbine to synchronize its output voltage and frequency with the grid, ensuring
that the wind turbine can deliver power to the grid in a stable and synchronized manner.
There are 4 types of wind turbine converters and type IV has become the most appropriate
one for offshore wind turbines.
Figure 1: Type 4 wind turbine converter structure
The Type 4 system uses full-scale power converters on both the rotor side and the grid
side of the turbine, as shown in Figure 1. The power converter on the rotor side, the rectifier,
converts the AC power generated by the rotor into DC power, which is then stored in the
DC link. The inverter (grid side) is then responsible for converting the DC power back into
AC power at the grid frequency and voltage level, which is then fed into the grid. Both
converters use an inner current control loop and an outer voltage control loop. Moreover,
the back-to-back converter system includes a DC link between the rotor side and the grid
side converters. The DC link capacitor acts as a filter to smooth out any fluctuations in the
DC voltage generated by the turbine and provide a stable input to the DC chopper. The
dc chopper is an electronic device that is used to control the output voltage of a DC power
source (the DC link voltage) by either connecting or disconnecting it from the circuit. It
is controlled by a high-frequency pulse-width modulated (PWM) signal, which adjusts the
duty cycle of the switch. It is generally considered that the DC voltage on the left-hand
side of the grid side converter (GSC) is maintained at a constant level using a combination
of the DC link capacitor and the DC chopper, in a wind turbine type IV system. When
the dc chopper is activated, the DC voltage is assumed to be steady and the modeling of
the machine-side converter can be ignored. The Figure 2 represents the electrical model of
the grid side converter of the turbines. The inner current control loop of the GSC regulates

the output current, that will be injected in the grid, by adjusting the DC voltage via the

the literature [1, 7], for reduced order modeling purposes, the inner current control loop of

Figure 2: Grid side converter [1]
The phase-locked-loop (PLL) is an essential component/control mechanism of the wind
turbine converter in grid following systems. Its primary function is to synchronize the wind
turbine generator’s output with the grid frequency. The PLL detects the phase and frequency
of the grid voltage and generates a signal used by the current control loop to synchronize the
output current with the grid voltage. The PLL control uses the dq coordinate system. The
dq system is a coordinate system used to describe the behavior of AC electrical machines
and power electronic systems and consists of two orthogonal axes, d and q, that rotate with
a speed that is proportional to the frequency of the AC signal. The d-axis is aligned with
the direct component of the magnetic field, while the q-axis is aligned with the quadrature
component of the magnetic field. The Park transformation is the mathematical transforma-
tion from the three-phase domain to the two-axis dq domain. The dq transformation is a
crucial part of many control strategies used in power electronics, such as the PLL control
loop.
A SRF (synchronous reference frame) PLL is commonly used, meaning that the PLL
operates in the synchronous reference frame, a coordinate system rotating at the same fre-
quency as the grid voltage. It extracts the fundamental frequency and phase angle of the grid
voltage (the voltage at the coupling point) and ensures the converter output is synchronized
with the grid voltage. To acquire the phase of the grid voltage, the quadrature axis reference
voltage tracks a zero reference through a PI controller, and the result is integrated. This will
then ensure that the phase angle of the rotating reference frame matches the phase angle of

The output of the SRF-PLL is the angle θdf = θPLL −θPCC representing the misalign-

the grid voltage frequency or phase changes, the SRF-PLL detects the change and adjusts
the converter output frequency and phase accordingly. This ensures that the converter out-
put is always synchronized with the grid voltage, which is critical for the stable and reliable
operation of the power system. The PLL controller has been identified as a main contributor
to instabilities caused by large-signal disturbances, such as severe grid faults [1], [5, 6]. It
will be a focus of the present analysis.
It is worth noting that the converter reference frame (used in the PLL control system)

being calculated, the states can be transformed to the converter reference frame (also called

2.2
Transient stability and grid codes requirement for fault ride
through
Transient stability means the ability of generators to remain in synchronism after being
subjected to a large disturbance, for instance, a three-phase short circuit or a transmission
line tripping, or any sudden change in the system’s operating conditions. Transient insta-
bility can lead to generator outages, load shedding, and even blackouts. It is significant to
analyze the influence of wind generators on transient stability and further develop mitigation
controls to maintain the secure operation of power systems. Transient stability is a critical
aspect of grid code compliance, as generators must be able to ride through transient events
without disconnecting or causing instability in the grid.
Grid codes are regulations that set out technical requirements for power system opera-
tion and the connection of generators to the grid. Grid codes typically specify requirements
for the performance of generators during transient events, including minimum requirements
for fault ride-through, low voltage ride-through, and other stability-related parameters. With
the rapid development in renewable energy technologies, the power generation share from
converter-interfaced offshore wind power plants is expected to increase considerably, which
is likely to introduce challenges in the stability control of future power grids. In this re-
gard, some system operators have started to impose strict grid code requirements, aiming
to operate the WPPs like a conventional power plant [2]. For example, the Figure 4 shows
the requirements of behavior of the wind power plants as a function of duration and level
of the event (voltage dip). In area A, the WPP must stay connected and maintain normal
production. In area B, it must stay connected but must provide voltage support by injecting
some amount of reactive current during the fault. Finally, it would be allowed to disconnect
in area C. This is valid for wind power plants with rated power above 1.5 MW (types C and
D of wind power plants).
Figure 4: Voltage sag tolerance requirements (grid codes) [2]

interactions and non-linear effects.
The current approach in the industry for investigat-
ing the transient stability of grid-connected wind turbines involves using EMT simulations.
However, this practice can be quite intensive computation - wise and the underlying models
are often black-boxed or unavailable. Reduced order models (ROMs) have been developed
to work around the need for EMT simulation, [1, 8, 7]. Their goal is to capture the essential
behavior of the EMT model while reducing its complexity. These developed ROM are then
used for transient stability analysis, by running batches of time domain simulations.

The above explains the structure of the type 4 wind turbine converter and shows the
importance of the transient stability of the connection between a grid system and a wind
turbine generator. As explained, in order to accurately study the instability due to grid

representative of the wind turbine generator and the connected grid system. In the literature

2.4
Problem formulation
The above section (Literature Review) highlighted:
- The general structure and functioning of the wind turbine converter and the assump-
tions generally taken in the literature, as well as the modeling challenges.
- The SRF-PLL control system, which has been identified as a contributor to large signal
disturbances instability and which has been selected as a focus of the present study
- The importance of the transient stability analysis and the method to carry out such
studies, among others: time domain simulations and EMT simulations. It has been
highlighted that analytical models such as ROMs must be validated with EMT simu-
lations (using software such as Simulink). It has also been emphasized that the EMT
simulations can be computationally intensive.
- The importance of developing a reduced order model as an alternative to EMT studies,
and one example of ROM WT model [1], which will be the starting point of the project.
- The benefits in terms of modeling brought by the paper [1], including its high degree
of applicability (in terms of events/disturbances).
- The gap in the literature in terms of modeling and applicability: the model is not
flexible enough to potentially integrate different or more detailed grid models.
The presented reduced order model enables time-domain simulations of the system to
be conducted, even in the presence of significant disturbances, therefore providing insight
into the stability of the converter’s connection with the grid.
However, the need for a
more generalized model was shown.
Hence, the methodology/modeling developed in [1]
needs to be extended in order to integrate a more complex model of the grid side model,
without losing the benefit brought by the existing reduced order model.
The objective
of the present project is to modify the existing wind turbine model by incorporating a
more comprehensive approach that includes the possibility of integrating a realistic grid
system, improving the degree of applicability of the model, and filling the literature’s gap.
This would allow for a more accurate and reliable representation of wind turbine behavior
in grid-connected systems, leading to a better understanding of the stability issues of the
converter-base power systems.

3
Reduced order modeling of the WTG for transient sta-
bility
Reduced order modeling (ROM) aims to simplify complex higher-order models by ap-
proximating their behavior with a lower-dimensional model, capturing the system’s key char-
acteristics and dynamics while reducing the computational burden. It involves extracting
dominant system modes or variables and constructing a reduced model that captures the
system’s key characteristics. This modelling technique has been identified as a suitable tech-
nique to study the transient stability of wind turbine converter, working around the need to
run EMT simulation. Reduced order models of the type 4 wind turbine converter already ex-
ist in the literature [1]. However, as discussed in the preceding section, the identified reduced
order model was only established for a simplified RL grid model. The simplicity of the grid
model in that paper resulted in equation simplifications, yielding a second-order differential
equation to describe the system. In this project, the aim is to design a more comprehensive
model that can accommodate a connected grid of greater complexity than a simple R, L
system. Consequently, the simplifications done in the paper [1] cannot be applied, and a
flexible model must be developed. In subsection 3.1, the modeling approach is described and
the model is built, yielding a final set of equations summarized in subsection 3.2. Finally,
the methodology to study the transient stability of the resulting system to grid disturbances
is detailed in subsection 3.3.

Application to lumped grids
In the previous section, the model’s equations have been derived and a methodology for

Analysis and discussions
6.1
Discussions of the results
6.1.1
Lumped cases
The model was tested with two simplified lumped grid models. Several case studies were
performed for each grid system: disturbances in the grid voltage source and disturbances of
the grid’s passive elements, with levels that sometimes imply a fault ride-through response
from the converter. The case studies consistently demonstrated that the proposed reduced
order model accurately captures the system’s behavior, including angle misalignment and
frequency, both during and after significant disturbances. Some high-frequency dynamics
observed in the EMT simulations are not captured in the ROM. This is expected as the
reduced order model ignores the fast dq control loop. Finally, in all cases, the overall tra-
jectory and the steady state were always matching.
The first case study (R-L system) demonstrated that the proposed model equals the
literature in terms of performance and validated the implementation of disturbances as fast
ramps. The second case study shows that the proposed model outperforms the literature as
it can integrate a more complex grid representation. However, it was shown that the grid
model was not well designed as some oscillations were observed initially, oscillations that
were damped by the addition of a series resistance in the capacitor branch. The two first
cases proved the concept.
6.1.2
CIGRE benchmark model
The last case study was supposed to prove that the model is capable of modeling a real
grid connection, modeled using the CIGRE benchmark model, [3]. However, some solver
difficulties were encountered. The solver chosen was not able to withstand the increased
complexity of the network model, which went from 2 states to 11 states. The model was
implemented in Simulink, using another solver, ode15s.
With this implementation, the model can run and simulate the behavior of a wind con-
verter connected to the grid, under grid disturbances. The comparison of the results showed
a very good match between the proposed model and the EMT simulations, even though some
small deviations in steady state were observed. The implementation in Simulink validated
the model and its applicability to a complex grid. However, there is still a need to resolve the
solver issues and to find a sustainable solution to simulate large and more detailed networks,
preferably in Python.

6.2
Disturbances implementation and applicability
6.2
Disturbances implementation and applicability
The implementation of step disturbances as fast ramp was validated by the two first case
studies of the report. All the disturbances that were studied in the literature were successfully
implemented, and the model demonstrates a high degree of applicability. For disturbances
that involve changes in the converter’s parameters such as the current references or in the
grid’s input such as the voltage source value and phase, the state space matrices remain
unaffected, and the disturbances can be handled flexibly by just modifying, via ramps, the
inputs. However, when disturbances result in variations in certain grid parameters, such as
the values of passive elements, the state space matrices need to be modified. Some types of
disturbances also impact the state space representation of the grid, for example, if some part
of the grid is isolated after a fault. Handling these faults becomes more complex, especially
as the grid expands and the size of the state space matrices increases.
For the first two case studies presented in section 4, the matrices are analytically derived
and described as functions of the grid’s parameters, denoted as A = A(L, ˙L, ...). Conse-
quently, to address disturbances like variations in the grid’s impedance, the corresponding
function is utilized as follows: before the fault occurs, A = A(Lpre, 0); during the fault,
A = A(Lduring, Lfastramp); and after the fault, A = A(Lpre, 0).
However, for future ap-
plications, the grid network model could be provided as an input. In section 5, the grid
network is derived using the method developed in [9]. Consequently, the matrices are pre-
sented as numerical values, making it more challenging to handle grid disturbances with
a high degree of applicability. Simulating grid’s disturbances would imply deriving a new
set of matrices for each grid condition. This is identified as a limitation of the current model.
6.3
Multi converter modelling
The interactions between multiple converters significantly influence the system’s overall
behavior. Notably, the instability issues arising from connecting converters to a power system
primarily manifest in multi-converter systems. Hence, the current model does not represent
the wind power plants’ behavior. Accurate modeling of converter-interfaced offshore wind
power plants necessitates the inclusion of multiple converters in the existing reduced-order
model, which currently only incorporates a single converter. By including multiple convert-
ers in the modeling process, the stability issues can be accurately captured and analyzed.
The incorporation of multiple converters into the modeling framework presents addi-
tional complexities. Expanding the modeling approach necessitates adaptations and modifi-
cations that may not have been initiated in the current model. It also requires the inclusion
of an offshore substation in the grid-side model, thereby adding complexity to the system
representation. Consequently, further research and work are needed to build a model that
includes several converters.

Conclusion
The high-level objective of this project was to address the stability issues introduced
by the integration of converter-interfaced offshore wind power plants into power systems.
The focus of this thesis was the synchronization stability between the grid and the turbine,
controlled by the phase-locked loop. The present thesis proposed a reduced order model of a
wind turbine converter connected to a generalized grid system in order to perform transient
stability analysis of the system under large disturbances.
An existing reduced-order model of a wind turbine converter, focusing on the instability
of the PLL control, served as the starting point for the proposed model. The model had
limitations, as it used a simplified grid model lumped as one inductor, that was not repre-
sentative of a real-world grid. The proposed model aimed to overcome these limitations by
interconnecting the converter model with a detailed and generalized state-space model of
the grid.
The model was tested and validated with simplified grid models. The results obtained
were in line with the detailed EMT simulations in Simulink. It was observed across the dif-
ferent case studies that the proposed reduced order model accurately captures the system’s
behavior (angle misalignment and frequency) during and after large disturbances, making
it a valuable tool for assessing the transient stability of wind farm connections, in various
applications. However, when the model was tested with a detailed grid connection state
space model, solver issues came up and made it difficult to simulate the behavior of the com-
bined system. It was only possible by changing the implementation and choosing a Simulink
solver. The results were good enough and yet again proved the concept of the proposed ROM.
The current model has limitations that need to be addressed. Most importantly, the
current implementation of the model is not as consistent as required, as the solver cannot
withstand large network models. This is a critical issue that prevents the model to be appli-
cable and scalable. Then, it only incorporates a single converter. It is crucial to introduce
multiple converters to accurately represent an entire wind power plant rather than just a
single turbine. The offshore substation may also need to be included in the overall model.
Finally, it lacks flexibility in disturbance analysis. It was indeed observed that for faults
that occur in the middle of the grid, the state space model describing the grid subsystem
changes. For every type of grid fault that needs to be simulated, the state space model rele-
vant to the new grid condition needs to be derived. The implementation of disturbances must
be improved to incorporate changes in the state space matrices in a more generalized manner.
In short, the developed reduced order model contributes to the ongoing efforts in en-
suring the reliable integration of converter-interfaced offshore wind power plants into power
systems. Further research and validation need to be carried out to refine and enhance the
model’s robustness and applicability to various scenarios, iin order to be able to carry out
transient stability analysis to any power system consisting of offshore wind power plants.
