Abstract
Scope 3 emissions, encompassing indirect greenhouse gas emissions across value chains,
often constitute the majority of a company’s carbon footprint. However, calculating these
emissions poses significant methodological challenges due to data scarcity, system bound­
ary ambiguity, and inconsistent emission factor sources. This thesis explores the implica­
tions of using emission factors from environmentally extended input­output (EEIO) model­
ing for these calculations, and compares it to the other main type of emission factor which
can be used to calculate Scope 3 emissions: process­based life cycle assessment (LCA)
and .
Focusing on the French ADEME database and the multi­regional EXIOBASE database,
the study investigates how emission factor structures—whether product­based or industry­
based—and temporal updates affect footprint results. Particular attention is paid to the
differences between product­by­product and industry­by­industry models in EXIOBASE
and to the implications of monetary valuation in emission factor expression. A detailed
examination of the two datasets’ evolution reveals considerable volatility in reported emis­
sions, attributable in part to inflation adjustments and methodological revisions, making it
difficult to identify the effects of wide­scale decarbonization.
The analysis highlights key trade­offs: EEIO methods offer completeness, scalability, and
a sometimes­desired coarseness to match activity data, but introduce aggregation and
inflation­related uncertainties. On the other hand, process­based methods provide gran­
ularity, at the cost of truncation and limited coverage. The findings underscore the neces­
sity of hybrid strategies that combine EEIO screening with process­specific refinement,
depending on the context and goal of the calculation, the materiality of the considered
Scope 3 category, and the quality and granularity of the underlying activity data.
This thesis contributes to the growing discourse on Scope 3 accountability by offering an
overview of the elements and effects to consider for carbon footprint practitioners and
emphasizing the importance of structural, temporal, and monetary considerations when
selecting emission factors. It also calls for greater harmonization and transparency across
datasets to enhance the robustness and credibility of climate disclosures and benchmark­
ings.

1.1
The Greenhouse Gas Protocol
Carbon emissions have emerged as a critical concern for corporations as a result of in­
creasing consumer awareness and the emergence of stringent regulatory frameworks.
The Corporate Standard of the Greenhouse Gas Protocol (GHG Protocol) is the main
existing framework for calculating the carbon footprint of corporations. The framework
classifies emissions into three Scopes: [1] [2] [3]
• Scope 1: Emissions directly produced by the company’s activities.
• Scope 2: Emissions linked to the energy purchased by the company to support
operations.
• Scope 3: Emissions from upstream and downstream activities that support the com­
pany’s operations.
Upstream Scope 3 emissions include categories such as the purchasing of materials and
services or employee travel. Downstream Scope 3 emissions include transportation of
products to consumers and product end­of­life emissions.
To calculate emissions from a specific activity, two types of data are necessary:
• Activity data: The quantitative level of activity carried out or outsourced by the com­
pany. Examples include euros spent on legal services, or liters of diesel consumed.
• Emission factors: The amount of emissions (in CO2 equivalent) associated with a
unit of the activity. For example, kilograms of CO2 per euro spent on legal services,
or per liter of diesel burned.
Multiplying activity data by emission factors yields the emissions resulting from each ac­
tivity. Activity data can be of two types: [1] [4]
• Physical: Measured in physical quantities such as liters, tons, or kilowatt­hours.
This data is often more accurate but can be difficult to collect, as corporations do
not always keep records of physical units.
• Monetary: Measured in monetary terms, such as euros spent. This data is gener­
ally easier to collect since it is embedded in the company’s accounting systems.
Emission factors can also be categorized into two types: [5] [6]
• Process­based: Constructed from the bottom­up by compiling all the subprocesses
involved in the activity with a defined level of granularity to avoid infinite dependency
loops. These factors are usually linked to physical activity data and are highly spe­
cific to the process under study. [7]
• Environmentally Extended Input­Output (EEIO) based: Constructed from the
top down by allocating emissions from the global economy to individual activities
based on sectoral monetary exchanges. These are exclusively used with monetary
activity data. They provide completeness, but lack specificity, making it difficult to
identify emission reduction levers and distinguish between products with different
environmental profiles but similar monetary values. [8]

2.1
Environmentally Extended Input Output Models
Environmentally Extended Input­Output (EEIO) models are analytical tools built upon the
economic input­output models originally developed by Wassily Leontief in 1949. These
models were designed to quantify the interdependencies between different industries
within an economy by examining how much input each industry requires from others
to meet a specific level of final demand. EEIO models extend this framework by incor­
porating environmental data—such as greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions, water use, or
resource depletion—through the use of environmental impact coefficients. This enhance­
ment enables researchers and policymakers to assess the environmental consequences
of economic activities in a comprehensive, systemic manner. [8] [5] [6]
Leontief’s economic input­output model assumes linear relationships between economic
outputs and inputs. This linearity entails three key assumptions: proportional input re­
quirements (e.g., doubling output necessitates doubling all inputs), fixed technical coeffi­
cients that do not evolve with efficiency or innovation, and a lack of substitution between
inputs. Consequently, EEIO models provide a static, averaged snapshot of economic and
environmental relationships for a given year.
For an economy comprising n industries, the foundation of the model lies in the direct
requirements matrix A ∈Rn×n, which quantifies the direct input requirements between in­
dustries. A final demand vector y ∈Rn represents the end­use demand for each industry’s
output. While the first­order demand is simply y, the second­order demand—accounting
for the inputs needed to produce y—is given by Ay, and higher­order demands follow as
A2y, A3y, . . ..
Theoretically, to capture the full indirect requirements across all tiers of production, this
process continues infinitely. However, as a result of the computational infeasibility of such
an approach, it is common to exploit the matrix identity:

To incorporate environmental analysis, an environmental intensity matrix S ∈Rm×n is
introduced, where each element expresses the environmental impact (e.g., kgCO2e) per
monetary unit of output for a given industry. This matrix can be seen as representing direct
(Scope 1) emissions. This matrix is the result of the following operation: F.diag(x−1),
where F is the satellite account matrix and x is the total output vector. [11] The total
environmental burden d ∈Rm associated with the final demand y is then calculated as:
d = Sx = S(I −A)−1y

The construction of matrix A typically begins with the intermediate transactions matrix Z,
which includes multifunctional processes. For example, industry A may produce outputs
not only for itself but also for other industries such as C, often as by­products. These
outputs must be carefully allocated or treated as negative inputs in Z to avoid overesti­
mation. To obtain A, each column of Z is divided by the total output of the corresponding
industry. Likewise, the environmental intensity matrix S is calculated by dividing the direct
environmental impact matrix F by industry outputs. [12] [11]
Additional components of the model include value added—such as operating surplus,
taxes, and wages—final demand disaggregated into household consumption, exports,
government purchases, and capital formation, and a direct emissions matrix Fy, which
accounts for emissions from final users such as households.

The granularity of industry classification critically affects model precision. A coarse classi­
fication may obscure key differences in environmental performance across sub­industries,
but finer resolution is limited by data availability for constructing matrices Z and F. A
product­based approach can be adopted instead of industry­based classification, yield­
ing similar results. In this case, consumption­based emission factors per euro of product
expenditure are derived from the matrix product S(I −A)−1.
EXIOBASE is a practical application of the EEIO framework at a global scale. It inte­
grates macroeconomic data—detailing monetary flows between sectors and regions—
with environmental and social impact metrics, including 126 distinct indicators such as
GHG emissions, water use, and land occupation. The model is implemented across 49
global regions and offers a time series from 1995 to 2022, with real data up to 2019 and
forecasted values thereafter. The current study utilizes EXIOBASE versions 3.8.2, re­
leased in October 2021, and 3.9.5, released in February 2025, with primary data inputs
from IMF, BACI, Comtrade, EDGAR, and IEA. [13]
EXIOBASE offers both industry­by­industry (ixi) and product­by­product (pxp) variants.
The pxp approach is employed in this thesis, which aligns with the use of the M matrix
to estimate emissions per euro of final consumption. The key matrices in EXIOBASE
mirror the EEIO structure: Z (intermediate transactions), A (technical coefficients), Y (final
demand), x (gross output), L = (I −A)−1 (Leontief inverse), F (total production­based
impacts), S (impact per euro produced), and M = SL (impact per euro consumed). [14]
The distinction between the S and M matrices is central to the correct interpretation of en­
vironmental footprints. The S matrix, being production­based, is appropriate for territorial
emissions accounting. It does not capture indirect supply chain effects. Conversely, the
M matrix embodies a consumption­based perspective, reflecting the full upstream impacts
of a product or service across international supply chains. [15] For example, a digital ser­
vice produced in India and consumed in France might exhibit a direct intensity of 0.0015
kgCO2e/ (from S in India) and a consumption­based intensity of 0.0807 kgCO2e/ (from
M in France), revealing a substantial amplification as a result of embedded supply chain
impacts. [6]

In this thesis, the M matrix is used as the default for estimating emissions, especially
when detailed supply chain or supplier data is unavailable. However, in cases where only
partial footprints are needed—such as when accounting for emissions from a company’s
equity­based production—filtered S values may be employed. If both the country of use
and the country of production are known, the M matrix is applied, filtered by the country
of production to improve geographic accuracy.
EXIOBASE relies on the NACE Rev. 2 classification system to define sectors. Accurate
analysis requires verifying whether a sector includes only production or also downstream
activities such as logistics and retail. This is done by consulting NACE code descriptions
or the sector mapping files provided by EXIOBASE or tools such as pymrio. Misclassifi­
cation can lead to footprint misestimation and risks of double­counting.
In summary, EEIO models provide a powerful and mathematically robust approach for
linking economic activity to environmental consequences. EXIOBASE operationalizes
this approach globally, enabling nuanced insights into both direct and indirect impacts of
consumption and production. The use of consumption­based metrics such as the M matrix
allows for more comprehensive carbon footprinting and policy assessment, particularly in
the context of globalized value chains. [5] Still, the following elements are not included
with this methodology:
• Value added: The value added of each industry (operating surplus, taxes, and
wages) constitutes a systematic difference to be accounted for between the value
of inputs and outputs.
• Final demand: The product outputs are fed into the industries, but also go in part to
exports, household consumption, government purchases, and capital expenditure.
• Direct emissions: A matrix Fy exists which describes the direct emissions from
households and other final users.
The total emissions of a country can be calculated in two ways:
• Production­based emissions are territorial impacts occurring within the borders of
the country: dprod = F + Fy
• Consumption­based emissions account for the upstream impacts from all consump­
tion in the country, and therefore disregard emissions from exported products: dcons =
S(I −A)−1y + Fy
The model’s precision strongly depends on the granularity adopted when defining the
considered industries in the economy. A coarse industry classification leads to imprecise
environmental impact results, but data availability to construct Z and F limits the granularity
of the industries.
In all of the above, a split of the economy by industry has been discussed. A split by
product can be adopted instead, yielding a similar result. Monetary emission factors for
the consumption of various products can be obtained from this type of model by multiplying
S by (I −A)−1, to capture the full­scope impacts of the industry.
This is a consumption­based approach as it reflects the impacts across the full upstream
supply­chain. A production­based approach would have been to use the matrix S directly,
thus representing the direct impacts of production. This approach is more suitable to ter­
ritorial analysis, while the consumption­based approach is relevant for footprint analysis.
From here on, the consumption­based approach is systematically considered.

Discrepancies between carbon footprint intensities calculated from EXIOBASE’s product­
based (pxp) and industry­based (ixi) representations—despite both using the same en­
vironmentally extended input­output framework (M = S × L)—stem primarily from struc­
tural and methodological differences in sector aggregation and flow propagation. While
both rely on the same underlying supply­use data and Leontief formulation, pxp matri­
ces allocate emissions based on product­level final demand, whereas ixi matrices are
oriented around industry outputs. This distinction reflects different technical coefficient
matrices and causes divergences in how emissions are attributed through supply chains.
[16] [14] Because industries often produce multiple products with heterogeneous emis­
sion profiles, the average intensity in the ixi system can mask high­impact outputs that
are more directly visible in the pxp framework. [17] Additionally, the construction of the
Leontief inverse differs across product­by­product and industry­by­industry models, in­
fluencing how indirect emissions propagate. [18] Furthermore, the domestic technology
assumption (DTA), which assumes imported goods are produced using domestic tech­
nology, is implemented differently in ixi and pxp structures, particularly affecting sectors
with significant trade exposure. [19] These structural features mean that although the
pxp and ixi matrices technically measure the same concept—consumption­based carbon
intensities—their results are not directly comparable without accounting for differences in
aggregation logic, product­industry mappings, and underlying economic flows.
Environmentally Extended Input­Output (EIO) models do not align directly with the cate­
gorization of greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions into Scope 1, Scope 2, and Scope 3 as
defined by the Greenhouse Gas Protocol. Scope 1 emissions—those occurring directly
from the activities of a reporting sector—are inherently captured in the direct emissions
attributed to each sector within the EIO framework. Indirect emissions from the supply
chain, which correspond to upstream Scope 2 and Scope 3 emissions, are incorporated
into the model via the inter­industry dependencies but are not distinguishable as sepa­
rate scopes. EIO models do not provide a mechanism for disaggregating these upstream
emissions into discrete Scope 2 or Scope 3 categories. Furthermore, downstream Scope
3 emissions, such as those occurring during the use phase of a product, are generally
excluded unless the model explicitly includes such processes. In the case of EXIOBASE,
downstream use­phase emissions are not modeled, which constitutes a significant limita­
tion for certain sectors.
This limitation becomes particularly salient in sectors where downstream emissions dom­
inate the life­cycle impact, such as in the oil and gas industry.
For instance, drilling
activities—typically the focus of sectoral analyses within EXIOBASE—account only for
the upstream component of the industry’s environmental footprint. The combustion of
fossil fuels, which represents the most significant portion of emissions in this sector, falls
outside the scope of what EXIOBASE captures. This methodological constraint has long
been a point of criticism and must be clearly acknowledged in studies relying on input­
output models for emission assessments in such industries.
Another practical consideration in environmental modeling arises when choosing between
process­based life cycle assessment (LCA) emission factors and monetary emission fac­
tors derived from EIO models. In some cases, LCA emission factors may appear overly
precise, potentially offering a false sense of accuracy given the uncertainty surrounding
process­level data. In such cases, a monetary average, although more aggregated, may
provide a more representative and balanced estimate. [20] However, determining the
more appropriate approach remains an open question in the literature and may require
additional testing using representative datasets. Where detailed process knowledge is
lacking, or when dealing with aggregated product categories, EIO models can offer a

pragmatic solution—particularly if the database includes “market for” datasets, as is some­
times the case in Ecoinvent [21]. These represent average emissions for a given product
or service and can serve as approximations in the absence of detailed process­specific

Currently, there is little documentation in the academic literature to guide this choice sys­
tematically. [22] If company­level data exist in both physical and monetary formats, it
may be possible to conduct comparisons and calibrate models accordingly. Otherwise,
efforts may be needed to create or source example datasets. In recent developments,
some researchers have explored the use of intelligent agents that autonomously search
for and define relevant environmental data parameters. For instance, a colleague has
developed an agent designed for “safe and sustainable by design” assessments of chem­
icals. This agent determines the type and sources of necessary data and activates without
user prompts, illustrating a promising direction for automating and refining data collection
processes in environmental modeling.
With regard to the temporal structure of EXIOBASE, it is essential to clarify what is repre­
sented by the 2019 data point. EXIOBASE version 3.8.2 provides time­series data from
1995 to 2022. However, only data up to 2015 are directly observed and grounded in
detailed national statistics. Data for 2016 through 2022 are extrapolated using statistical
and econometric forecasting methods. Consequently, the 2019 dataset does not rep­
resent actual measured values but rather a modeled approximation of global economic
and environmental flows, constructed from earlier data points—most notably those from
2011 and 2015. [23] Compared to version 3.8.2, version 3.9.5 incorporates more recent
observed data up to 2020, enhancing the reliability of analyses for that period, with now­
casted estimates available for 2021 and 2022. These now­casted data are generated
using global trade data, macroeconomic indicators from the International Monetary Fund
(IMF), and available environmental data. While useful for trend analysis, they should be
interpreted with an understanding of their modeled nature and potential limitations. Cau­
tion is especially advised when using them for analyses sensitive to the impacts of events
like the COVID­19 pandemic, as such effects may not be fully captured in the projections.
The construction of EXIOBASE 2019 follows a three­stage methodology. First, core Sup­
ply and Use Tables (SUTs) are built using observed data, integrating trade, production,
emissions, and material flow information. Second, a hybridization step combines both
monetary and physical units using the hybrid supply­use approach developed by Merciai
and Schmidt [19]. Third, the time series from 1995 to 2022 is constructed through the ap­
plication of extrapolation techniques, as detailed by Stadler et al. [24], incorporating real
growth rates for GDP, sectoral outputs, energy use, and emissions. Source databases
include WIOD, IEA, EDGAR, BACI, IMF, among others. Time­series consistency is en­
sured through RAS balancing and data harmonization methods. Where direct data is
unavailable, trends from earlier years are applied.

This explains why 2019 is commonly treated as the last “trustworthy” year in EXIOBASE.
The model’s creators and subsequent academic literature consistently emphasize that
data beyond 2015 are projections. The Zenodo documentation for EXIOBASE 3.8.2 ex­
plicitly states that “the most recent year for which we consider data to be solid is 2015,”
while data from 2016 onward are modeled [25]. Academic sources such as Cabernard et
al. [26] and Font Vivanco [27] also express caution regarding the use of post­2019 data,
particularly given the disruptions introduced by the COVID­19 pandemic.
These disruptions severely impacted global supply chains, energy consumption, trans­
portation, and production patterns—deviations that are not accounted for by EXIOBASE’s
projection algorithms, which are based on smooth historical trends. Examples include the
collapse of global air travel, shifts to remote work, and altered consumption behaviors as
a result of stimulus policies and inflation. As EXIOBASE is not designed to capture non­
linear shocks or structural breaks, it is unable to model these pandemic­related impacts
accurately.
Accordingly, 2019 is regarded as the last stable and credible year in EXIOBASE’s time
series. It precedes the onset of COVID­19 and aligns with the underlying assumptions of
the model. The preference for 2019 has become common practice among researchers
and institutions using EXIOBASE for footprinting, scenario building, or comparative policy
analysis. It serves as a defensible baseline for counterfactual studies and pre­pandemic
modeling exercises.
In summary, EXIOBASE 2019 is a modeled dataset constructed through interpolation and
extrapolation from earlier years, notably 2011 and 2015. While it provides a reasonably
robust and coherent approximation of global economic and environmental dynamics, it
should be treated as a synthetic estimate rather than observed data. Its use is appropriate
for macro­level assessments and long­term modeling, with 2019 widely accepted as the
last usable year before pandemic­related disruptions significantly impaired the validity of
projections.
2.2
Comparative Overview of Major EEIO Databases
The landscape of Environmentally Extended Input­Output (EEIO) databases includes sev­
eral prominent models, each developed with distinct objectives, assumptions, and levels
of resolution. Among the most widely used are EXIOBASE, EORA, and GTAP, each
representing a different trade­off between geographical coverage, sectoral detail, data
transparency, and environmental scope. Understanding their structural differences is es­
sential for interpreting and comparing carbon footprint results, especially in the context of
international studies. [28]
EXIOBASE was specifically constructed for environmental accounting and is distinguished
by its high resolution and methodological transparency. Developed primarily in Europe,
EXIOBASE offers harmonized product­by­product and industry­by­industry tables, cov­
ering 44 countries and a rest­of­world (RoW) region, with 163 industries and products
per country. Its environmental extensions are closely integrated with the economic ac­
counts, ensuring a high level of internal consistency. EXIOBASE is particularly suited

for applications where environmental precision is essential, such as national carbon foot­
print estimation, sustainability benchmarking, and policy evaluation within the European
context.
In contrast, the EORA MRIO database prioritizes completeness of global coverage, ex­
tending to over 190 countries, albeit with heterogeneous sectoral resolution. EORA uses
a “conformable” structure that allows each country’s IO table to retain its original level
of disaggregation, often as low as 25–30 sectors for developing economies. While this
design enables EORA to provide near­global emissions coverage, it introduces inconsis­
tencies in cross­country comparisons due to variable sector mappings. Moreover, EORA
combines a variety of data sources—including UN COMTRADE, national statistics, and
IEA energy balances—which, while comprehensive, complicate reproducibility and result
transparency. The environmental extensions in EORA are broad (covering CO2, CH4,
N20, etc.), but their alignment with monetary flows is not always as rigorous as in EX­
IOBASE.
GTAP, originally developed for computable general equilibrium modeling, represents a
third archetype. It covers around 140 countries and 65 sectors, offering strong integra­
tion with trade and tariff data, and is widely used in economic impact assessments of
policy scenarios (e.g., carbon taxes or trade agreements). However, GTAP’s environ­
mental modules are secondary additions rather than core components. As such, GTAP
is less suited for detailed product­level environmental analysis. Moreover, the model is
only partially open source, and its IO tables are based on older benchmark years updated
via balancing procedures, limiting the transparency and temporal granularity available in
EXIOBASE.
The methodological consequences of these design choices are well illustrated in a com­
parative study conducted by Beaufils (2019), in which national carbon footprints and sec­
toral intensities were calculated using EXIOBASE and EORA. [28] The study revealed that
while macroeconomic trends in emissions were relatively consistent across databases,
sector­level results varied considerably, particularly for carbon­intensive industries such
as energy, transport, and metallurgy. In some cases, differences in sectoral footprint es­
timates exceeded 30%, attributable to varying aggregation levels, import allocation rules,
and environmental satellite accounting practices. For instance, EORA’s simplified input
structure often results in inflated upstream emissions for intermediate goods, whereas
EXIOBASE’s higher resolution offers finer allocation along complex supply chains. Small
but relevant discrepancies exist in the territorial carbon dioxide emissions reported by EX­
IOBASE and GCP (Global Carbon Project) for China (up to 6% difference), attributed to
different emission inventories and trade modeling assumptions.
Furthermore, the treatment of trade flows and re­exports also varies across models. EX­
IOBASE uses detailed Eurostat and UN data to model re­exports and bilateral trade cor­
rections, while EORA applies simpler balancing techniques that may overlook complex
global value chains.
Similarly, environmental data integration is more tightly coupled
in EXIOBASE, which links emissions to specific activities (e.g., electricity generation by
source), whereas in EORA and GTAP, emissions are often mapped at more aggregated
sectoral levels.
Despite these differences, all three databases rely on the Leontief input­output framework
to link environmental burdens to final demand. They also share common limitations, such
as price variability, data harmonization challenges, and the potential underrepresentation
of capital goods, particularly in MRIO systems. These challenges have been extensively
discussed in the literature, and efforts to hybridize EEIO data with process­based life cycle

2.3
Critical comparisons of process and EEIO methods
In response to increasing regulatory, consumer, and stakeholder pressure, corporations
are seeking efficient tools to assess their environmental performance. Traditional bench­
marking methods, however, tend to be costly and time­intensive. To address this chal­
lenge, some researchers have proposed a screening­level approach using Economic
Input­Output Life Cycle Assessment (EIO­LCA), which enables rapid and cost­effective
comparisons of a company’s environmental impacts within its industry context [31].
Conventional Life Cycle Assessments (LCAs) are often constrained by high financial
costs, long implementation timelines, and limited access to proprietary data. Moreover,
many corporations lack robust internal accounting systems capable of tracking environ­
mental costs—particularly indirect costs such as waste management, long­term remedi­
ation, or future regulatory liabilities.
The Society of Environmental Toxicology and Chemistry (SETAC), a leading organization
in environmental science, played a foundational role in shaping modern LCA by estab­
lishing a standardized methodology consisting of four phases: goal and scope definition,
life cycle inventory (LCI), life cycle impact assessment (LCIA), and interpretation. Despite
this structured framework, traditional LCAs often suffer from narrow system boundaries,
reliance on incomplete datasets, and subjectivity in modeling choices. They typically omit
capital equipment, include only a limited range of suppliers, and can lead to contradic­
tory outcomes—such as those seen in ongoing debates over the environmental merits of
paper versus plastic—ultimately reducing their reliability for robust decision­making.
Critically, the majority of environmental impacts associated with products and services
are indirect, which renders system boundary choices in traditional LCA potentially prob­
lematic. Many indirect effects occurring deep within the supply chain risk being excluded.
For example, a $10,000 vehicle purchase may be associated with approximately 5 pounds
of direct toxic discharges, compared to 34 pounds of indirect discharges—nearly seven
times higher. In other cases, such as the production of computers, indirect emissions
can exceed direct emissions by a factor of 26, highlighting the substantial environmental
burden embedded in upstream supply chains [18].
By contrast, EIO­LCA offers a cost­effective and comprehensive alternative, particularly
well­suited for high­level evaluations such as material selection or policy analysis. It in­
tegrates national economic input­output tables with environmental emissions data to es­
timate both direct and indirect environmental burdens associated with changes in final
demand. This methodology excels at capturing upstream and systemic effects that tradi­
tional LCAs may overlook. Nonetheless, its reliance on sectoral averages limits its appli­
cability for firm­specific or product­level assessments. EIO­LCA also omits downstream
stages, such as product use and end­of­life, and lacks the resolution to distinguish be­
tween closely related alternatives—for example, different grades of PET plastic. More­

A study conducted by Steubing et al. (2022) [16], investigates the comparability of car­
bon footprint (CF) results between two widely used environmental databases: Ecoinvent,
a process­based Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) database, and EXIOBASE, a hybrid Envi­
ronmentally Extended Input­Output Analysis (EEIOA) database. The primary objective is
to evaluate how these databases differ in their sectoral and product coverage, and how
carbon footprint values compare across matched product categories.
To conduct the comparison, the authors used Ecoinvent version 3.4 (cut­off model) and
EXIOBASE version 3.3.18 (hybrid version). A total of 2,325 Ecoinvent product systems
were manually matched to 100 EXIOBASE products, considering consistency in prod­
uct names, functional units (kg, MJ, kWh), and regions (e.g., France, global average).
Comparisons were made at the highest resolution available for each database.
Three primary metrics were employed in the analysis: relative deviation (RD), Spearman
correlation coefficient (ρ), and the coefficient of variation (CV). RD measures the normal­
ized difference in CF values between databases for a given product; RD = 0 indicates
perfect agreement, RD = 1 corresponds to a factor of two difference. Log­transformed
RD values provide clearer insight into proportional disparities. Spearman’s ρ quantifies
the degree to which product rankings are preserved across databases, independent of
magnitude. High ρ values indicate similar trends in emission intensities. Finally, the CV
highlights the degree of dispersion within CF values of each database, signaling internal
variability as a result of either technological diversity (Ecoinvent) or regional heterogeneity
(EXIOBASE).

4
Comparison of the ADEME and
EXIOABSE databases
4.1
Method
To evaluate the consistency between two monetary­based approaches for estimating
greenhouse gas emissions, a quantitative comparison was conducted between ADEME’s
2019 monetary emission factors and French product­specific emission intensities from
EXIOBASE (2019 also). Sectoral mapping was applied to align both datasets prior to the
analysis.
The dataset construction being different, the emission factor perimeters are not perfectly
aligned between the ADEME and EXIOBASE. Both datasets are based on the NACE
classification of industries. The NACE classification (Nomenclature statistique des Activ­
ités économiques dans la Communauté Européenne) is the official European system for
categorizing economic activities. It provides a standardized framework for comparing sta­
tistical data across industries and sectors within the European Union, enabling consistent
reporting, analysis, and benchmarking. Two categories of emission factor associations
are distinguished in the analysis:
• Perfectly aligned matches, where the NACE category behind the emission factor is
the same.
• Imperfectly aligned matches, where the NACE perimeter of one of the emission
factors is more restrained than the other.
4.2
Results
The comparison between ADEME and EXIOBASE emission factors reveals important in­
sights into their alignment, both in terms of overall correlation and absolute deviation.
Across the full dataset, the two sources exhibit a strong monotonic relationship, as indi­
cated by a Spearman correlation coefficient of 0.78. However, the Pearson correlation,
measuring linear association, is somewhat lower at 0.69, suggesting that while the rank
order of emission factors is preserved, their absolute magnitudes are less consistently
aligned. This observation is reinforced by the relatively high average relative deviation of
58.31%, implying that although trends are similar, significant discrepancies exist between
corresponding values. The higher coefficient of variation (CV) observed for EXIOBASE
(2.04) compared to ADEME (1.24) further suggests that EXIOBASE emission factors dis­
play greater internal variability.
Focusing on the subset of imperfect correspondences—where the matching between
ADEME and EXIOBASE sectors is approximate rather than exact—yields interesting nu­
ances. Despite the imperfect alignment, the Spearman correlation remains stable at 0.78,
while the Pearson correlation rises to 0.77. This suggests that even though sectors are
not strictly equivalent, the quantitative relationship between the datasets is marginally
stronger in linear terms compared to the full dataset. Furthermore, the average relative
deviation decreases substantially to 42.81%, and the CVs for both ADEME (1.35) and
EXIOBASE (1.89) become more similar. These findings imply that imperfect matches,
counterintuitively, show better overall quantitative agreement than the dataset as a whole.
It appears that sector misalignments do not necessarily introduce more noise; in fact,

they may, in aggregate, smooth out discrepancies that are otherwise visible when strict
matches are enforced.
By contrast, the analysis of perfectly matched sectors—those with identical NACE codes
in both datasets—reveals a much sharper linear alignment, with the Pearson correlation
soaring to 0.96. This near­perfect linearity contrasts with the Spearman coefficient, which
remains constant at 0.78, indicating that improvements in linearity are not accompanied
by changes in monotonic trends. The homogeneity of emission factors is also improved,
as seen by the lower CVs for ADEME (1.15) and EXIOBASE (1.35). Yet paradoxically,
the average relative deviation for perfect matches rises markedly to 71.74%, the highest
among all subsets considered. This suggests that although the datasets track each other
precisely in relative terms, there remains a systematic scaling difference or bias between
their absolute values. In other words, while the proportional relationships between factors
are tightly preserved when sector boundaries match perfectly, their numerical magnitudes
diverge significantly.
Taken together, these results underscore a complex interaction between perimeter align­
ment and dataset comparability. Perfect matches enhance linear correspondence but
exacerbate absolute differences, whereas imperfect matches, despite less precise sector
definitions, produce closer average values. This highlights the importance of consider­
ing both correlation and absolute deviation when assessing the comparability of emission
factor datasets, and suggests that structural differences—rather than random noise—are
the primary source of divergence between ADEME and EXIOBASE values.

the linear regression, which confirms the systematic biases between the two datasets.
However, the value is still relatively high, showing an acceptable agreement between the
two datasets.
Together, these regression results highlight a good linear association with some system­
atic biases. The slight deviation from the identity line and the incomplete explanatory
power of the regression suggest that the datasets differ in their construction, coverage,
or methodological assumptions. Likely contributors to this divergence include differences
in initial data sources, price adjustments and harmonization (Exiobase potentially harmo­
nizing between countries as an international database, while ADEME focuses on France
only and does not need to revert to such harmonizations), and sectoral aggregation.

footprints in Carbometrix’s database
and Exiobase industry emission factors
7.1
Method
Carbometrix uses an online proprietary platform to carry out its activities, namely:
• Collect data from clients using a personalized data collection questionnaire, an
anonymous employee survey, and a supplier survey.
• Calculate the client’s carbon footprint using semi­automated formulas, hypotheses,
and emission factors.
• Keep track of completed carbon footprints.
The 1,217 carbon footprints stored in the database were extracted to compare the emis­
sions per € or revenue to the EXIOBASE emission factors per € of industry output. Given
that the Carbometrix carbon footprints are computed using as much physical activity data
and LCA emission factors as possible based on the type of activity measured and the
company’s available data, this comparison yields a comparison between LCA­heavy EEIO
emisison calculations. These include 404 carbon footprints delivered by Carbometrix, and
813 carbon footprints entered in the database from external reports found on corporations’
websites. The carbon footprints represented various years (Table 7.1), countries (Table
7.2), and sectors. Carbometrix sorts the carbon footprints into sectors internally. These
sectors were mapped to the EXIOBASE industry sectors, with correspondances detailed
in Appendix Table 1.
The sectors are split into two types: Product and Service sectors. For each country­year­
type trio in the database, an inflation compared to 2019 is calculated to adjust the Exiobase
emission factor. The following method is applied to calculate the inflation compared to
2019.
For Service types, the Harmonised Index of Consumer Prices (HCPI) is used. It is a mea­
sure of inflation that reflects the average change over time in the prices paid by households
for a basket of goods and services. It is termed ”harmonised” because it is calculated ac­
cording to a consistent methodology across the member states of the European Union,
ensuring comparability between countries. HCPI is designed to assess consumer price
inflation and is commonly used by central banks, particularly the European Central Bank
(ECB), to guide monetary policy. It includes categories such as food, energy, healthcare,
housing, and recreation, weighted according to their share in household consumption.
For Product types, the Producer Price Index (PPI) is used. It measures the average
change over time in the selling prices received by domestic producers for their output. It
captures price changes from the perspective of the producer, rather than the consumer,
and typically applies to goods at various stages of production: raw materials, intermediate
goods, and finished products. Unlike HCPI, the PPI does not include services and does
not measure final consumer prices. Instead, it offers insights into upstream inflationary
pressures that may eventually affect consumer prices, making it a useful leading indicator
for inflation forecasting and industrial sector analysis.

The Spearman correlation is the Pearson product­moment correlation between the ranks
of the values in each dataset. The coefficient of variation is, for each dataset, the stan­
dard deviation of the dataset divided by its average. The Spearman rank correlation co­
efficient of 0.56 indicates a moderate monotonic relationship between the two datasets.
This suggests that, while the rankings of emission factors are broadly preserved across
the datasets, significant deviations exist for a large part of the data.
The coefficients of variation (CV) further illustrate structural disparities between the datasets.
The EXIOBASE emission factors, corrected for inflation, exhibit a high CV of 26. This
high dispersion implies that the data within EXIOBASE vary considerably, likely reflecting
the database’s broad sectoral coverage and the diversity of economic activities captured
through input–output linkages. In contrast, the Carbometrix carbon footprints display a
substantially lower CV of 5, indicating a narrower distribution of values and potentially a
more homogenized or aggregated modeling approach. Such a difference may signal an
underrepresentation of technological variability in the Carbometrix dataset. This corrob­
orates the analysis made earlier about the range of Carbometrix corporations not being
representative of the entire industries.
The average relative deviation of 4 (i.e., 400%) denotes a substantial discrepancy in the
magnitude of emission factors reported by the two datasets. On average, values in Car­
bometrix differ from their EXIOBASE counterparts by a factor of five. This level of deviation
confirms the presence of systematic biases.
Taken together, these metrics provide evidence of both structural and numerical misalign­
ment between the two datasets. This degree of divergence highlights, among other things,
their differing methodological underpinnings, Carbometrix being more process­based and
EXIOBASE relying on environmentally extended input–output modeling.

therefore meadianed over each Exiobase sector to smooth over any strong outliers and
enable a visual representation. A scatter plot is created to represent the results of the
comparison (Figure 7.1. The numbers on the plot correspond to the Exiobase sectors
according to Table 7.3.

The relatively high coefficient of determination R2 = 0.63 for the fitted regression model
suggests a moderate to strong linear association between the two datasets. However, the
slope significantly greater than one and a negative intercept highlight a notable systematic
bias. Specifically, the slope of 1.86 implies that, on average, Carbometrix emission factors
are almost twice as large as those in EXIOBASE, particularly for higher values. Compar­
ing this slope to the of relative deviation of 4 over the entire dataset, it becomes evident
that there are stark outliers within the data which are smoothed over by the median, allow­
ing for a more reasonable comparison. The intercept of ­35.70 further suggests that for
lower emission values in EXIOBASE, the Carbometrix dataset underestimates emissions
or reports negative emissions, which is physically implausible and likely reflects either
measurement artifacts or misalignments in system boundaries or category definitions.
In contrast, the identity line (CarbometrixEF = ExiobaseEF ), which would represent per­
fect agreement between the two datasets, yields a substantially lower R2 of 0.32. This dis­
crepancy further confirms that a considerable proportion of the variance in CarbometrixEF
is not explained by a 1:1 relationship with ExiobaseEF. Such a result points to structural
differences between the two databases. These differences may arise from a range of
factors, including varying system boundaries (e.g., cradle­to­gate vs. cradle­to­grave),
geographical scope, allocation rules, data vintage, or sectoral aggregation. The relatvely
small number of corporations in the Carbometrix database compared to the entire indus­
tries may also account for some divergence: the corporations in the Carbometrix database
are potentially not representative of the entire industries.
This interpretation aligns with findings from Steubing et al. (2022) [16], who observed
that more than half of the matched carbon footprint results between Ecoinvent (a process­
based LCA database) and EXIOBASE (an EEIO database) differed by more than a factor
of two, with significant systematic discrepancies depending on sector and methodology.
Similarly, their work emphasized that mismatches in product definitions, technological
detail, and regionalization can account for substantial divergence.
In conclusion, while the datasets show a degree of correlation, the magnitude of sys­
tematic deviation and relatively low agreement along the identity line suggest that the
two sources are not directly interchangeable without careful consideration. These results
underscore the importance of methodological transparency and dataset alignment when
using emission factors for Scope 3 accounting or policy assessments.

Case Study: Comparison of physical

8.1
Method
In this section, a case study is run on purchase data from two of Carbometrix’s clients who
have provided Carbometrix with purchasing data in both monetary and physical units. For
each of the purchased products, the spent amount as well as the weight are given. The
goal of this analysis is to identify the differences in emission results when using monetary
data associated with EEIO emission factors, or when using physical data associated with
LCA emission factors.
The clients will remain anonymous, but a brief description can be given of the two busi­
nesses to provide useful context for the analysis of the results:
• Company A is a chain of all organic product supermarkets in France. Its data con­
cerns all of the products purchased to then be sold in the stores. These products are
mainly food products, but also other types such as hygiene and cleaning products.
• Company B is a chain of premium camping sites in France. Its data concerns pur­
chases of items for the restaurants and snacking bars on camping grounds, and
some cleaning products and such.
The datasets provided by both corporations are rather granular, providing details by type
of purchased products, such as ”Citrus” or ”Rice”. Each specific product is associated
with a corresponding physical emission factor. The emission factors are taken mostly
from the Agribalyse dataset, and some (for non­food products) from the Base Empreinte.
The Agribalyse dataset is a comprehensive French life cycle inventory (LCI) database
developed by ADEME (the French Agency for Ecological Transition) to quantify the en­
vironmental impacts of agricultural and food products consumed in France. It provides
detailed life cycle assessment (LCA) data across multiple environmental indicators, in­
cluding greenhouse gas emissions, eutrophication, acidification, and land use, among
others. Covering both raw agricultural commodities and processed food items, Agribalyse
is designed to support environmental labeling, policy­making, and research in sustainable
agriculture and food systems. Its methodological framework aligns with international LCA
standards, ensuring consistency, transparency, and scientific rigor. The Base Empreinte
dataset, developed by ADEME, is a national French database that compiles life cycle
inventory (LCI) and environmental impact data for a wide range of products, services,
and processes across diverse sectors. It is designed to support environmental footprint
calculations, particularly for carbon accounting and eco­design initiatives, by providing
consistent and standardized life cycle assessment (LCA) data.
The products are then associated to an EEIO emission factor from the Exiobase prod­
uct database, adjusted for inflation from 2019 to correspond to 2024 euros, which is the
unit of the corporations’ data. The Exiobase emission factors are much less granular
than the physical ones: there are only 15 different emission factors from Exiobase used,
compared to 93 from Agribalyse and Base Empreinte. Therefore, the a first clearly iden­
tifiable source of discrepancy between the results from the monetary and the physical

9
Discussion
This thesis set out to explore the implications of using different datasets and method­
ological approaches for calculating Scope 3 carbon footprints, with a particular focus on
environmentally extended input­output (EEIO) and process­based emission factors. By
drawing on empirical comparisons between ADEME and EXIOBASE datasets—spanning
both product­based (pxp) and industry­based (ixi) structures—this study has revealed sig­
nificant methodological tensions and practical trade­offs in carbon accounting practice.
9.1
Data Evolution and Volatility
One of the key observations is the dynamic nature of emission factors themselves. ADEME’s
dataset, widely used in France, has undergone substantial updates between 2019 and
2023. These updates include not only recalibrated emission intensities but also struc­
tural changes in sector definitions and inflation adjustments. As illustrated in the relative
change heatmaps, certain sectors—particularly in services and transportation—exhibited
emission factor volatility exceeding 30%.
These changes raise critical questions for practitioners who rely on carbon footprint met­
rics for year­on­year tracking. An organization reporting lower emissions in 2023 com­
pared to 2019 may simply be reflecting a dataset update rather than genuine decarboniza­
tion progress. This underscores the importance of dataset version control and method­
ological transparency when using national databases like ADEME.
9.2
Product vs. Industry Matching: pxp vs. ixi
The comparison between EXIOBASE’s pxp (product­by­product) and ixi (industry­by­industry)
variants revealed structural differences with implications for Scope 3 assessments. The
pxp approach, which aligns emissions with final products, tends to be more intuitive for
consumption­based accounting and is better aligned with procurement categories. The
ixi structure, on the other hand, is more suitable when the data source or reporting logic
is tied to producer sectors.
Case studies illustrated how mismatches between pxp and ixi perspectives can lead to
significant deviations in emission estimates, especially in hybrid or multi­functional sec­
tors such as ICT, marketing, and consultancy. These inconsistencies suggest that prac­
titioners must carefully select the structural layer that best fits the economic activity being
evaluated.
9.3
Comparability and Inflation Effects
A key methodological challenge in comparing datasets like ADEME and EXIOBASE lies
in their monetary basis. Both express emission intensities in kgCO2e per euro, but price
level assumptions differ. ADEME’s 2023 update incorporates inflation adjustments, while
EXIOBASE uses a constant­price base year (2019). When used for Scope 3 calculations,
especially in financial portfolios or large procurement categories, such inconsistencies can
distort results.
This highlights the importance of harmonizing price levels or at least documenting the
treatment of inflation when comparing or aggregating emission estimates across datasets.
In the absence of harmonization, emission reductions may be misinterpreted due to chang­
ing economic conditions rather than operational performance.

9.4
Methodological Trade­Offs and Practical Tensions
Throughout the thesis, a recurrent theme has been the balance between specificity and
completeness. ADEME offers high­resolution, French­contextualized data with more cur­
rent sector classifications, but it lacks full supply­chain traceability. EXIOBASE, while
coarser in classification, offers a globally consistent EEIO framework that potentially cap­
tures indirect and upstream effects more comprehensively.
For practitioners, this means that neither dataset should be systematically preferred. In­
stead, the choice depends on the use case: ADEME may be more appropriate for French
regulatory compliance or supplier­specific calculations, whereas EXIOBASE is advanta­
geous for broad screening, portfolio­level estimation, and comparative international anal­
ysis.
9.5
Crossed Insights
Bringing together the empirical and methodological parts of the thesis yields several inte­
grative insights:
• The structural form of a dataset (pxp vs. ixi) can be as impactful as the data source
and emission values themselves.
• Dataset volatility over time undermines naive longitudinal comparisons unless ad­
justed for.
• EEIO­based approaches (especially monetary ones) offer scale and completeness
but require caution regarding inflation and aggregation effects.
• Practitioners benefit from using EEIO factors as a first step—complemented by more
granular process­based data where impact is concentrated.
These insights underscore the importance of methodological pluralism and the value of
critical comparison when using carbon accounting data for decision­making. Based on
the findings in this study, it is clear that practitioners must take into account a multitude of
aspects when choosing the source and methodology of footprint data to use in Scope 3
emission calculations.
• Goal and context of the calculation: The goal of the calculation can vary case­by­
case. In the case of a simple sectoral analysis, EEIO methods are potentially much
easier to implement because they rely on more easily accessible financial data,
and require less granularity. In the context of the precise calculation of a single
company’s carbon footprint, both approaches can be considered, with a preference
for process­based approaches to obtain as much granularity as possible in case of
a future decarbonization ambition for the company. If a comparison across multiple
companies is to be derived, then a unified methodology should be applied as much
as possible between the companies. If this comparison is between international
companies, then for any EEIO­based calculations, a single international database
should be preferred to national databases.
• Geographical context: The geographical context of individual companies also plays
a role, as some countries may be very inaccurately represented by some databases
(for example, Exiobase has very little granularity in Africa and Latin America). This
lack of granularity, combined with the reliance of EEIO methods on monetary spend,
can strongly distort results if the cost of products and services in the considered
country is far from the average of the represented region.

• Temporal context: The studied year can influence the choice of method and database.
As seen in the thesis, using outdated EEIO data involved correcting for inflation,
which leads to increased uncertainty. Therefore, an EEIO database with recent
results should be preferred to an outdated one as much as possible. It is often
the case that such a recent database is unavailable, as corporate carbon footprint
calculations are typically done for the past year, while EEIO data requires time and
hindsight to be generated. This can be an argument in favor of process­based meth­
ods, which reflect a state of technological advancement, less volatile than product
and service prices, and therefore more robust to a few years of delay.
• Materiality of the considered Scope 3 category: When calculating the emissions
from an important Scope 3 category for a company, process­based methods may be
preferred, in order to provide as much technological detail in the results, and enable
the identification of potential decarbonization efforts.
• Quality of the activity data: The quality and granularity of the activity data may
be decisive in the choice of emission factors. Firstly, it is often easiest for compa­
nies to provide financial data, as this is more often tracked in accounting systems
than physical data such as weights or energy consumptions. Secondly, even in
the case of partially available physical data, the granularity of the purchases may
complicate the use of process­based emission factors, which typically do not offer
average product emission factors but rather refer to a specific product rather than a
category of products. As seen in the thesis, this may force the use of an excessively
specific emission factor not representative of the full scope of products considered.
In this case, EEIO methods may be less biased.

10
Conclusion
This thesis has examined the methodological and empirical underpinnings of Scope 3 car­
bon footprinting, with a focus on the comparative application of EEIO and process­based
emission factors. The central goal was to understand how dataset structure, monetary
valuation, and temporal evolution affect the reliability and interpretation of GHG estimates.
Several decisive conclusions emerge from this work:
• No single dataset is sufficient. Practitioners should combine EEIO and process­
based data to triangulate estimates, identify hotspots, and manage uncertainty.
• Structure matters. Whether emission factors are assigned to industries (ixi) or
products (pxp) significantly alters results. This structural choice must align with the
inventory logic—production or consumption—used by the organization.
• Dataset updates are not neutral. Changes in ADEME factors between 2019 and
2023 reveal that emission reductions may reflect methodological shifts rather than
real decarbonization. Practitioners must therefore document dataset versions and
interpret year­on­year changes with care.
• Monetary values introduce instability. Emission factors expressed per euro are
influenced by inflation and price variability. Without normalization, monetary EEIO
factors can lead to misleading comparisons over time or across countries.
• Scope 3 estimation is not plug­and­play. It requires active methodological choices,
transparency, and a clear understanding of data lineage.
For academics, this study underscores the need for greater harmonization and docu­
mentation across datasets. Harmonizing inflation treatment, sector classifications, and
regional mapping would enable more meaningful comparisons and facilitate hybrid mod­
eling efforts.
For practitioners, the thesis advocates for critical interpretation and the use of hybrid
strategies—combining broad EEIO screening with targeted process­level refinement—
with careful consideration of the context and goal of the calculation and the granularity
of the activity data. This dual approach balances completeness and specificity, enabling
more robust and defensible carbon accounting in a rapidly evolving regulatory and stake­
holder landscape.
Ultimately, carbon footprinting is as much a methodological exercise as it is a numerical
one. The quality and credibility of the results will depend not only on the emission factors
used, but also on the clarity and coherence of the assumptions behind them—in short on
the informed decisions taken by the practitioner.
