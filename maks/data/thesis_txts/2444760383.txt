Abstract
Most nonlinear frequency conversion laser sources today are based on complex, expensive and
bulky solid state or ﬁber lasers. The reason for this is the access to high intracavity ﬁelds,
providing high nonlinear frequency conversion eﬃciency. Using diode lasers are is much less bulky
and cheaper method to generate laser light compared to other laser sources. Combining those
with nonlinear frequency conversion, one obtains a simpler and cheaper laser source solution for
a range of applications. The light sources we present in this project show great potential for
tunable single-frequency nonlinear conversion, with the possibility to exchange the ampliﬁers and
crystal for accessing other wavelengths than those demonstrated.
The aim of this master’s thesis is to build, investigate, and characterize tunable single-
frequency green and mid-infrared light sources based on nonlinear frequency conversion, with a
mid-infrared light source as the main focus. The green light source showed to exhibit a tuning
range of 530.5-533 nm. The maximum average power was measured to be 0.493 W ± 14.7 %.
The mid-infrared light source tuning range, with a power above 1 mW, was measured to be
3.2-3.58 µm, with a maximum power of 5.4 mW ± 4 %.
The heart of the project is nonlinear frequency conversion in a coupled ring cavity with tapered
diode ampliﬁers. The system is a simple and inexpensive approach to obtain tunable green or
mid-infrared light. The concept is demonstrated experimentally with an optical breadboard setup,
and supported by numerical models.
The tapered diode ampliﬁers shows from characterization that they operate with a stable
and single-frequency behavior in short time frames, needed for proof of concept. We present
investigation of the instability and diﬀerent solutions, and from the experiments and through
thorough characterization of the ampliﬁers and the setups, we concluded that the most critical
part in the setups, is the frequency stability in order to obtain a high power and stable light
source.

Chapter
1
Introduction
This chapter serves to motivate the work done in the report, as well
as outline the project description and work prior to this project.
1.1 Motivation
As the laser industry grows, the need for more compact and cheaper
laser sources increases. Diode lasers has long been known to provide
exactly this, but due to the material properties, not all wavelengths
in the visible and infrared spectrum can be accessed, such as
the mid-infrared from 3 −5 µm.
A a method widely used for
accessing wavelength inaccessible with known gain materials used
for lasing is nonlinear frequency conversion with use of a nonlinear
crystal. Combining the diode laser compactness and low pricing,
with nonlinear frequency conversion is therefore ideal. Single pass
conversion eﬃciency is usually a few percent in most commonly
used nonlinear crystals, such as lithium niobate used in this project.
With a low single pass conversion eﬃciency, high power, i.e. 10’s of
Watts, is needed to obtain a satisfactory power of the converted
frequency. Diode lasers are however limited in output power due to
their dimensions, which can be compensated for by enhancing the
power with an external cavity.
==PAGE:16==
2
Chapter 1. Introduction
When building an external cavity, the laser frequency must always
mach the cavity. To avoid actively locking of the laser frequency, and
thereby simplifying the setup, the frequency of the laser is passively
locked by seeding it with the light from the external cavity. This
forms the coupled ring cavity.
The mid-infrared spectrum is especially interesting with regards
to molecular spectroscopy, as a large number of molecules have their
vibrational states in this frequency range. In order to increase the
resolution of the spectroscopy, the laser linewidth must be as narrow
as possible. Aside from that, a large range of wavelength must be
accessible, as the wider the range the larger variety of molecules will
the spectrometer detect.
To access wide tunability and high gain, thereby high power,
tapered diode ampliﬁer has been selected as gain medium, as its
broad emission bandwidth and high power output allows for a wide
tuning range and a high ampliﬁed ﬁeld power in the external cavity.
To ensure high power output of the nonlinear converted beam, the
beam properties of the tapered ampliﬁer must be shaped, as it does
not emit a Gaussian beam proﬁle due to its waveguide geometry.
Beam shaping is therefore an important part of building an eﬃcient
light source operating through nonlinear frequency conversion.
1.1.1 Project description
This project aims to build and characterize a widely tunable
mid-infrared laser source. This is done with a simple laser cavity
setup for enhancing the power output of a tapered diode ampliﬁer.
The cavity enhanced power is then used for nonlinear frequency
conversion to the mid-infrared spectrum. A tapered diode ampliﬁer
is used to obtain easy and widely tunable single-frequency nonlinear
conversion.
The project is mainly focused on single-frequency diﬀerence fre-
quecy generation (DFG) in a coupled ring cavity setup, with the
use of tapered diode ampliﬁers. Previous work within this type of
setup[1,2] lays the foundation, as this project is two fold. The ﬁrst
part is re-building and characterizing a coupled ring cavity setup
for second harmonic generation (SHG) but with cavity dimensions

1.1.2 Related work
The research ﬁeld within IR and visible light generation has been

for nonlinear conversion, the work prior to this project, is the work

It shows promising potential for widely tunable single-frequency

2.2 Optical resonators
Several types of optical resonators (optical cavities) are used, both
linear and ring resonators as seen in the previous section. The

3.1 Working principles
To understand the working principles of the diode laser, we begin
at the energy levels of the semiconductor gain material, known as
conduction and valance bands. When atoms form a crystal, energy
bands are formed as well. In a semiconductor at 0 K, one band, the
valance band, will be ﬁlled with electrons. Above the valance band,
the conduction band will be empty of electrons. Adding energy to
the crystal, either by heat, electricity or lightwaves, the electrons
from the valance band may be excited into the conduction band.
When an electron leaves the valance band, an unﬁlled state is formed
(a hole).

3.2 Fabrication
To exploit semiconductor materials in form of a diode laser, the
vertical structure must fulﬁll a number of criteria in order to conﬁne
carriers and photons. First of all, in order to avoid any defects in
the structure, leading to non-radiative recombination as mentioned
in the previous section, the materials used must be lattice matched.
This means that the lattice constants of diﬀerent materials must
match as closely as possible. If the lattice constants do not match,

thickness, can still be grown, although it introduces stress or strain to
the lattice. The stress/strain is introduced since the unit cell volume
must remain the same. To achieve conﬁnement of the carriers, at
least two diﬀerent materials with diﬀerent band gap must be used.
The materials used are III-V materials, which means it is materials
from the third and ﬁfth group in the periodic table. The material
that has the lowest band gap, i.e. the longest wavelength, deﬁnes
the wavelength of the emitted photons. To conﬁne the photons,
the materials chosen must have diﬀerent refractive indices. Luckily,
the material with the lowest band gap is often also the one with
the highest refractive index. Therefore the carrier conﬁnement and
optical conﬁnement overlaps. This way, it is possible to have optical
gain while simultaneously conﬁne the optical mode.

4.2 Phase matching
To maximize the conversion eﬃciency, the ∆k must remain 0 through
the entire crystal, for the given polarization and direction of the
incident beams. Phase matching can be achieved in two ways. By
controlling the AOI or by quasi phase matching (QPM).
In the case of SHG, the birefringence of the material can be
used for phase matching, as the refractive index for the crystal
depends not only on wavelength and temperature, but also on the
polarization of ﬁeld. The susceptibility tensor element in this case
is χxxz = 4.35 pm/V.[22] If the incoming ﬁeld is polarized in the
ordinary axis of the crystal, the second order nonlinear generated
ﬁeld will be generated along the extra-ordinary axis. This means that
∆k = no(λ, T) −ne(λ/2, T, θ), since the extra-ordinary refractive
index also depends on the angle of propagation. Therefore, phase
matching can be achieved by choosing the angled that minimizes
∆k.

5.4 Stability
It is seen from the power measurements that the nonlinear converted
beam power is unstable. The instability is high for the SHG with a
standard deviation of 14.2 % during 20 minutes, compared to the
DFG with a standard deviation of 7.4 % for a test of 45 minutes and
4 % for 10 minutes. The most crucial reason for this diﬀerence, is
the nonlinear conversion process. For SHG, the incident beam expe-

ampliﬁer cavity therefore experiences a loss at the frequency where

mode selection from the grating. This results in mode hops. To

larger free spectral range than the bow-tie cavity. Unfortunately,
the mode selection is not strong, since the surfaces are only 8 % re-
ﬂective. Therefore, even with the etalon the mode hops are observed.

another criteria is that both ampliﬁers must always run at the

Conclusion and Outlook
Widely tunable single frequency mid-infrared and green laser sources
have been constructed and characterized.
The green laser source proved to be single-frequency with a
linewidth of 2 pm, limited by the optical spectrum analyzer (OSA).
It also exhibit a tuning range of 530.5-533 nm, limited by the
temperature range of the crystal. The maximum average power
was measured to be 0.493 W ± 14.7 %. The setup suﬀered from
high instability, caused by the tapered diode ampliﬁers, the coupled
cavity design and the second harmonic generation (SHG).
The mid-infrared source also proved to be single-frequency, with
a linewidth limited by the OSA of 3 pm. The measured tuning
range with a power above 1 mW was 3.2-3.58 µm, with a maximum
power of 5.4 mW ± 4 %. The theoretical mid-infrared tuning range
with the ampliﬁers used for diﬀerence frequency generation is 1.3
µm, from 3.76 µm to 4.06 µm, given the crystal used can be phase
matched to all pump and signal wavelengths.
The two main challenges of the project were the frequency
stability of the tapered diode ampliﬁers and the overall frequency
instability of the system.
The ampliﬁers used in a ring cavity
showed to be very sensitive to the seed power. This was concluded
to be an important parameter when building the setup, as two
ampliﬁers was damaged while running the experiments.
The
frequency instability highly aﬀects the nonlinear converted beam
==PAGE:84==
70
Chapter 6. Conclusion and Outlook
power stability. A theoretical approach analyzing the electric ﬁeld
in the coupled system showed, that the lengths of the two ring
cavities must the considered when designing the setup, in order
to achieve stable output power of the nonlinear converted beam.
The individual lengths as well as the diﬀerence between them
are, according to the theory, important parameters to limit the
instability. The frequency instability in the coupled ring cavities
may also be solved by eliminating resonant frequency outside of the
phase match conditions of the nonlinear process. An attempt to do
so was inserting an etalon in the bow-tie cavity, this however only
improved the stability of the SHG. A main reason for the instability
of the diﬀerence frequency generated beam might therefore be the
frequency instability of the pump frequency, the 800 nm tapered
ampliﬁer, as this is not aﬀected by an etalon in the bow-tie cavity.
The setups also shows high sensitivity towards airﬂow and thermal
drifting.
The thermal drifting was limited by a water cooled
breadboard on which the setup was build.
Future work within the frame of this project is therefore amongst
other things to limit the instability. One approach could be to make
the setup more compact, and thereby increase the mode spacing of
the coupled cavities. To limit the thermal drifting, earlier work has
beneﬁted from the bow-tie cavity build on a temperature controlled
block of aluminum.[2]
To limit the frequency instability of the tapered ampliﬁers, the
system could be actively locked, although this will then make the
coupled ring cavity design irrelevant. Another approach is to limit
the number of mode hops could be by implementing a photorefractive
crystal for wavelength selection instead of a grating, as this may
lead to a higher mode selection resolution. A stable widely tunable
mid-infrared single-frequency source could be tested for use in mid-
infrared spectral imaging and spectroscopy.
As the system shows great potential for tunable single-frequency
nonlinear conversion, it is obvious to exchange the ampliﬁers and
crystal for generation of other wavelengths than those demonstrated
in this project.

Abstract 
Today, most nonlinear frequency conversion laser sources are based on solid state or fiber lasers, 
where high intracavity laser fields enable high frequency conversion. These lasers are complex, bulky 
and very expensive. Nonlinear frequency conversion of diode lasers on the other hand is a less 
complex, bulky and cheaper method to generate laser light in the visible and mid-infrared spectral 
range for a range of applications 
Diode lasers are very simple and compact and volume manufacturing results in very low cost. 
However, nonlinear frequency conversion of diode laser can only be performed in single-pass 
configurations using the light emitted from the diode laser cavity. Intracavity frequency conversion is 
not possible due to the low damage threshold of the diode laser material.  
The aim of this project is to enable high nonlinear conversion efficiency by use of a coupled cavity 
scheme in which the emitted light from the diode laser is resonantly enhanced. A specific goal is to 
efficiently generate widely tunable mid-infrared light by difference frequency generation. The work 
will be based on numerical modelling of the nonlinear interaction and optical system supporting the 
experimental demonstrations.
