Abstract
Deformation of austenitic stainless steels is known to inﬂuence the corrosion properties. In
this work, 0.2mm sheet material of two austenitic steel grades (AISI304 and AISI201) were de-
formed by uniaxial tension testing and investigated in terms of changes in the microstructure,
surface topography and corrosion properties. The work presented is related to the performance
of deep drawn ECG electrodes, where the best possible corrosion resistance is essential. The
ECG eletrods are included as case study and compared to the study of the deformed sheet
material. Changes in microstructure, phase fractions, surface topography and electrochem-
ical properties were examined by Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM), X-Ray Diﬀraction
(XRD), Light Optical Microscopy (LOM), confocal microscopy and potentiodynamic scans.
α’-martensite and roughness were found to increase by increasing deformation and are greatest
for AISI201. The pitting potential and open circuit potential decreased by increasing defor-
mation of AISI201, where the pitting potential increased at low deformation degree (10%
strain) and then decreased at increasing deformation of AISI304. SCC suceptibility of the
studs were found to be greater by larger residual stresses in the material. A greater fraction
of α’-martensite were found in the AISI201 studs along with a lower pitting potential than of
AISI304.

Introduction
Austenitic stainless steels possess excellent formability, high strength and good corrosion prop-
erties whereas it has been employed in many applications, ranging from nuclear power plants
to medical devices [1, 2, 3]. Cold deformation of austenitic steels does, however, introduce
stresses and martensite formation, which is found to aﬀect the corrosion properties [4, 5]. This
is, for example the case of the deep-drawn ECG electrode, where corrosion products and cracks
are visible on the steel stud. Therefore, a fundamental study will be carried out in this thesis
to understand how deformation of stainless steel is aﬀecting its microstructure, surface topog-
raphy and corrosion properties. Two metastable austenitic steels will be investigated in this
fundamental study, namely AISI201 and AISI304. ECG electrodes of AISI201 and AISI304
will furthermore be investigated as a case study and be compared to the fundamental study.
1.1
ECG electrodes
Electrocardiography (ECG) is a diagnostic tool, which is routinely used for assessing the
electrical and muscular functions of the heart. ECG electrodes are placed on the skin of the
patient and connected by wires to the ECG hardware to obtain an electrocardiograph. The
purpose of the electrode is to create a contact to the skin, transmit the signal and enable
connection to the hardware. Good electrical conductivity of the electrode is therefore crucial
for obtaining precise and reliable measurements. An ECG electrode is presented in ﬁgure
1.1.1.

eﬀect of deformation on the corrosion properties to answer the following research questions:
• How is the material aﬀected by plastic deformation?
• How are these changes aﬀecting the corrosion properties of the steel?
• What is the relation between the deformation and corrosion properties?
• How is the sheet material performing in comparison to the ECG studs?

2.2
Deforming steel
Many products are shaped by deformation, for example, in the case of the ECG electrode.
Deforming metals below its recrystallization temperature (roughly 450◦C for steels) intro-
duces changes in the microstructure, stress state, surface topography and corrosion proper-
ties [18]. Several deformation mechanisms can take place in the material during deforma-
tion. The Stacking Fault Energy (SFE) of the material determines whether dislocation glide
(>45mJ/m2), twinning (18-45mJ/m2) or martensite (<18mJ/m2) will be the dominating de-
formation mechanism [15, 19]. The SFE is evaluated as 14mJ/m2 for AISI201 and 10mJ/m2
for AISI304, according to the equation presented by [20].
The calculated SFEs therefore
suggest the martensitic transformation as the dominating deformation mechanism of both
steels.

2.2.1
Deformation induced martensite
AISI201 and 304 are metastable austenitic stainless steels which are known to form martensite
upon deformation, commonly referred to as deformation induced martensite (DIM). Marten-
site is a non-equilibrium phase occurring from a diﬀusionless transformation [18]. Two types
of martensite exists: The magnetic btc α’-martensite, which is thermodynamically much more
stable than the non-magnetic hcp ϵ-martensite [9, 10, 11, 21]. Shear bands serve as nucleation
sites for α’-martensite and may be in the form of ϵ-martensite, mechanical twins or dislo-
cations [22]. In literature, a controversy is, however, about the phase deformations taking
place. Mangonon and Thomas reports the martensitic transformation as being γ →ϵ →α’
[23]. Shen et al. conﬁrm this transformation process of AISI304, as only ϵ formed until a true
strain of 30% where α’ started to form [19]. A similar behaviour is reported by [24], where ϵ
appear as the ﬁrst phase, followed by α’ which at fracture accounted for 78% and only traces
of ϵ were to be seen. Kisko et al. reports the transformation as being directly from austen-
ite to α’-martensite [25]. When taking temperature into consideration, [22] documented the
transformation process to be temperature-dependent where deformation of AISI304 at -196◦C
resulted in the formation of both ϵ and α’, where deformation at -20◦C resulted in mechanical
twins. ϵ is therefore favoured at lower temperatures and accounts for a smaller fraction of the
formed shear bands at increasing temperatures. At room temperature, the process is therefore
γ →α’. This is conﬁrmed by a similar study, where samples deformed at -196◦C contained
less than 1% of ϵ-martensite and where no ϵ-martensite were to be found in samples deformed
at 25◦C [4].
The martensitic transformation is inﬂuenced by several factors, including the strain level,
strain rate, stress state and temperature at which the deformation is taking place [11, 19, 26].
Staudhammer et al. conducted strain tests of AISI304 and found: 1) at low strain levels
(<25%), the α’-martensite content increased by increasing strain rate due to shear band for-
mation and 2) at larger strain levels (>25%), high strain rates caused lower α’-martensite
content due to the local heating [27]. Moreover, the chemical composition plays a crucial
role in the martensite formation as some elements serve as austenite stabilisers. Increasing
the austenite stability hence suppresses the austenite-martensite transformation [28]. This is
underlined by the expression Md30, which is a parameter indicating the lowest temperature
where 50% vol. fraction has been transformed to martensite at a true strain of 0.3. This
parameter is calculated from the elemental composition, where a lower Md30 value indicate a
greater stability in regards to martensite formation [14]. The equation for calculating Md30

2.2.2
Residual stresses
Plastic deformation introduces stresses to the material which remains after removal of the
original stress-initiator, referred to as residual stresses. Such stresses are considered as two
types: macro-stresses extending over larger areas and micro-stresses ranging over fractions of
a millimeter and thereby are very local [34]. These stresses are caused by dislocations in the
crystal lattice and by martensite formation, as a volume change occurs from the change in the
crystal lattice. The γ →α’ causes a 2.6% volume expansion where γ →ϵ’ result in a volume
reduction of 0.8% [35].
2.2.3
Grain size
Another eﬀect of deformation is grain size reduction [36]. The grain size aﬀects the martensite
formation, as a larger grain size results in a larger fraction of martensite [33]. This is due

2.3
Corrosion of stainless steel
Iron is naturally found as iron oxides, such as Fe3O4 (magnetite) or Fe2O3 (hematite) where
it is at the lowest energy state. Upon extraction of Fe, the material is brought to a higher
energy state, which is thermodynamically unfavourable. As a consequence, corrosion happens
since the material returns to the low-energy state [7].
2.3.1
Passivity
Alloying iron-based alloys with >10.5wt% chromium will make them ’stainless’ [47], where an
increasing chromium content will increase the corrosion resistance [7]. The chromium reacts
with the surrounding oxygen and thereby form an oxide ﬁlm in close contact with the surface.
This ﬁlm acts as a barrier for the electron ﬂow due to the oxide’s limited ionic and electric
conductivity [48]. As the ﬁlm forms in close contact with the surface, it is greatly aﬀected
by discontinuities, such as changes in the crystal structure [7]. Therefore, the martensitic
phase plays a crucial role in changing the ﬁlm composition, as integrality and compactness of
the ﬁlm are destroyed [5]. This aﬀects the ability to limit the conductivity and to withstand
attacks, which is documented by [4] where the increase in deformation led to a decrease in
corrosion resistance.

2.3
Corrosion of stainless steel
2.3.2
Pitting corrosion
The oxide ﬁlm is conditional and under certain environmental conditions, breakdown of the
ﬁlm can happen [49]. Pitting corrosion is a local breakdown of the passive ﬁlm at the free
surface, where the ﬁlm is not able to repassivate. Pitting is a typical corrosion type of passive
metals, such as stainless steels, in the presence of chlorides. Chlorides (and halides in general)
are assumed to initiate the pitting corrosion as these penetrate weak points of the passive ﬁlm.
In the case of deformation induced martensite where a less dense ﬁlm is forming, pitting would
initiate. A local breakdown of the martensite would cause it to act anodic to the austenitic
phase, which is cathodically protected by the oxide ﬁlm [4]. There will always be a balance
between the anodic and cathodic reaction where a larger cathodic area will cause an increase
in the anodic reaction, causing a high corrosion rate of the martensite [7]. The adsorption of
chlorides strongly increases the ion conductivity, enabling the metal ions to migrate through
the ﬁlm. The pitting potential, Ep, deﬁnes the potential at which the local corrosion will take
place. This value is directly aﬀected by the chloride content, as can be seen in equation 2.3.1
which is an expression for a 18-8CrNi steel (AISI304) [7]
Ep = 0.168 −0.088 log aCl−(V, SHE).
(2.3.1)
aCl−is the Cl−concentration.
A diﬀerentiation is made between the pit initiation and pit growth. The resistance towards
pit initiation is expressed by the Pitting Resistance Equivalent Number (PREN), which relies
on the alloying elements. The formula for calculating PREN is presented in equation 2.3.2.
PREN = %Cr + 3.3%Mo + 16%N
(2.3.2)
The PREN of AISI201 and AISI304 are calculated to be 18.6 for both grades.
The equation states that an increase in Cr, Mo and N increases the resistance. Especially
N, which impact is 16 times greater than Cr. Pit growth is similarly aﬀected by the alloying
elements and is prevented by increasing the content of Cr, N and Mo and decreasing the
content of Si, S and C [7].
The grain size aﬀects the corrosion properties of steels, where a smaller grain size improves
the corrosion properties [50, 51, 52]. This improvement is achieved since a decrease in the
grain size causes a reduction in the passive current density, revealing a higher resistance of
the oxide ﬁlm towards pitting attacks. The pitting potential was, however, not aﬀected by
the change in grain size [53].

Corrosion of stainless steel
2.3.3
Crevice corrosion
Crevice corrosion is a corrosion type quite similar to pitting corrosion. Where pitting happens
at the free surface, crevice corrosion takes place where a stagnant solution is trapped near
a surface. The roughness of a surface plays a signiﬁcant role, as narrow valleys may act as
micro-crevices. A great surface roughness will cause the formation of numerous small local
corrosion attacks, while smoother surfaces will experience few, but larger attacks [7]. Stefec
and Franz performed studies on samples having similar roughness of diﬀerent deformation
degrees and found an increase in pit numbers and decrease in size by increasing deformation
[54].
2.3.4
Stress Corrosion Cracking
Stress Corrosion Cracking (SCC) is crack propagation under a tensile load in a chemically
active environment at which the material is susceptible [34]. Multiple studies document that
the presence of residual stresses increases the susceptibility of SCC [55, 56, 57]. Nam et al.
studied the eﬀect of residual stresses in pipelines introduced by weldings and found that an
increase in residual stresses led to a shorter SCC initiation time [58]. A similar study was
conducted, where diﬀerent deformation degrees of AISI304 were investigated in regards to
SCC susceptibility. The strain was found to increase by increasing deformation, which led
to a reduced crack initiation time [4]. A diﬀerence has also been reported on diﬀerent types
of residual stresses, where tensile stresses contributed to SCC and compressive revealed to
be beneﬁcial by delaying the SCC [59]. This is believed to be caused by the reduction of
interatomic spacing, which facilitates the formation and maintenance of the passive ﬁlm [60].

Discussion
5.1
Eﬀects from plastic deformation
Specimens were strained by tension testing to 10, 30 and 55 % strain. AISI201 work hardens
at a higher rate and to a greater level than AISI304, which is conﬁrmed by the hardness
measurements. This may be due to the reduction in grain size, as documented by the Hall-
Petch relationship [38]. Another possible reason is the formation of martensite, as this is a
phase with higher hardness than austenite [18]. These statements align with the micrographs,
where a change is to be seen by an increase in dislocation glide, possible deformation twins
and martensite formation and less deﬁned grains by increasing deformation. Martensite was
not only visible but also detectable. At 30%, AISI201 had a martensite volume fraction of
∼15%, which increased to 37% at 55% strain. The detectable fractions were not as great
in AISI304 as 1% martensite was to detect in RD and 0% in TD at 30% strain increasing
to ∼5% martensite at 55% strain. The low SFE of both materials suggested the martensitic
transformation as being the dominant deformation mechanism [15, 19]. This is not reﬂected in
the martensite fractions determined by XRD and the micrographs, where severe deformation
is evident.
A larger fraction of martensite was present in AISI201 than AISI304. This aligns with the
Md30 calculations of 44◦C for AISI201 and -20◦C for AISI304, suggesting a greater resistance
towards martensite formation of AISI304.
This was also observed in the literature study
presented in table 2.2.2, where AISI201 generally formed larger fractions of martensite. The
reported martensite fractions are, however, greater than observed in this study. This might
be attributed to the specimen straining, where slipping occurred in the gripping jaws causing
deformation outside the narrow region. The real strain may therefore not be equal the assumed
strain. This discrepancy between the real strain and assumed strain may be supported by
the roughness, where a linear relationship between strain and increased roughness [64] was
observed until 30% strain.
The roughness deviated from the linear tendencies at greater

Studhammer documented higher strain rates at deformation above 25% strain to result in
greater α’-martensite fractions [27]. The martensite content increased by increasing strain
of both grades. This is in accordance with the theory, where applying stress introduces the
transformation where a greater stress level will result in more transformations taking place.
Based upon studies conducted by [43] and [41], a similar roughness was expected as the grain
size of the as-received conditions were found to be similar to the XRD analysis. This deviation
from the results reported and the non-linear development may either be due to the mismatch
in expected deformation and real deformation or the larger martensite fraction forming in the
AISI201, as a greater volume expansion occurs. Consequently, a greater hardness, roughness
and martensite fraction formed in AISI201 by deformation.
5.2
Change in corrosion properties
Deformation of austenite has been documented to inﬂuence the corrosion properties, which
is caused by the formation of martensite [5], the introduction of residual stresses [4] and
increased roughness [7]. The corrosion properties of the strained sheet material were tested
by polarization where the corrosion potential and pitting potential decreased by increasing
deformation of AISI201. The pitting potential of AISI304, on the other hand, increases upon
10% strain and then decreases by increasing strain. A similar result has been reported by
[65], where the pitting potential of heavy cold worked AISI304 increased to higher values
than the as-received condition, which is due to the shift from tensile to compressive stresses

may be regraded as non-excising. It is, however, compressive stresses and they closer to 0

a relatively low roughness and no martensite. The data of AISI304 deformed to 0% only

more than 1100 mV vs SCE. These were excluded as no other cases have been found in the

process instead of metal oxidation. Another explanation is simply a measurement error. The
corrosion potential and pitting potentia from 10% strain follow the tendency of a decrease by
increasing deformation.
A diﬀerence was not only to be seen in the corrosion- and pitting potential, but also how the
pitting appeared. Pits increased in number and decreased in size by increased deformation.
This is, according to Bardal, due to the roughness increase [7] where Stefec found the increased
deformation to be the cause of increased pit number and decrease in size. The martensite was
not investigated in these studies but the diﬀerence in the oxide ﬁlm was addressed, which very
likely is due to the martensite formation. The increased roughness and martensite formation
therefore, in combination, enhance this change in pitting. This may be the explanation for
the rapid penetration causing sell leakage of AISI201 deformed to 55% strain, where a great
roughness is measured and high martensite content is present.
The surface investigation revealed many small black dots on anodically cleaned AISI201 sam-
ples. As a decrease is to be seen by increasing deformation, it is most likely not inclusions
as the same amount would be expected to be present in all deformation degrees. Therefore,
these are likely holes originating from the anodic rinse in NaOH. This does also align with
previously mentioned, where a greater deformation degree and surface roughness increases
the number of pits. Therefore, small pits may already have been formed on the sample prior
to conducting the polarization, underlining the eﬀect of increased martensite and roughness
decreases the pitting resistance. None of these pits were to be seen at any of the surfaces
of AISI304. This is also suggested by the roughness increase of the corroded samples, where
AISI201 increases from 1.15µm before polarization to 2.0µm and AISI304 which increased
from 0.75µm to 1.7µm.

a greater nobility of AISI201 by having an Ecorr of 53mV vs SCE where the on of AISI304

value at 55% of 397mV vs SCE. AISI304 does, despite a pitting potential similar to a greater

sheet material performed better at all deformation degrees. In regards to SCC, fewer cracks
originated at AISI201 in comparison to AISI304. The residual stress measurement of the sheet
material revealed a greater tensile stress state in AISI304 than in AISI201, which has been
documented to increase the SCC susceptibility and at a faster rate [25, 66]. This is, however,
a simpliﬁcation of the stress state, as the ECG electrode is produced under distinctly diﬀerent
circumstance and by a more complex shaping process. Drøhse documented a high stress level
in the necking, which may be related to a large martensite fraction. In combination with the
great roughness on the inside of the necking of Sa 4.7, pitting has most likely occurred where
from the stress could be relieved as cracks.

Conclusions
This thesis has investigated the eﬀect of deformation of austenitic stainless steel to understand
the eﬀect on the corrosion properties. Deformation of AISI201 and AISI304 was obtained by
uniaxial straining where changes in microstructure, surface topography and corrosion proper-
ties were quantiﬁed. The ECG electrodes have been investigated as a case study and compared
to the fundamental study conducted on the sheet material. The main conclusions obtained
from this work are:
• The α’-martensite fraction and roughness increased by increasing strain, where grain
size decreased by increasing strain.
• The deformation led to a decrease in both corrosion potential and pitting potential by
increased deformation of AISI201. For AISI304, 10% strain led to an increase in the
pitting potential, which then decreased by increasing deformation. AISI304 possesses a
greater pitting potential and nobility than AISI201 at all deformation degrees.
• Larger fractions of martensite were found in studs of AISI201 than AISI304. ECG studs
of AISI304 had a greater pitting potential along with a greater susceptibility to stress
corrosion cracking.
• A greater roughness was observed at the studs than of the most deformed sheet material.
AISI201 did in both the fundamental study and case study possess a greater fraction of
martensite and a lower corrosion and pitting potential than AISI304.
It was in general found that deformation of stainless steel has a signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the
corrosion properties of austenitic stainless steels due to martensite formation and a change in
the topography.

6.1
Further work
6.1
Further work
Work is still to be done to conclude whether martensite formation or roughness plays the great-
est role in the change of corrosion properties. This could be done by polarising samples with
equal martensite content where the surface roughness is gradually reduced by electropolishing.
Anodic cleanse of the sheet material showed improved corrosion properties. This test should
be applied on the studs to validate whether corrosion improvements could be obtained. Rough-
ness increase at the sheet material resulted in a decrease in Ep. Far greater roughness values
were observed at the stud, being highest at position #3 and #4. As pits nucleate and prop-
agate from the inside, roughness decrease of this area could be investigated further.
As
roughness plays a signiﬁcant role, the anodic rinse eﬀect on the surface roughness should
futhermore be tested.
Diﬀerent designs exists of the ECG steel stud. Polarisation could as well be conducted to
determine if one design performs better in regards to corrosion.
AISI201 formed larger fractions of martensite than AISI304. AISI202 possess a higher content
of Mn, Ni and N and a lower content of C, S and P, which theoretically provides a greater
austenite stabilisation and fewer impurities which both directly aﬀect the corrosion properties.
The martensite formation in AISI202 and AISI304 were compared and found to be far lower
in AISI202 [15]. It is therefore recommended to investigate AISI202 as a possible substitute
due to the lower martensite formation and lower impurity content.
