Abstract
Colloidal caesium lead halide perovskite nanocrystals have recently emerged as ver-
satile optoelectronic materials, with potential applications ranging from solar cells
to light-emitting diodes and non-classical light sources.
Their easy processabil-
ity, high wavelength tunability and near-optimum antibunching at room tempera-
ture makes them perfect candidates for on-chip single-photon sources for quantum
technology applications. However, to date, single-photon emission from electrically
driven caesium lead halide perovskite quantum dots has not been demonstrated
yet. Herein, a complete guide describing all the steps towards an electrically driven
single-photon source based on CsPbBr3 nanocrystals is presented. Firstly, zwitte-
rionic ligand capped quantum dots are synthesised by colloidal methods and the
resulting samples are characterised by transmission electron microscopy, revealing
a cubic crystalline phase. Secondly, a theoretical prediction of the excitonic levels
in cubic CsPbBr3 nanocrystals suggest the existence of three bright triplet states
and a dark singlet state. The validity of these predictions is conﬁrmed by photolu-
minescence experiments, which show three peaks corresponding to the three triplet
states. Optical characterisation also reveals certain intrinsic limitation of caesium
lead halide perovskite nanocrystals, such as blinking or degradation under ambient
conditions. Next, a recipe to fabricate a solution-processed electrically driven de-
vice is presented, putting especial emphasis on its scalability. Finally, a numerical
model compares the dynamics of optically and electrically driven devices. The re-
sults obtained suggest that, due to a fast trion Auger recombination, the biexciton
formation is less likely to occur in the electrically driven case. This is a beneﬁt for
single-photon emission, as biexciton radiative decay deteriorates the antibunching
properties of the source.

Preface
This thesis was intended to be carried out in the Quantum Photonics group at
Nanyang Technological University (NTU), in Singapore, for a period of four months.
At ﬁrst, it was given a fully experimental approach and, as such, laboratory exper-
iments were essential. The ultimate planned goal of this thesis was to fabricate
and test an electrically driven single-photon source based on perovskite quantum
dots. However, amid the global Covid-19 crisis, Singapore applied the so-called ‘cir-
cuit breaker’ for a period of 55 days, in which all the non-essential activities were
stopped. For this reason, only two out of the four planned months for laboratory
work were carried out. The thesis was then given a more theoretical approach, as
some of the expected experimental results were unattainable.
This report is structured as follows. Chapter 1 motivates the research on single-
photon sources and introduces the current status and future perspectives of per-
ovskites as optoelectronic materials. Chapter 2 describes the chemical synthesis of
caesium lead bromide perovskite quantum dots and shows the experimental results
obtained. Chapter 3 is a theoretical discussion of the band structure of caesium
lead bromide perovskite quantum dots, which is necessary to understand the optical
properties reported in chapter 4. Apart from experimental results, chapter 4 also
introduces the concept of second-order photon correlation, which was planned to be
investigated experimentally, but ﬁnally was not done due to the ‘circuit breaker’.
Next, chapter 5 explains the design of an electrically driven single-photon device. Al-
though the implementation of this design was done right before the ‘circuit breaker’,
no experimental data supports this. Thus, chapter 5 is kept under a theoretical
framework only. Finally, chapter 6 introduces a model to describe carrier dynamics
in electrically driven quantum dot single-photon sources and compares its beneﬁts
with respect to the optically driven case.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Quantum physics has established the bases of a revolution in information and com-
munication technologies.
During the past years, many algorithms and protocols
that exploit the spookiness of quantum nature to solve diﬃcult computational and
communication problems have been published [1]. A widespread implementation of
these algorithms may lead to an important impact not only in the scientiﬁc ﬁeld but
also in global economy and society [2].
As an example, the Shor algorithm proposes a method for integer factorisation
based on the superposition of quantum states [3]. For a classical computer, the com-
putation complexity for factorising a certain integer scales superpolynomially with
the number of digits [4]. Interestingly, for a quantum computer implementing the
Shor algorithm, this dependence is only polynomial. Current public key cryptogra-
phy systems are based on decomposing large prime numbers, which is a task that
takes several hundreds of years for classical computers. Instead, a quantum com-
puter might be able to solve the required calculations in a shorter time, being able to
decrypt the information eﬀectively. This has a tremendous impact on cybersecurity
and subsequently on society.
Another example is the BB84 protocol, which suggests a method to securely
share a key between two communication points [5].
It relies on the no-cloning
theorem, which states that it is impossible to create a copy of an arbitrary unknown
quantum state [6]. In the BB84 protocol, every qubit is randomly sent in one of
two bases, for instance, the horizontal-vertical photon polarization basis and its
Hadamard transform [7]. A strategy for an eavesdropper in this communication
channel would be to intercept the message, duplicate it, and send it again through
the same communication channel such that neither the receiver nor the emitter would
notice its presence. However, as dictated by the no-cloning theorem, it is impossible
to duplicate reliably all the qubits without knowledge of the basis in which they
are sent. For this reason, the eavesdropper would make a certain error rate when
sending the copied qubits, which imprints a signature of its presence in the channel.
By comparing a number of control bits, the emitter and the receiver can ensure
that the key has been shared between and only between the two communication
points or, alternatively, that there is an eavesdropper in the channel. This protocol
provides a solution for private key distribution schemes, allowing provably secure
communication. Again, this has a huge impact on cybersecurity and, in turn, on

The aforementioned quantum schemes, among many others proposed in recent
literature, have already been tested in the laboratory [8, 9]. Diﬀerent platforms have
been used to encode qubits, such as charges in Josephson junctions [10] or atomic
spins in optical lattices [11]. However, these platforms suﬀer from decoherence due
to strong interaction with the environment, mainly through phonon scattering. To
avoid this phenomenon, an actual implementation of these schemes requires very
low temperatures and a complex setup for qubit manipulation, thus making the
technology only available inside the laboratory.
Photons are a promising alternative to host qubits, where information can be
encoded, for instance, in the polarization or in the photon number basis. Due to their
weak interaction with the environment, photons are very robust to decoherence, even
at room temperature [12]. Furthermore, at optical frequencies, the thermal photon
density at room temperature is very low, thus providing a reduced background noise
[13]. For these reasons, photons are promising platforms to host qubits in quantum
devices that can have an actual industrial implementation.
One of the necessary components to implement certain quantum protocols are
single-photon sources. Reimer et al. described in [14] the desirable properties that a
single-photon source should fulﬁl. Firstly, it must emit one and only one photon at
a time, with vanishing probability of multiphoton emission. Secondly, photons must
be indistinguishable, meaning that they must have the same spectrum, polarization
and spatial mode. Thirdly, photons must be emitted on demand, ideally triggered by
an electrical impulse. As a fourth requirement, the photon source must have a high
extraction eﬃciency, possibly aided by a photonic crystal structure that increases
the spontaneous emission rate due to Purcell eﬀect [15]. Finally, all the fabrication
processes must be scalable and compatible with current semiconductor technology.
So far, one of the most promising single-photon sources, with potential to fulﬁl all
the aforementioned requirements, are quantum dots [16, 17]. Particularly, extensive
research has been done in III-V materials, such as InAs/GaAs systems [18]. When
a thin ﬁlm of InAs is grown on a GaAs substrate, the strain generated by the
lattice mismatch between the two materials makes the InAs molecules to aggregate,
forming small islands after a very thin wetting layer [19]. Due to a convenient band
alignment between the two compounds, these islands become potential wells for
charge carriers, eﬀectively creating quantum dots. This method is named Stranski-
Krastanov growth [20]. Furthermore, quantum dots can be embedded in an optical
cavity, such as a photonic crystal [21] or a nanowire [22], which maximises the
extraction eﬃciency while reducing the adverse eﬀects of phonon interaction [23].
However, this approach has certain drawbacks. Firstly, it requires very precise
growth techniques. For Stranski-Krastanov growth, molecular beam epitaxy is nor-
mally used. This method is, in general, not scalable due to its elevated operating
cost and limited throughput [19]. Even with it, the quantum dot formation is a
random process, and their location can hardly be controlled [24]. This makes it par-
ticularly laborious to tailor photonic structures around the quantum dot. Secondly,
the presence of the wetting layer creates a continuum of states very close in energy
to the conﬁned excitons. This provides a path for decoherence and therefore deterio-
rates the optical properties of the quantum dots [25]. Finally, rare earth metals are,
in general, scarce. Currently, indium and gallium are obtained as a by-product of
zinc and bauxite mining, respectively. This means that their production is limited

1.2
Scope
The reasons exposed so far justify the need for research on alternatives in scalable
single-photon sources. The scope of this project is to explore caesium lead halide
perovskite quantum dots as candidates with the potential to fulﬁl all the aforemen-
tioned requirements.
A perovskite is any material of general chemical formula ABX3 that forms a
crystal lattice in which the atom A is accommodated between four BX6 octahedra
[28]. In the most simple conﬁguration, this disposition may form a cubic lattice,
shown in ﬁgure 1.1A, where the atom A occupies the body center position (1

Of particular interest is the orthorhombic arrangement, which only diﬀers from the

organic metal halide perovskites, where A is an organic radical such as methylam-

monium, B is a metal such as lead and X is a halide element such as iodine. This
perovskite type has attracted attention due to its high optical absorption coeﬃcients
[32], long carrier diﬀusion lengths [33] and high quantum yield at room temperature
[34]. These characteristics make organic metal halide perovskites perfect candidates
as absorbing layers for solar cells [35].
Motivated by the success of their organic counterparts, extensive research has
started on all-inorganic lead halide perovskites. Although this material type has
been known for a long time [36], Protesescu et al. recently demonstrated in [37] a
novel procedure to synthesise caesium lead halide perovskite nanocrystals with col-
loidal methods. For this material type, A is caesium, B is lead and X is a halide ele-
ment such as bromine, iodine or chlorine. Due to their reduced size, the synthesised
nanocrystals eﬀectively act as quantum dots. Thanks to this, the aforementioned
article proved caesium lead halide perovskite nanocrystals to be outstanding photon
emitters with quantum yield as high as 90 %, emission linewidths in the order of
few tenths of nanometers and high emission wavelength tunability by changing the
halide proportion.
After this publication, many other works reported interesting results for caesium
lead halide perovskite nanocrystals [38]. Thanks to their high carrier mobility, large
diﬀusion lengths [39] and outstanding tolerance to defects [40], these materials show
potential applications ranging from solar cells [41, 42] to light-emitting diodes [43, 44]
and optically driven single-photon emitters [45], with near-optimum antibunching
even at room temperature [46]. However, to date, there has not been any attempt
to design an electrically driven single-photon source with this material. The present
work will explore the possibilities of caesium lead bromide (CsPbBr3) for this latter
application, putting particular emphasis on its scalability. The halide used in this
work is bromide. This is an arbitrary choice since similar optoelectronic properties
have been reported for other halides, such as iodine or chlorine, or even mixtures of
them [47]. The degree of freedom given by the halide composition allows to transfer
most of the results obtained for these materials to other wavelengths, conferring
them great ﬂexibility and applicability.
An informed reader may be disappointed by the fact that this work is based
on perovskites that contain lead. Lead is a highly poisonous element that aﬀects
human beings after long term exposure. Unfortunately, it has been widely used in
electronics for several years. A great percentage of the electronic residues generated
in Europe are exported to waste-receiving countries such as China, India or Ghana
[48]. These residues often end in a landﬁll, where they are unsafely recycled. It
has been shown that these recycling activities are linked to lead poisoning of the
workers in the landﬁlls [49], among which children are especially susceptible due to
the cumulative nature and long term eﬀects of this hazard. To tackle this problem,
the European Union developed the Restriction of Hazardous Substances directive
(RoHS), which sets a limit of 1000 parts per million of the amount of lead that
a commercial device can have [50]. A quick calculation shows that the number of
lead atoms in a single CsPbBr3 quantum dot is of the order of 106. Even if several
quantum dots are used as single-photon sources in a device, they would amount
an inﬁnitesimal fraction of the weight of the whole device, thus complying with
RoHS standards. Alternatively, recent publications have shown the possibility to
substitute lead by other elements with similar electronic conﬁguration, such as tin,
although at expenses of a lower quantum yield [51].

Chapter 2
Preparation of caesium lead halide
perovskite nanoparticles
The chemical synthesis of CsPbBr3 nanoparticles was a critical part of this work,
as it determined the optoelectronic properties of the resulting samples.
In this
chapter, the general method used to synthesise caesium lead halide nanocrystals,
and particularly CsPbBr3, is explained, as well as the characterisation of the ﬁnal
product with transmission electron microscopy.
2.1
Zwitterionic ligand capping
The high surface to volume ratio of nanoparticles results in a high reactivity and col-
loidal instability compared to their bulk counterparts [52]. To prevent nanoparticles
to aggregate and interact with each other, a surface capping ligand is generally used.
In the case of lead halide perovskite nanocrystals, the surface is typically terminated
with long-chain ionic molecules such as carboxylates or phosphonates, which bind to
excess surface ions [53]. Particularly, in CsPbBr3, organic molecules with functional
groups COOH– and NH +
3 bind to the oppositely charged surface ions in the crystal
to act as surface ligands. However, in presence of a solvent, the capping molecules
attached to the nanocrystal are in ionic equilibrium with their dissolved forms, as
shown in ﬁgure 2.1. The net eﬀect of this equilibrium is an enhanced desorption of
the capping molecules, which in its turn leads to poor stability of the nanocrystals.
To prevent this, Krieg et al. suggested in [54] the following approach. Instead of us-
ing two diﬀerent long-chain molecules hosting either the cationic or anionic ligands,
a single zwitterionic molecule, where both ligands are combined, was used. The
ionic equilibrium, which is shown in ﬁgure 2.2, is then displaced towards the state
where the capping molecules are bonded to the nanocrystals, eﬀectively isolating
them.
Zwitterionic ligand capped CsPbBr3 nanocrystals in this work were synthesised
using the aforementioned approach, which is thoroughly described in [54].
The
method includes, ﬁrstly, the preparation of the three reactants, which are caesium
oleate, lead (II) oleate and trioctylphosphine bromide. Secondly, the mixture of these
reactants to obtain CsPbBr3 nanoparticles and ﬁnally, the puriﬁcation process.

2.2.3
Synthesis of trioctylphosphine bromide
This step was slightly modiﬁed with respect to the recipe suggested by Krieg et al.
in [54]. Instead of directly adding bromine to trioctylphosphine, which triggers a
strongly exothermic reaction that forms a dense precipitate, trioctylphosphine was
ﬁrst dissolved in toluene. Thanks to this, the reaction upon addition of bromine was
more controllable and the precipitate was easily dissolved. Thus, using a three-neck
ﬂask, 6 mL of trioctylphosphine were kept under nitrogen atmosphere and stirred
with 18.7 mL of toluene. With the help of a burette, 0.6 mL of bromine were slowly
poured into the three-neck ﬂask, in which the following reaction occurred.
2 C24H51P + Br2 + H+
2 C24H52BrP
Due to the exothermic nature of the reaction, the three-neck ﬂask was cooled down
to room temperature using a water bath. This yielded a 0.46 M trioctylphosphine
bromide solution in toluene. The three-neck ﬂask was kept under nitrogen for further
use.
If other halide compositions were desired, this step would be modiﬁed to include
them instead of bromine. For the case of iodine, as it is presented in liquid form
at room temperature, the process would be equivalent to the one described above.
However, for the case of chlorine, which exists in gas form at room temperature, a
more elaborate gas handling system would be required.
2.3
Synthesis of CsPbBr3 nanocrystals
The prepared reactants were then used to obtain CsPbBr3 nanocrystals. Firstly,
0.4 mL of caesium oleate and 0.5 mL of lead (II) oleate stock solutions were mixed
in a three-neck ﬂask along with 0.1 mmol of 3-(N,N-dimethyloctadecylammonio)
propanesulfonate, which acted as the zwitterionic ligand. Next, 5 mL of 1-octadecene
were added before the mixture was stirred and heated up to 180 ◦C while kept un-
der nitrogen atmosphere. Subsequently, 0.5 mL of trioctylphosphine bromide stock
solution were injected. The three-neck ﬂask was immediately cooled down to room
temperature using a water bath. This yielded a crude solution of CsPbBr3 nanocrys-
tals capped with zwitterionic ligands in toluene and 1-octadecene. The formation of
the nanocrystals was accompanied by a notable change in the color of the solution,
which turned glowing green, especially when illuminated under ultraviolet light.

All three transitions from the triplet states are dipole allowed, while the transition

In a perfect CsPbBr3 cubic lattice, these three optically active states would be
degenerate. However, in reality, certain eﬀects distort the symmetry of the crystal.
In ﬁrst place, it is well known that the position of the Cs+ ion between the PbBr6
octahedra ﬂuctuates, generating a local dipole moment in the lattice [75]. Moreover,
the Pb orbital involved in the hybridisation possesses a pair of electrons that do not
take part in the binding, commonly known as lone pair. These stereochemical active
lone pairs may induce small displacements in the PbBr6 octahedra [76]. The two
aforementioned phenomena result in a local or global inversion symmetry breaking
of the nanocrystal cubic lattice [77], which in turn lifts the degeneracy between the
optically active excitonic states [78].
The optically active states represent three linear dipoles polarized along the
three ⟨100⟩directions in the crystal [79, 69].
The radiation pattern of a dipole
emitter surrounded by an homogeneous medium is omnidirectional in the plane
perpendicular to the polarisation direction and has a minimum along the polarisation
axis [80]. Thus, as it shall be discussed in chapter 4, the observability of these states
depend on the orientation of nanocrystal with respect to the optical setup.
Due to the fact that the exciton splitting is caused by asymmetries in the crystal
at local scale, it is diﬃcult to give an estimation of the value of this splitting.
However, it has been consistently observed experimentally by photoluminescence in
previous works, which obtained an oﬀset in energy ∆≈2 meV between the detected
peaks [45, 69].

4.1
Photoluminescence
The optical properties of the nanocrystals were explored by measuring their pho-
toluminescence. In this technique, the sample is excited with a laser beam with
photon energy larger than the bandgap of the material under study. Carriers are
generated and subsequently recombine, emitting photons with energy given by the
band structure of the sample. By measuring the wavelength of the emitted photons
with a spectrometer, an insight into the energy levels of the quantum dot can be
obtained. Furthermore, by scanning the surface of the sample, a spatial map of the
quantum dot distribution can be compiled.
Measurements were performed using the homemade setup shown in ﬁgure 4.1. A
ﬁber-coupled continuous-wave laser at 340 nm was used as the excitation source. The
light was input into the free-space optical system by means of a ﬁber coupler (FC1).
The collimated light beam in free-space was sent through a 50:50 beamsplitter (BS1)
that allowed to image the surface of the sample with a charge-coupled device (CCD)
camera. Upon reﬂection on a second 50:50 beamsplitter (BS2), which separated the
excitation and collection arms, the beam was deﬂected with a two-axis galvo motor.
Thanks to the small hysteresis of galvo scanners, this setup was able to raster the
surface of the sample with high precision and stability. The excitation beam was
focused onto the sample using a microscope objective.
A three-axis piezo stage
containing the sample holder allowed rough movements of the sample. Both the
microscope objective and the piezo stage with the sample were placed inside a closed-
loop helium cryostat capable of lowering the sample temperature down to 3.5 K. The
emission from the quantum dots was collected with the microscope objective and
guided through BS2. The excitation beam was removed using a longpass ﬁlter (F1).

4.2
Blinking and ﬂickering
It is well known that isolated quantum dots suﬀer from an intermittency in their
photoluminescence, even when driven under continuous wave illumination, which is
generally named blinking [81]. This eﬀect was ﬁrst reported by Nirmal et al. in [82]
for CdS quanutm dots, where they observed an on and oﬀbehavior in the timescale
of 0.5 s. Thereupon, many eﬀorts have been devoted to understand the physical
mechanisms behind this eﬀect, which damages the emissive properties of quantum
==PAGE:29==
Chapter 4. Optical characterisation
24
dots. There are two competing processes that explain blinking, which are Auger
recombination and surface trap induced recombination [83].
As it will be reviewed with detail in chapter 6, Auger recombination requires
the presence of at least three charge carriers in the quantum dot, as for example
two electrons and one hole, which form a negative trion. Upon recombination of
an electron-hole pair, instead of emitting a photon, the energy is transferred to the
third charge carrier, which is lifted to a highly energetic level in the same band [84].
Auger recombination rates for trions are much faster than radiative recombination
[85], which eﬀectively means that the photoluminescence is quenched when trions
are formed. As optical carrier injection implies charge conservation, quantum dot
charging is unlikely to occur. For the same reason, when the quantum dot is charged,
the injection of the complementary particle that balances the charge is also unlikely.
This explains the long on and oﬀtimes in Auger-assisted blinking.
The second mechanism that contributes to blinking, which is surface trap induced
recombination, is caused by the presence of uncoordinated atoms at the surface of
the crystal. These uncoordinated atoms create shallow trap states that allow a non-
radiative electron-hole recombination path [86, 87]. The desorption and absorption
of the capping ligands open and close these recombination paths, resulting in an
intermittent quenching of the photoluminescence.
It has been reported that, in
CsPbBr3 nanocrystals, this desorption process happens in a faster timescale than
Auger-assisted blinking [88]. Usually, when measured in a photoluminescence ex-
periment, the long acquisition times compared to the on and oﬀtimescale result in a
continuous increase and decrease of the signal rather than a sharp transition, which
is commonly known as ﬂickering.

Towards an electrically driven
device
To date, only optically driven single-photon emission has been achieved with CsPbBr3
quantum dots. The main purpose of this thesis, and what makes it distinct from
previous works, is to use CsPbBr3 quantum dots as single-photon sources in an elec-
trically driven device. In this chapter, the design of this device and its fabrication
is explained.
5.1
Carrier injection and device structure
Single-photon sources work in a very similar manner as other semiconductor light
sources, such as light-emitting diodes [93]. Electrons and holes are injected in a direct
bandgap semiconductor.
When their wave functions overlap, they may undergo
a recombination process, which emits a photon. In general, semiconductor light-
emitting diodes have three main regions, which are engineered to boost the photon
emission eﬃciency. These are the active region, the hole transport layer (HTL) and
the electron transport layer (ETL) [94]. The active region is where charge carriers
are accumulated and undergo the recombination process. The HTL injects holes
into the active region, but prevents the electrons to leak out from it. In a similar
way, the ETL brings electrons into the active regions but blocks the holes.
The novel device structure [95] proposed in this work is shown in ﬁgure 5.1. The
bottom layer consists of a commercially available glass substrate coated with indium
tin oxide (ITO), which is a transparent and conductive material. The HTL consists
of a 100 nm thick layer of poly(3,4-ethylenedioxythiophene):poly(styrene sulfonate)
(PEDOT:PSS) and a 10 nm layer of poly-N-vinylcarbazole (PVK). Quantum dot
light-emitting devices often suﬀer from a poor charge injection balance, which is
attributed to larger potential barriers in the HTL compared to the ETL [96]. The
conjunction of PEDOT:PSS and PVK form an intermediate step in the energy
levels of the injected holes that ease the injection [97], and has shown good results in
CsPbBr3 systems [43]. The active layer is a solution of the synthesised quantum dots
in poly(methyl methacrylate) (PMMA). PMMA is a non-conductive polymer which,
in this case, prevents electrons and holes to go through the active region without
transiting the quantum dots. Charge carriers enter the nanocrystals by tunneling
through the PMMA. As it is well known from quantum mechanics, the probability
of tunneling decays exponentially with the distance [71]. It is therefore clear that

5.2
Device fabrication
5.2.1
Substrate cleaning
The device fabrication started with cleaning the commercial ITO-coated glass sub-
strate. In ﬁrst place, the substrate was sonicated with three subsequent baths in
water with detergent, acetone and IPA [101]. Each bath had a duration of 10 min-
utes. The soap bath aimed to remove large organic particles, while the acetone bath
dissolved thin oil ﬁlms that may have been deposited during the fabrication and
handling processes. The IPA bath was used after acetone to remove the residues left
by the foregoing step. After the sonication procedure, the substrate was dried with a
nitrogen gun. To avoid touching the ITO surface when handling the substrate with
tweezers, it was attached to a larger glass plate by means of a double sided adhesive
tape. Next, the substrate was treated with oxygen plasma at a power of 100 W and
during 5 minutes. This was done to reduce the ITO surface energy, which in turn
led to a higher hydrophylicity and therefore a better homogeneity of the subsequent
spin coated ﬁlms [102].
5.2.2
Preparation of the solutions
All the layers in the device, except the metal contacts, were chosen to be solution
processed. A commercial solution of PEDOT:PSS in water at 1.3 % in weight was
ﬁltered with a 0.22 µm Whatman ﬁlter and kept in a ﬂask for further use. Next,
6 mg of PVK in powder were dissolved in 1 mL of chlorobenzene aided magnetic
stirring. The active layer preparation started with a commercial stock solution of
PMMA in anisole at 1% in volume. This solution was further diluted in anisole to
obtain a concentration of 0.5% in volume. This further dilution allowed to achieve
thinner PMMA layers, targeting the critical 15 nm thickness for the active layer
[103]. Subsequently, 0.25 µL of the nanocrystal solution were added to 0.2 mL of
the diluted PMMA solution. As reported in chapter 4, this concentration ensured
suﬃcient separation of the quantum dots in the polymeric matrix. Finally, the ETL
was elaborated by dissolving commercial ZnO nanopowder in deionized water. The
dissolution was aided by magnetic stirring. Prior to use, the solution was ﬁlter with
a 0.22 µm Whatman paper and sonicated for 10 minutes.
5.2.3
Spin coating
The substrate and the prepared solutions were transferred to a nitrogen glove box,
where the spin coater was located. Firstly, PEDOT:PSS was spin coated at 5000
revolutions per minute (rpm) during 60 seconds, followed by a soft bake in a hotplate
at 140◦C for 15 minutes. After, PVK was spin coated at 4000 rpm for 60 seconds
and baked at 120◦C for 10 minutes. The active layer was formed by spin coating the
==PAGE:34==
Chapter 5. Towards an electrically driven device
29
nanocrystal solution in PMMA at 5000 rpm during 60 seconds with a subsequent
baking at 110◦C for 5 minutes. Finally, the ZnO nanoparticle solution in water was
spin coated at 4000 rpm for 60 seconds and the solvent was dried by placing the
substrate in the hotplate at 140◦C for 5 minutes.
5.2.4
Metal contact deposition
After spin coating, all the layers formed homogeneous thin ﬁlms all across the sub-
strate. However, as shown in ﬁgure 5.1B, it is necessary to have electrical access
to the ITO surface. To do so, one side of the surface was scratched with a swab
stick soaked in acetone, which removed all the existing layers down to the ITO. The
device was then placed in a custom-made aluminum holder for evaporation of the
metal contacts. A mask with the contact shape was placed on top of the substrate,
ensuring that the large pad coincided with the scratched side. The holder was in-
troduced in a thermal evaporator furnace that deposited 2 nm of chromium and 20
nm of gold.

Device testing
The aim of this work was to test the device by measuring its electroluminescence,
using a similar collection setup as the one described in ﬁgure 4.1. Furthermore, it was
planned to perform second-order photon correlation measurements. However, due
to the time constrains imposed by the Covid-19 crisis, neither of these experiments
was ﬁnally carried out.

To understand the emissive properties of quantum dots and assess their viability
as single-photon sources, it is necessary to understand their carrier dynamics. Lin
et al. developed in [98] a rate equation model to compare single-photon emission
in optically driven and electrically driven CdSe/CdS quantum dots. Interestingly,
they found that electrical carrier injection prevents the formation of biexcitons,
which deteriorate the antibunching properties of the source. Here, a similar model
applied to CsPbBr3 quantum dots is introduced.

Numerical simulations
Equations 6.2 to 6.10 were numerically solved for steady state by setting all the time
derivatives equal to zero. Two diﬀerent cases were considered: optically driven,
where be = bh = 0 ̸= a and electrically driven, with a = 0 ̸= be = bh.
The
resulting steady state biexciton occupation probability for these two cases is shown
in ﬁgure 6.3.
It can be seen that the biexciton occupation probability is larger
for the optically driven case than for the electrically driven case.
As discussed
previously, the radiative biexciton decay deteriorates the antibunching properties
of the single-photon source. Larger biexciton occupation probability implies larger
g(2)(0). Therefore, an electrically driven device is expected to show better single-
photon emission than its optically driven counterpart.
The physical principle behind this interesting result can be understood as follows.
When optically driven, carriers are always generated in pairs. The only mechanism
to generate charged states is the carrier loss, with rates se and sh. Therefore, the
occupation probability when optically driven is concentrated in the three neutral
states, which are the ground state, single exciton and biexciton. To form a biexci-
ton, the quantum dot must undergo two consequent photon absorptions within the
lifetime of an exciton. Roughly, the probability of biexciton formation depends only
on the lifetime of the exciton and on the absorption rate. This situation is in stark
contrast with the electrically driven case. When electrically driven, every carrier is
injected individually. To reach the exciton state, the quantum dot must ﬁrst be in
the single electron state and receive a hole or, alternatively, be in the single hole
state and receive an electron. The same applies for the jump form the single exciton
to the biexciton: the quantum dot must go through a trion state before reaching the
biexciton state. However, because of Auger recombination, the trion lifetime is very
short. Therefore, the probability of injection of a charge carrier within the lifetime
of the trion is very small, preventing the formation of biexcitons. In other words,
the fast Auger decay of trions blocks the path from exciton to biexciton states in
electrically driven devices.
Moreover, the single exciton formation is not compromised by electrical injection.

similar in both cases. Actually, for injection rates bh = be > 2 · 108 s−1, electrical

trions. Even if they were accessible in energy, the occupation probability for further
states would be small enough to not be included.
Moreover, the model was tested for diﬀerent γ values. Details on these simula-
tions are shown in appendix C.2, where it is demonstrated that, as γ increases, the
biexciton occupation probability diminishes. This is explained as follows. Larger γ
means that the Coulomb blocking is less strong, and thus that it is easier to inject
a charge of the same sign in an already charged dot. The occupation probability
is therefore displaced towards charge unbalanced states, which makes biexciton for-
mation less likely. However, this is at expenses of decreasing the exciton occupation
probability as well. Since γ is dependent on the capacitance of the system, which
in turn is aﬀected by the relative permittivity of the dielectric matrix, the biexciton
formation probability can be modiﬁed by choosing another polymer. It shall be
noted, however, that the change in the occupation probabilities as a function of γ
is small, which ensures the robustness of the results against uncertainties in this
parameter.
Although not shown here, the validity of the simulation was checked by conﬁrm-
ing probability conservation, meaning that the sum of all the occupation probabili-
ties of the states must be equal to unity. The model is also tested in appendix C.3
for diﬀerent decay rates, obtaining a similar behaviour for the biexciton occupation
probability. This strengthens the robustness of the conclusions obtained from these
simulations. However, as a ﬁnal remark, the model does not meticulously describe
all the dynamics of the quantum dots.
As it considers occupation probabilities,
only the ensemble dynamics are reﬂected. Other eﬀects that arise from individual
quantum dots, such as blinking [88], are not patent in the results. To include these
eﬀects, a random walk model, in which an individual quantum dot would jump from
state to state with a probability proportional to the corresponding rate in table 6.1,
could be used.

Conclusion
Caesium lead halide perovskite quantum dots are disruptive optoelectronic materials
which have attracted the attention of the scientiﬁc community over recent years
due to their large carrier mobility, near-unity quantum yield and robustness to
defects, among others. This work has shown that the methods to synthesise CsPbBr3
nanocrystals are easily reproducible and highly customizable to yield quantum dots
emitting at the desired wavelength. Moreover, since these methods are based on wet
chemistry, they are easily scalable, which would allow a widespread implementation
of single-photon sources based on these novel materials. However, it is clear that
their poor stability under atmospheric conditions creates a challenge for practical
applications.
The experimental results presented in this work show that it is possible to dilute
the quantum dots in a polymeric matrix such that they are suﬃciently separated in
space to be studied individually. Upon analysis of the photoluminescence spectra,
up to three diﬀerent peaks were observed, depending on the nanocrystal orientation.
These peaks are in good agreement with the theoretical predictions that arise from
the analysis of the band structure of the material.
Despite the number of works that have been devoted to CsPbBr3, there are
still ongoing debates about critical aspects of it, such as its crystalline structure or
its exciton radiative lifetime. Hence, it is clear that further research is needed to
elucidate these matters and fully understand the optoelectronic properties of this
novel material.
The ultimate goal of the present work was to demonstrate single-photon emission
in electrically driven CsPbBr3 quantum dots. This has not been achieved due to the
time constrains imposed by the Covid-19 crisis. However, the design of a novel device
structure, which would potentially allow this demonstration, has been discussed.
Not only for research purpose, the suggested device is fabricated using solution-
processed materials, which allow an easy industrial implementation.
To render more interest on electrically driven single-photon sources, this work
presented a theoretical model of the charge carrier dynamics in quantum dots. The
results obtained suggest that, upon electrical injection, biexciton formation is less
likely to occur due to the fast Auger recombination channel of the trion states. This
may be a beneﬁt for single-photon emitters, as multiphoton emission from biexciton
states degrades the antibunching properties of the source. However, the suggested
model does not take into account important eﬀects in quantum dots such as blinking
and ﬂickering, which arise from the dynamics of individual quantum dots rather than
from ensembles.
Overall, caesium lead halide perovskite quantum dots will surely be valuable
candidates for single-photon sources over the next years, but further research is
needed to overcome the challenges that are still in sight.
