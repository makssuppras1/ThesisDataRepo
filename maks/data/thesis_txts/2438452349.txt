Abstract
Utilising carbon dioxide is an important development towards solving the current
environmental crisis. One key process could be the production of methanol from
carbon dioxide and hydrogen. This has been done commercially with the use of
catalysis since the 60’s, but the current technology requires too high pressures.
Working at milder condition would make the process cheaper and easier.
This project aims at the development of novel catalysts for the synthesis of methanol.
Going in the footsteps of previous studies these catalysts will be based on intermetallic
alloys containing highly active transition metals together with gallium. This approach
is possible through for example the Heusler alloys, which are ternary intermetallic
alloys residing in a structure very similar to the base centred cubic crystal.
These catalysts have been synthesised through incipient wetness impregnation of
high surface area fumed silica using nitrate-salts containing the metals in question,
and are subsequently reduced at high temperatures in hydrogen making the metals
alloy into nanoparticles. The initial approach has been to synthesise the known
alloys from literature Ni2FeGa and Co2FeGa, though they have not previously been
examined for catalytic activity.
Ex-situ characterisation of these nanoparticles using X-ray diﬀraction, electron mi-
croscopy with complementary spectroscopy methods show that intermetallic nanopar-
ticles have been produced by this synthesis-method. In-situ X-ray diﬀraction has
been used to link these other methods to the actual conditions of the methanol
synthesis.
Based on the mentioned synthesis-method a broader survey for catalytic activity
in the chemical region of Ni-Fe-Ga and Co-Fe-Ga has been executed using a plug-
ﬂow reactor and gas chromatography. These alloys show a wide range of diﬀerent
activities, but the selectivity towards methanol can be linked to those containing
gallium.
Especially Ni2FeGa/SiO2 and Co1.6Fe1.4Ga0.9/SiO2 has been found to
have the highest methanol yield of this study, though they do not outperform the
commercial catalyst Cu/ZnO/Al2, or the highly active Ni5Ga3 and Pd2Ga.
Further characterisation and activity-measurements of these two catalyst show
possible future optimisation in relation to the synthesis.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
As time goes by the size of the human population increases in size. Following the
past centuries we have increased our standards of living. As the future arrives, we
keep consuming more resources. This never ending growth in the human society
and its needs has changed the appearance of the world. The ﬁeld of engineering
has moved from solving problems of the every-day life, to making the every-day life
sustainable enough to solve the problems of the world. The majority of technologies
scalable to the needs of society are today still very much reliant on oil and coal dug
up from the ground, which with its consumption brings environmental problems with
it such as global warming[1].
As Broecker made clear in 1975 and what is openly obvious by now, CO2 levels
in the atmosphere are irreversibly and dangerously high and the consequences are
severe[1, 2, 3]: the temperature rises with consequent increase of the global water level
and extreme weather like droughts, ﬂoods and hurricanes will intensify. The world
countries have committed themselves to lower emission and keep the temperature-
change below 2.0 ◦C[4] above pre-industrial levels, but as Hansen et.al.[2] discuss, the
point has past where lowering the emission of green house gasses like CO2 will solve
the coming global climate change: we have to ﬁgure out how to extract the CO2
from the atmosphere by the end of this century. At the moment the technologies
of capturing CO2 from the air are quite expensive, but research and investment are
happening in this ﬁeld[5]. A more rapid solution right now would be to gather and
utilise the CO2, where we emit it.
Nobel-prize winner in chemistry George Olah has suggested a methanol economy,
where a chemical recycling of CO2 are in place to synthesise the needed fuels1 such
as methanol[6]. Such a synthesis requires hydrogen gas, which can be produced by
electrolysis. Thus will the synthesised fuel be an energy-carrier of excess electrical
energy. In that way the classical combustion-engine technology need not to be
replaced by electrical technologies, but rather have a sustainable fuel.

1.2
Heusler alloys as novel catalysts
How do you develop a novel methanol catalyst? Basically there are two ways to
go: predicting what to synthesise, which means theoretical work and simulations; or
trial-and-error, which means try something and work from there. Almost exclusively
both methods are used side by side, since the theoretical predictions almost never
hit the exact reality, but the trial-and-error can be very cumbersome and have no
guarantee of success.
This study will take its starting-point from intermetallics, alloying gallium with
other transition-metals. This is based on several previous discoveries of catalysts
having enhanced methanol activity and selectivity due to gallium: Pd2Ga/SiO2[9],
Ni5Ga3/SiO2[10] and Cu/ZnO/Ga/Al2O3[11].
A novel approach is the material class of Heusler-alloys, which on a few occasions has
shown to be catalytic enhancing over the elemental components and simpler alloys[12,
13]. They are ternary alloys of the chemical form X2YZ and form a base centred cubic

1.2.1
Aim of this project
For the reasons and motivation previously discussed, the aim of this project will be
to synthesise novel catalysts for synthesising methanol from hydrogen and carbon
dioxide. The entry catalysts will be based on Heusler alloys containing gallium.
These catalysts will be tested for their catalytic properties in a plug-ﬂow reactor, and
characterisation with use of X-rays and electron microscopy will give an understanding
into the formation and structural mechanisms of these catalysts. Based on the results
of these experiments, the catalysts will be altered so useful knowledge about the
behaviour can be achieved.
1.2.2
Choice of Materials
The entry catalysts will be the Ni2FeGa/SiO2 and the Co2FeGa/SiO2, since they
have been synthesised as nanoparticles and have been reported to be stable[14, 15,
16]. Furthermore Ni-Ga alloys has been shown to be very active towards making
methanol[10], so it is expected that alloys in the Ni-Fe-Ga phase should show some
degree of catalytic activity. Co and Fe are more commonly associated with Fischer-
Tropsch, but work has been done synthesising alcohols over alloys containing these
materials[17, 18]. The choice of fumed silica as support material is based on three
properties: it has a very high surface-area; it is amorphous thus diﬀraction patterns
will only feature broad peaks at a low diﬀraction-angle; it is chemically inert, making
the analysis of the catalytic metal-alloys easier.

2.1.2
Methanol Synthesis
Since the 1920’s the synthesis of methanol has been performed with heterogeneous
catalysis[20], among which copper and zinc-oxide as a catalyst has been proposed
early on[21]. The commercial success of methanol synthesis today is based on the
catalyst Cu/ZnO/Al2O3 derived from Cu/Zn/Cr-catalysts patented in the 60’s[22].
Most research into the micro-kinetics of methanol synthesis has evolved around
Cu-based catalysts, and are still popular as key components for novel catalysts[8].
Early on CO was used solely with hydrogen as syn-gas, but today this is done with
a combination of CO and CO2.
The reaction-scheme5 consist of hydrogenating carbonmonoxide (CO) into methanol
CO + 2H2 ⇌CH3OH,
∆H◦= −90.5 kJ · mol−1,
(2.9)
though the actual reactant forming methanol has shown to be carbondioxide[24, 25,
26] (CO2), which is formed through the water-gas-shift(WGS) reaction
CO + H2O ⇌CO2 + H2,
∆H◦= −41.2 kJ · mol−1,
(2.10)
and afterwards through reducing the CO2 forms methanol:
CO2 + 3H2 ⇌CH3OH + H2O,
∆H◦= −49.3 kJ · mol−1.
(2.11)
This was shown by isotope-labelling the carbon-species[24][26]. Liu et.al.[25] sup-
ported the idea of CO2 as reactant by showing that the initial reaction-rates over
Cu-ZnO would increase with the concentration of CO2, which on the other hand would
build up water from the synthesis and reversed-WGS deactivating the catalyst[25,
27].
All the above reactions are exothermic meaning in general that a low temperature is
required for a high yield of methanol. On the contrary a suﬃciently high temperature
is needed to increase the rates of individual elementary steps, so the overall reaction
can happen. The elementary steps of going from CO or CO2 to methanol has been
a very actively discussed subject the last two decades, where enhanced simulations
and better experiments have given complexity to the matter. Probably the simplest
model was proposed by Rasmussen et.al.[28] and is outlined in Table 2.1. This
includes two overall reactions, the WGS-reaction in step 1-8(including adsorption
of ligands), and the methanol formation from CO2 in step 9-13. Often step 10,
the hydrogenation of formate (HCOO), is assumed to be the rate-limiting step [29,
30], but as it is pointed out by Taylor et.al.[31], rather the energy-barrier along
the reaction-coordinate of an elementary step is determining the rate-limitation
compared to the depth of the energy-well of the intermediate species between steps.
Following this criteria they found that hydrogenation of dioxomethylene (H2COO) to
form methoxy (H3CO) was the rate-limiting step on Cu(100). The same conclusion
is found by Askgaard et.al.[32], who included three steps to the kinetic model for
the formation of formaldehyde (H2CO) as a biproduct.

Deactivation and secondary reactions
In heterogeneous catalysis the deactivation of a catalyst over time is a major concern.
This can happen due to a large number of reasons: sintering of the catalyst, leaching
of the active material, oxidation, phase-separation or poising of the active sites are
just a number ways the catalyst can be deactivated over time.
The ﬁrst case has been reported for Cu/ZnO/Al2O3, at elevated temperatures and a
high CO partial pressure, the high reduction potential increases the mobility of Cu
and starts sintering of the nanoparticles with the loss of surface area as a result[38].
On the other hand using a high gas feed of CO2 produce water through the methanol
synthesis and RWGS. Liu et.al.[25] found that H2O in the gas-feed will suppress
the production of methanol over the Cu/ZnO/Al2O3. Similar did Fujimoto and
Yu[27] conclude that the produced H2O from splitting CO2 oxidise the Cu, which
they repaired by introducing the noble metals Pd and Pt on the catalyst making a
spill-over eﬀect of adsorbed H-atoms towards the Cu.
Poisoning on the other hand can cause very rapid deactivation. In the methanol
synthesis from both CO and CO2 this is a major concern. In the case of a gas-feed
with CO, it poisons the surface through the Boudouard reaction:
2CO ⇌CO2 + C,

(2.12)
If the gas-feed is with CO2 instead, CO will be formed through the RWGS and
the above reaction therefore poisons the active sites. Due to the RWGS being

2.1.4
Development of novel catalysts
Cu/ZnO/Al2O3 has been used commercially as catalyst the methanol synthesis from
90:10:10 of H2:CO:CO2 since the 60’s. However it would be more ideal to synthesise
methanol directly from CO2, instead of CO/CO2. For that same reason research
is still ongoing with the goal of designing a catalyst that can synthesise methanol
from CO2 preferably in stiochiometric concentrations with H2 whilst working at so
low pressure and temperature that it is feasible. The next couple of paragraphs will
present some of the advances within novel methanol catalysts.
Cu-Zn-Ga
Toyir et.al.[42] did back in 2001 examine the behaviour of Ga promoted Cu/ZnO
on diﬀerent supports synthesising methanol from CO2 at 250 ◦C and 20-50 bar. It
performed reasonably well, but with higher level of impurities in the CO2 feed the
catalyst was reported to deactivate rapidly from initial activities. Kang et.al.[11]
have reported that promoting Cu/ZnO with Ga creates a greater dispersion of Cu,
thus increasing the active surface area and improving the deactivation by restricting
the Cu from sintering.10 More recently Cai et.al.[43] synthesised Cu/Zn-Ga both by
calcination of the Zn-Ga before impregnating Cu by incipient wetness impregnation;
and by exposing Zn-, Ga-nitratesalts with microwaves in nitrogen-gas before being
dried and impregnated with Cu. The catalysts in this study reached CO2 conversions
between 10-15% at 250-270◦, 3 MPa pressure and a stiochiometric gas-feed of CO2

methanol catalyst and the major draw-back is the cost and availability of palladium.
Ni5Ga3/SiO2
In a fairly recent study the fcc-(211) facet of Ni3Ga with mixed Ni-Ga sites has
been found to be as active towards CO2 based methanol synthesis as a Zn-doped
Cu-fcc(211) facet using DFT-calulations[10]. In the same work activity-measurements
of various Ni-Ga/SiO2 alloys synthesised by incipient wetness impregnation showed
that Ni5Ga3/SiO2 is as active as commercial Cu/ZnO/Al2O3, working in a PFR with
a stoichiometric gas-composition of CO2/H2, at ambient pressure and 160-260 ◦C.
Even though Ni5Ga3 produced less CO than its Cu-counterpart, these amounts were
much higher than the produced methanol. Furthermore the Ni5Ga3/SiO2 suﬀers from
deactivation, which has been explained by carbon-poisoning through dissociation of
CO and by phase-separation of Ni5Ga3 into Ni-Ga2O3 featuring less active sites[10,
48]. The Ni5Ga3 shows promise, but optimisation of the catalyst, both working
conditions and synthesis is needed, so the RWGS can be suppressed increasing the
stability and selectivity of this catalyst.
In2O3

2.2
Heusler Alloys
Heusler Alloys have their name after the discoverer Friedrich Heusler, who in 1903
found the alloy Cu2MnSn, a ferromagnetic alloy[55]. Since then Heusler alloys have
been made in a wide variety and contributed to the fundamental understanding of
magnetism[56], as well as showing promise in applications such as spintronics and
solar-cells[57].
The designation ”Heusler” is ambiguous, since it can refer both to the chemical
formula or to the crystalline structure of materials with ferromagnetic properties.
Some Heusler alloys have one but lack the other. In general Heusler alloys have the
chemical formula X2YZ, where the valence of X≥Y.11 These compounds often reside
in a L21 lattice12 which is exempliﬁed in Figure 2.3(a), but can also be in a similar
but tetragonal cell. The L21 lattice consists of four fcc-lattices with one compound
occupying each FCC sub-lattice, though the symmetry of the crystal resembles the
symmetry of simple bcc much more.

The Heuslers are alloyed between transition metals as their X and Y components,
and post-transition elements as their Z component[58]. They have bulk magnetic
structures: they exhibit Slater-Pauling behaviour and are half-metallic[55]; meaning
that the magnetic moment of the unit-cell in Bohr magnetons µB is simply the diﬀer-
ence in the occupancy of spin-up and -down states, and they are metallic/insulating
for diﬀerent spin-orientations. It is these properties that have given the Heusler
alloys their scientiﬁc interest in the past century.
The full Heuslers are all strong ferromagnets and features Curie temperatures as
high as 1100 K. Furthermore the Curie temperature for full Heuslers with Co as the
X-component have linearly increasing Curie temperature with the magnetic moment
per atom for Heuslers[55][59].
The examination of the surfaces of these Heusler alloys have mostly been theoretical
and towards the use for magnetoelectronics, which means thin ﬁlms. Most of the
research leans towards the half-metallic properties disappearing at the surface of the
alloys due to surface-states forming with lower energy for the minority compared to
majority spin[60, 61, 62]. On the other hand, the spin polarisation of the surfaces
depend very much on which layer is the terminating one: X- or YZ-components.
Theoretical work by Galanakis et.al.[60] found that the Heusler of Co2CrAl would
have one highly spin-polarised surface in the (001)-direction if terminated by CrAl

2.2.2
Heusler alloys in catalysis
In section 1.2 it has been motivated in non-technical terms why this project has
chosen Heusler alloys as its starting point for making novel catalysts. In this section
it will be reviewed, what exactly could be interesting for catalysis.
As it is for any intermetallic the Heusler alloys features many facets of mixed
metal-sites, which could seriously enhance the catalytic activity of those surfaces.
In the discovery of the Ni5Ga3 catalyst, it was originally proposed by theoretical
determinations of the TOF that Ni3Ga would have the most active mixed Ni-Ga
sites towards synthesising methanol from CO2[10]. Though this was not the case
experimentally, it was suggested that the Ni became poisoned by CO and Ni3Ga
phase-separated afterwards. Another study found, when testing Sn, Mo and Cu as
promoters for styrene oxidation over FeCo, that Sn worked as the best promoter
forming what has been recognised as the Heusler Fe2CoSn[13]. Lastly the previously
mentioned study by Kojima et.al.[12] found that alloying Co with Fe and Ge into
Co2FeGe showed an improvement in the catalytic activity for hydrogenating propyne
into propene and propane over elemental Co even though Fe and Ge is inactive.
Whether this is due to Fe or Ge working as promoters or simply a geometrical
enhancement of the Co-sites was not answered. Thus if the promoters of catalysts
are alloyed directly into the catalyst to form an intermetallic, then the quantity of
active mixed sites becomes much larger compared to catalysts and promoters forming
individual neighbouring phases making active sites at the phase-boundary. Since
this is a key-property found for catalytic active intermetallics, it makes the Heusler
alloys all the more interesting for catalysis.
In the case where mixed metal-sites of two metals are very active but not stable
when alloyed, adding a component can allow the formation, which of course could
be a Heusler alloy. Or at least the Heusler alloy can be an obvious starting point,
when searching for ternary alloys, since they have a great variety of stable alloys.
Thus one might say that the Heusler alloys are a balanced choice for a system that
both oﬀers open possibilities, but at the same time have systematic characteristics
making the starting-point of designing a novel catalysts comprehensible.

the surface for a number of the Heuslers, as seen in Figure 2.4, do inﬂuence the

3.1.1
Gas Chromatography
A Gas Chromatograph is a tool for separating diﬀerent gases. It consist of a sample-
volume containing the gas, which are ﬂushed through capillary columns2 using an
inert gas and the output is then detected. As the gas ﬂows through the column,
molecules will continuously adsorb and desorb on the column-walls. This behaviour
is dependant on multiple factors like mass, polarity and spacial extend of the diﬀerent
molecules making the ﬂow-resistance through the column diﬀerent and the gases will
therefore be separated on the basis of their retention time. In the current setup two
detectors were used: a Flame Ionization Detector (FID) and a Thermal Conductivity

Detector (TCD).
In the FID the sample-gas is lead through a hydrogen-ﬂame, which depending on
the amount of sample-gas will produce ions which are collected on an electrode.
The current will be proportional to the amount of sample-molecules containing
hydrocarbons[87]. This makes the FID blind towards common gases like CO, CO2,

In the TCD the sample-gas is lead through a column containing a heated resistor
and depending on the thermal conductivity the ohmic resistance will change. Using
a Wheatstone bridge with a reference gas being Helium on one of the other resistors,
the tension will change dependant on the thermal conductivity of the gas compared
to He.
What comes out is a spectrum from each of the detectors, with peaks for every
single compound contained in the initial gas-sample. The recognition of the diﬀerent
gas-peaks at diﬀerent retention times are two-fold. CO2, CO, CH4, H2 and Ar has

noted retention time[41]. When the peaks have been recognised they are integrated
and corrected by the sensitivity factor of each gas towards the detectors. These
factors have been calibrated towards the gasses that was expected to be seen in the
spectrum. These calibration experiments are reported in appendix C. The areas
of the diﬀerent peaks can either be ﬁtted3[88] or summed numerically. The latter
was done in this project using Python 3.7. This choice originates from the fact that
the numerical stability of the analytical function is very poor and that numerical
integration algorithms tend to be simpler and easier editable towards other problems,
making the programmed code more useful for others. The actual integration and
discussion of the method is more thoroughly explained in appendix D.

Quadrupole mass spectroscopy
A quadrupole mass-spec is an instrument for detecting masses, by ionisation, ﬁltering
and current-measuring[90]. On the In-situ XRD gas is leaked into a quadrupole
mass-spec from the outgoing-gas before it is vented out in air, which means it does

not analyse all gas passing through. The sample-gas captured by the sniﬀer is ionised
by exposure to electrons of energy 70-100 eV coming from a heated tungsten ﬁlament.
The positively ionised molecules are lightly focused by electrostatic lenses before
being led into the quadrupole rods. The quadrupole rods are a pair of rods with a
constant bias added with a oscillating bias, one pair with a positive constant and one
pair with a negative constant. The positive rods will eliminate masses that are to
light due to low inertia throwing them away from the oscillations. The negative rods
will oppositely eliminate heavy masses, since there inertia is to large to be ﬁxated
by the oscilations. The result is that only a speciﬁc mass is allowed through the
quadrupole rods, and the current is measured on the backside by a Faraday-cup or
similar.
Due to the energy deposited in the molecules through the ionisation process, they can
undergo fragmentatation meaning that for example O2 will appear as both masses
16 u and 32 u referred to as the electron ionisation mass spectrum. The larger
the molecules the more complex is their mass spectrum. Reference spectra can be
found at NIST, but they are only qualitative since the degree of fragmentation will
depend on the energy of electrons coming from the ﬁlament[91, 90]. Isotopes will
also contribute to the mass spectrum.
The data-treatment of the acquired data with the quadrupole mass-spec has been
qualitatively only, since quantitative data can only be achieved by calibration of
the gasses needed to be analysed. The calibration will depend on the change of
sniﬃng diﬀerent molecules and their degree of ionisation and loss through the focus
of ionised molecules toward the quadrupole rods.

3.5
Transmission electron microscopy
Transmission electron microscopy is in many ways similar to optical transmission
microscopy, but the extent of the electronic wave allows for surpassing any resolution
limit set by Rayleigh criterion[92]. The beam is transmitted through the sample
and the image is formed on the other side by an optical system. This means that
both bright-ﬁeld and diﬀraction images can be directly acquired by focusing the
corresponding plane down on a CCD-camera. By using apertures in the diﬀraction-
plane dark-ﬁeld images can be acquired as well. Furthermore the electron-beam can
also undergo inelastic scattering processes making spectroscopy by electron energy
loss spectroscopy possible. The inelastic scattering give rise to relaxation events
of the sample generating unique X-ray signal, analysed by energy dispersive X-ray
spectroscopy(EDX). If the beam is focused scanning TEM (STEM) can be done as
well.
Two diﬀerent microscopes have been used: Tecnai T20 G2(Tecnai) with a LaB6-
ﬁlament and Titan E-Cell 80-300ST TEM (E-TEM) with a Field emission gun(FEG),
both from FEI.8 The Tecnai has been operated at 200 keV and was used to execute
ex-situ bright-ﬁeld(BF) imaging, high resolution(HR) with resolution < 2 ˚A, and
STEM with EDX spectroscopy . The E-TEM has been operated at 300 keV and was
used for HRTEM with resolution < 0.8 ˚A, STEM-EDX/EELS and In-situ reduction
of the nanoparticles before doing HR-TEM.
For the ex-situ examination the powder-samples are ﬁtted on 400 mesh gold grids
with lacey-carbon ﬁlm, LC400-Au supplied by Electron Microscopy Science. The
procedure for deposing the samples is to take the grid by an inverted tweezer, and
shovel through the silica-powder and gently shake it oﬀagain, which afterwards will
leave microscopic silica-particles on the grid suitable for doing TEM.

supplied by DENS solutions, allowing heating upto 1300 ◦C. The procedure is to

3.5.1
Bright ﬁeld imaging
Bright ﬁeld images are the real representation of the sample, and the contrast in
this image comes mainly from diﬀraction of the electron-beam meaning that larger
thickness or core-charge will appear darker (signal has disappeared by diﬀraction).
When the real-plane is in focus, diﬀracted beams will be focused at the point they
were diﬀracted from, giving low contrast therefor. By going slightly out of focus the
contrast is enhanced. The contrast can further be enhanced by using an aperture in
the diﬀraction plane allowing only the 0’th order peak to reach the CCD.
These acquired images are used in this study to estimate the size-distribution of the
nanoparticles, by drawing up the nanoparticles by hand and both measuring the area
and ﬁtting a rotated ellipse to gain the minor and major axes of the nanoparticle.
This is done with the Fiji distribution of ImageJ[93, 94].

In high resolution TEM, the image is magniﬁed to the extent where contrast due
to phase-change of the electron wave will appear. The modulation of the contrast

and afterwards the out-going wave is distorted through the optical system. The
derivation can be read in [92], but it will be outlined in brief terms: the incoming
plane-wave propagates through the sample summing up phase while it simultaneously
interferes with itself. This outgoing wave will have an amplitude and a phase in
each point deﬁned by the complex function Ψ(x, y), if the propagation is chosen in z.
The actual object Ψ(x, y) can be solved by scattering theory in quantum mechanics,
which will not be dealt with further in this work. Each point in Ψ(x, y) is transferred
through the optical system to become a disk in the image-plane described by the
function g(x, y) by

3.5.3
Scanning transmission electron microscopy
In STEM the beam is converged into a probe of nm or ˚A size. The Tecnai utilise a
probe in the range of 1-2 nm and the ETEM a probe of about 5 ˚A. This probe is
then scanned across the sample in a raster-pattern, and a detector in the high-angle11

generate more signal in the pixel of the image when scanned. The contrast at these

images are usually easier than HRTEM images. Furthermore since the main part of
the beam is untouched EELS can be executed while scanning the image.
3.5.4
Electron energy loss spectroscopy
EELS is the analysis of loss mechanism carried by the beam: forward scattered
electrons. The beam is led through a magnetic prism which will focus the electrons
in velocity/energy on a dispersion plane. Gathering this signal with a CCD will give
an image of the spectrum. The EELS spectrum will feature inelastic transitions
given as energy-losses from zero energy: a large and narrow12 zero-loss peak, and
going to positive energies peaks or edges will appear for every allowed transition
of the electrons: plasmons, ionisation energies of the atoms, excitons and phonons.
Ionisation of the atoms give sharp edges, which will be followed by a new energy-loss
structure of plasmons etc., whose analysis is referred to as energy loss near edge
spectrum(ELNES). The background of high energy EELS (¿50 eV) is usually ﬁtted
by an inverse power-law[92].

3.5.5
Energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy
EDX spectroscopy is the analysis of emitted X-rays when excited electrons in an atom
relaxes. The spectrum will contain a lot of X-ray peaks on top of a ”whale”-shaped
background, the Bremsstrahlung. These peaks are the transitions of electrons relaxing
into lower energy-states, which is unique for every single element. The nomenclature
for EDX transitions are by the state the relax to: K denotes transitions towards the K
state, Kα is from the L-state, Kβ is from the M-state. If there is a visible spin-orbit
splitting, the they are denoted with a subscribt 1 for the largest cross-section and 2
for the smallest[92, 95].
By ﬁtting a gaussian to the EDX-peaks the spectrum can be quantiﬁed.
The
acquisition and data-treatment of the EDX-spectra are done in the Aztec-software
supplied from Oxford Instruments.
Since X-rays are generated and emitted throughout the beam-interaction volume of
the sample, these emitted X-rays will have the possibility of excite atoms which can
emit new X-rays. Therefore this spectroscopy method is strictly speaking not local,
and TEM-EDX-spectra do usually have distinct lines of the sample-grid.
3.5.6
Beam eﬀects
Beam-eﬀects are usually a great concern when doing electron-microscopy, since
the immense energy deposited by the beam will alter the sample making analysis
diﬃcult. Beam-eﬀects have been observed in this study through the melting and
recrystallisation of silica. This has been exempliﬁed in appendix E.
3.6
Scanning electron microscopy
The diﬀerence in SEM and TEM is that in SEM the signal is collected from the
surface and the energies of the beam is for the same reason much lower, between
0.5-30 keV. All optics preceding the interaction of the sample are in principle identical
to the ones found in a TEM doing STEM. A probe is converged on top of the surface
36
==PAGE:49==
CHAPTER 3. EXPERIMENTAL METHODS
of a sample, and scanned in a raster-pattern over the sample. Electrons interacting
with the sample will either scatter elastically (high energy electrons) or scatter
inelastically (low energy electrons)[95]. The high energy backscattered electrons
(BSE) are collected in a PN-junction round the pole-piece generating excitons when
hit by an electron. The amount of excitons will be proportional to the electron-energy,
and this detector is thus a dedicated BSE detector. The backscattering cross-section
will be very dependant on the charge of the atom and the contrast will therefore
show charge (weight) of the sample. Low energy electrons (secondary electrons, SE)
are gathered by an Everhart-Thronley detector (ETD) which with a positively biased
Faraday-cage will attract electrons toward the detector. The amount of SE coming
from the surface will be very dependant on edge-eﬀects thus the image will show
topographic contrast. The electron interaction-volume beneath the sample-surface
will be limiting for the resolution of the image together with the probe-diameter. At
30 keV and in silicon this interaction-volume is several microns deep.
Since the samples in question of this study are mainly silica and non-conductive,
low-vacuum SEM has been used to execute the SEM-EDX. The basic principles are
the same, but with water-vapour feeded into the sample chamber at pressures 10-300
Pa. Due to the pressure the normal ETD will not work. Instead the detector is
comprised of a large ﬁeld-plate situated over the sample and connected externally
to the back-side of the sample-stage with a bias making a capacitor between the
sample-surface and the detector. As SE are released from the surface they will ionise
the water-vapour and due to the bias from the surface to the detector, there will
be generated a current in the external circuit proportional to the amount of SE,
which when monitored gives the contrast of the image. The detector is referred to as
Large-ﬁeld detector (LFD) or Gaseous secondary electron detector (GSED).
The microscope used for SEM-EDX is a Quanta FEG 200 ESEM supplied by FEI,
running at 20 keV and 100 Pa pressure.
3.6.1
Energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy
EDX executed with a SEM is very similar to EDX in STEM. The physical inter-
action is completely the same as previously discussed, but due to the size of the
interaction-volume within the sample-surface beneath the probe, the resolution of
SEM-EDX is terrible compared to STEM-EDX. The quantiﬁcation is also more
complex because absorption of X-rays emitted deep within the sample will show in
the ﬁnal spectrum, which will be dependant on the material and energy of the X-rays.
These absorption-corrections are embedded within the used software Aztec supplied
by Oxford Instruments. To give an example of the error induced by absorption,
quantiﬁed EDX-spectra using both methods has been produced and can be viewed
in appendix F.
Since the SEM-EDX was executed in low-vacuum, oxygen could not be quantiﬁed
in the acquired EDX-spectra. Instead it has been assumed that the atomic ratio of
Si/O is 0.5, so the loading of the catalysts could be evaluated based on SEM-EDX.

Procedure
Several diﬀerent alloys have been synthesised during this project. Besides the weight
of diﬀerent precursor-salts the synthesis is identical. The generic overall procedure is
based on the one used by Sharafutdinov et.al.[48] and is as follows:
1. A precursor-solution of millipore-water and nitrate-salts with components for
the alloys are made, so formed alloy-concentration is 1 M. Nitrate-salts used
and CAS-numbers are found in table 4.1.
2. A diluted solution is made from the precursor, so 2.2 mL will contain 0.2 g
reduced nanoparticles. This will depend on the molar weight of the speciﬁc
alloy as well as the amount of nitrate and water in the nitrate-salts, Table 4.1.
3. 1 g of fumed silica (SiO2, high surface area catalyst support supplied by Alfa
Aesar) support material,1 particlesize of 105-205 µm, is impregnated with
solution giving a catalyst-loading of 16.7%.
4. The impregnated support-material is dried for 2 hours at 80 ◦C in atmosphere.

diameter of 9 mm with Quartz-wool (99% SiO2 supplied by Merck) to keep
it in place making a ﬂow-bed, Figure 4.1. The length of the bed containing
catalyst is 26 mm long.
6. The glass-tube containing the ﬂow-bed is placed in the reactor, Figure 3.1, and
reduced in a gas-ﬂow of 100 ml/min at 800 ◦C for 5 hours(heating and cooling
not included). The gas contains 90/10 of H2/Ar.2

Chapter 6
Conclusion
The purpose of this project has been to synthesise novel catalysts for the synthesis of
methanol from carbon dioxide and hydrogen, based on the idea that gallium is a key
component able to create methanol selective catalysts when residing in intermetallic
transition metal alloys.
This has been conceived by alloying nickel-iron and cobalt-iron together with gallium
to form the so called Heusler alloys Ni2FeGa and Co2FeGa, by using incipient
wetness impregnation of the corresponding dissolved metal-nitrate salts into high
surface-area fumed silica, which afterwards has been dried and reduced in hydrogen
at temperatures around 800 ◦C creating nanoparticles inside the silica.
The actual synthesis has been evaluated by X-ray diﬀraction measurements and
both scanning and transmission electron microscopy with EDX and EELS. It was
shown that the chemical composition of the synthesised nanoparticles resembled
that which was the aim for almost all the synthesised samples. TEM-images showed
that nanoparticles were present in the fumed silica, and size-distributions of these
nanoparticles were calculated based on these images. The XRD and high resolution
TEM images agreed with the characteristics of the Heusler crystal structure, but
have not been proved since many other transition metals in similar crystal structures
explain the same data. EELS and EDX with STEM showed that all metals could be
found within the same nanoparticles which substantiate that the crystal structure
contains all elements, and can thus be a Heusler structure. For Ni2FeGa it was
found that two diﬀerent phases was formed, where one is explained by the Heusler
structure and the other by a tetragonal Heusler. In-situ studies of Co2FeGa and
Ni2FeGa showed the formation of the same phases as was measured ex-situ. Finally
based on these results it is concluded that the synthesis process works in forming
alloyed nanoparticles, and it is assumed that these are in the Heusler structure or
the tetragonal Heusler, but this has yet to be proven.
Based on the incipient wetness impregnation synthesis, a small survey study of the
phase-spaces of Ni-Fe-Ga and Co-Fe-Ga has been executed evaluating the catalytic
activity of synthesising methanol. The following samples were tested: within Ni-
Fe-Ga, Ni2FeGa, Ni1.7FeGa1.3, NiFeGa1.9 and NiFe; within Co-Fe-Ga, Co2.1FeGa0.9,
Co1.6Fe1.4Ga and Co1.3Fe0.7. The most active catalyst was found in the following

Ni2FeGa > Co1.6Fe1.4Ga & Ni1.7FeGa1.3 > NiFeGa1.9 >
Co2.1FeGa0.9 > NiFe & Co1.3Fe0.7,

and the ability to form an active surface featuring mixed metals sites of gallium and

studied in [10, 48, 41] based on spacetime yield, and Ni2FeGa/SiO2 reaches about
58% of the activity of Cu/ZnO/Al2O3 and Ni5Ga3/SiO2, and 32% based on the TOF,
though the GHSV is one third higher than in the compared study.
Two deactivation processes has been found for all these catalysts: larger production of
carbon monoxide and water at elevated temperatures limiting the yield of methanol;
and deposition of coal through splitting of the carbon monoxide, which is also
increased at higher temperatures. The long term stability of the catalysts has not
been tested.
For the Ni2FeGa/SiO2 it has been found through in-situ studies with XRD that it
undergoes a phase-change at temperatures above 260 ◦C, which has been correlated
to activity and speculated that it could be a contributing factor for the deactivation.
This explanation has not been proved.
For the Co1.6Fe1.4Ga/SiO2 repeating the methanol synthesis experiments at high
temperatures made the catalyst more active on subsequent experiments, which is
ascribed to reformation of the surface of the nanoparticles rendering more active
sites. This was based on the amount of coal coming of the catalyst when reducing it
in hydrogen between experiments.
Thus has the project successfully synthesised, activity tested and characterised novel
methanol catalysts based on intermetallic alloys containing gallium.

Chapter 7
Outlook
The results of this project suggest that further development of these catalysts can
be done. Accomplishing this goal calls for more suitable characterisation methods.
Towards evaluating the synthesis synchrotron methods like extended X-ray absorption
ﬁne structure (EXAFS) and XRD with shorter wave-lengths would convincingly
tell, how the catalyst has formed. Inductively coupled plasma mass spectroscopy
(ICP-MS) would also be a much better tool for measuring the chemical composition.
Furthermore characterisation of the surface is of great importance if the cause of
catalytic activity should be found or enhanced, for example with X-ray photoelec-
tron spectroscopy (XPS), ion scattering spectroscopy (ISS) or secondary ion mass
spectroscopy (SIMS).
Development of catalysts by trial and error is like shooting from the hip: you are
gonna miss a lot more than you hit. This project had notions of this work process,
but with an idea that gallium in a Heusler alloy would show catalytic activity towards
synthesising methanol. This idea is of course not completely without merit taken
that Ni-Ga has been studied for the last couple of years. But as is the case of Ni-Ga,
future development should be based on a preceding computational study. Towards
that goal, it is suggested by the results of this project that the Heusler alloys are
examined theoretically for their ability in catalysis, since these alloys feature a stable
structure to which three elements can reside as an intermetallic. Also towards other
reactions that the synthesis of methanol.

In X-ray Diﬀraction experiments (XRD), the sample is beamed with monochromatic
X-rays. In all cases the samples were silica-supported nm-sized nanoparticles, which
makes the experiment a powder-diﬀraction experiment. This is an important char-

sequential scattering events occur due to the small phases, and secondly because all
the orientations of the crystal are equally present and diﬀraction peaks therefore
become rings, which makes the scanning of the spectrum one-dimensional.

sense. Since the incoming X-rays are electromagnetic waves with wavelength in

In the actual code, the integration is done from predeﬁned limits, chosen by the

end of the integration-limits, which is also deﬁned by the operator. In this work the
background is ﬁtted to 3 seconds at each limit, so a total of 6 seconds. This short a
time only works when the background is very stable. Examples of ﬁtted backgrounds
are shown in Figure D.2(b) for methane (orange), ethane (red) and an impurity of
CO2 (green).2
This kind of background has no physical merit or modelling to it, though it has been
chosen do to a number of very important properties: The integration is very simple
and robust if the integration-limits are chosen well; it will allow for integrating any
peak no matter how small an intensity, given that the background is very constant,
which have been the case. Since it is only a constructed function, it has an obvious
pit-fall, since intensity is actively deducted due to the step, being in the middle of the
peak, though this error is very small, if the peaks are properly isolated. For example
the error of ethane (red) in Figure D.2(b). The integrated intensity of this peak is
420 pAs. The step is 0.28 pA and the length after the step is 21 seconds, so possible
maximum error is 21 × 0.28 ∼6 pAs, which corresponds to a relative error of ∼1.5
%. This error decrease relatively with increasing gas-concentration. With the used
calibration 6 pAs in this exact spectrum correspond to circa 7 ppm of ethane.

D.2
Discussion of peak ﬁtting
In principle the propagation of gasses through a gas chromatography column is
controlled by the chance of sticking to the column surface. This can be thought of
like a series of independent interactions for the molecule down the column, where it
either sticks or passes: binomial distribution. For the same reason the peak should
be a Gaussian distribution. Referring to the ﬁgures of the previous section, it is
evident that this is not the case at all. The peaks in a spectrum usually have ”tails”
to some degree, making them asymmetrical. This has four basic causes[86]3: the
injection-proﬁle, overloading of the column, an inhomogeneous surface or unevenly
active adsorbtionsites of the column and uneven packing of the column. Therefore a
perfect Gaussian curve is not ﬁtting the curve.
A proposed proﬁle instead is the exponentially modiﬁed Gaussian[88], which in a
simple form can be written as
