Abstract
The structure of human sleep is highly dependant on age. This project focuses on the
opposite relationship, to see how well age can be predicted from sleep. Recent studies
have made progress in this ﬁeld, and have found correlations between various disorders
and deviance of estimated age from chronological age. However, all of these studies have
only worked with adults.
The brain goes through a lot of changes from Childhood to adolescense, yet research on
the changes in sleep electroencephalograﬁes in children are sparse. The primary purpose
of this study is to design a classiﬁcation model that estimates age from sleep electroen-
cephalograﬁes in children as a proof of concept. The Childhood Adenotonsillectomy Trial,
which contains sleep electroencephalograﬁes on children from age 5 to 10 years, database
is used to develop and test the model.
The model implemented was a binary classiﬁer based on ResNet. Many hyperparameters
were tested to ﬁnd the optimal model for the task. The model performed with an accuracy
of 82.5 ± 0.8 %. As a proof of concept, the study was successful. It has been shown that
a neural network can learn from sleep electroencephalograﬁes in children to predict their
ages.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Sleep has long been one of the greatest mysteries of life. Because of this it is one of the
most researched topics of human biology, especially in relation to brain activity. Thus,
the changes in sleep Electroencephalography (EEG) with age are well-documented and
can be predicted with great accuracy[1, 2, 3].
This has led to the idea of predicting
age using the information from EEG during sleep. In recent times, a few studies have
investigated the predictive power of EEG as an age estimator[4, 5, 6]. Furthermore, these
studies show that there is a correlation between deviation of the predicted age from the
chronological age and various psychological and neurological disorders. Hence, research in
brain age may ultimately lead to the discovery of biomarkers, that can aid in research and
treatment of psychological and neurological disorders as well as provide early intervention

2.1
Polysomnography
PSG is the golden standard procedure in various studies of sleep-related disorders. It is
a multi-parameter examination of human biological signals during sleep at a specialized
sleep center. The signals extracted during the PSG include electroencephalogram deriva-
tions, electrooculogram derivations, electromyogram derivations, airﬂow signals, respira-
tory eﬀort signals, oxygen saturation, body position and electrocardiogram according to
the American Academy of Sleep Medicine (AASM) guidelines [8]. Only the electroen-
cephalographies are used in this project.
The recorded signal is segmented into epochs for every 30 seconds. Each 30 second epoch
is labeled with one of the ﬁve sleep stages, wake, Rapid Eye Movements (REM), N1, N2
or N3. Sleep scoring is carried out manually by a sleep expert based on the contents of
the biological signals from the PSG.

Electroencephalography
EEG is a procedure in which brain activity is measured. For this purpose, electrodes are
positioned in speciﬁc locations on the scalp of the subject. The most common electrode
conﬁguration is the 10-20 conﬁguration as seen in Figure 2.1.
Figure 2.1: Illustrating the international 10-20 electroencephalography conﬁguration
A) perspective from the saggital plane B) perspective from the transverse plane. The
electrodes are always spaced with 10% or 20% of the total length from front to back of
the skull.[9]
The electrodes each record the weighted sum of the voltages of every neuron in the
brain. This sum is weighted because the contribution from a neuron to the total voltage
registered by an electrode, decreases with the square of the distance to the electrode.
Hence, each electrode mainly represents the neurons in its proximity.
The registered
voltage depends on the synchronicity and activity of the nearby neuron. If the neurons
are ﬁring asynchronously, the voltages even out, and the measured voltage amplitude
decreases.[10]
The recorded voltages are fed into an ampliﬁer, then through analog ﬁlters, and ﬁnally,
through an analog to digital conversion, it is stored on a digital storage device. Here it

2.3
The aging of the child brain
The brain goes through a lot of changes from childhood to adolescence. The activity of
diﬀerent brain regions vary a lot as the brain develops.
It has long been known that there are substantial changes in brain metabolism of
children. Chugani et al. found a signiﬁcant rise of brain metabolism at the age of 4-5
years. Furthermore, the brain metabolism remains high until the age of 10 years, when
it start decreasing until it is halved in adulthood[11].
Gogtay et al. observed early maturation of the gray matter in the frontal pole (cor-
responding to the electrodes Fp1 and Fp2), the primary motor/semsory cortex (corre-
sponding to C3, Cz and C4) and the occipital pole (corresponding to electrodes O1 and
O2). These areas are all almost fully matured at the age of 5 years.
Maturation of gray matter in the frontal lobes slowly begins in the primary motor cortex
(corresponding to electrodes C3, Cz and C4) and spreads anteriorly (corresponding to
electrodes F3, Fz and F4) from the age of 5 to 10 years. Gray matter in the frontal lobes
continues its development until it is fully matured around the age of 20 years.
In the parietal lobe the gray matter matures from the primary sensory area spreading
posteriorly (corresponding to electrodes P3, Pz and P4) from the age of 5 to 10 years,
and then laterally (corresponding to electrodes T3, T4, T5 and T6) from the age of 10
to 20 years. Hence, gray matter in the lateral temporal and lateral frontal lobes matures
last[12].

2.4.1
Micro- and macrostructures in sleep EEG children
Sleep patterns are usually divided into two groups when brain activity is analysed.
Macrostructures, such as sleep stages (wake, REM, N1, N2 and N3), and microstruc-
tures, such as rhtythms of a speciﬁc frequency or waves with certain characteristics.
==PAGE:26==
8
CHAPTER 2. CLINICAL BACKGROUND
Macrostructures refer to the temporal organization of sleep. The change of the
distribution and latency of sleep stages is a good descriptor of the aging of the brain. The
brain has diﬀerent needs as it ages, and this is reﬂected in the macrostructures of sleep.
The relative amount of N2 sleep increases through childhood and the amount of REM
sleep also changes signiﬁcantly, although not a lot. However, the changes in REM latency
are more pronounced, as this is negatively correlated with age, as is the amount Slow
Wave Sleep (SWS). SWS declines with approximately 7%, while N2 sleep increases with
the same amount per 5 years from the ages 5 to 15 years. Additionally, total sleep time
is negatively correlated with aging in children. However, total sleep time is profoundly
aﬀected by the sleep environment.[1].
Microstructures, on the other hand, occur during well-deﬁned sleep stages and tend
to describe frequency content or events such as sleep-spindles, k-complexes and arousals.
As the brain changes with age, so does the content of the recorded sleep EEG. Some wave
types appear in speciﬁc age-groups, and others change their characteristics.
At the age of around 3 to 4 years, the α rhythm is visibly developed. The frequency of
these rhythms increases from 8 Hz to 9 Hz when the child is 8 to 9 years old, and by
the age of 15 years, it reaches 10 Hz. When the α rhythm reaches 9 Hz, slow waves in
the occipital region may appear either superimposed or intermixed with the α rhythm.
Typically these slow waves are present between 6 to 12 years of age.
Cone waves are a type of wave usually appearing from the 6 months to 5 years of age. It
is a δ rhythm characterized by its positive, monophasic, high amplitude, and is shaped
like a cone. Cone waves are dominant in the occipital region and can usually be seen
during the N2 and N3 sleep stages[3].

Machine Learning
The concept of machine learning refers to computer models that can perform a given task
without being explicitly coded to perform this task. Instead, the model has been fed
suﬃcient data to change its structure in a way, that it can perform the task at hand.
Generally, machine learning models can be divided into two groups based on the way the
model learns, supervised and unsupervised learning. In unsupervised learning,
the machine learning model is trained on unlabeled data. An example of this is clustering,
where the model looks for similarities in diﬀerent inputs and groups them. In this project,
only supervised learning is used. Hence, a more detailed explanation of unsupervised
learning is not provided.
In supervised learning on the other hand, the training data fed to the model is labeled
with the solution to the problem the model is trying to solve. For every training iteration,
the model slowly adjusts itself to better solve a similar problem in the future. Typically,
two diﬀerent kinds of problems are solved by machine learning models; regression problems
and classiﬁcation problems. The approach of solving these problems mainly vary in the
way the model evaluates its error.

3.3.5
Batch normalization
Batch normalization aims to reduce covariate shift in the neural network by using normal-
ization to center the output around zero and scale it to have a standard deviation of 1.[16]
Covariate shift happens in deep neural networks during the training of the model. As
the parameters of a given layer are changed, the distribution of the next layer is aﬀected.
This considerably slows down the training speed, as lower learning rates are required.
By applying batch normalization throughout a deep neural network, higher learning
speeds, less likelihood of saturating non-linearities can be achieved. In some cases, batch
normalization can eliminate the need for using dropout.
3.4
Convolutional neural networks
A convolutional neural network is characterized by containing so-called convolutional
layers. This kind of network was initially designed for image classiﬁcation, but have since
then become very important in the performance of many states of the art deep learning
models. Due to its origin, the input of a convolutional neural network usually is 2-D.
However, it is possible to transfer the same concepts into 1 dimension.

3.4.2
Max pooling
A common variant of the convolutional layer is a max pooling layer, which is characterized
by returning the max value inside the kernels focus instead of doing the dot product as
the kernel slides across the input volume. Likewise, average, median, and other statistics
can be used. Usually, the stride is set to be equal to the kernel size, so that the kernels do
not overlap. The purpose of the pooling operations is to reduce the size of the matrices
being processed in the successive layers, thus reducing computational costs. Also, pooling
results in the network becoming less sensitive to small shifts in the input.

6.3
Segmentation
For the model to be trained on multiple samples from each subject, each PSG session
was split into smaller segments. A moving window with predetermined, constant epoch
length was used to extract each segment and store it in a matrix. If the step size of the
moving window was smaller than the epoch length, the segments will overlap.
In this project, the standard 30 second epoch length was used with no overlap. Addi-
tionally, an epoch length of 120 seconds with an overlap of 30 seconds was tested. These
parameters were chosen to decrease the loss of information on the edge of the signals as
well as give the model a broader view by feeding more uninterrupted data at the same
time.
Multiple channels:
When using multiple channels as input, the model takes advantage of the properties of the
convolution layer at the input. The segments from each channel at the same time recording
time were stacked on top of each other resulting in a 2-dimensional input vector.
If a segment was removed from one channel due to artifacts, the segments from the
remaining channels were also removed.

6.4
Artifact removal
Each segment was examined to see if it should be discarded as an artifact. If the ampli-
tude was either abnormally high or low, the segment was discarded as an artifact. The
amplitude of an epoch was regarded abnormally high if it had a standard deviation at
least 10 times greater than the standard deviation of the entire signal. Likewise, the
amplitude of the signal was regarded as abnormally low if it had a standard deviation of
3 times less than the standard deviation of the entire signal.
As the CHAT dataset does not come with indications of lights out and lights on, all
leading and trailing wake epochs have been discarded for all experiments. These epochs
also contain much noise from movement artifacts, which also are eliminated by removal
of these epochs.
6.5
Normalization
In the hope of reducing inter-variability between PSG sessions, normalization was used
on the signal. Normalization has the purpose of implementing a standard baseline and
range of amplitudes, which do not vary in between session[14].

7.1
Neural network architecture
The model used in this project was based on Residual Neural Network (ResNet) as this is
one of the top performing neural network architectures. Furthermore, it retains its high
performance while remaining less computationally demanding as well as having lower
memory requirements than the competing neural network structures [22, 23]. The sim-
plicity of implementing and modifying ResNet also played a noteworthy role in the ﬁnal
decision to use this archiecture.
ResNet was designed to counteract the negative eﬀect caused by adding more convolu-
tional layers to a network. When this is done in the traditional way as seen in Figure
7.1a), improvement of the performance will saturate and eventually worsen after enough
layers are added. This is due to what is called the vanishing gradient problem, where
the gradient gets smaller for each layer it propagates through during backpropagation.
The authors of ResNet adressed this problem by adding a shortcut around two or more
layers, called identity shortcuts, allowing the gradient to propagate around the layers.
This design is called a residual block of which a simple example can be seen in Figure
45
==PAGE:64==
46

7.1b).

b)
Figure 7.1: A comparison of a normal convolutional layer a) and a residual block b).
For the residual block, the input X passes through two convolutional layers and is then
added to the output.
Since ResNet was originally designed for image classiﬁcations, the kernels used in the
convolutional layers are 2-dimensional. Thus, it was necessary to modify the kernels into
1 dimension, in order for the convolutional layers to be able to convolve the 1-dimensional
EEG signals. In Figure 7.2 the model used in this project is shown.
The signal ﬁrst passes through a 7x1 convolutional layer with stride 2 and 64 ﬁlters, then
the batch is normalized, then passed trough a ReLU activation function max pooled with
stride 2. These steps have the purpose of greatly reducing the amount of data in the
network, while retaining all the important information in the signals.
After the last residual block, the output was average pooled, where the mean of each
kernel was calculated, resulting in a output vector of length 512. In addtition to having
a regularization eﬀect, the average pooling layer gives the same output size no matter
the length of the input signal. Therefore, no changes has to be made to the network
archtecture when changing the length of the input signal.

class more often. Hence, it will include more of the other classes. If the model is tuned to
optimize precision for a given class, it will only predict this class when it is almost sure
it is right.

Chapter 10
Discussion
In this chapter, the thourough analysis of the discoveries made in this project is pre-
sented. This analysis include a review of design choices of the pre-processing steps, the
model architecture and the experiment designs, as well as the limitations of the study.
Furthermore, the values of comparing this study to the state of the art is presented. Fi-
nally, the physiological ﬁndings are discussed and ideas for future implementations and
improvements are presented and justiﬁed.
10.1
Performance of the proposed Model
10.1.1
Number of channels
From the results shown in Table 9.1, it can be seen that the highest model performance is
acheived using the two EEG channels C3A2 and O1A2 in combination. The performance
using both channels clearly outperforms the model using either channel alone.
This
result is as expected, as it makes sense that the model learns better when provided with
more information. However, in the experiment using three channels, the performance
slightly drops. It is challenging to excactly pinpoint the cause for this behaviour, as the
performance is aﬀected from a lot of factors.
One explanation could be that the extra channel introduces noise e.g. from eye movement,
58
==PAGE:77==
10.1. PERFORMANCE OF THE PROPOSED MODEL
59
which could hide the essential data from the model both during training and evaluation,
thus lowering performance. Another cause could be that the F3A2 channels do not carry
much relevant information for this age group. As seen in Figure 2.3 most of the maturation
of the gray matter happens in the occipital (O1 and C2) and motor/sensory area (C3, Cz
and C4), while the maturation of the graymatter in the frontal and parietal poles is slow
until around the age of 10.
However, the epoch accuracy increases with the introduction of the third EEG channel,
which must mean that the learning itself is not impaired. This may speak to a weakness in
the design of the model, as the training optimizes prediction of age in epochs, although the
end goal is to predict age in subjects. Thus, a smarter implementation of the loss function
could potentially resolve this issue, allowing the model to train on all three chanels, which
could improve the accuracy of the age prediction of the subjects.
10.1.2
Inﬂuence of sleep stages
Excluding sleep stages did not have any eﬀect on the model performance as seen in Table
9.2. This outcome shows that the model handles the noise from the sleep stages with low
accuracy very well. Because of this it is not necesarry to remove sleep stages as a part
of pre-processing. This is an advantage as manual sleep staging is a slow and expensive
procedure with a high inter-scorer variability[24]. However, automatic sleep staging is
still starting to perform at a satisfying level[25].
Another interpretation of the lack of impact on the model performance from removing
sleep stages is that a lot of redundant data is being fed to the model. The model can be
regarded as a sort of ﬁlter that removes data, redundant to the estimation of age from
EEG. Thus, a lot of information about the nature of brain aging can potentially be found
by analyzing the output of the penultimate layer of the model. This has earlier been done
with success on other classiﬁcation problems[26].
10.1.3
Segmentation of EEG signal
The model performed best using 30 second epochs. The performance slightly decreased
when the segment size was increased and the overlap was kept constant.

10.1.4
Model architecture
Filter:
A cut oﬀfrequency of 35 Hz might have removed some relevant information
Normalization:
This project used an unconventional way of normalizing the EEG signals, which resolved
around normalizing every single epoch. Traditionally the entire signal is normalized before
segmentation. However, as the training of the model was designed to optimize classiﬁca-
tion of age in single epochs, it was decided to use normalization on every epoch.
Dividing by the 99-percentile might have resulted in very small input values. Since the
weights in the network have a limited number of decimals, it might have limited some
solutions to be found.
Dropout:
It is hard to tell whether a dropout of 50% was the right choice. This hyperparameter
was not included in the grid search as the amount of combinations per per experiment
was already very large.
10.1.5
Epoch accuracy compared to subject accuracy
Some of the subjects have a higher accuracy than others, meaning that the guesses are
not random, but some of the subjects actually are more diﬃcult to classify.
Relatively low epoch accuracy compared to subject accuracy could indicate that the
model has identiﬁed the most important epochs across all subjects and uses these to
estimate age. This is resulting in a very general model with a decent performance on
many subjects. On the other and, a relatively high epoch accuracy compared to subject
accuracy could indicate that the model has learned the characteristics of fewer subjects.
The model is then very good at classifying most of the epoch of few subjects but fails on
the remaining subjects.

10.1.6
Final model
Considering the limitations of the dataset, the model performed well with an accuracy
of 82.5 ± 0.8 % and F1 scores 0.744 and 0.814 for the younger group and older group
respectively. The model had diﬃculties with classifying subjects closer to the ages where
the classes were separated (i.e. 7 and 8 year olds in the 4,5,6,7, 8,9,10 binary classiﬁcation)
and thus, only performed around 60% on the classiﬁcation of 7 year olds.
However,
this was to be expected as previous studies have shown more than 6 years error in age
prediction using regression[4, 5, 6], and classiﬁcation has only been shown to work with
a gap of 3 years between the two age groups. It should be noted, however, that the brain
develops much faster in children than in adults (i.e. more changes happen in a child brain
within a year than in an adult brain) [1, 7, 12] and that the subject per age was much
higher in the dataset used than for other studies. Thus, it should be possible to acheive
a lower error than that of other studies predicting age from EEG.
10.2
Limitations
To the knowledge of the author, no other studies to this date have been done on the subject
of age prediction in children using sleep EEG. Hence, state of the art solely consists of
studies predicting age in adults, only one of which uses sleep EEG. Furthermore, all
studies present regression models, whereas this project focuses on classiﬁcation, due to
the limiting range and poor resolution of ages in the dataset.
Comparison of binary
classiﬁcation models such as the one proposed in this project with regression models
is not feasible, due to the big diﬀerence in the complexity of the task as well as the
performance measures.
Only one of the state of the art studies has also presented a classiﬁcation[6]. The binary
classiﬁcation model presented by Dimitriadis et al. is performed on adults with a much
broader band of ages and has a gap between the age groups of 3 years, which massively
simpliﬁes the binary classiﬁcation. Taken into context, the dataset used in this project
has an age range of 6 years. However, the dataset in this project provides a much larger
quantity of subjects in each age group, even if the age groups are much broader in the
study by Dimitriadis et al.
Sun et al. made a longitudal experiment in their study. This was possible because one
of their datasets contained a patient group with two PSG session separated by 5 years.
==PAGE:80==
62
CHAPTER 10. DISCUSSION
This kind of test was not possible using the CHAT dataset, even though it also has a
group of subjects with two PSG sessions. The reason for this is that the resolution of age
is 1 year and the separation of the two PSG sessions was only 7 months. Generally, this
low age resolution is a major constraint, especially in an age group that changes so much.
Due to these limitations, any comparison with the state of the art studies has to
be done very carefully, and direct comparisons of performances should be avoided. The
results from the state of the art, however, are still useful in the analysis of the performance
of the model proposed in this project. Additionally, techical and physiological ﬁndings in
the state of the art studies have been central in a series decisions made in this project.
The demographic information provided for the dataset was very poor. Only around
a third of the subjects had their demographics described. Because of that there is no
way to be sure that the dataset is equally distributed across race, gender and other
factors, which means that some strong assumptions would have to be taken for analysis
inclding all subjects. One solution could be to only use the subjects that lack demographic
information to train the network and perform analyses on the well-documented subjects.
10.3
Future Implementations
As the model implemented in this project was trained on individual epochs, it could
only analyze microstructures.
As Ohayon et al.
showed in their paper[1], there are
substantial changes in the macrostructures as the brain ages in this age group. Hence,
another neural network architecture, that takes the macrostructures into account, could
potentially improve the performance. One approach could be to implement a recurrent
neural network, which takes multiple epochs into context. This would also change the
models focus from predicting age of single epochs to predicting age of subjects as discussed
earlier in section 10.1.1.
To enhance the analyses of the dataset, the prediction resolution should be reduced.
The best way to do this is by using a regression model. However, this has proven diﬃcult
to implement on a dataset with such a small age range. Nevertheless, transfer learning
could potentially facilitate regression, by training a classiﬁcation network and using its
penultimate layer (as described in section 10.1.2) as input to a regression model. The
classiﬁcation model could learn the general features of aging and the regression model
could reﬁne these and bring a much higher resolution output. This is assuming that the

classiﬁcation has learned suﬃciently for the regression to work.
In this study only the EEG from the left hemisphere was used, due to the assumption
that there is not a lot of new information to be gained from using the mirrored electrodes.
This assumption was not tested and could very well be false. In the future more electrodes
should be added to see if the performance increases. More electrodes would possibly give
a more comple picture of the brain and could help the model register the changes in gray

Chapter 11
Conclusion
In this project a binary age classiﬁcation model was implemented to prove the concept of
age prediction in children using sleep EEG signals. The project was an explorative proof
of concept study, as no prior work on the subject had been done. Hence, comparison with
other sudies was not viable and other studies could mainly be used for inspiration.
One of the biggest challenges was the low range and poor resolution of age in the CHAT
dataset. This prevented a regression model and kept the accuracy.
The ﬁnal model produces an accuracy of 82.5 ± 0.8 %. It performed considerably better
on the subjects with a chronologial age further away from the split of classes. Removing
sleep stages did not have an eﬀect on the performance, which indicates that the model
handles noise well. The model had its ﬂaws including training to predict age for epochs
without regarding the ﬁnal age prediction for subjects.
Increasing the segment length had a negetive eﬀect on the performance
The ResNet architecture performed well, however another loss function should be imple-
mented to optimize the model to classify age of subjects rather than age of epochs. To
better take the macrostructures of sleep into account, a recurrent neural network could
be implemented.
