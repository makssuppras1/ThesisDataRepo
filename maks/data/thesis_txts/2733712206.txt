Summary 
 
Fucoidans are complex, fucose-rich, sulphated polysaccharides. They are naturally found primarily in 
brown macroalgae, where they contain diﬀerent backbone structures depending on the species from 
which they originate. Their structures also vary considerably regarding sulfaƟon paƩerns and the degree 
of branching, which are aﬀected by various factors such as the extracƟon method and harvest season of 
the fucoidan. Fucoidans have been reported to have a range of bioacƟviƟes, and it is thought that 
diﬀerent fucoidan structures can have diﬀerent bioacƟve properƟes. For medical applicaƟons, fucoidans 
must ﬁrst be depolymerized into homogeneous and chemically deﬁned products. EnzymaƟc treatment 
with fucoidanases can yield homogeneous fucoidan oligosaccharides with the potenƟal to be applied in 
the pharmaceuƟcal industry.  
RelaƟvely liƩle is known about fucoidanases, and only a few have been characterized. There is thus a 
strong incenƟve to discover and characterize novel fucoidanases, with the hope of learning more about 
these enzymes and potenƟally being able to produce a wider range of fucoidan oligosaccharides. This 
study focuses on the characterizaƟon of four fucoidanase, namely Fim28, Fim31, Fim6.1, and Fim299. 
These fucoidanases originate from a metagenome stemming from an Icelandic hot spring enriched with 
macroalgae. Fucoidans extracted from the brown macroalgae Saccharina laƟssima and Laminaria 
digitata were used throughout the characterizaƟons of each fucoidanase.  
All fucoidanases showed acƟvity around 30 °C, and had pH opƟma of around 7-8. Fim28 demonstrated 
halophilic properƟes, with no acƟvity observed below 400 mM NaCl. Fim6.1 and Fim299 were fast-acƟng 
enzymes and were robust with respect to temperature, pH, and NaCl concentraƟon.

1 IntroducƟon 
There is growing interest in the discovery and applicaƟon of novel bioacƟve products and sourcing them 
sustainably. Recent research has highlighted the marine environment as a valuable source of compounds 
with several health beneﬁts [4,5]. Marine macroalgae can serve as a sustainable source of such 
compounds, as it can be culƟvated without the need for fresh water, arable land, or ferƟlizer applicaƟon 
[6]. CulƟvaƟon of marine macroalgae can also have several posiƟve impacts on the marine environment. 
For example, it can help to miƟgate eutrophicaƟon and harmful algal blooms as a result of assimilaƟon 
and removal of nutrients like nitrogen and phosphor from the water column [7]. Certain types of 
macroalgae have also been recognized as foundaƟon species which provide habitat for other organisms, 
thereby contribuƟng to the biodiversity of the marine environment [8]. In addiƟon, the culƟvaƟon of 
macroalgae can potenƟally beneﬁt the climate through carbon sequestraƟon [9]. Algae ﬁx approximately 
50 gigatonnes of inorganic carbon annually, up to 80% of which can end up stored in algal glycans [10]. 
Glycans can eﬀecƟvely sequester carbon if their producƟon outweighs their remineralizaƟon by 
heterotrophic bacteria [10]. Fucoidans, glycans derived from brown algae, have been described as robust 
to bacterial degradaƟon and found to persist in the marine environment in an exuded form [9]. Anionic 
glycans can aggregate and sink to the deep ocean, which further increases their long-term carbon-
sequestering abiliƟes, as the occurrence of remineralizaƟon of the glycans in deeper waters delays the 
return of carbon to the atmosphere [10].  
 
Of parƟcular interest in this study are fucoidan polysaccharides, which are naturally found in brown 
macroalgae and certain marine invertebrates [11,12]. Due to their reported bioacƟvity, fucoidans have 
gained interest within the pharmacological ﬁeld [11]. For example, fucoidans have been shown to have 
anƟtumor, anƟoxidant, anƟcoagulant, anƟdiabeƟc, anƟ-inﬂammatory, and immunomodulatory 
properƟes [13–18]. DepolymerizaƟon of fucoidans to homogenous oligosaccharides with preserved 
sulfaƟon paƩerns is desirable for biomedical applicaƟons, as smaller fucoidans exhibit higher 
bioavailability and sulfaƟon is a key determinant for fucoidan’s bioacƟvity [19,20]. In addiƟon, 
homogenous products can be more easily characterized and invesƟgated for bioacƟvity compared to 
heterogenous products. Fucoidanases, enzymes which depolymerize fucoidans, are capable of producing 
homogeneous, chemically deﬁned fucoidan oligosaccharides while preserving fucoidan sulfaƟon 
paƩerns [21,22]. In this study, four novel fucoidanases are in focus.  
1.1 Macroalgae 
Macroalgae are a group of organisms with a large taxonomic diversity. They can be grouped based on 
their pigmentaƟon into three phyla: phylum Rhodophyta (red macroalgae), phylum Chlorophyta (green 
algae), and phylum Phaeophyta (brown macroalgae) [23]. The great geneƟc diversity amongst these 
phyla is reﬂected by the diﬀerences in their chemical composiƟon and bioacƟve compounds, including 
polysaccharides [24]. For example, carrageenan, ulvans, and fucoidans are sulfated polysaccharides 
commonly found in the cell walls of red, green, and brown macroalgae, respecƟvely [25]. Although each 
of these components have been shown to display bioacƟvity, fucoidan is the polysaccharide of interest 
for this study [26].  
1.2 Fucoidans 
Fucoidans are complex polysaccharides characterized by a backbone of sulfated fucose units [27]. The 
glycosidic linkage paƩern of the backbone is oŌen used to group fucoidans into two general classes, 
where Class 1 fucoidans consist of α-(1,3)-linked L-fucose residues, and Class II fucoidans are composed

of alternaƟng α-(1,3)- and α-(1,4)-linked L-fucose residues (Figure 1) [28]. The classiﬁcaƟon of a given 
fucoidan depends on the macroalgae species from which it derives. For example, brown macroalgae 
species belonging to the Laminariales and Ectocarpales orders have been found to contain Class I 
fucoidans, while some species belonging to the Fucales order have been shown to contain Class II 
fucoidans [28].

Apart from varying backbone structures, fucoidans can be highly heterogeneous in several ways. One of 
which is the degree of branching, ranging from being relaƟvely low in fucoidans from marine 
invertebrates to quite extensive in most brown algae-derived fucoidans [30]. For example, branch points 
have been reported to occur every two or three fucose residues in Fucus vesiculosus [31].  SulfaƟon 
paƩern and sulfate content can also diﬀer greatly between diﬀerent fucoidans [16]. Moreover, non-
fucose monosaccharides such as mannose, galactose, and xylose can be present in variable amounts 
[32,33]. This wide variety in fucoidan structure depends not only on the macroalgae species from which 
the fucoidan originates, but also on other factors such as the harvest season of the fucoidan, the 
extracƟon method of the fucoidan, and the environmental condiƟons in which the fucoidan grows such 
as exposure to mechanical stress [27,34,35]. 
The high heterogeneity of fucoidans is parƟally responsible for the range of bioacƟviƟes they display. For 
example, some studies have reported a relaƟon between sulfate content and anƟcoagulant acƟvity [36]. 
The molecular weight of fucoidans has also been shown to inﬂuence their funcƟonal properƟes, with 
low-molecular weight fucoidans (<10 kDa in size) demonstraƟng higher bioavailability and bioacƟvity 
compared to fucoidans of higher molecular weights [16,37]. 
This makes the depolymerizaƟon of naƟve fucoidans to low-molecular weight fucoidan (LMWF) 
oligosaccharides highly desirable [16]. Although LMWF can be produced by using chemical treatment,

Saccharina laƟssima (Sl) is a brown algae species belonging to the Laminariaceae family. It is culƟvated in

1.3 Fucoidanases 
Fucoidanases are enzymes which catalyze the hydrolysis of glycosidic linkages in fucoidans, and have 
been found primarily in marine bacteria, but also marine fungi and invertebrates [40]. The ExPASy portal 
classiﬁes endo-acƟng fucoidanases according to the type of linkage they cleave, where EC numbers 
3.2.1.211 and 3.2.1.212 describe endo-(1,3)- and endo-(1,4)-fucoidanases, respecƟvely. The CAZy 
(Carbohydrate AcƟve Enzymes) database oﬀers an alternaƟve classiﬁcaƟon based on sequence 
homology, where fucoidanases belong to either the glycoside hydrolase GH107 or GH168 family. The 
GH107 family currently includes 39 fucoidanases, of which 17 have been characterized. The GH168 
family currently contains 54 fucoidanases, of which only 1 has been characterized [47].   
The fucoidanases characterized in this study belong to the GH107 family. CharacterisƟc to the 
fucoidanases in this family is the presence of a (β/α)8  barrel comprising the catalyƟc domain, also 
referred to as the D1 domain [48]. In terms of catalyƟc mechanism, an asparƟc acid residue is thought to 
act as a catalyƟc nucleophile, and a hisƟdine residue as a catalyƟc acid/base. This thought is supported 
by the observaƟon of complete conservaƟon of these residues in the acƟve site, and the ﬁnding that 
mutaƟng the suspected catalyƟc hisƟdine in fucoidanase MfFcnA destroyed its acƟvity [48,49].  
 
Although there are some studies characterizing GH107 fucoidanases in the literature, there is sƟll much 
to be discovered about these enzymes. A beƩer understanding of the structure and funcƟon of 
fucoidanases, as well as the characterizaƟon of novel fucoidanases, has the potenƟal of enabling a more 
tailored approach to producing homogenous fucoidan oligosaccharides which would not only be useful 
for the structural elucidaƟon of fucoidans, but also for the invesƟgaƟon of fucoidan bioacƟvity [32].   
 
The fucoidanase-encoding plasmids used in this study were provided by MATIS Biotech, coordinator of 
the MARIKAT project. The fucoidanases characterized in this study originate from Skarðshver, an 
Icelandic geothermic seawater pool. CondiƟons at this site include a salinity of around 590 mM NaCl and 
reported temperatures of 14 °C and 34 °C [49,50].  The fucoidanase sequences were obtained from 
metagenomes stemming from this seawater pool post-enrichment with Fucus vesiculosus. The 
metagenomes were screened for potenƟal fucoidanase genes, and four of the putaƟve fucoidanases 
were chosen. Four plasmids were constructed to contain each of the respecƟve fucoidanase sequences. 
This study will refer to the fucoidanases as Fim (Fucoidanase Icelandic Metagenome) 28, Fim31, Fim6.1 
and Fim299. The numbering comes from the plasmid name on which the fucoidanase was encoded. In 
order to slow translaƟon to help with correct folding of Fim28 and Fim6.1, their sequences had been 
fused with a malE gene, which encodes the maltose-binding protein [49,51]. An overview of each 
fucoidanase can be seen in Table 1.

2 Materials and methods 
2.1 BioinformaƟcs analyses 
2.1.1 Sequence analysis  
For all fucoidanases, InterProScan (hƩps://www.ebi.ac.uk/interpro/search/sequence/), SignalP 
(hƩps://biolib.com/DTU/SignalP-6/), and DeepTMHMM (hƩps://dtu.biolib.com/DeepTMHMM) were 
used to predict the presence of domains, signal pepƟdes, and intermembrane regions, respecƟvely.  
MSA (mulƟple sequence alignment) was performed using EMBL-EBI’s MUSCLE tool. The MUSCLE tool 
generates sequence alignments based on the MulƟple Sequence Comparison by Log-ExpectaƟon 
(MUSCLE) method. In the ﬁrst alignment, the amino acid sequences (excluding any MalE fusions and C-
terminal His tags of each of the four invesƟgated fucoidanases, as well as sequences from known 
published fucoidanases were used. The aligned sequences were then trimmed to contain only the 
catalyƟc D1 domain, which was deﬁned as the enƟrety of the sequence for fucoidanase P5AFcnA [48]. A 
second alignment was performed on the trimmed sequences, and the resulƟng alignment was used to 
construct a Neighbor-joining tree using EMBL-EBI’s Simple Phylogeny tool with default seƫngs. 
VisualizaƟon of the constructed phylogeneƟc tree was then carried out in FigTree v1.4.4 [52].  
2.1.2 3D structure predicƟons 
3D structure predicƟons of the fucoidanases were generated using ColabFold v1.5.3, which generates 
predicƟons of protein structure using AlphaFold2 and Alphafold2-mulƟmer [53,54]. The query sequences 
used were the full fucoidanase amino acid sequences, including the C-terminal His tags in the C terminus 
and any MalE fusions. ColabFold was executed using default seƫngs. The ColabFold structure 
predicƟons were visualized in PyMOL (The PyMOL Molecular Graphics System, Version 2.3.0 Schrödinger, 
LLC.). Ribbon structures were displayed in the color ‘Grey 60’ on a white background. CatalyƟc amino 
acids idenƟﬁed from the sequence alignments were displayed as ‘licorice sƟcks’, and surface charges 
were displayed by applying the APBS ElectrostaƟcs plugin with default seƫngs.  
2.2 Fucoidanase expression and biomass harvest  
E. coli BL21 (DE3) cells were used as producƟon hosts for each of the four fucoidanases to be expressed, 
namely Fim28, Fim31, Fim6.1, and Fim299. Prior to this project, plasmids encoding these fucoidanase 
genes were ﬁrst ampliﬁed in E. coli DH5α cells, and then used to transform E. coli BL21 cells, resulƟng in 
the four BL21 strains used for expression. BL21 cells harbor the pGro7 chaperone on the pGro7 plasmid 
which contains an L-arabinose-inducible promoter and a chloramphenicol resistance gene. The 
fucoidanase-encoding plasmids are L-rhamnose-inducible and confer ampicillin resistance.   
First, overnight (O/N) cultures from each BL21 strain were produced. Using an inoculaƟon loop, a 
glycerol stock of the respecƟve strain was used to inoculate 10 mL LB media containing 34 μg/mL 
chloramphenicol (Cam) and 100 μg/mL ampicillin (Amp) evenly distributed into two culture tubes. The 
cultures were incubated O/N at 37 °C and 180 RPM. For each strain, the two corresponding O/N cultures 
were used to inoculate 500 mL LB media containing 0.05% (w/v) L-arabinose, 34 μg/mL Cam and 100 
μg/mL Amp in a 2000 mL shake ﬂask. The culture was incubated at 37 °C and 180 RPM. Once the OD600 
of the culture reached between 0.8 and 1.0, L-rhamnose was added to a ﬁnal concentraƟon of 0.15% to 
induce fucoidanase expression. The culture was then incubated at 20 °C and 180 RPM O/N.

2.3 Fucoidanase puriﬁcaƟon  
Fucoidanase puriﬁcaƟon was performed to recover the intracellular enzymes, and then isolate them 
from other cellular debris. The buﬀers used throughout the puriﬁcaƟon process contain the indicated 
NaCl concentraƟons, with the excepƟon that 800 mM NaCl was used in buﬀers used to purify Fim28. This 
was due to the previous ﬁnding that Fim28 has halophilic properƟes and shows liƩle to no acƟvity at low 
NaCl concentraƟons [49]. 
The harvested biomass pellets were thawed on ice and resuspended in ice-cold extracƟon buﬀer (20 mM 
Tris-HCl, 250mM NaCl, 20mM imidazole, 0.2 mg/mL lysozyme, pH 7.4). For each gram of biomass, 3 mL 
of extracƟon buﬀer were used. Using an UP 400S Ultrasonic processer (Hielscher Ultrasonics, Teltow, 
Germany) equipped with microƟp probe #4420, the resuspended mixture was sonicated on ice for two 
cycles of two minutes of pulse (10 seconds on, 40 seconds oﬀ, 30% amplitude) followed by one cycle of 
one minute of pulse with the same seƫngs. The sonicated soluƟon was centrifuged at 19,000 x g for 15 
minutes at 4 °C.  The supernatant was ﬁltered through a 0.45 μm ﬁlter, to remove large debris, onto 1 mL 
HisPur™ Ni-NTA resin (Thermo Fisher ScienƟﬁc, Waltham, MassachuseƩs, USA) which had been pre-
equilibrated. The pre-equilibraƟon consisted of incubaƟng the resin with 10 mL pre-equilibraƟon buﬀer 
(20 mM Tris-HCl, 250 mM NaCl, pH 7.4) for a minimum of 10 minutes on slow agitaƟon, centrifuging at 
2000 RPM for 2 minutes at 4 °C, and then gently removing the supernatant. The crude enzyme on resin 
was incubated on ice for a minimum of one hour under slow agitaƟon.  
The resin-enzyme mixture was then centrifuged at 2000 RPM for 2 minutes at 4 °C, and the supernatant 
gently removed. Following this, two washes of the resin-enzyme mixture were carried out by adding 10 
mL wash buﬀer (20 mM Tris-HCl, 250 mM NaCl, 20 mM imidazole, pH 7.4), incubaƟng on ice for 10 
minutes under slow agitaƟon, centrifugaƟng the soluƟon at 2000 RPM for 2 minutes at 4 °C, and again 
gently removing the supernatant so as to not disturb the resin. The resin-enzyme mixture was then 
resuspended in 10 mL wash buﬀer. In a cold room (approximately 8 °C), the resin-enzyme mixture was 
transferred to a gravity ﬂow column and the fucoidanase was eluted with 1 mL of ﬁve diﬀerent eluƟon 
buﬀers (20 mM Tris-HCl, 250 mM NaCl, pH 7.4) containing increasing amounts of imidazole (100 mM, 
150 mM, 200 mM, 250 mM, and 300 mM). AŌer determining the protein concentraƟon with a Bradford 
assay, the Fim28 eluates were diluted to 1 mg/mL before being stored at -80 °C. The three remaining 
fucoidanases were desalted on a PD MiniTrap column (PD MiniTrap G-25 columns, CyƟva, Marlborough, 
100 Results Way, United States) before being diluted to 1 mg/mL and stored at -80 °C.

A Bradford assay was used to determine the protein concentraƟons of the puriﬁcaƟon eluates. To 
prepare a standard, 10 μL of six diﬀerent concentraƟons (0, 0.125, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, and 1 mg/mL) of 
bovine serum albumin (BSA) were added to wells in a microƟter plate containing 190 μL Bradford 
reagent (Merck, Darmstadt, Germany).  
For each eluate, 1 μL was added at a Ɵme to 190 μL Bradford reagent unƟl the color of the mixture 
appeared to be within the range displayed by the standards. The microƟter plate was kept at room 
temperature for 10 minutes to allow a complete color change, followed by measurement of absorbance

3.1 BioinformaƟcs analyses 
3.1.1 Sequence analysis  
InterProScan was used to predict potenƟal domains and important sites in each fucoidanase. The only 
domain predicted was a glycoside hydrolase domain for Fim299 (Appendix 5). The SignalP tool was used 
to predict any signal pepƟdes. No signal pepƟdes were found for any of the fucoidanases (Appendix 6). 
Finally, Deep TMHMM was used to predict any intermembrane regions in the fucoidanases. No 
intermembrane regions were predicted for the fucoidanases (Appendix 7).  
A mulƟple sequence alignment (MSA) was carried out on the amino acid sequences for the fucoidanases 
to be characterized in this study, as well as the sequences of known published fucoidanases. A list of the 
sequences used in this ﬁrst MSA can be found in Appendix 1, and the results of the alignment itself in 
Appendix 2. A second MSA was then performed using the sequences trimmed to only contain the 
catalyƟc D1 domain. The sequences used and MSA results can be seen in Appendix 3 and Appendix 4, 
respecƟvely.  
From the second MSA output, phylogeneƟc tree data was obtained and then visualized using FigTree, as 
shown in Figure 3. The node values in the phylogeneƟc tree are branch lengths, which are an indicaƟon 
of how much geneƟc change has occurred from the previous node. In the phylogeneƟc tree, it appears 
that the alpha-(1,3)-linkage-speciﬁc fucoidanases generally cluster separately from the alpha-(1,4)-
linkage-speciﬁc fucoidanases. The one excepƟon would be that Mef1, an alpha-(1,4)-linkage-speciﬁc 
fucoidanase, clusters more closely to the alpha-(1,3)-linkage-speciﬁc fucoidanases than other alpha-
(1,4)-linkage-speciﬁc fucoidanases (Figure 3). The cluster containing all the alpha-(1,3)-linkage-speciﬁc 
fucoidanase also includes the four Fim fucoidanases, Mef1, and two fucoidanases of unknown linkage 
speciﬁcity. From these results, the four Fim fucoidanases might be expected to act on alpha-(1,3)-
linkages, but this would have to be conﬁrmed empirically.

structure predicƟons were visualized in PyMOL. Figure 4 displays the generated 3D structure predicƟons, 
as well as the average pLDDT score for each predicaƟon. The 3D structure predicƟons for Fim31 and 
Fim299 had a pLDDT score above 90, indicaƟng a high expected accuracy for these predicted models

conﬁdence, Fim28 and Fim6.1 also had relaƟvely high pLDDT scores of 88.4 and 89.0, respecƟvely. It is 
thought that the presence of the MalE fusion may contribute to the lower conﬁdence of these models. 
From the 3D structures, it can be seen that the four fucoidanases share a characterisƟc (α/β)8-barrel 
domain containing the two catalyƟc amino acids (Figure 4). However, they appear to vary in terms of 
their overall structure. In addiƟon, their acƟve sites seem to range from being strongly posiƟvely 
charged, as is the case for Fim28, to only having a mild posiƟve charge, as is the case for Fim31 (Figure 
4). The general observaƟon of a posiƟve charge at the acƟve site is in accordance with the fact that these 
enzymes accommodate a substrate with negaƟvely charged sulphate groups.

3.4 Timescale assays 
The purpose of the Ɵmescale assays was to select reacƟons Ɵmes for the coming assays at which both 
improved and worsened enzyme acƟvity would be able to be observed. All Ɵmescale assays were carried 
out at 30 °C and 500 RPM. The molar concentraƟons of Fim31, Fim6.1, and Fim299 were the same for 
the Ɵmescale and all following assays to enable a more accurate comparison across results. The fucoidan 
concentraƟons used in reacƟons for all the coming assays were 0.9% (w/v), with the excepƟon that the 
Fim28 reacƟons on Ld and Fim31 reacƟons on Sl included 1.8% (w/v) fucoidan. It was decided to double 
the fucoidan concentraƟons in these reacƟons due to the relaƟvely faint bands observed in Figure 6, 
where 0.9% (w/v) was used.    
All Ɵmescale assays were carried out in 70 μL of reacƟon; 20 μL were to be analyzed with C-PAGE, and 
the remaining 50 μL analyzed using high performance size-exclusion chromatography (HP-SEC) to 
determine the molecular weight distribuƟon of reacƟon products.  
To serve as a benchmark, two addiƟonal Ɵmescale assays were performed on Mef2, a fucoidanase 
described in Tran et. al (2022) [55]. The molar concentraƟon of Mef2 was adjusted to match that of 
Fim31, Fim6.1, and Fim299. The Mef2 Ɵmescale assays were carried out in a 20 μL reacƟon volume 
containing 0.9% (w/v) fucoidan.

4 Discussion 
A unique observaƟon in this study is the halophilic properƟes exhibited by Fim28, which is in accordance 
with the ﬁndings of Vangsgaard et. al (2023) [49]. There are various evoluƟonary adapƟons which allow 
halophilic enzymes to withstand high-salinity environments [61]. One feature commonly found in 
halophilic enzymes, which is also shared by Fim28, is a highly negaƟve surface charge resulƟng from a 
high proporƟon of acidic amino acids close to the enzyme surface [61]. It is thought that a high NaCl 
concentraƟon reduces the electrostaƟc repulsion between acidic residues through charge screening. This 
can enable a compact folding of the enzyme due to enhanced hydrophobic interacƟons [62]. The model 
==PAGE:50==
45 
 
of the 3D structure for Fim28 produced by AlphaFold Colab shows that Fim28 has a negaƟve surface 
charge apart from the acƟve site, which may contribute to its dependence on a high NaCl concentraƟon. 
In addiƟon, this negaƟve surface charge may help the negaƟvely charged fucoidan substrate bind the 
acƟve site. If the acƟve site is the only posiƟvely charged region of the enzyme, it may help recruit a 
negaƟvely charged fucoidan substrate to this locaƟon.  
Although halophilic fucoidanases have not previously been menƟoned in the literature, some halophilic 
gram-negaƟve marine bacteria have been reported to produce fucoidanases [63]. The fucoidanases 
described, being produced by halophilic bacteria, could potenƟally have halophilic properƟes. A 
comparison between the structural properƟes of these fucoidanases and those of Fim28 might help 
reveal a connecƟon between high salt tolerance and enzyme structure. To beƩer understand the 
reasoning behind Fim28’s high salt tolerance, it would also be interesƟng to know which organism 
produces this enzyme. However, as the sequence from Fim28 stems from a metagenome, its biological 
origins are unknown.  
Unique to the GH107 family is the presence of a proposed catalyƟc hisƟdine residue which acts as an 
acid/base. The results from mulƟple sequence alignment showed that the four invesƟgated fucoidanases 
shared a hisƟdine residue at same amino acid posiƟon corresponding to proposed catalyƟc hisƟdines 
from previous studies, conﬁrming that these fucoidanases indeed belong to the GH107 family [28,48]. 
Furthermore, GH107 fucoidanases have endo-acƟvity, while those belonging to the GH29 family have 
exo-acƟvity [48]. The disƟnct bands observed in the C-PAGE results for each of the four fucoidanases are 
an indicaƟon of endo-acƟvity, further conﬁrming that these are GH107 fucoidanases. An exo-acƟve 
fucoidanase would only be able to cleave one fucose unit at a Ɵme from the end of the fucoidan 
polysaccharide, resulƟng in an accumulaƟon of products one fucose unit in size as well as large fucoidan 
polysaccharides that decrease in size by only one fucose unit over Ɵme. On a C-PAGE gel, this product 
proﬁle would consist of a very small band at the boƩom of the gel corresponding to the singular fucose 
units, and a smear at the top of the gel corresponding to large fucoidan polysaccharides whose gradual 
reducƟon in size cannot be disƟnguished as separate bands. This banding paƩern was not observed on 
any of the C-PAGE gels, indicaƟng that these fucoidanases are not exo-acƟng.  
Although diﬀerenƟal substrate speciﬁcity has previously been reported for fucoidanases within the 
GH107 family, the four fucoidanases invesƟgated in this study were all acƟve on the same substrates, 
namely Sl and Ld [48]. However, very unique fucoidan oligosaccharide product proﬁles were produced by 
these four fucoidanases, suggesƟng diﬀerenƟal recogniƟon of cleavage sites on the same fucoidan 
substrate. SupporƟng this is the observed variaƟon in amino acid sequences adjacent to the catalyƟc 
residues and in the conserved domains around the -1 subsite, perhaps enabling the accommodaƟon of 
diﬀerent fucoidan structures in the acƟve site of the various fucoidanases. However, this would have to 
be conﬁrmed through structural elucidaƟon of the fucoidanases and fucoidan substrates, followed by 
modelling the fucoidanase-fucoidan interacƟons using tools such as docking.  
An interest in low-molecular weight fucoidan (LMWF) oligosaccharides, which are under 10 kDa in size, 
has been expressed in the literature due to their associated bioavailability and bioactivity [16]. Medium-
molecular weight fucoidan (MMWF) oligosaccharides, which are 10-10,000 kDa in size, have also been 
reported to exhibit significant antithrombic activity [64]. The size exclusion data from this study suggest 
that the four Fim fucoidanases may have potential in producing fucoidan oligosaccharides within these 
size ranges. For example, Fim28 digestion of Sl resulted in fucoidan products down to 15 kDa in size, 
corresponding to a rather small MMWF. Fim6.1 and Fim299 digestion of Sl produced fucoidan products 
==PAGE:51==
46 
 
as small as 7 kDa in size, falling within the LMWF range. As the product profiles created by the four Fim 
fucoidanases are visually quite different, as shown by the C-PAGE results, it would be interesting to see 
whether the various products exhibit unique bioactivities.   
 
The pH and temperature opƟma for the four Fim fucoidanases make sense considering the site from 
which they originate. The pH of the ocean lies at around 8, and the Fim fucoidanases were all acƟve at 
this pH. In addiƟon, temperatures of 14 °C and 34 °C have been reported at the geothermic seawater 
pool from which the Fim fucoidanases originate [50]. The range of temperature opƟma observed for 
each of the fucoidanases in this study overlaps with these reported temperatures of the site. However, 
there is less consistency between the results of the NaCl assays and the expected NaCl condiƟons in the 
seawater pool, which is around 590 mM [48]. While Fim28 was clearly acƟve at this NaCl concentraƟon, 
Fim31 only showed observable acƟvity at up to 400 mM NaCl. Moreover, the opƟmal NaCl concentraƟon 
for Fim6.1 and Fim299 seems to lie between 0 mM and 400 mM NaCl.  
This study is carried out as part of the MARIKAT project, which aims to discover and develop robust 
enzymes capable of reﬁning macroalgae for industrial applicaƟons. In this respect, the fucoidanases 
characterized in this study show great potenƟal. Fim28 not only tolerates but seems to thrive best at 
high NaCl concentraƟons of around 400-1000 mM, which is similar to the results reported by 
Vangsgaard et. al (2023) [49]. This fucoidanase could therefore prove useful in industrial seƫngs where 
seawater is used, as it should remain acƟve at a NaCl concentraƟon of 600 mM. Fim6.1 and Fim299 also 
demonstrated impressive robustness and acƟvity throughout the various characterizaƟon assays. Not 
only were they fast-acƟng enzymes with observable acƟvity aŌer just 1 minute of reacƟon, but they also 
displayed thermostability with acƟvity up to 60-70 °C. In addiƟon, they appeared to be quite robust with 
respect to pH and NaCl concentraƟons, with both toleraƟng up to 1000 mM NaCl. Fim6.1 and Fim299 
show great promise for industrial seƫngs based on these results, as many industrial reacƟons take place 
at relaƟvely high temperatures to increase reacƟon rate. Their robustness with respect to pH and NaCl 
concentraƟon also broadens the range of their potenƟal industrial applicaƟons.   
5 Conclusion  
In this study, four novel fucoidanases were recombinantly expressed and characterized with respect to 
substrate speciﬁcity, fucoidan oligosaccharide product proﬁle produced over Ɵme, and opƟma regarding 
temperature, pH and NaCl concentraƟon. AddiƟonally, 3D structures of each fucoidanase were 
generated with AlphaFold Colab, and mulƟple sequence alignments were carried out which idenƟﬁed 
the locaƟon of catalyƟc residues.
