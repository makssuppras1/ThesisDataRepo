Abstract
This thesis addresses the need for a practical optimization framework for oﬀshore wind-to-
hydrogen systems (OWHS) by developing a realistic yet computationally eﬃcient Model Pre-
dictive Control (MPC) framework. The research integrates both alkaline (ALK) and proton
exchange membrane (PEM) electrolyzers with a battery energy storage system (BESS), while
including important system dynamics such as electrolyzer eﬃciency, thermal management, and
degradation eﬀects.
The developed MPC framework uses Mixed Integer Linear Programming (MILP) to opti-
mize wind-to-hydrogen operations under ﬂuctuating wind conditions and electricity prices. A
case study was conducted using real wind power data from the Anholt Oﬀshore Wind Farm,
testing twelve unique scenarios across diﬀerent system conﬁgurations, wind conditions, and
with/without battery energy storage system. These scenarios were tested with a 1:1 ratio of
Alkaline (ALK):Proton Exchange Membrane (PEM) capacity. The hybrid conﬁguration with a
Battery Energy Storage System (BESS) was additionally tested for a 3:1 ALK:PEM capacity
ratio.
Key ﬁndings of the 1:1 ratio show that the hybrid ALK+PEM conﬁguration with BESS provides
the most balanced approach, using the cost-eﬀectiveness of the ALK electrolyzer for stable
base load operation while utilizing the ﬂexibility of the PEM electrolyzer for handling power
ﬂuctuations. The PEM electrolyzer handled 71.1% of the total hydrogen production in wind-
deﬁcit conditions due to its greater ﬂexibility compared to the ALK electrolyzer. However, this
conﬁguration showed higher power change penalties (1,337 in wind-deﬁcit and 992 in wind-
rich conditions) compared to the 3:1 ratio, indicating more frequent power ﬂuctuations that
increase electrolyzer degradation. The 1:1 conﬁguration also experienced greater curtailment
(1,784 MWh) in wind-rich conditions compared to the 3:1 ratio (872 MWh), representing less
eﬃcient utilization of available wind energy.
The 3:1 ratio outperformed the 1:1 ratio in the net total result (e) by 21% in wind-deﬁcit
conditions and 44.7% in wind-rich conditions. The BESS proved crucial for system ﬂexibility,
reducing curtailment and enabling more consistent electrolyzer operation, lowering electrolyzer
degradation. The power change penalty mechanism eﬀectively reduced electrolyzer degradation
by stimulating the MPC to provide stable operation, particularly for ALK electrolyzers.
This research proposes a more practical optimization framework for real-world implementation
of OWHS control systems, demonstrating how model-based control can operate an OWHS in
a ﬂexible way while limiting degradation eﬀects and minimizing the total operational costs.

Chapter 1 Introduction
1.1
Background and motivation
As global temperatures are still on the rise (see Figure 1.1) and climate change eﬀects are
getting more and more serious[1], there is still an urgent need to transition to sustainable energy
systems. Scientists from all over the world are researching sustainable energy alternatives to
reduce greenhouse gas emissions and combat climate change. One of these alternatives is to
produce sustainable fuels using renewable energy. Sustainable fuels, such as hydrogen, can
serve as an energy storage medium or replace fossil fuels (either directly or by converting it to
another fuel ﬁrst) in various sectors to provide a cleaner alternative.
Figure 1.1: Annual global mean temperature anomalies relative to a pre-industrial (1850-
1900) baseline shown from 1850 to 2024 Source: Data are from the six datasets indicated in
the legend[2].
Of renewable energy sources, wind power is one of the most advanced, cost-eﬀective, and
rapidly growing technologies, with global generation exceeding 2250 TWh in 2024 [3]. Wind
power can be generated either onshore or oﬀshore. Oﬀshore wind can have a capacity factor
of 60-70%, 4 to 5 times higher compared to onshore wind power [4], making it suitable for
larger electrolyzer energy systems. While oﬀshore wind can provide a signiﬁcant amount of
energy, its power output varies with weather conditions, making it diﬃcult to match power
supply and demand [5]. Converting surplus oﬀshore wind into hydrogen through electrolysis is
widely seen as a promising solution. Hydrogen can be stored and later used to provide energy
services, thereby improving system ﬂexibility and supporting decarbonization [6].
1.2
Oﬀshore wind-to-hydrogen systems
Oﬀshore Wind-to-Hydrogen System (OWHS) can be implemented in several ways, depending
on where the electrolysis process is located relative to the wind turbines. As illustrated in
Figure 1.2, the three primary conﬁgurations are:

1. Onshore electrolysis
Power is transmitted via cable from the oﬀshore wind farm to an onshore facility where
hydrogen is produced.
2. Centralized oﬀshore electrolysis
Electrolyzers are installed at an oﬀshore substation, converting power to hydrogen before
it is transported to shore via pipeline or ship.
3. Distributed oﬀshore electrolysis (near-turbine)
Smaller electrolyzer units are integrated directly with individual wind turbines or small
clusters, producing hydrogen locally.
Figure 1.2: Three diﬀerent oﬀshore wind-to-hydrogen system conﬁgurations. From top to
bottom: onshore electrolysis, centralized oﬀshore electrolysis, electrolysis near turbine [7].
The choice of conﬁguration involves trade-oﬀs between capital expenses (CAPEX), operational
expenses (OPEX), transmission losses, and maintenance logistics. This study does not focus
on selecting or optimizing one speciﬁc conﬁguration. Instead, its goal is to develop a Model
Predictive Control (MPC)-based optimal control framework for an OWHS. While the location
of the electrolyzers (onshore vs. oﬀshore) inﬂuences infrastructure and transport costs, it does
not inﬂuence the electrochemical and thermal characteristics that this study investigates. A
detailed analysis of the trade-oﬀs between diﬀerent conﬁgurations, including cost modeling
and system design for each scenario, can be found in other studies, such as the study done by
Travaglini et al. [8].
1.3
Research gap
The integration of electrolyzers into renewable energy systems faces several challenges. Current
studies show that electrolyzers can help provide grid stability and power frequency control, but
there are still uncertainties in how and how well they can do so in practice. For example, Cam-
mann et al. [9] found that ALK electrolyzers could participate in frequency containment reserve
markets but showed limitations caused by minimum load requirements. Similarly, Agredano-
Torres et al. [10] showed that combining ALK and PEM electrolyzers could improve ﬂexibility
for frequency regulation, but the lack of coordinated and economically optimized control re-
mains a challenge. Another issue lies in the quality of available electrolyzer simulation models.
Many models are either too simple -neglecting eﬃciency variations, electrolyzer ramping limits,
and thermal eﬀects- or too complex, using non-linear rather than linear models, which are
2

more accurate, but take more time to run. Qazi et al. [6] shows that current models often do
not couple electricity, heat, and hydrogen, making them less useful for control design. Huang
et al. [11] developed a Power-to-Hydrogen (P2H2) model that integrates electricity, heat, and
hydrogen dynamics, but its non-linear calculations make real-time use diﬃcult. To address
computational limits, Huang et al. [12] later proposed an improved MPC for hydrogen-based
microgrids, reducing computation time by 98%, though still using simpliﬁed assumptions about
(ﬁxed) eﬃciency. Khajeh et al. [13] also showed the value of robust optimization for wind-
hydrogen systems, but their linearized electrolyzer model simpliﬁed eﬃciency and degradation
eﬀects.
Previous electrolyzer system optimization studies are summarized in Table 1.1. Some studies
have covered degradation or temperature eﬀects in electrolyzers, which make a model more re-
alistic, but none combine these diﬀerent factors into an MPC-based optimal control framework
that provides a cost-eﬀective, stable, and ﬂexible control of OWHS.
In the subsequent chapters, the MPC-based optimal control framework will be referred to
simply as the "optimization framework".

While these studies make important contributions, they focus on a single aspect of system
design -such as degradation modeling or electrolyzer heat recovery. Furthermore, they study a
conﬁguration with either an ALK electrolyzer or a PEM electrolyzer, rather than a combined
conﬁguration with both ALK and PEM electrolyzers.
In the work of Miao [15], a ALK/PEM conﬁguration is used, but the optimization study focuses
on ﬁnding the optimal ALK and PEM capacity ratio, rather than implementing MPC. Huang
et al. [11] included detailed thermal coupling and made the model more realistic by including
eﬃciency modeling, however, no degradation and only an ALK electrolyzer is considered. The
study by Zheng et al. [14] does include degradation, but relies on more simple rule-based
control strategies and focuses exclusively on ALK electrolyzers. The work of Lu et al. [16]
concentrates solely on degradation modeling for PEM electrolyzers, not capturing aspects of
system design and the behaviour of a hybrid conﬁguration. The multi-objective approach in
Lei et al. [17] and Miao [15] is valuable for system design or long-term system management,
but not for short-term power scheduling.
These ﬁndings highlight a gap in research: there is no practical optimization framework that
is both realistic enough to represent real system behavior and computationally fast enough

for real-time predictive control. This limits the design of strategies that can provide reliable,

1.4
Research objectives and contributions
To further close this gap in research, this study proposes a realistic, ﬂexible, stable, and cost-
eﬀective optimization framework for an OWHS that integrates a BESS with both ALK and PEM
electrolyzers, incorporating the eﬀects of changing eﬃciency, thermal dynamics, heat recovery,
electrolyzer standby power requirements and degradation. The MPC uses a rolling horizon to
continuously optimize system operation under ﬂuctuating wind conditions and electricity prices,
with the objective of minimizing the total operational costs, while taking into consideration
the mentioned system dynamics. The central research question of this study is:
How can model-based optimal control of water electrolyzers support cost-eﬀective, stable,
and ﬂexible oﬀshore wind and hydrogen integration?
1.5
Research scope
The scope of this study covers an energy system that consists of an electrolyzer system (both
ALK and PEM) and a BESS. The Anholt Oﬀshore Wind Farm provides the main input power,
which can be supported by grid power when necessary. The system output is produced hydrogen.
The windfarm in itself, is treated as a black box and not included in the scope. This also applies
to the storage and potential use of the produced hydrogen. The cooling of the electrolyzer
system is included in the scope, but like the wind power curtailment, the potential utilization
of the waste heat is not considered. The energy system overview and the study’s scope is
shown in Figure 1.3, where the study’s scope is illustrated with the dashed box.

1.6
Thesis structure
Chapter 2: Methodology describes the computational approach used in this study to address
the research question. It describes the software and hardware environment and describes the
choice of a Mixed Integer Linear Program (MILP) solver, followed by a description of the
pre-processing and visualization of raw wind data. Furthermore, it describes the adaptation
and scaling of existing models for ALK and PEM electrolyzers, the BESS, and the MPC-based
optimal control framework. It presents the mathematical formulations for both the ALK and
PEM electrolyzers, detailing how their cell voltage, current, and eﬃciency are calculated based
on empirical data and adaptations from previous studies. Key aspects covered include the
calculation of electrolyzer eﬃciency using the Thermo-Neutral Voltage (TNV), the integration
of a thermal model to manage electrolyzer temperature, the rolling-horizon strategy of the
MPC, and the formulation of the MILP optimization problem. Finally, it explains how the
optimization framework integrates a penalty cost for power changes to simulate and limit
electrolyzer degradation.
Chapter 3: Results & Discussion discusses the results of the case study that was performed
on the optimization framework. It presents and analyzes the simulation results across twelve
unique scenarios. These scenarios describe combinations of ALK-only, PEM-only, and hybrid
ALK+PEM conﬁgurations, each tested with and without a BESS, under both low-wind (wind-
deﬁcit) and high-wind (wind-rich) conditions. The chapter uses ﬁgures and tables to illustrate
how the MPC schedules power, manages the BESS State Of Charge (SOC), controls elec-
trolyzer temperature, and responds to electricity prices. Furthermore, detailed comparison of
costs and energy usage across all scenarios is presented and discussed. Finally, the sources of
uncertainty that could have aﬀected the results are discussed.
Chapter 4: Conclusions and future work summarizes the ﬁndings of this study and describes
work that could be done in future research.
Finally, the Appendices, providing supplementary information, can be found at the end of the
thesis.

To limit the solving time of the optimization framework, it was decided to use a MILP solver.
Other methods, like non-linear optimization, were considered but rejected because they are
more complicated and take longer to solve, making them less suitable for real-time use. Using
a MILP solver was suitable because it was fast enough for real-time control, even though it
meant simplifying some of the physics. The trade-oﬀwas worth it because the core behavior
of the electrolyzers was still captured accurately.
As the ﬁrst step of this study, a literature review was carried out to identify the gap in research.
After identifying the research gap and establishing objectives and scope of this study, the raw
wind power data from the Anholt oﬀshore wind farm was processed and analyzed (described in
more detail in Section 2.2). After processing and analyzing the data and identifying relevant
trends in the input wind power, an ALK electrolyzer model, a PEM electrolyzer model, a BESS
model and a MPC were adapted to be integrated in an optimization framework.
2.2
Raw Data Analysis
The dataset used in this study originates from the SCADA system of the Anholt oﬀshore
wind farm. It spans the year 2014 and contains 10-minute resolution operational data for
111 Siemens 3.6 MW wind turbines. More information about the windfarm can be found in
Appendix A.
2.2.1
Pre-processing
The data was cleaned up and converted to the format used in Python. Columns and rows
with identical values or irrelevant names (StationId, GridId, etc.) were removed. Columns
and rows with missing values were inspected. Completely empty columns were removed. For
the remaining data, occasional missing values were left untreated, as they had no signiﬁcant
impact on the overall dataset. This reduced unnecessary data and kept only relevant data for
a more accurate analysis.

Power Duration Distribution
Figure 2.4 shows the power duration distribution of the Anholt windfarm in 2014. The curve

would not be fully utilized for more than one third of the year. This means an additional energy
buﬀer such as a BESS, with a capacity depending on the electrolyzer capacity, is required to
cover periods of insuﬃcient power and to support stable operation.

A total electrolyzer system capacity of 300 MW was studied to analyze system behavior under

2.3
Electrolyzer Modelling
To study the real-time control and optimization of the OWHS, two separate models were
developed, one for the PEM electrolyzer and another for the ALK electrolyzer. These models
are used by the MPC inside the optimization framework, where they are used to control
electrolyzer power, temperature, and eﬃciency.
The main models used in this study are based on several published works. The overall MPC
structure along with the ALK electrolyzer model was adapted from the work of Huang et al. [11],
which itself built on Ulleberg [18]s work on electrolyzers. The PEM electrolyzer model was
adapted from Miao [15]. The eﬃciency calculation done by Miao [15] was changed, because
the original calculation calculated the Faradaic eﬃciency, whereas in this study the approach
of Huang et al. [11] was adapted by calculating the TNV eﬃciency.

2.4
Optimization Framework: Rolling-Horizon MPC using MILP
The core of the optimization framework is the Model Predictive Control (MPC), adapted from
the work of Huang et al. [11].
The original optimization framework of Huang et al. [11]
included only an ALK electrolyzer.
In this study, a PEM electrolyzer model was included.
Huang et al. [11] also included a fuel cell and hydrogen storage model. These were excluded in
this study to reduce complexity of the optimization framework and to focus on the behaviour
of the electrolyzer and BESS combination speciﬁcally.
The MPC works on an hourly basis, using a rolling horizon strategy to look ahead at weather
forecast data and the Day Ahead Electricity Price (DAEP). With this information, it calculates
an optimal 24-hour control schedule for distributing available wind power between the ALK
and PEM electrolyzers, the BESS, the required cooling power and -if necessary- curtailment.
The MPC calculates the optimal schedule by solving an optimization problem (detailed in
Section 2.4.2). The MPC weighs positive and negative incentives in the form of revenue
and costs (listed in Table 2.2), essentially choosing between diﬀerent pathways to ﬁnd the
pathway with the lowest system operation costs. These pathways can be described as follows:
• Hydrogen Production (Revenue): The most desirable use of the wind power is direct
conversion to hydrogen via the electrolyzers.
Selling the produced hydrogen generates
revenue, stimulating the MPC to maximize production. This is constrained by maximum
hydrogen production rates and electrolyzer temperature and power ramping limits.
• Battery Storage (BESS) (Cyclic Degradation Cost): When wind power exceeds avail-
able electrolyzer capacity, excess energy can be stored in the BESS. While storage involves a
16
Optimal operation of oﬀshore wind-to-hydrogen systems
==PAGE:24==
cost (related to battery degradation), it is much lower than the penalty cost for curtailment.
Thus, the model prefers storage over curtailment.
• Curtailment (Penalty Cost): If excess energy cannot be utilized by the electrolyzers or the
BESS, it is curtailed (wasted). A high penalty cost is assigned to curtailment, stimulating
the MPC to prevent this.
2.4.1
Rolling Horizon
After the MPC has calculated the 24-hour control schedule, only the ﬁrst hour of the control
schedule is executed. In the next hour, the model "rolls" forward by one time-step, discards the
previous control schedule, and calculates a new 24-hour control schedule based on updated
weather forecasts and the system’s current state (e.g., battery SOC, battery (dis)charging
limits and electrolyzer temperature and power ramping limits).
This creates a continuous
feedback loop, allowing the system to adapt quickly to changing conditions without needing
to calculate an entire long-term schedule at once. This is crucial because calculating for a long
horizon like a year would take too long for real-time control. Furthermore, such a long-term
schedule would quickly become inaccurate as forecasts and conditions change.
2.4.2
MILP Optimization Problem
The MPC calculates the optimal schedule by solving an optimization problem that is formulated
as a Multi Integer Linear Program (MILP). Like the name suggests, it is a mathematical
problem that describes a system of linear equations in the form of constraints and a single or
multiple objective functions, that can either contain integer and non-integer values (hence the
term "mixed")[21].
Multi Integer Linear Programming is a powerful optimization method, capable of solving prob-
lems that involve both continuous variables (like power levels or temperatures) and discrete
(integer or binary) variables (like on/oﬀstates). It does this by formulating the problem as a
set of linear equations and inequalities, making it suitable for complex energy systems where
decisions must be made under (many) constraints, such as EV charging systems[22] or, like in
this study, optimal control of an OWHS.
2.4.3
Electrolyzer Degradation Modelling
As described by Miao [15], an electrolyzer needs a minimum amount of power to keep it in
a standby state, from where it can ramp up its hydrogen production quickly. This way, an
electrolyzer is more ﬂexible to handle power ﬂuctuations. In the case of a wind powered energy
system, or any renewable energy system for that matter, there are moments where there is
little to no power production. To provide the minimum electrolyzer power required during such
moments, an energy storage system such as a BESS is needed (as discussed in Chapter 3).
The BESS not only improves stability in providing the standby power of electrolyzers, it also
reduces the frequency of electrolyzer power changes, or "power ramping". Like many other
mechanical and electrical components, frequent power changes in electrolyzers are undesirable
because of degradation eﬀects. To include the eﬀect of electrolyzer degradation in this study, a
power change penalty cost was introduced. This was simulated in the optimization framework
by applying a penalty cost for every change in electrolyzer power (both increases and decreases)
(deﬁned by Equation 2.58). This penalty was applied to both the ALK and PEM electrolyzers.
Since ALK electrolyzers are more sensitive to power ramping, their penalty cost per MWh (since
the time-steps are in hours) was set higher than that of the PEM electrolyzer. The penalty
costs are set to 100 e/MWh and 20 e/MWh for the ALK and PEM electrolyzer respectively
(as shown in Table 2.2). The power change penalties formed a negative incentive for the
MPC, which means that it tried to minimize its eﬀect on the total operational cost. This
eﬀect is discussed in Chapter 3.

Assumptions Made
To develop an optimization framework that is both practical and realistic for real-world control
of an OWHS, some simpliﬁcations were necessary given the scope and timeframe of this
research. These simpliﬁcations are discussed below:
1. Exclusion of hydrogen storage model: The original hydrogen storage model used by
Huang et al. [11] was excluded from the optimization framework to simplify the overall
complexity and shift the primary focus to the P2H2 system components and their operational
dynamics. The hydrogen storage logic remains in the optimization framework for potential
future re-implementation.
2. Exclusion of recovered heat selling model: The model used by Huang et al. [11] for
selling recovered heat was excluded to reduce complexity and maintain focus on the core
P2H2 system analysis. The model for selling recovered heat remains in the optimization
framework for potential future re-implementation.

400 MW maximum capacity. This creates periods where the electrolyzer system can run at

full capacity, aswell as power deﬁcit periods that require energy management. Note that

4. Equal temperature parameters for ALK and PEM electrolyzers: Both electrolyzers’
temperature range and initial temperature are set equal to ALK speciﬁcations. This simpli-

and required cooling power to be calculated by combining the stacks, heat capacity, and

In reality, ALK and PEM electrolyzers would operate at diﬀerent temperatures, requiring
separate calculations.
5. BESS capacity scaled from original ALK: The BESS capacity is calculated by scaling
the original ALK parameter using the ALK scale factor. This same BESS capacity is applied
across all scenarios that include a BESS for comparability.
6. 1:1 ALK:PEM ratio in hybrid scenarios (baseline): A 1:1 ratio between ALK and PEM
electrolyzers is used as the baseline for hybrid conﬁguration analysis. While a 3:1 ratio
was also tested, the 1:1 ratio serves as the primary baseline for detailed presentation and
discussion due to time constraints in the study.
7. Initial electrolyzer temperature at average of min/max range: The initial electrolyzer
temperature is assumed to be the midpoint between minimum and maximum operational
temperatures to provide a reasonable starting baseline for simulation analysis.
8. Initial BESS SOC between min/max limits: The initial SOC for the BESS is assumed
to be the midpoint between minimum and maximum SOC to provide a starting point for
battery analysis across all scenarios.
9. Equal hydrogen production rate for ALK and PEM at 1:1 capacity ratio: Since
both electrolyzers are assumed to have equal capacity in the 1:1 ratio conﬁguration, their
hydrogen production rates are also assumed to be equal. This means the PEM electrolyzer’s
production rate is set to match the ALK’s speciﬁcations, consistent with the temperature
assumption.
10. PEM lifetime, operational cost, and power change penalty derived from ALK pa-
rameters multiplied by factors: These parameters for the PEM electrolyzer are estimated
by multiplying the corresponding ALK parameters by literature-based scaling factors, as
detailed in Table 2.2. This approach provides an estimation when direct PEM parameters
are less documented.

3.2
The Eﬀect of the Power Change Penalty (Limitation of
Degradation)
As a result of the power change penalty, the MPC is stimulated to keep the electrolyzer power
as constant as possible. As described in Chapter 2, the penalty for the ALK electrolyzer is
higher than the penalty for the PEM, stimulating the MPC to prioritize stable operation of the
ALK electrolyzer.
The stabilizing eﬀect on electrolyzer power is mostly visible when there is ﬂuctuating wind
power and therefore ﬂuctuating electrolyzer power, like in the wind-deﬁcit scenario. However,
since the power change penalties are of a higher magnitude than the peak power in the wind
deﬁcit scenario, the eﬀect is only visible at the higher wind power levels, like those which occur
in the wind-rich scenario. To illustrate the penalty eﬀect, a sensitivity analysis was performed
on the wind-deﬁcit day by increasing the input wind power using a multiplication factor of 1.0,
3.0 and 5.0 . The DAEP is not changed and is therefore presented as the single blue line as
previously. The results of the sensitivity analysis are presented in Figure 3.13. The ﬁgure
contains two graphs: the upper graph displays the total system power balance as seen for the
previous scenarios. The three wind power levels 1.0, 3.0 and 5.0 are presented by the dotted
line, dotted line with dot marker and dotted line with triangle marker respectively.
The lower graph speciﬁcally shows the power levels of the individual ALK and PEM electrolyz-
ers. The results show that when the wind power is increased to 5 times as much and upwards,
the MPC controls the ALK electrolyzer so it starts running at constant power. The MPC
also increases BESS charging (red bars) to use battery power strategically later on. It can be
observed that initially the power of the ALK electrolyzer increases, before that of the PEM elec-
trolyzer. The share of the PEM electrolyzer only starts to increase after the maximum capacity
of the ALK electrolyzer is reached. When the maximum capacity of the ALK electrolyzer is

reached, the MPC uses the PEM electrolyzer to handle the ﬂuctuations in the power (the lines
with triangle markers). This behaviour is also reﬂected by the bars for the electrolyzer powers
(ALK and PEM), where the height diﬀerence of the light grey bars of the ALK electrolyzer
decreases, while the height diﬀerence of the dark grey bars of the PEM increases.

Once the wind power factor reaches 5.0x, the preference of the MPC for stability becomes
clear: the ALK electrolyzer is pushed to its maximum capacity and held there as much as
possible, running stable and minimizing its power changes. The remaining, ﬂuctuating power
is assigned to be handle by the PEM electrolyzer, which is more tolerant of power changes
due to its characteristics, stimulated by its lower power change penalty. This result conﬁrms
that the MPC strategically uses the more ﬂexible PEM electrolyzer to handle changes in wind
power, thereby protecting the ALK electrolyzer from degradation and minimizing the total
operational cost.
3.3
The Eﬀect of the Battery Energy Storage System (BESS)
The results show that the addition of a BESS improves the operational ﬂexibility and cost-
eﬀectiveness of the OWHS. As demonstrated in scenarios 1, 2, and 3 (with BESS) compared
to scenarios 4, 5, and 6 (without BESS), the battery acts as a buﬀer that smooths out power
34
Optimal operation of oﬀshore wind-to-hydrogen systems
==PAGE:42==
ﬂuctuations from the wind source. In wind-deﬁcit conditions, the BESS supplies standby power
to the electrolyzers, reducing or eliminating the need to purchase expensive grid electricity. This
is particularly beneﬁcial for the ALK system, which has a higher standby power requirement.
During wind-rich periods, the BESS stores surplus energy that would otherwise be curtailed,
allowing it to be used later to support hydrogen production during low-wind periods. This
strategic storage reduces overall curtailment costs and enables more consistent electrolyzer
operation, thereby minimizing degradation penalties associated with frequent power changes.
While the BESS introduces its own utilization cost, the net result is a reduction in total
operational expenses, as shown in Section 3.4.

providing operational ﬂexibility.
The 3:1 ratio better balances the trade-oﬀbetween the lower operational cost and preference
for stable power of the ALK electrolyzer and the more expensive, but more ﬂexible the PEM
electrolyzer. By scheduling more power to the cheaper ALK electrolyzer while using the PEM
electrolyzer for handling ﬂuctuating power situations, the system achieves higher revenue and
lower costs, resulting in a higher net total.
In terms of degradation, the 3:1 ratio reduces the amount power changes, which is reﬂected
by the lower power change penalty cost result from the MPC. For the wind-deﬁcit conditions
this is e1,268 with the 3:1 ratio vs. e1,337 with the 1:1 ratio. For the wind-rich conditions
this is e0 with the 3:1 ratio vs. e992 with the 1:1 ratio. The higher capacity of the ALK
electrolyzer means it can handle more of the baseline load, while the PEM handles the power
ﬂuctuations, aligning with the diﬀerent degradation characteristics of these technologies.
3.6
Speed of the Optimization Framework
The optimization framework shows good computational eﬃciency, with a total execution time
of just 18.68 seconds for the most demanding scenario. This speed is more than suﬃcient for
real-time optimal control of an oﬀshore wind energy system, where decisions typically need to
be made on timescales of minutes or even hours to respond to changing wind conditions and
electricity prices. The ability to solve the MPC-MILP problem within this timeframe ensures
that the controller is computationally fast enough for real-time predictive control of an OWHS.
3.7
Sources of uncertainty
Several design choices were made in this study, which introduce some level of uncertainty into
the results. The design choices and their potential eﬀects on the results are discussed below:
1. Limited wind power data length: the optimization framework relies on data from the
Anholt wind farm for the year 2014. While this data is of high quality, it represents only
one location and one year. Weather patterns can vary from year to year and from site to
site. A single year of data may not capture all possible scenarios.
2. Misalignment of data periods: the periods of the wind power data and the DAEP data
used in this study are not aligned. The wind power data and the DAEP data are from the
year 2014 and 2018 respectively. The alignment of the data is no prerequisite for testing
the control of the OWHS by the MPC, but because of the misalignment, no conclusion
can be drawn about the relationship between wind power and electricity price. While in
practise, these factors are connected, where the price tends to be low when renewable power
production is high and vice versa.

3. Empirical electrolyzer data: Although the model calculates eﬃciency based on temper-
ature and power, it still uses simpliﬁed equations derived from published empirical data.
These equations might not perfectly match the behavior of every commercial electrolyzer
unit.
4. Limited simulation window: the simulation window of the MPC is 24 hours. Given the
input wind data which spans one year, the MPC could be tested for longer simulation
windows to analyze potential big eﬀects on the control strategy for the OWHS. By limiting
the window to 24 hours, only the MPC’s short-term controlling capability can be analyzed.
5. Sub-optimal time-step interval: the MPC currently operates with hourly time-steps,
while the input wind data is available at measurement intervals of 10 minutes. Therefore,
the MPC could be tested with a ﬁner, 10-minute resolution. Because the electricity price
input data is based on the DAEP -which is also hourly- a time-step interval of one hour
is fast enough to control the OWHS. However, as per October 2025, the DAEP of the
European electricity market moved from hourly to 15-minute intervals. This means that
the time-step interval in the optimization framework needs to be changed to work with
at least 15-min intervals to remain fast enough for real-time control. Fortunately, this is
relatively straightforward to implement in the optimization framework.
6. No technical hardware degradation model: the optimization framework in this study
includes a monetary penalty for power changes to simulate degradation, but it does not
model the actual physical wear-and-tear of the equipment over time. A more complex model
could track the technical aspects of the electrolyzer, such as lifetime and maintenance needs.
7. Fixed price assumptions: the proposed optimization framework uses ﬁxed prices for several
components, such as a ﬁxed hydrogen price and cost of curtailment. In reality, these prices
can change quickly and unpredictably. A more advanced economical model could track
actual prices live.
8. Idealized grid interaction: the model assumes the grid can always provide any needed
power instantly. In practice, grid constraints and connection fees can limit this ﬂexibility.

Chapter 4 Conclusions and Future Work
4.1
Conclusions
This study developed and tested a practical MPC-based optimal control framework for an
OWHS, to answer the research question of this study: How can model-based optimal control
of water electrolyzers support cost-eﬀective, stable, and ﬂexible oﬀshore wind and hydrogen
integration?. The main goal was to create a control model that is both realistic and fast
enough to be used for real-world controlling of an OWHS. The problem addressed was the lack
of existing models that could handle real-world factors like electrolyzer degradation, thermal
management, and hybrid operation of both ALK and PEM electrolyzers without becoming too
slow for practical use.
The optimization framework created in this study combines physics-based models for both
ALK and PEM electrolyzers with a BESS. The framework was tested using real wind power
data from the Anholt Oﬀshore Wind Farm. The results from the case study show that this
approach can eﬀectively manage the systems operation to minimize costs while maximizing
hydrogen production under changing wind and electricity price conditions.
The simulations conﬁrm that including degradation penalties in the MPC is a suitable method
for controlling the power scheduling of the electrolyzers, resulting in a more stable operation
and therefore preventing degradation and lowering operational cost. For example, the hybrid
ALK+PEM system with a BESS performed well by utilizing the strengths of each technology:
the PEMs fast response for short-term ﬂuctuations and the ALKs lower cost for continuous
production. The BESS was shown to be a crucial component, smoothing out power ﬂuctuations
and reducing grid purchases and curtailment.
This research demonstrates that a well-designed optimization framework can successfully bal-
ance the trade-oﬀs between operational cost, electrolyzer degradation, and hydrogen produc-
tion eﬃciency in oﬀshore wind-to-hydrogen systems. The implementation of power change
penalties proved eﬀective in stabilizing electrolyzer operation, with the 3:1 ALK:PEM ratio
conﬁguration showing better performance by utilizing the ALK electrolyzer’s cost advantage
for base load operation while using the PEM electrolyzer’s ﬂexibility to handle power ﬂuctua-
tions. The framework’s ability to reduce curtailment by 51.1% in wind-rich conditions while
maintaining electrolyzer stability represents an improvement toward practical implementation
of MPC-based optimal control for oﬀshore wind-to-hydrogen systems. The demonstrated 21-
44.7% improvement in net total results when using the optimal 3:1 ALK:PEM ratio indicates
the need for hybrid systems with an optimal balance between diﬀerent electrolyzer technologies.
The developed optimization framework in this study provides a starting point for real-world
implementation of OWHS control systems.
It demonstrates how model-based control can
operate an OWHS in a ﬂexible way while limiting degradation eﬀects and minimizing the
total operational costs, contributing to the broader goal of integrating renewable energy sys-
tems with hydrogen production, oﬀering a path toward more a ﬂexible and sustainable energy
infrastructure.
The optimization framework proposed in this work also provides a starting point for future
research in renewable energy integration. By successfully integrating multiple technical con-
straints into a computationally eﬃcient framework, it shows how complex energy systems can
be optimized without compromising accuracy. The approach of using monetary penalties to

simulate technical degradation eﬀects oﬀers a simple yet powerful and practical solution to a
common challenge in energy system modeling.
As oﬀshore wind capacity continues to expand globally, and hydrogen prices are expected to
decrease, the need for power-to-hydrogen conversion systems will only increase. This research
provides a practical optimization framework for a cost-eﬀective, stable, and ﬂexible control of
oﬀshore wind-to-hydrogen systems that can contribute to the energy transition. The presented
balance between technical accuracy and computational eﬃciency provides a step toward to the
practical implementation of these systems at commercial scale.
4.2
Future Work
While this study provides a solid foundation for realistic, real-time control of the OWHS,
there are several areas for future research to make the model even more useful for real-world
applications.
1. Exclusion of fuel cell and hydrogen storage models: as stated in Section 2.6, the fuel
cell and the hydrogen storage models are excluded from this study. Re-implementing these
models, the optimization framework could be made more realistic and usable for real-world
use. The fuel cell provides the option to produce electricity from the hydrogen produced
in previous time steps. Electricity produced by the fuel cell could lower the amount of
electricity that is bought from the grid. The fuel cell, combined with the hydrogen storage,
could also mean that a lower BESS capacity is needed.
2. Exclusion of heat selling mechanic: as stated in Section 2.6, the recovered heat selling
mechanic is excluded from this study. The thermal eﬀects of the electrolyzers are included,
but the potential use of the recovered heat as a result of the cooling is not considered.
Originally, Huang et al. [11] modelled an additional revenue stream by selling the recovered
heat to a district heating operator. This mechanic could be re-introduced to provide another
revenue stream next to the selling of produced hydrogen, therefore lowering the operational
costs.
3. To make the MPC-based optimal control framework relevant for use with the new DAEP
interval resolution introduced in Europe in October 2025, the time-step interval resolution
should be converted from an hourly resolution to at least 15-min resolution.
4. The model was tested with one year of wind power data from one location, while the periods
of the wind power and price data are not aligned. To make the results more general, future
studies could test the MPC with data from diﬀerent wind farms, while making sure to align
the periods of the data sets. Furthermore, the long-term (>24h) controlling capability of
the MPC can be studied to further test the model’s ﬂexibility and robustness.
5. Future studies could include a separate thermal model for the PEM electrolyzer to make
the heat generation and cooling power calculations more realistic.
6. Future studies could investigate the economic performance of the optimization framework,
as this was not included in the scope of this study.
7. The next step after simulation is to test the MPC on a real-world system. This would help
verify how well the model performs in a real environment.

Structure of ALK and PEM Electrolyzers
The ALK and PEM electrolyzers diﬀer mainly in how they handle ion transport and gas sepa-

ALK Electrolyzers
ALK electrolyzers are of simpler nature and lack the need of expensive metals for their con-
struction. This makes them cheaper to build, though they tend to be bulkier[29]. In ALK
systems, both electrodes are placed in a liquid electrolyte -usually Potassium Hydroxide (KOH)-
and are separated by a porous diaphragm that lets (OH−) ions pass while keeping hydrogen
and oxygen apart.
