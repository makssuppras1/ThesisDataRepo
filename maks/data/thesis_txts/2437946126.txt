ABSTRACT
S
oil bacterial distribution has been researched in a lot of studies based on the modern mi-
croscope technologies. Learning of soil bacterial distribution patterns can be helpful to
gain understand on how microbes in soil interact. Often thin section method is used to
study how bacteria distribute in the soil, but the limitation of this method is that the conditions
of soil are not easy to control. In this study, the soil samples were incubated on a modiﬁed porous
surface model and observed directly after few days incubation using ﬂuorescence microscopy. In-
oculation on different size of quartz sands with amended soil was also conducted to investigate
the effect of varying soil texture. Different conditions were applied to the samples to study the
relationship between soil bacterial distribution and environmental conditions. The distribution
patterns including single cells and colonies were analyzed quantitatively and qualitatively.
The observation of samples directly on the soil surface is possible but difﬁcult especially on
the coarse sands because the soil surface is rough, reducing area in focus. The bacteria clearly
occur at larger density when more nutrients and dryer conditions were applied.The bacteria and
fungi on soil surface were observed with the ﬂuorescent microscope after few days of incubation.
The hot spots of bacteria and colonies are very commonly seen in natural soil. The soil bacteria
seems have large dependence on the local distribution of organic nutrients which resemble roots
or dead insects, since much more cells can be observed nearby these unknown materials.
The population density of bacteria on both soil and inoculated sands had a decrease trend during
the incubation. Possibly because speciﬁc nutrients in soil were eliminated during the incubation
or the predating of protozoans. Most detected cells have their nearest neighbour within 100mum
and about half of them occurred with in distance of 20mum. The same was true for inoculated
quartz sands even there were much less cells on the inoculated sands. The distances between
cells tend to be larger in wet conditions than in dry conditions. No clear difference of bacteria
population on different sizes of quartz sands was observed.The distances between cells are gen-
erally large on the coarse sands, but the effect of different grain sizes on the distances seems
smaller than the effect caused by hydration condition. On both kind of sands, there were much
larger distance detected under the wet conditions than dry conditions. Colony coverage on soil
was always higher on dry conditions when there is no external nutrients applied, which is rea-
sonable as the dry conditions limit the spread of colonies.

INTRODUCTION
S
oil is one of the most important components for terrestrial life on earth and provides
living space for many microorganisms.Soil microbiology has been termed as the "Last
true scientiﬁc frontier on earth" (Wallenstein, 2007) since it has enormous unknown rel-
evance to ecosystem functioning. Soil microbial communities are among the most diverse and
thus remian poorly understood. For example, microbial cell density can reach 1010cells per gram
of soil with estimated diversity of 4×103 to 5×104 (Roesch et al., 2007).The microbial process in
the soil have been found important to the whole ecosystem, for example, the carbon mineraliza-
tion, nitriﬁcation and denitriﬁcation processes (Raynaud and Nunan, 2014).Those activities may
involve more than one species by cooperation and interactions. Attempts have been made to ﬁnd
the links between microbial diversity and functional activities using sequencing techniques (Wal-
lenstein, 2007), but the experiments didn’t show a strong relationship between them (Raynaud
and Nunan, 2014).Even though the study of Soil microbiology has undergone a big revolution
during the last century thanks to the development of molecular techniques, understanding of
soil microbiology communities is still limited (Wallenstein, 2007).
The interaction between species is highly dependent on how the cells distribute in the soil.
It is essential for understanding soil microbiology activities, such as mutualistic and compet-
itive interactions. It has been demonstrated that spatial separation of cells can contribute to
maintaining the bacterial diversity (Dechesne et al., 2008b) and that the distance between con-
stituent members can determine the stability of bacterial communities (Kim et al., 2008). The
spatial distribution inﬂuence the horizontal gene transfer between cells which is thought to be
important for bacterial evolution(Wallenstein, 2007). Therefore, studying the spatial distribu-
tion of cells is important for understanding relationships between microbial communities and

soil microbial activity.
This study aims at visualize and analyze soil microbial distribution in natural soil. The nat-
urally occurring soil microbial population was cultivated in porous media using a novel experi-
mental set up based on the porous surface model (PSM)(Dechesne et al., 2008a). The inﬂuence
of different conditions like hydration, nutrients and grain size were tested. Cell densities and
distances between cells or colonies were studied to discuss the possible relationships between
spatial distribution under different conditions.
1.1
Overview of Soil Microbiology
Soil microbiology is the study of microorganisms in soil and their functions. Soils are extremely
diverse habitats inhabiting bacteria, archaea and fungi. 1 g of soil can contain 104 −105 species.
The species are very import for soil environmental possesses and regulating nutrient cycling and
even soil physical properties.The microorganisms in soil can be classiﬁed as bacteria, archea,
fungi, algae and protozoa. The functions of soil microorganisms were ignored in ecosystem anal-
ysis eventhough those microorganisms including bacteria and archea are believed to play a very
important in maintaining the ecosystem functioning and sustainability, for example, decompose

Our knowledge about the soil microbes and their activities is still very limited. The main bar-

cannot grow in the environment we provide in the lab and they may need to survive with their
cooperators, so it is difﬁcult to isolate species from soil. It has been proven that one gram of
soil can contain 10 billion prokaryotes, 104 species of which less than 1% has been known. Most
of them are not culturable on agar plates, it has been estimated that the plates counts can be
100 to 1000 fold lower(Wallenstein, 2007) then the actual cell density in soil. It is important to
know the soil microbe population since we can have a concept about how strong the soil bacteria
functional activities can contribute to the ecosystem balance. Besides, the soil bacteria diversity
is also important to know since we can have an idea about how soil bacteria function to ecosys-
tem processes. Loreau et al developed a model which linked plants and bacterial decomposer
through cycling of limited nutrients (Loreau, 2001). Their model showed that the bacteria is a
key parameter which controls the ecosystem processes.

species is less than that in the normal soil.
1.1.2
Soil Microbial Activities and Interactions
.
Even though our knowledge about the functional activities is little, soil microbes are considered
to be essential for ecosystem stability and productivity (Nannipieri et al., 2003). Soil microor-
ganisms provide the soil fertility by degrading the dead organisms to nutritious compounds and
drives the C,N,P and S cycle in soil. The soil bacteria also actively participate the nitrogen cycle
in the ecosystem by ﬁxing nitrogen and doing denitriﬁcation, which provides sustainable and
available nitrogen for plants. 80%-90% reactions in soil are mediated by microbes(Nannipieri
et al., 2003).

can can exchange gene with bacteria and even with degraded plants (Wöstemeyer et al., 1997).
Understanding the spatial distribution of soil microorganisms are also important for developing
understanding on the how often the gene transfer process happen in the soil.

1.2.2
Soil Water and Mass Transport
Soil water is subject to several force ﬁelds,the combination of all ﬁelds decides the water state
and also the water availability for soil microorganisms.The forces which help to hold water cap-
illary forces and absorptive forces, while the force which drain soils is gravitational forces. The
combined effect of capillary forces and absorptive forces result in matric potential(ψ). In soil, wa-
ter ﬂows in direction of potential energy and usually the total water potential in soil is negative
due to negative matric potentials. The water potential leads the continuing water and solutes
to microorganisms through the passes and also hold water and nutrients in soil. When the soils
are not saturated, the matric potential is always negative and change with water content. The
relationship of water content and matric potential was ﬁtted to several models, for example van
Genuchten model(Van Genuchten, 1980). Fitting the model requires different parameter when
the soil textures are different. The ﬁtted models for different soils are shown in the Figure1.3.

1.4
Objectives of the Study
The previous studies mainly described the soil bacteria distribution based on the electron micro-
scope techniques and thin section methods. Electron microscopes technique provides informative
images of pores and bacteria with high resolution. However, usually by electron microscope, the
sample sizes are usually very small, so it is very difﬁcult to have larger view to observe how
bacteria distribute in the soil, for example, how large are the hot spots in soil and how those hot
spot distributed in the soil. In addition to that, the soil samples have to be ﬁxed by resin or oth-
erwise treated embedding before electron microscope, so we cannot perform online observation
and change conditions.
In this study, the soil samples and different sizes of inoculated quartz sands were incubated
on the porous surface model which allow us to observe the the soil distribution under controlled
hydration and nutrients conditions. The observation was conducted with the lower magniﬁca-
tions so that we can have a clear view of how the colonies distribute in the soils. The main
questions we will try to answer is:
1. How hydration will inﬂuence the bacterial distribution?
Increasing hydration is expected to change the bacteria distribution since the bacteria may have
larger mobility when there is a thicker water ﬁlm on the soil. It may also change the bacteria
growth rate because water is vital for bacteria.
2. How nutrients inﬂuence the spatial distribution of the cells?
Increasing nutrients is expected to increase the cell density and may also increase the density
of colonies. The distances may become smaller between cells.
3. How grain size (texture) inﬂuence the spatial distribution?
The cells well be inoculated on the quartz sands with different sizes. Our hypothesis is that
there might be more growth on ﬁne sands because with larger matric potential it can hold more
water and nutrients, while on quartz sands, the cells might be more localize because the cells
are isolated on different grains.
Qualitative observations and quantitative image analysis were performed to describe the bac-
terial distribution in the soil samples. Hopefully, the results can be helpful for people to un-
derstand the soil bacteria distribution and can be used as reference for modeling soil bacteria
activities.

use in extraction, but in the study (Lindahl and Bakken, 1995) where they compared the ATP

Different soil bacteria extraction were also compared by (Calbrix et al., 2005).The paper showed

bacteria can be extracted from soil in our study.
The soil samples taken from the ﬁeld were spread evenly in an container and soil samples were

microscope.10 times dilution of original extraction was prepared for numeration. Cells were
stained by mixing with SYTO 9 and propidium iodide with the ﬁnal concentration of 10µM and

2.5.1
Cell Detection and Counting
Image analysis processes in python is shown in the Figure 2.5. The image processes used in the
study are mainly developed based on the python image processing library Scikit-image (van der
Walt et al., 2014). The image was loaded as numpy arrays in python and was normalized by max-
imum value and minimum value. We denoised images using wavelet ﬁlter (Chang et al., 2000),
(Donoho and Johnstone, 1994) after estimating the standard derivation of Gaussian noise from
the images. As the soil surface is not ﬂat enough, not all area in the image is in focus and the
cells which are in the blur region cannot be detected. To calculate the cell density in cell number
per area, the area which is out of focus was excluded. Blur detection was conducted using the
method developed by (Su et al., 2011) based on singular value decomposition. A threshold is then
applied to the resulting blur map using the Li-Threshold ﬁlter (Li and Lee, 1993a) and the area
in focus (yellow area in step 2 of Figure 2.5). Afterwards, the contrast was enhanced by rescaling
(stretching) intensity histogram. The stretching was conducted between 50%-98% and We did
thresholding test to decide the percentile range(See in Chapeter 3). The bright blobs are there-
fore become more distinguishable from the background. Blobs light intensity basically follow
Gaussian distribution and the derivative of Gaussian distribution-Laplacian of Gaussian distri-
bution were used to detected cells by blob_log function in Scikit-image. Detection of blobs was
based on Laplacian of the Gaussian (LoG) and standard derivation σ of the distribution implies

4.1
Main Conclusions
In this study, we used ﬂorescence microscopy to observe the spatial distribution patterns of cells
in soil by quantitative and qualitative methods. It is feasible to observe soil bacteria distribu-
tion directly under microscope with applied suction and nutrients by porous surface model, but
the observation can be changing because the surface of soil is usually rough, so it is difﬁcult
to include more area in focus especially in the higher magniﬁcations. The cells can look larger
under microscope and a lot of cells can be even observed in 4X magniﬁcations because of ﬂuores-
cent halo. In this study, we chose to count cells under 10X magniﬁcation since it is statistically
reliable when compared to counting results obtained from higher magniﬁcation because it can
sample more cells for statistical analysis. The soil bacteria density measured in the study in
natural soil was about 6.36×108, but the results must have underestimate the density because
the cells in soil were not fully extracted.The bacterial aggregates has some persistence to me-
chanical disturbances as there were plenty microbial aggregates in the extractions.
Bacterial distribution is diverse in patterns and it may depend on the different species of bacte-
ria and the interaction with soil environments. A uniform distribution of cells in wet conditions
is frequently observed and cells tend to group more under dry conditions. In addition to that,
the development of bacteria population rely on the local nutrients such as unknown organic ma-
terials and sometimes fungi can play a role of nutrients "bridge". Increasing the nutrients level
in the soil increased the bacterial population in 2 days, but the bacterial population experienced
a decrease in 4 days incubation. This is probably a result of run out of the vital nutrients in the
original soil or the excess growth of bacteria produced a lot toxic metabolites. It is also possible
that the predating of protozoans decreased the cell numbers. When without the excess nutrients,

The reason why we choose to do the experiment on both natural soil and inoculated sands is
because they give different insights. For example, there are some signiﬁcant phenomenons can
be observed on the natural soil samples because we can include more sample cells in the statistic.
The natural soil can provide the real environments with the real soil populations, but sometimes
there is less control because of soil heterogeneity. On inoculated quartz sands, we can control
more things because there is almost no heterogeneity of the nutrients and soil structures, and
what’s more, we can chose particular grain sizes. However, the cells seems don’t really like the
nutrients we provided in the lab, so they don’t signiﬁcantly grow during the incubation. There-
fore, we can not include as many cells as on the natural soil. Some species might not survive in
this artiﬁcial environment, so the composition of bacterial community may change. In addition
to that, the background ﬂuorescence also cause problems for segmenting images. As a result,
we shouldn’t fully trust any results alone but should combine them and see if there is certain
relationships we can ﬁnd.
4.2
Limitation of the Study and The possibility for further
improvement
This study gave some insights about how the cells distribute on soils.The research period was
quite short and it may needs more experiments and data to get more convincing conclusions,
for example, adding more different hydration or nutrients points to study how exactly distri-
bution changed when increasing the hydration or the nutrients by steps. In this study we only
observed the surface layer of soil, but there could be some difference to the inside of soil, because
the species in the surface may be different from inside the soil. Except for that, the difference
between soils may be large, we only have studied 2 kinds of soil in this study and we observed
very abundant hyphae on the grass land soil, which even made it difﬁcult to observe cells, while
on the other soil sample taken from garden, the hyphae was barely seen. The difference between
dry soils and wet soils could be also very large. There is too much unpredictable variance that
could occur in the soil and it might be difﬁcult to say what we got are general conclusions. To
minimize the variance between samples, we inoculated the soil bacteria on sands. However, the
inoculation with lab media must have selected species, so the distribution behavior may not be
the same as in the natural soil. We may also need to adjust nutrients to increase the growth, so
that we can have more sample numbers for data analysis.

to quickly remove the excess liquid. In natural soil, the disturbance of the soil by precipitation
is very common, so the disturbing by drop of stainning liquid can be considered as a natural
event. Apart from that, in order to obtain better signal, the high concentration stains were used
in the experiments, but the toxicity of stains especially SYTO 9 to soil bacteria is still unclear, so
repeated staining may bring more uncertainty when we try to track the sample with time series.
The toxicity of SYTO 9 to cells requires further study.
The biggest challenge in image acquisition is that we couldn’t get more area in focus in higher
magniﬁcation because the soil surface is rough even though we tried different methods to make
it as ﬂat as possible. When we tried to use the higher magniﬁcations, it was difﬁcult to includes
information in focus and also we lost a lot of information of surface characteristics, so we only
choose the 4X to 10X magniﬁcations. The thin section methods were that were frequently uti-
lized in other studies to provide ﬂat surfaces for observation, but we lose the control of the
hydration and nutrients. Nonetheless it could be quite valuable to compare the results between
the 2 kinds of methods. Thin section can give more information of cells which located inside of
soil pores even more when combined with electron microscopy..
In image processing, only the cells can be detected in 10X magniﬁcation with the pixel size
of 0.912µm was considered, but there might be a lot of smaller cells which weren’t included.
Studying of those cells in higher magniﬁcations requires ﬂatter surfaces. The distribution of
those cells might be different from the larger ones. In the colony detection, the colony detection
method by clustering the detected cells is not so accurate since it often cluster some cells which
are not enough close to be called colonies, which makes the colonies area larger than the real val-
ues. It would, however, be possible to post process the data based on minimum density required
for classiﬁcation as a colony. When we processed the image taken from the inoculated sands, the
strong backgrounds come from lower layers make it difﬁcult to segment the images by the light
intensity, so maybe some other stable particles which are not transparent can be considered as
options.
