Abstract
Oil and gas platforms contribute significantly to the total emissions of oil majors due to their standard
power supply by gas turbines. Equinor has highlighted the decarbonization of those platforms as part
of the solution to achieve net-zero emission by 2050. From various technologies able to perform such a
transition, three are selected for the analysis: offshore wind, wind-battery system, and carbon capture
and storage (CCS). This study aims to develop to assess each technology’s decarbonization potential.
The central question arises: which cost is acceptable to transition the current power supply of oil and
gas platforms towards a zero-emission system and which technology is most favorable in the prospect
of future energy trends?
Based on data from Equinor, research knowledge, and scientific literature review, a mathematical model
is developed to analyze the economic potential of offshore wind, wind-battery systems, and CCS. For
each technology, a scenario with an underlying operation strategy and financial model is developed.
Mathematical models are implemented in Python using linear optimization and minimize gas turbines’
operation, respectively, their emissions.
The results indicate that offshore wind and foremost a wind-battery-system is an economically feasible
solution depending on the specific use case. Offshore wind can achieve significant emission reduction,
and combined with battery storage, zero-emission is possible. Compared to CCS, the wind-battery tech-
nologies have additional value after the lifetime of platforms themselves. Extended utilization of wind
farms through an onshore grid connection or offshore electrolysis is in line with recent trends towards
a decarbonized energy sector and the hydrogen economy. In contrast, CCS has enormous potential to
reduce emissions, but the economic benefit neither the technical practicality is given. The gas turbines
are not carbon-intensive enough to support CO2 based EOR on-site or to justify the transport and
storage costs of the captured carbon.
The comparison of offshore wind and CCS represents a comprehensive overview of two completely dif-
ferent ways to decarbonize oil and gas platforms. Although offshore wind is more economical if directly
utilized for electricity production, this study shows how offshore wind can find its way into oil and gas
majors’ product portfolios.
Keywords: decarbonization, offshore wind, carbon capture and storage (CCS), oil platform electrifi-
cation, economic analysis

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Production platforms of oil and gas are commonly powered by gas turbines (GT). In Norway, the
extraction and processing of oil resulted in an emission contribution of about 26% of the total CO2
emissions reported in Applied Energy 2011.
[29] According to Wood Mackenzie, the GTs consume
around 5% of the wellhead production annually, reducing the sales volume of natural gas. [46] Although
the Norwegian oil production has already peaked around 2000 and is further declining, some platforms
are expected to operate for decades to come - the Gullfak platforms will operate until 2034 and Snorre
until 2040. [13]

The mitigation of CO2 emissions through the electrification of platforms with offshore wind farms is of
high interest and investigated in this report. On the one hand, wind power reduces or even avoids the
emissions completely. On the other hand, the installation of offshore wind farms follows a global trend
that is continuing to accelerate. According to IRENA, offshore wind capacity grows from 542 GW in
2018 to 5044 GW in 2050. [22] The trend is evident: as the installed capacity rises with every year, the
production costs fall rapidly.
Figure 1.2: Installed wind power capacity worldwide [23]
With rising taxes on hydrocarbon fuels (currently 550 NOK per tonne CO2 which is equivalent to e52),
decarbonization of oil and gas platforms has great potential to reduce costs. Therefore, companies like
the Norwegian energy provider Equinor have announced to reach net-zero emissions in 2050. [14] To-
gether with the offshore wind trend, the project Hywind Tampen is supporting their emission objectives.
Hywind Tampen connects a floating wind farm of 88 MW with Gullfak A, B, C, and Snorre A and B.
The Tampen area has a water depth of 300 to 350m meaning that fixed bottom or jacket foundations
are unsuitable for installing wind turbines. The project is estimated to reduce the CO2 emissions by
200 000 tons and NOx by 1000 tons per year, the wind farm covers around 35% of the annual load
of the surrounding platforms. [15] With a distance of 125 km [32] to shore, a grid connection is un-
favorable due to its costs. However, the offshore wind farms can be directly connected to the oil and
gas platforms to save fuel and reduce emissions. [25] Since the wind farm’s lifetime will exceed the
platform’s remaining operating years, the question arises of how to utilize the wind farm further. One

way is to install cables to connect and sell the electricity to shore or produce hydrogen by electrolysis.
If produced offshore, hydrogen is expected to yield profitability with a price of e5/kg in 2030. [10]
Figure 1.3: Tampen area on the Norwegian continental shelf [15]
The Hywind Tampen project created the initial idea for this thesis in collaboration with Equinor: The
potential of different technologies to achieve net-zero emissions of a representative platform with a given
load supplied by two GT is investigated. Since CCS also plays an indispensable role in the future in
order to limit global warming to less than 2°C, this report compares its emission reduction potential
as an alternative to the wind farm. Without CCS, only 26% of fossil fuel reserves can be consumed to
stay within the temperature limit, whereas with CCS, up to 37% can be utilized. Hence, CCS projects
gain momentum where geological storage sites are available, like in the North Sea with its abundant
depleted oil and gas reservoirs. [4]
The purpose of this report is to outline opportunities for offshore wind and CCS technologies to achieve
emission reductions up to net-zero emission. An offshore wind farm and a battery storage system for bal-
ancing the power is compared to the emerging application of CCS to capture the emitted CO2 of the GT.
The main contributions of this thesis are:
• Optimization of GT operation strategy of a reference case
• Micro-grid of a wind farm and battery storage to substitute the GT
• Model of a CCS application to cut emissions of the GT
• Economic evaluation of the micro-grid and CCS system in order to conclude comparable project
KPI such as NPV, IRR, PBP, LCOE

CHAPTER 2
Theoretical introduction
2.1
Methodology
The study is based on information from Equinor, researchers from DTU, and a literature review. The
development of mathematical and economic models is derived from similar research projects. The initial
input is a data set of a representative annual load provided by supervisor Shi You. The load of the
platform ranges from 0 to 35 MW. Furthermore, a characteristic annual wind speed of the Tampen
area is given to obtain the annual wind power profile. Appendix A shows an excerpt of the input data.
These data sets and the requirement to have 2 GT for a secure energy supply are the study’s starting
point. The reference case is subject to improvement by adding wind power, a wind-battery system

2.8 Carbon capture and storage (CCS)

2.8
Carbon capture and storage (CCS)
Various technologies exist in the young field of carbon capture and storage, ranging from absorption,
adsorption, and membrane separation technologies.
The absorption technologies are split into two
main areas: chemical absorption and physical absorption. [29] During the chemical absorption process,
the solvent reacts with the CO2 to form a chemical compound from which CO2 is recovered later.
Whereas in the physical absorption process, the CO2 is chemically inert and fully absorbed by the
solvent. Favorable requirements are fast reaction rate, high CO2 recovery by the solvent, low heat
release during the process, and no by-products. [33]
2.8.1
Chemical and physical absorption
Chemical absorption is based on an exothermic reaction of the gas stream containing the CO2 with the
sorbent (absorbs liquids or gases). Afterward, the reaction is reversed at higher temperatures to obtain
high concentrated CO2. The chemical absorption is well-suited for low partial pressures of the CO2
typical for flue gases of natural gas combustion, and the most common solvents are amine-based.
Amines originate from ammonia (NH3), and the hydrogen atoms are substituted to achieve primary,
secondary or tertiary amines depending on how many hydrogen atoms are replaced. The most widely
applied amine for capturing CO2 is monoethanolamine (MEA). This primary amine (CH2CH2OH = R
in the following equations) is a weak base that neutralizes and absorbs the acidic CO2.

2.8.2
Geological storage
Besides various technologies for capturing CO2, injecting it into permeable subsurface rock formation
is the only successful demonstrated technology on a commercial scale. Methods and tools available in
the oil and gas industry are with minor adjustments applicable for geological storage. Besides using
EOR, depleted oil and gas fields and saline aquifers (groundwater that cannot be used as clean water)
are identified as the main sites for geological storage. For permanently storing CO2 underground, it
is injected as fluid instead of gas because of its lower mobility as a super-critical fluid. Therefore, the
hydrostatic pressure must be above 7.38 MPa, typically achieved at a depth of more than 800m below
the surface. [33]
Figure 2.11: Subsurface and strati-graphic structure for geological storage of CO2 [33]
The capacity of a storage site depends on the trapping mechanism. Table 2.7 presents the available
trapping mechanism and their typical characteristics, such as the time scale for effective trapping to
occur.
• Structural trapping: After injection, the CO2 migrates upwards due to its buoyancy until it is
trapped under a cap rock layer
• Residual trapping: Initially, the injected CO2 displaces fluid. The fluid will again replace the
CO2 but essentially trap CO2 droplets, which become immobile and residually stay in the rock
formation
• Solubility trapping: The CO2 dissolves into the formation brine. The long-term solubility trapping
depends on temperature, pressure, and geochemical composition of the formation brine
• Ionic trapping: If solubility trapping happens, CO2 can form carbonaceous ions for a pH greater
than 6.
• Mineral trapping: Solubility and ionic trapping are preconditions for the slow process of mineral
trapping. This type of storage makes the injected CO2 completely immobile. Over time the CO2
starts to react with its surrounding rocks and forms carbonate minerals (CaCO3, MgCO3, FeCO3)

2.8.3
Application
There are three different options to apply the MEA-based carbon capture systems: pre-combustion,
oxy-combustion and post-combustion.
Pre-combustion means the carbon source is separated before combustion. Natural gas can be converted
into hydrogen through steam, thermal reforming, or cracking.
However, this technology has high
investment costs, its installation is only applicable for new power plants, and hydrogen combustion is a
very new research field. Currently, hydrogen is burned in small concentration in standard combustion
engines, or it is blended with air, but current turbines cannot stand the extended heat of hydrogen
combustion of more than 1500 °C. [29]
Figure 2.12: Layout pre-combustion carbon capture [9]
Oxyfuel combustion uses pure oxygen instead of air in the combustion chamber. The result is a near
pure CO2 stream instead of a flue gas with a low CO2 concentration. However, the pure oxygen supply
requires expensive and energy-intensive separation units and thus is not favorable for offshore installa-
tions that are already in place. [33]
Figure 2.13: Layout oxy-combustion carbon capture [9]
The post-combustion capture process is widely applied for coal and gas power plants. The carbon from
the emitted flue gas is captured.
Coal-fired power plants usually have high CO2 concentrations in
the flue gas, but gas-fired power plants have a flue gas stream with a low concentration of 3-5% and
relatively low, near atmospheric pressure. Hence, the chemical absorption with MEA is well suited and
used to analyze the CCS potential for offshore oil and gas platforms.

Scenario 1: Offshore wind
Offshore wind energy has seen great developments over the last years. The main drivers are a request
for long-term energy security and clean energy without any greenhouse gases. Since many large cities
are close to the sea, offshore wind energy is available in close distance. Nevertheless, the technology
would not be deployed large scale if it was not mature enough. Not only is has the development seen a

substation, and (5) a transmission grid to shore. In this study, the energy transmission to shore is not
considered because it is directly used at the oil rigs. The great advantage of floating wind turbines is
the possibility to install wind farms far offshore where constant strong wind exists. [40]

Sensitivity analysis
On the one hand, the above economic evaluation already contains low and high scenarios for the CAPEX
and OPEX values, which is visualized here. On the other hand, there are further uncertainties within
the gas prices and tax rates. First, the proportions of the costs and revenues are analyzed. The funda-
mental part of the costs comes from capital expenses. The primary revenue, or saving, comes from the
CO2 tax, leading to the KPI’s high sensitivity regarding the change of these two parameters, CAPEX
and CO2 tax. As the NOx tax only contributes marginally, it is ignored for further analyses.
Once the wind turbines reach the end of their lifetime, they need to be removed from the site. The
costs connected to the decommissioning is called DECEX. In this report, they are not included in the
CAPEX or OPEX. Generally, they are relatively low, with around 2%, which could be further reduced
with appropriate recycling. [17, 50]

CHAPTER 4
Discussion
Decarbonization of oil and gas platforms has become a prominent topic in the oil and gas industry.
With rapidly evolving technologies over the past several years, such as offshore wind, battery storage
systems, and CCS, offsetting carbon emissions connects the advancement of new energy technologies,
reducing carbon tax expenses, and responding to climate change. Based on the Hywind project of
Equinor, this work reveals which incentives support future offshore wind and CCS projects to yield
profits.
The developed model is universally applicable for analyzing the technologies’ potential. Therefore, the
study has reproducibility for other use cases. However, the work implies limitations due to the following
issues:
• The wind profile at the Tampen area yields a load factor of around 60%, which is higher than
many other locations of wind farms
• Instead of floating wind turbine foundations, fixed-bottom installations might apply and improve
the economic evaluation
• Connections to the main electricity grid enable selling generated electricity and improve the per-
formance
• CCS is limited without reasonable possibilities to transport, store or utilize the captured carbon,
e.g., in the form of EOR. If such options are available, CCS can be the cheapest way to mitigate
emissions
• The operation of gas turbines is simplified by linear approximation in order to apply linear pro-
gramming for optimizing and minimizing the emissions.
Furthermore, the study is affected by excluding any subsidies that are frequently granted from gov-
ernments for renewable or CCS projects. Technology improvements are expected to happen but not
assured, and cost reductions over the project lifetime are therefore not included. This study is based
on knowledge and data from past years, but covered technologies evolve rapidly that their numbers
become outdated very soon.
Nevertheless, the study concludes that offshore wind combined with small battery storage systems is
the most feasible option to mitigate carbon and gas platforms’ carbon emissions.
