ABSTRACT 
This Master’s Thesis project proposes a systematic decision-making methodology for the 
identification of resource recovery potential. The work is centered on development of a 
generic framework, for which a computer-aided assistance is implementable via use of 
the proprietary tool, TaskGen. The objective of the computer-aided framework is to 
systematically 
identify 
feasible 
separation 
processing 
pathways, 
recognizing 
underutilized resource potential and reducing the otherwise required arduous testing 
process. The progression of framework subjects the identified potential to further analysis 
and ultimately, results in deciding if pursuing further investment investigation is justified. 
Thermodynamic insights, economic potential, and technology readiness level contribute 
significantly to the techno-economic assessment and identification of established and 
promising process solutions. The application of the framework is demonstrated through a 
case study from the biorefinery industry where the intention was to recover lucrative 
components from a wheat straw process stream, thereby improving economic potential 
and reducing waste. Based on the results of the demonstration of the proposed 
methodology, the conventional bio-ethanol production process could be feasibly 
diversified with the recovery of supplemental products, prior to the fermentation process. 
Finally, future database propagation, expansion into other emerging separation 
techniques, and approaches toward multivariable optimization are discussed.

1 INTRODUCTION 
1.1 PROBLEM STATEMENT 
The underutilized waste streams of bio-based production processes offer great potential 
in the field of resource recovery. This insufficiency is partially causal in the 
underdevelopment of closely associated separation processing pathways including 
techniques like membrane technology and pervaporation. Unlike other process industries 
such as petrochemical operations, where waste reintegration is practiced widely, the 
treatment of effluent is perceived as an obstacle rather than an opportunity [1]. This 
inadequacy of the bio-based industry can be partially attributed to apathy towards 
optimization resulting from high profit margins, and lack of competition. The 
conventional treatment of process streams focuses on meeting regulations, and 
purification of streams with impartiality towards its potentially valuable constituents. The 
separation of bulk contaminants and subsequent disposal voids the opportunity to recover 
value from the potentially lucrative components [2]. However, the variable nature of bio-
based processes waste stream composition (due to micro-organism activity, numerous 
contaminants, and dilute low purity products) provides an inherent challenge in 
identifying economically promising and technically viable recovery pathways. The 
current resource recovery potential identification process is greatly hindered by decision-
making challenges, thus rather arbitrary and under-realized. The methodology of 
subjecting all process stream constituents to rigorous experimental testing is archaic, 
arduous, and unnecessary. By implementing a concurrent assessment of both technical 
and economic aspects, flagging inappropriate pathways before the testing phase, this 
lengthy and costly process can be greatly condensed [1]. Procedure’s are in place to 
perform various aspects of the decision making process, such as preliminary economic, 
technological readiness, and separation technique applicability evaluation. However, the 
combination of such processes to form a comprehensive framework is an effort yet to be 
established. Moreover, a methodology that facilitates computer-aided assistance during 
the evaluation of the most complex and lengthy framework tasks would provide great 
value. The recent success in second and third generation biorefinery production processes 
with respect to circular economy and resource recovery potential identification provides 
an excellent initial demonstration point for the proposed framework and tool [3]. A 
successful industrial validation of the generic framework and associated tool will provide 
a strong basis for future developments in this topic.

1.2 HYPOTHESIS  
The development of a techno-economic framework centered on resource recovery will 
accelerate optimal resource-technology pathway establishment in bio-based processes, 
thus reducing selection dependence on conventional pathways and inappropriate 
candidate testing costs. Creation of a tool to tackle complex algorithms and associated 
progression methodology, will drastically shorten framework implementation and 
facilitate more efficient and innovative solutions. Structuring the framework solution 
strategy as a decomposition approach, considering environmental; economic; 
thermodynamic; and technical feasibility will facilitate certainty in final investment 
investigation decisions.

1.3 BACKGROUND 
1.3.1 Resource Recovery 
In 1972 the independent research group, the Club of Rome composed a report foreseeing 
a worldwide catastrophe by the year 2050 [4]. They predicted that the planets limit to 
growth would be reached by this date if the present growth trends in world population, 
industrialization, pollution, food production, and resource depletion continue unchanged. 
These factors are all dimensions of resource scarcity, an issue of paramount concern to 
past and prior administrations and international organizations [5]. Such concern has 
brought about the development of strategies, aimed at tackling the many facets resource 
scarcity. In direct response to the adverse effects of resource scarcity brought upon by 
rapid economic population growth in China, the circular economy model was developed 
[6]. Aiming to disrupt the traditional linear practice of resource consumption, the model 
bases itself of three main premises, ‘designing out’ waste; differentiation between 
consumable and durable through circularity; and fuelling of the cycle via renewable 
energy [6].  
In tune with the circular economy, one particularly relevant method of scarcity 
remediation is the use of the entire available resource, and the scrapping of obsolete 
capital [4] . Referred to as resource efficiency, the broad strategy encompasses all blocks 
of the material chain, including maximizing production efficiency; minimizing supply 
chain losses; increasing consumption efficiency; recycling or reusing waste materials; 
and changing consumption patterns [5]. As firms are profit driven, their attention is 
focussed primarily on the aspects of resource efficiency relating to the production 
processes.  Moreover, without introduction of international policies, a singular firm has 
little control over consumer habit. Resource recovery is central to improving the 
production aspect of resource efficiency, thus has become increasingly relevant in 
technology development. The beneficial effect of resource recovery can be multifaceted, 
in the recuperation of unreacted reagents, harvesting of lucrative trace products, or 
continuous removal of inhibitory components.  
The basic concept of resource recovery is not novel, and is characteristic of human 
nature, repeatedly manifesting itself throughout history. An early example of resource 
recovery in the bio-based production process industry is the discovery of rum in 17th 
centaury Caribbean. Free men forced into slavery by colonialists discovered that the 
discarded by-product of sugar production, molasses could be fermented into a sugar-wine 
equivalent. The later introduction of distillation technology, transformed the product into 
contemporary rum.

1.3.2 Separation Technology Selection Requirements 
The advancement of separation technologies is concomitant with the pursuit of resource 
recovery. Firms wishing to harness the potential recovery power offered by a 
technology’s maturation, invest in the development of emergent concepts. Prior to 
investment, companies must be able to compare the maturity of different types of 
technologies in a consistent manner. Aware of the inconsistencies involved with the 
interdisciplinary assessment of technology maturity, NASA developed a Technology 
Readiness Level index in the 1970’s [7]. With a project as complex as the 1981 Space 
Exploration Initiative, TRL assessment was developed to provide a systematic approach 
to communicate technology maturity between the hundreds of people involved in the 
space shuttle development [8]. The tool provided alleviation toward the three inevitable 
challenges of the development of a new system, performance; schedule; and budget, 
hence rapidly spread to other technological communities. However, conversely to 
NASA’s technology implementation orientated approach, firms sought a tool to drive 
technology commercialization [8]. Therefore, different organizations tailored the process 
to their respective requirements. Today the United States Department of Defense (US-
DoD) definitions of technology readiness level are used as a basis for immature 
technology design approaches [9]. The definitions can be seen in Table 1.

In addition to technical assessment, the applicability of resource recovery is closely 
intertwined with economic valuation. A lucrative or inhibitory component of the process 
stream must be first identified, in order for technical evaluation to occur. Further 
assessment of the stream cannot be justified if no strategic components are found. As in 
the technical appraisal, a systematic consistent approach must be undertaken. The 
economic heuristic levelling method most commonly applied is called Net Present Value 
(NPV) analysis. This tool allows the user to discount cash inflows and outflows of the 
investment opportunity over a period of time to a present value difference [10]. For 
resource recovery projects, implementation cost, time, and resources are balanced against 
the projected gain from reclamation. A resulting positive net balance indicates that the 
investment has a greater return than the current interest rate. As a number of 
==PAGE:10==
10 
 
unquantifiable stochastic and variable costs are associated with projects of this scale, an 
undeniable level of uncertainty is present. Due to this characteristic uncertainty, NPV 
analysis provides insufficient information to develop an optimum design that can 
guarantee suitable profitability [11]. A metric that encompasses uncertainty, by 
representing the firm’s risk thresholds in comparison to the requisite profit is required to 
sensibly evaluate an investment opportunity. By setting return on investment (ROI) and 
payback period thresholds, the metrics can be used in coordination to characterize the 
sum of the firm’s profitability expectations plus the “premium” needed to justify intrinsic 
business risk/uncertainty [11]. The use of this metric allows the firm to omit resource 
recovery pathways that do not fit into the risk tolerance of their investment portfolio. 
 
Once a target resource with preliminary economic potential has been identified, and the 
separation techniques classified in terms of their TRL, technology-component screening 
process can occur. A general approach to determining the applicability of separation 
techniques in relation to the target resource is a stepwise process, starting with broad 
criterion and steadily narrowing down. For commonly recovered components, previously 
established pathways are identified in literature, and compiled. If a group of components 
share similar recovery characteristics, they can often be lumped as a pseudo-component 
in order to determine pathway commonalities. The paring of unfamiliar components via 
identification of favourable parameters, conditions, or affinities is also possible, but often 
avoided due to the associated risk. The techniques perceived to be applicable for the 
target resource recovery are then subject to a net present value analysis, cumulating with 
a comparison of ROI and payback period. If the separation technique passes the 
economic appraisal, the final distinguishing step is testing. This cost-limiting step of the 
assessment maturates to an acceptable TRL and subsequently challenges the applicability 
of the technology-resource parings [2]. 
 
1.4 LITERATURE REVIEW 
With the current transition towards a circular economy due to mounting resource scarcity 
and continual development of stringent environmental standards, a greater incentive is 
posed towards resource recovery than when many conventional processes were 
established. The benefit of transitions from waste treatment facilities to resource recovery 
processes is multifaceted, not only aiding sustainability, but also providing financial 
value from target component recovery and inhibitor removal. However, for a given 
process stream, not all separation technologies provide feasible recovery or suitable 
profitability. To this end, the efficient identification of feasible and preferably optimal 
resource recovery process alternatives is an eminently challenging endeavour. The know-
how approach of emulating similar existing pathways, followed by trial and error testing, 
==PAGE:11==
11 
 
currently applied by firms adopting resource recovery projects leaves much to be desired. 
The rigorous testing, economic evaluations, and technology maturation procedures used 
to narrow down pairing candidates prove particularly insufficient. Whilst these aspects of 
the comprehensive analysis are essential for optimal paring, it is inefficient to subject this 
arduous process to more technology candidates than necessary. To counter this, a number 
of authors have suggested concurrent technological and economical assessment 
frameworks that work to screen substandard pathways before reaching the testing phase 
[6]. However, in these cases, inherent risk quantification was emphasized rather than 
applicability. In ignoring immature technologies or by applying parity to the candidates 
TRL’s, significant pitfalls to decision accuracy are introduced. The cost required for the 
developing a technology to a suitable TRL must be acknowledged, and evaluated against 
the associated benefit it provides. With this in mind, a systematic framework that 
considered techno-economics alongside TRL was constructed [2]. The framework 
considers that development costs and number of remaining considerations occur inversely 
and aims to narrow the search-space quickly.  
 
Once a decision-support framework is established, a user can employ a model-based 
approach to determining feasible separation techniques for recovery of relevant stream 
constituents (technology-resource pairing). This of course requires formation of a search-
space-reduction criteria that facilitates decision-making in each stage of the framework, 
coupled with an appropriate solution strategy. The simplest of the solution techniques, 
“stop go”, where the user intervenes at decisive checkpoints to decide the subsequent fate 
of the case study being subjected to the framework, has been implemented to the 
aforementioned framework, in the context of a reverse osmosis case study [12]. The 
strategy decomposes the framework into information generation and metric evaluation 
steps, in which the system is iterated if a decision gate is failed. Other facets of process 
engineering such as solvent selection and chemical product design have witnessed further 
advancement in regards to decomposition-based solution strategies employing decision 
metrics [13] [14]. These projects simplify the modelling complexity by providing the 
ability to provide model specific templates to a generic design methodology [15]. 
Another work applies a computer-aided framework approach to recovering waste heat by 
considering a predefined stepwise strategy [16]. The methodology follows a sequential 
process consisting of defining product targets; defining process targets; integrating 
product-process problem formulation; and designing program solution [16]. The first 
stages involve the specification of needs and constraints, and identification of candidates, 
for the later stages to convert and output mathematically. The design problem can then be 
solved with an appropriate strategy and programmed solver. Once initialized, there are 
different solution strategies available to solve the integrated problem, including the, 
generate-test; simultaneous solution; and decomposition approaches [15]. Generate and 
test considers subjects candidates to constraints, then ranks them in order of their 
==PAGE:12==
12 
 
objective value; simultaneous solution employs an optimization algorithm to determine 
the best candidate mathematically; and the decomposition approach decomposes the 
problem into sub-problems to be tackled independently in a predefined order [16]. 
Despite being product design orientated, the nonspecific nature of the decision 
framework allows for transparent transferal to a resource recovery application. 
Consequently, for the purposes of resource recovery it is deemed that the decomposition 
would be a suitable fit for the solution methodology, as numerous sub-issues of 
equivalent importance must be addressed. However, global optimality cannot be 
guaranteed with the decomposition methodology, thus a combination with the generate 
and test methodology would result in well-rounded solutions. The resultant solution 
strategy subjects the candidates to constraints within each sub-problem then ranks them 
correspondingly. Accordingly, the opportunity of integrating of a computerized 
methodology with a resource recovery focussed techno-economic guiding framework 
presents itself.  
 
To make any informed decision, knowledge must be drawn from prior experience, else 
amassed via information compilation. Decision-making tools are no exception and 
require a comprehensive knowledgebase to generate appropriate solutions. To this end, 
the calibre of the workflow’s solution is as good at the information it draws from; hence, 
it is clear that there is great value to be gained from the rigorous formation of extensive 
databases. The adaptable nature of generic methodologies facilitates the utilization of 
specific templates to provide further assessment opportunities for a given knowledgebase 
[15]. Moreover, the ability to link distinct databases with an abridging tool illustrates 
potential in implementing a modular approach. Despite being integral for characterizing 
process streams in the early steps of resource recovery potential identification 
methodologies, no such wealth of knowledge suitable for techno-economic evaluation 
has been accumulated. Amongst the fields that have taken steps towards database 
establishment, varying levels of metric detail have been applied. For solvent selection, 
computer-aided molecular design (CAMD) methodologies were practiced, employing 
insights into thermodynamic properties to reduce the mathematical complexity of the 
problem [13]. In addition, software such as ProPred, which employs the relationships 
between functional groups to predict properties, can be invaluable for rapid data 
generation. Conversely, a framework designed to achieve sustainable process 
performance considered a more inclusive approach, compiling data in increasing levels of 
specificity, incorporating, possible processing networks; parameters required to solve 
generic block model equations; and raw techno-economic data [17]. Of course, 
knowledgebase structure and level of required detail is subjective towards the problem 
faced, hence can be tailored in conjunction with the framework to ensure optimal 
assessment.  
==PAGE:13==

For the most part, resource scarcity, and inherently related and economic necessity has 
driven the implementation of resource recovery projects. Industries like petrochemical 
that exist in a dynamic market and witness constant pertinent technological advancement 
must adapt to survive. Due to economy of scale and environmental sanctions imposed 
upon petroleum refining, the field has seen great investment into physical and energetic 
resource recovery [18]. The vast scale of development in combination with the relative 
regularity of crude petroleum has entrenched near optimal resource-technology pathways. 
However, resource recovery is one of the many unmet needs faced by emerging 
industries, such as bioprocesses, that do not haves the means to identify prime candidates 
via brute force testing. As a result, the potential of resource recovery from process 
streams is regularly unmet. EU H2020 identifies bio-based processes as particularly 
culpable in this regard, with very few examples of facilities that can convert the organic 
fraction of process streams into anything other than compost or energy [19]. Ironically 
the ‘green revolution’ and subsequent rapid emergence of bio-based processes has been 
causal towards its resource recovery inadequacies [20]. The immature industry initially 
encountered resource abundance, thus perceived little demand for reuse of potentially 
valuable recoverable compounds [2]. Now with the discussion of resource scarcity 
becoming ever so pertinent, the sunken value is beginning to be realized. However, the 
recovery spectrum has been limited to a small amount of established target resources and 
accompanying pathways, such as residual fermentation broth centrifugation and 
evaporation to produce livestock feed [21]. Despite bio-based processes compatibility 
with emergent separation techniques such as membrane technology and hybrid processes, 
many contenders are not considered in favour of proven alternatives. Even projects that 
had identified preliminary economic potential; such as the isolation of whey from cheese 
process water, had been reluctant to implement recovery technologies until the TRL had 
been matured externally to a suitable degree [3]. The establishment of these 
unconventional pathways would significantly broaden the applicability of resource 
recovery in bio-based processes.  
 
In order to identify all potential technologies for a given process stream, a modular 
approach must be taken for database establishment. Once each technology’s database has 
been populated, a comprehensive suitability assessment can occur. A logical 
commencement point to implementing the decomposition based approach is with 
membrane technology; as of the separation techniques applied by bio-based processes, it 
poses the greatest unexploited potential, especially when integrated into a hybrid system 
[22]. The growing range of applications span from exclusive processes with no 
conventional alternatives to others that endure competitive markets [23]. The field’s 
unique situation is a result of a dichotomy between the settled and emergent technologies 
==PAGE:14==
14 
 
available. Industry leaders have performed excellent R&D work towards established 
technologies, such as pre-concentration steps and municipal wastewater membrane 
bioreactors; constantly improving material science, and energy consumption [22]. 
Conversely, a list of todays ‘emergent’ applications, including pervaporation and 
enzymatic membrane reactions, shares much in common with a list compiled in 1979 
[23] [24]. The absence of incentives for membrane producers to develop application-
specific membranes is particularly causal for this stagnation. Moreover, weighting 
attributed to ease of implementation often exceeds optimality of design, thus leading to 
superior adoption rates of conventional membrane modules over complex systems or 
hybrid processes. In response to this development void, a chapter outlining the challenges 
faced by membrane technology suggests better-focussed R&D, particularly in the 
exploration of potential applications using decision-making evaluation tools [22].

2 PROBLEM FORMULATION 
2.1 GENERAL OBJECTIVE FUNCTION 
The first and most instrumental task of framework construction is the problem definition. 
It is here that the aim, objectives, and scope of the design problem are outlined 
mathematically. The objective function is formulated to fit an agenda, and demonstrates 
the desired outcome of the design problem. Whilst presented mathematically, the solution 
is not solved simultaneously in the form of an optimization algorithm. Instead the 
objective function is calculated once the all the sub-problems have been tackled. 
Accompanying the objective function are the constraints that the design problem is 
subject to, specifying the restrictions and interactions as per user policy. An objective 
statement is intended to be tailored to the system studied, and must be carefully 
deliberated upon as it is the basis for the decision making process. However, whilst 
introduced at the very start of the decision process, the objective function is not applied 
until after the framework has run its course. At this point, all unsuitable options have 
been eliminated, and the enduring contenders must be optimized. As not all adopters are 
familiar with objective function synthesis, a generic template for the uninformed user 
must be curated. The untargeted template will generate a broader spectrum of potential 
candidates than its specialized counterparts and acts as a generic start point for 
uninformed problem formulation. The generic form of a constrained objective function

3 CONCEPTS AND THEORIES FOR FRAMEWORK DESIGN 
The following section outlines the logic, theories, and concepts behind the framework 
algorithms, presented in 4. The general format is to present the motivation behind each 
framework steps inclusion, processes, results significance, and generic examples. Bear in 
mind when reviewing the complex concepts that a tool and methodology pairing for 
computer-aided assistance of the framework is proposed in 5. 
3.1 TARGET STREAM CHARACTERIZATION 
Before the design of the framework can begin, the process stream to be studied must be 
characterized. In doing so, the user has a comprehensive portrayal of the stream 
composition and relevant provided properties, providing a strong basis for framework 
data collection. At this point, the relevant data require is the stream composition, and 
associated concentration. The user must decide at which concentration a component 
becomes relevant. Literature, information must be applied to determine if the pertinent 
components are reactants, products, or inhibitors in common reactions.  Components with 
similar properties may be grouped into lumped pseudo-components.  
The framework assesses particular stream on a case basis, to determine the optimal 
scenario. If no reactants or inhibitors are present, number of cases is equal to one. 
However, if these operations occur, more cases must be generated in order to explore all 
possibilities.  
 
3.2 ECONOMIC POTENTIAL 
Once the problem has been formulated, steps can be taken to narrow the field of 
candidates. In order to progress in the framework, a particular component must generate a 
positive economic potential. Higher thresholds are subjective to the user’s objectives, and 
can be set accordingly. The rudimentary approach to identifying economic potential is to 
find the quantity of a particular component present in the stream, and multiply by its unit 
price. However, as very few conventionally profitable bio-refineries exist, other impacts 
must be considered [25]. For streams that have to undergo a treatment process before 
expulsion, economic potential comes in the form of nulling abatement costs associated 
with the studied component. Furthermore, the value gained from recovering inhibiting 
agents manifests itself in the form of increased production, which must also be

3.3 POTENTIAL ENVIRONMENTAL IMPACT  
In addition to determining the societal cost of carbon emission, the ability to evaluate a 
compounds environmental degradation contribution is important. The environmental 
metric challenge arises in quantifying and finding common units to demonstrate a 
compounds effect. To address the incompatibility issue, the potential environmental 
impact (PEI) was developed as a universal indicator of the unrealized effect or impact 
that the emission of a compound would have on the environment [27]. As PEI is a 
conceptual quantity that cannot be evaluated directly, related measureable quantities must 
be combined with a unifying relation. The waste reduction algorithm (WAR), suffices 
this conundrum by providing the basis for a pollution balance, comprised of impact 
categories for which the specific chemical environmental impacts can be relatively 
quantified [28]. The eight environmental impact categories fall into two general areas,

Implementation of the WAR algorithm is to be part of the economic sustainability 
potential stage framework. As one of the mains intentions of the framework is to reduce 
testing, and manual input, impact category measurement for each chemical is not only 
infeasible but also counterproductive. Moreover, due to the laborious process required to 
determine environmental factors, many projects neglect to include PEI in favour of 
economic guidance. However, with the development of the proprietary software ProPred 
a chemical’s environmental category factors can be generated by applying group 
contribution relationships, dramatically reducing the time required. A compound’s 
environmental parameters are reliably estimated from the intrinsic functional groups that 
comprise it. ProPred develops these parameters by applying the combined group-
contribution method with the atom connectivity index [30]. 
Once determined, the overall potential environmental impact designation informs the user 
of the hazard posed by a specific compound. This should then be compared to a known 
toxic compound, such as HCL. In theory, if not recovered for economic motives, the 
constituents are treated with the prescribed standard method and disposed of. However, 
for highly environmentally hazardous materials, standard measures are often insufficient. 
The environmental impact assessment provides the opportunity for an environmentally 
vigilant operator to recognize such hazardous components and thus design their recovery 
strategy accordingly.  
3.3.1 Decision Gate 1 
The aim of the preliminary economic potential and environmental impact assessment 
analysis is to identify target components prematurely, and progress the associated 
compound through the framework for recovery. Consequently, as such high degree of 
discretion is attributed to making a suitable decision, manual input is required at this 
stage of the framework. If a component is deemed to not have the required economic 
potential or to pose a substantially threatening environmental impact, the component is 
not set as the target. If no targets are nominated, the case exits the framework.

3.4 SEPARATION TECHNOLOGY SELECTION 
If a component successfully advances through the economic and sustainability decision 
gateways, it is deemed to possess a sufficient potential to transition further through the 
framework. In the next stage of the process, viable separation technologies for the 
recovery of the stream constituents are identified. Such technologies can then be 
sequenced to provide a feasible process synthesis for the recovery of the afore-
determined target components. Systematic process design can be achieved by applying a 
approach developed by [31]. The methodology applies thermodynamic insights generated 
from physiochemical properties, their intertwined interactions, and relationships with 
separation techniques. The framework will employ aspects of the methodology for the 
formulation of the required separation process.  
The guiding philosophy of the method relies upon the close relation between separation 
process principles and the structural, chemical, and physical properties of the stream [31]. 
This is because separation techniques exploit the differences in the properties across the 
mixture components in order to recover the components. Most separation technologies 
rely on a small number of integral properties to perform their extraction duties. [31] has 
listed the properties that can be used to predict separation tasks, seen in Table 3. Once the 
necessary properties are obtained for all components, the binary pairs corresponding 
ratios can be used to evaluate the separation process feasibility. The magnitude of the 
ratios roughly corresponds inversely to the degree of difficulty of separating the two 
associated components. Minimum thresholds of property ratios required for feasible 
separation have been identified through literature review and are present in Table 4 [31]. 
These tables and the binary pair property ratios from the backbone of the separation

At this point the feasible separation techniques for all binary pairs can be identified. 
However, to generate an appropriately thermodynamically feasible flowsheet, separation 
task redundancy and ease of separation must also be considered. To uphold physical 
feasibility, it is apt to select the techniques that satisfy the easiest separation pathway. To 
do so without breaking thermodynamic principles, binary pairs are calculated from 
adjacent components, which are ordered in terms of their property rank. The highest 
resultant normalized ratio corresponds to the first separation task. To avoid redundant 
separation tasks, for example binary pairings generated from components on either side 
of the property split, new ratios and rankings must be determined for each node of the 
flowsheet. By following this procedure, the most feasible separation pathway 
(thermodynamically) is produced, and is considered the base case. However, it is possible 
to investigate other agendas by placing constraints on this method.

3.5 ECONOMIC INSIGHTS 
The guiding principle made in the separation task selection section is that a flowsheet 
comprised of the most thermodynamically feasible separation tasks requires the smallest 
driving force. Therefore, whilst low driving force can sometimes be translated to low 
operating costs, thermodynamically feasible flowsheets are generated rather than 
economically feasible ones. As profitability is a main influence in the implementation of 
resource recovery projects, economic sufficiency must be addressed by the framework.  
When considering a method to implement economics into the framework, the immediate 
challenge presented is the amount of information required to produce an accurate 
estimate. For instance, operating costs are difficult to predict without mass and energy 
balances. This challenge is compounded by that fact that at this point in the framework 
only the exploited property and mass flow capacity is known about the separation task. 
==PAGE:29==
29 
 
Due to these limitations, the inclusion of economics should be used as a screening 
method for clearly financially insufficient pathways rather than providing a marginal 
decision basis. The methodology developed by [32], and recently substantiated by [33] 
requires minimal inputs, and when combined with potential review passing through the 
process step, can provide this screening capability. A minimum payback period is the 
user specified threshold that scales the severity of the screening process.

3.6 REFINE AND EVALUATE 
Unlike the financial sufficiency screening method proposed in the last section, there is no 
way to automatically sense check the technical feasibility of the separation task selection. 
Whilst the minimum feasible property ratios in Table 4. are calibrated to industrial 
processes, due to dependencies on other properties or physical features, some separation 
tasks may be unsuitable or impractical [31]. For example, the normalized vapor pressure 
ratio between two components may be ranked the highest, but full separation by only 
exploiting the vapor pressure differential is impossible due to an azeotrope. Therefore, all 
tasks chosen by the separation selection step that have sufficiently passed the economic 
insights step, should be subject to a manual inspection. Here the user should apply their 
own knowledge with that of literature, to verify the suggested separation tasks can be 
suitably achieved using an established technology and at reasonable conditions. 
Literature searches should be performed to determine if similar processes have occurred, 
and to check for any potential concerns, such as azeotropes, transport mediums, mutual 
solubilities, component incompatibilities, etc. The presence of the possible issues does 
not immediately discount the separation task, as users must refer to the optional 
operations framework step before determining the tasks fate. As all technologies present 
==PAGE:31==
31 
 
in Table 4 are assumed to be at TRL 9, preference should not be placed on particular 
technologies due to their prominence [31]. However, it is possible to assign a lesser TRL 
value to a separation task if the technology is applied to an unproven application or at a 
vastly different scale [33]. If none of the suggested separation task’s associated 
technologies provide acceptable separation, the next highest ranked task should take its 
place. Replacing a task affects all downstream tasks, thus the framework should revert 
back to the separation selection step to regenerate the tasks. The refinement should be 
occur until all financially sufficient separation tasks have had their technical feasibility 
verified.  
3.7 OPTIONAL OPERATIONS 
A number of optional operations are supplemented to the framework to ensure all 
scenarios are accounted for. These additional steps are called upon at different points in 
the framework as illustrated in Figure 6. 
3.7.1 Force early separation 
The separation selection method operates indifferently of component value, hence often 
suggesting financially redundant tasks early in the separation process, and only 
recovering the target components in the ultimate tasks. This is likely due to the observed 
causaul relationship between component price and separation/production simplicity. In 
such cases it is possible to force the prompt recovery of target components at a sacrifice 
of thermodynamic ease. This can be achieved by intervening in the separation selection 
section and forcefully choosing the tasks that facilitate the lowest degree of separation for 
the target component. The degree of separation is represented by the property rankings of 
the target component. Once selected, the forced tasks progressed through the framework 
as normal, but inherently face more potential issues than the base case due to the sacrifice 
of separation ease. A generic demonstration of this optional step can be seen as follows.

3.7.2 Mass separation agent 
During the refine and evaluate framework step, some separation tasks are discovered to 
require mass separating agents (MSA’s) to fully recover components. Therefore, a 
systematic methodology for the selection of such agents must be developed, and 
appended as an optional step at this point in the main framework body. The authors of the 
separation selection methodology [31], proposed a procedure to identify suitable MSA’s, 
using solubility parameters. The method requires specifically the hydrogen bonding and 
dispersion parameters, which can be identified in literature or generated by ProPred [34]. 
When the solubility parameters of common MSA’s in Table 16 and two components 
requiring separation are plotted against one another, much can be learnt from the 
configurations of the resultant data points. A region of MSA feasibility is formed where 
the solubility parameters are similar to those of the component to be recovered (for high 
capacity) and dissimilar from those of the other constituent (for high selectivity) [31]. 
This can be visualized as the region on the solubility diagram that is on the side of the 
recoverable component furthest from the undesirable component [31]. MSA candidates 
that do not meet this requirement are screened. If experimental data is available for the 
mutual solubilities of the relevant components and common solvents, further screen 
candidates with unsuitable values. Finally, evaluate the properties related to the 
separation mechanism task. For example, for azeotropic distillation create the ternary 
diagram and compare azeotropic boiling points, whilst for liquid-liquid extraction 
compare densities. If several MSA’s remain after this comparative process, relate 
properties unrelated to the separation (price, toxicity, volatility) to select the optimal 
candidate. If no successful candidate is available, screen the separation task are move to 
the next highest ranked task in the separation selection section. A generic demonstration 
of a hypothetical MSA selection situation is presented as follows.

3.7.3 Transport medium addition 
As many separation pathways require a transport medium, such as water, some 
exceptions to the separation technology selection methodology must be made. For nearly 
all cases, it is assumed that the transport medium acts as an inert component and does not 
partake in the separation task selection process. Resultantly, streams are assumed to be 
aqueous in the transport medium. Moreover, unless stated otherwise, pure component 
==PAGE:34==
34 
 
streams are assumed to be easily separable from the aqueous phase. However, in the 
cases where the transport medium must be removed, such as when an azeotrope is 
formed, or a simple drying operation cannot be applied, a separation task must be created. 
When the user is performing the refine and evaluate step, attention should be applied to 
identifying such cases. The implementation of a transport medium separation task can be 
done by reverting back to the separation selection framework step. Finally, as the mass 
rate of the transport medium is not known at this stage in the framework, it is excluded 
from economic insight step calculations. The influence of the transport medium is 
accounted for in the process simulation step, and associated financial impact quantified in 
the economic analysis step.

3.8 PROCESS SIMULATION 
If a case successfully meets the threshold payback period requirement, the framework 
progresses to the process simulation step. The intention of the process simulation 
framework step is to highlight any overlooked infeasibilities and provide the required 
information for the subsequent economic analysis. The simulation can be viewed as a 
comprehensive mass and energy balance, generating the product flows, process unit 
throughputs, utility duties, and process inflows as an output. Therefore, the brand of 
process simulation software applied is inconsequential. What is important, is the 
approach taken to constructing the simulation from the verified separation task flowsheet.  
First of all, the two assumptions vitally significant for the previous framework steps; 
complete component separation, and disregarded transport medium impact, no longer 
apply. Next, the method in which the separation tasks are modelled must be addressed. 
Technologies whose operations are inbuilt into the software should be built with the 
palette unit blocks. Supplementary equipment should be employed to furnish the required 
operating conditions for separation. If a technology cannot be simulated by an inbuilt 
palette unit, its operation can be emulated with the inclusion of a component splitter unit. 
To do so, the separation factor and optimal operating conditions must be determined from 
literature, and mimicked with the component splitter system. Supplementary equipment 
required to meet these conditions must also be added. If the task requires a MSA, 
dilution, or a transport medium, a process inflow should be introduced. Model reactions 
to the degree at which information is known about the kinetics, thus apply a conversion 
reactor if condition dependent rate kinetics are not known. The equations for retention 
and separation factor can be seen as follows.

5.1 ORIGIN 
Upon reflecting on the finished framework, it was clear that the efforts to uphold 
comprehensiveness could potential be perceived as a downfall, in respect to the number 
of challenges that arise when attempting to apply manually, namely the complexity and 
time it takes to apply. This is an unintended consequence, as the framework was 
envisioned to aid users quickly through the resource recovery potential identification 
process. Consequently, computer aided functionalities were deemed as a necessary 
addition to streamline the process, thus TaskGen was conceived.   
5.2 OVERVIEW 
TaskGen is a tool created to aid users through the conceived resource recovery 
framework. When using the tool many of the aforementioned framework steps can be 
completed rapidly with use of the tool, thus drastically decreasing complexity and 
increasing ease of application. TaskGen facilitates swift generation of feasible 
flowsheets, thus allowing users to assess possibilities that may not have been previously 
considered, and eliminate unsuitable candidates early the evaluation process. An 
illustration of how framework implementation is assisted with the use of TaskGen can be 
seen in 5.6. The TaskGen MATLAB code can be seen in 0.

5.6 FRAMEWORK METHODOLOGY WITH TASKGEN 
The intention of the subsequent section is not to introduce another framework, thus no 
new concepts or operations are presented. Instead, a results orientated framework 
guidance methodology is proposed, facilitating use of the framework at an increased rate 
and accuracy. To achieve this, the functionalities of TaskGen are specifically designed to 
perform the most arduous and complex algorithms in the framework, such as the 
separation selection step. By simply just performing such functionalities in TaskGen, the 
significance of the inner workings can easily be overlooked. Consequently, due to the 
results orientated nature of the methodology, implementing the procedure without prior 
knowledge of the framework could leave to a knowledge void. It is therefore 
recommended that a user, acquires comprehension of the framework before proceeding. 
By doing so, the user can both benefit from and maintain an understanding of the results 
facilitated by TaskGen.

separation. Therefore, when TaskGen performs the rest of algorithm 2, the identified task 
is chosen as the soon as it is thermodynamically feasible, thus being forcefully separated 
early.  
5.6.7.2 Transport medium 
Implementing the effect of a transport medium in TaskGen is possible via exploiting the 
functionality of the reaction addition option, Table ‘J’. By following the reaction addition 
step of the 5.4 Case set up procedure, and setting the reaction’s products equal to the 
reactants plus the transport medium. Adjust the point at which the effect is applied, by 
adding constraints. Manifest the effect of the transport medium by clicking the ‘Reaction’ 
button (‘N’).

Algorithm 6.2: Economic analysis  
Objective: Identify most profitable case 
Step (i): Specify discount rate and project period in Table ‘P’. Add the relevant 
information (TRL, water and consumables inflows, duties, and target component 
outflows) gathered from the process simulation step to Table ‘Q’. The annual cashflow is 
automatically calculated in Table ‘R’ and subsequently converted into a present value in 
Table ‘T’. 
Step (ii): Update the relative throughput and characteristic scores in the Table ‘R’ if the 
process simulation provided different results to the preliminary estimates. Once 
populated, investment and development cost will be calculated automatically in Table 
‘T’. The subsequent values are applied with the cash flow present value to simultaneously 
determine NPV in table ‘T’.  
Decision 3: Pursue further investment investigation? 
Upon determination of NPV, payback period and return on investment are calculated 
automatically in Table ‘T’. If both metrics are above the set threshold in Table ‘P’, 
investigate the case further. If more than one case is greater than the thresholds, select the 
case with the highest NPV. If no cases meet the threshold requirements, exit the 
framework.  
*The financial decision should be verified with clear uncertainty analysis before pursuing

To determine the azeotropic distillation conditions the ternary diagram of the water-
ethanol-cyclohexane azeotrope was created in ICAS [34]. 
 
Figure 35. Water, cyclohexane, ethanol ternary azeotrope phase diagram 
The introduction of cyclohexane to the system creates a low boiling ternary azeotrope, as 
shown in Figure 35, (0.31E, 0.16W, 0.53C, at 62.6oC). As ethanol is present in higher 
molar quantities than water and cyclohexane, it exits the column as a nearly pure bottoms 
product. As wasting the vapor product is vastly uneconomic, cyclohexane and residual 
ethanol must be recovered. Fortunately, cyclohexane forms a heterogeneous azeotrope 
with water, meaning that the two components are immiscible in their liquid phase. This 
phenomenon can be observed as the black dotted line in Figure 35. The immiscibility can 
be exploited using a condenser decanter sequence at 36oC, producing cyclohexane rich 
(0.31E, 0.03W, 0.66C) and lean (0.31E, 0.68W, 0.01C) phases. The cyclohexane rich 
stream is fed back into the ethanol purification column, and the cyclohexane lean stream 
can be recycled with another distillation column operating at the ternary azeotropic 
conditions. As water is now present in higher molar quantities than the other constituents, 
it exits the recycle column as nearly pure bottoms product. The vapor stream is combined 
with a cyclohexane makeup stream and recycled back into the ethanol rich feed. The 
HYSYS component splitters were set up to closely mimic this complex process [56], as

6.6.2.8 Recycle streams

an equivalent capacity bioethanol plant. Moreover, the reintegration of the permeate 
stream monosaccharides emulates industry practices and facilitates greater overall 
ethanol recovery. Care was taken to converge adequately and avoid unsatisfactory

7 CONCLUSION 
In this work, the main accomplishment is the development of a comprehensive 
framework, supplemented with computer-aided functionalities to systematically identify 
resource recovery potential, thus facilitating more efficient and innovative solutions. The 
developed method considers a wide range of potential influences upon resource recover 
capacity including; environmental; economic; thermodynamic; and technical feasibility. 
The solution strategy of the framework relates such aspects with a decomposition 
approach, where the complexity of the various framework aspects is decomposed into a 
set of sub-problems subject to constraints and solved sequentially. If the framework 
successfully converges upon a range of solutions, all sufficing the set of constraints, 
implementing the objective function and associated optimization parameters properties, 
selects the final candidate. Ultimately, the framework is intended as a tool to identify 
recovery potential, synthesize associated separation pathways, and subject feasible 
alternatives to to technical and economic simulation, cumulating in the decision to 
investigate investment into a candidate’s development further.  
 
Since the separation task pathways generated by comparing adjacent property ratios to 
feasibility indices do not require complex experimental procedures, and little input data, 
numerous process alternatives can be quickly synthesized for a recovery problem. Prior 
to this step, recovery justification is assessed with environmental and economic 
considerations. Unfortunately, as the separation tasks proposed by the method are 
founded off thermodynamic insights, technical and industrial feasibility may be 
inadequate. Thus there is a need for fast and systematic regeneration of flowsheets that 
can be subjected infeasibility constraints, reaction implementation, and MSA/transport 
medium effects as they emerge throughout the iterative synthesis process. This daunting 
process of having to repeatedly perform the associated complex and lengthy algorithms 
manually for each impracticability emergence is the main motivation behind the 
development of the proprietary tool, TaskGen. The framework progression methodology 
developed alongside TaskGen facilitates rapid advancement through the most complex 
algorithms, further improving capacity for screening and potential identification. Apart 
from generating feasible separation task flowsheets, the framework evaluated the 
performance of alternative cases via process simulation and economic analyses, for which 
the final decision is founded off. The non-specific nature and component independence of 
the framework delivers strength in versatility and facilitates application to the vast 
majority of waste stream for a broad range of industries. The successful validation of the 
separation selection method with an industrial case of Isobutylene production paved the 
way for demonstrations of the complete framework application. The chosen The wheat 
bio-refinery case study was chosen, as it illustrates the applicability range in contrast to 
==PAGE:97==
97 
 
the petrochemical validation and possesses the characteristic features required to facilitate 
demonstration of all the steps of the framework. The generation of the two cases, 
monosaccharide extraction and ethanol production demonstrated that a variety of novel 
recovery solutions can be quickly formulated from a singular input waste stream. 
Moreover, the comparative assessment procedure simplifies the decision making process 
towards determining a candidate that justifies further investment investigation. Finally, 
the successful implementation of various functionalities into TaskGen illustrated promise 
for further integration with the framework and expansion into different assessment 
categories commonly associated with objective functions.
