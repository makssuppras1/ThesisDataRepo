ABSTRACT  
The corrosion-related failure of printed circuit boards (PCBs) is a growing concern, especially 
as electronic devices are increasingly exposed to humid and contaminated environments. This 
thesis investigates the influence of typical aerosol components on condensation behavior and 
electrochemical migration on PCB surfaces under direct current (DC) voltage. Initial 
experiments were conducted to identify optimal test conditions, including the selection of Milli-
Q water as the base solvent. Two main experimental approaches were employed. The first 
focused on the effect of salt type (NaCl, CaCl₂, MgCl₂) and concentration on both condensation 
and electrochemical behavior. In this approach, the salt solution was sprayed over the entire 
PCB, then dried before testing, allowing condensation to occur naturally. The second approach 
isolated electrochemical effects by placing the salt solution directly into a 3D-printed boundary, 
where condensation was predefined. In the first experiment, the presence of salts accelerated 
condensation and affected droplet size and distribution, with MgCl₂ producing the largest 
droplets. NaCl showed the most aggressive electrochemical response due to fast ion mobility, 
resulting in multiple high-current peaks. In the second experiment, current increased with both 
voltage and salt concentration. However, hydrogen bubbling and precipitate formation 
especially with MgCl₂ and CaCl₂ disrupted dendrite growth and reduced current. NaCl showed 
less surface precipitation but high overall electrochemical activity. Post-test characterization 
using light optical microscopy (LOM), scanning electron microscopy (SEM), and energy-
dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS) provided detailed insight into dendrite formation, 
precipitation behavior, and surface corrosion. Dendrites were observed at 10 V for all salt types, 
even at low concentrations (0.6 µg/cm²), while pure water produced extensive dendrite growth 
with minimal visible corrosion. An additional pH indicator test further confirmed the acidic 
nature of the anode and the alkaline character of the cathode, supporting observations of 
hydroxide formation and salt-dependent electrochemical behavior. These findings contribute to 
a deeper understanding of how common environmental contaminants affect PCBs from both 
condensation and electrochemical perspectives.

1 INTRODUCTION 
 Electronic devices have become an integral part of our daily lives. Over the past two to three 
decades, there has been a significant shift, with electronic devices transitioning from luxury 
items to consumable goods. The trend toward making devices smaller and lighter has resulted 
in a substantial reduction in component sizes, enabling tighter packing on printed circuit board 
assemblies (PCBAs). However, despite these advancements, a notable drawback is that 
electronic devices have become more susceptible to failures caused by corrosion. This is 
primarily due to the closer spacing between biased points on the Printed Circuit Board 
Assembly (PCBA) surface, which increases the electric field (E = V/d) and facilitates the 
formation of corrosion cells during local condensation in humid environments.[1]  This 
vulnerability is further exacerbated by exposure to harsh environments as electronics transition 
from protected indoor spaces to the demands of everyday use. Industrial electronics face similar 
challenges, with widespread applications in control systems operating under conditions ranging 
from mild to extremely harsh. [2] 
 Electrochemical migration (ECM) is a critical phenomenon in the corrosion of electronic 
devices. This process takes place when two closely spaced biased electrodes are connected 
through an aqueous electrolyte resulting in corrosion followed by migration of ionic species. 
Under favorable conditions, ECM leads to the formation of metallic dendrites that can bridge 
the two conductors, potentially causing an electric short circuit in a short period. If a dense 
water layer connects two metallic conductors, it acts as an electrolyte, allowing metal at the 
anode to dissolve into metal ions. These ions, depending on their thermodynamic properties 
and stability, migrate through the electrolyte to the cathode, where they can redeposit in their 
metallic form, like the process of electroplating. [3] 
  Residues on the surface of printed circuit boards (PCBs) significantly impact humidity 
resistance and contribute to corrosion issues in electronics. Cleanliness is crucial, as even trace 
contaminants from various sources can affect performance. These contaminants can be either 
ionic or nonionic, with ionic residues posing a greater risk due to their interaction with 
humidity. In general, contamination on a PCBA or similar electronic components arises from 
three main sources: (i) manufacturing process-related contamination, (ii) improper or 
suboptimal handling at different production stages, and (iii) contamination settling on the parts 
during the service life due to inadequate packaging. [4] 
 The reliability of printed circuit boards (PCBs) is critical across many sectors, from consumer 
electronics to automotive and aerospace applications. Depending on the packaging of the 
electronic equipment, external aerosol contamination, such as road salt in automotive 
environments, can settle on PCB surfaces. This chemical deposition can promote the formation 
of condensed water layers, ultimately leading to failure. 
1.1 MOTIVATION OF THE THESIS 
 
Corrosion-related failures of printed circuit boards (PCBs) threaten the reliability of modern 
electronic devices, especially as they are increasingly used in harsh or uncontrolled 
environments. While moisture and contamination are known to promote electrochemical 
migration and corrosion, there is limited understanding of how specific aerosol components 
such as road salts (NaCl, CaCl₂, MgCl₂) influence conductive water layer formation and 
corrosion processes under DC voltage. This is particularly relevant for industries like 
automotive and industrial electronics, where exposure to road salts and pollutants is common.

In Chapter 1, a brief introduction to printed circuit boards (PCBs), the concern of their failure, 
and the motivation behind this thesis was presented. This Chapter provides a detailed theoretical

It highlights how humidity and contaminants not only promote corrosion-related failures but

2.1 PRINTED CIRCUIT BOARD (PCB) 
 
A Printed Circuit Board (PCB) is a structure used to mechanically support and electrically 
connect electronic components in a unified circuit. It typically consists of a rigid, flexible, or 
hybrid non-conductive base material with conductive copper traces etched onto its surface. 
These traces act as electrical pathways that allow current to flow between components. Various 
electronic components such as resistors, diodes, inductors, and transistors are mounted on the 
PCB, enabling the circuit to perform its intended function efficiently and compactly. [5]  
A typical example of a PCB is presented in Figure 2.1. 
 
 
Figure 2.1. Example of a typical PCB [6] 
Almost every electronic device today contains at least one Printed Circuit Board (PCB). 
Whether it's a phone, computer, tablet, television, or even a car, PCBs are essential for enabling 
the complex functionality of modern electronics. 
The primary function of a PCB is to connect various electronic components and allow for 
communication between them. For instance, in a smartphone, the PCB connects the screen, 
buttons, processors, and other subsystems. Without this central board, these elements wouldn’t 
be able to interact, rendering the device inoperable. 
Beyond connectivity, PCBs also provide physical support and protection. Components 
mounted on the board are often shielded with metal layers that act as a Faraday cage, helping 
to protect sensitive electronics from external electromagnetic interference (EMI) and ensuring 
device reliability. [7]

2.1.1 Assembly 
 
Basic parts of PCBs: 
There are three main types of PCBs: single-sided, double-sided, and multilayer. Physically, 
PCBs can be rigid, flexible, or a combination of both. Regardless of these variations, all PCBs 
consist of four basic parts: 
1. A nonconductive substrate or laminate, 
2. Conductive circuits printed on or embedded within the substrate, and 
3. Solder mask 
4. Mounted electronic components 
A printed circuit board (PCB) consists of several key layers and components that work together 
to support and connect electronic devices. At its base is the nonconductive substrate or laminate, 
which provides mechanical support and electrical insulation. Among various substrate 
materials, FR4 (Flame Retardant 4) is the most widely used, it is made of glass fiber reinforced 
epoxy resin with a brominated flame retardant added to the epoxy matrix, offering excellent 
mechanical strength and flame resistance. On top of this substrate, conductive circuits, usually 
thin copper pathways, are printed or embedded to create electrical connections between 
components. Covering these circuits is the solder mask, a protective coating that insulates the 
copper traces, prevents oxidation, and stops solder from bridging unwanted areas during 
assembly. The solder mask leaves exposed pads where electronic components such as resistors, 
capacitors, and chips are mounted and soldered, completing the functional circuit on the PCB. 
[8] , [9] 
Electronic components are mounted directly onto the surface of printed circuit boards (PCBs) 
using Surface Mount Technology (SMT). Before the introduction of Surface Mount 
Technology (SMT) in the early 1980s, the dominant method for assembling electronic 
components onto printed circuit boards (PCBs) was the Lead through-hole technology (LTH). 
In this approach, components with long metal leads were inserted into holes drilled in the PCB 
and then soldered in place, typically using wave soldering, where the underside of the board 
was passed over a wave of molten solder. This process created strong mechanical and electrical 
connections and is still used today for certain large or high-stress components, such as 
connectors and transformers. 
In contrast, SMT revolutionized the electronics industry by allowing components to be mounted 
directly onto the surface of the PCB, eliminating the need for drilled holes. Boards using SMT 
are fabricated similarly to through-hole boards up to the point of solder mask and plating 
application. After that, the processes diverge. In SMT, solder paste, a mixture of fine solder 
particles and flux, is screen-printed onto flat pads on the board. Components, known as Surface 
Mount Devices (SMDs), are placed into the paste using automated pick-and-place machines. 
The entire board is then passed through a reflow oven, where the solder paste melts, solidifies, 
and forms reliable electrical connections between the component leads and the PCB. 
This shift from through-hole to SMT, along with the move from wave soldering to reflow 
soldering, has enabled the production of smaller, lighter, and more complex electronic devices 
with significantly higher component densities and faster manufacturing times. [10]

2.1.2 Soldering 
 
Soldering is a process used to join two or more metal surfaces by melting a filler metal, known 
as solder, which has a melting temperature below 450°C. The solder forms a strong electrical 
and mechanical bond between the metals as it cools, allowing for both structural stability and 
electrical conductivity. 
The solder is typically made from an alloy of tin and lead, where tin has a melting point of 
about 235°C and lead around 327°C. Interestingly, when tin and lead are mixed, the resulting 
alloy has a significantly lower melting point of approximately 183°C, known as a eutectic alloy. 
This property makes it ideal for use in electronics, as it allows for precise and reliable 
connections without damaging sensitive components. 
During soldering, the alloy is melted using a hot iron typically heated to around 316°C (600°F). 
As the molten solder cools, it creates a durable bond between the metal parts. 
Due to environmental and health concerns associated with lead, lead-free solders often made 
with tin, silver, and copper are increasingly being adopted in accordance with global 
regulations. [11] , [12] 
Types of Soldering Based on Heat and Strength 
• 
Soft Soldering (90 °C – 450 °C) 
Soft soldering is the most common method and operates at the lowest temperatures. It uses 
filler metals with melting points below 450°C, typically in the range of 150°C to 350°C. 
The solder is often a tin-lead alloy, with a typical eutectic composition of 63% tin and 37% 
lead, melting at 183°C. Other compositions like 50:50 or 58:42 (electrician’s solder) are 
also used. A flux is necessary to clean the metal surfaces and prevent oxidation. Common 
fluxes include zinc chloride (effective but corrosive) and rosin (non-corrosive, used in 
electronics). Heating is usually done with a soldering iron or a small blowtorch. This 
process is ideal for wiring, small component assembly, and light-duty metal joining. 
Although soft soldering causes minimal thermal stress, the resulting joints are mechanically 
weak and not suitable for load-bearing applications. 
• 
Hard or Silver Soldering (Above 450 °C) 
Hard soldering involves higher temperatures, generally between 600°C and 900°C, and 
creates stronger joints than soft soldering. It uses silver-based alloys as filler metals. These 
are stronger and have higher melting points than those used in soft soldering. The process 
requires flux paste, which is applied to the joint area before heating with a blowtorch. The 
==PAGE:12==
6 
 
flux helps clean the metal and improve bond strength. Hard soldering is commonly used in 
jewelry, metal fabrication, and mechanical assemblies where higher strength is needed. 
• 
Brazing (Above 450 °C) 
Brazing is similar to hard soldering but typically uses even higher melting point filler 
metals. The base metals are not melted but heated enough to allow molten filler to flow by 
capillary action. This method creates the strongest joints among soldering types. It is widely 
used for structural metal repairs, pipe joining, and industrial applications. Brazing ensures 
strong, durable bonds and is suitable for both ferrous and non-ferrous metals. [11], [12] 
In the manufacturing of electronic assemblies, particularly printed circuit boards (PCBs), wave 
soldering and reflow soldering are the two most widely used automated soft soldering 
techniques. Both methods are essential for forming reliable electrical and mechanical 
connections between components and PCB, but they differ in their processes and applications. 
Types of Soldering in PCB Assembly 
• 
Wave soldering: is commonly used alongside the Lead Through-Hole (LTH) assembly 
technique. In this process, a wave soldering machine generates a ripple of molten solder 
in a heated tank. Through-hole populated circuit boards are conveyed over the wave, 
allowing the solder to contact the underside of the board just once, forming joints in a 
controlled and synchronized manner. [10] 
 
• 
Reflow soldering: is a process where solder paste is first applied to the contact pads of 
a PCB, placing components on top, and heating the assembly to melt the solder and 
form connections. Instead of a soldering iron, heated air in a reflow oven melts the 
solder. This method is commonly used for soldering surface-mount technology (SMT) 
components. [13] 
2.2  CORROSION MECHANISMS IN ELECTRONICS 
 
There are two main corrosion-related failure modes in PCBs: electrochemical migration 
(ECM), which was briefly introduced in Chapter 1, and conductive anodic filament (CAF) 
formation. Both mechanisms are driven by the presence of moisture and ionic contaminants 
and can significantly compromise the long-term reliability of electronic assemblies. 
2.2.1 Electrochemical migration (ECM) 
 
Electrochemical Migration (ECM) is a critical failure mechanism in electronics that affects the 
reliability of assemblies, particularly under humid or climate-stressed conditions. It occurs 
when metal ions migrate across insulating surfaces under the influence of electric fields and the 
presence of moisture. This process can lead to intermittent malfunctions, permanent short 
circuits, and in severe cases, even overheating or fire hazards. Understanding the conditions 
that trigger ECM and its underlying mechanisms is essential for developing effective prevention 
strategies. 
Electrochemical migration involves three main steps. The first is anodic metal dissolution, 
where moisture reduces surface insulation and triggers electrolysis at the anode. This activates 
metal ions, which dissolve and begin to migrate across the surface. The second step is the 
migration of metal ions, which depends on voltage, spacing, and ion diffusion. The direction 
==PAGE:13==
7 
 
of migration is determined by the balance between electric and concentration gradients. The 
final step is the deposition of metal ions, where bridge formation occurs through metal 
deposition at the cathode or salt precipitation at the anode which can lead to dendrite growth 
from the cathode. The structure of these bridges varies with ion concentration and field strength, 
forming either dendritic or smoother growths. [14] All steps are illustrated in Figure 2.3 below.

2.2.2 Conductive Anodic Filament (CAF) 
 
In contrast to dendritic metal growth during ECM, Conductive Anodic Filament (CAF) occurs 
within the interior of the PCB, typically along the glass fiber/epoxy interface. The conductive 
filaments in CAF are primarily composed of copper-containing compounds or degraded 
products, rather than pure metal deposits, and they propagate from the anode toward the cathode 
under the influence of an electric field and moisture ingress. [1] CAF is a failure mechanism 
caused by electrochemical migration within the laminate, leading to the formation of these 
internal filaments that can bridge adjacent conductors. This process results in reduced insulation 
resistance or short circuits, particularly under high-humidity conditions, and can significantly 
compromise the reliability and performance of electronic devices. 
Conductive Anodic Filament (CAF) is more likely to develop under specific conditions such 
as high humidity, which allows moisture to penetrate PCB layers and promote ion migration. 
High voltage stress across the layers can further drive this migration, while prolonged 
operational or environmental stress accelerates filament growth. Additionally, material and 
manufacturing defects, such as poor resin systems, inadequate glass fiber bonding, or 
contamination can create pathways that facilitate CAF formation. The consequences of CAF 
include short circuits caused by unintended conductive paths between circuit nodes, as well as 
degraded insulation resistance, which can compromise signal integrity and overall circuit 
performance. 
The CAF (Conductive Anodic Filament) failure process begins with electrically driven ion 
migration, where a high voltage difference between two conductors on a PCB causes metal 
ions, typically copper, to migrate from the anode to the cathode, much like in an electrolytic 
cell. Moisture plays a critical role, as the PCB substrate can absorb water from the environment. 
This moisture creates conductive pathways and dissolves metal ions, enabling their movement 
under the electric field. As these ions migrate, they undergo electrochemical reactions and 
gradually deposit metal within the PCB material. Over time, this leads to the formation and 
growth of conductive filaments that may eventually bridge the gap between the anode and 
cathode, resulting in short circuits or reduced insulation resistance. The presence of material or 
manufacturing defects, such as voids, cracks, uneven glass fibers, or residual contaminants, can 
significantly accelerate this process by facilitating ion migration. Additionally, environmental 
stresses like temperature fluctuations and mechanical strain can alter the internal structure of 
the PCB, further promoting filament growth and increasing the risk of failure. [15]

2.3 ROLE OF HUMIDITY AND CONTAMINANTS IN PCB CORROSION 
 
 In electronic assemblies, corrosion can arise from different electrochemical mechanisms, two 
of the most relevant being galvanic corrosion and electrolysis. Galvanic corrosion occurs due 
to the natural electrochemical potential difference between two dissimilar metals in the 
presence of an electrolyte. In this spontaneous redox process, the more reactive metal (the 
anode) corrodes, while the less reactive metal (the cathode) is protected. In contrast, electrolysis 
is driven by an externally applied electrical current in a conductive environment. This can lead 
to rapid and often aggressive corrosion, regardless of the inherent reactivity of the metals 
involved. [16], [17] A representation of both galvanic and electrolytic corrosion can be seen in

and cathodic half-cell reactions. This division highlights the electron flow and the 
electrochemical nature of the process. 
Anode: is the site where the oxidation of the metal takes place, meaning the metal dissolves 
into the electrolyte as positively charged ions, releasing electrons in the process. The general 
anodic reaction can be written as:

reduction reaction at the cathode, typically involving species in the electrolyte, such as 
dissolved oxygen or hydrogen ions, allowing the corrosion process to continue.

Role of Humidity 
Humidity refers to the amount of water vapor in the air. The most common primary 
measurements of humidity include absolute humidity, relative humidity, and specific humidity. 
One of the key factors influencing corrosion on a printed circuit board assembly (PCBA) 
surface is the formation of a water layer when the electronics are exposed to humid 
environments. The thickness of this water film depends on several variables, including the 
ambient humidity level, the temperature difference between the surrounding air and the PCBA 
surface, the surface topography of the PCBA, and the presence of hygroscopic ionic residues 
on the surface.[20] 
Absolute Humidity 
Absolute humidity refers to the amount of water vapor present in the air, typically measured in 
grams per cubic meter or grams per kilogram. In the atmosphere, absolute humidity ranges from 
nearly zero to about 30 grams per cubic meter.

Due point (DP) 
The dew point (DP) is the temperature to which a surface must be cooled under constant 
pressure for condensation to occur. This happens when the vapor pressure of the surrounding 
moist air reaches its saturation point. In terms of relative humidity (RH), the dew point is 
reached when the surface temperature drops low enough for the nearby air to become fully 
saturated (100% RH), causing water vapor to condense onto the surface. At this stage, the air 
can no longer retain additional moisture, resulting in the formation of liquid water on the cooler 
surface. From the absolute humidity (AH) perspective, this corresponds to the saturation vapor 
pressure being reached due to the lower surface temperature. 
The dew point is a crucial humidity-related parameter that significantly impacts the corrosion 
reliability of electronic devices. It determines both the rate and thickness of water film 
formation on electronic surfaces, especially in environments where temperature and humidity 
fluctuate throughout the day. Furthermore, the presence of hygroscopic residues on the PCBA 
surface can lower the dew point even further, increasing the risk of significant water layer 
accumulation.[20] 
 
Mollier diagram 
The Mollier diagram is a psychrometric chart that helps visualize the relationship between air 
temperature, relative humidity (RH), and moisture content. It can be used to determine the dew 
point temperature under specific environmental conditions. In the context of electronics, it 
illustrates how even small temperature drops on PCB surfaces can lead to condensation. 
Moreover, in the presence of hygroscopic contaminants, deliquescence can initiate moisture 
film formation at higher temperatures and lower RH values than classical condensation, further 
increasing corrosion risk.

determined to be around 17 °C. This implies that if the surface temperature of a PCBA drops 
approximately 8 °C below the ambient temperature, moisture will condense on the surface, 
resulting in the formation of a liquid water layer. [20]

2.3.1 Deliquescence and Electrolyte Formation 
 
As previously discussed, humidity plays a critical role in the reliability of electronic systems 
by enabling the formation of galvanic or electrolytic cells, which can ultimately lead to PCB 
failure. While classical condensation, governed by the dew point, typically requires high 
relative humidity close to 100 percent to form a continuous water film on clean surfaces, 
hygroscopic surface contaminants can promote moisture accumulation through a different 
mechanism known as deliquescence. 
Deliquescence is a moisture-induced phase transformation in which a solid salt absorbs water 
vapor from the air and dissolves into an aqueous solution when the relative humidity exceeds a 
specific threshold called the Deliquescence Relative Humidity (DRH).  
As illustrated in Figure 2.6, the process begins with the adsorption of water molecules and 
capillary condensation on the salt surface. When the ambient relative humidity (RHambient) is 
below the deliquescence relative humidity (RH₀), the air is too dry for dissolution, so the 
hygroscopic residue, such as dried salt, remains solid with only a thin adsorbed water layer. 
Once RHambient reaches RH₀, the chemical potential of the vapor equals that of a saturated 
solution, initiating the formation of a thin electrolyte film and gradual salt dissolution, marking 
the transition from solid to liquid. As humidity rises above RH₀, more water is absorbed, 
creating a clear, stable saturated solution where all soluble solids dissolve and excess salt 
remains solid. At very high humidity, the solution becomes diluted and unsaturated by 
absorbing additional water, causing droplets to grow larger and less concentrated. [20] 
==PAGE:18==
12 
 
 
Figure 2.6. An overview of deliquescence phenomenon. [20] 
This mechanism is particularly important in electronics, because deliquescent salts such as 
NaCl, CaCl2 and MgCl2, often deposited on PCB surfaces through environmental aerosols, can 
initiate the formation of electrolyte layers even at moderate relative humidity levels. These 
layers can support electrochemical reactions, significantly increasing the likelihood of failure 
mechanisms such as Electrochemical Migration (ECM) and Conductive Anodic Filament 
(CAF) under environmental conditions that would otherwise seem non-critical. 
The threshold at which each salt begins to absorb moisture and dissolve depends on its specific 
DRH. Representative DRH values for common hygroscopic salts are summarized in Table 1.

2.4 EFFECT OF CONTAMINANTS 
2.4.1 Sources of Contaminants on PCBs 
 
Contaminants on Printed Circuit Boards (PCBs) can originate either from manufacturing 
processes or from external environmental exposure during the device's service life. These 
contaminants contribute significantly to failures such as corrosion, leakage currents, and 
electrochemical migration (ECM), especially in the presence of moisture. Both ionic and non-
ionic residues can be present in each category, but their origin determines how they interact 
with the system and when they are introduced. 
2.4.1.1 Contamination from Manufacturing Processes 
 
During PCB fabrication and assembly, several chemical and physical processes can leave 
behind residues that compromise long-term reliability. These include soldering, etching, and 
incomplete cleaning steps. 
==PAGE:19==
13 
 
• 
Non-ionic contaminants include substances like oils, grease, and resin. While these 
do not have conductive properties, they can interfere with adhesion or coating 
performance. 
• 
Ionic contaminants, on the other hand, consist of salts, acids, flux activators, and 
plating chemicals. These are conductive and significantly more harmful because they 
can cause leakage currents, corrosion, or short circuits, especially in the presence of 
moisture. 
Ionic residues can be introduced during PCB fabrication or assembly, such as from incomplete 
cleaning after soldering or etching. For instance, flux residues from the soldering process or 
etching chemicals used during patterning can remain on the board. 
Furthermore, moisture can become trapped within PCB layers or under components. When 
combined with ionic residues, this creates an ideal environment for electrochemical reactions, 
greatly increasing the risk of failure through dendritic growth or short circuits. [23] 
 
Furthermore, specifically regarding residues from soldering processes, Piotrowska et al. 
investigated the transformation of flux residues remaining on PCBAs after reflow soldering. 
Their study showed that these residues, often concealed beneath surface-mounted components, 
undergo significant morphological changes when exposed to high humidity conditions. Highly 
hygroscopic flux residues increase the absorption of moisture from the surroundings, which 
thickens the water layer on the surface. The greater the dissolution and ionization of these 
residues into the condensed water film, the higher the leakage current, potentially resulting in 
electronic failure modes such as electrochemical migration. This enhanced moisture absorption 
promotes the formation of a thin, conductive water layer that facilitates ion transport, triggering 
electrochemical corrosion and migration. These effects are particularly severe in areas where 
residues are trapped and drying is limited, reinforcing the critical importance of thorough 
residue removal and maintaining process cleanliness to prevent corrosion-related failures. [24] 
 
2.4.1.2 Contamination from external sources 
After manufacturing, printed circuit boards (PCBs) are susceptible to contamination from 
various external sources during storage, handling, or field operation. These environmental 
contaminants include chlorides, sulfates, nitrates, bromides, and dust particles. Among them, 
hygroscopic residues are especially problematic because they attract and retain moisture, 
enabling the formation of thin electrolyte layers that initiate electrochemical migration (ECM). 
Chloride contamination is particularly well studied due to its strong influence on ECM in tin 
(Sn) and Sn-based solder alloys. Typical chloride-containing salts include NaCl, CaCl₂, and 
MgCl₂. These compounds are commonly found in real-world environments, especially as 
components of road deicing agents (road salts), making them a frequent source of 
contamination in automotive and outdoor electronics. 
Due to their hygroscopic nature, these salts lower the humidity level at which condensation 
begins. Specifically, they can absorb moisture and dissolve into a liquid phase when the ambient 
relative humidity exceeds their deliquescence relative humidity (DRH), as shown in Table 1. 
This enables capillary condensation to occur even under moderate environmental conditions, 
especially within surface crevices or contaminant residues. 
==PAGE:20==
14 
 
Once an electrolyte layer is present, chlorides promote ECM through multiple mechanisms: 
• 
They enhance electrolyte conductivity, accelerating ion transport. 
• 
Chloride ions participate in anodic dissolution by breaking down or preventing the 
formation of protective oxide layers on Sn surfaces, either directly or through the 
formation of reactive chlorine species (e.g., Cl₂ gas). 
• 
They contribute to the formation of corrosion products such as Sn(OH)₄, SnO₂, and 
metal salts like AgCl and CuCl₂, resulting from intensified anodic and cathodic 
reactions. 
Interestingly, a study showed that while moderate chloride levels encourage dendritic growth, 
excessively high concentrations (e.g., >1000 ppm) may inhibit it. This happens because the 
number of precipitates increases due to the presence of chlorides, which act against dendrite 
growth, as fewer metal ions can reach the cathode to be deposited. [19] , [2]

2.5.2 Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) 
 
Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) is one of the most widely used techniques for 
characterizing nanomaterials and nanostructures. With a magnification range from 10× to over 
300,000×, SEM enables detailed analysis of specimens with resolutions reaching a few 
nanometers. Beyond surface topography, SEM can also provide insights into a material's 
chemical composition, crystal orientation, and stress distribution. 
Much like light microscopes rely on light for imaging, electron microscopes use electrons. 
SEMs generate images by detecting electrons that are reflected or ejected from the near-surface 
region of a sample. Since electrons have a much shorter wavelength than light, SEMs offer 
significantly higher resolution than traditional optical microscopes.[27] 
Interaction of Electron Beam with Specimen: 
When the primary electron beam hits a sample, it scatters and loses energy within a teardrop-
shaped interaction volume (ranging from <100 nm to ~10 μm deep). The size of this volume 
depends on the beam energy, atomic number, and density of the material (Figure 2.8). This 
interaction produces: 
• 
Backscattered Electrons (BSE): High-energy electrons deflected back toward the 
detector. Used to distinguish elements by atomic number, heavier elements appear 
brighter. Resolution ~1000 nm. 
• 
Secondary Electrons (SE): Low-energy electrons emitted from the surface, mainly used 
to show surface topography. High resolution (<10 nm). 
• 
Auger Electrons: Low-energy electrons from the surface that provide chemical 
composition details, especially light elements. Used mainly in materials science with a 
scanning Auger electron spectrometer. 
These signals are detected, amplified, and converted into digital images for analysis. Specimen 
current can also be used to generate images. [28]

16 
 
 
Figure 2.8. Interaction volume, showing the depth of penetration of the electron beam. [28] 
 
2.5.3 Energy Dispersive X-ray Spectroscopy (EDX) 
 
Energy Dispersive Spectroscopy (EDS) is a powerful microanalytical technique used to identify 
and quantify elements in a sample, often working in combination with Scanning Electron 
Microscopy (SEM) or Scanning Transmission Electron Microscopy (STEM) to generate 
elemental maps. EDS operates by detecting characteristic X-rays emitted when the sample is 
exposed to a high-energy electron beam, causing inner-shell electrons to be ejected and replaced 
by outer-shell electrons, which release excess energy as X-rays. These X-rays have unique 
energy signatures for each element, allowing for precise identification and quantification. A 
Silicon Drift Detector (SDD) captures these emissions, and specialized software analyzes the 
data to determine the sample’s composition. By integrating EDS with SEM, researchers can 
not only examine surface topography but also gain detailed chemical insights, making it a 
valuable tool for material characterization, failure analysis, and contamination detection. [29] 
 
Figure 2.9 illustrates how EDS functions. The letters K, L, and M represent electron shells, 
where K (n=1) is closest to the nucleus. The Greek letters α and β indicate the energy transition 
levels. Lα or Kα refer to electron relaxations from M to L or L to K, respectively, while a 
transition from M to K is called Kβ. This naming system is known as Siegbahn notation.

3.2 PCB SAMPLE PREPARATION 
 
To ensure the absence of residues from the manufacturing process, the PCBs were thoroughly 
cleaned prior to testing. Surface cleanliness is of utmost importance for electrochemical 
experiments, particularly in this thesis where the objective is to study dendrite growth due to 
electrochemical migration (ECM) without interference from surface contamination. An 
accurate cleaning protocol was therefore followed: the PCBs were first rinsed with ultra-pure 
Milli-Q water, followed by ethanol. Compressed air was then used to dry the surface 
completely. This entire process was repeated three times to ensure maximum cleanliness and 
minimize the risk of ionic contamination.

4.1.3 Discussion 
From the observations in Section 4.1, it can be concluded that water is the more suitable solvent 
compared to isopropanol (IPA). In the case of IPA, the salt did not dissolve properly and was 
seen to accumulate around the perimeter of the 3D-printed boundary, leading to uneven 
distribution. 
Condensation was observed only at locations where salt had been placed, even with the cooling 
stage turned off, as long as relative humidity exceeded 75% (the DRH of NaCl) (Figures 4.3–
4.6). This indicates that the presence of salt promotes localized condensation due to its 
hygroscopic nature. In contrast, the clean PCB (Figure 4.4) showed no condensation, even at 
98% RH. 
Comparing Figures 4.5 (NaCl at 1 µg/cm²) and 4.6 (NaCl at 4 µg/cm²), it is also evident that 
higher salt concentration leads to more extensive condensation. Therefore, condensation 
appears to increase both with rising humidity and higher salt concentration, which is consistent 
with the known behavior of hygroscopic salts.

results indicate that exposure time does not significantly influence droplet formation when the

4.2.1.3 Discussion 
 
In this experiment, both condensation analysis and electrochemical testing were performed. 
Combining these two approaches reveals several results that warrant further discussion. The 
results from Figures 4.7 to 4.15 show that condensation occurs earlier when salt is present. As 
seen in Figure 4.7 (clean PCB), no condensation was observed before 1 hour, whereas samples 
with salt showed earlier droplet formation. From Figure 4.16, comparing 1 hour to 4 hours, it 
is evident that over time, the number of droplets increases while their size decreases due to 
merging. Regarding concentration, at 0.6 µg/cm², the droplet size was more uniform compared 
to 3 µg/cm². Among all samples, MgCl₂ at 3 µg/cm² produced the largest droplets, as shown 
clearly in Figure 4.15, probably due to the low deliquescence point (see Table 1). 
From Figure 4.17 it can be observed that there is a noticeable increase in current for all salts 
with the increase of concentration. More specifically, MgCl₂ remained stable over time, with a 
sudden peak approximately 3 mA occurring at around 5 hours. This peak corresponds to 
dendrite formation, as visually confirmed in Figure 4.15, where dendrite growth is observed at 
==PAGE:42==
36 
 
the 5-hour mark. Where can be seen as well that the droplet size was bigger than 1mm (pitch 
distance) which is the critical diameter of droplet in order dendrite to form. 
NaCl exhibited the most aggressive electrochemical behavior, with multiple high-current peaks 
appearing after 3 hours. This contrasts with the initial assumption that NaCl would have the 
least impact compared to CaCl₂ and MgCl₂ due to its lower hygroscopicity. However, once 
water is present, NaCl dissociates quickly, and the highly mobile Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions form 
conductive pathways under high humidity and applied voltage. [35] , [36]  
A key reason for the high mobility of Na⁺ is its relatively small, hydrated radius. Although Mg²⁺ 
and Ca²⁺ have smaller ionic radius, their higher charges attract more water molecules, making 
their hydrated size larger. This larger hydration shell slows their movement, while Na⁺ ions, 
with a smaller hydrated radius, move faster and contribute to the stronger electrochemical 
effects seen with NaCl. [37] Additionally, under alkaline conditions near the cathode, 
hydroxide ions (OH⁻) can react with Mg²⁺ and Ca²⁺ to form insoluble precipitates such as 
Mg(OH)₂ and Ca(OH)₂. These precipitates can accumulate near the cathode, further affecting 
electrochemical behavior by blocking ion transport and disrupting dendrite growth. Differences 
in surface wetting and salt spreading may have also contributed to the electrochemical activity 
observed.

visible corrosion was observed, regardless of salt type or concentration. However, at the higher 
voltage of 10 V, dendritic structures appeared in all cases. Moreover, the dendrite density and 
size increased with rising salt concentration, confirming that both voltage level and ionic 
contamination play a critical role in promoting electrochemical migration and potential failure 
on PCB surfaces. Additionally, unlike NaCl, CaCl₂ and especially MgCl₂ samples exhibited the 
presence of precipitates alongside the dendrites, indicating further chemical complexity in the 
corrosion products.

4.2.2.6 Discussion 
 
In this experiment, condensation was predefined, as the salt solution was directly placed inside 
the 3D-printed boundary. Therefore, the focus of this test was on the electrochemical behavior 
under conditions where condensation had already occurred. Starting with Figures 4.18 and 4.20, 
it can be observed that at low voltage, the current remains significantly low for all salts. 
However, even at a low salt concentration, the current is higher than that of pure water, proving 
that even a concentration as low as 0.6 µg/cm² can influence electrochemical behavior. As the 
voltage increases to 10 V (Figures 4.19 and 4.21), it is evident that the current also increases. 
In this test, even though the surface concentration was the same for all salts, the actual quantity 
added also depends on the molar mass of each salt and each ion individually, as shown in the 
Appendix (Table A3). The percentages of cation mass and chlorine mass remain consistent 
across all concentrations. However, at very low concentrations, even small differences in 
chloride content could play a critical role, since the absolute amount is minimal. Given that a 
==PAGE:57==
51 
 
higher number of chloride ions likely contributes to greater ionic conductivity and higher 
current levels in solution. [39] , [40] , [41] This may explain why NaCl shows a more delayed 
and smaller current peak compared to the other two salts, MgCl₂ and CaCl₂ (Figure 4.19a). At 
higher concentrations, the amount of chlorine is sufficient and roughly the same for all salts 
(Table A3). Therefore, the main influence on electrochemical behavior comes from the cation. 
At the middle concentration, all salts exhibit more or less the same behavior. However, at a 
concentration of 3 µg/cm², all salts begin with a current peak, with MgCl₂ maintaining a higher 
current than the others before dropping, after 20 minutes, to the lowest current among them. 
Comparing the different concentrations (Figure 4.21), it can be seen that the lowest 
concentration shows the lowest current at the beginning of the experiment for all salts, while 
the 1.5 µg/cm² and 3 µg/cm² concentrations display very similar behavior. However, after 
around 5 minutes, the current decreases for all salts compared to that of pure water, which 
reaches a saturation point at about 5 minutes and remains stable throughout the experiment. 
One possible explanation for this behavior is that at higher voltages, the salts interact more 
aggressively, leading to significant bubbling at the electrode surface. This bubbling can 
physically disrupt or break the dendrites formed during current flow. As a result, even though 
the concentration increases, the current does not rise proportionally, as dendrite formation is 
repeatedly interrupted. The breaking of dendrites causes sudden drops in current. This 
phenomenon is visually confirmed by the images in the appendix, where a dendrite appears in 
one frame and is broken in the next (Figures A13 and A14), as well as in the SEM image shown 
in Figure 4.34. 
This behavior is also supported by Figures 4.23–4.26. In Figures 4.24–4.26, it is evident that in 
salt-containing solutions, intense and frequent bubble formation occurs at 10 V, likely 
disturbing the growth and stability of dendrites. In contrast, such bubbling is not observed in 
pure water (Figure 4.23), allowing dendrites to grow more continuously and remain intact. 
These observations are consistent with the findings in [42], which describe how hydrogen 
bubble growth during electrolysis significantly influences local current density and 
supersaturation near the cathode. The study suggests that higher ionic strength and applied 
voltage promote more frequent bubble nucleation and detachment, which can mechanically 
disrupt dendritic structures. This supports the hypothesis that intense bubble activity in salt 
solutions hindered dendrite development, whereas the relative absence of bubbles in pure water 
allowed dendrites to form and grow more prominently. 
Another important observation from Figures 4.23–4.26 relates to corrosion behavior. In pure 
water (Figure 4.23), although dendrite formation occurs, no visible corrosion is observed on 
either electrode. In contrast, all salt solutions show corrosion at the anode, with MgCl₂ 
exhibiting the least corrosion among them. Notably, NaCl has a clean cathode, whereas CaCl₂, 
and especially MgCl₂, show significant surface changes and deposits. 
In general, salts with higher chloride content, such as CaCl₂ and MgCl₂, release two chloride 
ions per formula unit, contributing to higher ionic strength and potentially more aggressive 
corrosion compared to salts like NaCl, which releases only one. However, in this study, the 
total mass of chloride was intentionally kept constant across all samples by adjusting the mass 
of each salt based on its molar mass and stoichiometry (Table A3). Therefore, the observed 
differences in corrosion and dendrite formation are attributed primarily to the nature of the 
cations. 
• 
MgCl₂ shows early dendrite formation but minimal corrosion at the anode, possibly 
due to the low mobility of Mg²⁺ ions caused by their large hydrated radius. 
==PAGE:58==
52 
 
• 
CaCl₂ leads to more intense anode corrosion, likely due to higher mobility and more 
aggressive electrochemical activity. 
• 
NaCl exhibits moderate corrosion and dendrite formation, driven by the fast-moving 
Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions forming conductive pathways. 
At the cathode, additional phenomena are observed. 
• 
In NaCl, the cathode remains clean. 
• 
In CaCl₂ and especially MgCl₂, visible deposits appear on the cathode surface. These 
are not traditional corrosion products but are likely precipitated hydroxides formed 
under alkaline conditions from the water reduction reaction: 
             2H₂O + 2e⁻ → H₂(g) + 2OH⁻ 
(4.1) 
 
• 
The hydroxide ions (OH⁻) react with divalent cations, forming insoluble compounds: 
 
Mg²⁺ + 2OH⁻ → Mg(OH)₂ 
 
(4.2) 
            Ca²⁺ + 2OH⁻ → Ca(OH)₂ 
(4.3) 
These precipitates can be observed in the EDS analysis (Figure 4.35) and optical microscope 
images (Figure 4.31-4.32) and supported by the pH measurements (Figures 4.27–4.29). MgCl₂, 
and to a lesser extent CaCl₂, show a more affected cathode surface due to increased precipitate 
formation compared to NaCl. Therefore, the decrease in current is not caused solely by 
hydrogen bubbling at the cathode, but also by the formation of oxide and hydroxide precipitates, 
which hinder dendrite formation. This observation aligns with findings from [2]. These 
precipitates, especially in the cases of MgCl₂ and CaCl₂, can block the surface or capture ions, 
further limiting ion transport and affecting electrochemical activity. Since anodic and cathodic 
reactions must proceed together, blocking the cathode with precipitates can also slow down 
anodic dissolution. This coupling effect helps explain why less corrosion was observed at the 
anode in these cases, despite the presence of chloride.

5 CONCLUSION  
• 
Water is the best solvent, as it enables uniform salt dissolution, unlike IPA, where salt 
tends to accumulate unevenly. 
• 
 Salt type and concentration influence condensation behavior: clean PCBs show no 
condensation even at high humidity when the cooling stage is off, and delayed onset 
compared to salt-contaminated samples when cooling stage is on. MgCl₂, being the 
most hygroscopic, leads to larger droplets. 
• 
Higher salt concentrations result in less uniform droplet size, and over time, droplets 
merge reducing in number but increasing in size. 
• 
Salt concentration influences electrochemical behavior, with higher concentrations 
resulting in stronger current peaks due to increased ion availability. 
• 
Dendrite formation is triggered when droplets reach a critical size of 1mm (pitch 
distance), linking condensation behavior directly to electrochemical failure. 
• 
Na⁺ ions have a smaller hydrated radius than Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺, allowing faster ion 
mobility and causing multiple current peaks in electrochemical tests. 
• 
At 10 V, current drops in salt solutions are caused by both hydrogen gas evolution 
(bubbling), which disrupts dendrite growth, and hydroxide precipitates (Mg(OH)₂, 
Ca(OH)₂) at the cathode, which block ion transport and further suppress dendrite 
formation. 
• 
These effects were not observed in pure water, where dendrites remained stable, current 
stayed consistent, and no corrosion was detected. 
• 
Corrosion intensity varies by salt: precipitate formation near the cathode may reduce 
anode dissolution, which explains why MgCl₂ showed the cleanest anode among the 
salts.

6 SUGGESTIONS FOR FUTURE WORK  
Since this thesis introduced a substantial amount of new information, examining three different 
salts at three concentrations under two voltage levels, along with their effects on condensation, 
it provides a solid foundation for future work. Based on these findings, future experiments could 
follow the same procedure but extend over longer durations.  
For instance, clean PCB samples could be taken directly from the lab without the addition of 
any salt, then exposed to a real environment. By measuring the PCBs before and after 
environmental exposure, it would be possible to assess how many contaminants were naturally 
deposited. This could be followed by surface analysis and electrochemical testing under applied 
voltage to evaluate real-world behavior. 
In this study, the mass of cations and anions was calculated based on known salt concentrations. 
Future work could incorporate Inductively Coupled Plasma (ICP) measurements to precisely 
quantify ionic content before and after testing, enabling a more accurate correlation with 
observed electrochemical activity. 
Finally, based on the experiments and results presented in this thesis, future work could involve 
testing coated PCBs under the same conditions to directly compare their performance with 
uncoated samples. While the tin finish protects the copper from oxidation it remains susceptible 
to corrosion from moisture and salts. Applying a conformal or protective coating over the tin 
layer provides an additional barrier that significantly reduces the exposure of the tin and 
underlying copper to corrosive agents such as salt deposits and water droplets. [43] This 
approach would help show how well different coatings protect PCBs from corrosion caused by 
moisture and salt.
