Abstract
Australia’s goals of increased penetration of renewable energy such as wind energy will inevitably lead
to increased uncertainty of the ramps in net load (load minus non-dispatchable renewable generation). This
increased uncertainty requires conventional generators to be more ﬂexible to ramp up or down if needed, but
currently this ﬂexibility is not fully integrated in market processes. The provision of additional ﬂexibility may
cause a reduction in economic efﬁciency, consumer surplus and/or producer surplus as conventional generators
may need to modify their output from the optimal level in order to provide ﬂexibility to account for future
uncertainty. As a solution to this problem, the Midwest and Californian Independent System Operators have
proposed ﬂexible ramping products as a mechanism to manage the uncertainty in net load ramps in an economi-
cally preferable manner. The mechanism essentially aims to schedule conventional generators to provide enough
ramping capability, or "ﬂexibility", to satisfy a ﬂexible ramping capability requirement. This requirement is
designed to ensure a certain range of ramps in the next interval could be met, whether the ramps actually occur
or not. This study aims to explore the implementation of ﬂexible ramping products in the speciﬁc context of the
Australian National Electricity Market (NEM), to determine whether or not they can be an effective mechanism
for integrating variable renewable energy in Australia in the coming decades.
The study presents a simpliﬁed model of the Australian NEM, in which a unit commitment and economic
dispatch model is designed with ﬂexible ramping products and a ﬂexible ramping requirement. The simpliﬁcation
of the NEM includes a grouping of the ﬁve states into two regions, and an aggregation of generators by offered
ramping speed and actual marginal costs. Actual load and wind generation data from the 2014/15 ﬁnancial year
is implemented in the model to attempt to simulate the market in a realistic manner. As there are several inputs to
the model which are uncertain, the simulation is then performed with adjustments for a range of sensitivities for
future scenarios and technical constraints of the system. Then, as there are many qualitative indicators which are
impacted, the performance of the ﬂexible ramping product mechanism is then assessed in a range of qualitative
indicators, such as the total cost of the system, total proﬁts for generators (including incentives for generators
with faster ramping limits), price spikes, penalties for load shedding, penalties for not satisfying the ﬂexible
ramping requirement, curtailment of wind, revenue for ﬂexible ramping products, and reliance on transmission
between the two regions.
The results of the study show that, while several intervals in the year experience a reduction in cost from the
implementation of ﬂexible ramping products, the total annual costs of the system did not reduce in any scenario.

ABSTRACT
An increase in the ﬂexible ramping requirement constrains the system to the point where total costs increase over
the whole year. However, other beneﬁts were observed to occur in several of the scenarios, such as increases in
proﬁts of generators, incentives towards faster ramping generators and reductions in price spikes. These beneﬁts
were observed to occur along with an increase in costs. The implication of these results is that the ﬂexible
ramping products can provide some beneﬁts to the system, albeit with a relative trade-off in the form of increased
costs. It was found that intervals with large ramps had a much higher likelihood of beneﬁting from ﬂexible
ramping products in the form of a reduction in cost. With regards to reducing price spikes, it was observed that the
intervals with a reduction of price spikes occurred much more often in large peak loads in morning and evening
hours. Also it was observed that allowing ﬂexible ramping products to be transmitted between regions resulted
in a reduced cost of implementation, and with less wind curtailment, but reduced proﬁts and increased price spikes.
As the penetration of wind energy in the NEM is currently at a very low level compared to the systems
of MISO and CAISO, there are still a few decades where further research can be done, and lessons from
across the Paciﬁc can be learned. It is recommended that further research is undertaken in the ﬁeld, which
uses more years of data, and includes reserves in the model. Pre-dispatch data should also be incorporated
to deﬁne ramps more accurately, to obtain as accurate as possible results. This study has shown that ﬂexible
ramping products can provide potential beneﬁts at a cost, and as such it is important that the mechanism is
carefully designed if it is to be effective. The results in this study show that in some time intervals there is
a reduction in costs from the implementation of ﬂexible ramping products, and that it is much more likely
to reduce costs in intervals with a large ramp. It is therefore recommended that signiﬁcant effort is put into
undertaking further research into designing a dynamic ﬂexible ramping requirement, based on analysis of ramps
and time of day, day of the week, size of bid, wind generation, etc. This dynamic ﬂexible ramping requirement
should aim to minimizing costs by changing to an optimal level based on operating conditions. Alternatives to
ﬂexible ramping products such as changes to the ancillary services markets, and improved forecasting of wind
energy, are also important factors to integrating renewable energy, such as wind energy, into the NEM, and these
should be considered along with ﬂexible ramping products in a holistic approach to solving the ﬂexibility problem.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
This study begins with a literature review, which describes various ramping issues, the functioning of the
ﬂexible ramping product mechanism and how this mechanism is designed and modeled. The introduction then
includes a section introducing the Australian National Electricity Market, and describes how the ﬂexible ramping
products can be modeled in the Australian context.
Following this, the method then explains how the model is designed and built, using real data when available.
The model is then adjusted to analyze sensitivity to a range of future scenarios and technical constraints. The
results then present a multi-criteria analysis of various modeled scenarios. Two key results are then assessed -
the reduction in costs and the reduction in price spikes - over the entire 2014/15 ﬁnancial year, and then conclude
with a comparison to a prominent model from the literature review. The conclusion then summarizes the results
and gives recommendations regarding future research and development of ﬂexible ramping products in the NEM.
1.1
Literature review
According to the Midwest Independent System Operator (MISO), "managing the ramp is the biggest
challenge in MISO operations" and "the increasing penetration of renewables is escalating the issue" [15]. Both
MISO and the California Independent System Operator (CAISO) have undertaken signiﬁcant research, analysis
and modeling of mitigating the ramping issue by utilizing ﬂexible ramping products, as a way to better integrate
renewables into the electricity market.
1.1.1
Deﬁnitions
Net load is deﬁned as the load, or demand, minus non-dispatchable renewable generation (such as wind or
solar) and demand response.
A ramp is deﬁned as a sharp and sudden change (increase or decrease) in net load. This change includes
variations and uncertainties. The difﬁculty in mathematically deﬁning a ramp is discussed further in section
1.1.2.
Variations are expected changes in net load, such as forecasted changes for load increases or decreases, as
well as wind forecasts.
Uncertainties are unanticipated differences from the forecasted net load, due to forecast errors from load, or
wind/solar generation.

1.1.2
Ramping issues
Up and down ramping has been a feature of electricity systems for decades, however, due to the increasing
levels of penetration of non-dispatchable renewable generation (mainly wind and solar), both net load variations
and uncertainties have increased signiﬁcantly in recent years [30]. Measuring and deﬁning what mathematically
constitutes a ramping event is not universally agreed upon, with several methods and deﬁnitions being used in
various parts of the world [17]. For example, CAISO analyses ramps using monthly maximum 3 hour ramps,
which are calculated by ﬁnding the maximum 180 minute difference in net load, using one minute time interval
data [11]. Another study, [20] analyzed wind ramp events in Bonneville Power Administration using two different
deﬁnitions of a ramp event - the slope of change between two points, and the minimum and maximum values of
generation between two points. Further, [34] utilized the rate of change of wind plant power over a 10-minute
interval to deﬁne ramps, in a study focusing on forecasting wind power ramp events. There has also been
promising analysis of irradiance time series data using a swinging door algorithm [18]. The standard deviation
of load and wind forecast errors between two consecutive 10 minute intervals, multiplied by a varying factor (to
be discussed in more detail later in the report) were used by [31]. By these various deﬁnitions it can be seen that
a ramping event has not a strict mathematical deﬁnition, but depends on the system and the area in which it is
located.
Figure 1.1: One minute and one hour wind ramps of wind power in Texas on February 26, 2008 [28]
The rate of ramping (usually expressed in MW/min) depends on many system characteristics, such as climatic

conditions, technologies used, and the size of the network. Ramps can be very signiﬁcant in size and speed, such
as the one observed of all wind farms in Texas on February 26, 2008 in 1.1. This example shows a ramp of wind
downwards of approximately 1400 MW in 3 hours from 15:30 to 18:30.
As there is a range of deﬁnitions as to what constitutes a ramping event, the most logical method to deﬁne
a ramp for a particular system is to analyze historical data for that system, considering the length of dispatch
intervals and the magnitude of load. This could be done by ﬁnding the distribution of ramping events over various
time intervals, such as 3 hour, 90 minute, 1 hour, 30 minutes, 15 minutes and 5 minutes. Deﬁning what is the
most appropriate time interval(s) depends on the system operation and on the objective of the analysis.
As ramping in net load increases due to increases in variations and uncertainties from non-dispatchable
renewable generation, dispatchable generating units are required to have ﬂexibility to ramp up or ramp down [15].
This requirement imposes additional costs on the operation of the system, as, for example, excessive ramping
may cause additional wear and tear on the generators and generators may be required to be available to provide
ﬂexibility and therefore reduce their economic efﬁciency. Also, in practice, a shortage of ramp capability in the
network can lead to an overreliance on the interconnection with neighboring regions.
Speciﬁcally, increasing uncertainty may cause reductions in social welfare, as generators may be required to
be scheduled sub-optimally to provide ﬂexibility. In fact, it is predominantly the uncertainty part of the ramp
that requires ﬂexibility, as even in "certain" net load ramps, ﬂexibility would not necessarily be required, as
the net load ramp could still be scheduled optimally. The reduction in social welfare as a result of increased
uncertainty may be through a reduction in consumer surplus (through price spikes) or producer surplus (through
negative proﬁts in periods of overgeneration), which is explained in the following theoretical example. Consider
a simple network with 4 generating units: a wind farm, an expensive but "fast" generating unit which can
start-up/shut-down and ramp up/down quickly, a cheap but "slow" generating unit which can generate a constant
level for a low price but cannot ramp up/down or start-up/shut-down quickly, and a "medium" priced unit which
is in the middle of the previous two generators in terms of "ramping speed" and price. The characteristics of

1.1.3
Flexible ramping products
Flexible ramping products are new market products that aim to achieve an end goal of better integrating
non-dispatchable renewables like wind and solar into the electricity market. The introduction of these products
aims to do this by ensuring that there is sufﬁcient system capacity to serve intra-hour ramping of the hourly net
load forecast from one hour to the next [15]. In MISO, the products are to be integrated into the day ahead and
==PAGE:21==
1.1. LITERATURE REVIEW
5
real time markets, alongside the energy, regulation, spinning reserve and supplemental reserve currently traded
on these markets [15].
There are three main differences between ﬂexible ramping products and spinning/non-spinning reserves,
according to [29]. The ﬁrst is that ﬂexible ramping capacity is designated from one interval to respond to potential
changes in net load in later intervals, whereas spinning and non-spinning reserves are designated for the event of
a predeﬁned system contingency which occurs in the same interval. The second difference is that spinning and
non-spinning reserves are only dispatched if one of the predeﬁned contingencies occur, whereas ﬂexible ramping
capacity is dispatched to provide in later intervals based on least-cost principles. Finally, the third difference is the
ﬂexible ramping products can provide both a "down" ramp, where capacity can rapidly move downward if there is
an unexpected decrease in net load, whereas spinning and non-spinning reserves are deﬁned only as an "upward"
product [29] [32]. In addition, according to [15], the deployment of ﬂexible ramping products are embedded
into the dispatch function in the real-time and thus no additional system is required as part of deployment strategy.
Before a description of ﬂexible ramping product is given, there are a few others factors which may manage
the ramping issue which should be considered. For example, improved forecasting of wind and solar could
assist in reducing uncertainties surrounding non-dispatchable production and ramping in net load [11]. This is
logical, since as uncertainties are reduced, the need for ﬂexibility would also be reduced. Another factor is the
size of the geographical footprint, as a larger geographical footprint may reduce the coincidence of production
patterns [11]. Increased interconnection between regions allows greater sourcing of ramping generators and
therefore is a potential factor to better integrate renewables with improved management of ramping. Another
factor is the use of stochastic programming, as opposed to deterministic scheduling, which would attempt to
minimize costs over a range of possible net load realizations and their probabilities. Stochastic programming is
heuristic by nature and thus would not require ﬂexible ramping products. However, stochastic programming
requires signiﬁcant computational power, and SOs in the USA, Europe and Australia currently only implement
deterministic scheduling models [29].
Currently, mechanisms for ensuring ramp capability are not fully integrated into the market processes,
which means they may lead to operational challenges and may not provide the ﬂexibility to avoid price spikes.
The resources which provide much needed ﬂexibility must not be ﬁnancially harmed in doing so, which is
the fundamental justiﬁcation of a ﬂexible ramping product design. It is argued that, without ﬂexible ramping
products, ﬂexible resources may be harmed as low cost capacity may be held back in one interval to provide
ramp capability for future intervals, thus incurring an opportunity cost in the ﬁrst interval. Theoretically, if a
generator does hold back production to provide ramp capability for the next interval, there is no guarantee that
the generator will be compensated for the opportunity cost by providing the ﬂexibility to ramp if needed (as they
would not receive revenue if the ramp is not actually required). Flexible ramping products aim to integrate this
ﬂexibility to the market, to make the system better positioned to handle net load volatility in operating conditions,
to reduce price spikes or volatility [15].
To clearly illustrate this, consider the previous example with three dispatchable generators, one wind producer
and 5 time intervals, with a ramp up and ramp down event. In this instance, the example includes the use of
ﬂexible ramping products and a ramping capability target of 70 MW/minute. Therefore, the slow generator

that the example without ﬂexible ramping products has a total cost of $14,600, which reduces to $14,300 with

ramp capability targets.

Under CAISO, the ﬂexible ramping product, called ﬂexiramp, aims to always satisfy a system ramping
capability target or requirement, which is the amount of ramp capability that the dispatch for the current interval
attempts to preserve for use in case it is necessary in future intervals [10]. The ramp capability target may
depend on time of day, load trajectory, weather conditions, current intermittent resource output, and differ for
upward/downward capability [10]. It has been stated by MISO [15] that "future ramping needs will be based on
anticipated future operating conditions and historical real-time ramping needs". Hourly values from historically
similar days with roughly the same level of wind generation predicted for the next day will be used in formulating
the MISO ramp capability needed. An estimate of 2.5 standard deviations of the net load forecast error, or 99%
of anticipated scenarios, need to be covered by the ﬂexible ramping capability [15], although the formulation of
the most appropriate quantity of ramp capability target is be discussed later.
The rationale behind the use of ﬂexible ramping products is to aim to simultaneously dispatch the units to
offer the required level of ﬂexibility and energy at the economically optimal cost instead of committing new units.
Currently, MISO often requires the commitment of an additional unit to meet ramping requirements, but with a
"ﬂexibility constrained dispatch", this commitment can be avoided [15]. Achieving this ﬂexibility constrained
dispatch may result in additional costs, but has the beneﬁt of a system with increased responsiveness, and a
reduction in severity and frequency of ramp scarcity events [22], which will be explained as follows.
==PAGE:23==
1.1. LITERATURE REVIEW
7
Ramp scarcity is a temporary condition which occurs when there is sufﬁcient total capacity on-line, but there
is insufﬁcient (up or down) ramp capacity to meet variations in the net load. In MISO, this means that if there is
a shortage in the "up" direction, there is a need for sacriﬁcing a product, such as on-line contingency reserve, or
if there is a shortage in the "down" direction, there is a need for regulation service [15]. Attempting to eliminate
all scarcity events may not be practical or economical, as there may be some very rare and severe occurrences
with large variations, in which case providing sufﬁcient ramp capability would be extremely expensive. Instead,
as recommended by [22], there should be a balance between the additional operating costs related to providing
ﬂexible ramp capability, and the avoided costs of prevented ramp scarcity events. This is related to the design
and modeling of ﬂexible ramping products, which is described in the next section.

1.2
Objectives and contributions
So far this introduction has focused on the issue of ramping and introduced the ﬂexible ramping product as
a mechanism to manage ramping and better integrate renewables, but has focused on two examples from the
USA. The purpose of this study is to model and analyze ﬂexible ramping products in the Australian National
Electricity Market (NEM) context.
1.2.1
An introduction to the Australian National Electricity Market
The NEM in Australia serves roughly 19 out of the 23 million population of Australia, stretching from far
north Queensland south to Tasmania and west to Adelaide. It is the longest AC system in the world, although
it serves a relatively small population, due to Australia’s low population density [4]. The NEM is a wholesale
electricity market, where generators sell electricity to retailers, who then sell it on to consumers. Many generators
and retailers operate in the NEM, making it into a competitive and efﬁcient market [2].
Characteristics of the NEM
The NEM uses a merit order curve to balance supply and demand, with generators offering to supply
electricity blocks at a speciﬁed quantity at a speciﬁed price. The Australian Energy Market Operator (AEMO)
then decides the "order", with the cheapest generators being deployed ﬁrst, until demand is balanced with
supply. The minimum, mean average and maximum prices are roughly AUD$-1,000/MWh, AUD$40/MWh and
AUD$12,900/MWh respectively. The maximum price, previously known as the Value of Lost Load (VoLL) is
the price the market goes to in case there is nothing to meet demand and load is lost, not necessarily meaning
anything will be dispatched. Dispatch occurs every 5 minutes, with 6 dispatch prices being averaged every half
hour to give a half-hourly spot price. Every 5 minute dispatch is based on a forecast made 5 minutes earlier.
This is in addition to a pre-dispatch, which is a forecast of the next 40 hours from AEMO, based on generator’s
current offers to the market and assumptions about demand from AEMO [21].
The NEM as a market is deﬁned by some key characteristics, notably that it is fast, large and a gross pool
single platform electricity market. It is an energy only market with only a single ﬁve minute (real-time) market.
Without the day-ahead market, market participants instead manage their commitment rather independently (with
the assistance of pre-dispatch forecasts from AEMO). Market participants may re-offer their capacity at any
==PAGE:28==
12
CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
time until immediately before the relevant ﬁve minute dispatch interval. Being a gross pool single platform
electricity market means that all energy is traded through a single real time market and participants manage risk
via separate contractual arrangements. Riesz et al [24] argue that this allows the NEM to be more liquid than net
pool markets or bilateral markets such as in Spain, Germany or Denmark. The NEM is also a very large market
by geographical area, which can signify an increase in geographic diversity, a reduction in variability in variable
generation, reduced uncertainty through reduced forecast errors, and access to a larger ﬂeet of conventional
generators (increased ﬂexibility), although the latter can be debated as the demand density in the NEM is so
low it may outweigh this factor. However, perhaps the most interesting characteristic of the NEM is the short
dispatch interval, which provides several beneﬁts. Firstly, the short length of dispatch intervals reduces the
requirement for secondary reserves. Secondly, "slow" markets - where dispatch intervals are longer - can require
additional capacity compared to "fast" markets, which constitutes an economic inefﬁciency, according to Riesz et
al [24]. And ﬁnally, another important beneﬁt of "fast" markets for the purpose of this study is that the forecasts
are more accurate in 5 minute intervals than in, for example, 1 hour intervals.
With regards to ramping and ﬂexibility, fast markets naturally incentivize ﬂexibility. This has been illustrated
in an example by Riesz et al [24], where if variable generation ramps suddenly upwards, there will temporarily
be an oversupply in the system, which in turns causes a lower price for the next dispatch interval. If this price
falls below the short-run marginal cost (SRMC) of a generator, and they are not ﬂexible enough to ramp down
quickly, they will be exposed to negative proﬁtability. The more ﬂexible a generator is, the more it can avoid
these periods of negative proﬁtability. Similarly if there is a large downwards ramp of variable generation,
additional units will be required to dispatch, causing the price to increase. However, if generators cannot ramp
up quickly enough to balance the market, more ﬂexible generators will be dispatched. These generators may
have a very high SRMC, causing price spikes. It is seen here that the the more ﬂexible generators can ramp up,
the more they can capture the potential beneﬁt, and as such there is a natural incentive for ﬂexibility. However,
as the NEM integrates increasing amounts of non-dispatchable renewables into the market, it is not yet certain if
this natural incentive for ﬂexibility will provide enough ﬂexible ramping capability, or whether implementing a
ﬂexible ramping product is necessary.
According to Riesz and MacGill [25], it is possible that the Frequency Control Ancillary Services (FCAS)
in the NEM is also a model ancillary services market for renewable integration due to several characteristics.
The market actually entails eight separate real time markets, including two for regulation (regulation raise and
regulation lower), as well as the delivery of contingency services (raise and lower for 6 second, 60 second and 5
minute response times). This contingency reserve requirement is determined dynamically in each ﬁve minute
dispatch interval. In the NEM, these eight markets are co-optimized along with energy dispatch, and FCAS
providers are paid regardless of whether this capability is actually called upon over that period. Importantly,
argue Riesz and MacGill [25], the regulation requirement is dynamically set, which dramatically reduces the
costs, as additional reserves are only procured when required. The regulation requirement should therefore
adjust naturally over time as more variable generation is installed, responding to the increased variability and
uncertainty (and reduced intertia) as required by the system [25].
Another aspect of the NEM FCAS which is argued by [25] to improve integration of renewables is the Causer
Pays Cost Recovery principle. As the name implies, this characteristic dictates that the market participants that
==PAGE:29==
1.2. OBJECTIVES AND CONTRIBUTIONS
13
cause the need for a service must pay for it. This gives transparent integration costs for market participants and
creates incentives for market participants to manage their variability and improve their forecasting. Also, like
the dispatch market, the ancillary services markets are also very fast. The rapid primary frequency response
market requires market participants to fully respond within 6 seconds following a frequency event. It should
also be noted that the FCAS approaches market participants in a technology neutral manner. This also applies to
providing FCAS, meaning that solar and wind generators can provide FCAS, assuming the generators satisfy
other requirements such as the minimum capacity, etc [25].
In addition to improved forecasting and the use of stochastic modeling, there are a few suggestions from
[25] as to how changes in the FCAS could speciﬁcally improve integration of renewables. A revision of the
contingency response times may be beneﬁcial, as for example wind turbines may be able to respond more rapidly
than 6 seconds, yet unable to sustain response for 60 seconds. Optimized response times may allow renewable
technologies to participate more effectively. A revision of the concept of inertial response is also recommended
by [25], in terms of whether incentives for inertia are introduced, or whether this could be replaced by a very fast
and large FCAS service. It is also stated by [25] that ﬂexible ramping products "do not appear to be required
in the NEM, with sufﬁcient ﬂexibility being available via the real-time ﬁve minute market". However, [25] also
indicate that ﬂexible ramping products, or another mechanism to ensure generators receive an accurate price
signal for the value of system ﬂexibility, may be necessary with increased penetration of renewables.
Future of the NEM’s Electricity Mix
Australia’s Renewable Energy Target (RET) is admittedly not the most ambitious in the world, as it aims at
only 23.5% of large scale renewable energy in the electricity mix by 2020 [16]. This will include dispatchable
renewable energy sources such as hydroelectricity (currently at 8% and not expected to grow as most large
scale hydroelectric projects have already been realized) and bioenergy (currently around 1%). Therefore, wind
(currently at around 4%) and solar photovoltaic (currently at around 1%) are expected to increase drastically if
Australia is to indeed reach this target. It would be assumed that the growth of wind and solar in the electricity
mix of the NEM would be at least equal to this, as it includes the states of South Australia and Victoria, which
have relatively high installed capacities of wind energy compared to Western Australia and Northern Territory,
which are the two states not connected to the NEM.
In comparison, CAISO estimated a production of around 25% of electricity in California from renewable
sources already in 2014, and has a target of 33% by 2020. The estimates from MISO range from between
3.6% to 10.5% of electricity from wind, depending on the month [15]. In comparison with CAISO and MISO,
Australia could be in a similar position in roughly 10-30 years, depending on market development and the
political environment. However, the penetration of non-dispatchable renewables is not the only factor, as ramping
and ﬂexibility is also related to the ramping rate of generators, interconnectedness and the robustness of the
power system.
1.2.2
Modeling ﬂexible ramping products in the Australian NEM context
As mentioned, the purpose of this study is to model and analyze the performance of ﬂexible ramping products
as a mechanism to integrate renewable energy in the Australian NEM context. Ramping in Australia has potential
==PAGE:30==
14
CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
to cause signiﬁcant issues with regards to social welfare. Flexible ramping products have been suggested by
CAISO and MISO as a mechanism which may manage issues related to ramping. This is of interest to the
Australian context, which expects to see an increase in non-dispatchable renewable generation, and therefore
there is cause to investigate ﬂexible ramping products in the Australian context. Therefore, a simpliﬁed model of
the Australian NEM is created, including ﬂexible ramping products. A brief analysis of the effective design of
ﬂexible ramping products is included, followed by a multi-criteria analysis of the impact on total costs, proﬁts,
price spikes, penalties, curtailment, ﬂexible ramping product revenue and ﬂow between the two regions. The
sensitivity of the model is then assessed with regards to future scenarios, such as wind penetration, size of the
penalty and increased load, as well as technical constraints such as transmission capacity, speed of generators,
length of ramp, and the ﬂow of ﬂexiramp between the two regions.
As the demand curve is not readily available, consumer surplus and social welfare cannot be assessed, as in
the CAISO model. Therefore, total costs is assessed as a characteristic as the main performance characteristic, as
in the MISO model. Producer surplus, or total proﬁts, are also assessed as this represents the beneﬁt seen by
generators. While reduction in total costs in perhaps the main aim of ﬂexible ramping products, another key
aspect is the reduction of price spikes. These price spikes are a source of signiﬁcant risk to retailers, particularly
those in the NEM who buy at the spot price and sell to customers at a price that is heavily regulated [7]. According
to [27], prices in electricity markets in Australia are more volatile than markets in Canada and the United States.
Therefore, this study also assesses how ﬂexible ramping products impact price spikes.

The ramping capability of the generators is an important characteristic when it comes to the modeling of
ﬂexible ramping products in the NEM. In order to aggregate many generators into a few, for simpler modeling,
the ramping capability of the generators in the NEM are assessed. One source of data is the registration list of

stated as the technical maximum limit the generator can ramp up and down. The wind generators and solar PV

technically provide it in the future. This means that there are 89 generators in the east and 105 generators in the

Marginal Cost Analysis
In addition to the ROC up/down limits, it is also important to analyze the marginal costs of generators in the
NEM, as this is a vital metric in the functioning of the model as the marginal cost essentially dictates the dispatch
order of the merit order curve. Each generator submits 10 bid bands which include a price and corresponding
quantity. In reality, many thermal generators do not bid their short run marginal costs (SRMCs), but rather
may offer bid bands reﬂecting their minimum or must-run load (through negative bids) or as part of strategic
bidding (high bids) [12]. However, to run the model as a true least cost dispatch, SRMCs must be found for each
generator. The SRMCs have been calculated, using fuel costs, thermal efﬁciencies, and variable operation and
maintenance costs by ACIL Tasman for the Interregional Planning Committee in 2009 [26], with forecasts for
each year up to 2028/29 ﬁnancial year. The SRMCs have been calculated with and without carbon costs, as the
political environment is very uncertain regarding carbon costs (it should be noted that at the time of writing, the
carbon cost was not in use). The 2014 SRMCs for each generating unit have been used in this model.
Aggregating generating units based on the ROC and marginal costs was done by using the same groups for
ROC, and then dividing these groups into 4 groups each based on marginal costs. The four groups include (1)
no-cost, which has a marginal cost of 0 (not shown in the ﬁgure), (2) low cost ($1-20/MWh), (3) average cost
($21-125/MWh), and (4) high cost (>$125/MWh). This grouping results in a near homogeneous fuel source for
each group. The no-cost generators are all hydroelectric power plants, whereas 64 out of 65 low cost generators
are coal ﬁred power plants, 76 out of 77 average cost generators are natural gas or coal seam methane ﬁred power
plants, and 7 out of 8 high cost generators are diesel or kerosene ﬁred power plants. Therefore, the groups are
renamed to represent the major fuel source. Finally, this analysis results in 16 possible categories for a generator

Load, wind and transmission
Real world load, wind and transmission constraint data are implemented to the model in an attempt to
realistically simulate the NEM. The non-dispatchable wind generation and load for the 2014/15 ﬁnancial year
can be accessed from AEMO’s website. The load shedding cost are implemented as AU$13,800/MWh, as was
the real price in the NEM in 2015 [4].
The transmission constraint is a simpliﬁed value from real world ﬁgures. The nominal capacity of the
Victoria-New South Wales transmission line is used a transmission constraint. According to [3], power can ﬂow
in either direction in this connection, but it is highly dependent on output of large hydroelectric power stations
in northern Victoria and southern New South Wales, as well as thermal overload limits of transformers. The
nominal capacity from Victoria to New South Wales is in the range of 700-1600 MW, and from New South
Wales to Victoria is 400-1350 MW, depending on these factors. For use in this model, the mean of these values

Forecast data
The pre-dispatch process gives a forecast demand change, from one interval to the next interval 5 minutes
ahead. However, this data is not readily available. Since ﬂexible ramping products should be able to account
for uncertainties and variabilities, unlike reserves which are designed to only account for variabilities [30], the
difference between the pre-dispatch forecast net load change and the actual net load change would represent
the uncertainties. To replicate this data, in a realistic way, dispatch data is used from the previous interval, to
imply that it is always assumed the net load change is zero. As the time interval is only 5 minutes, both the
actual change in net load and the forecast change in net load will not vary greatly. Assuming that the forecast
change in net load is more accurate than forecasting no change in net load, using this forecast data will result
in less accurate forecasts. However it is argued that this forecast data is still relevant, since this study aims to
examine the inﬂuence of increased wind penetration, which will reduce the accuracy of forecast net load in the
future. Furthermore, the size and length of this ramp is adjusted in following sections of this study, to identify
how sensitive the problem is to the size of ramp. However, this means the ramp used in this study is added to the
list of deﬁnitions, as shown in 2.7e.

2.4
Calculation of ﬂexible ramping capability requirement
As previously mentioned, a ramp is deﬁned as consisting of variability (expected change in net load) and
uncertainty (error in net load forecast, or the difference between the expected change and the actual change in
net load). The ﬂexible ramping capability requirement aims to manage the uncertainties in net load, and thus the
forecast errors must be analyzed to ﬁnd the ramp capability requirement. As these forecasts are not available as
previously mentioned, ramps, as deﬁned by 2.7e are analyzed instead.
In the ramps analyzed in the 2014/15 ﬁnancial year, there were a total of 54,253 and 55,191 intervals of down
ramp in the east and south region respectively, slightly more than the number of intervals in the up direction
(50,859 and 49,919 in east and south respectively). The mean size of up ramps were in both regions thus slightly
larger than down ramps, and the standard deviation was also slightly larger for up ramps than down ramps. In
terms of regional comparison, actually the east region had larger ramps than the south, although this is largely
due to the fact that the load is signiﬁcantly larger in the east region. When assessing a normalized ratio of the
mean ramp to net load, the south region has higher up and down ramp to net load ratios. This is possibly due to
the higher wind penetration in the south region.

In [30], the net load uncertainties are approximated by the standard deviation of net load forecast errors. This
standard deviation is then transformed into a conﬁdence level by a multiplying factor (a). This corresponds to a

enough to satisfy the error in net load. This is done because there may be a few periods with a very high net load
forecast error, and to design the ﬂexible ramping capability requirement to be large enough to account for these
periods would be very expensive.

3.1
Overview
As previously mentioned, the implementation of ﬂexible ramping products is expected to have a multi-
dimensional impact. Therefore, the performance of this simpliﬁed NEM model is evaluated in regards to a range
of qualitative indicators, including those listed as follows:
• The total cost is an important indicator to assess, as it represents the total cost of dispatching sufﬁcient
energy, as well as the cost of penalties for not achieving the ramp capability requirement and for not
achieving the necessary load (load shedding).
• Total proﬁts of all generators, which is also equivalent to the producer surplus
• Proﬁts for each group of generator by ramping rate or speed. This can potentially show incentive towards
a certain type of generator group.
• Two levels of price spikes: $30/MWh, to represent any time interval the average cost generators are the
marginal generator, and $60/MWh, representing the time intervals where the two most expensive average
cost generators are the marginal generators.
• The penalties for not achieving the ramp capability requirement and for not achieving the necessary load
(load shedding).
• Wind curtailment, in terms of MWh
• Flexible ramping product revenue
• Flow of power between the two regions
There are several scenarios in which these indicators will be assessed, as will be introduced in the following
section as the results are presented. These scenarios are separated into future scenarios and technical constraints
of the system. The initial base-case scenario includes the characteristics as introduced in the method section. All
of these characteristics are taken from current conditions, and as such, this base-case scenario aims to represent
the NEM as it currently operates, albeit in a much simpliﬁed manner. Then, ﬂexible ramping products are added
to this base-case scenario, with a ﬂexible ramping requirement, and penalties for not achieving the requirement.
To summarize, these base-case characteristics include:

Base case scenario - current operation

Total cost
The total cost of the system without ﬂexible ramping requirements and products is approximately $12.77
billion. Of this, only $1.433 billion, or 11.22% of the total cost is from the south region. This is due to the lower
load, higher wind penetration and slightly higher installed capacity of no-cost generators in the south (4667 MW
vs 4443 MW). Regarding groups of generators by fuel source, only the coal-ﬁred group (low cost) is incurring
costs, with the natural gas (average cost) and diesel (high cost) groups not being required to dispatch in the year.
This does not reﬂect the real generation in the NEM, and may be due to the assumptions that hydroelectric plants
have 100% capacity, and/or that there are no losses between generators and the demand within regions. In terms
of groups of generators by ramping rate or speed, the slowest group in the east region contributes to roughly 70%
of the cost of this $12.77 billion ﬁgure.
Implementing a ﬂexible ramping requirement of 1, 2.5, 4 or 6·σ results in exactly the same total cost of the
system. Hereby it is seen that the system does not incur any costs to satisfy these ﬂexible ramping requirements.
However, when the ﬂexible ramping requirement is increased to 8 or 10.35 standard deviations, the total cost
increases by $88.67 thousand and $120.1 million respectively, from the scenario without ﬂexible ramping
requirement. Percentage-wise, this corresponds to increases of 0.000694% and 0.941% of the total cost in the
base case scenario without the ﬂexible ramping requirement. The total costs for each implemented ﬂexible
ramping requirement (in number of standard deviations) is shown in 3.1.

Penalties for not achieving ﬂexible ramping requirement were not a signiﬁcant portion of the total costs in
any of the modeled scenarios. The penalties were only imposed in scenarios with 10.35·σ requirement, and
increased with increasing wind penetration. Interestingly, penalties were also only imposed for violating the
ramp requirement in the up direction. This was due to the generators being constrained by maximum output
limits in periods of high load and low wind, and thus not being able to provide capability to ramp up. Total
penalties for the year were less than $300, until the wind was expanded to both regions, where they increased
to $2,300 in the ﬁve times wind scenario, and $34,000 in the ten times wind scenario. This was still relatively
insigniﬁcant compared to total costs, and never more than 0.001% of the total costs.
On the other hand, penalties for load shedding are a few order of magnitudes higher than the penalties for not
achieving the ﬂexible ramping requirement. Load shedding penalties are only imposed in the scenario with ten
times the wind in both regions, and are the same for all levels of ﬂexible ramping requirement. The total load
shed is 146 MWh, which incurs a cost of roughly $2 million, or roughly 0.027% of the total cost. Interestingly,
increasing the ﬂexible ramping requirement fails to reduce the shed load.
Curtailment
Wind curtailment, like load shedding, only occurs in the ten times wind in both regions scenario. However,
due to the constraint of requiring ﬂexible ramping capability, actually the level of curtailment of wind increases
with increasing ﬂexible ramping requirement, as seen in 3.3. It should be noted though, that this scenario does
include a very large amount of wind, and the assumption of a simpliﬁed wind proﬁle potentially contributes to
this extremely high curtailment ﬁgure.

Sensitivity to load
As the design of ﬂexible ramping products is aimed at better integrating a higher penetration of renewables
in the coming decades, it is also of interest to investigate how the load, or demand, will change in future decades.
According to CSIRO [9], it is predicted that the load in the NEM will increase to roughly 250 TWh by 2035-36.
In 2014-15, the load was approximately 188 TWh, and therefore this increase represents roughly a 33% increase
in load. Therefore, a new scenario is investigated, using ﬁve times existing southern wind penetration in both
regions, and a 33% increase in load in both regions, to determine how this impacts the performance of ﬂexible
ramping products. Similar to the scenario with wind penetration, this is done by simply multiplying the load by a
factor.
Total cost
The total cost of the system with 133% of the load was roughly 175% of the cost of the original system
with 0, 2.5 and 10.35·σ ﬂexible ramping requirement. As the generators do not increase in size to match the
increase in the load, more generators are constrained by maximum output limits and higher cost generators are
required. Similarly to with the original load, an increase in costs is only observed when 10.35·σ requirement is
implemented. The increase in costs is roughly $152 million, compared to $120 million with the original load.
Proportionately however, this is only a 0.8% increase, compared to 1.2% increase in the scenario with the original
load. This is potentially due to the fact that the ramps are slightly larger with the increased load, and thus the
ﬂexible ramping requirement is inherently satisﬁed more often.
Proﬁts
The total proﬁt decreases when the ramping requirement is increased to the highest level. Slowest generators
are the only group to experience an increase in proﬁts from the increased ﬂexible ramping requirement with
the increased load, whereas in the scenario with the original load, slowest generators experience the greatest
reduction in proﬁts of all groups. Therefore with the increased load, any potential incentive beneﬁt is removed.
However it should be mentioned that this assumes no increase in installed capacity, which will potentially be
required to meet the increased load.

Interestingly, some of the beneﬁts of the ﬂexible ramping product observed with the original load are not
observed with this increased load. Price spikes above $30/MWh, for example, are observed to slightly increase
with increased ﬂexible ramping requirement, from 1988 to 1991 intervals in the year, whereas price spikes above
$60/MWh are consistent at 1245 intervals. This is contrary to the scenario with the original load, where price
spikes above $30/MWh decrease with increased ﬂexible ramping requirement. This occurs due to the system
having more generators constrained at maximum output to meet the increased load.
Penalties
There are no load shedding penalties observed in this scenario, and the penalties for not achieving the up
ﬂexible ramping requirement increase signiﬁcantly, from $2,360 to $163.2 thousand with the increased load.

As can be expected, the total cost reduces with an increase in transmission capacity (as the cost of installing

3.4.2
Sensitivity to ramping rate of generators
The aggregation of generators into categories based on ramping rate or speed results in a few very ﬂexible
generators. Covering such a large geographical area in the NEM, this is perhaps a limitation of the model which
should be investigated, as it assumes for example that a generator in far north Queensland can ramp quickly to
serve a change in load 2000 km south in Sydney. So far the results have shown that most of the time, the ﬂexible
ramping requirement is easily achieved, unless it is increased to a very high level. Potentially, the assumption
of aggregating generators has made the system more ﬂexible than it is in reality. This section evaluates the
performance of the model, and ﬂexible ramping products, to the system if the ramping rate of every generator
was half of the initially used ramping rate, again with double wind penetration.
Total cost
As can be expected, the total cost of the system is increased when the ramping rates of all generators are
halved. The total cost increases by roughly $24,000, or 0.2% with 0, 2.5 and 4·σ ﬂexible ramping requirement,
but this changes to $1.361 billion, or 10.66% when the ramping requirement is increased to 10.35·σ. So the
impact on total costs is greatly increased when the ﬂexible ramping requirement increases. Therefore the total
costs are very sensitive to the ramping rate of the ﬂeet when there is a high ﬂexible ramping requirement.
Proﬁts
In the half ramping rate scenario, the greatest incentive to be fast is observed. The fast, medium and slow
generators have proﬁts reduced by 19%, 41% and 17% with high ﬂexible ramping requirement, whereas the
slowest generators sustain a 266% loss of proﬁts, from $531 Million to -$882 Million. This is done as the slowest
generators are now so slow that they cannot reduce their dispatch quickly enough to avoid producing when the
clearing price is lower than their marginal costs. Therefore, it can be observed that as the generating ﬂeet is
slower, the incentive to be fast increases with increasing ﬂexible ramping requirement.
Price spikes
In the scenario with the highest ﬂexible ramping requirement, and half ramping rate generating ﬂeet, both
types of price spikes above $30/MWh and $60/MWh are reduced to 0 from 587 and 3, respectively. However, as
seen in the wind penetration sensitivity analysis, this comes at a large increase in costs.
Penalties
Penalties for violating the ﬂexible ramping requirement are signiﬁcant in the half ramping rate scenario with
the highest ﬂexible ramping requirement. Compared to only $280 in the original ramping rate scenario, the
penalties increase to approximately $33.8 million, of which roughly $27.2 million was in the down direction.
This still only corresponds to 0.24% of the total costs in this scenario however. Also, no load shedding penalties
were incurred in any of these scenarios.
Curtailment
In this scenario 6 MWh of wind is curtailed in the south region, and increasing the ﬂexible ramping
requirement does not reduce this at all.

proﬁts, with slowest and slow generators experiencing the greatest relative increase (2.6% and 2.2% respectively).
Thus the incentive to be fast is slightly reduced.

Only in the scenario where wind is ten times the south wind in both regions are some drawbacks of
transmission of ﬂexiramp observed. Again the total cost is lower and the total proﬁts are higher with transmission
of ﬂexiramp between the regions. However, in this case it can be observed that price spikes are much better

Penalties
In the double wind in the south region scenario, the ﬂow of ﬂexiramp effectively reduces penalties for
not achieving the up ﬂexible ramping requirement (in the east region) from $280 to 0. This is due to the new
capability of the system to transfer ﬂexiramp from the south to satisfy the ﬂexible ramping requirement in the east.
With ten times the wind in both regions, a similar impact is observed. The penalties for not achieving up and
down ﬂexible ramping requirement were originally $142.5 thousand and $15.05 million respectively. These were
reduced by 25.24% and 76.53% respectively, when the ﬂexiramp was allowed to ﬂow between the two regions.
This, added to the 7.45% reduction in dispatch costs, results in the lower total costs with the ﬂow of ﬂexiramp
between the regions.

3.6.1
Analysis of total costs throughout the year
So far the model has been made with a ﬂexible requirement based off a number of standard deviations of
observed ramps in the current system. This ﬂexible ramping requirement is static for both regions throughout
every dispatch interval of the year. However, as large ramps are very infrequent, a dynamic ﬂexible ramping
requirement, which responds to the conditions of the system and the likelihood of a large ramp, may prove to
be beneﬁcial. By requiring a large amount of ramp capability at every time interval, the system is potentially

very low. This section aims to analyze the performance of the ﬂexible ramping product and ﬂexible ramping
requirement throughout the year, to determine under which conditions it provides beneﬁts, and under which
conditions it incurs additional costs. In this way, some insight can be gained in regards to which time intervals,
under which conditions, the system with ﬂexible ramping products performs better than the system without
ﬂexible ramping products, and vice versa.

3.7
Comparison to MISO model
This section aims to compare the results from this model to the results in the MISO model and to identify
factors which caused the results to be different. The MISO model reports a reduction in total costs, which is not
observed in any of the scenarios presented in this study. As mentioned, the MISO model with available data
involves 4 generators over 4 time intervals and therefore 3 ramps (the larger MISO model does not have available
data). In this section, the simple model used by MISO is brieﬂy assessed to determine why the results were
different.
The four generators in the MISO model have ramp capabilities of 5, 20, 5 and 5 MW/5 minute interval. The
ramps observed in the ﬁrst two intervals are both 10.5 MW. This means that the ramps assessed were about half
the size of the ramp capability of the ’fastest’ generator. In comparison, using real load and wind data from the
NEM, the largest absolute ramps in the east and south regions were 965.3 MW (up) and 554.6 MW (down),
respectively. Comparing this to the ramping rate of generators reveals that the largest ramp in both regions is
actually larger than the ramping capability of the ’fastest’ generator in each region. This implies that the largest
ramp in the NEM, using current wind and load data, was larger than the ramps used in the MISO model relative
to the ramping rate of the fastest generator.
However, these are the largest ramps out of 105119·5 minute intervals in the entire 2014/15 ﬁnancial year.
Whereas in the MISO model, these are two of the 3 ramps (from the 4 time intervals) shown in the model. As
mentioned previously, a ramp requirement of 2.5·σ covers 98.25% of time intervals in the east and 97.64% in the
south. Comparing the size of this requirement to the ramping rate of generators reveals that 2 generators have
ramping rate which exceeds this 2.5·σ requirement in the east (the largest one by over 400%) and 4 generators
exceed the requirement in the south (the largest one by over 300%). Therefore, the overwhelming majority of
time intervals have ramps which are by far exceeded by the ramping capability of the generators, whereas the
MISO model focuses on two very large ramping events in the three ramps.

The simpliﬁed model includes one small ramping event, which actually results in a small increase in costs
with the ﬂexible ramping product. As seen in the model in this study, these small ramping events occur much
more frequently than larger ramp events in the NEM, resulting in an increase in costs over the whole period. It
should be noted that the MISO network has a higher penetration of wind than the NEM, and as such, higher ramps
are expected to occur more often in the MISO than in the NEM. These higher ramps have been shown in this
study to be more likely to have reduction in costs from ﬂexible ramping products. However, it is recommended
that the MISO model, with 3 ramps and 2 very large ones, should be expanded to using a larger time series with
realistic data, to determine whether the beneﬁts of intervals with large ramps would remain if weighed against
the rest of the time series.
However, and perhaps more importantly, the MISO model also utilizes a dynamic ﬂexible ramping require-
ment, which "may depend on time of a day, day of a week, bid costs or the levels of wind generation" and that "a
constant number of sigmas is ﬁrst selected for different periods or conditions". However, the MISO paper does
not show how this is actually implemented using real data. The ramps from the east and south regions of the
NEM in the ﬁnancial year 2014/15 do not seem to show obvious trends to design a dynamic ﬂexible requirement,
although this should be studied further in order to design an effective ﬂexible ramping product.

CHAPTER 4
Conclusion
Flexible ramping products are a new mechanism which potentially can assist in the integration of variable
renewable energy such as wind. Using simpliﬁed models, ﬂexible ramping products have shown to reduce system
costs in the Midwest and Californian models. In the Australian context however, it remains to be proven whether
or not ﬂexible ramping products can reduce system costs. This study has presented a unit commitment and
economic dispatch model with the ﬂexible ramping products in a simpliﬁed representation of the Australian NEM.
The performance of ﬂexible ramping products was assessed with regards to a range of qualitative indicators, and
the sensitivity of this performance to a range of future scenarios and technical constraints was analyzed. The
results presented in this study failed to show a reduction in costs in any of the modeled scenarios.
However, this does not conclusively prove that ﬂexible ramping products will never be a suitable mechanism
for the Australian NEM. In several of the modeled scenarios beneﬁts from ﬂexible ramping products were
observed, albeit at a relative trade-off in the form of increased costs. Some of the beneﬁts observed were a
reduction in price spikes, an increase in proﬁts for generators, and an incentive towards fast generators. It was
observed that intervals with large ramps had a much higher likelihood of experiencing a reduction in costs from
the implementation of ﬂexible ramping products than intervals with low ramps. However, as intervals with low
ramps were much more common, the total annual cost experiences an overall increase. In terms of reducing
price spikes, ﬂexible ramping products were observed to be more likely to reduce price spikes in the high peak
loads in morning and evening hours.
These beneﬁts of implementing ﬂexible ramping costs must however be weighed against the increase in
costs. To optimize this balance, it is recommended that the ﬂexible ramping requirement is carefully designed
as to receive beneﬁts such as increased proﬁts and reduced price spikes without constraining the system to a
point where costs increase too signiﬁcantly. Therefore, one conclusion from this study is that carefully designed
ﬂexible ramping products have the potential to cause beneﬁts to the system, with a trade-off in increased costs, or
even potentially a reduction in costs.
An important conclusion from this study is the implication that ﬂexible ramping products are potentially an
effective mechanism to assist in the integration of non-dispatchable variable renewable energy such as wind
energy, into the Australian NEM. However, further research into the effective design of ﬂexible ramping products
must ﬁrst be undertaken. The results from this study show that in very few intervals there is a reduction in cost

on historical data.
The model presented in this study is obviously very simpliﬁed as stated previously. The simpliﬁcations
may have impacted the results but the general conclusions are still assumed to be valid. Still it is important
to consider that assumptions such as, for example, simply multiplying the existing wind or load proﬁle is a
signiﬁcant assumption which may cause results to be impacted. While this study certainly used much more
realistic data than the papers from MISO and CAISO, it should be noted that the exclusion of FCAS from the
model may have also impacted the functioning of the model, and this must be considered along with the results
from the model. Also the cost of the ﬂexible ramping products should be considered as a characteristic when
planning to implement ﬂexible ramping products.
The MISO model used a dynamic ramping requirement, which could potentially be very effective in the
Australian NEM also in terms of reducing costs. As stated, it is highly recommended that further research is
done towards designing a dynamic ramping requirement, which changes depending on conditions, times of day,
season, etc. This could reduce the requirement in times or conditions with traditionally low ramps, and vice
versa, to optimize costs. In addition, the model could be enhanced by incorporating pre-dispatch data and reserves.
Therefore, the key recommendations from this study are:
• The model should be strengthened to include pre-dispatch data, realistic hydroelectric dispatch based on
historical data, more realistic wind and load proﬁles, ancillary services and more years of data.
• Further research should be undertaken into designing a dynamic ﬂexible ramping requirement
• The decision to implement ﬂexible ramping products or not should include consideration of other factors
such as improved forecasting of wind energy, reforms to the ancillary services market, and the increase in
prices to pay ﬂexible ramping product revenue.
In the coming decades, as non-dispatchable renewable generation penetration increases in Australia and the
issue of ramping needs to be addressed, ﬂexible ramping products may play a part in discussions. This study
has contributed to the research of this mechanism in the Australian NEM context, however further research
should aim to include reserves in the model, to include pre-dispatch forecast data, and to analyze ramp data to
design a dynamic ﬂexible ramping requirement to optimize performance of the mechanism. In addition, it is
recommended that the performance of ﬂexible ramping products is assessed in a wider overall context, with
other potential solutions such as increased forecasting accuracy, and reforms of the ancillary services market in
Australia.
