Abstract
The compression strength of unidirectional polymer matrix composites is controlled by the matrix
plasticity and the ﬁber imperfection. The inﬂuence of the matrix plasticity is studied through tensile testing
on an epoxy matrix material at different rates and temperatures, resulting to matrix materials with different
elastic-plastic properties. The elastic tensile properties of glass and carbon reinforcements are determined
through linear density and single ﬁber testing. A smeared out, ﬁnite element model, which accounts for the
kink band formation, is used for simulating a planar unidirectional composite assuming the tested matrices
and ﬁbers as the constituents. Subsequently, the compression strength of the different composite systems is
predicted at various ﬁber imperfection angles. It is found that the tested matrices at low speed and at elevated
temperature reduce signiﬁcantly the compression strength of unidirectional composites. Finally, the elastic
properties of the tested ﬁbers and the predicted compression strengths of the composite model are compared
to an experimental study on composite specimens of the same constituents, and almost similar results are
obtained.
Keywords: compression strength; unidirectional composites; kink band formation; matrix plasticity;
linear density test; ﬁber imperfection; tensile test; hardening curve

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation and objectives
The demand for light and strong mechanical structures in the automotive, aeronautical, wind turbine and
aerospace industries has revealed the beneﬁts of using ﬁber reinforced composite materials. In comparison
to other materials used extensively in the past, such as aluminum and steel, composite materials offer the
advantage that they are modiﬁed at the same time as produced. The type of reinforcement and matrix
material, the fraction of each, the number and the direction of plies can be chosen so as the ﬁnal product
to be strong and stiff in the desired directions rather than in other directions in which weaker performance
sufﬁces.
A typical ﬁber reinforced composite material consists of multiple layers, called plies or laminae. Each ply
consists of a matrix material reinforced by ﬁbers which are much stiffer than the matrix and their direction is
dominant for the stiffness of the ply. Unidirectional (UD) composites, such as used in wind turbine blade
caps which are the primary load-carrying members, provide high stiffness in the direction of the length of the
blade in order to constrain the blade deﬂection. However, several studies have shown that the compression
strength of UD polymer matrix composites is as low as 50 to 60 % of the tensile strength, Ref. [1]. Thus,
the compression strength has become a design limiting factor in the application of UD composites. While
the tensile strength of UD composites is primarily governed by the strength of the ﬁbers, the compression
strength is mainly dictated by the plasticity of the matrix, which provides the side support to the ﬁbers,
together with the ﬁber misalignment, Ref. [2].
The main goal of this thesis is to study the inﬂuence of using an elastic-plastic matrix material, tested
at different deformation rates and temperatures, on the compression strength of UD composites at various
imperfection angles.
1.2
Range of work
This project is organized as follows. Chapter 2 describes the compression failure mechanisms in UD
composites and presents a literature review of the approach to plastic microbuckling. Several micromechani-
cal models, which analyze this mechanism, along with the advantages of a smeared out model, are shown.
Finally, the implementation of the smeared out model in a ﬁnite element program with the User MATerial
(UMAT) subroutine, by Ref. [3], is presented. In chapter 3, the process of curing the matrix material and its
tensile testing at different speeds and at ambient and elevated temperatures is presented. Subsequently, the

CHAPTER 2
Theory and method
2.1
Compression failure mechanisms
Six distinct compression failure mechanisms have been observed in UD composites, such as sketched in
ﬁgure 2.1, Ref. [4]. In ﬁgure 2.1(a), the elastic microbuckling is presented, in which, the ﬁbers and the matrix
behave elastically, hence, an overall elastic deformation of the composite is observed. Subsequently, in
ﬁgure 2.1(b), the plastic microbuckling, also called kink band formation, is shown. In plastic microbuckling,
the plasticity of the matrix material and the misalignment of ﬁbers can cause a kink band formation which
will be discussed in the next section. In ﬁgure 2.1(c), the ﬁber crushing is illustrated. This occurs when the
matrix is stiff enough and has a high yield strength that the uniaxial strain reaches the crushing strain of
the ﬁbers ﬁrst. In ﬁgure 2.1(d), the failure mechanism of splitting is depicted. Splitting is occurred when
the matrix is stiffer than the ﬁbers and is seen as microcracks in the matrix parallel to the ﬁber direction.
However, in polymer matrix composites, the matrix tends to yield rather than crack, Ref. [2]. In ﬁgure 2.1(e),
the buckle driven delamination is shown, in which a surface layer is debonded in the direction transversely
to the loading direction. Finally, in composites with very low ﬁber volume fractions, similar to pure matrix
materials, shear band formation can yield at 45◦relative to the loading direction, as illustrated in ﬁgure 2.1(f).
Figure 2.1: Compression failure mechanisms in UD composites. (a) elastic microbuckling, (b) plastic
microbuckling, (c) ﬁber crushing, (d) splitting, (e) buckle driven delamination, (f) shear band formation, Ref.
[4].
From all described compression failure mechanisms, plastic microbuckling is seen as the governing
failure mechanism in UD polymer matrix composites under uniaxial compresssion, Ref. [2], [4].

2.2
Plastic microbuckling
Rosen (1965) considered that the ﬁbers act as slender beams embedded in an elastic matrix, Ref. [2].
He distinguished two microbuckling modes; a shear buckling mode and an extension buckling mode,
Ref. [5]. Even though the resulted compression strength is lower using the shear mode, it is still four
times greater compared with experimental data from carbon ﬁber/epoxy matrix composites, Ref. [2]. The
large difference between this elastic model and experimental data implied the plastic nature of microbuckling.
Argon (1972) and Budiansky (1983) introduced plastic yielding in the matrix and the ﬁber misalignment
as the governing factors of the compression strength in the kink band, Ref. [6]. They showed that in UD
composites the ﬁbers are misaligned and under uniaxial compression the matrix shears. By excluding the
additional ﬁber rotation to the initial misalignment and for zero kink band inclination β = 0, Argon (1972)
found that for a rigid-perfectly plastic matrix the compression strength is given by

Budiansky’s model was further optimized by Fleck and Budiansky (1991) to include the effect of kink
band inclination, Ref. [2]. The researchers postulated that a rigid perfectly plastic analysis is adequate for
plastic microbuckling. By applying combined axial and shear loading, they concluded that the compression
strength increases with decreasing ﬁber misalignment and increasing shear strength of the matrix.
Based on the above ﬁndings, several micromechanical models, with individual discretization of the ﬁber
and matrix materials in ﬁnite elements, have been thereafter implemented in order to analyze numerically
the compressive response of UD composites. Kyriakides et al. (1995) and Kyriakides and Ruff (1997)
modeled an AS4/PEEK composite as a 2D solid consisted of alternating layers of straight or sinusoidal
elastic ﬁbers, with uniform distribution, embedded in an elastic-plastic matrix, Ref. [7], [8]. They depicted
that the composite under uniaxial compression exhibits a limit load instability followed by localization of
deformation in an inclined kink band, in which the ﬁbers bend and rotate. In the same context, Hsu et al.
(1998) implemented a more realistic 3D model of circular elastic ﬁbers with a hexagonal distribution inside
an elastic-plastic matrix, Ref. [1]. It was concluded that the predicted compression strengths from the 3D
model are very similar to those from the 2D model.

2.3
Smeared out model
Micromechanical models are computationally expensive and can accommodate only sections with a
small number of ﬁbers. Implementing a detailed ﬁnite element model of the actual ﬁber/matrix material
requires many number of elements, that is not even possible. Hence, the modeling in this report is based on
the constitutive model by [9], in which, the actual ﬁbers and matrix are smeared out and the UD composite
is treated as a 2D anisotropic material consisting of isotropic ﬁbers and matrix. The smeared out model
accounts for the ﬁber misalignment and the non-linear properties of the constituents and treats the kink band
formation as localization of deformation, according to Ref. [10]. The plasticity of the ﬁber and the matrix is
described by the time-independent J2-ﬂow theory with isotropic hardening.
The model is based on a number of assumptions. In order to account for continuity between the ﬁbers
and the matrix, the material lines parallel with the ﬁbers are subjected to a common stretching and rotation.
In addition, the planes parallel with the ﬁbers are assumed to transmit identical stress rates perpendicular to
the ﬁbers. Finally, it is assumed that the material of the constituents, i.e, ﬁber and matrix, is either elastic or
elastic-plastic.

CHAPTER 3
Matrix curing and testing
In this chapter, the process of curing the matrix material is described. Subsequently, the experimental
study which includes uniaxial tensile testing on the fully cured matrix specimens, at different deformation
rates and temperature conditions, is presented. Tensile testing was conducted so as to extract reliable plastic
deformation data, since under compression, buckling may inﬂuence the results, Ref. [11]. The test data
are then analyzed in order to determine the Young’s modulus and the plastic deformation behavior of the
material. Eventually, the different mechanical responses of the matrix material at different test speeds and
temperatures are illustrated. In the last section, the properties and the tensile testing of a matrix material,
very similar to the matrix material of the present study, are presented.
3.1
Curing
The examined matrix material is the cured end product of the epoxy resin Araldite® LY 1564 SP reacted
with the Aradur® 3486 BD hardener. The two components form a low viscosity thermoset system with a
long pot life suitable for the production of large ﬁber reinforced composites, such as wind turbine blades,
using infusion processes. The mix ratio of 100 parts resin to 34 parts hardener by weight was applied
according to the label directions, Ref. [12]. The accurate proportions from the product containers were then
dispensed into a clean, dry plastic container, where they mixed thoroughly for approximately 2 minutes
using a stick of wood. During mixing, the bottom and the side surfaces of the mixing container were scraped
adequately in order to ensure that the resin will not stick to them. Next, the mixing container was placed into
a vacuum chamber in order to remove the entrapped air bubbles in the mixture.
Two plates made of steel were used for molding the mixture. These plates were ﬁrst polished and then
coated with a high-slip release agent (Zyvax® Multishield). At one of these plates a 250 mm long square
wooden frame with a thickness of 4 mm was installed using an epoxy adhesive (3M® DP410), so as the
frame to be sealed properly. The set of the plates was then clamped and placed in a vertical position. The
mixture was then poured into the frame in between the plates. The set-up is shown in ﬁgures 3.1 and 3.2.
Two-stage curing process was conducted in this study. At ﬁrst stage, the epoxy resin/hardener system
was cured at room temperature for approximately 36 hours. Subsequently, the steel plates and the frame
were removed and the extracted panel was subjected to post-curing in an oven at 72◦C for approximately 6
hours. The temperature in the panel was measured using a thermocouple wire. The measured temperatures
of the panel and the air in the oven during this stage are presented in ﬁgure 3.3. Finally, sixteen specimens of
a dog bone shape were extruded from the cured panel for testing.

3.2
Experimental
The tensile tests were performed in an Instron model OP 1740-3004 universal test machine with a
dynamic load cell of ± 10 kN and a hydraulic actuator. Two clip-on extensometers, with gauge length of 25
mm and axial travel of ± 2.5 mm corresponding to 10 % maximum measured axial strain were mounted
on the middle of the opposite faces of the test specimen, in order to eliminate possible non-uniform strains
caused by bending in the thickness direction.
The test specimens are labeled as "EpRoomA-xx", "EpRoomB-xx" and "Ep50A-xx" where, Ep, denotes
the type of material, i.e. Epoxy, Room and 50 denote the temperature conditions, i.e. the room temperature
and the elevated temperature of 50◦C, A, the deformation rate of 3 mm/min, B, the deformation rate of 300
mm/min and, xx, the tagged number on each specimen. The tested specimens are shown in Appendix 8.
Eight out of the sixteen specimens were tested at room temperature, under the two different deformation
rates. Preliminary inspection of the specimens was conducted in order to identify any surface ﬂaws induced
by manufacturing. Thus, one of the sixteen specimens was excluded a priori from testing due to existing

Fiber Testing
In this chapter, the tensile properties of three types of reinforcements, used in polymer composites of
wind turbine blades, are determined through single ﬁber tensile testing. Two types of glass ﬁbers; PPG
Hybon® 2026, E-glass fabric and a carbon ﬁber; Zoltek Panex® 35 are tested.
4.1
Test setup
The single ﬁber testing was conducted in the semiautomatic tensile tester Textechno FAVIMAT+. This
tester comprises a load cell of 610 cN and measuring systems which record the linear density, the resonance
frequency and the geometry of the single ﬁber.
The automatic system Textechno ROBOT 2 is used for feeding the ﬁbers to the tensile tester. This system
utilizes a sample storage unit and a transfer clamp. The storage unit can take up to 20 magazines and each
of which can carry up to 25 single ﬁbers. The transfer clamp unloads the single ﬁber from the magazine
and transfers it to the test section of the FAVIMAT+. The ﬁber is then held between the two clamps, in the
testing zone, and the tensile test is ready to start. The distance between the two clamps, which corresponds
to the gauge length of the ﬁber, is adjusted from the user in the range of 2 to 100 mm. After each test, a
suction system removes the ﬁber remains.
The loading of the single ﬁbers to the magazine is made manually. A section of the ﬁber roving is cut and
a cluster of ﬁbers is taken. From this cluster, single ﬁbers are picked up. Given the very small ﬁber diameter,
a high resolution microscope was used. When the single ﬁber is selected, a "ﬂoating" pre-tensioning weight
is clamped on one end of the ﬁber. This weight indicates the presence of the ﬁber and helps to keep the ﬁber
straight. The test set-up is shown in ﬁgure 4.1.

Finally, as it is seen in ﬁgure 4.6, the Panex ﬁbers exhibit a slight non-linearity, while Hybon ﬁbers and
E-glass fabric, in ﬁgures 4.4 and 4.5, respectively, behave linearly. Hence, the tensile Young’s moduli of the
Hybon ﬁbers and the E-glass fabric can be used to deﬁne the linear elastic behavior of the reinforcement in
the composite model under compression. On the other hand, the tensile Young’s modulus of the Panex ﬁbers
is expected to be lower in the composite model under compression. The observed non-linearity of the Panex
ﬁbers was expected, given the reorientation of the carbon planes under uniaxial tension, Ref. [25].
To conclude with, the values of Ef = 80 GPa, Ef = 75 GPa and Ef = 200 GPa are taken as the Young’s
moduli of the Hybon, E-glass fabric and Panex reinforcements, respectively. These values, together with the
assumed Poisson’s ratio of νf = 0.2, deﬁne the elastic behavior of the reinforcement in the composite model
in compression. Even though the tensile Young’s modulus of the Panex ﬁbers is Ef = 206 GPa, it is decided
to use the lower value of Ef = 200 GPa in the composite model in compression.

5.2.2
ROM model
Fiber reinforced composites are anisotropic, i.e., their mechanical properties vary in the three perpen-
dicular local material directions. The longitudinal ﬁber direction is denoted as 1, the in-plane transverse
direction as 2 and the out-of-plane transverse direction as 3. A UD composite exhibits transverse isotropy,
i.e., its properties in the transverse directions, 2 and 3, to the ﬁber direction 1, are the same, Ref. [27].
The elastic material properties of the UD composite are extracted by applying the ROM modeling, where
the ﬁbers are considered uniform, parallel and continuous, bonded perfectly with the matrix material, Ref.

5.4
Discussion
A ﬁnite element model of the uniform section of the tested matrix specimens is made in Abaqus/CAE
assuming elastic-plastic behavior. Using a plane stress element, the measured engineering stress-strain curve
ﬁts perfectly with the response of the model under the same uniaxial tension.
Subsequently, the UMAT subroutine is modiﬁed to account for a purely elastic composite. By changing
the material orientation with respect to the uniaxial tension, it was found that the apparent stiffness of the
composite using the UMAT subroutine is in good agreement with that resulted from elastic composite models
such as the ROM and the Voigt model. The validity of these models was also veriﬁed against a theoretical
formula for calculating the apparent stiffness of a UD composite at various rotation angles.
Finally, the UMAT subroutine is modiﬁed to account for a composite consisting of purely elastic-
plastic matrix (the reinforcement is neglected) and compared to the matrix model. Assuming a plane strain
element, the tensile response of the matrix model is the same either by deﬁning its elastic-plastic behavior in
Abaqus/CAE or in the UMAT subroutine.

orientation is assigned, corresponding to the kink band orientation as deﬁned in the ORIENT subroutine.

6.5
Comparison with experimental study
The calculated Young’s moduli, from the single ﬁber tensile testing, and the predicted compression
strengths, from the 8×40 composite model, are compared to an experimental study, conducted by the DTU
Wind Energy department.
In this experimental study, uniaxial tensile and compression tests were performed on composite speci-
mens consisting of the preliminary studied matrix with Panex or Hybon reinforcement. The measured ﬁber
volume fractions, V f, are 0.53 and 0.58, respectively. The tensile Young’s modulus of the Panex reinforced
composite was measured to 110.7 GPa and the tensile Young’s modulus of the Hybon reinforced composite
was measured to 47.6 GPa. Using the Rule of Mixtures, the back-calculated tensile Young’s moduli of the
Panex and the Hybon reinforcements, are 206.1 GPa and 79.8 GPa, respectively. These back-calculated
tensile Young’s moduli are in a very good agreement with those calculated with single ﬁber testing and were
presented in tables 4.3, 4.2, for Panex and Hybon ﬁbers, respectively.

In compression, the Young’s moduli of the Panex and Hybon reinforced composites were measured
to 104.3 GPa and 48.9 GPa, respectively. The back-calculated Young’s moduli of the Panex and Hybon
reinforcements are 194 GPa and 82 GPa, respectively. The lower Young’s modulus of Panex ﬁbers in
compression, was expected, given the exhibited non-linearity during tension, as it was shown in ﬁgure 4.6
and discussed in section 4.6. The small difference in the Young’s modulus of Hybon ﬁbers, between tension
and compression, is very close to the range of the uncertainty given by the nominal density, as it was shown
in table 4.2. Hence, the reliability of the elastic properties of the ﬁbers, such as used in the UMAT subroutine
for the prediction of the compression strength of the composite systems, is conﬁrmed.
The compression strength of the Panex and Hybon reinforced composite specimens was measured to
900 MPa and 810 MPa, respectively. Using the composite model with Panex reinforcement, the predicted
compression strengths are 1057 MPa for φm = 3◦and 735.7 MPa for φm = 5◦. Hence, assuming that the
actual ﬁber imperfection in the composite specimens follows the ﬁber imperfection pattern in the composite
model, the φm in the Panex reinforced composite specimens is between φm = 3◦and φm = 5◦. Furthermore,
using the composite model with Hybon reinforcement, the predicted compression strengths are 1207 MPa
for φm = 2◦and 716.1 MPa for φm = 5◦. Under the same assumption as above, the φm in the Hybon
reinforced composite specimens is between φm = 2◦and φm = 5◦.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
In this project, the compression strength of UD polymer composites was studied using the UMAT model
by [3]. The model accounts for the kink band formation, which is dictated by the matrix plasticity and the
imperfection of the elastic ﬁbers. The robustness of the model was validated for the cases of purely elastic
constituents and purely elastic-plastic matrix. Tensile testing was performed on a polymer matrix material,
at different deformations rates and temperatures, resulting to three different elastic-plastic behaviors of the
matrix material. The elastic behavior of three types of reinforcement was determined through linear density
and single ﬁber testing. By accommodating the elastic behavior of the different ﬁbers and the elastic-plastic
behavior of the different matrix materials into the UMAT model, the compression strength of the composite
was predicted at various imperfection angles.
It is concluded that the deformation rate and test temperature inﬂuences the elastic-plastic behavior of
the polymer matrix material at a level that affects signiﬁcantly the compression strength of UD composites.
The matrix tested at elevated temperature causes the most notable decrease in the compression strength of
the composite model.
In addition to, minimizing the ﬁber misalignment enhances signiﬁcantly the compression strength of the
composite. Hence, during the manufacturing of UD composites, special consideration on minimizing the
ﬁber misalignment should be taken.

CHAPTER 8
Future work
The dimensions of the 8×40 composite model are chosen in accordance with the dimensions of the
uniform section of the UD polymer composite specimens which have been already generated by the DTU
Wind Energy department. These specimens are consisted of the same matrix material and the different types
of reinforcement studied herein. Therefore, by performing uniaxial compression testing on the composite
specimens, at different speeds and temperature conditions, the predicted compression strengths of the
composite systems, such as found in this study through modeling, can be investigated experimentally.
Furthermore, this will allow for the comparison of the calculated stiffness of the E-glass fabric with the
back calculated stiffness from the composite, which is not be made herein. Given the predicted compression
strengths by assuming different values of maximum ﬁber imperfection φm, the ﬁber imperfection in the
composite specimen can also be estimated.
For a future study, a way to mimic the real ﬁber misalignment, is to replace the current imperfection, as
it is deﬁned in the ORIENT subroutine, with a sinusoidal ﬁber imperfection with amplitude and wavelength,
Ref. [1]. A more advanced method, in order to acquire the actual ﬁber misalignment, is to use the tool of
X-ray computed tomography imaging, Ref. [31].

DTU Wind Energy is a department of the Technical University of Denmark with a unique integration of research, education, innovation and 
public/private sector consulting in the field of wind energy. Our activities develop new opportunities and technology for the global and Danish 
exploitation of wind energy. Research focuses on key technical-scientific fields, which are central for the development, innovation and use of wind 
energy and provides the basis for advanced education at the education. 
 
We have more than 240 staff members of which approximately 60 are PhD students. Research is conducted within nine research programmes
