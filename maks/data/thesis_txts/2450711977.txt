and low area successive approximation register analog to digital converter.

project was carried out in the spring semester 2019 in the Department of Electrical

Abstract
A new design for a 14 eﬀective number of bits (ENOB) low power and low area
successive approximation register (SAR) analog to digital converter (ADC) with
digital calibration which is operating at 0.5 Msps is presented in this report. The
SAR ADC is presented in a V 0.13 µm Complementary Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor
(CMOS) process using 1.5 V Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor-Field-Eﬀect-Transistor
(MOSFET)s. The SAR ADC presented uses a comparator designed from a stron-
gARM latch which uses a high gain preampliﬁer to isolate kickback noise, reduce
oﬀset and have low quiescent power consumption. The strongARM latch and pream-
pliﬁer are designed to have low noise, low mismatch, high gain, low internal capac-
itance and low power. The digital to analog converter (DAC) in the SAR ADC is
a charge redistribution chosen for its ability to achieve high resolution while draw-
ing very low power and the charge redistribution’s two-in-one capability as both a
sample and hold circuit and a DAC. The charge redistribution DAC is a sub-radix-2
split capacitor array using a integer bridge capacitor to achieve a very low number
of capacitors in the DAC. The capacitors in the DAC are proprietary Metal-Oxide-
Metal (MOM) capacitors using a combination of fringe capacitance and parallel
plate capacitance and achieves a capacitance of 1.28 fF with a unit area of 3.33 µm2
per capacitor. The digital calibration presented is a digital foreground calibration
technique that can be incorporated into the normal SAR logic by only adding a test
signal for calibration as well as a memory for bit weights. The digital calibration uses
bit weights to compensate for the sub-radix-2 capacitor array which is calibrated
at start up of the device to compensate for mismatch and oﬀset in the SAR ADC.
The SAR ADC achieves 6.988 ENOB without calibration with a unit capacitance
of 1.28582 fF and a total capacitance of 468.0385 fF with a power consumption of
7.95 µW per conversion for the comparator and DAC. The total area is estimated
to be 2275 µm2. The presented design provides a basis for further development and
optimisation.

Introduction
Analog to digtial converters (ADC) are essential tools in any device for Integrated
Circuits (IC) designs as they are liaison between the analog and digital world. In
other words, they are creating a path between the real world where everything is
analog to the digital world. It is more eﬃcient and easier to manipulate digital
signals compared to analog signal and as ADCs enables this, they are of great
importance. As the process for IC designs becomes less demanding and more chips
are used in battery operated devices, a need for compact area and very low power
ADCs have arisen. High accuracy and bit resolution ADCs tend to become very
large area as the number of bit units within a traditional ADC doubles for each bit
and due to noise and mismatch these bit units has to be relatively large to keep
both resolution and accuracy intact in the ADC. This requirement unfortunately
both increase the area and power. The Successive Approximation Register (SAR)
method for ADCs has become more popular as it can have a relatively low area and
high resolution at moderate speeds consuming low power making it commonly used
in low power battery operated devices such as medical equipment or inside advanced
battery operated audio ampliﬁers. This is done by calibrating the SAR ADC using
a digital calibration scheme which can reduce the need for large bit units as the
negative eﬀects of mismatch and noise can be reduced by calibration of the ADC
either simultaneously or at start up of the device or as a combination of both.

the need for low area and power, recent literature as well as established books are
investigated to ﬁnd what could be a sensible solution to achieve this.

Successive Approximation Register
The SAR ADC is categorised as a medium resolution, medium speed with low area
and low power consumption ADC [1, 4, 11, 38, 44, 59]. It is eﬃcient in the frequency
range between roughly 2 kHz and 5 MHz and a resolution between 5 and 14 bits
[1, 2, 44]. This is not a deﬁnitive speciﬁcation range for the SAR ADC and it is
outmanoeuvred by other ADC topologies such as the ﬂash ADC for lower resolution
and higher speed and the sigma delta ADC with higher resolution but at lower speed
[1, 2, 44]. The SAR ADC uses an approximation method to convert from the analog
domain to the digital domain. This is accomplished using four main blocks [1, 4].
A high precision comparator, a DAC and SAR logic which consist of a shift register
and a memory register [1, 4]. In ﬁgure 1 the SAR ADC is shown [11, 44].
Figure 1: Block diagram of the SAR ADC
The DAC in this design samples the input while the SAR ADC converts input analog
voltage to a digital bit value. The comparator is converting the sampled analog input
from the dac to a single digital bit value which is fed to the SAR logic. The SAR logic
turn on one bit at a time starting with the MSB in the DAC. The output of the DAC is
compared to a common voltage on the comparator which decides whether the bit should
be kept on or oﬀin the SAR logic.
Figure 1 shows a diagram of how the SAR ADC can be build. The DAC in this
circuit samples the input signal and uses it during the conversion period [11]. This
3
==PAGE:17==
DAC output signal is connected to the minus pin of the comparator and is changing
according to the output of the SAR logic until the conversion is completed in the SAR
ADC [4, 44]. The minus pin on the comparator is connected to a common voltage
(Vcm) of the DAC this is used as a reference for the SAR ADC and is compared to
the output of the DAC [4, 44]. The SAR logic upon starting the conversion will turn
on the MSB of the DAC which will eventually produce an output which at MSB
ideally is equal to half of the reference voltage (Vref) [4, 27, 48, 60]. The comparator
will then compare the output of the DAC with Vcm and will either produce are
digital ”0” if the DAC output is higher than Vcm or a digital ”1” if it is lower than
Vcm [4, 27]. This approximation will continue until the LSB is reached meanwhile
each comparator output for each conversion is saved in the memory of the SAR
logic eventually giving a word length of bits creating the digital conversion from the
analog signal [1, 4]. The blocks within the SAR ADC will be further examined in
the following sections.

Speciﬁcation Measurements
The performance and quality of a converter are measured using several methods
which show how well the converter is performing when compared to an ideal con-
verter [35]. These test methods have been standardised by the Institute of Electrical
and Electronics Engineers (IEEE) standards association to produce a standard mea-
surement procedure for performance quality control and performance of converters
[19]. In ADCs linearity measurements are performed. These are gain error, oﬀset
error, Diﬀerential Non-Linearity (DNL), monotonicity and Integral Non-Linearity
(INL) [4, 19]. Furthermore, the bit accuracy and noise performance are measured to
show the Signal-to-Noise-and-Distortion Ratio (SINAD) which eﬀectively gives the
Eﬀective Numbers of Bits (ENOB) by rewriting the expression for SINAD [4, 19, 35].
The test methods all require either a very high resolution DAC compared to the res-
olution of the ADC or an ideal DAC to convert the digital output of the ADC to an
analog signal [4, 19]. The high resolution DAC is used when testing the ADC as a
Device Under Test (DUT) on a circuit board but can also be used during simulation.
An ideal DAC can be created using Verilog-A which is a hardware descriptive lan-
guage within the cadence virtuoso tools to simulate ideal analog circuits [4, 19, 52].
The ideal DAC circuit is used for simulations to exclude any deviation that may be
caused by other devices in the ADC which is being tested [19, 52]. The diﬀerent

Comparator
The comparator is an essential component in ADCs as it performs as a single bit
ADC and is usually the component that converts the analog signal to the digital
domain where multi-bit design is then able to convert the signal into multiple bits
for higher resolutions [1, 4, 25, 38, 44, 47, 64]. The comparator does the conversion
by comparing two analog signals with each other and a simple high or low will be
returned at the output depending on which of the inputs that are highest [1, 4, 25, 38,
44, 47, 64]. There are two common types of comparators a continuous comparator
and a latched comparator [1, 4, 25, 38, 44, 64]. The continuous comparator which can
be constructed from an operational ampliﬁer (OpAmp) in a open loop no feedback
conﬁguration constructed from four MOSFETs with a input pair and a current
mirror [1, 4, 25, 44].
The latched comparator circuit is more complex than the
continous comparator as it has added circuitry which consist of a positive feedback
within the comparator and it is controlled by a pair of clocked latches [1, 1, 4, 22, 25,
41, 44, 47]. The continuous comparator draws a continuously current as it always
sense and compare inputs even with it is not intended which furthermore at slow
speed it suﬀers from hysteresis and propagation delays due to the slow slew rate of
open loop OpAmps. The hysteresis can be removed but it requires added circuitry
consisting of a latch outside the comparator [1, 4, 25, 44]. The latched comparator
speciﬁcally the strongARM latch, ideally draws no current when it is idle due to
the latches within the comparator and shows very good performance in SAR ADCs
when low area and power are a requirement [7, 11, 29, 41, 47, 57, 60].

Digital to Analog Converter
There are three diﬀerent main topologies that are used to create Digital to Analog
Converter DACs in CMOS circuits: voltage scaling DAC using a resistor array,
charge redistribution DAC using a capacitor array and current steering DAC using
a array of MOSFETs to drive a current [1, 4, 44]. When the DACs are used in a
binary weighted conﬁguration, meaning a radix-2 setup (r) = 2, the number of units
of MOSFETs, resistors or capacitors will double for each bit in the DAC [1, 4, 38, 44].
This greatly increase the area of a DAC when it is to deliver a relative high number of
bits where high diﬀers for each topology [1, 4, 38, 44]. The current steering DAC and
voltage scaling DAC both draw high currents compared to the charge redistribution
DAC as the current steering DAC draws a constant current which is doubled for each
bit and the voltage scaling DAC draws a current through the resistors in the DAC
whereas the charge redistribution DAC ideally only draws current when charging
the capacitor array used at the beginning of a conversion. This charge is then later
redistributed across the capacitors doing a conversion. Capacitors suﬀers very low
process variation compared to resistors which can very by up to 20% from the desired
value [1, 4, 27, 38, 44]. This makes the charge redistribution DAC a preferred choice
when low power is a priority, especially in SAR ADCs as it is possible to achieve high
resolution and low area with charge redistribution DAC by utilising both design and
layout techniques to decrease the number of unit capacitors together with using a
very low unit capacitor [5, 13, 28, 36]. Furthermore, the charge redistribution DAC’s
capability to store a charge makes it possible to combine the S&H circuit with the
DAC eﬀectively removing the need for a separate S&H circuit [1, 4, 27].

very little variation in the process the other type is a Metal-Oxide-Metal (MOM)
capacitor which does not have an insulation between the metal layers. It only has
oxide which is placed between all metals [4, 9, 34, 44, 51]. In the PDK for this
process the MIM capacitors are hard constrained to a minimum size which the
foundry promises will be within their variation measurements. This hard constrain
limits how low Cu can become in the capacitor array of the DAC and still perform
within the speciﬁcations of the foundry. On the other hand, the MOM capacitor can
be created using the parasitic capacitance (CP) which occurs between metals making
it possible to create very small capacitors which use very low area if done correctly in
the layout which helps achieve both low area and low power [4, 9, 24, 32, 34, 44, 51].

7.3
Digital Calibration
Digital calibration is a tool used in ADCs to correct nonlinearities in the ADC which
are commonly caused by noise or mismatch of the DAC in the ADC [3, 23, 56, 62, 63].
Calibration of bits can eventually increase the dynamic range and hence the SINAD
by artiﬁcially increasing the dynamic range by including more bits without increasing
the size of the ADC by the use of redundancy which introduces the ability to correct
errors in real time when the ADC is performing a conversion [3, 23, 56, 62, 63].
Digital calibration can be done by performing calibration in the foreground and
background where foreground is either done at start up of the device or by demand.
Background calibration runs in parallel with the actual conversion [3, 23, 56, 62, 63].
It is possible to both correct errors caused by mismatch with the use of digital
calibration and redundancy as this can be calibrated with digital circuitry but also
correct errors either caused by noise or nonlinearities that may occur in the other
blocks of the ADC [3, 23, 56, 62, 63]. The redundancy prolongs the settling of a bit
making room for errors [3, 23, 56, 62, 63]. A foreground digital calibration technique
is favourable for low power and area as it can be implemented directly into the ADC
without introducing more circuitry besides an extra signal into the SAR logic for
the calibration [24, 56, 62].
7.3.1
Foreground Digital Calibration
Calibration is used to limit or completely eliminate the errors which may occur due
to mismatch caused by the small size of Cu as described in sections 7.1 and 7.2.1
[24, 62]. When using calibration the output of the SAR ADC is not the actual
digital output which is generated doing the bit cycling mode in the SAR ADC
[62]. It is instead an output that is calculated within the digital calibration logic
doing the conversion which is incorporated within the SAR logic [62]. This done
to be able to correct for conversion errors while the conversion is running, which is
similar to background calibration but the actual calibration is not in parallel with
the conversion instead this calibration type calibrates the MSBs capacitor within the
capacitor array of the DAC eﬀectively calculating the weight of each MSB capacitor
at start up of the device and correcting for mismatch [62]. These weights are added
or subtracted depending on the comparator output during a normal conversion [62].

The object of this project and the SAR ADC was to test the boundaries of low
power and low area in a high resolution SAR ADC by a using very small propri-
etary capacitors that far exceed the lower limit which is usually set by mismatch
and noise in DAC as this is strongly related to area and capacitance of the capaci-
tors. The nature of the design required a few failed designs especially for the DAC
as the DAC was intended to be very small which hinted at a split capacitor array
in the DAC from ﬁgure 34. This will normally require a bridge capacitor with a
capacitance which would have to be a fraction of the unit capacitor. With further
research many had proven that a integer bridge capacitor would perform just as well
as the conventional fraction bridge capacitor and even reduce mismatch because all
of the capacitors in the capacitor array have the same origin capacitance which
comes from the unit capacitor [48]. With the split capacitor design the number of
unit capacitors in the capacitor array is reduced to just a fraction of the original
number but with smaller capacitors the problem of noise and mismatch becomes an
inevitable problem. A digital calibration solution is used to reduce or fully remove
the problem caused by physical limitation of the capacitor array . There are many
solutions to digital calibration but many will require added circuitry and complexity
to do the calibration [9, 56]. A solution, as shown in this project, is used which can
easily be incorporated into the normal SAR logic only adding a test signal and a
memory register to store bit weights and the calibrated bit weights. The bit weights
in the digital calibration has been calculated with several iterations before the bit
weights was found. The bit weights ideally allow for at least full range of 14 ENOB.
Even though the lowest bit weight is eight, this is still well within the limit of 14
ENOB as the diﬀerence of one LSB in a 18-bit ADC is 16 times larger than the LSB
of 14-bit ADC. The capacitor array may be changed from a split capacitor array to
non split capacitor array to remove factoring problem that occur between the MSB
76
==PAGE:90==
and LSB side of the split capacitor array [1].
The next process for this design would be to categorise and organise error correction
of each block within the SAR ADC starting with the comparator. The comparator
has to be tested with a full corner simulation where oﬀset in the comparator is tested
though the corners which may be caused by mismatch, noise or a combination of
both. The oﬀset in strongARM latch which may occur may either be corrected by
physically modifying the strongARM latch either by sizing the MOSFETs within
the strongARM latch to have less noise and mismatch or by introducing oﬀset can-
cellation by using small unit capacitors which can be connected or disconnected to
the nodes in the strongARM latch [41]. A second method is to incorporate oﬀset
reduction with the digital calibration as this has been described by Zhang et al. [62].
This is done by using a bottom up calibration of the calibrated bit weights meaning
that with two foreground calibrations it is possible to correct for both mismatch in
the capacitor array and oﬀset by calibration for both oﬀset and mismatch in the ca-
pacitor array and the comparator [62]. This may be done by using the same digital
calibration as proposed by Zhang et al. [62]. Secondly, the capacitor array. The
capacitors in the capacitor array has to be carefully laid out when implementing
the design in a layout as the capacitance in the unit capacitor are purely parasitic
capacitance between two metal plates meaning that any metal close to the capac-
itor may introduce further parasitic capacitance to the capacitor. If the layout is
made poorly this real parasitic capacitor can have a large capacitance which will
eventually create large mismatch between the capacitors in the capacitor array and
completely ruin the decision for the ADC [9, 34, 61].

Conclusion
Development of a low power and low area SAR ADC is an interesting combination
that allows for a general purpose ADC that can be easily integrated into a new de-
sign. Furthermore, with reduction of the size of new devices and processes the need
for new ways to incorporate highly eﬃcient and high resolution ADCs are needed.
The conventional charge redistribution has a limit caused by noise and mismatch
which is strongly related to the size of the process. This shows that new techniques
for producing ADCs and especially DACs are essential.
The objective of this project was to investigate the possibility and to produce a
reasonable solution to a highly eﬃcient, high resolution, low area and power 500
ksps SAR ADC using digital calibration in a 1.5V 0.13 µm process. At the end of
this project a perfect design that comply with the given speciﬁcations is not ready,
however, a big leap towards a functional SAR ADC has been taken and many op-
portunities to develop and optimise the system has been investigated.
The results obtained might suggest that the split capacitor array produce a lot of
oﬀset due to the integer bridge capacitor which could result in poor performance
of the DAC in the SAR ADC and other options might need to be investigated.
Designing the SAR ADC was complicated by the digital calibration and the sub-
radix-2 capacitor array which proved to be time consuming to integrate in the design.
In conclusion, this project provided an initial design of the SAR ADC and a basis
for further investigations and optimisations.
