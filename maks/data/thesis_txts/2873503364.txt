Abstract
Herbal and plant-based food supplements are widely consumed, yet their elemental composition is of-
ten poorly regulated. These products are typically concentrated and taken repeatedly over time, which
increases the risk of toxic trace element accumulation in the body, such as aluminium, cadmium, lead,
nickel and arsenic. They are known to cause adverse e!ects including kidney damage, neuro-toxicity and
cancer upon prolonged exposure. This thesis presents a targeted elemental analysis of 27 commercially
available supplements in Denmark using inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-MS), fo-
cusing on toxic trace elements (Al, Ni, As, Cd and Pb). 13 additional essential trace- and macro-elements
were also analyzed. Samples included powders, capsules, gummies and liquids. Homogenization methods
were optimized based on sample form, and digestion was carried out using microwave-assisted acid diges-
tion using HNO3. The ICP-MS triple quadrupole was used to address interferences via reaction/collision
modes. Certiﬁed reference materials (CRMs) and blanks were used to validate method performance.
Quantiﬁable concentrations were obtained in 113 out of 135 element–sample combinations, indicating
widespread presence of toxic elements across the tested supplements. While all toxic element concentra-
tions were within EU regulation limits, a few products approached levels of concern when daily intake
and exposure were considered. Concentration ranges varied notably between elements and products. Al
concentrations were particularly variable, ranging from 1.4 to 1433 mg/kg, with outliers such as sample
P02 (a Brahmi-based powder) contributing up to 80% of the tolerable weekly intake (TWI) for a child.
Pb and Cd concentrations were generally low but showed notable spikes in the same sample. ICP-OES
analysis of macro-elements revealed that some products, particularly T01, contributed more than 40% of
the recommended intake (RI) for calcium, likely due to the use of excipients like dicalcium phosphate.
These elevated concentrations underline the need for continued regulatory attention and improved con-
sumer awareness regarding supplement safety, particularly given the repeated, long-term consumption of
concentrated herbal supplement products.

1
Introduction
The global consumption of dietary supplements has increased over the past two decades [1]. Previous
studies show that 60% of the Danish population take food supplements, with around 10% of people con-
suming plant based supplements [2]. While food supplements may o!er certain health beneﬁts, most
healthy individuals in Denmark do not require them if they maintain a balanced and varied diet. The only
general recommendation is a vitamin D supplement during the winter months, with iron supplements
advised in some cases for women [3]. Unlike pharmaceutical drugs, food supplements can be marketed
without pre-clinical or clinical trials (Directive 2002/46/EC; Regulation (EC) 1161/2011). Instead, they
are treated as foodstu! and must follow labeling requirements, such as listing the active substances, the
recommended daily intake and safety warnings [4, 5]. Food supplements are easily accessible and sold
not only in pharmacies but also in supermarkets, petrol stations and online [6].
While food supplements’ original purpose is to complement the daily diet, they are advertised for more
speciﬁc e!ects [7]. Among them, herbal food supplements form a rapidly growing subcategory. Derived
from plant materials such as roots, leaves, stems and seeds, these products are often marketed for their
reported health beneﬁts, including claims of stress relief, immune support, cognitive enhancement and
hormonal balance [8, 9]. However, in the EU, it is not allowed to claim or suggest that a supplement can
prevent, treat or relieve symptoms of disease. Such statements are considered as untrue medical claims
and are prohibited [5]. Nevertheless, many consumers continue to associate herbal food supplements
with therapeutic e!ects, unaware of the limited scientiﬁc evidence [6]. This trend is often based on the
perception that herbal food supplements, especially those labeled as "natural," are inherently healthy, safe
to use and unlikely to cause side e!ects or interact with other treatments [9, 10].
While herbal supplements are often perceived as safe due to their natural origin, their composition is
highly variable [9]. Such variability not only include essential elements but also the accumulation of
unwanted toxic elements, including cadmium (Cd), lead (Pb), arsenic (As), nickel (Ni) and aluminum
(Al) [11]. Heavy metal contamination rank among the most frequently reported product categories in
the Rapid Alert System for Food and Feed (RASFF), highlighting ongoing safety concerns. These toxic
elements can enter the supply chain through environmental exposure (soil, water and air), contamination
during processing, storage or packaging [12].

Given the above concerns, accurate quantiﬁcation of the elemental content in food supplements is essen-
tial for consumer safety and regulatory compliance. Analytical techniques such as inductively coupled
plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-MS) and inductively coupled optical emission spectroscopy (ICP-OES)

[13]. Both methods typically require thorough sample preparation, often through acid digestion using
nitric acid (HNO3), to e!ectively break down complex matrices and give accurate metal determination
[11]. This study investigates the elemental composition of 27 commercially available herbal food sup-
plements, with a particular focus on quantiﬁcation of toxic trace elements (Cd, Pb, As, Ni and Al). Both
essential trace and macro elements were analyzed, with only macro elements (Ca, K, Na, Mg, S, P), brieﬂy
discussed. The essential trace elements included Co, Mo, Se, Cu, Mn, Zn and Fe.

1.1
Sources for Toxic Trace Elements in Herbal Food Supplements
Herbal supplements, like other foodstu! and pharmaceuticals, are susceptible to trace element contamina-
tion through environmental and industrial sources. Plants can absorb toxic metals from polluted air, soil
and water during cultivation, which then carry over into the ﬁnal product [14]. The trace elements include
both essential and toxic metals, which can make their way from cultivation to the consumer if contamina-
tion is not properly controlled. While some essential nutrients like Fe and Zn may be intentionally added,
this study focuses on non-essential potentially toxic elements such as Cd, Pb, As, Al and Ni, due to their
health risks and lack of biological function.
Figure 2: Overview of the possible contamination sources associated with heavy metal contamination. Created
using PowerPoint (PP).
The main source of toxic trace element contamination in herbal food supplements is closely linked to
agricultural practices. Medicinal herbs can absorb toxic trace elements from their growing environment
through the air, soil or water, as well as fertilizers and pesticides used to improve yield or control pests [15,
16]. The soil can be polluted by long term use of industrial wastewater for irrigation or by the deposition of
airborne pollution from nearby metal mines, factories, smelters or busy roads [17]. Toxic trace elements
can therefore accumulate in plants over time when exposed to such contamination. Typically the roots
contain the highest concentrations followed by leaves, stems and then the ﬂowers although the pattern can
vary from species and soil conditions [18]. Some metals such as Ni are needed in plants in trace amounts,
but even these can accumulate to toxic levels in contaminated environments [19]. According to the US en-
vironmental protection Agency, over ten million sites globally have soils contaminated with various heavy
metals and metalloids, at concentrations exceeding safety limits [20]. In addition to concerns about metal
contamination, there is a growing attention on microbial contamination, which could spread infections
caused by multi-resistant bacteria [21]. Herbs commonly associated with traditional Chinese medicine
(TCM) and Ayurvedic medicine have become popular, but some of these plants contain substances that
can disrupt hormonal balance or interact with prescribed medications [10].

In addition to environmental uptake during growth, several other pathways can introduce trace metal con-
tamination into herbal supplements. Herbal supplement from the ocean (Marine supplements) like algae
also pose contamination risks, as they can accumulate metal(oids) like As and Cd directly from polluted
water [22]. Post-harvest contamination may occur through exposure to airborne dust or industrial envi-
ronments, especially if storage or transport conditions are inadequate [17]. Processing and equipment
contamination can happen during production of the supplement. For example, metals like Ni can be re-
leased during drying, grinding or extraction processes involving stainless steel equipment, while corroded
aluminum tools may shed Al. If cleaning is not done adequately between runs cross contamination could
also occur [17]. Packaging plays a dual role and can both prevent contamination or become a source of
it. Products sold in bulk without packaging have shown higher levels of Pb and Cd than those in sealed
containers [23].
In summary, the presence of As, Cd, Pb, Ni and Al in herbal supplements is driven by a combination
of environmental uptake, industrial inputs, processing practices and packaging materials. While some
contamination sources can be controlled through better manufacturing practices, others such as soil and
marine contamination, require more strict monitoring and sourcing standards. To assess the health risks
associated with these elements, it is important to understand their distinct chemical behavior and toxico-
logical e!ects.
1.2
Chemical and Toxicological Properties
1.2.1
Toxic Trace Elements in Supplements
Food supplements can contain a variety of elements, including essential trace elements added for their
nutritional value and toxic metals or metalloids that may be present as contaminants. Although some
elements are required for normal physiological function, both essential and non-essential elements can
pose health risks when present at elevated concentrations [24–28]. Even trace amounts (µg/kg levels) of
heavy metals can accumulate over time, leading to chronic health problems.
Arsenic (As, Atomic number 33, Atomic weight 74.92 g/mol) is a naturally accruing metalloid. As
has only one stable isotope, 75As, which accounts for 100% of naturally occurring arsenic [29]. It is
deﬁned as a metalloid but because of its toxicological e!ects it is sometimes also deﬁned as a heavy
metal [29]. Normally, it occurs in various chemical forms, which greatly inﬂuence its toxicity. Inorganic
arsenic (iAs) species from arsenite (As3+) and arsenate (As5+), are signiﬁcantly more toxic than organic
arsenic compounds. As3+ is particularly harmful due to its high a"nity for thiol groups in proteins,
impairing critical enzyme functions such as pyruvate dehydrogenase and disrupting cellular respiration
[24]. Chronic exposure to inorganic arsenic (iAs) through diet or drinking water is associated with an
increased risk of skin, lung and bladder cancer, as shown in human epidemiological studies [24, 30].

Cadmium (Cd, Atomic number 48, Atomic weight 112.41 g/mol) is a soft, bluish-white heavy metal,
found mainly in the crust of the earth. It is chemically similar to zinc but is signiﬁcantly more toxic as it has
no biological function [25]. Cd has eight naturally occurring isotopes, with 114Cd (28.7% abundance) and
111Cd (12.8% abundance) being the two most abundant [31]. Cd accumulates in the body and especially in
the liver and kidneys and have been linked to kidney failure and increased cancer risk [8, 32]. Cadmium is
most harmful in its charged form (Cd2+), which can accumulate inside cells. It disrupts the mitochondria
by interfering with key steps in ATP production, producing less ATP and more reactive oxygen species
(ROS) [25, 33]. This can weaken the body’s defenses and result in damage to DNA, proteins and lipids
[25, 33]. Due to its very long biological half-life of 30 years, cadmium can accumulate over decades,
making even trace exposures a signiﬁcant health concern [31, 33].
Lead (Pb, Atomic number 82, Atomic weight 207 g/mol) is a dense, bluish-gray metal, found in the Earths
crust [34]. Like Cd, Pb is a heavy metal and has no know biological function in humans [35]. Lead has
four stable isotopes: 204Pb (1.4%), 206Pb (24.1%), 207Pb(22.1%) and 208Pb being the most abundant at
52.4% [34]. Chemically, Pb is relatively unreactive, but biologically it is highly toxic [26, 36]. Pb is a
highly toxic metal that a!ects nearly all organ systems [37]. In adults, chronic exposure can cause anemia,
hypertension, kidney and brain damage and reproductive issues such as infertility and miscarriage [26,
37]. Lead is most harmful in its ionic form (Pb2+), which can enter cells and disrupt important enzymes
[26, 37]. Pb accumulates in the bones and is cleared from the body very slowly, with a biological half-life
of about 20 to 30 years [36]
Aluminium (Al, Atomic number 13, Atomic weight 26.98 g/mol) is a light silvery-white metal and used
in a broad range of stu! from beverage cans to airplanes [38, 39]. Al is the metal found in the greatest
abundance in the Earth’s crust. Al has only one stable isotope, 28Al, which accounts for 100% of nat-
ural Al [38]. While not a heavy metal, Al can still exert toxic e!ects if ingested in high concentrations
[27]. Al has no known biological function in humans. The ionic form of Al (Al3+) can bind to cellular
proteins, such as transferrin (90% is bound in the blood) which facilitates its distribution [27, 39]. Al
have a tendency to accumulate particularly bones [39]. Despite these potential health concerns, Al is not
considered carcinogenic at levels typically found in the diet [39]. Although Al is generally considered less
toxic than heavy metals, high exposure levels have been associated with neurotoxicity and bone disorders,
particularly in individuals with impaired kidney function [27, 39].

1.2.2
Essential Trace/Macro Elements in Supplements
Food supplements often include a range of essential trace and macro elements that support critical phys-
iological functions. Essential trace elements are required only in small amounts but are vital for many
processes. Cobalt (Co) is a structural component of vitamin B12 and is distributed though most of the
body [42]. Selenium (Se) helps protect cells in mammals by being part of enzymes like glutathione per-
oxidase, which reduce harmful oxidants and prevent cell damage [43]. Molybdenum (Mo) helps the body
process proteins, DNA and break down toxins [44]. Copper (Cu) is a key cofactor in enzymes essential for
energy and brain function [45]. Manganese (Mn) supports metabolism, antioxidant defense, bone health
and blood clotting [46]. Zinc (Zn) supports enzyme function, immunity, growth and cell processes. It
is also important for wound healing, taste and development during early stages of life [47]. Iron (Fe) is
essential for oxygen transport in the blood and muscles and supports energy, growth, brain function and
hormone production. The essential trace elements are required in amounts below 45 mg/day to support
essential functions in the body.
Macro elements, also known as bulk minerals, are needed in larger amounts. Calcium (Ca) is essential
for the development and maintenance of bones and teeth. It is also important for muscle contraction and
nerve transmission [48]. Magnesium (Mg) supports over 300 enzyme systems involved in processes like

protein synthesis, nerve and muscle function, blood sugar control and blood pressure regulation. It is also
essential for energy production, bone structure and the synthesis of DNA and RNA [49]. Potassium (K)
helps regulate nerve impulses and muscle contractions. Potassium is essential for normal cell function by
maintaining ﬂuid balance and works closely with sodium to regulate the ﬂuid volume, including blood
plasma [50]. Sodium (Na) is vital for regulating extracellular ﬂuid volume and blood pressure and it
works with K to maintain proper cell function. Its role in the sodium–potassium pump helps generate
electrical signals and maintain ﬂuid balance across cell membranes [51]. Sulfur (S), mainly obtained
from methionine in protein from the diet, supports metabolism and redox regulation, while methionine
speciﬁcally controls the initiation of protein synthesis [52]. Phosphorus (P) is an important component of
bones, teeth, DNA and RNA and contributes to the structure of cell membranes through phospholipids. It
is central to the body’s energy metabolism as ATP and is involved in the phosphorylation of proteins and
sugars. P also plays an important role in enzyme activation and the regulation of gene transcription [53].
All these essential macro elements/minerals are needed in amounts from 750 mg/day and 3500 mg/day.

2.1
Sample Preparation
An accurate trace metal analysis of food supplements require a thorough sample preparation, to ensure cor-
rect and representative measurements [13]. Sample preparation is a crucial step and di!erent methods can
be used to archive a sample ready for analysis. Typically in elemental analysis methods this consists of ho-
mogenization and acid digestion [66]. Homogenization ensures that a small aliquot taken for analysis will
correctly reﬂect the overall composition of the product. Following homogenization, microwave-assisted
digestion using acid is used to break down the matrix and release bound metals into solution. Together
these steps ensure that the analytes of interest are fully solubilized and provide a clear homogeneous so-
lution, which is necessary when wanting to analyze elements using ICP-MS and ICP-OES.

2.1.1
Homogenization
Food supplement (whether pills, capsules or powders) must be thoroughly homogenized prior to trace
metal analysis. It ensures repeatability and eliminate heterogeneity in a sample, by reducing the particle
size and distributing everything uniformly in the sample [13]. Even though food supplements are of-
ten mass produced and are expected to be fairly uniform, the composition can still exhibit variations in
composition. It might result in some subsamples containing higher or lower metal content than the true
average.
There is no speciﬁc recommended sample size for food supplements analysis. The appropriate number de-
pends on the analytical goal, the method used and the visual uniformity between units. Food supplements
are typically homogenized during manufacturing before being compressed into pill forms, which is why
we expect a certain degree of uniformity. Based on literature, a sample size of 20 units has been considered
acceptable for assessing homogeneity [67]. Di!erent homogenization methods are required depending on
sample type. Tablets and capsules because of their small size, have straightforward homogenization us-
ing relatively simple techniques. Other samples types, such as gummies, require di!erent considerations
due to composition. Once homogenized, replicate sub-portions (often triplicates) are typically analyzed
to check the precision of the preparation. High variability between replicates could indicate remaining
inhomogeneity, potentially a!ecting accuracy of the measured concentration.
2.1.2
Microwave Assisted Digestion
Microwave assisted digestion is one of the most used techniques in food element analysis [13]. It is
particularly essential for elemental analysis of solid samples, which must be broken down into liquids
before introduction to the ICP [68]. Typically 0.2-0.5 g of sample is placed into a quartz vial, which is
preferred for their resistance to acids and high temperatures, but other vial types might be better for other
acids. Along with the sample, between 4-8 mL of concentrated HNO3 is added to the vial [69]. HNO3
is highly oxidative, meaning it causes substances to lose electrons during a reaction [70]. In doing so,
a substance being digested is oxidized while HNO3 is reduced. For example when HNO3 is added to a
herbal food supplement it chemically breaks down organic molecules into more simple compounds by
oxidation [70].

2.2
ICP-MS Instrumental Conﬁguration
The general principle of ICP-MS is to use high temperature Argon (Ar) plasma to ionize the elements
which are going to be analyzed. ICP-MS detects the ion of the element based on their mass to charge ratio
(m/z) and reports the signal in counts per second (cps). ICP-MS generally has very low detection limits,
as low os parts per trillion (ppt) and is a good instrument to use when analyzing trace elements in food
supplement, especially trace metals that are usually found in very small amounts [66].
The focus will be on the general principles of ICP, with particular emphasis on the Thermo Fisher iCAP
TQ ICP-MS. The iCAP TQ is a triple quadropole (TQ) system. The sections will be described as follows:
(1) sample introduction, (2) Torch and plasma source, (3) Vacuume interface and ion optics, (4) mass
analyzer (Q1-Q2-Q3) and (5) ion detector which can be seen in Figure 5.

2.2.1
Instrument Parts
(1) Sample Introduction
The sample introduction system involves getting the liquid samples into the
plasma source for ion generation [13]. The sample introduction systems main focus is to generate the
aerosol which is needed for the plasma to ionize the sample. The system starts by the liquid sample being
carried into the instrument via a peristaltic pump, which delivers the sample to the nebulizer [75]. There
are di!erent kind of nebulizers, but the one used in the Thermo ﬁsher ICAP TQ ICP-MS is a concentric
nebulizer and in this case in PFA. A standard concentric nebulizer provides good sensitivity and signal
stability [13].
The liquid is drawn through a ﬁne capillary and converted into a ﬁne aerosol mist by the action of a high-
speed Ar gas stream. The liquid is sheared into ﬁne droplets by the pressure and velocity of the Ar gas
stream [13, 75]. This narrow capillary is prone to blockage when samples contain high levels of total
dissolved solids (TDS). The aerosol mist enters a spray chamber, which ﬁlters out larger droplets. In a
cyclonic spray chamber design, centrifugal force is used to separate droplets based on their size [75, 76].
As the Ar and aerosol swirl inside the chamber, the smaller droplets follow the gas stream while the bigger
droplets hit the chamber wall and drain away [77].
This produces a ﬁne, steady aerosol with a droplet size under 10 µm [13]. The smaller size of the aerosol
allows the sample to be ionized more e!ectively in the plasma. It improves stability and reduces matrix
e!ects. The resulting aerosol is carried to the torch via a small injector. The sample introduction system
is often called the Achilles’ heel of ICP-MS due to its ine"ciency. Typically, only about 2% of the sample
reaches the plasma [77]. Although this can vary depending on the matrix and the sample introduction
method, it remains the most limiting step in the analysis [13].

(2) Plasma Ion Source
The Aerosol is transported by the Ar-gas into the plasma torch, which is a quartz
tube surrounded by a Radio Frequency (RF) coil. A RF generator supplies energy to the coil, creating
an oscillating electromagnetic ﬁeld [78]. When the Ar gas is ignited, it forms a plasma with extremely
high temperatures, typically ranging from 6000 to 10,000 K [77]. It is made from a collision-induced
ionization of the Ar gas in a chain reaction [13]. When Ar ﬂows through, a high-voltage spark ionizes
some Ar atoms by stripping electrons. Electrons are accelerated and collide with other atoms, triggering
a chain reaction that forms a plasma of Ar-atoms, ions and electrons [13, 78]. The continuous RF energy
keeps the plasma sustained at this level. Ar is used because of its high ﬁrst ionization potential at 15.8 eV,
which is su"cient to ionize most elements in the periodic table under plasma conditions [13, 77].
The sample aerosol enters the center of the plasma and in a millisecond the solvent evaporates [13]. The
solid particle is vaporized forming a gas-phase molecule, which are broken into neutral atoms. Finally,
the atom is ionized by collision with the energetic Ar-electrons (Figure 6) [77, 79]. The positively charged
ions are then directed further into the mass analyzer.
Figure 6: Ion formation in the plasma source in ICP-MS. Created using PP with inspiration from [80].
In the ionization phase, the plasma’s extreme temperature (10,000 K) provides su"cient energy to remove
electrons from the neutral atom, which creates positively charged ions (M →M+ + e→) [13]. 70–90% of
the atoms are converted to ions for most elements, which is very e"cient even for elements with high ﬁrst
ionization potential, such as As and Se [77, 79].
The plasma torch typically consists of three concentric quartz tubes. The outer tube, middle tube and the
sample injector. Ar-gas ﬂows between the outer and middle tubes ( 12–17 L/min) to sustain the plasma,
while an auxiliary Ar ﬂow ( 1 L/min), between the middle tube and the injector, adjusts the plasma position.
A third ﬂow ( 1 L/min) carries the aerosolized sample through the injector and into the center of the plasma
[13]. Finally, the generated ions are extracted from the plasma and directed into the mass spectrometer
where they are separated based on their m/z [77].

(3) Interface and Ion Optics
After the ICP, the stream of ions along with the Ar-gas must be transferred
into a high vacuum mass spectrometer (MS). The interface region connects the plasma at atmospheric
pressure (760torr) to the high-vacuum mass analyzer (10→6torr), using two metal cones with oriﬁces (a
sampler cone and a skimmer cone) [78]. The sampler cone (oriﬁce at 0.8–1.2 mm), faces the plasma. It
samples the central part of the plasma, where analyte ions are most concentrated. Behind it, the skimmer
cone (with a smaller oriﬁce, 0.4–0.8mm) further shapes the ion beam [13, 78]. After passing through the
skimmer, the pressure drops to around 1–2 torr. The interface housing is water-cooled and made from
heat-conductive materials to withstand plasma temperatures [13].
After the skimmer cone, the ions pass into the ion lens system. The lenses help remove most neutral
particles and photons, guiding the positively charged ions toward the mass analyzer [77]. The ion optics
often include a quadrupole ion deﬂector and in Thermo instruments the ion beam is turned 90°to eliminate
photons and neutral gas [13, 81]. It optimizes transmission of analyte ions while minimizing background.
Their main role is to guide positively charged ions from the plasma, through the interface region, into the
mass analyzer [81]. At the same time, they prevent neutral species, photons and particulates from reaching
the detector. Unwanted species can increase background noise or cause contamination, leading to signal
instability and higher maintenance needs [77].
(4) Mass Analyzer
When the ions reach the mass spectrometer, they pass through several components.
The Thermo iCAP TQ ICP-MS is a triple quadrupole ICP-MS, which means it has a collision/reaction
cell (CRC) between two quadrupole mass ﬁlters [13]. Each quadrupole consists of four parallel rods with
RF/DC voltages that can be tuned to allow only ions of a particular m/z ratio [82]. The ﬁrst quadrupole
(Q1), sometimes called the precursor ﬁlter, is located before the CRC [83]. The second quadrupole (Q3),
the analyzer ﬁlter, is after the CRC. The CRC (Q2) is a multipole (quadrupole or hexapole) that operates
in di!erent modes depending on the chosen operational mode [13, 83]. When no cell gas is used, it can
act as a simple ion guide. When cell gas is introduced, the cell can either collide with interfering ions or
induce a chemical reaction. It can be ﬁlled with a gas such as helium, hydrogen, oxygen or ammonia [82].
An example of O2 and He gas in the CRC is visualized in Figue 7
For example, in He collision mode, polyatomic interfering ions undergo collisions with He atoms and
lose kinetic energy. Kinetic Energy Discrimination (KED) allows only the lighter and faster analyte ions
to pass through, while larger polyatomic interferences that lose more energy are e!ectively suppressed
(Figure 7) [13, 82]. In reaction mode, speciﬁc interfering ions can be converted into di!erent polyatomic
ions, or the analyte can be transformed into a new polyatomic ion with a distinct m/z to avoid interference.
The two mode are called "on-mass" and “mass-shift” techniques (Figue 7) [83]. Q3 then ensures only the
analyte or its product ion reaches the detector, free from most interferences. In summary, the conﬁguration
is Q1 – CRC – Q3.

(5) Ion Detector
After Q3, ions hit the detector which is typically an electron multiplier [13]. Each ion
that hits the ﬁrst dynode produces a cascade of electrons, amplifying the signal, which is then measured in
cps [84]. Modern ICP-MS detectors operate in two modes. Pulse counting is used for very low signals by
counting individual ions. Analog mode handles higher signal intensities. Pulse counting mode is typically
linear from 0 to around 106 cps, while analog mode is e!ective in the range of approximately 104 to 1010
cps [13]. Switching between these allows for a wide dynamic range, up to 9 orders of magnitude, making
it possible to measure both trace and major elements in a single run [13]. The ICP-MS’s detector registers
ion counts that the software converts into intensities which is proportional to an analyte concentration
[84].
2.2.2
Interferences in ICP-MS
Spectral interferences in ICP-MS occur when ions other than the analyte contribute to the signal at a given
m/z, leading to inaccurate results. Interferences can be broadly categorized into four main types: isobaric,
polyatomic, doubly charged ion interferences and spectral overlaps [13, 85]. Isobaric interferences hap-
pen when two di!erent elements have isotopes with the same m/z. Since the MS cannot distinguish
between ions of di!erent elements that share the same m/z, their signals are summed. For instance, 114Sn
and 114Cd both appear at m/z 114, so the presence of Tin (Sn) can interfere with the accurate quantiﬁca-
tion of Cd [86]. Polyatomic interferences occur when ions formed from combinations of plasma gases,
solvents or sample matrix components that have the same m/z as the analyte. These molecular species
are created in the plasma and can signiﬁcantly a!ect trace measurements. A common example is ArCl on
m/z 75 interfering with 75As, a!ecting As determination [85]. Doubly charged ion interferences occur
when some elements, particularly heavier ones, form ions with a +2 charge in the plasma [13]. Because

ICP-MS detects m/z, a doubly charged ion with mass M will appear at M/2, potentially overlapping with
the signal of a singly charged ion at that m/z. For example, 136Ba2+ appears at m/z 68, which is the same as
68Zn+, potentially leading to an overestimation of Zn if Ba is present [13, 85]. Spectral overlap, refers to
situations where an intense signal at one m/z spills over into neighboring masses due to limited resolution
or detector saturation. Such cases are particularly problematic when a highly abundant ion, such as 40Ar+,
interferes with nearby lower-mass ions like 39K+ [87]. Although their m/z values di!er, the intensity of the
Ar peak can disturb or distort the K signal. Together, these interferences represent a signiﬁcant challenge
in ICP-MS, especially for trace element analysis and must be addressed through instrument conﬁguration,
cell gas reactions or alternative isotope selection.

2.2.3
Calibration, Internal Standards and Matrix E!ects
Before ICP-MS analysis, samples must be diluted to avoid overloading the instrument by a too concen-
trated sample and to reduce matrix e!ects [90]. The dilution depends on expected analyte concentrations
and should be adjusted to match the calibration range. Quantiﬁcation is based on external calibration
using standard solutions of known concentration. A calibration curve is constructed by plotting signal
intensity in cps against concentration [13]. Blanks and standard checks are included to monitor accuracy
and contamination. Matrix e!ects can alter signal intensity due to ionization suppression or enhancement.
Dilution helps minimize these e!ects and ensures consistency between samples and standards [90]. Inter-
nal standards (ISTDs) correct for signal drift and residual matrix e!ects. While an ideal ISTDs has similar
mass and ionization properties to those of the analyte, this is less critical with modern instruments [91].
In this study, rhodium (Rh) was used for all analytes due to its stable response. ISTDs should be elements
with only one stable isotope to avoid confusion in signal interpretation. Analyte signals are normalized
to the ISTD response to improve precision and accuracy [13].
2.3
ICP-OES Instrumental Conﬁguration
For ICP-OES instrumentation, the sample introduction and the plasma source is the same (still dependent
on type of instrument manufacturer) as ICP-MS. The details are described in Section 2.2.1. The key
di!erence lies in the detection method. After the sample is nebulized and introduced into the plasma, it
undergoes desolvation, vaporization and atomization. In the high-temperature plasma, atoms and ions are
also thermally excited [13]. Excited species quickly relax back to lower energy states, emitting photons
at element-speciﬁc wavelengths. This emission, known as optical emission, is the fundamental basis for
detection in ICP-OES (Figure 8) [92].
The emitted light is collected by viewing the plasma either axially (through the central channel of the
plasma) or radially (perpendicular to the plasma) [13]. Optical components such as lenses or mirrors
focus the light into the entrance slit of a spectrometer, where it is dispersed by a di!raction grating into
its component wavelengths [93]. The detectable wavelength range typically spans from about 130 to 800
nm, covering both UV and visible regions.

In modern ICP-OES systems, spectrometers coupled with solid-state array detectors are used [92]. The
two detectors allow for simultaneous multi-element detection across a broad spectral range [13]. Older
systems uses photomultiplier tubes (PMTs) and scans individual wavelengths sequentially [13]. Simul-
taneous detection makes it possible to analyze multiple elements in a single short run, which increase
throughput and analytical e"ciency. The intensity of each emission line is proportional to the concentra-
tion of the corresponding element in the sample [13, 92]. Intensities are converted into electrical signals.
They are then matched to speciﬁc elements based on their wavelengths and quantiﬁed using external cali-
bration curves [93]. ICP-OES typically provides a wide linear dynamic range, often spanning 4 to 6 orders
of magnitude. ICP-OES is well-suited for aqueous samples with elemental concentrations in the low ppb
to parts per million (ppm) range, making it e!ective for trace analysis in environmental, pharmaceutical
and food matrices [92]. ICP-OES is not as sensitive as ICP-MS, however it has greater tolerance to matrix
e!ects, such as high TDS or organics. It is also less prone to memory e!ects because it measures photons
rather than ions, which makes maintenance simpler and reduces the risk of carryover between samples
[13].
In summary, ICP-OES and ICP-MS share the same plasma source and sample introduction system, but
di!er in detection. ICP-OES uses optical emission from excited atoms and ions, while ICP-MS detects
ions separated by their m/z ratio in a vacuum system. ICP-OES o!ers a robust, cost-e!ective solution
for routine, multi-element analysis with high throughput and su"cient sensitivity for most regulatory
applications. ICP-MS, on the other hand, provides signiﬁcantly higher sensitivity and lower detection
limits, making it more suitable for ultra-trace analysis [13].

amount of this usage, they can pose a signiﬁcant risk of contamination with toxic trace elements. Studies
have shown that these product often contain higher concentrations of toxic trace elements than synthetic
supplements [8, 66, 95]. Herbal supplements are derived from plant materials that may absorb metals
from soil, water and air during cultivation and the lack of regulation increases the likelihood of these
contaminations not being discovered. Despite their popularity and potential health risks, herbal food
supplements sold in Denmark have not been thoroughly studied for toxic metal content.
3.1
Aim of the Study
The aim of this thesis is to investigate the presence and concentration of selected toxic elements, Al,
Ni, As, Cd and Pb, in a range of herbal food supplements available on the Danish market. The study
is designed around primarily ICP-MS, supported by ICP-OES for higher-concentration macro-elements.
Although elements such as Mo, Co, Cu, Zn, Se, Fe, P, S, K, Ca, Na and Mg were also measured, they were
used primarily to give a general view of the elemental proﬁle of the supplements. The study followed the
following workﬂow:
Figure 9: Project workﬂow
The analytical workﬂow began with a literature review and the selection of representative herbal sup-
plements in various forms. Samples were prepared using formulation speciﬁc homogenization followed
by microwave-assisted acid digestion. Elemental analysis was performed using ICP-MS as the primary
technique and with ICP-OES for quantiﬁcation of macro elements. All experiments were subjected to
quality control to ensure the reliability and accuracy of the measurements. The measured concentrations
were compared to regulatory limits and literature values, followed by an exposure assessment based on
the product dosage recommendations.
3.2
Hypothesis
The central hypothesis of this study is: “Herbal food supplements on the Danish market contain measur-
able concentrations of toxic trace elements, with some products potentially exceeding regulatory limits or
contributing signiﬁcantly to consumer exposure.”

Sample Preperation
Sample preparation was carried out according to a workﬂow speciﬁc to each supplement type (Figure 10).
The process included pretreatment, homogenization (where needed), microwave digestion with HNO3,
centrifugation and dilution prior to analysis by ICP-MS or ICP-OES. Triplicates were made for all samples
to evaluate the repeatability.
Figure 10: Flowchart of sample preparation methods used, depending on the di!erent formulation type
of the supplements.
4.4.1
Homogenization Process
For each product, 50 units (tablets or capsules) were processed to minimize unit-to-unit variability in
trace element concentration. Tablets were homogenized using a co!ee grinder in three 15-second bursts.
Capsules were emptied manually and the powder homogenized in a clean container. Powders and liquids
needed no further homogenization. Gummies were homogenized using a freeze and blend method where
the samples were frozen to -80°C, blended for 2-3 seconds and then refrozen. This approach was repeated
3 times to ensure total homogenization. Freeze-drying was tested on 20 gummies but was not pursued
further, as no improved e"ciency was observed. Tinctures were not homogenized but gently shaken be-
fore processing. Due to their high ethanol content (up to 65%), ethanol was evaporated prior to digestion
using a nitrogen evaporator at 40°C in quartz digestion tubes.

4.4.2
Digestion Procedure
Microwave digestion was performed using an Anton Paar closed-vessel microwave system with quartz
tubes. Each run included 2–3 CRMs and at one BLK. Microwave digestion was conducted using the fol-
lowing settings; Ramp time: 20 minutes, Hold time: 10 minutes, Max temperature: 250°C, Max pressure:
140 bar. For solid samples (capsules, tablets, powders and homogenized gummies) 0.5 g of material was
weighed into quartz tubes. For liquid samples 1 g were weighted except liquid tinctures where 4.0 mL
was weighted before evaporation of ethanol before analysis. In all cases, 4 mL of 65% HNO3 was added
for digestion. The digestion vessels were loaded into a rotor with capacity of 18 positions.
After digestion, samples were transferred to pre-weighed 50 mL polypropylene tubes and diluted to ap-
proximately 20 mL with Milli-Q water. Final mass and density were recorded to enable calculation of
concentrations in the original mass. All samples were centrifuged at 14,000 rpm for 10 minutes to remove
undigested solids and prevent instrument blockage. Most samples were further diluted 25x to avoid severe
matrix a!ect and nebulizer blockage. Tinctures and CRMs were diluted 50x and 100x respectively to re-
duce matrix e!ects. This last dilution were adjusted to get a ﬁnal volume of 10 mL for each samples/CRM
and BLK.

Comparison Between ADD and HBGVs
HBGV describes with how much of an element can be ingested daily or weekly though a lifetime without
having severe e!ects. These values are often expressed by µg/kg bw/(day or week). To asses the risk
of the intake of a toxic trace element, concentrations obtained from all supplements were translated into
average daily doses (ADD) per body weight (bw), expressed by (µg/kg bw/day) and then compared with
the HBGV values by dividing the ADD by the weight of either a 30kg child or a 60 kg woman. The ADDs
was calculated using formula 6.
Average Daily Dose (µg/kg bw/day) = Dose weight (g) ↔Daily dose ↔Concentration (µg/g)

(6)
Where Dose weight is the weight of one unit (for example one pill or one gummy), Daily dose is the number
of units recommended per day and Concentration (µg/g) is the concentration of the given element in the
supplement. The concentration can be expressed in either mg/kg or (µg/g), but has been converted to
(µg/g) to match the units required for ADD calculations. The ADD was hereafter used to compare with
the HBGV values. Daily doses were taken from the information provided on the supplement labels and
the dose weight were measured in the laboratory.

Box plots (also known as box-and-whisker plots) were used to visualize the distribution of the data and
identify potential outliers. This method provides a standardized summary of the data based on ﬁve key
statistics: the minimum, ﬁrst quartile (Q1), median (Q2), third quartile (Q3) and maximum.
The central box in a box plot represents the interquartile range (IQR), which spans from Q1 to Q3 and
contains the middle 50% of the data. A line within the box marks the median. The "whiskers" extend
from the box to the smallest and largest data points that fall within 1.5 * IQR from Q1 and Q3. Data
points lying beyond these limits are considered outliers and are plotted individually. Outliers are deﬁned
as values smaller than (Equation 7) and larger than (Equation 8) [97].

overall experimental workﬂow. It begins with the sample preparation procedures, homogenization and
digestion, applied to various sample types, including gummies, liquids, tinctures, capsules, tablets and
powders.
Later sections describe experiments performed on the ICP-MS and in part also ICP-OES, focusing on
ﬁve toxic, non-essential elements. These sections include QC of measurements, the use of CRMs, ISTD
selection, mode optimization and dilution strategies. The study focuses primarily on ICP-MS, but ICP-
OES is also used for complementary analysis of some marco-elements. The workﬂow of the Thesis can
be seen in Figure 12.

5.1.1
Tablets, Capsules and Powders
Tablets
The homogenization of tablets was initially preformed using a standard laboratory blender. All
tablets appeared to be uniform in shape and weight, with no visible di!erences between individual units.
During blending, the tablet coating were observed to separate from the rest of the tablet material and
remain unhomogenized. This issue was likely due to the excessive air volume in the blender. Figure
14 illustrates the separated tablet coatings observed after blending. Extended blending periods did not
resolve the issue as the light-weight coatings continued to ﬂoat around in the blender. Switching to a
co!ee grinder resulted in a signiﬁcantly more uniform powder with the tablet coatings e!ectively being
homogenized.
Following this, the co!ee grinder was chosen as the primary homogenization tool for further sample prepa-
ration of tablets. The co!ee grinder was better suited for the tablet size compared to the blender. Co!ee

Capsules
The homogenization of capsules needed a di!erent approach, with a focus on the powder con-
tent. Although capsule shells are ingested and therefore relevant from a consumer exposure perspective,
the analytical focus of this thesis was on the herbal supplements themselves and the trace metal content
associated with the herbal material. Apart from a few bent capsules, no signiﬁcant variation between
individual capsules was observed. Homogenization by separating the capsules and emptying the content
was chosen to produce a more uniform powder. Some powder stuck to the inside of the capsule shells,
causing sample loss and introducing some uncertainty in the estimated dose. Since neither the weight
of the capsule shells nor the powder was provided on the packaging, the average dose was estimated by
dividing the total powder mass by the number of emptied capsules, introducing additional uncertainty. It
took a considerable amount of time to separate 50 capsules for each of the 12 capsule samples (a total of
524 capsules). The homogenization of the entire capsule appeared ﬁne, but visual inspection still revealed
a distinction between the capsule shell and the internal powder. One batch, homogenized with the shells,
was analyzed for comparison. In retrospect, blending the entire capsules would have been a more prac-
tical choice but also a more appropriate approach from a regulatory standpoint. Under European Union
Directive 2002/46/EC, the entire supplement, including the capsule shell, is considered the ﬁnal product
subject to safety and contaminant limits [99]. This means that the capsule shell should have been included
in the assessment of total trace metal exposure, as it is a part of what the consumer ingests. By focusing
on the powder content, the potential contribution of metals from the capsule shells was unintentionally
excluded.

5.1.2
Gummies
Gummies were initially frozen at -80°C and then blended brieﬂy. Freezing was necessary because the
gummies became sticky during blending. After just 5 seconds in the blender after freezing, the gummies
started to stick together again, which prevented further homogenization and made the cleaning of the
blender di"cult. To solve this, the gummies were blended for only 2–3 seconds at a time, then scooped up
and refrozen. These freeze-blend cycles was repeated three times to ensure proper homogenization. This
process was time-consuming and a repeating freezing and handling of the gummies could also increase
the risk of contamination.

5.1.3
Liquid Samples and Tinctures
Liquid samples, generally require no homogenization due to their homogeneity. Shaking was su"cient to
ensure uniformity before sub-sampling. Initially, a direct injection method was preformed for the liquid
tincture samples (LT01-LT04) where they were diluted with 2% HNO3. This method is the simplest
approach with minimal steps involved, minimizing the risk of contamination. The same TDF was tried
for the tinctures as for the solid samples. The tincture with the highest EtOH% content was LT01 at 65%,
while the others ranged between 46-49%. A 1000x dilution was chosen to match the TDF of the other
samples.
Further inspection revealed that when the tinctures were diluted 20x in 2% HNO3 visible sediment formed
over time, as shown in Figure 18. It indicated that some of the herbal components and possibly the target
elements were no longer in solution. Since ICP-MS can only measure elements in solution, proceeding
with direct injection under these conditions would risk getting inaccurate or misleading results. The
herbal components were soluble in ethanol but not in water, which explains why sedimentation occurred
after dilution in 2% HNO3 in MilliQ. Based on this, direct injection was abandoned in favor of ethanol
evaporation.

unsuccessful, highlighting why ethanol was originally chosen as the solvent.

To overcome the sedimentation issue, ethanol evaporation was performed directly in the quartz tube used
for microwave digestion, using a nitrogen evaporator consisting of a heat block and a direct ﬂow of nitrogen
to each tube, shown in Figure 20A+B. The nitrogen stream continuously removed evaporated ethanol from
the liquid surface, preventing vapor saturation, which would accelerate the evaporation process. Because
nitrogen is inert, it minimizes oxidation risk and keeps ambient contaminants like oxygen or moisture
from entering the sample.
The nitrogen evaporator setup had 12 positions, allowing all four tincture samples to be processed in
triplicates simultaneously. Initially, evaporation was carried out at 80°C, slightly above ethanol’s boiling
point, to ensure complete ethanol removal [102]. In the ﬁrst evaporation, no CRM or procedural blank
was included. For the second evaporation batch, the procedure was repeated at 40°C to minimize possible
losses of volatile analytes. In this second trial, both a procedural blank and a CRM (wine) were included to
monitor recovery and accuracy. Figure 20C illustrates the ﬁnal volume and appearance post-evaporation.
After evaporation and microwave digestion, each sample was diluted to 20 mL prior to ICP-MS analysis.
The initial applied DF was 25x, corresponding to a TDF of 125. However, this initial dilution was not
su"cient and resulted in low ISTD recoveries across several samples, likely due to matrix e!ects. In
some cases, such as for samples T02 and T03, ISTD recoveries were as low as 7–24%, indicating signif-
icant suppression. To improve the stability fo the ISTD, the DF was increased to 100x, giving a TDF of
500, which better matched the dilution levels used for the solid samples and provided more stable ISTD
performance.

To prevent solid residues from entering and potentially damaging the instrument, a clean-up step was
performed. Centrifugation was used to separate the sediment, and only the supernatant was used for
further dilution. This procedure was applied to all samples, regardless of whether visible sediment was
present or not, to ensure consistency and simplify the workﬂow.
Using HNO3 alone keeps the digestion simple, making it ideal for oxidative breakdown of most organic
matrices in ICP-MS analysis. It is cost-e!ective, pure and widely available. Figure 21 shows that the
digestion colors varied a lot from the digested samples, although most of the samples were like Figure
21(C). Figure 21(A+B) shows the digestion colors of two of the tinctures. This light yellow digestion
color suggests ﬁne digestion.
Figure 21: Di!erent digestion colors of di!erent supplements.
In this study, HNO3 digestion alone was applied and proved su"cient, as analyte recoveries for elements
of interest for NIST–3294 and VITA-1 were within acceptable limits. Although NIST–3294 and VITA-
1 does not include Al as a certiﬁed value, recoveries from NIST-1572 containing Al were satisfactory.
Additives such as SiO2, commonly present in tablet formulations, left undigested sediment, as observed in
several samples. While it is suggested that HF could potentially improve decomposition of silicate-bound
material, it was not applied here due to safety concerns and the absence of clear evidence for analyte losses
associated with silicates. Holmfred et al. [103] reported that including HF in their digestion of ibuprofen
tablets led to the formation of insoluble ﬂuorides, which complicated the analysis and was therefore not
included.
The use of HCl can enhance digestion completeness by dissolving minerals and stabilizing elements like
Ag, Sb, Pb and Hg via chloride complexation [104]. It would introduce polyatomic interferences such as
40Ar35Cl+ at m/z 75, if run without a TQ. In this thesis, the ICP-MS was operated in O2 mode for As,
e!ectively eliminating ArCl+ interference. Therefore, HCl could have been used to support digestion or
enhance element stabilization, but was deemed unnecessary given the satisfactory recoveries.
No optimization of microwave digestion conditions or acid mixtures was performed, although alternative
protocols were available in the laboratory. Filipiak-Szok et al. [9] demonstrated that HNO3-only digestion
provided reliable results for Al, Ni, As, Cd and Pb in herbal supplements, with no recovery issues. Leblond
et al.[105] also used HNO3 alone for the digestion of food supplements for analysis of Ni, Cd and Pb and
reported satisfactory recoveries and digestion e"ciency. This supports the use of only using HNO3 as
digestion acid for herbal supplements matrices.

5.3.4
CRM Comparison
CRMs are essential for validating trace element analysis, as they provide certiﬁed concentrations for com-
parison and help ensure trueness. In herbal dietary supplement analysis, perfectly matrix-matched CRMs
are rarely available due to the diversity and complexity of plant-based formulations. In this study, CRMs
were selected based on the nature of the samples and their matrices. Initially, the focus was on general
dietary supplements, so NIST SRM 3294 (Multielement Tablets) and NRC VITA-1 (Multivitamin) were
chosen. These CRMs were useful in the sense that they were also tablets and contained elements such as
silicon, which was relevant because many of the analyzed products included silicon dioxide as an anti-
caking agent (see Table 11). As the study progressed and the focus shifted to herbal products, it became
clear that these CRMs were not ideal. Both NIST 3294 and VITA-1 had much higher concentrations of
Page 44 of 68
==PAGE:51==

Saturday 2nd August, 2025
elements and a matrix that did not resemble the plant-based content of herbal supplements. As a result,
they showed more ISTD instability and less reliable recoveries for low-concentration elements.
To better match the matrix and concentration levels of the herbal samples, NIST SRM 1572 (Citrus Leaves)
was added later. The plant-based matrix and lower concentrations were a better ﬁt and provided more
stable ISTD signals at lower dilution. For liquid tinctures, a wine-based CRM was included to verify
whether the selected elements remained in solution during the evaporation process prior to digestion.
While the Wine CRM was limited to just four certiﬁed elements and with only two relevant to this study,
it still provided useful information on element recovery in the liquid matrix.

5.3.6
HBGVs vs ADDs
To accurately assess trace element concentration in the supplements, TDIs, TWIs and BMDLs, were
identiﬁed. The HBGVs used in this study for As are based on iAs, which is the most toxic form and it
should be noted that the amount of iAs in the food supplements samples has not been determined. Without
a speciation analysis, it is not possible to determine which forms are actually present in the samples.
Hedegaard et al. [110] showed that for herbal ingredients, inorganic As typically made up two-thirds or
more of the total As. In food supplements where algae were listed as the main ingredient, the proportion
of inorganic As was much lower, ranging from 5% to 34% of the total As content [110]. This should be
considered when evaluating the potential health risks.
Al and Cd have a TWI value which can be converted to TDI by dividing the weekly values by seven.
Ni, iAs and Pb have a BMDL. Ni had an established BMDL10, which was used directly. For iAs the
lowest BMDL01 value from the identiﬁed range (0.3–8 µg/kg bw/day) was selected to provide the most
conservative assessment. For Pb, the lowest BMDL01 value from EFSA was used directly. Using the
same units for all values makes them easier to compare.

than one supplement at the same time. Exposures from multiple products may accumulate, especially if
several of them contain Al or other trace elements. Supplements are of course not the only source of
Al exposure. Al intake from food, water and other contributes to the total exposure [112]. When these
sources are combined, the total exposure might exceed the HBGVs value even further, especially for
sensitive groups like children.
For the remaining samples, exposure levels were generally low compared to the HBGVs. Even in samples
with relatively higher concentrations of the individual elements (such as C04, C06 and T07), the ADD
remained well within safety margins for both adults and children.

positioned analyte signals within the calibration standards’ linear range.

5.4.4
Evaluation of ICP-OES Method Suitability
ICP-OES was included in this study to provide as an alternative method for determination of the general
content of elements in herbal food supplements. While it was not intended as the main focus of this thesis,
analyzing the general composition of the herbal supplements provided valuable insight and demonstrated
an alternative method for elemental analysis. Compared to ICP-MS, ICP-OES o!ers a simpler setup, less
demanding calibration and gives reliable results for elements present at higher concentrations. It is well
suited for quantifying major elements such as Ca, K, Mg and P, making it a convenient and cost-e!ective
option for screening these macro nutrients. Although ICP-OES has higher tolerance to complex matrices
and better robustness, it has lower sensitivity than ICP-MS, this could limit its ability to accurately quantify
elements such as Cd and Pb at low levels, which are typically found i herbal food supplements.

Conclusion
This thesis investigated selected toxic trace elements (Al, Ni, As, Cd and Pb) in 27 herbal food supplements
available on the Danish market, using a validated ICP-MS method supported by ICP-OES for selected
macro-elements. Consumer exposure was assessed via estimated daily intakes and compared them to
EFSA’s health-based guidance values (HBGVs) for both adults and children. Homogenization approach
was adjusted to di!erent formulation types, including powders, capsules, tinctures and gummies. Special
attention was given to tinctures, which required ethanol evaporation before digestion. Reaction/collision
modes minimized interferences and enabled broad quantiﬁcation.
Toxic element concentrations varied substantially between products, likely due to di!erences in raw mate-
rials or manufacturing. While none of the supplements exceeded current EU regulatory limits for cadmium
and lead, some products approached levels of concern when considered in the context of daily exposure.
Al levels were particularly variable, ranging from 1.4 to 1433 mg/kg, with outliers like P02 and C04 dis-
playing extreme concentrations. The average and median values aligned closely with previously reported
data, especially from other studies on plant based products. Sample P02 is a Brahmi-based powder that
exhibited the highest concentrations of Al and among the highest for Pb, As and Cd. Exposure estimates
for this supplement showed that for a 30 kg child, Al intake from P02 alone would constitute approximately
80% of the TWI and even for an adult woman, it would make up 40%. Exposure of Al from food, waterand
other sources are already elevated in Denmark, which raises concern. These ﬁndings conﬁrm the hypoth-
esis that a big part of herbal supplements contain measurable concentrations of toxic trace elements, but
with few contributing to potential consumer exposure.
Despite high concentrations in a subset of samples, most products showed relatively low levels of toxic
trace elements, with calculated ADDs remaining well below the HBGVs. However, certain outliers can
contribute signiﬁcantly to a total daily exposure highlights the need for stricter quality control. Unlike
food, repeated use of the same supplement increases chronic exposure risk. ICP-OES was used to quan-
tify macro-elements such as Ca, Mg, K, Na, P and S. High concentrations of some elements, linked to
excipients like dicalcium phosphate, contributed up to 40% of the RI for certain nutrients. Although ICP-
OES lacks the sensitivity for small concentrations of toxic trace elements, it was well suited for identifying
essential macro-elements and revealed how additives can strongly inﬂuence the elemental content of food
supplements.
In conclusion, although most of the supplements tested were within established safety margins and regula-
tory limits, variability in concentrations presented a problem. The high-level outliers showed a concerning
gap in quality control. The results supported the need for improved regulatory oversight of food supple-
ments, especially those derived from herbal sources. This thesis adds to the growing understanding that
“natural” is not synonymous with “harmless” and suggests that a routine elemental screening could play
a larger role in general product assessment.
