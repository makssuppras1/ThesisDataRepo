Abstract
Two listening experiments are conducted to investigate which perceptual differences oc-
cur in auralizations from measurements compared to three different acoustic simulation
softwares: Odeon, Treble, and Raven. Both tests are based on auralizations from three
different acoustic spaces, incorporating six systems from the simulation algorithms and
one measurement serving as reference.
One test is evaluating authenticity and plausibility of the simulations, which was tested on
21 untrained listeners. The results demonstrate that no simulations were indistinguish-
able from the reference. In the plausibility study, the task for the test subjects is to assess
the naturalness of the systems on a continuous scale. The results depend on the space,
and the simulations appear most unnatural in the large geometry.
The second test is conducted by 10 trained assessors, who evaluate the sound based
on six attributes: Treble Strength, Bass Strength, Clarity, Level of Reverberation, Local-
izability and Width. Most systems introduce obvious perceptual differences in multiple
attributes, and a general pattern shows that increasing geometry volume will increase in-
accuracy of the perceptual Level of Reverberation.
The change in spatial attributes is related to the simulation input parameters and algo-
rithm method, whereas attributes describing timbral balance/tone colour will be influenced
greatly by changing algorithm/software.

1
Introduction
Today’s use of computer simulation within several industries has become crucial in making
accurate estimations before construction. Within the acoustic domain, simulations aim to
recreate information about the sound characteristics of a space. The prediction of sound
fields stretches from tiny components used in e.g. hearing aids to large-scale models of
concert halls and even on a city scale. The simulation of sound wave propagation has a
significant impact on the architectural design process, as it makes the acoustic qualities
of a space audible before construction [1]. Moreover, acoustic simulation is increasingly
valuable in virtual reconstructions of historic architectural buildings [1][2], as well as audio
sensual gaming experiences and VR [3], indicating that the field of application is expand-
ing beyond its traditional domain. Simulating sound for immersive experiences makes it
relevant to examine the perceptual qualities of the different simulation methods and con-
sider the accuracy of sound reproduction.
The perceptual simulated sound is known as auralization, which can be described as the
hearing equivalent to visualization, and is a tool for demonstrating and experiencing au-
ditory cues in the listening environment [4].
For smart solutions with multi-functionality and integrated design, several tools are locally
embedded as plug-ins in favoured architectural- and design softwares, such as daylight
and thermal simulation. The possibility of working within the same platforms across disci-
plines enables streamlined communication. Still, acoustics is often an unknown territory
for architects, designers and engineers, and basic acoustic terms are often not embedded
in their respective professional vocabulary.
Auralization is a method for making the sound field audible and can be interpreted without
prior knowledge within acoustics or sound [4]. Auralization thereby enhances collabora-
tion and communication of acoustic concepts through shared auditory experiences [5].
Geometric Acoustics (GA) and Numerical Methods (NM) are the two primary methods
used in acoustic simulation. The choice of one over the other will essentially be a trade-
off between accuracy and computational efficiency. The development of hybrid acoustic
simulation, and increasing viability of NM with compelling results, shows the potential for
physically accurate simulations which are computationally less demanding [6].
1.1
Perceptual Studies
Earlier studies have assessed the perceptual accuracy and reliability of different simula-
tion methods. In a study by Aretz and Jauer [7], a perceptual analysis was conducted to
propose terminology and suitable listening tests for comparing measurements and sim-
ulations. A comparison was made between a GA model and a hybrid model using NM
in the low-frequency range and GA in the remaining frequency spectrum. The authors
claim, however, that a more refined test is required due to scattered results.
A more recent study by Brinkmann et al. [3] demonstrates differences in five various sim-
ulation algorithms based on GA, even though the inclusion of NM was originally planned
for the study. Based on the results from twenty-nine untrained listeners, the study shows
that neither software was able to create genuine auralizations when compared to a mea-
sured reference.
Comparing software is, however, highly dependent on the experience and expertise of the

user [8]. Inaccuracy in the simulations is usually caused by uncertainties regarding the
boundary conditions such as absorption and scattering [9], which is often obtained from
textbooks or databases [8]. Input parameters can be fitted to match objective acous-

This renders acoustic simulation unreliable for individuals lacking the necessary acoustic

The objective of this study is to assess the perceptual differences between measure-

GA and a hybrid of NM and GA. A perceptual test in similarity will be conducted to de-
termine whether the incorporation of NM contributes to more authentic outcomes. This
will be tested in three different geometries, two of which are from a database established
for the purpose of testing auralizations from room acoustical simulation, which includes
information about input parameters such as absorption and scattering.
A research question has been formulated to provide insights into this matter.
1. Are there significant differences in the auralizations produced by the software appli-
cations and measurements, and if so, what are the key distinctions?
1.2
Attribute Study
A different study by Olive [10][11] compares 13 loudspeaker models to uncover possible
correlation between objective measurements, listener preference, and underlying sound
attributes, tested on seven trained listeners.
For describing and evaluating sound, trained listeners are more reliable in their discrimi-
nation and judgement of sound attributes [12]. Therefore, as a secondary part, this study
will access a listener panel trained in evaluating and describing sound, and thereby give
objective measurements within the sensory modalities of hearing. The expert listeners
will get the task of evaluating audible differences between measurements and simula-
tions within six selected attributes.
Combining results from a similarity test and an attribute test will explore potential correla-
tions between attributes, and each attribute’s impact on the perception of similarity.
The second study seeks to answer the research question:
2. Which specific attributes contribute most to the perceptual similarity between mea-
surements and systems?

2.1
Room Acoustic Simulation
Room acoustic simulation is a tool for understanding and designing acoustic environ-
ments. Using different models and algorithms, it predicts how sound interacts with sur-
faces and objects. The simulations provide insights into objective acoustic parameters
and generate auralizations, aiding engineers and researchers in optimizing acoustic de-
sign.
For the simulation of sound propagation in a room, two main models are popularly used:
GA and NM.
2.2
Geometric Acoustics
Today, GA is the industry standard algorithm for room acoustic design and is popularly
used for consultancy [8]. GA is an approximate method that neglects the wave-phenomena
using straight rays. The sound field is estimated by the sound travelling in space until
reaching a boundary, where sound reflection is predicted statistically.

In commercial software GA simulation is based upon two common algorithms: image

Classic models based on GA neglect diffraction, which is typically caused by obstacles in

and advantage of GA models [4].
2.2.1
Image Source Method (ISM)
The ISM in GA is a deterministic model useful for predicting reverberation patterns and
understanding the distribution of sound energy in a space. Most acoustic simulation soft-
ware uses ISs for the early part of the decay due to its precision. ISs are two-fold and
can be energy-based and pressure-based [9]. The energy-based method neglects the
wave nature of sound propagation and phase information. This is where pressure-based
models with complex-valued and angle-dependent boundary conditions can improve ac-
curacy [16]. The ISM is an approximate image expansion which works by mirroring the
observed geometry with rooms surrounding the actual room. Assuming specular reflec-
tion patterns, when a sound wave reaches a reflective surface the original source in the
room is mirrored, creating an imaginary IS on the opposite side of the surface. Each IS is
then mirrored further to create a higher order of ISs. This process is repeated for multiple
reflections to simulate the interplay of sound waves in the space [17].
The contribution of each IS is a signal identical to the source signal with a time delay τ
and energy attenuation expressed with an amplitude factor A. Each reflection is added to
the Impulse Response (IR), and the Room Impulse Response (RIR) function, using the
original approach, can be defined by [18]

2.2.2
Ray Tracing
While ISs are useful in the early decay, the ray tracing algorithm is useful for the late
decay due to its computational efficiency. Ray tracing is a stochastic statistical model
where sound is radiated as a number of particles (or rays) [4]. A source is radiating
particles in various directions propagating at the speed of sound, until they reach room
surfaces, where they are being reflected, absorbed or diffracted. The rays continue the
propagation and reflection while reducing in energy in contact with surface absorption.
A detector registers the particle energy and time elapsed since it was emitted from the
source, and the model calculates energy distribution in the different locations of the space.
The method is valuable for predicting sound wave behaviour in complex environments at
high efficiency but is valid only at frequencies above the Schroeder frequency to make
the ray approximation acceptable [4].
Ray tracing is based on statistical probability and, in contrast to ISs, lacks the ability to
identify all specular paths. This is why incorporating a hybrid model, where ISs compute
the early rays and ray tracing models predict sound behaviour in the late rays, is advan-
tageous.

2.3
Numerical Methods
In spaces such as small studio rooms, living rooms, cars etc. the Schroeder frequency is
higher, and thus the low-frequency part below the Schroeder frequency is more significant,
which is inaccurate with energy-based GA [16]. This is where wave-based numerical
methods should be used [8]. NM can give more reliable results, by accurately including
acoustic phenomena such as interference, diffraction and scattering (as demonstrated
by Lokki et al. [20]), as well as natural frequencies [21]. NM consists in solving the
wave equation directly, which can be expressed as the linear first-order partial differential

2.4
Softwares
In this section, the three selected softwares, Odeon, Raven and Treble, are introduced
with their respective parameter settings for this study. Additions and changes in geometry
will be described.
2.4.1
Odeon
A popular commercial software based on GA methods using a combination of image
sources and ray tracing is the Odeon room acoustic software created by Odeon A/S.
In this study the installation Odeon Combined (version 17.15) is employed.
Framework Behind Odeon
For calculating the early reflections in Odeon three different simulation methods are used
in combination for optimal performance and accuracy: The ISM, Early Scattering Method
(ESM) and ray tracing. To calculate the late reflections part, a modified version of the ray
tracing method, called the Ray-Radiosity Method (RRM) [24], is used.
A transition order is determining where the early-reflections part ends and the late-reflections
part begins, as illustrated in Figure 2.4. Since the reflections occur at different times, the
methods will overlap in time because the transition order is not strictly linked to time, which
will create a smooth transition between the methods.
Figure 2.4: Illustration of early- and late-reflections based on the transition order. The
illustration is from the Odeon Manual [19].
The default value is set to a transition order of 2, meaning that the reflections are calcu-
lated according to the early-reflection part, whereas if the transition order exceeds 2, the
reflections are calculated according to both the early- and late-reflections part [19].
The two methods ISM and ESM provide high deterministic accuracy in the early-reflection
part, which is essentially the most important part of the IR. The early-reflection part plays
a large role in supporting speech intelligibility [25], enhancing the clarity of music, and
influencing the perception of source properties such as width, distance [5], and loudness
[4]. On the other hand, the late-reflection part impacts spatial cues like orientation [26]
and envelopment [5].
For optimization and efficiency, Odeon uses ray tracing to detect which ISs are visible

the actual amount of valid ISs.
ESM will not be explained in detail here, and an elaborate description can be found in

The RRM follows the classical principle of ray tracing where a source emits rays, which
are reflected and traced by the algorithm. Rays intersecting a detection sphere around
the receiver are added to the IR. The ray tracing method requires the simulation to run
for each new receiver, therefore Odeon has implemented an initial rendering part which
is receiver-independent and stores geometrical information, which increases simulation
efficiency.

sd [19]:

Diffraction is known to be hard for GA solvers to calculate and are not handled by classical
GA methods [6], however, Odeon implements a method for detecting one- or two-point
diffraction paths, when no direct sound is received from a source to a receiver [3][19]. The
implementation follows A. Pierce [27], which is known to be reasonably close to experi-
mental measurements in the perceptual frequency range and gives accurate estimations
of both the amplitude and phase of the diffracted waves [28]. The procedure includes the
detection of diffracting edges, which are then considered as edge image sources [29].
Brinkmann et al. [3], however, clearly pointed out the lack of diffraction modelling in all
GA methods [3].
Other than diffraction, Odeon’s algorithm is also one of the few GA algorithms that con-
sider energy loss of specular reflections that are caused by diffraction around objects
using scatter coefficient adjustments. Effects from diffraction caused by objects such as
stages, stage reflectors, and balcony edges can have a major impact on the sound in the
geometry [6].
Binauralization in Odeon
When binaural auralizations are calculated in Odeon with a Head And Torso Simulator
(HATS) as the receiver the corresponding Head Related Transfer Function (HRTF) is in-
serted into the binaural setup. The headphone filter should match the headphones used
for listening to the Binaural Room Impulse Responses (BRIRs) if available, and if no filter
matches, a diffuse inverse filter matched to the HRTF should be selected (cf. Odeon’s
User Manual page 90 [19]). However, the headphone compensation filter is added after
convolution within Odeon, and in this case, the raw RIRs are convolved in a separate
Matlab script. Therefore, the headphone filter should be left blank (the area will turn red),
similar to the process of calculating Inter-Aural Cross Correlation (IACC) using an HRTF.
The ambisonics order in Odeon and Raven does not have a perceptual influence when
simulating binauralizations, because for Nth order ambisonics, the amount of microphones
required is at least (N + 1)2. The default ambisonics order is selected for the GA simula-
tions.

Raven (Room Acoustics for Virtual ENvironments) is an open-source application soft-
ware developed at the Institute of Technical Acoustics, RWTH Aachen University [30].

Today, the software is a vital part of 3D sound-rendering implemented in the immersive
VR-system at RWTH Aachen University [31].
Framework Behind Raven
As with the Odeon software, Raven is a hybrid combining the deterministic ISM and an
algorithm for stochastic ray tracing and is also one of the GA methods including sound
transmission, scattering and diffraction [30]. Raven adapts, and partly expands, methods
accounting for diffraction for both ISM and ray tracing. Like Odeon, Raven includes the
energy that is bent into shadow zones caused by objects by introducing a type of sec-
ondary source called Diffraction Source (DS) which is located on top of each edge and is
mirrored on the room surfaces similarly to ISs, which creates Diffraction Image Sources
(DISs), as shown in Figure 2.5.
Figure 2.5: Diagram of the source (S) and receiver (R), and the visual representation of
ISs and DSs denoted with I and D. Some sound paths are left out for simplicity. The figure
is from [30].
For the ray tracing method, edge diffraction is included by introducing yet a new detec-
tor type called Deflection Cylinders (DCs). The cylindrical detectors have the same axis
as the respective edge, which will create a function for the edge influence proposed by
Stephenson [32], when hit by an energy particle. The idea is to deflect energy such that
the closer the particle passes an edge the more it gets diffracted into the shadow zone of
the wedge [30]. Stephenson shares that the model has frequency limits, because of the
far-near problem where objects that are considered ”far” away, with no influence of diffrac-
tion, become ”near” at low frequencies. The consequence of this is that diffracting objects
can be grouped and considered simultaneously, which will influence not only the energies
but also ray paths [32]. Nevertheless, this uncertainty-based diffraction model shows bet-
ter performance than alternative methods. Raven’s expanded version of Stephenson’s
uncertainty-based diffraction model provides accurate results and might be preferred for
complex geometries where the frequency range of interest is not too low [33].
A more comprehensive description of the software framework is introduced in [31].
2.4.3
Treble
The newest software, Treble, is developed by Treble Technologies, founded in early 2020.
They offer an Acoustic Simulation Software for generating interactive, real-time auraliza-
tions in a web-based client. Furthermore, a programmatic interface Software Develop-
ment Kit (SKD) is available, which gives the user freedom to customize functionality. Both
are utilizing a cloud-based service for powerful processing and calculation. The SDK is
employed for this project, to include more opportunities for inserting amongst other source
directivity patterns.
Treble uses a hybrid room acoustic simulation that combines NM at low frequencies with
GA at high frequencies. The low-frequency wave and modal behaviour are thereby cap-
tured while a fast calculation process is employed for frequencies above the Schroeder

9 of 80
==PAGE:24==
frequency. Treble is thereby the first commercial software to provide a hybridized NM and
GA acoustic software [34].
Like the Odeon software, Treble’s GA employs a RRM for tracing the energy, which does
not require a large amount of rays [35], and employs the dG-FEM framework described
in section 2.3.
For importing geometry into the software, a Treble add-on is available for SketchUp, which
is a BIM software for creating 3D geometries. The Treble add-on checks the geometry
to identify modelling errors, such as gaps in the geometry that prevent watertightness
(which is important for running the wave-based solver), and small geometry elements
that increase the calculation grid-size and thereby increase the simulation runtime. The
reported errors include small and negligible elements in the overall geometry and should
be fixed before proceeding to the simulation.
Treble offers the opportunity to select a pure numerical, pure geometrical or hybrid method,
the latter being most popular for accommodating accuracy as well as low computational
cost. The computations depend highly on how much the NM are calculating of the fre-
quency spectrum. The transition frequency, or cut-off frequency, indicates where the NM
are ending and the GA-based calculations take over. A large complex room will take a
longer time to simulate, however, with the Schroeder frequency being inversely propor-
tional with volume, an advantage is that GA can be utilized in a wider frequency range,
hence limiting the necessary use of numerical calculations. The geometry size therefore
influences the necessary extent of numerical solvers that are necessary for including the
underlying wave-phenomena.
2.5
Boundary Conditions
Inaccurate information about boundary conditions is one of the biggest uncertainties in
acoustic simulation [9]. Even experienced acoustic consultants may encounter inaccura-
cies resulting from the limited quality of input data. Adequate data from boundary condi-
tions are simply not available or possible to measure in situ with current technology [36][9].
Two individual acoustic consultants will therefore likely have slightly different results based
on their selected boundary conditions such as absorption and scattering. Related to per-
ceptual quality the auralization is dependent on both the auralization method and how
accurate input parameters of the geometry are [1]. Uncertainties in computer simulations
are therefore also rejected by some acousticians, due to unreliability [8]:
”Computer simulations are, however, also rejected by acousticians because
they have severe doubts about their reliability.” [8]
Accurate information about the boundary conditions of a space is therefore crucial for
simulating acoustics in a room [37].

2.5.2
Scattering
The scattering coefficient defines the ratio of sound energy that is reflected in other di-
rections than the specular direction. Scattering is simulated accurately by the NM, so the
scattering coefficient is only assigned to the GA from the geometrical information, and
most relevant at high frequencies.
Scattering is frequency-dependent and has the highest impact in higher frequencies.
Odeon uses a single number corresponding to a scattering coefficient at mid-frequencies
(707 Hz), and will automatically assign appropriate values for the other frequency bands
based on typical scattering behaviour. The scattering coefficient inserted into the simu-
lations will be an average between the coefficients from 500 Hz and 1 kHz, following the
same procedure as Marbjerg et al. [16].

2.6
Source and Receiver
Key to precise and convincing simulations is the incorporation of source and receiver
characteristics into the software, ensuring an accurate representation of speaker and mi-
crophone directivities.
2.6.1
Source Directivities
Most natural sound sources typically exhibit a spatial directivity pattern when radiating
sound. The directivity impacts the perception of the sound sources in a room and the
spatial energy distribution in both early- and late-reflections [26].
The softwares provide several loudspeaker directivities to select for the simulations. For
the speaker directivity application in Odeon, the energy coming out of the loudspeaker
depends on the direction it is sent to, such that the amount of energy relates to the source
directivity data. In Treble the energy contents using the numerical solvers and GA is
different, making it less straightforward when combining directivities from both methods.
For this reason, the source directivities for one of the speakers used in this study were
not yet implemented in the Treble simulation, and an omnidirectional source was used
instead. This is described further in subsection 2.8.1.
As of December 2023, a release update for the SDK includes the ability to import CLFs
(Common Loudspeaker Format) as directive sources.
In Odeon and Raven, speaker directivities can manually be applied to the simulations in
the format of DAFF (Directional Audio File Format) for Raven, and CLF or ASCII (American
Standard Code for Information Interchange) format for Odeon (which will recognize it and
convert it to a .SO8 Odeon specific file format).
2.6.2
Receiver HRTF
For binaural auralizations, the softwares provide HRTFs for a limited assortment of HATSs.
The general principle for adding HRTFs in Odeon is to add a scaled HRTF for each ray
that reaches the receiver according to its direction of arrival. These scaled filters then
contribute to creating the IR, and the HRTF values will hence influence the intensity of the
IRs.
The binaural response rendering in Treble is based on a spatial decomposition of the
sound field introduced by Scheaffer et al. [41]. The binaural results using this method
have been compared to binaural simulation based on ISM, which indicated good agree-
ment between the two methods. In the Treble simulations, the spatial resolution is set to
an ambisonics order of 16.
The HRTF can manually be inserted in the softwares in the file formats .ascii_hrtf for
Odeon, DAFF for Raven and SOFA for Treble.

Comparing Software
To ensure a fair comparison and prevent bias, the simulations are aligned in most param-
eters. However, they otherwise follow the recommended default values in each software.
This approach ensures that the simulations are utilized as intended, with performance
expectations aligned with their respective default configurations.
If everything were to be matched to equal values, the lowest number or setting would be
the limiting factor, which could potentially result in a software performing at a lower quality
than it is capable of.
For the simulations, the setup parameters relevant to review and, if easily translatable
from software to software, adjust to match each other are IS order, number of rays and
IR length.
The IS order is set to 2nd-order across all three softwares since an increase would cause

side only for a smooth simulation process (cf. Raven SketchUp tutorial2). The influence of

In the two models, chairs and benches on the floor and balconies are not modelled geo-

benches in the Auditorium model are inserted, decreasing the area of the seating material

2.11
Impulse Response Processing
After all IRs from the measurements and simulations in the three geometries are gath-
ered, they go through collective processing using Matlab and Adobe Audition.
When IRs are loaded into Matlab the sampling frequency is matched to the one of the
sample, if necessary. The IRs are aligned to start at 0 seconds and are convolved with
the sample using the Matlab function conv. The convolved output is normalized to adjust
the output based on the highest signal level present (peak normalization) to prevent clip-
ping.
The low anchor stimuli specified in the listening test method are created from the recorded
IRs and low-pass filtered to 3.5 kHz using a 4th order Butterworth filter, used in similar
studies such as Brinkman et. al [50]. The Butterworth filter is selected to provide a flat
response in the passband [51]. The filter is applied in Matlab using the built-in function
butter, which gives the coefficients necessary for the filter function.
The files are loaded into Audition, adjusted to the same signal length and sampling fre-
quency, and are loudness adjusted according to ITU-R BS.1770-3 [52] to a target loud-
ness of -23 LUFS (Loudness Unit relative to Full Scale), following the EBU R 128 standard
[53].

3
Listening Experiment
An audio signal can be measured by its physical characteristics, however, physical prop-
erties do not give a reliable indication of how the human auditory system will interpret it
[54]. In order to measure the quality of audio systems, subjective listening experiments
are recognized as being the most reliable method [55].
The evaluation of sound can involve assessing specific auditory characteristics or overall
sensory accuracy. The latter can be described with the terms plausibility and authenticity
[56] and are measures for different methods of assessment. Plausibility is an evaluation
with respect to the listener’s expectations and experience using an inner reference. This
concept assists in identifying quality features that enable a suitable reproduction of a par-
ticular application [57].
Comparing a system to an explicitly provided reference can indicate the degree of authen-
ticity, where a perfectly authentic reproduction would have identical physical aspects, and
for a person be perceptually indistinguishable from the reference [57].
In audio technology authentic reproduction is rarely required [58], and a reproduction can
be suitable for a given task without being authentic [57].
3.1
Experimental Procedures
One listening experiment is conducted to study the authenticity and plausibility of the
auralizations, while a second experiment is dedicated to testing individual auditory char-
acteristics. The different stages of sound evaluation can be explained by the Filter Model
shown in Figure 3.1. The initial stage involves physical measurements, such as RT. The
sound then undergoes assessment by the auditory system, serving as the first filter, which
measures individual auditory attributes such as loudness, similarity, etc. The perceived
impression of the sound is assessed through affective measurements or hedonic tests,
which provide subjective preferences [54].
Figure 3.1: The Filter Model illustrates the different sound evaluations though objective
and subjective tests. The model is originally made by Pedersen and Fog [59] and is
adapted by Zacharov and Beck [54]. The illustration is created by Michelle Herlufsen.

3.1.1
Test 1 - Consumer test
A consumer-oriented test comprises a sequence of two tests. The first part is intended
to obtain objective quantification of an overall impression of a sound stimulus. [54]. A
method which has been demonstrated suitable for assessing basic audio quality and giv-
ing reliable results is presented in Recommendation ITU-R BS.1534-3. This procedure is
a method used for subjective assessment of intermediate audio quality, and it is known
as MUlti Stimulus test with Hidden Reference and Anchor (MUSHRA). It is applicable in
situations where lower quality is acceptable or unavoidable, as opposed to Recommen-
dation ITU-R BS.1116, which is used for evaluating small impairments [55].
The objective in this case is to evaluate all identified differences between an object and
reference, with the overall audio quality being rated based on the degree of similarity be-
tween them [5].
The test will comprise 7 audio systems, resulting in a total of 9 test signals (1 reference,
6 audio systems, 1 hidden reference, and 1 hidden anchor). Test subjects can switch
between the known reference signal and any of the test signals. They can also zoom in
on a specific time segment in the signal to loop a portion of the sample. The minimum
loop duration is 500 ms, and a 5-ms raised-cosine-envelope fade-in and fade-out will be
applied to the looped signal content, as well as when switching between systems.
This method is characterized by the use of a hidden reference and anchor. Test subjects
will be asked to identify the hidden reference and rate it at the top of the scale, indicating
that the system and reference are identical. The remaining systems should be assessed
based on how different they are relative to the reference, using a scale that ranges from
very different to very similar1. This deviates from the MUSHRA standard where basic au-
dio quality is replaced by the sensory measure difference, using a scale similar to Lokki et
al. [60] and Pathre et al. [61]. Figure 3.2a below shows the user interface with the sound
systems, scales and description.

As a less strict criterion, plausibility is determined by the second part of the test evaluating
the single attribute Naturalness. Test subjects are tested to see if they identify simulated

similar to the plausibility study in Brinkmann et al. [3]. Test subjects were presented with
one sample per room and informed of the corresponding geometric context: Debussy in
the Concert Music Hall, a speech sample in the Auditorium, and Get Lucky in the Class-

their expectations of what it would sound like if they were physically present in the space,
or if it sounded simulated. The user interface is illustrated in Figure 3.2b.
In the test sequence, the MUSHRA test precedes the attribute test, which is to prevent
knowledge of room geometry details from influencing the MUSHRA test results. Addition-
ally, the repetition of a selection of the samples in the second part may aid in familiarity
and enhance the assessment of Naturalness.
The effect of asking sensory questions on overall differences before questions about the
sound characteristics is to prevent biases in the results.
Twenty-one people, comprising 5 women and 16 men (age median = 27 years, min = 22
years, max = 61 years), participated in the consumer test, and none reported any hearing
impairments.
For the MUSHRA test method, it is recommended to use experienced listeners, familiar

3.5.2

The Attribute test gives an idea of how different the systems are from the reference within
each attribute. However, a deviation in one attribute does not necessarily correspond to
the same perceptual level of difference in another attribute.
A combination of the MUSHRA and Attribute test will illustrate how an attribute influences
the rating in difference, and which of the six attributes result in the largest difference per-
ceptually. MLR is a statistical technique which can be found useful for this purpose. The
statistical method can help predict the value of the dependent variable when all the inde-
pendent variables are known, and can in this case be useful for analyzing the relationship

3.5.2.1
PCA
In short PCA is a variable reduction procedure, where a set of new variables are iden-
tified, based on linear combinations of the original variables. These new variables are
called Principal Components (PCs). While including all available PCs preserves the total
variance in the dataset, it is often possible to use a subset of the top PCs to capture the
most significant patterns. The procedure analyses the dataset and shows how many PCs
are necessary to reach a selected percentage of variance, such as 90 %. This means
that the data can be described from fewer independent variables and still include 90 % of
the original variance [69].
Consider the independent variables combined in a matrix, X, with the attributes as the
columns, and the MUSHRA test results in a Y-vector. The principal components seeks to
determine linear combinations, replacing the initial variables in new variables with differ-
ent properties [70].

The PCA can provide a correlation between the attributes and PCs. The attributes with
the highest value for each PC will be the ones describing most of the variance.
In the investigation conducted by Torresin et al. [73], PCA is also utilized to explore the
correlation between acoustic parameters and factors such as comfort, content, and famil-
iarity. The PCA was performed to reduce the attributes to a set of principal dimensions.
The attributes most strongly associated with the PCs are then interpreted.
Should the PCA fail to bring about a noteworthy reduction of the independent variables,
an alternative method for sample reduction is tested by removing one parameter at a time
and examining whether the initial regression model (comprising all attributes) significantly
differs from the regression model with reduced attributes.
The attribute with the highest p-value (Pr(>|t|)), in the initial regression model will be re-
moved for the second regression model. To assess significant differences, an ANOVA is
conducted in R. Performing parametric statistics using analysis of variance (ANOVA) is a
well-suited methodology and the most common means for statistical analysis for sensory
data [5] due to its robustness [55]. ANOVA is used to compare the means of populations
[65], and in this case, conclude if there is a significant difference in the means from the
two regression models.
If the p-value is >0.05 the models are not significantly different. The procedure of re-
moving the attribute is repeated until the ANOVA analysis comparing the original model
and the reduced model shows a p-value below 0.05. The most reduced model with an
ANOVA result indicating no significant differences will be accepted. The reduced model
will have eliminated attributes with multicollinearity, and their weighting of coefficients can
be considered.
The MLR gives an indication of the weighting of each attribute and their contribution to the
overall subjective similarity. An attribute with a high numerical coefficient will contribute
most to the similarity estimation.

5
Analysis
This study offers valuable insights into the realm of simulated sound and its processing.
The primary focus is on investigating whether simulations yield authentic auralizations,
and if they sound both realistic and natural. Additionally, the study explores how accu-
rately the simulations reproduce sound according to specific attributes. Combining the
attribute and MUSHRA test data the study aims to determine if certain attributes have
a higher perceptual weight, resulting in more pronounced differences in the perceptual
evaluation. These investigations encompass three distinct geometries and incorporate
varied simulation parameters and samples. Furthermore, the analysis delves into a com-
parative examination between a sample recorded directly in a room and the same sample
convolved with an impulse response from that room.
Hereafter, the term ”reference” refers to the measured impulse response convolved with
a sample, while the term ”recording” in the Classroom context denotes a sample directly
recorded within the space. It is essential to note that both the ”reference” and ”recording”
are captured in the Classroom, with the distinction arising from whether the audio has
undergone convolution or not.
5.1
Comparison of the MUSHRA Test Results
The outcomes of the MUSHRA test, as depicted in Figure 4.2, reveal a high level of con-
fidence in detecting the reference and anchor across all geometries. Additionally, a t-test
analysis indicates that all systems receive significantly different ratings from the reference,
with t-test results consistently below 0.05 for all systems. The t-test analysis is furthermore
created to compare the systems and assess the significance of the differences between
them, providing insights into the relative performance of each system. Table 4.1 with re-
sults from the t-test comparing selected systems internally is shown in subsection 4.2.1.
In the Concert Music Hall the systems A, B, E, and F are rated higher than C and D
from Figure 4.2a. The t-test analysis shows a significant difference between system E,
which has the highest mean score, and systems C and D. Furthermore, systems C and
D are also different from each other.
In this space, the systems A, B, E, and F are perceptually providing the most similar au-
ralizations compared to the reference, followed by system C. Finally, system D is rated
as the poorest auralization perceptually, in comparison to the other systems.
Some assessors explained that for the classical pieces, it was difficult to determine the ref-
erence. The classical and jazz music samples for this geometry do not introduce large en-
ergy distributions in the low frequencies below 60 Hz compared to e.g. rock song ”Money
For Nothing”, which can influence the audible differences in the low frequencies. This
may be a reason for the results being gathered around 60-80 in response.
The data from the Auditorium shown in Figure 4.2b is more separated and is generally
rated lower than in the two other geometries. The systems are divided into two groups
where B, D, and E are rated significantly higher than A, C, and F. The t-test shows no
significant differences internally in the groups.

6
Discussion
6.1
Parameter Selection, Materials and Sources of Error
Listening tests are useful for evaluating reliability on the perception of auralizations, but
give minimal insights into strengths and weaknesses of the simulation algorithms them-
selves [3]. The analysis of the two listening tests offers insights into the attributes that are
impacted when altering simulation parameters or algorithms.
This is interesting for questions raised on whether adjusting boundary conditions will influ-
ence the authenticity of the auralizations. If a reference room with known acoustic param-
eters is available, the simulations can be fitted to match the parameters. However, from
the data presented it is unlikely that e.g. absorption and scattering will influence other
perceptual attributes than reverberation, localizability and width significantly. Rather, it
is likely that the softwares will keep an overall audio fingerprint, and changing software
parameters can only potentially make the simulation perceptually closer to the reference
in limited attributes. Based on the data comparing the Treble hybrid to the Treble GA, a
change in the entire audio fingerprint would require a change in algorithm.
6.2
BRAS vs Recording
The results from the CRs, when compared to the Classroom, do not indicate a significant
increase in perceptual inaccuracy. Despite the choices and estimations made for the
boundary conditions, the Classroom still had perceptual differences from the reference
that were comparable to those observed in the Chamber Music Hall and Auditorium (and
not worse). Nevertheless, scientific evidence of this is lacking, making it difficult to draw
definitive conclusions.
6.3
Effect of Convolution
In the comparison of Reference and Recording in Figure 4.7 and Table 4.9, there are
significant differences in three attributes. The Recording shows perceptual differences
across all tests (MUSHRA, Naturalness and Attribute), even though a perceptual match
was expected.
A reason for the mismatch can be background noise disturbance due to the windy outdoor
conditions occurring that day. During the recording of the sample, clicking and lagging
disturbed the recording, and the output level had to be set lower to avoid this. The lack of
Bass Strength in the Recording could be caused by the lower output level or the Genelec
speaker’s frequency response1. The recorded samples were played back for listening
and to validate the recording’s quality. This validation process cannot be applied to the
IR as easily. The additional processing and convolution involve assumptions and simpli-
fications, potentially rendering the reference (IR) less accurate than the recording.
Equipment failure might also be an explanation as to why the curves are so different, how-
ever, the recordings of the impulse responses and samples were done in continuation of
each other. The Genelec speaker was used over the omnidirectional source since a quick
validation of the sound files using the Genelec speaker sounded more reliable than the
ones using the omnidirectional speaker.

6.4
Attribute Selection
When comparing the system’s differences to the reference, from the Attribute test and the
MUSHRA, they do not always agree. One answer is the weighting investigated through
the MLR analysis.
Another explanation could be, that in the Attribute test, experts are focusing on the spe-
cific attribute under investigation. They are not encouraged to listen to the overall sound
image and define their own vocabulary to explain the differences between reference and
system. The attributes are selected to be generally relevant for all systems, however, this
also means that a different attribute could potentially describe one of the systems in a
better way, and explain the cause of dissimilarity.
In the Classroom, two source locations were employed in the test. One attribute that
could have been interesting to add to the test, is the evaluation of left/right balance. This
would help determine whether the different source positions were perceived as being sim-
ulated accurately by the softwares. A specific attribute match for this was not identified in
the SAQI, and the attributes Localizabillity and Width were selected to cover this. How-
ever, if two auralizations provide the same perceptual width and a clear detection of the
source location, there can still be a difference in the perception of where the source is on
an azimuth/horizontal plane.
6.5
Statistics
The data from both the MUSHRA and Attribute tests are compared and analysed using
t-tests without the Bonferroni correction, which can cause slight noise in the data [65].
However, no significant conclusions were drawn from one p-value alone, and therefore
the Bonferroni correction is not critical.
An alternative post-hoc testing method is the Tukey HSD (honestly significant difference)
test [55][74], which can advantageously be applied for studies with less time constraints.
6.6
Naturalness Study
The plausibility test indicates how natural the simulations sound. The assessors are asked
to indicate whether BRIRs are identified as simulated similar to the method in Brinkmann
et al. [3], but with a continuous scale rather than a yes-no task. In their round robin study,
the plausibility test was conducted first without exposure to the test environment. In this
study the plausibility test follows the MUSHRA test.
Some subjects shared that they found the test difficult from the absence of visual ques,
particularly geometry and materials, and expressed a lack of confidence in anticipating the
acoustic characteristics associated with the room. It was emphasized that well-sounding
acoustics do not always align with realism/naturalness.
For future studies, it is recommended to follow the procedure from Brinkmann et al. and
simply give no environment details, or to follow the order procedure from this study (based
on the [75]) and include visual context for the plausibility test.

The attributes in the reduced regression model in Table 4.15 demonstrate a degree of
orthogonality in the biplot shown in Figure 4.15. Typically, changes in one of the orthogo-
nal attributes will affect the other, since they are connected (should not be confused with
correlated). For instance, an increase in reverberation can impact the localizability of the
source due to the added reflection, making the sound appear more diffuse. Similarly, an
increase in Treble Strength will decrease the relative level of the Bass.
While this may seem evident, it becomes interesting when considering the simulation’s
audio fingerprint. Brinkman et al. share that ”significant differences between different
simulation algorithms have to be expected” [3]. Level of Reverberation and Localizability
are mostly influenced by the boundary conditions, whereas Bass and Treble are likely to
remain relatively constant even with alterations in the selected boundary conditions. From
Table 4.8 the two hybrids are identical in almost all ratings of Bass and Treble Strength
across all geometries, only the Treble GA simulation differs significantly in the evaluations
of timbre. This implies that attributes explaining the tone colour are related to the algo-
rithm rather than input parameters in the simulations. Consequently, an experienced and
inexperienced simulation operator will likely not produce significant perceptual differences
in tone colour, when using the same simulation software and algorithm.

7
Conclusion
Perceptual similarities between measurement and simulation were examined through a
listening experiment involving 21 participants, focusing on authenticity and plausibility.
Additionally, a separate evaluation of sound attributes was conducted by 10 expert as-
sessors. The comparison involves three different geometries, with varying levels of confi-
dence in boundary conditions, implemented using three software applications, based on
NM and GA. The software applications Odeon, Treble, and Raven are used for simulating
the sound, and results are anonymized with letters A to F. It is obvious that due to the
anonymization, key conclusions based on comparison of GA and NM, are unspoken.
The results demonstrate that neither of the simulations produces authentic binauraliza-
tions with the current technology, thus none of the auralizations demonstrated indistin-
guishability from the reference. Systems B and E achieved better results compared to
the other systems across all three geometries, while the outcomes for the remaining sys-
tems vary depending on the geometry. The key distinctions between the reference and
systems also vary across geometries and are related to attributes describing tone colour,
source position, and spatial extent. Nevertheless, a trend suggests that the perceptual ac-
curacy of the Level of Reverberation decreases as the volume of the geometry increases.
Perceptual evaluations were conducted to explore the impact of changing the simulation
parameters, specifically the number of late rays in Odeon and the extent of simulating
with numerical methods in Treble. Not unexpected, the auralizations exhibit similarities
and maintain the same pattern of audio fingerprint (or sound attribute balance) across
all attributes. However, an exception is observed with Treble using the GA method only,
as it differentiates from the hybrid simulations, particularly in Bass Strength and Treble
Strength.
A PCA and MLR indicate that, among the six tested attributes, Localizability, Level of
Reverberation, Treble Strength, and Bass Strength collectively account for much of the
variance in the data. This observation is drawn from data obtained in the Chamber Music
Hall and Auditorium. Through the PCA and a sample reduction approach, Clarity and
Width are excluded from the essential attributes for describing the auralization, due to
multicollinearity with the four attributes.
Based on the comparison of the Treble hybrid and Treble GA simulations Treble Strength
and Bass Strength are impacted by the simulation algorithm. The change in tone colour
(or timbral balance) is therefore not something the user of the simulation can affect, but
it is rather internal software modifications which are necessary to perceptually align more
closely with the measurements.
While the influence of user input on the four attributes has not been thoroughly investi-
gated, this would need further investigation.
In acoustic applications, a compromise in authenticity is often deemed acceptable, and
when extending beyond the acoustic field, auralization serves as a valuable tool in estab-
lishing a shared sensory vocabulary.
The advancements and developments in acoustic simulation and auralization hold signifi-
cant potential, and the progress in computational power makes the utilization of NM more
relevant.

8
Further studies
8.1
Testing Convolution
All three tests reveal distinctions between the recorded sample and IR in the Classroom.
Upon listening to silent passages in the recorded samples, the background noise is no-
ticeable. Conducting recordings of samples and IRs with reliable equipment, in controlled
environments with minimal background noise, would be interesting to explore these dif-
ferences further. If perceptual differences persist, subsequent testing would be required
to determine whether the simulations align more closely with the recorded sample than
the recorded IR.
8.2
Calculated Vs Perceptual Level of Treble and Bass Strength
Examining frequency-related attributes such as Treble Strength and Bass Strength gives
an opportunity to assess the alignment of perceptual evaluations with relative treble and
bass levels of the systems. However, comparing these attributes is not straight-forward
and requires several considerations and assumptions. For instance, when quantifying
Bass Strength by integrating energy amplitude within the 20 Hz to 400 Hz range, the in-
fluence of Bass Depth (extension of sound in the low frequencies) remains uncertain. It
is unclear whether specific frequencies within this range (or even outside the range) will
have a larger weighting and give a more pronounced sense of bass in the evaluation from
the assessors.
This would be interesting to investigate and would allow the software companies to quan-
tify the IRs for improving the perceptual accuracy of auralizations.
8.3
Perceptual Differences Using Different Settings
Simulation software accuracy highly depends on the operator [8]. Previous studies, as
well as this, claim that the simulations under investigation do not provide authentic au-
ralizations. The audio fingerprint from the Attribute test provides a similar pattern for the
variations in the software applications, and it would be interesting to see how changes and
estimation in boundary condition estimation will change the audio fingerprint. A hypothe-
sis could be, that the operator is important for providing accurate results in the objective
parameters, whereas auralizations will not be significantly different perceptually, even with
inaccurate boundary conditions.
A convolved auralization has a higher complexity compared to examining objective results
within a single acoustic parameter. While objective measurements in individual acoustic
parameters offer a straightforward assessment, the auralization captures the interplay of
various acoustic elements, resulting in an auditory experience.
Accepting the hypothesis would make acoustic auralization a plausible tool even for peo-
ple inexperienced in the field of acoustics.
