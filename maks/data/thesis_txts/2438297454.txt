ABSTRACT
This MasterвҖҷs thesis is a design project which investigates a dome-like structure that can be repeated,
creating a larger spatial structure. Construction of concrete shells rarely occurs today as it is very labour
intensive and cumbersome to make. Furthermore, high labour cost, shortage of skilled workers and
increased prices of false-work and form-work make shells unattractive to developers. However, as a
shell structure is very efп¬Ғcient, the structure is able to be thin and light, yet covering large spans. With
an increasing interest and demand to reduce CO2 emission, it would be advantageous to construct shell
structures again, utilising their shape to reduce the amount of material needed. Nevertheless, reduction
of CO2 emission is not enough to boost the enthusiasm for shell structures. The structures must be
cost-effective in order to be more attractive. Consequently, the design project focuses on designing a
shell structure from п¬Ӯat prefabricated elements. Designing a shell structure, in which the construction
method is simpliп¬Ғed will greatly affect the cost of such a structure. It has therefore been of interest
to design a structure where the elements, connections, geometry and construction method is relatively
simple. Three different dome-like structures are investigated in which all are based on a dome with a
diameter of 30 meters and a height of 8.66 meters. In order to repeat the structures, the circular dome
base is transformed into a triangular, square and hexagonal base. The part of the dome that protrudes
outside of these polygons is cut away, creating pendentives in which the overlying structure is resting on.
The thesis tries to take the reader through the iterative design process in a best possible way. For the
design concept, solutions for the common preliminary design is п¬Ғrst investigated. The design aspects
explored in the preliminary design process are the overall geometry of the structure, geometry of the ele-
ments, connections, envelope and construction method. The structural design of the individual modules
are then explored, which concludes a п¬Ғnal design concept for the structure.

INTRODUCTION
1.1
Background
Shell structures have fascinated both structural engineers and architects for a long time and have been
built since ancient times. Great examples of this are the Pantheon in Rome and Hagia Sophia in Istanbul,
which were built in the early 2nd century and 6th century, respectively. Roman and Byzantine architecture
would later inspire many different dome structures, from Islamic architecture to renaissance and baroque
architecture. Even today, engineers look back to Roman structures for inspiration. As mentioned by John
Ochsendorf and Philippe Block [3], one can always learn a lot from studying historical structures, and
that this is especially true for shells.
Even though Pantheon, which still is the largest unreinforced concrete dome, was built in the 2nd century,
it would take many centuries before concrete shells would reappear in the world of architecture and
engineering. The art of concrete was lost in the dark ages and it took many years before it would
gradually resurface. In the middle of the 19th century, a French gardener named Joseph Monier made
the п¬Ғrst practical use of reinforced concrete allowing the concrete to resist much larger tensile stresses.
Numerous buildings were now built using the new material. However, it was not until the 1900s that
one would see concrete shell structures being frequently built to cover large spans. The invention of
reinforced concrete made it possible for shell structures to cover even longer spans than before, with even
smaller thicknesses. The reason for a shells efп¬Ғcient material use is its ability to transfer the external loads
to the supports mainly through membrane forces, such as compression and tension. This is a much more
effective way compared to the traditional way of taking the applied forces mostly by bending actions.
The 1920s to the 1960s were the golden age of thin-shell concrete structures, taking advantage of the
membrane actions. Pioneers such as Franz Dischinger, Eduardo Torroja, Pier Nervi and Felix Candela
made a signiп¬Ғcant impact on the architecture and structural engineering of the time. Leipzig market hall
(1929), Algeciras market hall (1934), Palazzetto Dello Sport (1957) and Los Manantiales (1958), stand
out as structural masterpieces and are still inspiring engineers with their thin and elegant shapes.
All though all these shells are elegant and effective for long spans, they were very labour-intensive and
cumbersome to make. Concrete shells require a signiп¬Ғcant amount of falsework and formwork. The
construction of reinforced concrete shells quickly declined after the 1960s, and are rarely built today.
Some of the reasons for this are the rising labour cost, shortage of skilled workers and increased prices
of form-work [3]. Looking at п¬Ғgure 1.1 of the Madrid shell bridge from 2009, one can see one of the
reasons for shell structures decline, and why so many are reluctant to undertake this type of construction.

1.3 Arches and Domes
1.3
Arches and Domes
By carefully analysing an arch, one can п¬Ғnd its ideal shape for any given load case. Robert Hooke
described this principle in the 17th century, summarised by the words, вҖқ As hangs the п¬Ӯexible line, so
but inverted will stand the rigid archвҖқ. By inverting the hanging chain, which only acts in pure tension,
one can create an equivalent arch that acts only in compression. For instance, the ideal shape for an arch
under its own weight forms the catenary. However, a horizontally distributed load would form a parabola
as the ideal shape. A thrust line, as seen in п¬Ғgure 1.6 illustrates the ideal shape of an arch without any
tensile strength. The line of thrust is the resultant path of the gravity load and the outward thrust in the
arch. The line of thrust must lie within the section for the structure to be in pure compression.
Figure 1.6: Line of thrust [11]
By revolving the arch about its central vertical axis, one would obtain a dome, which is a double curved
structure. In contrast to the arch, the weight of the dome increases with the distance from the centre.
Imagining a segment of a dome, one can see that the width increases, meaning that the ideal line of thrust
is not equal to that of an arch. The ideal shape of a dome under its own weight is therefore closer to a
parabolic shape. Furthermore, The dome acts very much like an arch along the meridian. However, the
dome introduces another behaviour, called hoop forces, which can be seen in п¬Ғgure 1.7a. These forces
are acting along the circumference and contribute signiп¬Ғcantly to the stability of the dome structure.
Therefore, if the line of thrust in a dome falls outside the section, it will cause an increase in the hoop
stresses. Figure 1.7b shows the transition from compressive to tensile circumferential hoop forces. A
shallow dome will in comparison to a hemispherical dome produce a higher outward thrust at the base
but will develop smaller tensile hoop stress.

It is evident that the Roman engineers were well aware of the п¬Ӯow of forces in a shell structure, which can
be seen by studying the section of the Pantheon in п¬Ғgure 1.8. The Pantheon is the largest unreinforced
concrete dome in the world, with a diameter of 43.3 meters, and supported by a cylindrical body as high
as its radius [21]. The oculus at the top forms a compression ring, as the circumferential hoop stresses
at this point is in compression. Furthermore, the engineers must have known where the hoop stresses
transform from compression to tension. The Romans took advantage of different types of concrete as
well as increasing the cross-sectional thickness, ranging from a thickness of 1.5 meters at the oculus to
roughly 6 meters at the base. The heaviest aggregate, such as a mixture of travertine, tuff and bricks were
used at the bottom to counteract the outward thrust. A lighter mix of tufa and scoria were used at the
top to reduce the weight of the dome, and therefore also the outward thrust. Furthermore, rectangular
indentations called coffers were designed to further reduce the weight by replacing concrete with empty
space.
The Roman engineers were also the п¬Ғrst to experiment with the use of pendentive domes, but they
had challenges supporting a dome over a square or polygonal space. However, the Byzantine engineers
perfected this construction method. One of the earliest and greatest examples of a pendentive dome is
the Hagia Sophia, where the central bay has a 30-meter square base. At the corners, piers were placed
to support four arches. Between the arches, triangular concave pendentives support the overlying dome,
allowing a transition from a square space to a circular space. This system allows the weight of the dome
to be transferred down to four points, however, without any structural system to withstand the lateral
thrust, it would collapse. The lateral thrust of the central dome is transferred to numerous half-domes,
buttress vaults and buttresses down to the foundation.

1.4 Shell surface classiп¬Ғcation
1.4
Shell surface classiп¬Ғcation
The geometry of a shell can be described by the curved shape of the surface. At any point p on the
surface, it has an inп¬Ғnite number of plane curves. One of these plane curves has a minimum curvature,
and another has a maximum curvature [5]. The two plane curves are called the principal sections in which
their curvatures are called the principal curvatures of the surface at point p. The principal curvatures can
be denoted k1 and k2, and the Gaussian curvature K of a surface is the product of the two principal
curvatures. For a surface to have a positive Gaussian curvature, as seen on the left in п¬Ғgure1.10, both k1
and k2 must have the same sign. The surface at that point is said to be synclastic. Furthermore, a surface
with the two principal curvatures, k1 and k2, being of opposite signs has a negative Gaussian curvature
and is said to be anticlastic. A negative Gaussian curvature can be seen on the right in п¬Ғgure 1.10. If a
surface has zero Gaussian curvature, as seen in the middle of п¬Ғgure 1.10, one or both principal curvatures
are zero. The surface has then a single curvature [5].
Figure 1.10: Shell surfaces
Another way of looking at the curvature of a shell can be done by imagining a п¬Ӯat sheet of paper. The
sheet has zero Gaussian curvature. When bending it or making it into a cone, the Gaussian curvature
remains zero. By п¬Ӯattening the sheet again, one can see that it has not been deformed by wrinkles or
tears. The surface can therefore be seen as developable. If one were to wrap the same sheet over a
sphere, which has a positive curvature, the sheet would need to wrinkle in order to match the surface
of the sphere. Moreover, when trying to wrap the sheet over a hyperbolic surface, which has a negative
curvature, one would need to tear the sheet to make it conform to the surface. A surface with negative
or positive Gaussian curvature is therefore said to be undevelopable. In other words, double curvature
surfaces cannot be developed. Furthermore, double curvature surfaces need more external energy to be
deformed compared to single curved surfaces. Consequently, undevelopable shell surfaces are in general
stronger and more stable compared to a developable shell surface with the same overall dimensions [5].

Super-light concrete
The idea of the super-light structure is to create a skeleton made from a strong material, such as an ordi-
nary or high-strength concrete. This strong skeleton interacts with a stabilising layer of light aggregate
concrete or foam concrete. Super-light structures take advantage of the compression zone, where the
strong concrete is placed. The compression zone can then be either partly or fully embedded in the in the
light stabilising material, like the SL deck in п¬Ғgure 1.11a and a super-light beam in п¬Ғgure 1.11b.
(a) SL deck [2]
(b) SL beam [7]
Figure 1.11: Super-light structures
Additionally, the light concrete protects the strong concrete against п¬Ғre and improves the acoustic proper-
ties. Furthermore, to take advantage of the strong concretes compressive strength, pearl-chain reinforce-
ment was invented to create a curved compression zone economically. Figure 1.11b shows an example of
utilising pearl-chain reinforcement as a curved compression zone that can be designed as a chain of pre-
fabricated segments. The high strength segments are then assembled into an arch by pre-stressing wires.
Moreover, the pre-stressed pearl-chains can withstand tensile stresses as unloaded compression so that
the stiffness of the concrete section is used in tension [15]. The pearl-chain reinforcement also allows
for bigger structures, such as arch bridges or other curved structures to build more economically. Since
arches and other curved structures can be optimised corresponding to a given load situation, pearl-chain
technology allows the designer to utilise the curved design to reduce material use and cost.
The principle of pearl-chain reinforcement works in such a way that each of the segments in the pearl-
chain has one or more holes going through the section. These holes allow for pre-stressed wires to pass
through each segment. When stressing the wires and anchoring the ends, the pearl-chain is pre-stressed.
Each of the segments in the pearl-chain is then joined in compression. This compression is not affected
by the path of the chain because the pre-stressing wire follows the same path [15]. Furthermore, if a
pearl chain is designed to be a tension zone, the pre-stressing must be large enough for the tension force
to be counteracted by unloaded compression of the segments in the pearl-chain. If the tension force in
the pearl-chain is larger than the pre-stressing force, the segments of the pearl-chain will no longer be
joined in compression, meaning that it would lose its stiffness.

It is therefore an

the triangular elements will eliminate in-plane shear stresses. Moreover, for a hemispherical dome, the

hemispherical dome. The dome itself would then not be as curved, but the number of elements would
decrease. Furthermore, creating a dome for a square-, triangular-, or hexagonal base that can be repeated
as part of a bigger structure, the triangular or hexagonal geodesic dome are not preferable as the number

The simplicity of a connection is essential, as it will reduce the cost concerning manufacturing as well
as facilitating a quick assembly process. Moreover, a simple connection reduces the risk of mistakes on
the construction site, and if one element in a connection has several purposes the chance of human error
is further reduced. Furthermore, as much as possible of the different elements in a connection should
therefore be embedded in the prefabricated element. As the intrados of the dome shows the exposed
structural elements, the aesthetics of the connections are important as well. If the connection detail is
not hidden, the structural connections can either be emphasised as part of the architecture or as a subtle
detail where the functions still are articulated. Furthermore, the joint must be durable, which means that
corrosion of steel elements and cracking or spalling of concrete must be avoided. There are several ways
of connecting elements, such as bolting, grouting reinforcement bars on site, welding and pre-stressing.
However, grouting between elements as well as pre-stressing is considered the most elegant and time

As discussed in section 1.3, a dome will be subjected to two main types of forces. The п¬Ғrst type is the
compression forces along the meridional lines of the dome. The second is the compressive or tensile
hoop forces along the circumferential lines of the dome.
The hand-calculation for the dome has been calculated by transforming the distributed load over an ele-
ment into an equivalent point load with an equivalent centre of gravity, represented by Pi. An assumption
has been made that the elements are working as a grid-shell, and in this case, only supported at the top
and bottom, as seen in п¬Ғgure 3.8.
The loads are assumed distributed in a unidirectional manner to the meridian ribs, and down to the
supports. By investigating the moment about the bottom part of the element, the point load Pi will cause
a horizontal force inwards at the top, which in this case is called Vi. Due to equilibrium, an equivalent
force Vi will press outwards at the bottom of the element. The uppermost element will as a result of this
give rise to a compression ring at the oculus, which acts as a three-dimensional keystone. The outwards
thrust of an element вҖқiвҖқ, is counteracted by the inwards force of the element below. However, if the load
of the element underneath is smaller, such that the inwards force Vi+1 is smaller than Vi, the elements
would no longer be joined in compression.

A п¬Ғnite element analysis of the three different structures was made to calculate the forces in each element

The п¬Ғnite element software used is called FEM-design and created by Strusoft. The method used for
the linear static analysis of FEM-design is the result of the equation KВ·u=Q, where K is the stiffness
matrix of the system. вҖқuвҖқ represents the matrix of node displacement and вҖқQвҖқ is load vector matrix.
The linear static analysis results are always the displacement of nodes, internal forces or stresses of the
elastic elements and the reaction forces [22]. Even though the software is good for understanding the
structure, it is important to know that making the wrong assumptions would give rise to fallible results.
Bill Addis states that вҖқstructural designers have often used small-scale physical models or analytical
models to raise the engineerвҖҷs conп¬Ғdence in the structural design. Nevertheless, computer models are
known to be fallible, due to programming errors or improper assumptions, such as boundary conditions,
in the building of the modelвҖқ [3]. For this reason, a lot of smaller test models have been made to analyse
and compare the difference between different types of elements, as well as comparing them to hand-
calculations. First, a test model was made to analyse the difference between shell elements and grid-shell

5.2
Triangular Module
For the triangular based structure, the pre-stressing cable
can be placed at the top of the edge arches. However,
this means that there are two connections below the pre-
stressing cable where the angle of the elements changes.
This will result in an outward thrust and tension in the
connections, as explained in section 5.1. For a layout with
triangular modules, there will always be an edge whose
forces cannot be counteracted by a neighbouring module.
This means that the outward thrust of the free edge has
to be dealt with. For the square base, the solution was
to change the angle of the lowest element, allowing the
forces to п¬Ӯow along the ribs, eliminating the tension in
the connection. However, this is not a good solution for
the triangular module. The two lowest levels of elements
would have to change the inclination to that of the third
level of elements.
Figure 5.15: Dimensions for triangular base
This would mean that the three lower elements would have an inclination of 25 degrees. Furthermore,
the structure would have to resist far greater bending moments. In addition, the ceiling height would be
reduced signiп¬Ғcantly. A second solution would be to counteract the lateral thrust with a semi-dome or
a buttress vault, like Hagia Sophia. The semi-dome support would not allow much light to enter into
the structure. It would also cause more elements to be manufactured. A buttress vault would reduce the
daylight less than the semi-dome, but would also cause more modules to be manufactured. Furthermore,
the triangular module is more sensitive to skew loads compared to the other two modules. Due to this
fact, and the fact that the edge arches are larger, the cross-section of the edge beams would need to be
substantially larger than the other two modules. In addition, the module does not cover as much roof area
as the two other modules, meaning that it would be more expensive to construct. For these reasons, the
triangular module design was scrapped, as the two other modules were seen as more promising.

CONCLUSION
This project set out to investigate and design a prefabricated spatial concrete shell structure with the
use of п¬Ӯat elements. It strives to reduce the complexity of construction and production of such a shell
structure in order to reduce cost.
The solution was a dome-like modular structure with a diameter of 30 meters and a height of 8.66 meters.
First of all, a dome is a doubly curved structure, which makes it very strong and stable. In addition, the
structure will mostly be subjected to compressive stresses, which utilises the compressive strength of the
concrete. The overall geometry of the structure allows for large spans and spatial volumes with few sup-
ports, without having to use a large amount of material. As a shell structure is able to transfer the external
loads to the supports mainly through membrane forces, they are very efп¬Ғcient. However, when designing
a shell structure out of larger п¬Ӯat elements, the shell must also be able to resist bending moments. The
structure will therefore not be as efп¬Ғcient. Nevertheless, the construction process with prefabricated ele-
ments will not be as cumbersome as a smooth in-situ cast shell. Furthermore, a dome-like structure was
chosen as it can be created by rotating a segment of elements around its central vertical axis. This allows
for few elements to be created. Moreover, the dome-like modular structure can be repeated, creating a
larger spatial structure. The systematic repetition of elements and modules throughout the structure is
not only an architectural feature. By creating few elements that can be used several times, allows for a
repetitive manufacturing process, which reduces the manufacturing costs.
Three different modules were investigated. In order to repeat the structures, the circular dome base is
transformed into a triangular, square and hexagonal base. The part of the dome that protrudes outside
of these polygons is cut away, creating pendentives in which the overlying structure is resting on. The
elements of the structure are created from trapezoidal rib elements. The ribbed element makes it possible
to concentrate the compressive forces where it is wanted, allowing the elements to be joined in compres-
sion. Moreover, the ribs facilitate the construction phase, as it is easier to connect the elements compared
to a uniform thin element. Furthermore, the simplicity of joints and structural elements ensures further
reduction of manufacturing cost as well as a cheaper construction process. Few elements reduce the
number of connections that must be treated on site, and therefore facilitating a quick assembly process.
Moreover, the simplicity of the connections reduces the risk of mistakes on the construction site.

==PAGE:74==
Chapter 6. CONCLUSION
The use of pre-stressing cables has multiple functions. The circumferential pre-stressing cable and pre-
stressing in the edge arches ensure that the outward thrust and tensile stresses can be taken as unloaded
compression in the concrete. Furthermore, by pre-stressing larger segments into pearl-chains, makes it
possible to construct the pendentives and edge arches in a simple way. The use of prefabricated elements
eliminates the need for formwork, which reduces the construction time. Furthermore, the proposed con-
struction method with the use of temporary guyed mast is seen as a good solution as it reduces the number
of temporary support elements needed. It also allows for use of tension members to hold the structure in
place until it is stable, which reduces the amount of material needed.
Furthermore, using super-light shear plates allows the prefabricated elements to have minimal weight
while protecting the strong concrete against п¬Ғre. In addition, the п¬Ӯexibility of the super-light shear plates
facilitates the production of massive concrete zones in which the pre-stressing cables are located.
Comparing the three different modules it can be concluded that the triangular module is not a good
solution as it has to be designed for large tensile stresses. The two other modular concepts show a much
greater potential, and there is not too much difference between the two. The most signiп¬Ғcant difference
between the two is that the amount of the original dome shape that is cut away, which inп¬Ӯuences the
strength and stability of the structure. This results in larger dimensions for both ribs and end arches
for the square based module. Furthermore, the dimensions of the reinforcement bars in the shear plates
need to be larger. Overall the two modules require the same number of individual elements as the square
module constructed with nine different roof elements and the hexagonal module is constructed with
seven roof elements and two column elements. In general, the choice of module is essentially a matter
of wanting a square or hexagonal modular п¬Ӯoor plan.

FURTHER WORK
In this chapter, recommendations for further work is outlined. First of all, a more detailed design of
the structure should be made. This includes a detailed veriп¬Ғcation of the structure regarding ultimate
state, serviceability and п¬Ғre safety. Consequently, detailed veriп¬Ғcation of the connections and elements
needs to be carried out. Furthermore, a design of the foundation and investigation of settlements must
be treated as well as deformation due to creep and shrinkage of the structure. In addition, a small-scale
physical model could be made for further analysis in order to get a better understanding of the structure.
The structure could also be optimised with regards to element sizes as the elements are designed with
respect to the most critical element. This would further reduce the weight of the structure.
For the concept of construction, a more detailed process should be established. Furthermore, a detailed
design of the temporary structures regarding the structures must be investigated. This includes the guyed
mast and rig for assembling the pearl-chains. Moreover, investigation of the critical condition during the
construction phase should be carried out, such as skew loads caused by elements lifted in place on just
half the structure, snow load and wind loads. In addition, to ensure the feasibility of the design concept,
a cost estimation of such a shell structure should be made.
On the design concept of structural elements, it could also be of interest to investigate a hexagonal model
with use of triangular elements. As mentioned earlier, the triangular elements are very rigid and will
eliminate in-plane shear stresses. It would therefore be interesting to investigate the difference between
trapezoidal elements and triangular elements.
This project has only investigated shell structures with a positive Gaussian curvature. However, the
author would like to point out that it could be interesting to investigate and design a shell structure with a
negative Gaussian curvature out of п¬Ӯat elements. For instance, a hyperbolic paraboloid shell made from
a square п¬Ӯoor plan can be created out of 7 individual triangular elements. By creating an element which
is symmetric about the centre-line, it could be used for both the negative curve direction and positive
curve direction.
