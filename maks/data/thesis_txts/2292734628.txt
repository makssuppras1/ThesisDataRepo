Summary (English)
In this thesis it will be investigated how eye-tracking can be integrated into
the development process of game designs. The aim is to create a plug-in that
can be used to analyse user’s level of emotional engagement through eye data.
The plug-in is created with the Unity game engine, because it provides an easy
customisable interface suited for prototyping. Game designers are involved in
designing the end product as they are the intended customer. The total work of
the thesis results in eight iterative development steps where the focus were to
create the visualisations that were suggested by the customer, these suggestion
were collected through surveys and a interviews. Scan paths are the ﬁrst visu-
alisation that are implemented throughout the iterations, making it possible to
display user’s eye-movement using ﬁxations and saccades. This make it possible
for future studies to extract a number of measurements that can be used to
further conduct statistical analysis of user’s emotional engagement. In order to
visualise scan paths in dynamic environment such as games it is assisted by a
playback system. With the playback system, scan paths are able to show were
user’s is looking at a speciﬁc moment, making it possible for game designer to
determine from the visualisations what element they were looking at. The last
visualisation that is implemented in this thesis is heat maps applied on to in-
game objects. Heat maps on object makes it possible to ﬁnd what part of an
object attracts most attention from the user.

Preface
This thesis was prepared at the department of Informatics and Mathematical
Modelling at the Technical University of Denmark to fulﬁl the requirements to
acquire a M.Sc degree in Digital Media Engineering.
The project period ran from the start of August 2015 to the start of January
2016. The total work put into this project corresponds to 30 ECTS points.
The thesis deals with analysing data from a low-budget eye-tracker. From the
eye data a number of visualisation are created in order to analyse people’s level
of emotional engagement while playing video games.
The visualisations are
intended to assist game designers in ﬁnding game design issues.
The thesis consists of the following chapters.
Chapter 1 is the introduction to the thesis.
Chapter 2 contains related work that builds a start foundation for this thesis.
Chapter 3-10 cover all the iterations of the product.
Chapter 11 is the conclusion where all results are presented.
Chapter 12 is a discussion of the overall work and mention further work that
can be explored.

Chapter 1
Introduction
For years, eye-tracking has been known to scientiﬁc communities, marketing
and UX (User experience) studies as a tool to research cognitive load and level
of emotional engagement of users when they read books, visit websites and
look at advertisements. Eye-tracking systems have been very expensive in the
past, making it almost impossible for independent developers to invests in such
equipment.
However because the technology has been a popular subject for
years, companies are now looking into producing low-cost eye-trackers that de-
liver almost the same level of details as their more expensive competitors. Eye
Tribe[Tri15] and Tobii[Tob15] are examples of such companies, they are pro-
ducing eye-tracking equipment that cost around 99-139$.
Gazepoint[Gaz15]
also produce low-cost eye-trackers but cost ﬁve times more compared to Eye
Tribe and Tobii.
1.1
Main measurements
The main measurements that are used in eye-tracking are ﬁxations - the moment
when the eyes are relatively stationary, Saccades - the quick eye movement
between ﬁxations. Other measurements are pupil size and blink rate. However
pupil size and blink rate can be determined by many factors, making them very
open to interpretation and are therefore less used[Bal03].
==PAGE:16==
2
Introduction
1.2
Game industry and Eye-tracking
There are three approaches to using eye-tracking, the ﬁrst is passive tracking it
is used to collect a number of eye measures on the user and is later analysed
to determine their cognitive or emotional states. The second approach is active
tracking, here the eye-tracker is used as an input device and enables the user
to control computer interfaces by using their eyes. To the game industry there
could be potential beneﬁts in using eye-tracking to gain knowledge on, where
gamers are looking, what is attracting their attention and when it is occurring.
Gaining insight on users eye behaviour through passive tracking may highlight
ﬂaws in the game design that is not easily found through traditional test meth-
ods. Game designers could also decide to create games where eye-trackers is
used in an active approach, used as the main controller1 to control the avatar,
or featured as an additional experience gimmick2.
Today gamers ﬁnd themselves more diverse in age, gender, preferences and
experience than the past. The need to ﬁnd games that are challenging, artistic
and fun that suits the individual gamers taste can be diﬃcult to ﬁnd. This is
where eye-tracking can help game designers by making the gaming experience
more personalised. By using passive tracking, you are able to build a behavioural
proﬁl on the user and adapt the gaming experience by dynamically changing in-
game elements so they suit that users taste, this third approach is called adaptive
tracking.
1.3
GameAnalytics vs Eye-tracking
A number of game studios are using metrics data to build behavioural proﬁles
of gamers. GameAnalytics[Gam15] are a known company that specialises in
collection in-game data and visualising it for their customers. However, they
have yet to support biometric data such as the data obtained with eye-tracking.
Eye-tracking could be a good addition to GameAnalytics enabling their userbase
to analyse user’s cognitive load or level of emotional engagement.

1.4
Project Description
This project is about involving low budget eye-tracking equipment into the
development process of game designs. Eye-tracking will be used to analyse the
level of emotional engagement of gamers in order to ﬁnd game design problems.
The ﬁrst step in this analysis will determine what attracts gamers attention
during play sessions.
Using the game engine Unity, a plugin tool will be developed, its purpose are
to visualise the eye data so it can be interpreted by the game designers.

Related Work
Related work that utilise eye-tracking have been researched, the articles have
been categories into passive or active tracking studies.
2.1
Passive tracking
2.1.1
Emotional processing
In a recent study[Alt15] it was found that emotional words have an eﬀect on
cognitive load when reading. Emotional words both negative and positive was
processed faster than neutral words.
2.1.2
Cognitive abilities measured in Games
Action games enhance players visual attention, their reaction time are faster
and the cognitive limit is better than players who play non-action games[Bav10].
However in a recent study[Eng15] they research the same subject, that video
games enhance cognitive abilities, by using extreme-group studies (large sample

group) their ﬁndings are in direct contrast with prior studies. In an HCI (Hu-
man Computer Interaction) study [Mül15] a silhouette avatar was controlled by
the users body, it was found that it attracted most of the users attention. It was
suggested that people focus on the objects that represent the tool that manip-
ulate the virtual environment. In the same study[Mül15] they also found when
users understood the controls their attention would drift from the silhouette
avatar to other stimuli. The ﬁnding indicated the level of experience in the user
and it seems to correlate with the ﬁndings from the study[Wic01], here seven
expert pilots and ten novice pilots had their scan pattern tested over 15 landing
trails. The ﬁndings showed that experienced pilots had more frequent ﬁxations
with shorter dwell time compared to novice pilots.
2.1.3
Usability tool
An interesting statement is found in the study[Rau08] "When game designer
have been involved in usability work they have a stake in it and are more likely
to acknowledge the results". However if that is not the case, game designers
are having a hard time getting involved with usability work. In their conclusion
they suggest, if videos can show eye scan pattern or heat maps, revealing what
players see or miss, it might involve the game designer in usability work or used
to improve the quality of existing retrospective evaluation tests.
2.1.4
Pupil dilation related to attention
A recent study [Lar15] made use of low-cost eye-tracking to measure pupil size
when a number of attentional tasks related to alertness, spatial orientation and
conﬂict resolution was introduced. It appeared that conﬂict resolution tasks in
contrast to the others required larger eﬀort, the right hemisphere of the brain
was activated in order to process the stimuli, and results indicated a dilation in
the left pupil occurred. The right hemisphere is known to contribute to attention
[dam15]. Pupil dilation has in previous studies been linked to other cognitive
and emotional states listed in[vdW11, p. 393 - 394].

2.2
Active tracking
2.2.1
Eye-tracking as input controller in games
Ubisoft has implemented support for eye-tracking in their game Assasin’s Creed
Rogue1. The in-game camera will pan to the position of where the user is look-
ing. In a study[Mar06] it was examined how well eye-tracking performed as an
input controller in ﬁrst person shooter (FPS) games compared to mouse+keyboard
and gamepad. The ﬁndings indicated the player would have a disadvantage when
gazing at targets in the distance because of the accuracy issues eye-trackers have,
however when comparing with the other input controllers the eye-tracker per-
formed similarly to that of a gamepad.

be able to do and most importantly conﬁrm a possibly marked exists.

4.2
Methods
The most common visualisation techniques that are used with eye-tracking is
scan paths and heat maps. In correlation with the visualisations area of interest
(AOI) is often used to gain additional measurements. Scan path, heat map and
AOI will be described in this section.
==PAGE:30==
16
Iteration 2
4.2.1
Scan paths
Scan paths (also referred to as "gaze path") visualises the movement of the eyes
that occurred on the screen. When a lot of eye points is gathered at a location
on the screen it is considered a ﬁxation. The average ﬁxation duration[Boj13]
range from less than 100ms to half a second, when scene viewing the range is
around 280 - 330ms. The current ﬁxation breaks when you look somewhere else.
The quick movement the eyes make when going from one ﬁxation to another is
called saccades.
A common visualisation of scan paths is ﬁxations are drawn as circles and the
size of the circle is determined by the dwell time. A number is assigned to the
circle in order to indicate the sequence of the recording, they are redrawn in
that order. Saccades are represented by simple lines. (See ﬁgure 4.1)
Figure 4.1: Scan path visualisation example.
4.2.1.1
Scan path measurements
A number of measurements can be extracted from scan paths, ﬁxation and
saccades. An excellent overview have been made in [Bal03] that mention the
measurements and their interpretation (see appendix E for ﬁxations, appendix
F for saccades and appendix G for scan path).

4.2 Methods

4.2.2
AOI
Area of interest (AOI) is a method that require areas(or regions) to be setup in
the test environment (website, picture etc.) before testing is performed. The
areas are set around objects of interest, which we in eye-tracking terms refer to
as stimulus/stimuli. Useful data can be obtained by implementing AOI and is a
method commonly used in quantitative analysis. The following list shows what
measurements[Boj13] can be obtained by AOI. (See ﬁgure 4.2)
4.2.2.1
AOI measurements
• Percentage of participants who ﬁxated on an AOI
• Number of ﬁxations prior to the ﬁrst ﬁxation on an AOI
• Time to the ﬁrst ﬁxation on an AOI
• Number of ﬁxations on an AOI
• Total dwell time on an AOI
• Percentage of time spent on an AOI
Figure 4.2: AOI visualisation example.

4.2.3
Heat maps
Heat maps (also referred to as attention maps) visualises regions with many
ﬁxations. Regions with many ﬁxations are highlighted with a warm colour (red,
yellow) and regions with fewer ﬁxations are assigned a cold colour (blue, green).
A transparent colour is assigned when no ﬁxations are present. (See ﬁgure 4.3).
Figure 4.3: Heat map visualisation example.
4.3
Scan path vs Heat map
The scan path and heat map both have the potential to visualise what is at-
tracting the users attention on the screen. Both are similar in a sense but still
diﬀers enough to speciﬁc tests scenario. Essentially the tests scenarios will be
in dynamic environments and in order to answer the question of "what" is at-
tracting the users attention, it is necessary to know "where" the user is looking
and "when".
Heat maps can be used to ﬁnd out "where" users are looking, but can’t tell us
"when" the user looked at a particular location because all data is visualised
at once[Boj13]. Usually, the data displayed are gathered from multiple persons
making heat map best suited for quantitive testing. For dynamic environments
such as games that change elements over time, it would be hard to implement
==PAGE:33==
4.4 Approach
19
heat maps, but it wouldn’t be impossible. However some genres in games feature
very static environments such as the "point and click adventure" and it would
be more suited to use heat maps here.
With scan paths it is possible to ﬁnd "where" the individual user looked and
"when" (the sequence) it happened, making it better suited for qualitative test-
ing. However as with heat maps, it will require some work to make scan paths
handle dynamic environments.
4.4
Approach
Reﬂecting back on the ﬁndings from iteration 1, the majority wanted a visu-
alisation of the gamers eye movement and this is what scan path solve. For
future iterations, it will also be beneﬁcial to start on implementing the scan
path ﬁrst. As mention in the description of scan path, the visualisation consists
of ﬁxations and saccades. Just by having ﬁxations and saccades a large number
of measurements become available and can be used to further analyse the user’s
level of emotional engagement.
In a later iteration, AOI and heat map could be combined to answer the question
by Michael from Playwood, "what part of an object attract most attention".
4.5
Results
The visualisation techniques and methods used in eye-tracking was researched to
ﬁgure out an approach that could be used to answer the research question "What
attracts gamers attention during play sessions". Scan paths were found to be
best suited to ﬁgure out "where" a user looked and "when" it occurred. Fur-
thermore by implementing scan paths, ﬁxations and saccades make a number of
measurement available for future work with users level of emotional engagement.

Scan path will visualise users eye movement when they are gazing on the screen.
To determine where ﬁxations occur during a play session an algorithm is needed

==PAGE:38==
24
Iteration 3
Figure 5.2: Dropdown menu for XML ﬁle selection. The unique name of the
XML ﬁle is determined by a date and a session Id.
because of its strength to dynamically ﬁnd distinctive clusters in the dataset
with relatively few predetermined parameters.
When comparing with other
cluster algorithms like the more common k-mean and hierarchical clustering,
they require that you guess on a number of resulting clusters beforehand and
are therefore not suited for ﬁnding ﬁxations.
The basic DBScan algorithm selects one point at the time, then visits all of that
points adjacent points, if they are within a predetermined distance they will be
marked to be part of a cluster. When enough points have been marked, this is
determined by a parameter, they will form a cluster. If points do not end up in
a cluster they are classiﬁed as outliers or noise.
During a play session, a common scenario will happen. The participant will look
at location A for some time, then proceed to look at some other location B, at
some point he will look close to location A again. The basic DBScan algorithm
cannot distinguish between the ﬁrst and the second visit to location A, only one
cluster would be formed. With eye-tracking ﬁxations may at some point overlap
as with the scenario, but should not be considered as one ﬁxation(cluster) which
is what the basic DBScan does. To get the wanted result, the DBScan algorithm
will need to be slightly changed. The custom algorithm should take the time
into consideration when forming clusters.
It is stated in [vdW11, p. 380] that the intake and processing of the ﬁxated po-
sition can start only after some 70-80ms after the start of the ﬁxation. Knowing
that piece of information, all the DBScan will have to do is break a ﬁxation after
3 eye points (corresponds to 80ms at 30Hz ), to indicate when a new ﬁxation is
formed.
After the custom algorithm has been implemented it returns the eye points in
clusters. Centroids can be calculated from the clusters and they are used to
draw circles representing the ﬁxations, a number is also displayed on the circle
that represents the sequence of eye movement. The circles are connected with
lines to visualise saccades (See ﬁgure 5.3).

To ﬁgure out where to start on designing a playback system for Unity some time

1http://www.gamasutra.com/view/feature/2029/developing_your_own_replay_system.php

a replay system and essentially it is the same as playback system, it appears
there are two approaches to designing one.
8.2.1
Deterministic
The ﬁrst approach requires that the developer is designing his game to be fully
deterministic from the very start. If that is the case it is only a matter of storing
the players controller input. The game will always react the same way when
the controller input is played back. It must be noted that even if your game
contains random events or elements that make use of a random function as long
as you store the seed number and play it back in the same sequence your game
will still remain deterministic.
8.2.2
Saving the states
The second approach requires that you store all the important information of
each object for each frame. As a start the most common information that are
stored is the object’s position and rotation. However when you are working
with complex 2d or 3d models they can consist of many parts. As an example, a
human model will consist of a head, body, left arm, right arm, left leg and right
leg. To complicate things, parts can contain further subparts and so on. Each
part have their own rotation and position and these will need to be stored. The
end result is you have a ﬁle where all information for each frame is stored and
as such it will be possible to recreate a frame to near perfection. This approach
takes time setting up but developers will not need to worry about if their game
is fully deterministic.
8.2.3
Design choice
In this project, there won’t be time to actual create a game from scratch. The
thesis will consist of small tests scene with primitive objects such as spheres,
cubes and predetermined events. So the deterministic approach would at ﬁrst
glance be the best choice. However the plugin is not meant to be used in such a
small and primitive environment it is intended to be used in games. Therefore
without knowing about the games the plugin will be used on, saving the states
of the game will be the safe choice to support as many game studios as possible.

10.2

Scan paths are a good way to get a visualisation of where players are looking
when playing a video game. By looking through a replay of a session, it may be
possible to determine on your own if a player has seen objects when the scan
path overlaps on the object. However from scan paths alone you have no real
idea as to what part of the object the player was drawing their attention to.
==PAGE:60==
46
Iteration 8
One way to visualise the intensity of attention on objects is to use a heat map.
When more time is spent on a particular part that area will be highlighted with
a warmer colour. A colder colour is assigned to areas that received less attention
from the player. Each object should have their own associated heat map.
10.3
Implementation
In this implementation, heat map’s will be used to map player’s attention to
game objects. In order to map eye points onto the heat map, AOI’s will be
placed around the objects.
10.3.1
AOI
There are many eye points recorded throughout a session. The eye points that
are relevant are the ones that overlap with an object, in order to collect them
AOI’s will be placed on the objects. In Unity, you can represent AOI by using
colliders. Colliders are diﬀerent for each object, a cube, for example, have a box
collider that surrounds it. However instead of complicating things by having
many diﬀerent colliders to handle, one can be used on all objects as long as it is
made of meshes. The collider is called, "mesh collider" and it will automatically
surround the mesh object and adapts to its shape.
In order to hit the objects colliders, a ray will be shot straight into the game
world from the eye points position.
When the ray hit an object it will be
transformed to a position that is relevant to the local space of the object. Points
that are stored in the object’s local space won’t be aﬀected when the object
moves around in the world space (game scene).
10.3.2
Gaussian kernel
To create a heat map texture, the ﬁrst step will be to calculate the object’s size
in pixel measurements. The next step is to create an empty 2D texture using
the pixel measurements. The points that are stored in the object’s local space
is then mapped onto the texture. Each eye point is represented in the texture
by increasing the pixel position value with 1. If the texture was visualised now
only small dots would appear, therefore, areas should be generated around the
pixels.
==PAGE:61==
10.3 Implementation
47
Figure 10.1: Guassian 2D function used to create the kernel.
A gaussian kernel is used to create areas around the pixels.
The kernel is
calculated with following the function 10.1. The kernel is rolled over the texture
adding value to neighbouring pixels and as a result areas, are formed. The last
step is to assign one of 3 colour’s, green, yellow or red to pixels that have a value
greater than 0 and a fully transparent colour to pixels that have a value of 0.
10.3.3
Object Heatmap
When the heat map texture has been created it only needs to be applied to a
surface. A simple rectangular plane is created, it’s height and width is the same
as the object. The heat map texture is applied to the plane. To make sure the
plane always stays as an overlay on top of the object, it is set to be a child of
that object, this will automatically update the plane’s position when the object
is moving around.

Chapter 11
Conclusion
In this thesis the ﬁrst steps to ﬁnding gamers level of engagement were made
in order to help game designers ﬁnd ﬂaws in their game design. By the end
of the project, eight iterations can be presented that feature the challenges
and solutions put into making a plug-in tool that visualises the gamers eye
movement.
In the ﬁrst iteration, an examination was conducted in order to verify a possibly
marked existed in the game industry for eye-tracking.
The results from the
survey and interview indicated that a possible marked did exist for involving
eye-tracking into the development of games. Furthermore, the game studies gave
a number of ideas as to what a product utilising eye-tracking data should be
able to show. This lead to the conclusion that a plug-in tool should be created
based on the customer needs.
The second iteration functioned as a theory study, looking into what visualisa-
tion techniques and methods were available when working with eye-tracking. It
was found that implementing scan paths ﬁrst would beneﬁt future case studies
involved in ﬁnding the level of emotional engagement and cognitive load because
a large number of measurements could be extracted from ﬁxations and saccades
alone.
In the third iteration, the work on the plug-in tool was started. In the end
==PAGE:64==
50
Conclusion
of the iteration, the prototype featured a basic scan path visualisation. Tests
were performed to ﬁnd the level of emotional engagement, by ﬁnding out what
attracted the users attention in the scene. However, the visualisation displayed
all data at once, making it impossible to interpret.
The fourth iteration continued the work on visualising scan paths. By creating
a ﬁlter, the amount of data displayed could be adjusted. The results indicated
that scan paths could map to static elements, but not dynamic elements.
In the ﬁfth iteration, it was examined how scan path could map onto dynamic
elements at the right time. It was thought that the functionality of a video
player could be the solution, a quick mockup was created in order to verify
that scan path could be mapped to dynamic elements through such a system.
The results indicated that a playback system would work, however, the current
mockup featured performance and storage problems and it was therefore decided
to create a custom playback system to tackle these issues.
In the six iteration, a design approach to a playback system was found. The
system was tested at the end of the iteration, recorded sessions could now be
played back inside of Unity. However a bug was identiﬁed that could lead to
misinterpretation of the scan paths, an object would not be removed when a
state was loaded where it didn’t exist anymore, and in turn, when reviewing the
session the scan path could in some cases be interpreted to map to objects that
were not supposed to be there. The current system also didn’t have control over
the frames being displayed.
The seventh iteration, continued the work on the playback system. The bug
from the previous iteration was ﬁxed and control elements were added to the
system.
Tests were then performed so the system to be compared with the
mockup in iteration ﬁve.
The results showed that it was possible to move
forward and backwards in a session and scan paths were observed to be able
to follow dynamic objects as with the mockup in iteration ﬁve.
It was also
found that the playback system compared to the mockup saved 35 times more
in storage and did not aﬀect the performance of Unity in any noticeable sense.
The conclusion is that the visualisation of scan path can now be used to ﬁnd
where the user is looking and map it to the corresponding frame (when), and the
result can be used to determine what element is attracting the user’s attention.
In the eight and ﬁnal iteration, a new visualisation is created to ﬁgure out what
part of an object attracts the most attention. The solution is a combination of
using AOI and a heat map, attaching both to the object. When a small test
is performed the heat map is drawn as an overlay on the object displaying the
intensity of the eye points, however the visualisation looks a bit weird because
the colour areas are box shaped also a bug is found when the objects are rotated,

that time.
These iterations were built with the assumption that it is possible to decide the
level of emotional engagement through visualisations alone. It must be noted
that test in iterations was performed with 1-2 participants at the time, the
results are therefore only based on small samples. As to what can be done dif-
ferently and worked on in future iterations will be discussed in the next chapter.
