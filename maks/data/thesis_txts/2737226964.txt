Abstract
This thesis introduces a new approach to the Danish High School Timetabling Problem,
by modifying the ways event chains were considered in a previously applied model, which
changes the government of one of the most complicated parts, event chains, from hard
constraints to soft constraints. Two metaheuristics are applied, an Adaptive Large Neigh-
borhood Search (ALNS) and a Tabu Search (TS), and their performance is assessed.
Furthermore, two strategies to apply the presented heuristics to schedules with planning
horizons of up to 40 weeks are introduced, a room categorization and a warm-start ap-
proach, and their performance is assessed.
Experimental results show the effectiveness of the ALNS in placing event chains correctly,
placing on average between 10 and 20 percentage points more event chains correctly
than the TS and outperforming the TS in 12 of 13 applied instances with event chains.
Additionally, the performance of the TS in placing regular events well is shown, as it out-
performs the ALNS in 5 of 7 applied instances without event chains. The significance
of utilizing strategies when creating schedules with longer planning horizons is shown,
with the utilization of the warm-start procedure resulting in on average 10 to 15 percent-
age points more event chains correctly than otherwise, and the utilization of the room
categorization strategy showing mildly better results than otherwise.
Future research should expand on the introduced penalty structure on event chains and
further develop the strategies applied in the design of the applied heuristics.

Introduction
Today, so much of our lives happen on a schedule. We wake up at a certain time, go to
work at a certain time, eat lunch at a certain time, go home at a certain time, eat dinner
at a certain time, and go to sleep at a certain time. Scheduling the life of a single person
in isolation is not very difficult, but once the schedule has to include other people, such
as a family, the difficulty increases. Children have to be brought to school in the morning,
picked up after soccer practice, and eat dinner in time for them to get to bed at the proper
time.
Children also experience scheduling in schools. Here, they are assigned lectures at cer-
tain times throughout the school year, where a teacher will teach them different subjects.
The schedule also has to take into account the availability of teachers and rooms, both
of which can be seen as required resources for the teaching events, together with the
students themselves. The schedules for high schools in Denmark were traditionally cre-
ated manually by teachers, who would attempt to find feasible schedules by hand that
performed well by certain measures. As schools have grown larger over time, this job be-
came more and more complex, and would often require long periods of work for dedicated
employees to get done each time a new schedule was required.
Since the advent of computers, much work has gone into researching the possibility of
automated scheduling using Mixed Integer Programming (MIP) modeling and Metaheuris-
tics. Many of these techniques are used in practice, but the results vary and as more and
more requirements are placed on the schedules by schools and legislators, the work con-
tinues to develop.
1.1
Project goals
Almost all of the high schools in Denmark utilize the same piece of ERP software to
manage most aspects of the schools, including scheduling: Lectio by MaCom A/S. The
scheduling part of Lectio was created in the early 2010s and has undergone little change
since then. It consists of a specialized implementation of a Adaptive Large Neighbor-
hood Search heuristic, created as a collaboration between MaCom A/S and the Technical
University of Denmark. The heuristic is used to create schedules of 1 or 2 weeks for a
school, taking into account requirements about availability of teachers and rooms, work
limits, and much more, and is based on an extensive MIP model. The school year in Den-
mark, however, consists of a total of 40 weeks, often divided into sections of 10 and 20
weeks, over which the schedule created by the heuristic is repeated to fill the entire year.
This does however introduce complications in cases where resources are not available
at certain points throughout the school year, in which case the produced schedule has to
be corrected by hand.
The aim of this thesis is three-fold. The first goal is to investigate the applicability of two
metaheuristics to the creation of schedules. The metaheuristics will be implemented and
tested on multiple instances, and the resulting schedules will be compared.
The second goal is investigating strategies to create schedules for planning horizons of 10,
20, and 40 weeks in a feasible amount of time, as opposed to the planning horizons of 1
or 2 weeks currently used. As the size of the schedule grows, the time required to create
decent schedules grows even more. Nonetheless, longer schedules permit schools to
take more information about the school year into account when creating schedules. To

do this, the MIP model used in the implementation from MaCom A/S has to be adapted
for longer planning horizons.
The third goal is to investigate a new strategy for scheduling event chains, by using soft
constraints as opposed to hard constraints. Event chains introduce additional constraints
on the assignment of timeslots to events, which results in increased complexity in finding a
feasible schedule. Soft constraints lessen this complexity by instead penalizing schedules
in which event chains are not correctly assigned. Stating when event chains are correctly
assigned requires a definition of this, which also will be investigated.
1.2
An example
To illustrate the complexity of scheduling, a small example is introduced here. A small
high school wants to create a schedule for its three students: Alice, Bob, and Charlie.
The schedule spans two days, each with three modules. Additionally, the school offers
five different courses, in which the students can enroll: English (ENG), Physics (PHYS),
Mathematics (MATH), History (HIS), and Chemistry (CHEM), among which each of the
students has to enroll in three of the five courses. Alice is not a big fan of History and
Physics and therefore chooses to enroll in the remaining courses. Similarly, Bob does not
like the English class and the History class and enrolls in the remaining courses. Lastly,
Charlie suffers from dyscalculia, which causes him to avoid Mathematics and Physics,
and he also enrolls in the remaining courses. Therefore, the students enroll as follows:
• Alice: English, Mathematics, and Chemistry
• Bob: Physics, Mathematics, and Chemistry
• Charlie: English, History, and Chemistry
The first observation that can be made from above is that all three students have chosen
to enroll in the Chemistry class. Because of this, none of the other classes can take place
at the same time as this, since the students would not be able to take both classes at the
same time. It is also seen that Alice and Bob are both enrolled in Mathematics, which
means that neither English, Physics, and Chemistry can be planned at the same time as
Mathematics, as either Alice or Bob then would not be able to participate in both classes.
Similarly, Alice and Charlie are both enrolled in English, meaning Mathematics, Chemistry,
and History cannot be scheduled at the same time as English. However, only Charlie has
chosen to enroll in History, which means the History class can be scheduled at the same
time as either the Mathematics or Physics class since Charlie does not participate in these.

1.3
Problem specification
In figure 1.1, an example of a schedule can be seen, along with the relations between
each part of the schedule.
The central problem in the Danish High School Timetabling Problem (DHSTP) is the as-
signment of timeslots to events. The events are usually lectures, but can, in theory, be
anything that has to be scheduled, such as extra-curricular events or faculty events. Ex-
actly when these events take place has to be decided, by assigning one of the possible
timeslots. These timeslots are split over the planning horizon, divided by day. Each day
is divided into several modules, each of which takes one timeslot. The specific number
of modules per day depends on the school but is constant over the planning horizon.
Each event is a single lesson and events are therefore grouped into classes, for which all
events generally are attended by the same group of students and teachers, and where
the same subject is taught. Colloquially, the word ”classes” is typically used in Denmark
to mean a group of students who generally take the same subjects, and are at the same
grade. Here, however, the word stamclass will be used to define this constellation. Stam-
classes can therefore be thought of as a collection of classes that share most of the same
students but can have different teachers.
As mentioned, students and teachers partake in events. Since these share a lot of at-
tributes, they will often be considered as a whole, in which case the term entity will be
used. In addition to entities, rooms are also required for events. Some events may have
specific requirements for the assigned rooms, such as events in Physics classes requir-
ing specific facilities for experiments only occurring in designated Physics rooms or gym
rooms for PE classes. Generally, however, most rooms in high schools are functionally
identical, meaning most events can take place in most rooms without issues.
The last part of the schedule is the part that makes the DHSTP stand out from other
High School Timetabling Problems: event chains. These are collections of events that
are required to take place at a specific time relative to each other, and can, for example,
be used to define cross-class or cross-stamclass collaborations, or contiguous blocks of
events in the same class. In Figure 1.1, an event chain can be seen between events e1
and e2, where e2 has to take place immediately following e1. The exact constellations of
events in the event chain differ, but they are almost always events that have to take place
at the same time or immediately after each other.
1.3.1
Evaluation of schedules
As mentioned in section 1.2, not all schedules are equally good. The evaluation of a
schedule can be split into two parts: Feasibility and desirability. The feasibility of a sched-
ule considers whether the schedule can be implemented in practice, and feasibility is
therefore a requirement for any schedule. Feasibility will therefore be implemented as
a series of hard constraints. The desirability of a schedule, on the other hand, is used
to rank feasible schedules based on a series of measurements. Ideally, the most desir-
able schedule possible should be found, so any measure deployed to generate sched-
ules needs to use the desirability measure. This will be implemented as a series of soft
constraints, which penalizes undesirable aspects of the schedule. In this way, the most
desirable schedule can be found as the schedule which incurs the smallest penalty.
The exact implementation of all feasibility requirements and desirability measures will be
discussed in chapter 3, but a brief description of the reasons for including them is given
here.

Feasibility
In general, the goal is to create schedules with all events assigned a timeslot and room.
In practice, however, this may not be feasible, since it is easy to imagine an instance for
which this is impossible, and since the assignment problem may be too difficult to solve. A
solution is therefore still considered feasible even if not all events are assigned timeslots
and rooms.
Some events may be locked to certain timeslots or rooms, meaning only one specific
timeslot or room may be assigned to the event. A schedule is therefore considered feasi-
ble only if all locked events are assigned their required timeslots or rooms.
For every event with a timeslot assigned, all required entities and rooms must be available.
This means that entities may not be assigned multiple events at the same timeslot, as well
as that multiple events may not take place in the same room at the same time. Additionally,
there may be certain timeslots in which an entity or room is not available, in which no event
that requires these may be scheduled.
Usually, homework is scheduled such that students have time to finish it between events.
Since homework is generally done at home, this means that separate events belonging
to the same class have to take place on different days. Therefore, schedules are only
feasible if this is enforced for all classes. Additionally, to homogenize the workload placed
on students with schedules spanning multiple weeks, it is also required that the difference
between the maximum and minimum number of days off scheduled each week for each
teacher is at most one.
Finally, there are a few requirements imposed on teachers, such that their workload lives
up to union and legal requirements. This includes a minimum number of days over the
planning horizon with no scheduled events, as well as a limit on the number of events
scheduled on a single day.
Desirability
When it comes to the desirability of a schedule, many different aspects are considered. It
is desired that as many events as possible are assigned timeslots and rooms, such that
the schedule produced covers as many of the events as possible.

==PAGE:16==
In the same way that events for the same class are prohibited from occurring on the same
day, it is also undesirable to have them take place on consecutive days, since this leaves
little time for homework. Additionally, it is desired for events in the same class to take place
in the same room. Although the importance of this is lower today than previously, due to
the prevalence of things such as online calendars, it is still included here for completeness.
Events for classes are furthermore desired as balanced as possible for schedules over
multiple weeks since this balances out the workload for students.
For teachers, it is desirable that as few days with only one scheduled event as possible
exist. Furthermore, it is desired that they have as few days as possible with any scheduled
events, to free time for other things that need to be done. On the other hand, it is desired
that students have events scheduled every day, such that the workload is as balanced
as possible. Students should also have as few idle timeslots as possible. These are
timeslots in the middle of the day where no events are scheduled, but where events are
scheduled earlier and later in the day. Lastly, it is desired that the schedules of students
begin and end as early as possible, such that the early timeslots are utilized over the late
timeslots.
Contradictory constraints
With the requirements mentioned above, it is still possible to create schedules that contain
contradictions. For example, it is possible to lock two events to the same timeslot and
room, meaning they have to occur at the same time in the same place. In this case, since
the locks were created by the planners from the school, it is assumed that they know
something we do not. In this case, this means that the locks placed take precedence over
other constraints.
In the same way, it is also possible to create event chains that require an entity to partake
in two events at the same time. It is again assumed that the planners know something
that we do not, and the event chains are therefore planned as defined.
Generally, the responsibility for the feasibility of anything input by planners is placed on the
planners themselves, which means any feasibility requirement needs to take this into ac-
count. This also leaves a bit of autonomy and influence over the schedule to the planners,
which in many cases leads to higher satisfaction with any tools used by the planners.

2
Literature overview
Timetabling problems in many different forms have existed in many aspects of society for
many years, from railway scheduling to schedules for school children, from job shifts to
planning of sports events. In all of these applications, schedules allow for better utilization
of available resources, by attempting to optimize the use of these.
This thesis focuses on Educational Timetabling, a subsection of timetabling related to the
creation of a combined schedule for participants in educational activities, such as students
and teachers, and optionally any resources required, such as rooms or specific facilities.
2.1
Educational Timetabling
Educational Timetabling can be divided into four sub-categories:
• University Course Timetabling Problem (UCTP)
• High School Timetabling Problem (HSTP)
• Examination Timetabling Problem (ETP)
• Student Sectioning (SS)
Here, High School Timetabling Problem (HSTP) is the primary focus. It aims at assign-
ing timeslots and rooms to a series of events, such that teachers and students all can
partake in their subset of required events. The specifics of exactly which constraints are
placed upon the assignments depend heavily on the rules and regulations of which coun-
try the high school is in, which means that investigating the problem in general as well as
benchmarking is not easy. Post, Ahmadi, et al. (2012) introduced the Generalized High
School Timetabling Problem (GHSTP) format, intended to contain a common set of con-
straints and objectives from High School Timetabling Problems in different countries. In
addition, they introduced the XHSTT data format, as a way to standardize the formatting
of instances for the GHSTP.
The XHSTT format was used by The International Timetabling Competition 2011 (ITC2011)
(Post, Di Gaspero, et al., 2013), which pitted many different approaches against each
other, most notably an evolutionary algorithm (Domrös and Homberger, 2012), a combi-
nation of Simulated Annealing (SA) and Iterated Local Search (Brito et al., 2012), and an
Adaptive Large Neighborhood Search (ALNS) heuristic (Sørensen and Stidsen, 2012a),
the latter of which served as inspiration for some of the work presented here.
Kristiansen, Sørensen, and Stidsen (2015) additionally compared an exact method to the
results of the finalists of Round 2 of ITC2011. They proved the optimality of one of the
solutions found by a finalist on one dataset and managed to find a better solution than
all finalists on another instance, but in general, the heuristics presented by the ITC2011
finalists proved better than the exact methods over the same running time.
In addition to the aforementioned, other metaheuristics have been applied to the HSTP,
such as in Schaerf (1999), where a Local Search (LS) and Tabu Search (TS) method
with an adaptive relaxation component, which varied weights on infeasibilities intended
to allow for better traversal of the search space during the LS part of the heuristic, were
applied. The heuristic was applied to two real schools and a dummy school, and pro-
duced schedules with a lower objective value than the ones produced by the commercial
software used by the schools.

Colorni, Dorigo, and Maniezzo (1998) compared Genetic Algorithm, Tabu Search, and
Simulated Annealing heuristics for the HSTP, with and without extensions, for two high
schools in Milan with a handmade schedule for the first school and one made by a com-
mercial package for the second. For the first school, the Tabu Search implementation with
a temporary problem relaxation (TS-PR) performed best, followed by a Genetic Algorithm
with a Local Search extension (GA-LS), significantly outperforming the other presented
algorithms. All algorithms and extensions did however manage to beat the handmade
solution by a non-trivial margin. The same picture was seen with the other school, where
the TS-PR algorithm performed best, followed by the GA-LS.
2.2
The Danish High School Timetabling Problem
The Danish High School Timetabling Problem (DHSTP) is a version of the HSTP special-
ized to Danish High Schools and is the version investigated in this thesis. A significant
difference between the DHSTP and HSTP is the inclusion of event chains, consisting of
a subset of the events in the schedule that must be placed at specific offsets relative to
each other (Further explanation of event chains can be seen in section 3.3.8).
Sørensen and Stidsen (2012b) established the Mixed Integer Programming (MIP) model
for the DHSTP that served as a basis for this thesis. They used the commercial Gurobi MIP
solver and a ALNS heuristic to solve 100 randomly sampled instances from the database
of the commercial product Lectio. Here, the ALNS heuristic significantly outperformed the
exact methods, even when using less computation time.
The presented MIP model was passed to the solver for 7200 seconds, to produce lower
bounds and gaps for the compared instances. In many cases, no lower bound was found
using the direct MIP approach, since the solver was not able to solve the LP-relaxation
in the root node of the Branch-and-Bound tree. Additionally, this method did not produce
the best results among the presented methods for any of the instances, highlighting the
complexity of the model used.
A Two-Stage decomposition method for the DHSTP model was also introduced, aiming at
reducing symmetry in the problem by splitting up the assignment process into two stages:
assigning timeslots and assigning rooms. Since many of the rooms in a practical instance
of the DHSTP are functionally identical, spending time considering exactly which of the
identical rooms to assign to which event is deferred to the second stage, thereby reducing
the complexity of the model. The second stage only consists of assigning rooms to events,
which reduces the complexity of the model significantly, and most of the computation time
is therefore focused on solving the first stage of the decomposition.
Lastly, an ALNS heuristic was presented, which repeatedly destroys parts of a solution
and rebuilds the schedule, to methodically search the space of feasible schedules. As op-
posed to the aforementioned MIP approaches, this method does not provide any bounds,
as it does not use a MIP solver, and is furthermore not guaranteed to find the globally
best solution. However, as the DHSTP is concerned with timetabling, sufficiently good
solutions can easily be implemented in practice, eliminating the need to explore the entire
search space.
The Two-Stage decomposition strategy was shown to be better than the pure IP model
but fell short of the ALNS heuristic of Sørensen and Stidsen (2012b) on more than 75%
of the studied instances.
Since the original introduction in Sørensen and Stidsen (2012b), many advances have
been made when it comes to commercial MIP solvers. The version of Gurobi used in

Sørensen and Stidsen (2012b) has not been supported by the Gurobi developers since
November 20121, and 6 new major releases of the Gurobi software has been released
since then. Therefore, Schjørring (2023) investigated if the improvements in commercial

Additionally, Schjørring (2023) presented three new solution methods for the DHSTP: a
Randomized Fix & Optimize (RF&O) heuristic, a Local Branching (LB) heuristic, and a
Proximity Search (PS) heuristic. The RF&O heuristic functioned similar to a constructor
in the ALNS heuristic, in that it only attempts to optimize a subset of events. By repeat-
edly changing which subset of the events to optimize, the found solution ideally moves
towards an optimum. The LB heuristic subdivides the solution space into partitions which
each can be solved to optimality, thereby reducing the space of non-explored solutions.
This means that the strategy works like a Branch-and-Bound framework, in which each
branching represents exploring solutions within and outside of one of the imposed subdivi-
sions. Lastly, the PS heuristic consists of repeatedly finding the solution with the smallest
hamming distance to the current incumbent, with the additional constraint of a required
decrease in the objective function, indicated by the parameter θ. This method did require
additional steps, since the original objective function in the model by Sørensen and Stid-
sen (2012b) forced variables to take certain values, which when minimizing the hamming
distance no longer would be the case.

most best solutions with 9 and 8 out of the 20 considered instances respectively. The
remaining three instances were solved best by the exact methods presented.

Dummy rooms and timeslots
To ensure feasibility in the MIP-formulation presented in sections 3.2-3.5, it is required to
introduce the additional concepts of a dummy room and a dummy timeslot. It is seen that
it is easy to create schedules where placing all events is not feasible, such as a schedule
with only one timeslot and one room, but two events. To prevent this, a dummy timeslot,
tD, and dummy room, rD, are introduced.
If an event is assigned to either of these, it will in practice signify that the event is not
assigned a timeslot or room respectively. It is therefore necessary to omit these from
some of the constraints presented in section 3.2, since assigning multiple events to the
dummy room at the same time, as well as assigning multiple events which otherwise
cannot occur at the same time to the dummy timeslot, is now permitted.
Similarly, a specific penalty is added to events assigned to the dummy timeslot or dummy
rooms, as these again signify the events not being assigned anything which is undesirable.
This will be handled in section 3.3.

3.3
Objectives
The model aims to solve a series of issues in the planning problem, each of which is
introduced in the form of soft constraints. The use of soft constraints here instead of
hard constraints intends to ensure that feasibility of the solutions is maintained easily as
neighboring solutions are explored. A few of the penalties described below, if implemented
as hard constraints instead of soft constraints, can easily limit the feasible area so much
that even finding a feasible starting solution can become very difficult which means that the
optimization process cannot even begin. Also, most of the penalties below are penalizing
things that in reality are only undesirable, not infeasible, meaning making them illegal
through hard constraints potentially means discarding a solution that would otherwise be
perfectly fine, and where the small part of the schedule being undesirable in practice can
be overlooked without any problems.
The specific values for the coefficients used in the penalties are described in section 3.4.

3.3.8
Event chains
A specific focus is here on event chains. These describe a requirement for a series of
events to be placed at certain module number offsets relative to each other, and can
therefore be used to describe various real-world cases, e.g.
• A group of events has to be assigned the same timeslot.

events in the chain to be placed at their correct offsets, for the chain to be legally placed.

3.6
Implementation in a metaheuristic
The model in (3.56a-3.56ag) needs a bit of translation for it to be implemented in a meta-
heuristic framework, and details surrounding this are discussed here.
3.6.1
Delta calculations
A large advantage can be gained from the ability to do delta calculations in metaheuristics.
These are evaluations of the change in objective value from acting on the schedule, with-
out requiring a full reevaluation of the entire schedule, and can therefore save valuable
computation time. These can be performed on the model used here, by keeping track of
the parts of the schedule that are affected by the performed action, for simple moves of 1
event.
First, ϕα only depends on the individual event and its assigned timeslot and room, and
can therefore easily be calculated by subtracting the value of αe, r, t appropriate before
the action and adding back the new value of αe, r, t after the action.
ϕβ is more complicated, as it is concerned with the amount of idle timeslots for entities
related to the event, on the specific day(s) the action is concerned with. Therefore, these
must also be calculated. Fortunately, these values only change when actions are per-
formed on the schedule, and they can therefore be monitored when performing any action
on the schedule. Taking inspiration from the formulation in (3.21), a simple counter can
be kept for the module index of the earliest and latest event on every day for every entity,
exactly as ha,d and ha,d, as well as counters for whether an event has been assigned for
each timeslot on every day.
A new idle timeslot is then created whenever an event is removed from an entity, which
was neither the first nor last event scheduled for the entity on the given day. However,
if the event was the first event scheduled, idle timeslots can be removed if they exist
immediately after the first event, and similarly with the last event. Similarly, idle timeslots
are removed whenever an event is added to an entity, which is between the first or last
event scheduled for the entity on the given day. However, if the event added becomes the
first event scheduled, idle timeslots can be added if they exist between the newly added
event and the previous first event, and similarly with the last event.
ϕη is concerned with the entities partaking in events, and if they have exactly one event
on the relevant days. An indicator of the amount of events scheduled can therefore be
kept on each entity for each day, and updated appropriately. Then, when adding an event
to an entity, an additional penalty of ηa is incurred iff the entity previously had 0 scheduled
events on that day but removed iff the entity previously had 1 scheduled event on that
day, and when removing an event, the penalty of ηa is removed iff the entity previously
had 1 scheduled event on that day and added iff the entity previously had 2 scheduled
events on that day.
ϕγ and ϕδ are concerned with the number of days off for entities. The same indicator of
the number of events scheduled for entities as above can be used for this. When adding
an event to the schedule, a penalty of γa −δa is incurred exactly when the entity has 0
scheduled events on the day, and when removing, a penalty of δa −γa is incurred exactly
when the entity has 1 scheduled event on the day. Note that the values of γa and δa used
in (3.51,3.52) means that this can be simplified slightly.
ϕζ is related to the number of neighbor day conflicts for classes. Therefore, it is required to
keep track of whether or not the class c has any events scheduled on day d. In practise, it is
preferable to keep a counter of the events scheduled, since this can easily be incremented
and decremented when adding or removing events. With this and the parameter Pd,d′

change when rooms are changed. For this, a counter of the amount of events scheduled
in each room r for class c can be kept, and updated appropriately. Then, a penalty of ϵ is
incurred when assigning an event to a room where the class c previously had 0 scheduled
events, provided this is not the first room assigned to class c. Conversely, a penalty of ϵ is
removed when removing an event from a room where the class c previously had exactly
1 scheduled events, provided this is not the last room assigned to class c.
Lastly, ϕσ, ϕω, and ϕψ are related to the placement of events in event chains. This will be
treated in two separate cases:
When the event assigned is the root event of event chain n, en, first the penalty of σ|En|
is removed from the schedule, since now the root event is placed. As a result of this, all
non-root events in the chain can now be evaluated, meaning a penalty of ψ is incurred for
each non-root event in the chain that is assigned the dummy timeslot tD, and a penalty of
ω is incurred for each non-root event in the chain that is assigned a timeslot t′ that does
not have the correct offset from the timeslot assigned to en (i.e. mt′ ̸= mt−Men,n+Me′,n).
Conversely, when removing a root event from the schedule, the penalty of σ|En| is added
back to the schedule, since now the root event is no longer placed. As a result of this,
all non-root events in the chain can no longer be evaluated, meaning a penalty of ψ is
removed for each non-root event in the chain that is assigned the dummy timeslot tD, and
a penalty of ω is removed for each non-root event in the chain that is assigned a timeslot
t′ that does not have the correct offset from the timeslot assigned to en.
When the event assigned, e, is not the root event of the event, a penalty is only incurred if
the root-event of the chain is placed. In this case, the event will move from being assigned
the dummy timeslot tD to another timeslot t′, whereby the penalty of ψ is first removed.
Then, if the new timeslot t′ does not have the correct offset from the timeslot assigned
to en, as mentioned above, a penalty of σ is incurred. When removing such an event,
a penalty is also only removed if the root-event of the chain is placed. A penalty of ψ is
incurred, and the penalty of σ is removed iff the previously assigned timeslot did not have
the correct offset from the timeslot assigned to en.

time. In certain cases, the delta value of a move does not change between iterations, and
it can therefore be very valuable to store and re-use these. This is also possible in this

3.6.2
Constraint management
When changing around the schedule, it is here ensured that all constraints in (3.56a-
3.56ag) are kept valid, to only produce feasible schedules. Therefore, an assessment of
the feasibility of assigning a timeslot or room to an event is needed. Many of the con-
straints described are either implicitly enforced by the metaheuristics framework, such as
(3.2), or are simple checks, such as (3.9) and (3.7), but a few constraints need additional
attention.
Firstly, when assigning an event, constraints (3.11) and (3.13) are checked to ensure that
teachers have an appropriate amount of days off and that these are balanced over the
planning horizon. This is done by keeping track of the amount of modules assigned to
entities each week. Constraint (3.10) is also checked, ensuring that teachers are not
assigned too many events on the same day.
Secondly, constraint (3.14) is also checked to ensure that classes are not assigned more
than one event each day. This is done by introducing a counter to all classes, indicating
the number of events assigned to them for each day, and ensuring that this is kept at an
appropriate level.
Lastly, constraint (3.17) is checked, to ensure that there are not added too many new
neighbor day conflicts. This is done by keeping a counter of the number of neighbor day
conflicts for class c, updated appropriately as described above with ϕζ, and ensuring that
this will not exceed Nc.
When removing an event, however, only constraint (3.13) is checked, since this is both an
upper and lower bound on the number of days off. This is done in a similar way to what
is described above.

Methods
First, initialization will be discussed. When it comes to the application of MIP-solvers,
providing an initial solution as a warm start can save a lot of time spent by the solver in
the root node of the branch-and-bound tree. In the same way, providing an initial solution
when using a metaheuristic shrinks the initial neighborhoods explored, which leads to
faster iteration times. However, the quality of the initial solution can also be a hindrance
to creating good solutions. Therefore, the creation of an initial solution is discussed first.
After generating an initial solution, the metaheuristic can start improving it. Here, both
Adaptive Large Neighborhood Search (ALNS) and Tabu Search (TS) heuristics are pre-
sented and applied. For the ALNS heuristic, the used constructors and destructors are
presented, along with a discussion of the motivation for including them. For the TS, the
neighborhood used is presented, along with the tabu list used and the Intensification and
Diversification measures used.
A focus area of this report is the application of metaheuristics on schedules with a high
number of weeks. As mentioned earlier, schedules are in practice generated for around
two weeks and then duplicated over a longer planning horizon to fit the school year, but this
means that information about the availability of resources, such as teachers and rooms, in
parts of the longer planning horizon cannot be taken into account. Therefore, procedures
for generating schedules that can take this information into account are presented. This
also includes the generation of instances spanning more weeks from the used dataset
and its validity.
Finally, a method for reducing the symmetry in the assignment of rooms to events is
presented. This is done by creating room categories, where rooms with identical facilities
are grouped together. This allows for the application of a two-stage heuristic, in which first
timeslots and room categories are assigned to events, and afterward, a specific room in
the assigned room categories is assigned to the planned events.
4.1
Initialization
When assigning rooms and timeslots to the first few events during an application of a
metaheuristic, very few events will be already assigned rooms and timeslots. This means
that any given event will most likely have as many possible feasible rooms and timeslots
as they will ever have. As a direct consequence of this, when evaluating which room and
timeslot is the best for the event, there are a lot of possibilities to evaluate. Combined
with the fact that there are also a lot of events to consider, it can take a long time to find a
good candidate event to be assigned a room and timeslot. If instead a lot of events were
already assigned rooms and timeslots, the amount of feasible combinations of rooms
and timeslots shrinks drastically, meaning more candidate solutions can be explored at
the beginning of the heuristic. Additionally, a few of the constraints in the model (3.56a-
3.56ag) only are relevant when a significant number of events are placed in the schedule,
such as (3.11) and (3.17).
Therefore, initialization is performed, resulting in a naive solution. This is constructed by
assigning events the first feasible combination of timeslot and room that can be found. A
description of this can be seen in Algorithm 1.

4.2
Adaptive Large Neighborhood Search
The Adaptive Large Neighborhood Search (ALNS) metaheuristic was introduced by Røpke
and Pisinger (2006) as an extension of Large Neighborhood Search (LNS) framework by
Shaw (1998). It aims at adaptively selecting amongst a pool of destructors and construc-
tors to destroy part of a feasible solution and rebuild it respectively.
Destructors and constructors are respectively relaxations and re-optimizations of a solu-
tion. In the case of the DHSTP, the relaxation consists of removing events from the current
solution, either partially (by removing only the assigned room) or completely (by removing
the assigned room and timeslot). Afterward, the constructor performs a re-optimization
by assigning timeslots and possibly rooms to the events without these. As a result, the
application of a destructor and constructor together can be seen as exploring a neigh-
borhood around the current solution, and, consequently, the selection of a destructor and
constructor can be seen as selecting which neighborhood to explore. Many possible de-
structors and constructors can be provided at the same time, whereby the total number
of possible combinations can grow rapidly.
After applying a destructor and constructor, an evaluation of the resulting solution is re-
quired. As some combinations of destructors and constructors may be useful for certain
solutions and not for others, the chosen combination may have performed poorly, e.g. if
constraints have been broken, resulting in a setback for the optimization process. There-
fore, acceptance criteria can be formulated, and the result of the application of a destructor
and constructor can be rejected if the criteria are not met.
If the acceptance criteria are set such that only very few solutions are accepted, the op-
timization process may quickly end up in a local minimum it cannot escape. Additionally,
if constraints have been broken, the resulting solution is not feasible w.r.t. the optimiza-
tion problem, meaning the solution should not be accepted. Here, the formulation of the
destructors and constructors, introduced in sections 4.2.1 and 4.2.2, are such that all con-
straints always are satisfied, and any solution generated is feasible. As a result of this,
any solution generated is accepted here.
Furthermore, a way to adaptively select which combination to use is required. Therefore,
the destructors and constructors are each assigned a weight, which is used in the selection
process. Given weight π−

4.2.2
Constructors
The other part of the ALNS framework is the constructors. These act as a way to re-
optimize the solution, by adding back in any events removed by the destructors, as well
as any events that did not previously have a timeslot or room assigned. As with the
destructors, there are a lot of different ways to perform this re-optimization. Three different
strategies are presented here, along with two different versions of the first two.
Best insert
The first approach, best insert, consists of greedily assigning timeslots and rooms to
events in the order that leads to the largest immediate reduction in objective value. This
will continue until no events can be assigned, and will therefore only run at most one more
iteration than the number of events that do not have a timeslot or room assigned.
To do this, a measure of the increase in objective values from assigning timeslots and
rooms to events is needed. Here, ∆ϕe,r,t denotes the increase in objective value incurred
by unassigning the room and timeslot currently assigned to event e, and afterward as-
signing room r and timeslot t to event e. Since assigning a room r ̸= rD as well as the
dummy timeslot tD is not permitted, ∆ϕe,r,tD = ∞for all rooms r ∈R. Additionally, if an
event e is locked to a room r or timeslot t, i.e. LRe,r = 1 or LTe,t = 1, assigning it to any
other room or timeslot is not permitted, and therefore ∆ϕe,r′,t′ = ∞if r′ ̸= r or t′ ̸= t.

Force insert
The last approach, force insert, is inspired by the tabu-neighborhood introduced in section
4.3.1. It is significantly more computationally intensive than both best insert and regret
insert above, and aims to achieve one main goal: placement of the event chains.
During testing using only best insert and regret insert, it was noted that parts of the event
chains could occasionally not be placed into the schedule, due to other parts of the sched-
ule blocking it by being in the same timeslot. This could e.g. be caused by a student or
teacher already having another event scheduled in that timeslot, or if a class had already
been scheduled to partake in another event on the same day. In this case, it could be
preferable to remove the blocking events to insert the missing part of the event chain, due
to the large size of the penalties on these (section 3.3.8). This is only a consideration that
can be made when inserting events into the schedule through a constructor, not when
applying the destructors, but neither best insert nor regret insert has this functionality.
Therefore, force insert is an extension of best insert with the added functionality of allowing
the removal of other events to correctly place parts of an event chain. For this, a new
measure for the change in objective value when assigning timeslots and rooms is needed.
This measure must take into account moving blocking events to the dummy timeslot and
dummy room, as well as assigning the desired timeslot and room to the event if it is part
of an event chain. Otherwise, the usual measure of ∆ϕe,r,t is used.

a new value must be introduced. ∆ϕ(F) is the increase in objective value incurred by

happen if the events are part of an event chain and have to be assigned the same timeslot,
since, when removing the root-event of the event chain, the values of all other events in

4.3
Tabu Search
The Tabu Search (TS) is an older metaheuristic than ALNS, as it was introduced by Glover
(1986) and Glover (1989). It is an extension of the hill climbing method and provides
methods to escape the local minima that hill climbing methods tend to get stuck in, thereby
permitting the exploration of a larger area of the feasible space and ideally finding better
solutions. This is done by keeping a short-term memory of applied moves in a tabu list, all
of which are declared tabu, meaning they cannot be undone or repeated for a while. Many
different strategies exist as to how to keep the tabu list, each of which has its upsides and
downsides. The strategy chosen here is presented in section 4.3.2.
The most complex part of Tabu Search is picking a feasible move to perform, to move
towards a better solution. The design of the neighborhood of feasible moves to pick
from is important since, contrary to the ALNS metaheuristic, Tabu Search only considers
one neighborhood. This neighborhood therefore needs to allow for a significant amount
of good solutions to pick from, to consistently produce good results. The neighborhood
chosen here inspired the construction of force insert constructor for the ALNS heuristic
presented in section 4.2.2, and will be presented in section 4.3.1.
Since the introduction of TS, the metaheuristic has been refined with extensions such as
intensification and diversification, both of which will be presented in section 4.3.3. These
also aim at improving the results of Tabu Search, by exploring previously unexplored
solutions, and also take part in the overall strategy of Tabu Search to avoid ending in
inescapable local minima.
The version of Tabu Search applied here is inspired by Burke and Kendall (2014, p.243-
264), and the general procedure can be seen in Algorithm 13.

4.3.2
Tabu list
The tabu list is the structure in the TS framework that contains the short-term memory
of the metaheuristic. It is used to avoid repeating moves that have already been per-
formed recently, which, although they led to the current solution, will lead to the same
local minimum that a naive hill climbing method would.
This list can be created in many different ways. Hertz (1992, p. 263-264) suggests adding
the pair (e, d) to the tabu list whenever the event e is moved from a timeslot on day d to a
different timeslot, possibly on a different day. As a result of this, it would not be possible
to move e back to a timeslot on day d for as long as the pair is in the tabu list. For this
case, however, since the heuristic will be used on schedules with many days (see section
4.4), there are still many other days that the event can be moved to, so the restriction
of not allowing moves back to day d may not seem very significant. However, many of
these days may already be infeasible due to some of the constraints in the model (3.56a-
3.56ag). For example, (3.10) can block days and (3.13) can deem entire weeks illegal.
It is also possible to denote specific timeslots and rooms as tabu, which would serve as a
less strict requirement than above, or the entire event itself, which would serve as a stricter
requirement as above. When denoting timeslots and/or rooms as tabu (e.g. adding the
triplet (e, t, r) to the tabu list), the event can still be assigned other timeslots and rooms
as long as these are not also in the tabu list. If for example the event is permitted to use
many different rooms, which often will be the case in the DHSTP, the event may just be
assigned to a different room on timeslot t instead. Due to many rooms being functionally
equivalent, a long tabu list would be needed to avoid this behavior, which is not desired.
Adding only the pair (e, t) to the tabu list functions largely like adding the pair (e, d). This is
in part due to the limited number of timeslots available on day d, which often will be below
10, but also due to the other events in the schedule likely blocking one or more of the
remaining timeslots on day d, meaning the event could not be assigned to them anyway.
The last considered version of a tabu list is to add only e to the tabu list. Doing this would
mean that any movement of event e is not permitted. This behavior can be interpreted as
the heuristic being firm on its decision to assign the event e to its new timeslot and room
for as long as it remains in the tabu list. This behavior is desirable for the use case here,
and this is therefore the version of the tabu list used.
Other than selecting the type of tabu list to use, a decision also has to be made regarding
the length of the tabu list. As mentioned, the tabu list constitutes a short-term memory
of the recent moves performed by the heuristic, and the length is therefore the duration
of this short-term memory. A natural upper-limit on the number of elements that can fit in
the tabu list exists, since there exist a finite number of moves that can be inserted into the
list. If at any point all possible moves are in the list, no feasible moves can possibly exist,
and the heuristic halts. Since the tabu list used here contains events, the feasible values
for the length of the tabu list is [1, |E| −1].
Since the TS heuristic is going to be applied to schedules of varying number of events,
the best length of the tabu list might be different from case to case. In an attempt to
accommodate the varying sizes, the length of the tabu list is here chosen to be proportional
to the number of events in the schedule. In order to balance between allowing good
moves and the heuristic being firm on its decision to assign events to their new timeslots
and rooms, the tabu list length |T| is here chosen to be

4.3.3
Intensification and Diversification
In Burke and Kendall (2014, p.252-253), two extensions of the TS heuristic that can aid in
solving difficult problems are mentioned: Intensification and Diversification. Both of these
measures are applied in the TS heuristic used here, alternating between each whenever
one is applied.
Various strategies exist as to when to apply the Intensification and Diversification exten-
sions. The overall idea of both is to perform a series of actions aiming at getting the
current solution away from an area of the solution space where no better solutions are
currently being found. Therefore, they can e.g. be applied when parts of the solution have
not changed in a while. This would be an indication of the heuristic being stuck in an area
around a local minimum, and it could therefore be useful to allow a large re-evaluation of
that part of the schedule. Similarly, the extensions can also be applied whenever parts of
the solution have not been used in a while. This will again force the re-evaluation of the
general sentiment of not applying these parts of the solution.
Here, the extensions are applied whenever n iterations have passed without a improve-
ment of the best solution found since last applying Intensification or Diversification being
found. This would indicate that the current area around the does not yield any better so-
lutions given the current tabu list and that something should be changed to avoid getting
stuck. The value of n should furthermore depend on the number of elements in the tabu
list, as the tabu list should be run through a few times to ensure that no better solutions
exist close to the current solution before moving away. Here, the following value of n is
used:
n = |E|

Intensification
Application of the intensification extension aims at exploring the area around the best
found solution more thoroughly. This can therefore simply be done by reverting to the
best known solution and emptying the tabu list. This would allow any feasible move from
there, instead of only the ones that previously were not deemed tabu. Especially with a
relatively long tabu list (4.19), many feasible solutions can exist around the current best
known solution that may not have been explored.
It may additionally be chosen to freeze parts of the schedule that have not been changed in
a while. This would be done based on an assumption that these parts have not changed
because they are desirable in the current schedule, and therefore also in any related
schedules. This is however not performed here, due in part to the chosen neighborhood
allowing for removal of events to place event chains, since this could benefit from removing
even parts of the schedule that have not changed in a while.

4.4
Longer planning horizons
In Schjørring (2023), schedules of 1 or 2 weeks were created. Since the school years
are generally longer than this, the found schedules are in practice treated as templates,
which are repeated over the school year, to plan everything. This does however have a
limitation, since not all information about the school year can be taken into account. It is
for example possible that a stamclass is not available during an entire week if a class trip
is planned. In this case, they are not available for the scheduled events, but since they
are required to receive a certain amount of education, these events need to take place
at some other time instead. In this case, the infeasible parts of the schedule would need
to be changed manually, which can be time-consuming, and partially defeats the idea of
automated timetabling.
Automatically creating schedules of more than 2 weeks is certainly useful. In Denmark,
a school year consists of 40 weeks, but often this will be partitioned into sections of 10
or 20 weeks by the planners, to allow e.g. an introductory period for new students or a
special schedule in months with many extraordinary events such as class trips. Therefore,
methods to create longer schedules are introduced here.

4.4.2
Cold start and warm start
The repetition procedure can be used at any point during the metaheuristic to extend the
schedule. This therefore allows for applying a metaheuristic to create a schedule for only
a few weeks, extending it to the desired number of weeks using Algorithm 18, and then
regaining feasibility of the solution using Algorithm 19. Afterward, a metaheuristic can
be applied again to attempt to re-assign all events that have been removed during the
reparation process, to further improve the schedule. This will be referred to as the ”warm
start”-procedure.
Compared to starting initially with an empty schedule of the desired number of weeks (here
referred to as the ”cold start”-procedure), this may offer significant benefits, as the first part
of the process can be seen as initializing the large schedule with a better schedule than
what would otherwise be created by the procedure introduced in section 4.1. However, the
creation of the initial solution can take significant time, in which the cold-start procedure
can get a head start in assigning events.
Especially when creating schedules of 20 or 40 weeks, the neighborhood in TS from
section 4.3 and ALNS constructors from section 4.2.2 has to explore a significant number
of combinations of events, rooms, and timeslots to find the best combination, which in
practice will take more time than doing the same for a schedule of 2 weeks.
The impact of going with the warm-start procedure over the cold-start procedure will be
explored in section 5.3.

Results
The methods described in chapter 4 have been implemented in C# and executed using
the ASP.NET Core 3.1.401 SDK and the ASP.NET Core 3.1.7 Runtime on the DTU High
Performance Computing (HPC) server cluster. Each run of the program has been submit-
ted as separate jobs, which means that no guarantee of identical hardware is given. As a
result of this, as well as the inherent stochasticity of the heuristics used, each combination
of solution method and dataset has been run 5 times. The implementation of the methods
is all single-threaded, and only a single core was requested for each job. The possibilities
of multi-threading will be discussed in section 6.2.4.
In order to permit comparison with Sørensen and Stidsen (2012b) and Schjørring (2023),
each solution method was given 2 hours of total computation time. However, due to
the computation time of each iteration of the heuristic, most notably the Force insert-
constructor in the ALNS heuristic (section 4.2.2), some runs exceeded the allotted 2-hour
maximum run-time significantly, and have been forced to stop after running for 2.5 hours.
This will be noted when applicable.

planning horizons, it is of interest to see how this range changes throughout the runtime
of the heuristic. Figure 5.1 shows the range of the best objective values found over the
runtime of the heuristics for three chosen instances.
Two of the three presented instances contain event chains, which means their objective
values are very large in the beginning when very few event chains are placed correctly.
For these instances, it is seen that many event chains are placed within the first 20 minutes
of runtime, after which not much further progress is made. Only after significant runtime
are new better solutions found, which usually corresponds to having placed one or two
more event chains correctly. For the last instance with no event chains, it is clear that
both the ALNS and TS heuristics relatively quickly reach local minima, from which both
have a difficult time escaping. The TS manages to escape after around 30 minutes, most
likely as a result of the diversification procedure from section 4.3.3.

is exactly as expected. As the heuristics explore more and more of the solution space,
meaning they are all more and more likely to have visited the same good solutions, the
upper limit of the range is expected to go down. Similarly, as the heuristics explore more
and more of the solution space, the remaining, unexplored part of the solution space
shrinks, meaning there are fewer and fewer better solutions that remain unexplored. Also,
due to the inherent lower bounds placed on the objective value, since a global minimum

5.3.1
10 week schedules
For the 10 week planning horizons, it is already expected that the Native versions should
start to struggle to efficiently explore the feasible solution space enough to find reasonable
solutions. The same run-constellation as mentioned in section 5.2 was used, but due to
the bigger size of the schedules, the RAM required increased to 4 to 6 GB per instance.
It is noted that this amount of RAM is quite high for this size of instance, and usually
during the execution of the implemented heuristics, most of this memory would remain
unused. However, significant spikes in RAM usage would appear at seemingly arbitrary
times throughout the 2-hour run-time, potentially halting the execution if sufficient memory
was not available. This trend continued with the larger schedules in section 5.3.2 and
5.3.3, and is assumed to be caused by the C# garbage collector not running at optimal
times, or as artifacts from running the code on the DTU HPC.
In Table 5.5, a comparison of the range of objective values for the Extended and Native
versions for both the ALNS and the TS heuristic can be seen. It is evident that the Ex-
tended version generally performs better than the Native version, with the median value
being lower on all but one instance for the ALNS and all instances for the TS.
It is however also seen that the variance in the solutions produced by the Extended version
is significantly higher on many instances than the Native version. This is likely due to the
amplification of the variance experienced in the first part of the procedure, where the
heuristic is run on the original instances of 1 and 2 weeks, which then gets amplified
by the repetition procedure. The repetition procedure essentially multiplies the objective
value by the parameter k which clearly amplifies the variance as well, since most of the
objective function scales linearly with the changes made by the repetition procedure. Only

5.3.3
40 week schedules
For the largest planning horizons of 40 weeks, it is expected that viable solutions cannot
be found for a large portion of the instances before the cutoff time, again due to the space
of feasible solution space being too large. Therefore, the Extended versions should have
a significant advantage over the other versions, even with the increased variance in the
reported solutions. The same run-constellation as mentioned in section 5.2 was used, but
due to the bigger size of the schedules, the RAM required was again increased to 28GB
to 40GB per instance. It is noted that these levels of memory required are significantly
higher than what was required for the schedules of 20 weeks. As previously mentioned,
large spikes in memory usage were experienced at arbitrary times throughout the 2-hour
run-time, halting execution if not enough memory was available. Generally, significantly
less memory was required to run both heuristics.
As previously, Table 5.13 is a comparison of the range of objective values for the Extended
and Native versions for both the ALNS and the TS heuristics. Again, the Extended version
generally performs significantly better than the Native version, with the median value being
lower on all instances for the ALNS heuristic and all but one instance for the TS heuristic.
The variance in the solutions found by all versions is very high, which again can only be
attributed to variance in the initial solution created (see section 4.1) and is not a result of
the heuristics themselves, as the iteration count for all instances is low.

The range of objective values for the Extended Two-Stage and Native Two-Stage versions
can be seen in Table 5.14. Also for the longest schedules, it is evident that the Extended
Two-Stage version generally performs better than the Native Two-Stage version, with the
median value being lower on all instances for both the ALNS and TS heuristics.
It is clear from Table 5.10 that the schedules have now reached a size where there are
significant problems with exploring the neighborhood around the current solution in a rea-
sonable amount of time since upwards of half of the instances are being terminated at
the 2.5 hour cutoff time without any solutions. For the same reason, some of the reported
solutions in Table 5.9 are again just the initial solution, meaning no iterations on the 40
week schedules finished in the 2.5 hour cutoff time frame.
The expected results are again seen when comparing Tables 5.13 and 5.14, with the
Extended and Extended Two-Stage versions performing the best overall. Exactly as pre-
viously, the addition of the Two-Stage heuristic to both the Extended and Native versions
leads to worse results on a significant number of instances, again assumed to be caused
by the reduced time required to explore solution neighborhoods being outperformed by
the variance in solutions created for the warm start, due to the long iteration time.
In Tables 5.15 and 5.16, the number of correctly placed event chains for all versions can
be seen. As previously, the versions with the warm start manage to place significantly
more event chains than the cold start versions on the instances that are comparable. As
many of the instances does not return a valid solution, the average percentages reported
are not immediately comparable. Only the Extended versions of ALNS and TS can be
compared, with the ALNS performing 20 percentage points better as expected.
For a significant number of instances, the time frame of 2 hours is not nearly enough to
properly explore the solution space, so the warm-start methods are really the only ways
to generate viable solutions for this long of a planning horizon. Additionally, the Two-
Stage heuristic is outperformed by the regular Extended version in all instances where
the Two-Stage heuristic manages to output a feasible solution. This may be caused by
the relatively small time spent in the second stage of the heuristic not managing to assign
rooms to most of the events. Therefore, the resulting schedule is penalized for not having
events assigned to rooms. This may be alleviated by spending longer time in this stage,
but deciding exactly in what way to split the available time a priori is not an easy task.

6
Discussion
It is evident that the methods presented here are not wholly achieving the goal of creating
the best possible schedule. As was previously seen in Sørensen and Stidsen (2012b)
and Schjørring (2023), these instances are exceedingly hard to plan, even when using
planning horizons of 1 or 2 weeks, which again is evident here. This does however warrant
further investigation into better ways of generating feasible schedules, as the applicability
and relevance of the problem is as high as ever.
Additionally, it is clear that the challenges of creating schedules for more than 2 weeks
at a time still are significant. However, both the warm-start and Two-Stage heuristics
presented here seem to be a good starting point, succeeding in creating significantly better
schedules than immediately starting with a schedule of the required duration.
A few notes on the challenges encountered during the creation of this thesis are listed
here, together with suggestions to future research on the DHSTP.
6.1
Challenges
First, implementing the model as a metaheuristic was not trivial, with a lot of the challenge
stemming from event chains. The chains require the introduction of multiple specialized
subsets in order to allow many of the constraints in the model to correctly permit event
chains to act as intended. This results in many edge cases to be handled when evaluating
the feasibility of a move, which in turn leads to significant iteration times.
Additionally, the large number of combinations of instances and heuristics meant that
the generation of the results in chapter 5 took significant time, even with the use of the
DTU HPC cluster. Therefore, evaluating the results of changes to the heuristics on all
instances, which is needed for a complete evaluation of a variety of cases, took significant
time, reducing the speed at which a new version of the implementations was happening.
The use of unit testing for simple changes aided in this, but for more significant changes,
such as the redesign of the TS neighborhood, more time was required to see the full
impact of a change.
Furthermore, significant attention needs to be placed on the adequate design of construc-
tors for the ALNS and the neighborhood for the TS. Here it was found that allowing the
possibility for events in event chains to be inserted correctly by removing events already
in the schedule led to significantly better results than not doing this, even though it in the
short term leads to fewer events in the schedule. The idea behind this was to keep the
feasibility of the solution whilst still prioritizing the insertion of the event chains. It is pos-
sible that the further insights gained here can lead to the design of even better methods
of exploring neighborhoods around a solution.
As noted, the design of the second version of the neighborhood for the TS inspired the
”Force insert” constructor for the ALNS, resulting in the best of the three presented con-
structors. This highlights that inspiration can be drawn from other heuristics and the ap-
proaches taken by these to perform appropriately on this problem.
6.1.1
Insights gained
The timeslots and rooms were considered in the same order in every iteration when finding
the best move. This means that the schedule was generally populated with a weight on
the first considered timeslots and rooms, creating an inherently unbalanced schedule.
Mathematical Optimization of Educational Planning
81
==PAGE:92==
Instead, if the timeslots were considered in a random order, the resulting schedule may
be more balanced during the creation process, resulting in better schedules, whilst still
fulfilling the idea of exploring the neighborhood by finding the move with the currently
lowest delta value.
Furthermore, it is clear from the results in chapter 5 that the TS heuristic performs best on
instances without event chains, whereas the ALNS heuristic performs best on instances
with event chains. A combination of these may therefore be possible, in which switching
between using the two heuristics can be done by e.g. measuring the number of incorrectly
placed event chains. The possibility also exists of e.g. applying the ALNS heuristic to
generate initial solutions and applying the TS as a part of the Two-Stage heuristic.
A reason as to why the TS heuristic does not perform well on instances with many event
chains is likely the lack of a way to re-evaluate the placement of the event chain once
parts of the chain has been placed. The placement of each event in the chain does not
take into consideration if all other events in the chain can be feasibly placed correctly, and
since the chain can only be moved one event at a time, any changes to the chain are
unlikely after the initial placement. A way to overcome this could be to temporarily permit
infeasible solutions, which can be fixed later. This could be done as soft constraints,
penalizing infeasibilities if these have been encountered, or through various extensions
to the TS heuristic, such as strategic oscillation (Glover, 1977).
6.2
Possible extensions and future research
During the development of this thesis, further points of interest were identified, which may
warrant further research. These are presented here, in no particular order.
6.2.1
A note on the use of soft constraints
The use of soft constraints for the event chains here, instead of hard constraints, carries
with it some extra implications, which here are remarked upon.
First, the use of soft constraints clearly indicates that setting the event chains correctly is
not a requirement that is to be satisfied at all times. If, in reality, the event chains must be
guaranteed to be placed correctly, the use of this model may require additionally removing
incorrect event chains, in order to gain compliance with the restrictions placed. However,
strictly enforcing the correctness of the event chains with hard constraints introduces ad-
ditional complexity to the problem, which makes properly exploring neighborhoods around
a solution more difficult.
Additionally, not all event chains actually need to be placed exactly as defined. Event
chains can for example be used to define groups of elective classes, which planners find
should take place at the same time. In this case, the event chains really only define a
preference rather than a strict demand, in which case a soft constraint would be very
applicable. In this case, it could be possible that placing the events in different places in
the schedule will reduce the incurred penalties from other parts of the objective function,
such as class stability (section 3.3.6) or idle timeslots (section 3.3.2).
Furthermore, the model presented here includes the possibility of partially placing the
events in the event chains, as opposed to the model in Schjørring (2023), which only
permits all or none of the events being assigned non-dummy timeslots at the same time.
In cases with many difficult-to-place event chains, this model here may therefore succeed
in placing more events into the schedule, but depending on the use case, this may be
more or less desirable than placing the event chains correctly.
82
Mathematical Optimization of Educational Planning
==PAGE:93==
A possible extension of the model here is therefore to permit both versions of event chains,
with hard and soft constraints. This would allow planners to indicate which events need to
be placed correctly relative to each other, such as educational days, and for which events
this is only preferred. In this way, the problem would likely become simpler to solve relative
to using the model in Schjørring (2023) with only hard constraints, and at the same time
more event chains would potentially be placed correctly relative to the model presented
here.
Lastly, defining exactly which soft constraints are needed to achieve the successful place-
ment of event chains is no simple task. The model presented here includes three different
penalties, but there may be interactions between events that are not accounted for. Ad-
ditional penalties may help guide the placement in a direction that permits more event
chains to be placed correctly. This would however require more investigation into the
best order in which to assign the events in event chains.
6.2.2
Other metaheuristics
The two metaheuristics chosen here, Adaptive Large Neighborhood Search and Tabu
Search, were chosen based on an assumption that they would perform well, due in part
to their ability to escape local minima. However, many other metaheuristics exist, which
previously also have showed good results.
For example, in Colorni, Dorigo, and Maniezzo (1998), a Genetic Algorithm (GA) per-
formed second best on the considered datasets. Generally, an Evolutionary Algorithm
(EA) such as Genetic Algorithms require significant amounts of memory in order to main-
tain a large enough population for cross-over events to produce sufficient variation in the
obtained solutions, which in the case of long planning horizons may become a consider-
able problem if the intent is for the schedule-generating algorithm to be run on personal
computers. However, EAs and ALNS share the trait of potentially changing large parts of
a solution at the same time: EAs through crossover steps and ALNS through destructors.
This means that entire event chains can potentially be moved at the same time, which
can be difficult when only moving one event at a time.
Also Simulated Annealing (SA) have been applied successfully to the HSTP (Brito et al.,
2012) and other scheduling problems, such as the Medical Student Scheduling Problem
(Zanazzo et al., 2024) or the Talent Scheduling Problem (Chen et al., 2020). Therefore,
it may also be applicable to the DHSTP, despite the extra complexity introduced by event
chains. SA may however spend a long time in local minima if the probability of accepting a
solution is not sufficiently high. Therefore SAs may require problem-specific tuning, which
can make generalized software more complex.
6.2.3
Using MIP solvers with metaheuristics
Here, a pure metaheuristics approach was taken, and in Schjørring (2023) a pure MIP and
matheuristics approach was taken. There may however be an advantage to be gained
from combining the two. A problem with the MIPs were the amount of time spent in solving
the root node of the Branch-and-Bound tree. If however the MIP solver were only used
to place parts of the schedule, such as when functioning as a constructor in an ALNS
implementation, the time spent would likely be significantly shorter, and a globally optimal
solution for the small part of the schedule could be reached faster. MIP solvers could
therefore also be used to place only a few events at a time into the schedule in a TS
implementation, by fixing the values of all other variables in the schedule.
This approach is very similar to the Randomized Fix & Optimize (RF&O) heuristic pre-
sented in Schjørring (2023), but here the chosen subset of events that should be optimized
Mathematical Optimization of Educational Planning
83
==PAGE:94==
would be chosen by the metaheuristic, which means it can be more or less focused on
solving specific problems in the current schedule.
Additionally, MIP solvers can also be used in combination with the methods introduced
here as a way to generate good initial solutions for the Extended versions. Better sched-
ules being used as initial solutions with more events assigned correctly will naturally result
in better schedules over the longer planning horizons.
6.2.4
Parallel computations
As mentioned, a lot of time is spent exploring the neighborhood around the current so-
lution, due to the large amount of possible combinations of events, timeslots, and rooms
that exist. Computers having multiple cores and/or technologies such as hyper-threading
can be exploited in multiple ways in metaheuristics to speed up computation time.
For example, parallelization can be used to explore neighborhoods faster, by considering
multiple moves at the same time. This would work in the ”Best insert” and ”Regret insert”
constructors of the ALNS heuristic, due to the availability of delta calculations. However,
both the ”Force insert” constructor for the ALNS heuristic and the neighborhood chosen
for the TS heuristic do not lend themselves to parallelization. This is due to the option
for events in event chains to remove other events in the schedule, which is actually per-
formed to evaluate the move, whereby the values of other moves can change. It is noted
that certain events may be entirely independent, where no teachers, students, or classes
are shared between them, such that the possible moves for these events can be explored
at the same time. Another option would be for each thread to get its own copy of the
schedule in memory to consider, and let them consider only a specific subset of the avail-
able timeslots. After completely exploring the neighborhood, the results from different
threads can be combined in order to find the best possible move.
Parallelization could also be done by starting one instance of a heuristic in multiple threads.
Due to the inherent stochasticity of both heuristics presented here, each instance will likely
explore different areas of the solution space. This will however lead to increased mem-
ory requirements, which, especially for longer planning horizons, may be a limitation in
practice.
6.2.5
Delta calculations for complicated moves
One of the things resulting in large computation time for the ”Force insert” constructor
and exploration of the TS neighborhood is the removal of other events when considering
parts of event chains. This does not permit simple delta calculations, since the removal
of events can change the delta values of inserting different events, in case they share
students or teachers, or if the same class partakes in both events, and the evaluation of
moves was therefore done by actually performing the moves whilst keeping track of the
relevant delta values. If delta calculation of these more complicated moves were included,
not only would parallelization be easier to achieve, but also the exploration of solutions in
a single-threaded implementation could be done significantly faster.
6.2.6
Heuristic extensions
It is possible to extend the included heuristics in many different ways which likely be appli-
cable here as well. For example, amongst the finalists of ITC2011 was a combination of
Simulated Annealing and Iterated Local Search (Brito et al., 2012) which produced very
good results, but also extensions which for example briefly permit infeasible solutions in
order to overcome local minima may very well be applicable here, as the local minima for
the DHSTP can be quite difficult to escape when only considering feasible solutions.

As previously mentioned, many extensions to the considered metaheuristics that tem-
porarily permits infeasible solutions also exist, such as Strategic Oscillation for TS (Glover,
1977). This could allow for easier movement around the solution space and as a conse-
quence potentially better solutions, as opposed to the approach taken here, where the
feasibility of all produced solutions is maintained.

Conclusion
In this thesis, the model from Sørensen and Stidsen (2012b) and Schjørring (2023) has
been extended to permit variable length planning horizons. Additionally, the model has
been modified such that event chains are no longer defined by hard constraints but by soft
constraints. This also permits event chains with any offsets, as opposed to prior models
which only supported simultaneous and contiguous events. Coefficients for the penalties
used by the added soft constraints have been proposed and motivated. In addition to this,
remarks have been made on the application of metaheuristics to the presented model in
general, and notes of both advantages and disadvantages have been made.
For the revised model, two metaheuristics have been detailed and implemented, an Adap-
tive Large Neighborhood Search (ALNS) heuristic and a Tabu Search (TS) heuristic, which
have been developed with the specific problem and the goal of placing event chains in
mind. The heuristics have been tested on a dataset of 20 instances with planning horizons
of 1 or 2 weeks, with varying event- and entity counts, and containing a varying number of
event chains. For the instances without any event chains, the TS heuristic performed best
on 5 of 7 instances, whereas the ALNS heuristic performed best on 12 of 13 instances
with event chains, largely due to it correctly assigning 23.9 percentage points more of the
event chains on average. Based on this, the importance of designing heuristics that take
event chains into account has been indicated, and the added difficulty from the inclusion
of these has been noted. The results found have been compared against previous re-
search where applicable, but due to the changes made to the used model, only partial
comparison has been possible.
Furthermore, two strategies for creating schedules with planning horizons of 10, 20, and
40 weeks have been shown: Room categorization and warm-start. The room catego-
rization strategy aimed at reducing the symmetry in room assignments by deferring the
decision of which room to assign to events to a later stage. The warm-start strategy, on the
other hand, aimed at producing a better initial solution by solving a scaled-down version
of the problem. Additionally, a procedure for generating instances containing 10, 20, and
40 weeks from the initial dataset has been outlined and used to test the two strategies on
both the ALNS and TS heuristics. Also here did the ALNS heuristic perform significantly
better than the TS heuristic with regards to event chain placement. It was also seen that
the use of the warm-start strategy led to significantly better placement of event chains,
with between 10 and 20 percentage points more event chains correctly placed. The use
of the room categorization strategy also produced better results than omitting it, but not to
the same degree as the warm start, and led to not producing feasible solutions for certain
instances, due to exceeding limits on computation time.
It is noted that both introduced heuristics perform well in different situations, indicating that
better results are likely possible if learnings from both are combined. In addition to this,
potential extensions and modifications to the approaches taken here has been outlined
and motivated.
