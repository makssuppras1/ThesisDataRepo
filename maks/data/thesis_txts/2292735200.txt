Abstract
Today’s modern power electronics converge towards higher operating frequencies which allows for
smaller size, higher efﬁciency and cost reductions. Resonant power conversion has proven to be a effective
path to highly effective and small power conversion devices since the switching losses is greatly reduced
through zero voltage switching (ZVS). In this work, the Class-DE inverter topology was investigated for
varying output power applications and a 5W prototype was built. The resulting prototype showed an
efﬁciency of η = 61% and correlation between theory and reality could be seen. It was concluded that the
Class-DE is not the optimum choice for varying output power applications.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Any developed society is highly dependant on energy, and in today’s modern world, most of the energy
is delivered to the end user by the electricity grid. Power conversion remains a key connection point between
the end user and the distributor. Power conversion electronics, or power electronics in short, are an important
component in all modern consumer electronics. Within recent years, the trend in power electronics has
converged towards smaller sizes[1][2] and with an signiﬁcant increase in the number of applications[3][4],
such LED lightning, cellphones and other consumer electronics, the need for small and efﬁcient power
electronics have never been bigger. Resonant power conversion have become be a viable and very attractive
path towards these goals[5][6]. The Class-DE resonating power inveter and converter has proven to be a
very efﬁcient[7][8][9] and reliable topology for resonating power conversion. It achieves the Zero Voltage
Switching (ZVS) and Zero Derivative Switching (ZDS) of the Class-E inverter but with lower component
stresses[10] and without the need for a large input choke inductor.
The Class-DE is driven at a single optimized point but it is of interest to see if the Class-DE inverter
would be suitable for varying output power levels by means of duty cycle control or frequency control.
To a form a basis for the analysis of the topology, a brief introduction to the concept of resonating power
conversion and ZVS is given. The analysis part outlines some of the challenges and consequences when the
inverter is removed from it optimum operational point by estimation of the switch node voltage, numerical
analysis and simulations. A prototype is also implemented to ﬁnd correlation between the predictions of the
analysis and reality. Finally conclusions are drawn on the basis of the ﬁndings and a outline of future work
in the area is drawn.

1.1
Concept of resonating power inversion
Any power system should be able to transform one type of energy into another form of energy[3]. This is
true no matter if the system is a power plant, turning chemical energy into electricity, a steam engine which
turns pressure and heat into torque or a power converter turning one form of electrical energy into an other
form.

Electronic power conversion can be divided into three different applications:
• DC-AC applications, termed inverters
• AC-DC applications, termed rectiﬁers
• DC-DC applications, termed converters.
AC-DC applications may be the best known form since the power in the outlets in the average home
is delivered in AC form which then needs to be converted into DC before being utilized by the consumer.
The common example of this would be a laptop charger or similar[11]. DC-DC applications are also well
known from circuits containing batteries and other DC supplies[12]. DC-AC applications are more rare
to the average consumer, but is used in many industrial applications, such as electronic ballasts[13] or in
uninterruptible power supplies(UPS)[14].
The general approach towards power electronics and power conversion have for many years been the so
called ’hard switched switch mode power supply’(SMPS)[1]. They have proven themselves to be a reliable
and highly efﬁcient way of power conversion with efﬁciencies of 90%[15] and above. SMPS have replaced
almost all other classes of converters with some exceptions in some highly specialized niche areas. SMPS
comes in many different topologies[16], but many are deduced from the conventional Buck converter. The
conventional Buck topology is shown in ﬁgure 1.2
The operation of the the Buck converter is well documented in literature[17][18][19] but can brieﬂy be
described as a combination of a switch network and a output ﬁlter. The switch network reduces the voltage
by altering the effective ’on’ time of the active devices, Q1 and Q2, and the output ﬁlter retrieves the desired
output. The output can be a DC voltage or some form of low frequency alternating power signal. Hard
switched SMPS have several disadvangeds however. For one they are very noisy and generate a large amount
of electro magnetic interference[20][21] (EMI) due to the nature of steep turn on and turn off ﬂanks of their
switches. This puts hard requirements on the output ﬁlter to meet noise regulations[22]. The output ﬁlter
size is also determined by the frequency at which the system is operated. Increased switching speeds have

The switch network is very similar to the switch network found in the conventional SMPS. In the
resonating SMPS the output ﬁlter is replaced by a resonant tank made up of some reactive elements tuned to

Background theory
As stated in the introduction, the scope of this work is to investigate if the Class-DE inverter topology is
suited for varying output power, in other words, can something act as a lever to control the output current of
the tank?
Two approaches springs to mind:
• Changing the operational frequency fsw
• Changing the on time of each active switch.
In the optimal situation any form of control would keep the inverter operating under ZVS conditions
but many obstacles are introduced when either of the above mentioned parameters are changed. No matter
is fsw changed or D1 and D2 is changed, the inverter will leave its optimum operational point which it is
inherently designed to. However, if the condition of maintained ZVS is relaxed and some capacicative losses
are accepted, these approaches could still be used to control the output current. The next sections indulges in
describing some of the consequences for the inverter if either of the two methods are used.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusions and future work
6.1
Conclusions
The consequences of operating an Class-DE inverter outside its optimum operation point was investigated.
This was done in order to see if this speciﬁc resonating inverter topology was suitable for varying output
power. The overall conclusion is that it seems no to be the case. Several important obstacles remains:
• Great effort is needed to precisely predict the behaviour of the inverter outside its optimum point
which makes the design process tedious and time consuming.
• The practical implementation showed that the model of Vnode(t) could predict the general behaviour
of Vnode and predicted voltage overshoots and altered wave forms.
• Numerical models and simulations showed that if D1 and D2 are changed only limited output current
variations is possible. Also voltage over- and undershoot could be expected.
• The prototype also showed that stray inductance was present at Vdd and GND. This reduced dv
dt of
Vnode when operated outside the optimal point.
• The prototype showed less variations of output power as a function of variations of D than predicted
by the models.
• Variations of Vg, showed that the ZVS condition could be maintained for a wide range of Vg. Also the
efﬁciency was unchanged, suggesting the Vg could be a variable to be controlled.
• Phase shift of the output current was shown to be present both in simulations and in the prototype
when D1 and D2 was changed which signiﬁcantly affected the operation.
6.2
Future Work
A similar investigation with a rectiﬁer stage should be conducted. Different rectiﬁer stages, synchronous
or passive could be used to change the operation from DC-AC to DC-DC[16].
If the inverter is allowed to operate in Class-D mode, some form of hybrid for different power levels
could perhaps be reached.
It was suggested that varying the Class-DE inverter power is if not feasible, at least a severe challenge.
However, other methods of control could be investigate such as burst control where the inverter periodically
on and off, at a much slower speed than that of the switching frequency[49].
