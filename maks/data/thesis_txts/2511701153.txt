Abstract
The environmental issues and the ﬁnite reserves of fossil fuels are making the use of renewable
energy sources, such as wind and solar power, mandatory. Following the increasing deployment
of the aforementioned renewable energy sources, and taking the fact that wind and sun are of
intermittent nature into consideration, the demand of energy storage systems such as batteries or
supercapacitors (SC) on a big scale is increasing rapidly. Both for power supply under normal
operation and for fast power release during a fault to support system.
In this thesis, the ability to provide support under faulty conditions from a SC is investigated. The
system is comprised by a SC and a power source which both are connected to a DC-link, making the
system DC-coupled. Under normal operation the SC is idle, not charging or discharging, since the
voltage on the DC-link is stable. On the other hand, under frequency events the SC is triggered
and provides support to the system by supplying active power. To trigger the SC the DC-link
voltage has to deviate from its nominal value. A control is implemented that adjusts the DC-link
voltage according to the frequency deviation.
The performance of the SC and control in providing fast frequency support is simulated under
various frequency events. Sensitivity studies are also carried out to identify the correct parameters
of the SC and the control.
Finally, the results from the simulations and the ﬁndings are presented. The results show that the
SC model is supporting the system during frequency events, and the frequency is kept within the
permitted limits.

1.1
Background and motivation
Moving forward to the future the need to replace fossil fuel-based energy production with renewable
energy sources is becoming obligatory. The pollution produced by fossil fuel usage and the fact that
they have ﬁnite reserves pushed the EU to take measures against their use. With the renewable
energy directive [EEA, 2009] EU establishes a policy for the energy production from renewable
sources. The directive requires that by the year 2020 EU should be able to cover a 20% of its
energy needs from renewable sources and by the year 2030 the target is 32%. In order to meet
those targets the use of wind power and solar installation is increasing rapidly [BloombergNEF,
c], and by June 2018 it reached 1,000GW of installed capacity from wind and solar, as can be seen
in ﬁgure 1.1. It is projected that the capacity will reach 12TW by 2050 [BloombergNEF, b].
With the increased use of renewable sources new challenges has arisen. Prior to the integration
of renewable sources into the grid, the task of frequency regulation was provided by synchronous
generators. Nowadays when more and more renewable sources are used such as wind turbines and
photo-voltaic systems, it makes the frequency regulation task more diﬃcult to be implemented
because of the intermittent nature of wind and solar energy. In order to tackle that issue, power
converters are used as an interface between the power grid and the renewable source. Moreover,
by regulating power converters as virtual synchronous generators (VSGs), they can exhibit a sim-
ilar frequency dynamic response. However, unlike synchronous generators, power converters are
incapable of absorbing/delivering any kinetic energy, which necessitates extra energy storage sys-
tems (ESSs)[Fang et al., 2018]. The combination of more than one energy source is called Hybrid

1.2
Objective and research questions
The objective of the study is to implement a SC in the HPP, and build the appropriate control,
that can successfully provide fast frequency support to the grid during a fault or a load event. To
carry that study; DigSILENT Power Factory - a power system analysis software application for
use in analyzing generation, transmission, distribution and industrial systems, is used[DIgSILENT,
2020].
This thesis aims to answer the following question:
• How can the SC be controlled? In order to control the SC, which is connected on the DC-link,
the DC-link voltage needs to be controlled. To make that possible, the control is implemented
in the grid side converter’s Vdc −Q controller.
• Which are the correct parameters of the inverter’s control and the SC? To answer that
question sensitivity studies are conducted.
• Can the WT’s frequency control (derivative (df/dt)) be suﬃciently replaced by the SC’s
fast frequency support? To answer that question, comparisons are made between the initial
model with the WTs and the new model with the SC.

1.3 Assumptions and Limitations
3
To validate the correct control operation, simulations are carried out. The simulations involve
frequency events which can be either caused by a load increase in the system, or a load disconnec-
tion. Moreover, to ﬁnd the correct parameters of the converter’s control and the SC, sensitivity
studies are carried out with various droop values, various time constants T (support duration) of
the frequency ﬁlter, and various capacitor values in respect to the support time T.
1.3
Assumptions and Limitations
The following assumptions and limitations are taken under consideration in this project:
• Economic and market aspects are not considered.
• The HPP is represented by a single power source with the same active power output.

2.1.1
Energy storage systems
As discussed in chapter 1, the demand of stored energy on a big scale for continuous energy supply
(e.g. PV systems accompanied with storage systems in order to provide power when there is no
sun), or for system support during a fault (power needs to be injected to the power system from
a battery or a SC), is increasing [BloombergNEF, a].
==PAGE:19==
2.1 Literature review
5
Figure 2.1: Global cumulative ESS installations [BloombergNEF, a]
Electric energy can be stored in two diﬀerent ways. The ﬁrst way to store energy is indirectly (e.g.
in batteries) as potentially available chemical energy, requiring Faradaic oxidation and reduction
of the electrochemically active reagents to release charges, that can perform electrical work when
they ﬂow between two electrodes that have diﬀerent electrode potentials. The second way to store
energy is directly in an electrostatic way as negative and positive charges (e.g. on the plates of a
capacitor) [Conway, 1999].
2.1.1.1
Capacitors
Capacitors are passive components that store electrical energy in an electrostatic ﬁeld. A capacitor
is consisted of two parallel electrodes, separated by a dielectric. By applying voltage across the

2.2
Power system balance
An electric power system is a system where power is produced, transmitted and consumed in real
time. Power is intermittent and is produced according to the demand. That means that in order for
the power system to be stable, power generation and power demand should always be in balance.
A change in this balance results in frequency deviation, and if that deviation exceeds speciﬁc limits
set by the European grid code [EEA, 2017], the power systems becomes unstable.
In Europe the steady state of frequency is set to 50Hz. A frequency of 50Hz means that power
generation and demand are in balance as ﬁgure 2.7 illustrates. Imbalances between generation and
demand will result in frequency imbalances. More speciﬁcally, there is an increase in frequency
(over-frequency) when the generation is bigger than the demand, and a decrease in frequency
(under-frequency) when generation is lower than demand. Transmission system operators (TSOs)
are responsible for maintaining the frequency in its nominal value. Generation and demand balance
==PAGE:25==

than generation, or generation shedding when generation is higher than demand [Hansen et al.,

2.2.1
Frequency control
As stated before, the frequency in the power system should be maintained around 50Hz and within
the limits set by the grid codes. Despite that, deviations in frequency may occur from an imbalance
between generation and demand. The frequency in the Nordic power system can be considered
stable when the deviation does not exceed ±0.1 Hz of the nominal 50 Hz, and the system is still
under normal operation condition. However, when the deviation is outside that limit, warnings
and actions take place. In the case of a big negative deviation of the frequency due to demand
being higher than the generation, the security of the power system is threatened and it can lead to
a blackout (loads in the system are disconnected) [Hansen et al., 2016]. A large positive frequency
deviation, due to generation being higher than the demand, is less harmful to the security of the
system and can easily be regulated. The legal operational power system states used in the Nordic
system can be seen in ﬁgure 2.8, and are the three following:

2.2.2
Actions to stabilize the frequency
The process of frequency stabilization to a steady state, is carried out by the frequency containment
process (FCP), while the process of frequency restoration back to the nominal frequency (50 Hz)
is carried out by the frequency restoration process (FRP), as can be seen in ﬁgure 2.9.
• FCP is provided automatically within 30 seconds after the frequency event, from fast gen-
erating units through a frequency droop control loop. The generating units start producing
more power to stop any further frequency drop. As shown in ﬁgure 2.9 the frequency returns
to an acceptable value and is stabilized to that value (lower than 50 Hz). The system remains
in alert condition.
• FRP is a slower control loop (provided within 15 minutes after the fault) than the FCP. FRP
shares the load between generators in order to restore the frequency to its nominal value.
To do that, the setpoints of the generators are adjusted to compensate for the frequency
deviation.
FRP is either automatically activated or manually activated from the system
operators [Hansen et al., 2016].

Chapter 5
Conclusions and future work
This chapter presents the conclusions from the simulations and from the overall project. Moreover,
this chapter recommends future work that can be done to improve and expand the project.
5.1
Conclusions
The objective of this thesis was to create a model comprised by a SC and a DC-current source
(replacing the WT, PV system and batteries from the initial model) both connected on the DC-link.
The model incorporates the necessary control for the SC. As the SC is a passive component
and therefore cannot be directly controlled, the control was implemented in the GrSC’s Vdc −Q
controller. The control was realized under the dv/dt principle. When the rate of change of voltage
is not equal to zero on the DC-link, the SC charges or discharges.
With that control and the physical properties of the SC, especially the capability of the SC to
discharge extremely fast, it was able to provide a qualitative fast frequency support to the system.
The SCs ability to provide fast frequency support has been tested under various parameters and
under various frequency events.
Parameters that has been tested:
• Droop values: 2%, 3% and 4%
• Support duration T: 10s, 20s and 30s
• Diﬀerent capacitance values depending on the support time T: 857 F, 1714 F and 2572 F
==PAGE:66==
52
Conclusions and future work
Load events that has been tested:
• Load increase from 150 MW to 225 MW resulting in a frequency drop
• Load increase to 225 MW and then back to its nominal value
• Load increase from 150 MW to 300 MW resulting in a frequency drop
• Load decrease by 50 MW resulting in a frequency rise
In the ﬁrst case with the load increase, the biggest improvement of the frequency’s nadir was
realized when the droop setting was at 2%. Combining the 2% droop with a support time T of
10 seconds, the frequency’s nadir was improved by 0.080% or by 0.04 Hz, compared to the base
without the SC. The choice of T=10s is because with T set to 10 seconds, there is no double dip
in the frequency as it happens with T set to 20 or 30 seconds. More importantly, the frequency’s
nadir is improved by 0.012 Hz compared to the simulation with T=30s.
The second case when the load initially increased, and then returned to its nominal value, was
carried out to test if the SC would be stable in that transition. The SC was stable through the
events, and provided frequency support for both under-frequency and over-frequency.
The third case which represents the rather extreme scenario that the load increases by 100%
to reach 300 MW from 150 MW. The SC supported the system by supplying 50.150 MW. The
frequency’s nadir improved by 0.17% or 0.085 Hz when the SC was connected and the control was
active.
The ﬁnal case with the load decrease was carried out in order to test the SC’s capabilities on
absorbing excess power when a load is disconnected. As expected the SC acted as a load and
absorbed the excess active power. The improvement of the frequency peak when using the SC
compared to when the SC is not installed, is 0.061% or 0.031 Hz.
Finally, the initial model was compared with the SC model. The SC model proved to be better
when it comes to fast frequency support in both under-frequency and over-frequency, by improving
the frequency’s nadir by 0.072% or by 0.036 Hz, and by improving the frequency’s peak by 0.053%
or by 0.027% respectively.
To conclude, the use of a SC for fast frequency support was proved to be very eﬃcient because of its
ability to release and absorb big amounts of energy in a very short time. Moreover, studies support
that its relatively high maximum recharging cycle life time allows it to be used for long-lifetime
applications [Kim and Chou, 2015].

A.1.1
Hybrid unit (DC coupled paper pdf)
The wind turbines (WTs), pv panels (PVs) and battery energy storage system (BESS) need to be
connected. There are two ways of connecting them, AC-coupling and DC-coupling. In AC-coupling,
assets are grouped by type (eg. all wind turbines connected as a group) and then the groups are
connected to the external grid through a transformer.
WTs are connected to the transformer
through a full-scale back-to-back converter, the PVs through a DC-DC converter and an inverter
and ﬁnally the BESS through an inverter. In DC-coupling the HPP consists of hybrid units. Each
hybrid unit consists of a WT, a PV system and a BESS. The assets are connected to the DC-link
and then through an inverter to the grid. In order to connect the WTs to the DC-link a rectiﬁer is
needed since WTs are AC units. The PVs and the BESS are connected to the DC-link through a
DC-DC converter. In this model the DC-coupling is used since there less components are needed
for the connection of the assets. The topology of DC-coupling can be seen on Figure A.1.

As illustrated in Figure A.2 the conﬁguration of the wind turbine consists of the wind turbine’s
mechanical level (aerodynamics, drivetrain and pitch angle control) and the wind turbine’s electrical
level (multi pole PMSG and full-scale frequency converter with its control). In this conﬁguration,
the generator is connected to the grid through a full-scale frequency converter system. The full-
scale frequency converter controls the speed of the generator and the power ﬂow to the grid. It
consists of a back-to-back voltage source converter (generator side converter and grid side converter
through the DC-link) which is controlled by insulated-gate bipolar transistor (IGBT) switches. The
converter keeps the PMSG’s terminal voltage to a desired voltage and adjusts its electric frequency
to the optimized mechanical frequency of the rotor and independently of the electric frequency and
the voltage of the AC grid since they are decoupled as previously stated. The permanent magnets
are mounted on the rotor and they provide a ﬁxed excitation to the generator. The power produced
by the generator is sent though the stator windings into the full-scale frequency converter which
in turn converts the varying generator frequency to the constant grid frequency.
Control system The control system consists of two controllers. The wind turbine controller (pitch
control) and the frequency converter controller.
Both controllers are using information of the
generator speed. The frequency converter is controlling the power ﬂow to the grid according to an
optimal power characteristic. The pitch controller controls the speed also but its only enabled at
high wind speeds. The PMSG can send active power to the gird but not reactive power because
it is connected through the back-to-back converter. The grid-side converter can be set to control
the reactive power and voltage of the grid because its electric frequency and voltage are ﬁxed to
the grid. The WT’s characteristics can be seen in table A.4.

A.1.3
DC-link
The DC-link is the bus that all the assets are connected (PV, BESS, WT). The LV terminal, AC
side, has a nominal voltage of 3.3kV so in order to enable active power transfer from one side
to the other the DC-link needs to be at a higher level. The nominal voltage of the DC-link is
5.4kV and it must remain constant. A shunt ﬁlter is connected on the DC-link. Voltage ripples
are created on the DC-link because during the switching frequency of the converter the shunt’s
current is intermittent. That voltage ripple must contained so the voltage across the DC-link is at
its nominal value.

A.1.7
Grid-side Converter
The grid-side converter (GrSC) is responsible for controlling the DC-link voltage and the reactive
power.
It is connected between the DC-link and the LV AC terminal and in addition to the
aforementioned controls the GrSC converts the DC voltage to AC voltage. As previously discussed,
the generator is fully decoupled from the power system so the GrSC is the only element that controls
the reactive power exchange with the grid. As can be seen in Figure A.11 the two controls are
realized by the GrSC independently. The voltage across the DC-link (Vdc) is controlled by the
V dc −Q controller by receiving the reference voltage V dcref from the WT’s damping system.
For the Q-control, the Qref signal is created according to the voltage on the LV terminal. By
comparing the LV terminal voltage with the LV nominal voltage the Qref is generated and when
a voltage diﬀerence occurs the reactive power corrects the voltage. The phase-locked loop (PLL)
block measures the voltage phase and frequency on the LV terminal and generates the cosref and
sinref signals.
