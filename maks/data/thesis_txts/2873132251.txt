Abstract
This thesis explores the verification and validation (V&V) of robotic arm simulation mod-
els using the Omniverse Isaac Sim platform. The objective is to develop a real-time V&V
framework based on an assembly task, ensuring that the simulation model not only ac-
curately reflects real-world behavior but also dynamically adapts to changing conditions.
A digital twin of the robotic arm is first constructed within Isaac Sim and connected to the
physical system, enabling real-time data exchange and facilitating an efficient V&V pro-
cess. The thesis collaborates with Novo Nordisk to implement robotic collaboration tasks
within the medical field, highlighting the practical application of the framework. The result
is a digital twin model that serves as a central component of the V&V process, enabling
automated, data-driven validation across diverse scenarios and significantly reducing the
reliance on manual inspection.

Introduction
1.1
Background
As Industry 4.0 continues to develop, digital twin (DT) technology has become an impor-
tant part of smart manufacturing. It connects physical systems with their virtual models,
making it possible to monitor equipment in real time, simulate production processes, and
predict potential failures. This helps reduce errors, lower downtime, and improve sys-
tem stability and efficiency [1, 2]. DT systems also use data and simulations to support
intelligent control and predict the behavior of complex production systems. This can im-
prove automation and help people make better decisions [3]. DT is used in many areas
such as product design, production planning, manufacturing, and maintenance, helping
to improve both quality and productivity.
Meanwhile, verification and validation (V&V) methods are important to assess the correct-
ness and reliability of cyber-physical production systems (CPPS) and numerical models.
CPPS refer to systems of systems that integrate physical components such as sensors
and actuators with digital control, autonomous decision-making, and real-time data con-
nectivity [4]. A robotic assembly system can be considered a CPPS when it combines
mechanical execution with digital coordination and feedback. In addition, V&V offers a
necessary assurance framework for building high-quality and reliable digital twin models.
Recent research has emphasized that it not only ensures the internal consistency and
accuracy of models but also plays a central role in establishing trust in digital twin appli-
cations within real production, especially in complex industrial environments [5]. Further-
more, the use of V&V as a continuous runtime process rather than a one-time evaluation
has been highlighted as essential for enabling adaptive and self-updating digital twins in
dynamic production settings [6].
At Novo Nordisk, digital twin technology is becoming increasingly important for improving
production speed and reusability. In clinical lines, DT combined with robotic systems
shows a great potential due to their flexibility, allowing fast transfer of simulation feedback
to new products. The assembly of products like the FlexPen [7] typically involves robotic
arms for precise alignment, part insertion, and assembly inspection. To ensure that these
robotic systems operate safely and efficiently as intended purpose, the digital twin model
could be fully validated before real production. Therefore, a V&V approach could be
established for these robotic systems to improve production stability and responsiveness
to change.
Similar strategies have been proposed and implemented in robotic assembly cells using
digital twin models to reproduce and monitor assembly operations in real time. For ex-
ample, this study [8] proposed a digital twin model of a robotic assembly cell that uses a

standardized communication protocol to synchronize virtual and physical systems. This
approach allows the virtual model to accurately reflect the operational state of the robotic
system, including kinematic behaviour, part handling and environmental interactions, in
order to support the verification, validation and optimisation of operations. In this thesis,
another communication methodology, Real-Time Data Exchange (RTDE), will be intro-

1.2
Research problem statement
In current industrial production, the ability to monitor task execution in real time has be-
come increasingly important. At the same time, building a reliable digital twin model that
truly reflects the real system is still a difficult task, as it requires precise modeling based
on real-world setups. In addition, to ensure that the digital twin behaves as expected, the
model must also be verified and validated. This involves not only checking its functionality
but also comparing it with real system during operation, as the data must be collected,
processed, and analyzed in real time. Therefore, there remains a lack of practical meth-
ods for runtime modeling and validation using digital twins, especially in monitoring model
deviations and validating safety constraints.
1.3
Research objectives
The objective of this study is to explore the verification and validation of robotic arm simu-
lation models on the Omniverse Isaac Sim platform. The aim is to achieve real-time V&V
for these simulation models in assembly process, ensuring they can accurately match
real-world behaviors and adjust over time as conditions change.
1.4
Research scope
In the early stages of the thesis, it focuses on reviewing literature related to verification
and validation frameworks for real-time systems to identify an approach suitable for the
chosen robotic platform. Subsequently, the robotic platform is extended to include both a
digital twin model and a real-world implementation, with data collected from both sources.
Verification ensures the technical correctness of the model during its initial development
through code reviews, structural checks, and data validation. Offline validation involves
recording data from a completed robotic process and determining methods to compare
and validate the data streams from the digital twin and real-world models. Subsequently,
online validation is conducted following CPPS frameworks, incorporating policies i.e., On-
Demand Validation, Periodic Validation, and Continuous Validation [9]. This V&V frame-
work aims to enable Isaac Sim to deliver reliable and adaptable robotic arm simulations
that accurately respond to real-time changes, ensuring both initial accuracy and long-term
consistency of the model for effective use in CPPS environments.

2
Literature Review
Digital Twin has become a powerful concept in modern manufacturing, allowing virtual
replication of physical systems for simulation, monitoring and optimization. Particularly in
cyber-physical production systems (CPPS) and robotic assembly lines, DT provides an
invaluable basis for improved decision-making, flexibility and productivity. The following
sections examine the current trends in robot-assisted production and the application of
digital twins. Both offline and online validation strategies are employed in this thesis,
along with a review of applicable methods.
2.1
Digital twin in robotic systems
Digital twin (DT) are increasingly being used to improve the accuracy, flexibility and effi-
ciency of robotic systems and assembly processes. By creating digital models of robots
and workflows, DT realizes simulation in verification of collision risk, process feasibil-
ity and path optimisation prior to actual execution. The concept of the digital twin was
initially articulated by Michael Grieves in the early 2000s as part of research on product
lifecycle management, which emphasizes a persistent digital model that reflects the evolv-
ing state of a physical system [10]. This idea was later refined and formally introduced
through collaboration with NASA around 2010, establishing the foundation for digital twin
as a key technology for monitoring, analysis, and predictive maintenance in complex en-
gineering systems [11]. As shown in Figure 2.1, it shows the history of digital twin. In
accordance with VDMA 24582, DT in Robotics supports comprehensive virtual testing of
assembly operations, enabling engineers to validate robot programs, optimise paths and
identify potential problems early in the integration process [12]. This capability not only
reduces commissioning time, but also improves production reliability by ensuring that the
behaviour of the physical system matches the digital model throughout the assembly life-
cycle. In this thesis, a robotic arm model is constructed to facilitate testing of the assembly
process.
The study [14] proposed a digital twin model for robot programming that integrates virtual
reality (VR) to enable precise online or remote control of robotic cells. By creating a 3D
virtual environment with a gaming engine, the model established two-way communication
between the digital and physical robots, allowing programs to be adjusted without inter-
rupting physical operations. The system, demonstrated on a FANUC M-10iA/12 robot,
achieved approximately 40 milliseconds of latency and a mean joint error of 0.28°, sug-
gesting it is well-suited for industrial applications.
In addition to this, there are also applications of digital twin in the field of mobile robotics. In
their study [15], the authors described a system in which material flow simulation not only
managed the Automated Guided Vehicles (AGVs), but also interacted with the digital twin

of the manufacturing cells to determine the precise processing times. In addition, they
visualized the entire process and establish communication via MQTT to ensure quasi-

These recent studies [16, 2] have applied digital twin to robotic systems and assembly pro-

highlighting how digital twin can improve production scheduling, enable adaptive control,
and increase overall assembly system efficiency.
These findings collectively demonstrate the feasibility of applying digital twin technology
to robotic arms, providing a solid theoretical foundation for constructing the digital twin
scenario proposed in this thesis.
2.2
Verification and validation methods for digital twin
Verification and validation is a key process for ensuring the accuracy, reliability and trust-
worthiness of digital twin models. In his seminal work, Sargent [18] outlined a compre-
hensive framework for simulation model verification and validation that remains relevant
to modern applications of digital twin. His approach integrates multiple levels of assess-
ment including data validation, conceptual model validation, computer model validation
and functional validation into model development. Verification and validation are espe-
cially important for digital twin, as they work closely with real-time data and help make
Verification and Validation for Digital Twin Models of Robotic Systems
5
==PAGE:13==
decisions on the spot. As Sargent observed, the validity of a model depends on the spe-
cific context. For digital twin in dynamic domains such as robotics, continuous verification
and validation is crucial. His approach provides a solid foundation for the credibility of the
model and the theoretical analysis for this thesis.
Figure 2.2: Internet of Things(IoT) framework for digital twin [19].
Furthermore, Lugaresi [6] proposed an online verification framework for assessing the
accuracy of digital twin in manufacturing systems in real time. This framework enables
real-time verification of digital twin in manufacturing by continuously comparing sensor
data from the physical system with the digital twin’s output. It assesses both time and
state deviations, checks whether they exceed defined thresholds, and uses feedback
to trigger model updates or issue warnings. This offers a useful direction for our work,
helping the digital twin remain aligned with the physical system during operation, thereby
enhancing its robustness.
To make digital twin work reliably in real industrial settings, it’s also important to under-
stand the standards used in real-world production. As shown in Figure 2.2, the ISO 23247
standard [19] defines a digital twin framework for manufacturing that links observable el-
ements (e.g., equipment, process) to their digital counterparts via device communica-
tion. It supports applications such as real-time control and predictive maintenance. V&V
is essential to ensure behavioral consistency, correct data mapping, and real-time re-
sponsiveness across the system lifecycle. Meanwhile, the National Institute of Standards
and Technology (NIST) has proposed a multi-layered data synchronisation and validation
framework for industrial robotic cells, aiming to standardise the way in which digital twin
match physical systems at both the structural and runtime levels [20]. These initiatives re-
flect a shift from practices to standardised, traceable and transparent V&V mechanisms,

laying the foundation for trusted, scalable and interoperable digital twin deployments.
Liu et al. proposed a model-based framework for constructing digital twin of shop floors,
combining the MagicGrid modeling method and the V-model of systems engineering [21].
Using a real NC machining shop floor as a case, they demonstrated that rigorous sys-
tem verification improves DT accuracy and integration. Sadeghi Tabar et al. proposed
a digital twin-based approach for sheet-metal assembly, which integrates part geometry
deviations and clamping force feedback to dynamically optimize the clamping sequence.
Their method significantly reduced geometric deviation and improved overall assembly
quality [22].
These studies [23, 24, 25] used case-based experiments as a means of verification to cali-
brate and validate digital twin systems, demonstrating how observed system performance
and behavior in different operational scenarios can effectively align the digital models with
their physical process. Moreover, several studies [26, 27, 22] validated the impact of digi-
tal twin on system performance, with case studies often involving real-world testing of DT
implementations to demonstrate their operational capability and ensure that the digital
models accurately reflect the behavior of the physical systems under practical conditions.
While digital twin applications in robotics are gaining attention, there is still limited research
specifically addressing how verification and validation can be integrated into DT models
for robotic arms. This thesis builds on that gap by exploring how V&V can be practically
applied within a DT-based setup for robot-assisted assembly.
2.3
Online and offline validation approaches
This section introduces the difference between offline and online validation and their im-
pact on the validation results. This study [9] presents offline and online validation meth-
ods within the proposed framework. In this framework, the model is initially run in offline
mode to collect historical data and validate the accuracy of the model prior to deployment.
The validated model is then used for online validation, where real-time data streams are
continuously analysed during the run. Three modes of validation are also mentioned,
continuous validation, periodic validation and on demand validation. Based on the online
validation results, if the model is considered valid, it continues to support subsequent de-
cisions; otherwise, the system triggers a recalibration of the model to restore accuracy
and reliability.
Traditionally, offline verification has been a key method of ensuring the correctness of dig-
ital twin models during the design and debugging phases. Through the use of historical
data and virtual simulation, offline verification can identify potential problems before the
system is deployed into a production environment. Makris et al. proposed a virtual com-
missioning approach for collaborative robot assembly that focuses on offline validation
of robot programs and system behaviors prior to implementation in the actual workplace
[28]. They emphasized that virtual commissioning cannot support real-time monitoring
Verification and Validation for Digital Twin Models of Robotic Systems
7
==PAGE:15==
and adaptive tuning in dynamic manufacturing, as it can only be performed in the robot
before commissioning and cannot provide validation during the manufacturing phase.
Negri et al. highlight that offline digital twin plays an important role in the design and inte-
gration phases of CPS-based manufacturing systems as they enable virtual deployment
and testing of system behaviour in different scenarios [29]. They used offline simulation
of cooperative robots in digital twin to detect potential deadlocks before deploying them in
assembly line layout design, virtual deployment of robot cells, simulation of path planning
and collision avoidance techniques, and performance testing of conveyor belt systems.
This example demonstrates the practical value of offline validation in the planning and
implementation phases, and highlights the need to update the digital twin during produc-
tion to maintain accuracy. However, it also highlights the fact that offline validation alone
cannot guarantee the accuracy of the model during operation, and therefore needs to be
continuously updated during system operation.
In order to make digital twin models remain accurate during operation, recent research has
increasingly focused on online validation methods. These methods continuously assess
the consistency between digital twin predictions and real-time system data, thus enabling
dynamic updates to maintain model reliability. Lugaresi et al. pointed out that most exist-
ing digital twin validation approaches rely on offline methods executed during the design
phase, which cannot guarantee consistent model accuracy [6]. They proposed an on-
line validation framework that compares real-time sensor data with digital twin outputs
to dynamically assess model validity at runtime. The developed digital twin-based work-
shop provided real-time reconstruction of the scene without the need for time-consuming
iterative algorithms.
In the study [30], online validation has been achieved by continuously comparing real-
time shop floor data streams with digital twin predictions. When the discrepancy exceeds
a threshold, the framework triggers model recalibration or rescheduling, ensuring that the
digital twin maintains accuracy and supports scheduling decisions under dynamic condi-
tions. For example, in the event of a sudden equipment failure, online validation quickly
identifies the difference between the DT state and the physical state, allowing for rapid
revision of the scheduling plan.
Both online and offline methods are used in this thesis to support the validation of the
digital twin setup.
2.4
Summary
This chapter reviewed three key aspects related to the verification and validation of digital
twin for robotic systems:
Firstly, verification and validation methods provide a theoretical basis for the accuracy and
reliability of digital twin, goffering a clear way to assess the performance of digital twin.

==PAGE:16==
Secondly, although digital twin has been applied in robotic systems and assembly pro-
cesses, such practical cases remain relatively limited. Nonetheless, they provide useful
references for building a research platform in this thesis.
Thirdly, online and offline validation methods are essential for digital twins, and prior eval-
uation approaches offer guidance for subsequent analyses. These aspects together form
a foundation for designing suitable validation schemes.
Overall, these insights show that an effective verification and validation framework needs
to combine suitable methods, take practical application requirements into account, and
choose between online or offline updates based on system constraints. This comprehen-
sive understanding can help guide the design of the methodology presented in the next
chapter.

Methodology
3.1
Digital twin models for simulation
In this work, the digital twin is implemented as a physical simulation of the UR5e robotic
arm developed in Isaac Sim. The virtual model is intended to reflect the kinematics and
dynamic behavior of the real robot, enabling simultaneous testing, control validation and
planning at the task level. The gripper attached to the UR5e is also modeled in the sim-
ulation so that detailed interaction tasks such as gripping and inserting objects can be
performed. Beyond this specific implementation, digital twin not only provides a basis
for modeling system behavior, but also for monitoring, testing and optimizing real-world
performance throughout the product lifecycle.

3.1.1
Digital twin framework
The digital twin system developed in this thesis consists of a virtual model implemented
in Isaac Sim, representing a real UR5e robotic arm. The framework allows for consistent
and measurable comparison between the physical and digital systems.
The physical robot operates under joint space control with a parallel gripper. It receives
target joint positions and provides real-time feedback, including joint angles and tool cen-
ter point (TCP) positions. These data are recorded for validation. The setup also includes
assembly components such as FlexPen and fixture.
Verification and Validation for Digital Twin Models of Robotic Systems

==PAGE:18==
The virtual system is based on the URDF description of the same robot and is implemented
in Isaac Sim. Motion is controlled using joint dynamics and joint-level commands via an
action graph. The gripper is also included to simulate interaction and manipulation.
To ensure structural and functional consistency, both systems are configured to follow the
same targets. TCP positions are computed in both environments using a shared world
coordinate system. Consistent transformation frames, such as between wrist_3_link
and the TCP, are defined to maintain alignment.
3.1.2
Physics simulation
The virtual system in the digital twin is created in NVIDIA Isaac Sim, which is a refer-
ence application built on NVIDIA Omniverse™that enables developers to simulate and
test robotics solutions in physically based virtual environments. The simulation design
matches the mechanics and interaction behavior of the real UR5e system and can be
verified at the trajectory.
The virtual robotic system is developed in Isaac Sim using a modular and physics-based
modeling approach. The core manipulator is a UR5e robot instantiated directly from a
URDF file containing kinematic and dynamic specifications including joint constraints, link
geometry and inertial properties. A parallel gripper is manually attached to the wrist of
the end-effector using a fixed transformation. In addition, a fixture and a target object
(FlexPen) are added into the scene to support contact-based manipulation and insertion
tasks.

Simulation timestep refers to the timestep used in the simulation environment, where 1/60
seconds means the simulation runs at an update frequency of 60Hz. Typically, this is set
to 60Hz as a standard for real-time simulations. However, in certain specific scenarios, a
higher timestep may be used to enhance the precision of physical actions in the simulation
as discussed in the Issac Sim forum [31]. For example, when using a finger to handle high-

precision parts, simulation step needs to be set to 150Hz, 300Hz, or even higher. This
adjustment is also applied in our assembly simulation process.
Static friction defines how strongly two objects resist sliding at the moment they come
into contact, which is critical for maintaining stable grip during the initial grasp. Dynamic
friction comes into play once sliding begins and helps ensure smooth movement during
processes like insertion. In this assembly task involving the target object (FlexPen), dif-
ferent components are assigned varying friction values. The static friction values range
from 0.2 to 0.4, while dynamic friction values fall between 0.1 and 0.3. These parameters
play an essential role in achieving realistic and stable behavior throughout the simulation.
Also, the parameter offset should also be considered. Contact offset defines the ”thresh-
old distance” for collision detection between two objects. When the distance between
the two objects is smaller than the contact offset, the physics engine considers them to
be in contact and begins generating collision responses. A larger contact offset means
that objects will be detected as in contact from a farther distance, potentially resulting in
smoother or more forgiving collision detection. In simulations, a lower contact offset is
often used to reduce unnecessary false collisions. Rest Offset describes the degree of
overlap depth when objects come into contact and remain stationary. Rest offset is typi-
cally set to a negative value or zero, indicating whether objects ”embed” into one another
after contact. In this thesis, for gripper-based tasks or insertion processes, rest offset is
set to zero to achieve higher stability. The explanation could be seen as Figure 3.2.

3.1.3
Software control and kinematics method
The robot’s actions are executed using Isaac Sims’ Action Graph framework, a block-
based visual programming environment based on OmniGraph. In this action graph, the

3.2
Verification and validation
In this project, the modeling process described by Sargent [18] is uesd as a reference
framework for building and analyzing our system. This framework breaks the model de-
velopment into three main parts: the real system, the conceptual model, and the simula-
tion model. A simplified version of this process is implemented to better fit the scope and
objectives of the system.
The real system refers to the actual physical setup, including both the robotic system
and its environment. The hardware, control systems and the surrounding conditions are
considered into verification and validation process. Based on that, a conceptual model
is built and serves as a logical or mathematical abstraction capturing the key behaviors
and structures relevant to objectives. This conceptual model is then translated into a
simulation model, which is implemented within a computational environment and used to
emulate the behavior of the real system under various conditions. Through this simulation,
it can conduct controlled experiments, explore the system’s response to various inputs,
and prepare for the final comparison between simulated and real-world performance.
This modeling structure not only clarifies the roles of different model representations, but
also establishes a clear path for systematically analyzing their alignment. In the following
sections, each model is built in sequence, beginning with the real system and progressing
through the conceptual and simulation stages.
3.2.1
Verification Methodology
Verification is defined as the process of ensuring that a simulation model has been im-
plemented correctly according to its conceptual specification, and that the internal logic,
computational structure, and software code faithfully reproduce the intended behavior[18,
35]. It does not address whether the model reflects reality, but instead confirms that the
model performs as designed. According to ISO/IEC 24765 [36], verification activities are
intended to demonstrate that all requirements have been implemented without logical,
syntactic, or computational errors.
The URDF model is reviewed to confirm that the joint structure, coordinate frames, and link
configurations are consistent with the actual UR5e robot. The kinematic calculations in the
simulation are tested using known joint values to verify that the computed TCP positions
are consistent with expected results. The control system implements using Action Graph
is also examined to ensure that joint-space commands like moveJ are correctly interpreted
and executed.
To ensure that comparisons between the simulation and physical system are meaning-
ful, particular attention is given to the coordinate transformations between the robot arm
and the gripper. This transformation directly affects the computed TCP pose, joint po-
sitions, and velocities, and is therefore verified against the real robot configuration. The
implementation is checked at the code level to confirm that the transformation logic is con-

sistent with the actual hardware setup. In parallel, the simulation output is reviewed for

Verification is carried out before comparing trajectories between the simulation and the

3.2.2
Validation methodology
Model validation is about checking whether a simulation model can represent the real
system well enough for its intended use. As Sargent [18] points out, the key question is
whether right model has been built, not just whether it runs. Validation involves more than
just looking at outputs. It also includes examining the quality of the data used, whether
the assumptions behind the model make sense, and how closely the results match the
real system under the same conditions.
Data validation is an important step before comparing the simulation model with the real
robot. In this project, the main purpose of data validation is to ensure that the recorded
motion data from both systems are complete, consistent, and comparable. This includes
joint positions and, in particular, the position of the tool center point over time. To check
Verification and Validation for Digital Twin Models of Robotic Systems
16
==PAGE:24==
for consistency, the TCP trajectories from the simulation and the real robot are plotted
side by side. The shapes and trends of these curves are examined to determine whether
they follow the same path and exhibit similar behavior. If one curve shows a sudden
jump, drift, or mismatch in direction, it often indicates a problem with coordinate frames,
logging, or transformation logic. This visual method proves useful for quickly identifying
such issues. In addition, the data are recorded in the correct order and contain no missing
values. These steps ensure that the data used in the later validation stage are trustworthy,
and that any observed differences can be attributed to the model itself rather than to data
errors.
Conceptual model validation concerns whether the developed model appropriately rep-
resents the intended real-world system. It involves looking at the overall structure of the
model and asking whether it includes the right components, relationships, and logic based
on how the real system works. In this project, the kinematic chain, joint structure, and grip-
per setup are reviewed to ensure they match the real UR5e robot. Some simplifications
are made to keep the model manageable, but they are considered acceptable for the type
of motion-level tasks addressed, such as trajectory following and insertion. The goal is
not to model every detail, but to ensure that the main behavior of the system is preserved.
Operational validation is about checking whether the simulation behaves similarly enough
to the real robot for the purpose it is designed for. In this project, the goal is to validate
motion behavior. To do this, Dynamic Time Warping is used to align the simulation and
real robot data over time. After alignment, joint positions and tcp poses are compared
between the two systems. This helps to see whether the robot moved in the same way
internally, not just at the end-effector, but throughout the arm. If the differences stays
within an acceptable range, the simulation is considered accurate enough for tasks like
trajectory planning or insertion analysis.

discrepancies are found, the model must be reviewed and adjusted before further use.

3.4
Communication methodology
To communicate with the real UR5e robot, the RTDE interface provided by Universal
Robots is used. As discussed in the literature review (Chapter 2), different communication
protocols are suited to different application contexts. RTDE is selected for this project.
It operates over a TCP (Transmission Control Protocol) socket and enables structured,
high-frequency data exchange between an external computer and the robot controller. It
is primarily used to collect joint positions, joint velocities, and the Cartesian position of the
tool center point. These data are essential for comparing the robot’s actual behavior with
the simulation results.
The RTDE interface is accessed through the open-source ur-rt Python library, which pro-
vides a convenient API for establishing connections, configuring data streams, and man-
aging logging. During each experiment, the robot is controlled using preloaded programs,
while relevant motion and state data are continuously streamed to an external script via the
RTDE client. The interface supports a default sampling frequency of 500 Hz; however, in
this implementation, the frequency is set to approximately 60 Hz to reduce computational
load and ensure stable system performance during prolonged data acquisition. This fre-
quency remains sufficient for accurately capturing the robot’s motion and provides reliable
results for comparison with simulated data.
In addition to data streaming, RTDE is also used to trigger program execution on the robot
controller. Compared to other interfaces such as MODBUS or ROS-based communica-
tion, RTDE provides a more direct and real-time connection to the robot’s internal state,
making it well suited for synchronized experiments and model validation.

4.1.1
System objectives
The goal of the system is to automate an insertion-based assembly task using a UR5e
robotic arm. The setup includes a Robotiq parallel gripper mounted on the robot, and the
object being manipulated is a FlexPen. The task involves picking up the pen, aligning it
with a target hole, and completing the insertion using a combination of position and force
control strategies.
The objective is to evaluate the system’s ability to perform precise and reliable insertions

This system aims to automate a FlexPen assembly task using a UR5e robotic arm equipped
with a Robotiq parallel gripper. The object FlexPen needs to be assembled, which con-

The assembly procedure is divided into two stages:
Stage 1: Scale Drum to Housing: The scale drum must be inserted into the housing via
a helical structure. The robot performs a counterclockwise rotational motion to align and
lock the components together. Once the correct alignment is reached, gravity assists the
final insertion step.
Stage 2: Connector Pipe Insertion: The connector pipe is inserted into the pre-assembled
scale drum and housing unit. This step does not require rotational motion or force appli-
cation; it is completed by allowing the pipe to drop naturally into place under gravity.
The objective of the system is to execute both stages reliably and efficiently, utilizing
position and force control as appropriate. Force mode motion is used in the first stage to
detect alignment during rotation and detects the final insertion state after second stage.

Experiment Design
Based on the previously established real system and validated conceptual simulation
model, a verification step is first performed to ensure the correctness of the simulation
setup, including the kinematic models, control interfaces, and system configurations.
Following successful verification, the digital twin model is validated in two stages: offline
and online. The performance of the simulation is then compared with the real system in
an assembly task.
Finally, the application of these procedures is demonstrated across multiple use cases,
and the performance of the DT model is evaluated in different task scenarios.
5.1
Verification for predefined model
5.1.1
Verification Objective
Prior to the offline and online validation experiments, a verification phase is conducted to
ensure that the digital twin setup is implemented correctly and functionally consistent with
the real UR5e robotics system.
In the verification phase, the full execution pipeline of both the real system and the simu-
lation model is run to confirm that the model is constructed correctly and that the control
logic is functionally identical.
Following the model setup, a conceptual model validation is first performed to ensure that
the workflow and functional structure operated as intended. For instance, within the Action
Graph framework, it is verified that each node executed sequentially, according to its
dependency logic. Based on this foundation, verification is then conducted to examine the
correctness of the implementation. Key elements included the robot’s motion trajectory,
the opening and closing of the gripper, the configuration of sensors, and the accuracy of
coordinate transformations. These steps serves as a prerequisite for subsequent data
validation and operational validation.
In the assembly case presented, two stages are identified and evaluated separately. For
the process of inserting the scale drum into the housing, both the real system and the
simulation model are capable of performing individual subtasks such as pickup, place-
ment, and completion of the assembly. As previously described in the model setup, the
simulation did not include a physical force model. Therefore, force-based interactions
during final insertion are excluded from the later validation. The validation focused on
task stages from the initial configuration through pickup and placement, excluding the in-
sertion phase. Similarly, for the connector pipe insertion process, consistency between
the simulated and real executions is examined across the full procedure.

Overall, this verification phase addressed the simulation model at the levels of system
logic, code implementation, and functional task execution, thereby confirming its readi-

5.2
Offline validation
5.2.1
Validation Objective
The main objective of offline validation is to establish a structured, measurable method
to determine whether the digital twin model accurately reflects the behaviour of the real
robot. This validation does not rely on the results of subjective judgements, but rather
evaluates the model quantitatively through data.
Offline validation is performed in the two stages mentioned above. The idea is to give
the same target pose, as well as velocity and acceleration, in both the physical robot and
the digital twin model, and then compare their outputs at the joint and end-effector level.
Specifically, our goal is to determine whether the simulated joint motions exhibit similar
spatial characteristics to the real robot, even in the presence of numerical differences
between solvers or control strategies. Another key aspect is to assess whether the TCP
positions resulting from the forward kinematics used in the simulation are sufficiently close
to real-world measurements, both in terms of trajectory shape and in Cartesian coordi-
nates.
In this way, the validation process is not just about checking if the model works, it also
helps define clear and measurable criteria to decide whether the digital twin is accurate
enough for its intended use.
5.2.2
Procedure
1.Data collection:
During the data collection phase, the joint positions of both the real UR5e robot and the
simulated digital twin robot are recorded while performing the same task. These joint tra-
jectories are denoted as qreal(t) and qsim(t), respectively. The TCP trajectories preal(t) and
psim(t) in motion are also recorded. These trajectories are then visualised and compared
to assess the consistency between the real robot and the digital twin.
After data collection, alignment between the simulation and real system datasets is per-
formed. Although the same velocity is specified, identical velocity calculations could not
be guaranteed in the simulation and real system, since the real system may include filters
or other electronic components. With a simulation step size of 60, slight differences in the
duration of each phase occurred. Furthermore, positional accuracy is prioritized over tim-
ing, as it is critical for completing the entire process. For data alignment, the DTW method
is applied, using the fastdtw library in Python to achieve efficient alignment. At the same
time, a comparison is made between the aligned data to evaluate their similarity.

5.3
Online validation
5.3.1
Validation Objective
The purpose of online validation is to assess the performance of a digital twin under real-
time conditions while a physical robot is actively performing a task. Unlike offline valida-
tion, which relies on previously recorded data, online validation continuously compares
analogue outputs with real-time sensor feedback from the real system.
Online validation will be performed in the two stages mentioned above. Joint and TCP
errors are monitored in real-time during execution to assess whether the digital twin re-
Verification and Validation for Digital Twin Models of Robotic Systems
33
==PAGE:41==
mains accurate throughout the run. These transient errors are evaluated against prede-
fined thresholds to ensure that the model behaves consistently within acceptable limits.
Data similarity after DTW is compared as well.
Longer runs may introduce additional sources of error, such as thermal effects or me-
chanical wear and tear, which are not always captured by simulation. For this reason,
the system needs to be observed over a longer period of time to determine if the error
increases gradually. That is, this suggests that sustained physical stress or overheating
may lead to error drift. A thorough analysis of these aspects will help provide insight into
the suitability of digital twins for real-time decision support and long-term implementation
Together, these aspects provide insight into the digital twin’s suitability for use in real-time
decision support and long-duration deployments.
5.3.2
Procedure
1. Data Collection
Building on the previously collected joint positions and end-effector trajectories from both
the real robot and the digital twin, the temporal consistency between the two systems is
further examined.
First, the time taken to complete a specific sub-process, such as the insertion phase, is
recorded for both the real and simulated executions. This enables direct comparison of
task durations to assess whether the simulation reflects correct execution timing.
In addition, the potential accumulation of joint-level errors over time is analyzed by com-
puting and plotting the time series of absolute joint differences.
2. Model Comparison
In addition to comparing joint positions and TCP trajectories between the real robot and
the digital twin, errors in both joint angles and end-effector positions are evaluated. Task
completion in Stage 2 is also assessed by monitoring whether the insertion succeeds. A
success flag indicates whether the task is completed correctly, and if so, it triggers the
next assembly operation.
In addition, joint-level error accumulation is analyzed in relation to increasing controller
temperature by plotting time series of absolute joint differences. The increasing trend of
these error signals may indicate a shift in physical system behavior due to temperature.
For evaluating joint errors and TCP trajectories, a periodic validation strategy is adopted,
where validation results are generated online at regular time intervals. In addition, for
certain critical or error-prone positions, such as pick-up and place locations. On-demand
validation is performed to ensure local alignment. Finally, the overall DTW alignment
across the entire process is evaluated after completing five assembly cycles as one batch,
to assess consistency between the real and simulated trajectories.

false and the process is halted.

slowdowns or activate protective mechanisms. This scenario is examined by running two

5.4
Use case design
5.4.1
Overview
This section presents three use cases that explore how well the digital twin tracks the
real robot under different runtime conditions. Rather than evaluating accuracy in static or
ideal scenarios, these tests introduce variations in the real system to examine how the
simulation responds.
As discussed in Verification and Validation of Digital Twins and Virtual Testbeds [48], a
validated simulation model, once properly calibrated, can serve as a reliable proxy to
explain and analyze the behavior of the corresponding physical system. This supports
the idea that well-calibrated digital twins can be applied not only for offline planning, but
also for interpreting real-world task outcomes and determining safe operating boundaries.
To explore these capabilities in practice, three representative cases are selected. Each
case is designed to reveal different aspects of the digital twin’s behavior when subjected to
real-world variations. The first case investigates the upper and lower limits of acceptable
error by applying interference to a real robot and monitoring the resulting deviations. The
second case evaluates timing consistency by comparing the task execution time between
the simulation model and the real system. The third case assesses the digital twin’s ability
to follow a modified motion path, highlighting its sensitivity to dynamic timing behavior.
Together, these use cases provide a practical assessment of the digital twin’s robustness,
real-time responsiveness, and reliability limits.
5.4.2
Case 1 Deviation Bound Identification
This use case investigates how much task goal deviation a physical robot can tolerate
while still completing actions like picking and insertion successfully. The digital twin model
remains fixed and calibrated to the nominal target pose. In contrast, the real robot was
subjected to slight perturbations of the target position while performing the task to simulate
task-level variations in the real-world environment. Each trial modifies the target pose for
the task, e.g., changing the position or orientation of the object to be grasped by the
real system while keeping the sim model target constant. Then, the robot arm is allowed
to move to the target position. Depending on the degree of perturbation, the task may
succeed or fail.

deviations need to be measured in joint space and Cartesian space:

5.4.3
Case 2 Task Validation via Duration
In this use case, a validated simulation model is used to evaluate task execution by com-
paring the duration of specific processes in the simulation and the real system.
The validated model is used to simulate a pre-defined task. During the execution of this
task, process durations are recorded in both the simulation and the actual robotic system,
e.g., the time taken from the start to the end of sim and real pick and place operations are
recorded. If the durations in the two models are approximately equal, it indicates that the
dynamics of the task execution in the simulation are very similar to those in the physical
system.
This use case focuses on the process of inserting a connector pipe into an assembled pen.
This process also involves examining the impact of simulation step size on the results. As
discussed in a previous chapter, the simulation step size can definitely affect the physical
behavior of the simulation. Therefore, the task duration in simulation is compared under
two different step size like 60 and 150, and analyze how these durations align with the
execution time of the same task in the real system. In addition, the effect of changes has
been analysed in simulation step size on the observed task durations in the simulations.
For real-world industrial applications, this temporal consistency suggests that the sim-
ulation accurately captures the timing behavior of the physical system. As a result, the
validated model can be reliably used to predict or verify similar tasks in production scenar-
ios. Furthermore, the simulation can be used for further planning, testing, or optimization
without the need for repeated real system trials.

7
Conclusion and Future Work
7.1
Conclusion
This thesis investigates the use of a digital twin model to support a robotic arm in executing
an assembly task, focusing on how accurately the simulation reflects real-world behavior.
Using the Omniverse Isaac Sim platform, a framework is developed for real-time verifica-
tion and validation of simulation models, applied across both offline and online phases of
the task.
The study first deploys both the simulation and real robotic models. After model setup,
multiple validation strategies are applied, including on-demand and periodic validation.
Verification ensures that the simulation model can logically follow the real system. Offline
validation confirms that the simulation parameters align with the real robot’s behavior.
Online validation not only demonstrates that the validated simulation model can closely
and reliably track the real system in real time, but also provides insight into the current
operational state of the real robot by comparing it against the expected simulation be-
havior. This allows deviations to be detected early and supports dynamic monitoring of
task execution. Experimental results across different stages and use cases show that
joint-level and end-effector-level errors stay within defined thresholds, indicating a strong
match between the simulation and real system in terms of motion behavior.
In addition, the study shows that runtime validation is capable of detecting significant devi-
ations caused by external disturbances, and that simulation time serves as a reasonable
approximation of real execution time under certain simulation frequencies. By introducing
controlled disturbances, the study identifies the upper and lower bounds within which the
DT model remains valid without affecting task completion. Furthermore, the robustness of
the simulation model is validated by modifying the motion path and observing consistent
system behavior.
Despite the results are promising, several limitations have been identified.
Although the current simulation model replicates system behavior well, it lacks force con-
trol during the insertion phase in Stage 1, which limits the physical realism of contact
interactions.
For high-precision assembly simulations in Isaac sim, detailed modeling of contact and
friction significantly increases the computational load. As a result, frame rates may de-
crease and GPU usage may rise. It becomes necessary to balance simulation accuracy
with available computational resources.
Regarding data collection, While a 60 Hz sampling rate is sufficient for the current task,
achieving higher frequencies such as 500 Hz may be constrained by the simulation up-

==PAGE:58==
date rate of the Action Graph in Isaac Sim, as the current on_tick trigger operates at
approximately 60 Hz.
7.2
Future work
In future work, the simulation can be improved by implementing a force-mode controller,
which would allow the system to better handle contact during the insertion process. This
could help the model respond more realistically to physical interactions and bring its be-
havior closer to that of the real robot. Additionally, ROS-native simulators such as Gazebo
could be considered as complementary tools for controller testing.
For the validation process, another promising direction is to integrate validation outcomes
into decision support for human operators. If deviations beyond acceptable thresholds are
detected and input variability and external disturbances have been considered, adaptive
methods such as those based on reinforcement learning could be used to adjust the sim-
ulation model online. This would allow the system to maintain alignment with the physical
process even under changing conditions.
Finally, for data collection, it can be improved by separating it from the Action Graph. A
dedicated high-frequency process would allow more accurate and timely capture, espe-
cially when sampling rates above 60 Hz are required.
Overall, this research presents a data-driven framework for the verification and validation
of robotic simulation models, developed in the context of industrial automation tasks at
Novo Nordisk. Its application in real industrial scenarios shows the practical potential of
the digital twin model and provides a basis for further improvement and broader use in
the future.
