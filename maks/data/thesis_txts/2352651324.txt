Abstract
The objective of this study is to motivate a design for a Kerr switch using elliptic core ﬁbers.
A numerical model, based on a set of previously derived coupling equations, estimates the
required interaction length to observe maximum mode conversion, as well as evaluating
how well the switch is able to convert between modes depending on the supplied pump
power and the detuning between modes.
A comparison study of two experimental methods, one based on long period ﬁber gratings
(LPFG) and a prism coupling method, is conducted to investigate their ability to track
the beat lengths between modes.
The prism coupling method is found to be superior for this purpose in most ways, capable
of predicting beat lengths between closely spaced modes, which are split due to the core
ellipticity. A disadvantage in using the prism coupling method as opposed to the LPFG
measurement is the increased diﬃculty in preparing the prism coupling sample, mainly
due to the need of polishing the ﬁber.
For future studies, a set of diﬀerential coupling equations modelling a high power pulse
beam (on the form of Abel equations) have been derived from the same perspective as the
low power probe pulse modelled, with a few additional assumptions.
Together with the numerical tool and the experimental procedure presented and executed,
the results documented can act as stepping stones towards future projects on intermodal
coupling with the objective of investigating Kerr switches, or devices building on similar
principles.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
In the early years of ﬁber communication, intermodal coupling was mainly viewed as an
undesired nuisance, which degraded the use of ﬁbers supporting higher order modes and
promoted research within single mode ﬁbers (SMF). But the ﬁeld of intermodal coupling
has vast potential in many implementations. The ability to switch between modes on
demand by means of light interaction has already proven useful in the following ﬁelds:
Optical communication tools[1], all-optical tunable shaping of a laser’s output[2, 3] and
parametric frequency conversion in four wave mixing (FWM)[4]. The early dispose of the
phenomenon may have caused a dismiss in eﬀorts towards research in the ﬁeld, leaving
technological innovations and improvements hidden, waiting to be discovered.
In regard to the use within optical signal processing for communication systems, the
continuous increase in bit rate magnitudes induces sharper requirements on optical com-
munication systems, such as demultiplexing devices. Previously, the speed of bit-rates has
been limited by frequency responses of laser diodes, photodiodes and electrical switching
circuits[5], but can be improved signiﬁcantly by conversion to an all-optical scheme. For
instance, to demultiplex a time-division multiplexed (TDM) signal of 100 Gb
s , a switching
speed of 5 ps is required[1]. All-optical switching based on the optical Kerr eﬀect is an
interesting application due to the ultrafast Kerr response of less than a few fs[1], which
makes it a useful prospect in fast data signal processing.
Also reported as a low-loss
switching mechanism, it candidates as a useful component for low-loss applications such
as that of quantum information processing[9].
1.1.1
Occurrences of intermodal coupling
Intermodal coupling can occur under diﬀerent circumstances and in diﬀerent ways. As
already touched upon, FWM is one occurence of intermodal coupling ([19]Ch. 2.2) (see
1
==PAGE:16==
2
CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
Section A.1).
FWM may be used for optical frequency conversion as FWM can partake in an act of
ampliﬁcation of a desired signal. This phenomenon is known as parametric ampliﬁcation.
Compared to Raman frequency conversion, FWM is advantageous in the sense that it
allows for performing frequency up-conversion, as well as down-conversion in a broader
range of frequency shifts[4].
Linear coupling between modes can occur as a result of ﬁber imperfections, causing pertur-
bations to the relative dielectric permittivity[6, 7]. This kind of linear coupling is diﬃcult
to come about as the imperfections come in the form of bends, twists and core deforma-
tions, all of which are present in most practical ﬁbers. This kind of mechanically induced
mode coupling is often much more diﬃcult to control as it is random in nature. It arises
from inevitable imperfections and is therefore often associated as a disturbance to more
well-managed intermodal coupling techniques compared to the nonlinear coupling meth-
ods. This is the case in Agrawal et al.[8], where rapidly varying birefringence eﬀects in
space-division multiplexed ﬁber systems are seen to reduce the impact of nonlinear eﬀects
through numerical simulations.
But linear coupling is not just an undesired nuisance whose presence is never appreciated.
If controlled in a periodic manner, such as in long period ﬁber gratings[10] (LPFG), where
periodic changes in the eﬀective refractive index are induced from pressure induction. This
results in speciﬁc couplings if a certain phase matching condition is met. The LPFG is far
from the only device that can produce this kind of periodic grating. Much research has
been made in the ﬁeld of acoustic wave induced gratings, which have shown great promise
in shaping the spectral proﬁle of ﬁber lasers[11].
Another kind of intermodal coupling is that used in the so-called Kerr switch. As the
name indicates, it involves the use of the Kerr eﬀect. The principle of operation is that a
change in the refractive index, resulting from the optical Kerr eﬀect caused by an increase
in intensity (perhaps from the presence of a higher power pump beam), can induce a phase
diﬀerence between modes depending on the diﬀerence in the refractive index. This results
in a diﬀerential phase shift between the modes[2]. If controlled correctly, it can be used
to shift resonance conditions, eﬀectively making a switch.
The optical Kerr eﬀect has found many uses in intermodal coupling and continues to be an
interesting ﬁeld of research due to its potential within high speed optical communication,
incorporated in all-optical systems. Therefore, the Kerr switch is the main focus of this
thesis.

1.2.2
Ultrafast All-Optical Switching Utilizing the Optical Kerr Eﬀect
in Polarization-Maintaining Single-Mode Fibers
Another study by M. Saruwatari et al. [5], investigated the potential speed of an all-optical
switching scheme based on the optical Kerr-eﬀect.
They achieved stable optical Kerr
modulation and all-optical demultiplexing of a 30 ps pulse train using a 1.97 GHz frequency
signal. This was in switching between two orthogonal polarizations of the same spatial
mode. The set-up used was comprised of two single-mode (SM) polarization maintaining
(PM) ﬁbers. These were identical but with the fast axis crossed at 90◦with respect to

The authors proposed that by choosing an appropriate set of wavelengths for the pump

compensating for birefringence eﬀects coming from the ﬁber and temperature inﬂuences,
it is potentially possible to perform (de)multiplexing with bandwidths over 100 GHz.
1.2.3
All optical switching in a highly birefringent and a standard tele-
com ﬁber using a Faraday mirror stabilization scheme
Yet another study, by N. Gisin et al. [1], concerns the stability of an all-optical switch
based on the optical Kerr eﬀect. More speciﬁcally two types of ﬁber: A PM and a standard
telecom ﬁber, were tested in a set-up tracking a polarizing beam splitter’s ability to stably
switch power into the line - and switch ports as a function of the control signal power.
The output polarization state is inﬂuenced by temperature variations and pressure changes
along the ﬁber, which changes the birefringence properties. In order to come about this
instability, a Faraday mirror stabilization scheme is implemented, making a double pass
through the ﬁber (see Figure B.1.1a). Thereby the polarization propagating along the
fast axis upon entry will propagate along the orthogonal slow axis upon the return trip,
leading to every input polarization having the same accumulated phase when leaving the
ﬁber[1].

in an eventual Kerr switch. One requirement for the Kerr switch to work is that the angle

Low-power on-chip all-optical Kerr switch with silica microcavity
A relatively recent study by T. Tanabe et al.[9] (2015) investigates optical microcavity

the transmission characteristics depending on the control signal power, eﬀectively making

2.1
Fiber modes
A mode is a solution to Maxwell’s equation described by a certain light pattern that
propagates in a waveguide with a certain propagation constant. When light propagates
in a waveguide, such as a ﬁber, only a limited number of modes are supported, referred
to as guided modes. Other modes will be lossy, and will not be able to propagate any
siginiﬁcant length inside the ﬁber before dissipating. In many cases, it is desired to have
a ﬁber supporting only the fundamental mode, as opposed to ﬁbers supporting more than
a single mode, referred to as multi-mode ﬁbers (MMF).

which is directly controllable by the power level supplied, for instance from a pump beam.
This creates the basis of an all-optical switching device (see for instance Eq. (1.2.1)).

3.3
Kerr switch simulation
The coupling equations between the LP01 and the LP11 modes in the Kerr switch presented
by B. Y. Kim et al.[3] (derived in Section A.3) were not used for direct numerical modelling
at the time they were found. But sweeping over key factors, such as the pump power, can
give useful insight in how the output can be manipulated as speciﬁc parameters are tuned.
Such simulations may also serve as a guideline when choosing a proper set of equipment
for constructing a Kerr switch.
More speciﬁcally, the parameters analysed here are the power supplied to the pump wave
and the detuning between the two modes. Their inﬂuence is tracked in the interaction
length required to reach maximum conversion from the launched LP01 mode to the LP11
mode, and on the magnitude of the latter at this point.
As a directly tunable parameter for the all-optical switching method, the pump power
controls the induced grating strength, ∆n, and thereby the phase diﬀerence between the
modes. Assuming a Gaussian shaped pulse, diﬀerent peak powers can be converted into a
total laser power using the repetition rate and the temporal full width at half maximum

One precise way of determining the eﬀective refractive indices is by the prism coupling

polished in order to ensure that the waves can escape the ﬁber. Placed in a quartz block,
the ﬁber is connected to a prism, added index matching oil in the layer between in order
to prevent air gaps and undesired escape of the outcoupled light. The angle at which the
radiated light escapes the prism is used to track the eﬀective refractive index of the light
as ([15]Eq. (2))

(4.3.2)

diﬀerent heights of the photodetector axis. It is therefore possible to determine all the
guided modes directly from this measurement, if the resolution of the set-up is high enough
to separate the refracted beams.

of a piezomotor. It was stopped to record the height every time a peak was registered
on the chart recorder. Both the front and rear ends of the quartz block were measured
to average out height deviations. Having measured the heights for each mode, as well as
recording from the rear and front end of the quartz block, the geometric data is converted
into eﬀective angles, θ. This was done by using a program designed to do so based on the

5.1
Discussion
A numerical mode solver based on the c-core step index ﬁber has been constructed and
approved from an orthogonality check. This has been used to visualize the spatial ﬁeld
pattern of various modes, and more importantly, to compute a theoretical beat length.
In the context of an e-core ﬁber, however, the usefulness of the beat lengths computed is
limited: This is visualized from the range of eﬀective radii that can be implemented to
mimic the e-core ﬁber and the inﬂuence that this has on the uncertainty of the resulting
beat length. Another ﬂaw of basing the e-core ﬁber analysis on a c-core ﬁber model is its
inability to account for the splitting of modes in even, odd, x- and y-polarization states.
Although the model inherently has diﬃculties in predicting the beat lengths due to the
aforementioned reasons, it did estimate the beat length of the LP01 and LP11 quite well.
The estimate is close to matching such a conversion in the LPFG analysis using the
Redfern Fiber #51 which is supposedly the conversion between the y-polarized states of
the LP01 and even LP11 modes in the prism coupling measurement. This happened when
modelled using the major axis as the eﬀective radius, the axis of which the lobes lie across
in the even mode. The respective beat lengths from the three methods in the wavelength
range of (1520-1620)nm are (420-425)µm for the c-core model, ∼446.6 µm for the LPFG
measurement and (461.7-469.7)µm for the prism coupling model. The most contributing
deviations arising from the theoretical model is probably caused by the diﬀerences between
the c-core and the e-core ﬁber, having diﬃculties ﬁnding the proper eﬀective radius to link
the two.
Estimates of the beat lengths between the fundamental mode and cladding modes range
between (230-305)µm using either r = a or r =
√

and 940 µm resulted in spectral loss, linking the three as harmonics of the same kind of
coupling. But from the mode conversion inspection, this theory could not be conclusively
validated, partially due to the state that the used CCD camera was in and because of the

did ﬁnd that some orientations had signiﬁcantly weaker power drops. With the knowledge
found in the prism coupling measurement, the diﬀerences in weak coupling scenarios for
the ∼446.6 µm and (621.6-648.5)µm must arise from the polarization state diﬀerences.
The prism coupling measurement on the Redfern Fiber #51 was easier to interpret in
terms of guided modes. The beat lengths were more easily found, tracking these directly
from simple theory once the eﬀective refractive indices were known. Also being able to ﬁnd
results for the e-core ﬁber splitting of modes, the results extend beyond that of the theo-
retical c-core model. This explained the spectral loss occurring around (621.6−648.5)µm
in the LPFG as a coupling between the two x-polarized LP01x mode and even LP11ex
modes, close to the deduced beat length of (643.4-655.8)µm found in the prism coupling
measurement. Although the LP11oy mode is believed not to be supposed guided, the beat
length between that and the LP01y was found to be between (314.7-320.5)µm. This gives
a more reliable explanation as to why the spectral drops occur in the LPFG rather than
that stated from the approximated theoretical model. Deviations can have been caused in
the LPFG measurement from the pressure induction, as it may have altered the eﬀective
refractive indices if pressed too hard.
This sheds new light on the LPFG mode inspection analysis, where the CCD camera’s
decreased sensitivity in the inspected wavelength of 1565 nm may have overshadowed an
eventual LP11 pattern when the grating of period 628.3 µm was applied. Likewise, the
318.7 µm grating period at 1530 nm wavelength may have shown LP11 coupling.
Ultimately, the prism coupling produced the most reliable and easily interpretable results.
The Achilles’ heel of the experiment is the diﬃculty of producing the sample in the matter
of polishing the ﬁber, as the removal of the cladding is a tedious and cumbersome process
for the uninitiated. It is here the LPFG has its advantage, being easier to deploy, but
lacks in the department of producing interpretable and unambiguous results.
The coupling equations presented by B. Y. Kim et al.[3] have been derived and simulated
in the context of a Kerr switch. This was done by tracking the contributions that the
directly manipulatable pump power has on the conversion length and on the conversion
ratio, as well as simulating the impact of the magnitude that the detuning between the
LP01 and LP11 modes has on the same parameters. Conclusively, it was found that the
pump power mainly regulates the conversion length, i.e. being the parameter to control
when modifying the output in terms of the presence of whichever mode desired. The de-
tuning mainly decreased the ability to convert one mode completely into the other. It was
also found that the drawback caused by the detuning could be diminished by increasing
the pump power, usable in situations where there is no way of decreasing the detuning.
The stated eﬀect of the pump power is well known in Kerr switches and has been exper-
imentally veriﬁed, for instance by B. Y. Kim et al.[3], but the detuning impact is a less
known factor. Testing this matter experimentally is more complicated, as the detuning is

less controllable and harder to keep track of.
Among other things, the model assumes that a temporally broad pulse is used, eﬀectively
making a Kerr induced grating spanning the entire ﬁber felt uniformly by the probe pulse
as it propagates within the ﬁber. Modern sources that can approximate this property
do exist, one of which has been simulated here. But the majority of present day sources
produce shorter pulses, to which the model needs an improvement if it should be able to
account for the temporally limited pulse span.
The simulated coupling equations deal with the situation of two modes in a low power
probe pulse, but they are unsuited for modelling a high power pulse that can induce such
a grating itself. A seemingly simple set of nonlinear diﬀerential equations describing the
amplitudes of modes in exactly such a pulse have been derived from the same starting
point as the probe pulse. They have not been modelled successfully as of yet, and could
be used for a future project.
Collectively, the results found in this study can be used to prepare and set up a design
for a Kerr switch, both in the choice of a proper source given an appropriate ﬁber for the
purpose and in the experimental inspection of the ﬁber. The proposed high power pulse
coupling scheme can be expanded on to research within the ﬁeld of intermodal coupling,
perhaps with the scope of constructing an alternative switch.
5.2
Conclusion
Kerr switches have been studied numerically through a set of coupling equations and
through experimental methods, the latter of which is constituted of preliminary measure-
ments relevant to constructing Kerr switches. This includes a comparison study evaluating
whether ﬁnding the beat length between guided modes using a LPFG or a prism coupling
method is most viable.
The constructed numerical tool can be used to judge whether a given set-up constituted
of a source and a ﬁber is able to experience signiﬁcant intermodal coupling.
This is
useful when searching for the proper tools for an eventual experiment. Furthermore, the
numerical tool reveals how the pump power can be used to modulate the coupling length
or overpower an eventual detuning between modes, which lowers the ability to convert
between modes non-linearly.
Analysing the e-core Redfern Fiber #51 in the wavelength range (1520-1620)nm, inter-
modal coupling is witnessed ﬁrst hand in the LPFG analysis. Stimulated coupling between
the fundamental and second order modes, split into the even, odd, x and y components due
to elliptical asymmetry, are observed. Specifying the mode conversions has been revealed
from the prism coupling study.
A numerical study basing e-core ﬁbers on a c-core ﬁber model is proven to be inadequate
in describing most mode couplings observed in this study, seeing as the model is unable
to account for mode splitting. This property, essential to the choice of using e-core ﬁbers,
==PAGE:58==
44
CHAPTER 5. DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSION
requires a more developed model in order to be accounted for. However, one transition
was predicted quite accurately: The beat length between the LP01 and LP11 modes in
the c-core model with the major axis as radius matches the LP01y to LP11ey in the prism
coupling. This is perhaps explained by the chosen radius, which mimics the separation
between the lobes in the second order mode quite well.
The LPFG analysis produced rather consistent results upon rotation with respect to the
angle of pressure inducted along with the fact that it is easier to use compared to the
prism coupling method. But it is more time consuming, has a low resolution and does not
reveal unambiguous results when tracking the beat length compared to the prism coupling
method.
The prism coupling method reveals accurate information (potentially better than 10−4) on
the eﬀective refractive indices, directly relatable to the guided modes, which in turn grants
precise beat lengths in a well resolved mode pattern accounting for closely split modes.
Although the polishing of the cladding makes the prism coupling sample more diﬃcult to
create compared to the LPFG stage, the prism coupling is strongly recommended for the
purpose of tracking beat lengths. This is both due to the better quality of results and
because of the comparably quicker measurement once the stage is set.
A model has been derived, which is constituted of two coupling diﬀerential equations.
It describes the coupling between modes in a high power pulse. Both equations can be
deduced to a simpliﬁed form of an Abel equation with several zero-coeﬃcients under proper
assumptions.
Conclusively, the results presented in this project can be used to work on designs for Kerr
switches, passing on the baton for future projects.
