Executive Summary 
Executive Summary 
The guidelines for the design of support mechanisms for energy and environmental protection by the 
European Commission set a clear focus on feed-in premiums granted through auctions for the future 
support of renewable energy in the EU. Furthermore, the Commission recently expressed concerns that the 
public service obligation to finance the support payments could entail discriminations against imports 
within the meaning of articles 30 and 110 of the treaty of the functioning of the European Union. To 
mitigate this, Denmark and Germany plan to partially open their future auctions to other EU member 
states. In this light, cross border auctions will play a more important role in the near future. The thesis 
addresses the new challenges for a successful auction design stemming from differences in country specific 
costs and resulting possible distortions in the competition. 
 
Solar PV and wind power installations are capital intensive investments, which require a high up-front 
investment and run at very low marginal costs. Therefore, the cost of capital is a crucial parameter for the 
overall economic feasibility of those projects. Recent studies expect the cost of capital to have a higher 
influence on the overall power generation cost than differences in resource availability as irradiation and 
wind conditions. 
 
The study uses a cash-flow based model applied on the Danish solar PV pilot auction, which is partly 
opened to Germany. An optimisation approach for the cost of capital is used to reflect the impacts of 
differences in cost of capital. 
 
The simulation results in required support levels of 227 – 388 DKK/MWh for projects located in Germany 
and of 262 – 569 DKK/MWh for projects located in Denmark as a fixed premium support for 20 years. The 
projects in Denmark are only competitive to the ones in Germany if they are developed by German 
investors. It is shown that the reason for that lies in the differences in access to investment capital between 
Denmark and Germany. Danish projects can have an internal discount rate of above 6% while German 
projects rarely show a rate above 4%. The main reason for that is in the better access to debt due to 
preferential loans provided by the KfW bank rather than the differences in cost of equity. 
 
A possible measure to mitigate this distortion would be to establish common credit programs for investors 
participating in the cross border auction. Danish projects having the same loan conditions as provided by 
the KfW could increase their debt share of the total investment by 20% and therewith reduce the internal 
discount rate to a level that the projects could be fully competitive with German projects. Other location 
specific parameters, as different tax levels, depreciation rules, market value factors and capacity factors 
were found to level each other out. 
 
Overall, the thesis contributes to a deeper understanding of the impact of different auction designs as well 
as differences in location specific parameters and costs on the level of required support payments of 
renewable energy projects.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Motivation 
In 2009, the European Parliament and the Council of the European Union adopted the directive for the 
deployment of renewable energy sources (RES) in the European Union (EU)(European Parliament, 2009). 
The goal is mainly defined as a 20% share of RES in overall energy consumption by 2020. To achieve the 
goal the directive sets mandatory national targets for the overall share of electricity generated from 
renewable sources for each of the 27 member states (now also including Croatia as the 28th member state). 
However, it is up to each country to achieve the individual targets by national legislation. Specifically, it is 
mentioned in preamble 25 that member states operate different schemes for support of energy from RES. 
 
The free choice of support schemes resulted in 4 different main support schemes functioning in parallel in 
the EU in 2011 (Kitzing, 2014), namely feed-in tariffs (FiT, applied in 21 countries), feed-in premiums (FiP, 
applied in 7 countries), auctions (applied in 5 countries) and quota obligations with tradable green 
certificates (TGC, applied in 6 countries)(Kitzing, 2014). 
 
In 2014, the European Commission published guidelines for the design of support mechanisms for energy 
and environmental protection (EUC, 2014). The guidelines set a framework for the design of support 
schemes for the period 2014 to 2020 and the focus is put on FiP granted through a competitive bidding 
process (auctions). From 2017 on, all operational aid for renewable energy installations shall be adopted in 
this way (Almunia, 2014). 
 
In this light, auctions will become an important measure for promoting renewable energy sources in the 
period until 2020 and after. However, the support instrument is not trivial to implement. There is a broad 
range of different auction designs which can lead to very different auction outcomes. Due to the relatively 
low experience in the use of auctions compared to other RES support mechanisms, deeper insight in the 
implications of different auction designs on the outcome and specifically on the resulting bid prices is 
needed to make the instrument as effective and efficient as it promises to be. 
1.2 Task description and outline 
In this thesis, a cash flow based model is developed, which can give more insight in the price formation in 
RES auctions depending on different design parameters and country specific conditions. The tool will be 
adapted on the specific application case of an auction for utility scale photovoltaic power in Denmark, 
which will be conducted in 2016. The case differs from other auctions in the EU so far, since it is designed 
as a cross border auction where the support can be partly allocated to projects in Germany. Several 
implications result from the opening. Especially the different access to financing sources in Denmark and 
Germany, different power prices and the different correlation of the technology specific production profile 
with the spot price variations could possibly lead to competitive advantages for projects in one of the two 
countries.

2 
Broader context of auctions for RES support in Denmark and the EU 
The following research questions are addressed in the thesis: 
 
How do the costs for solar PV projects differ between Denmark and Germany? 
 
Do the expected bid price levels for Danish and German projects differ systematically? 
 
How does the remuneration design influence the bid price formation of different representative 
investor groups? 
 
How can possible market distortions, which result from the opening to Germany, be mitigated? 
Special attention is paid on the effects of capital costs on the required support level. It is tried to transfer 
the general findings to another application case, specifically the nearshore wind power auction in Denmark. 
1.3 Broader context of auctions for RES support in Denmark and the EU 
In the “guidance for the design of renewables support schemes” the European Commission clearly points 
out that the promotion of renewable energy by support mechanisms is part of the Union policy of 
energy(European Commission, 2013). It is stated that feed-in systems should be phased out. Clear focus is 
put on competitive allocation methods to define the support levels in order to let the market participate 
more actively in revealing the true costs of each technology. 
 
So far, auctions have been used in Denmark for the allocation of support payments for offshore wind 
installations (Kitzing & Wendring, 2015). For utility scale Solar PV installations the support was paid as a 
fixed FiP set by law to 600 DKK/MWh in the first 10 years of operation and 400 DKK/MWh in the following 
10 years. The support mechanism was abolished in May 2016 after facing a huge increase in applications 
for support, while the capacity which could be supported additionally per year was not limited in the 
legislation(Lilleholt, 2016). In the context of a broader use of auctions and the new state aid guidelines by 
the European Commission, Denmark decided to allocate support for solar PV installations in a competitive 
bidding procedure. A pilot auction for a total capacity of 20 MW is planned for 2016.  
 
State aid granted for renewable energy production has to be approved by the Directorate-General (DG) for 
competition of the European Commission. 
The state aid measure for the Danish offshore wind farm Horns Rev 3 was approved in the beginning of 
2015. However, the commission expressed their concerns that the public service obligation (PSO) to finance 
the support payments could entail discriminations against imports within the meaning of articles 30 and 
110 of the treaty of the functioning of the European Union (TFEU). Therefore (and also for two other state 
aid cases1), the Danish government committed to open up the pilot tenders for renewable energy projects 
taking place in 2015 and 2016 for production established in other EU member states with up to 2.4 MW, 
which is considered to be a satisfying mitigation measure (European Commission, 2015). 
In this context the Danish parliament adopted a pilot auction for utility scale PV power plants for the total 
capacity of 20 MW, where 2.4 MW should be opened to Germany, Sweden or Norway (Lilleholt, 2015). 
The Danish Energy Agency (ENS) is obliged to conduct the auction in 2016 and to define the detailed 
auction design.

Also the German ministry for economy and energy (BMWI) plans to open the auction to allocate support 
payments for utility scale Solar PV installations to other EU member states. It is planned to open a pilot 
auction with 100 MW capacity in 2016 and from 2017 on 5% of the yearly new installed capacity(BMWI, 
2016b).  
In 2016 it was decided that the pilot auction in Denmark will be opened to Germany and the pilot auction in 
Germany will be opened to Denmark. An agreement on the mutual openings between the two countries is 
expected to be signed in summer 2016 (Joas, 2016). 
 
The two cases show, that the cross border design of auctions will play a more important role in the near 
future. However, this introduces new challenges for a successful auction design since different conditions 
and price levels in the participating member countries can create competition distortion and lead to a 
preferential position of investors from one of the countries. Even if the meaning of auctions is to award the 
cheapest projects, a one-sided outcome of the allocation process can be problematic both in political terms 
and in distributional effects when the electricity production becomes concentrated in one region.  
1.4 Scope of the thesis 
The developed DCF model functions in two steps. In the first step the financial structure of the different 
actors is optimised taking into account more detailed financing calculations. The second step focuses then 
on calculating a range of required support levels using the optimised capital structure and the resulting 
discount factor. The ranges result from different cost assumptions. The placement of a bid within this range 
will probably depend mainly on strategic considerations of the individual actors. This behaviour is not 
modelled within the scope of the thesis and it is assumed that the bid prices will be mainly oriented on the 
required support levels. A graphical representation of the workflow is given in Figure 1. 
 
The model application will focus primarily on the solar PV pilot auction conducted in Denmark. The general 
findings will be transferred to the case of nearshore wind power auctions in Denmark as far as this is 
possible. The nearshore auction will not explicitly be modelled.

Methods

2 Methods 
2.1 Description of the planned auction designs 
 
The draft conditions for the auctioning process of support for solar PV installations were published on 22 
February 2016 by the Danish Energy Agency (Energistyrelsen, 2016b).  
For the solar PV auction there will be no pre-qualification round. For the nearshore auction the 
prequalification round resulted in the choice of three prequalified investors in June 2015 (Cramer & Svane-
Petersen, 2015). Both auctions will be organised as sealed-bid auctions. 
2.2 Solar PV auction 
The main objective to introduce auctions for solar PV installations in Denmark is to change the allocation of 
support for the technology to a competitive process. Furthermore, an important aspect of the auction is 
the partial opening of the scheme to Germany to fulfil the condition negotiated with the European 
Commission in the approval of support schemes for former projects, mainly the offshore wind farm Horns 
Rev 3. 
The design features presented in the following paragraph are taken from Energistyrrelsen (2016). 
2.2.1 Main features 
The total auctioned volume will be 20 MW, which can contain up to 2.4 MW of installations located in 
Germany (if they are price competitive). The size limit applying for each single bid is 2.4 MW. One bid is 
considered as an installation with a common grid connection. A participant is allowed to place several 
independent bids. 
The auction will be a one shot auction and will be conducted in the second half of 2016. Until now it is not 
clear, whether there will be a consecutive auction at a later time. 
 
A tenderer must find suitable locations for the solar PV installations and is responsible for obtaining all 
licenses and permits relevant for the construction. The licenses do not have to be provided at the time the 
bid is submitted but can be obtained during the construction phase. 
2.2.2 Specific design elements 
The auction will be organised as homogenous multi-item, capacity based auction. Bids will have to include 
the installed capacity and the location of each installation. A pay-as bid pricing approach will be used to 
allocate the support payments. The remuneration will be a fixed feed-in premium paid for each MWh 
generated and sold on the electricity market. The premium will be provided for a period of 20 years after 
the installation supplies electricity to the grid for the first time. However, no premium will be paid after a 
period of 23 years from establishment of a contract. Premium will not be paid for hours when the 
electricity spot price in the relevant area (DK1, DK2 or DE/AT) where the installation is connected to the 
grid is not positive. All costs related to the sale of production (incl. balancing costs) have to be borne by the 
successful tenderer. The premium will be fixed in nominal terms (not indexed).

There is no price limit for single bids. However, the contracting authority reserves the right to cancel the 
auction without awarding contracts if the bid prices are deemed too high. This is not further defined. 
 
No pre-qualification criteria will apply. To be valid, bids have to contain the address of each installation and 
a bank guarantee of 180 DKK/kW which will lapse when at least 95% of the awarded capacity will be 
connected to the grid. Furthermore a solemn declaration has to be attached declaring that the tenderer 
has no unpaid debt due to Danish Authorities exceeding DKK 100,000. 
 
A non-compliance penalty will have to be paid immediately if an awarded tenderer will declare not to 
construct the installation covered by the contract or if the installation is not connected to the grid within 
two years after establishment of the contract. The penalty will be calculated as DKK 180 per kW installed 
capacity covered by the contract. If one of the above conditions relates to a part of the total contracted 
capacity the penalty will apply proportionally.  
For a delay of operation start, no explicit penalty is defined. However, the support period will be effectively 
shortened when the operation is delayed by more than three years (due to the maximum support duration 
of 23 years after contracting). 
2.3 Nearshore wind power auction 
With the auction for nearshore wind power, the installation of offshore wind turbines in nearshore areas 
which was part of the energy agreement of the Danish parliament in March 2012 should be realised. The 
design features presented in the following paragraphs are taken from Energistyrelsen (2015) and 
Energistyrelsen (2016b). 
2.3.1 Main features 
The total auctioned volume will be 350 MW, which can be built at five pre-defined sites in the North Sea 
and the Baltic Sea. The maximum installed capacity which can be established on each area is limited to 200 
MW (or 50 MW at the Bornholm area). A prequalified participant is allowed to place several independent 
bids for different areas and windfarm sizes which can also be mutual exclusive. The prequalification took 
place in 2015 and led to three prequalified investors. 
The auction is planned as a one shot auction and best and final offers have to be delivered until the 1 
September 2016. 
2.3.2 Specific design elements 
The auction will be a multi-item, capacity based auction. Each offer has to contain a support price in DKK 
øre/kWh. Winning bids will then be selected in the way that for all possible combinations of bids adding up 
to 350 MW an average price per MW will be calculated. The combination offering the lowest average price 
will win the concessions. However, there is a price limit for the total average price of 700 DKK/MWh. Since 
this only applies for the average price, a single bid can be above this value and still be valid. In case that the 
cheapest combination leads to an average price above the limit, only bids which offered a lower price will 
be awarded, while the ones above will be forwarded to parliamentary discussion to decide if they are

A pay-as bid pricing approach will be applied, i.e. the remuneration for each won concession will be a paid 
as a sliding premium, which amounts to the individual best and final offer for 50000 full load hours (FLH) of 
production. This can correspond to different support durations in years depending on the capacity factor of 
the individual wind farm. When it is assumed that typical capacity factors of nearshore wind farms are 
between 35% and 45%, the support will be paid for a period between 12.6 and 16.3 years. 
 
The costs for the establishment and operation of the grid from the farm to the next onshore connection 
point have to be paid by the winning investor. Additional costs for a winning investor in this auction will be 
the costs for all preliminary surveys and the environmental impact assessment (EIA) conducted for the site 
the wind farm is placed and possible compensation payments for the loss of value of properties close to the 
wind farm. Depending on the location, the costs for EIA range between 23.3 mio. DKK and 26.1 mio. DKK. 
 
A non-compliance penalty will have to be paid if an awarded bidder declares not to construct the wind farm 
covered by the concession at all or if the construction work is not commenced by 31 December 2019. The 
penalty will amount to 100 mio. DKK per awarded concession. 
In case that a wind farm is not connected to the grid with at least 95% of the total capacity on 1 January 
2021 the energy production eligible for support will be reduced by 0.1 TWh and for further 0.1 TWh for 
each subsequent six month the 95% connection is not fulfilled. This can again correspond to different 
effective shortenings of the support period in years depending on the capacity factor and the size of the 
farm. 
2.4 Parallels and differences in the auction designs 
Besides some common elements as the pay-as bid pricing approach and the multi-item format, the two 
auction schemes for solar PV and nearshore wind power show fundamental differences in their design.  
 
While the project location in the solar PV auction can be chosen by the investor, the sites for the nearshore 
wind farms are pre-defined. In this way the PV auction offers more possibilities for site specific cost 
advantages, which can have a big influence in the light of the partial opening to Germany.  
After the pre-qualification round only three investors are left to bid on the auctioned nearshore capacity. In 
the solar PV auction there is no pre-qualification round and a much higher competition in terms of the 
number of participants can be expected. 
 
Regarding the remuneration and penalties the auction schemes are also very different. While the solar PV 
auction awards a 20 years fixed premium winners in the nearshore auction are awarded a sliding premium 
payment for a shorter time period. The effect of different remuneration types will be analysed in more 
detail in this study. The price limit in the nearshore auction sets a maximum cap on the amount of support 
payments (however, the exact amount depends on the market price development). In the solar PV auction 
the support payments are not limited since there is no maximum price applying. However, the payments 
are predictable and do not depend on the market price anymore from the moment the projects are 
awarded. 
 
The non-compliance penalty level in the nearshore auction is much higher than in the solar PV auction. This 
corresponds to the expected higher capital investment for this type of technology. Both auctions shorten 
==PAGE:18==

the support period if the operation start is delayed. In case of solar PV the shortening exactly corresponds 
to the delay (applying from 3 years after contracting). An assumed nearshore farm of 150 MW installed 
capacity and a capacity factor of 40% will face a shortening of approx. 2.3 month for each 6 month of delay 
(applying from 4 years after contracting). Therefore, the penalty for delay can be deemed higher in the 
solar PV auction. Reasoning for that could be that the probability for delay of the installation of a nearshore 
wind farm is higher due to the need for scarce construction parameters as for example special vessels for 
offshore construction.

2.5.4 Market value factor 
When estimating the revenues from selling the produced electricity on the market it is important to 
correlate the Solar PV production profile with the hourly profile of spot market prices. In this way a market 
value factor (MVF) can be defined. This factor has to be multiplied with the average electricity price to 
estimate the market price which is really “seen” by the technology. Solar PV produces mostly in the hours 
around noon when also the electricity demand is high and with it the wholesale prices. Solar PV can 
therefore sell for higher prices at the market than the average price over all hours. However, this effect is 
limited when more and more solar production enters the market at the same time. Due to the low marginal 
costs an increased share of solar power in the power mix leads to a lower market value factor of this 
technology in a marginal pricing energy only market.

market value factor and overall market share of solar power (Hirth, 2015):

2.5.5 Balancing Costs 
In case that a PV plant will receive support payments after winning in the auction the owner is exposed to 
all costs for balancing power in both countries. Generally, it is not straightforward to estimate the balancing 
costs of a single power generator. The costs mainly depend on two parameters, which are: 
 
The average cost for balancing power in the respective market area 
 
The amount of imbalances the single producer faces compared to his overall energy output  
Recent studies about the implications of integration of renewable energy on the balancing costs face many 
challenges when trying to quantify balancing cost. One main challenge is that the balancing market in most 
regions consists of two parts, which is a capacity part and an energy part (the actual activation of these 
reserves). The energy part is distributed among the balance responsible parties (BRP) while the costs for 
the capacity part are often borne by the final consumers. Another challenge is whether to include only 
costs for balancing power or also other costs which occur after the closure of the day-ahead market, e.g. 
intra-day trading (Fürstenwerth, Pescia, & Litz, 2015). 
Other studies find that the average cost of balancing power is correlated with the overall penetration of 
wind power in the respective power system (Hirth, Ueckerdt, & Edenhofer, 2015). 
For solar power there are fewer estimations regarding the balancing costs than for wind power. Recent 
studies point out costs of 0.5 – 1 €/MWh (Fürstenwerth et al., 2015), 1.5 – 2.6 €/MWh (Hirth et al., 2015) 
and 1.9 – 3 €/MWh (Hirth, 2015).  
It is also pointed out that the balancing costs in the Nordic system are lower due to more interconnections 
and more flexible thermal power generation (Fürstenwerth et al., 2015). 
 
Taking all cited studies into account it is decided to use cost assumptions of 11.2 DKK/MWh (1.5 €/MWh) in 
Denmark and at 14.9 DKK/MWh (2 €/MWh) in Germany for the model.

2.5.7 Degradation factor 
The degradation factor is an annual decrease of the capacity factor of solar plants in % due to ageing of the 
modules. Recent measurements identify an annual degradation factor of 0.1% for a solar PV system 
(Fraunhofer ISE, 2012). To have a more conservative approach the degradation factor is assumed to be 
0.2%. It is assumed that the degradation only depends on the type of used solar cell and does thus not vary 
with the location of the PV plant.

2.5.10 Depreciation 
In Denmark there is the possibility for a yearly depreciation of up to 25% for solar PV plants with an 
installed capacity of less than 1 MW and up to 15% for installation sizes of over 1 MW (Skatteministeriet, 
2015). The depreciation follows the declining balance method for the expected lifetime of the plant which 
is here assumed to be 25 years. In this way the depreciation percentage is used for the remaining total 
investment in each year. If the remaining total investment is 12,300 DKK or less the total amount can be 
depreciated (Skatteministeriet, 2015). 
 
In Germany linear depreciation over 20 years is the general rule for utility scale solar PV installations. The 
possibility for degressive depreciation was abolished in 2011. For smaller companies there is the possibility 
of special depreciation of 20% within the first 4 years of operation (F&S Solar GmbH, 2016). For the model 
it is decided to use linear depreciation for projects based in Germany. 
 
The applicable depreciation rule is the one from the country where the PV installation is placed (BMWI, 
2016b).

2.6.2 Cost of debt and loan maturity 
The cost of debt and the loan maturity mainly depend on the type of loan contract, which can be obtained 
by the individual investor. Recent studies which focus on the cost of capital for renewable energy 
investments point out that the cost of debt in Germany is considerably lower than in Denmark. This is 
mainly caused by the special credit program of the development bank KfW and generally decreasing credit 
rates (Noothout, Jager, Rooijen, & Angelopoulos, 2016). In the cited study, the cost of debt are determined 
based on interviews with investors in the renewable energy sector in Denmark (1 association, 2 utilities, 1 
banker) and Germany (1 project developer, 2 utilities, 3 bankers). The interviewees pointed out that in 
Denmark the cost of debt ranges between 4.5 – 5.5% while in Germany the rates range between 1.8 – 
3.2%. For Denmark it was further mentioned that economically potent investors could push down the cost 
of debt to 3%.  
 
The German KfW bank offers a special credit program for solar PV installations (loan nr. 274) in their “KfW 
Renewable Energies Program – Standard”(KfW Group, 2016a). Here, the loan conditions depend mainly on 
the economic liability of the loan taker. For an assumed medium to good liability of the loan taker an 
interest rate of 1.95 – 3.05% for a 10 year loan and an interest rate of 2.15 – 3.25% for a 20 year loan are 
applicable (KfW Group, 2016b). The loan conditions could in principle differ depending on the technology 
the investment goes to (due to e.g. limited access to special funding programs for certain technologies). 
However, the KfW bank offers the same conditions for onshore wind energy investments and investments 
in solar PV projects (KfW Group, 2016c).

debt can be raised for the different investment projects. The single cost components for debt and equity 
are therefore taken from the findings in Noothout et al. (2016).  
2.6.3 Debt equity ratio  
A crucial factor for all calculations related to the discount rate is the debt to equity ratio in the project 
financing. This should be the starting point for all calculations. However, the financing structure depends on 
many parameters as the specific needs of the undertaking project developer or the chosen financing model  
and can vary widely (IFC, 2015).  
 
While the share of equity is generally only limited by the availability there is a limit on how much debt a 
project can raise. Here the determining factor is the Annual Debt Service Coverage Ratio (ADSCR) which has 
to fulfil the requirements of the lenders. The level of required ADSCR depends also on the regulatory 
framework and the risk implications, where generally projects with higher return risks require also a higher 
debt coverage.  In this light the debt equity ratio can be calculated backwards by first estimating the annual 
==PAGE:31==
Methods 
 
21 
cash flows available for debt service and then determine the maximum amount of debt a project can raise 
(Yescombe, 2014).  
For the model it is assumed that a minimum required ADSCR of 1.2 during the whole loan maturity is 
required for all loan contracts. 
2.7 Model application 
2.7.1 Goal seek for highest possible debt equity ratio (financial structure calculator) 
Instead of defining the debt equity ratio for each project form the beginning, the model seeks for reaching 
the highest debt share a project can sustain. This is depending on the loan conditions a project developer is 
facing and on the question if the required ADSCR can be sustained with the net cash flows generated by the 
project( when assuming project financing). It is assumed that the project developers seek for the maximum 
amount of debt. However, in case of an auction, the annual cash flows depend on the awarded 
remuneration, thus on the bid price. 
 
Therefore, an optimisation is used to find the optimal debt share while fulfilling two requirements, which 
are: 
 
Finding the lowest possible support level which can create a project NPV of 0 
 
Finding the highest amount of debt the project can raise under the given loan conditions and 
support level 
In this way it is assumed that the allocation risk (i.e. the risk of not winning in the auction due to a too high 
bid) is perceived as high and the investors seek for a low bid price on the first priority. The debt share has 
then to be adjusted to the expected income with the given support level.

2.7.2 Remuneration type 
While the Danish pilot auction will include a fixed premium paid on top of the received market price the 
German pilot auction design will allocate a sliding premium which covers the difference between the 
achieved market price and the auctioned remuneration level (Energistyrelsen, 2016a). Also the nearshore 
wind auction uses sliding premium remuneration. 
In the future there could be a concept joint auction schemes instead of mutual opened auctions with a 
common auction design (Joas, 2016).  
To examine the impact of the type of remuneration on the auction outcome the required support levels are 
modelled with both a fixed premium and a sliding premium. In case of a sliding premium it is assumed that

4 Analysis and Discussion  
4.1 Main implications of the simulation findings 
4.1.1 Required support levels and competitiveness 
Regardless of the type of remuneration there is a split in the required support level into three groups in 
Figure 5 and Figure 6. The 12 projects requiring the lowest support level are in a comparative range where 
the sensitivities in the cost assumptions could easily lead to the placement of each project in each position. 
However, Danish project developers can only compete on these support levels if they build the project in 
Germany.  
The main reason for that is the access to better financing conditions, namely the lower interest rate and 
longer loan maturity in case of a German special credit program. In this way the projects can achieve a 
similar financing structure in terms of debt/equity ratio which results in comparable levels of the discount 
rate. Pure Danish projects which have no access to the better loan conditions can only sustain a lower debt 
share in their capital investment which leads to a significantly higher discount rate. 
 
A project located in DK-EAST needs a lower support level than a project located in DE-NW in case of similar 
financing conditions. The reason for that are the lower tax rate and a higher market value factor. The cost 
advantage from the higher market value factor has a higher significance when choosing a fixed premium 
than a sliding premium. Therefore, fixed premium remuneration makes projects located in Denmark more 
competitive while sliding premium remuneration mitigates the advantage of higher received market prices 
and strengthens the location specific advantages of DE-NW (compare Figure 6).  
In case of a project location in DE-SE this is not the case anymore. The preferable location specific 
parameters in DE-SE and here especially the higher capacity factor can even outweigh a slightly higher 
WACC. 
 
Utility companies can be hardly competitive in all cases due to their high WACC. This is mainly caused by 
the fact that they are assumed to maintain a high share of equity in the total financing combined with the 
high cost of equity. However, in case of comparing a project location in DK-WEST developed by a Danish 
investor with a project location in DE-SE developed by a German investor, a utility company could be on a 
competitive level with a Danish project developing company even if the financing costs are slightly higher. 
4.1.2 Influencing parameters 
The parameters which have the highest influence on the differences in required support level between 
Denmark and Germany were identified as lower OPEX and CAPEX and higher capacity factors as preferable 
conditions for projects located in Germany and lower tax rate and higher market value factor preferable for 
projects located in Denmark. Besides these factors, the main barrier for competitiveness can be caused by 
different access to investment capital and therefore differences in the discount rates. Furthermore, the 
depreciation method can have a significant influence on the required support. However, in this case the 
better conditions depend mainly on the level of the discount rate. On one hand, a high WACC makes a 
declining depreciation more attractive because the tax payments can be reduced to a higher extend in the 
beginning of the project lifetime. On the other hand, a lower and linear depreciation can be preferable, if

the yearly depreciation in the beginning of the project would otherwise be too high compared to the 
taxable income, especially if the discount rate is not very high. Since the analysis does not include any 
balance sheet calculation, the effect of the depreciation cannot be reflected in its whole. To do this, the tax 
effects on the developer’s total balance sheet should be taken into account as well as different effects of 
adjusting the depreciation rate in case of a declining balance method. 
 
The differences in land lease costs, costs for balancing power and also the different levels of sunk cost in 
case of non-compliance do not play a significant role for the differences in required support. Also the 
differences in the power price do not play a significant role since a similar development of the wholesale 
prices in Denmark and Germany is assumed. If the power prices would differ a lot, a sliding premium 
combined with a long support period could be a helpful measure to mitigate the differences implied by the 
different conditions. 
 
Among the influencing factors, the MVF, the capacity factor and CAPEX and OPEX are hardly controllable by 
policy makers when designing an auction. Nevertheless, the effect of differences in the MVF could be 
counteracted by choosing sliding premium remuneration instead of fixed premium remuneration. 
Differences in tax levels and depreciation could be removed when agreeing on common taxation rules for 
projects awarded in cross border auctions. 
 
In case of Denmark and Germany, differences in the access to investment capital could cause a distortion in 
competition. Therefore, it is worth to investigate how the access to capital for a Danish project developer 
should be changed to be in a competitive position to a German project developer. 
4.1.3 Differences caused by financing costs 
A significant difference in the required support level was simulated when comparing a project located in 
Denmark developed by a Danish project developer with a project located in Germany developed by a 
German project developer. Under the given assumptions the best conditions (low cost assumptions) for the 
Danish project lead to a required support level of 393.1 DKK/MWh fixed price premium while the required 
support level for the German project under medium assumptions is 322.1 DKK/MWh even for a project 
location in DE-NW. Thus, the two projects cannot be seen as competitive with each other.  
The main reason for this is the difference in discount rate with 6.86% for the Danish project and only 3.61% 
for the German project.  
Two factors mainly lead to this outcome. First of all, the Danish project has no access to the credit program 
of the German KfW bank and therefore faces a higher cost of debt of 5%. Secondly, the loan maturity is 
assumed to be only 10 years compared to 20 years in Germany. In this way the Danish project can sustain a 
lower debt share in the capital investment of 51% compared to 64% in the German case which increases 
the overall WACC. In case of access to better loan conditions of a maturity of 20 years combined with the 
lower cost of debt of 3.2%, the Danish project could increase the debt share to 72% which would reduce 
the WACC to 4.62%. 
The remaining difference is caused by the assumed higher cost of equity of 10% for Danish project 
developers compared to 6% for German project developers. The required support level under medium 
assumptions would then reduce to 373 DKK/MWh and with best conditions to 209 DKK/MWh. This would 
make the project competitive with the German project.

4.1.4 Support payments 
The results regarding the support payments are only valid for the assumed power price scenario. Actually 
they very much depend on the development of the market prices. In case of a sliding premium the 
payments are directly correlated with the price development. However, within the scope of this thesis the 
analysis is not performed more deeply. 
 
The analysis showed that a sliding premium can reduce the overall support payments significantly. Even for 
a project located in Denmark and developed by a Danish developer the reduction in overall support 
payments was calculated to be 262945 DKK per MW installed capacity. When it is assumed that this 
difference is valid for all participating projects, the overall support payments related to an auction of 20 
MW could be reduced by approximately 5.26 mio. DKK. by choosing sliding premium remuneration. 
However, it could be also seen that sliding premium remuneration will put projects located in Germany in 
an advantageous position compared to projects located in Denmark since it counteracts the Danish location 
advantage of a higher MVF.  
 
Regarding the support duration it was shown that the NPV of total support payments would decrease by 
shortening the support period if the projects have a higher internal discount rate than the socio economic 
discount rate applied for discounting the support payments. When looking at the simulated internal 
discount rates for fixed premium remuneration in Table 15 it can be seen that there are some projects with 
higher discount rates and some projects with lower discount rates compared to an assumed socio 
economic discount rate of 4%. However, all utilities as well as all pure Danish projects have discount rates 
higher than 4%. For these projects a shortening of the support period to 15 years could decrease the NPV 
of support payments. When assuming that the total auctioned capacity will be developed with a discount 
rate of 6.9% (as for the example of a Danish project developer in Figure 9) a shortening of the support 
period to 15 years would decrease the NPV of the support payments by 2.7 mio. DKK. 
4.2 Most sensitive parameters in the model 
The most sensitive parameters in the model are the power price assumptions and the capacity factor 
influencing the project revenues. Furthermore, the applied discount rate has a high influence on the 
obtained results. Since the WACC is calculated using an optimisation approach it is directly influenced by 
the minimum ADSCR and the loan maturity. 
4.2.1 Debt service coverage ratio 
The limiting parameter for the debt share and therefore for lowering the WACC below a certain level is the 
requirement that the projects have to maintain a minimum ADSCR of 1.2 in every year of the loan maturity. 
The minimum ADSCR occurs either in the first year or in the last year of the loan repayment period for all 
projects depending on the type of remuneration. In case of a fixed premium the net cash flows increase 
over the project life time under the increasing market price assumption. Therefore, the minimum DSCR 
always occurs in the first operational year. In case of a sliding premium the net cash flows decrease over 
the life time since the price premium is assumed not to be indexed. Figure 10 shows the trend of the ADSCR 
for a project placed in DK-WEST developed by a German project developer under different remuneration

4.3.1 Simulating a past auction with known outcome 
A way of testing the validity of the model is to simulate an auction with known outcome. However, this 
approach is limited in a way that the outcome of real auctions is based on bid prices which do not 
necessarily reflect the exact amount of needed support. Due to strategical behavior the bid prices can 
deviate from the required support level in both directions.  
For this study, the first solar PV auction conducted in Germany in April 2015 is simulated with the used 
model. Only projects located in Germany could participate in this auction. The support awarded was a 
sliding premium paid out for 20 years after production start. The penalty for non-compliance amounts to 4€ 
per kWp (29800 DKK/MWp). 
Simulating the auction with the given design leads to a required support level between 671.6 DKK/MWh for 
a project located in DE-SE with a WACC of 2.62% developed by a project consortium and 843.0 DKK/MWh 
for a project located in DE-NW with a WACC of 3.95% developed by a project developer. These results are 
slightly higher than the bids seen in the first auction round in Germany (see Table 22). However, the results 
are in a similar range and the model result can therefore be considered as reasonable.  
Looking at the utilities, the simulated required support level reaches from 1000 to 1300 DKK/MWh, which is 
182 – 482 DKK/MWh above the highest bids seen in the German auctions. This means either that the cost 
of capital for utilities is overestimated or that the participating utilities bid under their actual required 
support level due to strategic reasons.  
 
As it was described in paragraphs 4.2.1 and 4.2.2 the assumptions on the loan conditions have a high 
influence on the model results. Normally, these conditions are subject to negotiations between the 
individual project developer and a financial institution. The simulated discount rates seem to be reasonable 
when comparing them to the findings of recent studies based on interviews (Noothout et al., 2016).  
 
The debt shares resulting from the optimisation step in the model range from approx. 50 - 75%, which 
seems to be lower than the findings of the studies. This could be partially explained by the fact that the 
objective in the optimisation is to find the lowest possible support level rather than to maximise the debt 
share. In reality, this could be the other way around, i.e. it is first decided what to invest with a certain debt 
share and the bid price is then formed based on the resulting WACC. Furthermore, the optimisation 
assumes project financing as a financing method in all projects. In this way the project is seen as an isolated 
investment and the cash flows for serving the loan are only the ones generated from the project. In reality, 
there are more possibilities as balance sheet financing and asset based financing (Yescombe, 2014). When 
these other types of debt financing are chosen, the constraint of a minimum ADSCR only from the project 
cash flows is not realistic.

4.4 Assessment of the auction design for solar PV 
In the following, some specific aspects of the planned auction design will be evaluated based on the 
modelling outcomes. 
4.4.1 Limitation of opened capacity 
In the planed solar PV auction the capacity, which can be awarded to projects located in Germany will be 
limited to 2.4 MW. This means that there will be 17.6 MW of capacity which cannot be awarded to projects 
located in Germany even if their bid prices were more competitive. This could increase the total amount of 
support payments awarded in the auction by 7.18 mio. DKK. To calculate this amount it is assumed that 
projects located in DE-SE developed by a German project developer could bid with the medium required 
support level of 267.0 DKK/MWh fixed premium and will be replaced by projects located in DK-WEST which 
bid the medium required support level of 313.4 DKK/MWh due to the capacity limitation. This results in a 
difference in total amount of support payments for the two projects of 407804 DKK/MW as given in Table 
21.  
When assuming that the projects located in DE-SE are replaced by projects located in DK-WEST developed 
by a Danish project developer the increase in awarded support payments would amount to 48.5 mio. DKK. 
This is based on the result that the required total support payments for the replacing projects are 2756763 
DKK/MW higher than for the replaced projects. 
4.4.2 Penalty levels 
According to the findings of the simulation, the penalty level of 180000 DKK/MWp for non-compliance has 
no significant impact on the resulting required support levels. Table 23 shows the required support levels

Under the simulation assumptions the used penalty for non-compliance does not have a significant impact

penalties.  
The question of introducing penalties or not should therefore be driven more by the possible deterrence 
penalties can create and the resulting general lower competition in the auction. Taking into account the 
fact that the auction has no maximum price, the level of competition should be maintained high to 
circumvent projects with very high bid prices to win. 
The penalty for non-compliance could be increased in order to reach a higher realisation rate. Also, a 
penalty for project delay could be introduced to increase the number of projects which start operation on 
time. Based on the conducted simulation both measures are not expected to increase the bid price level a 
lot given that the level of competition is not reduced too much. 
4.4.3 No price limit 
The simulation results show a big spread of required support levels with high outliers for utilities with high 
return expectations. Setting no price limit could lead to the situation that projects with high bids above 700 
DKK/MWh could be awarded if the competition in the auction is not very high. However, taking the total 
amount of only 20 MW auctioned capacity into account this scenario is not very likely. For possible future 
auctions with a higher total capacity a price cap which could be oriented on the maximum bids in the 
German auction (see Table 22) could be a way of avoiding very high successful bids. 
 
4.4.4 Remuneration type 
The simulation showed two main effects of the chosen remuneration type being a fixed premium 
compared to sliding premium remuneration. Firstly, a fixed premium puts projects located in Denmark in a 
slightly better position, since the projects can then profit from the considerably higher market value factor 
solar PV has in Denmark compared to the German market area. Secondly, a fixed premium leads to higher 
total support payments due to an increased risk for project developers and a resulting higher discount rate. 
==PAGE:55==
Analysis and Discussion 
 
45 
In the light of the goal of auctions to reduce the amount of necessary support payments and to reach a high 
cost-effectiveness (i.e. awarding projects with the lowest generation costs) it should be considered to 
change the type of remuneration.  
On one hand, a sliding premium could have a shield-effect from price signals, which could be seen as a 
disadvantage. On the other hand this effect can allow investors to realise the projects at lower cost of 
capital.  
Changing the remuneration type could help to reduce the WACC and with it also to reduce the level of 
expected bid prices. This could be of special interest in times when the general level of cost of capital for 
solar PV investments is high. This can be the case when for example the long term development regarding 
the support payment policy for the technology is unclear or stable and cheap credit programs are missing  
However, it has to be mentioned that a sliding premium can also easily result in higher overall support costs 
in case of unexpected low electricity market prices. 
4.5 Implications on the auction design for nearshore wind 
Generally it has to be said that the results found in the simulations cannot be transferred quantitatively to 
the application of the nearshore auction. For that, exact price assumptions regarding OPEX and CAPEX 
would have to be implemented in the model. Also, the capacity factors and the MVF would have to be 
adjusted for simulating the other technology. However, some general findings concerning the auction 
design can be qualitatively applied on the nearshore auction. 
 
For the nearshore auctions the prequalified investors are Wpd HOFOR Danish Offshore Consortium, 
European Energy Nearshore Consortium and Vattenfall Vindkraft A/S (Energistyrelsen, 2015a). In this way 
there will be bids from two consortiums and one utility. When applying the found sources for the financing 
cost it can be expected that the internal discount rates will be above 4%. In this case the shorter support 
period of 12 to 16 years will have a lowering effect on the NPV of the total support payments when 
assuming the used price scenario by energinet (compared to a support period of 20 years in the solar PV 
auction). Based on the simulation outcomes, the sliding premium can reduce the WACC and in this case 
reduce the required support levels and total support payments.  
Since there was a prequalification round it is already clear that only three investors will participate in the 
auction. Knowing that, the penalty level cannot reduce the level of competition anymore. The higher non-
compliance penalty can then lead to a higher realisation rate. The simulation showed that the penalty level 
does not have a significant impact on the resulting required support level. 
 
Regarding the price limit of 700 DKK/MWh it can be said that this will not leave a big margin for project 
developers. The simulation of a support period of 15 years for solar PV projects and assuming sliding 
premium remuneration resulted in 887.2 DKK/MWh required support for a German project developer and 
a project location DK-WEST. For a Danish project developer it resulted in 1145.7 DKK/MWh. Both numbers 
are calculated with medium cost assumptions. These numbers give a vague indication that a cap of 700 
DKK/MWh could be quite low. However, the numbers are not directly comparable as described above.

Possible applications and limitations of the developed model 
4.6 Possible applications and limitations of the developed model 
Overall, it can be stated that the model cannot solve the problem of finding correct assumptions for the 
financing costs. Nevertheless it can serve as a tool to show the influence of different implications of the 
support scheme on the financing possibilities of different projects and to quantify the resulting differences 
in the required support level. The results underline the high impact of this parameter which should be 
regarded of having a similar importance on the total project cost as location specific parameters as 
resource availability or cost for land and labour. 
For the future application of cross border auctions the model can serve to identify possible sources of 
competition distortions. In these cases exact data of the involved countries is needed both for technological 
cost and operation assumptions and financing conditions. 
The model can only predict required support levels based on economic calculations. It cannot predict exact 
bid prices or the overall outcome of an auction. In order to do so, an additional step is needed which mainly 
includes strategic considerations and which should take into account the market conditions at the time the 
auction takes place. This could be for example the expected level of competition or the willingness to win in 
an auction due to secondary benefits. Furthermore, a demand curve would have to be modelled and the 
number and size of bids in different groups of specific projects would have to be estimated in order to 
model an overall outcome of a specific auction.

5 Conclusion 
5.1 General conclusions 
When opening an auction to another country there are many factors which can possibly distort the 
competition. Those factors can be either technology specific costs for investment or operation and 
maintenance, differences in revenue generation from the power market due to differences in the capacity 
factor or different market value factors or differences in local taxation and depreciation regulations.  
 
For the specific case of utility scale solar PV auctions in Denmark opened to Germany, these factors level 
each other out when comparing project locations in Denmark with project locations in Germany. On one 
hand a lower tax rate and a higher market value factor are the main preferential conditions for placing a PV 
installation in Denmark. On the other hand slightly lower investment and maintenance cost and a higher 
capacity factor (especially when placing the modules in the south-east of Germany) can be seen as location 
specific cost advantages for projects located in Germany. 
However, some of those factors depend on current market conditions and can change in the future. For the 
market value factor recent research assumes a declining linear dependency of this factor with the overall 
market share of the respective technology in the countries power mix. If the share of solar power in the 
Danish power system increases, the location advantage in the MVF could vanish. Besides the higher 
capacity factor the lower capital investment and O&M costs in Germany mainly stem from lower labour 
costs. On a long term view these conditions could possibly change as well. 
 
A main source of distortion in competitiveness, which could be influenced, can result from the different 
conditions in access to the investment capital. This factor was proven to have a crucial influence on the 
required support level of single renewable energy investment. For the case of solar PV a special credit 
program offered by the German KfW bank gives access to very cheap long term loans. Using a cheap loan 
like this can increase the debt share in the project financing by more than 10% which in combination with a 
low interest rate and a long repayment period of 20 years lowers the internal discount rate of the projects 
significantly. Since the access to the credit program is limited for projects which have a reference to 
Germany, projects located in Denmark and developed by a Danish project developer have to face a higher 
internal discount rate. In an opened auction those projects have to compete with each other even if the 
cost of capital can differ significantly. 
 
The limitation of the opened capacity to 2.4 MW will prevent that projects located in Germany can win the 
whole auctioned capacity even if they might be able to bid lower prices due to the lower financing costs. 
From one point of view this helps to make the opening of the auction politically more acceptable. From 
another point of view it counteracts the basic idea of auctions to award the projects which can deliver the 
electricity with the lowest required support level.  
 
Regarding the nearshore wind auction the results can only be transferred qualitatively. However, the main 
implications are also valid for this auction. Especially the different remuneration type of a sliding premium 
can lead to lower financing costs and lower bid prices. Moreover, the shorter support period can be 
beneficial if the projects have a WACC which lies above the socio economic discount rate of 4%. The

5.2 Answers to the research questions 
After undertaking the analysis, the addressed research questions can be answered as follows: 
- 
How do the costs for solar PV projects differ between Denmark and Germany? 
The investment costs for a solar PV plant are slightly lower in Germany than they are in Denmark. This is 
mainly caused by a lower general level of labour costs which make up approx. 13% of the total CAPEX. The 
same can be said for the OPEX level where labour costs are assumed to stand for 50%. Furhtermore, the 
cost for land lease can be lower in Germany in case that there are special conversion areas available. 
Projects connected to the German grid face slightly higher costs for power balancing, but they do not play a 
significant role in total project costs. 
 
There are differences existing in the financing cost. Based on the simulation conducted in the analysis and 
taking into account recent research in the field of financing cost for renewable energy investments the 
internal discount rate can be above 6% for pure Danish projects (i.e. with a Danish project developer and a 
project location in Denmark) while for projects with a relation to Germany (either project location or 
developer location) the internal discount rate rarely lies above 4%. For capital intensive projects requiring a 
high upfront investment this difference has a strong impact on the required revenues and so on the 
required support level. The reasons for the cost differences are preferential loan conditions in Germany 
and slightly higher cost of equity estimations for Danish project developers. 
- 
Do the expected bid price levels for Danish and German projects differ 
systematically? 
The analysis shows that the expected bid price levels do not differ systematically if the project developers 
have the same access to investment capital. However, it is doubted that pure Danish projects have the 
same access to investment capital for solar PV projects as projects related to Germany. Therefore it is 
expected that the bid prices for Danish projects will be above the ones for German projects unless the 
investor has a relation to Germany and can profit from a preferential loan. However, the bid prices are not 
easily predictable since they cannot be derived directly from the required support levels calculated in the 
simulation. The simulated results will occur most likely if there is a high competition in the auction and the 
bidders try to follow their true costs in the bid formation rather than placing strategic bids.  
- 
How does the remuneration design influence the bid price formation of different 
representative investor groups? 
The chosen fixed premium remuneration can potentially prefer projects located in Denmark since they can 
profit from a higher MVF. This factor depends on the overall market share of solar power in the respective 
electricity system. In Germany the continuous development of solar power can be expected and is already 
announced to amount to 500 MW per year in recent publications of the ministry of economy (BMWI, 
2016a). In this case the MVF for solar PV in Germany can be expected to drop further in the future. In 
Denmark the plans for the development of solar PV power are less clear yet. If the development is expected 
==PAGE:59==
Conclusion 
 
49 
to be rather low the MVF can also be expected to stay on a high level. The resulting cost advantage of PV 
projects placed in Denmark could be partly removed when choosing sliding premium remuneration. 
- 
How can possible market distortions, which result from the opening to Germany, be 
mitigated? 
The largest possible source for distortion in competition which could be identified stems from the different 
access to investment capital and the higher related financing costs in Denmark. A possible mitigation 
measure would be to establish a common credit program for solar PV projects participating in the auction. 
Another possible measure could be to define a more stable development plan for solar PV in Denmark in 
order to strengthen the trust in the technology and reduce the perceived risk. This could help to lower the 
cost of equity in solar PV investments. 
A sliding premium could be a measure to remove the distortion created by the different market value 
factor. A common tax and depreciation regime for projects receiving support from an opened auction could 
further help to remove cost differences created by regulatory differences. 
5.3 Possible changes of the auction design for solar PV 
Not having a price limit in the auction could lead to the situation that very high bids are placed by some 
actors with high return expectations and a resulting high internal discount rate. In case of a total auction 
volume of only 20 MW it can be expected that those bids will not be successful due to a sufficient level of 
competition. However, in future cases with a possible higher auction volume a price limit could help to 
reduce the risk of overcompensation. The maximum bid prices which were seen in the German solar PV 
auction rounds could serve as a reference for designing the price limits. 
 
In order to make sure that awarded projects are built on time an additional penalty payment for delay of 
operation of the same size as the penalty for non-compliance could be introduced. This could be 
implemented as a one-off payment additionally to the shortened support period if the start of operation is 
delayed by more than three years. According to the simulation outcome such a penalty would not 
significantly affect the required support level. However, it should be made sure that the penalty does not 
discourage too many actors to participate in the auction at all to maintain a high competition level. 
Therefore the grace period should be designed long enough, e.g. two year after signing the contract until 
production start.  
 
The analysis showed that the total amount of necessary support payments could be reduced by paying it 
out as a sliding premium instead of a fixed premium. Of course, this depends on the development of the 
market price. Under constant market price assumptions this would not be the case anymore since the 
support payments under a sliding premium could increase drastically if the market price will not increase as 
expected. However, the sliding premium can lower the perceived project risk for investors and can 
therewith lower the internal discount rate. For projects with high upfront investments as solar PV plants 
this can lower the required support level significantly.

Future work to do 
5.4 Future work to do 
The implications of differences in the financing cost can have a big influence on the outcome of opened 
auctions in Europe in general. Recent research on this topic clearly points out large differences in the access 
to capital for investing in renewable energy projects among the European countries. When auctions are 
fully opened this can lead to the situation where all winning projects are located in countries which show 
the most preferential financing conditions even if the internal rate of return is higher at another place. For 
the future it would be worth investigating how cross border auctions can help to level out differences in 
financing costs.  
Furthermore it could be a good idea to think of common credit programs for projects in the auctioned 
technologies which should be established between the opening countries. Based on the undertaken 
analysis this would offer two advantages which are 
 
Reducing the distortion in competition due to different access to investment capital 
 
Facilitate the simulation and analysis of the auction outcomes by establishing more standardised 
financing options 
One main characteristic which makes auctions different to the risk expression in other support mechanisms 
is the inherent allocation risk each project developer faces when participating in an auction.  
In auctions the allocation risk, i.e. the risk of not winning in an auction and not being contracted for a 
project at all, in a way counteracts with the implications of a higher variance in expected return. If the bid 
price is put higher due to a higher expected variance of the return this increases at the same time the 
allocation risk. In this light it will be increasingly important to combine cash flow modelling with game 
theoretic approaches where the allocation risk can be included when it comes to modelling auctions for 
renewable energy support. This will put a higher focus on the position of the individual investor in the 
market compared to the competitors. The optimisation approach which was used in the model to find a 
trade-off between debt share and bid price level could be expended to also take a trade-off between 
allocation risk and risk of return into account.
