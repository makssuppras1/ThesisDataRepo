Method for Identification of Dominant Oscillatory Modes of a
Type-IV Wind Turbine Generator
Miguel Albrecht Paschoarelli
Abstract
This thesis proposes a measurement-based method for identifying dominant oscillatory modes focusing on
type-IV wind turbine generator data. While a small-signal state-space model of the wind turbine generator
can be used for stability assessments, it cannot investigate specific oscillation events measured by the data
logging system of an operational wind turbine generator (WTG) or wind power plant. This thesis addresses
this limitation using the Prony Analysis and the Matrix Pencil to extract the modal information directly
from the system’s response to an oscillation event. It begins by validating the accuracy of the developed
Python algorithm against standardised test systems. Next, data from the wind turbine Electromagnetic
Transient simulation model in PSCAD™is used to identify its oscillatory modes.
The results are then
compared with those obtained from the small-signal stability state-space model of the type-IV wind turbine
in MATLAB™. This helps establish correlations of which wind turbine converter controller contributes to
the analysed oscillation responses. Subsequently, the identification methods are applied to real wind turbine
systems: a Grid-Converter test bench, a Nacelle test bench, and an Offshore Wind Power Plant.
The
results demonstrate that the developed Prony and Pencil algorithms can reconstruct the analysed signals
using only dominant oscillatory modes. The framework developed in this thesis can be valuable for WTG
manufacturers in comparing WTG model responses with real prototypes and for power system operators
in evaluating stability metrics of oscillation events that occurred in the grid.
Furthermore, this tool is
extendable and useful in identifying the stability properties of other active devices in power systems, such
as high-voltage direct current equipment, photovoltaic power plants, and black-box models, benefitting the
power systems sector.

Chapter 1
Introduction
This chapter discusses the growing importance of wind energy for future electricity production and the
challenges associated with assessing the stability margins of operational wind turbine generators (WTG).
To address these challenges, this master’s thesis proposes the use of a measurement-based approach that
employs the Prony and Pencil methods for identifying oscillatory modes. Additionally, it includes a brief
literature review, outlines the objectives and tasks for developing this approach, and presents the thesis
structure.
1.1
Background
To address the impact of climate change, a significant percentage of future energy generation is planned to
come from renewable sources [1]. At the 28th United Nations Climate Change Conference (COP28) in 2023,
nearly 200 governments agreed to triple the global renewable energy installed capacity by 2030, aiming to
limit global warming to 1.5 °C. Wind energy is expected to play a crucial role in the energy transition, with
a target of producing over 35 % of global electricity, compared to the current 7 % [2]. Figure 1.1 shows the
historical development of total wind turbine installations in GW.
As the share of wind power in the electricity generation mix increases, grid codes increasingly require wind
power plants (WPP) to provide system stability support similar to conventional power stations with syn-
chronous generators. Grid codes are technical specifications that define the requirements for any facility
connected to electricity grids. Adapting these grid code requirements for WPPs is an ongoing process, and
they are expected to continue evolving [3].
The current trend indicates that as the number of operational WTG and WPP increases, it becomes more
important to conduct grid compliance studies regularly. However, assessing a WTG’s or a WPP’s overall
compliance over its lifetime is a complex task due to factors such as the upgradability of parameters, dif-
ferent control software versions, changing grid characteristics, and eventual changes in equipment hardware
characteristics [4]. Figure 1.2 illustrates the timeline from the WTG design to the WPP operation phase,

1.2
Oscillatory Mode Identification Methods
Throughout the lifetime of a WTG, a large amount of voltage and current measurement data is recorded.
However, this data is not fully utilized for monitoring the dynamics of operational WTGs. Utilizing this
data to calculate the turbine’s oscillatory modes and parameters, including frequency and damping ratio,
could provide valuable insights into its stability margins and potential changes over time.
WTG manufacturers typically use analytical models and offline tools such as the WTG’s state-space model to
calculate their power converter controllers’ oscillatory modes and stability parameters. A state-space model
represents a dynamic system using a set of input, output, and state variables related by simultaneous first-
order differential equations. In this thesis, a small-signal state space model is used, incorporating relevant

control functions and blocks from the WTG software transformed into the frequency (Laplace) domain and
connected to the state-space model of the grid. This linearized time-invariant state-space representation, in
this thesis referred to as the Frequency Domain Model (FDM) [5], is utilized to perform small signal stability
analysis and frequency-domain analysis on a WTG or on a WPP system. It allows for a detailed analysis of
the dynamics involved in modes, separating a system into components for individual analysis and identifying
stability margins.
However, creating an adequate reduced-order and linearized model for operating wind turbines or wind
power plant systems can be quite complex, especially when certain parameters must be accurately known
for stability assessments. The eigenvalue approach obtained from the state-space matrices of the system
requires the full system model information [6]. Models can’t derive information from specific oscillation
events occurring in the power system. On the other hand, measurement-based methods have the advantage
of being applicable for identifying oscillatory modes for active devices connected to a power system, such
as WTGs, high-voltage direct current (HVDC) equipment, photovoltaics (PV) power plants and black-box
models 1 from manufacturers.
Developing a measurement data-driven tool capable of calculating WTG oscillatory modes can potentially
have a valuable industrial application for operating wind power plants and serve as a method for evaluating
stability metrics, such as frequency and damping ratio.
The results could also contribute to academic
research and be used as a benchmark against the existing FDM. Figure 1.3 shows how the measurement-

1.3
Literature Review
In [11] and in [12], the Prony analysis, the Matrix Pencil method, and the Eigensystem Realization Algorithm
are discussed as the three major methods for identifying power system eigenvalues from measurement data.
One limitation of the identification methods is that they can only compute the modes present in the signals
used for identification. However, their advantage lies in their applicability in a WTG operating system, where
it may be too complex to produce an adequate reduced-order and linearized model, and some parameters
are not known with sufficient accuracy. In such cases, one can extract the modal information directly from
the system’s response to a perturbation composed of several modes that could be identified.
In the literature, various modifications and variations of the Prony method have been discussed, such as
those presented in [13], [14], and [15]. Similarly, variations of the Matrix Pencil method have been proposed,
as seen in [16] and in [17]. Despite these variations, the core principles of the Prony and Pencil methods
remain unchanged.
These variations distinguish themselves by adapting the identification method to a
specific application.
The present study develops a Python algorithm for the standard core calculation of the Prony and Pencil
methods in its own way and introduces additional steps. These steps include initialising the data processing

1.4
Objective and Tasks
The primary objective of this thesis is to develop a method for identifying the dominant oscillatory modes
exhibited by the converter controllers of type-IV wind turbine generators to evaluate their stability metrics.
The methodology to achieve this objective is summarized in the following tasks:
1. Validate the developed Prony and Pencil algorithms for accuracy against standardized test systems
such as the two-area Kundur power system [18].
2. Use the developed algorithms for identifying oscillatory modes exhibited by a WTG EMT simulation
model in PSCAD. Several oscillation cases are processed where phase jump perturbations are applied to
the WTG EMT model, and the dominant oscillatory modes of these oscillation responses are calculated.
• Analyze the identified oscillatory modes and compare these results with those obtained by the
wind turbine state-space model results.
3. Apply the identification methods to real type-IV wind turbine converters.
4. Finally, use the Prony and Pencil methods to identify oscillation modes from an oscillation seen by an
offshore wind power plant.
1.5
Thesis Structure
The structure of this thesis is as follows.
Chapter 2 provides a detailed discussion of the methods for
identifying system eigenvalues based on measurements. Following that, the existing simulation models of a
type-IV wind turbine used in this work and their validation process are explained in Chapter 3. Chapter 4
gives a detailed explanation of how the algorithms are developed and the main analysis criteria. Subsequently,
the results are presented and discussed in chapter 5. Finally, an overall conclusion is drawn in Chapter 6,
and potential future work will be suggested.

Chapter 2
Measurement-based Methods for
Oscillatory Modes Identification
This chapter details the two measurement-based techniques that have been implemented: the Prony and the
Pencil methods. It also presents the mathematical tools required for the development of these methods.
2.1
Mathematical concepts and tools
The following subsections introduce relevant concepts such as the Toeplitz, the Hankel and the Vandermonde
matrices and techniques like the Single Value Decomposition and the Peseude-Inverse. More details about
these matrices and matrices operations can be found in [19].
Toeplitz matrix
Matrices with constant entries along each diagonal arise in many applications and are called Toeplitz matrices.

The Frequency Domain Model (FDM) consists of a small-signal state-space model for the converter grid-side
converter utilized for small-signal stability and frequency domain analyses. It incorporates relevant electrical
components, network-bridge control blocks, digital filters, and delays to investigate the WTG converter’s

It is linearized
around a specific operating point, which makes it representative of small-signal behaviour at and around
that particular point. The resulting system matrix is used to derive the eigenvalues. The eigenvalues, termed
“modes”, indicate the frequency and damping of oscillatory components in the transient response [32]. More
details about this specific WTG FDM model can be found in [5].

EMT Model
The industrial EMT model is a wind turbine simulation developed using PSCAD™. It operates in the time

Considerations on the WTG Models
The key takeaways of this section regarding the two main type-IV WTG models utilised in this work are
that:
• The FDM is described as the mathematical derivations of the wind turbine control code and represents
the small-signal behaviour of the network bridge in the frequency domain.
• The EMT model is a time-domain wind turbine simulation developed using PSCAD™with discrete-time
EMT models of power hardware and power system components.
In the EMT model, it is only possible to see the sum of all components interacting. In the FDM, a parti-
cipation factor analysis can be performed to identify active components for a given mode of concern and to
detect margins to instability.
Concerning these models’ validation, this section has explained that the manufacturer of this study’s wind
turbine has successfully:
• Conducted a verification of the FDM against the EMT model in PSCAD.
• Validated the EMT model against an operational WTG in [37].
• Verified the FDM against the WTG converter in a converter test rig with grid and generator emulation
in [33].
Developing a method that uses simulation data from the WTG EMT model to identify its oscillatory modes
contributes to understanding the Wind Turbine Converter Control (WTCC) response to external disturb-
ances. These oscillatory modes can then be compared with the results obtained from the Frequency Domain
Model (FDM)’s state space analysis, with the expectation that there will be some correlation with the actual
behaviour of a type-IV Wind Turbine Generator (WTG), as both models were verified against a real wind
turbine converter.
It’s important to note that the FDM and the EMT model are simplified representations of a real type-IV
WTG connected to a power grid. The FDM is a linearized state-space model based on a specific operating
point, while the PSCAD model is a dynamic EMT simulation model. Because of these differences, it is not
realistic to expect the two models to produce matching results in terms of identifying oscillatory modes.

Define Oscillation Start and End
The algorithm should only process data from the interval when the oscillation begins and ends because
the data selection strongly impacts the mode identification results. Processing data outside of this interval
could lead to incorrect mode identification. As stated in [38], the data must be carefully selected to provide
accurate information about the system’s behaviour during the disturbance.
A method was developed to identify the start and end times of oscillations in signal data. The cases file is
a time series data where each signal is indexed with timestamps. The calculation works as follows:
1. First, the signal’s standard deviation is calculated using a rolling window to evaluate its variability
over time.
2. Detection of Oscillation Start: The algorithm then searches for the beginning of an oscillation. This is
done by identifying the point where the signal’s standard deviation significantly exceeds the average
standard deviation up to that point. The start of an oscillation is detected when the standard deviation
at a given time point i surpasses a defined threshold. This threshold factor helps distinguish relevant
oscillations from normal fluctuations in the signal.
3. Detection of Oscillation End: The end of oscillation is determined when the standard deviation drops
below a certain threshold. This threshold is calculated by multiplying the mean standard deviation
of the window just before the current point by another specified threshold. The algorithm stops the
oscillation if the standard deviation at the current position is lower than this calculated threshold,
indicating a stabilization or decrease in signal variability.
The dataset is then filtered to include only the data points that occur between the oscillation start and
end.
Data Normalization
The signal amplitude is normalized to ensure the algorithm starts with y(0) = 0.
This is achieved by
subtracting the initial amplitude value from the average of a pre-defined number of samples. These samples
represent the steady state condition before the perturbation is applied. States normalization is employed in
a variety of dynamic systems and is a well-established method in the study of such systems [39].

Low Pass Filter
The discrete data patterns and noises do not originate from the dominant dynamics of the system but rather
from the model solution, sampling, and switching dynamics. These can cause issues while identifying the
oscillatory modes. Thus, it is helpful to use an appropriate low-pass digital filter to smooth out the data in
use. The filter was designed empirically and has the following settings:
• The cutoff frequency was set at 300 Hz to keep the signal components of interest while reducing
frequencies above this level.
• The sampling frequency (fs) is calculated based on the inverse of the timestep ( 1
∆t), ensuring adapt-
ability to varying data acquisition rates. The Nyquist frequency, which is half the sampling rate, is
used as a reference point for normalizing the cutoff frequency, allowing the filter to be applied across
different sampling conditions.
• The Butterworth was empirically chosen because of its flat frequency response in the passband, min-
imizing distortion within the frequency range of interest. A filter order of 5 was selected to balance the
filter’s sharpness, its ability to distinguish between the passband and the stopband, and computational
efficiency.
Define Iteration Range and Step
It’s important to carefully choose the iteration range and step to identify the mode and minimize processing
time accurately. The algorithm goes through various values to find the best one for reconstructing the signal.
It iterates over L values for the Pencil method and nmodes, the number of modes, or polynomial order,
variable in the Prony algorithm in Python. As stated in [15], Prony’s method performance is associated with
the correct choice of the polynomial order to obtain satisfactory parameter estimation results of oscillating
signals.
For the Pencil method, the goal is to find the best value of the pencil parameter L to get the most accurate
result from a signal of length N. The method loops through a range of L values and checks which one has the
lowest root-mean-square error (RMSE) compared to the original signal. According to [40], when L = N/2,
the method’s performance is nearly as good as the optimal Cramer-Rao bound, which estimates the best
possible result a technique can achieve in a noisy environment. Therefore, the proposed method iterates
through L values around N/2, starting at N/2 and ending at 2 × N/3, which are considered good choices
according to [41]. The same technique was applied to find the optimal nmodes for the Prony method.
After several attempts, it was found that lower L or nmodes values result in less accurate signal reconstruction,
while larger values lead to better accuracy but increase the time required to execute the method. So, to
decrease the execution time, one can increase the iteration step from 1 to 3. However, a greater step could
skip valuable Pencil parameters or Prony order that could provide a good reconstruction.

4.3.3
Mode Identification Statistical Analysis
This section presents a statistical analysis created to compare the identification method’s results with the
FDM modes based on metrics. A criterion is defined to determine if a mode identified by the Prony or Pencil
methods matches an existing mode in the FDM. According to this criterion, each FDM mode has a circle
drawn around it, and a mode identified by the method is considered a match if it falls inside this circle. This
page 38 of 60
==PAGE:48==
Chapter 4. Methodology
concept was developed by comparing the method with the FDM results. The comparison showed that while
the identified modes may not always align perfectly with those calculated by the FDM, they are very close
in distance on the Cartesian plane. Therefore, it is necessary to measure their distance in order to determine
a match.
Dsitance Between Identified Modes and FDM Modes
Each mode is defined as a coordinate of its frequency and damping factor values. These coordinates are
then used to compute the distance between the FDM and identified modes. The Euclidean distance (D)
determines the distance between the FDM’s mode frequency and damping factor coordinates and those
identified by the Pencil method. The Euclidean distance between the identified mode and each FDM is
computed for the Pencil method using the formula:
DFDM–Pencil =
q
(σPencil – σFDM)2 + (fPencil – fFDM)2
(4.16)
Similarly, the distance between the identified modes and the FDM mode is calculated for the Prony method
as follows:
DFDM–Prony =
q
(σProny – σFDM)2 + (fProny – fFDM)2
(4.17)
where D denotes the Cartesian distance, σ denotes the damping factor, and f denotes the frequency.
A circle is formed around the FDM modes based on a defined threshold. Modes belong to the same circle if
their distance falls within this threshold.
Identification Rate
The identification rate refers to the percentage of identified modes that match an FDM mode within the
clustering threshold. Identification rates for each signal and FDM mode are calculated on a per-case basis
and globally across all cases. To calculate the identification rate, the ratio of successful matches to the total
dominant modes that were set to be identified during the method initialization is computed. For instance,
if the Prony/Pencil algorithm is used to reconstruct a signal with 10 dominant modes, and only 2 identified
modes are close enough to be inside an FDM circle. The identification rate of this simulation is 20%.

• The FDM is linearized around a specific operating point, representing small-signal behaviour near that

identification methods input oscillation data is generated in the time domain in the EMT model.
• Since different perturbations excite different modes and only phase jump perturbations have been
applied so far, the results are limited to that specific type of disturbance, as only specific converter
controllers are excited by a phase jump.

5.2.3
Discussion on G-CTB and Nacelle testing results
The Pencil identification method’s results for the phase jump tests applied at the G-CTB and the Nacelle
test bench are satisfactory because they are similar to those obtained using the phase jumps from the EMT
simulation. In both cases, Mode E, which is related to the PLL, is the most accurately identified. It is
important to note that the presence of noise in the measurement did not negatively impact the quality of the
curve fitting. This is because the signal reconstruction was set in the Prony and Pencil Python algorithms
to be carried out only on seven dominant modes. As a result, small oscillations caused by noise were not
identified as a mode.
Furthermore, even though both G-CTB and Nacelle test bench tests performed a high phase angle jump
amplitude of 20°, the results of the identified modes could still be compared with those from the FDM.
It is explained in Section 5.1 why a precise match between the identified modes by the small-signal state-
space model (FDM) and the EMT model should not be expected due to the nature of the FDM model,
which is linearized around a specific operating point. The higher the perturbation, the more challenging
it is to compare the EMT model with the FDM. This means that when transitioning the data input from
the EMT model to real WTCC tests, there’s even less expectation to identify oscillatory modes within the
range calculated by the FDM, as the higher perturbation applied at the EMT model was phase jumps of
10°. Despite these assumptions, Figures 5.10 and 5.14 show that some identified modes by the identification
method are within the proximity circle around the FDM mode, indicating the robustness of the developed
identification method.

6.1
Conclusion
The thesis presents two measurement-based methods for identifying dominant oscillatory modes using Prony
Analysis and the Matrix Pencil Method.
The research began by creating the algorithms for these methods in Python and testing their accuracy with
simple systems. Then, the algorithms were used to identify oscillatory modes in simulation data generated by
the WTG EMT model in PSCAD. Symmetrical phase angle jumps were applied to the grid voltage to which
the WTG is connected at the various operating points, resulting in a total of 12 EMT simulation cases with
different SCRs and phase jump magnitudes. These cases were processed using the Prony and the Matrix
Pencil methods to identify dominant oscillation modes exhibited by the Wind Turbine Converter Controller
(WTCC). An analysis was then conducted to compare these results with those obtained using the FDM, a
small-signal state-space model in MATLAB of the WTG. The overall performance of the Prony and Matrix
Pencil methods in identifying specific modes of the FDM was found to be 31 % and 29 %, respectively. It
was discussed that a precise match should not be expected due to the nature of the FDM model, which is
linearized around a specific operating point, whereas the EMT is a time-domain model.
Furthermore, the identification methods were applied to process real WTCC response data to a phase jump
applied during tests at the Grid-Converter test bench and the Nacelle test bench. The main correlation found
between these WTCC responses and the identified modes was between the PLL and a mode in the range of
2 Hz and 40 % damping ratio. This mode was frequently identified because the perturbation applied in the
simulation cases was a phase angle jump. When a sudden phase jump is applied to the grid voltage where
the WTG is connected to the PLL, the controller quickly responds to realign the output phase, explaining
the relationship between the excited mode and the PLL.

Chapter 6. Conclusion
Lastly, measurement data from an operating Offshore Wind Power Plant was used to identify the oscillatory
modes of power system events. The results demonstrate that the developed Prony and Pencil algorithms
can reconstruct the analysed signals using only dominant oscillatory modes.
Overall, the measurement data-driven methods have potential industrial applications for WTG manufactur-
ers, allowing them to compare the WTG models’ response to oscillation events with real WTG prototypes.
The methods’ capability of calculating stability metrics, such as frequency and damping ratio, is also extend-
able and useful in identifying stability properties of other active devices connected to a power system as it
applies to black-box models, and this could be beneficial to power system operators and the power systems
sector.
6.2
Future works
The present section suggests several research directions to facilitate the tool’s implementation.
Dominant mode identification method
As described in section 4.1.2, the RMSE is the metric used to evaluate the quality of the curve fitting. The
assumption was that the lowest RMSE of all iterations would give the best fit. However, it was learned
that for certain Pencil or Prony orders, having the lowest RMSE does not always mean having the best
mode identification. Therefore, combining the mode energy calculation with other metrics could improve
the identification method’s overall performance.
Simulating other types of perturbation
As discussed in section 5.1, since most of the analyzed data from type-IV wind turbines are in response to
a phase jump perturbation, the results are limited to modes excited by this type of perturbation. Other
perturbations could include frequency step response or a grid voltage step dip. This approach could reveal
more findings in terms of establishing correlations between the wind turbine converter controllers’ responses
to different kinds of oscillation events and analysed signals.
Using other data beyond the positive sequence component
As explained in section 4.3.2, the positive sequence component is the main data type used for analyses. The
WTG control is based on the synchronous reference frame current control scheme. The voltage and current
are expressed in the dq reference frame with their corresponding direct axis and quadrature axes components.
Because of that, it is worth further investigation to apply measurement-based methods like Prony and Pencil
inputting WTG signals in this reference frame. While the signals’ samples in the dq-frame could be derived
using the three-phase voltage and current measurements and tracking the PLL angle, attempts to reconstruct
the dq-frame signal were not successful. It was also challenging to derive Alpha-Beta frame signals from the
page 55 of 60
==PAGE:65==
Chapter 6. Conclusion
voltage and current measurements. It’s recommended that processing signals in the dq-frame and alpha-beta
frame be tried for future work.
Real-time monitoring
The method for identifying dominant oscillatory modes of type-IV wind turbine generators could be more
widely used in the industry if it could process real-time measurement data from WTGs or wind power plants.
For instance, a setup could be developed where the Prony or Pencil methods are periodically applied to track
the frequency and damping ratio of oscillations observed in the WTG and active devices connected to the
power grid. This could provide valuable insights into the stability margins and potential changes over time.
