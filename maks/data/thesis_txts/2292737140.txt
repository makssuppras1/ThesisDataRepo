Abstract 
Worldwide the incidences of cancer increases every year. A strong immune system is important for fighting 
cancer, and cancer immunotherapy is gaining increasing interest as a means of re-establishing or improving 
the ability of the immune system to fight cancer. Studies have shown that liposomes with a certain positive 
surface charge specifically target monocytes of the immune system. Thus, by encapsulating 
immunotherapeutics, like toll-like receptor agonists, in these liposomes, these carriers can be used to 
activate monocytes and thereby boost the immune response against tumors. In the current Master Project 
such cationic liposomes containing the toll-like receptor 7 agonist TMX-202 were studied.  
To improve treatment effects many immunotherapeutic drugs are tested in combination with radiation, 
and various clinical trials are currently testing the combination of different immunotherapeutic drugs and 
radiation. Thus, the drug delivery system comprising cationic liposomes with TMX-202 was tested in 
combination with radiation in C57 black 6 mice bearing B16 melanoma tumors. The survival time was 
increased significantly (P<0.01) and in a similar degree for the mice receiving single dose radiation (8 Gy) 
combined with liposomes and the mice receiving single dose radiation (8 Gy) as a monotherapy compared 
with the control mice. The effect seemed to be due to the radiation rather than the combined treatment, 
and overall it seemed that a higher radiation dose was needed for a more effective treatment when using 
this cancer model.  
In the current Master Project it was demonstrated that the cationic liposomes associated relatively fast 
with blood cells in human blood and that the liposomes exhibited a high degree of association to blood 
cells. Furthermore, the cationic liposomes containing TMX-202 were able to induce systemic immune 
activation in BALB/C mice by inducing production of the cytokines IL-12p40 and IL-6, which indicated that 
the liposomes were taken up efficiently and that TMX-202 was successfully delivered to the endosomes 
where toll-like receptor 7 is situated. Moreover, positron emission tomography was used to examine the 
distribution of radiolabeled cationic liposomes in BALB/C mice to investigate the accumulation of the 
liposomes in different tissues and organs. It was shown that the cationic liposomes accumulated in spleen 
and liver and that they were quickly cleared from the blood circulation. In both the positron emission 
tomography and the cytokine production studies, PEGylated cationic liposomes were included. The 
PEGylated cationic liposomes demonstrated a significantly longer circulation time than non-PEGylated 
cationic liposomes. Furthermore, the PEGylated cationic liposomes induced similar systemic immune 
activation as the non-PEGylated cationic liposomes. Hence these results indicate that the PEGylated 
cationic liposomes might be as potent as the non-PEGylated cationic liposomes in vivo.     
Overall, the results indicated that the cationic liposomes associated with blood cells to a high degree, 
presumably monocytes, and that they despite a relatively fast clearance from the blood stream were able 
to induce systemic immune responses in BALB/C mice.

Resumé 
På verdensplan stiger antallet af kræfttilfælde hvert år. Et stærkt immunsystem er vigtigt for at bekæmpe 
kræft, og immunterapi tilskriver sig en stigende interesse som en måde hvorpå immunsystemet kan 
genoprettes og forbedres, så det kan bekæmpe kræften. Studier har vist at liposomer med en bestemt 
positiv overfladeladning targeterer immunforsvarets monocytter. Ved at indkapsle immunterapi, som toll-
like receptor agonister, i disse liposomer, vil de kunne blive brugt til at aktivere monocytterne og derved 
forøge immunresponset mod kræften.  I dette kandidat projekt blev positivt ladede liposomer, som 
indeholdte toll-like receptor 7 agonisten TMX-202, studeret.  
For at forbedre behandlings effektiviteten bliver mange immunterapier testet i kombination med 
strålingsterapi, og forskellige kliniske forsøg er i øjeblikket i gang med at teste kombinations-effekten af 
immunterapi og stråling. Det var derfor også interessant at teste det omtalte liposom drug delivery system, 
som består af liposomer med TMX-202, i sammenhæng med stråling. Dette blev testet i C57 black 6 mus 
med B16 melanoma tumorer. For mus som modtog enkelt dosis stråling (8 Gy) kombineret med liposo-
merne og mus som modtog enkelt dosis stråling (8 Gy) alene forøgedes overlevelsestiden signifikant 
(P<0.01) og i lige stor grad, når der sammenlignedes med kontrol-musene.  Resultaterne tydede således på 
at effekten primært var grundet strålingen og ikke den kombinerede behandling. Overordnet virkede det til 
at en højere strålingsdosis er nødvendig for en effektiv behandling af denne type tumor. 
I dette kandidat projekt blev det desuden demonstreret at positivt ladede liposomer både associerede 
hurtigt og i høj grad til blodceller. Herudover blev det vist at TMX-202 liposomerne var i stand til at 
inducere et systemisk immunrespons i BALB/C mus ved at stimulere cytokin-produktionen af IL-12p40 og IL-
6. Dette indikerede at liposomerne effektivt blev taget op af blodcellerne og at TMX-202 blev bragt til 
endosomerne, hvor toll-like receptor 7 befinder sig.  Derudover blev positron emission tomography brugt til 
at undersøge fordelingen af radiomærkede liposomer i BALB/C mus, for at undersøge ophobningen af 
liposomer i forskelligt væv og organer. Det blev vist at positivt ladede liposomer ophobede sig i milten og 
leveren og at liposomerne hurtigt blev fjernet fra blodcirkulationen. PEGylerede liposomer blev desuden 
inkluderet i både positron emission tomography og cytokin studierne. PEGylerede positivt ladede liposomer 
udviste en længere cirkulationstid i blodet end ikke-PEGylerede positivt ladede liposomer. Udover dette 
blev det vist at PEGylerede og ikke-PEGylerede liposomer inducerede en sammenlignelig systemisk 
immunaktivering. Disse resultater indikerer således at de PEGylerede liposomer måske er lige så potente 
som de ikke-PEGylerede liposomer in vIvo.  
Overordnet, indikerede resultaterne at de positivt ladede liposomer associerede til blodcellerne i høj grad i 
blodet, formentlig til monocyter, og at selvom de relativt hurtigt blev fjernet fra blodet, var de i stand til at 
stimulere et systemisk immunrespons i BALB/C mus.

Introduction 
Cancer is a major research area since the number of people diagnosed with cancer increases every year in 
the whole world. In Denmark, around 35.000 people are diagnosed with cancer every year (numbers from 
2008-2012). The main reasons why the number of new cancer cases keeps increasing is lifestyle changes 
and the fact that people on average grow older. In fact, more than half of the cancer cases are diagnosed in 
people that are more than 65 years old. For people diagnosed with cancer, one of the biggest risks of dying 
is that the cancer spreads to other parts of the body, referred to as metastasis, since more than 90 % of 
people dying from cancer die due to metastasis (The Danish Cancer Society, 2015; American Cancer Society, 
2013).   
For many years radiotherapy (RT) and surgery were the main methods used for treatment of cancer. 
However, the treatment methods have changed considerably during the last decades, and the tumor 
microenvironment appears to be the cornerstone of new cancer therapies, including immunotherapy 
(Mellman et al., 2011; Urruticoechea et al., 2010). Immunotherapy is a way of using the body’s own 
immune system to treat diseases. Hence, cancer immunotherapy aims at re-establishing or improving the 
ability of the immune system to fight cancer, and the therapy has gained interest as a strategy to improve 
survival and promote recovery of patients with metastatic cancer. Many immunotherapeutic drugs are 
emerging, of which some have been tested in phase I-III clinical trials, and some have been approved for 
treatment of metastatic cancer (Brahmer et al., 2012; Hodi et al., 2010; Robert et al., 2011, 2014, 2015; U.S. 
Food and Drug Administration). Moreover, to improve treatment effects many immunotherapeutic drugs 
are tested in combination with RT or other cancer therapies (Crittenden et al., 2015).  
In general, the main goals of disease treatment are to enhance patient recovery and reduce harmful side-
effects associated with the treatment. However, harmful side-effects are often associated with cancer 
therapy, and various approaches have been examined to selectively deliver the drugs to specific tissues, 
organs or cells and thereby overcome some of the side-effects. Nanoparticle carriers, like liposomes, are 
suitable for this purpose, and liposomes have gained increased interest as drug delivery systems over the 
last decades (Immordino et al., 2006).  
Monta Biosciences is a start-up company that focuses on cancer immunotherapy. By using liposomes, the 
company has developed a drug delivery technology that can deliver immunotherapeutic drugs to 
monocytes. The company’s lead candidate comprises a positively charged liposome carrying a Toll-like 
receptor (TLR)-7 agonist, which is an effective stimulator of innate immune responses. The hypothesis is 
that using the lead candidate along with radiotherapy will result in induced anti-tumor immune responses 
and immunological memory against the tumor. Furthermore, it is believed that an immunological memory 
against the tumor can promote regression of metastasis originating from the primary tumor. Thus, the 
theory behind the combined treatment of the liposome formulation and radiotherapy is that monocytes 
will become activated by the TLR-7 agonist, differentiate into dendritic cells (DCs) that take up tumor 
antigens (provided by radiotherapy), migrate to the draining lymph node and thereby initiate adaptive 
immune responses and immunological memory against the tumor antigens. Hence, this will boost the host 
immune system to fight the cancer by recognizing and eliminating the primary tumor and metastasis. It is 
hypothesized that the uptake of liposomes is complement mediated (MonTa, unpublished data).

Aim 
This Master Project is part of some studies conducted by MonTa Biosciences aiming at moving their lead 
liposome formulation towards Phase I clinical trials.  
The aim of this Master Project was to examine pharmacodynamics and pharmacokinetics of cationic 
liposomes, like the lead candidate of MonTa Biosciences, in human blood, mouse blood and mouse cancer 
models. This was done by testing the effect of cationic liposomes in combination with radiation on tumor 
growth and overall survival in mice with B16 melanoma tumors. Furthermore, a study was performed to 
examine the blood circulation of different liposomes and their accumulation in organs and tissues of mice. 
Moreover, since it was found in previous studies (Johansen et al., 2015) that cationic liposomes with certain 
formulations are specifically taken up by monocytes, the current study investigated the proportion of these 
liposomes that associated to blood cells, compared to liposomes in the plasma fraction of blood. 
Furthermore, cytokine profile studies were conducted to check whether monocytes were activated both ex 
vivo (in humans and mice) and in vivo (in mice).  
Besides cationic liposomes, neutral and anionic liposomes were included in the studies for comparisons. 
Furthermore, PEGylated liposomes were tested in some of the studies, since it is assumed that non-
PEGylated liposomes might target or bind other cell types than monocytes, e.g. endothelial cells.

Background 
The Immune System and Cancer 
The human body is constantly exposed to various pathogens. In order for the body to combat these 
pathogens it has evolved the immune system which is a complex defense system. The immune system can 
be divided into the innate immune system, also called the first line of defense, and the adaptive immune 
system. Innate immune cells comprise mast cells, macrophages, natural killer (NK) cells and DCs, which in 
response to disturbance of tissue homeostasis, e.g. by pathogens, recruit circulating lymphocytes to the 
tissue by secreting chemokines, cytokines and other soluble factors. Antigen presenting cells (APCs), like 
DCs, sample antigens in the tissue and migrate to the draining lymph nodes where they present antigens to 
T-cells, and thereby activate naïve T-cells that recognize the presented antigens. This initiates adaptive 
immune responses involving antigen-specific CD4+ T-helper (Th) cells, CD8+ cytotoxic T-cells (often referred 
to as cytotoxic T-lymphocytes (CTLs)) and B-cells that target the invading pathogen. Furthermore, immune 
responses by adaptive cells lead to immunological memory (Johansson et al., 2008). CD4+ T-cells can 
differentiate into different subsets, and the differentiation is highly affected by cytokines present 
throughout as well as after the antigen presentation. The main CD4+ T-cell subsets include Th1, Th2, Th17 
and regulatory T-cells (Treg), which secrete diverse cytokines and possess different functions (Johansson et 
al., 2008).  
The purpose of the immune system is to protect the body from foreign agents and to recognize and 
eliminate malignant cells. It is believed that immune protection mechanisms might be defect in people who 
develop cancer. This theory is supported by the fact that people with immunodeficiency disorders or 
patients treated with immunosuppressive medicine have a high risk of cancer development (Fearon, 1997; 
Penn, 1999). Furthermore, some common risk factors for cancer, besides genetic susceptibility to cancer, 
include age, nutrition, alcohol consumption, smoking, stress and depression, and many of these factors are 
associated with immune system impairments (Perera, 1997; Reiche et al., 2004). Moreover, people with 
familial breast cancer and patients with advanced cancer have been found to have low NK cell activity, 
which indicate that people with decreased NK cell activity have an elevated risk of developing cancer 
(Whiteside, 2006). Overall, accumulating evidence from various studies indicate that loss of host immune 
competence is an essential risk factor of cancer. Thus, a strong immune system is important for fighting 
cancer.     
Immune Suppression in Cancer 
Animal cancer models and cancer patients have shown that the body often produces an immune response 
to the tumor. However, since tumor antigens often are identical or closely related to self-antigens, 
tolerance to self often hinders efficient anti-tumor immune responses, even though the host is 
immunocompetent (Whiteside, 2006). Besides tolerance to self, effective immune responses are also 
prevented by various tumor immunosuppressive mechanisms, some of which will be elaborated below.    
In the microenvironment of tumors, infiltrating immune cells like macrophages, T-cells, DCs and NK cells 
interact with the tumor cells. However, tumors often evade interactions with immune cells by different 
methods. Tumor cells can for example shed surface antigens or decrease expression of specific molecules 
required for interaction with cells of the immune system. Thus, the tumor cells can avoid the immune 
system by being both poor immune cell stimulators and poor targets for tumor-specific immune cells 
==PAGE:13==
13 
 
(Whiteside, 2006). Tumor cells often make erroneous processing of tumor-associated antigens (TAAs) 
leading to incomplete TAA peptides or peptides that cannot fit in the human leukocyte antigen (HLA) class I 
molecule. Consequently, the HLA class I molecule is not expressed on the surface, or it is expressed, but not 
recognizable by CTLs, thereby facilitating immune surveillance escape (Whiteside, 2006). 
       
In patients with squamous cell carcinoma of the head and neck (SCCHN) immune abnormalities such as 
defects in or down-regulation of antigen processing machinery (APM) components and HLA class I 
molecules are frequently found. The impairments probably affect the generation of TAA-specific immune 
responses and thereby facilitate escape of the cancer cells from immune surveillance (Ferris et al., 2005; 
Meissner et al., 2005). 
Furthermore, the immune escape strategies of tumors also include secretion of the immunosuppressive 
molecules interleukin (IL)-10, transforming growth factor (TGF)-β and adenosine, down-regulated surface 
expression of co-stimulatory molecules, and expression of programmed cell-death ligand (PD-L) and Fas 
ligand (Fas-L) (Oleinika et al., 2013; Whiteside, 2006; Zhang, 2010). PD-L is expressed in several human 
cancers. Tumor expression of PD-L promotes interaction with its receptor on tumor antigen-specific T-cells, 
inducing apoptosis of the T-cells (Ahmadzadeh et al., 2009; Dong et al., 2002). Like PD-L, Fas-L induces 
apoptosis of cells that express its receptor. Tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs) express increased levels of 
Fas receptor compared with peripheral blood lymphocytes, and this makes them susceptible to Fas-
mediated apoptosis by tumor cells (O’Connell et al., 1999).  
As already mentioned, TAAs are mainly self-antigens and since Tregs are important for the maintenance of 
self-tolerance, the tumor environment is believed to promote Treg immune responses. TAA-specific 
immune responses are often weak, and it is thought that Tregs suppress the activity of TAA-specific 
lymphocytes and tumor regression. Furthermore, for many types of cancer, increased numbers of Treg cells 
in the tumor is associated with poor prognosis (Oleinika et al., 2013). Besides Tregs, a couple of other cells 
play an important role in down-regulating anti-tumor immune responses. These cells include plasmacytoid 
DCs, immature APCs, myeloid derived suppressor cells (MDSCs) and M2 tumor associated macrophages 
(TAMs) (Oleinika et al., 2013).    
The three phases of cancer development has been described in the immuneediting hypothesis. The phases 
comprise Elimination, Equilibrium and Escape (figure 1). Elimination is often the early phase in tumor 
development where tumor cells are recognized and destroyed by the innate and adaptive immune 
responses, due to expression of tumor antigens and major histocompatibility complex (MHC) class I 
molecules. Furthermore, dying tumor cells release damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs), like 
high mobility group box 1 (HMGB1), that further promote immune responses. The tumors are seldom 
clinically visible at this stage. Tumor cells that survive this phase enter the Equilibrium phase, in which the 
tumor and the adaptive immune response co-exist. Finally, in the Escape phase the tumor establish an 
immunosuppressive environment characterized by poor antigen expression, immunosuppressive molecules 
and expression of negative regulatory receptors, like PD-1, on T-cells. In this phase the tumor cells begin to 
grow and expand in an uncontrolled manner, and the tumor becomes clinically visible (Kalbasi et al., 2013; 
Mittal et al., 2014; Schreiber et al., 2011). Figure 1 is an illustration of the three phases. Most of the 
molecules and cells have been described briefly, and the remaining will be described in the following 
sections.

Immunotherapy 
Since tumor cells possess different escape mechanisms and thereby prevent anti-tumor immune responses, 
methods for reawakening and activating the immune response against tumors are highly investigated. 
Immunotherapy is one of the methods investigated, and it can be used to promote important steps in the 
generation of effective anti-tumor immunity or to target various tumor immunosuppressive mechanisms. 
One example of immunotherapy is DC vaccines, where DCs are isolated from patients with cancer and 
subsequently activated and loaded with tumor-antigens ex vivo, whereafter they are inserted back into the 
patient (Mellman et al., 2011). Another immunotherapy approach is to block cytotoxic T-lymphocyte-
associated antigen (CTLA)-4 by using a monoclonal antibody. CTLA-4 is a negative regulator that is 
expressed on the surface of activated T-cells and prevents further T-cell activation and expansion. Thus, 
blocking CTLA-4 is believed to unconstrain immune responses and thereby allow new anti-tumor immune 
responses to be triggered. Furthermore, Treg function is believed to be inhibited by anti-CTLA-4 treatment  
(Mellman et al., 2011). Ipilimumab is a monoclonal antibody that blocks CTLA-4, and it has been shown to 
improve overall survival in patients with metastatic melanoma in two phase III trials (Hodi et al., 2010; 
Robert et al., 2011). Furthermore, clinical studies have tested the anti-tumor effects of antibodies targeting 
either PD-L1 or PD-1. The antibodies have been shown to induce tumor regression and improve overall 
survival in cancer patients (Brahmer et al., 2012; Hamid et al., 2013; Robert et al., 2015). Furthermore, 
ligands for toll-like receptors (TLRs) can be used to provide activation signals to innate cells, like DCs, 
allowing them to differentiate and mature, and thereby promote immune responses. In the following 
section the function of TLRs will be explained and examples of immunotherapy will be included.  
Toll-like Receptors  
The innate immune response is not antigen-specific in contrast to the adaptive immune response. Instead, 
the innate immune system recognizes conserved molecular structures from pathogens, called pathogen 
associated molecular patterns (PAMPs), via pattern-recognition receptors and thereby discriminate self 
from non-self. The pattern-recognition receptors include TLRs, which is a family of transmembrane 
receptors that belong to the innate immune system (Medzhitov, 2001). Besides the transmembrane 
domain, TLRs consist of an intracellular domain and an extracellular domain. The extracellular domain 
contains leucine-rich repeats and is responsible for pathogen recognition (Hashimoto et al., 1988). The 
individual TLRs are distributed differently within the cell and each receptor recognizes specific molecular 
patterns from virus, bacteria, fungi or protozoa. TLR 1, 2, 4, 5 and 6 are expressed on the cell surface and 
thus, recognize external pathogens. TLR 3, 7, 8 and 9 are expressed in intracellular compartments, like 
endosomes, and recognize bacterial and viral nucleic acids (Takeda & Akira, 2005). Mammalian TLRs share a 
common intracellular domain which is called the TIR (for Toll-IL-1 receptor) domain. The domain is so called 
because it is homologous to the cytoplasmic domain of IL-1 receptors  (Gay & Keith, 1991; Schneider et al., 
1991) . Recognition of PAMPs by TLRs induces dimerization of the extracellular domains, which brings the 
intracellular TIR domains close together allowing them to interact with TIR domains of adaptor molecules, 
like the myeloid differentiation factor 88 (MyD88)  (Botos et al., 2011; Hultmark, 1994; Jin et al., 2007; Liu 
et al., 2009). This interaction initiates a signaling cascade that results in the activation of NF-kappaB (NF-kB) 
transcription factors among others. A study by Kawai and colleagues has confirmed the essential role of 
MyD88 in TLR signaling by showing that mice which lack MyD88 are deficient in TLR signaling (Kawai et al., 
1999).

Anti-Tumor Immunity and TLR-7 
It has been suggested that anti-tumor immunity can be mediated by some interferons (IFNs), partly by sti-
mulating polarization toward Th1 responses (Johansson et al., 2008). The differentiation of CD4+ T-cells 
toward Th1 immune responses is dependent on both IFN-γ and IL-12 (Hsieh et al., 1993; Lighvani et al., 
2001; Seder et al., 1993). Th1 cells secrete IL-2 and IFN-γ, they are important for immune responses against 
intracellular pathogens and help stimulate activation and expansion of CTLs. In order to control tumors and 
promote tumor regression CTL immune responses are especially important, since CTLs can recognize and 
subsequently kill tumor cells that display specific tumor-antigens (Johansson et al., 2008). Besides the 
helper function provided by Th1 cells, activation of CTLs in anti-tumor immune responses requires cross-
priming of tumor antigens and their subsequent presentation on MHC class I molecules (Larsson et al., 
2001).     
During tumor growth, TLRs play an essential role in polarizing the immune response, and it has been shown 
that activation of TLR-7 can give rise to Th1 polarized immune responses. As mentioned above, Th1 
responses are important for fighting cancer. TLR-7 activation results in initiation of a MyD88-dependent 
signaling pathway that leads to activation of the transcription factors NF-kB, activator-protein 1 (AP-1) and 
interferon regulatory factor 7 (IRF-7) which induce production of type I IFNs (IFN-α and IFN-β) that can 
promote Th1 differentiation in humans (Kawai & Akira, 2006; O’Shea & Visconti, 2000). Furthermore, a 
study by Lee and colleagues has demonstrated that besides type I IFNs, TLR-7 agonists can induce 
production of IL-6, IL-12 and IFN-γ in mouse splenocytes and IL-12 and TNF-α in human peripheral blood 
leukocytes, many of which are important for Th1 differentiation. Moreover, Lee and colleagues 
demonstrated that TLR-7 agonists can up-regulate MHC class I and II molecules and the co-stimulatory 
molecules CD40 and CD86 in mouse DCs (Lee et al., 2003).    
TLR7 is expressed in monocytes, plasmacytoid DCs, myeloid DCs, NK cells, T-cells, B-cells and eosinophils 
(Cheong et al., 2010; Hart et al., 2005; Ito et al., 2002; Nagase et al., 2003). Studies have shown that 
guanosine and/or uridine rich single-stranded RNA (ssRNA) is the natural ligand for TLR7 and TLR8 (Diebold 
et al., 2004; Heil et al., 2004; Lund et al., 2004). Furthermore, a number of synthetic small molecules, that 
can activate TLR7, have been found, and many of these have been tested for their potential as 
immunotherapeutics in cancer treatment. The molecules include the imidazoquinolines Imiquimod, 
Resiquimod (R848) and 852A (3M-001) (Gorden et al., 2005; Hemmi et al., 2002), the guanosine analog 
Loxoribine (Heil et al., 2003), SC-1 (Hamm et al., 2009), DSR-6434 (Adlard et al., 2014) and TMX-202 which 
is a TLR7-ligand phospholipid conjugate (Crain et al., 2014; Johansen et al., 2015).   
The TLR7 agonist Imiquimod has been formulated in a cream for treatment of superficial basal cell 
carcinoma, and phase III clinical trials have shown that the cream (5% Imiquimod) promote tumor 
clearance (Geisse et al., 2004). Moreover, a phase I clinical trial has tested the effects of the TLR7 agonist 
852A, which was well tolerated in cancer patients and able to induce systemic immune activation (Dudek et 
al., 2007).  
Besides immunotherapy, cancer patients are commonly treated by using RT, chemotherapy and surgery. 
However, only RT will be elaborated in the Background section.

Radiation 
Radiation has been used in cancer treatment for many years since it induces DNA damage, which is more 
lethal to rapidly proliferating cells, like cancer cells, than it is to normal tissue cells. However, evidence has 
recently suggested that the tumor responses observed after radiation are not only caused by the direct 
damage from RT, but also by significant changes in the tumor microenvironment caused by RT. The 
alterations of the tumor microenvironment make the tumor more accessible to the immune cells and more 
susceptible to the immune system. Overall, the alterations enhance the efficiency of the anti-tumor 
immune responses in the host – not only at the site of radiation, but also at remote sites in the body (de la 
Cruz-Merinoet al., 2014; Shiao & Coussens, 2010).  
Radiation and the Tumor Microenvironment 
Tumor cells that are dying due to RT give rise to some “danger signals” which possess immunogenic 
properties, and when leukocytes in the tissues detect the “danger signals” immune responses involving 
both innate and adaptive immune cells are activated (Shiao & Coussens, 2010).  
 
    
The radiation induced danger signals include translocation of calreticulin to the cell surface of apoptotic 
tumor cells, thereby mediating phagocytosis by DCs, and potentially anti-tumor immune responses  (Obeid 
et al., 2007; Perez et al., 2009). Cells damaged by RT may also up-regulate the surface expression of the 
proapoptotic receptor Fas. Immune cells expressing the Fas-L may thereby induce apoptosis of damaged 
tumor cells (de la Cruz-Merino et al., 2014). Moreover, radiation of tumor cells leads to the release of the 
protein HMGB1 which is a ligand for TLR-4. By binding to TLR4, which is expressed on DCs, HMGB1 induces 
an inflammatory immune response (Apetoh et al., 2007; Park et al., 2006). Additionally, tumor cells that 
undergo apoptotic or necrotic cell death after radiation represent an important source of TAAs that 
potentially can stimulate an immune response when taken up by DCs (Chen et al., 2001; Kotera et al., 
2001).  
Furthermore, it has been demonstrated that radiation enhances the migration of DCs, which have taken up 
tumor-antigens, to the draining lymph nodes. In the draining lymph nodes, DCs active naïve T-cells and 
thereby generate tumor-specific CD8+ T-cells that can migrate to the tumor site (Lee et al., 2009; Lugade et 
al., 2005). This was shown by an increased number of TILs and CD8+ T-cells that secreted IFN-γ upon 
stimulation with tumor peptides in radiated tumors and lymph nodes draining radiated tumors (Lugade et 
al., 2005). Various studies have demonstrated that CD8+ cells are essential for tumor regression (Dovedi et 
al., 2013; Lee et al., 2009; Lim et al., 2014).   
The activated T-cells from the draining lymph nodes are recruited to the tumor by a chemokine gradient 
triggered by RT. This was demonstrated by Matsumura and co-workers who showed that radiation can up-
regulate secretion of the chemokine CXCL16, the ligand for CXCR6, in human and mouse breast cancer cells, 
and that radiation enhances tumor-migration of CD8+ CXCR6+ T-cells in a breast cancer mouse model  
(Matsumura et al., 2008).  
 
 
 
 
                         
Tumor vessels possess various hindrances that obstruct immune cell infiltration of the tumor, including 
decreased expression of adhesion molecules on endothelial cells. However, radiation has been shown to 
increase expression of the cell adhesion molecules E-selectin and intercellular adhesion molecule 1 (ICAM-
1) on human endothelial cells, thereby facilitating the extravasation of lymphocytes (Hallahan et al., 1996). 
Expression of the same adhesion molecules was found in blood vessels from radiated patients with SCCHN 
==PAGE:18==
18 
 
(Handschel et al., 1999). Furthermore, studies have shown that radiation up-regulates the expression of 
MHC class I molecules on the surface of different tumor cells, including murine GL261 glioma tumor cells 
(Newcomb et al., 2006) and human MelJuSo melanoma cells (Reits et al., 2006). In the last study it was 
additionally found that RT changes the peptide repertoire used for MHC class I presentation to also include 
radiation-specific peptides. Overall, this indicates that radiation boost tumor availability and recognition by 
increasing expression of chemokines used for recruitment, cell adhesion molecules important for tumor 
infiltration and MHC class I crucial for tumor recognition.   
Besides affecting the radiated tumor and its environment, RT has been shown to induce effects outside the 
radiated area. This has given rise to the relatively new concept called the abscopal effect which refers to 
systemic anti-tumor effects induced by RT. Thus, tumor-specific immune cells generated by RT can target 
tumor cells that are distant from the radiated primary tumor and thereby induce regression of metastasis 
around the body. However the exact immune mechanisms behind the abscopal effect are not fully 
understood. Nevertheless, it has been found that the reduction of distant tumors is in accordance with an 
elevated priming of anti-tumor specific T-cells which infiltrate the tumor. The abscopal effect is a rare 
event, which reflects the fact that radiation alone is not adequate at subverting the immunosuppressive 
tumor microenvironment (de la Cruz-Merino et al., 2014; Formenti & Demaria, 2009). 
The efficacy of radiation in cancer treatment is limited by a couple of factors. One example is that radiation 
can induce expression of PD-L1 on tumor cells (Dovedi et al., 2014), which might attenuate anti-tumor 
immune responses. Furthermore, it has been shown that immune cells present within tumors, like antigen 
presenting cells and T-cells, undergo apoptotic cell death upon tumor radiation (Lugade et al., 2005). 
Moreover, studies have shown that Treg cells are more radiation resistant than other T-cells, and that they 
increase in number upon radiation (Kachikwu et al., 2011; Qu et al., 2010). Thus, since radiation might kill 
infiltrating immune cells, these findings have to be taken into account when planning radiation schedules in 
cancer treatment.  
Radiation Schemes 
Patients treated with radiation can be treated by either a single dose or fractionated dosing. Contradictory 
finding have been made concerning the effectiveness of the different radiation dosing schedules. In a study 
by Schaue and colleagues the effectiveness of single dose radiation and fractionated radiation was 
examined in female C57 black 6 (C57BL/6) mice with B16-OVA murine melanoma tumors 7 days after 
treatment. When examining single dose radiation between 5 and 15 Gy it was found that 15 Gy resulted in 
the greatest reduction in tumor growth. However, the reduction in tumor growth was associated with both 
increased numbers of tumor specific T-cells (measured by IFN-γ production) and Treg cells in the spleen. In 
contrast, single dose radiation of 7.5 Gy and 10 Gy slowed tumor growth significantly, increased tumor-
specific T cell responses and down-regulated the amount of Treg cells. Furthermore, fractionated radiation 
doses separated by six hours were examined (3 Gy x 5, 5 Gy x 3, 7.5 Gy x 2) and it was found that all dosing 
schemes slowed tumor growth significantly and induced tumor-specific T cell responses. However, most 
fractionated doses also increased the proportion of Treg cells in the spleen compared to control mice, 
although the results were not significant. Overall, no clear indication on the most potent radiation scheme 
was obtained in this study. Nevertheless, fractionated radiation with 7.5 Gy x 2 resulted in the most 
effective tumor control and tumor immunity, while a relatively low proportion of Tregs was maintained in 
the spleen (Schaue etal., 2012).

In contrast, a study by Lee and co-workers used mouse melanoma models to show that a single dose of 
radiation (20 Gy) was more effective at reducing the tumor size over time than fractionated radiation (5 Gy 
x 4) (Lee et al., 2009). However, the fractionated radiation used in the study was administered over two 
weeks in contrast to the study by Schaue and co-workers where the fractionated doses were administered 
with 6 hours intervals, which mean that the results are not fully comparable. Moreover, in the study by 
Schaue and co-workers, the tumors were only allowed to grow for 7 days, and the further growth course is 
therefore not known.  
 
 
 
 
                      
Another study, using mouse lymphoma models, compared single dose radiation and fractionated dosing. It 
was found that fractionated radiation of 2 Gy x 5 slowed tumor growth more than a single dose of 10 Gy.  
Furthermore, when combining the radiation schemes with the TLR-7 agonist Resiquimod it was found that 
treatment with 2 Gy x 5 and Resiquimod eradicated tumors in all mice, whereas treatment with 10 Gy and 
Resiquimod mostly slowed tumor growth (Dovedi et al., 2013). Likewise, a study by Dewan and colleagues 
showed that besides reducing tumor growth of primary tumors, combination of anti-CTLA-4 antibodies and 
fractionated RT significantly reduced tumor growth of secondary tumors, which had not been radiated. 
Combination of anti-CTLA-4 antibodies and single dose radiation did not inhibit secondary tumor growth 
significantly (Dewan et al., 2009). 
Overall, many studies indicate increased treatment effects when using fractionated radiation compared to 
single dose radiation. However, it can be speculated whether immune cells that have been recruited to the 
tumor due to radiation will die by further radiation, thereby limiting the anti-tumor immune response. 
Moreover, this would hinder the circulation of the immune cells and thereby the development of systemic 
protection. On the other hand, when lymphocytes, like CTLs, have infiltrated the tumor, further RT might 
strengthen the immune response against the tumor by inducing the death of additional tumor cells. The 
dying tumor cells provide additional TAAs and danger signals, like the expression of death receptors, which 
leads to tumor cell recognition and elimination by CTLs (de la Cruz-Merino et al., 2014).  
In view of the fact that radiation induces several immune-mediated mechanisms many researchers have 
investigated the potential of promoting the RT induced immune response by using immunotherapy in 
combination with RT for future cancer treatment. The following section will give some examples of 
treatments combining RT and immunotherapy.  
Combined Treatment 
Researchers have studied the potential of combining radiation with various immunotherapeutic drugs, 
including pro-inflammatory cytokines, DC injection, TLR-agonists, co-stimulatory molecules and inhibitors of 
CTLA-4 and PD-1 (Adlard et al., 2014; Demaria et al., 2005; Dewan et al., 2009; Dovedi et al., 2014; Dovedi 
et al., 2013; Shiao & Coussens, 2010). In fact, numerous clinical trials are currently testing combinations of 
immunotherapeutic drugs and radiation (Crittenden et al., 2015). Several studies have demonstrated that 
combination therapy with local radiation and immunotherapy can generate systemic anti-tumor immune 
responses, and some studies have found that the combination is effective at controlling metastasis 
compared to radiation or the immunotherapy alone (Adlard et al., 2014; Demaria et al., 2004, 2005; Dewan 
et al., 2009; Lee et al., 2009). Furthermore, studies have shown that combination therapy with radiation 
and immunotherapeutics can generate anti-tumor memory immune responses (Dovedi et al., 2013; Lim et 
al., 2014). In the following section only TLR agonists in combination with RT will be described.

In a study by Dovedi and co-workers the anti-tumor effects of combining local RT with IV injections of 
Resiquimod were examined in C57Bl/6 and BALB/c mice with T- and B-cell lymphomas. Both injection of 
Resiquimod (3 mg/kg) and exposure to RT was performed seven days after inoculation, when tumors were 
approximately 100mm3. The study showed that the therapeutic efficiency of radiation (10 Gy x 1) was 
increased by systemic weekly administration of Resiquimod. Additionally, it was shown that combined 
treatment of RT and Resiquimod dosed weekly was more effective than RT combined with a single dose of 
Resiquimod. The combined treatment engendered anti-tumor immune responses that were dependent on 
CD8+ T-cell activity and generated immunologic memory that protected against tumor re-challenge. As 
already mentioned, it was also found that the therapeutic efficiency of combining radiation and Resiquimod 
was further enhanced when the radiation was administered in fractions (2 Gy x 5), instead of as a single 
dose (10 Gy x 1). The mice treated with the combination of fractionated radiation and Resiquimod showed 
complete tumor regression (Dovedi et al., 2013).  
In a study from 2014, the anti-tumor effect of combining radiation with injections of the potent TLR-7 
agonist DSR-6434 was examined. The study used female mice with colon carcinoma (CT26) and sarcoma 
(KHT) tumors. Treatment was initiated when tumors reached a volume around 180-220mm3. Radiation was 
given as fractionated doses (2 Gy x 5) and the TLR-7 agonist (DSR-6434) was administered weekly (0.1 
mg/kg) like in the study by Dovedi and co-workers (Dovedi et al., 2013).The study showed that 
administration of DSR-6434 to mice bearing CT26 tumors increased the potency of radiation and generated 
specific anti-tumor CD8+ T-cell responses. In fact, the combined treatment of DSR-6434 and fractionated 
radiation resulted in prolonged survival and complete tumor regression for 55% of the CT26 tumor bearing 
mice. Furthermore, the combined treatment engendered immunological memory that was tumor-antigen 
specific. Additionally, it was shown that radiation of primary tumors (15 Gy x 1) in combination with DSR-
6434 treatment reduced the number of lung metastases in C3H mice with metastatic KHT tumors (Adlard et 
al., 2014).  
Overall, the studies show that weekly systemic administration of TLR-7 agonists augment the anti-tumor 
efficacy of radiation as a monotherapy. The combined treatment generates tumor-specific immune 
responses that increase tumor regression, prolong survival time and generate immunological memory, 
overall indicating that combined treatment help conquer the aforementioned immune suppressive tumor 
environment. Moreover, the study by Dovedi and co-workers demonstrated that fractionated radiation, as 
opposed to single dose radiation, may further enhance the therapeutic efficacy of combining radiation and 
TLR-7 agonists. Overall, the two studies support the idea of combining radiation with immunotherapy to 
increase effectiveness of tumor treatment, both concerning primary tumors and metastatic burden (Adlard 
et al., 2014; Dovedi et al., 2013).  
The combination of RT and immunotherapy implies some timing challenges. Immunotherapeutic agents 
can be administered prior to RT, simultaneously with RT or after RT. Immunotherapeutic drugs 
administered before RT can enhance immune responses induced by RT. However, if the 
immunotherapeutic drugs are administered well ahead of RT and has stimulated anti-tumor immune 
responses at the time of radiation, the cytotoxic effect of radiation might disrupt immune responses. 
Immunotherapeutic drugs could also be administered after RT, however if radiation has not generated 
tumor antigens and enhanced tumor accumulation at the time of administration, the effectiveness of the 
==PAGE:21==

drug might be diminished. Furthermore, administering immunotherapeutic drugs after RT can also be 
ineffective due to transient immune activation by radiation (Kalbasi et al., 2013).      
Monocytes  
Monocytes are the largest circulating blood leukocytes. They play an essential role in innate immunity 
where their functions include cytokine production and phagocytosis (Auffray et al., 2009; Gordon & Taylor, 
2005). Monocytes are part of the mononuclear phagocyte system, which will be described in a section 
below.   
 
 
 
                  
 
                  
Monocytes develop from dividing myeloid precursors in the bone marrow.  The newly formed monocytes 
are released as non-dividing cells from the bone marrow into the peripheral blood where they circulate 
with a half-life of 1-3 days.  From the peripheral circulation the monocytes migrate into tissues throughout 
the body (van Furth et al., 1979; Ziegler-Heitbrock, 2000). This happens during both homeostasis and 
inflammation. In the tissues the monocytes can further differentiate into macrophages or DCs and they 
have therefore been considered to be primarily transitional cells whose further differentiation depends on 
the environment that they accumulate. However, renewed interest in monocytes has shown that they are 
not only transitional cells; monocytes are functionally heterogeneous in various species and can serve as 
effectors of the immune system, thus they do not need to differentiate into macrophages or DCs to 
contribute to the immune defense (Auffray et al., 2009; Robbins & Swirski, 2010).  
Monocyte Subsets 
Monocytes in both humans and mice show different expression patterns of chemokine receptors and other 
surface molecules, which allows for the division into different subgroups.  In 2001 Grage-Griebenow and 
co-workers proposed the existence of three human monocyte subgroups which differ in phenotype and 
immune function (Grage-Griebenow et al., 2001). Thus, human monocytes are divided into classical, non-
classical and intermediate monocytes, and the division is based on the surface expression of CD14 (part of 
the lipopolysaccharide receptor complex) and CD16 (Fcγ receptor III) (Shi & Pamer, 2011).  
The majority of peripheral blood monocytes, representing 80-90% of the circulating human monocytes, are 
termed classical monocytes and consist of CD14++CD16 – monocytes. These monocytes are phagocytic and 
inflammatory and have a low expression of HLA-DR (Robbins & Swirski, 2010; Serbina et al., 2008). The two 
other monocyte subgroups are both CD16 positive (CD16+). The intermediate monocyte subgroup 
constitutes the CD14++CD16+ monocytes, whereas the non-classical subgroup constitute CD14+CD16++ 
monocytes (Serbina et al., 2008; Shi & Pamer, 2011).  
The non-classical monocytes (CD14+ CD16++) are known to expand during infection and inflammation 
(Emminger et al., 2001; Katayama et al., 2000; Ziegler-Heitbrock et al., 1993). They are regarded as pro-
inflammatory since they produce little IL-10 and more of the proinflammatory cytokine tumor necrosis 
factor (TNF)-α than the other monocyte subgroups upon lipopolysaccharide (LPS) stimulation (Belge et al., 
2002; Skrzeczyńska-Moncznik et al., 2008). Besides producing TNF-α, the non-classical monocytes produce 
the inflammatory cytokines IL-1 and IL-6 upon stimulation with LPS (Frankenberger et al.,  1996). 
Furthermore, the non-classical monocytes have been shown to be the most efficient APCs since they induce 
a higher IFN-γ release by T-cells than the other monocyte subgroups (Skrzeczyńska-Moncznik et al., 2008).  
Since macrophages express CD16 and it has been shown that CD16 – monocytes can upregulate their 
expression of CD16, it is thought that the CD16++ monocytes (the non-classical monocytes) represent a 
==PAGE:22==
22 
 
more mature version of the CD16 – monocyte with macrophage properties (Ziegler-Heitbrock et al., 1993).  
In contrast, intermediate monocytes (CD14++CD16+) are thought to represent a transitory stage of 
monocyte differentiation (Frankenberger et al., 1996). These monocytes have been shown to be the main 
producers of IL-10 and have a higher phagocytic activity than non-classical monocytes. The intermediate 
monocytes are regarded as anti-inflammatory (Skrzeczyńska-Moncznik et al., 2008).   
Besides the functionally different monocyte subgroups that are found in humans, distinct monocyte 
subgroups with similarities to the human subgroups are also found in mice (Robbins & Swirski, 2010).  
Monocyte Differentiation 
During infection, monocytes can take up antigens and carry them to local lymph nodes. In the lymph nodes, 
the monocytes can either differentiate into monocyte-derived DCs (Mo-DCs) or transfer the antigens to 
classical DCs. Both Mo-DCs and classical DCs are capable of activating CD4+ and CD8+ T-cells, and thereby 
stimulate adaptive immune responses (Cheong et al., 2010; Shi & Pamer, 2011). It has been shown that 
monocytes can differentiate into Mo-DCs when treated with the TLR-7 agonist TMX-202 and the TLR-4 
agonist LPS (Cheong et al., 2010; Johansen et al., 2015). Both monocytes and DCs can produce the pro-
inflammatory cytokine IL-12 upon stimulation. IL-12 is important for both stimulating and maintaining cell-
mediated immune responses  (Trinchieri, 1995). Furthermore, activated monocytes secrete the chemokine 
IFN-γ-inducible protein-10 (IP-10) (referred to as CXCL-10). As the name says, the chemokine expression is 
induced by IFN-γ stimulation. CXCL-10 binds to the chemokine receptor CXCR3 , which is mostly expressed 
on activated T-cells in humans, and promote migration of the activated T-cells (Loetscher et al., 1996; 
Luster, Unkeless, & Ravetch, 1985; Noguchi et al., 2007; Qin et al., 1998). 
As already mentioned, monocytes can also differentiate into macrophages. Macrophages are functionally 
plastic and can play both pro-inflammatory and anti-inflammatory roles in immune responses. The 
polarization of macrophages is influenced by environmental signals, and the M1 macrophage phenotype is 
activated by microbial products or IFN-γ. The M1 macrophages are potent antigen presenting cells, they 
produce pro-inflammatory cytokines, like IL-12 and IL-23, and are capable of eliminating pathogens and 
cancer cells.  In contrast, M2 macrophages possess anti-inflammatory functions and are believed to support 
tumor growth. Macrophages are polarized towards the M2 macrophage phenotype by factors like IL-10 and 
IL-4 (Sica et al., 2008). 
The Mononuclear Phagocyte System 
The mononuclear phagocyte system (MPS) is a complex cellular system which includes monocytes, 
macrophages and DCs, whose main roles are phagocytosis and presentation of pathogenic antigens (Kelly 
et al., 2011). The cells of the MPS engulf and eliminate various targets like apoptotic cells, pathogens and 
other foreign particles by different cellular processes. Opsonized pathogens are for example recognized and 
engulfed via Fc receptor- and complement receptor-mediated mechanisms, whereas apoptotic cells are 
recognized by engulfment-receptors, like CD14, lectin and scavenger receptors, which recognize “eat-me” 
signals expressed on the surface of the apoptotic cells (Ravichandran & Lorenz, 2007). As foreign particles, 
liposomes are also targets of the MPS system and hence, these carriers can be used to carry drugs to MPS 
cells and thereby specifically target these cells (Kelly et al., 2011).

Liposomes 
Liposomes are small spherical vesicles that consist of amphiphilic molecules, like phospholipids. When the 
phospholipids are exposed to aqueous media they self-assemble to liposomes consisting of one or more 
lipid bilayers (Figure 2). Liposomes often also contain cholesterol, since this compound appears to stabilize 
the liposomes in different ways. Liposomes can be used as drug delivery systems by entrapping drugs in 
either the hydrophilic center or the hydrophobic bilayer of the liposomes. One of the ideas behind using 
liposomes in drug delivery is that they can limit harmful side effects by encapsulating the drug and deliver it 
to specific cells, organs or tissues in the body (Immordino et al., 2006). Both immunotherapeutic agents, 
such as antigens, cytokines and TLR agonists, and chemotherapeutics, like doxorubicin, have been loaded 
into these carriers for the treatment of various diseases, including cancer (Altin et al., 2004; Chikh & 
Schutze-Redelmeir, 2002; Gabizon et al., 2003; Immordino et al., 2006; Thomann et al., 2011). 
The composition of liposomes provides advantages like 
biocompatibility, low immunogenicity and 
biodegradability. Furthermore, as drug delivery systems 
liposomes can provide protection of the drug as well as 
cell specificity (Kelly et al., 2011). The cell-targeting 
capacity of liposomes depends on different 
physicochemical properties like liposome size, charge and 
lipid composition, and on the insertion of liposome 
surface ligands like peptides, polysaccharides, antibodies 
and lectins. Furthermore, a positive liposome surface 
charge can ease cell uptake due to electrostatic 
interactions with the negative surface of cells (Kelly et al., 
2011).  
One of the disadvantages in using liposomes for drug 
delivery is the rapid clearance of liposomes from the 
circulation which is caused by cells of the MPS that 
recognize and phagocytose liposomes upon intravenous administration. However, liposomes can be 
designed to escape phagocytosis by incorporating poly-ethylene-glycol (PEG) on the liposome surface. This 
shields the liposomes from the MPS and thereby their circulatory lifetime can be increased. Liposomes with 
PEG coating are referred to as PEGylated liposomes. Liposome aggregation can be avoided by PEG coating, 
which improves formulation stability (Immordino et al., 2006). Moreover, it has been found that long-
circulating liposomes accumulate in tumor areas to a high degree, since the endothelial lining of the 
vasculature often is discontinuous in tumor areas and lymphatic drainage is lacking. This effect is referred 
to as the enhanced permeability and retention (EPR) effect (Immordino et al., 2006).   
The fact that liposomes are natural targets for MPS cells can be exploited in drug delivery systems, where 
liposomes can be used to carry drugs specifically to MPS cells. The MPS cells sense liposomes by 
recognizing serum proteins, called opsonins that have bound to the liposome surface. Thus, the first signal 
for clearance of liposomes from the circulation is the binding of various opsonins, like immunoglobulins and 
complement proteins, to the liposome surface. Activation of the complement system induces lysis of the 
liposome membrane and facilitate uptake by MPS cells (Immordino et al., 2006).

Materials and Methods 
The experiments were designed, planned and supervised by the MonTa Biosciences team and employees at 
Department of Micro- and Nanotechnology at the Technical University of Denmark.  
Materials 
Phosphate Buffered Saline (PBS) and RPMI-1640 Medium (RPMI), Bovine serum albumin, Tween® 20, 
sulfuric acid, nitric acid, hydrochloric acid, hydrogen peroxide and dimethyl sulphoxide (DMSO) were 
purchased from Sigma-Aldrich. Hirudin (Roche), ethylene-diamineteraacetic acid (EDTA) (BD) and heparin 
(BD) tubes were used for blood collection.  
Liposomes 
All liposomes used in this Master Project were prepared by Associate Professor at Department of Chemistry 
Jonas Rosager Henriksen (JHEN).  
The liposomes consisted of cholesterol (Chol), 1,2-dioleoyl-3-trimethylammonium-propane (DOTAP), 1-
palmitoyl-2-oleoyl-sn-glycero-3-phospho-(1'-rac-glycerol) (POPG), 1-palmitoyl-2-oleoyl-sn-glycero-3-
phosphocholine (POPC) and 1,2-dioleoyl-sn-glycero-3-phosphoethanolamine-N-[methoxy(polyethylene 
glycol)-2000]  (DOPE-PEG2k) which were obtained from Avanti Polar Lipids, Inc. Some liposomes also 
contained 2-(4-{[6-Amino-2-(2-methoxyethoxy)-8-oxo-7H-purin-9(8H)-yl]methyl}benzamido)ethyl 2,3- 
Bis(dodecanoyloxy)propyl phosphate (TMX-202) (Telormedix), 64Cu (produced at the Hevesy laboratory at 
DTU Nutech), 1,4,7,10-Tetraazacyclododecane-1,4,7,10-tetraacetic acid (DOTA) (Macrocyclics), 
Diethylenetriaminepentaacetic acid gadolinium(III) dihydrogen salt hydrate (GdDTPA) (Sigma Aldrich) 
and/or 1,2-dioleoyl-sn-glycero-3-phosphoethanolamine-N-(lissamine rhodamine B sulfonyl) (DOPE-RhB) 
(Avanti Polar Lipids, Inc.). Additional materials used for liposome preparation are written in the appendix 
for each liposome batch.  
When liposome formulations are represented in a table or in the text, the numbers in the parenthesis 
represents the mol% ratios of the different constituents. See the appendix 1 for an overview of the primary 
liposome constituents used. Furthermore, it should be noted that DOTAP is a cationic phospholipid, POPG 
is an anionic phospholipid, POPC is a neutral phospholipid and DOPE-PEG2k is a PEGylated phospholipid.  
Mice and cell lines 
C57BL/6 and BALB/C female mice were obtained from Charles River and the mice weighted around 20-22g 
upon arrival. The animal studies were performed under conditions approved by The Animal Experiments 
Inspectorate in Denmark. The murine melanoma cell line B16-F10 (C57BL/6 background) and the murine 
colon carcinoma cell line CT26 (BALB/C background) were obtained from ATCC and maintained by the 
animal facility in RPMI with supplements (Sigma-Aldrich). The tumor models were performed in 
immunocompetent animals, since an intact immune system is required in order to determine the anti-
tumor effects of immune stimulatory agents. 
In all experiments, mice were randomized to different treatment groups. The mice were fed with standard 
chow ad libitum and had unlimited access to water. All mice were housed in filter top cages with maximum 
8 mice per cage, and under controlled environmental conditions with light from 8am to 8pm, temperature: 
23ºC  1º C, air humidity: 35%  10 and ventilation:  >10 times per day. Sevoflurane (Abbott) was used to 
anaesthetize the mice in all studies.

B16 Melanoma Study 
C57BL/6 female mice were allowed to acclimatize for one week before they were inoculated 
subcutaneously with 2.5x105  B16-F10 melanoma cells in their right flank (day 1). Mice were randomly 
assigned to six treatment groups (8 mice per group). 
 An overview of the groups and the treatments is presented in table 1. Radiation treatment was initiated on 
day 11 when the tumors averaged 100mm3, and the fractionated radiation was given with 24 hour 
intervals. The mice were anesthetized prior to radiation and placed in a plastic mold covered with a lead 
shield only exposing the right tumor bearing leg. The leg was irradiated with 320 kV and 12.5mA with a 
dose rate of 1Gy/minute (min) using a dedicated small animal irradiation device (X-RAD 320 from Precision 
X-Ray, Inc.). The cationic liposomes were administered intravenously (IV) by tail vein injection on day 12 
and subsequently every seventh day. The cationic liposomes were well tolerated when dosed using 
200nmol/mouse TMX-202. The lipid concentration of empty liposomes was 4µmol/mouse (corresponding 
to the lipid concentration injected into the mice receiving cationic liposomes with TMX-202). The tumors 
were measured using a digital caliper every second to third day. The tumor volume was calculated using the 
formula (length x width2)/2.  Tumor volume was allowed to reach 1000mm3. Mice displaying signs of 
discomfort, not thriving, displaying weight loss >15% of initial body weight or developing ulceration on their 
tumors were sacrificed. All formulations were well tolerated by the mice. During the study the mice did not 
show any disturbing loss or gain in body weight between the groups.

inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-MS). Hence, human or mouse whole blood was 
incubated with cationic, anionic or neutral liposomes (table 2) containing Gd to examine the distribution of 
the liposomes between the plasma and blood cell fraction in whole blood. A couple of optimization 
experiments were ran during the project, and steps were added or changed in the experimental procedure. 
However, a general experimental procedure was followed in all the experiments (figure 5), and the 
following section describes this procedure.

Statistics 
Statistical tests were performed using the software GraphPad Prism version 6 (GraphPad Software, Inc., San 
Diego, California, USA). For all statistics performed a confidence level of p < 0.05 was considered significant.  
Statistical Analysis for the B16 Melanoma Study survival curves was done using a log-rank (Mantel-Cox) 
test. All animals that died in the cages during the study or were sacrificed due to ulceration on their tumors 
were censored.  
 
ELISA was conducted on blood from human donors and female BALB/C mice. Based on standard curves 
generated for each ELISA, the measured absorbances were converted to cytokine concentrations. For some 
datasets a few absorbances were higher than the measured absorbances within the standard curve. Due to 
lacking material (blood) and the appearance of the dataset it was decided to use the data even though 
some data was outside the standard curve.   
 
 
 
    
The data analysis of the PET study was done while being blinded to the treatment groups, due to the 
analysis being relatively subjective. 
 
In the Gadolinium Liposome Distribution Study, the Cytokine and Chemokine Study and the PET Study 
statistical analyses were performed using ordinary one-way ANOVA, or if stated in the figure-text: Repeated 
measures one-way ANOVA. The one-way ANOVAs were followed by Tukey’s multiple comparisons post-hoc 
tests that compare every mean with every other mean of the groups analyzed. The data was assumed to be 
Gaussian distributed.

Gadolinium Liposome Distribution Study 
The aim of the experiment was to expose human and mouse whole blood to Gadolinium (Gd) liposomes of 
cationic, anionic and neutral surface charge to examine the distribution of the liposomes between the 
plasma and blood cell fractions. 
 
 
 
 
     
The first experiments (experiment A-B) examined how the liposome distribution was affected by the choice 
of anticoagulant tube for blood sampling and by the incubation time. The next experiments (experiment C) 
examined whether the distribution of liposomes was concentration dependent.  Figure 5 in Materials and 
Methods presents an overview of the experiment.  
Experiment A – Anticoagulant Tubes 
Experiment A was a pilot experiment examining whether anticoagulant tubes with EDTA, heparin and 
hirudin had an influence on the association of liposomes to blood cells. In previous experiments by MonTa 
Biosciences, anticoagulant tubes with EDTA were used for blood collection. In addition to this 
anticoagulant, heparin, which is another commonly used anticoagulant, and hirudin, an anti-coagulant that 
is suggested not to interfere with complement activation (Bexborn et al., 2009), were tested in this 
experiment. The latter was used as it is hypothesized that the uptake of the current liposomes by 
monocytes is complement mediated. Moreover, heparin is assumed to interfere with the complement 
pathway (Bexborn et al., 2009; Logue, 1977).   
The pilot experiment included human blood from one donor, and EDTA tubes, hirudin tubes and heparin 
tubes were tested using 500 µM anionic, cationic and neutral liposomes. Since only one donor was used in 
the experiment, it was only used as an indication for the most optimal anti-coagulant tubes for future 
experiments.   
 
 
 
 
   
      
As shown in figure 5, the liposome treated blood samples were separated by centrifugation into a plasma 
and a blood cell fraction respectively. It was assumed that the sum of the Gd counts from the plasma 
sample and the blood cell sample represented the total Gd count in the whole sample. The percentage of 
liposomes in the blood cell fraction was calculated by dividing the blood cell sample Gd counts with the 
sum of blood cell sample and plasma sample Gd counts   
                
                               .  
Figure 11 shows the percentage of cationic, anionic and neutral liposomes in the blood cell fraction after 
blood collection in EDTA, hirudin and heparin tubes respectively.

Figure 14 shows that at low liposome concentrations (20 µM and 50 µM) less than 50 % of the cationic 
liposomes were found in the blood cell fraction, and thereby more liposomes were present in the plasma 
fraction than in the blood cell fraction. At the concentration of 100 µM the distribution was shifted, and the

significantly higher than the cell association percentages obtained when using 1000 µM neutral liposome 
(P≤0.05) (see figure 14). No statistical significant differences were found between the cell association 
percentages of cationic liposomes at different concentrations or anionic liposomes at different 
concentrations.   
During the project a couple of optimization steps were added to the experimental procedure to improve 
the experiment. As already mentioned, the first experiments only included plasma and blood cell samples 
(see figure 5 for an overview of the experimental procedure). However, when looking at the summarized 
Gd counts from blood cell and plasma samples for one concentration, the counts did not always sum up to 
the same Gd counts. It was therefore decided to include an extra sample called Total Gd count in the 
following experiments. The sample was collected from the human whole blood with liposome dilution 
before the plasma was separated from the blood cells by centrifugation (figure 6).

vivo cytokine profiles are similar to cytokine profiles observed after blood incubation (figure 19). Since 
Cationic #3 TMX and Cationic #4 TMX induced comparable IL-12p40 levels, it was decided to use the 
Cationic #4 TMX liposomes along with Cationic TMX in the in vivo study to examine batch variation.

Cytokine Profile In Vivo 
6 groups of 4 female BALB/C mice were used in this study. The groups of mice were injected with 200nmol 
free Resiquimod, free TMX-202, Cationic TMX, Cationic #4 TMX, Cationic #10+PEG TMX (corresponding 
molar dose of free TMX-202) or PBS, and blood samples were drawn 2, 6, 24 and 48 hours after injections.   
Graph A in figure 20 shows the IL-12p40 cytokine concentrations in plasma 2, 6, 24 and 48 hours after 
injection of the different formulations. 2 and 6 hours after injection of the different formulations, relatively 
high levels of IL-12p40 was observed for all mice that had received liposomes, free TMX-202 or Resiquimod 
compared to mice that had received PBS. After 6, 24 and 48 hours the IL-12p40 levels induced by the 
liposomes were higher than the levels induced by free TMX-202. Moreover, it seemed that IL-12p40 levels 
induced by free Resiquimod and TMX-202 dropped faster than the IL-12p40 levels induced by the three 
liposomes, which dropped at a similar pace to each other (graph A, figure 20).    
Graph B in figure 20 shows the IL-6 plasma concentrations 2 and 6 hours after injection of the different 
formulations. IL-6 was not detectable in the blood 24 and 48 hours after injection. From graph B in figure 
20 it is observed that neither PBS nor free TMX-202 induced IL-6 secretion. After 2 hours the level of IL-6 
induced by Resiquimod was higher than the levels induced by the three liposomes. However, the IL-6 level 
induced by Resiquimod seemed to drop faster than the levels induced by the three liposomes, which 
dropped at a similar pace. 
 
 
 
 
 
  
From graph A and B in figure 20 it can be observed that the IL-6 levels in general are lower than the IL-
12p40 levels, and that the IL-6 levels drops quicker than the IL-12p40 levels.   
The area under the cytokine concentration curves reflects the total cytokine secretion over time after 
administration of the different formulations. Therefore, the areas under the IL-12p40 and IL-6 
concentration curves were calculated to compare the cytokine levels induced by each treatment (graph A 
and B in figure 21). For the IL-12p40 cytokine levels it was found that the area under the IL-12p40 curve 
after treatment with PBS was significantly lower than after treatment with free Resiquimod, TMX-202 and 
the three liposomes (P<0.0001). Furthermore, all three liposomes induced significantly higher IL-12p40 
levels than free TMX-202 (P<0.0001) (see graph A, figure 21). Likewise, no IL-6 concentration was induced 
by free TMX-202 whereas all liposomes induced production of the cytokine. Furthermore, statistical 
significant differences were observed between the IL-6 levels induced by Cationic #10+PEG TMX liposome 
and by Cationic TMX and Cationic #4 TMX liposomes (P≤0.05) (see graph B, figure 21).

Discussion 
B16 Melanoma Study 
The B16 melanoma study was inspired by the study conducted by Dovedi and co-workers in 2013, where 
the TLR-7 agonist Resiquimod was administered (injected IV once weekly) in combination with either single 
dose radiation (10 Gy) or fractionated radiation (2 Gy x 5) (Dovedi et al., 2013). In the current B16 
Melanoma Study, cationic liposomes containing the TLR-7 agonist TMX-202 were injected IV once weekly 
and the effect of combining the liposomes with single dose and fractionated radiation was investigated.      
It had previously been shown that liposomes with a similar formulation, that lacked cholesterol, associated 
to monocytes, and that monocytes treated with TMX-202 differentiated into monocyte-derived DCs (DC-
SIGN+/CD14+) (Johansen et al., 2015). The hypothesis was therefore that when combining the TMX-202 
liposomes with radiation (similar to when combining Resiquimod with radiation) it would result in anti-
tumor immune responses, initiated by stimulated monocytes and radiation, and thereby the treatment 
would result in decreased tumor growth and increased survival.  
 
                       
Despite the parallels between the experimental setups, some significant differences between the current 
B16 Melanoma Study and the study conducted by Dovedi and co-workers included the choice of TLR-7 
agonist (TMX-202 versus Resiquimod), the delivery of the TLR-7 agonist (encapsulated versus free), the 
mouse tumor model (melanoma versus lymphoma) and the single dose radiation (8 Gy versus 10 Gy). 
Furthermore, in the study by Dovedi and co-workers, the first radiation and Resiquimod injection took 
place on the same day, whereas in the current study, the radiation was performed one day ahead of the 
first liposome injection. It was hypothesized that the staggered treatment with radiation and liposomes 
would improve the anti-tumor effects of the combined treatment. Furthermore, the rationale for using B16 
melanoma tumors was to enable comparisons to previous efficacy studies on TMX-liposomes (unpublished 
data) and the radiation doses were based on in-house experience at the animal facility (personal 
communication). It was though that treatment responses similar to the responses observed in the study by 
Dovedi and co-workers could be obtained in the B16 Melanoma Study.    
In the study by Dovedi and co-workers a minor but significant difference in survival time was observed 
between the control group and the group dosed weekly with Resiquimod (Dovedi et al., 2013). In the 
current study, a modest increase in survival time was observed in the TMX-202 liposome group compared 
to the control group, however the difference in survival time between the two groups was not statistically 
significant (figure 10). Dovedi and co-workers also showed that a single dose of local radiation (10 Gy) 
significantly enhanced long-term survival in mice bearing EG7 lymphoma tumors compared to the control 
mice (Dovedi et al., 2013). Similar results were obtained in the B16 Melanoma Study where a statistical 
significant increase in survival time was observed for the group receiving 8 Gy radiation compared to the 
control group (figure 10). Moreover, in the study by Dovedi and colleagues it was shown that the long-term 
survival could be further enhanced when combining single dose radiation with weekly IV administration of 
Resiquimod. The same trend was not observed in the current study, since the survival curves for the mice 
receiving 8 Gy alone and 8 Gy in combination with weekly TMX-202 liposome injections were similar (figure 
10). Thus, the statistical significant difference in survival time observed when comparing the control group 
with the group receiving 8 Gy radiation and TMX-202 liposomes was speculated to be due to the effect of 
the 8 Gy radiation rather than the combination of 8 Gy and TMX-202 liposomes.  
==PAGE:63==
63 
 
As already mentioned, the study by Dovedi and co-workers also tested the treatment efficacy of combining 
fractionated radiation, instead of single dose radiation, with Resiquimod. They showed that the 
combination resulted in significantly increased survival time compared with control mice and mice 
receiving either fractionated radiation or Resiquimod alone (Dovedi et al., 2013). Similar results were 
obtained in a study by Adlard and co-workers, who tested the TLR-7 agonist DSR-6434 in combination with 
fractionated radiation (2 Gy x 5) in CT26 tumor bearing mice (Adlard et al., 2014). In correlation with the 
results obtained in the above mentioned studies, the B16 Melanoma Study showed a modest increase in 
survival time for the mice receiving the combined treatment of fractionated radiation and TMX-202 
liposomes compared with control mice or mice receiving either therapy as a monotherapy. However, no 
statistical significant differences were observed in this study, and it can thereby not be concluded whether 
combined treatment of RT and liposomes with TMX-202 represents an effective cancer treatment at this 
point. Relevant changes that could lead to a more optimal outcome of the experimental setup will be 
discussed further down.  
As already mentioned, the study by Dovedi and colleagues showed that the treatment effect of combining 
radiation and Resiquimod was greatest when using fractionated radiation rather than single dose radiation 
(Dovedi et al., 2013). These results are somewhat different to the results obtained in the B16 Melanoma 
Study, since the survival curves for the two combination groups did not demonstrate any significant 
difference. However, when comparing the results it should be taken into consideration that the studies had 
some characteristic differences, like the choice of tumor cell lines. Dovedi and co-workers used lymphoma 
tumors, whereas B16 melanoma tumors were used in the current study. Thus, it was speculated that the 
B16 melanoma tumors are less radiosensitive than the tumor cell lines used in both the study by Dovedi 
and co-workers and the study by Adlard and co-workers (Adlard et al., 2014; Dovedi et al., 2013).  
Indeed, B16 melanoma tumors grow rapidly, and are relatively radio-resistant (Herlyn & Fukunaga-Kalabis, 
2010; Schaue et al., 2012). In the study by Schaue and colleagues, who used mice bearing B16-OVA tumors, 
local radiation doses from 5 Gy – 15 Gy were examined. It was shown that the lowest tumor growth was 
observed when using 15 Gy as a single dose or fractionated doses adding up to 15 Gy (Schaue et al., 2012). 
In the study by Lee and co-workers single dose local radiation of 20 Gy was used to slow tumor growth in 
mice bearing B16 tumors and for some mice the treatment resulted in complete tumor regression (Lee et 
al., 2009). Another study examining the effect of local radiation on B16 tumor regression used single dose 
radiation of 15 Gy or fractionated doses of the same total size (3 Gy x 5). Both treatments slowed tumor 
growth, however only a significant difference in tumor growth was observed between the control group (0 
Gy) and the 15 Gy single dose group (Lugade et al., 2005). Moreover, a study examined the treatment 
effect of combining 15 Gy local radiation with an immunotherapy vaccine (Listeria monocytogenes based 
cancer vaccine) in mice bearing B16-OVA tumors. The study showed significant tumor growth delay in the 
mice that had received the combination treatment compared to mice treated with the vaccine only. 
Furthermore, for some mice the treatment resulted in complete tumor regression (Lim et al., 2014). 
Overall, these in vivo B16 melanoma studies showed significant tumor growth delay or complete tumor 
regression when using single dose local radiation of 15 Gy or more or fractionated doses adding up to 15 
Gy. Thus, it is speculated whether greater effects on tumor regression could have been obtained in the 
current study if the radiation doses had been higher.  
==PAGE:64==
64 
 
Recently, the use of combined treatments for curing cancer has gained increasing interest. However, it can 
be difficult to compare the studies since various administration orders often are used. Adlard and co-
workers for example administered the TLR-7 agonist DSR-6434 4 hours prior to RT, whereas Dovedi and co-
workers state that the treatments were initiated on the same day but do not specify the time in between 
the administration of the immunotherapy and RT (Dovedi et al., 2014; Dovedi et al., 2013). In contrast, the 
study by Lim and co-workers initiated radiation one day before the immunotherapy vaccine (Lim et al., 
2014). Overall, optimal schedules for combined treatments of RT and immunotherapeutic drugs are lacking 
and Dovedi and colleagues have tested different schedules for combining RT and PD-1 (Dovedi et al., 2014). 
However, since PD-1 expression is enhanced by RT these results were not comparable to the current study.    
Furthermore, comparing the effectiveness of the different treatments is made further complex when the 
studies do not state when the treatments were initiated and what the rational in the administration 
scheme was. Some studies state that the treatment was initiated when tumors reached certain sizes, like 
approximately 100mm3 (Dovedi et al., 2013; Dovedi et al., 2014), approximately 180-220mm3 (Adlard et al., 
2014), around 4mm in diameter (Schaue et al., 2012) or when tumors were palpable (Dewan et al., 2009) or 
simply seven days after injection of tumor cells (Lim et al., 2014; Lugade et al., 2005). If the different studies 
all aim at different tumor sizes at initiation of treatment, it will be complicated to compare the 
effectiveness of treatments, since it can be hypothesized that the treatments have to be more potent at 
generating tumor regression in large tumors compared to small tumors. Besides the size of the tumors, 
factors like type of mice, type of tumor, effectiveness of radiation beam, distance from radiation beam etc. 
can influence the study results and thereby whether the treatments seems to be effective or not.  
Since the B16 Melanoma Study did not work out as expected it was decided to investigate the liposomes 
further before eventual new animal studies with combined treatment of liposomes and RT.   
Gadolinium Liposome Distribution Study 
From the studies by Johansen and co-workers it was found that cationic liposomes with certain amounts of 
DOTAP (7.5-10 mol%) showed specific targeting to monocytes, with approximately 45-65 % association to 
monocytes in human blood (POPC:DOTAP (92.5:7.5) and POPC:DOTAP (90:10)) (Johansen et al., 2015). The 
aim of the human Gd distribution studies was therefore to examine the proportion of liposomes that 
associated with blood cells in general compared to the proportion of liposomes in the plasma fraction. 
Cationic, anionic and neutral liposomes with similar formulations as used in the study by Johansen and co-
workers (with the inclusion of cholesterol in the formulations in the current study) were prepared. 
For the cationic liposomes, the results indicated a donor dependent variation with regards to extent of cell 
association, yet a trend demonstrating that the cell association was dose-dependent. Moreover, relatively 
large donor variations were observed for the cell association of neutral liposomes, and no clear dose-
dependent cell association was found in the studies (figure 12, 14 and 18). It was not tested whether 
cationic liposomes associated to blood cells to a significantly higher degree than neutral liposomes at each 
concentration due to large donor variations for the neutral liposomes. It is suggested that more donors are 
included for such studies. In all the experiments using human blood it was shown that the anionic 
liposomes associated poorly with blood cells, and it is assumed that the association observed was due to 
unspecific association (figure 11-14 and 18).  
==PAGE:65==
65 
 
Furthermore, a Gd distribution study examining the influence of incubation time on liposome cell 
association was performed. The study demonstrated that the incubation time did not influence the cell 
association of anionic and neutral liposomes. However, the results indicated that the incubation time 
affected the proportion of cationic liposomes associating to blood cells, and an increase in cationic 
liposome cell association (50 % to 70 % on average) was observed already from 10 to 30 min incubation 
(figure 13). Furthermore, the results indicated that after 30 min incubation a plateau was reached, and a 
minor amount of additional liposomes were associated to the blood cells after more than 30 min 
incubation. These results confirmed that 30 min incubation was sufficient for further studies. Additionally, 
the results indicated that liposomes associated fast with blood cells. It is assumed that the association to 
blood cells primarily represents the association to monocytes based on the results by Johansen and co-
workers (Johansen et al., 2015).  In vivo, it is crucial that the injected liposomes associate fast and efficient 
with monocytes since non-PEGylated liposomes potentially can adhere to and accumulate in other cell 
types than monocytes, e.g. endothelial cells (Gabizon et al., 1991).  
In addition, Gd distribution studies in mice were included in the current Master Project. The studies were 
performed to examine the distribution of especially cationic liposomes and to enable comparisons between 
the liposome distribution in human blood and mouse blood. The results from studies using mouse blood 
indicated that the association of cationic liposomes to blood cells was dose-dependent, like observed in 
human blood. Furthermore, it was found that both anionic and neutral liposomes associated poorly with 
blood cells, although the anionic liposomes seemed to associate to the blood cells to a slightly higher 
degree than the neutral liposomes (figure 15-17). Furthermore, a considerable difference in cell association 
of cationic liposomes was observed between the pilot experiment (Experiment D) and the two other mouse 
experiments (experiment E-F). This is assumed to be due to the use of both different mouse types and 
treated mice in the pilot experiments.    
As part of the studies aiming at optimizing the experimental procedure it was found that anticoagulant 
tubes containing heparin seemed to inhibit the cell association of cationic and neutral liposomes (figure 
11). This is in accordance with the hypothesis that the uptake of liposomes by monocytes is complement 
mediated, since heparin interferes with the complement pathways (Bexborn et al., 2009; Logue, 1977). 
However, the experiment was only performed in blood from one human donor and should therefore be 
repeated before clear conclusions can be drawn.      
Finally, it should be mentioned that the experimental procedure was optimized during the current Master 
Project (as explained in Results, Gadolinium Liposome Distribution) and that the initial datasets therefore 
were not technically optimal. During the optimization process it was found that some liposomes were 
present in the wash liquid. Thus, collecting the wash liquid and measuring the Gd counts in these samples 
besides introducing weighing at critical steps resulted in more reliable data.

Cytokine and Chemokine Studies 
Cytokine Profile after Incubation in BALB/C Blood 
In this study it was demonstrated that liposomes containing TMX-202 can induce expression of IL-12p40, a 
subunit of the Th1 cytokine IL-12, and thus activate immune cells in the blood. This was shown by 
incubating the liposomes in blood from BALB/C mice. The potency to induce IL-12p40 expression varied 
between the liposome formulations (figure 19). It was demonstrated that the Cationic TMX and Cationic #4 
TMX liposomes were more potent inducers of IL-12p40 expression than both Neutral TMX and Anionic TMX 
liposomes (figure 19). This is in accordance with results obtained by Johansen and co-workers which 
demonstrated that cationic liposomes containing TMX-202 induced higher IL-12p40 levels than anionic 
liposomes containing TMX-202 in human blood (Johansen et al., 2015). The formulation of the cationic 
liposome used in the study by Johansen and co-workers (POPC:DOTAP:TMX-202 (81:14:5)) was similar to 
the formulation of Cationic TMX and Cationic #4 TMX liposomes, and the anionic liposome formulation 
(POPC:TMX-202 (95:5)) was relatively similar to the formulation of Anionic TMX. Furthermore, the results 
are in accordance with the results from the Gd liposome distribution studies where it was shown that 
mainly cationic liposomes associated to blood cells when using blood from BALB/C mice. The formulation of 
the cationic liposomes used in the Gadolinium Liposome Distribution studies (POPC:Chol:DOTAP (60:30:10)) 
were comparable to the formulation of Cationic TMX and Cationic #4 TMX liposomes, with the exception of 
TMX-202 content and the corresponding DOTAP content for charge balance. Likewise the neutral and 
anionic liposomes from the Gd liposome and cytokine studies were comparable in their formulations, 
except for the difference in TMX-202 content and the corresponding DOTAP content for charge balance 
(see table2 and 3 in Materials and Methods).     
Results for the neutral and anionic liposomes used in the Gd liposome distribution and cytokine studies 
using BALB/C mouse blood, demonstrated that the neutral liposomes were more potent at inducing IL-
12p40 expression than the anionic liposomes (figure 19). However the association of anionic liposomes to 
blood cells was moderately higher than the association of neutral liposomes to blood cells (figure 16-17). 
This could indicate that the anionic liposomes only associated to the cell surface, whereas the neutral 
liposomes could be taken up and thereby induce IL-12p40 expression.  
Furthermore, the results from the BALB/C incubation study demonstrated that PEGylated cationic 
liposomes induced lower expression of IL-12p40 than non-PEGylated cationic liposomes (figure 19), 
indicating that PEGylated liposomes are taken up by blood cells to a lesser extent than non-PEGylated 
liposomes. This is in accordance with the results from the PET scannings (see discussion section on Positron 
Emission Tomography), and the literature on PEGylated liposomes (Immordino et al., 2006; Johansen et al., 
2015). Moreover, the fact that the IL-12p40 levels increased with increasing DOTAP content in the 
PEGylated liposomes is in accordance with results presented by Johansen and co-workers who found that 
increasing DOTAP concentrations in non-PEGylated liposomes resulted in increased monocyte cell 
association to a certain extent before also targeting other cells (Johansen et al., 2015).  
Cytokine Profile In Vivo 
After confirming the immune activation potency of the cationic liposomes in BALB/C blood it was decided 
to determine whether intravenous administration of cationic TMX-202 liposomes could induce systemic 
immune activation in BALB/C mice. The tested formulations, which included Cationic TMX, Cationic #4 TMX, 
==PAGE:67==
67 
 
Cationic #10 +PEG TMX, free TMX-202, Resiquimod and PBS, appeared to be well tolerated when injected 
IV into the tails of the mice. 
The data from the in vivo BALB/C mouse study demonstrated that liposomes containing TMX-202 can 
induce systemic IL-12p40 and IL-6 secretion. Furthermore, the IL-12p40 levels induced by the liposomes 
were higher than levels induced by free TMX-202 and Resiquimod 6, 24 and 48 hours after injection, 
indicating a prolonged effect when delivering the TLR-7 agonist in liposomes (graph A, figure 20). A similar 
prolonged effect was observed for the IL-6 levels induced by the liposomes (graph B, figure 20). 
Furthermore, the IL-12p40 levels induced by TMX-202 seemed to be prolonged in comparison to the levels 
induced by Resiquimod, supporting the claim that TMX-202 is more potent than Resiquimod (Simon Skjøde 
Jensen, unpublished data). However, the calculated Area Under the Curve indicated that within the 48 
hours where cytokine levels were measured, Resiquimod was more potent at inducing IL-12p40 production 
than TMX-202 (P<0.001, not shown in the graph). Similar results were obtained for the IL-6 cytokine levels, 
since neither TMX-202 nor PBS induced IL-6 expression at any timepoint. These data thereby indicate that 
Resiquimod may have a slightly different cytokine profile than TMX-202 in this mouse model.  
       
In contrast to IL-12p40 levels, IL-6 concentrations dropped fast and were not detectable 24 hours after 
injection of the formulations. Furthermore, the higher level of IL-12p40 compared to IL-6 induced by all 
formulations might indicate the induction of a Th1 immune response.  
Interestingly, the IL-6 levels induced by Cationic #10 + PEG TMX liposomes were higher than the levels 
induced by the other two liposomes. This was not expected, since the PEGylated liposomes are assumed to 
circulate longer and thereby associate to and activate cells to a lesser degree than non-PEGylated 
liposomes. Hence, it is suggested to repeat the experiment for further clarification. 
   
Cationic TMX and Cationic #4 TMX induced similar levels of IL-6 at both time points and comparable levels 
of IL-12p40 2, 24 and 48 hours after injection of the liposomes. However, 6 hours after the injections, the 
Cationic TMX liposomes induced higher levels of IL-12p40 than the Cationic #4 TMX liposomes (figure 20). 
Furthermore, the two liposomes induced different levels of IL-12p40 in the incubation study, although 
opposite from the above mentioned study. These conflicting results might be due to the different 
experimental set-ups and batch to batch variation. As already described, the liposome formulations were 
the same in the Cationic TMX and Cationic #4 TMX liposomes, but the lipid to TMX-202 ratios might be 
slightly different due to 10% uncertainty of composition and concentration determination when preparing 
the liposomes. Thus, one of the batches might have a higher TMX-202 to lipid concentration and thereby be 
more potent than the other batch.   
The results from the in vivo BALB/C mouse study correlate well with studies by both Baenziger and co-
workers and Dovedi and co-workers, since these studies have shown that the TLR-7 agonist Resiquimod can 
induce expression of IL-6 and IL-12p40 in mice (Baenziger et al., 2009; Dovedi et al., 2013). In the study by 
Dovedi and colleagues from 2013 systemic cytokine levels were measured in mice with lymphoma tumors 
after IV injection of 3mg/kg Resiquimod. Blood samples were tapped 2 and 4 hours after Resiquimod 
administration. The IL-6 level in the study by Dovedi and colleagues was relatively high (13000 pg/mL) 2 
hours after injection of Resiquimod. The IL-6 levels induced by Resiquimod and the liposomes in the current 
study, 2 hours after injection, were approximately 10 times lower, even though an equimolar amount of 
Resiquimod and TMX-202 (in the liposomes) had been used (200nmol/mouse) (figure 20). Though these 
==PAGE:68==
68 
 
differences in IL-6 concentrations are not yet clarified, differences between the two studies exist that may 
partly result in the inconsistencies observed. First of all, the mice in the study by Dovedi and co-workers 
bore EG7 lymphoma tumors, whereas the mice from the current study were tumor free. Furthermore, in 
the paper by Dovedi and co-workers it is not stated whether the mice were C57BL/6 mice or BALB/C mice, 
but it is assumed that they were C57BL/6 mice since EG7 is derived from a C57BL/6 mouse lymphoma cell 
line (ATCC, 2014).  
However, when looking at the IL-6 curves from the current study (graph B, figure 20) and assuming that the 
level above 4 hours (approximately 500-700pg/mL) represents the actual level, if measured, it is observed 
that the IL-6 levels induced by Resiquimod and liposomes are more comparable to the levels observed in 
the study by Dovedi and co-workers (Dovedi et al., 2013).  
It is noteworthy that no IL-6 concentration was measured in the BALB/C incubation study when 
concentrations were measured in the in vivo study. However, when looking at the difference in IL-12p40 
concentrations from the BALB/C incubation study and the in vivo study considerable concentration 
differences are observed. Thus, since the IL-6 concentrations were noticeably smaller than the IL-12p40 
concentrations in the in vivo study it is speculated whether the IL-6 levels in the incubation study were too 
close to the sensitivity threshold of the assay.  
Furthermore, in the study by Dovedi and colleagues they measured the level of IL-12p70 (Dovedi et al., 
2013), instead of IL-12p40 which is a subunit in both IL-12 and IL-23. Thus, by measuring IL-12p70 the levels 
observed represents IL-12, whereas the levels of IL-12p40 represents levels of a subunit that can either 
become part of the Th1 cytokine IL-12 or the Th17 cytokine IL-23 (Lin & Karin, 2007). Thus, when measuring 
the IL-12p40 it cannot be stated that the levels measured represents IL-12, but rather it can be stated that 
the levels measured probably represent IL-12 since it is expected that a TLR-7 agonist like TMX-202 will 
activate expression of IL-12 based on the literature (described in the Background). However, no IL-12p70 
was found in the study by Dovedi and colleagues.  
In both the BALB/C incubation study and in vivo study, CXCL-10 levels were measured. However, in neither 
of the studies the levels were higher than vehicle levels. These results are contradictory to CXCL-10 results 
presented in the paper by Adlard and co-workers since they found that the TLR-7 agonist DSR-6434 
stimulated CXCL-10 expression (Adlard et al., 2014). It could be speculated that the differences in the 
results are due to the potency differences between DSR-6434 and TMX-202, and the sensitivity of the assay 
used to measure the cytokine levels.    
Cytokine and Chemokine profile after Incubation in Human Blood 
The data demonstrated that both free TMX-202 and liposomes containing TMX-202 could stimulate IL-
12p40, IL-6 and CXCL-10 expression, and thereby activate immune cells. It was expected that the IL-12p40 
and IL-6 levels would be comparable to the results from the study by Johansen and co-workers for Cationic 
TMX and Anionic TMX liposomes, since the liposome formulations were comparable. However, in the 
current study the IL-6 levels induced by both Cationic TMX and Anionic TMX liposomes were higher than 
the corresponding levels observed in the study by Johansen and co-workers. Furthermore, the IL-12p40 
levels were not comparable between the two studies since Anionic TMX liposomes induced higher levels 
than the anionic liposomes used by Johansen and co-workers, whereas the Cationic TMX liposomes induced 
==PAGE:69==
69 
 
lower levels than the cationic liposomes used by Johansen and co-workers (Johansen et al., 2015). Thus, 
neither cytokine levels nor cytokine profile trends were comparable.   
In the current study, Anionic TMX liposomes and free TMX-202 induced relatively similar levels of IL-6 and 
IL-12p40, and were more potent inducers of the two cytokines than Cationic TMX and Neutral TMX 
liposomes. This was not observed for IL-12p40 in the corresponding BALB/C mouse study (figure 19), where 
the IL-12p40 levels were much lower in the samples that had incubated with Anionic TMX liposomes 
compared to free TMX-202. The IL-12p40 concentration trend among free TMX-202, Cationic TMX and 
Neutral TMX liposomes was similar in the human and BALB/C mouse cytokine studies (figure19 and 22), 
which indicated that the Anionic TMX liposomes might have undergone some changes between the BALB/C 
mouse study (figure 19) and the human study (figure 22). It was speculated whether the TMX-202 could 
have detached from the Anionic TMX liposomes. This could explain why the Anionic TMX liposomes had 
similar potency as free TMX-202.   
To test whether TMX-202 had detached from the Anionic TMX liposomes the liposome diameters and zeta-
potentials were measured. The Cationic TMX and Neutral TMX liposomes were also examined (see 
appendix 9).  
For the Cationic TMX liposomes (JHEN150216-1) the measurements for both diameter and zeta-potential 
were similar to original measurements (see appendix 9). For the Neutral TMX liposomes (JHEN150216-2) 
the diameter measurements were similar to original measurements, but the zeta-potential had changed 
considerably. It was speculated whether this was due to leakage of TMX-202 from the neutral liposomes. 
However, no changes in cytokine profile were observed for the Neutral TMX liposomes in contrast to the 
Anionic TMX liposomes. The zeta-potential of Anionic TMX liposomes (JHEN150216-3) was relatively low (-
13.51±1) compared to the zeta-potential measured after liposome preparation (-19.5±2). Furthermore, the 
liposome diameters were large compared to original measurements made after liposome preparation and 
the measurements varied to a great extent (appendix 9). It was speculated whether the size measurements 
could be due to the Anionic TMX liposomes aggregating, and whether the aggregation was initiated by 
detachment of TMX-202 molecules.  
As already mentioned, TMX-202 is a TLR7-ligand phospholipid conjugate. Furthermore, TMX-202 is 
negatively charged and its phospholipid chain is a C12 hydrocarbon chain. The phospholipid molecules in 
the liposomes used in this project were composed of C18 hydrocarbon chains (see appendix 1). In the 
cationic liposomes, the TMX-202 molecules are anchored strongly in the lipid bilayer due to electrostatic 
attraction between the negatively charged TMX-202 and the positively charged head groups of DOTAP, and 
due to the hydrophobic interactions between the hydrophobic C12 hydrocarbon chain of TMX-202 and the 
hydrophobic C18 hydrocarbon tails of the phospholipids, that constitute the lipid bilayer. The anchoring of 
TMX-202 in the neutral liposomes is mediated by the same interactions, although weaker since less DOTAP 
is present in the liposomes. However, the anionic liposomes do not comprise DOTAP and therefore TMX-
202 is only anchored in the lipid bilayer by hydrophobic interactions with the hydrophobic tails of the 
phospholipids POPC and POPG. Furthermore, the negatively charged TMX-202 might repel the negatively 
charged headgroup of POPG, which makes the anchoring of TMX-202 in the lipid bilayer even less stable. It 
is therefore believed that TMX-202 might be able to detach from the negatively charged liposomes. The 
detachment would create samples with both free TMX-202 and anionic liposomes with various TMX-202 
concentrations. Thus, in the context of this project the possible detachment of TMX-202 from negatively 
==PAGE:70==
70 
 
charged liposomal membranes might make the Anionic TMX sample functionally similar to samples 
containing free TMX-202. This theory could explain the similar levels of IL-12p40 and IL-6 observed after 
human blood incubation with both free TMX-202 and Anionic TMX liposomes (figure 22). The theory might 
also explain the more positive zeta-potential of the neutral liposomes, since the relatively weak 
electrostatic interaction with DOTAP in the neutral liposomes might facilitate partial TMX-202 detachment 
over time.   
Hydrolysis of liposomes and the loss of TMX-202 can cause aggregation of the liposomes, which might 
explain the varying liposome sizes measured for the Anionic TMX liposomes (JHEN150216-3).  
Over time liposomes can start to hydrolyse and hydrolyzation of phospholipids creates lysolipids. MALDI-
TOF was used to test whether lysolipids were present in the Anionic TMX liposome sample. However, it was 
not possible to detect lysolipids in the liposome preparation by means of MALDI-TOF. Nevertheless, 
lysolipids might still be present in the liposome preparation since the MALDI-TOF might not have been able 
to ionize the molecules. A HPLC assay could be used to measure TMX-202 concentration in the liposomes, 
however this was not done due to limited time.  
Due to the contradictory results between the liposome measurements performed after preparation and the 
measurements made after the human cytokine studies, the data is not reliable (figure 22).

Positron Emission Tomography Study 
The aim of the study was to examine the pharmacokinetic profile of the cationic liposomes and study 
where they accumulate in the body. The PET results indicated that most of the liposomes ended up in the 
liver and the spleen, or were expelled in the urine and thereby present in the bladder. However, it should 
be mentioned that the copper not encapsulated in liposomes directly ends up in the bladder and therefore 
the bladder concentrations were relatively high at the first timepoint (15 min after injection). For all 
liposomes, a relatively low proportion seemed to be circulating in the bloodstream compared to the 
proportion of liposomes in the liver, spleen and bladder. However, the PEGylated liposomes showed higher 
concentrations in the blood, and thereby longer circulation time, than the non-PEGylated liposomes. Thus, 
this indicated that the PEGylated liposomes were taken up to a lesser degree by cells of the MPS than non-
PEGylated liposomes, which is consistent with the literature (Immordino et al., 2006; Johansen et al., 2015).   
As previously mentioned, liposomes are taken up by cells of the MPS, like monocytes. MPS cells are present 
in both liver and spleen, and therefore it is believed that the injected liposomes that accumulated in these 
organs were taken up by cells of the MPS (Torchilin, 2005). Since the spleen samples antigens in the blood 
and is involved in immune responses to blood borne microorganisms (Mebius & Kraal, 2005), it is assumed 
that the MPS cells of the spleen differentiate into DCs upon encounter with TMX-202 (Johansen et al., 
2015), which will be delivered by liposomes. In contrast, the liver is an organ important for degradation and 
it is therefore more uncertain whether the MPS cells of the liver will differentiate into DCs upon 
phagocytoses of liposomes containing TMX-202.  
In order to avoid liposome degradation in the liver and thereby obtain efficient targeting of monocytes by 
liposomes, it is important that the liposomes are rapidly associated to the monocytes. In agreement with 
this, the results obtained in the time course study of Gd liposome distribution indicated that the cationic 
liposomes associate rapidly to blood cells in human whole blood (figure 13). It is assumed that the 
liposomes which associated to blood cells mainly associated to monocytes based on the results obtained in 
the study by Johansen and co-workers since the liposome formulations used in the study by Johansen and 
co-workers were similar to the liposomes used in the current study (Johansen et al., 2015). 
The concentration of liposomes accumulating in the tumors was also measured in the mice, and the PET 
scanning data demonstrated low concentrations of liposomes in the tumors. The low concentrations may 
be explained by the fact that no radiation was used in the current study and thereby immune cells, which 
had taken up liposomes, were not recruited to the tumor site. Furthermore, recruitment of immune cells to 
the tumors is facilitated by activation of the immune cells. Formulation C (64Cu-loaded 
POPC:Chol:DOTAP:TMX-202 (50:30:15:5)) was the only liposome formulation that contained TMX-202, and 
thereby had the capacity to activate immune cells (although liposomes in themselves might activate 
immune cells to a low degree). However, formulation C liposomes were not present to a higher degree in 
the tumors than the other liposomes (figure 27). Thus, this could be explained by the lack of radiation of 
the tumor and the time interval of the study, since it might take more than 24 hours for the cells to become 
activated and migrate to the tumor site. Moreover, it was assumed that the PEGylated liposomes 
(formulation B) would be present to a higher degree in the tumors than the other liposomes due to a 
longer circulation time and the EPR effect (Immordino et al., 2006; Johansen et al., 2015). However, this 
was not observed, and it might be due to the PEGylated liposomes having a positive surface charge rather 
than a neutral surface charge, since PEGylated cationic liposomes with similar formulations have been 
==PAGE:72==
72 
 
shown to be taken up by monocytes to a higher degree than neutral PEGylated liposomes when incubated 
in human blood (Johansen et al., 2015).  
The PET study also included liposomes that had pre-incubated in blood before injection (formulation A and 
B). The distribution of pre-incubated liposomes was investigated since it is assumed that the administration 
method might dampen potential toxicity of the liposomes. Furthermore, the pre-incubation might provide 
enhanced cell targeting compared to In Vivo cell targeting and might also decrease the clearance of 
liposomes in the liver. However, the results indicated that pre-incubated liposomes were targeted faster to 
the liver than the directly injected liposomes (figure 23). Furthermore, the pre-incubated PEGylated 
liposomes showed a higher association to the spleen than the remaining liposomes 15 min after injection 
(figure 23). Overall, the study did not indicate any enhanced effect of pre-incubating the liposomes in blood 
before administration.  
Finally, the encapsulation of TMX-202 (formulation C) did not seem to change the distribution pattern of 
the cationic liposome considerably compared to the empty cationic liposome (formulation A).  
Taken together, the results from the PET scannings indicate that the cationic liposomes mainly accumulate 
in the liver and spleen. The liposomes are also present in the bloodstream, but the concentration of non-
PEGylated liposomes drops fast. It is assumed that the injected liposomes are taken up by monocytes in the 
bloodstream, since they associate to monocytes in HWB (Johansen et al., 2015). Thus, the rational is that 
the monocytes from the bloodstream and the spleen, which have taken up liposomes containing TMX-202, 
will differentiate into DCs or M1-macrophages. The liposome therapy will be combined with radiation, since 
radiation mediates recruitment of the cells to the tumor site, as described in the Background. At the tumor 
site, the DCs will encounter TAAs and subsequently migrate to the draining lymph nodes to initiate anti-
tumor immune responses.

Conclusion and Perspectives 
Worldwide, cancer is a leading cause of death and thereby also a highly investigated area. Treatments that 
combine different therapies, like RT and immunotherapy, are gaining increasing interest within the field of 
oncology. The current project aimed at investigating liposomes encapsulating the immunotherapeutic drug, 
TMX-202, and its effect when combined with radiation. Especially cationic liposomes with a specific overall 
formulation were investigated since previous studies had shown specific targeting to monocytes with these 
liposomes (Johansen et al., 2015).  
The combined effect of radiation and cationic liposomes containing TMX-202 was investigated in a mouse 
B16 melanoma model. The study demonstrated that the combined treatment was not effective in the 
experimental setup used and it was speculated that it was due to the radiation dose not being sufficient.  
The cationic liposome candidates were subsequently examined in different ways, and it was found that 
when incubating the liposomes in human blood, the majority of the cationic liposomes associated relatively 
fast to the blood cells. Furthermore, similar results were obtained when incubating the liposomes in blood 
from BALB/C mice, although the data from these distribution studies was not fully technically reliable, but 
only indicative. Based on results from previous studies, it was expected that the liposomes were primarily 
taken up by monocytes in the blood, and therefore it was tested whether liposomes containing TMX-202 
were able to activate these immune cells in both human and mouse blood. It was shown that the lead 
candidate liposome was able to induce systemic immune responses by the enhanced production of IL-6 and 
IL-12p40, compared to mice receiving vehicle. Moreover, a PET study was conducted to test the circulation 
of liposomes and their accumulation in different tissues. It was found that PEGylated cationic liposomes 
circulated for a longer time than non-PEGylated cationic liposomes. Furthermore, the studies demonstrated 
that the cationic liposomes, both PEGylated and non-PEGylated, accumulated in the spleen and liver. Since 
the PEGylated cationic liposomes induced similar systemic IL-6 and IL-12p40 responses as the non-
PEGylated cationic liposomes it could be speculated whether future studies should include these liposomes.    
Overall, the results indicated that the cationic liposomes associated with blood cells to a high degree, 
presumably monocytes, and that they despite a relatively fast clearance from the blood stream were able 
to induce systemic immune responses.    
Considering that Dovedi and co-workers successfully treated mice with lymphoma tumors by using the TLR-
7 agonist Resiquimod in combination with radiotherapy (Dovedi et al., 2013), makes it interesting to 
perform an almost identical study with the primary difference being the use of liposomes containing TMX-
202 instead of free Resiquimod. Thus, if the mouse type, tumor, radiation dose and treatment scheme were 
identical to the parameters used in the study by Dovedi and co-workers it would shed light on the effect of 
the liposomes containing TMX-202 in combination with RT. Such a study would thereby give a more clear 
answer to whether the combination therapy will work in patients in a long-term base and take the MonTa 
lead candidate a step closer to Phase I clinical trials.

1. Prepare Chloroform-Methanol (9:1), place in fume-hood.

3.  Prepare glass vials (8mL) by labelling.

stay open on these vials as they are being dehydrated anyway. 
7. Place vials under N2 dehydration for about 15 minutes at approximately 35-40oC. 
8. When samples are dry, cover vials with parafilm and punch 10 holes in the film.

Characterization 
 
Size 
1. Dust is removed by washing the cuvette with sterile filtered HEPES buffer (10mM HEPES, pH7.4, 
290mOsm/kg, 150mM NaCl). 
2. 2µL of the formulation is diluted in 2ml sterile filtered HEPES buffer. 
3. The sample is recorded for 3 x 30 seconds. 
4. A minimal countrate of 400kcps is accepted. 
 
Zeta-potential 
1. The electrode is cleaned in ethanol and water and following conditioned by running 300 cycles in 
1M KCl. The electrode is subsequently rinsed with MQ water. 
2. Dust is removed by washing the cuvette with sterile filtered Sucrose buffer (10% Sucrose, 10mM 
HEPES pH7.4, 1mM CaCl2). 
3. 4µL of the formulation is diluted in 2ml sterile filtered Sucrose buffer. 
4. The electrode is inserted and 10runs is conducted. 
5. The average Zeta-potential is recorded.

Appendix 6 
Positron Emission Tomography  
This section is based on the book PET and PET-CT in Oncology by Peter Oehr, Hans-Jürgen Biersack and R. 
Edward Coleman and the book Molecular Imaging: Radiopharmaceuticals for PET and SPECT by Shankar 
Vallabhajosula.  
Positron Emission Tomography (PET) is a nuclear medical imaging technique that can be used to trace 
biochemical substances, like pharmaceuticals, in patients over time. The biochemical substances of interest 
are labeled with positron emitters (radioactive tracers) such as 15O, 13N or 11C and injected into the patient. 
Subsequently, the patient is placed in the PET-scanner where detectors register radiation from the body. 
Consequently, mathematical algorithms are used to construct tomography1 images of the temporal and 
spatial distribution of radioactive tracer in organs and tissues. Thus, PET is a non-invasive method that 
allows for determination of the time-course of substance concentrations in specific locations within the 
body.  
However, since the PET images alone provide poor anatomical details retrospective image registering and 
fusion of the information provided by PET scannings with corresponding anatomical details from Computed 
Tomograpgy (CT) imaging is widely used. Nevertheless, the fusion of the PET and CT images can be 
complicated due to differences in the image data sets like changes in the position of the patient. This 
problem has been overcome by developing devices where the two tomographs are placed in-line or where 
a detector system for both scans is used, which makes patient repositioning unnecessary. The combined 
CT/PET scanners have been available commercially since around 2000.   
As already mentioned, PET is based on radiation. More specifically, PET is based on annihilation of positrons 
and electrons. The positrons and electrons are created during β+-decay of the radioactive tracers used in 
substance labeling. Collision of the positron and electron results in annihilation of the two which creates 
two gamma rays that are emitted back-to back. Hence, the tracers used to label biochemical substances 
emit pairs of gamma rays which can be detected by the PET system and thereby the location of the 
annihilation process can be calculated. Thus, the PET system detects the position of the annihilation 
process and not the position of the positron emission which can result in uncertainty of tracer localization 
in the millimeter range.  
The concentration in a specific body location of interest is determined by the use of a region of interest 
(ROI). When drawing ROIs on the three-dimensional fused images (PET and CT images) the software will
