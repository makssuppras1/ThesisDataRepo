ABSTRACT
In recent years, with the burgeoning popularity of streaming short videos, the use of video trans-
mission devices in daily life has become increasingly widespread. The imperative challenge currently
is the efficient encoding of videos and the swift transmission and storage of video data. Addressing
these concerns, thesis work focuses on the streaming of online meetings based on H.265 and aims
at an optimized search algorithm rooted in motion estimation.
In order to optimize video encoding efficiency, I conducted a thorough analysis of the encoding
performance of the six currently implemented search algorithms in the x265 encoder, by utilizing
the concepts of motion estimation in inter-frame prediction. This investigation culminated in the
proposal of an improved Star search algorithm. In this optimized method, I incorporate an early
termination criterion for searches, which is derived from a distribution analysis of the optimal cost
in video pictures. By increasing the number of early exits from the search process upon locating
the optimal cost, I observed a substantial reduction in video encoding time. Empirical tests across
various video sequences demonstrated that the optimized Star search algorithm not only upholds
the encoding quality but also reduces encoding time when based on specific video characteristics
and encoding time preferences. This signifies a more precise and efficient video codec realization.

stigning i popularitet af streaming af korte videoer. Deraf følger større forventninger om høj bil-

videoer. Dette projekt undersøger optimering af H.265-videokodning for streaming af online møder
og med særlig fokus på søgealgoritmer til bevægelsesestimation.
Jeg gennemførte først en analyse af kodningsydelsen af de seks søgealgoritmer implementeret i
x265-koderen (en open source H.265/HEVC-koder). Efter denne undersøgelse har jeg designet en

forskellige videosekvenser viser, at den optimerede Star-Search algoritme reducerer kodningstiden
uden at påvirke billedkvaliteten. Dette betyder en mere effektiv videokodning.

Introduction
1.1
Background and Demand for Online Video Conferencing Systems
Live streaming and quick video social interactions have become a huge part of our daily lives in
recent years. The use of video transmission technology has significantly decreased communication
costs between people while also improving work efficiency. There is a significant surge in the volume
of video information with the rise in video resolution. Videos take up the majority of network traffic
used for data transmission. When being transmitted over the network, this increased volume uses
more network bandwidth, and when being stored, it takes up more memory.
Achieving effective video encoding has become crucial, especially with the move to high-definition
(HD) video and the spread of HD surveillance equipment. Practical difficulties have arisen due to
the requirement for real-time transmission and storage of such massive amounts of video data. Video
encoding, which aims to remove redundant data from videos and improve the efficiency of real-time
video transmission, is a more effective way to deal with these problems. The original video can be
recovered through decoding. Figure 1 illustrates the fundamental steps in the encoding, decoding,
and transmission of real-time video.
Figur 1: Real-time Video Encoding and Decoding and Real-time Transmission Process
The ability to encode has consistently been one of the crucial benchmarks for measuring system
performance in video transmission systems. Transmission latency can be reduced by using effective
encoding algorithms to lower the network bandwidth occupancy rate[1]. Common codecs like H.263,
H.264, and MPEG are frequently used, but due to their poor encoding efficiency, they struggle to
meet the technical requirements of encoding modern ultra-high-definition videos like 1080P, 2K,
and 4K.
The H.265 (or HEVC) codec was launched to satisfy this changing demand. The H.265 codec boasts
50% higher compression rates and 50% lower bit rates when compared to its forerunner, H.264[2].
A notable issue with HEVC, though, is the sharp rise in encoding complexity that occurs as bit
rates drop[3]. Given the variability in HEVC’s encoding speed and compression rates across different
encoding parameter combinations, it is vital to harness the capabilities of HEVC’s open-source en-
coding libraries. It is crucial to experimentally validate and analyze these parameter combinations.
Furthermore, introducing new and improved fast search estimation algorithms can boost compres-
sion effectiveness even more, easing the load on video storage and enabling more fluid real-time
transmission.

1.2
Development of H.265 Coding and Motion Compensation
Video codec standards are primarily established by the International Telecommunication Union’s
Telecommunication Standardization Sector (ITU-T) and the International Standardization Orga-
nization (ISO) in conjunction with the International Electrotechnical Commission (IEC)[4]. ITU-
T developed all of the widely used protocols, including H.261[5], H.262, H.263[6], H.264[7], and
H.265/HEVC[8]. The H.26X series of protocols is predominantly designed for low-latency scenarios
like video calls, video conferences, and the like. H.265/HEVC, the most recent iteration, is utilized
in a wide range of video scenarios, including but not limited to video storage, video transmission,
and immersive video experiences.
H.26x Series
In 1988, the ITU-T unveiled the H.261, its inaugural video codec standard, uti-
lizing the intermediate CIF format with a 352x288 resolution at 30FPS, tailored for low-bitrate,
high real-time contexts[9]. The H.262 standard had appeared by 1992, offering better resolutions
primarily aimed at digital TV broadcasting and AV devices. Following H.263, which supported
five different resolutions[10], improved image quality. Compared to H.263, H.263+ optimized intra-
frame prediction algorithms. The H.264 standard delved into inter-frame correlations, introducing
17 intra-frame prediction techniques. This standard found widespread application in fields like re-
mote surveillance and video conferencing because it provided strong support and adaptability for
various bitrates and resolutions of network image transmissions[11]. While H.265 and H.264 employ
the same hybrid encoding framework, the former expands its intra-frame prediction techniques to
35. Compared to H.264, H.265 achieved a 50% bitrate reduction and a 50% increase in compression
ratio. Additionally, it added adaptive compensation encoding, expanding resolution and application
support while enhancing robustness during compression and error recovery capabilities[12][13].
Motion Estimation in H.265
In H. 265/HEVC as the widely used video codec standard, mo-
tion estimation emerges as a technologically demanding component with the highest temporal
complexity[14]. Motion search algorithms in motion estimation can generally be bifurcated into
two categories: full search algorithm and fast search algorithms. The full search algorithm syste-
matically scans every candidate block within a given search range in a specific order, identifying
the best matching block[14]. Compared to fast search algorithms, the full search method boasts the
highest encoding efficiency but also demands the most significant computational effort.
The UMHexagons method was introduced within the H.264 architecture, utilizing a hexagonal grid
search pattern[15]. In contrast, HEVC’s innovative TZSearch algorithm adopts an adaptive search
range[13]. This adaptive approach refines the search process using either rectangular or diamond-
shaped search templates.

2.1
H.265/HEVC Coding Technology
This chapter delves into the hybrid video encoding framework and pivotal techniques of High Ef-
ficiency Video Coding (HEVC) as defined by the H.265 standard[16]. Initially, the principles of
intra-frame and inter-frame encoding are introduced in Section 2.1.1, grounded in the H.265/HEVC
video hybrid encoding framework. Subsequently, Section 2.1.2 outlines the distinctive features
of H.265/HEVC, contrasting them with its predecessor, H.264/Advanced Video Coding (AVC),
while briefly highlighting the advantages of HEVC. It is important to remember that while the
H.265/HEVC standard specifies the decoding procedure, the encoding methodology is left open-
ended, allowing any method that results in a bitstream that complies.
2.1.1
Hybrid Encoding Framework of H.265/HEVC
Like H.264/AVC, H.265/HEVC also adopts a hybrid encoding framework. A hybrid encoding fra-
mework is formed by combining intra frame encoding and inter frame encoding techniques. In
particular, the video encoder first chooses a keyframe (typically using intra frame encoding), and
then uses inter frame encoding for subsequent frames, so that only the differences between the en-
coding and the keyframe are required. By combining the benefits of intra and inter frame encoding,
this technique achieves greater compression efficiency than a single technique.
The general processing flow is prediction, transformation, quantization, and entropy encoding,
and each process is processed in blocks. The principles of multiple frameworks, including intra
frame coding framework, inter frame coding framework, hybrid coding framework, and decoding
framework, will be introduced in the following.

Decoding Framework
After encoding, decoding is required to obtain playable video sequences.
The decoding framework is shown in Figure 5.
Figur 5: Block Diagram of Decoder
The detailed process is presented below:
1. Input H.265 encoded data for entropy decoding.
2. Perform inverse quantization and inverse transform to get the residual frame.
3. Find the corresponding prediction block in the reference frame based on the decoded motion

4. Combine the prediction block and residual block mentioned above and then come through a

5. Store the reconstructed frame in the image buffer as a reference frame for the next predicted

6. Repeat the steps above until decoding ends.
2.1.2
Key Technologies of H.265/HEVC
Coding Tree Unit(CTU) and Coding Tree Block(CTB)
In previous coding standards,
the core of the encoding layer was macroblocks, but in HEVC, the corresponding structure CTU
is chosen by the encoder and can be larger than a traditional macroblock[2]. The size of CTU is
typically set to a default of 64x64 pixels. To smaller dimensions like 16x16 or even 32x32, this can be

into several CUs, and one CU can be further split into multiple prediction units(PUs) and transform
units(TUs)[18]. The CU has a minimum size of 8x8 and can extend up to the maximum size of 64x64.
The size of the CTU essentially determines the maximum size of a CU. As illustrated in Figure 6,
each CTU recursively divides into four identical CUs, where every non-leaf node encompasses four

Intra-Picture Prediction
The objective of intra-picture prediction is to reduce spatial redun-
dancy within a single image frame by utilizing neighboring pixels of the target encoding block as
reference pixels. The residual block, in video coding terminology, represents the difference between
the original block and its predicted version[2]. Instead of sending or storing the whole block, it
is often better to encode the residual, which usually has smaller values because of the prediction
process. The principle of intra-picture prediction is the elimination of similar blocks inside a given
frame while preserving solely the residual data. This process enables the subsequent restoration of
the original data during the decompression phase.
As shown in Figure 9, the intra-prediction module in the HEVC standard can support up to 35
different angular prediction modes. The DC mode[20], the Planar mode, and 33 various angular
modes are among the 35 prediction modes. This variety of modes enables a more exact alignment
with the content structure of image blocks, significantly improving intra-prediction accuracy.

Inter-Picture Prediction
The inter-picture prediction adopts block-based motion compensation
technology. The principle is: for each pixel block of the current frame, a best-matching block is sought
in a reference frame that was previously encoded. This process is known as Motion Estimation (ME).
The frame that has been encoded and utilized for prediction is commonly known as the reference
frame. The displacement from the reference block to the current pixel block is termed the Motion
Vector (MV), while the distinction between the current pixel block and the reference block is referred
to as the Prediction Residual. In H.265/HEVC, the Merge and AMVP (Advanced Motion Vector
Predictor) algorithms are the most commonly used[22].
Transform and Quantization
Image frame data compression is accomplished by the utilization
of transformation coding and quantization techniques, which facilitate the conversion between the ti-
me and frequency domains. The aforementioned procedure can be classified as lossy compression[23].
In-Loop Filter
Filtering is used in a variety of situations to remove noise. During the H.265
encoding and decoding procedure, the encoded video frame data encompasses many interferences
that have the potential to result in image blurring, pixelation, and other related problems. In order to
recover the original image, H.265 utilizes in-loop filters to alleviate image distortions resulting from
the previously mentioned intra/inter-picture prediction and transform/quantization procedures. In-
Loop filters primarily consist of two modules: Deblocking Filter (DF) and Sample Adaptive Offset
(SAO)[24]. The utilization of SAO has been introduced as a novel approach within the H.265/HEVC
framework. The process of SAO involves the classification of the reconstructed image according
to the disparities seen between the present pixel and its neighboring pixels[25]. This technique
then proceeds to modify the pixel values by either increasing or decreasing them, with the aim of
minimizing distortion and improving both the objective and subjective quality of the video image.
Entropy Encoding
H.265/HEVC standard utilizes entropy coding as a means to eliminate re-
dundant data in the process of video compression. The principle is the utilization of binary encoding
to transform the data produced in the preceding stages into a string format, hence resulting in a
reduction in data size. The utilization of this coding technique results in a notable reduction in

2.2
Principles of Motion Estimation in Inter-Picture Prediction
The H.265/HEVC standard employs a block-based Motion Estimation (ME) technique, where a
video frame is partitioned into several blocks. ME refers to the process of seeking the best matching
block for a current block within the already encoded frames. This process involves the calculation
of the Motion Vector (MV)[27]. Adjacent frames are divided into independent pixel blocks using
CTU. Based on the search range, the algorithm looks for the block with the least mistake in the
reference frame. It does this by using block-matching principles to figure out how well the search
block matches the block in the current frame. During the encoding of the current frame, only the
reference image’s position information and the determined MV need to be sent. Using the reference
frame and residual information, the current frame is anticipated during decoding.
Block Matching Criteria
ME requires a criterion to determine the degree of similarity betwe-
en the search blocks. Various block-matching criteria exhibit different levels of precision, leading
to variances in the coefficients of the residual blocks. Consequently, these changes can result in
discrepancies in the compression rate. These criteria include Mean Square Error (MSE), Absolute
Difference (AD), Sum of Absolute Differences (SAD), and Maximum Number of Matching Pixels
(MPC).

3.1.1
FFmpeg
FFmpeg is a versatile technical framework designed for the processing of audio and video data,
capable of functioning across many operating systems. It occupies a prominent role within the
domain of audio and video processing. FFmpeg possesses a robust capability for processing audio and
video and supports the majority of audio and video encoding standards[29]. FFmpeg encompasses
three command-line tools: ffmpeg, ffplay, and ffprobe. Specifically, ffmpeg is a tool for audio and
video processing, ffplay serves as a media player for audio and video, and ffprobe is utilized for
analyzing audio and video files.
3.1.2
x265
The open-source HEVC video encoding project, x265, was initiated by MulticoreWare Inc. in 2013.
The primary objective of this endeavor was to offer customers an HEVC video encoder that is both
highly efficient and capable of delivering exceptional performance[30]. The architectural framework
of x265 is similar to that of the open-source H.264/AVC encoder, x264. x265 provides encoding
software for both the Windows and Linux operating systems. The source code of x265 is implemented
in the C++ programming language and it also includes a functional interface that is targeted towards
the C programming language. The x265 library is an open-source software that provides support
for the HEVC standard. It offers anyone the freedom to access and alter its source code without
any cost.
3.1.3
UDP Transmission
During the encoding process, it is necessary to transfer encoded video data across many endpoints
over the internet. This transmission necessitates the careful selection of an appropriate network
transmission protocol. There are typically two types of network transmission protocols: Transmis-
sion Control Protocol (TCP) and User Datagram Protocol (UDP).
UDP is a protocol from the transport layer of the network model. One notable attribute of this
technology is its ability to transmit data without the need for establishing a formal connection. The
elimination of the handshake process between endpoints is justified by the absence of a necessity
for a connection. Likewise, there’s no mechanism to detect disconnection. In addition, it should
be noted that UDP does not provide a guarantee of the successful delivery of data packets to the
intended recipient. Rather, it just transmits the packets onto the network, delivering them to the
best of its ability. The low dependability of the UDP protocol is a consequence of this. Therefore,
it is frequently utilized in situations when the preservation of data integrity is not of paramount
importance.
Compared to the connection process of UDP, TCP is considerably more complex. Beyond the
three-way handshake and four-step termination, it also involves mechanisms such as resuming from
breakpoints, disconnection detection, flow control, and congestion control[31]. Therefore, due to its
simplicity, UDP was chosen for the applications discussed in this paper.

SI (Spatial Information) and TI (Temporal Information) are two crucial parameters to measure the
complexity of video content and are widely used in video codec and quality assessment[4].
SI
Spatial Information (SI) represents a measurement that indicates the amount of spatial detail
within a frame of a video, which has been mentioned in ITU-T Rec. P.910[4]. According to P.910,
the Simula of SI calculation for a video sequence is shown in Eq 7.
SI = maxtime {stdspace [Sobel (Fn)]} [32]
(7)
where Fn represents the frame n, Sober is the Sober Fliter, stdspace is the standard deviation for
filtered images. The maximum value among these frames is selected as SI.

variations in a video sequence[4]. This information can show the movement difference between
adjacent frames. The calculation formula for TI is as follows:
TI = max {std [Fn (i, j) −Fn−1 (i, j)]} [33]
(8)
where Fn is the pixel located at the i-th row and j-th column, corresponding to the n-th time
position. Fn(i, j) −Fn−1(i, j) is the movement difference between two adjacent frames.

Comparison of Six Motion Search Algorithms
In the H.265/HEVC standard, inter-frame prediction stands out as the most computationally inten-
sive key technology. In order to predict the pixel values of the current frame, inter-frame prediction
uses the pixel values of previously encoded frames as a reference. In contrast to intra-frame coding,
temporal redundancies are removed by encoding the residual signal between the current and predi-
cted frames. This increases coding efficiency. Motion Estimation (ME) and Motion Compensation
(MC) are the main techniques used for this[35]. During the encoding-decoding process, the encoder
transmits the motion data information of the current frame. The decoder then reconstructs the
current frame using motion compensation based on the received data.
As presented in Section 3.1.2, x265 is a free software library and application for encoding video
streams into the H.265/HEVC compression format. The six intrinsic motion search methods avai-
lable within x265-dia, hex, umh, star, sea, and full[16]-will be compared in this chapter. The com-
parison in this chapter will make use of the exact same transmission channel that was established in
Section 3 using ffmpeg. By doing this, it is ensured that the testing environment will always reflect
relevant real-world scenarios.
4.1
Motion Search Algorithms: An Overview and Principle
In the process of ME, many search algorithms are utilized to locate the optimal matching block.
It is part of the encoding process that has the highest time complexity. Currently, the effectiveness
and image quality of motion search algorithms is primarily evaluated based on Peak Signal-to-Noise
Ratio (PSNR), computational complexity, and bitrate[36]. An efficient motion estimation algorithm
must strike a balance among these three aspects to achieve optimal overall performance. In x265,
there are six motion search methods. Diamond search is one of the best-performing algorithms.
Hexagon search is marginally complicated. Uneven Multi-Hexagon is a modified version of the
search algorithm employed by x264 for its slower presets[16]. Star algorithm is a modified version of
the HM encoder’s[37] three-step search method. It involves a star-pattern search as the initial step,
which can be followed by an optional radix scan and an optional star-search refinement. Full is a
thorough search that is an order of magnitude slower than all other searches but not significantly
better than umh or star[16]. SEA algorithm bears a resemblance to x264’s ESA implementation and
can be viewed as a performance enhancement of full search method.
4.1.1

DIA
Diamond search(DIA) is one of the current fast block-matching algorithms with good per-
formance. Diamond search algorithm was proposed by Zoran et al in [38], which aims to reduce the
complexity of computation even more.

matching point, and the associated block is identified as the most suitable matching block. If the
MBD point isn’t at the template’s center, the search process continues by utilizing the current MBD

STAR
The specific process of star search is as follows:
Step 1: AMVP technology[41] is used to provide multiple candidate MVs. The distortion cost for
each MV in candidate MVs is calculated. The smallest MV is identified as the starting point.
Step 2: The step size is subject to variation, commencing at 1 and progressively escalating by a
factor of 2. This process is employed in an 8-point diamond search pattern, encompassing upward,
downward, leftward, rightward, and diagonal directions, while not exceeding the maximum number
of searches.
Step 3: If the optimal step size obtained in step two is one, two-point diamond search will be
employed.

SEA
Successive elimination algorithm(SEA) is an efficient motion estimation search method. Its
primary objective is to reduce computational complexity by eliminating candidate points that are
deemed unlikely to constitute the optimal motion vector. The specific steps are as follows:
Step 1: Determine Search Range: The range of search in the x and y directions is determined based
on the original motion vector(OMV).
Step 2: Initialize: The cost of x and y in MV is computed beforehand and saved to reduce the
computation cost. Next, initialize other related parameters.
Step 3: Set Search Mode and Integration Plane: Appropriate search mode and integration plane is
chosen according to the size and shape of PU.
Step 4: DC Pre-Filtering: Before calculating the complete SAD (Sum of Absolute Differences), pre
filter by comparing DC values.
Step 5: Step-by-Step Search: Iterate through all y-coordinates within the predefined range. For each
y-coordinate, filter using pre-calculated costs. Use the ADS (Adaptive Search) to further reduce the
number of candidate points. To determine the remaining candidate points, the SAD should be
calculated. The best match point and its corresponding cost will be updated at the same time.
Step 6: Cleanup: Release the previously allocated temporary buffer.
FULL
Full search(FULL) is a motion search algorithm with good search performance and longest
search time. It systematically explores every candidate position within the designated search window
using a spiral search pattern. At each candidate position, the algorithm computes the matching
difference and subsequently evaluates the residual difference to identify the optimal matching point.

In-depth Analysis of the Star Search Algorithm and
Improvements Based on Cost Threshold
It became clear in Section 4 that the Star Search algorithm is particularly suitable for our video
transmission system. In-depth analysis of the Star Search Pattern’s intricate details, a list of poten-
tial improvements, and an examination of the viability of particular changes are all covered in this
section. Through testing, I hope to determine how effective these changes are at maintaining the
core of the Star Search algorithm while also enhancing it for better performance in the transmission
framework.
5.1
Summary of the Star Search Algorithm
5.1.1
Overview of Basic Steps
As previously described, the Star Search algorithm employs a diamond search pattern as it advances
iteratively to improve the motion vectors (MV). Its modus operandi begins by prioritizing the MV
with the minimum distortion cost. As the algorithm progresses, it employs an adaptive step size
that iteratively expands, yet always remains within a pre-defined maximum boundary. There are
moments when the search narrows to a precise two-point diamond pattern, specifically when certain
conditions are met. Additionally, the algorithm is equipped with an inherent check, a threshold for
the optimal step size, ensuring the search doesn’t overshoot its mark. As part of its core iterative
nature, the best matching point from the previous iteration often becomes the starting point for the
next, fostering continuous refinement. This iterative process is sustained until there’s convergence,
post which the most optimal MVs and SADs are duly stored for subsequent operations.
5.1.2
Principles and Mechanism
Based on the source code of x265[42], the implementation of the Star Search algorithm within x265
is illustrated in Figure 23.
Here are explanations of some parameters mentioned in the pipeline:
• Best Point Number(bPointNr): the number of the point in the current search iteration that
has the lowest cost obtained from the search. This point will be marked as the best point for
the current iteration.
• Best Distance(bDistance): the distance between the optimal point of the current iteration and
the center point. In the star search algorithm, bDistance is used to determine search accuracy
and whether further refinement of the search is necessary. For example, if the bDistance is
very small, a further search may not be necessary because a sufficiently good motion vector
has been found. On the contrary, if the bDistance is large, further refinement of the search
may be necessary.
• EarlyExitIters: the number of iterations at which the search is given up. This is done to save
computational time when it is thought unlikely that more iterations will result in a motion
vector candidate that is noticeably better.
• Best Motion Vector(bmv): the best motion vector discovered so far in the search process. If a
better motion vector (one that results in a lower cost when encoding the block) is discovered
as the search goes on, bmv will be updated to this new motion vector.

5.2
Motivation Behind Introducing Cost Threshold
Based on the principles of the star search algorithm described in the preceding section, the algorithm
determines the cost for each search point in each iteration, even when EarlyExistLiter is used to
reduce the number of searches. By integrating a cost threshold, the algorithm can preemptively
terminate the search once a certain level of matching or distortion is achieved. According to [43],
adding an adaptive cost threshold has been shown to increase the search algorithm’s effectiveness.
In order to further increase the coding efficiency, I considered to add a fixed cost threshold in Star
search algorithm.

Strategy for Calculating the Cost Threshold
The source code of x265 is written in C++. To facilitate the modification, compilation, and execution
of the source code, version x265-3.3 was chosen as the basis for my work. It should be noted that
compatibility is crucial when working with such complex tools; consequently, given that the most
recent supported version, vc15, is compatible with Visual Studio 2017 (VS2017), I chose to use
VS2017 as our main development environment. This guarantees a smooth process for development
and testing.

search algorithm has a mixed impact on encoding time across different videos and compression
levels (crf values). Sometimes the threshold can lead to time savings, sometimes it increases the
time. Bitrate is marginally affected by the threshold, showing a very slight increase. However, in
terms of perceptual quality (PSNR), the threshold seems to have a negligible effect. Therefore, rather
than focusing on video quality, the choice of whether to use the threshold optimization in the star
search algorithm should be based on specific video characteristics and encoding time preferences.

6
Conclusion
6.1
Summary of the Main Findings of the Research
This research centered on enhancing fast search estimation techniques within the ambit of inter-
frame prediction, grounded in the H.265/HEVC encoding framework. With the creation of a real-
time video transmission system founded on the H.265/HEVC standard, the integration of improved
algorithms yielded marked advancements in video encoding efficiency.
Detailed analyses were presented, emphasizing two critical parameters: Constant Rate Factor (CRF)
and bitrate. For a fixed CRF scenario, four algorithms - dia, hex, umh, and star - proved to be su-
perior, characterized by their shortened encoding times and stable bitrates. Conversely, when the
bitrate was held constant, these algorithms demonstrated consistent performance with minimal
fluctuations in the Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio (PSNR).
In order to explore the interaction between other video resolutions and the aforementioned mo-
tion search algorithms, the transmission performance of different resolutions from 720p to 4K was
analyzed. Preliminary results indicated that the four algorithms demonstrated comparable outco-
mes. In this deep dive, the ’ultrafast’ preset of the x265 encoder was a focal point, primarily due to
its latency benefits crucial for real-time online meetings.
Furthermore, the research unveiled that for video transmission in this system, the star search algo-
rithm stood out. It managed to harmonize efficiency, manifested as reduced encoding times, with an
impressive bitrate. Building on the core tenets of the star search algorithm, the research proposed
an enhancement. Introducing a cost threshold mechanism could potentially preemptively halt the
search, optimizing efficiency.
Experimental setups validated these hypotheses, employing videos recorded at a consistent 720P
resolution. Both the standard and the enhanced versions of the star search algorithm underwent
rigorous testing, revealing the benefits and trade-offs of incorporating a cost threshold. On balance,
while the optimized version exhibited marginally inferior image encoding quality, it showcased a
dramatic reduction in encoding time when based on specific video characteristics and encoding time
preferences.
6.2
Discussion of the Limitations of the Study and Suggestions for Future Re-
search
The study, though extensive, presents some limitations. The experiments conducted were predomi-
nantly in controlled environments, potentially not replicating the dynamism of real-world scenarios.
A key limitation is the fixation on a 32x32 coding block size.
Future avenues could explore:
• Analysis of different coding block sizes and their implications on motion estimation.
• Real-time video transmission, pushing the boundaries of the optimized algorithms.
• The interplay between latency, video quality, and resolution, especially for online transmis-
sions.

Preset Options in H.265
x265 has ten predefined −−preset options that optimize the trade-off between encoding speed
(encoded frames per second) and compression efficiency (quality per bit in the bitstream). The
default preset is medium. It does a reasonably good job of finding the best possible quality without
spending excessive CPU cycles looking for the absolute most efficient way to achieve that quality.
When you use faster presets, the encoder takes shortcuts to improve performance at the expense of
quality and compression efficiency. When you use slower presets, x265 tests more encoding options,
using more computations to achieve the best quality at your selected bit rate (or in the case of –crf
rate control, the lowest bit rate at the selected quality).
The presets adjust encoder parameters as shown in the following table. Any parameters below
that are specified in your command-line will be changed from the value specified by the preset.
• ultrafast
• superfast
• veryfast
• faster
• fast
• medium (default)
• slow
• slower
• veryslower
• placebo
